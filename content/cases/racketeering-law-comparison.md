## Summary

Racketeering -- the operation of illegal business schemes through organized criminal enterprises -- is targeted by distinct but converging legal frameworks across jurisdictions. The United States pioneered enterprise-based prosecution with the Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations (RICO) Act of 1970, which allows authorities to charge entire criminal organizations based on a pattern of predicate offenses, with penalties of up to 20 years imprisonment and asset forfeiture. South Korea addresses organized crime through overlapping statutes: the Criminal Act provisions on criminal syndicates (Article 114) and the Punishment of Violence Act, which imposes penalties up to death or life imprisonment for leaders of violent criminal groups. Germany criminalizes membership in criminal organizations under Section 129 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), punishing founders and members with up to five years imprisonment and ringleaders with up to ten years, without requiring proof of participation in any specific crime. The European Union harmonizes organized crime prosecution through Council Framework Decision 2008/841/JHA, which requires member states to criminalize participation in criminal organizations with maximum penalties of at least two to five years, while the European Court of Human Rights constrains these prosecutions through fair trial guarantees under Article 6 and privacy protections under Article 8 of the Convention.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea addresses organized crime through multiple overlapping statutes rather than a single comprehensive racketeering law comparable to RICO.

**Criminal Act, Article 114 (Criminal Syndicate Formation):** Any person who organizes a syndicate or group with the intent to commit a crime punishable by death, life imprisonment, or imprisonment of at least four years, or who joins such a syndicate, shall be punished according to the penalty prescribed for the intended crime, subject to possible mitigation. This provision targets the organizational structure itself, criminalizing group formation even before substantive offenses are committed.

**Punishment of Violence Act (Punbeop, 폭력행위등처벌에관한법률):** Originally enacted in 1961 and repeatedly amended, this special statute imposes aggravated penalties for violent crimes committed in organized contexts:

| Role | Penalty |
|------|---------|
| Leader of violent criminal group | Death, life imprisonment, or min. 10 years |
| Principal member | Life imprisonment or min. 7 years |
| Other members | Min. 2 years imprisonment |

These penalties apply even absent proof of specific criminal acts by the individual -- membership and rank alone establish criminal liability.

**Act on Aggravated Punishment of Specific Crimes:** This statute enhances penalties for organized crime figures who commit specific offenses including drug trafficking, kidnapping for ransom, and robbery leading to death or injury.

**Proceeds of Crime Act:** Enacted to strip criminal organizations of their economic base, this law authorizes the confiscation and recovery of proceeds derived from criminal activity, including assets laundered through legitimate businesses.

### Key Principles

**Collective criminal liability:** Korean courts allow conviction for organizational membership without requiring evidence of direct participation in specific predicate offenses. This approach mirrors the German model more closely than the American RICO framework, which demands proof of a pattern of racketeering activity.

**Gang-related sentencing enhancement:** When violent crimes are committed by members of organized groups, the Punishment of Violence Act mandates penalties significantly above the base sentencing range. Courts examine organizational hierarchy and the defendant's role when determining the applicable penalty tier.

### Landmark Developments

In 2023-2024, the Korean National Police Agency conducted large-scale crackdowns on organized crime groups, particularly targeting younger gang members recruited through social media. The Supreme Prosecutors' Office has increasingly applied the Punishment of Violence Act in combination with financial crime statutes to pursue both the violent and economic dimensions of criminal organizations.

South Korea's Constitutional Court has upheld the Punishment of Violence Act's aggravated penalties against challenges that membership-based punishment violates the presumption of innocence, ruling that the state's interest in suppressing organized violence justifies enhanced penalties for those who voluntarily associate with criminal groups.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act (18 U.S.C. SS 1961-1968), enacted as Title IX of the Organized Crime Control Act of 1970, represents the most expansive enterprise-based criminal prosecution tool in comparative law.

**Key Provisions:**

- **S 1962(a):** Prohibits investing income derived from racketeering in any enterprise engaged in interstate commerce.
- **S 1962(b):** Prohibits acquiring or maintaining an interest in an enterprise through racketeering.
- **S 1962(c):** Prohibits conducting an enterprise's affairs through a pattern of racketeering activity.
- **S 1962(d):** Prohibits conspiracy to violate any of the above provisions.

**Definitions:**

- **Racketeering activity (S 1961(1)):** Encompasses a broad catalogue of predicate offenses, including murder, kidnapping, gambling, arson, robbery, bribery, extortion, drug dealing, mail fraud, wire fraud, financial institution fraud, money laundering, and obstruction of justice -- among approximately 35 federal and numerous state offenses.
- **Pattern of racketeering (S 1961(5)):** Requires at least two predicate acts within a ten-year period.
- **Enterprise (S 1961(4)):** Any individual, partnership, corporation, association, or other legal entity, and any union or group of individuals associated in fact although not a legal entity.

### Criminal Penalties

Violation of S 1962 carries up to 20 years imprisonment per count, or life imprisonment if the predicate racketeering activity carries a life sentence. Mandatory criminal forfeiture applies to all property constituting or derived from proceeds of the racketeering activity, as well as any interest in the enterprise itself.

### Civil RICO

Section 1964(c) provides a private right of action for any person injured by reason of a RICO violation, entitling successful plaintiffs to recover treble damages (three times actual damages) plus reasonable attorney's fees and costs. Civil RICO has been widely used in commercial litigation, securities fraud cases, and insurance disputes, extending far beyond the statute's original anti-Mafia intent.

### State RICO Statutes

Approximately 33 states and territories have enacted their own RICO or organized crime statutes, often with broader definitions than the federal law. Georgia's RICO statute (O.C.G.A. S 16-14-1 et seq.) is notably expansive, requiring only a single predicate act rather than the federal minimum of two.

### Landmark Cases

**United States v. Turkette, 452 U.S. 576 (1981):** The Supreme Court held that RICO's definition of "enterprise" encompasses both legitimate and illegitimate organizations. The government need not prove the enterprise had any lawful purpose -- a purely criminal association qualifies.

**H.J. Inc. v. Northwestern Bell Telephone Co., 492 U.S. 229 (1989):** The Court clarified the "pattern" requirement, holding that prosecutors must demonstrate the predicate acts are both related to each other and amount to, or pose a threat of, continued criminal activity. This dual requirement of "relatedness" and "continuity" prevents RICO from reaching isolated criminal acts.

**Boyle v. United States, 556 U.S. 938 (2009):** The Court held that an "associated-in-fact" enterprise need not have a formal hierarchical structure. A group with a common purpose, relationships among members, and sufficient longevity to pursue that purpose qualifies as a RICO enterprise, even without bylaws, regular meetings, or defined roles.

**Fulton County v. Trump et al. (2023):** Fulton County District Attorney Fani Willis brought charges under Georgia's state RICO Act against Donald Trump and 18 co-defendants alleging a conspiracy to overturn the 2020 presidential election results in Georgia. The case represented one of the most high-profile applications of state RICO statutes in American history.

### Statistical Overview

According to the U.S. Sentencing Commission's 2024 statistical brief, federal courts sentenced defendants in RICO cases with a median sentence of approximately 78 months (6.5 years). RICO cases frequently involve lengthy multi-defendant trials and complex plea negotiations.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany addresses organized crime primarily through Section 129 of the Strafgesetzbuch (Criminal Code) and related provisions, adopting a membership-based model rather than the enterprise-conduct model used in U.S. RICO law.

**Section 129 StGB -- Forming Criminal Organizations:**

Anyone who founds or participates as a member in an organization whose purpose or activity is directed at the commission of offenses punishable by a maximum term of imprisonment of at least two years is liable to imprisonment of up to five years or a fine.

Anyone who supports such an organization or recruits members or supporters faces imprisonment of up to three years or a fine.

**Section 129a StGB -- Forming Terrorist Organizations:**

Enhanced penalties apply to organizations directed at specific serious crimes including murder (S 211), manslaughter (S 212), offenses against personal liberty (SS 239a, 239b), and causing explosions (S 308). Founders and members face one to ten years imprisonment; supporters face six months to ten years.

**Section 129b StGB -- Foreign Criminal and Terrorist Organizations:**

Extends the reach of SS 129 and 129a to organizations based outside Germany, provided prosecution is authorized by the Federal Ministry of Justice. This provision was enacted in 2002 in response to transnational terrorism.

### Penalty Structure

| Offense | Basic Penalty | Aggravated (Ringleader/Serious Case) |
|---------|--------------|--------------------------------------|
| Founding/membership (S 129) | Up to 5 years or fine | 6 months to 5 years; up to 10 years for serious offenses |
| Supporting/recruiting (S 129) | Up to 3 years or fine | -- |
| Terrorist organization (S 129a) | 1 to 10 years | 1 to 10 years |
| Supporting terrorist org. (S 129a) | 6 months to 10 years | -- |

### Definition of Criminal Organization

Under S 129(2), an organization requires:
- An organized group of **more than two persons** (i.e., at least three)
- Pursuit of an **overriding common interest**
- Designed to last for a **longer period of time**
- Independent of any definition of roles, continuity of membership, or formal structure

This definition was reformed in 2017 to align with EU Framework Decision 2008/841/JHA.

### Key Legal Peculiarity

Under S 129, it is **not necessary** to prove the defendant's personal participation in any specific criminal act. Proof of belonging to the criminal organization is sufficient for conviction. This distinguishes the German approach from the American RICO model, which requires proof that the defendant conducted the enterprise's affairs through at least two predicate offenses.

### Landmark Cases

**BGH, June 2, 2021 (3 StR 61/21 and 3 StR 21/21):** The Bundesgerichtshof (Federal Court of Justice) affirmed the existence of a criminal organization in the context of an illegal hawala banking network that transferred over 356 million euros from Germany to Turkey. The court held that operating an unlicensed financial transfer system with guaranteed anonymity constituted a criminal organization under S 129, even though individual transactions were carried out by different participants.

**BGH decisions on organized drug trafficking:** German courts have applied S 129 in combination with the Narcotics Act (BtMG) to prosecute organized drug trafficking networks, imposing penalties of up to fifteen years when gang-related drug offenses (bandenmassiger Drogenhandel) under S 30a BtMG are established.

### Asset Forfeiture

Under SS 73 ff. StGB, German courts may order confiscation of assets derived from criminal activity. The 2017 reform of asset recovery law (Vermogensabschopfungsreform) significantly expanded forfeiture powers, allowing confiscation of assets of obscure origin from members of criminal organizations even without conviction for specific predicate offenses, subject to proportionality review.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Legal Framework

The European Union addresses organized crime through a combination of framework decisions, directives, and institutional cooperation mechanisms.

**Council Framework Decision 2008/841/JHA (October 24, 2008):** The primary EU instrument for harmonizing organized crime prosecution across member states. It defines a "criminal organisation" as a structured association of more than two persons, established over a period of time, acting in concert with a view to committing offences punishable by deprivation of liberty of a maximum of at least four years, in order to obtain a financial or material benefit.

Member states must criminalize at least one of the following:

1. **Active participation** in a criminal organization's activities, with knowledge of its aims or criminal intentions, carrying a maximum sentence of at least two to five years imprisonment.
2. **Agreement/conspiracy** to commit offenses covered by the framework decision, carrying equivalent penalties.

**Directive 2014/42/EU on Freezing and Confiscation:** Establishes minimum rules for the freezing and confiscation of instrumentalities and proceeds of crime, including extended confiscation powers. Member states must ensure that proceeds can be confiscated following a final conviction, including where the assets have been transformed or converted.

**Directive 2024/1226 (April 24, 2024):** While focused on violations of EU restrictive measures (sanctions), this directive significantly strengthens the criminal law framework applicable to organized economic crime. Penalties include:

| Offense | Maximum Fine | Maximum Imprisonment |
|---------|-------------|---------------------|
| Basic violation of EU sanctions | EUR 750,000 | 5 years |
| Aggravated (within criminal organization framework) | EUR 1,500,000 | 10 years |

Member states were required to transpose this directive by May 20, 2025.

### Institutional Architecture

- **Europol:** Provides operational and analytical support for cross-border organized crime investigations. Its annual Serious and Organised Crime Threat Assessment (SOCTA) identifies priority crime areas.
- **Eurojust:** Coordinates judicial cooperation in cross-border cases, facilitating joint investigation teams and mutual legal assistance.
- **European Public Prosecutor's Office (EPPO):** Since June 2021, the EPPO has authority to investigate and prosecute crimes affecting the EU's financial interests, including fraud and money laundering with organized crime dimensions.

### ECHR Constraints on Organized Crime Prosecution

The European Court of Human Rights does not criminalize racketeering directly but plays a critical role in constraining how states prosecute organized crime, ensuring compliance with Convention rights.

**Article 6 -- Right to a Fair Trial:**

- **Anonymous witnesses:** In cases involving organized crime, states frequently seek to protect witness identities. The ECHR permits anonymous testimony only where strictly necessary and subject to counterbalancing safeguards. In *Doorson v. Netherlands* (1996), the Court held that anonymous witness evidence could be used but must not be the sole or decisive basis for conviction.
- **Entrapment:** In *Teixeira de Castro v. Portugal* (1998), the Court found a violation of Article 6 where undercover police officers instigated a drug offense rather than investigating pre-existing criminal activity. This established that state-incited offenses cannot form the basis of a fair prosecution.
- **Ibrahim and Others v. United Kingdom* (2016):** The Grand Chamber held that public interest in combating serious organized crime cannot justify measures that extinguish the essence of defense rights, including access to counsel during interrogation.

**Article 8 -- Right to Private Life (Surveillance):**

- *Bykov v. Russia* (2009), *Khan v. United Kingdom* (2000), and *Dragojevic v. Croatia* (2015): These cases address the use of covert surveillance evidence in organized crime prosecutions. The Court requires that surveillance be "in accordance with the law," pursue a legitimate aim, and be "necessary in a democratic society." Evidence obtained in violation of Article 8 may render a trial unfair under Article 6.
- *Szabo and Vissy v. Hungary* (2016): The Court struck down Hungary's secret surveillance regime as lacking adequate safeguards, even though it was ostensibly aimed at combating terrorism and organized crime.

**Article 7 -- No Punishment Without Law:**

The principle of legal certainty requires that criminal offenses, including organized crime offenses, be defined with sufficient precision. The ECHR has examined whether vaguely defined "criminal organization" statutes satisfy Article 7's foreseeability requirement.

### Ongoing Reform

The European Commission has acknowledged that Framework Decision 2008/841/JHA suffers from implementation gaps, with wide divergences among member states in definitions, penalties, and investigative tools. A 2022 Commission assessment found that the minimum penalty thresholds are insufficiently harmonized and that the framework decision's definitions do not adequately capture modern forms of organized crime, including cyber-enabled networks and criminal infiltration of legal economies. Discussions on a replacement directive remain ongoing.

## Comparative Analysis

### Structural Approaches

The four jurisdictions represent three distinct models for criminalizing organized crime:

1. **Enterprise-conduct model (United States):** RICO requires proof that the defendant conducted the affairs of an enterprise through a pattern of at least two predicate offenses. This model targets behavior within the organization rather than mere membership.

2. **Membership model (Germany, South Korea):** Sections 129 StGB and Korea's Punishment of Violence Act criminalize organizational affiliation itself. Conviction requires proof of belonging to a qualifying group but not personal commission of substantive offenses. This approach allows earlier intervention but raises due process concerns about guilt by association.

3. **Harmonization model (EU/ECHR):** Framework Decision 2008/841/JHA permits member states to choose between criminalizing active participation or conspiracy, reflecting the diversity of national legal traditions. The ECHR overlays procedural safeguards that constrain all approaches.

### Penalty Severity

South Korea imposes the harshest penalties for organizational leaders, with the possibility of death or life imprisonment for heads of violent criminal groups. The United States follows with up to 20 years per RICO count (or life where predicates permit), enhanced by mandatory forfeiture and civil treble damages. Germany's penalties are comparatively moderate, with a maximum of five years for basic S 129 membership, though aggravated cases involving terrorist organizations reach ten years. The EU framework sets a floor rather than a ceiling, requiring maximum penalties of at least two to five years.

### Scope of Application

RICO's breadth is unmatched: it covers approximately 35 categories of federal predicate offenses and numerous state crimes, and applies to both criminal and civil proceedings. Civil RICO's treble damages provision has transformed it into a weapon of commercial litigation far beyond its original purpose. By contrast, Germany's S 129 applies only when the organization targets offenses carrying at least two years imprisonment, and the EU framework applies only to offenses carrying at least four years.

### Asset Recovery

All four jurisdictions recognize the importance of stripping criminal organizations of their economic base, but approaches vary. The United States mandates criminal forfeiture in RICO cases and permits civil forfeiture without criminal conviction. Germany's 2017 reforms expanded confiscation powers significantly, including assets of unclear provenance. The EU's Directive 2014/42/EU establishes minimum standards for freezing and confiscation across member states.

### Civil Remedies

The most significant divergence lies in civil remedies. Only the United States provides a private right of action with treble damages under RICO, creating powerful incentives for private enforcement. Neither Germany nor South Korea offers a comparable civil organized crime statute, and the EU framework does not require member states to create one.
