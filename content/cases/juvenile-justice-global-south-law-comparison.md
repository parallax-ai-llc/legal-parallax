## Summary

Juvenile justice systems across the Global South reveal the tension between the international children's rights framework -- anchored in the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child (CRC, 1989), the Beijing Rules (1985), the Riyadh Guidelines (1990), and the Havana Rules (1990) -- and domestic realities of child poverty, inadequate institutional infrastructure, cultural norms favoring punitive responses, and, in conflict-affected regions, the use of child soldiers. China's juvenile justice operates through the revised Law on the Protection of Minors (未成年人保护法, 2020 revision) and the Law on the Prevention of Juvenile Delinquency (预防未成年人犯罪法, 2020 revision), with the minimum age of criminal responsibility (MACR) at 12 for the most serious offenses (as amended in 2020) and 14 for general offenses. India's Juvenile Justice (Care and Protection of Children) Act 2015 (JJ Act 2015), which replaced the 2000 Act in the aftermath of the 2012 Delhi gang rape case, controversially allowed children aged 16-18 accused of "heinous offenses" to be tried as adults. Nigeria operates a dual system -- the Child Rights Act 2003 (adopted by 26 of 36 states, with most northern states not adopting it) sets the MACR at 12, while northern states applying Sharia criminal law consider puberty as the threshold for criminal responsibility. South Africa's Child Justice Act 75 of 2008 sets the MACR at 12 (raised from 7) and establishes a diversionary framework. Brazil's Estatuto da Crianca e do Adolescente (ECA, Law 8.069/1990) sets the MACR at 18 but provides "socio-educational measures" (medidas socioeducativas) for adolescent offenders aged 12-17, while Mexico's 2005 constitutional reform and implementing legislation established a specialized juvenile justice system. Indonesia's Juvenile Criminal Justice System Law (Law No. 11 of 2012) sets the MACR at 12 and mandates diversionary processes, while the Philippines' Juvenile Justice and Welfare Act (RA 9344 of 2006, amended by RA 10630 of 2013) establishes a minimum age of criminal responsibility at 15.

## China

### Legal Framework

China's juvenile justice underwent significant reform with the comprehensive revision of the Law on the Protection of Minors (未成年人保护法, effective June 1, 2021) and the Law on the Prevention of Juvenile Delinquency (预防未成年人犯罪法, effective June 1, 2021). These laws, together with the Criminal Law, the Criminal Procedure Law (Articles 277-283, "Special Procedures for Juvenile Cases"), and the Law on the Protection of Minors' Personal Information (effective January 1, 2024), form the juvenile justice framework.

The Criminal Law, as amended by the Eleventh Amendment (2020), lowered the MACR from 14 to 12 for the specific offenses of intentional homicide or intentional injury causing death or serious injury "with extremely cruel means" (情节恶劣, qingjie elie), subject to approval by the Supreme People's Procuratorate (Article 17, paragraph 3). This amendment was prompted by several high-profile cases of children under 14 committing violent crimes, particularly the 2019 Dalian case where a 13-year-old murdered a 10-year-old girl. For offenders aged 14-16, criminal responsibility exists only for eight specified offenses (intentional homicide, intentional injury causing serious injury or death, rape, robbery, drug trafficking, arson, explosion, poisoning). For ages 16-18, full criminal responsibility applies but with a mandatory sentencing reduction (Article 17, paragraph 4: shall not be sentenced to death).

### Specialized Education and Correction

The 2020 revision replaced the former "shelter for rehabilitation and education" (收容教养, shourong jiaoyang) with "specialized correctional education" (专门矫治教育, zhuanmen jiaozheng jiaoyu), administered through specialized schools (专门学校, zhuanmen xuexiao). These schools are classified into three tiers: ordinary specialized education for students with behavioral problems, specialized education with correctional features for those who have committed criminal acts but are below the MACR, and intensive specialized education for serious offenders. The People's Procuratorate has prosecutorial oversight over these facilities.

The SPP's conditional non-prosecution (附条件不起诉, futiaojian bu qisu) mechanism under Criminal Procedure Law Article 282 allows procurators to attach conditions (supervision, community service, education) to a decision not to prosecute juvenile suspects aged 14-18 accused of offenses punishable by one year or less, with a supervision period of six months to one year. The SPP reported that conditional non-prosecution was applied in over 30,000 juvenile cases in 2022.

### Sealed Records

Criminal Procedure Law Article 286 mandates that criminal records of offenders who were under 18 at the time of the offense and sentenced to five years' imprisonment or less shall be sealed (犯罪记录封存, fanzui jilu fengcun). Sealed records may not be disclosed to any unit or individual except for inquiry by judicial organs for case-handling needs or by relevant units conducting employment background checks for positions specified by law. The 2022 SPC/SPP/MPS/MOJ Joint Regulation on Sealing Juvenile Criminal Records strengthened implementation, prohibiting discrimination in education and employment based on sealed juvenile records.

## India

### JJ Act 2015 and the Nirbhaya Catalyst

The Juvenile Justice (Care and Protection of Children) Act 2015 was enacted following public outrage over the 2012 Delhi gang rape ("Nirbhaya" case), in which one of the six perpetrators was a 17-year-old juvenile who was released after three years in a reformation home -- the maximum permissible sentence under the Juvenile Justice Act 2000. The JJ Act 2015 introduced the controversial provision in Section 15 allowing children in conflict with law (CICL) aged 16-18, accused of "heinous offenses" (defined in Section 2(33) as offenses punishable with imprisonment of seven years or more), to be assessed by the Juvenile Justice Board (JJB) for their physical and mental capacity, ability to understand consequences, and circumstances of the offense. If the preliminary assessment concludes that the child should be tried as an adult, the JJB may transfer the case to the Children's Court.

The Supreme Court in Shilpa Mittal v. State of NCT of Delhi (2020) clarified that "heinous offenses" for the purpose of Section 15 are only those where the minimum sentence is seven years or more, excluding offenses where the minimum is less than seven years even if the maximum exceeds seven years. This interpretation significantly limited the transfer provision's scope.

### Institutional Framework

The JJ Act 2015 establishes Juvenile Justice Boards (Section 4) in every district, composed of a Metropolitan/Judicial Magistrate and two social workers (at least one woman). Child Welfare Committees (Section 27) address children in need of care and protection. Observation Homes (Section 47), Special Homes (Section 48), and Places of Safety (Section 49) provide tiered institutional responses. The Act mandates individual care plans (Section 39) and periodic review of institutional placement.

The Juvenile Justice (Care and Protection of Children) Amendment Act 2021 transferred adoption-related functions to District Magistrates and authorized District Magistrates to issue adoption orders, previously a function exclusively of courts. The Act also strengthened provisions on "serious offenses" and enhanced penalties for cruelty against children.

### Juvenile Justice Boards in Practice

The functioning of Juvenile Justice Boards (JJBs) varies significantly across India's 766 districts. The Supreme Court in Sampurna Behrua v. Union of India (2018) directed states to ensure JJBs are constituted in every district and function regularly. However, a 2023 National Commission for Protection of Child Rights (NCPCR) audit found that many JJBs lack adequate infrastructure, trained social workers, or psychological support services. The backlog of cases before JJBs -- with some pending for years -- undermines the Act's rehabilitative purpose.

### Child Labor and Juvenile Delinquency Nexus

India's juvenile justice system operates in the context of widespread child labor and poverty. The Child Labour (Prohibition and Regulation) Amendment Act 2016 prohibited employment of children below 14 in all occupations (with exceptions for family enterprises and entertainment) and designated employment of adolescents (14-18) in hazardous occupations as a cognizable offense. The NCRB reported approximately 35,000 juveniles apprehended in 2022, with theft, hurt/grievous hurt, and burglary as the most common offenses. The relationship between poverty, child labor, street children, and juvenile offending remains a systemic challenge that the JJ Act's rehabilitation provisions have not adequately addressed.

### Children in Armed Conflict

The use of children by Maoist/Naxalite groups (CPI(Maoist) and affiliates) in India's "Red Corridor" states (Chhattisgarh, Jharkhand, Odisha, Bihar, West Bengal, Andhra Pradesh, Telangana, Maharashtra) has been documented by the NHRC and the Ministry of Home Affairs. The Protocol Additional to the Geneva Conventions (Additional Protocol II), the Optional Protocol to the CRC on the Involvement of Children in Armed Conflict (ratified by India in 2005), and the Commission for Protection of Child Rights Act 2005 (establishing the National and State Commissions for Protection of Child Rights) provide the normative framework. However, enforcement in conflict-affected areas remains limited.

## Africa

### Nigeria: Dual Systems

Nigeria's Child Rights Act 2003 (CRA), implementing the CRC and the African Charter on the Rights and Welfare of the Child (ACRWC, 1990), establishes the MACR at 12 (Section 209) and creates a specialized Family Court system (Section 149). However, the CRA has been adopted by only 26 of 36 states (as of 2025), with most northern states rejecting it as inconsistent with Islamic law. In non-CRA states, the Children and Young Persons Act (Cap C50 LFN 2004) -- a colonial-era statute -- applies, with a lower MACR of 7 in some interpretations.

In northern states applying Sharia penal codes, the age of criminal responsibility is linked to puberty (bulugh) rather than a fixed numerical age. This has produced cases of children subjected to Sharia criminal sanctions, including the widely publicized 2000 case of Bariya Ibrahim Magazu, a 17-year-old sentenced to 100 lashes for premarital sex in Zamfara State. The conflict between the CRA's fixed MACR and Sharia's puberty-based threshold remains unresolved, reflecting broader tensions between the federal constitution and religious law.

The Boko Haram conflict has created a massive child protection crisis. UNICEF estimated that Boko Haram recruited over 8,000 children between 2009 and 2020. The Civilian Joint Task Force (CJTF) was listed in the UN Secretary-General's Annual Report on Children and Armed Conflict for recruiting children, signing an action plan for the release of children in 2017. The Borno State government and UNICEF operate the "Safe Schools Initiative" and reintegration programs for children formerly associated with armed groups, though psychosocial support remains grossly inadequate.

### South Africa: Child Justice Act

South Africa's Child Justice Act 75 of 2008 (CJA), which entered into force on April 1, 2010 after a 12-year legislative process, established a comprehensive juvenile justice framework. The Act raised the MACR from 7 (a colonial-era common law threshold) to 10, later effectively to 12 through the criminal capacity presumption (Section 7: a child of 10-14 is presumed to lack criminal capacity unless the state proves otherwise). The Constitutional Court's pre-CJA jurisprudence, particularly S v. Williams 1995 (3) SA 632 (CC) (abolishing juvenile whipping) and Centre for Child Law v. Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development 2009 (6) SA 632 (CC) (mandating automatic review of juvenile sentences), informed the Act's development.

The CJA's centerpiece is its diversion framework (Chapter 6). Section 41 requires a preliminary inquiry magistrate to consider diversion before proceeding to trial for Schedule 1 and 2 offenses. Diversion options (Section 53) include formal caution, community service, family group conference, victim-offender mediation, and placement in a residential program. Section 69 restricts imprisonment of children: children under 14 may not be sentenced to imprisonment; children aged 14-18 may be sentenced to imprisonment only as a measure of last resort and for the shortest appropriate period, not exceeding 25 years for Schedule 3 offenses (including murder, rape, robbery with aggravating circumstances). Minimum sentencing legislation (Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997) does not apply to children.

The Secure Care facilities operated by the Department of Social Development and the Child and Youth Care Centres under the Children's Act 38 of 2005 provide institutional alternatives to imprisonment. However, the Department of Correctional Services reported that approximately 200 sentenced children remained in adult correctional facilities in 2023, contrary to the CJA's requirements, reflecting institutional capacity constraints.

## Latin America

### Brazil: ECA and Constitutional Protection

Brazil's Constitution of 1988 Article 228 establishes 18 as the age of criminal responsibility (inimputabilidade penal), a provision that several attempts to amend through Proposta de Emenda Constitucional (PEC) have failed to alter -- the most prominent being PEC 171/1993, which proposed lowering the MACR to 16 and was approved by the Chamber of Deputies in 2015 but never voted on by the Senate.

The Estatuto da Crianca e do Adolescente (ECA, Law 8.069/1990) provides a comprehensive framework implementing the CRC's "integral protection doctrine" (doutrina da protecao integral). Adolescents aged 12-17 who commit "atos infracionais" (infractional acts, i.e., conduct equivalent to a crime) are subject to "medidas socioeducativas" (socio-educational measures) under Article 112:

- Advertencia (warning)
- Obrigacao de reparar o dano (obligation to repair damage)
- Prestacao de servicos a comunidade (community service, up to 6 months)
- Liberdade assistida (assisted liberty/supervised probation, minimum 6 months)
- Semi-liberdade (semi-liberty regime)
- Internacao (internment in an educational establishment -- last resort) Internment (Article 121) is a measure of last resort, limited to three years maximum, with mandatory reassessment every six months, and applicable only for acts involving violence or serious threat, repeated infractions, or non-compliance with prior measures.

The Sistema Nacional de Atendimento Socioeducativo (SINASE, Law 12.594/2012) operationalized the ECA's socio-educational framework, establishing national standards for facilities, individualized care plans (Plano Individual de Atendimento, PIA), and oversight mechanisms. Despite this sophisticated normative framework, the Conselho Nacional de Justica (CNJ) reported 18,086 adolescents in socio-educational internment in 2022, with facilities frequently overcrowded, understaffed, and marked by violence. The Inter-American Court of Human Rights found Brazil responsible for deaths at the FEBEM institution in Sao Paulo in the landmark case Instituto de Reeducacion del Menor v. Paraguay (2004), and similar conditions persist in Brazilian facilities.

### Mexico: 2005 Constitutional Reform

Mexico's 2005 constitutional reform (amending Article 18) established that the federal and state systems must guarantee the rights of adolescents and apply measures proportional to their conduct. The National Law on Comprehensive Criminal Justice for Adolescents (Ley Nacional del Sistema Integral de Justicia Penal para Adolescentes, 2016) created a unified national framework replacing 33 disparate state and federal systems. The law distinguishes between adolescents aged 12-14 (for whom only non-custodial measures apply) and those aged 14-18 (who may receive custodial measures up to a maximum of five years). Diversion (soluciones alternas and mecanismos de justicia restaurativa) is mandated as a first option.

Child and adolescent recruitment by drug trafficking organizations -- particularly in states like Guerrero, Michoacan, Tamaulipas, and Sinaloa -- represents a severe challenge. The Network for the Rights of the Child in Mexico (REDIM) estimated that 30,000-50,000 children are involved in organized crime activities. The Federal Law against Organized Crime does not specifically address child recruitment, though the General Law on the Rights of Children and Adolescents (2014) prohibits it. The Inter-American Commission on Human Rights has expressed concern about Mexico's failure to protect children from cartel recruitment.

## Southeast Asia

### Indonesia: Law 11/2012

Indonesia's Juvenile Criminal Justice System Law (Law No. 11 of 2012, Sistem Peradilan Pidana Anak, SPPA) replaced the Juvenile Court Law of 1997 (Law No. 3 of 1997), raising the MACR from 8 to 12. The law mandates diversionary processes (diversi) for offenses carrying a maximum sentence of seven years or less (Article 7), requiring agreement between the child, victim, parents/guardians, community adviser, social worker, and facilitator. Diversion outcomes include reconciliation, restorative justice, and return to parents. For offenses exceeding seven years, or where diversion fails, cases proceed to the Juvenile Court.

Article 81 limits juvenile detention to a maximum of one-half of the adult maximum sentence. Article 82 mandates that children may not be sentenced to death or life imprisonment (with the latter commuted to a maximum of 10 years). Specialized juvenile courts operate within the general court system, with judges trained in child protection. The Lembaga Pembinaan Khusus Anak (LPKA, Special Child Guidance Institution) provides the correctional facility framework, replacing the adult prison system for juveniles. However, the Ministry of Law and Human Rights reported that approximately 2,900 children were detained in LPKA and detention centers in 2023, with conditions varying significantly across Indonesia's 34 provinces.

The Indonesian Child Protection Commission (Komisi Perlindungan Anak Indonesia, KPAI), established by the Child Protection Law (Law No. 23 of 2002, amended by Law No. 35 of 2014), monitors implementation of children's rights including in the criminal justice context. KPAI has reported persistent issues including children detained with adults, lack of legal representation, and excessive use of detention for minor offenses.

### Philippines: RA 9344 and MACR Debate

The Juvenile Justice and Welfare Act (RA 9344 of 2006) established the MACR at 15 (raised from 9) and created a comprehensive child-in-conflict-with-the-law (CICL) system centered on intervention, diversion, and rehabilitation rather than punishment. Section 20 provides that a child aged 15-18 who commits an offense but cannot "discern" the wrongfulness of the act is exempt from criminal liability. Section 23 mandates diversion at the community (katarungang pambarangay), law enforcement, and prosecutorial levels.

RA 10630 (2013) amended RA 9344 to strengthen the institutional framework, establishing Bahay Pag-asa (House of Hope) facilities for CICLs requiring intensive intervention, and a Juvenile Justice and Welfare Council (JJWC) as the primary coordinating body. During the Duterte administration, multiple bills were filed seeking to lower the MACR to 9 or 12 (House Bill 8858, House Bill 2, Senate Bill 2023), partially in response to concerns about syndicates exploiting children below the MACR for criminal activities. These bills provoked intense opposition from child rights organizations, the JJWC, UNICEF, and the Catholic Bishops' Conference of the Philippines (CBCP). None were enacted, and the MACR remains at 15.

The Moro conflict area has historically involved children in armed groups. The Comprehensive Agreement on the Bangsamoro (2014) included provisions on transitional justice for children formerly associated with armed groups. The MILF committed to a children-specific Action Plan with the UN, and UNICEF and the JJWC operate reintegration programs in the BARMM.

The syndicalization of children -- whereby organized crime groups exploit children below the MACR to commit theft, drug distribution, and other offenses -- remains a significant concern. RA 9344 Section 22 provides that the child involved in syndicalized criminal activities shall be exempt from criminal liability, while the syndicate members exploiting the child face enhanced penalties. The Juvenile Justice and Welfare Council (JJWC) coordinates the Comprehensive Juvenile Intervention Program (CJIP), which includes:

- Community-based intervention for children at risk
- Diversion programs at barangay (village), law enforcement, and prosecutorial levels
- Bahay Pag-asa residential facilities for intensive intervention
- Aftercare and reintegration support

Despite this comprehensive normative framework, the JJWC's 2023 report documented only 56 operational Bahay Pag-asa facilities nationwide, far short of the requirement for facilities in every province and highly urbanized city.

## Comparative Analysis

### Minimum Age of Criminal Responsibility

The Global South displays wide variation in MACR: China at 12/14, India effectively 16-18 for heinous offenses (with JJB assessment), Nigeria at 12 (CRA states) or puberty-based (Sharia states), South Africa at 12, Brazil at 18 (constitutionally protected), Mexico at 12/14, Indonesia at 12, and the Philippines at 15. The UN Committee on the Rights of the Child's General Comment No. 24 (2019) recommends an MACR of at least 14, a standard met by only the Philippines and India (for most offenses) among the comparator jurisdictions. Brazil's constitutional MACR of 18 exceeds all international standards but faces persistent political pressure for reduction.

### Rehabilitation vs. Retribution

The normative frameworks across all jurisdictions emphasize rehabilitation, diversion, and the "best interests of the child" principle (CRC Article 3). However, implementation gaps are vast. China's specialized correctional education schools, India's observation and special homes, Nigeria's borstal institutions, South Africa's Secure Care facilities, Brazil's SINASE institutions, Indonesia's LPKA centers, and the Philippines' Bahay Pag-asa all face chronic underfunding, overcrowding, and inadequate professional staffing. The result is that detention, which all frameworks designate as a last resort, remains the default response in practice.

### Child Soldiers and Armed Conflict

The recruitment and use of children by armed groups is a distinctive Global South challenge absent from Western juvenile justice discourse. Nigeria's Boko Haram, Colombia's FARC-EP and ELN, the Philippines' MILF/BIFF/ASG, and India's Maoist groups have all used children as combatants, spies, porters, and sexual slaves. The legal response -- the Optional Protocol to the CRC on Children in Armed Conflict, the Rome Statute Article 8(2)(b)(xxvi) and 8(2)(e)(vii), and domestic implementing legislation -- provides normative frameworks, but reintegration programs remain inadequate. The JEP in Colombia represents the most developed transitional justice mechanism addressing child recruitment, with Case 07 specifically examining recruitment of children by FARC-EP.

### Gender Dimensions

Girls in the juvenile justice system face distinct challenges across the Global South. In India, girls in conflict with the law are frequently institutionalized for "status offenses" related to sexual activity, elopement, or running away from home. In Nigeria, girls may be subjected to Sharia punishments for premarital sex. In Brazil, the intersection of race, gender, and class produces disproportionate impacts on Afro-Brazilian girls. Indonesia and the Philippines face issues of trafficking and sexual exploitation of minors that intersect with juvenile justice. South Africa's CJA Section 97 requires gender-sensitive approaches, but implementation remains uneven.
