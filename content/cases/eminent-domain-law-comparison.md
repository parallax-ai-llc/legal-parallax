## Summary

Eminent domain -- the power of the state to compulsorily acquire private property for public use upon payment of just compensation -- represents a fundamental tension between sovereign authority and individual property rights that every legal system must resolve, with the scope of permissible takings, the definition of "public use" or "public purpose," the methodology for determining compensation, and the procedural protections available to property owners varying dramatically across jurisdictions. South Korea's compulsory acquisition regime is governed by the Act on Acquisition of and Compensation for Land for Public Works Projects (공익사업을 위한 토지 등의 취득 및 보상에 관한 법률, commonly known as the Land Compensation Act), enacted in 2002 to consolidate prior legislation, operating within the constitutional framework of Article 23 of the Constitution (헌법 제23조), which guarantees property rights, requires compensation for expropriation, and mandates that the content and limits of property rights be determined by law; Korean courts and the Constitutional Court have developed extensive jurisprudence on the meaning of "just compensation" (정당한 보상) and the permissible scope of public purpose acquisitions. The United States derives eminent domain authority from the Fifth Amendment to the Constitution ("nor shall private property be taken for public use, without just compensation"), with procedural requirements governed by the Uniform Relocation Assistance and Real Property Acquisition Policies Act of 1970 (42 U.S.C. Sections 4601-4655) and state condemnation statutes; the Supreme Court's controversial decision in Kelo v. City of New London, 545 U.S. 469 (2005), which upheld the use of eminent domain for economic development purposes, triggered a nationwide legislative backlash with 44 states enacting restrictions on economic development takings. Germany protects property under Article 14 of the Grundgesetz (Basic Law), which establishes a three-tier system distinguishing between the definition of property rights (Inhalts- und Schrankenbestimmung, Article 14(1)), social obligations of property (Sozialbindung, Article 14(2)), and expropriation (Enteignung, Article 14(3)), with the Bundesverfassungsgericht (Federal Constitutional Court) developing sophisticated doctrines on the boundaries between these categories. The ECHR protects property rights under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 to the Convention, with the European Court of Human Rights applying a proportionality test that requires a fair balance between the demands of the general interest and the protection of individual fundamental rights, while allowing states a wide margin of appreciation in determining public interest and compensation levels.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's eminent domain system operates within a constitutional framework that both enables and constrains state acquisition of private property.

**Constitutional Foundation (헌법 제23조):** Article 23 of the Constitution provides three principles: property rights are guaranteed (paragraph 1); the content and limits of property rights are determined by law (paragraph 1); the exercise of property rights shall conform to the public welfare (paragraph 2); and expropriation, use, or restriction of private property for public necessity requires compensation (paragraph 3). The Constitutional Court (헌법재판소) has held that "just compensation" under Article 23(3) means compensation equivalent to the property's objective market value, reflecting the complete loss suffered by the property owner.

**Land Compensation Act (공익사업을 위한 토지 등의 취득 및 보상에 관한 법률):** Enacted in 2002, this Act consolidated the former Land Expropriation Act and the Act on Special Cases Concerning Compensation for Losses from Public Works. The Act establishes the categories of public works projects eligible for compulsory acquisition, the procedures for negotiation, assessment, and acquisition, and the standards for determining compensation.

### Eligible Public Works Projects

Article 4 of the Land Compensation Act enumerates categories of projects eligible for compulsory acquisition:

| Category | Examples |
|----------|---------|
| National defense and military facilities | Military bases, training areas |
| Transportation infrastructure | Roads, railways, airports, ports, bridges |
| Water resources and energy | Dams, power plants, transmission lines |
| Educational and cultural facilities | Schools, museums, libraries |
| Housing and urban development | Public housing, urban renewal projects, new town development |
| Environmental protection | Water treatment plants, waste disposal facilities, national parks |
| Industrial development | Industrial complexes, free economic zones |

### Compensation Framework

The Act requires compensation to be based on the property's market value as determined by licensed appraisers (감정평가사). The compensation framework includes:

**Land Compensation:** Based on the officially assessed individual land price (개별공시지가) as a reference point, adjusted by licensed appraisers to reflect actual market conditions. The Supreme Court has held that compensation must reflect the property's value at the time of the actual taking, excluding any increase or decrease in value attributable to the public works project itself.

**Building and Structure Compensation:** Assessed at reproduction cost minus depreciation, or at market value if available.

**Relocation Compensation:** Includes moving expenses, temporary housing costs, and livelihood stabilization support for displaced residents. The Act provides enhanced relocation assistance for low-income households and elderly residents.

### Land Compensation Committee

The Central Land Compensation Committee (중앙토지수용위원회) and Local Land Compensation Committees review disputed compensation determinations. Property owners who are dissatisfied with the Committee's determination may file an administrative lawsuit in the competent court within 60 days. The court conducts de novo review of the compensation amount.

### Major Infrastructure Projects

South Korea's rapid industrialization and urbanization have generated extensive eminent domain practice. Major projects including the Gyeongbu Expressway (1968-1970), Seoul Metropolitan Subway construction (1974-present), Incheon International Airport (1992-2001), Sejong City development (2007-present), and high-speed rail (KTX) construction have required massive land acquisitions. The new town development programs (신도시) around Seoul, including Bundang, Ilsan, Pangyo, and the recent 3rd-generation new towns, have been particularly significant, involving acquisition of thousands of hectares and displacement of tens of thousands of residents.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States derives eminent domain authority from the Fifth Amendment to the Constitution, made applicable to the states through the Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause.

**Fifth Amendment:** "...nor shall private property be taken for public use, without just compensation." This clause both grants the power of eminent domain (by implication) and constrains it through two requirements: the taking must be for "public use" and "just compensation" must be paid.

**Just Compensation:** The Supreme Court has defined just compensation as the fair market value of the property at the time of the taking -- "the amount that a willing buyer would pay to a willing seller in a voluntary transaction" (United States v. 564.54 Acres of Land, 441 U.S. 506 (1979)). Compensation generally does not include the owner's subjective or sentimental value, relocation costs (though these are separately covered by the Uniform Relocation Act), or lost business goodwill (in most jurisdictions).

### Kelo v. City of New London (2005)

The Supreme Court's 5-4 decision in Kelo v. City of New London, 545 U.S. 469 (2005), held that the transfer of private property from one private owner to another for the purpose of economic development constitutes a permissible "public use" under the Fifth Amendment. Justice Stevens, writing for the majority, held that "public use" encompasses "public purpose" and that courts should defer to legislative determinations of public purpose. Justice O'Connor's dissent warned that the decision effectively eliminated the public use requirement, permitting government to take property from any owner and transfer it to another who would make "more productive" use of it.

### Post-Kelo State Legislative Response

The Kelo decision provoked an unprecedented legislative response:

| Response Category | Number of States | Examples |
|-------------------|-----------------|----------|
| Constitutional amendment restricting economic development takings | 10 states | Florida, Nevada, Louisiana, Georgia |
| Statutory prohibition or restriction on economic development takings | 34 states | Texas, Michigan, Ohio, Virginia |
| Study commissions or interim measures | 7 states | Various |
| No significant legislative action | ~6 states | New York, New Jersey |

### Regulatory Takings

The regulatory takings doctrine addresses government regulations that reduce property value without physically taking the property. The Supreme Court has established two frameworks:

**Per Se Taking (Lucas v. South Carolina Coastal Council, 505 U.S. 1003 (1992)):** A regulation that deprives a property owner of all economically beneficial use constitutes a per se taking requiring compensation, unless the restriction inheres in the title itself (e.g., nuisance law limitations).

**Penn Central Balancing Test (Penn Central Transportation Co. v. City of New York, 438 U.S. 104 (1978)):** For regulations that reduce but do not eliminate property value, courts apply a multi-factor balancing test considering the economic impact of the regulation, the extent to which the regulation interferes with distinct investment-backed expectations, and the character of the governmental action.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's property protection and expropriation framework under Article 14 of the Grundgesetz is notable for its sophisticated three-tier distinction developed by the Bundesverfassungsgericht (Federal Constitutional Court).

**Article 14(1) Grundgesetz -- Property Guarantee and Content Determination:** Property and the right of inheritance are guaranteed. Their content and limits are determined by law. This provision both guarantees property as a fundamental right and authorizes the legislature to define its scope and limitations (Inhalts- und Schrankenbestimmung).

**Article 14(2) Grundgesetz -- Social Obligation:** Property entails obligations. Its use shall also serve the public good. This Sozialbindung (social obligation) clause provides the constitutional foundation for regulations that restrict property use in the public interest without triggering a compensation obligation.

**Article 14(3) Grundgesetz -- Expropriation:** Expropriation is only permissible for the public good (zum Wohle der Allgemeinheit). It may only be ordered by or pursuant to a law that determines the nature and extent of compensation. Compensation must be determined by establishing an equitable balance between the public interest and the interests of those affected (Junktimklausel).

### The Nassauskiesung Decision (BVerfGE 58, 300 (1981))

The Federal Constitutional Court's landmark Nassauskiesung (gravel extraction) decision established the foundational distinction between content and limitations determinations (which do not require compensation under Article 14(1)-(2)) and expropriations (which do require compensation under Article 14(3)). The Court held that the determination of whether a measure constitutes an expropriation must be made at the legislative level, not on a case-by-case basis -- rejecting the earlier case-by-case approach (Einzelaktstheorie) in favor of a categorical approach (Schwellentheorie).

### Compensation Standard

Article 14(3) requires "appropriate compensation" (angemessene Entschädigung) rather than "full compensation" or "just compensation." The Federal Constitutional Court has held that appropriate compensation must reflect an equitable balance (gerechte Abwägung) between the public interest in the expropriation and the owner's interest in retaining the property. While compensation is typically based on market value, the equitable balancing principle permits deviations in both directions depending on the circumstances.

### Baugesetzbuch (Federal Building Code)

The Baugesetzbuch (BauGB) contains the most commonly invoked expropriation provisions in German law. Sections 85-122 BauGB authorize expropriation for urban development purposes, including the implementation of binding land use plans (Bebauungspläne), the remediation of urban planning deficiencies, and the development of urban renewal areas. Expropriation under the BauGB is subject to the principle of subsidiarity (Subsidiaritätsprinzip) -- it is permissible only as a last resort when the public purpose cannot be achieved by other means, including voluntary acquisition.

### Salvatorische Entschädigungsklausel

German law requires that any statute authorizing expropriation must contain a provision specifying the nature and extent of compensation (Junktimklausel under Article 14(3) sentence 3 GG). A statute that authorizes expropriation without providing for compensation is unconstitutional and void. This constitutional requirement ensures that the legislature considers the compensation implications of every expropriation authorization at the time of enactment.

## ECHR & EU

### Protocol No. 1, Article 1 ECHR

Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 to the European Convention on Human Rights provides the supranational framework for property protection:

"Every natural or legal person is entitled to the peaceful enjoyment of his possessions. No one shall be deprived of his possessions except in the public interest and subject to the conditions provided for by law and by the general principles of international law. The preceding provisions shall not, however, in any way impair the right of a State to enforce such laws as it deems necessary to control the use of property in accordance with the general interest or to secure the payment of taxes or other contributions or penalties."

### Three Rules Framework

The European Court of Human Rights has identified three distinct rules within Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (Sporrong and Lönnroth v. Sweden, Application Nos. 7151/75 and 7152/75, 1982):

**Rule 1 (Peaceful Enjoyment):** The general principle of peaceful enjoyment of possessions.

**Rule 2 (Deprivation):** Deprivation of possessions is permitted only in the public interest and subject to conditions provided by law and general principles of international law.

**Rule 3 (Control of Use):** States may control the use of property in accordance with the general interest.

### Proportionality and Fair Balance

The Court applies a proportionality test requiring a "fair balance" between the demands of the general interest and the protection of individual rights. This test requires that the interference with property rights is lawful (prescribed by law), pursues a legitimate aim (in the public interest), and is proportionate (a fair balance between the means employed and the aim sought, without imposing an excessive individual burden).

### Compensation Jurisprudence

**James and Others v. United Kingdom (Application No. 8793/79, 1986):** The Court held that compensation at less than full market value may be justified in the public interest, but that a complete absence of compensation could only be justified in exceptional circumstances. The Court afforded states a wide margin of appreciation in determining compensation levels.

**Scordino v. Italy (No. 1) (Application No. 36813/97, Grand Chamber, 2006):** The Grand Chamber held that compensation must be "reasonably related" to the market value of the property and that Italian legislation providing compensation at approximately 50 percent of market value violated Article 1 of Protocol No. 1.

**Vistiņš and Perepjolkins v. Latvia (Application No. 71243/01, Grand Chamber, 2012):** The Court held that compensation significantly below market value for land expropriated for port development violated Article 1 of Protocol No. 1, while reaffirming that legitimate public interest objectives may justify compensation below full market value.

### EU Law and Expropriation

The EU does not have a harmonized expropriation directive. However, EU law affects national expropriation practice in several ways: Article 17 of the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights provides property protection comparable to Protocol No. 1; EU environmental law (particularly the Habitats Directive 92/43/EEC and the Birds Directive 2009/147/EC) may require member states to restrict property use or acquire land for conservation purposes; and EU state aid rules (Articles 107-108 TFEU) may apply where expropriation is used to transfer property to private undertakings at below-market compensation.

## Comparative Analysis

### Public Use/Purpose

The definition of permissible public use or purpose represents the most significant doctrinal divergence. The United States, following Kelo, adopted the broadest interpretation, treating "public use" as synonymous with "public purpose" and deferring to legislative determinations -- though 44 states have since restricted this through legislation or constitutional amendments. Germany requires expropriation to serve the "public good" and applies a strict subsidiarity principle, permitting expropriation only as a last resort. South Korea enumerates specific categories of eligible public works projects, providing greater predictability but less flexibility. The ECHR affords states a wide margin of appreciation in determining public interest, rarely finding that a taking lacks a legitimate public purpose.

### Compensation Standards

The compensation standards reveal important differences in how property rights are valued. The United States mandates "just compensation" equal to fair market value, applied relatively mechanically without equitable balancing. Germany requires "appropriate compensation" determined through equitable balancing, which may result in compensation above or below market value depending on circumstances. South Korea requires "just compensation" based on market value, similar to the U.S. approach. The ECHR requires compensation "reasonably related" to market value but permits below-market compensation in the public interest.

### Regulatory Takings

The treatment of regulations that reduce property value without physical appropriation differs dramatically. The United States has developed the most elaborate regulatory takings doctrine, treating regulations that eliminate all economically beneficial use as per se takings (Lucas) and applying multi-factor balancing for partial diminutions in value (Penn Central). Germany addresses this through the Inhalts- und Schrankenbestimmung framework, which permits substantial property restrictions without compensation as long as they serve the social obligation of property. The ECHR's "control of use" rule permits extensive regulation without compensation, subject to proportionality review. South Korea's Constitutional Court has recognized regulatory takings in principle but has applied the doctrine restrictively.
