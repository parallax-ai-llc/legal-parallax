## Summary

The constitutional amendment process -- the formal mechanism by which a nation's supreme legal document is altered, supplemented, or revised -- represents a critical balancing act between democratic adaptability and constitutional stability, varying dramatically across jurisdictions in terms of procedural complexity, institutional involvement, and the presence of unamendable provisions. South Korea's Constitution (대한민국 헌법) prescribes a rigorous amendment process under Articles 128-130, requiring proposal by a majority of the National Assembly or by the President, passage by two-thirds of the National Assembly, and ratification by a national referendum within thirty days, with Article 128(2) prohibiting any extension of the presidential term through amendment affecting the incumbent; Korea has experienced nine constitutions since 1948, with the current Ninth Constitution (제9차 개헌) adopted through the landmark democratic transition of 1987, and no amendments have been successfully enacted since. The United States Constitution, under Article V, provides two methods of proposal (two-thirds of both houses of Congress, or a convention called by two-thirds of state legislatures) and two methods of ratification (three-fourths of state legislatures, or conventions in three-fourths of the states), creating one of the most difficult amendment processes in the world; only 27 amendments have been ratified since 1789, with the most recent -- the Twenty-Seventh Amendment concerning congressional pay -- ratified in 1992 after a 202-year ratification period. Germany's Grundgesetz, under Article 79, requires a two-thirds majority in both the Bundestag and the Bundesrat, while Article 79(3) establishes an "eternity clause" (Ewigkeitsklausel) that absolutely prohibits amendments affecting the basic principles of Articles 1 (human dignity) and 20 (democratic and social federal state); Germany has amended its Grundgesetz over 60 times since 1949, reflecting a more adaptive constitutional culture. The European framework, while not involving amendment of a single constitutional text, includes the EU Treaty revision process under Article 48 TEU (ordinary and simplified revision procedures), the ECHR's amendment through protocols requiring ratification by all or a specified number of parties, and the Council of Europe's Venice Commission guidelines on constitutional amendment procedures as safeguards of democratic governance.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

The constitutional amendment process in South Korea is governed by Chapter X of the Constitution (헌법 제10장 헌법개정), comprising Articles 128 through 130.

**Article 128 (Proposal):** Amendments may be proposed by a majority of the total members of the National Assembly (국회) or by the President. Article 128(2) provides that amendments extending the term of the President or permitting re-election of the President shall not be effective for the President in office at the time of the proposal -- a provision designed to prevent self-serving constitutional manipulation.

**Article 129 (Public Notice):** Proposed amendments must be publicly announced by the President for twenty or more days.

**Article 130 (Passage and Ratification):** The National Assembly must act on the proposed amendment within sixty days of public notice and approve it by a concurrent vote of two-thirds or more of the total members. The approved amendment must then be submitted to a national referendum within thirty days, where it must receive the affirmative votes of more than half of all voters eligible to vote in elections for members of the National Assembly, with more than half of all eligible voters participating.

### The 1987 Democratic Constitution

The current Ninth Amendment was adopted on October 29, 1987, following the June Democratic Struggle (6월 민주항쟁). The June 29 Declaration by Roh Tae-woo conceded to popular demands for direct presidential election and other democratic reforms. The amendment was approved by national referendum on October 27, 1987, with 93.1 percent voting in favor and a turnout of 78.2 percent. Key changes included restoration of direct presidential election, limitation of the president to a single five-year term, expansion of the Bill of Rights, strengthening of the National Assembly's powers, and establishment of the Constitutional Court (헌법재판소).

### Contemporary Amendment Debates

Since 1987, multiple amendment proposals have been debated but none have succeeded. Major proposals have included:

- **Four-year renewable presidential term:** Multiple proposals to replace the single five-year term with a four-year term renewable once, modeled on the U.S. system.
- **Decentralization:** Proposals to constitutionalize greater local autonomy and fiscal federalism.
- **Fundamental rights expansion:** Proposals to add rights to a clean environment, information privacy, and gender equality.
- **Power-sharing:** Proposals for a semi-presidential system similar to France's Fifth Republic.

In 2018, President Moon Jae-in formally proposed a constitutional amendment that included a four-year renewable presidential term, expanded fundamental rights, and strengthened local autonomy. The proposal failed to achieve the required two-thirds majority in the National Assembly on May 24, 2018, receiving only 174 votes (out of 293 members present; 200 votes were needed).

## United States

### Legal Framework

Article V of the United States Constitution establishes two methods for proposing amendments and two methods for ratification:

**Proposal:** (1) Two-thirds vote of both the House of Representatives and the Senate; or (2) a convention called by Congress upon application of the legislatures of two-thirds of the states. Only the first method has ever been used.

**Ratification:** (1) Approval by the legislatures of three-fourths of the states; or (2) approval by conventions in three-fourths of the states. The convention method has been used only once, for the Twenty-First Amendment (repeal of Prohibition) in 1933.

### Amendment Statistics

Of the more than 11,000 constitutional amendments proposed in Congress since 1789, only 33 have been submitted to the states for ratification, and only 27 have been ratified. The first ten amendments (the Bill of Rights) were ratified in 1791 as a package. The most recent ratification -- the Twenty-Seventh Amendment, originally proposed in 1789 -- occurred in 1992.

### Key Constitutional Jurisprudence

**Coleman v. Miller, 307 U.S. 433 (1939):** The Supreme Court held that questions regarding the validity of the amendment process -- specifically, whether a state's ratification of an amendment remains effective after a prior rejection and whether a proposed amendment has lost its vitality due to the passage of time -- are political questions committed to Congress.

**National Prohibition Cases, 253 U.S. 350 (1920):** The Court upheld the Eighteenth Amendment (Prohibition), rejecting arguments that its subject matter was beyond the scope of the amendment power and that the two-thirds vote requirement referred to two-thirds of total membership rather than two-thirds of a quorum.

**Hollingsworth v. Virginia, 3 U.S. 378 (1798):** The Court held that the President has no role in the constitutional amendment process -- presidential signature is not required, and the President has no veto power over proposed amendments.

### The Equal Rights Amendment Controversy

The Equal Rights Amendment (ERA), proposed in 1972 with a seven-year ratification deadline later extended to 1982, illustrates the complexities of the amendment process. Three additional states ratified after the original deadline (Nevada in 2017, Illinois in 2018, Virginia in 2020), potentially reaching the three-fourths threshold. However, the National Archivist has not certified the amendment, and litigation in Equal Rights Amendment v. Archivist of the United States (D.C. Circuit, 2023) addressed whether the expired deadline invalidated later ratifications.

### Article V Convention

Despite never being convened, the Article V convention mechanism has generated significant legal scholarship and political activity. As of 2026, several convention campaigns remain active, including calls for a balanced budget amendment and term limits. Legal uncertainty surrounds the scope of an Article V convention -- whether it could be limited to a single topic or would be a "runaway convention" with plenary power.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Article 79 of the Grundgesetz governs constitutional amendments:

**Article 79(1):** The Grundgesetz may be amended only by a law that expressly amends or supplements the text. In matters of international treaties, a law declaring consent may indicate that the provisions of the Grundgesetz are not being amended.

**Article 79(2):** Such a law requires the approval of two-thirds of the members of the Bundestag and two-thirds of the votes of the Bundesrat.

**Article 79(3) -- Ewigkeitsklausel (Eternity Clause):** Amendments affecting the division of the Federation into Länder, their participation in principle in the legislative process, or the principles laid down in Articles 1 and 20 are inadmissible. Article 1 protects human dignity; Article 20 establishes Germany as a democratic and social federal state with separation of powers, rule of law, and popular sovereignty.

### Amendment Frequency

Germany has amended the Grundgesetz more than 67 times since its adoption in 1949, reflecting a constitutional culture that views amendment as a normal part of democratic governance rather than an extraordinary event. Major amendments include:

| Year | Amendment | Subject |
|------|-----------|---------|
| 1956 | Defense amendment | Creation of Bundeswehr; parliamentary army |
| 1968 | Emergency amendment | Emergency powers provisions (Notstandsgesetze) |
| 1990 | Reunification | Art. 146 revised; new Länder accession under Art. 23 |
| 1992 | Maastricht amendment | Art. 23 (European Union); Art. 28(1) (EU citizen voting rights) |
| 1994 | Gender equality | Art. 3(2) strengthened |
| 2006 | Federalism Reform I | Redistribution of federal-state legislative competences |
| 2009 | Debt brake | Art. 109, 115 (Schuldenbremse -- balanced budget requirement) |
| 2017 | Federal fiscal equalization | Comprehensive reform of federal-state financial relations |
| 2022 | Sondervermögen | Art. 143g: 100 billion EUR special fund for Bundeswehr |
| 2024 | Federal Constitutional Court | Art. 93, 94 amended to constitutionally entrench BVerfG structure |

### Judicial Review of Amendments

The Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) exercises review authority over constitutional amendments under Article 79(3). In the Southwest State Case (BVerfGE 1, 14, 1951), the Court established that constitutional norms may be unconstitutional if they violate fundamental principles protected by Article 79(3). In the Lisbon Treaty Decision (BVerfGE 123, 267, 2009), the Court reviewed whether the EU Treaty of Lisbon was compatible with the constitutional identity protected by the eternity clause. The 2024 amendment to constitutionally protect the BVerfG's structure was itself partly motivated by concerns about democratic backsliding, following the Polish and Hungarian experiences.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Treaty Revision (Article 48 TEU)

The Treaty on European Union establishes two revision procedures:

**Ordinary Revision Procedure:** Proposals may be submitted by any member state government, the European Parliament, or the Commission. A Convention composed of representatives of national parliaments, heads of state/government, the European Parliament, and the Commission examines the proposals. The Convention's recommendations are considered by an Intergovernmental Conference (IGC), and amendments must be ratified by all member states in accordance with their respective constitutional requirements.

**Simplified Revision Procedure:** For amendments to Part Three of the TFEU (internal EU policies), the European Council may adopt a decision amending these provisions unanimously, subject to consultation with the European Parliament and the Commission, and approval by member states in accordance with their constitutional requirements. No Convention or IGC is required.

### ECHR Protocols

The European Convention on Human Rights is amended through protocols, which require ratification by contracting parties. Protocols adding new rights (e.g., Protocol No. 1 on property rights, Protocol No. 6 on abolition of the death penalty) require ratification by the state to be bound. Procedural protocols amending the Convention machinery (e.g., Protocol No. 11 establishing a permanent Court, Protocol No. 14 on efficiency reforms) generally require ratification by all parties.

### Venice Commission Guidelines

The European Commission for Democracy through Law (Venice Commission) has issued several opinions on constitutional amendment processes, establishing principles including:

- Constitutional amendment procedures should be clearly defined and not subject to manipulation.
- The amendment process should ensure broad consensus and protect minority rights.
- Substantive limits on amendment (eternity clauses) may be desirable to protect core democratic principles.
- Referendum requirements should include appropriate safeguards against manipulation.
- Constitutional courts should have the authority to review the constitutionality of amendments.

The Venice Commission's Opinion No. 469/2008 on Constitutional Amendment provides a comprehensive comparative analysis of amendment procedures across Council of Europe member states.

### EU Constitutional Identity

The CJEU has recognized that EU law must respect the constitutional identity of member states under Article 4(2) TEU. In cases such as Gauweiler (C-62/14, 2015) and subsequent rulings, the relationship between EU treaty obligations and national constitutional amendment processes has been examined, particularly regarding the extent to which EU integration may effectively amend national constitutional frameworks without formal domestic amendment procedures.

## Comparative Analysis

### Rigidity Spectrum

The four jurisdictions span a wide spectrum of constitutional rigidity. The United States has the most rigid formal amendment process, as evidenced by only 27 amendments in over 230 years. South Korea's process is similarly demanding in theory (two-thirds legislative majority plus referendum), though the 1987 experience demonstrates that popular mobilization can overcome these barriers. Germany's two-thirds double majority is procedurally demanding but has proven workable in practice, with over 67 amendments. The EU treaty revision process, requiring unanimity of all member states, is arguably the most rigid of all, though simplified procedures provide flexibility for certain matters.

### Role of Popular Participation

South Korea is the only jurisdiction among the four that requires a national referendum for constitutional amendment, ensuring direct democratic legitimacy for constitutional change. The United States has never used the referendum mechanism, relying instead on indirect popular participation through elected state legislators. Germany deliberately excludes national referendums from the amendment process, a choice rooted in the negative experience of plebiscitary democracy during the Weimar Republic. The EU process delegates the question of referendum requirements to national constitutional law, with some member states (Ireland, Denmark) requiring referendums for treaty ratification and others not.

### Unamendable Provisions

Germany's eternity clause (Article 79(3)) represents the most explicit and enforceable limitation on amendment power. South Korea's Article 128(2), while not a true eternity clause, prevents self-serving presidential term extensions. The United States Constitution contains no explicit eternity clause, though some scholars argue that the guarantee of equal suffrage in the Senate (Article V's final clause) functions as one. The ECHR's non-derogable rights under Article 15(2) serve a similar function within the Convention framework.

### Judicial Review of Amendments

The four jurisdictions take different positions on whether courts may review the substance of constitutional amendments. Germany's BVerfG actively reviews amendments under Article 79(3). The U.S. Supreme Court has largely treated amendment process questions as political questions (Coleman v. Miller). South Korea's Constitutional Court has not had occasion to rule on the constitutionality of an amendment, though its authority to do so is debated. The CJEU reviews EU treaty amendments for compatibility with fundamental principles.
