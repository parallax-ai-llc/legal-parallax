## Summary

Air piracy and hijacking -- the unlawful seizure or exercise of control over an aircraft through force, threat, or other forms of intimidation -- constitute among the most severely punished offenses in comparative criminal law, reflecting the catastrophic potential for mass casualties and the unique vulnerability of persons aboard an aircraft in flight. South Korea addresses aircraft hijacking through the Aviation Security Act (항공보안법), which provides for the death penalty or life imprisonment for hijacking an aircraft in service, and through the Act on the Punishment of Crimes relating to Aircraft Navigation (항공기운항안전법), imposing penalties of up to life imprisonment for acts endangering aviation safety, with the Korean legal framework shaped significantly by the 1958 Korean Air Lines YS-11 hijacking to North Korea and the 1987 Korean Air Flight 858 bombing. The United States criminalizes air piracy under 49 U.S.C. Section 46502 (aircraft piracy) with penalties up to life imprisonment or death, supplemented by 18 U.S.C. Section 32 (destruction of aircraft), and has built the world's most extensive aviation security apparatus through the Transportation Security Administration (TSA) following the September 11, 2001 attacks. Germany addresses aircraft hijacking through Section 316c of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB, Angriffe auf den Luft- und Seeverkehr), carrying penalties of five to fifteen years' imprisonment or life imprisonment where death results, integrated within a broader European aviation security framework. At the international level, three foundational treaties -- the Tokyo Convention (1963), the Hague Convention (1970), and the Montreal Convention (1971) -- establish the aut dedere aut judicare (extradite or prosecute) principle, supplemented by the Beijing Convention (2010), while the ECHR has addressed aviation security through Articles 2 and 5 and the EU has implemented comprehensive aviation security regulations through Regulation (EC) No 300/2008.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's aviation security legislation reflects the country's direct experience with aircraft hijackings and bombings, particularly those perpetrated by North Korean agents during the Cold War period.

**Aviation Security Act (항공보안법):** This Act establishes the primary framework for aviation security, criminalizing hijacking and related offenses.

### Penalty Structure

| Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|
| Hijacking an aircraft in service | Death or life imprisonment |
| Hijacking attempt | Up to life imprisonment |
| Violence or threats to persons on aircraft | Up to 10 years imprisonment |
| Endangering aircraft navigation safety | Up to life imprisonment |
| Placing dangerous substance on aircraft | 3-15 years imprisonment |
| False bomb threat | Up to 5 years imprisonment |
| Damage to aviation facilities | Up to 10 years imprisonment |

### Historical Context: Korean Air Incidents

**Korean Air Lines YS-11 Hijacking (1958/1969):** Multiple Korean Air flights were hijacked to North Korea during the 1960s and 1970s. The most significant was the hijacking of Korean Air Lines Flight YS-11 on December 11, 1969, when a Korean Air domestic flight with 51 passengers was diverted to Wonsan, North Korea. Eleven crew and passengers were never returned, remaining in North Korea.

**Korean Air Flight 858 Bombing (1987):** On November 29, 1987, North Korean agents Kim Hyon-hui and Kim Sung-il planted a bomb on Korean Air Flight 858 en route from Baghdad to Seoul via Abu Dhabi and Bangkok, killing all 115 persons on board. The bombing was ordered by the North Korean government in an attempt to destabilize South Korea ahead of the 1988 Seoul Olympics. Kim Hyon-hui was arrested, convicted, and sentenced to death, later receiving a presidential pardon. This incident directly influenced South Korea's subsequent aviation security legislation.

### National Aviation Security Committee

South Korea established the National Aviation Security Committee (국가항공보안위원회) under the Aviation Security Act, chaired by the Minister of Land, Infrastructure and Transport, to coordinate aviation security policy. The Korea Office of Civil Aviation (KOCA) implements security measures at all Korean airports, including mandatory screening of all passengers, baggage, and cargo.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States maintains the world's most extensive aviation security legal framework, dramatically expanded following the September 11, 2001 terrorist attacks.

**49 U.S.C. Section 46502 (Aircraft Piracy):** The core federal anti-hijacking statute provides: "An individual who, while aboard an aircraft in the special aircraft jurisdiction of the United States, commits or attempts to commit aircraft piracy shall be imprisoned for not less than 20 years. If the death of another person results from the commission or attempted commission of an aircraft piracy, the individual shall be put to death or imprisoned for life."

**18 U.S.C. Section 32 (Destruction of Aircraft or Aircraft Facilities):** Criminalizes the willful destruction or disabling of an aircraft, with penalties of up to 20 years' imprisonment, or life imprisonment or death where death results.

### Additional Federal Provisions

| Statute | Scope | Penalty |
|---------|-------|---------|
| 49 U.S.C. § 46503 | Interference with security screening | Up to 10 years |
| 49 U.S.C. § 46504 | Assault on flight crew with dangerous weapon | Up to life imprisonment |
| 49 U.S.C. § 46505 | Carrying weapon or explosive aboard aircraft | Up to 15 years |
| 49 U.S.C. § 46506 | Application of certain criminal laws to acts on aircraft | Varies |
| 18 U.S.C. § 2332a | Use of weapons of mass destruction (including on aircraft) | Up to life; death if death results |

### Post-September 11 Framework

The Aviation and Transportation Security Act (ATSA) of November 19, 2001, created the Transportation Security Administration (TSA) within the Department of Homeland Security and fundamentally transformed U.S. aviation security:

- Federalized airport security screening, replacing private contractors with federal employees
- Mandated reinforced cockpit doors on all commercial aircraft
- Established the Federal Air Marshal Service within TSA
- Created the Secure Flight passenger prescreening program
- Implemented the No Fly List and Selectee List under the Terrorist Screening Database

### Key Cases

**United States v. Rezaq, 134 F.3d 1121 (D.C. Cir. 1998):** The D.C. Circuit upheld the conviction of Omar Mohammed Ali Rezaq for air piracy in connection with the 1985 hijacking of EgyptAir Flight 648. The court confirmed that U.S. courts have jurisdiction over aircraft piracy committed aboard foreign aircraft where the defendant is later found in the United States, under the aut dedere aut judicare principle.

**United States v. Reid, 369 F.3d 619 (1st Cir. 2004):** Richard Reid, the "shoe bomber," was convicted of attempted destruction of an aircraft and sentenced to life imprisonment for his December 22, 2001 attempt to detonate explosives hidden in his shoes aboard American Airlines Flight 63 from Paris to Miami.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany addresses aircraft hijacking through Section 316c of the Strafgesetzbuch (Angriffe auf den Luft- und Seeverkehr), which covers attacks on both air and sea transport.

**Section 316c(1) StGB:** Whoever, using force, threat of force or another serious harm, or through trickery, seizes or exercises control of a civil aircraft or a vehicle used in civilian shipping shall be punished with imprisonment of not less than five years.

### Penalty Structure

| Offense | Provision | Penalty |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Seizure of aircraft | Section 316c(1) | 5-15 years imprisonment |
| Attack on persons aboard to facilitate seizure | Section 316c(1) No. 2 | 5-15 years imprisonment |
| Use of firearms or explosives | Section 316c(1) No. 3 | 5-15 years imprisonment |
| Death of victim resulting | Section 316c(3) | Life imprisonment |
| Less serious case | Section 316c(4) | 1-10 years imprisonment |
| Attempt | Section 316c(2) | Punishable |

### Mogadishu Rescue (GSG 9, 1977)

The most significant German aviation security incident was the hijacking of Lufthansa Flight 181 on October 13, 1977, as part of the "German Autumn" (Deutscher Herbst) terrorist campaign. Palestinian hijackers from the Popular Front for the Liberation of Palestine (PFLP), acting in coordination with the Red Army Faction (RAF), seized the Boeing 737 with 86 passengers and crew, demanding the release of imprisoned RAF members. After five days and diversions through Rome, Larnaca, Bahrain, Dubai, and Aden (where the hijackers murdered pilot Jürgen Schumann), the aircraft landed in Mogadishu, Somalia, where the GSG 9 (Grenzschutzgruppe 9) counter-terrorism unit stormed the plane, rescuing all hostages and killing three of the four hijackers.

### Aviation Security Administration

The Bundespolizei (Federal Police) is responsible for aviation security at German airports under the Aviation Security Act (Luftsicherheitsgesetz, LuftSiG) of January 11, 2005. The Bundespolizei conducts passenger and baggage screening, while the Federal Office for Information Security (BSI) addresses cybersecurity threats to aviation infrastructure.

### Luftsicherheitsgesetz and Shoot-Down Authorization

The Luftsicherheitsgesetz of 2005 controversially included a provision (Section 14(3)) authorizing the military to shoot down aircraft being used as weapons against human life. The Bundesverfassungsgericht (Federal Constitutional Court) struck down this provision in its landmark decision of February 15, 2006 (BVerfGE 115, 118), holding that the authorization to kill innocent passengers violated their right to life (Article 2(2) GG) and their human dignity (Article 1(1) GG). The court held that the state may not treat innocent passengers as mere objects to be sacrificed, even to save a greater number of lives.

## ECHR & EU

### International Conventions

The international legal framework for combating aircraft hijacking rests on a series of conventions adopted under the auspices of the International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO):

**Tokyo Convention (1963):** The Convention on Offences and Certain Other Acts Committed on Board Aircraft establishes the jurisdiction of the state of registration over offenses committed on board and authorizes the aircraft commander to take reasonable measures to restrain persons who jeopardize the safety of the aircraft.

**Hague Convention (1970):** The Convention for the Suppression of Unlawful Seizure of Aircraft (the Hijacking Convention) requires states parties to make hijacking punishable by severe penalties and establishes the aut dedere aut judicare obligation: states must either extradite the alleged offender or submit the case to prosecution.

**Montreal Convention (1971):** The Convention for the Suppression of Unlawful Acts Against the Safety of Civil Aviation extends the framework to acts of sabotage and violence against aircraft, passengers, and aviation facilities.

**Beijing Convention (2010):** The Convention on the Suppression of Unlawful Acts Relating to International Civil Aviation updates and expands the Montreal Convention to address new threats, including the use of aircraft as weapons, the use of biological, chemical, nuclear, or radioactive substances against aircraft, and cyberattacks on aviation systems.

### European Court of Human Rights

**McCann and Others v. United Kingdom (Application No. 18984/91, 1995):** While not an aviation case, the Grand Chamber's strict scrutiny of the use of lethal force by state agents in counter-terrorism operations established principles directly applicable to aviation security operations. The Court found a violation of Article 2, holding that the planning and control of the operation must minimize risk to life.

**Article 5 and Aviation Security Detention:** The ECHR's jurisprudence on Article 5 (right to liberty) is relevant to the detention of suspected hijackers and persons deemed security threats. States must ensure that detention based on aviation security concerns complies with Article 5's requirements of lawfulness, necessity, and proportionality.

### EU Aviation Security Framework

**Regulation (EC) No 300/2008:** This regulation establishes common rules in the field of civil aviation security, replacing earlier Regulation 2320/2002 adopted in the immediate aftermath of September 11. The regulation mandates common basic standards for airport security, aircraft security, passenger and cabin baggage screening, and cargo/mail security.

**Regulation (EU) 2015/1998:** Commission Implementing Regulation laying down detailed measures for the implementation of the common basic standards on aviation security, including specific screening methods and technology requirements.

**EU Passenger Name Record (PNR) Directive 2016/681:** Requires airlines operating flights to/from the EU to transfer PNR data to member state authorities for the purpose of preventing, detecting, investigating, and prosecuting terrorist offenses and serious crime, including aircraft hijacking.

## Comparative Analysis

### Penalty Severity

Aircraft hijacking is treated as one of the most serious criminal offenses across all jurisdictions. South Korea and the United States both maintain the death penalty for hijacking resulting in death. Germany imposes mandatory life imprisonment for the same outcome. The international conventions do not specify particular penalties but require "severe" punishment.

### Jurisdictional Approaches

The jurisdictional frameworks reflect the inherently international nature of aircraft hijacking. The Hague Convention's aut dedere aut judicare principle ensures that alleged hijackers cannot escape prosecution by fleeing to another state party. The United States asserts jurisdiction on the broadest basis, including under the "special aircraft jurisdiction" concept that extends to any aircraft in which a U.S. national is aboard.

### Aviation Security Models

The jurisdictions have adopted different administrative models for aviation security. The United States centralized security under the federal TSA following September 11. Germany assigns primary responsibility to the Bundespolizei. South Korea utilizes the Korea Office of Civil Aviation. The EU has harmonized security standards across member states through Regulation 300/2008 while leaving implementation to national authorities.

### Shoot-Down Authorization

The question of whether military aircraft may shoot down a hijacked civilian aircraft being used as a weapon presents the most extreme legal dilemma. Germany's Federal Constitutional Court categorically rejected this authorization. The United States, by contrast, has maintained presidential authority to order shoot-downs under extreme circumstances, as reflected in the authorization given on September 11, 2001.
