## Summary

Orphan works are copyrighted works whose rights holders cannot be identified or located after a diligent search, creating a barrier to the use, reproduction, digitization, and dissemination of vast quantities of cultural heritage material held in libraries, archives, museums, and other cultural institutions. The orphan works problem is particularly acute in the context of mass digitization projects, where millions of works must be cleared for copyright before they can be made available online, and the cost of conducting individual diligent searches for each work is prohibitively high. South Korea addresses orphan works through the statutory license system (법정허락) under Articles 50-52 of the Copyright Act (저작권법), administered by the Ministry of Culture, Sports and Tourism (문화체육관광부) and the Korea Copyright Commission (한국저작권위원회), which permits the use of orphan works upon application, payment of a compensation fee into escrow, and demonstration of a diligent but unsuccessful search for the rights holder; the 2020 amendments significantly streamlined the statutory license process for mass digitization by cultural heritage institutions. The United States has no comprehensive orphan works legislation despite extensive legislative efforts, including the Orphan Works Act of 2006 (which passed the Senate but not the House), the Orphan Works and Mass Digitization Act of 2008, and the Copyright Office's 2015 Report on Orphan Works and Mass Digitization; the absence of a legislative solution means that users must rely on fair use (17 U.S.C. Section 107), the Section 108 library exception, or risk-based assessments of infringement liability, with the Authors Guild v. Google (804 F.3d 202 (2d Cir. 2015)) decision providing limited guidance on mass digitization as fair use. Germany implements the EU Orphan Works Directive (2012/28/EU) through Sections 61-61c of the Urheberrechtsgesetz (UrhG), permitting publicly accessible cultural heritage institutions to reproduce and make available online orphan works contained in their collections after conducting a diligent search, with the results recorded in the EUIPO Orphan Works Database; additionally, Germany enacted an extended collective licensing (ECL) mechanism in the 2021 UrhG reform (Section 51 VGG) to address out-of-commerce works. The ECHR/EU framework provides the most developed orphan works regime through Directive 2012/28/EU (Orphan Works Directive), which establishes a mutual recognition system for orphan works status across the EU, supplemented by Directive (EU) 2019/790 (Digital Single Market Directive) Articles 8-11 on out-of-commerce works licensing through collective management organizations, and the EUIPO Orphan Works Database serving as the centralized registry.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's statutory license system for orphan works is among the most established and actively used in the world, reflecting the country's commitment to expanding access to cultural heritage and knowledge resources.

**Copyright Act Articles 50-52 (법정허락):** The statutory license system permits any person who wishes to use a copyrighted work to apply to the Ministry of Culture, Sports and Tourism for a statutory license when: (1) the identity or location of the copyright holder cannot be determined despite a considerable effort to find them (Article 50); (2) the copyright holder's whereabouts are unknown after a published work's normal period of exploitation has expired (Article 51); or (3) negotiations for a license have failed (Article 52, covering compulsory licensing for broadcasting).

### Diligent Search Requirements

The Ministry of Culture has issued detailed guidelines on what constitutes a "considerable effort" (상당한 노력) to locate the rights holder:

**Search Steps:** (1) Consultation of the Korea Copyright Commission's copyright registration database (저작권 등록 데이터베이스); (2) inquiries to relevant collective management organizations (한국음악저작권협회 (KOMCA), 한국복제전송저작권협회 (KORRA), etc.); (3) searches of publisher and author databases; (4) public notice through the Ministry's official gazette and website for a period of at least 10 days; (5) searches of international databases including the International Standard Name Identifier (ISNI) and the VIAF (Virtual International Authority File).

**2020 Streamlined Procedure:** The 2020 amendments introduced a simplified diligent search procedure for cultural heritage institutions (문화유산기관) undertaking mass digitization projects. Under this procedure, institutions may conduct batch searches covering multiple works simultaneously, and the Korea Copyright Commission provides enhanced search support, including automated database matching and reduced public notice periods for works older than a specified threshold.

### Compensation and Escrow

Upon approval of a statutory license application, the applicant must deposit a compensation fee (보상금) with the Korea Copyright Commission. The fee is determined by the Commission based on prevailing market rates for comparable licenses, considering factors such as the type of work, scope of intended use, and commercial or non-commercial character of the use. The deposited funds are held in escrow for a period of 10 years, during which a rights holder who comes forward may claim the compensation. Unclaimed funds are transferred to the Korean Copyright Commission's cultural fund (문화기금).

### Statistical Data

The Korean statutory license system processes a significant volume of applications annually. In 2024, the Korea Copyright Commission processed approximately 3,500 statutory license applications, with the majority relating to the digitization of out-of-print books, academic publications, and audiovisual materials by the National Library of Korea (국립중앙도서관) and university libraries. The approval rate exceeds 90%, reflecting the well-established nature of the diligent search process.

### Cultural Heritage Digitization Initiatives

South Korea has undertaken ambitious digitization projects that rely on the statutory license system:

**National Library of Korea Digital Library (국립중앙도서관 디지털도서관):** The National Library's mass digitization program has digitized over 5 million items, with the statutory license system used for works where the rights holder cannot be located. The Library's "Korean Knowledge Portal" provides open access to digitized materials, including orphan works used under statutory license.

**Korean Studies Digital Archive:** The Academy of Korean Studies (한국학중앙연구원) operates a digital archive of historical and cultural materials, using the statutory license system for 20th-century materials with unlocatable rights holders.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has failed to enact comprehensive orphan works legislation despite decades of effort, leaving cultural institutions and other users in a state of legal uncertainty.

**Legislative History:**

**2006 Copyright Office Report:** The U.S. Copyright Office published a comprehensive report on orphan works in January 2006, recommending legislation that would limit remedies (both injunctive relief and damages) for users who conducted a reasonably diligent search and failed to locate the copyright owner. The report proposed that qualifying users would be limited to paying "reasonable compensation" if the rights holder later appeared.

**Orphan Works Act of 2006 (S. 2913):** Senator Hatch introduced the Orphan Works Act, which passed the Senate Judiciary Committee but was not enacted before the 109th Congress adjourned.

**Orphan Works and Mass Digitization Act of 2008 (S. 2913, H.R. 5889):** The Shawn Bentley Orphan Works Act of 2008 passed the Senate unanimously, but the House version was not brought to a floor vote before the 110th Congress ended. The bill would have provided a limitation on remedies for orphan work users who conducted a qualifying search and included a mass digitization pilot program for libraries and archives.

**2015 Copyright Office Report:** The Copyright Office issued an updated orphan works report in June 2015, reaffirming its recommendations for legislation and proposing an extended collective licensing (ECL) system for mass digitization, modeled on Nordic ECL systems. Congress has not acted on these recommendations.

### Fair Use as a Partial Solution

In the absence of legislation, fair use (17 U.S.C. Section 107) serves as the primary legal basis for uses of orphan works in the United States:

**Authors Guild v. Google, Inc. (804 F.3d 202 (2d Cir. 2015)):** The Second Circuit held that Google's mass digitization of millions of books for its Google Books project, including the creation of a searchable database and display of limited "snippets," constituted fair use. The court emphasized the transformative purpose of the project (creating a searchable index and enabling research) and the limited nature of the display (snippets rather than full text). While this decision did not specifically address orphan works, it provided significant support for mass digitization projects.

**Authors Guild v. HathiTrust (755 F.3d 87 (2d Cir. 2014)):** The Second Circuit held that the HathiTrust Digital Library's creation of a full-text searchable database of digitized works, provision of access for the print-disabled, and preservation activities constituted fair use. The court explicitly addressed orphan works, noting that the library's practice of identifying and making available orphan works served important public interests.

**Google Books Settlement (Rejected):** The proposed Google Books settlement (2009), which would have created a comprehensive framework for the use of orphan works including a Book Rights Registry and revenue-sharing mechanism, was rejected by Judge Chin in Authors Guild v. Google (770 F. Supp. 2d 666 (S.D.N.Y. 2011)) on the grounds that it effectively rewrote copyright law and raised antitrust concerns.

### Section 108 Library Exception

17 U.S.C. Section 108 provides limited exceptions for libraries and archives to reproduce and distribute copies for preservation, replacement of damaged copies, and interlibrary loan. However, Section 108 does not specifically address orphan works or mass digitization, and its scope is narrower than what most cultural institutions need for comprehensive digitization programs. The Copyright Office's ongoing Section 108 Study Group has recommended modernizing the provision, but Congress has not acted.

### Risk-Based Approaches

In practice, many U.S. cultural institutions adopt risk-based approaches to orphan works, assessing the likelihood and potential cost of a rights holder emerging and asserting a claim. Factors considered include the age of the work, the nature of the institution's use (non-commercial, educational), the availability of alternative sources, and the reputational risk of infringement litigation. Major institutions including the Library of Congress, the Smithsonian Institution, and university libraries have developed internal orphan works policies based on such risk assessments.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany has implemented a multi-layered approach to orphan works, combining the EU Orphan Works Directive, extended collective licensing for out-of-commerce works, and traditional copyright limitations.

**Sections 61-61c UrhG (Orphan Works Directive Implementation):** Enacted in 2014 to transpose Directive 2012/28/EU, these provisions permit publicly accessible libraries, educational establishments, museums, archives, film and audio heritage institutions, and public-service broadcasting organizations to reproduce and make available online orphan works contained in their collections after conducting a diligent search in accordance with the sources listed in the Annex to the Directive.

### Diligent Search Standards

The diligent search must be conducted in good faith and must consult the appropriate sources for the category of work in question:

**Published Books and Pamphlets:** Legal deposit records, publishers' databases, the Verzeichnis Lieferbarer Bücher (VLB, German Books in Print), collective management organization records (VG WORT), ISBN databases, and the VIAF.

**Newspapers, Magazines, and Periodicals:** ISSN databases, publishers' databases, collective management organization records, and legal deposit holdings.

**Visual Works (including photographs):** Collective management organization records (VG Bild-Kunst), image databases, publishers' archives, and professional photography associations.

**Audiovisual and Sound Recordings:** Film heritage institution databases (Deutsche Kinemathek), collective management organization records (GEMA, GVL), broadcasting archives, and the European Film Gateway.

### Orphan Works Status and EUIPO Database

Upon completing the diligent search, the institution must record the results with the European Union Intellectual Property Office (EUIPO), which maintains the Orphan Works Database. Once registered, the orphan works status is recognized across all EU member states under the mutual recognition principle (Article 4 of the Directive), permitting cultural institutions in other member states to use the same work without conducting a separate diligent search.

### End of Orphan Works Status (Section 61b UrhG)

If a rights holder comes forward and claims ownership, the orphan works status ends prospectively. The rights holder is entitled to "fair compensation" (angemessene Vergütung) for past use, negotiated with the institution or determined through collective management organization mediation. The institution must cease use or obtain a standard license.

### Extended Collective Licensing for Out-of-Commerce Works

The 2021 reform of the Verwertungsgesellschaftengesetz (VGG, Collective Management Organizations Act) introduced an ECL mechanism for out-of-commerce works under Section 51 VGG, transposing Articles 8-11 of the DSM Directive (2019/790):

**Scope:** Cultural heritage institutions may obtain licenses from representative collective management organizations to digitize and make available online works in their collections that are permanently out of commerce, including orphan works.

**Extended Effect:** The ECL mechanism extends the license to cover works by non-members of the collective management organization, provided the CMO is sufficiently representative of the relevant category of rights holders.

**Out-of-Commerce Works Portal:** The EUIPO maintains an Out-of-Commerce Works Portal where institutions register their intended use, and rights holders may opt out of the licensing arrangement within a specified period.

### Deutsche Digitale Bibliothek

The Deutsche Digitale Bibliothek (DDB, German Digital Library), Germany's national digital library initiative, relies on both the orphan works provisions and the ECL mechanism for out-of-commerce works to build its online collection. The DDB aggregates digitized content from over 4,000 cultural institutions across Germany, including museums, libraries, archives, and research institutions.

## ECHR & EU

### Orphan Works Directive (2012/28/EU)

The EU Orphan Works Directive, adopted on October 25, 2012, and implemented by member states by October 29, 2014, establishes the primary EU-wide framework for the use of orphan works by cultural heritage institutions.

**Scope (Article 1):** The Directive covers: (1) works published in the form of books, journals, newspapers, magazines, or other writings in the collections of publicly accessible libraries, educational establishments, or museums, as well as in the collections of archives or film or audio heritage institutions; (2) cinematographic or audiovisual works and phonograms in the collections of these institutions; and (3) works and phonograms produced by public-service broadcasting organizations.

**Diligent Search (Article 3):** Before a work may be designated as orphan, a diligent search must be conducted in good faith for each work, consulting the appropriate sources for the category of work in question, as specified in the Annex. The search must be conducted in the member state of first publication or, if the work was not published, the member state of first broadcast.

**Permitted Uses (Article 6):** Cultural heritage institutions may: (1) make orphan works available to the public (in the sense of Article 3 of Directive 2001/29/EC); and (2) reproduce orphan works for the purposes of digitization, making available, indexing, cataloguing, preservation, or restoration.

**Mutual Recognition (Article 4):** A work considered orphan in one member state shall be considered orphan in all member states, and the work may be used in accordance with the Directive in all member states.

**Revenue Generation (Article 6(2)):** The Directive permits cultural heritage institutions to generate revenues from the use of orphan works, provided revenues are used exclusively to cover the costs of digitizing and making available the works.

### Digital Single Market Directive -- Out-of-Commerce Works (Articles 8-11)

The DSM Directive (2019/790) significantly expanded the EU framework for culturally valuable works that are difficult to license:

**Article 8 (Collective Licensing with Extended Effect):** Member states must provide that a collective management organization may conclude a non-exclusive license for non-commercial purposes with a cultural heritage institution for the reproduction and making available of out-of-commerce works, where the CMO is sufficiently representative of the rights holders for the relevant type of work.

**Article 9 (Cross-Border Uses):** Out-of-commerce works made available under an ECL license may be accessed in all member states, and member states shall provide for publicity measures to ensure rights holders are informed and can opt out.

**Article 10 (Fallback Exception):** Where no sufficiently representative CMO exists for a particular type of work, member states must provide a copyright exception permitting cultural heritage institutions to make out-of-commerce works available online for non-commercial purposes, subject to rights holder opt-out.

**Article 11 (Stakeholder Dialogue):** Member states must encourage stakeholder dialogue between rights holders, CMOs, and cultural heritage institutions to ensure the effective application of the out-of-commerce works regime.

### EUIPO Orphan Works Database

The EUIPO Orphan Works Database, established under Article 3(6) of the Directive, maintains a public register of orphan works identified by cultural heritage institutions across the EU. As of 2025, the database contains records for over 14,000 orphan works, with the largest contributions from institutions in the United Kingdom (pre-Brexit entries), Germany, France, and the Netherlands. The database facilitates mutual recognition and provides transparency regarding the orphan works status of specific works.

### ECHR Considerations

**Article 1 Protocol No. 1 (Property Rights):** The use of orphan works without explicit rights holder consent raises potential issues under the ECHR's protection of property rights. The Orphan Works Directive addresses this through the diligent search requirement and the rights holder's ability to put an end to orphan works status and claim fair compensation, mechanisms designed to achieve a proportionate balance between cultural access and property rights.

**Article 10 (Freedom of Expression):** The orphan works problem also engages Article 10 rights, as restrictions on access to cultural heritage material may impede the receipt and imparting of information. The ECHR's balancing jurisprudence (e.g., Ashby Donald v. France, Application No. 36769/08, 2013) provides the framework for assessing whether orphan works regimes achieve an appropriate balance between copyright protection and access to information.

## Comparative Analysis

### Legislative Solutions versus Judicial Solutions

The most striking comparative feature is the contrast between jurisdictions that have enacted legislative solutions (South Korea, Germany/EU) and the United States, which has relied on judicial solutions through fair use jurisprudence. The legislative approaches provide greater legal certainty for institutions and users, with defined search requirements, clear permitted uses, and established compensation mechanisms. The U.S. fair use approach, while flexible, creates persistent uncertainty that inhibits the full-scale digitization and dissemination of orphan works by risk-averse institutions.

### Diligent Search: Rigor versus Feasibility

All legislative solutions require a diligent search as a precondition for orphan works use, but the rigor and cost of diligent search requirements vary significantly. The EU Directive's detailed source-specific search requirements, while thorough, have been criticized as excessively burdensome for mass digitization projects. South Korea's 2020 streamlined procedure for cultural heritage institutions represents an attempt to balance search rigor with mass digitization feasibility. The DSM Directive's out-of-commerce works provisions (ECL and fallback exception) effectively bypass the individual diligent search requirement for works that are permanently out of commerce, providing a more scalable solution.

### Compensation Models

The jurisdictions differ in their approaches to compensating rights holders who emerge after orphan works use has commenced. South Korea requires upfront escrow deposits, creating a fund from which emerging rights holders can be compensated. The EU Orphan Works Directive requires "fair compensation" but does not mandate upfront payment. The U.S. orphan works proposals (never enacted) would have limited remedies to "reasonable compensation" for non-commercial uses and actual damages for commercial uses, with no injunctive relief against qualifying users. These differences reflect varying assessments of the appropriate balance between incentivizing diligent searches and facilitating legitimate use.

### Extended Collective Licensing: The Scandinavian Innovation

The ECL mechanism, originating in the Nordic countries (Denmark, Finland, Iceland, Norway, Sweden) and now adopted across the EU through the DSM Directive, represents the most innovative approach to the orphan works problem. By extending the effect of licenses negotiated by representative CMOs to cover works by non-members (including unlocatable rights holders), ECL provides a market-based solution that avoids the need for individual diligent searches while preserving rights holder opt-out. South Korea's statutory license system shares some features with ECL, though it operates through government authorization rather than collective management organization licensing.

### Mass Digitization: The Unresolved Challenge

Despite progress in all jurisdictions, mass digitization of cultural heritage collections remains a largely unresolved challenge. The scale of the problem -- tens of millions of works in library and archive collections whose rights holders cannot be located -- exceeds the capacity of even streamlined diligent search procedures. The EU's combination of orphan works designation, ECL for out-of-commerce works, and the fallback exception represents the most comprehensive attempt to address mass digitization, but implementation across member states has been uneven. The United States' reliance on fair use, while validated by Google Books and HathiTrust, provides insufficient certainty for many institutions.
