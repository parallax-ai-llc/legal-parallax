## Summary

Space law encompasses the body of international treaties, national legislation, and regulatory frameworks governing activities in outer space, including the exploration and use of celestial bodies, the operation of satellites and space stations, liability for space objects, commercial space activities, and the emerging fields of space resource utilization and space traffic management. South Korea, a rapidly emerging spacefaring nation, governs its space program through the Space Development Promotion Act of 2005 and the Space Damage Compensation Act of 2007, administered by the newly established Korea AeroSpace Administration (KASA), founded in May 2024 as the country's first dedicated national space agency. The United States maintains the most developed commercial space regulatory framework globally, anchored by the Commercial Space Launch Act (51 U.S.C. Chapter 509), the 2015 Commercial Space Launch Competitiveness Act (which grants U.S. citizens property rights over extracted space resources), and a 2025 executive order streamlining regulation of novel space activities under Article VI of the Outer Space Treaty. Germany, which lacks a comprehensive national space law but is developing the Weltraumgesetz (German Space Act, WRG) following key points published by the Federal Cabinet in September 2024, currently regulates space activities through sector-specific legislation and its obligations under EU law and the European Space Agency (ESA). At the international level, the Outer Space Treaty of 1967, with 118 states parties as of 2025, establishes the foundational principles of space law, including the prohibition of national appropriation, the peaceful use of outer space, and state responsibility for national space activities. Countries including Japan, India, the United Arab Emirates, Australia, Luxembourg, and Italy have enacted national space legislation reflecting the rapid commercialization and diversification of space activities.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's space law framework has evolved rapidly as the country has developed indigenous launch capabilities and expanded its commercial space sector.

| Statute | Purpose | Key Provisions |
|---------|---------|----------------|
| Space Development Promotion Act (우주개발진흥법, Act No. 7538, 2005) | Comprehensive framework for space development | National Space Committee; space development plans; registration of space objects; technology transfer controls |
| Space Damage Compensation Act (우주손해배상법, Act No. 8714, 2007) | Liability for damage caused by space objects | Strict liability of launch operators; mandatory liability insurance; government indemnity |
| Korea AeroSpace Administration Establishment Act (2024) | Creation of dedicated space agency | Establishment of KASA; transfer of MSIT space functions; oversight of all space policy |
| Satellite Navigation System Act (위성항법시스템법) | Regulation of satellite navigation infrastructure | Korean Positioning System development; signal integrity requirements |

**Space Development Promotion Act:** This foundational statute establishes the institutional framework for South Korea's space program. Key elements include:

- **National Space Committee:** Chaired by the Minister of Science and ICT (now transitioning to KASA oversight), the committee sets national space policy and approves long-term space development plans.
- **Space Object Registration:** South Korea maintains a national registry of space objects in accordance with the Registration Convention (1975), with the Ministry of Science and ICT (now KASA) designated as the national registration authority.
- **Technology Transfer Controls:** The Act includes provisions restricting the transfer of sensitive space technologies, aligned with the Missile Technology Control Regime (MTCR), which South Korea joined in 2001.

**Space Damage Compensation Act:** This Act implements South Korea's obligations under the Liability Convention (1972):

- **Strict Liability:** The launching state (or the operator acting on its behalf) bears absolute liability for damage caused by its space objects on the surface of the Earth or to aircraft in flight (mirroring Article II of the Liability Convention).
- **Fault-Based Liability:** For damage caused in outer space, liability requires proof of fault (mirroring Article III of the Liability Convention).
- **Mandatory Insurance:** Launch operators must maintain liability insurance or other financial guarantee in amounts prescribed by Presidential Decree before conducting any launch operation.
- **Government Indemnity:** The government may provide supplementary compensation beyond the operator's insurance limit for damage of exceptional magnitude.

### Key Principles

**KASA Establishment (May 2024):** The Korea AeroSpace Administration was established on 27 May 2024 as South Korea's first dedicated national space agency. KASA consolidates space-related policy and oversight functions previously distributed across MSIT, KARI (Korea Aerospace Research Institute), and other agencies. KASA oversees all aspects of space policy, including exploration, industry development, national security applications, and international cooperation.

**Nuri Launch Vehicle (KSLV-II):** South Korea achieved a significant milestone with the successful orbital launch of the Nuri rocket (KSLV-II) in June 2022, demonstrating indigenous heavy-lift launch capability. The three-stage, all-Korean-engine launch vehicle can deliver 1.5-ton payloads to Sun-synchronous orbit. The regulatory framework for commercial launches is being developed as KASA prepares to license private-sector operators to use Nuri-derived technology.

**4th Basic Plan for Space Development (2023-2042):** This long-term plan targets Korea becoming a top-five space nation, with goals including development of the KSLV-III next-generation launch vehicle, a Korean lunar lander by 2032, and Mars exploration by 2045.

### Notable Cases

**Arirang Satellite Series Liability Provisions:** The Arirang (KOMPSAT) Earth observation satellite series, operated by KARI (now under KASA), has been launched on foreign rockets (including Russian and Indian launch vehicles), raising cross-jurisdictional liability questions addressed through bilateral launch services agreements that allocate risk between the launching state and South Korea as the state of registry.

**Danuri Lunar Orbiter (2022):** South Korea's first lunar mission, launched on a SpaceX Falcon 9, was governed by a bilateral framework agreement between Korea and the United States, addressing launch licensing (FAA jurisdiction), orbital operations, and data sharing. The mission demonstrated the interplay between national licensing regimes when a state conducts activities using another state's launch services.

**Nuri Commercial Launch Services:** Following the successful Nuri launches, KASA is developing a regulatory framework for commercial launch services using Nuri-derived technology. Private companies including Innospace (which successfully launched its HANBIT-TLV suborbital vehicle from Brazil's Alcantara Launch Center in March 2024) and Perigee Aerospace are developing commercial launch vehicles that will require domestic licensing under the evolving regulatory framework. The transition from government-exclusive launches to a mixed government-commercial launch market represents a significant regulatory challenge for the young agency.

**Korea-Artemis Accords (2021):** South Korea became a signatory to the Artemis Accords in May 2021, committing to principles of transparency, interoperability, emergency assistance, registration of space objects, release of scientific data, preservation of space heritage, space resource utilization, and orbital debris mitigation. This commitment has implications for South Korea's future participation in lunar surface activities and the development of domestic legislation on space resource rights.

## United States

### Federal Framework

The United States has the most comprehensive and longest-established commercial space regulatory framework in the world, with multiple federal agencies sharing jurisdiction.

| Statute / Authority | Citation | Purpose |
|---------------------|----------|---------|
| Outer Space Treaty (ratified 1967) | Treaty Document | International obligations: Art. VI responsibility, Art. VII liability |
| National Aeronautics and Space Act | 51 U.S.C. Chapter 201 | Establishes NASA; civilian space activities |
| Commercial Space Launch Act | 51 U.S.C. Chapter 509 | FAA licensing of commercial launches and reentries |
| Communications Act of 1934 (as amended) | 47 U.S.C. | FCC licensing of commercial satellites and spectrum allocation |
| Land Remote Sensing Policy Act | 51 U.S.C. Chapter 601 | NOAA licensing of commercial remote sensing systems |
| Commercial Space Launch Competitiveness Act (2015) | Pub. L. 114-90 | Space resource rights (Title IV); liability provisions; ISS extension |
| National Space Council (Executive Order, 2017) | | Policy coordination across federal agencies |

**FAA Commercial Space Launch Licensing:** The Federal Aviation Administration's Office of Commercial Space Transportation (FAA/AST) licenses all commercial launches and reentries from U.S. territory or by U.S. citizens anywhere in the world, pursuant to 51 U.S.C. section 50904. Requirements include:

- **Launch License:** Demonstration of safety compliance, financial responsibility, and environmental review.
- **Maximum Probable Loss (MPL) Determination:** The FAA calculates the maximum probable loss from a launch failure, which determines the operator's required insurance amount, up to a statutory maximum of $500 million per launch (adjusted for inflation).
- **Government Indemnification:** Under the Commercial Space Launch Act, the U.S. government provides indemnification for third-party claims exceeding the operator's required insurance, up to $1.5 billion (adjusted for inflation), above which Congress must authorize additional payments.

**Space Resource Utilization (Title IV, SPACE Act of 2015):** Section 51303 of Title 51 provides that "[a] United States citizen engaged in commercial recovery of an asteroid resource or a space resource under this chapter shall be entitled to any asteroid resource or space resource obtained, including to possess, own, transport, use, and sell the asteroid resource or space resource obtained in accordance with applicable law." This was the world's first national legislation explicitly granting property rights over extracted space resources.

**2025 Executive Order on Commercial Space:** In August 2025, the White House issued an executive order directing the Secretary of Commerce to propose, within 150 days, a streamlined authorization process for "novel space activities" covered by Article VI of the Outer Space Treaty but not clearly governed by existing regulatory frameworks. The order aims to reduce regulatory burdens on commercial operators conducting in-space manufacturing, on-orbit servicing, debris removal, and other emerging activities.

### State Variations

Several U.S. states have enacted legislation to attract commercial space activities:

- **Florida** operates the Cape Canaveral Spaceport and has enacted spaceport liability and informed consent legislation.
- **Texas** hosts SpaceX's Starbase facility and has enacted state spaceport and spaceflight participant liability frameworks.
- **New Mexico** operates Spaceport America and enacted the Space Flight Informed Consent Act.
- **Virginia, Colorado, and Alabama** maintain significant space industry infrastructure and supporting state legislation.

### Notable Cases

***Nemitz v. United States* (No. CV-N030599-HDM, D. Nev. 2004):** Gregory Nemitz filed a claim asserting property rights over the asteroid 433 Eros and demanded parking fees from NASA. The court dismissed the claim, finding no recognized basis in law for private property claims to celestial bodies, predating the 2015 SPACE Act's resource rights provisions.

**FAA Commercial Crew Licensing (SpaceX, Boeing):** The development of commercial crew transportation to the ISS required novel regulatory frameworks addressing human spaceflight safety, passenger informed consent, and the interface between FAA launch licensing and NASA human rating standards.

**Orbital Debris Liability (2023-2025):** Increasing attention to space debris has led to FCC enforcement actions requiring post-mission disposal plans and the FAA's updated debris mitigation guidelines, raising questions about long-term liability for debris-generating events under the Liability Convention.

**FCC Dish Network Fine (2023):** In October 2023, the FCC issued its first-ever fine for a space debris violation, ordering Dish Network to pay $150,000 for failing to properly deorbit the EchoStar-7 satellite to the required disposal orbit. The satellite was moved to an orbit approximately 122 km below the disposal orbit specified in its license, creating potential long-term collision risks. This enforcement action signaled that U.S. regulatory agencies are prepared to impose financial penalties for non-compliance with debris mitigation requirements.

**Starlink Constellation Licensing:** SpaceX's deployment of the Starlink mega-constellation (over 6,000 satellites in orbit as of 2025) has presented unprecedented regulatory challenges, including FCC spectrum coordination, FAA launch licensing at an unprecedented cadence, NOAA concerns about astronomical observation impacts, and NASA coordination regarding collision avoidance in low Earth orbit. The Starlink experience has driven regulatory evolution in how the United States manages very large constellations.

**Cosmos 954 Precedent:** The 1978 reentry of the Soviet nuclear-powered Cosmos 954 satellite over Canadian territory resulted in the only successful claim under the Liability Convention, with the Soviet Union paying Canada $3 million in settlement. This case remains the foundational precedent for state-to-state liability claims for space object damage on Earth.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany currently lacks a comprehensive national space law but is in the process of developing the Weltraumgesetz (WRG). Space activities are regulated through a patchwork of existing legislation and international obligations.

| Legal Source | Relevance |
|-------------|-----------|
| Weltraumgesetz (WRG, in development) | Comprehensive space act; key points published September 2024 |
| Satellitendatensicherheitsgesetz (SatDSiG, 2007) | Security of satellite data, particularly high-resolution remote sensing |
| Telekommunikationsgesetz (TKG) | Telecommunications regulation including satellite services |
| Luftverkehrsgesetz (LuftVG) | Aviation law; interface with suborbital flight |
| Outer Space Treaty (ratified 1971) | International obligations: Art. VI state responsibility |
| Liability Convention (ratified 1975) | International liability for damage caused by space objects |
| Registration Convention (ratified 1979) | National registration of space objects |
| ESA Convention (founding member, 1975) | European Space Agency membership obligations |

**Weltraumgesetz Key Points (September 2024):** The Federal Cabinet published a paper setting out the guidelines for the future German Space Act:

- **Scope:** The WRG will apply to space activities by German persons and entities conducted from within or outside Germany, as well as activities launched from German territory, ships, or aircraft registered in Germany. It will not apply to federal government activities, ESA operations, EUMETSAT, NATO, EU, or ISS activities.
- **Authorization Requirement:** Space activities and operation of launch facilities will require government authorization. Modifications and transfers of operations will also require approval.
- **Operator Obligations:** Operators will be required to take measures against space debris, including post-mission disposal, and maintain financial security for potential damage.
- **Registry:** Germany will establish a national registry of space objects consistent with the Registration Convention.
- **Objectives:** The WRG aims to strengthen Germany's space industry, particularly benefiting start-ups and SMEs, while ensuring public safety, national security, and compliance with international obligations.

**Satellitendatensicherheitsgesetz (SatDSiG, 2007):** Germany's most significant existing space-specific legislation regulates the security of data collected by high-resolution Earth observation satellites. Operators of such satellites must obtain a license, and the distribution of high-resolution imagery is subject to government screening to protect national security interests.

### Key Principles

**ESA Membership:** Germany is a founding member of the European Space Agency and its largest financial contributor (approximately EUR 4 billion in the 2025-2029 period). ESA activities are governed by the ESA Convention and are exempt from the future WRG. Germany participates in major ESA programs including Ariane 6, Galileo, Copernicus, and the ExoMars mission.

**EU Space Programme:** As an EU Member State, Germany participates in the EU Space Programme Regulation (EU) 2021/696, which governs Galileo (satellite navigation), Copernicus (Earth observation), GOVSATCOM (governmental satellite communications), and the Space Situational Awareness (SSA) component.

**International Responsibility:** Under Article VI of the Outer Space Treaty, Germany bears international responsibility for national space activities, whether carried out by governmental agencies or non-governmental entities. The absence of a comprehensive national space law has created a regulatory gap that the WRG is intended to fill, ensuring that Germany can authorize and supervise the growing number of private German space companies (including Isar Aerospace, Rocket Factory Augsburg, and OHB SE).

### Notable Cases

**Isar Aerospace and Rocket Factory Augsburg Licensing:** Germany's emerging launch companies have highlighted the regulatory gap created by the absence of a national space law. Until the WRG is enacted, launch activities by German companies are conducted from foreign launch sites (such as Andoya Space in Norway) under bilateral arrangements, with Germany relying on the host state's licensing regime supplemented by German export control laws.

**DLR-Copernicus Data Policy:** The German Aerospace Center (DLR) operates Copernicus contributing missions and manages the distribution of Copernicus data in Germany, raising legal questions about data ownership, access rights, and the interface between the EU's free and open data policy and Germany's SatDSiG restrictions on high-resolution imagery.

**German Orbital Hub and In-Space Manufacturing:** Germany has been a leader in advocating for legal frameworks for in-space manufacturing and on-orbit servicing through DLR's participation in ESA programs and bilateral cooperation with NASA. The WRG key points specifically address the need for authorization procedures for novel space activities, reflecting Germany's recognition that traditional launch-and-operate regulatory models are insufficient for the emerging in-space economy.

**SatDSiG Enforcement Cases:** The Federal Office of Economics and Export Control (BAFA) has processed numerous applications under the SatDSiG for the distribution of high-resolution satellite imagery. The law requires that any entity distributing satellite data with a ground resolution below 2.5 meters must undergo a sensitivity check, with the government retaining the authority to restrict distribution of imagery that could compromise national security. This framework has been cited as a model for other countries developing satellite data regulation.

## ECHR / EU Framework

### Convention Articles

The ECHR does not directly address outer space activities. However, several Convention rights have potential relevance:

- **Article 2 (Right to Life):** States have positive obligations to protect life from risks associated with hazardous activities, potentially including launch operations, space debris reentry, and nuclear-powered spacecraft. The "Oneryildiz v. Turkey" doctrine on hazardous activities could extend to space launch operations near populated areas.
- **Article 8 (Right to Respect for Private and Family Life):** Satellite surveillance and Earth observation raise privacy concerns. The collection and distribution of high-resolution satellite imagery depicting identifiable individuals or private property may engage Article 8 protections.
- **Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (Protection of Property):** Regulatory restrictions on space activities (such as denial of launch licenses or data distribution restrictions) may constitute interference with property rights requiring justification under the proportionality test.
- **Article 10 (Freedom of Expression):** Restrictions on the dissemination of satellite data and imagery raise freedom of information concerns, particularly when governments classify or restrict commercially obtained data.

### Key Case Law

While no ECtHR case has directly addressed space law, several decisions establish relevant principles:

***Oneryildiz v. Turkey* (App. No. 48939/99, Grand Chamber, 2004):** The Grand Chamber held that states have positive obligations under Article 2 to protect life in the context of dangerous industrial activities, including requirements for risk assessment, public information, and emergency planning. This doctrine is directly applicable to launch operations and the potential risks of uncontrolled reentry of space objects.

***Weber and Saravia v. Germany* (App. No. 54934/00, 2006):** The Court addressed the lawfulness of strategic telecommunications surveillance, establishing principles applicable to satellite-based communications interception, including requirements for legal basis, necessity, and proportionality.

***Centrum for Rattvisa v. Sweden* (App. No. 35252/08, Grand Chamber, 2018):** The Grand Chamber addressed bulk signals intelligence collection, establishing minimum safeguards applicable to satellite communications interception that parallel principles relevant to space-based surveillance.

### Member State Obligations

**EU Space Programme Regulation (EU) 2021/696:** Establishes the legal framework for the EU's flagship space programs:

| Programme | Purpose | Legal Status |
|-----------|---------|-------------|
| **Galileo** | Global satellite navigation system | Operational; free access guaranteed by regulation |
| **Copernicus** | Earth observation and monitoring | Operational; free, full, and open data policy |
| **EGNOS** | European Geostationary Navigation Overlay Service | Operational; aviation safety certification |
| **GOVSATCOM** | Governmental satellite communications | Developing; secure communications for EU institutions and Member States |
| **SSA/STM** | Space Situational Awareness / Space Traffic Management | Developing; space surveillance and tracking |
| **IRIS2** | Resilient, interconnected, and secure satellite constellation | Approved 2023; secure connectivity infrastructure |

**Outer Space Treaty Obligations of EU Member States:** All 27 EU Member States are parties to the Outer Space Treaty. Article VI requires each state to authorize and continuously supervise the space activities of its non-governmental entities. The absence of national space legislation in several Member States (including Germany, pending the WRG) has been identified as a compliance gap. States with comprehensive national space laws include France (Loi relative aux operations spatiales, 2008), the United Kingdom (Space Industry Act, 2018), and the Netherlands (Space Activities Act, 2007).

**European Space Agency (ESA):** While not an EU institution, ESA cooperates closely with the EU under the Financial Framework Partnership Agreement (FFPA). ESA's 22 Member States (not all EU members) participate in optional programs based on geographic return principles. ESA liability is governed by the ESA Convention and bilateral agreements with launch service providers and cooperating states.

**Ariane 6 Launch Vehicle:** ESA's Ariane 6 launcher, developed primarily by ArianeGroup (a joint venture of Airbus and Safran), conducted its maiden flight in July 2024 from the Guiana Space Centre in Kourou, French Guiana. The Ariane 6 program is governed by the ESA Convention's decision-making framework, with participating states sharing development costs according to a geographic return formula. The launcher's operational license is issued by CNES (France's national space agency) under the French Loi relative aux operations spatiales, as launches occur from French overseas territory.

**EU Space Regulation and Sovereign Access:** The EU has increasingly emphasized "sovereign access to space" as a strategic priority, reflected in the 2023 Joint Communication on European Space Strategy for Security and Defence. This has led to discussions about EU-level launch capability requirements, potential EU launch service procurement preferences, and the relationship between EU institutional demand and the commercial viability of European launch services.

**Space Traffic Management (STM):** The EU is developing a comprehensive approach to space traffic management, including the Space Surveillance and Tracking (SST) Partnership established under Decision (EU) 2014/541. The EU SST Partnership, comprising national SST assets from France, Germany, Italy, Poland, Portugal, Romania, and Spain, provides conjunction warnings, reentry predictions, and fragmentation analysis. The development of an EU-wide STM regulatory framework is under active consideration as part of the EU Space Programme's evolution.

### Additional Jurisdictions

- **Japan** enacted the Act on Launching of Spacecraft (Space Activities Act) in 2016 and the Act on Ensuring Appropriate Handling of Satellite Remote Sensing Records in 2016. Japan's 2021 Act on the Promotion of Business Activities for the Exploration and Development of Space Resources establishes a licensing framework for space resource utilization.
- **India** enacted the Indian Space Activities Bill (draft, 2023) to regulate private space activities and established IN-SPACe (Indian National Space Promotion and Authorization Center) in 2020 to authorize and supervise non-governmental space activities.
- **Luxembourg** enacted the Law on the Exploration and Use of Space Resources (2017), becoming the first European country and the second nation globally to establish a legal framework for space resource utilization.
- **United Arab Emirates** enacted Federal Law No. 12 of 2019 on the Regulation of the Space Sector and the National Space Policy of 2020, establishing one of the most comprehensive space regulatory frameworks in the Middle East, including provisions for space resource utilization.
- **Australia** regulates space activities under the Space (Launches and Returns) Act 2018, administered by the Australian Space Agency (est. 2018), with a focus on commercial launch licensing from its advantageous equatorial proximity.
- **Italy** enacted its national space law (Legge Spazio) in 2025, introducing a national registry for space objects, operator licensing requirements, and provisions for space resource utilization under Article 4.
- **South Africa** enacted the Space Affairs Act (No. 84 of 1993), one of Africa's earliest national space laws, establishing a licensing framework for space activities conducted from South African territory.
- **Brazil** operates the Alcantara Launch Center, one of the most equatorially advantageous launch sites in the world, under the Brazilian Space Agency (AEB). The Brazil-U.S. Technology Safeguards Agreement (signed 2019, ratified 2020) enables commercial launches from Alcantara using U.S.-origin technology, requiring technology protection protocols consistent with MTCR obligations.

### Emerging Legal Issues

**Space Resource Utilization:** The legal framework for extracting and using resources from celestial bodies remains one of the most contested areas of space law. Article II of the Outer Space Treaty prohibits national appropriation of celestial bodies, but a growing number of states argue that resource extraction does not constitute "appropriation." The Artemis Accords (Section 10) and the national laws of the United States (2015), Luxembourg (2017), UAE (2019), and Japan (2021) support this interpretation. The Hague International Space Resources Governance Working Group published "Building Blocks" in 2019 proposing an international framework for space resource activities, though no binding multilateral instrument has been adopted.

**Active Debris Removal (ADR):** The legal framework for removing existing space debris raises novel questions about jurisdiction, liability, and property rights over defunct space objects. Under Article VIII of the Outer Space Treaty, the state of registry retains jurisdiction and control over its space objects, meaning that removing another state's debris without consent could constitute interference. The ESA-funded ClearSpace-1 mission (planned for 2026), intended to remove a Vega rocket adapter, is being developed with explicit authorization from the state of registry, establishing a precedent for consensual ADR operations.

**Planetary Defense:** The increasing focus on asteroid deflection capabilities, demonstrated by NASA's Double Asteroid Redirection Test (DART) mission in 2022, raises questions about the legal authority to deflect or destroy potentially hazardous asteroids, the allocation of liability if deflection causes unintended harm, and the governance framework for coordinating planetary defense operations among spacefaring nations.

### Artemis Accords and the Future of Space Governance

The Artemis Accords, signed in October 2020 by the United States, Australia, Canada, Italy, Japan, Luxembourg, the United Arab Emirates, and the United Kingdom, and now exceeding 50 signatories as of 2025, represent a significant development in the governance of international space cooperation. While not legally binding per se, the Accords establish operational norms for:

- **Transparency:** Signatories commit to publicly describing their space policies and plans.
- **Interoperability:** Space systems should use open international standards.
- **Emergency Assistance:** Consistent with the 1968 Rescue Agreement.
- **Registration:** Consistent with the 1975 Registration Convention.
- **Release of Scientific Data:** Commitment to open sharing of scientific data from space missions.
- **Preservation of Heritage:** Protection of historically significant sites on celestial bodies (e.g., the Apollo landing sites).
- **Space Resources:** Affirm that resource extraction does not constitute national appropriation.
- **Deconfliction:** Notification of activities that may cause harmful interference.
- **Orbital Debris Mitigation:** Consistent with IADC Guidelines and national standards.

The Accords represent a United States-led alternative to multilateral treaty negotiation through COPUOS, with some states (notably Russia and China) viewing them as an attempt to establish norms outside the established UN framework. China and Russia have proposed a separate International Lunar Research Station (ILRS) partnership, creating a potential bifurcation of lunar governance norms.
