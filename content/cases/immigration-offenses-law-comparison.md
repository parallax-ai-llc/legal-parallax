## Summary

Immigration offenses — encompassing illegal entry, unauthorized residence, visa overstay, document fraud, and human smuggling — occupy a unique position in criminal and administrative law, sitting at the intersection of national sovereignty, human rights, and international cooperation. The legal treatment of these offenses varies dramatically across jurisdictions, reflecting divergent philosophies on criminalization, proportionality, and the balance between enforcement and protection. South Korea addresses immigration violations primarily through the Immigration Control Act (출입국관리법), imposing criminal penalties for unauthorized entry and stay while operating periodic voluntary departure programs. The United States criminalizes improper entry under 8 U.S.C. Section 1325 and illegal reentry under 8 U.S.C. Section 1326, making immigration prosecutions the single largest category of federal criminal cases. Germany penalizes illegal entry and unauthorized residence under Sections 95-98 of the Residence Act (Aufenthaltsgesetz, AufenthG), while also imposing severe penalties for human smuggling (Einschleusen von Auslaendern). At the supranational level, the European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR) constrains member states' enforcement powers through Articles 3, 5, 8, and 13, and Protocol No. 4, while EU legislation — including Directive 2002/90/EC on facilitation of unauthorized entry, Framework Decision 2002/946/JHA, and the Return Directive 2008/115/EC — establishes harmonized minimum standards for returns, detention, and the criminalization of smuggling. The tension between sovereign border control and fundamental rights guarantees defines this area of law more than perhaps any other field of criminal justice.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea regulates immigration offenses through the Immigration Control Act (출입국관리법, revised most recently through amendments effective in 2025-2026). The Act establishes a comprehensive regime governing entry, stay, and departure of foreign nationals, with criminal penalties set out in Articles 93 through 99.

**Article 93-3 — Illegal Entry:** Any person who enters the Republic of Korea without undergoing the required entry inspection under Article 12 is subject to imprisonment with labor for up to five years or a fine not exceeding KRW 30,000,000 (approximately USD 22,000). This provision applies to both clandestine border crossings and entry through ports of entry without submitting to immigration inspection.

**Article 94 — Unauthorized Activities and Departure Violations:** Individuals who violate conditions attached to their entry permission, engage in activities outside the scope of their authorized status of stay without obtaining separate permission, violate residence or activity restrictions imposed by the Minister of Justice, or depart Korea without undergoing departure inspection face imprisonment with labor for up to three years or a fine not exceeding KRW 20,000,000 (approximately USD 15,000).

**Article 95 — Escape from Detention and Violation of Release Conditions:** Persons who flee while detained, temporarily detained, or while being escorted for detention or deportation, as well as those who violate conditions of release from detention, are subject to criminal penalties.

| Article | Offense | Maximum Imprisonment | Maximum Fine (KRW) |
|---------|---------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Art. 93-3 | Illegal entry | 5 years | 30,000,000 |
| Art. 94 | Unauthorized activities / departure without inspection | 3 years | 20,000,000 |
| Art. 95 | Escape from detention / violation of release | 3 years | 20,000,000 |

### Enforcement and Deportation

The Immigration Control Act establishes a three-tier enforcement framework, escalating from administrative measures to forced removal:

1. **Departure Recommendation (출국권고):** A non-compulsory measure applied to first-time or minor violators, particularly those with short-term unauthorized stays or passport-carrying violations. The foreigner is advised to leave voluntarily.

2. **Deportation Order (강제퇴거명령):** Issued when the foreigner does not comply with a departure recommendation or has committed more serious violations. This order carries fines and compulsory enforcement.

3. **Forced Eviction (강제퇴거):** The most severe measure, applied in cases of false identity document use, long-term illegal stay, or repeated violations. The person is physically removed from Korea regardless of their will.

**Entry Bans:** Re-entry restrictions following deportation are structured by duration of violation: overstay of less than one year typically results in a one-year entry ban; overstay of one to three years results in bans of two to five years; overstay exceeding three years or repeat offenses may result in bans of up to ten years or permanent exclusion.

### Detention and Constitutional Oversight

South Korea's immigration detention system came under significant constitutional scrutiny. In 2023, the Constitutional Court of Korea (헌법재판소) ruled that the indefinite detention of foreigners subject to deportation orders was unconstitutional, finding that the absence of a statutory time limit on immigration detention unduly restricted the physical freedom of detainees. Prior to this ruling, foreigners could be held indefinitely in immigration detention centers while deportation proceedings were pending or while removal could not be carried out.

Following the Constitutional Court's decision, the Ministry of Justice proposed amendments to the Immigration Control Act establishing a maximum detention period of 36 months (3 years), bringing the system closer to — though still significantly exceeding — the European standard of 18 months under the EU Return Directive.

### Voluntary Departure Program (2025-2026)

The Ministry of Justice announced a 90-day voluntary departure program running from 1 December 2025 to 28 February 2026, allowing foreign nationals in an irregular status to self-deport with exemptions from fines and delayed entry restrictions. However, individuals who entered Korea illegally, used forged documents, committed criminal offenses, failed to comply with previous departure orders, or became undocumented after 1 December 2025 were excluded from the program.

### Key Cases

**Constitutional Court Decision on Indefinite Detention (2023):** The court found that provisions of the Immigration Control Act permitting detention without a statutory maximum violated the Constitution's guarantee of personal liberty. The decision mandated legislative reform to impose a definite time limit on immigration detention, marking a watershed moment in the protection of non-citizens' rights under Korean constitutional law.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States criminalizes immigration offenses through a comprehensive statutory scheme within Title 8 (Aliens and Nationality) and Title 18 (Crimes and Criminal Procedure) of the United States Code. The Immigration and Nationality Act (INA), codified at 8 U.S.C. Chapter 12, provides the foundational framework, with key criminal provisions in Sections 1325, 1326, and 1324.

**8 U.S.C. Section 1325 — Improper Entry by Alien:** This statute criminalizes entry or attempted entry into the United States at any time or place other than as designated by immigration officers, evasion of examination or inspection by immigration officers, or entry by willfully false or misleading representation or willful concealment of a material fact.

| Offense | Classification | Penalty |
|---------|---------------|---------|
| First offense improper entry | Misdemeanor | Fine or up to 6 months imprisonment, or both |
| Subsequent offense | Misdemeanor | Fine or up to 2 years imprisonment, or both |
| Marriage fraud | Felony | Fine or up to 5 years imprisonment, or both |
| Immigration-related entrepreneurship fraud | Felony | Fine or up to 10 years imprisonment, or both |

Civil penalties also apply: aliens apprehended while entering at improper times or places are subject to civil fines of at least USD 50 to USD 250 for each entry or attempted entry.

**8 U.S.C. Section 1326 — Reentry of Removed Aliens:** This felony statute makes it a crime to unlawfully reenter, attempt to reenter, or be found in the United States after having been deported, ordered removed, or denied admission.

| Prior Record | Maximum Penalty |
|-------------|----------------|
| No aggravating prior | 2 years |
| Prior conviction for 3+ misdemeanors (drugs/crimes against person) or 1 felony (non-aggravated) | 10 years |
| Prior aggravated felony conviction | 20 years |

**8 U.S.C. Section 1324 — Bringing in and Harboring Aliens:** This provision criminalizes the smuggling, transporting, harboring, or shielding of unauthorized aliens, with penalties of up to 5 years for basic offenses, up to 10 years for offenses committed for commercial advantage or private financial gain, up to 20 years if the offense causes serious bodily injury, and life imprisonment or the death penalty if the offense results in death.

**18 U.S.C. Section 1546 — Fraud and Misuse of Visas, Permits, and Other Documents:** Criminalizes the knowing use, attempted use, possession, or creation of fraudulent immigration documents, with penalties of up to 10 years for basic offenses, up to 15 years if committed to facilitate drug trafficking, and up to 25 years if committed to facilitate terrorism.

### Scale of Prosecution

Immigration offenses constitute the single largest category of federal criminal prosecutions in the United States. By December 2018, violations of Sections 1325 and 1326 combined accounted for 65 percent of all federal criminal prosecutions. This unprecedented volume has driven the creation of expedited adjudication programs such as Operation Streamline, which processes large numbers of immigration defendants through group proceedings in border district courts.

### Landmark Cases

**Arizona v. United States, 567 U.S. 387 (2012):** The Supreme Court struck down three of four provisions of Arizona's SB 1070, a state law that sought to expand local law enforcement's role in immigration enforcement. Justice Kennedy's majority opinion held that the federal government possesses preemptive authority over immigration law and enforcement. The Court invalidated provisions requiring non-citizens to carry registration documents at all times, authorizing state officers to arrest individuals on suspicion of removable immigration status, and prohibiting unauthorized immigrants from seeking employment. The decision established that immigration law and its enforcement remain fundamentally a federal, not state, power.

**Padilla v. Kentucky, 559 U.S. 356 (2010):** The Supreme Court held that criminal defense attorneys have a constitutional obligation under the Sixth Amendment to advise noncitizen clients about the deportation consequences of a guilty plea. Where the immigration consequences of a conviction are clear, counsel must advise that deportation will result; where consequences are uncertain, counsel must advise that deportation may result. Failure to provide such advice constitutes ineffective assistance of counsel. This landmark ruling recognized deportation as a penalty intimately related to the criminal process rather than a mere collateral consequence.

**INS v. St. Cyr, 533 U.S. 289 (2001):** The Supreme Court held that the writ of habeas corpus under 28 U.S.C. Section 2241 remained available to challenge deportation orders, even after the Illegal Immigration Reform and Immigrant Responsibility Act of 1996 (IIRIRA) and the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA) restricted judicial review of removal orders.

### Civil vs. Criminal Enforcement

A distinctive feature of the U.S. system is the parallel operation of civil and criminal enforcement. Removal proceedings are civil and administrative, conducted by immigration judges within the Executive Office for Immigration Review (EOIR) under the Department of Justice. Criminal prosecution occurs separately in federal district courts. An individual may simultaneously face criminal charges for illegal entry (Section 1325) and civil removal proceedings — a dual-track approach that has drawn criticism from civil liberties organizations as imposing disproportionate consequences on migrants.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany criminalizes immigration offenses primarily through the Residence Act (Aufenthaltsgesetz, AufenthG), which consolidated and replaced the former Aliens Act (Auslaendergesetz) in 2005. The penal provisions are concentrated in Sections 95 through 98.

**Section 95 — Criminal Offenses (Straftaten):** This central provision defines two tiers of immigration offenses:

- **Section 95(1):** Punishable by up to one year's imprisonment or a fine. Covers unauthorized residence without a valid passport or residence title, providing false information about identity, age, or nationality, and failure to comply with reporting obligations.

- **Section 95(2):** Punishable by up to three years' imprisonment. Covers unauthorized entry (crossing the border without the necessary legal authorization per Section 14(1) AufenthG), repeated violations of Section 95(1) offenses, and entry using documents obtained through threats, bribery, collusion, or fraud.

**Section 96 — Smuggling of Foreigners (Einschleusen von Auslaendern):** This provision elevates assistance to an immigration offense into an independent offense. Following significant amendments in 2024, the basic offense under Section 96(1) now carries a custodial sentence from six months to ten years. Gang-related smuggling committed for gain on a continuing basis carries a prison sentence of one to ten years. Where smuggling results in the death of the smuggled person, the minimum sentence is three years' imprisonment.

**Section 97 — Smuggling Resulting in Death; Commercial and Gang-Related Smuggling:** Addresses the most serious smuggling offenses with enhanced penalties.

**Section 98 — Administrative Offenses (Ordnungswidrigkeiten):** Covers less serious violations punishable by fines rather than imprisonment, including failure to carry identification documents and minor reporting violations.

| Section | Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|---------|
| § 95(1) | Unauthorized residence, false identity information | Up to 1 year or fine |
| § 95(2) | Unauthorized entry, repeated violations, fraudulent documents | Up to 3 years |
| § 96(1) | Smuggling of foreigners (basic) | 6 months – 10 years |
| § 96 | Gang-related smuggling for gain | 1 – 10 years |
| § 96/97 | Smuggling resulting in death | Minimum 3 years |
| § 98 | Administrative offenses | Fine |

### Definition of Illegal Entry

Under Section 14(1) of the AufenthG, illegal entry (unerlaubte Einreise) occurs when a foreign national crosses the German border without the required legal authorization. This includes crossing without an appropriate residence permit or valid travel documents, as well as using documents obtained through threats, bribery, collusion, or through the use of incomplete or false information.

### Employer Sanctions

Employers who employ foreign nationals without the required work authorization face both administrative fines and criminal prosecution. The sanctions regime is designed to target the demand side of irregular migration by removing the economic incentive for hiring unauthorized workers.

### Recent Policy Developments (2025-2026)

Germany's coalition agreement of 2025 introduced significant policy changes affecting immigration enforcement:

- Rejection of asylum seekers at borders in coordination with European neighboring countries
- Continuation of temporary internal border controls within the Schengen area
- Further restrictions on family reunification policies
- Enhanced cooperation with the United Kingdom on counter-smuggling operations, including landmark legislation passed in December 2024 increasing maximum penalties for criminal smuggling gangs to 10 years' imprisonment

### Landmark Cases

**BGH, 3 StR 173/25 (24 June 2025):** The German Federal Court of Justice (Bundesgerichtshof) clarified the legal conditions for criminal liability in smuggling cases resulting in death under Sections 96-97 AufenthG, establishing important boundaries on what prosecutors must prove regarding the causal connection between the smuggling activity and the victim's death.

**Asylum Seekers' Right to Dignity (BVerfG, 2012):** The Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) held in its landmark decision of 18 July 2012 (1 BvL 10/10) that the benefits provided to asylum seekers under the Asylum Seekers' Benefits Act were unconstitutionally low, as human dignity under Article 1(1) of the Basic Law is not subject to immigration status and guarantees a minimum subsistence level for all persons on German territory.

## ECHR & EU

### European Convention on Human Rights

The ECHR does not directly criminalize or prohibit immigration offenses, but it establishes fundamental rights constraints on how member states of the Council of Europe may enforce immigration laws. Several articles are directly relevant:

**Article 3 — Prohibition of Torture and Inhuman or Degrading Treatment:** This absolute right prohibits member states from deporting or removing a person to a country where there are substantial grounds for believing they would face torture or inhuman treatment (the principle of non-refoulement). Article 3 cannot be derogated from under any circumstances, including national security concerns.

**Article 5 — Right to Liberty and Security:** Permits deprivation of liberty in immigration contexts only under Article 5(1)(f), which allows the lawful arrest or detention of a person to prevent unauthorized entry or of a person against whom action is being taken with a view to deportation or extradition. Detention must be lawful, not arbitrary, proportionate, and subject to prompt judicial review.

**Article 8 — Right to Respect for Private and Family Life:** Removal of a foreign national may violate Article 8 where it would disproportionately interfere with established family or private life ties in the host country. Deportation of long-term residents, parents of minor children, or persons with deep social integration requires a proportionality assessment balancing state interests against individual rights.

**Article 13 — Right to an Effective Remedy:** Guarantees access to an effective domestic remedy for arguable claims under the Convention, including in immigration and asylum contexts.

**Article 4, Protocol No. 4 — Prohibition of Collective Expulsion of Aliens:** Prohibits the collective expulsion of aliens and requires that each individual's case be individually assessed before removal.

### Landmark ECHR Cases

**Saadi v. United Kingdom [GC], No. 13229/03 (29 January 2008):** The Grand Chamber addressed the lawfulness of detaining asylum seekers under Article 5(1)(f). Shayan Baram Saadi, an Iraqi Kurd, was detained for seven days at Oakington Reception Centre upon arrival at London Heathrow in 2000. The Court found no violation of Article 5(1) by 11 votes to 6, holding that until a state has authorized entry, any entry remains unauthorized, and detention to prevent unauthorized entry is permissible even where the person has presented themselves to immigration authorities. However, the Court unanimously found a violation of Article 5(2) because Saadi was not informed of the reasons for his detention for 76 hours — well beyond the "promptly" requirement. This case established that good-faith, non-arbitrary immigration detention is compatible with Article 5, while affirming the right to prompt notification of reasons.

**Hirsi Jamaa and Others v. Italy [GC], No. 27765/09 (23 February 2012):** This landmark Grand Chamber judgment was the first case in which the ECHR addressed interception at sea and "push-back" operations. In May 2009, Italian military vessels intercepted boats carrying Somali and Eritrean nationals and transferred them to Tripoli, Libya, without individual assessment. The Court found violations of Article 3 (exposing applicants to ill-treatment in Libya and risk of repatriation to Somalia and Eritrea), Article 4 of Protocol No. 4 (collective expulsion), and Article 13 (denial of effective remedy). The judgment established that the ECHR's protections apply extraterritorially when a state exercises jurisdiction over individuals, even on the high seas, and that push-back operations without individual screening are incompatible with the Convention.

**Amuur v. France, No. 19776/92 (25 June 1996):** The Court held that holding asylum seekers in the international zone of Paris-Orly airport constituted a deprivation of liberty under Article 5, requiring lawful authorization and judicial oversight. This case established that confinement in transit zones is not merely a restriction on freedom of movement but an actual deprivation of liberty.

**Chahal v. United Kingdom [GC], No. 22414/93 (15 November 1996):** The Grand Chamber held that the prohibition on refoulement under Article 3 is absolute and applies regardless of the perceived dangerousness of the individual or national security concerns. The deportation of a Sikh separatist to India was found to violate Article 3 due to the risk of torture.

### EU Legislative Framework

**Directive 2002/90/EC — Defining Facilitation of Unauthorized Entry:** Adopted 28 November 2002, this directive requires member states to adopt sanctions against any person who intentionally assists a non-EU national to enter or transit through a member state's territory in breach of its immigration laws, or who, for financial gain, assists a non-EU national to reside within a member state in breach of its residence laws. Member states may decide not to impose sanctions where the facilitation was aimed at providing humanitarian assistance — a provision that has generated significant debate regarding the criminalization of NGO rescue operations in the Mediterranean.

**Framework Decision 2002/946/JHA — Strengthening the Penal Framework:** Complements Directive 2002/90/EC by requiring that facilitation offenses be punishable by effective, proportionate, and dissuasive criminal penalties which may entail extradition. Aggravated offenses committed for financial gain must be punishable by custodial sentences with a maximum of not less than eight years where committed as part of organized crime or while endangering lives.

**Directive 2008/115/EC — Return Directive:** Establishes common standards for the return of irregularly staying third-country nationals. Key provisions include:

| Provision | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| Return decision | Must be issued to any illegally staying third-country national |
| Voluntary departure period | 7-30 days as a general rule |
| Entry ban | Up to 5 years; accompanies removal orders |
| Detention | Only as last resort; maximum 6 months, extendable to 18 months |
| Removal of minors | Best interests of the child must be a primary consideration |
| Judicial review | Right to appeal return decisions before a judicial authority |

**Directive 2009/52/EC — Employer Sanctions:** Establishes minimum standards for sanctions and measures against employers of illegally staying third-country nationals, targeting demand-side drivers of irregular migration.

### Humanitarian Exception Debate

The humanitarian exception in Directive 2002/90/EC — allowing member states to refrain from imposing sanctions where facilitation is aimed at humanitarian assistance — has become one of the most contested provisions in European immigration law. Several member states have prosecuted humanitarian workers and NGO personnel for search-and-rescue operations in the Mediterranean, raising fundamental questions about the boundary between smuggling and humanitarian aid. The European Commission issued guidance in September 2020 clarifying that humanitarian assistance mandated by law should not be criminalized, but implementation remains inconsistent across member states.

## Comparative Analysis

### Criminalization Approaches

The four jurisdictions exhibit fundamentally different approaches to the criminalization of immigration violations:

**South Korea** treats unauthorized entry as a serious criminal offense punishable by up to five years' imprisonment, placing it among the more punitive systems globally. However, the enforcement culture emphasizes graduated responses, beginning with departure recommendations before escalating to forced removal.

**The United States** maintains a unique dual-track system of civil immigration enforcement and criminal prosecution. While first-offense improper entry is a misdemeanor with modest maximum penalties (6 months), the sheer scale of prosecution — 65 percent of all federal criminal cases — creates a system of mass criminalization unparalleled in any other Western democracy. The felony reentry statute (Section 1326) with penalties up to 20 years is among the harshest in the world.

**Germany** positions immigration offenses at the lower end of its criminal sentencing framework, with basic unauthorized residence punishable by up to one year. However, the 2024 amendments to the smuggling provisions (Section 96) dramatically increased penalties to a maximum of 10 years, reflecting a European-wide trend toward treating smuggling as a serious organized crime.

**The ECHR/EU framework** does not directly criminalize immigration offenses but establishes both floors (minimum penalties for smuggling) and ceilings (maximum detention periods, procedural safeguards) that constrain member states' enforcement choices. The Return Directive's 18-month maximum detention limit stands in stark contrast to the absence of any statutory maximum in the U.S. system and the 36-month limit recently adopted in South Korea.

### Detention Regimes

| Jurisdiction | Maximum Detention | Judicial Review | Constitutional/Human Rights Constraint |
|-------------|-------------------|-----------------|----------------------------------------|
| South Korea | 36 months (post-2025 reform) | Constitutional Court review | 2023 Constitutional Court ruling on indefinite detention |
| United States | No statutory maximum | Habeas corpus; Zadvydas v. Davis 6-month presumption | Due Process Clause (5th/14th Amendments) |
| Germany | 6-18 months (per Return Directive) | Judicial authorization required | Basic Law Art. 1 (dignity), Art. 2 (liberty) |
| ECHR/EU | 18 months maximum | Art. 5(4) ECHR; Return Directive Art. 15 | Art. 5 ECHR; absolute prohibition on arbitrary detention |

### Treatment of Smuggling vs. Smuggled Persons

A critical distinction across all jurisdictions is the differential treatment of smugglers and smuggled persons. All four systems impose significantly harsher penalties on smugglers (facilitators) than on the migrants themselves. Germany's 2024 reforms exemplify this trend, raising smuggling penalties to 6 months-10 years while keeping basic unauthorized residence at a maximum of one year. The EU framework explicitly requires aggravated penalties for organized smuggling (minimum 8-year maximum sentence) while the Return Directive treats irregular migrants primarily through administrative removal rather than criminal punishment.

### Rights Protections

The ECHR framework provides the most developed rights-based constraints on immigration enforcement, with absolute prohibitions on refoulement (Article 3), collective expulsion (Protocol 4, Article 4), and mandatory procedural safeguards. The U.S. system provides constitutional protections through due process guarantees and habeas corpus, reinforced by the Supreme Court's Padilla decision requiring effective counsel. South Korea's Constitutional Court has emerged as an important guardian of non-citizen rights, as demonstrated by the 2023 indefinite detention ruling. Germany's Basic Law, particularly the dignity guarantee of Article 1(1), provides a constitutional floor that the Federal Constitutional Court has applied without distinction based on immigration status.
