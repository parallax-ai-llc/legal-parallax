## Summary

Non-fungible tokens (NFTs) present a unique regulatory challenge due to their hybrid nature as technological artifacts that can simultaneously function as digital collectibles, investment vehicles, intellectual property carriers, proof-of-ownership instruments, and access tokens. The legal classification of NFTs -- whether as securities, commodities, property, or a sui generis category -- varies dramatically across jurisdictions and determines the applicable regulatory regime. The European Union addressed NFTs indirectly through the Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA, Regulation (EU) 2023/1114), which generally excludes "unique and non-fungible" crypto-assets from its scope while acknowledging that fractional NFTs or large series of NFTs may fall within its purview. The United States applies a case-by-case analysis under the Howey test to determine whether specific NFTs constitute securities, with the SEC bringing enforcement actions against NFT projects while the CFTC has asserted that certain NFTs may be commodities. South Korea regulates NFTs primarily through the Virtual Asset User Protection Act (가상자산이용자보호법, 2024), which initially excluded most NFTs but has been subject to expanding interpretation. Germany, as an EU member state, applies MiCA while its Federal Financial Supervisory Authority (BaFin) has developed national guidance on NFT classification.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's approach to NFT regulation reflects a careful balancing of its vibrant digital asset market with consumer protection concerns:

- **Virtual Asset User Protection Act (가상자산이용자보호법, effective 19 July 2024):** The Act defines "virtual assets" (가상자산) as electronically stored tokens with economic value that can be traded or transferred. Article 2(3) initially excluded NFTs from the definition, based on the rationale that non-fungible tokens are not primarily used as payment or investment instruments. However, the Financial Services Commission (FSC) Enforcement Decree (effective January 2025) established criteria for determining when NFTs fall within the Act's scope:
  - NFTs issued in large series (generally over 100 identical or substantially similar tokens) may be treated as fungible virtual assets.
  - NFTs that are fractionalized into divisible units are treated as virtual assets.
  - NFTs marketed primarily as investment products or used as payment instruments fall within the Act's scope.
- **Financial Investment Services and Capital Markets Act (자본시장과 금융투자업에 관한 법률, FSCMA):** Where an NFT meets the definition of a "security" under FSCMA Article 4 -- specifically, an "investment contract security" (투자계약증권) where investors contribute assets with the expectation of profit derived from the efforts of others -- it is subject to full securities regulation, including registration, disclosure, and investor protection requirements.
- **Copyright Act (저작권법):** NFT purchases do not automatically transfer copyright ownership. Article 45 (right of reproduction) and Article 46 (right of distribution) remain with the copyright holder unless explicitly transferred. The Korea Copyright Commission has published guidance clarifying that NFT minting constitutes a reproduction under copyright law, requiring the authorization of the copyright holder.
- **Act on the Consumer Protection in Electronic Commerce (전자상거래법):** Applies to NFT marketplace transactions, imposing obligations on platform operators regarding information disclosure, cooling-off rights (Article 17, 7-day withdrawal period for digital content), and dispute resolution.

### Key Principles

The Financial Services Commission has adopted a "substance over form" approach to NFT classification, examining the economic reality of each NFT project rather than its technical designation. Key classification factors include: the degree of fungibility, the primary purpose (collectible vs. investment vs. utility), the marketing approach (whether profits are promised or implied), and the existence of a secondary market with speculative trading.

The Korea Blockchain Association (한국블록체인협회) published self-regulatory guidelines for NFT platforms in 2024, establishing voluntary standards for creator verification, IP rights disclosure, secondary market royalty enforcement, and consumer protection. Compliance with these guidelines is not legally mandatory but is treated as a mitigating factor in regulatory assessments.

### Notable Cases

- **Dunamu (Upbit) NFT Platform Investigation (2024):** The FSC investigated Upbit's NFT marketplace for potentially offering NFTs that functioned as de facto securities, particularly "profile picture" (PFP) collections marketed with promises of future utility, staking rewards, and token airdrops that created profit expectations. The investigation resulted in enhanced disclosure requirements for NFT platforms.
- **KakaoTalk Klay NFT Tax Case (2024):** The National Tax Service assessed capital gains tax on profits from the resale of NFTs traded on the Klip NFT platform, establishing administrative precedent that NFT trading profits are subject to "other income" taxation under the Income Tax Act Article 21, with a tax rate of 22% (including local income tax) on gains exceeding KRW 2.5 million.
- **Metakongz Copyright Infringement Suit (2023):** The Seoul Central District Court ruled that the unauthorized minting of NFTs using copyrighted artwork constituted infringement of the reproduction right and the right of making available to the public under the Copyright Act, awarding damages of KRW 85 million.

## United States

### Federal Framework

The United States lacks NFT-specific federal legislation, resulting in a complex regulatory landscape where multiple agencies assert overlapping jurisdiction:

- **SEC Enforcement -- Securities Classification:** The SEC has applied the Howey test (SEC v. W.J. Howey Co., 328 U.S. 293 (1946)) to determine whether specific NFTs constitute "investment contracts" and thus securities. The four-prong test examines whether there is: (1) an investment of money, (2) in a common enterprise, (3) with a reasonable expectation of profits, (4) derived from the efforts of others. The SEC has brought enforcement actions against NFT projects where promotional activities created expectations of profit.
  - **SEC v. Impact Theory, LLC (2023):** The SEC's first NFT-specific enforcement action, where the agency alleged that Impact Theory's "Founder's Key" NFTs were unregistered securities because purchasers were led to expect profits from the company's efforts to build a media empire. Impact Theory settled for approximately USD 6.1 million without admitting or denying the findings.
  - **SEC v. Stoner Cats 2, LLC (2023):** The SEC found that NFTs associated with an animated series produced by Mila Kunis's production company constituted securities because purchasers expected profit from secondary market trading enabled by the project's promotional efforts. Settlement: USD 1 million.
- **CFTC Jurisdiction -- Commodity Classification:** The Commodity Futures Trading Commission has asserted that certain NFTs may be "commodities" under the Commodity Exchange Act (7 U.S.C. § 1a(9)). In CFTC v. Ooki DAO (2022), the CFTC established that digital assets can be commodities, with implications for NFTs used in derivative transactions or leveraged trading.
- **IRS Tax Treatment:** IRS Notice 2023-27 and Revenue Ruling 2023-14 established that NFTs are treated as "digital assets" and constitute property for federal tax purposes. The tax treatment depends on whether the NFT is a collectible (subject to higher capital gains rates of up to 28% under IRC § 408(m)) or a standard capital asset (subject to long-term capital gains rates of 0%, 15%, or 20%). The IRS has proposed a "look-through" analysis: if the NFT represents a right to an underlying collectible (artwork, antiques, gems), it is taxed as a collectible.
- **Copyright -- License vs. Ownership:** The purchase of an NFT typically conveys only a license to display the associated digital content, not copyright ownership, unless the smart contract or terms of sale explicitly transfer copyright. This principle was reinforced in Hermes International v. Rothschild (S.D.N.Y. 2023), where the court analyzed the intersection of NFTs and trademark rights.

### State Variations

- **Utah Digital Innovation Act (2023):** Created a regulatory sandbox for blockchain and NFT innovations, exempting qualifying projects from state securities registration requirements for a limited period.
- **Wyoming Digital Asset Law (W.S. § 34-29-101, 2019-2023):** Wyoming's pioneering digital asset legislation recognizes digital assets as property and establishes a classification framework distinguishing digital consumer assets, digital securities, and virtual currencies, with NFTs potentially falling into any category depending on their characteristics.
- **New York BitLicense (23 NYCRR Part 200):** The New York Department of Financial Services has maintained that NFT platforms facilitating trading may require a BitLicense, though enforcement has been inconsistent.

### Notable Cases

- **Hermes International v. Rothschild (S.D.N.Y. 2023):** A jury found Mason Rothschild liable for trademark infringement for selling "MetaBirkins" NFTs depicting digital fur-covered handbags resembling Hermes Birkin bags. The court rejected the defendant's First Amendment defense, holding that NFTs sold for profit are not purely expressive works and that the Rogers v. Grimaldi artistic relevance test was satisfied by the jury's finding of consumer confusion. Damages: USD 133,000.
- **Yuga Labs v. Ryder Ripps (C.D. Cal. 2023):** The court granted summary judgment to Bored Ape Yacht Club creator Yuga Labs, finding that Ripps's creation of copycat "RR/BAYC" NFTs constituted trademark infringement and false endorsement under the Lanham Act (15 U.S.C. § 1125(a)).
- **Dapper Labs Litigation (S.D.N.Y. 2023):** In Friel v. Dapper Labs, Inc., the court denied Dapper Labs' motion to dismiss claims that NBA Top Shot Moments NFTs were unregistered securities, finding that plaintiffs plausibly alleged the Howey test elements, particularly because Dapper Labs controlled the Flow blockchain and secondary marketplace.
- **OpenSea Insider Trading Case (United States v. Chastain, S.D.N.Y. 2023):** A former OpenSea employee was convicted of wire fraud and money laundering for trading on confidential information about which NFTs would be featured on OpenSea's homepage, establishing that insider trading principles can apply to NFT markets even absent a specific securities designation.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany applies the MiCA framework alongside national financial regulatory provisions:

- **MiCA (Regulation (EU) 2023/1114):** MiCA Recital 10 states that crypto-assets that are "unique and not fungible with other crypto-assets" are generally excluded from the Regulation's scope. However, Article 4(2) provides that fractional parts of unique NFTs or NFTs issued as part of a "large series or collection" should not be considered unique and non-fungible and may fall within MiCA's scope. The European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) and the European Banking Authority (EBA) are tasked with developing regulatory technical standards to further define the boundary.
- **German Banking Act (KWG) § 1(11) Sentence 2 No. 10:** Prior to MiCA's full application, BaFin regulated crypto-assets (Kryptowerte) as a category of financial instrument under the KWG, which was amended in 2020 to include crypto-assets. BaFin's administrative practice has been to assess NFTs individually: those with investment characteristics may be classified as securities (Wertpapiere) or investment products (Vermögensanlagen), while purely collectible NFTs fall outside financial regulation.
- **Securities Trading Act (WpHG):** Where BaFin classifies an NFT as a security, the full WpHG regime applies, including prospectus requirements (under the EU Prospectus Regulation (EU) 2017/1129), market abuse prohibitions, and disclosure obligations.
- **German Copyright Act (Urheberrechtsgesetz, UrhG):** Under UrhG § 15, copyright comprises the exclusive right to exploit a work. The minting of an NFT incorporating copyrighted content constitutes a reproduction (§ 16) and, when offered publicly, a making available to the public (§ 19a). NFT purchases transfer only the rights expressly granted in the transaction; the Zweckübertragungslehre (purpose transfer doctrine, § 31(5)) dictates that only rights necessary for the contractual purpose are deemed transferred.
- **Income Tax Act (EStG) § 23:** Gains from the sale of NFTs held for less than one year are taxable as speculation income (Spekulationsgewinne). The Federal Ministry of Finance (BMF) published guidance in 2024 confirming that NFT disposals are subject to § 23 with a tax-free threshold of EUR 600 per calendar year. NFTs held for more than one year can generally be sold tax-free (for private holders).
- **VAT Act (UStG):** The VAT treatment of NFT transactions depends on their classification. The supply of a digital collectible NFT is treated as a supply of services subject to standard VAT (19%), while NFTs representing access rights or goods may receive different treatment.

### Key Principles

BaFin has adopted a "functional equivalence" approach, assessing whether an NFT functions economically like a traditional financial product regardless of its technological form. In its 2024 guidance paper on NFTs, BaFin identified four categories:

1. **Pure collectible NFTs:** Not financial instruments; subject to general consumer protection and copyright law.
2. **Investment NFTs:** Where the primary purpose is capital appreciation and returns are linked to an enterprise's efforts, classified as securities or investment products.
3. **Fractionalized NFTs:** Treated as crypto-assets under MiCA/KWG due to fungibility of fractional units.
4. **Utility NFTs:** Granting access to services or content; may be regulated under consumer protection law but generally not financial regulation.

### Notable Cases

- **BaFin NFT Securities Classification (2023):** BaFin determined that a German-based NFT project offering tokenized fractional ownership of luxury watches constituted the offering of securities (specifically, tokenized investment products under VermAnlG), and required the issuers to publish a securities information sheet (Wertpapier-Informationsblatt). Failure to comply resulted in a cease-and-desist order.
- **LG Berlin Virtual Art Ownership Dispute (2024):** The Landgericht Berlin ruled on a dispute between an NFT purchaser and a digital artist, holding that the NFT purchase agreement, governed by BGB contract law, conveyed a non-exclusive license to display the associated digital artwork but did not transfer copyright ownership (UrhG § 29(1) -- copyright is not transferable under German law; only exploitation rights can be licensed).
- **Cologne Tax Court NFT Mining Decision (2024):** The Finanzgericht Köln ruled that income from NFT minting activities constituted commercial income (Einkünfte aus Gewerbebetrieb) under EStG § 15, subject to both income tax and trade tax, rather than other income under § 22.

## ECHR / EU Framework

### Convention Articles

The European Convention on Human Rights provides a framework of principles applicable to NFT regulation:

- **Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 -- Protection of Property:** The most directly relevant Convention provision for NFT disputes. The ECtHR's expansive interpretation of "possessions" encompasses any asset with economic value, including contractual claims and legitimate expectations. NFTs with demonstrated market value could constitute "possessions" under the Convention, meaning that regulatory confiscation, platform seizure, or retroactive classification changes could engage Protocol 1 protections. The Court's three-rule test (peaceful enjoyment, deprivation of possessions, and control of use) would apply to government actions affecting NFT ownership.
- **Article 10 -- Freedom of Expression:** NFTs that embody artistic expression (digital art, generative art, literary works) are protected by Article 10. Regulatory restrictions on NFT creation, sale, or display must satisfy the Article 10(2) test: prescribed by law, pursuing a legitimate aim, and necessary in a democratic society.
- **Article 6 -- Right to a Fair Trial:** Where NFT classification disputes result in quasi-criminal penalties (such as significant administrative fines), Article 6's fair trial guarantees apply, including the right to be heard, the presumption of innocence, and access to judicial review.

### EU Legislative Framework

- **MiCA (Regulation (EU) 2023/1114):** The primary EU framework for crypto-asset regulation. MiCA entered into full application on 30 December 2024 for crypto-asset service providers (CASPs). Key provisions affecting NFTs:
  - **Recital 10 and Article 2(3):** Exclude unique and non-fungible crypto-assets from MiCA's scope.
  - **Article 4(2):** Provides that fractional NFTs and large-series NFTs should not be considered unique and non-fungible.
  - **ESMA Consultation on NFT Classification (2024):** ESMA published a consultation paper seeking input on technical criteria for distinguishing genuine NFTs from crypto-assets marketed as NFTs that function as fungible tokens. Proposed criteria include: number of tokens in a collection, degree of interchangeability, marketing emphasis on investment returns, and existence of active secondary markets.
- **MiFID II (Directive 2014/65/EU):** NFTs that qualify as "financial instruments" under MiFID II Article 4(1)(15) -- including transferable securities, money-market instruments, or derivatives -- fall under the full MiFID II regime, requiring investment firm authorization, best execution, suitability assessments, and investor protection.
- **Anti-Money Laundering Regulation (AMLR, (EU) 2024/1624):** The new EU AML framework, applicable from 2026, extends anti-money laundering obligations to crypto-asset service providers, including NFT platforms where transactions exceed EUR 10,000.
- **Consumer Rights Directive (2011/83/EU):** NFT purchases by consumers may constitute "distance contracts" for "digital content" under the Directive, triggering pre-contractual information requirements, a 14-day right of withdrawal (unless performance has begun with the consumer's express consent and acknowledgment of the loss of withdrawal rights), and conformity guarantees.

### Key Principles and Guidance

The EU's approach to NFT regulation reflects several core principles:

1. **Technology Neutrality:** The same regulatory obligations apply regardless of whether an asset is tokenized as an NFT or exists in traditional form.
2. **Substance Over Form:** Regulatory classification depends on the economic function of the NFT, not its technical characteristics.
3. **Proportionality:** Regulation should be proportionate to the risks posed, with lighter requirements for low-risk collectible NFTs and full financial regulation for investment-grade NFTs.
4. **Consumer Protection:** The EU's consumer protection acquis applies to NFT transactions, ensuring that purchasers have access to information, withdrawal rights, and dispute resolution.

## Key Regulatory Challenges

### The Fungibility Spectrum

A core challenge across all jurisdictions is that NFTs exist on a spectrum of fungibility rather than falling neatly into "fungible" or "non-fungible" categories. A one-of-one digital artwork is clearly non-fungible, but a collection of 10,000 algorithmically generated profile pictures with similar traits and interchangeable market pricing may be functionally fungible. The EU's MiCA attempts to address this through the "large series" concept, while the SEC's case-by-case approach under the Howey test examines economic reality rather than technical designation. South Korea's FSC Enforcement Decree establishes a quantitative threshold (100+ similar tokens), while Germany's BaFin applies functional equivalence analysis. None of these approaches has achieved a stable, universally applicable definition.

### Royalty Enforcement and Creator Rights

NFT creators initially relied on marketplace-enforced royalties (typically 5-10% of secondary sales), encoded in smart contract metadata. However, the emergence of royalty-bypassing marketplaces and tools has undermined this model. No jurisdiction currently mandates on-chain royalty enforcement. South Korea's Copyright Act and Germany's droit de suite provisions (UrhG § 26, implementing Directive 2001/84/EC on resale right) provide partial legal bases for creator compensation on secondary sales, but enforcement against pseudonymous blockchain transactions remains practically challenging.

### Environmental Concerns and Regulatory Response

The environmental impact of blockchain networks supporting NFTs has prompted regulatory attention. The EU's proposed sustainability disclosure requirements under the revised Energy Efficiency Directive (2023/1791) may require NFT platforms to disclose the energy consumption and carbon footprint of their blockchain infrastructure. South Korea's Environmental Impact Assessment Act does not currently apply to digital infrastructure, though legislative proposals have been introduced.
