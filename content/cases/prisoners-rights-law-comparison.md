## Summary

Prisoners' rights -- the fundamental legal protections and entitlements that incarcerated persons retain despite their confinement, encompassing rights to humane treatment, healthcare, communication, religious exercise, access to courts, and protection from violence -- reflect the foundational principle that imprisonment, while restricting liberty, does not strip individuals of their inherent human dignity or the entirety of their constitutional and human rights. South Korea's prisoners' rights framework is governed by the Act on the Execution of Criminal Sentences and Treatment of Inmates (형의 집행 및 수용자의 처우에 관한 법률), which underwent major reform in 2007 replacing the colonial-era Prison Act (행형법, originally enacted under Japanese colonial rule in 1950), establishing rights to correspondence, visitation, medical care, religious exercise, and petition, with oversight provided by the National Human Rights Commission of Korea (국가인권위원회) and the Constitutional Court, which has issued landmark decisions including 2001HeonMa546 (2004) recognizing inmates' constitutional right to dignity and 2013HeonMa142 (2016) finding unconstitutional the blanket censorship of inmate correspondence. The United States has developed prisoners' rights primarily through constitutional litigation under the Eighth Amendment (cruel and unusual punishment), the First Amendment (religion, expression), the Fourteenth Amendment (due process, equal protection), and the Religious Land Use and Institutionalized Persons Act (RLUIPA, 42 U.S.C. Section 2000cc), with the Supreme Court establishing in cases such as Turner v. Safley, 482 U.S. 78 (1987) that prison regulations restricting inmates' constitutional rights must be reasonably related to legitimate penological interests -- while the Prison Litigation Reform Act of 1995 (PLRA) imposed significant procedural barriers on prisoner lawsuits. Germany's prisoner rights framework is among the most protective in the world, following the Federal Constitutional Court's landmark decisions in BVerfGE 33, 1 (1972) establishing that prisoners retain all fundamental rights not necessarily restricted by the purpose of imprisonment, and BVerfGE 40, 276 (1975) requiring that restrictions be imposed by statute (not merely administrative regulation), leading to the enactment of the Federal Prison Act (Strafvollzugsgesetz) in 1976 (federalized to Länder in 2006) with the explicit statutory goal of enabling prisoners to lead socially responsible lives without committing criminal offenses (resocialization goal, Resozialisierungsziel). The European framework is shaped by the European Prison Rules (revised 2020), the CPT's detention standards, the ECHR's extensive jurisprudence under Article 3 (prohibition of inhuman treatment, generating the most prison-related violation findings of any Convention provision), and EU secondary legislation including Directive 2012/29/EU on victims' rights and Framework Decision 2008/909/JHA on the transfer of prisoners.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's prisoner rights framework underwent fundamental transformation with the 2007 enactment of the Act on the Execution of Criminal Sentences and Treatment of Inmates (형의 집행 및 수용자의 처우에 관한 법률), which replaced the colonial-era Prison Act (행형법) and established a modern rights-based approach to corrections.

### Constitutional Protections

The Constitutional Court has established that inmates retain constitutional rights subject to proportionate limitations:

**Decision 2001HeonMa546 (2004):** The Court held that inmates possess the constitutional right to human dignity under Article 10 and that restrictions on inmates' rights must satisfy the proportionality test under Article 37(2) of the Constitution. The decision established the framework for evaluating the constitutionality of prison regulations.

**Decision 2013HeonMa142 (2016):** The Court found unconstitutional the blanket censorship of inmates' correspondence, holding that while content-based screening for security purposes may be justified, systematic reading of all correspondence without specific security justification violates the right to privacy and freedom of correspondence under Article 17 and Article 18 of the Constitution.

### Statutory Rights

The Act provides the following rights:

| Right | Provision |
|-------|----------|
| Medical care | Art. 37-40: equivalent healthcare to community standards; psychiatric care for mentally ill inmates |
| Correspondence | Art. 43-44: right to send and receive correspondence; limited censorship for security |
| Visitation | Art. 41-42: regular family visits; video visits introduced 2020 |
| Religious exercise | Art. 45: right to religious services, counseling, dietary accommodations |
| Petition | Art. 117: right to petition the warden, Ministry of Justice, or NHRC |
| Education | Art. 63-64: access to educational programs and vocational training |
| Work | Art. 65-73: right to work; compensation for prison labor |
| Outdoor exercise | Art. 33: daily outdoor exercise (minimum 1 hour) |

### National Human Rights Commission Oversight

The NHRC (국가인권위원회) exercises oversight through:
- Regular inspections of correctional facilities
- Investigation of individual complaints by inmates
- Recommendations to the Ministry of Justice on systemic issues
- Publication of reports on detention conditions

Since 2007, the NHRC has investigated over 15,000 inmate complaints and issued numerous recommendations addressing overcrowding, healthcare quality, use of restraints, solitary confinement, and protection of vulnerable populations.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has developed prisoners' rights primarily through constitutional litigation, creating an extensive body of case law that both recognizes and limits inmates' rights.

### Eighth Amendment -- Conditions of Confinement

**Estelle v. Gamble, 429 U.S. 97 (1976):** The Supreme Court held that deliberate indifference to a prisoner's serious medical needs constitutes cruel and unusual punishment, establishing the right to healthcare in prison.

**Farmer v. Brennan, 511 U.S. 825 (1994):** The Court held that prison officials violate the Eighth Amendment when they are deliberately indifferent to a substantial risk of serious harm to an inmate, including the risk of violence from other inmates.

**Brown v. Plata, 563 U.S. 493 (2011):** The Court upheld a three-judge panel's order requiring California to reduce its prison population to 137.5% of design capacity, finding that extreme overcrowding resulted in constitutionally inadequate healthcare that violated the Eighth Amendment.

### First Amendment -- Religion and Expression

**Turner v. Safley, 482 U.S. 78 (1987):** The Court established the standard of review for constitutional claims by prisoners: a prison regulation is valid if it is reasonably related to legitimate penological interests. The four Turner factors are: (1) rational connection to a legitimate interest, (2) availability of alternative means of exercising the right, (3) impact on guards, other inmates, and prison resources, and (4) absence of ready alternatives to the regulation.

**Holt v. Hobbs, 574 U.S. 352 (2015):** The Court held that an Arkansas prison policy prohibiting beards substantially burdened a Muslim inmate's religious exercise under RLUIPA, applying strict scrutiny (compelling interest + least restrictive means) rather than the deferential Turner standard.

### Prison Litigation Reform Act (PLRA, 1995)

The PLRA imposed significant barriers to prisoner litigation:
- **Exhaustion requirement:** Prisoners must exhaust all available administrative remedies before filing suit (42 U.S.C. Section 1997e(a))
- **Physical injury requirement:** No federal civil action for mental or emotional injury without a prior showing of physical injury (42 U.S.C. Section 1997e(e))
- **Three strikes rule:** Prisoners who have had three or more actions dismissed as frivolous, malicious, or failing to state a claim are barred from proceeding in forma pauperis
- **Filing fee provisions:** Prisoners must pay the full filing fee over time

### Voting Rights

U.S. prisoner disenfranchisement varies dramatically by state:
- Two states (Maine and Vermont) never disenfranchise
- 14 states and DC restore rights upon release from prison
- 16 states restore rights after completion of sentence (including parole/probation)
- Several states impose additional waiting periods or require executive clemency

As of 2025, an estimated 4.6 million Americans are disenfranchised due to felony convictions.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's prisoner rights framework is widely regarded as among the most protective in the world, grounded in the Federal Constitutional Court's recognition that prisoners retain all fundamental rights not necessarily restricted by imprisonment.

### Constitutional Foundation

**BVerfGE 33, 1 (1972) -- Prisoner Rights Decision:** The Court established the foundational principle that inmates retain all fundamental rights except those necessarily restricted by the purpose of imprisonment. Restrictions must be imposed by statute (not merely administrative regulation) and must satisfy the proportionality principle.

**BVerfGE 40, 276 (1975) -- Statutory Reservation:** The Court held that restrictions on prisoners' fundamental rights require a formal statutory basis (Gesetzesvorbehalt), not merely internal prison regulations. This decision prompted the enactment of the Federal Prison Act (Strafvollzugsgesetz) in 1976.

**BVerfGE 116, 69 (2006) -- Resocialization Goal:** The Court reaffirmed that the state has an affirmative obligation to pursue the goal of resocialization and that prison conditions must be structured to enable inmates to lead law-abiding lives upon release.

### Resocialization Goal (Resozialisierungsziel)

The statutory goal of imprisonment in Germany is resocialization -- enabling prisoners to lead socially responsible lives without committing criminal offenses after release. This goal shapes all aspects of prison administration:
- Treatment programs and therapeutic interventions are mandatory offerings
- Vocational training and employment preparation are prioritized
- Family contact is actively facilitated
- Prison leave (Hafturlaub) and open prison (offener Vollzug) are standard elements of sentence execution

### Rights and Conditions

| Right | Standard |
|-------|---------|
| Cell space | Minimum 5.5-6 sq.m single occupancy (varies by Land) |
| Healthcare | Equivalence of care principle: prison healthcare must match community standards |
| Work | Right to work; compensation at regulated rates |
| Education | Right to education and vocational training |
| Visitation | Minimum 1 hour per month; most facilities exceed minimum significantly |
| Correspondence | Right to correspondence; censorship only for specific security reasons |
| Religious exercise | Full right to religious services and pastoral care |
| Voting | Retained in most cases; loss only by specific court order (Section 45 StGB) |
| Leave | Regular leave possible; open prison as standard progression |
| Complaints | Right to judicial review of prison decisions (Section 109 StVollzG) |

### Judicial Review

Section 109 of the Federal Prison Act (now replicated in Länder acts) provides inmates with the right to challenge any prison decision before the Strafvollstreckungskammer (sentence execution chamber) of the Landgericht. This judicial review mechanism is significantly more accessible than the U.S. system, with no equivalent to the PLRA's procedural barriers.

### Incarceration Rate

Germany's incarceration rate (approximately 64 per 100,000 in 2024) is among the lowest in Europe and approximately one-tenth of the U.S. rate (approximately 650 per 100,000), reflecting fundamentally different approaches to the use of imprisonment.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Article 3 -- Conditions of Detention

The ECHR's Article 3 jurisprudence on prison conditions is among its most extensive:

**Muršić v. Croatia [GC], Application No. 7334/13, Judgment of October 20, 2016:** The Grand Chamber established that a minimum personal space of 3 square meters of floor surface per detainee is a relevant standard for Article 3 assessment. Where personal space falls below 3 sq.m, a strong presumption of a violation arises, which can only be rebutted by demonstrating that the reduction was short, occasional, and minor, accompanied by sufficient freedom of movement outside the cell and adequate activities.

**Ananyev and Others v. Russia, Applications Nos. 42525/07, 60800/08, Judgment of January 10, 2012:** A pilot judgment finding systemic violations of Article 3 due to prison overcrowding in Russia, requiring structural reforms and the creation of effective domestic remedies.

**Vinter and Others v. United Kingdom [GC], Applications Nos. 66069/09 et al., Judgment of July 9, 2013:** Life sentences without the possibility of review violate Article 3, establishing the principle that every prisoner must have hope of release and the possibility of demonstrating rehabilitation.

### European Prison Rules (2020 Revision)

The Committee of Ministers revised the European Prison Rules (Recommendation CM/Rec(2006)2, revised 2020) establishing comprehensive standards:
- Rule 18: Accommodation must respect human dignity; minimum space standards
- Rule 22: Nutritional standards with provision for religious and medical dietary requirements
- Rule 24: Right to healthcare equivalent to community standards
- Rule 39: Freedom of thought, conscience, and religion
- Rule 43: Access to education
- Rule 49: Right to vote unless legally deprived by court order

### CPT Standards

The European Committee for the Prevention of Torture conducts regular inspections of all places of detention across Council of Europe member states and publishes detailed reports and standards addressing:
- Minimum cell size (at least 6 sq.m for single occupancy recommended)
- Daily outdoor exercise (minimum 1 hour)
- Access to healthcare
- Protection against inter-prisoner violence
- Staff training requirements
- Special protections for women, juveniles, and mentally ill prisoners

### EU Framework Decision 2008/909/JHA

This framework decision enables the transfer of sentenced prisoners between EU member states to serve their sentences in their member state of nationality or habitual residence, facilitating resocialization by allowing prisoners to serve sentences closer to their families and communities.

## Comparative Analysis

### Goal of Imprisonment

The most fundamental divergence concerns the stated purpose of imprisonment. Germany's explicit resocialization goal shapes all aspects of prison administration and conditions. South Korea's 2007 reform moved toward rehabilitation but retains a stronger emphasis on correction. The United States has no unified federal statement of purpose, and retributive and incapacitative goals predominate in practice despite some state-level rehabilitation initiatives.

### Rights Retention

Germany applies the most protective standard: prisoners retain all fundamental rights not necessarily restricted by imprisonment. The ECHR applies absolute protection under Article 3 (no derogation possible) combined with proportionality analysis under other provisions. South Korea applies constitutional proportionality review. The U.S. Turner standard is the most deferential to prison administrators, requiring only a "reasonable relationship" to legitimate penological interests.

### Healthcare

Germany and the ECHR/CPT require healthcare equivalent to community standards (the equivalence of care principle). The U.S. standard (deliberate indifference to serious medical needs) is significantly lower, requiring a showing that prison officials subjectively knew of and disregarded a substantial risk of serious harm. South Korea provides a statutory right to healthcare with NHRC oversight.

### Voting Rights

The ECHR held in Hirst v. United Kingdom (No. 2) (2005) that blanket prisoner disenfranchisement violates Protocol 1, Article 3. Germany retains voting rights for most prisoners. South Korea suspends voting rights during imprisonment. The United States has the most restrictive approach, with millions of citizens disenfranchised.
