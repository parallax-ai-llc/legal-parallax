## Summary

Climate change legislation across the Global South reveals fundamentally divergent legal philosophies shaped by colonial legacies, economic development priorities, and distinct constitutional traditions. China's socialist legal system treats climate action as a matter of centralized state planning, operationalized through the 14th Five-Year Plan (2021-2025) and the "dual carbon" goals (碳达峰碳中和) announced by President Xi Jinping at the UN General Assembly in September 2020, targeting carbon peaking before 2030 and carbon neutrality before 2060. India's hybrid common law system channels climate policy through executive action and judicial innovation, with the National Action Plan on Climate Change (NAPCC, 2008) and landmark judicial recognition of the right to a clean environment under Article 21 of the Constitution. In Africa, South Africa's Carbon Tax Act (No. 15 of 2019) represents the continent's first carbon pricing mechanism, while Nigeria's Climate Change Act 2021 establishes a legally binding net-zero target by 2060. Latin American neo-constitutionalism has produced the world's most rights-centered climate frameworks, including Brazil's National Policy on Climate Change (Lei 12.187/2009), Colombia's constitutional recognition of the Amazon as a subject of rights (STC 4360-2018), and Mexico's General Law on Climate Change (LGCC, 2012). Southeast Asia's adat and religious law traditions intersect with modern environmental statutes, exemplified by the Philippines' People's Survival Fund (Republic Act No. 10174, 2012) and Indonesia's Presidential Regulation No. 98/2021 on carbon economic values.

## China (中国)

### Legal Framework

China's climate governance operates through the socialist legal system's characteristic blend of Communist Party directives, State Council administrative regulations, and ministerial rules, which together carry legal force despite not always taking the form of legislation passed by the National People's Congress (NPC). The foundational legal architecture includes:

- **Environmental Protection Law (环境保护法):** Comprehensively revised in 2014 (effective 1 January 2015), this "basic law" of environmental governance introduced consecutive daily penalties for violations (Art. 59), public interest litigation standing for registered environmental NGOs (Art. 58), and mandatory environmental impact assessment. It replaced the 1989 version that had been widely regarded as ineffective.
- **Dual Carbon "1+N" Policy System (碳达峰碳中和"1+N"政策体系):** Anchored by the CPC Central Committee and State Council's "Opinions on Completely, Accurately, and Comprehensively Implementing the New Development Concept to Achieve Carbon Peaking and Carbon Neutrality" (October 2021) and the State Council's "Action Plan for Carbon Dioxide Peaking Before 2030" (October 2021). The "1" refers to these two apex documents; the "N" refers to sector-specific implementation plans covering energy, industry, transport, buildings, agriculture, and carbon sinks.
- **National Carbon Emissions Trading Market:** Launched 16 July 2021 under the "Interim Regulations on the Management of Carbon Emissions Trading" (State Council Order No. 775, effective 1 May 2024), superseding earlier MEE ministerial rules. The ETS initially covers the power generation sector (approximately 2,162 key emitting units representing over 5 billion tonnes of CO2), making it the world's largest carbon market by coverage volume.
- **Renewable Energy Law (可再生能源法):** Enacted 2005, amended 2009, establishing feed-in tariff obligations, grid connection guarantees, and a renewable energy development fund.

### Key Principles

China's climate legal framework reflects the concept of "ecological civilization" (生态文明), elevated to constitutional status through the 2018 amendment to the PRC Constitution (Preamble and Article 26). This paradigm positions environmental governance as inseparable from the CPC's governance legitimacy, embedding ecological targets into the cadre evaluation system (干部考核) through the 2015 "Measures for Evaluating Ecological Civilization Construction Targets" (生态文明建设目标评价考核办法).

The principle of "common but differentiated responsibilities" (CBDR) is central to China's international climate posture, codified domestically through repeated State Council white papers. China insists on its developing country status under the UNFCCC despite being the world's largest absolute emitter, arguing that per capita emissions and historical cumulative emissions are the appropriate metrics.

The "dual control" (双控) system has evolved from controlling both energy consumption intensity and total energy consumption to controlling carbon emission intensity and total carbon emissions, as signaled by the 2024 State Council "Opinions on Accelerating the Transition from Dual Control of Energy Consumption to Dual Control of Carbon Emissions."

### Notable Cases and Enforcement

- **State Grid Corporation Carbon Market Compliance (2021-2023):** The first compliance cycle (2019-2020 emissions) achieved 99.5% compliance among covered entities, with provincial ecology bureaus imposing fines on non-compliant entities under the interim administrative measures.
- **Kunming Biodiversity COP15 (2022):** China hosted the second phase of COP15, resulting in the Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework, reinforcing China's self-positioning as a global environmental governance leader.
- **Provincial Climate Litigation:** The Lüliang Intermediate People's Court in Shanxi Province ordered a chemical company to pay RMB 21.27 million in ecological compensation for excessive carbon emissions and environmental damage (2022), representing one of China's first judicial decisions explicitly linking carbon emissions to liability.
- **Central Environmental Inspections (中央生态环境保护督察):** Since 2016, the CPC Central Committee and State Council have dispatched inspection teams to all provinces, resulting in over 280,000 enforcement actions, 18,199 officials held accountable, and RMB 2.89 billion in fines during the first two rounds (2016-2022).

## India (भारत)

### Legal Framework

India's climate legal architecture reflects its common law heritage overlaid with constitutional environmental rights and directive principles, creating a system where judicial activism has often driven climate governance more effectively than legislative action:

- **National Action Plan on Climate Change (NAPCC, 2008):** Launched by the Prime Minister's Council on Climate Change under Dr. Manmohan Singh, the NAPCC established eight national missions: National Solar Mission, National Mission for Enhanced Energy Efficiency, National Mission on Sustainable Habitat, National Water Mission, National Mission for Sustaining the Himalayan Ecosystem, National Mission for a Green India, National Mission for Sustainable Agriculture, and National Mission on Strategic Knowledge for Climate Change.
- **Energy Conservation (Amendment) Act, 2022:** Amended the Energy Conservation Act 2001 to introduce a domestic carbon market framework, enabling the Bureau of Energy Efficiency (BEE) to establish a carbon credit trading scheme (CCTS) under the Indian Carbon Market. The Carbon Credit Trading Scheme 2023 was notified on 28 June 2023 under Section 14AA.
- **Perform, Achieve, Trade (PAT) Scheme:** Operating since 2012 under the National Mission for Enhanced Energy Efficiency, PAT is an energy efficiency certificate trading mechanism covering 13 industrial sectors with approximately 1,003 designated consumers. PAT Cycle I (2012-2015) achieved energy savings of 8.67 million tonnes of oil equivalent (Mtoe) against a target of 6.686 Mtoe.
- **Environment (Protection) Act, 1986 (EPA):** The umbrella legislation passed following the Bhopal gas disaster, providing the Central Government with broad rule-making authority under Sections 3 and 6. Climate-relevant rules issued under the EPA include the Environment Impact Assessment Notification 2006 (S.O. 1533(E)), Coastal Regulation Zone Notification 2019, and Wetlands (Conservation and Management) Rules 2017.

### Key Principles

The Indian judiciary has been central to climate governance through expansive interpretation of Article 21 (right to life and personal liberty) and Article 48A (directive principle requiring the State to protect and improve the environment). The Supreme Court in M.C. Mehta v. Union of India (1987) 1 SCC 395 first established the right to a pollution-free environment as part of the right to life. This line was extended in Subhash Kumar v. State of Bihar (1991) 1 SCC 598.

The "polluter pays" and "precautionary" principles were incorporated into Indian environmental jurisprudence through Vellore Citizens Welfare Forum v. Union of India (1996) 5 SCC 647, where Justice Kuldip Singh held these principles to be part of domestic law under Article 21 and Section 3(3) of the EPA.

The public trust doctrine, imported from American jurisprudence, was adopted in M.C. Mehta v. Kamal Nath (1997) 1 SCC 388, establishing that natural resources are held in trust by the state for public benefit.

### Notable Cases

- **Ridhima Pandey v. Union of India (NGT, 2017):** A nine-year-old petitioner challenged India's failure to take adequate climate action. The NGT dismissed the petition but acknowledged the importance of climate obligations, directing attention to the NAPCC's implementation.
- **M.K. Ranjitsinh v. Union of India (2024) SCC OnLine SC 1228:** The Supreme Court, in a case concerning the Great Indian Bustard, recognized the right against the adverse effects of climate change as a fundamental right under Articles 14 and 21, marking a landmark integration of climate rights into constitutional jurisprudence.
- **Council for Social Justice and Awareness Trust v. Union of India (NGT, 2023):** The NGT directed the Central Government to formulate a comprehensive framework for addressing air pollution and climate change in the Indo-Gangetic Plain, linking air quality governance to climate obligations.
- **Indian Council for Enviro-Legal Action v. Union of India (1996) 3 SCC 212:** The "Bichhri case" established that hazardous industries causing environmental damage are absolutely liable regardless of fault, applying the principle from M.C. Mehta v. Union of India (Oleum Gas Leak case, 1987).

## Africa (Nigeria and South Africa)

### South Africa

#### Legal Framework

South Africa possesses the most developed climate legal framework on the African continent, grounded in Section 24 of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996, which guarantees the right to an environment that is not harmful to health or well-being and the right to have the environment protected through reasonable legislative and other measures:

- **Carbon Tax Act (No. 15 of 2019):** Effective 1 June 2019, South Africa became the first African country and one of the first emerging economies to implement a carbon tax. The tax initially applied at ZAR 134 per tonne of CO2e (Phase 1, 2019-2025), increasing to ZAR 190 per tonne by 2024. Substantial tax-free allowances of 60-95% were provided during Phase 1, resulting in an effective rate of approximately ZAR 6-48 per tonne. Phase 2 (2026-2030) is expected to reduce allowances progressively. The tax covers Scope 1 emissions from fuel combustion, industrial processes, and fugitive emissions above a threshold of 0.1 MtCO2e per facility.
- **Climate Change Act (Bill B-9 of 2022):** After years in development, the Climate Change Bill was approved by Parliament in 2024. It provides for a national climate change response framework, sectoral emission targets (SETs), carbon budgets for major emitters, and a national adaptation strategy. It establishes the Presidential Climate Commission (PCC) as an advisory body.
- **National Environmental Management Act (NEMA, No. 107 of 1998):** The framework environmental legislation implements Section 24 of the Constitution and establishes principles of sustainable development, the polluter pays principle, and environmental impact assessment requirements.
- **Renewable Energy Independent Power Producer Procurement Programme (REIPPPP):** While not legislation per se, this procurement framework launched in 2011 under the Electricity Regulation Act (No. 4 of 2006) has been South Africa's primary mechanism for renewable energy deployment, allocating 6,422 MW across seven bid windows by 2024.

#### Notable Cases

- **Earthlife Africa Johannesburg v. Minister of Environmental Affairs (2017) ZAGPPHC 58:** The Thabametsi coal power station case, in which the Gauteng Division of the High Court held that climate change considerations must be assessed as part of the environmental impact assessment process under NEMA. Judge Sardiwalla ruled that the failure to consider climate impacts rendered the environmental authorization reviewable and set it aside.
- **Sustaining the Wild Coast NPC v. Minister of Mineral Resources (2022) ZAECMKHC 14:** The Makhanda High Court interdicted Shell's seismic survey off the Wild Coast, finding failures in the consultation process with affected communities and inadequate consideration of environmental impacts, invoking both customary rights and constitutional environmental protections.
- **Groundwork Trust v. Minister of Environmental Affairs (2017):** Environmental organizations challenged the Highveld Priority Area air quality management plan, leading to a precedent-setting order requiring the Minister to enforce compliance with ambient air quality standards.

### Nigeria

#### Legal Framework

Nigeria's climate legal framework reflects the tensions between its federal structure, oil-dependent economy, and the non-justiciable status of environmental provisions in Chapter II of the 1999 Constitution:

- **Climate Change Act 2021:** Signed by President Buhari on 17 November 2021, this Act established the National Council on Climate Change (NCCC) as a corporate body chaired by the President. Section 1(2)(a) commits Nigeria to achieving net-zero emissions between 2050 and 2070. The Act mandates the preparation of a National Climate Change Action Plan, carbon budgets, and sectoral performance standards.
- **Section 20 of the 1999 Constitution (CFRN):** Provides that "the State shall protect and improve the environment and safeguard the water, air and land, forest and wild life of Nigeria." However, as a Directive Principle of State Policy under Chapter II, it is non-justiciable under Section 6(6)(c), severely limiting its utility as a basis for environmental rights claims.
- **National Environmental Standards and Regulations Enforcement Agency (NESREA) Act 2007:** Establishes NESREA as the primary environmental regulatory body, replacing the Federal Environmental Protection Agency (FEPA). However, Section 7(g) explicitly excludes the oil and gas sector from NESREA's mandate, creating a critical regulatory gap.
- **Petroleum Industry Act 2021 (PIA):** While primarily an oil sector reform law, the PIA includes environmental provisions under Chapter 3, including environmental management plans, decommissioning obligations, and a host community development trust funded at 3% of operating expenditure.

#### Notable Cases

- **Gbemre v. Shell Petroleum Development Company (2005) FHC/B/CS/53/05:** Justice C.V. Nwokorie of the Federal High Court, Benin, held that gas flaring in the Niger Delta violated the right to life and dignity (Sections 33 and 34 of the CFRN) and the right to a clean environment under Article 24 of the African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights (domesticated via the African Charter Act, Cap A9 LFN 2004). This decision pioneered the justiciability of environmental rights through the African Charter.
- **Jonah Gbemre v. SPDC (enforcement):** Despite the landmark ruling, enforcement has been minimal. Gas flaring continues in the Niger Delta, with Nigeria remaining among the top seven gas-flaring countries globally (World Bank Global Gas Flaring Tracker, 2024).
- **SERAP v. Federal Republic of Nigeria (ECOWAS Court, ECW/CCJ/JUD/18/12):** The ECOWAS Community Court of Justice held Nigeria liable for failing to prevent environmental degradation in the Niger Delta, finding violations of the right to a clean environment and the right to health under the African Charter.

## Latin America (Brazil, Mexico, and Colombia)

### Legal Framework

Latin American climate governance is characterized by "neo-constitutionalism" -- a legal tradition embedding expansive environmental rights and ecological duties in constitutional texts, often reinforced by progressive judicial interpretation:

- **Brazil -- National Policy on Climate Change (PNMC, Lei 12.187/2009):** Establishes Brazil's voluntary commitment to reduce greenhouse gas emissions by 36.1-38.9% from projected 2020 levels. The policy operates through sectoral plans covering deforestation (PPCDAm), industry, energy, agriculture (ABC Plan), and transport. The Brazilian Forest Code (Lei 12.651/2012) mandates Legal Reserves (20-80% of rural properties in the Amazon) and Areas of Permanent Preservation (APPs) along waterways and slopes.
- **Mexico -- General Law on Climate Change (Ley General de Cambio Climático, LGCC, 2012):** Mexico was the second country globally (after the UK) to enact comprehensive climate legislation. The LGCC establishes a National Climate Change System (SINACC), the Inter-ministerial Commission on Climate Change (CICC), the National Institute of Ecology and Climate Change (INECC), and binding emissions reduction targets. The 2018 amendment strengthened adaptation provisions and introduced a pilot emissions trading system (SCE), linked to California's cap-and-trade system.
- **Colombia -- Law 1931 of 2018:** Establishes the National System for Climate Change (SISCLIMA), the National Climate Change Council, and Integral Plans for Climate Change Management (PIGCCS). Colombia's 2020 NDC committed to a 51% emissions reduction by 2030.
- **Colombia -- Constitutional Court Judgment STC 4360-2018:** In a case brought by 25 youth plaintiffs, the Supreme Court of Justice declared the Colombian Amazon a "subject of rights" (sujeto de derechos) and ordered the government to formulate an intergenerational pact for the life of the Amazon. This decision drew on the rights of nature doctrine developing across Latin America.

### Key Principles

The Latin American climate legal tradition is distinguished by several features absent from or less developed in other Global South regions:

1. **Constitutional Environmentalism:** Brazil's Article 225 of the 1988 Constitution establishes the right to an ecologically balanced environment as a right of all, imposing duties on both the State and the collective. Colombia's Constitution (Arts. 79-80) guarantees the right to a healthy environment and imposes planning obligations for sustainable development. Mexico's Article 4 (amended 2012) guarantees the right to a healthy environment.

2. **Acción de Tutela / Acción Popular / Ação Civil Pública:** These constitutional remedies provide accessible judicial mechanisms for environmental enforcement. Colombia's acción de tutela allows any individual to seek immediate judicial protection of fundamental rights. Brazil's ação civil pública (Lei 7.347/1985) grants the Ministério Público and civil society organizations standing to bring collective environmental claims.

3. **Rights of Nature:** Ecuador's 2008 Constitution (Art. 71-74) pioneered constitutional rights of nature, influencing Colombia's recognition of the Amazon, the Atrato River (T-622/2016), and the Cauca River as subjects of rights.

### Notable Cases

- **PSB v. Brazil (STF, ADPF 708, 2022):** The Supreme Federal Tribunal (STF) declared unconstitutional the federal government's paralysis of the Amazon Fund (Fundo Amazônia) and ordered the reactivation of the fund. Justice Luís Roberto Barroso's opinion established the "state of unconstitutional affairs" (estado de coisas inconstitucional) in Brazilian climate governance, drawing on Colombian constitutional methodology.
- **Future Generations v. Ministry of Environment (Colombia, STC 4360-2018):** The 25 youth plaintiffs successfully argued that deforestation in the Colombian Amazon violated their rights to life, health, food, and water. The Court ordered zero net deforestation in the Amazon by 2020, though compliance has been partial.
- **Lhaka Honhat v. Argentina (IACtHR, 2020):** The Inter-American Court of Human Rights recognized the right to a healthy environment as an autonomous right under Article 26 of the American Convention, impacting climate litigation across the region.
- **Mexico -- Greenpeace v. SEMARNAT (2020):** Environmental organizations challenged the government's energy policy prioritizing fossil fuels through executive decrees that undermined the LGCC's renewable energy targets, securing interim injunctions from federal courts.

## Southeast Asia (Indonesia and Philippines)

### Legal Framework

Southeast Asian climate governance reflects a layered legal system where modern environmental statutes coexist with adat (customary) law, religious legal traditions, and post-colonial administrative structures:

- **Philippines -- Climate Change Act (Republic Act No. 9729, 2009, as amended by RA 10174, 2012):** The Philippines was the first developing country to establish a dedicated Climate Change Commission (CCC) as a policy-making body attached to the Office of the President. RA 10174 (2012) created the People's Survival Fund (PSF), a dedicated annual appropriation of PHP 1 billion (approximately USD 18 million) for local government adaptation projects. The PSF represents the first legislatively mandated, nationally funded climate adaptation finance mechanism in a developing country.
- **Philippines -- Philippine Clean Air Act (RA 8749, 1999) and Ecological Solid Waste Management Act (RA 9003, 2000):** These statutes provide the regulatory framework for emissions standards and waste management, indirectly addressing GHG emissions from industry, transport, and waste sectors.
- **Indonesia -- Presidential Regulation No. 98/2021 on Carbon Economic Values (Nilai Ekonomi Karbon, NEK):** Establishes Indonesia's carbon pricing framework, including carbon trading, results-based payments, carbon levies, and other mechanisms. The regulation creates a national registry and trading platform administered by the Ministry of Environment and Forestry (KLHK).
- **Indonesia -- Law No. 7/2021 on Harmonization of Tax Regulations (HPP Law):** Article 13 introduced a carbon tax initially set at IDR 30,000 per tonne of CO2e (approximately USD 2), to be imposed on coal-fired power plants above a cap-and-tax threshold. Implementation has been repeatedly postponed from the original April 2022 date.
- **Indonesia -- Government Regulation No. 21/2024 on Carbon Trading:** Implements the carbon exchange (Bursa Karbon) launched in September 2023 by the Indonesia Stock Exchange (IDX), enabling trading of carbon credit certificates.
- **Indonesia -- Long-Term Strategy for Low Carbon and Climate Resilience 2050 (LTS-LCCR 2050):** Submitted to the UNFCCC in 2021, targeting net-zero emissions by 2060 or sooner.

### Key Principles

Southeast Asian climate law is shaped by distinctive legal and political features:

1. **Adat and Community-Based Natural Resource Management:** Indonesian law formally recognizes adat (customary) communities' rights over natural resources under Constitutional Court Decision No. 35/PUU-X/2012 (the "MK35" decision), which held that customary forests (hutan adat) are not state forests. This has climate implications by enabling community-based forest management and REDD+ participation.

2. **Climate Vulnerability as Legal Argument:** The Philippines' position as the most climate-vulnerable major economy has shaped its legal approach. The Philippines' National Framework Strategy on Climate Change 2010-2022 explicitly frames climate action as a matter of national survival, and the Commission on Human Rights investigation into the responsibility of carbon majors reflects this vulnerability-centered jurisprudence.

3. **ASEAN Cooperation Frameworks:** The ASEAN Agreement on Transboundary Haze Pollution (2002, entered into force 2003) is the region's primary multilateral environmental agreement, though its consensus-based and non-coercive approach has been criticized for insufficient enforcement.

4. **Religious Dimensions:** Indonesia's Majelis Ulama Indonesia (MUI) issued Fatwa No. 30/2016 on forest and land burning, declaring deliberate burning for land clearing to be haram (prohibited) under Islamic law. While not directly enforceable as positive law, MUI fatwas carry significant moral authority and influence judicial reasoning in Indonesia's dual legal system.

### Notable Cases

- **Greenpeace Southeast Asia et al. v. Carbon Majors (Commission on Human Rights of the Philippines, National Inquiry on Climate Change, 2022):** The CHR concluded its investigation into the responsibility of 47 "carbon major" companies (investor-owned and state-owned fossil fuel and cement entities) for climate change impacts on Filipino human rights. The December 2022 findings held that carbon majors can be held legally and morally responsible for human rights harms resulting from climate change, and that they may be subject to civil liability under domestic tort law and the duty of care under international human rights law. This was the first national human rights body determination linking corporate carbon emissions to human rights obligations.
- **Segovia v. Climate Change Commission (G.R. No. 211010, 2014):** The Philippine Supreme Court, through Justice Antonio Carpio, upheld the standing of petitioners challenging the CCC's inaction, reinforcing the justiciability of climate obligations under the constitutional right to a balanced and healthful ecology (Art. II, Sec. 16).
- **Walhi v. PT Adaro Energy (Indonesia, 2023):** The Indonesian environmental organization Wahana Lingkungan Hidup Indonesia (WALHI) filed climate-related complaints against coal companies, attempting to establish corporate climate liability under Indonesian tort law (Articles 1365-1366 of the Civil Code / Kitab Undang-Undang Hukum Perdata).
- **Samarinda Coal Mining Case (Indonesia, PTUN Decision):** Administrative courts in East Kalimantan have adjudicated challenges to mining permits on environmental grounds, though climate change arguments remain subordinate to local pollution and land rights claims.

## Comparative Analysis

### Structural Divergences

The five legal traditions examined reveal structural divergences in how the Global South governs climate change:

1. **Legislative vs. Executive Dominance:** China and Indonesia rely predominantly on executive-issued regulations and administrative rules, while Brazil, Mexico, and Colombia have enacted comprehensive climate legislation through their legislatures. India occupies a middle position where executive policies (NAPCC) coexist with increasingly active judicial governance. The Philippines combines legislative frameworks (RA 9729) with executive leadership through the Climate Change Commission.

2. **Justiciability of Climate Rights:** Latin American jurisdictions lead the Global South in justiciable climate rights claims, enabled by constitutional environmental rights and accessible judicial remedies. South Africa's Section 24 similarly provides a justiciable environmental right. India's judiciary has progressively expanded justiciable climate rights through Article 21 interpretation. Nigeria and Indonesia face significant justiciability barriers -- Nigeria due to the non-justiciable status of Chapter II CFRN provisions, Indonesia due to the dominance of administrative over constitutional adjudication in environmental matters.

3. **Carbon Pricing Adoption:** China and South Africa have implemented carbon pricing (ETS and carbon tax respectively), while India's PAT scheme represents an indirect pricing mechanism. Indonesia has legislated but not implemented its carbon tax. Brazil's carbon market legislation remains under legislative consideration. The Philippines has not adopted carbon pricing. This variation reflects differing assessments of economic development constraints and fossil fuel dependency.

4. **Customary and Religious Law Intersections:** Indonesia and Nigeria feature the most complex intersections between customary/religious law and climate governance. Indonesia's adat rights recognition and MUI fatwas create parallel governance channels. Nigeria's customary land tenure systems in the Niger Delta complicate environmental remediation. These dimensions are largely absent from China's unitary socialist system and less prominent in Latin American and Indian climate frameworks.

5. **International Climate Finance Dependence:** All five regions have structured their climate governance partly around access to international climate finance (Green Climate Fund, Global Environment Facility, bilateral REDD+ payments). The Philippines' People's Survival Fund is distinctive as a nationally funded mechanism, reducing dependence on international finance for adaptation.

### Common Challenges

Despite divergent legal structures, all five regions share challenges of enforcement gaps, fossil fuel dependency, corruption in environmental governance, and tension between development priorities and climate commitments. The "implementation deficit" -- the gap between law on the books and law in action -- is a defining feature of climate governance across the Global South.
