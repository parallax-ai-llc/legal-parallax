## Summary

The laws of war -- also known as international humanitarian law (IHL) or the law of armed conflict (LOAC) -- comprise the body of international rules that govern the conduct of hostilities and the protection of persons who are not or are no longer participating in armed conflict, seeking to balance military necessity against humanitarian considerations. The foundational treaties include the four Geneva Conventions of 1949, their Additional Protocols of 1977 and 2005, and the Hague Conventions of 1899 and 1907, supplemented by customary international humanitarian law as documented in the ICRC's 2005 study identifying 161 rules of customary IHL. South Korea implements IHL through the Geneva Conventions Act and relevant provisions of the Military Criminal Act (군형법), the Criminal Act (형법), and the ICC Crimes Act (국제형사재판소 관할 범죄의 처벌 등에 관한 법률), with the unique circumstance that the Korean Peninsula remains technically in a state of armistice under the 1953 Korean Armistice Agreement, making the application of IHL to the inter-Korean relationship a live legal question. The United States has one of the most developed military legal systems in the world, implementing IHL through the Uniform Code of Military Justice (UCMJ, 10 U.S.C. Sections 801-946), the War Crimes Act (18 U.S.C. Section 2441), the Department of Defense Law of War Manual (2015, updated 2016 and 2023), and the Detainee Treatment Act of 2005, while the Supreme Court's Hamdan v. Rumsfeld (2006) decision affirmed the application of Common Article 3 of the Geneva Conventions to the conflict with al-Qaeda. Germany implements IHL through the Völkerstrafgesetzbuch (VStGB, 2002), the Soldatengesetz (Military Service Act), and the Wehrstrafgesetz (Military Criminal Code), with the Federal Constitutional Court and federal courts addressing IHL questions in the context of the Kunduz airstrike and other Afghanistan-related incidents. At the European level, the ECHR has developed jurisprudence on the applicability of human rights law during armed conflict alongside IHL, the ECtHR's landmark decisions in Bankovic v. Belgium (2001), Al-Skeini v. United Kingdom (2011), and Hassan v. United Kingdom (2014) have shaped the relationship between IHL and human rights law, and EU common positions support adherence to IHL by member states and third parties.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea acceded to the four Geneva Conventions of 1949 on August 16, 1966, and ratified Additional Protocols I and II on January 15, 1982. Domestic implementation of IHL is accomplished through several legal instruments. The Military Criminal Act (군형법) contains provisions criminalizing violations of the laws of war committed by military personnel. The Criminal Act (형법) applies to civilians who commit war crimes. The ICC Crimes Act (2007) provides comprehensive domestic definitions of war crimes aligned with the Rome Statute.

### Korean Armistice and IHL

The Korean Armistice Agreement, signed on July 27, 1953, suspended active hostilities between the United Nations Command (representing the Republic of Korea and allied forces) and the Korean People's Army and Chinese People's Volunteer Army. The armistice is not a peace treaty, leaving the two Koreas technically in a state of armed conflict. This legal status has implications for the application of IHL: the Geneva Conventions and Additional Protocols theoretically continue to apply to the inter-Korean relationship, and the Demilitarized Zone (비무장지대, DMZ) is governed by the armistice terms. The inter-Korean Basic Agreement of 1991 and subsequent agreements, including the Panmunjom Declaration of 2018, have sought to establish a peace regime but lack the legal character of a formal peace treaty.

### Vietnam War Responsibility

South Korea deployed approximately 320,000 troops to Vietnam between 1964 and 1973. Allegations of civilian massacres committed by Korean forces, particularly in Phong Nhi and Phong Nhat (1968) and Ha My (1968), have been the subject of Vietnamese civilian claims in Korean courts. In February 2023, a Seoul Central District Court awarded 30 million KRW to a Vietnamese civilian plaintiff, marking the first successful war crimes damages claim against the Korean government for Vietnam War conduct.

### Military Criminal Act Provisions

| Offense | Penalty under Military Criminal Act |
|---------|--------------------------------------|
| Murder of prisoners of war or civilians | Death, life imprisonment, or minimum 5 years |
| Torture of POWs | Up to 10 years imprisonment |
| Plunder of civilian property | Up to 10 years imprisonment |
| Use of prohibited weapons | Up to 7 years imprisonment |
| Destruction of protected cultural property | Up to 5 years imprisonment |

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States ratified the four Geneva Conventions on August 2, 1955, but has not ratified Additional Protocols I or II (signed December 12, 1977). The US implements IHL through a comprehensive legal framework combining statutory law, military regulations, and executive guidance.

**Uniform Code of Military Justice (UCMJ):** The UCMJ (10 U.S.C. Sections 801-946) provides the statutory basis for military criminal law, including provisions applicable to IHL violations. Article 18 grants general courts-martial jurisdiction over violations of the law of war. Article 134 (the "General Article") criminalizes conduct prejudicial to good order and discipline, which can encompass IHL violations.

**War Crimes Act (18 U.S.C. Section 2441):** This statute criminalizes grave breaches of the Geneva Conventions and other specified war crimes committed by or against US nationals or members of the US armed forces. The Military Commissions Act of 2006 retroactively amended the War Crimes Act to narrow the scope of prohibited conduct under Common Article 3.

**DoD Law of War Manual (2015):** The Department of Defense Law of War Manual, first issued in June 2015 and updated in December 2016 and 2023, provides comprehensive guidance on the application of IHL by US armed forces. It covers the principles of distinction, proportionality, military necessity, and humanity, as well as specific rules regarding targeting, detention, occupation, and weapons.

### Hamdan v. Rumsfeld (2006)

In Hamdan v. Rumsfeld, 548 U.S. 557 (2006), the Supreme Court held that the military commissions established by the Bush administration to try detainees at Guantánamo Bay were not properly constituted under the UCMJ or the Geneva Conventions. Critically, the Court held that Common Article 3 of the Geneva Conventions -- which provides minimum humanitarian protections in armed conflicts "not of an international character" -- applied to the conflict with al-Qaeda, rejecting the government's argument that the War on Terror fell entirely outside the Geneva framework.

### Key Incidents and Accountability

| Incident | Response |
|----------|----------|
| My Lai Massacre (1968) | Lt. William Calley convicted (1971); sentence reduced; pardoned |
| Abu Ghraib (2004) | Multiple courts-martial; 11 soldiers convicted of detainee abuse |
| Haditha Killings (2005) | 8 Marines charged; 1 convicted (dereliction of duty); charges dropped or acquitted for others |
| Eddie Gallagher Case (2019) | Navy SEAL tried for war crimes; acquitted of murder; convicted of posing with corpse; presidential pardon |

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany ratified the Geneva Conventions on September 3, 1954, and Additional Protocols I and II on February 14, 1991. Germany's IHL implementation is among the most comprehensive globally, centered on the Völkerstrafgesetzbuch (VStGB, 2002).

**VStGB War Crimes Provisions:** Sections 8-12 of the VStGB codify war crimes in detail: Section 8 covers war crimes against persons (including wilful killing, torture, sexual violence, and use of human shields); Section 9 covers war crimes against property and other rights; Section 10 covers war crimes against humanitarian operations and emblems; Section 11 covers war crimes involving prohibited methods of warfare (including starvation, perfidy, and denial of quarter); and Section 12 covers war crimes involving prohibited means of warfare (including prohibited weapons).

**Wehrstrafgesetz (Military Criminal Code):** The Wehrstrafgesetz applies specifically to military personnel and addresses offenses such as disobedience of orders, desertion, and conduct endangering military security. Section 18 addresses the obligation to refuse manifestly illegal orders.

### Kunduz Airstrike Jurisprudence

The September 4, 2009 airstrike near Kunduz, Afghanistan, ordered by German Colonel Georg Klein during the ISAF mission, targeted two fuel tankers captured by Taliban forces. The airstrike killed approximately 142 people, including civilians. The case generated extensive legal proceedings: the Generalbundesanwalt (Federal Prosecutor General) declined to prosecute Klein, finding that he acted within the framework of IHL; parliamentary investigation committees examined the decision-making process; and civil claims were brought in German courts. The BGH ruled in 2016 (III ZR 140/15) that the Amtshaftung framework applied but found no breach of IHL obligations on the facts.

### German Military Manual

The Humanitäres Völkerrecht in bewaffneten Konflikten -- Handbuch (ZDv 15/2, later renamed to A-2141/1) serves as Germany's IHL military manual. It provides binding rules of engagement and IHL guidance for Bundeswehr personnel deployed in armed conflicts.

### Penalties under VStGB

| Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|
| War crimes against persons -- killing (§ 8(1) No. 1) | Life imprisonment |
| War crimes against persons -- torture (§ 8(1) No. 3) | Minimum 3 years imprisonment |
| War crimes against persons -- sexual violence (§ 8(1) No. 4) | Minimum 2 years imprisonment |
| War crimes -- prohibited methods of warfare (§ 11) | Minimum 1 year imprisonment |
| War crimes -- prohibited weapons (§ 12) | Minimum 1 year imprisonment |
| Command responsibility failure to prevent (§ 13) | Up to 5 years imprisonment |

## ECHR & EU

### IHL and ECHR Concurrent Application

The ECtHR has developed critical jurisprudence on the relationship between international humanitarian law and the European Convention on Human Rights during armed conflict.

**Bankovic and Others v. Belgium and Others (Application No. 52207/99, Grand Chamber Decision of December 12, 2001):** The Grand Chamber declared inadmissible a claim arising from the NATO bombing of the Radio Televizija Srbije building in Belgrade, holding that the ECHR does not apply extraterritorially to military operations unless the state exercises effective control over the area. The decision established a restrictive approach to extraterritorial jurisdiction.

**Al-Skeini and Others v. United Kingdom (Application No. 55721/07, Grand Chamber Judgment of July 7, 2011):** The Grand Chamber expanded the extraterritorial application of the ECHR, holding that the UK was bound by Article 2 (right to life) obligations during its military occupation of southern Iraq. The judgment established that state jurisdiction under Article 1 ECHR could arise from the exercise of physical power and control over individuals (personal model of jurisdiction) or from effective control over territory.

**Hassan v. United Kingdom (Application No. 29750/09, Grand Chamber Judgment of September 16, 2014):** The Grand Chamber held that Article 5 ECHR (right to liberty) must be interpreted and applied in light of IHL during international armed conflicts. The Court accepted that internment (security detention) could be permissible under Article 5 when it is governed by IHL, provided that the conditions of the Third and Fourth Geneva Conventions are met. This landmark decision formally recognized the co-application of IHL and the ECHR.

**Georgia v. Russia (II) (Application No. 38263/08, Grand Chamber Judgment of January 21, 2021):** The Grand Chamber addressed Russia's actions during the August 2008 armed conflict in Georgia, finding violations of Articles 2, 3, and 8 ECHR, and Article 1 of Protocol No. 1. The Court applied the ECHR extraterritorially based on Russia's effective control and affirmed the concurrent application of IHL and ECHR.

### EU Support for IHL

The EU has adopted guidelines and common positions supporting IHL compliance: the EU Guidelines on Promoting Compliance with International Humanitarian Law (OJ C 303, December 15, 2005, updated 2009) provide a framework for EU action to promote IHL compliance by third states and non-state actors. The EU Common Position on the International Criminal Court supports prosecution of war crimes.

### ICRC Customary IHL Study

The International Committee of the Red Cross published its landmark study on Customary International Humanitarian Law in 2005, identifying 161 rules of customary IHL applicable in international and non-international armed conflicts. The study has been influential in domestic and international court proceedings, though some states, including the United States, have contested specific findings.

## Comparative Analysis

### Treaty Ratification

A significant divergence exists in treaty ratification. Germany and South Korea have ratified both Additional Protocols, while the United States has not, relying instead on the position that many AP I and AP II provisions reflect customary international law binding on all states. The US non-ratification of AP I is primarily related to disagreements over the treatment of guerrilla fighters and national liberation movements.

### Prosecution Frameworks

Germany's VStGB provides the most comprehensive domestic war crimes prosecution framework, with universal jurisdiction and no nexus requirement. The United States relies on the UCMJ (military justice) and the War Crimes Act (federal courts), with military commissions as a controversial third track. South Korea's framework combines military criminal law with the ICC Crimes Act.

### Accountability Records

All jurisdictions have faced challenges in holding military personnel accountable for IHL violations. The United States has the most extensive record of prosecution attempts, though outcomes have been uneven (My Lai, Abu Ghraib, Haditha). Germany's Kunduz airstrike case demonstrated the difficulty of holding commanders accountable for targeting decisions. South Korea's Vietnam War accountability has only recently begun with the 2023 court ruling.

### IHL-IHRL Relationship

The ECtHR's Hassan decision represents the most developed judicial statement on the concurrent application of IHL and human rights law, holding that the two bodies of law can be applied harmoniously. This approach has implications for all CoE member states, including Germany. The US position has generally emphasized the lex specialis nature of IHL during armed conflict, suggesting that IHL displaces rather than coexists with human rights law.
