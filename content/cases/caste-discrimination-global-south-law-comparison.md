## Summary

Caste, class, and analogous systems of hereditary or structural discrimination permeate legal systems across the Global South, yet receive markedly different legal treatment depending on each jurisdiction's historical trajectory, constitutional framework, and political culture. India's constitutional and legislative anti-caste architecture is the most elaborate in the world, rooted in Part III (Fundamental Rights) and Part IV (Directive Principles) of the 1950 Constitution, the system of reservations (affirmative action) under Articles 15(4), 16(4), and 46, and the Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes (Prevention of Atrocities) Act, 1989 (SC/ST Act). China's socialist legal system formally abolished class distinctions with the 1949 revolution, yet the hukou (household registration) system creates a de facto hereditary caste-like division between urban and rural populations, with profound legal consequences for access to education, employment, social services, and property rights. Nigeria's constitutional prohibition on ethnic and religious discrimination (Section 42 of the 1999 Constitution) coexists with deeply entrenched systems of social stratification, including the Osu caste system among the Igbo people and the stratified social hierarchies within Hausa-Fulani emirate structures. South Africa's post-apartheid Constitution (1996) created the world's most comprehensive anti-discrimination framework through the Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act 4 of 2000 (PEPUDA), addressing the legacy of racial caste through broad equality provisions. Brazil's legal order confronts the myth of "racial democracy" (democracia racial) through the Statute of Racial Equality (Lei No. 12.288/2010), affirmative action in university admissions (Lei No. 12.711/2012), and the criminalization of racism (Lei No. 7.716/1989), while the Philippines' class stratification intersects with colonial legacies through the Indigenous Peoples' Rights Act (RA 8371, 1997) and the Magna Carta for the Poor (RA 11291, 2019). Indonesia's constitutional equality provisions (Article 28I) operate alongside persistent discrimination against ethnic Chinese, indigenous Papuans, and communities marginalized through the transmission of political stigma from the 1965-66 anti-communist purges.

## China

### The Hukou System as De Facto Caste

While China's Constitution declares in Article 33 that "all citizens of the People's Republic of China are equal before the law" and Article 4 prohibits discrimination against any ethnic group, the hukou (户口, household registration) system creates structural inequalities that scholars including Kam Wing Chan and Fei-Ling Wang have characterized as resembling a caste system.

Established through the Household Registration Regulations of 1958 (全国人大常委会关于户口登记条例), the hukou system categorizes every Chinese citizen as either agricultural (nongye) or non-agricultural (fei nongye) and registers them in a specific locality. This classification historically determined access to state-provided education, healthcare, housing, food rations, and employment. Despite decades of reform, the system continues to structure inequality:

- **Education:** Children of rural migrant workers in cities often cannot attend local public schools or sit for the gaokao (national college entrance examination) in their city of residence, forcing them to return to their hukou locality. The State Council's 2014 reform opinions (Guofa [2014] No. 25) initiated changes, but megacities maintain restrictive points-based systems.
- **Social Insurance:** As detailed in the Social Insurance Law 2010, entitlements to medical insurance, pension, and unemployment insurance are administered at the municipal level, disadvantaging inter-provincial migrants.
- **Housing:** Rural hukou holders cannot purchase commercial housing in many cities; conversely, rural land use rights are tied to rural hukou status.
- **Employment:** Despite the Employment Promotion Law of 2007 (Article 31, prohibiting employment discrimination based on household registration), studies document persistent wage differentials of 30-40% between urban hukou holders and rural migrants performing identical work.

### Historical Class Labels (Chengfen)

During the Mao era (1949-1976), the party-state assigned class labels (阶级成分, jieji chengfen) to every family -- landlord, rich peasant, middle peasant, poor peasant, worker, capitalist, etc. These labels, transmitted patrilineally, determined political status, educational opportunity, employment, marriage prospects, and vulnerability to political campaigns. The "four bad elements" (landlords, rich peasants, counter-revolutionaries, and rightists) and their descendants faced systematic discrimination amounting to political caste status.

The class label system was formally abolished by the CPC Central Committee in January 1979 (Document No. 4, "Decision on Removing Rightist Labels") and the system fully dismantled through the 1980s. However, scholars such as Andrew Walder have documented the persistence of class-label effects across generations, with descendants of "bad class" families showing measurably lower educational attainment and political participation even decades after formal abolition.

### Ethnic Minority Preferential Policies

China's Regional Ethnic Autonomy Law (1984, revised 2001) and various State Council regulations establish preferential policies (youhui zhengce) for the 55 officially recognized ethnic minorities (shaoshu minzu), comprising approximately 8.9% of the population (125 million). These include:

- Bonus points on the gaokao (typically 5-20 points depending on province and minority status)
- Preferential admissions to universities, including dedicated minority preparatory classes (minzu yuke ban)
- Exemptions from the one-child policy (prior to its abolition in 2015; minorities were generally permitted two or three children depending on urban/rural status)
- Economic development subsidies for ethnic autonomous areas

However, these preferential policies have been progressively curtailed since 2020, with several provinces reducing or eliminating gaokao bonus points for minorities, reflecting a policy shift under Xi Jinping toward "ethnic unity" (minzu tuanjie) and the "forging of a strong sense of community for the Chinese nation" (zhonghua minzu gongtongti yishi).

## India

### Constitutional Anti-Caste Framework

India's Constitution contains the world's most detailed constitutional provisions addressing caste discrimination. The foundational provisions include:

- **Article 14:** Equality before the law and equal protection of the laws.
- **Article 15(1):** Prohibition of discrimination on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth.
- **Article 15(4):** Enables the state to make special provisions for the advancement of socially and educationally backward classes or for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes (added by the First Amendment, 1951).
- **Article 16(4):** Enables the state to make provisions for reservation in public employment in favor of backward classes not adequately represented.
- **Article 17:** Abolition of untouchability and its practice in any form declared a punishable offense.
- **Article 46 (DPSP):** Directs the state to promote educational and economic interests of the weaker sections, particularly Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes, and protect them from social injustice and exploitation.

### The Reservation System

India's reservation (affirmative action) system reserves seats in public educational institutions, government employment, and legislative bodies for designated groups:

- **Scheduled Castes (SCs):** 15% reservation (corresponding to their approximately 16.6% population share in the 2011 Census)
- **Scheduled Tribes (STs):** 7.5% reservation (approximately 8.6% of population)
- **Other Backward Classes (OBCs):** 27% reservation in central government and institutions (implemented following the Mandal Commission Report, 1980, and upheld in Indra Sawhney v. Union of India (1992 Supp (3) SCC 217))
- **Economically Weaker Sections (EWS):** 10% reservation added by the 103rd Constitutional Amendment (2019), upheld in Janhit Abhiyan v. Union of India (2022 SCC OnLine SC 1540)

The landmark **Indra Sawhney v. Union of India (1992)** established foundational principles: a 50% ceiling on total reservations (subject to extraordinary circumstances); exclusion of the "creamy layer" (economically advanced members) from OBC reservations; prohibition of reservations in promotions for OBCs (though permitted for SCs/STs under Articles 16(4A) and 16(4B), added by the 77th and 85th Amendments); and the requirement that backwardness be determined by social and educational criteria, not solely caste.

In **State of Punjab v. Davinder Singh (2024)**, the Supreme Court's seven-judge bench held that states are constitutionally permitted to sub-classify Scheduled Castes for the purpose of reservations, allowing preferential treatment to the most marginalized sub-castes within the SC category. This landmark ruling overturned the five-judge bench decision in E.V. Chinnaiah v. State of Andhra Pradesh (2005) and opened the door to addressing intra-caste hierarchies.

### SC/ST (Prevention of Atrocities) Act, 1989

The Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes (Prevention of Atrocities) Act, 1989, as amended in 2015 and reinforced by the Supreme Court's review in Subhash Kashinath Mahajan v. State of Maharashtra (2018), provides enhanced criminal penalties for offenses committed against members of Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes by non-members. Key provisions include:

- **Section 3:** Defines atrocities including forcing consumption of noxious substances, dumping excreta on premises, wrongful occupation of land, compelling bonded labor, intimidating voters, instituting false proceedings, assaulting or sexually exploiting SC/ST women, and using a position of dominance to exploit.
- **Section 14:** Establishment of Special Courts for expeditious trial.
- **Section 15A (added 2015):** Rights of victims and witnesses, including the right to reasonable, accurate, and timely information about the case.
- **Section 18:** Presumption of offense -- if the accused was aware of the victim's SC/ST status, the court shall presume the offense was committed.
- **Section 18A (added 2018 by Supreme Court direction, legislated 2018):** No anticipatory bail for offenses under the Act (reinstating the original bar after the Supreme Court temporarily allowed anticipatory bail in the Mahajan decision, overturned by the 2018 Amendment Act following massive protests).

### National Crime Records Bureau data for 2022 reported 57,582 cases of crimes against Scheduled Castes (a rate of 25.4 per 100,000 SC population) and 11,399 cases against Scheduled Tribes. Conviction rates remain low -- approximately 32% for SC cases and 26% for ST cases -- reflecting systemic challenges in investigation and prosecution.

## Africa

### South Africa: From Racial Caste to Constitutional Equality

South Africa's apartheid system (1948-1994) constituted a legislated racial caste system of extraordinary specificity, classifying the population into four racial groups (White, Coloured, Indian/Asian, Black/African) through the Population Registration Act 30 of 1950, and assigning differential rights through over 300 statutes and regulations governing residence (Group Areas Act 41 of 1950), education (Bantu Education Act 47 of 1953), labor (Industrial Conciliation Act, various iterations), mobility (Pass Laws, various), marriage (Prohibition of Mixed Marriages Act 55 of 1949), and sexual relations (Immorality Act 21 of 1950).

The 1996 Constitution established a transformative anti-discrimination framework:

- **Section 1:** The Republic is founded on values including "human dignity, the achievement of equality and the advancement of human rights and freedoms; non-racialism and non-sexism."
- **Section 9(1):** Everyone is equal before the law.
- **Section 9(2):** Equality includes the full and equal enjoyment of all rights and freedoms, and the state may take legislative measures "designed to protect or advance persons, or categories of persons, disadvantaged by unfair discrimination."
- **Section 9(3):** Prohibits unfair discrimination on 17 listed grounds including race, gender, sex, ethnic or social origin, colour, and birth.
- **Section 9(5):** Discrimination on a listed ground is presumed unfair unless established to be fair.

The **Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act 4 of 2000 (PEPUDA)** operationalizes Section 9, establishing Equality Courts in every Magistrate's Court and the High Court, defining prohibited conduct (unfair discrimination, hate speech, harassment), and placing the burden on the respondent to prove that discrimination is fair.

The **Employment Equity Act 55 of 1998** mandates designated employers to implement affirmative action measures achieving equitable representation of designated groups (Black, Coloured, Indian, women, persons with disabilities) at all occupational levels. The **Broad-Based Black Economic Empowerment Act 53 of 2003 (BBBEE)** requires compliance with empowerment scorecards measuring ownership, management control, skills development, enterprise development, and socio-economic development indicators.

In **Minister of Finance v. Van Heerden (2004 (6) SA 121 (CC))**, the Constitutional Court established the framework for evaluating affirmative action measures under Section 9(2), holding that restitutionary measures need not satisfy the Section 36 limitation clause but must: (1) target persons disadvantaged by unfair discrimination; (2) be designed to protect or advance such persons; and (3) promote the achievement of equality.

### Nigeria: Ethnic Discrimination and the Osu System

Nigeria's Constitution addresses discrimination primarily through Section 42: "A citizen of Nigeria of a particular community, ethnic group, place of origin, sex, religion or political opinion shall not, by reason only that he is such a person, be subjected to any disability or deprivation." The Federal Character Principle (Section 14(3)) requires that "the composition of the Government of the Federation or any of its agencies and the conduct of its affairs shall be carried out in such a manner as to reflect the federal character of Nigeria and the need to promote national unity, and also to command national loyalty, thereby ensuring that there shall be no predominance of persons from a few States or from a few ethnic or other sectional groups in that Government or in any of its agencies."

However, Nigeria lacks a comprehensive anti-discrimination statute. The Osu caste system among the Igbo people of southeastern Nigeria designates certain families as descendants of persons dedicated to traditional deities, imposing social exclusion, marriage restrictions, and political marginalization. While several southeastern state laws have attempted abolition -- the Osu (Abolition) Law, enacted by the Eastern Region in 1956 and subsequently adopted by Anambra, Imo, Enugu, Ebonyi, and Abia States -- enforcement is negligible, and Osu discrimination persists in marriage, burial, land allocation, and political candidacy.

The National Human Rights Commission (established by the National Human Rights Commission Act 1995, amended 2010) investigates complaints of human rights violations including discrimination, but lacks enforcement powers comparable to South Africa's Equality Courts.

## Latin America

### Brazil: Confronting the Myth of Racial Democracy

Brazil's legal engagement with racial discrimination has evolved from denial to active confrontation. The "racial democracy" (democracia racial) thesis, associated with sociologist Gilberto Freyre and long embraced by the Brazilian state, posited that Brazil's extensive racial mixing (mesticagem) had produced a harmonious multiracial society free from the rigid racial caste of the United States. This myth served to suppress discussion of structural racism despite Brazil's status as the last country in the Western Hemisphere to abolish slavery (Lei Aurea, 1888) and evidence of persistent racial inequality.

The 1988 Constitution broke with this tradition:

- **Article 3(IV):** A fundamental objective of the Republic is "to promote the well-being of all, without prejudice on grounds of origin, race, sex, colour, age, or any other form of discrimination."
- **Article 5(XLII):** "The practice of racism constitutes a non-bailable crime not subject to a statute of limitations (imprescritivel), subject to imprisonment."

The **Lei Cao (Lei No. 7.716/1989)** criminalized acts of racial prejudice (preconceito de raca ou de cor), establishing penalties of one to five years' imprisonment for acts such as denying access to public or commercial establishments, employment, or educational institutions based on race, color, ethnicity, religion, or national origin. The Supreme Court (STF) in **HC 82.424-2 (Ellwanger case, 2003)** expanded the definition of racism to include anti-Semitism, establishing that racism is a social construct not limited to biological race.

### Affirmative Action

The **Lei de Cotas (Lei No. 12.711/2012)** requires federal universities and federal technical institutions to reserve 50% of their admission slots for students who attended public secondary schools, with sub-quotas for self-declared Black (pretos), mixed-race (pardos), and indigenous students proportional to their share of the state population, and sub-quotas for low-income students (family per capita income up to 1.5 minimum wages). The STF unanimously upheld racial quotas in higher education in **ADPF 186 (2012)**, with Relator Justice Ricardo Lewandowski holding that affirmative action policies constitute legitimate instruments for achieving substantive equality.

The **Statute of Racial Equality (Estatuto da Igualdade Racial, Lei No. 12.288/2010)** established a comprehensive framework for promoting racial equality, addressing health (creating the National Policy for the Integral Health of the Black Population), education (mandating Afro-Brazilian history and culture in school curricula per Lei No. 10.639/2003), culture, sport, leisure, media, and religious freedom (protecting Afro-Brazilian religious traditions). The statute also created the National System for the Promotion of Racial Equality (SINAPIR).

In 2023, Lei No. 14.723 renewed and strengthened the quota law for an additional 10 years, maintaining the 50% public school reservation while introducing quilombola (Afro-Brazilian rural community) beneficiaries as a protected category.

### Intersectionality with Class

Brazil's racial stratification is inseparable from class: data from IBGE (Brazilian Institute of Geography and Statistics) consistently shows that Black and mixed-race Brazilians earn approximately 57% of what white Brazilians earn, have higher rates of poverty (32.9% vs. 14.4% for white Brazilians in 2022), lower educational attainment, and are disproportionately represented among victims of police violence and incarceration. The STF has increasingly recognized this intersectionality, with ADPF 347 (2015) declaring the Brazilian prison system an "unconstitutional state of affairs" (estado de coisas inconstitucional), noting the massively disproportionate incarceration of Black Brazilians.

## Southeast Asia

### Indonesia: Post-1965 Political Stigma and Ethnic Discrimination

Indonesia's experience with caste-like discrimination operates through multiple overlapping systems. The 1945 Constitution, as amended, provides in Article 28I(2): "Every person has the right to be free from discriminatory treatment on any ground whatsoever and has the right to protection against such discriminatory treatment." Law No. 40 of 2008 on the Elimination of Racial and Ethnic Discrimination defines racial and ethnic discrimination and establishes a framework for prevention and response, though enforcement mechanisms remain weak.

**Anti-Chinese Discrimination:** Indonesia's ethnic Chinese community (approximately 2.7% of the population) has faced systematic legal discrimination since the colonial era. Post-independence measures included Presidential Instruction No. 14 of 1967 restricting Chinese religious practices, Presidential Decree No. 127 of 1966 barring ethnic Chinese from retail trade in rural areas, and Government Regulation No. 10 of 1959 restricting alien (often Chinese-owned) business activities. The May 1998 anti-Chinese riots, including widespread sexual violence against Chinese-Indonesian women, represented the most severe modern instance. President Abdurrahman Wahid (Gus Dur) repealed several discriminatory regulations in 2000, and Presidential Decree No. 6 of 2000 declared Chinese New Year a national holiday. However, social discrimination persists, particularly in political representation.

**1965-66 Political Stigma:** Following the failed September 30, 1965 coup attempt (G30S), the Suharto regime oversaw the mass killing of an estimated 500,000 to 1 million alleged communists and sympathizers. Survivors and their descendants were classified as ex-tapol (political prisoners) or marked with "ET" (eks-tapol) designations on identity documents, restricting their access to government employment, military service, and even marriage certificates. While the ET marking was formally removed from identity cards in the late 1990s, the International People's Tribunal on 1965 Crimes Against Humanity (held in The Hague, 2015) found that the stigmatization constituted an ongoing crime against humanity. President Joko Widodo's 2023 acknowledgment of "gross human rights violations" was a significant step but fell short of formal legal accountability.

### Philippines: Class, Colonialism, and Indigenous Marginalization

The Philippines' class stratification reflects the legacy of Spanish colonial land concentration, American colonial institutional structures, and post-independence elite capture. The 1987 Constitution contains robust equality provisions:

- **Article III, Section 1:** "No person shall be deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process of law, nor shall any person be denied the equal protection of the laws."
- **Article XIII, Section 1:** Congress shall give highest priority to "the enactment of measures that protect and enhance the right of all the people to human dignity, reduce social, economic, and political inequalities."

The **Indigenous Peoples' Rights Act (IPRA, RA 8371, 1997)** recognizes the rights of Indigenous Cultural Communities/Indigenous Peoples (ICCs/IPs) to ancestral domains, self-governance, social justice, cultural integrity, and free, prior and informed consent (FPIC) for activities affecting their domains. In **Cruz v. Secretary of DENR (2000, G.R. No. 135385)**, the Supreme Court, in a fractured 7-7 decision (no majority), effectively upheld IPRA's constitutionality. The National Commission on Indigenous Peoples (NCIP) administers IPRA, though implementation has been criticized for inadequate FPIC processes and conflicts with mining and development interests.

The **Magna Carta of the Poor (RA 11291, 2019)** establishes a framework for reducing poverty and addressing class-based deprivation, requiring the government to provide basic necessities, livelihood, and social protection to persons living below the poverty threshold.

## Comparative Analysis

### Typology of Discrimination Systems

The Global South exhibits distinct types of structural discrimination:

1. **Birth-Based Caste (India):** The most explicit and legally recognized system, with millennia-old religious and social roots, comprehensive constitutional and statutory responses, and ongoing inter-caste violence.

2. **Administrative Caste (China):** The hukou system creates hereditary, spatially anchored inequality through bureaucratic mechanisms rather than explicit social hierarchy, making it both pervasive and legally opaque.

3. **Racial Hierarchy (Brazil, South Africa):** Systems rooted in colonialism and slavery, with explicit legal dismantlement but persistent structural inequality. South Africa's response has been more aggressive than Brazil's due to the recency and visibility of apartheid.

4. **Ethnic/Religious Stratification (Nigeria, Indonesia):** Complex, cross-cutting inequalities based on ethnicity, religion, and colonial-era divisions, with limited legal frameworks for redress.

5. **Political Caste (Indonesia post-1965):** Discrimination based on political affiliation or familial association with a political event, creating intergenerational stigma resembling caste dynamics.

### Affirmative Action Approaches

The Global South has generated diverse models of affirmative action:

- **India's reservation system** is the most extensive, with constitutionally mandated numerical quotas in education, employment, and legislative representation, generating continuous political contestation and jurisprudence.
- **South Africa's transformative constitutionalism** embeds affirmative action within a broader framework of substantive equality, using scorecards and targets rather than rigid quotas.
- **Brazil's quota system** is notable for its reliance on self-declared racial identity, creating unique challenges around racial classification in a highly mixed population.
- **China's ethnic minority preferences** are explicitly framed as national unity measures rather than anti-discrimination remedies, and have been progressively reduced.
- **Nigeria's Federal Character Principle** addresses regional/ethnic balance in government appointments but does not address caste or class discrimination.
