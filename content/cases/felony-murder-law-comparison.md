## Summary

The felony murder rule -- a legal doctrine that imposes murder liability on a person who causes a death during the commission or attempted commission of a dangerous felony, regardless of whether the killing was intentional or even foreseeable -- is one of the most controversial doctrines in criminal law and reflects a sharp divide between common law and civil law traditions. South Korea does not recognize a general felony murder rule, instead addressing deaths occurring during the commission of felonies through result-qualified offenses (결과적 가중범) codified in the Criminal Act (형법), such as Article 337 (강도치사, robbery resulting in death, punishable by imprisonment of seven or more years or life) and Article 301 (강간치사, rape resulting in death, punishable by life imprisonment or imprisonment of ten or more years); these provisions require at minimum negligence (과실) regarding the death result, representing a compromise between the strict liability of the felony murder rule and a pure intent-based approach. The United States is the primary global proponent of the felony murder rule, applied in most states and at the federal level, with the doctrine allowing conviction for first-degree murder when a death occurs during the commission of enumerated dangerous felonies (typically burglary, arson, robbery, kidnapping, and sexual assault -- the "BARKS" felonies) regardless of intent to kill; however, the rule has faced increasing criticism and reform, with California's SB 1437 (2018) and subsequent SB 775 (2021) dramatically narrowing felony murder liability by requiring that the defendant was the actual killer, acted with intent to kill, or was a major participant who acted with reckless indifference to human life. Germany categorically rejects the felony murder doctrine as incompatible with the constitutional guilt principle (Schuldprinzip, Article 1 in conjunction with Article 20 GG), instead treating deaths during felonies through result-qualified offenses (erfolgsqualifizierte Delikte) under specific provisions such as Section 251 StGB (robbery resulting in death, Raub mit Todesfolge), which require at minimum recklessness (Leichtfertigkeit) regarding the lethal result as mandated by Section 18 StGB. The European Court of Human Rights has not directly addressed the felony murder rule, but its Article 7 (legality principle) jurisprudence and the principle of individual culpability would likely subject strict felony murder liability to rigorous scrutiny, and no EU member state applies the doctrine in its traditional Anglo-American form.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea addresses deaths during felonies through result-qualified offenses (결과적 가중범), which impose enhanced penalties for certain crimes when a more serious result (typically death or serious injury) occurs.

**Result-Qualified Offenses (결과적 가중범):** These provisions aggravate the penalty for a base crime when a specific result occurs, requiring at minimum negligence regarding that result. The Supreme Court of Korea has consistently held that mere causation is insufficient -- the aggravated result must be at least negligently caused.

### Specific Provisions

| Provision | Base Crime | Result | Penalty |
|-----------|-----------|--------|---------|
| Article 337 (강도치사) | Robbery | Death | 7+ years or life imprisonment |
| Article 338 (강도살인) | Robbery | Intentional killing | Death or life imprisonment |
| Article 301 (강간치사) | Rape | Death | Life or 10+ years imprisonment |
| Article 301-2 (강간살인) | Rape | Intentional killing | Death or life imprisonment |
| Article 259 (상해치사) | Assault causing injury | Death | 3+ years imprisonment |
| Article 277 (중체포·중감금치사) | Unlawful arrest/confinement (aggravated) | Death | 3+ years imprisonment |

### Mental State Requirement

**Supreme Court Decision 90도2058 (1990):** The Court established that for result-qualified offenses, the prosecution must prove at minimum a negligent connection between the base crime and the aggravated result: "It is insufficient that the death merely occurred during the commission of the underlying offense; there must be a causal relationship between the criminal conduct and the death, and at minimum a foreseeable possibility that such a result could occur."

**Supreme Court Decision 2008도9867 (2009):** The Court clarified that the foreseeability requirement applies objectively: the question is whether a reasonable person in the offender's position could have foreseen the possibility of death, not whether the specific offender actually foresaw it.

### Distinction from Intentional Killing During Felony

Korean law carefully distinguishes between result-qualified offenses (where death is unintended) and intentional killing during a felony:
- **Article 337 (강도치사):** Robbery resulting in death (unintended) -- 7+ years or life
- **Article 338 (강도살인):** Murder during robbery (intended) -- death or life imprisonment

This distinction reflects the principle that intentional and unintentional killings merit different levels of punishment, rejecting the felony murder rule's equalization of intent.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The felony murder rule has deep roots in American common law, derived from English common law principles articulated by Lord Coke and Blackstone. The traditional rule imposes murder liability -- often first-degree murder -- on any person who causes a death during the commission of a dangerous felony, regardless of intent.

**Traditional Formulation:** If a death results during the commission or attempted commission of an inherently dangerous felony, the felon is guilty of murder. The malice aforethought required for murder is imputed from the intent to commit the underlying felony.

**Enumerated Felonies (BARKS):** Most jurisdictions limit first-degree felony murder to deaths occurring during specifically enumerated felonies:
- **B**urglary
- **A**rson
- **R**obbery
- **K**idnapping
- **S**exual assault/rape

Additional felonies may be added by statute. Some jurisdictions also recognize second-degree felony murder for deaths during other dangerous felonies.

### Federal Application

While there is no explicit federal felony murder statute, federal courts have applied the doctrine through judicial interpretation. The Eighth Circuit in United States v. Heinlein, 490 F.2d 725 (8th Cir. 1974), recognized felony murder in the context of federal bank robbery resulting in death (18 U.S.C. Section 2113(e)).

### Limitations on the Rule

Courts have developed several doctrines to limit the felony murder rule's reach:

**Merger Doctrine:** The underlying felony must be independent of the killing itself. If the felony "merges" with the homicide (e.g., assault), the felony murder rule does not apply. This prevents the rule from converting every assault resulting in death into first-degree murder.

**Inherently Dangerous Felony Limitation:** Some jurisdictions limit the rule to felonies that are inherently dangerous to human life. California courts historically applied this limitation in the abstract (People v. Phillips, 64 Cal. 2d 574 (1966)), while other jurisdictions apply it based on the circumstances of the particular case.

**Agency vs. Proximate Cause:** Jurisdictions split on whether the rule applies when a third party (such as a police officer or victim) causes the death:
- **Agency theory:** Felony murder applies only to killings committed by the felon or co-felons (majority approach)
- **Proximate cause theory:** Felony murder applies to any death that is a proximate result of the felony, including those caused by third parties (minority approach)

**Temporal and Causal Limits:** The death must occur "during" the felony, including the immediate flight from the crime scene. There must also be a causal connection between the felony and the death.

### California Reform (SB 1437 and SB 775)

The most significant felony murder reform in American history occurred in California:

**SB 1437 (2018):** Amended California Penal Code Sections 188 and 189 to require that a felony murder defendant either: (1) was the actual killer; (2) was not the actual killer but acted with intent to kill, aided, abetted, counseled, commanded, induced, solicited, requested, or assisted the actual killer; or (3) was a "major participant" in the underlying felony and acted with "reckless indifference to human life."

**SB 775 (2021):** Expanded the resentencing provisions of SB 1437 to cover attempted murder convictions based on the natural and probable consequences doctrine.

**People v. Banks, 61 Cal. 4th 788 (2015) and People v. Clark, 63 Cal. 4th 522 (2016):** The California Supreme Court provided detailed multi-factor tests for determining "major participant" and "reckless indifference to human life," examining factors such as the defendant's knowledge of weapons, proximity to the killing, opportunity to intervene, and duration of the felony.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany categorically rejects the felony murder doctrine. The Federal Constitutional Court and academic consensus hold that imposing murder liability without proof of at least recklessness regarding the death violates the constitutional guilt principle (Schuldprinzip).

**Section 18 StGB -- Severe Consequences (Schwerere Strafe bei besonderen Tatfolgen):** "Where the law provides for a more severe penalty based on a special consequence of the act, this more severe penalty shall apply to the perpetrator or participant only where they are at least reckless (leichtfertig) with respect to this consequence." This provision establishes a minimum mental state requirement for all result-qualified offenses, directly contradicting the strict liability of the felony murder rule.

### Result-Qualified Offenses (Erfolgsqualifizierte Delikte)

German law addresses deaths during felonies through specific result-qualified provisions:

| Provision | Base Offense | Result | Penalty |
|-----------|-------------|--------|---------|
| Section 227 (Körperverletzung mit Todesfolge) | Intentional bodily harm | Death | 3-15 years |
| Section 251 (Raub mit Todesfolge) | Robbery | Death | 10+ years or life |
| Section 239a(3) (Erpresserischer Menschenraub mit Todesfolge) | Kidnapping for extortion | Death | 10+ years or life |
| Section 306c (Brandstiftung mit Todesfolge) | Arson | Death | 10+ years or life |
| Section 176d (Sexueller Missbrauch mit Todesfolge) | Sexual abuse of children | Death | Life or 10+ years |

### Recklessness Requirement (Leichtfertigkeit)

**Section 18 StGB** requires at minimum "recklessness" (Leichtfertigkeit) regarding the death. German courts define Leichtfertigkeit as a heightened form of negligence, roughly equivalent to gross negligence or recklessness:

**BGH, Judgment of November 22, 2007 (3 StR 400/07):** The Court defined Leichtfertigkeit as "a particularly severe, subjectively inexcusable violation of the duty of care, where the particularly high degree of foreseeable danger would have been evident to any reasonable person."

**BGH, Judgment of September 8, 2016 (4 StR 293/16):** The Court applied the Leichtfertigkeit standard to Section 251, holding that a robber who struck the victim's head with a heavy object was reckless regarding the risk of death, as the danger of fatal injury was obvious.

### Constitutional Foundation

**BVerfG, Decision of March 14, 1972 (2 BvR 41/71):** The Federal Constitutional Court established that criminal penalties must be proportionate to the offender's personal guilt, derived from the principle of human dignity (Article 1 GG) in conjunction with the Rechtsstaatsprinzip (rule of law, Article 20 GG). This constitutional framework precludes the imposition of murder liability without proof of a culpable mental state regarding the death.

### Academic Critique

German legal scholars have been particularly critical of the felony murder rule. Professor Claus Roxin described it as a "relic of archaic strict liability" incompatible with modern principles of culpability. Professor Hans-Heinrich Jescheck similarly characterized the doctrine as violating the fundamental requirement that punishment correspond to individual guilt.

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR has not directly ruled on the felony murder doctrine, but several Convention principles bear directly on its compatibility:

**Article 7 -- Legality Principle:** The Court's Article 7 jurisprudence requires that criminal offenses and penalties be clearly defined and foreseeable. The felony murder rule's imputation of murder intent from an unrelated felony raises questions about whether the resulting conviction for murder is "foreseeable" within the meaning of Article 7.

**Vinter and Others v. United Kingdom (Application No. 66069/09, Grand Chamber Judgment of July 9, 2013):** While addressing whole-life sentences rather than felony murder directly, the Grand Chamber established that disproportionate sentences may violate Article 3 (prohibition of inhuman treatment), requiring that all life sentences include a prospect of review. This principle could be relevant to mandatory life sentences imposed under felony murder rules.

**Principle of Individual Culpability:** The Court has repeatedly emphasized that criminal responsibility must be based on individual culpability. In cases such as G. v. United Kingdom (Application No. 37334/08, Decision of August 30, 2011), the Court recognized the principle that criminal punishment should be proportionate to individual blameworthiness.

### European Legal Landscape

No EU member state applies the felony murder rule in its traditional Anglo-American form:
- **England and Wales:** Abolished the constructive murder rule with the Homicide Act 1957, Section 1
- **Ireland:** Abolished constructive murder by the Criminal Justice Act 1964
- **All civil law jurisdictions:** Require at minimum negligence or recklessness regarding death

The EU has not adopted any harmonization instrument that would require or authorize felony murder liability. EU criminal law harmonization instruments consistently require proportionate penalties based on individual culpability.

### International Criminal Law

International criminal law provides an instructive parallel. The Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court (Article 30) requires that material elements of crimes under the Statute be committed with "intent and knowledge," rejecting strict liability approaches including the felony murder doctrine.

## Comparative Analysis

### The Fundamental Divide

The felony murder rule represents perhaps the clearest divide between Anglo-American and continental European criminal law traditions:

**Anglo-American (United States):** The rule reflects a policy of deterrence -- imposing the most severe punishment for any death during a felony is intended to deter criminals from committing dangerous felonies. The rule also reflects a view that felons assume all risks of their criminal enterprise.

**Continental European (South Korea, Germany):** Both jurisdictions reject strict liability for death, requiring at minimum negligence or recklessness regarding the fatal result. This reflects the principle that punishment must correspond to individual culpability.

### Reform Trajectory

The global trend moves decisively against the felony murder rule:
- England abolished it in 1957
- California dramatically narrowed it in 2018
- Several other U.S. states have enacted limitations
- No jurisdiction has adopted the rule in recent decades

### Proportionality Concerns

The most frequent criticism of the felony murder rule concerns proportionality: a getaway driver who had no role in the violence, no knowledge that a killing would occur, and no opportunity to prevent it may be convicted of first-degree murder and sentenced to life imprisonment (or, prior to Enmund, death). Critics argue this violates basic principles of proportional punishment.
