## Summary

Name change law -- the legal framework governing an individual's right to alter their given name, surname, or both -- reflects deeply embedded cultural assumptions about personal identity, family lineage, gender, and the state's interest in maintaining reliable identification systems, with approaches ranging from judicial petition systems requiring demonstration of justifiable cause to administrative registration systems permitting largely unrestricted self-determination. South Korea regulates name changes through Article 8 of the Family Register Act (가족관계의 등록 등에 관한 법률) and the Supreme Court's established criteria for permissible name changes (개명허가 기준), requiring a family court petition demonstrating "justifiable cause" (정당한 사유); historically one of the most restrictive systems among developed nations, Korean courts dramatically liberalized name change approval beginning with the Supreme Court's landmark 2005 decision (2005스26) that expanded justifiable cause to include personal dissatisfaction with one's name, resulting in approval rates exceeding 95 percent and over 150,000 name change petitions annually by the 2020s. The United States permits name changes through state court petitions under varying state statutes, with most states requiring publication of the name change in a newspaper (waivable in some jurisdictions for safety reasons), residency in the county of filing, absence of fraudulent intent, and no intent to evade debts or criminal liability; common law name change through usage (without court order) remains theoretically available in most states but is insufficient for updating government-issued identification. Germany regulates name changes through the Namensänderungsgesetz (NÄG, Name Change Act of 1938, as amended) for first names and the Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB) for surnames, with given name changes requiring an "important reason" (wichtiger Grund) approved by the Standesamt (civil registry office) or administrative authority; the Selbstbestimmungsgesetz (Self-Determination Act, SBGG), which entered into force on November 1, 2024, fundamentally reformed name change procedures for transgender and non-binary persons, replacing the restrictive Transsexuellengesetz (1980) with a self-declaration system. At the European level, the ECHR has addressed name change rights under Article 8 (right to respect for private and family life), with the European Court of Human Rights establishing in Burghartz v. Switzerland (1994) and Johansson v. Finland (2007) that unjustified restrictions on name changes violate the Convention, while EU Regulation 2016/1191 on public documents facilitates cross-border recognition of name change documents.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's name change system has undergone a remarkable transformation from one of the most restrictive in the developed world to a broadly accessible administrative process.

**Family Register Act (가족관계의 등록 등에 관한 법률) Article 8:** This provision establishes that a person who wishes to change their name (given name, 이름) must file a petition with the family court (가정법원) demonstrating "justifiable cause" (정당한 사유). The court reviews the petition and, if satisfied, issues a name change permission order (개명허가). The petitioner must then register the new name with the municipal office (주민센터).

**Supreme Court Decision of 2005 (2005스26):** This landmark ruling fundamentally expanded the interpretation of "justifiable cause," holding that personal dissatisfaction with one's name -- including difficulty in social life, negative associations, or simple personal preference -- constitutes sufficient justification. The Court departed from the prior restrictive interpretation that required objective hardship such as ridicule, discrimination, or confusion with criminals. The decision stated: "A person's name is a symbol of their personality and individuality, and the freedom to determine one's own name is an aspect of the right to pursue happiness guaranteed by the Constitution."

**Surname Change:** Surname changes (성 변경) are governed separately under Article 781 of the Civil Act (민법), which was amended in 2005 to permit a child to adopt the surname of their stepfather or mother in cases where the child's welfare would be served. Prior to this amendment, Korean law strictly maintained the patrilineal surname system. The Constitutional Court upheld the constitutionality of the patrilineal surname presumption in 2005 (2003헌가5) while affirming the legislature's authority to permit exceptions.

### Statistics and Trends

| Year | Name Change Petitions | Approval Rate |
|------|----------------------|---------------|
| 2000 | ~4,000 | ~35% |

| 2015 | ~148,000 | ~97% |
| 2020 | ~155,000 | ~97% |
| 2023 | ~160,000 | ~98% |

The dramatic increase reflects the 2005 liberalization. Common reasons for name changes include: (1) dissatisfaction with names chosen based on hanja (Chinese character) fortune-telling (작명) traditions; (2) names that sound similar to negative words; (3) adoption of more modern or unique names; (4) gender transition.

### Cultural Context

Korean naming culture is deeply influenced by Confucian traditions emphasizing generational names (돌림자) and hanja character selection based on saju (사주, Four Pillars of Destiny) fortune-telling. The post-2005 liberalization reflects a societal shift toward individual identity expression over traditional family naming conventions. The growing trend of "unique names" (특이한 이름) using native Korean words rather than Sino-Korean characters has also driven name changes among young adults.

### Gender-Related Name Changes

Transgender individuals in South Korea must petition the family court for both a name change and gender marker change. Following the Supreme Court's 2006 guidelines (2004스42), courts require medical documentation including psychiatric diagnosis, hormone therapy history, and sex reassignment surgery (though some lower courts have waived the surgery requirement in recent years). The name change component generally follows the standard "justifiable cause" analysis.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Name change procedures in the United States are governed by state statutes, with significant variation in requirements and procedures.

**Common Elements Across States:**
- **Court Petition:** The petitioner files a name change petition with the appropriate state court (usually county or district court), stating the current legal name, desired new name, and reason for the change.
- **Publication Requirement:** Most states require publication of the name change petition in a newspaper of general circulation for a specified period (typically 1-4 weeks) before the hearing. Publication may be waived for victims of domestic violence, stalking, or human trafficking, and some states (California, New York) have eliminated or reduced the requirement.
- **Hearing:** A judge reviews the petition, considers any objections, and grants or denies the change. Denial is typically based on fraudulent intent, intent to evade debts or criminal liability, or potential confusion with existing legal obligations.
- **No Specific Justification Required:** Unlike South Korea's "justifiable cause" standard, most U.S. states do not require a specific reason for the name change beyond the absence of improper motive.

**Common Law Name Change:** Under common law, an individual may adopt any name through consistent usage without court order. This principle, recognized in most states, derives from the English common law tradition. However, common law name change is insufficient for updating government identification (driver's licenses, passports, Social Security records), which require a court order or statutory basis (such as marriage).

**Federal Recognition:** A court-ordered state name change is recognized federally for purposes of passport issuance (22 C.F.R. Section 51.25), Social Security records (20 C.F.R. Section 422.110), and immigration documents.

### Key Case Law

**In re Bicknell, 771 N.W.2d 905 (Minn. 2009):** The Minnesota Supreme Court held that courts may deny a name change petition only for specific statutory reasons (fraudulent intent, interference with the rights of others) and may not deny based on the court's personal opinion of the proposed name.

**In re Harris, 236 Cal. App. 4th 17 (2015):** The California Court of Appeal held that a name change petition could not be denied based on the potential for identity confusion where the petitioner had no fraudulent intent.

**In re McIntyre (2017):** A Montana court addressed transgender name changes, holding that gender dysphoria provides a legitimate basis for a name change and that publication requirements could be waived for privacy and safety concerns.

### Gender-Related Name Changes

Procedures for transgender name changes vary significantly by state. As of 2025, approximately 20 states have adopted streamlined or self-declaration procedures for gender-related name changes. California's Gender Recognition Act (SB 179, 2017) established a simplified procedure for name and gender changes, including a non-binary gender marker option. Several states have eliminated the publication requirement for gender-related changes to protect privacy and safety.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's name change framework reflects the civil law tradition's emphasis on administrative procedure and public order.

**Namensänderungsgesetz (NÄG) -- Name Change Act (1938, as amended):** This statute governs administrative name changes for given names. Section 3(1) requires an "important reason" (wichtiger Grund) for a name change, interpreted restrictively by administrative courts. Important reasons may include:

- Names causing significant ridicule or social disadvantage
- Names identical to notorious criminals
- Religious conversion justifying adoption of a name from the new faith
- Integration of foreign nationals into German society
- Gender non-conforming names (now largely superseded by the SBGG)

The application is filed with the local Standesamt (civil registry office), which forwards it to the competent administrative authority (typically the Kreis or kreisfreie Stadt). The administrative authority's decision is subject to judicial review by the Verwaltungsgericht (administrative court).

**BGB Surname Provisions:** Surname changes are governed by BGB provisions on marriage (Section 1355), divorce (Section 1355(5)), adoption (Section 1757), and parent-child relationships (Section 1617). Upon marriage, spouses may choose one spouse's surname as the common family name (Ehename), retain their own surnames, or add a double name (Doppelname) with a hyphen.

**Selbstbestimmungsgesetz (SBGG) -- Self-Determination Act (2024):** This landmark statute, which entered into force on November 1, 2024, replaced the Transsexuellengesetz (TSG) of 1980 and fundamentally reformed the procedure for gender-related name and gender marker changes. Key provisions:

- **Section 2:** Any person whose gender identity does not correspond to their registered gender may change their gender entry and given name(s) through self-declaration at the Standesamt.
- **Section 3:** The declaration takes effect three months after submission, providing a reflection period.
- **Section 4:** Minors aged 14 and over may submit the declaration with parental consent; for minors under 14, the parents submit on the child's behalf.
- **Section 5:** The gender entry may not be changed again for one year after the declaration becomes effective (Sperrfrist).
- **Section 6:** No medical certificates, psychiatric diagnoses, or surgical requirements.

The SBGG replaced the TSG, which had required two independent expert opinions confirming "transsexual imprinting" lasting at least three years and had originally required sterilization and divorce as prerequisites (provisions struck down by the BVerfG in 2008, 2009, and 2011).

### Cultural Context

German naming law has historically been relatively restrictive. Until 2008, the Standesamt could refuse registration of a given name that was not recognized as a name, was gender-ambiguous, or could cause the child harm. The Federal Constitutional Court's decision of December 5, 2008 (1 BvR 576/07) relaxed these requirements, and the 2009 Dienstanweisung (administrative instruction) for Standesämter further liberalized naming practices.

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights -- Article 8

The ECHR has developed significant jurisprudence on name change rights as a component of the right to respect for private life under Article 8.

**Burghartz v. Switzerland (Application No. 16213/90, Judgment of February 22, 1994):** The Court held that Switzerland's refusal to allow a husband to use his wife's surname as a prefix to his own violated Article 8 read with Article 14 (non-discrimination). The judgment established that a person's name is a component of private and family life, as a means of identification and a link to a family.

**Johansson v. Finland (Application No. 10163/02, Judgment of September 6, 2007):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where Finland refused to permit a child to be given a first name (Axl) on the ground that it did not conform to Finnish naming conventions. The Court held that the refusal was disproportionate to the legitimate aim of protecting the child's interests.

**Garnaga v. Ukraine (Application No. 20390/07, Judgment of May 16, 2013):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where Ukraine refused to register a patronymic name change, holding that excessive procedural barriers to name changes interfere with the right to private life.

**Cismigeanu v. Romania (Application No. 62729/00, Judgment of January 15, 2009):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where Romania refused a name change that would have corrected an administrative error in the petitioner's surname.

**A.P., Garçon and Nicot v. France (Application No. 79885/12, Judgment of April 6, 2017):** The Court held that the requirement of sterilization as a condition for legal gender recognition (and associated name change) violated Article 8, establishing that states may not impose irreversible medical procedures as prerequisites for legal gender transition.

### EU Regulation 2016/1191 -- Public Documents

Regulation (EU) 2016/1191 on promoting the free movement of citizens by simplifying requirements for presenting certain public documents, applicable from February 16, 2019, eliminates the requirement for apostille or legalization for certain public documents including name change certificates. The Regulation also provides multilingual standard forms to facilitate cross-border recognition.

### CJEU -- Garcia Avello (Case C-148/02, 2003)

The Court of Justice held that Belgium's refusal to allow children with Belgian-Spanish dual nationality to use the Spanish-style double surname violated Article 18 EC (now Article 21 TFEU) on non-discrimination based on nationality. The judgment established that EU citizens have a right to recognition of their name as registered in another EU member state, creating important protections for name portability within the EU.

### Grunkin-Paul (Case C-353/06, 2008)

The CJEU extended the Garcia Avello principle, holding that Germany's refusal to recognize a double surname lawfully granted to a German child in Denmark violated the freedom of movement. The judgment confirmed that member states must recognize names lawfully established in other EU member states.

## Comparative Analysis

### Ease of Access

The four jurisdictions span a spectrum from self-declaration (Germany's SBGG for gender-related changes) to judicial petition (South Korea, United States) to administrative approval (Germany's NÄG). South Korea's post-2005 liberalization has made its system among the most accessible despite formal court involvement, with approval rates exceeding 95 percent and minimal costs.

### Cultural Attitudes

Korean naming culture's roots in Confucian tradition and Chinese character symbolism create unique dynamics: the explosion of name changes after 2005 reflects a generational shift from collective family identity to individual self-expression. American name change culture emphasizes individual autonomy with minimal state intervention. German naming law traditionally reflects the state's interest in orderly civil registration but has undergone significant liberalization, particularly through the SBGG.

### Gender Identity and Name Change

The treatment of gender-related name changes represents the sharpest divergence. Germany's SBGG establishes pure self-declaration without medical requirements. The United States varies dramatically by state, from self-declaration to requirements for medical documentation. South Korea maintains requirements for medical documentation and, in practice, sex reassignment surgery. The ECHR has established that medical requirements for gender recognition (and associated name change) must be proportionate and may not include sterilization.

### Cross-Border Recognition

The EU framework (Regulation 2016/1191, Garcia Avello, Grunkin-Paul) provides the strongest protections for cross-border name recognition. South Korea and the United States lack comparable frameworks, though bilateral agreements and international private law principles provide some protections.
