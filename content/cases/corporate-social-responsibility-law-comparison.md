## Summary

Corporate social responsibility (CSR) and its contemporary manifestation as Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) regulation -- the evolving body of law requiring or incentivizing corporations to account for their impacts on the environment, human rights, labor conditions, and governance standards -- has undergone a remarkable transformation from voluntary corporate initiative to mandatory legal obligation across all major jurisdictions. South Korea has advanced CSR through the K-ESG Guidelines (한국형 ESG 가이드라인) published by the Ministry of Trade, Industry and Energy in 2021, mandatory ESG disclosure requirements for listed companies phased in from 2025, the Act on the Promotion of Social Enterprises (사회적기업 육성법) of 2007, and the Serious Accidents Punishment Act (중대재해 처벌 등에 관한 법률) of 2022 imposing criminal liability and punitive damages for corporate safety failures. The United States approaches CSR primarily through SEC disclosure requirements, including the climate-related disclosure rules adopted in March 2024 (subsequently stayed pending litigation), state-level ESG mandates such as California's Climate Corporate Data Accountability Act (SB 253, 2023) and Climate-Related Financial Risk Act (SB 261, 2023), and a contested political landscape in which anti-ESG legislation in Republican-controlled states has created regulatory fragmentation. Germany has enacted one of the world's most comprehensive CSR frameworks through the Supply Chain Due Diligence Act (Lieferkettensorgfaltspflichtengesetz, LkSG) effective January 2023, the CSR Directive Implementation Act (CSR-Richtlinie-Umsetzungsgesetz) of 2017, and the Federal Climate Change Act (Bundes-Klimaschutzgesetz, KSG) of 2019. At the EU level, the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD, 2022/2464) mandates comprehensive sustainability reporting under European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS), the Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD, 2024/1760) imposes human rights and environmental due diligence obligations across value chains with civil liability, and the EU Taxonomy Regulation (2020/852) classifies environmentally sustainable economic activities.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea has pursued CSR through a combination of government-led guidelines, mandatory disclosure, and targeted criminal liability.

### K-ESG Guidelines

The Ministry of Trade, Industry and Energy published the K-ESG Guidelines (한국형 ESG 가이드라인) in December 2021, establishing 61 core indicators across environmental, social, and governance categories. While initially voluntary, these guidelines form the basis for mandatory disclosure requirements being phased in for listed companies.

### Mandatory ESG Disclosure

The Financial Services Commission (금융위원회) announced in 2021 a phased ESG disclosure mandate for KOSPI-listed companies: companies with assets exceeding 2 trillion KRW from 2025, and all KOSPI-listed companies from 2030. The disclosure requirements align with international standards including the ISSB (International Sustainability Standards Board) standards and the TCFD (Task Force on Climate-Related Financial Disclosures) framework.

### Serious Accidents Punishment Act (중대재해 처벌 등에 관한 법률)

This landmark Act, effective January 27, 2022, imposes criminal liability on business owners and responsible officers when failure to comply with safety and health duties results in death or serious injury. Penalties include imprisonment for at least one year and punitive damages of up to five times actual damages. The Act applies to both industrial accidents and public safety incidents, extending to companies with 50 or more employees (lowered from the initial 5 or more employees threshold from January 2024).

### Korea Emissions Trading Scheme (K-ETS)

South Korea launched the K-ETS in January 2015 as the first national emissions trading system in East Asia. The scheme covers approximately 684 entities responsible for about 73.5% of national greenhouse gas emissions. The third phase (2021-2025) introduced auctioning of allowances and expanded sector coverage.

### Social Enterprises

The Act on the Promotion of Social Enterprises (사회적기업 육성법) of 2007 created a formal certification framework for social enterprises. Certified social enterprises (인증 사회적기업) receive tax incentives, preferential government procurement, and financial support. As of 2024, over 3,600 entities have been certified.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States presents a uniquely fragmented CSR landscape, with federal regulation, state mandates, and state anti-ESG legislation pulling in different directions.

### SEC Climate Disclosure

The SEC adopted final climate-related disclosure rules on March 6, 2024, requiring public companies to disclose material climate-related risks, risk management processes, Scope 1 and Scope 2 greenhouse gas emissions (for large accelerated and accelerated filers), and the financial impacts of severe weather events and other natural conditions. The rules were immediately challenged in multiple federal courts, and the SEC voluntarily stayed the rules pending judicial review.

### State ESG Mandates

**California SB 253 (Climate Corporate Data Accountability Act, 2023):** Requires companies with annual revenues exceeding $1 billion doing business in California to report Scope 1, 2, and 3 greenhouse gas emissions beginning in 2026 (Scopes 1 and 2) and 2027 (Scope 3).

**California SB 261 (Climate-Related Financial Risk Act, 2023):** Requires companies with annual revenues exceeding $500 million to prepare climate-related financial risk reports in accordance with TCFD recommendations biennially beginning in 2026.

### Anti-ESG Movement

Republican-controlled state legislatures have enacted laws restricting ESG considerations in public investment:

| State | Legislation | Effect |
|-------|-----------|--------|
| Texas | SB 13 (2021) | Prohibits state agencies from contracting with financial companies that "boycott" fossil fuel companies |
| Florida | HB 3 (2023) | Prohibits state pension funds from considering ESG factors in investment decisions |
| Kentucky | HB 236 (2023) | Requires financial institutions to certify they do not boycott energy companies |
| Indiana | HB 1008 (2023) | Prohibits state funds from considering ESG factors in fiduciary decisions |

### Benefit Corporations

Thirty-seven states and the District of Columbia have enacted benefit corporation statutes, creating a corporate form that legally requires directors to consider the impact of decisions on all stakeholders, not just shareholders. Delaware's Public Benefit Corporation (PBC) statute (DGCL Subchapter XV) is the most influential model. The certified B Corporation designation (administered by B Lab) provides a voluntary alternative for conventional corporations.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany has established one of the world's most comprehensive mandatory CSR frameworks.

### Supply Chain Due Diligence Act (LkSG)

The Lieferkettensorgfaltspflichtengesetz (LkSG), effective January 1, 2023, requires companies with their registered office, principal place of business, or administrative headquarters in Germany to establish due diligence processes for identifying, preventing, and remedying human rights and environmental risks in their supply chains.

**Scope:** Companies with 1,000+ employees (since January 2024; initially 3,000+ from 2023).

**Obligations:** Risk analysis, preventive measures, remedial actions, grievance mechanisms, documentation, and annual reporting.

**Enforcement:** The Federal Office for Economic Affairs and Export Control (Bundesamt für Wirtschaft und Ausfuhrkontrolle, BAFA) oversees compliance. Administrative fines of up to 2% of average annual global turnover may be imposed for violations. Companies fined above certain thresholds may be excluded from public procurement for up to three years.

**No Civil Liability:** Unlike the EU CSDDD, the LkSG does not create a direct civil cause of action for supply chain violations, though affected parties may bring claims under general tort law.

### Climate Protection

The Bundes-Klimaschutzgesetz (Federal Climate Change Act, KSG) of 2019, as amended in 2021 following the Federal Constitutional Court's landmark Neubauer decision (BVerfGE 157, 30, March 24, 2021), sets binding sectoral emission reduction targets leading to climate neutrality by 2045. The Constitutional Court held that insufficient climate protection measures violated the fundamental rights of future generations under Articles 2 and 20a of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz).

### Non-Financial Reporting

The CSR-Richtlinie-Umsetzungsgesetz (CSR Directive Implementation Act) of 2017, implementing EU Directive 2014/95/EU, introduced non-financial reporting requirements for large capital-market-oriented companies. These requirements are being superseded by the CSRD implementation through the HGB (Handelsgesetzbuch) amendments.

## ECHR & EU

### Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD)

Directive 2022/2464 (CSRD), adopted in December 2022, fundamentally transforms sustainability reporting in the EU:

**Scope:** Applies to all large EU companies (meeting two of three criteria: 250+ employees, EUR 50 million+ turnover, EUR 25 million+ total assets), all listed companies (except listed micro-enterprises), and non-EU companies with EUR 150 million+ EU turnover and an EU subsidiary or branch.

**Standards:** Companies must report under the European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS), developed by the European Financial Reporting Advisory Group (EFRAG). The first set of ESRS was adopted in July 2023 covering environment, social, and governance topics.

**Double Materiality:** The CSRD introduces a "double materiality" perspective: companies must report both on how sustainability matters affect the company (financial materiality) and how the company affects people and the environment (impact materiality).

**Assurance:** Sustainability reports must be subject to limited assurance by an independent auditor, with the Commission assessing the feasibility of moving to reasonable assurance by 2028.

### Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD)

Directive 2024/1760 (CSDDD), adopted June 13, 2024, imposes mandatory human rights and environmental due diligence:

**Civil Liability (Article 29):** Companies are liable for damages when they fail to comply with due diligence obligations and the failure leads to an adverse impact that should have been identified, prevented, mitigated, or brought to an end. This represents a breakthrough in holding companies legally accountable for value chain impacts.

**Climate Transition Plans (Article 22):** Companies must adopt and implement climate transition plans compatible with the 1.5-degree target of the Paris Agreement.

### EU Taxonomy Regulation (2020/852)

The Taxonomy Regulation establishes a classification system for identifying environmentally sustainable economic activities. Companies subject to CSRD reporting must disclose the proportion of their turnover, capital expenditure, and operating expenditure associated with taxonomy-aligned activities across six environmental objectives: climate change mitigation, climate change adaptation, sustainable use of water, transition to a circular economy, pollution prevention, and biodiversity protection.

### ECHR and Environmental Rights

**Klimaseniorinnen v. Switzerland (Application No. 53600/20, Grand Chamber Judgment of April 9, 2024):** The Grand Chamber held that Switzerland violated Article 8 by failing to adopt sufficient measures to combat climate change. This decision establishes that states have affirmative obligations under the Convention to address corporate contributions to climate change through adequate regulatory frameworks.

## Comparative Analysis

### Mandatory vs. Voluntary Frameworks

The jurisdictions span a spectrum from mandatory to voluntary CSR approaches:

**Most Mandatory (EU/Germany):** The CSRD, CSDDD, and LkSG create comprehensive mandatory obligations with civil liability provisions and administrative enforcement.

**Increasingly Mandatory (South Korea):** Korea is transitioning from voluntary K-ESG guidelines to mandatory disclosure, with criminal liability for safety failures under the Serious Accidents Act.

**Fragmented (United States):** Federal ESG regulation faces legal and political challenges, while state-level mandates (California) and anti-ESG laws (Texas, Florida) create a patchwork of contradictory requirements.

### Supply Chain Accountability

Germany's LkSG and the EU's CSDDD represent the global frontier of supply chain due diligence legislation. The key difference is that the CSDDD includes civil liability for supply chain failures, while the LkSG relies on administrative enforcement. The United States has targeted legislation (Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act) but no comprehensive supply chain due diligence mandate.

### Climate Litigation

All four jurisdictions are witnessing increasing climate litigation, but through different legal vehicles. The German Neubauer decision used constitutional rights. The ECHR's Klimaseniorinnen decision used human rights. American climate litigation uses tort law (nuisance, negligence). Korean climate litigation is emerging through administrative and constitutional challenges.
