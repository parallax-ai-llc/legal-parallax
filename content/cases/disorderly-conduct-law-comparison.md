## Summary

Disorderly conduct -- a broad category of offenses encompassing behavior that disturbs public peace, order, or decency -- serves as one of the most commonly charged criminal offenses worldwide, yet its definition varies dramatically across jurisdictions and raises persistent constitutional and human rights concerns regarding vagueness and potential for abuse against political protesters, minority communities, and marginalized groups. South Korea addresses disorderly conduct primarily through the Minor Offenses Act (경범죄처벌법), enacted in 1954 and most recently amended in 2021, which catalogues over 50 specific minor public order offenses including disorderly behavior in public places (공공장소에서의 소란행위), obstruction of passage (통행방해), and public obscenity, punishable by fines of up to 600,000 KRW (approximately $500), detention for up to 30 days, or a warning (경고); more serious public disturbance conduct may be prosecuted under Article 314 of the Criminal Act (형법) for obstruction of business (업무방해) or under the Assembly and Demonstration Act (집회 및 시위에 관한 법률). The United States treats disorderly conduct through state and local ordinances varying enormously in scope, with the Model Penal Code Section 250.2 defining it as engaging in fighting, threatening, or violent behavior, creating a hazardous or physically offensive condition, or making unreasonable noise, with the purpose of causing public inconvenience, annoyance, or alarm; the Supreme Court has struck down overly broad disorderly conduct statutes as unconstitutionally vague under the Fourteenth Amendment Due Process Clause, most notably in Coates v. City of Cincinnati, 402 U.S. 611 (1971), and has limited the criminalization of speech-based disorderly conduct through the "fighting words" doctrine of Chaplinsky v. New Hampshire, 315 U.S. 568 (1942). Germany addresses public order offenses through a combination of criminal provisions, including Section 123 StGB (Hausfriedensbruch/trespass), Section 125 StGB (Landfriedensbruch/breach of the public peace), and Section 183 StGB (exhibitionism), supplemented by the Ordnungswidrigkeitengesetz (OWiG) for less serious regulatory infractions; the German approach strictly distinguishes between criminal offenses and administrative infractions, with most minor disorderly conduct addressed through the OWiG rather than criminal prosecution. The ECHR has addressed disorderly conduct prosecutions extensively through Article 10 (freedom of expression) and Article 11 (freedom of assembly), with cases such as Ezelin v. France (Application No. 11800/85, 1991) establishing that criminal sanctions for disorderly conduct during protests must satisfy the strict necessity and proportionality requirements of the Convention.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

**Minor Offenses Act (경범죄처벌법, Act No. 300 of 1954, as amended):** This act serves as the primary vehicle for addressing disorderly conduct in South Korea. Article 3 enumerates specific offenses organized into categories:

**Public Disturbance Offenses:**
- Disorderly behavior in public places (공공장소에서의 소란행위)
- Obstruction of passage on roads or streets (통행방해)
- Making excessive noise (소음) during quiet hours
- Fighting or brawling in public (공공장소에서의 투전/난투)

**Public Decency Offenses:**
- Public urination or defecation (노상방뇨)
- Exposure of the body in a manner causing offense (과다노출)
- Public intoxication causing disturbance (음주소란)

**Nuisance Offenses:**
- Unauthorized posting of bills or advertisements
- Obstruction of public facilities
- Begging in an aggressive manner (구걸행위)

### Penalties

| Category | Penalty |
|----------|---------|
| Standard minor offense | Fine up to 600,000 KRW or detention up to 30 days |
| On-the-spot fine (범칙금) | 50,000-100,000 KRW (police discretion) |
| Warning (경고) | No monetary penalty; recorded |

### Obstruction of Business (업무방해)

More serious disorderly conduct may be prosecuted under Article 314 of the Criminal Act:

**Article 314 -- Obstruction of Business (업무방해):** "A person who obstructs the business of another by the means prescribed in Article 313 [fraud or intimidation] or by force shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than 5 years or a fine not exceeding 15 million KRW."

This provision has been controversially applied to labor strikes and protests. The Supreme Court held in Decision 2007도482 (2011) that certain forms of protest could constitute obstruction of business, drawing criticism from labor unions and human rights organizations.

### Assembly and Demonstration Act (집시법)

The Assembly and Demonstration Act (집회 및 시위에 관한 법률) regulates public assemblies and demonstrations, requiring advance notification to police and prohibiting assemblies within specified distances of government buildings, foreign embassies, and courts. Violations carry penalties of up to two years imprisonment or fines up to 2 million KRW.

The Constitutional Court of Korea (헌법재판소) has issued several significant decisions on the Act:

**Constitutional Court Decision 2003헌가2 (2003):** The Court upheld the prohibition on nighttime outdoor assemblies but ruled that the blanket ban was unconstitutional, requiring exceptions for peaceful assemblies.

**Constitutional Court Decision 2014헌가3 (2018):** The Court struck down the blanket prohibition on assemblies within 100 meters of the National Assembly building as an unconstitutional restriction on assembly rights.

## United States

### Legal Framework

**Model Penal Code Section 250.2 -- Disorderly Conduct:** "A person is guilty of disorderly conduct, a petty misdemeanor, if, with purpose to cause public inconvenience, annoyance or alarm, or recklessly creating a risk thereof, he: (a) engages in fighting or threatening, or in violent or tumultuous behavior; or (b) makes unreasonable noise or offensively coarse utterance, gesture or display, or addresses abusive language to any person present; or (c) creates a hazardous or physically offensive condition by any act which is not performed under any authorized license or permit."

### Constitutional Limitations

The Supreme Court has imposed significant constitutional limits on disorderly conduct statutes:

**Vagueness Doctrine:**

**Coates v. City of Cincinnati, 402 U.S. 611 (1971):** The Court struck down a Cincinnati ordinance making it a criminal offense for "three or more persons to assemble ... on any of the sidewalks ... and there conduct themselves in a manner annoying to persons passing by." The Court held the ordinance unconstitutionally vague and overbroad, as it provided no objective standard for what constituted "annoying" conduct.

**Papachristou v. City of Jacksonville, 405 U.S. 156 (1972):** The Court struck down a vagrancy ordinance that criminalized behaviors such as "rogues and vagabonds," "habitual loafers," and "persons wandering from place to place without any lawful purpose," holding it void for vagueness and incompatible with due process.

**Fighting Words Doctrine:**

**Chaplinsky v. New Hampshire, 315 U.S. 568 (1942):** The Court established that "fighting words" -- words that "by their very utterance, inflict injury or tend to incite an immediate breach of the peace" -- are not protected by the First Amendment. However, subsequent cases have dramatically narrowed this exception.

**Cohen v. California, 403 U.S. 15 (1971):** The Court reversed a disorderly conduct conviction for wearing a jacket bearing "Fuck the Draft" in a courthouse, holding that the government cannot punish the display of offensive words absent a showing of intent to incite imminent lawless action.

### Racial Disparities

Empirical studies have documented significant racial disparities in disorderly conduct enforcement:

The Vera Institute of Justice reported in 2018 that Black Americans are arrested for disorderly conduct at rates disproportionate to their share of the population. In New York City, analysis of NYPD data showed that the "disorderly conduct" charge was disproportionately applied in communities of color.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany distinguishes sharply between criminal offenses (Straftaten) and administrative infractions (Ordnungswidrigkeiten):

### Criminal Provisions (StGB)

**Section 123 -- Trespass (Hausfriedensbruch):** "Whoever unlawfully enters the dwelling, business premises or enclosed property of another, or the enclosed premises designated for public service or transport, or whoever remains therein without authorization after being ordered to leave, shall be punished with imprisonment for a term not exceeding one year or a fine." This provision is relevant to disorderly conduct in specific locations.

**Section 125 -- Breach of the Public Peace (Landfriedensbruch):** "Whoever, as a perpetrator or participant, (1) commits acts of violence against persons or property or (2) threatens persons with acts of violence from a crowd that commits such acts with united force and thereby endangers public safety, shall be punished with imprisonment for a term not exceeding three years or a fine, unless the act is punishable under other provisions with a more severe penalty."

**Section 183 -- Exhibitionism (Exhibitionistische Handlungen):** Addresses public exposure; punishable by imprisonment up to one year or a fine.

**Section 185 -- Insult (Beleidigung):** "Insult shall be punished with imprisonment for a term not exceeding two years or a fine." This broad provision covers verbal abuse, obscene gestures, and other conduct that would be classified as disorderly conduct in common law jurisdictions.

### Administrative Infractions (OWiG)

The Ordnungswidrigkeitengesetz provides for administrative fines for minor public order violations without criminal conviction:

| Infraction | Typical Fine |
|-----------|-------------|
| Excessive noise (Ruhestörung) | €5-1,000 |
| Minor public nuisance | €5-500 |
| Violation of local ordinances | Varies by municipality |

### Assembly Law (Versammlungsgesetz)

The federal Versammlungsgesetz (Assembly Act) and state-level assembly laws regulate public demonstrations. Article 8 of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz) guarantees the right to peaceful assembly without registration for indoor assemblies and with notification (not permission) for outdoor assemblies.

**BVerfG, Brokdorf Decision (May 14, 1985, 1 BvR 233/81):** The Federal Constitutional Court established the foundational principle that assembly rights must be broadly interpreted and that restrictions are permissible only to prevent immediate dangers to public safety. This decision established that:
- The right to assemble is a fundamental component of democratic self-governance
- Authorities must pursue a cooperation-based approach (kooperativer Ansatz) with demonstrators
- Criminal sanctions for protest-related conduct must be a last resort

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR has developed extensive jurisprudence on disorderly conduct in the context of political protest and freedom of expression:

**Article 10 -- Freedom of Expression:**

**Ezelin v. France (Application No. 11800/85, Judgment of April 26, 1991):** The Court held that the disciplinary sanction imposed on a lawyer for participating in a demonstration during which some participants engaged in disorderly conduct violated Article 10. The Court established that "the freedom to take part in a peaceful assembly is of such importance that it cannot be restricted in any way ... so long as the person concerned does not himself commit any reprehensible act on such an occasion."

**Savda v. Turkey (Application No. 42730/05, Judgment of June 12, 2012):** The Court found a violation of Article 10 where a conscientious objector was convicted of disorderly conduct for publicly declaring his refusal to perform military service.

**Article 11 -- Freedom of Assembly:**

**Kudrevičius and Others v. Lithuania (Application No. 37553/05, Grand Chamber Judgment of October 15, 2015):** The Grand Chamber addressed farmers' protests that blocked public roads, finding that while the disruption was intentional, the criminal sanctions imposed were disproportionate. The Court established that "even where demonstrators do not engage in acts of violence, it is open to the domestic authorities to deploy a certain degree of tolerance toward peaceful gatherings."

**Navalnyy and Yashin v. Russia (Application No. 76204/11, Judgment of December 4, 2014):** The Court found violations of Articles 10 and 11 where opposition activists were convicted of disorderly conduct for participating in political protests.

### EU Framework

The EU has not adopted specific harmonization instruments for disorderly conduct. However:

**EU Charter of Fundamental Rights, Article 12:** Guarantees freedom of assembly and association.

**EU Charter, Article 11:** Guarantees freedom of expression and information.

These Charter provisions apply when member states implement EU law and may be invoked in challenges to disproportionate disorderly conduct enforcement.

## Comparative Analysis

### Breadth of Criminalization

The jurisdictions show significant variation in the scope of disorderly conduct offenses:

- **South Korea:** Extensive enumeration of specific minor offenses (50+ categories)
- **United States:** Broad, flexible definitions vulnerable to vagueness challenges
- **Germany:** Sharp criminal/administrative distinction; narrower criminal scope
- **ECHR:** Strong proportionality constraints on criminalization

### Protest-Related Enforcement

The treatment of protest-related disorderly conduct reveals fundamental differences in the balance between public order and civil liberties:

- **South Korea:** Assembly and Demonstration Act imposes prior notification requirements and geographic restrictions
- **United States:** First Amendment provides strong but not absolute protection; time/place/manner restrictions permitted
- **Germany:** Strong constitutional protection for assembly; cooperation-based approach
- **ECHR:** Criminal sanctions for peaceful protest conduct must satisfy strict necessity and proportionality

### Discriminatory Enforcement

All jurisdictions face challenges with discriminatory enforcement of disorderly conduct laws, which grant significant police discretion:

- **United States:** Documented racial disparities in arrest rates
- **South Korea:** Concerns about selective enforcement against labor activists and political dissenters
- **Germany:** OWiG enforcement concerns regarding minority communities
- **ECHR:** Article 14 prohibits discriminatory application of otherwise lawful restrictions
