## Summary

Identity theft -- the fraudulent acquisition, possession, or use of another person's personal identifying information without authorization -- has become one of the most pervasive criminal phenomena of the digital age. Legal frameworks addressing identity theft vary significantly across jurisdictions in scope, classification, and severity. South Korea addresses identity theft through a combination of the Criminal Act (형법), the Personal Information Protection Act (개인정보 보호법, PIPA), and the Resident Registration Act (주민등록법), imposing penalties of up to ten years imprisonment for fraud-based identity crimes and up to five years for unauthorized use of personal information. The United States treats identity theft as a standalone federal offense under 18 U.S.C. sections 1028 and 1028A, with penalties reaching fifteen years imprisonment for basic identity fraud and a mandatory consecutive two-year sentence for aggravated identity theft, alongside a comprehensive network of state-level statutes. Germany lacks a specific "identity theft" offense but prosecutes such conduct under multiple provisions of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), including fraud (section 263), computer fraud (section 263a), data espionage (section 202a), and forgery offenses (sections 267-271), with penalties of up to ten years for serious fraud cases. At the supranational level, the European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR) Article 8 imposes positive obligations on states to protect individuals from identity-related violations, while EU Directive 2013/40/EU on Attacks against Information Systems explicitly recognizes identity theft as an aggravating circumstance in cybercrime, and the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) provides a robust data protection framework that underpins identity theft prevention and victim remediation across all EU member states.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea does not have a single, standalone identity theft statute. Instead, identity theft is prosecuted under a combination of criminal and regulatory provisions that together form a comprehensive framework.

#### Criminal Act (형법)

The core criminal provisions applicable to identity theft are found in the Korean Criminal Act:

- **Article 347 -- Fraud (사기):** A person who defrauds another, thereby obtaining property or pecuniary advantage, shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than ten years or by a fine not exceeding 20 million won (approximately $15,000). Using a stolen identity to conduct fraudulent transactions falls squarely within this provision.
- **Article 347-2 -- Computer Fraud (컴퓨터등 사용사기):** Any person who acquires pecuniary benefits by inputting false information or improper commands, or inputting or altering data without authority, into a computer or other data processor shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than ten years or a fine not exceeding 20 million won. This provision is frequently invoked in digital identity theft cases involving online banking or e-commerce fraud.
- **Articles 231-234 -- Forgery of Private Documents:** Creating documents by assuming the identity of another person constitutes forgery of private documents, punishable by imprisonment for up to five years or a fine of up to 10 million won. Submitting such forged documents is separately punishable under Article 234 (use of forged private documents).

#### Resident Registration Act (주민등록법)

South Korea's Resident Registration Number (주민등록번호, RRN) system assigns a unique 13-digit identifier to every citizen and registered resident. Given its centrality to Korean life -- used for banking, telecommunications, healthcare, and government services -- the misuse of RRNs is specifically criminalized:

- **Article 37(10):** Any person who uses another person's resident registration number shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than three years or a fine not exceeding 10 million won.
- The law was significantly strengthened following widespread public complaints about RRN-based identity theft. The government implemented stiff penalties and expanded enforcement to cover online misuse of RRNs.

#### Personal Information Protection Act (개인정보 보호법, PIPA)

PIPA, first enacted in 2011 and significantly amended in 2020, 2023, and most recently in February 2026, provides the overarching data protection framework:

- **Unauthorized Use or Provision of Personal Information:** Imprisonment of up to five years or a fine of up to 50 million won.
- **Obtaining Personal Information Through Deception, Improper Means, or Coercion:** Imprisonment of up to three years or a fine of up to 30 million won.
- **2026 Amendment:** The National Assembly on February 12, 2026, authorized administrative fines of up to 10% of a company's total revenue for high-severity data breach cases involving intentional or grossly negligent conduct, repeat violations within three years, or breaches affecting 10 million or more individuals.

### Landmark Cases

**Auction.co.kr Data Breach (2008):** Hackers compromised the personal data of approximately 10.8 million users of South Korea's major auction platform, including names, RRNs, and bank account information. The case was among the first major identity theft incidents to prompt legislative reform and led to the strengthening of data protection enforcement.

**KT Corporation Data Breach (2012):** The personal information of approximately 8.7 million KT telecom customers was stolen and sold to telemarketing firms. The perpetrators were convicted under the Criminal Act and the former Information and Communications Network Act, receiving sentences of imprisonment ranging from two to four years.

**Resident Registration Number Reform (2014):** Following a series of large-scale data breaches affecting over 100 million records across financial institutions and telecom companies, the government prohibited the collection of RRNs by private entities unless specifically authorized by law, fundamentally changing Korea's identity verification infrastructure.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has the most developed standalone identity theft legal framework among the jurisdictions surveyed, combining federal statutes, all fifty state criminal codes, and comprehensive consumer protection mechanisms.

#### Federal Statutes

**18 U.S.C. section 1028 -- Fraud and Related Activity in Connection with Identification Documents:**

This is the primary federal identity theft statute, criminalizing the production, transfer, possession, and use of fraudulent identification documents and stolen personal information. The statute defines "means of identification" broadly to include names, Social Security numbers, dates of birth, driver's license numbers, bank account numbers, credit card numbers, biometric data, unique electronic identification numbers, and telecommunications access devices.

Penalties under section 1028 vary based on the circumstances:

| Offense Category | Maximum Penalty |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| Basic identity fraud | Up to 15 years imprisonment |
| Connection to drug trafficking or violent crime; prior conviction | Up to 20 years imprisonment |
| Connection to domestic or international terrorism | Up to 30 years imprisonment |
| Facilitating an act of international terrorism | Up to 30 years imprisonment |

**18 U.S.C. section 1028A -- Aggravated Identity Theft:**

Enacted by the Identity Theft Penalty Enhancement Act of 2004, this provision imposes a mandatory consecutive sentence on top of any sentence for a predicate felony:

- **Two years** mandatory consecutive imprisonment for using another person's identity during the commission of an enumerated felony (including wire fraud, bank fraud, immigration fraud, and mail fraud).
- **Five years** mandatory consecutive imprisonment when the predicate offense is terrorism-related.

This sentence cannot run concurrently with any other term and cannot be reduced through probation or early release.

**Identity Theft and Assumption Deterrence Act (1998):** Made it a federal crime to knowingly transfer or use, without lawful authority, a means of identification of another person with the intent to commit, aid, or abet any unlawful activity. Previously, federal law criminalized the production and possession of false identity documents but not the theft of another person's identifying information per se.

#### Consumer Protection Framework

**Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA), as amended by FACTA (2003):** Requires consumer reporting agencies and creditors to assist identity theft victims through fraud alerts, credit freezes, and free annual credit reports. Victims have the right to dispute inaccurate information resulting from identity theft, and agencies must investigate and correct such information.

**Fair and Accurate Credit Transactions Act (FACTA, 2003):** Added identity theft provisions to the FCRA, including requirements for financial institutions to develop identity theft prevention programs (the "Red Flags Rule"), truncation of credit card numbers on receipts, and procedures for blocking information resulting from identity theft from consumer reports.

**FTC Enforcement:** The Federal Trade Commission operates the IdentityTheft.gov platform for victims to report incidents and receive personalized recovery plans. The FTC also brings enforcement actions against companies whose inadequate data security practices contribute to identity theft.

#### State Laws

All fifty states and the District of Columbia have enacted identity theft criminal statutes. Penalties vary considerably, from misdemeanor treatment for minor offenses to felony penalties of up to twenty years in some states. Many states have also enacted data breach notification laws requiring entities to notify affected individuals when personal information is compromised.

### Landmark Cases

**Flores-Figueroa v. United States, 556 U.S. 646 (2009):** The Supreme Court unanimously held that prosecution under 18 U.S.C. section 1028A (aggravated identity theft) requires proof that the defendant knew that the means of identification he used actually belonged to another real person. Ignacio Flores-Figueroa, an undocumented immigrant, had used forged documents containing Social Security and alien registration numbers that happened to belong to real people, but he did not know this. Justice Breyer, writing for the Court, held that the word "knowingly" in the statute applies to all elements of the offense, including that the identification belonged to "another person." The decision established a critical mens rea standard for identity theft prosecution.

**United States v. Cotterman, 709 F.3d 952 (9th Cir. 2013) (en banc):** The Ninth Circuit held that border searches of electronic devices containing personal data require reasonable suspicion when they involve forensic examination, recognizing the privacy implications of identity-related digital information.

**Equifax Data Breach (2017):** The breach of approximately 147 million consumer records, including Social Security numbers, birth dates, and addresses, resulted in a $575 million settlement with the FTC, the largest data breach settlement at the time. The incident led to renewed calls for comprehensive federal data privacy legislation.

| Statute | Maximum Imprisonment | Fines |
|---------|---------------------|-------|
| 18 U.S.C. section 1028 (basic) | 15 years | Up to $250,000 |
| 18 U.S.C. section 1028 (drug/violence nexus) | 20 years | Up to $250,000 |
| 18 U.S.C. section 1028 (terrorism nexus) | 30 years | Up to $250,000 |
| 18 U.S.C. section 1028A (aggravated) | +2 years mandatory consecutive | -- |

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany does not have a standalone identity theft offense in its criminal code. Instead, identity theft is prosecuted through a combination of existing criminal provisions in the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), supplemented by the comprehensive data protection framework of the GDPR as implemented through the Bundesdatenschutzgesetz (BDSG, Federal Data Protection Act).

#### Criminal Code (Strafgesetzbuch -- StGB)

**Section 263 -- Fraud (Betrug):**

The principal provision used to prosecute identity theft. Whoever, with the intention of obtaining an unlawful pecuniary advantage for himself or a third party, damages the property of another by creating or maintaining a mistake by false pretenses, distortion, or suppression of facts, shall be punished:

- **Basic offense:** Imprisonment for up to five years or a fine.
- **Particularly serious cases (section 263(3)):** Imprisonment from six months to ten years. Particularly serious cases include acting on a commercial basis, causing large-scale property damage, or exploiting a position of trust.

**Section 263a -- Computer Fraud (Computerbetrug):**

Specifically targets manipulation of data-processing operations. A person who, with the intention of obtaining an unlawful pecuniary advantage, influences the result of a data-processing operation by incorrectly configuring a program, using incorrect or incomplete data, making unauthorized use of data, or otherwise exerting unauthorized influence on the process is punished with imprisonment for up to five years or a fine. The critical distinction from section 263 is that no human person need be deceived -- only a data-processing system is manipulated.

- **Preparatory acts (section 263a(3)):** Producing, procuring, offering, or distributing computer programs designed for the commission of computer fraud is punishable by imprisonment for up to three years or a fine.

**Sections 202a and 202b -- Data Espionage and Data Interception (Ausspähen und Abfangen von Daten):**

- **Section 202a (Data Espionage):** Whoever obtains without authorization data that are not intended for him and are specially protected against unauthorized access shall be punished by imprisonment for up to three years or a fine. This covers the initial acquisition of personal data used in identity theft.
- **Section 202b (Data Interception):** Whoever unlawfully intercepts data not intended for him from a non-public data transmission or from the electromagnetic emissions of a data-processing system shall be punished by imprisonment for up to two years or a fine.
- **Section 202c (Preparatory Acts):** Producing, procuring, selling, or distributing tools designed for data espionage or interception is punishable by imprisonment for up to two years or a fine.

**Sections 267-271 -- Forgery Offenses (Urkundenfälschung):**

- **Section 267 (Forgery of Documents):** Producing a false document, falsifying a genuine document, or using a falsified document is punishable by imprisonment for up to five years or a fine. In particularly serious cases (commercial activity, large-scale damage), the penalty increases to six months to ten years.
- **Section 269 (Forgery of Data Intended to Provide Proof):** The digital equivalent of document forgery; storing or altering data that would be considered a falsified document if it were physical is punishable by imprisonment for up to five years or a fine.
- **Section 270 (Deception in Legal Transactions by Data Processing):** Clarifies that sections 267-269 apply equally to automated data processing in legal transactions.

#### Data Protection Framework

The GDPR, directly applicable in Germany, and the BDSG provide additional enforcement mechanisms:

- **GDPR Article 83:** Administrative fines of up to EUR 20 million or 4% of total worldwide annual turnover for violations of data protection principles.
- **GDPR Article 82:** Right to compensation for material or non-material damage resulting from data protection violations.
- **BDSG section 42:** Criminal penalties for unauthorized commercial use or fraudulent acquisition of personal data, punishable by imprisonment for up to three years or a fine.

### Landmark Cases

**Braunschweig Higher Regional Court -- Bitcoin Theft Decision (July 11, 2025):** The court ruled that Bitcoin does not qualify as a "thing" (Sache) under section 242 StGB and thus cannot be the subject of theft. However, the court explicitly clarified that unauthorized acquisition of Bitcoin may be prosecuted as computer fraud under section 263a StGB when it involves deception or manipulation of electronic data, including through identity theft techniques. This decision has significant implications for the prosecution of cryptocurrency-related identity fraud.

**LG Duisburg Decision (April 5, 2017):** The Duisburg Regional Court applied sections 263a and 202a StGB in a case involving large-scale phishing operations targeting German bank customers. The defendants used stolen identity data to manipulate online banking systems, and the court imposed sentences of three to five years imprisonment, applying the aggravated fraud provisions due to the commercial scale of the operation.

**Federal Constitutional Court -- Online Searches (BVerfG, 1 BvR 370/07, February 27, 2008):** The court established a fundamental right to the confidentiality and integrity of information technology systems (Grundrecht auf Gewährleistung der Vertraulichkeit und Integrität informationstechnischer Systeme), derived from the general personality right. This decision has significant implications for identity theft prosecution, as it established constitutional limits on state surveillance while affirming the importance of protecting digital identity.

## ECHR & EU

### Legal Framework

The European framework for addressing identity theft operates on multiple levels: the European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR) provides fundamental rights protections, EU directives establish harmonized criminal law standards, and the GDPR creates a comprehensive data protection regime.

#### European Convention on Human Rights -- Article 8

Article 8 of the ECHR guarantees the right to respect for private and family life, home, and correspondence. The European Court of Human Rights has consistently interpreted this provision as encompassing protection of personal identity and personal data, imposing both negative obligations (states must not interfere with identity rights) and positive obligations (states must take affirmative steps to protect individuals from identity violations by private parties).

Key principles established through case law:

- **Personal Identity as a Core Component of Private Life:** The Court has held that respect for private life requires that everyone should be able to establish details of their identity as individual human beings, and that authorities should not obstruct access to basic identity information without specific justification (Gaskin v. United Kingdom, 1989).
- **Positive Obligation to Protect Digital Identity:** In K.U. v. Finland (2008), the Court held that states must provide effective legal mechanisms to identify persons who misuse others' identities online. The case involved an advertisement placed on a dating site in the name of a twelve-year-old boy. Finland was found in violation of Article 8 because its domestic law prevented the identification of the perpetrator.
- **Data Protection as an Element of Article 8:** The Court has recognized that the protection of personal data is of fundamental importance to a person's enjoyment of the right to respect for private and family life (S. and Marper v. United Kingdom, 2008).

#### EU Directive 2013/40/EU -- Attacks against Information Systems

This directive, adopted on August 12, 2013, establishes minimum rules concerning the definition of criminal offenses and sanctions in the area of attacks against information systems. It explicitly addresses identity theft as an aggravating circumstance:

- **Article 9(5):** Member states must ensure that committing an offense "by misusing the personal data of another person, with the aim of gaining the trust of a third party, thereby causing prejudice to the rightful identity owner" is regarded as an aggravating circumstance.
- **Minimum Maximum Penalties:** The directive requires member states to provide for maximum terms of imprisonment of at least two years for illegal system interference and at least three years for offenses committed using botnets or causing serious damage. For aggravated offenses (including those involving identity theft), the minimum maximum penalty is at least five years.

Member states were required to transpose the directive into national law by September 4, 2015.

#### General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR -- Regulation 2016/679)

The GDPR, applicable since May 25, 2018, provides the overarching data protection framework relevant to identity theft across all EU/EEA member states:

- **Recital 75:** Explicitly identifies "identity theft or identity fraud" as a type of harm that may result from the processing of personal data, requiring appropriate security measures.
- **Article 5(1)(f) -- Integrity and Confidentiality:** Personal data must be processed in a manner ensuring appropriate security, including protection against unauthorized processing and accidental loss, using appropriate technical and organizational measures.
- **Article 33 -- Breach Notification:** Data controllers must notify the supervisory authority within 72 hours of becoming aware of a personal data breach likely to result in a risk to natural persons' rights and freedoms.
- **Article 34 -- Communication to Data Subjects:** When a breach is likely to result in a high risk to rights and freedoms (including identity theft), the data controller must communicate the breach to affected individuals without undue delay.
- **Articles 77-82 -- Remedies and Compensation:** Data subjects have the right to lodge complaints with supervisory authorities, seek judicial remedies, and obtain compensation for material or non-material damage resulting from GDPR violations, including those leading to identity theft.

The Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU) has clarified in multiple rulings that the mere unauthorized access to personal data does not automatically constitute "identity theft" under the GDPR; the term requires active misuse of data to assume another person's identity, consistent with the concept of "identity fraud."

#### NIS2 Directive (Directive 2022/2555)

While primarily focused on cybersecurity requirements for critical infrastructure, the NIS2 Directive (effective January 2023, transposition deadline October 17, 2024) strengthens the organizational security framework that underpins identity theft prevention. It requires essential and important entities to implement risk management measures, report significant incidents, and cooperate with national authorities -- all of which contribute to the prevention and detection of large-scale identity theft schemes.

#### Budapest Convention on Cybercrime (2001)

The Council of Europe's Convention on Cybercrime remains the primary international treaty framework. While it does not specifically define identity theft as a standalone offense, its provisions on computer-related fraud (Article 8), computer-related forgery (Article 7), and illegal access (Article 2) provide the basis for cross-border cooperation in identity theft investigations. As of 2025, 81 states are parties to the convention.

### Landmark ECHR and CJEU Cases

**K.U. v. Finland (Application No. 2872/02, 2008):** The Court unanimously found a violation of Article 8 where Finnish law prevented the identification of a person who had placed a sexual advertisement in the name of a twelve-year-old boy on an internet dating site. The Court held that states have a positive obligation to ensure that effective criminal law mechanisms exist to protect individuals, particularly children, from identity misuse online.

**Gaskin v. United Kingdom (Application No. 10454/83, 1989):** The Court held that respect for private life requires that individuals be able to establish details of their identity, and that procedures denying access to personal identity records must include an independent review mechanism. While not an identity theft case per se, the decision established that personal identity information is protected under Article 8.

**S. and Marper v. United Kingdom (Application Nos. 30562/04 and 30566/04, 2008):** The Grand Chamber held that the indefinite retention of fingerprints, cellular samples, and DNA profiles of persons not convicted of any offense violated Article 8. The decision emphasized that personal data, including biometric identifiers, is fundamentally connected to identity and must be protected from misuse.

**CJEU -- Natsionalna agentsia za prihodite (Case C-340/21, 2023):** The CJEU clarified that the fear of potential misuse of personal data following a data breach may itself constitute non-material damage under GDPR Article 82, even absent actual identity theft. However, the mere breach does not create an irrebuttable presumption of inadequate security measures.

## Comparative Analysis

### Structural Approaches

The four jurisdictions represent three distinct structural approaches to identity theft criminalization:

1. **Standalone Offense Model (United States):** The US treats identity theft as a distinct criminal offense through dedicated statutes (18 U.S.C. sections 1028 and 1028A), providing clear definitional boundaries, graduated penalties based on severity and nexus to other crimes, and a mandatory enhancement mechanism (aggravated identity theft). This approach offers prosecutorial clarity but may create gaps where conduct does not fit neatly within statutory definitions.

2. **Composite Offense Model (South Korea, Germany):** Both South Korea and Germany prosecute identity theft through existing criminal provisions -- primarily fraud, computer fraud, forgery, and data protection violations. This approach provides flexibility to adapt to novel forms of identity misuse but can result in inconsistent treatment and may undervalue the distinctive harm of identity theft as compared to other property crimes.

3. **Framework Harmonization Model (ECHR/EU):** The EU relies on directives to establish minimum standards that member states must implement through national legislation, combined with directly applicable regulations (GDPR) for data protection. This approach accommodates diverse legal traditions while establishing baseline protections, but enforcement varies significantly across member states.

### Penalty Severity

The United States imposes the most severe penalties for identity theft among the surveyed jurisdictions, with maximum imprisonment of up to thirty years for terrorism-related identity fraud and mandatory consecutive sentences for aggravated identity theft. Germany's penalties are comparatively moderate, with a general maximum of five years for basic fraud and ten years for aggravated cases. South Korea falls between the two, with a ten-year maximum for fraud-based identity theft. The EU framework requires minimum maximum penalties of at least five years for aggravated cases involving identity misuse, but actual penalties vary across member states.

### Mens Rea Standards

The Flores-Figueroa decision in the United States established a rigorous knowledge requirement: the prosecution must prove the defendant knew the identification belonged to a real person. South Korea and Germany apply general intent (dolus) requirements for their respective fraud and forgery provisions, but do not impose the same heightened knowledge standard for the identity element specifically.

### Victim Remediation

The United States offers the most developed victim remediation framework, with statutory rights to fraud alerts, credit freezes, free credit reports, and personalized recovery plans through the FTC. The GDPR provides a comprehensive right to compensation for both material and non-material damage across EU member states. South Korea's PIPA grants data subject rights including notification and correction, but the remediation infrastructure for identity theft victims is less formalized. Germany relies primarily on GDPR rights and general civil tort claims under the Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB).

### Unique National Identifier Vulnerability

South Korea's Resident Registration Number system presents a unique vulnerability not present in the same form in other surveyed jurisdictions. While the US Social Security Number serves a similar function, South Korea's RRN was historically more deeply integrated into daily life, serving as the universal key for banking, telecommunications, healthcare, and online verification. The legislative reforms since 2014, restricting private-sector collection of RRNs, represent a significant structural response to this systemic vulnerability.
