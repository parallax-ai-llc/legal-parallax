## Summary

Universal jurisdiction -- the principle that a state may exercise criminal jurisdiction over individuals accused of certain grave international crimes regardless of where the crime was committed and regardless of the nationality of the perpetrator or victim -- represents one of the most expansive and controversial doctrines in international criminal law, challenging traditional notions of territorial sovereignty while seeking to ensure accountability for the most serious offenses against international law. South Korea provides a limited framework for universal jurisdiction through Article 296-2 of the Criminal Act (형법), which addresses crimes against humanity, and the Act on Punishment of Crimes under Jurisdiction of the International Criminal Court (국제형사재판소 관할 범죄의 처벌 등에 관한 법률, ICC Crimes Act) enacted in 2007, which domestically implements the Rome Statute obligations and extends Korean criminal jurisdiction to genocide, crimes against humanity, and war crimes committed outside Korean territory. The United States exercises universal jurisdiction through a limited set of federal statutes targeting specific international crimes, including the Alien Tort Statute (28 U.S.C. Section 1350) for civil claims, the Torture Victim Protection Act of 1991, the Military Extraterritorial Jurisdiction Act (MEJA), and the War Crimes Act (18 U.S.C. Section 2441), though the Supreme Court's decisions in Kiobel v. Royal Dutch Petroleum (2013) and Nestlé USA v. Doe (2021) have significantly narrowed the scope of civil universal jurisdiction under the ATS. Germany has emerged as the global leader in exercising universal jurisdiction, with the Völkerstrafgesetzbuch (Code of Crimes against International Law, VStGB) of 2002 establishing comprehensive jurisdiction over genocide, crimes against humanity, and war crimes regardless of any territorial or nationality connection to Germany, and federal prosecutors having brought landmark structural investigation cases against Syrian regime officials, including the historic conviction of Anwar Raslan in the Koblenz trial (2022). At the European level, the ECHR has addressed universal jurisdiction primarily through the lens of Article 6 (fair trial) and Article 7 (no punishment without law), the EU has encouraged member states to exercise universal jurisdiction through Joint Investigation Teams and Eurojust coordination, and Belgium's controversial 1993 universal jurisdiction law and its subsequent amendment under international pressure illustrate the political complexities of the doctrine.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's universal jurisdiction framework is anchored in the Act on Punishment of Crimes under Jurisdiction of the International Criminal Court (국제형사재판소 관할 범죄의 처벌 등에 관한 법률), enacted on December 21, 2007, implementing South Korea's obligations under the Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court, which Korea ratified on November 13, 2002.

The ICC Crimes Act defines and criminalizes genocide (Articles 8-9), crimes against humanity (Articles 9-10), and war crimes (Articles 11-14) in Korean domestic law. Article 3 extends the Act's territorial scope to crimes committed outside Korean territory, establishing a form of universal jurisdiction, though in practice the exercise of this jurisdiction typically requires some nexus to Korea, such as the suspect's presence in Korean territory.

### Criminal Act Provisions

Article 296-2 of the Criminal Act (형법), added by amendment, addresses crimes against humanity. The general principles of Korean criminal jurisdiction are set out in Articles 2-7 of the Criminal Act: Article 2 establishes territorial jurisdiction; Article 3 extends jurisdiction to crimes committed by Korean nationals abroad; Article 5 establishes jurisdiction over certain crimes committed against Korea regardless of the perpetrator's nationality; and Article 6 covers crimes committed by foreigners against Korean nationals abroad.

### Practical Application

South Korea has had limited practical experience exercising universal jurisdiction. The country's focus has been more on cooperation with the ICC and other international tribunals than on conducting its own universal jurisdiction prosecutions. However, the legal framework exists for such prosecutions, and civil society organizations have advocated for more active exercise of universal jurisdiction, particularly in relation to North Korean human rights abuses.

### Penalties

| Crime | Penalty under ICC Crimes Act |
|-------|------------------------------|
| Genocide | Death or life imprisonment; minimum 5 years |
| Crimes against humanity (murder, extermination) | Death or life imprisonment; minimum 5 years |
| Crimes against humanity (other acts) | Imprisonment from 3 years to life |
| War crimes (grave breaches) | Death or life imprisonment; minimum 5 years |
| War crimes (other serious violations) | Imprisonment from 1 year to life |
| Command responsibility | Same as underlying crime |

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States exercises universal jurisdiction through a fragmented set of federal statutes, each targeting specific categories of international crimes.

**Alien Tort Statute (ATS), 28 U.S.C. Section 1350:** Originally enacted as part of the Judiciary Act of 1789, the ATS provides that "the district courts shall have original jurisdiction of any civil action by an alien for a tort only, committed in violation of the law of nations or a treaty of the United States." The ATS was revived in Filártiga v. Peña-Irala, 630 F.2d 876 (2d Cir. 1980), which held that federal courts have jurisdiction over civil claims for torture committed abroad.

**Kiobel v. Royal Dutch Petroleum, 569 U.S. 108 (2013):** The Supreme Court held that the presumption against extraterritoriality applies to the ATS, and that claims under the ATS must "touch and concern" the territory of the United States with "sufficient force" to displace the presumption. This decision substantially limited the scope of ATS litigation.

**Nestlé USA, Inc. v. Doe, 593 U.S. 628 (2021):** The Supreme Court further narrowed ATS claims, holding that mere corporate presence in the United States is insufficient to sustain an ATS claim based on overseas conduct.

**War Crimes Act, 18 U.S.C. Section 2441:** Criminalizes grave breaches of the Geneva Conventions and other specified war crimes committed by or against US nationals or members of the US armed forces.

**Torture Victim Protection Act (TVPA, 1991):** Provides a civil cause of action for individuals who were subjected to torture or extrajudicial killing by an individual acting under color of law of a foreign nation.

**Genocide Convention Implementation Act (18 U.S.C. Section 1091):** Criminalizes genocide committed within the United States or by US nationals abroad. The "found in" provision was added by amendment, extending jurisdiction where the alleged offender is found in the United States.

### Notable Cases

**United States v. Chuckie Taylor (2008):** Charles "Chuckie" Taylor, son of former Liberian President Charles Taylor, was convicted in Miami under the federal Torture Act (18 U.S.C. Section 2340A) for torture committed in Liberia. This was the first conviction under the federal torture statute.

**Jesner v. Arab Bank (2018):** The Supreme Court held that foreign corporations are not subject to ATS liability, further narrowing the scope of ATS-based universal jurisdiction.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany has established itself as the world's most active practitioner of universal jurisdiction through the Völkerstrafgesetzbuch (Code of Crimes against International Law, VStGB), enacted on June 26, 2002, and effective from the same date. The VStGB was enacted simultaneously with Germany's ratification of the Rome Statute to create a comprehensive domestic legal framework for prosecuting international crimes.

**Section 1 VStGB (Principle of Universal Jurisdiction):** The VStGB applies to offenses described therein "even when the offense was committed abroad and bears no relation to Germany" (auch dann, wenn die Tat im Ausland begangen wurde und keinen Bezug zum Inland aufweist). This establishes pure universal jurisdiction without any nexus requirement -- the broadest formulation of the principle in any national legal system.

**Structural Investigations:** The Federal Prosecutor General (Generalbundesanwalt, GBA) pioneered the concept of "structural investigations" (Strukturermittlungsverfahren), which involve collecting and preserving evidence about international crimes even before specific suspects are identified or apprehended. These investigations focus on understanding the organizational structure and command responsibility within state or armed group hierarchies.

### The Koblenz Trial -- Al-Khatib Case

The trial of Anwar Raslan and Eyad al-Gharib before the Oberlandesgericht (Higher Regional Court) Koblenz represented the world's first criminal trial of a former Syrian government official for crimes against humanity.

**Eyad al-Gharib:** Convicted on February 24, 2021, of aiding and abetting crimes against humanity (torture and deprivation of liberty) for his role as a member of the Syrian General Intelligence Directorate (Branch 251). Sentenced to 4 years and 6 months imprisonment.

**Anwar Raslan:** Convicted on January 13, 2022, of crimes against humanity including torture, murder (27 counts), sexual violence, and deprivation of liberty committed as head of the investigation division at Branch 251 in Damascus. Sentenced to life imprisonment. The conviction was the first worldwide for state-sponsored torture in Syria.

### ZBKV -- Specialized Investigation Unit

The Zentralstelle für die Bekämpfung von Kriegsverbrechen und weiterer Straftaten nach dem Völkerstrafgesetzbuch (Central Unit for the Fight against War Crimes, ZBKV), established within the Federal Criminal Police Office (BKA), serves as the specialized investigation unit for VStGB offenses. As of 2024, the ZBKV maintained structural investigations concerning Syria, Iraq, The Gambia, and other situations.

### Penalties

| Crime under VStGB | Penalty |
|-------------------|---------|
| Genocide (Section 6) | Life imprisonment |
| Crimes against humanity -- murder (Section 7(1) No. 1) | Life imprisonment |
| Crimes against humanity -- other acts (Section 7(1) Nos. 2-10) | 3 years to life imprisonment |
| War crimes against persons (Section 8) | 3 years to life imprisonment |
| War crimes against property (Section 9) | 1 to 10 years imprisonment |
| Command/superior responsibility (Section 4) | Same as underlying crime |

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECtHR has addressed universal jurisdiction primarily through the lens of fair trial rights (Article 6), legality of criminal law (Article 7), and the right to liberty (Article 5).

**Jorgic v. Germany (Application No. 74613/01, Judgment of July 12, 2007):** The Court upheld Germany's exercise of universal jurisdiction over the genocide prosecution of Nikola Jorgić, a Bosnian Serb convicted by a German court for acts of genocide committed in Bosnia. The Court found no violation of Article 6(1) (the exercise of jurisdiction was based on the principle of universal jurisdiction recognized in international law) or Article 7 (the applicant's conviction was not unforeseeable).

**Ould Dah v. France (Application No. 13113/03, Decision of March 17, 2009):** The Court declared inadmissible a complaint by a Mauritanian army officer convicted in absentia by a French court for acts of torture in Mauritania. The Court found that France's exercise of universal jurisdiction under the Convention against Torture was foreseeable and that the prosecution did not violate Articles 6 or 7.

### EU Frameworks

**Eurojust:** The EU Agency for Criminal Justice Cooperation, established by Council Decision 2002/187/JHA and operating under Regulation (EU) 2018/1727, facilitates coordination between national prosecutors in cross-border criminal cases, including universal jurisdiction cases. The Eurojust Genocide Network, established in 2002, brings together national contact points for the investigation and prosecution of genocide, crimes against humanity, and war crimes.

**Joint Investigation Teams (JITs):** Under the Convention on Mutual Assistance in Criminal Matters between the Member States of the EU (2000) and Framework Decision 2002/465/JHA, member states can establish JITs for cross-border investigations, including universal jurisdiction cases.

### Belgium's Universal Jurisdiction Experience

Belgium's experience provides a cautionary tale about the political limits of universal jurisdiction. The Loi relative à la répression des violations graves du droit international humanitaire (1993, amended 1999) established one of the broadest universal jurisdiction frameworks in the world, permitting prosecution of international crimes regardless of any nexus to Belgium. The law led to complaints filed against numerous world leaders, including US Secretary of Defense Donald Rumsfeld, Israeli Prime Minister Ariel Sharon, and Congolese President Laurent-Désiré Kabila. In the Case Concerning the Arrest Warrant of 11 April 2000 (Democratic Republic of the Congo v. Belgium), the ICJ ruled in 2002 that an international arrest warrant issued by Belgium against Congolese Foreign Minister Yerodia Ndombasi violated the sovereign immunity of the incumbent foreign minister. Under diplomatic pressure -- particularly from the United States, which threatened to move NATO headquarters from Brussels -- Belgium substantially amended the law in 2003, introducing nexus requirements.

## Comparative Analysis

### Scope of Jurisdiction

Germany exercises the broadest form of universal jurisdiction, requiring no nexus whatsoever to Germany. South Korea and the United States impose varying nexus requirements. The ECHR permits universal jurisdiction but subjects its exercise to proportionality review.

### Practical Exercise

Germany is by far the most active in exercising universal jurisdiction, with multiple ongoing structural investigations and the landmark Koblenz convictions. The United States has shifted toward narrowing civil universal jurisdiction while maintaining limited criminal universal jurisdiction. South Korea has the statutory framework but has not yet conducted significant universal jurisdiction prosecutions.

### Institutional Capacity

Germany's success is attributable in part to dedicated institutional capacity, particularly the ZBKV specialized investigation unit and the GBA's structural investigation methodology. The United States disperses responsibility across multiple agencies (DOJ, military prosecutors). South Korea lacks a specialized universal jurisdiction investigation unit.

### Political Constraints

Belgium's experience demonstrates that universal jurisdiction can generate significant diplomatic backlash, particularly when exercised against officials of powerful states. Germany has navigated this by focusing on mid-level officials from states experiencing conflict (Syria, Iraq) rather than senior officials of major powers.
