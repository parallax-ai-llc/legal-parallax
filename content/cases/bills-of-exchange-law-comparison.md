## Summary

Bills of exchange and negotiable instruments -- written orders by which one party (the drawer) directs another party (the drawee) to pay a specified sum to a third party (the payee) or to the bearer -- have been fundamental to commercial law since medieval merchant banking, and their regulation reveals deep structural differences between common law and civil law traditions. South Korea governs bills of exchange and promissory notes through the Bills of Exchange and Promissory Notes Act (어음법, Act No. 1001, enacted January 20, 1962), which is directly modeled on the Geneva Uniform Law on Bills of Exchange and Promissory Notes (1930 Geneva Conventions), supplemented by the Cheque Act (수표법) for cheques and the Electronic Bills of Exchange Act (전자어음의 발행 및 유통에 관한 법률, 2004) for electronic instruments. The United States governs negotiable instruments through Article 3 of the Uniform Commercial Code (U.C.C. §§ 3-101 to 3-605), as revised in 1990 and adopted in all fifty states, together with Article 4 (Bank Deposits and Collections), representing the Anglo-American common law tradition that developed independently from the Geneva system. Germany's Wechselgesetz (WG, Act on Bills of Exchange, originally enacted June 21, 1933) implements the Geneva Uniform Law directly, providing the continental European model that influenced both Korean and Japanese negotiable instruments law, while the Scheckgesetz (ScheckG, 1933) governs cheques under the Geneva Uniform Law on Cheques. The European Union has not harmonized negotiable instruments law, leaving member states to apply either the Geneva Uniform Law system or common law principles, though the ECHR protects rights arising from negotiable instruments as possessions under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's negotiable instruments law is firmly rooted in the Geneva Uniform Law tradition, adopted through Japan's influence during the colonial period and retained in the independent Korean legal system.

**Bills of Exchange and Promissory Notes Act (어음법):** Enacted as Act No. 1001 on January 20, 1962, the 어음법 closely follows the Geneva Uniform Law on Bills of Exchange and Promissory Notes (Convention of June 7, 1930). The Act is divided into two main parts:

| Part | Subject | Key Articles |
|------|---------|-------------|
| Part I | Bills of Exchange (환어음) | Articles 1-74 |
| Part II | Promissory Notes (약속어음) | Articles 75-78 |

**Essential Elements of a Bill of Exchange (Article 1):** A valid bill of exchange must contain:
1. The denomination "bill of exchange" (환어음) in the text of the instrument
2. An unconditional order to pay a determinate sum of money
3. The name of the drawee
4. The maturity date (or payable on demand if unstated)
5. The place of payment
6. The name of the payee
7. The date and place of drawing
8. The signature of the drawer

**Cheque Act (수표법):** The Cheque Act (Act No. 1002, 1962) implements the Geneva Uniform Law on Cheques (Convention of March 19, 1931). Korean cheques are payable on demand only and must be drawn on a bank.

### Acceptance and Payment

**Acceptance (인수):** Under Article 21, the holder of a bill of exchange may present it to the drawee for acceptance at any time before maturity. Acceptance must be written on the bill itself (Article 25). The acceptor becomes the principal debtor on the instrument.

**Payment (지급):** Article 38 requires presentation for payment on the maturity date or within two business days thereafter. Failure to present for payment within the prescribed period results in loss of recourse rights against endorsers and the drawer (Article 53).

### Transfer and Endorsement

**Endorsement (배서):** Bills of exchange are transferred by endorsement (배서) and delivery under Articles 11-20. An endorsement must be written on the bill or an allonge (보전) attached thereto. The endorser guarantees both acceptance and payment to subsequent holders (Article 15).

**Good Faith Acquisition (선의취득):** Article 16, paragraph 2, provides that a person who acquires a bill of exchange through an uninterrupted chain of endorsements, in good faith and without gross negligence, becomes the lawful holder even if a prior endorser lacked authority to transfer the instrument. This provision protects the circulation function of negotiable instruments.

### Electronic Bills of Exchange

**Electronic Bills of Exchange Act (전자어음의 발행 및 유통에 관한 법률, 2004):** Enacted to modernize Korean trade finance, this Act permits the issuance, endorsement, and settlement of promissory notes in electronic form through the Korea Financial Telecommunications and Clearings Institute (KFTC) electronic bill system. Key provisions include:

- Article 3: Electronic bills of exchange must be registered in the electronic bill management institution's system
- Article 6: Electronic endorsement has the same legal effect as paper endorsement
- Article 11: The issuer of an electronic bill must have a bank account designated for settlement

As of 2025, electronic bills account for approximately 85 percent of all promissory notes issued in South Korea, reflecting the country's advanced digital infrastructure.

### Dishonor and Sanctions

South Korea maintains a uniquely punitive system for dishonored negotiable instruments. The Bank of Korea's bill clearinghouse system (어음교환소) imposes severe consequences:

- First dishonor: Warning and registration in the credit information system
- Second dishonor within one year: Suspension of all checking and bill transactions for two years (거래정지처분)
- Criminal sanctions: Issuance of a bill of exchange or cheque with knowledge that sufficient funds will not be available may constitute fraud (사기죄) under Article 347 of the Criminal Code (형법)

**Supreme Court Decision 2007도3468 (2007):** The Court upheld the criminal liability of a bill issuer who issued a promissory note knowing that funds would be insufficient at maturity, finding that such conduct constitutes fraud when combined with an intent to deceive the payee.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States follows the Anglo-American common law tradition for negotiable instruments, now codified in U.C.C. Article 3, which diverges significantly from the Geneva Uniform Law system.

**U.C.C. Article 3 (Revised 1990):** Article 3 governs negotiable instruments, defined in § 3-104 as unconditional promises or orders to pay a fixed amount of money, payable to bearer or to order, payable on demand or at a definite time, and containing no other undertaking or instruction.

| Section | Content |
|---------|---------|
| § 3-104 | Definition of negotiable instrument |
| § 3-201 to 3-206 | Negotiation, transfer, endorsement |
| § 3-302 to 3-306 | Holder in due course; claims and defenses |
| § 3-401 to 3-420 | Liability of parties |
| § 3-501 to 3-505 | Presentment, dishonor, protest |
| § 3-601 to 3-605 | Discharge |

**Types of Instruments:**

| Type | Definition | U.C.C. Section |
|------|-----------|---------------|
| Note (Promissory note) | Promise to pay | § 3-104(e) |
| Draft (Bill of exchange) | Order to pay | § 3-104(e) |
| Check | Draft drawn on a bank, payable on demand | § 3-104(f) |
| Cashier's check | Draft drawn by bank on itself | § 3-104(g) |
| Teller's check | Draft drawn by one bank on another bank | § 3-104(h) |

### Holder in Due Course

**U.C.C. § 3-302:** A holder in due course (HDC) is a holder who takes the instrument: (1) for value; (2) in good faith; (3) without notice that the instrument is overdue, has been dishonored, or is subject to any claim or defense.

The HDC doctrine provides powerful protection: under § 3-305, an HDC takes free of all claims to the instrument and free of most defenses, except "real" defenses including infancy, duress, lack of legal capacity, illegality, fraud in the factum, and discharge in insolvency proceedings.

**Federal Trade Commission Holder Rule (16 C.F.R. § 433.2):** In consumer transactions, the FTC Holder Rule preserves consumer defenses against HDCs by requiring consumer credit contracts to include a notice that any holder of the contract is subject to all claims and defenses that the debtor could assert against the seller. This significantly limits the HDC doctrine in consumer contexts.

### Distinguishing Features from Geneva System

The U.C.C. Article 3 system differs from the Geneva Uniform Law in several critical respects:

- **No requirement that the word "bill of exchange" or "note" appear on the instrument** (Geneva system requires the denomination in the text)
- **Protest is generally not required** for dishonor under § 3-505 (Geneva system requires protest as a condition of recourse)
- **Broader definition of negotiability** -- U.C.C. instruments need not specify a place of payment
- **Accommodation parties** are specifically addressed in § 3-419 (suretyship principles)
- **Discharge by payment** is more flexible than under the Geneva system

### Check 21 Act and Electronic Instruments

The Check Clearing for the 21st Century Act (Check 21, Pub. L. 108-100, 2003) facilitates electronic check processing by creating a new negotiable instrument called a "substitute check" -- a paper reproduction of an original check that can be processed electronically. This legislation dramatically accelerated the decline of physical check processing in the United States.

**Uniform Electronic Transactions Act (UETA) and E-SIGN Act:** While U.C.C. Article 3 was drafted for paper instruments, the federal Electronic Signatures in Global and National Commerce Act (E-SIGN, 15 U.S.C. §§ 7001-7031) and state adoptions of UETA provide legal recognition for electronic negotiable instruments.

**Transferable Records:** U.C.C. § 7-106 (revised Article 7) and UETA § 16 recognize "transferable records" -- electronic records that would be negotiable instruments if in writing -- establishing functional equivalence between paper and electronic instruments.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's Wechselgesetz (WG) of June 21, 1933, implements the Geneva Uniform Law on Bills of Exchange and Promissory Notes, to which Germany was an original signatory. The WG has remained substantially unchanged since enactment, reflecting the stability of the Geneva system.

**Wechselgesetz Structure:**

| Section | Subject | Articles |
|---------|---------|----------|
| Part I | Drawn Bills of Exchange (Gezogener Wechsel) | Arts. 1-74 |
| Part II | Promissory Notes (Eigener Wechsel) | Arts. 75-78 |
| Part III | Supplementary Provisions | Arts. 79-98 |

**Essential Elements (Article 1 WG):** A valid Wechsel must contain:
1. The word "Wechsel" in the text of the instrument (Wechselklausel)
2. An unconditional order to pay a determinate sum (unbedingte Anweisung)
3. The name of the drawee (Bezogener)
4. The maturity date (Verfalltag)
5. The place of payment (Zahlungsort)
6. The name of the payee (Remittent)
7. The date and place of drawing
8. The signature of the drawer (Aussteller)

**Scheckgesetz (ScheckG):** The Scheckgesetz of August 14, 1933, implements the Geneva Uniform Law on Cheques. A Scheck must be drawn on a bank (Bezogener muss ein Kreditinstitut sein) and is always payable on demand.

### Wechselstrenge (Formal Rigor)

German bill of exchange law is characterized by extreme formalism (Wechselstrenge). The absence of any essential element renders the instrument void as a Wechsel, though it may still function as an ordinary civil law obligation.

**BGH, October 25, 1990 (IX ZR 62/90):** The Court held that an instrument lacking the word "Wechsel" in its text body cannot be treated as a valid bill of exchange, even if the parties clearly intended to create a Wechsel obligation. The Wechselstrenge principle requires strict adherence to formal requirements.

### Wechselprozess (Summary Bill Proceedings)

German procedural law provides an expedited enforcement procedure for bills of exchange through the Wechselprozess (§§ 602-605 ZPO, Zivilprozessordnung). This procedure:

- Allows the holder to obtain a provisional judgment (Vorbehaltsurteil) on the basis of the instrument alone
- Limits the debtor's defenses to those that can be proved immediately (sofort beweisbare Einwendungen)
- Provides for provisional enforcement (vorläufige Vollstreckbarkeit) without security
- The debtor may pursue a Nachverfahren (subsequent proceedings) to raise additional defenses

**Significance:** The Wechselprozess exemplifies the principle that negotiable instruments serve a circulation function (Umlaufsfunktion) that requires rapid and reliable enforcement.

### Decline of Bills of Exchange in Practice

The practical use of bills of exchange has declined significantly in Germany. The Bundesbank discontinued Wechsel-based refinancing operations in 1998 with the transition to the ECB and the euro. Bills of exchange are now primarily used in:

- Export financing (Exportfinanzierung)
- Supplier credit arrangements (Lieferantenkredit)
- Forfaiting transactions
- Certain traditional industries (particularly in the Mittelstand)

### Transfer and Endorsement (Indossament)

**Articles 11-20 WG:** A Wechsel payable to order is transferred by endorsement (Indossament) and delivery. The endorsement must be written on the Wechsel itself or on an allonge (Anhang) attached thereto.

**Blankoindossament (Blank Endorsement):** Article 13 permits a blank endorsement consisting only of the endorser's signature on the reverse of the instrument. A bill endorsed in blank becomes transferable by mere delivery (Article 14).

**Gutgläubiger Erwerb (Good Faith Acquisition):** Article 16, paragraph 2, protects a person who acquires a Wechsel through an uninterrupted chain of endorsements, acting in good faith and without gross negligence, even if a prior holder lacked authority to transfer.

## ECHR / EU

### Absence of EU Harmonization

The European Union has not harmonized the law of negotiable instruments, reflecting both the entrenched nature of national systems and the division between Geneva Uniform Law jurisdictions (most EU member states) and common law traditions (formerly the United Kingdom and Ireland).

**Historical Background:** The 1930 Geneva Conventions (Convention Providing a Uniform Law for Bills of Exchange and Promissory Notes; Convention for the Settlement of Certain Conflicts of Laws in Connection with Bills of Exchange and Promissory Notes; Convention on the Stamp Laws in Connection with Bills of Exchange and Promissory Notes) were signed by most European states but not by the United Kingdom or Ireland, creating a permanent division in European negotiable instruments law.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Rights Under Negotiable Instruments as "Possessions":**

The ECHR has addressed negotiable instruments primarily through the lens of Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (protection of property) and Article 6(1) (right to a fair hearing).

**Stran Greek Refineries and Stratis Andreadis v. Greece (Application No. 13427/87, ECtHR Judgment of December 9, 1994):** While concerning an arbitral award rather than a negotiable instrument directly, the Court established that a confirmed debt claim -- including claims arising from negotiable instruments -- constitutes a "possession" within the meaning of Article 1 of Protocol No. 1.

**Raffineries Grecques Stran v. Greece:** The Court's analysis confirms that rights arising from commercial instruments, including bills of exchange and promissory notes, are protected as possessions where they constitute enforceable claims.

**Kotov v. Russia (Application No. 54522/00, ECtHR Grand Chamber Judgment of April 3, 2012):** The Grand Chamber addressed priority of claims in insolvency proceedings, with implications for holders of negotiable instruments. The Court held that the deprivation of a creditor's priority position constitutes an interference with possessions requiring justification.

### EU Payment and Settlement Frameworks

While not directly regulating negotiable instruments, several EU instruments affect their use:

**Single Euro Payments Area (SEPA):** SEPA Regulation (EU) No 260/2012 has effectively replaced checks and bills of exchange for intra-EU euro payments, mandating migration to SEPA Credit Transfers and SEPA Direct Debits.

**Settlement Finality Directive (Directive 98/26/EC):** This Directive protects payment and settlement systems from the effects of insolvency proceedings, indirectly affecting the settlement of negotiable instruments through clearing systems.

**Financial Collateral Directive (Directive 2002/47/EC):** Negotiable instruments may constitute "financial collateral" under this Directive, which simplifies the creation and enforcement of security interests in financial instruments.

### Cross-Border Enforcement

**Brussels I Regulation (Recast) (Regulation (EU) No 1215/2012):** Cross-border enforcement of judgments based on negotiable instruments within the EU is governed by the Brussels I Regulation. The Wechselprozess-type summary proceedings available in Geneva system countries may create issues under Article 45(1)(b), which permits refusal of recognition where the judgment was given in default and the defendant was not served in sufficient time to arrange for a defense.

**European Enforcement Order (Regulation (EC) No 805/2004):** Uncontested claims arising from negotiable instruments may be certified as European Enforcement Orders, enabling direct enforcement across EU member states without an exequatur proceeding.

### UNCITRAL Initiatives

**United Nations Convention on International Bills of Exchange and International Promissory Notes (1988):** This Convention, drafted by UNCITRAL to bridge the gap between the Geneva and Anglo-American systems, has not entered into force due to insufficient ratifications. Only five states have ratified or acceded to the Convention, illustrating the difficulty of harmonizing negotiable instruments law across legal traditions.

**UNCITRAL Model Law on Transferable Records (2017):** This Model Law addresses electronic transferable records, including electronic bills of exchange, providing a framework for functional equivalence between paper and electronic instruments. The Model Law has influenced domestic legislation in Singapore (Electronic Transactions Act amendments, 2021) and is under consideration in several EU member states.

## Comparative Analysis

### Geneva System vs. Anglo-American System

The most fundamental divide in negotiable instruments law is between the Geneva Uniform Law system and the Anglo-American U.C.C./common law system:

| Feature | Geneva System (Korea, Germany) | U.C.C. System (United States) |
|---------|-------------------------------|------------------------------|
| Formal requirements | Strict: denomination must appear in text | Flexible: no specific form words required |
| Protest | Required for recourse | Generally abolished |
| Aval (guaranty) | Specific institution (WG Art. 30-32; 어음법 Art. 30-32) | No equivalent; accommodation party (§ 3-419) |
| Maturity types | Four types specified (sight, time after sight, time after date, fixed date) | Demand or definite time (§ 3-108) |
| Wechselstrenge/formalism | Very high; defective instruments void | Moderate; substantial compliance may suffice |
| Summary proceedings | Wechselprozess available | No equivalent summary proceeding |
| Consumer protection | Limited | FTC Holder Rule limits HDC doctrine |

### Holder Protection

All jurisdictions protect good faith acquirers of negotiable instruments, but with differing scope:

- **South Korea (어음법 Art. 16):** Good faith acquisition requires an uninterrupted chain of endorsements and the absence of gross negligence. The burden of proving bad faith or gross negligence falls on the party challenging the acquisition.
- **United States (U.C.C. § 3-302):** The holder in due course doctrine provides the broadest protection, cutting off personal defenses but not "real" defenses. However, the FTC Holder Rule significantly limits HDC protection in consumer transactions.
- **Germany (WG Art. 16):** Gutgläubiger Erwerb operates similarly to Korean law, reflecting their shared Geneva Uniform Law foundation. The BGH has applied the standard strictly, requiring actual good faith at the time of acquisition.

### Electronic Instruments: Convergence

The digitization of negotiable instruments represents an area of emerging convergence:

- **South Korea** leads with the Electronic Bills of Exchange Act (2004), achieving approximately 85 percent electronic adoption for promissory notes
- **United States** addresses electronic instruments through E-SIGN, UETA, and the Check 21 Act, though U.C.C. Article 3 was designed for paper
- **Germany** has been slower to adopt electronic negotiable instruments, though blockchain-based projects and the UNCITRAL Model Law on Transferable Records are prompting legislative consideration
- The **EU** provides a framework through eIDAS but has not specifically addressed electronic negotiable instruments

### Criminal Sanctions

The treatment of dishonored instruments reveals significant policy differences:

- **South Korea:** Maintains the most punitive system, with criminal prosecution possible for fraudulent issuance and banking system suspension for repeated dishonors
- **United States:** Passing a bad check may constitute a criminal offense under state law, but the penalties are generally less severe than in Korea
- **Germany:** Intentional issuance of uncovered checks may constitute Betrug (fraud) under § 263 StGB, but criminal enforcement for dishonored Wechsel is rare in practice

### Practical Decline and Modern Relevance

Bills of exchange have declined in practical importance across all jurisdictions, replaced by electronic payment systems, but they remain relevant in specific contexts:

| Jurisdiction | Primary Current Uses |
|-------------|---------------------|
| South Korea | SME financing; supplier credit; electronic bills through KFTC |
| United States | International trade drafts; bank acceptances; promissory notes in lending |
| Germany | Export financing; forfaiting; Mittelstand supplier credit |
| EU | Cross-border trade (declining); forfaiting; specific industries |
