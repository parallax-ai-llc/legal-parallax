## Summary

Judicial torture -- the infliction of pain by public authority to extract confessions, obtain testimony, or punish offenders within a formal legal framework -- represents one of the most troubling and historically widespread practices in the history of law, employed by civilizations that simultaneously produced sophisticated doctrines of justice, equity, and due process. Unlike extrajudicial violence or arbitrary cruelty, judicial torture was a regulated legal procedure, governed by detailed rules about who could be tortured, under what circumstances, by what methods, and to what ends -- a paradox that has fascinated and horrified legal historians in equal measure. Ancient Near Eastern civilizations employed torture as an investigative tool, with the Egyptian tomb robbery papyri (c. 1150 BCE) recording systematic "examination" of suspects by beating with a double rod. Roman law developed the most doctrinally elaborate system of judicial torture (quaestio) in the ancient world, originally limited to slaves but progressively extended to free persons under the Empire, with detailed rules about corroborating evidence, prohibited subjects, and the evidentiary weight of torture-obtained confessions found in the Digest and Code of Justinian. East Asian legal traditions, particularly the Chinese system codified in the Tang Code (唐律疏議, 653 CE) and its Korean derivatives, made confession the centerpiece of criminal procedure and authorized regulated forms of judicial torture (刑訊, xingxun) to obtain confessions when other evidence was insufficient, creating a system that persisted for over a millennium and generated both sophisticated procedural safeguards and notorious abuses. Islamic law adopted a distinctive position, with the majority of classical jurists rejecting judicial torture as a means of obtaining evidence while permitting ta'zir (discretionary) corporal punishment, though in practice Ottoman and other Muslim-majority states employed torture despite juristic disapproval. Germanic and medieval European law employed torture on an ad hoc basis before the 13th century, after which the reception of Roman-canonical law and the abolition of the ordeal (1215) led to the systematic adoption of judicial torture as the centerpiece of the inquisitorial procedure, formalized in the Constitutio Criminalis Carolina (1532) and practiced across Continental Europe until the Enlightenment abolition movement led by Beccaria, Voltaire, and others secured its gradual prohibition during the 18th and 19th centuries. English common law stands as a notable exception to the European pattern, with torture never becoming part of ordinary criminal procedure, though its use under royal prerogative (particularly by the Star Chamber and Privy Council) demonstrated that the common law's rejection of torture was not absolute.

## Ancient Near East

### Egyptian Judicial Torture

The most detailed evidence for judicial torture in the ancient Near East comes from Egyptian judicial papyri of the New Kingdom period.

**Tomb Robbery Papyri (c. 1150-1070 BCE):**

The papyri recording the investigation of tomb robberies during the reigns of Ramesses IX through Ramesses XI provide the most extensive ancient documentation of judicial torture:

- **Papyrus Abbott and Papyrus Amherst:** These documents record the "examination" (iri smtr, literally "making inquiry") of suspects. The standard formula was: "He was examined by beating with a double rod (bḥs), with the bastinado on the feet, and with twisting of the hands." Suspects who confessed under this treatment had their confessions recorded; those who maintained their innocence were sometimes tortured repeatedly.
- **Procedure:** The examination was conducted by a commission of officials (scribes, priests, military officers) and the results were recorded in detail. The papyri distinguish between initial questioning and escalated physical coercion, suggesting a graduated procedure.
- **Results:** The records show that some suspects confessed under torture and identified accomplices, leading to further arrests and examinations. Others maintained their innocence despite repeated torture, and some of these were ultimately acquitted or transferred to different proceedings.

**Mesopotamian Evidence:**

Mesopotamian evidence for judicial torture is less direct than Egyptian evidence, but several sources suggest its use:

- The Code of Hammurabi does not explicitly authorize torture as an investigative tool, but the river ordeal (Laws 2, 132) imposed severe physical danger on the accused.
- Neo-Assyrian royal inscriptions frequently describe torture inflicted on defeated enemies and rebels, though this was punishment rather than investigation.
- Letters from the Mari archive (c. 1775 BCE) reference the "examination" of suspects in ways that may imply physical coercion.

## Roman Law

### The Quaestio

Roman judicial torture was the most legally regulated system of torture in the ancient world, with detailed rules found throughout the Digest and Code of Justinian.

**Republican Period:**

Under the Republic, torture (quaestio, literally "inquiry") was strictly limited to slaves. The rationale was brutally practical: slaves were not considered legally capable of the oath (iusiurandum) that bound free persons' testimony, and their testimony was presumed unreliable unless extracted under pain. Free citizens were protected by their civic status (caput) from judicial torture.

Key Republican principles:
- Slaves could be tortured to obtain evidence in criminal cases.
- A slave could not be tortured to give evidence against his own master (a rule reflecting the master's property right in the slave).
- Evidence obtained through torture was not automatically credited; it required corroboration.

**Imperial Expansion:**

Under the Empire, the prohibition on torturing free persons eroded progressively:

- **Tiberius (r. 14-37 CE):** Extended torture to free persons in cases of maiestas (treason). Tacitus (Annals 2.30, 4.29) recorded the use of torture against free citizens accused of political offenses.
- **Diocletian and later emperors:** Extended torture to humiliores (lower-class citizens) in a broad range of criminal cases. Only honestiores (upper-class citizens -- senators, equestrians, decurions, military officers) retained the privilege of exemption from torture, and even this exemption was narrowed over time.

**Detailed Regulations (Digest 48.18; Codex 9.41):**

The classical jurists developed elaborate rules governing torture:

- **Ulpian (D. 48.18.1):** "Torture ought not to be applied as the first recourse; but when it is likely and probable [that the accused committed the crime], and other evidence is lacking to reach the truth, then the resort to torture is appropriate." This principle -- that torture was a subsidiary means of proof, not a primary one -- was reiterated by multiple jurists.
- **D. 48.18.1.23:** "Torture should not be applied to a child below puberty."
- **D. 48.18.10 (Arcadius Charisius):** Enumerated the categories of persons exempt from torture: children below fourteen, pregnant women, soldiers, veterans, decurions, and their children. These exemptions were subject to exception in cases of maiestas and certain other offenses.
- **D. 48.18.1.17 (Ulpian):** "The emperor Hadrian wrote in a rescript to Sennius Sabinus that torture should not be demanded indiscriminately, but when the crime is of such gravity that it requires investigation by torture, and when the accused is a person of such character that torture is appropriate."
- **D. 48.18.1.21:** "Evidence from torture is weak and dangerous, and alien to the truth. For most persons, by endurance or the severity of the torture, come to hold confession in contempt, so that the truth can in no way be elicited from them. Others are so impatient of pain that they prefer to lie rather than endure torture, and so it happens that they confess to different things in different ways, accusing not only themselves but also others." This passage by Ulpian is one of the most remarkable statements in ancient legal literature -- a Roman jurist articulating, in the 3rd century CE, essentially the same critique of torture that Beccaria would make in 1764.

**Confirmation Requirement:** Roman law required that a confession obtained under torture be confirmed (confirmatio) by the accused in a subsequent session, free from coercion. If the accused recanted, the confession was not admissible. However, in practice, the threat of renewed torture often ensured confirmation.

**Methods:** Roman sources describe various methods of judicial torture:
- The equuleus (rack): The accused was stretched on a wooden frame.
- Flagellation: Whipping with various instruments.
- Ungulae (claws): Metal instruments used to tear flesh.
- Fire: Application of heated metal or burning.
- The fidiculae: A form of compression or stretching.

## East Asian Law

### Chinese Judicial Torture

The Chinese legal tradition made confession the cornerstone of criminal proof and authorized regulated torture to obtain confessions, creating the most enduring system of judicial torture in world history.

**Tang Code (唐律疏議, 653 CE):**

The Tang Code provided detailed regulations for judicial torture:

- **Article 476 (拷囚, Torturing Prisoners):** "When investigating a case, first use the Five Listens to examine the facts. If the evidence and testimony are consistent and the defendant's account contradicts them, then apply torture. The total number of blows shall not exceed two hundred. After applying torture, allow a rest period of twenty days before applying torture again. The total number of sessions shall not exceed three."

- **Instruments:** The authorized instrument of torture was the heavy bamboo stick (杖, zhang), applied to the buttocks. The specifications were regulated: the stick was to be of prescribed dimensions (three feet five inches in length, with specified width), and the blows were to be applied to the designated area of the body.

- **Prohibited Practices:** Article 477 prohibited the use of unauthorized torture methods: "All who use methods of pressing, or who apply devices that crush limbs, or who otherwise use cruel and unusual methods of torture, shall be punished by two years of penal servitude. If the prisoner dies as a result, the official shall be punished by penal servitude of three years." This provision simultaneously reveals that unauthorized methods were employed in practice and that the code attempted to limit them.

- **Confession and Recantation:** Article 478 provided that a defendant who confessed under torture could subsequently recant the confession and demand a new hearing. If the defendant recanted three times, the case was to be submitted to a higher authority for review. This recantation provision was a significant procedural safeguard, though its effectiveness depended on the willingness of local magistrates to honor it.

**Song Dynasty Developments (960-1279):**

- **Song Xing Tong (宋刑統, 963):** The Song dynasty criminal code retained the Tang torture regulations with modifications. The Song period saw increasing concern about miscarriages of justice caused by coerced confessions.
- **The Xiyuanji (洗冤集錄, 1247):** Song Ci's pioneering forensic manual argued that physical evidence (examination of wounds, analysis of bloodstains, observation of decomposition) should supplement or replace confession as the basis for conviction. This work represented a significant intellectual challenge to the confession-centered system, though it did not displace torture as a legal institution.

**Ming and Qing Dynasties:**

The Ming Code (大明律, 1397) and Qing Code (大清律例, 1740) retained the authorization of judicial torture with regulations similar to the Tang model. The Qing dynasty saw extensive use of extralegal torture methods despite formal prohibitions, including:
- Ankle pressing (夾棍, jiagon): Wooden boards compressed against the ankles.
- Finger pressing (拶指, zanzi): A similar device applied to the fingers.
- The kneeling chain (跪鏈, guilean): Forced kneeling on chains.

### Korean Judicial Torture

**Joseon Dynasty (1392-1897):**

Korean judicial procedure followed the Chinese model, with confession as the essential proof and regulated torture as the mechanism for obtaining it:

- **Authorized Methods:** The Gyeongguk Daejeon (經國大典) authorized the use of the gon (棍, heavy stick) for judicial torture. As in Chinese law, the number of blows and the intervals between sessions were regulated.
- **Jurirang (周牢, Shin Jang):** Certain severe methods of torture, including pressing the shins with heavy boards (亂杖, nanjang or 周牢刑, jurirahyeong), were employed in serious cases. The Annals of the Joseon Dynasty record numerous instances of prisoners dying under torture, provoking periodic royal edicts restricting its use.
- **Royal Review:** As with capital punishment, the use of severe torture required authorization from higher authorities. In politically sensitive cases, the Uigeumbu (義禁府, Royal Investigation Bureau) conducted interrogations under direct royal oversight.
- **Heumheumsinseo (欽欽新書):** Jeong Yak-yong's judicial handbook (early 19th century) argued forcefully for reliance on physical evidence and logical reasoning over coerced confession, anticipating the reform arguments that would ultimately lead to the abolition of judicial torture in Korea.

**Abolition:** Judicial torture was formally abolished in Korea as part of the Gabo Reform (甲午改革, 1894) and subsequent modernization of the legal system during the Korean Empire period (1897-1910), though its complete elimination was a gradual process.

## Islamic Law

### The Juristic Rejection of Torture

Islamic law occupies a distinctive position in the comparative history of judicial torture, with the majority of classical jurists rejecting torture as a legitimate means of obtaining evidence.

**The Juristic Consensus:**

The four Sunni schools of jurisprudence generally agreed that:

1. **Confession must be voluntary:** A confession obtained under duress (ikrah) was not legally valid. The hadith "My community is absolved of error, forgetfulness, and what they are coerced into" (Sunan Ibn Majah 2045) established the principle that coerced statements had no legal effect.
2. **Confession is retractable:** A confessor could retract at any time, even during the execution of a hadd punishment (as in the case of Ma'iz ibn Malik, who attempted to flee during his stoning, prompting debate about whether the execution should have been halted).
3. **The burden is on the prosecutor:** The foundational evidentiary principle -- "the burden of proof is on the claimant" (al-bayyina 'ala al-mudda'i) -- placed the obligation of proof on the prosecution through witnesses, not on the defendant through confession.

**The Hanbali Position:** Ibn Qayyim al-Jawziyya (d. 1350, Al-Turuq al-Hukmiyyah fi al-Siyasa al-Shar'iyya) discussed the use of beating to extract confession in the context of ta'zir (discretionary punishment) and hisba (public order enforcement). He argued that a judge could beat a suspect "of ill repute" to obtain a confession, but only when other evidence strongly indicated guilt. This position was more permissive than the other schools but still imposed conditions on the use of physical coercion.

**Al-Mawardi (d. 1058, Al-Ahkam al-Sultaniyyah):** The Shafi'i jurist addressed the muhtasib's (market inspector's) authority to punish market fraud through ta'zir, which could include beating. However, he distinguished this punitive function from the extraction of testimony, maintaining that judicial evidence required voluntary statements.

**Ottoman Practice:**

Despite the juristic rejection of torture, Ottoman practice frequently employed it:
- The siyaset (political/discretionary) jurisdiction of the Ottoman sultan permitted methods that went beyond what the Sharia courts authorized.
- The falaka (bastinado -- beating the soles of the feet) was widely employed in Ottoman legal practice, though jurists questioned its legitimacy.
- Ottoman kanun (administrative law) authorized corporal methods of investigation that were not sanctioned by fiqh, creating a tension between Sharia ideals and political realities that persisted throughout the Ottoman period.

## Medieval European Law

### The Continental Inquisitorial System

The systematic use of judicial torture in medieval and early modern Europe was a direct consequence of the abolition of the ordeal in 1215 and the reception of Roman-canonical law.

**The Evidentiary Problem:**

The abolition of the ordeal created a procedural crisis: how to determine guilt without divine judgment? The solution adopted across Continental Europe was the Roman-canonical system of "legal proofs" (probationes plenae), which required:

- **Full Proof (probatio plena):** For conviction in a capital case, full proof was required. Full proof consisted of either (a) the testimony of two unimpeachable eyewitnesses or (b) the defendant's own confession. Circumstantial evidence alone, no matter how compelling, did not constitute full proof.
- **Half Proof (probatio semiplena):** The testimony of one eyewitness, or strong circumstantial evidence, constituted half proof. Half proof authorized the application of torture to obtain the confession that would complete the proof.

This system created an internal logic for torture: since confession was the only alternative to dual eyewitness testimony (which was rarely available in criminal cases), and since torture was the authorized means of obtaining confession, torture became functionally indispensable to the criminal justice system.

**The Constitutio Criminalis Carolina (1532):**

Emperor Charles V's criminal code, the most influential criminal statute in European history before the Napoleonic Code, provided detailed regulations for judicial torture:

- **Article 20 (Indicia):** Before torture could be applied, there must be sufficient preliminary evidence (indicia) of guilt. One eyewitness, or strong circumstantial evidence, was required as a minimum.
- **Article 25:** Listed specific indicia warranting torture: the testimony of one good witness (ein guter Zeug), the suspect's flight from the scene, the suspect's association with known criminals, the suspect's previous criminal record, and threatening statements by the suspect.
- **Article 56-58 (Conduct of Torture):** The torturer was to be "an experienced person" (ein erfahrner Mensch). A physician was to be present. The judge, two assessors (Schöffen), and a court clerk were to attend and record the proceedings. Torture was not to be applied on feast days.
- **Article 59:** The confession obtained under torture had to be confirmed (ratifiziert) by the accused at a subsequent hearing, held at a different location and not less than one day later. If the accused recanted, torture could be applied again, but no more than three times total.
- **Article 60:** Leading questions were prohibited during torture -- the accused was to narrate the facts freely, and details that could be verified (location of stolen goods, description of the crime scene) were to be checked against known facts.

**Methods:**

The most common methods of judicial torture in Continental Europe included:

1. **Strappado (Seilverhör):** The accused's hands were tied behind the back, and the body was hoisted by the wrists using a pulley. Weights could be attached to the feet for additional torment.
2. **The Rack (Leiter):** The accused was stretched on a frame, with ropes attached to wrists and ankles pulled in opposite directions.
3. **Thumbscrews (Daumschrauben):** Metal vises applied to the thumbs.
4. **Leg screws (Beinschrauben/Spanische Stiefel, "Spanish boots"):** Metal or wooden vises applied to the shins.
5. **Water torture:** Forced ingestion of water, or dripping of water onto the forehead.

### The Enlightenment Abolition Movement

The abolition of judicial torture was one of the signature achievements of the European Enlightenment.

**Key Figures:**

- **Christian Thomasius (1655-1728):** The German jurist published *De tortura* (1705), one of the first systematic arguments for the abolition of judicial torture, arguing that it was unreliable, unjust, and contrary to natural law.
- **Montesquieu (1689-1755):** In *De l'Esprit des Lois* (1748), Book VI, Chapter 17, Montesquieu argued that torture was unnecessary in a well-ordered state and that confession should not be required for conviction.
- **Cesare Beccaria (1738-1794):** In *Dei delitti e delle pene* (*On Crimes and Punishments*, 1764), Chapter XVI, Beccaria delivered the most influential critique of judicial torture: "The impression of pain may increase to such a degree that, filling up the whole sensibility, it leaves no freedom to the sufferer to do anything but choose the shortest way to relieve himself for the present. The response of the accused is then as inevitable as the impressions of fire and water." Beccaria argued that torture punished the accused before conviction, extracted unreliable confessions from the weak while allowing the strong-willed guilty to resist, and was fundamentally incompatible with the presumption of innocence.
- **Voltaire (1694-1778):** Publicized the case of Jean Calas (1762), a Protestant falsely convicted and executed for the murder of his son on the basis of a confession obtained under torture. The Calas affair became a cause célèbre of the Enlightenment critique of judicial torture.

**Legislative Abolition:**
- **Prussia (1740):** Frederick the Great abolished judicial torture immediately upon taking the throne, with limited exceptions for treason and mass murder cases.
- **Saxony (1770):** Abolished torture.
- **Austria (1776):** Maria Theresa abolished judicial torture.
- **France (1780-1789):** Louis XVI abolished the question préparatoire (torture for confession) in 1780; the Revolution abolished all forms of judicial torture in 1789.
- **Bavaria (1806):** Among the last German states to formally abolish torture.

## Common Law Origins

### England's Exceptional Position

English common law occupied a distinctive position in the European history of judicial torture, never incorporating torture into ordinary criminal procedure while tolerating its extralegal use under royal prerogative.

**The Common Law's Rejection:**

English criminal procedure, based on the jury trial rather than the inquisitorial system, did not require confession for conviction. The jury could convict on the basis of testimony and circumstantial evidence alone, eliminating the structural necessity for torture that drove its adoption on the Continent.

Sir John Fortescue, in *De Laudibus Legum Angliae* (c. 1470), contrasted English and Continental practice: "I would indeed prefer twenty guilty men to escape through their innocence, than for one innocent person to be condemned unjustly." Fortescue praised the English jury system as superior to the Continental reliance on judicial torture.

**Torture Under Prerogative:**

Despite the common law's formal rejection of torture, the Tudor and Stuart monarchs employed it through the exercise of the royal prerogative:

- **The Tower of London:** The principal site of judicial torture in England, housing the rack and other instruments. Records from the Tower indicate that approximately eighty torture warrants were issued between 1540 and 1640.
- **The Star Chamber:** The prerogative court of the Star Chamber (abolished 1641) employed torture, particularly in cases of sedition, treason, and religious dissent. The torture of Edmund Campion (1581, Jesuit priest accused of treason) and Guy Fawkes (1605, Gunpowder Plot) are among the best-documented cases.
- **The Privy Council:** Torture warrants were issued by the Privy Council rather than by common law courts, maintaining the legal fiction that torture was an act of prerogative rather than a judicial procedure.

**The End of English Torture:**

- **Felton's Case (1628):** After John Felton assassinated the Duke of Buckingham, the Privy Council considered torturing him to reveal accomplices. The common law judges, led by Chief Justice Hyde, advised that torture was contrary to the laws of England. The king reluctantly abandoned the plan.
- **Abolition of the Star Chamber (1641):** The Long Parliament abolished the Star Chamber and the Court of High Commission, eliminating the principal institutional mechanisms for torture in England.
- **Habeas Corpus Act (1679):** By strengthening the writ of habeas corpus, this act reinforced the procedural protections against arbitrary detention and coerced interrogation.

**Sir William Blackstone (Commentaries on the Laws of England, 1765-1769):** Blackstone declared that "the rack is an engine of state, not of law." He argued that the common law had never authorized judicial torture, that its use under prerogative was an abuse, and that the right against self-incrimination (nemo tenetur seipsum accusare) was fundamental to English liberty.

## Historical Significance and Modern Legacy

The history of judicial torture illuminates several profound truths about law, power, and human nature:

**The Paradox of Regulated Cruelty:** The most striking feature of judicial torture across all traditions is its meticulous regulation. Roman, Chinese, and European legal systems all developed detailed rules about who could be tortured, when, how many times, by what methods, and with what safeguards. This regulated character distinguishes judicial torture from arbitrary violence and raises the disturbing question of whether law's capacity to rationalize and normalize cruelty is itself a danger greater than lawlessness.

**Confession and Truth:** The assumption underlying judicial torture -- that physical pain reveals truth -- has been challenged across all traditions. Ulpian's observation that torture extracts lies from the weak and fails to break the strong, Beccaria's critique of confession's unreliability, and Song Ci's argument for forensic evidence over coerced confession all express the same fundamental insight: torture is an unreliable means of discovering truth. Modern interrogation science has confirmed this ancient critique.

**The Structural Imperative:** The history of European judicial torture demonstrates that procedural structures can create demand for torture. The requirement of confession or dual eyewitness testimony for conviction in the Roman-canonical system made torture structurally necessary. England's jury system, which allowed conviction on the basis of any evidence the jury found persuasive, removed this structural imperative. The lesson is that the abolition of torture requires not merely moral condemnation but structural reform of the evidentiary system.

**Modern Resonance:** Despite the near-universal prohibition of torture in modern international law (UDHR Article 5; ICCPR Article 7; UN Convention Against Torture, 1984), the practice persists. The debates following the September 11 attacks -- including the "ticking bomb" hypothetical and the controversy over "enhanced interrogation techniques" -- demonstrated that the arguments used to justify judicial torture in the 13th century can be revived in the 21st.
