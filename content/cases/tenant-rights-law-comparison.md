## Summary

Tenant protection and housing rights laws vary enormously across jurisdictions, reflecting fundamentally different conceptions of the balance between property rights and the right to housing, the role of the state in housing markets, and the social function of residential property. South Korea operates a unique dual rental system under the Housing Lease Protection Act (주택임대차보호법, 1981, most recently amended 2020), featuring the distinctive jeonse (전세) lump-sum deposit system alongside monthly rent (wolse, 월세), with statutory protections including a guaranteed minimum two-year lease term, a one-time two-year renewal right, and a 5% cap on rent increases during renewal. Germany provides what is widely regarded as the strongest tenant protection regime among major economies, anchored in the Civil Code (BGB §§ 535-580a), the Mietpreisbremse (rent brake) provision (§ 556d BGB), and constitutional recognition of housing as a social right, with near-unlimited lease durations, strict eviction protections requiring "legitimate interest" (berechtigtes Interesse), and rent increase caps tied to local comparative rents (Mietspiegel). The United States presents a highly fragmented landscape with no federal tenant protection statute, leaving housing law to the states, where provisions range from robust rent stabilization programs in New York City and Oregon to minimal tenant protections in many southern and rural states where landlords may terminate month-to-month tenancies with as little as 30 days' notice and no cause required. The ECHR framework protects housing rights through Article 8 (right to respect for the home) and Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (protection of property), with the Court balancing tenant protections against landlord property rights through its margin of appreciation doctrine.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's tenant protection regime is built on the Housing Lease Protection Act (주택임대차보호법, HLPA), enacted in 1981 and significantly strengthened in 2020, operating alongside general lease provisions in the Civil Act (민법, Articles 618-654):

**Housing Lease Protection Act (HLPA) Key Provisions:**

- **Article 4 -- Minimum Lease Term (존속기간):** Any residential lease is deemed to have a minimum duration of two years, regardless of the contractual term agreed between the parties. If the lease period is not specified or is set for less than two years, the tenant may assert a two-year term. However, the tenant may assert a shorter period if desired.
- **Article 6-3 -- Contract Renewal Right (계약갱신요구권, added 2020):** The tenant has a one-time right to request renewal of the lease for an additional two years, exercisable between six and two months before the contract's expiration. The landlord may refuse renewal only in limited circumstances: the tenant's default on two or more rent payments, the tenant's breach causing significant damage to the property, mutual consent, or the landlord's personal use of the property. This provision effectively extends the potential lease duration from two years to four years.
- **Article 7 -- Rent Increase Cap (차임 등의 증감청구권, amended 2020):** During a renewal, the landlord may increase the rent (or jeonse deposit) by no more than 5% of the existing amount. This cap applies to both monthly rent (wolse) and jeonse deposit increases.
- **Article 3 -- Third-Party Effectiveness (대항력):** A tenant who has taken possession of the premises and registered a move-in date (전입신고) at the local administrative office acquires "opposing force" (대항력) against subsequent purchasers of the property, meaning the lease survives a change of ownership.
- **Article 3-2 -- Priority Repayment Right (우선변제권):** A tenant who has both taken possession and obtained a fixed date stamp (확정일자) on the lease agreement acquires a priority lien on the property for the deposit amount, enforceable against the property in foreclosure or sale proceedings.
- **Article 8 -- Small-Amount Deposit Protection (소액임차인):** Tenants whose deposits fall below a threshold set by presidential decree (varying by region; e.g., KRW 50 million in Seoul) receive a super-priority claim on the property's sale proceeds, paid before even secured creditors up to a specified amount.

**Jeonse System (전세제도):**

South Korea's unique jeonse system involves the tenant paying a large lump-sum deposit -- typically 50-80% of the property's market value -- in lieu of monthly rent. The landlord uses the deposit as investment capital during the lease term and returns it in full upon lease expiration. The system is governed by both the HLPA and the Civil Act's provisions on jeonse rights (전세권, Articles 303-319 of the Civil Act).

**Jeonse Deposit Protection:**

- The Korea Housing and Urban Guarantee Corporation (HUG, 주택도시보증공사) offers jeonse deposit return guarantee insurance (전세보증금반환보증), protecting tenants against landlords who fail to return deposits upon lease termination.
- The Housing Lease Protection Act's priority lien system allows tenants with properly registered leases to recover deposits from the proceeds of property sales or foreclosures.
- Following the "jeonse fraud" crisis of 2022-2023, in which organized landlord fraud schemes left thousands of tenants unable to recover deposits, the government enacted emergency measures including enhanced criminal penalties for landlord fraud and expanded HUG guarantee coverage.

### Key Principles

South Korea's tenant protection framework has evolved from minimal protections in the 1980s to one of the strongest in Asia. The 2020 amendments (commonly known as the "Tenant Three Laws," 임대차 3법) were politically contentious: supporters argued they were necessary to protect tenants from rapid rent increases and displacement in Seoul's overheated housing market, while critics contended they reduced rental supply and actually increased rents for new tenants excluded from the cap protections. Empirical studies published in 2025 confirmed persistent regulatory effects: while renewal rights and rent caps benefited incumbent tenants, landlords compensated by setting higher initial rents for new tenants, and the supply of jeonse units continued to decline as landlords converted to wolse (monthly rent) to avoid deposit return obligations.

The jeonse system, once dominant, has been declining as a proportion of residential leases -- from approximately 60% in the early 2000s to under 35% by 2025 -- as landlords shift to monthly rent arrangements amid low interest rates and deposit fraud concerns.

### Notable Cases

- **Supreme Court 2013Da77198 (2014):** The Supreme Court held that a tenant's third-party effectiveness (대항력) under HLPA Article 3 protects the tenant even against auction purchasers, confirming that registered tenants survive foreclosure.
- **Constitutional Court 2019Hun-Ma1294 (2021):** The Court upheld the constitutionality of the 2020 "Tenant Three Laws" amendments, finding that the contract renewal right and 5% rent increase cap constituted a permissible restriction on property rights under Article 23 of the Constitution in pursuit of the social welfare objectives of Article 35(3) (right to comfortable housing).
- **Jeonse Fraud Prosecutions (2022-2024):** Multiple criminal prosecutions of organized jeonse fraud rings, including the "Villagate" (빌라왕) cases in which serial landlords purchased properties using jeonse deposits without sufficient equity, resulting in cascading defaults when property values declined. Over 30,000 victims were identified, and the principal defendants received sentences of up to 20 years imprisonment for fraud and breach of trust.

## United States

### Federal Framework

The United States has no comprehensive federal tenant protection statute. Federal involvement in housing law is limited to:

**Federal Constitutional Framework:**

- **Due Process Clause (5th and 14th Amendments):** Protects against arbitrary deprivation of property interests, including leasehold interests. However, there is no federal constitutional right to housing.
- **Takings Clause (5th Amendment):** Limits the government's ability to impose rent control regulations that constitute a regulatory taking. In Yee v. City of Escondido, 503 U.S. 519 (1992), the Supreme Court held that a mobile home rent control ordinance did not constitute a per se taking. In Lingle v. Chevron U.S.A., 544 U.S. 528 (2005), the Court clarified the framework for evaluating regulatory takings challenges to rent control.

**Federal Statutes:**

- **Fair Housing Act (42 U.S.C. §§ 3601-3619):** Prohibits discrimination in housing based on race, color, religion, sex, familial status, national origin, and disability. Does not regulate rent levels or lease terms but prohibits discriminatory evictions.
- **Uniform Residential Landlord and Tenant Act (URLTA, 1972):** A model act adopted in whole or part by approximately 21 states, establishing baseline standards for lease agreements, security deposits, landlord maintenance obligations, and eviction procedures.
- **CARES Act (2020) and CDC Eviction Moratorium (2020-2021):** Temporary pandemic-era federal protections prohibiting eviction of tenants in federally backed housing (CARES Act § 4024) and a broader CDC moratorium. The Supreme Court struck down the CDC moratorium in Alabama Association of Realtors v. HHS, 594 U.S. 758 (2021).
- **Section 8 Housing Choice Voucher Program (42 U.S.C. § 1437f):** Provides federal rental subsidies to low-income tenants, with voucher recipients choosing housing on the private market. Source of income discrimination protections vary by state and locality.

### State Variations

**Strong Tenant Protection:**

- **New York City Rent Stabilization:** The Rent Stabilization Law of 1969 (NYC Admin. Code § 26-501 et seq.) and Housing Stability and Tenant Protection Act (HSTPA, 2019) protect approximately 1 million rent-stabilized units. Tenants have rights to lease renewals, rent increases limited to Rent Guidelines Board determinations (typically 2-5% annually), succession rights for family members, and just-cause eviction protections. The HSTPA eliminated vacancy decontrol, meaning units remain rent-stabilized indefinitely.
- **Oregon Statewide Rent Control (SB 608, 2019):** The first statewide rent control law in the US, capping annual rent increases at 7% plus the consumer price index (CPI). Exempts buildings less than 15 years old. Requires just-cause eviction for tenants with more than one year of tenancy.
- **California Tenant Protection Act (AB 1482, 2019):** Caps annual rent increases at 5% plus local CPI (maximum 10%) for covered units and requires just-cause eviction for tenants of 12+ months. Exempts single-family homes (with notice) and units less than 15 years old.
- **Washington State (SB 5961, 2024):** Enacted just-cause eviction protections statewide, prohibiting landlords from terminating month-to-month tenancies without specified legitimate grounds.

**Minimal Tenant Protection:**

- **Texas:** No state rent control (Tex. Prop. Code § 214.902 prohibits local rent control ordinances). No just-cause eviction requirement. Landlords may terminate month-to-month tenancies with one month's notice. No statutory limit on late fees. Security deposit return required within 30 days (Tex. Prop. Code § 92.103).
- **Georgia:** No rent control. No just-cause eviction requirement. Landlords may retain security deposits if the tenant fails to provide proper notice. No implied warranty of habitability under common law (though statutory requirements exist).
- **Arkansas:** One of the few states that retains criminal penalties for tenants who fail to vacate after lease termination, with "failure to vacate" constituting a misdemeanor.

### Notable Cases

- **Alabama Association of Realtors v. HHS, 594 U.S. 758 (2021):** The Supreme Court vacated the CDC's nationwide eviction moratorium, holding that the CDC exceeded its statutory authority under 42 U.S.C. § 264a, which authorizes "regulations...necessary to prevent the introduction, transmission, or spread of communicable diseases."
- **Community Housing Improvement Program (CHIP) v. City of New York (2024):** Landlord groups challenged New York's rent stabilization framework as an unconstitutional taking under the Fifth Amendment. The Second Circuit upheld the rent stabilization system in 2024, and the Supreme Court declined certiorari, leaving the most significant challenge to rent stabilization in decades unresolved.
- **Lindsey v. Normet, 405 U.S. 56 (1972):** The Supreme Court held that there is no federal constitutional right to housing and that the need for "decent shelter" does not require Oregon to provide it. This remains the foundational precedent limiting federal constitutional challenges to inadequate housing.
- **Block v. Hirsh, 256 U.S. 135 (1921):** The Supreme Court upheld the constitutionality of rent control as a legitimate exercise of the police power, establishing the enduring principle that rent regulation does not per se constitute an unconstitutional taking.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany provides comprehensive tenant protection through the Civil Code (BGB), supplemented by federal and state housing legislation:

**BGB Tenancy Provisions (§§ 535-580a):**

- **§ 535 -- Lease Agreement (Mietvertrag):** The landlord is obligated to provide and maintain the leased premises in a condition suitable for the contractually agreed use. The tenant is obligated to pay the agreed rent.
- **§ 536 -- Rent Reduction for Defects (Mietminderung):** If the premises have defects that substantially impair their suitability for the contractual use, the tenant's rent obligation is reduced proportionally. No notice or court order is required; the reduction applies automatically by operation of law.
- **§ 543 -- Extraordinary Termination for Important Cause (Außerordentliche fristlose Kündigung):** Either party may terminate the lease without notice for important cause. For the landlord, this includes non-payment of rent for two consecutive months or a total of two months' rent in arrears.
- **§ 556d -- Rent Brake (Mietpreisbremse):** In areas designated by state governments as having a "tight housing market" (angespannter Wohnungsmarkt), the rent for new leases may not exceed 10% above the local comparative rent (ortsübliche Vergleichsmiete) as determined by the Mietspiegel (rent index). Exemptions apply to newly constructed buildings (first occupation after 1 October 2014) and extensively modernized apartments.
- **§ 557-558 -- Rent Increase Cap (Kappungsgrenze):** For existing leases, rent increases are limited to 20% over any three-year period (reduced to 15% in designated tight markets). Increases must be justified by reference to the Mietspiegel, comparable rents, or an expert appraisal. The tenant must consent to the increase; if the tenant refuses, the landlord must seek a court order.
- **§ 559 -- Modernization Rent Increase (Modernisierungsmieterhöhung):** After modernization works, the landlord may increase the annual rent by up to 8% of the modernization costs (reduced from 11% in 2019). A hardship cap limits modernization increases to EUR 3 per square meter over six years (EUR 2 for apartments with rent below EUR 7/sqm).
- **§ 566 -- Principle of "Purchase Does Not Break Lease" (Kauf bricht nicht Miete):** If the property is sold, the buyer steps into the landlord's position and is bound by the existing lease. This is one of the strongest protections in German tenancy law.
- **§ 573 -- Legitimate Interest for Termination (Berechtigtes Interesse an der Kündigung):** The landlord may terminate a residential lease only if there is a "legitimate interest," which is defined by statute as: (1) a significant breach of contractual obligations by the tenant; (2) the landlord's own need for the premises (Eigenbedarf); or (3) the prevention of substantial economic disadvantage to the landlord. Termination for the purpose of achieving a higher rent is explicitly excluded as a legitimate interest.
- **§ 574 -- Social Hardship Clause (Sozialklausel):** Even if the landlord demonstrates a legitimate interest in termination, the tenant may object if the termination would constitute undue hardship that is not justified by the landlord's interests. Factors include the tenant's age, health, pregnancy, duration of tenancy, and availability of comparable housing. Courts frequently apply this provision to protect elderly and long-term tenants.
- **§ 551 -- Security Deposit (Mietkaution):** The deposit may not exceed three months' cold rent (Nettokaltmiete). The landlord must hold the deposit in a separate interest-bearing account. The deposit must be returned within a reasonable period after the end of the tenancy (typically 3-6 months).

**Mietspiegel (Rent Index):**

The Mietspiegel is a statistical survey of local comparative rents, compiled by municipalities in cooperation with landlord and tenant associations. Since the Mietspiegelreform (2022), municipalities with more than 50,000 inhabitants are required to produce a Mietspiegel. It serves as the primary reference point for permissible rent levels and rent increase justifications.

### Key Principles

Germany's tenant protection regime is rooted in the constitutional principle of the social obligation of property (Sozialpflichtigkeit des Eigentums, Article 14(2) GG: "Property entails obligations. Its use shall also serve the public good") and the social state principle (Sozialstaatsprinzip, Article 20(1) GG). The Federal Constitutional Court has consistently held that rent control and tenant protection measures constitute permissible limitations on property rights under Article 14(1) GG.

Germany is a nation of renters: approximately 50% of German households rent (rising to over 80% in Berlin), the highest rental rate among major economies. This demographic reality gives tenant protection exceptional political salience. The Berlin rent cap experiment (Mietendeckel, 2020-2021) -- a state-level law that froze rents and mandated reductions for overpriced apartments -- was struck down by the Federal Constitutional Court in April 2021 (BVerfG, 2 BvF 1/20) on the grounds that federal law (the BGB) preempted state-level rent regulation, not on substantive constitutional grounds. This ruling confirmed that rent control authority resides with the federal government.

Notice periods for landlord terminations are among the longest in the world: three months for tenancies up to five years, six months for tenancies of five to eight years, and nine months for tenancies of eight or more years (§ 573c BGB). Tenants may always terminate with three months' notice regardless of tenancy duration.

### Notable Cases

- **BVerfG, 2 BvF 1/20 (25 March 2021) -- Berlin Mietendeckel Decision:** The Federal Constitutional Court struck down Berlin's rent cap law (Gesetz zur Mietenbegrenzung im Wohnungswesen in Berlin, MietenWoG Bln) as unconstitutional, holding that the federal government has exclusive competence over tenancy law under Article 74(1) No. 1 GG, and that federal law (BGB §§ 556d et seq.) exhaustively regulates permissible rent levels.
- **BVerfG, 1 BvR 1711/18 (18 July 2019) -- Mietpreisbremse Decision:** The Federal Constitutional Court upheld the constitutionality of the Mietpreisbremse (rent brake), finding that the 10% cap above the Mietspiegel for new leases is a proportionate limitation on property rights serving the legitimate goal of ensuring affordable housing.
- **BGH, VIII ZR 109/20 (12 January 2022):** The Federal Court of Justice ruled that landlords must disclose specific grounds for Eigenbedarf (personal need) terminations and that pretextual Eigenbedarf claims constitute fraud entitling the tenant to damages and reinstatement.
- **BGH, VIII ZR 277/16 (22 November 2017):** Addressed the scope of the social hardship defense (§ 574), holding that advanced age and long duration of tenancy can constitute sufficient hardship to override a landlord's legitimate interest in termination, even for Eigenbedarf.

## ECHR / EU Framework

### Convention Articles

- **Article 8 -- Right to Respect for the Home (Recht auf Achtung der Wohnung):** The ECtHR has held that Article 8 protects not only the physical home but also the right to peaceful enjoyment of one's dwelling. States must ensure that evictions comply with procedural safeguards and proportionality requirements. The loss of one's home is "the most extreme form of interference with the right to respect for the home" (Connors v. the United Kingdom, 2004).
- **Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 -- Protection of Property:** Protects both landlord property rights and tenant possessory interests. The ECtHR applies a proportionality test balancing the general interest in housing policy against individual property rights. Rent control and tenant protection measures fall within the state's "margin of appreciation" but must not impose a "disproportionate and excessive burden" on landlords.
- **Article 14 -- Non-Discrimination:** Prohibits discriminatory application of housing laws, including differential treatment based on race, ethnicity, or other protected characteristics in access to housing or eviction proceedings.

### Key Case Law

- **Hutten-Czapska v. Poland (Grand Chamber, 2006, App. No. 35014/97):** The landmark case on rent control and landlord property rights. The Court found that Poland's rent control system, which limited rent to levels far below market value and maintenance costs, violated Article 1 of Protocol No. 1. The Court applied the "pilot judgment" procedure, finding a systemic problem affecting approximately 100,000 landlords and ordering Poland to reform its rent control system. The judgment established that while rent control is permissible in principle, it must not deprive landlords of a reasonable return on their investment.
- **Connors v. the United Kingdom (2004, App. No. 66746/01):** The Court held that the eviction of a Roma family from a local authority caravan site without adequate procedural safeguards violated Article 8. The case established that any person at risk of losing their home must have access to an independent tribunal to assess the proportionality of the eviction.
- **Gladysheva v. Russia (2011, App. No. 7097/10):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 and Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 where a tenant was evicted from a flat she had purchased in good faith because of a prior fraudulent transaction in the property's chain of title, establishing that tenants and occupiers may have property rights enforceable against the state.
- **FH v. Sweden (2024, App. No. 69311/17, admissibility decision):** The Court considered whether Sweden's failure to provide adequate social housing to a homeless family violated Article 8, contributing to the developing jurisprudence on positive obligations in housing.
- **Bitto and Others v. Slovakia (2014, App. No. 30255/09):** Following the pilot judgment approach of Hutten-Czapska, the Court found that Slovakia's rent control system similarly violated landlord property rights by maintaining artificially low regulated rents.

### Member State Obligations

Under the ECHR and European Social Charter framework, states must:

- Ensure that eviction proceedings comply with procedural fairness and proportionality requirements under Article 8.
- Maintain rent control systems that balance tenant protection with landlord property rights, ensuring landlords can receive a reasonable return.
- Provide legal aid and access to courts for tenants facing eviction.
- Take positive measures to address homelessness and ensure adequate housing (European Social Charter, Article 31).
- Prohibit discrimination in housing (Article 14 ECHR; EU Race Equality Directive 2000/43/EC; EU Equal Treatment Directive 2006/54/EC).

The **Revised European Social Charter (1996), Article 31** provides the most explicit international legal protection for housing rights: "With a view to ensuring the effective exercise of the right to housing, the Parties undertake to take measures designed to promote access to housing of an adequate standard, to prevent and reduce homelessness with a view to its gradual elimination, and to make the price of housing accessible to those without adequate resources."

## Eviction Processes and Tenant Defenses

### Eviction Procedures Compared

The eviction process varies dramatically across jurisdictions, reflecting fundamentally different approaches to the balance between landlord property rights and tenant security of tenure:

**South Korea:**

Under the HLPA, eviction of a residential tenant is extremely difficult during the initial two-year lease term and the renewal period. The landlord may refuse a renewal request only on specific statutory grounds (Article 6-3). If the landlord wishes to recover possession at the end of the lease, the landlord must provide notice of non-renewal at least six months before the lease expires; failure to provide timely notice results in the lease being deemed renewed under the same terms. Eviction for non-payment of rent requires judicial proceedings before the district court (지방법원), which typically take 3-6 months. Even after obtaining a court order, enforcement through the bailiff (집행관) may take an additional 1-3 months.

**United States:**

Eviction procedures vary enormously by state:

- **Fast-track states (e.g., Texas, Georgia):** Eviction proceedings (forcible entry and detainer) can be completed in as few as 21-30 days from initial notice to physical removal. No just-cause requirement for month-to-month tenancies.
- **Slow-track states (e.g., New York, New Jersey):** Eviction proceedings routinely take 6-12 months or longer, particularly in rent-stabilized units. New York City housing court processes over 200,000 eviction filings annually, with significant backlogs. The right to counsel for tenants facing eviction, enacted in New York City (Intro. 214-B, 2017) and several other jurisdictions, has reduced default judgments by approximately 30%.
- **Pandemic-era protections:** Many states enacted temporary eviction moratoria during 2020-2021 that have since expired. Some jurisdictions retained expanded tenant protections permanently, including right-to-cure periods and mandatory rental assistance referrals before filing for eviction.

**Germany:**

Eviction in Germany is among the most difficult and time-consuming processes in the developed world:

1. The landlord must first provide written notice of termination (Kündigung) with statutory notice periods: 3 months (tenancy under 5 years), 6 months (5-8 years), or 9 months (over 8 years).
2. The termination must state a "legitimate interest" (§ 573 BGB).
3. The tenant may assert the social hardship defense (§ 574 BGB).
4. If the tenant does not vacate, the landlord must file an eviction lawsuit (Räumungsklage) in the district court (Amtsgericht).
5. Court proceedings typically take 6-12 months; complex cases may take longer.
6. Even after judgment, the court may grant the tenant an "eviction protection period" (Räumungsfrist) of up to one year (§ 721 ZPO).
7. Physical eviction is carried out by the court bailiff (Gerichtsvollzieher) and requires a separate execution procedure.

Total time from initial notice to physical eviction: typically 12-24 months; in exceptional cases, longer.

### Tenant Defenses

Common tenant defenses across jurisdictions include:

| Defense | South Korea | United States | Germany | ECHR |
|---------|-------------|---------------|---------|------|
| **Retaliatory eviction** | Implicit protection under HLPA | Statutory protection in ~45 states | Protected under general principles of good faith (§ 242 BGB) | Proportionality assessment |
| **Habitability defense** | Civil Act Article 623 (landlord maintenance duty) | Implied warranty of habitability (most states) | § 536 BGB (automatic rent reduction for defects) | Article 8 positive obligations |
| **Procedural defects** | Strict compliance with notice requirements | Strict compliance; defective notice voids proceeding | Strict compliance; any procedural error invalidates termination | Fair trial (Article 6) |
| **Discrimination** | Anti-discrimination provisions | Fair Housing Act; state/local human rights laws | General Equal Treatment Act (AGG) | Article 14 non-discrimination |
| **Social hardship** | Not codified separately | Limited (elderly/disabled protections in some jurisdictions) | § 574 BGB (comprehensive social hardship clause) | Proportionality balancing |

## Global Perspectives

- **Japan:** The Land and Building Lease Act (借地借家法, 1991) provides strong tenant protections, including a "just cause" requirement for landlord termination and a statutory right of renewal. Japanese courts are highly protective of tenants, and eviction without cause is effectively impossible, leading to the phenomenon of "akiya" (empty houses) as landlords find it easier to leave properties vacant than to deal with tenant protections.
- **Brazil:** The Urban Lease Law (Lei do Inquilinato, Law No. 8,245/1991) provides a comprehensive framework for residential and commercial leases. Residential tenants have a right to renewal for an additional 30-month term, and eviction requires judicial authorization except in limited circumstances. Rent increases are linked to the IGP-M index.
- **India:** Rent control laws vary by state, with some states maintaining decades-old controlled rents that have become virtually nominal. The Model Tenancy Act, 2021, proposed by the central government, seeks to balance landlord and tenant interests with a cap on security deposits (two months' rent for residential), mandatory written agreements, and establishment of rent authorities and rent courts. Adoption by states has been slow.
- **Australia:** Residential tenancy laws are state-based. Victoria, Queensland, and the Australian Capital Territory enacted reforms in 2023-2024 limiting rent increases and restricting no-cause evictions. Nationally, rents increased by approximately 8% in 2024, generating significant political pressure for stronger federal involvement.
- **Nigeria:** The Tenancy Law of Lagos State (2011) provides a framework for residential tenancies in Lagos, including requirements for six-month notice before rent increases and prohibition of more than one year's rent in advance. Outside Lagos, tenant protections are governed by the Recovery of Premises Act (1990) and state-level laws with varying degrees of protection. The "Rent-in-Advance" culture, where landlords demand 1-2 years' rent upfront, remains a significant barrier to housing access.
- **Sweden:** The "hyresreglering" (rent regulation) system sets rents through collective bargaining between landlord organizations and the Swedish Union of Tenants, resulting in rents that are typically 20-40% below market rates. The system is under increasing strain due to housing shortages and the parallel growth of an unregulated subletting market.

## Rent Control Models and Economic Debate

### Types of Rent Control

Rent control systems across jurisdictions fall into several distinct categories:

- **First-generation rent control (hard controls):** Direct price ceilings on rent, typically set well below market rates. Examples include post-WWII rent freezes in the US and Europe. Most jurisdictions have moved away from this model due to well-documented adverse effects on housing supply and quality.
- **Second-generation rent control (soft controls / rent stabilization):** Limits on rent increases rather than absolute price ceilings. Examples include Germany's Mietpreisbremse (10% above Mietspiegel for new leases), Oregon's 7%+CPI cap, and South Korea's 5% renewal cap. These systems attempt to moderate rent increases while allowing rents to adjust over time.
- **Vacancy decontrol:** Systems where rent controls apply during a tenancy but are lifted upon vacancy, allowing landlords to reset rents to market levels for new tenants. This was the model in New York City before the 2019 HSTPA eliminated vacancy decontrol for rent-stabilized units.
- **Collective bargaining models:** Sweden's unique system where rents are set through negotiations between landlord organizations and the Swedish Union of Tenants (Hyresgästföreningen), producing "utility value rents" (bruksvärdeshyra) rather than market-based rents.

### Economic Arguments

The economic debate over rent control remains among the most contested in housing policy:

**Arguments for rent regulation:**

1. Prevents displacement of long-term residents and maintains community stability.
2. Protects tenants from market power asymmetries, particularly in tight housing markets with low vacancy rates.
3. Reduces inequality by preventing housing cost-driven poverty.
4. Germany's experience demonstrates that a well-designed regulatory system can coexist with a functional rental market: Germany has the highest rental rate among major economies (~50%) and a relatively stable housing market.

**Arguments against rent regulation:**

1. Reduces incentives for new housing construction, exacerbating supply shortages over time.
2. Creates misallocation as tenants in rent-controlled units have reduced incentive to move even when the unit no longer matches their needs.
3. South Korea's experience illustrates the "two-tier market" effect: the 5% renewal cap benefits incumbent tenants but has been associated with higher initial rents for new tenants, as landlords front-load anticipated rent increases.
4. The economic literature (Diamond et al., 2019, studying San Francisco) found that rent control reduced rental supply by 15% through conversion to condominiums and other strategies.
