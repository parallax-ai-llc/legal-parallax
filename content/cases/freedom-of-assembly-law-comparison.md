## Summary

Freedom of assembly -- the right to gather peacefully for common purposes including political protest, religious worship, and civic participation -- is fundamental to democratic governance, yet every legal system imposes some limitations on this right in the interests of public order, safety, and the rights of others. South Korea guarantees freedom of assembly and association under Article 21 of the Constitution (대한민국 헌법 제21조) and regulates assemblies through the Assembly and Demonstration Act (집회 및 시위에 관한 법률), which requires advance notification for outdoor assemblies and imposes restrictions on assemblies near certain government buildings, foreign diplomatic missions, and courts; the Constitutional Court (헌법재판소) has struck down several provisions as unconstitutional while upholding others, navigating the tension between a robust protest culture and public order concerns in a country where mass demonstrations -- including the 2016-2017 candlelight protests that led to President Park Geun-hye's impeachment -- have played a defining role in democratic development. The United States protects the right of assembly through the First Amendment, with the Supreme Court having developed a framework distinguishing between traditional public forums (streets, sidewalks, parks), designated public forums, and nonpublic forums, and permitting reasonable time, place, and manner restrictions that are content-neutral and leave open ample alternative channels of communication. Germany protects freedom of assembly under Article 8 of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz), which guarantees the right to assemble peacefully and without arms, with the Brokdorf decision (BVerfGE 69, 315 (1985)) establishing that assembly is a fundamental element of democratic self-governance and that restrictions must satisfy strict proportionality requirements. The European Convention on Human Rights protects freedom of peaceful assembly under Article 11, with the European Court of Human Rights holding that states bear positive obligations to facilitate peaceful assembly and may not impose blanket bans or disproportionate restrictions, while the OSCE Guidelines on Freedom of Peaceful Assembly provide detailed best-practice standards.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Article 21(1) of the Constitution guarantees that "all citizens shall enjoy the freedom of speech and the press, and the freedom of assembly and association." Article 21(2) provides that "licensing of assembly and association shall not be recognized," establishing a constitutional prohibition against prior licensing or permit systems.

The Assembly and Demonstration Act (집회 및 시위에 관한 법률), first enacted in 1962 and significantly amended multiple times, is the primary statute regulating assemblies. The Act requires organizers of outdoor assemblies or demonstrations to notify the competent police authority at least 48 hours before the planned event. This notification system is theoretically distinct from a permit system, but critics argue that police exercise de facto veto power through the ability to issue prohibition notices.

### Constitutional Court Jurisprudence

The Constitutional Court has played a critical role in defining the boundaries of assembly rights:

**Nighttime Assembly Ban (2009헌가17, 2014):** The Court struck down Article 10 of the Assembly and Demonstration Act, which prohibited outdoor assemblies after sunset and before sunrise, finding it an excessive restriction on freedom of assembly that failed the proportionality test. The decision was prompted in part by the 2008 candlelight protests against U.S. beef imports, during which thousands of citizens were technically in violation of the nighttime ban.

**Buffer Zones (2003헌가18, 2003):** The Court struck down the absolute prohibition on assemblies within 100 meters of foreign diplomatic missions, ruling that a blanket ban was disproportionate and that the restriction should be applied only when an assembly posed a concrete threat to diplomatic functions.

**National Assembly Buffer Zone:** The Court upheld restrictions on assemblies near the National Assembly during sessions, finding a sufficient justification in protecting the legislative function.

### Candlelight Revolution (촛불혁명, 2016-2017)

The 2016-2017 candlelight protests represent the most significant exercise of assembly rights in South Korean history. Following revelations of influence-peddling by President Park Geun-hye's confidante Choi Soon-sil, millions of citizens participated in weekly Saturday demonstrations in central Seoul from October 2016 through March 2017. An estimated cumulative total of 17 million people participated in the largely peaceful protests, which culminated in the National Assembly's vote to impeach President Park on December 9, 2016, and the Constitutional Court's unanimous confirmation of the impeachment on March 10, 2017.

### Trade Union Assemblies

The Trade Union and Labor Relations Adjustment Act (노동조합 및 노동관계조정법) provides additional protections for labor-related assemblies and strikes, though South Korea has historically imposed significant restrictions on public-sector labor organization and has faced criticism from the International Labour Organization for limitations on trade union rights.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The First Amendment protects "the right of the people peaceably to assemble, and to petition the Government for a redress of grievances." The Supreme Court has incorporated this right against state and local governments through the Fourteenth Amendment and developed the public forum doctrine to govern the extent of government regulation.

### Public Forum Doctrine

| Forum Type | Examples | Standard of Review |
|-----------|----------|-------------------|
| Traditional public forum | Streets, sidewalks, public parks | Content-based: strict scrutiny; content-neutral time/place/manner: intermediate scrutiny |
| Designated public forum | Government-opened spaces for expression | Same as traditional public forum while designation remains |
| Limited public forum | Spaces opened for specific speakers/subjects | Restrictions must be viewpoint-neutral and reasonable |
| Nonpublic forum | Military bases, airports, government workplaces | Restrictions must be viewpoint-neutral and reasonable |

### Key Decisions

**Hague v. Committee for Industrial Organization, 307 U.S. 496 (1939):** Established that streets and parks "have immemorially been held in trust for the use of the public" for assembly and discussion.

**Cox v. Louisiana, 379 U.S. 536 (1965):** Upheld the right to demonstrate near a courthouse while recognizing the state's interest in maintaining judicial proceedings free from outside pressure.

**National Socialist Party of America v. Village of Skokie, 432 U.S. 43 (1977):** Held that even deeply offensive speech and assembly -- a neo-Nazi march through a community of Holocaust survivors -- is constitutionally protected.

**McCullen v. Coakley, 573 U.S. 464 (2014):** Struck down Massachusetts's 35-foot buffer zone around abortion clinics as not narrowly tailored, while reaffirming that content-neutral buffer zones may be permissible if carefully drawn.

### Modern Issues

The right of assembly has been tested by the Black Lives Matter protests of 2020, the January 6, 2021, breach of the U.S. Capitol, and increasing use of anti-protest legislation by state legislatures. Since 2017, numerous states have enacted laws increasing penalties for protest-related conduct, including blocking roadways and interfering with critical infrastructure, raising concerns about the chilling effect on constitutionally protected assembly.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Article 8 of the Basic Law provides: "(1) All Germans shall have the right to assemble peacefully and without arms without prior notification or permission. (2) In the case of outdoor assemblies, this right may be restricted by or pursuant to a law."

The Versammlungsgesetz (Assembly Act), enacted in 1953, establishes the federal framework for regulating assemblies. Following the 2006 federalism reform, the Länder gained legislative competence over assembly law, and several states have enacted their own assembly statutes, including Bavaria (Bayerisches Versammlungsgesetz, 2008), Saxony-Anhalt, Niedersachsen, and Schleswig-Holstein.

### Brokdorf Decision (BVerfGE 69, 315 (1985))

The Federal Constitutional Court's Brokdorf decision is the foundational ruling on assembly rights in Germany. Arising from protests against a nuclear power plant, the Court held that the right of assembly is essential to democratic self-governance and functions as a means by which individuals who lack institutional power can exercise collective political influence. The Court established several key principles:

- Assemblies are presumptively permitted; restrictions are the exception requiring justification
- The state bears a duty to facilitate peaceful assembly, not merely tolerate it
- The notification requirement serves cooperative purposes (enabling police to protect the assembly and maintain public order), not authorization purposes
- A blanket prohibition on assemblies is permissible only when there is an immediate, concrete threat to public safety that cannot be addressed through less restrictive means

### Bannmeile (Buffer Zone) Provisions

The Gesetz über befriedete Bezirke für Verfassungsorgane des Bundes (Act on Pacified Districts for Constitutional Organs of the Federation) establishes restricted zones around the Bundestag, Bundesrat, and Federal Constitutional Court. Assemblies within these zones require permission and may be prohibited if they threaten to impair the functioning of the constitutional organs.

### Vermummungsverbot (Masking Prohibition)

Section 17a of the Versammlungsgesetz prohibits wearing face coverings at assemblies or carrying objects intended for concealment. Violation is punishable by imprisonment up to one year or a fine. This provision has been upheld by the Federal Constitutional Court as a proportionate restriction.

## ECHR & EU

### Article 11 ECHR

Article 11 provides: "(1) Everyone has the right to freedom of peaceful assembly and to freedom of association with others... (2) No restrictions shall be placed on the exercise of these rights other than such as are prescribed by law and are necessary in a democratic society in the interests of national security or public safety, for the prevention of disorder or crime, for the protection of health or morals or for the protection of the rights and freedoms of others."

### Key ECHR Jurisprudence

**Plattform "Ärzte für das Leben" v. Austria (Application No. 10126/82, 1988):** The Court established that Article 11 imposes positive obligations on states to take reasonable and appropriate measures to enable lawful demonstrations to proceed peacefully, including protection from counter-demonstrators who seek to disrupt the assembly.

**Ezelin v. France (Application No. 11800/85, 1991):** The Court held that the freedom to take part in a peaceful assembly is of such importance that it cannot be restricted in any way so long as the person concerned does not himself commit any reprehensible act on such an occasion.

**Bukta and Others v. Hungary (Application No. 25691/04, 2007):** The Court held that the automatic dissolution of an assembly solely because it lacked prior notification violated Article 11, recognizing the right of spontaneous assembly where prior notification is impracticable due to the urgency of the subject matter.

**Kudrevičius and Others v. Lithuania (Application No. 37553/05, Grand Chamber, 2015):** The Grand Chamber held that intentional disruption of traffic by farmers blocking roads during a protest went beyond the "normal" disruption inherent in peaceful assembly and could be subject to proportionate penalties, while reaffirming that some disruption of ordinary life must be tolerated.

**Navalnyy and Yashin v. Russia (Application No. 76204/11, 2014):** The Court found that Russia's arrest and conviction of opposition activists for participating in an unauthorized assembly violated Article 11, emphasizing that dispersal of a peaceful assembly and criminal prosecution of participants must be justified by a pressing social need.

### OSCE/ODIHR Guidelines

The OSCE/ODIHR Guidelines on Freedom of Peaceful Assembly (4th edition, 2019, prepared jointly with the Venice Commission) provide detailed best-practice standards including the presumption in favor of assembly, the principle of proportionality, the duty to protect peaceful assembly, and the prohibition on blanket bans based solely on prior notification failures.

## Comparative Analysis

### Notification vs. Permit Systems

South Korea and Germany both require 48-hour advance notification for outdoor assemblies, while the United States permits local governments to impose permit requirements subject to content-neutrality constraints. All four systems distinguish between notification (informing authorities) and permission (requiring authorization), with the ECHR framework explicitly holding that lack of notification alone cannot justify dissolution of a peaceful assembly. In practice, the distinction between notification and permission is often blurred, particularly in South Korea where police prohibition notices effectively function as permit denials.

### Spontaneous Assembly Protection

All four jurisdictions recognize the right to spontaneous assembly when the subject matter requires an immediate response and prior notification is impracticable. The ECHR's Bukta decision provides the clearest articulation, while Germany's Brokdorf framework and the U.S. public forum doctrine achieve similar results through different doctrinal pathways. South Korea's Constitutional Court has recognized spontaneous assembly rights, though enforcement has been inconsistent.

### Protest and Public Order Policing

The jurisdictions diverge significantly on police powers during assemblies. Germany's proportionality principle and duty to facilitate assembly impose strict constraints on police intervention. The ECHR framework requires states to protect peaceful assemblies and prohibits excessive force under Article 3. The United States applies a Fourth Amendment reasonableness standard to police use of force. South Korea has faced criticism for aggressive policing of assemblies, including the use of water cannons and mass arrests during the 2008 and 2015 protests, though the 2016-2017 candlelight protests were notable for largely cooperative police-protester relations.

### Digital and Virtual Assembly

The concept of assembly is evolving to encompass digital gatherings and virtual protests. South Korea's candlelight protests utilized social media for coordination, raising questions about whether online organizing constitutes assembly. The United States has not yet addressed whether virtual gatherings receive the same First Amendment protections as physical assemblies. Germany's Federal Constitutional Court has not specifically addressed digital assembly rights, though existing principles of assembly law likely extend to the organizational aspects of online coordination. The ECHR has not yet developed specific jurisprudence on digital assembly, though the Court's recognition of social media as a modern public forum (in the free expression context) suggests potential future extension.

### Assembly and Public Health

The COVID-19 pandemic created an unprecedented tension between assembly rights and public health:

**South Korea:** The government restricted large gatherings through the Infectious Disease Control and Prevention Act, though the Constitutional Court did not directly address the constitutionality of these restrictions. Protests continued on a reduced scale, with courts issuing mixed rulings on the permissibility of specific demonstration bans.

**United States:** In South Bay United Pentecostal Church v. Newsom, 590 U.S. ___ (2021), and Tandon v. Newsom, 593 U.S. ___ (2021), the Supreme Court struck down COVID-19 restrictions on religious gatherings, establishing that government may not treat religious gatherings less favorably than comparable secular activities. These decisions had implications for assembly rights generally.

**Germany:** The Federal Constitutional Court addressed pandemic assembly restrictions in multiple decisions, holding that blanket bans on all assemblies were disproportionate and that authorities must consider whether assemblies could be conducted safely with public health measures (masks, distancing). The Court emphasized that the right to assembly cannot be suspended entirely, even during a pandemic.

**ECHR:** The Court has not yet issued a judgment directly addressing COVID-19 assembly restrictions, though several cases are pending. The Venice Commission issued guidance emphasizing that pandemic-related assembly restrictions must be proportionate, time-limited, and subject to judicial review.

### Freedom of Association

Closely related to freedom of assembly is freedom of association -- the right to form and join organizations. All four jurisdictions protect this right, though with important differences:

South Korea's Constitution (Article 21) guarantees freedom of association, but the Trade Union and Labor Relations Adjustment Act restricts certain categories of workers from forming unions. The United States protects freedom of association through the First Amendment, with the Supreme Court recognizing both expressive association (NAACP v. Alabama, 1958) and intimate association. Germany protects freedom of association under Article 9 GG, which also specifically guarantees the right to form trade unions. The ECHR protects freedom of association under Article 11, with the Court developing extensive jurisprudence on trade union rights, political party registration, and NGO operations.

### Counterterrorism and Assembly Rights

Anti-terrorism legislation has imposed additional constraints on assembly rights across all jurisdictions. The United States' material support statute (18 U.S.C. Section 2339B), upheld in Holder v. Humanitarian Law Project (2010), criminalizes providing "material support" to designated terrorist organizations, potentially affecting expressive activities. Germany's Vereinsverbot (association ban) under Article 9(2) GG permits the prohibition of organizations whose activities violate criminal law or are directed against constitutional order. South Korea's National Security Act constrains assemblies related to pro-North Korean organizations. The ECHR framework permits restrictions on assemblies connected to terrorist organizations under Article 11(2).

### Private Property and Assembly

The extent to which assembly rights extend to private property raises distinct issues. The United States has generally held that the First Amendment does not apply to private property (Lloyd Corp. v. Tanner, 1972), though some state constitutions provide broader protections (Pruneyard Shopping Center v. Robins, 1980). Germany distinguishes between public and private spaces, with assembly rights applying primarily to public forums. South Korea's assembly law regulates outdoor public assemblies but does not compel private property owners to permit assemblies.
