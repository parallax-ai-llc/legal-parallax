## Summary

Juvenile justice -- the body of law governing the treatment of persons below the age of majority who are suspected or convicted of criminal offenses -- reflects fundamentally different philosophies across jurisdictions regarding the capacity of young people for criminal responsibility, the relative weight of punishment versus rehabilitation, and the procedural safeguards owed to minors in conflict with the law. South Korea's system, governed by the Juvenile Act (소년법) and Article 9 of the Criminal Act (형법), sets the minimum age of criminal responsibility at 14 and channels offenders aged 10 to 18 through a protective disposition system emphasizing rehabilitation, though mounting public pressure following high-profile school violence cases has prompted serious legislative debate about lowering the criminal age threshold. The United States operates a decentralized system in which federal law -- principally the Juvenile Justice and Delinquency Prevention Act (JJDPA) of 1974 and the Federal Juvenile Delinquency Act (18 U.S.C. Chapter 403) -- sets baseline protections, while fifty states independently define ages of jurisdiction, transfer mechanisms to adult court, and sentencing ranges, all subject to constitutional constraints established by the U.S. Supreme Court in landmark decisions from *In re Gault* (1967) through *Montgomery v. Louisiana* (2016). Germany's Jugendgerichtsgesetz (JGG, Youth Courts Act) of 1953, as amended, applies a distinctive education-centered model covering juveniles aged 14 to 17 and extending youth criminal law to young adults aged 18 to 20 based on individual maturity assessments, with youth imprisonment reserved as a strict last resort. At the supranational level, the European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR), as interpreted by the European Court of Human Rights in cases such as *T v. United Kingdom* (1999) and *Bouamar v. Belgium* (1988), together with EU Directive 2016/800 on procedural safeguards for children in criminal proceedings and the Council of Europe's Guidelines on Child-Friendly Justice (2010), collectively establish minimum standards for fair trial rights, effective participation, detention conditions, and the primacy of the best interests of the child in juvenile criminal proceedings.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's juvenile justice system is governed by two principal statutes -- the Criminal Act (형법) and the Juvenile Act (소년법) -- supplemented by the Act on the Treatment of Protected Juveniles (보호소년 등의 처우에 관한 법률) and related enforcement decrees.

**Criminal Act (형법), Article 9:** The foundational provision on juvenile criminal capacity states: "The act of a person under fourteen years of age shall not be punished." This provision, unchanged since the Criminal Act's enactment in 1953, establishes 14 as the minimum age of criminal responsibility (MACR). Article 9 uses Korean age; under international age calculation, this effectively applies to children under approximately 13 years old.

**Criminal Act, Article 72 (Sentencing Limitations):**

| Situation | Sentencing Rule |
|-----------|----------------|
| Offense punishable by death or life imprisonment | Maximum sentence of 15 years for defendants under 18 |
| Offense punishable by imprisonment of a fixed term (2+ years) | Indeterminate sentence with maximum not exceeding 10 years and minimum not exceeding 5 years |
| Discretionary reduction for youth | Sentence may be mitigated when the defendant is under 19 at the time of the offense |

**Juvenile Act (소년법):** The Juvenile Act defines "juvenile" as a person under 19 years of age (Korean age) and establishes specialized juvenile courts and protective disposition procedures. The Act classifies juvenile offenders into three categories:

| Category | Age Range | Legal Treatment |
|----------|-----------|-----------------|
| Criminal juveniles (범죄소년) | 14-18 | Subject to criminal prosecution or protective disposition |
| Law-contacting juveniles (촉법소년) | 10-13 | Subject to protective disposition only; not criminally punishable |
| Pre-delinquent juveniles (우범소년) | 10-18 | Subject to protective disposition for status offenses or risk factors |

### Protective Dispositions

The Juvenile Act provides for ten graduated levels of protective disposition (보호처분), ordered by the juvenile court:

| Disposition No. | Measure | Maximum Duration |
|-----------------|---------|------------------|
| 1 | Consignment to care of guardian or suitable person | 6 months |
| 2 | Attendance at counseling/education lectures | 12 months |
| 3 | Community service order | 6 months |
| 4 | Short-term probation | 1 year |
| 5 | Long-term probation | 2 years |
| 6 | Consignment to child welfare facility or foster home | 6 months |
| 7 | Consignment to hospital or therapeutic facility | 6 months |
| 8 | Short-term commitment to juvenile reformatory (소년원) | 1 month |
| 9 | Medium-term commitment to juvenile reformatory | 6 months |
| 10 | Long-term commitment to juvenile reformatory | 2 years |

### Landmark Cases and Developments

**The Busan Middle School Assault Case (2017):** A group of middle school girls in Busan subjected a 14-year-old classmate to prolonged, severe physical violence. When the investigation began, the public realized that under the Juvenile Act the attackers would be processed through juvenile court with its rehabilitative orientation rather than the adult criminal system. One of the principal attackers was identified as 13 years old, rendering her entirely immune from criminal prosecution under Article 9. A Blue House petition demanding reform of the Juvenile Act garnered over 290,000 signatures. Three of the attackers were ultimately tried as adults; two received two-year sentences and the third received a shorter term. The case became the most significant catalyst for the ongoing debate over lowering the age of criminal responsibility.[^1]

**The Incheon Elementary School Assault (2022):** A series of violent assaults by elementary school students against classmates in 2022 reignited public anger over the perceived inadequacy of the juvenile justice system. Justice Minister Han Dong-hoon ordered the Ministry of Justice to review lowering the MACR from 14 to 12, and the Ministry pledged to work with lawmakers to revise juvenile laws.[^2]

**2025-2026 Legislative Deliberation:** Following President Lee Jae Myung's signal of support for reducing the MACR from 14 to 13, the government established a public deliberation committee comprising government officials, legal experts, and citizens to evaluate whether to lower the threshold for the first time in over 70 years. The National Court Administration and the National Human Rights Commission of Korea have expressed opposition, emphasizing the importance of rehabilitation-focused procedures and citing international standards that recommend maintaining or raising the MACR.[^3]

### Criticisms

South Korea's juvenile justice system faces criticism from two opposing directions. Victims' advocates and the general public increasingly view the system as excessively lenient, particularly in cases of school violence and gang-related crime involving minors who exploit knowledge of their legal immunity. Conversely, human rights organizations and child welfare experts argue that lowering the MACR would violate the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child (to which South Korea is a party) and would not effectively deter juvenile crime, pointing to research showing that harsher punishments for juveniles correlate with higher recidivism rates.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States juvenile justice system is a federalist patchwork in which federal law establishes structural protections while states exercise primary jurisdiction over juvenile offenders.

**Juvenile Justice and Delinquency Prevention Act (JJDPA):** Originally enacted in 1974 (Public Law 93-415) and most recently reauthorized in 2018 as the Juvenile Justice Reform Act, the JJDPA conditions federal grant funding on state compliance with four core protections:

| Core Protection | Requirement |
|----------------|-------------|
| Deinstitutionalization of Status Offenders (DSO) | Status offenders and non-offenders may not be held in secure detention or correctional facilities |
| Sight and Sound Separation | Juveniles held in adult facilities must be completely separated from adult inmates |
| Jail Removal | Juveniles may not be detained in adult jails or lockups except under limited, specified circumstances |
| Racial and Ethnic Disparities | States must address disproportionate minority contact with the juvenile justice system |

**Federal Juvenile Delinquency Act (18 U.S.C. Chapter 403):** This statute governs the processing of juvenile offenders in the federal system. Under 18 U.S.C. Section 5032, a juvenile may be prosecuted as an adult in federal court for certain violent felonies or drug offenses if the juvenile was 15 or older at the time of the offense and a federal court finds that transfer serves the interests of justice.

**State Variation in Age Boundaries:**

| Feature | Range Across States |
|---------|-------------------|
| Minimum age of juvenile court jurisdiction | No minimum (most states) to 10 (some states) |
| Maximum age of juvenile court jurisdiction | 15 (Georgia, Michigan, Texas, Wisconsin) to 17 (majority of states) |
| Extended juvenile court jurisdiction | Up to age 21-25 in some states |
| Minimum age for adult transfer | No minimum (some states) to 16 (others) |

### Landmark Cases

The U.S. Supreme Court has established a constitutional framework for juvenile justice through a series of landmark decisions:

**Kent v. United States, 383 U.S. 541 (1966):** The Court held that juveniles facing transfer to adult court are entitled to a hearing, access to counsel, a statement of reasons for the transfer decision, and access to social records. Justice Fortas wrote that juvenile courts must provide "the essentials of due process and fair treatment" rather than subject youth to "the worst of both worlds" -- neither the protections of adult court nor the rehabilitative benefits of juvenile court.[^4]

**In re Gault, 387 U.S. 1 (1967):** The watershed decision establishing that juveniles in delinquency proceedings are entitled to fundamental due process protections under the Fourteenth Amendment. The Court held that 15-year-old Gerald Gault, committed to a state industrial school for up to six years for making an obscene phone call (an offense carrying a maximum $50 fine for adults), had been denied notice of charges, the right to counsel, the right to confront and cross-examine witnesses, and the privilege against self-incrimination. The decision transformed juvenile courts from informal, paternalistic tribunals into adjudicative bodies bound by constitutional procedural requirements.[^5]

**Roper v. Simmons, 543 U.S. 551 (2005):** The Court held 5-4 that the Eighth and Fourteenth Amendments prohibit the execution of persons who committed their crimes before the age of 18. The Court identified three general differences between juveniles and adults: a lack of maturity and an underdeveloped sense of responsibility; vulnerability to negative influences and outside pressures; and the fact that a juvenile's character is not yet fully formed. The decision overruled Stanford v. Kentucky (1989) and invalidated capital punishment statutes in 19 states as applied to juveniles.[^6]

**Graham v. Florida, 560 U.S. 48 (2010):** The Court held that the Eighth Amendment prohibits life-without-parole (LWOP) sentences for juvenile offenders convicted of non-homicide offenses, requiring states to provide a "meaningful opportunity to obtain release based on demonstrated maturity and rehabilitation."[^7]

**Miller v. Alabama, 567 U.S. 460 (2012):** The Court held that mandatory LWOP sentences for juvenile homicide offenders violate the Eighth Amendment, requiring sentencing courts to consider the offender's youth and attendant characteristics before imposing the harshest possible penalty.[^8]

**Montgomery v. Louisiana, 577 U.S. 190 (2016):** The Court held that Miller's prohibition on mandatory LWOP for juveniles applies retroactively, requiring states to provide new sentencing hearings for all inmates serving mandatory LWOP sentences for offenses committed as juveniles. The Court clarified that Miller requires sentencers to consider youth-related mitigating factors and that LWOP should be reserved for the rare juvenile offender whose crime reflects "irreparable corruption" rather than "transient immaturity."[^9]

### Sentencing Framework

| Offense Category | Juvenile Court | Adult Court (Transferred Juvenile) |
|-----------------|---------------|-----------------------------------|
| Status offenses | Community-based services; no secure detention under JJDPA | Not applicable |
| Misdemeanors | Probation, community service, restitution, counseling | State-dependent; typically probation to 1 year |
| Non-homicide felonies | Commitment to juvenile facility (typically until age 21); no LWOP | State-dependent; LWOP prohibited (Graham) |
| Homicide | Commitment to juvenile facility; blended sentencing in some states | Mandatory LWOP prohibited (Miller); discretionary LWOP permitted for irreparably corrupt offenders |

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's juvenile justice system, widely regarded as one of the most education-oriented in the world, is governed by the Youth Courts Act (Jugendgerichtsgesetz, JGG) of 1953, as amended. The system is grounded in the principle of Erziehungsgedanke -- the primacy of education over punishment.

**Age of Criminal Responsibility (Section 19 StGB; Section 3 JGG):** Section 19 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB, Criminal Code) provides that children under 14 are not criminally responsible (schuldunfahig). After World War II, the legislature raised the MACR from 12 (as lowered during the Nazi era) back to 14, and it has remained at that threshold since.

For juveniles aged 14-17, criminal responsibility is conditional under Section 3 JGG: a juvenile bears criminal liability only if, at the time of the act, they possessed "sufficient moral and intellectual maturity to understand the wrongfulness of the act and to act in accordance with that understanding." If such maturity is found lacking, the juvenile court may order educational measures under the Youth Welfare Act (Kinder- und Jugendhilfegesetz, KJHG/SGB VIII) but may not impose criminal sanctions.

**Application to Young Adults (Section 105 JGG):** A distinctive feature of German law is the extension of youth criminal law to young adults aged 18-20. Under Section 105 JGG, youth criminal law applies to a young adult offender if:

- At the time of the act, the offender was equivalent to a juvenile in terms of moral and intellectual development; or
- The type, circumstances, and motives of the act indicate that it constituted "youth misconduct" (Jugendverfehlung).

In practice, approximately two-thirds of young adult offenders aged 18-20 are sentenced under youth criminal law rather than the general criminal code.

### Sanctions Structure

The JGG provides for three categories of sanctions, applied according to the principle of minimum intervention (Subsidiaritat):

**Educational Measures (Erziehungsmaregeln, Sections 9-12 JGG):**

| Measure | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Directions (Weisungen) | Instructions regarding conduct, including residence requirements, training obligations, community service, mediation, participation in social training courses, or traffic education |
| Educational assistance (Hilfe zur Erziehung) | Placement in supervised living arrangements or assignment of a probation counselor |

**Disciplinary Measures (Zuchtmittel, Sections 13-16 JGG):**

| Measure | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Formal warning (Verwarnung) | Judicial admonition |
| Conditions (Auflagen) | Restitution, apology to the victim, community service, or payment of a monetary sum |
| Youth detention (Jugendarrest) | Short-term detention: up to 2 weekends (Freizeitarrest), or up to 4 weeks continuous (Dauerarrest) |

**Youth Imprisonment (Jugendstrafe, Sections 17-18 JGG):**

| Parameter | Rule |
|-----------|------|
| Minimum term | 6 months |
| Maximum term (standard) | 5 years |
| Maximum term (serious offenses carrying 10+ years for adults) | 10 years |
| Suspension on probation | Permitted for sentences up to 2 years |
| Application rate | Approximately 2% of all juvenile and young adult cases |

### Diversion

A hallmark of the German system is the extensive use of diversion (Sections 45 and 47 JGG). The public prosecutor may dismiss proceedings without court involvement if an educational measure has already been initiated or if the offense is minor (Section 45). The court may similarly dismiss a case after imposing an educational measure or determining that the proceedings themselves have served an educative purpose (Section 47). Diversion accounts for approximately 70% of all juvenile cases, reflecting the system's commitment to the principle that formal court proceedings should be the exception rather than the norm.

### Landmark Developments

**Post-War Reform (1953):** The enactment of the JGG in 1953 restored the MACR to 14 (from 12 under the Nazi-era Reichsjugendgerichtsgesetz of 1943) and established the education-centered framework that remains in place today.[^10]

**First JGG Reform Act (1990):** This amendment formalized the priority of diversion and educational measures, expanded community-based sanctions, and strengthened the role of the Jugendgerichtshilfe (youth court assistance service) in providing the court with individual assessments of juvenile offenders.

**Ongoing Debate on Young Adults:** Germany periodically debates whether to make the application of youth criminal law to 18-20-year-olds mandatory (rather than conditional on individual assessment). Proponents argue that neuroscientific research supports treating young adults as developmentally similar to juveniles; opponents contend that the current discretionary system functions adequately.

## ECHR & EU

### European Convention on Human Rights

The European Convention on Human Rights does not contain provisions specifically addressed to juvenile justice, but the European Court of Human Rights has developed a substantial body of case law applying general Convention rights to juvenile criminal proceedings.

**Article 3 (Prohibition of Torture and Inhuman or Degrading Treatment):** The Court has held that detention conditions for juveniles must account for their age and vulnerability. Subjecting a minor to conditions of detention inappropriate for their age or developmental needs may violate Article 3.

**Article 5 (Right to Liberty and Security):**

*Bouamar v. Belgium, Application No. 9106/80 (1988):* The Court held that the repeated placement of a juvenile in an adult remand prison for short periods (nine placements over a period of 291 days) violated Article 5(1)(d), which permits the detention of a minor only "for the purpose of educational supervision" or for bringing the minor before a competent legal authority. Belgium's failure to provide an appropriate institutional framework for juvenile detention rendered the deprivation of liberty unlawful.[^11]

**Article 6 (Right to a Fair Trial):**

*T v. United Kingdom, Application No. 24724/94 (1999) and V v. United Kingdom, Application No. 24888/94 (1999):* These companion cases arose from the trial of two 11-year-old boys for the murder of 2-year-old James Bulger. The Court held that the boys' trial in an adult Crown Court, with its elevated dock, wigged and gowned counsel, intense media scrutiny, and public gallery, had denied them the right to effective participation in their trial in violation of Article 6(1). The Court established that "it is essential that a child charged with an offence is dealt with in a manner which takes full account of his age, level of maturity and intellectual and emotional capacities, and that steps are taken to promote his ability to understand and participate in the proceedings." The Court also found that the Secretary of State's involvement in setting the tariff (minimum term) violated Article 6(1) (right to an independent and impartial tribunal).[^12]

*Nortier v. the Netherlands, Application No. 13924/88 (1993):* The Court addressed the compatibility of the dual role of a juvenile judge (who both investigates and adjudicates) with the requirement of impartiality under Article 6(1), holding that the mere combination of investigative and adjudicative functions does not automatically violate the Convention, provided the judge's impartiality is not objectively compromised.[^13]

**Article 8 (Right to Respect for Private Life):** The Court has recognized that the publication of identifying information about juvenile offenders may violate their Article 8 rights. Article 6(1) itself permits the exclusion of the public from proceedings where required in the interests of juveniles.

### EU Directive 2016/800

Directive (EU) 2016/800 of the European Parliament and of the Council of 11 May 2016 on procedural safeguards for children who are suspects or accused persons in criminal proceedings establishes minimum standards across EU member states:

| Provision | Safeguard |
|-----------|-----------|
| Article 4 | Right to information: children must be informed promptly of their rights in simple, accessible language |
| Article 6 | Right of access to a lawyer; mandatory legal assistance during questioning, court hearings, and detention decisions |
| Article 7 | Right to individual assessment of personality, maturity, economic/social/family background, and specific vulnerabilities |
| Article 9 | Right to medical examination, including by a physician, when deprived of liberty |
| Article 11 | Audio-visual recording of questioning where proportionate in the circumstances |
| Article 12 | Limitation on deprivation of liberty: detention must be a measure of last resort, for the shortest appropriate period, and subject to periodic judicial review |
| Article 14 | Right to privacy: court hearings involving children should generally be held in camera |
| Article 15 | Right of the child to be accompanied by the holder of parental responsibility during proceedings |
| Article 20 | Training requirements for judges, prosecutors, police, and legal practitioners who handle cases involving children |

### Council of Europe Guidelines on Child-Friendly Justice (2010)

Adopted by the Committee of Ministers on 17 November 2010, these non-binding guidelines provide comprehensive guidance on adapting justice systems to the specific needs of children. Key principles include:

- **Best interests of the child** as a primary consideration in all proceedings
- **Participation:** the right of the child to be heard and to express views
- **Dignity:** treatment with care, sensitivity, fairness, and respect
- **Protection from discrimination** on any ground
- **Rule of law:** the same guarantees as those applied to adults, with additional protections as required by the child's age and maturity

### Interaction Between ECHR and National Systems

The ECHR framework does not prescribe a minimum age of criminal responsibility, but the Committee on the Rights of the Child (CRC) -- while a UN rather than a Council of Europe body -- has recommended that states parties maintain a MACR of no lower than 14, and this recommendation has influenced Council of Europe policy positions. The European Committee for the Prevention of Torture (CPT) conducts regular inspections of juvenile detention facilities across Council of Europe member states, applying standards that supplement the ECHR's Article 3 protections.

## Comparative Analysis

### Age of Criminal Responsibility

The four jurisdictions reflect a global lack of consensus on the appropriate MACR. South Korea and Germany both set the threshold at 14, consistent with the CRC's recommendation. The United States has no uniform federal MACR, with some states setting no minimum age at all, while the ECHR framework defers to national determination but encourages higher thresholds. The trend in South Korea toward lowering the MACR stands in tension with the international consensus, as reflected in the CRC General Comment No. 24 (2019), which explicitly recommends that states not lower their MACR below 14.

### Rehabilitation vs. Punishment

Germany's system most fully embodies the rehabilitative ideal, with 70% diversion rates and youth imprisonment at approximately 2% of cases. The United States occupies the most punitive end of the spectrum, permitting (though no longer requiring) LWOP for juvenile homicide offenders and maintaining robust transfer mechanisms to adult court. South Korea occupies a middle position, with a formal commitment to rehabilitation that is increasingly strained by public demands for harsher treatment of juvenile offenders. The ECHR/EU framework mandates rehabilitation as a guiding principle but allows wide discretion in implementation.

### Procedural Protections

All four systems provide procedural safeguards for juvenile defendants, but the depth and specificity vary. The United States, through the In re Gault line of cases, provides constitutional due process protections including the right to counsel, notice of charges, and the privilege against self-incrimination. EU Directive 2016/800 provides the most detailed and systematic set of procedural safeguards, including mandatory legal assistance, individual assessment, and audio-visual recording of questioning. Germany's system emphasizes the role of the Jugendgerichtshilfe (youth court assistance) in providing individualized assessments. The ECHR's contribution centers on the right to effective participation, as established in T v. United Kingdom.

### Treatment of Young Adults

Germany stands alone in systematically extending juvenile justice protections to young adults aged 18-20, reflecting neuroscientific research on brain development continuing into the mid-twenties. South Korea and the United States generally treat persons 18 and older as adults, though some U.S. states extend juvenile court jurisdiction to age 21 or beyond for dispositional purposes. The ECHR/EU framework defines "child" as a person under 18 in line with the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child, and does not specifically address young adults.
