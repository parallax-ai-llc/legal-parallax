## Summary

Utility models -- also referred to as petty patents, innovation patents, or Gebrauchsmuster -- are a form of intellectual property protection that provides patent-like rights for technical innovations that may not meet the higher inventive step threshold required for full patent protection, typically offering shorter protection periods, lower costs, faster registration, and simpler procedures. Approximately 80 countries worldwide provide some form of utility model protection, though the scope, requirements, and enforcement mechanisms vary dramatically. South Korea maintains one of the world's most active utility model systems under the Utility Model Act (실용신안법), administered by the Korean Intellectual Property Office (특허청, KIPO), which protects the shape, structure, or combination of articles of manufacture for 10 years from the filing date through a substantive examination system (changed from the previous registration-without-examination system in 2006); South Korea consistently ranks among the top three countries globally in utility model filings. The United States does not have a utility model system and provides no equivalent to petty patent protection, instead relying solely on the utility patent system under 35 U.S.C. with its full novelty and non-obviousness requirements, and the design patent system under 35 U.S.C. Chapter 16 for ornamental aspects of articles of manufacture; proposals to introduce a utility model system have been repeatedly considered and rejected by Congress and the USPTO. Germany operates the original and most influential utility model system through the Gebrauchsmustergesetz (GebrMG, Utility Model Act), which provides 10-year protection through a registration system without substantive examination (Registrierungsprinzip), covering technical inventions concerning the shape or arrangement of a device (Arbeitsgerät) or its parts; the Deutsches Patent- und Markenamt (DPMA, German Patent and Trade Mark Office) administers the system. The EU does not have a harmonized utility model system despite the European Commission's 1997 Green Paper on the Community Utility Model and the 1999 proposal for a Directive (COM(1999) 309), which was never adopted; member states maintain their own national utility model systems with significant variations, and utility models are not available through the European Patent Office (EPO) or the Unified Patent Court (UPC) system.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's utility model system has undergone significant reform, evolving from a registration-only system to a substantive examination system that more closely resembles the patent system while maintaining distinct advantages for incremental innovations.

**Utility Model Act (실용신안법):** The Act protects devices (물품) relating to the shape, structure, or combination of articles of manufacture that are industrially applicable, novel, and involve a creative step (진보성). The creative step threshold for utility models is explicitly lower than the inventive step (발명의 진보성) required for patents under the Patent Act (특허법), enabling protection for incremental improvements that contribute to practical utility.

### Registration and Examination

**2006 Reform:** Prior to the 2006 amendment, South Korea operated a dual-track system with utility model registration without substantive examination and a separate technical evaluation (기술평가) procedure. The 2006 reform introduced mandatory substantive examination for utility model applications, aligning the procedural framework with the patent examination system while maintaining the lower inventive step threshold. This reform was motivated by concerns about the quality of registered utility models and the burden placed on third parties by unexamined rights.

**Filing and Examination Procedure:** Utility model applications are filed with KIPO and undergo formality examination followed by substantive examination for industrial applicability, novelty, and creative step. The examination process typically takes 12-18 months, significantly shorter than the patent examination timeline. Accelerated examination is available for utility models that are being commercialized, relate to green technology, or are the subject of an infringement dispute.

### Scope of Protection

The utility model system in South Korea is limited to devices -- articles of manufacture with a definite shape or structure. The following are excluded from utility model protection:

| Excluded Subject Matter | Alternative Protection |
|------------------------|----------------------|
| Methods and processes | Patent Act (특허법) |
| Chemical substances and compositions | Patent Act |
| Computer programs per se | Copyright Act (저작권법); Patent Act (as method claims) |
| Plant varieties | Plant Variety Protection Act (식물신품종보호법) |
| Designs (aesthetic appearance) | Design Protection Act (디자인보호법) |

### Enforcement and Remedies

Utility model holders enjoy enforcement rights substantially equivalent to patent holders, including: injunctive relief (Articles 78-80), damages (Article 81, including lost profits, reasonable royalties, and statutory damages up to 30 million KRW), and criminal penalties for willful infringement (Article 93, up to 7 years imprisonment or 100 million KRW fine). The Intellectual Property Trial and Appeal Board (특허심판원) handles invalidity proceedings, and appeals proceed to the Patent Court (특허법원) and the Supreme Court (대법원).

### Statistical Significance

South Korea consistently ranks among the world's top utility model jurisdictions. In 2024, KIPO received approximately 4,200 utility model applications, predominantly from domestic applicants in the fields of daily necessities, mechanical engineering, and construction. While utility model filings have declined from their peak of over 40,000 annual applications in the late 1990s (reflecting the shift of many applicants to the patent system following the 2006 reform), the system remains significant for small and medium enterprises and individual inventors.

### Dual Filing Strategy

Korean patent practitioners frequently employ a dual filing strategy, filing both a patent application and a utility model application for the same invention (or the device and method aspects respectively). Under Article 10 of the Utility Model Act, applicants may convert a utility model application to a patent application (and vice versa) within 30 days of receiving a decision on the original application, providing flexibility to optimize protection based on examination outcomes.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States is the most notable jurisdiction that does not provide utility model protection, relying exclusively on the full patent system for the protection of technical innovations.

**Utility Patents (35 U.S.C.):** The U.S. patent system requires inventions to be novel (Section 102), non-obvious (Section 103), and useful (Section 101), with the non-obviousness standard representing a significantly higher threshold than the inventive step required for utility models in other jurisdictions. The standard was established by the Supreme Court in Graham v. John Deere Co., 383 U.S. 1 (1966), and refined in KSR International Co. v. Teleflex Inc., 550 U.S. 398 (2007), which expanded the range of prior art combinations that may render an invention obvious.

**Design Patents (35 U.S.C. Chapter 16):** While not equivalent to utility models, design patents protect the ornamental appearance of articles of manufacture for 15 years from grant. Design patents have experienced dramatic growth, with filings increasing from approximately 25,000 in 2010 to over 50,000 annually, partly filling a gap that utility models address in other jurisdictions for innovations with both functional and aesthetic aspects.

### Historical Proposals for Utility Model Protection

The question of introducing a utility model system in the United States has been debated periodically:

**1960s-1970s:** The President's Commission on the Patent System (1966) considered but rejected a petty patent proposal, concluding that it would create uncertainty and litigation costs without sufficient benefits.

**1994 Advisory Commission on Patent Law Reform:** The Advisory Commission considered a utility model proposal as part of its post-TRIPS harmonization recommendations but did not advance it.

**2010s IP Policy Discussions:** Academic commentators including Professors Mark Janis and Jay Kesan have advocated for a U.S. utility model system, arguing that it would benefit small businesses and individual inventors who are disproportionately burdened by the costs and delays of the full patent system. The USPTO has acknowledged the debate but has not formally proposed legislation.

### The Innovation Gap

The absence of a utility model system creates a protection gap for incremental innovations that are novel and useful but may not meet the non-obviousness threshold:

**Trade Secret Protection:** Some incremental innovations are protected through trade secret law under the Defend Trade Secrets Act (18 U.S.C. Section 1836), though this requires secrecy and does not protect against independent development.

**First-Mover Advantage:** Many businesses rely on first-mover advantage, branding, and speed to market rather than IP protection for incremental innovations.

**Provisional Patent Applications:** The provisional patent application system (35 U.S.C. Section 111(b)) provides a low-cost initial filing that establishes a priority date, but the inventor must ultimately file and prosecute a full utility patent application within 12 months.

### Comparative Disadvantage Analysis

The lack of a utility model system has been criticized as placing U.S. small businesses and individual inventors at a competitive disadvantage relative to their counterparts in jurisdictions with utility model systems. In South Korea, Germany, China, and Japan, small-scale innovators can obtain rapid, low-cost IP protection for incremental improvements, enabling them to license, secure investment, and deter copying during the critical early commercialization phase. U.S. innovators face the choice between expensive full patent prosecution (average costs of $10,000-$30,000 with multi-year pendency) and no formal IP protection.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's Gebrauchsmuster (utility model) system, established by the Gebrauchsmustergesetz of 1891 (current version: GebrMG as amended through 2021), is the oldest and most influential utility model system in the world, having served as the model for utility model legislation in numerous jurisdictions including South Korea, Japan, and China.

**Gebrauchsmustergesetz (GebrMG):** The Act provides protection for technical inventions that are new, involve an inventive step (erfinderischer Schritt), and are commercially applicable (gewerblich anwendbar). The critical distinction from patent law is the lower inventive step threshold: while the Patentgesetz (PatG) requires an invention that is not obvious to a person skilled in the art (erfinderische Tätigkeit), the GebrMG requires only an inventive step that goes beyond mere professional skill (handwerkliches Können).

### Registration System (Registrierungsprinzip)

Unlike the patent system, utility model registration proceeds without substantive examination of novelty and inventive step. The DPMA examines only formal requirements and ensures the subject matter falls within the scope of utility model protection. Registration typically occurs within 2-4 months of filing, providing significantly faster protection than the patent examination process (which averages 2-3 years at the DPMA).

**Consequences of Non-Examination:** Because utility models are registered without substantive examination, the validity of a utility model may be challenged through cancellation proceedings (Löschungsverfahren) before the DPMA (Section 15 GebrMG) or through invalidity defenses in infringement proceedings. The burden of establishing invalidity falls on the challenger, creating a registered right that provides de facto protection until successfully challenged.

### Scope of Protection and Exclusions

The GebrMG limits protection to technical inventions concerning the construction or arrangement of a device (Raumform) or its parts. The following are excluded:

**Methods and Processes (Section 2 No. 3):** Unlike patents, utility models cannot protect methods of manufacture, working methods, or uses. This is the most significant limitation of the German utility model system.

**Chemical Substances (Section 2 No. 3):** Chemical substances, food products, and pharmaceutical substances per se are excluded from utility model protection, though devices incorporating such substances may be protected.

**Biotechnological Inventions (Section 1(2) No. 5):** Plant and animal varieties and essentially biological processes for the production of plants or animals are excluded.

### Duration and Maintenance

Utility model protection lasts a maximum of 10 years from the filing date, with maintenance fees due at the end of the 3rd, 6th, and 8th years (Section 23 GebrMG). The fee structure (3+3+2+2 year periods) provides flexibility for rights holders to discontinue protection for innovations that have lost commercial value.

### Branching from Patent Applications (Abzweigung)

One of the most strategically significant features of the German utility model system is the ability to "branch off" (abzweigen) a utility model application from a pending patent application under Section 5 GebrMG. This enables applicants to:

(1) Obtain rapid registered protection through the utility model while the patent application undergoes substantive examination;
(2) Preserve the priority date of the original patent application for the utility model;
(3) Maintain utility model protection even if the patent application is refused or narrowed during examination.

The Abzweigung is widely used as a strategic tool in patent prosecution, particularly in infringement disputes where the patentee needs an enforceable right before the patent is granted. The Bundesgerichtshof (BGH) has confirmed the validity of this practice in numerous decisions, including BGH X ZB 7/03 (2004).

### Enforcement

Utility model infringement proceedings are heard by the Landgerichte (Regional Courts) with specialized IP chambers, the same courts that hear patent infringement cases. The bifurcated system (Trennungsprinzip) separating infringement proceedings (before the Landgericht) from validity proceedings (before the DPMA and Bundespatentgericht) applies to utility models as well as patents. This bifurcation creates a strategic advantage for utility model holders, who may obtain an infringement injunction from the Landgericht while the alleged infringer pursues cancellation before the DPMA, a process that can take 1-2 years.

### Relationship with the Unified Patent Court

The Unified Patent Court (UPC), which commenced operations on June 1, 2023, does not have jurisdiction over utility models. Utility models remain within the exclusive jurisdiction of national courts, creating a parallel enforcement system for rights holders who hold both European patents (or unitary patents) and German utility models covering similar subject matter.

## ECHR & EU

### Absence of EU Harmonization

Despite several initiatives, the EU has not achieved harmonization of utility model law across member states:

**1997 Green Paper on the Community Utility Model (COM(97) 691):** The European Commission published a Green Paper exploring the feasibility of a Community-wide utility model system, identifying significant divergences among member state systems and the potential benefits of harmonization for the single market.

**1999 Proposed Directive (COM(1999) 309):** The Commission proposed a directive to harmonize national utility model laws, establishing common minimum standards for subject matter, novelty, inventive step, duration (10 years), and enforcement. The proposal was never adopted, primarily due to disagreements among member states on the scope of protection and the relationship between utility models and patents.

**Current Status:** The Commission has not revived the harmonization initiative, and utility model protection in the EU remains a matter of national law. The resulting patchwork creates compliance challenges for businesses operating across the single market.

### Member State Divergences

The lack of EU harmonization has resulted in significant divergences among member state utility model systems:

| Country | Duration | Examination | Subject Matter Scope |
|---------|----------|-------------|---------------------|
| Germany | 10 years | Registration only | Devices (shape/arrangement) |
| France | 6 years (certificat d'utilité) | Registration only (examination since 2020) | All patentable inventions |
| Italy | 10 years (modello di utilità) | Registration only | Shape/configuration of machines, tools, implements |
| Spain | 10 years (modelo de utilidad) | Examination (novelty, industrial application) | Shape, structure, configuration of objects |
| Austria | 10 years (Gebrauchsmuster) | Registration only | Technical inventions except methods |
| Poland | 10 years (wzór użytkowy) | Examination | Technical solutions concerning shape/structure |
| Netherlands | No utility model system | N/A | N/A |
| Belgium | No utility model system (abolished 2019) | N/A | N/A |

Notable divergences include: France's 2020 reform introducing substantive examination for certificats d'utilité and extending their duration from 6 to 10 years; Belgium's abolition of its utility model system in 2019; and the wide variation in excluded subject matter across member states.

### International Framework

**Paris Convention (Article 1(2)):** The Paris Convention for the Protection of Industrial Property includes utility models within the scope of industrial property, ensuring that utility model applicants benefit from the national treatment and priority right provisions.

**TRIPS Agreement (Article 1):** While the TRIPS Agreement does not specifically require WTO members to provide utility model protection, it recognizes utility models as a form of intellectual property. The absence of a TRIPS obligation is cited as a key reason why the United States has not adopted a utility model system.

**Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT):** The PCT does not cover utility model applications directly, but many jurisdictions (including South Korea and Germany) permit the national phase entry of PCT applications as utility model applications, providing an international filing route for utility model protection.

### CJEU and Utility Models

The CJEU has addressed utility models in limited contexts:

**Gat v. LuK (Case C-4/03, 2006):** While primarily a patent jurisdiction case under the Brussels Regulation, the Court's holding that courts may not be denied jurisdiction to assess the validity of foreign patents has implications for cross-border utility model disputes within the EU.

**SPC and Utility Models:** The CJEU has clarified that supplementary protection certificates (SPCs) under Regulation (EC) No. 469/2009 are available only for products protected by patents, not utility models (consistent with the Regulation's text).

### ECHR Considerations

The ECHR's protection of property rights under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 extends to intellectual property rights, including utility models. The European Court of Human Rights has recognized that the revocation or limitation of IP rights may constitute an interference with property rights requiring justification under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (see Anheuser-Busch Inc. v. Portugal, Application No. 73049/01, 2007, regarding trademark rights).

## Comparative Analysis

### The Utility Model Gap: A Systemic Divergence

The most fundamental comparative issue is the binary divide between jurisdictions that provide utility model protection (South Korea, Germany, and most EU member states) and the United States, which does not. This divergence has profound implications for innovation policy:

**Small and Medium Enterprise Innovation:** In jurisdictions with utility model systems, SMEs and individual inventors can obtain rapid, affordable IP protection for incremental innovations, facilitating access to investment, licensing revenue, and enforcement remedies. The German Gebrauchsmuster and the Korean utility model system are specifically designed to serve this constituency. In the United States, the absence of such a system forces SMEs to choose between expensive full patent prosecution, trade secret protection (which does not protect against independent development), or no formal IP protection.

**Strategic Use and Abuse:** The rapid registration of utility models without substantive examination (in Germany and many other jurisdictions) creates risks of strategic abuse, where rights holders register utility models for innovations of questionable validity and use them as litigation tools. The 2006 Korean reform introducing substantive examination was motivated in part by concerns about such abuse. The German system addresses this through the cancellation procedure, but the bifurcated enforcement system can create temporary advantages for utility model holders before validity is resolved.

### Examination versus Registration: Quality and Speed Tradeoffs

South Korea's shift to substantive examination (2006) and France's similar reform (2020) reflect a growing recognition that unexamined utility models can impose costs on third parties and the judicial system. Germany's retention of the registration system reflects the countervailing value placed on speed and accessibility. The comparative evidence suggests that examination systems produce higher-quality utility models but at the cost of longer pendency and higher fees, potentially undermining the accessibility advantages that justify utility model systems in the first place.

### Subject Matter Limitations: Methods and Processes

The exclusion of methods and processes from utility model protection in both South Korea and Germany (and most other jurisdictions) represents the most significant limitation compared to full patent protection. This exclusion reflects the original conception of utility models as protecting tangible "small inventions" -- improvements to physical devices and tools. However, in an increasingly service-oriented and digitalized economy, the methods exclusion limits the relevance of utility models for many types of innovation.

### International Harmonization Prospects

The failure of the EU's harmonization initiative (1997-2010) and the absence of utility model provisions in the TRIPS Agreement suggest that international harmonization of utility model law is unlikely in the near term. The WIPO Standing Committee on the Law of Patents (SCP) has studied utility model systems but has not initiated harmonization negotiations. This fragmented landscape creates challenges for international patent strategy, particularly for SMEs that may benefit most from utility model protection but lack the resources to navigate multiple national systems.
