## Summary

Inheritance and succession law in the Global South operates at the intersection of family structure, religious obligation, gender equality, and economic redistribution, producing legal frameworks of remarkable complexity and diversity. China's Succession Law (1985), now incorporated into the Civil Code (2021, Book Six: Succession, Articles 1119-1163), establishes a statutory scheme of forced heirship across two orders of priority while permitting testamentary disposition that cannot defeat the rights of persons lacking the capacity to work and without a source of income (Article 1141: "necessary inheritance share," 必留份); uniquely, the Civil Code also recognizes "agreements on succession-maintenance" (遗赠扶养协议, Article 1158), under which an heir or organization agrees to support the decedent in exchange for succession rights. India's inheritance law is perhaps the most pluralistic in the world, with the Hindu Succession Act 1956 (as amended in 2005), the Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act 1937 applying Sunni Hanafi or Shia Ithna Ashari inheritance rules, the Indian Succession Act 1925 governing Christians and Parsis, and tribal customary inheritance recognized in Schedule V and VI areas -- the 2005 Amendment to the Hindu Succession Act, giving daughters equal coparcenary rights in Hindu Undivided Family (HUF) property, was a watershed moment given retroactive effect by the Supreme Court in Vineeta Sharma v. Rakesh Sharma (2020) 9 SCC 1. Nigerian inheritance law combines the Administration of Estates Law (applicable in Lagos and southern states) with Islamic inheritance (fara'id) applied in northern states and ethnically diverse customary inheritance rules that vary dramatically -- the Supreme Court's decisions in Ukeje v. Ukeje [2014] 11 NWLR (Pt. 1418) 384 and Anekwe v. Nweke [2014] 9 NWLR (Pt. 1412) 393 struck down Igbo customs that disinherited female children and widows as unconstitutional, while South Africa's Constitutional Court in Bhe v. Magistrate, Khayelitsha [2004] ZACC 17 invalidated the customary rule of male primogeniture in succession. Brazil's Civil Code (2002) establishes one of the strongest forced heirship (legítima) systems, reserving 50% of the estate to "necessary heirs" (herdeiros necessários: descendants, ascendants, and surviving spouse) under Article 1846, while the surviving spouse's inheritance rights depend on the marital property regime -- a complexity the STF and Superior Court of Justice (STJ) have repeatedly grappled with. Indonesia's inheritance system is perhaps the most pluralistic in the world, operating simultaneously under three regimes: Islamic inheritance (applied in Religious Courts under the KHI), adat (customary) inheritance varying by ethnic group, and the Civil Code (BW) applicable to those who opted into the colonial civil law system -- the Supreme Court in Decision No. 179 K/SIP/1961 established that heirs are free to choose which system applies, though in practice the choice is often determined by community and religious identity.

## China

### Civil Code Succession Framework

China's succession law, codified in Book Six of the Civil Code (Articles 1119-1163), replaced the Succession Law of 1985. The system reflects socialist principles of gender equality and family support obligations while accommodating economic reforms.

**Intestate Succession (法定继承, Articles 1127-1132):**
- **First Order:** Spouse, children (including adopted and stepchildren who maintained a support relationship), and parents (including adoptive and stepparents who maintained a support relationship).
- **Second Order:** Siblings, paternal grandparents, maternal grandparents.
- First-order heirs inherit to the exclusion of second-order heirs.
- Article 1130: Shares are generally equal, but may be adjusted for heirs who had special difficulty in life (lacking capacity to work and income), who performed principal caregiving duties, or who had the ability to support but failed to do so.

**Testamentary Succession (遗嘱继承, Articles 1133-1144):**
- Recognized will forms include: notarial wills (公证遗嘱), self-written wills (自书遗嘱), wills written by another (代书遗嘱), printed wills (打印遗嘱, newly added in 2021), audio-visual wills (录音录像遗嘱, expanded from the previous "audio" category), nuncupative/oral wills (口头遗嘱, only in emergency circumstances), and holographic wills.
- The 2021 Civil Code eliminated the prior rule that notarial wills had superior legal effect over other will forms. Under the new Article 1142, where there are conflicting wills, the latest in time prevails regardless of form -- a significant change from the 1985 law.

**Necessary Share (必留份, Article 1141):** A testator must reserve a necessary share for heirs who lack the capacity to work and have no source of income. This is narrower than the European forced heirship model: it protects only incapacitated dependents, not all statutory heirs, and the share amount is determined by the court based on the individual circumstances.

**Succession-Maintenance Agreements (遗赠扶养协议, Article 1158):** A distinctive Chinese institution in which a natural person may enter into an agreement with an heir, organization, or individual under which the latter supports the former in exchange for the right to the decedent's property. These agreements take priority over both testamentary and intestate succession, reflecting the Chinese legal system's emphasis on actual caregiving over formal status.

### Practical Issues

Rural land use rights (农村土地承包经营权) present a distinctive succession challenge. Since land is collectively owned, what is inherited is the contractual right to use the land, not ownership. Under the Rural Land Contract Law (2018 revision), contracted land rights within a household transfer to surviving household members; only "self-reserved plots" (自留地) and income from contracted land are subject to standard succession rules.

The household registration (hukou) system affects inheritance practice because property ownership, particularly housing, is often tied to hukou status. Urban housing purchased under the housing reform of the 1990s (房改房) and housing acquired with housing provident fund contributions create complex succession issues when family members hold different hukou statuses.

## India

### Hindu Succession

The Hindu Succession Act, 1956 (HSA) governs intestate succession for Hindus, Buddhists, Jains, and Sikhs. It distinguishes between two categories of property:

**Coparcenary/Joint Family Property (Mitakshara):** Under the Mitakshara system (applicable throughout India except parts of West Bengal and Assam where the Dayabhaga system historically applied), a Hindu Undivided Family (HUF) holds ancestral property in which male descendants traditionally had a coparcenary interest by birth. The **Hindu Succession (Amendment) Act, 2005** (effective September 9, 2005) amended Section 6 to grant daughters equal coparcenary rights:

> "On and from the commencement of the Hindu Succession (Amendment) Act, 2005, in a Joint Hindu family governed by the Mitakshara law, the daughter of a coparcener shall by birth become a coparcener in her own right in the same manner as the son."

The Supreme Court in **Vineeta Sharma v. Rakesh Sharma (2020) 9 SCC 1** (a three-judge bench) resolved conflicting High Court decisions by holding that the 2005 Amendment has retroactive effect: a daughter's coparcenary right is by birth, not contingent on the coparcener father being alive on September 9, 2005. This overruled the contrary view in Prakash v. Phulavati (2016) 2 SCC 36.

**Self-Acquired Property:** Governed by the general succession rules in Sections 8-16 (male Hindu dying intestate) and Sections 15-16 (female Hindu dying intestate). Section 8 establishes four classes of heirs for a male Hindu; Class I heirs (including sons, daughters, widow, mother, and specified descendants) inherit simultaneously to the exclusion of other classes.

### Muslim Inheritance (Fara'id)

Muslim inheritance in India follows uncodified personal law as recognized by the Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act, 1937. The two primary schools applied in India are:

**Sunni Hanafi Law:** The majority system, classifying heirs into:
- Sharers (Dhawu'l-fara'id): 12 specified relatives who receive fixed Quranic shares (e.g., daughter receives 1/2 if sole daughter or 2/3 collectively if two or more daughters; widow receives 1/8 if there are children)
- Residuaries (Asabah): Male agnatic relatives who take the remainder after sharers
- Distant Kindred (Dhawu'l-arham): Cognatic relatives who inherit only in the absence of sharers and residuaries

**Shia Ithna Ashari Law:** Classifies heirs into three classes based on proximity, with Class I (parents and lineal descendants) excluding all others.

Key principles: testamentary power is limited to 1/3 of the estate (the remaining 2/3 is subject to forced inheritance); bequests to heirs are invalid without the consent of other heirs; and there is no concept of joint family property or coparcenary.

The gender disparity in Islamic inheritance (daughters receiving half the share of sons) has been debated but not legislatively addressed, as courts have treated personal law as beyond the scope of Article 14 equality guarantees -- though this position is increasingly questioned after the triple talaq decision.

### Indian Succession Act, 1925

Governs Christians and (Part V) Parsis. Sections 31-49 provide intestate succession rules: the surviving spouse and lineal descendants share the estate (spouse receives 1/3 if there are lineal descendants, 1/2 if only kindred). Full testamentary freedom is permitted, with no forced heirship for Christians.

## Africa

### Nigeria: Three Inheritance Systems

**Customary Inheritance:** Varies by ethnic group:
- **Yoruba (Western Nigeria):** Historically, the Idi-Igi system distributed property per stirpes among the deceased's children (through wives), while the Ori-Ojori system distributed per capita. The family house (ile) typically cannot be alienated and is held for the family. Female children's inheritance rights varied by locality.
- **Igbo (Eastern Nigeria):** Traditionally practiced male primogeniture, with the eldest son inheriting the lion's share. The Supreme Court in **Ukeje v. Ukeje [2014] 11 NWLR (Pt. 1418) 384** declared the Igbo custom of disinheriting female children unconstitutional, holding that it violated Section 42 of the 1999 Constitution (right to freedom from discrimination). In **Anekwe v. Nweke [2014] 9 NWLR (Pt. 1412) 393**, the Court struck down the Awka custom preventing widows from inheriting their deceased husband's property.
- **Hausa-Fulani and Northern States:** Islamic inheritance (fara'id) applies, administered by Sharia courts.

**Statutory Inheritance:** The Administration of Estates Law (applicable in Lagos and some southern states, modeled on the English Administration of Estates Act 1925) provides rules of intestate succession giving the surviving spouse a life interest in the matrimonial home and a portion of the residue, with children sharing the remainder.

**Islamic Inheritance (Fara'id):** Applied in the twelve northern states with Sharia court systems. The Quranic shares are applied as described above under Muslim inheritance.

### South Africa: Constitutional Transformation

**Pre-Constitutional Position:** Under Black Administration Act 38 of 1927, succession to African customary-law estates was governed by customary law, which in most traditions followed the rule of male primogeniture -- the eldest male descendant inherited the family head's estate and assumed obligations of support for the family.

**Bhe v. Magistrate, Khayelitsha [2004] ZACC 17:** The Constitutional Court declared the customary rule of male primogeniture in succession unconstitutional, finding it violated section 9 (equality) of the Constitution. The Court struck down section 23 of the Black Administration Act and ordered that the Intestate Succession Act 81 of 1987 apply to all intestate estates pending legislative reform.

**Reform of Customary Law of Succession and Regulation of Related Matters Act 11 of 2009:** Enacted in response to Bhe, this Act extends the Intestate Succession Act to estates governed by customary law, while preserving certain customary allocations (e.g., the family home may be allocated to a specific heir who assumes support obligations). Section 2(1) provides that intestate succession to customary-law estates is governed by the Intestate Succession Act, subject to modifications.

**Intestate Succession Act 81 of 1987:** Provides that if the deceased is survived by a spouse and descendants, the spouse inherits a child's share or R250,000 (whichever is greater), with the remainder divided equally among descendants. If survived by a spouse only, the spouse inherits the entire estate.

**Testamentary Freedom:** South Africa generally permits full testamentary freedom -- there is no forced heirship. However, the Maintenance of Surviving Spouses Act 27 of 1990 allows a surviving spouse to claim reasonable maintenance from the estate, and the court may vary a will that fails to provide adequately.

## Latin America

### Brazil: Legítima and Forced Heirship

Brazil's succession law (Civil Code 2002, Articles 1784-1856) establishes one of the most protective forced heirship systems:

**Necessary Heirs (Herdeiros Necessários, Article 1845):** Descendants, ascendants, and the surviving spouse are "necessary heirs" entitled to the legítima -- 50% of the estate (Article 1846). The testator may freely dispose of only the remaining 50% (the disposable portion, porção disponível).

**Order of Intestate Succession (Article 1829):**
1. Descendants in concurrence with the surviving spouse (unless married under universal community of property, or partial community with no private assets)
2. Ascendants in concurrence with the surviving spouse
3. Surviving spouse alone
4. Collateral relatives to the fourth degree

**Surviving Spouse's Complex Position:** The interaction between succession and marital property regime creates significant complexity:
- Under partial community of property (comunhão parcial), the spouse first receives their meação (50% of community property as a property right, not inheritance), then inherits a share of the deceased's separate property alongside descendants.
- The STJ in **REsp 1.472.945/RJ (2014)** and subsequent decisions has grappled with whether the surviving spouse inherits from the community property or only from separate property.
- Article 1831 provides the surviving spouse a right of habitation (direito real de habitação) in the family home, regardless of property regime -- an in rem right that cannot be defeated by testamentary disposition.

**Stable Union Partners:** The STF in **RE 878.694 (Tema 809, 2017)** held that partners in a stable union (união estável) have the same succession rights as married spouses, declaring unconstitutional the differentiated treatment in Article 1790 of the Civil Code. This decision, with general binding effect (repercussão geral), equalized the succession position of stable union partners.

### Colombia: Legítima and Mejora

Colombian inheritance law divides the estate into three portions:
- **Legítima rigurosa (50%):** Reserved equally for legitimaries (descendants, or in their absence, ascendants)
- **Cuarta de mejora (25%):** May be allocated among legitimaries at the testator's discretion to favor one over another
- **Cuarta de libre disposición (25%):** Freely disposable by will
The surviving spouse is not a forced heir but has a right to the "porción conyugal" (marital portion) if in economic need, and receives 50% of community property as meation.

## Southeast Asia

### Indonesia: Triple Inheritance System

Indonesia's inheritance law is uniquely pluralistic, operating under three co-existing systems:

**Islamic Inheritance (Kewarisan Islam):** Applied by Religious Courts under the Compilation of Islamic Law (KHI, Articles 171-214). Follows classical fara'id with Shafi'i school modifications:
- Fixed shares (furudh) for designated heirs (e.g., daughter: 1/2 alone, 2/3 collectively; wife: 1/8 with children)
- Residuaries (asabah) take the remainder
- Testamentary disposition (wasiat) limited to 1/3 of estate
- **Wasiat Wajibah (Obligatory Bequest):** Articles 209 provides that adopted children and adoptive parents who do not inherit under Islamic law may receive up to 1/3 of the estate through wasiat wajibah -- an innovation not found in classical fiqh, reflecting Indonesian Islamic legal development.
- The Supreme Court has applied wasiat wajibah to non-Muslim heirs of Muslim decedents (Decision No. 368 K/AG/1995 and subsequent decisions), providing a mechanism to include otherwise excluded heirs.

**Adat (Customary) Inheritance:** Varies dramatically by ethnic group:
- **Javanese:** Bilateral inheritance; children inherit equally regardless of gender in many communities. The concept of segendong sepikul traditionally gave sons twice the share of daughters, but this has been increasingly replaced by equal distribution.
- **Minangkabau (West Sumatra):** Matrilineal system; ancestral property (harta pusaka) passes through the female line from mother to daughter, managed by the mamak (maternal uncle). Self-acquired property (harta pencaharian) may be disposed of more freely.
- **Balinese:** Patrilineal system; sons inherit, with the eldest son (or the son who performs death rites) receiving the primary share. Daughters inherit only in the absence of sons, though the 2010 MUDP (Majelis Utama Desa Pakraman) Decision No. 01/Kep/PSM-3/MDP Bali/X/2010 recognized limited inheritance rights for daughters.
- **Batak (North Sumatra):** Patrilineal; traditionally only sons inherit. Evolving through court decisions that have recognized daughters' rights.

**Civil Code (BW) Inheritance:** The colonial civil code (Burgerlijk Wetboek) inheritance provisions remain applicable to those who historically fell under its jurisdiction (primarily Chinese-Indonesians and others who opted in). Provides a forced heirship system similar to European models: the legitime (legitieme portie) reserves a fraction of the estate for descendants (1/2 for one child, 2/3 for two, 3/4 for three or more).

### Philippines: Legitime System

The Philippine Civil Code (Articles 774-1105) establishes a forced heirship system:

**Compulsory Heirs and Legitime (Articles 887-903):**
- Legitimate children: 1/2 of the estate
- Surviving spouse: 1/4 of the estate if surviving with legitimate children
- Illegitimate children: 1/2 of the share of legitimate children
- Legitimate parents (in absence of legitimate children): 1/2 of estate

**Free Portion:** The remainder after the legitime is the free portion, disposable by will. If there are no compulsory heirs, full testamentary freedom applies.

**Reserva Troncal (Article 891):** An ascendant who inherits from a descendant property that the descendant received gratuitously from another ascendant (or sibling) must reserve the property for relatives within the third degree of the line from which the property originated. This unique provision, derived from Spanish law, prevents property from leaving the family line.

**Muslim Filipino Succession:** Under PD 1083, Muslim Filipinos may opt to follow Islamic inheritance rules through Sharia courts. If they die intestate without electing, the general Civil Code rules apply.

## Comparative Analysis

### Forced Heirship vs. Testamentary Freedom

The Global South presents a spectrum from strict forced heirship (Brazil's 50% legítima, Islamic fara'id covering 2/3 of the estate) to broad testamentary freedom (South Africa, Indian Christian law). China occupies a middle position with its narrow necessary-share protection for incapacitated dependents. The philosophical foundations differ: Islamic inheritance is divinely mandated (Quranic verses 4:11-12); Latin American legítima derives from Roman-Iberian tradition protecting family solidarity; China's approach reflects socialist welfare principles.

### Gender Equality in Succession

The trajectory across the Global South is toward gender equalization, but the pace and mechanisms differ dramatically. India's 2005 HSA Amendment and Nigeria's Ukeje/Anekwe decisions achieved equalization through legislation and constitutional adjudication, respectively. South Africa's Bhe decision used constitutional supremacy to override customary law. China has maintained formal gender equality since 1985. However, Islamic inheritance rules maintaining the male-female differential persist in India, Nigeria, Indonesia, and the Philippines (for Muslim populations), protected by religious freedom guarantees and the political sensitivity of personal law reform.

### Pluralism and Choice of Law

India and Indonesia face the most complex choice-of-law challenges. Both countries maintain parallel inheritance systems based on religious and ethnic identity. Indonesia's Supreme Court has affirmed the principle of party autonomy -- heirs can choose which system applies -- but this operates within social constraints. India's system is more rigid, with applicable law determined by the deceased's religious identity. The Uttarakhand UCC (2024) would collapse these parallel systems within its jurisdiction, serving as a test case for national unification.
