## Summary

Tax systems across the Global South reveal profound structural differences that reflect each jurisdiction's fiscal capacity, economic development model, political economy, and administrative sophistication. China's tax system, anchored in the Golden Tax System (金税工程) -- now in its fourth phase (Golden Tax Phase IV) integrating big data, artificial intelligence, and blockchain for comprehensive tax monitoring -- has achieved remarkable compliance rates through technological enforcement, generating total tax revenue exceeding RMB 17 trillion (USD 2.4 trillion) in 2024. India's Goods and Services Tax (GST), implemented on July 1, 2017 through the 101st Constitutional Amendment and the Central GST Act, 2017, represents the most significant indirect tax reform in Indian history, subsuming 17 central and state taxes into a dual GST structure -- yet its five-rate structure (0%, 5%, 12%, 18%, 28%) and compliance complexity continue to generate litigation. Nigeria faces a structural tax-to-GDP crisis, with a ratio of approximately 6-8% (excluding oil revenue) -- among the lowest in the world -- despite reforms through the Finance Acts of 2019, 2020, and 2023 and the establishment of the Tax Reform Committee (2023). Brazil's tax system is infamous as arguably the most complex in the world, with the estimated 1,500 hours annually required for tax compliance (World Bank Doing Business, 2020), prompting the historic Constitutional Amendment 132/2023 (Reforma Tributária) that will replace five consumption taxes (PIS, COFINS, IPI, ICMS, ISS) with a dual VAT system (CBS + IBS) by 2033. Indonesia's tax amnesty programs (2016-2017 and 2022) and the Harmonized Tax Law (UU HPP, Law 7/2021) illustrate the revenue mobilization challenge, while the Philippines' Comprehensive Tax Reform Program (TRAIN Law, RA 10963, 2017 and CREATE Act, RA 11534, 2021) modernized a system with colonial-era origins.

## China

### Legal Framework

China's tax system operates through a civil law framework where the principal statutes are enacted by the National People's Congress (NPC) or its Standing Committee, supplemented by State Council regulations and Ministry of Finance/State Taxation Administration (STA) circulars and announcements. The foundational statute is the Tax Collection and Administration Law (税收征收管理法, 2001, last amended 2015), which establishes general principles of tax collection, taxpayer rights and obligations, and administrative enforcement procedures.

Major tax statutes include:

- **Enterprise Income Tax Law** (企业所得税法, 2007, amended 2018): Unified the previously separate tax regimes for domestic enterprises and foreign-invested enterprises at a standard rate of 25%. Article 28 provides reduced rates: 20% for qualifying small and micro enterprises (further reduced to an effective 5% through preferential policies) and 15% for High and New Technology Enterprises (HNTEs) certified under the joint MOST-MOF-STA criteria. The 200% R&D expense super-deduction (Article 30, expanded by State Council policies) incentivizes technology investment.
- **Individual Income Tax Law** (个人所得税法, 2018 revision, effective January 1, 2019): Introduced comprehensive income taxation combining four income categories (wages, author's remuneration, royalties, and personal services) with a progressive rate structure of 3-45%. The 2018 reform added special additional deductions for children's education (RMB 2,000/month per child from 2023), continuing education, housing mortgage interest, housing rent, elderly parent support, and infant childcare, significantly reducing the effective tax burden on middle-income earners.
- **Value-Added Tax Law** (增值税法, adopted December 25, 2024, effective January 1, 2026): After years of operating VAT under provisional regulations (暂行条例, 1993, revised 2008 and 2017), China finally enacted a formal VAT law. The law maintains the current rate structure: 13% standard rate, 9% for agricultural products, utilities, transportation, and basic necessities, and 6% for services including financial services, modern services, and lifestyle services. The "VAT reform" (营改增, 2012-2016) that replaced the Business Tax with VAT was completed in May 2016 when the final sectors (construction, real estate, financial services, and lifestyle services) were included.

### Golden Tax System

China's Golden Tax System (金税工程) is the most technologically advanced tax administration system in the Global South:

- **Golden Tax Phase I-III** (1994-2016): Progressively computerized VAT invoice management, cross-referencing of invoices between buyers and sellers, and taxpayer information databases. Phase III (2013-2016) unified the national tax information system, replacing previously fragmented provincial systems.
- **Golden Tax Phase IV** (2021-present): Integrates big data analytics, artificial intelligence, blockchain-based electronic invoicing (全电发票, fully electronic invoices rolled out nationwide by 2023), and cross-department data sharing with banking, customs, social insurance, and market regulation databases. The system enables real-time risk assessment of taxpayer behavior, anomaly detection for tax evasion patterns, and automated audit case selection.
- **Social Credit Integration**: Tax compliance data feeds into China's Social Credit System, with consequences for non-compliance including restrictions on government procurement participation, access to government subsidies, corporate credit ratings, and senior management travel and luxury consumption restrictions.

The State Taxation Administration (国家税务总局), a ministry-level institution directly under the State Council, administers both national and local taxes following the 2018 merger of national and local tax bureaus. This consolidation eliminated the dual-bureau system that had existed since the 1994 tax-sharing reform (分税制) and created a unified collection apparatus.

### Tax Sharing System

China's fiscal federalism operates through the tax-sharing system (分税制, fenshuizhi) established in 1994, which allocates tax revenues between central and local governments:

- **Central taxes**: Customs duties, consumption tax, vehicle purchase tax
- **Shared taxes**: VAT (50:50 central-local since 2016, previously 75:25), enterprise income tax (60:40 central-local), individual income tax (60:40)
- **Local taxes**: Urban land use tax, farmland occupation tax, property tax (pilot programs in Shanghai and Chongqing since 2011, broader reform under discussion), deed tax, stamp duty

The imbalance between local government expenditure responsibilities (approximately 85% of total government expenditure) and local revenue share (approximately 55% of total revenue) has driven local government reliance on land transfer fees (土地出让金) and local government financing vehicles (LGFVs, 地方政府融资平台), creating fiscal sustainability concerns addressed through the 2014 Budget Law reform and ongoing local government debt management measures.

## India

### Legal Framework

India's tax system is constitutionally delineated by the Seventh Schedule to the Constitution, which distributes taxing powers between the Union (List I) and States (List II), with residuary powers vested in Parliament (Article 248). The 101st Constitutional Amendment Act, 2016 created Article 246A empowering both Parliament and State legislatures to make laws with respect to GST, and established the GST Council (Article 279A) as a constitutional body with the Union Finance Minister as Chairperson and state Finance Ministers as members.

**Direct Taxes**:

- **Income Tax Act, 1961**: The foundational direct tax statute with over 300 sections and extensive rules. Corporate tax rates were rationalized by the Taxation Laws (Amendment) Act, 2019 (effective from AY 2020-21): 22% for existing companies foregoing exemptions (Section 115BAA) and 15% for new manufacturing companies incorporated after October 1, 2019 and commencing production before March 31, 2024 (Section 115BAB, extended).
- **Faceless Assessment**: Sections 144B and 144C (introduced by Finance Act, 2020) mandate faceless assessment and faceless appeals, removing human interface from tax proceedings. The National Faceless Assessment Centre (NaFAC) allocates cases to assessment units across India through an automated system, with review and verification units providing checks. The Supreme Court in Union of India v. Ashish Agarwal (2022) validated the conversion of Section 148 reassessment notices into the new Section 148A procedural framework.

**GST Framework**:

The GST is structured as a "dual GST" where:

- **Central GST (CGST)**: Levied by the Union on intra-state supplies under the Central GST Act, 2017
- **State GST (SGST)**: Levied by each State/Union Territory on intra-state supplies under respective State GST Acts
- **Integrated GST (IGST)**: Levied by the Union on inter-state supplies and imports under the IGST Act, 2017
- **GST Compensation Cess**: Levied on luxury and sin goods (aerated beverages, tobacco, automobiles, coal) under the GST (Compensation to States) Act, 2017 to compensate states for revenue shortfall during the transition period (originally 5 years, extended to June 2022)

The GST rate structure comprises five slabs: 0% (essential goods), 5% (mass consumption goods), 12% (standard goods), 18% (standard services and goods), and 28% (luxury and demerit goods including automobiles, cement, white goods). The GST Council has progressively rationalized items across slabs, but the multi-rate structure and frequent rate changes generate compliance complexity and litigation.

### GST Network (GSTN)

The Goods and Services Tax Network (GSTN), a Section 8 (not-for-profit) company with government majority ownership, operates the technology backbone:

- **E-invoicing**: Mandatory for businesses with aggregate turnover exceeding INR 5 crore (threshold progressively reduced from INR 500 crore at launch in October 2020). Invoice Registration Portal (IRP) generates a unique Invoice Reference Number (IRN) and QR code for every B2B invoice, enabling real-time reporting and matching.
- **E-way bills**: Mandatory electronic permits for movement of goods exceeding INR 50,000 in value, generated on the E-Way Bill Portal, enabling real-time tracking of goods movement and reducing transit-related tax evasion.
- **GSTR Filing**: Monthly returns (GSTR-1 for outward supplies, GSTR-3B for summary and tax payment) and annual return (GSTR-9). The original scheme of three-stage return filing (GSTR-1, GSTR-2, GSTR-3 with automatic matching) was abandoned due to implementation challenges and replaced by the simplified system.

### Landmark GST Litigation

- **Union of India v. Mohit Minerals Pvt. Ltd., (2022) 2 SCC 321**: The Supreme Court held that GST Council recommendations are not binding on Parliament or state legislatures, being recommendations that carry persuasive weight. This decision, while technically consistent with cooperative federalism, raised concerns about potential divergence in GST implementation across states.
- **Chief Commissioner of CGST v. Safari Retreats Pvt. Ltd., (2024)**: The Supreme Court clarified that construction of immovable property for letting out qualifies as a plant eligible for input tax credit under Section 17(5)(d) of the CGST Act, resolving a contentious issue affecting the real estate sector.

## Africa

### Nigeria

Nigeria's tax system is administered by the Federal Inland Revenue Service (FIRS) for federal taxes and the State Internal Revenue Service (SIRS) for state taxes, with a Joint Tax Board (JTB) coordinating between levels. The structural challenge is severe: Nigeria's non-oil tax-to-GDP ratio of approximately 6% (2024) is among the lowest in the world, reflecting a large informal economy (estimated at 50-65% of GDP), weak enforcement capacity, narrow tax base, and cultural resistance to taxation.

Key statutes include:

- **Companies Income Tax Act** (CITA, Cap C21, LFN 2004, as amended): Standard rate of 30% for companies with turnover exceeding NGN 100 million; 20% for medium companies (turnover NGN 25-100 million); and 0% for small companies (turnover below NGN 25 million, introduced by Finance Act 2019).
- **Value Added Tax Act** (Cap V1, LFN 2004, as amended by Finance Acts): VAT rate increased from 5% to 7.5% effective February 1, 2020. The VAT rate remains among the lowest globally, and the Federal Government has proposed further increases as part of tax reform discussions.
- **Personal Income Tax Act** (PITA, Cap P8, LFN 2004, as amended): Progressive rates from 7% to 24%, with a consolidated relief allowance of NGN 200,000 or 1% of gross income plus 20% of gross income.

The Finance Act 2023 introduced:

- **Section 30**: Significant economic presence (SEP) rules for taxing non-resident digital companies, requiring companies earning above NGN 25 million from Nigeria to register and pay tax.
- **Section 33**: Green bond incentives providing tax exemptions for certified green bonds.
- **TaxProMax Platform**: The FIRS launched the Taxpayer Identification Number Auto Registration and Maximization (TaxProMax) system for electronic filing, payment, and assessment administration.

The Rivers State v. FIRS (2021) case in the Supreme Court raised fundamental questions about VAT jurisdiction between federal and state governments, with Rivers State arguing that VAT is a consumption tax constitutionally allocated to states. The Court's decision maintained FIRS authority but prompted calls for comprehensive fiscal federalism reform.

### South Africa

The South African Revenue Service (SARS), established by the SARS Act 34 of 1997, administers a sophisticated tax system that generates a tax-to-GDP ratio of approximately 25% -- the highest in the Global South comparison:

- **Income Tax Act 58 of 1962**: The foundational statute with standard corporate income tax rate of 27% (reduced from 28% effective for years of assessment ending on or after March 31, 2023). Individual income tax is progressive with rates from 18% to 45% (for taxable income exceeding ZAR 1,817,000).
- **Value-Added Tax Act 89 of 1991**: Standard rate of 15% (increased from 14% effective April 1, 2018). Zero-rating of basic foodstuffs (19 items including brown bread, maize meal, dried beans, rice, vegetables, fruits, milk, and eggs) addresses regressivity concerns. The Davis Tax Committee (2013-2018) recommended expanding the zero-rated basket.
- **Tax Administration Act 28 of 2011**: Unified procedural framework for all taxes, establishing a comprehensive regulatory framework for SARS's investigative, assessment, dispute resolution, and collection powers. Section 225-233 provide for advance tax rulings.
- **BEE-Related Tax Provisions**: Section 12H (learnership allowances), Section 12I (industrial policy project incentives, sunset 2027), and SEZ tax incentives (Section 12R, 15% corporate tax rate for qualifying companies in Special Economic Zones) incentivize investment and transformation.

SARS underwent a significant institutional crisis (2014-2019) when revelations of state capture and the dismantling of investigative capacity under former Commissioner Tom Moyane led to declining compliance and revenue. Commissioner Edward Kieswetter's appointment (2019) and the Nugent Commission of Inquiry report (2018) drove institutional rebuilding.

## Latin America

### Brazil

Brazil's tax system operates across three levels of government (Union, states, municipalities), creating what is widely recognized as the world's most complex tax environment:

- **Federal Taxes**: IRPJ (corporate income tax, 15% + 10% surcharge on profit exceeding BRL 20,000/month); CSLL (social contribution on net profit, 9%); PIS/COFINS (social contributions on revenue, cumulative regime at 3.65% combined or non-cumulative at 9.25% combined); IPI (industrial products tax, varying rates); IOF (financial operations tax); import duties.
- **State Taxes**: ICMS (Imposto sobre Circulação de Mercadorias e Serviços, essentially a state-level VAT on goods and specified services, with 27 different sets of rules, rates ranging from 4% to 35% depending on the state and product); IPVA (vehicle property tax); ITCMD (inheritance and gift tax, rates up to 8%).
- **Municipal Taxes**: ISS (services tax, rates 2-5%); IPTU (urban property tax); ITBI (real property transfer tax).

**Tax Reform (Reforma Tributária)**:

Constitutional Amendment 132/2023 (Emenda Constitucional 132, promulgated December 20, 2023) represents the most significant tax reform in Brazilian history, replacing five consumption taxes with a dual VAT:

- **CBS (Contribuição sobre Bens e Serviços)**: Federal tax replacing PIS, COFINS, and IPI, administered by the Receita Federal do Brasil (RFB).
- **IBS (Imposto sobre Bens e Serviços)**: Subnational tax replacing ICMS and ISS, administered by a newly created IBS Committee (Comitê Gestor do IBS) with state and municipal representation.
- **IS (Imposto Seletivo)**: Selective tax on goods and services harmful to health and the environment (tobacco, alcohol, sugary beverages, mining products), replacing the current IPI/ICMS selective taxation.

The transition period runs from 2026 to 2033, with CBS phasing in from 2027 (0.9% test rate in 2026) and IBS from 2029, while ICMS and ISS are progressively reduced through 2033. The combined CBS + IBS reference rate is estimated at 26.5%, which would be among the highest VAT rates globally but is expected to be offset by the elimination of cascading effects and reduced compliance costs.

The Complementary Law implementing the reform (LC 214/2025, enacted January 16, 2025) established detailed rules including: cashback mechanism for low-income families (returning CBS and IBS on basic consumption); reduced rates (60% discount) for education, healthcare, public transport, and agricultural inputs; zero rate for the basic food basket (cesta básica nacional); and special regimes for financial services, real estate, and small businesses under Simples Nacional.

**Simples Nacional**: The simplified tax regime for micro and small enterprises (annual revenue up to BRL 4.8 million) consolidates up to eight federal, state, and municipal taxes into a single monthly payment with rates from 4% to 33% on a progressive scale. Approximately 20 million enterprises are enrolled in Simples Nacional.

**SPED (Sistema Público de Escrituração Digital)**: Brazil's electronic tax bookkeeping system requires businesses to transmit detailed digital accounting and tax records (EFD-ICMS/IPI, EFD-Contribuições, ECD, ECF) to tax authorities, enabling automated cross-referencing and audit selection. Nota Fiscal Eletrônica (NF-e) has been mandatory since 2008, creating one of the world's most comprehensive real-time transaction reporting systems.

### Mexico

Mexico's tax system, administered by the Servicio de Administración Tributaria (SAT), features:

- **ISR (Impuesto sobre la Renta)**: Corporate income tax at 30%; individual income tax progressive to 35%.
- **IVA (Impuesto al Valor Agregado)**: 16% standard rate; 0% on food and medicine; exempt for medical, educational, and residential rental services.
- **CFDI (Comprobante Fiscal Digital por Internet)**: Mandatory electronic invoicing since 2014 (expanded in phases), with real-time transmission to SAT and XML standardized format. Mexico's CFDI system is considered one of the most advanced e-invoicing systems globally, processing over 10 billion invoices annually and significantly reducing VAT fraud.
- **Maquiladora/IMMEX Regime**: Special import duty and VAT treatment for manufacturing operations that import raw materials for assembly and re-export, governed by the IMMEX Decree (2006, as amended).

## Southeast Asia

### Indonesia

Indonesia's tax system, administered by the Directorate General of Taxes (DJP, Direktorat Jenderal Pajak) under the Ministry of Finance, has pursued aggressive revenue mobilization through amnesty programs and base broadening:

**Tax Amnesty Programs**:

- **Tax Amnesty I (2016-2017)**: Law 11/2016 offered taxpayers the opportunity to declare previously unreported assets and repatriate offshore wealth in exchange for reduced penalties (2-5% redemption rate on net declared assets, depending on timing and repatriation). The program declared IDR 4,855 trillion in assets (approximately USD 366 billion, equivalent to ~40% of GDP), with IDR 1,036 trillion repatriated and IDR 114 trillion in redemption payments -- making it the most successful tax amnesty in global history by declared assets.
- **Voluntary Disclosure Program (2022)**: Under the Harmonized Tax Law (UU HPP, Law 7/2021), a follow-up program targeted assets acquired through 2020 (for former amnesty participants) and through 2015 (for individual taxpayers), with net asset rates of 6-11% for domestic/repatriated assets and 12-18% for offshore assets.

**Harmonized Tax Law (UU HPP, Law 7/2021)**:

- **Income Tax**: Individual tax rates revised to five brackets with a new top rate of 35% for income exceeding IDR 5 billion (previously 30% for income exceeding IDR 500 million). Corporate income tax maintained at 22%.
- **VAT**: Rate increased from 10% to 11% (effective April 1, 2022) with a further increase to 12% initially planned for January 1, 2025 but deferred following public opposition to 2026, applying initially only to luxury goods.
- **Carbon Tax**: Indonesia became the first ASEAN country to legislate a carbon tax (Article 13 of UU HPP), though implementation has been repeatedly postponed from the initial April 2022 target. The rate is set at IDR 30,000/tonne of CO2 equivalent (approximately USD 2/tonne).
- **Tax Administration**: Integration of NIK (National Identity Number) as taxpayer identification number, simplifying registration and enabling data matching with population databases.

**Core Tax System (PSIAP)**: Indonesia launched its new Core Tax Administration System (Pembaruan Sistem Inti Administrasi Perpajakan) in 2024, replacing the DJP Online system with a Coretax DJP platform built on SAP technology. The system aims to automate compliance monitoring, pre-populate returns, and enable risk-based audit selection.

### Philippines

The Philippines' tax system, administered by the Bureau of Internal Revenue (BIR) under the Department of Finance, has undergone modernization through the Comprehensive Tax Reform Program (CTRP):

- **TRAIN Law (RA 10963, 2017)**: Package 1 of the CTRP, effective January 1, 2018, restructured personal income tax (reducing the top marginal rate from 32% to 35% but significantly reducing rates for lower and middle-income brackets; an individual earning PHP 250,000 annually or less pays no income tax), expanded VAT base (removing exemptions for certain raw materials, capital goods, and transactions), increased excise taxes on petroleum, automobiles, sweetened beverages, and tobacco, and imposed estate tax at a flat 6%.
- **CREATE Act (RA 11534, 2021)**: Package 2, effective April 11, 2021 (retroactive to July 1, 2020), reduced corporate income tax from 30% to 25% (20% for domestic corporations with net taxable income not exceeding PHP 5 million and total assets not exceeding PHP 100 million). The CREATE Act also rationalized fiscal incentives through a unified system administered by the Fiscal Incentives Review Board (FIRB), replacing the prior regime where different investment promotion agencies (PEZA, BOI, TIEZA, BCDA, etc.) offered overlapping and sometimes inconsistent incentives.
- **CREATE MORE Act (RA 12066, 2024)**: Enhanced the fiscal incentive regime by extending the enhanced deductions period, allowing the stacking of income tax holidays with enhanced deductions, and providing for a 50% additional deduction for power costs of registered export enterprises.

**VAT**: Standard rate of 12% (increased from 10% in 2006 under RA 9337). VAT-exempt transactions include sale of agricultural food products, educational services, and residential house and lot (value not exceeding PHP 3.6 million). The Philippines' VAT exemption threshold for small businesses is PHP 3 million annual gross sales.

**eFPS and BIR Digitalization**: The Electronic Filing and Payment System (eFPS) has been mandatory for large taxpayers since 2005. The BIR's digitization efforts have been slower compared to Brazil and Mexico, with ongoing projects for e-invoicing and electronic books of accounts.

## Comparative Analysis

### Revenue Mobilization and the Informal Economy

The tax-to-GDP ratios across the Global South starkly illustrate the revenue mobilization challenge: Brazil's 33% approximates OECD averages, South Africa's 25% reflects effective administration despite economic challenges, while Nigeria's 6% (non-oil) and Indonesia's 10.2% represent massive untapped fiscal potential. India's 11.5% ratio is rising rapidly post-GST but remains constrained by agricultural income exemption (Article 10(1) of the Income Tax Act) and the large informal sector. The primary drivers of low ratios are informal economies (estimated at 50-65% in Nigeria, 40-50% in India, 30-40% in Indonesia), political resistance to taxation (particularly property taxes), and administrative capacity limitations.

### Technology-Driven Enforcement

The Global South is leading innovation in digital tax administration. China's Golden Tax Phase IV, Brazil's NF-e/SPED system, Mexico's CFDI, and India's GST e-invoicing represent four of the most advanced real-time transaction monitoring systems globally, surpassing many OECD countries. These systems generate massive datasets enabling AI-driven risk assessment and audit selection. Indonesia's Core Tax System and the Philippines' BIR digitization are catching up. The common trajectory is from paper-based, audit-driven enforcement toward automated, data-driven compliance management.

### Consumption Tax Design

The Global South reveals different approaches to consumption tax design and regressivity mitigation. India's multi-rate GST attempts to address regressivity through rate differentiation (0% on essentials, 28% on luxury goods) but creates classification disputes and compliance costs. Brazil's historic reform replaces the world's most complex consumption tax system with a dual VAT incorporating an innovative cashback mechanism for low-income families. South Africa's zero-rated basic foodstuff list addresses regressivity through targeted exemptions. Nigeria's 7.5% VAT rate is politically constrained despite being far below revenue-maximizing levels.

### Tax Incentives and Competition

All surveyed jurisdictions use tax incentives to attract investment, raising BEPS (Base Erosion and Profit Shifting) concerns. China's 15% HNTE rate, India's 15% manufacturing rate, South Africa's 15% SEZ rate, Indonesia's tax holidays, and the Philippines' PEZA/CREATE incentives create inter-jurisdictional competition within the Global South. The OECD/G20 Inclusive Framework Pillar Two Global Minimum Tax (15% effective rate) will reshape these incentive structures, with China, India, South Africa, Nigeria, Brazil, Indonesia, and the Philippines all participating in the Inclusive Framework though implementing at varying speeds.
