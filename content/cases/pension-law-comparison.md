## Summary

Pension law -- the legal framework governing retirement income provision through public social insurance, employer-sponsored plans, and individual savings mechanisms -- addresses one of the most consequential long-term financial obligations of any society, with profound implications for fiscal sustainability, intergenerational equity, and individual welfare. South Korea operates the National Pension Service (국민연금, NPS), established by the National Pension Act (국민연금법) of 1986 and effective January 1, 1988, as a defined-benefit public pension providing retirement income at a target replacement rate that has been progressively reduced from 70 percent (1988) to 40 percent (2028 target, phased reduction since 2008), supplemented by a Basic Pension (기초연금) for low-income elderly and employer-sponsored retirement pension plans under the Employee Retirement Benefit Security Act (근로자퇴직급여 보장법) of 2005; the NPS faces significant long-term sustainability challenges, with its fund projected to be depleted by 2055 under current contribution and benefit parameters, prompting ongoing reform debates. The United States provides retirement income through three pillars: Social Security (Old-Age, Survivors, and Disability Insurance, OASDI) under Title II of the Social Security Act of 1935, employer-sponsored defined benefit and defined contribution plans regulated by the Employee Retirement Income Security Act (ERISA) of 1974 (29 U.S.C. Section 1001 et seq.), and individual retirement accounts (IRAs) established by ERISA and subsequent legislation; the Social Security Trust Fund is projected to be depleted by 2033-2035, after which incoming payroll tax revenue would cover approximately 79 percent of scheduled benefits. Germany operates a three-pillar system comprising the Gesetzliche Rentenversicherung (GRV, statutory pension insurance) under Sozialgesetzbuch VI (SGB VI) providing earnings-related retirement benefits, the Betriebliche Altersversorgung (occupational pension) regulated by the Betriebsrentengesetz (BetrAVG) of 1974, and the Riester-Rente (subsidized private pension) introduced in 2001; Germany's pension reforms of 2004 and 2007 raised the retirement age from 65 to 67 and introduced the Nachhaltigkeitsfaktor (sustainability factor) linking benefits to the ratio of contributors to beneficiaries. At the European level, the EU addresses cross-border pension coordination through Regulation (EC) No 883/2004, the Institutions for Occupational Retirement Provision Directive (IORP II, 2016/2341), and the Pan-European Personal Pension Product (PEPP) Regulation (EU) 2019/1238, while the European Commission's Green Paper on Pensions (2010) and subsequent policy initiatives promote adequate, sustainable, and safe pension systems.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's pension system operates through three tiers: the public National Pension (국민연금), employer-sponsored retirement pension (퇴직연금), and voluntary individual pension savings (개인연금).

### National Pension (국민연금)

**Establishment and Coverage:** The National Pension Act (국민연금법), enacted on December 31, 1986, established the NPS effective January 1, 1988. Initially covering workplaces with 10 or more employees, coverage was progressively extended to all citizens aged 18-59, including the self-employed (1999) and farmers and fishermen (1995). As of 2023, approximately 22.2 million persons are enrolled.

**Contribution Rate:** Currently 9 percent of monthly income (4.5 percent employer, 4.5 percent employee), with the contribution ceiling set at 5.9 million KRW monthly (2024). This rate is significantly lower than the rates in Germany (18.6 percent) or the United States (12.4 percent), contributing to long-term sustainability concerns.

**Benefit Formula:** The pension benefit is calculated based on the insured person's average standard monthly income (A-value: the average income of all insured persons, and B-value: the individual's career average income), the contribution period, and the replacement rate. The target replacement rate for a 40-year contributor has been reduced:

| Period | Target Replacement Rate |
|--------|----------------------|
| 1988-1998 | 70% |
| 1999-2007 | 60% |
| 2008 | 50% (beginning of phase-down) |
| 2009-2027 | Declining by 0.5% annually |
| 2028 onward | 40% |

**Retirement Age:** The pensionable age is being gradually raised:

| Birth Year | Pensionable Age |
|-----------|----------------|
| Before 1953 | 60 |
| 1953-1956 | 61 |
| 1957-1960 | 62 |
| 1961-1964 | 63 |
| 1965-1968 | 64 |
| 1969 onward | 65 |

### Basic Pension (기초연금)

The Basic Pension Act (기초연금법) of 2014 provides means-tested income support to elderly persons aged 65 and older in the bottom 70 percent of the income distribution. The maximum monthly benefit was 334,810 KRW in 2024. The Basic Pension supplements the National Pension for low-income elderly and provides a safety net for those who did not accumulate sufficient NPS contributions.

### Sustainability Challenge

The NPS fund, which held approximately 1,000 trillion KRW (approximately $750 billion) as of 2023 and is the world's third-largest pension fund, is projected to begin running deficits by 2041 and to be depleted by 2055. This projection has driven intense reform debates, with proposals including raising the contribution rate to 13-15 percent, further raising the retirement age, adjusting the benefit formula, and introducing automatic adjustment mechanisms.

### 2024-2025 Reform Proposals

The Yoon administration proposed a phased increase of the contribution rate from 9 percent to 13 percent over several years, combined with raising the target replacement rate from 40 percent to 42 percent. The National Assembly continues to debate reform legislation.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The U.S. retirement system operates through three pillars: Social Security, employer-sponsored plans, and individual retirement savings.

### Social Security (OASDI)

**Establishment:** The Social Security Act of 1935, signed by President Franklin D. Roosevelt on August 14, 1935, established the Old-Age Insurance program. Survivors benefits were added in 1939, disability insurance in 1956. The program is financed through payroll taxes (FICA) of 12.4 percent (6.2 percent each from employer and employee) on earnings up to the taxable maximum ($168,600 in 2024).

**Benefits:** Monthly retirement benefits are based on the worker's Average Indexed Monthly Earnings (AIME) over the highest 35 years of earnings, applied to a progressive benefit formula (Primary Insurance Amount, PIA). The formula replaces 90 percent of the first $1,174 of AIME, 32 percent of AIME between $1,174 and $7,078, and 15 percent of AIME above $7,078 (2024 bend points). The resulting average replacement rate for a medium earner is approximately 40 percent.

**Full Retirement Age:** Gradually increasing from 65 to 67 for those born in 1960 or later. Early retirement is available at age 62 with reduced benefits (approximately 30 percent reduction at 62 for those with FRA of 67). Delayed retirement credits of 8 percent per year apply until age 70.

**Trust Fund Depletion:** The 2024 Social Security Trustees Report projects that the OASI Trust Fund will be depleted by 2033, after which incoming payroll tax revenue would cover approximately 79 percent of scheduled benefits. Combined OASDI trust fund depletion is projected for 2035.

### Employer-Sponsored Plans (ERISA)

The Employee Retirement Income Security Act (ERISA) of 1974 established comprehensive federal regulation of employer-sponsored retirement plans:

| Plan Type | Description | Key Feature |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| Defined Benefit (DB) | Employer promises specified retirement benefit | Risk on employer; declining prevalence |
| 401(k) (DC) | Employee contributes from salary; employer may match | Risk on employee; dominant private-sector model |
| 403(b) | DC plan for tax-exempt organizations and schools | Similar to 401(k) |
| 457(b) | Deferred compensation for government and some nonprofits | Special catch-up provisions |

The shift from DB to DC plans has transferred investment risk from employers to employees. In 1980, approximately 38 percent of private-sector workers participated in DB plans; by 2023, this had fallen to approximately 15 percent, while DC plan participation reached approximately 34 percent.

### SECURE Act and SECURE 2.0

The Setting Every Community Up for Retirement Enhancement (SECURE) Act of 2019 and SECURE 2.0 Act of 2022 enacted significant reforms including mandatory automatic enrollment in new 401(k) and 403(b) plans (effective 2025), increased required minimum distribution ages (from 72 to 73, eventually to 75), expanded part-time worker access to retirement plans, student loan matching contributions, and enhanced catch-up contribution limits.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's pension system comprises three pillars: statutory pension insurance (Gesetzliche Rentenversicherung), occupational pensions (Betriebliche Altersversorgung), and private pensions (Riester-Rente and Rürup-Rente).

### Gesetzliche Rentenversicherung (GRV)

**Establishment:** Germany established the world's first national pension system through Otto von Bismarck's Gesetz betreffend die Invaliditäts- und Altersversicherung (Invalidity and Old-Age Insurance Act) of 1889. The system is now codified in Sozialgesetzbuch VI (SGB VI).

**Structure:** The GRV is a pay-as-you-go system financed by payroll contributions of 18.6 percent (9.3 percent employer, 9.3 percent employee), supplemented by a federal subsidy (Bundeszuschuss) covering approximately 24 percent of total expenditure. It covers virtually all employees and certain categories of self-employed persons.

**Benefit Calculation:** The pension formula is based on Entgeltpunkte (earnings points): one year of average earnings yields one earnings point. The pension amount equals the number of earnings points multiplied by the current Rentenwert (pension point value), which was EUR 39.32 per month (West) as of July 2024. The standard pension for 45 years of average earnings (Standardrente or Eckrente) provides approximately 48 percent gross (before tax) replacement of average earnings.

**Retirement Age:** The 2007 reform (RV-Altersgrenzenanpassungsgesetz) raised the normal retirement age from 65 to 67, phased in between 2012 and 2031:

| Birth Year | Normal Retirement Age |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Before 1947 | 65 |
| 1947-1963 | 65 + 1-2 months per year |
| 1964 onward | 67 |

### Nachhaltigkeitsfaktor (Sustainability Factor)

Introduced by the 2004 reform (RV-Nachhaltigkeitsgesetz), the sustainability factor automatically adjusts pension increases based on the ratio of contributors to pensioners (Rentnerquotient). When the ratio deteriorates (more pensioners per contributor), pension increases are moderated. This mechanism limits the growth of pension expenditure without requiring explicit legislative action.

### Rente mit 63

The 2014 Rentenpaket introduced the "Rente mit 63" (pension at 63) allowing workers with 45 years of contributions to retire at 63 without actuarial reduction. The qualifying age has been gradually increasing and reached 64 years and 4 months for those born in 1961. This provision was politically popular but criticized by economists for working against the retirement age increase.

### Betriebliche Altersversorgung (Occupational Pensions)

The Betriebsrentengesetz (BetrAVG) of 1974 regulates employer-sponsored pensions. German law provides five implementation pathways (Durchführungswege):

| Pathway | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Direktzusage (book reserve) | Employer books pension obligations on balance sheet |
| Unterstützungskasse (support fund) | External fund managed by employer |
| Pensionskasse (pension fund) | Insurance-type vehicle |
| Direktversicherung (direct insurance) | Life insurance policy |
| Pensionsfonds (pension fund) | Capital-market-based fund |

The 2018 Betriebsrentenstärkungsgesetz (Occupational Pension Strengthening Act) introduced the Sozialpartnermodell, allowing collective agreements to establish pure defined contribution plans without employer guarantees, a significant departure from Germany's traditionally guaranteed occupational pension model.

### Riester-Rente

Introduced by the 2001 Altersvermögensgesetz, the Riester-Rente is a subsidized private pension savings plan named after then-Labor Minister Walter Riester. Participants receive government subsidies (Zulagen) of up to EUR 175 per year (plus EUR 185-300 per child) and/or tax deductions. While over 16 million contracts have been concluded, the Riester-Rente has been criticized for high fees, poor returns, and low participation among low-income workers. The federal government has signaled potential reform, with a 2024 proposal to replace it with a new "Altersvorsorgedepot" offering more flexible investment options.

## ECHR & EU

### Social Security Coordination (Regulation 883/2004)

EU Regulation 883/2004 coordinates pension rights for mobile workers across member states. Key principles include aggregation of insurance periods (a worker's contribution periods in different member states are combined for eligibility), pro rata calculation (each member state pays a pension proportional to the periods completed under its system), and export of benefits (pensions must be paid regardless of the beneficiary's country of residence within the EU).

### IORP II Directive (2016/2341)

The revised Directive on Institutions for Occupational Retirement Provision, adopted on December 14, 2016, strengthens governance, transparency, and cross-border activity of occupational pension institutions. Key provisions include enhanced risk assessment (own-risk assessment), revised governance requirements including key function requirements, improved information disclosure through pension benefit statements, and facilitation of cross-border IORPs.

### Pan-European Personal Pension Product (PEPP)

Regulation (EU) 2019/1238, adopted on June 20, 2019, and applicable since March 22, 2022, creates a voluntary EU-wide personal pension product that individuals can carry across member states without losing tax advantages. The PEPP features a default investment option (basic PEPP) with fees capped at 1 percent of accumulated capital per year.

### European Pillar of Social Rights

Principle 15 of the European Pillar of Social Rights (2017) provides: "Workers and the self-employed in retirement have the right to a pension commensurate to their contributions and ensuring an adequate income. Women and men shall have equal opportunities to acquire pension rights."

### ECHR

The European Court of Human Rights has addressed pension rights primarily under Article 1 of Protocol 1 (protection of property). In Stec and Others v. United Kingdom (2006), the Grand Chamber confirmed that pension benefits constitute "possessions" and that differential treatment must satisfy Article 14 non-discrimination requirements. In Carson v. United Kingdom (Application No. 42184/05, 2010), the Grand Chamber held that the UK's policy of not uprating pensions for retirees in certain non-EU countries did not violate Article 14 taken with Article 1 Protocol 1.

## Comparative Analysis

### Sustainability Challenges

All three jurisdictions face significant demographic pressure on their public pension systems due to aging populations and declining birth rates. South Korea faces the most acute challenge, with a total fertility rate of 0.72 (2023) -- the world's lowest -- and a projected fund depletion by 2055. The United States faces depletion of the OASI Trust Fund by 2033. Germany's pay-as-you-go system avoids fund depletion but requires increasing federal subsidies, which consumed approximately EUR 112 billion (approximately 24 percent of the federal budget) in 2024.

### Contribution Rates

South Korea's 9 percent contribution rate is substantially lower than Germany's 18.6 percent or the United States' 12.4 percent. Increasing the Korean rate is widely recognized as necessary but politically difficult. Germany's rate is the highest among the three but produces the highest replacement rate for average earners.

### Multi-Pillar Architecture

All three jurisdictions have adopted multi-pillar approaches, but the balance differs significantly. Germany and the United States rely more heavily on employer-sponsored and private pensions to supplement public benefits. South Korea's employer pension system remains relatively underdeveloped, with many small employers still offering lump-sum severance rather than funded pension plans.

### Retirement Age

All three jurisdictions are raising retirement ages: South Korea to 65, the United States to 67, and Germany to 67. However, effective retirement ages remain below statutory ages due to early retirement provisions, disability pathways, and labor market conditions.
