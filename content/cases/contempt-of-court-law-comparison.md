## Summary

Contempt of court -- the power of courts to punish conduct that defies, disrespects, or obstructs judicial authority -- is one of the oldest and most essential instruments for maintaining the rule of law. Yet its scope, classification, and severity vary dramatically across legal systems. South Korea addresses contempt through a dual framework: Article 138 of the Criminal Act criminalizes courtroom disturbance with up to 3 years imprisonment, while Article 61 of the Court Organization Act grants presiding judges the power to impose summary detention of up to 20 days or fines up to 1 million won for disruptive conduct. The United States, inheriting the English common law tradition, draws a fundamental distinction between civil contempt (coercive, designed to compel compliance) and criminal contempt (punitive, designed to vindicate court authority), codified primarily in 18 U.S.C. sections 401-402 with no fixed statutory maximum for criminal contempt. Germany, lacking a unified "contempt of court" doctrine, relies instead on section 178 of the Courts Constitution Act (GVG), which empowers judges to impose fines up to EUR 1,000 or detention up to one week for courtroom misconduct (Ungebuehr), supplemented by civil enforcement mechanisms under section 890 ZPO for non-compliance with court orders. The ECHR does not itself create contempt offenses but exerts powerful constraining influence through Article 10 (freedom of expression) and Article 6 (fair trial), requiring that any national contempt sanction be prescribed by law, pursue a legitimate aim, and remain proportionate -- a framework that has led to landmark rulings striking down contempt convictions in Kyprianou v. Cyprus and Sunday Times v. United Kingdom.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea addresses contempt of court through two principal legal instruments operating at different levels of severity and procedural formality.

**Criminal Act Article 138 (Contempt of Court or National Assembly):** A person who insults a court or the National Assembly, or who creates a disturbance in or near a court or the National Assembly for the purpose of obstructing or threatening the conduct of judicial proceedings or legislative deliberation, shall be punished by imprisonment with labor for not more than 3 years or by a fine not exceeding 7 million won. This provision, enacted as part of the original Criminal Act (Act No. 293 of September 18, 1953), treats courtroom contempt as a substantive criminal offense requiring full criminal prosecution, including indictment by a prosecutor and trial with standard evidentiary procedures.

**Court Organization Act Article 61 (Detention and Fines for Courtroom Misconduct):** The Court Organization Act grants the presiding judge a separate, summary power to maintain courtroom order. Under Article 61, the court may impose detention of up to 20 days or a fine of up to 1 million won, or both, by ruling against anyone who: (1) violates orders issued under Article 58(2) regarding courtroom conduct; (2) obstructs court proceedings through abusive language, disruption, or other conduct; or (3) significantly damages the dignity of the trial. This power -- known as the "courtroom police power" (beobjeong gyeongchalgwon) -- is considered an essential attribute of judicial authority, though it is delegated broadly to the presiding judge to ensure swift enforcement.

**Court Organization Act Article 58(2) (Courtroom Order):** The presiding judge may order the exclusion or removal from the courtroom of anyone who threatens the dignity and order of the court, or may issue other necessary orders to maintain courtroom order.

### Related Provisions

**Criminal Act Article 311 (Insult):** While not specific to courts, this general insult provision -- punishing public insult with up to 1 year imprisonment or a fine of up to 2 million won -- can apply when courtroom behavior constitutes a broader attack on a person's honor. Korean courts distinguish between "insult" (abstract expressions of contempt that cannot be proved true or false) and "defamation" (statements alleging specific facts).

**Criminal Act Articles 136-137 (Obstruction of Official Duties):** These provisions criminalize the use of force or threats to obstruct public officials in the execution of their duties, potentially applicable when courtroom disruption escalates to physical interference with judges, prosecutors, or court officers.

### Key Principles

**Dual-Track System:** The coexistence of Article 138 (criminal prosecution) and Article 61 (summary judicial power) creates a dual-track system. The presiding judge may impose immediate sanctions under Article 61 without involving prosecutors, while more serious or premeditated contempt is referred for formal criminal prosecution under Article 138.

**Proportionality:** Korean courts have emphasized that Article 61 sanctions must be proportionate to the disruption caused, and that the presiding judge's courtroom police power should be exercised with restraint to avoid chilling legitimate advocacy.

**Immediate Appealability:** Rulings under Article 61 are subject to immediate appeal (jeuksi hanggo), providing a procedural safeguard against arbitrary exercise of summary contempt power.

### Landmark Cases

**Supreme Court Decision 2007JeongMo3:** The Supreme Court clarified the scope of the presiding judge's courtroom police power under Article 61, establishing that this power is an inherent attribute of judicial authority essential to the fair and orderly conduct of proceedings, but must be exercised within statutory limits.

**Constitutional Court Decision on Article 61 (2014):** The Constitutional Court reviewed the constitutionality of the summary detention power, holding that while detention without formal criminal proceedings raises concerns under the presumption of innocence, the provision is constitutionally justified by the compelling need to maintain courtroom order, provided adequate procedural safeguards -- including the right to immediate appeal -- are preserved.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has one of the most developed and complex bodies of contempt law in the world, rooted in English common law and codified across federal and state statutes. The fundamental distinction between civil and criminal contempt, though often blurred in practice, drives both the procedural protections available to the contemnor and the permissible scope of sanctions.

**18 U.S.C. s 401 -- Power of Court:** A court of the United States has the power to punish by fine or imprisonment, or both, contempt of its authority in three categories: (1) misbehavior of any person in its presence or so near thereto as to obstruct the administration of justice; (2) misbehavior of any of its officers in their official transactions; and (3) disobedience or resistance to its lawful writ, process, order, rule, decree, or command. The statute does not prescribe a maximum penalty for criminal contempt, though constitutional protections apply.

**18 U.S.C. s 402 -- Contempts Constituting Crimes:** Acts or omissions constituting criminal contempt may also be punishable as separate criminal offenses. This section establishes that contempt proceedings are not precluded by the availability of independent criminal charges for the same conduct.

**Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 42:** This rule distinguishes between summary contempt (direct contempt occurring in the court's presence, which may be punished summarily) and non-summary contempt (indirect contempt requiring notice, a hearing, and other procedural protections including appointment of a prosecutor other than an interested party).

### Civil vs. Criminal Contempt

**Civil Contempt:** The sanction is coercive and conditional -- the contemnor "carries the keys of the prison in his own pocket," meaning compliance with the court's order will result in release or termination of the sanction. Civil contempt requires no jury trial, no proof beyond a reasonable doubt, and no specific maximum sentence. However, indefinite civil confinement raises due process concerns if compliance becomes impossible, and courts must periodically review whether continued confinement retains its coercive purpose.

**Criminal Contempt:** The sanction is punitive and unconditional -- it punishes past disobedience rather than compelling future compliance. Criminal contempt is classified as a "serious" offense requiring a jury trial if the sentence exceeds six months imprisonment (Bloom v. Illinois, 391 U.S. 194, 1968). The contemnor is entitled to the full panoply of criminal procedural protections: presumption of innocence, proof beyond a reasonable doubt, right to counsel, and the right against self-incrimination.

**Summary Contempt:** For direct contempt committed in the court's presence, the judge may impose punishment immediately without the procedural formalities ordinarily required. This summary power is justified by the court's need to maintain order in its own proceedings but has been narrowly construed by the Supreme Court to prevent abuse.

### Penalties

Federal law prescribes no fixed maximum for criminal contempt under section 401, but constitutional constraints apply. In practice, criminal contempt sentences in excess of six months require a jury trial. State contempt statutes vary widely: some impose fixed statutory maximums (e.g., 30 days, 6 months, or 1 year), while others mirror the open-ended federal approach.

Civil contempt fines may be compensatory (paid to the aggrieved party to compensate for losses caused by non-compliance) or coercive (accumulating daily fines designed to compel compliance). In International Union, United Mine Workers v. Bagwell (1994), the Supreme Court held that non-compensatory fines exceeding $64 million levied against a union for violating an injunction were criminal in nature and required the protections of a criminal proceeding.

### Landmark Cases

**International Union, United Mine Workers v. Bagwell, 512 U.S. 821 (1994):** A Virginia court imposed over $64 million in ostensibly coercive fines against the United Mine Workers union for violating an injunction during a strike. The Supreme Court held that these non-compensatory fines were criminal in character, requiring criminal procedural protections including a jury trial. The case established that the imposition of large, non-compensatory contempt fines for violation of complex injunctions will generally require criminal proceedings.

**Bloom v. Illinois, 391 U.S. 194 (1968):** The Supreme Court held that criminal contempt is a crime in every fundamental respect, and that serious contempt charges (those carrying potential imprisonment exceeding six months) are entitled to a jury trial under the Sixth Amendment.

**Cooke v. United States, 267 U.S. 517 (1925):** The Court held that summary adjudication is inappropriate for indirect contempts -- those occurring outside the court's immediate presence. An attorney and his client had been jailed for presenting a letter impugning the judge's impartiality. The Court remanded, establishing that indirect contempts require notice, a hearing, and full criminal procedural protections.

**Young v. United States ex rel. Vuitton et Fils, 481 U.S. 787 (1987):** The Supreme Court held that appointing the attorneys of the party benefiting from a court order as special prosecutors in the resulting criminal contempt proceeding violated due process, establishing that criminal contempt prosecutors must be disinterested to ensure impartiality.

**Gompers v. Bucks Stove & Range Co., 221 U.S. 418 (1911):** The Supreme Court articulated the foundational distinction between civil and criminal contempt: civil contempt sanctions are remedial and coercive, while criminal contempt sanctions are punitive. This framework continues to govern contempt classification.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany does not recognize a unified doctrine of "contempt of court" (Missachtung des Gerichts) comparable to the Anglo-American tradition. Instead, German law addresses courtroom misconduct and non-compliance with court orders through several distinct statutory mechanisms, each with its own procedural rules and penalty structure.

**GVG Section 178 (Courtroom Misconduct -- Ungebuehr):** Section 178 of the Courts Constitution Act (Gerichtsverfassungsgesetz) provides the primary mechanism for maintaining courtroom order. Against parties, defendants, witnesses, experts, or uninvolved persons who commit "improper conduct" (Ungebuehr) during a court session, the court may impose an order fine (Ordnungsgeld) of up to EUR 1,000 or order detention (Ordnungshaft) of up to one week, with immediate enforcement. When an order fine is imposed, the court must simultaneously determine to what extent detention may substitute if the fine cannot be collected. The decision is made by the presiding judge alone for uninvolved persons, and by the full court for parties and other participants. Before imposing sanctions, the affected person must be heard and given an opportunity to explain and apologize.

**GVG Section 176 (Sitzungspolizei -- Session Police Power):** The presiding judge holds the "session police power" to maintain order during court proceedings, including the authority to remove disruptive persons from the courtroom, restrict public access, and issue other necessary orders. This is the foundational authority upon which section 178 sanctions are built.

**ZPO Section 890 (Enforcement of Non-Fungible Obligations):** In civil proceedings, when a debtor fails to comply with a judgment requiring specific performance or restraint from a particular act, the court may impose an Ordnungsgeld (regulatory fine) of up to EUR 250,000 or Ordnungshaft (regulatory detention) of up to six months for each violation, upon application by the creditor. The debtor must have been warned of these consequences in the original judgment. This mechanism is functionally equivalent to civil contempt in common law systems but is classified as a regulatory enforcement measure (Zwangsmittel) rather than a criminal sanction.

**StGB Section 185 (Insult -- Beleidigung):** Insulting a judge, prosecutor, or other court participant may be prosecuted under the general insult provision, which carries a fine or imprisonment of up to one year for simple insult, and up to two years if committed publicly or through physical assault. Section 193 provides a defense for statements made in pursuit of legitimate interests (Wahrnehmung berechtigter Interessen), which can protect vigorous courtroom advocacy.

### Key Principles

**No Common Law Contempt Power:** German judges do not possess the inherent, open-ended contempt power available to Anglo-American courts. All judicial sanctions must be grounded in specific statutory authority, reflecting the German constitutional principle of Gesetzesvorbehalt (reservation of law), which requires that any restriction of individual rights have an explicit statutory basis.

**Proportionality (Verhaeltnismaessigkeit):** Constitutional principles require that courtroom sanctions be proportionate to the misconduct. The relatively modest penalties available under GVG section 178 (maximum EUR 1,000 or one week detention) reflect this principle, standing in stark contrast to the potentially unlimited sanctions available in Anglo-American systems.

**Right to Be Heard (Rechtliches Gehoer):** Before imposing an Ordnungsgeld under section 178, the court must afford the affected person an opportunity to be heard (Article 103(1) of the Basic Law). This procedural safeguard distinguishes the German approach from summary contempt in common law jurisdictions, where immediate punishment without a prior hearing is permissible for direct contempt.

**Separation of Criminal and Procedural Sanctions:** German law maintains a clear distinction between criminal penalties (Strafen) imposed through the Criminal Code and procedural enforcement measures (Ordnungsmittel and Zwangsmittel) imposed under procedural codes. Courtroom misconduct under GVG section 178 falls into the latter category, with significant consequences for procedural protections and appeal rights.

### Landmark Cases

**BVerfG (Federal Constitutional Court), 1 BvR 1036/99 (2001):** The Constitutional Court held that imposing an Ordnungsgeld under GVG section 178 without first giving the affected person an opportunity to be heard violates Article 103(1) of the Basic Law. The ruling reinforced that even the need for swift courtroom discipline does not override the constitutional guarantee of a hearing.

**BGH (Federal Court of Justice) Decisions on ZPO s 890:** The Federal Court of Justice has repeatedly emphasized that Ordnungsmittel under section 890 ZPO serve a dual purpose: they are both punitive (addressing past non-compliance) and preventive (deterring future violations). The characterization affects the standard of proof and the permissible cumulation of sanctions for repeated violations.

## ECHR & EU

### Legal Framework

The European Convention on Human Rights does not itself create contempt of court offenses. Instead, it operates as a supranational constraint on how member states may define and enforce contempt, establishing minimum standards of procedural fairness and proportionality that national contempt regimes must satisfy.

**Article 10 -- Freedom of Expression:** Article 10(1) guarantees the right to freedom of expression, including the freedom to hold opinions and to receive and impart information. Under Article 10(2), restrictions on this right must satisfy a "triple test": they must be (1) prescribed by law, (2) pursue a legitimate aim (including maintaining the authority and impartiality of the judiciary), and (3) be necessary in a democratic society. Contempt of court convictions that punish courtroom speech or media reporting on pending cases are subject to this balancing test.

**Article 6 -- Right to a Fair Trial:** Article 6 requires that contempt proceedings -- particularly those resulting in criminal sanctions -- satisfy fair trial guarantees, including the right to an independent and impartial tribunal, adequate time and facilities for defense preparation, and the presumption of innocence. The Court has found violations where the same judges who felt personally affronted by alleged contempt also adjudicated the contempt charge.

**Article 5 -- Right to Liberty:** Detention for contempt must comply with Article 5, which permits deprivation of liberty only in specified circumstances and subject to procedural safeguards, including prompt judicial review.

**TFEU Article 260 (EU Framework):** While not contempt in the traditional sense, Article 260 of the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union empowers the Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU) to impose financial sanctions on member states that fail to comply with its judgments. Penalties take two forms: (1) periodic penalty payments to induce immediate compliance, and (2) lump sum payments to address the consequences of prolonged non-compliance. The Lisbon Treaty (2009) accelerated this procedure by eliminating the reasoned opinion stage and introduced Article 260(3), which allows financial sanctions already at the conclusion of the first infringement proceeding for failure to notify measures transposing a directive.

### Key Principles

**Proportionality as the Master Principle:** The ECHR's approach to contempt centers on proportionality. Even when a contempt conviction serves a legitimate aim -- such as maintaining judicial authority -- the Court will find a violation if the sanction is disproportionate to the conduct. Imprisonment for courtroom speech by a lawyer, for example, has been found disproportionate where a fine or reprimand would have sufficed.

**Chilling Effect Doctrine:** The Court has recognized that disproportionate contempt sanctions create a "chilling effect" not only on the individual punished but on the legal profession as a whole, deterring vigorous advocacy and thereby undermining the right to effective legal representation guaranteed under Article 6(3)(c).

**Structural Impartiality:** Where summary contempt proceedings are conducted by the same judge who was the target of the alleged contemptuous behavior, Article 6 concerns arise regarding the impartiality of the tribunal. The Court has held that in such cases, the matter should be referred to a different judge or panel.

**Margin of Appreciation:** Member states retain a margin of appreciation in defining and enforcing contempt, but this margin narrows where the contempt sanction restricts rights protected by Articles 6 and 10, and particularly where the sanction involves deprivation of liberty.

### Landmark Cases

**Sunday Times v. United Kingdom (No. 1), App. No. 6538/74 (1979):** The Sunday Times published articles on settlement negotiations in the thalidomide disaster and was enjoined under English contempt of court law for "prejudging" pending litigation. The European Court of Human Rights held that while the injunction was "prescribed by law" and pursued the legitimate aim of maintaining judicial authority, it was not "necessary in a democratic society" because the public had a vital interest in receiving information about a matter of undisputed public concern. The ruling established that contempt laws restricting press reporting must be strictly justified and narrowly tailored.

**Kyprianou v. Cyprus, App. No. 73797/01 (Grand Chamber, 2005):** A Cypriot lawyer was sentenced to five days' imprisonment for contempt after making statements during cross-examination that the court considered disrespectful. The Grand Chamber found violations of both Article 10 (freedom of expression) and Article 6 (fair trial). The Court held that the same judges who felt personally offended by the lawyer's remarks could not impartially adjudicate the contempt charge, and that imprisonment was a disproportionate sanction that would have a "chilling effect, not only on the particular lawyer concerned but on the profession of lawyers as a whole."

**Ravnsborg v. Sweden, App. No. 14220/88 (1994):** A litigant was fined for offensive submissions to the court. The Court held that the fines imposed under Swedish law for courtroom misconduct did not constitute "criminal charges" within the meaning of Article 6, and thus the full panoply of criminal procedural protections did not apply. The ruling established that minor regulatory sanctions for courtroom misconduct may fall outside the criminal limb of Article 6, provided they are sufficiently limited in severity.

**Nikula v. Finland, App. No. 31611/96 (2002):** A defense lawyer was convicted for criticizing a prosecutor's conduct during trial. The Court found a violation of Article 10, holding that lawyers must enjoy broad latitude to criticize the conduct of proceedings, and that the distinction between criticism of a prosecutor (more protected) and criticism of a judge (less protected) is relevant to the proportionality analysis.

**Skouloudis v. Greece, App. No. 36524/04 (2009):** The Court examined Greek contempt proceedings and emphasized that the principle of immediacy in contempt sanctions must be balanced against the right to prepare a defense, particularly when the alleged contempt is not so disruptive as to require immediate action.

### EU Enforcement Mechanism

The CJEU's power under TFEU Article 260 represents a sui generis enforcement mechanism distinct from traditional contempt of court. Notable applications include:

**Commission v. France, Case C-304/02 (2005):** The CJEU imposed both a periodic penalty payment of EUR 57,761,250 per six-month period and a lump sum of EUR 20 million on France for failing to comply with a prior judgment regarding fisheries conservation regulations -- the first case in which both types of sanctions were imposed simultaneously.

**Commission v. Belgium (Article 260(3) TFEU, 2019):** The CJEU for the first time interpreted and applied Article 260(3), imposing a financial penalty on Belgium for failure to notify measures transposing a directive, establishing that the simplified enforcement procedure introduced by the Lisbon Treaty is fully operative.

## Comparative Analysis

### Conceptual Foundations

The four jurisdictions reflect fundamentally different approaches to the theoretical basis of contempt power. The United States treats contempt as an inherent judicial power, rooted in the very concept of a court and predating statutory codification. Germany rejects this notion entirely, insisting that judicial coercive power must be grounded in specific statutory authorization. South Korea occupies a middle position, recognizing an inherent courtroom police power but channeling it through specific statutory provisions. The ECHR does not take a position on the theoretical source of contempt power but insists that whatever its basis, its exercise must be proportionate and procedurally fair.

### Severity Spectrum

The jurisdictions arrange along a clear spectrum of severity. The United States occupies the extreme end, with criminal contempt carrying no fixed statutory maximum and civil contempt permitting indefinite confinement. South Korea's Criminal Act Article 138 allows up to 3 years imprisonment, while the summary Article 61 power is limited to 20 days detention. Germany's courtroom misconduct sanctions under GVG section 178 are the most modest -- a maximum of EUR 1,000 or one week detention -- though civil enforcement under ZPO section 890 can reach EUR 250,000 per violation. The ECHR constrains all national regimes through proportionality review, with the practical effect of pushing sanctions toward the German end of the spectrum.

### Procedural Protections

The most significant variation concerns the procedural protections available to the accused contemnor. The United States provides the most elaborate protections for criminal contempt (jury trial, proof beyond a reasonable doubt, right to counsel) but permits summary punishment for direct contempt. Germany requires a hearing before imposing any sanction but does not provide for jury trial. South Korea permits summary sanctions under Article 61 but guarantees immediate appeal. The ECHR requires at minimum that contempt proceedings satisfy Article 6 fair trial standards when the sanction is sufficiently severe to qualify as a "criminal charge."

### Press and Free Expression

The tension between contempt power and freedom of expression is most acute in the ECHR framework, where the Sunday Times and Nikula cases have established that contempt sanctions restricting speech must pass strict proportionality review. The United States addresses this tension through the First Amendment, which constrains but does not eliminate contempt power over speech. South Korea and Germany rely on general proportionality principles rather than specific free expression doctrines in the contempt context.

### Civil Enforcement

The coercive enforcement of court orders reveals the sharpest divergence. The American system of civil contempt -- with its potential for indefinite confinement -- has no true counterpart in the other jurisdictions. Germany's ZPO section 890 enforcement mechanism, while capable of imposing substantial fines, is structurally different: each violation is sanctioned separately rather than through ongoing coercive confinement. South Korea relies primarily on criminal sanctions for defiance of court orders rather than a dedicated civil enforcement mechanism. The ECHR has not developed a comprehensive doctrine on civil contempt but would likely subject indefinite coercive confinement to rigorous scrutiny under Article 5.
