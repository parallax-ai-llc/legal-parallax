## Summary

Social welfare and safety net programs in the Global South operate through fundamentally different legal, institutional, and ideological frameworks, yet share the common challenge of extending state protection to massive populations living in poverty and informality. China's social welfare architecture rests on the Minimum Living Standard Guarantee (dibao, established 1999) and the rural pension system (New Rural Social Pension Insurance, 2009), embedded within a socialist legal tradition that treats social assistance as a state obligation administered through party-state bureaucratic channels rather than individual justiciable entitlements. India operates the world's largest workfare program, the Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act (MGNREGA, 2005), which uniquely establishes a legal right to employment, alongside the Public Distribution System (PDS) providing subsidized food grains to approximately 800 million people under the National Food Security Act (NFSA, 2013). South Africa's social grants system, administered by the South African Social Security Agency (SASSA) under the Social Assistance Act 13 of 2004, delivers cash transfers to over 18 million beneficiaries (nearly one-third of the population), representing the largest unconditional cash transfer program in Africa. Brazil's Bolsa Familia, relaunched in 2023 under Law No. 14.601 after its replacement by Auxilio Brasil (2021-2022), provides conditional cash transfers to approximately 21 million families, while the broader social assistance framework is governed by the Organic Law of Social Assistance (Lei Organica da Assistencia Social, LOAS, Lei No. 8.742/1993) and the 1988 Constitution's comprehensive social security provisions (Articles 194-204). The Philippines' Pantawid Pamilyang Pilipino Program (4Ps), institutionalized through Republic Act No. 11310 (2019), reaches over 4 million households through conditional cash transfers modeled on Latin American programs, while Indonesia's Program Keluarga Harapan (PKH) provides conditional transfers to approximately 10 million families under Presidential Regulation No. 63 of 2017. These programs collectively demonstrate that the Global South has become the world's laboratory for social protection innovation, generating models that challenge Northern welfare state assumptions.

## China

### Minimum Living Standard Guarantee (Dibao)

The dibao (最低生活保障) system constitutes China's foundational social assistance program. Urban dibao was established by the State Council's "Notice on Establishing a Minimum Living Standard Guarantee System for Urban Residents in All Parts of the Country" (Guofa [1997] No. 29), with nationwide implementation mandated from 1999. Rural dibao followed through the State Council's "Notice on Establishing a Minimum Living Standard Guarantee System for Rural Residents in All Parts of the Country" (Guofa [2007] No. 19).

The legal framework was consolidated in the "Interim Measures for Social Assistance" (State Council Decree No. 649, 2014), which provides comprehensive regulations covering dibao eligibility, application procedures, benefit calculation, and oversight. Eligibility is determined at the household level based on per capita income falling below the locally determined minimum living standard line, combined with a household assets test. As of 2023, the urban dibao standard averaged 753 RMB per person per month nationally (approximately USD 104), while the rural standard averaged 621 RMB per month.

The dibao system's administration operates through a tiered bureaucratic structure: applicants submit requests to their neighborhood committee (shequ) or village committee (cunweihui), which conducts initial investigation; the sub-district office (jiedao banshichu) or township government reviews and verifies; and the county-level civil affairs bureau (minzhengju) makes the final determination. This process involves household economic status verification (jiating jingji zhuangkuang hecha), which since 2012 has increasingly utilized the Household Economic Status Verification Information System (jiating jingji zhuangkuang hecha xinxi xitong), linking data from banks, real estate registries, vehicle registration, and tax authorities.

### Rural Pension System

China's rural pension system has evolved from the failed Old Rural Pension Scheme (1992, largely collapsed by early 2000s) to the New Rural Social Pension Insurance (Xinxing Nongcun Shehui Yanglao Baoxian, NRSPI), established in 2009 through State Council guidance documents (Guofa [2009] No. 32). In 2014, NRSPI merged with the Urban Resident Social Pension Insurance (URSPI) to form the Urban-Rural Resident Basic Pension Insurance (Chengxiang Jumin Jiben Yanglao Baoxian), covering all residents not enrolled in the Urban Employee Basic Pension Insurance.

The scheme operates on a two-component model: (1) a government-funded basic pension (jichu yangjin), set at a minimum of 103 RMB per month nationally since 2023, with localities supplementing based on fiscal capacity; and (2) an individual account funded by participant contributions (ranging from 200 to several thousand RMB annually), local government subsidies, and collective supplements. As of 2023, approximately 160 million rural and urban residents received pension benefits under this scheme, while over 540 million residents were enrolled as contributors.

### Hukou-Linked Exclusion

The household registration (hukou) system profoundly shapes social welfare access. Despite reforms, approximately 376 million internal migrants (as reported by the 2023 National Bureau of Statistics) hold rural hukou registrations while living in urban areas, restricting their access to urban dibao, local social insurance, and public services. The State Council's 2014 "Opinions on Further Promoting the Reform of the Household Registration System" (Guofa [2014] No. 25) initiated gradual reform, with small and medium cities largely eliminating hukou transfer restrictions, but megacities like Beijing, Shanghai, and Guangzhou maintain strict points-based systems that effectively exclude most migrants from local social welfare programs.

### Social Insurance Law 2010

The Social Insurance Law of the PRC (2010) consolidated five social insurance schemes (pension, medical, unemployment, work injury, maternity) into a unified statutory framework. Article 2 declares that the state shall establish a social insurance system comprising basic pension, basic medical, work injury, unemployment, and maternity insurance to guarantee citizens' right to receive material assistance from the state and society in conformity with the law. The Law mandates employer and employee contributions, establishes portability mechanisms (particularly Articles 19 and 32 on pension transfer), and provides for government fiscal responsibility to fill funding gaps (Article 65).

## India

### MGNREGA: The Right to Work

The Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act (MGNREGA), enacted on August 25, 2005 (initially as NREGA; renamed in 2009), is a legislative landmark in global social protection. It establishes a legally enforceable right to employment for every rural household, guaranteeing at least 100 days of unskilled manual work per financial year at the statutory minimum wage rate.

Key legal provisions include:

- **Section 3(1):** The state government shall provide to every household whose adult members volunteer to do unskilled manual work not less than one hundred days of such work in a financial year.
- **Section 3(4):** If an applicant for employment is not provided work within fifteen days of receipt of the application, the applicant shall be entitled to a daily unemployment allowance.
- **Section 16:** The District Programme Coordinator and Programme Officer have statutory obligations to ensure employment is provided within the prescribed time and geographical limits (within 5 km of the applicant's village, or with 10% additional wage for greater distances).

The Act's accountability mechanisms include mandatory social audits (Section 17), transparency provisions requiring public disclosure of muster rolls and expenditure records, and grievance redressal through the Programme Officer, District Programme Coordinator, and the State Employment Guarantee Council (Section 12). The Supreme Court has repeatedly upheld MGNREGA's justiciability, most notably in Sanjit Roy v. State of Rajasthan (1983 AIR SC 328, a precursor case on the right to work) and through PIL interventions monitoring implementation.

In FY 2023-24, MGNREGA provided employment to approximately 60 million households, generating 2.7 billion person-days of work. The annual budget allocation was INR 60,000 crore (approximately USD 7.2 billion). However, persistent implementation challenges include delayed wage payments (the Supreme Court addressed this in Ratan Lal v. State of Rajasthan), suppressed demand through administrative barriers, inadequate work quality, and exclusion errors linked to Aadhaar (Unique Identification) authentication failures.

### National Food Security Act (NFSA) 2013

The NFSA (2013) converts the existing Public Distribution System (PDS) from an administrative welfare scheme into a statutory right. The Act entitles approximately 75% of the rural population and 50% of the urban population (roughly 800 million people) to receive subsidized foodgrains: 5 kg per person per month of rice at INR 3/kg, wheat at INR 2/kg, and coarse grains at INR 1/kg for priority households; and 35 kg per household per month for Antyodaya (poorest of the poor) households.

The Act's genesis lies in the Supreme Court's sustained intervention through the People's Union for Civil Liberties (PUCL) v. Union of India (Writ Petition (Civil) No. 196 of 2001), where the Court issued a series of interim orders from 2001 onward converting executive food programs into judicially enforced entitlements, appointing Commissioners to monitor implementation, and directing universalization of the midday meal scheme and the Integrated Child Development Services (ICDS). The NFSA codified many of these judicial orders into statutory form.

### Aadhaar and Welfare Delivery

India's welfare delivery infrastructure has been transformed by the Aadhaar (Unique Identification) system, given statutory backing by the Aadhaar (Targeted Delivery of Financial and Other Subsidies, Benefits and Services) Act, 2016. The Act enables Aadhaar-based authentication for receipt of subsidies, benefits, and services from the Consolidated Fund of India. In K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India (2018 (5) SCC 1), the Supreme Court upheld Aadhaar's validity for receipt of government subsidies and tax filing (Sections 7 and 57) but struck down Section 57 insofar as it mandated Aadhaar for bank accounts and mobile phone SIM cards, affirming the fundamental right to privacy under Article 21.

The Direct Benefit Transfer (DBT) program, launched in 2013, uses Aadhaar-linked bank accounts to transfer subsidies directly to beneficiaries, bypassing intermediaries. By 2024, the government reported cumulative DBT transfers of over INR 30 lakh crore (approximately USD 360 billion) across 315 schemes, with estimated savings of INR 3.5 lakh crore from eliminated leakage and ghost beneficiaries. However, civil society organizations and academic researchers have documented significant exclusion errors resulting from Aadhaar authentication failures, particularly affecting the elderly, disabled, manual laborers (worn fingerprints), and tribal populations.

## Africa

### South Africa: The Social Grants System

South Africa operates the largest social assistance program in Africa, delivering cash transfers to over 18.5 million beneficiaries (approximately 30% of the population) through the South African Social Security Agency (SASSA). The constitutional foundation is Section 27(1)(c) of the 1996 Constitution: "Everyone has the right to have access to social security, including, if they are unable to support themselves and their dependants, appropriate social assistance."

The Social Assistance Act 13 of 2004, as amended, provides for seven categories of social grants:

1. **Old Age Grant:** ZAR 2,180 per month (2024/25) for persons aged 60 and over meeting the means test. Over 3.9 million recipients.
2. **Child Support Grant (CSG):** ZAR 510 per month per child for the primary caregiver of children under 18 meeting the means test. Over 7.2 million children covered -- the single largest grant category.
3. **Disability Grant:** ZAR 2,180 per month for persons aged 18-59 with a disability lasting longer than 6 months. Approximately 1 million recipients.
4. **Foster Child Grant:** ZAR 1,180 per month per foster child placed by a court order. Approximately 280,000 recipients.
5. **Care Dependency Grant:** ZAR 2,180 per month for caregivers of children with severe disabilities requiring permanent care. Approximately 160,000 recipients.
6. **War Veterans Grant:** ZAR 2,200 per month for veterans of the Second World War or the Korean War.
7. **Grant-in-Aid:** ZAR 510 per month as a supplement for grant recipients requiring full-time care.

The Social Relief of Distress (SRD) Grant of ZAR 370 per month, introduced as an emergency COVID-19 measure in May 2020, has been repeatedly extended and as of 2026 reaches approximately 8.5 million unemployed persons aged 18-59. The SRD grant has become a de facto Basic Income Grant (BIG), with the Department of Social Development's 2022 Green Paper on Comprehensive Social Security Reform and the 2023 report of the Expert Panel on Basic Income Support recommending permanent institutionalization.

### Constitutional Court Jurisprudence

The Constitutional Court has been central to enforcing and expanding social assistance rights:

- **Khosa v. Minister of Social Development (2004 (6) SA 505 (CC)):** The Court struck down provisions of the Social Assistance Act that excluded permanent residents from social grants, holding that the exclusion violated the right to social security (Section 27) and the right to equality (Section 9). Justice Mokgoro's judgment emphasized that "the exclusion of permanent residents from the social grants scheme amounts to unfair discrimination."

- **Mashavha v. President of the RSA (2004 (12) BCLR 1243 (CC)):** The Court addressed administrative failures in grant delivery, affirming the positive obligation on the state to ensure effective administrative processes for social security delivery.

- **Black Sash Trust v. Minister of Social Development (2017 (3) SA 335 (CC)):** In this landmark case arising from the SASSA-Cash Paymaster Services (CPS) crisis, the Constitutional Court intervened to ensure continuity of grant payments when the contract with CPS (a subsidiary of Net1 UEPS Technologies) expired without a replacement payment system. The Court imposed personal cost orders on the Minister and declared direct oversight of the transition process.

### Nigeria: Emerging but Fragmented

Nigeria's social protection framework remains nascent. The National Social Safety Net Coordinating Office (NASSCO), established in 2016, coordinates the National Social Investment Programme (NSIP), which includes:

- **Conditional Cash Transfer (HUP):** The Household Uplifting Programme delivers bi-monthly cash transfers of NGN 5,000 to approximately 1.2 million poor households identified through the National Social Register (NSR).
- **N-Power:** A youth employment program providing monthly stipends to graduates deployed in education, health, and agriculture.
- **Government Enterprise and Empowerment Programme (GEEP):** Micro-credit scheme for petty traders and entrepreneurs.
- **National Home Grown School Feeding Programme (NHGSFP):** Provides meals to approximately 10 million primary school pupils.

The constitutional basis remains weak -- Section 16(2)(d) of the 1999 Constitution directs the state to provide adequate facilities for leisure and recreation and to ensure that "suitable and adequate shelter, suitable and adequate food, reasonable national minimum living wage, old age care and pensions, and unemployment, sick benefits and welfare of the disabled are provided for all citizens." However, these remain non-justiciable Directive Principles under Section 6(6)(c).

## Latin America

### Brazil: Bolsa Familia and the Social Assistance Framework

Brazil's social assistance framework is among the most comprehensive in the Global South. The 1988 Constitution dedicates Articles 194-204 to social security (seguridade social), encompassing health (saude), social insurance (previdencia social), and social assistance (assistencia social). Article 203 provides that social assistance shall be rendered to those in need, regardless of contribution to social security, and Article 203(V) guarantees a minimum monthly benefit of one minimum wage to elderly persons (aged 65+) and disabled persons who prove they lack means to provide for themselves and their families -- this is the Continuous Cash Benefit (Beneficio de Prestacao Continuada, BPC), established by the Organic Law of Social Assistance (LOAS, Lei No. 8.742/1993).

**Bolsa Familia** was created in 2003 by President Lula da Silva through Medida Provisoria No. 132 (later converted to Lei No. 10.836/2004), consolidating four pre-existing programs (Bolsa Escola, Bolsa Alimentacao, Cartao Alimentacao, and Auxilio-Gas). After being replaced by Auxilio Brasil under the Bolsonaro government (Lei No. 14.284/2021), Bolsa Familia was relaunched in March 2023 under Lei No. 14.601/2023.

The relaunched Bolsa Familia provides:
- **Beneficio de Renda de Cidadania (BRC):** BRL 142 per family member per month for families with per capita monthly income up to BRL 218
- **Beneficio Complementar:** Top-up to ensure minimum family benefit of BRL 600 per month
- **Beneficio Primeira Infancia:** BRL 150 additional per child aged 0-6
- **Beneficio Variavel Familiar:** BRL 50 additional per pregnant woman, nursing mother, or child/adolescent aged 7-18

Conditionalities require: (1) school attendance of at least 75% for children aged 4-5 and 60% for adolescents aged 6-17; (2) vaccination compliance for children under 7; and (3) prenatal care visits for pregnant women. Non-compliance triggers a graduated response from warnings to benefit suspension.

### The Cadastro Unico

Brazil's Cadastro Unico para Programas Sociais (CadUnico), established by Decreto No. 6.135/2007 and regulated by Decreto No. 11.016/2022, serves as the unified registry for all federal social programs targeting low-income families (monthly per capita income up to half the minimum wage). As of 2024, CadUnico contained records of approximately 40 million families (93 million individuals). The registry uses a multidimensional poverty assessment incorporating income, housing conditions, access to public services, and family composition. CadUnico data drives targeting for Bolsa Familia, Tarifa Social de Energia Eletrica (social electricity tariff), Programa Minha Casa Minha Vida (housing), and dozens of other federal, state, and municipal programs.

### Mexico's Social Protection Evolution

Mexico's social protection architecture has undergone dramatic restructuring. The Oportunidades/Prospera conditional cash transfer program (1997-2019), once a global model for CCTs, was terminated by the Lopez Obrador administration and replaced with a suite of unconditional programs: Pension para el Bienestar de las Personas Adultas Mayores (universal pension for persons aged 65+), Becas para el Bienestar Benito Juarez (education scholarships), and Sembrando Vida (agricultural support). These programs lack the conditionality mechanisms and rigorous impact evaluation that characterized Prospera. The constitutional reform of 2020 elevated several social programs to constitutional status (Article 4), attempting to protect them from elimination by future governments.

### Colombia's Familias en Accion

Colombia's Familias en Accion, established in 2001 under Law 1532 of 2012, provides conditional cash transfers to approximately 2.5 million families in extreme poverty and poverty. The program is integrated with the Sistema de Identificacion de Potenciales Beneficiarios de Programas Sociales (SISBEN IV), Colombia's proxy means test targeting system. The Colombian Constitutional Court has addressed social protection rights through the desarrollo progresivo (progressive development) doctrine, holding in multiple tutela decisions that the state has minimum core obligations to provide basic social assistance to the most vulnerable.

## Southeast Asia

### Philippines: Pantawid Pamilyang Pilipino Program (4Ps)

The 4Ps program, launched as a pilot in 2008 and institutionalized through Republic Act No. 11310 (Pantawid Pamilyang Pilipino Program Act, signed April 17, 2019), is the Philippines' flagship social protection intervention. The Act provides the legal framework for conditional cash transfers to poor households identified through the Listahanan (National Household Targeting System for Poverty Reduction).

Key provisions of RA 11310 include:

- **Section 4 (Target Beneficiaries):** Poor households with children aged 0-18 or pregnant members, identified through Listahanan assessment
- **Section 5 (Health Grant):** PHP 750 per month per household conditioned on children's preventive health checkups, growth monitoring, deworming, and vaccination; prenatal/postnatal care for pregnant members
- **Section 6 (Education Grant):** PHP 300-700 per month per child (varying by grade level) conditioned on minimum 85% school attendance
- **Section 7 (Rice Subsidy):** PHP 600 per month per household (added in 2023)
- **Section 9 (Modified Conditional Cash Transfer):** For homeless individuals, indigenous peoples in geographically isolated areas, and families in need of special protection
- **Section 14 (Grievance Redress System):** Establishment of a formal grievance mechanism at municipal and city levels

As of 2024, 4Ps reached approximately 4.4 million households across all 81 provinces. Total program expenditure was approximately PHP 100 billion (USD 1.8 billion) annually. Impact evaluations conducted by the World Bank and the Philippine Institute for Development Studies have documented positive effects on school enrollment, health utilization, and poverty reduction, though concerns persist regarding graduation pathways and the program's limited effect on chronic poverty.

### Indonesia: Program Keluarga Harapan (PKH)

Indonesia's PKH was established in 2007 as a pilot conditional cash transfer and scaled nationally under Presidential Regulation No. 63 of 2017 on Social Assistance (Bantuan Sosial). The program targets the poorest 10% of households, identified through the Data Terpadu Kesejahteraan Sosial (DTKS, Integrated Social Welfare Database) maintained by the Ministry of Social Affairs.

PKH provides quarterly cash transfers to households meeting conditionalities related to education (school enrollment and attendance for children aged 6-21) and health (prenatal/postnatal care, vaccination, nutrition monitoring for children under 6). Benefit levels vary by household composition, ranging from IDR 900,000 to IDR 3,000,000 per year per component (pregnancy, early childhood, primary education, secondary education, elderly, disabled).

As of 2024, PKH covered approximately 10 million beneficiary families. The program has been complemented by other social assistance programs: Program Indonesia Pintar (education cash assistance for all school-age children in poor households), Bantuan Pangan Non-Tunai (BPNT, cashless food assistance), and Bantuan Langsung Tunai (BLT, unconditional cash transfers introduced during COVID-19).

### Indonesia: Legal Challenges

Indonesia's social protection legal framework remains predominantly executive/regulatory rather than legislative/statutory. The constitutional basis in Articles 28H and 34 provides broad aspirational language, but unlike India's MGNREGA or Brazil's constitutional social security provisions, Indonesia lacks a comprehensive statutory social assistance act. Law No. 11 of 2009 on Social Welfare provides the broadest framework, establishing the state's obligation to provide social rehabilitation, social empowerment, social security, and social protection, but implementation relies heavily on presidential regulations and ministerial decrees that can be modified without legislative approval.

The Indonesian Constitutional Court (Mahkamah Konstitusi) reviewed aspects of the social protection framework in Decision No. 31/PUU-XI/2013, which upheld the constitutionality of BPJS but required the government to ensure that the transition from multiple social insurance providers to a single BPJS entity did not reduce existing benefit levels.

## Comparative Analysis

### Rights-Based versus Program-Based Approaches

The fundamental legal distinction across Global South social welfare systems lies in the degree to which social assistance constitutes an enforceable right versus a discretionary program:

1. **Statutory Right (Strongest):** India's MGNREGA establishes a legal right to 100 days of employment, with statutory penalties for non-provision. South Africa's Constitutional Court enforces the right to social security under Section 27 as a directly justiciable provision. Brazil's BPC constitutional guarantee of a minimum wage benefit to elderly and disabled persons is directly enforceable.

2. **Constitutional Directive (Intermediate):** Brazil's broader social assistance framework operates as a constitutional obligation subject to progressive realization. India's NFSA creates a statutory right to food. Colombia's Constitutional Court enforces minimum core social protection obligations through tutela.

3. **Executive Program (Weakest):** China's dibao, Indonesia's PKH, Nigeria's NASSCO, and (historically) Bolsa Familia operate primarily through executive or regulatory authority, modifiable without legislative approval. The Philippines' institutionalization of 4Ps through RA 11310 moved the program from this category to a stronger statutory basis.

### Targeting Mechanisms

Global South welfare programs employ diverse targeting methodologies reflecting different administrative capacities and political economies:

- **China:** Household economic status verification (jiating jingji zhuangkuang hecha) using linked administrative databases; community-based identification through neighborhood/village committees with elements of democratic evaluation (minzhu pingyi).
- **India:** Mixed methods -- SECC 2011 census data for AB-PMJAY; self-targeting through work requirement for MGNREGA; BPL/APL categorization (increasingly replaced by NFSA criteria) for PDS.
- **South Africa:** Individual means test administered by SASSA; income and asset thresholds for each grant category.
- **Brazil:** Cadastro Unico multidimensional assessment with self-declared income verified through administrative data cross-checks.
- **Indonesia/Philippines:** Proxy means test (PMT) through DTKS/Listahanan, using household asset indicators as poverty proxies; community validation processes.

### Fiscal Sustainability

Social protection expenditure as a share of GDP varies significantly: Brazil leads at approximately 2.5% of GDP for Bolsa Familia and BPC combined; South Africa's social grants cost approximately 3.4% of GDP; India's MGNREGA and NFSA together account for approximately 1.2% of GDP; China's dibao costs approximately 0.3% of GDP; and the Philippines' 4Ps costs approximately 0.4% of GDP. These figures reflect both different poverty levels and different political commitments to redistribution.
