## Summary

Capital punishment across the Global South encompasses the full spectrum from the world's most prolific executioner (China, estimated 2,000-5,000 annually) to complete abolition (South Africa, Brazil, Mexico, Colombia, the Philippines). The death penalty's trajectory in these jurisdictions illuminates fundamental questions about sovereignty, development, human rights universalism, cultural relativism, and the relationship between the state and its citizens. China's Criminal Law prescribes death for 46 offenses (reduced from 68 by the Eighth and Ninth Amendments), with execution carried out by shooting or lethal injection following mandatory SPC review since 2007. India retains the death penalty under the "rarest of rare" doctrine established in Bachan Singh v. State of Punjab (1980) 2 SCC 684, with approximately 539 prisoners on death row as of 2024 (National Law University Delhi, Project 39A data) and an average of fewer than four executions per decade since 2000. Nigeria retains the death penalty under both secular law (Criminal Code, Penal Code) and Sharia law (including stoning for adultery, though no Sharia stoning sentence has been carried out), with over 3,000 prisoners on death row -- one of the world's largest -- and a de facto moratorium on federal executions since 2016. South Africa abolished the death penalty in the landmark S v. Makwanyane 1995 (3) SA 391 (CC), the Constitutional Court's first major decision. Brazil abolished the death penalty for ordinary crimes in 1979 and constitutionalized abolition in the 1988 Constitution (Article 5, XLVII(a)), retaining it only for military crimes in wartime. Mexico abolished the death penalty through constitutional amendment in 2005 (Article 22). Colombia's 1991 Constitution (Article 11) prohibits the death penalty. Indonesia retains capital punishment under the Criminal Code, the Narcotics Law (Law 35/2009), and the Anti-Terrorism Law, with a controversial practice of executing foreign nationals for drug offenses. The Philippines has oscillated -- abolishing the death penalty in 1987, reinstating it in 1993 (RA 7659), and abolishing it again in 2006 (RA 9346) -- with periodic legislative attempts at reinstatement.

## China

### Legal Framework and Scope

China's Criminal Law prescribes the death penalty for 46 offenses across categories including: crimes endangering national security (Articles 102-113), crimes endangering public security (Articles 114-139), crimes undermining the socialist market economic order (Article 141, substandard drugs causing death; Article 153, smuggling in especially serious cases), crimes against the person (Articles 232-236, murder, injury causing death, rape with aggravating circumstances), crimes against property (Article 263, robbery with extremely serious circumstances), and corruption/bribery (Articles 383, 386). The Eighth Amendment (2011) eliminated the death penalty for 13 offenses (including smuggling of cultural relics, fraud, and theft), and the Ninth Amendment (2015) eliminated it for 9 more (including counterfeiting currency, organizing prostitution, and forcing prostitution), reducing the total from 68 to 46.

### SPC Review and Death Penalty Restraint

The 2007 recentralization of death penalty review authority to the SPC -- previously delegated to provincial High People's Courts by the 1983 "Strike Hard" decision of the NPC Standing Committee -- represented the most significant reform in China's death penalty practice. SPC President Xiao Yang stated that the SPC would ensure that the death penalty was applied only to "an extremely small number of criminals guilty of extremely serious crimes." The SPC's internal review process involves a panel of three judges reviewing each case, with the power to remand for retrial, change the verdict, or commute the sentence. Estimates suggest the reform reduced executions by approximately 30-50%.

The death penalty with a two-year suspension (死刑缓期二年执行, sixing huanqi liangnian zhixing, "si huan") is a uniquely Chinese institution. Article 50 of the Criminal Law provides that if a convict sentenced to death with suspension does not commit an intentional crime during the two-year period, the sentence is commuted to life imprisonment; if the convict performs meritorious service, it may be commuted to 15-20 years' imprisonment. The Ninth Amendment added that for cases of corruption involving especially huge amounts, the court may simultaneously decide that the sentence cannot be commuted or paroled after commutation to life imprisonment (Article 383(4)). In practice, the vast majority of death sentences with suspension result in commutation, making "si huan" functionally equivalent to life imprisonment.

### Execution Methods and Secrecy

China transitioned from shooting (枪决, qiangjue) as the primary execution method to lethal injection (注射死刑, zhushe sixing) following the 1997 Criminal Law revision and SPC Order No. 7 (2008) promoting lethal injection. Mobile execution vans (流动执行车, liudong zhixing che), equipped with lethal injection facilities, are used in areas without fixed execution facilities. Execution statistics remain classified as state secrets under the Law on Guarding State Secrets (保守国家秘密法, 2010 revision), Article 9. The Dui Hua Foundation, Cornell Center on the Death Penalty Worldwide, and Amnesty International provide estimates based on partial data, informant information, and media monitoring, consistently placing China's annual executions in the thousands.

### Organ Harvesting Controversy

China officially ended the practice of harvesting organs from executed prisoners in January 2015, with the China Organ Transplant Response System (COTRS) established as the mandatory allocation platform. Prior to 2015, executed prisoners were a primary source of transplant organs. The Independent Tribunal into Forced Organ Harvesting from Prisoners of Conscience in China (the "China Tribunal," chaired by Sir Geoffrey Nice QC) concluded in June 2019 that forced organ harvesting had taken place "on a significant scale" from Falun Gong practitioners and that the practice "probably continues." China categorically denies these findings. The WHO and the Transplantation Society have engaged with China on transplant reform, with the Hangzhou Resolution (2013) and Declaration of Istanbul (2008) providing the international normative framework.

## India

### "Rarest of Rare" Doctrine

The Supreme Court's framework for death penalty application rests on the landmark judgment in Bachan Singh v. State of Punjab (1980) 2 SCC 684, which upheld the constitutionality of the death penalty under Articles 14, 19, and 21 of the Constitution but established that it should be imposed only in the "rarest of rare cases when the alternative option is unquestionably foreclosed." Justice Sarkaria's majority opinion established a balancing test weighing aggravating circumstances (e.g., premeditation, extreme brutality, victim vulnerability, anti-social or abhorrent nature) against mitigating circumstances (e.g., youth, mental condition, possibility of reform, no prior criminal history).

The Court in Machhi Singh v. State of Punjab (1983) 3 SCC 470 attempted to categorize cases warranting the death penalty: (1) manner of commission (extremely brutal, grotesque, diabolical); (2) motive (exhibiting total depravity and meanness); (3) anti-social or socially abhorrent nature; (4) magnitude (victim count, community impact); (5) personality of victim (child, helpless, protected person). Despite these guidelines, empirical research by the National Law University Delhi's Project 39A has documented significant inconsistency: similarly situated defendants receive different sentences based on judicial preferences, regional variation, and quality of legal representation. Project 39A's "Death Penalty India Report" (2016) found that 74.1% of death row prisoners are from economically vulnerable backgrounds and 76% are from lower castes (Dalits, OBCs) or religious minorities.

### Recent Executions

India's last execution was in March 2020, when four convicts in the 2012 Delhi gang rape and murder case (Mukesh Kumar v. State, NCT of Delhi (2017) 6 SCC 1) were hanged at Tihar Jail. The execution, after multiple reviews and mercy petitions, was the first since the 2015 execution of Yakub Memon (convicted for the 1993 Bombay bombings, Yakub Abdul Razak Memon v. State of Maharashtra (2013) 13 SCC 1) and only the fourth since 2000 (the others being Dhananjoy Chatterjee in 2004 and Ajmal Kasab in 2012). The Supreme Court in Shatrughan Chauhan v. Union of India (2014) 3 SCC 1 commuted the death sentences of 15 prisoners on the ground of inordinate delay in the disposal of mercy petitions, holding that unexplained delay constitutes a supervening circumstance warranting commutation.

### Commutation and Clemency

The commutation process in India involves both judicial and executive dimensions. Courts may commute death sentences to life imprisonment on appeal, while the President (Article 72) and Governors (Article 161) exercise mercy powers.

### Death Row and Commutation

As of 2024, Project 39A documented approximately 539 prisoners on death row in India, the highest number in decades, reflecting both increased sentencing and reduced commutation/mercy rates. The President's power to grant mercy under Article 72 and the Governor's power under Article 161 of the Constitution have been subject to judicial review: in Kehar Singh v. Union of India (1989) 1 SCC 204, the Supreme Court held that the exercise of mercy power is judicially reviewable; in Epuru Sudhakar v. Government of Andhra Pradesh (2006) 8 SCC 161, the Court identified grounds for review including non-application of mind, mala fide exercise, and violation of Article 14.

The Supreme Court in Manoj v. State of Madhya Pradesh (2023) revisited sentencing procedures, mandating that trial courts must allow a separate hearing on sentence after conviction and must call for mitigation evidence including social investigation reports. This decision acknowledged the failure of the "rarest of rare" doctrine to prevent arbitrary application and sought to institutionalize meaningful mitigation.

## Africa

### Nigeria: Retention and De Facto Moratorium

Nigeria retains the death penalty under both secular and religious law. Under the Criminal Code Act (southern states), Section 319(1) prescribes a mandatory death sentence for murder. Under the Penal Code (northern states), Section 221 prescribes death for culpable homicide punishable with death. The Armed Robbery and Firearms (Special Provisions) Act (Cap A15 LFN 2004) prescribes death for armed robbery. Treason (Criminal Code Section 37; Penal Code Section 410) carries the death penalty.

In Sharia-implementing states, capital offenses include intentional homicide (qisas), armed robbery (hirabah), adultery (zina), and apostasy (ridda). The prescribed methods include hanging (for secular convictions), stoning (rajm, for Sharia convictions of adultery), and crucifixion (for hirabah in some interpretations). In practice, no Sharia stoning sentence has been carried out: all sentences have been overturned on appeal due to procedural defects, evidentiary insufficiency, or legal errors. The case of Amina Lawal, sentenced to death by stoning for adultery by a Sharia court in Katsina State in 2002, attracted global attention; the sentence was overturned by the Katsina State Sharia Court of Appeal in 2003.

Nigeria had an estimated 3,000+ prisoners on death row as of 2024, one of the world's largest death row populations (Cornell Center on the Death Penalty Worldwide). The federal government has observed a de facto moratorium on executions since 2016, though state-level executions occasionally occur (Edo State executed 4 prisoners in 2023). The Presidential Advisory Committee on the Prerogative of Mercy reviews death sentences, and successive presidents have commuted death sentences, particularly on independence and democracy days. The Nigerian Supreme Court in Kalu v. State (1998) NWLR upheld the constitutionality of the death penalty, finding it consistent with Section 33(1) of the 1999 Constitution (right to life "save in execution of the sentence of a court in respect of a criminal offence of which he has been found guilty").

### South Africa: Abolition and Constitutional Foundation

South Africa's abolition of the death penalty in S v. Makwanyane 1995 (3) SA 391 (CC) was the newly established Constitutional Court's first substantive decision and set the tone for South African constitutional jurisprudence. President Chaskalson, writing for a unanimous 11-member bench, held that the death penalty was incompatible with Section 9 (right to life), Section 10 (human dignity), and Section 11(2) (prohibition of cruel, inhuman or degrading treatment or punishment) of the Interim Constitution. Each justice wrote a separate concurrence, creating a rich multi-perspectival judgment drawing on Ubuntu philosophy, international human rights law, and the experiences of political violence under apartheid.

The Court applied the limitations analysis of Section 33 of the Interim Constitution (now Section 36 of the 1996 Constitution), finding that the death penalty failed the proportionality test: the state interest in deterrence and retribution could not justify the irrevocable taking of life, given the availability of life imprisonment as an alternative. The Court noted that the death penalty under apartheid had been disproportionately imposed on Black South Africans, with racial bias permeating every stage of the criminal justice process. Justice Sachs, himself a victim of a car bombing by apartheid security forces, wrote that "the most eloquent arguments for abolition came from the very government which was responsible for the hangings."

The abolition was consolidated in the 1996 Constitution (Section 11: "Everyone has the right to life") and the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 replaced death sentences with life imprisonment as the maximum penalty. Approximately 453 death row prisoners had their sentences commuted. Public opinion consistently favors reinstatement (polls showing 60-70% support), but the constitutional protection requires a constitutional amendment with a two-thirds parliamentary majority, which no party has pursued.

## Latin America

### Brazil: Deep-Rooted Abolition

Brazil's abolition trajectory is among the longest in the world. The last civilian execution occurred in 1876 (the slave Joao, in Pilar, Alagoas). Emperor Dom Pedro II, a committed abolitionist, systematically commuted all death sentences from the 1870s. The Republic's first Penal Code (1890) formally abolished the death penalty for common crimes, retaining it only for military crimes in wartime. The 1988 Constitution (Article 5, XLVII(a)) prohibits the death penalty "except in cases of declared war" under Military Penal Code provisions. Brazil ratified the American Convention on Human Rights (Article 4(3), prohibiting reestablishment of the death penalty once abolished) and the Protocol to the American Convention to Abolish the Death Penalty (2003), effectively making reinstatement a violation of international obligations.

Despite constitutional prohibition, public support for the death penalty remains significant (around 48-55% in Datafolha polling), particularly in the context of Brazil's extremely high homicide rates. Legislative proposals for reinstatement (propostas de emenda constitucional) have been filed in virtually every legislative session but have never gained sufficient traction. The STF has not been called upon to adjudicate the issue directly, as the constitutional text is unambiguous.

### Mexico: Constitutional Abolition

Mexico abolished the death penalty through the constitutional reform of December 9, 2005, which amended Article 22 to prohibit "the death penalty, mutilation, infamy, branding, whipping, beating, torment of any kind, excessive fines, confiscation of property, and any other unusual or transcendental penalties." The last formal military execution occurred in 1961. Mexico has ratified the Second Optional Protocol to the ICCPR and the Protocol to the American Convention to Abolish the Death Penalty (2007).

Mexico's abolition coexists with extraordinary levels of criminal violence (30,000+ homicides annually), creating a complex public discourse. Polling indicates significant public support for reinstatement, particularly for kidnapping and organized crime offenses. However, the constitutional prohibition, reinforced by international treaty obligations, makes reinstatement legally complex.

### Colombia: Century of Abolition

Colombia abolished the death penalty in 1910 by constitutional amendment (Legislative Act 3 of 1910, Article 3), making it one of the earliest abolitionist countries. The 1991 Constitution (Article 11) provides: "The right to life is inviolable. There will be no death penalty." The Constitutional Court in Sentencia C-144/1997 reaffirmed the absolute nature of the prohibition, holding that it cannot be suspended even under states of emergency. Colombia's abolition has persisted through decades of armed conflict, drug cartel violence, and paramilitary massacres, demonstrating that abolition does not require the absence of violence.

The Inter-American Court of Human Rights, headquartered in San Jose, Costa Rica, has developed extensive jurisprudence restricting the death penalty in the Americas under Article 4 of the American Convention. In advisory opinions OC-3/83 (Restrictions to the Death Penalty) and the contentious case of Hilaire, Constantine and Benjamin et al. v. Trinidad and Tobago (2002), the Court held that mandatory death sentences violate the Convention. This jurisprudence has influenced the broader Latin American abolitionist consensus.

## Southeast Asia

### Indonesia: Drug Executions and Diplomatic Crises

Indonesia retains the death penalty for offenses including premeditated murder (Criminal Code), drug trafficking (Law 35/2009, Article 114), terrorism (Law 5/2018), and treason. The execution method is firing squad (tembak mati), carried out at an undisclosed location, typically on Nusa Kambangan prison island (Central Java). Indonesia's death row population stood at approximately 350 as of 2024, with drug trafficking convicts constituting the majority.

Indonesia executed 14 drug convicts in 2015 (in two rounds, April and January) and 4 in 2016, provoking intense diplomatic reactions. The 2015 executions included Brazilian Marco Archer Cardoso Moreira and Dutch citizen Ang Kiem Soei, along with nationals of Nigeria, Vietnam, Australia (Sukumaran and Chan), and other countries. Australia recalled its ambassador temporarily. President Joko Widodo justified the executions by citing Indonesia's drug emergency, claiming 50 deaths per day from drug abuse (a figure contested by public health experts). Brazil and the Netherlands also recalled ambassadors.

The Constitutional Court in Decision No. 2-3/PUU-V/2007 upheld the death penalty for drug offenses, finding it consistent with Article 28A (right to life) read together with Article 28J(2) (limitations clause: rights are subject to limitations established by law "solely for the purpose of guaranteeing recognition and respect for the rights and freedoms of others and of satisfying just demands based on considerations of morality, religious values, security, and public order in a democratic society"). The Court noted that the right to life in the Indonesian Constitution is not absolute, distinguishing it from the European Convention's more robust protections. However, the Court mandated a minimum 10-year waiting period on death row before execution could proceed, and in 2024, the government announced a moratorium on executions pending review of the new KUHP's provisions.

### Philippines: Abolition-Reinstatement Cycle

The Philippines' oscillation on the death penalty is unique globally. The 1987 Constitution (Article III, Section 19(1)) provided: "Excessive fines shall not be imposed, nor cruel, degrading or inhuman punishment inflicted. Neither shall death penalty be imposed, unless, for compelling reasons involving heinous crimes, the Congress hereafter provides for it." Republic Act 7659 (1993) reinstated the death penalty for heinous crimes including murder, rape, kidnapping, drug trafficking, and plunder. Between 1999 and 2006, the Philippines executed 7 persons by lethal injection (the first being Leo Echegaray in 1999, following the Supreme Court's ruling in People v. Echegaray, G.R. No. 117472, 1996, upholding RA 7659's constitutionality).

Republic Act 9346 (2006) abolished the death penalty, providing that "the death penalty shall not be imposed" and that persons sentenced to death shall have their sentences reduced to reclusion perpetua without eligibility for parole. The Act was signed by President Gloria Macapagal Arroyo following an appeal from Pope Benedict XVI during her Vatican visit.

During the Duterte administration (2016-2022), the House of Representatives passed House Bill 4727 reinstating the death penalty for drug-related offenses (March 2017), but the Senate did not act on it. The Marcos administration has not prioritized reinstatement, though individual legislators continue to file reinstatement bills. The Philippines ratified the Second Optional Protocol to the ICCPR (aimed at abolition) in 2007 under RA 9346's implementing framework, creating an international law obstacle to reinstatement.

## Comparative Analysis

### Abolition Trajectories

The Global South demonstrates that abolition is not a linear or irreversible process. South Africa achieved abolition through constitutional transformation after apartheid. Brazil's abolition has deep historical roots predating the modern human rights movement. Colombia abolished the death penalty over a century ago despite ongoing armed conflict. Mexico abolished constitutionally in 2005. The Philippines abolished, reinstated, and abolished again. China has reduced capital offenses but shows no trajectory toward abolition. India maintains the death penalty but rarely executes. Nigeria retains it in law but observes a de facto moratorium. Indonesia actively executes, primarily for drug offenses. These diverse trajectories challenge the teleological assumption that all states move inevitably toward abolition.

### Constitutional Frameworks

The constitutional treatment of the death penalty varies significantly. South Africa's Constitution absolutely prohibits it (Section 11, as interpreted in Makwanyane). Brazil's Constitution prohibits it except in wartime (Article 5 XLVII(a)). Colombia's Constitution absolutely prohibits it (Article 11). Mexico's Constitution absolutely prohibits it (Article 22). India's Constitution does not explicitly address the death penalty, leaving it to the "rarest of rare" judicial doctrine. Nigeria's Constitution permits it explicitly (Section 33(1)). Indonesia's Constitution protects the right to life but permits limitations (Articles 28A and 28J(2)). China's Constitution does not address the death penalty, and constitutional review of NPC legislation is not practiced.

### Race, Class, and the Death Penalty

Across all retentionist Global South jurisdictions, the death penalty falls disproportionately on the poor and marginalized. India's Project 39A data shows 74.1% of death row prisoners come from economically vulnerable backgrounds. Nigeria's death row is overwhelmingly poor, with many inmates lacking legal representation at trial. China's execution of corrupt officials (often of substantial means) represents a partial exception, though rank-and-file offenders on death row are predominantly from disadvantaged backgrounds. Indonesia's execution of foreign nationals for drug offenses raises distinct issues of nationality-based discrimination. This pattern echoes -- and reinforces -- the racialized application of the death penalty documented in the South African experience under apartheid.

### International Law and Regional Norms

Regional human rights systems play a decisive role. The Inter-American system effectively prohibits reinstatement of the death penalty once abolished (ACHR Article 4(3)), creating a ratchet effect that has consolidated abolition in Latin America. The African Commission on Human and Peoples' Rights has moved toward an abolitionist position through resolutions and the draft Additional Protocol to the African Charter on the Abolition of the Death Penalty in Africa (adopted 2015, not yet in force). ASEAN has no binding human rights instrument restricting the death penalty, and the ASEAN Human Rights Declaration (2012) Article 11 protects the right to life without explicitly addressing capital punishment. The absence of a binding Asian human rights system permits China, India, and Indonesia to maintain capital punishment without regional judicial oversight.

### Public Opinion and Political Will

Public opinion across the Global South generally favors retention or reinstatement of the death penalty, yet abolition has proceeded in multiple jurisdictions. South Africa abolished despite 60-70% public support for retention. Brazil maintains abolition despite approximately 50% support for reinstatement. This "counter-majoritarian" abolition suggests that constitutional frameworks, judicial independence, and international treaty obligations can insulate abolition from populist pressure -- but only where these institutions are sufficiently robust.
