## Summary

Condominium and apartment ownership law -- the legal framework governing individual ownership of units within multi-unit buildings combined with shared ownership of common elements -- represents one of the most practically significant areas of property law in urbanized societies, addressing the complex interplay between individual property rights, collective governance, construction quality standards, and financial obligations. South Korea regulates condominium-type ownership primarily through the Act on Ownership and Management of Aggregate Buildings (집합건물의 소유 및 관리에 관한 법률, the "Aggregate Buildings Act") enacted in 1984 and substantially amended multiple times, which establishes the legal framework for sectional ownership (구분소유), common areas, management bodies (관리단), and dispute resolution, supplemented by the Housing Act (주택법) and the Multi-Family Housing Management Act (공동주택관리법) for residential apartment complexes (아파트). The United States regulates condominiums through state-level condominium acts, with most states having adopted some version of the Uniform Condominium Act (1977/1980) or the Uniform Common Interest Ownership Act (UCIOA, 1982/2008), which govern creation, governance through homeowners associations (HOAs), common elements, assessments, construction defect liability, and developer obligations, with significant variation among states. Germany enacted the Wohnungseigentumsgesetz (WEG, Act on Apartment Ownership) in 1951, comprehensively reformed in 2020 through the Wohnungseigentumsmodernisierungsgesetz (WEMoG), which establishes the legal framework for apartment ownership (Wohnungseigentum), separate ownership of individual units (Sondereigentum), co-ownership of common property (gemeinschaftliches Eigentum), and governance through the owners' association (Wohnungseigentümergemeinschaft). The ECHR protects condominium owners' rights under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 and Article 8 (right to respect for home), requiring proportionality in restrictions imposed by condominium associations and state regulation, while EU law affects condominium ownership through consumer protection directives, energy efficiency requirements, and cross-border property acquisition rules.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's condominium law reflects the country's massive urbanization and the dominance of apartment living (아파트) in Korean housing. As of 2025, approximately 63% of Korean households live in apartment complexes, making condominium governance a matter of profound social and economic importance.

**Aggregate Buildings Act (집합건물의 소유 및 관리에 관한 법률):** Enacted in 1984 and modeled partly on Japan's Act on Building Unit Ownership, this statute establishes the fundamental legal framework for sectional ownership. It defines the conditions under which a building may be divided into separately owned units (구분소유권), establishes rules for common areas and shared facilities, creates the management body framework, and provides for dispute resolution.

**Multi-Family Housing Management Act (공동주택관리법):** Separated from the Housing Act in 2016, this statute specifically governs the management of residential apartment complexes with 300 or more units (or 150+ units with elevators or central heating), imposing detailed requirements for professional management, accounting transparency, long-term repair reserves, and competitive bidding for maintenance contracts.

### Sectional Ownership (구분소유)

Sectional ownership arises when a building is structurally divided into units capable of independent use. Each sectional owner (구분소유자) holds:

**Exclusive Ownership (전유부분):** The right to exclusively own and use their individual unit, defined by the structural boundaries of the unit (typically the inner surfaces of walls, floors, and ceilings).

**Share in Common Areas (공용부분):** An undivided share in the common areas proportional to the exclusive ownership area. Common areas include structural elements (foundations, load-bearing walls, roof), shared corridors, stairways, elevators, lobbies, parking facilities, and building systems (electrical, plumbing, HVAC).

**Site Usage Right (대지사용권):** A right to use the land on which the building stands, proportional to the exclusive ownership area. Under Article 20 of the Aggregate Buildings Act, the site usage right is presumed inseparable from the sectional ownership and cannot be separately disposed of unless the governing rules provide otherwise.

### Management Structure

**Management Body (관리단):** Automatically established by operation of law when sectional ownership exists (Article 23). All sectional owners are members. The management body has legal capacity to sue and be sued in its own name.

**Management Body Meeting (관리단집회):** The decision-making organ, with regular meetings at least annually and special meetings as needed. Ordinary resolutions require a majority of attending members who hold a majority of voting rights. Special resolutions (including amendments to the governing rules and major reconstruction) require supermajority approval -- typically three-quarters or four-fifths of all sectional owners.

**Management Office (관리사무소):** For large apartment complexes, professional management is mandatory. The management office handles day-to-day operations including maintenance, accounting, security, and facility management. The Multi-Family Housing Management Act imposes detailed requirements on the selection process, qualifications, and oversight of management companies.

### Financial Obligations

**Monthly Management Fees (관리비):** Sectional owners pay monthly fees covering common area maintenance, utilities, insurance, and administrative costs. The Multi-Family Housing Management Act requires detailed itemization and public disclosure of management fees and expenditures.

**Long-Term Repair Reserve (장기수선충당금):** Apartment complexes must maintain a reserve fund for major repairs and replacements (roof, exterior, elevators, plumbing systems), based on a long-term repair plan established for 20-40 year cycles. The reserve is collected as part of monthly fees and may only be used for designated purposes. Failure to maintain adequate reserves has been a recurring source of disputes, particularly in aging complexes.

**Special Assessments (특별수선충당금):** Where the long-term repair reserve is insufficient for necessary repairs, the management body may levy special assessments, typically requiring a special resolution.

### Construction Defects

The developer (시행사/시공사) bears liability for construction defects under multiple legal frameworks. The Civil Code provides general warranty provisions, while the Aggregate Buildings Act and Housing Act establish specific defect liability periods: 10 years for structural defects, 5 years for significant non-structural defects, and 2-3 years for minor defects. The management body may collectively pursue construction defect claims on behalf of all sectional owners. Korea's large-scale apartment construction industry has generated extensive litigation over construction defects, particularly regarding waterproofing failures, structural cracks, and noise transmission between units.

## United States

### Legal Framework

American condominium law is exclusively state law, with no federal condominium statute. The two principal model acts are:

**Uniform Condominium Act (UCA, 1977/1980):** Developed by the Uniform Law Commission, adopted in modified form by approximately 16 states. Establishes comprehensive rules for creation, governance, common elements, and developer obligations.

**Uniform Common Interest Ownership Act (UCIOA, 1982/2008):** A broader model act covering condominiums, cooperatives, and planned communities. Adopted in modified form by approximately 8 states. Provides more detailed protections for unit owners and more extensive developer disclosure requirements.

Many states, including major markets like California (Davis-Stirling Common Interest Development Act), Florida (Condominium Act, Chapter 718), and New York (Condominium Act, Article 9-B RPL), have developed their own comprehensive statutory frameworks.

### Creation and Declaration

A condominium is created by recording a declaration (sometimes called a master deed or declaration of condominium) that describes the property, identifies the units and common elements, establishes the owners' association, and sets forth the governing documents including bylaws, CC&Rs (covenants, conditions, and restrictions), and rules and regulations. The declaration typically also includes a condominium plat or plan showing the physical boundaries of each unit and the common elements.

### Homeowners Association (HOA) Governance

**Board of Directors:** The HOA is governed by a board of directors elected by the unit owners. The board has fiduciary duties to the association, including the duty of care, duty of loyalty, and duty to act within the scope of authority granted by the governing documents and applicable statute. The business judgment rule generally protects board decisions from judicial second-guessing, provided the board acts in good faith, with reasonable care, and within its authority.

**Meetings and Voting:** Annual meetings of unit owners are required for board elections and other matters requiring owner approval. State statutes typically establish quorum requirements, voting procedures, and notice requirements. Most states require that certain significant actions (amending the declaration, imposing special assessments above a threshold, terminating the condominium) receive supermajority approval from unit owners.

**Assessment Authority:** The board has authority to levy regular assessments (monthly dues) to fund the common expenses budget, and special assessments for extraordinary expenses. Many states impose limits on the board's ability to levy special assessments without owner approval. Failure to pay assessments typically gives rise to an assessment lien on the unit, which in many states has priority over all liens except property tax liens and, in some states, first mortgages.

### Construction Defect Liability

Construction defect liability in condominiums is governed by a complex interaction of contract law (express warranties from the developer), tort law (negligence), statutory warranties (implied warranty of habitability and fitness), and specific condominium statutes. Key issues include:

**Standing:** Most states allow the HOA to bring construction defect claims on behalf of unit owners for defects in common elements and, in some states, for defects affecting individual units that share a common cause.

**Statute of Repose:** States impose varying statutes of repose (absolute time limits from completion of construction) ranging from 4 years (Virginia) to 12 years (Florida) or longer. These statutes bar claims regardless of when the defect is discovered.

**Developer Transition:** During the "developer control period," the developer controls the HOA board. The transition to owner control is regulated by statute and is a critical period for identifying and preserving construction defect claims. Several states require the HOA to conduct a transition inspection.

**Right to Repair Statutes:** Many states (including California's SB 800, Colorado's Construction Defect Action Reform Act) have enacted pre-litigation notice and repair procedures requiring the HOA to notify the developer of claimed defects and allow the developer an opportunity to inspect and repair before filing suit.

### Reserve Studies and Funding

Following the Champlain Towers South collapse in Surfside, Florida (June 2021), which killed 98 people, many states have significantly strengthened reserve study and structural inspection requirements. Florida enacted SB 4-D (2022) requiring milestone structural inspections for buildings three stories or taller that are 30 years old (25 years within three miles of the coast) and prohibiting waiver of structural reserve funding. Similar legislation has been enacted or proposed in multiple states.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

The Wohnungseigentumsgesetz (WEG) of 1951 was enacted to promote post-war housing reconstruction by enabling individual apartment ownership, which was not possible under the traditional BGB framework that treated buildings as essential components (wesentliche Bestandteile) of land. The 2020 reform through the WEMoG (effective December 1, 2020) was the most significant overhaul in the statute's history.

### Ownership Structure

**Apartment Ownership (Wohnungseigentum):** Consists of separate ownership (Sondereigentum) of a specific apartment unit combined with a co-ownership share (Miteigentumsanteil) in the common property (gemeinschaftliches Eigentum). The two components are legally inseparable.

**Separate Ownership (Sondereigentum):** Encompasses the interior spaces of the unit, including non-load-bearing interior walls, floor coverings, and interior installations. The WEMoG expanded the scope of Sondereigentum to include parking spaces in garages and outdoor parking areas, resolving longstanding uncertainty.

**Common Property (Gemeinschaftliches Eigentum):** Includes the building's structural elements (load-bearing walls, roof, foundation, facade), stairways, corridors, elevators, and building systems (heating, plumbing, electrical). The common property is owned collectively by all apartment owners in their co-ownership shares.

### Governance: The Owners' Association

**Legal Capacity (WEMoG Reform):** The 2020 reform recognized the Wohnungseigentümergemeinschaft (apartment owners' association) as a fully legally capable entity (rechtsfähige Gemeinschaft), able to acquire rights, incur obligations, own property, and sue and be sued in its own name. This was a fundamental change from the prior regime, which treated the association as a partially capable entity with uncertain legal status.

**Owners' Meeting (Eigentümerversammlung):** The decision-making organ, meeting at least annually. Resolutions are adopted by simple majority of votes cast (Kopfprinzip or Wertprinzip depending on the Teilungserklärung), unless a qualified majority is required. The WEMoG broadened the scope of decisions that can be made by simple majority, including structural modifications (bauliche Veränderungen) that previously required unanimous consent.

**Administrator (Verwalter):** The WEG requires appointment of a professional administrator to manage the property's day-to-day affairs. The WEMoG strengthened the administrator's position by granting the administrator expanded authority to act on behalf of the association externally (Vertretungsmacht), while also strengthening owners' rights to dismiss the administrator without cause (§ 26 WEG).

### Financial Management

**Hausgeld (Monthly Contributions):** Apartment owners pay monthly contributions (Hausgeld or Wohngeld) to cover common expenses, including maintenance, administration, insurance, and reserve funding. The Wirtschaftsplan (economic plan) is adopted annually by the owners' meeting and sets forth the anticipated expenses and each owner's share.

**Instandhaltungsrücklage (Maintenance Reserve):** The association must maintain an adequate reserve for major repairs and replacements. The WEMoG renamed this the Erhaltungsrücklage and clarified that it is the association's property (not the individual owners'), simplifying issues relating to transfer upon sale.

**Special Assessments (Sonderumlagen):** Where the reserve is insufficient, the owners' meeting may adopt a special assessment by resolution. The allocation follows the Teilungserklärung (declaration of division) unless modified by resolution.

### Structural Modifications (Bauliche Veränderungen)

The WEMoG fundamentally reformed the law of structural modifications. Under the prior regime, structural modifications (bauliche Veränderungen) that went beyond ordinary maintenance required the consent of all disadvantaged owners, creating a practical veto right that frequently blocked beneficial improvements. Under the reformed § 20 WEG, structural modifications may be approved by simple majority resolution, though owners who did not vote in favor are generally not required to bear the costs unless the modification constitutes a privileged measure (e.g., EV charging stations, barrier-free access, fiber optic connections, burglary protection).

### Construction Defects

The developer (Bauträger) bears warranty liability for construction defects under BGB §§ 633 ff. (contract for work) with a standard limitation period of five years from acceptance (Abnahme). A critical issue in WEG practice is the collective exercise of defect claims: the WEMoG clarified that the association (rather than individual owners) has the authority to assert and exercise defect claims relating to common property, a matter that had generated extensive litigation and conflicting court decisions.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Framework

The European Court of Human Rights addresses condominium law primarily through Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (property protection) and Article 8 (respect for private and family life, including the home).

**Association Decisions and Property Rights:** The Court has recognized that condominium association decisions restricting individual owners' property rights must satisfy proportionality requirements. In *Herrmann v. Germany* (Application No. 9300/07, Grand Chamber, 2012), addressing hunting rights on private property, the Court articulated principles applicable to collective decision-making over shared property, emphasizing that minority owners must have meaningful procedural recourse against majority decisions that disproportionately burden their rights.

**Forced Sale and Exclusion:** Some national laws permit the exclusion or forced sale of a unit owner's property for persistent and serious violations of community obligations. The ECHR requires that such extreme measures be proportionate, preceded by adequate notice and opportunity to remedy, and subject to judicial review. The German WEG's forced sale provision (Entziehung des Wohnungseigentums, § 17 WEG) has been upheld as compatible with Convention requirements where applied as a last resort.

**Housing Rights:** Article 8 ECHR protects the right to respect for one's home, which extends to condominium units. State regulations affecting condominium living conditions -- including noise regulations, building safety requirements, and habitability standards -- must be assessed against Article 8 standards.

### EU Consumer Protection

EU consumer protection directives significantly affect condominium law in member states:

**Consumer Sales Directive (EU) 2019/771 and Digital Content Directive (EU) 2019/770:** While primarily targeting goods and digital content, these directives' conformity and warranty frameworks influence national approaches to construction quality standards and developer warranties for newly constructed condominiums.

**Energy Performance of Buildings Directive (EPBD, Directive 2010/31/EU, revised 2024):** Imposes energy efficiency requirements on buildings, including existing condominiums. The 2024 revision requires member states to establish renovation strategies targeting the worst-performing buildings, directly affecting condominium associations' obligations to undertake energy-efficient renovations. This intersects with WEG governance when renovation decisions require owner approval and financial commitments.

**Unfair Contract Terms Directive (93/13/EEC):** Applicable to developer-purchaser contracts for new condominium units, protecting individual purchasers against unfair terms in pre-formulated contracts. The CJEU has developed extensive case law on the unfairness of various clauses in real estate purchase contracts.

### Cross-Border Acquisition

The EU's free movement of capital (Article 63 TFEU) restricts member states' ability to impose discriminatory barriers on cross-border acquisition of condominium units. However, the Treaty recognizes that restrictions may be justified on grounds of public policy or public security, and certain member states (notably Austria, Denmark, and Malta) maintain restrictions on acquisition of secondary residences by non-residents under specific Treaty derogations.

## Comparative Analysis

### Ownership Models

All four jurisdictions have adopted fundamentally similar ownership models combining individual unit ownership with shared ownership of common elements, reflecting the universal practical need to enable separate ownership within multi-unit buildings. The German Sondereigentum/gemeinschaftliches Eigentum distinction, the Korean 전유부분/공용부분 framework, and the American unit/common elements structure are functionally equivalent, though they differ in the precise boundary between individual and common ownership (e.g., treatment of balconies, parking spaces, and exterior walls).

### Governance Complexity

The governance challenges of condominium living are remarkably similar across jurisdictions: balancing majority rule with minority protection, ensuring professional management while maintaining owner oversight, funding adequate reserves for long-term maintenance, and resolving disputes among neighbors with divergent interests. The German WEMoG reform of 2020 and Korea's separation of the Multi-Family Housing Management Act in 2016 both reflect ongoing efforts to modernize governance frameworks for increasingly large and complex residential developments.

### Construction Safety

The Champlain Towers collapse in Florida (2021) highlighted the catastrophic consequences of inadequate structural maintenance and insufficient reserve funding, prompting legislative reforms across the United States. Korea and Germany have more established frameworks for mandatory structural inspections and reserve funding requirements, though aging building stock in all jurisdictions presents growing challenges. The ECHR's Article 2 (right to life) jurisprudence imposes positive obligations on states to protect against foreseeable risks to life, which extends to ensuring adequate building safety regulation.

### Energy Transition

The EU's energy efficiency requirements are driving significant changes in condominium governance across European member states, requiring collective investment decisions for building renovation that may impose substantial financial burdens on individual owners. Germany's reformed WEG facilitates this by allowing majority approval of structural modifications, while the ECHR's proportionality framework requires that the costs of energy-efficient renovation be distributed fairly among owners.
