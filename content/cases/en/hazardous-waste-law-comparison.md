## Summary

Hazardous waste regulation -- the legal framework governing the generation, transportation, treatment, storage, and disposal of wastes that pose substantial risks to human health and the environment due to their toxic, corrosive, flammable, or reactive characteristics -- emerged as a critical area of environmental law following high-profile contamination disasters in the 1970s and 1980s that revealed the devastating consequences of uncontrolled hazardous waste disposal. South Korea regulates hazardous waste through the Wastes Control Act (폐기물관리법) and the Act on the Control of Transboundary Movement of Hazardous Wastes and Their Disposal (유해폐기물의 국가간 이동 및 그 처리에 관한 법률), classifying designated wastes (지정폐기물) as those requiring special management due to hazardous characteristics, with the Korea Environment Corporation (한국환경공단) operating the national Allbaro waste tracking system. The United States established the world's most comprehensive hazardous waste regulatory framework through the Resource Conservation and Recovery Act (RCRA, 42 U.S.C. Sections 6901-6992k), creating a "cradle-to-grave" tracking system administered by the EPA, supplemented by the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act (CERCLA/Superfund, 42 U.S.C. Sections 9601-9675) imposing strict, joint and several, and retroactive liability for contaminated site cleanup. Germany regulates hazardous waste under the Kreislaufwirtschaftsgesetz (KrWG, Circular Economy Act) and the Abfallverzeichnisverordnung (AVV) implementing the EU European Waste Catalogue, with the waste hierarchy principle (Abfallhierarchie) of prevention, preparation for reuse, recycling, energy recovery, and disposal enshrined in law. The EU established a comprehensive hazardous waste framework through the Waste Framework Directive (2008/98/EC), the Waste Shipment Regulation (EC) No 1013/2006 (revised as Regulation (EU) 2024/1157), and the Industrial Emissions Directive (2010/75/EU) governing waste incineration, all operating within the framework of the Basel Convention on the Control of Transboundary Movements of Hazardous Wastes (1989).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's hazardous waste management is governed primarily by the Wastes Control Act (폐기물관리법, Act No. 19953), which establishes the classification system, permit requirements, and management obligations for all waste types including designated wastes (지정폐기물). The Act defines designated wastes as those with hazardous characteristics including toxicity, corrosivity, flammability, infectiousness, or other properties that may harm human health or the environment.

### Classification System

| Waste Category | Examples | Regulatory Requirement |
|---------------|----------|----------------------|
| Designated waste - organic solvents | Halogenated and non-halogenated solvents | Licensed treatment facility; manifest tracking |
| Designated waste - waste acids/alkalis | Sulfuric acid, hydrochloric acid, caustic soda | Neutralization or licensed treatment |
| Designated waste - waste oils | Used lubricating oils, cutting oils | Recycling or licensed incineration |
| Designated waste - medical waste | Infectious waste from healthcare facilities | Specialized incineration; separate tracking |
| Designated waste - heavy metals | Lead, mercury, cadmium-containing waste | Stabilization/solidification; secure landfill |

### Allbaro System

The Allbaro (올바로) system is Korea's comprehensive electronic waste management information system, operated by the Korea Environment Corporation (한국환경공단). The system tracks waste from generation through final disposal, requiring real-time electronic manifest entries by generators, transporters, and disposal facilities. All designated waste generators must register with Allbaro and maintain electronic records.

### Extended Producer Responsibility

South Korea's Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) system (생산자책임재활용제도) requires manufacturers and importers of designated products to take responsibility for collecting and recycling their products after consumer use. The system covers packaging, batteries, tires, lubricants, electronics, and other products.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States addresses hazardous waste through two complementary statutes: RCRA (governing ongoing waste management) and CERCLA (governing cleanup of contaminated sites).

### RCRA -- Cradle-to-Grave System

RCRA Subtitle C establishes the hazardous waste management framework:

**Identification:** Wastes are hazardous if they appear on EPA's four listed waste lists (F-list: non-specific source; K-list: specific source; P-list: acutely toxic discarded commercial chemicals; U-list: toxic discarded commercial chemicals) or exhibit one of four characteristics (ignitability, corrosivity, reactivity, toxicity as measured by the Toxicity Characteristic Leaching Procedure).

**Generator Requirements:** Generators are classified as Large Quantity Generators (LQGs), Small Quantity Generators (SQGs), or Very Small Quantity Generators (VSQGs) based on monthly generation volumes, with corresponding obligations for accumulation, labeling, and reporting.

**Manifest System:** The EPA's e-Manifest system (launched June 30, 2018) requires electronic tracking of hazardous waste shipments from generator to designated treatment, storage, or disposal facility (TSDF).

### CERCLA/Superfund

CERCLA imposes liability for cleanup of contaminated sites:

| Liable Party | Basis |
|-------------|-------|
| Current owner/operator | Strict liability regardless of fault |
| Former owner/operator (at time of disposal) | Strict liability |
| Person who arranged for disposal | Arranger liability |
| Transporter who selected disposal site | Transporter liability |

Liability is strict (no fault required), joint and several (any single party can be held liable for entire cleanup cost), and retroactive (applies to disposal that occurred before CERCLA's enactment). The National Priorities List (NPL) identifies the most contaminated sites, with over 1,300 sites currently listed.

### Landmark Cases

**Burlington Northern & Santa Fe Railway Co. v. United States, 556 U.S. 599 (2009):** The Supreme Court held that CERCLA arranger liability requires proof of intent to dispose of hazardous substances, and that joint and several liability is not mandatory when there is a reasonable basis for apportioning harm.

**United States v. Bestfoods, 524 U.S. 51 (1998):** The Court clarified the standard for parent corporation liability under CERCLA, holding that a parent can be directly liable as an "operator" only if it actually operated the polluting facility.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's hazardous waste regulation operates under the Kreislaufwirtschaftsgesetz (KrWG, Circular Economy Act), which transposes the EU Waste Framework Directive and establishes the waste hierarchy as a binding legal obligation. Hazardous waste classification follows the European Waste Catalogue as implemented through the Abfallverzeichnisverordnung (AVV).

### Waste Hierarchy (Section 6 KrWG)

The five-step waste hierarchy is legally binding:

1. **Vermeidung** (Prevention): Highest priority; waste generation must be avoided where possible
2. **Vorbereitung zur Wiederverwendung** (Preparation for reuse)
3. **Recycling** (Material recovery)
4. **Sonstige Verwertung** (Other recovery, including energy recovery)
5. **Beseitigung** (Disposal): Last resort

### Tracking and Documentation (Nachweisverordnung)

The Nachweisverordnung (NachwV) establishes comprehensive tracking requirements for hazardous waste. Electronic tracking through the eANV (elektronisches Abfallnachweisverfahren) system requires:

- Preliminary documentation (Entsorgungsnachweis) confirming the disposal route before waste movement
- Accompanying documentation (Begleitschein) for each individual transport
- Annual waste balance reports (Abfallbilanzen) from generators and disposal facilities

### Soil Contamination and Cleanup

The Bundes-Bodenschutzgesetz (BBodSchG, Federal Soil Protection Act) and the Bundes-Bodenschutzverordnung (BBodSchV) govern the investigation and remediation of contaminated sites. The polluter-pays principle applies, with responsible parties including the polluter (Verursacher), site owner (Grundstückseigentümer), and site possessor (Inhaber der tatsächlichen Gewalt). Germany maintains registers of contaminated and suspected contaminated sites (Altlastenkataster) at the state level.

### Extended Producer Responsibility

Germany has been a pioneer in EPR, starting with the Verpackungsverordnung (Packaging Ordinance) in 1991, now replaced by the Verpackungsgesetz (VerpackG). The Elektro- und Elektronikgerätegesetz (ElektroG) implements the EU WEEE Directive, requiring producers to finance collection and recycling of electrical and electronic equipment.

## ECHR & EU

### Waste Framework Directive (2008/98/EC)

The WFD establishes the overarching framework for waste management in the EU:

- **Waste hierarchy** (Article 4): Binding priority order from prevention to disposal
- **Polluter-pays principle** (Article 14): Costs of waste management borne by the waste producer or holder
- **Self-sufficiency and proximity principles** (Articles 16): Waste should be disposed of in the nearest appropriate facility
- **Hazardous waste mixing ban** (Article 18): Prohibition on mixing different categories of hazardous waste or mixing hazardous with non-hazardous waste

### Waste Shipment Regulation

The revised Waste Shipment Regulation (EU) 2024/1157, adopted on April 24, 2024, significantly strengthens controls on transboundary waste movements:

- Prohibits export of plastic waste to non-OECD countries
- Requires electronic tracking of waste shipments (replacing paper-based notification)
- Strengthens enforcement powers and penalties for illegal shipments
- Implements Basel Convention Ban Amendment provisions within EU law

### Environmental Liability Directive (2004/35/CE)

The ELD establishes a framework for environmental liability based on the polluter-pays principle. For operators of activities listed in Annex III (including hazardous waste management), strict liability applies for environmental damage to water, land, and biodiversity. For non-listed activities, fault-based liability applies.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**López Ostra v. Spain (Application No. 16798/90, 1994):** Landmark case establishing that severe environmental pollution, including from a waste treatment plant, can amount to a violation of Article 8 (right to respect for private and family life), even without seriously endangering health.

**Öneryıldız v. Turkey (Application No. 48939/99, Grand Chamber, 2004):** Following a methane explosion at an unregulated municipal waste dump that killed 39 people, the Grand Chamber found violations of Article 2 (right to life) due to the state's failure to regulate hazardous waste disposal and protect nearby residents.

**Di Sarno and Others v. Italy (Application No. 30765/08, 2012):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 arising from the Naples waste crisis, where the state's prolonged failure to ensure adequate waste management resulted in uncollected hazardous waste accumulating in public areas.

## Comparative Analysis

### Classification Approaches

The United States' dual system of listed and characteristic wastes is the most detailed, providing a specific regulatory identity for hundreds of waste streams. Germany and the EU use the European Waste Catalogue with "mirror entries" that require case-by-case assessment of hazard properties. South Korea's designated waste categories are less granular but cover the major hazardous waste streams.

### Liability Regimes

CERCLA's strict, joint and several, retroactive liability regime is the most aggressive in the world, creating powerful incentives for responsible waste management and voluntary cleanup. Germany's BBodSchG applies a similar polluter-pays principle but without the joint and several element. The EU's Environmental Liability Directive provides a harmonized but less stringent framework. South Korea's liability provisions are developing but less established.

### Circular Economy Integration

Germany and the EU lead in integrating hazardous waste management within a circular economy framework, with the legally binding waste hierarchy and ambitious recycling targets. South Korea has made significant progress through its EPR system. The United States' RCRA, while establishing a preference for waste minimization, is primarily a linear management system focused on safe disposal rather than circularity.

### Transboundary Controls

The revised EU Waste Shipment Regulation represents the strictest transboundary waste movement controls, going beyond the Basel Convention by prohibiting plastic waste exports to non-OECD countries. The United States, which did not ratify the Basel Ban Amendment until 2023, historically maintained more permissive export policies. South Korea enforces Basel Convention obligations through its dedicated transboundary waste act.
