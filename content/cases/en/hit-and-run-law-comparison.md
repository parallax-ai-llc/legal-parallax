## Summary

Hit and run -- the act of fleeing the scene of a traffic accident without fulfilling legal obligations to stop, render aid, and identify oneself -- represents one of the most universally condemned traffic offenses, bridging criminal law and road safety regulation across all major legal systems. South Korea addresses hit and run primarily through the Act on Special Cases Concerning the Settlement of Traffic Accidents (교통사고처리 특례법) and the Specific Crimes Punishment Act (특정범죄 가중처벌 등에 관한 법률), Article 5-3, which imposes severe penalties including imprisonment for life or not less than five years when a driver flees the scene and the victim dies; the Korean approach is notably punitive, reflecting deep societal condemnation amplified by a series of high-profile incidents including the 2022 Gangnam hit-and-run case that killed nine pedestrians. The United States treats hit and run under individual state criminal codes -- with penalties ranging from misdemeanors for property-damage-only incidents to felonies carrying up to fifteen years' imprisonment when death or serious bodily injury results -- while federal law addresses the issue only on federal property and military installations. Germany criminalizes hit and run through Section 142 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB, Unerlaubtes Entfernen vom Unfallort, literally "unauthorized departure from the scene of an accident"), which uniquely imposes obligations not only on drivers who cause accidents but on all persons involved in traffic accidents, regardless of fault, with penalties of up to three years' imprisonment or a fine, and automatic driver's license suspension. At the European level, the Vienna Convention on Road Traffic (1968) establishes baseline obligations for accident scene conduct, the EU's Motor Insurance Directive (2009/103/EC, consolidated by Directive 2021/2118) ensures victim compensation through guarantee funds when hit-and-run drivers cannot be identified, and the ECHR has recognized positive obligations under Article 2 (right to life) requiring states to maintain effective investigation and enforcement frameworks for fatal traffic incidents.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's approach to hit and run is among the most punitive in the world, reflecting cultural attitudes that view fleeing the scene of an accident as a profound moral failure beyond the underlying traffic offense. The primary legal framework consists of two statutes operating in tandem.

**Specific Crimes Punishment Act, Article 5-3 (특정범죄 가중처벌 등에 관한 법률 제5조의3):** This provision imposes aggravated penalties for drivers who cause injuries through negligent driving and then flee the scene. The penalties escalate dramatically based on the outcome:

### Penalty Structure

| Outcome | Penalty |
|---------|---------|
| Victim dies due to failure to render aid (도주 후 사망) | Life imprisonment or imprisonment for not less than 5 years |
| Victim suffers serious injury due to failure to render aid | Imprisonment for not less than 3 years |
| Victim injured, driver flees | Imprisonment of 1 year to 30 years or fine |
| Property damage only, driver flees | Fine or up to 5 years imprisonment |

**Traffic Accident Settlement Act (교통사고처리 특례법):** This act provides a general framework for the settlement of traffic accidents, establishing obligations for drivers involved in accidents and providing for insurance-based resolution of minor incidents. Critically, the Act contains exceptions to its settlement framework for hit-and-run offenses -- a driver who flees the scene cannot benefit from the Act's provisions for reduced criminal liability through insurance settlements.

### Key Judicial Decisions

The Supreme Court of Korea (대법원) has established important principles for hit-and-run cases:

**Supreme Court Decision 2012Do6141:** The court held that the duty to remain at the scene and render aid arises immediately upon awareness that an accident has occurred, regardless of the driver's subjective assessment of the severity of injuries. A driver who observes that a pedestrian has been struck cannot escape liability by claiming the injuries appeared minor.

**Supreme Court Decision 2018Do10112:** The court clarified that "fleeing" (도주) requires not merely physical departure from the scene but the intent to evade the legal obligations of identification and aid. A driver who leaves briefly to call emergency services from a nearby location, with the intention of returning, does not commit a hit-and-run offense.

### The Yoon Chang-ho Act (2018)

Following the death of 11-year-old Yoon Chang-ho, who was killed by a speeding vehicle in a school zone in September 2019, the National Assembly enacted amendments commonly known as the "Minsik's Law" (민식이법) strengthening protections in school zones. While primarily addressing speeding, the legislation heightened penalties for hit-and-run offenses in school zones and reinforced the obligation to remain at the scene and render immediate aid to child victims.

### Insurance and Compensation

South Korea operates a comprehensive motor vehicle insurance system under the Automobile Damage Compensation Guarantee Act (자동차손해배상 보장법). When a hit-and-run driver cannot be identified, the government's guarantee fund compensates victims for personal injuries up to statutory limits. The fund is financed by contributions from insurance companies and recovers costs from identified hit-and-run drivers through subrogation.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Hit-and-run offenses in the United States are governed primarily by state law, with each of the fifty states maintaining its own statute. There is no general federal hit-and-run statute, though federal law applies on federal property under the Assimilative Crimes Act (18 U.S.C. Section 13).

### State Penalty Comparison

| State | Property Damage | Injury | Death |
|-------|----------------|--------|-------|
| California (Veh. Code §§ 20001-20004) | Misdemeanor: up to 6 months, $1,000 fine | Felony: 16 months to 3 years state prison | Felony: 2-4 years state prison |
| New York (VTL §§ 600-601) | Class B misdemeanor: up to 90 days | Class E felony: up to 4 years | Class D felony: up to 7 years |
| Texas (Transp. Code § 550.021) | Class B misdemeanor: up to 180 days | 3rd degree felony: 2-10 years | 2nd degree felony: 2-20 years |
| Florida (§ 316.061-062) | 2nd degree misdemeanor: up to 60 days | 3rd/2nd degree felony: up to 15 years | 1st degree felony: up to 30 years |
| Illinois (625 ILCS 5/11-401) | Class A misdemeanor: up to 1 year | Class 4 felony: 1-3 years | Class 1 felony: 4-15 years |

### Constitutional Considerations

Hit-and-run statutes have survived Fifth Amendment challenges. In California v. Byers, 402 U.S. 424 (1971), the Supreme Court upheld the constitutionality of California's reporting requirement, rejecting the argument that requiring a driver to stop and identify themselves at an accident scene violates the privilege against self-incrimination. The plurality reasoned that the reporting requirement is directed at the general public, serves a regulatory purpose, and does not target a group inherently suspect of criminal activity.

### Aaron's Law and Related Reforms

Several states have enacted enhanced hit-and-run penalties following high-profile cases. Colorado's "Aaron's Law" (2014), named after hit-and-run victim Aaron Whitehead, increased the maximum penalty for vehicular hit-and-run causing death from six to twelve years. Washington State's "Cooper Jones Act" established enhanced penalties and mandatory felony prosecution for hit-and-run causing death.

### Uninsured Motorist Coverage

In the absence of a national guarantee fund, U.S. law addresses hit-and-run victim compensation primarily through uninsured/underinsured motorist (UM/UIM) coverage. Most states require or encourage UM coverage as part of standard automobile insurance policies. This provides compensation to hit-and-run victims through their own insurance policies when the at-fault driver cannot be identified. However, this system leaves uninsured victims without direct compensation from a public fund.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's hit-and-run provision, Section 142 StGB (Unerlaubtes Entfernen vom Unfallort), is distinctive in several respects. The statute imposes obligations on all persons "involved" (beteiligt) in a traffic accident, regardless of fault, and defines involvement broadly to include any person whose behavior may have contributed to the accident.

**Section 142(1) StGB:** A person involved in an accident in road traffic who leaves the scene of the accident before having enabled the identification of their person, their vehicle, and the nature of their involvement is punished with imprisonment for not more than three years or a fine.

**Section 142(2):** If the person involved in the accident waited at the scene for a reasonable period without anyone being prepared to ascertain the required information, they must report the accident immediately to the nearest police station and provide identification information. The waiting period must be appropriate to the circumstances; case law generally requires 15-30 minutes for minor accidents and longer for serious incidents.

### Penalty Structure and License Consequences

| Offense | Penalty | License Consequence |
|---------|---------|---------------------|
| Basic offense (Section 142(1)) | Fine or up to 3 years imprisonment | License suspension (Section 69 StGB) |
| Failure to report after waiting (Section 142(2)) | Fine or up to 3 years imprisonment | License suspension |
| Active repentance (Section 142(4)) | Court may mitigate or waive punishment | Possible mitigation of suspension |
| In conjunction with negligent homicide (Section 222) | Combined sentencing | Mandatory license revocation |

### Active Repentance (Tätige Reue)

Section 142(4) provides a unique "active repentance" provision: if the person involved in the accident voluntarily and within 24 hours after the accident reports their participation to a person entitled to ascertain it (typically police), the court may mitigate the punishment or dispense with it entirely. However, this provision applies only when the accident resulted in property damage or minor personal injury -- not when the accident caused death or significant personal injury.

### Bundesgerichtshof Jurisprudence

The BGH has addressed numerous interpretive questions:

**BGH 4 StR 78/08:** The court held that "leaving the scene" requires a voluntary departure; a driver who is taken from the scene by ambulance due to their own injuries does not violate Section 142, though they remain obligated to report the accident as soon as they are able.

**BGH 4 StR 230/17:** The court clarified the scope of "involvement," holding that a parked vehicle whose position contributed to an accident is "involved" even if the driver was not present at the time, creating a subsequent obligation to report upon learning of the accident.

### Verkehrsopferhilfe e.V.

Germany's Traffic Victims' Aid Association (Verkehrsopferhilfe e.V.) serves as the guarantee fund for victims of unidentified or uninsured drivers. Funded by contributions from all motor vehicle insurers operating in Germany, the association compensates victims for personal injuries and, in limited circumstances, property damage when the responsible driver cannot be identified following a hit-and-run incident.

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR has addressed hit-and-run incidents primarily through the positive obligations doctrine under Article 2 (right to life) and the procedural obligation to conduct effective investigations.

**Anna Todorova v. Bulgaria (Application No. 23302/03, 2009):** The Court found a procedural violation of Article 2 where Bulgarian authorities failed to conduct an effective investigation into a fatal hit-and-run incident. The investigation was characterized by significant delays, failure to secure forensic evidence at the scene, and ultimately resulted in the perpetrator's impunity. The Court emphasized that the procedural obligation under Article 2 requires states to put in place an effective judicial system capable of establishing the facts, holding accountable those at fault, and providing appropriate redress.

**Smiljanić v. Croatia (Application No. 35983/14, 2021):** The Court found a violation of Article 2 in its procedural aspect, holding that Croatian authorities' investigation into a fatal hit-and-run was ineffective due to failures to collect and preserve evidence, excessive delays, and the absence of meaningful participation by the victim's family in the proceedings.

### Vienna Convention on Road Traffic (1968)

The Vienna Convention on Road Traffic, adopted on November 8, 1968, and entering into force on May 21, 1977, establishes international standards for road traffic conduct. Article 31 requires drivers involved in road traffic accidents to stop, and if there are injured persons, to call for assistance and render aid. Article 31 also requires drivers to identify themselves to other parties involved in the accident.

### EU Motor Insurance Directives

The EU has addressed hit-and-run victim compensation primarily through the Motor Insurance Directive framework:

**Directive 2009/103/EC (Codified Motor Insurance Directive):** Article 10 requires each member state to establish or authorize a body responsible for providing compensation for damage to property or personal injuries caused by an unidentified vehicle or a vehicle for which the insurance obligation has not been satisfied. This guarantee fund mechanism ensures that hit-and-run victims receive compensation regardless of whether the responsible driver is identified.

**Directive 2021/2118 (Amending Directive):** This amendment modernized the motor insurance framework, addressing issues including the minimum amounts of coverage, cross-border claims procedures, and the scope of the guarantee fund obligation. Member states were required to transpose the directive by December 23, 2023.

**CJEU Jurisprudence:** In Vnuk v. Zavarovalnica Triglav (Case C-162/13, 2014), the Court of Justice of the European Union interpreted the Motor Insurance Directive broadly, holding that it applies to any use of a vehicle consistent with its normal function, including incidents on private land. This expansive interpretation has implications for the scope of guarantee fund coverage in hit-and-run cases.

### Cross-Border Hit-and-Run Claims

Regulation (EC) No 44/2001 (Brussels I, now Regulation 1215/2012) and the Fourth Motor Insurance Directive (Directive 2000/26/EC) established mechanisms for cross-border claims. When a hit-and-run occurs in one member state involving a victim from another, the victim can file a claim with the guarantee fund in the country of the accident. The Green Card system further facilitates international motor insurance recognition.

## Comparative Analysis

### Severity of Penalties

South Korea imposes by far the most severe penalties for hit-and-run offenses, with life imprisonment possible when a victim dies due to the driver's failure to render aid. This reflects a distinct legal philosophy treating the flight itself -- and the consequent denial of potentially life-saving assistance -- as nearly equivalent to intentional homicide.

Germany, by contrast, maintains a maximum of three years' imprisonment for the hit-and-run offense itself, treating the flight as a separate offense from the underlying traffic violation. Where death results, the driver faces combined sentencing for hit-and-run and negligent homicide (Section 222 StGB), which can result in significantly longer imprisonment.

U.S. penalties vary enormously by state, ranging from Florida's maximum of 30 years for felony hit-and-run causing death to states with maximums of 5-7 years.

### Scope of Obligation

Germany's approach is the broadest, imposing scene-remaining obligations on all persons "involved" in an accident regardless of fault. South Korea and the United States generally limit the obligation to drivers whose conduct caused or contributed to the accident. This distinction matters in cases involving multi-vehicle accidents or accidents where fault is disputed.

### Victim Compensation

The EU guarantee fund system provides the most comprehensive victim compensation framework, requiring all member states to maintain publicly accessible funds for hit-and-run victims. Germany's Verkehrsopferhilfe operates within this framework. South Korea maintains a similar government guarantee fund. The United States relies on private insurance (uninsured motorist coverage), leaving gaps for uninsured victims.
