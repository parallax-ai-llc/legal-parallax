## Summary

Squatting laws -- the legal provisions governing the unauthorized occupation of land or buildings without the owner's permission, and the circumstances under which squatters may acquire legal rights to property through adverse possession or similar doctrines -- represent a fundamental tension in property law between the protection of ownership rights and the social function of property, with particular relevance in jurisdictions facing housing crises, abandoned building proliferation, or inadequate social housing. South Korea addresses unauthorized occupation through the Civil Act (민법) provisions on possession (점유) and acquisitive prescription (취득시효, Articles 245-246), which permit a possessor who continuously, peacefully, and openly occupies another's real property for 20 years (or 10 years with good faith and just cause) to acquire ownership through registration; criminal trespass is addressed under Article 319 of the Criminal Act (형법), punishing unauthorized entry into another's building or fenced property with up to 3 years imprisonment, but Korean law does not specifically criminalize squatting as a distinct offense. The United States addresses squatting through state-level adverse possession statutes requiring continuous, open, notorious, hostile, and exclusive possession for statutory periods ranging from 5 to 20 years depending on the state, while criminal trespass is prosecuted under state penal codes; the post-2008 foreclosure crisis generated significant legal controversy around squatting in vacant bank-owned properties, and several states have enacted expedited removal statutes to address so-called "professional squatters." Germany treats unauthorized occupation primarily as a civil matter under the BGB's possession and ownership provisions (Sections 854-872, 985-1007), with criminal liability for Hausfriedensbruch (trespass) under Section 123 StGB punishable by up to one year imprisonment; Germany does not recognize adverse possession of registered land (Ersitzung applies only to movables under Section 937 BGB), and the Grundbuch (land register) system provides virtually absolute protection of registered ownership; Berlin's history of Hausbesetzung (house squatting) in the post-reunification era led to unique political and legal responses including selective legalization of established squats. At the European level, the ECHR protects property rights under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 while also recognizing that states have positive obligations to ensure adequate housing under Article 8, creating a potential tension between owner and occupier rights; the European Court of Human Rights addressed adverse possession in J.A. Pye (Oxford) Ltd v. United Kingdom (2007), holding that England's adverse possession regime did not violate Protocol 1, and has considered the proportionality of eviction orders affecting long-term occupants in multiple Article 8 cases.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's approach to squatting operates through the interaction of civil possession law and criminal trespass provisions.

**Civil Act (민법) -- Acquisitive Prescription (취득시효):**

- **Article 245(1):** A person who possesses another's real property peacefully, openly, and continuously for 20 years with the intent to possess as owner may acquire ownership by completing registration.
- **Article 245(2):** A person who possesses another's real property peacefully, openly, and continuously for 10 years with good faith and without negligence regarding the belief of ownership may acquire ownership by completing registration.
- **Article 246:** Acquisitive prescription of movable property requires 10 years of peaceful, open possession (5 years with good faith).
- **Article 197:** A possessor is presumed to possess peacefully, openly, and with the intent to possess as owner, shifting the burden of proof to the party challenging the presumption.

**Criminal Act (형법) Article 319 -- Trespass (주거침입):** A person who enters another's residence, building, ship, aircraft, or fenced property without permission, or refuses to leave when requested, faces imprisonment of up to 3 years or a fine of up to KRW 5 million. The Supreme Court has interpreted "residence" broadly to include any space where a person's private life is conducted, including commercial premises and construction sites.

**Supreme Court Jurisprudence on Acquisitive Prescription:** The Supreme Court has established several important principles:

- **Supreme Court Decision 96다14036 (1996):** Clarified that acquisitive prescription requires actual continuous physical possession, not merely legal or constructive possession.
- **Supreme Court Decision 2009다40462 (2009):** Held that possession through an unauthorized building on another's land may satisfy the requirements for acquisitive prescription if all other conditions are met.
- **Supreme Court Decision 2017다224127 (2018):** Confirmed that state-owned land (국유지) may be subject to acquisitive prescription claims by private possessors under certain conditions.

### Practical Context

Urban squatting in the contemporary Korean context typically involves unauthorized occupation of abandoned buildings, construction site dormitories, or vacant government-owned land. The rapid urbanization of the 1960s-1980s created extensive informal settlements (판자촌, shanty towns) that were gradually formalized or demolished. The Yongsan Tragedy (용산 참사) of January 20, 2009, in which police forcibly evicted unauthorized occupants from a commercial building slated for redevelopment, resulting in 6 deaths, highlighted the dangerous intersection of eviction enforcement, urban redevelopment, and housing rights in Korea.

### Housing Protections

The Housing Lease Protection Act (주택임대차보호법) provides significant protections for residential tenants but does not extend to unauthorized occupants. The Act establishes minimum lease terms, security deposit protections (전세금 보호), and priority claims in insolvency, but these protections are predicated on a valid lease agreement.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Adverse possession in the United States is governed by state statutes and common law, with significant variation in requirements and time periods.

**Common Elements:** Most states require five elements for adverse possession: (1) actual physical possession, (2) open and notorious occupation, (3) hostile/adverse to the true owner's interest, (4) exclusive possession (not shared with the owner or public), and (5) continuous possession for the statutory period. Some states additionally require payment of property taxes during the possession period.

**State Variation:**

| State | Statutory Period | Additional Requirements |
|-------|-----------------|------------------------|
| California | 5 years | Tax payment required (CCP 325) |
| Texas | 3-25 years | Depends on type of claim |
| New York | 10 years | RPAPL Article 5 |
| Florida | 7 years | Color of title required for shortened period |
| New Jersey | 30 years | Longest statutory period |

**Criminal Trespass:** State penal codes criminalize unauthorized entry and occupancy. Penalties range from infractions to felonies depending on the circumstances. Florida's SB 1436 (2024) created an expedited process for removing squatters, allowing property owners to request law enforcement removal with sworn affidavit evidence of unauthorized occupancy, bypassing the standard eviction process.

**Post-Foreclosure Squatting:** The 2008 financial crisis and subsequent foreclosure wave created approximately 8 million vacant bank-owned properties, many of which attracted squatters. The phenomenon generated new legal responses including:

- **Florida:** Enacted the "squatter removal" statute allowing property owners to file a verified complaint for expedited removal.
- **Georgia:** SB 196 (2023) created criminal penalties for fraudulent claims of ownership or lease rights to occupy another's property.
- **California:** AB 1779 (2024) enhanced penalties for squatters who commit property damage.

### Landmark Cases

**Nome 2000 v. Fagerstrom, 799 P.2d 304 (Alaska 1990):** The Alaska Supreme Court upheld an adverse possession claim based on seasonal use of rural land, establishing that the character of possession required is determined by the nature of the property and the uses to which it is ordinarily put.

**Tioga Coal Co. v. Supermarkets General Corp., 519 A.2d 992 (Pa. Super. 1987):** The court held that a squatter's subjective belief about ownership is irrelevant; what matters is that the occupation is hostile to the true owner's rights.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's legal treatment of squatting is fundamentally shaped by the Grundbuch (land register) system, which provides near-absolute protection of registered property rights.

**No Adverse Possession of Registered Land:** Unlike common law systems, German law does not recognize adverse possession (Ersitzung) of registered real property. BGB Section 937 limits Ersitzung to movable property (requiring 10 years of good-faith possession). The Grundbuch system, established by the Grundbuchordnung (GBO), creates a presumption of correctness (Section 891 BGB) that cannot be overcome by mere physical possession, regardless of duration.

**BGB Section 985 -- Vindication Claim (Herausgabeanspruch):** The registered owner may demand return of property from any possessor at any time. This right does not expire through passage of time (unlike the common law, where adverse possession extinguishes the owner's title).

**Section 123 StGB -- Hausfriedensbruch (Trespass):** Unauthorized entry into another's residence, business premises, or other enclosed property, or remaining after being asked to leave, is punishable by imprisonment up to one year or a fine. Section 124 StGB addresses aggravated trespass by groups. Hausfriedensbruch is an Antragsdelikt (complaint offense), meaning prosecution typically requires the victim to file a criminal complaint.

**Räumungsklage (Eviction Action):** Property owners must obtain a court order through civil proceedings to lawfully evict unauthorized occupants. Self-help eviction is generally prohibited except under the narrow conditions of BGB Section 229 (Selbsthilfe), which permits reasonable self-help to prevent irreparable harm when state assistance is not available in time. The eviction process can take 3-12 months depending on the court's caseload.

### Berlin Hausbesetzung (House Squatting)

Berlin's post-reunification squatting movement represents the most significant squatting phenomenon in modern German history. Following reunification in 1990, hundreds of vacant and decaying buildings in East Berlin, particularly in Friedrichshain, Prenzlauer Berg, and Mitte, were occupied by squatters. At its peak, approximately 130 buildings were occupied. The Berlin Senate adopted a pragmatic approach, negotiating contracts (Verträge) with many squatter collectives, offering lease agreements or purchase options at below-market rates. As of 2025, several former squats continue to operate as legal housing cooperatives or cultural centers, including Rigaer Straße 94, Liebigstraße 34 (evicted in 2020), and Köpi (evicted in 2021).

**Rigaer Straße 94:** This building in Friedrichshain has been the subject of prolonged legal and political conflict. Multiple eviction attempts have been met with significant resistance and police operations. In 2021, a partial eviction was carried out under police protection, but portions of the building remain occupied under disputed legal status.

### Constitutional Dimensions

Article 14 GG protects property rights while also establishing that property entails social obligations (Sozialbindung des Eigentums). The BVerfG has held that this social obligation may require property owners to tolerate certain limitations on their exclusive use rights, particularly when housing social functions are at stake. However, the Court has not extended this principle to protect unauthorized occupants against eviction.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR -- Article 1 Protocol 1 (Property)

**J.A. Pye (Oxford) Ltd v. United Kingdom (Application No. 44302/02, Grand Chamber Judgment of August 30, 2007):** The Grand Chamber held by 10 votes to 7 that England's adverse possession regime under the Limitation Act 1980 did not violate Article 1 Protocol 1. The applicants lost title to 23 hectares of agricultural land through adverse possession after 12 years. The Grand Chamber found that the adverse possession regime served a legitimate aim in the public interest (legal certainty, preventing stale claims, facilitating land transactions) and fell within the state's wide margin of appreciation in regulating property rights.

**Depalle v. France (Application No. 34044/02, Grand Chamber Judgment of March 29, 2010):** The Grand Chamber addressed the demolition of a house built on public maritime domain, holding that even without formal property rights, the applicant's long-standing occupation created a "possession" within the meaning of Protocol 1, though the interference was justified.

### ECHR -- Article 8 (Housing Rights)

**Connors v. United Kingdom (Application No. 66746/01, Judgment of May 27, 2004):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where UK authorities evicted a Gypsy family from a local authority caravan site without adequate procedural safeguards. The judgment established that any person at risk of losing their home should be entitled to have the proportionality of the eviction reviewed by an independent tribunal.

**McCann v. United Kingdom (Application No. 19009/04, Judgment of May 13, 2008):** The Court held that Article 8 requires that any eviction from a person's home be subject to proportionality review by an independent tribunal, regardless of whether the occupant has formal property rights.

**Yordanova and Others v. Bulgaria (Application No. 25446/06, Judgment of April 24, 2012):** The Court found that the eviction of Roma families from an informal settlement without considering their vulnerability and the availability of alternative accommodation would violate Article 8.

### EU Charter of Fundamental Rights

Article 7 of the Charter (respect for private and family life) and Article 34(3) (right to social and housing assistance) provide additional protection for persons facing eviction within the scope of EU law. The CJEU has addressed eviction-related issues in the context of consumer protection, notably in mortgage enforcement cases.

### Comparative National Approaches

Several EU member states have enacted specific anti-squatting legislation:
- **England/Wales:** Legal Aid, Sentencing and Punishment of Offenders Act 2012 (LASPO), Section 144, criminalized residential squatting with penalties of up to 6 months imprisonment and/or an unlimited fine.
- **Netherlands:** The Wet kraken en leegstand (Squatting and Vacancy Act, 2010) criminalized squatting, reversing decades of legal tolerance.
- **Spain:** Article 245 of the Código Penal criminalizes usurpación (occupation of another's property), though enforcement is complicated by Article 47 of the Spanish Constitution recognizing the right to adequate housing.

## Comparative Analysis

### Property Registration Systems

The most fundamental difference lies in land registration. Germany's Grundbuch system makes adverse possession of registered land impossible, providing maximum certainty for registered owners. South Korea and the United States both permit acquisitive prescription/adverse possession, reflecting systems where title certainty historically depended on physical possession and use rather than comprehensive registration.

### Criminal vs. Civil Treatment

All four jurisdictions criminalize unauthorized entry (trespass), but the emphasis differs. Germany treats squatting primarily as a civil matter requiring court-ordered eviction. The United States has been moving toward stronger criminal treatment through expedited removal statutes. South Korea's criminal provisions are rarely applied to squatting situations, with civil remedies preferred.

### Housing Rights Balance

The ECHR's Article 8 jurisprudence creates a unique balancing requirement between property rights and housing needs that does not exist in the national legal systems to the same degree. The McCann and Yordanova principles require proportionality review of evictions affecting long-term occupants, potentially limiting the absolute property rights recognized in domestic law.

### Cultural and Historical Context

Berlin's Hausbesetzung movement and its pragmatic resolution through negotiated legalization represents a unique approach to squatting governance. South Korea's history of informal settlements during industrialization and the Yongsan Tragedy illustrate the dangers of forcible eviction. The United States' post-2008 squatting phenomenon highlights how economic crises can challenge conventional property law frameworks.
