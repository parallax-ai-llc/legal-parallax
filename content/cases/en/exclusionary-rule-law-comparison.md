## Summary

The exclusionary rule -- the principle that evidence obtained in violation of constitutional or statutory rights must be excluded from use in criminal proceedings -- serves as the primary enforcement mechanism for constitutional protections against unlawful state action in criminal investigations, though its scope, rationale, and application vary dramatically across legal systems. South Korea has adopted an increasingly robust exclusionary rule through Article 308-2 of the Criminal Procedure Act (형사소송법 제308조의2), enacted in the 2007 reform, which provides that "evidence that was collected in violation of due process shall not be admitted as evidence," with the Supreme Court developing the rule through a series of landmark decisions including the 2007 Decision 2007도3061 establishing that illegally obtained evidence and its derivative evidence (fruits of the poisonous tree) may be excluded; the Korean approach represents a deliberate move away from the historical practice of admitting virtually all relevant evidence regardless of the manner of collection. The United States possesses the most developed exclusionary rule jurisprudence in the world, originating in Weeks v. United States (1914) for federal proceedings and extended to the states through Mapp v. Ohio, 367 U.S. 643 (1961), with the Supreme Court subsequently creating significant exceptions including the good faith exception (United States v. Leon, 1984), the inevitable discovery doctrine (Nix v. Williams, 1984), the independent source doctrine (Murray v. United States, 1988), and the attenuation doctrine (Utah v. Strieff, 2016). Germany applies a differentiated approach through the Beweisverwertungsverbot (evidence use prohibition) doctrine, distinguishing between absolute prohibitions on evidence obtained through violations of core constitutional values (such as torture or violations of the Kernbereich privater Lebensgestaltung, the core area of private life) and relative prohibitions subject to a proportionality balancing test weighing the severity of the procedural violation against the seriousness of the offense and the importance of the evidence; Germany does not apply a blanket exclusionary rule but rather conducts case-by-case analysis based on constitutional and statutory principles. The ECHR has declined to adopt a categorical exclusionary rule, instead assessing under Article 6 whether the admission of improperly obtained evidence renders the overall proceedings unfair, as established in Schenk v. Switzerland (1988) and refined through subsequent Grand Chamber decisions including Gäfgen v. Germany (2010) and Bykov v. Russia (2009), while EU Directive 2016/343 on the presumption of innocence establishes minimum standards for the treatment of evidence obtained in violation of procedural rights.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

**Criminal Procedure Act Article 308-2 (위법수집증거 배제규칙):** "Evidence that was collected in violation of due process of law shall not be admitted as evidence." This provision, enacted in the 2007 reform of the Criminal Procedure Act, codified the exclusionary rule for the first time in Korean statutory law.

**Article 309 (Inadmissibility of Confessions):** "A confession of the defendant which is suspected to have been obtained by means of torture, violence, threat, unduly prolonged detention, deception, or other methods that are suspected of undermining the voluntariness of the confession shall not be admitted as evidence."

### Supreme Court Development

**Supreme Court Decision 2007도3061 (2007):** The leading case on the scope of Article 308-2. The court established a two-part framework:

1. **Primary exclusion:** Evidence obtained in direct violation of due process is excluded.
2. **Derivative evidence (fruits of the poisonous tree):** Evidence derived from the primary illegally obtained evidence is also excludable, but the court applies a totality-of-circumstances analysis rather than automatic exclusion. Factors include the degree of the constitutional violation, the proximity between the violation and the derivative evidence, the existence of intervening circumstances, and the importance of the evidence to the prosecution.

**Supreme Court Decision 2009도10092 (2011):** The court applied the exclusionary rule to exclude a confession obtained after an illegally extended period of detention. The court held that even where the confession appeared voluntary on its face, the underlying illegality of the detention tainted the voluntariness of the statement.

**Supreme Court Decision 2015도14483 (2016):** The court addressed the exclusionary rule in the context of digital evidence, holding that evidence obtained through a digital search that exceeded the scope authorized by the warrant must be excluded, along with any derivative evidence obtained as a result of reviewing the improperly seized data.

### Balancing Factors

The Korean Supreme Court has identified the following factors for determining whether illegally obtained evidence should be excluded:

| Factor | Consideration |
|--------|--------------|
| Seriousness of constitutional violation | More serious violations more likely to result in exclusion |
| Intentionality of the violation | Deliberate violations treated more severely than inadvertent ones |
| Availability of alternative evidence | Exclusion more likely when other evidence exists |
| Seriousness of the underlying offense | Courts may be more reluctant to exclude in serious crime cases |
| Impact on the fairness of the trial | Central consideration in all cases |
| Deterrent effect on future police conduct | Relevant but not primary rationale |

### Ongoing Debate

The Korean legal community continues to debate the appropriate scope of the exclusionary rule. Some scholars advocate for a stronger, more automatic rule modeled on the U.S. approach, arguing that the current balancing test allows courts to admit illegally obtained evidence in serious cases, undermining the deterrent purpose. Others argue that the balancing approach is more appropriate for the Korean context, where the criminal justice system is still transitioning from its historical reliance on confessions.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The U.S. exclusionary rule is a judicially created remedy, not an explicitly constitutional provision.

### Foundational Cases

| Case | Year | Holding |
|------|------|---------|
| Weeks v. United States | 1914 | Evidence obtained by federal agents in violation of Fourth Amendment excluded from federal trials |
| Silverthorne Lumber Co. v. United States | 1920 | Derivative evidence ("fruit of the poisonous tree") also excluded |
| Mapp v. Ohio | 1961 | Exclusionary rule incorporated against states through Fourteenth Amendment |
| Wong Sun v. United States | 1963 | Fruit of the poisonous tree doctrine elaborated |

### Exceptions to the Exclusionary Rule

| Exception | Leading Case | Application |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| Good faith | United States v. Leon (1984) | Evidence obtained in reasonable reliance on facially valid warrant |
| Independent source | Murray v. United States (1988) | Evidence discovered through genuinely independent lawful means |
| Inevitable discovery | Nix v. Williams (1984) | Evidence that would inevitably have been discovered through lawful means |
| Attenuation | Utah v. Strieff (2016) | Sufficient intervening circumstances between illegality and discovery |
| Impeachment | Harris v. New York (1971) | Illegally obtained statements admissible to impeach defendant's testimony |

### Davis v. United States (2011)

The Supreme Court held that the exclusionary rule does not apply when police reasonably rely on binding appellate precedent that is subsequently overruled. This decision extended the good faith exception beyond reliance on warrants to reliance on judicial decisions, further narrowing the rule's scope.

### Utah v. Strieff (2016)

The Court held that the discovery of a pre-existing arrest warrant during an unconstitutional stop attenuated the connection between the illegal stop and the evidence subsequently found in a search incident to arrest. Justice Sotomayor's vigorous dissent warned that the decision effectively incentivized unconstitutional stops by allowing police to benefit from outstanding warrants discovered during such stops.

### Deterrence Rationale

Since Hudson v. Michigan, 547 U.S. 586 (2006), the Court has emphasized that the sole purpose of the exclusionary rule is deterrence of police misconduct. This shift from earlier rationales (judicial integrity, constitutional compulsion) has enabled the progressive narrowing of the rule, as the Court has found deterrence insufficient to justify exclusion in an expanding range of circumstances.

### Criticisms and Alternatives

The exclusionary rule faces persistent criticism. Conservative jurists argue it imposes excessive costs by freeing guilty defendants. Liberal jurists argue the many exceptions have rendered the rule ineffective. Proposed alternatives include: administrative sanctions against officers, civil liability under 42 U.S.C. Section 1983, and enhanced internal police disciplinary mechanisms. However, empirical research suggests these alternatives are insufficient as standalone deterrents.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany does not have a single statutory exclusionary rule. Instead, the Beweisverwertungsverbot (evidence use prohibition) doctrine operates through a complex system of constitutional, statutory, and judicially created rules.

### Categories of Evidence Exclusion

| Category | Standard | Examples |
|----------|----------|---------|
| **Absolute prohibition (Absolutes Beweisverwertungsverbot)** | Evidence always excluded regardless of circumstances | Statements obtained through torture (§ 136a StPO); violations of Kernbereich privater Lebensgestaltung |
| **Statutory prohibition** | Exclusion mandated by specific statute | § 136a StPO violations; certain telecommunications surveillance violations |
| **Dependent prohibition (Unselbständiges Beweisverwertungsverbot)** | Exclusion follows from violation of an evidence-gathering rule | Warrantless search violations; interrogation without notification of rights |
| **Independent prohibition (Selbständiges Beweisverwertungsverbot)** | Derived from constitutional rights regardless of specific statutory rule | Right to informational self-determination; right to fair trial |

### The Balancing Approach (Abwägungslehre)

For cases falling outside absolute prohibitions, German courts apply a balancing test weighing:

| Factor Favoring Exclusion | Factor Favoring Admission |
|---------------------------|--------------------------|
| Severity of procedural violation | Seriousness of the offense |
| Deliberate police misconduct | Good faith of officers |
| Impact on defendant's core rights | Importance of evidence |
| Availability of alternative evidence | Public interest in prosecution |
| Deterrent effect needed | Minor nature of violation |

### Bundesgerichtshof Jurisprudence

**BGH 5 StR 174/14 (2015):** The court held that evidence obtained through an unlawful search of a vehicle, where police lacked probable cause and conducted the search without a warrant, must be excluded. The court applied the balancing test and found that the severity of the Fourth Amendment equivalent violation (Article 13 GG) outweighed the prosecution's interest in the evidence.

**BGH 1 StR 701/16 (2017):** The court addressed the fruits of the poisonous tree (Fernwirkung) doctrine in German law. While acknowledging that German law does not automatically exclude derivative evidence, the court held that where the primary violation was severe (here, an unlawful telecommunications interception), derivative evidence obtained as a direct consequence must also be excluded.

### The Kernbereich Doctrine

The most absolute form of exclusion in German law applies to evidence obtained from the Kernbereich privater Lebensgestaltung (core area of private life). This includes intimate communications between spouses, diary entries, and private conversations about deeply personal matters. Evidence touching the Kernbereich is subject to automatic exclusion regardless of the seriousness of the offense or the importance of the evidence, as established by the Bundesverfassungsgericht in BVerfGE 109, 279 (2004) and BVerfGE 120, 274 (2008).

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Article 6 -- Overall Fairness

The Court has consistently declined to adopt a categorical exclusionary rule, instead assessing whether the admission of improperly obtained evidence renders the overall proceedings unfair.

**Schenk v. Switzerland (Application No. 10862/84, 1988):** The Court held that Article 6 does not lay down rules on the admissibility of evidence, which is primarily a matter for regulation under national law. The Court's task is to ascertain whether the proceedings as a whole were fair, including the way evidence was obtained.

**Gäfgen v. Germany [GC] (Application No. 22978/05, 2010):** The Grand Chamber established a near-absolute exclusionary rule for evidence obtained through Article 3 violations. Where a real evidence lead was obtained through treatment amounting to torture or inhuman treatment, the admission of that evidence will almost always render the proceedings unfair, regardless of the probative value and regardless of whether the evidence was decisive for conviction.

**Khan v. United Kingdom (Application No. 35394/97, 2000):** The Court found no violation of Article 6 where covertly recorded evidence was admitted despite being obtained in violation of domestic law, because the applicant had the opportunity to challenge the authenticity and reliability of the evidence, and the recording was not the only evidence.

### EU Directive 2016/343

**Article 10 (Remedies):** "Member States shall ensure that in the assessment of statements made by suspects and accused persons or of evidence obtained in breach of their right to remain silent or their right not to incriminate themselves, the rights of the defence and the fairness of the proceedings are respected."

While the Directive does not mandate a specific exclusionary rule, it requires that evidence obtained in breach of the right to silence and the privilege against self-incrimination be subject to safeguards ensuring overall trial fairness.

### EU Directive 2013/48/EU

Article 12 requires member states to ensure that the rights of the defense and fairness of proceedings are respected when assessing statements made by suspects who did not have access to a lawyer. While not creating a categorical exclusionary rule, the provision establishes a minimum standard for the treatment of evidence obtained in violation of the right to counsel.

## Comparative Analysis

### Exclusionary Rule Strength

The jurisdictions can be ranked by the strength of their exclusionary rules:

1. **United States:** Strongest categorical rule, though significantly limited by exceptions.
2. **South Korea:** Statutory rule with developing case law; balancing approach.
3. **Germany:** No categorical rule; differentiated approach with absolute exclusion only for core area violations.
4. **ECHR:** No exclusionary rule; overall fairness assessment only.

### Rationale

The jurisdictions differ in their primary rationale for excluding evidence:

- **United States:** Deterrence of police misconduct (since Hudson).
- **South Korea:** Due process protection and judicial integrity.
- **Germany:** Protection of fundamental rights; proportionality.
- **ECHR:** Fair trial guarantee.

### Fruits of the Poisonous Tree

The derivative evidence (fruits) doctrine is strongest in the United States (Wong Sun), developing in South Korea (2007도3061), limited in Germany (Fernwirkung recognized but not automatic), and not independently applied by the ECHR (assessed under overall fairness).

### Practical Impact

The practical impact of exclusionary rules on conviction rates is debated across all jurisdictions. U.S. studies suggest that the exclusionary rule leads to the suppression of evidence in approximately 1-3 percent of cases, with a smaller proportion resulting in dismissals solely due to evidence suppression. German studies suggest that their Beweisverwertungsverbot has a similarly limited quantitative impact but serves an important signaling function for police conduct.
