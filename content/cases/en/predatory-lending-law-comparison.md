## Summary

Predatory lending -- the practice of imposing unfair, deceptive, or abusive loan terms on borrowers, often targeting financially vulnerable populations with high interest rates, excessive fees, and loan structures designed to trap borrowers in cycles of debt -- has been a persistent source of consumer harm and financial instability across legal systems, most dramatically illustrated by the 2008 global financial crisis triggered in part by predatory subprime mortgage practices in the United States. South Korea regulates predatory lending through a multi-layered framework including the Interest Limitation Act (이자제한법) capping maximum interest rates at 20 percent per annum (reduced from 24 percent in 2021), the Act on Registration of Credit Business and Protection of Finance Users (대부업 등의 등록 및 금융이용자 보호에 관한 법률) establishing licensing requirements and conduct standards for non-bank lenders (대부업자), and the Financial Consumer Protection Act (금융소비자 보호에 관한 법률, enacted 2020, effective 2021) introducing comprehensive suitability requirements and a right of withdrawal for financial products; the regulatory response was shaped by Korea's history of household debt crises and the social devastation caused by illegal private lending (사채). The United States addressed predatory lending through a patchwork of federal and state laws, with the Truth in Lending Act (TILA), Home Ownership and Equity Protection Act (HOEPA), and the comprehensive Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act of 2010 creating the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) with broad authority over mortgage lending, consumer credit, and non-bank financial companies; state-level anti-predatory lending laws such as North Carolina's pioneering 1999 statute and New York's subprime lending law supplement federal protections. Germany addresses predatory lending through the BGB's provisions on usury (Wucher, Section 138(2)) declaring loans with grossly disproportionate consideration void, the consumer credit provisions of BGB Sections 491-505 transposing the EU Consumer Credit Directive, and the banking supervision framework under the Kreditwesengesetz (KWG) and Finanzdienstleistungsaufsichtsgesetz (FinDAG) administered by BaFin; Germany's relatively low incidence of predatory lending reflects both strong regulatory oversight and the structure of its banking market, which is dominated by cooperative banks (Genossenschaftsbanken) and savings banks (Sparkassen). At the European level, the Consumer Credit Directive (now recast as Directive (EU) 2023/2225), the Mortgage Credit Directive 2014/17/EU, and the European Banking Authority's (EBA) Guidelines on Creditworthiness Assessment establish a harmonized framework for responsible lending, while the ECHR has addressed excessive interest rates under Article 1 of Protocol 1 (property) and the principle of proportionality.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's anti-predatory lending framework reflects the country's experience with devastating household debt crises and the social harms of illegal private lending.

**Interest Limitation Act (이자제한법):** Originally enacted in 1962 and reformed multiple times, the Act sets maximum interest rates for all loans. The current cap, effective July 7, 2021, is 20 percent per annum, reduced from the previous 24 percent cap. Interest charged above this rate is void and unenforceable. The cap applies to all types of loans, including those from registered credit businesses (대부업자) and private money lenders.

**Rate Cap History:**

| Period | Maximum Rate |
|--------|-------------|
| Before 2007 | 66% (de facto under Credit Business Act) |
| 2007-2010 | 49% |
| 2010-2014 | 44% (reduced incrementally) |
| 2014-2016 | 34.9% |
| 2016-2018 | 27.9% |
| 2018-2021 | 24% |
| 2021-present | 20% |

### Credit Business Act (대부업법)

The Act on Registration of Credit Business and Protection of Finance Users (대부업 등의 등록 및 금융이용자 보호에 관한 법률) establishes:
- **Registration Requirement:** Non-bank lenders must register with the relevant local government
- **Advertising Restrictions:** Prohibition on misleading advertising; mandatory disclosure of interest rates and fees
- **Prohibited Practices:** (1) Use of violence or intimidation in collection; (2) requiring access to borrower's wages or government benefits; (3) lending to minors without parental consent; (4) charging fees not disclosed at contract formation
- **Annual Reporting:** Registered lenders must file annual reports with financial authorities

### Financial Consumer Protection Act (금융소비자 보호에 관한 법률)

Enacted in March 2020 and effective March 25, 2021, this comprehensive statute introduces:
- **Suitability Principle (적합성 원칙):** Financial institutions must not recommend products unsuitable for the consumer's financial situation, risk tolerance, and needs
- **Adequacy Principle (적정성 원칙):** If a consumer seeks a product that is not suitable, the institution must inform the consumer of this fact
- **Prohibition on Unjust Solicitation:** Prohibition on providing false information, making unsubstantiated predictions of returns, or failing to disclose material risks
- **Right of Withdrawal (청약 철회권):** Consumers may withdraw from certain financial contracts within 15 to 30 days
- **Right to Request Termination (계약 해지 요구권):** Consumers may terminate continuing financial contracts that were entered into based on misleading information

### Illegal Private Lending (사채)

Despite the comprehensive regulatory framework, illegal private lending remains a significant problem in South Korea, particularly targeting the self-employed, migrants, and individuals with poor credit histories. Illegal lenders charge interest rates far above the statutory cap, often reaching 100-300 percent annualized. The police and prosecution authorities conduct regular crackdowns on illegal lending operations, with approximately 3,000-4,000 criminal cases prosecuted annually.

### Household Debt Crisis

South Korea's household debt-to-GDP ratio exceeded 100 percent in 2021, one of the highest in the OECD. The government has implemented multiple macroprudential measures including debt-to-income (DTI) and loan-to-value (LTV) ratio caps for mortgage lending, administered through the Financial Services Commission's lending guidelines.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States' anti-predatory lending framework was dramatically reshaped by the 2008 financial crisis, which exposed the devastating consequences of inadequate regulation of subprime mortgage lending.

### Pre-Crisis Framework

**Truth in Lending Act (TILA, 1968):** Requires lenders to disclose key terms including APR, total cost of credit, and payment schedule. Does not cap interest rates or prohibit specific loan terms.

**Home Ownership and Equity Protection Act (HOEPA, 1994):** Amends TILA to impose additional disclosure requirements and substantive protections for "high-cost" mortgages (triggered by APR or fee thresholds). Prohibits balloon payments, negative amortization, and certain prepayment penalties in high-cost mortgages.

**State Anti-Predatory Lending Laws:** North Carolina enacted the first state anti-predatory lending law in 1999 (N.C. Gen. Stat. §24-10.2), followed by dozens of states. These laws typically impose additional restrictions on high-cost loans beyond HOEPA, including rate caps, fee limits, and mandatory counseling.

### Dodd-Frank Act (2010)

The Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act (Pub. L. 111-203) was the most comprehensive financial regulatory reform since the Great Depression:

**Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB):**
- Created as an independent agency within the Federal Reserve System
- Broad authority to regulate consumer financial products and services
- Enforcement authority over banks, non-bank lenders, mortgage servicers, debt collectors, and others
- Authority to declare practices "unfair, deceptive, or abusive" (UDAAP)

**Ability-to-Repay (ATR) Rule (TILA Section 129C):**
- Lenders must make a reasonable, good-faith determination that the borrower has the ability to repay the mortgage
- Eight specific factors must be considered, including income, assets, debts, employment, and credit history
- Qualified Mortgage (QM) safe harbor: loans meeting specific criteria (points and fees ≤3% of loan amount; no negative amortization, interest-only, or balloon features; maximum 30-year term; DTI ratio ≤43% or meeting appendix Q standards) receive a presumption of ATR compliance

**Mortgage Originator Compensation Rules:**
- Prohibition on compensation based on loan terms (e.g., steering borrowers to higher-rate loans for higher commissions)
- Prohibition on dual compensation (originator cannot be compensated by both borrower and creditor)

### State Interest Rate Caps

Unlike most jurisdictions, the United States does not have a uniform federal interest rate cap for consumer loans. State usury laws vary dramatically:

| Category | Rate Range |
|----------|-----------|
| States with no usury cap for most loans | Delaware, Utah, South Dakota (due to bank charter arbitrage) |
| States with moderate caps | New York (16% civil usury; 25% criminal usury); California (10% for non-exempt loans) |
| Payday lending caps | 15 states + D.C. have effectively banned payday lending through rate caps ≤36% |
| Military Lending Act | 36% MAPR cap for active-duty servicemembers |

### National Bank Preemption

The ability of nationally chartered banks to "export" the interest rates of their chartering state to borrowers in other states (under Marquette National Bank v. First of Omaha Service Corp., 1978) has significantly undermined state usury laws. Banks chartered in states with no usury caps (e.g., Delaware, South Dakota) can lend at any rate nationwide, creating regulatory arbitrage.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's approach to predatory lending relies on a combination of civil law doctrines, consumer protection statutes, and banking supervision.

**BGB Section 138(2) -- Wucher (Usury):**
"A legal transaction is void in which a person, by exploiting the predicament, inexperience, lack of judgment or considerable weakness of will of another, causes himself or a third party, in exchange for an act of performance, to be promised or granted pecuniary advantages which are clearly disproportionate to the performance."

The BGH has established that an interest rate approximately double the market rate creates a rebuttable presumption of usury (BGH, XI ZR 274/17, 2019). The subjective element (exploitation of the borrower's vulnerability) must also be established, though it is presumed when the objective disproportion is extreme.

**StGB Section 291 -- Wucher (Criminal Usury):**
Criminal usury is punishable by imprisonment of up to three years or a fine. Aggravated cases (commercial or gang-related usury) carry a penalty of six months to ten years imprisonment.

### Consumer Credit Protection (BGB §§491-505)

The BGB's consumer credit provisions, implementing EU directives, provide:
- **Pre-contractual Information (§491a):** Comprehensive disclosure of APR, total cost, installment amounts, consequences of default
- **Creditworthiness Assessment (§505a-b):** The creditor must assess the consumer's creditworthiness before concluding the credit agreement, based on sufficient information obtained from the consumer and, where necessary, from relevant databases
- **Residential Real Estate Credit (§505a-e):** Enhanced protections for mortgage lending, implementing the Mortgage Credit Directive
- **Right of Withdrawal (§495):** 14-day right of withdrawal for consumer credit agreements

### BaFin Supervision

The Bundesanstalt für Finanzdienstleistungsaufsicht (BaFin) supervises all credit institutions and financial services providers. Non-bank lenders require authorization under the Kreditwesengesetz (KWG) if they engage in banking business (accepting deposits or granting loans). This contrasts with the lighter registration requirements in some other jurisdictions and significantly limits the scope of unregulated lending.

### Market Structure and Predatory Lending Risk

Germany's banking market structure reduces predatory lending risk:
- **Sparkassen (Savings Banks):** Publicly owned local banks with a mandate to serve all customers regardless of credit profile
- **Genossenschaftsbanken (Cooperative Banks):** Member-owned banks with community focus
- Together, Sparkassen and cooperative banks hold approximately 50% of all German banking assets

These institutions' community mandates and non-profit orientations reduce incentives for predatory lending compared to profit-driven private banks. The Schufa (credit bureau) system provides comprehensive credit information that enables responsible lending assessments.

### Schuldnerberatung (Debt Counseling)

Germany's extensive network of publicly funded debt counseling services (Schuldnerberatungsstellen) provides free advice to over-indebted consumers, including assistance with debt restructuring and insolvency proceedings. These services serve as a safety net for victims of predatory or irresponsible lending.

## ECHR & EU

### Consumer Credit Directive (EU) 2023/2225

The recast Consumer Credit Directive, adopted in October 2023 (replacing Directive 2008/48/EC), significantly strengthens anti-predatory lending protections:

**Creditworthiness Assessment (Article 18):** Creditors must carry out a thorough assessment of the consumer's creditworthiness before concluding a credit agreement. The assessment must take into account all relevant factors, including income, expenses, other debts, and the consumer's overall financial situation. The result must be "positive" -- credit may not be granted where the assessment indicates inability to repay.

**Adequate Explanations (Article 11):** Creditors must provide adequate explanations enabling the consumer to assess whether the credit agreement is adapted to their needs and financial situation.

**Right of Withdrawal (Article 26):** 14-day withdrawal period from the conclusion of the credit agreement.

**Total Cost of Credit (Article 3):** Comprehensive definition ensuring all costs are included in the APR calculation.

**Caps on Costs:** Article 31 requires member states to introduce interest rate or APR caps for consumer credit, or caps on the total cost of credit. This is a significant new requirement that mandates rate caps at the EU level for the first time.

### Mortgage Credit Directive 2014/17/EU

The MCD establishes the framework for residential mortgage lending:
- **Creditworthiness Assessment (Article 18):** Comprehensive assessment required; mortgage must not be granted where negative
- **Annual Percentage Rate of Charge (Article 17):** Standardized APR calculation for comparability
- **Adequate Explanations (Article 16):** Personalized information enabling informed borrowing decisions
- **Right of Reflection or Withdrawal (Article 14):** Either a 7-day reflection period before the offer becomes binding or a 7-day withdrawal period after conclusion
- **Mortgage Credit Intermediary Regulation (Articles 29-34):** Registration, conduct, and disclosure requirements

### European Banking Authority (EBA) Guidelines

The EBA's Guidelines on Loan Origination and Monitoring (EBA/GL/2020/06) establish detailed standards for:
- Collection and verification of borrower income information
- Assessment of borrower expenses and existing financial commitments
- Stress testing of borrower repayment capacity at higher interest rates
- Documentation and governance of credit decision processes

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Zolotas v. Greece (Application No. 66610/09, 2013):** The Court addressed compound interest on bank loans, holding that the application of anatocism (interest on interest) at rates that effectively made repayment impossible raised issues under Article 1 of Protocol 1, though the case was decided on specific facts.

The ECHR's general approach is that states have a wide margin of appreciation in regulating interest rates and lending practices, but must ensure that the regulatory framework provides effective protection against exploitative lending.

## Comparative Analysis

### Interest Rate Caps

The approach to interest rate caps varies dramatically. South Korea imposes a clear statutory cap (20%), which has been progressively reduced over two decades. The EU now mandates that member states introduce some form of rate or cost cap (CCD 2023/2225, Article 31). Germany relies on the case-by-case Wucher doctrine rather than a statutory cap, with the "approximately double the market rate" presumption providing de facto regulation. The United States lacks a federal cap and has significant state-level variation, with bank charter arbitrage effectively undermining state usury protections -- the most significant regulatory gap among the four jurisdictions.

### Regulatory Architecture

South Korea and the United States have both created centralized consumer financial protection agencies (Financial Consumer Protection Department within FSS; CFPB) to address predatory lending. Germany relies on BaFin's banking supervision combined with civil law protections. The EU coordinates through the EBA and national authorities. The U.S. CFPB model is the most comprehensive in combining rule-making, supervision, and enforcement authority in a single consumer-focused agency, though its authority has been politically contested.

### Market Structure

Germany's unique banking market structure -- dominated by community-oriented savings banks and cooperatives -- provides a structural defense against predatory lending that legal regulation alone cannot achieve. South Korea's banking market is more concentrated in large commercial banks, and the United States' market is the most diverse with significant non-bank lender participation. The degree of non-bank lender activity correlates with predatory lending risk, as non-bank lenders are often subject to lighter regulation.

### Affordability Assessment

All jurisdictions now require some form of affordability or creditworthiness assessment, but the rigor varies. The EU/Germany framework is most explicit in requiring that credit must not be granted where the assessment is negative. The U.S. ATR rule for mortgages is robust but does not extend to all consumer credit. South Korea's Financial Consumer Protection Act introduces suitability requirements but relies more on disclosure than on prohibition of unaffordable lending.
