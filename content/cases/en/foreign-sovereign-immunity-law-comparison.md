## Summary

Foreign sovereign immunity -- the doctrine that a sovereign state cannot be subjected to the jurisdiction of the courts of another state without its consent -- has evolved from an absolute principle grounded in the equality of states to a restrictive theory that distinguishes between sovereign acts (acta jure imperii) and commercial or private-law acts (acta jure gestionis), with modern practice permitting suits against foreign states for the latter category. South Korea applies the restrictive theory of sovereign immunity through judicial interpretation, with the Supreme Court (대법원) establishing in its landmark 1998 decision (대법원 1998. 12. 17. 선고 97다39216 판결) that foreign states do not enjoy immunity from Korean jurisdiction for acts of a commercial nature, while the unique context of claims against Japan for wartime forced labor and comfort women has pushed Korean courts to recognize a human rights exception to sovereign immunity in the controversial 2021 comfort women ruling. The United States codified the restrictive theory through the Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act of 1976 (FSIA, 28 U.S.C. Sections 1602-1611), which provides the sole basis for obtaining jurisdiction over foreign states in US courts and enumerates specific exceptions including commercial activity, expropriation, tortious acts in the United States, and state-sponsored terrorism. Germany has contributed foundationally to the development of sovereign immunity doctrine through its jurisprudence in the Italian war reparations cases and the ICJ's Jurisdictional Immunities of the State judgment (2012), while domestically applying the restrictive theory through the Gerichtsverfassungsgesetz (GVG) Sections 18-20 and general principles of international law under Article 25 GG. At the European level, the European Convention on State Immunity of 1972 (Basel Convention) provides a regional framework, the ICJ's 2012 judgment in Jurisdictional Immunities (Germany v. Italy) definitively upheld sovereign immunity even for serious violations of international humanitarian law, and the ECtHR has balanced sovereign immunity against Article 6 access-to-court rights in a series of landmark judgments.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea does not have a comprehensive statutory framework for foreign sovereign immunity analogous to the US FSIA. Instead, sovereign immunity is applied through judicial interpretation based on customary international law, incorporated into domestic law through Article 6(1) of the Constitution (헌법).

### Supreme Court 1998 Decision

The Supreme Court's decision of December 17, 1998 (대법원 1998. 12. 17. 선고 97다39216 판결) marked Korea's formal adoption of the restrictive theory of sovereign immunity. The Court held that foreign states enjoy immunity from Korean jurisdiction for acts of a sovereign nature (주권적 행위) but not for acts of a private or commercial nature (사법적/상업적 행위). The case involved a contract dispute with a foreign government entity, and the Court applied the distinction between acta jure imperii and acta jure gestionis consistently with international trends.

### Comfort Women Ruling (2021)

The Seoul Central District Court's decision of January 8, 2021, ordering the Japanese government to pay 100 million KRW each to twelve surviving former comfort women, was one of the most controversial sovereign immunity decisions in recent international legal history. The court held that Japan was not entitled to sovereign immunity because the acts in question -- systematic sexual slavery -- constituted violations of jus cogens (peremptory norms of international law) that could not be shielded by sovereign immunity. The court cited the Italian Ferrini line of cases as precedent.

However, the same court reached the opposite conclusion in a subsequent case (April 21, 2021), holding that sovereign immunity barred similar claims by other comfort women plaintiffs, creating an internal jurisprudential inconsistency. Japan refused to participate in the proceedings and rejected the January 2021 judgment.

### Forced Labor Cases

The Supreme Court's 2018 decisions ordering Japanese companies (Nippon Steel, Mitsubishi Heavy Industries) to compensate Korean forced labor victims raised related but distinct issues, as the defendants were private corporations rather than the Japanese state. However, the subsequent asset seizure proceedings against Japanese corporate assets in Korea have generated diplomatic tensions with implications for sovereign immunity doctrine.

| Principle | Korean Application |

| Human rights/jus cogens exception | Applied in January 2021 comfort women case; contradicted in April 2021 case |

## United States

### Legal Framework

The Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act of 1976 (FSIA, 28 U.S.C. Sections 1602-1611) provides the comprehensive statutory framework for foreign sovereign immunity in the United States. The FSIA is the "sole basis for obtaining jurisdiction over a foreign state in our courts" (Argentine Republic v. Amerada Hess Shipping Corp., 488 U.S. 428 (1989)).

### FSIA Exceptions

The FSIA provides several enumerated exceptions to sovereign immunity:

**Section 1605(a)(2) -- Commercial Activity:** The most frequently litigated exception. A foreign state is not immune where the action is based upon (1) a commercial activity carried on in the United States, (2) an act performed in the United States in connection with commercial activity elsewhere, or (3) an act outside the United States in connection with commercial activity elsewhere that causes a direct effect in the United States. The Supreme Court defined "commercial activity" in Republic of Argentina v. Weltover, Inc., 504 U.S. 607 (1992), as activity of the type that a private party could engage in.

**Section 1605(a)(3) -- Expropriation:** Exception for cases involving property taken in violation of international law.

**Section 1605(a)(5) -- Noncommercial Tort:** Exception for personal injury or death, or damage to property, occurring in the United States, caused by the tortious act of a foreign state. This exception does not apply to claims based on the exercise of a discretionary function.

**Section 1605A -- State-Sponsored Terrorism:** Added by the National Defense Authorization Act for FY 2008, this exception permits claims against designated state sponsors of terrorism for acts of torture, extrajudicial killing, aircraft sabotage, or hostage taking. It has been the basis for multi-billion-dollar default judgments against Iran.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany applies foreign sovereign immunity through the general rules of international law, which have supra-statutory force under Article 25 GG, and through the Gerichtsverfassungsgesetz (GVG) Sections 18-20, which exempt persons enjoying immunity under international law from German jurisdiction. Germany does not have a comprehensive statutory equivalent to the US FSIA but applies customary international law principles through judicial interpretation.

### ICJ Jurisdictional Immunities (2012)

Germany's most significant contribution to sovereign immunity jurisprudence arose from the Jurisdictional Immunities of the State case (Germany v. Italy: Greece Intervening, ICJ Judgment of February 3, 2012). The case originated from Italian court decisions (beginning with Corte di Cassazione, Ferrini v. Federal Republic of Germany, No. 5044/2004) that denied Germany sovereign immunity for civil claims arising from Nazi war crimes, including the Distomo massacre in Greece and the deportation of Italian military internees for forced labor.

The ICJ ruled 12-3 that Italy had violated Germany's sovereign immunity by allowing civil claims for WWII-era violations of international humanitarian law. The Court held that sovereign immunity was a procedural rule and could not be overridden by the substantive seriousness of the underlying violation, even where jus cogens norms were at issue. The Court explicitly rejected the argument that a jus cogens exception to sovereign immunity existed in customary international law.

### German Constitutional Court Response

Following the ICJ judgment, Italy's Constitutional Court (Corte Costituzionale, Judgment No. 238/2014) declared that the Italian law implementing the ICJ judgment was unconstitutional, reasserting Italian courts' jurisdiction over German WWII claims. This created a direct tension between ICJ authority and Italian constitutional law. Germany has not sought further enforcement of the ICJ judgment, and the situation remains unresolved.

| Principle | German Application |

| IHL violations exception | Rejected (ICJ 2012); Germany successfully defended immunity |

| Execution against state assets | Extremely restricted; BVerfG applies strict immunity for sovereign assets |

## ECHR & EU

### ECtHR Jurisprudence

The ECtHR has addressed the tension between sovereign immunity and Article 6(1) ECHR (right of access to a court) in multiple landmark decisions.

**Al-Adsani v. United Kingdom (Application No. 35763/97, Grand Chamber Judgment of November 21, 2001):** The Grand Chamber held, by nine votes to eight, that the UK's grant of sovereign immunity to Kuwait in a civil claim for torture did not violate Article 6(1). The Court recognized that the right of access to a court could be subject to limitations, including those imposed by sovereign immunity, provided the limitations pursue a legitimate aim and are proportionate. The narrow majority and strong dissents (arguing that jus cogens norms should override immunity) made this one of the most debated ECHR decisions.

**Jones and Others v. United Kingdom (Applications Nos. 34356/06 and 40528/06, Judgment of January 14, 2014):** The Court reaffirmed Al-Adsani, holding that UK courts' grant of sovereign immunity to Saudi Arabia and its officials in civil claims for torture did not violate Article 6(1). The Court noted the ICJ's 2012 Jurisdictional Immunities judgment and observed that the international law position had not evolved sufficiently to support a human rights exception to sovereign immunity.

**Cudak v. Lithuania and Sabeh El Leil v. France:** As discussed in the diplomatic immunity analysis, these employment-related cases established that blanket sovereign immunity in employment disputes may violate Article 6.

### International Frameworks

**UN Convention on Jurisdictional Immunities of States and Their Property (2004):** Adopted by the UN General Assembly on December 2, 2004, this Convention codifies the restrictive theory of sovereign immunity with enumerated exceptions (commercial transactions, employment contracts, personal injuries, property rights, IP, participation in companies, state-owned vessels, and arbitration agreements). The Convention has not yet entered into force (requiring 30 ratifications; as of 2026, approximately 22 states have ratified).

**European Convention on State Immunity (Basel Convention, 1972):** This Council of Europe convention, which entered into force in 1976, was one of the first multilateral treaties to codify the restrictive theory. It has been ratified by only eight states (Austria, Belgium, Cyprus, Germany, Luxembourg, Netherlands, Switzerland, and the United Kingdom).

### Enforcement Challenges

Enforcement of judgments against foreign states remains one of the most difficult aspects of sovereign immunity law. The FSIA Section 1610 provides limited exceptions to execution immunity. In practice, diplomatic property and central bank reserves enjoy near-absolute immunity from execution. The Terrorism Risk Insurance Act (TRIA) Section 201 provides a special enforcement mechanism for terrorism-related judgments against blocked assets.

## Comparative Analysis

### Statutory vs. Judicial Frameworks

The United States alone has a comprehensive statutory framework (FSIA). South Korea and Germany apply customary international law through judicial interpretation. The difference has practical consequences: the FSIA's enumerated exceptions provide greater predictability, while judicial approaches allow for case-by-case evolution -- as demonstrated by the Korean comfort women ruling's attempted recognition of a jus cogens exception.

### Human Rights Exception

The most contentious issue is whether serious human rights violations create an exception to sovereign immunity. The Korean January 2021 comfort women ruling recognized such an exception; the ICJ in 2012 rejected it; the ECtHR in Al-Adsani declined to require it. The Italian Constitutional Court's defiance of the ICJ judgment adds further complexity. The trend in international law remains against a human rights exception, but persistent advocacy suggests the issue is not settled.

### Terrorism Exception

Only the United States has enacted a specific terrorism exception to sovereign immunity (FSIA Section 1605A). This exception is unique to US law and has been criticized as politicized, as it applies only to designated state sponsors of terrorism. No other jurisdiction has adopted an equivalent provision.

### Enforcement

All jurisdictions face significant challenges in enforcing judgments against foreign state assets. The US provides the most developed execution framework under FSIA Section 1610, supplemented by TRIA for terrorism cases. South Korea, Germany, and European jurisdictions apply strict execution immunity for sovereign assets, making actual recovery extremely difficult.
