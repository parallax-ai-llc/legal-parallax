## Summary

The statute of limitations — the legal deadline after which prosecution or civil claims become time-barred — varies dramatically across jurisdictions, reflecting fundamentally different philosophies about justice, finality, and the rights of both victims and the accused. South Korea abolished the statute of limitations for murder in 2015 following public outrage over unsolved serial killings, while maintaining tiered limitation periods for other offenses under the Criminal Procedure Act (형사소송법). Germany eliminated its limitation period for murder in 1979 in direct response to the need to prosecute Nazi-era war criminals, and has since extended this exemption to genocide, crimes against humanity, and war crimes. The United States applies a default five-year federal limitation period under 18 U.S.C. § 3282 but imposes no time limit on capital offenses, while state-level rules vary enormously. The European Court of Human Rights does not prescribe specific limitation periods but has held that national statutes of limitations must not render the right to access courts or the right to life illusory under Articles 6 and 2 of the Convention. The EU's PIF Directive (2017/1371) represents a rare instance of harmonized limitation periods at the supranational level.

## South Korea

### Criminal Statute of Limitations (공소시효)

The Criminal Procedure Act (형사소송법) Articles 249 through 253 establish a tiered system of limitation periods based on the maximum penalty for each offense:

| Maximum Penalty | Limitation Period |
|----------------|-------------------|
| Death penalty | No limitation (abolished 2015) |
| Life imprisonment or life detention | 15 years |
| Imprisonment of 10+ years | 10 years |
| Imprisonment under 10 years | 7 years |
| Imprisonment under 5 years | 5 years |
| Occupational disqualification of 10+ years | 5 years |
| Occupational disqualification of 5+ years | 3 years |
| Minor fines, detention, or confiscation | 1 year |

A critical additional rule provides that crimes prosecuted more than 25 years after indictment are considered to have exceeded the statute of limitations.

### The 2015 Abolition for Murder

The National Assembly passed an amendment on July 24, 2015, abolishing the 25-year statute of limitations for first-degree murder in a near-unanimous vote. This followed a prior extension from 15 to 25 years in December 2007. The amendment was driven by sustained public demand, particularly in connection with the Hwaseong serial murders (화성 연쇄 살인 사건), in which at least 10 women were raped and killed between 1986 and 1991. The statute of limitations for the last of those crimes expired in April 2006.

**Non-retroactivity:** The revised law cannot be applied retroactively. In 2019, when DNA evidence identified Lee Chun-jae as the Hwaseong killer, he could not be prosecuted because the limitation period had already expired. In 2024, lawmakers introduced a special bill (Article 253-2) seeking to retroactively invalidate the expired limitations for the Hwaseong case, though this raises significant constitutional concerns.

### Suspension and Tolling

Under Article 253(3) of the Criminal Procedure Act, the statute of limitations is suspended when a perpetrator is abroad for the purpose of escaping criminal punishment. The limitation period resumes upon the suspect's return to Korean jurisdiction.

### Civil Statute of Limitations

Korean civil law (민법) applies the following limitation periods:

- **General claims (contracts):** 10 years from the date the claim could first be asserted (Art. 162)
- **Commercial disputes:** 5 years under the Commercial Act (상법)
- **Tort claims:** The earlier of 3 years from knowledge of the damage and the tortfeasor's identity, or 10 years from the date of the tort (Art. 766)
- **Parties cannot contractually waive** the statute of limitations defense in advance

### Landmark Cases

**Hwaseong Serial Murders (1986-1991):** The case that catalyzed the 2015 abolition. Lee Chun-jae was identified through DNA in 2019, but the expired limitation period prevented prosecution. He confessed to 14 murders and additional rapes while already serving a life sentence for the 1994 rape and murder of his sister-in-law.

**May 18 Democratization Movement (2026 Supreme Court ruling):** The Korean Supreme Court addressed statute of limitations issues related to the 1980 Gwangju massacre, examining whether the state's failure to investigate constitutes grounds for tolling.

## United States

### Federal Criminal Statute of Limitations

The general federal statute of limitations is five years for non-capital offenses under 18 U.S.C. § 3282. Capital offenses carry no limitation period under 18 U.S.C. § 3281.

| Offense Category | Limitation Period | Statutory Basis |
|-------------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Capital offenses (murder) | No limitation | 18 U.S.C. § 3281 |
| Most federal crimes (default) | 5 years | 18 U.S.C. § 3282 |
| Federal tax crimes | 6 years | 26 U.S.C. § 6531 |
| Fraud against federal government (>$1M) | 7 years | 18 U.S.C. § 3293 |
| Major fraud against the United States | 7 years | 18 U.S.C. § 3293 |
| Terrorism offenses | 8 years | 18 U.S.C. § 3286 |
| Arson | 10 years | 18 U.S.C. § 3295 |
| Child abduction/sex offenses against minors | 10 years or lifetime of the child | Various provisions |
| Art theft (>$100,000) | 20 years | 18 U.S.C. § 3294 |

### State-Level Variations

State statutes of limitations vary enormously. While most states impose no limitation on murder, other offenses differ widely:

- **Felonies:** Range from 2 years (Louisiana) to 10 years (Wyoming) to no limit (some states for certain felonies)
- **Misdemeanors:** Typically 1-3 years
- **Sexual assault:** Many states have extended or abolished limitation periods following the #MeToo movement; some states (e.g., New York, California) have enacted "lookback windows" for civil claims
- **Civil personal injury:** Generally 1-6 years depending on the state

### Constitutional Limits: Stogner v. California (2003)

In *Stogner v. California*, 539 U.S. 607 (2003), the Supreme Court held 5-4 that a California law retroactively reviving time-barred prosecutions for child sexual abuse violated the Ex Post Facto Clause of the Constitution. The petitioner had been indicted in 1998 for abuse committed between 1955 and 1973, long after the original three-year limitation period had expired.

**Key holding:** "A law enacted after expiration of a previously applicable limitations period violates the Ex Post Facto Clause when it is applied to revive a previously time-barred prosecution." The Court traced this principle to the Reconstruction Congress of 1867, which rejected a bill to revive time-barred treason prosecutions against Jefferson Davis.

**Distinction — tolling vs. revival:** The Court distinguished between (1) extending a limitation period that has not yet expired (generally permissible), and (2) reviving a prosecution after the limitation period has already run (unconstitutional). This distinction is critical: states may prospectively extend limitation periods or toll them during a defendant's absence, but they cannot resurrect dead claims.

### Tolling Doctrines

- **Fugitive tolling:** The limitation period is tolled while the defendant is a fugitive from justice (18 U.S.C. § 3290)
- **DNA evidence:** Some states toll or restart the limitation period upon discovery of DNA evidence
- **Discovery rule:** In civil cases, the limitation period may begin when the plaintiff discovers (or should have discovered) the injury rather than when it occurred
- **Wartime suspension:** The Wartime Suspension of Limitations Act (18 U.S.C. § 3287) tolls limitations for fraud against the government during wartime

## Germany

### Criminal Statute of Limitations (Verfolgungsverjährung)

The German Criminal Code (Strafgesetzbuch, StGB) §§ 78-78c establishes limitation periods based on maximum penalty:

| Maximum Penalty | Limitation Period | StGB Provision |
|----------------|-------------------|----------------|
| Murder (Mord, § 211) | No limitation | § 78(2) |
| Genocide, crimes against humanity, war crimes | No limitation | § 78(2), VStGB |
| Life imprisonment (other offenses) | 30 years | § 78(3) Nr. 1 |
| Imprisonment over 10 years | 20 years | § 78(3) Nr. 2 |
| Imprisonment 5-10 years | 10 years | § 78(3) Nr. 3 |
| Imprisonment 1-5 years | 5 years | § 78(3) Nr. 4 |
| Imprisonment under 1 year or fine | 3 years | § 78(3) Nr. 5 |

### Historical Abolition: The Nazi War Crimes Debate (1965-1979)

Germany's path to abolishing the statute of limitations for murder is one of the most significant chapters in post-war legal history:

- **1965:** The Bundestag first extended the 20-year limitation period as the deadline for prosecuting Nazi-era murders approached. The debate was intensely political, with some arguing that continued prosecution was necessary for justice and national reconciliation, while others contended that legal finality should prevail.
- **1969:** The limitation period was extended to 30 years, pushing the deadline to 1979.
- **July 3, 1979:** After seven hours of debate, the Bundestag voted 253 to 228 to abolish the statute of limitations for murder entirely. Justice Minister Hans-Jochen Vogel declared: "After Auschwitz, there can be no statute of limitation for murder in Germany." Chancellor Helmut Schmidt led the parliamentary fight.

This abolition applied only to murder (Mord), not to manslaughter (Totschlag) or other offenses.

### Absolute Limitation Period (Absolute Verjährung)

For offenses other than murder, § 78c(3) StGB imposes an absolute maximum limitation period equal to twice the standard limitation period. Once this absolute period expires, prosecution is barred regardless of any interruptions or suspensions. For example, an offense with a 5-year limitation has an absolute cap of 10 years.

### Interruption of Limitation (§ 78c StGB)

The limitation period can be interrupted by specific judicial and prosecutorial acts, including:
- The first interrogation of the accused
- A judicial order for expert examination
- A judicial seizure or search warrant
- An arrest warrant
- The filing of an indictment
- The opening of the main trial

Each interruption restarts the limitation clock, but the absolute limitation cap still applies.

### Civil Statute of Limitations (Verjährung under BGB)

The German Civil Code (Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch, BGB) establishes:

| Claim Type | Limitation Period | BGB Provision |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------|
| Regular claims (default) | 3 years | § 195 |
| Claims relating to land | 10 years | § 196 |
| Heritable building rights and similar | 30 years | § 197 |
| Personal injury (absolute cap) | 30 years | § 199(2) |
| Other claims (absolute cap) | 10 years from accrual | § 199(4) |

A distinctive feature of German law: under § 199(1) BGB, the standard 3-year period begins at the end of the calendar year in which the claim arose and the creditor obtained (or should have obtained) knowledge of the relevant facts. A claim arising on January 2 therefore does not expire until December 31 three years later — effectively providing nearly four years.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Framework

The European Convention on Human Rights does not establish specific limitation periods. Instead, the European Court of Human Rights evaluates national statutes of limitations against the Convention's fundamental rights guarantees, particularly:

**Article 6(1) — Right of Access to Court:** The Court recognizes that statutes of limitations serve legitimate purposes (legal certainty, protection of defendants, preservation of evidence) but holds that limitation periods must not impair the very essence of the right of access to a court. A limitation period that is disproportionately short or that begins running before a claimant could reasonably have known of their cause of action may violate Article 6.

**Article 2 — Right to Life (Procedural Obligation):** States bear a procedural obligation to investigate deaths effectively. The Court has held that national statutes of limitations cannot absolve states of this duty when the investigation was inadequate.

**Article 7 — No Punishment Without Law (Nulla Poena Sine Lege):** Retroactive extension of criminal limitation periods must comply with Article 7, which prohibits the imposition of heavier penalties than those applicable at the time an offense was committed. However, the Court has generally treated limitation periods as procedural rather than substantive, meaning their modification is more readily permissible than changes to penalties.

### Landmark ECHR Cases

**Stubbings v. United Kingdom (1996):** The Court upheld a six-year limitation period for childhood sexual abuse claims running from the claimant's eighteenth birthday. The majority found this was a proportionate restriction on the right of access to court, though the decision has been criticized for failing to account for the psychological barriers to reporting childhood abuse.

**Howald Moor v. Switzerland (2014):** The Court found that Switzerland's ten-year absolute limitation period for asbestos-related disease claims violated Article 6(1). Because asbestos-related mesothelioma has a latency period of 20-50 years, the limitation period expired before victims could possibly know they were ill, rendering the right of access to court "theoretical and illusory" rather than "practical and effective."

**Mocanu and Others v. Romania (2014):** The Grand Chamber addressed the June 1990 events in Bucharest. The Court found Romania in violation of Articles 2 and 3, holding that the state's failure to conduct an effective investigation — partly due to the running of the statute of limitations — violated the procedural obligation to investigate killings and ill-treatment. The Grand Chamber overturned an earlier Chamber decision that had dismissed Mr. Stoica's claim on limitation grounds.

**Silih v. Slovenia (2009):** The Grand Chamber established the "genuine connection" test for temporal jurisdiction. Where a death occurred before a state ratified the Convention, the Court may still examine procedural failures (inadequate investigation) that occurred after ratification, provided there is a genuine connection between the death and the post-ratification acts or omissions.

### EU Legislative Framework

**Directive (EU) 2017/1371 (PIF Directive):** This landmark directive harmonizes criminal law protections for the EU's financial interests and includes the first EU-level harmonization of limitation periods:

- **Covered offenses:** Fraud (including cross-border VAT fraud exceeding EUR 10 million), corruption, money laundering, and misappropriation affecting the EU budget
- **Minimum limitation period:** At least 5 years from the commission of the offense for the institution of criminal proceedings
- **Enforcement period:** At least 5 years from the date of final conviction for the enforcement of penalties
- **Interruption and suspension:** The Directive acknowledges that member states may provide for interruption or suspension of limitation periods
- **Transposition deadline:** July 6, 2019

**Convention on the Protection of the EU's Financial Interests (1995 PIF Convention):** The predecessor instrument, which the PIF Directive largely replaced, also addressed limitation periods but with less prescriptive requirements.

**Proposed Anti-Corruption Directive (2023):** The European Commission's proposal to amend the PIF Directive through a broader anti-corruption directive includes provisions on limitation periods for corruption offenses, reflecting the EU's expanding role in harmonizing criminal procedural standards.

### Council of Europe Conventions

**Convention on the Non-Applicability of Statutory Limitations to Crimes Against Humanity and War Crimes (1974):** This Council of Europe treaty requires state parties to ensure that no limitation period applies to crimes against humanity and war crimes as defined in the Convention. However, it has been ratified by relatively few states.

## Comparative Analysis

### Philosophical Foundations

The four jurisdictions reflect distinct philosophical approaches to the tension between justice and finality:

1. **South Korea** prioritizes victim-centered justice, as demonstrated by the public-driven abolition of murder limitations following the Hwaseong case. Korean law also reflects a Confucian emphasis on social harmony, treating the failure to prosecute serious crimes as a continuing wound to the social fabric.

2. **The United States** privileges constitutional constraints, particularly the Ex Post Facto Clause. The *Stogner* decision reflects a strong commitment to due process and individual rights that limits even well-intentioned legislative reforms. The federal system also produces extreme fragmentation, with limitation periods varying dramatically across states.

3. **Germany** confronted the statute of limitations as a matter of historical reckoning. The 1979 abolition was explicitly motivated by the moral imperative to prosecute Holocaust perpetrators, making it perhaps the most politically consequential limitation-period reform in modern legal history.

4. **The ECHR/EU framework** takes a proportionality-based approach, neither prescribing specific periods nor prohibiting limitations but requiring that national rules do not render fundamental rights illusory. This reflects the European human rights tradition of balancing competing interests through case-by-case assessment.

### Key Differences

**Retroactivity:** This is the sharpest divergence. The United States categorically prohibits the retroactive revival of expired limitation periods under the Ex Post Facto Clause. Germany historically allowed retroactive extensions (1965, 1969, 1979), treating limitation periods as procedural rules not subject to retroactivity constraints. South Korea nominally prohibits retroactive application but has seen legislative attempts to circumvent this principle. The ECHR treats limitation rules as generally procedural but requires compliance with Article 7.

**Scope of abolition:** All three national jurisdictions have abolished limitation periods for murder, but with different scope. Germany extends the exemption to genocide, crimes against humanity, and war crimes under the Code of Crimes Against International Law (Völkerstrafgesetzbuch). South Korea's abolition applies specifically to murder. The United States exempts capital offenses at the federal level, with state-level variations.

**Civil limitations:** Germany's 3-year default (with the end-of-year rule) is notably short by international standards. South Korea's 10-year default for contractual claims is among the longest. The United States lacks a uniform federal rule for most civil matters, creating a patchwork of state-level regimes.

### Convergence Trends

Despite these differences, several convergent trends are observable:

- **Abolition for the most serious crimes:** All jurisdictions have moved toward eliminating or extending limitation periods for murder and crimes against humanity
- **Victim-centered reforms:** Sexual assault limitation periods have been extended across all jurisdictions, reflecting growing recognition that victims often delay reporting
- **DNA evidence exceptions:** Both South Korea and U.S. states have introduced provisions to toll or restart limitation periods when new forensic evidence emerges
- **Harmonization pressures:** The EU's PIF Directive represents a nascent trend toward supranational harmonization of limitation periods, particularly for transnational financial crimes
