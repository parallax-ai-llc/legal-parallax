## Summary

Airspace sovereignty -- the principle that every state has complete and exclusive sovereignty over the airspace above its territory, including its territorial sea -- is a foundational principle of international law with profound implications for civil aviation, military defense, drone operations, and the emerging boundary between airspace and outer space. The principle is codified in Article 1 of the Convention on International Civil Aviation (Chicago Convention, December 7, 1944), which provides that "every State has complete and exclusive sovereignty over the airspace above its territory," and is reinforced by Article 2, defining territory as "the land areas and territorial waters adjacent thereto under the sovereignty, suzerainty, protection or mandate of such State." South Korea's airspace sovereignty framework is shaped by the extraordinary security conditions of the Korean Peninsula, where the Korean Air Defense Identification Zone (KADIZ, 방공식별구역), expanded in 2013 to include Ieodo and the Marado area, overlaps with China's Air Defense Identification Zone (ADIZ) declared in November 2013, and where the Military Armistice Agreement of 1953 established restrictions on military aviation near the Demilitarized Zone (DMZ); Korean airspace regulation is governed by the Aviation Safety Act (항공안전법, 2017), the Military Aviation Act (군용항공기운용 등에 관한 법률), and the Act on Measures Against Aerial Infiltration (공중침투방지법). The United States exercises sovereignty over the National Airspace System (NAS) through the Federal Aviation Administration (FAA), maintains the world's most extensive Air Defense Identification Zone system (established by executive order in 1950 and codified in 14 C.F.R. Part 99), and has led the development of rules for unmanned aircraft systems (UAS/drones) through FAA Part 107 and the Remote ID Rule, while the shoot-down authority is governed by classified rules of engagement implemented after the September 11, 2001 attacks. Germany exercises airspace sovereignty within the EU Single European Sky framework (Regulations (EC) 549-552/2004, as amended by Regulation (EU) 2018/1139), with the Luftverkehrsgesetz (Aviation Act) providing the national legal basis, NATO integrated air defense providing the military framework, and the Luftsicherheitsgesetz (Aviation Security Act) addressing the authority to use force against renegade aircraft -- a provision partially struck down by the Federal Constitutional Court in BVerfGE 115, 118 (2006) as violating the right to life and human dignity. The ECHR and EU framework addresses airspace sovereignty through the Single European Sky initiative, EU competence in aviation safety under EASA Regulation (EU) 2018/1139, and the European Court of Human Rights' jurisprudence on the use of lethal force in the context of aircraft interception, including the right to life under Article 2 of the Convention.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's airspace sovereignty operates under extraordinary conditions arising from the division of the Korean Peninsula and the technically ongoing state of war (the Korean War ended with an armistice in 1953, not a peace treaty). The Aviation Safety Act (항공안전법, 2017) governs civil aviation, while the Military Aviation Act (군용항공기운용 등에 관한 법률) addresses military aviation. The Act on Measures Against Aerial Infiltration (공중침투방지법) provides specific authority to intercept and, if necessary, destroy aircraft making unauthorized incursions into Korean airspace.

The Korean airspace management system is divided between civil and military control. The Seoul Regional Aviation Administration (서울지방항공청) manages civil air traffic, while the Republic of Korea Air Force (대한민국 공군) maintains air defense responsibility. The United States Forces Korea (USFK) retains operational control during wartime under the Combined Forces Command, with implications for airspace management during contingencies.

### Korean Air Defense Identification Zone (KADIZ)

The KADIZ (방공식별구역) was originally established in 1951 by the U.S. Pacific Air Forces during the Korean War and adopted by the ROK upon the transfer of operational authority. On December 8, 2013, South Korea expanded the KADIZ southward to include the airspace above Ieodo (이어도) and Marado (마라도), Korea's southernmost island. The expansion was prompted by China's unilateral declaration of an East China Sea Air Defense Identification Zone (ADIZ) on November 23, 2013, which overlapped with the pre-existing KADIZ.

The expanded KADIZ now overlaps with both China's ADIZ and Japan's ADIZ, creating a tripartite overlap zone in the East China Sea airspace. Unlike the Chinese ADIZ declaration, which required all aircraft transiting the zone (including those not intending to enter Chinese airspace) to identify themselves, Korea's KADIZ applies only to aircraft intending to enter Korean airspace.

### North Korean Airspace Incursions

North Korean aerial provocations have been a persistent security challenge. Notable incidents include multiple drone incursions, most significantly the discovery of crashed North Korean reconnaissance drones in 2014 (found in Paju, Baengnyeong Island, and Samcheok) and a major drone incursion in December 2022, when five North Korean drones crossed the Military Demarcation Line, with one reaching airspace near Seoul before the ROK Air Force could intercept. The December 2022 incident exposed deficiencies in Korea's counter-drone capabilities and led to the establishment of a dedicated counter-unmanned aerial system (C-UAS) unit.

### Drone Regulation

The Aviation Safety Act, Chapter 10, establishes the regulatory framework for unmanned aircraft systems in Korean airspace. Drones weighing over 250 grams must be registered, and operators must obtain certification. Flights in controlled airspace, near airports, above 150 meters, and at night require prior authorization from the Ministry of Land, Infrastructure and Transport (MOLIT). The Special Act on Drone Utilization and Promotion (드론 활용의 촉진 및 기반조성에 관한 법률, 2020) establishes the policy framework for expanding commercial drone operations, including urban air mobility (UAM).

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States exercises sovereignty over its airspace pursuant to the Federal Aviation Act (49 U.S.C. Section 40103), which provides that "the United States Government has exclusive sovereignty of airspace of the United States." The FAA manages the National Airspace System (NAS), the world's largest and most complex air traffic management system, serving over 45,000 flights daily. Military airspace management is coordinated through the Department of Defense and the North American Aerospace Defense Command (NORAD), a binational U.S.-Canadian organization responsible for aerospace warning and control.

### Air Defense Identification Zone (ADIZ)

The United States established the first modern ADIZ system in 1950, during the Cold War, to provide early warning of potential hostile aircraft approaching U.S. territory. Currently codified in 14 C.F.R. Part 99, the U.S. ADIZ requires that all aircraft intending to enter U.S. territorial airspace from the ADIZ file a flight plan, report their position, and identify themselves. The Washington, D.C. Special Flight Rules Area (SFRA), established after the September 11 attacks, imposes heightened identification and authorization requirements within a 30-nautical-mile radius of Ronald Reagan Washington National Airport.

The legal status of ADIZs under international law remains debated. ADIZs extend into international airspace (over the high seas), and the requirement that aircraft identify themselves before entering national airspace is not explicitly authorized by the Chicago Convention. The United States maintains that ADIZ requirements are a reasonable security measure applied to the exercise of overflight rights, while some commentators argue that they constitute an assertion of jurisdiction over international airspace inconsistent with the freedom of overflight.

### Post-9/11 Shoot-Down Authority

Following the September 11, 2001 terrorist attacks, the U.S. government formalized procedures for the authorization to shoot down civilian aircraft deemed to pose an imminent threat. The specific rules of engagement are classified, but it is publicly known that the authority to order the destruction of a hijacked civilian aircraft rests with the President or the Secretary of Defense. On September 11 itself, Vice President Cheney authorized the shoot-down of hijacked aircraft, though the authorization's constitutional basis was subsequently debated. The 9/11 Commission Report documented the chaotic chain of command issues that arose on that day.

NORAD maintains air sovereignty alert sites (fighter aircraft on strip alert) at locations across the United States and Canada, capable of scrambling interceptors within minutes to respond to potential airspace violations. Intercepts of civilian aircraft that deviate from their flight plans or fail to respond to communications are routine (approximately 100 per year).

### Drone Regulation

The FAA regulates unmanned aircraft systems under 14 C.F.R. Part 107 (Small Unmanned Aircraft Rule, effective August 29, 2016) for commercial drones under 55 pounds. The Remote Identification Rule (effective September 16, 2023) requires most drones to broadcast identification and location information, enabling airspace integration and law enforcement. The FAA Extension, Safety, and Security Act of 2016 and subsequent legislation have addressed recreational drone operations, drone delivery, and beyond-visual-line-of-sight operations.

Counter-drone authority in the United States is legally constrained. The Preventing Emerging Threats Act of 2018 (Title 6 U.S.C. Sections 124n-124o) authorized the Department of Homeland Security and the Department of Justice to detect, identify, track, and in certain circumstances disable or destroy drones that pose a credible threat to critical infrastructure or government facilities. However, state and local law enforcement generally lack counter-drone authority, creating enforcement gaps.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's airspace sovereignty is exercised within the framework of EU aviation regulation and NATO integrated air defense. The Luftverkehrsgesetz (LuftVG, Aviation Act) provides the national statutory basis for airspace sovereignty and management. The Luftsicherheitsgesetz (LuftSiG, Aviation Security Act, 2005) addresses aviation security measures, including the controversial authority to use force against renegade aircraft.

Air traffic control services in German airspace are provided by DFS Deutsche Flugsicherung GmbH, a federally owned company established in 1993 as part of the corporatization of German ATC. Military air traffic control is separate but coordinated through the NATO Air Command at Ramstein Air Base.

### Single European Sky

Germany participates in the Single European Sky (SES) initiative, established by the SES Regulations (EC) 549-552/2004 and amended by SES II (Regulation (EC) 1070/2009). The SES aims to reform the architecture of European air traffic management by defragmenting national airspace systems into functional airspace blocks (FABs). Germany is a member of the Functional Airspace Block Europe Central (FABEC), together with France, Belgium, Luxembourg, the Netherlands, and Switzerland.

The SES framework incorporates the Flexible Use of Airspace (FUA) concept, which allocates airspace between civil and military users on a real-time basis rather than through rigid segregation. This is particularly relevant for Germany, which hosts significant NATO military aviation activity alongside dense civil air traffic in the heart of Europe.

### Aviation Security Act and Shoot-Down Authority

The Luftsicherheitsgesetz (Aviation Security Act), enacted on January 11, 2005, was Germany's legislative response to the September 11, 2001 attacks and the specific threat posed by the Landshut hijacking anniversary. Section 14(3) of the Act authorized the Minister of Defense to order the use of force against a civilian aircraft that was "being used as a weapon" against human life, as a last resort after all other means had been exhausted.

In a landmark decision on February 15, 2006 (BVerfGE 115, 118, the Aviation Security Act Case), the Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) declared Section 14(3) unconstitutional. The Court held that ordering the destruction of a hijacked aircraft carrying innocent passengers violated the passengers' right to life under Article 2(2) of the Basic Law and their right to human dignity under Article 1(1), which the Court characterized as "inviolable" and not subject to balancing against other interests. The Court reasoned that treating the passengers as objects to be sacrificed for the greater good was incompatible with the guarantee of human dignity. However, the Court left open the question of whether the use of force against an aircraft carrying only the perpetrators (and no innocent passengers) might be constitutionally permissible.

The decision has been widely debated in German and international legal scholarship. Some commentators argue that the Court's absolute prohibition is unworkable in a scenario where an aircraft is being used as a weapon against a densely populated area, while others regard it as a principled application of the Kantian prohibition on instrumentalizing human beings.

### Drone Regulation

Germany implements the EU drone regulatory framework established by Commission Delegated Regulation (EU) 2019/945 (drone product requirements) and Commission Implementing Regulation (EU) 2019/947 (drone operations). The EU framework classifies drone operations into three categories: "open" (low risk, no authorization required), "specific" (medium risk, authorization required), and "certified" (high risk, certified like manned aviation). The German implementation is supplemented by the Luftverkehrs-Ordnung (Aviation Traffic Regulation) and supervised by the Luftfahrt-Bundesamt (LBA, Federal Aviation Office).

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| Unauthorized entry into German airspace | Military interception; criminal liability |
| Violation of restricted airspace zones | Fine or imprisonment up to 2 years under LuftVG |
| Operating drone without required authorization | Fine up to 50,000 EUR |
| Endangering air traffic | Imprisonment up to 10 years under StGB Section 315 |
| Failure to comply with ATC instructions | Fine or imprisonment under LuftVG |

## ECHR & EU

### EU Single European Sky

The Single European Sky (SES) initiative represents the most ambitious effort to harmonize airspace management across sovereign states. The SES framework comprises four basic regulations: the Framework Regulation (EC) 549/2004, the Service Provision Regulation (EC) 550/2004, the Airspace Regulation (EC) 551/2004, and the Interoperability Regulation (EC) 552/2004, as amended by SES II (Regulation (EC) 1070/2009). The SES ATM Research (SESAR) program develops the technological infrastructure for modernized European air traffic management.

Despite ambitious goals, the SES has been hindered by member states' reluctance to cede sovereignty over their airspace. The European Commission proposed SES2+ reforms in 2013 and revised proposals in 2020, aiming to strengthen Eurocontrol's role, establish an EU-level performance review body, and incentivize cross-border airspace management. Military airspace sovereignty remains a sensitive issue, with member states retaining full control over military aviation operations.

### ECHR Article 2 and Use of Force Against Aircraft

The European Convention on Human Rights, Article 2 (right to life), provides the framework for assessing the legality of using lethal force against aircraft. Article 2(2) permits the use of force "which is no more than absolutely necessary" for purposes including defense against unlawful violence and lawful arrest.

The ECtHR has not directly adjudicated a case involving the shoot-down of a civilian aircraft, but its general principles on the use of lethal force, developed in cases such as McCann v. United Kingdom (Application No. 18984/91, Grand Chamber Judgment of September 27, 1995) and Finogenov v. Russia (Application No. 18299/03, 2011, concerning the Moscow theater hostage siege), establish strict proportionality requirements. The McCann test requires that the force used be "no more than absolutely necessary," a stricter standard than the proportionality tests applied in other areas of Convention law.

The German BVerfG's Aviation Security Act decision (2006) has been analyzed through the lens of ECHR Article 2, with commentators debating whether the ECHR would permit the shoot-down of a hijacked aircraft carrying innocent passengers in extreme circumstances (e.g., an imminent collision with a nuclear facility or densely populated area).

### EU Drone Regulation Framework

The EU has established a comprehensive harmonized framework for drone regulation through Commission Delegated Regulation (EU) 2019/945 and Commission Implementing Regulation (EU) 2019/947. The framework creates a risk-based approach with three categories (open, specific, certified), standardized pilot competency requirements, drone registration requirements, and geo-awareness systems. EASA maintains the regulatory authority for drone operations across EU member states.

The EU's U-space Regulation (Commission Implementing Regulation (EU) 2021/664), which entered into application on January 26, 2023, establishes the framework for managing drone traffic in defined airspace volumes, equivalent to air traffic management for manned aviation. U-space services include network identification, geo-awareness, flight authorization, and traffic information.

### Karman Line and the Air-Space Boundary

The question of where national airspace sovereignty ends and outer space begins remains unresolved in international law. The most commonly cited boundary is the Karman line at 100 kilometers above sea level, named after physicist Theodore von Karman and recognized by the Federation Aeronautique Internationale (FAI). However, no binding international instrument establishes this boundary. The Outer Space Treaty (1967) establishes the principle that outer space is not subject to national appropriation but does not define where outer space begins. The EU has not adopted a formal position, though ESA uses the 100-km threshold. Neither South Korea, the United States, nor Germany has formally adopted the Karman line as a legal boundary.

## Comparative Analysis

### Airspace Sovereignty in Theory and Practice

While all four jurisdictions accept the Chicago Convention's principle of complete and exclusive airspace sovereignty, the practical exercise of this sovereignty differs dramatically. South Korea's airspace sovereignty is constrained by the security environment of the Korean Peninsula, including U.S. military operational control and the proximity of North Korean military aviation. The United States exercises the most robust airspace sovereignty regime, backed by NORAD's air defense capabilities. Germany's airspace sovereignty is exercised within the EU SES framework and NATO integrated air defense, reflecting the pooled sovereignty model. The EU seeks to harmonize airspace management while respecting member state sovereignty, an inherently tension-filled enterprise.

### ADIZ Regimes

ADIZs represent an extension of airspace-related authority into international airspace that has no explicit basis in the Chicago Convention. The U.S. ADIZ system is the oldest and most established. South Korea's KADIZ, expanded in 2013, reflects the competitive security environment in Northeast Asia. Germany operates within NATO's integrated air defense rather than maintaining a separate ADIZ. China's 2013 ADIZ declaration, which precipitated Korea's KADIZ expansion, was notable for its expansive requirements and the overlapping claims it created.

### Shoot-Down Authority: Constitutional Constraints

The German BVerfG's Aviation Security Act decision represents the most restrictive judicial position on shoot-down authority among the four jurisdictions, holding that the state may never intentionally kill innocent passengers aboard a hijacked aircraft. The United States maintains classified rules of engagement that permit presidential authorization for shoot-down. South Korea's statutory framework authorizes the use of force against infiltrating aircraft. The ECHR's Article 2 framework would require "absolute necessity," suggesting a position between the German absolute prohibition and the U.S. executive discretion model.

### Drone Regulation Convergence

All four jurisdictions are converging toward risk-based drone regulation frameworks, though at different paces. The EU's comprehensive framework (2019/945, 2019/947, U-space) is the most detailed. The U.S. FAA Part 107 and Remote ID Rule establish a robust but still-evolving system. South Korea is developing its framework with particular attention to urban air mobility. The challenge common to all jurisdictions is integrating drones into airspace safely while enabling commercial innovation.
