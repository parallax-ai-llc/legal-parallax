## Summary

The grand jury -- a body of citizens convened to review evidence presented by the prosecution and to determine whether sufficient probable cause exists to bring criminal charges (indictment) against a suspect -- is a distinctively Anglo-American institution that has no direct equivalent in most civil law systems, reflecting fundamentally different approaches to the question of who should control the gateway to criminal prosecution and how citizen participation in the criminal process should be structured. South Korea does not employ a grand jury system but has developed functional equivalents through the citizen participation trial system (국민참여재판), introduced in 2008 under the Act on Citizen Participation in Criminal Trials (국민의 형사재판 참여에 관한 법률), which allows citizens to serve as jurors in the trial phase (though not the charging phase), and through the Prosecutorial Review Committee (검찰시민위원회, established 2018) and the Article 260 judicial review mechanism (재정신청) that provide external checks on prosecutorial charging decisions. The United States is the only jurisdiction examined that maintains a constitutionally mandated grand jury system, with the Fifth Amendment requiring grand jury indictment for "capital, or otherwise infamous crime" in federal cases, though this requirement has not been incorporated against the states through the Fourteenth Amendment (Hurtado v. California, 110 U.S. 516 (1884)); approximately half the states require grand jury indictment for felonies, while the remainder permit prosecution by information filed by the prosecutor. Germany has never employed a grand jury, relying instead on the Legalitätsprinzip (mandatory prosecution principle), the judicial preliminary examination (Eröffnungsbeschluss under Section 203 StPO -- the court's independent determination that sufficient suspicion exists to open main proceedings), and the Klageerzwingungsverfahren (compelled prosecution procedure under Section 172 StPO) as its mechanisms for screening prosecutorial charging decisions and ensuring accountability. The European legal tradition generally relies on judicial control of the charging process rather than citizen participation, with the ECHR requiring under Article 6 that the defendant be informed promptly of the nature and cause of the accusation, and the EU Directive 2012/13/EU on the right to information in criminal proceedings establishing minimum standards for notification of charges.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's criminal justice system does not include a grand jury. The prosecutorial indictment (기소) has been the exclusive gateway to criminal prosecution, with external checks provided through judicial and civilian oversight mechanisms.

### Prosecutorial Monopoly and Charging

Under Article 246 of the Criminal Procedure Act (공소는 검사가 제기하여 행한다 -- "public prosecution shall be instituted and conducted by a public prosecutor"), prosecutors hold the exclusive authority to institute criminal proceedings. This monopoly, while challenged by the 2020-2022 reforms creating the CIO, remains the fundamental structural feature of Korean charging practice.

### Article 260 -- Judicial Review of Non-Prosecution (재정신청)

Article 260 of the Criminal Procedure Act provides a mechanism functionally analogous to one aspect of the grand jury -- citizen participation in the charging decision:

The victim or complainant may file an application (재정신청) with the High Court challenging the prosecutor's decision not to prosecute. The court reviews the evidence and determines whether there is sufficient basis for prosecution. If the court grants the application, the case is referred to the district court as if an indictment had been filed, and the court appoints a lawyer to act as prosecutor.

This mechanism was significantly expanded by the 2007 amendment, which broadened eligibility from a narrow category of offenses to all criminal offenses, and by subsequent Constitutional Court decisions affirming the importance of this check on prosecutorial power.

### Citizen Participation Trial System (국민참여재판)

While not a grand jury, Korea's citizen participation trial system (established 2008) introduced citizen involvement in criminal proceedings:

| Feature | Korean System |
|---------|--------------|
| Name | 국민참여재판 (Citizen Participation Trial) |
| Legal basis | Act on Citizen Participation in Criminal Trials (2007) |
| Number of jurors | 5, 7, or 9 depending on offense severity |
| Eligible cases | Serious felonies (murder, robbery, sexual assault, etc.) |
| Verdict | Advisory (not binding on judge, though followed in most cases) |
| Defendant's choice | Defendant must request or consent to jury trial |

### Prosecutorial Review Committee (검찰시민위원회)

Established in 2018, this committee of external civilian members reviews prosecutorial decisions in cases of significant public interest, providing a form of civilian oversight analogous to the grand jury's democratic legitimacy function. The committee may recommend reconsideration of non-prosecution decisions.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States is the only country in the world that maintains the grand jury as a constitutional institution in its modern criminal justice system.

### Fifth Amendment

The Fifth Amendment provides: "No person shall be held to answer for a capital, or otherwise infamous crime, unless on a presentment or indictment of a Grand Jury, except in cases arising in the land or naval forces, or in the Militia, when in actual service in time of War or public danger."

### Constitutional Scope

**Hurtado v. California, 110 U.S. 516 (1884):** The Supreme Court held that the Fifth Amendment grand jury requirement is not incorporated against the states through the Fourteenth Amendment. This remains one of the few Bill of Rights provisions not incorporated, reflecting the grand jury's unique historical status.

As a result:
- **Federal:** Grand jury indictment required for all felonies
- **States:** Approximately 23 states require grand jury indictment for felonies; others permit prosecution by information

### Federal Grand Jury Procedure

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Size | 16-23 members |
| Quorum | 16 members |
| Indictment | Requires concurrence of at least 12 |
| Term | Up to 18 months (may be extended to 24) |
| Standard | Probable cause |
| Rules of evidence | Do not apply |
| Defense counsel | Not permitted in grand jury room |
| Secrecy | Strict (Fed. R. Crim. P. 6(e)) |

### Powers and Functions

The grand jury possesses broad investigative powers:
- **Subpoena power:** May compel testimony and production of documents; contempt for non-compliance
- **Investigative function:** May investigate criminal activity independent of the prosecutor (though in practice, prosecutors control investigations)
- **Screening function:** Reviews evidence to determine probable cause for indictment
- **Protective function:** Shields citizens from unfounded prosecution (the historical justification)

### Criticism and "Rubber Stamp" Concern

The grand jury has faced sustained criticism as a "rubber stamp" for prosecutorial decisions. Federal grand juries indict in approximately 99.9% of cases presented, prompting Judge Sol Wachtler's famous observation that a prosecutor could "indict a ham sandwich." Critics argue that:
- Prosecutors control all evidence presented
- Defendants have no right to appear or present evidence
- Grand jurors lack independent legal guidance
- The secrecy of proceedings prevents public accountability
- The one-sided nature of grand jury proceedings fails to serve the screening function

### Special Grand Juries

Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 6(a)(1) authorizes special grand juries in districts with populations exceeding 5 million, which may serve for up to 36 months and have enhanced investigative powers, particularly for organized crime and public corruption cases.

### Grand Jury and Police Use of Force

Grand juries have become a focus of controversy in cases involving police use of force, particularly after the non-indictments in the shooting of Michael Brown in Ferguson, Missouri (2014) and the death of Eric Garner in New York City (2014). Critics argued that the grand jury process is structurally biased in favor of police officers because the same prosecutors who depend on police cooperation present evidence to the grand jury.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany has never employed a grand jury system. The screening of prosecutorial charging decisions is accomplished through judicial mechanisms and the Legalitätsprinzip.

### Eröffnungsbeschluss (Opening Decision) -- Section 203 StPO

When the prosecution files an indictment (Anklageschrift), the court must independently assess whether there is "sufficient suspicion" (hinreichender Tatverdacht) that the accused committed the charged offense before opening main proceedings. Section 203 StPO provides: "The court shall decide to open the main proceedings if, after the results of the preliminary investigation, the accused appears sufficiently suspected of the criminal offense."

This judicial screening serves a function analogous to the grand jury's protective role:
- The court reviews the prosecution file independently
- The court may request additional investigation under Section 202 StPO
- The court may refuse to open proceedings if sufficient suspicion is lacking
- The defendant has the right to respond to the indictment before the court decides

### Klageerzwingungsverfahren -- Section 172 StPO

The compelled prosecution procedure allows victims to challenge prosecutorial non-prosecution, providing a check on prosecutorial discretion that serves a function analogous to the grand jury's supervisory role over the prosecution.

### Schöffen (Lay Judges)

While not equivalent to grand jurors, Schöffen (lay judges) participate in the adjudication of criminal cases at the Amtsgericht (local court) level and at the Landgericht (regional court) level. Schöffen are citizens selected from community lists who sit alongside professional judges and have full voting rights on questions of guilt and sentencing.

| Feature | Schöffen System |
|---------|----------------|
| Court level | Amtsgericht (Schöffengericht); Landgericht (Große Strafkammer) |
| Number of Schöffen | 2 (Schöffengericht); 2 (Große Strafkammer) |
| Professional judges | 1 (Schöffengericht); 2-3 (Große Strafkammer) |
| Selection | Random from community lists; 5-year term |
| Decision authority | Full vote on guilt and sentencing |
| Scope | Moderate felonies (Schöffengericht); serious felonies (Landgericht) |

### Historical Note

Germany briefly experimented with jury trials (Schwurgericht) in the 19th century, influenced by French revolutionary ideals, but abolished them in 1924, replacing them with the mixed court system that includes Schöffen. The abandonment of the pure jury system reflected a preference for combining lay and professional judgment rather than separating them.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR -- Right to Information About Charges

Article 6(3)(a) ECHR provides that everyone charged with a criminal offence has the right "to be informed promptly, in a language which he understands and in detail, of the nature and cause of the accusation against him." This provision governs the charging process but does not prescribe a particular mechanism (grand jury or otherwise).

**Pelissier and Sassi v. France [GC], Application No. 25444/94, Judgment of March 25, 1999:** The Grand Chamber held that the right to be informed of the nature and cause of the accusation must be given a broad interpretation, including the legal characterization of the offense. Conviction on a charge substantially different from that of which the defendant was informed violates Article 6(3)(a).

### EU Directive 2012/13/EU

Directive 2012/13/EU on the right to information in criminal proceedings (adopted May 22, 2012) establishes minimum standards:

**Article 6 -- Right to Information About the Accusation:** Suspects and accused persons must be provided with information about the criminal act they are suspected or accused of having committed and the legal classification of the offense, in sufficient detail to enable effective exercise of the rights of the defense.

**Letter of Rights:** Article 4 requires that suspects are provided with a written letter of rights upon arrest or detention, informing them of their procedural rights.

### Citizen Participation in Criminal Justice -- European Models

While no European jurisdiction maintains a grand jury, several forms of citizen participation exist:
- **Jury trials:** England and Wales, Ireland, Belgium, Austria (for serious offenses)
- **Mixed courts (lay + professional judges):** Germany, France (Cour d'assises), Denmark, Sweden, Norway
- **People's assessors:** Eastern European post-communist systems

## Comparative Analysis

### Citizen Control of Charging

The grand jury represents the most direct form of citizen control over the charging process, interposing lay judgment between the prosecutor and the defendant. Germany's Eröffnungsbeschluss provides judicial (professional) screening rather than citizen screening. South Korea's Article 260 mechanism provides victim-initiated judicial review. In practice, however, the U.S. grand jury's near-universal rate of indictment casts doubt on whether citizen control is meaningfully exercised.

### Transparency vs. Secrecy

The U.S. grand jury operates under strict secrecy, justified by the need to protect reputations of persons investigated but not charged, to prevent flight of suspects, and to ensure candid testimony. Germany's preliminary examination is not public but the indictment is disclosed to the defendant. South Korea's Article 260 proceedings are judicial and subject to court procedures. The tension between secrecy and accountability remains a central issue.

### Accountability for Non-Prosecution

Germany provides the strongest mechanism for challenging non-prosecution through the Klageerzwingungsverfahren, with judicial power to compel prosecution. South Korea's Article 260 provides a comparable mechanism. The United States provides no effective mechanism, as grand jury proceedings are controlled by the prosecutor who has already decided to seek (or not seek) indictment.

### Democratic Legitimacy

Both the U.S. grand jury and South Korea's citizen participation trial system derive legitimacy from citizen involvement in the criminal process. Germany's Schöffen system serves a similar democratic function at the trial stage. The grand jury is unique in placing citizen judgment at the charging stage, but its effectiveness as a democratic check is widely questioned.
