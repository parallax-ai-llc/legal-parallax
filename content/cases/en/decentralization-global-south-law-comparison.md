## Summary

Decentralization and local governance in the Global South encompass a remarkable diversity of institutional arrangements, from China's unique township-village enterprise (TVE, 乡镇企业) model and village self-governance experiments to India's constitutionally mandated panchayati raj system, from Nigeria's contested local government architecture to Brazil's constitutionally autonomous municipios, and from the Philippines' comprehensive Local Government Code to Indonesia's post-Suharto "Big Bang" devolution to hundreds of districts and cities. These arrangements reflect fundamentally different theories of local governance: China's model integrates economic development and administrative control under CPC leadership at the grassroots level through the township-village governance structure codified in the Organic Law of the Villagers' Committees (村民委员会组织法, 1987, revised 1998, further revised 2010) and the Organic Law of the Urban Residents' Committees (城市居民委员会组织法, 1989), which establish village and neighborhood self-governance as the basic unit of democratic participation within the CPC-led system. India's 73rd Constitutional Amendment (1992), inserting Part IX (Articles 243-243O) on Panchayats, and the 74th Constitutional Amendment (1992), inserting Part IXA (Articles 243P-243ZG) on Municipalities, constitutionalized a three-tier local government system (gram panchayat, panchayat samiti/block, and zilla parishad/district) with mandatory elections, reserved seats for Scheduled Castes, Scheduled Tribes, and women (not less than one-third of total seats, raised to fifty percent in many states), and the devolution of 29 subjects listed in the Eleventh Schedule for panchayats and 18 subjects in the Twelfth Schedule for municipalities. Nigeria's 774 local government areas (LGAs) are constitutionally recognized (Section 7 of the 1999 Constitution, Fourth Schedule), but their autonomy is severely constrained by state governors' control over the State Joint Local Government Account (SJLGA, Section 162(6)), which channels federal allocations through state governments, effectively creating what the 2018 Joint Tax Board described as "fiscal strangulation" of local government. Brazil's 5,570 municipios enjoy constitutional autonomy under Article 29 of the 1988 Constitution, including the power to adopt their own Organic Laws (Lei Organica do Municipio), levy certain taxes (ISS/ISSQN on services, IPTU on urban property), and manage education and health services under the Sistema Unico de Saude (SUS) decentralized framework. The Philippine Local Government Code (Republic Act No. 7160, 1991) devolved significant powers to provinces, cities, municipalities, and barangays (the smallest political unit, numbering over 42,000), including the Internal Revenue Allotment (IRA, comprising 40% of internal revenue taxes collected by the national government three fiscal years prior, as upheld and expanded by the Supreme Court in *Mandanas v. Ochoa*, G.R. No. 199802, 2018). Indonesia's decentralization, implemented through successive laws (Law No. 22/1999, Law No. 32/2004, Law No. 23/2014 on Regional Government), transferred extensive administrative authority to over 500 kabupaten (regencies) and kota (cities), governed by directly elected bupati/walikota (regents/mayors) under Law No. 1/2015 on the election of regional heads, creating one of the most decentralized governance systems in Asia while also generating challenges of local elite capture, corruption, and uneven service delivery.

## China (中国)

### The Township-Village Governance System

China's local governance system operates through a distinctive hierarchy that combines state administration (at the township level and above) with self-governance mechanisms (at the village and urban neighborhood levels), all under CPC organizational leadership.

**Township and Village Enterprises (TVEs, 乡镇企业):** TVEs were collectively owned enterprises that operated under township and village government auspices, primarily during the 1980s and 1990s. At their peak in the mid-1990s, TVEs employed over 135 million workers and generated approximately 30% of China's GDP. The TVE model, analyzed extensively by scholars such as Jean Oi (who coined the term "local state corporatism") and Andrew Walder, represented a unique form of local economic governance where township and village officials functioned as de facto corporate managers, leveraging local government authority to mobilize resources, negotiate with higher-level authorities, and manage market entry. The Township Enterprise Law (乡镇企业法, 1996) provided the legislative framework, though most TVEs were subsequently privatized or restructured during the late 1990s and 2000s.

**Organic Law of the Villagers' Committees (村民委员会组织法):**

The Villagers' Committee (村民委员会, cunmin weiyuanhui) is constitutionally defined as a "self-governing mass organization at the grassroots level" (Article 111 of the Constitution), not a state organ. The Organic Law establishes:

- **Article 2:** The villagers' committee is the self-governing mass organization at the grassroots level, in which the villagers manage their own affairs, educate themselves, and serve their own needs.
- **Article 11:** The villagers' committee chair, deputy chair, and members are directly elected by villagers for a three-year term (extended to five years by the 2018 revision in some areas). Elections must involve more candidates than positions (差额选举).
- **Article 22:** The Village Assembly (村民会议, cunmin huiyi), comprising all villagers aged 18 and above (or household representatives), is the supreme decision-making body of the village, with authority over village financial plans, land use, collective economic management, and public welfare.
- **Article 24:** Matters involving villagers' interests, including the distribution of collectively owned property, land compensation fees, and the use of collective funds, must be submitted to the Village Assembly or Village Representative Assembly for discussion and decision.

**The Village Party Branch:** Despite the formal self-governance structure, the CPC village Party branch (村党支部, cun dangzhibu) exercises leadership over village affairs. The tension between the elected villagers' committee chair and the appointed Party branch secretary is a persistent feature of village governance. The CPC's 2019 Regulations on the Work of Rural Grassroots Party Organizations (中国共产党农村基层组织工作条例) establish that the village Party organization is the "leadership core" (领导核心) of village organizations and work. In many villages, this tension is resolved by having the same person serve as both Party branch secretary and villagers' committee chair (一肩挑, yi jiantiao, "carrying both on one shoulder"), a practice actively promoted by the CPC since 2019.

### Urban Community Governance

The Organic Law of the Urban Residents' Committees (城市居民委员会组织法, 1989) establishes residents' committees (居民委员会, jumin weiyuanhui) as the urban equivalent of villagers' committees. In practice, urban governance has evolved toward the "community" (社区, shequ) model, with community service centers, community Party organizations, and grid management systems (网格化管理, wanggehua guanli) that divide neighborhoods into grids (typically 300-500 households per grid) with assigned grid managers responsible for service delivery, information collection, and social management.

### The Village Fund System (Post-2014)

Since the targeted poverty alleviation campaign (精准扶贫, jingzhun fupin) launched in 2013-2014, the central government has dramatically increased fiscal transfers to the village level. The Village Revitalization Strategy (乡村振兴战略, xiangcun zhenxing zhanlue), formalized by the Rural Revitalization Promotion Law (乡村振兴促进法, 2021), provides the legal framework for comprehensive rural development including infrastructure, public services, environmental governance, and cultural preservation.

## India (भारत)

### The 73rd and 74th Constitutional Amendments

The 73rd Amendment (1992), which inserted Part IX (Articles 243-243O) on panchayats, and the 74th Amendment (1992), which inserted Part IXA (Articles 243P-243ZG) on municipalities, constitutionalized local self-governance in India.

**Key Provisions of the 73rd Amendment:**

- **Article 243B:** Three-tier panchayat system at the village (gram panchayat), intermediate (panchayat samiti/block), and district (zilla parishad) levels, for states with populations over 20 lakh (2 million). States with populations below 20 lakh may omit the intermediate level.
- **Article 243C:** Composition of panchayats, with seats allocated in proportion to population. Chairpersons of intermediate and district panchayats are elected from among the elected members.
- **Article 243D:** Reservation of seats for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes in proportion to their population, with not less than one-third of the total reserved seats further reserved for women. Not less than one-third of the total number of seats (including unreserved seats) are reserved for women. Reservation for the office of Chairperson follows the same principles, with rotation.
- **Article 243E:** Five-year term for panchayats; elections must be completed before the expiry of the term or within six months of dissolution.
- **Article 243G:** The state legislature may endow panchayats with powers and authority necessary for them to function as institutions of self-government, including with respect to the preparation of plans for economic development and social justice in relation to matters listed in the Eleventh Schedule (29 subjects including agriculture, land improvement, minor irrigation, animal husbandry, fisheries, social forestry, small-scale industries, housing, drinking water, roads, education, health, and women and child development).
- **Article 243H:** The state legislature may authorize panchayats to levy, collect, and appropriate taxes, duties, tolls, and fees.
- **Article 243I:** The Governor shall constitute a State Finance Commission (SFC) every five years to review the financial position of panchayats and make recommendations regarding the distribution of tax proceeds, grants-in-aid, and measures to improve the financial position of panchayats.

### The Gram Sabha

The Gram Sabha (village assembly, Article 243A), comprising all persons registered as voters in the electoral roll for the panchayat area, is the foundational institution of panchayati raj. The Gram Sabha functions as a deliberative and accountability mechanism, with the power to approve plans and budgets, review annual accounts, and hold elected representatives accountable. The Panchayats (Extension to Scheduled Areas) Act, 1996 (PESA) strengthened the Gram Sabha's role in Scheduled Areas (Fifth Schedule tribal areas), requiring gram sabha consent for land acquisition, minor forest produce management, and mineral extraction.

### Women's Reservation and Its Impact

The one-third reservation for women (raised to fifty percent in states including Bihar, Rajasthan, Madhya Pradesh, Himachal Pradesh, Uttarakhand, Chhattisgarh, Jharkhand, Kerala, Maharashtra, Tripura, Odisha, Andhra Pradesh, and Telangana) has produced the world's largest cohort of elected women representatives. As of 2024, approximately 1.4 million women serve as elected panchayat representatives, constituting approximately 46% of all panchayat members nationally. Research by Esther Duflo and Raghabendra Chattopadhyay (2004) found that women-led gram panchayats in West Bengal and Rajasthan invested more in public goods aligned with women's preferences (drinking water, roads) and that exposure to women leaders reduced gender bias in subsequent elections.

### The 74th Amendment and Urban Governance

The 74th Amendment establishes three categories of urban local bodies: nagar panchayats (transitional areas), municipal councils (smaller urban areas), and municipal corporations (larger urban areas). The Twelfth Schedule lists 18 subjects for municipalities, including urban planning, regulation of land use, public health, water supply, slum improvement, and urban poverty alleviation. However, implementation has been uneven, with many states retaining significant control over municipal functions through parastatals and development authorities.

## Africa (Nigeria and South Africa)

### Nigeria: The Local Government Autonomy Debate

Nigeria's 774 local government areas are recognized in the Constitution (Section 7: "The system of local government by democratically elected local government councils is under this Constitution guaranteed"), and the Fourth Schedule specifies their functions (including economic planning, collection of rates, radio and television licenses, establishment and maintenance of cemeteries, and regulation of certain markets and motor parks).

**The State Joint Local Government Account (SJLGA):** Section 162(6) requires that each state maintain a "State Joint Local Government Account into which shall be paid all allocations to the local government councils of the State from the Federation Account and from the Government of the State." In practice, state governors have used the SJLGA to divert local government funds, leading to the chronic underfunding of local government services. The Supreme Court in *Attorney-General of Lagos State v. Attorney-General of the Federation* [2004] 18 NWLR (Pt 904) 1 addressed federal-state fiscal disputes but did not directly resolve the local government fiscal autonomy question.

**The Local Government Autonomy Movement:** The 2022 constitutional amendment proposals included provisions for direct allocation of federal funds to local governments (bypassing state governments) and guaranteed democratic elections for local government councils (which state governors frequently postpone, appointing caretaker committees instead). The Supreme Court's 2024 ruling in *Attorney-General of the Federation v. Attorney-General of Abia State and Others* held that local government allocations from the Federation Account must be paid directly to local government councils rather than through the SJLGA, a landmark decision for local government fiscal autonomy.

**Traditional Institutions:** Nigeria's local governance includes traditional rulers (obas, emirs, obis, and chiefs) whose authority derives from customary law rather than the Constitution. The relationship between traditional institutions and elected local government councils varies by state: some states (e.g., Kano, Oyo) grant traditional rulers significant advisory and ceremonial roles, while others marginalize them. The Chiefs Law (varying by state) typically establishes the recognition, appointment, and disciplinary procedures for traditional rulers.

### South Africa: Developmental Local Government

South Africa's Constitution (Chapter 7, Sections 151-164) establishes municipalities as a third sphere of government (alongside national and provincial), with the mandate of "developmental local government" -- governance that works with communities to find sustainable ways to meet their social, economic, and material needs and improve the quality of their lives.

**Municipal Structures Act 117 of 1998:** Establishes three categories of municipalities:
- **Category A (Metropolitan):** Eight metropolitan municipalities (e.g., City of Johannesburg, City of Cape Town, eThekwini) with exclusive municipal executive and legislative authority in their areas.
- **Category B (Local):** 226 local municipalities that share executive and legislative authority with Category C (district) municipalities.
- **Category C (District):** 44 district municipalities providing district-wide planning, infrastructure, and service coordination.

**Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000:** Establishes the framework for community participation, including Integrated Development Plans (IDPs), which every municipality must adopt and review annually, and which must be informed by community participation. Section 16 requires municipalities to develop a culture of community participation, while Section 17 requires the establishment of ward committees (chaired by the ward councillor) as mechanisms for community input.

**Municipal Finance Management Act 56 of 2003 (MFMA):** Establishes the financial management framework for municipalities, including requirements for multi-year budgeting aligned with IDPs, quarterly reporting, and audit by the Auditor-General. Despite this framework, many municipalities face severe governance and financial challenges: the Auditor-General's 2022/23 report found that only 34 of 257 municipalities (13%) achieved clean audits.

## Latin America (Brazil, Mexico, Colombia)

### Brazil: Municipal Autonomy and Participatory Budgeting

Brazil's municipios are constitutionally autonomous entities (Article 18) with the power to:
- **Adopt their own Organic Law (Lei Organica do Municipio, Article 29):** This municipal "constitution" must be approved by two-thirds of the municipal chamber (camara municipal) in two rounds of voting.
- **Levy taxes:** ISS/ISSQN (Imposto Sobre Servicos de Qualquer Natureza, tax on services), IPTU (Imposto Predial e Territorial Urbano, urban property tax), and ITBI (Imposto sobre a Transmissao de Bens Imoveis, real estate transfer tax) under Article 156.
- **Manage education and health services:** Municipios are primarily responsible for basic education (ensino fundamental) and primary health care under the SUS (Sistema Unico de Saude) decentralized framework.

**Participatory Budgeting (Orcamento Participativo, OP):** Pioneered in Porto Alegre in 1989 under the Workers' Party (PT) administration, participatory budgeting allows citizens to deliberate and decide on the allocation of a portion of the municipal budget through community assemblies. The Porto Alegre model has been studied extensively (Boaventura de Sousa Santos, Archon Fung, Brian Wampler) and replicated in over 1,500 municipalities worldwide. In Brazil, the practice has been adopted by municipalities across the political spectrum, though its depth and impact vary significantly. The City Statute (Estatuto da Cidade, Law 10.257/2001) provides the legal framework for democratic urban management, requiring public participation in master plan development.

**Fundo de Participacao dos Municipios (FPM):** Municipalities receive 24.5% of federal income tax and IPI revenue through the FPM, distributed based on population-weighted criteria. Smaller municipalities are disproportionately dependent on FPM transfers, creating what scholars call the "fiscal illusion" of municipal autonomy without fiscal self-sufficiency. The tax reform (Constitutional Amendment 132/2023) will restructure intergovernmental fiscal relations as the new IBS (Imposto sobre Bens e Servicos) replaces the ISS.

### Mexico: Usos y Costumbres

Mexico's Article 115 (reformed significantly in 1983 and 1999) establishes the municipio libre (free municipality) as the basis of territorial organization, governed by an ayuntamiento (municipal council) headed by the presidente municipal (municipal president), elected by direct vote.

**Indigenous Self-Governance (Usos y Costumbres):** In the state of Oaxaca, Law of the Rights of the Indigenous Peoples and Communities of the State of Oaxaca (2001) recognizes the right of indigenous communities to elect their authorities through their own normative systems (usos y costumbres), including communal assemblies, cargo systems (a rotating system of community service), and consensus-based decision-making. As of 2024, approximately 417 of Oaxaca's 570 municipalities govern through usos y costumbres rather than partisan elections. The SCJN and the TEPJF have addressed the tension between usos y costumbres and individual rights (particularly women's political participation rights) in cases such as *SUP-JDC-9167/2011* (Eufrosina Cruz Mendoza case), holding that usos y costumbres cannot exclude women from political participation.

### Colombia: Community Action Boards (Juntas de Accion Comunal, JAC)

Colombia's system of juntas de accion comunal (community action boards), regulated by Law 743 of 2002, constitutes one of the densest networks of community-level organizations in the Americas, with approximately 72,000 JACs involving over 6 million members. JACs operate at the neighborhood and village level, organizing community works (roads, water systems, community centers), mediating local disputes, and serving as interlocutors between communities and municipal governments. The relationship between JACs and formal local government is complementary but sometimes conflictual, particularly regarding the allocation of resources and the recognition of community priorities.

## Southeast Asia (Indonesia and Philippines)

### Indonesia: Village Governance and the Dana Desa

Indonesia's village governance has been transformed by the Village Law (Undang-Undang Desa, Law No. 6/2014), which establishes a comprehensive framework for village governance, recognizing both administrative villages (desa) and customary villages (desa adat).

**Key Provisions of the Village Law:**
- **Article 1:** Defines desa (village) as a legal community unit with territorial boundaries that is authorized to regulate and manage government affairs, local community interests, and community initiatives based on community initiative, origin rights, and/or traditional rights recognized and respected in the governance system of the Unitary State of the Republic of Indonesia.
- **Article 18:** The village head (kepala desa) is elected directly by village residents for a six-year term, renewable once.
- **Article 26:** Village heads have authority over village governance, community development, community empowerment, and community participation.
- **Article 54:** The Village Deliberation Assembly (Musyawarah Desa, Musdes) is the highest deliberative forum in the village, attended by the Village Consultative Body (BPD), the village government, and community representatives.
- **Article 72:** Village income sources include village original income (PADes), allocation from the APBN (national budget) -- the Dana Desa (Village Fund), part of the APBD (regional budget) -- the Alokasi Dana Desa (ADD), tax and retribution sharing, financial assistance from the APBD of provinces and districts/cities, grants, and donations.

**Dana Desa (Village Fund):** Established by Government Regulation No. 60/2014, the Dana Desa transfers funds directly from the national budget to approximately 75,000 villages. Since its inception in 2015, the Dana Desa has distributed over Rp 500 trillion (approximately USD 32 billion) cumulatively, funding infrastructure (roads, bridges, clean water systems), social services (village health posts, early childhood education), and economic empowerment programs. Research has shown significant impacts on rural infrastructure but also identified challenges including elite capture, corruption (the KPK has prosecuted numerous village heads for Dana Desa misappropriation), and the bureaucratization of village governance.

**Desa Adat (Customary Villages):** Article 96 of the Village Law recognizes the right of communities to establish desa adat based on origin rights (hak asal usul), with authority over customary norms, communal land management, and dispute resolution according to customary law. As of 2024, the implementation of desa adat has been limited, with only Bali comprehensively recognizing desa adat through Provincial Regulation No. 4/2019 on Desa Adat, which grants legal personality to Bali's approximately 1,493 desa adat alongside the formal administrative desa dinas.

### Philippines: The Local Government Code and Barangay Governance

The Local Government Code (RA 7160, 1991) is one of the most comprehensive decentralization statutes in Asia, devolving powers to four levels of local government: provinces, cities, municipalities, and barangays.

**The Barangay:** The barangay is the smallest political unit in the Philippines, with over 42,000 barangays nationwide, each governed by a Barangay Captain (Punong Barangay) and seven Barangay Kagawads (council members), plus the Sangguniang Kabataan (Youth Council) chair and the Barangay Secretary and Treasurer (ex officio). Barangays exercise authority over:

- **Section 389 (RA 7160):** The Punong Barangay enforces laws and ordinances applicable to the barangay, maintains public order, and calls and presides over the Sangguniang Barangay sessions.
- **Katarungang Pambarangay (Barangay Justice System, Sections 399-422):** A mandatory conciliation/mediation system for disputes between residents of the same or adjacent barangays, implemented through the Lupon Tagapamayapa (Peace Committee). No complaint involving disputes between parties residing in the same city/municipality shall be filed or instituted directly in court or any government office for adjudication unless the parties have undergone barangay conciliation, except in cases involving public officers, offenses punishable by imprisonment exceeding one year or a fine exceeding P5,000, and other specified exceptions.

**The Internal Revenue Allotment (IRA):**
- **Section 284 (RA 7160):** Local government units receive a share of national internal revenue taxes equal to 40% of the collections three fiscal years preceding the current fiscal year.
- **Distribution:** Provinces (23%), cities (23%), municipalities (34%), and barangays (20%).
- **Mandanas v. Ochoa (G.R. No. 199802, 2018):** The Supreme Court ruled that the IRA base should include all national taxes, not just Bureau of Internal Revenue collections, effectively increasing the IRA by approximately P200 billion annually beginning in 2022. The Court held that the Local Government Code's reference to "national internal revenue taxes" encompasses customs duties and other national tax collections.

**People's Organizations and NGO Participation:** Sections 34-36 of the Local Government Code require local government units to promote the establishment and operation of people's organizations and NGOs, and to provide for their representation in local special bodies (local development councils, school boards, health boards, peace and order councils). At least one-fourth of the members of local development councils must come from NGOs and people's organizations.

## Comparative Analysis

### Fiscal Decentralization vs. Administrative Decentralization

A consistent pattern across the Global South is the gap between administrative decentralization (the transfer of functions and responsibilities to local governments) and fiscal decentralization (the transfer of revenue-raising authority and financial resources). India's panchayats, despite constitutional mandates, generate very little own-source revenue, relying overwhelmingly on state and central transfers. Nigeria's LGAs are fiscally strangled by state control over the SJLGA (though the 2024 Supreme Court ruling may change this). Indonesia's kabupaten/kota receive approximately 90% of their revenue from central transfers (DAU, DAK, DBH). Even Brazil's municipios, despite having constitutionally assigned taxes, are heavily dependent on FPM transfers. Only South Africa's metropolitan municipalities have achieved significant own-source revenue through property rates and service charges, and this is limited to the major metros.

### Democratic Deepening vs. Elite Capture

Decentralization in the Global South has produced both democratic deepening (broader participation, accountability, responsiveness to local needs) and elite capture (local power holders using decentralized authority for personal enrichment and political control). India's panchayati raj has empowered women, Dalits, and Adivasis through reservation, while also creating new spaces for local caste and class politics. Indonesia's direct election of bupati/walikota has created responsive local leaders (such as Joko Widodo, who rose from Solo mayor to president) but also local dynasties and corruption networks. The Philippines' barangay system provides grassroots democratic institutions but is also the foundation for political machines and clientelist networks. Brazil's participatory budgeting represents a genuine innovation in democratic participation but requires sustained political commitment to function effectively.

### Customary Governance and Modern Local Government

The relationship between customary governance institutions and formal local government structures remains a central challenge. Nigeria's traditional rulers, Indonesia's adat communities, India's tribal councils under PESA, Mexico's usos y costumbres municipalities, South Africa's traditional authorities under Chapter 12 of the Constitution (Sections 211-212), and the Philippines' indigenous peoples' governance under the Indigenous Peoples Rights Act (RA 8371, 1997) all represent attempts to accommodate pre-existing governance systems within modern administrative frameworks. The tension between customary authority (often hierarchical, gender-stratified, and based on hereditary or communal status) and democratic principles (equality, individual rights, elected representation) is a persistent challenge for constitutional design in the Global South.
