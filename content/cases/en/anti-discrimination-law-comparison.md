## Summary

Anti-discrimination law -- the body of rules prohibiting differential treatment based on protected characteristics such as race, sex, religion, disability, age, and sexual orientation -- has expanded dramatically across all major legal systems over the past half century, yet the scope of protected categories, the conceptual frameworks for identifying discrimination, and the available remedies diverge in ways that reflect distinct constitutional traditions and social priorities. South Korea prohibits discrimination under Article 11 of the Constitution (대한민국 헌법 제11조), which guarantees equality before the law and prohibits discrimination in all areas of political, economic, social, and cultural life on grounds of sex, religion, or social status, while the National Human Rights Commission Act (국가인권위원회법) enacted in 2001 establishes the National Human Rights Commission of Korea (국가인권위원회) with investigatory and recommendatory powers covering 19 protected grounds; however, South Korea notably lacks a comprehensive Anti-Discrimination Act (차별금지법), with proposals having failed to pass the National Assembly on over ten occasions since 2007 due to opposition primarily centered on the inclusion of sexual orientation and gender identity as protected grounds. The United States has developed an extensive anti-discrimination framework through constitutional equal protection (Fourteenth Amendment), landmark federal statutes including the Civil Rights Act of 1964, the Age Discrimination in Employment Act of 1967, the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990, and Section 1983, with the Supreme Court applying tiered scrutiny analysis (strict, intermediate, rational basis) depending on the classification at issue. Germany prohibits discrimination through Article 3 of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz), which guarantees equality before the law and prohibits discrimination based on sex, parentage, race, language, homeland and origin, faith, and religious or political opinions, supplemented by the General Equal Treatment Act (Allgemeines Gleichbehandlungsgesetz, AGG) of 2006 implementing EU directives. The European framework provides the most comprehensive supranational anti-discrimination regime through ECHR Article 14, Protocol 12 (general prohibition of discrimination), the EU Charter Article 21, and a suite of equality directives including the Racial Equality Directive (2000/43/EC), the Employment Equality Directive (2000/78/EC), and the Gender Equality Directive (recast 2006/54/EC), with the Court of Justice of the European Union developing concepts of direct discrimination, indirect discrimination, harassment, and reasonable accommodation.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Article 11 of the Constitution provides: "(1) All citizens shall be equal before the law, and there shall be no discrimination in political, economic, social, or cultural life on account of sex, religion, or social status. (2) No privileged caste shall be recognized or ever established in any form."

The National Human Rights Commission Act (국가인권위원회법), enacted in 2001, established the National Human Rights Commission (NHRC, 국가인권위원회) as an independent institution with the mandate to investigate complaints of discrimination. Article 2(3) defines discriminatory acts as those involving unfavorable treatment based on 19 grounds including sex, religion, disability, age, social status, region of origin, national origin, ethnicity, physical condition, marital status, pregnancy, family status, race, skin color, ideology or political opinion, criminal record, sexual orientation, educational background, and medical history.

### Enforcement Limitations

The NHRC lacks binding enforcement power. It may investigate complaints, mediate disputes, and issue recommendations, but cannot impose penalties or order remedies. Compliance with NHRC recommendations is voluntary, and implementation rates have been inconsistent. This enforcement gap represents the most significant weakness in South Korea's anti-discrimination framework.

### Proposed Anti-Discrimination Act (차별금지법)

Comprehensive anti-discrimination legislation has been proposed in the National Assembly more than ten times since the first bill was introduced by then-Justice Minister Kim Seong-ho in 2007. Every proposal has failed to pass, primarily due to organized opposition from conservative religious groups who object to the inclusion of sexual orientation and gender identity as protected grounds. The most recent major attempt occurred in 2021-2022 when multiple competing bills were introduced by lawmakers from both the ruling and opposition parties. The ongoing failure to enact comprehensive legislation has been criticized by the UN Human Rights Council, the UN Committee on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights, and numerous international human rights organizations.

### Sector-Specific Protections

In the absence of comprehensive legislation, anti-discrimination protections exist in scattered sectoral laws:

| Law | Protected Ground | Scope |
|-----|-----------------|-------|
| Equal Employment Opportunity Act (남녀고용평등법) | Sex | Employment |
| Act on Prohibition of Age Discrimination in Employment (고용상 연령차별금지법) | Age | Employment |
| Act on Prohibition of Discrimination Against Persons with Disabilities (장애인차별금지법) | Disability | All areas |
| National Human Rights Commission Act (국가인권위원회법) | 19 grounds | All areas (advisory) |

## United States

### Legal Framework

The Fourteenth Amendment (1868) provides: "No State shall... deny to any person within its jurisdiction the equal protection of the laws." The Supreme Court applies tiered scrutiny:

| Classification | Scrutiny Level | Standard |
|---------------|---------------|----------|
| Race, national origin | Strict scrutiny | Narrowly tailored to a compelling governmental interest |
| Sex, gender | Intermediate scrutiny | Substantially related to an important governmental interest |
| Age, disability, sexual orientation | Rational basis (with variations) | Rationally related to a legitimate governmental interest |

### Federal Civil Rights Statutes

**Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 (42 U.S.C. Section 2000e):** Prohibits employment discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, and national origin. The Supreme Court in Bostock v. Clayton County, 590 U.S. 644 (2020), held that Title VII's prohibition on sex discrimination encompasses discrimination based on sexual orientation and transgender status.

**Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990 (ADA, 42 U.S.C. Section 12101):** Prohibits disability discrimination in employment (Title I), public services (Title II), and public accommodations (Title III). Requires reasonable accommodations for qualified individuals with disabilities.

**Age Discrimination in Employment Act of 1967 (ADEA, 29 U.S.C. Section 621):** Prohibits employment discrimination against individuals aged 40 and older.

**Fair Housing Act of 1968 (42 U.S.C. Section 3601):** Prohibits discrimination in housing based on race, color, religion, sex, national origin, familial status, and disability.

### Key Doctrinal Developments

**Griggs v. Duke Power Co., 401 U.S. 424 (1971):** Established the disparate impact theory, holding that employment practices that are facially neutral but disproportionately burden a protected class are unlawful unless justified by business necessity.

**McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green, 411 U.S. 792 (1973):** Established the burden-shifting framework for individual disparate treatment claims: plaintiff establishes prima facie case; employer articulates legitimate, non-discriminatory reason; plaintiff demonstrates pretext.

**Students for Fair Admissions v. Harvard, 600 U.S. 181 (2023):** Held that race-conscious admissions programs at Harvard and the University of North Carolina violated the Equal Protection Clause, effectively ending affirmative action in higher education admissions.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Article 3 of the Basic Law provides: "(1) All persons shall be equal before the law. (2) Men and women shall have equal rights... (3) No person shall be favoured or disfavoured because of sex, parentage, race, language, homeland and origin, faith, or religious or political opinions. No person shall be disfavoured because of disability."

### Allgemeines Gleichbehandlungsgesetz (AGG, 2006)

The AGG, enacted on August 14, 2006, implements four EU anti-discrimination directives into German law. Key provisions:

**Protected Grounds (Section 1):** Race, ethnic origin, sex, religion or belief, disability, age, and sexual orientation.

**Scope (Sections 2, 6-24):** Employment (including hiring, promotion, training, and dismissal), social protection (including social security and healthcare), education, and access to goods and services, including housing. Race and ethnic origin receive the broadest protection, covering all of these areas. Religion, disability, age, and sexual orientation are protected primarily in employment.

**Forms of Discrimination:**
- Direct discrimination (Section 3(1)): Less favorable treatment because of a protected ground
- Indirect discrimination (Section 3(2)): Apparently neutral provisions that disproportionately disadvantage persons with a protected characteristic
- Harassment (Section 3(3)): Unwanted conduct that violates a person's dignity
- Sexual harassment (Section 3(4)): Unwanted conduct of a sexual nature
- Instruction to discriminate (Section 3(5))

**Remedies (Section 15):** Compensation for material damage and appropriate monetary compensation for non-material damage. Claims must be filed within two months of the discriminatory act. The AGG does not provide for punitive damages.

### Federal Anti-Discrimination Agency (Antidiskriminierungsstelle des Bundes)

The FADA, established under Section 25 AGG, serves as the independent body responsible for supporting persons affected by discrimination, conducting research, publishing reports, and making recommendations. Like South Korea's NHRC, the FADA lacks binding enforcement power but plays an important advisory and awareness-raising role.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Article 14 and Protocol 12

Article 14 prohibits discrimination in the enjoyment of Convention rights and freedoms. Protocol 12, opened for signature in 2000, provides a freestanding prohibition of discrimination not limited to Convention rights. However, as of 2026, only 20 states have ratified Protocol 12, limiting its practical impact.

### EU Equality Directives

| Directive | Year | Protected Grounds | Scope |
|-----------|------|------------------|-------|
| Racial Equality Directive (2000/43/EC) | 2000 | Race, ethnic origin | Employment, education, social protection, goods and services |
| Employment Equality Directive (2000/78/EC) | 2000 | Religion, disability, age, sexual orientation | Employment only |
| Gender Equality Directive (recast, 2006/54/EC) | 2006 | Sex | Employment, pay, social security |
| Gender Goods and Services Directive (2004/113/EC) | 2004 | Sex | Access to goods and services |

### Key CJEU Jurisprudence

**Defrenne v. Sabena (Case 43/75, 1976):** Established the direct effect of the equal pay principle, holding that the principle of equal pay for equal work in the (then) EEC Treaty created individual rights enforceable in national courts.

**Mangold v. Helm (C-144/04, 2005):** Held that the general principle of non-discrimination on grounds of age is a general principle of EU law, applicable even before the transposition deadline of Directive 2000/78/EC.

**Achbita v. G4S Secure Solutions (C-157/15, 2017):** Held that an employer's internal rule prohibiting visible political, philosophical, or religious signs does not constitute direct discrimination on grounds of religion but may constitute indirect discrimination requiring justification.

### Key ECHR Jurisprudence

**D.H. and Others v. Czech Republic (Application No. 57325/00, Grand Chamber, 2007):** The Grand Chamber found that the disproportionate placement of Roma children in special schools constituted indirect racial discrimination in violation of Article 14 read with Article 2 of Protocol No. 1 (right to education), establishing statistical evidence as sufficient to shift the burden of proof.

**Thlimmenos v. Greece (Application No. 34369/97, 2000):** Established that Article 14 is violated not only when states treat differently persons in analogous situations without objective justification, but also when states fail to treat differently persons whose situations are significantly different.

## Comparative Analysis

### Scope of Protection

The jurisdictions reflect different approaches to defining protected grounds. The EU framework and Germany's AGG enumerate seven specific grounds. The United States covers a similar range through multiple statutes. South Korea's NHRC Act lists 19 grounds -- the broadest enumeration -- but lacks enforcement power, creating a paradox of broad definitional scope combined with weak implementation.

### Comprehensive vs. Sector-Specific Approach

Germany, the United States, and the EU have adopted comprehensive (though structurally different) anti-discrimination frameworks. South Korea remains the notable exception among developed democracies, relying on sector-specific statutes and a non-binding advisory body. This gap has significant practical consequences, particularly for groups such as LGBTQ+ individuals who lack enforceable legal protections outside the employment context.

### Enforcement Models

The United States provides the most robust private enforcement through private litigation with the possibility of compensatory and punitive damages. The EU framework requires member states to ensure "effective, proportionate and dissuasive" remedies and allows national courts to award compensation. Germany's AGG limits remedies to compensation and imposes a two-month filing deadline. South Korea's NHRC can only issue recommendations.

### Indirect Discrimination

The concept of indirect discrimination -- facially neutral rules that disproportionately affect protected groups -- is most developed in EU law and U.S. disparate impact doctrine. Germany has incorporated the EU concept through the AGG. South Korea lacks a fully developed indirect discrimination framework, though the NHRC has applied the concept in its advisory opinions.
