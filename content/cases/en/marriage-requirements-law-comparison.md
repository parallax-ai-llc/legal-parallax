## Summary

Marriage requirements -- the legal conditions that must be satisfied before a valid marriage can be formed -- constitute one of the most culturally variable yet legally consequential areas of family law, touching upon capacity, consent, prohibited degrees of relationship, formal solemnization, and registration. South Korea regulates marriage through Articles 807 through 814 of the Civil Act (민법), requiring mutual consent of the parties, a minimum age of 18 (raised from 16 for females and 18 for males by the 2007 amendment to achieve gender equality), absence of prohibited degrees of kinship, and formal registration with the family registry (가족관계등록부); the Constitutional Court of Korea has issued several landmark decisions on marriage, including the 2005 decision abolishing the Hoju (호주) patriarchal family registry system. The United States leaves marriage regulation primarily to the states under the Tenth Amendment, resulting in significant variation in minimum age requirements, waiting periods, blood test requirements, and officiants, while the Supreme Court's decision in Obergefell v. Hodges, 576 U.S. 644 (2015), established same-sex marriage as a fundamental right under the Fourteenth Amendment; the Respect for Marriage Act of 2022 further codified federal recognition of same-sex and interracial marriages. Germany governs marriage through Sections 1303 through 1322 of the Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB), requiring civil ceremony before a Standesbeamter (registrar), minimum age of 18 with no exceptions after the 2017 abolition of the judicial dispensation for 16-year-olds, and absence of impediments including prior undissolved marriage and close kinship. At the European level, Article 12 of the ECHR guarantees the right to marry, the CJEU has addressed cross-border marriage recognition in cases such as Coman v. Romania (C-673/16), and EU Regulation 2019/1111 (Brussels IIb) provides a framework for jurisdiction in matrimonial matters.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Marriage in South Korea is governed by Book IV (Family Law) of the Civil Act (민법), specifically Articles 807 through 814. South Korea follows a registration-based system in which marriage obtains legal validity only upon registration with the family relationship registry (가족관계등록부), regardless of whether any ceremony -- civil, religious, or traditional -- has been conducted. This system was fundamentally reformed in 2008 when the family relationship registration system replaced the traditional Hoju (호주) system following the Constitutional Court's 2005 landmark decision.

### Substantive Requirements

**Age:** Both parties must have attained the age of 18 (Article 807). Prior to the 2007 amendment, the minimum age was 18 for males and 16 for females. The amendment aligned the ages in accordance with the constitutional gender equality principle (Article 11 of the Constitution, 헌법 제11조).

**Consent:** The marriage must be based on the mutual, voluntary agreement of both parties (Article 808). For persons aged 18 who are still minors under Korean law (majority age being 19), parental consent is required (Article 808, Paragraph 1).

**Prohibited Relationships:** Marriage is prohibited between direct blood relatives (직계혈족) and between collateral blood relatives within the eighth degree of kinship (8촌 이내의 방계혈족) (Article 809). The original Article 809 also prohibited marriage between persons sharing the same surname and ancestral origin (동성동본 금혼), a remnant of Confucian tradition. This prohibition was declared unconstitutional by the Constitutional Court in 1997 (헌재결 1997.7.16. 95헌가6-13) and formally removed by the 2005 Civil Act amendment.

**Absence of Existing Marriage:** Neither party may have an existing undissolved marriage (Article 810). Bigamy is a criminal offense under Article 241 of the Criminal Act (형법), although the adultery provision of Article 241 was struck down by the Constitutional Court in 2015 (2009헌바17).

### Registration Process

Marriage registration is submitted to the local district office (시/군/구청) responsible for the family relationship registry. The application requires identification documents of both parties, two witnesses' signatures, and family relationship certificates. There is no mandatory waiting period or prior publication requirement.

### Constitutional Court Decisions

**Abolition of Hoju System (2005):** In its decision of February 3, 2005 (2001헌가9-15), the Constitutional Court declared the Hoju system -- which designated the eldest male as family head -- unconstitutional for violating gender equality. This led to the Family Relationship Registration Act of 2007 (가족관계의 등록 등에 관한 법률), which replaced the patrilineal family registry with an individual-centered registration system.

**Same-Surname Marriage Ban (1997):** The Constitutional Court declared the prohibition on marriage between persons of the same surname and ancestral origin unconstitutional, finding it an unreasonable restriction on the right to marry that lacked rational justification in modern society.

### Same-Sex Marriage

South Korea does not legally recognize same-sex marriage. In February 2023, the Seoul Western District Court dismissed a case seeking legal recognition of a same-sex marriage. However, in a landmark social insurance ruling in June 2023, the Supreme Court recognized a same-sex partner's right to dependent status under the National Health Insurance system, signaling potential evolution in legal treatment of same-sex relationships.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Marriage law in the United States is primarily a matter of state law, resulting in significant variation across jurisdictions. However, the U.S. Constitution, as interpreted by the Supreme Court, establishes fundamental parameters that all states must observe.

### Constitutional Framework

**Loving v. Virginia, 388 U.S. 1 (1967):** The Supreme Court struck down Virginia's ban on interracial marriage, holding that the freedom to marry is a fundamental right protected by the Due Process and Equal Protection Clauses of the Fourteenth Amendment.

**Obergefell v. Hodges, 576 U.S. 644 (2015):** The Court held that the Fourteenth Amendment requires all states to grant and recognize same-sex marriages, finding that the right to marry is a fundamental liberty that cannot be denied to same-sex couples.

**Respect for Marriage Act (2022):** Enacted on December 13, 2022, this federal statute requires the federal government to recognize any marriage that is valid in the state where it was performed, provides that states must give full faith and credit to out-of-state marriages regardless of sex, race, ethnicity, or national origin, and repealed the Defense of Marriage Act (DOMA) of 1996.

### State Requirements

| Requirement | Majority Rule | Notable Variations |
|-------------|---------------|-------------------|
| Minimum age without parental consent | 18 | Nebraska: 19; Mississippi: 21 (males), 15 (females) until recent reforms |
| Minimum age with parental consent | 16 in most states | Several states have eliminated child marriage entirely since 2018 |
| Waiting period | None in most states | Texas: 72 hours; South Carolina: 24 hours |
| Blood test | Not required in most states | Montana: still required; most states abolished by 2000s |
| License validity | 30-90 days | Varies by state |
| Common-law marriage | Not recognized in most states | Recognized in approximately 8 states plus D.C. |
| First-cousin marriage | Prohibited in approximately 25 states | Legal in approximately 20 states; restricted in approximately 5 |

### Child Marriage Reform Movement

Since 2016, a nationwide movement to end child marriage has resulted in significant legislative changes. Delaware became the first state to ban all marriages under age 18 (2016), followed by New Jersey (2018), Pennsylvania (2020), Minnesota (2020), and several others. As of 2025, approximately 12 states have set the minimum marriage age at 18 with no exceptions. Historically, most states permitted marriage at 16 or even younger with parental or judicial consent, with documented cases of children as young as 12 being married in some jurisdictions.

### Uniform Marriage and Divorce Act

The National Conference of Commissioners on Uniform State Laws promulgated the Uniform Marriage and Divorce Act (UMDA) in 1970, which has influenced but not been uniformly adopted across states. The UMDA established model provisions for marriage requirements, solemnization, and dissolution.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Marriage in Germany is governed by the Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB), specifically Sections 1303 through 1322. Germany follows the mandatory civil marriage principle (obligatorische Zivilehe), established since 1875 through the Reichsgesetz über die Beurkundung des Personenstandes und die Eheschließung (Imperial Civil Status Act). Only a civil ceremony performed before a Standesbeamter (civil registrar) creates a legally valid marriage.

### Substantive Requirements

**Age -- Section 1303 BGB:** Since the Gesetz zur Bekämpfung von Kinderehen (Act to Combat Child Marriages), effective July 22, 2017, the minimum marriage age is 18 with no exceptions. Previously, the family court could grant dispensation for 16-year-olds if the intended spouse was of full age. The 2017 reform also introduced provisions to annul marriages entered into abroad where one party was under 16 at the time of marriage, and to allow annulment where one party was between 16 and 18.

**Capacity to Consent -- Section 1304 BGB:** A person who is incapable of consenting (geschäftsunfähig) due to a pathological mental disorder may not enter into marriage.

**Prohibition of Bigamy -- Section 1306 BGB:** A marriage may not be entered into if one party is already bound by a valid marriage or registered partnership (Lebenspartnerschaft).

**Prohibited Relationships -- Section 1307 BGB:** Marriage between direct-line blood relatives (Verwandte in gerader Linie) and between full and half siblings (Geschwister) is prohibited. Unlike South Korea's former broad kinship prohibition, German law restricts the prohibition to close relatives.

**Waiting Period and Publication:** The historical requirement of an Aufgebot (public posting of intent to marry) was abolished in 1998. No mandatory waiting period exists.

### Same-Sex Marriage -- Eheöffnungsgesetz (2017)

On June 30, 2017, the Bundestag passed the Gesetz zur Einführung des Rechts auf Eheschließung für Personen gleichen Geschlechts (Act to Introduce the Right to Marriage for Persons of the Same Sex), which entered into force on October 1, 2017. The law amended Section 1353 BGB to read: "Marriage is entered into for life by two persons of different or the same sex." Existing registered partnerships (Lebenspartnerschaften) under the Lebenspartnerschaftsgesetz of 2001 could be converted into marriages upon application.

### Formal Requirements

| Step | Requirement |
|------|------------|
| Application (Anmeldung) | Both parties apply in person at the Standesamt |
| Documentation | Birth certificates, identification, proof of residence, proof that no impediment exists |
| Foreign nationals | Ehefähigkeitszeugnis (certificate of capacity to marry) from home country, or exemption by Oberlandesgericht |
| Ceremony | Civil ceremony before Standesbeamter with at least one witness (since 1998 reform; previously two witnesses required) |
| Registration | Entry in the marriage register (Eheregister) |

### Bundesverfassungsgericht (Federal Constitutional Court) Jurisprudence

The Federal Constitutional Court has addressed marriage requirements in several significant decisions. In BVerfGE 105, 313 (2002), the Court upheld the Lebenspartnerschaftsgesetz, holding that extending partnership rights to same-sex couples did not violate the constitutional protection of marriage under Article 6(1) of the Basic Law. In its 2013 decision (1 BvR 1164/07), the Court held that excluding same-sex life partners from the income-splitting tax benefit (Ehegattensplitting) violated the equality principle of Article 3(1) GG.

## ECHR & EU

### Article 12 ECHR -- Right to Marry

Article 12 of the European Convention on Human Rights provides: "Men and women of marriageable age have the right to marry and to found a family, according to the national laws governing the exercise of this right." The European Court of Human Rights has interpreted this provision to afford states a wide margin of appreciation regarding the conditions and restrictions on marriage.

**Schalk and Kopf v. Austria (Application No. 30141/04, Judgment of June 24, 2010):** The Court held that Article 12 does not impose an obligation on states to grant same-sex couples access to marriage. However, the Court acknowledged that it could no longer consider the right to marry as exclusively limited to marriage between persons of the opposite sex, noting evolving European and international consensus. The Court also found no violation of Article 14 taken in conjunction with Article 8.

**Hämäläinen v. Finland (Application No. 37359/09, Grand Chamber Judgment of July 16, 2014):** The Court held that Finland's requirement that a married person undergoing gender reassignment obtain spousal consent to maintain the marriage (or convert it to a registered partnership) did not violate Articles 8, 12, or 14. The judgment reaffirmed states' margin of appreciation in regulating marriage.

**Muñoz Díaz v. Spain (Application No. 49151/07, Judgment of December 8, 2009):** The Court found a violation of Article 14 taken in conjunction with Article 1 of Protocol 1 where Spain refused to recognize a Roma marriage for the purpose of a survivor's pension, despite having treated the couple as married for other purposes.

### EU Framework

**Regulation (EU) 2019/1111 -- Brussels IIb:** This Regulation, which replaced Brussels IIa (Regulation 2201/2003) effective August 1, 2022, establishes rules on jurisdiction, recognition, and enforcement of decisions in matrimonial matters and matters of parental responsibility. It does not harmonize substantive marriage requirements but ensures that marriages validly concluded in one member state are recognized in others for procedural purposes.

**Coman and Others v. Romania (Case C-673/16, CJEU Judgment of June 5, 2018):** In this landmark decision, the Court of Justice of the European Union held that the term "spouse" in the Free Movement Directive (2004/38/EC) includes same-sex spouses for the purposes of granting a derived right of residence to a third-country national. Romania was required to recognize a same-sex marriage concluded in Belgium for immigration purposes, even though Romanian law did not permit same-sex marriage.

**Pancharevo (Case C-490/20, CJEU Judgment of December 14, 2021):** The CJEU ruled that Bulgaria must recognize the parent-child relationship established in a birth certificate issued by Spain listing two mothers, for the purpose of issuing identity documents. The case extended the principles of Coman to parentage derived from same-sex marriages.

### Council of Europe Conventions

The European Convention on the Legal Status of Children Born out of Wedlock (1975) and the European Convention on Nationality (1997) address collateral issues related to marriage requirements, particularly regarding the rights of children and nationality consequences of marriage.

## Comparative Analysis

### Age Requirements

All four jurisdictions now require a minimum age of 18 for marriage, though the paths to this standard differ significantly. Germany's 2017 Kinderehen Act represented the most dramatic shift, eliminating all exceptions to the age-18 requirement and providing mechanisms to annul child marriages performed abroad. The United States remains the most fragmented, with ongoing state-by-state reform efforts. South Korea achieved a uniform age of 18 in 2007 by raising the female minimum age from 16.

### Civil vs. Religious Marriage

Germany maintains the strictest separation: only civil ceremonies create legal marriages, and religious ceremonies have no legal effect. South Korea similarly requires civil registration but has no formal ceremony requirement. The United States uniquely recognizes religious ceremonies as legally sufficient when performed by authorized officiants, and several states still recognize common-law marriages formed without any ceremony.

### Same-Sex Marriage

The jurisdictions span the full spectrum: the United States and Germany have legalized same-sex marriage through judicial and legislative action respectively, the ECHR and EU do not require states to permit same-sex marriage but mandate recognition for free movement purposes, and South Korea does not recognize same-sex marriage though incremental changes in social insurance rights suggest potential evolution.

### International Marriage Recognition

The EU framework through Brussels IIb and the Coman jurisprudence provides the most developed system for cross-border marriage recognition. The United States addresses this through the Full Faith and Credit Clause and the Respect for Marriage Act. South Korea and Germany apply private international law rules (국제사법 and EGBGB respectively) to determine the validity of marriages performed abroad.
