## Summary

Inheritance law -- the body of rules governing the transmission of property, rights, and obligations from a deceased person to their heirs and beneficiaries -- reflects some of the deepest cultural, familial, and economic values of a society, with fundamental divergences across jurisdictions on questions of testamentary freedom versus forced heirship, the rights of surviving spouses versus children, and the treatment of intestate succession. South Korea's inheritance law, governed by Part V of the Civil Act (민법 제5편 상속), mandates a system of statutory shares (법정상속분) under Article 1009 with a compulsory share (유류분) system under Articles 1112-1118 that protects close family members from complete disinheritance, limiting testamentary freedom to the portion not reserved for forced heirs; the statutory intestacy scheme prioritizes lineal descendants, lineal ascendants, siblings, and collateral relatives within the fourth degree, with the surviving spouse receiving a supplementary share (가산분) of 50 percent above a co-inheriting child's share; the Constitutional Court's 2005 decision (2001헌가9-15) abolishing the patrilineal family headship system (호주제) and subsequent 2014 decisions on inheritance gender equality have modernized Korean succession law. The United States follows a primarily common law tradition emphasizing near-absolute testamentary freedom, with the only significant limitation being the surviving spouse's elective share (available in most states), while intestacy is governed by state law often influenced by the Uniform Probate Code (UPC, most recently revised in 2019); the Supreme Court's decisions in cases including Hillman v. Maretta, 569 U.S. 483 (2013) and Sveen v. Melin, 584 U.S. 171 (2018) address federal preemption and constitutional issues in succession law. Germany's inheritance law (Erbrecht), governed by Book 5 of the Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB Sections 1922-2385), combines testamentary freedom with a robust compulsory portion (Pflichtteil) system that guarantees excluded statutory heirs half of their intestate share as a monetary claim, a system upheld by the Bundesverfassungsgericht in its landmark 2005 decision (BVerfGE 112, 332) as constitutionally mandated by Articles 6(1) and 14(1) of the Grundgesetz. The European framework includes the EU Succession Regulation (No 650/2012), which establishes uniform conflict-of-law rules allowing individuals to choose the law of their nationality to govern their succession, the ECHR's protection of inheritance rights under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 and Article 14 (non-discrimination), and the European Certificate of Succession facilitating cross-border estate administration.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Korean inheritance law is governed by Part V of the Civil Act (민법 제5편 상속편), comprising Articles 997 through 1118.

### Order of Intestate Succession (법정상속순위)

**Article 1000:** Statutory heirs in order of priority:

| Priority | Heirs | Civil Act |
|----------|-------|-----------|
| 1st | Lineal descendants (직계비속) | Art. 1000(1)(1) |
| 2nd | Lineal ascendants (직계존속) | Art. 1000(1)(2) |
| 3rd | Siblings (형제자매) | Art. 1000(1)(3) |
| 4th | Collateral relatives within 4th degree (4촌 이내의 방계혈족) | Art. 1000(1)(4) |

**Surviving Spouse (배우자):** Under Article 1003, the surviving spouse co-inherits with heirs of the first or second priority. When co-inheriting with lineal descendants, the spouse receives a share equal to 1.5 times the share of each child. When co-inheriting with lineal ascendants, the spouse receives a share equal to 1.5 times each ascendant's share. If there are no first or second priority heirs, the surviving spouse is the sole heir.

### Compulsory Share (유류분)

**Articles 1112-1118:** The compulsory share system protects close family members from complete disinheritance:

| Heir | Compulsory Share (유류분) |
|------|------------------------|
| Lineal descendants | 50% of their statutory intestate share |
| Surviving spouse | 50% of their statutory intestate share |
| Lineal ascendants | 33% of their statutory intestate share |
| Siblings | 33% of their statutory intestate share |

The compulsory share is enforced through a compulsory share restitution claim (유류분반환청구권, Article 1115), which must be exercised within one year of learning of the infringement or ten years of the commencement of succession.

### Will Forms

**Article 1060:** Korean law recognizes five forms of will:

| Form | Article | Requirements |
|------|---------|-------------|
| Holographic (자필증서) | Art. 1066 | Entirely handwritten; date, address, name, seal/signature |
| Recorded (녹음) | Art. 1067 | Oral recording with witness attestation |
| Notarized (공정증서) | Art. 1068 | Before notary with 2 witnesses |
| Sealed (비밀증서) | Art. 1069 | Sealed document; presented to notary with 2 witnesses |
| Oral (구수증서) | Art. 1070 | Emergency; 2 witnesses; court confirmation within 7 days |

### Qualified Acceptance and Renunciation

Heirs may accept the inheritance without limitation (단순승인, Article 1025), with limitation (한정승인, Article 1028, accepting only to the extent of inherited assets), or renounce (상속포기, Article 1041). Qualified acceptance and renunciation must be declared to the family court within three months of learning of the succession.

### Gender Equality Reforms

Historically, Korean inheritance law heavily favored sons. The Constitutional Court's 2005 decision abolishing the patrilineal family headship system (호주제) and subsequent equal inheritance share requirements have eliminated gender-based discrimination in succession law. Under current law, all children of the decedent inherit equally regardless of gender.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Inheritance law in the United States is primarily governed by state law, with the Uniform Probate Code (UPC) serving as a widely adopted model statute (adopted in whole or in part by approximately 18 states).

### Testamentary Freedom

The United States provides near-absolute testamentary freedom -- a testator may disinherit children, parents, and siblings without limitation. The only significant restriction is the surviving spouse's elective share.

**Elective Share:** In common law property states, the surviving spouse may elect to take a statutory share of the deceased spouse's estate regardless of the will's provisions. The UPC (Section 2-202) provides a graduated elective share ranging from 3% to 50% based on the length of the marriage, supplemented by a $75,000 minimum. State variations are significant:

| State | Elective Share |
|-------|---------------|
| New York | Greater of $50,000 or one-third of augmented estate |
| Florida | 30% of augmented estate |
| UPC states | Graduated 3-50% based on marriage length |
| Community property states | No elective share (surviving spouse already owns half) |

### Intestacy

The UPC provides a model intestacy scheme:

| Situation | Surviving Spouse's Share |
|-----------|------------------------|
| No descendants or parents | Entire estate |
| All descendants are also spouse's descendants | First $150,000 + 50% of remainder |
| Spouse has descendants not shared with decedent | First $100,000 + 50% of remainder |
| Decedent has descendants not shared with spouse | First $150,000 + 50% of remainder |

### Federal Estate Tax

The federal estate tax applies to estates exceeding $13.61 million (2024 exemption, indexed for inflation) at a maximum rate of 40%. The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 doubled the exemption from approximately $5.5 million, but this increase is scheduled to expire after December 31, 2025, reverting to approximately $7 million (adjusted for inflation). The unlimited marital deduction (26 U.S.C. Section 2056) allows tax-free transfers to the surviving spouse.

### Will Formalities

Most states require a witnessed will (typically signed by the testator in the presence of at least two witnesses). The UPC permits holographic wills (Section 2-502) if the material portions are in the testator's handwriting. Electronic wills are increasingly recognized, with Nevada (2001), Indiana (2018), and several other states having enacted e-will statutes.

### Trust Law

The United States has developed extensive trust law as a succession planning tool. Revocable living trusts (inter vivos trusts) are widely used to avoid probate, provide for incapacity, and achieve tax planning objectives. The Uniform Trust Code (UTC, 2000) has been adopted in over 35 states.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German inheritance law (Erbrecht) is governed by Book 5 of the BGB (Sections 1922-2385). The foundational principle is universal succession (Gesamtrechtsnachfolge, Section 1922): upon death, the entire estate passes directly to the heir(s) as a whole, including both assets and liabilities.

### Intestate Succession

German intestacy follows the parentelic system (Parentelsystem):

| Order | Heirs | BGB Section |
|-------|-------|-------------|
| 1st Order | Descendants (Abkömmlinge) | Section 1924 |
| 2nd Order | Parents and their descendants (siblings, nephews/nieces) | Section 1925 |
| 3rd Order | Grandparents and their descendants | Section 1926 |
| 4th+ Order | Great-grandparents, etc. | Section 1928+ |

**Surviving Spouse:** The surviving spouse's intestate share depends on the number of children and the matrimonial property regime:

| Situation | Spouse's Share |
|-----------|---------------|
| With children (Zugewinngemeinschaft) | 25% + 25% flat-rate Zugewinnausgleich = 50% |
| With children (Gütertrennung) | Equal share with children; at least 25% |
| Without children (with parents/siblings) | 50% + 25% Zugewinnausgleich = 75% |
| No relatives of 1st or 2nd order | 100% |

### Compulsory Portion (Pflichtteil)

**BGB Sections 2303-2338:** The Pflichtteil guarantees excluded statutory heirs a monetary claim against the estate equal to half of the value of their intestate share. Unlike South Korean and French forced heirship, the German Pflichtteil is purely a monetary claim (Geldanspruch), not a share of the estate itself. The heir has no right to specific assets or to participate in estate administration.

**Eligible claimants:** Descendants, parents (if no descendants), and the surviving spouse.

**BVerfG Decision (BVerfGE 112, 332, April 19, 2005):** The Federal Constitutional Court held that the Pflichtteil is constitutionally mandated by Article 14(1) (property and inheritance) and Article 6(1) (protection of marriage and family) of the Grundgesetz. Complete disinheritance of children would violate the constitutional minimum participation in the deceased's estate that flows from the family relationship.

### Will Forms

**BGB Section 2231:** Two forms of will:

| Form | Requirements |
|------|-------------|
| Holographic (eigenhändiges Testament, Section 2247) | Entirely handwritten; signed; date and place recommended |
| Notarial (öffentliches Testament, Section 2232) | Declared orally to notary or written document submitted to notary |

**Joint Will (gemeinschaftliches Testament, Section 2265):** Available only to spouses; permits mutual provisions including the "Berliner Testament" (mutual appointment as sole heir with children as final heirs), the most common testamentary arrangement in Germany.

### Inheritance Tax

The Erbschaftsteuergesetz (ErbStG) imposes inheritance tax based on the relationship between the deceased and the heir:

| Tax Class | Relationship | Exemption | Rate |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|------|
| I | Spouse, children, grandchildren | 500,000 EUR (spouse); 400,000 EUR (children) | 7-30% |
| II | Siblings, nieces/nephews, in-laws | 20,000 EUR | 15-43% |
| III | All others | 20,000 EUR | 30-50% |

## ECHR & EU

### EU Succession Regulation (No 650/2012)

The EU Succession Regulation, effective August 17, 2015, establishes uniform conflict-of-law rules for cross-border successions within the EU (except Denmark and Ireland).

**Applicable Law:** The general rule is that the law of the deceased's last habitual residence governs the entire succession (Article 21). However, a person may choose the law of their nationality to govern their succession (Article 22).

**European Certificate of Succession:** The Regulation creates a European Certificate of Succession (Article 62), which can be used to prove the status of heir, legatee, executor, or administrator in any participating member state without further formalities.

**Adaptation:** The Regulation addresses the adaptation of rights in rem (Article 31), providing that where a right created or transferred under the applicable succession law is unknown in the member state where it is invoked, the right shall be adapted to the closest equivalent right under that state's law.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Marckx v. Belgium (Application No. 6833/74, June 13, 1979):** The Court found violations of Articles 8 and 14 in Belgian inheritance law that discriminated against children born out of wedlock and their mothers. This landmark decision required equal treatment of children regardless of the circumstances of their birth.

**Inze v. Austria (Application No. 8695/79, October 28, 1987):** The Court found a violation of Article 14 read with Article 1 Protocol 1 in Austrian inheritance law that gave preferential treatment to legitimate children over illegitimate children in the succession of agricultural property.

**Fabris v. France (Application No. 16574/08, Grand Chamber, February 7, 2013):** The Grand Chamber found a violation of Article 14 and Article 1 Protocol 1 where transitional legislation prevented a child born out of wedlock from benefiting from a new law equalizing inheritance rights.

**Briani and Others v. Italy (Application No. 33817/18, November 14, 2023):** The Court addressed the right of heirs to challenge a will on grounds of undue influence under Article 1 Protocol 1.

### ECHR Article 1 Protocol 1

The right to peaceful enjoyment of possessions under Article 1 Protocol 1 applies to inheritance:

- Inheritance rights constitute "possessions" where they have been recognized by domestic law
- States enjoy a wide margin of appreciation in regulating inheritance
- Discriminatory inheritance rules may violate Article 14 read with Article 1 Protocol 1
- Compulsory heirship systems are compatible with the Convention (margin of appreciation)

### Variation in Forced Heirship

| Country | System | Share |
|---------|--------|-------|
| South Korea | Compulsory share (유류분) | 50% of statutory share (descendants/spouse); 33% (others) |
| Germany | Pflichtteil (monetary claim) | 50% of statutory share |
| France | Réserve héréditaire | 50% (1 child); 66% (2 children); 75% (3+ children) |
| Italy | Legittima | Varies by family composition |
| England & Wales | None (family provision possible) | Reasonable financial provision |
| United States | None (spousal elective share only) | Varies by state |

## Comparative Analysis

### Testamentary Freedom vs. Forced Heirship

The most fundamental divergence is between the Anglo-American tradition of near-absolute testamentary freedom (United States, England) and the civil law tradition of forced heirship (South Korea, Germany, France). The United States allows a testator to disinherit children entirely, while South Korea and Germany guarantee children a compulsory share. This reflects different conceptions of family obligation and individual autonomy.

### Universal vs. Administered Succession

Germany and South Korea follow the universal succession principle, under which the estate passes directly to heirs by operation of law at the moment of death. The United States follows an administered succession model, under which the estate passes through a personal representative (executor/administrator) who manages the probate process. The universal succession model provides immediate rights to heirs but also immediate liability for estate debts; the administered model provides orderly settlement but delays beneficiaries' access.

### Spousal Protection

The mechanisms for spousal protection differ fundamentally. In the United States, the elective share provides a safety net against disinheritance. In Germany, the Zugewinnausgleich (gain equalization) operates alongside the Pflichtteil to protect the surviving spouse. In South Korea, the spouse's supplementary share (1.5x) in intestacy and compulsory share system provides protection. All systems recognize the surviving spouse's priority, but the mechanisms reflect different conceptions of marriage's economic partnership.

### Taxation

Inheritance/estate tax structures vary dramatically. The United States exempts estates below $13.61 million (effectively exempting over 99% of estates), while Germany and South Korea tax with lower exemptions but graduated rates and family-class-based thresholds. The U.S. approach concentrates taxation on the very wealthy; the European and Korean approaches tax a broader base at lower rates.
