## Summary

Gerrymandering -- the deliberate manipulation of electoral district boundaries to advantage or disadvantage particular political parties, candidates, or demographic groups -- represents one of the most persistent threats to democratic representation, capable of producing legislative majorities that diverge dramatically from the preferences of the electorate. South Korea's constituency delimitation is governed by the Public Official Election Act (공직선거법) and conducted by the National Assembly Constituency Delimitation Committee (국회의원지역선거구획정위원회), an independent body that operates under Constitutional Court constraints requiring maximum population deviation of 2:1 between the largest and smallest constituencies, as established in the landmark decisions of 2001HunMa560 (2001) and 2012HunMa192 (2014), though the Committee's composition and the National Assembly's final approval authority have generated persistent allegations of partisan manipulation. The United States has the most extensive gerrymandering jurisprudence of any country, with the Supreme Court holding in Rucho v. Common Cause, 588 U.S. 684 (2019), that partisan gerrymandering claims present nonjusticiable political questions under the federal Constitution, while racial gerrymandering remains subject to strict scrutiny under the Equal Protection Clause and the Voting Rights Act of 1965 (52 U.S.C. §§ 10301-10314), leaving redistricting primarily to state legislatures with growing adoption of independent redistricting commissions. Germany's electoral district delimitation is conducted by the permanent Wahlkreiskommission (Electoral District Commission) under the Bundeswahlgesetz (BWahlG, Federal Election Act), with the mixed-member proportional (MMP) representation system (personalisiertes Verhältniswahlrecht) substantially mitigating the effects of constituency-level gerrymandering by compensating for distortions through proportional list seats, while the EU framework, including the Venice Commission's Code of Good Practice in Electoral Matters (CDL-AD(2002)023) and the ECHR's Article 3 of Protocol No. 1 (right to free elections), establishes international standards for fair and impartial boundary delimitation, with the Venice Commission recommending that redistricting be conducted by independent bodies using objective criteria.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea uses a parallel mixed electoral system combining single-member constituency seats with proportional representation seats, though the proportional component does not compensate for constituency-level distortions (unlike Germany's MMP system).

**Constituency Delimitation Committee (국회의원지역선거구획정위원회):**
Established under Article 24 of the Public Official Election Act, the Committee is constituted approximately one year before each National Assembly election:

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Composition | 9 members (3 nominated by National Assembly Speaker; 3 by ruling party; 3 by opposition) |
| Independence | Members serve independently; no instructions from appointing authorities |
| Criteria | Population equality; administrative boundaries; geographic proximity; transportation |
| Timeline | Report submitted to National Assembly 13 months before election |
| Final authority | National Assembly enacts redistricting plan through legislation |

### Constitutional Court Jurisprudence

**Decision 95HunMa224 (1995):** The Court first addressed malapportionment, finding a violation of electoral equality where the population ratio between the largest and smallest constituencies exceeded 4:1.

**Decision 2001HunMa560 (2001):** The Court established a maximum permissible population deviation of 3:1 (later tightened), requiring redistricting to achieve greater equality.

**Decision 2012HunMa192 (2014):** The landmark decision tightened the maximum permissible population deviation to 2:1 (each constituency must be within 33.3 percent of the national average constituency population). This decision required significant redistricting and eliminated several severely malapportioned rural constituencies.

**Decision 2018HunMa928 (2020):** The Court upheld the 2:1 ratio but urged the legislature to consider moving toward a stricter standard, noting that many democracies apply ratios of 1.5:1 or less.

### Partisan Gerrymandering Concerns

While the Constitutional Court has addressed population equality, partisan gerrymandering remains an issue:
- The National Assembly retains final authority to approve or modify the Delimitation Committee's recommendations
- The Committee's composition (party-nominated members) creates potential for partisan influence
- Historical examples include the division of opposition strongholds to dilute voting power
- The parallel proportional representation component (46 seats out of 300) provides limited mitigation compared to Germany's compensatory MMP system

### Urban-Rural Divide

South Korea's population concentration in the Seoul Capital Area (approximately 50 percent of the national population) creates structural tensions:
- The 2:1 population deviation standard allows significant overrepresentation of rural constituencies
- Rural legislators resist further tightening of the deviation standard
- Urban constituencies in Seoul and Gyeonggi Province are systematically underrepresented relative to rural areas

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has the most extensive and contested redistricting landscape of any democracy.

**Constitutional Framework:**
- Article I, Section 2: House members apportioned among states based on population (decennial census)
- Article I, Section 4 (Elections Clause): "The Times, Places and Manner of holding Elections for Senators and Representatives, shall be prescribed in each State by the Legislature thereof; but the Congress may at any time by Law make or alter such Regulations"
- Fourteenth Amendment, Equal Protection Clause: basis for one-person-one-vote and racial gerrymandering claims

### Supreme Court Jurisprudence

**Baker v. Carr, 369 U.S. 186 (1962):** Malapportionment claims are justiciable under the Equal Protection Clause, overruling the political question doctrine's previous application.

**Reynolds v. Sims, 377 U.S. 533 (1964):** "One person, one vote" -- both chambers of a state legislature must be apportioned on the basis of population.

**Shaw v. Reno, 509 U.S. 630 (1993):** Racial gerrymandering (drawing districts primarily on the basis of race) is subject to strict scrutiny under the Equal Protection Clause, even when intended to benefit minority groups.

**Rucho v. Common Cause, 588 U.S. 684 (2019):** The Court held 5-4 that partisan gerrymandering claims present nonjusticiable political questions under the federal Constitution. Chief Justice Roberts wrote that federal courts lack "manageable standards" to adjudicate such claims. Justice Kagan's dissent characterized partisan gerrymandering as "the most partisan decision of this Court."

**Allen v. Milligan, 599 U.S. 1 (2023):** The Court (5-4) upheld Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act's application to redistricting, rejecting Alabama's argument that race-neutral redistricting algorithms could supersede VRA analysis. The decision preserved the VRA's role as a constraint on racial gerrymandering.

### Redistricting Bodies

| Type | States | Examples |
|------|--------|---------|
| State legislature (exclusive) | ~30 states | Texas, North Carolina, Ohio |
| Independent commission | ~8 states | California (CRC), Arizona (IRC), Michigan (ICRC) |
| Politician commission | ~4 states | New Jersey, Hawaii |
| Hybrid/advisory commission | ~8 states | Iowa (advisory), Virginia (bipartisan commission) |

**California Citizens Redistricting Commission (CRC):** Established by Proposition 11 (2008) and Proposition 20 (2010), the CRC comprises 14 members (5 Democrats, 5 Republicans, 4 from neither party) selected through a rigorous application and random selection process. The commission draws Congressional, State Senate, State Assembly, and Board of Equalization districts. Its criteria, in priority order: compliance with federal law; geographic contiguity; respect for city, county, and community boundaries; compactness; nesting.

### Quantitative Approaches

Modern redistricting litigation increasingly relies on quantitative measures:
- **Efficiency gap:** Measures "wasted votes" (votes for losing candidates + excess votes for winning candidates beyond what was needed to win)
- **Partisan symmetry:** Whether each party would win the same proportion of seats given the same proportion of votes
- **Mean-median difference:** Difference between a party's mean vote share and median vote share across districts
- **Ensemble analysis:** Comparing enacted maps against algorithmically generated ensembles of maps meeting neutral criteria

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's mixed-member proportional (MMP) system fundamentally reduces the impact of gerrymandering by compensating for constituency-level distortions through proportional list seats.

**Bundeswahlgesetz (BWahlG, Federal Election Act):**

**Electoral System (§ 1 BWahlG):** Each voter casts two votes: a first vote (Erststimme) for a constituency candidate (FPTP, 299 constituencies) and a second vote (Zweitstimme) for a party list. Total Bundestag seats are allocated proportionally based on second votes, with constituency seats deducted from the proportional allocation.

**Wahlkreiskommission (Electoral District Commission, § 3 BWahlG):**

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Composition | President of the Federal Statistical Office (chair) + 1 federal judge (BVerwG) + 5 further members |
| Appointment | Appointed by the Federal President |
| Term | Permanent (appointed for the Bundestag electoral term) |
| Task | Report to the Federal Government within 15 months of each federal election on population changes and recommended boundary adjustments |
| Criteria | Population: maximum 15% deviation from average (25% triggers mandatory redistricting); geographic contiguity; Länder boundaries; municipal boundaries |

**Population Deviation Standards (§ 3(1) BWahlG):**

| Deviation | Consequence |
|-----------|-------------|
| Up to 15% from average | Permissible |
| 15-25% from average | Adjustment should be considered |
| Over 25% from average | Mandatory redistricting |

### MMP as Anti-Gerrymandering Mechanism

The proportional compensation mechanism of Germany's MMP system serves as an inherent anti-gerrymandering feature:
- Even if constituency boundaries are drawn to favor one party, total Bundestag seats are allocated proportionally
- Overhang seats (Überhangmandate) -- where a party wins more constituency seats than its proportional share entitles -- are now fully compensated through additional leveling seats (Ausgleichsmandate) since the 2013 reform
- The 2023 reform (effective for the 2025 election) eliminated overhang and leveling seats entirely, capping the Bundestag at 630 seats and eliminating constituency-based distortions through a "second vote coverage" requirement

**BVerfGE 130, 212 (2012, Negative Voting Weight):** The Court struck down the then-existing electoral law for creating negative voting weight (negatives Stimmgewicht), where a party gaining more votes in one Land could paradoxically lose seats nationally. The Court required that the electoral system produce results consistent with proportional representation principles.

### State Level

Länder elections use various electoral systems, mostly proportional or mixed, further reducing gerrymandering concerns at the subnational level.

## ECHR / EU

### Venice Commission Standards

The Venice Commission's Code of Good Practice in Electoral Matters (CDL-AD(2002)023) provides the most authoritative international guidelines on boundary delimitation:

**Section I.2 -- Equality of Voting Power:**
- Constituency seats must be distributed proportionally to population
- Maximum permissible deviation: 10 percent from the national average; never more than 15 percent except in special geographic circumstances
- Population data must be based on a census conducted no more than 10 years before delimitation

**Section II.2 -- Boundary Delimitation Standards:**
- Delimitation must be conducted by an independent body (preferably including geographers, sociologists, and balanced political representation)
- The body should be permanent rather than ad hoc
- Criteria: population equality; geographic continuity; respect for existing administrative boundaries; compactness
- Partisan advantage should not be a criterion
- Redistricting should occur at least every 10 years

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Yumak and Sadak v. Turkey (Application No. 10226/03, Grand Chamber, July 8, 2008):** The Court upheld Turkey's 10 percent national electoral threshold, finding no violation of Article 3 of Protocol No. 1. While the threshold was the highest in Europe, the Court found it fell within Turkey's margin of appreciation given the aim of ensuring governmental stability and preventing parliamentary fragmentation. The decision establishes that states have wide discretion in choosing electoral systems.

**Mathieu-Mohin and Clerfayt v. Belgium (Application No. 9267/81, March 2, 1987):** The foundational case on Article 3, Protocol No. 1. The Court held that the right to free elections implies universal suffrage, the right to stand for election, and the principle that elections must be held at reasonable intervals by secret ballot. States enjoy a wide margin of appreciation in organizing electoral systems, but the essence of the right must not be impaired.

**Oran v. Turkey (Application No. 28881/07, April 15, 2014):** The Court addressed the interaction between constituency design and electoral equality, noting that significant malapportionment could raise issues under Article 3, Protocol No. 1.

### OSCE/ODIHR Standards

The OSCE Office for Democratic Institutions and Human Rights (ODIHR) provides election observation and assessment, including evaluation of redistricting processes:
- Redistricting should be based on transparent, predetermined criteria
- The process should involve public participation and consultation
- Boundary changes close to elections should be avoided
- ODIHR has criticized redistricting processes in multiple Council of Europe member states

## Comparative Analysis

### Electoral System as the Primary Safeguard

The most effective structural safeguard against gerrymandering is proportional representation, not judicial review:

**Germany (MMP):** Proportional compensation eliminates the ability of gerrymandered constituencies to distort overall representation. This makes gerrymandering of constituency boundaries largely irrelevant to party composition of the Bundestag.

**South Korea (parallel mixed):** The limited proportional component (46/300 seats) provides minimal correction for constituency-level distortions.

**United States (FPTP):** Pure first-past-the-post elections make gerrymandering maximally effective, as there is no compensatory mechanism.

### Institutional Design

Redistricting institutions vary in independence:
- **Germany:** Most independent (permanent, non-partisan commission)
- **United States (commission states):** Independent but recent (California since 2010; Michigan since 2018)
- **South Korea:** Semi-independent (party-nominated members; National Assembly final approval)
- **United States (legislature states):** Least independent (partisan legislatures draw their own districts)

### Population Equality Standards

| Jurisdiction | Standard | Stringency |
|-------------|----------|-----------|
| United States (federal) | Near-zero deviation (Kirkpatrick v. Preisler) | Most strict |
| Venice Commission | 10% deviation; max 15% | Moderate |
| Germany | 15% deviation; mandatory at 25% | Moderate |
| South Korea | 2:1 ratio (~33% deviation) | Least strict |

### Technology and Gerrymandering

Modern technology has transformed both the practice of gerrymandering and the tools available to detect it:

**Gerrymandering Technology:**
- Geographic Information Systems (GIS) allow precise manipulation of district boundaries at the census block level
- Voter file data combined with partisan modeling enables sophisticated partisan optimization
- "Auto-redistricting" algorithms can generate maps maximizing partisan advantage while meeting all legal criteria

**Detection Technology:**
- **Ensemble analysis:** Algorithms generate thousands of maps meeting neutral criteria; the enacted map is compared against this ensemble to assess partisan outlier status
- **Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC):** Statistical sampling method producing representative samples of valid redistricting plans
- **DRA (Dave's Redistricting App):** Publicly available tool enabling citizens to create and evaluate redistricting plans

**Jurisprudential Impact:** While the Supreme Court in Rucho held that federal courts lack manageable standards for partisan gerrymandering, several state courts have adopted quantitative measures:
- **North Carolina (Harper v. Hall, 2022):** State Supreme Court used statistical analysis to find partisan gerrymandering violated the state constitution (later reversed after court composition changed)
- **Ohio (League of Women Voters of Ohio v. Ohio Redistricting Commission, 2022):** State court repeatedly rejected gerrymandered maps as violating state constitutional requirements
- **Pennsylvania (League of Women Voters v. Commonwealth, 2018):** State Supreme Court struck down Congressional map as partisan gerrymandering under state constitutional free and equal elections clause

### Prison Gerrymandering

An emerging issue in redistricting involves the counting of incarcerated populations:
- Under traditional census methodology, prisoners are counted at the facility location rather than their home address
- This practice inflates the population of rural districts with prisons while deflating urban districts where most prisoners' families reside
- **United States:** At least 12 states have enacted legislation to count prisoners at their home addresses for redistricting purposes
- **South Korea and Germany:** Prison populations are too small relative to constituency size to create significant distortions

### Future of Redistricting

The trend toward independent redistricting commissions continues to grow:
- In the United States, citizen-initiated ballot measures have created commissions in Michigan (2018), Virginia (2020, advisory), and Colorado (2018)
- The Fair Maps Act and other federal proposals would require independent redistricting nationwide, though no federal legislation has been enacted
- International best practice (Venice Commission) increasingly favors independent, transparent, criteria-based redistricting

### Minority Representation and Redistricting

The intersection of redistricting and minority representation raises distinct legal issues:

- **United States:** Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act requires creation of "majority-minority" districts where minority populations are sufficiently large and geographically compact; Allen v. Milligan (2023) reaffirmed the Gingles test for Section 2 claims; tension between Section 2 requirements and the racial gerrymandering prohibition (Shaw v. Reno, 1993) creates a "constitutional straightjacket"
- **South Korea:** No minority representation requirement in constituency design; the Constitutional Court has focused on population equality rather than community of interest criteria
- **Germany:** BWahlG does not require minority representation in constituency design; the party list system (Zweitstimme) provides alternative representation pathways; special exemption from the 5 percent threshold for recognized national minorities (Grundmandatsklausel for SSW, the Danish minority party in Schleswig-Holstein)
- **ECHR:** The Court has held that electoral systems must not systematically disadvantage ethnic minorities (Sejdic and Finci v. Bosnia and Herzegovina, Grand Chamber, 2009), though states retain wide discretion in electoral system design
