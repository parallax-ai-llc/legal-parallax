## Summary

Domestic violence -- the pattern of coercive, threatening, or violent behavior exercised by one person over another within a familial or intimate relationship -- has undergone a dramatic transformation in legal treatment worldwide over the past three decades. South Korea's dual-statute framework, comprising the Act on the Prevention of Domestic Violence and Protection of Victims (가정폭력방지법) and the Act on Special Cases Concerning the Punishment of Crimes of Domestic Violence (가정폭력처벌법), was enacted in 1997 and has been progressively amended to expand the definition of domestic violence and strengthen protective orders. The United States addresses domestic violence through a combination of state criminal codes and the federal Violence Against Women Act (VAWA), first enacted in 1994 and most recently reauthorized in 2022, which provides funding, enforcement mechanisms, and expanded protections including tribal jurisdiction over non-Native perpetrators. Germany's Gewaltschutzgesetz (Protection Against Violence Act), enacted in 2002 and amended in 2021, introduced civil protection orders and the principle that perpetrators -- not victims -- should be removed from the shared home, with a 2025 draft amendment proposing electronic ankle monitoring of offenders. At the supranational level, the Council of Europe's Istanbul Convention (2011) established the most comprehensive international framework for combating domestic violence, structuring state obligations around four pillars -- prevention, protection, prosecution, and coordinated policies -- while the European Court of Human Rights has imposed positive obligations on states to protect domestic violence victims under Articles 2, 3, 8, and 14 of the ECHR.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's domestic violence framework rests on two complementary statutes, both enacted in 1997 and amended on several occasions (notably in 2011, 2014, and 2020):

**Act on the Prevention of Domestic Violence and Protection of Victims (가정폭력방지법):** This statute establishes the administrative and social welfare infrastructure for addressing domestic violence, including counseling centers, shelters, and public education programs. The state and local governments bear responsibility for prevention policies, and the Act mandates domestic violence awareness training for public officials.

**Act on Special Cases Concerning the Punishment of Crimes of Domestic Violence (가정폭력처벌법):** This statute governs the criminal justice response. Domestic violence crimes are defined as acts of assault, injury, abandonment, abuse, arrest, confinement, and intimidation between family members that inflict physical, mental, or property damage. Under the October 2020 amendments, the definition was expanded to include home invasion, noncompliance with eviction orders, and crimes of illegal filming.

| Category | Provision | Scope |
|----------|-----------|-------|
| Violence / Assault | Criminal Act Art. 260 | Physical force against family member |
| Bodily Injury | Criminal Act Art. 257 | Causing diagnosable physical harm |
| Confinement | Criminal Act Art. 276 | Restricting freedom of movement |
| Intimidation / Threats | Criminal Act Art. 283 | Threatening harm against family member |
| Property Destruction | Criminal Act Art. 366 | Destroying family member's property |
| Illegal Filming | Special Act (2020 amendment) | Non-consensual recording within the home |

### Protective Measures

Under the Special Act, a judge may issue emergency temporary measures and formal protective dispositions:

**Emergency Temporary Measures:** Police officers responding to domestic violence calls may separate the perpetrator from the victim, issue an emergency restraining order for up to 72 hours, and refer the case to the prosecution for a formal protective disposition request.

**Protective Dispositions (보호처분):** A family court judge may order the offender's access to victims or family members restricted in person or through telecommunications, restrict parental authority, order social service attendance, place the offender on probation, entrust custody in protective facilities, or refer the offender to medical or counseling institutions. These dispositions can last up to six months, with one six-month extension permitted.

**Victim Protection Orders:** Courts may order the removal of the offender from the shared residence, denial of access within 100 meters of the victim's residence or workplace, prohibition of contact through telecommunications, and restriction on the exercise of parental rights.

### Criticisms and Ongoing Reform

South Korea's domestic violence framework has faced significant criticism. Historically, the system's reliance on family preservation (가정보전) led to a tendency to treat domestic violence as a private family matter rather than a criminal offense. The controversial clause allowing victims to block prosecution was removed from the anti-stalking law in 2023, but similar complainant-dependent prosecution mechanisms still affect some domestic violence cases. Nine bills relating to dating violence are currently pending in the National Assembly, proposing either new dating violence prevention legislation or amendments to existing domestic violence and stalking laws to include dating relationships.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Domestic violence law in the United States operates across federal, state, local, and tribal jurisdictions. At the federal level, the Violence Against Women Act (VAWA) serves as the primary legislative framework, while individual states maintain their own criminal statutes, protective order systems, and law enforcement protocols.

**Violence Against Women Act (VAWA):** Originally enacted in 1994 as Title IV of the Violent Crime Control and Law Enforcement Act, VAWA has been reauthorized in 2000, 2005, 2013, and most recently in 2022 as Division W of the Consolidated Appropriations Act. VAWA does not itself create a freestanding federal domestic violence crime but provides federal funding for investigation and prosecution, establishes grant programs, and creates federal offenses related to interstate domestic violence and violations of protection orders.

**Key Federal Provisions:**

| Provision | Statute | Effect |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| Interstate domestic violence | 18 U.S.C. Section 2261 | Federal crime to cross state lines to commit domestic violence |
| Interstate violation of protection order | 18 U.S.C. Section 2262 | Federal crime to cross state lines to violate a protection order |
| Firearms prohibition | 18 U.S.C. Section 922(g)(8)-(9) | Prohibits firearm possession by persons subject to restraining orders or convicted of domestic violence misdemeanors |
| Full faith and credit for protection orders | 18 U.S.C. Section 2265 | Requires all states to enforce protection orders issued by other states |
| STOP Grant Program | 34 U.S.C. Section 10441 | Formula grants to states for law enforcement, prosecution, and victim services |
| Sexual Assault Services Program (SASP) | 34 U.S.C. Section 12511 | Federal funding for rape crisis centers and sexual assault services |

### 2022 Reauthorization

The VAWA Reauthorization Act of 2022 introduced several significant expansions:

**Expanded Tribal Jurisdiction:** The Act recognized expanded special tribal criminal jurisdiction over non-Native perpetrators of sexual violence, child violence, stalking, assaults on tribal law enforcement officers, and sex trafficking on tribal lands, in addition to the domestic and dating violence jurisdiction established in 2013.

**Technology-Facilitated Abuse:** New programs address cyberstalking, the nonconsensual dissemination of intimate images, and other forms of technology-facilitated gender-based violence.

**Housing Protections:** The Act improved existing housing protections under VAWA and increased access to emergency and short-term housing for survivors.

**Culturally Specific Services:** Dedicated investments in culturally specific service providers ensure survivors of color, immigrant survivors, and LGBTQ+ survivors receive appropriate support.

### Landmark Case: United States v. Rahimi (2024)

In 2024, the Supreme Court ruled 8-1 in United States v. Rahimi to uphold the constitutionality of 18 U.S.C. Section 922(g)(8), which prohibits individuals subject to domestic violence restraining orders from possessing firearms. The Court held that this restriction is consistent with the nation's historical tradition of firearm regulation, affirming the government's authority to disarm individuals who pose a credible threat to the physical safety of others.

### State-Level Variation

State domestic violence laws vary significantly in their definitions of protected relationships, available protective orders, mandatory arrest policies, and penalties:

| Element | Mandatory Arrest States | Discretionary States |
|---------|------------------------|---------------------|
| Arrest policy | Officer must arrest if probable cause exists | Officer exercises discretion |
| Number of states | Approximately 22 states + D.C. | Approximately 28 states |
| Rationale | Remove discretion, protect victims | Respect officer judgment, avoid unintended consequences |
| Dual arrest concern | Higher risk of both parties arrested | Lower dual arrest rates |

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany addresses domestic violence through a combination of criminal law provisions in the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), the civil law Gewaltschutzgesetz (Protection Against Violence Act), and police law provisions at the state (Laender) level. This multi-layered approach reflects the principle that domestic violence is simultaneously a criminal justice matter, a civil rights matter, and a public safety matter.

**Gewaltschutzgesetz (Protection Against Violence Act, 2002):** This landmark statute, which came into force on January 1, 2002, introduced two fundamental innovations into German domestic violence law:

1. **Perpetrator removal principle (Wer schlaegt, der geht -- "Whoever hits, goes"):** The Act established the principle that perpetrators, not victims, should leave the shared home. Courts can order the exclusive use of the shared dwelling by the victim for at least six months.

2. **Civil protection orders:** The Act empowers civil courts to issue stay-away orders (Naeherungsverbote) prohibiting the offender from entering the victim's home, approaching the victim, or initiating contact. These orders may be issued for an unlimited period.

**Criminal Law Provisions (StGB):**

| Offense | Section | Penalty |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Simple bodily harm | Section 223 | Up to 5 years / fine |
| Dangerous bodily harm | Section 224 | 6 months - 10 years |
| Abuse of persons in care | Section 225 | 6 months - 10 years |
| Grievous bodily harm | Section 226 | 1-10 years |
| Threats | Section 241 | Up to 3 years / fine |
| Stalking (Nachstellung) | Section 238 | Up to 3 years / fine |
| Coercion | Section 240 | Up to 3 years / fine |

**Police Law (Polizeigesetz):** Each of Germany's 16 federal states has enacted police law provisions authorizing police officers to issue immediate eviction orders (Platzverweis or Wohnungsverweisung) against domestic violence perpetrators. These orders typically last 10 to 14 days, providing a bridge period during which victims can apply for civil protection orders.

### 2021 Amendment and 2025 Draft Reform

The Gewaltschutzgesetz was amended in 2021 to strengthen enforcement provisions. In January 2025, the German government published a draft for a further amendment that would introduce:

**Electronic Monitoring (Elektronische Aufenthaltsueberwachung):** Family courts would be empowered to order electronic ankle monitors for domestic violence offenders for up to three months, with a possible three-month extension. An alert would be triggered if the offender violates the protection order, enabling police to respond preventively and notify the victim.

**Warning Devices for Victims:** Victims may be provided with devices that alert them when perpetrators approach within close proximity, enhancing personal safety.

**Mandatory Perpetrator Programs (Taeterarbeit):** Courts would be authorized to mandate offender participation in structured social training programs, including anti-violence courses.

Violations of court protection orders under the Gewaltschutzgesetz are punishable by imprisonment of up to one year or by a fine under Section 4 of the Act.

### Institutional Framework

Germany's domestic violence intervention system operates through a network of approximately 350 women's shelters (Frauenhaeuser), over 40 intervention centers (Interventionsstellen), and specialized counseling services. The Federal Ministry for Family Affairs, Senior Citizens, Women and Youth (BMFSFJ) operates the national domestic violence hotline (Hilfetelefon) offering 24/7 multilingual support.

## European Framework

### Istanbul Convention

The Council of Europe Convention on Preventing and Combating Violence Against Women and Domestic Violence (Istanbul Convention), adopted on April 7, 2011, and entered into force on August 1, 2014, represents the most comprehensive international treaty addressing domestic violence. As of 2025, 39 Council of Europe member states have ratified the Convention, and the European Union itself ratified on June 28, 2023 (entering into force October 1, 2023).

**The Four Pillars:**

| Pillar | Obligation | Key Articles |
|--------|-----------|--------------|
| Prevention | Awareness campaigns, education, training of professionals, perpetrator programs | Articles 12-17 |
| Protection | Emergency barring orders, restraining orders, shelters, helplines, support services | Articles 18-28 |
| Prosecution | Criminalization of physical violence, sexual violence, stalking, forced marriage, FGM, psychological violence | Articles 29-48 |
| Integrated Policies | National action plans, coordination bodies, data collection, research | Articles 7-11 |

**Monitoring Mechanism -- GREVIO:** The Group of Experts on Action Against Violence Against Women and Domestic Violence (GREVIO), an independent expert body, monitors implementation through country-by-country evaluation reports and publishes recommendations for strengthening compliance.

**Ratification Challenges:** Despite its comprehensive framework, the Istanbul Convention has faced political resistance. Turkey, one of the Convention's first signatories, withdrew on July 1, 2021. In September 2025, Latvia's Saeima voted to begin the process of withdrawing from the Convention. Bulgaria, Czechia, Hungary, Lithuania, and Slovakia remain EU member states that have not ratified the treaty.

### ECHR Jurisprudence on Domestic Violence

The European Court of Human Rights has developed a substantial body of case law imposing positive obligations on states to protect individuals from domestic violence:

**Opuz v. Turkey (2009), App. No. 33401/02:** In this landmark judgment, the Court found violations of Articles 2 (right to life), 3 (prohibition of torture/inhuman treatment), and 14 (prohibition of discrimination) in a case where the applicant's mother was killed by the applicant's husband despite repeated complaints to authorities. The Court held that the state's failure to protect the victims amounted to a violation of positive obligations, and that the overall unresponsiveness of the judicial system to domestic violence constituted gender-based discrimination.

**Bevacqua and S. v. Bulgaria (2008), App. No. 71127/01:** The Court found a violation of Article 8 (right to private and family life) where the Bulgarian authorities treated ongoing domestic violence as a "private matter" and failed to adopt sufficient measures to protect the applicant and her child.

**Eremia v. Republic of Moldova (2013), App. No. 3564/11:** The Court held that Moldova violated Articles 3, 8, and 14 where a police officer subjected his wife to repeated domestic violence and the authorities failed to enforce a protection order. The judgment emphasized that judicial tolerance of domestic violence perpetuates discrimination against women.

**Talpis v. Italy (2017), App. No. 41237/14:** The Court found Italy violated Articles 2, 3, and 14 due to authorities' failure to act promptly on the applicant's complaints of domestic violence, which ultimately resulted in the murder of her son and attempted murder of the applicant by her husband.

**Kurt v. Austria (2021), App. No. 62903/15 (Grand Chamber):** The Grand Chamber refined the standard for assessing state responsibility in domestic violence cases, holding that authorities must conduct an autonomous, proactive, and comprehensive risk assessment when domestic violence is reported, rather than relying primarily on the victim's own assessment of the risk.

## Comparative Analysis

| Dimension | South Korea | United States | Germany | European Framework |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|---------|-------------------|
| **Primary statute** | Special Act (1997) / Prevention Act (1997) | VAWA (1994, reauth. 2022) + state codes | Gewaltschutzgesetz (2002) + StGB | Istanbul Convention (2011) / ECHR |
| **Legal tradition** | Civil law (German-influenced) | Common law (federal system) | Civil law (continental) | Supranational treaty law |
| **Definition scope** | Family members (blood, marriage, de facto) | Varies by state; VAWA covers spouses, cohabitants, dating partners | All persons regardless of relationship | Gender-based violence and domestic violence broadly |
| **Protection order system** | Victim protection orders via family court | Civil protection orders (state), full faith and credit (federal) | Civil stay-away orders (unlimited duration) | Mandatory emergency barring orders (Art. 52) |
| **Perpetrator removal** | Available via protective disposition | Available via protection orders (varies by state) | Core principle ("Wer schlaegt, der geht") | Required under Art. 52 |
| **Firearms restriction** | Not a primary mechanism | 18 U.S.C. Section 922(g)(8)-(9); upheld in Rahimi (2024) | Weapons Act restrictions | Not specifically addressed |
| **Electronic monitoring** | Not yet implemented | Available in some states | Proposed in 2025 draft amendment | Encouraged under Istanbul Convention |
| **Mandatory arrest** | Not mandatory; emergency separation available | Mandatory in approx. 22 states + D.C. | Not mandatory; police eviction orders available | Not specified; left to states |
| **Victim consent to prosecute** | Some offenses require victim complaint | Generally no; state prosecutes regardless | Prosecution on complaint for simple bodily harm | States must ensure ex officio prosecution (Istanbul Conv. Art. 55) |
| **Specialized courts** | Family court handles protective dispositions | Specialized domestic violence courts in some jurisdictions | Family courts handle protection orders | Not specified |
| **Shelters / services** | Government-funded counseling centers and shelters | VAWA-funded services; National DV Hotline | ~350 women's shelters; national Hilfetelefon | Mandatory under Istanbul Convention Art. 23 |
| **Monitoring body** | Ministry of Gender Equality and Family | DOJ Office on Violence Against Women | BMFSFJ | GREVIO |

## Career Timeline

This section traces the historical development of domestic violence law across the compared jurisdictions.

- **1871:** Germany's Reichsstrafgesetzbuch codifies bodily harm offenses but contains no specific domestic violence provisions; violence within marriage is largely considered a private matter.
- **1950:** The European Convention on Human Rights is opened for signature; Article 3 establishes the absolute prohibition on torture and inhuman or degrading treatment, though its application to domestic violence would not be recognized for decades.
- **1953:** South Korea enacts its Criminal Act (Act No. 293), including assault and injury offenses; domestic violence is not addressed as a distinct category.
- **1976:** Pennsylvania enacts the first U.S. state Protection from Abuse Act, pioneering the civil protection order model for domestic violence.
- **1984:** The U.S. Attorney General's Task Force on Family Violence recommends treating domestic violence as a criminal matter rather than a family dispute.
- **1994:** The United States enacts the Violence Against Women Act (VAWA) as Title IV of the Violent Crime Control and Law Enforcement Act, creating federal remedies and funding for domestic violence services.
- **1997:** South Korea enacts both the Act on the Prevention of Domestic Violence and Protection of Victims (가정폭력방지법) and the Act on Special Cases Concerning the Punishment of Crimes of Domestic Violence (가정폭력처벌법), establishing the first comprehensive domestic violence legal framework in Korean history.
- **2000:** VAWA is reauthorized with expanded provisions for immigrant victims, legal aid, and supervised visitation programs.
- **2002:** Germany enacts the Gewaltschutzgesetz (Protection Against Violence Act), establishing the "Wer schlaegt, der geht" principle and civil protection orders.
- **2005:** VAWA is reauthorized a second time, adding provisions for healthcare professionals, culturally specific services, and prevention programs.
- **2008:** Bevacqua and S. v. Bulgaria establishes that states violate Article 8 ECHR by treating domestic violence as a "private matter."
- **2009:** Opuz v. Turkey becomes the landmark ECHR judgment on domestic violence, finding violations of Articles 2, 3, and 14 and establishing that systemic failure to protect victims constitutes gender-based discrimination.
- **2011:** The Council of Europe adopts the Istanbul Convention on April 7, establishing the most comprehensive international treaty on preventing and combating violence against women and domestic violence.
- **2013:** VAWA is reauthorized with expanded coverage for same-sex partners, Native American tribal jurisdiction over non-Native perpetrators, and immigrant protections. The Eremia v. Republic of Moldova judgment reinforces positive obligations under the ECHR.
- **2014:** The Istanbul Convention enters into force on August 1 after receiving the required ten ratifications. South Korea amends its domestic violence statutes to strengthen protective dispositions.
- **2017:** Talpis v. Italy underscores state liability for failure to act promptly on domestic violence complaints.
- **2020:** South Korea amends the Special Act for the Punishment of Domestic Violence to expand the definition of domestic violence crimes to include home invasion, noncompliance with eviction, and illegal filming.
- **2021:** Turkey withdraws from the Istanbul Convention on July 1, provoking international condemnation. Germany amends the Gewaltschutzgesetz to strengthen enforcement. The Grand Chamber in Kurt v. Austria refines the state obligation to conduct autonomous risk assessments in domestic violence cases.
- **2022:** VAWA is reauthorized for the fourth time as part of the Consolidated Appropriations Act, expanding tribal jurisdiction, addressing technology-facilitated abuse, and increasing housing protections.
- **2023:** The European Union ratifies the Istanbul Convention on June 28 (effective October 1, 2023), binding all EU member states to its provisions on matters within EU competence. South Korea removes the victim-consent clause from the anti-stalking law.
- **2024:** The U.S. Supreme Court upholds the domestic violence firearms prohibition in United States v. Rahimi (8-1). The Department of Justice announces over $690 million in VAWA grant funding for FY 2024. VAWA celebrates its 30th anniversary.
- **2025:** Germany publishes a draft amendment to the Gewaltschutzgesetz introducing electronic ankle monitoring for domestic violence offenders and mandatory perpetrator intervention programs. Latvia begins the process of withdrawing from the Istanbul Convention.
