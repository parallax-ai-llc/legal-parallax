## Summary

Anti-doping regulation operates at the intersection of sports governance, criminal law, and human rights, with a global harmonization framework centered on the World Anti-Doping Code (WADC) administered by the World Anti-Doping Agency (WADA), supplemented by national legislation that varies dramatically in scope and severity. South Korea enacted the Anti-Doping in Sport Act (스포츠에서의 도핑 방지에 관한 법률) in 2007, establishing the Korea Anti-Doping Agency (한국도핑방지위원회, KADA) as the national anti-doping organization responsible for testing, education, and results management; South Korea ratified the UNESCO International Convention against Doping in Sport and has achieved WADA Code compliance, though its anti-doping framework does not criminalize individual athlete doping, focusing instead on administrative sanctions through sports federations. The United States operates a dual framework: the U.S. Anti-Doping Agency (USADA) administers the WADA Code for Olympic and Paralympic sports, while professional leagues (NFL, NBA, MLB, NHL) maintain independent anti-doping programs outside WADA jurisdiction; the Rodchenkov Anti-Doping Act (21 U.S.C. Section 2401 et seq., enacted 2020) created the first U.S. federal criminal statute specifically targeting international doping fraud conspiracies, imposing penalties of up to 10 years imprisonment for schemes involving performance-enhancing drugs at international sporting events in which U.S. athletes, sponsors, or broadcasters participate. Germany enacted one of the world's most comprehensive anti-doping criminal statutes, the Anti-Doping Act (Anti-Doping-Gesetz, AntiDopG) of 2015, which criminalizes not only the trafficking and administration of prohibited substances but also self-doping by elite athletes, with penalties of up to 3 years imprisonment for self-doping and up to 10 years for trafficking; the Act was a direct response to decades of systematic doping in East German sports (documented by the Franke-Berendonk research) and ongoing revelations in professional cycling, athletics, and other sports. At the international level, WADA administers the World Anti-Doping Code (most recently revised in 2021, effective January 1, 2021), which harmonizes anti-doping rules across 700+ sports organizations and 190+ national anti-doping organizations; the Court of Arbitration for Sport (CAS) in Lausanne serves as the supreme appellate body for doping disputes, with landmark decisions including CAS 2020/O/6689 (WADA v. RUSADA), which imposed a two-year ban on Russian athletes competing under their national flag following the state-sponsored doping scandal; the Council of Europe's Anti-Doping Convention (1989) and its Additional Protocol (2002) established the first binding international legal framework for government anti-doping cooperation.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

**Anti-Doping in Sport Act (스포츠에서의 도핑 방지에 관한 법률, 2007):** This statute establishes the legal framework for anti-doping activities in South Korea, including:
- Establishment and governance of the Korea Anti-Doping Agency (KADA)
- Adoption of WADA Code anti-doping rule violations
- Testing procedures and athlete whereabouts requirements
- Results management and sanctions
- Education and prevention programs

The Act does not criminalize self-doping by athletes. Anti-doping rule violations result in administrative sanctions -- primarily competition bans -- imposed by sports federations in accordance with the WADA Code.

**KADA (한국도핑방지위원회):** Established in 2006, KADA serves as South Korea's national anti-doping organization, conducting approximately 5,000-6,000 doping tests annually (in-competition and out-of-competition). KADA operates a WADA-accredited laboratory at the Korea Institute of Science and Technology (KIST) in Seoul.

### Notable Cases

**Park Tae-hwan (Swimming):** South Korea's most celebrated swimmer, Park Tae-hwan (2008 Olympic gold medalist in 400m freestyle), tested positive for testosterone in 2014 and received an 18-month ban from the Court of Arbitration for Sport. The case generated intense public debate about the Korean Olympic Committee's initial attempt to block Park from the 2016 Rio Olympics beyond his ban period, which was overturned after legal challenge.

**Korean Baseball Organization (KBO):** The KBO has maintained its own anti-doping program since 2007, conducting approximately 1,000 tests annually. Several players have received suspensions for positive tests, though the KBO's sanctions (typically 50-80 game suspensions for first offenses) are generally considered less severe than those in Olympic sports.

### Therapeutic Use Exemptions

KADA manages the therapeutic use exemption (TUE) process for Korean athletes, which has been a source of controversy. The 2018 PyeongChang Winter Olympics heightened scrutiny of Korean anti-doping practices, with KADA implementing enhanced testing protocols and educational programs in the lead-up to the Games.

## United States

### Legal Framework

**USADA and Olympic Sports:** The United States Anti-Doping Agency, established in 2000, administers the anti-doping program for all Olympic and Paralympic sports in the United States under authority delegated by the U.S. Olympic & Paralympic Committee pursuant to the Ted Stevens Olympic and Amateur Sports Act (36 U.S.C. Sections 220501-220529). USADA conducts approximately 8,000-10,000 tests annually and operates one of the world's most aggressive out-of-competition testing programs.

**Rodchenkov Anti-Doping Act (21 U.S.C. Section 2401 et seq., 2020):** Named after Dr. Grigory Rodchenkov, the former director of Russia's WADA-accredited anti-doping laboratory who became a whistleblower exposing Russia's state-sponsored doping program, this federal criminal statute:
- Criminalizes participation in a scheme involving the use of prohibited substances or methods "in connection with a major international sport competition" in which U.S. athletes, sponsors, or broadcasters participate
- Imposes penalties of up to 10 years imprisonment and fines of up to $250,000 (or $1 million for organizations)
- Explicitly excludes individual athletes from prosecution (athletes cannot be charged under the Act for their own doping)
- Establishes extraterritorial jurisdiction over doping conspiracies affecting U.S. interests
- Includes whistleblower protections and financial rewards for individuals who provide information leading to successful prosecutions
- Authorizes restitution for defrauded athletes, sponsors, and event organizers

**Professional Sports League Programs:** U.S. professional sports leagues operate independent anti-doping programs outside WADA jurisdiction:

| League | Program | Key Features |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| NFL | NFL Policy on Performance-Enhancing Substances | Jointly administered with NFLPA; 6-game suspension for first PED offense; HGH blood testing since 2014 |
| MLB | Joint Drug Prevention and Treatment Program | 80-game suspension for first PED offense; 162 games for second; lifetime ban for third |
| NBA | NBA/NBPA Anti-Drug Agreement | 25-game suspension for first PED offense; biological passport program |
| NHL | NHL/NHLPA Performance Enhancing Substances Program | 20-game suspension for first offense; no HGH testing until recently |

### Key Cases

**BALCO Investigation (2003-2007):** The Bay Area Laboratory Co-operative scandal exposed a sophisticated designer steroid distribution network serving elite athletes including Marion Jones (track), Barry Bonds (baseball), and Jason Giambi (baseball). BALCO founder Victor Conte served four months in prison for conspiracy to distribute steroids. Marion Jones was sentenced to six months in prison for lying to federal investigators about her steroid use.

**Lance Armstrong/USADA Investigation (2012):** USADA issued a "reasoned decision" finding that Lance Armstrong had engaged in systematic doping throughout his cycling career, resulting in the stripping of his seven Tour de France titles. Armstrong was banned from sanctioned sport for life. The case demonstrated USADA's investigative capacity and the effectiveness of non-analytical evidence (testimony, financial records, communications) in doping cases.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

**Anti-Doping Act (Anti-Doping-Gesetz, AntiDopG, 2015):** Germany's anti-doping statute is among the world's most comprehensive, uniquely criminalizing self-doping by elite athletes.

**Key Provisions:**

| Section | Offense | Maximum Penalty |
|---------|---------|----------------|
| Section 2 | Prohibition on use of doping substances/methods in organized sport | Administrative/sporting sanction |
| Section 3 | Prohibition on possession of significant quantities of doping substances | Criminal |
| Section 4(1) | Manufacturing, trafficking, or prescribing doping substances | 3 years imprisonment (10 years for gang/commercial trafficking) |
| Section 4(2) | Application of doping substances to another person | 3 years imprisonment |
| Section 4(7) | Self-doping by elite athletes (Spitzensportler) | 3 years imprisonment |

The criminalization of self-doping under Section 4(7) applies only to "elite athletes" as defined by the Act -- those who generate significant revenue from sport, are members of a testing pool, or receive government sports funding. The provision was designed to target athletes who benefit financially from doping while avoiding criminalization of amateur and recreational athletes.

**Constitutional Challenges:** The criminalization of self-doping raises constitutional concerns under Article 2(1) GG (right to free development of personality) and Article 1(1) GG (human dignity). The Federal Constitutional Court has not directly ruled on the provision's constitutionality, though lower courts have applied it in several cases. Legal scholars remain divided on whether criminalizing what is essentially self-harm can be justified under the limited paternalism permitted by German constitutional law.

### Historical Context: East German Doping

Germany's aggressive anti-doping stance is directly connected to the systematic state-sponsored doping program of the German Democratic Republic (DDR). Between 1968 and 1989, approximately 10,000 athletes were administered anabolic steroids and other performance-enhancing substances -- often without their knowledge or consent -- under State Plan 14.25. The program was documented by researchers Werner Franke and Brigitte Berendonk and led to criminal proceedings after reunification. In 2000, the Berlin Regional Court convicted former GDR sports officials Manfred Ewald and Manfred Höppner for causing bodily harm to athletes. The Federal Doping Victims Aid Act (Dopingopfer-Hilfegesetz, 2002) established a compensation fund for victims of state-sponsored doping.

### NADA Germany

The National Anti-Doping Agency Germany (NADA), established in 2002, conducts approximately 15,000 tests annually and operates a comprehensive whereabouts program for elite athletes. NADA's results management function operates in coordination with the criminal investigation authority of the Bundeskriminalamt (BKA) and state prosecutors under the AntiDopG.

## ECHR / EU

### World Anti-Doping Code (2021)

The WADA Code (most recent version effective January 1, 2021) establishes harmonized anti-doping rules applicable to all Olympic and Paralympic sports and their member federations:

**Anti-Doping Rule Violations (Article 2):**
1. Presence of a prohibited substance
2. Use or attempted use
3. Evading, refusing, or failing to submit to sample collection
4. Whereabouts failures
5. Tampering with any part of doping control
6. Possession of a prohibited substance or method
7. Trafficking
8. Administration
9. Complicity
10. Prohibited association
11. Acts by athletes or other persons to discourage reporting

**Standard Sanctions:** First violation for presence/use: 4-year ban (if intentional) or 2-year ban (if not intentional). Specified substances (lower risk): reprimand to 2-year ban. Trafficking/administration: 4-year to lifetime ban.

### Court of Arbitration for Sport (CAS)

CAS serves as the final appellate body for doping disputes, with its decisions creating a de facto body of international sports law.

**CAS 2020/O/6689 (WADA v. RUSADA):** Following the exposure of Russia's state-sponsored doping program -- involving sample swapping at the 2014 Sochi Winter Olympics, laboratory manipulation, and destruction of evidence -- WADA sought a four-year ban on Russian participation in major international competitions. CAS imposed a reduced two-year ban (December 2020 to December 2022), during which Russian athletes could compete as neutral athletes but not under the Russian flag or with Russian insignia.

**CAS 2019/A/6148 (Sun Yang v. WADA):** Chinese swimmer Sun Yang received an 8-year ban (reduced from lifetime) for tampering with a doping control sample, including the destruction of blood vials with a hammer during an out-of-competition test. The CAS panel found that Sun Yang had been warned that his actions would constitute an anti-doping rule violation.

### Council of Europe Anti-Doping Convention (1989)

The Anti-Doping Convention (ETS No. 135), opened for signature in 1989, was the first binding international legal instrument addressing doping in sport. It requires states parties to adopt legislative, regulatory, or administrative measures to restrict the availability of doping substances, establish or support anti-doping laboratories, support education programs, and cooperate internationally. The Additional Protocol (2002, ETS No. 188) established the monitoring mechanism for the Convention. As of 2026, 52 states have ratified the Convention.

### UNESCO International Convention against Doping in Sport (2005)

This Convention provides the international legal framework for governments' anti-doping obligations, complementing the WADA Code (which binds sports organizations rather than governments). The Convention requires states parties to adopt measures consistent with the principles of the WADA Code, support national anti-doping organizations, and facilitate doping controls. As of 2026, 191 states have ratified the Convention -- the most widely ratified UNESCO convention.

### ECHR and Athletes' Rights

**FNASS and Others v. France (Application No. 48151/11, 2018):** French athletes challenged the whereabouts obligations imposed by the French Anti-Doping Agency (requiring athletes to provide their location for one hour each day for out-of-competition testing) as a violation of Article 8 (right to private life). The Court found no violation, holding that the whereabouts system pursued the legitimate aim of protecting health and ensuring fair competition, and that the interference with privacy was proportionate given the voluntary nature of elite sport and the importance of effective anti-doping controls.

**Mutu and Pechstein v. Switzerland (2018):** The Court examined whether CAS proceedings satisfied Article 6 (right to a fair trial). The Court found that CAS constituted an "independent and impartial tribunal" within the meaning of Article 6 but identified concerns about the lack of public hearings, ultimately finding a violation of Article 6(1) in the Pechstein case due to the absence of a public hearing.

## Comparative Analysis

### Criminalization Spectrum

The jurisdictions span the full criminalization spectrum. Germany criminalizes both trafficking and self-doping by elite athletes -- the most comprehensive approach. The United States criminalizes doping conspiracies affecting international competitions (Rodchenkov Act) and doping substance trafficking (Controlled Substances Act) but explicitly exempts individual athletes from prosecution. South Korea does not criminalize any aspect of doping, relying entirely on administrative and sporting sanctions. The WADA Code itself imposes only sporting sanctions, not criminal penalties.

### Professional vs. Olympic Sports

The United States uniquely maintains separate anti-doping regimes for Olympic sports (USADA/WADA) and professional leagues (independent programs). This bifurcation reflects the historical autonomy of professional sports leagues under collective bargaining agreements and the absence of government funding that would create a nexus for WADA Code application. South Korea, Germany, and the EU framework apply WADA Code standards broadly to both Olympic and professional sports.

### State-Sponsored Doping Response

The international community's response to Russia's state-sponsored doping program has been the defining issue of twenty-first-century anti-doping policy. CAS's two-year consequence (rather than the four years WADA sought) was criticized as insufficiently deterrent. The Rodchenkov Act represents the U.S. assertion of extraterritorial criminal jurisdiction over doping conspiracies -- a significant escalation that has been both praised as a necessary enforcement tool and criticized as overreach.

### Whistleblower Protection

Germany's AntiDopG, the U.S. Rodchenkov Act, and the WADA Code all include whistleblower protections, reflecting the recognition that insider information is essential to uncovering sophisticated doping schemes that evade analytical testing.
