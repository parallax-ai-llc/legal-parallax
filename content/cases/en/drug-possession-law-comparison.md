## Summary

Drug possession -- the act of having a controlled substance on one's person or within one's constructive control without authorization -- is universally regulated yet subject to radically different legal philosophies across jurisdictions. South Korea enforces one of the world's strictest zero-tolerance regimes under the Narcotics Control Act (mayangnyugwanribeop, 마약류관리법), where even overseas consumption by Korean nationals is prosecuted upon reentry and simple possession carries up to 10 years of imprisonment. The United States operates a dual federal-state system under the Controlled Substances Act (CSA) of 1970, where federal law criminalizes possession of all Schedule I-V substances, yet 24 states and the District of Columbia have legalized recreational cannabis, creating profound legal contradictions. Germany's Narcotics Act (Betaeubungsmittelgesetz, BtMG) criminalizes possession but empowers prosecutors to dismiss cases involving small quantities for personal use under Section 31a, and the landmark Cannabis Act (Cannabisgesetz, CanG) of 2024 legalized personal cannabis possession for adults. The European framework, shaped by EU Council Framework Decision 2004/757/JHA and the jurisprudence of the European Court of Human Rights (ECHR), explicitly excludes personal possession from its minimum penalty provisions, treating drug use primarily as a public health concern rather than a criminal matter. These divergent approaches illuminate a fundamental global tension: punitive prohibition models rooted in moral condemnation versus harm-reduction frameworks that distinguish between personal consumption and distribution.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's approach to drug possession is anchored in the Narcotics Control Act (마약류관리법), which classifies controlled substances into three categories: narcotics (mayak, including heroin, cocaine, and opium), psychotropic substances (hyangjeongsinseong uiyakpum, including methamphetamine, MDMA, and LSD), and cannabis (daema). All three categories are subject to criminal penalties for unauthorized possession.

**Penalties for Simple Possession:**

| Substance Category | Provision | Maximum Penalty |
|---------------------|-----------|-----------------|
| Narcotics (heroin, cocaine, opium) | Art. 59 | Up to 10 years imprisonment or up to 100 million won |
| Psychotropic substances (meth, MDMA) | Art. 60 | Up to 10 years imprisonment or up to 100 million won |
| Cannabis (marijuana, hashish) | Art. 61 | Up to 5 years imprisonment or up to 50 million won |
| Possession for distribution purposes | Art. 58 | 5 years to life imprisonment |

### Extraterritorial Jurisdiction

South Korea stands among the most aggressive jurisdictions globally in exercising extraterritorial jurisdiction over drug offenses. Under the principle of active personality jurisdiction, Korean nationals who consume drugs abroad -- including in jurisdictions where such consumption is legal, such as Canada, the Netherlands, or certain U.S. states -- remain subject to prosecution under the Narcotics Control Act upon return to South Korea. Enforcement is operationalized through mandatory drug testing, including hair follicle analysis capable of detecting substances like THC from prior overseas use. Authorities have conducted targeted testing at airports on individuals returning from countries with permissive drug laws.

### Key Principles

**Zero-tolerance culture:** Korean society treats drug offenses with exceptional moral severity. Unlike property crimes or even violent offenses, drug possession carries intense social stigma that extends well beyond the courtroom. Offenders face near-certain career destruction, particularly in public-facing professions. K-pop idols and actors investigated for drug use have been permanently blacklisted, with their existing works removed from streaming platforms and broadcast archives.

**Mandatory reporting:** Healthcare professionals who become aware of drug use by patients may be required to report such information to authorities, creating a chilling effect on voluntary treatment-seeking behavior.

**Rising drug crime:** South Korea recorded 27,611 drug offenders in 2023, the highest figure in the nation's history. The Supreme Prosecutors' Office responded by establishing a dedicated Narcotics Crime Special Investigation Unit, and the National Police Agency launched targeted operations against both domestic and international trafficking networks.

### Landmark Cases and Developments

**Celebrity prosecution cases:** High-profile drug possession cases have reinforced the zero-tolerance norm. Rapper G-Dragon (Kwon Ji-yong), leader of Big Bang, was booked without detention for suspected violation of the Narcotics Control Act, joining a growing list of entertainment figures including actors Lee Sun-kyun and Yoo Ah-in investigated for illegal drug use. K-pop stars arrested for cannabis use have received sentences ranging from 25 days to 10 months of imprisonment.

**Supreme Court THC ruling (2025):** On June 23, 2025, the Supreme Court ruled that THC or CBD extracted from non-leaf parts of hemp plants (such as stems or roots) qualifies as a narcotic if the compound mirrors leaf-derived substances, overturning prior exemptions and significantly tightening controls on hemp byproducts.

**Hwang Hana case:** Hwang Hana, granddaughter of the founder of Namyang Dairy Products, was formally indicted for violations of the Narcotics Control Act, accused of encouraging acquaintances to use methamphetamine and personally administering the drug via injection, illustrating that social privilege provides no shield against aggressive prosecution.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Federal drug possession law is governed by the Controlled Substances Act (CSA) of 1970, codified at 21 U.S.C. Section 844. The CSA classifies drugs into five Schedules based on medical utility and abuse potential, with Schedule I substances (heroin, LSD, cannabis, ecstasy, psilocybin) considered to have no accepted medical use and high abuse potential.

**Federal Penalties for Simple Possession (21 U.S.C. SS 844):**

| Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|
| First offense: possession of any controlled substance | Up to 1 year imprisonment and/or minimum $1,000 fine |
| Second offense | 15 days to 2 years imprisonment and minimum $2,500 fine |
| Third or subsequent offense | 90 days to 3 years imprisonment and minimum $5,000 fine |
| Civil penalty (alternative to criminal prosecution) | Up to $10,000 per violation |

**Collateral consequences** extend far beyond criminal penalties: convicted individuals face inability to purchase or possess firearms, ineligibility for federal student loans and grants, disqualification from federal housing programs, potential deportation for non-citizens, and loss of professional licenses.

### Federal-State Tension

The most distinctive feature of U.S. drug possession law is the acute tension between federal prohibition and state-level liberalization:

**Cannabis legalization:** As of 2025, 24 states and the District of Columbia have legalized recreational cannabis for adults. Oregon became the first state to decriminalize possession of small amounts of cannabis in 1973, and in 2014 became among the first to legalize adult recreational use. Adults in legal states may possess specified quantities (e.g., up to 2 ounces in Oregon) without penalty.

**Oregon's Measure 110 experiment:** In 2021, Oregon went further by decriminalizing possession of small amounts of all drugs, including heroin, cocaine, and methamphetamine, replacing criminal penalties with $100 citations and referrals to treatment services. This experiment faced significant political backlash, and by 2024, state legislators were exploring recriminalization measures.

**Federal scheduling stasis:** Despite widespread state legalization, cannabis remains classified as Schedule I under federal law. The Department of Justice's enforcement posture has fluctuated between administrations. The Cole Memorandum (2013) established a hands-off approach to state-legal cannabis, which Attorney General Sessions rescinded in 2018.

**HALT Fentanyl Act (2025):** Congress permanently scheduled "fentanyl-related substances," responding to a 255.7% increase in federal fentanyl cases since fiscal year 2020. By fiscal year 2024, fentanyl represented the second most common drug type in federal drug trafficking cases, comprising 20.2% of all such cases.

### Landmark Cases

**Robinson v. California, 370 U.S. 660 (1962):** The Supreme Court ruled 7-2 that a California law criminalizing the status of being a drug addict -- without requiring evidence of drug possession or use -- constituted cruel and unusual punishment under the Eighth Amendment. Justice Potter Stewart's majority opinion characterized addiction as a medical condition rather than a criminal offense. The decision also established that the Eighth Amendment applies to the states through the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. However, its scope was limited by Powell v. Texas (1968), which upheld punishment for public intoxication by a chronic alcoholic.

**Gonzales v. Raich, 545 U.S. 15 (2005):** The Supreme Court held that Congress's Commerce Clause authority extends to prohibiting the local cultivation and use of cannabis even where state law permits it, effectively confirming federal supremacy over state marijuana legalization programs.

**United States v. Booker, 543 U.S. 220 (2005):** While primarily a sentencing case, Booker rendered the Federal Sentencing Guidelines advisory rather than mandatory, giving judges greater discretion in drug possession sentencing. This decision particularly affected drug cases by allowing departures from guideline ranges based on individual circumstances.

### Sentencing Reform Trends

The U.S. Sentencing Commission published proposed 2026 amendments to the Federal Sentencing Guidelines in December 2025, continuing a multi-year trend toward recalibrating drug offense sentences. Drug courts, which divert non-violent drug possession offenders into supervised treatment programs, operate in all 50 states and have demonstrated reduced recidivism rates compared to traditional incarceration.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's drug possession law is governed by two primary statutes: the Narcotics Act (Betaeubungsmittelgesetz, BtMG) for controlled substances generally, and since April 1, 2024, the Cannabis Act (Cannabisgesetz, CanG) for cannabis specifically.

**BtMG Penalties for Drug Possession (Section 29):**

| Offense | Provision | Penalty |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Purchase or possession of narcotics | SS 29(1) BtMG | Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine |
| Possession of "not small" quantities | SS 29a BtMG | 1 to 15 years imprisonment |
| Trafficking (import/export, sale) | SS 30 BtMG | 2 to 15 years imprisonment |
| Armed trafficking or gang-related | SS 30a BtMG | 5 to 15 years imprisonment |

### Small Quantity Discretion (Section 31a BtMG)

The most distinctive feature of German drug possession law is Section 31a of the BtMG, which permits public prosecutors to discontinue criminal proceedings when all of the following conditions are met: the offense involves possession or acquisition of narcotics solely for the offender's own consumption; the quantity is deemed "small" (geringe Menge); the offender's culpability is minor; there is no prevailing public interest in prosecution; and the offender has no relevant prior convictions.

**Small quantity thresholds vary by substance and by federal state (Land):**

| Substance | Typical Small Quantity Threshold |
|-----------|----------------------------------|
| Cannabis (pre-CanG, non-cannabis states) | 6g (Bavaria) to 15g (Berlin, North Rhine-Westphalia) |
| Heroin | Up to 1g |
| Cocaine | 0.5g to 3g (depending on state) |
| Amphetamines | 0.5g to 3g (depending on state) |
| Ecstasy/MDMA | 3 to fewer than 20 tablets |

Case law defines a "small quantity" as up to three units of consumption based on a non-dependent user, where a unit of consumption is the quantity necessary and sufficient to achieve a state of intoxication through the usual form of consumption.

**Constitutional basis:** The Federal Constitutional Court's landmark decision in BVerfGE 90, 145 (Cannabis Decision, March 9, 1994) affirmed the constitutionality of criminal prohibitions on cannabis but held that prosecution of possession of small quantities for personal use could violate the principle of proportionality. The Court criticized divergent prosecutorial practices across the German states and demanded more uniform implementation of Section 31a, establishing that criminal punishment for minimal personal consumption must be proportionate to the legitimate aim of protecting public health.

### Cannabis Act (CanG) 2024

The Cannabis Act, which entered into force on April 1, 2024, represents the most significant liberalization of drug law in German history:

**Legal possession limits for adults (18+):**

| Context | Maximum Quantity |
|---------|-----------------|
| Public possession | 25 grams of dried cannabis |
| Home possession | 50 grams of dried cannabis |
| Home cultivation | Up to 3 cannabis plants |
| Daily purchase limit | 25 grams |
| Monthly purchase limit | 50 grams |

**Cannabis Social Clubs:** From July 1, 2024, adult-only non-profit cultivation associations (Anbauvereinigungen) with a maximum of 500 members became legal. Members may receive up to 25 grams per day and 50 grams per month.

**Youth protections:** Adults under 21 may receive a maximum of 30 grams per month with a THC content of no more than 10%.

**Possession above legal limits:** Possession exceeding the legal thresholds (e.g., more than 25g in public) remains a criminal offense under both the CanG and remaining BtMG provisions.

### Therapy Over Punishment (Section 35 BtMG)

Section 35 BtMG provides a pathway for drug-dependent offenders to substitute imprisonment with drug treatment. When an offender is sentenced to no more than two years of imprisonment and the offense was committed in connection with their addiction, the court may suspend the sentence and order mandatory drug therapy. Successful completion of treatment results in the suspension of the remaining sentence.

### Landmark Cases

**BVerfGE 90, 145 -- Cannabis Decision (1994):** The Federal Constitutional Court ruled 7-1 that while the criminal prohibition of cannabis products was constitutional, the prosecution of possession of small quantities exclusively for personal use must respect the principle of proportionality. The decision required prosecutors to exercise discretion under Section 31a BtMG and criticized the lack of uniform application across federal states.

**BVerfG 2 BvL 8/22 (2023):** The Federal Constitutional Court declared judicial referrals regarding the constitutionality of cannabis criminalization inadmissible, declining to revisit the 1994 Cannabis Decision on procedural grounds while the legislative process for the Cannabis Act was already underway.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Framework Decision 2004/757/JHA

The principal EU legislative instrument on drug offenses is Council Framework Decision 2004/757/JHA of October 25, 2004, which lays down minimum provisions on the constituent elements of criminal acts and penalties in the field of illicit drug trafficking. The Framework Decision establishes minimum penalty thresholds that member states must meet:

| Offense Category | Minimum Maximum Penalty |
|------------------|------------------------|
| Drug trafficking (basic offense) | At least 1-3 years imprisonment |
| Trafficking within a criminal organization | At least 10 years imprisonment |
| Precursor offenses linked to organizations | 5-10 years imprisonment |

**Critical carve-out for personal possession:** Article 2(2) explicitly provides that the Framework Decision does not cover conduct "committed by its perpetrators exclusively for their own personal consumption as defined by national law." This exclusion reflects the EU's deliberate decision to leave personal drug possession to member state discretion, recognizing the diversity of national approaches.

**Directive (EU) 2017/2103** amended the Framework Decision to include new psychoactive substances (NPS) within the definition of "drug," enabling faster scheduling of emerging synthetic substances at the EU level.

### EU Drugs Strategy and Action Plans

**EU Drugs Strategy 2021-2025:** This strategic document established an integrated, balanced, and evidence-based approach to drug policy built on equal pillars of supply reduction, demand reduction, and harm reduction. The Strategy explicitly acknowledges that "drug consumption and/or drug possession for personal use or possession of small amounts do not constitute a criminal offence in many Member States, or there is the option to refrain from imposing criminal sanctions."

**Council Conclusions of March 2018** specifically promote the use of alternatives to coercive sanctions for drug-using offenders, endorsing treatment, rehabilitation, and social reintegration programs over imprisonment.

**EU Drugs Strategy 2026-2030:** In December 2025, the European Commission presented a new EU Drugs Strategy and Action Plan, outlining key actions to be implemented from 2026 to 2030. The Commission announced plans to propose stricter rules against organised crime and to evaluate the existing Framework Decision on drug trafficking by 2026, potentially leading to updated minimum penalty provisions while maintaining the personal-use exclusion.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

The European Court of Human Rights has addressed drug possession tangentially through several Convention articles:

**Article 8 (Right to respect for private life):** While the ECHR has not established a right to drug use under Article 8, the Court's proportionality analysis requires that any interference with private life -- including criminal penalties for personal drug consumption -- must correspond to a "pressing social need" and remain proportionate to the legitimate aim pursued. States enjoy a wide margin of appreciation in drug policy, but disproportionate penalties for minimal personal possession could potentially engage Article 8 protections.

**Article 3 (Prohibition of torture and inhuman treatment):** The Court has held that failure to provide adequate medical treatment to drug-dependent prisoners may violate Article 3. States have an obligation to ensure that drug-dependent individuals in custody receive appropriate care, including substitution therapy where medically indicated.

**Article 5 (Right to liberty):** Pre-trial detention for minor drug possession offenses must comply with the proportionality requirements of Article 5. Extended detention for simple possession without adequate judicial review may constitute an arbitrary deprivation of liberty.

**Article 14 (Prohibition of discrimination):** Discriminatory enforcement of drug possession laws -- targeting specific ethnic, racial, or social groups for disproportionate prosecution -- could engage Article 14 in conjunction with other Convention rights.

### Diversity of Member State Approaches

EU member states exhibit a wide spectrum of approaches to drug possession, broadly falling into three categories:

**Decriminalization models (e.g., Portugal):** Since 2001, Portugal has treated possession of up to 10 days' supply of any drug as an administrative rather than criminal offense, referring users to Dissuasion Commissions that can impose non-criminal sanctions or recommend treatment. Portugal's model is widely cited as evidence that decriminalization does not increase drug use rates while reducing overdose deaths and HIV transmission.

**Prosecutorial discretion models (e.g., Germany, Netherlands):** These countries maintain criminal prohibitions but employ formal or informal policies of non-prosecution for small quantities, effectively decriminalizing personal possession in practice without changing the statutory framework.

**Criminal prohibition with limited mitigation (e.g., Sweden, Hungary):** These countries maintain strict criminal penalties for all drug possession, including personal use, with limited diversion options. Sweden's approach, often described as "zero tolerance," imposes fines or up to three years' imprisonment for personal use.

### European Union Drugs Agency (EUDA)

The European Union Drugs Agency (formerly EMCDDA, rebranded as EUDA) monitors drug trends across member states and provides comparative data on legal approaches. EUDA reporting consistently documents a trend across the EU toward distinguishing personal possession from trafficking, with a growing number of member states adopting alternatives to criminal prosecution for drug users.

## Comparative Analysis

### Philosophical Divergence

The four jurisdictions examined reflect three fundamentally different philosophical orientations toward drug possession:

**1. Moral prohibitionism (South Korea):** Drug possession is treated as a moral failing that threatens the social fabric. The state's role is to suppress all drug use through deterrence, exemplified by extraterritorial jurisdiction, mandatory testing, and severe social consequences. There is no meaningful distinction between personal possession and distribution at the philosophical level -- both are viewed as components of the same social harm.

**2. Graduated prohibitionism (United States):** Drug possession is formally criminal at the federal level, but a patchwork of state-level reforms reflects growing skepticism about the efficacy and justice of criminalization for personal use. The system is marked by internal contradiction: cannabis is simultaneously legal and illegal depending on which sovereign's law applies, and racial disparities in enforcement have eroded the moral authority of prohibition.

**3. Proportional harm reduction (Germany and EU):** Drug possession is recognized as a public health concern first and a criminal matter second. The legal framework maintains formal prohibition while building in systematic mechanisms to avoid criminal punishment for personal consumption. Germany's Cannabis Act represents the most advanced expression of this approach, fully legalizing possession of one substance while maintaining graduated penalties for others.

### Enforcement Realities

**Prosecution rates diverge from statutory severity.** South Korea prosecutes virtually all detected possession cases. The United States prosecutes a significant fraction at the federal level but varies enormously by state and jurisdiction. Germany dismisses the majority of simple possession cases under Section 31a. Many EU member states have effectively decriminalized possession through non-enforcement even where statutes remain on the books.

**Detection methods reflect policy priorities.** South Korea's use of mandatory hair follicle testing at airports represents the most intrusive detection regime. The United States relies primarily on searches incident to arrest, traffic stops, and surveillance operations. Germany and most EU states generally encounter possession cases incidentally during other law enforcement activities rather than through targeted drug-use detection.

### Sentencing Severity Spectrum

Arranged from most to least severe for simple first-offense possession of a personal quantity:

1. **South Korea:** Up to 10 years imprisonment for narcotics/psychotropics; up to 5 years for cannabis
2. **United States (federal):** Up to 1 year imprisonment and $1,000 minimum fine
3. **Germany (non-cannabis):** Up to 5 years imprisonment, but most cases dismissed under Section 31a
4. **Germany (cannabis):** Legal up to 25g in public / 50g at home
5. **EU/ECHR framework:** No minimum penalties prescribed; personal use excluded from Framework Decision

### The Cannabis Divergence

Cannabis possession most starkly illustrates jurisdictional divergence. A person carrying 10 grams of cannabis would face:

- **South Korea:** Criminal prosecution with up to 5 years imprisonment; prosecution even if consumed abroad
- **United States:** Federal criminal liability (up to 1 year); legal in 24+ states with no penalty
- **Germany:** Fully legal with no consequences (within the 25g public limit)
- **EU member states:** Ranges from full legality (e.g., Germany, Malta) to administrative fines (e.g., Portugal, Czech Republic) to criminal penalties (e.g., Sweden, Hungary)
