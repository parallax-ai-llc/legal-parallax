## Summary

Parallel imports -- also known as gray market goods -- refer to genuine branded products that are imported into a market without the authorization of the intellectual property rights holder, exploiting price differentials between countries. The legal treatment of parallel imports is fundamentally determined by the doctrine of exhaustion of intellectual property rights: whether placing goods on the market in one jurisdiction exhausts the IP holder's right to control further distribution of those specific goods in another jurisdiction. South Korea applies a regime of international exhaustion for patents under the Patent Act (특허법) as interpreted by the Supreme Court, and national exhaustion for trademarks under the Trademark Act (상표법), with the Korean Intellectual Property Office (특허청, KIPO) and the courts permitting parallel imports of trademarked goods where the goods are genuine and material differences are absent; the Fair Trade Commission (공정거래위원회) also scrutinizes restrictions on parallel imports as potential antitrust violations. The United States applies a complex, IP-type-specific regime: for patents, the Supreme Court in Impression Products, Inc. v. Lexmark International, Inc., 581 U.S. 1 (2017) established international exhaustion; for copyrights, Kirtsaeng v. John Wiley & Sons, Inc., 568 U.S. 519 (2013) established international exhaustion under the first sale doctrine (17 U.S.C. Section 109); for trademarks, the common control exception and material differences test apply under Section 526 of the Tariff Act of 1930 (19 U.S.C. Section 1526) and Customs Service regulations. Germany, as an EU member state, applies the EU-wide regional exhaustion principle under which IP rights are exhausted within the European Economic Area (EEA) once goods are placed on the market within the EEA by the rights holder or with their consent, but rights are not exhausted for goods first placed on the market outside the EEA; this regime is established through CJEU jurisprudence and EU directives including the Trade Mark Directive (EU) 2015/2436 and Directive 2001/29/EC. The ECHR/EU framework enforces regional (EEA-wide) exhaustion as a cornerstone of the single market and free movement of goods under Articles 34-36 TFEU, with the CJEU consistently holding in cases such as Silhouette International Schmied v. Hartlauer (Case C-355/96, 1998) that member states may not adopt international exhaustion for trademarks.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's approach to parallel imports has evolved significantly, reflecting the country's transition from a net importer of IP-intensive goods to a major exporter with interests on both sides of the parallel import debate.

**Patent Act (특허법):** The Korean Supreme Court has recognized international exhaustion for patents, holding that once patented goods are lawfully sold by the patent holder or with their consent in any country, the patent holder's rights with respect to those specific goods are exhausted. In the landmark decision 2002Da13782 (Supreme Court, May 14, 2004), the Court reasoned that the patent holder receives adequate compensation at the point of first sale and that restricting further distribution would unduly limit the free circulation of goods in international trade.

**Trademark Act (상표법):** For trademarks, the position is more nuanced. The Trademark Act does not explicitly address parallel imports, but the courts have permitted the importation of genuine trademarked goods where: (1) the goods originate from the trademark holder or a party with a legitimate economic connection to the holder; (2) the goods are materially identical to those sold domestically; and (3) the quality control maintained by the trademark holder applies equally to the imported goods. The Supreme Court decision 2002Da62052 (2004) established these principles.

### Customs and Border Measures

The Korea Customs Service (관세청) administers border enforcement measures related to IP-infringing goods. Under the Customs Act (관세법), rights holders may register their IP rights and request customs authorities to suspend the clearance of suspected infringing goods. However, the customs authorities distinguish between counterfeit goods (which are subject to seizure) and genuine parallel imports (which generally are not), unless the parallel imports infringe trademark rights due to material differences or unauthorized modification.

### Competition Law Intersection

The Korea Fair Trade Commission (공정거래위원회, KFTC) has actively scrutinized contractual restrictions on parallel imports as potential violations of the Monopoly Regulation and Fair Trade Act (독점규제 및 공정거래에 관한 법률). In KFTC Decision 2010-082, the Commission found that exclusive distribution agreements containing absolute territorial restrictions that prevented parallel imports constituted unfair trade practices. The KFTC's Guidelines on Unfair International Contract Practices specifically address restrictions on parallel importation.

### Industry Impact

Parallel imports have particular significance in South Korea's pharmaceutical sector, where the Ministry of Food and Drug Safety (식품의약품안전처) regulates the safety and labeling of parallel-imported pharmaceuticals, and in the automotive parts sector, where parallel imports of genuine parts offer consumers lower-cost alternatives to authorized dealer networks. The 2019 amendments to the Pharmaceutical Affairs Act (약사법) streamlined the approval process for parallel-imported medicines, recognizing their role in price competition.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has the most jurisprudentially developed parallel imports framework among the jurisdictions compared, with distinct rules for each category of intellectual property.

**Patent Exhaustion -- Impression Products v. Lexmark (2017):** The Supreme Court's landmark decision in Impression Products, Inc. v. Lexmark International, Inc., 581 U.S. 1 (2017), established international exhaustion for patents. Chief Justice Roberts, writing for the majority, held that "an authorized sale outside the United States, just as one within the United States, exhausts all rights under the Patent Act." The Court rejected Lexmark's argument that post-sale restrictions and conditional sales agreements could preserve patent rights, overruling the Federal Circuit's contrary holding in Jazz Photo Corp. v. International Trade Commission, 264 F.3d 1094 (Fed. Cir. 2001). The decision resolved a long-standing circuit split and aligned patent exhaustion with the copyright first sale doctrine.

**Copyright Exhaustion -- Kirtsaeng v. John Wiley & Sons (2013):** In Kirtsaeng v. John Wiley & Sons, Inc., 568 U.S. 519 (2013), the Supreme Court held that the first sale doctrine under 17 U.S.C. Section 109(a) applies to copies of copyrighted works lawfully made abroad, establishing international exhaustion. Justice Breyer's majority opinion held that the phrase "lawfully made under this title" in Section 109(a) does not impose a geographical limitation, permitting the importation and resale of genuine copyrighted works purchased abroad at lower prices.

**Trademark Exhaustion and Gray Market Goods:** Trademark parallel imports are governed by a complex regulatory and judicial framework:

| Source | Rule |
|--------|------|
| Section 526, Tariff Act of 1930 (19 U.S.C. Section 1526) | Prohibits importation of goods bearing trademarks owned by U.S. persons without written consent |
| Customs Service Regulations (19 C.F.R. Section 133.21) | Common control exception: permits importation if foreign and U.S. trademark owners are in a parent-subsidiary or common ownership relationship |
| Lever Brothers Rule (Lever Bros. Co. v. United States, 981 F.2d 1330 (D.C. Cir. 1993)) | Material differences test: even genuine goods from a common-control entity may be excluded if they differ materially from the U.S. version |

### Material Differences Doctrine

The material differences test has been extensively developed through trademark parallel import litigation. In Gamut Trading Co. v. International Trade Commission, 200 F.3d 775 (Fed. Cir. 1999), the Federal Circuit held that material differences need only be physical differences that consumers would likely consider relevant to a purchasing decision. Courts have found material differences in factors including warranty coverage, language of instructions, product formulation, and packaging differences. The Customs and Border Protection (CBP) enforces the Lever Rule by requiring appropriate labeling of materially different gray market goods.

### Antitrust Considerations

U.S. antitrust law limits the ability of IP holders to prevent parallel imports through territorial restrictions. Vertical agreements that establish absolute territorial protection may violate Section 1 of the Sherman Act (15 U.S.C. Section 1), and the FTC has challenged contractual arrangements designed to prevent parallel imports as unfair methods of competition. However, the Supreme Court's decision in Leegin Creative Leather Products, Inc. v. PSKS, Inc., 551 U.S. 877 (2007), which applied the rule of reason to vertical price restraints, has complicated the antitrust analysis of distribution restrictions that indirectly affect parallel trade.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany applies the EU regional exhaustion principle, under which IP rights are exhausted within the European Economic Area upon first marketing within the EEA by the rights holder or with their consent, but are not exhausted for goods first placed on the market outside the EEA.

**Patent Act (Patentgesetz, PatG):** Section 9b PatG, read in conjunction with CJEU jurisprudence, establishes that the patent holder's exclusive rights are exhausted when the patented product is placed on the market within the EEA by the patent holder or with their consent. For goods first sold outside the EEA, the patent holder retains the right to prevent importation into Germany.

**Trademark Act (Markengesetz, MarkenG):** Section 24 MarkenG transposes Article 15 of the Trade Mark Directive (EU) 2015/2436, providing that the trademark owner may not prohibit the use of the trademark for goods placed on the market in the EEA by the owner or with their consent. However, the trademark owner may oppose further commercialization where the condition of the goods has been changed or impaired after being placed on the market.

### Repackaging and Relabeling Jurisprudence

German courts have extensively addressed the repackaging of parallel-imported goods, particularly pharmaceuticals, following the CJEU's jurisprudence:

**Bristol-Myers Squibb Conditions (BMS v. Paranova, Joined Cases C-427/93, C-429/93, C-436/93, 1996):** The CJEU established five cumulative conditions under which a parallel importer may repackage trademarked pharmaceutical products: (1) repackaging is objectively necessary to market the products in the importing member state; (2) the repackaging does not affect the original condition of the product; (3) the new packaging clearly identifies the manufacturer and parallel importer; (4) the presentation of the repackaged product does not damage the reputation of the trademark; and (5) the importer gives advance notice to the trademark owner and provides a sample upon request.

The Bundesgerichtshof (Federal Court of Justice) has applied these conditions in numerous decisions, including BGH I ZR 135/18 (2021), refining the "objective necessity" requirement and the standard for assessing reputational damage.

### Competition Law Interface

The Gesetz gegen Wettbewerbsbeschränkungen (GWB, Act Against Restraints of Competition) and EU competition law (Articles 101-102 TFEU) play a significant role in the parallel imports context. The Bundeskartellamt (Federal Cartel Office) has investigated and sanctioned distribution agreements that seek to partition markets and prevent parallel trade within the EU. In the automotive sector, the European Commission's Block Exemption Regulation for Motor Vehicle Distribution (Regulation 461/2010) specifically addresses parallel trade in motor vehicles and spare parts.

### Pharmaceutical Parallel Imports

Germany is one of the largest markets for parallel-imported pharmaceuticals in the EU. Parallel importers such as Kohlpharma GmbH and Eurim-Pharm operate under the regulatory framework of the Arzneimittelgesetz (AMG, Medicines Act), which requires parallel importers to obtain marketing authorization from the Bundesinstitut für Arzneimittel und Medizinprodukte (BfArM) for each parallel-imported pharmaceutical product. German statutory health insurance (gesetzliche Krankenversicherung) encourages parallel imports through pricing incentives: Section 129(1) SGB V (Social Code Book V) establishes import quotas requiring pharmacies to dispense a minimum percentage of parallel-imported pharmaceuticals.

## ECHR & EU

### Free Movement of Goods and IP Exhaustion

The EU regional exhaustion regime is a cornerstone of the single market, balancing the free movement of goods under Articles 34-36 TFEU with intellectual property rights protection.

**Silhouette International Schmied v. Hartlauer (Case C-355/96, 1998):** The CJEU's foundational decision established that the Trade Mark Directive mandates regional (EEA) exhaustion and that member states may not provide for international exhaustion of trademark rights. The Court held that the Directive exhaustively harmonized the exhaustion question, precluding national deviations toward international exhaustion.

**Davidoff & Cie SA v. A&G Imports (Joined Cases C-414/99 to C-416/99, 2001):** The Court held that consent to marketing within the EEA must be expressed positively and unequivocally, and may not be implied from the rights holder's silence or failure to communicate objection.

**Copad SA v. Christian Dior Couture SA (Case C-59/08, 2009):** The Court addressed exhaustion in the context of selective distribution, holding that a licensee who sells goods in breach of a provision in the license agreement may be treated as acting without the trademark owner's consent, preventing exhaustion.

### Digital Exhaustion

The CJEU has addressed the applicability of exhaustion to digital goods:

**UsedSoft GmbH v. Oracle International Corp. (Case C-128/11, 2012):** The Court held that the exhaustion principle under the Software Directive (2009/24/EC) applies to the resale of used software licenses, including those originally acquired by download. However, in Tom Kabinet (Case C-263/18, 2019), the Court held that the exhaustion principle under the Information Society Directive (2001/29/EC) does not apply to the resale of e-books, distinguishing digital content from software.

### Customs Enforcement

EU Regulation 608/2013 concerning customs enforcement of intellectual property rights establishes the procedural framework for rights holders to apply for customs action against suspected IP-infringing goods. The regulation covers counterfeit goods but expressly excludes goods placed on the market by the rights holder or with their consent (i.e., genuine parallel imports from outside the EEA are not covered by the customs enforcement regulation and must be addressed through civil proceedings).

### Competition Law and Parallel Trade

EU competition law strongly supports parallel trade within the single market. The European Commission and CJEU have consistently condemned agreements that partition national markets and prevent parallel imports:

**Consten and Grundig v. Commission (Joined Cases 56/64 and 58/64, 1966):** The foundational judgment establishing that agreements granting absolute territorial protection by preventing parallel imports violate Article 101(1) TFEU.

**GlaxoSmithKline Services v. Commission (Case C-501/06 P, 2009):** The CJEU confirmed that agreements restricting parallel trade in pharmaceuticals violate Article 101(1) TFEU, rejecting the argument that the pharmaceutical sector's regulatory features justify an exemption from this principle.

**Vertical Block Exemption Regulation (EU) 2022/720:** The revised VBER and accompanying Guidelines on Vertical Restraints address territorial and customer restrictions in distribution agreements, identifying restrictions on parallel trade as hardcore restrictions that fall outside the block exemption.

## Comparative Analysis

### Exhaustion Models: A Tripartite Divergence

The three principal exhaustion models -- international, national, and regional -- reflect fundamentally different policy orientations:

**International Exhaustion (United States for patents and copyright):** Prioritizes free trade, price competition, and consumer welfare by preventing IP holders from segmenting markets and maintaining price discrimination across borders. The U.S. Supreme Court's decisions in Kirtsaeng and Impression Products represent the strongest endorsement of international exhaustion among major jurisdictions, reflecting the traditional emphasis on free alienability of property.

**Regional Exhaustion (EU/EEA):** Represents a compromise that supports the internal market objective of free movement of goods within the EEA while preserving the ability of rights holders to maintain price differences between the EEA and third countries. This model serves the dual purpose of promoting intra-EEA competition while protecting the competitiveness of EEA-based industries against competition from goods manufactured for lower-priced markets.

**National/Hybrid Exhaustion (South Korea):** South Korea's differentiated approach, with international exhaustion for patents and a more restrictive approach for trademarks, reflects the pragmatic balancing of industrial policy objectives: encouraging access to patented technology while protecting the integrity of domestic trademark-based distribution networks.

### Pharmaceutical Sector: A Critical Case Study

The pharmaceutical sector illustrates the practical impact of differing exhaustion regimes most starkly. Germany's system actively promotes pharmaceutical parallel imports through regulatory streamlining and insurance reimbursement incentives, viewing them as a mechanism for containing healthcare costs. The United States permits pharmaceutical parallel imports in theory (post-Impression Products for patents), but regulatory barriers under the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act effectively restrict pharmaceutical importation. South Korea occupies a middle ground, having streamlined pharmaceutical parallel import approvals while maintaining safety and labeling requirements.

### Digital Goods: An Emerging Frontier

The applicability of exhaustion to digital goods remains contested across all jurisdictions. The CJEU's contrasting decisions in UsedSoft (permitting software license resale) and Tom Kabinet (rejecting e-book resale) highlight the difficulty of applying physical goods exhaustion principles to digital content. In the United States, the first sale doctrine's applicability to digital goods is complicated by the prevalence of license-based rather than sale-based transactions (Vernor v. Autodesk, Inc., 621 F.3d 1102 (9th Cir. 2010)). South Korea and Germany face similar challenges as digital content markets expand.

### Competition Law as a Corrective Mechanism

Across all jurisdictions, competition law serves as a corrective mechanism that limits the ability of IP holders to use contractual arrangements to prevent parallel trade even where exhaustion doctrine might not fully apply. The EU's approach is the most aggressive, treating restrictions on parallel trade within the single market as hardcore competition violations. The United States, South Korea, and Germany all scrutinize territorial restrictions through their respective antitrust frameworks, though the standard of review and enforcement intensity vary.
