## Summary

Homelessness law encompasses the regulation of rough sleeping, the right to shelter, housing-first policies, the criminalization of activities associated with homelessness, and the obligations of the state toward persons without adequate housing. Legal frameworks range from punitive approaches that criminalize the status or conduct of homeless persons to rights-based approaches that recognize enforceable entitlements to housing or shelter. South Korea addresses homelessness primarily through the Act on the Protection and Support of Homeless Persons (노숙인 등의 복지 및 자립지원에 관한 법률, 2011), which established a comprehensive welfare framework including emergency shelters, transitional housing, and rehabilitation services, though enforcement remains inconsistent and the legal framework does not establish a justiciable right to housing despite constitutional provisions. The United States has no federal right to shelter, and homelessness regulation occurs primarily at the state and local level; the Supreme Court's decision in City of Grants Pass v. Johnson (603 U.S. ___, 2024) held that municipal ordinances criminalizing sleeping outdoors when no shelter is available do not violate the Eighth Amendment's prohibition on cruel and unusual punishment, overturning the Ninth Circuit's approach in Martin v. Boise (920 F.3d 584, 9th Cir. 2019). Germany recognizes a right to a minimum subsistence level (Existenzminimum) derived from Article 1(1) (human dignity) and Article 20(1) (social state principle) of the Basic Law, with municipalities bearing the primary obligation to provide emergency shelter under state police law (Polizeirecht) and the Social Code (SGB XII, Section 67), creating what is effectively an enforceable right to shelter. The ECHR framework addresses homelessness through Article 3 (prohibition of inhuman and degrading treatment), Article 8 (respect for home), and the European Social Charter Article 31 (right to housing), as interpreted by the European Committee of Social Rights in landmark collective complaints.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's approach to homelessness is primarily welfare-based, anchored in the **Act on the Protection and Support of Homeless Persons** (노숙인 등의 복지 및 자립지원에 관한 법률), enacted on June 7, 2011.

### Constitutional Provisions

**Article 35(3) of the Constitution:** "The State shall endeavor to ensure comfortable housing for all citizens through housing development policies and the like." This provision is generally interpreted as a programmatic directive (Staatszielbestimmung) rather than a justiciable individual right. The Constitutional Court has not recognized an enforceable right to housing derived from this provision.

**Article 34 (Right to a Life Worthy of Human Beings):** Paragraph 1 guarantees the right to a life worthy of human dignity, and Paragraph 5 provides that "citizens who are incapable of earning a livelihood due to a physical disability, disease, old age, or other reasons shall be protected by the State under the conditions as prescribed by Act."

### Act on Protection of Homeless Persons (2011)

Key provisions:

- **Article 2 (Definition):** Defines a "homeless person" (노숙인) as a person who lives in inadequate housing including on the streets, in parks, at train stations, or in facilities not intended for habitation, or a person at risk of homelessness.
- **Article 3 (Responsibility of the State and Local Governments):** The state and local governments have the responsibility to develop and implement policies for the welfare and self-reliance of homeless persons.
- **Article 8 (Emergency Shelters, 일시보호시설):** Local governments shall establish and operate emergency shelters providing temporary accommodation, meals, and basic medical care.
- **Article 9 (Rehabilitation Facilities, 자활시설):** Provides for transitional housing with self-reliance support programs including vocational training, employment counseling, and substance abuse treatment.
- **Article 10 (Medical Support):** Homeless persons are eligible for medical support through the Medical Aid Program (의료급여).

### Shelter System

As of 2023, Korea operates approximately 42 emergency shelters and 120 transitional/rehabilitation facilities nationwide. The system is concentrated in Seoul (which accounts for approximately 55% of Korea's homeless population):

| Facility Type | Number (2023) | Capacity | Average Occupancy |
|---------------|---------------|----------|-------------------|
| Emergency shelters (일시보호시설) | 42 | ~3,500 | 78% |
| Rehabilitation facilities (자활시설) | 72 | ~4,200 | 85% |
| Transitional housing (주거지원시설) | 48 | ~2,800 | 90% |

### Decriminalization

South Korea does not criminalize rough sleeping. The **Minor Offenses Act** (경범죄 처벌법) previously contained provisions on vagrancy that could be applied to homeless persons, but these have been progressively narrowed and are not enforced against persons sleeping rough due to lack of housing.

### Housing-First Approach

In 2021, the Ministry of Health and Welfare announced a shift toward a Housing First model, emphasizing permanent supportive housing over the traditional shelter-rehabilitation-housing continuum. However, implementation has been slow, with housing supply constraints in Seoul and other major cities limiting the availability of affordable permanent housing for program participants.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States lacks a federal right to shelter or housing, and the legal landscape is defined by the tension between efforts to assist homeless persons and municipal ordinances that criminalize behaviors associated with homelessness.

### Federal Framework

**McKinney-Vento Homeless Assistance Act (42 U.S.C. Section 11301 et seq., 1987):** The primary federal homelessness legislation, providing funding for emergency shelters, transitional housing, permanent supportive housing, and homeless prevention programs through the Continuum of Care (CoC) program administered by HUD. The Act was substantially amended by the **HEARTH Act (2009)**, which:

- Expanded the definition of homelessness to include persons at imminent risk of losing housing.
- Consolidated HUD homeless assistance programs into the Continuum of Care and Emergency Solutions Grants programs.
- Emphasized "rapid re-housing" and permanent supportive housing over transitional housing.
- Required CoC programs to implement a coordinated entry system.

**McKinney-Vento Education Provisions (42 U.S.C. Section 11431 et seq.):** Require public schools to provide educational access to children experiencing homelessness, including enrollment without documentation, transportation to the school of origin, and designation of a local homeless education liaison.

### Criminalization: Martin v. Boise and City of Grants Pass

The most significant recent legal development concerned whether municipalities could criminalize sleeping outdoors when no shelter beds are available:

**Martin v. Boise (920 F.3d 584, 9th Cir. 2019):** The Ninth Circuit held that the Eighth Amendment prohibits cities from enforcing anti-camping ordinances against homeless individuals when there are more homeless persons than available shelter beds. The Court reasoned that criminalizing involuntary conduct (sleeping) that is a universal human necessity violates the prohibition on cruel and unusual punishment, relying on Robinson v. California (370 U.S. 660, 1962).

**City of Grants Pass v. Johnson (603 U.S. ___, 2024):** The Supreme Court reversed the Ninth Circuit's approach in a 6-3 decision. Writing for the majority, Justice Gorsuch held that:

- Ordinances prohibiting public camping regulate conduct, not status, and therefore Robinson v. California does not apply.
- The Eighth Amendment's Cruel and Unusual Punishments Clause constrains the methods and severity of punishment, not the underlying criminalization decision.
- Determining how to address homelessness is a policy question for elected officials, not a constitutional question for courts.

Justice Sotomayor dissented, arguing that punishing people for sleeping outside when they have no alternative constitutes cruel and unusual punishment.

Post-Grants Pass, numerous cities have enacted or enforced anti-camping ordinances, including sweeps of encampments. Governor Gavin Newsom issued Executive Order N-1-24 directing California state agencies to clear encampments on state property.

### Right to Shelter Jurisdictions

Despite the absence of a federal right, some jurisdictions have established statutory or judicially mandated rights to shelter:

- **New York City:** The **Callahan Consent Decree (1981)**, arising from Callahan v. Carey (N.Y. Sup. Ct., 1979), established a right to shelter for all homeless men in NYC. Extended to women (Eldredge v. Koch, 1983) and families (McCain v. Koch, 1986). NYC is legally obligated to provide shelter to every person who requests it, at a cost exceeding $4 billion annually (2024).
- **Massachusetts (Chapter 450, Acts of 1983):** The Emergency Assistance (EA) program provides a statutory right to shelter for families with children meeting eligibility criteria.
- **Washington, D.C. (Homeless Services Reform Act, 2005):** Provides a right to year-round shelter for individuals and families.

| Jurisdiction | Legal Basis | Scope | Annual Cost (est.) |
|-------------|------------|-------|-------------------|
| New York City | Callahan Consent Decree (1981) | All individuals and families | $4+ billion |
| Massachusetts | Chapter 450 (1983) | Families with children | $400 million |
| Washington, D.C. | Homeless Services Reform Act (2005) | Individuals and families | $200 million |

### Housing First

HUD has promoted Housing First as the evidence-based model since the Obama administration. The **U.S. Interagency Council on Homelessness (USICH)** adopted Housing First principles in its strategic plan, and many CoC programs prioritize permanent supportive housing for chronically homeless individuals. Houston's model, which reduced homelessness by 63% between 2011 and 2023, is frequently cited as a Housing First success story.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany provides what is effectively the most robust right to shelter among the four jurisdictions, derived from constitutional principles and implemented through social welfare and police law.

### Constitutional Foundation

**Article 1(1) of the Basic Law (Human Dignity):** "Human dignity shall be inviolable. To respect and protect it shall be the duty of all state authority." Combined with **Article 20(1) (Social State Principle):** "The Federal Republic of Germany is a democratic and social federal state."

The **Federal Constitutional Court (BVerfG)** has derived from these provisions a **right to a minimum subsistence level** (Recht auf Gewaehrleistung eines menschenwuerdigen Existenzminimums). In **BVerfGE 125, 175 (February 9, 2010, "Hartz IV decision")**, the Court held that the state must guarantee the material conditions necessary for human dignity, including adequate housing.

**BVerfGE 132, 134 (July 18, 2012, "Asylbewerberleistungsgesetz decision"):** The Court confirmed that the right to a dignified minimum existence applies to all persons in Germany, regardless of citizenship or residence status.

### Emergency Shelter Obligation (Unterbringungspflicht)

Under state **police law** (Polizeirecht/Ordnungsrecht), municipalities have a mandatory obligation to prevent homelessness and provide emergency shelter:

- The obligation derives from the police law's general clause requiring authorities to avert dangers to public safety, combined with the constitutional duty to protect human dignity.
- The **Federal Administrative Court** held in **BVerwGE 22, 286 (1966)** that homelessness constitutes a danger to public safety that triggers the municipal duty to act.
- Municipalities must provide shelter that meets minimum standards of human dignity: a heated room, a bed, access to sanitary facilities, and basic furnishings. Merely pointing to a waiting list does not fulfill the obligation.

### Social Code XII (Sozialgesetzbuch XII)

**SGB XII, Section 67 (Assistance for Overcoming Special Social Difficulties):** Provides for comprehensive social assistance for persons in "particular social difficulties," including homelessness. Benefits include:

- Emergency accommodation and transitional housing
- Social counseling and case management
- Assistance with securing permanent housing
- Integration services (employment support, addiction treatment, debt counseling)
- Health insurance coverage (homeless persons without insurance are entitled to treatment under Section 264 SGB V or the Asylbewerberleistungsgesetz)

**SGB XII, Section 36 (Assistance for Housing):** The social welfare authority may provide assistance to prevent homelessness, including payment of rent arrears to prevent eviction, and assistance in securing new housing.

### Eviction Prevention

German law provides extensive protections against eviction that serve as the primary homelessness prevention mechanism:

- **BGB Section 543 (Extraordinary Termination for Cause):** Landlords may terminate a lease for serious breach, including persistent non-payment of rent (arrears of two months or more).
- **BGB Section 569(3)(2) (Nachholungsrecht):** A tenant can nullify the termination by paying all arrears within two months of the eviction lawsuit being served. This right can be exercised once every two years.
- **Social Clause (Sozialklausel, BGB Section 574):** Even where a landlord has a valid ground for termination, the tenant may object if termination would cause "unreasonable hardship" (unzumutbare Haerte) -- such as homelessness, particularly where elderly persons, children, or persons with disabilities are affected.
- **Court Eviction Requirement:** All evictions require a court order; self-help evictions are illegal. The eviction process typically takes 6-12 months.

### Housing First in Germany

Several German cities have adopted Housing First approaches:

- **Berlin Housing First Pilot (2018):** Funded by the Senate Department for Integration, Labour and Social Affairs, the pilot provides permanent apartments and intensive support to chronically homeless individuals. An evaluation published in 2023 found that 97% of participants retained their housing after one year.
- **North Rhine-Westphalia Housing First (2020):** A statewide program operating in Cologne, Duesseldorf, and other cities.
- **National Action Plan on Overcoming Homelessness (2024):** The federal government adopted a comprehensive plan targeting the reduction of homelessness to as close to zero as possible by 2030, with Housing First as a central pillar.

### Homeless Population

The **Bundesarbeitsgemeinschaft Wohnungslosenhilfe (BAG W)** estimated approximately 607,000 persons were homeless in Germany in 2024, of which approximately 50,000 were sleeping rough. The increase from approximately 335,000 in 2016 is largely attributed to refugee arrivals and rising housing costs in major cities.

## ECHR & EU

### European Convention on Human Rights

**Article 3 (Prohibition of Inhuman or Degrading Treatment):**

**M.S.S. v. Belgium and Greece (Application No. 30696/09, Grand Chamber, January 21, 2011):** An Afghan asylum seeker was returned from Belgium to Greece under the Dublin Regulation, where he was left destitute and homeless. The Grand Chamber found a violation of Article 3 by Greece, holding that the applicant's living conditions -- months of homelessness, sleeping in parks, without access to sanitary facilities, food, or healthcare -- amounted to degrading treatment. This landmark decision established that state inaction in the face of severe destitution, including homelessness, can violate Article 3.

**V.M. and Others v. Belgium (Application No. 60125/11, Grand Chamber, November 17, 2016):** Concerned a Roma family with children left homeless after Belgian authorities failed to provide accommodation. The Grand Chamber found a violation of Article 3, emphasizing the particular vulnerability of children.

**Article 8 (Right to Respect for Home):**

**Connors v. United Kingdom (Application No. 66746/01, May 27, 2004):** Concerned the eviction of a Gypsy/Traveller family from a local authority caravan site. The Court found a violation of Article 8, holding that the summary eviction procedure lacked adequate procedural safeguards.

**McCann v. United Kingdom (Application No. 19009/04, May 13, 2008):** The Court held that Article 8 requires that any person at risk of losing their home must be able to have the proportionality of the eviction reviewed by an independent tribunal.

**Winterstein and Others v. France (Application No. 27013/07, October 17, 2013):** The eviction of Travellers from land they had occupied for decades violated Article 8 where the authorities failed to provide adequate alternative housing.

### European Social Charter

**Article 31 (Right to Housing):**
"With a view to ensuring the effective exercise of the right to housing, the Parties undertake to take measures designed:

1. to promote access to housing of an adequate standard;
2. to prevent and reduce homelessness with a view to its gradual elimination;
3. to make the price of housing accessible to those without adequate resources."

The **European Committee of Social Rights (ECSR)** has found violations of Article 31 in numerous collective complaints:

**FEANTSA v. France (Complaint No. 39/2006, 2007):** The ECSR found France in violation of Article 31 due to insufficient efforts to reduce homelessness and inadequate emergency accommodation capacity.

**FEANTSA v. Netherlands (Complaint No. 86/2012, 2014):** Found the Netherlands in violation for failing to provide shelter to irregular migrants.

**FEANTSA v. Slovenia (Complaint No. 53/2008, 2010):** Found violations regarding inadequate access to social housing and insufficient homelessness prevention measures.

### EU Framework

**European Platform on Combatting Homelessness (Lisbon Declaration, June 2021):** All EU member states, the European Commission, and the European Parliament signed the Lisbon Declaration committing to:

- Work toward ending homelessness by 2030.
- Promote Housing First and other evidence-based approaches.
- Ensure no one sleeps rough for lack of accessible, safe, and appropriate emergency accommodation.
- Review and reform national legal frameworks that criminalize homelessness.

**European Pillar of Social Rights (Principle 19):** "Access to social housing or housing assistance of good quality shall be provided for those in need. Vulnerable people have the right to appropriate assistance and protection against forced eviction."

**EU Structural Funds:** The European Social Fund Plus (ESF+) and the European Regional Development Fund (ERDF) allocate significant funding for homelessness prevention and Housing First programs in the 2021-2027 programming period.

## Comparative Analysis

### Right to Shelter Spectrum

The jurisdictions represent a spectrum of legal entitlements:

- **Germany** provides the strongest right through the constitutional Existenzminimum combined with the municipal emergency shelter obligation. This right is enforceable through administrative courts.
- **South Korea** provides a welfare framework without a justiciable right to shelter, relying on programmatic obligations under the 2011 Act.
- **The United States** has no federal right; the handful of jurisdictions with shelter mandates (NYC, Massachusetts) represent exceptions rather than the rule, and Grants Pass eliminated the Eighth Amendment as a constraint on criminalization.
- **The ECHR/EU** establishes aspirational rights through the European Social Charter and the Lisbon Declaration, with the ECHR providing a floor through Article 3's prohibition on degrading treatment.

### Criminalization vs. Welfare Approaches

The sharpest divergence is between criminalization and welfare approaches:

- The United States, post-Grants Pass, has moved toward increased criminalization, with cities using anti-camping ordinances and encampment sweeps as primary responses to visible homelessness.
- Germany and South Korea do not criminalize homelessness and approach it as a social welfare issue.
- The EU Lisbon Declaration explicitly calls for decriminalization, placing the U.S. approach outside the international consensus.

### Housing First Adoption

Housing First has achieved broad policy consensus across all four jurisdictions as the evidence-based model for addressing chronic homelessness, but implementation varies dramatically due to housing supply constraints, funding levels, and political will.

### Eviction Prevention as Homelessness Prevention

Germany's approach illustrates how robust eviction protections (Sozialklausel, Nachholungsrecht, court-ordered evictions only) serve as the most effective homelessness prevention mechanism. The United States, with weaker eviction protections and no federal rent regulation, generates homelessness at higher rates despite spending billions on emergency shelter.
