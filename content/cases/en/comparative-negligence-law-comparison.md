## Summary

Comparative negligence -- the legal doctrine by which a plaintiff's own fault is weighed against the defendant's fault to determine the allocation of liability and the measure of recoverable damages -- addresses one of tort law's most enduring challenges: how to fairly apportion loss when both parties have contributed to the harm. South Korea applies a comprehensive comparative fault system (과실상계) under Civil Act (민법) Article 396, incorporated into tort claims through Article 763, under which courts reduce damages proportionally based on the plaintiff's share of fault with no threshold bar, and the Supreme Court has further expanded the doctrine to include the plaintiff's predisposition to injury (소인경합) and failure to mitigate as factors reducing recovery. The United States is divided among four distinct approaches -- pure contributory negligence (a complete bar in five jurisdictions), pure comparative negligence (plaintiff recovers regardless of fault percentage), modified comparative negligence with a 50% bar, and modified comparative negligence with a 51% bar -- following a nationwide reform movement that began with Florida's adoption of comparative negligence in Hoffman v. Jones (1973) and Wisconsin's earlier statutory adoption in 1931, with the Supreme Court applying comparative fault in maritime cases under United States v. Reliable Transfer Co. (1975). Germany codifies comparative fault in BGB Section 254 (Mitverschulden), applying a proportional reduction system that considers both the causal contribution and the degree of culpability of each party, with the Bundesgerichtshof developing detailed allocation guidelines for specific fact patterns. At the European level, the ECHR applies comparative fault principles in awarding just satisfaction under Article 41, the Principles of European Tort Law (PETL) Article 8:101 provides a model comparative negligence rule, and EU directives in product liability and environmental liability incorporate contributory negligence provisions.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's comparative negligence system is rooted in the Civil Act's contract law provisions, applied to tort claims by statutory cross-reference.

### Civil Act Article 396 and Article 763

Article 396 provides: "If the obligee has been negligent in causing the failure to perform or in the occurrence of damage, the court shall take this into account in determining the liability for and the amount of damages" (채권자에게 과실이 있는 때에는 법원은 손해배상의 책임 및 그 금액을 정함에 이를 참작하여야 한다). Article 763 incorporates this provision into tort law by reference.

### Sua Sponte Application

A distinctive feature of Korean comparative negligence is that courts may raise and apply comparative fault sua sponte, without the defendant raising it as an affirmative defense. The Supreme Court confirmed this principle in Supreme Court Decision 2007Da18883, holding that comparative fault is a matter of law that the court must consider in determining fair and just damages, regardless of whether the parties have raised the issue.

### Expanded Comparative Fault Concepts

Korean courts have extended the comparative fault doctrine beyond the plaintiff's direct negligence:

**Victim Predisposition (소인경합):** When the plaintiff's pre-existing physical or psychological condition contributes to the severity of injury, courts may reduce damages to account for the predisposition. The Supreme Court established the framework in Supreme Court Decision 2002Da45185 (2005), holding that a plaintiff's pre-existing spinal condition warranted a 30% reduction in damages for a traffic accident injury.

**Failure to Mitigate:** Courts treat the plaintiff's failure to obtain appropriate medical treatment or take reasonable steps to minimize loss as a form of comparative fault, reducing recovery accordingly.

**Minor's Parents:** When a minor is injured, the comparative fault of the minor's parents or guardians (감독의무 태만) may be imputed to reduce the child's recovery. The Supreme Court has applied this principle in playground accident and traffic injury cases involving children.

### Allocation Methodology

Korean courts determine fault allocation based on the totality of circumstances. Unlike American jury systems that assign specific percentages, Korean judges exercise broad discretion in determining what reduction is appropriate. The Supreme Court has indicated that allocation should consider the nature and severity of each party's negligence, the causal contribution of each party's conduct, and the overall fairness of the result.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States presents the greatest internal variation in comparative negligence approaches of any single jurisdiction.

### Historical Development

The traditional common law rule of contributory negligence, derived from Butterfield v. Forrester (1809), barred a plaintiff from any recovery if the plaintiff's own negligence contributed to the injury, however slight. This harsh rule was progressively abandoned starting in the twentieth century.

**Li v. Yellow Cab Co. of California, 13 Cal.3d 804 (1975):** The California Supreme Court judicially adopted pure comparative negligence, abandoning contributory negligence. Justice Sullivan wrote: "The all-or-nothing approach of the contributory negligence rule is inequitable both because it fails to distribute responsibility in proportion to fault and because it fails to provide a just remedy."

### Current Landscape

| System | States | Rule | Example |
|--------|--------|------|---------|
| **Pure contributory negligence** | Alabama, Maryland, North Carolina, Virginia, DC | Any plaintiff fault bars recovery entirely | Plaintiff 1% at fault: $0 recovery |
| **Pure comparative fault** | California, New York, Florida, Alaska, Arizona, Kentucky, Louisiana, Mississippi, Missouri, New Mexico, Rhode Island, South Dakota, Washington | Recovery reduced by plaintiff's percentage of fault | Plaintiff 90% at fault: recovers 10% of damages |
| **Modified (50% bar)** | Colorado, Georgia, Idaho, Kansas, Maine, Nebraska, North Dakota, Oklahoma, Tennessee, Utah, West Virginia | Plaintiff barred if 50% or more at fault | Plaintiff 49%: recovers; 50%: barred |
| **Modified (51% bar)** | Texas, Pennsylvania, Ohio, Connecticut, Delaware, Hawaii, Illinois, Indiana, Iowa, Massachusetts, Michigan, Minnesota, Montana, Nevada, New Hampshire, New Jersey, Oregon, South Carolina, Vermont, Wisconsin, Wyoming | Plaintiff barred if 51% or more at fault | Plaintiff 50%: recovers; 51%: barred |

### Joint and Several Liability Reforms

The adoption of comparative negligence has profoundly affected joint and several liability. Many states have replaced traditional joint and several liability with proportionate (several) liability, or hybrid systems:

**Several Liability Only:** Approximately 15 states (including Kansas, Utah, Vermont) impose liability only for each defendant's proportionate share of fault.

**Hybrid Systems:** Many states retain joint and several liability for economic damages but impose several liability for non-economic damages (e.g., California Proposition 51, 1986).

### Special Doctrines

**Last Clear Chance:** This doctrine, which permitted a contributorily negligent plaintiff to recover if the defendant had the last clear opportunity to avoid the harm, has been largely abolished in comparative negligence jurisdictions as unnecessary.

**Assumption of Risk:** Express assumption of risk remains a complete defense. Implied assumption of risk has been merged into comparative fault in many jurisdictions following Knight v. Jewett, 3 Cal.4th 296 (1992).

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany applies a sophisticated comparative fault system under BGB Section 254, which has been elaborated through extensive judicial development.

### BGB Section 254 -- Mitverschulden

Section 254 provides: "(1) If fault on the part of the injured person has contributed to the occurrence of the damage, the duty to compensate and the extent of compensation to be made depend upon the circumstances, in particular upon the extent to which the damage has been caused predominantly by the one or the other party. (2) This also applies if the fault of the injured person consisted of a failure to draw the attention of the debtor to the danger of unusually extensive damage which the debtor neither knew nor ought to have known, or a failure to avert or reduce the damage."

### Dual-Factor Analysis

The BGH applies a two-factor analysis to determine fault allocation:

**Causal Contribution (Verursachungsbeitrag):** The relative contribution of each party's conduct to the occurrence and extent of the damage.

**Degree of Fault (Verschuldensgrad):** The relative culpability of each party, ranging from slight negligence (leichte Fahrlässigkeit) through ordinary negligence (einfache Fahrlässigkeit) to gross negligence (grobe Fahrlässigkeit) and intent (Vorsatz).

### Specific Allocation Guidelines

German courts have developed relatively predictable allocation percentages for common fact patterns:

| Situation | Typical Plaintiff Reduction |
|-----------|---------------------------|
| Failure to wear seat belt | 20-25% (BGH consistent line of decisions) |
| Motorcycle rider without helmet | 20-30% |
| Pedestrian crossing against signal | 30-50% depending on circumstances |
| Intoxicated pedestrian in traffic accident | 25-50% depending on BAC and circumstances |
| Failure to seek medical treatment | 10-30% depending on severity and accessibility |
| Passenger with intoxicated driver | 25-50% (BGH Decision of January 18, 2000, VI ZR 175/99) |

### Children and Incapacity

BGB Section 828 establishes age-based liability thresholds for children. Children under seven are not responsible in tort (under ten for traffic accidents). For children between seven/ten and eighteen, comparative fault is assessed based on the child's individual capacity for discernment (Einsichtsfähigkeit).

### Strict Liability Contexts

In strict liability situations (e.g., traffic accidents under StVG Section 7), comparative fault under Section 254 interacts with the "operational risk" (Betriebsgefahr) of the vehicle. The BGH allocates liability considering the operational risk as a baseline, adjusted by the comparative fault of both parties.

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR applies comparative fault principles when determining just satisfaction under Article 41. The Court reduces awards when the applicant's own conduct contributed to the violation or its consequences.

**McCann and Others v. United Kingdom (Application No. 18984/91, 1995):** Although finding an Article 2 violation, the Court declined to award pecuniary damages, considering that the victims' involvement in a planned terrorist operation was relevant to the just satisfaction assessment.

**Finogenov and Others v. Russia (Application No. 18299/03, Judgment of December 20, 2011):** The Court considered the complexities of the Moscow theater hostage crisis, including the hostage-takers' conduct, in assessing the extent of state responsibility for deaths during the rescue operation.

### Principles of European Tort Law (PETL)

The PETL, published by the European Group on Tort Law in 2005, provide a model framework for comparative negligence:

**Article 8:101 -- Contributory Conduct or Activity of the Victim:** "(1) Liability can be excluded or reduced to such extent as is considered just having regard to the contributory conduct of the victim. (2) In cases of damage caused intentionally, the contributory conduct of the victim by mere negligence does not lead to a reduction of compensation. (3) In other cases, the damages are to be reduced according to the degree of fault of the respective parties, and having regard to any other relevant circumstances."

### EU Product Liability

Directive 2024/2853 (replacing 85/374/EEC) provides in Article 12 that the liability of the producer may be reduced when the damage is caused jointly by a defect in the product and by the fault of the injured person or a person for whom the injured person is responsible. Member states may not exclude this defense.

### EU Environmental Liability

Directive 2004/35/CE, Article 8(3), provides that an operator shall not be required to bear costs of remedial actions where the damage was caused by a third party and occurred despite appropriate safety measures being in place. While not a traditional comparative fault provision, it introduces allocation principles into environmental liability.

## Comparative Analysis

### Complete Bar vs. Proportional Reduction

The most dramatic doctrinal difference remains the divide between jurisdictions that bar recovery entirely when the plaintiff is at fault (pure contributory negligence -- five American jurisdictions) and those that reduce recovery proportionally (all other systems). The survival of contributory negligence in Alabama, Maryland, North Carolina, Virginia, and the District of Columbia is widely criticized but has resisted legislative reform.

### Threshold Bars

The modified comparative negligence systems (50% and 51% bars) create significant litigation consequences around the threshold percentage. In modified jurisdictions, the difference between 49% and 50% (or 50% and 51%) plaintiff fault is the difference between partial recovery and no recovery at all. This creates a cliff effect absent from pure comparative systems like those in South Korea and Germany.

### Judicial vs. Jury Allocation

South Korea and Germany vest fault allocation in the judge, while the United States relies primarily on jury determination. Judge-determined allocation tends to produce more consistent and predictable results, as evidenced by Germany's relatively stable allocation percentages for common fact patterns. Jury-determined allocation may produce more variable results but benefits from community input on fault standards.

### Sua Sponte Application

South Korea's practice of allowing courts to apply comparative fault sua sponte is unusual globally. In the United States, comparative negligence must be pleaded as an affirmative defense, and failure to raise it results in waiver. Germany allows courts to consider comparative fault without party initiative, similar to the Korean approach.
