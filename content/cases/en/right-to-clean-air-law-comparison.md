## Summary

The right to clean air has emerged as one of the most dynamic and consequential areas of environmental law, driven by growing scientific evidence of the devastating health impacts of air pollution, the rise of climate litigation, and increasing recognition of environmental rights as fundamental human rights. South Korea addresses air quality through the Framework Act on Environmental Policy (환경정책기본법), the Clean Air Conservation Act (대기환경보전법), and the Special Act on the Reduction and Management of Fine Dust (미세먼지 저감 및 관리에 관한 특별법), with the Constitutional Court recognizing a constitutional right to a healthy environment under Article 35 of the Constitution (헌법 제35조). The United States regulates air quality primarily through the Clean Air Act (42 U.S.C. Sections 7401-7671q), which establishes the National Ambient Air Quality Standards (NAAQS) system, enforced by the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), though federal courts have not recognized a constitutional right to clean air. Germany implements EU air quality law through the Federal Immission Control Act (Bundes-Immissionsschutzgesetz, BImSchG) and has been the subject of groundbreaking litigation by Deutsche Umwelthilfe (DUH) compelling cities to enforce EU air quality limits. At the EU and ECHR level, Directive 2008/50/EC on ambient air quality establishes binding limit values, while the ECHR has increasingly recognized that severe air pollution can violate Article 2 (right to life) and Article 8 (right to private and family life), as demonstrated in the landmark advisory opinion on climate obligations and the growing body of Urgenda-style climate litigation.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea has developed a comprehensive air quality regulatory framework driven by severe fine dust (미세먼지) pollution affecting the Korean Peninsula, originating both from domestic sources and transboundary pollution from China.

### Constitutional Foundation

Article 35 of the Constitution of the Republic of Korea (대한민국헌법 제35조) provides that "all citizens shall have the right to a healthy and pleasant environment" and that "the State and all citizens shall endeavor to protect the environment." The Constitutional Court (헌법재판소) has recognized this as a fundamental right with both subjective and objective dimensions.

**Constitutional Court Decision 2019Hun-Ma1154 (2021):** The Court ruled on a petition challenging the government's failure to take adequate measures against fine dust pollution, holding that while Article 35 imposes an obligation on the state to protect environmental rights, the legislature retains broad discretion in determining the specific measures to adopt. The Court declined to find a violation but emphasized the state's duty to maintain minimum air quality standards.

### Clean Air Conservation Act (대기환경보전법)

This act, first enacted in 1990 and substantially amended multiple times, establishes the emission standards framework for stationary and mobile sources, air quality monitoring requirements, and enforcement mechanisms. The Ministry of Environment (환경부) sets ambient air quality standards and emission limits, while metropolitan and local governments implement regional air quality management plans.

### Special Act on Fine Dust (미세먼지 저감 및 관리에 관한 특별법)

Enacted in 2019 in response to public outcry over severe fine dust episodes, this special act established the National Fine Dust Information Center, mandated seasonal fine dust management measures (December-March), authorized emergency reduction measures including restrictions on vehicle operation and construction activities, and created the Fine Dust Management Comprehensive Plan.

**Key Measures:** Grade 5 emission vehicle operation restrictions in the Seoul Metropolitan Area (수도권) during high pollution episodes; mandatory installation of air quality monitoring in schools and public facilities; coal-fired power plant seasonal shutdowns; and a national emissions inventory system.

### Seoul Metropolitan Area Air Quality Improvement

The Special Act on Seoul Metropolitan Area Air Quality Improvement (수도권 대기환경개선에 관한 특별법), enacted in 2003, established a cap-and-trade system for NOx and SOx emissions from large stationary sources in the Seoul Metropolitan Area, alongside total pollution load management (총량규제) for industrial facilities.

### Transboundary Air Pollution

South Korea has engaged in bilateral environmental cooperation with China through the Korea-China Environmental Cooperation Joint Committee and the Korea-China-Japan Trilateral Environment Ministers Meeting. The Agreement on Cooperation on Transboundary Air Pollution between Korea, China, and Japan remains a diplomatic framework rather than a binding legal instrument, creating frustration among Korean citizens regarding the inability to compel Chinese emission reductions.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has the world's most developed statutory framework for air quality regulation through the Clean Air Act (CAA), though it lacks constitutional environmental rights and has experienced significant regulatory rollbacks and judicial limitations on federal agency authority.

### Clean Air Act Structure

**National Ambient Air Quality Standards (NAAQS):** The EPA sets primary standards (protecting public health with an adequate margin of safety) and secondary standards (protecting public welfare including visibility, crops, and buildings) for six criteria pollutants: PM2.5 and PM10, ozone, NO2, SO2, CO, and lead.

**2024 PM2.5 Standard Revision:** In February 2024, the EPA finalized a strengthened annual PM2.5 standard from 12 μg/m³ to 9 μg/m³, the most significant tightening in over a decade, bringing the US standard closer to WHO recommendations.

**State Implementation Plans (SIPs):** States must develop and implement plans to achieve NAAQS, with EPA retaining authority to impose a Federal Implementation Plan (FIP) if state plans are inadequate.

**New Source Performance Standards (NSPS):** Section 111 authorizes EPA to set emission standards for new and modified stationary sources.

### Key Litigation

**Massachusetts v. EPA, 549 U.S. 497 (2007):** The Supreme Court held that greenhouse gases are "air pollutants" under the Clean Air Act and that EPA had authority and obligation to regulate them. This landmark decision established that states have standing to challenge federal agency inaction on climate-related air pollution.

**West Virginia v. EPA, 597 U.S. 697 (2022):** The Supreme Court invoked the "major questions doctrine" to hold that EPA could not use Section 111(d) of the Clean Air Act to implement the Clean Power Plan's generation-shifting approach to reducing power sector emissions. This decision significantly constrained EPA's authority to address climate change through existing statutory authority.

**Juliana v. United States, 947 F.3d 1159 (9th Cir. 2020):** Twenty-one youth plaintiffs alleged that the federal government's actions in causing climate change violated their constitutional rights, including a claimed substantive due process right to a clean environment. The Ninth Circuit held that while the plaintiffs had demonstrated standing, the relief sought -- an order compelling the government to develop a comprehensive climate plan -- was not within the judicial power. The case was remanded and ultimately dismissed, though it inspired climate litigation worldwide.

### Environmental Justice

**Executive Order 12898 (1994):** Directed federal agencies to identify and address disproportionate environmental and health effects of federal programs on minority and low-income populations.

**Cumulative Impact Assessments:** California's AB 617 (2017) requires air quality monitoring and emission reduction in communities with high cumulative exposure burdens, representing a state-level model for environmental justice in air quality regulation.

### California's Special Role

The Clean Air Act Section 209(b) permits California to obtain a waiver to set stricter vehicle emission standards than federal standards. California's Advanced Clean Cars II regulation, requiring 100% zero-emission vehicle sales by 2035, and the adoption of California standards by 17 other states create a de facto dual federal-state regulatory system.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's air quality law operates within the EU legal framework, with domestic litigation serving as a primary enforcement mechanism for EU air quality standards.

### Bundes-Immissionsschutzgesetz (BImSchG)

The Federal Immission Control Act, originally enacted in 1974, is the foundational German legislation on air pollution control. It establishes the framework for emission permits (Genehmigungen), immission protection requirements, and air quality planning.

**39. BImSchV:** The 39th Ordinance implementing the BImSchG transposes EU Directive 2008/50/EC into German law, establishing limit values for PM10, PM2.5, NO2, SO2, lead, benzene, CO, and ozone. Exceedances of limit values trigger obligations for competent authorities to adopt air quality action plans (Luftqualitätspläne or Luftreinhaltepläne).

### Deutsche Umwelthilfe (DUH) Air Quality Litigation

The environmental organization DUH has been the most aggressive litigant in European air quality enforcement, bringing successful cases against dozens of German cities for failing to comply with EU NO2 limit values.

**BVerwG, Urteil vom 27.02.2018 -- 7 C 30.17 (Stuttgart):** The Federal Administrative Court (Bundesverwaltungsgericht) held that cities may impose diesel driving bans as part of air quality action plans when other measures are insufficient to achieve EU limit values. The decision established that proportionality permits zonal diesel bans (initially for Euro 4 and below, later extended to Euro 5) and that the lack of a specific statutory basis for driving bans did not preclude them, as they could be imposed under general air quality planning authority.

**BVerwG, Urteil vom 27.02.2018 -- 7 C 26.16 (Düsseldorf):** The companion case confirmed the same principles for Düsseldorf, leading to a wave of diesel driving ban litigation across Germany.

**Subsequent Implementations:** Stuttgart, Hamburg, Berlin, Darmstadt, and other cities have implemented zonal diesel driving bans for older diesel vehicles, with significant impacts on urban mobility and vehicle resale values.

### Neubauer v. Germany (BVerfG, 1 BvR 2656/18, March 24, 2021)

The Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) held in this landmark climate case that Germany's Federal Climate Protection Act (Bundes-Klimaschutzgesetz, KSG) was partially unconstitutional because its emissions reduction targets for the period after 2030 were insufficiently specified, effectively deferring excessive reduction burdens to future generations. The Court found that Article 20a GG (state obligation to protect natural foundations of life) in conjunction with fundamental rights created an "intertemporal guarantee of freedom" (intertemporale Freiheitssicherung) prohibiting the state from consuming the remaining carbon budget so rapidly that future generations would face disproportionate restrictions on their freedoms.

**Impact:** The decision prompted the German government to amend the KSG within months, advancing the climate neutrality target from 2050 to 2045 and tightening intermediate targets.

### Clean Air Plans and Urban Mobility

German cities are required under Section 47 BImSchG to adopt clean air plans when air quality limit values are exceeded. These plans typically include measures such as low emission zones (Umweltzonen), promotion of electric mobility, expansion of public transport, and traffic management. The green zone sticker system (Umweltplakette) restricts entry to city center zones based on vehicle emission standards.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Directive 2008/50/EC on Ambient Air Quality

This directive establishes legally binding limit values for air pollutants that member states must achieve by specified deadlines. The limit values for NO2 (40 μg/m³ annual mean, to be achieved by 2010/2015) and PM10 (40 μg/m³ annual mean, 50 μg/m³ 24-hour with 35 exceedances permitted) have been the primary focus of enforcement litigation.

**Commission v. Bulgaria (C-488/15, 2017):** The CJEU held that Bulgaria had systematically and persistently exceeded PM10 limit values, constituting an infringement of Directive 2008/50/EC.

**Commission v. Germany (C-635/18, 2021):** The CJEU found that Germany had systematically exceeded NO2 limit values in 26 zones, including Berlin, Munich, Stuttgart, and Hamburg, constituting a breach of the directive.

**ClientEarth v. Secretary of State for the Environment (C-404/13, 2014):** The CJEU held that individuals and environmental organizations may invoke Directive 2008/50/EC before national courts to compel adoption of air quality plans, and that national courts must order competent authorities to draw up plans within the shortest possible time.

### Proposed Revised Air Quality Directive (2024)

The European Commission proposed a revision of Directive 2008/50/EC in October 2022, with political agreement reached in 2024. Key changes include: aligning EU limit values more closely with WHO 2021 guidelines (PM2.5 annual limit from 25 to 10 μg/m³; NO2 annual limit from 40 to 20 μg/m³); strengthening citizens' right to compensation for health damage caused by air quality standard violations; enhancing access to justice provisions; and establishing a requirement for air quality roadmaps for member states that cannot meet the new standards by 2030.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Fadeyeva v. Russia (Application No. 55723/00, Judgment of June 9, 2005):** The Court held that the state's failure to relocate residents living near a severely polluting steel plant violated Article 8. The Court established that environmental pollution, even without direct physical harm, can constitute an interference with private and family life when it reaches a certain level of severity.

**Tatar v. Romania (Application No. 67021/01, Judgment of January 27, 2009):** Following a sodium cyanide spill from a gold mine, the Court held that the state violated Article 8 by failing to protect the applicants from environmental harm, emphasizing the precautionary principle and the state's positive obligations to regulate hazardous activities.

**Cordella and Others v. Italy (Application Nos. 54414/13 and 54264/15, Judgment of January 24, 2019):** The Court found violations of Articles 8 and 13 regarding the severe pollution caused by the ILVA steel plant in Taranto, holding that Italy had failed to take effective measures to protect the health of the population despite decades of evidence of harmful emissions.

**KlimaSeniorinnen v. Switzerland (Application No. 53600/20, Grand Chamber, April 9, 2024):** In this landmark judgment, the ECHR Grand Chamber held for the first time that a state's failure to adequately address climate change violated Article 8 of the Convention. The Court found that Switzerland had failed to comply with its positive obligations by not having an adequate framework for climate change mitigation, including quantified emission reduction targets and a carbon budget. While the Court rejected individual standing for the applicants, it recognized the standing of the applicant association (Verein KlimaSeniorinnen Schweiz) and established that Convention obligations encompass climate change mitigation.

### Urgenda-Style Litigation Across Europe

**Urgenda Foundation v. State of the Netherlands (Hoge Raad, December 20, 2019, ECLI:NL:HR:2019:2007):** The Dutch Supreme Court upheld the order requiring the Netherlands to reduce greenhouse gas emissions by at least 25% by 2020 compared to 1990, grounding the obligation in Articles 2 and 8 ECHR. This was the first case in which a court ordered a government to reduce emissions based on human rights obligations.

**Affaire de l'Environnement -- Grande-Synthe (Conseil d'État, November 19, 2020, No. 427301):** The French Conseil d'État ordered the government to demonstrate compliance with the Paris Agreement reduction trajectory, finding that the commune of Grande-Synthe had standing due to its coastal vulnerability to climate change.

## Comparative Analysis

### Constitutional vs. Statutory Rights

The jurisdictions examined reveal a spectrum of legal protection for clean air. South Korea and Germany provide constitutional foundations (Article 35 and Article 20a GG, respectively), though their justiciability differs significantly -- the Korean right is more directly individually enforceable while the German provision is a state objective clause (Staatszielbestimmung) that gains individual force only through fundamental rights. The United States relies entirely on statutory protection through the Clean Air Act, with federal courts consistently declining to recognize a constitutional right to a clean environment. The ECHR framework has developed the most dynamic approach, deriving clean air protections from existing rights to life and private life.

### Enforcement Mechanisms

The most significant divergence lies in enforcement. The US Clean Air Act's citizen suit provision (Section 304) permits individuals to sue the EPA or polluters for violations, though standing requirements post-Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife, 504 U.S. 555 (1992), limit access. Germany has seen the most effective enforcement through DUH litigation compelling diesel bans and air quality plan improvements. The EU Commission's infringement proceedings provide a systemic enforcement mechanism, while the ECHR increasingly provides a human rights-based enforcement pathway.

### Climate-Air Quality Nexus

The integration of climate change into air quality law represents a frontier across all jurisdictions. The German Constitutional Court's Neubauer decision, the ECHR's KlimaSeniorinnen judgment, and the Dutch Supreme Court's Urgenda ruling establish that states have legally binding obligations to mitigate climate change, with implications for air quality regulation. The US Supreme Court's West Virginia v. EPA decision, by contrast, constrains regulatory authority to address climate-related air quality.

### Transboundary Challenges

Air pollution inherently crosses borders. South Korea faces distinctive transboundary challenges from Chinese pollution with limited legal remedies. The EU framework provides supranational standards and enforcement, but transboundary pollution from non-EU sources (maritime shipping, industrial emissions from Eastern neighbors) remains problematic. International law, including the UNECE Convention on Long-Range Transboundary Air Pollution (CLRTAP), provides cooperative frameworks but limited enforcement.
