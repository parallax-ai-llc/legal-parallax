## Summary

Religious freedom across the Global South presents a fundamentally different landscape from the Western church-state separation model, shaped by socialist state atheism, syncretic religious traditions, colonial-era religious law codification, and constitutional frameworks that simultaneously guarantee religious freedom while institutionalizing religious identity in governance. China operates under a system of state-regulated religion, where the CPC's "Sinicization of religion" (zongjiao zhongguohua) policy, formalized under Xi Jinping from 2015, requires all religions to adapt their doctrines, practices, and organizational structures to conform to "socialist core values" and Chinese cultural norms; the Regulations on Religious Affairs (2017, effective February 2018) establish a comprehensive licensing regime under which only five recognized "patriotic religious associations" (Buddhism, Taoism, Islam, Catholicism, Protestantism) may operate legally. India's constitutional "secularism" (dharma nirpekshata) -- a concept the Supreme Court has described as "equal treatment of all religions" rather than separation of religion and state -- coexists with an elaborate system of religious personal laws governing marriage, divorce, inheritance, and adoption differently for Hindus, Muslims, Christians, Sikhs, and Parsis; the rise of Hindutva political ideology and anti-conversion legislation in multiple states has generated acute contemporary tensions, culminating in cases like Indian Young Lawyers Association v. State of Kerala (2018) (Sabarimala temple entry) and the politically charged Ayodhya verdict in M. Siddiq v. Mahant Suresh Das (2019). Nigeria operates under a tripartite legal system where federal constitutional rights coexist with Sharia law in twelve northern states (expanded from 1999-2002 under civilian governors) and customary law in southern states; the Sharia criminal codes have generated international attention for provisions including hudud punishments. South Africa's Constitution (Section 15) guarantees freedom of religion while permitting recognition of religious marriages and personal law systems, tested in cases like Minister of Home Affairs v. Fourie (2006) where same-sex marriage rights conflicted with religious objections. Brazil, historically the world's largest Catholic country, has experienced a dramatic Protestant evangelical surge, with the 1988 Constitution's Article 5(VI) guaranteeing inviolability of freedom of conscience and belief alongside Article 19(I) prohibiting establishment or subsidization of religious organizations; meanwhile, Afro-Brazilian religions (Candomble, Umbanda) face persistent discrimination despite legal protection. Indonesia's Pancasila framework mandates belief in "the One and Only God" (Ketuhanan Yang Maha Esa) as its first principle, operationalized through recognition of six official religions (Islam, Protestantism, Catholicism, Hinduism, Buddhism, Confucianism), while indigenous belief systems (aliran kepercayaan) gained partial recognition only through Constitutional Court Decision No. 97/PUU-XIV/2016; the Philippines, as Southeast Asia's only majority-Catholic nation, navigates its 1987 Constitution's non-establishment clause alongside the Bangsamoro Organic Law (Republic Act No. 11054, 2018) creating a Muslim-majority autonomous region with limited Sharia jurisdiction.

## China

### Constitutional Framework and Party Doctrine

Article 36 of the PRC Constitution provides: "Citizens of the People's Republic of China enjoy freedom of religious belief. No State organ, public organization, or individual may compel citizens to believe in, or not to believe in, any religion; nor may they discriminate against citizens who believe in, or do not believe in, any religion." Article 36(3) adds: "The State protects normal religious activities," while clarifying that "no one may make use of religion to engage in activities that disrupt public order, impair the health of citizens, or interfere with the educational system of the State." Article 36(4) prohibits religious bodies and affairs from being "subject to any foreign domination."

The CPC's relationship with religion is governed by the 1982 Document No. 19 ("The Basic Viewpoint and Policy on the Religious Question during Our Country's Socialist Period"), which frames religion as a historical phenomenon that will naturally wither under socialism but must be managed in the interim. CPC members are expressly prohibited from holding religious beliefs under Party discipline regulations.

### Sinicization of Religion

Xi Jinping's directive at the 2015 United Front Work Conference that religions must "Sinicize" (zhongguohua) was formalized in the Revised Regulations on Religious Affairs (Zongjiao Shiwu Tiaoli), promulgated by the State Council on September 7, 2017, effective February 1, 2018. Key provisions include: Article 4 (religious affairs administered by the State Council's Religious Affairs Bureau); Article 22 (religious venues must register and accept inspection); Article 41 (prohibition on religious education outside registered venues); and Article 57 (prohibition on organizing religious activities without approval, with penalties including fines of RMB 100,000-300,000).

The five "patriotic religious associations" -- the Buddhist Association of China, Chinese Taoist Association, Islamic Association of China, Chinese Patriotic Catholic Association, and the Three-Self Patriotic Movement (Protestant) -- serve as the organizational intermediaries between the state and believers. The Vatican-China Provisional Agreement (September 2018, renewed 2020 and 2022) partially addressed the appointment of Catholic bishops, though the "underground" Catholic church loyal to Rome continues to face persecution.

### Uyghur Muslims and Tibetan Buddhists

The systematic repression of Uyghur Muslims in Xinjiang -- documented by the UN Office of the High Commissioner for Human Rights in its August 2022 assessment finding "serious human rights violations" that "may constitute international crimes, in particular crimes against humanity" -- represents the most extreme manifestation of China's religious control. The "vocational education and training centers" (zhiye jineng jiaoyu peixun zhongxin), operational from approximately 2017, held an estimated one million or more Uyghurs. The Xinjiang Uyghur Autonomous Region Regulation on De-extremification (2017) prohibits "abnormal" beards, veils, and refusal to watch state television.

In Tibet, the State Religious Affairs Bureau Order No. 5 (Management Measures for the Reincarnation of Living Buddhas in Tibetan Buddhism, 2007) asserts state authority over the selection of reincarnated lamas, including the eventual successor to the Dalai Lama. The Panchen Lama dispute -- with China's selected Gyaltsen Norbu replacing the Dalai Lama's recognized Gedhun Choekyi Nyima, detained since 1995 at age six -- exemplifies state control over religious succession.

### Underground Christianity

Unregistered Protestant house churches (jiating jiaohui) and Catholic communities loyal to Rome constitute an estimated 60-100 million believers (Pew Research Center estimates). The 2018 Regulations tightened enforcement against unregistered venues, and cross-removal campaigns in Zhejiang Province (2014-2016) saw over 1,500 crosses removed from registered churches. The detention of Pastor Wang Yi of Early Rain Covenant Church (Chengdu) in December 2018, sentenced to nine years for "inciting subversion," illustrated enforcement against prominent house church leaders.

## India

### Constitutional Secularism

Articles 25 through 28 of the Indian Constitution form the religious freedom architecture. Article 25(1) guarantees "freedom of conscience and the right freely to profess, practise and propagate religion," subject to "public order, morality and health" and other fundamental rights. Article 25(2) empowers the State to regulate "economic, financial, political or other secular activity which may be associated with religious practice" and to provide for "social welfare and reform or the throwing open of Hindu religious institutions of a public character to all classes and sections of Hindus." Article 26 guarantees religious denominations the right to manage their own affairs, establish institutions, and own property. Article 27 prohibits compelling any person to pay taxes for promotion of any religion. Article 28 restricts religious instruction in state-funded educational institutions.

The word "secular" was added to the Preamble by the 42nd Amendment (1976), though the Supreme Court had already recognized secularism as a basic feature of the Constitution in S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994) 3 SCC 1. In this decision, a nine-judge bench held that secularism meant "equal treatment of all religions" rather than Western-style separation, and that the dismissal of state governments for communal activities was constitutionally valid.

### Religious Personal Laws

India's retention of religion-specific personal laws represents one of the most significant departures from the uniform civil code model. The Hindu Marriage Act (1955), Hindu Succession Act (1956), Hindu Minority and Guardianship Act (1956), and Hindu Adoptions and Maintenance Act (1956) govern Hindus (including Sikhs, Jains, and Buddhists by legal definition). Muslim personal law is governed primarily by the Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act (1937), supplemented by judicial interpretation.

The Supreme Court's decision in Shah Bano v. Mohd. Ahmed Khan (1985 (2) SCC 556) -- granting maintenance to a divorced Muslim woman under Section 125 CrPC rather than solely under Muslim personal law -- triggered massive political controversy, leading Parliament to enact the Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Divorce) Act, 1986, effectively overriding the judgment. The Court later reinterpreted this statute in Danial Latifi v. Union of India (2001 (7) SCC 740) to restore substantive maintenance rights.

The Triple Talaq (instant divorce) case, Shayara Bano v. Union of India (2017 (9) SCC 1), saw a five-judge bench strike down the practice of instantaneous triple talaq (talaq-e-biddat) as unconstitutional by a 3:2 majority, leading to the Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Marriage) Act, 2019, which criminalized the practice with up to three years imprisonment.

Article 44 of the Constitution (Directive Principles) states: "The State shall endeavour to secure for the citizens a uniform civil code throughout the territory of India." This provision has remained unimplemented at the national level, though Goa maintains a Uniform Civil Code inherited from the Portuguese Civil Code of 1867.

### Anti-Conversion Laws and Hindutva

Multiple Indian states have enacted "Freedom of Religion" acts prohibiting conversion by "force, allurement, or fraudulent means." The earliest was Odisha's Freedom of Religion Act (1967). The most controversial recent enactment is the Uttar Pradesh Prohibition of Unlawful Conversion of Religion Act (2021), which presumes conversions motivated by marriage (so-called "love jihad") to be unlawful and places the burden of proof on the accused. The Allahabad High Court in Salamat Ansari v. State of UP (2020) held that the right to choose a life partner is a fundamental right under Article 21, challenging the constitutional validity of such provisions.

### Sabarimala and Ayodhya

Indian Young Lawyers Association v. State of Kerala (2018 (SC)), the Sabarimala case, saw a 4:1 majority hold that the exclusion of women of menstruating age (10-50) from the Sabarimala temple violated Articles 14, 15, 25, and 26. Justice D.Y. Chandrachud wrote that "patriarchy in religion cannot be permitted to trump the right to worship." The dissent by Justice Indu Malhotra argued for non-interference with essential religious practices. A nine-judge bench review was subsequently ordered but remains pending.

In M. Siddiq (D) Thr. Lrs. v. Mahant Suresh Das (2019), the Supreme Court unanimously awarded the disputed Ayodhya site (where the Babri Masjid had stood until its demolition in 1992) to the Hindu parties for the construction of a Ram Temple, while directing the government to provide an alternative five-acre plot to the Muslim parties. The Court found that the demolition of the mosque was "an egregious violation of the rule of law" but relied on archaeological and historical evidence to determine the property title.

## Africa

### Nigeria: Sharia and Constitutional Tension

Nigeria's Constitution (1999) in Section 10 states: "The Government of the Federation or of a State shall not adopt any religion as State Religion." Section 38(1) guarantees "freedom of thought, conscience and religion, including freedom to change his religion or belief." Yet twelve northern states -- Zamfara (1999, first), Kano, Sokoto, Katsina, Bauchi, Borno, Jigawa, Kebbi, Niger, Gombe, Yobe, and Kaduna (partially) -- adopted expanded Sharia criminal codes from 1999 to 2002, applying hudud punishments including amputation for theft and stoning for adultery.

The constitutionality of these Sharia codes has never been definitively resolved by the Supreme Court, though they are technically limited in application to Muslims. The case of Amina Lawal, sentenced to death by stoning for adultery by a Sharia court in Katsina State in 2002 (overturned on appeal in 2003 by the Sharia Court of Appeal), generated international attention and revealed the tension between federal constitutional rights and state Sharia jurisdiction. Section 275 of the Constitution establishes Sharia Courts of Appeal in states that require them, while Section 277 limits their jurisdiction to questions of Islamic personal law and criminal law applicable to Muslims.

The Boko Haram insurgency (from 2009) and its offshoot ISWAP have added a violent dimension to Nigeria's religious landscape. The National Human Rights Commission and Amnesty International have documented extrajudicial killings, mass detention, and forced disappearances in the military response, raising questions about the protection of Muslim civilians' religious rights in conflict zones.

### South Africa: Religious Pluralism in the Rainbow Nation

Section 15(1) of the South African Constitution provides: "Everyone has the right to freedom of conscience, religion, thought, belief and opinion." Section 15(2) permits "religious observances... at state or state-aided institutions, provided that those observances follow rules made by the appropriate public authorities, that they are conducted on an equitable basis, and that attendance at them is free and voluntary." Section 15(3) allows Parliament to enact legislation recognizing marriages concluded under "any tradition, or a system of religious, personal or family law."

The Constitutional Court addressed the tension between religious freedom and equality rights in Minister of Home Affairs v. Fourie (2005 (1) SA 524 (CC)), holding that the exclusion of same-sex couples from marriage was unconstitutional. Justice Sachs's opinion acknowledged religious objections but held that "the Constitution does not permit the state to discriminate against people on the basis of sexual orientation."

In Christian Education South Africa v. Minister of Education (2000 (4) SA 757 (CC)), the Court upheld the prohibition on corporal punishment in schools despite religious objections, establishing that religious freedom does not override constitutional protections for children's dignity and bodily integrity.

The Recognition of Customary Marriages Act (120 of 1998) provides statutory recognition of marriages concluded under African customary law, including polygynous marriages (Section 2(3)), while the South African Law Reform Commission has been working on proposed legislation to recognize Muslim marriages, a process that has extended for over two decades without resolution. The Women's Legal Centre Trust v. President of the Republic of South Africa (2018) case declared the government's failure to enact Muslim marriage legislation unconstitutional.

### African Union Framework

The African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights (1981), Article 8, provides: "Freedom of conscience, the profession and free practice of religion shall be guaranteed. No one may, subject to law and order, be submitted to measures restricting the exercise of these freedoms." The Protocol to the African Charter on the Rights of Women in Africa (Maputo Protocol, 2003) addresses the intersection of religious practices and women's rights, requiring states in Article 5 to prohibit harmful practices including those justified by religion.

## Latin America

### Brazilian Religious Landscape

Brazil's Constitution of 1988 (Article 5(VI)) provides: "Freedom of conscience and of belief is inviolable, the free exercise of religious practices being ensured and, according to law, protection of places of worship and their rites being guaranteed." Article 5(VIII) states that "no one shall be deprived of rights by reason of religious belief or philosophical or political conviction." Article 19(I) prohibits the state from "establishing religious sects or churches, subsidizing them, embarrassing their functioning, or maintaining with them or their representatives dependency or alliance relations."

Despite this secular framework, Afro-Brazilian religions face persistent discrimination. The Federal Public Ministry reported over 500 cases of religious intolerance registered between 2015 and 2023, with Candomble and Umbanda practitioners comprising the majority of victims. Rio de Janeiro's State Law No. 3,459/2000 prohibiting religious discrimination was one of the first specific protections. The STF in RE 494601 (2019) upheld the constitutionality of animal sacrifice in religious ceremonies (relevant to Candomble practice), an important ruling recognizing Afro-Brazilian religious practices as constitutionally protected.

The explosive growth of evangelical Protestantism (approximately 31% of Brazilians by 2022 census, up from 15% in 2000) has transformed the political landscape. The bancada evangelica (evangelical caucus) in the National Congress has pushed for legislation reflecting conservative religious values, including opposition to LGBTQ+ rights and proposed restrictions on "religious proselytization" that critics argue would limit Afro-Brazilian religious expression.

### Colombian and Mexican Secularism

Colombia transitioned from constitutional Catholicism (the 1886 Constitution's Concordat with the Vatican) to religious pluralism with the 1991 Constitution (Article 19: "Freedom of worship is guaranteed. Everyone has the right to profess their religion freely and to disseminate it individually or collectively"). The Constitutional Court in Sentencia C-350/94 struck down provisions of the Concordat that granted the Catholic Church exclusive authority over marriage registration.

Mexico's relationship with religion is historically among the most fraught in Latin America. The Reform War (1857-1861) and the Cristero War (1926-1929) produced a constitutional framework of extreme laicidad (secularism). The 1917 Constitution (Article 130) stripped churches of legal personality, prohibited clergy from voting or holding office, and nationalized all church property. Reforms in 1992 (under President Salinas) restored limited legal recognition through the Law of Religious Associations and Public Worship (Ley de Asociaciones Religiosas y Culto Publico), granting churches the right to own property and clergy the right to vote, while maintaining strict separation of church and state in education and politics.

## Southeast Asia

### Indonesian Pancasila: Mandated Monotheism

Indonesia's foundational philosophy, Pancasila, places "Belief in the One and Only God" (Ketuhanan Yang Maha Esa) as its first principle. Article 29(1) of the 1945 Constitution states: "The State shall be based upon the belief in the One and Only God." Article 29(2) provides: "The State guarantees all persons the freedom of worship, each according to his/her own religion or belief."

Until 2017, Indonesian identity cards (KTP) required citizens to select one of six recognized religions: Islam, Protestantism, Catholicism, Hinduism, Buddhism, or Confucianism (reinstated in 2006 after its removal during the Suharto era). Indigenous belief systems (aliran kepercayaan or penghayat kepercayaan) were classified separately from "religion" and faced systematic discrimination. Constitutional Court Decision No. 97/PUU-XIV/2016 ruled that adherents of indigenous beliefs could leave the religion column blank or list their specific belief system on identity documents, a partial but significant recognition.

The Ahmadiyya Muslim community faces particular persecution. Joint Ministerial Decree (SKB) No. 3/2008 "warned" Ahmadis to cease "spreading interpretations and activities that deviate from the principal teachings of Islam," effectively restricting their religious practice. Local regulations in at least 28 districts have banned Ahmadiyya activities. The National Human Rights Commission (Komnas HAM) has repeatedly criticized these restrictions as violating constitutional guarantees.

Article 156a of the Indonesian Criminal Code (KUHP) -- the blasphemy provision, inserted by Presidential Decree No. 1/PNPS/1965 and elevated to statute by Law No. 5/1969 -- criminalizes "intentional public expression of feelings or committing an act that in principle has the character of hostility, abuse, or defamation of a religion adhered to in Indonesia." The Constitutional Court upheld this provision in Decision No. 140/PUU-VII/2009, with the majority holding that it was necessary to maintain religious harmony. Between 2004 and 2023, the Setara Institute documented over 150 blasphemy prosecutions, with a conviction rate exceeding 97%.

### Philippine Religious Pluralism and the Bangsamoro

The Philippine Constitution (1987) Article III, Section 5 provides: "No law shall be made respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof. The free exercise and enjoyment of religious profession and worship, without discrimination or preference, shall forever be allowed." Section 6 provides that "the separation of Church and State shall be inviolable."

Despite this non-establishment clause, the Catholic Church exercises enormous social and political influence. The Reproductive Health Law (Republic Act No. 10354, 2012), which guaranteed universal access to contraception and reproductive health education, was challenged by Catholic bishops and subjected to Supreme Court review in Imbong v. Ochoa (G.R. No. 204819, 2014), where the Court upheld its constitutionality with limited exceptions.

The Bangsamoro Organic Law (Republic Act No. 11054, 2018), ratified by plebiscite in 2019, created the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao (BARMM), replacing the Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao (ARMM). The BOL provides for a Sharia judicial system (Article IX) with jurisdiction over personal law matters for Muslims, including family law and commercial disputes where all parties are Muslims. The Sharia courts operate under the Code of Muslim Personal Laws (Presidential Decree No. 1083, 1977), which permits polygamy (up to four wives) for Muslim men, in contrast to the Family Code of the Philippines (Executive Order No. 209, 1987) which mandates monogamy for non-Muslims.

## Comparative Analysis

### Models of State-Religion Relations

This five-region comparison reveals at least four distinct models of state-religion relations in the Global South, none of which maps neatly onto the Western church-state separation paradigm.

First, China represents the "state management" model, where religion is viewed as a potential threat to Party authority that must be channeled through state-controlled organizations. Religious freedom exists only within the boundaries set by the Party, and the concept of "Sinicization" explicitly requires religions to subordinate their theological content to political directives.

Second, India and Indonesia represent "pluralist management" models, where the state does not adopt a single religion but actively manages religious plurality through personal law systems, recognized-religion frameworks, and anti-blasphemy provisions. Both countries treat religion as a constitutive element of citizenship rather than a private matter.

Third, Nigeria illustrates the "dual system" model, where a secular federal constitution coexists with sub-national Sharia systems, creating persistent jurisdictional tensions that remain unresolved decades after implementation.

Fourth, Brazil and the Philippines represent "formal secularism with cultural hegemony" models, where constitutional non-establishment coexists with dominant religious influence on legislation and social norms.

### Blasphemy and Religious Harmony

The persistence of blasphemy provisions across the Global South -- Indonesia's Article 156a, India's Sections 295A/299, Nigeria's criminal codes, and the practical effect of China's "religious harmony" regulations -- reveals a shared commitment to protecting religious feelings that contrasts with the Western trend toward abolishing blasphemy laws. The philosophical basis differs: Indonesia and India frame blasphemy laws as protecting communal harmony in multi-religious societies, while China frames religious regulation as protecting social stability and Party authority.

### Gender and Religious Law

The intersection of religious freedom and women's rights presents the sharpest tensions. India's personal law system, Nigeria's Sharia codes, Indonesia's marriage law (which permits polygamy under religious law), and the Philippines' Code of Muslim Personal Laws all create situations where women's constitutional equality rights may be subordinated to religious norms. The Maputo Protocol's Article 5 obligation to eliminate harmful practices "justified by tradition, culture, religion or superstition" articulates the tension without fully resolving it.
