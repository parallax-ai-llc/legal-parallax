## Summary

Military justice -- the system of laws, procedures, and institutions governing the discipline and criminal prosecution of members of the armed forces -- occupies a unique position at the intersection of criminal law, constitutional governance, and military necessity, with each jurisdiction striking a different balance between command authority and judicial independence. South Korea operates a distinct military court system under the Military Court Act (군사법원법) and the Military Criminal Act (군형법), which was substantially reformed in 2021 to transfer most peacetime criminal jurisdiction from military to civilian courts following decades of criticism over command influence and inadequate protections for victims of military sexual violence. The United States administers military justice through the Uniform Code of Military Justice (UCMJ, 10 U.S.C. Sections 801-946), originally enacted in 1950 and most recently reformed by the Military Justice Improvement and Increasing Prevention Act of 2021 (effective December 2023), which removed commanders' authority to decide whether to prosecute serious offenses including sexual assault, transferring that power to independent special trial counsel. Germany abolished separate military courts entirely under Article 96(2) of the Grundgesetz (Basic Law), which permits their establishment only during a state of defense (Verteidigungsfall); in peacetime, all service members are subject to the ordinary criminal courts, with disciplinary matters handled through the Wehrdisziplinarordnung (Military Discipline Code) and judicial review by the Truppendienstgerichte (Military Service Courts). At the European level, the European Court of Human Rights has developed substantial jurisprudence on military justice under Articles 5, 6, and 13 of the Convention, consistently requiring that military tribunals meet the same standards of independence and impartiality as civilian courts, as established in landmark cases including Findlay v. United Kingdom (1997) and Morris v. United Kingdom (2002).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's military justice system reflects the country's status as a conscription-based military force facing an ongoing security threat from North Korea. The constitutional basis for military courts is found in Article 110 of the Constitution of the Republic of Korea (대한민국 헌법), which provides for the establishment of military courts to exercise jurisdiction over military criminal cases. The system operates under two principal statutes: the Military Court Act (군사법원법), governing procedure and court organization, and the Military Criminal Act (군형법), defining substantive military offenses.

### 2021 Reform

The Military Court Act was comprehensively amended effective July 1, 2022, following the death of Air Force Master Sergeant Lee Ye-ram, who died by suicide in May 2021 after reporting sexual assault by a fellow service member and facing retaliation and institutional obstruction. The case provoked national outrage and accelerated reform efforts that had been under consideration for over a decade.

**Key Changes:**
- Transfer of peacetime jurisdiction over sexual violence, murder, and death-resulting offenses from military to civilian courts
- Reduction of commanding officers' authority to convene and influence courts-martial
- Establishment of an independent military prosecution organization (군검찰단) separate from the chain of command
- Enhanced victim protection measures including mandatory victim advocates and restricted contact orders

### Military Criminal Act (군형법)

The Military Criminal Act defines offenses specific to military service, including:

| Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|
| Desertion (탈영, Art. 30) | 1-10 years imprisonment |
| Insubordination (항명, Art. 44) | Up to 7 years imprisonment |
| Mutiny (반란, Art. 5) | Death, life imprisonment, or 5+ years |
| Espionage (간첩, Art. 13) | Death or life imprisonment |
| Absence without leave (무단이탈) | Up to 3 years imprisonment |
| Assault on superior officer (상관폭행, Art. 39) | Up to 7 years imprisonment |

### Constitutional Court Decisions

The Constitutional Court of Korea (헌법재판소) has issued several landmark decisions shaping military justice:

- **2013Hun-Ka12 (2016):** Struck down the Military Criminal Act provision criminalizing consensual sexual conduct between same-sex service members (Article 92-6), though this decision was subsequently reversed in a narrow ruling in 2023 before the provision was finally struck down again in 2024.
- **2008Hun-Ka22 (2009):** Found that the military court system's lack of adequate independence from commanding officers violated the right to a fair trial under Article 27 of the Constitution.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The Uniform Code of Military Justice (UCMJ), enacted by Congress on May 5, 1950, and codified at 10 U.S.C. Sections 801-946, provides the comprehensive statutory framework for U.S. military justice. The UCMJ applies to all members of the armed forces, including reserves on active duty, cadets, midshipmen, and in certain circumstances, civilians accompanying the armed forces.

**Court-Martial System:** The UCMJ establishes three types of courts-martial:
- **General Court-Martial:** Jurisdiction over all offenses; authorized to impose any punishment including death (for applicable offenses), confinement, dishonorable discharge, and forfeiture of all pay and allowances
- **Special Court-Martial:** Intermediate jurisdiction; authorized to impose confinement up to one year, bad-conduct discharge, and forfeiture of two-thirds pay for up to one year
- **Summary Court-Martial:** Minor offenses; maximum confinement of 30 days

### Military Justice Improvement and Increasing Prevention Act (2021)

The most significant reform to the UCMJ since its enactment, the MJIPA was included in the National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 2022 (Public Law 117-81). Effective December 28, 2023, the Act:

- Created the Office of Special Trial Counsel (OSTC) within each military service
- Transferred prosecutorial authority over serious offenses -- including murder, sexual assault, kidnapping, and domestic violence -- from military commanders to independent Special Trial Counsel (licensed attorneys with significant litigation experience)
- Retained commander authority over purely military offenses (AWOL, insubordination, dereliction of duty)

The reform was driven by decades of congressional concern over military sexual assault prosecution rates and the findings of the Independent Review Commission on Sexual Assault in the Military (IRC), chaired by Lynn Rosenthal, which delivered its recommendations in July 2021.

### Appellate Structure

Military appellate review proceeds through four levels:
1. Convening authority review (post-trial)
2. Service Courts of Criminal Appeals (Army, Navy-Marine Corps, Air Force, Coast Guard)
3. United States Court of Appeals for the Armed Forces (CAAF) -- an Article I court composed of five civilian judges appointed by the President
4. United States Supreme Court (by certiorari)

### Landmark Cases

**Parker v. Levy, 417 U.S. 733 (1974):** The Supreme Court upheld the constitutionality of broad UCMJ provisions, holding that the military is "a specialized society separate from civilian society" and that the UCMJ's general articles (Articles 133 and 134) were not unconstitutionally vague.

**Solorio v. United States, 483 U.S. 435 (1987):** The Supreme Court held that military jurisdiction depends solely on the accused's status as a member of the armed forces, overruling the "service connection" test of O'Callahan v. Parker (1969).

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany represents the most civilian-integrated approach to military justice among the jurisdictions compared. Article 96(2) of the Grundgesetz (Basic Law) permits the establishment of federal military criminal courts only during a formally declared state of defense (Verteidigungsfall) under Article 115a GG. In peacetime, all criminal offenses committed by service members -- whether military-specific or ordinary crimes -- are prosecuted in civilian criminal courts under the general Strafgesetzbuch (StGB) and the Wehrstrafgesetz (WStG -- Military Criminal Law).

**Wehrstrafgesetz (WStG):** The Military Criminal Law defines service-specific offenses including desertion (Fahnenflucht, Section 16), unauthorized absence (Eigenmächtige Abwesenheit, Section 15), insubordination (Ungehorsam, Section 19-21), mutiny (Meuterei, Section 27), and cowardice before the enemy (Feigheit vor dem Feind, Section 7). These offenses are prosecuted by civilian state prosecutors (Staatsanwaltschaften) in ordinary criminal courts (Amtsgerichte, Landgerichte).

### Disciplinary System

The Wehrdisziplinarordnung (WDO -- Military Discipline Code) governs non-criminal disciplinary proceedings. Minor disciplinary infractions are handled through simple disciplinary measures imposed by military superiors (einfache Disziplinarmaßnahmen), including reprimand, restriction to quarters, and fines up to one month's basic pay. More serious disciplinary matters requiring judicial disciplinary measures (gerichtliche Disziplinarmaßnahmen) -- including reduction in rank, forfeiture of pension rights, and removal from service -- are adjudicated by the Truppendienstgerichte (Military Service Courts), which are specialized chambers within the administrative court system staffed by professional judges.

### Parliamentary Commissioner for the Armed Forces

The Wehrbeauftragte des Deutschen Bundestages (Parliamentary Commissioner for the Armed Forces), established under Article 45b GG, serves as an ombudsperson for service members, receiving and investigating complaints regarding violations of fundamental rights and principles of Innere Führung (leadership and civic education). The Commissioner submits an annual report to the Bundestag detailing systemic issues in military governance.

### Penalties under Wehrstrafgesetz

| Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|
| Desertion (Section 16 WStG) | Up to 5 years imprisonment |
| Unauthorized absence (Section 15 WStG) | Up to 3 years imprisonment |
| Insubordination -- refusal to obey order (Section 20 WStG) | Up to 3 years imprisonment |
| Mutiny (Section 27 WStG) | 6 months to 5 years imprisonment |
| Ill-treatment of subordinates (Section 30 WStG) | 3 months to 5 years imprisonment |
| Cowardice before the enemy (Section 7 WStG) | Up to 5 years; aggravated: up to 10 years |

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights Jurisprudence

The ECtHR has developed extensive case law on military justice, consistently applying Article 6 (right to a fair trial) requirements to military tribunals.

**Findlay v. United Kingdom (Application No. 22107/93, Judgment of February 25, 1997):** The Court found that the UK court-martial system violated Article 6(1) because the convening officer -- who also served as the confirming officer and was the accused's superior -- played a central role in the prosecution, undermining the tribunal's independence and impartiality. The judgment catalyzed comprehensive reform of the British military justice system through the Armed Forces Act 2006.

**Morris v. United Kingdom (Application No. 38784/97, Judgment of February 26, 2002):** Despite post-Findlay reforms, the Court found that the presence of relatively junior military officers on the court-martial panel, who were subordinate in rank to the prosecuting authority, raised legitimate doubts about independence. The judgment required further structural reforms to ensure panel members were not subject to military hierarchical pressure.

**Ergin v. Turkey (No. 6) (Application No. 47533/99, Judgment of May 4, 2006):** The Court held that the trial of a civilian (a journalist criticizing military service) before a military court violated Article 6(1), as the participation of a military judge in the trial of a civilian could not be justified.

**Jaloud v. Netherlands (Application No. 47708/08, Judgment of November 20, 2014):** The Grand Chamber found the Netherlands had violated Article 2 (right to life) by failing to conduct an effective investigation into the death of an Iraqi civilian shot at a military checkpoint, establishing standards for military accountability in overseas operations.

### Council of Europe Guidelines

The Committee of Ministers of the Council of Europe adopted Recommendation CM/Rec(2010)4 on human rights of members of the armed forces, affirming that service members are entitled to the full protection of Convention rights, subject only to such restrictions as are strictly necessary for the effective discharge of military duties. Key principles include:

- Military justice must comply with Article 6 requirements of independence, impartiality, and fair trial
- Military courts should not have jurisdiction over civilians
- Disciplinary proceedings must include adequate procedural safeguards
- Service members retain rights to freedom of expression, assembly, and association, subject to proportionate restrictions

### EU Dimension

While the EU has limited direct competence over military justice, the Charter of Fundamental Rights of the European Union (Article 47 -- right to an effective remedy and fair trial, and Article 48 -- presumption of innocence) applies to the extent that member states act within the scope of EU law. The Common Security and Defence Policy (CSDP) framework for EU military missions addresses disciplinary jurisdiction through Status of Forces Agreements (SOFAs) negotiated for each operation.

### Military Justice in Overseas Operations

The application of military justice to operations abroad raises distinct challenges:

**South Korea:** South Korean forces deployed under UN or multinational operations are subject to Status of Forces Agreements (SOFAs) that typically grant sending-state jurisdiction over criminal offenses committed by service members. The U.S.-ROK SOFA governs jurisdiction for crimes committed by Korean or U.S. service members on each other's territory.

**United States:** The UCMJ applies worldwide to U.S. service members. The Military Extraterritorial Jurisdiction Act (MEJA) extends federal criminal jurisdiction to civilians accompanying the forces abroad. However, the Insular Cases and subsequent jurisprudence have created complex questions about constitutional rights in overseas military proceedings.

**Germany:** Since the Bundeswehr began overseas deployments (Kosovo, Afghanistan), the application of German criminal law extraterritorially to service members has been managed through civilian prosecutors. The Kunduz air strike case (2009), in which a German colonel ordered an airstrike near Kunduz, Afghanistan that killed civilians, was investigated by civilian prosecutors in Dresden but resulted in no charges. The Bundeswehr's Inspector General conducted a separate administrative review.

## Comparative Analysis

### Separation of Military and Civilian Justice

The four jurisdictions represent a spectrum of separation between military and civilian justice systems. Germany stands at one extreme with complete integration in peacetime -- no separate military courts exist, and all criminal matters are handled by civilian prosecutors and judges. The United States occupies the opposite end, maintaining a fully separate court-martial system with its own prosecutors, judges, and appellate courts. South Korea has moved from a position close to the U.S. model toward greater civilian court involvement through its 2021 reforms. The ECHR does not prohibit military courts but requires them to meet the same standards of independence and impartiality as civilian tribunals.

### Command Authority over Prosecution

The most significant convergence across jurisdictions has been the progressive removal of commanders' authority to control prosecutorial decisions. Both South Korea (2021) and the United States (2021/2023) enacted reforms transferring serious offense prosecution authority to independent legal professionals -- a change long advocated by victim advocates and military justice scholars but resisted by military leadership. Germany never vested prosecutorial authority in commanders, maintaining civilian prosecutorial control over all criminal matters involving service members.

### Sexual Violence Reform

The treatment of sexual violence within the military has been a primary driver of reform in both South Korea and the United States. Both jurisdictions enacted legislation specifically in response to high-profile cases of military sexual assault and institutional failure. South Korea transferred sexual assault jurisdiction entirely to civilian courts, while the United States created the Special Trial Counsel mechanism within the military system. Germany's approach of civilian court jurisdiction over all military criminal matters has avoided the structural conflicts of interest inherent in command-controlled prosecution.

### Conscription and Rights

South Korea's universal male conscription system raises distinct military justice issues, as conscripts -- many aged 18-20 -- are subject to military discipline and criminal jurisdiction without having voluntarily assumed that status. The Constitutional Court of Korea has been attentive to conscripts' fundamental rights, while the ECHR has established that conscripts retain full Convention protections.
