## Summary

Statelessness -- the condition of an individual who is not considered a national by any state under the operation of its law, as defined by Article 1 of the 1954 Convention Relating to the Status of Stateless Persons -- affects an estimated 4.4 million persons globally according to UNHCR, though the actual number is believed to be significantly higher due to identification challenges, and represents one of the most severe forms of legal marginalization, as stateless persons are frequently denied access to education, healthcare, employment, property ownership, freedom of movement, and the right to vote. South Korea, which adheres to jus sanguinis under the Nationality Act (국적법), addresses statelessness through limited provisions including Article 2(2) granting Korean nationality to persons born in Korea whose parents are unknown or stateless, and Article 8 permitting the Minister of Justice to grant nationality to stateless persons through special naturalization, while the country is not party to either the 1954 Convention or the 1961 Convention on the Reduction of Statelessness. The United States, as the preeminent practitioner of unconditional jus soli through the Fourteenth Amendment, effectively prevents statelessness of persons born on its territory, though it is party only to the 1954 Convention (not the 1961 Convention) and its INA provisions on nationality loss (8 U.S.C. § 1481) include safeguards requiring voluntariness with intent to relinquish nationality. Germany addresses statelessness through the Grundgesetz Article 16(1) prohibition on deprivation of citizenship that would cause statelessness, StAG provisions granting nationality to foundlings and stateless children born in Germany, and a dedicated statelessness determination procedure established by the Staatenlosigkeitsübereinkommen-Ausführungsgesetz, while the EU framework including the ECHR's jurisprudence on Articles 3, 5, 8, and 14, the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights, and the CJEU's nationality/EU citizenship case law provides the most developed supranational legal framework for addressing statelessness.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's approach to statelessness is largely shaped by its jus sanguinis nationality system and its non-accession to the two core international statelessness conventions.

**Nationality Act Provisions:**

| Article | Provision |
|---------|-----------|
| Article 2(2) | Nationality for persons born in Korea whose father and mother are both unknown or stateless |
| Article 2(3) | Foundlings found in Korea deemed born in Korea |
| Article 8 | Special naturalization: Minister of Justice may grant nationality to stateless persons or others in special circumstances |
| Article 15 | Revocation of nationality acquisition: may not render person stateless (implied by proportionality) |

**Limitations:** Korea lacks a formal statelessness determination procedure (SDP). Persons claiming to be stateless must navigate the general immigration system, which does not have a dedicated pathway for statelessness claims. The absence of an SDP means that many stateless persons in Korea exist in a legal grey zone, unable to establish their statelessness through a recognized procedure.

### Statelessness in Korea

The main populations at risk of statelessness in Korea include:

**Undocumented children:** Children born in Korea to undocumented foreign parents who cannot register the birth with either Korean or foreign authorities. While Article 2(2) of the Nationality Act theoretically covers children whose parents are "unknown," documented but undocumented parents do not meet this criterion, leaving their children in a precarious legal position.

**North Korean defectors:** While the Constitution of the Republic of Korea (Article 3) considers the territory of the Korean Peninsula and its adjacent islands as the territory of the Republic, and thus North Korean nationals are technically Korean nationals, practical challenges arise in the recognition and documentation process. The North Korean Refugees Protection and Settlement Support Act (북한이탈주민의 보호 및 정착지원에 관한 법률) provides a pathway to South Korean documentation.

**Overseas ethnic Koreans (해외동포):** Some ethnic Korean populations, particularly in China and Central Asia (Koryo-saram), face documentation challenges that may approach statelessness, though most hold foreign nationalities.

### Civil Society Advocacy

Korean civil society organizations, including the Refugee Rights Center (피난처) and the National Human Rights Commission (국가인권위원회), have advocated for Korea's accession to the 1954 and 1961 Conventions and the establishment of a formal statelessness determination procedure. The NHRCK issued recommendations in 2019 and 2022 urging the government to address the rights of stateless persons and children at risk of statelessness.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States' unconditional jus soli system makes it highly effective at preventing statelessness within its territory, though gaps exist in its international obligations and domestic procedures.

**Constitutional Foundation:** The Fourteenth Amendment's guarantee that "all persons born . . . in the United States, and subject to the jurisdiction thereof, are citizens of the United States" is the most effective constitutional mechanism against statelessness in any country. With narrow exceptions (children of diplomats, children of enemy forces in hostile occupation), every person born in the United States acquires U.S. citizenship regardless of parental nationality or immigration status.

**1954 Convention Obligations:** The United States acceded to the 1954 Convention in 1981, accepting the definition of a stateless person and the obligation to provide identity papers and travel documents. However, implementation has been uneven:
- No federal statelessness determination procedure exists
- USCIS has developed informal guidance for officers encountering potential statelessness claims
- Stateless persons may apply for immigration benefits (asylum, withholding, adjustment of status) but no specific relief exists for statelessness itself

**Nationality Loss (8 U.S.C. § 1481):** U.S. law provides for loss of nationality through:

| Act | Provision |
|-----|-----------|
| Naturalization in a foreign state | § 1481(a)(1) |
| Formal oath of allegiance to foreign state | § 1481(a)(2) |
| Service in foreign armed forces | § 1481(a)(3) |
| Employment with foreign government | § 1481(a)(4) |
| Formal renunciation | § 1481(a)(5)-(6) |
| Treason, sedition, conspiracy | § 1481(a)(7) |

Critically, all of these provisions require that the act be performed "voluntarily . . . with the intention of relinquishing United States nationality" (§ 1481(b)). The Supreme Court's decision in Afroyim v. Rusk, 387 U.S. 253 (1967), established that Congress may not involuntarily divest a person of U.S. citizenship. This high threshold significantly reduces the risk of statelessness resulting from loss of U.S. nationality.

### Stateless Populations in the United States

An estimated 218,000 persons in the United States are stateless or at risk of statelessness, including:
- Persons from countries that have ceased to exist (former Soviet Union, Yugoslavia)
- Persons denationalized by their countries of origin (Palestinians, Rohingya, some Eritreans)
- Persons unable to prove nationality due to lack of documentation
- Children born to stateless parents outside the United States

**Legislative Proposals:** The Stateless Protection Act, introduced in multiple Congresses (most recently 2023), would create a dedicated immigration status for stateless persons, including employment authorization and a pathway to permanent residence. The bill has not passed.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany has the most comprehensive legal framework for addressing statelessness among the four jurisdictions, reflecting its ratification of both the 1954 and 1961 Conventions and its constitutional protections.

**Constitutional Protection:** Article 16(1) GG provides: "No German may be deprived of his or her citizenship. Loss of citizenship may arise only pursuant to a law, and against the will of the person affected only if he or she does not thereby become stateless." This is the strongest constitutional safeguard against statelessness in any major democracy.

**StAG Provisions:**

| Section | Provision |
|---------|-----------|
| § 4(2) | Foundlings found in Germany are deemed children of German nationals until contrary proof |
| § 4(4) | German nationality for children born in Germany who would otherwise be stateless (since 2009) |
| § 10(1)(4) | Naturalization applicants need not renounce prior nationality if renunciation would be impossible or unreasonable |
| § 8 | Discretionary naturalization for stateless persons with 6 years of habitual residence (reduced from standard 8/5 years) |

**Statelessness Determination Procedure:** Germany enacted the Staatenlosigkeitsübereinkommen-Ausführungsgesetz (Act on the Implementation of the Convention Relating to the Status of Stateless Persons) with a formal SDP effective June 2024. The procedure:
- Application to the Federal Office for Migration and Refugees (BAMF)
- Burden of proof: applicant must demonstrate that no state considers them a national
- Standard of proof: balance of probabilities; co-operation duties on applicant
- Positive determination: entitles the person to a travel document and residence considerations
- Appeal: administrative court review under the VwGO

### Registered Stateless Persons

As of 2025, approximately 29,000 persons are registered as stateless in Germany, with an additional ~95,000 registered as "nationality unknown" (ungeklärte Staatsangehörigkeit). The largest groups include:
- Former Soviet Union citizens who did not acquire successor state nationality
- Palestinians (many registered as stateless rather than as nationality of a PA-controlled territory)
- Persons from the former Yugoslavia
- Rohingya and other de facto stateless populations

### Rights of Stateless Persons in Germany

Under the 1954 Convention and German implementing legislation, recognized stateless persons in Germany are entitled to:
- Convention travel document (Reiseausweis für Staatenlose)
- Right to employment (comparable to recognized refugees)
- Access to social welfare, education, and healthcare
- Pathway to naturalization with reduced residence requirement

## ECHR / EU

### International Conventions

**1954 Convention Relating to the Status of Stateless Persons:**
- Definition of stateless person (Article 1)
- Minimum rights: personal status (Art. 12), movable and immovable property (Art. 13), gainful employment (Arts. 17-19), welfare (Arts. 20-24), identity papers (Art. 27), travel documents (Art. 28)
- 96 states parties as of 2025

**1961 Convention on the Reduction of Statelessness:**
- Birth in territory: states must grant nationality to persons born in their territory who would otherwise be stateless (Art. 1)
- Foundlings: presumption of birth in territory (Art. 2)
- Safeguards against loss of nationality: loss/deprivation shall not result in statelessness (Arts. 5-9)
- Exception: nationality obtained through fraud may be withdrawn even if it results in statelessness (Art. 8(2)(b))
- 78 states parties as of 2025

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Kuric and Others v. Slovenia (Grand Chamber, 2012):** The Court found violations of Articles 8, 13, and 14 regarding the "erased" persons -- former Yugoslav citizens removed from Slovenia's population register after independence, rendering them de facto stateless. The judgment emphasized that prolonged failure to regularize the legal status of persons who had been lawfully resident constitutes a continuing violation.

**Hoti v. Croatia (Application No. 63311/14, April 26, 2018):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where Croatian authorities failed for over 20 years to resolve the applicant's statelessness, despite repeated applications for citizenship and the applicant having been born and resided in Croatia his entire life.

**Sudita Keita v. Hungary (Application No. 42321/15, May 12, 2020):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where the statelessness determination procedure was unreasonably protracted and the applicant was left in legal limbo, establishing that states must provide effective procedures for determining statelessness within a reasonable time.

**Article 5 (Liberty) and Statelessness:** In Kim v. Russia (Application No. 44260/13, July 17, 2014), the Court found a violation of Article 5(1) where a stateless person was detained pending deportation despite there being no realistic prospect of removal. The Court held that detention of stateless persons is only justified under Article 5(1)(f) if deportation proceedings are conducted with "due diligence" and a "realistic prospect of expulsion" exists.

### EU Framework

**EU Charter of Fundamental Rights:**
- Article 18: Right to asylum
- Article 19(1): Prohibition of collective expulsion
- Article 19(2): Non-refoulement

**CJEU and Statelessness:**

**Case C-221/17, Tjebbes (2019):** Automatic loss of Dutch nationality for dual nationals residing outside the EU, which could result in statelessness if the other nationality was also lost, must allow individual proportionality review.

**Case C-118/20, JY v. Wiener Landesregierung (2022):** Denial of Austrian naturalization after the applicant renounced her prior nationality (as required by Austrian law), resulting in de facto statelessness, must be proportionate under EU law principles.

### Council of Europe Initiatives

The Council of Europe has been active in addressing statelessness:
- **Recommendation CM/Rec(2009)13** on the nationality of children
- **European Convention on Nationality (ECN, 1997):** Article 4(b) requires that "statelessness shall be avoided"
- **Council of Europe Convention on the Avoidance of Statelessness in Relation to State Succession (2006):** Addresses statelessness arising from state succession (ratified by 8 states)

## Comparative Analysis

### Prevention Mechanisms

The most effective prevention mechanism is unconditional jus soli (United States), which eliminates statelessness at birth for all persons born in the territory. Germany's combined approach (constitutional prohibition on deprivation + targeted jus soli for otherwise stateless children) is the next most comprehensive. South Korea's limited provisions leave significant gaps.

### Determination Procedures

- **Germany:** Formal SDP since 2024 (most developed among the four)
- **United States:** No formal federal SDP despite 1954 Convention obligations
- **South Korea:** No formal SDP
- The absence of formal determination procedures means that many stateless persons cannot establish their status and access Convention rights

### Rights and Status

Once identified, stateless persons receive varying levels of protection:
- **Germany:** Convention travel documents; social rights; facilitated naturalization
- **United States:** Limited; no specific immigration status for stateless persons
- **South Korea:** Minimal; treated under general immigration law
- **EU/ECHR:** Supranational floor of rights through Convention and Charter obligations

### Root Causes

Statelessness arises from different causes in each jurisdiction:
- **Jus sanguinis gaps (South Korea):** Children born to undocumented parents may fall through nationality transmission gaps
- **State succession (Germany/EU):** Dissolution of states (Soviet Union, Yugoslavia) created large stateless populations
- **Discrimination (Global):** Gender-discriminatory nationality laws, ethnic discrimination (Rohingya, Roma)
- **Administrative failure:** Lack of birth registration; loss of documentation

### Detention of Stateless Persons

Stateless persons are particularly vulnerable to prolonged immigration detention because deportation is often impossible:

| Jurisdiction | Detention Framework | Stateless-Specific Protections |
|-------------|--------------------|-----------------------------|
| South Korea | Immigration Act; no maximum period | Limited; no specific statelessness provisions |
| United States | INA; Zadvydas 6-month presumptive limit | Zadvydas applies to stateless persons; but enforcement varies |
| Germany | AufenthG § 62; maximum 18 months | Duldung status if deportation impossible; alternatives to detention |
| EU | Return Directive; maximum 18 months | Article 15: detention only for deportation; must be "realistic prospect of removal" |

The ECHR has held in Kim v. Russia (2014) that detention of stateless persons without a realistic prospect of removal violates Article 5(1)(f). This principle has been applied by national courts across Council of Europe member states.

### Birth Registration and Documentation

The connection between birth registration and statelessness prevention is critical:
- **South Korea:** Birth registration is tied to family registry (가족관계등록부); children of undocumented parents may not be registered, creating de facto statelessness
- **United States:** Birth certificates issued by state vital statistics offices regardless of parental status; strongest documentation system
- **Germany:** Standesamt (civil registry office) registers all births in Germany regardless of parental nationality
- **UNICEF estimates:** Approximately 166 million children under 5 worldwide are not registered, placing them at risk of statelessness

The Korean National Assembly has considered amendments to the family registration system to enable birth registration of all children born in Korea regardless of parental documentation status, but no legislation has been enacted as of 2026.

### UNHCR #IBelong Campaign

UNHCR's Global Action Plan to End Statelessness 2014-2024 set ten goals:
1. Resolve existing major situations of statelessness
2. Ensure no child is born stateless
3. Remove gender discrimination from nationality laws
4. Prevent denial, loss, or deprivation of nationality on discriminatory grounds
5. Prevent statelessness in cases of state succession
6. Grant protection status and facilitate naturalization of stateless persons
7. Ensure birth registration for prevention of statelessness
8. Issue nationality documentation
9. Accede to UN Statelessness Conventions
10. Improve quantitative and qualitative data on stateless populations

Progress has been uneven: while several countries reformed discriminatory nationality laws, South Korea and the United States have not acceded to the 1961 Convention, and global statelessness numbers have not significantly decreased.

### Statelessness Determination Procedures

The existence and quality of formal statelessness determination procedures (SDPs) varies significantly:

- **Germany:** Formal SDP established under AufenthG § 25(5); recognized stateless persons receive a residence permit and travel document (Reiseausweis für Staatenlose) in accordance with the 1954 Convention
- **United States:** No formal SDP exists; stateless persons must navigate the immigration system through asylum, withholding of removal, or other existing channels; USCIS Policy Manual Volume 12 provides guidance on statelessness in the naturalization context
- **South Korea:** No formal SDP; stateless persons may apply for recognition under the Immigration Act, but lack of dedicated procedures means outcomes depend on administrative discretion
- **EU:** The Commission has recommended that all member states establish formal SDPs, but only 12 member states had done so as of 2025

### Rohingya, Palestinian, and Other Major Stateless Populations

Global statelessness affects an estimated 4.4 million persons identified by UNHCR, though the actual number is believed to be significantly higher:

- **Rohingya (Myanmar):** Over 1 million Rohingya stripped of citizenship under the 1982 Burma Citizenship Law; largest stateless population globally; ICJ ordered provisional measures in The Gambia v. Myanmar (2020) regarding prevention of genocide
- **Palestinians:** Complex nationality situation arising from the 1948 and 1967 conflicts; UNRWA (not UNHCR) provides international protection; approximately 5.9 million registered Palestine refugees
- **Bidoon (Gulf States):** Approximately 100,000 stateless residents in Kuwait and smaller populations in other Gulf states
- **Dominicans of Haitian descent:** Sentence TC/0168/13 of the Dominican Constitutional Tribunal retroactively denaturalized an estimated 200,000 persons of Haitian descent; Inter-American Court condemned the ruling in Expelled Dominicans and Haitians v. Dominican Republic (2014)
