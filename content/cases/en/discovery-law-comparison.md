## Summary

Civil discovery -- the pre-trial process by which parties obtain evidence from each other and third parties -- represents one of the deepest structural divides between common law and civil law legal systems, reflecting fundamentally different conceptions of the role of parties, courts, and truth-finding in litigation. South Korea follows the civil law model under the Civil Procedure Act (민사소송법, Act No. 547, comprehensively revised 2002), in which the court controls evidence-gathering through document submission orders (문서제출명령, Article 344), requests for information (사실조회, Article 294), and party examination (당사자심문, Articles 367-376), with no equivalent to U.S.-style broad pre-trial discovery, though the 2002 procedural reforms introduced limited document disclosure obligations and the Supreme Court in Decision 2006Da22852 (2007) expanded the scope of compulsory document production. The United States has the broadest discovery regime in the world, governed by Federal Rules of Civil Procedure 26-37, encompassing depositions (Rules 30-32), interrogatories (Rule 33), document production (Rule 34), physical and mental examinations (Rule 35), and requests for admission (Rule 36), with the scope of discovery extending to any non-privileged matter relevant to a party's claim or defense and proportional to the needs of the case under the 2015 amendments to Rule 26(b)(1), and with an elaborate framework for electronic discovery (e-discovery) developed through judicial opinions such as Zubulake v. UBS Warburg LLC (S.D.N.Y. 2003-2004) and codified in part by the 2006 and 2015 amendments to the Federal Rules. Germany employs a fundamentally different approach under the ZPO, in which there is no general pre-trial discovery phase, the principle of party presentation (Beibringungsgrundsatz) requires each party to identify and present its own evidence, document production from opponents is limited to specific documents under Sections 142 and 421-427 ZPO, and the court takes an active role in evidence-gathering through its own authority to order document production under Section 142 ZPO and to appoint experts, with the BGH consistently rejecting attempts to import U.S.-style discovery through the Hague Evidence Convention. At the European level, the Hague Convention on the Taking of Evidence Abroad in Civil or Commercial Matters (1970) provides the primary framework for cross-border evidence gathering, with the EU's Evidence Regulation (Regulation (EU) 2020/1783, replacing Regulation (EC) No. 1206/2001) establishing a simplified procedure for taking evidence between EU member states, while the European Court of Human Rights under Article 6 ECHR has recognized that equality of arms requires parties to have adequate access to evidence relevant to their claims, as developed in Dombo Beheer BV v. The Netherlands (1993).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's evidence system follows the civil law tradition, with court-controlled evidence procedures and no pre-trial discovery phase. The 2002 comprehensive revision of the Civil Procedure Act introduced several reforms to improve evidence access.

### Document Submission Orders (문서제출명령)

**Article 344 of the Civil Procedure Act** provides the primary mechanism for obtaining documents from opposing parties:

- **Article 344(1):** A party holding a document is obligated to submit it when: (a) the party referred to the document in the proceedings; (b) the requesting party is entitled to delivery or inspection of the document; or (c) the document was prepared for the benefit of the requesting party or concerns a legal relationship between the parties.
- **Article 344(2) (General Duty of Submission):** Added in the 2002 reform, this provision establishes a general duty to submit documents except for: documents containing information relating to the holder's or a close relative's criminal liability, professional secrets (attorney, physician, etc.), exclusively internal documents prepared for the holder's own use, and documents covered by official secrecy.

**Supreme Court Decision 2006Da22852 (2007):** The Court broadly interpreted the "exclusively internal" document exception, holding that documents prepared in the ordinary course of business that are relevant to the dispute and not prepared solely for the holder's internal decision-making process may be subject to compulsory production.

### Requests for Information (사실조회)

**Article 294** authorizes the court, upon a party's application, to request information from public agencies, educational institutions, and other organizations regarding specific factual matters. This procedure is not directed at the opposing party but at third-party institutions.

### Witness Examination

Witnesses are examined at trial under Articles 303-322. Unlike the U.S. deposition system, there is no pre-trial examination of witnesses. The court examines the witness first, followed by the parties. The 2002 reform introduced provisions for witness preparation statements (증인신문사항) to improve the efficiency of trial examination.

### Evidence Preservation (증거보전)

**Articles 375-384** provide for pre-trial evidence preservation when there is a risk that evidence may be destroyed or become unavailable. The court may order inspection of objects, examination of witnesses, or production of documents before the main proceedings begin.

### Expert Evidence

Court-appointed experts (감정인) under Articles 333-342 provide the primary mechanism for technical evidence. Party-appointed experts are not part of the formal evidence system, though parties may submit expert opinions as documentary evidence. The court-appointed expert submits a written opinion and may be examined at the hearing.

### Limitations and Reform Discussions

Korean scholars and practitioners have increasingly debated whether to expand discovery mechanisms. The Korean Bar Association's 2019 proposal recommended introducing limited document disclosure obligations modeled on English disclosure rather than U.S.-style broad discovery, emphasizing proportionality and judicial oversight.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States operates the most expansive civil discovery system in the world, established by the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure (adopted 1938, substantially amended 1970, 1993, 2000, 2006, 2015) and supplemented by state procedural rules.

### Scope of Discovery

**Rule 26(b)(1) (as amended 2015):** Parties may obtain discovery regarding any nonprivileged matter that is relevant to any party's claim or defense and proportional to the needs of the case, considering the importance of the issues at stake, the amount in controversy, the parties' relative access to relevant information, the parties' resources, the importance of the discovery in resolving the issues, and whether the burden or expense of the proposed discovery outweighs its likely benefit.

The 2015 amendment eliminated the prior distinction between matter relevant to claims/defenses (discoverable as of right) and matter relevant to the subject matter (discoverable only by court order), narrowing the scope while adding the proportionality requirement.

### Discovery Devices

| Device | Rule | Description |
|--------|------|-------------|
| **Initial Disclosures** | Rule 26(a)(1) | Mandatory disclosure of witnesses, documents, damages computation, and insurance without discovery request |
| **Depositions (Oral)** | Rule 30 | Oral examination of witnesses under oath; limited to 10 per side, 7 hours per deposition |
| **Depositions (Written)** | Rule 31 | Written questions to witness, answered under oath |
| **Interrogatories** | Rule 33 | Written questions to parties; limited to 25 per party |
| **Document Production** | Rule 34 | Production of documents, electronically stored information (ESI), and tangible things |
| **Physical/Mental Examination** | Rule 35 | Court-ordered examination when physical/mental condition is in controversy |
| **Requests for Admission** | Rule 36 | Requests to admit facts or the genuineness of documents |
| **Subpoenas** | Rule 45 | Compulsory process to non-parties for documents, testimony, or inspection |

### Electronic Discovery (E-Discovery)

The 2006 amendments to the Federal Rules formally addressed electronically stored information (ESI):

- **Rule 26(a)(1)(A)(ii):** Initial disclosures must include ESI.
- **Rule 26(b)(2)(B):** ESI from sources that are not reasonably accessible because of undue burden or cost need not be produced unless the requesting party shows good cause.
- **Rule 34(b)(2)(E):** ESI must be produced in the form in which it is ordinarily maintained or in a reasonably usable form.
- **Rule 37(e) (2015 amendment):** Governs sanctions for failure to preserve ESI, requiring a finding that the party acted with intent to deprive another party of the information before the most severe sanctions (adverse inference instruction, dismissal, default judgment) may be imposed.

**Zubulake v. UBS Warburg LLC (S.D.N.Y. 2003-2004):** Judge Scheindlin's series of five opinions established foundational principles for e-discovery, including the duty to preserve potentially relevant evidence once litigation is reasonably anticipated, the allocation of costs for restoring inaccessible electronic data, and the scope of the litigation hold obligation.

### Discovery Abuse and Proportionality

Discovery abuse has been a persistent concern. The 2015 amendments emphasized proportionality as a response to the escalating costs of modern discovery. Key reforms included:
- Relocating the proportionality factors to Rule 26(b)(1) to make them part of the definition of discoverable matter.
- Narrowing the scope from "subject matter" to "claims or defenses."
- Encouraging early judicial management through Rule 16 case management conferences.

### Privileges and Protections

**Attorney-Client Privilege:** Protects confidential communications between attorney and client made for the purpose of seeking or providing legal advice. The crime-fraud exception permits discovery of otherwise privileged communications made in furtherance of a crime or fraud.

**Work Product Doctrine (Hickman v. Taylor, 329 U.S. 495 (1947); codified in Rule 26(b)(3)):** Protects documents and tangible things prepared in anticipation of litigation. Fact work product is discoverable upon a showing of substantial need and inability to obtain the equivalent without undue hardship. Opinion work product (mental impressions, conclusions, legal theories) receives near-absolute protection.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany follows the principle of party presentation (Beibringungsgrundsatz), under which each party bears the responsibility for identifying, locating, and presenting the evidence supporting its claims and defenses. There is no general pre-trial discovery phase, and the concept of broad, party-driven evidence gathering is fundamentally inconsistent with the German procedural tradition.

### Document Production Under the ZPO

**Section 142 ZPO (Court-Ordered Document Production):** The court may, on its own motion or upon a party's application, order a party or third party to produce documents or objects in its possession that have been referred to in the proceedings. The 2002 reform of Section 142 strengthened the court's power, but production remains limited to specifically identified documents rather than categories of documents.

**Sections 421-427 ZPO (Party's Obligation to Produce Documents):**

- **Section 421:** A party may request the court to order the opposing party to produce a specific document if the requesting party can identify the document and demonstrate a substantive law basis for production.
- **Section 422:** The opposing party is obligated to produce documents to which the requesting party has a substantive law right of inspection or delivery.
- **Section 423:** Documents that the opposing party has referred to in the proceedings must be produced.
- **Section 427 (Adverse Inference):** If a party fails to produce a document without adequate justification, the court may treat the requesting party's assertions about the document's contents as established.

### No Fishing Expeditions

German courts consistently reject requests that resemble "fishing expeditions" -- broadly framed requests designed to uncover unknown evidence. The requesting party must specifically identify the documents sought and articulate why they are relevant. The BGH in Decision III ZR 163/08 (2009) held that Section 142 ZPO does not authorize document production requests aimed at enabling a party to substantiate claims it cannot yet prove.

### Third-Party Evidence

**Section 142(1) ZPO** also applies to third parties, but third parties may refuse production on grounds that apply to witnesses, including professional privilege and self-incrimination. Third parties may also claim undue burden.

### Witness Evidence

Witnesses are examined at trial under Sections 373-401 ZPO. The court conducts the primary examination, with parties having the right to pose supplementary questions. There is no pre-trial deposition procedure. Written witness statements (schriftliche Zeugenaussage) may be obtained under Section 377(3) ZPO in simplified proceedings.

### Expert Evidence

Germany primarily relies on court-appointed experts (Sachverständige) under Sections 402-414 ZPO. The court formulates the questions for the expert, and parties may propose additional questions. Party-appointed experts may submit opinions, but these carry less weight than court-appointed expert evidence.

### Cross-Border Discovery Conflicts

Germany has been a prominent opponent of U.S.-style extraterritorial discovery. The German Blocking Statute (Section 14 of the Introductory Act to the ZPO, EG-ZPO) restricts compliance with foreign discovery requests that exceed the scope of German domestic evidence procedures. In Aerojet-General Corp. v. Transport Indemnity Insurance (1983), the OLG Düsseldorf refused to execute U.S. discovery letters rogatory that sought broad document production.

### Hague Evidence Convention and German Practice

Germany ratified the Hague Evidence Convention in 1979 with reservations under Articles 23 and 33. The Article 23 reservation declares that Germany will not execute Letters of Request that require "pre-trial discovery of documents" -- effectively blocking U.S.-style discovery requests through the Convention. German courts interpret this reservation broadly, refusing requests that seek categories of documents rather than specifically identified items.

## ECHR & EU

### Hague Evidence Convention (1970)

The Hague Convention on the Taking of Evidence Abroad in Civil or Commercial Matters provides the primary multilateral framework for cross-border evidence gathering:

- **Chapter I (Letters of Request):** A judicial authority in one Contracting State may request the competent authority of another Contracting State to obtain evidence or perform other judicial acts. The requested State executes the request using its own procedures (Article 9).
- **Chapter II (Taking of Evidence by Diplomatic Officers, Consular Agents and Commissioners):** Permits evidence taking in the territory of a Contracting State by diplomatic/consular officers or commissioners, subject to the State's consent.
- **Article 23 Reservation:** Permits Contracting States to declare that they will not execute Letters of Request for pre-trial discovery of documents. Most civil law countries, including Germany, France, and South Korea, have made this reservation.

**Societe Nationale Industrielle Aerospatiale v. U.S. District Court, 482 U.S. 522 (1987):** The U.S. Supreme Court held that the Hague Evidence Convention does not provide the exclusive means for obtaining evidence from foreign litigants in U.S. courts. Courts may apply the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure directly, though the Convention should be considered as an option. This decision created a significant tension between U.S. and European approaches.

### EU Evidence Regulation (Regulation (EU) 2020/1783)

Replacing Regulation (EC) No. 1206/2001, the current Evidence Regulation establishes a streamlined procedure for taking evidence between EU member states:

- **Direct Transmission:** Requests are transmitted directly between courts without the intervention of central authorities.
- **Videoconference Evidence:** Article 20 promotes the use of videoconferencing and other distance communication technology for taking evidence.
- **Execution Period:** The requested court must execute the request within 90 days (Article 12).
- **Direct Taking of Evidence:** Under Article 19, a requesting court may take evidence directly in another member state with the approval of the central body of the requested state.

### ECHR Equality of Arms and Evidence Access

**Dombo Beheer BV v. The Netherlands, Application No. 14448/88, Judgment of October 27, 1993:** The Court held that Article 6(1) requires that each party be given a reasonable opportunity to present their case under conditions that do not place them at a substantial disadvantage vis-a-vis their opponent. The Court found a violation where one party was prevented from calling a witness who could testify to the same transaction as a witness called by the opposing party.

**Niderost-Huber v. Switzerland, Application No. 18990/91, Judgment of February 18, 1997:** The Court held that the right to adversarial proceedings under Article 6 requires that parties have access to all evidence and observations submitted to the court that may influence its decision.

**Regner v. Czech Republic [GC], Application No. 35289/11, Judgment of September 19, 2017:** The Grand Chamber addressed limitations on evidence disclosure for national security reasons, holding that while non-disclosure may be justified, sufficient procedural safeguards must exist to protect the party's interests.

### EU Directive on the Protection of Trade Secrets

**Directive (EU) 2016/943** on trade secret protection includes provisions for evidence preservation and disclosure in trade secret litigation:

- **Article 7:** Courts may order preservation of evidence, including seizure or delivery up of suspected infringing goods and materials.
- **Article 9:** Courts must limit access to confidential information disclosed during proceedings, including restricting the circle of persons who may access evidence.

## Comparative Analysis

### Fundamental Structural Differences

The discovery divide between common law and civil law systems represents one of the most significant structural differences in global civil procedure. The U.S. system, built on the premise that broad access to evidence promotes truth-finding and settlement, stands in stark contrast to the German and Korean approaches, which vest evidence control primarily in the court and limit pre-trial evidence gathering to prevent abuse and protect privacy.

### Document Production Scope

The scope of compulsory document production varies dramatically. U.S. Rule 34 permits requests for broad categories of documents, including ESI, with the producing party bearing the initial burden of objecting. Germany limits production to specifically identified documents with a substantive law basis. South Korea occupies a middle position, with its 2002 reforms expanding the general duty of document submission but retaining court control over production orders.

### E-Discovery and Technology

The United States leads in developing e-discovery frameworks, driven by the volume of electronic communications in modern litigation. Neither Germany nor South Korea has developed comparable e-discovery rules, though both face increasing pressure as electronically stored information becomes central to commercial disputes.

### Cross-Border Evidence Conflicts

The intersection of U.S. discovery with foreign legal systems generates persistent conflict. The Aerospatiale decision's rejection of Hague Convention exclusivity, combined with Article 23 reservations by civil law countries, creates an unresolved tension. The EU Evidence Regulation represents a successful intra-European solution but does not bridge the transatlantic divide.

### Privacy and Data Protection

The GDPR (Regulation (EU) 2016/679) has introduced an additional layer of complexity to cross-border discovery. U.S. discovery requests seeking personal data held in the EU must navigate GDPR requirements, including lawful basis for processing, data minimization, and transfer restrictions. German and other EU blocking statutes reinforce these protections.
