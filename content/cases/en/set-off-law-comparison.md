## Summary

Set-off -- the extinguishment of mutual debts by treating reciprocal obligations as cancelling each other, in whole or in part -- is a fundamental doctrine of obligations law that operates as both a substantive defense and a mechanism for simplifying the settlement of cross-claims, though its requirements, operation, and scope differ significantly across legal traditions. South Korea codifies set-off (상계) in the Civil Code (민법) under Articles 492-499, providing that when two persons owe each other debts of the same kind that are both due and performable, either debtor may extinguish both obligations to the extent of the lesser amount by a unilateral declaration (상계의 의사표시); Korean law follows the German model in treating set-off as operating through a unilateral declaration with retroactive effect to the point when the set-off first became possible, and the Supreme Court of Korea (대법원) has developed extensive jurisprudence on the scope of set-off in insolvency, banking, and cross-border contexts. The United States recognizes multiple forms of set-off, including legal set-off (recoupment and counterclaim), equitable set-off, banker's right of set-off, and insolvency set-off under the Bankruptcy Code (11 U.S.C. § 553), with the Supreme Court in Citizens Bank of Maryland v. Strumpf (516 U.S. 16, 1995) and Studley v. Boylston National Bank (229 U.S. 523, 1913) addressing the relationship between set-off rights and the automatic stay in bankruptcy; the distinction between legal and equitable set-off, and between set-off and recoupment, has significant procedural and bankruptcy consequences. Germany provides the most systematically developed set-off regime through BGB §§ 387-396 (Aufrechnung), which requires that the claims be mutual, of the same kind, due and performable (fällig and erfüllbar), and free from defenses excluding set-off; German law treats set-off as a unilateral Gestaltungsrecht (formative right) that extinguishes obligations retroactively (ex tunc) to the point of Aufrechnungslage (the moment when set-off first became available), and the insolvency set-off provisions under InsO §§ 94-96 represent a critical creditor protection in German bankruptcy law. The ECHR has examined set-off under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (property protection) and Article 6(1) (fair trial), while EU law addresses cross-border set-off through the Insolvency Regulation (2015/848), the Rome I Regulation (593/2008, Article 17), and the Financial Collateral Directive (2002/47/EC), which protect close-out netting and set-off arrangements in financial markets.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Korean set-off law (상계) is codified in Articles 492-499 of the Civil Code, reflecting the systematic approach inherited from the German BGB. Set-off is classified as a unilateral juridical act (단독행위) -- it requires only the declaration of one party and does not require the other party's consent.

### Requirements for Set-Off (Article 492)

**Mutuality (상호성):** Both parties must simultaneously be debtor and creditor of each other. The claims must exist between the same two parties in the same capacity. A guarantor may set off the principal debtor's claim against the creditor, but this is treated as a special statutory authorization rather than an exception to mutuality.

**Same Kind (동종의 목적):** The obligations must have the same object. In practice, this requirement is almost always satisfied by monetary claims. Non-monetary obligations of the same kind (e.g., mutual obligations to deliver identical fungible goods) may theoretically be set off, but this is rare in practice.

**Due and Performable (이행기의 도래):** The claim of the party declaring set-off (the "active claim" or 자동채권) must be due. The claim against which set-off is declared (the "passive claim" or 수동채권) must be at least performable (the debtor must be permitted to perform it), though it need not yet be due.

### Declaration and Effect

**Article 493 (Declaration):** Set-off is effected by a unilateral declaration to the other party. The declaration is a formative right (형성권) -- once made, it cannot be revoked without the other party's consent.

**Article 493(2) (Retroactive Effect):** The set-off takes retroactive effect to the point when the two claims first became capable of being set off (상계적상). This means that interest on both claims ceases to accrue from the date of the Aufrechnungslage, and any late-payment damages that would otherwise have accrued after that date are extinguished.

**Prohibition of Conditions and Time Limits (Article 493(1)):** The declaration of set-off may not be made subject to conditions or time limits, as this would create uncertainty about the parties' obligations.

### Prohibited Set-Off

**Article 496 (Tort Claims):** A debtor whose obligation arose from an intentional tort may not set off against the creditor's claim. The purpose is to ensure that tort victims receive actual payment rather than merely having an obligation extinguished on paper, and to discourage intentional wrongdoing.

**Article 497 (Claims Immune from Attachment):** Set-off may not be declared against claims that are immune from attachment (압류금지채권), such as minimum wage claims and subsistence allowances. This protects the social welfare function of attachment-exempt claims.

**Article 498 (Claims Acquired After Attachment):** When a claim has been attached (압류) by a third party, the debtor may not set off against the attached claim with a claim acquired after the attachment. This protects the attaching creditor's priority.

### Supreme Court Jurisprudence

**Decision 2010Da18850 (2012):** The Supreme Court addressed the relationship between contractual set-off clauses (상계예약) and statutory set-off, holding that contractual set-off agreements (commonly found in banking and commercial contracts) are valid and enforceable, and may provide for automatic set-off without the need for a separate declaration.

**Decision 2005Da47205 (2008):** The Court held that set-off in insolvency proceedings is governed by the Debtor Rehabilitation and Bankruptcy Act, which modifies the Civil Code rules to balance creditor protection with equitable distribution principles.

**Decision 2016Da241515 (2019):** The Supreme Court clarified that a time-barred claim (소멸시효가 완성된 채권) may still be used for set-off if the set-off situation (상계적상) arose before the limitation period expired (Article 495), preserving the debtor's reasonable expectation that the mutual debts would cancel each other.

### Insolvency Set-Off

The Debtor Rehabilitation and Bankruptcy Act (채무자 회생 및 파산에 관한 법률) governs set-off in insolvency:

**Article 144 (Rehabilitation Proceedings):** Creditors who owed debts to the debtor at the time rehabilitation proceedings commenced may set off their claims against their debts within the rehabilitation proceedings, subject to restrictions designed to prevent abuse.

**Article 416 (Bankruptcy Proceedings):** Bankruptcy creditors who owed debts to the bankrupt estate may set off their claims against these debts at any time during the bankruptcy proceedings.

**Article 422 (Restrictions):** Set-off is prohibited in bankruptcy where the creditor acquired the claim or debt after the commencement of bankruptcy, or where the creditor assumed the debt knowing of the debtor's cessation of payments or application for bankruptcy.

## United States

### Legal Framework

American set-off law is more fragmented than its civil law counterparts, drawing from multiple sources including common law, equity, the Uniform Commercial Code, the Bankruptcy Code, and federal banking regulations. The terminology itself varies: "set-off" (or "setoff") generally refers to the mutual cancellation of debts between parties, while "recoupment" refers to the reduction of a plaintiff's claim by a defendant's claim arising from the same transaction.

### Legal Set-Off

At common law, set-off (originally "set-off" under the English Statutes of Set-Off of 1729 and 1735) requires:

**Mutual debts:** The debts must be between the same parties in the same capacity.

**Liquidated claims:** Both claims must be for definite, ascertained amounts.

**Matured claims:** Both debts must be due and payable.

**Same capacity:** The parties must owe each other in the same right (e.g., a debt owed individually cannot be set off against a debt owed in a fiduciary capacity).

### Equitable Set-Off

Equity permits set-off in broader circumstances than the common law, including where the claims are unliquidated, arise from different transactions, or involve equitable rather than legal claims. Courts exercise discretion in permitting equitable set-off, considering factors such as the connection between the claims, the relative equities, and whether denying set-off would result in injustice.

### Recoupment

Recoupment is narrower than set-off: it permits a defendant to reduce the plaintiff's claim only by amounts arising from the same transaction. Unlike set-off, recoupment is not subject to the mutuality requirement (in the strict sense) and is not affected by the automatic stay in bankruptcy, as established in In re Holford, 896 F.2d 176 (5th Cir. 1990), because it does not involve a separate claim but merely limits the plaintiff's recovery on its own claim.

### Banker's Right of Set-Off

Banks hold a common law and statutory right to set off deposits against debts owed by the depositor. This right is recognized under the common law of virtually all states and is codified in various banking regulations. In Citizens Bank of Maryland v. Strumpf, 516 U.S. 16 (1995), the Supreme Court held that a bank's temporary "administrative freeze" on a debtor's account, pending a formal set-off, did not violate the automatic stay in bankruptcy, as the freeze was not itself a set-off but merely a preservation of the bank's right to seek relief from the stay to effectuate set-off.

### Insolvency Set-Off (11 U.S.C. § 553)

Section 553 of the Bankruptcy Code preserves pre-existing set-off rights in bankruptcy, subject to specific limitations:

**§ 553(a):** The Code does not affect any right of a creditor to offset a mutual debt owing by the creditor to the debtor against a claim of the creditor against the debtor, subject to the automatic stay (11 U.S.C. § 362(a)(7)) and the specific exceptions in § 553(a)(1)-(3).

**§ 553(a)(1):** Set-off is disallowed where the creditor's claim against the debtor was transferred to the creditor from a third party after the commencement of the case (or within 90 days before commencement with knowledge of insolvency).

**§ 553(a)(2)-(3):** Set-off is disallowed where the debt owed by the creditor to the debtor was incurred after the commencement of the case, or was incurred within 90 days before commencement for the purpose of obtaining a set-off right.

**§ 553(b):** The trustee may recover the amount by which a creditor's set-off improved its position during the 90-day period before bankruptcy (the "improvement in position" test), which is analogous to preference avoidance.

### Studley v. Boylston National Bank

**Studley v. Boylston National Bank, 229 U.S. 523 (1913):** The Supreme Court established that the right of set-off is a substantive right that survives the commencement of bankruptcy proceedings, reasoning that a creditor who also owes a debt to the debtor "has a right which is more than a mere right of action, more than a mere right of procedure -- it is a right to have the full performance of the contract under which he is liable."

### Cross-Claim Set-Off in Federal Litigation

Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 13 governs counterclaims, which serve as the procedural vehicle for asserting set-off in federal litigation. Compulsory counterclaims (Rule 13(a)) -- claims arising out of the same transaction or occurrence -- must be asserted or are waived. Permissive counterclaims (Rule 13(b)) may be asserted at the defendant's option.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

The German Aufrechnung (set-off) is codified in BGB §§ 387-396 and represents one of the most systematically developed set-off regimes in comparative law. Set-off is classified as a Gestaltungsrecht (formative right) -- a unilateral right to alter the legal relationship between the parties.

### Requirements (BGB § 387)

**Gegenseitigkeit (Mutuality):** Two persons must owe each other performance. The claims must exist between the same parties, and each must be both creditor and debtor of the other.

**Gleichartigkeit (Same Kind):** The performances owed must be of the same kind. This is satisfied when both claims are for money, which is the overwhelmingly common scenario.

**Fälligkeit und Einredefreiheit der Gegenforderung (Due and Defense-Free Active Claim):** The claim with which the declaring party sets off (Gegenforderung or active claim) must be due (fällig) and free from defenses (einredefrei). A claim against which the Verjährung defense has been raised cannot be used as an active claim for set-off, except under the provision of BGB § 215.

**Erfüllbarkeit der Hauptforderung (Performability of Passive Claim):** The claim against which set-off is declared (Hauptforderung or passive claim) must be performable (erfüllbar) -- meaning the debtor is permitted to perform, even if the claim is not yet due.

### Declaration and Effect

**BGB § 388 (Aufrechnungserklärung):** Set-off is effected by declaration to the other party. The declaration is irrevocable once received and may not be made subject to conditions or time limits.

**BGB § 389 (Retroactive Effect):** The mutual claims are deemed to have been extinguished at the point in time when they first existed in opposition to each other (Aufrechnungslage). This retroactive effect means that interest on both claims ceases from the date of the Aufrechnungslage, not from the date of the declaration.

**BGB § 215 (Set-Off with Time-Barred Claims):** A claim that has become time-barred (verjährt) may still be used for set-off if the Aufrechnungslage arose before the limitation period expired. This provision protects the declaring party's reasonable expectation of mutual cancellation.

### Prohibited Set-Off

**BGB § 393 (Intentional Tort Claims):** A claim arising from an intentional unlawful act (vorsätzliche unerlaubte Handlung) may not be extinguished by set-off against it. The debtor of such a claim must make actual payment. The purpose is to prevent wrongdoers from benefiting from their own misconduct and to ensure tort victims receive actual compensation.

**BGB § 394 (Unattachable Claims):** Set-off against a claim that is exempt from attachment (unpfändbare Forderung) is prohibited. This protects subsistence-level income, social welfare benefits, and similar claims essential to the creditor's livelihood.

**BGB § 392 (Attached Claims):** After a claim has been attached by a third party (gepfändet), the debtor may not extinguish the claim by declaring set-off with a claim acquired after the attachment or after learning of the attachment. This preserves the attaching creditor's priority.

### Contractual Set-Off (Aufrechnungsvereinbarung)

Parties may modify the statutory set-off rules by agreement. Contractual set-off clauses (Aufrechnungsklauseln) may broaden or restrict the right of set-off, provide for automatic set-off without declaration, or extend set-off to non-mutual claims (e.g., within corporate groups). However, in consumer contracts, clauses that exclude or restrict the consumer's right of set-off are subject to review under BGB § 309 No. 3, which prohibits clauses that exclude the consumer's right to set off with an uncontested or legally established (rechtskräftig festgestellt) counterclaim.

### Insolvency Set-Off (InsO §§ 94-96)

The Insolvenzordnung (InsO) provides robust protection for set-off rights in insolvency, reflecting the German view that set-off functions as a form of security interest:

**InsO § 94 (Preservation of Set-Off):** If a creditor of the insolvency estate is also a debtor of the estate, and the set-off conditions (Aufrechnungslage) existed at the time insolvency proceedings were opened, the opening of proceedings does not affect the right of set-off. The creditor may declare set-off in accordance with the BGB provisions.

**InsO § 95 (Set-Off with Immature Claims):** Even if the claims were not yet due or were subject to a suspensive condition at the time of the opening of proceedings, set-off is permitted once the conditions are met, unless the estate's claim matures before the creditor's counterclaim.

**InsO § 96 (Restrictions):** Set-off is prohibited where: (1) the creditor acquired the claim against the estate after the opening of proceedings; (2) the creditor acquired the claim by transfer from another creditor after the opening; (3) the creditor incurred the debt to the estate through a transaction voidable under the InsO avoidance provisions; or (4) the creditor acquired the right to set off through a voidable transaction within the critical period before insolvency.

### Aufrechnung in Litigation

In German civil procedure, set-off may be raised as a defense (Einrede) or asserted by way of Hilfsaufrechnung (conditional set-off). In Hilfsaufrechnung, the defendant declares set-off conditionally -- only in the event the court finds the plaintiff's claim to be valid. The BGH has recognized this procedural device, which allows the defendant to preserve the counterclaim for separate enforcement if the main claim is dismissed.

A critical procedural consequence is the res judicata effect: under ZPO § 322(2), the court's decision on a set-off defense has res judicata effect with respect to the counterclaim, up to the amount for which set-off was asserted. This means the counterclaim is consumed to that extent, regardless of whether set-off was successful.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Considerations

The European Court of Human Rights has addressed set-off primarily under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (protection of property) and Article 6(1) (fair trial).

**National & Provincial Building Society v. United Kingdom (Application No. 21319/93, 1997):** The Court examined whether a government set-off of tax liabilities against amounts owed to building societies constituted a deprivation of property, holding that the set-off served a legitimate public interest and was proportionate.

**Pressos Compania Naviera S.A. v. Belgium (Application No. 17849/91, 1995):** The Court confirmed that a claim constitutes a "possession" under Protocol 1 once it is sufficiently established, and that extinguishment through retroactive legislation (which has functional similarities to compulsory set-off) must be justified and proportionate.

The ECHR has recognized that set-off is a legitimate tool for the efficient settlement of mutual obligations, but has required that its application in specific cases not deprive individuals of fundamental rights or constitute a disproportionate interference with property.

### Rome I Regulation (593/2008, Article 17)

**Article 17 (Set-Off):** "Where the right to set-off is not agreed by the parties, set-off shall be governed by the law applicable to the claim against which the right to set-off is asserted." This conflict-of-laws rule determines which national set-off law applies in cross-border disputes within the EU. The provision favors the law of the passive claim (the claim being reduced or extinguished), reflecting the principle that the creditor of the passive claim should have the security of knowing which set-off rules apply to their claim.

### Insolvency Regulation (2015/848)

**Article 9(1):** "The opening of insolvency proceedings shall not affect the right of creditors to demand the set-off of their claims against the claims of the debtor, where such a set-off is permitted by the law applicable to the insolvent debtor's claim." This provision preserves set-off rights in cross-border insolvencies within the EU.

**Article 9(2):** "Paragraph 1 shall not preclude actions for voidness, voidability or unenforceability as referred to in Article 7(2)(m)." This carve-out allows the insolvency administrator to challenge set-off transactions that constitute preferences or otherwise harm the insolvency estate, even where the set-off would be permissible under the applicable law.

### Financial Collateral Directive (2002/47/EC)

The Financial Collateral Directive provides special protection for close-out netting and set-off arrangements in financial markets:

**Article 7 (Close-Out Netting):** Member states must ensure that close-out netting provisions in financial collateral arrangements can take effect in accordance with their terms, notwithstanding the commencement of insolvency proceedings. This protects the enforceability of master netting agreements (such as ISDA Master Agreements) that rely on set-off mechanisms for systemic risk management.

**Recital 14:** The Directive recognizes that close-out netting and set-off serve an essential function in financial markets by reducing counterparty credit risk and systemic risk, justifying their protection even in insolvency.

### Settlement Finality Directive (98/26/EC)

**Article 3:** Transfer orders entered into payment and securities settlement systems before the opening of insolvency proceedings are enforceable and binding on third parties, even if insolvency proceedings are opened against a participant. The netting and set-off mechanisms within these systems are protected from the effects of insolvency.

### Cross-Border Set-Off Challenges

Cross-border set-off presents particular challenges when the two claims are governed by different laws. The Rome I Regulation's rule (Article 17) provides a starting point but does not resolve all conflicts. Problems arise when: one legal system permits set-off with time-barred claims and the other does not; one system requires judicial declaration and the other permits unilateral set-off; the claims are denominated in different currencies; or one of the parties is subject to insolvency proceedings in a jurisdiction with restrictive set-off rules.

## Comparative Analysis

### Unilateral Declaration vs. Judicial Determination

The most fundamental structural difference is between systems that treat set-off as a unilateral right (Germany, South Korea) and those that require judicial involvement (aspects of common law set-off in the U.S.). In Germany and Korea, set-off operates through a simple declaration by one party, with retroactive effect and without court involvement (though the declaration's validity may be disputed in subsequent litigation). In the U.S., legal set-off traditionally required assertion in court proceedings, though banker's set-off and contractual set-off operate extra-judicially.

### Retroactive Effect

German and Korean law both provide for retroactive extinction of obligations to the date of the Aufrechnungslage/상계적상. This means that interest, late-payment penalties, and default consequences are eliminated retroactively. American law generally does not apply set-off retroactively -- set-off takes effect when exercised or when ordered by a court, and interest continues to accrue on both claims until that point. This difference can have significant financial consequences in long-running disputes.

### Insolvency Protection

All systems recognize the importance of protecting set-off rights in insolvency, but the scope of protection varies. Germany provides the broadest protection under InsO § 94, treating the creditor's set-off position as functionally equivalent to a security interest. The U.S. Bankruptcy Code preserves pre-petition set-off rights under § 553 but subjects them to the automatic stay and preference analysis. Korean insolvency law follows a middle path, preserving set-off rights subject to restrictions on claims acquired near the onset of insolvency. The EU Insolvency Regulation preserves set-off rights under the law applicable to the debtor's claim, while the Financial Collateral Directive provides enhanced protection for financial market set-off.

### Prohibited Set-Off Categories

All systems prohibit set-off in certain categories to protect vulnerable creditors or to serve policy objectives. The prohibition on set-off against intentional tort claims (Germany: BGB § 393; Korea: Article 496) ensures that wrongdoers cannot benefit from their misconduct. The prohibition on set-off against unattachable claims (Germany: BGB § 394; Korea: Article 497) protects subsistence-level income. The U.S. achieves similar protection through exemption laws that prevent set-off against exempt property.

### Financial Market Netting

The protection of financial market netting and set-off represents an area of growing international convergence. The ISDA Master Agreement, used in over-the-counter derivatives markets worldwide, relies on close-out netting -- a form of contractual set-off that calculates a single net obligation upon a party's default or insolvency. The EU Financial Collateral Directive, the U.S. Bankruptcy Code's safe harbors for financial contracts (11 U.S.C. §§ 555-561), and the Korean Capital Markets Act all provide special protection for these arrangements, reflecting the systemic importance of netting in reducing counterparty risk.
