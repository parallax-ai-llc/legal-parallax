## Summary

Maritime liens -- privileged claims attaching to a vessel by operation of law, without registration or the consent of the owner, securing debts arising from the vessel's operation or from services rendered to or damage caused by the vessel -- represent one of the most ancient and distinctive features of admiralty law, tracing their origins to medieval maritime codes and embodying the legal fiction that the vessel itself is the offending or obligated party. The law of maritime liens and ship arrest varies dramatically across jurisdictions, reflecting deep differences between the common law tradition of in rem jurisdiction (proceedings against the vessel itself) and the civil law tradition of privileged maritime claims (privilèges maritimes), with international harmonization attempted through the International Convention on Maritime Liens and Mortgages 1993 (Geneva, May 6, 1993) and the International Convention Relating to the Arrest of Sea-Going Ships 1952 (Brussels). South Korea governs maritime liens through the Korean Commercial Code (상법), Part 5 (Maritime Commerce, 해상편), Articles 777-787, supplemented by the Ship Registration Act (선박등기법) and the Ship Mortgage Act (선박담보법), within a framework influenced by Japanese maritime law and the civil law privilege tradition. The United States maintains the most expansive maritime lien regime among major jurisdictions, grounded in the Federal Maritime Lien Act (46 U.S.C. §§ 31301-31343, formerly the Ship Mortgage Act 1920), the Supplemental Rules for Admiralty or Maritime Claims (Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, Supplemental Rules B, C, D, and E), and centuries of judicial development by federal admiralty courts under Article III, Section 2 of the U.S. Constitution. Germany regulates maritime liens (Schiffsgläubigerrechte) under the German Commercial Code (Handelsgesetzbuch, HGB), §§ 596-604, and ship mortgages (Schiffshypotheken) under the Gesetz über Rechte an eingetragenen Schiffen und Schiffsbauwerken (SchiffsRG, Act on Rights in Registered Ships and Ships Under Construction, 1940), while the ECHR and EU framework addresses maritime liens through the lens of the 1993 Geneva Convention, the EU Ship Recycling Regulation (EU) No 1257/2013, and the protection of property rights under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 to the ECHR.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Korean maritime lien law is codified in Part 5 (해상편, Maritime Commerce) of the Commercial Code, drawing primarily from the Japanese Commercial Code tradition and the civil law privilege model.

**Commercial Code, Articles 777-787:** These provisions establish the categories of maritime liens (선박우선특권, ship priority rights), their ranking, and their enforcement.

### Categories of Maritime Liens

**Article 777:** Korean law recognizes the following categories of maritime liens, in order of priority:

| Priority | Category | Description |
|----------|----------|-------------|
| 1st | Court costs and vessel-related expenses | Costs of judicial sale; costs of preserving the vessel in custody; port dues and pilotage fees |
| 2nd | Crew wages and related claims | Wages of the master (선장) and crew (해원); repatriation costs; social insurance contributions |
| 3rd | Salvage and general average | Salvage remuneration (해난구조료); general average contributions (공동해손분담금) |
| 4th | Tort claims | Claims for loss of life or personal injury arising from the operation of the vessel |
| 5th | Collision and property damage | Claims for damage to port works, docks, waterways, and aids to navigation |

**Exclusions:** Unlike U.S. law, Korean maritime law does not recognize a maritime lien for "necessaries" (supplies, repairs, and services furnished to the vessel). Suppliers of necessaries have only a contractual claim against the shipowner, without a lien on the vessel.

### Characteristics of Korean Maritime Liens

**Article 778 (Following the Vessel):** Maritime liens attach to the vessel and follow it regardless of changes in ownership (droit de suite). A bona fide purchaser of the vessel acquires it subject to existing maritime liens.

**Article 781 (No Registration Required):** Maritime liens arise by operation of law without registration. They take priority over registered ship mortgages (선박저당권) and other registered encumbrances.

**Article 786 (Prescription):** Maritime liens are extinguished one year from the date the claim arose. The prescription period is not subject to interruption or suspension.

**Article 786 (Inverse Chronological Priority):** Among liens of the same category, those arising from a later voyage have priority over those from an earlier voyage. Within the same voyage, liens rank equally and share pro rata if the vessel's value is insufficient to satisfy all claims.

### Ship Arrest in Korea

**Civil Execution Act (민사집행법):** Ship arrest in Korea is governed by the Civil Execution Act, which provides for both conservatory arrest (보전처분) and arrest in execution (강제집행):

- **Conservatory Arrest (Article 276):** A creditor may apply for arrest of a vessel as a provisional measure to secure a maritime claim. The applicant must demonstrate a prima facie claim and risk that the vessel will depart Korean waters.
- **Arrest in Execution:** Following a judgment or arbitral award, a creditor may arrest and sell the vessel through judicial sale.

**Supreme Court Decision 2009다30513 (2010):** The Court held that a conservatory arrest of a vessel may be granted on the basis of a maritime lien, even where the personal claim is disputed, provided the claimant establishes a prima facie case for the existence of the lien.

### Ship Mortgages

**Ship Mortgage Act (선박담보법) and Ship Registration Act (선박등기법):** Korean law permits the registration of mortgages (저당권) on vessels of 20 gross tons or more. Ship mortgages must be registered in the Ship Register (선박등기부) maintained by the relevant district court.

**Priority:** Ship mortgages rank after all maritime liens (Article 787). Among competing mortgages, priority is determined by order of registration (first in time, first in right).

### 1999 Arrest Convention

South Korea acceded to the International Convention on Arrest of Ships 1999 (Geneva), which entered into force for Korea on September 14, 2011. The Convention provides:

- A claimant may arrest a ship in respect of any "maritime claim" as defined in Article 1(1) (list of 22 categories of claims)
- A ship may be arrested only by court order (Article 2)
- The claimant is liable for damages caused by wrongful arrest (Article 6)

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States maintains the most expansive maritime lien regime in the world, grounded in the constitutional grant of admiralty jurisdiction (U.S. Constitution, Art. III, § 2) and federal statutory and judge-made law.

**Federal Maritime Lien Act (46 U.S.C. §§ 31301-31343):** Originally enacted as part of the Ship Mortgage Act of 1920 and substantially revised in 1988 and 2006, this Act codifies the principal categories of maritime liens and establishes the preferred ship mortgage.

**In Rem Jurisdiction:** The most distinctive feature of U.S. admiralty law is the in rem action -- a proceeding against the vessel itself, based on the legal fiction that the vessel is the "offending thing" (res). The vessel may be arrested and sold to satisfy the claim, regardless of whether the owner is before the court.

### Categories of Maritime Liens

U.S. law recognizes a broader range of maritime liens than any other jurisdiction:

| Category | Legal Basis | Notes |
|----------|------------|-------|
| Seamen's wages | 46 U.S.C. § 31301(5)(D); The John G. Stevens, 170 U.S. 113 (1898) | "Sacred lien"; highest priority in practice |
| Salvage | 46 U.S.C. § 31301(5)(A); The Sabine, 101 U.S. 384 (1879) | Includes both contract and pure salvage |
| General average | 46 U.S.C. § 31301(5)(C) | Contributions to general average |
| Tort (maritime) | 46 U.S.C. § 31301(5)(B); The Bold Buccleugh, 7 Moo. P.C. 267 (1851) | Personal injury, death, property damage arising from vessel operations |
| Necessaries | 46 U.S.C. § 31342 | Supplies, repairs, towage, pilotage, dock charges furnished to vessel on order of owner or authorized person |
| Preferred mortgage | 46 U.S.C. § 31301(6) | Statutory lien created by mortgage filing |
| Bottomry and respondentia | Historical; largely obsolete | Emergency borrowing secured by vessel (bottomry) or cargo (respondentia) |

### The Lien for Necessaries

**46 U.S.C. § 31342:** The statutory lien for "necessaries" is one of the most important and distinctive features of U.S. maritime law. A person providing necessaries to a vessel on the order of the owner or a person authorized by the owner has a maritime lien on the vessel.

**"Necessaries" Defined (§ 31301(4)):** Includes "repairs, supplies, towage, and the use of a dry dock or marine railway."

**Presumption of Authority (§ 31341):** The master, charterer, managing operator, or agent of the owner is presumed to have authority to bind the vessel for necessaries. This presumption is broadly applied and extremely difficult for the owner to overcome.

**Equilease Trust v. M/V Sampson, 793 F.2d 598 (5th Cir. 1986):** The court held that fuel, provisions, and equipment furnished to a vessel constitute necessaries giving rise to a maritime lien, regardless of whether the supplier knew the identity of the vessel's owner.

### Priority of Maritime Liens

U.S. admiralty courts apply the following general priority scheme in the distribution of proceeds from a judicial sale:

| Priority | Claim Type |
|----------|-----------|
| 1 | Custodia legis expenses (costs of arrest, custody, and sale) |
| 2 | Seamen's wages |
| 3 | Salvage |
| 4 | Tort liens (personal injury, death, property damage) |
| 5 | Liens for necessaries |
| 6 | Preferred ship mortgages |
| 7 | Non-preferred mortgages and state law liens |
| 8 | Unsecured claims |

**"Last in Time, First in Right" Rule:** Within the same category of liens, later-arising liens generally have priority over earlier-arising liens. This rule reflects the admiralty principle that the most recent services are likely to have preserved the vessel and benefited all prior lienholders.

**The John G. Stevens, 170 U.S. 113 (1898):** The Supreme Court affirmed that seamen's wages are the most favored liens in admiralty, entitled to priority over virtually all other claims.

### Ship Arrest and Judicial Sale

**Supplemental Rules for Admiralty or Maritime Claims:**

| Rule | Subject |
|------|---------|
| Rule B | Attachment: process of attachment of defendant's property |
| Rule C | Actions in rem: procedures for arrest of vessel |
| Rule D | Possessory, petitory, and partition actions |
| Rule E | Actions in rem and quasi in rem: general provisions |

**Rule C Procedure:**
1. Plaintiff files a verified complaint identifying the vessel and the maritime lien
2. Court issues a warrant of arrest
3. U.S. Marshal arrests the vessel (physically taking custody)
4. Notice is given to all persons with claims against the vessel
5. Competing claimants file claims
6. Court conducts a hearing on the merits and orders distribution

**Judicial Sale:** A judicial sale in admiralty transfers title to the vessel free and clear of all liens -- both recognized and unrecognized. The buyer at a Marshal's sale acquires clean title. This is one of the most powerful features of U.S. admiralty law.

### Preferred Ship Mortgage

**46 U.S.C. § 31322:** A preferred ship mortgage must be:
- Endorsed on the vessel's certificate of documentation
- Recorded with the U.S. Coast Guard National Vessel Documentation Center
- Include the entire vessel as security

**Priority:** A preferred ship mortgage has priority over all claims against the vessel except:
- Maritime liens arising before the mortgage was filed
- Custodia legis expenses
- Seamen's wages
- Salvage (including contract salvage)
- Tort liens for personal injury or death

### Laches

Maritime lien claims are subject to the equitable doctrine of laches rather than a fixed statute of limitations. Courts consider:
- Unreasonable delay in asserting the claim
- Prejudice to the defendant from the delay

**In practice:** Courts frequently apply an analogous limitations period (typically the state statute of limitations for the most comparable claim, or the one-year period under the 1993 Geneva Convention) as a benchmark for determining laches.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German maritime lien law is codified in the Handelsgesetzbuch (HGB), Book 5 (Seehandel, Maritime Commerce), §§ 596-604, substantially reformed by the Seehandelsrechtsreformgesetz (Maritime Commerce Reform Act) of 2013.

**HGB §§ 596-604:** These provisions establish the Schiffsgläubigerrechte (ship creditor rights), which constitute privileged claims against the vessel.

### Categories of Maritime Liens

**§ 596 HGB:** German law recognizes the following categories of maritime liens:

| Priority | Category | Description |
|----------|----------|-------------|
| 1st | Wages and social security contributions | Claims of the master and crew for wages (Heuerforderungen), social security contributions, and repatriation costs |
| 2nd | Loss of life and personal injury | Claims for loss of life or personal injury arising directly from the operation of the vessel |
| 3rd | Salvage remuneration | Claims for salvage remuneration (Bergungslohn) |
| 4th | Port and canal charges | Claims for port, canal, and other waterway dues, and pilotage |
| 5th | Tort claims (property) | Claims for property damage arising from the operation of the vessel |

**Alignment with 1993 Geneva Convention:** The 2013 reform aligned the German categories with Article 4 of the International Convention on Maritime Liens and Mortgages 1993. Germany ratified the 1993 Convention, which entered into force on September 5, 2004.

### Characteristics of German Maritime Liens

**§ 597 HGB (No Registration):** Maritime liens arise by operation of law without registration and are not subject to publicity requirements. They attach to the vessel and its accessories (Zubehör).

**§ 598 HGB (Priority over Registered Rights):** Maritime liens take priority over all registered rights in the vessel, including Schiffshypotheken (ship mortgages) and other registered encumbrances.

**§ 599 HGB (Droit de Suite):** Maritime liens follow the vessel regardless of changes in ownership. A purchaser acquires the vessel subject to all existing maritime liens.

**§ 600 HGB (Ranking):** Among liens in the same category, those arising from a later event have priority (inverse chronological order). Liens arising from the same event rank equally and share pro rata.

**§ 601 HGB (Prescription):** Maritime liens are extinguished one year from the date the claim arose. The prescription period runs regardless of whether the vessel is in the jurisdiction or accessible for arrest.

### Ship Mortgage (Schiffshypothek)

**Gesetz über Rechte an eingetragenen Schiffen und Schiffsbauwerken (SchiffsRG, 1940):** The SchiffsRG governs the creation, registration, transfer, and enforcement of ship mortgages:

- **§ 8 SchiffsRG:** A Schiffshypothek is created by agreement between the shipowner and the mortgagee and registration in the Schiffsregister (ship register) maintained by the Amtsgericht (local court) of the vessel's home port
- **§ 24 SchiffsRG:** The mortgagee may enforce the mortgage through judicial sale (Zwangsversteigerung) of the vessel under the provisions of the Gesetz über die Zwangsversteigerung und Zwangsverwaltung (ZVG, Act on Compulsory Auction and Receivership)
- **Priority:** Ship mortgages rank after maritime liens (Schiffsgläubigerrechte) but before unsecured claims. Among competing mortgages, priority is determined by order of registration

### Ship Arrest

**ZPO § 917 and § 916 (Arrest):** Ship arrest in Germany is governed by the general arrest provisions of the Zivilprozessordnung (ZPO):

- **Arrest zur Sicherung (Conservatory Arrest):** The creditor must establish a claim (Arrestanspruch) and a ground for arrest (Arrestgrund, § 917 ZPO) -- typically the risk that the vessel will leave German jurisdiction
- **Judgment Enforcement:** Following a judgment, the creditor may levy execution on the vessel through Zwangsvollstreckung procedures

**1952 Arrest Convention:** Germany is a party to the International Convention Relating to the Arrest of Sea-Going Ships 1952 (Brussels), which provides a framework for ship arrest in contracting states.

**Hamburg Maritime Court (Schifffahrtsobergericht):** Maritime disputes in Germany are concentrated in the Hamburg courts, which have developed specialized expertise in ship arrest and maritime lien proceedings.

### 2013 Maritime Commerce Reform

The Seehandelsrechtsreformgesetz of 2013, effective April 25, 2013, represented the most comprehensive reform of German maritime commercial law since the enactment of the HGB in 1897:

- Modernized maritime lien categories to align with the 1993 Geneva Convention
- Reformed carrier liability under the Hague-Visby Rules and Hamburg Rules
- Updated general average provisions
- Revised salvage law to reflect the 1989 Salvage Convention

## ECHR / EU

### International Convention on Maritime Liens and Mortgages 1993

The International Convention on Maritime Liens and Mortgages (Geneva, May 6, 1993) represents the primary international instrument for harmonizing maritime lien law:

**Article 4 (Maritime Liens):** The Convention recognizes five categories of maritime liens, listed in order of priority:

| Priority | Category |
|----------|----------|
| 1 | Claims for wages and other sums due to the master, officers, and other members of the crew |
| 2 | Claims for loss of life or personal injury occurring in direct connection with the operation of the vessel |
| 3 | Claims for reward for salvage of the vessel |
| 4 | Claims for port, canal, and other waterway dues, and pilotage dues |
| 5 | Claims based on tort for physical loss or damage caused by the operation of the vessel other than loss of or damage to cargo and containers |

**Article 5 (Priority):**
- Maritime liens take priority over registered mortgages and hypothèques
- Among liens in different categories, priority follows the order in Article 4
- Among liens in the same category, liens rank pari passu
- Exception: Salvage liens have inverse chronological priority (later salvage operations rank higher)

**Article 6 (Mortgages and Hypothèques):** Registered mortgages and hypothèques complying with the Convention are protected in all contracting states.

**Article 9 (Extinction):** Maritime liens are extinguished after one year from the date the lien arose, unless the vessel has been arrested or sold beforehand.

**Ratification Status:** As of 2025, the 1993 Convention has been ratified by 21 states, including Germany and Russia, but not by the United States, South Korea, or the United Kingdom. Its influence extends beyond ratifying states through its adoption as a model for national legislation.

### International Convention Relating to the Arrest of Sea-Going Ships 1952

The 1952 Arrest Convention (Brussels) governs ship arrest in most European states:

**Article 1:** Defines "maritime claim" as a claim arising out of one or more of 17 enumerated categories, including damage caused by a ship, salvage, agreements relating to the use or hire of a ship, carriage of goods, and maritime liens.

**Article 3:** A claimant may arrest either: (a) the particular ship in respect of which the claim arose; or (b) any other ship owned by the person who was the owner of the particular ship at the time the claim arose (sister ship arrest).

**Article 7:** The courts of the state in which the arrest is effected have jurisdiction over the merits if domestic law confers such jurisdiction, or if the arrest is effected in the state where the claim arose.

### International Convention on Arrest of Ships 1999

The 1999 Arrest Convention (Geneva) modernizes and replaces the 1952 Convention for states that have ratified it:

- Expanded definition of "maritime claim" (22 categories under Article 1)
- Permits arrest of ships flying the flag of a non-state party (Article 8)
- Addresses wrongful arrest liability (Article 6)
- Permits rearrrest and multiple arrests under specific conditions (Article 5)

**Ratification Status:** The 1999 Convention has been ratified by 16 states as of 2025, including South Korea. Most EU member states remain parties to the 1952 Convention.

### EU Framework

**No EU-Wide Maritime Lien Harmonization:** The EU has not harmonized maritime lien law, leaving it to the national laws of member states. The European Commission explored harmonization in the context of the EU Maritime Safety Package but did not include maritime liens in its legislative proposals.

**Regulation (EU) No 1215/2012 (Brussels I Recast):** The Brussels I Regulation excludes arbitration from its scope but applies to court proceedings for maritime claims. However, Article 71 preserves the application of specialized conventions, including the 1952 and 1999 Arrest Conventions, for EU member states that are parties.

**EU Ship Recycling Regulation (EU) No 1257/2013:** This Regulation requires EU-flagged ships to maintain an inventory of hazardous materials and to be recycled only at approved facilities. Maritime lien claims may arise in the context of ship recycling, particularly regarding crew wages, environmental contamination, and hull disposal costs.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Article 1, Protocol No. 1 (Protection of Property):** Ship mortgages, maritime liens, and interests in vessels constitute "possessions" protected by the Convention.

**Credito Fondiario v. Italy (Application No. 26772/95, ECtHR Judgment of February 15, 2001):** While concerning a land mortgage rather than a ship mortgage, the Court's analysis of the priority of secured claims in enforcement proceedings is directly applicable to maritime lien priorities. The Court held that the legislative reordering of priority claims in enforcement proceedings may constitute interference with possessions requiring proportionality analysis.

**Breyer Group plc and Others v. Ukraine (Application No. 39698/09, ECtHR Judgment of October 13, 2016):** The Court found a violation of Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 where a state's failure to enforce a maritime arbitral award against a state-owned shipping company effectively deprived the applicant of the economic value of its claim.

**Article 6(1) (Right to a Fair Trial):** The right to access a court to enforce maritime liens is protected under Article 6(1). Excessive procedural barriers to ship arrest or judicial sale may constitute a denial of access to justice.

## Comparative Analysis

### Scope of Maritime Liens

The jurisdictions examined reveal a fundamental divide in the scope of recognized maritime liens:

| Claim Type | South Korea | United States | Germany | 1993 Convention |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|---------|----------------|
| Crew wages | Yes (highest priority) | Yes (highest priority) | Yes (highest priority) | Yes (Art. 4(1)(a)) |
| Salvage | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes (Art. 4(1)(c)) |
| Tort -- personal injury | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes (Art. 4(1)(b)) |
| Tort -- property damage | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes (Art. 4(1)(e)) |
| Port and canal dues | Yes | Not typically a lien | Yes | Yes (Art. 4(1)(d)) |
| Necessaries (repairs, supplies) | **No** | **Yes** (§ 31342) | **No** | **No** |
| Cargo damage | No | Yes (as tort) | No | No |
| Bottomry | Historical | Historical | No | No |

The U.S. lien for necessaries (supplies, repairs, fuel, provisions) is the most significant divergence, creating a substantially larger universe of maritime lien claims in U.S. waters than in any other jurisdiction. This has profound practical implications for ship finance, as vessels trading in U.S. waters are subject to a wider range of potential liens.

### In Rem vs. Privilege Model

The jurisdictional model for enforcing maritime claims represents a fundamental structural difference:

- **United States (In Rem):** The vessel itself is the defendant. The action proceeds against the res, and the vessel may be arrested and sold to satisfy the claim. Personal jurisdiction over the owner is not required. This model provides the most powerful enforcement mechanism for maritime claimants.
- **South Korea (Hybrid):** Korean law recognizes maritime liens as privileged claims (우선특권) that attach to the vessel, but the enforcement model is primarily through arrest for security followed by personal proceedings against the shipowner.
- **Germany (Privilege Model):** Maritime liens are Schiffsgläubigerrechte -- real rights in the vessel -- enforced through arrest and judicial sale, but within the civil law framework rather than the common law in rem tradition.

### Priority of Claims

Priority rules determine the practical value of maritime liens and mortgages:

| Priority Level | South Korea | United States | Germany | 1993 Convention |
|---------------|-------------|---------------|---------|----------------|
| 1st | Crew wages | Custodia legis costs | Crew wages | Crew wages |
| 2nd | Salvage | Crew wages | Personal injury/death | Personal injury/death |
| 3rd | Personal injury | Salvage | Salvage | Salvage |
| 4th | Port dues | Tort liens | Port/canal dues | Port dues |
| 5th | Property damage | Necessaries | Property damage | Property damage |
| 6th | Ship mortgages | Preferred mortgages | Ship mortgages | Mortgages/hypothèques |

### Ship Finance Implications

The divergence in maritime lien law creates significant challenges for ship finance:

- **Priming Risk:** Maritime liens take priority over ship mortgages in all jurisdictions. The broader scope of U.S. maritime liens creates greater priming risk for mortgagees whose vessels trade in U.S. waters.
- **Due Diligence:** Ship mortgage lenders must investigate potential maritime liens against the vessel before advancing funds. The absence of a registration requirement for maritime liens makes this investigation difficult.
- **Flag State Choice:** The flag state's law governs the creation and priority of ship mortgages, while the law of the arrest jurisdiction governs enforcement priority. This creates complex choice-of-law issues.
- **Vessel Inspection Funds:** Ship finance documentation typically requires the borrower to maintain liens-free certificates and to provide indemnification against maritime lien claims.

### Arrest Practices

Ship arrest procedures differ significantly in practice:

| Feature | South Korea | United States | Germany |
|---------|-------------|---------------|---------|
| Arresting authority | Court (Civil Execution Act) | U.S. Marshal (Supplemental Rule C) | Court (ZPO § 917) |
| Security requirement | Bond or guarantee typically required | Counter-security may be required (Rule E(5)) | Arrest bond required |
| Sister ship arrest | 1999 Convention permits | Yes (admiralty tradition) | 1952 Convention permits |
| Release mechanism | Counter-security / P&I Club letter of undertaking | Counter-security bond | Counter-security / P&I letter |
| Wrongful arrest liability | Yes (1999 Convention Art. 6) | Bad faith or gross negligence standard | Yes (§ 945 ZPO) |
| Time to arrest | Days to weeks | Hours to days | Days |
