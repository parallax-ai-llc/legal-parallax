## Summary

Indigenous peoples' rights across the Global South illuminate fundamental tensions between nation-state sovereignty, territorial integrity, cultural preservation, and the recognition of pre-colonial legal orders. Unlike the settler-colonial frameworks of Australia, Canada, New Zealand, and the United States, where indigenous rights discourse centers on treaties and the doctrine of discovery, the Global South presents distinct configurations: China's Regional Ethnic Autonomy Law (minzu quyu zizhi fa, 1984/2001) governs 55 officially recognized "minority nationalities" (shaoshu minzu) comprising approximately 8.9% of the population across five autonomous regions, 30 autonomous prefectures, and 120 autonomous counties, but the system prioritizes national unity and CPC authority over genuine self-determination; India's constitutional architecture for Scheduled Tribes (Adivasi) under Articles 244, 244A, and the Fifth and Sixth Schedules, supplemented by the Forest Rights Act (2006) and the Panchayats (Extension to Scheduled Areas) Act (PESA, 1996), represents one of the most elaborate indigenous governance frameworks in the Global South, yet implementation is plagued by displacement for development projects and exploitation of tribal lands; South Africa's recognition of traditional leaders and customary law under Chapter 12 of the Constitution (Sections 211-212) and the Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act (2003) coexists with the Ingonyama Trust controversy and ongoing land reform debates, while Nigeria's Land Use Act (1978) effectively abolished customary communal tenure; Brazil's Constitution of 1988 (Articles 231-232) provides the strongest textual protection for indigenous land rights in the Americas, recognizing "original rights" (direitos originarios) to traditionally occupied lands, but the "temporal framework" (marco temporal) thesis endorsed by agribusiness interests and partially addressed by the Supreme Tribunal Federal in the Xokleng/Terra Indigena Ibirama-Laklano case (RE 1.017.365, 2023) threatened to restrict these protections; Colombia's Constitutional Court has developed the world's most progressive indigenous rights jurisprudence through the "prior consultation" (consulta previa) doctrine and recognition of indigenous territorial entities; Mexico's 2001 constitutional reform (Article 2) recognized indigenous peoples' right to self-determination and autonomy within the framework of national unity; Indonesia's recognition of adat (customary law) communities through Constitutional Court Decision No. 35/PUU-X/2012 reversing the classification of adat forests as state forests was a landmark for Southeast Asian indigenous rights; and the Philippines' Indigenous Peoples Rights Act (IPRA, Republic Act No. 8371, 1997) established the National Commission on Indigenous Peoples (NCIP) and recognized ancestral domain rights, though enforcement has been undermined by mining interests and militarization.

## China

### The Minority Nationalities Framework

China's approach to indigenous rights operates through the conceptual framework of "minority nationalities" (shaoshu minzu) rather than "indigenous peoples" (yuanzhumin). The PRC does not use the concept of indigenous peoples in domestic law and has consistently stated that all Chinese nationalities are indigenous to China. The ethnic classification project (minzu shibie) of the 1950s, modeled on Stalin's criteria for nationhood (common territory, language, economic life, and psychological character), identified 55 minority nationalities alongside the Han majority.

Article 4 of the PRC Constitution provides: "All nationalities in the People's Republic of China are equal. The State protects the lawful rights and interests of the minority nationalities and upholds and develops the relationship of equality, unity and mutual assistance among all of China's nationalities." It further states: "Regional autonomy is practiced in areas where people of minority nationalities live in compact communities; in these areas organs of self-government are established for the exercise of the right of autonomy."

The Regional Ethnic Autonomy Law (REAL, 1984, amended 2001) operationalizes this framework. Article 15 requires that the head of government (chairman/governor) of each autonomous region, prefecture, or county be a citizen of the nationality exercising autonomy. Article 19 grants autonomous governments the power to "formulate regulations on the exercise of autonomy (zizhi tiaoli) and separate regulations (danxing tiaoli) in light of the political, economic and cultural characteristics of the nationality." Articles 25-28 address economic development, while Articles 36-39 address education and culture, including the right to "use and develop their own spoken and written languages."

### Autonomy in Practice

The five autonomous regions -- Tibet (Xizang), Xinjiang Uyghur, Inner Mongolia (Nei Mongol), Guangxi Zhuang, and Ningxia Hui -- nominally exercise substantial self-governance powers. In practice, the CPC maintains control through several mechanisms: the Party secretary (invariably Han in Tibet and Xinjiang) holds greater authority than the government chairman; the "patriotic education" campaigns in Tibet require monks and nuns to denounce the Dalai Lama; and the "Strike Hard" (yanli daji) campaigns in Xinjiang target Uyghur cultural and religious practice.

The 2010 and 2020 work conferences on Tibet and Xinjiang formalized policies of accelerated economic development combined with intensified political control. In Inner Mongolia, the 2020 policy requiring Mandarin-medium instruction in schools previously teaching in Mongolian provoked rare public protests and highlighted the tension between cultural autonomy and national language policy.

The Tibetan Autonomous Region Regulation on the Protection of the Tibetan Language (2002) nominally guarantees bilingual education, but implementation has increasingly favored Mandarin instruction. Human Rights Watch documented in "China's Bilingual Education Policy in Tibet" (2020) that boarding schools separating Tibetan children from their communities have expanded dramatically, with an estimated 900,000 to 1 million Tibetan children in residential schools as of 2021.

### Resource Extraction and Environmental Impact

Minority autonomous regions contain a disproportionate share of China's natural resources: approximately 50% of hydroelectric potential, significant uranium deposits, and vast pastoral and forestland. The Western Development Strategy (xibu da kaifa, 2000) accelerated infrastructure and extraction in these regions. The Golmud-Lhasa railway (Qinghai-Tibet Railway, 2006), Three Gorges Dam displacement of Tujia and Miao communities, and rare earth mining in Inner Mongolia exemplify development projects proceeding without meaningful indigenous consent.

## India

### Constitutional Architecture for Scheduled Tribes

The Indian Constitution creates an elaborate framework for tribal governance. The President designates communities as "Scheduled Tribes" (STs) under Article 342, based on criteria including "indications of primitive traits, distinctive culture, geographical isolation, shyness of contact with the community at large, and backwardness." The 2011 census recorded 104.3 million STs (8.6% of the population) across 705 designated communities.

The Fifth Schedule (Article 244(1)) applies to "Scheduled Areas" in nine states: Andhra Pradesh, Telangana, Chhattisgarh, Gujarat, Himachal Pradesh, Jharkhand, Madhya Pradesh, Maharashtra, Odisha, and Rajasthan. The Governor has power to direct that parliamentary or state legislative acts do not apply to Scheduled Areas or apply with modifications (Fifth Schedule, Para. 5). Tribes Advisory Councils (Para. 4) advise on tribal welfare.

The Sixth Schedule (Article 244(2)) applies to tribal areas in Assam, Meghalaya, Tripura, and Mizoram, creating Autonomous District Councils (ADCs) and Regional Councils with legislative, executive, and judicial powers. ADCs may make laws on land allotment, forest management (other than reserved forests), inheritance of property, marriage and social customs, and village administration.

### Forest Rights Act, 2006

The Scheduled Tribes and Other Traditional Forest Dwellers (Recognition of Forest Rights) Act, 2006 (FRA), enacted after decades of tribal rights activism, represented a paradigm shift in Indian forest governance. The FRA recognized that "the forest rights on ancestral lands and their habitat were not adequately recognised in the consolidation of State forests during the colonial period as well as in independent India, resulting in historical injustice to the forest dwelling Scheduled Tribes and other traditional forest dwellers."

Key provisions include: recognition of individual forest rights (Section 3(1)(a)) for land under occupation before December 13, 2005; community forest rights (Section 3(1)(b-l)) including rights over common property resources, community tenures, habitat rights for Particularly Vulnerable Tribal Groups (PVTGs), and the right to protect, regenerate, conserve, and manage community forest resources (Section 3(1)(i)); and the critical provision that no member of a forest-dwelling community can be evicted without completion of the rights recognition process (Section 4(5)).

The Supreme Court's order in Wildlife First v. Ministry of Environment and Forests (February 13, 2019), directing the eviction of millions of forest dwellers whose FRA claims had been rejected, sent shockwaves through tribal communities before being stayed on February 28, 2019. The case highlighted implementation failures: rejected claims often reflected bureaucratic failures rather than invalid occupancy, and gram sabha verification processes were frequently inadequate.

### PESA and Displacement

The Panchayats (Extension to Scheduled Areas) Act, 1996 (PESA), extended Panchayati Raj (local self-governance) to Fifth Schedule areas while requiring that the gram sabha (village assembly) be "competent to safeguard and preserve the traditions and customs of the people, their cultural identity, community resources and the customary mode of dispute resolution" (Section 4(d)). Section 4(i) required consultation with gram sabhas before land acquisition for development projects.

Despite PESA, tribal displacement for development projects has continued at scale. The Narmada dam projects displaced an estimated 320,000 people, predominantly tribals, as documented in the Narmada Bachao Andolan v. Union of India (2000) case. The Posco-India project in Odisha, the Tata Nano factory displacement in Singur (West Bengal), and coal mining in Jharkhand's Scheduled Areas illustrate ongoing conflicts between development imperatives and tribal land rights. The Right to Fair Compensation and Transparency in Land Acquisition, Rehabilitation and Resettlement Act, 2013 (LARR) introduced consent requirements (Section 2(2): 80% consent for private projects, 70% for PPP projects) and social impact assessments, but amendments and state-level dilutions have weakened these protections.

## Africa

### South Africa: Traditional Leadership and Land

Sections 211 and 212 of the South African Constitution recognize the "institution, status and role of traditional leadership, according to customary law" subject to the Constitution, and permit national or provincial legislation to provide for "a role for traditional leadership as an institution at local level on matters affecting local communities." The Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act (Act 41 of 2003) regulates the establishment and functions of traditional councils, but these operate in an advisory capacity to municipalities rather than exercising autonomous legislative or judicial authority.

The Ingonyama Trust, established by the KwaZulu-Natal Ingonyama Trust Act (3KZ of 1994) on the eve of South Africa's democratic transition, vests approximately 2.8 million hectares of land (about 30% of KwaZulu-Natal) in the Zulu monarch as trustee for the benefit of communities subject to customary law. The Trust's conversion of Permission to Occupy (PTO) rights to leases in the early 2000s was challenged in the Ingonyama Trust and Others v. eThekwini Municipality case and became a major controversy when the High-Level Panel on the Assessment of Key Legislation (the "Motlanthe Panel," 2017) recommended its dissolution. The Traditional and Khoi-San Leadership Act (Act 3 of 2019) extended recognition to Khoi-San communities but has been criticized for vesting excessive power in traditional leaders at the expense of community members' rights.

The Communal Land Rights Act (CLaRA, Act 11 of 2004), which would have vested authority over communal land in traditional councils, was struck down by the Constitutional Court in Tongoane v. National Minister for Agriculture and Land Affairs (2010 (6) SA 214 (CC)) on the grounds that it should have been referred to the National Council of Provinces as it affected provincial competences. The underlying substantive critique -- that CLaRA would have dispossessed community members of their tenure rights in favor of traditional leaders -- was central to civil society opposition led by the Legal Resources Centre and the Rural Women's Movement.

Land reform under the Restitution of Land Rights Act (22 of 1994) and the Redistribution programme has proceeded slowly; the Constitutional Court in Land Access Movement of South Africa v. Chairperson of the National Council of Provinces (2016 (5) SA 635 (CC)) struck down the Restitution of Land Rights Amendment Act (2014) for inadequate public participation, illustrating procedural challenges.

### Nigeria: The Land Use Act and Ethnic Tensions

Nigeria does not employ an "indigenous peoples" legal category; the country comprises over 250 ethnic groups, each with claims to ancestral territory. The Land Use Act (1978), enacted under military rule and incorporated into the Constitution (Section 315(5)(d)), vests all land in each state in the Governor, who holds it "in trust and administered for the use and common benefit of all Nigerians." This effectively extinguished communal customary tenure, requiring communities to obtain "rights of occupancy" from the state.

The Niger Delta conflict exemplifies the consequences: Ogoni, Ijaw, and other communities in oil-producing regions have seen their customary lands subjected to extraction by multinational corporations and the Nigerian National Petroleum Corporation (NNPC) without adequate compensation or environmental protection. The UNEP Environmental Assessment of Ogoniland (2011) documented extensive contamination, and the ECOWAS Community Court in SERAP v. Nigeria (2012) found that Nigeria had violated the right to an adequate standard of living through failure to address environmental degradation.

The African Commission on Human and Peoples' Rights in Social and Economic Rights Action Centre (SERAC) and Centre for Economic and Social Rights (CESR) v. Nigeria (Communication 155/96, 2001) -- the "Ogoni case" -- found that Nigeria had violated multiple provisions of the African Charter, including Article 21 (right to freely dispose of wealth and natural resources) and Article 24 (right to a generally satisfactory environment), by facilitating oil company operations that destroyed Ogoni farming and fishing resources.

### African Union Framework

The African Commission's Working Group of Experts on Indigenous Populations/Communities adopted an Advisory Opinion (2007) acknowledging that the concept of indigenous peoples applies in Africa, identifying pastoralists, hunter-gatherers, and other marginalized communities as indigenous within the African Charter framework. The African Court on Human and Peoples' Rights in African Commission on Human and Peoples' Rights v. Republic of Kenya (Application No. 006/2012, 2017) -- the Ogiek case -- found that Kenya had violated the Ogiek people's rights to land (Article 14), culture (Article 17), religion (Article 8), natural resources (Article 21), and development (Article 22) by evicting them from the Mau Forest, in the first indigenous land rights decision by a regional human rights court in Africa.

## Latin America

### Brazil: Constitutional Protection and the Marco Temporal

Articles 231 and 232 of the Brazilian Constitution of 1988 provide the strongest textual protection for indigenous land rights in any Global South constitution. Article 231 recognizes indigenous peoples' "original rights over the lands they traditionally occupy, it being incumbent upon the Union to demarcate them, protect and ensure respect for all of their property." Article 231, para. 1 defines traditionally occupied lands as "those on which they live on a permanent basis, those used for their productive activities, those indispensable for the preservation of the environmental resources necessary for their well-being and for their physical and cultural reproduction, according to their uses, customs and traditions." Article 231, para. 2 provides that these lands "are intended for their permanent possession and they shall have the exclusive usufruct of the riches of the soil, the rivers and the lakes existing therein." Article 232 grants indigenous communities standing to sue in defense of their rights.

The Fundacao Nacional dos Povos Indigenas (FUNAI, formerly FUNAI - Fundacao Nacional do Indio) is responsible for demarcation of indigenous territories. As of 2024, 736 indigenous territories had been demarcated or were in process, covering approximately 13.8% of Brazil's territory (117 million hectares), predominantly in the Amazon.

The marco temporal (temporal framework) thesis -- the argument that indigenous peoples can only claim rights to lands they physically occupied or were actively contesting on October 5, 1988 (the date the Constitution was promulgated) -- was advanced by agribusiness interests and endorsed by the AGU (Advocacia-Geral da Uniao) under the Temer administration in Parecer 001/2017. The STF's decision in RE 1.017.365 (the Xokleng/Terra Indigena Ibirama-Laklano case, September 2023) rejected the marco temporal thesis by a 9-2 vote, holding that the Constitution's recognition of "original rights" (direitos originarios) was not subject to a temporal cutoff. However, Congress subsequently enacted Law No. 14,701/2023 reinstating elements of the temporal framework, which President Lula partially vetoed; the constitutionality of the remaining provisions was immediately challenged before the STF.

### Colombian Consulta Previa

Colombia has developed the most progressive jurisprudence on indigenous consultation in the Global South. The Constitutional Court in Sentencia SU-039/97 (the U'wa case, involving Occidental Petroleum exploration on U'wa territory) established that prior consultation with indigenous communities is a fundamental right derived from Article 330 of the Constitution, ILO Convention 169 (ratified by Colombia in 1991), and the right to cultural integrity. Sentencia T-129/11 elevated the standard from consultation to consent (consentimiento) for projects that would fundamentally alter indigenous communities' way of life, territory, or cultural survival.

Colombia's resguardo system provides indigenous communities with collective, inalienable territorial units governed by their own authorities (cabildos). The 1991 Constitution (Article 286) recognizes indigenous territories as territorial entities with rights to govern themselves, administer resources, and receive fiscal transfers. Approximately 800 resguardos cover 31.5 million hectares (27.6% of Colombia's territory), though many overlap with extractive concessions.

### Mexican Indigenous Autonomy

Mexico's 2001 constitutional reform (Article 2) -- enacted in response to the Zapatista uprising and the San Andres Accords (1996) -- recognized indigenous peoples' right to self-determination (libre determinacion) exercised as autonomy within the constitutional framework. The reform recognized indigenous peoples' right to "apply their own normative systems in the regulation and resolution of internal conflicts" and to "elect, in accordance with their norms, procedures and practices, the authorities or representatives for the exercise of their own forms of internal government."

Oaxaca's Indigenous Rights Law (Ley de Derechos de los Pueblos y Comunidades Indigenas del Estado de Oaxaca, 1998, reformed 2001) permits 417 of the state's 570 municipalities to elect authorities through usos y costumbres (customary practices) rather than political parties. The Inter-American Court of Human Rights in Yatama v. Nicaragua (2005) held that requiring indigenous communities to organize as political parties to participate in elections violated the American Convention, reinforcing the right to political participation through traditional governance structures.

## Southeast Asia

### Indonesian Adat Rights

Indonesia's recognition of adat (customary law) communities operates through Constitutional Article 18B(2): "The State recognizes and respects traditional communities along with their traditional customary rights as long as these remain in existence and are in accordance with the societal development and the principles of the Unitary State of the Republic of Indonesia, and are regulated by law."

The landmark Constitutional Court Decision No. 35/PUU-X/2012 (MK 35) overturned the classification of adat forests (hutan adat) as state forests under the Forestry Law (Law No. 41 of 1999). The Court held that "adat forests are not state forests" and that customary forests belong to adat communities, not the state. This decision potentially affects millions of hectares but implementation has been slow; by early 2026, only approximately 130,000 hectares of adat forest had been formally recognized through the Ministry of Environment and Forestry's social forestry program.

The Village Law (Law No. 6 of 2014) recognizes "adat villages" (desa adat) alongside administrative villages, permitting adat communities to govern internal affairs according to customary norms. The Agrarian Reform (Reforma Agraria) program under President Jokowi (2014-2024) included targets for recognizing adat territory through the One Map Policy (Kebijakan Satu Peta), but overlapping land claims between adat communities, corporations holding plantation permits (HGU), and state forest designations remain a central obstacle.

The Aliansi Masyarakat Adat Nusantara (AMAN, Indigenous Peoples Alliance of the Archipelago), Indonesia's largest indigenous organization, estimates that adat communities number approximately 50-70 million people across 2,591 registered communities, with traditional territories covering an estimated 40 million hectares. The Constitutional Court in Decision No. 55/PUU-VIII/2010 recognized that the state obligation to recognize adat communities was not conditioned on the enactment of specific legislation but could be implemented through existing regulatory frameworks.

### Philippine IPRA

The Indigenous Peoples Rights Act (IPRA, Republic Act No. 8371, 1997) is the most comprehensive indigenous rights statute in Southeast Asia. IPRA recognizes four bundles of rights: (1) rights to ancestral domains and lands (Sections 4-12); (2) right to self-governance and empowerment (Sections 13-20); (3) social justice and human rights (Sections 21-28); and (4) right to cultural integrity (Sections 29-37).

The ancestral domain rights provisions authorize the issuance of Certificates of Ancestral Domain Title (CADTs), which recognize the rights of indigenous cultural communities (ICCs) to their ancestral domains, including lands, waters, and natural resources. CADTs are communal titles that are "inalienable, imprescriptible, and indefeasable" (Section 7(a)). The National Commission on Indigenous Peoples (NCIP) administers the CADT process and serves as the primary government agency for indigenous affairs.

Free, Prior and Informed Consent (FPIC) under IPRA (Section 59) requires that ICCs give their consent before any project or activity is undertaken within ancestral domains. NCIP Administrative Order No. 3, Series of 2012 (the "FPIC Guidelines") establishes the procedural requirements. However, enforcement has been criticized: the Philippine Indigenous Peoples Links (PIPLinks) and the International Work Group for Indigenous Affairs (IWGIA) have documented cases where FPIC processes were manipulated, community consent was obtained from non-representative leaders, or projects proceeded despite community opposition.

The Supreme Court in Cruz v. Secretary of DENR (G.R. No. 135385, December 6, 2000) upheld the constitutionality of IPRA by an equally divided court (7-7), with Justice Puno's opinion arguing that IPRA's recognition of ancestral domain rights was consistent with the Regalian Doctrine (state ownership of natural resources) because it recognized prior vested rights rather than creating new ones. The practical tension between IPRA and the Philippine Mining Act (Republic Act No. 7942, 1995) remains unresolved, with mining permits frequently overlapping ancestral domains.

The militarization of indigenous territories, particularly in Mindanao and the Cordillera, has generated documented cases of extrajudicial killings, forced displacement, and "red-tagging" (labeling indigenous activists as communist insurgents). The UN Special Rapporteur on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples, in her 2018 report on the Philippines (A/HRC/39/17/Add.3), expressed concern about "the alarming number of indigenous leaders and human rights defenders who have been killed."

## Comparative Analysis

### Models of Indigenous Recognition

The five-region comparison reveals three broad models of indigenous recognition in the Global South. First, the "nationality/ethnic minority" model (China, and to some extent Nigeria) does not recognize indigenous peoples as a distinct legal category, instead incorporating ethnic diversity into a framework of national unity and equal citizenship. Second, the "scheduled" or "listed" model (India, Indonesia, Philippines) recognizes specific communities through administrative processes, granting them defined rights and protections. Third, the "constitutional autonomy" model (Brazil, Colombia, Mexico) recognizes indigenous peoples as collective rights-holders with territorial and governance autonomy.

### Land Rights as the Central Question

Across all five regions, land rights constitute the most contested dimension of indigenous rights. China's state ownership model, India's forest governance framework, Nigeria's Land Use Act, Brazil's demarcation process, and Indonesia's state forest classification all represent different mechanisms through which state sovereignty claims conflict with indigenous territorial rights. The trend across the Global South is toward recognition -- Brazil's Article 231, Colombia's resguardos, Philippines' CADTs, Indonesia's MK 35 -- but implementation consistently lags behind legal recognition, particularly where extractive industries are involved.

### FPIC: The Implementation Gap

Free, Prior and Informed Consent has emerged as the central procedural right in indigenous rights discourse, recognized in the UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP, 2007), ILO Convention 169, and the jurisprudence of the Inter-American Court of Human Rights. Among the jurisdictions examined, Colombia's consulta previa doctrine and the Philippines' IPRA provide the most developed FPIC frameworks, while China and Nigeria lack any meaningful FPIC mechanism. India's FRA and PESA include consultation requirements that fall short of full FPIC. The persistent gap between FPIC requirements and actual practice -- documented across all regions -- suggests that procedural rights alone are insufficient without robust enforcement mechanisms and genuine power-sharing.
