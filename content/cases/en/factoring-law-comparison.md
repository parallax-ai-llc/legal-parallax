## Summary

Factoring -- the purchase and financing of trade receivables -- is a critical component of global trade finance, with global factoring volume exceeding EUR 3.5 trillion annually. The legal frameworks governing the assignment of receivables, notification requirements, debtor protection, and regulatory oversight vary considerably across jurisdictions, creating complexity for cross-border factoring transactions. South Korea regulates factoring primarily through the Civil Act (민법) provisions on assignment of claims (채권양도), supplemented by the Act on Registration of Assignment of Movable Property and Claims (동산·채권 등의 담보에 관한 법률) and financial regulatory oversight by the Financial Services Commission (금융위원회). The United States governs receivables financing through Article 9 of the Uniform Commercial Code (UCC), which establishes a comprehensive secured transactions framework where assignments of accounts are treated as security interests requiring perfection through filing, alongside the Assignment of Claims Act (41 U.S.C. Section 6305) governing federal receivables. Germany provides a sophisticated legal framework through the Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB) provisions on assignment (Abtretung, Sections 398-413), the Handelsgesetzbuch (HGB), and specialized jurisprudence of the Bundesgerichtshof (BGH) on factoring agreements, including the landmark treatment of the Globalzession (global assignment) and its interaction with the extended retention of title (verlängerter Eigentumsvorbehalt). At the international level, the UNIDROIT Convention on International Factoring (Ottawa Convention, 1988), the United Nations Convention on the Assignment of Receivables in International Trade (2001), and the EU Late Payment Directive 2011/7/EU provide the multilateral framework.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's factoring law reflects a civil law tradition supplemented by modern registration systems designed to facilitate secured lending using receivables as collateral.

### Civil Act Provisions on Assignment (채권양도)

The Korean Civil Act (민법) governs the assignment of claims in Articles 449 through 452. Assignment is effected by agreement between the assignor and assignee without any requirement of debtor consent or notification for validity between the parties.

**Article 449 (Assignability of Claims):** Claims are freely assignable unless the nature of the claim precludes assignment, or the parties have agreed to prohibit assignment. Anti-assignment clauses are enforceable against third parties unless the third party was unaware of the restriction.

**Article 450 (Notification and Consent):** An assignment cannot be asserted against the debtor or other third parties unless the assignor has notified the debtor or the debtor has consented. Notification or consent must bear a fixed date (확정일자) to be effective against third parties other than the debtor. This date-certain requirement is critical for establishing priority among competing assignees.

**Article 451 (Defenses):** The debtor may assert against the assignee any defense that could have been raised against the assignor at the time of notification or consent. Set-off rights are preserved to the extent the debtor's cross-claim arose before notification.

### Act on Registration of Assignment of Movable Property and Claims (동산·채권 등의 담보에 관한 법률)

Enacted in 2010 and effective June 2012, this act introduced a registration system for assignments of claims (and security interests in movable property), allowing assignees to perfect their rights through registration with the Supreme Court Internet Registry (대법원 인터넷등기소) as an alternative to debtor notification with a fixed date.

**Registration vs. Notification:** Registration and notification with a fixed date are alternative means of obtaining third-party effectiveness (대항력). When there is conflict between a registered assignee and a notified assignee, priority is determined by the earlier of the registration date or the notification date.

**Scope:** The registration system is available for commercial claims (trade receivables), making it particularly useful for factoring transactions involving large portfolios of receivables where individual debtor notification would be impractical.

### Factoring Industry and Regulation

Factoring in Korea is conducted primarily by credit-specialized financial companies (여신전문금융회사) regulated under the Specialized Credit Financial Business Act (여신전문금융업법) and by banks. The Financial Services Commission (금융위원회) and Financial Supervisory Service (금융감독원) provide regulatory oversight.

**Market Structure:** The Korean factoring market is dominated by subsidiaries of major financial groups (Samsung Card, Hyundai Capital, Shinhan Card) and specialized factoring companies. Reverse factoring (공급망 금융) has grown significantly, particularly in the supply chains of major conglomerates (재벌).

### Key Case Law

**Supreme Court Decision 2010Da50637 (2012):** The Court clarified the priority rules for competing assignments, holding that as between two assignees who have both notified the debtor with fixed dates, the earlier fixed date prevails, regardless of the order of actual notification.

**Supreme Court Decision 2016Da220143 (2018):** The Court addressed the interaction between factoring assignments and the debtor's right of set-off, holding that a debtor who received notification of assignment may set off a cross-claim that became due for payment before the notification, even if the cross-claim arose from a separate transaction.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States provides the world's most developed and systematized framework for receivables financing through UCC Article 9, which treats most assignments of receivables as secured transactions subject to a comprehensive filing and priority system.

### UCC Article 9 -- Secured Transactions

**Scope:** Article 9 applies to any transaction that creates a security interest in personal property, including "accounts" (rights to payment for goods sold, leased, licensed, or services rendered) and "payment intangibles." Crucially, Article 9 applies to outright sales of accounts and chattel paper as well as assignments for security, meaning that factoring transactions are governed by Article 9 regardless of whether they are structured as true sales or secured loans.

**Attachment and Perfection:** A security interest attaches when (1) value has been given, (2) the debtor has rights in the collateral, and (3) the debtor has authenticated a security agreement describing the collateral (Section 9-203). Perfection of a security interest in accounts requires filing a UCC-1 financing statement with the appropriate state filing office (Section 9-310).

**Priority:** The general rule is first to file or perfect (Section 9-322). A perfected security interest takes priority over an unperfected interest. Among competing perfected interests, the first to file or perfect prevails.

### Anti-Assignment Clauses

**Section 9-406(d):** A term in an agreement between an account debtor and an assignor that prohibits, restricts, or requires consent to assignment of accounts or payment intangibles is ineffective. This statutory override is a cornerstone of American receivables financing, enabling factors to purchase receivables without concern for contractual assignment restrictions.

**Exception -- Government Contracts:** The federal Assignment of Claims Act (41 U.S.C. Section 6305) restricts the assignment of claims against the United States, requiring compliance with specific procedures including written notice to the contracting officer and the surety on any payment bond. The Federal Acquisition Regulation (FAR 32.8) implements these requirements.

### True Sale Analysis

A critical issue in US factoring law is whether a transaction constitutes a "true sale" of receivables or a secured loan. The distinction matters for bankruptcy purposes: if the receivables have been truly sold, they are not property of the seller's bankruptcy estate, and the factor is not subject to the automatic stay. Courts analyze true sale using factors including recourse obligations, the transfer of credit risk, the factor's control over collection, and the pricing structure.

**Major's Furniture Mart, Inc. v. Castle Credit Corp., 602 F.2d 538 (3d Cir. 1979):** Established the multi-factor test for true sale analysis, examining whether the assignee or assignor bears the risk of loss, retains the benefits of ownership, and exercises control over the receivables.

**In re LTV Steel Co., Inc., 274 B.R. 278 (Bankr. N.D. Ohio 2001):** The bankruptcy court controversially recharacterized a securitization as a secured loan despite the parties' true sale structuring, creating uncertainty in the receivables finance market that was subsequently addressed by market practices and legislative proposals.

### Factoring Disclosure

Several US states, including New York (S5470, signed 2020), California (SB 1235, 2018), Virginia, and Utah, have enacted commercial financing disclosure laws requiring factoring companies to provide standardized disclosures to small business borrowers, including estimated annualized cost of financing, total repayment amount, and payment schedule.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's factoring law operates within the BGB's general assignment framework but has been shaped by extensive BGH jurisprudence addressing the commercial realities of factoring, particularly the interaction between global assignments and retention of title arrangements.

### BGB Assignment Framework (Abtretung)

**Section 398 BGB:** A creditor may transfer a claim to another person by agreement (Abtretungsvertrag). Upon assignment, the new creditor steps into the position of the previous creditor. No particular form is required for the assignment agreement.

**Section 399 BGB:** A claim cannot be assigned if performance to a third party would alter its content, or if the assignment is excluded by agreement between the creditor and debtor. However, Section 354a HGB overrides contractual anti-assignment clauses for monetary claims arising from commercial transactions.

**Section 404 BGB:** The debtor may raise against the new creditor any defense that was available against the previous creditor at the time of assignment.

**Section 407 BGB:** If the debtor performs to the assignor without knowledge of the assignment, or if legal transactions (including set-off) occur between the debtor and the assignor, the debtor is protected against the assignee. This provision creates the practical importance of notification: while not legally required for the validity of the assignment, notification prevents the debtor from discharging the debt by payment to the assignor.

### Factoring Contract Law

German law distinguishes between "echtes Factoring" (non-recourse factoring, where the factor assumes the credit risk) and "unechtes Factoring" (recourse factoring, where the assignor retains the credit risk). This distinction has significant consequences for accounting treatment, insolvency law classification, and regulatory requirements.

**BGH, Urteil vom 19.09.1977 -- VIII ZR 169/76:** The BGH established the foundational principles for the legal classification of factoring agreements, holding that non-recourse factoring constitutes a sale of receivables (Forderungskauf), while recourse factoring is more properly classified as a loan secured by assigned receivables.

### Global Assignment and Retention of Title Conflict

**Globalzession (Global Assignment):** In German commercial practice, banks frequently require borrowers to assign all present and future trade receivables as security (Sicherungsglobalzession). Simultaneously, suppliers commonly sell goods subject to extended retention of title (verlängerter Eigentumsvorbehalt), under which the buyer/debtor assigns the proceeds of resale to the supplier as security.

**BGH, Urteil vom 25.10.1952 -- I ZR 48/52:** The BGH addressed the conflict between global assignments to banks and extended retention of title, holding that the retention of title assignment prevails because it is chronologically prior (arising at the time of each individual sale, while the global assignment purports to capture the receivable upon creation). This principle has been refined through extensive subsequent case law.

**Priority of Factor vs. Extended Retention of Title:** In factoring transactions, the factor's position may be affected by existing retention of title arrangements. The BGH has held that a factor who purchases receivables with knowledge of the supplier's extended retention of title cannot claim priority over the supplier's assigned interest.

### Regulatory Framework

Since November 25, 2008, factoring has been classified as a financial service (Finanzdienstleistung) under Section 1(1a) No. 9 of the German Banking Act (Kreditwesengesetz, KWG). Factoring companies require a license from the Federal Financial Supervisory Authority (Bundesanstalt für Finanzdienstleistungsaufsicht, BaFin) and are subject to anti-money laundering requirements, capital adequacy standards, and ongoing supervisory reporting.

**Impact of Licensing Requirement:** The BaFin licensing requirement has professionalized the German factoring industry and increased consumer/debtor protection but has also raised barriers to entry, consolidating the market among larger financial institutions.

## ECHR & EU

### UNIDROIT Convention on International Factoring (Ottawa Convention, 1988)

The Ottawa Convention, adopted on May 28, 1988, was the first international instrument specifically addressing factoring. It entered into force on May 1, 1995, and has been ratified by relatively few states (including France, Italy, Germany, Hungary, Latvia, and Nigeria, but not South Korea, the United States, or the United Kingdom).

**Article 1 -- Scope:** The Convention applies to factoring contracts where the factor performs at least two of the following functions: financing the supplier, maintenance of accounts relating to receivables, collection of receivables, and protection against default in payment by debtors.

**Article 6 -- Override of Anti-Assignment Clauses:** The assignment of a receivable by the supplier to the factor is effective notwithstanding any agreement between the supplier and the debtor prohibiting such assignment. This provision was groundbreaking at the time of adoption and influenced subsequent developments in national law.

**Article 9 -- Debtor's Defenses:** The debtor may raise against the factor all defenses arising from the contract giving rise to the receivable, preserving the debtor's position.

### United Nations Convention on the Assignment of Receivables in International Trade (2001)

The UN Convention, adopted by the General Assembly on December 12, 2001, has not yet entered into force (requiring five ratifications). Despite this, it has significantly influenced the development of national law and model legislation.

**Article 9 -- Effectiveness of Assignment Despite Anti-Assignment Clauses:** An assignment of a receivable is effective notwithstanding any agreement between the initial or any subsequent assignor and the debtor or any subsequent assignee limiting in any way the assignor's right to assign its receivables. This override is broader than the Ottawa Convention's provision.

**UNCITRAL Legislative Guide on Secured Transactions (2007):** This guide recommends that states adopt comprehensive secured transactions regimes that include receivables financing, drawing heavily on the UN Convention's principles.

### EU Late Payment Directive 2011/7/EU

The directive combats late payment in commercial transactions by establishing maximum payment terms (30 days for public authorities, 60 days for business-to-business unless expressly agreed) and automatic interest on late payments. By reducing payment uncertainty, the directive indirectly supports factoring by making receivables more predictable and valuable.

### EU Anti-Money Laundering Framework

Factoring companies are "obliged entities" under the EU Anti-Money Laundering Directives (most recently the AML Regulation (EU) 2024/1624 and Directive (EU) 2024/1640). They must conduct customer due diligence, maintain records, and report suspicious transactions to national financial intelligence units.

### European Banking Authority (EBA) Guidelines

The EBA has issued guidelines relevant to factoring, including Guidelines on loan origination and monitoring (EBA/GL/2020/06), which apply to credit institutions and financial institutions engaged in factoring, and address credit risk assessment, pricing, collateral valuation, and monitoring standards.

### ECHR Considerations

**Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (Protection of Property):** Receivables constitute "possessions" protected under the Convention. Regulatory measures that impair the value of receivables or restrict assignment may engage property rights, though states enjoy a wide margin of appreciation. The ECHR held in Stran Greek Refineries and Stratis Andreadis v. Greece (Application No. 13427/87, 1994) that a legislative act retroactively nullifying an arbitral award constituted a violation of property rights.

**Article 6 (Right to a Fair Trial):** Disputes between factors, debtors, and assignors involving competing claims to receivables must be resolved through fair judicial proceedings.

## Comparative Analysis

### Anti-Assignment Clause Treatment

The treatment of contractual anti-assignment clauses reveals the most significant policy divergence. The United States provides the strongest override through UCC Section 9-406, rendering anti-assignment clauses ineffective for all accounts and payment intangibles. Germany provides a commercial override through Section 354a HGB for monetary claims in commercial transactions. South Korea generally enforces anti-assignment clauses against assignees with knowledge. The international conventions (Ottawa Convention Article 6, UN Convention Article 9) support override of anti-assignment clauses to facilitate receivables financing.

### Priority Systems

Priority determination varies fundamentally. The United States employs a registration-based priority system (first to file or perfect). South Korea uses a notification-based system with a date-certain requirement, supplemented by a newer registration alternative. Germany relies on the chronological priority of assignments (first in time) without a mandatory registration system, creating particular complexity in the Globalzession context. The lack of harmonized international priority rules remains a significant barrier to cross-border factoring.

### Debtor Protection

All jurisdictions preserve the debtor's defenses against the factor, reflecting the principle that assignment should not worsen the debtor's position. The mechanisms differ: the US protects debtors through UCC Section 9-404 (defense preservation) and Section 9-406 (notification provisions); Germany through BGB Sections 404-407; South Korea through Civil Act Articles 451-452; and the international conventions through explicit defense-preservation provisions.

### Regulatory Classification

The regulatory treatment of factoring varies significantly. Germany classifies factoring as a regulated financial service requiring BaFin licensing. The United States has traditionally treated factoring as lightly regulated, though state commercial financing disclosure laws represent a trend toward greater regulation. South Korea requires licensing for credit-specialized financial businesses. The EU anti-money laundering framework applies across all member states.

### True Sale vs. Security Interest

The characterization of factoring as a true sale or secured loan has different consequences in each jurisdiction. The US approach of applying Article 9 to both true sales and security assignments of accounts reduces the practical significance of the distinction for priority purposes but preserves it for bankruptcy analysis. Germany's distinction between echtes and unechtes Factoring has clearer doctrinal consequences. The international conventions generally avoid forcing a characterization, focusing instead on the effectiveness and priority of assignments.
