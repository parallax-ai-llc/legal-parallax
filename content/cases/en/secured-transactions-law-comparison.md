## Summary

Secured transactions law governs the creation, perfection, priority, and enforcement of security interests in movable (personal) property, providing creditors with preferential claims against a debtor's assets to secure the repayment of obligations and thus enabling credit extension at lower cost. The legal frameworks differ dramatically between common law and civil law traditions, with significant ongoing harmonization efforts. South Korea regulates security interests in movables through a dual system: the Korean Civil Code (민법) provisions on pledge (질권, Articles 329-355) and the Act on Security over Movable Property, Claims, etc. (동산·채권 등의 담보에 관한 법률, Act No. 10366 of 2010, effective 2012), which introduced a modern registration-based non-possessory security interest (동산담보권) modeled in part on UCC Article 9 and the UNCITRAL Legislative Guide on Secured Transactions; prior to this Act, Korean practice relied heavily on the judicially recognized yangdo dambo (양도담보, security transfer of title), which remains available alongside the statutory regime. The United States governs secured transactions through the Uniform Commercial Code (UCC) Article 9 (Secured Transactions), as revised in 2001 and adopted in all 50 states, which provides a comprehensive unitary framework encompassing virtually all consensual security interests in personal property, including accounts, chattel paper, deposit accounts, equipment, farm products, general intangibles, instruments, inventory, investment property, and letter-of-credit rights, with perfection primarily through filing a financing statement with the state filing office. Germany does not have a unitary secured transactions law; instead, security interests in movables are created through the Civil Code (BGB) possessory pledge (Pfandrecht, §§ 1204-1259), the judicially developed non-possessory security transfer (Sicherungsübereignung, based on §§ 929-931 BGB), and the security assignment of claims (Sicherungszession, based on §§ 398ff. BGB), while the Grundbuch system governs real property security (Grundschuld and Hypothek); the German system lacks a public registration system for movable security interests, creating priority uncertainties resolved through complex case law. The EU/ECHR framework includes the UNCITRAL Model Law on Secured Transactions (2016), the DCFR Book IX on Proprietary Security in Movable Assets, the Cape Town Convention on International Interests in Mobile Equipment (2001), and the EU's own initiatives toward a European framework for secured transactions, including Directive 2002/47/EC on financial collateral arrangements and the European Commission's work on cross-border aspects of secured transactions.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's secured transactions law underwent a fundamental transformation with the enactment of the Act on Security over Movable Property, Claims, etc. (동산·채권 등의 담보에 관한 법률) in 2010, which came into force on June 11, 2012. This Act created a modern registration-based security interest system alongside the pre-existing Civil Code pledge and judicially developed yangdo dambo.

**Civil Code Pledge (질권, Articles 329-355):** The traditional possessory pledge requires delivery of possession of the movable to the pledgee (Article 330). The pledgee has a right of priority satisfaction (우선변제권) from the pledged property. Because the pledge requires dispossession, it is impractical for business inventory and equipment financing, which led to the development of non-possessory security devices.

**Yangdo Dambo (양도담보, Security Transfer of Title):** Prior to the 2010 Act, Korean practice extensively used the yangdo dambo, a judicially recognized security device in which the debtor transfers ownership of movable property to the creditor as security, while retaining possession and use. The Korean Supreme Court has recognized yangdo dambo since the 1960s, developing extensive case law on its creation, priority, and enforcement:

- **Supreme Court Decision 66Da1343 (1966):** First systematic recognition of yangdo dambo as a valid security device.
- **Supreme Court Decision 2004Da21923 (2005):** Held that the yangdo dambo creditor acquires ownership but the economic substance is security; upon full repayment, title reverts to the debtor.
- **Supreme Court Decision 2002Da20465 (2004):** Addressed competing yangdo dambo interests, holding that priority is determined by the date of creation (agreement date), not by registration.

### The 2010 Act: Registration-Based Security

**Creation (Articles 3-8):** A security right in movable property (동산담보권) is created by a security agreement (담보약정) between the debtor and creditor, specifying the secured obligation, the collateral, and the maximum secured amount (채권최고액). The collateral must be described with sufficient specificity to be identifiable.

**Registration (Articles 7, 47):** The security right is perfected by registration with the movable property security registration office (동산담보등기소) maintained by the Supreme Court's electronic registry system. Registration is notice-based, similar to UCC Article 9 filing.

**Priority (Article 7(1)):** Among registered security rights, priority is determined by the order of registration (선등기 우선의 원칙). A registered security right takes priority over an unregistered yangdo dambo created after the registration date.

**After-Acquired Property (Article 3(3)):** The Act permits security over after-acquired movable property, a significant innovation for inventory financing and revolving credit facilities.

**Enforcement (Articles 21-27):** The secured creditor may enforce through judicial sale (경매) or, if agreed in the security agreement, through private disposition. Article 21 provides for the right of direct appropriation (직접변제충당) where the parties have agreed and the fair value of the collateral does not exceed 150% of the secured obligation.

### Security over Claims (채권담보)

The 2010 Act also provides for registration-based security over claims (receivables). Previously, security assignment of claims was possible under Civil Code Article 449 (assignment of claims), but lacked a public registration mechanism. Under the Act, a security right in claims is created by agreement and perfected by registration, providing certainty of priority and enabling receivables-based financing.

## United States

### Legal Framework

UCC Article 9, as revised in 2001 (with subsequent amendments in 2010), provides the most comprehensive secured transactions framework among the studied jurisdictions. It applies a unitary concept of "security interest" (§ 1-201(b)(35)) to virtually all consensual liens on personal property, regardless of the form of the transaction.

**Scope (§ 9-109):** Article 9 applies to any transaction, regardless of form, that creates a security interest in personal property or fixtures by contract, including sales of accounts, chattel paper, payment intangibles, and promissory notes, as well as agricultural liens, consignments, and certain lease transactions that are security interests in substance.

### Attachment and Perfection

**Attachment (§ 9-203):** A security interest attaches (becomes enforceable against the debtor) when: value has been given, the debtor has rights in the collateral, and the debtor has authenticated a security agreement that provides a description of the collateral (or the secured party has possession or control of the collateral).

**Perfection Methods:**

| Method | Applicable Collateral | UCC Provision |
|--------|----------------------|---------------|
| Filing (financing statement) | Most types of collateral | § 9-310 |
| Possession | Goods, instruments, money, negotiable documents, tangible chattel paper | § 9-313 |
| Control | Deposit accounts, electronic chattel paper, investment property, letter-of-credit rights | § 9-314 |
| Automatic perfection | Purchase-money security interest in consumer goods; assignment of certain receivables | § 9-309 |

**Financing Statement (§ 9-502):** A financing statement (UCC-1) need only contain the name of the debtor, the name of the secured party, and an indication of the collateral. The financing statement is filed with the designated state filing office (typically the Secretary of State). Filing is effective for five years and may be continued by filing a continuation statement (§ 9-515).

### Priority Rules

**General Rule (§ 9-322(a)):** Among perfected security interests, priority is determined by the first to file or perfect. Among unperfected security interests, the first to attach has priority. A perfected security interest has priority over an unperfected security interest.

**Purchase-Money Security Interest (PMSI) (§ 9-324):** A PMSI in goods other than inventory has priority over a conflicting security interest if the PMSI is perfected within 20 days after the debtor receives possession. A PMSI in inventory has priority if the purchase-money secured party files before the debtor receives the inventory and sends authenticated notification to holders of conflicting security interests.

**Buyers in Ordinary Course (§ 9-320(a)):** A buyer in ordinary course of business takes free of a security interest created by the buyer's seller, even if the security interest is perfected and the buyer knows of its existence.

### Enforcement (Part 6)

**Self-Help Repossession (§ 9-609):** After default, the secured party may take possession of the collateral without judicial process, provided this can be done without breach of the peace. This extrajudicial remedy is a distinctive feature of American law.

**Disposition (§ 9-610):** The secured party may sell, lease, license, or otherwise dispose of the collateral in a commercially reasonable manner. The debtor is entitled to any surplus; the debtor is liable for any deficiency (§§ 9-615, 9-608).

**Strict Foreclosure (§ 9-620):** The secured party may propose to retain the collateral in full or partial satisfaction of the obligation, subject to the debtor's right to object.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany lacks a unitary secured transactions law. Security interests in movable property are created through multiple legal devices, each with distinct rules for creation, perfection, and priority.

**Pfandrecht (Possessory Pledge, BGB §§ 1204-1259):** The Pfandrecht is the only statutory security interest in movables. It requires: an agreement between pledgor and pledgee, delivery of the thing to the pledgee (§ 1205), and an existing secured claim (Akzessorietät -- accessory nature). Because the Pfandrecht requires dispossession, it is impractical for most commercial financing and has been largely supplanted in practice by the Sicherungsübereignung.

**Sicherungsübereignung (Security Transfer of Ownership):** The dominant non-possessory security device in German law, developed through case law rather than statute. The Sicherungsübereignung is constructed using the general property transfer rules of BGB §§ 929-931:

- **BGB § 929:** Transfer of ownership requires agreement (Einigung) and delivery (Übergabe).
- **BGB § 930 (Constitutum Possessorium):** Delivery may be replaced by an agreement that the transferor continues to hold the thing on behalf of the transferee. This enables the debtor to transfer ownership to the creditor while retaining physical possession.
- **Security Agreement (Sicherungsvertrag):** The internal agreement between debtor and creditor specifying the secured obligation, the conditions for enforcement, and the debtor's right to retransfer upon satisfaction of the debt. The security agreement is separate from the property transfer (Trennungsprinzip and Abstraktionsprinzip).

**Sicherungszession (Security Assignment of Claims, BGB §§ 398ff.):** Claims (Forderungen) may be assigned to a creditor as security. The assignment is effective upon agreement between the assignor and assignee (§ 398); no consent or notification of the debtor of the assigned claim is required for effectiveness (though notification affects the debtor's right to discharge by paying the original creditor). The Sicherungszession is governed by a separate security agreement analogous to that used for Sicherungsübereignung.

### Absence of Registration

A fundamental characteristic of German law is the absence of a general public registration system for security interests in movable property. The Sicherungsübereignung is "invisible" to third parties because the debtor retains possession and no registry records the security transfer. This creates significant problems:

**Priority Disputes:** Priority among competing Sicherungsübereignungen is determined by the date of creation (Zeitpunkt der Eigentumsübertragung), which is difficult to prove. In practice, the first creditor to obtain a valid Sicherungsübereignung has priority, but the lack of publicity means that subsequent creditors may be unaware of prior security interests.

**Bona Fide Acquisition (§ 932 BGB):** A third party who acquires the movable in good faith from the possessing debtor may obtain ownership free of the security interest, because the debtor's possession creates an appearance of unencumbered ownership. This risk to the secured creditor is an inherent weakness of the German system.

### Extended and Enlarged Security

German practice has developed sophisticated extensions of the basic security devices:

| Device | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| Raumsicherungsübereignung | Security transfer of all movables within a defined space (warehouse, factory floor) |
| Globalzession | Assignment of all present and future receivables from specified business relationships |
| Verlängerter Eigentumsvorbehalt | Extended retention of title covering proceeds from resale of goods |
| Erweiterter Eigentumsvorbehalt | Enlarged retention of title securing all claims between the parties (Kontokorrentvorbehalt) |

### BGH Case Law

- **BGH, Urteil vom 30.01.1997 -- IX ZR 89/96:** Established that a Sicherungsübereignung of future goods (Vorausabtretung) is effective upon the debtor's acquisition of the goods, but may be defeated by a retention of title seller's prior claim.
- **BGH, Urteil vom 09.11.1978 -- VII ZR 54/77:** Addressed the conflict between Globalzession and extended retention of title (verlängerter Eigentumsvorbehalt), holding that the retention of title supplier's extended claim to proceeds prevails over a prior Globalzession if the Globalzession agreement excludes claims arising under retention of title.
- **BGH, Urteil vom 20.03.1997 -- IX ZR 71/96:** Held that a Sicherungsübereignung of inventory (Warenlager) must describe the collateral with sufficient determinacy (Bestimmtheit) to be effective.

## ECHR & EU

### UNCITRAL Model Law on Secured Transactions (2016)

The UNCITRAL Model Law, adopted in 2016, provides a comprehensive framework for secured transactions law reform based on the UNCITRAL Legislative Guide on Secured Transactions (2007). Key features relevant to the studied jurisdictions:

**Unitary Security Right (Article 2):** The Model Law adopts a unitary concept of security right, functionally defined as any right in movable property created by agreement to secure payment or other performance of an obligation, regardless of the form of the transaction or the terminology used. This approach mirrors UCC Article 9 and contrasts with the German multiple-device system.

**Registration (Articles 18-28):** The Model Law recommends a general security rights registry based on notice filing. The filing of a notice is sufficient for perfection; the filed notice need not describe the collateral in detail. The registry is searchable by debtor name, enabling prospective creditors to discover existing security rights.

**Priority (Articles 29-43):** Among registered security interests, priority is based on the order of registration. Acquisition financing security rights (equivalent to UCC PMSI) enjoy super-priority if registered within a specified short period.

### DCFR Book IX: Proprietary Security in Movable Assets

The Draft Common Frame of Reference (DCFR) Book IX, developed by the Study Group on a European Civil Code, proposes a comprehensive European framework for security interests in movable property. Key elements:

- **Unitary Approach (IX.-1:102):** A single category of proprietary security right, whether created by retention of ownership (including retention of title and financial leasing) or by grant of security right (including pledge, charge, and security transfer).
- **Registration (IX.-3:301ff.):** A European register for security rights in movable property, accessible electronically.
- **Enforcement (IX.-7:101ff.):** Extrajudicial enforcement permitted, including appropriation and sale, subject to debtor protections.

### Cape Town Convention (2001)

The Convention on International Interests in Mobile Equipment (Cape Town Convention), together with its Protocols, establishes an international regime for the creation, registration, and enforcement of security interests in high-value mobile equipment:

| Protocol | Asset Category | Status |
|----------|---------------|--------|
| Aircraft Protocol (2001) | Aircraft objects (airframes, engines, helicopters) | In force; 82 states parties (including South Korea, Germany, US) |
| Luxembourg Rail Protocol (2007) | Railway rolling stock | In force 2023 |
| Space Assets Protocol (2012) | Space assets | Not yet in force |

**International Registry:** The Cape Town Convention establishes an International Registry for each asset category, providing a public, searchable, electronic registration system. Registration establishes priority (first-to-register rule). The International Registry for aircraft objects, maintained by Aviareto (Dublin), has been operational since 2006.

### EU Financial Collateral Directive (2002/47/EC)

Directive 2002/47/EC on financial collateral arrangements simplifies the creation, perfection, and enforcement of security interests in financial collateral (cash and financial instruments) between specified financial market participants. The Directive eliminates formal requirements (such as registration, notarization, and notification) for the creation and perfection of financial collateral arrangements, permits close-out netting and appropriation of collateral, and disapplies certain insolvency provisions that would otherwise restrict enforcement.

### EU Insolvency Regulation (2015/848)

Regulation (EU) 2015/848 on insolvency proceedings addresses the cross-border effects of security interests in insolvency. Article 8 provides that the opening of insolvency proceedings does not affect the rights in rem of creditors in respect of assets situated in another member state at the time of opening. This lex rei sitae rule preserves the priority of security interests created under the law of the state where the assets are located, even in cross-border insolvencies.

## Comparative Analysis

### Unitary vs. Multiple Security Devices

The most fundamental structural difference is between the American unitary approach (single "security interest" concept) and the German multiple-device system (Pfandrecht, Sicherungsübereignung, Sicherungszession). South Korea occupies a middle position: the 2010 Act introduced a unitary registration-based security right for movables and claims, but the yangdo dambo and Civil Code pledge remain available, creating a hybrid system. The international trend, reflected in the UNCITRAL Model Law and DCFR Book IX, strongly favors the unitary functional approach.

### Registration vs. Publicity Gap

The American system achieves publicity through the UCC filing system, which provides a searchable, low-cost registry. South Korea's 2010 Act similarly provides an electronic registry. Germany's lack of a movable property security register is widely recognized as a significant weakness, creating uncertainty for subsequent creditors and complicating priority disputes. The UNCITRAL Model Law, DCFR Book IX, and Cape Town Convention all mandate or recommend registration-based systems.

### Self-Help vs. Judicial Enforcement

The American self-help repossession right (§ 9-609) contrasts sharply with the German requirement of judicial enforcement. South Korea permits private enforcement only where agreed and subject to value limits. The UNCITRAL Model Law and DCFR Book IX recommend permitting extrajudicial enforcement, subject to debtor protections, reflecting a trend toward efficiency in enforcement.

### After-Acquired Property and Floating Security

UCC Article 9 freely permits security interests in after-acquired property and floating liens on changing collateral (such as inventory). The German Sicherungsübereignung of a defined space (Raumsicherungsübereignung) achieves a similar practical result for inventory, but through a different conceptual mechanism (transfer of ownership of all items within the space). South Korea's 2010 Act expressly permits after-acquired property clauses. The harmonization efforts uniformly endorse the permissibility of security over after-acquired and future assets.

### Cross-Border Challenges

Cross-border secured transactions face the fundamental challenge of determining which law governs the creation, perfection, and priority of security interests in movable property that crosses borders. The EU Insolvency Regulation's lex rei sitae rule, the Cape Town Convention's international registration system, and the UNCITRAL Model Law's provisions on applicable law represent different approaches to this challenge. The absence of a general EU-wide movable property security register remains a significant gap.
