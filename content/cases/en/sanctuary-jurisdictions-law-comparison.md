## Summary

Sanctuary jurisdictions -- subnational government entities that limit their cooperation with national immigration enforcement authorities, typically by restricting local law enforcement from inquiring about immigration status, declining to honor immigration detainer requests, or refusing to share immigration-related information with federal agencies -- represent a distinctive phenomenon at the intersection of immigration law, federalism, and local governance. South Korea does not have a formal sanctuary city movement, as immigration enforcement is centralized under the Ministry of Justice's Korea Immigration Service (출입국관리사무소), with no subnational government authority over immigration matters, though some local governments have adopted "multicultural city" (다문화도시) declarations providing social services to foreign residents regardless of immigration status and certain municipalities have informally limited cooperation with immigration raids in markets and residential areas with large immigrant populations. The United States is the global center of the sanctuary jurisdiction phenomenon, with over 200 state and local jurisdictions adopting some form of sanctuary policy, ranging from California's landmark Senate Bill 54 (California Values Act, 2017, codified at Cal. Gov. Code §§ 7284-7284.12) to executive orders by cities including New York, Chicago, Philadelphia, and San Francisco, with federal courts addressing the constitutional limits of federal compulsion of local cooperation under the Tenth Amendment's anti-commandeering doctrine, as articulated in Printz v. United States, 521 U.S. 898 (1997), and the Spending Clause limitations on conditional federal funding. Germany does not have sanctuary cities in the American sense, as immigration enforcement is a shared federal-state (Länder) competence, but some municipalities have joined the "Seebrücke" (Sea Bridge) movement declaring willingness to accept additional refugees beyond federal allocation quotas, and the Kirchenasyl (church asylum) tradition provides informal sanctuary within religious institutions, while at the EU level, mandatory solidarity mechanisms under the New Pact on Migration and Asylum (2024) and the ECHR's non-refoulement obligations create a supranational framework within which subnational resistance to immigration enforcement must operate.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's unitary government structure does not provide for subnational autonomy in immigration enforcement. All immigration authority is vested in the central government under the Immigration Act (출입국관리법), and local governments have no independent immigration enforcement power.

**Multicultural Family Support Act (다문화가족지원법, 2008):** While not a sanctuary policy, this Act represents the closest Korean analogue. It requires local governments to provide support services to multicultural families (다문화가정), defined as families with at least one member who obtained Korean nationality through marriage or naturalization:

| Service | Provision |
|---------|-----------|
| Korean language education | Free Korean classes for marriage immigrants |
| Counseling services | Family, employment, and legal counseling |
| Childcare support | Bilingual education; multicultural understanding programs |
| Employment training | Vocational training; job placement assistance |
| Domestic violence protection | Specialized shelters and hotlines |

**Local Government Initiatives:**
Several municipalities have adopted inclusive policies for foreign residents:
- **Ansan (안산시):** Designated "International Culture Special Zone" in Wongok-dong; provides social services to foreign residents regardless of status
- **Seoul:** Operates Global Village Centers (글로벌빌리지센터) providing language, legal, and social services
- **Gimhae (김해시):** Adopted multicultural city ordinance providing comprehensive support programs

### Informal Non-Cooperation

While no Korean city formally refuses to cooperate with immigration enforcement, informal practices exist:
- Some municipalities have declined to report undocumented residents seeking social services or emergency medical care
- Labor union advocacy organizations have negotiated informal moratoriums on immigration raids during labor dispute proceedings
- The National Human Rights Commission has recommended that immigration enforcement not be conducted at hospitals, schools, or shelters

### Constitutional Limits

Under the Korean Constitution and the Local Autonomy Act (지방자치법), local governments have self-governing authority in local affairs, but immigration control is exclusively a national government function. Any formal sanctuary policy would likely be struck down as ultra vires under the current constitutional framework.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The sanctuary jurisdiction movement in the United States is rooted in constitutional federalism principles and has generated extensive litigation.

**Anti-Commandeering Doctrine:**

The Tenth Amendment provides: "The powers not delegated to the United States by the Constitution, nor prohibited by it to the States, are reserved to the States respectively, or to the people."

**Printz v. United States, 521 U.S. 898 (1997):** The Supreme Court held that the federal government may not "commandeer" state or local officials to administer or enforce federal regulatory programs. This decision provides the constitutional foundation for sanctuary policies: the federal government cannot compel state or local law enforcement to enforce immigration law.

**Murphy v. National Collegiate Athletic Association, 584 U.S. 453 (2018):** The Court extended the anti-commandeering doctrine, holding that Congress may not issue direct orders to state legislatures, even negative commands prohibiting states from taking certain actions.

### Key State Legislation

**California SB 54 (California Values Act, 2017, Cal. Gov. Code §§ 7284-7284.12):**

| Provision | Effect |
|-----------|--------|
| § 7284.6(a) | State and local law enforcement agencies shall not use money or personnel to investigate, interrogate, detain, detect, or arrest persons for immigration enforcement |
| § 7284.6(b) | Prohibits responding to ICE detainer requests or notification requests (with exceptions) |
| § 7284.6(c) | Prohibits providing personal information about individuals for immigration enforcement |
| § 7284.4(a) | Exceptions: convicted of certain serious felonies; subject to federal criminal warrant |
| § 7284.10 | Attorney General to publish model policies |

**Illinois TRUST Act (2017):** Prohibits state and local law enforcement from detaining individuals solely on the basis of an immigration detainer.

**Oregon Revised Statutes § 181A.820:** Oregon's 1987 sanctuary law -- the first state-level sanctuary statute -- prohibits state and local resources from being used to detect or apprehend persons whose only violation is federal immigration law.

### ICE Detainer Issues

ICE immigration detainers (Form I-247A) are requests to state and local law enforcement agencies to hold individuals for up to 48 hours beyond their release date to allow ICE to take custody. Multiple federal courts have addressed their legal status:

**Galarza v. Szalczyk, 745 F.3d 634 (3d Cir. 2014):** ICE detainers are requests, not commands; local law enforcement has no obligation to comply.

**Miranda-Olivares v. Clackamas County, No. 3:12-cv-02317 (D. Or. 2014):** Holding individuals on ICE detainers without probable cause violates the Fourth Amendment.

**Morales v. Chadbourne, 793 F.3d 208 (1st Cir. 2015):** Continued detention on an ICE detainer after the basis for state custody has ended constitutes a new seizure requiring probable cause.

### Federal Funding Battles

The federal government has attempted to leverage grant funding to compel cooperation:

**Executive Order 13768 (January 25, 2017):** Directed DHS and DOJ to deny federal grants to sanctuary jurisdictions. Multiple federal courts blocked implementation.

**City of Chicago v. Sessions, 321 F. Supp. 3d 855 (N.D. Ill. 2018):** DOJ conditions requiring cooperation with ICE as a prerequisite for Byrne JAG grants exceeded DOJ authority and violated the Spending Clause.

**City and County of San Francisco v. Trump, 897 F.3d 1225 (9th Cir. 2018):** The court struck down the executive order's broad defunding provisions as exceeding executive authority under the Spending Clause (only Congress can attach conditions to federal spending).

**United States v. California, 921 F.3d 865 (9th Cir. 2019):** The Ninth Circuit largely upheld California SB 54, finding that the state was not obstructing federal enforcement but simply declining to participate. However, the court struck down a provision restricting private employers from voluntarily cooperating with ICE as preempted by federal law.

### 2025 Federal Enforcement

The Trump administration (2025) has escalated federal enforcement in sanctuary jurisdictions:
- Executive orders directing federal agencies to identify and defund sanctuary jurisdictions
- Deployment of federal law enforcement operations in sanctuary cities
- Criminal referrals for local officials alleged to be "harboring" undocumented immigrants
- DOJ lawsuits against sanctuary policies under the theory of federal preemption
- Multiple ongoing federal court challenges to these enforcement actions

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's federal structure assigns immigration enforcement to the Länder (states) as a matter of concurrent legislation (Article 74(1)(4) GG), with the federal government setting the legal framework and the Länder implementing it through local Ausländerbehörden (foreigners' authorities).

**Seebrücke (Sea Bridge) Movement:** Launched in 2018 in response to the Mediterranean refugee crisis, the Seebrücke movement has seen over 250 German municipalities declare themselves "safe harbors" (sichere Häfen) willing to accept additional refugees beyond their allocation quotas:

| City | Declaration Year | Key Commitments |
|------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Berlin | 2018 | Declared safe harbor; established state-level reception program |
| Potsdam | 2018 | Voluntary additional refugee acceptance |
| Düsseldorf | 2019 | Safe harbor declaration; integration programs |
| Freiburg | 2018 | Safe harbor; additional resettlement commitments |

These declarations are primarily symbolic, as the actual distribution of refugees is governed by the Königstein Key (Königsteiner Schlüssel), a formula based on state tax revenue and population.

**Kirchenasyl (Church Asylum):** A tradition dating to the medieval period, Kirchenasyl involves religious communities providing shelter to individuals facing deportation. The practice operates in a legal grey zone:
- No statutory legal basis; relies on long-standing convention
- Agreement between churches and BAMF (Bundesamt für Migration und Flüchtlinge) since 2015: churches notify BAMF of asylum cases within their walls
- BAMF reviews the case and may reconsider the deportation decision
- Approximately 1,000-1,500 persons in Kirchenasyl annually
- Police have generally refrained from entering churches to enforce deportation orders, though this restraint has no legal basis and some Länder interior ministers have threatened to end the practice

### Länder Discretion in Deportation

While the legal framework for deportation is set by federal law (AufenthG), Länder have discretion in implementation:
- **Abschiebestopp (deportation moratorium):** Länder interior ministers may suspend deportations to specific countries based on safety assessments (e.g., moratoriums on deportations to Afghanistan, Syria at various times)
- **Duldung (toleration):** Länder authorities issue Duldung status when deportation is temporarily suspended
- **Hardship commissions (Härtefallkommissionen):** Each Land has a commission that can recommend residence permits for exceptional hardship cases

### Political Dynamics

The tension between federal deportation policy and local implementation has intensified:
- The federal government has sought to centralize deportation implementation through the Rückführungsverbesserungsgesetz (Return Improvement Act) of 2024
- Some Länder (notably Green-governed states) have been more reluctant to execute deportations
- The AfD has criticized perceived non-enforcement by certain Länder as a form of sanctuary policy

## ECHR / EU

### EU Framework

The EU does not have a direct equivalent of the U.S. sanctuary city phenomenon, but analogous tensions exist between EU-level immigration policy and member state implementation.

**New Pact on Migration and Asylum (2024):**
The comprehensive reform package includes mandatory solidarity mechanisms:
- Regulation (EU) 2024/1351 (Asylum and Migration Management Regulation): mandatory solidarity contributions from all member states (relocations, financial contributions, or operational support)
- Regulation (EU) 2024/1348 (Asylum Procedure Regulation): accelerated border procedures for certain asylum claims
- Regulation (EU) 2024/1346 (Reception Conditions Directive recast)
- Member states that refuse to participate in mandatory solidarity face financial consequences (€20,000 per person not relocated)

**Article 258 TFEU (Infringement Proceedings):** The Commission may bring infringement proceedings against member states that fail to implement EU immigration law. In 2020, the CJEU ruled in Joined Cases C-715/17, C-718/17, and C-719/17 (Commission v. Poland, Hungary, Czech Republic) that these states' refusal to participate in the 2015 Emergency Relocation Decisions violated EU law.

### ECHR Considerations

The ECHR does not directly address sanctuary policies but establishes relevant principles:

**Article 3 (Non-Refoulement):** States may not remove individuals to countries where they face a real risk of torture or inhuman/degrading treatment. Local authorities implementing deportation orders bear responsibility under the Convention.

**Article 8 (Family and Private Life):** Deportation decisions must respect family unity and proportionality, potentially supporting arguments for non-enforcement in cases with strong family ties.

**M.S.S. v. Belgium and Greece (Application No. 30696/09, Grand Chamber, January 21, 2011):** The Court found violations by both Belgium (for transferring an asylum seeker to Greece under Dublin II) and Greece (for detention conditions and asylum procedure deficiencies), establishing that EU member states cannot blindly apply EU rules when doing so would result in Convention violations.

### Comparative Municipal Solidarity

Beyond Germany's Seebrücke movement, other European municipal solidarity initiatives include:
- **Italy:** Some mayors (notably Catania, Riace before judicial proceedings) defied national anti-immigration policies
- **Spain:** Barcelona declared itself a "refuge city" (ciutat refugi) in 2015
- **United Kingdom:** City of Sanctuary network involving over 120 groups promoting welcoming communities

## Comparative Analysis

### Structural Determinants

The existence and form of sanctuary policies is determined by constitutional structure:

**Federal systems with subnational police power (United States):** Most conducive to sanctuary policies; local police are not agents of the federal government; anti-commandeering doctrine prevents federal commandeering

**Federal systems with shared immigration competence (Germany):** Länder have implementation discretion but not policy-making autonomy; sanctuary-like effects through enforcement discretion

**Unitary systems (South Korea):** Least conducive; immigration enforcement is centralized; no constitutional basis for local non-cooperation

### Legal vs. Practical Sanctuary

The distinction between legal sanctuary (statutory non-cooperation) and practical sanctuary (informal non-enforcement) is critical:
- **United States:** Both legal (SB 54) and practical (local police policies) sanctuary
- **Germany:** Primarily practical (Kirchenasyl; Länder discretion; Seebrücke as political declaration)
- **South Korea:** Limited practical sanctuary through informal service provision to undocumented residents

### Federal Government Responses

Federal responses to sanctuary policies follow predictable patterns:
1. **Funding leverage:** Threatening withdrawal of federal grants (United States)
2. **Direct enforcement:** Deploying federal agents for enforcement operations (United States, 2025)
3. **Legal challenges:** Preemption lawsuits (United States); infringement proceedings (EU)
4. **Legislative override:** Centralizing enforcement authority (Germany's 2024 Return Improvement Act)

### Public Safety Arguments

The public safety implications of sanctuary policies are intensely debated:

**Arguments Supporting Sanctuary Policies:**
- Immigrant community cooperation with local police increases when fear of immigration enforcement is reduced
- Multiple studies (including Tom K. Wong, UC San Diego, 2017) found sanctuary counties have lower crime rates than comparable non-sanctuary counties
- Victims and witnesses of crime are more likely to report to police when immigration consequences are not feared
- Local police resources are preserved for local law enforcement rather than federal immigration enforcement

**Arguments Opposing Sanctuary Policies:**
- Releasing individuals with immigration detainers may result in individuals committing subsequent crimes
- Federal-local law enforcement cooperation is essential for public safety
- Non-cooperation with federal authorities undermines the rule of law
- High-profile cases (such as the 2015 Steinle shooting in San Francisco) have been used to argue sanctuary policies endanger public safety

### Sanctuary in Education and Healthcare

Beyond law enforcement, sanctuary principles extend to other institutional contexts:

| Institution | Sanctuary Practice | Legal Basis |
|------------|-------------------|-------------|
| Public schools | No immigration status inquiry; enrollment regardless of status | Plyler v. Doe, 457 U.S. 202 (1982) (US); universal education rights |
| Hospitals | Emergency treatment regardless of status; no ICE access policies | EMTALA (US); constitutional right to life (Korea, Germany) |
| Courts | ICE enforcement restricted in courthouses (some jurisdictions) | Judicial policy; due process concerns |
| Religious institutions | Kirchenasyl (Germany); sanctuary movement (US) | Traditional practice; First Amendment (US) |

### Anti-Sanctuary Legislation

Some U.S. states have enacted anti-sanctuary legislation prohibiting local sanctuary policies:
- **Texas SB 4 (2017):** Required local officials to comply with ICE detainers; imposed criminal penalties on officials who adopted sanctuary policies; largely upheld by the Fifth Circuit
- **Florida SB 168 (2019):** Required state and local law enforcement to honor ICE detainers
- **Georgia HB 452 (2024):** Prohibited local governments from adopting sanctuary policies
- These state-level anti-sanctuary laws create a three-level conflict: federal-state-local, with conflicting mandates at each level

### Data Sharing and Privacy Implications

Sanctuary policies intersect with data privacy and information sharing frameworks:

- **United States:** 8 U.S.C. § 1373 prohibits state and local governments from restricting communication of immigration status information to federal authorities; sanctuary jurisdictions argue that § 1373 does not compel affirmative data collection or detainer compliance; the DOJ has conditioned federal grants (Byrne JAG) on immigration cooperation
- **EU:** GDPR and law enforcement data protection rules (LED Directive 2016/680) constrain information sharing between immigration authorities and local service providers; the principle of purpose limitation prevents data collected for education or healthcare purposes from being used for immigration enforcement
- **South Korea:** Personal Information Protection Act (개인정보 보호법) governs data sharing between government agencies; Multicultural Family Support Centers operate independently from immigration enforcement to encourage service access by irregular migrants
