## Summary

Mediation -- the process by which a neutral third party assists disputing parties in reaching a mutually acceptable resolution without imposing a binding decision -- has evolved from an informal practice to a regulated feature of civil justice systems worldwide, driven by concerns over court congestion, litigation costs, and the recognition that consensual dispute resolution may better preserve relationships and produce more durable outcomes than adversarial adjudication. South Korea has developed a comprehensive mediation framework encompassing court-annexed mediation (조정) under the Judicial Conciliation of Civil Disputes Act (민사조정법, Act No. 4202, enacted 1990, amended 2016), mandatory mediation in family disputes under the Family Litigation Act (가사소송법), and sector-specific mediation through specialized agencies including the Korea Medical Dispute Mediation and Arbitration Agency (한국의료분쟁조정중재원) established under the Medical Dispute Mediation Act (의료분쟁조정법, 2011), with court-annexed mediation resolving approximately 60 percent of referred cases in 2023 and the Supreme Court actively promoting mediation through its Judicial Policy Research Institute recommendations and mediation training programs for judges. The United States has the most decentralized and diverse mediation landscape, with no single federal mediation statute but rather a patchwork of federal court mediation programs authorized under the Alternative Dispute Resolution Act of 1998 (ADRA, 28 U.S.C. Sections 651-658), state mediation statutes including the Uniform Mediation Act (UMA, adopted by 12 states and the District of Columbia as of 2024), court-ordered mediation programs in virtually every federal district and state court system, and a robust private mediation market, with significant ongoing debates over mandatory mediation, mediator qualifications, and the enforceability of mediation clauses, as addressed by the Supreme Court's arbitration-adjacent jurisprudence and the growing adoption of the Singapore Convention on Mediation (2019). Germany enacted the Mediation Act (Mediationsgesetz, MediationsG) on July 21, 2012, transposing Directive 2008/52/EC and establishing a comprehensive framework for mediation including confidentiality protections (Section 4), mediator qualifications (Section 5), and the distinction between out-of-court mediation and court-internal mediation conducted by a designated judge (güterichterliche Mediation) under Section 278(5) ZPO, while several German states maintain mandatory conciliation procedures (obligatorische Streitschlichtung) under Section 15a of the Introductory Act to the ZPO (EGZPO) for certain categories of disputes. At the European level, the EU Mediation Directive (Directive 2008/52/EC) established minimum standards for cross-border mediation including enforceability of mediated agreements, while the Singapore Convention on Mediation (United Nations Convention on International Settlement Agreements Resulting from Mediation, 2019) provides for cross-border enforcement of mediated settlement agreements, and the ECHR has addressed the compatibility of mandatory mediation with the right of access to courts under Article 6, with the Court in Momcilovic v. Croatia (2015) holding that mandatory mediation does not per se violate Article 6 provided that access to court is not effectively barred.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's mediation system operates through three principal channels: court-annexed mediation, administrative dispute resolution, and private mediation.

### Court-Annexed Mediation (조정)

**Judicial Conciliation of Civil Disputes Act (민사조정법):** This act, enacted in 1990 and amended multiple times including in 2016, provides the primary framework for court-annexed mediation in civil and commercial disputes.

- **Article 2 (Scope):** The Act applies to civil disputes that may be the subject of civil litigation. Certain matters are excluded, including family disputes (governed by the Family Litigation Act) and labor disputes (governed by labor-specific mediation procedures).
- **Article 3 (Referral):** A party may apply for mediation, or the court may refer a pending case to mediation at any stage of proceedings, either on its own motion or upon a party's application.
- **Article 7 (Mediation Committee):** Mediation is conducted by a mediation committee (조정위원회) consisting of a judge (presiding) and two or more lay mediators (조정위원) selected from a panel of qualified persons.
- **Article 18 (Mediation Hearing):** The mediation committee conducts a hearing at which both parties present their positions. The hearing is informal and not bound by the rules of evidence.
- **Article 26 (Mediation Proposal):** If the parties cannot reach agreement, the mediation committee may propose a resolution (조정안). The parties have 14 days to accept or reject the proposal.
- **Article 28 (Deemed Mediation):** If neither party objects within 14 days, the mediation proposal is deemed accepted and has the same effect as a judicial settlement.
- **Article 29 (Effect of Mediation):** A completed mediation has the same effect as a court judgment (재판상 화해), creating an enforceable title.

### Family Court Mediation

**Family Litigation Act (가사소송법):** Mediation is mandatory in family disputes prior to trial:

- **Article 50 (Mandatory Mediation):** Family cases classified as Category B (이류 가사사건), including divorce, custody, and property division, must undergo mediation before proceeding to adjudication.
- **Article 51 (Mediation Judge):** A family court judge presides over the mediation, assisted by family mediators.
- **Article 59 (Mediation Effect):** Completed family mediation has the same effect as a final judgment.

The mandatory mediation requirement has contributed to a settlement rate exceeding 50 percent in family disputes, with the Seoul Family Court reporting a 58 percent mediation success rate in 2023.

### Mediator Qualifications

Korea does not have unified statutory qualification requirements for mediators. Court-annexed mediation committees include judges and lay mediators, with lay mediators typically drawn from attorneys, retired judges, academics, and subject-matter experts. The Supreme Court maintains panels of qualified mediators and provides training programs through the Judicial Research and Training Institute (법학연수원).

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has the most decentralized mediation system, operating through federal programs, diverse state statutes, court rules, and a robust private mediation market.

### Federal Framework

**Alternative Dispute Resolution Act of 1998 (ADRA, 28 U.S.C. Sections 651-658):**

- **Section 651:** Each federal district court shall authorize the use of ADR processes in all civil actions.
- **Section 652:** Each court shall devise and implement its own ADR program, designating the categories of actions for which ADR is made available or required.
- **Section 653:** Mediators and other neutral third parties must be qualified and trained. Courts must establish procedures for selection.
- **Section 654:** Confidentiality of ADR proceedings must be protected.

### Uniform Mediation Act (UMA)

The UMA, promulgated by the Uniform Law Commission in 2001 and amended in 2003, has been adopted by 12 states and the District of Columbia. Key provisions include:

- **Section 4 (Mediation Privilege):** Mediation communications are privileged and not subject to discovery or admissible in evidence, with limited exceptions including signed agreements, threats of violence, and professional misconduct proceedings.
- **Section 5 (Waiver and Preclusion):** The privilege may be waived by all parties and the mediator.
- **Section 6 (Exceptions):** Exceptions to the privilege include criminal proceedings where mediation communications are sought as evidence of a felony, and proceedings to prove or disprove abuse or neglect.
- **Section 9 (Mediator Disclosure):** The mediator must disclose any known facts that a reasonable individual would consider likely to affect the mediator's impartiality.

### Court-Ordered Mediation

Virtually every federal district court and most state courts operate court-annexed mediation programs. Key features include:

- **Mandatory participation:** Many jurisdictions require parties to participate in mediation before trial, though they cannot compel agreement. The distinction between mandating participation and mandating settlement is constitutionally significant.
- **Timing:** Courts typically order mediation early in the case, often at the Rule 16 case management conference.
- **Good faith participation:** Most court rules require good-faith participation, though defining and enforcing this requirement has proven difficult. In Nick v. Morgan's Foods (W.D. Va. 2001), the court sanctioned a party for failing to participate in mediation in good faith.

### Mediator Qualifications and Ethics

No federal statute prescribes mediator qualifications. State requirements vary widely:

| Jurisdiction | Requirements |
|-------------|-------------|
| Florida | 100+ hours training; mentoring; continuing education (Fla. R. Med. 10.100) |
| California | No statutory requirements for private mediators; court mediators must complete training |
| New York | Court program mediators: 40 hours training; community mediators: 25 hours |
| Virginia | Certified mediators: 20 hours basic training; supervised mediation experience |

### Enforceability of Mediated Agreements

Mediated settlement agreements are generally enforceable as contracts. However, enforceability issues arise regarding:

- **Agreements to mediate:** Courts generally enforce contractual mediation clauses, staying litigation pending mediation. The distinction from arbitration clauses under the FAA has generated litigation.
- **Mediated settlement agreements:** Enforceable through contract law; some states provide expedited judicial confirmation procedures.
- **Singapore Convention:** The United States signed the Singapore Convention on August 7, 2019, but has not yet ratified it as of 2026. Ratification would enable direct enforcement of international mediated settlement agreements.

### Online Dispute Resolution

ODR has expanded rapidly, particularly following COVID-19. Platforms including Tyler Technologies (modria), Matterhorn, and court-developed systems provide online mediation for consumer disputes, small claims, and family matters. The National Center for State Courts has issued guidelines for ODR implementation.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's mediation framework is built on the Mediation Act of 2012 and the court-internal mediation provisions of the ZPO.

### Mediationsgesetz (Mediation Act, 2012)

The Mediationsgesetz, enacted on July 21, 2012, transposed EU Directive 2008/52/EC and established a comprehensive framework for mediation in Germany:

- **Section 1 (Definition):** Mediation is a confidential and structured process in which the parties, with the assistance of one or more mediators, voluntarily and self-responsibly seek to reach an amicable resolution of their conflict. The mediator is an independent and neutral person without decision-making authority who guides the parties through the mediation process.
- **Section 2 (Mediation Procedure):** The mediator ensures that the parties understand the principles and procedure of mediation. The mediator may conduct separate meetings (caucuses) with individual parties if all parties consent. The mediation ends with an agreement or with a declaration by a party or the mediator that the mediation has failed.
- **Section 3 (Mediator's Duties):** The mediator must: (a) treat the parties equally; (b) disclose any circumstances that may affect impartiality; (c) ensure that the parties act on an informed basis; and (d) assist the parties in formulating the agreement.

### Confidentiality (Section 4)

**Section 4(1):** The mediator and persons involved in the administration of the mediation are bound by confidentiality unless the parties release them from this obligation or disclosure is required by law.

**Section 4(2):** The mediator may not be called as a witness regarding facts learned in the mediation, unless all parties consent or disclosure is necessary to implement or enforce the mediated agreement.

### Mediator Qualifications (Section 5)

**Section 5(1):** The mediator must ensure, through appropriate training and regular continuing education, that he or she possesses the theoretical knowledge and practical experience necessary to guide the parties through the mediation competently.

**Section 5(2):** A mediator may use the title "certified mediator" (zertifizierter Mediator) if he or she has completed training that meets the requirements established by statutory regulation.

**Zertifizierte-Mediatoren-Ausbildungsverordnung (ZMediatAusbV, 2016):** This regulation specifies that certified mediator training must comprise at least 120 hours of instruction covering:
- Fundamentals of mediation
- Negotiation and communication techniques
- Conflict management
- Legal framework
- Practical exercises and role plays
- Supervised mediation sessions

### Court-Internal Mediation (Güterichter)

**Section 278(5) ZPO:** The court may refer the parties to a designated judge (Güterichter) who is not responsible for deciding the case and who may use all methods of conflict resolution, including mediation. The Güterichter does not render a decision but facilitates agreement.

This provision creates a unique German institution: a judge who acts as a mediator within the court system but separate from the adjudicating judge. The Güterichter model has been adopted by most German courts and has proven effective in complex commercial and construction disputes.

### Obligatorische Streitschlichtung (Mandatory Conciliation)

**Section 15a EGZPO** authorizes German states (Länder) to require mandatory conciliation before filing certain civil actions:

- **Applicable disputes:** Neighbor disputes, defamation claims, and certain monetary claims up to EUR 750 (threshold varies by state).
- **Implementing states:** Baden-Württemberg, Bavaria, Brandenburg, Hesse, North Rhine-Westphalia, Saarland, Saxony-Anhalt, and Schleswig-Holstein.
- **Procedure:** The conciliation is conducted by a certified conciliation body (Gütestelle). If conciliation fails, the conciliation body issues a certificate of failure, which is a prerequisite for filing the court action.

### Enforceability of Mediated Agreements

Mediated agreements are enforceable as contracts under general contract law. To obtain an enforceable title (vollstreckbarer Titel) without further court proceedings, the parties may:

1. **Notarial deed (Section 794(1)(5) ZPO):** The mediated agreement can be recorded in a notarial deed with immediate enforceability (vollstreckbare Urkunde).
2. **Court-approved settlement (Section 794(1)(1) ZPO):** If the mediation occurs during pending court proceedings, the agreement can be recorded as a court settlement (Prozessvergleich).
3. **Attorney settlement (Section 796a ZPO):** An agreement reached with the participation of attorneys on both sides may be declared enforceable by the court.

### Mediation Statistics

The German mediation market has grown since the enactment of the Mediationsgesetz, though uptake has been slower than anticipated. A 2017 study commissioned by the Federal Ministry of Justice found that approximately 7,500 to 10,000 mediations were conducted annually in Germany, with the highest usage in family law, employment law, and commercial disputes.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Mediation Directive (Directive 2008/52/EC)

The EU Mediation Directive, adopted on May 21, 2008, applies to cross-border disputes in civil and commercial matters (excluding revenue, customs, and administrative matters):

- **Article 3 (Definition):** Mediation is a structured process in which two or more parties to a dispute attempt to reach an agreement with the assistance of a mediator.
- **Article 4 (Quality of Mediation):** Member states shall encourage the development of and adherence to voluntary codes of conduct by mediators and organizations providing mediation services.
- **Article 5 (Recourse to Mediation):** Courts may invite or require parties to use mediation, provided this does not prevent the parties from exercising their right of access to court.
- **Article 6 (Enforceability):** Member states shall ensure that parties can request that a mediated agreement be made enforceable through a court judgment, decision, or authentic instrument.
- **Article 7 (Confidentiality):** Mediators and those involved in mediation administration shall not be compelled to give evidence in civil or commercial proceedings regarding information arising from mediation, except to protect the best interests of children, prevent harm to physical or psychological integrity, or where disclosure is necessary for public policy reasons.
- **Article 8 (Limitation Periods):** Member states shall ensure that limitation and prescription periods do not expire during the mediation process.

### CJEU Jurisprudence on Mandatory Mediation

**Menini and Rampanelli v. Banco Popolare (C-75/16, 2017):** The CJEU held that Article 1 of Directive 2013/11/EU (Consumer ADR Directive) does not preclude national legislation that makes mediation a mandatory prerequisite for filing court proceedings in consumer disputes, provided that:
1. The mediation requirement does not prevent the parties from exercising their right of access to the judicial system.
2. The mediation procedure does not result in a binding decision.
3. The mediation does not cause substantial delay.
4. The limitation periods are suspended during the mediation.

### Singapore Convention on Mediation (2019)

The United Nations Convention on International Settlement Agreements Resulting from Mediation (Singapore Convention), adopted on December 20, 2018, and open for signature from August 7, 2019, provides for direct enforcement of international mediated settlement agreements:

- **Article 1 (Scope):** Applies to international settlement agreements resulting from mediation, concluded in writing, to resolve commercial disputes.
- **Article 3 (General Principles):** Each party to the Convention shall enforce settlement agreements in accordance with its rules of procedure and under the conditions laid down in the Convention.
- **Article 4 (Reliance on Settlement Agreements):** A party relying on a settlement agreement must supply the signed agreement and evidence that the agreement resulted from mediation.
- **Article 5 (Grounds for Refusing):** A competent authority may refuse to grant relief if: the agreement was obtained by fraud or corruption; the mediator seriously breached applicable standards; a party lacked capacity; the agreement is null, void, or incapable of being performed; or the subject matter is not capable of settlement by mediation under the law of the state.

**Ratification Status:** As of 2026, the Singapore Convention has been signed by 57 states and ratified by 14, including Singapore, Saudi Arabia, Belarus, Ecuador, and Fiji. The European Union has not signed or ratified the Convention; the European Commission indicated in 2023 that it was assessing potential EU accession.

### ECHR and Mandatory Mediation

**Momcilovic v. Croatia, Application No. 11839/15, Decision of October 29, 2015:** The Court held that mandatory mediation before court proceedings does not per se violate Article 6(1) ECHR, provided that: (a) the mediation requirement is proportionate to the legitimate aim pursued; (b) the parties retain access to the court system if mediation fails; and (c) the mediation process does not cause unreasonable delay.

**Siginur v. Iceland, Application No. 48283/17, Judgment of May 7, 2020:** The Court reiterated that ADR mechanisms, including mandatory mediation, are compatible with the right of access to court provided they do not impose disproportionate restrictions and leave the substance of the right to a judicial determination intact.

## Comparative Analysis

### Mandatory vs. Voluntary Mediation

The jurisdictions diverge significantly on whether mediation should be mandatory or voluntary. South Korea mandates mediation in family disputes and has extensive court-referred mediation. The United States widely uses court-ordered mediation while maintaining the voluntary principle for agreement. Germany maintains a predominantly voluntary framework with limited mandatory conciliation in specific disputes. The ECHR and CJEU have approved mandatory mediation subject to preservation of access to courts.

### Mediator Qualifications

The most significant regulatory divergence concerns mediator qualifications. Germany's Mediationsgesetz establishes the certified mediator framework with specific training requirements. The U.S. approach is fragmented, with wide variation between jurisdictions. South Korea relies on court-administered panels without unified statutory requirements. The EU Mediation Directive encourages but does not mandate specific qualifications.

### Confidentiality

All jurisdictions recognize the importance of mediation confidentiality, but the scope and exceptions vary. The UMA provides the most comprehensive privilege framework, with detailed exceptions. Germany's MediationsG establishes a straightforward confidentiality duty with witness privilege. South Korea's confidentiality protections are more limited, primarily restricting access to court mediation records. The EU Directive provides minimum confidentiality standards for cross-border mediation.

### Enforceability

The enforceability of mediated agreements reveals important differences. South Korea's court-annexed mediation produces agreements with the force of a court judgment. The United States treats mediated agreements as enforceable contracts, with varying judicial confirmation procedures. Germany offers multiple paths to enforceability, including notarial deed and court approval. The Singapore Convention represents an emerging international framework for cross-border enforcement but has limited adoption.
