## Summary

Disability rights -- the legal protections ensuring that persons with disabilities enjoy equal participation in society, freedom from discrimination, accessibility, and reasonable accommodation -- have undergone a paradigm shift from a medical/charitable model to a social/human rights model over the past several decades, with the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD, 2006) serving as the catalytic international instrument. South Korea enacted the Act on the Prohibition of Discrimination Against Persons with Disabilities, Remedy Against Infringement of Rights, Etc. (장애인차별금지 및 권리구제 등에 관한 법률, commonly known as the Disability Discrimination Act) in 2007, effective April 2008, establishing comprehensive anti-discrimination protections including reasonable accommodation requirements, accessibility standards, and enforcement through the National Human Rights Commission (국가인권위원회), while the Disability Welfare Act (장애인복지법) and the Act on the Employment Promotion and Vocational Rehabilitation of Persons with Disabilities (장애인고용촉진 및 직업재활법) provide welfare services and employment quotas; South Korea ratified the CRPD in 2008 and its Optional Protocol in 2022. The United States enacted the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA, 42 U.S.C. Section 12101) in 1990, a landmark civil rights statute that prohibits disability discrimination in employment (Title I), public services (Title II), and public accommodations (Title III), and requires reasonable accommodations; the ADA was strengthened by the ADA Amendments Act of 2008 (ADAAA), and the Supreme Court has addressed its scope in numerous decisions. Germany protects disability rights through Article 3(3) of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz), which provides that "no person shall be disfavoured because of disability," the Behindertengleichstellungsgesetz (BGG, 2002) establishing accessibility requirements for federal authorities, and the Social Code Book IX (SGB IX) governing rehabilitation and participation, including the five percent employment quota for severe disability; Germany ratified the CRPD in 2009. The European framework encompasses the EU Charter Article 26 (integration of persons with disabilities), the Employment Equality Directive (2000/78/EC) requiring reasonable accommodation in employment, and the European Accessibility Act (Directive 2019/882), which establishes EU-wide accessibility requirements for products and services, while the ECHR has addressed disability rights through Articles 3, 8, and 14.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's disability rights framework comprises multiple statutes:

**Disability Discrimination Act (장애인차별금지법, DDA, 2007):** Prohibits discrimination based on disability in employment, education, provision of goods and services, judicial and administrative proceedings, and political participation. The Act defines disability broadly and includes mental disabilities, intellectual disabilities, and autism spectrum disorders. Key provisions include:

- Prohibition of direct and indirect discrimination (Articles 4-6)
- Duty to provide reasonable accommodation (정당한 편의, Article 4)
- Prohibition of disability harassment (Article 32)
- Accessibility requirements for buildings, transportation, and information

**Disability Welfare Act (장애인복지법):** Establishes the framework for disability welfare services, including disability registration, classification, and benefit programs. South Korea uses a disability registration system where individuals must register their disability to access welfare services. The system recognizes 15 disability categories.

**Employment Promotion Act (장애인고용촉진법):** Mandates disability employment quotas of 3.6 percent for government agencies and 3.1 percent for private employers with 50 or more employees. Non-compliant employers must pay a levy (장애인고용부담금).

### Deinstitutionalization

South Korea has faced significant criticism regarding the institutionalization of persons with disabilities. According to government statistics, approximately 30,000 persons with disabilities reside in residential institutions. The CRPD Committee, in its 2014 Concluding Observations, recommended that South Korea develop a comprehensive deinstitutionalization strategy with clear timelines and benchmarks. The Disability Rights Guarantee Act (장애인 탈시설 지원 등에 관한 법률), introduced in 2021, aims to facilitate the transition from institutional to community-based living.

### Accessibility

The Act on the Promotion of the Convenience of Persons with Disabilities, the Aged, Pregnant Women, Etc. (장애인·노인·임산부 등의 편의증진 보장에 관한 법률) establishes accessibility standards for buildings and facilities. The Act on the Promotion of Accessibility Enhancement for Mobility-Disadvantaged Persons (교통약자의 이동편의 증진법) addresses transportation accessibility. Despite these statutory requirements, compliance remains uneven, particularly in older buildings and rural areas.

## United States

### Legal Framework

**Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA, 1990):** The ADA is one of the most comprehensive civil rights laws for persons with disabilities globally.

| Title | Coverage | Key Requirements |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| Title I | Employment (15+ employees) | Non-discrimination; reasonable accommodation; undue hardship defense |
| Title II | Public services and transportation | Program access; new construction accessibility; transit requirements |
| Title III | Public accommodations | Non-discrimination; auxiliary aids; barrier removal; new construction standards |
| Title IV | Telecommunications | Relay services for hearing/speech-impaired |
| Title V | Miscellaneous | Anti-retaliation; attorney's fees; relationship to other laws |

**ADA Amendments Act of 2008 (ADAAA):** Broadened the definition of disability in response to restrictive Supreme Court interpretations. The ADAAA established that the definition of disability should be construed broadly, that mitigating measures (other than ordinary eyeglasses and contact lenses) should not be considered in determining whether an individual has a disability, and that episodic conditions and conditions in remission may constitute disabilities.

**Rehabilitation Act of 1973:** Section 504 prohibits disability discrimination by recipients of federal financial assistance. Section 503 requires affirmative action by federal contractors.

### Key Supreme Court Decisions

**Olmstead v. L.C., 527 U.S. 581 (1999):** Held that unjustified institutionalization of persons with disabilities constitutes discrimination under Title II of the ADA. The decision established the principle that states must provide community-based services for persons with disabilities when appropriate, when the affected person does not oppose such placement, and when community placement can be reasonably accommodated.

**PGA Tour, Inc. v. Martin, 532 U.S. 661 (2001):** Held that the ADA applies to professional golf tournaments as public accommodations and requires reasonable modifications for disabled competitors unless they would fundamentally alter the nature of the competition.

**Tennessee v. Lane, 541 U.S. 509 (2004):** Upheld Title II of the ADA as valid exercise of Congressional enforcement power under Section 5 of the Fourteenth Amendment, in a case involving courthouse accessibility.

### Web Accessibility

Title III of the ADA has been interpreted by the DOJ and federal courts to encompass website accessibility, though no formal regulations have been promulgated. Numerous lawsuits have been filed against businesses with inaccessible websites, and the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.1 AA standard has emerged as the de facto standard for ADA web compliance.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

**Article 3(3) GG:** Added in 1994, provides that "no person shall be disfavoured because of disability." This constitutional protection has been interpreted by the Federal Constitutional Court as prohibiting both direct discrimination and unjustified failure to accommodate.

**Behindertengleichstellungsgesetz (BGG, 2002):** The Federal Disability Equality Act establishes accessibility requirements for federal public authorities, including accessible communication, barrier-free buildings and transportation, sign language recognition, and accessible information technology.

**Social Code Book IX (SGB IX):** Governs rehabilitation and participation of persons with disabilities. Part 1 addresses rehabilitation services. Part 2 (Schwerbehindertenrecht) addresses employment protections for persons with severe disabilities, including the five percent employment quota, special dismissal protection (Sonderkündigungsschutz, Section 168), and the additional paid leave entitlement (five days per year, Section 208).

### Employment Protections

The employment quota system under SGB IX requires employers with 20 or more employees to ensure that five percent of positions are held by persons with severe disabilities (schwerbehinderte Menschen, defined as persons with a degree of disability of 50 or more). The compensatory levy (Ausgleichsabgabe) for non-compliance is:

| Quota Fulfillment | Monthly Levy per Unfilled Position |
|-------------------|-----------------------------------|
| 3% to less than 5% | EUR 140 |
| 2% to less than 3% | EUR 245 |
| Less than 2% | EUR 360 |

Special dismissal protection (Sonderkündigungsschutz) requires employers to obtain prior consent from the Integration Office (Integrationsamt) before dismissing a severely disabled employee.

### Barrierefreiheitsstärkungsgesetz (BFSG, 2021)

The BFSG transposes the EU European Accessibility Act (Directive 2019/882) into German law, establishing accessibility requirements for products (computers, smartphones, ATMs, ticketing machines) and services (e-commerce, banking, telecommunications) provided by the private sector, with requirements taking effect from June 28, 2025.

## ECHR & EU

### UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD, 2006)

The CRPD, adopted by the UN General Assembly on December 13, 2006, is the primary international human rights treaty for disability rights. The EU ratified the CRPD in 2010, making it the first international human rights treaty ratified by the EU as a whole. Key provisions include:

- Article 5: Equality and non-discrimination
- Article 9: Accessibility
- Article 12: Equal recognition before the law (legal capacity)
- Article 19: Living independently and being included in the community
- Article 24: Education (inclusive education)
- Article 27: Work and employment

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Glor v. Switzerland (Application No. 13444/04, 2009):** The Court held that Switzerland's requirement that an individual exempted from military service due to a minor disability pay a military service exemption tax, while exempting individuals with more severe disabilities, violated Article 14 (non-discrimination) read with Article 8 (private life).

**Alajos Kiss v. Hungary (Application No. 38832/06, 2010):** The Court held that Hungary's automatic disenfranchisement of all persons under partial guardianship violated Article 3 of Protocol 1 (right to free elections), establishing that blanket restrictions on voting rights based on disability are disproportionate.

**Çam v. Turkey (Application No. 51500/08, 2016):** The Court held that Turkey's refusal to enroll a blind student in a music academy, without considering possible accommodations, violated Article 14 read with Article 2 of Protocol 1 (right to education), recognizing that failure to provide reasonable accommodation constitutes discrimination.

### EU Accessibility Act (Directive 2019/882)

The European Accessibility Act, adopted on April 17, 2019, with transposition deadline of June 28, 2022, and requirements applicable from June 28, 2025, establishes common accessibility requirements for products and services including:

- Computers and operating systems
- ATMs, ticketing and check-in machines
- Smartphones and telecommunications equipment
- E-commerce services
- Banking services
- E-books and e-readers

### Employment Equality Directive (2000/78/EC)

Article 5 of Directive 2000/78/EC requires employers to provide reasonable accommodation for persons with disabilities, defined as appropriate measures to enable access to, participation in, and advancement in employment unless such measures would impose a disproportionate burden.

## Comparative Analysis

### Medical vs. Social Model

The four jurisdictions reflect the global shift from the medical model (disability as individual deficit requiring treatment) to the social model (disability as the result of societal barriers requiring removal). The CRPD embodies the social model, and all four jurisdictions have incorporated social model elements into their legal frameworks, though implementation varies. South Korea's disability registration system retains significant medical model features, requiring individuals to undergo medical assessment and categorization to access services.

### Quota vs. Anti-Discrimination Models

South Korea and Germany use mandatory employment quotas with compensatory levies, while the United States relies on the anti-discrimination and reasonable accommodation framework without quotas. Both approaches have strengths and limitations: quotas provide measurable targets and financial incentives but may lead to tokenistic compliance; anti-discrimination laws provide individual rights but may be difficult to enforce.

### Accessibility

The EU European Accessibility Act and Germany's BFSG represent the most comprehensive private-sector accessibility requirements. The ADA's Title III provides broad public accommodation accessibility but lacks specific digital accessibility regulations. South Korea has enacted accessibility statutes but faces implementation challenges, particularly in older infrastructure.

### Deinstitutionalization

The Olmstead decision in the United States established the strongest legal mandate for deinstitutionalization, requiring community-based services as an alternative to institutional placement. Germany has significantly reduced institutional placement through its system of integration assistance (Eingliederungshilfe) under SGB IX. South Korea faces the greatest deinstitutionalization challenge, with approximately 30,000 persons still residing in institutions.
