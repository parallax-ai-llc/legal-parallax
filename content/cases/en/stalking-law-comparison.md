## Summary

Stalking -- the repeated, unwanted pursuit, surveillance, or harassment of an individual that causes fear for personal safety -- has evolved from a largely unrecognized behavioral pattern to a distinct criminal offense across most legal systems over the past three decades. South Korea enacted the Act on Punishment of Crime of Stalking (스토킹범죄의 처벌 등에 관한 법률) in April 2021, effective October 2021, following more than twenty years of failed legislative attempts; the law was significantly amended in July 2023 to remove a controversial victim-consent requirement for prosecution and to incorporate cyberstalking. The United States was the first jurisdiction to criminalize stalking, beginning with California Penal Code Section 646.9 in 1990, and now addresses stalking through all fifty state criminal codes plus the federal Interstate Stalking Punishment and Prevention Act (18 U.S.C. Section 2261A). Germany introduced the offense of Nachstellung (persistent pursuit) under Section 238 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB) in 2007, reforming it twice -- in 2017 to convert it from a result crime to a suitability crime, and again in 2021 to lower the threshold from "persistent" to "repeated" conduct. At the supranational European level, the Council of Europe's Istanbul Convention (2011) requires states parties to criminalize stalking under Article 34, the European Court of Human Rights has recognized positive obligations on states to protect stalking victims under Articles 3 and 8 of the ECHR, and EU Directive 2024/1385 on combating violence against women and domestic violence explicitly criminalizes cyberstalking with mandatory minimum penalties across all member states, with transposition due by June 2027.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's anti-stalking legislation came into force on October 21, 2021, following over two decades of advocacy and failed bills. The Act on Punishment of Crime of Stalking (스토킹범죄의 처벌 등에 관한 법률) defines stalking as repeated acts directed at a specific person -- or their family, cohabitants, or associates -- against the person's will, including approaching or following, monitoring or waiting at the person's residence, workplace, school, or other commonly frequented locations, sending letters, emails, or other communications, and placing objects at the person's residence or workplace. A companion statute, the Act on Prevention of Stalking and Protection of Victims, establishes victim support infrastructure.

### Penalties

| Offense Category | Penalty |
|-----------------|---------|
| Basic stalking offense | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine up to 30 million KRW (approximately $25,500) |
| Stalking with weapon or dangerous instrument | Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine up to 50 million KRW (approximately $42,500) |
| Violation of court restraining order | Up to 2 years imprisonment |

### 2023 Amendment

The Act underwent its first major amendment on July 11, 2023, driven largely by the Sindang Station murder case. Key changes included:

**Removal of Victim-Consent Clause:** The original law contained a provision prohibiting prosecution against the victim's expressed objection (반의사불벌죄). This clause was eliminated, enabling prosecutors to pursue cases regardless of whether the victim wishes to proceed -- a critical reform given that stalking victims are frequently coerced or intimidated into withdrawing complaints.

**Expanded Definition:** The amendment widened the statutory definition to encompass cyberstalking activities, including the non-consensual exposure of a victim's personal information online with intent to intimidate.

**Enhanced Police Powers:** Law enforcement officers were granted authority to issue emergency restraining orders and take preemptive protective measures, with subsequent court authorization.

### Landmark Case: Sindang Station Murder (2022)

The case that catalyzed reform involved Seoul Metro employee Jeon Joo-hwan, who stalked and ultimately murdered a female colleague at Sindang Station on the Seoul Metropolitan Subway on September 14, 2022. The perpetrator had sent over 350 unwanted text messages over a two-year period and had been reported to police for stalking and illegal filming. Despite the existence of the 2021 anti-stalking law, systemic shortcomings -- including the victim-consent clause and insufficient police follow-up -- failed to prevent the fatal attack. Jeon was convicted and sentenced to 40 years in prison in February 2023. The case exposed critical gaps in enforcement and precipitated the 2023 legislative amendment.

### Enforcement Statistics

Reported stalking cases have surged since the law's enactment, reflecting both increased enforcement capacity and greater victim willingness to report. Cases rose from approximately 7,981 reported incidents as of mid-2024 to 13,269 in full-year 2024 -- a roughly 70 percent increase over two years, according to Korea Times reporting.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States was the first country to criminalize stalking as a distinct offense. The legal framework operates on two levels: state criminal codes and federal statute.

**Origin -- California Penal Code Section 646.9 (1990):** The first anti-stalking statute in the world was developed by Orange County Municipal Court Judge John Watson and introduced with U.S. Representative Ed Royce in 1990. The law was a direct response to two high-profile cases: the 1982 stabbing of actress Theresa Saldana and the 1989 murder of actress Rebecca Schaeffer by obsessed fans. The statute was revolutionary because it criminalized the pattern of behavior itself rather than requiring individual acts of assault or threat to be prosecuted in isolation. Within three years of California's enactment, all 50 states had adopted anti-stalking statutes under varying nomenclature, including criminal harassment and criminal menace.

**Federal Law -- 18 U.S.C. Section 2261A:** Congress enacted the first federal stalking statute in 1996 as part of the National Defense Authorization Act. The statute criminalizes traveling in interstate or foreign commerce, or within the special maritime and territorial jurisdiction of the United States, with the intent to kill, injure, harass, intimidate, or place under surveillance with intent to harm another person. It also covers the use of mail, interactive computer services, electronic communication services, or other facilities of interstate or foreign commerce to engage in a course of conduct that causes, attempts to cause, or would reasonably be expected to cause substantial emotional distress or reasonable fear of death or serious bodily injury.

### Penalties

| Offense Level | Federal Penalty (18 U.S.C. Section 2261A) |
|---------------|-------------------------------------------|
| Basic stalking | Up to 5 years imprisonment and/or fine up to $250,000 |
| Stalking resulting in serious bodily injury or involving dangerous weapon | Up to 10 years imprisonment |
| Stalking resulting in permanent disfigurement or life-threatening bodily injury | Up to 20 years imprisonment |
| Stalking resulting in death of victim | Life imprisonment possible under general federal sentencing provisions |

State penalties vary substantially. California Penal Code Section 646.9 provides for up to one year in county jail (misdemeanor) or up to five years in state prison (felony), with enhanced penalties when the victim has an active restraining order.

### Related Federal Provisions

| Provision | Effect |
|-----------|--------|
| 18 U.S.C. Section 2261 -- Interstate domestic violence | Criminalizes crossing state lines to commit domestic violence |
| 18 U.S.C. Section 2262 -- Interstate violation of protection order | Federal offense to cross state lines and violate a protection order |
| 18 U.S.C. Section 922(g)(8) -- Firearms prohibition | Prohibits firearm possession by persons subject to stalking-related restraining orders |
| Driver's Privacy Protection Act (DPPA), 18 U.S.C. Section 2721 | Enacted 1994; restricts release of personal information from motor vehicle records, prompted by stalking cases |
| VAWA Reauthorization 2022 | Expanded tribal jurisdiction; new programs addressing cyberstalking and technology-facilitated abuse |

### Institutional Innovations

The Los Angeles Police Department founded the first Threat Management Unit in the United States in 1990, the same year California enacted its stalking statute. This unit, established by LAPD Captain Robert Martin, became a model for specialized stalking investigation units nationwide. The National Center for Victims of Crime operates the Stalking Resource Center, providing training and technical assistance to law enforcement, prosecutors, and victim advocates across all jurisdictions.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany criminalized stalking through the introduction of Section 238 StGB (Nachstellung) in March 2007. The provision has undergone two significant reforms -- in 2017 and 2021 -- each aimed at lowering the threshold for criminal liability and improving conviction rates.

**Section 238 StGB -- Nachstellung:** The statute enumerates specific stalking acts directed at another person in an unauthorized manner, including seeking physical proximity, attempting to establish contact through telecommunications or other means of communication, misusing the person's personal data to order goods or services or to cause third parties to make contact, threatening the person or a person close to them with injury to life, bodily integrity, health, or freedom, and committing comparable acts.

### Evolution of Legal Standard

| Reform | Previous Standard | New Standard | Significance |
|--------|-------------------|--------------|--------------|
| 2007 (Original) | N/A | Result crime: victim's lifestyle must be "seriously impaired" in fact | High burden of proof; victims had to demonstrate actual changes to daily life |
| 2017 Reform | Result crime | Suitability crime: conduct need only be "suitable" to cause serious lifestyle impairment | Removed requirement that victim actually change behavior; theoretical capability sufficient |
| 2021 Reform | "Persistent" (beharrlich) + "serious" impairment | "Repeated" (wiederholt) + "not insignificant" impairment | Substantially lowered both frequency and impact thresholds |

### Penalties

| Offense Level | Penalty under Section 238 StGB |
|---------------|-------------------------------|
| Basic offense (Section 238(1)) | Fine or imprisonment up to 3 years |
| Particularly serious cases (Section 238(2)) | Imprisonment from 3 months to 5 years |
| Stalking resulting in victim's death (Section 238(3)) | Imprisonment from 1 year to 10 years |

### Enforcement Challenges

Despite a police investigation rate exceeding 90 percent for reported stalking cases, only approximately 1 percent of cases resulted in criminal convictions prior to the 2021 reform. This stark disparity between investigation and conviction rates was a primary driver of the 2021 legislative changes. The requirement to prove "persistent" conduct and "serious" lifestyle impairment created evidentiary hurdles that prosecutors frequently could not overcome, particularly in cases involving cyberstalking or psychological harassment without physical proximity.

### Civil Law Protections

In addition to criminal prosecution under Section 238 StGB, victims may seek civil protection orders under the Gewaltschutzgesetz (Protection Against Violence Act), enacted in 2002. These orders can prohibit the perpetrator from approaching the victim's residence or workplace, contacting the victim, and arranging meetings with the victim. Violation of a civil protection order constitutes a separate criminal offense.

## ECHR & EU

### Council of Europe -- Istanbul Convention (2011)

The Council of Europe Convention on Preventing and Combating Violence against Women and Domestic Violence (Istanbul Convention), opened for signature on May 11, 2011, provides the most comprehensive international legal framework addressing stalking.

**Article 34 -- Stalking:** "Parties shall take the necessary legislative or other measures to ensure that the intentional conduct of repeatedly engaging in threatening conduct directed at another person, causing her or him to fear for her or his safety, is criminalised." The Convention requires states parties to establish stalking as a specific criminal offense rather than relying solely on general harassment or threat statutes.

**Protective Measures:** The Convention mandates the availability of restraining orders and protection orders for all victims of violence covered by its scope, including stalking victims. States must ensure that emergency barring orders, restraining orders, and protection orders are available, effective, and carry adequate sanctions for breach.

As of 2026, the Istanbul Convention has been ratified by 38 states. Turkey withdrew from the Convention in 2021, drawing significant international criticism.

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR has developed stalking-relevant jurisprudence primarily through the positive obligations doctrine under Articles 3 (prohibition of inhuman or degrading treatment) and 8 (right to respect for private and family life).

**Volodina v. Russia (No. 1) (Application No. 41261/17, Judgment of July 9, 2019):** The Court found violations of Articles 3 and 14, holding that Russia had failed to establish an effective legal framework to combat domestic violence. The judgment established that states have a positive obligation under the Convention to create and apply a system for punishing all forms of domestic violence and providing sufficient safeguards for victims.

**Volodina v. Russia (No. 2) (Application No. 40419/19, Judgment of September 14, 2021):** The case concerned the applicant's allegation that Russian authorities failed to protect her against repeated cyberviolence by her partner, who created fake profiles in her name, published intimate photographs without consent, tracked her movements via GPS, and sent death threats through social media. The Court unanimously found a violation of Article 8, holding that while Russian law formally included provisions protecting individuals against non-consensual dissemination of personal data, these safeguards were not effectively implemented. The two-year delay in opening a criminal investigation and the perpetrator's resulting impunity constituted a failure to discharge the state's positive obligations.

**Key Principles Established:**
- States are obliged to establish and apply effectively a system for punishing all forms of domestic violence and stalking, whether occurring offline or online.
- Formal existence of criminal provisions is insufficient; the state must demonstrate effective implementation.
- Investigative delays and procedural failures that result in impunity breach positive obligations under Article 8.

### EU Directive 2024/1385

Directive (EU) 2024/1385 of the European Parliament and of the Council, adopted on May 14, 2024, represents the first EU-wide law combating violence against women and domestic violence. The Directive was approved by the European Parliament on April 24, 2024, and adopted by the Council on May 7, 2024.

**Cyberstalking Criminalization:** The Directive explicitly criminalizes the repeated or continuous surveillance of a person without their consent, capturing the use of GPS tracking applications, keylogging software, and other covert monitoring technologies. This provision aligns with the existing standards and recommendations of the Istanbul Convention.

**Scope of Criminalized Conduct:** The Directive criminalizes six categories of offense: female genital mutilation, forced marriage, non-consensual sharing of intimate images, cyberstalking, cyber harassment, and cyber incitement to hatred or violence.

**Penalties:** Committing the offenses defined in the Directive is punishable by prison sentences ranging from at least one year to at least five years, depending on the specific offense.

**Transposition Deadline:** Member states must transpose the Directive into national law by June 14, 2027.

### European Protection Order

The European Protection Order (EPO) Directive 2011/99/EU, which entered into force on January 10, 2012, allows victims of stalking and other forms of violence who have been granted a protection order in one EU member state to continue enjoying that protection when they move to another member state. The EPO works in tandem with Regulation (EU) No 606/2013, which introduces a simplified certification procedure so that civil protection measures issued in one EU country can be quickly recognized in another.

## Comparative Analysis

### Definitional Approaches

The four jurisdictions reflect three distinct approaches to defining stalking:

**Enumerated Acts (South Korea, Germany):** Both South Korea and Germany define stalking through a statutory list of specified behaviors -- approaching, following, communicating, monitoring -- directed at the victim. This approach provides legal certainty but risks leaving gaps where novel forms of harassment (particularly technology-facilitated conduct) fall outside the enumerated categories. Both jurisdictions have amended their statutes to address cyberstalking following recognition of these gaps.

**Intent-Based (United States):** The federal statute focuses on the perpetrator's intent -- to kill, injure, harass, intimidate, or place under surveillance -- rather than enumerating specific acts. This approach provides broader coverage but may raise due process concerns regarding the subjective nature of intent determination.

**Impact-Based (Istanbul Convention, ECHR):** The Istanbul Convention defines stalking by reference to its effect on the victim -- "causing her or him to fear for her or his safety" -- rather than specifying particular acts. The ECHR jurisprudence focuses on whether the state's response was sufficient to protect the victim's Convention rights. These approaches prioritize victim experience over perpetrator conduct.

### Prosecution Models

A critical divergence concerns whether prosecution requires victim initiative:

South Korea originally required victim consent for prosecution (반의사불벌죄), a provision that was exploited by perpetrators who intimidated victims into withdrawing complaints. The removal of this requirement in 2023 aligned South Korea with the other jurisdictions, all of which permit ex officio prosecution. Germany originally classified stalking as a complaint offense (Antragsdelikt) but converted it to an offense prosecutable ex officio in 2017.

### Penalty Severity

The United States imposes the most severe maximum penalties (up to life imprisonment where death results), followed by Germany (up to 10 years where death results), and South Korea (maximum 5 years for aggravated stalking). The EU Directive establishes a minimum floor of at least one year imprisonment rather than setting maximum penalties.

### Cross-Border Enforcement

Only the EU framework specifically addresses cross-border enforcement through the European Protection Order system. The United States addresses interstate issues through the federal stalking statute and the Full Faith and Credit provision for protection orders (18 U.S.C. Section 2265). South Korea and Germany lack specific cross-border stalking enforcement mechanisms beyond general mutual legal assistance treaties.
