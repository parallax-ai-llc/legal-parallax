## Summary

Joint and several liability -- the legal principle under which each of two or more responsible parties may be held individually liable for the full amount of harm caused by their combined actions, giving the injured party the right to collect the entire judgment from any single defendant regardless of that defendant's proportionate share of fault -- is a foundational doctrine in tort, contract, and regulatory law that has undergone significant reform in recent decades as jurisdictions grapple with the tension between protecting injured parties' ability to recover full compensation and shielding defendants from bearing disproportionate shares of liability due to the insolvency or unavailability of co-defendants. South Korea establishes joint and several liability (연대책임) for joint tortfeasors under Civil Act (민법) Article 760 and for solidary obligations (연대채무) under Articles 413-427, with the Supreme Court of Korea (대법원) applying these provisions broadly to concurrent tortfeasors, employers vicariously liable under Article 756, and government entities liable under the State Compensation Act (국가배상법) Article 2; South Korea has not enacted tort reform legislation limiting joint and several liability. The United States has experienced the most dramatic reform movement, with over forty states modifying the common-law rule of joint and several liability since the 1980s; reforms range from complete abolition (replacing with proportionate/several-only liability) to hybrid systems that retain joint and several liability only for defendants exceeding a specified fault threshold, with the Restatement (Third) of Torts: Apportionment of Liability (2000) cataloging five distinct approaches. Germany maintains a robust regime of joint and several liability (Gesamtschuld) under BGB Section 421-426 and applies it to tortfeasors under Section 840, with the Gesamtschuld concept constituting one of the most doctrinally developed areas of German obligation law; German law has not adopted proportionate-only liability for tortfeasors. The ECHR and EU framework engage joint and several liability through the Product Liability Directive, environmental liability directives, competition law damages (Directive 2014/104/EU), and the Rome II Regulation's choice-of-law rules for multiple tortfeasors.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea maintains traditional joint and several liability without the reform movements that have reshaped the doctrine in many common-law jurisdictions.

**Civil Act Article 760 -- Joint Tortfeasors (공동불법행위):**

- **Article 760(1):** "Where several persons have jointly committed a tortious act, they shall be jointly and severally liable for the resulting damage." The concept of "jointly" (공동으로) has been interpreted broadly by the Supreme Court to include not only concerted action but also concurrent independent acts that combine to produce indivisible harm.
- **Article 760(2):** "The same shall apply to instigators and accessories." This extends joint liability to parties who encourage or assist the tortious conduct.
- **Article 760(3):** "Where it cannot be known which of several persons committed the tortious act, they shall be jointly and severally liable." This alternative-liability provision addresses the evidentiary problem of indeterminate tortfeasors.

**Civil Act Articles 413-427 -- Solidary Obligations (연대채무):** These articles provide the general framework for joint and several obligations:

- **Article 413:** Defines solidary obligation as one where multiple debtors each owe the full obligation, and satisfaction by one discharges all.
- **Article 414-415:** Establishes the creditor's right to demand performance from any or all debtors.
- **Article 418:** An act of release (면제) by the creditor with respect to one solidary debtor releases the others only to the extent of that debtor's share.
- **Article 419:** A judgment against one solidary debtor does not affect the others.
- **Article 425:** A solidary debtor who has satisfied the creditor has a right of contribution against co-debtors for the excess beyond their share.

**Civil Act Article 756 -- Employer Liability (사용자의 책임):** Establishes employer vicarious liability for the torts of employees committed within the scope of employment. The employer and employee are jointly and severally liable to the injured party, with the employer having a right of recourse against the employee. In practice, the Supreme Court has limited the employer's recourse right, holding that the employer may recover only a portion reflecting the employee's degree of fault relative to the employer's organizational responsibility.

### Key Case Law

**Supreme Court Decision 98Da31264 (November 12, 1999):** Established the broad interpretation of "joint" action under Article 760(1), holding that concurrent tortfeasors need not have acted pursuant to a common plan or design; it is sufficient that their independent acts were objectively connected and combined to produce a single harm. This effectively treats concurrent independent tortfeasors the same as conspirators for purposes of joint and several liability.

**Supreme Court Decision 2011Da7437 (July 22, 2011):** Addressed the relationship between joint tort liability and proportionate fault. The Court held that while all joint tortfeasors are jointly and severally liable for the victim's full damages, the internal allocation of liability among tortfeasors is based on comparative fault. A tortfeasor who has paid more than their proportionate share may seek contribution from co-tortfeasors based on their respective fault percentages.

**Supreme Court Decision 2009Da66549 (April 29, 2010):** Addressed joint and several liability in environmental pollution cases involving multiple emitters. The Court held that where the pollution damage is indivisible and caused by the combined emissions of multiple factories, all emitters are jointly and severally liable under Article 760(1), regardless of each emitter's individual contribution to the total pollution level.

### State Compensation

Under the State Compensation Act (국가배상법) Article 2, the State and the public official who committed the wrongful act bear joint and several liability to the injured party. Article 2(2) provides the State with a right of recourse against the public official who acted with intent or gross negligence.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has experienced the most extensive and diverse reform of joint and several liability of any major legal system, driven by the tort reform movement of the 1980s-2000s.

**Common-Law Rule:** Under traditional common law, all tortfeasors whose conduct was a proximate cause of indivisible harm were jointly and severally liable for the full amount of damages. The injured party could collect the entire judgment from any single defendant, regardless of that defendant's relative fault. This rule was justified by the principle that between an innocent plaintiff and multiple guilty defendants, the risk of a co-defendant's insolvency should fall on the defendants rather than the plaintiff.

**Restatement (Third) of Torts: Apportionment of Liability (2000):** The Restatement identifies five distinct approaches adopted across American jurisdictions:

- **Track A -- Pure Joint and Several Liability:** Retained in a minority of states (including New York for most torts). Each defendant is liable for the entire judgment. N.Y. C.P.L.R. Section 1601 partially modifies this for non-economic damages.
- **Track B -- Pure Several (Proportionate) Liability:** Each defendant is liable only for their proportionate share of fault. Adopted by states including Arizona (A.R.S. Section 12-2506), Colorado (C.R.S. Section 13-21-111.5), and Utah (Utah Code Section 78B-5-818).
- **Track C -- Joint and Several for Economic Damages Only:** Defendants are jointly and severally liable for economic damages but severally liable for non-economic damages. California (Cal. Civ. Code Section 1431.2, Proposition 51) is the most prominent example.
- **Track D -- Threshold Percentage:** Joint and several liability applies only to defendants whose fault exceeds a specified threshold (commonly 50% or 51%). Iowa (Iowa Code Section 668.4) and Oregon (O.R.S. Section 31.600) are examples.
- **Track E -- Hybrid Systems:** Various combinations, such as retaining joint and several liability for certain categories of defendants (e.g., those acting in concert, environmental polluters, or parties violating safety statutes).

### Key Cases

**Walt Disney World Co. v. Wood, 515 So. 2d 198 (Fla. 1987):** The Florida Supreme Court retained joint and several liability alongside comparative fault, reasoning that the doctrine protects plaintiffs' right to full compensation and that the risk of an insolvent co-defendant should fall on culpable defendants rather than the innocent victim. Florida subsequently enacted statutory reform in 2006 (Fla. Stat. Section 768.81) limiting joint and several liability.

**Hymowitz v. Eli Lilly & Co., 73 N.Y.2d 487 (1989):** In the DES litigation, the New York Court of Appeals adopted a market-share liability theory, holding manufacturers of the defective drug liable in proportion to their share of the national DES market, effectively replacing traditional joint and several liability with proportionate liability in this specific context.

**Summers v. Tice, 33 Cal. 2d 80 (1948):** The foundational alternative-liability case, where two hunters simultaneously fired shotguns and one pellet struck the plaintiff, but the plaintiff could not identify which hunter's shot caused the injury. The court held both defendants jointly and severally liable, shifting the burden to each defendant to prove that the other's shot caused the harm.

### Federal Joint and Several Liability

Federal statutes create specific joint and several liability regimes:

- **CERCLA Section 107(a) (42 U.S.C. Section 9607(a)):** Imposes joint and several liability on potentially responsible parties (PRPs) for environmental contamination cleanup costs, following Burlington Northern & Santa Fe Railway Co. v. United States, 556 U.S. 599 (2009), which recognized divisibility as a defense.
- **Section 10(b)/Rule 10b-5 (Securities):** The Private Securities Litigation Reform Act of 1995 (PSLRA) replaced joint and several liability with proportionate liability for securities fraud defendants who did not act knowingly, retaining joint and several liability only for knowing violators.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's Gesamtschuld (solidary obligation/joint and several liability) is one of the most doctrinally developed areas of German obligation law, providing a comprehensive framework that has remained largely unchanged since the BGB's enactment in 1900.

**BGB Section 421 -- Joint and Several Debtors (Gesamtschuldner):** "Where several persons owe a performance in such a way that each is obligated to effect the entire performance, but the creditor is entitled to demand the performance only once (joint and several debtors), the creditor may demand the performance in whole or in part from each debtor at the creditor's discretion. Until the entire performance has been effected, all debtors remain obligated."

**BGB Section 422 -- Effect of Satisfaction (Wirkung der Erfuellung):** Satisfaction by one joint debtor also operates in favor of the other debtors. The claim is extinguished for all.

**BGB Section 423 -- Effect of Release (Wirkung des Erlasses):** A release granted by the creditor to one joint debtor also releases the others if the creditor's intention was to extinguish the entire debt. Otherwise, the release reduces the remaining debtors' obligation by the released debtor's internal share.

**BGB Section 425 -- Effect of Other Facts (Wirkung anderer Tatsachen):** As a general rule, other facts (delay, impossibility, limitation) affecting one joint debtor do not affect the others.

**BGB Section 426 -- Internal Relationship (Ausgleichspflicht, Forderungsuebergang):** The critical internal allocation provision. Section 426(1) establishes the principle of equal shares unless "otherwise determined" (ein anderes bestimmt ist), which in tort cases leads to proportionate fault allocation. Section 426(2) provides for statutory subrogation (cessio legis).

**BGB Section 840 -- Liability of Multiple Tortfeasors:** "If several persons are liable side by side for damage arising from a tort, they are liable as joint and several debtors." This provision automatically triggers the Gesamtschuld regime for concurrent tortfeasors.

### Doctrinal Development

**Unechte Gesamtschuld (Imperfect Solidary Obligation):** German doctrine historically distinguished between echte Gesamtschuld (true solidary obligation) arising from a common legal ground and unechte Gesamtschuld (imperfect solidary obligation) arising from different legal grounds (e.g., a tortfeasor and a contractual obligor both liable for the same harm). The practical significance has diminished, but the distinction remains relevant for determining the effect of acts by or against one debtor on the others.

**Gestoeerte Gesamtschuld (Disturbed Solidary Obligation):** A particularly important German doctrine addressing the situation where the internal contribution rights among joint debtors are "disturbed" by a limitation of liability (Haftungsprivileg) benefiting one debtor. For example, where an employer's liability to an injured employee is limited to workers' compensation, the employer's immunity "disturbs" the contribution relationship with a third-party co-tortfeasor. The BGH has developed several approaches:

- **BGH, Judgment of September 12, 2006 (VI ZR 116/05):** Held that where one joint debtor benefits from a statutory limitation of liability, the remaining debtors' liability to the victim is reduced by the privileged debtor's hypothetical internal share.
- **BGH, Judgment of November 24, 2009 (VI ZR 219/08):** Confirmed the gestoeerte Gesamtschuld doctrine and held that the privileged debtor's hypothetical contribution share must be determined as if no privilege existed.

**BGB Section 831 -- Liability for Auxiliaries (Haftung fuer Verrichtungsgehilfen):** Unlike the strict vicarious liability found in South Korea and the United States, Section 831 allows the employer an exculpation defense (Entlastungsbeweis): the employer is not liable if they prove they exercised due care in selecting and supervising the auxiliary. In practice, the BGH has set the standard so high that exculpation rarely succeeds, and Section 831 is often circumvented through organizational liability (Organisationsverschulden) or vicarious liability for executive employees (leitende Angestellte) under Section 31.

### Limitation Periods

The BGH has held that the Section 426(1) independent contribution claim is subject to the general three-year limitation period under Section 195, beginning when the claim arises (i.e., when the joint obligation is established). The Section 426(2) subrogated claim is subject to the limitation period of the original creditor's claim.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Directives Addressing Joint and Several Liability

**Damages Directive (Directive 2014/104/EU) Article 11 -- Joint and Several Liability in Competition Law:** Establishes that undertakings that infringe competition law through joint behavior are jointly and severally liable for the resulting harm. However, Article 11(2)-(5) introduces significant exceptions:

- **SME Exception (Article 11(2)):** A small or medium-sized enterprise (SME) is liable only to its own direct and indirect purchasers where its market share was below 5% and full joint and several liability would irretrievably jeopardize its economic viability.
- **Leniency Recipient (Article 11(4)):** An immunity recipient (under a leniency program) is jointly and severally liable only to its own direct and indirect purchasers, unless other injured persons cannot obtain full compensation from the other infringers.
- **Contribution (Article 11(5)):** Infringers have a right to contribution from co-infringers, with shares determined by their relative responsibility.

**Environmental Liability Directive (Directive 2004/35/CE):** Article 3 establishes liability for environmental damage based on the "polluter pays" principle. Where multiple operators contribute to environmental damage, member states determine whether liability is joint and several or proportionate.

**Product Liability Directive (EU) 2024/2853:** Article 14 establishes that where multiple persons are liable for the same damage under the Directive (e.g., manufacturer and component supplier), they are liable jointly and severally, without prejudice to national rules on contribution and recourse.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Alisic and Others v. Bosnia and Herzegovina, Croatia, Serbia, Slovenia, and the Former Yugoslav Republic of Macedonia (Application No. 60642/08, Grand Chamber, 2014):** The Grand Chamber held multiple successor states jointly responsible for ensuring the right of depositors to access their old foreign-currency savings, applying principles analogous to joint and several liability in the state responsibility context.

**Varga and Others v. Hungary (Applications Nos. 14097/12 et al., 2015):** Addressed the systemic problem of prison overcrowding, holding Hungary responsible for violations of Article 3. While not a joint liability case per se, it illustrates the Court's willingness to impose comprehensive liability on a single responsible entity for systemic harm.

### Choice of Law

**Rome II Regulation Article 20:** The law applicable to a tortfeasor's non-contractual obligation to the victim governs that tortfeasor's right to demand compensation from co-tortfeasors. This means that in cross-border torts, the law governing joint and several liability may differ from the law governing internal contribution, creating potential conflicts.

## Comparative Analysis

### Stability vs. Reform

The most striking comparative feature is the divergent trajectory of joint and several liability reform. South Korea and Germany have maintained traditional full joint and several liability without significant reform, reflecting the civil-law view that solidary obligation effectively protects victims and that internal contribution adequately protects co-defendants. The United States has fragmented into at least five distinct approaches, reflecting a common-law system in which tort reform has been primarily a legislative and political process rather than a doctrinal one.

### Victim Protection vs. Defendant Fairness

The fundamental policy tension in joint and several liability -- protecting the plaintiff's right to full recovery versus protecting defendants from bearing disproportionate shares of liability -- is resolved differently across jurisdictions. South Korea and Germany prioritize victim protection by maintaining full joint and several liability and allocating the insolvency risk to co-defendants through internal contribution. U.S. proportionate-liability states shift the insolvency risk to the plaintiff, reasoning that it is unjust for a marginally culpable defendant to bear the full loss simply because a more culpable co-defendant is insolvent.

### Gestoeerte Gesamtschuld

Germany's gestoeerte Gesamtschuld doctrine addresses a problem that exists in all jurisdictions but is handled less systematically elsewhere: what happens when one co-tortfeasor benefits from a statutory immunity or limitation of liability. The German solution -- reducing the remaining defendants' liability by the privileged party's hypothetical share -- represents a compromise between full victim protection and fairness to non-privileged defendants. South Korea and the United States address similar situations through ad hoc judicial decisions rather than a unified doctrine.

### EU Sectoral Harmonization

The EU has selectively harmonized joint and several liability in specific sectors (competition law, product liability, environmental liability) while leaving the general tort regime to member states. The Damages Directive's exceptions for SMEs and leniency recipients represent policy-driven modifications of the solidary principle that have no equivalent in the other jurisdictions studied.
