## Summary

Transitional justice -- the set of judicial and non-judicial measures implemented to redress the legacy of massive human rights violations -- has been a defining challenge for Global South legal systems confronting histories of state violence, authoritarianism, colonialism, and armed conflict. China's approach to the Cultural Revolution (1966-1976) represents the most constrained model of transitional accountability, limited to an intra-party resolution condemning the ultra-left excesses while shielding the Communist Party itself and Mao Zedong's overall legacy from systemic examination; the 1981 "Resolution on Certain Questions in the History of Our Party Since the Founding of the People's Republic of China" acknowledged that the Cultural Revolution was "responsible for the most severe setback and the heaviest losses suffered by the Party, the country, and the people since the founding of the People's Republic." India's post-partition legacy has produced scattered accountability mechanisms for communal violence, most notably the Justice Ranganath Misra Commission (2004-2007) on the 1984 anti-Sikh riots and the Nanavati-Mehta Commission (2002-2014) on the 2002 Gujarat violence, though systematic transitional justice frameworks remain absent. South Africa's Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC, 1995-2002), established under the Promotion of National Unity and Reconciliation Act 34 of 1995, is the world's most studied transitional justice mechanism, combining truth-telling, victim testimony, perpetrator amnesty (conditional on full disclosure), and reparations recommendations. Colombia's Comprehensive System of Truth, Justice, Reparation, and Non-Repetition, created by the 2016 Final Peace Agreement with the FARC-EP and implemented through Legislative Act 01 of 2017, establishes the Special Jurisdiction for Peace (Jurisdiccion Especial para la Paz, JEP), the Commission for the Clarification of Truth (CEV), and the Unit for the Search for Persons Deemed as Missing (UBPD) -- the most comprehensive transitional justice architecture ever constructed. The Philippines' reckoning with the Ferdinand Marcos dictatorship (1972-1986) has produced the Human Rights Victims Reparation and Recognition Act of 2013 (Republic Act No. 10368), which established the Human Rights Victims' Claims Board to process compensation claims, though the 2022 election of Ferdinand Marcos Jr. as president has complicated ongoing accountability efforts.

## China

### The Cultural Revolution and Its Legal Aftermath

The Great Proletarian Cultural Revolution (1966-1976), launched by Mao Zedong through the May 16 Notification (1966) and the Sixteen Points (August 8, 1966), resulted in an estimated 500,000 to 2 million deaths, the persecution of millions, the destruction of cultural heritage, and the complete dismantlement of the legal system. Courts, procuratorates, and legal education were shuttered; the Constitution of 1954 was effectively suspended; and extrajudicial violence by Red Guards and revolutionary committees replaced legal process.

The transitional accountability measures following Mao's death (September 9, 1976) were carefully calibrated to preserve the party's legitimacy:

**Trial of the Gang of Four (1980-1981):** The Special Court of the Supreme People's Court, established by the Standing Committee of the National People's Congress, tried 10 defendants (the "Gang of Four" -- Jiang Qing, Zhang Chunqiao, Yao Wenyuan, Wang Hongwen -- plus six associates of Lin Biao). Jiang Qing and Zhang Chunqiao received death sentences (later commuted to life imprisonment); others received sentences of 16 to 20 years. The trial served to channel blame onto a small group while insulating the party apparatus and Mao's broader legacy.

**1981 Resolution:** The Sixth Plenary Session of the 11th CPC Central Committee (June 27, 1981) adopted the "Resolution on Certain Questions in the History of Our Party Since the Founding of the People's Republic of China." The Resolution declared the Cultural Revolution "initiated and led by Comrade Mao Zedong" and acknowledged it was "responsible for the most severe setback and the heaviest losses." However, it simultaneously affirmed that Mao's contributions were "primary" and his mistakes "secondary" (七三开, "seven parts good, three parts bad"), preserving the ideological foundation of party rule.

**Rehabilitation:** Between 1978 and the mid-1980s, the party rehabilitated (pingyuan) an estimated 3 million individuals and families wrongly persecuted during the Cultural Revolution and the Anti-Rightist Campaign (1957-1958). Rehabilitation involved restoration of party membership, return to positions, back-pay, and return of confiscated property where possible. However, the process was administered entirely through party channels rather than judicial mechanisms, was subject to political discretion, and provided no forum for public testimony or accountability.

### Tiananmen Square (1989): Suppressed Memory

The June 4, 1989 military crackdown on pro-democracy protests in and around Tiananmen Square resulted in deaths estimated from several hundred to several thousand (the Chinese Red Cross initially reported 2,600 before retracting the figure). No transitional justice mechanism has been established. The Tiananmen Mothers (天安门母亲), a group of victims' family members led by Ding Zilin, has documented 203 confirmed deaths and repeatedly petitioned the government for accountability, compensation, and truth-telling -- all requests have been rejected. Discussion of the event remains heavily censored; criminal punishment continues for individuals who commemorate the anniversary or share information about it.

### Contemporary Accountability Gaps

China's approach to state violence against Uyghur Muslims in Xinjiang (documented by the UN Office of the High Commissioner for Human Rights in its August 2022 assessment, which found "serious human rights violations" including "credible" evidence of torture) has followed the same pattern of non-accountability. The State Council's White Paper on "Vocational Education and Training in Xinjiang" (August 2019) frames the internment camps as counter-terrorism and de-radicalization measures, while no domestic legal mechanism exists for affected individuals to seek redress.

## India

### Partition Violence (1947)

The partition of British India into India and Pakistan (August 1947) triggered one of the largest mass migrations in human history (estimated 10-20 million displaced) and communal violence resulting in an estimated 200,000 to 2 million deaths. No formal transitional justice mechanism was established. The Constituent Assembly debates reflected a deliberate choice to look forward rather than backward, with the Constitution's focus on fundamental rights and DPSP serving as the primary response to partition's legacy.

The displaced persons were addressed through the Administration of Evacuee Property Act, 1950 (governing property abandoned by Muslims who migrated to Pakistan), the Displaced Persons (Compensation and Rehabilitation) Act, 1954, and the Displaced Persons (Claims) Supplementary Act, 1954. These laws provided for property compensation pools but did not address violence accountability.

### 1984 Anti-Sikh Riots

Following the assassination of Prime Minister Indira Gandhi on October 31, 1984, anti-Sikh pogroms in Delhi and other cities resulted in an estimated 2,800-3,000 deaths. The justice process has been characterized by prolonged impunity:

- **Multiple Commissions:** The Ranganath Misra Commission (1986), Jain-Banerjee Committee (1987), Potti-Rosha Committee (1990), Ahuja Committee (1990), Jain-Aggarwal Committee (1993), and Nanavati Commission (2000) investigated the violence, each producing recommendations largely unimplemented.
- **Criminal Prosecution:** Despite an estimated 587 cases registered, convictions were scarce. In **Sajjan Kumar v. CBI (2018)**, the Delhi High Court convicted former Congress MP Sajjan Kumar for his role in the massacre in Raj Nagar area, sentencing him to life imprisonment, overturning his acquittal by the trial court. The Court observed: "The criminals have enjoyed political patronage and the victims have suffered immensely."
- **SIT (2015):** The Supreme Court constituted a Special Investigation Team in 2015 to re-examine cases closed by the police. The SIT recommended reopening several cases.

### 2002 Gujarat Violence

The communal violence in Gujarat following the Godhra train burning (February 27, 2002), resulting in an estimated 1,000-2,000 deaths (predominantly Muslim), generated extensive but fragmented accountability processes:

- **Nanavati-Mehta Commission (2002-2014):** The two-member commission appointed by the Gujarat government filed its final report in 2014, largely exonerating the state government while acknowledging failures in police response.
- **Supreme Court Intervention:** In **Zahira Habibullah Sheikh v. State of Gujarat (Best Bakery case, 2004 (4) SCC 158)**, the Supreme Court ordered retrial of a massacre case where witnesses had turned hostile, transferring the case to Maharashtra. In **Zakia Ahsan Jafri v. State of Gujarat (2022)**, the Supreme Court upheld the SIT's closure report giving a clean chit to then-Chief Minister Narendra Modi, while Justices on the bench observed that the SIT had conducted a thorough investigation.
- **Bilkis Bano Case:** Bilkis Bano, gang-raped during the riots while pregnant, obtained conviction of 11 perpetrators by a Mumbai special court in 2008 (confirmed by the Bombay High Court). In 2022, the Gujarat government remitted the sentences of all 11 convicts under its remission policy. In **Bilkis Yakub Rasool v. Union of India (2024)**, the Supreme Court quashed the remission orders as illegal, finding that the appropriate remission authority was the state where the trial took place (Maharashtra), not Gujarat.

## South Africa

### Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC)

South Africa's TRC, established under the Promotion of National Unity and Reconciliation Act 34 of 1995, is the global reference point for transitional justice mechanisms. Chaired by Archbishop Desmond Tutu, the TRC operated through three committees:

**1. Human Rights Violations Committee (HRVC):** Received over 21,000 statements from victims of gross human rights violations committed between March 1, 1960, and May 10, 1994. The Committee held public hearings across South Africa, providing a nationally televised platform for victims to tell their stories.

**2. Amnesty Committee:** Received 7,112 applications for amnesty; granted amnesty to 1,500 applicants. Amnesty was conditional on: (a) the act being associated with a political objective; (b) full disclosure of all relevant facts; and (c) the act being proportionate to the political objective. The Committee denied amnesty where applicants failed to make full disclosure or where acts were motivated by personal malice rather than political objectives.

The constitutional validity of the amnesty provisions was upheld by the Constitutional Court in **Azanian Peoples Organisation (AZAPO) v. President of the Republic of South Africa (1996 (4) SA 671 (CC))**. Justice Mahomed, writing for the Court, held that the amnesty provisions, as embodied in the Epilogue to the Interim Constitution (1993), were constitutionally valid as a necessary mechanism for achieving the transition from apartheid to democracy, even though they limited victims' right of access to courts under Section 22 of the Interim Constitution.

**3. Reparation and Rehabilitation Committee:** Recommended individual reparation grants (initially recommended at R23,023 per year for six years), community rehabilitation programs, and symbolic reparation measures. The government ultimately provided one-off payments of R30,000 to approximately 19,000 identified victims in 2003 -- a fraction of the recommended amount and paid years after the TRC's recommendations.

### Post-TRC Accountability

The TRC's final report (1998, with Amnesty Committee volumes completed in 2003) identified individuals for potential prosecution who had not applied for or had been denied amnesty. However, the National Prosecuting Authority (NPA) has pursued very few cases:

- **The Inquest into the Death of Ahmed Timol (2017):** A Gauteng High Court inquest found that anti-apartheid activist Ahmed Timol did not commit suicide by jumping from the tenth floor of John Vorster Square police station in 1971, as the apartheid-era inquest had concluded, but was murdered. Joao Rodrigues, the security police officer, was charged with murder, though the case has been subject to extensive procedural delays.
- **The Apartheid-era Chemical and Biological Warfare Program:** Wouter Basson, head of the apartheid military's Project Coast, was acquitted in a criminal trial in 2002. The Health Professions Council of South Africa (HPCSA) found him guilty of ethical misconduct in 2013, but the finding was overturned by the Supreme Court of Appeal in 2015 and then reinstated by the Constitutional Court in **Basson v. Hugo NO (2018 (1) SA 1 (CC))**.

### Land Restitution as Transitional Justice

The Restitution of Land Rights Act 22 of 1994 established the Land Claims Court and the Commission on Restitution of Land Rights to process claims from persons dispossessed of land rights after June 19, 1913 (the date of the Natives Land Act) as a result of racially discriminatory laws. By 2024, over 80,000 claims had been settled, though approximately 4,000 remained outstanding. The Constitutional Court's decision in **Department of Land Affairs v. Goedgelegen Tropical Fruits (2007 (6) SA 199 (CC))** clarified that labor tenants and their descendants qualified as claimants under the Act.

## Latin America

### Colombia: The JEP and Comprehensive Transitional Justice

Colombia's Comprehensive System of Truth, Justice, Reparation, and Non-Repetition (Sistema Integral de Verdad, Justicia, Reparacion y No Repeticion, SIVJRNR) represents the most architecturally ambitious transitional justice framework in history, created to address the armed conflict between the state, guerrilla groups (FARC-EP, ELN), and paramilitaries that lasted over five decades and produced an estimated 8.9 million victims (Registro Unico de Victimas).

**Special Jurisdiction for Peace (JEP):** Established by Legislative Act 01 of 2017 (constitutional amendment) and Law 1957 of 2019 (JEP Statutory Law), the JEP has jurisdiction over conflict-related conduct committed before December 1, 2016, by: FARC-EP members, state security forces, and third parties who decisively contributed to the conflict. The JEP applies "restorative sanctions" -- restrictions on liberty (5-8 years) involving reparative work benefiting victims -- to those who fully acknowledge truth and responsibility. Those who refuse to acknowledge responsibility or are found guilty after adversarial proceedings face "ordinary sanctions" (15-20 years of effective imprisonment).

Key JEP proceedings include:
- **Case 01 (Hostage-taking):** In June 2022, the JEP issued its first macro-case resolution, finding 8 former FARC-EP Secretariat members responsible for war crimes and crimes against humanity for the systematic kidnapping of approximately 21,396 persons. Former FARC leader Rodrigo Londono ("Timochenko") acknowledged responsibility and committed to restorative sanctions.
- **Case 03 (Extrajudicial killings / "false positives"):** The JEP is investigating the systematic practice by military units of killing civilians and disguising them as guerrilla combatants to inflate kill counts. The case involves an estimated 6,402 victims between 2002 and 2008.
- **Case 04 (Situation in Urab Antioqueño):** Examining state, guerrilla, and paramilitary violence in the Urab region.

**Commission for the Clarification of Truth, Coexistence, and Non-Repetition (CEV):** Operated from 2018 to 2022 under the leadership of Father Francisco de Roux, S.J. The CEV presented its Final Report ("Hay futuro si hay verdad") in June 2022, documenting patterns of violence, root causes, and impacts, with recommendations for non-repetition. Unlike the South African TRC, the CEV had no power to grant amnesty and its findings had no judicial effect.

**Victims and Land Restitution Law (Law 1448 of 2011):** Pre-dating the peace agreement, this law established a comprehensive framework for victim recognition, reparation, and land restitution. The law recognized victims of the armed conflict occurring after January 1, 1985, and created the institutional apparatus for individual reparations (indemnizacion administrativa), rehabilitation, satisfaction measures (dignification), and guarantees of non-repetition. The land restitution component reversed the burden of proof, requiring alleged good-faith possessors to prove they did not benefit from forced displacement.

### Constitutional Court Oversight

The Colombian Constitutional Court has exercised extensive oversight of transitional justice, including:

- **Sentencia C-579/2013:** Upheld the Legal Framework for Peace (Marco Juridico para la Paz), allowing Congress to prioritize prosecution of those most responsible for the most serious crimes while applying alternative sanctions, establishing the constitutional foundation for the JEP's selective prosecution model.
- **Sentencia C-674/2017:** Reviewed the JEP's constitutional amendment, upholding most provisions while striking down a provision that would have prevented the JEP from ordering the extradition of persons subject to its jurisdiction for drug trafficking offenses.

### Brazil: Military Dictatorship (1964-1985)

Brazil's transitional justice for the military dictatorship (1964-1985) has been notably constrained. The 1979 Amnesty Law (Lei No. 6.683) provided blanket amnesty for political crimes committed between September 2, 1961, and August 15, 1979, benefiting both state agents and opposition militants. In **ADPF 153 (2010)**, the STF upheld the Amnesty Law by a 7-2 vote, holding that it was a bilateral political agreement necessary for the democratic transition, despite the Inter-American Court of Human Rights' contrary ruling in **Gomes Lund v. Brazil (2010)** that the Amnesty Law was incompatible with the American Convention on Human Rights.

The National Truth Commission (Comissao Nacional da Verdade, CNV), established by Lei No. 12.528/2011, operated from 2012 to 2014 and documented 434 deaths and disappearances attributable to the military regime. Its final report recommended prosecution of 377 individuals, but no criminal prosecutions have been initiated due to the Amnesty Law.

## Southeast Asia

### Philippines: Marcos-Era Accountability

The Philippines' engagement with the Marcos dictatorship (September 21, 1972 martial law declaration to February 25, 1986 People Power Revolution) has produced significant but incomplete accountability:

**Presidential Commission on Good Government (PCGG):** Established by President Corazon Aquino through Executive Order No. 1 (February 28, 1986) to recover the estimated USD 5-10 billion in ill-gotten wealth accumulated by the Marcos family and their cronies. The PCGG has filed cases before the Sandiganbayan (anti-graft court) and recovered approximately USD 3.3 billion, including the 2003 forfeiture of USD 658 million in Marcos Swiss bank deposits.

**Republic Act No. 10368 (Human Rights Victims Reparation and Recognition Act of 2013):** The Act established the Human Rights Victims' Claims Board (HRVCB) to receive and evaluate claims of human rights violations during the Marcos regime (September 21, 1972 to February 25, 1986). The law drew upon the USD 10 billion (PHP 10 billion) recovered through the Marcos ill-gotten wealth cases. The HRVCB accepted 75,749 applications and approved 11,103 claims (as of the Board's final report). Compensation was classified into:
- Victims of killing: PHP 250,000
- Victims of enforced disappearance: PHP 200,000
- Victims of torture: PHP 100,000 (varying by severity)
- Victims of other violations: scaled amounts

The landmark case of **Republic v. Sandiganbayan (Marcos Swiss Deposits, G.R. No. 152154, 2003)** upheld the forfeiture of USD 658 million in Swiss bank deposits under Republic Act No. 1379 (An Act Declaring Forfeiture in Favor of the State Any Property Found to Have Been Unlawfully Acquired by Any Public Officer or Employee).

**Hawaii Class Action (Hilao v. Estate of Marcos):** In 1995, a US federal jury in Hawaii awarded USD 1.97 billion in damages to 9,539 Filipino victims of the Marcos regime in a class action under the Alien Tort Statute. The Ninth Circuit upheld the verdict, though collection has been difficult.

### Challenges: The Marcos Return

The election of Ferdinand Marcos Jr. (Bongbong Marcos) as President of the Philippines in May 2022 has created unprecedented challenges for transitional justice. The Marcos family has consistently denied that the Marcos dictatorship committed systematic human rights violations, and President Marcos Jr. has not acknowledged the HRVCB's findings. Historical revisionism through social media has been documented by multiple studies. The remaining PCGG cases continue before the Sandiganbayan, but political support for aggressive asset recovery has diminished.

### Indonesia: 1965-66 and East Timor

Indonesia faces two major unresolved transitional justice challenges:

**1965-66 Mass Killings:** The military-led mass killings of an estimated 500,000 to 1 million alleged communists following the September 30, 1965 coup attempt remain the largest unresolved transitional justice issue in Southeast Asia. The National Human Rights Commission (Komnas HAM) conducted a pro-justicia inquiry (2008-2012) and concluded that gross human rights violations had occurred, recommending the establishment of an ad hoc human rights court. However, the Attorney General has declined to follow up, citing insufficient evidence. President Joko Widodo's January 2023 statement acknowledging "gross human rights violations" at 12 historical events (including 1965-66) was an important symbolic step but did not lead to judicial proceedings or a formal truth commission.

**East Timor/Timor-Leste:** Indonesia's occupation of East Timor (1975-1999) resulted in an estimated 100,000-180,000 deaths. The Indonesian Ad Hoc Human Rights Court for East Timor (established under Law No. 26 of 2000 on Human Rights Courts) tried 18 defendants in 2002-2003 but convicted only Eurico Guterres (later acquitted on appeal) -- widely criticized as a sham process. Timor-Leste's Commission for Reception, Truth and Reconciliation (CAVR, 2002-2005) produced the comprehensive Chega! report documenting the occupation's human rights record. The bilateral Indonesia-Timor-Leste Commission of Truth and Friendship (CTF, 2005-2008) acknowledged institutional responsibility for violence but recommended no prosecutions, prioritizing diplomatic reconciliation.

## Comparative Analysis

### Models of Transitional Justice

The Global South exhibits a spectrum of transitional justice approaches:

1. **Comprehensive Institutional Architecture (Colombia):** The SIVJRNR represents the maximum model, combining a special court (JEP), truth commission (CEV), missing persons unit (UBPD), victim reparations, and constitutional court oversight within a single integrated framework.

2. **Truth Commission with Conditional Amnesty (South Africa):** The TRC model balances truth-telling against prosecution through conditional individual amnesty, prioritizing national reconciliation over retributive justice.

3. **Selective Prosecution with Reparations (Philippines):** Asset recovery, victim compensation through legislation, and targeted prosecution (PCGG/Sandiganbayan) without a comprehensive truth commission.

4. **Ad Hoc and Fragmented (India, Brazil):** Multiple commissions of inquiry without systematic follow-through; de facto impunity for state actors through political inaction (India) or legislated amnesty (Brazil).

5. **Intra-Party Resolution with Suppressed Memory (China):** Transitional accountability entirely contained within party mechanisms, with no independent judicial or truth-telling process and active suppression of public memory.

### The Amnesty Debate

The fundamental tension between amnesty and accountability plays out differently across jurisdictions:

- **South Africa's conditional amnesty** was designed to incentivize truth-telling: perpetrators who disclosed fully received individual amnesty; those who did not remained liable to prosecution. The Constitutional Court's AZAPO decision upheld this compromise. However, the NPA's failure to prosecute non-amnestied perpetrators has undermined the system's credibility.
- **Colombia's JEP** avoids the word "amnesty," instead offering "restorative sanctions" (restrictions on liberty involving community service) to those who acknowledge responsibility, with genuine imprisonment reserved for non-cooperators.
- **Brazil's 1979 Amnesty Law** provided unconditional blanket amnesty, which the STF upheld despite the Inter-American Court's ruling that blanket amnesties violate international law.
- **China** operates a system of de facto impunity without any formal amnesty framework, simply declining to investigate or prosecute.

### Victim Participation

The degree of victim participation varies dramatically:
- **Colombia's JEP** provides the most extensive victim participation rights, with victims as formal parties in proceedings, the right to present evidence, and the right to contest proposed sanctions.
- **South Africa's TRC** enabled victims to give public testimony, creating powerful narratives but without formal rights within the amnesty process.
- **The Philippines' HRVCB** processed individual claims but operated as an administrative claims board rather than a participatory justice mechanism.
- **India's** commissions of inquiry take victim testimony but operate as fact-finding bodies without binding recommendations or enforcement powers.
