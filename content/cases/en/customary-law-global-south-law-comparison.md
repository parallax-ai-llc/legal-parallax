## Summary

The integration of customary, traditional, and indigenous legal systems into formal state law represents one of the most consequential and contested areas of legal development in the Global South. Unlike Western legal orders that largely subordinated or eliminated customary law during state formation, most Global South jurisdictions operate systems of legal pluralism in which customary norms coexist -- sometimes harmoniously, often tensely -- with received colonial law, constitutional principles, and statutory enactments. China's legal framework accommodates minority customs through the Regional Ethnic Autonomy Law (1984, revised 2001) and specific provisions in the Civil Code (2020) allowing ethnic minorities to retain customary marriage and inheritance practices, while maintaining the Communist Party's overriding authority. India recognizes tribal customary law through the Sixth Schedule to the Constitution (governing tribal areas in the northeastern states) and exemptions from general legislation for Scheduled Tribes, creating autonomous governance structures under the Panchayats (Extension to Scheduled Areas) Act, 1996 (PESA). Africa presents the most developed systems of legal pluralism: South Africa's Constitution (Section 211) recognizes customary law subject to the Bill of Rights, operationalized through the Recognition of Customary Marriages Act 120 of 1998 and the Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act 41 of 2003, while Nigeria operates a tripartite system of common law, customary law, and Islamic law (Sharia), each with dedicated court hierarchies. Brazil's 1988 Constitution (Articles 231-232) recognizes indigenous peoples' social organization, customs, languages, beliefs, and traditions, and their original rights to lands they traditionally occupy, implemented through the Indian Statute (Lei No. 6.001/1973, partially superseded) and case law from the Supreme Court (STF). Indonesia's adat (customary) law tradition, extensively studied by Dutch colonial scholars and partially codified in the Van Vollenhoven adat law compilations, is constitutionally recognized under Article 18B(2) of the 1945 Constitution and implemented through the Village Law (Law No. 6 of 2014), Constitutional Court jurisprudence, and emerging regional regulations (peraturan daerah) on adat communities. The Philippines recognizes indigenous customary law through the Indigenous Peoples' Rights Act (IPRA, RA 8371, 1997), which establishes indigenous peoples' right to self-governance and the application of customary law within ancestral domains, adjudicated through tribal justice systems and the National Commission on Indigenous Peoples.

## China

### Constitutional and Legal Framework for Minority Customs

China's approach to customary law is channeled primarily through its ethnic minority (shaoshu minzu) governance framework. The Constitution (Article 4) declares:

> "All ethnic groups in the People's Republic of China are equal. The state protects the lawful rights and interests of the ethnic minorities and upholds and develops a relationship of equality, unity and mutual assistance among all of China's ethnic groups."

The **Regional Ethnic Autonomy Law (REAL)** of 1984, revised in 2001, establishes the legal framework for self-governance in 155 ethnic autonomous areas (5 autonomous regions, 30 autonomous prefectures, and 120 autonomous counties/banners). Article 10 provides that organs of self-government in ethnic autonomous areas shall guarantee the freedom of ethnic minorities to use and develop their own spoken and written languages and to preserve or reform their own customs and ways.

Critically, Article 19 of the REAL grants autonomous area people's congresses the power to enact "regulations on the exercise of autonomy" (zizhi tiaoli) and "separate regulations" (danxing tiaoli), which may modify provisions of national laws and administrative regulations to accommodate local ethnic conditions. In practice, this has been used primarily for:

- **Marriage:** The Marriage Law of 1980 (Article 50) provides that ethnic minority autonomous areas may enact "flexible and supplementary provisions" (biantong he buchong guiding). Several autonomous areas have permitted marriage below the national minimum age (20 for males, 18 for females) for ethnic minorities.
- **Family Planning:** Prior to the abolition of the one-child policy, ethnic minorities in autonomous areas were generally permitted two or three children depending on population density and local regulations.
- **Inheritance:** The Civil Code of 2020 (Article 1153) provides that in handling inheritance disputes in ethnic autonomous areas, autonomy and separate regulations shall apply.

### Limitations: Party Supremacy and Assimilation Pressures

The exercise of customary law autonomy is fundamentally constrained by the Chinese Communist Party's (CPC) supremacy. Article 5 of the REAL requires that organs of self-government in ethnic autonomous areas "must uphold the unity of the country" and ensure that local regulations do not contravene the Constitution or national laws. In practice, the Party exercises decisive control over the appointment of ethnic autonomous area leaders, policy direction, and the permissible scope of customary practice.

Since 2017, the intensification of policies in Xinjiang Uyghur Autonomous Region -- including mass internment of Uyghur Muslims in "vocational education and training centers" (zhiye jiaoyu peixun zhongxin), restrictions on religious practice, and cultural assimilation programs -- has fundamentally challenged the promise of ethnic autonomy. The Xinjiang De-extremification Regulation (2017, revised 2018) prohibits a range of customary and religious practices deemed "extremist." Similarly, the Regulations on Ethnic Unity and Progress in Tibet Autonomous Region (2020) emphasize "national unity" and the "Chinese national identity" over cultural preservation.

### Village-Level Customary Dispute Resolution

Outside the ethnic autonomy framework, customary dispute resolution persists at the village level through the People's Mediation Committee (renmin tiaojie weiyuanhui) system. The People's Mediation Law of 2010 provides a legal basis for mediation committees, which handle millions of disputes annually (over 10 million in 2023 according to Ministry of Justice statistics). While formally distinct from customary law, village mediation often incorporates local customs (fengsu xiguan) and community norms, particularly in rural areas concerning land disputes, family matters, and neighborhood conflicts.

## India

### Sixth Schedule: Tribal Autonomous Governance

India's most developed customary law framework operates through the Sixth Schedule to the Constitution, which applies to tribal areas in the northeastern states of Assam, Meghalaya, Mizoram, and Tripura. The Sixth Schedule establishes Autonomous District Councils and Regional Councils with legislative, judicial, and executive powers over tribal areas:

- **Paragraph 3:** District and Regional Councils may make laws on: allotment, occupation, and use of land; management of forests (excluding reserved forests); use of canals or watercourses for agriculture; regulation of practice of jhum (shifting cultivation); establishment of village or town committees; inheritance of property; marriage and divorce; social customs.
- **Paragraph 4:** Administration of justice by village councils or courts constituted under the Sixth Schedule; these courts apply customary law in civil and criminal matters.
- **Paragraph 5:** The District Council may make regulations for the allotment of land, with the proviso that no land shall be transferred from tribal members to non-tribal persons.

The Sixth Schedule has been consequential in Meghalaya, where the Khasi, Garo, and Jaintia communities maintain matrilineal inheritance customs; in Nagaland, where Article 371(A) of the Constitution provides special protections for Naga customary law and procedure; and in Mizoram, where the Mizo customary law system governs personal matters through village councils (lal upa).

### PESA: Extension to Scheduled Areas

The Panchayats (Extension to Scheduled Areas) Act, 1996 (PESA) extended Panchayati Raj (local self-governance) institutions to tribal areas covered by the Fifth Schedule of the Constitution (tribal areas in states other than the northeastern states). PESA uniquely mandates that state Panchayat legislation shall:

- **Section 4(d):** Be "in consonance with the customary law, social and religious practices and traditional management practices of community resources."
- **Section 4(e):** Provide that every Gram Sabha (village assembly) shall be competent to safeguard and preserve the traditions and customs of the people, their cultural identity, community resources, and the customary mode of dispute resolution.
- **Section 4(i):** Ensure that the Gram Sabha or Panchayats at the appropriate level shall have the ownership of minor forest produce.

The Supreme Court in **Nandini Sundar v. State of Chhattisgarh (2011 (7) SCC 547)** (the Salwa Judum case) emphasized the constitutional significance of tribal self-governance under the Fifth Schedule, striking down the state's arming of tribal youth as counter-insurgency forces.

### Customary Law and Individual Rights

The relationship between tribal customary law and constitutional rights remains deeply contested. In **Madhu Kishwar v. State of Bihar (1996 (5) SCC 125)**, the Supreme Court addressed the conflict between Chotanagpur tribal customary law (which denied women inheritance rights to land) and the constitutional right to equality. While the Court acknowledged the discriminatory impact on women, it declined to declare the customary law unconstitutional, instead directing legislative reform.

Article 13(3)(a) of the Constitution defines "law" to include "any custom or usage having the force of law" within the territory of India, potentially subjecting customary law to fundamental rights review under Article 13(1)-(2). However, courts have been cautious in applying this provision to tribal customs, recognizing the tension between individual rights and community autonomy.

## Africa

### South Africa: Constitutional Recognition with Rights Limitations

South Africa's Constitution provides the most explicit constitutional framework for customary law in Africa:

- **Section 211(1):** "The institution, status and role of traditional leadership, according to customary law, are recognised, subject to the Constitution."
- **Section 211(2):** "A traditional authority that observes a system of customary law may function subject to any applicable legislation and customs, which includes amendments to, or repeal of, that legislation or those customs."
- **Section 211(3):** "The courts must apply customary law when that law is applicable, subject to the Constitution and any legislation that specifically deals with customary law."

This framework establishes what scholars call "conditional recognition" -- customary law is recognized but must comply with the Bill of Rights. Section 39(2) provides that when interpreting any legislation, and when developing the common law or customary law, every court must promote the spirit, purport, and objects of the Bill of Rights.

### Landmark Cases

The Constitutional Court has transformed customary law through rights-based adjudication:

- **Bhe v. Magistrate, Khayelitsha (2005 (1) SA 580 (CC)):** The Court struck down the customary law rule of male primogeniture in inheritance (the rule that the eldest male heir inherits the entire estate to the exclusion of female family members), finding it violated the right to equality (Section 9) and the right to dignity (Section 10). Justice Langa held that customary law must "be permitted to develop in accordance with the Constitution" and that the rule of primogeniture was "incompatible with the Bill of Rights."

- **Shilubana v. Nwamitwa (2008 (9) BCLR 914 (CC)):** The Court upheld the Valoyi traditional community's decision to appoint a woman as traditional leader (hosi), recognizing the community's own authority to develop customary law in accordance with constitutional values. This decision affirmed the "living law" (as opposed to "official customary law" codified during colonialism) approach.

- **Gumede v. President of the Republic of South Africa (2009 (3) SA 152 (CC)):** The Court struck down provisions of the KwaZulu Act on the Code of Zulu Law 16 of 1985 and the Recognition of Customary Marriages Act 120 of 1998 that discriminated against women in customary marriages contracted before the Act's commencement by denying them equal property rights.

- **Alexkor Ltd v. Richtersveld Community (2003 (12) BCLR 1301 (CC)):** The Court recognized indigenous land rights rooted in customary law, ordering restitution of diamond-mining land to the Richtersveld community. This case established that customary law rights to land survived colonial annexation and apartheid-era dispossession.

### Traditional Courts Act (2024)

After decades of debate, South Africa enacted the Traditional Courts Act in 2024, replacing the colonial-era Black Administration Act 38 of 1927 (which had regulated traditional courts under apartheid). The Act provides for the establishment and regulation of traditional courts exercising jurisdiction over customary law disputes, with important limitations: no jurisdiction over constitutional matters, criminal offenses carrying a prescribed minimum sentence, or matters involving children, sexual offenses, or domestic violence. The Act guarantees the right of women to participate in traditional court proceedings on an equal basis and the right to opt out of traditional court jurisdiction.

### Nigeria: Tripartite Legal Pluralism

Nigeria operates the most complex system of legal pluralism in the Global South, with three parallel legal traditions:

1. **Common Law/English Law:** The received English common law, doctrines of equity, and statutes of general application (as modified by Nigerian legislation) apply through the courts of record established under the Constitution. The High Courts of the states exercise general civil and criminal jurisdiction.

2. **Customary Law:** Each ethnic community maintains distinct customary law governing marriage, inheritance, land tenure, and chieftaincy. Customary Courts (in southern states) and Area Courts (in northern states) apply customary law, with appeals to the Customary Court of Appeal of each state (Constitution, Section 280). Customary law is subject to the "repugnancy test": it must not be "repugnant to natural justice, equity, and good conscience" (a standard inherited from the colonial Supreme Court Ordinance of 1914).

3. **Sharia (Islamic Law):** Applied in the twelve northern states that adopted expanded Sharia criminal codes between 1999 and 2003, and in personal law matters (marriage, divorce, inheritance, guardianship) through Sharia Courts of Appeal (Constitution, Section 275). The scope of Sharia application has been extensively litigated, as discussed in the religious personal law document.

### Customary Land Tenure

In Nigeria, the Land Use Act of 1978 (Cap L5 LFN 2004), given constitutional protection under Section 315, vests all land in each state in the Governor "in trust for the use and common benefit of all Nigerians." This Act fundamentally disrupted customary land tenure by substituting statutory rights of occupancy for customary ownership. However, in practice, customary land tenure persists extensively, particularly in rural areas where the Land Use Act's registration requirements remain unenforced. The Supreme Court in **Abioye v. Yakubu (1991) 5 NWLR (Pt. 190) 130** recognized that customary land rights established before the Land Use Act remain valid as "deemed grants" of customary rights of occupancy.

## Latin America

### Brazil: Indigenous Legal Pluralism

Brazil's 1988 Constitution broke decisively with the assimilationist paradigm of the 1973 Indian Statute (Estatuto do Indio, Lei No. 6.001/1973), which had categorized indigenous peoples on a spectrum from "isolated" to "integrated" and placed "non-integrated" indigenous persons under state tutelage. Articles 231-232 provide:

- **Article 231:** "Indigenous peoples are recognized for their social organization, customs, languages, beliefs and traditions, and for their original rights to the lands they traditionally occupy, it being incumbent upon the Union to demarcate them, protect and ensure respect for all their property."
- **Article 232:** "Indigenous peoples, their communities and organizations have standing to bring legal proceedings in defense of their rights and interests, the Public Ministry intervening in all stages of the proceedings."

The phrase "original rights" (direitos originarios) establishes that indigenous land rights predate and are independent of state recognition -- they are not granted by the state but merely recognized.

### STF Landmark: Raposa Serra do Sol

In **Pet. 3.388 (Raposa Serra do Sol, 2009)**, the STF upheld the continuous (contiguous) demarcation of the Raposa Serra do Sol Indigenous Territory in Roraima, ordering the removal of non-indigenous rice farmers. The decision established 19 conditions (salvaguardas) for indigenous land demarcation, including federal government control over environmental policy, mineral exploitation, and strategic defense within indigenous territories. Controversially, the STF in **RE 1.017.365 (2023)** rejected the "marco temporal" (temporal framework) thesis -- which would have limited indigenous land claims to areas occupied as of October 5, 1988 (the date of the Constitution's promulgation) -- holding that indigenous land rights exist independently of the date of occupation. However, Congress subsequently passed Lei No. 14.701/2023, which legislatively imposed the marco temporal, creating a direct conflict between legislative and judicial authority that remains unresolved.

### Colombia: Indigenous Jurisdiction

Colombia's 1991 Constitution is the most advanced in Latin America regarding indigenous legal pluralism:

- **Article 246:** "The authorities of indigenous peoples may exercise jurisdictional functions within their territorial ambit, in accordance with their own rules and procedures, provided they are not contrary to the Constitution and laws of the Republic."
- **Article 330:** Indigenous territories are governed by councils formed and regulated according to the uses and customs of their communities.

The Constitutional Court (Corte Constitucional) has developed an extensive jurisprudence on the scope and limits of indigenous jurisdiction through tutela decisions:

- **Sentencia T-349/1996:** Established that indigenous jurisdiction is limited by four "constitutional minimums": the right to life, the prohibition of slavery, the prohibition of torture, and the principle of legality in criminal matters.
- **Sentencia T-523/1997 (Case of Jambaló):** Upheld the Paez indigenous community's customary punishment of 60 lashes (fuetazos) as a legitimate exercise of indigenous jurisdiction, finding it did not constitute torture under the community's cultural understanding.
- **Sentencia T-921/2013:** Addressed conflicts of jurisdiction between indigenous and ordinary courts, establishing criteria for determining when a case falls within indigenous jurisdictional competence.

### Bolivia and Ecuador: Plurinational States

Bolivia's 2009 Constitution establishes the "Plurinational State" (Estado Plurinacional), recognizing indigenous campesino jurisdiction (jurisdiccion indigena originario campesina) as co-equal with ordinary jurisdiction (Article 179). The Jurisdictional Delimitation Law (Ley de Deslinde Jurisdiccional, No. 073, 2010) defines the scope and limits of indigenous jurisdiction, though critics argue it excessively restricts indigenous competence by excluding criminal, labor, agrarian, and other matters from indigenous courts.

Ecuador's 2008 Constitution similarly recognizes the plurinational character of the state and indigenous justice systems (Article 171), with the Organic Code of the Judicial Function (2009) providing implementing provisions. The Constitutional Court of Ecuador has addressed conflicts between indigenous and state jurisdiction in several cases, generally affirming indigenous jurisdictional competence in cases of intra-community disputes.

## Southeast Asia

### Indonesia: Adat Law Tradition

Indonesia has one of the richest traditions of customary law scholarship in the world, rooted in the Dutch colonial study of adat law (adatrecht). The term "adat" -- from Arabic adah (custom) -- refers to the diverse customary legal systems of Indonesia's hundreds of ethnic groups. Cornelis van Vollenhoven's monumental work "Het Adatrecht van Nederlandsch-Indie" (1918-1933) identified 19 adat law areas (adatrechtskringen) across the archipelago, each with distinct rules on land tenure, marriage, inheritance, and governance.

The 1945 Constitution, as amended in 2000, provides in Article 18B(2):

> "The state recognizes and respects adat law communities and their traditional rights insofar as they are still alive and in accordance with societal development and the principles of the Unitary State of the Republic of Indonesia, as regulated by law."

This "conditional recognition" -- alive, in accordance with societal development, consistent with the unitary state -- establishes significant limitations that the Constitutional Court (Mahkamah Konstitusi) has progressively clarified.

### Constitutional Court Jurisprudence

The MK has issued several landmark decisions on adat rights:

- **MK Decision No. 35/PUU-X/2012 (AMAN Decision):** In the most consequential adat law decision in Indonesian history, the MK ruled that customary forests (hutan adat) are not state forests. The Court struck down the word "negara" (state) from the provision in the Forestry Law (Law No. 41 of 1999) that classified customary forests as a subset of state forests, holding that adat communities hold rights to their forests independent of and prior to state recognition. This decision had massive implications for the approximately 40 million hectares of customary forest claimed by adat communities.

- **MK Decision No. 31/PUU-V/2007:** The Court addressed the criteria for recognition of adat communities, holding that the conditions in Article 18B(2) must be interpreted in a manner that protects rather than undermines adat rights, and that the "still alive" criterion should be assessed based on the community's continued existence and not solely on the maintenance of all traditional practices.

- **MK Decision No. 55/PUU-VIII/2010:** The Court reviewed the Plantation Law (Law No. 18 of 2004), affirming that criminal sanctions against individuals occupying plantation land cannot be applied to adat communities exercising traditional rights to their ancestral lands.

### Village Law (Law No. 6 of 2014)

The Village Law (Undang-Undang Desa) represents a significant advancement in adat recognition. It distinguishes between regular villages (desa) and customary law villages (desa adat), the latter defined as communities "whose unity is based on the genealogical or territorial line of descent, adat law, property rights under adat law, or adat government in their territory." Desa adat have the authority to regulate internal governance structures according to customary norms, administer customary dispute resolution, and manage communal assets including tanah ulayat (communal customary land).

Implementation has been uneven. As of 2024, only a small number of regions have formally established desa adat. The process requires provincial regulations (peraturan daerah) identifying adat communities, which most provinces have not enacted. West Sumatra (Minangkabau adat) and Bali (Balinese adat, with the desa adat/desa pakraman system) have made the most progress.

### Tanah Ulayat (Communal Customary Land)

The tension between adat land rights and state land claims is Indonesia's most contentious legal pluralism issue. The Basic Agrarian Law of 1960 (UUPA, Law No. 5 of 1960) recognized adat land rights (hak ulayat) in Article 3 but subordinated them to the "national interest" and the "higher" provisions of the law. Ministerial Regulation of the Minister of Agrarian Affairs No. 5 of 1999 (Pedoman Penyelesaian Masalah Hak Ulayat) provided guidelines for recognizing communal land claims but placed the burden of proof on adat communities and required registration with state authorities.

The 2021 Job Creation Law (Omnibus Law, Law No. 11 of 2020, replaced by Government Regulation in Lieu of Law No. 2 of 2022, subsequently ratified as Law No. 6 of 2023) modified provisions affecting adat land rights, drawing criticism from indigenous rights organizations including Aliansi Masyarakat Adat Nusantara (AMAN) for weakening protections against land conversion.

### Philippines: IPRA and Tribal Justice

The Indigenous Peoples' Rights Act (IPRA, RA 8371, 1997) creates the most comprehensive statutory framework for indigenous customary law in Southeast Asia:

- **Section 15 (Justice System):** ICCs/IPs shall have the right to use their own commonly accepted justice systems, conflict resolution institutions, peace-building processes, or mechanisms, and other customary laws and practices within their respective communities and as may be compatible with the national legal system and internationally recognized human rights.
- **Section 65 (Primacy of Customary Laws):** When disputes involve ICCs/IPs, customary laws and practices shall be used to resolve the dispute.
- **Section 66 (Judicial Systems):** ICCs/IPs shall have the right to maintain their own tribal justice systems.

The National Commission on Indigenous Peoples (NCIP), the primary government agency for indigenous affairs, adjudicates disputes involving ancestral domains and indigenous rights through its regional hearing offices. NCIP Administrative Order No. 3, Series of 2012, provides rules of procedure for ancestral domain claims, while Administrative Order No. 1, Series of 2006, establishes guidelines for free, prior and informed consent (FPIC).

In practice, indigenous customary law operates through varied structures: the Ifugao indigenous justice system uses the munhapuy (mediation) and hip'ang (council of elders) processes; the Manobo peoples use the datu system for dispute resolution; and the Muslim minorities in Mindanao apply Islamic personal law through the Code of Muslim Personal Laws (Presidential Decree No. 1083, 1977) and the Sharia courts system.

## Comparative Analysis

### Models of Legal Pluralism

Global South jurisdictions exhibit four distinct models of customary law integration:

1. **Constitutional Pluralism (South Africa, Colombia, Bolivia, Ecuador):** The constitution explicitly recognizes customary/indigenous law as a distinct legal order operating within constitutional limits, with dedicated institutional mechanisms (customary courts, indigenous jurisdiction) and constitutional court oversight.

2. **Statutory Pluralism (Nigeria, Philippines, Indonesia):** Legislation creates spaces for customary law application through designated courts, administrative bodies, or procedural rules, but customary law remains formally subordinate to statutory law and subject to repugnancy or compatibility tests.

3. **Autonomy-Based Pluralism (China, India):** Customary law operates within geographically or ethnically defined autonomous areas, with self-governing institutions exercising limited legislative and adjudicative authority under central government oversight.

4. **Reluctant Pluralism (Brazil):** The state recognizes indigenous legal autonomy in principle but has not created formal indigenous courts or jurisdictional mechanisms; customary law operates informally within indigenous communities with FUNAI (National Indigenous Foundation) mediation and state court oversight.

### The Gender Equality Tension

The most universal challenge across all jurisdictions is reconciling customary law's frequently patriarchal norms with constitutional gender equality guarantees. South Africa's Constitutional Court has been the most aggressive in striking down customary rules that discriminate against women (Bhe, Gumede, Shilubana). India's courts have been more cautious with tribal customary law (Madhu Kishwar). Colombia's approach of "constitutional minimums" attempts to balance cultural autonomy with fundamental rights but has faced criticism for cultural relativism. China's approach subsumes gender equality within the party-state's modernization project, overriding discriminatory customs through legislation rather than adjudication.

### Land Rights as the Central Battleground

Across all five regions, land tenure is the primary domain of contestation between customary and state law. Indonesia's MK Decision No. 35 (customary forests), South Africa's Alexkor (diamond lands), Brazil's Raposa Serra do Sol (indigenous territory), India's Fifth Schedule protections, and Nigeria's Land Use Act-customary tenure tension all illustrate how control over land and natural resources drives legal pluralism debates. The economic stakes -- mining concessions, plantation agriculture, infrastructure development -- ensure that customary land rights remain politically contested regardless of legal recognition.
