## Summary

Carjacking -- the forcible taking of a motor vehicle from its occupant through violence, intimidation, or threat of force -- sits at the intersection of robbery, assault, and property crime. It has attracted dedicated statutory treatment in some jurisdictions while being prosecuted under general robbery provisions in others. South Korea addresses carjacking through its graduated robbery framework in Articles 333-346 of the Criminal Act, with Article 334 on Special Robbery (teuksu-gangdo) covering armed or joint-perpetrator scenarios that commonly arise in vehicle theft by force. The United States is the only jurisdiction among those surveyed to have enacted a standalone federal carjacking statute, 18 U.S.C. Section 2119, alongside numerous state-level carjacking laws with widely varying penalties. Germany's Strafgesetzbuch contains Section 316a, a distinctive provision specifically targeting violent attacks on motorists (Raeuberischer Angriff auf Kraftfahrer) that predates the modern concept of carjacking and carries a minimum sentence of five years. The European Convention on Human Rights and the EU legal framework do not define carjacking as an autonomous offense but address it through positive obligations under Articles 2 and 3 ECHR, the Victims' Rights Directive (2012/29/EU), and the European Arrest Warrant framework that facilitates cross-border prosecution of robbery offenses.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea does not have a standalone carjacking statute. Carjacking is prosecuted under the general robbery provisions of the Korean Criminal Act (hyeongbeop), primarily Articles 333 and 334 of Chapter XXXVII. The statutory framework applies a graduated penalty system based on the manner of commission and resulting harm:

| Article | Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Art. 333 | Basic Robbery (gangdo) -- taking property by force or intimidation | 3 years or more imprisonment |
| Art. 334 | Special Robbery (teuksu-gangdo) -- robbery with deadly weapons or by 2+ persons acting jointly | 5 years or more imprisonment |
| Art. 335 | Quasi-Robbery (jun-gangdo) -- use of force after theft to retain property or escape | Same as Art. 333 |
| Art. 337 | Robbery Causing Bodily Injury (gangdo-sanghae) | Life imprisonment or 7+ years |
| Art. 338 | Robbery Causing Murder (gangdo-sarin) | Death or life imprisonment |
| Art. 339 | Robbery and Rape (gangdo-ganggan) | Life imprisonment or 10+ years |
| Art. 341 | Habitual Robbery (sangsup-gangdo) | Life imprisonment or 10+ years |
| Art. 343 | Preparation and Conspiracy for Robbery | Up to 7 years imprisonment |

### Application to Carjacking

A typical carjacking scenario in South Korea would be prosecuted under Article 333 (basic robbery) if committed by a single unarmed perpetrator, or under Article 334 (special robbery) if the perpetrator uses a dangerous weapon or acts in concert with one or more accomplices. The Supreme Court of Korea has established that the force or intimidation must be sufficient to suppress the victim's resistance (banhang eogap), assessed objectively based on the totality of circumstances.

The Act on Punishment of Specific Violent Crimes (teukjeong-beomjoe gajung-cheobeol deung-e gwanhan beopryul) provides for enhanced penalties when robbery, including vehicle-related robbery, is committed by organized crime groups or involves repeat offenders.

### Sentencing Practice

The Korean Sentencing Commission (yanghyeong-wiwonhoe) guidelines for robbery offenses direct courts to consider aggravating factors such as: vulnerability of the victim, degree of violence used, value of property taken, and premeditation. Imprisonment terms in South Korea may be either definite (up to 30 years, extendable to 50 years for aggravated cases) or indefinite.

## United States

### Federal Law

The United States is unique among the jurisdictions surveyed in having a dedicated federal carjacking statute. Enacted as part of the Anti-Car Theft Act of 1992 (Public Law 102-519), the federal carjacking provision is codified at 18 U.S.C. Section 2119.

**Statutory Text (18 U.S.C. Section 2119):**
Whoever, with the intent to cause death or serious bodily harm, takes a motor vehicle that has been transported, shipped, or received in interstate or foreign commerce, from the person or presence of another by force and violence or by intimidation, or attempts to do so, shall be punished according to a graduated penalty scale:

| Circumstance | Maximum Penalty |
|-------------|-----------------|
| No bodily injury | Up to 15 years imprisonment and/or $250,000 fine |
| Serious bodily injury results | Up to 25 years imprisonment |
| Death results | Life imprisonment or death |

**Jurisdictional Element:** Federal prosecution requires an interstate commerce nexus -- the motor vehicle must have been transported, shipped, or received in interstate or foreign commerce. In practice, virtually all commercially manufactured vehicles satisfy this requirement.

**Firearm Enhancements:** Under 18 U.S.C. Section 924(c), if a firearm is used during a carjacking, mandatory consecutive sentences apply: 5 years for possession, 7 years for brandishing, and 10 years for discharge. A second or subsequent Section 924(c) conviction carries 25 years to life.

### State Laws

Many states have enacted standalone carjacking statutes with significant penalty variations:

**New Jersey (N.J.S.A. 2C:15-2):**
- Classified as a first-degree-plus crime
- 10 to 30 years imprisonment
- Must serve at least 85% of sentence before parole eligibility under the No Early Release Act (NERA)
- Among the most severe state-level carjacking penalties in the country

**California (Penal Code Section 215):**
- Carjacking defined as the felonious taking of a motor vehicle in the possession of another, from his or her person or immediate presence, against his or her will, by means of force or fear
- Imprisonment of 3, 5, or 9 years in state prison
- Qualifies as a "strike" offense under California's Three Strikes law
- Firearm enhancements add 10 years (use) or 20 years (discharge)

**Louisiana, Michigan, Indiana, and other states** have similar standalone carjacking provisions, with penalties generally ranging from 5 years to life imprisonment depending on aggravating factors.

### Landmark Cases

**Holloway v. United States, 526 U.S. 1 (1999):**
The United States Supreme Court addressed the intent element of 18 U.S.C. Section 2119 in a 7-2 decision. The Court held that "conditional intent" satisfies the statute's requirement of "intent to cause death or serious bodily harm." A defendant who intends to kill or seriously harm the driver only if the driver refuses to surrender the vehicle possesses sufficient intent for conviction. The Court reasoned that Congress intended to criminalize the typical carjacking carried out by means of a deliberate threat of violence, not merely the rare case of unconditional intent to harm regardless of the victim's response. Justice Stevens, writing for the majority, stated that the statute's intent element is satisfied when the government proves that at the moment the defendant demanded the car, he possessed the intent to seriously harm or kill the driver if necessary to steal the vehicle.

**United States v. Randolph, 93 F.3d 656 (9th Cir. 1996):**
The Ninth Circuit upheld the constitutionality of Section 2119 under the Commerce Clause, finding that the interstate commerce nexus requirement was satisfied by the vehicle having been manufactured and shipped across state lines.

### Legislative History

The Anti-Car Theft Act of 1992 was introduced by Representative Charles Schumer (D-NY) in response to a sharp increase in carjacking incidents in major metropolitan areas during the late 1980s and early 1990s. The original statute required proof that the defendant possessed a firearm; the Violent Crime Control and Law Enforcement Act of 1994 amended Section 2119 to remove the firearm requirement and added the death penalty provision. The statute was further refined in 1996 to include the "intent to cause death or serious bodily harm" language that the Supreme Court interpreted in Holloway.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany possesses one of the oldest dedicated anti-carjacking provisions in the world. Section 316a of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), titled "Raeuberischer Angriff auf Kraftfahrer" (Violent Attack on Motorists), specifically targets attacks exploiting the vulnerabilities of road traffic.

**Statutory Text (Section 316a StGB):**

**(1)** Whoever, for the purpose of committing a robbery (Section 249), a robbery-like extortion (Section 255), or a theft with violence (Section 252), commits an attack on the life, physical integrity, or freedom of decision of the driver of a motor vehicle or a passenger, exploiting the special conditions of road traffic, shall be punished with imprisonment for not less than five years.

**(2)** In less serious cases, the penalty shall be imprisonment from one year to ten years.

**(3)** If the offender causes the death of another person through the offense, at least through negligence, the penalty shall be imprisonment for life or for not less than ten years.

### Historical Origins

Section 316a has a remarkable legislative history. Its predecessor, the "Gesetz gegen Raeuberischen Angriff auf Kraftfahrer" (Law Against Robbery Attacks on Motorists), was enacted on June 22, 1938. The statute was prompted by notorious robbery attacks carried out by the brothers Max and Walter Goetze, who used road traps and ambushes to rob motorists on German highways. The law was initially classified as a Verbrechen (serious crime) carrying a mandatory death penalty. After the fall of the Nazi regime, the provision was retained in the Federal Republic's criminal code but reformed to its current penalty structure.

### Key Elements

**Exploitation of road traffic conditions (Ausnutzung der besonderen Verhaeltnisse des Strassenverkehrs):** The offense requires that the perpetrator exploit the specific vulnerabilities arising from road traffic -- such as the driver's limited ability to defend themselves while operating a vehicle, reduced visibility, or isolation on remote roads. The Bundesgerichtshof (BGH) has interpreted this element to require a functional connection between the attack and the traffic situation.

**Attack on freedom of decision (Angriff auf die Entschlussfreiheit):** The BGH has held that the attack need not involve physical violence; an assault on the driver's freedom of decision-making suffices. This includes threats, deception that causes the driver to stop, or creating road hazards.

**Purpose offenses:** Section 316a is a purpose offense (Absichtsdelikt), requiring that the attack be committed with the specific intent to facilitate a robbery (Section 249), robbery-like extortion (Section 255), or theft with violence (Section 252).

### Typical Scenarios

German courts have applied Section 316a to a range of situations:
- Attacks on taxi drivers by passengers who intend to rob them
- Stopping motorists on highways through road blockades or simulated accidents
- Robbing truck drivers at rest stops by exploiting their vulnerability during rest periods
- Threatening ride-share drivers to seize the vehicle

### Relationship to General Robbery Provisions

Section 316a exists alongside the general robbery provisions in Sections 249-252 StGB:
- Section 249 (Raub/Robbery): Not less than 1 year imprisonment
- Section 250 (Schwerer Raub/Aggravated Robbery): Not less than 3 years (weapons, gang membership, danger to life)
- Section 251 (Raub mit Todesfolge/Robbery Resulting in Death): Not less than 10 years or life imprisonment

The significantly higher minimum sentence under Section 316a (5 years) compared to basic robbery (1 year) reflects the legislature's assessment of the heightened danger posed by attacks exploiting road traffic conditions.

## ECHR & EU

### European Convention on Human Rights

The European Convention on Human Rights does not contain criminal law provisions defining specific offenses such as carjacking. However, the European Court of Human Rights (ECtHR) jurisprudence imposes significant obligations on Convention states relevant to carjacking:

**Article 2 (Right to Life):**
States bear a positive obligation to take preventive operational measures to protect individuals whose lives are at risk from criminal acts. In cases of carjacking resulting in death, a state's failure to investigate effectively or to maintain adequate law enforcement may constitute a violation. The ECtHR has consistently held that Article 2 requires an effective official investigation when individuals have been killed as a result of the use of force (McCann and Others v. United Kingdom, 1995).

**Article 3 (Prohibition of Torture and Inhuman Treatment):**
Violent carjacking may engage Article 3 obligations. States must ensure that their criminal law frameworks provide adequate deterrence against violent crime, and victims of violent carjacking may invoke Article 3 if state authorities fail to investigate or prosecute effectively.

**Article 6 (Right to a Fair Trial):**
Persons accused of carjacking are entitled to the full panoply of fair trial guarantees, including the presumption of innocence, access to legal representation, and the right to examine witnesses. The ECtHR has addressed proportionality of sentencing for violent property offenses, though it affords states a wide margin of appreciation in determining appropriate penalties.

**Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (Protection of Property):**
This provision protects the peaceful enjoyment of possessions, creating an additional dimension to the state's obligation to provide legal frameworks that deter and punish property crime including carjacking.

### EU Legislative Framework

**Victims' Rights Directive (2012/29/EU):**
Adopted on October 25, 2012, this Directive establishes minimum standards on the rights, support, and protection of victims of crime across all EU member states. It applies directly to victims of carjacking and guarantees:
- The right to receive information about their case from first contact with authorities
- The right to interpretation and translation in criminal proceedings
- Access to victim support services, including specialized services for victims of violent crime
- The right to be heard and to participate in criminal proceedings
- Protection from secondary and repeat victimization
- Individual assessment of victims to identify specific protection needs

Victims of violent carjacking are likely to be assessed as having specific protection needs under Article 22 of the Directive, entitling them to enhanced protective measures during criminal proceedings.

**European Arrest Warrant (Framework Decision 2002/584/JHA):**
The EAW framework facilitates cross-border surrender of suspects for serious criminal offenses. Article 2(2) lists 32 categories of offenses for which the dual criminality requirement is waived, provided the offense carries a maximum penalty of at least three years in the issuing member state. "Robbery" is expressly included among these 32 listed offenses, meaning that a carjacking prosecuted as robbery can be the subject of a European Arrest Warrant without verification that the specific conduct also constitutes an offense in the executing member state.

**EU Compensation Directive (2004/80/EC):**
This Directive establishes a system of cooperation between member states for the transmission of compensation applications in cross-border situations. Victims of intentional violent crime, including carjacking, who suffer injury in a member state other than their state of residence are entitled to apply for compensation from the member state where the crime was committed.

**Organized Crime Framework:**
The EU's Strategy to Tackle Organised Crime (2021-2025) identifies vehicle theft networks as a significant organized crime threat. Europol's Serious and Organised Crime Threat Assessment (SOCTA) has consistently flagged cross-border vehicle theft and carjacking rings as priority areas, particularly along the EU's eastern borders.

### Member State Implementation

EU member states retain full competence to define and penalize carjacking under their domestic criminal law. Approaches vary:
- **France:** Carjacking is prosecuted as vol avec violence (theft with violence) under Articles 311-1 to 311-11 of the Code Penal, with penalties ranging from 5 to 30 years depending on aggravating circumstances.
- **Italy:** Rapina (robbery) under Articles 628-629 of the Codice Penale applies, with aggravated robbery carrying 5 to 20 years imprisonment.
- **Netherlands:** Diefstal met geweld (theft with violence) under Article 312 of the Wetboek van Strafrecht carries up to 9 years, increasing to 12 years with aggravating factors.

## Comparative Analysis

### Definitional Approaches

The four jurisdictions display fundamentally different approaches to defining carjacking as a criminal offense:

1. **Dedicated Carjacking Statute (United States):** The U.S. is the only jurisdiction with a federal statute specifically naming and defining "carjacking" as an autonomous offense. This reflects the distinctly American legislative response to the carjacking epidemic of the early 1990s.

2. **Motorist-Specific Robbery Provision (Germany):** Section 316a StGB does not use the term "carjacking" but specifically targets attacks on motorists that exploit road traffic conditions. This provision is both broader (covering any robbery-related attack on motorists) and narrower (requiring exploitation of traffic conditions) than the American approach.

3. **General Robbery Framework (South Korea):** Korean law treats carjacking as an application of its general robbery statutes, with Article 334 (Special Robbery) serving as the primary provision when weapons or multiple perpetrators are involved.

4. **Supranational Standards Framework (ECHR/EU):** The European framework does not define offenses but establishes procedural rights, victim protections, and cross-border cooperation mechanisms applicable to carjacking.

### Penalty Severity

Germany imposes the highest minimum sentence for a standard carjacking scenario: five years under Section 316a, compared to three years (South Korea, Art. 333) and no mandatory minimum at the federal level in the United States. However, the U.S. achieves severe outcomes through firearm enhancements under Section 924(c) and state-level penalties, particularly in New Jersey (10-30 years). For carjacking resulting in death, all three domestic jurisdictions permit life imprisonment, with both South Korea and the United States retaining the possibility of the death penalty.

### Intent Requirements

The U.S. Supreme Court's decision in Holloway v. United States (1999) established that conditional intent -- the intent to harm if necessary -- satisfies the federal carjacking statute. This relatively low mens rea threshold contrasts with Germany's requirement of specific purpose (Absicht) to commit robbery, extortion, or theft with violence. South Korea requires that force or intimidation be sufficient to suppress resistance, an objective standard focused on the nature of the conduct rather than the defendant's subjective purpose.

## Timeline

- **1938** -- Germany enacts the Law Against Robbery Attacks on Motorists following the Goetze brothers' highway robbery spree, introducing what becomes Section 316a StGB.
- **1950** -- Post-war reform of German criminal code retains Section 316a but replaces the mandatory death penalty with graduated imprisonment.
- **1953** -- South Korea adopts its Criminal Act, establishing the robbery framework in Articles 333-346 that remains the basis for carjacking prosecution.
- **1992** -- United States enacts the Anti-Car Theft Act of 1992, creating the first federal carjacking offense at 18 U.S.C. Section 2119, signed by President George H.W. Bush on October 25.
- **1993** -- New Jersey enacts one of the strictest state carjacking laws, classifying carjacking as a first-degree-plus offense with 10-30 years imprisonment.
- **1994** -- The Violent Crime Control and Law Enforcement Act amends Section 2119 to remove the firearm element and add the death penalty for carjacking resulting in death.
- **1996** -- Congress amends Section 2119 to include the "intent to cause death or serious bodily harm" element.
- **1999** -- The U.S. Supreme Court decides Holloway v. United States, holding that conditional intent satisfies the federal carjacking statute's mens rea requirement.
- **2002** -- The EU adopts the European Arrest Warrant Framework Decision, listing robbery among 32 offenses exempt from dual criminality verification.
- **2004** -- EU Directive 2004/80/EC establishes cross-border crime victim compensation.
- **2012** -- The EU adopts the Victims' Rights Directive (2012/29/EU), establishing minimum standards for the rights, support, and protection of crime victims.
