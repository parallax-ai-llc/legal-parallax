## Summary

False imprisonment -- the unlawful restraint of a person's freedom of movement without legal authority or justification -- exists at the intersection of criminal law, civil liability, and human rights protection, functioning simultaneously as a criminal offense, a civil tort, and a potential violation of fundamental rights. South Korea addresses false imprisonment through Article 276 of the Criminal Act (형법), which criminalizes unlawful arrest or confinement (체포/감금) with imprisonment of up to five years, and through aggravated provisions for confinement by persons exercising authority (Article 277, up to seven years) and confinement resulting in death or injury (Article 281, three or more years to life imprisonment); Korean law has confronted false imprisonment in high-profile contexts including the 2014 revelation of forced confinement in "salvation facilities" (구원시설) operated by certain religious organizations, and the ongoing issue of mandatory military service-related false imprisonment complaints. The United States treats false imprisonment as both a criminal offense and a civil tort, with criminal penalties varying by state from misdemeanors to felonies depending on circumstances, and civil liability allowing victims to recover compensatory and punitive damages; federal civil rights law under 42 U.S.C. Section 1983 provides a cause of action against state actors who falsely imprison individuals under color of law, with the Supreme Court addressing police-related false imprisonment in cases such as Manuel v. City of Joliet, 580 U.S. 357 (2017). Germany addresses false imprisonment through Section 239 StGB (Freiheitsberaubung), which criminalizes depriving another person of their personal liberty with penalties of up to five years imprisonment, and through Section 239a (Erpresserischer Menschenraub, kidnapping for extortion) and Section 239b (Geiselnahme, hostage-taking), which carry penalties of up to fifteen years or life imprisonment; the Federal Constitutional Court has emphasized the constitutional dimension of personal liberty under Article 2(2) and Article 104 of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz). The ECHR provides robust protection against false imprisonment through Article 5 (right to liberty and security), which exhaustively enumerates the permissible grounds for deprivation of liberty and has generated extensive case law from the European Court of Human Rights establishing that any detention must be "lawful," pursue a specified legitimate aim, and satisfy procedural safeguards including the right to challenge detention before a court, while the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights mirrors these protections in Article 6.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

**Criminal Act (형법), Chapter 31 -- Crimes Against Personal Liberty (체포와 감금의 죄):**

**Article 276 -- Arrest and Confinement (체포, 감금):**
- Paragraph 1: "A person who arrests or confines another shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than 5 years or a fine not exceeding 7 million KRW."
- Paragraph 2: "A person who arrests or confines a lineal ascendant shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than 10 years or a fine not exceeding 15 million KRW."

**Article 277 -- Aggravated Arrest and Confinement (중체포, 중감금):** "When a person commits the acts under Article 276 with violence, intimidation, or a dangerous instrument, the punishment shall be imprisonment for not more than 7 years."

**Article 278 -- Arrest and Confinement by Special Status (특수체포, 특수감금):** Addresses arrest and confinement by multiple persons acting jointly.

**Article 280 -- Attempted Arrest and Confinement:** Attempt is punishable.

**Article 281 -- Arrest or Confinement Resulting in Death or Injury (체포·감금치사상):**
- Death or injury from basic confinement: imprisonment for not less than 1 year
- Death or injury from aggravated confinement: imprisonment for not less than 3 years

### Distinction Between Arrest (체포) and Confinement (감금)

Korean law distinguishes between:
- **체포 (Arrest):** Direct physical restraint of a person's body through force, rendering movement impossible
- **감금 (Confinement):** Restriction of a person's freedom of movement to a particular area, whether through physical barriers, threats, or other means

**Supreme Court Decision 2007도1767 (2008):** The Court held that confinement does not require physical barriers -- psychological coercion or intimidation that effectively prevents a person from leaving a location constitutes confinement.

### Religious Organization Confinement Cases

Korean courts have addressed cases involving religious organizations that confined members:

**Salvation Facilities (구원시설) Cases:** Multiple religious organizations have been prosecuted for confining members in isolation facilities, often under the guise of "spiritual training" or "exorcism." Courts have consistently held that religious justifications do not negate the unlawful nature of confinement.

**Supreme Court Decision 2012도7190 (2013):** The Court upheld convictions for confinement where a religious organization confined members in a remote facility for weeks without their free consent, holding that initial consent vitiated by subsequent coercion or inability to leave does not constitute valid continuing consent.

### Psychiatric Hospitalization

Involuntary psychiatric hospitalization is regulated by the Mental Health and Welfare Act (정신건강증진 및 정신질환자 복지서비스 지원에 관한 법률), which was significantly reformed in 2016:
- Two independent psychiatric evaluations required for involuntary admission
- Periodic review by a Mental Health Review Committee
- Maximum initial involuntary admission period of 3 months

## United States

### Legal Framework

**Criminal False Imprisonment:** Defined as the unlawful confinement of a person without that person's consent and without legal authority. Elements:
1. Willful detention of another person
2. Without that person's consent
3. Without legal authority

**Civil Tort of False Imprisonment:** A common law intentional tort allowing the victim to recover damages. Elements:
1. An act by the defendant intending to confine the plaintiff
2. Actual confinement of the plaintiff
3. The plaintiff was conscious of the confinement or harmed by it

### Federal Civil Rights

**42 U.S.C. Section 1983:** Provides a cause of action against any person who, acting under color of state law, deprives another of rights, privileges, or immunities secured by the Constitution.

**Manuel v. City of Joliet, 580 U.S. 357 (2017):** The Supreme Court held that pretrial detention without probable cause violates the Fourth Amendment and can support a Section 1983 claim for false imprisonment.

**Qualified Immunity:** Government officials, including law enforcement officers, may raise qualified immunity as a defense to Section 1983 claims, shielding them from liability unless they violated "clearly established" constitutional rights (Harlow v. Fitzgerald, 457 U.S. 800 (1982)).

### State Criminal Penalties

| State | Classification | Maximum Penalty |
|-------|---------------|----------------|
| New York (PL 135.05) | Class A misdemeanor | Up to 1 year |
| California (PC 236) | Wobbler (misdemeanor or felony) | Up to 3 years |
| Texas (PC 20.02) | Class A misdemeanor; SJF if under 14 | Up to 1 year; up to 2 years |
| Florida (787.02) | Third-degree felony | Up to 5 years |
| Illinois (720 ILCS 5/10-3) | Class 4 felony | 1-3 years |

### Shopkeeper's Privilege

The "shopkeeper's privilege" or "merchant's defense" allows retailers to briefly detain persons suspected of shoplifting for a reasonable time and in a reasonable manner. This privilege exists by statute in most states and provides a defense to false imprisonment claims. Key limitations:
- Reasonable suspicion of theft required
- Detention must be for a reasonable time
- Force used must be reasonable
- Purpose must be to investigate and summon law enforcement

## Germany

### Legal Framework

**Section 239 StGB -- Deprivation of Liberty (Freiheitsberaubung):**
- Paragraph 1: "Whoever deprives a person of their personal liberty shall be punished with imprisonment for a term not exceeding five years or a fine."
- Paragraph 2: "The attempt is punishable."
- Paragraph 3: "In particularly serious cases, the penalty shall be imprisonment from one year to ten years."
- Paragraph 4: "If the perpetrator causes the death of the victim through the deprivation of liberty, imprisonment of not less than three years shall be imposed."

### Related Offenses

**Section 239a -- Kidnapping for Extortion (Erpresserischer Menschenraub):** Addresses abduction or seizure of a person to use as leverage for extortion. Penalty: not less than 5 years; life imprisonment in particularly serious cases.

**Section 239b -- Hostage-Taking (Geiselnahme):** Addresses seizing a person and threatening their life or limb to compel a third party to act. Penalty: not less than 5 years; life imprisonment possible.

**Section 240 -- Coercion (Nötigung):** While not directly addressing confinement, coercion provisions may apply where the victim's freedom of movement is restricted through threats or force. Penalty: up to 3 years or fine.

### Constitutional Dimension

**Article 2(2) GG -- Right to Personal Liberty:** "Every person shall have the right to life and physical integrity. Freedom of the person shall be inviolable. These rights may be interfered with only pursuant to a law."

**Article 104 GG -- Deprivation of Liberty:** Provides detailed procedural requirements for any deprivation of liberty:
- Paragraph 1: Only on the basis of a formal law and subject to the forms prescribed therein
- Paragraph 2: Only a judge may decide on the permissibility and continuation of any deprivation of liberty; police custody without judicial order limited to end of the day following arrest
- Paragraph 3: Person held on suspicion must be brought before a judge by end of day following arrest
- Paragraph 4: A relative or person of confidence must be notified without delay

### Key Cases

**BGH, Judgment of September 5, 2017 (4 StR 116/17):** The Court held that locking a person in a room for even a brief period constitutes deprivation of liberty under Section 239, regardless of the duration, because the offense protects the victim's potential freedom of movement.

**BGH, Judgment of March 12, 2019 (2 StR 535/18):** The Court addressed the question of confinement by deception, holding that fraudulently preventing a person from leaving (e.g., by falsely claiming the door is locked when it is not) may constitute deprivation of liberty if the victim is effectively prevented from exercising their freedom of movement.

### Civil Liability

Victims of false imprisonment may pursue civil claims under:
- **BGB Section 823(1):** Tort liability for unlawful infringement of personal liberty
- **BGB Section 823(2):** Tort liability for violation of a protective statute (Section 239 StGB)
- **BGB Section 253(2):** Pain and suffering damages (Schmerzensgeld) for deprivation of liberty

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

Article 5 of the ECHR provides the most detailed international framework for the protection against false imprisonment:

**Article 5(1):** "Everyone has the right to liberty and security of person. No one shall be deprived of his liberty save in the following cases and in accordance with a procedure prescribed by law:" followed by an exhaustive list of six permissible grounds for detention (lawful arrest, contempt of court, preventive detention, minors, infectious disease/unsound mind/vagrants, immigration).

**Engel and Others v. Netherlands (Application Nos. 5100/71 et al., Judgment of June 8, 1976):** The Court established criteria for determining whether a restriction constitutes a "deprivation of liberty" under Article 5 as opposed to a mere restriction on freedom of movement under Article 2 of Protocol No. 4: the determination depends on the "type, duration, effects and manner of implementation" of the measure.

**Storck v. Germany (Application No. 61603/00, Judgment of June 16, 2005):** The Court found violations of Articles 5 and 8 where the applicant was confined in a private psychiatric clinic for over 20 months without valid legal authorization or judicial review. The judgment established that states have positive obligations to protect individuals from arbitrary deprivation of liberty by private parties.

**Stanev v. Bulgaria (Application No. 36760/06, Grand Chamber Judgment of January 17, 2012):** The Grand Chamber found a violation of Article 5 where a person with mental disabilities was placed in a social care home without judicial authorization and without an effective remedy to challenge the placement.

**Article 5(4) -- Habeas Corpus:** "Everyone who is deprived of his liberty by arrest or detention shall be entitled to take proceedings by which the lawfulness of his detention shall be decided speedily by a court and his release ordered if the detention is not lawful."

**Article 5(5) -- Right to Compensation:** "Everyone who has been the victim of arrest or detention in contravention of the provisions of this Article shall have an enforceable right to compensation."

### EU Framework

**EU Charter of Fundamental Rights, Article 6:** "Everyone has the right to liberty and security of person." This mirrors Article 5 ECHR.

**Directive 2012/13/EU on the Right to Information in Criminal Proceedings:** Requires that persons deprived of liberty are promptly informed of their rights, including the right to challenge detention.

**Directive 2013/48/EU on the Right of Access to a Lawyer:** Guarantees access to legal counsel for persons deprived of liberty.

## Comparative Analysis

### Scope of Protection

All four systems protect against unlawful deprivation of liberty, but the scope varies:

- **ECHR (broadest):** Article 5 applies to any deprivation of liberty by state or private actors; exhaustive list of permissible grounds
- **Germany:** Strong constitutional protection (Articles 2(2), 104 GG) with detailed procedural requirements
- **South Korea:** Criminal and civil protection; reformed psychiatric hospitalization framework
- **United States:** Fourth Amendment (state actors); common law tort; Section 1983 civil rights

### State Actor vs. Private Actor

The treatment of false imprisonment by private actors varies:

- **ECHR:** Positive obligation on states to protect against private deprivation of liberty (Storck)
- **Germany:** Section 239 StGB applies equally to state and private actors; state liability under Art. 34 GG
- **South Korea:** Criminal provisions apply universally; State Compensation Act for state actors
- **United States:** Section 1983 applies only to state actors; common law tort applies to private actors

### Duration Threshold

Most jurisdictions do not require a minimum duration for false imprisonment:
- **Germany:** Even momentary confinement suffices (BGH 2017)
- **United States:** Very brief detention may suffice; reasonableness considered
- **ECHR:** Duration is one factor in determining whether Article 5 is engaged (Engel)
