## Summary

Punitive damages -- monetary awards exceeding compensatory damages, imposed to punish particularly egregious conduct and deter future wrongdoing -- represent one of the most significant divergences in civil liability philosophy among major legal systems. South Korea has gradually introduced punitive damages through a series of sector-specific statutes, beginning with the Fair Trade Act (공정거래법) treble damages provision in 2004 and expanding through the Product Liability Act (제조물 책임법) amendment of 2017 (up to three times actual damages), the Personal Information Protection Act (개인정보 보호법) amendment, and the Serious Accidents Punishment Act (중대재해 처벌 등에 관한 법률) of 2022, with up to five times damages available under several statutes. The United States is the global epicenter of punitive damages jurisprudence, with awards governed by state common law and constrained by the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment as interpreted in BMW of North America, Inc. v. Gore (1996) and State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Co. v. Campbell (2003), which established that single-digit ratios between punitive and compensatory damages are generally the constitutional maximum. Germany categorically rejects punitive damages (Strafschadensersatz) as incompatible with the compensatory principle of civil law, as affirmed by the Bundesgerichtshof in its landmark 1992 decision on the non-enforceability of American punitive damages judgments, though recent scholarly debate has questioned whether exemplary damages for privacy violations might be consistent with EU law. At the European level, the ECHR has upheld awards of just satisfaction under Article 41 that include a punitive element in cases of particularly grave state violations, while EU Directive 2020/1828 on representative actions explicitly prohibits punitive damages, and the Court of Justice of the European Union has held in Arjona Camacho v. Securitas Seguridad España (2019) that EU anti-discrimination law may require damages exceeding actual loss to serve a deterrent function.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea has undergone a remarkable shift toward punitive damages over the past two decades, moving from a purely compensatory civil law tradition to a system that now permits punitive awards in numerous statutory contexts. This evolution reflects legislative frustration with the perceived inadequacy of compensatory damages to deter corporate misconduct.

### Statutory Punitive Damages Provisions

| Statute | Year | Maximum Multiplier | Triggering Conduct |
|---------|------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Fair Trade Act (공정거래법) Art. 56(3) | 2004 (amended 2018) | 3x actual damages | Unfair trade practices, cartel damages |
| Subcontracting Act (하도급법) | 2011 | 3x actual damages | Abuse of subcontractors |
| Product Liability Act (제조물 책임법) Art. 3(2) | 2017 | 3x actual damages | Manufacturer knew of defect, failed to act |
| Personal Information Protection Act (개인정보 보호법) | 2020 | 3x actual damages | Intentional or grossly negligent data breach |
| Serious Accidents Punishment Act (중대재해 처벌 등에 관한 법률) | 2022 | 5x actual damages | Employer failure to comply with safety duties causing death/injury |
| Trade Secret Protection Act (부정경쟁방지법) | 2019 | 3x actual damages | Willful trade secret misappropriation |
| Agency Workers Act (파견근로자보호법) | 2020 | 3x actual damages | Unfair treatment of dispatched workers |

### Constitutional Considerations

The Constitutional Court of Korea (헌법재판소) has not declared punitive damages unconstitutional, distinguishing them from criminal penalties on the basis that they remain civil in nature and serve the legitimate purpose of deterrence and victim compensation enhancement. In Constitutional Court Decision 2015HunBa77 (2018), the Court upheld the constitutionality of treble damages provisions in the Subcontracting Act, holding that they do not violate the prohibition on excessive punishment under Article 37(2) of the Constitution.

### Judicial Application

Korean courts have been relatively conservative in applying punitive damage multipliers. The Supreme Court of Korea (대법원) has emphasized that the maximum multiplier represents an upper limit, not a presumptive award, and that courts should consider the degree of culpability, the financial capacity of the defendant, the severity of the damage, and the defendant's post-violation conduct in determining the appropriate multiplier.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States permits punitive damages in virtually all tort claims where the defendant's conduct is sufficiently egregious, making it the most permissive major jurisdiction for punitive awards.

### Constitutional Limitations

The Supreme Court has imposed due process constraints on punitive damages through a series of landmark decisions:

**BMW of North America, Inc. v. Gore, 517 U.S. 559 (1996):** The Court identified three "guideposts" for evaluating the constitutionality of punitive damages awards: (1) the degree of reprehensibility of the defendant's misconduct, (2) the disparity between the actual or potential harm and the punitive damages award, and (3) the difference between the punitive damages and civil penalties authorized or imposed in comparable cases.

**State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Co. v. Campbell, 538 U.S. 408 (2003):** The Court held that "few awards exceeding a single-digit ratio between punitive and compensatory damages, to a significant degree, will satisfy due process," reducing a $145 million punitive award (a 145:1 ratio) to $9 million (a 9:1 ratio). The Court also prohibited the use of out-of-state conduct to calculate punitive damages unless the conduct had a nexus to the specific harm suffered.

**Philip Morris USA v. Williams, 549 U.S. 346 (2007):** The Court held that the Due Process Clause forbids a state from using a punitive damages award to punish a defendant for harm caused to nonparties, though evidence of harm to nonparties may be considered in assessing reprehensibility.

### Antitrust Treble Damages

Section 4 of the Clayton Act (15 U.S.C. Section 15) provides that "any person who shall be injured in his business or property by reason of anything forbidden in the antitrust laws may sue therefor... and shall recover threefold the damages by him sustained." Treble damages in antitrust are mandatory, not discretionary, and have been a cornerstone of private antitrust enforcement since 1914.

### Notable Awards

Significant punitive damages verdicts include the Exxon Valdez oil spill ($5 billion, later reduced to $507.5 million by the Supreme Court in Exxon Shipping Co. v. Baker, 554 U.S. 471 (2008)), the Monsanto Roundup litigation ($2 billion verdict reduced to $86.7 million in Pilliod v. Monsanto Co.), and Johnson & Johnson talcum powder litigation ($4.69 billion verdict in Ingham v. Johnson & Johnson (2018), later reduced on appeal).

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany categorically rejects punitive damages as incompatible with the fundamental principles of German civil liability law. The compensatory principle (Kompensationsprinzip) holds that the purpose of damages in civil law is exclusively to restore the injured party to the position they would have been in absent the tortious act (Naturalrestitution under BGB Section 249), not to punish the tortfeasor.

### BGH Decision on Non-Enforcement (1992)

The Bundesgerichtshof (Federal Court of Justice) established the leading precedent in its decision of June 4, 1992 (IX ZR 149/91), holding that American punitive damages judgments are unenforceable in Germany on public policy (ordre public) grounds under Section 328(1)(4) of the Zivilprozessordnung (ZPO). The Court reasoned that punitive damages constitute a penal sanction disguised as a civil remedy, violating the German constitutional principle of separation between civil and criminal law (Trennungsprinzip).

### Pain and Suffering (Schmerzensgeld)

BGB Section 253(2) permits compensation for non-material damage (Schmerzensgeld) in cases of injury to body, health, freedom, or sexual self-determination. While German courts have increased Schmerzensgeld awards over time, these awards remain compensatory in nature. The Bundesgerichtshof has recognized a dual function of Schmerzensgeld -- compensation and satisfaction (Genugtuungsfunktion) -- but the satisfaction function has been increasingly downplayed in modern jurisprudence.

### Scholarly Debate

German legal scholarship has engaged in active debate regarding whether limited forms of punitive damages might be appropriate, particularly in the contexts of personality rights violations (Persönlichkeitsrechtsverletzungen) and data protection. The Bundesgerichtshof's "Caroline von Monaco" decisions awarded damages for media violations of personality rights at levels arguably exceeding pure compensation, though the Court characterized these as preventive compensation rather than punitive damages.

### GDPR Damages

Article 82 of the GDPR provides that "any person who has suffered material or non-material damage as a result of an infringement of this Regulation shall have the right to receive compensation." German courts have debated whether GDPR damages should include a deterrent component beyond pure compensation. The CJEU's decision in Österreichische Post AG (C-300/21, 2023) held that compensation under Article 82 is purely compensatory and does not serve a punitive function, settling the question at the EU level.

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR awards "just satisfaction" under Article 41 to injured parties when national law does not provide adequate reparation. While just satisfaction is formally compensatory, several Grand Chamber decisions have included amounts that appear to serve a punitive or deterrent function.

**Varnava and Others v. Turkey (Application No. 16064/90, Grand Chamber Judgment of September 18, 2009):** In cases concerning Turkey's failure to investigate the fate of missing persons in Northern Cyprus, the Court awarded EUR 12,000 per applicant for non-pecuniary damage in circumstances where the amounts appeared to reflect the gravity of the violation rather than purely compensatory considerations.

**Cyprus v. Turkey (Application No. 25781/94, Just Satisfaction Judgment of May 12, 2014):** The Grand Chamber awarded EUR 90 million in non-pecuniary damages in an inter-state case -- the first monetary award in an inter-state application -- in circumstances that suggested a partially punitive purpose, though the Court framed the award as compensatory.

### EU Directive 2020/1828

Article 9(3) of Directive 2020/1828 on representative actions explicitly provides that "Member States shall ensure that punitive damages... are not available in representative actions." This prohibition reflects the dominant European position that civil damages should serve a compensatory rather than punitive function.

### Anti-Discrimination Deterrent Damages

EU anti-discrimination directives require member states to provide for "effective, proportionate and dissuasive" sanctions for discrimination. The CJEU has interpreted this language as potentially requiring damages exceeding actual loss:

**Arjona Camacho v. Securitas Seguridad España (C-407/14, Judgment of December 17, 2015):** The Court held that national legislation may provide for the award of punitive damages in discrimination cases where necessary to ensure a real deterrent effect, particularly in cases involving gender-based dismissal.

**Braathens Regional Aviation AB v. Sting (C-30/19, Judgment of April 15, 2021):** The Court held that the requirement for effective and dissuasive sanctions means that compensation for non-material damage must genuinely contribute to deterring discrimination, going beyond a purely symbolic amount.

### Recognition and Enforcement

Regulation (EU) No 1215/2012 (Brussels I Recast) governs the recognition and enforcement of judgments within the EU. Article 45(1)(a) permits refusal of recognition where enforcement would be "manifestly contrary to public policy." Most EU member states invoke this provision to refuse enforcement of American punitive damages judgments, though some scholars argue that the compensatory component should be separated from the punitive component and enforced.

## Comparative Analysis

### Philosophical Foundations

The four jurisdictions reflect fundamentally different views on the purpose of civil damages:

**Compensation Only (Germany):** Damages serve exclusively to make the victim whole. Punishment is the domain of criminal law, and mixing civil and criminal functions violates the Trennungsprinzip.

**Compensation Plus Deterrence (United States):** Damages serve both compensatory and deterrent functions. Punitive damages are justified by the need to deter conduct that compensatory damages alone cannot adequately discourage, particularly where defendants' gains exceed victims' losses.

**Incremental Adoption (South Korea):** Originally adhering to the compensatory principle, Korea has adopted punitive damages in specific statutory contexts where legislative findings indicate that compensatory damages are insufficient to deter corporate misconduct.

**Limited Deterrent Overlay (EU):** While formally committed to the compensatory principle, EU law requires "effective, proportionate and dissuasive" sanctions in specific contexts, creating pressure toward supra-compensatory awards without formally endorsing punitive damages.

### Enforcement Asymmetry

The non-enforceability of American punitive damages judgments in Germany and most EU jurisdictions creates a significant asymmetry in cross-border litigation. American plaintiffs cannot collect punitive awards against defendants with assets primarily located in Europe, while European plaintiffs cannot obtain punitive damages in their home jurisdictions. This asymmetry incentivizes forum shopping and raises questions about the adequacy of deterrence for multinational corporations.

### Trend Toward Convergence

Despite doctrinal differences, a trend toward partial convergence is observable. South Korea's expansion of punitive damages reflects dissatisfaction with purely compensatory regimes. Germany's increasing Schmerzensgeld awards and the GDPR damages debate suggest growing recognition that compensation alone may be insufficient. The EU's requirement for "dissuasive" sanctions creates functional equivalence to punitive damages in certain contexts.
