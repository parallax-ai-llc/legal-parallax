## Summary

Aviation law -- the body of rules governing the operation of aircraft, the rights and duties of airlines and passengers, liability for accidents, airspace sovereignty, and the international regulatory framework for civil aviation -- is one of the most highly internationalized areas of law, reflecting the inherently cross-border nature of air travel. The foundational international instruments include the Chicago Convention on International Civil Aviation (1944), which established the International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO) and the principle of airspace sovereignty, and the Warsaw Convention (1929) and its successor the Montreal Convention (1999), which govern airline liability for passenger injury and death, baggage loss, and cargo damage. South Korea regulates aviation through the Aviation Safety Act (항공안전법), the Aviation Business Act (항공사업법), and the Airport Facilities Act (공항시설법), all substantially revised in 2017 from the former unified Aviation Act, administered by the Ministry of Land, Infrastructure and Transport (국토교통부) and the Korea Office of Civil Aviation (항공안전정책관); the crash of Korean Air Flight 801 in Guam (1997) and the Asiana Airlines Flight 214 crash in San Francisco (2013) were defining events for Korean aviation safety regulation. The United States, as the world's largest aviation market, regulates through the Federal Aviation Act of 1958 (codified in 49 U.S.C. Subtitle VII), the Federal Aviation Administration (FAA), the National Transportation Safety Board (NTSB), and the Department of Transportation (DOT), with the Airline Deregulation Act of 1978 having fundamentally reshaped the domestic industry and the Boeing 737 MAX crisis of 2018-2019 prompting major regulatory reforms. Germany applies the EU aviation regulatory framework, including the EASA Basic Regulation (EU) 2018/1139 and EU Regulation 261/2004 on passenger rights, supplemented by the Luftverkehrsgesetz (Aviation Act) and the Luftverkehrs-Zulassungs-Ordnung (Aviation Licensing Regulation), with the German Federal Bureau of Aircraft Accident Investigation (BFU) conducting independent accident investigations. At the European level, the European Union Aviation Safety Agency (EASA), established in 2002, provides comprehensive aviation safety regulation across 32 states, EU Regulation 261/2004 establishes the world's most protective passenger compensation regime, and the EU-US Open Skies Agreement (2007) liberalized transatlantic air services.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's aviation regulatory framework was comprehensively restructured in 2017 when the former unified Aviation Act (항공법) was split into three separate statutes: the Aviation Safety Act (항공안전법), the Aviation Business Act (항공사업법), and the Airport Facilities Act (공항시설법). This restructuring was intended to modernize the regulatory framework and align it with ICAO standards.

The Ministry of Land, Infrastructure and Transport (국토교통부, MOLIT) serves as the primary regulatory authority, with the Korea Office of Civil Aviation (항공안전정책관) handling day-to-day safety regulation. The Aviation and Railway Accident Investigation Board (항공·철도사고조사위원회, ARAIB) conducts independent accident investigations.

### Korean Air Flight 801 (1997)

The crash of Korean Air Flight 801 on approach to Guam's A.B. Won Pat International Airport on August 6, 1997, killing 228 of 254 persons aboard, was a watershed event for Korean aviation safety. The NTSB investigation attributed the crash to the captain's failure to properly execute a non-precision approach, inadequate monitoring by the first officer and flight engineer, and the captain's fatigue. The investigation also identified systemic cultural factors, particularly the hierarchical cockpit culture (culture of deference to the captain) that inhibited crew resource management. The disaster led to a comprehensive overhaul of Korean Air's safety culture, including restructured pilot training, adoption of Western-style crew resource management (CRM) programs, and external safety audits.

### Korean Air-Asiana Merger

In 2020, Hanjin Group's Korean Air announced plans to acquire Asiana Airlines. The merger was approved by the European Commission (with conditions) in 2024, subject to divestitures of certain route slots. The merger creates Korea's sole major full-service carrier, raising competition concerns addressed through the Korea Fair Trade Commission (공정거래위원회) and foreign regulatory approvals.

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| Operating aircraft without certification | Up to 3 years imprisonment or 30 million KRW fine |
| Violation of aviation safety standards | Up to 5 years imprisonment or 50 million KRW fine |
| Operating drone in restricted airspace | Up to 1 year imprisonment or 10 million KRW fine |
| Airline operating without business license | Up to 3 years imprisonment or 30 million KRW fine |

## United States

### Legal Framework

The Federal Aviation Act of 1958 (codified in 49 U.S.C. Subtitle VII) established the Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) and provides the comprehensive framework for aviation regulation. The Airline Deregulation Act of 1978 eliminated federal government control over fares, routes, and market entry, fundamentally transforming the domestic airline industry.

**FAA:** The FAA is responsible for regulating all aspects of civil aviation safety, including aircraft certification (14 C.F.R. Parts 21-39), pilot certification and training (14 C.F.R. Part 61), air carrier operations (14 C.F.R. Part 121), and airspace management (14 C.F.R. Part 91).

**NTSB:** The National Transportation Safety Board, an independent federal agency, investigates aviation accidents and issues safety recommendations. NTSB investigations are non-punitive, with findings inadmissible in court proceedings under 49 U.S.C. Section 1154(b).

### Boeing 737 MAX Crisis

The crashes of Lion Air Flight 610 (October 29, 2018) and Ethiopian Airlines Flight 302 (March 10, 2019), both involving Boeing 737 MAX aircraft, killed 346 people and exposed fundamental failures in the FAA certification process. Investigations revealed that Boeing had designed the Maneuvering Characteristics Augmentation System (MCAS) with inadequate safety margins and that the FAA had delegated excessive certification authority to Boeing under the Organization Designation Authorization (ODA) program.

The Aircraft Certification, Safety, and Accountability Act (Public Law 116-260, enacted December 27, 2020) reformed the FAA certification process, limiting the delegation of certification authority to manufacturers, requiring safety management systems, and establishing a new Office of Safety Oversight. Boeing agreed to pay $2.5 billion in a deferred prosecution agreement with the DOJ in January 2021.

### Passenger Rights

Unlike the EU, the United States does not mandate fixed compensation for flight delays or cancellations. The DOT's consumer protection rules (14 C.F.R. Part 250) govern denied boarding compensation (up to $1,550 for oversold flights). In 2024, the DOT finalized rules requiring automatic cash refunds for cancelled and significantly delayed flights.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's aviation regulation operates within the EU framework, combining EU regulations with national legislation. The Luftverkehrsgesetz (LuftVG, Aviation Act) provides the national statutory basis, supplemented by the Luftverkehrs-Zulassungs-Ordnung (LuftVZO, Aviation Licensing Regulation) and the Luftverkehrs-Ordnung (LuftVO, Aviation Traffic Regulation).

**EASA:** The European Union Aviation Safety Agency, headquartered in Cologne, Germany, is the central EU authority for aviation safety. Under the EASA Basic Regulation (EU) 2018/1139, EASA is responsible for aircraft type certification, design and production organization approval, and safety rule-making for all EU/EFTA member states. National aviation authorities (in Germany, the Luftfahrt-Bundesamt, LBA) implement EASA rules and conduct operational oversight.

**Accident Investigation:** The Bundesstelle für Flugunfalluntersuchung (BFU, German Federal Bureau of Aircraft Accident Investigation) conducts independent accident investigations pursuant to EU Regulation 996/2010.

### Germanwings Flight 9525 (2015)

The deliberate crash of Germanwings Flight 9525 into the French Alps on March 24, 2015, by co-pilot Andreas Lubitz, killing all 150 persons aboard, led to significant regulatory reforms. The investigation revealed that Lubitz had concealed severe depression and suicidal ideation from his employer. Regulatory responses included: EASA recommendation for the two-person cockpit rule (requiring at least two persons in the cockpit at all times); enhanced medical fitness requirements for pilots; and the establishment of pilot support programs for mental health.

### EU Passenger Rights (Regulation 261/2004)

EU Regulation 261/2004 establishes the world's most protective passenger rights regime, providing fixed compensation for flight cancellations and long delays:

| Distance | Compensation | Delay Threshold |
|----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Up to 1,500 km | 250 EUR | 2+ hours |
| 1,500-3,500 km | 400 EUR | 3+ hours |
| Over 3,500 km | 600 EUR | 4+ hours |

The CJEU's decision in Sturgeon v. Condor (Joined Cases C-402/07 and C-432/07, 2009) extended the compensation right to long delays (not just cancellations), significantly expanding the regulation's scope.

### Penalties under LuftVG

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| Operating aircraft without authorization | Fine or imprisonment up to 2 years |
| Endangering air traffic | Fine or imprisonment up to 5 years |
| Drone operation in restricted zone | Fine up to 50,000 EUR |
| Airline operating without license | Administrative fine |

## ECHR & EU

### EU Single Aviation Market

The EU Single Aviation Market, completed through successive liberalization packages (1987, 1990, 1992), permits any EU airline to operate on any route within the EU, including domestic routes (cabotage). Regulation (EC) No 1008/2008 establishes common rules for the operation of air services in the Community.

### EU-US Open Skies Agreement

The EU-US Air Transport Agreement, signed on April 30, 2007, and amended in 2010, liberalized transatlantic air services by permitting any EU or US airline to fly between any point in the EU and any point in the US. The agreement replaced individual bilateral air service agreements between the US and EU member states.

### Montreal Convention

The Convention for the Unification of Certain Rules for International Carriage by Air (Montreal Convention, 1999), which entered into force on November 4, 2003, modernized the liability regime for international air transport. Key provisions include: Article 17 (carrier liability for passenger death or injury); Article 19 (liability for delay); Article 21 (two-tier liability system: strict liability up to 128,821 SDR, presumptive liability above that amount); and Article 22 (liability limits for baggage and cargo).

### EASA Drone Regulation

The EU drone regulatory framework, established through Commission Delegated Regulation (EU) 2019/945 and Commission Implementing Regulation (EU) 2019/947, creates a comprehensive system applicable across all EU member states. Drones are classified into "open," "specific," and "certified" categories based on risk level, with corresponding requirements for registration, pilot competency, and operational limitations.

### ECtHR and Aviation

**Bosphorus Hava Yolları Turizm ve Ticaret Anonim Şirketi v. Ireland (Application No. 45036/98, Grand Chamber Judgment of June 30, 2005):** Although primarily an EU law case, Bosphorus arose from Ireland's impoundment of a Yugoslav-leased aircraft under EU sanctions regulations. The Grand Chamber established the "Bosphorus presumption" that EU law provides equivalent protection to the ECHR, applicable to measures taken by member states in compliance with EU obligations.

## Comparative Analysis

### Regulatory Structure

The jurisdictions differ fundamentally in regulatory architecture. The US centralizes aviation regulation in a single federal agency (FAA). The EU distributes authority between EASA (type certification, safety rulemaking) and national aviation authorities (operational oversight). South Korea combines ICAO-aligned national regulation with the split three-statute framework adopted in 2017.

### Passenger Rights

EU Regulation 261/2004 provides the most protective regime, with fixed compensation for delays and cancellations. The US has historically relied on market forces and limited DOT rules, though the 2024 automatic refund rule represents a step toward greater protection. South Korea's passenger rights framework relies on general consumer protection law supplemented by airline terms of carriage.

### Safety Investigation

All jurisdictions maintain independent accident investigation bodies (NTSB, BFU, ARAIB), consistent with ICAO Annex 13 requirements. The Boeing 737 MAX crisis demonstrated weaknesses in the FAA's delegated certification model, prompting reforms that may influence global certification practices.

### Market Structure

The US airline industry, deregulated since 1978, has undergone massive consolidation (four major carriers control approximately 80% of the domestic market). The EU single market permits cross-border operations but national champions remain significant. The Korean Air-Asiana merger creates a dominant single carrier, raising unique competition issues.
