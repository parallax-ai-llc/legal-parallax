## Summary

Vicarious liability -- the doctrine by which one party is held legally responsible for the wrongful acts of another, most commonly an employer for the torts of an employee committed within the scope of employment -- is a universal feature of modern legal systems but varies substantially in its theoretical foundations, scope of application, and available defenses. South Korea codifies employer liability under Civil Act (민법) Article 756, which establishes a presumption of employer fault for damage caused by employees in the execution of their duties (사용자 책임), with the employer bearing the burden of proving that they exercised due care in selection and supervision -- a defense that Korean courts have rendered virtually impossible to establish in practice. The United States applies vicarious liability primarily through the respondeat superior doctrine, holding employers strictly liable for employee torts committed within the scope of employment as articulated in the Restatement (Third) of Agency Section 2.04, with significant recent litigation focusing on the distinction between employees and independent contractors following the California Supreme Court's adoption of the ABC test in Dynamex Operations West, Inc. v. Superior Court (2018). Germany addresses vicarious liability through BGB Section 831, which formally imposes only a presumed-fault liability (vermutetes Verschulden) with an exculpation defense, creating a notable gap filled by judicial development of strict organizational liability (Organisationsverschulden) and statutory strict vicarious liability under special legislation. At the EU level, the ECHR has recognized state vicarious liability for acts of state agents under Articles 2, 3, and 8, while EU directives in employment discrimination, data protection, and product safety impose employer and organizational liability frameworks, and the Court of Justice has interpreted Directive 2000/78/EC as requiring member states to ensure employer liability for workplace harassment by employees.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's vicarious liability regime is codified in Civil Act (민법) Article 756, which provides: "A person who employs another to execute a certain task shall be liable for damages inflicted upon a third party by the employee in the execution of such task" (타인을 사용하여 어느 사무에 종사하게 한 자는 피용자가 그 사무집행에 관하여 제3자에게 가한 손해를 배상할 책임이 있다). The employer may escape liability by proving due care in selection and supervision of the employee, or that the damage would have occurred despite such care.

### Effective Strict Liability

Although Article 756 formally provides an exculpation defense, the Supreme Court of Korea (대법원) has interpreted this defense so narrowly that it is virtually never successful. In Supreme Court Decision 2006Da49995 (2009), the Court held that the employer must prove not merely that reasonable care was taken in hiring and day-to-day supervision, but that the organizational structure, training protocols, internal controls, and monitoring systems were all adequate to prevent the type of harm at issue. This effectively transforms presumed-fault liability into strict liability.

### Scope of Employment

Korean courts apply an expansive interpretation of "in the execution of duties" (사무집행에 관하여). The Supreme Court has held that this includes acts that are objectively related to the employee's duties from the perspective of external appearance (외형이론), even if the specific act was unauthorized, prohibited, or performed for the employee's personal benefit. In Supreme Court Decision 2009Da66910 (2012), the Court held an employer vicariously liable for an employee's criminal assault committed during work hours at the workplace, reasoning that the act was objectively connected to the employment relationship.

### Employer's Right of Recourse

Article 756(3) grants the employer a right of indemnity against the employee who caused the damage. However, the Supreme Court has limited this right by applying proportional allocation of responsibility, holding that the employer cannot recover the full amount paid to the victim but only a proportion reflecting the employee's relative fault (Supreme Court Decision 95Da36289, 1997). This limitation reflects the recognition that the employer bears organizational responsibility for creating the conditions in which the harm occurred.

### Corporate Officer Liability

The Commercial Code (상법) Articles 389 and 401 establish personal liability for directors who cause damage to third parties through intentional misconduct or gross negligence. Article 401-2 extends this to de facto directors (shadow directors), and Article 399 provides for joint and several liability among directors who participate in unlawful decisions.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States applies the respondeat superior doctrine as the primary basis for vicarious liability, holding employers strictly liable for employee torts committed within the scope of employment. The Restatement (Third) of Agency Section 2.04 (2006) codifies the modern formulation.

### Scope of Employment

The scope of employment analysis considers whether the employee's conduct was of the kind the employee was hired to perform, occurred substantially within the authorized time and space limits, and was motivated at least in part by a purpose to serve the employer. The Restatement (Third) adopts a broader "enterprise risk" approach, asking whether the employee's tort was a foreseeable risk of the enterprise.

**Faragher v. City of Boca Raton, 524 U.S. 775 (1998):** The Supreme Court held that an employer is vicariously liable for a supervisor's sexual harassment of a subordinate, subject to an affirmative defense that the employer exercised reasonable care to prevent and correct harassing behavior and the plaintiff unreasonably failed to take advantage of corrective opportunities. This "Faragher-Ellerth defense" applies only when the harassment does not culminate in a tangible employment action.

### Employee vs. Independent Contractor

The distinction between employees and independent contractors is critical because respondeat superior generally does not extend to the principals of independent contractors. This distinction has been the subject of intense recent litigation:

**Dynamex Operations West, Inc. v. Superior Court, 4 Cal.5th 903 (2018):** The California Supreme Court adopted the ABC test for determining employee status: a worker is presumed an employee unless the hiring entity proves (A) the worker is free from control and direction, (B) the work is outside the usual course of the hiring entity's business, and (C) the worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade. This test was codified in California Assembly Bill 5 (AB5, 2019).

**National Labor Relations Board v. FedEx Home Delivery, 563 F.3d 492 (D.C. Cir. 2009):** The court applied a multifactor "entrepreneurial opportunity" test to determine that FedEx delivery drivers were employees, not independent contractors, under the NLRA.

### Exceptions for Independent Contractors

Even where a worker is an independent contractor, vicarious liability may arise under several exceptions: non-delegable duties (inherently dangerous activities), apparent authority (the contractor reasonably appears to act on the principal's behalf), and retained control (the principal exercises significant control over the contractor's methods).

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's vicarious liability framework is notably more restrictive than those of South Korea and the United States due to BGB Section 831's exculpation defense, which has led to extensive judicial gap-filling.

### BGB Section 831 -- Presumed Fault

Section 831(1) provides: "A person who appoints another person to perform a task is liable to make compensation for the damage which the other unlawfully inflicts on a third party when carrying out the task" (Wer einen anderen zu einer Verrichtung bestellt, ist zum Ersatz des Schadens verpflichtet, den der andere in Ausführung der Verrichtung einem Dritten widerrechtlich zufügt). However, the second sentence provides an exculpation defense: liability is excluded if the principal proves that they exercised due care in selecting and, if applicable, supervising the employee, or that the damage would have occurred even with due care.

### Judicial Response: Organisationsverschulden

Because the exculpation defense under Section 831 created an unacceptable gap in victim protection, German courts developed the doctrine of Organisationsverschulden (organizational fault). Under this doctrine, the employer is liable under BGB Section 823(1) for its own negligence in organizing, supervising, and monitoring its operations. The employer's organizational fault is established when the harm results from deficiencies in the organizational structure, training, selection of supervisory personnel, or internal controls. Since Organisationsverschulden is based on the employer's own fault (not vicarious liability), the exculpation defense under Section 831 does not apply.

### Decentralized Exculpation (Dezentralisierter Entlastungsbeweis)

A significant limitation of Section 831 is the "decentralized exculpation" problem. An employer can escape liability by showing that it carefully selected a competent supervisor (Verrichtungsgehilfe), who in turn was responsible for supervising the actual tortfeasor. The supervisor's own negligence does not create vicarious liability for the employer because the supervisor is not the "appointed person" who committed the tort. This gap is filled by Organisationsverschulden and, in specific contexts, by strict vicarious liability statutes.

### Strict Vicarious Liability Statutes

Several German statutes impose strict vicarious liability without an exculpation defense:

| Statute | Scope |
|---------|-------|
| Section 31 BGB | Legal persons liable for acts of organs (Vorstand, directors) |
| Section 278 BGB | Contractual liability for auxiliaries (Erfüllungsgehilfen) |
| Section 831 BGB analogous application | Extended by courts to cover Organverschulden |
| Product Liability Act (ProdHaftG) | Producer liable regardless of fault for organizational failures |

### Employee Liability Limitation

German labor law limits employees' personal liability to employers through the doctrine of limited employee liability (beschränkte Arbeitnehmerhaftung), developed by the Bundesarbeitsgericht (Federal Labor Court). For slight negligence (leichte Fahrlässigkeit), the employee bears no liability; for ordinary negligence, liability is shared proportionally; and for gross negligence or intent, the employee bears full liability.

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR has developed extensive jurisprudence on state vicarious liability, holding states responsible for acts of their agents under the positive obligations doctrine.

**McCann and Others v. United Kingdom (Application No. 18984/91, Grand Chamber Judgment of September 27, 1995):** The Court held the United Kingdom responsible under Article 2 for the deaths of three IRA members killed by SAS soldiers in Gibraltar, establishing that the state bears vicarious liability for the use of lethal force by its security agents.

**El-Masri v. The Former Yugoslav Republic of Macedonia (Application No. 39630/09, Grand Chamber Judgment of December 13, 2012):** The Court found Macedonia vicariously liable under Articles 3, 5, 8, and 13 for the actions of CIA agents on its territory, holding that a state cannot absolve itself of responsibility by delegating functions to foreign state agents operating within its jurisdiction.

### EU Employment Law

EU anti-discrimination directives establish employer liability for workplace harassment and discrimination:

**Directive 2006/54/EC (Recast Gender Equality Directive):** Article 2(2)(a) defines harassment as unwanted conduct related to sex "with the purpose or effect of violating the dignity of a person." Member states must ensure effective sanctions and remedies, which national courts have interpreted as requiring employer vicarious liability for employee harassment.

**CJEU Decision in Centrum voor Gelijkheid van Kansen en voor Racismebestrijding v. Firma Feryn NV (C-54/07, 2008):** The Court held that an employer's public statement that it would not hire persons of a certain ethnic origin constituted direct discrimination, establishing organizational liability for discriminatory policies.

### GDPR Vicarious Liability

Article 82(1) of the GDPR provides that controllers and processors are liable for damages resulting from GDPR infringements. Article 82(3) provides an exculpation defense where the controller or processor "is not in any way responsible for the event giving rise to the damage." The CJEU in Österreichische Post AG (C-300/21, 2023) confirmed that this liability extends to the acts of employees processing personal data.

## Comparative Analysis

### Liability Standards

The four jurisdictions demonstrate a spectrum from strict to fault-based vicarious liability:

**Strictest -- United States:** Respondeat superior imposes true strict liability with no exculpation defense. The employer is liable regardless of the care exercised in hiring, training, and supervising the employee.

**Effectively Strict -- South Korea:** While formally preserving an exculpation defense, judicial interpretation has made the defense virtually impossible to establish, creating de facto strict liability.

**Formally Fault-Based, Functionally Strict -- Germany:** BGB Section 831's exculpation defense remains available, but judicial development of Organisationsverschulden and strict liability through BGB Section 31 and Section 278 substantially closes the gap.

### Scope of Employment

All four jurisdictions grapple with the boundary between acts within the scope of employment (for which the employer is liable) and purely personal acts (for which the employer is not). South Korea's "external appearance theory" (외형이론) is the broadest standard, extending liability to any act objectively related to the employment from an external perspective. The American "enterprise risk" theory is similarly broad. Germany applies a narrower "internal connection" (innerer Zusammenhang) test.

### The Gig Economy Challenge

The rise of platform-based work has challenged traditional vicarious liability frameworks across all jurisdictions. The employee/independent contractor distinction, which determines the availability of vicarious liability, is under intense pressure as jurisdictions grapple with the classification of gig workers. California's ABC test (Dynamex/AB5), the EU's proposed Platform Work Directive, and Korean courts' emerging gig worker jurisprudence all reflect this challenge.
