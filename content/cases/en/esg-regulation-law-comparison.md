## Summary

Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) disclosure regulation -- the legal frameworks mandating or guiding corporate reporting on sustainability-related risks, impacts, and performance metrics -- has undergone a transformative acceleration across major jurisdictions, driven by investor demand for decision-useful sustainability data, regulatory concern about systemic risks (particularly climate-related), and growing recognition that voluntary frameworks have produced inconsistent, incomparable, and often unreliable disclosures. South Korea introduced mandatory ESG disclosure requirements through amendments to the Capital Markets and Financial Investment Business Act (자본시장과 금융투자업에 관한 법률) and related regulations by the Financial Services Commission (FSC, 금융위원회), establishing phased mandatory sustainability reporting for KOSPI-listed companies with assets exceeding KRW 2 trillion beginning in 2025 and extending to all KOSPI-listed companies by 2030, aligned with International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) standards; the Korean Sustainability Disclosure Standards (KSDS) were finalized in 2024 drawing heavily on IFRS S1 and S2. The United States has pursued ESG disclosure primarily through the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC), which adopted final rules on March 6, 2024, requiring climate-related disclosures by public companies under the Securities Act of 1933 and the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, including Scope 1 and 2 greenhouse gas emissions for large accelerated and accelerated filers, climate risk governance, strategy, and risk management processes, and financial statement impacts of severe weather events; the rules faced immediate legal challenges and were stayed by the Eighth Circuit in April 2024, with the SEC subsequently pausing implementation pending judicial resolution. Germany implements ESG disclosure through the EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD, Directive 2022/2464), transposed into national law through amendments to the Handelsgesetzbuch (HGB) Sections 289b-289e and 315b-315e and the CSR-Richtlinie-Umsetzungsgesetz (CSR-RUG), requiring detailed sustainability reporting under the European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS) with mandatory third-party assurance. The EU's CSRD, adopted on December 14, 2022, represents the most comprehensive mandatory ESG disclosure regime globally, applying to approximately 50,000 companies including large EU companies, listed SMEs, and certain non-EU companies with significant EU activities, requiring reporting under detailed ESRS developed by EFRAG, with a phased rollout beginning January 1, 2024, for companies already subject to the Non-Financial Reporting Directive (NFRD).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's ESG disclosure regime is anchored in amendments to the Capital Markets Act (자본시장법) and implementing regulations issued by the Financial Services Commission (FSC) and the Financial Supervisory Service (FSS, 금융감독원). The approach reflects a deliberate strategy of phased implementation aligned with international standards to minimize disruption to capital markets while ensuring global comparability.

### Korean Sustainability Disclosure Standards (KSDS)

The FSC, working through the Korea Accounting Standards Board (KASB, 한국회계기준원), finalized the KSDS in late 2024, comprising two core standards modeled on the ISSB framework:

| Standard | Content | ISSB Equivalent |
|----------|---------|-----------------|
| KSDS 1 | General requirements for sustainability-related financial disclosures | IFRS S1 |
| KSDS 2 | Climate-related disclosures | IFRS S2 |

The KSDS adopts the ISSB's single-materiality (financial materiality) approach, focusing on sustainability information material to investment decisions, rather than the EU's double-materiality framework. However, Korea-specific modifications include enhanced disclosure requirements for supply chain labor practices and environmental compliance relevant to the Korean industrial context.

### Phased Implementation

The FSC announced a phased mandatory timeline:
- **2025:** Companies with assets exceeding KRW 2 trillion (approximately $1.5 billion) must file sustainability reports
- **2027:** Companies with assets exceeding KRW 1 trillion
- **2030:** All KOSPI-listed companies

Voluntary early adoption is permitted and encouraged through the Korea Exchange's (KRX) ESG information platform, which has operated since 2021 collecting voluntary sustainability disclosures.

### Assurance Requirements

Korea requires limited assurance from the initial mandatory reporting date, with a transition to reasonable assurance planned within five years of mandatory adoption. Assurance providers must be registered with the FSS, and both accounting firms and specialized sustainability assurance providers are eligible, subject to independence and competency requirements.

### Institutional Infrastructure

The Korean government has invested significantly in ESG infrastructure: the Korea Institute of Corporate Governance and Sustainability (KCGS, 한국ESG기준원) provides ESG ratings for listed companies; the National Pension Service (NPS, 국민연금공단), one of the world's largest pension funds, adopted responsible investment principles requiring ESG integration in its approximately KRW 900 trillion portfolio.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has pursued ESG disclosure through regulatory action by the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) under existing securities law authority, rather than through dedicated ESG legislation. This approach has proven legally contentious, with significant uncertainty about the scope of SEC authority over sustainability disclosures.

### SEC Climate Disclosure Rules (2024)

On March 6, 2024, the SEC adopted final rules titled "The Enhancement and Standardization of Climate-Related Disclosures for Investors" (Release Nos. 33-11275; 34-99678), requiring:

- **Governance:** Board oversight and management roles regarding climate-related risks
- **Strategy:** Material climate-related risks, including physical and transition risks, and their impact on business strategy
- **Risk Management:** Processes for identifying, assessing, and managing material climate-related risks
- **GHG Emissions:** Scope 1 and Scope 2 emissions for large accelerated filers and accelerated filers (the proposed Scope 3 requirement was dropped from the final rule due to industry opposition and measurement challenges)
- **Financial Statement Disclosures:** Costs and losses from severe weather events and natural conditions exceeding a de minimis threshold (1% of pretax income or stockholders' equity)

### Legal Challenges and Stay

The SEC rules faced immediate legal challenges from both industry groups (arguing the SEC exceeded its statutory authority) and environmental advocates (arguing the rules were too weak). In Iowa v. SEC and Liberty Energy Inc. v. SEC, consolidated before the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Eighth Circuit, the court issued a stay of the rules on April 4, 2024. The SEC subsequently issued an order staying the rules voluntarily "pending the completion of judicial review." The legal challenges raise fundamental questions about whether the SEC's authority under the Securities Act and Securities Exchange Act extends to mandating specific environmental disclosures.

### State-Level Action

California enacted two landmark ESG disclosure laws in 2023:
- **Climate Corporate Data Accountability Act (SB 253):** Requires companies with annual revenues exceeding $1 billion doing business in California to report Scope 1, 2, and 3 GHG emissions starting 2026
- **Climate-Related Financial Risk Act (SB 261):** Requires companies with revenues exceeding $500 million to prepare TCFD-aligned climate risk reports biennially starting 2026

These laws were challenged by business groups in Chamber of Commerce v. California Air Resources Board (C.D. Cal. 2024), with a federal district court issuing a preliminary injunction on certain provisions.

### Existing SEC Requirements

Even without the new rules, existing SEC requirements address certain ESG matters: Regulation S-K Item 101 (description of business), Item 103 (legal proceedings), Item 105 (risk factors), and Item 303 (management's discussion and analysis) may require climate-related disclosures when material. The SEC has brought enforcement actions for misleading ESG claims, including a $19 million penalty against Goldman Sachs Asset Management in November 2022 for ESG-related compliance failures, and a $4 million penalty against Vale S.A. in 2023 related to dam safety disclosures.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany implements ESG disclosure requirements primarily through EU directives transposed into national law. The Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) was transposed through amendments to the Handelsgesetzbuch (HGB), supplementing the existing CSR-Richtlinie-Umsetzungsgesetz (CSR-RUG) that had transposed the earlier Non-Financial Reporting Directive (NFRD, Directive 2014/95/EU).

### CSRD Transposition

Germany's transposition of the CSRD into the HGB requires covered companies to include a sustainability report (Nachhaltigkeitsbericht) as a dedicated section of the management report (Lagebericht), covering:

- Environmental matters (climate change, pollution, water, biodiversity, resource use, circular economy)
- Social matters (own workforce, workers in the value chain, affected communities, consumers)
- Governance matters (business conduct, including anti-corruption, corporate culture, lobbying, supplier relationships)

The reporting must follow the European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS) and apply the principle of double materiality, requiring disclosure of both the company's impact on sustainability matters (impact materiality) and the financial effects of sustainability matters on the company (financial materiality).

### German-Specific Implementation Features

Germany's transposition includes certain national specificities:
- The Wirtschaftsprüfer (statutory auditor) or an independent assurance services provider may provide the required sustainability assurance
- The Bundesamt für Justiz (Federal Office of Justice) enforces compliance with filing obligations and can impose administrative fines (Ordnungsgelder) up to EUR 10 million or 5% of net annual turnover
- The Abschlussprüferaufsichtsstelle (APAS) oversees the quality of sustainability assurance engagements
- BaFin (Bundesanstalt für Finanzdienstleistungsaufsicht) enforces sustainability-related disclosure for financial institutions under the EU Sustainable Finance Disclosure Regulation (SFDR)

### Supply Chain Reporting Integration

Germany's Lieferkettensorgfaltspflichtengesetz (LkSG, Supply Chain Due Diligence Act), effective January 1, 2023, creates additional ESG reporting obligations that overlap with CSRD requirements, requiring covered companies to report on human rights and environmental due diligence in their supply chains. This creates a layered reporting architecture unique among EU member states.

### Enforcement and Liability

German law creates multiple enforcement pathways: the Federal Office of Justice can impose administrative fines for non-compliance with filing obligations; BaFin can act against misleading sustainability claims by financial market participants; and private enforcement through competition law (Gesetz gegen den unlauteren Wettbewerb, UWG) allows competitors and consumer associations to challenge greenwashing claims in sustainability reports.

## ECHR & EU

### Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD)

The CSRD (Directive 2022/2464), adopted on December 14, 2022, fundamentally overhauls EU sustainability reporting. It replaces and expands the NFRD, extending mandatory reporting to approximately 50,000 companies from approximately 11,700 under the NFRD.

### Phased Application

| Phase | Effective Date | Companies Covered |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|
| Phase 1 | January 1, 2024 (reports in 2025) | Companies already subject to NFRD (large public-interest entities with >500 employees) |
| Phase 2 | January 1, 2025 (reports in 2026) | Other large companies meeting 2 of 3 criteria: >250 employees, >EUR 50M turnover, >EUR 25M total assets |
| Phase 3 | January 1, 2026 (reports in 2027) | Listed SMEs, small and non-complex credit institutions, captive insurance undertakings |
| Phase 4 | January 1, 2028 (reports in 2029) | Non-EU companies with net EU turnover >EUR 150M and at least one EU subsidiary or branch |

### European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS)

EFRAG developed 12 ESRS adopted as delegated acts by the European Commission:

**Cross-cutting Standards:**
- ESRS 1: General Requirements
- ESRS 2: General Disclosures (mandatory for all covered companies)

**Environmental Standards:**
- ESRS E1: Climate Change
- ESRS E2: Pollution
- ESRS E3: Water and Marine Resources
- ESRS E4: Biodiversity and Ecosystems
- ESRS E5: Resource Use and Circular Economy

**Social Standards:**
- ESRS S1: Own Workforce
- ESRS S2: Workers in the Value Chain
- ESRS S3: Affected Communities
- ESRS S4: Consumers and End-Users

**Governance Standards:**
- ESRS G1: Business Conduct

### Sustainable Finance Disclosure Regulation (SFDR)

Regulation (EU) 2019/2088 (SFDR) requires financial market participants and financial advisers to disclose sustainability risks and adverse sustainability impacts at entity and product levels. Financial products must be classified as Article 6 (no specific sustainability characteristics), Article 8 ("light green" -- promoting environmental or social characteristics), or Article 9 ("dark green" -- sustainable investment as objective). The European Commission is considering a comprehensive SFDR review to address concerns about the Article 8/9 classification being treated as a labeling regime despite not being designed as such.

### EU Taxonomy Regulation

Regulation (EU) 2020/852 establishes a classification system for environmentally sustainable economic activities, requiring companies subject to the CSRD to disclose the proportion of turnover, capital expenditure, and operating expenditure aligned with the taxonomy's six environmental objectives: climate change mitigation, climate change adaptation, sustainable use and protection of water and marine resources, transition to a circular economy, pollution prevention and control, and protection and restoration of biodiversity and ecosystems.

### ECHR Dimensions

The ECtHR has not directly addressed ESG disclosure, but its jurisprudence on environmental information rights under Article 8 (right to private and family life) and Article 10 (freedom of expression, including the right to receive information) supports access to corporate environmental data. In Guerra v. Italy (1998), the Court held that states have positive obligations to provide environmental information to affected communities, establishing a principle that could extend to corporate sustainability disclosures.

## Comparative Analysis

### Mandatory vs. Voluntary Approaches

The EU has adopted the most comprehensive mandatory approach through the CSRD/ESRS framework, with detailed prescriptive standards and mandatory assurance. South Korea is following a similar trajectory with phased mandatory adoption aligned to ISSB standards. The United States remains in limbo, with the SEC's attempt at mandatory climate disclosure legally challenged and no alternative federal legislation on the horizon, leaving a patchwork of state laws and voluntary frameworks.

### Materiality Paradigms

A fundamental divergence exists between double materiality (EU) and financial materiality (ISSB/US/Korea). The EU's double materiality requires companies to report on their impacts on society and the environment regardless of financial materiality to investors, reflecting a stakeholder-oriented philosophy. The ISSB/SEC approach focuses on information material to investment decisions, reflecting a shareholder-primacy orientation. This divergence creates compliance complexity for multinational companies and risks fragmenting the global sustainability reporting landscape.

### Interoperability Challenges

The ISSB and EFRAG have published interoperability guidance, but significant gaps remain. Companies reporting under ESRS will substantially comply with ISSB requirements, but the reverse is not true -- ISSB-compliant reports will not satisfy ESRS double materiality requirements. This asymmetry creates particular challenges for Korean and American companies with EU operations.

### Enforcement Intensity

Enforcement intensity varies dramatically. The EU framework relies on member state transposition with varying penalties, but the combination of mandatory assurance, filing requirements, and greenwashing enforcement through competition law and consumer protection creates a multi-layered enforcement architecture. The SEC has enforcement authority but faces legal uncertainty about the scope of its mandate. South Korea's FSC has robust enforcement powers but is adopting a gradual approach to encourage compliance.

### Scope 3 Emissions

The treatment of Scope 3 (value chain) emissions remains the most contentious disclosure issue. The EU (ESRS E1) and California (SB 253) require Scope 3 reporting; the SEC dropped it from its final rules; and Korea is phasing it in. Given that Scope 3 represents 70-90% of most companies' total emissions, this divergence has significant implications for the completeness and comparability of climate disclosures.
