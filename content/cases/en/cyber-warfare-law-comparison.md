## Summary

Cyber warfare -- the use of digital attacks by state or state-sponsored actors against another nation's information systems, critical infrastructure, or military networks for the purpose of espionage, disruption, or destruction -- represents one of the most rapidly evolving and legally unsettled domains of modern conflict, where traditional concepts of sovereignty, use of force, and armed attack are being adapted to a fundamentally new operational environment. South Korea, facing persistent and sophisticated cyber threats from North Korea, has developed a comprehensive cyber defense framework through the National Cybersecurity Strategy (국가사이버안보전략, 2019), the Act on the Protection of Information and Communications Infrastructure (정보통신기반보호법), and the National Intelligence Service Act (국가정보원법), with the Cyber Operations Command (사이버작전사령부) of the ROK Armed Forces serving as the primary military cyber operations entity; North Korean cyber attacks, including the 2013 DarkSeoul attacks on Korean banks and broadcasters and the ongoing campaigns attributed to the Lazarus Group, have driven continuous legal and institutional adaptation. The United States has developed the most extensive national cyber warfare framework, with the Department of Defense Cyber Strategy, U.S. Cyber Command (USCYBERCOM) as a unified combatant command since 2018, the doctrine of "defend forward" and "persistent engagement" articulated by General Paul Nakasone, statutory authorities under Title 10 (military operations) and Title 50 (intelligence activities), and the 2018 National Defense Authorization Act Section 1642 authorizing clandestine military cyber operations as traditional military activities. Germany has established the Cyber- und Informationsraum (CIR -- Cyber and Information Domain Service) as a sixth branch of the Bundeswehr since 2017, with legal authority derived from the Grundgesetz provisions on defense and constrained by the Parlamentsvorbehalt (parliamentary reservation) requiring Bundestag approval for armed forces deployments, raising novel questions about whether offensive cyber operations constitute "deployment" within the meaning of the Federal Constitutional Court's jurisprudence. At the international level, the Tallinn Manual on the International Law Applicable to Cyber Operations (2013, 2017) provides the most comprehensive academic articulation of how existing international law applies to cyber operations, while the UN Group of Governmental Experts (GGE) 2013 and 2015 reports affirmed that international law, including the UN Charter, applies to cyberspace, and NATO has recognized cyberspace as an operational domain since 2016.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's cyber defense framework has been shaped primarily by the persistent and escalating threat from North Korea, which maintains one of the world's most active state-sponsored cyber warfare programs.

**Act on the Protection of Information and Communications Infrastructure (정보통신기반보호법):** Enacted in 2001 and repeatedly amended, this Act designates critical infrastructure systems requiring enhanced cybersecurity protection, establishes vulnerability assessment requirements, and provides for incident response coordination through the Korea Internet & Security Agency (KISA, 한국인터넷진흥원).

**National Cybersecurity Strategy (국가사이버안보전략, 2019):** This strategy document establishes six strategic tasks: strengthening cybersecurity of national core infrastructure, enhancing cyber attack response capabilities, building a trust-based governance system, establishing a cybersecurity industry ecosystem, fostering a cybersecurity culture, and leading international cooperation.

**National Intelligence Service Act (국가정보원법):** The NIS serves as the lead agency for national cyber intelligence and coordinates cyber threat response at the strategic level. The NIS Cybersecurity Center monitors threats to government networks and critical infrastructure.

### Military Cyber Operations

The ROK Cyber Operations Command (사이버작전사령부), established in 2010 and elevated to a direct-reporting command in 2019, is responsible for military cyber defense and operations. The command operates under the Joint Chiefs of Staff (합동참모본부) and coordinates with USCYBERCOM through the U.S.-ROK alliance framework.

| Violation | Penalty |

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has developed the most sophisticated and extensive legal framework for military cyber operations, structured across multiple statutory authorities.

**Title 10 (Armed Forces):** Traditional military cyber operations are authorized under the same Title 10 authorities that govern kinetic military operations. Section 394 of Title 10 (added by NDAA FY2019) establishes that clandestine military activities in cyberspace conducted to prepare the environment for military operations constitute "traditional military activities" not subject to the covert action reporting requirements of Title 50.

**Title 50 (Intelligence):** Cyber intelligence operations conducted by the NSA, CIA, and other intelligence community elements operate under Title 50 authorities, including presidential findings for covert action and FISA for domestic surveillance authorities.

**Executive Authority:** National Security Presidential Memorandum 13 (NSPM-13, 2018) streamlined the presidential authorization process for offensive cyber operations, delegating certain authorities to the Secretary of Defense and reducing interagency review requirements.

### U.S. Cyber Command (USCYBERCOM)

USCYBERCOM was established as a sub-unified command in 2009 and elevated to a full unified combatant command on May 4, 2018. The command is dual-hatted with the National Security Agency (NSA), with the commander serving simultaneously as NSA Director.

**"Defend Forward" Doctrine:** Articulated by General Paul Nakasone, the doctrine of "persistent engagement" and "defend forward" authorizes USCYBERCOM to conduct operations outside U.S. networks to disrupt adversary cyber activities before they reach American systems. This proactive approach has been applied in operations against Russian interference (2018 midterm elections), ISIS communications infrastructure, and Iranian military networks.

### Cyber Operations Authorization

| Authority | Scope | Oversight |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| NSPM-13 (2018) | Streamlined offensive cyber operations | SecDef/NSC process; reduced interagency review |
| NDAA Section 1642 (2018) | Clandestine operations as traditional military activities | Congressional Armed Services Committees |
| Title 50 covert action | Intelligence cyber operations | Congressional Intelligence Committees; presidential finding required |
| Standing Rules of Engagement | Defensive cyber operations | Commander authorities; no additional authorization required |

### Notable Operations

**Stuxnet (attributed, 2010):** The computer worm that damaged approximately 1,000 Iranian uranium enrichment centrifuges at the Natanz facility, widely attributed to a joint U.S.-Israeli operation (code-named "Olympic Games"), represented the first known use of a cyber weapon to cause physical destruction of critical infrastructure.

**2018 Midterm Election Defense:** USCYBERCOM conducted operations to disrupt Russian Internet Research Agency activities targeting U.S. elections, the first acknowledged offensive cyber operation for election defense purposes.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's cyber warfare framework operates within the constitutional constraints of the Grundgesetz and the requirement for parliamentary authorization of military deployments.

**Cyber- und Informationsraum (CIR):** Established on April 1, 2017, as the Bundeswehr's sixth organizational area (alongside Army, Navy, Air Force, Joint Support Service, and Joint Medical Service), the CIR comprises approximately 14,500 personnel as of 2025. The CIR is responsible for military IT operations, cyber defense, geoinformation, electronic warfare, and information operations.

**IT-Sicherheitsgesetz 2.0 (IT Security Act 2.0, 2021):** This Act expanded the Bundesamt für Sicherheit in der Informationstechnik (BSI -- Federal Office for Information Security) authorities, introducing mandatory cybersecurity certification for critical infrastructure operators, expanded reporting obligations, and the authority to detect and respond to malicious software on German networks.

**Parlamentsvorbehalt (Parliamentary Reservation):** The Federal Constitutional Court's landmark decision in BVerfGE 90, 286 (1994) established that any deployment of the Bundeswehr "armed forces" requires prior Bundestag approval. Whether offensive cyber operations constitute a "deployment" triggering this requirement remains legally unresolved. The Bundestag's Parliamentary Commissioner for the Armed Forces (Wehrbeauftragter) has called for clarification.

### Bundesamt für Sicherheit in der Informationstechnik (BSI)

The BSI, established under the BSI-Gesetz (BSI Act), serves as the national cybersecurity authority for civilian critical infrastructure protection. The BSI operates the Computer Emergency Response Team for the Federal Administration (CERT-Bund) and publishes the annual "Die Lage der IT-Sicherheit in Deutschland" report assessing the national cyber threat landscape.

### Offensive Cyber Capabilities

Germany's development of offensive cyber capabilities has been characterized by legal caution and institutional complexity. The Zentrale Stelle für Informationstechnik im Sicherheitsbereich (ZITiS -- Central Office for Information Technology in the Security Sector), established in 2017, develops tools and capabilities for federal security agencies. However, the use of offensive cyber tools by intelligence agencies (BND, BfV) for intelligence purposes versus their use by the Bundeswehr for military operations involves different legal authorities and oversight mechanisms.

### Key Incidents

Germany has been the target of significant state-sponsored cyber attacks:
- **Bundestag hack (2015):** Attribution to Russian GRU (APT28/Fancy Bear); compromise of the parliamentary IT network, including the email account of Chancellor Merkel
- **Federal Foreign Office breach (2017-2018):** Attribution to Russian group Turla/Snake
- **Hospital attack, Düsseldorf (2020):** Ransomware attack on Düsseldorf University Hospital, the first cyber attack believed to have contributed to a patient death

## ECHR & EU / International

### Tallinn Manual

The Tallinn Manual on the International Law Applicable to Cyber Operations, produced by an international group of experts under NATO CCDCOE auspices, provides the most comprehensive academic analysis of how existing international law applies to cyber operations.

**Tallinn Manual 1.0 (2013):** Focused on cyber operations during armed conflict, addressing jus ad bellum (when cyber operations constitute a use of force or armed attack) and jus in bello (how international humanitarian law applies to cyber operations during armed conflict).

**Tallinn Manual 2.0 (2017):** Expanded analysis to peacetime cyber operations, addressing sovereignty, due diligence, jurisdiction, state responsibility, and human rights law in cyberspace.

Key principles from the Manual:
- A cyber operation constitutes a "use of force" under Article 2(4) UN Charter if its scale and effects are comparable to those of a kinetic use of force
- A cyber operation can constitute an "armed attack" triggering the right of self-defense under Article 51 if its effects are comparable to those of a kinetic armed attack
- States have sovereignty over cyber infrastructure within their territory
- International humanitarian law principles of distinction, proportionality, and precaution apply to cyber operations during armed conflict

### UN Group of Governmental Experts (GGE)

The UN GGE on Developments in the Field of Information and Telecommunications in the Context of International Security has produced consensus reports affirming the applicability of international law to cyberspace:

- **2013 Report:** Affirmed that international law, and in particular the UN Charter, is applicable to the use of ICTs by states
- **2015 Report:** Identified 11 norms of responsible state behavior in cyberspace, including that states should not conduct or knowingly support ICT activities contrary to their obligations under international law
- **2021 Report:** Reaffirmed and elaborated on previous findings; established the continuing validity of international law in cyberspace

### NATO Cyber Defence

NATO recognized cyberspace as an operational domain at the Warsaw Summit in July 2016, alongside land, sea, air, and space. The Cyber Defence Pledge, adopted at the same summit, commits allies to enhancing their national cyber defenses. NATO's Cooperative Cyber Defence Centre of Excellence (CCDCOE), established in Tallinn, Estonia in 2008, conducts research, training, and exercises including the annual Locked Shields exercise.

Article 5 (collective defense) can be invoked in response to cyber attacks. NATO's 2022 Strategic Concept affirms that "cyber space is contested at all times" and that "the impact of significant malicious cumulative cyber activities might, in certain circumstances, be considered as amounting to an armed attack."

### EU NIS2 Directive

Directive (EU) 2022/2555 (NIS2 Directive), applicable from October 18, 2024, significantly strengthens cybersecurity requirements across the EU, expanding the scope of covered entities, imposing stricter incident reporting obligations (24-hour initial notification), requiring supply chain risk assessments, and establishing administrative fines up to 10 million EUR or 2% of global annual turnover. The Directive enhances the mandate of ENISA (EU Agency for Cybersecurity) and establishes the EU Cyber Crisis Liaison Organisation Network (CyCLONe).

### Ransomware as a National Security Threat

Ransomware attacks -- while often criminal rather than military in nature -- increasingly blur the line between cybercrime and cyber warfare:

**South Korea:** The Korea Internet & Security Agency (KISA) reported a significant increase in ransomware attacks targeting Korean businesses and government agencies. The 2021 attack on Kia Motors (attributed to a ransomware group with possible North Korean links) highlighted the intersection of cybercrime and state-sponsored activity.

**United States:** The Colonial Pipeline attack (May 2021) and JBS Foods attack (June 2021) prompted the creation of the Ransomware and Digital Extortion Task Force within the DOJ. Executive Order 14028 (May 2021) on improving the nation's cybersecurity addressed supply chain security, information sharing, and incident response. The 2022 Cyber Incident Reporting for Critical Infrastructure Act (CIRCIA) mandated reporting of significant cyber incidents to CISA within 72 hours.

**Germany:** The Düsseldorf University Hospital attack (September 2020), which was initially considered the first ransomware attack to cause a death (a patient was diverted to another hospital and died during transport), catalyzed German policy action. The BSI has classified ransomware as the top cyber threat in its annual reports since 2021.

**International:** The Counter Ransomware Initiative, a U.S.-led coalition of over 40 countries including South Korea and Germany, coordinates international responses to ransomware, including information sharing, deterrence, and efforts to counter illicit cryptocurrency flows used for ransom payments.

### Cyber Operations and International Humanitarian Law

The application of international humanitarian law (IHL) to cyber operations during armed conflict raises unresolved questions:

| IHL Principle | Application to Cyber Operations | Status |
|--------------|--------------------------------|--------|
| Distinction | Must distinguish between military and civilian cyber infrastructure | Tallinn Manual: yes, but dual-use infrastructure creates challenges |
| Proportionality | Civilian harm from cyber attacks must not be excessive relative to military advantage | Difficult to assess when effects are uncertain |
| Precaution | Feasible precautions must be taken to minimize civilian cyber harm | Requires intelligence about interconnected systems |
| Neutrality | Neutral state cyber infrastructure must be respected | Frequently violated in practice; routing through neutral states common |

## Comparative Analysis

### Legal Authority for Offensive Operations

The jurisdictions exhibit dramatically different approaches to authorizing offensive cyber operations. The United States has progressively streamlined authorization through NSPM-13 and NDAA provisions, enabling rapid response. Germany faces constitutional constraints requiring parliamentary authorization for military deployments, creating uncertainty about offensive cyber operations. South Korea's authorities are largely classified but operate within the alliance framework. International law provides no clear threshold for when a cyber operation constitutes a "use of force" or "armed attack."

### Attribution and Response

Attribution of cyber attacks to state actors remains a fundamental legal and technical challenge. The United States has adopted an aggressive public attribution policy, including criminal indictments of foreign military and intelligence personnel (e.g., GRU officers indicted in 2018 for election interference). Germany and South Korea have been more cautious in public attribution, reflecting smaller intelligence capabilities and diplomatic considerations. The absence of agreed international attribution standards creates ambiguity about when responsive measures are legally justified.

### Civil-Military Coordination

All three national jurisdictions face the challenge of coordinating military, intelligence, and civilian cybersecurity authorities operating under different legal frameworks. The U.S. dual-hatting of USCYBERCOM and NSA facilitates but also complicates this coordination. Germany's creation of ZITiS alongside CIR and BSI creates institutional complexity. South Korea's coordination operates through the National Intelligence Service's cross-cutting role.
