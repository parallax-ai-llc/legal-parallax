## Summary

Agency law governs the legal relationship in which one person (the agent) acts on behalf of another (the principal) to create legal relations with third parties, addressing fundamental questions of authority, liability, fiduciary duties, and the binding effect of an agent's actions on the principal that arise in virtually every area of commercial and civil life. South Korea regulates agency through the Korean Civil Code (민법) Articles 114-136 (대리, agency/representation) and the Korean Commercial Code (상법) provisions on commercial agency (상사대리), including specific rules for brokers (중개인, Articles 93-100), commission agents (위탁매매인, Articles 101-113), and forwarding agents (운송주선인, Articles 114-124); the distinction between direct agency (직접대리), where the agent acts in the principal's name, and indirect agency (간접대리), where the agent acts in their own name but for the principal's account, mirrors the civil law tradition's approach to representation. The United States governs agency through the common law as restated in the Restatement (Third) of Agency (2006), which recognizes actual authority (express and implied), apparent authority, estoppel, and ratification as bases for binding the principal, supplemented by specific statutory frameworks for securities broker-dealers, insurance agents, real estate brokers, and commercial agents. Germany codifies agency in the BGB under the concept of Stellvertretung (representation, Sections 164-181), distinguishing sharply between the external authority (Vollmacht) and the internal relationship (Grundverhältnis) between principal and agent, while commercial agency is regulated under the Handelsgesetzbuch (HGB) Sections 84-92c (Handelsvertreter), which implements EU Commercial Agents Directive 86/653/EEC, providing agents with significant protections including a mandatory goodwill indemnity (Ausgleichsanspruch) upon termination. The EU's primary contribution to agency law is the Commercial Agents Directive 86/653/EEC, which harmonizes the rights and obligations of self-employed commercial agents across member states, with the CJEU developing extensive jurisprudence on the indemnity/compensation right, the scope of the agent's market, and the non-derogability of agent protections.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's agency law is codified in both the Civil Code and the Commercial Code, following the civil law tradition of distinguishing between general civil agency and specific forms of commercial agency.

**Civil Code (민법) -- Agency/Representation (대리):** Articles 114-136 establish the general framework for agency relationships. Article 114 provides that a juridical act performed by an agent within the scope of authority in the name of the principal takes effect directly for and against the principal. Article 115 states that the validity of the agent's act is not affected by the agent's limited capacity, reflecting the principle that it is the principal's legal sphere that is affected. Article 116 addresses the question of the agent's knowledge or willful conduct, providing that the principal is affected by the agent's knowledge or negligence in performing the authorized act.

### Types of Authority

**Express Authority (명시적 수권):** The principal explicitly grants authority to the agent through a power of attorney (위임장) or other authorization. Article 117 provides that an unauthorized agent's act does not bind the principal, but the principal may ratify the act under Article 130 (추인).

**Apparent Authority (표견대리):** Korean law recognizes apparent authority through three statutory provisions: Article 125 (agent who exceeds scope of authority where there are grounds for the third party to believe the agent has authority); Article 126 (agent whose authority has been terminated but the third party did not know of termination); and Article 129 (agent who was entrusted with specific authority and the third party reasonably believed the agent had broader authority).

### Commercial Agency under the Commercial Code

The Korean Commercial Code provides specific rules for three types of commercial agents:

| Agent Type | Articles | Key Features |
|-----------|----------|--------------|
| Broker (중개인) | Articles 93-100 | Mediates transactions without authority to conclude on behalf of parties; entitled to commission from both parties unless agreed otherwise |
| Commission agent (위탁매매인) | Articles 101-113 | Acts in own name but for principal's account; acquires rights and obligations from third-party transactions |
| Forwarding agent (운송주선인) | Articles 114-124 | Arranges carriage of goods for account of principal; may issue own transport document |

### Mandate (위임) and Employment Agency

The Civil Code also governs agency through the mandate contract (위임, Articles 680-692), which establishes the internal relationship between principal and agent. Article 681 imposes on the agent a duty of care equivalent to that of a good manager (선관주의의무, duty of care of a good manager). Article 684 requires the agent to deliver to the principal any money, property, or benefits received in connection with the mandate. Article 689 permits either party to terminate the mandate at any time, subject to liability for damages caused by termination at an inopportune time.

### Recent Developments

South Korean courts have increasingly addressed agency issues in the context of digital commerce, platform economy relationships, and franchise systems. The Supreme Court has examined whether delivery platform workers, franchisees, and online marketplace sellers act as agents of the platforms they operate through, with implications for labor law, consumer protection, and tort liability.

## United States

### Legal Framework

American agency law is primarily a common law doctrine, systematically stated in the Restatement (Third) of Agency (2006), published by the American Law Institute. The Restatement is not binding law but is highly influential and widely cited by courts across all jurisdictions.

**Bases of Authority:** The Restatement recognizes four principal bases on which an agent's act may bind the principal:

**Actual Authority (Section 2.01):** Arises when the agent reasonably believes, based on the principal's manifestations to the agent, that the principal wishes the agent to act. Actual authority includes express authority (explicitly communicated) and implied authority (incidental to express authority or customary in the agent's position).

**Apparent Authority (Section 2.03):** Arises when a third party reasonably believes the actor has authority to act on behalf of the principal, based on the principal's manifestations to the third party. This doctrine protects third parties who deal with agents in reasonable reliance on the appearance of authority created by the principal.

**Estoppel (Section 2.05):** The principal may be bound where the principal's negligence or intentional conduct creates an appearance of authority and the third party detrimentally relies on that appearance.

**Ratification (Section 4.01):** The principal may retroactively authorize an unauthorized act by manifesting assent to be bound by it or by conduct inconsistent with treating the act as unauthorized.

### Fiduciary Duties

Agency creates a fiduciary relationship imposing on the agent duties of loyalty (Section 8.01), performance (Section 8.08), obedience (Section 8.09), disclosure (Section 8.11), and accounting (Section 8.12). The duty of loyalty prohibits the agent from acting for the agent's own benefit or for the benefit of another in matters connected with the agency relationship, acquiring a material benefit from a third party in connection with agency transactions, and using or communicating confidential information of the principal for the agent's own purposes or those of a third party.

### Undisclosed Principal Doctrine

A distinctive feature of American agency law is the fully developed undisclosed principal doctrine. Under Section 2.06 of the Restatement, an undisclosed principal is bound by the agent's authorized acts even though the third party is unaware of the principal's existence. Conversely, the undisclosed principal may enforce the contract against the third party, subject to certain limitations protecting the third party's reasonable expectations.

### Statutory Agency Frameworks

Federal and state statutes create specific agency regimes for regulated industries:

| Industry | Governing Framework |
|----------|-------------------|
| Securities broker-dealers | Securities Exchange Act Section 15; FINRA rules |
| Insurance agents | State insurance codes; distinction between agents and brokers |
| Real estate agents | State real estate licensing laws; fiduciary duty variations |
| Talent agents | State talent agency acts (e.g., California Labor Code Sections 1700-1700.47) |

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German agency law is characterized by the Abstraktionsprinzip (principle of abstraction), which sharply separates the external authority (Vollmacht) from the internal relationship (Grundverhältnis) between principal and agent.

**BGB Sections 164-181 -- Stellvertretung (Representation):** Section 164(1) provides that a declaration of intent made by an agent within the scope of authority in the name of the principal takes effect directly for and against the principal. The fundamental principle is that the agent must act "in the name of" the principal (Offenkundigkeitsprinzip -- disclosure principle); undisclosed agency is not recognized under the Stellvertretung framework.

**Vollmacht (Power of Attorney, Sections 166-176):** The Vollmacht is the external authorization that the principal grants to the agent. Under the Abstraktionsprinzip, the Vollmacht exists independently of the internal relationship (which may be a mandate, employment contract, or other legal relationship). This means that the external authority may persist even after the internal relationship has terminated, unless the Vollmacht is separately revoked.

### Types of Commercial Agency

**Handelsvertreter (Commercial Agent, HGB Sections 84-92c):** A self-employed person who is permanently entrusted with negotiating or concluding transactions on behalf of another entrepreneur. The HGB provisions, which implement EU Directive 86/653/EEC, provide comprehensive regulation:

| Provision | Content |
|-----------|---------|
| Section 86 | Agent's duties: diligently pursue transactions; observe principal's interests |
| Section 86a | Principal's duties: provide agent with necessary documentation and information |
| Section 87 | Commission entitlement: agent entitled to commission on transactions within assigned territory or customer base |
| Section 89 | Termination notice periods: minimum 1 month (first year), 2 months (second year), 3 months (third year and beyond) |
| Section 89b | Ausgleichsanspruch (goodwill indemnity): mandatory compensation upon termination for new customers brought by agent or substantially expanded business |

### Ausgleichsanspruch (Section 89b HGB)

The goodwill indemnity is a cornerstone of German commercial agency law. Section 89b(1) provides that upon termination of the agency contract, the agent is entitled to an indemnity if and to the extent that the agent has brought new customers or significantly increased the volume of business with existing customers, the principal continues to derive substantial benefits from business with such customers after termination, and the payment of indemnity is equitable having regard to all circumstances. The indemnity is capped at one year's average annual commission calculated over the last five years (or the contract duration if shorter). This right cannot be contractually excluded before the contract expires (Section 89b(4)).

### Insichgeschäft (Self-Dealing, Section 181 BGB)

Section 181 BGB prohibits self-dealing by agents: an agent may not, unless otherwise permitted, enter into a transaction in the name of the principal with themselves (in their own name or as agent of a third party). This prohibition applies unless the principal has expressly permitted the self-dealing or the transaction consists solely in the performance of an existing obligation.

## ECHR & EU

### Commercial Agents Directive 86/653/EEC

Council Directive 86/653/EEC of December 18, 1986, on the coordination of the laws of the member states relating to self-employed commercial agents, is the principal EU instrument on agency law. The Directive harmonizes the rights and obligations of commercial agents and their principals across the EU.

**Scope (Article 1):** The Directive applies to self-employed intermediaries who have continuing authority to negotiate or conclude the sale or purchase of goods on behalf of their principals. It does not apply to agents whose activities are unpaid, commercial agents operating on commodity exchanges or in commodity markets, or Crown agents.

**Agent's Obligations (Article 3):** The agent must look after the principal's interests and act dutifully and in good faith, make proper efforts to negotiate and conclude transactions, communicate necessary information to the principal, and comply with reasonable instructions.

**Principal's Obligations (Article 4):** The principal must act dutifully and in good faith, provide the agent with necessary documentation, inform the agent of acceptance, refusal, or non-execution of transactions, and notify the agent when the volume of transactions is likely to be significantly lower than expected.

### Indemnity versus Compensation

Article 17 of the Directive offers member states a choice between two systems for compensating agents upon termination:

**Indemnity System (Article 17(2)):** The agent is entitled to an indemnity if the agent has brought new customers or significantly increased the volume of business with existing customers, the principal continues to benefit from such business, and the indemnity is equitable. Maximum: one year's average annual remuneration. Adopted by Germany, Austria, the Netherlands, and most other member states.

**Compensation System (Article 17(3)):** The agent is entitled to compensation for damage suffered as a result of termination, including loss of commission and failure to amortize costs and expenses incurred on the principal's advice. Adopted by France, Ireland, and the United Kingdom (pre-Brexit).

### CJEU Landmark Decisions

**Ingmar GB Ltd v. Eaton Leonard Technologies Inc. (Case C-381/98, 2000):** The Court held that the mandatory provisions of the Directive regarding the agent's indemnity/compensation right apply even where the agency contract designates the law of a non-member state as the governing law, provided the agent carries on activities within a member state. This decision established the overriding mandatory character of the Directive's protective provisions.

**Honyvem Informazioni Commerciali Srl v. Mariella De Zotti (Case C-465/04, 2006):** The Court ruled that the indemnity under Article 17(2) and damages under general law are cumulative, not alternative -- the agent may claim the indemnity and additionally claim damages for the principal's failure to observe the notice period.

**Marchon Germany GmbH v. Yvonne Karaszkiewicz (Case C-315/14, 2016):** The Court clarified that the Directive applies to agents who negotiate and conclude contracts on behalf of their principal, regardless of whether the agent is described as an "agent" or given another designation, provided the substantive criteria of Article 1 are met.

**Unamar NV v. Navigation Maritime Bulgare (Case C-184/12, 2013):** The Court held that national rules implementing the Directive that provide more extensive protection for agents than the Directive's minimum requirements may be applied as overriding mandatory provisions under the Rome I Regulation, displacing the otherwise applicable law of a non-member state.

## Comparative Analysis

### Structural Approaches

The four legal systems reveal fundamentally different structural approaches to agency law. Germany follows the Abstraktionsprinzip, separating external authority from the internal relationship and requiring disclosure of the principal. The United States recognizes both disclosed and undisclosed agency, with the undisclosed principal doctrine having no equivalent in German law. South Korea follows the civil law approach, distinguishing between direct agency (acting in the principal's name) and indirect agency (acting in the agent's own name), which corresponds broadly to the German distinction between Stellvertretung and Kommission. The EU Directive focuses exclusively on commercial agents in goods transactions, leaving the general law of agency to member states.

### Agent Protection

The most dramatic divergence concerns the protection of commercial agents upon termination. The EU Directive and German HGB mandate goodwill indemnity/compensation upon termination, creating a vested right that cannot be contractually waived. The United States provides no comparable general protection, treating agency relationships as terminable at will absent contractual provisions to the contrary. South Korea occupies a middle position, with limited judicial recognition of agent goodwill claims but no statutory mandate comparable to the EU/German system.

### Apparent Authority

The treatment of apparent authority differs in important respects. American law provides the broadest apparent authority doctrine, binding the principal based on manifestations to the third party that create a reasonable belief in the agent's authority. German law recognizes Anscheinsvollmacht (apparent authority) and Duldungsvollmacht (tolerated authority) as judicially developed doctrines, but applies them more restrictively than American apparent authority. South Korean law codifies specific apparent authority situations in Civil Code Articles 125-129 rather than recognizing a general apparent authority principle.

### Fiduciary Duties

All four systems impose loyalty and care obligations on agents, but the intensity and enforcement mechanisms differ. American law treats agency as inherently fiduciary, with robust remedies including disgorgement of profits and constructive trusts. German and South Korean law impose similar duties but within the framework of contractual obligations (Vertragspflichten) rather than a separate fiduciary category. The EU Directive imposes a duty of good faith but leaves the specification of remedies to member states.
