## Summary

Official language laws -- the legal designation of one or more languages as the official medium of government communication, legislation, judicial proceedings, education, and public administration -- reflect fundamental choices about national identity, minority rights, linguistic diversity, and the practical requirements of governance, with approaches ranging from constitutional entrenchment of a single national language to multilingual recognition frameworks accommodating dozens of languages. South Korea's Constitution (대한민국 헌법) does not explicitly designate Korean as the official language, but the Framework Act on Korean Language (국어기본법), enacted in 2005, establishes Korean (국어) as the language of government, public documents, and public education; the Korean Language Framework Act requires all public institutions to use Korean in official documents (Article 14) and mandates the establishment of the National Institute of Korean Language (국립국어원) for language standardization and promotion; the Hangul Proclamation Day (한글날) is a national holiday celebrating the creation of the Korean alphabet. The United States has no constitutionally or federally designated official language, despite numerous legislative attempts, making it one of the few developed nations without a formal official language; the "English-only" movement has resulted in 32 states designating English as their official language through state constitutional amendments or statutes, while Executive Order 13166 (2000) requires federal agencies to provide meaningful access to services for persons with limited English proficiency (LEP). Germany designates German as the official language through Article 23(1) of the Verwaltungsverfahrensgesetz (Administrative Procedure Act) rather than through the Basic Law, though the BVerfG has recognized the German language as an implicit constitutional requirement; minority languages including Sorbian, Danish, North Frisian, and Romani are protected under the European Charter for Regional or Minority Languages. At the European level, the EU operates with 24 official languages under Article 342 TFEU and Regulation No 1/1958, the European Charter for Regional or Minority Languages (1992) establishes protections for minority and regional languages, and the ECHR has addressed language rights under Article 14 (non-discrimination) read with Article 8 (private life) and Article 2 of Protocol No. 1 (right to education).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea operates as one of the world's most linguistically homogeneous nations, with Korean as the near-universal native language and no legally recognized linguistic minorities.

**Framework Act on Korean Language (국어기본법):** Enacted on January 27, 2005, and effective July 28, 2005, this Act establishes the legal foundation for Korean language policy:

- **Article 3:** Defines "Korean" (국어) as the Korean language spoken and used by the Korean people.
- **Article 14:** All public institutions shall use Korean in official documents. The use of foreign languages in official documents is permitted only when necessary for international communication or when Korean terminology is unavailable.
- **Article 16:** The government shall promote the international dissemination of the Korean language through the King Sejong Institute Foundation (세종학당재단).
- **Article 19:** The government shall support research on the Korean language and establish the National Institute of Korean Language (국립국어원).

**Court Organization Act (법원조직법) Article 62:** Judicial proceedings shall be conducted in Korean. Parties who do not understand Korean are entitled to interpretation services.

**Framework Act on the Treatment of Foreigners Residing in the Republic of Korea (재한외국인 처우 기본법):** Enacted in 2007, this Act mandates government support for Korean language education for foreign residents and multicultural families, reflecting increasing immigration and international marriage.

### Hangul and Language Policy

The Korean alphabet, Hangul (한글), was created by King Sejong the Great (세종대왕) and promulgated in 1446 as the Hunminjeongeum (훈민정음). Hangul Proclamation Day (한글날, October 9) is a national holiday. The government's language purification (국어 순화) policy, ongoing since the 1990s, seeks to replace foreign loanwords and Japanese colonial-era terminology (일제 잔재) with native Korean alternatives.

### Multicultural Integration

South Korea's foreign resident population has grown from approximately 500,000 in 2000 to over 2.5 million in 2024. The Korean Immigration and Integration Program (사회통합프로그램, KIIP) provides free Korean language education as a pathway to permanent residency and naturalization. The Multicultural Families Support Act (다문화가족지원법, 2008) mandates Korean language education and cultural adaptation programs for multicultural families.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States is unique among major developed nations in lacking a constitutionally or federally designated official language.

**Federal Level:** No federal statute designates English as the official language. Numerous bills have been introduced, including the English Language Unity Act (repeatedly introduced since 2003) and the proposed English Language Amendment to the Constitution, but none has been enacted. The Founders deliberately chose not to establish an official language, reflecting the multilingual reality of the early Republic (English, German, Dutch, French, and numerous indigenous languages).

**State Official English Laws:** As of 2025, 32 states have designated English as their official language through constitutional amendments (e.g., California Proposition 63, 1986; Arizona Article XXVIII, 2006) or statutes (e.g., Iowa Code Section 1.18, 2002). Hawaii is the only state that constitutionally designates two official languages: English and Hawaiian (Hawaii Constitution Article XV, Section 4).

**Executive Order 13166 (2000):** Signed by President Clinton on August 11, 2000, this EO requires federal agencies and recipients of federal financial assistance to ensure meaningful access to programs and activities for persons with limited English proficiency (LEP). Implementing guidance from the Department of Justice requires consideration of the number of LEP persons served, the frequency of contact, the nature and importance of the program, and the resources available.

**Native American Languages Act (NALA, 25 U.S.C. Section 2901, 1990):** Establishes federal policy to preserve, protect, and promote the rights of Native Americans to use, practice, and develop Native American languages. The Esther Martinez Native American Languages Programs Reauthorization Act (2019) funds immersion language programs for endangered indigenous languages.

### Key Case Law

**Lau v. Nichols, 414 U.S. 563 (1974):** The Supreme Court held that a San Francisco school district's failure to provide English language instruction to Chinese-speaking students violated Title VI of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, establishing the principle that language-based discrimination may constitute national origin discrimination.

**Meyer v. Nebraska, 262 U.S. 390 (1923):** The Court struck down a Nebraska statute prohibiting the teaching of foreign languages to children below eighth grade, holding that the Fourteenth Amendment's liberty clause protects the right to study and teach foreign languages.

**Yniguez v. Arizonans for Official English, 520 U.S. 43 (1997):** The Supreme Court vacated a Ninth Circuit ruling that had struck down Arizona's official English amendment as violating the First Amendment, on mootness grounds. The substantive constitutional question of whether official English laws violate the First Amendment remains unresolved.

### Bilingual Education

The Bilingual Education Act (Title VII of the Elementary and Secondary Education Act, 1968) was the first federal legislation recognizing the needs of limited English-speaking students. It was replaced by Title III of the No Child Left Behind Act (2001), which shifted emphasis from bilingual instruction to English language acquisition. States vary dramatically in their approach to bilingual education, from dual-language immersion programs to English-only instruction mandates.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany designates German as the administrative language through statute rather than the Basic Law, while providing notable protections for recognized minority languages.

**Verwaltungsverfahrensgesetz (VwVfG) Section 23(1):** "The official language is German" (Die Amtssprache ist Deutsch). This provision applies to all federal administrative proceedings. Corresponding provisions exist in Länder administrative procedure laws.

**Gerichtsverfassungsgesetz (GVG) Section 184:** "The court language is German" (Die Gerichtssprache ist deutsch). All judicial proceedings, filings, and decisions must be in German. Parties who do not command the German language are entitled to an interpreter at state expense in criminal proceedings (under StPO Section 185) and at their own expense in civil proceedings (unless legal aid covers interpreter costs).

**European Charter for Regional or Minority Languages:** Germany ratified the Charter in 1998, recognizing the following regional or minority languages:

| Language | Protected Status | Region |
|----------|-----------------|--------|
| Sorbian (Sorbisch) | Part III (enhanced protection) | Brandenburg, Saxony |
| Danish (Dänisch) | Part III | Schleswig-Holstein |
| North Frisian (Nordfriesisch) | Part III | Schleswig-Holstein |
| Sater Frisian (Saterfriesisch) | Part III | Lower Saxony |
| Low German (Niederdeutsch) | Part III | Northern Germany (8 Länder) |
| Romani | Part II (basic protection) | Nationwide |

**Brandenburgische Verfassung Article 25:** The Brandenburg constitution explicitly protects the rights of the Sorbian (Wendish) minority, including the right to use the Sorbian language in public life and education. In the bilingual area (Siedlungsgebiet), public signs, school instruction, and administrative services must be available in both German and Sorbian.

### Integration and Language Requirements

The Aufenthaltsgesetz (Residence Act) Section 44 establishes the Integrationskurs (integration course) system, requiring most immigrants to complete 600-900 hours of German language instruction plus 100 hours of civic orientation. The Einbürgerungstest (naturalization test) requires B1-level German language proficiency under the Common European Framework of Reference (CEFR). The 2024 reform of the citizenship law (Staatsangehörigkeitsgesetz) maintained the B1 language requirement while reducing the residency requirement from 8 to 5 years.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Multilingualism

**Article 342 TFEU:** Empowers the Council to determine the rules governing the languages of EU institutions. Regulation No 1/1958, as amended, establishes the 24 official and working languages of the EU corresponding to the official languages of member states. All EU legislation must be published in all 24 official languages, and citizens have the right to correspond with EU institutions and receive responses in any official language.

**EU Multilingualism Policy:** The European Commission's Communication on Multilingualism (COM(2008) 566) established the "mother tongue plus two" principle, recommending that all EU citizens learn at least two languages in addition to their mother tongue.

### European Charter for Regional or Minority Languages (ECRML, 1992)

The Charter, opened for signature on November 5, 1992, and in force since March 1, 1998, establishes principles and obligations for the protection of regional or minority languages. As of 2026, 25 states have ratified the Charter. Part II establishes general principles applicable to all regional or minority languages. Part III establishes enhanced protections that states may select on a provision-by-provision basis for specifically designated languages.

Notably, France has signed but not ratified the Charter, citing conflict with Article 2 of the French Constitution, which provides that "the language of the Republic is French." The Conseil constitutionnel ruled in 1999 that the Charter's provisions were incompatible with the Constitution.

### Framework Convention for the Protection of National Minorities (FCNM, 1995)

The FCNM, in force since February 1, 1998, includes language rights provisions. Article 10 guarantees the right to use minority languages in private and public. Article 14 establishes the right to learn minority languages and to receive instruction in them where sufficient demand exists.

### European Court of Human Rights

**Belgian Linguistic Case (Application Nos. 1474/62 et al., Judgment of July 23, 1968):** This landmark case, one of the earliest ECHR judgments, addressed the language of education in Belgium. The Court held that Article 2 of Protocol 1 (right to education) does not require states to provide education in any particular language but prohibits discriminatory denial of access to existing educational facilities on language grounds.

**Catan and Others v. Moldova and Russia (Applications Nos. 43370/04, 8252/05, 18454/06, Grand Chamber Judgment of October 19, 2012):** The Court found violations of Article 2 of Protocol 1 where authorities in the Transnistrian region of Moldova forced Latin-script Romanian-language schools to switch to Cyrillic script, holding that the right to education encompasses the language of instruction.

**Mentzen v. Latvia (Application No. 71074/01, Decision of December 7, 2004):** The Court declared inadmissible a challenge to Latvia's requirement that personal names be transcribed into Latvian orthography in official documents, holding that the requirement pursued legitimate aims and was proportionate.

**Güzel Erdagöz v. Turkey (Application No. 37483/02, Judgment of October 21, 2008):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where Turkey refused to register the applicant's name using the Kurdish letter "W" (not present in the Turkish alphabet), holding that the refusal interfered with the right to private life.

## Comparative Analysis

### Constitutional Status

The four jurisdictions take markedly different approaches to the constitutional status of language. South Korea and Germany designate their official languages through statute rather than constitution. The United States has no federal designation at all. The EU operates with 24 co-equal official languages, a unique arrangement reflecting its supranational nature.

### Minority Language Protection

Germany provides the strongest minority language protections among the national jurisdictions, with constitutional-level protection for Sorbian in Brandenburg and ratification of the European Charter. South Korea has no recognized linguistic minorities. The United States provides limited protections for Native American languages but no comprehensive minority language framework. The EU Charter establishes the international standard, though implementation varies significantly across ratifying states.

### Language and Immigration

All three national jurisdictions have linked language proficiency to immigration and naturalization. Germany's Integrationskurs system is the most structured. South Korea's KIIP program reflects the country's relatively recent experience with significant immigration. The United States relies on the naturalization examination requirement of English language ability (INA Section 312) while simultaneously requiring LEP access to government services under EO 13166, creating a tension between integration and access goals.

### Language Rights vs. Language Obligations

A fundamental tension exists between language as a right (the right to use one's own language) and language as an obligation (the requirement to use the official language). Germany's GVG Section 184 and Korea's Court Organization Act Article 62 impose German and Korean respectively as mandatory court languages. The ECHR's Article 6(3)(e) guarantees the right to an interpreter in criminal proceedings, mediating between official language requirements and individual access to justice.
