## Summary

Resisting arrest -- the act of opposing, obstructing, or impeding a law enforcement officer in the performance of their official duties, particularly during the execution of an arrest -- occupies a contentious position in criminal law, implicating tensions between the state's authority to enforce laws and the individual's right to personal liberty, bodily autonomy, and freedom from unlawful detention. South Korea addresses this offense primarily through Article 136 of the Criminal Act (형법 제136조, 공무집행방해죄), which criminalizes obstruction of official duties through assault or intimidation with penalties of up to five years' imprisonment or a fine of up to ten million KRW, and has been the subject of significant controversy in the context of labor disputes and political protests where the provision has been applied broadly against demonstrators. The United States addresses resisting arrest through state criminal codes -- with every state maintaining some form of resisting arrest statute -- alongside federal provisions such as 18 U.S.C. Section 111 (assaulting, resisting, or impeding federal officers), with the Supreme Court having addressed the right to resist unlawful arrest in Bad Elk v. United States (1900) and substantially all states having since abolished the common law right to resist even an unlawful arrest. Germany criminalizes resistance to enforcement officers under Section 113 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB, Widerstand gegen Vollstreckungsbeamte), reformed in 2017 to increase penalties to up to three years' imprisonment for basic resistance and up to five years for carrying weapons or engaging in violence, with the unique provision under Section 113(3) that the offender is not punishable if the official act was itself unlawful. At the European level, the ECHR has developed extensive jurisprudence on the proportionality of force used during arrest under Articles 3 and 5, establishing that states may not use excessive force to overcome resistance, while simultaneously recognizing the legitimate authority of police to effect lawful arrests.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Article 136 of the Criminal Act (공무집행방해죄) is the primary provision: "A person who assaults or intimidates a public official in the execution of official duties shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than five years or by a fine not exceeding ten million won."

### Penalty Structure

| Offense | Provision | Penalty |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Obstruction of official duties | Art. 136(1) | Up to 5 years or fine up to 10 million KRW |
| Obstruction with weapon | Art. 144(1) | Up to 7 years imprisonment |
| Group obstruction (collective action) | Art. 144(2) | Up to 10 years imprisonment |
| Injury to public official during obstruction | Art. 136 + Art. 257 (concurrent) | Combined sentencing |

### Broad Application Controversies

The scope of Article 136 has been a subject of recurring controversy in South Korea. Unlike many jurisdictions that limit the offense to resisting arrest specifically, the Korean provision applies to any obstruction of any public official performing any official duty. This breadth has led to prosecutions in contexts that have drawn criticism from civil liberties organizations:

**Labor Disputes:** Workers and union leaders have been charged with obstruction of official duties during labor strikes and demonstrations. The Korean Confederation of Trade Unions (전국민주노동조합총연맹) has documented cases where peaceful protest activities were prosecuted under Article 136.

**Supreme Court Decision 2009Do5732:** The court held that for Article 136 to apply, the official duty being obstructed must be lawful. If the official act itself is unlawful -- for example, an arrest without legal authority -- the resistance does not constitute obstruction of official duties. However, the court placed the burden of proving the unlawfulness of the official act on the defendant.

**Supreme Court Decision 2011Do3682:** The court clarified that "assault" (폭행) in the context of Article 136 includes any application of physical force directed at a public official, including pushing, grabbing, or blocking the officer's movement, even if no injury results. However, purely verbal protest or passive non-compliance does not constitute "assault."

### 2022 Itaewon Crowd Crush Investigation

The 2022 Itaewon crowd crush, which killed 159 people during Halloween celebrations, raised questions about police accountability and the use of Article 136. During the aftermath, there was significant public debate about whether the broad scope of the obstruction provision inhibited legitimate accountability demands against police officers whose negligence may have contributed to the disaster.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Every state in the United States criminalizes resisting arrest in some form. The common elements include: an officer was engaged in the lawful performance of their duties, the defendant knew or should have known the person was an officer, and the defendant intentionally resisted, delayed, or obstructed the officer.

**18 U.S.C. Section 111 (Federal):** Provides graduated penalties for forcibly assaulting, resisting, opposing, impeding, intimidating, or interfering with federal officers:

| Subsection | Conduct | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| (a)(1) | Simple assault | Up to 1 year (misdemeanor) or 8 years (physical contact/intent to commit felony) |
| (a)(2) | Using deadly or dangerous weapon or inflicting bodily injury | Up to 20 years |
| (b) | Enhanced penalty for use of deadly weapon | Up to 20 years |

### Abolition of Common Law Right to Resist

In Bad Elk v. United States, 177 U.S. 529 (1900), the Supreme Court recognized a common law right to resist an unlawful arrest, including with reasonable force. However, substantially all states have since legislatively or judicially abolished this right:

**New York (PL § 35.27):** "A person may not use physical force to resist an arrest, whether authorized or unauthorized, which is being effected or attempted by a police officer or peace officer when it would reasonably appear that the latter is a police officer or peace officer."

The rationale for abolition is that modern legal systems provide adequate judicial remedies for unlawful arrests -- through suppression of evidence, civil rights lawsuits under 42 U.S.C. Section 1983, and criminal prosecution of officers -- making physical resistance unnecessary and dangerous.

### Racial Disparities

Research has documented significant racial disparities in the application of resisting arrest charges. A 2020 study by the Vera Institute of Justice found that Black individuals were disproportionately charged with resisting arrest compared to white individuals in comparable situations. Critics have argued that resisting arrest charges are sometimes used as "cover charges" to justify the use of force by officers, particularly in cases where the underlying arrest lacked probable cause.

### State Variations on Passive Resistance

States differ on whether passive resistance (going limp, refusing to place hands behind one's back) constitutes resisting arrest. California courts have held that passive resistance may constitute "delaying" an officer under Penal Code Section 148(a)(1). New York courts have generally held that passive non-compliance does not constitute "physical force" under the resisting arrest statute.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's resisting arrest provision, Section 113 StGB (Widerstand gegen Vollstreckungsbeamte), was significantly reformed by the Act to Strengthen the Protection of Enforcement Officers and Rescue Workers (Gesetz zur Stärkung des Schutzes von Vollstreckungsbeamten und Rettungskräften) of May 2017.

**Section 113(1) StGB:** "Whoever, by force or by threat of force, offers resistance to a public official or soldier of the Bundeswehr who is charged with the enforcement of laws, regulations, judgments, court orders, or orders, in the execution of such enforcement, or whoever physically assaults such person in connection therewith, shall be punished with imprisonment for not more than three years or a fine."

### Penalty Structure

| Offense | Provision | Penalty |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Basic resistance | Section 113(1) | Up to 3 years or fine |
| Carrying weapon or dangerous instrument | Section 113(2) No. 1 | 6 months to 5 years |
| Using violence causing danger to life | Section 113(2) No. 2 | 6 months to 5 years |
| Acting jointly with others | Section 113(2) No. 3 | 6 months to 5 years |
| Attack on enforcement officers (Tätlicher Angriff) | Section 114 (added 2017) | 3 months to 5 years |

### Unlawful Act Defense -- Section 113(3)

Section 113(3) provides a unique defense: "The act is not punishable under this provision if the enforcement act is not lawful. This also applies if the perpetrator erroneously assumes that the enforcement act is lawful." This means:

- If the arrest is unlawful, resistance is not criminal under Section 113.
- However, even if the arrest is lawful but the perpetrator erroneously believes it is unlawful, the perpetrator is still not punishable under Section 113 -- though they may be punishable under general assault provisions (Section 223 StGB).

### 2017 Reform Context

The 2017 reform was prompted by increasing reports of assaults on police officers and emergency responders in Germany. The reform introduced Section 114 (Tätlicher Angriff auf Vollstreckungsbeamte) as a separate offense, criminalizing physical attacks on enforcement officers even outside the context of active enforcement operations. This expansion was controversial, with critics arguing it was unnecessary given existing assault provisions and could chill legitimate protest.

### Bundesverfassungsgericht Jurisprudence

The Federal Constitutional Court has addressed Section 113 in the context of assembly rights. In its jurisprudence on the Brokdorf demonstration case (BVerfGE 69, 315), the court emphasized that the application of Section 113 in the context of political demonstrations must be tempered by the fundamental right of assembly. Police operations to disperse demonstrations must themselves be lawful; if they are not, resistance to such operations is protected by Section 113(3).

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR has addressed resisting arrest through multiple Convention provisions:

**Article 3 -- Prohibition of Inhuman Treatment During Arrest:**

**Rehbock v. Slovenia (Application No. 29462/95, 2000):** The Court found a violation of Article 3 where police officers inflicted severe injuries on a suspect during arrest. The Court established that even where a person is suspected of a serious offense and is resisting arrest, the use of force must be proportionate and must not exceed what is strictly necessary.

**Bouyid v. Belgium [GC] (Application No. 23380/09, 2015):** The Grand Chamber found a violation of Article 3 where police officers slapped detained persons. The Court held that any use of physical force against a person deprived of their liberty which is not strictly necessitated by the person's own conduct diminishes human dignity and is in principle an infringement of Article 3.

**Article 5 -- Right to Liberty:**

**Fox, Campbell and Hartley v. United Kingdom (Application No. 12244/86, 1990):** The Court held that the lawfulness of an arrest under Article 5(1)(c) requires reasonable suspicion of an offense. If the arrest itself lacks legal basis, the use of force to effect it cannot be justified.

### EU Framework

While the EU has not adopted specific legislation on resisting arrest, several instruments are relevant:

**Directive 2012/13/EU (Right to Information in Criminal Proceedings):** This Directive establishes the right of persons suspected or accused of criminal offenses to be informed of their rights, including the right to be informed of the reason for arrest. This information obligation is relevant because failure to provide it may affect the lawfulness of the arrest and, consequently, the criminality of resistance.

**Directive 2013/48/EU (Right of Access to a Lawyer):** Establishes the right of access to a lawyer from the moment of deprivation of liberty, including during arrest. Any obstruction of this right by officers may affect the lawfulness of the overall arrest process.

### Council of Europe Standards

The European Committee for the Prevention of Torture (CPT) has published standards on the treatment of persons during arrest, emphasizing that officers must use minimum necessary force and must refrain from retaliatory violence against persons who have resisted arrest once resistance has ceased.

## Comparative Analysis

### Right to Resist Unlawful Arrest

The most significant divergence concerns the right to resist unlawful arrest. Germany explicitly provides this right under Section 113(3), reflecting the principle that citizens should not be required to submit to manifestly unlawful state action. The United States has nearly universally abolished this right, preferring ex post judicial remedies. South Korea occupies a middle position, with the Supreme Court recognizing that resistance to unlawful official acts does not constitute the offense but placing the burden of proof on the defendant.

### Passive vs. Active Resistance

Germany clearly limits Section 113 to active resistance (force or threat of force), excluding passive non-compliance. South Korea similarly limits Article 136 to "assault or intimidation." U.S. jurisdictions are split, with some broadly interpreting "resisting" to include passive resistance such as going limp or refusing to comply with commands.

### Scope of Protected Officials

South Korea's Article 136 applies to all public officials performing any official duty -- the broadest scope among the jurisdictions studied. Germany limits Section 113 to enforcement officers performing enforcement operations. U.S. statutes typically protect law enforcement officers, though some extend to other officials such as firefighters and emergency medical personnel.

### Use of Force Standards

The ECHR's proportionality framework provides the most developed standard for evaluating the use of force during arrest. The Court's decisions in Rehbock and Bouyid establish that force must be strictly necessary and proportionate, even against resisting suspects. This standard has influenced domestic law across Council of Europe member states.
