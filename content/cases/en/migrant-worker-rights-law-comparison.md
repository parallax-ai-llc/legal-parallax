## Summary

Migrant worker rights -- the legal protections afforded to foreign nationals engaged in employment outside their country of nationality, encompassing labor standards, social security, freedom of association, workplace safety, and protection against exploitation -- represent a critical intersection of immigration law, labor law, and international human rights law. South Korea's Employment Permit System (고용허가제, EPS), established by the Act on the Employment of Foreign Workers (외국인근로자의 고용 등에 관한 법률) of 2003, governs the admission and employment of approximately 270,000 low-skilled migrant workers from 16 countries through bilateral memoranda of understanding, guaranteeing equal application of the Labor Standards Act (근로기준법) and minimum wage protections but imposing restrictions on workplace mobility (limited to 3 employer changes within 4 years and 10 months), generating persistent criticism from labor unions and human rights organizations. The United States extends most federal labor protections to migrant workers regardless of immigration status under the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA, 29 U.S.C. §§ 201-219), Occupational Safety and Health Act (OSHA, 29 U.S.C. §§ 651-678), and National Labor Relations Act (NLRA, 29 U.S.C. §§ 151-169), though enforcement is complicated by the intersection with immigration enforcement, the visa-dependent status of many workers (particularly H-2A and H-2B visa holders), and the Supreme Court's decision in Hoffman Plastic Compounds v. NLRB, 535 U.S. 137 (2002), limiting back pay remedies for undocumented workers. Germany protects migrant workers through the comprehensive Arbeitnehmerüberlassungsgesetz (AÜG, Temporary Agency Work Act), Mindestlohngesetz (MiLoG, Minimum Wage Act), and Arbeitsschutzkontrollgesetz (Supply Chain Labor Protection Act), within the EU's framework of the Posted Workers Directive (Directive 96/71/EC, revised by Directive (EU) 2018/957), the Seasonal Workers Directive (Directive 2014/36/EU), and the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights, while the ECHR's jurisprudence under Article 4 (prohibition of forced labor and trafficking), Article 8 (private and family life), and Article 14 (non-discrimination) has established binding obligations on states to prevent and punish labor exploitation of migrant workers.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's Employment Permit System is one of the most structured temporary labor migration programs globally.

**Act on the Employment of Foreign Workers (외국인근로자의 고용 등에 관한 법률):**

| Article | Provision |
|---------|-----------|
| Article 6 | Equal treatment: labor-related laws apply equally to foreign workers |
| Article 8 | Foreign Workforce Policy Committee (외국인력정책위원회) determines annual quotas |
| Article 9 | Bilateral MOUs with sending countries |
| Article 12 | Standard labor contract requirement |
| Article 22 | Employment insurance coverage |
| Article 25 | Workplace change restrictions |

**EPS Structure:**

| Component | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Visa category | E-9 (non-professional employment) |
| Source countries | 16 (Philippines, Thailand, Indonesia, Vietnam, Mongolia, Sri Lanka, Cambodia, Nepal, Myanmar, Uzbekistan, Bangladesh, Pakistan, China, East Timor, Laos, Kyrgyzstan) |
| Maximum stay | 4 years 10 months (extendable to 9 years 8 months with re-entry) |
| Workplace changes | Maximum 3 times (employer fault or unavoidable circumstances) |
| Korean language test | EPS-TOPIK (Employment Permit System Test of Proficiency in Korean) required before departure |

### Labor Rights

Foreign workers on E-9 visas are legally entitled to the same protections as Korean workers:
- **Minimum wage:** 9,860 KRW/hour (2024), same as Korean workers
- **Working hours:** LSA limits (40 hours/week standard; overtime limits)
- **Industrial accident insurance:** Full coverage under the Industrial Accident Compensation Insurance Act
- **National health insurance:** Mandatory enrollment
- **National pension:** Mandatory contributions (reimbursement agreements with some sending countries)
- **Departure guarantee insurance (출국만기보험):** Employer-funded insurance paid upon departure

### Workplace Mobility Restrictions

The most criticized aspect of the EPS is the restriction on workplace mobility. Under Article 25, workers may change employers only for reasons attributable to the employer (non-payment, workplace closure, abuse, unsafe conditions) or unavoidable reasons, up to a maximum of 3 times. This "tied worker" system has been condemned by:

- **ILO Committee of Experts:** Repeated observations that workplace mobility restrictions create conditions conducive to forced labor
- **National Human Rights Commission (국가인권위원회):** 2019 recommendation to abolish the 3-change limit
- **UN Committee on the Elimination of Racial Discrimination:** 2018 concluding observations recommending flexibility in employer changes

### Agricultural Workers

Agricultural sector migrant workers face particularly challenging conditions:
- Seasonal Work Visa (E-8): introduced 2015 for agricultural labor
- Housing provided by employers (often substandard dormitories)
- Extended working hours common during harvest seasons
- Cases of death and injury at agricultural workplaces have prompted government inspections and reforms
- 2021 "Cambodian Workers' Death" case at a greenhouse in Pocheon highlighted systemic exploitation

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States extends most federal labor protections to migrant workers regardless of immigration status, though enforcement gaps persist.

**Key Federal Labor Protections:**

| Statute | Protection | Applies to Undocumented |
|---------|-----------|------------------------|
| FLSA (29 U.S.C. §§ 201-219) | Minimum wage, overtime | Yes |
| OSHA (29 U.S.C. §§ 651-678) | Workplace safety | Yes |
| NLRA (29 U.S.C. §§ 151-169) | Right to organize | Yes (but limited remedies) |
| Title VII (42 U.S.C. § 2000e) | Anti-discrimination | Yes |
| MSPA (29 U.S.C. §§ 1801-1872) | Migrant seasonal agricultural workers | Yes |

**Hoffman Plastic Compounds v. NLRB, 535 U.S. 137 (2002):** The Supreme Court held that the NLRA does not authorize the National Labor Relations Board to award back pay to undocumented workers who were illegally fired for union organizing. This decision significantly undermined labor protections for undocumented workers by removing a key enforcement tool, though other remedies (minimum wage recovery, discrimination damages) remain available.

### H-2A Agricultural Workers

The H-2A temporary agricultural worker program is the primary legal pathway for seasonal agricultural labor:

| Feature | Requirement |
|---------|-------------|
| Employer obligation | Demonstrate insufficient domestic workers |
| Adverse Effect Wage Rate (AEWR) | Regionally set ($14.53-$18.65/hr, 2024) |
| Housing | Free housing meeting federal/state standards |
| Transportation | Inbound/outbound transportation; daily transportation |
| 3/4 guarantee | Must offer at least 75% of contract period hours |
| Workers' compensation | Required in most states |
| Contract | Written; DOL-approved |

### H-2B Non-Agricultural Workers

The H-2B program for temporary non-agricultural labor (hospitality, landscaping, seafood processing) is capped at 66,000 per year (with supplemental allocations). Workers face similar employer-dependency issues as H-2A workers.

### Trafficking Protections

The Trafficking Victims Protection Act (TVPA) of 2000, as reauthorized, provides:
- T-visa: for victims of trafficking (up to 5,000 per year)
- U-visa: for victims of qualifying crimes (including labor exploitation) who assist law enforcement (10,000 per year)
- Continued Presence: DHS authority to permit victims to remain during investigation/prosecution

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's migrant worker protections operate within a comprehensive framework of domestic labor law and EU regulations.

**Universal Labor Law Application:**

All workers in Germany, regardless of nationality or immigration status, are protected by:
- Mindestlohngesetz (MiLoG): statutory minimum wage of €12.41/hour (2024), €12.82 (2025)
- Arbeitszeitgesetz (ArbZG): working time limitations (8 hours/day; maximum 10 with compensation)
- Bundesurlaubsgesetz (BUrlG): minimum 24 working days paid annual leave
- Arbeitsschutzgesetz (ArbSchG): occupational safety and health
- Full social insurance coverage (health, pension, unemployment, accident, long-term care)

**Arbeitsschutzkontrollgesetz (2021):** Following the massive COVID-19 outbreak at Tönnies meat processing plant in June 2020, which infected over 1,500 workers (predominantly Eastern European migrant workers), the Bundestag enacted the Arbeitsschutzkontrollgesetz:
- Prohibited subcontracting (Werkverträge) in the core meat processing industry
- Banned the use of temporary agency workers in the meat industry's core activities
- Introduced mandatory electronic working time recording for the meat industry
- Established minimum standards for employer-provided housing
- Increased labor inspection frequency

**Finanzkontrolle Schwarzarbeit (FKS):** The customs authority's division for combating illegal employment conducts workplace inspections to enforce minimum wage compliance, social insurance obligations, and detect illegal employment. The FKS has approximately 7,300 officers and conducted over 50,000 employer inspections in 2023.

### EU Free Movement Workers

EU/EEA nationals enjoy full freedom of movement under Article 45 TFEU and Regulation (EU) No 492/2011:
- No work permit required
- Full equal treatment with nationals
- Family reunification rights
- Social security coordination under Regulation (EC) No 883/2004
- Posted workers protected under Directive (EU) 2018/957

### Seasonal Workers

The EU Seasonal Workers Directive (2014/36/EU), transposed into German law, provides:
- Maximum stay of 5-9 months in any 12-month period
- Equal treatment in working conditions, terms of employment, and occupational health and safety
- Adequate housing standards (if provided by employer)
- Facilitated re-entry for returning seasonal workers

## ECHR / EU

### ECHR Article 4 -- Prohibition of Forced Labor and Trafficking

The ECHR has developed significant jurisprudence on labor exploitation of migrant workers:

**Siliadin v. France (Application No. 73316/01, July 26, 2005):** The Court found a violation of Article 4 where a Togolese minor was held in conditions of domestic servitude. The Court established that states have positive obligations under Article 4 to criminalize and effectively investigate forced labor and servitude.

**Rantsev v. Cyprus and Russia (Application No. 25965/04, January 7, 2010):** The Court held that trafficking in human beings falls within the scope of Article 4. States have positive obligations to: (1) establish a legal and administrative framework to prohibit and punish trafficking; (2) protect victims when authorities are aware or ought to be aware of circumstances giving rise to a credible suspicion of trafficking; and (3) investigate potential trafficking.

**Chowdury and Others v. Greece (Application No. 21884/15, March 30, 2017):** The Court found a violation of Article 4 regarding Bangladeshi strawberry pickers in Manolada, Greece, who worked under conditions of forced labor (threat of violence, non-payment of wages, confiscation of documents). The judgment established that even where migrant workers initially consented to employment, subsequent exploitative conditions can constitute forced labor or trafficking.

**C.N. and V. v. France (Application No. 67724/09, October 11, 2012):** The Court clarified the distinction between servitude and forced labor: servitude involves an obligation to provide services imposed by coercion and linked to the victim's living conditions, particularly the impossibility of altering the conditions.

### EU Posted Workers Directive

Directive (EU) 2018/957 (revising Directive 96/71/EC) established the "equal pay for equal work at the same place" principle:

| Element | Provision |
|---------|-----------|
| Remuneration | Host country minimum + all mandatory elements of remuneration |
| Duration | After 12 months (extendable to 18): all host country terms and conditions apply |
| Temporary agency workers | Equal treatment with comparable host country workers |
| Accommodation | Host country standards where employer provides accommodation |
| Allowances | Travel, accommodation, meal allowances in addition to remuneration |

### EU Anti-Trafficking Framework

**Directive 2011/36/EU on preventing and combating trafficking in human beings:**
- Comprehensive definition of trafficking for labor exploitation
- Maximum penalty: at least 5 years imprisonment (10 years for aggravated cases)
- Non-punishment principle: victims should not be prosecuted for crimes committed as a consequence of trafficking
- Residence permit for victims (Directive 2004/81/EC)
- Establishment of national rapporteurs or equivalent mechanisms

### ILO Conventions

Relevant ILO instruments ratified by multiple jurisdictions include:
- Convention No. 29 (Forced Labour, 1930)
- Convention No. 97 (Migration for Employment, 1949)
- Convention No. 143 (Migrant Workers, 1975)
- Convention No. 189 (Domestic Workers, 2011)
- Protocol of 2014 to the Forced Labour Convention

## Comparative Analysis

### Workplace Mobility

Employer-dependent visa systems create structural vulnerability:
- **South Korea (EPS):** Most restrictive; 3 employer changes maximum
- **United States (H-2A/H-2B):** Employer-specific visa; portability limited
- **Germany:** EU workers fully mobile; third-country workers increasingly flexible after initial period
- **EU framework:** Posted Workers Directive addresses temporary cross-border work

All jurisdictions face the fundamental tension between employer-specific immigration controls (which serve labor market policy goals) and worker freedom (which prevents exploitation).

### Enforcement Mechanisms

| Jurisdiction | Primary Enforcement | Inspection Capacity |
|-------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| South Korea | Ministry of Employment and Labor | ~2,000 inspectors |
| United States | DOL Wage and Hour Division; OSHA | ~750 WHD investigators; ~1,850 OSHA inspectors |
| Germany | FKS (customs); Gewerbeaufsicht (trade supervision) | ~7,300 FKS officers; state trade supervisory authorities |
| EU | National authorities; European Labour Authority (ELA) | ELA coordination; national resources vary |

### Social Security Access

Migrant workers' access to social security varies significantly:
- **South Korea:** Full access to health insurance and pension (with bilateral reimbursement agreements)
- **United States:** SS and Medicare taxes required; benefit access limited by immigration status and minimum quarter requirements
- **Germany:** Full social insurance for legal workers; EU coordination for cross-border cases
- **EU:** Regulation (EC) No 883/2004 coordinates social security for workers moving within the EU

### Recruitment Fee Regulation

Exploitative recruitment practices are a major driver of migrant worker vulnerability:

- **South Korea:** EPS operates on a government-to-government basis, eliminating private recruitment intermediaries; worker-borne costs average $900-$1,500 (among the lowest globally for labor migration programs)
- **United States:** Fair Recruitment Initiative and state laws (e.g., California AB 450) prohibit employer-charged recruitment fees; however, enforcement in source countries is limited; H-2A and H-2B visa holders frequently pay thousands in recruitment fees despite formal prohibitions
- **Germany:** Bundesagentur für Arbeit (Federal Employment Agency) manages government-to-government recruitment through bilateral agreements; private recruitment permitted but regulated under Arbeitnehmerüberlassungsgesetz (AÜG)
- **ILO:** General Principles and Operational Guidelines for Fair Recruitment (2019) establish that workers should not be charged recruitment fees or related costs; the "employer pays" principle is increasingly recognized as best practice

### Platform Work and Gig Economy

The rise of digital labor platforms challenges traditional migrant worker protection frameworks:

- **EU:** Platform Workers Directive (2024) establishes a rebuttable presumption of employment relationship for platform workers, with significant implications for migrant gig workers
- **South Korea:** Korean Labour Standards Act coverage does not extend to independent contractors; migrant gig workers face dual exclusion from labor protection and immigration support
- **United States:** Classification of gig workers varies by state; California AB 5 (2019) and Proposition 22 (2020) illustrate the contested terrain; migrant gig workers on employment-tied visas face particular vulnerability
- Digital labor platforms increasingly operate across borders, creating jurisdictional challenges for enforcement of labor standards
