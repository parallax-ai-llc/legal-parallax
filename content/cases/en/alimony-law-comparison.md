## Summary

Alimony -- the financial support paid by one former spouse to the other following divorce, variously termed spousal maintenance, spousal support, or post-marital support -- reflects fundamentally different legal philosophies about the economic consequences of marriage dissolution, ranging from fault-based compensation models to need-based support systems and increasingly toward time-limited rehabilitative approaches emphasizing post-divorce self-sufficiency. South Korea recognizes two distinct financial claims upon divorce: consolation money (위자료) paid by the at-fault spouse as compensation for emotional harm under Article 843 of the Civil Act (민법), and property division (재산분할) under Article 839-2 which may incorporate spousal support elements; unlike Western alimony systems, Korean law does not have a standalone post-divorce maintenance obligation, though courts may factor ongoing support needs into property division calculations, and the Supreme Court has recognized that property division may include a "support element" (부양적 요소) alongside the "liquidation element" (청산적 요소) reflecting each spouse's contributions to marital property. The United States governs alimony through state family law codes, with a marked national trend toward reform, including the 2012 Massachusetts Alimony Reform Act establishing durational limits tied to marriage length, several states eliminating permanent alimony in favor of durational or rehabilitative maintenance, and the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act eliminating the federal tax deduction for alimony payments and income inclusion for recipients in divorces finalized after December 31, 2018, fundamentally altering the economics of alimony negotiations. Germany provides post-divorce spousal maintenance (nachehelicher Unterhalt) under BGB Sections 1569-1586b, governed since the 2008 Unterhaltsrechtsänderungsgesetz reform by the principle of self-responsibility (Eigenverantwortung), which requires each spouse to provide for their own support after divorce with maintenance awarded only in enumerated circumstances including childcare, age, illness, or inability to find employment, and limited in duration to what is equitable. The European framework lacks harmonization of spousal maintenance rules but provides conflict-of-law and enforcement mechanisms through the EU Maintenance Regulation (No 4/2009) and the 2007 Hague Protocol on applicable law, while the ECHR has addressed spousal maintenance primarily in the context of gender discrimination and procedural fairness.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea does not have a Western-style alimony (ongoing spousal maintenance) system. Instead, the financial consequences of divorce are addressed through two mechanisms:

**Consolation Money (위자료, Article 843 Civil Act):** The at-fault spouse may be ordered to pay consolation money to the innocent spouse as compensation for emotional suffering caused by the marital breakdown. The amount is determined by considering the degree of fault, the duration of the marriage, the circumstances of the divorce, and the economic status of both parties. Typical awards range from 20 to 100 million KRW, though amounts vary significantly.

**Property Division (재산분할, Article 839-2 Civil Act):** Upon divorce, either party may claim division of marital property. The Supreme Court (대법원) has established that property division serves two functions:

| Function | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Liquidation element (청산적 요소) | Division of property accumulated through joint efforts during the marriage |
| Support element (부양적 요소) | Provision for the economically disadvantaged spouse's post-divorce livelihood |

### Supreme Court Jurisprudence

**Supreme Court Decision 2013므568 (2013):** The Court confirmed that property division may include a support element (부양적 요소), allowing courts to award a larger share to the economically weaker spouse beyond what would be justified by their contribution alone. This decision effectively incorporated spousal support into the property division framework.

**Supreme Court Decision 93므1002 (1993):** The Court established that homemaking contributions constitute a basis for property division claims, recognizing domestic labor as economic contribution to marriage.

### Practical Application

In practice, Korean courts typically award the economically weaker spouse (usually the wife) approximately 30-50 percent of marital property, with the percentage adjusted upward in long-term marriages or where one spouse made substantial homemaking contributions. The support element allows the court to exceed the contribution-based calculation when the requesting spouse would face economic hardship after divorce.

### Consolation Money vs. Western Alimony

The Korean consolation money system differs fundamentally from Western alimony:

| Feature | Korean 위자료 | Western Alimony |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| Basis | Fault/emotional harm | Need/economic disparity |
| Payment structure | Typically lump sum | Typically periodic payments |
| Duration | One-time | Ongoing (months to permanent) |
| Modifiability | Generally not modifiable | Usually modifiable |
| Tax treatment | No special treatment | May have tax implications |

## United States

### Legal Framework

Alimony in the United States is governed entirely by state law, with no federal alimony statute. The Uniform Marriage and Divorce Act (1970) influenced state reforms but was not universally adopted. Courts generally consider:

| Factor | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| Duration of marriage | Longer marriages more likely to produce alimony awards |
| Standard of living | Marital standard of living as benchmark |
| Earning capacity | Each spouse's ability to be self-supporting |
| Age and health | Physical and mental health considerations |
| Contributions | Homemaking, career sacrifice, supporting spouse's education |
| Marital misconduct | Considered in some states (not all) |

### Types of Alimony

| Type | Purpose | Duration |
|------|---------|----------|
| Permanent | Ongoing support for spouse unable to become self-sufficient | Until death, remarriage, or modification |
| Rehabilitative | Support during transition to self-sufficiency | Specific period (often tied to education/training) |
| Durational/Bridge-the-gap | Time-limited support | Fixed period, often tied to marriage duration |
| Reimbursement | Compensation for supporting spouse's education | Lump sum or period based on investment |
| Pendente lite | Temporary support during divorce proceedings | Until final judgment |

### Reform Movement

A significant reform movement has sought to limit or eliminate permanent alimony:

**Massachusetts Alimony Reform Act (2012):** Established durational limits:
- Marriage of 5 years or less: alimony not to exceed 50% of marriage length
- Marriage of 10 years or less: not to exceed 60%
- Marriage of 15 years or less: not to exceed 70%
- Marriage of 20 years or less: not to exceed 80%
- Marriage exceeding 20 years: court may order indefinite alimony

**Florida:** After multiple failed attempts, Florida enacted alimony reform in 2023, eliminating permanent alimony prospectively and establishing a formula-based approach with durational caps.

**Texas:** Limits spousal maintenance to a maximum of $5,000 per month or 20% of the obligor's average monthly gross income, whichever is less, with a maximum duration of 5-10 years depending on marriage length.

### Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (2017)

The TCJA fundamentally altered alimony economics for divorces finalized after December 31, 2018:

| Feature | Pre-TCJA | Post-TCJA |
|---------|----------|-----------|
| Payor | Tax deductible | Not deductible |
| Recipient | Includable as income | Not includable |
| Effect | Shifted tax burden to lower-bracket recipient | Higher after-tax cost for payor; lower incentive to agree |

This change reduced the "tax subsidy" that had facilitated alimony agreements and is estimated to have significantly impacted settlement dynamics.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Post-divorce spousal maintenance (nachehelicher Unterhalt) is governed by BGB Sections 1569-1586b. The 2008 Unterhaltsrechtsänderungsgesetz fundamentally reformed the system, shifting from a presumption of ongoing support to a presumption of self-responsibility.

### Principle of Self-Responsibility (Eigenverantwortung)

**Section 1569 BGB:** After the divorce, each spouse shall provide for their own maintenance. Only if a spouse is not in a position to do so shall they have a claim for maintenance against the other spouse in accordance with the following provisions.

### Enumerated Maintenance Grounds

| Ground | BGB Section | Description |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| Childcare (Betreuungsunterhalt) | Section 1570 | Caring for a child up to age 3; may be extended |
| Age (Altersunterhalt) | Section 1571 | Inability to find employment due to age |
| Illness (Krankheitsunterhalt) | Section 1572 | Inability to work due to illness |
| Unemployment (Erwerbslosigkeitsunterhalt) | Section 1573 | Inability to find appropriate employment |
| Top-up (Aufstockungsunterhalt) | Section 1573(2) | Income insufficient despite employment |
| Education (Ausbildungsunterhalt) | Section 1575 | Education, training, or retraining |
| Equity (Billigkeitsunterhalt) | Section 1576 | Other serious reasons |

### Limitation and Hardship

**Section 1578b BGB:** Maintenance may be limited in duration or amount to what is equitable, considering the extent to which a disadvantage resulting from the marriage has been overcome through income or assets of the claimant. The marriage-related disadvantage (ehebedingte Nachteile) concept is central: maintenance is primarily justified to the extent that the marriage caused economic disadvantage to the claimant (e.g., career interruption for childcare).

**Section 1579 BGB (Hardship Clause):** Maintenance may be denied, reduced, or time-limited in cases of gross inequity, including:
- Very short marriage duration
- Serious misconduct against the obligor
- Deliberate creation of need
- Other comparably serious reasons

### Maintenance Calculation

German courts calculate maintenance using the "three-sevenths/four-sevenths" or "difference method": the maintenance creditor is entitled to three-sevenths of the difference between the parties' adjusted net incomes (allowing the obligor to retain four-sevenths as an "employment incentive bonus" -- Erwerbstätigenbonus).

### Realsplitting (Tax Treatment)

Under the Realsplitting system (Section 10(1a) EStG), the maintenance payor may deduct maintenance payments up to 13,805 EUR per year from taxable income, provided the recipient consents and includes the payments as income. This mirrors the pre-TCJA U.S. treatment.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Maintenance Regulation (No 4/2009)

The EU Maintenance Regulation governs cross-border spousal maintenance claims with the same framework as child support. For spousal maintenance, the applicable law is determined by the 2007 Hague Protocol on the Law Applicable to Maintenance Obligations:

- **General Rule:** The law of the creditor's habitual residence (Article 3).
- **Exception:** The law of the last common habitual residence if one party objects and that law has a closer connection (Article 5).
- **Choice of Law:** Parties may agree on the applicable law from specified options (Article 8).

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Burden v. United Kingdom (Application No. 13378/05, Grand Chamber, April 29, 2008):** While primarily about tax benefits for cohabitants, the Grand Chamber distinguished married couples from other relationships in terms of financial obligations, noting that the legal consequences of marriage, including maintenance obligations, justify differential treatment.

**Airey v. Ireland (1979):** The requirement of effective access to courts for family proceedings extends to claims for spousal maintenance, requiring legal aid where the complexity of proceedings demands it.

**Muňoz Díaz v. Spain (Application No. 49151/07, December 8, 2009):** The Court found a violation of Article 14 where Roma customary marriage was not recognized for the purpose of survivor's pension, with implications for the recognition of maintenance obligations arising from non-registered unions.

### Variation Across EU Member States

| Member State | System | Key Features |
|-------------|--------|--------------|
| Germany | Need-based + self-responsibility | Limited duration; marriage-related disadvantage focus |
| France | Prestation compensatoire | Typically lump sum; compensatory principle |
| Netherlands | Needs-based | Maximum 5 years (or 12 years for long marriages/children) |
| Sweden | Very limited | Short-term transitional support only |
| Italy | Needs-based + standard of living | Reform in 2017 (Cass. SU 18287/2018): self-sufficiency focus |
| Spain | Pensión compensatoria | Compensatory for economic imbalance |

## Comparative Analysis

### Compensation vs. Need vs. Self-Sufficiency

The jurisdictions reflect three distinct philosophical approaches. South Korea's consolation money system is primarily compensatory and fault-based. The United States historically emphasized need and marital standard of living but is moving toward self-sufficiency and durational limits. Germany most fully embodies the self-responsibility principle, with maintenance limited to compensating for marriage-related disadvantage. The EU framework does not harmonize these approaches but facilitates their cross-border application.

### Fault Relevance

Fault plays a diminishing but still significant role. In South Korea, fault is central to consolation money. In the United States, about half of states consider fault in alimony determinations. Germany limits fault's relevance to the hardship clause (Section 1579 BGB). The general trend across all jurisdictions is toward reducing fault's significance in favor of economic-need-based analysis.

### Temporal Limitations

All jurisdictions have moved toward limiting the duration of spousal support. Germany's Section 1578b emphasis on marriage-related disadvantage inherently limits duration. The Massachusetts Alimony Reform Act and similar U.S. state reforms impose explicit durational caps. South Korea's lump-sum consolation money is inherently time-limited. Only in exceptional circumstances (very long marriages, severe disability) do any of these systems contemplate truly indefinite support.

### Tax Interaction

Tax treatment significantly affects alimony economics. Germany's Realsplitting and the pre-2018 U.S. deduction/inclusion system both created a tax incentive for alimony by shifting income to the lower-bracket recipient. The TCJA's elimination of this incentive in the United States has demonstrably affected settlement dynamics, typically reducing the total amount of alimony negotiated. South Korea offers no special tax treatment for consolation money.
