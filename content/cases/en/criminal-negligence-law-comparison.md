## Summary

Criminal negligence -- the failure to exercise a standard of care that a reasonable person would observe, resulting in harm that rises to the level warranting criminal rather than merely civil liability -- represents a critical boundary concept in criminal law, marking the point at which careless conduct becomes punishable by the state. South Korea addresses criminal negligence through a comprehensive framework in the Criminal Act (형법), distinguishing between ordinary negligence (과실, Article 14), gross negligence (중과실), and occupational negligence (업무상 과실, Article 268), with the latter imposing heightened liability on professionals and persons engaged in occupational activities; the Sewol ferry disaster of April 16, 2014, which killed 304 people including 250 high school students, became a watershed moment for Korean criminal negligence law, leading to convictions of the ferry captain for homicide through abandonment and prompting sweeping reforms to safety regulation and disaster response. The United States applies criminal negligence through a patchwork of federal and state standards, with the Model Penal Code Section 2.02(2)(d) defining negligence as a failure to be aware of "a substantial and unjustifiable risk" that constitutes "a gross deviation from the standard of care that a reasonable person would observe," while federal regulatory negligence statutes such as the Clean Water Act (33 U.S.C. Section 1319(c)(1)) impose criminal penalties for negligent violations of environmental standards; the distinction between civil and criminal negligence remains contested, with some jurisdictions requiring only ordinary negligence for certain regulatory offenses. Germany employs a sophisticated negligence (Fahrlässigkeit) framework under Section 15 StGB, distinguishing between ordinary negligence (einfache Fahrlässigkeit) and gross negligence (grobe Fahrlässigkeit), and requiring that every negligent offense be expressly designated by statute (Section 15 StGB: "Only intentional conduct is punishable unless the law expressly provides punishment for negligent conduct"); German courts have developed the doctrine of objective duty of care (objektive Sorgfaltspflicht) as the central standard. The ECHR has addressed criminal negligence through the positive obligations doctrine, requiring states to maintain effective criminal law frameworks for negligent conduct causing death (Article 2) or serious harm, while EU directives increasingly impose criminal negligence standards for environmental protection, workplace safety, and financial regulation.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's Criminal Act establishes a tiered framework for criminal negligence:

**Article 14 -- Negligent Conduct (과실):** "Conduct that a person fails to exercise due care although the person could have foreseen the occurrence of a criminal fact is deemed negligent." This defines negligence through two elements: (1) foreseeability of the harmful result, and (2) failure to exercise due care (주의의무 위반).

**Article 266 -- Negligent Bodily Injury (과실치상):** "A person who causes bodily injury to another through negligence shall be punished by a fine not exceeding 5 million KRW."

**Article 267 -- Negligent Homicide (과실치사):** "A person who causes the death of another through negligence shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than 2 years or a fine not exceeding 7 million KRW."

**Article 268 -- Occupational Negligent Homicide/Injury (업무상 과실치사상):** "A person who causes the death or bodily injury of another through occupational negligence or gross negligence shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than 5 years or a fine not exceeding 20 million KRW." This enhanced penalty reflects the higher duty of care expected of professionals.

### Occupational Negligence (업무상 과실)

The concept of "occupational" (업무상) negligence extends liability to persons engaged in professional or occupational activities that require specialized knowledge or skill. The Supreme Court has defined "occupation" (업무) broadly:

**Supreme Court Decision 85도2433 (1986):** The Court held that "occupation" encompasses any activity that a person continuously or repeatedly engages in based on their social status, regardless of whether it constitutes their primary profession.

**Supreme Court Decision 2014도12789 (2015):** In the aftermath of the Sewol disaster, the Court addressed the scope of occupational negligence for ship captains, holding that the captain's duty of care encompasses not only navigation but also passenger safety, including the duty to order evacuation.

### The Sewol Ferry Disaster (2014)

The sinking of the MV Sewol on April 16, 2014, became the most significant criminal negligence case in modern Korean history:

**Facts:** The 6,825-ton ferry capsized while sailing from Incheon to Jeju Island, killing 304 of 476 passengers, including 250 students from Danwon High School in Ansan. The disaster was attributed to a combination of factors: illegal overloading, inadequate ballast, a sharp turn in a strong current, and catastrophic failure in emergency response.

**Captain Lee Jun-seok's Trial:** Captain Lee was initially convicted of negligent homicide but the prosecution appealed. The appellate court convicted him of homicide through abandonment (부작위에 의한 살인) for the deaths of passengers in areas he could have evacuated, holding that his duty to order evacuation and his failure to do so amounted to intentional killing through omission. He was sentenced to life imprisonment.

**Crew Convictions:** Fourteen other crew members received sentences ranging from 18 months to 36 years for their roles in the disaster.

**Regulatory Aftermath:** The disaster led to the dissolution of the Korean Coast Guard, the establishment of the Ministry of Public Safety and Security, and comprehensive reforms to maritime safety regulation.

### Traffic Accident Negligence

The Act on Special Cases Concerning the Settlement of Traffic Accidents (교통사고처리 특례법) provides a separate framework for vehicular negligence, generally allowing civil settlement to substitute for criminal prosecution except in cases involving specific aggravating factors such as drunk driving, hit-and-run, or violation of traffic signals.

## United States

### Legal Framework

**Model Penal Code Section 2.02(2)(d) -- Negligently:** "A person acts negligently with respect to a material element of an offense when he should be aware of a substantial and unjustifiable risk that the material element exists or will result from his conduct. The risk must be of such a nature and degree that the actor's failure to perceive it, considering the nature and purpose of his conduct and the circumstances known to him, involves a gross deviation from the standard of care that a reasonable person would observe in the actor's situation."

This definition establishes the critical distinction between civil negligence (ordinary carelessness) and criminal negligence (gross deviation from reasonable care).

### Federal Regulatory Negligence

The United States is distinctive in imposing criminal liability for negligent violations of regulatory standards:

| Statute | Standard | Penalty |
|---------|----------|---------|
| Clean Water Act, 33 U.S.C. Section 1319(c)(1) | Negligent violation of discharge permits | Up to 1 year per violation; 2 years for repeat |
| Clean Air Act, 42 U.S.C. Section 7413(c)(1) | Negligent violation of emission standards | Up to 1 year per violation |
| OSHA, 29 U.S.C. Section 666(e) | Willful violation causing death | Up to 6 months (misdemeanor) |
| Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act, 21 U.S.C. Section 333 | Negligent violation | Up to 1 year |
| Responsible Corporate Officer Doctrine | Persons in responsible relationship to violation | See United States v. Park, 421 U.S. 658 (1975) |

### State Approaches

State criminal negligence law varies significantly:

**Involuntary Manslaughter (Common Law):** Most states define involuntary manslaughter as an unintentional killing resulting from criminal negligence (gross negligence) or during the commission of an unlawful act not amounting to a felony.

**Vehicular Homicide:** Many states have created specific vehicular homicide statutes with varying negligence standards:
- California Penal Code Section 191.5: Gross vehicular manslaughter while intoxicated (3-10 years)
- Texas Penal Code Section 19.04: Manslaughter (2-20 years, second-degree felony)
- New York Penal Law Section 125.10: Criminally negligent homicide (Class E felony, up to 4 years)

### Key Cases

**United States v. Park, 421 U.S. 658 (1975):** The Supreme Court upheld the conviction of a corporate officer for negligent violations of the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act under the "responsible corporate officer" doctrine, holding that persons with authority to prevent regulatory violations can be held criminally liable for their failure to do so.

**State v. Williams, 484 P.2d 1167 (Wash. App. 1971):** A widely discussed case in which parents were convicted of manslaughter for failing to seek medical treatment for their child's infected tooth, which led to the child's death. The court applied an ordinary negligence standard under Washington law.

**People v. Hall, 999 P.2d 207 (Colo. 2000):** The Colorado Supreme Court held that a skier who caused the death of another skier through reckless skiing could be charged with reckless manslaughter, establishing that recreational activities are not exempt from criminal negligence standards.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

**Section 15 StGB -- Intentional and Negligent Conduct (Vorsätzliches und fahrlässiges Handeln):** "Only intentional conduct is punishable unless the law expressly provides punishment for negligent conduct" (Strafbar ist nur vorsätzliches Handeln, wenn nicht das Gesetz fahrlässiges Handeln ausdrücklich mit Strafe bedroht). This provision establishes that criminal negligence must be specifically authorized by statute for each offense -- there is no general negligence liability.

**Section 222 StGB -- Negligent Homicide (Fahrlässige Tötung):** "Whoever causes the death of a person through negligence shall be punished with imprisonment for a term not exceeding five years or a fine."

**Section 229 StGB -- Negligent Bodily Harm (Fahrlässige Körperverletzung):** "Whoever negligently causes bodily harm to another person shall be punished with imprisonment for a term not exceeding three years or a fine."

### Elements of Negligence

German criminal law defines negligence through two components:

**Objective Duty of Care (Objektive Sorgfaltspflicht):** The offender must have violated the standard of care expected of a reasonable person in the same situation. This standard is determined objectively, drawing on professional standards, statutory regulations, industry norms (such as DIN standards), and general life experience.

**Objective Foreseeability (Objektive Vorhersehbarkeit):** The harmful result must have been objectively foreseeable to a reasonable person in the offender's position.

**Subjective Negligence (Subjektive Fahrlässigkeit):** Beyond the objective elements, the offender must have been personally capable of recognizing and complying with the duty of care. This individual capacity assessment distinguishes criminal negligence from a purely objective standard.

### Forms of Negligence

**Ordinary Negligence (Einfache Fahrlässigkeit):** Inadvertent failure to recognize and avoid a foreseeable risk. The offender did not realize their conduct was dangerous.

**Gross Negligence (Grobe Fahrlässigkeit):** Particularly serious failure to exercise care, where the offender disregarded obvious risks. Some statutes require gross negligence for criminal liability.

**Conscious Negligence (Bewusste Fahrlässigkeit):** The offender recognized the risk but trusted it would not materialize. This borders on conditional intent (dolus eventualis/bedingter Vorsatz), and the distinction between the two is one of the most debated issues in German criminal law theory.

### Landmark Cases

**BGH, Judgment of November 11, 2005 (5 StR 301/05 -- Ledersprayprozess):** The Federal Court of Justice addressed corporate criminal negligence in a case involving contaminated leather spray that caused numerous lung injuries. The Court held that corporate decision-makers who failed to recall a product they knew to be dangerous could be convicted of negligent bodily harm.

**BGH, Judgment of July 20, 2017 (4 StR 83/17):** The Court addressed medical negligence, holding that a surgeon who performed a procedure beyond their competence could be convicted of negligent bodily harm even if the procedure was performed with reasonable technical skill, because the duty of care required referring the patient to a specialist.

### Regulatory Negligence

Germany addresses regulatory negligence primarily through the Ordnungswidrigkeitengesetz (OWiG, Act on Regulatory Offenses), which imposes administrative fines (Geldbußen) rather than criminal penalties for negligent regulatory violations. This system operates parallel to and separate from criminal law, allowing regulatory enforcement without the stigma and procedural requirements of criminal prosecution.

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR addresses criminal negligence primarily through the positive obligations doctrine under Articles 2 (right to life) and 8 (respect for private life):

**Article 2 -- Right to Life:** The Court has established that states must maintain an effective criminal law framework to deter offenses against the person, including negligent conduct causing death.

**Öneryildiz v. Turkey (Application No. 48939/99, Grand Chamber Judgment of November 30, 2004):** The Court found a violation of Article 2 in connection with a methane gas explosion at a municipal waste tip that killed 39 residents of an adjacent shantytown. The Court held that the criminal proceedings against the responsible officials (who received minimal sentences) were inadequate to satisfy the state's procedural obligation under Article 2. The judgment established that states must ensure that criminal negligence provisions carry penalties that are adequate and not disproportionately lenient.

**Budayeva and Others v. Russia (Application No. 15339/02, Judgment of March 20, 2008):** The Court found violations of Article 2 arising from the authorities' failure to implement land-planning and emergency evacuation policies in an area known to be at risk of mudslides, resulting in deaths. The Court emphasized that the positive obligation under Article 2 encompasses preventive measures against environmental hazards.

**Kolyadenko and Others v. Russia (Application No. 17423/05, Judgment of February 28, 2012):** The Court addressed state negligence in flood prevention, finding that the authorities' failure to maintain a reservoir and properly warn residents of flood risks violated Article 2.

### EU Framework

**Directive (EU) 2024/1226 on Environmental Crime:** This directive significantly expands criminal negligence liability for environmental offenses across the EU:
- Article 3 requires member states to ensure that certain environmental offenses are punishable when committed with "at least serious negligence"
- The directive establishes minimum maximum penalties for negligent environmental offenses
- Article 7 requires member states to ensure corporate liability for negligent environmental offenses

**Framework Directive 89/391/EEC on Workplace Safety:** Establishes the obligation for employers to ensure worker safety and health, forming the basis for criminal negligence liability in workplace accidents across EU member states.

**Directive 2008/99/EC on the Protection of the Environment Through Criminal Law:** Required member states to criminalize certain environmental offenses committed with "at least serious negligence," establishing a common standard across the EU.

## Comparative Analysis

### The Civil-Criminal Negligence Threshold

All four jurisdictions recognize a distinction between civil and criminal negligence, but the threshold varies:

- **United States (MPC):** "Gross deviation" from reasonable standard -- the most explicit formulation
- **Germany:** Violation of "objective duty of care" with foreseeability -- a structured two-element test
- **South Korea:** Failure to exercise "due care" despite foreseeability -- similar to Germany but less formally structured
- **ECHR/EU:** No unified standard; focus on effectiveness of criminal framework

### Corporate Criminal Negligence

The jurisdictions diverge sharply on corporate criminal liability:

- **United States:** Broadest corporate criminal liability; respondeat superior doctrine; responsible corporate officer doctrine
- **South Korea:** Limited corporate criminal liability; focus on individual officers
- **Germany:** No corporate criminal liability (societas delinquere non potest); administrative fines under OWiG
- **EU:** Directive 2024/1226 requires member states to establish corporate liability for environmental offenses, challenging Germany's traditional position

### Occupational Negligence

South Korea's explicit occupational negligence provision (Article 268) has no direct equivalent in American or German general criminal law, though both systems achieve similar results through different mechanisms -- the United States through regulatory statutes and professional licensing, Germany through heightened objective duty of care standards for professionals.
