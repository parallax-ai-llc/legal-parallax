## Summary

Elder abuse -- encompassing physical, emotional, sexual, and financial exploitation of older persons, as well as neglect and abandonment -- has emerged as a critical legal and social policy concern across all developed nations as populations age rapidly. South Korea enacted the Act on the Welfare of Senior Citizens (노인복지법) in 1981, which was substantially amended in 2004 to establish the Elder Protective Services system and criminalize various forms of elder abuse, with further amendments in 2017 and 2020 strengthening mandatory reporting requirements and increasing penalties; the National Human Rights Commission of Korea has documented a steady rise in reported cases, from 3,818 in 2010 to over 19,000 in 2023. The United States addresses elder abuse primarily at the state level through Adult Protective Services (APS) statutes enacted in all 50 states, supplemented by the federal Elder Justice Act of 2010 (42 U.S.C. Sections 1397j-1397m), which was the first comprehensive federal elder abuse legislation and established the Elder Justice Coordinating Council and dedicated funding streams. Germany addresses elder abuse through a combination of criminal provisions in the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB) -- particularly Sections 223 (assault), 225 (abuse of wards), and 266 (breach of trust) -- alongside the Heimgesetz (Residential Homes Act) and its successor state-level nursing home statutes, while the Pflegestärkungsgesetz reforms (2015-2017) enhanced quality oversight of long-term care facilities. At the European level, the Council of Europe has addressed elder abuse through Recommendation CM/Rec(2014)2 on the promotion of human rights of older persons, the European Court of Human Rights has developed a jurisprudence of positive obligations under Articles 3 and 8 of the ECHR to protect vulnerable adults from abuse, and the EU has incorporated elder protection into the European Pillar of Social Rights proclaimed in 2017, with Principle 18 guaranteeing the right to affordable long-term care services of good quality.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's legal framework for elder protection centers on the Act on the Welfare of Senior Citizens (노인복지법), originally enacted in 1981 and fundamentally restructured through a major amendment in 2004 that introduced Chapter V-2, specifically addressing the prevention of elder abuse. The 2004 amendment was catalyzed by growing public awareness of institutional neglect and domestic abuse of elderly persons, and it established the Elder Protective Services (노인보호전문기관) system at both national and regional levels.

### Categories of Prohibited Conduct

Article 39-9 of the Act enumerates prohibited acts against senior citizens:

| Category | Description | Penalty |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| Physical abuse (신체적 학대) | Inflicting physical pain or injury | Up to 5 years imprisonment or 50 million KRW fine |
| Emotional abuse (정서적 학대) | Causing psychological distress through verbal or behavioral means | Up to 5 years imprisonment or 50 million KRW fine |
| Sexual abuse (성적 학대) | Sexual assault or harassment of elderly persons | Up to 7 years imprisonment or 50 million KRW fine |
| Financial exploitation (경제적 착취) | Misappropriating or exploiting an elder's property or finances | Up to 5 years imprisonment or 50 million KRW fine |
| Neglect (방임) | Failing to provide basic necessities to a dependent elder | Up to 3 years imprisonment or 30 million KRW fine |
| Abandonment (유기) | Abandoning an elder who requires care | Up to 5 years imprisonment or 50 million KRW fine |

### Mandatory Reporting System

The 2017 amendment significantly expanded the mandatory reporting framework. Under Article 39-6, the following professionals must report suspected elder abuse to the Elder Protective Services or law enforcement: medical personnel, social workers, nursing home and care facility staff, home care service providers, government officials in welfare-related departments, and emergency medical technicians. Failure to report carries a fine of up to 5 million KRW. The 2020 amendment further extended the mandatory reporting obligation to financial institution employees who encounter suspected financial exploitation.

### Institutional Infrastructure

The Ministry of Health and Welfare oversees the Central Elder Protective Services Center (중앙노인보호전문기관), which coordinates 34 regional centers across the country. These centers receive reports, conduct investigations, provide emergency shelter, offer counseling and support services, and compile national statistics. In 2023, the system received over 19,000 reports, with substantiated cases involving neglect (approximately 42 percent), emotional abuse (approximately 28 percent), and physical abuse (approximately 18 percent).

### Landmark Developments

The tragic death of an 85-year-old nursing home resident in Namyangju in 2014, following systematic physical abuse by care workers, prompted a national outcry and led to the 2017 mandatory CCTV installation requirement in communal areas of elderly care facilities under the Welfare of Senior Citizens Act. This provision was upheld by the Constitutional Court of Korea in 2019 as a proportionate restriction on privacy rights given the vulnerability of institutional care residents.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States addresses elder abuse through a dual framework of state and federal law. All 50 states, the District of Columbia, and U.S. territories have enacted Adult Protective Services (APS) statutes that establish investigation and intervention systems for reports of abuse, neglect, and exploitation of vulnerable adults, including older persons. At the federal level, the Elder Justice Act (EJA) of 2010, enacted as Subtitle H of the Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act (42 U.S.C. Sections 1397j through 1397m), represents the first comprehensive federal legislation specifically addressing elder abuse.

### Federal Framework -- Elder Justice Act of 2010

The EJA established several institutional mechanisms:

**Elder Justice Coordinating Council:** Chaired by the Secretary of Health and Human Services, the Council coordinates federal elder justice activities across departments, including HHS, DOJ, FTC, and CFPB.

**Advisory Board on Elder Abuse, Neglect, and Exploitation:** Provides recommendations to the Elder Justice Coordinating Council on policy, research, and practice.

**Funding Provisions:** The EJA authorized $777 million over four years for APS programs, long-term care ombudsman programs, forensic centers, and research. However, congressional appropriations have consistently fallen far short of authorizations.

**Nursing Home Reporting Requirement:** Section 1150B of the Social Security Act, added by the EJA, requires owners, operators, and employees of long-term care facilities that receive federal funding to report reasonable suspicions of crimes against residents to law enforcement and the state survey agency within specified timeframes (2 hours for serious bodily injury; 24 hours for other suspected crimes). Failure to report carries civil monetary penalties of up to $200,000 for facilities and up to $50,000 for individuals.

### State Variations

| Feature | Majority Approach | Notable Exceptions |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Age threshold | 60 or 65 years | Some states use vulnerability-based definitions regardless of age |
| Mandatory reporting | All states require reporting by some categories | New York requires all persons to report; several states exempt clergy |
| Criminal penalties | Felony for serious abuse | Severity classifications vary widely |
| Financial exploitation | Separate offense in most states | Enhanced penalties in approximately 30 states |
| Self-neglect coverage | Included in approximately 40 states | Several states explicitly exclude self-neglect |

### Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB)

The CFPB established the Office for Older Americans in 2013, which addresses financial exploitation of older persons through policy research, public education, and coordination with financial institutions. In 2016, the CFPB issued Advisory 2016-01, encouraging financial institutions to report suspected elder financial exploitation and recommending practices for detecting and preventing exploitation.

### Notable Federal Prosecutions

The Department of Justice launched the Elder Justice Initiative in 2016, coordinating federal prosecutions of elder abuse and fraud. Operation Guardian, initiated in 2020, resulted in criminal charges against over 400 defendants across 58 federal districts for schemes targeting older Americans, with total losses exceeding $1.1 billion.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany does not have a single comprehensive elder abuse statute. Instead, protection of older persons from abuse is achieved through the intersection of general criminal law provisions in the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), civil law protections, and specialized regulatory frameworks governing residential care.

### Criminal Law Provisions

| StGB Section | Offense | Maximum Penalty |
|-------------|---------|----------------|
| § 223 -- Körperverletzung | Assault/bodily harm | 5 years imprisonment |
| § 224 -- Gefährliche Körperverletzung | Dangerous bodily harm (including against defenseless persons) | 10 years imprisonment |
| § 225 -- Misshandlung von Schutzbefohlenen | Abuse of wards/persons in care | 10 years imprisonment |
| § 266 -- Untreue | Breach of trust/embezzlement | 5 years imprisonment |
| § 171 -- Verletzung der Fürsorge- oder Erziehungspflicht | Violation of duty of care | 3 years imprisonment |
| § 221 -- Aussetzung | Abandonment/exposure | 5 years imprisonment; 10 years if death results |

Section 225 StGB (Misshandlung von Schutzbefohlenen) is particularly relevant, as it specifically criminalizes the tormenting, rough mistreatment, malicious neglect, or health-damaging conduct directed at a person in the perpetrator's care. Although originally designed primarily for child protection, it applies equally to dependent adults, including elderly persons in institutional or domestic care.

### Institutional Oversight

**Heimgesetz and State Successors:** The federal Heimgesetz (Residential Homes Act), originally enacted in 1974, established minimum standards for residential care facilities and provided for state inspections. Following the 2006 federalism reform, regulatory authority over nursing homes was transferred to the Länder, which enacted their own successor statutes (e.g., the Wohn- und Teilhabegesetz in North Rhine-Westphalia, the Pflege- und Wohnqualitätsgesetz in Bavaria).

**Medizinischer Dienst (MD):** The MD (formerly MDK -- Medizinischer Dienst der Krankenversicherung) conducts quality inspections of all nursing homes and home care services at least once annually. Inspection results have been publicly available since 2009 through the Pflege-TÜV transparency system, reformed in 2019 to include more meaningful quality indicators.

### Pflegestärkungsgesetze (Care Strengthening Acts)

Between 2015 and 2017, Germany enacted three Pflegestärkungsgesetze (PSG I, II, III) that fundamentally reformed the long-term care system:

**PSG II (2017):** Replaced the three-level care classification (Pflegestufen) with a five-grade system (Pflegegrade) that better accounts for cognitive impairments and dementia, ensuring broader eligibility for care services and thereby reducing neglect risks.

**PSG III (2017):** Strengthened municipal-level advisory services and enhanced the role of local authorities in coordinating elder care, including abuse prevention.

### Betreuungsrecht (Guardianship Law)

The Betreuungsrecht reform of 2023 (effective January 1, 2023) strengthened the autonomy of persons under legal guardianship (Betreuung). The reform codified the principle that guardians must act in accordance with the wishes of the person under guardianship rather than substituting their own judgment, thereby reducing the potential for guardian-perpetrated abuse. The reform also introduced mandatory personal contact between guardians and their wards.

## ECHR & EU

### European Convention on Human Rights

The European Court of Human Rights has developed a body of jurisprudence establishing positive obligations on states to protect vulnerable adults, including older persons, from abuse.

**Article 3 -- Prohibition of Torture and Inhuman or Degrading Treatment:** The Court has held that Article 3 requires states to take measures to ensure that individuals within their jurisdiction are not subjected to treatment falling below the minimum severity threshold, including at the hands of private individuals.

**Dodov v. Bulgaria (Application No. 59548/00, Judgment of January 17, 2008):** The case concerned the disappearance of a 74-year-old Alzheimer's patient from a state-run nursing home in Sofia. The Court found a violation of Article 2 (right to life), holding that the state had failed in its positive obligation to protect the life of a vulnerable person in its care. The judgment established that states bear enhanced responsibility for the safety of persons placed in state-run care institutions.

**Heinisch v. Germany (Application No. 28274/08, Judgment of July 21, 2011):** A geriatric nurse was dismissed for filing a criminal complaint against her employer, a state-owned nursing home operator, alleging systematic deficiencies in elder care. The Court found a violation of Article 10 (freedom of expression), holding that the public interest in information about deficiencies in institutional elder care outweighed the employer's interest in protecting its reputation. This case established important protections for whistleblowers in elder care settings.

**Centre for Legal Resources on behalf of Valentin Câmpeanu v. Romania (Application No. 47848/08, Grand Chamber Judgment of July 17, 2014):** While concerning a young disabled man rather than an elder, this Grand Chamber judgment established the principle that NGOs may bring cases on behalf of vulnerable persons who died in institutional care without next-of-kin, broadening access to the Court for institutional abuse victims.

### Council of Europe Recommendation CM/Rec(2014)2

Adopted on February 19, 2014, this Recommendation on the promotion of human rights of older persons calls on member states to ensure that older persons enjoy their human rights and fundamental freedoms on an equal basis with others. It addresses independence and autonomy, participation, protection from violence and abuse, social protection, and access to justice. While not legally binding, it represents the most comprehensive European intergovernmental instrument on elder rights.

### EU Legal Framework

**European Pillar of Social Rights (2017):** Principle 18 establishes that "everyone has the right to affordable long-term care services of good quality, in particular home-care and community-based services." The Pillar was proclaimed jointly by the European Parliament, the Council, and the Commission at the Gothenburg Social Summit in November 2017.

**European Care Strategy (2022):** Adopted by the Commission on September 7, 2022, the Strategy includes a Council Recommendation on access to affordable high-quality long-term care, which calls on member states to develop or update national long-term care frameworks, ensure adequate staffing in care facilities, strengthen quality assurance mechanisms, and improve working conditions for care workers to reduce turnover and associated risks of neglect.

**EU Agency for Fundamental Rights (FRA):** The FRA published its report "Shifting perceptions: towards a rights-based approach to ageing" in 2018, documenting the prevalence of elder abuse across EU member states and recommending legal reforms to strengthen protection.

## Comparative Analysis

### Definitional Challenges

A fundamental challenge across jurisdictions is the lack of a universally agreed definition of elder abuse. South Korea and most U.S. states define elder abuse through enumerated categories (physical, emotional, sexual, financial, neglect, abandonment), while Germany relies on general criminal provisions without a specific elder abuse classification. The ECHR takes a vulnerability-based approach that does not depend on age thresholds. This definitional divergence complicates cross-jurisdictional data comparison and policy analysis.

### Mandatory Reporting

South Korea and the United States have developed extensive mandatory reporting systems, though their scope and enforcement differ significantly. South Korea has progressively expanded its list of mandated reporters through the 2017 and 2020 amendments, while U.S. states vary considerably in their designation of mandated reporters -- from universal reporting obligations (New York) to narrower lists covering only certain professionals. Germany has limited mandatory reporting, primarily within institutional care settings, relying instead on regular inspections and quality monitoring.

### Institutional vs. Domestic Abuse

All jurisdictions face a fundamental tension between institutional and domestic elder abuse. Institutional abuse is generally easier to detect and regulate through inspections, quality standards, and reporting requirements. Domestic abuse, which accounts for the majority of cases across all jurisdictions, is harder to identify and address due to victim reluctance to report, perpetrator intimacy, and cultural norms regarding family privacy. South Korea's Confucian cultural context, which emphasizes filial piety (효도) and family hierarchy, creates particular barriers to reporting domestic elder abuse by family members.

### Financial Exploitation

Financial exploitation of older persons has received increasing legislative attention across all jurisdictions. The United States has been most proactive, with the CFPB's Office for Older Americans and state-level enhanced penalties for financial exploitation. South Korea's 2020 amendment extending mandatory reporting to financial institution employees reflects growing recognition of financial exploitation. Germany relies primarily on the general breach of trust provision (§ 266 StGB) without elder-specific financial exploitation statutes.
