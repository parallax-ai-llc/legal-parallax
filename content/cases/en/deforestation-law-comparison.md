## Summary

Deforestation regulation -- the legal framework governing the clearing, conversion, and degradation of forests -- has become a central pillar of both domestic environmental policy and international trade law as the connection between forest loss, climate change, and biodiversity decline has become undeniable, with the Food and Agriculture Organization estimating that approximately 10 million hectares of forest are lost annually. South Korea, having experienced severe deforestation during the Japanese colonial period and Korean War that left only 35 percent of the country's forests intact by 1955, undertook one of the world's most successful reforestation campaigns, regulated through the Forest Protection Act (산림보호법) and the Creation and Management of Forest Resources Act (산림자원의 조성 및 관리에 관한 법률), with the Korea Forest Service (산림청) overseeing national forest policy that has restored forest cover to approximately 63 percent of the national territory. The United States addresses deforestation through a decentralized framework combining federal forest management under the National Forest Management Act of 1976 (NFMA, 16 U.S.C. Sections 1600-1687), state forest practice acts, and the Lacey Act (16 U.S.C. Sections 3371-3378) as amended in 2008 to prohibit trade in illegally sourced timber, with growing emphasis on supply chain due diligence following the FOREST Act proposals. Germany regulates forests through the Bundeswaldgesetz (BWaldG, Federal Forest Act) requiring sustainable management and prohibiting forest conversion without permission, implemented within the broader EU framework that now includes the landmark EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR, Regulation (EU) 2023/1115) requiring companies to demonstrate that commodities placed on the EU market -- including soy, palm oil, beef, cocoa, coffee, rubber, and wood -- have not been produced on land deforested after December 31, 2020, representing the world's most ambitious supply chain deforestation regulation.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's forest regulation is governed by multiple statutes administered by the Korea Forest Service (산림청): the Forest Protection Act (산림보호법), the Creation and Management of Forest Resources Act (산림자원의 조성 및 관리에 관한 법률), the Mountainous Districts Management Act (산지관리법), and the Forestry Culture and Recreation Act (산림문화·휴양에 관한 법률).

### Reforestation Success Story

South Korea's reforestation represents one of the most remarkable environmental recovery narratives in modern history. Following the Korean War, approximately 65 percent of Korea's mountains were denuded. The government launched systematic reforestation campaigns beginning with the First National Forest Plan (1973-1982):

| Forest Plan Period | Key Achievements |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| First (1973-1982) | 10.8 billion trees planted; erosion control; fuel wood plantations |
| Second (1983-1987) | Shift to commercial timber; forest road development |
| Third (1988-1997) | Multipurpose forests; recreational forest development |
| Fourth (1998-2007) | Sustainable forest management; biodiversity conservation |
| Fifth (2008-2017) | Climate change adaptation; green growth forests |
| Sixth (2018-2037) | Forest carbon sinks; smart forestry; demilitarized zone forest restoration |

Today, South Korea's forest cover stands at approximately 63 percent of national territory (about 6.3 million hectares), a dramatic recovery that has been studied globally as a model for developing countries.

### Forest Conversion Regulation

The Mountainous Districts Management Act (산지관리법) requires permission (산지전용허가) for any conversion of forest land to other uses. The permit system categorizes forests into:

- **Preservation forest zones (보전산지):** Strictly protected; conversion generally prohibited
- **Semi-preservation zones (준보전산지):** Limited conversion permitted with conditions
- **Non-forest zones:** Conversion permitted subject to impact assessment

Mandatory reforestation obligations (대체산림자원조성비) require developers converting forest land to either reforest an equivalent area or pay compensatory fees to the Forest Development Fund.

## United States

### Legal Framework

U.S. forest regulation is divided between federal public lands management and regulation of private forest practices.

### National Forest Management Act (1976)

The NFMA governs the management of 193 million acres of national forests and grasslands administered by the U.S. Forest Service:

- Requires Land and Resource Management Plans for each national forest
- Mandates sustained yield management and consideration of biodiversity
- Requires restocking of harvested areas within five years
- Establishes public participation in forest planning

### Lacey Act (Amended 2008)

The 2008 Lacey Act amendments (Pub. L. 110-234, Title VIII) extended the prohibition on trafficking in illegally sourced wildlife to include plants and plant products, including timber. Key provisions:

| Element | Requirement |
|---------|-------------|
| Prohibition | Unlawful to import, export, or sell plants taken in violation of any law (foreign or domestic) |
| Declaration | Importers must declare country of harvest, species, and value |
| Due care standard | Violators must have exercised "due care" to avoid trafficking in illegally sourced products |
| Penalties | Civil: up to $10,000 per violation; Criminal: up to $250,000 fine and 5 years imprisonment (felony for knowing violations) |

### Enforcement Example -- Lumber Liquidators

In 2015, Lumber Liquidators (now LL Flooring) pleaded guilty to Lacey Act violations for importing flooring products made from illegally harvested hardwood from the Russian Far East, paying approximately $13.2 million in fines and forfeiture. The case demonstrated the Lacey Act's reach over global timber supply chains.

### FOREST Act Proposals

The Fostering Overseas Rule of Law and Environmentally Sound Trade (FOREST) Act has been introduced in multiple Congressional sessions, proposing to prohibit the import of commodities (palm oil, soy, cocoa, rubber, cattle products, wood pulp) produced on illegally deforested land. As of 2026, the act has not been enacted.

### State Forest Practice Acts

State regulation of private forest practices varies dramatically. Oregon and Washington have comprehensive forest practice acts requiring reforestation after harvest. Many eastern and southern states have weaker or voluntary programs.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's domestic forest regulation is governed by the Bundeswaldgesetz (BWaldG, Federal Forest Act), which establishes the principle that forests must be managed sustainably (ordnungsgemäße Forstwirtschaft) and that forest conversion requires permission. State forest laws (Landeswaldgesetze) supplement the federal framework with detailed requirements.

### BWaldG Key Provisions

**Section 9 -- Forest Conversion:** Conversion of forest to non-forest use requires permission from the competent authority. Permission may only be granted if the conversion serves overriding public interests and cannot reasonably be achieved on non-forest land. Compensatory reforestation (Ersatzaufforstung) of equivalent area and quality is generally required.

**Section 11 -- Sustainable Management:** Forest owners are obligated to manage their forests in an orderly and sustainable manner. This includes maintaining forest health, regeneration capacity, and ecological functions.

**Certification:** Approximately 75 percent of German forests are certified under PEFC (Programme for the Endorsement of Forest Certification) or FSC (Forest Stewardship Council) standards.

### EUDR Implementation in Germany

Germany is implementing the EU Deforestation Regulation through the designated competent authority (Bundesanstalt für Landwirtschaft und Ernährung, BLE) and adaptation of existing due diligence infrastructure from the predecessor EU Timber Regulation. German companies placing relevant commodities on the market must conduct due diligence including:

- Geolocation data for the production plot
- Verification that the land was not deforested after December 31, 2020
- Compliance with the laws of the country of production
- Assessment and mitigation of risk through a risk management framework

### Forest Climate Contribution

Germany's forests serve as a significant carbon sink, sequestering approximately 62 million tonnes of CO2 equivalent annually. However, drought, bark beetle infestations, and storms have severely damaged German forests since 2018, reducing the carbon sink function. The Federal Ministry of Food and Agriculture allocated EUR 900 million for forest climate adaptation (Waldklimafonds) through 2026.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR, Regulation 2023/1115)

Adopted on May 31, 2023, the EUDR is the world's most ambitious supply chain deforestation regulation:

**Covered Commodities:** Cattle, cocoa, coffee, oil palm, rubber, soya, and wood, plus derived products (leather, chocolate, furniture, paper, etc.).

**Key Requirements:**

| Requirement | Detail |
|-------------|--------|
| Deforestation-free | Products must not have been produced on land deforested after December 31, 2020 |
| Legality | Production must comply with all relevant laws of the country of production |
| Due diligence | Operators and traders must conduct risk assessment using geolocation data |
| Traceability | Full supply chain traceability to the plot of production |
| Country benchmarking | Commission classifies countries as low, standard, or high risk, determining due diligence intensity |

**Timeline:**
- December 30, 2024: Application date for large operators (delayed to December 30, 2025 following implementation challenges)
- June 30, 2025: Application for SMEs (delayed to June 30, 2026)

### EU Timber Regulation (EUTR, Regulation 995/2010)

The EUDR's predecessor, the EUTR, prohibited the placing of illegally harvested timber on the EU market and required operators to exercise due diligence. The EUTR was replaced by the EUDR, which significantly expanded scope, strengthened requirements, and added the deforestation-free criterion.

### LULUCF Regulation

Regulation (EU) 2018/841 on Land Use, Land Use Change and Forestry (LULUCF) requires member states to ensure that accounted emissions from land use are offset by equivalent removals. The revised LULUCF Regulation (2023) sets a target of 310 million tonnes CO2 equivalent of net removals from the LULUCF sector by 2030.

### International Frameworks

**REDD+:** The Reducing Emissions from Deforestation and Forest Degradation framework under the UNFCCC provides financial incentives for developing countries to reduce deforestation. The EU, South Korea, and the United States all support REDD+ programs.

**Glasgow Leaders' Declaration on Forests and Land Use (2021):** Signed by 145 countries representing over 90 percent of the world's forests, committing to halt and reverse forest loss by 2030.

### ECHR and Forest Rights

**Hamer v. Belgium (Application No. 21861/03, 2007):** The Court held that the demolition of a house built in a designated forest zone did not violate Protocol 1, Article 1 (property rights), recognizing the public interest in environmental protection and forest conservation.

## Comparative Analysis

### Domestic vs. Supply Chain Regulation

The most significant divergence is between domestic forest management (where all jurisdictions have frameworks) and supply chain regulation of imported deforestation. The EU's EUDR represents a paradigm shift, imposing deforestation-free requirements on global supply chains. The U.S. Lacey Act addresses illegal logging but not legal deforestation. South Korea has limited supply chain regulation.

### Reforestation Models

South Korea's government-led mass reforestation campaign provides a unique model of rapid recovery through centralized planning and public mobilization. Germany maintains forests through regulatory requirements (compensatory reforestation) and market mechanisms (certification). The United States uses regulatory requirements on public lands but largely voluntary approaches on private land.

### Climate-Forest Nexus

All jurisdictions increasingly frame forest policy in climate terms. The EU's LULUCF regulation makes forest carbon accounting legally binding. Germany's Waldklimafonds addresses climate adaptation for forests. South Korea positions forests as climate mitigation tools in its NDC. The U.S. IRA includes forest-related climate provisions but no binding forest carbon requirements.

### Enforcement Challenges

The EUDR faces significant implementation challenges including smallholder inclusion, geolocation data requirements in complex supply chains, and potential WTO compatibility concerns. The Lacey Act's "due care" standard has proven effective but enforcement is resource-intensive. South Korea's domestic enforcement is strong but supply chain reach is limited.
