## Summary

Unjust enrichment -- the legal principle that a person who has been enriched at another's expense without legal justification must make restitution -- represents one of the foundational concepts of private law, recognized in all major legal systems though with fundamentally different doctrinal structures, terminologies, and remedial frameworks. South Korea codifies unjust enrichment (부당이득, budang ideuk) in the Civil Code (민법) Articles 741-749, adopting a unified model derived from the German BGB tradition, under which a person who has received a benefit from the property or labor of another without legal cause (법률상 원인 없이) is obligated to return that benefit; the Supreme Court of Korea (대법원) has developed extensive case law distinguishing between performance-based enrichment (급여부당이득) and non-performance-based enrichment (비급여부당이득), addressing the measure of restitution, good faith and bad faith enrichment, and the interaction between unjust enrichment and other civil code remedies. The United States recognizes unjust enrichment as an independent basis for restitutionary relief under common law and equity, with the Restatement (Third) of Restitution and Unjust Enrichment (2011) providing the most comprehensive modern statement of the law, encompassing claims for quantum meruit, quantum valebant, constructive trust, equitable lien, subrogation, and disgorgement of wrongful gains, though the precise contours of unjust enrichment vary significantly among states. Germany provides the most elaborately codified unjust enrichment law (Bereicherungsrecht) in the BGB §§ 812-822, establishing a sophisticated system of condictio claims (Leistungskondiktion for performance-based enrichment and Nichtleistungskondiktion for enrichment "in other ways") with detailed rules for the measure of enrichment, the defense of loss of enrichment (Entreicherungseinrede, § 818 Abs. 3), enhanced liability for bad-faith recipients, and specific subsidiary provisions, representing a highly developed doctrinal framework that has influenced civil law systems worldwide including Korea and Japan. The ECHR has addressed unjust enrichment in the context of state seizure of assets, taxation, and property restitution under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1, while EU law increasingly recognizes restitutionary principles in consumer protection (restitution following contract avoidance for unfair terms), competition law (damages and disgorgement for antitrust violations), and cross-border private law through the Rome II Regulation (EC) No. 864/2007 on the law applicable to non-contractual obligations.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's unjust enrichment law, codified in Civil Code Articles 741-749, reflects strong German influence mediated through Japan's Civil Code, though Korean judicial interpretation has developed distinctively.

**Article 741 (General Provision):** "A person who has received a benefit from the property or labor of another without legal cause, thereby causing loss to the other, is obligated to return that benefit." This single provision establishes the four elements of an unjust enrichment claim: (1) enrichment of the defendant; (2) at the expense (loss) of the plaintiff; (3) a causal connection between the enrichment and the loss; and (4) the absence of legal cause (법률상 원인).

### Categories of Unjust Enrichment

The Supreme Court of Korea has adopted the German distinction between performance-based and non-performance-based enrichment:

**Performance-Based Enrichment (급여부당이득):** Arises when a person makes a performance (급여) -- typically payment or delivery of property -- that is later determined to lack legal cause. Examples include payment under a void or avoided contract, overpayment, and payment under a condition that fails. The "absence of legal cause" is assessed with reference to the legal relationship that was intended to justify the performance.

**Non-Performance-Based Enrichment (비급여부당이득):** Arises when enrichment occurs through means other than a voluntary performance by the plaintiff. Examples include: unauthorized use of another's property (무단점유사용), expenditure of resources that benefit another's property (비용지출), interference with another's intellectual property rights, and receipt of benefits from a third party that properly belong to another.

### Measure of Restitution

**Article 748 (Good Faith and Bad Faith Recipients):**

Paragraph 1: A good-faith recipient (선의의 수익자) is obligated to return only the benefit "to the extent it currently exists" (현존하는 이익의 한도에서). If the enrichment has been dissipated, consumed, or lost, the good-faith recipient's obligation is reduced accordingly. The Supreme Court has interpreted "currently existing benefit" broadly, holding that if the recipient used the enrichment to pay debts or acquire other assets, the enrichment continues to exist in the form of the debt discharged or asset acquired.

Paragraph 2: A bad-faith recipient (악의의 수익자) must return the benefit received plus interest (이자), and if there is additional damage (손해), must compensate for it. The Supreme Court has held that a recipient is in bad faith from the time they knew or should have known of the absence of legal cause. Under Article 749, a good-faith recipient who is subsequently sued becomes a bad-faith recipient from the time of service of the lawsuit.

### Supreme Court Jurisprudence

**Void Contracts and Mutual Restitution:** Where a contract is void (무효) or avoided (취소), the Supreme Court has held that both parties have unjust enrichment claims for the return of their respective performances. The Court has addressed the complex issue of whether the party who performed services (rather than delivering tangible property) may recover the objective value of those services or only the actual benefit received by the other party (Supreme Court Decision 2003Da24215).

**Unjust Enrichment and Real Property Registration:** A significant body of Korean unjust enrichment law concerns situations where real property is registered in the name of a person who is not the true owner. The Supreme Court has consistently held that the registered owner who is not the true owner holds the registered title as unjust enrichment, and the true owner may bring an unjust enrichment claim for transfer of registration (등기이전청구) alongside or in lieu of a real right (물권) claim.

**Three-Party Situations (삼각관계):** Where enrichment involves three parties (e.g., A pays B on C's instruction, or A's property is transferred to B through C's wrongful act), the Supreme Court has developed rules for determining which party has the unjust enrichment claim against which other party, drawing on the German Dreipersonenverhältnis doctrine while adapting it to Korean circumstances.

**Statute of Limitations:** Unjust enrichment claims are subject to the general 10-year statute of limitations under Article 162 of the Civil Code, running from the time the claim becomes exercisable. The Supreme Court has held that the claim becomes exercisable when the enrichment occurs (not when the plaintiff discovers it), though good faith/bad faith distinctions affect the measure of recovery.

## United States

### Legal Framework

American unjust enrichment law has undergone a remarkable renaissance in recent decades, catalyzed by the American Law Institute's Restatement (Third) of Restitution and Unjust Enrichment (2011), which systematized and clarified a body of law that had long been characterized as fragmented and doctrinally incoherent.

### Doctrinal Foundations

**Unjust Enrichment as Independent Claim:** The Restatement (Third) establishes unjust enrichment as an independent basis for liability, separate from contract and tort. Section 1 states: "A person who is unjustly enriched at the expense of another is subject to liability in restitution." This broad principle encompasses a wide range of claims previously known by various names, including quasi-contract, implied-in-law contract, constructive trust, equitable lien, and quantum meruit.

**Absence of "Absence of Legal Cause":** Unlike the civil law formulation (German "ohne rechtlichen Grund," Korean "법률상 원인 없이"), American law does not define unjust enrichment by the absence of a legal ground for the enrichment. Instead, the concept of "unjust" enrichment is defined circumstantially: enrichment is unjust when it results from mistake, coercion, fraud, breach of fiduciary duty, or other circumstances making retention unconscionable. The Restatement identifies specific categories of unjust enrichment rather than relying on a single abstract criterion.

### Categories of Restitutionary Claims

**Benefits Conferred by Mistake (Restatement §§ 5-9):** A person who confers a benefit on another by mistake is entitled to restitution. Mistakes of fact and law are both cognizable. The defense of good faith change of position is available to the recipient who, in reliance on the benefit received, has so changed their position that requiring full restitution would be inequitable (Restatement § 65).

**Benefits Conferred Under Void or Unenforceable Contracts (Restatement §§ 31-37):** Where a contract is void, voidable, or unenforceable (e.g., for lack of capacity, illegality, statute of frauds noncompliance, or frustration), the performing party may recover in restitution for benefits conferred. This allows recovery even when the contract itself cannot be enforced.

**Benefits Conferred Through Wrongdoing (Restatement §§ 40-46):** A person who obtains a benefit through fraud, duress, undue influence, or breach of fiduciary duty is subject to restitution. Importantly, the measure of restitution for conscious wrongdoing may be the wrongdoer's profit (disgorgement) rather than the plaintiff's loss, ensuring that wrongdoing does not pay.

**Constructive Trust:** The most distinctive feature of American restitution law, having no direct equivalent in civil law systems. A constructive trust is an equitable remedy that treats the defendant as a trustee of specific property for the plaintiff's benefit. It provides proprietary restitution -- the plaintiff obtains a priority claim to identified property rather than merely a personal claim against the defendant -- which is particularly valuable where the defendant is insolvent. A constructive trust may be imposed on property acquired through fraud, breach of fiduciary duty, mistake, or other circumstances making the defendant's retention unjust. The remedy traces through exchanges, following the plaintiff's property into substitute assets (tracing).

**Equitable Lien:** Similar to a constructive trust but creating a security interest in the defendant's property rather than full ownership. Equitable liens are often used where the plaintiff's contribution has been commingled with the defendant's assets.

**Quantum Meruit:** Recovery for the reasonable value of services rendered, available where services were provided under an unenforceable or void contract, in emergency situations, or where the recipient knowingly accepted services with an opportunity to decline.

### Disgorgement of Wrongful Gains

American law uniquely provides for disgorgement -- measuring restitution by the defendant's gain rather than the plaintiff's loss. In *Snepp v. United States*, 444 U.S. 507 (1980), the Supreme Court imposed a constructive trust on profits from a former CIA agent's book published in breach of his confidentiality agreement, measured by the agent's royalties rather than the government's losses. Disgorgement is available for conscious wrongdoing, breach of fiduciary duty, and certain intellectual property violations.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German unjust enrichment law (Bereicherungsrecht) under BGB §§ 812-822 is among the most doctrinally elaborate bodies of private law in any jurisdiction, reflecting over a century of scholarly development and judicial refinement.

### BGB § 812: The General Enrichment Claim

**§ 812 Abs. 1 Satz 1:** "A person who obtains something through the performance of another person (Leistung) or in some other way (in sonstiger Weise) at the expense of another person without legal ground (ohne rechtlichen Grund) is obligated to return it."

This single provision establishes the two fundamental categories:

**Leistungskondiktion (Performance Condiction, § 812 I 1 Alt. 1):** The claim for restitution of a performance (Leistung) made without legal ground. "Leistung" is defined as a conscious, purposeful increase of another's assets (bewusste und zweckgerichtete Mehrung fremden Vermögens). The purpose of the performance is determined from the perspective of the performing party and the recipient, based on the legal relationship that was intended to justify the performance (Leistungszweck).

**Nichtleistungskondiktion (Non-Performance Condiction, § 812 I 1 Alt. 2):** The claim for restitution of enrichment obtained "in some other way" than through a performance. This residual category covers situations including: unauthorized use of another's property (Eingriffskondiktion), expenditures benefiting another's property (Verwendungskondiktion or Aufwendungskondiktion), and receipt from a third party of benefits belonging to another (Rückgriffskondiktion). The Nichtleistungskondiktion is subsidiary to the Leistungskondiktion -- where enrichment results from a performance, only the person who performed may bring the condiction, and only against the recipient of the performance.

### Specific Condiction Claims

**§ 812 Abs. 1 Satz 2 Alt. 1 -- Condictio ob causam finitam:** Recovery of a performance whose legal ground subsequently falls away (e.g., performance under a condition subsequent that is fulfilled, or under a contract that is later avoided).

**§ 812 Abs. 1 Satz 2 Alt. 2 -- Condictio ob rem:** Recovery of a performance made for a purpose (Zweck) that fails to materialize (e.g., payment in anticipation of a contract that is never concluded, or a gift made in expectation of marriage that does not occur). Distinguished from the general Leistungskondiktion in that the "purpose" is not a legal obligation but an expected future result.

**§ 817 -- Condictio ob turpem vel iniustam causam:** Where the purpose of the performance was such that acceptance violated a statutory prohibition or good morals (Sittenwidrigkeit), the recipient must make restitution. However, § 817 Satz 2 provides that restitution is excluded if the claimant was equally in violation, reflecting the in pari delicto doctrine -- a provision that has generated extensive scholarly debate and judicial refinement.

### Measure of Enrichment and Defense of Loss

**§ 818 Abs. 1-2 (Measure of Enrichment):** The obligation extends to the return of the benefit received (das Erlangte) including fruits (Früchte) and benefits from use (Nutzungen). Where return in kind is impossible (e.g., because the enrichment consisted of services or has been consumed), the recipient must pay the objective value (Wertersatz).

**§ 818 Abs. 3 (Loss of Enrichment -- Entreicherung):** The obligation to return enrichment ceases to the extent the recipient is no longer enriched (soweit der Empfänger nicht mehr bereichert ist). This is the most significant defense in German enrichment law, available only to good-faith recipients. The Bundesgerichtshof (BGH) has developed elaborate rules for determining when enrichment is "no longer existing": the enrichment survives if the recipient used it to satisfy debts, acquire assets, or save expenses they would otherwise have incurred (ersparte Aufwendungen), but is lost if the recipient spent it on luxuries or consumption they would not otherwise have undertaken.

**§§ 818 Abs. 4, 819-820 (Enhanced Liability for Bad Faith):** A recipient who knew of the absence of legal ground at the time of receipt (§ 819) or who subsequently learns of it (§ 818 Abs. 4) is subject to enhanced liability under the rules of return of unjust enrichment corresponding to the rules governing the return obligations after pending litigation (Rechtshängigkeit). This eliminates the loss of enrichment defense and imposes liability for interest, consequential damages, and deterioration of the enrichment.

### Three-Party Situations (Dreipersonenverhältnisse)

The BGH has developed sophisticated rules for three-party enrichment situations, guided by the priority of the Leistungskondiktion and the principle that enrichment claims should follow the performance chains:

**Instruction Cases (Anweisungsfälle):** Where A instructs B to make a payment to C, the BGH treats this as involving two performances: A's performance to C (through B) in the "cover relationship" (Deckungsverhältnis), and A's performance to B in the "value relationship" (Valutaverhältnis). If the instruction is defective, C must return the enrichment to A (not to B); if the value relationship is defective, B must claim against A.

**Double Deficiency Cases (Doppelmangel):** Where both the instruction and the underlying obligation are defective, the BGH generally maintains the principle that the Leistungskondiktion follows the performance chain, though exceptions apply where direct condiction (Durchgriffskondiktion) is necessary to avoid unjust results.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Framework

The ECHR has addressed unjust enrichment principles primarily in the context of state conduct and property restitution.

**State Enrichment and Property:** In *Stran Greek Refineries and Stratis Andreadis v. Greece* (Application No. 13427/87, 1994), the Court held that Greece's retroactive annulment of an arbitral award that would have required the state to pay compensation to the applicants constituted a violation of Article 1 of Protocol No. 1, effectively recognizing that the state's retention of benefits without paying contractually agreed compensation was disproportionate.

**Property Restitution in Transitional States:** The Court has extensively addressed restitution of property confiscated under former communist regimes, holding that while states have a wide margin of appreciation in designing restitution schemes, they must ensure that the schemes operate proportionately and do not impose individual and excessive burdens. In *Broniowski v. Poland* (Application No. 31443/96, Grand Chamber, 2004), the Court identified a systemic violation arising from Poland's failure to implement the statutory right to compensatory property for persons displaced from territories beyond the Bug River.

### EU Unjust Enrichment Framework

**Rome II Regulation (EC) No. 864/2007, Article 10:** Establishes choice-of-law rules for non-contractual obligations arising out of unjust enrichment. Where the obligation arises in connection with a pre-existing relationship (such as a contract), the law governing that relationship applies. Otherwise, the applicable law is determined by: (1) the common habitual residence of the parties; or (2) the law of the country where the enrichment took place; subject to a manifestly closer connection escape clause.

**Consumer Protection Restitution:** Following annulment of an unfair contract term under the Unfair Contract Terms Directive (93/13/EEC), the CJEU has held that consumers are entitled to restitution of amounts paid under the unfair term. In *Banco Español de Crédito* (Case C-618/10, 2012) and subsequent cases, the CJEU developed the principle that national courts must ensure effective restitution, including restoration of the legal and factual situation the consumer would have been in had the unfair term not existed.

**Competition Law Disgorgement:** EU competition law incorporates restitutionary principles through the Damages Directive (2014/104/EU), which facilitates private claims for damages suffered through infringement of competition law, and through the European Commission's power to impose fines that may include disgorgement elements. The relationship between public enforcement, private damages, and restitutionary recovery remains an active area of development.

### Principles of European Private Law

The Draft Common Frame of Reference (DCFR), Books VII (Unjustified Enrichment), though not binding law, represents the most ambitious effort to harmonize European enrichment law. The DCFR adopts a civilian-influenced structure distinguishing between performance-based and non-performance-based enrichment, while incorporating common law concepts such as change of position and proprietary restitution.

## Comparative Analysis

### Structural Models

The jurisdictions reveal three fundamentally different approaches to structuring unjust enrichment law. Germany's highly codified, category-based system provides doctrinal precision but considerable complexity, particularly in three-party situations. Korea has adopted the German structure but with a somewhat more flexible judicial approach. The United States takes a pragmatic, case-type approach, organizing restitutionary claims by the circumstances of enrichment (mistake, fraud, breach of duty) rather than by abstract doctrinal categories. The ECHR and EU operate at a higher level of abstraction, applying unjust enrichment principles through proportionality analysis and specific regulatory contexts rather than through a general enrichment doctrine.

### Proprietary vs. Personal Restitution

The most significant structural difference is between the American system, which provides proprietary restitution through constructive trusts and equitable liens, and the German and Korean systems, which provide only personal (in personam) claims. Proprietary restitution gives the claimant priority over the defendant's other creditors in insolvency -- a powerful advantage that reflects the equitable tradition of common law. German and Korean law achieve some analogous results through other doctrines (e.g., Aussonderung in insolvency for property that never belonged to the debtor), but lack a general mechanism for imposing proprietary restitutionary claims.

### Loss of Enrichment and Change of Position

All systems recognize that a good-faith recipient who has lost the enrichment should have a defense, but the doctrinal implementation differs. Germany's § 818 Abs. 3 operates as a strict defense of loss of enrichment, while the American change of position defense (Restatement § 65) requires detrimental reliance on the receipt, introducing an element of causation absent from the German formulation. Korea follows the German approach but with some judicial flexibility.

### Disgorgement

The availability of gain-based recovery (measuring restitution by the defendant's profit rather than the plaintiff's loss) varies significantly. American law most broadly provides for disgorgement, particularly for conscious wrongdoing and breach of fiduciary duty. German law provides disgorgement through the Eingriffskondiktion (recovery of the objective value of unauthorized use) and through the GoA (Geschäftsführung ohne Auftrag) provisions, but the measure is generally the objective value of what was taken rather than the subjective profit earned by the defendant. Korean law follows the German approach, with the Supreme Court increasingly willing to measure enrichment by the objective value of unauthorized use.
