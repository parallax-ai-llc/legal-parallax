## Summary

Territorial disputes -- legal and political contests over sovereignty, boundaries, and jurisdiction over land and maritime areas -- remain among the most intractable challenges in international law, implicating principles of self-determination, territorial integrity, uti possidetis, effective occupation, and historic title. South Korea is directly involved in one of Asia's most sensitive territorial disputes over the Dokdo/Takeshima islands (독도/竹島) with Japan, which Korea has effectively controlled since 1954 while Japan claims sovereignty; additionally, the division of the Korean Peninsula itself represents the world's most enduring territorial and sovereignty dispute, with both the Republic of Korea and the Democratic People's Republic of Korea claiming sovereignty over the entire peninsula under their respective constitutions. The United States has been involved in numerous territorial disputes both historically and in the present, ranging from resolved bilateral disputes (the Alaska boundary dispute, 1903; the Oregon boundary dispute, 1846) to ongoing questions regarding the status of territories such as Guam, Puerto Rico, American Samoa, and the US Virgin Islands, with the Supreme Court's Insular Cases (1901-1922) and the ongoing Territories Clause jurisprudence defining the constitutional framework. Germany's territorial configuration was fundamentally reshaped by the post-World War II settlement, the division into East and West Germany, and German reunification through the Two Plus Four Treaty (1990); Germany settled its post-war border disputes definitively through the German-Polish Border Treaty of 1990 confirming the Oder-Neisse line. At the European level, the International Court of Justice has adjudicated numerous territorial disputes applying principles of uti possidetis, effective occupation, and treaty interpretation, the ECHR has addressed territorial sovereignty questions through the lens of jurisdiction under Article 1 and the right to property under Protocol 1, and the EU's enlargement and association frameworks have required resolution of territorial disputes as preconditions for membership.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's territorial framework is anchored in Article 3 of the Constitution (헌법): "The territory of the Republic of Korea shall consist of the Korean peninsula and its adjacent islands." This provision, dating from the original 1948 Constitution, asserts sovereignty over the entire Korean peninsula, including the territory controlled by the DPRK.

### Dokdo/Takeshima Dispute (독도 분쟁)

The Dokdo islands (독도, known in Japan as Takeshima/竹島) are a group of small islets in the East Sea/Sea of Japan, effectively controlled by South Korea since 1954 when Korea stationed a police garrison. Japan claims sovereignty, asserting incorporation of the islands into Shimane Prefecture in 1905.

**Korean Legal Position:** Korea's claim rests on historical title dating to the Shilla Dynasty (512 AD, annexation of Usan-guk) and the Joseon Dynasty's documented governance. The Dokdo Sustainable Use Act (독도의 지속가능한 이용에 관한 법률) and the Ordinance on the Address of Dokdo (독도 주소) establish domestic administrative governance. Korea maintains that Japan's 1905 incorporation was void as an act of colonial aggression predating the 1910 annexation.

**Japan's Position:** Japan argues that it incorporated terra nullius in 1905 and that the 1951 San Francisco Peace Treaty did not list Takeshima among territories Japan was required to renounce. Japan has proposed referring the dispute to the ICJ three times (1954, 1962, 2012), each time rejected by Korea on the grounds that there is no dispute to adjudicate.

### Korean Peninsula Division

The division of Korea following World War II and the Korean War represents a unique territorial situation. The 1953 Armistice Agreement established the Military Demarcation Line (MDL) and the Demilitarized Zone (DMZ), but no peace treaty has been concluded. Both constitutions claim sovereignty over the entire peninsula. The Northern Limit Line (NLL, 북방한계선) in the West Sea/Yellow Sea, unilaterally established by the UN Command in 1953, has been a particular source of tension, with North Korea disputing its validity.

### Maritime Boundaries

South Korea ratified UNCLOS on January 29, 1996, and declared an Exclusive Economic Zone (EEZ) of 200 nautical miles. Overlapping EEZ claims with Japan and China require bilateral delimitation, complicated by the Dokdo/Takeshima dispute and the presence of Ieodo/Suyan Rock (이어도/蘇岩礁) -- a submerged rock in the overlap zone with China, on which Korea has built the Ieodo Ocean Research Station.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The US Constitution addresses territory in Article IV, Section 3 ("The Congress shall have Power to dispose of and make all needful Rules and Regulations respecting the Territory or other Property belonging to the United States") and through the Admissions Clause for new states. The Treaty Power (Article II, Section 2) governs territorial acquisitions and boundary settlements.

### Territorial Acquisitions and Resolved Disputes

The United States resolved numerous territorial disputes through treaties and arbitration: the Louisiana Purchase (1803); the Oregon Treaty (1846) resolving the US-British boundary at the 49th parallel; the Alaska Purchase (1867); the Alaska Boundary Tribunal (1903) resolving the boundary with Canada; and the Treaty of Paris (1898) acquiring territories from Spain.

### Insular Cases and Territorial Status

The Insular Cases (1901-1922), a series of Supreme Court decisions, established the constitutional framework for US territories. Downes v. Bidwell, 182 U.S. 244 (1901), held that the Constitution does not automatically extend all rights to unincorporated territories. This doctrine -- which Justice Gorsuch criticized in United States v. Vaello Madero, 596 U.S. 159 (2022) as rooted in "ugly racial stereotypes" -- continues to define the legal status of approximately 3.6 million residents of US territories.

**Puerto Rico:** The most significant ongoing territorial status question. Puerto Rico has held multiple status referendums (1967, 1993, 1998, 2012, 2017, 2020), with the 2020 referendum showing 52.5% support for statehood. The Puerto Rico Status Act was introduced in Congress in 2022 but not enacted.

### Maritime Boundaries

The United States has not ratified UNCLOS but recognizes most provisions as reflective of customary international law. The US has resolved maritime boundary disputes with numerous states through bilateral treaties and has delineated maritime boundaries with Canada, Mexico, Cuba, Russia, and other states.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's territorial configuration was definitively settled through the reunification process. Article 23 of the Grundgesetz originally listed the Länder constituting the Federal Republic; upon reunification on October 3, 1990, the five eastern Länder acceded under this provision. Article 23 was subsequently amended to address European integration rather than territorial accession.

### Two Plus Four Treaty (1990)

The Treaty on the Final Settlement with Respect to Germany (Vertrag über die abschließende Regelung in bezug auf Deutschland), signed on September 12, 1990, by the Federal Republic of Germany, the German Democratic Republic, and the Four Powers (United States, Soviet Union, United Kingdom, France), definitively settled Germany's post-war territorial status. Article 1 confirmed that the united Germany would comprise the territories of the FRG, GDR, and all of Berlin. Article 1(2) confirmed the existing German-Polish border along the Oder-Neisse line.

### German-Polish Border Treaty (1990)

The German-Polish Border Treaty (Deutsch-polnischer Grenzvertrag), signed on November 14, 1990, confirmed the Oder-Neisse line as the permanent border between Germany and Poland. This treaty resolved one of Europe's most contentious post-war territorial questions, with Germany definitively renouncing claims to territories east of the Oder-Neisse line (Silesia, Pomerania, East Prussia).

### Saar Question

The Saar Protectorate, administered by France after World War II, was reintegrated into the Federal Republic on January 1, 1957, following a 1955 referendum in which Saar residents rejected an autonomous European status. This was the last territorial change involving Germany before reunification.

### Post-Reunification Status

| Element | Status |
|---------|--------|
| Oder-Neisse line | Confirmed as permanent border (German-Polish Border Treaty 1990) |
| Berlin | Unified; all sectors incorporated into Federal Republic |
| Eastern territories (Silesia, Pomerania, E. Prussia) | Definitively renounced under Two Plus Four Treaty |
| Maritime boundaries (Baltic/North Sea) | Delimited under UNCLOS and bilateral treaties |

## ECHR & EU

### International Court of Justice

The ICJ has adjudicated numerous territorial disputes, establishing fundamental principles of international law.

**Sovereignty over Pedra Branca/Pulau Batu Puteh (Malaysia/Singapore, 2008):** The Court applied principles of original title, effectivités (effective administration), and acquiescence to resolve competing claims.

**Territorial and Maritime Dispute (Nicaragua v. Colombia, 2012):** The Court applied uti possidetis juris and treaty interpretation to determine sovereignty over islands and delimit maritime boundaries.

**Sovereignty over Certain Frontier Land (Belgium/Netherlands, 1959):** An early ICJ case applying treaty interpretation and acquiescence to resolve boundary disputes.

### ECHR and Territorial Jurisdiction

**Loizidou v. Turkey (Application No. 15318/89, Grand Chamber Judgment of December 18, 1996):** The landmark case established that Turkey's effective control over northern Cyprus brought that territory within Turkey's jurisdiction under Article 1 ECHR. The Court held that Turkey was responsible for ECHR compliance in areas under its effective military control, even if not formally part of Turkish territory. This principle has been applied extensively in subsequent cases involving military occupation.

**Cyprus v. Turkey (Application No. 25781/94, Grand Chamber Judgment of May 10, 2001):** The Court found Turkey responsible for widespread and continuing ECHR violations in northern Cyprus, including violations of property rights (Article 1 Protocol 1) of displaced Greek Cypriot owners.

**Ilascu and Others v. Moldova and Russia (Application No. 48787/99, Grand Chamber Judgment of July 8, 2004):** The Court addressed the situation in Transnistria, holding that both Moldova (for failing to take measures within its power) and Russia (for effective military, economic, and political support of the separatist regime) bore responsibility under the ECHR.

### EU Enlargement and Territorial Disputes

The EU has used the enlargement process to encourage resolution of territorial disputes. The accession of Cyprus in 2004 (with the acquis communautaire suspended in northern Cyprus) demonstrates both the EU's willingness to admit states with unresolved territorial issues and the complications that result. The EU's insistence on good neighborly relations and border dispute resolution as accession conditions has influenced disputes in the Western Balkans (Serbia-Kosovo, North Macedonia-Greece name dispute resolved in 2018 Prespa Agreement).

## Comparative Analysis

### Nature of Disputes

The jurisdictions face fundamentally different types of territorial challenges. South Korea confronts an island sovereignty dispute (Dokdo) and a peninsula-wide sovereignty question. The United States deals with questions of territorial governance and decolonization status. Germany has resolved its territorial disputes definitively through the reunification treaties. The EU and ECHR frameworks address territorial sovereignty primarily through jurisdiction and human rights lenses.

### Dispute Resolution Preferences

The jurisdictions differ significantly in their preferred dispute resolution mechanisms. South Korea refuses ICJ jurisdiction for Dokdo, maintaining that bilateral negotiation is inappropriate for an issue it considers settled. The United States has used ICJ jurisdiction selectively and increasingly prefers bilateral negotiation. Germany settled its disputes through multilateral treaty. The EU uses accession conditionality as a soft power tool for dispute resolution.

### Legal Principles

Different legal principles predominate in each jurisdiction's approach. South Korea emphasizes historical title and effective control. The United States relies on treaty law and constitutional provisions. Germany's approach was governed by the unique post-war settlement framework. The ICJ and ECHR apply principles of uti possidetis, effective occupation, and jurisdiction-based analysis.
