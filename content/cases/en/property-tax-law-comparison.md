## Summary

Property tax -- a recurring levy on the ownership of real property (land and improvements) and in some jurisdictions personal property -- constitutes one of the oldest and most stable forms of taxation, serving as the primary revenue source for local governments in many countries while generating persistent debates over assessment fairness, regressivity, and the appropriate balance between funding public services and protecting homeowners from excessive tax burdens. South Korea imposes property tax (재산세) under the Local Tax Act (지방세법) at rates ranging from 0.1 percent to 0.5 percent of assessed value for residential property, supplemented by the Comprehensive Real Estate Tax (종합부동산세) under its dedicated statute targeting high-value property holdings with progressive rates of 0.5 percent to 2.7 percent, a national-level tax introduced in 2005 specifically to address speculative real estate accumulation. The United States relies on property taxes as the principal local government revenue source, with rates and assessment practices determined at the state and local level, resulting in enormous variation across approximately 87,000 taxing jurisdictions, with effective rates ranging from 0.27 percent (Hawaii) to over 2.2 percent (New Jersey) of market value, constrained by constitutional limitations such as California's Proposition 13 (1978) capping annual increases at 2 percent. Germany has undergone a historic transformation of its property tax (Grundsteuer) following the Bundesverfassungsgericht's landmark decision of April 10, 2018 (1 BvL 11/14) declaring the existing system unconstitutional due to reliance on 1964 (West) and 1935 (East) property valuations, with the Grundsteuer-Reform taking full effect on January 1, 2025, implementing a value-based federal model while allowing Länder to adopt alternative models under an opening clause. At the EU level, property taxation remains entirely within member state competence, though the European Commission has repeatedly recommended shifting taxation from labor to property as less harmful to economic growth, and the ECHR has confirmed that property taxes constitute legitimate interference with property rights under Protocol 1, Article 1, subject to proportionality requirements.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea operates a two-tier property tax system. The base-level property tax (재산세) is a local tax governed by the Local Tax Act (지방세법), collected by municipal governments (시/군/구). The Comprehensive Real Estate Tax (종합부동산세, CRET) is a national-level tax under its own statute (종합부동산세법, Act No. 19926), functioning as a supplementary progressive tax on aggregate real property holdings above specified thresholds. Revenue from the CRET is distributed to local governments through the local share tax system.

### Property Tax (재산세) Rate Structure

| Property Type | Assessed Value Bracket | Rate |
|--------------|----------------------|------|
| Residential | Up to 60 million KRW | 0.1% |
| Residential | 60-150 million KRW | 0.15% |
| Residential | 150-300 million KRW | 0.25% |
| Residential | Over 300 million KRW | 0.4% |
| Commercial/Office | General rate | 0.25% |
| Land (general) | Progressive: 0.2-0.5% | Varies |
| Land (separate taxation) | Varies | 0.2-0.4% |

The assessed value (공시가격) is determined by the Ministry of Land, Infrastructure and Transport through the officially announced land price (공시지가) system and apartment price (공동주택공시가격) system. The government has been gradually increasing the ratio of assessed value to market value (시가반영률), which rose from approximately 55 percent in 2017 to approximately 69 percent in 2023.

### Comprehensive Real Estate Tax (종합부동산세)

The CRET was introduced in 2005 under the Roh Moo-hyun administration as a tool to combat real estate speculation. It applies to aggregate holdings above:

| Property Type | Threshold |
|--------------|-----------|
| Residential (general) | 900 million KRW (공시가격) |
| Residential (single homeowner) | 1.2 billion KRW |
| Land (comprehensive) | 500 million KRW |
| Land (separate) | 8 billion KRW |

CRET rates for residential property (post-2023 reform):

| Taxable Base | Rate (General) | Rate (Multi-homeowner in regulated area) |
|-------------|---------------|----------------------------------------|
| Up to 300 million KRW | 0.5% | 0.5% |
| 300 million - 600 million | 0.7% | 0.7% |
| 600 million - 1.2 billion | 1.0% | 1.0% |
| 1.2 billion - 2.5 billion | 1.3% | 1.3% |
| 2.5 billion - 5 billion | 1.5% | 1.5% |
| Over 5 billion | 2.7% | 2.7% |

The Moon Jae-in administration dramatically increased CRET rates for multi-homeowners in 2020, with rates reaching up to 6 percent. The Yoon Suk-yeol administration reduced these rates in 2023 and eliminated the differential between general and multi-homeowner rates.

### Constitutional Challenges

The Constitutional Court of Korea (헌법재판소) in Decision 2006Hun-Ba112 (2008) upheld the constitutionality of the CRET, rejecting arguments that the tax violated property rights under Article 23 and the equality principle under Article 11 of the Constitution. The Court held that the tax served the legitimate purpose of stabilizing housing prices and correcting wealth concentration.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Property taxation in the United States is almost exclusively a state and local matter. No federal property tax exists. Each state's constitution and statutes establish the framework, with approximately 87,000 local taxing jurisdictions (counties, municipalities, school districts, special districts) setting their own rates within state-imposed constraints. The Fourteenth Amendment's Equal Protection Clause provides the federal constitutional floor for assessment equity.

### Assessment and Valuation

Most states require assessment at "fair market value," though assessment ratios vary:

| State Example | Assessment Ratio | Effective Rate |
|--------------|-----------------|----------------|
| New Jersey | 100% of market value | ~2.23% |
| Illinois (Cook County) | 10% (residential) of market value | ~2.07% |
| California | Acquisition value + max 2%/year (Prop 13) | ~0.71% |
| Hawaii | 100% of market value | ~0.27% |
| Texas | 100% of market value (with 10% homestead cap) | ~1.60% |

Assessment is typically performed by county assessors or appraisers using mass appraisal techniques. Property owners may challenge assessments through administrative appeals (boards of equalization or assessment review boards) and ultimately through judicial review.

### Constitutional Limitations

**Proposition 13 (California, 1978):** Article XIIIA of the California Constitution limits the ad valorem tax rate to 1 percent of assessed value, with assessed value based on the 1975-76 assessment or subsequent acquisition value, increasing by no more than 2 percent annually unless the property changes ownership or new construction occurs. Proposition 19 (2020) modified the intergenerational transfer exclusion, limiting the ability of children to inherit the parent's low assessment unless they use the property as a primary residence.

**Other State Caps:** Michigan (Proposal A, 1994): assessment increases capped at 5 percent or inflation annually. Oregon (Measure 50, 1997): assessed value frozen at 90 percent of 1995 value, increasing by no more than 3 percent annually. Florida (Amendment 10, 1992): Save Our Homes cap of 3 percent annual increase for homesteaded property.

### Homestead Exemptions

Most states provide homestead exemptions reducing the taxable value of owner-occupied primary residences:

- **Texas:** $100,000 homestead exemption for school district taxes; additional $10,000 for over-65 or disabled
- **Florida:** $50,000 homestead exemption ($25,000 off all taxes, additional $25,000 off non-school taxes)
- **Georgia:** Various local homestead exemptions; statewide $2,000 exemption
- **New York:** STAR program (School Tax Relief) providing exemptions of $30,000 (basic) or income-based (enhanced for seniors)

### Landmark Cases

**Allegheny Pittsburgh Coal Co. v. County Commission, 488 U.S. 336 (1989):** The Supreme Court held that a county assessor's practice of assessing recently purchased property at the acquisition price while failing to update assessments of comparable property violated the Equal Protection Clause.

**Nordlinger v. Hahn, 505 U.S. 1 (1992):** The Court upheld California's Proposition 13 acquisition-value system against an Equal Protection challenge, finding a rational basis in the state's interest in neighborhood stability and protecting homeowners from displacement by rising assessments.

**Kelo v. City of New London, 545 U.S. 469 (2005):** While primarily a takings case, the decision highlighted the connection between property tax revenue and local government power, as the economic development plan at issue was partly motivated by expanding the tax base.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's property tax (Grundsteuer) is governed by the Grundsteuergesetz (GrStG) and the Bewertungsgesetz (BewG). It is a municipal tax, with Gemeinden (municipalities) setting a local multiplier (Hebesatz) applied to the assessed value (Grundsteuermessbetrag). The tax is divided into Grundsteuer A (agricultural and forestry land) and Grundsteuer B (all other real property, including residential and commercial).

### The Grundsteuer Reform

The Bundesverfassungsgericht's decision of April 10, 2018 (1 BvL 11/14, et al.) declared the existing Grundsteuer unconstitutional because the underlying property valuations (Einheitswerte) were based on property values from January 1, 1964 (West Germany) and January 1, 1935 (East Germany). The Court found that the massive divergence between these outdated valuations and actual property values violated the principle of equality under Article 3(1) of the Grundgesetz, as properties of similar value could face vastly different tax burdens depending on when they were last valued.

The Court gave the legislature until December 31, 2019, to enact new legislation, with a transition period until December 31, 2024. The new law took full effect on January 1, 2025.

### Federal Model (Bundesmodell)

The federal model, championed by then-Finance Minister Olaf Scholz, calculates the Grundsteuerwert based on:

- **For residential property (Wohngrundstücke):** A simplified capitalized earnings value method (Ertragswertverfahren) considering the property's standard land value (Bodenrichtwert), building type, year of construction, living area, and statistical rent levels (Mietniveaustufen)
- **For commercial property (Nichtwohngrundstücke):** A simplified asset value method (Sachwertverfahren) considering the standard land value and the building's replacement cost less depreciation
- **For undeveloped land (unbebaute Grundstücke):** The product of the land area and the standard land value (Bodenrichtwert)

The three-step calculation: (1) Grundsteuerwert (assessed property value) x (2) Steuermesszahl (base rate: 0.031% for residential, 0.034% for commercial) = Grundsteuermessbetrag, then x (3) Hebesatz (municipal multiplier, typically 300-900%).

### Länder Models (Öffnungsklausel)

The constitutional amendment (Article 72(3) sentence 1 No. 7 GG) permits Länder to deviate from the federal model:

| Land | Model |
|------|-------|
| Baden-Württemberg | Modified land value model (Bodenwertmodell) based solely on land value and area |
| Bavaria | Area-based model (Flächenmodell) using only land area and building area, without value component |
| Hamburg | Wohnlagenmodell with residential location surcharges |
| Hessen | Flächen-Faktor-Verfahren combining area with a location factor |
| Lower Saxony | Flächen-Lage-Modell with area and location factors |
| Saxony | Modified federal model with adjusted Steuermesszahl |

Bavaria's area-based model is the most radical departure, deliberately avoiding any value component to simplify administration and prevent valuation disputes. Critics argue it violates the ability-to-pay principle by taxing a modest house on a large lot more heavily than a luxury apartment.

### Revenue Neutrality Commitment

The federal and Länder governments pledged that the reform would be revenue-neutral overall, though individual tax burdens will shift significantly. Municipalities were urged to adjust their Hebesätze to offset the revaluation effects. In practice, some municipalities have increased aggregate Grundsteuer revenue, drawing criticism from taxpayer associations.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Property Tax Jurisprudence

The ECHR has consistently held that property taxes constitute lawful interference with property rights:

**Sporrong and Lönnroth v. Sweden (Application No. 7151/75, 1982):** While focused on expropriation, this landmark case established the framework for analyzing property rights interference, requiring that any interference serve a legitimate aim and maintain a "fair balance" between public interest and individual rights.

**Gasus Dosier- und Fördertechnik GmbH v. Netherlands (Application No. 15375/89, 1995):** The Court confirmed that tax measures fall within the second paragraph of Protocol 1, Article 1 (right to secure payment of taxes), which affords states a wide margin of appreciation. The Court noted that "a financial liability arising out of the raising of taxes or contributions may adversely affect the guarantee secured under this provision if it places an excessive burden on the person or entity concerned."

**N.K.M. v. Hungary (Application No. 66529/11, 2013):** The Court found a violation where a 98 percent retroactive tax on severance payments was confiscatory, establishing that even under the wide margin of appreciation for tax matters, rates that effectively amount to confiscation violate Protocol 1, Article 1.

### European Commission Recommendations

The European Commission has repeatedly recommended that member states shift taxation from labor (income and social contributions) toward property as less damaging to economic growth:

- **Country-Specific Recommendations (European Semester):** The Commission has regularly recommended that member states with low recurrent property taxation (notably Germany prior to the reform, and several Eastern European states) increase reliance on property taxes to reduce the tax wedge on labor.
- **Tax Reforms in EU Member States (2015 Report):** Identified recurrent property taxation as the "least harmful tax for growth" and noted that most member states under-utilize this revenue source.

### Cross-Border Property Taxation

The CJEU has generally not intervened in property tax design, as property taxation does not typically create cross-border obstacles. However, in Halley (C-132/10, 2011), the Court examined whether a Belgian property tax surcharge applied differently to Belgian and foreign property could be challenged under the free movement of capital.

## Comparative Analysis

### Revenue Role and Local Government Funding

The United States is uniquely dependent on property taxation for local government funding, with property taxes representing 30 to 70 percent of local own-source revenue. South Korea's property tax contributes approximately 20 percent of local tax revenue, supplemented by national transfers. Germany's Grundsteuer constitutes only about 15 percent of municipal tax revenue, with municipalities relying more heavily on the Gewerbesteuer (local business tax) and share allocations from income and value-added tax.

### Assessment Approaches

The three jurisdictions represent fundamentally different assessment philosophies. The United States generally aims for market value assessment with periodic reappraisal, though acquisition-value systems (California, Michigan) create significant horizontal inequity. South Korea's officially announced price (공시가격) system provides centralized, annually updated assessments that historically understate market value but have been gradually converging. Germany's new system represents a compromise between value-based and area-based approaches, with Länder free to choose the degree of value sensitivity.

### Speculation Deterrence

South Korea is unique in explicitly using property taxation as a tool to combat real estate speculation, through the Comprehensive Real Estate Tax targeting high-value and multi-property holdings. Neither the United States nor Germany employs property taxation with a comparable anti-speculative purpose, though both may impose higher rates on vacant land or non-owner-occupied property at the local level.

### Constitutional Constraints

All three jurisdictions have seen significant constitutional litigation over property taxation. The U.S. Supreme Court has permitted wide variation under rational basis review (Nordlinger). Germany's Bundesverfassungsgericht required a complete system overhaul based on the equality principle. South Korea's Constitutional Court upheld the CRET as a legitimate exercise of the taxing power. These different outcomes reflect differing judicial approaches to equality in taxation.

### Reform Trajectories

Germany's Grundsteuer reform represents the most fundamental restructuring of property taxation in any major economy in decades. The United States faces growing pressure to address assessment inequities, particularly the racial disparities in property tax assessments documented by researchers such as Avenancio-León and Howard (2022). South Korea continues to adjust CRET rates and thresholds as political power alternates between progressive and conservative governments.
