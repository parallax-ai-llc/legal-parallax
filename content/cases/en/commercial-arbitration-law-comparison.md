## Summary

International commercial arbitration -- the resolution of cross-border business disputes by private tribunals whose awards are binding and enforceable across national boundaries -- has become the dominant mechanism for resolving international commercial disputes, supported by a global framework of treaties, national arbitration laws, and institutional rules that provide parties with neutrality, expertise, confidentiality, and enforceability advantages over national court litigation. South Korea adopted the Arbitration Act (중재법) in 1966, substantially revised it in 1999 to align with the UNCITRAL Model Law on International Commercial Arbitration, and amended it again in 2016 to incorporate the 2006 Model Law revisions; the Korean Commercial Arbitration Board (대한상사중재원, KCAB), established in 1966 and reorganized with separate domestic and international divisions (KCAB International) in 2012, administers arbitrations under its own rules and has positioned Seoul as a growing arbitration hub in the Asia-Pacific region. The United States governs international commercial arbitration through the Federal Arbitration Act (FAA, 9 U.S.C. Sections 1-16), Chapter 2 of which implements the New York Convention on the Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards (1958), while major arbitral institutions including the American Arbitration Association's International Centre for Dispute Resolution (AAA-ICDR), JAMS International, and the Silicon Valley Arbitration & Mediation Center provide institutional support. Germany enacted its modern arbitration law through the Tenth Book of the Zivilprozessordnung (ZPO Sections 1025-1066), adopted in 1998 based on the UNCITRAL Model Law, and hosts a well-developed arbitration ecosystem centered on the German Institution of Arbitration (Deutsche Institution für Schiedsgerichtsbarkeit, DIS), which published revised DIS Rules in 2018. At the international level, the 1958 New York Convention (ratified by 172 states), the UNCITRAL Model Law (adopted by over 85 states), the ICSID Convention for investor-state disputes, and institutional rules from the ICC International Court of Arbitration, the London Court of International Arbitration (LCIA), and the Singapore International Arbitration Centre (SIAC) provide the foundational architecture for the global arbitration system.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's arbitration law reflects a deliberate strategy to establish Seoul as a major international arbitration seat. The Arbitration Act (중재법), originally enacted in 1966, was comprehensively rewritten in 1999 to adopt the UNCITRAL Model Law on International Commercial Arbitration and further amended in 2016 to incorporate the 2006 Model Law revisions on interim measures and the form of the arbitration agreement.

**Key Provisions:** The Act applies to both domestic and international arbitrations seated in South Korea. Article 3 adopts the Model Law's definition of an arbitration agreement as an agreement to submit present or future disputes to arbitration. Article 8 requires courts to refer parties to arbitration when seized of a matter subject to a valid arbitration agreement, unless the agreement is null and void, inoperative, or incapable of being performed.

### KCAB and KCAB International

The Korean Commercial Arbitration Board (대한상사중재원), established in 1966, is the principal arbitral institution in South Korea. In 2012, KCAB established KCAB International as a separate division with its own rules designed specifically for international arbitrations. The KCAB International Arbitration Rules, most recently revised in 2016, provide for a streamlined appointment process, emergency arbitrator provisions, and expedited procedures for low-value disputes.

**Caseload Statistics:** KCAB administered approximately 580 arbitration cases in 2024, of which roughly 120 involved at least one foreign party. The most common sectors include construction, shipbuilding, international trade, and technology licensing.

### Seoul International Dispute Resolution Center (SIDRC)

The Seoul International Dispute Resolution Center, established in 2013 with government support, provides state-of-the-art hearing facilities for international arbitrations seated in Seoul. The Center offers hearing rooms, breakout rooms, document management facilities, and simultaneous interpretation services, positioning Seoul as a competitive alternative to established Asian arbitration seats such as Singapore and Hong Kong.

### Judicial Support and Review

Korean courts have developed a pro-arbitration jurisprudence consistent with Model Law principles. The Supreme Court of Korea (대법원) has affirmed the principle of competence-competence (allowing arbitral tribunals to rule on their own jurisdiction), enforced arbitration agreements broadly, and limited judicial review of arbitral awards to the grounds specified in Article 36 of the Arbitration Act (corresponding to Model Law Article 34 and New York Convention Article V).

### Recognition and Enforcement

South Korea acceded to the New York Convention in 1973 with a reciprocity reservation (applying the Convention only to awards made in other contracting states) and a commercial reservation (applying the Convention only to commercial disputes). Korean courts have consistently recognized and enforced foreign arbitral awards, with refusal rates among the lowest in the Asia-Pacific region.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States approaches international commercial arbitration through the Federal Arbitration Act, supplemented by state arbitration laws and extensive federal court jurisprudence.

**FAA Chapter 1 (9 U.S.C. Sections 1-16):** Establishes the general federal policy favoring arbitration, providing for enforcement of arbitration agreements (Section 2), stays of litigation pending arbitration (Section 3), compulsion of arbitration (Section 4), and judicial confirmation (Section 9) and vacatur (Section 10) of arbitral awards.

**FAA Chapter 2 (9 U.S.C. Sections 201-208):** Implements the New York Convention, providing federal jurisdiction for actions relating to arbitration agreements and awards falling under the Convention. Section 207 requires federal courts to confirm Convention awards unless one of the grounds for refusal under Article V of the Convention applies.

### Grounds for Vacatur

The grounds for setting aside domestic arbitral awards under FAA Section 10 are extremely narrow:

| Ground | Section 10 Provision |
|--------|---------------------|
| Corruption, fraud, or undue means | Section 10(a)(1) |
| Evident partiality or corruption of arbitrator | Section 10(a)(2) |
| Arbitrator misconduct in refusing to postpone hearing, excluding pertinent evidence, or other misbehavior | Section 10(a)(3) |
| Arbitrators exceeded powers or imperfectly executed them | Section 10(a)(4) |

The "manifest disregard of the law" standard, once recognized as a non-statutory ground for vacatur, has been the subject of circuit court disagreement following the Supreme Court's decision in Hall Street Associates, L.L.C. v. Mattel, Inc., 552 U.S. 576 (2008), which held that the FAA Section 10 grounds are exclusive but left open whether manifest disregard survives as a judicial gloss on those grounds.

### Major Arbitral Institutions

The American Arbitration Association's International Centre for Dispute Resolution (ICDR) is the primary U.S.-based international arbitral institution, administering approximately 1,000 international cases annually under its International Dispute Resolution Procedures. JAMS International provides an alternative institutional framework, while ad hoc arbitrations under the UNCITRAL Arbitration Rules are also common for disputes involving U.S. parties.

### Pro-Arbitration Federal Policy

The Supreme Court has consistently upheld a strong federal policy favoring arbitration, as established in Moses H. Cone Memorial Hospital v. Mercury Construction Corp., 460 U.S. 1 (1983), and extended through decisions including Mitsubishi Motors Corp. v. Soler Chrysler-Plymouth, Inc., 473 U.S. 614 (1985) (antitrust claims arbitrable), Scherk v. Alberto-Culver Co., 417 U.S. 506 (1974) (securities claims in international contracts arbitrable), and BG Group plc v. Republic of Argentina, 572 U.S. 25 (2014) (procedural prerequisites to arbitration are generally for arbitrators to decide).

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's arbitration law, codified in the Tenth Book of the Zivilprozessordnung (ZPO Sections 1025-1066), was adopted in 1998 as a faithful implementation of the UNCITRAL Model Law. The reform replaced the prior German arbitration law, which dated from 1879 with amendments in 1986, and established Germany as a Model Law jurisdiction with a modern, internationally recognized arbitration framework.

**ZPO Section 1029 -- Arbitration Agreement:** Defines the arbitration agreement as an agreement by the parties to submit present or future disputes to arbitration. The agreement must be contained in a document signed by the parties or in an exchange of letters, telefaxes, telegrams, or other means of telecommunication providing a record of the agreement. Section 1031 provides specific form requirements for consumer arbitration agreements.

**ZPO Section 1040 -- Competence-Competence:** The arbitral tribunal may rule on its own jurisdiction, including objections to the existence or validity of the arbitration agreement. This provision implements the Kompetenz-Kompetenz principle, which is a fundamental feature of both the UNCITRAL Model Law and German arbitration practice.

### DIS (Deutsche Institution für Schiedsgerichtsbarkeit)

The German Institution of Arbitration (DIS), founded in 1920 and reorganized in 1992, is Germany's principal arbitral institution. The DIS published substantially revised DIS Arbitration Rules in 2018, introducing case management conferences, a procedure for early dismissal of manifestly unmeritorious claims and defenses, multi-party and multi-contract arbitration provisions, and emergency arbitrator procedures.

**DIS Caseload:** The DIS administers approximately 150-200 new arbitration cases per year, with a significant proportion involving international parties. Common sectors include engineering, construction, M&A (post-acquisition disputes), licensing, and joint ventures.

### Judicial Review

**ZPO Section 1059 -- Setting Aside:** Arbitral awards may be set aside by the Oberlandesgericht (Higher Regional Court) at the seat of the arbitration on the grounds specified in Article 34 of the Model Law, including invalidity of the arbitration agreement, denial of the opportunity to present the case, the award dealing with matters beyond the scope of the submission, irregular composition of the tribunal, and the award being contrary to public policy (ordre public). German courts have interpreted the public policy ground narrowly, consistent with international standards.

### Recognition and Enforcement

Germany ratified the New York Convention in 1961 without reservations. ZPO Section 1061 provides that foreign arbitral awards are recognized and enforced in accordance with the Convention. German courts have an excellent track record of enforcing foreign arbitral awards, with refusal based on public policy grounds being exceedingly rare.

### Arbitrability

German law takes a broad approach to arbitrability (Schiedsfähigkeit). Under ZPO Section 1030, any claim involving an economic interest (vermögensrechtlicher Anspruch) is arbitrable. Non-economic claims are arbitrable if the parties are entitled to settle the dispute. Certain matters remain non-arbitrable under specific legislation, including tenancy disputes under the Mietrecht provisions and employment disputes subject to the Arbeitsgerichtsgesetz.

## ECHR & EU

### New York Convention (1958)

The Convention on the Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards, adopted on June 10, 1958, is the cornerstone of the international commercial arbitration system. With 172 contracting states as of 2026, the Convention provides a near-universal framework for the enforcement of arbitration agreements (Article II) and arbitral awards (Articles III-V).

**Article V Grounds for Refusal:** The Convention provides an exhaustive list of grounds on which recognition and enforcement may be refused: incapacity of a party or invalidity of the arbitration agreement; lack of proper notice or inability to present the case; the award deals with matters beyond the submission; irregular composition of the tribunal or procedure; the award has been set aside at the seat; and the subject matter is not arbitrable or enforcement would be contrary to public policy of the enforcement state.

### UNCITRAL Model Law

The UNCITRAL Model Law on International Commercial Arbitration, adopted in 1985 and revised in 2006, has been adopted by over 85 states and serves as the template for modern national arbitration legislation. The 2006 revisions addressed interim measures (Article 17), providing a comprehensive framework for tribunal-ordered interim measures and their enforcement, and liberalized the form requirements for arbitration agreements (Option I of Article 7).

### ICC International Court of Arbitration

The International Chamber of Commerce (ICC) Court of Arbitration, established in 1923, is the world's leading international arbitral institution. The ICC Rules of Arbitration, most recently revised effective January 1, 2021, include provisions for expedited procedures (Article 30 and Annex VI), emergency arbitrator proceedings (Article 29 and Annex V), multi-party and multi-contract arbitrations (Articles 7-10), and terms of reference (Article 23). The ICC registered 898 new cases in 2023, involving parties from 138 countries.

### EU Law and Arbitration

The relationship between EU law and international commercial arbitration has generated significant jurisprudence. The CJEU's controversial decision in Achmea BV v. Slovak Republic (Case C-284/16, 2018) held that arbitration clauses in intra-EU bilateral investment treaties are incompatible with EU law because they remove disputes involving EU law from the EU judicial system. While Achmea concerned investor-state arbitration rather than commercial arbitration, its implications have been debated extensively.

**Brussels I Regulation (Recast) -- Regulation (EU) 1215/2012:** Article 1(2)(d) excludes arbitration from the Regulation's scope, preserving the separate regime established by the New York Convention and national arbitration laws. However, the interface between arbitration and EU jurisdictional rules remains complex, particularly regarding anti-suit injunctions and the effect of arbitration agreements on parallel court proceedings.

### Investor-State Arbitration and ECHR

The ECHR has addressed arbitration primarily in the context of investor-state disputes. In Stran Greek Refineries and Stratis Andreadis v. Greece (Application No. 13427/87, 1994), the Court held that an arbitral award constitutes a "possession" within the meaning of Article 1 of Protocol No. 1, and that annulment of the award by legislative intervention violated the applicant's property rights. This decision established that states cannot use legislative means to retroactively annul arbitral awards rendered against them.

## Comparative Analysis

### Model Law Adoption

A fundamental structural distinction is whether the jurisdiction has adopted the UNCITRAL Model Law. South Korea and Germany are Model Law jurisdictions, ensuring a high degree of consistency with international standards regarding arbitration agreement formation, tribunal constitution, interim measures, and award setting-aside procedures. The United States has not adopted the Model Law, instead relying on the independently developed Federal Arbitration Act, which predates the Model Law by over 60 years. While the FAA achieves similar pro-arbitration outcomes, its different statutory structure creates interpretive challenges, particularly regarding the scope of judicial review.

### Institutional Development

All four systems support robust institutional arbitration frameworks. The ICC, as the preeminent global institution, provides a neutral forum for disputes involving parties from any jurisdiction. The regional institutions -- KCAB International, ICDR, and DIS -- serve their domestic and regional markets while increasingly competing for international cases. South Korea's strategic investment in institutional infrastructure, including the establishment of SIDRC, reflects a deliberate policy to develop Seoul as an arbitration hub competing with Singapore and Hong Kong.

### Judicial Review Standards

The permissible scope of judicial review of arbitral awards represents one of the most significant practical differences across jurisdictions. Germany and South Korea, as Model Law jurisdictions, apply the Article 34 grounds for setting aside, which are identical to the New York Convention refusal grounds. The United States applies the narrower FAA Section 10 grounds, which do not include some Model Law grounds (such as invalidity of the arbitration agreement) but may include the contested "manifest disregard" standard. All three jurisdictions interpret the public policy ground narrowly, but the precise boundaries of public policy differ.

### Confidentiality

Confidentiality treatment varies significantly. The KCAB International Rules provide default confidentiality obligations. The DIS Rules similarly impose confidentiality. U.S. law provides no default statutory confidentiality for arbitration proceedings, and the ICDR Rules impose limited confidentiality obligations. At the international level, ICC proceedings are confidential but awards may be published in redacted form, while UNCITRAL Rules do not impose confidentiality obligations. This divergence has practical significance for parties seeking to maintain the privacy of their disputes.
