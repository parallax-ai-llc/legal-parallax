## Summary

Deepfakes (딥페이크) -- synthetic media generated through artificial intelligence techniques, particularly deep learning algorithms, that create realistic but fabricated images, audio, or video depicting real individuals -- have emerged as one of the most urgent regulatory challenges at the intersection of technology, privacy, and freedom of expression. South Korea has led global enforcement, enacting aggressive legislation through amendments to the Act on Special Cases Concerning the Punishment of Sexual Crimes (성폭력범죄의 처벌 등에 관한 특례법), which criminalizes the production, distribution, and possession of sexually explicit deepfakes with penalties of up to 5 years imprisonment; the 2024 national crackdown on deepfake sexual exploitation, particularly targeting Telegram-based distribution networks exploiting minors and young women, resulted in over 800 arrests and prompted further legislative strengthening. The United States addresses deepfakes through a growing patchwork of state laws -- with over 40 states having enacted some form of deepfake legislation by 2025, including Texas (first state to criminalize election-related deepfakes in 2019), California (AB 602 and AB 730 addressing pornographic and electoral deepfakes), and Virginia -- supplemented by the federal DEEPFAKES Accountability Act proposals and the bipartisan DEFIANCE Act of 2024 (signed into law) creating a federal civil cause of action for non-consensual intimate deepfake imagery. Germany regulates deepfakes through existing criminal law provisions including Sections 201a (violation of intimate privacy by images), 186-187 (defamation), and 238 StGB (stalking), supplemented by the GDPR's biometric data protections and the Kunsturhebergesetz (KUG, Art Copyright Act) right to one's own image (Recht am eigenen Bild). At the EU level, the AI Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689) imposes transparency obligations requiring disclosure when content has been artificially generated, while EU Directive 2024/1385 on combating violence against women explicitly criminalizes non-consensual intimate image manipulation, and the Digital Services Act requires platforms to address the dissemination of harmful deepfake content.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea has emerged as a global leader in deepfake enforcement, driven by severe deepfake sexual exploitation crises that have galvanized public demand and legislative action.

**Act on Special Cases Concerning the Punishment of Sexual Crimes (성폭력범죄의 처벌 등에 관한 특례법):** Article 14-2, added in June 2020, specifically criminalizes the production, distribution, and possession of sexually explicit deepfakes. The provision targets any person who produces, edits, or distributes fabricated sexual imagery (허위영상물) depicting a real person's face, body, or voice without consent.

### Penalties

| Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|
| Producing sexually explicit deepfakes | Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine up to 50 million KRW |
| Distributing sexually explicit deepfakes | Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine up to 50 million KRW |
| Possessing sexually explicit deepfakes with intent to distribute | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine up to 30 million KRW |
| Offenses involving minors | Up to 7 years imprisonment (enhanced penalty) |
| Commercial distribution | Enhanced penalties; proceeds subject to forfeiture |

### 2024 National Crackdown

In August-September 2024, South Korea launched an unprecedented national enforcement campaign against deepfake sexual exploitation following revelations that Telegram-based chat rooms were being used to create and distribute sexually explicit deepfake images of schoolgirls, university students, and military service members. The crisis, sometimes compared to the 2019-2020 "Nth Room" digital sex crime case, triggered:

**Enforcement Scale:** Over 800 individuals were arrested within four months. The National Police Agency (경찰청) established dedicated deepfake investigation teams in all 18 metropolitan and provincial police agencies.

**Legislative Response:** The National Assembly (국회) fast-tracked amendments to strengthen penalties for deepfake crimes, expand the definition of sexual exploitation to explicitly include AI-generated content, and impose mandatory reporting obligations on platform operators and internet service providers.

**Platform Accountability:** Korean authorities pressured Telegram to cooperate with investigations and threatened to restrict the platform's operations in South Korea. The Korea Communications Standards Commission (방송통신심의위원회) ordered the blocking of deepfake distribution channels.

### Nth Room Case Legacy

The 2019-2020 "Nth Room" case (n번방 사건), in which Telegram chat rooms were used to distribute non-consensual sexual imagery including manipulated content involving minors, was the catalyst for South Korea's initial deepfake legislation. The principal perpetrator, Cho Ju-bin (조주빈), received a 42-year sentence. The case led to the Act on Prevention of Illegal Distribution of Filming Using Cameras (카메라등을이용한촬영물 불법유통 방지법) and accelerated amendments to the sexual crimes punishment act.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has adopted a rapidly expanding but fragmented approach to deepfake regulation through both federal and state legislation.

**DEFIANCE Act of 2024 (Disrupt Explicit Forged Images and Non-Consensual Edits):** Signed into law in 2024, the DEFIANCE Act creates a federal civil cause of action allowing victims of non-consensual intimate deepfake imagery to sue for damages. Victims may recover actual damages, statutory damages, and attorney's fees. The Act specifically targets digitally altered or AI-generated intimate images created without consent.

**State Legislation:** By 2025, over 40 states had enacted some form of deepfake legislation:

| State | Year | Key Provision |
|-------|------|---------------|
| Texas | 2019 | SB 751: First state to criminalize election-related deepfakes (within 30 days of election) |
| California | 2019 | AB 602: Civil cause of action for sexually explicit deepfakes; AB 730: Election deepfake restrictions |
| Virginia | 2019 | First state to include deepfakes in non-consensual pornography criminal statute |
| New York | 2023 | Comprehensive deepfake bill covering both sexual and election-related content |
| Minnesota | 2023 | Criminalized both sexual and election deepfakes with specific AI provisions |
| Tennessee | 2024 | ELVIS Act: Protects against AI-generated voice and likeness appropriation |
| Florida | 2024 | Criminalized sexual deepfakes targeting minors with enhanced penalties |

### Federal Proposals and Actions

Beyond the DEFIANCE Act, federal activity includes:

- **DEEPFAKES Accountability Act** (proposed): Would require disclosure labels on AI-generated content and impose penalties for failure to disclose.
- **AI Labeling Act** (proposed): Would mandate watermarking of AI-generated content.
- **FCC Ruling (2024):** Declared AI-generated voices in robocalls to be "artificial" under the Telephone Consumer Protection Act (TCPA), enabling enforcement against deepfake voice calls.
- **FTC Action:** The FTC has pursued enforcement against deceptive commercial use of deepfakes under Section 5 of the FTC Act.

### First Amendment Considerations

Deepfake regulation in the United States must navigate First Amendment constraints. Content-based restrictions on expression are subject to strict scrutiny and must serve a compelling government interest through narrowly tailored means. Courts have generally recognized compelling interests in preventing non-consensual intimate imagery and election fraud, but broader deepfake bans may face constitutional challenges.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany regulates deepfakes through a combination of existing criminal law, civil law, and data protection provisions, supplemented by EU-level regulation.

**Section 201a StGB (Violation of Intimate Privacy by Images):** This provision criminalizes the production and distribution of images that violate the intimate sphere of another person. Courts have applied it to deepfake-generated intimate images, interpreting "images" to include AI-generated fabrications depicting real individuals.

**Sections 186-187 StGB (Defamation):** Deepfakes that make false factual claims about individuals may be prosecuted as defamation (üble Nachrede, Section 186) or intentional defamation (Verleumdung, Section 187), with the latter carrying penalties of up to 5 years imprisonment.

**Section 184b StGB (Distribution of Child Sexual Abuse Material):** Deepfake-generated child sexual abuse material falls within Section 184b, which criminalizes the production, distribution, and possession of such material regardless of whether it depicts real or fictional minors. Penalties range up to 10 years imprisonment for distribution.

### Recht am eigenen Bild (Right to One's Own Image)

The Kunsturhebergesetz (KUG, Art Copyright Act) Sections 22-23 establish a right to one's own image (Recht am eigenen Bild), requiring consent for the distribution and public display of a person's image. This right has been applied to deepfakes by German courts, with the Hamburg Regional Court (Landgericht Hamburg) ruling in 2023 that AI-generated images depicting identifiable real persons are subject to the same consent requirements as authentic photographs.

### Gewerblicher Rechtsschutz (Intellectual Property)

The general personality right (allgemeines Persönlichkeitsrecht, derived from Articles 1 and 2 of the Grundgesetz) provides additional civil law protection against deepfakes that appropriate a person's identity or likeness, enabling injunctive relief and damages claims through civil courts.

### AI Act Implementation

With the AI Act's entry into force, Germany is implementing Article 50's transparency obligations, requiring that persons deploying AI systems generating synthetic audio, image, video, or text content ensure that the outputs are marked in a machine-readable format and recognizable as artificially generated or manipulated.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Artificial Intelligence Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689)

The AI Act addresses deepfakes primarily through transparency requirements:

**Article 50 -- Transparency Obligations for Certain AI Systems:** Providers and deployers of AI systems that generate synthetic audio, image, video, or text content must ensure that the outputs are marked in a machine-readable format and disclosed as artificially generated or manipulated. This applies to all deepfake generation systems regardless of whether the content is harmful. Exemptions exist for artistic, satirical, or manifestly fictional content.

**Article 50(4):** Specifically addresses deep fakes, requiring that deployers of AI systems generating or manipulating images, audio, or video content constituting a "deep fake" disclose that the content has been artificially generated or manipulated. Exception is made for law enforcement purposes where disclosure would prejudice ongoing investigations.

### EU Directive 2024/1385 (Violence Against Women)

Directive 2024/1385, adopted May 14, 2024, explicitly criminalizes the non-consensual creation and sharing of intimate images, including AI-generated or manipulated content. Member states must ensure that producing, manipulating, and distributing intimate images without consent is a criminal offense punishable by imprisonment of at least one year. The Directive specifically addresses technology-facilitated abuse including deepfake pornography.

### Digital Services Act

The DSA (Regulation 2022/2065) requires online platforms to address the dissemination of harmful deepfake content through notice-and-action mechanisms, transparent content moderation policies, and systemic risk assessments by very large online platforms (VLOPs) that must specifically address risks related to disinformation and non-consensual intimate imagery.

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR's jurisprudence on image rights and privacy provides a framework for deepfake-related claims:

**Von Hannover v. Germany (No. 2) (Applications Nos. 40660/08 and 60641/08, Grand Chamber Judgment of February 7, 2012):** The Court established that states must balance privacy rights (Article 8) against freedom of expression (Article 10) in cases involving the publication of images, considering factors including contribution to public debate, the role of the person depicted, and the circumstances of the image's creation. This balancing framework applies to deepfakes.

**Bărbulescu v. Romania (Application No. 61496/08, Grand Chamber Judgment of September 5, 2017):** The Court confirmed that digital communications and digital identity are protected under Article 8, providing a foundation for arguing that deepfake appropriation of digital identity engages Convention protections.

### Council of Europe Framework Convention on AI

The Council of Europe Framework Convention on Artificial Intelligence and Human Rights, Democracy and the Rule of Law, adopted on May 17, 2024, establishes principles applicable to deepfakes, including requirements for transparency, accountability, and respect for human dignity in AI deployment.

## Comparative Analysis

### Legislative Responses to Harm Categories

Jurisdictions have prioritized different harm categories. South Korea has focused overwhelmingly on sexual exploitation deepfakes, driven by severe domestic crises. The United States has addressed both sexual and electoral deepfakes, reflecting its dual concerns with privacy and democratic integrity. Germany relies on existing general-purpose criminal provisions supplemented by personality rights. The EU's approach is the most comprehensive, addressing sexual exploitation (Directive 2024/1385), transparency (AI Act), and platform responsibility (DSA) simultaneously.

### Enforcement Intensity

South Korea's enforcement response stands out for its scale and urgency -- over 800 arrests in a single enforcement campaign in 2024 is unprecedented globally. The United States relies primarily on civil litigation (BIPA-style claims and the DEFIANCE Act) rather than mass criminal enforcement. German enforcement operates through data protection authorities and traditional criminal prosecution. The EU's enforcement is institutional, through the DSA and AI Act's supervisory mechanisms.

### Definitional Challenges

All jurisdictions struggle with defining deepfakes in technologically precise yet future-proof terms. South Korea's approach of targeting "fabricated imagery" (허위영상물) is broad but may not capture all synthetic media forms. The US approach varies by state. The AI Act's reference to "synthetic" content generated by AI systems is technology-neutral but requires ongoing interpretation as AI capabilities evolve.

### Platform Accountability

The approach to platform liability diverges significantly. South Korea has imposed direct pressure on platforms (particularly Telegram) to cooperate with investigations. The EU's DSA creates structured notice-and-action obligations. The United States' Section 230 continues to provide broad platform immunity, though erosion is underway through specific deepfake legislation.
