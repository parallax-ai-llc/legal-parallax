## Summary

Fraud -- the deliberate deception of another person for financial or personal gain -- is criminalized across every major legal system, yet its statutory definitions, evidentiary requirements, and penalties differ substantially. South Korea's Criminal Act treats fraud (사기죄) as a unified offense under Article 347, requiring proof of deception, victim disposal of property, and the offender's pecuniary gain, with penalties of up to 10 years imprisonment. The United States prosecutes fraud primarily through its expansive federal mail and wire fraud statutes (18 U.S.C. sections 1341 and 1343), which carry up to 20 years imprisonment -- or 30 years when a financial institution is affected -- and have become the federal prosecutor's most versatile weapons against white-collar crime. Germany's Betrug under section 263 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB) requires a strict four-element causal chain -- deception, error, property disposition, and immediate financial loss -- and carries up to 5 years imprisonment in ordinary cases or up to 10 years for commercial or gang-related fraud. At the supranational level, the European Union's PIF Directive (Directive 2017/1371) harmonizes Member State criminal laws to protect the EU's financial interests, supported by the investigative work of OLAF (European Anti-Fraud Office) and the prosecutorial authority of the European Public Prosecutor's Office (EPPO).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Under Article 347 of the Korean Criminal Act (형법 제347조), a person who defrauds another, thereby obtaining property or pecuniary advantage, shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than 10 years or by a fine not exceeding 20 million won. Article 347-2 separately addresses computer fraud, covering cases where a person acquires property benefits by inputting false information or improper commands into a data processing system.

### Elements of the Offense

To establish fraud (사기죄) under Korean law, four elements must be proved:

1. **Deception (기망행위):** The accused must have misled another person by falsely representing facts. Deception can take the form of affirmative false statements, misleading conduct, or, in circumstances where a legal duty to disclose exists, deliberate omission.
2. **Disposal of Property (재산처분행위):** The victim must have disposed of property or other legal rights as a direct result of the deception.
3. **Obtaining Gain (재산취득):** The accused must have obtained property or pecuniary advantage as a result of the victim's disposal.
4. **Intent (고의):** The accused must have acted with the intention to commit fraud, including awareness that the representations were false.

### Aggravated Forms

The Specific Economic Crimes Aggravated Punishment Act (특정경제범죄 가중처벌 등에 관한 법률) imposes enhanced penalties for large-scale fraud:

| Fraud Amount | Penalty |
|-------------|---------|
| 500 million won or more | 3 years to life imprisonment |
| 5 billion won or more | 5 years to life imprisonment, or life imprisonment |

### Landmark Cases

**Daewoo Group Accounting Fraud (1999-2006):** Daewoo founder Kim Woo-choong was convicted of securing illegal bank loans, fabricating accounts to falsify approximately $43 billion in assets, and embezzling funds from the conglomerate. He was sentenced to 10 years imprisonment and ordered to forfeit $22 billion, making it the largest financial fraud prosecution in Korean history at the time.

**Stock Manipulation Scheme (2023-2025):** A Seoul court convicted 25 individuals in South Korea's largest stock manipulation case, involving a scheme worth approximately 730 billion won ($500 million) using contracts for difference (CFDs) to artificially inflate stock prices. Ra Deok-yeon, the scheme's architect, was sentenced to 25 years imprisonment for market manipulation and tax fraud.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States prosecutes fraud through an extensive web of federal and state statutes. The two most powerful and frequently invoked are the federal mail fraud and wire fraud statutes:

- **Mail Fraud (18 U.S.C. section 1341):** Prohibits the use of the United States Postal Service or any interstate mail carrier in furtherance of a scheme to defraud. Enacted in 1872, it is one of the oldest federal criminal statutes.
- **Wire Fraud (18 U.S.C. section 1343):** Prohibits the use of interstate wire communications -- including telephone, email, internet, television, and radio -- in furtherance of a scheme to defraud. Enacted in 1952 to parallel the mail fraud statute.

Additional federal fraud statutes include securities fraud (15 U.S.C. section 78j(b) and SEC Rule 10b-5), bank fraud (18 U.S.C. section 1344), healthcare fraud (18 U.S.C. section 1347), and honest services fraud (18 U.S.C. section 1346).

### Elements of the Offense

For both mail and wire fraud, the prosecution must prove beyond a reasonable doubt:

1. The existence of a **scheme or artifice to defraud**, or to obtain money or property by means of false or fraudulent pretenses, representations, or promises.
2. The defendant's **knowing and willful participation** in the scheme with intent to defraud.
3. The **use of the mails or interstate wire communications** in furtherance of the scheme (the "jurisdictional hook").

Importantly, the victim need not actually be defrauded or suffer a loss -- the scheme itself, coupled with use of the mails or wires, completes the offense.

### Penalties

| Offense | Maximum Imprisonment | Maximum Fine |
|---------|---------------------|-------------|
| Mail fraud (section 1341) | 20 years | $250,000 |
| Wire fraud (section 1343) | 20 years | $250,000 |
| Fraud affecting a financial institution | 30 years | $1,000,000 |
| Securities fraud (section 1348) | 25 years | $250,000 |
| Bank fraud (section 1344) | 30 years | $1,000,000 |

### Landmark Cases

**Enron Corporation (2001-2006):** The Enron scandal remains one of the most consequential corporate fraud cases in American history. Enron executives, led by CEO Kenneth Lay and President Jeffrey Skilling, engaged in a wide-ranging scheme from 1999 to 2001 to deceive investors, the SEC, and the public about the company's true financial performance through the use of off-balance-sheet special purpose entities and mark-to-market accounting manipulation. Lay was convicted on all six counts (conspiracy, wire fraud, and securities fraud) but died before sentencing. Skilling was convicted on 19 of 28 counts and sentenced to 24 years and 4 months in prison (later reduced to 14 years). Twenty-two individuals were ultimately convicted. The scandal also led to the dissolution of Arthur Andersen, then one of the world's five largest accounting firms, and directly prompted passage of the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002.

**Bernie Madoff Ponzi Scheme (2008-2009):** Bernard Madoff operated the largest Ponzi scheme in history, defrauding investors of an estimated $65 billion in stated account values (with actual losses of approximately $17.5 billion in principal). Arrested on December 11, 2008, after confessing to his sons that his investment advisory business was "a giant Ponzi scheme," Madoff pleaded guilty to 11 federal charges including securities fraud, wire fraud, mail fraud, and money laundering. He was sentenced to 150 years in prison -- the statutory maximum -- with restitution of $170 billion. Judge Denny Chin called the crimes "extraordinarily evil." Madoff died in federal prison in April 2021. As of 2025, the Department of Justice had distributed over $4.3 billion to more than 40,000 victims.

**McNally v. United States (1987) and Skilling v. United States (2010):** These Supreme Court decisions narrowed the scope of federal fraud statutes. In McNally, the Court held that mail and wire fraud statutes did not reach schemes to defraud citizens of their intangible right to honest government services. Congress responded by enacting section 1346 to restore honest services fraud. In Skilling, arising from the Enron prosecution, the Court construed section 1346 narrowly to cover only bribery and kickback schemes, invalidating the broader "undisclosed self-dealing" theory.

**Ciminelli v. United States (2023):** The Supreme Court unanimously rejected the "right to control" theory of fraud, holding that the right to valuable economic information needed for discretionary economic decisions is not a traditional property interest sufficient to support a mail or wire fraud conviction.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Fraud (Betrug) under section 263 of the German Criminal Code (Strafgesetzbuch, StGB) is the central property offense in German criminal law. It covers any deception-induced, self-damaging action by the victim that results in an immediate financial loss (Vermogensschaden).

### Elements of the Offense

German fraud law requires a strict four-element causal chain, each element flowing directly from the preceding one:

1. **Deception (Tauschung):** The deliberate influence on another person's mental image with the aim of causing an error. This can be accomplished through explicit false statements (ausdruckliche Tauschung), implied conduct (konkludente Tauschung), or in certain duty-to-disclose situations, through omission (Tauschung durch Unterlassen).
2. **Error (Irrtum):** The victim must actually form a mistaken belief as a result of the deception. Unlike U.S. law, which requires no actual deception of the victim, German law demands that the victim's cognition be affected.
3. **Property Disposition (Vermogensverfugung):** The victim, acting under the influence of the error, must undertake a disposition of property -- whether an act, tolerance, or omission -- that directly causes financial loss.
4. **Financial Loss (Vermogensschaden):** The victim must suffer an immediate, measurable financial loss that corresponds to the disposition.

On the subjective side, the offender must act with intent (Vorsatz) regarding all objective elements, plus an intent to obtain an unlawful enrichment (Bereicherungsabsicht) that directly corresponds to the victim's loss.

### Penalties

| Category | Provision | Penalty |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| Basic fraud | section 263(1) StGB | Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine |
| Attempted fraud | section 263(2) StGB | Punishable (reduced penalty) |
| Particularly serious cases | section 263(3) StGB | 6 months to 10 years imprisonment |
| Commercial fraud (gewerbsmassig) | section 263(3) No. 1 StGB | 6 months to 10 years imprisonment |
| Gang-related fraud (bandenmassig) | section 263(5) StGB | 1 to 10 years imprisonment |
| Computer fraud | section 263a StGB | Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine |

Particularly serious cases under section 263(3) include fraud committed on a commercial basis (gewerbsmassig), as a member of a gang, causing large-scale financial loss, or exploiting a position of trust.

### Landmark Cases

**Wirecard Scandal (2020-present):** The collapse of Wirecard AG in June 2020 exposed the largest accounting fraud in Germany's post-war history. The Munich-based payment processor disclosed that 1.9 billion euros listed on its balance sheet did not exist. CEO Markus Braun was arrested and charged with fraud, breach of trust, and market manipulation. Prosecutors allege that Wirecard executives inflated the company's earnings starting at least in 2015 by inventing revenue streams from fictitious third-party acquirer (TPA) businesses in Dubai, the Philippines, and Singapore. The trial, which opened in Munich in December 2022, is one of the most complex white-collar criminal proceedings in German history. In September 2024, the Munich Regional Court ordered former Wirecard board members to pay 140 million euros in damages. COO Jan Marsalek remains a fugitive.

**Herstatt Bank Collapse (1974):** The failure of Bankhaus Herstatt due to unauthorized foreign exchange speculation led to criminal fraud prosecutions and fundamentally reshaped international banking regulation, contributing to the creation of the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision.

## European Framework

### The PIF Directive

Directive (EU) 2017/1371 on the fight against fraud to the Union's financial interests by means of criminal law (the "PIF Directive"), adopted on 5 July 2017, establishes the EU's primary framework for harmonizing Member State criminal laws related to fraud affecting the EU budget. It replaced the 1995 Convention on the Protection of the European Communities' Financial Interests (the "PIF Convention") and its three protocols.

### Scope and Offenses

The PIF Directive defines four categories of criminal offenses ("PIF offences"):

1. **Fraud** -- including cross-border VAT fraud involving total damage of at least 10 million euros
2. **Corruption** -- both active and passive
3. **Money laundering** -- of proceeds from PIF offences
4. **Misappropriation** -- of EU funds by public officials

### Penalties

The Directive requires Member States to impose effective, proportionate, and dissuasive criminal penalties, including:

- A maximum term of imprisonment of at least 4 years when the criminal conduct involves considerable damage or advantage (generally defined as exceeding 100,000 euros)
- Criminal liability for legal persons (companies), with sanctions including criminal or non-criminal fines and other measures such as exclusion from public benefits or judicial winding-up

### Institutional Framework

**OLAF (European Anti-Fraud Office):** Established in 1999, OLAF conducts administrative investigations into fraud, corruption, and other offenses affecting the EU's financial interests. OLAF investigates both external fraud (misuse of EU funds by recipients) and internal matters (misconduct by EU staff). OLAF has no judicial powers but issues recommendations to national authorities for financial recovery, administrative action, and criminal prosecution. The estimated financial damage to the EU budget from fraud exceeds 500 million euros annually.

**EPPO (European Public Prosecutor's Office):** Operational since June 2021, the EPPO is an independent EU body with authority to investigate and prosecute crimes against the EU's financial interests. Unlike OLAF, the EPPO has full prosecutorial powers and can direct national law enforcement authorities to carry out searches, seizures, and arrests. As of 2025, 22 EU Member States participate in the EPPO. Notable non-participants include Hungary, Poland, Sweden, Denmark, and Ireland.

**Cooperation between OLAF and EPPO:** OLAF reports any criminal conduct within the EPPO's competence to the European prosecutors. The EPPO may request OLAF's support during criminal investigations, and OLAF may conduct complementary administrative investigations alongside EPPO's criminal proceedings, ensuring both financial recovery and criminal accountability.

## Comparative Analysis

### Structural Comparison

| Feature | South Korea | United States | Germany | EU Framework |
|---------|------------|---------------|---------|-------------|
| **Primary Statute** | Criminal Act Art. 347 | 18 U.S.C. sections 1341, 1343 | section 263 StGB | Directive 2017/1371 |
| **Legal Tradition** | Civil law (German-influenced) | Common law | Civil law | Supranational harmonization |
| **Basic Penalty** | Up to 10 years / 20M won fine | Up to 20 years / $250K fine | Up to 5 years or fine | Minimum 4 years maximum for serious cases |
| **Aggravated Penalty** | Up to life (over 5B won) | Up to 30 years / $1M fine | 6 months to 10 years | Member State discretion |
| **Victim Deception Required** | Yes (victim must be deceived) | No (scheme to defraud suffices) | Yes (victim must form error) | Varies by Member State |
| **Actual Loss Required** | Yes (property must be obtained) | No (scheme + use of mails/wires) | Yes (immediate financial loss) | Yes (damage to EU interests) |
| **Attempt Punishable** | Yes | Yes (as part of scheme) | Yes (section 263(2)) | Member State discretion |
| **Corporate Liability** | Yes (dual liability) | Yes (respondeat superior) | Limited (administrative fines) | Required for legal persons |
| **Computer Fraud** | Art. 347-2 (separate offense) | CFAA (18 U.S.C. section 1030) | section 263a StGB | Covered under general fraud |

### Key Differences

**Breadth of the Offense:** The U.S. wire and mail fraud statutes are notably the broadest among the compared jurisdictions. They require neither actual victim deception nor actual financial loss -- the existence of a scheme to defraud plus use of interstate communications suffices. This gives federal prosecutors extraordinary flexibility. By contrast, South Korea and Germany both require proof that the victim was actually deceived and suffered a concrete loss.

**Penalty Severity:** The United States imposes by far the harshest penalties, with base maximums of 20 years and enhanced maximums of 30 years. South Korea's aggravated penalties for large-scale economic fraud (life imprisonment for fraud exceeding 5 billion won) can match or exceed U.S. penalties in absolute terms. Germany's penalties are comparatively moderate, reflecting its broader philosophy of proportionality and rehabilitation.

**Jurisdictional Structure:** The U.S. system is uniquely fragmented, with fraud prosecuted at both federal and state levels under different statutes. South Korea and Germany each maintain unified national criminal codes. The EU framework operates as a harmonization layer above Member State law rather than as a direct criminal jurisdiction (with the exception of the EPPO's prosecutorial authority).

**Causal Chain Requirements:** Germany imposes the strictest causal requirements, demanding a four-link chain from deception through error, disposition, to financial loss. Each link must directly cause the next. South Korea similarly requires a causal connection between deception and property disposal. The U.S. approach is less demanding, focusing on the existence of a fraudulent scheme and the use of instrumentalities of interstate commerce.
