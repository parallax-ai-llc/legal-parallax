## Summary

Corporate governance -- the system of rules, practices, and processes by which companies are directed, controlled, and held accountable to shareholders and other stakeholders -- has undergone dramatic transformation worldwide in response to financial crises, corporate scandals, and evolving expectations of corporate responsibility. South Korea's corporate governance framework is shaped by the Commercial Code (상법), the Financial Investment Services and Capital Markets Act (자본시장과 금융투자업에 관한 법률), and the Corporate Governance Code issued by the Korea Corporate Governance Service (한국기업지배구조원), with the distinctive challenge of chaebol (재벌) conglomerate structures that concentrate control through circular shareholding and family management, prompting ongoing reforms including mandatory appointment of independent directors (사외이사), audit committees for large corporations, and the 2020 "stewardship code" under the Korea Stewardship Code guidelines. The United States regulates corporate governance through state corporate law (principally Delaware General Corporation Law), federal securities regulation (Securities Exchange Act of 1934, Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002, Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform Act of 2010), stock exchange listing standards (NYSE, NASDAQ), and the influential Delaware Court of Chancery jurisprudence on fiduciary duties, business judgment rule, and shareholder activism. Germany employs a mandatory two-tier board system (Vorstand and Aufsichtsrat) under the Aktiengesetz (AktG), incorporating employee codetermination (Mitbestimmung) through the Codetermination Act (Mitbestimmungsgesetz, MitbestG) of 1976 and the German Corporate Governance Code (Deutscher Corporate Governance Kodex, DCGK). At the EU level, the Shareholder Rights Directive II (2017/828) mandates say-on-pay votes and related party transaction oversight, the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD, 2022/2464) requires comprehensive ESG disclosure, and the Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD, 2024) imposes supply chain human rights and environmental due diligence obligations.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's corporate governance system has been shaped by the unique challenges of the chaebol conglomerate structure and a series of reforms triggered by the 1997 Asian financial crisis.

### Commercial Code (상법)

The Commercial Code provides the basic framework for corporate organization, director duties, and shareholder rights. Key governance provisions include:

**Board of Directors:** All stock corporations (주식회사) must establish a board of directors (이사회). Listed companies with total assets exceeding 2 trillion KRW must have a board with a majority of independent directors (사외이사) and must establish an audit committee, a nomination committee for independent directors, and other board committees.

**Director Duties:** Directors owe duties of loyalty (충실의무, Article 382-3) and care (선관주의의무, Article 382(2)). The Supreme Court has held that these duties require directors to act in the best interest of the corporation, considering the interests of shareholders, employees, creditors, and the broader community.

### Chaebol Governance Reforms

The chaebol system -- characterized by cross-shareholding, family control, and complex intra-group transactions -- has been the primary target of governance reform:

**Prohibition of Circular Shareholding:** The Monopoly Regulation and Fair Trade Act (독점규제 및 공정거래에 관한 법률) was amended in 2014 to prohibit new circular shareholding among affiliates of large business groups (공시대상 기업집단).

**Enhanced Disclosure:** Chaebol affiliates must file detailed disclosures regarding intra-group transactions, ownership structures, and the business activities of controlling family members with the Korea Fair Trade Commission (공정거래위원회).

**Stewardship Code:** The Korea Stewardship Code, adopted in 2016 and modeled on the UK Stewardship Code, encourages institutional investors to actively engage with portfolio companies on governance matters. The National Pension Service (국민연금) adopted the Code in 2018, significantly increasing institutional shareholder activism.

### Recent Reforms

The "Corporate Value-Up Program" (기업 가치 제고 계획), announced in 2024, encourages listed companies to adopt voluntary plans to improve corporate valuation through better governance, enhanced shareholder returns, and improved disclosure. The program was modeled partly on Japan's similar initiative and responds to the persistent "Korea discount" in stock market valuations attributed to governance concerns.

## United States

### Legal Framework

American corporate governance operates through a complex interaction of state law, federal regulation, stock exchange rules, and market practices.

### Delaware Corporate Law

Approximately 68% of Fortune 500 companies and over 50% of all publicly traded companies are incorporated in Delaware. The Delaware General Corporation Law (DGCL) and the jurisprudence of the Delaware Court of Chancery provide the dominant governance framework.

**Business Judgment Rule:** The Court of Chancery applies a presumption that directors' business decisions are made on an informed basis, in good faith, and in the honest belief that the action was in the best interest of the company (Aronson v. Lewis, 473 A.2d 805 (Del. 1984)). This presumption is rebutted only if the plaintiff demonstrates that directors acted with a conflict of interest, in bad faith, or without adequate information.

**Entire Fairness:** When the business judgment presumption is rebutted (typically in conflict-of-interest transactions), the burden shifts to the defendants to prove that the transaction was entirely fair -- both in terms of fair dealing (process) and fair price.

### Federal Regulation

**Sarbanes-Oxley Act (2002):** Enacted in response to the Enron and WorldCom scandals, SOX imposed mandatory audit committee independence (Section 301), CEO/CFO certification of financial statements (Section 302), internal control requirements (Section 404), and whistleblower protections (Section 806).

**Dodd-Frank Act (2010):** Key governance provisions include mandatory say-on-pay advisory votes (Section 951), pay ratio disclosure (Section 953(b)), clawback provisions for executive compensation based on misstated financials (Section 954), and proxy access (Section 971).

### Shareholder Activism

The United States has the most developed shareholder activism ecosystem globally. Activist hedge funds (such as Elliott Management, Third Point, and Trian Partners) regularly seek board seats, strategic changes, and governance reforms. The SEC's universal proxy rule (effective September 2022) allows shareholders to use a single proxy card listing both management and dissident nominees, significantly facilitating contested elections.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's corporate governance system is distinguished by its mandatory two-tier board structure and employee codetermination.

### Two-Tier Board (Vorstand and Aufsichtsrat)

The Aktiengesetz (AktG) requires Aktiengesellschaften (AGs) to maintain two separate boards:

**Vorstand (Management Board):** Responsible for managing the company's day-to-day operations. Members are appointed by the Aufsichtsrat for terms of up to five years (AktG Section 84). The Vorstand acts independently; the Aufsichtsrat cannot give it instructions on management matters.

**Aufsichtsrat (Supervisory Board):** Responsible for appointing and dismissing Vorstand members, overseeing management, and approving certain major transactions. The Aufsichtsrat meets regularly to review management's performance and strategy.

### Codetermination (Mitbestimmung)

The Mitbestimmungsgesetz (MitbestG) of 1976 requires companies with more than 2,000 employees to have equal representation (parity codetermination) on the Aufsichtsrat: half of the members are elected by shareholders and half by employees. The Aufsichtsrat chair, elected by the shareholder representatives, has a casting vote in the event of a tie. Companies with 500-2,000 employees are subject to one-third codetermination under the Drittelbeteiligungsgesetz (DrittelbG).

### German Corporate Governance Code (DCGK)

The DCGK, most recently updated in 2022, operates on a "comply or explain" basis under AktG Section 161. Listed companies must annually declare which recommendations they follow and explain any deviations. Key recommendations include separation of Vorstand chair and Aufsichtsrat chair, audit committee composition and expertise, diversity targets, and executive compensation transparency.

### Supply Chain Due Diligence Act (LkSG)

The Lieferkettensorgfaltspflichtengesetz (LkSG), effective January 1, 2023, requires companies with more than 1,000 employees (lowered from 3,000 in 2024) to establish due diligence processes for identifying, preventing, and remedying human rights and environmental risks in their supply chains. The Act imposes administrative fines of up to 2% of average annual global turnover.

## ECHR & EU

### Shareholder Rights Directive II (2017/828)

Directive 2017/828 (SRD II), amending Directive 2007/36/EC, strengthens shareholder engagement and corporate governance transparency:

**Say-on-Pay (Article 9a-9b):** Shareholders have the right to vote on the remuneration policy (binding) and the remuneration report (advisory or binding, as determined by member states).

**Related Party Transactions (Article 9c):** Material related party transactions must be publicly announced and subject to shareholder or supervisory body approval. Member states define materiality thresholds.

**Shareholder Identification (Article 3a-3c):** Companies have the right to identify their shareholders, and intermediaries must facilitate communication between companies and shareholders.

### Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD)

Directive 2022/2464 (CSRD), replacing the Non-Financial Reporting Directive, requires large companies and all listed companies (except micro-enterprises) to report detailed sustainability information in accordance with the European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS). The CSRD applies in phases: large public-interest entities from 2024, large companies from 2025, and listed SMEs from 2026.

### Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD)

Directive 2024/1760 (CSDDD), adopted on June 13, 2024, requires large EU and non-EU companies to identify, prevent, mitigate, and account for adverse human rights and environmental impacts in their operations and value chains. Companies must adopt climate transition plans aligned with the Paris Agreement. The Directive provides for civil liability when companies fail to comply with due diligence obligations and damage results.

### EU Taxonomy Regulation

Regulation 2020/852 establishes a classification system for environmentally sustainable economic activities, directly affecting corporate governance by requiring listed companies to disclose the proportion of their turnover, capital expenditures, and operating expenditures associated with taxonomy-aligned activities.

## Comparative Analysis

### Board Structure

The most visible structural difference is between unitary boards (South Korea, United States) and the mandatory two-tier system (Germany). The unitary board model provides flexibility and direct oversight but may create conflicts between management and oversight roles. The two-tier model institutionalizes separation of management and oversight but may slow decision-making and create information asymmetries between boards.

### Stakeholder vs. Shareholder Orientation

Germany's codetermination system embodies a stakeholder governance model, giving employees formal representation in corporate decision-making. The United States maintains the most shareholder-centric model, with fiduciary duties primarily oriented toward shareholder wealth maximization. South Korea occupies an intermediate position, with the Supreme Court recognizing broader stakeholder interests while the chaebol structure often prioritizes controlling family interests. EU directives increasingly mandate stakeholder consideration through sustainability reporting and due diligence requirements.

### Controlling Shareholder Challenge

South Korea's chaebol governance challenges and the United States' shareholder activism ecosystem represent opposite ends of a spectrum. Korean reforms aim to constrain controlling family influence, while American reforms increasingly empower dispersed shareholders. Germany's codetermination system addresses a different dimension of the same fundamental question -- who should control the corporation.
