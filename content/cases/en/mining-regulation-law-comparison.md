## Summary

Mining regulation -- the legal framework governing the exploration, extraction, processing, and remediation of mineral resources -- occupies a complex position at the intersection of property law, environmental protection, occupational safety, and economic development, with jurisdictions balancing the economic imperative of mineral extraction against its often severe environmental and social consequences. South Korea regulates mining through the Mining Industry Act (광업법), which establishes the mining rights system (광업권) granting exclusive rights to explore and extract designated minerals, supplemented by environmental requirements under the Environmental Impact Assessment Act (환경영향평가법) and the Mine Reclamation Act (광산피해의 방지 및 복구에 관한 법률), with the country's historical mining legacy -- particularly abandoned metal mines from the Japanese colonial era -- creating ongoing environmental remediation challenges addressed through the Mine Reclamation Corporation (광해관리공단, now Korea Mine Reclamation Corporation). The United States regulates mining through a dual system: the General Mining Law of 1872 (30 U.S.C. Sections 22-47) governing hardrock mining on federal public lands under the claim-staking system with no federal royalty requirement, and the Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act of 1977 (SMCRA, 30 U.S.C. Sections 1201-1328) regulating coal mining with mandatory reclamation, supplemented by the Clean Water Act, NEPA, and the Comprehensive Environmental Response Act. Germany regulates mining under the Bundesberggesetz (BBergG, Federal Mining Act), which establishes a concession system (Bewilligung) for mineral extraction and imposes comprehensive operational and environmental requirements, with particular contemporary significance around the lignite coal phase-out, the controversial Lützerath village removal, and the EU Critical Raw Materials Act (Regulation 2024/1252) designed to secure supply chains for minerals essential to the green and digital transitions.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's mining regulation is governed by the Mining Industry Act (광업법, Act No. 19953), which classifies minerals into categories requiring mining rights and establishes the procedures for granting, transferring, and revoking those rights. The Ministry of Trade, Industry and Energy (산업통상자원부) administers the Act.

### Mining Rights System

| Right Type | Description | Duration |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| Exploration right (탐사권) | Right to explore for designated minerals | Up to 6 years (renewable) |
| Mining right (채굴권) | Right to extract designated minerals | Up to 25 years (renewable) |

Mining rights are real property rights (물권) that can be transferred, leased, or used as collateral. The Act designates approximately 60 minerals subject to the mining rights system.

### Environmental Requirements

Mining projects must undergo environmental impact assessment under the Environmental Impact Assessment Act (환경영향평가법) for projects above designated thresholds. The Water Environment Conservation Act and Soil Environment Conservation Act impose additional requirements for water pollution prevention and soil contamination remediation at mine sites.

### Mine Reclamation

The Mine Reclamation Act (광산피해의 방지 및 복구에 관한 법률) addresses both active mine reclamation and the remediation of abandoned mines. South Korea has approximately 5,400 abandoned mines, many dating to the Japanese colonial period (1910-1945), which continue to discharge acid mine drainage and heavy metals. The Korea Mine Reclamation Corporation (한국광해광업공단, formerly 광해관리공단) manages remediation of abandoned mines, funded by mining industry contributions and government appropriations.

### Critical Minerals Strategy

South Korea has identified 35 critical minerals essential for semiconductor manufacturing, battery production, and other strategic industries. The Act on Securing Critical Minerals (첨단전략산업 특별법, 2023) provides government support for overseas mineral development, stockpiling, and recycling.

## United States

### Legal Framework

U.S. mining law is famously fragmented, with different regimes for hardrock minerals, coal, and oil and gas, and overlapping federal, state, and tribal jurisdiction.

### General Mining Law of 1872

The General Mining Law (30 U.S.C. Sections 22-47) allows any U.S. citizen to explore and stake mining claims on open federal public lands for hardrock minerals (gold, silver, copper, uranium, etc.). Key features:

- **Free Access:** No lease or government permission required to prospect
- **No Federal Royalty:** Hardrock miners pay no royalty to the federal government (unique globally)
- **Patenting:** Historical ability to obtain fee simple title for $2.50-$5.00/acre (moratorium on new patents since 1994)
- **Discovery Requirement:** Valid mining claim requires discovery of a valuable mineral deposit

Multiple reform attempts have failed in Congress, with the most recent comprehensive proposals including the Clean Energy Minerals Reform Act seeking to impose royalties, modernize claim procedures, and strengthen environmental protections.

### SMCRA (Coal Mining)

The Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act of 1977 provides comprehensive regulation of coal mining:

- **Permits:** All surface coal mining operations require permits with detailed operational and reclamation plans
- **Performance Standards:** Mandatory standards for topsoil preservation, hydrologic protection, and approximate original contour restoration
- **Bonding:** Financial assurance required for reclamation obligations
- **AML Fund:** Abandoned Mine Land Reclamation Fund, funded by a per-ton fee on coal production, finances cleanup of pre-1977 abandoned coal mines

### Environmental Overlap

| Statute | Application to Mining |
|---------|----------------------|
| Clean Water Act Section 404 | Permits for dredge/fill in waters (critical for mountaintop removal) |
| NEPA | EIS required for mining on federal land |
| CERCLA | Superfund liability for contaminated mine sites |
| Clean Air Act | Emission permits for mining operations and processing |
| Endangered Species Act | Section 7 consultation for mining on federal land |

### Landmark Cases

**United States v. General Motors Corp. (CERCLA mine cases):** Numerous CERCLA cases have imposed Superfund liability on mining companies for historical contamination, including the Bunker Hill Mining Complex in Idaho's Silver Valley.

**Coeur Alaska v. Southeast Alaska Conservation Council, 557 U.S. 261 (2009):** The Supreme Court held that the Army Corps of Engineers, rather than the EPA, had authority to permit the discharge of mining slurry into a lake under CWA Section 404.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's mining regulation is governed by the Bundesberggesetz (BBergG), which establishes a comprehensive framework for mineral exploration and extraction. The Act distinguishes between bergfreie Bodenschätze (minerals free from land ownership -- requiring state concession) and grundeigene Bodenschätze (minerals belonging to the landowner).

### Concession System

| Mineral Category | Authorization Required |
|-----------------|----------------------|
| Bergfreie minerals (coal, ores, salt, hydrocarbons) | Exploration permit (Erlaubnis), extraction concession (Bewilligung), or mining property (Bergwerkseigentum) |
| Grundeigene minerals (gravel, sand, clay, limestone) | Landowner may extract; state mining authority oversight for safety |

### Operating Plan System (Betriebsplanverfahren)

All mining operations require approved operating plans (Betriebspläne):
- **Framework operating plan (Rahmenbetriebsplan):** Long-term plan for the entire mining project including closure
- **Main operating plan (Hauptbetriebsplan):** Detailed plan for a two-year period
- **Special operating plans (Sonderbetriebspläne):** For specific aspects (e.g., water management, blasting)
- **Closure plan (Abschlussbetriebsplan):** Detailed reclamation and post-mining plan

### Lignite Phase-Out and Lützerath

Germany's lignite (Braunkohle) mining has been among the most contentious environmental issues. The coal phase-out (Kohleausstieg), legislated through the Kohleverstromungsbeendungsgesetz (2020), originally set a 2038 deadline for ending coal power, with the 2022 coalition agreement accelerating the North Rhine-Westphalia phase-out to 2030. Despite this, the village of Lützerath was demolished in January 2023 to allow expansion of the Garzweiler II open-pit lignite mine, prompting massive protests. The Federal Constitutional Court declined to issue an interim order preventing the demolition.

### Mine Safety

The BBergG contains comprehensive mine safety provisions, supplemented by the Allgemeine Bundesbergverordnung (ABBergV) and specific ordinances for different mining methods. State mining authorities (Bergämter) conduct inspections and enforce safety requirements.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Critical Raw Materials Act (Regulation 2024/1252)

Adopted on March 11, 2024, the CRM Act addresses the EU's dependence on imported critical minerals for the green and digital transitions:

| Target | Benchmark by 2030 |
|--------|-------------------|
| Domestic extraction | At least 10% of EU annual consumption |
| Domestic processing | At least 40% of EU annual consumption |
| Domestic recycling | At least 25% of EU annual consumption |
| Single-country dependency | No more than 65% of any critical material from a single third country |

Strategic projects receive expedited permitting (24 months for extraction; 12 months for processing) and streamlined environmental assessment.

### Extractive Waste Directive (2006/21/EC)

The Directive establishes requirements for the management of waste from extractive industries, including tailings management facilities. Following the 2000 Baia Mare cyanide spill in Romania and the 2010 Ajka alumina sludge disaster in Hungary, the Directive was strengthened with provisions for:
- Waste management plans for all extractive waste facilities
- Classification of facilities based on risk (Category A facilities subject to enhanced requirements)
- Financial guarantee requirements for closure and after-care
- Major accident prevention policies

### EU Mining Waste BAT Reference Document

The Best Available Techniques (BAT) Reference Document for the Management of Waste from Extractive Industries, published under the Extractive Waste Directive, establishes BAT conclusions for tailings management, acid mine drainage prevention, and progressive rehabilitation.

### ECHR and Mining

**Tatar v. Romania (Application No. 67021/01, 2009):** Following the Baia Mare cyanide spill from a gold mine, the Court found a violation of Article 8, holding that Romania failed to protect residents from environmental contamination caused by mining operations.

**Brincat and Others v. Malta (Application No. 60908/11, 2014):** While concerning asbestos exposure at a shipyard, the Court's principles regarding state obligations to protect workers from known environmental health hazards apply equally to mining operations.

**Kolyadenko and Others v. Russia (Application No. 17423/05, 2012):** The Court found violations of Articles 2 and 8 relating to flooding caused by inadequate management of a mining-related dam, establishing positive obligations for dam safety.

## Comparative Analysis

### Mineral Rights Systems

The jurisdictions employ fundamentally different approaches to mineral ownership. The United States' claim-staking system under the 1872 Mining Law is uniquely permissive, allowing private acquisition of mineral rights on public land without competitive bidding or royalties. Germany's concession system grants exclusive extraction rights but retains state ownership of bergfreie minerals. South Korea's mining rights system similarly grants rights subject to government approval. The EU does not harmonize mineral rights but coordinates critical mineral access.

### Environmental Standards

Environmental protection in mining varies dramatically. SMCRA provides comprehensive standards for coal mining but no equivalent exists for U.S. hardrock mining on federal land. Germany applies the full suite of environmental law (UVP, BImSchG, water protection) to mining operations. South Korea requires EIA and has dedicated mine reclamation legislation. The EU's Extractive Waste Directive provides a harmonized framework for mining waste management.

### Abandoned Mine Legacy

All jurisdictions face significant abandoned mine remediation challenges. The United States has an estimated 500,000 abandoned hardrock mines with no dedicated federal cleanup fund. South Korea's 5,400 abandoned mines from the colonial period require ongoing remediation. Germany faces extensive lignite mine reclamation obligations, with the Lausitzer und Mitteldeutsche Bergbau-Verwaltungsgesellschaft (LMBV) managing post-mining landscapes.

### Critical Minerals and Energy Transition

The energy transition has transformed mining regulation from a primarily environmental concern to a strategic industrial policy issue. The EU's Critical Raw Materials Act, South Korea's critical minerals legislation, and U.S. efforts to streamline permitting for critical mineral projects all reflect the tension between environmental protection and securing mineral supply chains for batteries, semiconductors, and renewable energy.
