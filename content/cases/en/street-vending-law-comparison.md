## Summary

Street vending regulation -- the legal framework governing the sale of goods and food from sidewalks, public spaces, and mobile units, encompassing licensing, health and safety standards, spatial allocation, and enforcement against unauthorized vending -- sits at the intersection of economic freedom, public space management, food safety, urban planning, and informal economy governance, with approaches ranging from outright prohibition to formal licensing systems to deliberate tolerance of informal commerce. South Korea addresses street vending through a complex interaction of the Food Sanitation Act (식품위생법), the Road Act (도로법), and local government ordinances, with an estimated 300,000-400,000 street vendors (노점상) operating throughout the country despite the absence of a comprehensive national street vending permit system; the government has oscillated between crackdown and accommodation, with the 2009 Special Act on Support for Traditional Markets and Commercial Areas (전통시장 및 상점가 육성을 위한 특별법) providing limited recognition of street vendor zones within traditional market areas, and major incidents including the Yongsan Tragedy (2009) and repeated Myeongdong enforcement operations highlighting the political sensitivity of street vendor regulation. The United States regulates street vending through municipal ordinances that vary dramatically between cities, from New York City's capped permit system limiting the number of food vendor licenses to approximately 5,100 with a waiting list exceeding 10,000 applicants, to Portland, Oregon's relatively permissive food cart pod model, to Los Angeles's Sidewalk Vending Program legalized by SB 946 (2018), which decriminalized sidewalk vending statewide in California; the Supreme Court's Fourteenth Amendment jurisprudence has generally upheld municipal authority to regulate street vending, though the First Amendment protects certain expressive vending activities. Germany regulates street vending through the Gewerbeordnung (GewO, Trade Regulation Act), which requires a Reisegewerbekarte (itinerant trade license) for mobile commercial activities under Section 55, and through municipal Sondernutzungssatzungen (special use ordinances) governing the commercial use of public space; Wochenmärkte (weekly markets) are a traditional and regulated institution, and the Lebensmittelhygiene-Verordnung (LMHV) establishes food safety standards for mobile food vendors. At the European level, the Services Directive 2006/123/EC limits member state restrictions on commercial activities including street vending to non-discriminatory, justified, and proportionate measures, Regulation (EC) No 852/2004 on food hygiene applies to mobile food operations, and the ECHR has addressed the tension between property rights, freedom to conduct business, and municipal regulatory authority in cases involving market traders and itinerant vendors.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's street vending landscape is characterized by a large informal sector operating in a legal gray zone, with regulation fragmented across multiple statutes.

**Food Sanitation Act (식품위생법):** Article 37 requires all food service businesses to obtain a permit (허가), registration (등록), or notification (신고) from the local government. Street food vendors technically require notification as food service operators, but the vast majority operate without formal registration. Article 97 provides penalties of up to 3 years imprisonment or KRW 30 million fine for operating a food business without required permits.

**Road Act (도로법) Article 61:** Prohibits the unauthorized occupation of public roads for commercial purposes. Violation carries administrative fines and removal orders. Article 61 is the primary legal basis for enforcement actions against street vendors occupying sidewalks and roadways.

**Special Act on Support for Traditional Markets (전통시장 및 상점가 육성을 위한 특별법):** Article 64-2, added in 2009, authorizes local governments to designate "street vendor permitted zones" (노점 허용 구역) within or adjacent to traditional market areas. Vendors operating within these zones receive limited legal protection and must comply with basic food safety and sanitation requirements.

### Political and Social Context

Street vending has been a politically contentious issue in South Korea. The estimated 300,000-400,000 street vendors represent a significant informal employment sector, with many vendors being elderly, disabled, or economically marginalized individuals for whom street vending provides essential income. Street vendor associations (노점상연합) have been active in advocating for recognition and legalization.

Major enforcement incidents include:
- **Yongsan Tragedy (2009):** While primarily involving building occupants, the incident highlighted the dangers of aggressive enforcement against informal occupants and vendors.
- **Myeongdong Crackdowns:** Seoul's Myeongdong shopping district has been the site of recurring conflicts between authorities seeking to clear street vendors for urban aesthetics and tourism purposes, and vendors defending their livelihoods.
- **COVID-19 Impact:** The pandemic disproportionately affected street vendors, who could not operate under social distancing rules. The government provided limited emergency relief (긴급재난지원금) to registered vendors but many informal operators were excluded.

### Seoul Metropolitan Government Approach

Seoul has adopted a pragmatic approach, designating approximately 3,000 "tolerated" (묵인) vendor locations while maintaining enforcement authority over unauthorized locations. The Seoul Street Vendor Consultation Committee (서울특별시 노점상 협의회) serves as a dialogue platform between vendors, merchants, and city officials.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Street vending regulation in the United States is overwhelmingly a municipal matter, creating vast variation between cities.

**New York City:** The City operates a highly restrictive permit system. Mobile food vendor licenses are capped at approximately 5,100, with a waiting list that has historically exceeded 10,000 applicants and wait times of 15-20 years. Street Vendor Project advocacy led to the passage of Int. 1116 (2021), which created 400 new food vending permits and established a Street Vendor Advisory Board. Penalties for vending without a license range from $200 to $1,000 per violation.

**Los Angeles / California SB 946 (2018):** California's Safe Sidewalk Vending Act, signed by Governor Jerry Brown on September 17, 2018, decriminalized sidewalk vending statewide and prohibited cities from imposing criminal penalties on vendors. Cities may adopt regulatory frameworks including permits, food safety requirements, and spatial restrictions but cannot criminalize vending itself. Los Angeles implemented the Sidewalk Vending Program in 2022 with a permit fee of $541 annually.

**Portland, Oregon:** Portland's "food cart pod" model has been internationally recognized as an innovative approach to street food regulation. The city permits food carts on private lots (requiring lot owner permission and business registration) with relatively minimal regulatory barriers, creating vibrant food cart clusters that have become integral to Portland's food culture and urban identity.

**First Amendment Protections:** The sale of written materials, artwork, and expressive merchandise may be protected by the First Amendment. In Bery v. City of New York, 97 F.3d 689 (2nd Cir. 1996), the Second Circuit held that artists displaying and selling their work on public sidewalks were engaged in protected expression, limiting the city's ability to require vending licenses for expressive activities.

### Key Case Law

**City of New York v. American School Publications, Inc., 69 N.Y.2d 576 (1987):** The New York Court of Appeals held that general vending regulations are valid exercises of the police power and do not violate due process or equal protection unless they are arbitrary or discriminatory.

**Planned Parenthood Ass'n v. City of Chicago, 139 F.3d 563 (7th Cir. 1998):** The Seventh Circuit distinguished between commercial vending (regulable) and expressive distribution (protected), establishing that the character of the activity determines the applicable constitutional standard.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's street vending regulation reflects its tradition of orderly public space management and high commercial formalization.

**Gewerbeordnung (GewO) Section 55 -- Reisegewerbe (Itinerant Trade):** Mobile commercial activity -- including street vending, door-to-door sales, and market trading -- requires a Reisegewerbekarte (itinerant trade license) issued by the Ordnungsamt (public order office). The license is granted upon application and proof of reliability (Zuverlässigkeit). Certain activities are exempt, including the sale of self-produced agricultural products and charitable sales. Operating without the required license constitutes an Ordnungswidrigkeit punishable by fines up to EUR 5,000 (Section 145 GewO).

**Sondernutzungssatzung (Special Use Ordinance):** Municipal governments regulate the commercial use of public space through Sondernutzungssatzungen, which require permits for any commercial activity on public sidewalks, squares, or other municipal property. Permit fees, spatial restrictions, and operating hours are determined by each municipality.

**Wochenmärkte (Weekly Markets):** Weekly markets are a traditional and legally recognized institution in German municipal life. Under the GewO and municipal market ordinances (Marktordnungen), municipalities establish, operate, and regulate weekly markets, assigning stall locations, setting operating hours (typically 7:00 AM to 2:00 PM), and enforcing quality and hygiene standards. Approximately 3,000 weekly markets operate across Germany, providing a formalized and culturally valued framework for outdoor food vending.

**Lebensmittelhygiene-Verordnung (LMHV) and Regulation 852/2004:** All food vendors, including street food and market vendors, must comply with food hygiene requirements under the LMHV (which transposes EU Regulation 852/2004). Requirements include food handler certification (Gesundheitszeugnis from the Gesundheitsamt), proper food storage and temperature control, handwashing facilities, and traceability documentation.

### Berlin Street Food Scene

Berlin's street food scene has expanded significantly since the 2010s, with food truck festivals (Street Food Thursday at Markthalle Neun), mobile food markets, and food truck parks becoming popular cultural institutions. The city has adapted its regulatory framework to accommodate this trend, issuing Sondernutzungserlaubnisse for food trucks at designated locations while maintaining strict food safety standards.

### Informal Vending

Unlike South Korea and many other countries, Germany has a relatively small informal vending sector. The combination of comprehensive social welfare (Bürgergeld), universal health insurance, and strong labor market institutions reduces the economic necessity of informal vending. When informal vending occurs, it is primarily associated with undocumented migrants and may involve enforcement by both the Ordnungsamt (for vending violations) and the Zoll (customs/labor enforcement, for employment law violations).

## ECHR & EU

### EU Services Directive 2006/123/EC

The Services Directive applies to street vending as a commercial service activity. Key principles:

- **Article 9:** Authorization schemes for commercial activities must be non-discriminatory, justified by an overriding reason related to the public interest, and proportionate.
- **Article 12:** Where the number of authorizations is limited (as in New York City's permit cap system), member states must apply transparent, impartial selection criteria.
- **Article 15:** Member states must examine whether their regulation of commercial activities contains unjustified territorial restrictions, quantitative limits, or requirements that restrict market entry.

### EU Food Hygiene Regulation 852/2004

Regulation (EC) No 852/2004 on the hygiene of foodstuffs applies to all food business operators, including street food vendors. Chapter III of Annex II establishes requirements for movable and/or temporary premises, including: maintenance of cleanliness, adequate handwashing and sanitizing conditions, adequate water supply, safe food storage and display, and waste disposal measures.

### European Court of Human Rights

**Stec and Others v. United Kingdom (Grand Chamber, 2006):** While addressing welfare benefits, the Court's autonomous interpretation of "possessions" under Protocol 1 may extend to established market trading positions or vendor permits where they have economic value.

**Könyv-Tár Kft and Others v. Hungary (Application No. 21623/13, Judgment of October 16, 2018):** The Court addressed the revocation of tobacco retail licenses, holding that vested trading rights constitute possessions. By analogy, established vendor permits may enjoy Protocol 1 protection against arbitrary revocation.

### ILO Recommendation 204 (2015)

While not legally binding in European or national law, ILO Recommendation No. 204 concerning the Transition from the Informal to the Formal Economy (2015) provides influential international guidance on street vending policy. The Recommendation advocates for policies that facilitate formalization of informal enterprises including street vendors through simplified registration, accessible permits, and progressive integration into social protection systems.

## Comparative Analysis

### Formalization Spectrum

The four jurisdictions represent a spectrum of formalization approaches. Germany maintains the highest degree of formalization through its Reisegewerbekarte and Sondernutzung systems, with minimal informal vending. The United States varies dramatically by city, from New York's restrictive cap to California's liberalized approach. South Korea operates with a large informal sector coexisting alongside limited formal recognition zones. The EU framework encourages formalization through the Services Directive's proportionality requirements, which limit member states' ability to impose disproportionate barriers to market entry.

### Economic Function

Street vending serves fundamentally different economic functions across jurisdictions. In South Korea, it provides essential income for economically marginalized populations, with an estimated 80 percent of street vendors being persons over 50 years of age or from low-income households. In the United States, it ranges from survival employment (particularly for immigrant communities) to artisanal food entrepreneurship, with the food truck and food cart industries generating an estimated $1.2 billion in annual revenue. In Germany, street vending is primarily associated with the established Wochenmarkt tradition and emerging food truck culture rather than economic necessity, reflecting the country's strong social safety net and formalized labor market.

### Enforcement Challenges

All jurisdictions face enforcement challenges, though of different kinds. South Korea struggles with the political and humanitarian costs of enforcing against a large informal sector -- eviction of elderly vendors generates significant media coverage and public sympathy, creating political risks for enforcement actions. U.S. cities grapple with permit scarcity, wait lists, and equity concerns, with advocacy organizations arguing that permit caps disproportionately exclude immigrant vendors. Germany faces relatively minor enforcement challenges due to its high formalization rate but must manage the tension between regulatory compliance costs and market innovation, particularly as the food truck trend encounters traditional Sondernutzung frameworks designed for stationary kiosks.

### Health and Safety

Food safety regulation converges across jurisdictions, with all requiring some form of food handler certification, facility standards, and inspection. The EU framework (Regulation 852/2004) provides the most harmonized approach, establishing consistent hygiene standards for mobile food operations across 27 member states. South Korea's enforcement of food safety standards for street vendors is inconsistent, creating public health concerns -- a 2022 survey by the Korea Consumer Agency found that only 23 percent of informal street food vendors met all applicable food safety standards. The United States and Germany generally maintain effective food safety enforcement for licensed vendors, though the growing number of unlicensed vendors in some U.S. cities raises food safety concerns.

### Vendor Rights and Social Protection

A critical emerging issue across all jurisdictions is the social protection status of street vendors. In South Korea, informal vendors lack access to workers' compensation, health insurance subsidies linked to formal employment, and retirement benefits. In the United States, street vendors are typically classified as independent contractors or self-employed, excluding them from employer-provided benefits. Germany's comprehensive social insurance system covers self-employed vendors who register, but the administrative burden of registration can be a barrier. The ILO's Recommendation 204 advocates for progressive extension of social protection to informal economy workers, including street vendors, through simplified registration and contribution mechanisms.

### Spatial Justice and Public Space

Street vending regulation inevitably raises questions of spatial justice -- who has the right to use public space for economic activity, and whose interests prevail when vendor activities conflict with pedestrian access, traffic flow, or competing businesses. South Korea's approach of designating tolerated zones attempts to balance these interests but creates spatial inequalities between permitted and prohibited areas. The U.S. approach varies, with some cities designating vendor zones and others using cap-and-permit systems that create scarcity. Germany's Sondernutzung framework provides orderly allocation but may favor established vendors over new entrants. The ECHR has not directly addressed street vendor spatial rights, but its Article 1 Protocol 1 jurisprudence on market trading positions suggests that established vendor rights may enjoy property-like protection against arbitrary revocation.
