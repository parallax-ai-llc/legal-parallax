## Summary

Energy deregulation -- the restructuring of electricity and gas markets from vertically integrated monopolies to competitive markets with separated generation, transmission, and retail functions -- represents one of the most consequential regulatory transformations of the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries, with outcomes ranging from lower consumer prices and innovation to catastrophic market failures and price volatility. South Korea operates a partially deregulated electricity market through the Korea Power Exchange (전력거래소, KPX), established under the Electricity Business Act (전기사업법), with generation competition among multiple producers but a single transmission and distribution monopoly held by the Korea Electric Power Corporation (한국전력공사, KEPCO); retail electricity prices remain regulated by the Ministry of Trade, Industry and Energy, and full retail competition has been repeatedly postponed since the initial restructuring plan of 1999. The United States presents a patchwork of deregulated and regulated electricity markets, with approximately twenty states and the District of Columbia having introduced retail electricity competition since the 1990s, while the remaining states maintain traditional regulated utility monopolies; the 2000-2001 California electricity crisis -- marked by rolling blackouts, price spikes exceeding 800% above normal levels, and the bankruptcy of Pacific Gas and Electric Company -- and the Enron scandal exposed the risks of poorly designed deregulation, leading several states to abandon or freeze restructuring efforts; the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC) oversees wholesale markets through regional transmission organizations (RTOs) and independent system operators (ISOs). Germany undertook comprehensive energy market liberalization through the Energy Industry Act (Energiewirtschaftsgesetz, EnWG) of 1998, implementing EU unbundling requirements that separated generation, transmission, and distribution functions; Germany now has over 1,000 electricity retailers competing for approximately 48 million customer accounts, though the four major companies (E.ON, RWE, EnBW, and Vattenfall) retain dominant market positions, and the 2022 energy crisis prompted emergency government interventions including a gas price brake and electricity price cap that temporarily overrode market mechanisms. At the European level, the EU's three energy liberalization packages (1996/1998, 2003, and 2009) and the Clean Energy Package (2019) have progressively required member states to open electricity and gas markets to competition, unbundle vertically integrated utilities, and establish independent national regulatory authorities; the 2022-2023 energy crisis led to the adoption of emergency market intervention measures under Council Regulation (EU) 2022/1854 and prompted a fundamental reassessment of EU electricity market design, culminating in the Electricity Market Design Reform Regulation (EU) 2024/1747.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's electricity sector operates under a partially deregulated structure established by the Electricity Business Act (전기사업법, originally enacted 1990, comprehensively amended 2000) and administered by the Korea Power Exchange.

**Restructuring History:** The Asian financial crisis of 1997 prompted the Korean government to adopt a comprehensive electricity restructuring plan in 1999, modeled on the UK privatization experience. The plan envisioned four phases:

| Phase | Goal | Status |
|-------|------|--------|
| Phase 1 (2001) | Establish competitive generation market; create Korea Power Exchange | Completed |
| Phase 2 (2003-2004) | Separate generation subsidiaries from KEPCO; introduce wholesale competition | Partially completed |
| Phase 3 (2007-2009) | Introduce retail competition; customer choice of electricity supplier | Indefinitely postponed |
| Phase 4 (2009+) | Full privatization of generation and distribution | Abandoned |

**Current Structure:** KEPCO (한국전력공사) was partially unbundled in 2001, with generation separated into six subsidiary companies (Korea Hydro & Nuclear Power, Korea South-East Power, Korea Midland Power, Korea Western Power, Korea Southern Power, Korea East-West Power). These subsidiaries compete in the wholesale market through cost-based bidding on the Korea Power Exchange. However, KEPCO retains ownership of all six generation subsidiaries, the transmission network, and the distribution system, and serves as the sole retail electricity provider.

**Regulated Pricing:** Retail electricity tariffs are set by the Ministry of Trade, Industry and Energy (산업통상자원부) through a tariff adjustment mechanism. Tariffs have historically been kept below cost-recovery levels for social and political reasons, leading to chronic financial losses for KEPCO -- cumulative debt exceeded 200 trillion KRW (approximately $150 billion) by 2023. The gap between wholesale procurement costs and regulated retail prices widened dramatically during the 2022 energy crisis.

### Reform Debates

The question of whether to proceed with retail electricity competition remains politically contentious. Proponents argue that competition would improve efficiency, encourage innovation, and reduce KEPCO's debt burden. Opponents, including labor unions and consumer groups, argue that deregulation would lead to higher prices for residential consumers, threaten universal service obligations, and create the conditions for California-style market failures. The current policy consensus favors incremental wholesale market reform while maintaining regulated retail pricing.

### Gas Market

The gas market remains fully regulated, with KOGAS (한국가스공사) holding a monopoly on LNG import, regasification, and wholesale supply. City gas companies serve retail customers under regulated franchise arrangements.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States operates the world's most complex energy regulatory system, reflecting the constitutional division of authority between federal and state governments.

**Federal Regulation -- FERC:** The Federal Energy Regulatory Commission, an independent agency within the Department of Energy, regulates interstate electricity transmission and wholesale electricity sales under the Federal Power Act (16 U.S.C. Sections 791a-828c). Key FERC orders shaping deregulation include:

| Order | Year | Effect |
|-------|------|--------|
| Order 888 | 1996 | Required transmission-owning utilities to provide open access to their transmission systems |
| Order 889 | 1996 | Established Open Access Same-Time Information System (OASIS) for transmission scheduling |
| Order 2000 | 1999 | Encouraged formation of Regional Transmission Organizations (RTOs) |
| Order 1000 | 2011 | Reformed transmission planning and cost allocation |
| Order 2222 | 2020 | Required RTOs to accommodate distributed energy resource aggregations |

**Regional Wholesale Markets:** Seven RTOs/ISOs operate competitive wholesale electricity markets covering approximately two-thirds of U.S. electricity consumers: PJM Interconnection, Midcontinent ISO (MISO), ISO New England, New York ISO, Southwest Power Pool (SPP), California ISO (CAISO), and Electric Reliability Council of Texas (ERCOT, which is not FERC-regulated).

**State Retail Deregulation:** Beginning with Rhode Island in 1996, approximately twenty states and the District of Columbia introduced retail electricity competition. Key restructured states include Texas, Pennsylvania, Ohio, Illinois, New York, and Connecticut. The remaining states maintain traditional cost-of-service regulation with vertically integrated utilities.

### California Electricity Crisis (2000-2001)

The California crisis remains the defining cautionary tale of energy deregulation:

- California's restructuring law (AB 1890, 1996) froze retail rates while deregulating wholesale markets
- Wholesale prices surged from approximately $30/MWh to over $300/MWh during 2000-2001
- Market manipulation by Enron and other traders exacerbated price spikes through strategies including "Fat Boy," "Death Star," and "Get Shorty"
- Pacific Gas and Electric declared bankruptcy in April 2001
- Governor Gray Davis ordered emergency long-term power purchase contracts at above-market prices
- The crisis contributed to Governor Davis's recall in 2003

**FERC v. Electric Power Supply Association (577 U.S. 260, 2016):** The Supreme Court upheld FERC's authority to regulate demand response programs in wholesale markets, confirming the Commission's broad jurisdiction over wholesale market design.

### Texas Winter Storm Uri (2021)

The February 2021 winter storm exposed critical vulnerabilities in Texas's deregulated electricity market. ERCOT experienced a cascading generation failure as extreme cold disabled natural gas, wind, and nuclear facilities simultaneously. Wholesale prices reached the $9,000/MWh cap for several consecutive days. The crisis resulted in an estimated 246 deaths, approximately $195 billion in damages, and the bankruptcy of Brazos Electric Power Cooperative and retail provider Griddy. Texas subsequently enacted SB 3 (2021) mandating weatherization of generation and gas facilities.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany implemented comprehensive energy market liberalization beginning in 1998, driven by EU directive requirements and domestic competition policy.

**Energiewirtschaftsgesetz (EnWG):** The Energy Industry Act of 1998 opened the German electricity and gas markets to competition, replacing the system of regional monopolies that had prevailed since the early twentieth century. The EnWG was comprehensively revised in 2005 to implement the EU's Second Energy Package, and again amended to implement the Third Energy Package's unbundling requirements.

**Unbundling:** Germany implemented legal unbundling (separate legal entities for network and competitive activities) for all vertically integrated utilities and ownership unbundling for the four major transmission system operators (TSOs): TenneT (formerly E.ON Netz), Amprion (formerly RWE Transportnetz Strom), TransnetBW (EnBW subsidiary), and 50Hertz (formerly Vattenfall Europe Transmission). Distribution system operators (DSOs) are subject to functional unbundling requirements.

**Regulatory Authority:** The Federal Network Agency (Bundesnetzagentur, BNetzA), established in 2005, regulates network access, network tariffs, and market conditions for electricity and gas. The BNetzA operates under an incentive regulation framework (Anreizregulierung) that sets revenue caps for network operators based on efficiency benchmarking.

### Market Structure

Germany's electricity retail market features over 1,000 active suppliers competing for approximately 48 million customer accounts. The switching rate -- the percentage of customers who change their electricity supplier annually -- has increased from approximately 6% in 2010 to approximately 14% in 2022, though the "Big Four" (E.ON, RWE, EnBW, Vattenfall) retain collectively approximately 40% of retail market share.

The wholesale market operates through the European Energy Exchange (EEX, based in Leipzig) for futures and derivatives, and EPEX Spot (based in Paris) for day-ahead and intraday electricity trading. German wholesale prices are coupled with neighboring markets through the EU's single day-ahead coupling (SDAC) mechanism.

### 2022-2023 Energy Crisis Response

The energy crisis triggered by the Russia-Ukraine conflict prompted unprecedented government intervention in Germany's liberalized energy markets:

**Gas Price Brake (Gaspreisbremse):** Effective March 2023, the government capped residential gas prices at 12 cents per kWh for 80% of the previous year's consumption. The measure was funded through a federal economic stabilization fund.

**Electricity Price Brake (Strompreisbremse):** Effective January 2023, residential electricity prices were capped at 40 cents per kWh for 80% of the previous year's consumption, with revenue from inframarginal electricity generators (renewables, nuclear) clawed back to fund the subsidy.

**Nationalization of Uniper:** The German government acquired a 99% stake in Uniper, the country's largest gas importer, for approximately 1 euro in September 2022 after the company faced insolvency due to the loss of Russian gas supplies and the obligation to procure replacement gas at dramatically higher spot market prices.

## ECHR / EU

### Three Energy Liberalization Packages

The EU has driven energy market liberalization through three successive legislative packages:

**First Package (1996/1998):** Directive 96/92/EC (electricity) and Directive 98/30/EC (gas) introduced limited market opening, requiring member states to allow large industrial customers to choose their electricity and gas suppliers.

**Second Package (2003):** Directive 2003/54/EC (electricity) and Directive 2003/55/EC (gas) mandated full market opening by July 1, 2007, requiring all customers -- including households -- to have the right to choose their supplier. The package established requirements for legal unbundling of network activities.

**Third Package (2009):** Directive 2009/72/EC (electricity) and Directive 2009/73/EC (gas) strengthened unbundling requirements (ownership unbundling as the preferred model), established the Agency for the Cooperation of Energy Regulators (ACER), and created the European Networks of Transmission System Operators (ENTSO-E and ENTSOG) for cross-border coordination.

### Clean Energy Package (2019)

The Clean Energy for All Europeans Package updated the EU electricity market design to accommodate the energy transition. Key elements include Regulation (EU) 2019/943 on the internal market for electricity and Directive (EU) 2019/944 on common rules for the internal market for electricity. The package introduced provisions for active consumers ("prosumers"), energy communities, and demand-side flexibility.

### 2022-2023 Crisis and Market Reform

The energy price crisis prompted emergency EU measures:

**Council Regulation (EU) 2022/1854:** Adopted in October 2022, this emergency regulation imposed:
- A mandatory 5% reduction in electricity consumption during peak hours
- A revenue cap of 180 euros per MWh for inframarginal generators
- A solidarity contribution from fossil fuel companies' excess profits

**Electricity Market Design Reform (2024):** Regulation (EU) 2024/1747 and Directive (EU) 2024/1711 reformed the EU electricity market design to:
- Promote long-term contracts (power purchase agreements and contracts for difference) to reduce exposure to short-term price volatility
- Strengthen consumer protection through the right to fixed-price contracts and supplier-of-last-resort obligations
- Maintain the marginal pricing model for short-term markets while shielding consumers from extreme price spikes
- Enhance the role of flexibility and storage in market design

### ACER and Cross-Border Market Regulation

The EU Agency for the Cooperation of Energy Regulators (ACER), established by Regulation (EC) No 713/2009 and strengthened by Regulation (EU) 2019/942, coordinates national regulatory authorities and oversees cross-border market issues. ACER monitors wholesale energy market integrity under the REMIT Regulation (Regulation (EU) No 1227/2011), which prohibits market manipulation and insider trading in wholesale energy markets.

## Comparative Analysis

### Degree of Liberalization

Germany and the EU represent the most complete liberalization model, with full retail competition and ownership unbundling. The United States occupies an intermediate position, with comprehensive wholesale market reform but state-by-state retail deregulation. South Korea has implemented wholesale competition within a state-owned structure but has not introduced retail competition. This spectrum reflects different assessments of the trade-off between competition benefits and the risks of market failure.

### Crisis Vulnerability

All three jurisdictions with competitive markets have experienced significant market crises. California (2000-2001), Texas (2021), and the EU/Germany (2022-2023) each demonstrated that deregulated energy markets can produce extreme price volatility and supply failures under stress conditions. The policy response in each case involved temporary re-regulation: California restored some retail regulation, Texas mandated weatherization, and the EU imposed emergency revenue caps and price brakes. South Korea's regulated retail pricing, while producing chronic utility losses, shielded consumers from the worst effects of the 2022 energy price surge.

### Institutional Design

The independent regulatory authority model has been adopted across all jurisdictions but with varying degrees of independence. FERC and BNetzA operate with significant autonomy, while South Korea's MOTIE retains direct authority over retail pricing. The EU's ACER has grown in authority but remains dependent on cooperation from national regulators. The quality of regulatory institutions has proven to be the single most important determinant of deregulation outcomes.

### Energy Transition Implications

Energy deregulation frameworks are now being redesigned to accommodate the transition to renewable energy. The marginal cost pricing model used in competitive wholesale markets -- where the most expensive generator dispatched sets the price for all -- produces high consumer prices when gas sets the marginal price, even though renewable energy has near-zero marginal costs. The EU's 2024 market design reform and similar debates in U.S. RTOs and South Korea's KPX reflect the search for market designs that efficiently integrate renewables while providing investment signals and consumer protection.
