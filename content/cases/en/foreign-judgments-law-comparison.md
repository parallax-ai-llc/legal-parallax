## Summary

The recognition and enforcement of foreign judgments -- the process by which a court in one jurisdiction gives legal effect to a judicial decision rendered in another -- is a cornerstone of international commercial relations and private international law, determining whether successful litigants can realize the value of their judgments across borders. South Korea governs foreign judgment recognition through Article 217 of the Civil Procedure Act (민사소송법) and Article 26-27 of the Civil Execution Act (민사집행법), which establish four conditions for recognition: jurisdiction of the foreign court, proper service on the defendant, absence of conflict with Korean public policy and good morals, and reciprocity (상호보증), with the Korean Supreme Court (대법원) having developed substantial jurisprudence on each element, most notably expanding the reciprocity requirement in Decision 2009Da22549 (2012) and addressing the recognition of foreign punitive damages awards in Decision 2015Da207747 (2018). The United States lacks a federal statute on foreign judgment recognition and relies on state law, with the Uniform Foreign-Country Money Judgments Recognition Act (UFCMJRA, 2005), adopted by approximately 30 states, and its predecessor the Uniform Foreign Money-Judgments Recognition Act (1962) providing the primary framework, supplemented by common law principles of comity, while the Supreme Court established the foundational principle of comity-based recognition in Hilton v. Guyot (1895) and the Hague Judgments Convention (2019), which the United States signed in 2022, represents a potential transformative development. Germany recognizes foreign judgments under ZPO Section 328, requiring proper jurisdiction, proper service, absence of conflicting domestic judgments, compatibility with public policy, and reciprocity, and also participates in the comprehensive EU framework under Regulation (EU) No. 1215/2012 (Brussels I Recast) which provides for virtually automatic recognition and enforcement among EU member states. The EU's Brussels I Recast Regulation represents the most advanced system of mutual recognition of judgments globally, abolishing exequatur proceedings for most categories of judgments, while the ECHR's Article 6 jurisprudence establishes that the right to enforcement of judgments is an integral part of the right to a fair trial, and the Hague Conference's 2019 Convention on the Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Judgments in Civil or Commercial Matters promises to create a global framework analogous to the New York Convention for arbitral awards.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's foreign judgment recognition regime is governed by the Civil Procedure Act (민사소송법) Article 217 and the Civil Execution Act (민사집행법) Articles 26-27.

**Article 217 (Recognition Requirements / 외국재판의 승인):** A final and conclusive judgment of a foreign court is recognized in Korea when all of the following conditions are met:

1. **Jurisdiction (관할권):** The foreign court had jurisdiction under Korean law principles or international conventions. The Korean Supreme Court applies Korean jurisdictional rules to assess whether the foreign court had appropriate jurisdiction.

2. **Service (송달):** The losing defendant received lawful service of the summons or equivalent document in sufficient time to prepare a defense, or responded without such service.

3. **Public Policy (공서양속):** Recognition of the judgment must not be contrary to the good morals or social order of the Republic of Korea.

4. **Reciprocity (상호보증):** There must be reciprocal guarantees between Korea and the country of the foreign court, either by treaty or by mutual recognition in practice.

**Article 26-27 of the Civil Execution Act:** For enforcement (as opposed to mere recognition), the judgment creditor must obtain an enforcement judgment (집행판결) from a Korean court. The court examines whether the Article 217 conditions are met but does not review the merits of the foreign judgment.

### Landmark Cases

**Supreme Court Decision 2009Da22549 (2012) -- Reciprocity Standard:** The Supreme Court significantly clarified the reciprocity requirement, holding that reciprocity exists when the foreign country recognizes Korean judgments under conditions that are not materially more burdensome than Korea's own recognition conditions. The Court found reciprocity with the United States (specifically, certain state courts) based on the UFCMJRA framework.

**Supreme Court Decision 2015Da207747 (2018) -- Punitive Damages Recognition:** In a landmark ruling, the Supreme Court addressed recognition of a U.S. punitive damages award. The Court held that while the purely punitive component of a foreign damages award may be contrary to Korean public policy under Article 217(3), the portion of the award that serves compensatory purposes (including damages for non-economic harm) may be recognized. The Court reduced the punitive damages to the compensatory equivalent.

**Supreme Court Decision 2016Da237624 (2019) -- Chinese Judgment Recognition:** The Court denied recognition of a Chinese court judgment on reciprocity grounds, finding that China's conditions for recognizing Korean judgments were materially more burdensome than Korea's conditions for recognizing Chinese judgments.

### Penalties for Non-Compliance

There are no penalties for failing to voluntarily comply with a foreign judgment. The judgment creditor must pursue recognition and enforcement through the Korean courts. Once an enforcement judgment is obtained, standard civil execution procedures (property seizure, wage garnishment, etc.) apply.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has no federal statute governing the recognition of foreign-country judgments. Recognition is primarily a matter of state law.

**Uniform Foreign-Country Money Judgments Recognition Act (UFCMJRA, 2005):** Adopted by approximately 30 states and the District of Columbia, the UFCMJRA provides a systematic framework for recognizing foreign-country money judgments. Key provisions:

- **Section 3 (Applicability):** Applies to foreign-country judgments that grant or deny recovery of a sum of money and that are final, conclusive, and enforceable where rendered
- **Section 4 (Mandatory Grounds for Non-Recognition):** The judgment shall not be recognized if: (a) it was rendered under a judicial system that does not provide impartial tribunals or procedures compatible with due process; (b) the foreign court lacked personal jurisdiction over the defendant; or (c) the foreign court lacked subject matter jurisdiction
- **Section 4 (Discretionary Grounds for Non-Recognition):** The court may deny recognition if: the defendant did not receive notice; the judgment was obtained by fraud; the claim is repugnant to public policy; the judgment conflicts with another final judgment; the proceedings contravened an agreement between the parties; jurisdiction was based on improper service; the foreign jurisdiction was seriously inconvenient; or the integrity of the foreign judgment is in substantial doubt

**Hilton v. Guyot, 159 U.S. 113 (1895):** The Supreme Court established the principle of comity as the foundation for foreign judgment recognition, famously defining comity as "the recognition which one nation allows within its territory to the legislative, executive or judicial acts of another nation." The Court held that reciprocity was required for recognition, though most states have since abandoned the reciprocity requirement under the UFCMJRA.

**No Reciprocity Requirement (Majority Rule):** Unlike South Korea and Germany, the UFCMJRA does not condition recognition on reciprocity. This has created a significant asymmetry: U.S. courts readily recognize foreign judgments while many foreign courts require reciprocity before recognizing U.S. judgments.

### Hague Judgments Convention (2019)

The Hague Convention of July 2, 2019, on the Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Judgments in Civil or Commercial Matters represents a potentially transformative development. The United States signed the Convention on March 2, 2022, but has not yet ratified it. If widely adopted, the Convention would create a global framework for judgment recognition analogous to the New York Convention for arbitral awards.

Key Convention provisions:
- Indirect jurisdictional filters (Article 5): recognition is required only where the originating court had jurisdiction under specified bases
- Public policy exception (Article 7(1)(c)): recognition may be refused if manifestly incompatible with public policy
- No reciprocity requirement
- Exclusions for tax, customs, IP, and certain other categories

### Enforcement of Sister-State Judgments

Within the United States, the Full Faith and Credit Clause (Article IV, Section 1) and its implementing statute (28 U.S.C. Section 1738) require each state to give full faith and credit to the judicial proceedings of every other state. Foreign-country judgments do not receive full faith and credit protection.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany operates a dual framework: EU law governs recognition of judgments from other EU member states, while ZPO Section 328 governs recognition of judgments from non-EU countries.

**ZPO Section 328 (Third-Country Judgments):** Recognition of a foreign judgment is excluded if:
1. The courts of the foreign state lack jurisdiction under German law principles
2. The defendant was not properly served and could not defend
3. The judgment conflicts with a prior German judgment or a previously recognized foreign judgment
4. Recognition would be manifestly incompatible with fundamental principles of German law (ordre public)
5. Reciprocity is not guaranteed

**Enforcement:** For enforcement of recognized third-country judgments, ZPO Section 722 requires an enforcement judgment (Vollstreckungsurteil) from a German court, obtained through a proceeding in which the court examines only the conditions of Section 328 without reviewing the merits.

### EU Framework -- Brussels I Recast (Regulation 1215/2012)

Within the EU, Germany applies the comprehensive framework of the Brussels I Recast Regulation, which provides for virtually automatic recognition and enforcement:

**Article 36 (Recognition):** A judgment given in a member state shall be recognized in the other member states without any special procedure being required.

**Article 39 (Enforcement):** A judgment given in a member state which is enforceable in that member state shall be enforceable in the other member states without any declaration of enforceability being required (abolition of exequatur).

**Article 45 (Grounds for Refusal):** Recognition or enforcement may be refused only on limited grounds: (a) manifestly contrary to public policy; (b) default judgment where the defendant was not served in sufficient time; (c) irreconcilable with a judgment given between the same parties; (d) irreconcilable with an earlier judgment given in another member state or third state.

### Punitive Damages and Public Policy

German courts have consistently held that foreign punitive damages awards are partially contrary to German public policy (ordre public). In BGH Decision IX ZB 2/97 (2001), the Federal Court of Justice held that recognition of a U.S. punitive damages award is excluded to the extent that the damages exceed the actual harm suffered. However, compensatory elements embedded within a "punitive damages" label may be recognized. This approach mirrors the Korean Supreme Court's subsequent treatment.

### Lugano Convention

Germany (as an EU member state) is party to the Lugano Convention on Jurisdiction and the Recognition and Enforcement of Judgments in Civil and Commercial Matters (2007), which extends the Brussels regime to Switzerland, Norway, and Iceland.

## ECHR / EU

### ECHR Article 6 -- Right to Enforcement

**Hornsby v. Greece (Application No. 18357/91, March 19, 1997):** The Court held that the right to enforcement of judicial decisions is an integral part of the right to a fair trial under Article 6(1). The Court stated: "the right to a court would be illusory if a Contracting State's domestic legal system allowed a final, binding judicial decision to remain inoperative to the detriment of one party."

**Apostol v. Georgia (Application No. 40765/02, November 28, 2006):** The Court confirmed that the right to enforcement extends to foreign judgments that have been recognized by the domestic legal system, holding that unreasonable delay in enforcing a recognized foreign judgment violates Article 6.

### Brussels I Recast -- Abolition of Exequatur

The Brussels I Recast Regulation (EU) No. 1215/2012, applicable since January 10, 2015, represents the culmination of decades of EU judgment recognition harmonization:

- **Brussels Convention (1968):** Original convention requiring exequatur proceedings
- **Brussels I Regulation (Regulation (EC) No. 44/2001):** Streamlined exequatur but retained it as a requirement
- **Brussels I Recast (Regulation (EU) No. 1215/2012):** Abolished exequatur for most judgments, enabling direct enforcement

### Hague Judgments Convention (2019)

The Convention, concluded on July 2, 2019, at the Twenty-Second Session of the Hague Conference on Private International Law, entered into force on September 1, 2023, initially between the EU (which ratified on August 29, 2022) and Ukraine. The Convention is open for accession and has been signed by the United States, Israel, and several other states.

**Article 4 (General Provisions):** A judgment given by a court of a Contracting State (State of origin) shall be recognized and enforced in another Contracting State (requested State) in accordance with the Convention.

**Article 5 (Bases for Recognition and Enforcement):** Enumerates exclusive jurisdictional bases, including habitual residence, principal place of business, performance of contractual obligations, and tort location.

**Article 7 (Refusal of Recognition):** Recognition or enforcement may be refused if: (a) the document instituting proceedings was not notified to the defendant in sufficient time; (b) the judgment was obtained by fraud; (c) recognition or enforcement is manifestly incompatible with public policy; (d) inconsistency with another judgment; (e) the proceedings contravened a choice-of-court agreement.

### CJEU Jurisprudence

**Case C-559/14, Meroni v. Recoletos (2016):** The CJEU addressed the relationship between the Brussels I Recast and public policy, holding that the public policy exception must be interpreted strictly and applied only where recognition or enforcement would be manifestly incompatible with the legal order of the requested state, affecting a fundamental principle.

**Case C-681/13, Diageo Brands v. Simiramida (2015):** The CJEU held that an error of law by the court of origin, even regarding EU law, does not in itself constitute a ground for refusing recognition under the public policy exception. The Court emphasized the principle of mutual trust between EU member states.

## Comparative Analysis

### Reciprocity

The reciprocity requirement is the most significant divergence among the systems. South Korea and Germany (for third-country judgments) require reciprocity, creating potential barriers to enforcement. The United States and the EU do not require reciprocity. The Hague Convention does not require reciprocity among contracting states, potentially resolving this issue if widely adopted.

### Public Policy Exception

All four systems include a public policy exception, but its application varies. The most frequent public policy obstacle is punitive damages: Korean, German, and most EU courts will not enforce the purely punitive component of foreign damages awards. The CJEU requires a strict interpretation of public policy within the EU, while national courts retain broader discretion for third-country judgments.

### Degree of Automation

The EU Brussels I Recast system is the most automated, abolishing exequatur and enabling direct enforcement. South Korea and Germany require an enforcement judgment proceeding for third-country judgments. The United States requires a recognition proceeding under state law. The Hague Convention, if widely ratified, would create an intermediate system between the EU's near-automatic recognition and the more burdensome national proceedings.

### Punitive Damages

The treatment of punitive damages reveals a fundamental divergence in legal philosophy. The United States routinely awards punitive damages as a component of its tort system. Korea has introduced limited punitive damages in specific statutes. Germany and most EU states view punitive damages as contrary to their compensatory-only damages philosophy, recognizing only the compensatory components of foreign awards.
