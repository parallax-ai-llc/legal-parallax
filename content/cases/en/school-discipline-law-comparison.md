## Summary

School discipline law governs the rights and procedures applicable when students face punishment, suspension, expulsion, or other disciplinary measures for misconduct. This area of law must balance the educational institution's authority to maintain order and safety against the student's fundamental rights, including the right to education, due process, dignity, and freedom from cruel or degrading punishment. South Korea has undergone a significant transformation from a culture of teacher-directed corporal punishment to formal prohibition, with the Elementary and Secondary Education Act (초·중등교육법) establishing student rights protections and the Seoul Student Rights Ordinance (서울특별시 학생인권조례, 2012) pioneering comprehensive student rights frameworks, though the recent "teacher rights" backlash following a teacher's suicide in 2023 has shifted the discourse toward protecting educators from student and parent misconduct. The United States provides limited federal constitutional protections through the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment, as established in Goss v. Lopez (419 U.S. 565, 1975), while the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA, 20 U.S.C. Section 1400 et seq.) imposes heightened procedural protections for students with disabilities; notably, corporal punishment remains legal in 17 states under Ingraham v. Wright (430 U.S. 651, 1977). Germany prohibits corporal punishment in all settings including schools under Section 1631(2) BGB and the respective state education acts (Schulgesetze), with detailed procedural requirements for suspensions and expulsions governed by administrative law principles of proportionality. The ECHR framework protects students' rights under Article 2 of Protocol No. 1 (right to education), Article 3 (prohibition of torture and degrading punishment), and Article 8 (respect for private life), as developed through landmark cases including Campbell and Cosans v. United Kingdom (1982) and Costello-Roberts v. United Kingdom (1993).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's school discipline framework has evolved significantly over the past two decades, shifting from widespread acceptance of corporal punishment to a comprehensive student rights framework.

### Elementary and Secondary Education Act (초·중등교육법, ESEA)

The ESEA provides the foundational framework:

- **Article 18 (Student Discipline):** Schools may impose disciplinary measures on students who violate school rules, including warnings (훈계), community service (봉사활동), suspension from school (출석정지), transfer to another school (전학), and expulsion (퇴학, for high school students only; compulsory education students cannot be expelled).
- **Article 18-2 (School Disciplinary Committee, 학교폭력대책심의위원회):** Establishes disciplinary committees at the school and regional education office levels to review serious disciplinary matters, particularly those involving school violence.
- **Article 18-4 (Student Rights):** Added in 2007, provides that school principals must respect students' human rights in exercising disciplinary authority.

### Prohibition of Corporal Punishment

**Enforcement Decree of the ESEA, Article 31 (Disciplinary Methods):** Amended in November 2011 to explicitly prohibit corporal punishment (체벌) in all educational settings. The prior provision had permitted "indirect physical punishment" (간접체벌) such as requiring students to stand with arms raised, but this was eliminated. Teachers who administer corporal punishment may face:

- Disciplinary action under the State Public Officials Act (국가공무원법), ranging from reprimand to dismissal.
- Criminal liability for assault (형법 Article 260) or abuse of authority (직권남용).

Prior to the 2011 amendment, a 2010 survey by the National Human Rights Commission found that 45.2% of middle and high school students had experienced corporal punishment within the previous year.

### Student Rights Ordinances

Beginning with the **Seoul Student Rights Ordinance** (서울특별시 학생인권조례, January 26, 2012), several metropolitan and provincial education offices enacted comprehensive student rights protections:

- **Seoul (2012):** Prohibited all forms of corporal punishment; guaranteed rights to education, privacy, freedom of expression (including hair and dress codes), and freedom from discrimination; established a Student Rights Advocate (학생인권옹호관).
- **Gwangju (2011):** First Student Rights Ordinance in Korea.
- **Gyeonggi-do (2010):** Pioneering ordinance that served as a model for subsequent enactments.
- **Jeonbuk (2013), Chungnam (2020), Jeju (2021):** Additional jurisdictions adopting student rights ordinances.

These ordinances went beyond the ESEA in guaranteeing substantive student rights, including restrictions on mandatory haircuts, prohibitions on political discrimination, and protections for LGBTQ students.

### The 2023 Teacher Rights Backlash

On July 18, 2023, a 23-year-old elementary school teacher in Seoul's Seocho district died by suicide, reportedly after sustained harassment from parents of a student she had disciplined. This tragedy triggered massive protests by teachers (the "Black Ribbon" movement) and a legislative backlash against perceived overprotection of student rights:

- **Teacher Rights Protection Act (교원의 지위 향상 및 교육활동 보호를 위한 특별법, amended September 2023):** Strengthened protections against parental interference with educational activities; established the right of teachers to discipline students without fear of malicious complaints; created mandatory investigation procedures for interference with teaching activities.
- **Student Rights Ordinance Rollback Proposals:** Several conservative education superintendents proposed repealing or amending student rights ordinances. In 2024, the Chungnam Student Rights Ordinance was suspended pending review, and debate continued over balancing student rights with teacher authority.

### Due Process for Expulsion and Transfer

The disciplinary process for serious measures follows a structured hierarchy:

1. **School-Level Committee Review:** The School Violence Prevention Committee (학교폭력대책심의위원회) or school disciplinary committee reviews the case, with the student and parent given an opportunity to present their position.
2. **Regional Education Office Review:** For transfers and expulsions, the regional education office (교육지원청) reviews the committee's recommendation.
3. **Administrative Appeal:** Students may appeal disciplinary decisions to the regional or metropolitan/provincial Board of Education.
4. **Administrative Court (행정법원):** Final judicial review is available through the administrative court system. Courts have overturned disciplinary decisions on proportionality and procedural grounds.

| Disciplinary Measure | Authority | Due Process Required | Appeal Route |
|----------------------|-----------|---------------------|-------------|
| Warning/Reprimand | School principal | Notice to student/parent | School complaint |
| Community service | School principal | Committee review | Education office |
| Suspension (1-10 days) | School principal | Committee review; hearing | Education office |
| Transfer | Regional education office | Full hearing; committee recommendation | Board of Education; Administrative Court |
| Expulsion (high school only) | Regional education office | Full hearing; committee recommendation | Board of Education; Administrative Court |

## United States

### Legal Framework

School discipline in the United States operates within a constitutional framework established by the Supreme Court, supplemented by federal statutes (primarily IDEA) and extensive state education codes.

### Constitutional Framework

**Goss v. Lopez (419 U.S. 565, 1975):** The Supreme Court held that students facing suspension of 10 days or less have a property interest in their education and a liberty interest in their reputation, both protected by the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. The minimum due process required for short suspensions is:

- **Oral or written notice** of the charges against the student.
- An explanation of the evidence against the student.
- An **opportunity to present the student's side** of the story.

For longer suspensions or expulsions, the Court indicated that more formal procedures may be required, potentially including the right to counsel, cross-examination of witnesses, and a written decision.

**Ingraham v. Wright (430 U.S. 651, 1977):** The Supreme Court held that corporal punishment in public schools does not constitute "cruel and unusual punishment" under the Eighth Amendment, which applies only to persons convicted of crimes. The Court also held that existing common-law remedies (tort actions for excessive force) provided sufficient due process protection, and no prior hearing was constitutionally required before administering corporal punishment.

As of 2025, **17 states** still permit corporal punishment in public schools: Alabama, Arizona, Arkansas, Colorado (limited), Florida, Georgia, Idaho, Indiana, Kansas, Kentucky, Louisiana, Mississippi, Missouri, North Carolina, Oklahoma, Tennessee, and Texas. Mississippi administers the most, with approximately 22,000 instances per year (OCR data).

**Tinker v. Des Moines (393 U.S. 503, 1969):** Established that students do not "shed their constitutional rights at the schoolhouse gate" and that disciplinary action for expression requires a showing that the expression would "materially and substantially interfere with the requirements of appropriate discipline in the operation of the school."

### IDEA and Students with Disabilities

The **Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA, 20 U.S.C. Section 1400 et seq.)** provides heightened protections for students with disabilities:

- **10-Day Rule (34 CFR Section 300.530):** School personnel may remove a child with a disability to an interim alternative educational setting for up to 10 school days without triggering additional protections.
- **Manifestation Determination (34 CFR Section 300.530(e)):** For removals exceeding 10 cumulative school days, the school must convene a meeting within 10 school days to determine whether the conduct was "caused by, or had a direct and substantial relationship to, the child's disability" or was "the direct result of the LEA's failure to implement the IEP."
- **If Manifestation Found:** The child must be returned to the prior placement (unless the parent agrees otherwise), and the school must conduct a functional behavioral assessment and implement a behavioral intervention plan.
- **Special Circumstances (34 CFR Section 300.530(g)):** Schools may remove students to interim alternative educational settings for up to 45 school days regardless of manifestation determination for offenses involving weapons, illegal drugs, or serious bodily injury.
- **Stay-Put (20 U.S.C. Section 1415(j)):** During the pendency of any due process proceedings, the child remains in the then-current educational placement.

### Zero Tolerance Policies

The **Gun-Free Schools Act (20 U.S.C. Section 7961)** requires states receiving federal education funding to adopt laws requiring expulsion of at least one year for students who bring firearms to school (with case-by-case modification by the chief administering officer). This catalyzed broader zero tolerance policies at the state and district levels covering drugs, weapons, and violence.

Research has consistently documented racially disparate impact: Black students are 3.8 times more likely to receive out-of-school suspensions than white students, and students with disabilities are twice as likely to be suspended (OCR Civil Rights Data Collection, 2021-2022). The Obama administration issued guidance discouraging zero tolerance policies (2014, rescinded 2018), and the Biden administration reissued similar guidance in 2023.

### Restorative Justice Movement

Growing numbers of school districts have adopted restorative justice practices as alternatives to exclusionary discipline:

- **Oakland Unified School District (2009):** Pioneer in implementing restorative justice; reported 87% reduction in suspensions for African American males.
- **Denver Public Schools (2007):** Adopted restorative justice district-wide; reported 47% reduction in out-of-school suspensions.
- **Illinois (SB 100, 2016):** Requires schools to exhaust all non-exclusionary disciplinary measures before resorting to suspension or expulsion.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

School discipline in Germany is governed by the **education acts of the 16 Laender** (Schulgesetze), as education falls within the exclusive competence of the states under Articles 30 and 70 of the Basic Law. Despite this fragmentation, common principles emerge from constitutional requirements and administrative law.

### Prohibition of Corporal Punishment

Corporal punishment is absolutely prohibited in German schools. This prohibition derives from multiple sources:

- **Section 1631(2) BGB:** Enacted in its current form on November 8, 2000, provides that "children have a right to be raised free from violence. Physical punishments, psychological injuries, and other degrading measures are impermissible."
- **State Education Acts:** All 16 Schulgesetze explicitly prohibit corporal punishment. For example, Section 63(2) of the North Rhine-Westphalia School Act (Schulgesetz NRW) prohibits "all forms of corporal punishment and comparable degrading measures."
- **Criminal Law:** A teacher who administers corporal punishment commits Koerperverletzung (bodily harm, Section 223 StGB) and cannot invoke the historical "right of correction" (Zuechtungsrecht), which was formally abolished.

### Disciplinary Measures and Proportionality

German education law distinguishes between **educational measures** (Erziehungsmassnahmen) and **formal disciplinary measures** (Ordnungsmassnahmen):

**Educational Measures (informal):**
- Verbal reprimand (muendliche Ermahnung)
- Written reprimand (schriftliche Ermahnung)
- Additional homework or tasks
- Temporary exclusion from a specific class period
- Conference with parents (Elterngespraech)

These measures do not require formal proceedings and are not subject to administrative appeal.

**Formal Disciplinary Measures (Ordnungsmassnahmen):**
Governed by detailed provisions in each state's education act, these require formal procedure:

| Measure | Example (NRW SchulG Section 53) | Procedure Required |
|---------|----------------------------------|--------------------|
| Written warning (schriftlicher Verweis) | Section 53(3)(1) | Written notice to parents; student hearing |
| Temporary exclusion from class (bis 2 Wochen) | Section 53(3)(2) | School conference decision; student/parent hearing |
| Transfer to parallel class | Section 53(3)(3) | School conference decision; student/parent hearing |
| Threat of dismissal (Androhung der Entlassung) | Section 53(3)(4) | School conference decision; formal hearing; written decision |
| Dismissal from school (Entlassung) | Section 53(3)(5) | School conference decision; formal hearing; regional school authority approval; written decision with legal instruction |
| Permanent exclusion from all public schools (Verweisung) | Section 53(3)(6) | Most severe; ministerial approval; applicable only post-compulsory education |

### Proportionality Principle (Verhaeltnismaessigkeit)

All disciplinary measures must comply with the constitutional principle of proportionality (Verhaeltnismaessigkeitsprinzip), requiring:

1. **Legitimate Purpose (Legitimer Zweck):** The measure must serve a legitimate educational purpose (maintaining order, protecting others, educational development of the student).
2. **Suitability (Geeignetheit):** The measure must be suitable for achieving the purpose.
3. **Necessity (Erforderlichkeit):** No less restrictive measure is available that would equally serve the purpose.
4. **Proportionality in the Narrow Sense (Angemessenheit):** The severity of the measure must be proportionate to the gravity of the misconduct.

The **Federal Administrative Court (Bundesverwaltungsgericht)** has applied these principles rigorously. In **BVerwGE 56, 155 (1978)**, the Court held that the right to education under state constitutions and the Basic Law requires that expulsion be reserved for the most serious cases and that the student's individual circumstances be fully considered.

### Judicial Review

All formal disciplinary measures are administrative decisions (Verwaltungsakte) subject to judicial review by the **Administrative Courts (Verwaltungsgerichte)**. Students (through their parents/guardians if minors) may:

1. File an **objection (Widerspruch)** with the school authority.
2. Appeal to the **Verwaltungsgericht** (first instance administrative court).
3. Seek **provisional relief (einstweiliger Rechtsschutz)** under Section 80(5) VwGO to suspend the measure pending final adjudication.

Courts regularly overturn school disciplinary decisions for procedural violations, disproportionality, or failure to adequately consider alternatives.

### Inclusive Education

Under **SGB IX** and the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (ratified by Germany in 2009), students with disabilities have the right to inclusive education. Disciplinary measures against students with disabilities require enhanced consideration of the disability's relationship to the behavior, similar in principle to the U.S. IDEA framework but implemented through administrative law rather than a specific federal statute.

## ECHR & EU

### European Convention on Human Rights

**Article 2 of Protocol No. 1 (Right to Education):**
"No person shall be denied the right to education." This provision constrains school discipline by requiring that exclusionary measures (suspension, expulsion) be proportionate and not effectively deny the right to education. The right is not absolute, and states may regulate education including through reasonable disciplinary rules, but permanent exclusion without alternative educational provision would violate this article.

**Article 3 (Prohibition of Torture and Degrading Treatment):**

**Campbell and Cosans v. United Kingdom (Application Nos. 7511/76, 7743/76, February 25, 1982):** Two Scottish mothers challenged the use of the tawse (leather strap) as corporal punishment in state schools. The Court held that the threat of corporal punishment constituted "degrading treatment" within the meaning of Article 3, and that the failure to respect the parents' philosophical convictions against corporal punishment violated Article 2 of Protocol No. 1. This landmark decision effectively compelled the UK to abolish corporal punishment in state schools (Education (No. 2) Act 1986).

**Costello-Roberts v. United Kingdom (Application No. 13134/87, March 25, 1993):** A seven-year-old boy at a private school received three "whacks" on his buttocks through his shorts with a rubber-soled gym shoe. The Court found no violation of Article 3, holding that the punishment did not reach the minimum threshold of severity. However, the Court confirmed that the state has a positive obligation to protect children from treatment contrary to Article 3 even in private schools.

**Tyrer v. United Kingdom (Application No. 5856/72, April 25, 1978):** While addressing judicial (not school) corporal punishment in the Isle of Man, the Court's finding that judicial birching constituted degrading punishment established principles applicable to school contexts.

### Article 8 (Right to Respect for Private Life)

School disciplinary records, surveillance in schools, and searches of students' personal belongings engage Article 8. The Court requires that such interferences be "in accordance with the law" and "necessary in a democratic society."

### UN Convention on the Rights of the Child (CRC)

While not an ECHR instrument, the CRC is ratified by all EU member states and informs ECHR interpretation:

- **Article 19:** States shall take all appropriate measures to protect children from all forms of violence, including while in the care of educational institutions.
- **Article 28(2):** States shall ensure that school discipline is administered in a manner consistent with the child's human dignity and in conformity with the CRC.
- **Article 37(a):** No child shall be subjected to cruel, inhuman, or degrading treatment or punishment.

The UN Committee on the Rights of the Child has consistently interpreted Article 19 as requiring complete prohibition of corporal punishment in all settings, including schools (General Comment No. 8, 2006).

### EU Framework

The EU has limited direct competence over education, but several instruments are relevant:

- **EU Charter of Fundamental Rights, Article 14:** Guarantees the right to education, including the right to receive free compulsory education.
- **EU Charter, Article 24 (Rights of the Child):** Children have the right to such protection and care as is necessary for their well-being; in all actions relating to children, the child's best interests must be a primary consideration.
- **Council of Europe Guidelines on Child-Friendly Justice (2010):** Provide detailed guidance on protecting children's rights in disciplinary and judicial proceedings, including the right to be heard, the right to information in age-appropriate language, and the right to legal representation.

## Comparative Analysis

### Corporal Punishment

The most striking divergence is on corporal punishment:

- **Germany** and **South Korea** have achieved complete prohibition, with Germany's 2000 BGB amendment and South Korea's 2011 ESEA amendment representing decisive legislative shifts.
- **The ECHR** framework effectively requires prohibition through Article 3 jurisprudence, and all Council of Europe member states have abolished corporal punishment in schools.
- **The United States** remains an outlier among developed nations, with 17 states still permitting corporal punishment, protected by the Supreme Court's 1977 holding that the Eighth Amendment does not apply to school settings.

### Due Process Standards

Due process protections exist on a spectrum:

- **Germany** provides the most rigorous procedural protections, with formal administrative law procedures, written decisions, and full judicial review for all significant disciplinary measures.
- **The United States** provides constitutional minimum due process (Goss v. Lopez) supplemented by IDEA protections for disabled students, but the level of procedural protection varies enormously by state and can be minimal for short suspensions.
- **South Korea** provides increasingly structured procedural protections through the school violence committee system, with recent reforms strengthening both student and teacher rights.
- **The ECHR** requires proportionality and access to effective remedies but does not prescribe specific procedural requirements.

### Restorative vs. Punitive Approaches

All jurisdictions are increasingly recognizing the limitations of exclusively punitive approaches:

- Germany's emphasis on educational measures (Erziehungsmassnahmen) before formal discipline reflects a longstanding restorative orientation.
- The United States has seen significant growth in restorative justice programs, though adoption remains uneven.
- South Korea's student rights ordinances emphasize counseling and mediation, though the 2023 teacher rights backlash has complicated this trajectory.
