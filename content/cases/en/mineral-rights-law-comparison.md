## Summary

Mineral rights law governs the ownership, extraction, and regulation of subsurface mineral resources including oil, gas, coal, metals, and other geological deposits, addressing the fundamental question of whether mineral wealth belongs to the landowner, the state, or some combination of both, with profound implications for economic development, environmental protection, energy security, and the distribution of natural resource wealth. South Korea's mineral rights regime is governed by the Mining Industry Act (광업법), which establishes state ownership of all subsurface minerals (국유주의) and requires mining rights (광업권) to be obtained through application to and registration with the Ministry of Trade, Industry and Energy (산업통상자원부); the Korea Mine Reclamation Corporation (한국광해광업공단) manages environmental remediation of abandoned mines and promotes sustainable mineral resource development, reflecting the transition from South Korea's historical reliance on domestic coal and metals mining to its current status as a major mineral-importing industrial economy. The United States uniquely among major jurisdictions recognizes private ownership of mineral rights that can be severed from surface ownership, creating the doctrine of the "severed mineral estate" -- a distinctive feature of American property law that has generated vast economic activity, complex legal relationships between surface and mineral owners, and extensive case law on topics including the dominant estate doctrine, implied covenants of mineral leases, pooling and unitization, and the rule of capture; federal minerals on public lands are governed by the Mineral Leasing Act of 1920 (30 U.S.C. Sections 181-287) and the General Mining Law of 1872 (30 U.S.C. Sections 22-54). Germany follows the continental European model of state mineral sovereignty (Berghoheit) through the Bundesberggesetz (BBergG, Federal Mining Act of 1980), which classifies minerals into bergfreie Mineralien (minerals free for mining, including oil, gas, coal, ores, and most economically significant minerals) owned by the state and grundeigene Mineralien (landowner's minerals, including sand, gravel, clay, and certain stones) owned by the surface landowner, with mining rights (Bergbauberechtigungen) granted through a permit and licensing system administered by the Bergbehörden (mining authorities) of the federal states. The EU affects mineral rights primarily through environmental regulation, including the Extractive Waste Directive 2006/21/EC, the Environmental Impact Assessment Directive, and the Critical Raw Materials Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1252), while the ECHR has addressed mineral rights under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 and Article 8, particularly regarding the environmental impacts of mining on nearby residents.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's mining law reflects the country's history as a significant mining nation (historically producing coal, tungsten, gold, and other minerals) and its current reality as a major mineral importer dependent on global supply chains for critical raw materials.

**Mining Industry Act (광업법):** The Act, originally enacted in 1951 and most recently comprehensively amended in 2017, establishes state ownership of all subsurface minerals. Article 2 defines "minerals" broadly to include gold, silver, copper, lead, zinc, iron, tungsten, molybdenum, coal, petroleum, natural gas, uranium, and approximately 60 other mineral species. No person may explore for or extract minerals without obtaining mining rights (광업권) from the Ministry of Trade, Industry and Energy.

### Mining Rights System

The Mining Industry Act establishes a two-tier rights system:

**Exploration Rights (탐사권):** Grants the right to explore for specific minerals within a defined area for a limited period (typically 2-5 years, renewable). Exploration rights do not include the right to extract and sell minerals.

**Mining Rights (채굴권):** Grants the exclusive right to extract specific minerals within a defined mining area. Mining rights are granted for up to 25 years and are renewable. The rights holder must commence mining operations within a specified period and maintain minimum production levels to avoid forfeiture.

### Mine Reclamation

The Korea Mine Reclamation Corporation (한국광해광업공단, MIRECO), established in 2005, manages the environmental remediation of approximately 5,400 abandoned mines in South Korea. Many of these mines were closed during the government-managed coal mine restructuring program of the 1980s-1990s, which reduced the number of operating coal mines from over 400 to fewer than 10. MIRECO's activities include acid mine drainage treatment, soil and water contamination remediation, structural stabilization, and the conversion of abandoned mine sites for tourism and community purposes.

### Critical Minerals Strategy

As a major manufacturer of semiconductors, batteries, and electronics, South Korea has developed a national strategy for securing critical mineral supply chains. The Special Act on the Establishment of a Stable Supply Chain for Core Minerals (핵심광물 안정적 공급망 구축에 관한 특별법), enacted in 2023, establishes a framework for overseas mineral resource development, strategic stockpiling, recycling and urban mining, and international cooperation agreements for critical mineral access.

### Mineral Statistics

South Korea produced approximately 67 million metric tons of minerals in 2024, predominantly construction minerals (sand, gravel, aggregate) with limited metallic ore production. South Korea imports virtually all of its petroleum, natural gas, iron ore, copper, and most other industrial minerals, making it one of the world's largest mineral importers.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States is unique among major jurisdictions in recognizing private ownership of mineral rights that can be severed from surface ownership, creating complex property relationships that are central to the nation's energy and mining industries.

### Severed Mineral Estate

American property law recognizes that the surface and mineral estates can be separately owned, creating two distinct property interests in the same parcel:

**Dominant Estate Doctrine:** When surface and mineral rights are severed, the mineral estate is traditionally considered the "dominant" estate, meaning the mineral owner has an implied right to use so much of the surface as is reasonably necessary for mineral exploration and extraction. The scope of this implied right has been limited by the "accommodation doctrine" (recognized in Texas, West Virginia, and other states), which requires the mineral owner to accommodate existing surface uses where alternative methods of mineral extraction are available.

**Mineral Lease:** Rather than extracting minerals themselves, mineral rights owners typically grant mineral leases to operating companies. Oil and gas leases typically include a primary term (during which the lessee must commence drilling or the lease expires), royalty provisions (typically 12.5-25 percent of production value), bonus payments, delay rental provisions, and implied covenants (including the implied covenant to develop, the implied covenant to protect against drainage, and the implied covenant of reasonable development).

### Federal Mineral Rights

**General Mining Law of 1872 (30 U.S.C. Sections 22-54):** One of the oldest federal land statutes still in effect, the General Mining Law permits U.S. citizens to locate, claim, and patent (acquire ownership of) deposits of "valuable mineral deposits" on federal public domain lands. The law has been the subject of persistent reform proposals due to its lack of royalty requirements for hardrock minerals, environmental concerns, and outdated claim procedures.

**Mineral Leasing Act of 1920 (30 U.S.C. Sections 181-287):** Governs the leasing of oil, gas, coal, phosphate, sodium, potassium, oil shale, and sulfur on federal public domain lands. Federal mineral leases require royalty payments (12.5 percent onshore for oil and gas, increased to 16.67 percent by the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022; 18.75 percent for offshore leases) and compliance with environmental requirements.

### Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act (SMCRA, 1977)

SMCRA (30 U.S.C. Sections 1201-1328) establishes the most comprehensive mine reclamation framework in U.S. law, requiring coal mining operations to obtain permits, post performance bonds, restore mined land to approximate original contour, revegetate, and comply with water quality standards. The Office of Surface Mining Reclamation and Enforcement (OSMRE) administers the Act, while state programs implement most regulatory functions.

### Rule of Capture

The common law rule of capture, applicable to oil and gas in most states, provides that a landowner (or mineral rights holder) owns the oil and gas produced from wells on their property, even if the oil or gas migrated from beneath neighboring land. This rule has been modified by conservation regulations including well spacing requirements, pooling and unitization orders, and production allowables.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's mining law follows the continental European tradition of state mineral sovereignty (Berghoheit), which has roots in medieval regalian rights (Bergregal) first formalized in German-speaking lands during the Middle Ages.

**Bundesberggesetz (BBergG, Federal Mining Act of 1980):** The BBergG replaced the prior patchwork of state mining laws with a unified federal framework. The Act classifies minerals into two categories:

| Category | Ownership | Examples | Regulatory Regime |
|----------|-----------|---------|-------------------|
| Bergfreie Mineralien (free minerals) | State; no landowner ownership | Oil, gas, coal, ores (iron, copper, zinc, lead), salt, potash, barite, fluorspar | Bergbauberechtigung (mining authorization) required |
| Grundeigene Mineralien (landowner's minerals) | Surface landowner | Sand, gravel, clay, certain stones, peat | Landowner may extract; subject to planning law and environmental requirements |

### Mining Authorization System

The BBergG establishes three levels of mining authorization for bergfreie Mineralien:

**Erlaubnis (Exploration Permit, Sections 7-13):** Grants the exclusive right to explore for specified minerals in a defined area. The exploration permit does not include the right to extract minerals commercially.

**Bewilligung (Mining License, Sections 8, 12):** Grants the right to extract specified minerals from a defined area for a limited period. The mining license provides greater legal security than the exploration permit and is required before commercial mining operations commence.

**Bergwerkseigentum (Mining Property, Sections 9, 13-18):** A real property right (grundstücksgleiches Recht) that is registered in the land register (Grundbuch) and may be transferred, mortgaged, and inherited like surface property. Mining property provides the highest level of legal security and is typically granted for major, long-term mining operations.

### Environmental Framework

German mining is subject to comprehensive environmental regulation:

**Mine Operation Plan (Betriebsplan, Sections 51-57 BBergG):** All mining operations require an approved mine operation plan covering the technical, safety, and environmental aspects of mining. The plan must address environmental impacts, including land disturbance, water management, air emissions, noise, and waste disposal. Environmental impact assessment under the UVPG (Umweltverträglichkeitsprüfungsgesetz) is required for major mining projects.

**Reclamation (Wiedernutzbarmachung, Section 4(4) BBergG):** Mining operators are required to restore mined land to a condition suitable for future use. The mine closure plan must address land surface restoration, water management, structural safety, and long-term monitoring.

### Post-Mining Transition

Germany is undergoing a historic transition from coal mining, with the phase-out of hard coal mining completed in December 2018 (closure of the last hard coal mine, Prosper-Haniel in Bottrop) and the phase-out of lignite (brown coal) mining targeted for 2038 (potentially accelerated to 2030 in some states). The Strukturstärkungsgesetz Kohleregionen (Structural Reinforcement Act for Coal Regions, 2020) provides up to 40 billion euros in federal support for the economic transformation of former coal mining regions.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Regulatory Framework

The EU does not harmonize mineral rights ownership or allocation but significantly affects mineral extraction through environmental and resource policy.

**Extractive Waste Directive 2006/21/EC:** Requires member states to ensure that extractive waste (waste from extraction, treatment, and storage of mineral resources) is managed without endangering human health or the environment. Operators must prepare waste management plans, maintain financial guarantees for rehabilitation, and comply with facility classification requirements.

**Critical Raw Materials Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1252):** Adopted on March 18, 2024, this Regulation establishes a framework for ensuring a secure and sustainable supply of critical raw materials for the EU. Key provisions include streamlined permitting for strategic raw material projects (maximum 27 months for extraction permits), domestic production benchmarks (10 percent of EU annual consumption from domestic extraction by 2030), recycling benchmarks (25 percent from recycling by 2030), and diversification requirements (no more than 65 percent of any critical raw material from a single third country).

### EIA and Environmental Directives

The Environmental Impact Assessment Directive 2011/92/EU (as amended by 2014/52/EU) requires member states to conduct environmental impact assessments for mining projects above specified thresholds. The Water Framework Directive 2000/60/EC affects mining through requirements to prevent deterioration of water body status and achieve good water status, with mining operations required to obtain discharge permits and manage mine water in accordance with WFD objectives.

### ECHR and Mining

The ECHR has addressed mining-related issues under several Convention provisions:

**Taşkın and Others v. Turkey (Application No. 46117/99, 2004):** The Court found violations of Article 8 where gold mining activities using cyanide leaching threatened the health and environment of nearby residents, and the domestic authorities failed to implement court decisions ordering the cessation of mining operations.

**Fadeyeva v. Russia (Application No. 55723/00, 2005):** While concerning a steel plant rather than mining specifically, the Court established important principles regarding the state's positive obligation to protect residents from industrial pollution, principles directly applicable to mining operations.

**Tatar v. Romania (Application No. 67021/01, 2009):** The Court addressed the environmental disaster caused by cyanide contamination from a gold mine in Baia Mare, finding a violation of Article 8 based on the authorities' failure to properly assess and mitigate environmental risks.

### EU Energy Transition and Mining

The EU's energy transition and climate policy create complex interactions with mineral rights law. The increased demand for critical minerals (lithium, cobalt, rare earth elements, graphite) for batteries, wind turbines, and solar panels requires expanded mining activity, while environmental protection objectives and community opposition create permitting challenges. The Critical Raw Materials Act attempts to balance these competing objectives through streamlined permitting and environmental sustainability requirements.

## Comparative Analysis

### Ownership Models

The mineral rights ownership question represents the most fundamental divergence across jurisdictions. The United States uniquely recognizes private ownership of mineral rights severable from surface ownership, creating a commodified property right that can be freely bought, sold, and leased. South Korea and Germany (for bergfreie Mineralien) follow the state ownership model, treating subsurface mineral resources as public assets allocated through administrative permits. The EU does not harmonize ownership but increasingly affects mineral extraction through environmental regulation and critical raw materials policy.

### Economic Implications

The ownership models have profound economic implications. The U.S. private mineral rights system enables rapid private investment in mineral development, as mineral owners can negotiate leases directly with operating companies without government allocation decisions. The state ownership systems in South Korea and Germany provide the government with greater control over the pace and location of mineral development but may create bureaucratic delays and reduce private investment incentives.

### Environmental Protection

All four systems require environmental impact assessment and mine reclamation, but the stringency and enforcement differ. Germany's BBergG mine operation plan system and the U.S. SMCRA reclamation framework provide the most detailed regulatory requirements. The EU's Extractive Waste Directive and environmental directives establish minimum standards across member states. South Korea's mine reclamation program, implemented through MIRECO, addresses the legacy of historical mining while current operations are subject to environmental assessment requirements.

### Critical Minerals and Energy Transition

The global energy transition has elevated mineral rights law to a strategic policy concern in all jurisdictions. South Korea's critical minerals legislation, the EU's Critical Raw Materials Act, the U.S. Inflation Reduction Act's domestic mineral incentives, and Germany's post-coal transition all reflect the growing importance of mineral resources for economic security and climate policy.
