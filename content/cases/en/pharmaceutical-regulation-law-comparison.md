## Summary

Pharmaceutical regulation (의약품 규제) -- the legal framework governing the development, approval, manufacturing, marketing, pricing, and post-market surveillance of medicinal products -- is among the most technically complex and consequential areas of healthcare law, directly affecting access to medicines, patient safety, and public health outcomes. South Korea regulates pharmaceuticals through the Pharmaceutical Affairs Act (약사법) as administered by the Ministry of Food and Drug Safety (식품의약품안전처, MFDS), which oversees drug approval through a multi-phase review process, manufacturing quality standards, and post-market safety surveillance; the National Health Insurance Service (국민건강보험공단, NHIS) and the Health Insurance Review and Assessment Service (건강보험심사평가원, HIRA) control drug pricing and reimbursement through the positive list system (선별등재제도) that requires cost-effectiveness analysis for national health insurance coverage. The United States operates the world's largest pharmaceutical regulatory system through the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (FDCA, 21 U.S.C.) as enforced by the Food and Drug Administration (FDA), with drug approval through the New Drug Application (NDA) process, abbreviated pathways for generics (ANDA under the Hatch-Waxman Act), and expedited pathways including accelerated approval, breakthrough therapy, fast track, and priority review; the United States is distinctive for its lack of direct government drug price controls, though the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 introduced Medicare drug price negotiation for the first time. Germany regulates pharmaceuticals through the Arzneimittelgesetz (AMG, Medicinal Products Act) and EU-level legislation, with the Bundesinstitut für Arzneimittel und Medizinprodukte (BfArM) serving as the national competent authority for drug authorization, the Gemeinsamer Bundesausschuss (G-BA, Federal Joint Committee) conducting early benefit assessments (Frühe Nutzenbewertung) under the AMNOG framework to determine reimbursement pricing, and the Pharmaceutical Market Restructuring Act (AMNOG, 2011) establishing a reference-based pricing negotiation system. At the EU level, the European Medicines Agency (EMA) operates the centralized marketing authorization procedure under Regulation (EC) No 726/2004, the EU pharmaceutical legislation reform proposed in 2023 seeks to modernize the regulatory framework, and the European Court of Human Rights has addressed access-to-medicines issues under Articles 2 and 8 of the Convention.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's pharmaceutical regulatory system has evolved into one of the most sophisticated in Asia, with MFDS recognized as an ICH (International Council for Harmonisation) founding regulatory member.

**Pharmaceutical Affairs Act (약사법):** The primary statute governing all aspects of pharmaceutical regulation, from manufacturing licensing through marketing authorization, distribution, advertising, and post-market surveillance.

### Drug Approval Process

MFDS operates a structured approval process:

| Phase | Duration (Approx.) | Key Requirements |
|-------|-------------------|------------------|
| Pre-IND consultation | Optional | Scientific advice on development plan |
| IND approval | 30 working days | Preclinical data; protocol review |
| Clinical trials (Phase I-III) | 3-7 years | KGCP compliance; IRB approval |
| New Drug Application | 120 working days (standard); 100 (priority) | Quality, safety, efficacy data package |
| Post-market (Phase IV) | Ongoing | Re-examination period; pharmacovigilance |

**Priority Review:** MFDS grants expedited review for drugs addressing serious or life-threatening conditions, orphan drugs, and drugs with significant therapeutic advancement. The review period is reduced from 120 to 100 working days.

### Pricing and Reimbursement

**Positive List System (선별등재제도):** Since 2006, South Korea has operated a positive list system where only drugs demonstrating cost-effectiveness are eligible for National Health Insurance reimbursement. The process involves:

1. **HIRA Economic Evaluation:** After MFDS marketing approval, the manufacturer submits a pharmacoeconomic dossier to HIRA, which conducts an independent cost-effectiveness analysis.
2. **Drug Reimbursement Evaluation Committee:** HIRA's committee evaluates clinical benefit, cost-effectiveness, budget impact, and patient need.
3. **Price Negotiation:** If listed, the price is negotiated between NHIS and the manufacturer.
4. **Price Cuts for Generics:** When the first generic enters the market, the original drug's reimbursement price is reduced to 70 percent, and subsequent generic prices are set at a percentage of the original.

### Korea Adverse Event Reporting System (KAERS)

MFDS operates KAERS (한국의약품안전관리원) as the national pharmacovigilance database, collecting adverse drug reaction reports from healthcare professionals, patients, and pharmaceutical companies. Periodic safety update reports (PSURs) are required for all marketed drugs during the re-examination period.

## United States

### Legal Framework

**Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (FDCA, 21 U.S.C.):** The FDCA provides the statutory foundation for FDA regulation of drugs, biologics, and medical devices. Key provisions include the requirement for pre-market approval demonstrating safety and effectiveness (21 U.S.C. Section 355), manufacturing quality standards (Current Good Manufacturing Practice, cGMP), and post-market reporting requirements.

### Drug Approval Pathways

**New Drug Application (NDA):** The standard approval pathway requires substantial evidence of safety and effectiveness from adequate and well-controlled clinical investigations.

**Abbreviated New Drug Application (ANDA):** Under the Hatch-Waxman Act (Drug Price Competition and Patent Term Restoration Act of 1984), generic drug applicants may rely on the safety and effectiveness data of the reference listed drug, demonstrating only bioequivalence.

**Expedited Programs:**

| Pathway | Criteria | Benefit |
|---------|----------|---------|
| Fast Track | Serious condition; potential to address unmet need | Rolling review; frequent FDA meetings |
| Breakthrough Therapy | Serious condition; preliminary evidence of substantial improvement | Intensive FDA guidance; rolling review; organizational commitment |
| Accelerated Approval | Serious condition; surrogate endpoint reasonably likely to predict clinical benefit | Approval on surrogate; confirmatory trials required post-approval |
| Priority Review | Significant improvement in safety or effectiveness | 6-month review (vs. 10-month standard) |

### Drug Pricing

The United States has historically lacked direct government drug price controls, resulting in the highest pharmaceutical prices globally. The Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 introduced the first federal drug price negotiation program:

- **Medicare Drug Price Negotiation:** CMS negotiates maximum fair prices for selected high-expenditure drugs covered under Medicare Part D and Part B. The first 10 drugs for negotiation were selected in 2023, with negotiated prices effective January 2026.
- **Inflation Cap:** Drug manufacturers must pay rebates to Medicare if they raise prices faster than inflation.
- **Out-of-Pocket Cap:** Medicare Part D out-of-pocket costs are capped at $2,000 annually (effective 2025).

### Post-Market Safety

The FDA's post-market surveillance system includes MedWatch (adverse event reporting), Risk Evaluation and Mitigation Strategies (REMS) for drugs with known serious risks, and the Sentinel System (active surveillance using electronic health data from over 100 million patients).

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's pharmaceutical regulation operates within the EU harmonized framework, with distinctive national pricing and reimbursement mechanisms.

**Arzneimittelgesetz (AMG):** The AMG governs manufacturing, marketing authorization, distribution, and pharmacovigilance for medicinal products not subject to the EU centralized procedure. BfArM processes national marketing authorization applications, conducts benefit-risk assessments, and operates pharmacovigilance systems.

### Marketing Authorization Routes

German marketing authorizations may be obtained through:
- **EU Centralized Procedure (EMA):** Mandatory for biotech products, orphan drugs, and certain other categories. Valid in all EU member states.
- **Decentralized Procedure (DCP):** For simultaneous authorization in multiple member states with no existing national authorization.
- **Mutual Recognition Procedure (MRP):** Extension of existing national authorization to other member states.
- **National Procedure:** For products marketed only in Germany.

### AMNOG and Early Benefit Assessment

The Arzneimittelmarktneuordnungsgesetz (AMNOG, Pharmaceutical Market Restructuring Act, 2011) introduced Germany's distinctive drug pricing framework:

**Frühe Nutzenbewertung (Early Benefit Assessment):** Within 3 months of market launch at a free price, the G-BA (Gemeinsamer Bundesausschuss, Federal Joint Committee) assesses the additional therapeutic benefit of the new drug compared to the appropriate comparator therapy (zweckmäßige Vergleichstherapie). The IQWiG (Institut für Qualität und Wirtschaftlichkeit im Gesundheitswesen, Institute for Quality and Efficiency in Healthcare) provides scientific evaluation.

**Benefit Categories:**

| Category | Description | Pricing Consequence |
|----------|------------|-------------------|
| Major additional benefit (erheblicher Zusatznutzen) | Significant improvement; cure or major symptom improvement | Price premium negotiated with GKV-SV |
| Considerable additional benefit (beträchtlicher Zusatznutzen) | Notable improvement in efficacy or safety | Price premium negotiated |
| Minor additional benefit (geringer Zusatznutzen) | Small improvement | Limited price premium |
| Non-quantifiable additional benefit | Benefit acknowledged but not quantifiable | Price negotiated |
| No additional benefit proven | Insufficient evidence | Price set at reference level of comparator |
| Less benefit | Inferior to comparator | Lower than comparator price |

**Price Negotiation:** Following the G-BA assessment, the manufacturer negotiates a reimbursement price with the GKV-Spitzenverband (National Association of Statutory Health Insurance Funds). If negotiations fail, an arbitration board sets the price. The negotiated price applies retroactively from 12 months after market launch.

### Reference Pricing (Festbetragssystem)

For drugs grouped with therapeutic equivalents, the GKV-SV sets reference prices (Festbeträge) that represent the maximum reimbursement amount. Patients pay any difference between the retail price and the reference price out of pocket.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Pharmaceutical Legislation

**Regulation (EC) No 726/2004:** Establishes the EMA and the centralized marketing authorization procedure. The centralized procedure is mandatory for biotechnological products, orphan medicinal products, and drugs for HIV/AIDS, cancer, neurodegenerative disorders, and diabetes.

**Directive 2001/83/EC:** Establishes the EU code relating to medicinal products for human use, covering national and decentralized authorization procedures, labeling, pharmacovigilance, and advertising.

### 2023 Pharmaceutical Legislation Reform

The European Commission proposed a comprehensive reform of EU pharmaceutical legislation in April 2023, including:
- **Streamlined Authorization:** Reduced review timelines; enhanced use of real-world evidence.
- **Revised Exclusivity Periods:** Proposed reduction of regulatory data protection from 8 to 6 years, with additional periods for unmet medical needs, comparative trials, and launch in all member states.
- **Antimicrobial Resistance:** Transferable exclusivity vouchers to incentivize antibiotic development.
- **Environmental Considerations:** Environmental risk assessment as part of the authorization process.
- **Supply Security:** Obligations on marketing authorization holders to ensure continuous supply; early warning systems for shortages.

### EMA Pharmacovigilance

EudraVigilance is the EU system for managing and analyzing information on suspected adverse reactions to medicines. The Pharmacovigilance Risk Assessment Committee (PRAC) is the EMA committee responsible for assessing and monitoring the safety of human medicines.

### European Court of Human Rights

**Hristozov and Others v. Bulgaria (2012):** The Court addressed access to unauthorized medicines for terminally ill patients, finding no violation of Articles 2 or 8 and recognizing states' wide margin of appreciation in pharmaceutical regulation.

**Duarte Agostinho and Others v. Portugal and 32 Other States (2024):** While primarily a climate change case, the Grand Chamber's consideration of health impacts under Article 8 has broader implications for pharmaceutical regulation, particularly regarding environmental health effects of pharmaceutical manufacturing.

The ECHR's Article 2 (right to life) jurisprudence imposes positive obligations on states to ensure access to essential medicines within public healthcare systems, though the Court grants a wide margin of appreciation in resource allocation decisions.

## Comparative Analysis

### Drug Pricing

The most stark divergence concerns drug pricing. The US lacks systematic price controls (though the IRA introduced limited Medicare negotiation). Germany's AMNOG system links price to demonstrated therapeutic benefit. South Korea's positive list requires cost-effectiveness for reimbursement. The EU has no harmonized pricing framework, leaving significant discretion to member states. These differences create substantial price disparities, with US prices often 2-5 times higher than EU or Korean prices for the same products.

### Generic Competition

The Hatch-Waxman framework enables early generic entry in the US through the ANDA pathway and Paragraph IV patent challenges. The EU's 8+2+1 system (8 years data protection, 2 years market protection, 1 year for new indications) delays generic entry longer than the US system. South Korea's patent linkage system (adopted 2015) mirrors certain Hatch-Waxman features. The proposed EU reform would reduce data protection periods, potentially accelerating generic access.

### Regulatory Convergence

The ICH harmonization process has significantly aligned technical requirements across the US (FDA), EU (EMA), Japan (PMDA), and participating jurisdictions including South Korea (MFDS). ICH guidelines on GCP (E6), clinical trial design (E8-E9), and safety reporting (E2B) provide a common foundation, though approval timelines, pricing, and reimbursement remain jurisdiction-specific.

### Access and Equity

The tension between innovation incentives (strong intellectual property, premium pricing) and access to medicines (affordable generics, universal coverage) manifests differently across jurisdictions. South Korea's universal NHIS system provides broad coverage through price negotiation. Germany's statutory health insurance covers approximately 90 percent of the population. The US's fragmented insurance system creates significant access disparities.
