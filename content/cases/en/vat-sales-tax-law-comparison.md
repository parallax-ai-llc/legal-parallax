## Summary

Value-added tax (VAT) and sales tax -- indirect taxes levied on the consumption of goods and services -- constitute the most widespread form of consumption taxation globally, with over 170 countries operating a VAT or goods and services tax (GST) system, the notable exception being the United States, which relies instead on state-level retail sales taxes. South Korea imposes a value-added tax (부가가치세) at a standard rate of 10 percent under the Value-Added Tax Act (부가가치세법), enacted in 1976 as part of a comprehensive tax reform that replaced multiple indirect taxes with a single-rate VAT; the system was modernized in 2013 with a complete rewrite of the Act and further amended in 2023 to extend digital platform obligations. The United States does not impose a federal VAT or national sales tax; instead, 45 states and the District of Columbia levy state sales taxes at rates ranging from 2.9 percent (Colorado) to 7.25 percent (California), often supplemented by local taxes, creating a patchwork of over 13,000 distinct sales tax jurisdictions following the Supreme Court's landmark decision in South Dakota v. Wayfair, Inc. (2018) permitting economic nexus-based taxation. Germany levies VAT (Umsatzsteuer or Mehrwertsteuer) at a standard rate of 19 percent and a reduced rate of 7 percent under the Umsatzsteuergesetz (UStG), harmonized with the EU VAT Directive (2006/112/EC) which requires a minimum standard rate of 15 percent and permits up to two reduced rates of no less than 5 percent, supplemented by the 2022 VAT in the Digital Age (ViDA) reform proposal aimed at modernizing VAT compliance through mandatory electronic invoicing and real-time digital reporting across all member states.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's VAT system was introduced on July 1, 1977, replacing eight existing indirect taxes with a single-rate VAT. The current legal basis is the Value-Added Tax Act (부가가치세법, Act No. 19933), comprehensively rewritten in 2013 (Act No. 11873) to modernize and simplify the framework. The Act is supplemented by the Enforcement Decree of the Value-Added Tax Act (부가가치세법 시행령) and administered by the National Tax Service (국세청).

### Structure and Rate

South Korea maintains a single standard rate of 10 percent, one of the lowest VAT rates among OECD countries. There are no reduced rates; instead, certain goods and services are zero-rated (영세율) or exempt (면세). Zero-rated supplies include exports and international transport services, while exempt supplies include unprocessed foodstuffs, medical and health services, educational services, financial and insurance services, and public housing.

### Digital Economy Provisions

In 2015, South Korea became one of the first Asian countries to require foreign digital service providers to register for and collect VAT on services supplied to Korean consumers. The scope was expanded in subsequent amendments:

| Year | Reform |
|------|--------|
| 2015 | Foreign providers of electronic services (게임, 음성, 영상 등) required to register |
| 2019 | Scope expanded to include cloud computing and advertising services |
| 2023 | Platform obligations introduced; digital platforms required to collect and remit VAT on behalf of foreign sellers |

### Simplified Taxation Scheme

Businesses with annual turnover below 48 million KRW (approximately $40,000) may opt for the simplified taxation scheme (간이과세), which applies a lower effective VAT burden through industry-specific value-added rates ranging from 5 to 30 percent of turnover. The threshold was raised from 30 million KRW to 48 million KRW in 2021 and further to 80 million KRW for certain sectors in 2024.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States is the only OECD member that does not impose a national VAT or GST. Instead, consumption taxation occurs at the state and local level through retail sales taxes. There is no federal constitutional prohibition on a national consumption tax, but political considerations have prevented its adoption. Sales tax is governed by individual state statutes, with no federal coordination mechanism beyond the voluntary Streamlined Sales and Use Tax Agreement (SSUTA).

### State Sales Tax Rates

| State | State Rate | Max Combined Rate (with local) |
|-------|-----------|-------------------------------|
| California | 7.25% | 10.25% |
| Tennessee | 7.0% | 9.75% |
| Louisiana | 4.45% | 11.45% |
| New York | 4.0% | 8.875% |
| Texas | 6.25% | 8.25% |
| Oregon | 0% (no sales tax) | 0% |
| Delaware | 0% (no sales tax) | 0% |

Five states -- Alaska, Delaware, Montana, New Hampshire, and Oregon (the "NOMAD" states) -- do not impose a state sales tax, though some Alaska municipalities levy local sales taxes.

### Wayfair Decision and Economic Nexus

**South Dakota v. Wayfair, Inc., 585 U.S. 162 (2018):** The Supreme Court overruled the physical presence requirement for state sales tax collection established in Quill Corp. v. North Dakota, 504 U.S. 298 (1992) and National Bellas Hess, Inc. v. Department of Revenue of Illinois, 386 U.S. 753 (1967). The Court held that South Dakota's economic nexus statute -- requiring sales tax collection by sellers with either $100,000 in sales or 200 transactions in the state -- satisfied the Commerce Clause requirements. By 2020, all states with a sales tax had adopted economic nexus provisions.

### Use Tax

To complement sales tax, all sales tax states impose a use tax on the use, storage, or consumption of tangible personal property purchased from out-of-state sellers without payment of sales tax. Use tax is technically owed by the consumer, but compliance rates are notoriously low absent seller collection obligations -- a central issue resolved by the Wayfair decision.

### Marketplace Facilitator Laws

Following Wayfair, states rapidly enacted marketplace facilitator laws requiring online marketplaces (Amazon, eBay, Etsy) to collect and remit sales tax on behalf of third-party sellers. As of 2026, all states with a sales tax have adopted marketplace facilitator provisions, dramatically increasing remote sales tax collection.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's VAT (Umsatzsteuer) is governed by the Umsatzsteuergesetz (UStG), harmonized with the EU VAT Directive (2006/112/EC). The UStG implements EU requirements while exercising permitted member state options. VAT is administered by the state tax offices (Finanzämter) under federal coordination.

### Rate Structure

| Category | Rate | Examples |
|----------|------|----------|
| Standard rate | 19% | Most goods and services |
| Reduced rate | 7% | Food (except restaurant meals at standard rate since 2024), books, newspapers, local public transport, cultural events |
| Zero rate | 0% | Intra-Community supplies; exports; certain photovoltaic installations (since 2023) |

Germany temporarily reduced the standard rate to 16 percent and the reduced rate to 5 percent from July 1 to December 31, 2020, as a COVID-19 economic stimulus measure. A reduced rate of 7 percent was applied to restaurant and catering services from July 2020 through December 2023, reverting to the standard 19 percent rate on January 1, 2024.

### Small Business Exemption (Kleinunternehmerregelung)

Under Section 19 UStG, businesses with annual turnover not exceeding EUR 22,000 in the prior year and not expected to exceed EUR 50,000 in the current year may elect the Kleinunternehmerregelung. Such businesses do not charge VAT on their supplies but also cannot claim input tax credits (Vorsteuerabzug). The EU has proposed harmonizing small business thresholds at EUR 85,000 under the SME Directive proposal.

### Electronic Invoicing

Germany has been progressively implementing mandatory electronic invoicing (E-Rechnung) for B2B transactions. Starting January 1, 2025, all businesses must be capable of receiving electronic invoices in structured formats (XRechnung, ZUGFeRD). Full mandatory issuance of electronic invoices is being phased in through 2027, aligning with the EU's ViDA proposal.

### Reverse Charge Mechanism

Germany extensively uses the reverse charge mechanism (Umkehr der Steuerschuldnerschaft) under Section 13b UStG, shifting the VAT liability from the supplier to the recipient in specified transactions, including construction services, scrap metal sales, and certain cross-border services.

## ECHR & EU

### EU VAT Directive (2006/112/EC)

The EU VAT Directive provides the comprehensive legal framework for VAT across all 27 member states. Key provisions include:

**Rate Requirements:** A minimum standard rate of 15 percent (Article 97); member states may apply up to two reduced rates of not less than 5 percent to specified categories of goods and services (Annex III). The 2022 reform (Directive 2022/542) significantly expanded the list of goods and services eligible for reduced rates and introduced the possibility of a zero rate and a "super-reduced" rate below 5 percent for specific essentials.

**Exemptions:** Mandatory exemptions include financial and insurance services, educational services, medical care, and postal services (Articles 132-137). Member states may apply exemptions for small enterprises below a national threshold.

**One-Stop Shop (OSS):** Effective July 1, 2021, the expanded OSS system allows businesses making cross-border B2C supplies within the EU to register in a single member state and file a single VAT return covering all EU sales, significantly reducing administrative burden.

### VAT in the Digital Age (ViDA)

The European Commission proposed the VAT in the Digital Age (ViDA) package on December 8, 2022, with political agreement reached in November 2024 after extensive negotiation. The three pillars of ViDA are:

| Pillar | Measure | Timeline |
|--------|---------|----------|
| Digital Reporting Requirements | Mandatory structured electronic invoicing for intra-Community B2B transactions; real-time digital reporting | July 1, 2030 |
| Platform Economy | Deemed supplier rules for short-term accommodation and passenger transport platforms | July 1, 2027 |
| Single VAT Registration | Extension of OSS to cover all B2C supplies and domestic reverse charge | July 1, 2027 |

### VAT Gap and Fraud

The EU VAT gap -- the difference between expected VAT revenue and actual collections -- was estimated at EUR 61 billion in 2022 (approximately 5.3 percent of total VAT liability). Missing trader intra-Community (MTIC) fraud, commonly known as carousel fraud, remains a significant challenge, with estimated annual losses of EUR 40-60 billion. The EU has implemented various anti-fraud measures including the Quick Fixes (Directive 2018/1910), mandatory reverse charge for certain domestic supplies, and the ViDA reporting requirements.

### CJEU VAT Jurisprudence

The Court of Justice of the European Union has developed extensive VAT case law:

- **Halifax plc (C-255/02, 2006):** Established the principle of abuse of rights in VAT, allowing member states to deny VAT advantages obtained through artificial arrangements.
- **Kittel and Recolta (Joined Cases C-439/04 and C-440/04, 2006):** Held that input VAT deduction may be denied where the taxable person knew or should have known that the transaction was connected to VAT fraud.

## Comparative Analysis

### Structural Differences: VAT vs. Sales Tax

The fundamental structural difference between the VAT (employed by South Korea, Germany, and all EU member states) and the retail sales tax (employed by U.S. states) concerns the stage of collection. VAT is a multi-stage tax collected at each point of production and distribution, with businesses claiming input tax credits for VAT paid on purchases. Sales tax is a single-stage tax collected only at the final retail sale. The VAT's self-policing mechanism -- where each business in the chain has an incentive to ensure its suppliers have properly charged VAT -- provides significantly higher compliance rates compared to the single-stage sales tax.

### Rate Levels and Revenue Efficiency

VAT systems generally achieve higher revenue as a share of GDP due to broader bases and higher compliance. South Korea's 10 percent rate generates approximately 4.2 percent of GDP in revenue. Germany's 19 percent rate generates approximately 7 percent of GDP. By contrast, U.S. state and local sales taxes generate approximately 2.5 percent of GDP combined, reflecting both lower rates and narrower bases (many states exempt services and essential goods).

### Digital Economy Adaptation

All jurisdictions have struggled to adapt consumption taxation to the digital economy. South Korea and the EU moved first to require foreign digital service providers to register and collect VAT. The U.S. addressed the issue indirectly through the Wayfair decision enabling economic nexus for sales tax. The EU's ViDA proposal represents the most ambitious attempt at systematic digital transformation of consumption tax administration.

### Distributional Concerns

The regressive nature of consumption taxation -- falling disproportionately on lower-income households who spend a larger share of income -- is addressed differently. Germany and the EU use reduced rates for essentials (food, medicine). South Korea exempts basic foodstuffs and medical services. U.S. states exempt food and medicine to varying degrees. None of these measures fully eliminate regressivity.
