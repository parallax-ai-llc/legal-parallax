## Summary

Religious courts and parallel legal systems -- judicial or quasi-judicial bodies that apply religious law (canon law, sharia, halakha, or other religious legal traditions) to disputes among adherents -- present complex questions at the intersection of religious freedom, rule of law, and access to justice in virtually every legal system. South Korea operates a strictly secular judiciary with no formal recognition of religious courts, though religious organizations maintain internal dispute resolution bodies whose decisions the Korean Supreme Court (대법원) has addressed in several notable cases regarding church property disputes and clergy discipline under the principle of religious autonomy (종교단체 자율권). The United States does not formally incorporate religious courts into its judicial system but recognizes religious arbitration agreements under the Federal Arbitration Act (9 U.S.C. Section 1 et seq.) and has developed extensive jurisprudence on the "church autonomy" and "ministerial exception" doctrines through cases including Our Lady of Guadalupe School v. Morrissey-Berru (2020) and the "neutral principles" approach to church property disputes from Jones v. Wolf (1979). Germany, through its cooperative church-state model under Articles 140 GG and Weimar Constitution Article 137(3), grants religious communities with public law corporation status substantial self-governance including the operation of church courts (Kirchengerichte) for internal ecclesiastical matters and a distinctive "Third Way" (Dritter Weg) labor relations system exempt from general labor law, though the Federal Constitutional Court and the Federal Labor Court have increasingly imposed fundamental rights constraints on church employment decisions. The European Court of Human Rights has addressed the recognition of religious legal systems primarily through Article 9 (religious freedom) and Article 6 (right to a fair trial), establishing in cases such as Molla Sali v. Greece (2018) that compulsory application of religious law without the individual's consent violates the Convention, while recognizing the autonomy of religious communities in their internal governance under Article 9.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's Constitution (대한민국 헌법) establishes a strictly secular legal system. Article 20(2) mandates that "church and state shall be separated" (종교와 정치는 분리된다), and Article 27(1) guarantees all citizens the right to be tried by judges qualified under the Constitution and law in courts established by the Constitution and law. No provision exists for religious courts within the formal judicial structure.

However, religious organizations operate extensive internal dispute resolution mechanisms:

**Protestant Churches (개신교):** Korean Protestant denominations, particularly the large Presbyterian bodies (대한예수교장로회), maintain internal judicial systems (치리회) with hierarchical courts from the local session (당회) through presbytery (노회) to the General Assembly Judicial Commission (총회재판국). These bodies adjudicate disputes over clergy discipline, doctrinal matters, and church governance.

**Buddhist Temples (사찰):** The Jogye Order (대한불교조계종), Korea's largest Buddhist denomination, operates an internal arbitration system for temple disputes, administered under the Jogye Order Constitution (종헌) and bylaws (종법).

**Catholic Church:** The Korean Catholic Church applies the Code of Canon Law (1983) through diocesan tribunals, primarily for marriage annulment proceedings.

### Supreme Court Jurisprudence

**Supreme Court Decision 2006Da23312 (2009) -- Church Property Dispute:** The Korean Supreme Court established that civil courts have jurisdiction over church property disputes and should apply "neutral principles of law" rather than deferring to religious bodies on property ownership questions. The Court held that where a local congregation separates from a denomination, property ownership is determined by the articles of incorporation, property registration, and applicable civil law principles.

**Supreme Court Decision 2011Da48764 (2013) -- Clergy Discipline:** The Court recognized the principle of religious autonomy, holding that civil courts should generally refrain from reviewing internal disciplinary decisions of religious organizations where the dispute is purely ecclesiastical. However, the Court reserved the right to intervene where fundamental civil rights are at stake or where the religious body's own procedures were not followed.

**Supreme Court Decision 2014Da11888 (2017) -- Temple Governance:** Addressing a dispute within the Jogye Order over head monk appointments, the Court held that internal governance decisions of religious organizations are not justiciable in civil courts unless they involve property rights or fundamental rights violations.

### Penalties and Enforcement

Religious tribunal decisions in South Korea have no legal enforcement mechanism in the civil courts. Members who refuse to comply with internal religious disciplinary decisions (such as excommunication or removal from office) may face only internal religious consequences. If a dispute involves civil rights, property, or contractual obligations, the aggrieved party must seek relief through the regular court system.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has no formal religious court system but has developed two significant doctrines governing the interaction between civil courts and religious dispute resolution.

**Church Autonomy Doctrine:** Rooted in Watson v. Jones, 80 U.S. 679 (1872), this doctrine holds that civil courts must defer to the highest religious authority within a hierarchical church on matters of religious doctrine, discipline, and governance. The doctrine was constitutionalized in Serbian Eastern Orthodox Diocese v. Milivojevich, 426 U.S. 696 (1976), where the Supreme Court held that the First Amendment prohibits civil courts from resolving controversies over religious doctrine, practice, or ecclesiastical governance.

**Neutral Principles Approach:** In Jones v. Wolf, 443 U.S. 595 (1979), the Supreme Court approved an alternative approach permitting courts to resolve church property disputes through "neutral principles of law" -- examining deeds, state statutes, corporate charters, and church constitutions without interpreting religious doctrine. States may choose between the deference and neutral principles approaches.

**Ministerial Exception:** In Hosanna-Tabor Evangelical Lutheran Church and School v. EEOC, 565 U.S. 171 (2012), the Supreme Court unanimously recognized a "ministerial exception" to employment discrimination laws, holding that the First Amendment bars suits brought on behalf of ministers against their churches. In Our Lady of Guadalupe School v. Morrissey-Berru, 591 U.S. 732 (2020), the Court expanded the exception, holding that teachers at Catholic schools who incorporated religion into their classes qualified as "ministers" even without formal religious titles.

### Religious Arbitration

Religious arbitration is legally enforceable under the Federal Arbitration Act (FAA, 9 U.S.C. Section 1 et seq.) when parties voluntarily agree to submit disputes to a religious tribunal:

**Beth Din (Jewish):** Jewish religious courts (batei din) regularly arbitrate commercial and family law disputes under halakha (Jewish law). Decisions are enforceable as arbitration awards under the FAA. In Meshel v. Ohev Sholom Talmud Torah, the D.C. Circuit confirmed that beth din awards are enforceable under standard arbitration law principles.

**Islamic Arbitration:** Muslim arbitration tribunals have operated in various U.S. communities, resolving family and commercial disputes under Islamic jurisprudence (fiqh). State courts have generally enforced such awards when they meet FAA requirements and do not violate public policy.

**Anti-Sharia Legislation:** Several states have enacted legislation restricting the application of "foreign law" in state courts, often motivated by opposition to Islamic law. Oklahoma's "Save Our State" amendment was struck down in Awad v. Ziriax, 670 F.3d 1111 (10th Cir. 2012), as violating the Establishment Clause. Other states have enacted more narrowly tailored "foreign law" provisions.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's cooperative church-state model grants religious communities, particularly those with public law corporation status, extensive self-governance authority, including the operation of internal court systems.

**Article 140 GG / Weimar Constitution Article 137(3):** "Every religious society shall regulate and administer its affairs independently within the limits of the law that applies to all. It shall confer its offices without the participation of the State or the civil community."

### Church Courts (Kirchengerichte)

Both the Evangelical Church in Germany (Evangelische Kirche in Deutschland, EKD) and the Catholic Church operate comprehensive internal court systems:

**Catholic Church:** Diocesan tribunals (Diözesangerichte) apply the Code of Canon Law (Codex Iuris Canonici, 1983) to marriage annulment cases, clergy discipline, and administrative disputes. The Metropolitan Tribunal serves as an appellate body, with the Roman Rota available as a final appellate court. Canon law marriage annulments have civil legal significance in Germany to the extent that they may be recognized as relevant evidence in civil divorce proceedings, though they do not substitute for civil dissolution of marriage.

**Evangelical Church:** The EKD operates a system of church administrative courts (Kirchliche Verwaltungsgerichte) and disciplinary courts (Disziplinarkammern). The Church Court of the EKD (Kirchengerichtshof der EKD) serves as the highest appellate body. These courts apply church law (Kirchenrecht) to disputes involving church employment, governance, and discipline.

### "Third Way" Labor Relations (Dritter Weg)

Germany's most significant parallel legal system involves church employment law. Religious communities with public law corporation status are exempt from the general collective bargaining framework (Tarifvertragsgesetz). Instead, the major churches operate a "Third Way" (Dritter Weg) system in which working conditions are determined by joint commissions (Arbeitsrechtliche Kommissionen) composed of employer and employee representatives, rather than through collective bargaining agreements or unilateral employer decision. Church employees historically could not strike, and churches imposed loyalty obligations (Loyalitätsobliegenheiten) requiring adherence to church moral teaching in private life.

### Federal Constitutional Court and Federal Labor Court Jurisprudence

**BVerfGE 137, 273 (2014) -- Chefarzt-Entscheidung (Chief Physician Decision):** The Federal Constitutional Court addressed the dismissal of a Catholic hospital chief physician who remarried after divorce without obtaining a canonical annulment. The Court held that while church autonomy under Article 137(3) Weimar Constitution protects the church's right to impose loyalty obligations, the civil courts must balance church autonomy against the employee's fundamental rights, particularly the right to private life (Article 2(1) GG) and the prohibition of discrimination (Article 3 GG). The Court required a proportionality analysis weighing the nature of the employment, the proximity of the employee's role to the church's religious mission, and the severity of the consequences.

**BAG (Federal Labor Court), Decision of October 25, 2018 (8 AZR 501/14) -- Egenberger:** Following the CJEU's preliminary ruling in Case C-414/16 (Egenberger v. Evangelisches Werk), the Federal Labor Court held that a church's requirement that a job applicant be a member of the relevant denomination is subject to judicial review to determine whether it constitutes a genuine, legitimate, and justified occupational requirement. The decision significantly limited the churches' previously broad discretion in setting employment requirements.

| Issue | Legal Consequence |
|-------|-------------------|
| Non-compliance with church court decision (internal) | Ecclesiastical sanctions (removal from office, excommunication) |
| Challenge to church employment decision in civil court | Civil courts apply proportionality analysis |
| Violation of "Third Way" labor standards | Church internal enforcement; increasing civil court review |

## ECHR / EU

### ECHR Article 9 and Religious Autonomy

The European Court of Human Rights has developed a nuanced jurisprudence balancing religious community autonomy against individual rights.

**Hasan and Chaush v. Bulgaria (Application No. 30985/96, Grand Chamber, October 26, 2000):** The Grand Chamber held that state interference in the internal leadership disputes of a religious community violated Article 9. The Court established that "the autonomous existence of religious communities is indispensable for pluralism in a democratic society and is thus an issue at the very heart of the protection which Article 9 affords."

**Fernández Martínez v. Spain (Application No. 56030/07, Grand Chamber, June 12, 2014):** The Grand Chamber held that Spain's failure to renew the contract of a Catholic religion teacher who publicly advocated against church doctrine did not violate Article 8 (private life). The Court recognized the autonomy of religious communities in selecting their religious education representatives, applying a proportionality analysis that weighed the individual's rights against the church's institutional autonomy.

### Compulsory Application of Religious Law

**Molla Sali v. Greece (Application No. 20452/14, Grand Chamber, December 19, 2018):** The Grand Chamber held that Greece's compulsory application of Islamic inheritance law to members of the Muslim minority in Western Thrace, pursuant to the Treaty of Lausanne (1923), violated Article 14 (prohibition of discrimination) read with Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (protection of property). The Court emphasized that the compulsory application of religious law without the individual's explicit consent is incompatible with the Convention. Following this judgment, Greece enacted Law 4511/2018 making application of Islamic law in family and inheritance matters optional rather than compulsory.

### Church Employment and EU Anti-Discrimination Law

**Case C-414/16, Egenberger v. Evangelisches Werk (CJEU, April 17, 2018):** The Court of Justice held that a church employer's requirement that job applicants belong to the employer's denomination must be subject to effective judicial review by national courts under Directive 2000/78/EC (Equal Treatment in Employment). The church must demonstrate that the religious requirement is a genuine, legitimate, and justified occupational requirement given the nature of the activities concerned. This decision directly challenged the breadth of church employment autonomy as practiced in Germany.

**Case C-68/17, IR v. JQ (CJEU, September 11, 2018):** The CJEU ruled that a Catholic hospital's dismissal of a doctor for remarrying after divorce could constitute discrimination on grounds of religion under Directive 2000/78/EC if the requirement of adherence to Catholic doctrine on marriage was not applied equally to employees of other faiths or no faith in comparable positions.

**Obst v. Germany (Application No. 425/03, ECtHR, September 23, 2010) and Schüth v. Germany (Application No. 1620/03, ECtHR, September 23, 2010):** In these companion cases, the Court found no violation of Article 8 in the dismissal of a Mormon public relations director for adultery (Obst) but found a violation in the dismissal of a Catholic parish organist for starting a new relationship after separation (Schüth). The divergent outcomes reflected the proportionality analysis: the director's proximity to the religious mission justified the church's loyalty requirement, while the organist's role was primarily musical.

## Comparative Analysis

### Scope of Religious Judicial Authority

The four jurisdictions range from no recognition (South Korea) to structured cooperation (Germany). South Korea maintains the strictest separation, with civil courts exercising full jurisdiction and recognizing religious tribunal decisions only as internal organizational matters. The United States occupies a middle position, granting religious arbitration awards legal enforceability under the FAA while prohibiting civil courts from resolving religious doctrine disputes. Germany goes furthest in granting religious communities quasi-governmental judicial authority, particularly through the church court system and the "Third Way" labor relations framework.

### Employment Law

The most active area of development concerns religious organizations' employment decisions. All jurisdictions recognize some form of autonomy for religious employers, but the trend across all four is toward increasing judicial review. The CJEU's Egenberger decision, the German Federal Constitutional Court's Chefarzt decision, and the ECHR's proportionality-based approach in Obst and Schüth all reflect a convergence toward balancing religious autonomy against individual rights rather than granting blanket deference. The U.S. ministerial exception, as expanded in Our Lady of Guadalupe, represents a partial counter-trend.

### Consent and Voluntariness

The ECHR's Molla Sali decision established a critical principle: the compulsory application of religious law to individuals without their explicit consent violates the Convention. This principle resonates across all jurisdictions. South Korea applies religious law only internally; the United States requires a voluntary arbitration agreement; Germany's church law applies only to members (who may exit through Kirchenaustritt). The emerging consensus is that religious legal systems may operate as parallel frameworks only on the basis of genuine, informed consent.
