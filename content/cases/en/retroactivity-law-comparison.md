## Summary

The prohibition on retroactive legislation -- the principle that laws should not apply to conduct or transactions that occurred before their enactment -- constitutes a fundamental element of the rule of law across all major legal systems, though the scope, exceptions, and doctrinal sophistication of the prohibition vary dramatically across jurisdictions. South Korea's Constitutional Court (헌법재판소) has developed a nuanced retroactivity doctrine under the Rechtsstaatsprinzip (법치국가원리) derived from Articles 12 and 13 of the Constitution, distinguishing between "genuine retroactivity" (진정소급입법), which applies new law to facts that were completed before the law's enactment and is presumptively unconstitutional, and "non-genuine retroactivity" (부진정소급입법), which applies new law to ongoing situations that began before but continue after the law's enactment and is permissible if proportionate; the Court has drawn this distinction directly from German doctrine and has applied it extensively in cases involving tax legislation, pension reform, and criminal sentencing. The United States prohibits ex post facto laws through Article I, Sections 9 and 10 of the Constitution, which apply to federal and state governments respectively, but the Supreme Court has limited the Ex Post Facto Clause to criminal legislation since Calder v. Bull (1798), leaving retroactive civil legislation subject only to Due Process Clause scrutiny under a deferential rational basis standard; the Court has developed additional protective doctrines through the Contracts Clause (Article I, Section 10), the Takings Clause (Fifth Amendment), and the presumption against retroactivity in statutory interpretation established in Landgraf v. USI Film Products (1994). Germany provides the most doctrinally elaborate framework through the Federal Constitutional Court's Rückwirkungsverbot (prohibition of retroactivity), derived from the Rechtsstaatsprinzip (Article 20(3) GG) and the principles of legal certainty (Rechtssicherheit) and protection of legitimate expectations (Vertrauensschutz), distinguishing between echte Rückwirkung (genuine retroactivity, affecting completed past situations) and unechte Rückwirkung (non-genuine retroactivity, affecting ongoing situations), with the former presumptively unconstitutional and the latter subject to proportionality review; this framework has been extensively developed through tax law, social security, and criminal law jurisprudence. The European framework addresses retroactivity through ECHR Article 7 (no punishment without law, nulla poena sine lege), which the European Court of Human Rights has interpreted to prohibit retrospective criminal offenses and heavier retrospective penalties while permitting gradual judicial development of criminal law, and through general principles of EU law as confirmed by the CJEU in cases such as Racke (C-162/96, 1998) and Gemeente Steenbergen (C-138/12, 2013).

## South Korea

### Constitutional Framework

**Article 12 Constitution:** The comprehensive due process provision establishes that "no citizen shall be prosecuted for an act which does not constitute a crime under Act at the time it was committed, nor shall he be placed in double jeopardy."

**Article 13 Constitution:**
1. "No citizen shall be prosecuted for an act which does not constitute a crime under Act in force at the time of commission; nor shall any person be subject to punishment more than once for the same offense (모든 국민은 행위시의 법률에 의하여 범죄를 구성하지 아니하는 행위로 소추되지 아니하며, 동일한 범죄에 대하여 거듭 처벌받지 아니한다)."
2. "No citizen shall be subject to retroactive legislation that restricts political rights or divests rights of property (모든 국민은 소급입법에 의하여 참정권의 제한을 받거나 재산권을 박탈당하지 아니한다)."

### The Genuine/Non-Genuine Distinction

The Constitutional Court adopted the German distinction between genuine and non-genuine retroactivity:

**Genuine Retroactivity (진정소급입법):** Applies to legal relationships or facts that were already concluded before the new law took effect. This is presumptively unconstitutional and can only be justified in exceptional circumstances where:
- The prior legal situation was unclear or confused
- The affected persons could not have formed legitimate expectations
- The public interest in the retroactive regulation is overwhelming
- The harm to individual rights is minimal

**Non-Genuine Retroactivity (부진정소급입법):** Applies to legal relationships that originated in the past but had not yet concluded when the new law took effect. This is permissible in principle but subject to proportionality review, particularly the protection of legitimate expectations (신뢰보호의 원칙).

### Landmark Decisions

**Constitutional Court Decision 96Hun-Ka2 (1998) (Tax Retroactivity):** The Court struck down a retroactive tax provision that applied an increased tax rate to income earned before the law's enactment. The Court held that this constituted genuine retroactivity and violated the principles of legal certainty and protection of legitimate expectations. The Court established that retroactive tax legislation receives particularly strict scrutiny because taxpayers arrange their affairs based on existing tax law.

**Constitutional Court Decision 2001Hun-Ba36 (2004) (Pension Reform):** The Court upheld pension reform legislation that reduced future benefits for persons already in the pension system, characterizing this as non-genuine retroactivity affecting ongoing legal relationships. The Court applied proportionality analysis, finding that the public interest in fiscal sustainability outweighed the individual interest in maintaining expected pension levels, provided that adequate transitional provisions were included.

**Constitutional Court Decision 2013Hun-Ba168 (2015) (Criminal Sentencing):** The Court addressed the retroactive application of enhanced criminal sentencing guidelines, holding that Article 13(1) prohibits not only the creation of new criminal offenses but also the retroactive increase of penalties for existing offenses. The prohibition is absolute and admits of no exceptions.

**Constitutional Court Decision 2015Hun-Ma1047 (2018) (Public Official Qualification):** The Court examined retroactive disqualification provisions applied to public officials convicted of corruption, holding that while the measure constituted genuine retroactivity with respect to past convictions, it was justified by the overwhelming public interest in public integrity and the relatively limited burden on affected individuals.

### Retroactive Criminal Law: Lex Mitior

While Article 13(1) explicitly prohibits retroactive imposition of criminal liability, the Korean Criminal Act (형법) Article 1(2) provides that where a law is changed after the commission of an offense but before final judgment, the law most favorable to the defendant (유리한 법률) shall apply. This lex mitior principle ensures that decriminalization or penalty reduction benefits defendants retroactively.

## United States

### Constitutional Framework

**Article I, Section 9, Clause 3:** "No Bill of Attainder or ex post facto Law shall be passed" (applicable to the federal government).

**Article I, Section 10, Clause 1:** "No State shall ... pass any Bill of Attainder, ex post facto Law, or Law impairing the Obligation of Contracts."

### Calder v. Bull and the Criminal Limitation

**Calder v. Bull, 3 U.S. (3 Dall.) 386 (1798):** Justice Chase's opinion defined four categories of ex post facto laws:
1. Every law that makes an action done before the passing of the law, and which was innocent when done, criminal.
2. Every law that aggravates a crime, or makes it greater than it was when committed.
3. Every law that changes the punishment, and inflicts a greater punishment than the law annexed to the crime when committed.
4. Every law that alters the legal rules of evidence, and receives less or different testimony than the law required at the time of the commission of the offense.

Critically, Justice Chase held that the Ex Post Facto Clause applies only to criminal and penal legislation, not to retroactive civil legislation. This limitation has been consistently maintained, most recently confirmed in Collins v. Youngblood, 497 U.S. 37 (1990), and Smith v. Doe, 538 U.S. 84 (2003).

### Ex Post Facto Clause Jurisprudence

**Stogner v. California, 539 U.S. 607 (2003):** The Court held 5-4 that a California statute reviving previously time-barred criminal prosecutions for child sexual abuse violated the Ex Post Facto Clause, as applying it to cases where the statute of limitations had already expired constituted retroactive criminal legislation.

**Smith v. Doe, 538 U.S. 84 (2003):** The Court held that Alaska's sex offender registration law was not punitive in nature and therefore did not violate the Ex Post Facto Clause when applied retroactively to offenders convicted before the law's enactment. The Court applied a multi-factor test from Kennedy v. Mendoza-Martinez (1963) to distinguish regulatory from punitive measures.

**Peugh v. United States, 569 U.S. 530 (2013):** The Court held that retroactive application of amended federal sentencing guidelines that increased the recommended sentencing range violated the Ex Post Facto Clause, even though the guidelines are advisory rather than mandatory after United States v. Booker (2005).

### Retroactive Civil Legislation

In the absence of Ex Post Facto Clause protection, retroactive civil legislation is reviewed under:

**Due Process Clause:** Retroactive civil legislation is subject to rational basis review under Usery v. Turner Elkhorn Mining Co., 428 U.S. 1 (1976), which requires only that the retroactive application be supported by a legitimate legislative purpose furthered by rational means. This is an extremely deferential standard.

**Contracts Clause:** Article I, Section 10 prohibits states from impairing the obligation of contracts. The Court applies a three-part test from Energy Reserves Group v. Kansas Power & Light Co., 459 U.S. 400 (1983): whether the law substantially impairs a contractual relationship, whether the impairment serves a significant and legitimate public purpose, and whether the adjustment of rights is based on reasonable conditions.

**Takings Clause:** The Fifth Amendment's prohibition on taking private property without just compensation limits retroactive legislation that destroys vested property rights. Eastern Enterprises v. Apfel, 524 U.S. 498 (1998), applied the Takings Clause (in a plurality opinion) to retroactive imposition of coal miner health benefit liability.

### Presumption Against Retroactivity

**Landgraf v. USI Film Products, 511 U.S. 244 (1994):** The Court established a two-step framework for interpreting federal statutes:
1. First, determine whether Congress has clearly prescribed the statute's temporal reach.
2. If not, apply the presumption against retroactive application: a statute should not be applied retroactively unless Congress has clearly manifested its intent to that effect.

This interpretive presumption provides significant practical protection against retroactivity even in the absence of strong constitutional constraints on retroactive civil legislation.

## Germany

### Constitutional Framework

**Article 20(3) Grundgesetz:** "The legislature shall be bound by the constitutional order, the executive and the judiciary by law and justice (Gesetz und Recht)." The Federal Constitutional Court derives the Rückwirkungsverbot from this provision, specifically from the Rechtsstaatsprinzip and its components of Rechtssicherheit (legal certainty) and Vertrauensschutz (protection of legitimate expectations).

**Article 103(2) Grundgesetz:** "An act may be punished only if it was defined by a law as a criminal offence before the act was committed (Eine Tat kann nur bestraft werden, wenn die Strafbarkeit gesetzlich bestimmt war, bevor die Tat begangen wurde)." This provision establishes the absolute nulla poena sine lege principle for criminal law, which the BVerfG has held cannot be overridden by any proportionality analysis.

### Echte vs. Unechte Rückwirkung

**Echte Rückwirkung (Genuine Retroactivity / Rückbewirkung von Rechtsfolgen):** A statute has genuine retroactive effect when it attaches new legal consequences to a set of facts that were already concluded (abgeschlossener Tatbestand) before the statute's promulgation. This is presumptively unconstitutional.

Exceptions recognized by the BVerfG:
1. The prior legal situation was unclear or confused (unklare oder verworrene Rechtslage)
2. The citizen could not have trusted in the continued existence of the prior legal situation
3. The prior norm was invalid or unconstitutional
4. The retroactive change provides only minor or negligible disadvantage
5. Overriding public interests (überragende Gemeinwohlgründe) of exceptional urgency require retroactive regulation

**Unechte Rückwirkung (Non-Genuine Retroactivity / tatbestandliche Rückanknüpfung):** A statute has non-genuine retroactive effect when it applies to facts that originated in the past but whose legal consequences had not yet been finally determined at the time of the statute's promulgation. This is permissible in principle but must satisfy proportionality review, with particular attention to Vertrauensschutz.

### Landmark Cases

**BVerfGE 13, 261 (1961) (Retroactive Tax Law):** The Court established the basic framework, holding that retroactive tax legislation affecting completed tax periods constitutes echte Rückwirkung and is presumptively unconstitutional. The Court laid down the principle that the legislature may not impose new tax burdens on transactions that were completed under a different legal regime.

**BVerfGE 72, 200 (1986) (Termination of Pension Entitlements):** The Court applied the unechte Rückwirkung framework to pension reform legislation that reduced future benefits for current recipients. The Court held that while future pension payments constitute ongoing legal relationships (and therefore the reduction constitutes non-genuine retroactivity), the legislature must provide adequate transitional provisions (Übergangsregelungen) to protect legitimate expectations formed under the prior system.

**BVerfGE 95, 64 (1996) (Retroactive Tax Assessment):** The Court refined the tax retroactivity framework, distinguishing between the date of legislative announcement (Verkündung), the date of parliamentary introduction of the bill, and the date on which the citizen could no longer reasonably expect the legal situation to remain unchanged. The Court held that citizens' legitimate expectations may be diminished once a legislative change has been publicly announced, even before enactment.

**BVerfGE 132, 302 (2012) (Nuclear Phase-Out):** The Court addressed the retroactive acceleration of the nuclear power phase-out (Atomausstieg) following the Fukushima disaster. The Court held that the shortening of operating periods for existing nuclear power plants constituted a combination of echte and unechte Rückwirkung, with the echte component requiring compensatory measures for the destruction of concrete investment expectations.

**BVerfGE 135, 1 (2013) (Succession Tax):** The Court struck down retroactive provisions of the inheritance tax reform, holding that the legislature's delay in implementing a mandated reform did not justify retroactively increasing tax burdens on successions that occurred during the transitional period.

### Criminal Law: Nulla Poena Sine Lege

Article 103(2) GG establishes four sub-principles:
- **Bestimmtheitsgebot:** Criminal norms must be sufficiently determinate.
- **Rückwirkungsverbot:** Criminal norms may not be applied retroactively.
- **Analogieverbot:** Criminal norms may not be applied by analogy to the detriment of the accused.
- **Gewohnheitsrechtsverbot:** Customary law may not form the basis of criminal liability.

The prohibition is absolute and applies to both the definition of offenses and the severity of penalties. StGB (Criminal Code) Section 2(1) codifies this: "The penalty and its associated measures shall be determined by the law which was in force at the time of the act." Section 2(3) provides the lex mitior rule: if the law in force at the time of judgment differs from that in force at the time of the act, the more lenient law shall apply.

### The Special Case of German Unification

The reunification of Germany in 1990 presented unique retroactivity challenges. The Federal Constitutional Court addressed the retroactive application of West German law to acts committed under the GDR legal system:

**BVerfGE 95, 96 (1996) (Mauerschützen/Border Guards):** The Court upheld the criminal conviction of GDR border guards for shooting persons attempting to cross the Berlin Wall, holding that the killings violated GDR law as properly interpreted and international human rights obligations, even though the GDR regime had in practice encouraged such shootings. The Court held that the retroactivity prohibition does not protect expectations formed in reliance on state practice that was itself unlawful.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Article 7

**Article 7(1):** "No one shall be held guilty of any criminal offence on account of any act or omission which did not constitute a criminal offence under national or international law at the time when it was committed. Nor shall a heavier penalty be imposed than the one that was applicable at the time the criminal offence was committed."

**Article 7(2):** "This Article shall not prejudice the trial and punishment of any person for any act or omission which, at the time when it was committed, was criminal according to the general principles of law recognised by civilised nations."

Article 7 is non-derogable under Article 15(2) ECHR, reflecting its foundational character.

### ECtHR Jurisprudence

**S.W. v. United Kingdom and C.R. v. United Kingdom (Applications Nos. 20166/92 and 20190/92, 1995):** The Court held that Article 7 does not prohibit the gradual judicial development of criminal law, including the removal of the marital rape immunity. The Court acknowledged that criminal law may be progressively clarified through judicial interpretation, provided that the development is consistent with the essence of the offense and could reasonably be foreseen.

**Streletz, Kessler and Krenz v. Germany (Applications Nos. 34044/96, 35532/97, 44801/98, Grand Chamber 2001):** The Court upheld the conviction of former GDR officials for the border shooting regime, applying Article 7(2) and finding that the conduct was criminal under international law and under GDR law properly construed. This decision paralleled the BVerfG's Mauerschützen jurisprudence.

**Scoppola v. Italy (No. 2) (Application No. 10249/03, Grand Chamber 2009):** The Court held that Article 7(1) ECHR guarantees not only the non-retroactivity of heavier penalties but also the retroactive application of a lighter penalty (lex mitior). This landmark decision expanded the scope of Article 7 beyond its literal text, aligning it with Article 15(1) of the ICCPR and the general trend in domestic systems.

**Del Rio Prada v. Spain (Application No. 42750/09, Grand Chamber 2013):** The Court found a violation of Article 7 where Spain retroactively changed the method of calculating prison sentence reductions (the "Parot doctrine"), effectively extending the applicant's imprisonment beyond what the law in force at the time of sentencing would have required. The Court emphasized that Article 7 prohibits not only retroactive creation of offenses but also retroactive increase of the effective penalty through changes in sentencing methodology.

**Rohlena v. Czech Republic (Application No. 59552/08, Grand Chamber 2015):** The Court upheld the Czech Republic's prosecution of continuing domestic violence under a statute enacted during the course of the defendant's ongoing criminal conduct, holding that Article 7 permits application of new criminal law to continuing offenses where the conduct persists after the law's enactment.

### EU Law

**General Principle of Non-Retroactivity:** The CJEU recognizes the prohibition of retroactivity as a general principle of EU law, derived from the rule of law and the protection of legitimate expectations.

**Racke v. Hauptzollamt Mainz (C-162/96, 1998):** The Court affirmed that "the principle of legal certainty precludes a Community measure from taking effect from a point in time before its publication" unless exceptionally required by the purpose to be achieved and the legitimate expectations of those concerned are duly respected.

**Article 49 EU Charter:** "No one shall be held guilty of any criminal offence on account of any act or omission which did not constitute a criminal offence under national law or international law at the time when it was committed. Nor shall a heavier penalty be imposed than the one that was applicable at the time the criminal offence was committed. If, subsequent to the commission of a criminal offence, the law provides for a lighter penalty, that penalty shall apply." Notably, the Charter expressly codifies the lex mitior principle, going beyond the original text of ECHR Article 7.

### EU Retroactive Regulations and Directives

The CJEU has developed specific rules regarding the retroactivity of EU legislation:
- EU regulations and directives may not generally have retroactive effect.
- The EU legislature may exceptionally provide for retroactive effect where the purpose of the measure requires it and legitimate expectations are respected.
- Retroactive effect of EU tax legislation is subject to strict scrutiny.

## Comparative Analysis

### Scope of Protection

The most significant structural difference is the scope of the retroactivity prohibition. Germany and South Korea apply comprehensive retroactivity frameworks to both criminal and civil legislation, with the genuine/non-genuine distinction providing calibrated protection. The United States limits constitutional retroactivity protection primarily to criminal legislation (Ex Post Facto Clause), with civil retroactivity receiving only minimal Due Process review, though the interpretive presumption from Landgraf provides practical protection. The ECHR's Article 7 addresses only criminal retroactivity, leaving civil retroactivity largely to domestic law.

### The Genuine/Non-Genuine Distinction

The German-Korean distinction between genuine and non-genuine retroactivity provides a more sophisticated analytical framework than the binary approaches of the United States and ECHR. This distinction acknowledges that legal situations exist on a spectrum from fully concluded to fully ongoing, and that the strength of the retroactivity prohibition should correspond to the degree to which the affected legal relationship was completed before the new law's enactment.

### Tax Retroactivity

Tax retroactivity reveals particularly sharp differences. Germany has developed detailed rules specific to tax legislation, distinguishing between different types of tax retroactivity and calibrating protection based on the timing of legislative announcements. South Korea applies similarly strict scrutiny to retroactive tax provisions. The United States is notably more permissive, with the Court in United States v. Carlton, 512 U.S. 26 (1994), upholding retroactive tax legislation under rational basis review.

### Lex Mitior

The treatment of favorable retroactive criminal law varies: ECHR Article 7 as interpreted in Scoppola requires retroactive application of lighter penalties; the EU Charter Article 49 expressly codifies this requirement; Germany provides the lex mitior principle through StGB Section 2(3) but does not derive it from the constitutional prohibition; South Korea provides it through Criminal Act Article 1(2); the United States has no constitutional requirement for retroactive application of more lenient criminal law, as confirmed in Dorsey v. United States, 567 U.S. 260 (2012) (interpreting but not constitutionally requiring retroactive application of the Fair Sentencing Act).
