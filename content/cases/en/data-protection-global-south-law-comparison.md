## Summary

Data protection legislation across the Global South has undergone rapid development since 2018, with major economies enacting comprehensive privacy frameworks that depart significantly from both the European GDPR model and the American sectoral approach. China's Personal Information Protection Law (个人信息保护法, PIPL, effective 1 November 2021) represents the world's most restrictive cross-border data transfer regime, requiring security assessments by the Cyberspace Administration of China (CAC) for transfers exceeding specified thresholds, while simultaneously preserving broad state access to personal data under the national security framework. India's Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023 (DPDPA, Act No. 22 of 2023, notified 11 August 2023) adopts a consent-and-notice model with unique features including deemed consent for "certain legitimate uses," a Digital Personal Data Protection Board as the adjudicatory body, and penalties up to INR 250 crore (approximately USD 30 million). South Africa's Protection of Personal Information Act (POPIA, Act No. 4 of 2013, fully effective 1 July 2021) was Africa's first comprehensive data protection law modeled on European principles, establishing the Information Regulator as an independent supervisory authority. Brazil's Lei Geral de Proteção de Dados (LGPD, Lei 13.709/2018, effective September 2020) closely mirrors the GDPR while incorporating Brazilian constitutional principles of personality rights and informational self-determination. The Philippines' Data Privacy Act (DPA, Republic Act No. 10173, 2012) and the National Privacy Commission (NPC) have created Southeast Asia's most mature data protection enforcement regime.

## China (中国)

### Legal Framework

China's data protection architecture is a "three-legged stool" comprising three interlocking laws enacted in rapid succession:

- **Personal Information Protection Law (个人信息保护法, PIPL, effective 1 November 2021):** China's comprehensive data protection statute, applicable to all personal information processing activities within China and extraterritorially to processing of Chinese residents' personal information by overseas entities (Art. 3). Key provisions include:
  - **Legal Bases (Art. 13):** Seven bases for processing -- consent; contract necessity; legal obligation; public health emergency; news reporting/public opinion supervision in the public interest; processing of publicly available information within a reasonable scope; and "other circumstances provided by laws and administrative regulations." Consent must be informed, voluntary, and explicit for sensitive personal information (Art. 29).
  - **Sensitive Personal Information (Art. 28):** Defined as information that, once leaked or illegally used, may easily infringe on the dignity, personal safety, or property security of natural persons, including biometrics, religious belief, specific identity, medical health, financial accounts, whereabouts and tracking, and all personal information of minors under 14.
  - **Cross-Border Transfers (Arts. 38-39):** Three pathways: (1) CAC security assessment (mandatory for critical information infrastructure operators and processors handling data exceeding specified thresholds -- initially 1 million individuals, subsequently relaxed); (2) personal information protection certification by a professional institution; or (3) standard contractual clauses with the overseas recipient. The CAC's "Measures on the Standard Contract for Cross-Border Transfer of Personal Information" (effective 1 June 2023) specify the standard contract template.
  - **Penalties (Art. 66):** For serious violations: up to RMB 50 million or 5% of previous year's revenue; suspension of relevant business; revocation of business license. Individual liability: fines of RMB 100,000-1,000,000 and prohibition from serving as director, supervisor, or senior manager.
- **Data Security Law (数据安全法, DSL, effective 1 September 2021):** Establishes a tiered data classification and grading protection system (Art. 21), designating "important data" and "core data" subject to enhanced protection. Requires security assessments for cross-border transfers of important data (Art. 31). The DSL interacts with the PIPL for personal data that also qualifies as important or core data.
- **Cybersecurity Law (网络安全法, CSL, effective 1 June 2017):** The foundational cybersecurity statute requiring: network security grading protection (Art. 21); real-name registration for network services (Art. 24); data localization for critical information infrastructure operators (CIIOs) -- personal information and important data collected and generated in China must be stored domestically (Art. 37); and security assessments for any cross-border transfer.

### Key Principles

China's data protection model embeds privacy within a broader national security and digital sovereignty framework, distinguishing it from rights-based models elsewhere. The state retains broad access to personal data through the National Security Law (2015), the Anti-Terrorism Law (2015), and the Counter-Espionage Law (2023 revision). The PIPL's Article 13(2)-(7) legal bases allow extensive government data processing without individual consent.

The "inform-consent" (告知-同意) principle is central to private-sector data processing, requiring clear and prominent disclosure of processing purposes, methods, categories, retention periods, and the method for exercising rights (Art. 17). Separate consent is required for sensitive information processing, cross-border transfers, and third-party sharing.

### Notable Enforcement

- **Didi Global CAC Investigation (2021-2022):** The CAC launched a cybersecurity review of Didi Global (滴滴出行) on 2 July 2021, two days after its USD 4.4 billion NYSE IPO, ordering Didi's app removed from app stores. On 21 July 2022, the CAC imposed a fine of RMB 8.026 billion (approximately USD 1.19 billion) on Didi for violating the CSL, DSL, and PIPL. Violations included illegally collecting users' clipboard information, photo albums, facial recognition data, and precise geolocation without consent, and transferring data abroad without security assessment. CEO Cheng Wei and President Liu Qing were each fined RMB 1 million.
- **PIPL Standard Contract Filing Deadline (November 2023):** Over 300 organizations filed standard contracts for cross-border personal information transfers with provincial CAC offices by the deadline, reflecting broad compliance efforts.
- **Algorithm Regulation:** The CAC's "Provisions on the Management of Algorithmic Recommendations in Internet Information Services" (effective 1 March 2022) regulate algorithmic recommendation systems, requiring algorithm filing and prohibiting practices such as unreasonable differential pricing based on consumer profiling.

## India (भारत)

### Legal Framework

India's data protection journey has been protracted, beginning with the Supreme Court's recognition of privacy as a fundamental right in 2017 and culminating in the DPDPA six years later:

- **Justice K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India (2017) 10 SCC 1:** A nine-judge Supreme Court bench unanimously held that the right to privacy is a fundamental right protected under Articles 14, 19, and 21 of the Constitution. Justice Chandrachud's lead opinion established a four-part test: any restriction on privacy must be (1) sanctioned by law, (2) for a legitimate state aim, (3) proportionate to the need, and (4) procedurally fair. This decision laid the constitutional foundation for data protection legislation.
- **Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 (DPDPA, Act No. 22 of 2023):** Key provisions:
  - **Applicability (s3-4):** Applies to processing of digital personal data within India and to processing outside India if related to offering goods or services to, or profiling of, data principals in India.
  - **Consent and Notice (s5-6):** Data fiduciaries must provide a notice in clear and plain language (in English or any of the 22 scheduled languages) before obtaining consent. Consent must be free, specific, informed, unconditional, and unambiguous, given through a clear affirmative action.
  - **Deemed Consent (s7):** Processing without explicit consent is permitted for "certain legitimate uses" including: performance of state functions (permits, licenses, benefits, services); compliance with law or court orders; response to medical emergencies; employment purposes; and legitimate interests of the data fiduciary (the scope of which will be defined by rules).
  - **Data Principal Rights (s11-14):** Right to access information about processing, right to correction and erasure, right to grievance redress, and right to nominate another person to exercise rights in case of death or incapacity.
  - **Significant Data Fiduciaries (s10):** The Central Government may designate certain fiduciaries as "significant" based on volume, sensitivity, risk of harm, and impact on sovereignty/security. SDFs must appoint a Data Protection Officer (DPO) based in India, an independent data auditor, and conduct Data Protection Impact Assessments.
  - **Penalties (s33):** Up to INR 250 crore per instance; penalties specified for specific violations (e.g., INR 200 crore for failure to take security measures; INR 150 crore for breach notification failures).
  - **Cross-Border Transfers (s16-17):** Personal data may be transferred to any country not specifically restricted by the Central Government through notification. This "blacklist" approach (restricting specified countries) contrasts with the GDPR's "whitelist" adequacy approach.
  - **Government Exemptions (s17):** The Central Government may exempt any government instrumentality from the entire Act or any provision thereof, in the interest of sovereignty, integrity, security of the state, public order, or friendly relations with foreign states.
- **Information Technology Act, 2000 (IT Act) -- Section 43A and SPDI Rules 2011:** Prior to the DPDPA, Section 43A (inserted by the 2008 amendment) required body corporates possessing, dealing with, or handling "sensitive personal data or information" (SPDI) to implement reasonable security practices. The Information Technology (Reasonable Security Practices and Procedures and Sensitive Personal Data or Information) Rules, 2011, specified categories of SPDI and required privacy policies. These provisions will be superseded once the DPDPA rules are notified and operational.

### Key Principles

The DPDPA is notable for what it omits compared to the GDPR and earlier Indian proposals: it does not include a right to data portability, does not regulate non-personal data, does not require data protection impact assessments for all high-risk processing (only for SDFs), and does not provide for a right to object to automated decision-making. The broad government exemptions under Section 17 have drawn criticism from civil liberties organizations, who argue they undermine the Puttaswamy privacy framework.

The Act adopts a "digital by design" approach, with the DPDPB established as a digital-first adjudicatory body. All complaints, hearings, and orders are intended to be conducted electronically.

### Notable Cases

- **Justice K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India (Aadhaar, 2018) 1 SCC 809:** The five-judge Constitution Bench upheld the constitutional validity of the Aadhaar biometric identification system under the Aadhaar (Targeted Delivery of Financial and Other Subsidies, Benefits and Services) Act, 2016, but struck down Section 57 (allowing private entities to use Aadhaar), holding that mandatory Aadhaar linkage to bank accounts and mobile numbers was disproportionate.
- **WhatsApp Privacy Policy Challenge (2021):** The Delhi High Court and Competition Commission of India (CCI) both scrutinized WhatsApp's 2021 privacy policy update requiring data sharing with Meta. The CCI imposed a fine of INR 213 crore on WhatsApp for anti-competitive conduct related to the privacy policy.
- **Pegasus Spyware Investigation (2021-2022):** The Supreme Court constituted a Technical Committee to investigate alleged use of Pegasus spyware against journalists, politicians, and activists, highlighting the tension between surveillance powers and the privacy right established in Puttaswamy.

## Africa (Nigeria and South Africa)

### South Africa

#### Legal Framework

- **Protection of Personal Information Act (POPIA, Act No. 4 of 2013):** Signed into law in 2013, with sections on the Information Regulator commencing in 2014, and the substantive processing provisions becoming fully effective on 1 July 2021 after a one-year grace period. POPIA is modeled on the EU Data Protection Directive 95/46/EC (the GDPR's predecessor) and covers processing of personal information by public and private bodies:
  - **Eight Processing Conditions (Chapter 3):** Accountability; processing limitation; purpose specification; further processing limitation; information quality; openness; security safeguards; and data subject participation. These conditions closely mirror the GDPR's principles but use South African constitutional language.
  - **Special Personal Information (s26-33):** Enhanced protection for religious or philosophical beliefs, race or ethnic origin, trade union membership, political persuasion, health, sex life, biometric information, and criminal behavior. Processing is generally prohibited unless specific exemptions apply.
  - **Cross-Border Transfers (s72):** Transfer only to recipients in jurisdictions with adequate protection, or with binding corporate rules, consent, contract necessity, or substantial public interest. The Information Regulator has not yet published adequacy determinations.
  - **Information Regulator:** Independent body established under s39, with powers to monitor compliance, conduct assessments, issue enforcement notices, and refer criminal prosecutions. The Regulator is also responsible for the Promotion of Access to Information Act (PAIA) enforcement.
  - **Penalties (s107-109):** Offenses punishable by fines of up to ZAR 10 million and/or imprisonment of up to 10 years for obstruction, hindering, or unlawfully obtaining personal information.

#### Notable Enforcement

- **Department of Justice and Constitutional Development Data Breach (2021):** A ransomware attack on the DoJ&CD compromised personal information in the Master's Office system (wills, estates, trusts). The Information Regulator issued an enforcement notice, the first major exercise of POPIA enforcement powers.
- **TransUnion Africa Data Breach (2022):** The Information Regulator investigated a data breach affecting 54 million records following a cyberattack by the N4ughtySec group. The Regulator issued an enforcement notice requiring TransUnion to notify all affected data subjects and implement enhanced security measures.
- **Information Regulator v. Department of Home Affairs (2023):** The Information Regulator took enforcement action against the Department of Home Affairs for POPIA non-compliance in the processing of biometric data for the Smart ID Card system.

### Nigeria

#### Legal Framework

- **Nigeria Data Protection Act 2023 (NDPA):** Signed by President Tinubu in June 2023, replacing the Nigeria Data Protection Regulation (NDPR) 2019 (which was issued by NITDA under the NITDA Act 2007). The NDPA establishes:
  - **Nigeria Data Protection Commission (NDPC):** An independent regulatory body replacing NITDA's data protection role, with commissioners appointed by the President on the recommendation of the Minister of Communications.
  - **Legal Bases (s25-29):** Consent; contract performance; legitimate interest; vital interest; legal obligation; and public interest -- closely mirroring the GDPR.
  - **Cross-Border Transfers (s40-42):** Adequacy assessment by NDPC; appropriate safeguards (binding corporate rules, standard contractual clauses, codes of conduct); or derogations (consent, contract, vital interest, legal claims, public interest). The NDPC is empowered to conduct adequacy assessments and publish a whitelist.
  - **Penalties (s48):** Administrative penalties of up to NGN 10 million or 2% of annual gross revenue (whichever is higher) for data controllers; criminal penalties for individuals engaging in unlawful data processing.
  - **Data Protection Compliance Organizations (DPCOs):** A unique Nigerian innovation from the NDPR era, licensing private sector auditors to conduct data protection compliance audits on behalf of data controllers, addressing capacity constraints at the regulatory level.

## Latin America (Brazil, Mexico, and Colombia)

### Legal Framework

Latin America has the most mature data protection tradition in the Global South, with multiple countries having enacted comprehensive legislation well before the GDPR:

- **Brazil -- Lei Geral de Proteção de Dados (LGPD, Lei 13.709/2018, effective 18 September 2020):** Brazil's comprehensive data protection law, closely modeled on the GDPR but incorporating distinctive Brazilian elements:
  - **Ten Legal Bases (Art. 7):** Consent; legal obligation; public policy; research (anonymized where possible); contract performance; exercise of rights in judicial, administrative, or arbitration proceedings; protection of life; health protection; legitimate interest; and credit protection. The "credit protection" basis is unique to the LGPD, reflecting the importance of credit bureaus (cadastro positivo) in Brazilian commerce.
  - **Autoridade Nacional de Proteção de Dados (ANPD):** Initially created as a body linked to the Presidency (raising independence concerns), converted to a special independent authority by Law 14.460/2022. The ANPD issues regulations, receives complaints, and imposes administrative sanctions.
  - **Administrative Sanctions (Art. 52):** Warning; simple fine of up to 2% of revenue in Brazil per violation, capped at BRL 50 million per violation; daily fine; publicization of the violation; blocking of personal data; deletion of personal data; suspension of database operations; and prohibition of data processing activities.
  - **Data Protection Officer (Encarregado, Art. 41):** Every data controller must appoint a DPO (encarregado), though the ANPD has issued resolutions exempting micro-enterprises and small businesses from certain obligations (Resolution CD/ANPD No. 2/2022).
  - **International Data Transfers (Art. 33):** Permitted to countries with adequate protection; with appropriate safeguards (standard contractual clauses, binding corporate rules, codes of conduct); with specific and prominent consent; and other enumerated derogations. The ANPD published Regulation CD/ANPD No. 19/2024 on standard contractual clauses and international data transfer mechanisms.

- **Mexico -- Ley Federal de Protección de Datos Personales en Posesión de los Particulares (LFPDPPP, 2010):** Mexico enacted comprehensive data protection law for the private sector in 2010, one of the earliest in Latin America. Key features include the ARCO rights (Acceso, Rectificación, Cancelación, Oposición) enforceable through the National Institute for Transparency, Access to Information and Personal Data Protection (INAI). The 2017 General Law on Protection of Personal Data Held by Obligated Subjects (Ley General de Protección de Datos Personales en Posesión de Sujetos Obligados) extended protection to the public sector.

- **Colombia -- Law 1581/2012 (Statutory Law on Data Protection):** Establishes the right to habeas data (constitutional right to know, update, and rectify personal information held in databases, Art. 15 Constitution) as the foundation for data protection. The Superintendence of Industry and Commerce (SIC) serves as the supervisory authority, with powers to impose fines of up to 2,000 SMMLV (salarios mínimos mensuales legales vigentes, approximately COP 2.6 billion or USD 650,000 in 2024). Colombia's National Registry of Databases (Registro Nacional de Bases de Datos) requires all data controllers to register their databases with the SIC.

### Notable Enforcement

- **Brazil -- ANPD First Major Fine (2023):** The ANPD imposed its first significant administrative fine on Telekall Infoservice for unlawful sale of WhatsApp voter contact lists for political marketing purposes during the 2022 elections, imposing a BRL 14,400 fine (modest, given the small size of the company). The case established the ANPD's enforcement posture.
- **Brazil -- ANPD v. INSS (2024):** The ANPD investigated the National Social Security Institute (INSS) for data sharing practices that resulted in unsolicited marketing calls to pension beneficiaries, highlighting public sector compliance challenges.
- **Mexico -- INAI v. Cambridge Analytica (2018):** INAI investigated the Cambridge Analytica scandal's impact on Mexican users of Facebook, resulting in recommendations for enhanced transparency and consent mechanisms.
- **Colombia -- SIC v. Rappi (2020):** The SIC sanctioned the delivery app Rappi for unlawful processing of personal data, including biometric data of delivery workers, and for sharing personal data with third parties without adequate consent.

## Southeast Asia (Indonesia and Philippines)

### Legal Framework

- **Philippines -- Data Privacy Act (DPA, RA 10173, 2012):** The Philippines enacted comprehensive data protection legislation a decade before most of its ASEAN peers:
  - **National Privacy Commission (NPC):** Established under the DPA as an independent body with quasi-judicial functions, the NPC has become one of the most active data protection authorities in Asia. The NPC issues Circulars, Advisory Opinions, and compliance orders.
  - **Processing Criteria (s12):** Consent; contract performance; legal obligation; vital interest; constitutional or public authority functions; and legitimate interest of the personal information controller or third party. The NPC has issued extensive guidance on legitimate interest through Advisory Opinions.
  - **Penalties (s33-34):** Unauthorized processing: 1-3 years imprisonment and PHP 500,000-2,000,000 fine. Processing for unauthorized purposes: 1.5-5 years and PHP 500,000-2,000,000. Malicious disclosure: 1.5-5 years and PHP 500,000-1,000,000. Unauthorized access or intentional breach: 1-3 years and PHP 500,000-2,000,000. Criminal penalties reflect the Philippines' enforcement philosophy of using imprisonment as a deterrent.
  - **Mandatory Breach Notification (NPC Circular 16-03):** Personal information controllers must notify the NPC and affected data subjects within 72 hours of discovery of a personal data breach involving sensitive personal information that may lead to identity fraud, serious harm, or involves at least 100 individuals.
  - **Data Protection Officers (NPC Circular 16-01):** Mandatory appointment for all personal information controllers and processors, regardless of size, which is more expansive than the GDPR's conditional requirement.

- **Indonesia -- Personal Data Protection Law (Undang-Undang Pelindungan Data Pribadi, UU PDP, Law No. 27/2022, effective 17 October 2024):** Indonesia's first comprehensive data protection law, enacted after over a decade of legislative deliberation:
  - **Legal Bases (Art. 20):** Consent; contract performance; legal obligation; vital interest; legitimate interest; and statutory authority. Consent must be explicit, in written or recorded form.
  - **Cross-Border Transfers (Art. 56):** Transfers to countries with "equivalent or higher" data protection standards, or with adequate safeguards (binding corporate rules, standard contractual clauses). The transferring party must ensure equivalent protection.
  - **Penalties (Arts. 57-58):** Administrative sanctions include written warning, temporary suspension, deletion of personal data, and fines of up to 2% of annual revenue. Criminal sanctions (Arts. 67-73) include up to 5 years imprisonment and IDR 5 billion fine for unlawful collection, up to 6 years and IDR 6 billion for unlawful disclosure.
  - **Supervisory Authority:** The law mandates establishment of an independent supervisory authority, but its institutional form remains under Government Regulation (pending as of early 2026). In the interim, the Ministry of Communication and Information Technology (Kominfo/Komdigi) exercises oversight.

### Notable Enforcement

- **Philippines -- NPC v. Grab Philippines (2019):** The NPC investigated Grab following a data breach affecting 24,000 drivers' and passengers' personal information. The NPC imposed a compliance order requiring enhanced security measures and regular vulnerability assessments.
- **Philippines -- NPC Advisory Opinion on Facial Recognition (2022-02-015):** The NPC ruled that collection of facial biometric data for attendance monitoring constitutes processing of sensitive personal information requiring explicit consent and a legitimate purpose, setting a precedent for biometric regulation across ASEAN.
- **Indonesia -- Bjorka Data Breach Incidents (2022):** A hacker known as "Bjorka" claimed to have breached multiple Indonesian government databases (election commission, SIM card registry, COVID-19 contact tracing), exposing hundreds of millions of records. The incidents accelerated passage of the PDP Law and highlighted the urgency of an independent supervisory authority.
- **Philippines -- NPC Enforcement Statistics:** The NPC processed over 5,800 data breach notifications between 2017 and 2024, issued 47 compliance orders, and imposed penalties in 23 cases, making it one of the most active DPAs in the developing world.

## Comparative Analysis

### Regulatory Architecture

The five regions exhibit distinct supervisory architectures: (1) **Multi-agency model** (China), where the CAC, Ministry of Public Security, and sector regulators share overlapping jurisdiction; (2) **Digital-first tribunal** (India), with the DPDPB as a specialized adjudicatory body without proactive supervisory functions; (3) **Independent regulator** (South Africa, Brazil, Philippines, Nigeria), following the European model of an independent data protection authority; and (4) **Ministry-led interim model** (Indonesia), where the ministry oversees compliance pending establishment of an independent authority.

### Cross-Border Transfer Regimes

Cross-border data transfer rules reveal the most significant divergences. China's security assessment regime for large-scale transfers (though relaxed by the 2024 "Provisions on Promoting and Regulating Cross-Border Data Flows") remains the most restrictive, reflecting data sovereignty priorities. India's "blacklist" approach (allowing transfers everywhere except to restricted countries) is the most permissive in design, though the Central Government's restriction power creates uncertainty. Brazil and the Philippines follow adequacy-plus-safeguards models similar to the GDPR. South Africa's POPIA requires adequate protection in the recipient jurisdiction, but the Information Regulator's delay in issuing adequacy determinations has created a compliance vacuum.

### State Access Exemptions

The breadth of government exemptions is a critical differentiator. China's framework preserves comprehensive state access through national security legislation. India's DPDPA Section 17 exemptions for state instrumentalities have been criticized as potentially allowing mass surveillance programs to continue outside the data protection framework. Brazil's LGPD applies to public and private sector equally (with narrower public interest bases), reflecting the GDPR approach. The Philippines' DPA provides exceptions for national security but subjects government processing to NPC oversight. These variations reflect fundamental differences in the state-citizen relationship across the five regions.

### Enforcement Maturity

The Philippines stands out as having the most enforcement experience among the five regions (DPA effective since 2012). China's PIPL enforcement has been dramatic but selective (the Didi case). Brazil's ANPD is building capacity but enforcement remains limited. South Africa's Information Regulator is active but resource-constrained. India's DPDPB has not yet commenced operations. Nigeria's NDPC is in the process of building institutional capacity.
