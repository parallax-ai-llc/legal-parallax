## Summary

Animal welfare in farming -- the legal frameworks governing the treatment, housing, transport, and slaughter of animals raised for food production -- reflects an evolving balance between agricultural efficiency, consumer expectations, ethical considerations, and scientific understanding of animal sentience. South Korea enacted the Animal Protection Act (동물보호법, originally 1991, comprehensively reformed 2007 and 2023) which establishes basic welfare standards for farm animals including prohibitions on cruelty and requirements for adequate shelter, food, and water, though enforcement has been criticized as inadequate, particularly in the intensive poultry and pork sectors that house approximately 180 million chickens and 11 million pigs; the 2023 amendment strengthened penalties for animal cruelty and introduced the concept of "animal welfare certification" (동물복지 인증) for farms meeting enhanced standards. The United States has the weakest federal farm animal welfare protections among the jurisdictions examined: the Animal Welfare Act (7 U.S.C. Sections 2131-2159) explicitly excludes farm animals used in food production; the Humane Methods of Slaughter Act (7 U.S.C. Section 1901) covers only cattle, calves, horses, mules, sheep, swine, and goats at federally inspected slaughter plants (excluding poultry, which represents over 95% of animals slaughtered); and the Twenty-Eight Hour Law (49 U.S.C. Section 80502) limits transport duration but applies only to rail and vessel transport, not trucks; state-level ballot initiatives, particularly California's Proposition 12 (2018, upheld by the Supreme Court in National Pork Producers Council v. Ross, 598 U.S. 356, 2023), have driven the most significant welfare improvements by banning the sale of products from animals confined in extreme conditions. Germany, operating within the EU framework, maintains among the world's most comprehensive farm animal welfare laws through the Animal Welfare Act (Tierschutzgesetz, TierSchG) which recognizes animals as sentient beings and requires species-appropriate husbandry, supplemented by the Livestock Farming Ordinance (Tierschutz-Nutztierhaltungsverordnung, TierSchNutztV) setting detailed housing standards; Article 20a of the Basic Law constitutionally protects animals (the "state objective animal welfare"), making Germany one of the few countries with constitutional-level animal welfare provisions; the 2023 amendment to the TierSchG introduced a mandatory animal welfare labeling system (Tierhaltungskennzeichnung) for pork products. At the European level, the EU recognizes animal sentience in Article 13 of the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union (TFEU), and regulates farm animal welfare through a comprehensive framework including Directive 98/58/EC (general farm animal protection), specific directives on calves, pigs, and laying hens, and Regulation (EC) No 1099/2009 on slaughter; the European Commission's 2023 proposal to revise and strengthen the animal welfare legislative framework -- including a ban on caged farming and new transport rules -- represents the most significant potential reform in two decades; the ECHR has addressed animal welfare primarily through Article 9 (freedom of religion in the context of ritual slaughter) in cases such as Cha'are Shalom Ve Tsedek v. France (2000) and the CJEU's ruling in Centraal Israëlitisch Consistorie van België (C-336/19, 2020) upholding Belgium's ban on unstunned slaughter.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

**Animal Protection Act (동물보호법):** Originally enacted in 1991 as a basic anti-cruelty statute, the Act has been progressively strengthened through major amendments in 2007, 2013, 2017, 2020, and 2023. Key provisions relevant to farm animals:

- **Article 8:** Prohibits cruelty to animals, including causing unnecessary pain, failing to provide adequate food and water, and abandonment
- **Article 29:** Establishes the animal welfare certification system for farms
- **Article 46-49:** Penalties including fines and imprisonment for cruelty violations

**Animal Welfare Certification (동물복지 인증):** The Ministry of Agriculture, Food and Rural Affairs operates a voluntary certification program for farms meeting enhanced welfare standards. Certified farms must meet species-specific requirements for space allowances, environmental enrichment, access to outdoors (for some species), and restriction of mutilation procedures. As of 2025, approximately 450 farms are certified, representing a small fraction of total livestock operations.

### 2023 Amendment

The 2023 amendment significantly strengthened the Act:
- Maximum penalties for animal cruelty increased from 2 years to 3 years imprisonment and from 20 million to 30 million KRW in fines
- New provisions on animal welfare in the care of sick or injured farm animals
- Strengthened enforcement authority for animal protection inspectors
- Requirements for CCTV installation in certain slaughter facilities

### Industry Structure

South Korea's livestock industry is characterized by rapid industrialization and concentration:

| Species | Population (approximate) | Key Welfare Concern |
|---------|------------------------|-------------------|
| Chickens (layers) | ~80 million | Battery cages (space: approximately 500 cm² per bird) |
| Chickens (broilers) | ~100 million annually | Rapid growth rates; high stocking density |
| Pigs | ~11 million | Gestation crates; tail docking without anesthesia |
| Cattle | ~3.6 million | Generally lower welfare concerns (semi-intensive/extensive) |

### Avian Influenza and Welfare

South Korea has experienced devastating avian influenza outbreaks (most notably H5N1 in 2008, 2016-17, and 2020-21) that resulted in the culling of tens of millions of poultry. The culling methods employed -- including burial of live animals during the 2016-17 outbreak -- drew severe criticism from animal welfare organizations and prompted legislative reform requiring more humane depopulation methods.

## United States

### Legal Framework

**Absence of Federal Farm Animal Welfare Law:** The United States is unique among developed countries in having no comprehensive federal statute governing the welfare of farm animals during their lives on farms. The Animal Welfare Act (7 U.S.C. Section 2132(g)) explicitly excludes "farm animals, such as, but not limited to livestock or poultry, used or intended for use as food or fiber."

**Humane Methods of Slaughter Act (HMSA, 7 U.S.C. Section 1901):** Requires that covered animals (cattle, calves, horses, mules, sheep, swine, goats) be rendered insensible to pain by a "single blow or gunshot or an electrical, chemical or other means that is rapid and effective" before shackling, hoisting, throwing, casting, or cutting. The HMSA exempts slaughter "in accordance with the ritual requirements of the Jewish faith or any other religious faith" from the stunning requirement. Critically, the HMSA does not cover poultry -- more than 9 billion chickens and turkeys slaughtered annually -- under its protections.

**State Ballot Initiatives -- Proposition 12:**

California's Proposition 12 (Prevention of Cruelty to Farm Animals Act, 2018) established minimum space requirements for calves raised for veal, breeding pigs, and egg-laying hens, and -- critically -- banned the sale in California of products from animals that did not meet these standards, regardless of where the animals were raised.

**National Pork Producers Council v. Ross (598 U.S. 356, 2023):** The pork industry challenged Proposition 12 as violating the dormant Commerce Clause by regulating out-of-state conduct. The Supreme Court, in a 5-4 decision, upheld Proposition 12, holding that the law did not discriminate against interstate commerce and that the dormant Commerce Clause does not invalidate state laws simply because they have significant effects on interstate markets. The decision was a landmark for state-level animal welfare regulation, confirming that states may use their market power to set welfare standards that effectively apply nationwide.

**Other State Laws:** As of 2026, 14 states have enacted laws restricting extreme confinement of farm animals:

| State | Gestation Crates | Battery Cages | Veal Crates |
|-------|-----------------|---------------|-------------|
| California | Banned (Prop 12) | Banned (Prop 12: cage-free by 2024) | Banned (Prop 12) |
| Massachusetts | Banned (Question 3, 2016) | Banned (cage-free) | Banned |
| Michigan | Phase-out | Phase-out | Phase-out |
| Colorado | Phase-out by 2026 | Cage-free by 2025 | N/A |
| Oregon | Banned | Cage-free by 2024 | N/A |
| Washington | Banned by 2026 | Cage-free by 2024 | N/A |

## Germany

### Legal Framework

**Grundgesetz Article 20a (Constitutional Animal Welfare):** Since 2002, the German Basic Law has included animal welfare as a state objective: "The state shall protect the natural foundations of life and animals by legislation and, in accordance with law and justice, by executive and judicial action" (Der Staat schützt auch in Verantwortung für die künftigen Generationen die natürlichen Lebensgrundlagen und die Tiere). This constitutional provision elevates animal welfare to a value that must be balanced against other constitutional rights, including freedom of religion (Article 4) and freedom of occupation (Article 12).

**Tierschutzgesetz (TierSchG):** Germany's Animal Welfare Act, originally enacted in 1972, establishes the fundamental principle that "no one may cause an animal pain, suffering, or harm without reasonable cause" (Section 1). For farm animals, the Act requires species-appropriate husbandry (artgerechte Haltung), adequate space and movement, appropriate feeding, and minimization of stress during transport and slaughter.

**Tierschutz-Nutztierhaltungsverordnung (TierSchNutztV, Livestock Farming Ordinance):** This ordinance establishes detailed, species-specific housing and management standards:

| Species | Key Standards |
|---------|--------------|
| Laying hens | EU battery cage ban implemented; enriched cages being phased out; minimum 750 cm² per bird (enriched) or alternative systems |
| Broilers | Maximum stocking density: 39 kg/m²; lighting and litter requirements |
| Pigs | Sow stall restriction (partially; full ban under discussion); group housing for pregnant sows after 4 weeks; minimum space per finishing pig |
| Cattle | Tethering restrictions; minimum space; group housing for calves after 8 weeks |

### Mandatory Welfare Labeling (Tierhaltungskennzeichnung)

The 2023 Animal Welfare Labeling Act establishes a mandatory five-tier labeling system for pork products:

| Level | Label | Housing Standard |
|-------|-------|-----------------|
| 1 | Stall (Stall) | Minimum legal standard |
| 2 | Stall+Platz (Stall+Space) | 20% more space than minimum |
| 3 | Frischluftstall (Fresh Air) | Outdoor climate access; 46% more space |
| 4 | Auslauf/Freiland (Outdoor/Free Range) | Outdoor access |
| 5 | Bio (Organic) | Organic certification standards |

The labeling system is being progressively extended to other animal products (poultry, dairy, eggs).

### Borchert Commission

The Borchert Commission (Kompetenznetzwerk Nutztierhaltung), chaired by former Agriculture Minister Jochen Borchert, recommended in 2020 a comprehensive transformation of German livestock farming toward higher welfare standards, estimated to require approximately 3-4 billion euros annually in public support. The Commission proposed mandatory welfare labeling, farm restructuring support, and outcome-based welfare standards. Implementation has been partial, with the labeling system adopted but the funding mechanism not fully established.

## ECHR / EU

### EU Treaty Basis

**TFEU Article 13:** "In formulating and implementing the Union's agriculture, fisheries, transport, internal market, research and technological development and space policies, the Union and the Member States shall, since animals are sentient beings, pay full regard to the welfare requirements of animals." This provision, introduced by the Lisbon Treaty, creates a legal obligation to consider animal welfare across all relevant EU policy areas.

### Current Legislative Framework

**Directive 98/58/EC (General Farm Animals):** Establishes minimum welfare standards applicable to all farm animals, including requirements for adequate food and water, appropriate housing, veterinary care, and freedom of movement. The Directive implements the Council of Europe Convention for the Protection of Animals Kept for Farming Purposes (ETS No. 087, 1976).

**Species-Specific Legislation:**
- **Directive 1999/74/EC (Laying Hens):** Banned conventional battery cages from January 1, 2012; permits enriched cages and alternative systems
- **Directive 2008/120/EC (Pigs):** Restricts sow stalls to first 4 weeks of pregnancy and 1 week before farrowing; requires group housing for pregnant sows; prohibits routine tail docking
- **Directive 2008/119/EC (Calves):** Prohibits individual housing for calves over 8 weeks; minimum space requirements
- **Regulation (EC) No 1/2005 (Transport):** Maximum journey times (8 hours standard; up to 24 hours for cattle and pigs with rest stops); vehicle standards; fitness for transport requirements
- **Regulation (EC) No 1099/2009 (Slaughter):** Requires effective stunning before slaughter; permits religious exemption for ritual slaughter; requires operator training and competence

### Proposed EU Animal Welfare Legislative Revision (2023)

The European Commission proposed a comprehensive revision of the animal welfare framework:
- **End of caged farming:** Progressive ban on all caged systems (including enriched cages for laying hens, farrowing crates for sows, and cages for rabbits, quail, and ducks), following the successful European Citizens' Initiative "End the Cage Age" (signed by 1.4 million citizens)
- **Transport reform:** Reduced maximum journey times; ban on export of live animals to third countries; temperature limits for transport
- **Welfare labeling:** Mandatory EU-wide animal welfare labeling system
- **Digital monitoring:** Requirements for CCTV in slaughterhouses

As of early 2026, legislative negotiations continue, with strong opposition from some member states and livestock industry groups regarding the cage ban timeline and transport restrictions.

### Ritual Slaughter

**Cha'are Shalom Ve Tsedek v. France (Grand Chamber, Application No. 27417/95, 2000):** A Jewish association challenged France's refusal to approve it for ritual slaughter (only the main Jewish association was approved). The Court found no violation of Article 9, holding that the applicants could obtain meat slaughtered according to their religious prescriptions through the approved association.

**Centraal Israëlitisch Consistorie van België (CJEU, C-336/19, 2020):** The CJEU upheld the Flemish and Walloon bans on slaughter without prior stunning, including ritual slaughter, finding that Regulation 1099/2009 permits member states to require stunning before slaughter even for religious slaughter, and that such a requirement is compatible with the freedom of religion under Article 10 of the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights. The Court applied a proportionality test, finding that reversible stunning methods (which do not cause the animal's death) represent a proportionate restriction on religious freedom.

## Comparative Analysis

### Federal Protection Gaps

The United States stands out for the absence of federal farm animal welfare standards during the animals' lives on farms. The HMSA covers only slaughter, and the AWA excludes farm animals entirely. South Korea's Animal Protection Act covers farm animals but enforcement is weak. Germany and the EU provide the most comprehensive framework, covering housing, transport, and slaughter with species-specific standards.

### State/Subnational Action

In both the United States and Germany, subnational entities have driven welfare improvements. U.S. state ballot initiatives (particularly California's Proposition 12) have created market-based welfare standards with nationwide effect. German Länder veterinary authorities enforce federal and EU welfare standards, and some Länder have adopted stricter enforcement policies.

### Market-Based vs. Regulatory Approaches

The U.S. experience with Proposition 12 demonstrates a market-based approach: states use their consumer market power to set standards that effectively regulate producers nationwide. The EU approach is directly regulatory: standards are set at the EU level and applied to all producers. South Korea's voluntary welfare certification represents a middle path, using market incentives (premium pricing) to encourage higher welfare without mandating it.

### Ritual Slaughter

The tension between animal welfare and religious freedom is most acute regarding ritual slaughter. The CJEU's Belgium ruling permits member states to require stunning even for religious slaughter, while the ECHR's Cha'are Shalom decision takes a deferential approach. The United States and South Korea both exempt religious slaughter from stunning requirements.
