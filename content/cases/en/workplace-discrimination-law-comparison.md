## Summary

Workplace discrimination law -- the legal framework prohibiting adverse employment decisions based on protected characteristics such as race, sex, disability, age, religion, and sexual orientation -- reflects evolving societal understandings of equality and the varying constitutional and legislative responses of different legal systems. South Korea addresses employment discrimination through a fragmented multi-statute framework comprising the National Human Rights Commission Act (국가인권위원회법, 2001), the Act on Equal Employment and Support for Work-Family Reconciliation (남녀고용평등과 일·가정 양립 지원에 관한 법률, originally enacted 1987), the Act on the Prohibition of Discrimination against Persons with Disabilities (장애인차별금지 및 권리구제 등에 관한 법률, 2007), and the Act on the Prohibition of Age Discrimination in Employment and Elderly Employment Promotion (고용상 연령차별금지 및 고령자고용촉진에 관한 법률, 2008); despite decades of advocacy, South Korea has not enacted a comprehensive anti-discrimination act (포괄적 차별금지법), with bills repeatedly failing due to political opposition centered on sexual orientation protections. The United States operates the most litigious employment discrimination system globally, centered on Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 (42 U.S.C. Section 2000e), the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) of 1990, the Age Discrimination in Employment Act (ADEA) of 1967, and the Supreme Court's landmark decision in Bostock v. Clayton County, 590 U.S. 644 (2020), which extended Title VII's sex discrimination prohibition to cover sexual orientation and gender identity. Germany implements EU anti-discrimination directives through the Allgemeines Gleichbehandlungsgesetz (AGG, General Equal Treatment Act) of 2006, which prohibits discrimination on grounds of race, ethnic origin, sex, religion or belief, disability, age, and sexual orientation, with enforcement through the Antidiskriminierungsstelle des Bundes (Federal Anti-Discrimination Agency). At the European level, the EU has developed the most comprehensive supranational anti-discrimination framework through the Racial Equality Directive 2000/43/EC, the Employment Equality Directive 2000/78/EC, the Gender Equality Directive (recast) 2006/54/EC, and the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights Articles 21 and 23, while the ECHR prohibits discrimination under Article 14 and Protocol 12.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's employment discrimination law is characterized by its fragmented, ground-specific approach, with no single comprehensive anti-discrimination statute.

### Key Statutes

**Act on Equal Employment and Support for Work-Family Reconciliation (남녀고용평등법):** Originally enacted in 1987 as the Equal Employment Act, this statute prohibits sex discrimination in recruitment, hiring, wages, education, promotion, retirement age, and dismissal. It also addresses sexual harassment (Article 12) and provides for maternity/paternity leave protections. Enforcement is through the Ministry of Employment and Labor.

**National Human Rights Commission Act (국가인권위원회법, 2001):** Establishes the National Human Rights Commission of Korea (NHRC, 국가인권위원회) and prohibits discrimination on 19 enumerated grounds including sex, religion, disability, age, social status, place of origin, national origin, appearance, marital status, pregnancy/childbirth, family type, race, skin color, ideology/political opinion, criminal record, sexual orientation, academic background, and medical history. However, the NHRC's authority is limited to investigation, mediation, and non-binding recommendations; it cannot impose penalties or order remedies.

**Disability Discrimination Act (장애인차별금지법, 2007):** Prohibits discrimination against persons with disabilities in employment, education, goods and services, and public facilities. Provides for legally binding corrective orders by the NHRC (uniquely among NHRC-administered statutes) and permits damages claims in court.

**Age Discrimination Act (고용상 연령차별금지법, 2008):** Prohibits age discrimination in recruitment, hiring, wages, and other employment conditions. Exceptions are permitted where age is a bona fide occupational qualification.

### Comprehensive Anti-Discrimination Act Debate

South Korea's most significant legislative gap is the absence of a comprehensive anti-discrimination act (포괄적 차별금지법). Anti-discrimination bills have been introduced in every National Assembly session since 2007 but have repeatedly failed. Opposition has centered primarily on the inclusion of sexual orientation as a protected ground, with conservative religious organizations mounting sustained campaigns against passage. In 2023, a bill introduced by Justice Party Representative Jang Hye-young again failed to advance through committee. The absence of comprehensive legislation leaves significant gaps, particularly for LGBTQ+ workers who lack explicit employment discrimination protections.

### NHRC Complaints

In 2023, the NHRC received approximately 12,500 discrimination complaints, of which approximately 35 percent related to employment. The most frequently cited grounds were disability (28 percent), age (18 percent), and sex (15 percent). Critics note that the NHRC's non-binding recommendations result in compliance rates of only approximately 70 percent.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has developed the most extensively litigated employment discrimination system in the world, centered on federal statutes administered by the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC).

### Federal Statutes

| Statute | Year | Protected Ground | Coverage |
|---------|------|-----------------|----------|
| Title VII, Civil Rights Act | 1964 | Race, color, religion, sex, national origin | 15+ employees |
| Age Discrimination in Employment Act (ADEA) | 1967 | Age (40+) | 20+ employees |
| Rehabilitation Act, Section 501 | 1973 | Disability | Federal government |
| Pregnancy Discrimination Act | 1978 | Pregnancy, childbirth | 15+ employees |
| Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) | 1990 | Disability | 15+ employees |
| Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act (GINA) | 2008 | Genetic information | 15+ employees |
| Pregnant Workers Fairness Act | 2022 | Pregnancy-related conditions (reasonable accommodations) | 15+ employees |

### Bostock v. Clayton County (2020)

The Supreme Court's 6-3 decision in Bostock v. Clayton County, 590 U.S. 644, held that Title VII's prohibition of discrimination "because of sex" encompasses discrimination based on sexual orientation and gender identity. Justice Gorsuch, writing for the majority, reasoned that it is impossible to discriminate against a person for being homosexual or transgender without discriminating against that individual based on sex. The decision extended federal employment discrimination protection to an estimated 7.1 million LGBTQ+ workers.

### Analytical Frameworks

Courts apply different analytical frameworks depending on the type of discrimination alleged:

**McDonnell Douglas Framework (Disparate Treatment):** The complainant must establish a prima facie case (member of protected class, qualified, adverse action, circumstances suggesting discrimination). The employer then offers a legitimate non-discriminatory reason. The complainant must prove this reason is pretextual.

**Griggs Framework (Disparate Impact):** Established in Griggs v. Duke Power Co., 401 U.S. 424 (1971), this framework prohibits facially neutral employment practices that disproportionately affect protected groups, unless the employer demonstrates business necessity.

### Remedies

Title VII caps compensatory and punitive damages based on employer size: $50,000 (15-100 employees), $100,000 (101-200), $200,000 (201-500), $300,000 (500+). The ADA follows the same caps. The ADEA does not permit compensatory or punitive damages but allows liquidated (double) damages for willful violations. State laws may provide uncapped damages.

### EEOC Enforcement

The EEOC received approximately 81,055 charges of employment discrimination in fiscal year 2023, recovering $665 million in monetary relief. The charge process requires administrative exhaustion before filing suit, with the EEOC conducting investigation and attempting conciliation. The EEOC may file suit on behalf of complainants or issue a "right to sue" letter permitting private litigation.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany implemented the EU Employment Equality Directive (2000/78/EC) and the Racial Equality Directive (2000/43/EC) through the Allgemeines Gleichbehandlungsgesetz (AGG, General Equal Treatment Act), enacted on August 14, 2006. The AGG was Germany's first comprehensive anti-discrimination statute covering employment and extends to civil law transactions (housing, goods, services) for race and ethnic origin.

### Protected Grounds (Section 1 AGG)

The AGG prohibits discrimination on the basis of race, ethnic origin, sex, religion or belief, disability, age, and sexual orientation. This list tracks the EU Directives and cannot be expanded at the national level without separate legislation. Notably, the AGG does not include social origin, appearance, or political opinion as protected grounds.

### Forms of Discrimination

| Form | AGG Section | Description |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| Direct discrimination (unmittelbare Benachteiligung) | § 3(1) | Less favorable treatment because of a protected ground |
| Indirect discrimination (mittelbare Benachteiligung) | § 3(2) | Apparently neutral provision disproportionately disadvantaging protected group without objective justification |
| Harassment (Belästigung) | § 3(3) | Unwanted conduct related to protected ground creating hostile, degrading, or humiliating environment |
| Sexual harassment (sexuelle Belästigung) | § 3(4) | Unwanted sexual conduct including comments, gestures, physical contact |
| Instruction to discriminate | § 3(5) | Directing another person to discriminate |

### Burden of Proof (Section 22 AGG)

The AGG adopts the EU Directives' shared burden of proof: if the complainant establishes facts giving rise to a presumption of discrimination (Indizienbeweis), the burden shifts to the respondent to prove that the discrimination did not occur. This is less favorable to claimants than a full reversal of burden but more favorable than requiring the claimant to prove discriminatory intent.

### Remedies (Section 15 AGG)

**Material Damages (§ 15(1)):** Full compensation for material losses resulting from discrimination, subject to proof of fault (intent or negligence) on the employer's part.

**Immaterial Damages (§ 15(2)):** Compensation for non-material harm (Schmerzensgeld) not exceeding three months' salary for hiring discrimination cases. This cap has been criticized as inadequate.

**No Reinstatement (§ 15(6)):** The AGG explicitly provides that discrimination does not give rise to a claim for establishment of an employment relationship -- a complainant denied a job cannot demand hiring. This limitation is controversial as it reduces deterrence.

### Antidiskriminierungsstelle des Bundes (ADS)

The Federal Anti-Discrimination Agency, established under Section 25 AGG, provides information and support to discrimination victims, conducts mediation, commissions research, and publishes regular reports. Unlike the U.S. EEOC, the ADS does not have enforcement power or the ability to file lawsuits. Victims must pursue claims through the Arbeitsgericht (labor court) system.

### Recent Developments

A 2023 reform proposal by the Federal Anti-Discrimination Commissioner sought to expand the AGG's protected grounds to include social status, chronic illness, and family care responsibilities, and to extend the filing deadline from two months (§ 15(4)) to twelve months. The reform remained under legislative consideration as of early 2026.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Anti-Discrimination Directives

**Racial Equality Directive 2000/43/EC:** Prohibits discrimination on grounds of racial or ethnic origin in employment, education, social protection, social advantages, and access to goods and services. Broadest material scope of any EU anti-discrimination directive.

**Employment Equality Directive 2000/78/EC:** Prohibits discrimination on grounds of religion or belief, disability, age, and sexual orientation specifically in the field of employment. Does not extend to goods and services (a proposed horizontal directive has been blocked in Council since 2008).

**Gender Equality Directive (Recast) 2006/54/EC:** Consolidates and updates EU gender equality law in employment, including equal pay, equal treatment in working conditions, occupational social security, and pregnancy/maternity protections.

**Pay Transparency Directive 2023/970:** Adopted on May 10, 2023, this Directive requires employers with 100+ workers to report gender pay gaps, grants job applicants the right to information about pay ranges, and shifts the burden of proof to employers in equal pay disputes. Transposition deadline: June 7, 2026.

### CJEU Landmark Cases

**P v. S and Cornwall County Council (Case C-13/94, 1996):** The Court held that dismissal of a transgender employee on grounds of gender reassignment constitutes sex discrimination under the Equal Treatment Directive.

**Mangold v. Helm (Case C-144/04, 2005):** The Court established that the principle of non-discrimination on grounds of age is a general principle of EU law, applicable even before the transposition deadline of Directive 2000/78/EC.

**Achbita v. G4S (Case C-157/15, 2017) and Bougnaoui v. Micropole (Case C-188/15, 2017):** These companion cases addressed employer policies prohibiting visible religious symbols. In Achbita, the Court held that a neutral dress code prohibiting all visible religious, political, and philosophical signs does not constitute direct discrimination. In Bougnaoui, the Court held that a customer's wish not to have services provided by a worker wearing an Islamic headscarf does not constitute a genuine occupational requirement.

### ECHR

**Article 14:** Prohibits discrimination in the enjoyment of Convention rights. It is not a freestanding equality provision -- it must be invoked in conjunction with another Convention right. Protocol 12 (2000) provides a freestanding discrimination prohibition but has been ratified by only 20 states.

**Eweida and Others v. United Kingdom (Application No. 48420/10, Judgment of January 15, 2013):** Four joined cases addressing religious discrimination in the workplace. The Court found a violation of Article 9 (freedom of religion) for a British Airways employee prohibited from wearing a visible cross, but found no violation for a nurse subject to a jewelry ban, a marriage registrar who refused to perform same-sex civil partnerships, or a counselor who refused to provide services to same-sex couples.

## Comparative Analysis

### Scope of Protection

The EU framework provides the most systematic coverage through harmonized directives, though the long-blocked horizontal directive leaves gaps in non-employment contexts for some grounds. The United States provides robust protection through multiple specific statutes, with Bostock extending coverage to LGBTQ+ workers. South Korea's fragmented approach creates both overlap and gaps, most critically the absence of explicit sexual orientation protection in employment law. Germany's AGG closely tracks EU Directives but its limited remedies have been criticized as undermining effectiveness.

### Enforcement Models

The United States' EEOC model combines administrative investigation with litigation authority, creating a powerful enforcement mechanism. South Korea's NHRC model is weak by comparison, limited to non-binding recommendations. Germany's ADS has even less enforcement power, serving primarily as an advisory and support body. The EU Directives require member states to establish equality bodies but leave significant discretion in defining their powers.

### Remedies and Deterrence

The United States provides the strongest remedial framework, including compensatory and punitive damages (though capped), attorney's fees, and equitable relief. Germany's AGG provides only limited damages with no reinstatement right. South Korea's primary mechanism (NHRC recommendations) carries no binding legal force, though court actions for damages are available. The effectiveness of anti-discrimination law correlates strongly with the robustness of remedies available.
