## Summary

Murder, the unlawful killing of another human being with intent, is universally condemned but defined and punished in strikingly different ways across legal systems. South Korea classifies murder into five severity tiers with penalties from 3 years to life imprisonment, while maintaining a de facto moratorium on the death penalty since 1998. Germany draws a rigid line between Mord (murder, mandatory life sentence) and Totschlag (manslaughter, 5-15 years) based on the presence of specific aggravating characteristics. The United States fragments murder law across 50 state jurisdictions, preserving capital punishment in some while abolishing it in others. The European Court of Human Rights, meanwhile, imposes positive obligations on states to protect life and investigate deaths under Article 2 of the Convention.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Under Article 250 of the Korean Criminal Act, murder carries a penalty ranging from 5 years imprisonment to the death penalty. However, the Korean Sentencing Commission (양형위원회) provides detailed guidelines that categorize homicides into five tiers:

| Category | Description | Penalty Range |
|----------|-------------|---------------|
| Type 1 | Basic murder (e.g., mercy killing, provocation) | 3-5 years |
| Type 2 | Ordinary murder | 7-12 years |
| Type 3 | Aggravated murder | 10-16 years |
| Type 4 | Heinous murder (e.g., multiple victims, cruelty) | 17-22 years |
| Type 5 | Extremely aggravated murder | 20-25 years / Life |

### Key Principles

**Dolus eventualis (미필적 고의):** Korean courts determine murder intent by examining motive, weapon type, targeted body parts, number of strikes, and post-crime conduct. A defendant need not have desired the death — awareness that death was a likely consequence suffices.

**Omission liability (부작위범):** In the landmark Sewol Ferry case (Supreme Court 2015Do6809), the captain was convicted of murder by omission for failing to order passenger evacuation. The court held that persons with a legal duty of care bear criminal liability equivalent to active killing when they deliberately fail to act.

**Statute of limitations:** In 2015, Korea abolished the 25-year statute of limitations for first-degree murder, allowing prosecution regardless of elapsed time.

### Death Penalty Status

South Korea retains the death penalty in law but has not carried out an execution since December 1997. President Kim Dae-jung's moratorium remains in effect, making Korea a de facto abolitionist state. As of 2025, approximately 59 inmates remain on death row.

## United States

### Legal Framework

U.S. murder law varies significantly by state but generally divides into:

- **First-degree murder:** Premeditated, deliberate killing. Penalties range from 25 years to life imprisonment or death.
- **Second-degree murder:** Intentional but not premeditated. Typically 15 years to life.
- **Felony murder:** Death occurring during commission of a dangerous felony, even without intent to kill.
- **Manslaughter:** Voluntary (heat of passion) or involuntary (criminal negligence).

### Landmark Cases

**Glossip v. Oklahoma, 604 U.S. ___ (2025):** The Supreme Court reversed Richard Glossip's murder conviction and death sentence, finding that prosecutors violated due process by allowing the key witness to testify falsely about never seeing a psychiatrist, while suppressing evidence of his bipolar diagnosis and lithium prescription. Justice Sotomayor, writing for the majority with Roberts, Kagan, Kavanaugh, and Jackson, held that the concealed psychiatric evidence was material because it undermined the sole witness connecting Glossip to the murder-for-hire.

**Dempsey v. State (Georgia, 2026):** The Georgia Supreme Court upheld a felony murder conviction based entirely on circumstantial and digital evidence — notably the defendant's Instagram account linking him to a drug deal that preceded two deaths. No eyewitness identified the defendant; no physical evidence placed him at the scene.

### Death Penalty

As of 2025, 27 states retain the death penalty, though actual executions are concentrated in a handful of states (Texas, Oklahoma, Florida). Federal executions resumed in 2020 after a 17-year hiatus but were again paused under the Biden administration.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German criminal law draws a uniquely sharp distinction between murder (Mord, §211 StGB) and manslaughter (Totschlag, §212 StGB). Unlike common law systems, the distinction rests not on premeditation but on the presence of specific "murder characteristics."

**Mord (§211) — Mandatory life imprisonment** when the killer acts:
- Out of bloodlust, sexual gratification, greed, or other base motives
- Perfidiously (heimtückisch) or cruelly
- By means constituting public danger
- To enable or conceal another crime

**Totschlag (§212) — 5 to 15 years**, or life in particularly severe cases. Lesser cases under §213 carry 1-10 years.

### Key Differences from Common Law

The German system has no "degrees" of murder. Instead, all intentional killings are Totschlag unless one of the enumerated murder characteristics applies — at which point the sentence jumps mandatorily to life imprisonment with no judicial discretion. This binary system has faced sustained criticism from legal scholars who argue it produces unjust outcomes, particularly in cases of battered women who kill abusers "perfidiously" (e.g., while asleep) and receive automatic life sentences.

### Reform Debate

An expert commission convened in 2014 recommended replacing the rigid §211/§212 system with a unified homicide provision allowing graduated sentencing. As of 2025, no legislative reform has been enacted.

## European Court of Human Rights

### Article 2: Right to Life

The ECHR imposes obligations on signatory states that extend beyond merely prohibiting state-sanctioned killing:

1. **Negative obligation:** States must not take life except in strictly defined circumstances (defense against unlawful violence, lawful arrest, quelling insurrection).
2. **Positive obligation (Osman duty):** States must take preventive measures to protect individuals whose lives are at known and immediate risk. Failure to act can itself violate Article 2.
3. **Procedural obligation:** States must conduct effective investigations into suspicious deaths.

### Landmark Cases

**McCann v. United Kingdom (1995):** British SAS soldiers shot three IRA members in Gibraltar. The Court found that while the soldiers' honest belief in an imminent threat was reasonable, the overall planning and control of the operation used disproportionate force, violating Article 2.

**Osman v. United Kingdom (1998):** A stalker killed the applicant's husband after police failed to act on repeated death threats. The Court established that states have a positive duty to take preventive operational measures to protect life when authorities know, or ought to know, of a real and immediate threat.

**Akkoç v. Turkey (2000):** The applicant's husband received death threats that were reported to and ignored by authorities before he was shot dead. The Court found a breach of the positive obligation under Article 2.
