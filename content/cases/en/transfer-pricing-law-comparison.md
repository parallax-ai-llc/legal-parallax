## Summary

Transfer pricing -- the pricing of transactions between related entities within a multinational enterprise group -- is the most significant and contentious area of international tax law, with an estimated 60 percent of global trade occurring between related parties and transfer pricing adjustments representing the largest category of international tax disputes by value. South Korea regulates transfer pricing under the International Tax Coordination Act (국제조세조정에 관한 법률), which requires that transactions between related parties conform to the arm's length principle (정상가격 원칙) and mandates comprehensive documentation including a Local File, Master File, and Country-by-Country Report (CbCR) for qualifying multinational groups, with the National Tax Service (국세청) maintaining an active Advance Pricing Agreement (APA) program and conducting aggressive transfer pricing audits that have resulted in some of the largest tax assessments in Korean history. The United States was the first jurisdiction to develop detailed transfer pricing regulations under Section 482 of the Internal Revenue Code and the accompanying Treasury Regulations (Treas. Reg. 1.482), introducing the concept of "commensurate with income" for intangible property transfers in the Tax Reform Act of 1986 and the "best method rule" in the 1994 final regulations, and recently expanding transfer pricing enforcement through the IRS Large Business and International Division. Germany applies the arm's length principle through Section 1 of the Außensteuergesetz (AStG), supplemented by the Funktionsverlagerungsverordnung (FVerlV) specifically addressing the transfer of business functions, and the Gewinnabgrenzungsaufzeichnungsverordnung (GAufzV) prescribing documentation requirements; German courts, particularly the Bundesfinanzhof (Federal Fiscal Court), have developed significant jurisprudence on the application of the arm's length standard. The OECD Transfer Pricing Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises and Tax Administrations, most recently updated in 2022, provide the international consensus framework, while the EU has supplemented this through the Joint Transfer Pricing Forum, the Arbitration Convention (90/436/EEC), and increasingly through state aid enforcement challenging transfer pricing rulings that deviate from the arm's length principle.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's transfer pricing regime is governed by the International Tax Coordination Act (국제조세조정에 관한 법률, Act No. 19929) and its Enforcement Decree. The Act adopts the arm's length principle (정상가격 원칙) as the fundamental standard for pricing transactions between related parties (특수관계인). A "related party" is defined broadly to include entities where one party directly or indirectly holds 50 percent or more of voting rights or exercises effective control.

### Transfer Pricing Methods

The Act recognizes the following methods, in alignment with the OECD Guidelines:

| Method | Korean Term |
|--------|-------------|
| Comparable Uncontrolled Price (CUP) | 비교가능 제3자 가격방법 |
| Resale Price Method (RPM) | 재판매가격방법 |
| Cost Plus Method (CPM) | 원가가산방법 |
| Transactional Net Margin Method (TNMM) | 거래순이익률방법 |
| Profit Split Method (PSM) | 이익분할방법 |
| Other reasonable methods | 기타 합리적 방법 |

There is no statutory hierarchy among methods; taxpayers must select the most appropriate method given the facts and circumstances.

### Documentation Requirements

Following OECD BEPS Action 13, South Korea implemented the three-tiered documentation approach:

- **Master File (통합기업보고서):** Required for groups with consolidated revenue exceeding 100 billion KRW.
- **Local File (개별기업보고서):** Required for entities with related-party transactions exceeding 5 billion KRW annually.
- **Country-by-Country Report (국제거래정보통합보고서):** Required for groups with consolidated revenue exceeding 100 billion KRW.

### Enforcement and Notable Cases

The Korean NTS has been increasingly aggressive in transfer pricing audits, particularly targeting technology companies, pharmaceutical firms, and financial institutions. In a high-profile case, the NTS assessed approximately 800 billion KRW in additional taxes against a major Korean conglomerate's subsidiaries for underpricing technology licenses transferred to overseas affiliates. The Tax Tribunal and courts have developed substantial precedent on the selection of comparable companies and appropriate profit level indicators.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Section 482 of the Internal Revenue Code grants the IRS authority to "distribute, apportion, or allocate gross income, deductions, credits, or allowances" among commonly controlled organizations "to prevent evasion of taxes or clearly to reflect the income" of each entity. The comprehensive Treasury Regulations under Section 1.482 provide detailed guidance on methods, comparability analysis, and specific rules for tangible property, intangible property, services, and cost sharing arrangements.

### Best Method Rule

The U.S. regulations apply a "best method rule" (Treas. Reg. 1.482-1(c)), requiring the taxpayer to use the method that provides the most reliable measure of arm's length result given the facts and circumstances. Unlike some jurisdictions, the U.S. does not impose a formal hierarchy among methods, though the regulations provide extensive guidance on factors affecting reliability.

### Intangible Property -- "Commensurate with Income"

The Tax Reform Act of 1986 added the "commensurate with income" standard to Section 482, requiring that the income from intangible property transfers be commensurate with the income attributable to the intangible. This standard, unique to the United States, allows the IRS to make periodic adjustments to royalty rates to reflect actual income generated, even if the original pricing was arm's length at the time of the transaction.

### Cost Sharing Arrangements

Treas. Reg. 1.482-7 provides detailed rules for qualified cost sharing arrangements (QCSAs), under which related parties share the costs and risks of developing intangible property. The regulations require "platform contribution transactions" (PCTs) to be priced at arm's length, capturing the value of pre-existing intangible property contributed to the arrangement.

### Landmark Cases

**Altera Corp. v. Commissioner, 941 F.3d 1200 (9th Cir. 2019):** The Ninth Circuit upheld the Treasury Regulation requiring stock-based compensation costs to be shared in cost sharing arrangements, reversing the Tax Court's invalidation of the regulation.

**Medtronic v. Commissioner, Tax Court Docket No. 6944-11 (ongoing):** A landmark case involving the pricing of intercompany royalties for medical device technology transferred to a Puerto Rico subsidiary, with the IRS proposing adjustments of over $1.4 billion.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's transfer pricing framework is based on Section 1 AStG, which requires that cross-border transactions between related parties be priced in accordance with the arm's length principle (Fremdvergleichsgrundsatz). The AStG was significantly amended in 2022, effective June 2022, to modernize the transfer pricing provisions and align them more closely with the OECD Guidelines.

### Function Transfer Rules (Funktionsverlagerung)

Germany's most distinctive transfer pricing provision is the Funktionsverlagerungsverordnung (FVerlV), which addresses the transfer of business functions from Germany to related entities abroad. Under these rules, a "transfer package" (Transferpaket) approach applies: the entire function -- including tangible and intangible assets, opportunities, risks, and going concern value -- must be valued and compensated at arm's length. This approach prevents piecemeal transfers that might undervalue the overall function.

| Element | Requirement |
|---------|-------------|
| Definition | Transfer of a function including associated opportunities and risks |
| Valuation | Hypothetical arm's length test; discounted cash flow analysis |
| Escape clauses | Not applicable if the function transfer involves only individual assets of limited value |
| Documentation | Detailed documentation of function transfer including business reasons and valuation methodology |

### Documentation (GAufzV)

The Gewinnabgrenzungsaufzeichnungsverordnung (GAufzV) prescribes documentation requirements for transfer pricing:

- Documentation must be prepared contemporaneously and presented within 30 days of a tax authority request (60 days for extraordinary transactions).
- Failure to prepare adequate documentation shifts the burden of proof to the taxpayer and allows the tax authority to estimate income, adjusting to the upper end of the arm's length range.

### Bundesfinanzhof Jurisprudence

The BFH has addressed transfer pricing in numerous decisions:

**BFH I R 73/11 (2014):** Addressed the standard for determining the arm's length range and held that the median of the interquartile range should not be automatically applied where the taxpayer's result falls outside the range.

**BFH I R 4/17 (2021):** Clarified the interaction between the hypothetical arm's length test for function transfers and the standard comparability analysis under the OECD Guidelines.

## ECHR & EU

### OECD Transfer Pricing Guidelines

The OECD Transfer Pricing Guidelines, first published in 1995 and most recently updated in January 2022, provide the international consensus framework. Key developments include:

- **2010 Revision:** Comprehensive update elevating the Transactional Net Margin Method and Profit Split Method.
- **2015 BEPS Reports (Actions 8-10):** Fundamental revisions addressing intangibles (DEMPE analysis), risk and capital, and high-risk transactions.
- **2017 Update:** Incorporated BEPS revisions; added guidance on hard-to-value intangibles and commodity transactions.
- **2022 Update:** Added guidance on financial transactions (Pillar I Amount B simplified approach for baseline marketing and distribution activities).

### EU State Aid and Transfer Pricing

The European Commission has used state aid rules to challenge member state transfer pricing rulings that deviate from the arm's length principle:

**Apple/Ireland:** The Commission found that Ireland's tax rulings allowed Apple to allocate almost all profits to a "head office" with no employees or physical presence, resulting in an effective tax rate of 0.005 percent in 2014. The CJEU upheld the decision in September 2024.

**Fiat/Luxembourg:** The General Court upheld the Commission's finding that Luxembourg's transfer pricing ruling for Fiat's financing company deviated from the arm's length principle by applying an inappropriately narrow equity base for calculating remuneration.

**Amazon/Luxembourg:** The Commission found that Luxembourg granted selective tax advantages through a transfer pricing ruling that undervalued a royalty paid by Amazon's Luxembourg operating company to a holding entity.

### EU Joint Transfer Pricing Forum

The EU Joint Transfer Pricing Forum (JTPF), established in 2002, provides non-binding guidance on transfer pricing issues within the EU, including guidelines on low-value intra-group services, documentation, and compensating adjustments. The Forum complements the binding dispute resolution mechanisms of the Arbitration Convention and the Dispute Resolution Directive.

### Arbitration Convention and Dispute Resolution

The Arbitration Convention (90/436/EEC) provides a specific mechanism for resolving transfer pricing disputes between EU member states, with mandatory arbitration if mutual agreement is not reached within two years. The EU Dispute Resolution Directive (2017/1852) extends binding resolution to all double taxation disputes, not just transfer pricing.

## Comparative Analysis

### Methodological Approaches

While all jurisdictions subscribe to the arm's length principle, implementation differs. The United States' "best method rule" is the most flexible, allowing any method that produces the most reliable result. South Korea and Germany follow the OECD Guidelines more closely, with Germany adding the unique "hypothetical arm's length test" for function transfers. The EU increasingly influences methodological choices through state aid review of transfer pricing rulings.

### Intangible Property Treatment

The treatment of intangible property transfers represents the greatest divergence. The U.S. "commensurate with income" standard allows retrospective adjustments, creating uncertainty but capturing actual value. Germany's function transfer rules value the entire "transfer package" including going concern value. South Korea and the OECD DEMPE framework focus on which entities perform development, enhancement, maintenance, protection, and exploitation functions.

### Documentation Burden

All jurisdictions have converged on the OECD three-tiered documentation approach (Master File, Local File, CbCR) following BEPS Action 13. However, thresholds and timelines differ: Germany requires presentation within 30 days of request, while South Korea provides annual filing deadlines. The U.S. relies more heavily on existing reporting forms (5471, 5472) supplemented by the penalty protection documentation requirements of Section 6662(e).

### Enforcement Intensity

Transfer pricing enforcement intensity has increased globally. The United States, with the IRS Large Business and International Division, conducts extensive transfer pricing audits. South Korea's NTS has become one of the most aggressive transfer pricing enforcers in Asia. Germany's approach is decentralized across state tax offices but increasingly coordinated at the federal level through the BZSt.
