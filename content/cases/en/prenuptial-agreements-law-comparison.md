## Summary

Prenuptial agreements -- contracts entered into by prospective spouses before marriage to define property rights, financial obligations, and the division of assets upon divorce or death -- occupy a contested intersection of contract law and family law, with jurisdictions differing fundamentally on whether and to what extent the autonomy principles of contract law should govern intimate domestic arrangements. South Korea recognizes prenuptial property agreements (혼전 재산계약) under Article 829 of the Civil Act (민법), which allows prospective spouses to agree on their matrimonial property regime prior to marriage, with the agreement becoming effective upon registration before the marriage; however, prenuptial agreements remain uncommon in Korean practice, with estimates suggesting fewer than 1 percent of marriages involve a formal prenuptial contract, partly due to cultural stigma and partly due to the limited scope of matters that may be addressed -- Korean law restricts prenuptial agreements primarily to property regime modifications and does not permit advance waiver of the property division right (재산분할청구권) upon divorce. The United States has progressively embraced prenuptial agreements since the landmark Florida Supreme Court decision in Posner v. Posner (1970), with the Uniform Premarital Agreement Act (UPAA, 1983) and the Uniform Premarital and Marital Agreements Act (UPMAA, 2012) providing model legislation adopted in various forms by a majority of states; enforceability requirements include voluntary execution, fair and reasonable disclosure, and absence of unconscionability, with states divided on whether substantive unconscionability alone suffices to invalidate an agreement or whether procedural defects must also be present. Germany has a well-established tradition of marital agreements (Ehevertrag) under BGB Section 1408, which must be notarized (notarielle Beurkundung) to be valid and may address the matrimonial property regime, post-divorce maintenance, and pension equalization; the Bundesgerichtshof has developed significant judicial control of marital agreements through the Kernbereich (core area) doctrine, under which agreements that cumulatively eliminate all financial protections may be set aside as contrary to good morals (sittenwidrig) or subject to equitable adjustment. The European framework includes the EU Matrimonial Property Regulation (2016/1103), which permits spouses to choose the applicable law governing their property regime, the Hague Convention on the Law Applicable to Matrimonial Property Regimes (1978), and comparative research by the Commission on European Family Law promoting the recognition of party autonomy in matrimonial property matters while establishing minimum protections.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

**Article 829 Civil Act (민법 제829조):** Prospective spouses may enter into an agreement regarding their matrimonial property before marriage. The agreement must be registered before the marriage takes place to be effective. If no agreement is registered, the default separate property system (별산제, Article 830) applies.

### Scope and Limitations

Korean prenuptial agreements may address:

| Permitted | Not Permitted |
|-----------|--------------|
| Choice of property regime | Advance waiver of property division right (Art. 839-2) |
| Designation of separate vs. jointly owned property | Predetermination of child custody |
| Management arrangements for specific assets | Waiver of consolation money (위자료) claim |
| Debt allocation | Agreements contrary to public order |

### Enforceability Requirements

| Requirement | Detail |
|-------------|--------|
| Timing | Must be concluded and registered before the marriage |
| Registration | Must be registered with the family court or relevant authority |
| Capacity | Both parties must have full legal capacity |
| Voluntariness | Must be entered into freely |
| Post-marriage modification | Not permitted after marriage (Article 829(2)) |

The prohibition on post-marriage modification (Article 829(2)) is a distinctive and restrictive feature of Korean law, preventing spouses from adjusting their property regime after marriage even by mutual agreement.

### Practical Rarity

Prenuptial agreements remain extremely uncommon in South Korea. Cultural factors include:

- **Social stigma:** Discussing financial arrangements before marriage is widely perceived as indicative of distrust and incompatible with the emotional basis of marriage.
- **Gender dynamics:** The traditional expectation that women contribute a substantial wedding payment (혼수) creates different financial dynamics than in Western contexts.
- **Legal limitations:** The narrow scope of permitted agreements reduces the practical utility of prenuptial contracts.
- **Default regime adequacy:** The separate property system and judicial property division upon divorce are seen as adequate by most couples.

### Judicial Treatment

There is limited Korean case law on prenuptial agreements due to their rarity. The Supreme Court has addressed the broader principle that the property division right under Article 839-2 is an independent right arising from the marriage relationship that cannot be waived by prior agreement (대법원 2000므837, 2002). This significantly limits the utility of prenuptial agreements for wealthy individuals seeking to protect assets.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Prenuptial agreements in the United States evolved from being largely unenforceable (as contrary to public policy by encouraging divorce) to widespread acceptance following Posner v. Posner, 233 So.2d 381 (Fla. 1970), which recognized their validity when entered into freely and with adequate disclosure.

### Uniform Premarital Agreement Act (UPAA, 1983)

The UPAA has been adopted in 28 states. Key provisions:

**Section 3 (Content):** Parties may contract regarding property rights and obligations, disposition of property upon death, modification or elimination of spousal support (subject to state limits), choice of law, and any other matter not violating public policy or criminal law.

**Section 6 (Enforceability):** An agreement is unenforceable if:
- It was not voluntary, or
- It was unconscionable when executed AND the party seeking to avoid it was not provided fair and reasonable disclosure and did not voluntarily waive disclosure

### Uniform Premarital and Marital Agreements Act (UPMAA, 2012)

The UPMAA updated the UPAA and extended coverage to postnuptial agreements. Key changes:

| UPAA (1983) | UPMAA (2012) |
|-------------|-------------|
| Prenuptial agreements only | Prenuptial and postnuptial agreements |
| Unconscionability + lack of disclosure | Unconscionability OR involuntariness (independent grounds) |
| No independent counsel requirement | Encourages independent legal representation |
| No waiting period | No specific waiting period but voluntariness emphasized |

### State Variations

| Issue | Majority Rule | Notable Exceptions |
|-------|--------------|-------------------|
| Spousal support waiver | Permitted (subject to unconscionability) | California: may not waive entirely if unconscionable at enforcement |
| Sunset clauses | Enforceable | Varies |
| Disclosure standard | Fair and reasonable | Connecticut: no statutory requirement |
| Independent counsel | Encouraged but not required | California: required if waiving spousal support |
| Consideration | Marriage itself is adequate | Some states require additional consideration for postnuptial |

### Key Cases

**In re Marriage of Bonds, 24 Cal.4th 1 (2000):** The California Supreme Court enforced a prenuptial agreement signed by baseball player Barry Bonds and his wife, despite the wife's claim that she did not fully understand it and had no independent counsel, finding that voluntariness is assessed under the totality of circumstances.

**DeMatteo v. DeMatteo, 436 Mass. 18 (2002):** The Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court established a two-stage review: the agreement must have been fair and reasonable at the time of execution, and enforcement must not be unconscionable at the time of divorce.

**Simeone v. Simeone, 581 A.2d 162 (Pa. 1990):** The Pennsylvania Supreme Court rejected the traditional paternalistic approach to prenuptial agreements, holding that they should be evaluated under ordinary contract principles without special protections for the less financially sophisticated party.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German marital agreements (Ehevertrag) are governed by BGB Section 1408 and must be concluded in notarized form (notarielle Beurkundung, BGB Section 1410). They may be entered into before or during the marriage.

### Scope

| Subject | Modifiable by Agreement |
|---------|------------------------|
| Matrimonial property regime | Yes (choice of Zugewinngemeinschaft, Gütertrennung, or Gütergemeinschaft) |
| Post-divorce maintenance | Yes (but subject to Kernbereich limits) |
| Pension equalization (Versorgungsausgleich) | Yes (but subject to court review; BGB Section 1408(2)) |
| Inheritance rights | Partially (through combined Erbvertrag) |
| Child support | No |
| Custody | No |

### Notarization Requirement

The notarization requirement (Beurkundungspflicht) serves multiple protective functions:

- **Documentation:** Creates a clear, authentic record of the agreement.
- **Counseling:** The notary is required to advise both parties independently, explain the legal consequences of the agreement, and ensure that both parties understand its implications.
- **Warning function (Warnfunktion):** The formality of the notarial process alerts parties to the significance of the agreement.
- **Evidence:** The notarial deed constitutes prima facie evidence of its contents.

### Kernbereich (Core Area) Doctrine

The BGH developed the Kernbereich doctrine through a series of decisions beginning with BGH XII ZR 265/99 (March 11, 2004). The doctrine establishes:

**Three-Tier Hierarchy of Protection:**

| Tier | Protected Interest | Agreement Restriction |
|------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Core (Kernbereich) | Child-related maintenance (Betreuungsunterhalt); age/illness maintenance | Waiver most strongly disfavored |
| Middle area | Pension equalization; unemployment maintenance | Waiver possible but subject to scrutiny |
| Outer area | Property equalization (Zugewinnausgleich) | Waiver generally permissible |

**Two-Stage Judicial Review:**

1. **Wirksamkeitskontrolle (Validity Control):** Is the agreement void under BGB Section 138 (Sittenwidrigkeit -- contrary to good morals) because it imposes a one-sided burden resulting from unequal bargaining power?

2. **Ausübungskontrolle (Exercise Control):** Even if valid at formation, is enforcement inequitable under BGB Section 242 (Treu und Glauben -- good faith) due to changed circumstances that the agreement did not anticipate?

### Practical Application

Approximately 10-12 percent of German marriages involve a marital agreement. Common scenarios include:

- Business owners protecting business assets from equalization
- Spouses with significantly disparate wealth seeking Gütertrennung
- Cross-border marriages requiring clarity on applicable law
- Second marriages where both spouses have separate property from first marriages

## ECHR & EU

### EU Matrimonial Property Regulation (2016/1103)

The Regulation permits spouses to choose the applicable law governing their matrimonial property regime (Article 22), enabling cross-border couples to select the law most favorable to their agreement. This choice-of-law provision effectively facilitates prenuptial agreements by allowing parties to choose a legal system that best accommodates their arrangements.

### Hague Convention on Matrimonial Property Regimes (1978)

The Hague Convention of March 14, 1978, on the Law Applicable to Matrimonial Property Regimes (in force in France, Luxembourg, and the Netherlands) provides conflict-of-law rules for matrimonial property, including recognition of party autonomy in choosing the applicable law.

### ECHR Considerations

The ECHR has not directly addressed prenuptial agreements but relevant principles include:

- **Article 8 (Private and Family Life):** Prenuptial agreements that affect family arrangements may engage Article 8 rights, particularly where enforcement produces significant disparities in post-divorce living standards.
- **Article 1 Protocol 1 (Property):** Property rights arising from marriage may be affected by prenuptial agreements, and states must ensure that their enforcement regimes provide adequate protection.
- **Article 14 (Non-discrimination):** Prenuptial agreements that discriminate on the basis of gender may violate Article 14, though parties generally have freedom to agree on terms.

### Comparative Trends in Europe

| Country | Prenuptial Agreement Status | Key Features |
|---------|---------------------------|--------------|
| Germany | Recognized; notarization required | Kernbereich judicial control |
| France | Recognized; notarization required | Choice among statutory regimes |
| England & Wales | Not binding contracts; "persuasive" weight since Radmacher v. Granatino [2010] | Supreme Court gave significant weight |
| Netherlands | Recognized; notarization required | Default: limited community of property (since 2018) |
| Sweden | Recognized; written and registered | May cover property only |
| Italy | Recognized; notarization required | Choice between community and separation |

**Radmacher v. Granatino [2010] UKSC 42:** While not an ECHR case, this UK Supreme Court decision significantly advanced prenuptial agreement recognition in a common law jurisdiction that had traditionally refused to enforce them, holding that the court should give effect to a nuptial agreement that is freely entered into by each party with a full appreciation of its implications unless in the circumstances prevailing it would not be fair to hold the parties to their agreement.

## Comparative Analysis

### Formality Requirements

Germany imposes the most rigorous formal requirements (notarization), which also provides built-in consumer protection through the notary's advisory role. The United States generally requires only a written document with signatures, though some states require witnesses or notarization. South Korea requires registration before the marriage. The German approach best protects parties from hasty or poorly understood agreements, while the U.S. approach maximizes accessibility.

### Scope Limitations

All jurisdictions prohibit prenuptial waiver of child support, reflecting the universal principle that children's interests are not subject to parental bargaining. Spousal maintenance waiver is most restricted in South Korea (not effectively available) and most permissive in the United States (available in most states subject to unconscionability review). Germany occupies a middle position with its Kernbereich hierarchy distinguishing between different types of maintenance.

### Judicial Control Mechanisms

The three jurisdictions employ different mechanisms for judicial control. Germany's two-stage Kernbereich analysis (validity control + exercise control) is the most sophisticated. U.S. courts apply unconscionability analysis, with variation between procedural and substantive unconscionability requirements. South Korea's limited case law suggests reliance on the non-waivability of statutory rights rather than developed judicial control doctrine.

### Cultural Dimensions

Prenuptial agreement prevalence varies dramatically due to cultural factors. South Korea's very low usage (<1%) reflects cultural attitudes that prioritize the romantic and communal aspects of marriage over individual financial planning. The United States' moderate and growing usage (10-15%) reflects the influence of high-profile celebrity prenuptials and increasing acceptance of financial planning within marriage. Germany's similar moderate usage (10-12%) reflects a pragmatic legal culture facilitated by the notarial system.
