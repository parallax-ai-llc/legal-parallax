## Summary

The jury system -- the practice of empaneling lay citizens to determine questions of fact or render verdicts in legal proceedings -- represents one of the most fundamental divergences in procedural design across modern legal systems. South Korea introduced a unique citizen participation system (국민참여재판) through the Act on Citizen Participation in Criminal Trials (국민의 형사재판 참여에 관한 법률) in 2008, which employs advisory juries whose verdicts are not binding on the presiding judge, a compromise reflecting the civil law tradition's emphasis on professional adjudication while incorporating democratic participation values. The United States maintains the most robust jury trial system in the world, with the Sixth Amendment guaranteeing the right to a jury trial in criminal cases and the Seventh Amendment preserving the right in civil cases exceeding twenty dollars; the Supreme Court's incorporation jurisprudence in Duncan v. Louisiana (1968) extended the criminal jury right to state proceedings, and subsequent decisions in Apprendi v. New Jersey (2000) and Blakely v. Washington (2004) reinforced the jury's role as the sole finder of facts that increase criminal penalties beyond statutory maximums. Germany operates an entirely judge-centered system following the abolition of traditional juries (Schwurgerichte) in 1924, replaced by mixed courts (Schöffengerichte) in which two lay judges (Schöffen) sit alongside one or three professional judges to deliberate and decide cases jointly. At the European level, the European Convention on Human Rights does not mandate jury trials, and the European Court of Human Rights has consistently held that Article 6 (right to a fair trial) is compatible with various adjudicative models, though it has imposed transparency requirements on jury systems -- notably in Taxquet v. Belgium (2010), where the Grand Chamber held that unreasoned jury verdicts may violate Article 6 if the accused cannot understand the basis for conviction.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's citizen participation trial system (국민참여재판) was established by the Act on Citizen Participation in Criminal Trials (국민의 형사재판 참여에 관한 법률, Act No. 8495), enacted on June 1, 2007, and implemented on January 1, 2008. The system was introduced as a five-year pilot program and made permanent through amendment in 2012. It represents a significant departure for a civil law jurisdiction that historically relied exclusively on professional judges.

The Act applies to serious criminal offenses tried in district courts, including crimes punishable by death, life imprisonment, or imprisonment for a minimum term exceeding one year, as well as certain designated offenses. Participation is elective -- the defendant must affirmatively request a citizen participation trial, and the court retains discretion to deny the request if participation would substantially delay proceedings or if the case involves issues of national security.

### Jury Composition and Selection

| Case Type | Number of Citizen Jurors |
|-----------|------------------------|
| Cases eligible for death penalty or life imprisonment | 9 jurors |
| Cases with contested facts | 7 jurors |
| Cases with admitted facts or simple issues | 5 jurors |

Citizen jurors are selected from the resident registration system. Candidates must be Korean citizens aged 20 or older (reduced from 20 to 18 following the 2020 amendment to the Public Official Election Act). Exclusions apply to legal professionals, law enforcement officers, active military personnel, and persons with felony convictions. The selection process involves questionnaires and voir dire conducted by the presiding judge, with limited peremptory challenges available to both prosecution and defense.

### Advisory Nature of Verdicts

The most distinctive feature of the Korean system is that jury verdicts are advisory (권고적 효력). The presiding judge is not bound by the jury's determination of guilt or innocence and may enter a different verdict with written justification. In practice, judges concur with jury verdicts in approximately 90 percent of cases, according to data from the Supreme Court of Korea. When the jury reaches a unanimous verdict on guilt, the judge must give that finding significant weight. When the verdict is by majority, the judge has broader discretion.

This advisory mechanism was a deliberate constitutional compromise. The Constitution of the Republic of Korea (대한민국 헌법) Article 27(1) guarantees the right to trial by a judge "qualified under the Constitution and the law," which the Constitutional Court interpreted as potentially precluding binding lay adjudication. The advisory model preserves judicial authority while introducing democratic participation.

### Landmark Case: Constitutional Court Decision 2009Hun-Ba17 (2015)

The Constitutional Court of Korea upheld the constitutionality of the citizen participation system in a 2015 decision, finding that the advisory nature of jury verdicts did not violate the constitutional right to a trial by judges. The Court emphasized that the system enhances judicial transparency and public trust without displacing professional adjudication.

### Participation Statistics

Since implementation in 2008, citizen participation trials have been requested in a growing number of cases, though they remain a small fraction of total criminal proceedings. The Supreme Court of Korea reported that approximately 2,500 citizen participation trials were conducted between 2008 and 2024, with conviction rates comparable to bench trials. The acquittal rate in citizen participation trials has been marginally higher than in bench trials, prompting scholarly debate about the system's impact on prosecutorial standards.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States maintains the most extensive jury trial system in the world, rooted in constitutional guarantees predating the nation's founding.

**Sixth Amendment (1791):** "In all criminal prosecutions, the accused shall enjoy the right to a speedy and public trial, by an impartial jury of the State and district wherein the crime shall have been committed." The Supreme Court incorporated this right against the states in Duncan v. Louisiana, 391 U.S. 145 (1968), holding that the right to a jury trial in criminal cases is "fundamental to the American scheme of justice."

**Seventh Amendment (1791):** "In suits at common law, where the value in controversy shall exceed twenty dollars, the right of trial by jury shall be preserved." This right has not been incorporated against the states, and state civil jury trial rights derive from state constitutions.

### Key Supreme Court Jurisprudence

| Case | Year | Holding |
|------|------|---------|
| Duncan v. Louisiana | 1968 | Right to criminal jury trial incorporated against states via Fourteenth Amendment |
| Williams v. Florida | 1970 | Six-person jury permissible in state criminal trials |
| Apprendi v. New Jersey | 2000 | Any fact (other than prior conviction) that increases penalty beyond statutory maximum must be submitted to jury and proved beyond reasonable doubt |
| Blakely v. Washington | 2004 | Extended Apprendi to state sentencing guidelines |
| United States v. Booker | 2005 | Federal Sentencing Guidelines rendered advisory to comply with Sixth Amendment |
| Ramos v. Louisiana | 2020 | Sixth Amendment requires unanimous jury verdicts in state criminal trials |

### Jury Size and Composition

Federal criminal juries consist of twelve persons under Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 23(b). State criminal juries may consist of as few as six persons (Williams v. Florida), but a five-person jury was held unconstitutional in Ballew v. Georgia (1978). Civil juries in federal court consist of at least six and no more than twelve members under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 48.

The jury selection process involves venire (jury pool) assembly from voter registration lists, driver's license records, and other source lists, followed by voir dire examination. Both sides may exercise challenges for cause (unlimited) and peremptory challenges (limited by statute; subject to Batson v. Kentucky (1986) prohibition on race-based strikes and J.E.B. v. Alabama (1994) prohibition on gender-based strikes).

### Grand Jury

The Fifth Amendment requires federal felony prosecutions to proceed by grand jury indictment. Grand juries consist of 16 to 23 members and serve an investigative and accusatory function. Approximately half of U.S. states require grand jury indictments for felonies; the remainder permit prosecution by information filed by the prosecutor.

### Jury Nullification

Jury nullification -- the power of a jury to acquit a defendant despite evidence of guilt -- is a de facto feature of the American system resulting from the Double Jeopardy Clause's prohibition on retrying acquitted defendants. While courts uniformly hold that defendants have no right to a jury nullification instruction (United States v. Dougherty, 1972), the phenomenon remains a significant aspect of the jury's unreviewable authority.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany abandoned the traditional jury system (Schwurgerichte) in 1924 during the Weimar Republic, replacing it with mixed courts that combine professional and lay judges. The current system is governed by the Courts Constitution Act (Gerichtsverfassungsgesetz, GVG), particularly Sections 28 through 58, and the German Criminal Procedure Code (Strafprozessordnung, StPO).

### Court Structure with Lay Participation

| Court | Composition | Jurisdiction |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| Schöffengericht (Lay Assessor Court) | 1 professional judge + 2 Schöffen | Criminal cases with expected sentence of 2-4 years |
| Große Strafkammer (Grand Criminal Chamber) | 3 professional judges + 2 Schöffen | Serious criminal cases; appeals from Schöffengericht |
| Schwurgericht (Assize Court) | 3 professional judges + 2 Schöffen | Murder, manslaughter, and other capital offenses |
| Handelskammer (Commercial Chamber) | 1 professional judge + 2 commercial lay judges | Commercial disputes |

### Lay Judges (Schöffen)

Schöffen are selected from lists compiled by municipal councils for four-year terms. Candidates must be German citizens between 25 and 69 years of age. Unlike American jurors who are selected for individual cases, Schöffen serve regular sessions throughout their term. They have full voting rights equal to professional judges on questions of both guilt and sentencing. Conviction requires a two-thirds majority of the full panel (including both professional judges and Schöffen), meaning that in a Schöffengericht (one professional judge plus two Schöffen), both Schöffen could theoretically outvote the professional judge on acquittal.

### Historical Context

The abolition of the traditional jury system in 1924 (Emminger Reform) was motivated by several factors: the perception that lay juries were susceptible to emotional appeals and political influence during the turbulent Weimar period, the desire for reasoned verdicts that could be subject to appellate review (impossible with unreasoned general verdicts), and the civil law tradition's emphasis on professional legal reasoning. The Nazi regime retained the mixed court system, and post-war Germany continued it in both the Federal Republic and the German Democratic Republic.

### Constitutional Protections

Article 101(1) of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz) provides: "Extraordinary courts shall not be allowed. No one may be removed from the jurisdiction of his lawful judge." This provision ensures the right to a lawfully constituted court but does not guarantee jury trial. The Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) has confirmed that the mixed court system satisfies the fair trial requirements of Article 101 GG and Article 6 ECHR.

## ECHR & EU

### European Convention on Human Rights -- Article 6

The ECHR does not guarantee a right to jury trial. Article 6(1) requires a "fair and public hearing ... by an independent and impartial tribunal established by law." The European Court of Human Rights has consistently held that this provision is compatible with various adjudicative models, including professional judge systems, mixed courts, and traditional juries, provided that the overall fairness of the proceedings is maintained.

### Taxquet v. Belgium (Grand Chamber, 2010)

The landmark case concerning jury systems under the ECHR is Taxquet v. Belgium (Application No. 926/05, Grand Chamber Judgment of November 16, 2010). The applicant was convicted of murder and attempted murder by a Belgian assize court jury that returned a guilty verdict without providing reasons for its decision. The Grand Chamber held that Article 6(1) requires that the accused be able to understand the verdict rendered against them, which serves as a safeguard against arbitrariness.

**Key Principles:**
- The ECHR does not require any particular form of adjudication, including jury trial.
- However, when a state chooses to employ a jury system, the proceedings must satisfy Article 6 fairness requirements.
- Unreasoned general verdicts are not automatically incompatible with Article 6, but sufficient procedural safeguards must exist to allow the accused to understand the basis for conviction.
- Such safeguards may include precise questions put to the jury, adequate jury instructions, and the availability of appellate review.

### Judge v. United Kingdom (2011)

In Judge v. United Kingdom (Application No. 35863/10, Decision of February 8, 2011), the ECHR confirmed that the English and Welsh jury system, which employs unreasoned general verdicts, was compatible with Article 6 due to the presence of sufficient procedural safeguards, including detailed jury directions from the trial judge and the availability of appeal.

### EU Framework

The European Union does not harmonize member states' jury or adjudication systems. The principle of procedural autonomy reserves court organization and trial procedures to national competence. However, several EU instruments indirectly affect jury-related issues:

**Directive 2012/13/EU (Right to Information in Criminal Proceedings):** Requires that accused persons be informed of the charges against them, applicable in both jury and non-jury systems.

**Directive 2016/343/EU (Presumption of Innocence):** Strengthens the presumption of innocence and the right to be present at trial. The Directive applies regardless of whether the trial is conducted before a jury, mixed court, or professional judges.

**EU Charter of Fundamental Rights, Article 47:** Guarantees the right to an effective remedy and a fair trial, mirroring Article 6 ECHR without mandating any particular adjudicative format.

### Council of Europe Recommendations

The Council of Europe Committee of Ministers has issued several recommendations relevant to lay participation in justice:

**Recommendation Rec(2004)12** on the processes of reform of legal aid systems encourages member states to consider diverse models of adjudication, including citizen participation mechanisms.

The European Commission for the Efficiency of Justice (CEPEJ) conducts regular evaluations of member states' judicial systems, including the role of lay participation. Its reports document significant variation across Council of Europe member states, from full jury systems (England and Wales, Ireland, Belgium, Austria, Spain) to pure professional judge systems (Netherlands, Luxembourg) to mixed court systems (Germany, France, Italy, Denmark, Sweden, Norway, Finland).

## Comparative Analysis

### Democratic Legitimacy vs. Professional Expertise

The fundamental tension in jury system design is between democratic participation in justice and the perceived need for professional legal expertise. The United States resolves this tension most strongly in favor of democratic participation, treating the jury as a constitutional bulwark against government overreach -- a function explicitly recognized in Duncan v. Louisiana's characterization of the jury as "an inestimable safeguard against the corrupt or overzealous prosecutor and against the compliant, biased, or eccentric judge." Germany resolves the tension in favor of professional expertise tempered by lay participation, with Schöffen serving as a democratic check within a professional framework. South Korea occupies a middle position, introducing lay participation while preserving judicial supremacy through the advisory verdict mechanism.

### Verdict Accountability

A critical distinction concerns whether and how verdicts must be justified. German mixed courts must provide written reasoning for all judgments, enabling meaningful appellate review. American juries issue general verdicts without stated reasons, relying on procedural safeguards (jury instructions, evidentiary rules, appellate review of legal errors) to ensure fairness. South Korean citizen juries provide advisory verdicts, but the professional judge must supply written reasons for the final judgment. The ECHR's Taxquet jurisprudence has imposed a minimum requirement that the accused be able to understand the basis for conviction, regardless of the adjudicative model employed.

### Scope of Jury Participation

The United States applies jury trial most broadly, encompassing both criminal and civil proceedings across federal and state systems. South Korea limits citizen participation to serious criminal cases at the defendant's election. Germany employs lay judges across criminal cases of moderate to high severity and in commercial matters. No European system extends lay participation to civil cases as broadly as the American system.

### Selection vs. Service Models

The American model selects jurors for individual cases through a process designed to produce an impartial panel, with extensive voir dire and challenge procedures. The German model appoints Schöffen for four-year terms of regular service, creating a corps of experienced lay adjudicators. The South Korean model follows the American case-by-case selection approach. These different models produce different dynamics: case-specific selection maximizes impartiality but sacrifices experience, while term-based service develops lay judicial competence but may reduce diversity of perspective over time.
