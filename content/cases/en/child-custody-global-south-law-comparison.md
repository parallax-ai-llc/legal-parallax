## Summary

Child custody law in the Global South reflects deep tensions between parental rights, children's welfare, gender roles, extended family structures, and religious norms. China's custody framework, now governed by the Civil Code (2021, Book Five, Articles 1084-1086), operates in the shadow of decades of the one-child policy (1979-2015) and its aftermath: the relaxation to a two-child policy (2015) and three-child policy (2021) has created novel custody dynamics in a society where only children were the norm for a generation, and grandparents who served as primary caregivers during the mass internal migration of the "left-behind children" (留守儿童) phenomenon present distinctive custody claims; Article 1084 establishes the "best interests of the child" (最有利于未成年子女) as the governing standard, with a presumption favoring the mother for children under two years old. India's custody law is fragmented across the Hindu Minority and Guardianship Act 1956 (HMGA), the Guardians and Wards Act 1890 (GWA), and Muslim personal law, with the Supreme Court progressively establishing the "welfare of the child" as the paramount consideration overriding statutory presumptions -- in Yashita Sahu v. State of Rajasthan (2020) 3 SCC 67, the Court reiterated that the child's welfare transcends statutory rights of either parent. Nigerian custody law operates through the Child Rights Act 2003 (where domesticated), state-level children's laws, and customary norms that traditionally vest paternal custody automatically upon weaning -- the tension between customary patrilineal custody and the statutory "best interest" standard remains unresolved in many jurisdictions; South Africa's Children's Act 38 of 2005 introduced the concept of "parental responsibilities and rights" replacing the older "custody and access" terminology, with section 7 establishing a comprehensive list of best-interest factors. Brazil's shared custody law (Lei No. 13.058/2014) established shared custody (guarda compartilhada) as the default rule even when parents cannot agree, a bold legislative intervention reflecting the influence of the fathers' rights movement and the constitutional principle of equal parenting under Article 229 of the 1988 Constitution; earlier, the "Parental Alienation Law" (Lei No. 12.318/2010) criminalized parental alienation, though this statute has been criticized by domestic violence advocates. The Philippines maintains a strong maternal preference under Article 213 of the Family Code: "No child under seven years of age shall be separated from the mother, unless the court finds compelling reasons to order otherwise," a provision that the Supreme Court has interpreted strictly in cases like Tonog v. Court of Appeals (G.R. No. 122906, 2005); Indonesia's system, administered through Religious Courts for Muslims and District Courts for non-Muslims, applies a general standard of child welfare while maintaining Islamic law principles that assign hadhanah (custody) to the mother for children under the threshold age (mumayyiz, typically 12 years under KHI Article 105).

## China

### Legal Framework

Child custody in China is governed by the Civil Code (2021), Book Five: Marriage and Family, and Book Four: Personality Rights, supplemented by the Law on the Protection of Minors (2020 revision) and Supreme People's Court judicial interpretations.

**Article 1084:** After divorce, children under two years of age shall in principle be raised by the mother. For children who have reached two years of age, if the parents fail to reach an agreement on custody, the People's Court shall render a judgment based on the principle of what is most favorable to the minor child (最有利于未成年子女的原则), taking into account the specific circumstances of both parents.

**Article 1085:** After divorce, the parent who does not directly raise the children shall bear part or all of the child support expenses. The amount and duration of such expenses shall be agreed upon by both parents; if they cannot reach an agreement, the court shall render a judgment.

**Article 1086:** After divorce, the parent who does not directly raise the children has the right to visit the children, and the other parent has the obligation to assist. The manner and time of visitation shall be agreed upon by the parties; if they cannot reach an agreement, the court shall render a judgment. If a parent's visitation is detrimental to the physical and mental health of the child, the People's Court shall suspend visitation in accordance with law; when the grounds for suspension cease, visitation shall be resumed.

### The One-Child Policy Legacy

China's one-child policy (独生子女政策), in effect from 1979 to 2015, created a distinctive custody landscape:

**Only-Child Custody Disputes:** With the vast majority of divorcing couples having only one child, custody disputes took on existential significance for both parents and grandparents. The "4-2-1" family structure (four grandparents, two parents, one child) meant that custody decisions affected the entire extended family's emotional and economic investment.

**Left-Behind Children (留守儿童):** China's internal migration created an estimated 9 million rural "left-behind children" (as of 2020, down from over 60 million in earlier decades) raised primarily by grandparents while parents worked in cities. In custody proceedings, courts must grapple with who the child's de facto caregiver has been -- often a grandparent rather than either parent. The SPC's guidance documents recognize actual caregiving relationship as a significant factor in custody determination.

**Grandparent Rights:** The Civil Code does not explicitly provide grandparent visitation rights, but the SPC in its 2021 Interpretation of the Marriage and Family Book acknowledged that grandparents and other close relatives may apply for visitation in appropriate circumstances, particularly where they served as primary caregivers.

**Three-Child Policy Effects:** Since the relaxation to three children (2021), custody disputes in families with multiple children have introduced new complexities around sibling separation. Chinese courts generally disfavor separating siblings (不宜拆散原则) but may do so when individual children's best interests diverge.

### SPC Judicial Guidance

The Supreme People's Court has issued detailed guidance on custody determination factors:
- Stable living environment and education continuity
- Each parent's economic capacity and living conditions
- The child's attachment and habitual living arrangement
- For children aged 8 and above, the child's own preference should be given weight (民法典规定8周岁以上的未成年人为限制民事行为能力人)
- History of domestic violence or substance abuse
- Whether either parent has remarried and the new family's circumstances

## India

### Hindu Minority and Guardianship Act, 1956

The HMGA governs guardianship of Hindu minors (Hindus, Buddhists, Jains, Sikhs):

**Section 6 (Natural Guardians):**
- For a legitimate boy or unmarried girl: the father, and after him, the mother (provided the custody of a child under five years of age shall ordinarily be with the mother)
- For an illegitimate child: the mother, and after her, the father
- For a married girl: the husband

The statutory preference for the father as natural guardian has been progressively eroded by judicial interpretation. The Supreme Court in **Githa Hariharan v. Reserve Bank of India (1999) 2 SCC 228** interpreted "after" in Section 6 to mean "in the absence of" rather than "after the death of," holding that the mother could act as natural guardian even during the father's lifetime when the father was absent or neglectful. This interpretation, while not overruling the statute, effectively created a welfare-based overlay on the statutory paternal preference.

### Guardians and Wards Act, 1890

The GWA is a secular statute applicable to all religions, providing a framework for appointment of guardians by courts. Section 17 establishes that in appointing a guardian, the court shall be guided by what appears to be for the welfare of the minor, having regard to:
- The age, sex, and religion of the minor
- The character and capacity of the proposed guardian and his nearness of kin
- The wishes of a deceased parent
- Any existing or previous relations of the proposed guardian with the minor or the minor's property
- The wishes of the minor if old enough to form an intelligent preference

### Muslim Personal Law Custody (Hizanat)

Under Hanafi law (majority in India):
- Mother has the right of custody (hizanat) for a boy until age 7 and a girl until puberty
- After the mother, custody passes to the maternal grandmother, then to other female relatives
- The father has the right of guardianship (wilayat) -- supervision of education, marriage, and property -- throughout

The Supreme Court has consistently held that in all communities, the welfare of the child is paramount and overrides personal law presumptions. In **Mausami Moitra Ganguli v. Jayant Ganguli (2008) 7 SCC 673**, the Court stated: "In determining the question as to who should be given custody of a minor child, the paramount consideration is the welfare of the child and not the rights of the parents under a statute."

### Recent Developments

The Supreme Court in **Vasudha Sethi v. Kiran V. Bhaskar (2021)** emphasized that custody orders must facilitate meaningful contact with both parents, moving toward a de facto shared parenting model even absent explicit legislation. The Court in **Yashita Sahu v. State of Rajasthan (2020) 3 SCC 67** invoked its powers under Article 142 to fashion custody arrangements transcending the limitations of existing personal laws.

The debate over a Uniform Civil Code has direct implications for custody law: the Uttarakhand UCC (2024) establishes uniform custody provisions regardless of religion, prioritizing the child's best interest and eliminating personal-law-based presumptions.

## Africa

### Nigeria: Customary Custody Norms vs. Statutory Standards

**Customary Law Custody:** Traditional custody practices vary significantly:
- **Yoruba:** Children typically remain with the mother during infancy, transitioning to the father's family at a customary age (often around 7). The extended family (ebi) plays a central role in custody decisions.
- **Igbo:** Patrilineal custody is the norm -- children belong to the father's lineage, and upon divorce, the father retains custody, particularly of boys. The bride-price payment is considered to establish paternal rights over children of the marriage.
- **Hausa-Fulani:** Islamic hadhanah rules apply, with maternal custody during early childhood and paternal custody thereafter.

**Child Rights Act, 2003:** Section 1 establishes the best interest of the child as the primary consideration in all actions concerning children. The Act (where domesticated) provides:
- Section 71: In custody proceedings, the court shall regard the best interest of the child as the paramount consideration
- Section 72: The court may grant custody to either parent, any relative, or any other person
- Sections 82-83: The court must consider the child's physical, emotional, and educational needs, the likely effect of change, age, sex, background, harm suffered or at risk, parents' capacity

However, the CRA has been domesticated in only 26 of 36 states, and many northern states have explicitly refused adoption, citing incompatibility with Islamic law. In states without the CRA, custody continues to be governed by customary law and general child welfare principles derived from English common law through the Matrimonial Causes Act and Children and Young Persons Laws.

### South Africa: Children's Act 38 of 2005

South Africa's Children's Act fundamentally reformed custody law, replacing the concepts of "custody" and "access" with "care" and "contact" as components of "parental responsibilities and rights":

**Section 18 (Parental Responsibilities and Rights):** Include the responsibility and right to: care for the child, maintain contact, act as guardian, contribute to the child's maintenance.

**Section 7 (Best Interest Standard):** The most comprehensive statutory best-interest checklist in the Global South:
- The nature of the personal relationship between the child and the parents
- The attitude of the parents toward the child and the exercise of parental responsibilities
- The capacity of the parents to provide for the child's needs
- The likely effect of any change in the child's circumstances
- The practical difficulty and expense of the child maintaining contact with both parents
- The need for the child to remain in a stable home environment
- The need to protect the child from physical or psychological harm from maltreatment, abuse, neglect, exploitation, or degradation
- Any family violence involving the child or a family member
- The child's age, maturity, stage of development, gender, background, and any other relevant characteristics
- **The child's views** (section 10: "every child that is of such an age, maturity, and stage of development as to be able to participate in any matter concerning that child has the right to participate")

**Co-Holders of Parental Responsibilities (Section 19-21):** Both parents automatically hold parental responsibilities regardless of marital status if the father's paternity is established. Unmarried fathers acquire full parental responsibilities by contributing to the child's upbringing or maintenance (section 21(1)(b)), a progressive provision that the Constitutional Court affirmed in relevant decisions.

**Mediation:** The Act encourages mediation before litigation (section 21(3)), and the 2014 Regulations established the Office of the Family Advocate to assist in custody matters.

## Latin America

### Brazil: The Shared Custody Default

Brazil's custody law has undergone rapid transformation:

**Shared Custody (Guarda Compartilhada):** Law No. 11.698/2008 introduced shared custody as an option, but Lei No. 13.058/2014 made it the **default rule** even when parents cannot agree. Article 1584, §2 of the Civil Code (as amended) provides: "When there is no agreement between the mother and father regarding the custody of the child, and both parents are able to exercise the power of the family, shared custody shall be applied." The court may designate a "base city" (cidade-base) for the child's residence and establish a balanced time-sharing arrangement.

The law generated significant controversy:
- **Supporters** (notably fathers' rights organizations like the Instituto Brasileiro de Direito de Família/IBDFAM) argued that shared custody promotes children's healthy development and gender equality in parenting.
- **Critics** (including feminist organizations and domestic violence advocates) argued that imposing shared custody in high-conflict situations, particularly involving domestic violence, can harm children and enable continued control by abusive partners.

**Parental Alienation Law (Lei No. 12.318/2010):** Defines parental alienation as interference in the child's psychological formation to repudiate a parent or harm the parent-child bond. Sanctions range from warnings to reversal of custody. Article 2 lists acts constituting alienation, including: conducting a defamation campaign, making it difficult for the other parent to exercise authority, hindering contact, omitting relevant information, and filing false abuse reports.

This law has been criticized by domestic violence advocates who argue it is weaponized against protective mothers who report legitimate abuse. The Brazilian Bar Association (OAB) and the National Council for the Rights of Children and Adolescents (CONANDA) have debated potential reforms. In 2024, proposals to repeal or amend the Parental Alienation Law advanced in Congress.

**Child and Adolescent Statute (Estatuto da Criança e do Adolescente, ECA -- Lei No. 8.069/1990):** The foundational framework for children's rights, implementing the "integral protection doctrine" (doutrina da proteção integral). Article 3 establishes that children enjoy all fundamental rights inherent to the human person, and Article 4 establishes shared responsibility among family, community, society, and the state.

### Mexico and Colombia

Mexico's custody law varies by state but generally applies the best-interest standard (interés superior del niño). The SCJN has issued amparo decisions establishing that the best-interest principle requires individualized analysis and cannot be satisfied by gender-based presumptions alone. Mexico City's Civil Code establishes shared custody as the preferred arrangement.

Colombia's Code for Children and Adolescents (Ley 1098 de 2006) establishes the "prevalence of children's rights" (prevalencia de los derechos de los niños) as a constitutional principle (Article 44, Constitution). Article 22 establishes the right of children to have and grow up in a family. The Constitutional Court in Sentencia T-557/2011 emphasized that custody decisions must prioritize the child's integral development.

## Southeast Asia

### Indonesia: Hadhanah in the Religious Courts

For Muslim Indonesians, child custody (hadhanah) is governed by the Compilation of Islamic Law (KHI):

**Article 105:** In the event of divorce:
- A child who is not yet mumayyiz (has not reached the age of 12) is under the hadhanah of the mother
- A child who is already mumayyiz has the right to choose between the father and mother as the holder of hadhanah
- The costs of hadhanah are borne by the father

**Article 156:** Addresses custody when the mother who holds hadhanah remarries, dies, or is unable to guarantee the child's physical and spiritual safety:
- The hadhanah transfers to specified female relatives (maternal grandmother, then paternal relatives) before transferring to the father

The Religious Courts apply these provisions while also considering the child's welfare (kemaslahatan anak). The Supreme Court in Decision No. 110 K/AG/2007 held that the mumayyiz child's choice is not absolute -- the court retains discretion to override the child's preference if the chosen parent is unfit.

**Non-Muslim Custody:** District Courts apply the general civil law principle of the child's best interest, without the specific hadhanah age thresholds. Government Regulation No. 9 of 1975 provides minimal guidance, leaving significant judicial discretion.

**Adat Considerations:** In some communities, customary law influences custody outcomes. Matrilineal Minangkabau tradition places children within the mother's clan (suku), creating a strong cultural presumption of maternal custody that reinforces the Islamic hadhanah principle. Patrilineal Batak tradition, conversely, places children within the father's marga (clan), potentially conflicting with hadhanah rules for young children.

### Philippines: Maternal Preference

**Article 213, Family Code:** "No child under seven years of age shall be separated from the mother, unless the court finds compelling reasons to order otherwise."

This provision creates one of the strongest maternal preferences in any legal system. The Supreme Court in **Tonog v. Court of Appeals (G.R. No. 122906, February 7, 2005)** upheld the maternal preference, holding that the use of "shall" makes the provision mandatory, not merely directory. "Compelling reasons" have been narrowly interpreted to include: neglect, abandonment, unemployment, immorality, habitual drunkenness, drug addiction, maltreatment, insanity, and affliction with a communicable disease.

For children seven and above, Article 213 does not apply, and the court exercises discretion based on the child's best interest. The Supreme Court in **Silva v. Court of Appeals (G.R. No. 114742, 1997)** held that for older children, the totality of circumstances governs.

**Illegitimate Children:** Article 176 of the Family Code vests sole parental authority over illegitimate children in the mother, a provision that the Supreme Court has upheld as reflecting the reality that illegitimate children are typically raised by their mothers.

**Muslim Filipinos:** Under PD 1083, hadhanah rules similar to those in classical Islamic law apply: the mother has custody of children below the discriminating age (typically 7 for boys, puberty for girls), transferable to the father thereafter.

**Proposed Reforms:** The pending Absolute Divorce Bill (HB 1979) includes custody provisions that would modify the strict maternal preference, introducing a best-interest standard with consideration of both parents' fitness and the child's preference for older children.

## Comparative Analysis

### Maternal Preference vs. Best Interest

The Global South shows a clear trend from gender-based presumptions toward best-interest standards, but the pace varies enormously. South Africa and Brazil have moved furthest toward gender-neutral frameworks. China maintains a limited maternal preference (under 2 years) within an otherwise gender-neutral system. India, Indonesia, and the Philippines maintain significant maternal preferences, though India's judiciary has substantially qualified the statutory preference through welfare-based reasoning. Nigeria's customary law patrilineal custody traditions represent the opposite presumption -- favoring fathers -- which the CRA's best-interest standard is only gradually displacing.

### Extended Family and Community

The role of extended family in custody is a distinctive feature of Global South legal systems. China's grandparent caregiving (left-behind children phenomenon), Nigeria's customary family-based custody, Indonesia's adat clan structures, and India's joint family system all create custody dynamics absent in nuclear-family-focused Western frameworks. South Africa's Children's Act explicitly accommodates this by allowing "any person having an interest in the care, well-being or development of a child" to apply for parental responsibilities.

### Shared Custody as Default

Brazil stands alone in the Global South in mandating shared custody as the default. This reflects a particular political economy: the convergence of fathers' rights advocacy, constitutional gender equality principles, and a judiciary willing to impose structural remedies. Other Global South jurisdictions maintain sole custody models with visitation rights for the non-custodial parent.
