## Summary

The right to be forgotten (잊힐 권리) -- the principle that individuals may, under certain conditions, require the deletion or de-indexing of personal data that is no longer relevant, necessary, or accurate -- represents one of the most significant and contested developments in modern privacy law, sitting at the intersection of data protection, freedom of expression, and the permanent nature of digital information. South Korea recognized a form of the right to be forgotten through the Personal Information Protection Act (개인정보 보호법, PIPA), which grants individuals the right to request deletion of personal information under Article 36, and through landmark Supreme Court (대법원) decisions including the 2016 ruling establishing the right to de-indexing of search results; the 2023 PIPA amendment further strengthened data subject deletion rights and created clearer procedures for exercising the right against search engines and online platforms. The United States has not recognized a general right to be forgotten, reflecting the strong constitutional protection afforded to free speech under the First Amendment; California's Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA/CPRA, Cal. Civ. Code Sections 1798.100-1798.199.100) grants a right to deletion of personal information held by businesses but is explicitly limited in scope and does not extend to search engine de-indexing. Germany, as part of the EU framework, applies the right to erasure under Article 17 of the GDPR and has developed significant national case law through the Bundesgerichtshof (BGH) and the Bundesverfassungsgericht (Federal Constitutional Court), balancing the right to be forgotten against press freedom and the public's right to information. At the EU level, the landmark CJEU judgment in Google Spain SL v. AEPD (Case C-131/12, 2014) established the right to de-indexing as a fundamental data protection right, the GDPR Article 17 codified the right to erasure with defined exceptions, and the CJEU's subsequent Google v. CNIL (Case C-507/17, 2019) clarified the territorial scope of de-indexing obligations, while the European Court of Human Rights has addressed the balancing of privacy and expression in the digital age through key judgments including Hurbain v. Belgium (2023).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea has recognized the right to be forgotten through a combination of statutory provisions and judicial development, reflecting the growing importance of digital privacy in Korean society.

**Personal Information Protection Act (PIPA, 개인정보 보호법):** Article 36 grants individuals the right to request correction or deletion (삭제) of their personal information from data controllers. Where personal information is found to be inaccurate, unnecessary, or processed in violation of the law, the data controller must take corrective action without delay. The 2023 amendment clarified that this right extends to personal information accessible through search engines and online platforms, providing a clearer procedural framework for de-indexing requests.

**Information and Communications Network Act (정보통신망 이용촉진 및 정보보호 등에 관한 법률):** This Act complements PIPA by addressing personal information processing by telecommunications service providers and online service providers. Article 44-2 establishes a right to request deletion of information that infringes personal rights, with service providers required to take temporary measures within 30 days.

### Supreme Court Decision (2016)

The Supreme Court of Korea (대법원) issued a landmark decision in 2016 (Case No. 2014다235080) recognizing the right to de-indexing of search results. The Court held that individuals may request the removal of search results linking to personal information where:

- The information is no longer relevant or has become outdated.
- The individual's privacy interest outweighs the public interest in continued accessibility.
- The information causes ongoing harm disproportionate to its informational value.

The Court adopted a balancing test considering the nature of the information, the individual's public role, the passage of time, and the severity of harm. This framework parallels the CJEU's Google Spain approach but is grounded in Korean constitutional and statutory law.

### PIPC Enforcement

The Personal Information Protection Commission (PIPC, 개인정보보호위원회) has processed thousands of de-indexing and deletion requests since its establishment as a unified supervisory authority. In 2024, the PIPC reported receiving approximately 8,500 data deletion requests, of which approximately 65 percent were fully or partially granted. The Commission has issued guidelines specifying the factors to be considered in balancing privacy against public interest, including specific guidance for search engine operators and news aggregation platforms.

### Korean Search Engine Compliance

Naver (네이버), South Korea's dominant search engine, has implemented a dedicated right-to-be-forgotten request processing system. Between 2020 and 2024, Naver received over 35,000 de-indexing requests, granting approximately 55 percent. Kakao and other Korean platforms maintain similar systems under PIPC oversight.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has not recognized a general right to be forgotten, reflecting the constitutional primacy of free speech under the First Amendment and a tradition of limited government regulation of information flows.

**First Amendment Constraints:** The First Amendment to the United States Constitution prohibits government restrictions on speech and press, creating a fundamental obstacle to right-to-be-forgotten legislation. Courts have held that the publication of truthful information is generally protected speech. In Sorrell v. IMS Health Inc. (564 U.S. 552, 2011), the Supreme Court held that restrictions on the dissemination of truthful information are subject to heightened scrutiny, even in commercial contexts.

**California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA/CPRA):** California provides the most extensive deletion right in the United States through the CCPA (effective 2020) and its amendment, the California Privacy Rights Act (CPRA, effective 2023). Cal. Civ. Code Section 1798.105 grants consumers the right to request deletion of personal information collected by businesses. However, this right is subject to extensive exceptions and does not extend to search engine de-indexing or the removal of publicly available information.

**CCPA Deletion Right Limitations:**

| Exception Category | Description |
|-------------------|-------------|
| Transaction completion | Personal information necessary to complete a transaction |
| Security purposes | Information needed to detect security incidents |
| Legal compliance | Information required by law to be maintained |
| Free speech | Exercising free speech or another right provided by law |
| Public interest research | Information used for public interest research purposes |
| Internal business uses | Uses reasonably aligned with consumer expectations |

### Judicial Resistance to Right to Be Forgotten

U.S. courts have consistently rejected attempts to establish a right to be forgotten. In Garcia v. Google (786 F.3d 733, 9th Cir. 2015, en banc), the Ninth Circuit held that a court order requiring Google to remove a video from YouTube constituted an impermissible prior restraint on speech. In Martin v. Hearst Corporation (2021, 2d Cir.), the court rejected a defamation-based claim seeking removal of an online news article about a later-dismissed criminal charge, holding that truthful reporting of public records is protected.

### State-Level Developments

Several states have enacted limited versions of the deletion right through comprehensive privacy laws, including Virginia (VCDPA), Colorado (CPA), Connecticut (CTDPA), and others. These laws provide data deletion rights but are narrower in scope than the EU's right to erasure and do not include de-indexing obligations.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's application of the right to be forgotten benefits from both the directly applicable GDPR framework and a rich domestic constitutional tradition of personality rights (Persönlichkeitsrecht).

**GDPR Article 17 -- Right to Erasure:** As directly applicable EU law, Article 17 grants data subjects the right to obtain erasure of personal data without undue delay where the data is no longer necessary, consent is withdrawn, the data subject objects to processing, or the data has been unlawfully processed. The right is limited by exceptions for freedom of expression and information, compliance with legal obligations, public interest archiving, and the establishment or exercise of legal claims.

**Grundgesetz (Basic Law) -- General Personality Right:** Articles 1(1) (human dignity) and 2(1) (free development of personality) of the German Basic Law, as interpreted by the Federal Constitutional Court, establish a general personality right (allgemeines Persönlichkeitsrecht) that encompasses the right to informational self-determination (Recht auf informationelle Selbstbestimmung). This constitutional right, established in the landmark Census Decision (Volkszählungsurteil, BVerfGE 65, 1, 1983), provides an independent basis for right-to-be-forgotten claims beyond GDPR Article 17.

### Bundesverfassungsgericht (Federal Constitutional Court) Decisions

**"Right to Be Forgotten I" (BVerfG, Order of November 6, 2019, 1 BvR 16/13):** The Court held that where EU law (GDPR) fully harmonizes the field, the relevant fundamental rights standard is the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights, not the German Basic Law. The case concerned a request for de-indexing of search results linking to a 1982 murder conviction, and the Court referred the matter back for re-examination under EU fundamental rights standards.

**"Right to Be Forgotten II" (BVerfG, Order of November 6, 2019, 1 BvR 276/17):** Where EU law does not fully harmonize (here, an online news archive of a public broadcaster), the German Basic Law applies. The Court held that the general personality right requires media outlets to consider requests for de-indexing from their own search functions after a significant passage of time, balancing the severity of the reported events, the person's role, and the degree to which the information remains relevant.

### Bundesgerichtshof (Federal Court of Justice) Case Law

**BGH, Judgment of July 27, 2020, VI ZR 405/18 (Google De-Indexing):** The BGH applied the CJEU's Google Spain framework, holding that Google must de-index search results linking to a news article where the information has become irrelevant due to the passage of time and causes ongoing privacy harm. The court emphasized that search engine operators must conduct a case-by-case balancing of the competing interests.

### Data Protection Authority Enforcement

German state data protection authorities (Landesdatenschutzbeauftragte) process de-indexing complaints and can order search engines to comply. The Berlin Commissioner for Data Protection has been particularly active, issuing guidance on the criteria for granting or refusing de-indexing requests.

## ECHR & EU

### CJEU -- Google Spain SL v. AEPD (Case C-131/12, Judgment of May 13, 2014)

The foundational judgment establishing the right to de-indexing in EU law. The CJEU held:

- A search engine operator processes personal data when it indexes web pages containing personal information and is therefore a "controller" under the Data Protection Directive (95/46/EC).
- Individuals have the right to request that search engine operators remove links to web pages containing personal information that is inadequate, irrelevant, no longer relevant, or excessive in relation to the purposes of processing.
- The search engine operator must balance the individual's right to privacy against the public interest in access to information, considering the individual's role in public life, the nature of the information, and the passage of time.
- The right applies even where the original publication remains lawful -- de-indexing from search results is distinct from removal from the source website.

### GDPR Article 17 -- Right to Erasure

The GDPR codified and expanded the right established in Google Spain:

**Grounds for Erasure (Article 17(1)):**
- Data no longer necessary for original purpose
- Consent withdrawn and no other legal ground exists
- Data subject objects and no overriding legitimate grounds exist
- Unlawful processing
- Legal obligation to erase
- Data collected in relation to information society services offered to children

**Exceptions (Article 17(3)):**
- Freedom of expression and information
- Compliance with legal obligations
- Public health purposes
- Archiving in the public interest, scientific or historical research, or statistical purposes
- Establishment, exercise, or defense of legal claims

### CJEU -- Google v. CNIL (Case C-507/17, Judgment of September 24, 2019)

The CJEU addressed the territorial scope of the right to de-indexing, holding that EU law does not require a search engine to de-index results on all its global domain versions. However, the search engine must effectively prevent EU-based users from accessing de-indexed results, using geo-blocking or similar technical measures. This ruling rejected the French data protection authority's (CNIL) demand for global de-indexing.

### European Court of Human Rights

**Hurbain v. Belgium (Application No. 57292/16, Grand Chamber Judgment of July 4, 2023):** The Grand Chamber held that a Belgian court order requiring a newspaper to anonymize an online archive article about a fatal traffic accident did not violate Article 10 (freedom of expression). The Court established detailed criteria for balancing privacy (Article 8) and expression (Article 10) in the context of online news archives, including the nature and severity of the reported events, the time elapsed, the person's contemporary notoriety, and the impact of continued accessibility.

**M.L. and W.W. v. Germany (Applications Nos. 60798/10 and 65599/10, Judgment of June 28, 2018):** The Court found no violation of Article 8 where German courts refused to order media outlets to anonymize online archive articles about the applicants' criminal convictions. The Court acknowledged that online news archives serve an important function in preserving the historical record and contributing to public debate.

## Comparative Analysis

### Existence and Scope of the Right

The most fundamental divergence concerns whether the right exists at all. The EU and South Korea recognize a right to de-indexing and erasure grounded in data protection principles. The United States rejects such a right, treating it as incompatible with First Amendment protections. Germany occupies a unique position within the EU framework, having developed both EU-derived and constitutionally independent bases for the right.

### Balancing Frameworks

All jurisdictions that recognize the right employ balancing tests, but with different default positions. The EU approach (Google Spain, GDPR Article 17) favors the data subject, placing the burden on the data controller to justify continued processing. South Korea's Supreme Court framework similarly requires balancing but with less developed criteria. The ECHR's Hurbain framework provides the most detailed balancing test, applicable across Convention states. The US approach, by contrast, defaults to protecting information availability, with privacy interests requiring compelling justification to override expression rights.

### Territorial Reach

The CJEU's Google v. CNIL decision limiting de-indexing to EU jurisdictions reflects the tension between the universal nature of the internet and territorial sovereignty. South Korea's enforcement is similarly limited to Korean platforms and Korean-accessible content. The US approach, which does not recognize the right, renders the territorial question moot domestically but creates international friction when EU de-indexing orders affect globally accessible content.

### Practical Effectiveness

Google has processed over 5.5 million URL de-indexing requests under the GDPR as of 2025, removing approximately 50 percent of requested URLs. Naver's compliance rate in South Korea is approximately 55 percent. These statistics suggest the right is meaningfully exercised but by no means absolute, with search engines serving as primary decision-makers in the initial evaluation.
