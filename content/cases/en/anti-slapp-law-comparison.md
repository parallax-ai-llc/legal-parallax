## Summary

Anti-SLAPP laws -- legislation designed to provide early dismissal of strategic lawsuits against public participation, where plaintiffs use litigation to silence critics, whistleblowers, journalists, and civic activists by burdening them with the cost and stress of legal defense -- have emerged as a critical tool for protecting freedom of expression and democratic participation across jurisdictions. South Korea does not have a specific anti-SLAPP statute but addresses the phenomenon through criminal defamation law reform efforts, the Constitutional Court's (헌법재판소) jurisprudence on freedom of expression under Article 21 of the Constitution (표현의 자유), and provisions of the Civil Code (민법) Article 750 and the Civil Procedure Act (민사소송법) regarding abuse of litigation rights, with the National Human Rights Commission (국가인권위원회) having recommended anti-SLAPP legislation in 2013 and several legislative proposals having been introduced but not enacted, while the persistence of criminal defamation (형법 Article 307) has been identified by international observers as a structural facilitator of SLAPP suits. The United States has the most developed anti-SLAPP framework globally, with 33 states and the District of Columbia having enacted anti-SLAPP statutes as of 2025, led by California's pioneering Code of Civil Procedure Section 425.16 (1992) and Texas's Texas Citizens Participation Act (TCPA, 2011, amended 2019), though no federal anti-SLAPP statute exists, and the applicability of state anti-SLAPP laws in federal court has generated significant circuit splits. Germany lacks a specific anti-SLAPP statute but addresses abusive litigation through the prohibition of abuse of rights (Rechtsmissbrauch) under BGB Section 242, the "loser pays" fee-shifting system under ZPO Section 91 which deters frivolous claims, and the increasing judicial willingness to award litigation costs against SLAPP plaintiffs, while the EU has adopted Directive (EU) 2024/1069 on protecting persons who engage in public participation from manifestly unfounded or abusive court proceedings (the "Anti-SLAPP Directive"), requiring all member states to transpose anti-SLAPP protections by May 7, 2026, and the ECHR's Article 10 jurisprudence, particularly Steel and Morris v. United Kingdom (2005), has established that the right to freedom of expression may be violated by disproportionate defamation proceedings that chill public participation.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea lacks dedicated anti-SLAPP legislation, creating a significant gap in protection for public participation. The legal landscape is defined by the interaction between defamation law, constitutional free expression protections, and general civil procedure rules.

**Criminal Defamation (형법 Articles 307-312):** South Korea's criminal defamation provisions are frequently identified as the primary structural enabler of SLAPP-type conduct:

- **Article 307(1):** Defamation by publicly alleging facts that damage another's reputation is punishable by up to 2 years imprisonment or a fine up to 5 million KRW
- **Article 307(2):** Defamation by publicly alleging false facts is punishable by up to 5 years imprisonment or a fine up to 10 million KRW
- **Article 309:** Defamation through publication (press, internet) increases penalties to up to 3 years (true facts) or 7 years (false facts) imprisonment
- **Article 310 (Justification / 위법성 조각사유):** Defamation is not punishable if the statement is true, concerns a matter of public interest, and is made solely in the public interest

The use of criminal defamation complaints as SLAPP tools is well-documented. According to the Korean Press Freedom Index reports, powerful individuals and corporations routinely file criminal defamation complaints against journalists, activists, and online commentators to silence criticism, exploiting the criminal justice system's investigatory power as leverage.

### Constitutional Court Jurisprudence

**Constitutional Court Decision 2013Hun-Ba127 (2016):** The Constitutional Court upheld the constitutionality of Article 307(1) (defamation by true facts), finding by a 5-4 vote that criminal punishment for truthful defamation serves the legitimate purpose of protecting personal honor and does not disproportionately restrict freedom of expression under Article 21. The four dissenting justices argued that criminal punishment for truthful statements on matters of public interest constitutes a disproportionate restriction on free expression.

**Constitutional Court Decision 2008Hun-Ma324 (2012):** The Court addressed internet real-name verification requirements, striking down provisions of the Act on Promotion of Information and Communications Network Utilization (정보통신망법) that required real-name registration for internet posting. While not directly an anti-SLAPP case, the decision recognized that mechanisms facilitating identification of anonymous speakers can enable retaliatory litigation.

### Legislative Proposals

Several anti-SLAPP bills have been introduced in the National Assembly:

- **2013 Proposal (Representative Park Young-sun):** Proposed amendment to the Civil Procedure Act to create an early dismissal mechanism for suits targeting public participation, modeled on California's anti-SLAPP statute
- **2018 Proposal (Representative Kim Kyeong-hyup):** Proposed a standalone Anti-SLAPP Act with early dismissal motions, mandatory fee-shifting, and prohibition of retaliatory criminal defamation complaints
- **2022 Proposal (Representative Jeon Hae-cheol):** Proposed amendments to the Civil Procedure Act and Criminal Act to protect whistleblowers and journalists from retaliatory litigation

None of these proposals have been enacted, reportedly due to opposition from corporate interests and concerns about interfering with the right to pursue defamation claims.

### National Human Rights Commission

The National Human Rights Commission (국가인권위원회) issued a recommendation in 2013 calling on the National Assembly to enact anti-SLAPP legislation, finding that strategic lawsuits against public participation constitute a serious threat to freedom of expression and democratic participation. The Commission specifically recommended: early dismissal procedures for claims targeting public participation, mandatory fee-shifting against SLAPP plaintiffs, and reform of criminal defamation to eliminate imprisonment for truthful statements on public interest matters.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has the most developed anti-SLAPP framework globally, though the absence of a federal statute creates significant variation.

**California Code of Civil Procedure Section 425.16 (1992):** California enacted the first anti-SLAPP statute in 1992. The statute provides:

- **Section 425.16(b)(1):** A cause of action arising from any act in furtherance of the person's right of petition or free speech in connection with a public issue shall be subject to a special motion to strike unless the court determines that the plaintiff has established a probability of prevailing on the claim
- **Two-Step Analysis:** (1) The defendant must show that the challenged claim arises from protected activity (petitioning, speech on public issue, speech in public forum, or speech in connection with an issue of public interest); (2) the burden shifts to the plaintiff to demonstrate a probability of prevailing on the merits
- **Mandatory Fee Award:** A defendant who prevails on an anti-SLAPP motion is entitled to recover attorney's fees and costs

**Texas Citizens Participation Act (TCPA, Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code Chapter 27, 2011, amended 2019):** Texas's broad anti-SLAPP law was significantly amended in 2019 to narrow its scope after concerns about abuse by commercial defendants:

- Protects the right of free speech, the right to petition, and the right of association
- Provides for dismissal within 60 days of filing unless the plaintiff establishes "clear and specific evidence" of a prima facie case
- Automatic stay of discovery pending resolution of the motion
- Mandatory attorney fees for prevailing defendants

**No Federal Anti-SLAPP Statute:** Despite multiple legislative proposals (SPEAK FREE Act, 2015, 2017), no federal anti-SLAPP statute has been enacted. The applicability of state anti-SLAPP laws in federal diversity cases has generated circuit splits:

- **9th Circuit (Newsham v. Lockheed Missiles, 190 F.3d 963 (1999)):** California's anti-SLAPP law applies in federal court
- **D.C. Circuit (Abbas v. Foreign Policy Group, 783 F.3d 1328 (2015)):** D.C.'s anti-SLAPP law applies in federal court
- **1st Circuit (Godin v. Schencks, 629 F.3d 79 (2010)):** Maine's anti-SLAPP law applies in federal court
- **11th Circuit (Carbone v. CNN, 910 F.3d 1345 (2018)):** Georgia's anti-SLAPP law does not apply in federal court (procedural, conflicting with FRCP)

### Key Cases

**Batzel v. Smith, 333 F.3d 1018 (9th Cir. 2003):** Established that anti-SLAPP protections extend to internet speech on matters of public interest.

**FilmOn.com v. DoubleVerify, 7 Cal. 5th 133 (2019):** The California Supreme Court narrowed the scope of anti-SLAPP protection, holding that commercial speech does not qualify as speech "in connection with a public issue" merely because it concerns a topic of public interest.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany lacks a specific anti-SLAPP statute but addresses abusive litigation through general legal principles and the inherent deterrent effect of its procedural fee structure.

**ZPO Section 91 ("Loser Pays" / Kostentragungspflicht):** Germany's fee-shifting rule, requiring the losing party to bear all litigation costs including the prevailing party's attorney fees, serves as a structural deterrent against frivolous SLAPP suits. Unlike the American system where each party bears its own costs, a SLAPP plaintiff in Germany faces the prospect of paying the defendant's legal costs if the claim fails.

**BGB Section 242 (Rechtsmissbrauch / Abuse of Rights):** The prohibition of abuse of rights under the good faith principle provides a potential basis for defending against SLAPP suits. Courts may dismiss claims that constitute an abuse of the litigation process, though this doctrine is rarely invoked specifically in the SLAPP context.

**Criminal Defamation (StGB Sections 186-187):** Germany retains criminal defamation provisions:
- **Section 186 (Üble Nachrede):** Defamation by asserting or disseminating facts that are not demonstrably true and that damage another's reputation -- fine or imprisonment up to 1 year (2 years if public dissemination)
- **Section 187 (Verleumdung):** Intentional defamation by asserting false facts -- fine or imprisonment up to 2 years (5 years if public dissemination)

While these provisions are rarely used as SLAPP tools compared to Korea, criminal defamation complaints have been filed against journalists and activists, particularly by public officials.

### EU Anti-SLAPP Directive Transposition

Germany must transpose Directive (EU) 2024/1069 by May 7, 2026. The transposition is expected to require enactment of:
- An early dismissal mechanism for manifestly unfounded claims targeting public participation
- Mandatory cost awards for SLAPP victims
- Safeguards to ensure that the mechanism does not undermine legitimate defamation claims
- Provisions addressing cross-border SLAPP suits

The German Ministry of Justice initiated a public consultation on transposition options in 2025, with stakeholders debating the scope of protected activity and the procedural mechanics of the early dismissal mechanism.

## ECHR / EU

### ECHR Article 10 and SLAPP Suits

**Steel and Morris v. United Kingdom (Application No. 68416/01, February 15, 2005):** The "McLibel" case is the foundational ECHR anti-SLAPP decision. Two environmental activists distributed leaflets criticizing McDonald's Corporation, which sued for defamation. The proceedings lasted over 10 years. The Court found a violation of Article 10 (freedom of expression) and Article 6 (fair trial/access to justice due to denial of legal aid). The Court held that the disproportionate nature of the defamation proceedings -- brought by a multinational corporation against two individuals of modest means -- violated the activists' right to free expression, as the disparity in resources between the parties and the length of proceedings had a chilling effect on public participation.

**Margulev v. Russia (Application No. 15449/09, October 8, 2019):** The Court found a violation of Article 10 where a Russian court awarded disproportionate damages in a defamation case brought by a public official against a journalist, holding that the chilling effect of excessive damages awards on press freedom violates the Convention.

### EU Anti-SLAPP Directive (EU) 2024/1069

Adopted on April 11, 2024, the Directive on protecting persons who engage in public participation from manifestly unfounded or abusive court proceedings ("Anti-SLAPP Directive") establishes minimum EU-wide protections:

**Article 1 (Subject Matter):** The Directive establishes safeguards against manifestly unfounded or abusive court proceedings brought against natural or legal persons on account of their engagement in public participation.

**Article 3 (Scope):** Applies to civil and commercial court proceedings with cross-border implications, brought against natural or legal persons on account of their engagement in public participation. Member states are encouraged to extend the protections to purely domestic proceedings.

**Article 5 (Early Dismissal):** Member states must ensure that courts can dismiss, wholly or partly, manifestly unfounded claims against public participation at an early stage of proceedings upon application by the defendant. The burden of proof lies with the claimant to demonstrate that the claim is not manifestly unfounded.

**Article 6 (Procedural Safeguards):** Courts may order the claimant to provide security for costs and may award damages to the defendant for harm suffered as a result of the abusive proceedings.

**Article 8 (Costs):** The defendant who prevails in early dismissal proceedings may recover costs, including reasonable attorney fees.

**Article 9 (Penalties):** Member states must provide for effective, proportionate, and dissuasive penalties for bringing abusive court proceedings against public participation.

**Transposition Deadline:** May 7, 2026.

### European Parliament Resolution (2021)

Prior to the Directive, the European Parliament adopted a Resolution on November 11, 2021, calling for comprehensive anti-SLAPP legislation, prompted by the assassination of Maltese journalist Daphne Caruana Galizia in 2017, who faced 47 civil and criminal defamation lawsuits at the time of her death. The Resolution identified SLAPP suits as a serious threat to press freedom and democratic participation across the EU.

## Comparative Analysis

### Structural Protections

The United States provides the most robust anti-SLAPP protections through specialized statutory mechanisms in 33 states. The EU Directive will create a minimum floor across all 27 member states, though initially limited to cross-border cases. Germany's "loser pays" system provides a structural deterrent that does not exist in the United States. South Korea has the weakest protections, with criminal defamation serving as a tool readily available to SLAPP plaintiffs.

### Criminal Defamation

The persistence of criminal defamation is the most critical variable. South Korea's criminal defamation regime, with penalties of up to 7 years imprisonment for media defamation, provides uniquely powerful leverage for SLAPP plaintiffs. Germany retains criminal defamation but rarely prosecutes. The United States has effectively abolished criminal defamation. The ECHR permits criminal defamation but requires proportionality under Article 10.

### Fee-Shifting

The three systems address litigation cost allocation differently, with significant implications for SLAPP deterrence. Germany's "loser pays" rule deters frivolous claims generally. U.S. anti-SLAPP statutes provide one-way fee-shifting (defendants recover fees if they prevail). South Korea's limited fee-shifting provides minimal deterrence. The EU Directive requires cost awards for successful anti-SLAPP defendants.

### Cross-Border Dimension

The EU Directive specifically addresses cross-border SLAPP suits, where plaintiffs may forum-shop for jurisdictions with weaker protections. This "libel tourism" concern -- exemplified by the phenomenon of UK-based defamation claims against U.S. journalists, which prompted the U.S. SPEECH Act of 2010 (28 U.S.C. Section 4102) -- is increasingly relevant in the digital age where speech crosses borders instantaneously.
