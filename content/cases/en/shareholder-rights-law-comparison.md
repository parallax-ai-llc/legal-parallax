## Summary

Shareholder rights -- the bundle of legal entitlements held by equity owners in a corporation, including voting rights, dividend rights, information rights, and the right to bring derivative and direct claims against management -- form the foundation of corporate accountability and capital market confidence. South Korea grants shareholders extensive rights under the Commercial Code (상법), including the right to bring derivative suits (주주대표소송) with a relatively low ownership threshold of 0.01% for listed companies, the right to inspect corporate books and records, and enhanced minority shareholder protections enacted in response to chaebol governance concerns, with the 2020 amendment introducing multiple derivative suits (다중대표소송) permitting parent company shareholders to sue subsidiary directors. The United States provides shareholders with voting rights, inspection rights, and litigation rights under state corporate law (primarily Delaware), with derivative suits governed by the demand requirement and special litigation committees under Zapata Corp. v. Maldonado (1981), and federal securities law (Securities Exchange Act Section 14(a)) protecting proxy voting rights, supplemented by the PSLRA's heightened pleading standards for securities fraud class actions. Germany grants shareholders rights under the Aktiengesetz (AktG), including the right to challenge shareholder meeting resolutions (Anfechtungsklage, Section 246 AktG), limited derivative suit authority under Section 148 AktG introduced in 2005, and extensive information rights at shareholder meetings (Auskunftsrecht, Section 131 AktG), with the distinctive feature that major corporate decisions require supermajority shareholder approval. At the EU level, the Shareholder Rights Directive (2007/36/EC, as amended by Directive 2017/828) harmonizes cross-border voting rights, mandates say-on-pay, and requires related party transaction oversight, while the ECHR has recognized shareholder rights as property rights protected under Protocol 1, Article 1.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea has progressively strengthened minority shareholder rights, driven largely by the need to counterbalance chaebol controlling shareholder power.

### Derivative Suits (주주대표소송)

The Commercial Code Article 403 permits shareholders holding 0.01% or more of outstanding shares for at least six months to bring derivative suits on behalf of the corporation against directors who have breached their duties. This threshold is among the lowest globally, reflecting Korea's policy priority of enabling minority shareholder enforcement.

**Multiple Derivative Suits (다중대표소송):** The 2020 Commercial Code amendment introduced Article 406-2, permitting shareholders of a parent company to bring derivative suits against directors of a subsidiary company. This reform addressed the long-standing problem of chaebol groups using subsidiary structures to insulate controlling family directors from shareholder litigation. The parent company shareholder must hold 0.5% of the parent's shares for six months.

**Key Case -- Samsung Biologics Accounting Fraud (2018-present):** Minority shareholders of Samsung C&T brought derivative actions alleging that Samsung Biologics' accounting practices were manipulated to benefit the Lee family's succession plan. The litigation highlighted the critical role of derivative suits in chaebol accountability.

### Shareholder Proposals and Voting

Shareholders holding 0.5% of outstanding shares for at least six months may propose agenda items for shareholder meetings (Article 363-2). The Commercial Code was amended in 2024 to permit dual-class shares (차등의결권 주식) for technology startups, reversing Korea's long-standing one-share-one-vote principle. The amendment limits dual-class structures to companies listed on the KOSDAQ market within specified time limits.

### Book and Record Inspection

Shareholders holding 3% or more of outstanding shares may inspect the corporation's accounting books and records (Article 466). The Supreme Court has held that the inspection right extends to subsidiary records when the parent-subsidiary relationship is relevant to the shareholder's legitimate purpose.

### Appraisal Rights

Shareholders who oppose certain fundamental transactions -- including mergers (Article 374-2), comprehensive asset transfers, and stock swaps -- have the right to demand that the corporation purchase their shares at fair value. The fair value determination is subject to court review if the parties cannot agree.

## United States

### Legal Framework

American shareholder rights are governed by state corporate law and federal securities regulation, with Delaware law serving as the de facto national standard.

### Derivative Litigation

Delaware law permits any stockholder to bring derivative claims on behalf of the corporation, subject to the demand requirement: the plaintiff must first demand that the board of directors take action, unless demand would be futile.

**Aronson v. Lewis, 473 A.2d 805 (Del. 1984):** Established the two-prong demand futility test: demand is excused if a reasonable doubt exists that (1) the directors are disinterested and independent, or (2) the challenged transaction was the product of a valid business judgment.

**Zapata Corp. v. Maldonado, 430 A.2d 779 (Del. 1981):** Established that a special litigation committee (SLC) of independent directors may seek dismissal of derivative claims, subject to a two-step judicial inquiry: (1) the SLC must demonstrate its independence and good faith investigation, and (2) the court may exercise its own business judgment to determine whether dismissal is appropriate.

### Shareholder Proposals (SEC Rule 14a-8)

SEC Rule 14a-8 permits shareholders meeting eligibility requirements to include proposals in the company's proxy statement. The 2020 amendments raised eligibility thresholds: shareholders must hold at least $25,000 in shares for one year, $15,000 for two years, or $2,000 for three years. Companies may exclude proposals that violate law, relate to ordinary business operations, or address significantly less than 5% of total assets.

### Books and Records Inspection

DGCL Section 220 grants stockholders the right to inspect the company's stock ledger and other books and records for any "proper purpose." The Delaware Court of Chancery has interpreted "proper purpose" broadly to include investigating potential mismanagement, waste, or wrongdoing. In AmerisourceBergen Corp. v. Lebanon County Employees' Retirement Fund (Del. 2020), the Delaware Supreme Court emphasized that Section 220 demands are an "essential" tool for shareholders.

### Federal Securities Protections

The Securities Exchange Act Section 10(b) and SEC Rule 10b-5 provide a private right of action for securities fraud, including misstatements and omissions in proxy solicitations. The PSLRA (1995) imposed heightened pleading requirements (scienter, loss causation) and a discovery stay, while SLUSA (1998) preempted state securities class actions involving nationally traded securities.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German shareholder rights reflect a balance between shareholder protection and the institutional stability of the two-tier board structure.

### Shareholder Meeting Rights

German shareholders exercise their rights primarily at the annual general meeting (Hauptversammlung). The AktG grants extensive meeting rights:

**Information Right (Auskunftsrecht, Section 131):** Each shareholder has the right to be informed by the Vorstand about company affairs at the general meeting, insofar as the information is necessary for the proper assessment of an agenda item. The Vorstand may refuse information only in narrowly defined circumstances (trade secrets, ongoing litigation, tax assessment risks).

**Right to Challenge Resolutions (Anfechtungsklage, Section 246):** Shareholders may challenge general meeting resolutions that violate the law or the articles of association within one month of the resolution. This right has been extensively used, leading to concerns about abusive challenge litigation (räuberische Aktionäre) that has prompted legislative reforms requiring plaintiffs to demonstrate minimum shareholding thresholds.

### Derivative Suits (Section 148 AktG)

Germany introduced a limited derivative suit mechanism in 2005 through Section 148 AktG. Shareholders holding 1% of share capital or shares with a nominal value of EUR 100,000 may petition the court to authorize a suit against directors. The court grants authorization if facts justify the suspicion that the company suffered damage due to dishonesty or gross violation of law or articles of association, the suit is in the company's interest, and no overriding company interests oppose it. In practice, Section 148 has been used rarely due to the high threshold and procedural complexity.

### Squeeze-Out (Section 327a AktG)

A majority shareholder holding 95% of share capital may request the general meeting to approve the transfer of minority shareholders' shares in exchange for adequate cash compensation. The compensation adequacy is subject to court review in appraisal proceedings (Spruchverfahren, Section 327f AktG).

### Shareholder Forum (Aktionärsforum)

Section 127a AktG provides an electronic shareholder forum where shareholders can publish proposals, counterproposals, and calls for coordinated voting on the electronic Federal Gazette (Bundesanzeiger).

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Protection of Shareholder Rights

The ECHR has recognized shareholder rights as property rights protected under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1.

**Sovtransavto Holding v. Ukraine (Application No. 48553/99, Judgment of July 25, 2002):** The Court found a violation of Article 1, Protocol 1 where Ukrainian authorities interfered with the applicant's shareholding through irregular registration of share transfers. The decision established that shares constitute "possessions" within the meaning of the Convention.

**Marini v. Albania (Application No. 3738/02, Judgment of December 18, 2007):** The Court held that the failure of Albanian courts to effectively adjudicate a shareholder dispute constituted a violation of Article 6 (fair trial) and Article 1, Protocol 1.

### Shareholder Rights Directive (SRD/SRD II)

The original Shareholder Rights Directive (2007/36/EC) and its amendment (Directive 2017/828, SRD II) establish minimum shareholder rights across the EU:

**Cross-Border Voting (Articles 3a-3c):** Intermediaries must facilitate the exercise of shareholder rights across borders, including transmission of voting rights and confirmation of vote receipt.

**Say-on-Pay (Articles 9a-9b):** Shareholders have the right to vote on the remuneration policy (binding vote at least every four years) and the remuneration report (annual advisory or binding vote).

**Related Party Transactions (Article 9c):** Material transactions with related parties must be publicly announced and subject to approval by the shareholders or supervisory body.

**Proxy Advisors (Article 3j):** Proxy advisory firms must disclose their methodologies, potential conflicts of interest, and codes of conduct.

### EU Capital Markets Union

The EU's Capital Markets Union initiative has produced several instruments affecting shareholder rights, including the Listing Act reform package (2024), which aims to simplify listing requirements and make EU capital markets more attractive, and the proposed harmonization of insolvency frameworks that would affect creditor and shareholder hierarchies in corporate restructuring.

## Comparative Analysis

### Derivative Suit Accessibility

South Korea provides the most accessible derivative suit mechanism, with a 0.01% ownership threshold for listed companies. The United States has no minimum ownership threshold but imposes significant procedural hurdles (demand requirement, special litigation committees, PSLRA pleading standards). Germany's 1% threshold and additional court authorization requirements make derivative suits the least accessible.

### Dual-Class Shares

The jurisdictions reflect different philosophies on voting power concentration. The United States widely permits dual-class structures, with major technology companies (Alphabet, Meta, Snap) maintaining multi-class shares. Germany prohibits multiple voting rights. South Korea only recently (2024) permitted limited dual-class structures for KOSDAQ-listed technology startups. The EU does not harmonize this issue, but the Listing Act reform package addresses multiple-vote share structures.

### Shareholder Activism Culture

The United States has the most developed activist ecosystem, with hedge fund activists routinely seeking board representation and strategic changes. South Korea has seen growing activism, led by the National Pension Service and activist investors challenging chaebol governance. German activism remains comparatively muted, constrained by the two-tier board structure and codetermination system.
