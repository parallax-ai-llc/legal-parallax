## Summary

Church-state separation -- the constitutional and legal principle governing the relationship between governmental authority and religious institutions -- represents one of the most varied and contested areas of comparative constitutional law, with models ranging from strict separationism to established state religions. South Korea's Constitution (대한민국 헌법) establishes a separationist framework through Article 20, which guarantees freedom of religion (종교의 자유) in paragraph 1 and explicitly provides that "no state religion shall be recognized, and church and state shall be separated" (국교는 인정되지 아니하며, 종교와 정치는 분리된다) in paragraph 2, with the Constitutional Court (헌법재판소) interpreting these provisions in landmark decisions addressing religious education in public schools, military chaplaincy, and the tax-exempt status of religious organizations. The United States Constitution addresses the relationship through the First Amendment's Establishment Clause ("Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion") and Free Exercise Clause ("or prohibiting the free exercise thereof"), interpreted through two centuries of Supreme Court jurisprudence from Everson v. Board of Education (1947) through Kennedy v. Bremerton School District (2022), with the current Court favoring a historical practices approach over the previously dominant Lemon test. Germany operates a unique cooperative model under Articles 140 of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz) incorporating Articles 136-141 of the Weimar Constitution, granting major religious communities the status of public law corporations (Körperschaften des öffentlichen Rechts) with the right to levy church taxes (Kirchensteuer) collected by the state, while simultaneously guaranteeing religious freedom under Article 4 GG. The European framework under Article 9 ECHR protects freedom of thought, conscience, and religion, with the European Court of Human Rights recognizing a wide margin of appreciation for member states to determine their own church-state arrangements, resulting in the coexistence of established churches (United Kingdom, Denmark, Greece), cooperative models (Germany, Austria), and laïcité (France) within a single human rights framework.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's church-state separation framework is rooted in Article 20 of the Constitution (대한민국 헌법), which provides two fundamental guarantees:

**Article 20(1) (종교의 자유):** "All citizens shall enjoy freedom of religion." This provision encompasses the freedom to hold religious beliefs, to manifest religion through worship, observance, practice, and teaching, and the freedom not to hold or practice any religion.

**Article 20(2) (정교분리):** "No state religion shall be recognized, and church and state shall be separated." (국교는 인정되지 아니하며, 종교와 정치는 분리된다.) This provision explicitly prohibits the establishment of a state religion and mandates the institutional separation of religious and governmental authority.

**Article 11(1) (평등원칙):** "All citizens shall be equal before the law, and there shall be no discrimination in political, economic, social, or cultural life on account of sex, religion, or social status."

### Constitutional Court Jurisprudence

**Constitutional Court Decision 2010Hun-Ba430 (2013) -- Buddhist Chaplaincy Case:** The Constitutional Court upheld the military chaplaincy system, including the appointment of Buddhist, Protestant, and Catholic chaplains as military officers, holding that the system served the legitimate purpose of protecting the religious freedom of military personnel who are restricted in their ability to access civilian religious institutions. The Court applied a proportionality analysis, finding that the system did not amount to state endorsement of particular religions because chaplains of multiple faiths were appointed.

**Constitutional Court Decision 2001Hun-Ma687 (2007) -- Religious Education Case:** The Court addressed the constitutionality of mandatory religious education at private schools receiving government subsidies. The Court held that while private religious schools enjoy freedom of education (Article 31) and religious freedom (Article 20(1)), students' negative religious freedom -- the right not to be compelled to receive religious instruction -- must also be protected. The Court ruled that alternative classes must be provided for students who object to religious instruction.

**Constitutional Court Decision 96Hun-Ba2 (1998) -- Temple Stay Case:** The Court addressed government funding for Buddhist temple preservation and cultural programs, upholding the funding as permissible under the cultural heritage protection rationale rather than constituting state support for religion.

### Religious Organizations and Taxation

Religious organizations in South Korea are exempt from corporate income tax on revenue from religious activities under the Corporate Tax Act (법인세법). However, significant public debate has surrounded the taxation of clergy income. The Income Tax Act (소득세법) was amended effective January 1, 2018, to require religious leaders to report and pay income tax on their compensation, after decades during which clergy income was effectively untaxed. The amendment was the product of more than 15 years of legislative debate and faced significant opposition from religious communities.

### Current Issues

South Korea's religious landscape -- approximately 29 percent Protestant Christian, 23 percent Buddhist, 11 percent Catholic, and 37 percent non-religious as of the 2015 Census -- creates ongoing church-state tensions. The Shincheonji Church's role in the early COVID-19 outbreak in Daegu (February 2020) led to government orders requiring religious organizations to comply with public health measures, raising questions about the scope of government authority to regulate religious gatherings during public health emergencies.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The First Amendment to the United States Constitution provides: "Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof." These sixteen words have generated more Supreme Court litigation than virtually any other constitutional provision.

**Establishment Clause:** Prohibits the government from establishing, endorsing, or providing preferential support to religion. Applied to state and local governments through the Fourteenth Amendment in Everson v. Board of Education, 330 U.S. 1 (1947).

**Free Exercise Clause:** Protects the right of individuals and organizations to practice their religion free from government interference. The scope of this protection has varied significantly through judicial interpretation.

### Doctrinal Evolution

**Lemon Test (1971-2022):** In Lemon v. Kurtzman, 403 U.S. 602 (1971), the Supreme Court established a three-part test for Establishment Clause cases: (1) the government action must have a secular legislative purpose; (2) its principal or primary effect must be one that neither advances nor inhibits religion; and (3) it must not foster excessive government entanglement with religion.

**Endorsement Test (1984):** Justice O'Connor proposed in Lynch v. Donnelly, 465 U.S. 668 (1984), that the Establishment Clause prohibits government action that conveys a message of endorsement or disapproval of religion.

**Coercion Test:** Applied primarily in school prayer cases, including Lee v. Weisman, 505 U.S. 577 (1992), prohibiting government coercion of religious activity.

**Historical Practices Test (2022):** In Kennedy v. Bremerton School District, 597 U.S. 507 (2022), the Supreme Court effectively replaced the Lemon test with a historical practices and understandings approach, holding that the Establishment Clause must be interpreted "by reference to historical practices and understandings." The 6-3 decision permitted a public school football coach to pray on the field after games, marking a significant doctrinal shift.

### Tax Exemption

Religious organizations are exempt from federal income tax under IRC Section 501(c)(3). The Supreme Court upheld the constitutionality of this exemption in Walz v. Tax Commission, 397 U.S. 664 (1970), reasoning that taxation of churches would create greater entanglement than exemption. Religious organizations, unlike other 501(c)(3) entities, are not required to file annual Form 990 information returns with the IRS.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's cooperative separation model is constitutionally anchored in multiple provisions of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz):

**Article 4 GG (Glaubensfreiheit):** "(1) Freedom of faith, of conscience, and freedom of professing a religious or philosophical creed shall be inviolable. (2) The undisturbed practice of religion shall be guaranteed."

**Article 140 GG:** Incorporates Articles 136 through 141 of the Weimar Constitution of 1919 as integral parts of the Basic Law. These provisions establish the framework for the cooperative relationship between state and religious communities.

**Weimar Constitution Article 137:** "(1) There shall be no state church. (5) Religious societies which are corporations under public law shall be entitled to levy taxes on the basis of the civil tax lists in accordance with Land law."

### Church Tax (Kirchensteuer)

The most distinctive feature of Germany's church-state model is the Kirchensteuer (church tax), a surcharge of 8 percent (in Bavaria and Baden-Württemberg) or 9 percent (in other states) on the income tax of registered members of tax-collecting religious communities. The tax is collected by the state tax authorities and transferred to the religious communities. In 2023, the church tax generated approximately EUR 13 billion for the Catholic and Protestant churches combined. Taxpayers may cease paying church tax by formally declaring their exit from their religious community (Kirchenaustritt) at a civil registry office or court, a process that has accelerated dramatically -- approximately 900,000 people left the two major churches in 2022 alone.

### Public Law Corporation Status

Major religious communities in Germany hold the status of corporations under public law (Körperschaften des öffentlichen Rechts), granting them privileges including the right to levy church tax, exemption from certain civil service regulations, special employment law provisions (the so-called "Third Way" in labor relations), and the right to provide religious instruction in public schools. The Federal Constitutional Court confirmed in BVerfGE 102, 370 (2000) that religious communities may obtain public law corporation status if they demonstrate permanence and organizational structure, regardless of their size or the extent of their public activity.

### Religious Instruction in Public Schools

**Article 7(3) GG:** "Religious instruction shall form part of the regular curriculum in state schools, with the exception of non-denominational schools. Without prejudice to the State's right of supervision, religious instruction shall be given in accordance with the tenets of the religious communities." This provision guarantees confessional religious instruction as a regular school subject. Students (or their parents) may opt out, and alternative ethics instruction is available. The Federal Constitutional Court upheld this system in BVerfGE 74, 244 (1987), finding that it represents a constitutionally mandated cooperation between state and church rather than a violation of religious neutrality.

### Crucifix Decision

**BVerfGE 93, 1 (1995) -- Kruzifix-Beschluss:** The Federal Constitutional Court ruled that a Bavarian ordinance requiring crucifixes in all public school classrooms violated the religious freedom of non-Christian students under Article 4 GG. The decision provoked enormous public controversy, particularly in Bavaria, and led to a compromise regulation allowing removal of crucifixes only upon specific objection.

### Federal Constitutional Court Jurisprudence

**BVerfGE 108, 282 (2003) -- Headscarf Decision (Ludin):** The Court held that a blanket prohibition on teachers wearing religious headscarves in public schools required a specific legislative basis and could not be imposed by administrative order alone. Several German states subsequently enacted legislation restricting religious symbols for public school teachers.

## ECHR / EU

### Article 9 ECHR

Article 9 of the European Convention on Human Rights provides: "(1) Everyone has the right to freedom of thought, conscience and religion; this right includes freedom to change his religion or belief and freedom, either alone or in community with others and in public or private, to manifest his religion or belief, in worship, teaching, practice and observance. (2) Freedom to manifest one's religion or beliefs shall be subject only to such limitations as are prescribed by law and are necessary in a democratic society in the interests of public safety, for the protection of public order, health or morals, or for the protection of the rights and freedoms of others."

### Margin of Appreciation

The European Court of Human Rights has consistently recognized a wide margin of appreciation for states in determining their church-state arrangements, reflecting the diversity of European traditions.

**Lautsi v. Italy (Application No. 30814/06, Grand Chamber, March 18, 2011):** The Grand Chamber reversed the Chamber's finding and held that Italy's display of crucifixes in public school classrooms did not violate Article 2 of Protocol No. 1 (right to education) read with Article 9 ECHR. The Court held that the crucifix was an "essentially passive symbol" and that the decision whether to perpetuate such a tradition fell within the margin of appreciation.

**S.A.S. v. France (Application No. 43835/11, Grand Chamber, July 1, 2014):** The Grand Chamber upheld France's ban on full-face covering in public places, finding no violation of Article 9. The Court accepted France's argument that the ban served the legitimate aim of protecting the conditions for "living together" (vivre ensemble).

**Fernández Martínez v. Spain (Application No. 56030/07, Grand Chamber, June 12, 2014):** The Court held that Spain did not violate Article 8 (private life) by declining to renew the contract of a Catholic religion teacher who had publicly advocated for the abolition of priestly celibacy. The Court recognized the autonomy of religious communities in selecting their representatives for religious instruction in state schools.

### EU Framework

The EU does not regulate church-state relations directly. Article 17 of the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union (TFEU) provides: "(1) The Union respects and does not prejudice the status under national law of churches and religious associations or communities in the Member States. (2) The Union equally respects the status under national law of philosophical and non-confessional organisations. (3) Recognising their identity and their specific contribution, the Union shall maintain an open, transparent and regular dialogue with these churches and organisations."

Article 10 of the Charter of Fundamental Rights of the European Union mirrors Article 9 ECHR in protecting freedom of thought, conscience, and religion.

## Comparative Analysis

### Separation Models

The four jurisdictions illustrate three distinct models of church-state relations:

**Strict Separation (South Korea):** South Korea's constitutional text mandates the clearest separation, explicitly prohibiting state religion and requiring institutional separation. The Korean model most closely resembles the French laïcité tradition, though without the same degree of restriction on religious expression in public spaces.

**Evolving Separation (United States):** The U.S. model has shifted from the strict separationism of the mid-20th century toward greater accommodation of religion in public life, as evidenced by the Kennedy v. Bremerton decision's replacement of the Lemon test. The current trajectory favors permitting public religious expression that would have been considered unconstitutional under earlier doctrine.

**Cooperative Separation (Germany):** Germany's model rejects both strict separation and established religion in favor of structured cooperation. The church tax, confessional religious instruction in public schools, and public law corporation status for religious communities distinguish this approach fundamentally from the Korean and American models.

**Pluralistic (ECHR/EU):** The European framework accommodates all three models within a single human rights system through the margin of appreciation doctrine, requiring only that member states protect the core of religious freedom while permitting wide variation in institutional arrangements.

### Religious Tax

Germany stands alone among the four jurisdictions in maintaining a state-collected religious tax. South Korea and the United States exempt religious organizations from taxation but do not actively collect revenue on their behalf. The church tax model enables substantial religious institutional activity but has also been cited as a contributing factor in mass church departures, as formal exit from the religious community is the only way to cease tax liability.

### Religious Education

The divergence in approaches to religious education in public schools is stark: Germany constitutionally guarantees confessional religious instruction, South Korea and the United States prohibit religious instruction in public schools, and the ECHR permits both approaches under the margin of appreciation.
