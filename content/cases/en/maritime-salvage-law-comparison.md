## Summary

Maritime salvage -- the legal framework governing the voluntary rescue of a vessel, its cargo, or persons from peril at sea, and the salvor's right to a reward for successful rescue -- is one of the oldest branches of maritime law, dating back to Rhodian sea law and the medieval Rolls of Oléron, and codified in the modern era primarily through the International Convention on Salvage (London, 1989), which replaced the earlier Brussels Convention for the Unification of Certain Rules of Law Relating to Assistance and Salvage at Sea (1910). South Korea regulates maritime salvage through Chapter 8 of the Commercial Act (상법 제8편 해상편), specifically Articles 882-888, which establish the conditions for salvage claims, the duty to render assistance, and the criteria for determining salvage awards, supplemented by the Korean Maritime Safety Act (해사안전법) and Korea's participation in international maritime conventions administered by the International Maritime Organization (IMO). The United States, with one of the world's most developed admiralty law traditions, governs salvage through a combination of the Salvage Convention of 1989 (ratified in 1992), general maritime law as developed by federal courts, and the Salvage Act (46 U.S.C. Section 80101-80107), with federal courts applying equitable principles to determine salvage awards based on the "Blackwall factors" established by the Supreme Court in The Blackwall, 77 U.S. 1 (1869). Germany implements the 1989 Salvage Convention through the Handelsgesetzbuch (HGB, Commercial Code) Sections 740-753, with maritime claims adjudicated by specialized maritime chambers (Kammern für Handelssachen) in German courts, and Lloyd's Open Form (LOF) contracts widely used in the German maritime industry. At the European level, the 1989 Salvage Convention provides the primary framework ratified by most EU member states, the International Maritime Organization develops and updates salvage-related conventions, and the Nairobi International Convention on the Removal of Wrecks (2007) addresses the gap between salvage law and wreck removal obligations.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's maritime salvage law is codified in Chapter 8 of the Commercial Act (상법), Part 5 on Maritime Commerce (해상편). Articles 882 through 888 establish the basic framework for salvage operations and awards. South Korea is a party to the International Convention on Salvage (1989) and the International Convention for the Safety of Life at Sea (SOLAS, 1974).

### Key Provisions

Article 882 of the Commercial Act establishes the right to a salvage award for successful assistance to a vessel or cargo in danger at sea. The traditional "no cure, no pay" principle applies: a salvor is entitled to a reward only if the salvage operation is successful in preserving or recovering the vessel or cargo. Article 883 enumerates the criteria for determining salvage awards, largely mirroring Article 13 of the 1989 Salvage Convention: the salved value; the skill and effort of the salvors; the nature and degree of danger; the time and expenses incurred; the risk of liability incurred by the salvors; the promptness of services; the availability and use of vessels or equipment; and the state of readiness and efficiency of the salvor's equipment.

### Korean Maritime Industry Context

South Korea is one of the world's largest shipbuilding nations and a major maritime trading state, giving salvage law significant economic importance. Korean shipping companies, including HMM (formerly Hyundai Merchant Marine) and SM Line, operate extensive fleets. The Sewol ferry disaster of April 16, 2014, which killed 304 people (mostly high school students), while primarily a maritime safety and liability case, raised complex salvage and wreck removal questions during the subsequent hull recovery operation conducted by Shanghai Salvage Company in 2017.

| Environmental protection | Special compensation for preventing pollution |

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has one of the most developed salvage law traditions in the world, with federal courts exercising admiralty jurisdiction under Article III, Section 2 of the Constitution and 28 U.S.C. Section 1333. The 1989 Salvage Convention was ratified by the US in 1992 and implemented through the Salvage Act (46 U.S.C. Sections 80101-80107).

**The Blackwall, 77 U.S. (10 Wall.) 1 (1869):** The Supreme Court established the foundational criteria for determining salvage awards under US general maritime law, identifying factors including: the labor expended; the promptitude, skill, and energy displayed; the value of the property employed in the salvage; the risk incurred; the value of the property saved; and the degree of danger from which the property was rescued. These "Blackwall factors" remain the primary framework supplemented by the Convention's Article 13 criteria.

**Duty to Render Assistance:** 46 U.S.C. Section 80102 codifies the duty of masters of vessels to render assistance to persons in danger at sea, consistent with SOLAS obligations. Failure to render assistance is subject to penalties including fine and imprisonment.

### Pure vs. Contract Salvage

US law distinguishes between pure (voluntary) salvage and contract salvage. Pure salvage arises when a salvor renders assistance without a pre-existing agreement, with the award determined by the court. Contract salvage arises under a salvage agreement -- most commonly Lloyd's Open Form (LOF) or BIMCO standard forms -- which typically provide for arbitration of the salvage award.

### Environmental Salvage

The interaction between salvage law and environmental liability has been a significant issue. The Oil Pollution Act of 1990 (OPA, 33 U.S.C. Section 2701 et seq.) and the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act (CERCLA) create liability frameworks that may interact with salvage operations. The 1989 Convention's Article 14 special compensation provision addresses environmental salvage by providing that a salvor who has failed to earn a salvage reward but has prevented or minimized environmental damage is entitled to special compensation from the vessel owner.

### Notable Salvage Cases

**Treasure Salvors, Inc. v. The Unidentified Wrecked and Abandoned Sailing Vessel (Nuestra Señora de Atocha, 1982):** A landmark case involving the salvage of the Spanish galleon Atocha, establishing principles for the application of the law of finds versus the law of salvage to historic shipwrecks.

**The Tug Ocean Prince (M/V Prestige, 2002):** The salvage of the oil tanker Prestige off the coast of Spain raised complex questions about environmental salvage compensation.

| Blackwall Factor | Weight |
|-----------------|--------|
| Labor expended by salvors | Significant |
| Skill and energy displayed | High for professional salvors |
| Value of salvor's equipment risked | Proportional to risk |
| Value of property saved | Primary benchmark (award typically 5-25% of salved value) |
| Degree of danger | Higher danger, higher award |
| Promptness and success | Rewarded with higher percentage |

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany implements the 1989 Salvage Convention through Sections 740-753 of the Handelsgesetzbuch (HGB, Commercial Code), as amended by the Gesetz zur Reform des Seehandelsrechts (Maritime Trade Law Reform Act) of 2013, which comprehensively modernized German maritime commercial law. Germany is a party to the 1989 Salvage Convention.

### HGB Salvage Provisions

**Section 740 HGB (Duty to Render Assistance):** The master of a vessel is obligated to render assistance to any person in danger at sea, provided this can be done without serious danger to the master's own vessel, crew, and passengers. This provision implements SOLAS Chapter V, Regulation 33.

**Section 743 HGB (Salvage Award):** A salvor who has achieved a useful result has a right to a salvage award (Bergelohn). The award is determined based on the criteria in Section 744, which mirrors Article 13 of the 1989 Convention. The award may not exceed the salved value.

**Section 745 HGB (Special Compensation):** Implements Article 14 of the 1989 Convention, providing for special compensation to salvors who have prevented or minimized environmental damage in cases where the salvage award under Section 744 is insufficient.

### SCOPIC Clause

The Special Compensation P&I Clubs (SCOPIC) clause, developed by the International Group of P&I Clubs and incorporated into Lloyd's Open Form contracts, provides an alternative mechanism for calculating special compensation for environmental salvage. The SCOPIC clause is widely used in German maritime practice and provides more predictable compensation than the Convention's Article 14 mechanism.

### Hamburg as Maritime Center

Hamburg, Germany's largest port and one of Europe's busiest, serves as a major center for maritime arbitration and dispute resolution. The German Maritime Arbitration Association (Deutscher Verein für internationales Seerecht) and Hamburg arbitration chambers handle significant volumes of salvage disputes. Many German salvage cases are also resolved through London arbitration under LOF contracts.

### Penalties

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| Failure to render assistance (HGB § 740) | Criminal prosecution under StGB § 323c (failure to render assistance); maritime disciplinary proceedings |
| Fraudulent salvage claim | Criminal fraud prosecution; civil damages |
| Environmental damage during salvage | Liability under HGB § 750; environmental regulation penalties |

## ECHR & EU

### International Convention on Salvage (1989)

The International Convention on Salvage, adopted on April 28, 1989, at a diplomatic conference convened by the IMO, entered into force on July 14, 1996. It replaced the earlier 1910 Brussels Salvage Convention and incorporated modern developments, particularly regarding environmental protection.

**Article 12 (No Cure, No Pay):** Salvage operations that have had a useful result give right to a reward. If salvage operations produce no useful result, no reward is due (subject to the environmental special compensation in Article 14).

**Article 13 (Criteria for Fixing the Reward):** Ten criteria are specified: (a) salved value; (b) skill and effort in preventing environmental damage; (c) measure of success; (d) nature and degree of danger; (e) skill and effort of salvors; (f) time, expense, and losses of salvors; (g) risk of liability; (h) promptness; (i) availability and use of equipment; and (j) state of readiness and efficiency.

**Article 14 (Special Compensation):** Provides that a salvor who has carried out salvage operations for a vessel that threatened environmental damage is entitled to special compensation from the vessel owner if the salvage award under Article 13 does not adequately compensate the salvor's efforts to prevent environmental harm.

### Nairobi International Convention on the Removal of Wrecks (2007)

The Nairobi Convention, adopted on May 18, 2007, and entered into force on April 14, 2015, addresses the gap between salvage law (which focuses on saving property) and wreck removal (which focuses on eliminating navigational and environmental hazards). The Convention establishes state authority to locate, mark, and remove wrecks that pose hazards, and requires shipowners to maintain wreck removal insurance. The EU encouraged member state ratification.

### EU Environmental Liability

Directive 2004/35/CE on environmental liability applies to maritime environmental damage, creating liability obligations that intersect with salvage law. Where salvage operations cause environmental damage, the Environmental Liability Directive may impose remediation obligations on the vessel owner or salvor.

### IMO Role

The International Maritime Organization, headquartered in London, serves as the UN specialized agency responsible for maritime safety and environmental protection. IMO conventions relevant to salvage include: SOLAS (duty to render assistance); MARPOL (pollution prevention obligations during salvage); and the International Convention on Oil Pollution Preparedness, Response and Co-operation (OPRC, 1990).

## Comparative Analysis

### Legal Tradition

All four jurisdictions have adopted the 1989 Salvage Convention framework, creating substantial harmonization. Differences arise in domestic implementation: the US supplements the Convention with its rich common law tradition (Blackwall factors); Germany integrates Convention provisions into the HGB; and South Korea incorporates them into the Commercial Act.

### No Cure, No Pay

The fundamental "no cure, no pay" principle is universal but has been modified by the environmental salvage provisions of Article 14 and the SCOPIC clause. These modifications reflect recognition that the traditional incentive structure (based solely on salvage success) inadequately incentivizes environmental protection efforts.

### Environmental Salvage

Environmental salvage represents the most significant recent development in salvage law. The 1989 Convention's Article 14 introduced the concept, but its practical application proved difficult, leading to the development of the SCOPIC clause as a more workable alternative. The interaction between environmental salvage compensation and pollution liability regimes (OPA in the US, Environmental Liability Directive in the EU) remains complex.

### Dispute Resolution

Salvage disputes are resolved through multiple forums: national admiralty courts, London arbitration (the dominant forum for LOF contracts), and specialized maritime arbitration centers. The concentration of salvage arbitration in London reflects the historical dominance of Lloyd's of London in the marine insurance market.
