## Summary

Litigation funding -- the provision of financial resources by third parties to support legal proceedings in exchange for a share of any recovery or other financial return -- has transformed from an obscure and often prohibited practice into a multi-billion-dollar global industry that raises fundamental questions about access to justice, the commodification of legal claims, and the potential for abusive litigation. South Korea does not have a comprehensive statutory framework governing litigation funding but addresses the practice through Civil Code provisions on assignment of claims (채권양도), the Attorney-at-Law Act (변호사법) prohibiting contingency fee arrangements above certain thresholds, and Supreme Court jurisprudence on the enforceability of litigation funding agreements, with the Korean legal market showing growing interest in third-party funding particularly in international arbitration and patent litigation contexts. The United States has seen the most rapid growth in litigation funding, with the market estimated at over $15 billion in deployed capital as of 2024, governed by a patchwork of state common law doctrines (the historical prohibitions of champerty and maintenance having been abolished or substantially relaxed in most states), federal procedural rules addressing disclosure, and varying state regulations, with the Supreme Court's decision in Burford Capital Management v. Purdue Pharma highlighting the growing significance of funder involvement in major litigation. Germany has a well-established litigation funding market, with Processfinanz AG having pioneered commercial litigation funding in 1998, operating within a framework of general contract law (BGB), the Rechtsdienstleistungsgesetz (Legal Services Act) of 2008, and the "loser pays" fee-shifting rule under ZPO Section 91 which creates unique market dynamics, while the Federal Court of Justice (BGH) has consistently upheld the legality of litigation funding arrangements. The EU has addressed litigation funding through the Representative Actions Directive (EU) 2020/1828 requiring transparency and safeguards for third-party funded collective actions, the European Parliament's 2022 Resolution calling for EU-wide regulation, and the ECHR's jurisprudence on access to justice under Article 6 which supports the principle that litigation funding can enhance the right of access to court while requiring proportionality in any regulatory restrictions.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea lacks a dedicated litigation funding statute. The practice is addressed through general legal principles and sector-specific regulations.

**Civil Code (민법) -- Assignment of Claims (채권양도):** Articles 449-452 of the Civil Code permit the assignment of claims, providing the basic legal mechanism through which litigation funding can be structured. A funder may acquire a partial interest in a claim or may provide funding in exchange for a contractual right to a portion of any recovery.

**Attorney-at-Law Act (변호사법):** Article 31 prohibits attorneys from receiving compensation exceeding a reasonable amount, and Attorney Ethics Rules limit contingency fee arrangements. The Korean Bar Association's Attorney Fee Standards provide that contingency fee arrangements should not exceed specific thresholds (typically 10-15 percent of the recovery amount in most cases, though higher percentages are permitted for small-value claims). These restrictions on attorney fees indirectly constrain the economics of litigation funding by limiting the cost structure of funded claims.

**Supreme Court Decision 2015Da200111 (2016):** The Supreme Court addressed the validity of a litigation funding arrangement in which a third party provided financial support for litigation in exchange for a percentage of the recovery. The Court held that such arrangements are not per se invalid under Korean law but are subject to public policy limitations under Civil Code Article 103 (공서양속에 반하는 법률행위 -- juridical acts contrary to good morals and social order). The Court indicated that agreements imposing unconscionable terms on the funded party or designed to promote frivolous litigation may be voided.

### Emerging Market

The Korean litigation funding market is in its early stages but growing, driven by:

- **International Arbitration:** Korean companies involved in international commercial and investor-state arbitration have increasingly utilized foreign-based litigation funders. The Korean Commercial Arbitration Board (KCAB) has acknowledged the practice without formal regulation.
- **Patent Litigation:** The growth of patent disputes, particularly involving technology companies, has created demand for litigation funding. Several Korean patent assertion entities operate with third-party funding support.
- **Collective Actions:** The limited scope of Korean class action mechanisms (securities only for damages) has constrained the development of funded collective litigation, though consumer organization suits may attract funding support.

### Regulatory Proposals

The Korean Ministry of Justice has conducted preliminary research on litigation funding regulation, examining the Australian, UK, and Singapore models. As of 2025, no formal legislative proposal has been introduced, but the ministry's 2023 report recommended that any future regulation should address disclosure requirements, funder capital adequacy, and protection of funded party independence.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has the largest and most dynamic litigation funding market globally, though regulatory fragmentation creates significant uncertainty.

**Historical Doctrines -- Champerty and Maintenance:** Common law doctrines prohibiting champerty (financing another's litigation for a share of the proceeds) and maintenance (officious intermeddling in another's litigation) have been abolished or substantially relaxed in most states. As of 2025, approximately 28 states have formally abolished the doctrines, while the remaining states maintain them to varying degrees, often with judicial exceptions for commercial litigation funding.

**States Permitting Litigation Funding:**

| Approach | States (Examples) |
|----------|-------------------|
| Champerty abolished or substantially relaxed | Delaware, New York, California, Texas, Arizona, Ohio |
| Champerty retained but with commercial exceptions | Florida, Pennsylvania, Georgia |
| Champerty retained with significant restrictions | Alabama, Minnesota, Colorado |

**Federal Procedural Developments:** Several federal courts have adopted or proposed disclosure requirements for litigation funding:
- **Northern District of California (Local Rule 3-15, 2017):** Requires disclosure of third-party funding agreements in class actions
- **New Jersey (District Court Standing Order, 2021):** Requires disclosure of litigation funding in all cases
- **Proposed Federal Rule Amendment:** The Advisory Committee on Civil Rules has considered amendments to FRCP that would require disclosure of litigation funding arrangements, though no rule has been adopted as of 2025

**State Regulations:**

| State | Regulation |
|-------|-----------|
| Arizona | Permits litigation funding; ethical opinion allows attorney cooperation with funders |
| Wisconsin | Supreme Court Rule 20:1.8(e) modified to permit litigation funding |
| West Virginia | Consumer Litigation Funding Act (2019) -- requires licensing and caps annual charges |
| Indiana | Litigation Financing Act (2019) -- requires registration and contract disclosures |
| Nevada | Regulates consumer legal funding (NRS Chapter 604C) |

### Market Structure

The U.S. litigation funding market is estimated at over $15 billion in deployed capital (2024), dominated by institutional funders including Burford Capital (listed on NYSE), Longford Capital, Bentham IMF, and Litigation Capital Management. Funding is available for:
- Commercial litigation (including antitrust, IP, and breach of contract)
- Mass torts and class actions
- International arbitration
- Patent assertion campaigns
- Consumer legal funding (pre-settlement advances to individual plaintiffs)

### Key Cases

**Burford Capital Management v. Purdue Pharma (2024):** This high-profile case involved Burford Capital's claim for a share of recoveries in the opioid litigation, raising questions about funder standing and the enforceability of portfolio funding arrangements across related matters.

**Charge Injection Technologies v. JM Test Systems (Del. Ch. 2016):** The Delaware Court of Chancery upheld the validity of a litigation funding arrangement, confirming that Delaware does not recognize champerty as a defense and that litigation funding agreements are enforceable as ordinary contracts.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany has a well-developed litigation funding industry operating within a clear legal framework established through contract law and judicial precedent.

**BGH Jurisprudence:** The Federal Court of Justice (Bundesgerichtshof) has repeatedly confirmed the legality of litigation funding. In BGH Decision IV ZR 110/09 (2011), the Court held that commercial litigation funding arrangements are valid contracts that do not violate public policy (Section 138 BGB) or constitute unauthorized provision of legal services. The Court emphasized that litigation funding promotes access to justice by enabling parties who could not otherwise afford litigation to pursue meritorious claims.

**Rechtsdienstleistungsgesetz (RDG, Legal Services Act, 2008):** The RDG regulates the provision of extrajudicial legal services. Litigation funders generally fall outside the scope of the RDG because they provide financial services rather than legal advice. However, if a funder exercises control over litigation strategy, this may constitute an unauthorized legal service, violating the RDG.

**Rechtsanwaltsvergütungsgesetz (RVG, Attorney Fee Act):** German attorneys are generally required to charge statutory fees (Gebührenordnung) rather than contingency fees. A limited success fee (Erfolgshonorar) is permitted under RVG Section 4a only when the client would otherwise be unable to pursue the claim and alternative funding (including Prozesskostenhilfe) is unavailable. This restriction on attorney fee structures creates a natural market niche for commercial litigation funding.

### Market Dynamics -- "Loser Pays" Rule

Germany's "loser pays" fee-shifting rule (ZPO Section 91) creates distinctive dynamics for litigation funding:

- **Risk Allocation:** The funder assumes the risk of paying the opponent's attorney fees and court costs if the funded claim fails, in addition to the direct litigation costs. This significantly increases the funder's risk exposure compared to the American market.
- **After-the-Event (ATE) Insurance:** Litigation funders in Germany typically procure ATE insurance to cover adverse cost exposure, adding an additional cost layer.
- **Return Expectations:** German funders typically require higher return multiples (3-5x investment) than U.S. funders to compensate for the enhanced adverse cost risk.

### BGH Decision III ZR 389/21 (2023)

The Federal Court of Justice addressed the permissible scope of funder involvement, holding that while funders may receive regular reports on case progress and provide input on settlement discussions, they may not exercise decision-making authority over litigation strategy or settlement. Decisions regarding the conduct of litigation must remain with the funded party and their attorney, consistent with the principle of attorney independence (anwaltliche Unabhängigkeit).

## ECHR / EU

### Directive (EU) 2020/1828 -- Representative Actions

The Representative Actions Directive addresses litigation funding specifically in the context of collective consumer actions:

**Article 10 (Funding):** Member states must ensure that:
- Courts and administrative authorities can assess the funding of representative actions to prevent conflicts of interest
- Third-party funders cannot influence procedural decisions of the qualified entity, including settlement decisions
- Decisions to bring the representative action are not unduly influenced by the third-party funder
- Where the action is funded by a third party, there is no conflict of interest between the funder and the qualified entity

**Transparency Requirements:** Qualified entities bringing funded representative actions must disclose to the court the source of their funding, including the identity of the funder and the financial terms of the funding arrangement.

### European Parliament Resolution (2022)

On September 13, 2022, the European Parliament adopted a Resolution on responsible private funding of litigation calling for EU-wide regulation of third-party litigation funding. The Resolution recommended:
- Mandatory licensing or registration for litigation funders
- Capital adequacy requirements
- Caps on funder returns (not exceeding a "reasonable" percentage of the recovery)
- Mandatory disclosure of funding arrangements to courts and opponents
- Prohibition of funder control over litigation decisions
- Ethical standards for funders

### ECHR Access to Justice

The ECHR's jurisprudence on access to justice under Article 6 supports the principle that litigation funding can enhance the right of access to court:

**Kreuz v. Poland (Application No. 28249/95, June 19, 2001):** The Court held that the imposition of court fees that effectively prevent access to court may violate Article 6(1). By analogy, litigation funding that overcomes financial barriers to litigation serves the Article 6 objective of ensuring effective access to justice.

**Stanev v. Bulgaria (Application No. 36760/06, Grand Chamber, January 17, 2012):** The Grand Chamber confirmed that the right of access to court must be practical and effective, not merely theoretical and illusory.

### CJEU Jurisprudence

**Case C-279/09, DEB Deutsche Energiehandels- und Beratungsgesellschaft (2010):** The CJEU held that the right to effective judicial protection under Article 47 of the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights may require the provision of legal aid to legal persons (corporations) where the denial of aid would make it effectively impossible to exercise EU law rights. While not directly addressing private litigation funding, the decision underscores the EU's commitment to effective access to justice, a value that commercial litigation funding can serve.

## Comparative Analysis

### Legal Status

All four jurisdictions permit litigation funding, but with varying degrees of regulatory specificity. Germany has the most established market with the clearest judicial endorsement (BGH precedent). The United States has the largest market but the most fragmented regulatory landscape. South Korea's market is emerging with limited specific regulation. The EU is moving toward harmonized regulation through the Representative Actions Directive and the 2022 Parliament Resolution.

### Fee Structures

The prohibition or restriction on contingency fees in South Korea and Germany creates market space for litigation funders, who effectively provide contingency-like risk-shifting outside the attorney fee framework. The United States' permissive contingency fee environment means that litigation funding supplements rather than substitutes for attorney risk-taking, operating primarily in cases too large or risky for law firms to finance alone.

### Fee-Shifting Impact

The "American Rule" (each party bears its own costs) makes U.S. litigation funding less risky for funders, as there is no adverse cost exposure. Germany's "loser pays" rule significantly increases funder risk and requires higher return expectations. South Korea's partial fee-shifting creates moderate additional risk. The ECHR/EU framework generally respects member state fee-shifting rules.

### Access to Justice

All four jurisdictions recognize that litigation funding can enhance access to justice by enabling financially constrained parties to pursue meritorious claims. However, concerns about funder influence, abuse, and the commodification of justice claims persist across all systems. The EU's approach of requiring transparency and prohibiting funder control over litigation decisions represents an emerging consensus on appropriate safeguards.
