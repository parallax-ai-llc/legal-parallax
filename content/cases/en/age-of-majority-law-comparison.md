## Summary

The age of majority -- the legally defined threshold at which a person transitions from minor to adult status, acquiring full legal capacity to enter contracts, vote, marry without parental consent, and bear independent legal responsibility -- varies significantly across jurisdictions and has undergone considerable reform in recent decades. South Korea undertook a landmark change effective June 19, 2021, when its Civil Act (민법) was amended to lower the age of majority from 19 to 18 years of age under Article 4, aligning with the majority of nations worldwide and responding to longstanding arguments that Korean youth who could serve in the military and obtain a driver's license at 18 should also possess full civil capacity. The United States adopted 18 as the general federal age of majority through the 26th Amendment (1971), which lowered the voting age from 21 to 18, though individual states retain the authority to set varying ages of majority for different purposes such as alcohol consumption (21 under the National Minimum Drinking Age Act of 1984), tobacco purchase (21 under federal law since 2019), and contractual capacity, creating a patchwork of age thresholds across the country. Germany sets the age of majority at 18 under Section 2 of the Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB), having lowered it from 21 in 1975, while retaining transitional provisions for young adults aged 18 to 21 in criminal law under the Jugendgerichtsgesetz (JGG), which allows courts to apply juvenile sentencing to offenders in this age bracket based on maturity assessments. At the European level, the United Nations Convention on the Rights of the Child (UNCRC) defines a child as any person under 18, and the European Court of Human Rights has adjudicated cases involving the intersection of minority status with fundamental rights, while the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights guarantees specific protections to children without harmonizing the age of majority across member states.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's age of majority is governed by Article 4 of the Civil Act (민법 제4조), which was amended by Act No. 17905 on January 26, 2021, effective June 19, 2021. The amendment reduced the age of majority from 19 to 18, marking the first change to this threshold since the Civil Act's original enactment in 1958. Under Article 5 of the Civil Act (민법 제5조), legal acts performed by minors without the consent of their legal representative are voidable. Upon reaching the age of majority, individuals acquire full legal capacity to enter into contracts, manage their own property, and undertake legal proceedings independently.

### Historical Development

The original 1958 Civil Act set the age of majority at 20, consistent with the Japanese Civil Code upon which it was modeled. In 2013, the age was lowered from 20 to 19 as part of broader civil law reforms. The further reduction to 18 in 2021 was the culmination of a legislative debate spanning more than a decade, with proponents arguing that South Korea was an outlier among OECD nations in maintaining an age of majority above 18.

### Voting Age Reform

Separately from the civil age of majority, the Public Official Election Act (공직선거법) was amended in December 2019 to lower the voting age from 19 to 18, effective for the April 2020 National Assembly elections. This marked the first reduction in the voting age since it was set at 20 in 1960. Approximately 540,000 additional voters became eligible under the reform. The Youth Protection Act (청소년보호법) continues to define a youth as a person under 19, creating a discrepancy between the civil age of majority and the youth protection threshold.

### Constitutional Considerations

The Constitutional Court of Korea (헌법재판소) has considered age-based classifications under Article 11 (equality) and Article 10 (dignity and pursuit of happiness) of the Constitution. In Decision 2017Hun-Ma1264 (2020), the Court addressed whether the exclusion of 18-year-olds from voting constituted an unconstitutional infringement on the right to political participation, contributing to the legislative momentum for the 2019 voting age reform.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States lacks a single, uniform age of majority. The 26th Amendment to the Constitution, ratified on July 1, 1971, establishes that the right to vote shall not be denied to citizens eighteen years of age or older, effectively creating a constitutional floor for political majority. However, individual states retain the power to define the age of majority for civil purposes under the Tenth Amendment's reservation of powers.

**State Variation:** Most states set the general age of majority at 18, but Alabama and Nebraska set it at 19, and Mississippi sets it at 21. These variations primarily affect contractual capacity, the right to sue in one's own name, and the termination of parental support obligations.

### National Minimum Drinking Age Act (1984)

The most significant federal intervention regarding age thresholds beyond voting was the National Minimum Drinking Age Act of 1984 (23 U.S.C. Section 158), which required states to raise their minimum drinking age to 21 or face a reduction in federal highway funding. The Supreme Court upheld this statute in South Dakota v. Dole, 483 U.S. 203 (1987), finding that the spending condition was a valid exercise of Congress's power under the Spending Clause and did not constitute unconstitutional coercion of the states.

### Tobacco 21

In December 2019, Congress amended the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act to raise the minimum age for sale of tobacco products from 18 to 21 nationwide, effective immediately. This federal floor preempted the patchwork of state laws that had previously governed tobacco sales ages.

### Constitutional Jurisprudence

The Supreme Court has addressed age-based classifications under the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. In Craig v. Boren, 429 U.S. 190 (1976), the Court struck down an Oklahoma statute that permitted women aged 18-20 to purchase 3.2% beer but required men to be 21, establishing the intermediate scrutiny standard for gender-based classifications. More recently, in National Rifle Association of America v. Bondi, 61 F.4th 1317 (11th Cir. 2023), the en banc Eleventh Circuit upheld Florida's law prohibiting the sale of firearms to persons under 21, though the constitutional landscape for age-based firearms restrictions remains contested.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

The age of majority (Volljährigkeit) in Germany is set at 18 under Section 2 of the Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB). This threshold was lowered from 21 to 18 by the Act on the Revision of the Age of Majority (Gesetz zur Neuregelung des Volljährigkeitsalters), which entered into force on January 1, 1975. Upon reaching majority, a person acquires full legal capacity (volle Geschäftsfähigkeit) under BGB Section 104 and the right to enter into contracts, manage property, and take legal action without a legal representative.

### Graduated Capacity System

German law establishes a sophisticated graduated system of legal capacity:

| Age Range | Legal Status | BGB Provision |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| Under 7 | Legally incapable (geschäftsunfähig) | BGB Section 104 |
| 7 to 17 | Limited legal capacity (beschränkte Geschäftsfähigkeit) | BGB Sections 106-113 |
| 18+ | Full legal capacity (volle Geschäftsfähigkeit) | BGB Section 2 |

The "pocket money paragraph" (Taschengeldparagraph, BGB Section 110) permits minors between 7 and 17 to enter into contracts that they can fulfill with means available to them (typically pocket money), providing practical flexibility within the graduated capacity framework.

### Young Adult Criminal Law

Germany's Jugendgerichtsgesetz (JGG, Youth Courts Act) creates a distinctive intermediate category for young adults aged 18 to 21. Under JGG Section 105, courts may apply juvenile criminal law (Jugendstrafrecht) to offenders in this age bracket if the court determines that the offender's moral and psychological development at the time of the offense was equivalent to that of a juvenile, or if the offense was a "juvenile offense" (Jugendverfehlung) by its nature, circumstances, or motivation. In practice, approximately two-thirds of offenders aged 18-21 are sentenced under juvenile rather than adult criminal law.

### Marriage Age Reform

The Act to Combat Child Marriage (Gesetz zur Bekämpfung von Kinderehen) entered into force on July 22, 2017, setting the minimum marriage age at 18 with no exceptions. Previously, persons aged 16 could marry with family court approval. The 2017 law also rendered void any marriage entered into where one spouse was under 16 and annullable those where a spouse was between 16 and 18 at the time of the marriage. The Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) addressed the retroactive application of this law in its decision of March 29, 2023 (BVerfG, 1 BvL 7/18), finding that a blanket annulment of marriages involving minors aged 16-17 without individual case assessment was partially unconstitutional, requiring the legislature to craft a more nuanced approach.

## ECHR & EU

### United Nations Convention on the Rights of the Child

The UNCRC, adopted by the UN General Assembly on November 20, 1989, defines a child as "every human being below the age of eighteen years unless under the law applicable to the child, majority is attained earlier" (Article 1). All EU member states and Council of Europe members except the United States have ratified the UNCRC. The Convention establishes rights including the best interests of the child principle (Article 3), the right to be heard (Article 12), and protection from economic exploitation (Article 32).

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR has addressed age of majority issues primarily in the context of:

**T. v. United Kingdom and V. v. United Kingdom (Applications No. 24724/94 and 24888/94, Grand Chamber, December 16, 1999):** The Court held that the trial of two ten-year-olds (the James Bulger case) as adults in a Crown Court violated Article 6 (right to a fair trial), emphasizing that the youth of the defendants required procedural accommodations. The judgment did not mandate a specific age of criminal responsibility but established that the treatment of minors must be adapted to their age and maturity.

**Zaunegger v. Germany (Application No. 22028/04, December 3, 2009):** The Court found a violation of Article 14 read in conjunction with Article 8, holding that an unmarried father's inability to obtain joint custody without the mother's consent constituted discrimination. While not directly about the age of majority, this case illustrates how minority status intersects with parental rights and capacity.

### EU Charter of Fundamental Rights

Article 24 of the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights provides specific protections for children, including the right to protection and care necessary for their well-being, the right to express views freely according to their age and maturity, and the requirement that the best interests of the child be a primary consideration in all actions relating to children. The Charter does not harmonize the age of majority across member states.

### Council of Europe Recommendations

The Committee of Ministers has issued several recommendations concerning the age of majority and related thresholds:

- Recommendation CM/Rec(2008)11 on the European Rules for Juvenile Offenders Subject to Sanctions or Measures: recommends that young adult offenders up to age 21 may be treated as juveniles.
- Recommendation CM/Rec(2003)20 concerning New Ways of Dealing with Juvenile Delinquency: encourages member states to consider extending juvenile justice provisions to young adults.

## Comparative Analysis

### Global Convergence on 18

There is a near-universal global convergence on 18 as the standard age of majority, with the UNCRC's definition of a child as a person under 18 serving as the international benchmark. South Korea's 2021 reduction from 19 to 18 and Germany's earlier reduction from 21 to 18 in 1975 both reflect this trend. The United States, while maintaining 18 as the general age of majority and voting age, is notable for its higher thresholds for alcohol (21), tobacco (21), and handgun purchases (21), reflecting policy choices that decouple civil majority from behavioral regulation.

### Graduated vs. Bright-Line Approaches

Germany's graduated capacity system (incapable under 7, limited capacity 7-17, full capacity at 18) represents the most nuanced approach, acknowledging that legal capacity develops progressively. South Korea and the United States employ a more binary approach, distinguishing primarily between minor and adult status. Germany's extension of juvenile criminal law to young adults aged 18-21 under the JGG further demonstrates a willingness to recognize developmental realities beyond the formal age of majority.

### Functional Fragmentation

All jurisdictions exhibit some degree of functional fragmentation, where different age thresholds apply to different legal domains. The United States exhibits the most extreme fragmentation, with different ages for voting (18), military service (18), alcohol (21), tobacco (21), and firearms (18 or 21 depending on the type). South Korea similarly maintains different ages for civil majority (18), alcohol and tobacco purchase (19), and criminal responsibility (14). This fragmentation reflects the tension between the administrative convenience of a single bright-line age and the policy recognition that different activities involve different risks and require different levels of maturity.

### Constitutional Scrutiny

All four jurisdictions subject age-based distinctions to some form of constitutional or fundamental rights review. The U.S. Supreme Court applies varying levels of scrutiny depending on the right affected (strict scrutiny for voting under the 26th Amendment; intermediate scrutiny for gender-based age distinctions). The German Federal Constitutional Court has shown willingness to strike down age-based rules that lack sufficient justification, as in its 2023 child marriage decision. The ECHR applies the proportionality principle to assess whether age-based restrictions serve legitimate aims and are necessary in a democratic society.
