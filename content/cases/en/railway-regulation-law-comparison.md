## Summary

Railway regulation -- the legal framework governing rail infrastructure, train operations, safety standards, economic regulation, and passenger rights -- has undergone profound transformation worldwide as governments balance public service obligations, privatization pressures, safety imperatives, and high-speed rail development. South Korea operates one of the world's most advanced railway systems, with the Korea Railroad Corporation (한국철도공사, KORAIL) managing conventional and high-speed rail services, the Korea Train Express (KTX) having commenced operations in 2004, and regulation governed by the Railroad Safety Act (철도안전법), the Railroad Industry Development Act (철도산업발전기본법), and the Railroad Business Act (철도사업법), overseen by the Ministry of Land, Infrastructure and Transport (국토교통부) and the Korea Rail Network Authority (한국철도시설공단). The United States, which historically pioneered railroad regulation through the Interstate Commerce Act of 1887 (the first federal regulatory statute), now regulates freight railroads primarily through the Surface Transportation Board (STB) and safety through the Federal Railroad Administration (FRA), while passenger rail operates largely through the federally-funded National Railroad Passenger Corporation (Amtrak), with the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act of 2021 providing historic funding for rail modernization. Germany operates one of Europe's largest rail networks through Deutsche Bahn AG (a state-owned joint-stock company following the 1994 Bahnreform), regulated by the Bundesnetzagentur (Federal Network Agency) for competition and access, and the Eisenbahn-Bundesamt (Federal Railway Authority) for safety, within the EU framework of the Fourth Railway Package establishing a Single European Railway Area. At the European level, the EU Fourth Railway Package (2016) requires the separation of infrastructure management from transport operations, the European Railway Agency (ERA) provides unified safety certification, Regulation (EC) No 1371/2007 establishes rail passenger rights, and the Trans-European Transport Network (TEN-T) program funds cross-border rail infrastructure.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's railway regulatory framework operates through three principal statutes: the Railroad Industry Development Act (철도산업발전기본법), which establishes the overall policy framework; the Railroad Business Act (철도사업법), which governs licensing and operations; and the Railroad Safety Act (철도안전법), which addresses safety standards and accident investigation.

### Organizational Structure

The Korean railway system underwent a major restructuring in 2004-2005. The former Korean National Railroad was split into two entities: KORAIL (한국철도공사, Korea Railroad Corporation), which operates train services, and the Korea Rail Network Authority (한국철도시설공단, KR), which manages and develops railway infrastructure. This vertical separation model partially aligns with EU principles of infrastructure-operator separation.

### Korea Train Express (KTX)

The KTX high-speed rail system, which commenced operations on April 1, 2004, connects Seoul to Busan in approximately 2 hours 15 minutes at speeds up to 305 km/h. The system uses French TGV-based technology (KTX-I) and domestically developed trains (KTX-Sancheon, KTX-Eum). A second high-speed operator, SR Corporation, operates the SRT (Super Rapid Train) on the Suseo-Pyeongtaek High-Speed Line since 2016, introducing competition into Korean high-speed rail.

### Safety Regulation

The Aviation and Railway Accident Investigation Board (항공·철도사고조사위원회, ARAIB) conducts independent investigations of railway accidents. The Railroad Safety Act establishes mandatory safety management systems for railway operators, periodic safety inspections, and requirements for train operator certification.

### Penalties

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| Operating railway without license | Up to 5 years imprisonment or 50 million KRW fine |
| Violation of safety standards | Up to 3 years imprisonment or 30 million KRW fine |
| Failure to report accident | Up to 1 year imprisonment or 10 million KRW fine |
| Interference with railway operations | Up to 10 years imprisonment (Railroad Safety Act) |

## United States

### Legal Framework

US railroad regulation has a uniquely long history, beginning with the Interstate Commerce Act of 1887 -- the first federal regulatory statute -- which created the Interstate Commerce Commission (ICC) to regulate railroad rates and practices. Modern regulation is divided between safety regulation by the Federal Railroad Administration (FRA) and economic regulation by the Surface Transportation Board (STB).

**FRA (Safety):** The Federal Railroad Administration, within the Department of Transportation, regulates railroad safety under the Federal Railroad Safety Act (49 U.S.C. Chapter 201). FRA regulations (49 C.F.R. Parts 200-299) cover track standards, signal systems, locomotive and rolling stock safety, grade crossing safety, and positive train control (PTC).

**STB (Economic):** The Surface Transportation Board, established by the ICC Termination Act of 1995, exercises economic regulatory jurisdiction over freight railroads, including rate reasonableness, service adequacy, mergers and acquisitions, and line abandonments.

### Amtrak

The National Railroad Passenger Corporation (Amtrak), created by the Rail Passenger Service Act of 1970 (Public Law 91-518), operates nearly all intercity passenger rail service in the United States. Amtrak is a government-supported corporation that operates over approximately 21,400 route miles. The Amtrak Reform and Accountability Act of 1997 and subsequent reauthorizations govern its structure and funding.

### Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (2021)

The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA, Public Law 117-58, November 15, 2021) provided $66 billion for rail investments -- the largest federal rail investment since the creation of Amtrak. Key programs include the Federal-State Partnership for Intercity Passenger Rail ($36 billion), the Consolidated Rail Infrastructure and Safety Improvements (CRISI) program, and funding for the Northeast Corridor.

### Positive Train Control

PTC, mandated by the Rail Safety Improvement Act of 2008 (Public Law 110-432) following the Chatsworth, California collision that killed 25 people, is an automated system designed to prevent train-to-train collisions, over-speed derailments, and unauthorized movement into work zones. Full PTC implementation was achieved on all mandated routes by December 2020 after multiple deadline extensions.

| Body | Jurisdiction |
|------|-------------|
| FRA | Railroad safety standards and enforcement |
| STB | Economic regulation of freight railroads |
| NTSB | Independent accident investigation |
| Amtrak | Intercity passenger operations |
| Commuter rail agencies | Metropolitan commuter rail operations |

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's railway regulatory framework was fundamentally restructured by the Bahnreform (Railway Reform) of 1994, which transformed the former state railways (Deutsche Bundesbahn in the West and Deutsche Reichsbahn in the East) into Deutsche Bahn AG (DB AG), a state-owned joint-stock company. The constitutional amendment of Article 87e GG provided the legal basis for the reform.

**Allgemeines Eisenbahngesetz (AEiG, General Railway Act):** This statute establishes the basic framework for railway regulation, including licensing, infrastructure access, and safety requirements.

**Eisenbahn-Bundesamt (EBA, Federal Railway Authority):** The EBA is the regulatory and safety authority for railway transport in Germany, responsible for safety supervision, approval of rolling stock, and infrastructure oversight.

**Bundesnetzagentur (Federal Network Agency):** Since 2006, the railway division of the Bundesnetzagentur regulates access to railway infrastructure, ensuring non-discriminatory access for competing train operating companies. This competition oversight function was transferred from the EBA to address concerns about DB AG's dual role as both infrastructure manager and dominant train operator.

### Deutsche Bahn Structure

DB AG operates as a holding company with subsidiaries including DB Fernverkehr (long-distance passenger), DB Regio (regional passenger), DB Cargo (freight), and DB Netz (infrastructure). The question of structural separation -- whether to fully separate DB Netz from the transport operations -- has been a persistent policy debate. The Deutschlandtakt (Germany-wide timetable) initiative, announced in 2019, aims to create a coordinated nationwide rail timetable with regular-interval scheduling.

### Deutschlandticket

The Deutschlandticket (49-Euro-Ticket, later adjusted in price), introduced on May 1, 2023, as a monthly subscription pass valid on all local and regional public transport across Germany, was a landmark policy innovation. The ticket replaced the temporary 9-Euro-Ticket of summer 2022 and represents one of the most ambitious public transport pricing experiments in Europe.

### Safety and Accident Investigation

Railway safety in Germany is governed by EU Directive (EU) 2016/798 on Railway Safety, implemented through the AEiG and EBA regulations. The Eisenbahn-Unfalluntersuchungsstelle des Bundes (Federal Railway Accident Investigation Bureau) conducts independent accident investigations. Germany's ICE high-speed network has maintained a strong safety record, though the Eschede train disaster of June 3, 1998 (101 deaths from wheel failure on ICE 884) remains the worst high-speed rail accident in German history.

### Penalties

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| Operating without authorization (AEiG) | Administrative fine up to 500,000 EUR |
| Safety standard violations | Administrative fine; license revocation |
| Discrimination in infrastructure access | Bundesnetzagentur enforcement; fines |
| Endangering rail safety | Criminal prosecution under StGB |

## ECHR & EU

### EU Fourth Railway Package (2016)

The Fourth Railway Package, adopted in 2016, comprises the "technical pillar" (Directives/Regulations on safety, interoperability, and ERA) and the "market pillar" (Directive 2016/2370 on opening domestic passenger markets). Key elements include:

**Mandatory Market Opening:** Directive 2016/2370 requires member states to open domestic passenger rail markets to competition through either open access or competitive tendering of public service contracts, effective December 2023.

**Single Safety Certificate:** Regulation (EU) 2016/796 expanded the European Union Agency for Railways (ERA) to issue single safety certificates valid across the EU, replacing individual national certification processes.

**Vehicle Authorization:** ERA now issues vehicle authorizations valid across participating member states, streamlining the approval process for rolling stock.

### EU Passenger Rights

Regulation (EC) No 1371/2007 on rail passengers' rights and obligations, recast by Regulation (EU) 2021/782 (effective June 7, 2023), establishes minimum passenger rights across the EU. Key provisions include: minimum 25% compensation for delays of 60-119 minutes; minimum 50% compensation for delays of 120+ minutes; right to re-routing or refund; assistance (meals, accommodation) during delays; and accessibility requirements for persons with reduced mobility.

### Trans-European Transport Network (TEN-T)

The TEN-T Regulation (EU) No 1315/2013, revised by Regulation (EU) 2024/1679, establishes the framework for developing a comprehensive European transport network. The core network, targeted for completion by 2030 (extended to 2040 in the revision), includes nine core network corridors with major cross-border rail projects including: Rhine-Alpine Corridor; Scandinavian-Mediterranean Corridor; and Atlantic Corridor.

### OTIF-COTIF

The Convention concerning International Carriage by Rail (COTIF), administered by the Intergovernmental Organisation for International Carriage by Rail (OTIF), provides the legal framework for international rail transport in Europe and beyond. The Uniform Rules concerning the Contract of International Carriage of Passengers by Rail (CIV) establish liability rules for international passenger transport.

## Comparative Analysis

### Structural Models

The jurisdictions represent three distinct structural models. South Korea has implemented partial vertical separation (KORAIL operations, KR infrastructure) with limited competition (SRT). Germany maintains integrated operations under DB AG with regulated third-party access and growing competition. The United States has a unique model with private freight railroads owning infrastructure and a government-supported passenger carrier (Amtrak) operating over freight-owned track.

### High-Speed Rail

South Korea and Germany operate mature, successful high-speed rail networks. The United States has no comparable system despite decades of proposals, though the IIJA provides unprecedented funding. The EU's TEN-T program aims to connect European high-speed networks into a continental system.

### Passenger Rights

EU passenger rights under Regulation 2021/782 are the most protective, providing mandatory compensation for delays and cancellations. South Korea relies on consumer protection law and KORAIL service standards. The United States has no federal passenger compensation mandate, with Amtrak's terms of carriage providing the primary framework.

### Safety Records

All jurisdictions maintain strong safety records for mainline operations, though catastrophic accidents (Eschede 1998, Chatsworth 2008) have driven regulatory reforms. The EU's unified safety certification through ERA aims to maintain high standards across a heterogeneous network of operators.
