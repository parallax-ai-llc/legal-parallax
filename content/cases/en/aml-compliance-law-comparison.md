## Summary

Anti-money laundering (AML) compliance -- the legal and institutional framework requiring financial institutions and other obliged entities to detect, prevent, and report the laundering of proceeds from criminal activity -- has become one of the most heavily regulated areas of financial law worldwide, driven by concerns about organized crime, terrorism financing, corruption, and tax evasion. South Korea's AML framework is anchored by the Act on Reporting and Using Specified Financial Transaction Information (특정 금융거래정보의 보고 및 이용 등에 관한 법률, commonly known as the Special Financial Transaction Reports Act), first enacted in 2001, and the Proceeds of Crime Act (범죄수익은닉의 규제 및 처벌 등에 관한 법률) of 2001, administered by the Korea Financial Intelligence Unit (KoFIU, 금융정보분석원) within the Financial Services Commission. The United States operates the most extensive AML regime globally, centered on the Bank Secrecy Act (BSA) of 1970 (31 U.S.C. §§ 5311-5332), as strengthened by the USA PATRIOT Act of 2001 (particularly Title III, the International Money Laundering Abatement and Anti-Terrorist Financing Act), the Anti-Money Laundering Act of 2020, and the Corporate Transparency Act of 2021 (31 U.S.C. §§ 5336), enforced by the Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN), the Department of Justice, and prudential banking regulators. Germany's AML obligations are codified in the Geldwäschegesetz (GwG, Money Laundering Act), originally enacted in 1993 and comprehensively revised in 2017 and 2020 to transpose the EU's Fourth and Fifth Anti-Money Laundering Directives, with enforcement shared between BaFin, the Zentralstelle für Finanztransaktionsuntersuchungen (FIU), and state-level supervisory authorities. At the European level, the EU has adopted six Anti-Money Laundering Directives since 1991, with the transformative AML Package of 2024 -- comprising Regulation (EU) 2024/1624 (AMLR), Directive (EU) 2024/1640 (AMLD6), and Regulation (EU) 2024/1620 establishing the Anti-Money Laundering Authority (AMLA) -- representing a fundamental shift from directive-based harmonization to directly applicable regulation and centralized EU-level supervision.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's AML framework was developed in response to the country's accession to the Financial Action Task Force (FATF) in 2009 and earlier compliance with FATF recommendations.

**Special Financial Transaction Reports Act (특정 금융거래정보의 보고 및 이용 등에 관한 법률):** Enacted in September 2001, this Act establishes the obligation for financial institutions to report suspicious transactions and currency transactions above specified thresholds to KoFIU. Key provisions include:

| Provision | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| Article 4 | Suspicious Transaction Reports (STRs): mandatory for all transactions suspected of involving criminal proceeds or terrorism financing |
| Article 4-2 | Currency Transaction Reports (CTRs): mandatory for cash transactions exceeding 20 million KRW (approximately $15,000) |
| Article 5-2 | Customer Due Diligence (CDD): identity verification and purpose of transaction |
| Article 5-3 | Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) for politically exposed persons (PEPs) and high-risk jurisdictions |

**Proceeds of Crime Act (범죄수익은닉의 규제 및 처벌 등에 관한 법률):** Enacted simultaneously in 2001, this Act criminalizes money laundering and provides for the confiscation and recovery of criminal proceeds. Article 3 establishes the offense of concealing or disguising criminal proceeds, punishable by up to 5 years imprisonment or a fine up to 30 million KRW.

### Virtual Asset Service Providers

The March 2021 amendment to the Special Financial Transaction Reports Act brought Virtual Asset Service Providers (VASPs) under AML regulation. All VASPs must register with KoFIU and comply with:
- Real-name verification through partnerships with certified banks
- Information Security Management System (ISMS) certification from the Korea Internet & Security Agency (KISA)
- Suspicious transaction reporting obligations
- Customer identification and verification

As of September 2023, only 35 of approximately 200 operating exchanges had obtained full registration, with KoFIU ordering the closure of non-compliant platforms.

### FATF Mutual Evaluation

South Korea underwent its fourth FATF Mutual Evaluation in 2024, receiving largely compliant ratings across most FATF Recommendations. The evaluation noted strengths in the legal framework and institutional coordination but identified areas for improvement in beneficial ownership transparency and supervision of designated non-financial businesses and professions (DNFBPs).

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has the most extensive and heavily enforced AML regime in the world, reflecting both its role as the center of the global financial system and its aggressive law enforcement posture.

**Bank Secrecy Act (BSA) of 1970 (31 U.S.C. §§ 5311-5332):** The foundational AML statute requires financial institutions to maintain records and file reports that are useful in detecting and preventing money laundering. Key requirements include:

| Requirement | Legal Basis | Threshold |
|------------|-------------|-----------|
| Currency Transaction Report (CTR) | 31 U.S.C. § 5313; 31 C.F.R. § 1010.310 | Cash transactions exceeding $10,000 |
| Suspicious Activity Report (SAR) | 31 C.F.R. § 1020.320 (banks) | Transactions of $5,000+ involving suspected illegal activity |
| Customer Identification Program (CIP) | 31 C.F.R. § 1020.220 | All new accounts |
| Beneficial Ownership (CDD Rule) | 31 C.F.R. § 1010.230 | Legal entity customers (25% ownership threshold) |

**USA PATRIOT Act Title III (2001):** The International Money Laundering Abatement and Anti-Terrorist Financing Act of 2001 significantly expanded the BSA framework:
- **Section 311:** Special measures for jurisdictions, institutions, or transactions of primary money laundering concern
- **Section 312:** Enhanced due diligence for correspondent accounts and private banking accounts
- **Section 314(a):** Information sharing between law enforcement and financial institutions
- **Section 314(b):** Voluntary information sharing among financial institutions (safe harbor)
- **Section 326:** Customer identification programs

**Anti-Money Laundering Act of 2020:** Enacted as part of the National Defense Authorization Act for FY2021, this Act modernized the BSA framework by establishing a national AML/CFT priority list, whistleblower incentives and protections, expanded subpoena authority for foreign bank records, and enhanced penalties.

**Corporate Transparency Act of 2021 (31 U.S.C. § 5336):** Requires most corporations, LLCs, and similar entities to report beneficial ownership information to FinCEN. The reporting requirement covers individuals exercising substantial control or holding 25 percent or more ownership interests. Implementation began January 1, 2024, for new entities.

### Criminal Penalties

| Offense | Statute | Maximum Penalty |
|---------|---------|-----------------|
| Money laundering (transaction) | 18 U.S.C. § 1956 | 20 years imprisonment; fine up to $500,000 or twice the transaction value |
| Money laundering (monetary instrument) | 18 U.S.C. § 1957 | 10 years imprisonment |
| BSA willful violation | 31 U.S.C. § 5322 | 5 years imprisonment; $250,000 fine |
| Structuring | 31 U.S.C. § 5324 | 5 years imprisonment; $250,000 fine |
| Bulk cash smuggling | 31 U.S.C. § 5332 | 5 years imprisonment |

### Enforcement Actions

Major U.S. AML enforcement actions have imposed billions of dollars in penalties:
- **HSBC (2012):** $1.92 billion settlement with DOJ for AML failures involving Mexican drug cartels and sanctioned nations
- **BNP Paribas (2014):** $8.9 billion penalty for sanctions and AML violations
- **Danske Bank (2022):** $2 billion settlement for failures related to $230 billion in suspicious transactions through its Estonian branch
- **TD Bank (2024):** $3.09 billion penalty for systemic AML failures; first U.S. bank to plead guilty to BSA conspiracy

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's AML obligations are primarily codified in the Geldwäschegesetz (GwG), comprehensively revised in 2017 to transpose the Fourth EU Anti-Money Laundering Directive (Directive (EU) 2015/849) and amended in 2020 for the Fifth Directive (Directive (EU) 2018/843).

**GwG Key Provisions:**

| Section | Requirement |
|---------|-------------|
| § 2 | Obliged entities (Verpflichtete): banks, insurers, real estate agents, lawyers, notaries, tax advisors, dealers in high-value goods |
| §§ 10-17 | Customer due diligence obligations; risk-based approach |
| § 15 | Enhanced due diligence for high-risk situations, PEPs, correspondent banking |
| § 20 | Transparency Register (Transparenzregister) obligations |
| § 43 | Suspicious transaction reporting to FIU |
| § 56 | Administrative fines up to €5 million or 10% of annual turnover |

**Criminal Liability -- § 261 StGB (Geldwäsche):** Money laundering is criminalized under Section 261 of the Strafgesetzbuch. The 2021 reform (effective March 2021) fundamentally expanded the offense by removing the requirement that predicate offenses be drawn from a closed catalogue; all criminal offenses now qualify as potential predicates (Allkriminalitätsansatz). Maximum penalty: 10 years imprisonment for aggravated cases (§ 261(4) StGB).

### Transparency Register

Germany transitioned from a catch-all register (relying on existing commercial and association registers) to a full transparency register (Vollregister) effective August 1, 2021. All legal entities must now actively report their beneficial owners to the Transparenzregister, regardless of whether the information is available in other registers. The register is maintained by the Bundesanzeiger Verlag and accessible to FIU, law enforcement, obliged entities, and -- following the CJEU's ruling in Joined Cases C-37/20 and C-601/20 (WM and Sovim, November 22, 2022) -- no longer to the general public.

### FIU Challenges

Germany's Financial Intelligence Unit (Zentralstelle für Finanztransaktionsuntersuchungen), transferred from police authority to customs authority in 2017, has faced significant criticism for processing backlogs. A 2020 parliamentary inquiry revealed that the FIU had accumulated approximately 30,000 unprocessed suspicious transaction reports. The unit was restructured in 2022 with increased staffing and improved IT systems.

## ECHR / EU

### EU AML Framework Evolution

The EU has progressively strengthened its AML framework through six generations of anti-money laundering directives:

| Directive | Year | Key Innovation |
|-----------|------|----------------|
| First AMLD (91/308/EEC) | 1991 | Drug trafficking as predicate offense; basic CDD |
| Second AMLD (2001/97/EC) | 2001 | Extended predicate offenses; covered non-financial professionals |
| Third AMLD (2005/60/EC) | 2005 | Risk-based approach; PEP requirements; CFT integration |
| Fourth AMLD (2015/849/EU) | 2015 | Beneficial ownership registers; enhanced CDD; lower thresholds |
| Fifth AMLD (2018/843/EU) | 2018 | Public access to BO registers; virtual currency coverage; centralized bank account registries |
| Sixth AMLD (2018/1673/EU) | 2018 | Harmonized predicate offenses; aiding and abetting; minimum 4-year maximum penalty |

### 2024 AML Package

The EU's transformative 2024 AML Package represents a paradigm shift from directive-based harmonization to directly applicable regulation and centralized supervision:

**Regulation (EU) 2024/1624 (AMLR):** A single rulebook directly applicable across all member states, replacing the directive approach. Key provisions include:
- EU-wide cash payment limit of €10,000 (Article 59)
- Harmonized CDD rules with maximum 25 percent beneficial ownership threshold (Articles 16-29)
- Comprehensive coverage of crypto-asset service providers (CASPs)
- Third-country policy requirements

**Regulation (EU) 2024/1620 (AMLA):** Establishes the Anti-Money Laundering Authority headquartered in Frankfurt, Germany. AMLA will:
- Directly supervise the highest-risk cross-border obliged entities (initially up to 40 selected entities)
- Coordinate and oversee national FIUs
- Develop regulatory technical standards
- Maintain a central AML/CFT database

**Directive (EU) 2024/1640 (AMLD6):** Addresses matters requiring national transposition, including FIU powers and cooperation, national supervisory architecture, and beneficial ownership register access rules.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Michaud v. France (Application No. 12323/11, ECtHR Grand Chamber, December 6, 2012):** The Court upheld France's requirement that lawyers report suspicious transactions to the FIU, finding no violation of Article 8 (right to respect for private life and correspondence). The Court held that the reporting obligation was prescribed by law, pursued the legitimate aim of preventing crime, and was proportionate given the filtering mechanism through the bar association president.

**Joined Cases C-37/20 and C-601/20, WM and Sovim (CJEU, November 22, 2022):** The CJEU invalidated the Fifth AMLD's provision granting general public access to beneficial ownership registers, finding it disproportionately interfered with the fundamental rights to privacy and data protection under Articles 7 and 8 of the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights.

**Grande Stevens v. Italy (Application No. 18640/10, ECtHR, March 4, 2014):** While primarily concerning market abuse, this case established important principles regarding the ne bis in idem principle (prohibition of double jeopardy) in financial regulation, relevant to concurrent AML administrative and criminal proceedings.

## Comparative Analysis

### Enforcement Philosophy

The jurisdictions reflect starkly different enforcement intensities:

**United States:** The most aggressive enforcement regime globally, characterized by massive monetary penalties, criminal prosecution of institutions and individuals, and extraterritorial reach through the correspondent banking system. The DOJ and FinCEN have imposed penalties exceeding $30 billion on financial institutions since 2012.

**EU/Germany:** Enforcement has historically been weaker than in the United States, though the 2024 AML Package aims to address this gap through AMLA's direct supervisory authority and harmonized penalty frameworks. Germany's record of AML enforcement has been criticized, particularly following the Wirecard scandal of 2020.

**South Korea:** Enforcement has strengthened significantly since FATF membership in 2009, with KoFIU imposing increasing numbers of administrative sanctions and referrals for criminal prosecution.

### Beneficial Ownership Transparency

The evolution toward beneficial ownership transparency has proceeded at different paces:
- **United States:** Late adopter; Corporate Transparency Act enacted 2021, FinCEN BOI registry operational 2024
- **EU:** Pioneer through Fourth and Fifth AMLDs; public access curtailed by CJEU in 2022
- **South Korea:** Limited corporate transparency; reliance on financial institution CDD rather than central registry
- **Germany:** Full transparency register since 2021; public access restricted post-CJEU ruling

### Virtual Asset Regulation

All four jurisdictions now cover virtual assets in their AML frameworks, but approaches vary:
- **South Korea:** Registration-based system with strict real-name bank account requirements
- **United States:** VASPs treated as money service businesses; FinCEN registration and BSA compliance
- **Germany:** Crypto custody licensing under KWG § 1(1a)(6); GwG obligations for VASPs
- **EU:** Comprehensive framework under MiCA Regulation and AMLR for CASPs

### De-Risking and Financial Inclusion

AML compliance costs have contributed to "de-risking" -- the withdrawal of banking services from higher-risk customers, sectors, and regions:

| Affected Group | Impact | Jurisdiction Response |
|---------------|--------|----------------------|
| Money transfer operators (remittances) | Loss of correspondent banking relationships | All: FATF guidance on maintaining access |
| Non-profit organizations | Difficulty opening/maintaining bank accounts | US: Treasury guidelines on charitable giving |
| Developing country correspondent banks | Reduction in correspondent banking networks | EU: AMLA mandate to address de-risking |
| Cannabis businesses (US) | Federal illegality prevents banking access | US: SAFE Banking Act proposals (not enacted) |

De-risking undermines financial inclusion goals and may push transactions into less transparent channels, paradoxically increasing AML risks.

### Whistleblower Programs

AML enforcement increasingly relies on whistleblower programs:
- **United States:** Anti-Money Laundering Act of 2020 established AML whistleblower incentives and protections modeled on SEC and IRS programs (up to 30 percent of monetary sanctions exceeding $1 million)
- **EU:** AMLD6 requires member states to establish whistleblower protection for AML reporting
- **South Korea:** Act on Protection of Public Interest Whistleblowers provides general whistleblower protection applicable to AML
- **Germany:** Hinweisgeberschutzgesetz (Whistleblower Protection Act, 2023) implements EU Whistleblower Directive

### Emerging AML Challenges

New typologies and technologies are challenging AML frameworks:
- **Trade-based money laundering (TBML):** Increasingly recognized as one of the largest money laundering channels globally, involving manipulation of trade invoices to transfer value across borders
- **Privacy-enhancing cryptocurrencies:** Monero, Zcash, and mixing services create traceability challenges
- **Decentralized finance (DeFi):** Autonomous protocols without identifiable intermediaries challenge the obliged-entity model of AML regulation
- **AI and AML:** Machine learning for transaction monitoring (reducing false positives from approximately 95 percent to below 50 percent in some implementations)

### Beneficial Ownership Transparency

Establishing the true beneficial owners of legal entities is foundational to effective AML:

- **United States:** Corporate Transparency Act (CTA, 2021) requires reporting of beneficial ownership information to FinCEN; reporting commenced January 2024 for new entities; existing entities required to report by January 2025; constitutional challenge in National Small Business United v. Yellen (2024) created enforcement uncertainty
- **EU:** AMLD5 required member states to establish beneficial ownership registers accessible to persons with a "legitimate interest"; CJEU in WM and Sovim (C-37/20, November 2022) struck down unrestricted public access as disproportionate under Article 7 and 8 CFREU; AMLD6/AMLR establish revised access framework
- **South Korea:** Special Financial Transaction Reports Act requires identification of beneficial owners for customer due diligence; no centralized beneficial ownership registry exists
- **Germany:** Transparenzregister (Transparency Register) transitioned from a "catch-all" to a "full" register in 2021; GwG § 20 mandates reporting by all legal entities

### AML Enforcement Actions and Penalties

The scale of AML enforcement has escalated dramatically:

| Jurisdiction | Notable Enforcement Action | Penalty |
|-------------|--------------------------|---------|
| United States | HSBC (2012) -- Mexican drug cartel laundering | $1.9 billion |
| United States | Danske Bank Estonian branch (2022) | $2 billion (guilty plea) |
| Germany | Deutsche Bank (2017) -- Russian mirror trading | $630 million (combined US/UK) |
| South Korea | Woori Bank (2016) -- failure to report suspicious transactions | 4.5 billion KRW |
| EU | ING Bank Netherlands (2018) | €775 million |

These enforcement actions have driven a significant increase in compliance spending, estimated at over $274 billion globally in 2024, with AML compliance costs consuming 3-5 percent of total operating expenses for large financial institutions.
