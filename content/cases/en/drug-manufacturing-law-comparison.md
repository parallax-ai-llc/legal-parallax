## Summary

Drug manufacturing -- the production, synthesis, cultivation, processing, or preparation of controlled substances -- represents one of the most severely punished categories of criminal offense worldwide, reflecting the perceived gravity of the drug supply chain's impact on public health, social order, and national security. South Korea maintains an exceptionally strict anti-narcotics regime through the Narcotics Control Act (마약류 관리에 관한 법률), which imposes penalties ranging from five years' imprisonment to the death penalty for manufacturing narcotics, with the law reflecting South Korea's historically low but recently increasing rates of drug use and the government's "zero tolerance" policy that has been praised for public health outcomes but criticized for its severity and impact on treatment-seeking behavior. The United States addresses drug manufacturing through the Controlled Substances Act (21 U.S.C. Section 841), which establishes a five-schedule classification system with penalties ranging from fines and probation for small-scale manufacturing of lower-schedule substances to mandatory life imprisonment for large-scale manufacturing of Schedule I or II drugs, supplemented by extensive federal sentencing guidelines that have been the subject of ongoing reform debates following the Supreme Court's advisory ruling in United States v. Booker (2005) and the bipartisan First Step Act of 2018. Germany criminalizes drug manufacturing under the Betäubungsmittelgesetz (BtMG, Narcotics Act), Section 29, with penalties of up to five years' imprisonment for basic manufacturing offenses and up to fifteen years for manufacturing in non-trivial quantities (nicht geringe Menge), with the Bundesverfassungsgericht's landmark cannabis decision of 1994 (BVerfGE 90, 145) and the subsequent legalization of recreational cannabis through the Cannabisgesetz (CanG) of April 1, 2024, fundamentally reshaping Germany's approach to cannabis production. At the international level, the UN Single Convention on Narcotic Drugs (1961), the Convention on Psychotropic Substances (1971), and the Convention Against Illicit Traffic in Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances (1988) establish the global prohibition framework, while the EU has addressed drug manufacturing through Framework Decision 2004/757/JHA on drug trafficking and the European Monitoring Centre for Drugs and Drug Addiction (EMCDDA, now the EU Drugs Agency from July 2024) monitors drug manufacturing trends across the Union.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's Narcotics Control Act classifies controlled substances into four categories: narcotics (마약), psychotropic substances (향정신성의약품), cannabis (대마), and precursor chemicals (원료물질).

### Penalty Structure for Manufacturing

| Substance Category | Basic Manufacturing | Large-Scale / For-Profit |
|-------------------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Narcotics (heroin, cocaine, opium) | 5+ years imprisonment | Life imprisonment or death penalty |
| Psychotropic substances (methamphetamine, MDMA) | 5+ years imprisonment | Life imprisonment or death penalty |
| Cannabis | 5+ years imprisonment | Up to life imprisonment |
| Precursor chemicals (illegal diversion) | Up to 10 years | Enhanced penalties |

### Zero Tolerance Policy

South Korea maintains what is widely characterized as a "zero tolerance" approach to narcotics. The country's drug policy is shaped by several factors: historically low prevalence of drug use (though rising), cultural attitudes associating drug use with moral failure, and the influence of Confucian values emphasizing social harmony and self-discipline. The Narcotics Control Act does not distinguish between personal use quantities and trafficking quantities for manufacturing offenses -- any production of controlled substances triggers the severe penalty provisions.

### Extraterritorial Jurisdiction

Article 4 of the Narcotics Control Act extends jurisdiction to Korean nationals who manufacture narcotics abroad, reflecting the government's global enforcement posture. Korean nationals have been prosecuted for drug offenses committed in other countries, and the Korean National Police Agency maintains liaison officers in key countries to facilitate international narcotics investigations.

### Rising Drug Concerns

Despite historically low rates, South Korea has experienced a significant increase in drug-related arrests and seizures in recent years. The Supreme Prosecutors' Office reported a 25 percent increase in narcotics cases between 2022 and 2024, driven primarily by methamphetamine (필로폰) and synthetic drugs. The proliferation of online drug markets and cryptocurrency transactions has complicated enforcement efforts.

### Treatment and Rehabilitation

The Narcotics Control Act includes provisions for treatment referral (치료보호), allowing prosecutors to refer persons with substance use disorders to treatment facilities. However, the treatment system has been criticized as underdeveloped relative to the severity of criminal penalties, with human rights organizations arguing that the criminalization-heavy approach discourages individuals from seeking help.

## United States

### Legal Framework

**21 U.S.C. Section 841(a):** "It shall be unlawful for any person knowingly or intentionally to manufacture, distribute, or dispense, or possess with intent to manufacture, distribute, or dispense, a controlled substance."

### Federal Penalty Structure

| Substance & Quantity | First Offense | Second Offense |
|---------------------|---------------|----------------|
| Heroin (1 kg+) | 10 years to life; $10M fine | 20 years to life |
| Cocaine (5 kg+) | 10 years to life; $10M fine | 20 years to life |
| Methamphetamine (50g+ pure) | 10 years to life; $10M fine | 20 years to life |
| Marijuana (1,000+ plants) | 10 years to life; $10M fine | 20 years to life |
| Fentanyl (400g+) | 10 years to life; $10M fine | 20 years to life |
| Schedule III substances | Up to 10 years; $500K fine | Up to 20 years |
| Schedule IV substances | Up to 5 years; $250K fine | Up to 10 years |

### Clandestine Laboratory Seizures

The Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA) has documented significant shifts in domestic drug manufacturing patterns. Methamphetamine lab seizures peaked in 2004 at over 17,000 incidents following the proliferation of small-scale "shake and bake" production methods. The Combat Methamphetamine Epidemic Act of 2005, which restricted the retail sale of pseudoephedrine, dramatically reduced domestic production but led to increased importation from Mexican super-labs.

### Cannabis Manufacturing: Federal-State Divergence

As of 2026, more than 24 states and the District of Columbia have legalized recreational cannabis, including regulated commercial production. However, cannabis remains a Schedule I substance under federal law, creating a fundamental conflict between state and federal authority. The Cole Memorandum (2013, rescinded 2018) and subsequent DOJ guidance have generally deprioritized prosecution of state-compliant cannabis operations, but federal law continues to criminalize all cannabis manufacturing.

### First Step Act of 2018

The First Step Act reformed federal sentencing for drug offenses, including manufacturing, by expanding the "safety valve" provision allowing courts to sentence below mandatory minimums, reducing the mandatory minimum for certain repeat offenders from life to 25 years, and making retroactive the Fair Sentencing Act of 2010's reduction of the crack-cocaine sentencing disparity.

### Fentanyl Crisis

The emergence of illicitly manufactured fentanyl and its analogs as the leading cause of drug overdose deaths in the United States has prompted legislative responses including the FENTANYL Results Act and the Temporary Schedule I classification of fentanyl-related substances. The DEA has identified Mexican cartels as the primary manufacturers of illicit fentanyl using Chinese-sourced precursor chemicals.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's Betäubungsmittelgesetz (BtMG) establishes the regulatory and criminal framework for narcotics control. The Act classifies substances into three annexes: Annex I (non-marketable narcotics), Annex II (marketable but non-prescribable narcotics), and Annex III (prescribable narcotics).

### Penalty Structure

| Offense | Provision | Penalty |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Basic manufacturing | Section 29(1) No. 1 | Up to 5 years or fine |
| Manufacturing for supply (Handeltreiben) | Section 29(1) No. 1 | Up to 5 years or fine |
| Non-trivial quantity (nicht geringe Menge) | Section 29a(1) No. 2 | 1-15 years |
| As member of gang | Section 30(1) No. 1 | 2-15 years |
| Gang + non-trivial quantity | Section 30a | 5-15 years |
| Manufacturing with firearm | Section 30a(2) No. 2 | 5-15 years |

### Cannabis Legalization (Cannabisgesetz, 2024)

The Gesetz zum kontrollierten Umgang mit Cannabis (CanG), effective April 1, 2024, fundamentally changed Germany's approach to cannabis. Key provisions affecting manufacturing:

- Adults (18+) may cultivate up to three cannabis plants for personal use.
- Cannabis social clubs (Anbauvereinigungen) of up to 500 members may collectively cultivate cannabis for distribution to members.
- Commercial production remains prohibited; no licensed retail sales system was established.
- Possession limits: 25 grams in public, 50 grams at home.

The legalization generated significant debate within Germany and the EU. The European Commission examined whether the CanG was compatible with EU Framework Decision 2004/757/JHA, ultimately deferring to Germany's interpretation that the Decision allows member states to exclude personal cultivation from criminal penalties.

### BVerfG Cannabis Decision (BVerfGE 90, 145, 1994)

In its landmark 1994 decision, the Bundesverfassungsgericht upheld the constitutionality of cannabis prohibition but established that prosecution for possession of small quantities for personal use must be dropped in accordance with the principle of proportionality. This decision did not directly address manufacturing but established the proportionality framework that ultimately underpinned the 2024 legalization.

### Therapy Instead of Punishment (Therapie statt Strafe)

Section 35 BtMG allows courts to defer or suspend imprisonment for drug offenders who undergo treatment, provided the offense was committed in connection with the offender's own drug dependence. This provision applies to manufacturing offenses where the defendant produced substances for personal consumption.

## ECHR & EU

### UN Conventions

The international drug control framework rests on three conventions:

**Single Convention on Narcotic Drugs (1961, amended 1972):** Establishes the scheduling system for narcotic drugs and requires state parties to limit the production, manufacture, and trade of scheduled substances to medical and scientific purposes.

**Convention on Psychotropic Substances (1971):** Extends the control framework to synthetic drugs including amphetamines, barbiturates, and hallucinogens.

**Convention Against Illicit Traffic (1988):** Specifically targets drug trafficking and manufacturing, requiring criminalization of the production of controlled substances and precursor chemicals, and establishing frameworks for international cooperation, extradition, and asset forfeiture.

### EU Framework Decision 2004/757/JHA

The Framework Decision establishes minimum rules on the constituent elements of criminal acts and penalties in the field of illicit drug trafficking:

- Article 2 requires member states to criminalize the production and manufacturing of drugs.
- Article 4 establishes minimum maximum penalties of 1-3 years for basic offenses and 5-10 years for offenses involving large quantities or participation in criminal organizations.
- Member states may exclude personal cultivation from criminal penalties.

### EU Drugs Agency (EUDA)

The European Monitoring Centre for Drugs and Drug Addiction (EMCDDA), transformed into the EU Drugs Agency (EUDA) effective July 2, 2024, monitors drug manufacturing trends, issues early warnings on new psychoactive substances, and supports member state law enforcement efforts.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Torreggiani and Others v. Italy (Application No. 43517/09, 2013):** While primarily a prison conditions case, the Court's findings regarding prison overcrowding driven by drug offense convictions have influenced the debate on proportionality of drug manufacturing penalties.

## Comparative Analysis

### Penalty Severity

South Korea imposes the most severe penalties, including the death penalty for large-scale narcotics manufacturing. The United States follows with mandatory life imprisonment for major manufacturing offenses. Germany occupies the most moderate position, with a maximum of fifteen years for the most serious manufacturing offenses and recent cannabis legalization reflecting a shift toward harm reduction.

### Cannabis Divergence

The treatment of cannabis manufacturing represents the most dramatic divergence. South Korea maintains criminal penalties of five or more years. The United States maintains federal prohibition alongside state-level legalization. Germany has legalized personal cultivation and social clubs while maintaining criminal penalties for commercial production. This divergence challenges the international consensus embodied in the UN Conventions.

### Treatment Alternatives

All jurisdictions recognize some form of treatment alternative for drug-dependent manufacturers, but the availability and scope differ. Germany's Section 35 BtMG provides the most structured framework. The United States has developed an extensive drug court system. South Korea's treatment infrastructure is less developed relative to its punitive framework.
