## Summary

Letters of credit -- documentary credit instruments through which a bank undertakes to pay a beneficiary upon presentation of conforming documents -- constitute one of the most critical mechanisms in international trade finance, providing payment security while allocating risk among buyers, sellers, and financial intermediaries. The legal framework governing letters of credit varies considerably across jurisdictions, though the International Chamber of Commerce's Uniform Customs and Practice for Documentary Credits (UCP 600, ICC Publication No. 600, effective July 1, 2007) provides a widely adopted set of rules that parties may incorporate by reference. South Korea regulates letters of credit primarily through the Korean Civil Code (민법) and the Korean Commercial Code (상법), supplemented by the Foreign Exchange Transactions Act (외국환거래법) and Bank of Korea regulations, with Korean courts generally applying UCP 600 as incorporated trade usage under Article 1 of the Commercial Code. The United States codifies letter of credit law in Article 5 of the Uniform Commercial Code (U.C.C. § 5-101 et seq.), as revised in 1995, which establishes a comprehensive statutory framework for both commercial and standby letters of credit, while the International Standby Practices (ISP98, ICC Publication No. 590) governs standby letters of credit. Germany treats letters of credit (Akkreditiv) as a sui generis banking transaction governed by general contract law under the Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB) and commercial usage under the Handelsgesetzbuch (HGB), with German banks routinely incorporating UCP 600 through their Allgemeine Geschäftsbedingungen (general terms and conditions), while the European Court of Human Rights and EU framework address letters of credit primarily through the lens of property rights under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 to the ECHR and EU directives on payment services and trade finance.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea lacks a dedicated statute governing letters of credit, instead relying on a combination of the Commercial Code, Civil Code, and international trade practice, particularly UCP 600.

**Commercial Code (상법) Application:** Article 1 of the Korean Commercial Code provides that in the absence of specific commercial legislation, commercial customs and usages apply, and failing that, the Civil Code. Korean courts have consistently recognized UCP 600 (and its predecessor UCP 500) as applicable trade usage in letter of credit disputes. The Supreme Court in Decision 2003다60828 (November 28, 2003) affirmed that UCP provisions constitute binding contractual terms when incorporated by reference in the credit instrument.

**Foreign Exchange Transactions Act (외국환거래법):** Letters of credit involving foreign currency transactions are subject to the Foreign Exchange Transactions Act (Act No. 5550, as amended), which requires banks engaging in trade finance to hold appropriate foreign exchange business authorization from the Bank of Korea. Article 8 of the Act requires reporting of foreign exchange transactions exceeding specified thresholds.

### Independence Principle

Korean courts firmly uphold the independence principle (독립성의 원칙), recognizing that the bank's obligation under a letter of credit is independent of the underlying sales contract.

**Supreme Court Decision 2003다60828 (2003):** The Court held that a confirming bank's obligation to pay under a letter of credit is independent of disputes between the applicant and beneficiary regarding the underlying contract of sale, and that the bank must honor complying presentations regardless of claims raised between the commercial parties.

**Supreme Court Decision 99다67602 (2000):** The Court reaffirmed that the issuing bank cannot refuse payment based on defenses arising from the underlying transaction, establishing that the independence principle is a fundamental feature of Korean letter of credit law.

### Fraud Exception

Korean law recognizes a narrow fraud exception to the independence principle, though its application is strictly circumscribed.

**Supreme Court Decision 94다12304 (1994):** The Court acknowledged that payment under a letter of credit may be enjoined where the beneficiary has engaged in fraud in the transaction, but held that the fraud must be clear and obvious (명백한 사기), not merely alleged by the applicant. The Court emphasized that injunctive relief should be granted only in exceptional circumstances to preserve the commercial utility of letters of credit.

**Injunctive Relief Standards:** Korean courts apply a four-part test for injunctive relief against payment under a letter of credit:
1. Clear evidence of beneficiary fraud
2. The fraud must vitiate the entire transaction
3. The bank must not have already accepted or paid the documents
4. The balance of equities must favor the applicant

### Strict Compliance Standard

**Supreme Court Decision 2001다67600 (2002):** The Court adopted the strict compliance doctrine, holding that banks must examine documents with reasonable care to determine whether they appear on their face to constitute a complying presentation. Minor discrepancies in document descriptions that do not create genuine ambiguity may not justify dishonor, but the standard remains substantially stricter than a "functional equivalence" approach.

### Recent Developments

The Korea International Trade Association (KITA) and Korean banks have increasingly adopted electronic letters of credit through the KTNET (Korea Trade Network) electronic trade platform, which handles over 70 percent of Korea's trade documentation electronically. The adoption of the ICC's eUCP supplement (version 2.0, effective July 1, 2019) has facilitated electronic presentation of documents under letters of credit issued by Korean banks.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States provides the most comprehensive statutory treatment of letters of credit among the jurisdictions examined, through Article 5 of the Uniform Commercial Code.

**U.C.C. Article 5 (Revised 1995):** Article 5 governs both commercial documentary credits and standby letters of credit. Key provisions include:

| Section | Content |
|---------|---------|
| § 5-102 | Definitions, including "letter of credit," "issuer," "beneficiary," "applicant" |
| § 5-103 | Scope; deference to UCP and other practice rules |
| § 5-104 | Formal requirements; permits electronic credits |
| § 5-106 | Issuance, amendment, cancellation, and duration |
| § 5-108 | Issuer's rights and obligations; strict compliance standard |
| § 5-109 | Fraud and forgery; injunctive relief standards |
| § 5-111 | Remedies for wrongful dishonor or wrongful honor |
| § 5-115 | Statute of limitations (one year) |
| § 5-116 | Choice of law and forum |

**Relationship with UCP 600:** U.C.C. § 5-103(c) provides that Article 5 provisions may be varied by agreement, including by incorporation of UCP 600. Where UCP 600 is incorporated, its provisions govern to the extent they do not conflict with non-variable provisions of Article 5. In practice, virtually all commercial letters of credit issued by U.S. banks incorporate UCP 600.

**International Standby Practices (ISP98):** ICC Publication No. 590, developed under the leadership of Professor James E. Byrne at the Institute of International Banking Law and Practice, governs standby letters of credit and is widely used in the United States as an alternative to UCP 600 for standby credits.

### Independence Principle

The independence principle is codified in U.C.C. § 5-103(d), which provides that "[r]ights and obligations of an issuer to a beneficiary or a nominated person under a letter of credit are independent of the existence, performance, or nonperformance of a contract or arrangement out of which the letter of credit arises."

**Sztejn v. J. Henry Schroder Banking Corp., 177 Misc. 719, 31 N.Y.S.2d 631 (Sup. Ct. 1941):** This seminal case established the fraud exception to the independence principle in American law. Justice Shientag held that where the seller's fraud has been called to the bank's attention before the documents have been presented for payment, the bank is not obligated to pay.

### Fraud Exception

**U.C.C. § 5-109:** The fraud exception is codified and provides detailed standards:

- § 5-109(a): An issuer acting in good faith may honor a presentation that appears on its face to strictly comply, even if the presentation is forged or materially fraudulent, and the issuer incurs no liability for such honor.
- § 5-109(a)(1): An issuer shall honor a presentation if a nominated person or transferee beneficiary has given value in good faith and without notice of fraud or forgery.
- § 5-109(b): A court may enjoin honor only if: (1) the relief is not prohibited under applicable law; (2) a beneficiary, issuer, or nominated person who may be adversely affected is adequately protected; (3) all conditions for issuance of the injunction are met; and (4) on the basis of the information available, the applicant is more likely than not to succeed on the merits showing material fraud by the beneficiary.

**Mid-America Tire, Inc. v. PTZ Trading Ltd., 95 Ohio St.3d 367 (2002):** The Ohio Supreme Court applied § 5-109 and held that material fraud must be narrowly defined -- fraud must have "so vitiated the entire transaction that the legitimate purposes of the independence of the issuer's obligation would no longer be served."

### Standby Letters of Credit

Standby letters of credit, which function as guarantee instruments rather than payment mechanisms, are extensively used in the United States due to historical restrictions on banks issuing guarantees directly. U.S. banks issue the majority of standby letters of credit worldwide, with outstanding standby obligations exceeding $400 billion.

**Regulatory Treatment:** The Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (OCC) regulates standby letters of credit issued by national banks under 12 C.F.R. § 7.1016. Standby letters of credit are treated as credit equivalents for capital adequacy purposes under Basel III, requiring 100 percent credit conversion factor.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany treats the letter of credit (Dokumentenakkreditiv) as an atypical banking transaction (atypisches Bankgeschäft) not specifically regulated by statute. The legal framework derives from general contract law principles under the BGB, commercial law under the HGB, and banking practice.

**Contractual Structure:** German doctrine analyzes the letter of credit as a series of interrelated but independent contracts:

| Relationship | Legal Basis | Nature |
|-------------|-------------|--------|
| Applicant-Issuing Bank | § 675 BGB (Geschäftsbesorgungsvertrag) | Agency/service contract |
| Issuing Bank-Beneficiary | Abstract payment promise (abstraktes Schuldversprechen, § 780 BGB) | Independent obligation |
| Issuing Bank-Confirming Bank | § 675 BGB | Commission/agency |
| Confirming Bank-Beneficiary | Abstract payment promise (§ 780 BGB) | Independent obligation |

**UCP 600 Incorporation:** German banks routinely incorporate UCP 600 through their Allgemeine Geschäftsbedingungen (AGB). The Bundesgerichtshof (BGH) has held that UCP provisions incorporated through AGB are subject to the AGB-Kontrolle (standard terms review) under §§ 305-310 BGB, though in practice UCP provisions have been upheld as reflecting established international trade usage.

### Independence Principle (Abstraktionsprinzip)

The German Abstraktionsprinzip provides a strong doctrinal foundation for the independence of letter of credit obligations. The BGH has consistently held that the bank's payment obligation under a confirmed letter of credit is abstract (losgelöst) from the underlying commercial transaction.

**BGH, November 10, 1998 (XI ZR 370/97):** The Court held that the confirming bank's obligation to pay against conforming documents is independent of the underlying sale contract and cannot be affected by disputes between buyer and seller.

### Fraud Exception (Rechtsmissbrauchseinwand)

German law recognizes a fraud exception grounded in the general principle of good faith (Treu und Glauben) under § 242 BGB. The BGH has established that a bank may refuse payment where:

1. The beneficiary's demand for payment constitutes an abuse of right (Rechtsmissbrauch)
2. The fraud is evident (liquide beweisbar) and not merely alleged
3. The documents, while facially compliant, are demonstrably fraudulent

**BGH, January 21, 1999 (IX ZR 429/97):** The Court held that the fraud exception applies only where the fraud is "clearly and obviously proven" (klar und eindeutig nachgewiesen), and that the bank bears no duty of independent investigation beyond facial examination of documents.

**Einstweiliger Rechtsschutz (Interim Relief):** German courts may grant preliminary injunctions (einstweilige Verfügung) under §§ 935, 940 ZPO to restrain payment under a letter of credit in cases of fraud. The applicant must demonstrate both a claim (Verfügungsanspruch) and urgency (Verfügungsgrund).

### Strict Compliance (Dokumentenstrenge)

German banking law applies the principle of strict document compliance (strenge Dokumentenkonformität). The issuing bank must examine documents solely on their face (formelle Dokumentenprüfung) and may not look behind the documents to verify their accuracy against the actual goods or performance.

**BGH, March 14, 2000 (XI ZR 287/99):** The Court confirmed that the examination standard is purely documentary, and the bank has neither the right nor the obligation to investigate the factual accuracy of document contents.

### Recent Developments

German banks have participated in blockchain-based letter of credit platforms, with Commerzbank and Deutsche Bank executing pilot transactions on the Marco Polo and Contour networks. BaFin has issued guidance on the regulatory treatment of digitally native trade finance instruments, requiring compliance with existing KWG licensing requirements.

## ECHR / EU

### EU Legal Framework

The European Union has not adopted harmonized legislation specifically governing letters of credit, leaving substantive regulation to the national laws of member states and the contractual incorporation of UCP 600.

**Payment Services Directive (PSD2, Directive (EU) 2015/2366):** While PSD2 primarily governs payment transactions, its scope potentially extends to certain aspects of letter of credit payment processing. Article 3(h) excludes from PSD2's scope payment transactions based on paper-based documents such as drafts and cheques, but electronic letter of credit processing may fall within PSD2 requirements.

**Trade Finance and Sanctions Compliance:** EU Regulation 833/2014 (as amended) concerning restrictive measures in view of Russia's actions destabilizing the situation in Ukraine has significantly impacted letter of credit operations in Europe. Banks must verify that letter of credit transactions do not involve sanctioned parties or prohibited goods, creating tension with the independence principle.

**EU Anti-Money Laundering Directives:** The Sixth Anti-Money Laundering Directive (Directive (EU) 2024/1640) requires banks to apply customer due diligence measures to letter of credit transactions, including verification of the identities of applicants and beneficiaries. The Anti-Money Laundering Authority (AMLA) established under Regulation (EU) 2024/1620 will provide supervisory guidance on trade-based money laundering through letters of credit.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Property Rights (Article 1, Protocol No. 1):** The European Court of Human Rights has recognized that rights arising under letters of credit may constitute "possessions" within the meaning of Article 1 of Protocol No. 1. A beneficiary's right to receive payment under a confirmed credit constitutes a legitimate expectation of property where the presentation conforms to the credit terms.

**Agrokompleks v. Ukraine (Application No. 23465/03, ECtHR Judgment of December 6, 2011):** While not directly involving a letter of credit, the Court's analysis of state interference with commercial payment obligations under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 is instructive. The Court held that state measures preventing the enforcement of a commercial payment obligation may constitute a deprivation of possessions requiring justification under the legitimate aims of the Protocol.

**Regent Company v. Ukraine (Application No. 773/03, ECtHR Judgment of April 3, 2008):** The Court found a violation of Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 where domestic courts failed to enforce a legitimate commercial claim, establishing that the right to have a debt paid constitutes a possession protected by the Convention.

### Cross-Border Letter of Credit Disputes in the EU

**Brussels I Regulation (Recast) (Regulation (EU) No 1215/2012):** Jurisdictional disputes in cross-border letter of credit transactions within the EU are governed by the Brussels I Regulation. The CJEU's interpretation of the "place of performance" under Article 7(1) is critical for determining jurisdiction in letter of credit disputes.

**Rome I Regulation (Regulation (EC) No 593/2008):** The choice of law for letter of credit obligations is determined by the Rome I Regulation. In the absence of a choice of law clause, Article 4 provides that the applicable law is that of the country of the party required to effect the characteristic performance -- which, in the case of a letter of credit, is typically the law of the issuing bank's place of business.

### ICC Banking Commission Standards

The ICC Banking Commission, based in Paris, serves as the primary global rule-making body for documentary credit practice:

## Comparative Analysis

### Independence Principle: Convergence and Variation

All four jurisdictions recognize the independence principle as fundamental to letter of credit law, but the doctrinal basis differs:

- **South Korea:** Jurisprudential recognition based on trade usage and commercial custom, integrated through the Commercial Code's deference to established mercantile practice
- **United States:** Statutory codification in U.C.C. § 5-103(d), providing the clearest and most explicit legislative statement of the principle
- **Germany:** Grounded in the Abstraktionsprinzip, a broader doctrinal principle of German private law that separates abstract obligations from their underlying causal transactions
- **ECHR/EU:** Recognized through national laws of member states; no EU-level harmonization, though the independence principle is universally accepted in European trade finance practice

### Fraud Exception: Divergent Standards

The most significant divergence among jurisdictions concerns the fraud exception:

| Jurisdiction | Standard | Burden of Proof | Injunctive Relief |
|-------------|----------|----------------|-------------------|
| South Korea | Clear and obvious fraud (명백한 사기) | Applicant; high threshold | Available but rarely granted |
| United States | Material fraud (U.C.C. § 5-109) | More likely than not to succeed | § 5-109(b) four-part test |
| Germany | Evident abuse of right (Rechtsmissbrauch) | Clearly and obviously proven | Einstweilige Verfügung (§§ 935, 940 ZPO) |
| ECHR/EU | National law applies; no harmonization | Varies by member state | Varies by member state |

The United States provides the most detailed statutory framework for the fraud exception, while South Korea and Germany rely on judicial development of general principles. The U.S. standard of "material fraud" is somewhat more accessible to applicants than the German or Korean requirements of "clear and obvious" fraud.

### Standby Letters of Credit

The treatment of standby letters of credit reveals significant structural differences:

- **United States:** Standby letters of credit are widely used as guarantee substitutes due to historical restrictions on bank guarantees. U.C.C. Article 5 expressly covers standby credits. The United States accounts for the majority of global standby letter of credit issuance.
- **Germany:** German banks may issue both letters of credit and bank guarantees (Bankgarantien), reducing the functional need for standby credits. When standby credits are used, they are typically governed by ISP98.
- **South Korea:** Standby letters of credit are used primarily in international transactions. Korean banks distinguish between payment guarantees (지급보증) and standby credits, with the latter subject to Foreign Exchange Transactions Act reporting requirements.
- **EU:** No harmonized framework for standby letters of credit; each member state applies its own national law.

### Electronic Letters of Credit

The transition to electronic documentation presents both convergence and divergence:

- All jurisdictions accept electronic letters of credit in principle
- The ICC's eUCP v2.0 supplement provides a uniform framework for electronic presentation where incorporated
- The United States permits electronic records under U.C.C. § 5-104
- The EU's eIDAS Regulation provides a framework for electronic identification and trust services that supports electronic trade finance
- South Korea's KTNET platform represents the most advanced national electronic trade documentation system
- Blockchain-based platforms (Contour, Marco Polo, Komgo) are being piloted across all jurisdictions

### Sanctions Compliance and the Independence Principle

Post-2022 sanctions regimes have created unprecedented tension with the independence principle:

- Banks in all jurisdictions must screen letter of credit transactions against sanctions lists before processing
- EU sanctions regulations (particularly Regulation (EU) 833/2014 as amended) may require banks to freeze or reject letter of credit payments involving sanctioned parties, even where documents conform
- This effectively creates a statutory override of the independence principle for sanctions compliance purposes
- The tension between sanctions obligations and the independence principle remains an area of active legal development across all jurisdictions
