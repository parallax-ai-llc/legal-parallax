## Summary

Nuclear liability law -- the legal framework governing civil liability for damage caused by nuclear incidents -- addresses a unique challenge: the potential for catastrophic, widespread harm from a low-probability event, combined with the strategic importance of nuclear energy and the impossibility of any single operator bearing the full cost of a major accident. South Korea's Act on Compensation for Nuclear Damage (원자력손해배상법) establishes strict liability for nuclear operators with a mandatory insurance requirement of 500 billion KRW (approximately $380 million) per installation, supplemented by government indemnification for damages exceeding insurance coverage; the Act was significantly amended following the 2011 Fukushima disaster and Korea's own nuclear safety concerns surrounding the Wolsong and Shin-Kori nuclear plants. The United States operates under the Price-Anderson Nuclear Industries Indemnity Act (42 U.S.C. Sections 2210 et seq.), originally enacted in 1957 and most recently reauthorized by the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act of 2021, which establishes a two-tier insurance system providing approximately $13.7 billion in liability coverage through mandatory private insurance ($500 million per reactor) and a retrospective pooling mechanism requiring all licensed reactor operators to contribute up to approximately $121 million per reactor per incident; the Act channels all liability to the nuclear operator (economic channeling) and provides federal jurisdiction over nuclear tort claims. Germany's Nuclear Energy Act (Atomgesetz, AtG) Section 25 establishes unlimited operator liability for nuclear damage -- the strictest standard among nuclear nations -- combined with a mandatory financial security requirement of 2.5 billion euros per installation under the Nuclear Financial Security Ordinance (Atomrechtliche Deckungsvorsorge-Verordnung); Germany's nuclear liability framework is particularly significant given the country's decision to phase out nuclear power entirely, with the last three reactors shutting down on April 15, 2023. At the international level, the nuclear liability regime is built on two parallel treaty systems -- the Paris Convention (1960, OECD) and the Vienna Convention (1963, IAEA) -- linked by the Joint Protocol (1988), with the Brussels Supplementary Convention (1963, amended 2004) and the Convention on Supplementary Compensation for Nuclear Damage (CSC, 1997) providing additional compensation layers; the European Court of Human Rights has addressed nuclear safety through the environmental rights framework of Articles 2 and 8 of the Convention, establishing in cases such as Balmer-Schafroth v. Switzerland (1997) and Tatar v. Romania (2009) that states have positive obligations to protect individuals from nuclear and environmental hazards.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea operates 25 commercial nuclear reactors at four sites (Kori/Shin-Kori, Hanbit, Hanul, and Wolsong), generating approximately 30% of the country's electricity. The nuclear liability framework is governed by the Act on Compensation for Nuclear Damage (원자력손해배상법), originally enacted in 1969 and comprehensively amended in 2015.

**Strict Liability:** Article 3 of the Act imposes strict (무과실) liability on the nuclear operator for all nuclear damage caused by nuclear material or radiation from the operator's nuclear installation, regardless of fault. The operator is liable for damage arising from normal operations as well as accidents.

**Unlimited Liability (2015 Amendment):** Prior to the 2015 amendment, operator liability was capped at 500 billion KRW. Following the Fukushima disaster and growing public concern about nuclear safety, the National Assembly removed the liability cap, making operator liability unlimited in principle. However, the mandatory financial security (insurance) requirement remains at 500 billion KRW, creating a gap between the unlimited liability and the insured amount.

**Legal Channeling:** The Act channels all liability to the nuclear operator, meaning that victims may claim only against the operator, not against suppliers, contractors, or other third parties. This channeling principle facilitates the insurance and indemnification system by concentrating risk on a single insurable entity.

**Government Indemnification:** For damages exceeding the mandatory financial security amount, the government may provide indemnification under a government indemnity agreement (정부보상계약). The terms and extent of government indemnification are determined by the Nuclear Safety and Security Commission (원자력안전위원회).

### Wolsong-1 Early Closure Controversy

The early closure of the Wolsong-1 reactor in 2019, ostensibly for economic reasons, became a major political controversy when allegations surfaced that the economic assessment justifying closure had been manipulated. The Board of Audit and Inspection (감사원) found in 2020 that Korea Hydro & Nuclear Power had underestimated the reactor's remaining economic value. The case raised important questions about the intersection of nuclear liability (decommissioning liability allocation), energy policy, and regulatory integrity.

### Nuclear Phase-In Policy

Unlike Germany, South Korea under the Yoon administration has embraced nuclear energy as a pillar of the energy transition, planning to increase nuclear's share of electricity generation to approximately 32-34% by 2036 and pursuing nuclear reactor exports. This policy orientation increases the importance of the nuclear liability framework as new reactors are planned and existing ones receive life extensions.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The Price-Anderson Nuclear Industries Indemnity Act (42 U.S.C. Section 2210), first enacted in 1957, establishes the world's oldest nuclear liability system and has served as a model for nuclear compensation frameworks globally.

**Two-Tier Insurance System:**

| Tier | Source | Amount (as of 2025) |
|------|--------|-------------------|
| First Tier | Private nuclear liability insurance (American Nuclear Insurers pool) | $500 million per reactor site |
| Second Tier | Retrospective premium pooling (all licensed reactor operators contribute) | ~$121.255 million per reactor per incident (deferred premium); maximum total pool ~$13.7 billion |

If damages exceed the total two-tier amount, Congress is required to "thoroughly review the particular incident" and "take whatever action is determined to be necessary" -- a provision that implicitly but not explicitly commits the government to covering excess damages.

**Economic Channeling:** Unlike the legal channeling model used in most other countries, Price-Anderson employs economic channeling: the operator's insurance covers all claims arising from a nuclear incident, but suppliers and contractors are not formally shielded from liability in theory. In practice, the omnibus coverage provided by the insurance system effectively protects all parties involved in the nuclear enterprise.

**Extraordinary Nuclear Occurrence (ENO):** When the Nuclear Regulatory Commission (NRC) or the courts determine that an extraordinary nuclear occurrence has taken place, the Act waives certain legal defenses including contributory negligence, assumption of risk, charitable immunity, and statutes of limitations, converting the standard from strict liability to something approaching absolute liability.

**Federal Jurisdiction:** The Act provides that any public liability action arising from a nuclear incident shall be in the federal district court in which the nuclear incident occurs, with the option of consolidation through multidistrict litigation procedures.

### Key Litigation

**In re TMI Litigation (Three Mile Island, 1979):** Following the partial meltdown at Three Mile Island Unit 2 in Pennsylvania, approximately 2,400 personal injury claims and thousands of property damage claims were filed. The cases were consolidated in the U.S. District Court for the Middle District of Pennsylvania. Most claims were settled, with the total payout from the insurance pool reaching approximately $71 million -- well within the first tier of Price-Anderson coverage. The TMI litigation established important precedents for causation standards in nuclear tort cases.

**Reauthorization History:** Price-Anderson has been reauthorized and amended five times since 1957 (1966, 1975, 1988, 2005, and 2021). The 2021 reauthorization, included in the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (Pub. L. 117-58), extended the Act through 2045 and included provisions for advanced reactor technologies and small modular reactors (SMRs).

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany established the most stringent nuclear liability regime among nuclear nations, with unlimited operator liability and substantial financial security requirements.

**Atomgesetz (AtG) Sections 25-40:** The Nuclear Energy Act establishes the following principles:

**Unlimited Liability (Section 25 AtG):** The nuclear installation operator is liable without limit for all damage caused by a nuclear incident. Germany is the only major nuclear nation to impose truly unlimited liability, a policy choice that reflects the political influence of the anti-nuclear movement and the Bundestag's determination to ensure that nuclear operators bear the full social cost of their activities.

**Financial Security (Section 13 AtG; Atomrechtliche Deckungsvorsorge-Verordnung):** Operators must maintain financial security of 2.5 billion euros per installation, typically through a combination of liability insurance and direct operator guarantees. The financial security amount was increased from 500 million DM to 2.5 billion euros in the 2002 amendment to the AtG.

**Legal Channeling:** Section 25(1) AtG channels liability exclusively to the nuclear operator. Suppliers, contractors, and transporters are shielded from direct claims by victims, with the operator's right of recourse against negligent suppliers preserved.

**Prescription Periods:** Claims for nuclear damage are subject to a 30-year prescription period from the date of the nuclear incident (Section 32 AtG), significantly longer than the standard German tort prescription period of three years.

### Nuclear Phase-Out and Liability

Germany's decision to phase out nuclear power, originally enacted in 2002 (AtG amendment), reversed by the Merkel government in 2010, and definitively reinstated following Fukushima in 2011 (13th AtG amendment), had significant liability implications.

**Decommissioning Liability:** The 2017 Act on the Reorganization of Responsibility in Nuclear Waste Management (Gesetz zur Neuordnung der Verantwortung in der kerntechnischen Entsorgung) established a Nuclear Waste Management Fund (Fonds zur Finanzierung der kerntechnischen Entsorgung) into which the four nuclear operators (E.ON, RWE, EnBW, and Vattenfall) paid a total of approximately 24.1 billion euros. The fund assumed financial responsibility for intermediate and final storage of nuclear waste, while the operators retained responsibility for decommissioning the plants themselves.

**Constitutional Compensation:** The Federal Constitutional Court ruled in BVerfGE 143, 246 (2016) that the accelerated nuclear phase-out (13th AtG amendment) constituted a partial expropriation requiring compensation under Article 14(3) GG. The operators received compensation totaling approximately 2.4 billion euros under a 2021 settlement.

### Last Reactors Shutdown

Germany's final three nuclear reactors -- Isar 2, Neckarwestheim 2, and Emsland -- shut down on April 15, 2023. The nuclear liability framework remains relevant for decades due to the long tail of decommissioning activities and nuclear waste storage.

## ECHR / EU

### International Nuclear Liability Conventions

The international nuclear liability regime is built on two parallel systems of conventions:

**Paris Convention System (OECD/NEA):**
- Paris Convention on Third Party Liability in the Field of Nuclear Energy (1960, amended 1964, 1982, 2004)
- Brussels Supplementary Convention (1963, amended 2004)
- Parties: primarily Western European states (France, Germany, UK, Belgium, etc.)

**Vienna Convention System (IAEA):**
- Vienna Convention on Civil Liability for Nuclear Damage (1963, amended 1997)
- Convention on Supplementary Compensation for Nuclear Damage (CSC, 1997)
- Parties: broader global membership including Argentina, Romania, Russia, etc.

**Joint Protocol (1988):** Links the two systems, ensuring that victims in Paris Convention states can claim against operators in Vienna Convention states, and vice versa.

**2004 Paris Protocol (not yet in force as of 2026):** Would increase operator liability to SDR 700 million (approximately 860 million euros), expand the definition of nuclear damage to include environmental damage, preventive measures, and economic loss, and extend the personal injury prescription period to 30 years.

### Compensation Tiers

The Brussels Supplementary Convention establishes a three-tier compensation system (under the 2004 amendments, not yet in force):

| Tier | Source | Amount |
|------|--------|--------|
| First | Operator insurance/financial security | SDR 700 million |
| Second | State of the installation | Between SDR 700 million and SDR 1.2 billion |
| Third | Collective contributions by all contracting parties | Between SDR 1.2 billion and SDR 1.5 billion |

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Balmer-Schafroth v. Switzerland (Application No. 22110/93, 1997):** Applicants living near the Mühleberg nuclear power plant challenged the extension of the plant's operating license, alleging violations of Article 6 (right to a fair hearing) and Article 8 (right to private life). The Grand Chamber found no Article 6 violation, holding that the applicants had not established a sufficiently direct link between the operating conditions challenged and their right to protection of their physical integrity. The case set a relatively high threshold for standing in nuclear safety cases.

**Tatar v. Romania (Application No. 67021/01, 2009):** While not a nuclear case, the Court's finding that states must conduct adequate environmental and health impact assessments before authorizing potentially hazardous industrial activities has been applied analogically to nuclear licensing decisions.

**L.C.B. v. United Kingdom (Application No. 23413/94, 1998):** The applicant, daughter of a serviceman present at Christmas Island nuclear tests, alleged that the UK violated Article 2 (right to life) by failing to warn her parents of the risk of her developing leukemia. While the Court found no violation on the facts, it confirmed that Article 2 can require states to take positive measures to protect individuals from known radiation risks.

### EU Nuclear Safety Directive

Directive 2009/71/Euratom (Nuclear Safety Directive), as amended by Directive 2014/87/Euratom, establishes a community framework for the nuclear safety of nuclear installations. While focused on safety rather than liability, the directive's requirements for safety assessments, peer reviews, and transparency complement the liability framework by reducing the probability of incidents triggering liability claims.

## Comparative Analysis

### Liability Caps vs. Unlimited Liability

The most striking comparative divergence concerns liability caps. Germany imposes unlimited liability, reflecting a policy of full internalization of nuclear risk. The United States caps total industry liability at approximately $13.7 billion -- an enormous amount but theoretically insufficient for a Fukushima-scale disaster (Japan's compensation costs have exceeded $200 billion). South Korea removed its liability cap in 2015 while maintaining a limited insurance requirement. The international conventions establish relatively low minimum liability amounts that many scholars regard as inadequate.

### Financial Security Gap

All jurisdictions face a "financial security gap" -- the difference between an operator's available financial security and the potential cost of a worst-case nuclear incident. Germany's 2.5 billion euros in mandatory financial security covers only a fraction of potential unlimited liability. The U.S. two-tier system provides approximately $13.7 billion but relies on the implicit assumption that Congress would appropriate additional funds. South Korea's 500 billion KRW insurance against unlimited liability creates a similar gap covered by government indemnification.

### Channeling Models

Legal channeling (Paris Convention model, used by South Korea, Germany, and most European states) formally protects suppliers from victim claims. Economic channeling (U.S. model) achieves a similar result through the structure of insurance coverage. Both models are designed to facilitate the nuclear insurance market by concentrating risk on a single entity, but they have different implications for supplier accountability and recourse rights.

### Nuclear Phase-Out and Continuing Liability

Germany's experience demonstrates that nuclear liability obligations persist long after reactors cease generating electricity. The 24.1 billion euros paid into the Nuclear Waste Management Fund reflects the decades-long tail risk of nuclear waste management, and the constitutional compensation required for the accelerated phase-out illustrates the intersection of nuclear liability, property rights, and energy policy transitions.
