## Summary

Currency crimes -- including counterfeiting, forgery of banknotes and coins, uttering (knowingly passing counterfeit currency), and related offenses such as defacement and unauthorized reproduction -- rank among the oldest criminal offenses in legal history, traditionally treated with extraordinary severity because they strike at the sovereign's authority to issue money and threaten the integrity of the monetary system upon which all economic activity depends. South Korea criminalizes currency offenses through Articles 207 through 213 of the Criminal Act (형법), treating counterfeiting of Korean Won (대한민국의 통용하는 화폐 위조) as one of the most severely punished property-related offenses with imprisonment of two or more years for counterfeiting domestic currency (Article 207(1)) and one or more years for counterfeiting foreign currency (Article 207(2)), while the Bank of Korea Act (한국은행법) and the Framework Act on Electronic Documents and Electronic Transactions address emerging threats from digital counterfeiting; Korean authorities have been particularly concerned with high-quality "supernotes" -- counterfeit US$100 bills allegedly produced in North Korea. The United States treats counterfeiting as a federal offense under 18 U.S.C. Sections 470-514, with the Secret Service (originally established in 1865 specifically to combat counterfeiting) serving as the primary enforcement agency, and penalties reaching up to 20 years imprisonment for manufacturing counterfeit currency (18 U.S.C. Section 471) and up to 25 years for counterfeiting in connection with a financial institution (18 U.S.C. Section 474); the United States has also aggressively pursued the "supernote" phenomenon, indicting North Korean entities and designating Banco Delta Asia in Macau under Section 311 of the USA PATRIOT Act for its role in facilitating North Korean counterfeiting. Germany addresses currency crimes through Sections 146 through 152b of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), with Section 146 (Geldfälschung, money counterfeiting) carrying penalties of one to fifteen years imprisonment for counterfeiting or altering money with intent to introduce it into circulation, reflecting the traditional severity with which German law treats threats to monetary integrity; Germany's role as a member of the Eurozone adds the dimension of European Central Bank coordination and the EU's framework for protecting the Euro. The ECHR has addressed currency crime prosecutions through fair trial guarantees under Article 6, while EU law provides a comprehensive framework for the protection of the Euro through Council Framework Decision 2000/383/JHA (as amended by Framework Decision 2001/888/JHA) and Directive 2014/62/EU on the protection of the Euro and other currencies against counterfeiting by criminal law, which harmonizes minimum penalties and definitions across all member states.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

**Criminal Act (형법), Chapter 16 -- Crimes Relating to Currency (화폐에 관한 죄):**

**Article 207 -- Counterfeiting Currency (화폐의 위조):**
- Paragraph 1: "A person who counterfeits or alters currency, banknotes, or bank certificates currently in circulation in the Republic of Korea shall be punished by imprisonment for not less than 2 years."
- Paragraph 2: "A person who counterfeits or alters foreign currency, banknotes, or bank certificates currently in circulation shall be punished by imprisonment for not less than 1 year."

**Article 208 -- Counterfeiting with Intent to Exercise (행사할 목적의 위조):** Addresses counterfeiting with the specific intent of putting the currency into circulation, carrying enhanced penalties.

**Article 209 -- Exercising Counterfeit Currency (위조통화의 행사):** "A person who exercises [puts into circulation] counterfeited or altered currency, banknotes, or bank certificates, or who imports or exports them from abroad with the purpose of exercise, shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than 2 years or a fine not exceeding 5 million KRW."

**Article 210 -- Knowledge of Counterfeit (위조통화취득후 지정행사):** "A person who, after acquiring currency, banknotes, or bank certificates, learns that they are counterfeited or altered, and exercises them at face value, shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than 2 years or a fine not exceeding 5 million KRW."

**Article 211 -- Counterfeiting Instruments (위조통화 제조기구):** Criminalizes the production, acquisition, or possession of instruments for counterfeiting.

### The "Supernote" Issue

South Korea has been directly affected by the North Korean counterfeiting program:

**Background:** Since the 1980s, North Korea has allegedly produced high-quality counterfeit US$100 bills (the "supernote" or "superdollar") that are nearly indistinguishable from genuine currency. These notes have been intercepted in South Korea and throughout Asia.

**Korean Peninsula Context:** The supernote issue has been intertwined with inter-Korean relations, with South Korean authorities cooperating with the U.S. Secret Service and Interpol to track counterfeiting networks operating across borders.

**Legal Response:** South Korea's currency crime provisions apply to both domestic and foreign currency counterfeiting, enabling prosecution of persons involved in distributing North Korean-produced counterfeits within South Korea.

### Statistics

The Bank of Korea (한국은행) reports annual counterfeiting statistics:
- Approximately 2,000-3,000 counterfeit notes are detected annually
- The most commonly counterfeited denomination is the 50,000 KRW note
- Digital printing technology has increased the quality of domestic counterfeits

## United States

### Legal Framework

**18 U.S.C. Sections 470-514** provide the most comprehensive federal currency crime framework:

**Section 471 -- Obligations or Securities of United States:** "Whoever, with intent to defraud, falsely makes, forges, counterfeits, or alters any obligation or other security of the United States, shall be fined under this title or imprisoned not more than 20 years, or both."

**Section 472 -- Uttering Counterfeit Obligations or Securities:** Same penalty (up to 20 years) for knowingly passing counterfeit currency.

**Section 473 -- Dealing in Counterfeit Obligations or Securities:** Addresses the business of buying, selling, or exchanging counterfeits.

**Section 474 -- Plates, Dies, and Other Implements:** Criminalizes possession of counterfeiting equipment and digital files (up to 25 years).

**Section 485 -- Coins of Foreign Countries:** Criminalizes counterfeiting foreign coins (up to 5 years).

### The U.S. Secret Service

The Secret Service was established on April 14, 1865, the day President Lincoln was assassinated, though its creation was unrelated to the assassination. Its original and continuing mission is the suppression of counterfeiting.

**Organizational Facts:**
- Transferred from Treasury to DHS in 2003
- Maintains offices in over 150 cities worldwide
- Investigates approximately 3,000 counterfeiting cases annually
- Suppressed over $600 million in counterfeit currency in fiscal year 2023

### The Supernote Investigation

**Banco Delta Asia Designation (2005):** The U.S. Treasury Department designated Banco Delta Asia in Macau as a "primary money laundering concern" under Section 311 of the USA PATRIOT Act for facilitating North Korean counterfeiting, freezing approximately $25 million in North Korean assets.

**United States v. Sean Garland (2005):** Federal prosecutors indicted Sean Garland, leader of Ireland's Workers' Party, for allegedly distributing North Korean supernotes in Europe. The case highlighted the international dimension of the supernote problem.

### Digital and Cryptocurrency

The Secret Service has expanded its mandate to address digital currency crimes:

| Threat | Legal Response |
|--------|---------------|
| Digital counterfeit note production | Section 474 covers digital plates and files |
| Cryptocurrency fraud | Investigated under wire fraud (18 U.S.C. Section 1343) and money laundering statutes |
| Digital currency counterfeiting | Emerging legal framework |

### Defacement

**18 U.S.C. Section 333 -- Mutilation of National Bank Obligations:** "Whoever mutilates, cuts, defaces, disfigures, or perforates, or unites or cements together, or does any other thing to any bank bill, draft, note, or other evidence of debt issued by any national banking association, or Federal Reserve bank, or the Federal Reserve System, with intent to render such bank bill, draft, note, or other evidence of debt unfit to be reissued, shall be fined under this title or imprisoned not more than six months, or both."

## Germany

### Legal Framework

**Section 146 StGB -- Counterfeiting of Money (Geldfälschung):**
- Paragraph 1: "Whoever (1) counterfeits money with the intention of putting it into circulation as genuine, or of making such putting into circulation possible, (2) procures counterfeit money for such a purpose, or (3) puts counterfeit money into circulation as genuine, shall be punished with imprisonment for a term not less than one year."
- Paragraph 2: "In less serious cases, the penalty shall be imprisonment from three months to five years."
- Paragraph 3 (formerly): The attempt is punishable.

**Section 147 StGB -- Altering of Money (Geldfälschung durch Wertveränderung):** Addresses altering genuine currency to appear of higher value. Penalty: 3 months to 5 years.

**Section 148 StGB -- Counterfeiting of Securities (Wertpapieren):** Extends counterfeiting provisions to securities, stamps, and other instruments.

**Section 149 StGB -- Preparatory Acts (Vorbereitung der Fälschung):** Criminalizes the preparation of counterfeiting, including producing, procuring, or storing plates, forms, printing blocks, or other equipment. Penalty: up to 5 years or fine.

**Section 150 StGB -- Rewarding Counterfeit Money (Einziehung):** Addresses the seizure and confiscation of counterfeit currency.

**Section 152a StGB -- Counterfeiting of Debit Cards and Other Cards (Fälschung von Zahlungskarten):** Extended to cover digital payment instruments. Penalty: 1-10 years.

### Euro Protection

As a Eurozone member, Germany participates in the ECB's anti-counterfeiting framework:

**European Central Bank:** The ECB coordinates the production of Euro banknotes across Eurozone national central banks and maintains technical security features. The ECB's Counterfeit Analysis Centre (CAC) and Counterfeit Currency Database (CCDB) support law enforcement.

**Bundesbank Role:** The Deutsche Bundesbank operates the National Analysis Centre (NAC) for Germany, analyzing recovered counterfeits and coordinating with the ECB and Europol.

### Statistics

According to the Bundesbank's annual counterfeiting report:
- Approximately 55,000-60,000 counterfeit Euro banknotes are recovered in Germany annually
- The most commonly counterfeited denominations are €20 and €50 notes
- Total damage from counterfeiting in Germany: approximately €3-4 million annually

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR has addressed currency crimes primarily through procedural guarantees:

**Article 6 -- Fair Trial:** Counterfeiting prosecutions must comply with Article 6 guarantees, including the right to a fair hearing, the presumption of innocence, and the right to legal representation.

**Salduz v. Turkey (Application No. 36391/02, Grand Chamber Judgment of November 27, 2008):** While not a counterfeiting case, this landmark decision on access to legal counsel during police questioning has significant implications for counterfeiting investigations, which often involve complex financial evidence and lengthy interrogations.

### EU Framework

**Directive 2014/62/EU on the Protection of the Euro and Other Currencies Against Counterfeiting by Criminal Law:**

This directive, replacing Framework Decision 2000/383/JHA (as amended by 2001/888/JHA), establishes minimum standards for the criminalization of Euro counterfeiting:

**Article 3 -- Offenses:** Requires member states to criminalize:
- Fraudulent making or altering of currency
- Fraudulent uttering of counterfeit currency
- Import, export, transport, receiving, or obtaining of counterfeit currency for uttering
- Fraudulent making, receiving, obtaining, or possession of instruments for counterfeiting

**Article 5 -- Penalties:**
- Manufacturing and altering: Maximum penalty of at least 8 years
- Uttering and related offenses: Effective, proportionate, and dissuasive penalties
- The 8-year minimum maximum for manufacturing reflects the "particular gravity" that the EU attaches to counterfeiting threats to the Euro

**Article 8 -- Investigation Tools:** Member states must ensure that effective investigation tools comparable to those used for organized crime are available for counterfeiting cases.

### Europol's Role

Europol operates the European Counterfeit Currency Programme:
- Europol Analysis File "Currency" tracks counterfeiting trends
- Joint Investigation Teams (JITs) coordinate cross-border investigations
- Europol's European Financial and Economic Crime Centre (EFECC) supports member state investigations

## Comparative Analysis

### Severity of Penalties

Currency counterfeiting is treated with notable severity across all jurisdictions:

| Jurisdiction | Manufacturing Penalty | Rationale |
|-------------|----------------------|-----------|
| South Korea | 2+ years imprisonment | Sovereignty and economic stability |
| United States | Up to 20 years | Federal authority; economic integrity |
| Germany | 1-15 years | Protection of monetary system |
| EU (minimum) | At least 8 years maximum | "Particular gravity" of Euro threats |

### Enforcement Models

- **United States:** Specialized agency (Secret Service) with international reach
- **South Korea:** General police and prosecutorial enforcement; Bank of Korea coordination
- **Germany:** BKA with Bundesbank coordination; Europol integration
- **EU:** Europol coordination with national enforcement

### Digital Challenges

All jurisdictions face new challenges from digital technology:
- High-quality digital printing enables more sophisticated counterfeits
- Cryptocurrency creates new forms of monetary fraud
- Digital payment card counterfeiting crosses traditional currency crime boundaries
- 3D printing technology may enable new forms of coin counterfeiting
