## Summary

Electric vehicle (EV) regulation has become a central pillar of climate policy worldwide, with jurisdictions adopting divergent strategies ranging from outright bans on internal combustion engine (ICE) vehicles to market-driven incentive structures. The European Union enacted Regulation (EU) 2023/851, amending CO2 emission performance standards to mandate a 100% reduction in fleet-wide CO2 emissions for new passenger cars and light commercial vehicles from 2035, effectively banning ICE vehicle sales. South Korea has pursued aggressive EV adoption targets under the Framework Act on Carbon Neutrality and Green Growth (탄소중립기본법, Act No. 18469), with a goal of 7.85 million registered EVs by 2030 and substantial purchase subsidies administered through the Ministry of Environment. The United States has adopted a fragmented approach, with federal tax credits under the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 (IRA, Pub. L. 117-169) conditioned on domestic content and assembly requirements, while California's Advanced Clean Cars II regulation mandates 100% zero-emission vehicle (ZEV) sales by 2035 under a Clean Air Act Section 209 waiver. Germany, as the EU's largest automotive market, supplements the EU framework with national incentive programs and the Schnellladegesetz (Fast-Charging Law) mandating nationwide charging infrastructure deployment.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea has established one of the most comprehensive EV regulatory frameworks in Asia through several interconnected statutes:

- **Framework Act on Carbon Neutrality and Green Growth (탄소중립·녹색성장 기본법, Act No. 18469, 2021):** Establishes South Korea's binding target of net-zero emissions by 2050 and a 40% reduction from 2018 levels by 2030. Article 25 specifically mandates the promotion of zero-emission vehicles as a key decarbonization strategy. Article 26 requires the government to establish and implement plans for expanding eco-friendly vehicle supply.
- **Act on Development and Distribution of Eco-Friendly Vehicles (친환경자동차의 개발 및 보급 촉진에 관한 법률):** The primary statute governing EV incentives, mandating purchase subsidies, public fleet procurement requirements, and charging infrastructure deployment. Article 10 requires national and local governments to prioritize eco-friendly vehicles in public procurement, with a mandatory ratio increasing annually.
- **Electric Vehicle Charging Infrastructure Promotion Act:** Mandates the installation of EV charging stations in new residential buildings (apartments with more than 100 units must include chargers in at least 5% of parking spaces, increasing to 10% by 2027), commercial buildings, and public facilities. Article 11 imposes penalties on building owners who fail to comply.
- **Clean Air Conservation Act (대기환경보전법):** Establishes emission standards for vehicles and empowers the Ministry of Environment to designate low-emission zones in urban areas where high-emission vehicles face access restrictions.

### Subsidy Structure

South Korea's EV subsidy program is among the most generous globally:

- **National Subsidy (2025):** Up to KRW 6.8 million for passenger BEVs, with the amount varying based on vehicle price, range, energy efficiency, and domestic manufacturing. Vehicles priced above KRW 55 million receive reduced subsidies; those above KRW 90 million are ineligible.
- **Local Government Subsidy:** Additional subsidies of KRW 1.0-2.1 million depending on the municipality, bringing the maximum combined subsidy to approximately KRW 8.9 million (approximately USD 6,700).
- **Tax Benefits:** Exemption from individual consumption tax (up to KRW 3 million), education tax, and acquisition tax (up to KRW 1.4 million) for EV purchasers.
- **Commercial Vehicle Incentives:** Enhanced subsidies for electric buses (up to KRW 100 million) and electric trucks (up to KRW 24 million) to accelerate commercial fleet electrification.

### Notable Developments

- **Hyundai-Kia Charging Network Mandate (2024):** The Ministry of Environment required Hyundai Motor Group to establish a public charging network as a condition of its EV subsidy eligibility, addressing consumer concerns about charging accessibility.
- **EV Battery Fire Safety Regulations (2024):** Following a series of EV battery fires in the Cheongna underground parking garage incident (August 2024), the Ministry of Land, Infrastructure and Transport (MOLIT) issued emergency regulations requiring enhanced fire suppression systems in underground parking facilities and mandatory battery management system (BMS) disclosure by manufacturers.
- **Seoul Low-Emission Zone Expansion (2025):** Seoul Metropolitan Government expanded its Grade 5 vehicle access restriction zone to cover all areas within the green belt, effectively banning the oldest diesel vehicles from the metropolitan area and incentivizing EV adoption.

## United States

### Federal Framework

The US EV regulatory landscape is defined by the interplay of federal incentives, emissions standards, and state-level mandates:

- **Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 (IRA, Pub. L. 117-169):** Section 13401 (amending IRC § 30D) provides a federal tax credit of up to $7,500 for new clean vehicles, divided into two components: $3,750 for meeting critical mineral sourcing requirements (a specified percentage must be extracted or processed in the US or a country with a free trade agreement) and $3,750 for meeting battery component manufacturing requirements (a specified percentage must be manufactured or assembled in North America). Income caps: $150,000 (single), $300,000 (joint). Vehicle price caps: $55,000 (sedans), $80,000 (SUVs/trucks/vans). Section 13402 provides a $4,000 credit for used clean vehicles.
- **EPA Tailpipe Emission Standards (2024):** Under Clean Air Act Section 202 (42 U.S.C. § 7521), the EPA finalized multi-pollutant emission standards for light-duty and medium-duty vehicles for model years 2027-2032, projected to result in EVs comprising 56% of new light-duty vehicle sales by 2032. The standards were challenged by state attorneys general and industry groups in multiple courts.
- **NHTSA CAFE Standards:** Under the Energy Policy and Conservation Act (49 U.S.C. § 32902), NHTSA sets Corporate Average Fuel Economy standards. The 2024 final rule established targets of 50.4 mpg for passenger cars and 58.0 mpg for light trucks by model year 2031.
- **National Electric Vehicle Infrastructure (NEVI) Formula Program:** Established under the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law (BIL, Pub. L. 117-58, § 11401), allocating $7.5 billion for EV charging infrastructure. The program requires states to submit EV Infrastructure Deployment Plans and prioritizes build-out along designated Alternative Fuel Corridors (AFCs). Minimum standards include DC fast chargers with at least 150 kW output and 97% uptime reliability.

### State-Level Regulation

- **California Advanced Clean Cars II (ACC II, 2022):** The California Air Resources Board (CARB) adopted regulations mandating that 100% of new passenger car and light truck sales be ZEVs by 2035, with interim targets of 35% by 2026 and 68% by 2030. Adopted by 17 other states under Clean Air Act Section 177 (42 U.S.C. § 7507), which allows states to adopt California's emission standards.
- **California Advanced Clean Trucks (2020):** Requires manufacturers to sell an increasing percentage of zero-emission medium- and heavy-duty vehicles, reaching 40-75% (depending on vehicle class) by 2035.
- **Texas EV Fee (2023):** Texas SB 505 imposed a $400 annual registration fee on EVs and a $200 fee on plug-in hybrids, addressing concerns about road maintenance funding as gas tax revenue declines.
- **Right-to-Charge Laws:** Multiple states, including California (AB 1738), Colorado (HB 22-1218), and Virginia (HB 2282), have enacted laws prohibiting homeowners' associations from restricting EV charger installation.

### Notable Cases

- **Ohio v. EPA (pending, 2025):** Coalition of 25 state attorneys general challenging the EPA's 2024 tailpipe emission standards as exceeding the agency's authority under the Clean Air Act and constituting a de facto EV mandate.
- **Texas v. California (pending):** Texas and other states challenging California's authority to set ZEV mandates that effectively impose nationwide standards through Section 177 state adoption.
- **Tesla v. Louisiana (2024):** Tesla's ongoing challenge to state dealership franchise laws that prohibit direct-to-consumer EV sales, raising questions about the regulatory treatment of EV-only manufacturers versus traditional automakers.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany, as the EU's largest automotive market and home to major automakers (Volkswagen, BMW, Mercedes-Benz), occupies a unique position in EV regulation:

- **Regulation (EU) 2023/851 (CO2 Emission Performance Standards):** Directly applicable in Germany, mandating a 100% reduction in fleet-wide average CO2 emissions for new passenger cars from 2035. Germany played a controversial role in the regulation's adoption, initially blocking it in the Council until securing a last-minute exception allowing vehicles running on carbon-neutral synthetic fuels (e-fuels) to be sold after 2035 -- a provision inserted under Article 14a's review clause to protect German automakers' investments in synthetic fuel technology.
- **Schnellladegesetz (SchnellLG, Fast-Charging Law, 2021):** Mandates the establishment of a nationwide network of at least 1,000 fast-charging hubs by 2030, with each hub providing multiple high-power charging points (150-300 kW). The law authorizes the Federal Ministry for Digital and Transport (BMDV) to award concession contracts through competitive tendering. The Deutschlandnetz (Germany Network) program was launched to implement this mandate, with three lots covering the entire federal highway and urban network.
- **Ladesäulenverordnung (LSV, Charging Infrastructure Ordinance, 2016, amended 2021):** Establishes technical standards for publicly accessible EV charging points, including interoperability requirements (Type 2 connectors for AC, CCS for DC), ad-hoc payment capability (no mandatory membership), transparent pricing display, and reporting obligations to the Bundesnetzagentur.
- **Gebäude-Elektromobilitätsinfrastruktur-Gesetz (GEIG, Building Electromobility Infrastructure Act, 2021):** Transposing EU Directive 2018/844, requires new residential buildings with more than five parking spaces to include cable conduit infrastructure in every space, and new non-residential buildings with more than six parking spaces to install at least one charging point plus conduit in every fifth space.

### Subsidy History and Evolution

Germany's EV subsidy landscape has undergone dramatic changes:

- **Umweltbonus (Environmental Bonus, 2016-2023):** Germany offered purchase subsidies of up to EUR 6,750 for battery electric vehicles (EUR 4,500 federal + EUR 2,250 manufacturer contribution). The program was abruptly terminated on 17 December 2023 following a Federal Constitutional Court ruling (BVerfG, 2 BvF 1/22, 15 November 2023) that struck down the government's reallocation of EUR 60 billion in pandemic recovery funds, triggering a fiscal crisis that necessitated subsidy cuts.
- **Company Car Tax Benefit (§ 6(1) No. 4 EStG):** EVs with a gross list price up to EUR 70,000 benefit from a reduced company car tax rate of 0.25% (compared to 1% for ICE vehicles), making company car electrification a significant policy lever in Germany where over 60% of new car registrations are fleet vehicles.
- **KfW Charging Infrastructure Programs:** The state-owned KfW development bank has administered multiple rounds of charging infrastructure grants, including the 2022-2023 program providing up to EUR 9,600 per charging point for private residential installations.

### Notable Developments

- **Volkswagen Emissions Aftermath and EV Pivot:** Following the Dieselgate scandal (BGH, VI ZR 252/19, 2020), Volkswagen committed over EUR 180 billion to electrification. The legal fallout, including investor lawsuits (OLG Braunschweig, 3 Kap 1/16), accelerated the German automotive industry's EV transition.
- **BVerfG Budget Ruling Impact (2023):** The Constitutional Court's ruling in 2 BvF 1/22 forced the termination of the Umweltbonus, causing a 30% drop in German EV sales in Q1 2024 and demonstrating the vulnerability of EV adoption to fiscal policy disruptions.
- **Bidirectional Charging Regulation (2025):** Germany published draft regulations enabling vehicle-to-grid (V2G) technology, allowing EV owners to sell stored electricity back to the grid, addressing both grid stability and EV ownership economics.

## ECHR / EU Framework

### Convention Articles

- **Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 -- Protection of Property:** ICE vehicle bans and depreciation of existing ICE vehicle value raise property rights questions. The ECtHR's proportionality analysis would consider whether the environmental justification for phasing out ICE vehicles constitutes a legitimate public interest that justifies the interference with property rights.
- **Article 8 -- Right to Respect for Private and Family Life:** Environmental protection has been increasingly recognized as an aspect of Article 8, as established in Lopez Ostra v. Spain (1994, App. No. 16798/90) and more recently in Verein KlimaSeniorinnen Schweiz v. Switzerland (Grand Chamber, 2024, App. No. 53600/20), where the Court recognized for the first time that states have positive obligations under Article 8 to take adequate measures to combat climate change.

### EU Regulatory Framework

The EU's EV regulatory architecture comprises multiple interconnected instruments:

- **Regulation (EU) 2023/851 (CO2 Emission Standards):** Amends Regulation (EU) 2019/631, establishing fleet-wide average emission targets: 93.6 g CO2/km for new cars by 2025 (15% reduction from 2021 baseline); 49.5 g CO2/km by 2030 (55% reduction); and 0 g CO2/km by 2035 (100% reduction). Article 14a mandates a review in 2026 to assess the contribution of carbon-neutral fuels (e-fuels), with a potential implementing act allowing registration of vehicles powered exclusively by such fuels after 2035.
- **Regulation (EU) 2023/1542 (EU Battery Regulation):** Replaces Directive 2006/66/EC, establishing comprehensive lifecycle requirements for batteries, including: mandatory carbon footprint declarations (from February 2025); minimum recycled content thresholds (16% cobalt, 6% lithium, 6% nickel by 2031, increasing to 26% cobalt, 12% lithium, 15% nickel by 2036); due diligence obligations for the battery supply chain; digital battery passports for all EV batteries (from February 2027); and collection and recycling targets.
- **Regulation (EU) 2023/1804 (Alternative Fuels Infrastructure Regulation, AFIR):** Replaces Directive 2014/94/EU, establishing binding targets for EV charging infrastructure deployment across the EU: member states must ensure publicly accessible recharging pools along the TEN-T core network with at least 400 kW capacity every 60 km by 2025, increasing to 600 kW by 2027; publicly accessible hydrogen refueling stations along the TEN-T core network every 200 km by 2030; and transparent, non-discriminatory pricing at all publicly accessible recharging points with ad-hoc payment capability.
- **Directive (EU) 2024/1275 (Energy Performance of Buildings Directive, EPBD Recast):** Requires member states to ensure that new buildings and buildings undergoing major renovations include EV charging pre-cabling, supporting the integration of vehicle-to-building (V2B) energy systems.

### Key Case Law

- **Verein KlimaSeniorinnen Schweiz v. Switzerland (ECtHR Grand Chamber, 2024, App. No. 53600/20):** Landmark ruling establishing that states have positive obligations under ECHR Article 8 to take adequate measures against climate change, providing a human rights basis for policies promoting EV adoption and ICE phase-outs.
- **CJEU, Case C-873/19, Deutsche Umwelthilfe v. Germany (2022):** The CJEU ruled that environmental organizations have standing to challenge member state air quality plans, reinforcing the legal basis for low-emission zones that incentivize EV adoption.
- **German Federal Administrative Court, BVerwG 7 C 26.16 (2018):** Upheld the legality of diesel driving bans in German cities to meet EU air quality limits under Directive 2008/50/EC, providing a judicial precedent for restricting ICE vehicle use in urban areas.
