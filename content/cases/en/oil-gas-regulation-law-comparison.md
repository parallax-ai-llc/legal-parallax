## Summary

Oil and gas regulation encompasses the legal frameworks governing exploration, extraction, refining, transportation, and sale of petroleum products -- a sector that intersects national security, economic development, environmental protection, and increasingly, climate change mitigation policy. South Korea, as a country with minimal domestic petroleum reserves, regulates the sector primarily through the Petroleum and Petroleum Substitute Fuel Business Act (석유 및 석유대체연료 사업법) governing downstream distribution and pricing, and the Overseas Resources Development Business Act (해외자원개발 사업법) encouraging Korean companies to secure upstream interests abroad; the Korea National Oil Corporation (한국석유공사, KNOC) serves as the state oil company responsible for strategic petroleum reserves management and overseas exploration. The United States operates the world's largest oil and gas production industry under a complex federal-state regulatory framework, with the Mineral Leasing Act of 1920 (30 U.S.C. Sections 181-287) governing extraction on federal lands, the Outer Continental Shelf Lands Act (43 U.S.C. Sections 1331-1356b) regulating offshore drilling, and state conservation commissions -- particularly the Texas Railroad Commission -- managing production on state and private lands; the Biden administration's Inflation Reduction Act (2022) simultaneously expanded federal oil and gas leasing (a concession to secure passage) while imposing a first-ever federal methane emissions charge. Germany, which produces negligible domestic oil and gas, regulates the sector through the Federal Mining Act (Bundesberggesetz, BBergG) for extraction licensing, and energy supply through the Energy Industry Act (Energiewirtschaftsgesetz, EnWG); the 2022 energy crisis triggered by the Russia-Ukraine conflict fundamentally reshaped German gas policy, with the construction of LNG import terminals under emergency legislation (LNG-Beschleunigungsgesetz, 2022) and the termination of Nord Stream pipeline imports. At the European level, the EU regulates oil and gas through the Offshore Safety Directive (2013/30/EU) following the Deepwater Horizon disaster, the EU Methane Regulation (Regulation (EU) 2024/1787) imposing methane intensity standards on imported fossil fuels, and the European Green Deal framework that aims to phase out unabated fossil fuel consumption by mid-century; the Energy Charter Treaty (1994), which protected fossil fuel investments through investor-state dispute settlement, faced mass withdrawal by EU member states beginning in 2022 over concerns that its protections conflicted with climate policy.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

As the world's fifth-largest crude oil importer and third-largest LNG importer, South Korea's oil and gas regulation focuses heavily on supply security, strategic reserves, and downstream market regulation rather than domestic upstream production.

**Petroleum and Petroleum Substitute Fuel Business Act (석유 및 석유대체연료 사업법):** This statute governs the entire downstream petroleum value chain including refining, distribution, retail sales, and quality standards. The Korea Petroleum Association reports that four major refining companies -- SK Energy, GS Caltex, S-Oil, and Hyundai Oilbank -- control virtually all domestic refining capacity of approximately 3.3 million barrels per day. The Act regulates market entry licensing, fuel quality specifications, pricing transparency, and the operation of gas stations. The Ministry of Trade, Industry and Energy (산업통상자원부) exercises primary regulatory authority.

**Overseas Resources Development Business Act (해외자원개발 사업법):** This statute provides the legal framework for Korean companies to invest in overseas oil and gas exploration and production. The Act authorizes financial support including exploration subsidies, equity investment through KNOC, and diplomatic support for resource acquisition. Korean overseas resource development expenditure peaked at approximately $5.8 billion in 2012 before declining following unsuccessful investments in several overseas projects.

**Korea National Oil Corporation (한국석유공사, KNOC):** Established in 1979, KNOC serves as South Korea's state oil company with responsibilities including management of the national strategic petroleum reserve (currently approximately 97 million barrels stored in underground salt cavern and above-ground tank facilities at Yeosu, Geoje, and Ulsan), overseas exploration and production, and oil stockpiling coordination.

### Strategic Petroleum Reserve

South Korea maintains oil stocks equivalent to approximately 92 days of net imports, exceeding the International Energy Agency's (IEA) minimum 90-day stockholding obligation. The strategic reserve is managed jointly by KNOC (government-held stocks) and the private sector (industry-held stocks) under the Petroleum Business Act and the Petroleum Stockpiling Act (석유비축법).

### Energy Transition and Petroleum

The 2050 Carbon Neutrality Act requires the progressive decarbonization of the energy sector, creating tension with continued reliance on petroleum imports. The 10th Basic Plan for Long-term Electricity Supply and Demand (제10차 전력수급기본계획, 2023) reduced the projected role of LNG in the power mix while increasing nuclear and renewable shares, reflecting the Yoon administration's energy policy reorientation.

### Pricing and Market Regulation

South Korea deregulated retail petroleum prices in 1997 following IMF conditionality during the Asian financial crisis. However, the government retains the authority to impose temporary fuel tax reductions during periods of high international oil prices. In October 2022, the government reduced fuel taxes by 37% (the statutory maximum) in response to energy price surges following the Russia-Ukraine conflict. The Fair Trade Commission (공정거래위원회) monitors oil pricing for anti-competitive behavior, and the Opinet price transparency system (operated by KNOC) provides real-time fuel price information to consumers.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States operates the world's largest oil and gas production industry, producing approximately 13 million barrels per day of crude oil and approximately 103 billion cubic feet per day of natural gas as of 2024.

**Federal Lands and Offshore:**
- **Mineral Leasing Act of 1920 (30 U.S.C. Sections 181-287):** Governs oil, gas, coal, and other mineral leasing on federal public domain lands, managed by the Bureau of Land Management (BLM). Leases are granted through competitive auctions with a minimum bid of $10 per acre (increased from $2 per acre by the IRA in 2022), a 16.67% royalty rate (increased from 12.5% by the IRA), and ten-year primary terms.
- **Outer Continental Shelf Lands Act (43 U.S.C. Sections 1331-1356b):** Authorizes leasing for oil and gas exploration on the outer continental shelf, administered by the Bureau of Ocean Energy Management (BOEM). Following the 2010 Deepwater Horizon disaster, the Bureau of Safety and Environmental Enforcement (BSEE) was created to oversee offshore safety.

**State Regulation:** On state and private lands (which account for approximately 76% of U.S. oil production), state agencies exercise primary regulatory authority. The Texas Railroad Commission -- the oldest state oil and gas regulatory body, established in 1891 -- regulates well spacing, production allowances, and plugging requirements for Texas, which produces approximately 43% of all U.S. crude oil.

### Environmental Regulation

**National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA):** Requires environmental impact statements for major federal actions, including federal oil and gas lease sales. The Biden administration's CEQ NEPA Phase 2 regulations (2024) required consideration of greenhouse gas emissions and climate change impacts in environmental reviews, though these regulations face ongoing legal challenges.

**Clean Air Act -- Methane Regulation:** The EPA finalized comprehensive methane emissions standards for the oil and gas sector in December 2023, requiring monitoring, leak detection, and repair of methane emissions from wells, pipelines, and processing facilities. The IRA's Waste Emissions Charge (26 U.S.C. Section 136) imposes a fee of $900 per metric ton on methane emissions exceeding specified thresholds from facilities reporting under the Greenhouse Gas Reporting Program, rising to $1,500 per metric ton by 2026.

**Clean Water Act -- Hydraulic Fracturing:** The Energy Policy Act of 2005 (the so-called "Halliburton loophole") exempted hydraulic fracturing fluids from the Safe Drinking Water Act's underground injection control program. Fracking-related water contamination remains regulated under the Clean Water Act's discharge permit system and state groundwater protection laws.

### Inflation Reduction Act -- Oil and Gas Provisions

The IRA contained significant oil and gas provisions alongside its clean energy investments:
- Mandated federal oil and gas lease sales on at least 2 million acres of public land and 60 million acres of offshore territory annually as a prerequisite for renewable energy lease sales
- Increased onshore and offshore royalty rates and minimum bids
- Imposed the first federal methane emissions charge
- Reinstated Gulf of Mexico Lease Sale 257 (previously vacated by a federal court)

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany produces less than 2% of its oil and approximately 5% of its gas consumption domestically, making it overwhelmingly dependent on imports -- a vulnerability exposed dramatically by the 2022 energy crisis.

**Federal Mining Act (Bundesberggesetz, BBergG):** Governs the exploration and extraction of underground mineral resources including oil and gas. Exploration and extraction licenses are granted by state mining authorities (Bergämter). The Act requires environmental impact assessments, rehabilitation of extraction sites, and compliance with water protection regulations.

**Energy Industry Act (Energiewirtschaftsgesetz, EnWG):** Regulates the supply of electricity and gas, including network access, tariff regulation, and consumer protection. The Federal Network Agency (Bundesnetzagentur) serves as the primary regulatory authority for gas network access and pipeline operations.

### 2022 Energy Crisis and Policy Response

Germany's pre-2022 energy strategy relied heavily on Russian pipeline gas, which supplied approximately 55% of German gas imports. The Russia-Ukraine conflict and subsequent sanctions triggered a fundamental restructuring:

**LNG Acceleration Act (LNG-Beschleunigungsgesetz, May 2022):** Enacted under emergency procedures, this law drastically shortened the permitting timeline for floating storage and regasification units (FSRUs) and onshore LNG terminals from potentially years to months. Germany commissioned its first FSRU at Wilhelmshaven in December 2022 -- just seven months after the law's passage -- and had five operational LNG terminals by end of 2023.

**Gas Storage Act (Gasspeichergesetz, April 2022):** Amended the EnWG to mandate minimum gas storage levels: 80% by October 1, 90% by November 1, and 40% by February 1 of each year. The law also authorized the Federal Network Agency to assume operational control of critical gas infrastructure.

**Nord Stream Aftermath:** The destruction of the Nord Stream 1 and 2 pipelines in September 2022 eliminated the physical infrastructure for Russian gas imports. Germany's gas import portfolio diversified rapidly toward Norwegian pipeline gas, Dutch pipeline gas, and LNG from the United States, Qatar, and other suppliers.

### Upstream Activity

Germany's domestic oil production (concentrated in Schleswig-Holstein and Lower Saxony) amounts to approximately 37,000 barrels per day. Domestic natural gas production (primarily from the Elbe-Weser and North German Basin fields) has declined from approximately 20 billion cubic meters in 2000 to under 5 billion cubic meters by 2024. The decline reflects both geological depletion and restrictive regulatory conditions, including the prohibition on hydraulic fracturing for unconventional gas extraction under the Federal Water Resources Act (Wasserhaushaltsgesetz) amendment of 2016.

### Phase-Out Commitments

While Germany committed to coal phase-out by 2038 (potentially accelerated to 2030 in some states), the role of natural gas as a "bridge fuel" in the energy transition remains contested. The amended KSG emissions budgets assume continued but declining gas use in the power sector, with gas turbines serving as flexible backup for variable renewable generation.

## ECHR / EU

### Offshore Safety Directive (2013/30/EU)

Adopted in response to the 2010 Deepwater Horizon disaster in the Gulf of Mexico, this directive establishes minimum safety requirements for offshore oil and gas operations in EU waters.

**Key Requirements:**
- Operators must prepare a Major Hazard Report before commencing operations
- Member states must establish independent competent authorities for offshore safety
- Environmental impact assessments required for all offshore operations
- Emergency response plans mandated with cross-border coordination mechanisms
- Transparency requirements including public reporting of safety performance indicators

### EU Methane Regulation (Regulation (EU) 2024/1787)

Adopted in May 2024, this regulation represents the first EU legislation directly targeting methane emissions from the energy sector.

**Domestic Requirements:**
- Mandatory leak detection and repair programs for oil, gas, and coal operations
- Prohibition on routine venting and flaring (with limited exceptions)
- Quantification and reporting of methane emissions from all fossil fuel operations

**Import Standards (effective 2030):**
- Methane intensity limits for imported fossil fuels
- Importers must provide methane performance data for upstream production
- The Commission will establish a global methane monitoring tool
- Non-compliant imports may face penalties or exclusion

### Energy Charter Treaty Withdrawal

The Energy Charter Treaty (ECT, 1994) provided investment protection for energy sector investors, including investor-state dispute settlement (ISDS) mechanisms. Several EU member states -- beginning with Italy (2016), followed by France, Germany, Spain, Netherlands, and others in 2022-2023 -- withdrew from the ECT, arguing that its protections for fossil fuel investments were incompatible with climate policy and the European Green Deal. The withdrawals will take effect after the ECT's "sunset clause" period of twenty years.

### European Green Deal and Fossil Fuel Phase-Out

The European Green Deal Communication (COM(2019) 640) and subsequent legislation envision the progressive phase-out of unabated fossil fuel consumption. The Fit for 55 package, including the strengthened EU ETS and carbon border adjustment mechanism (CBAM), creates economic incentives for the transition away from oil and gas. However, the EU has not established a binding timeline for oil and gas production or consumption phase-out, and several member states continue to authorize new hydrocarbon exploration.

### ECHR Climate Jurisprudence

The KlimaSeniorinnen v. Switzerland decision (2024) established positive obligations under Article 8 ECHR to address climate change, which has indirect implications for oil and gas regulation. States that fail to adequately regulate fossil fuel production or consumption contributing to climate change may face Convention liability, though the Court emphasized the wide margin of appreciation in policy choice.

## Comparative Analysis

### Producer vs. Consumer Jurisdictions

The most fundamental comparative distinction is between producer (United States) and consumer (South Korea, Germany) jurisdictions. The United States regulates a massive domestic industry with competing interests in production, employment, and environmental protection. South Korea and Germany focus primarily on supply security, import diversification, and downstream regulation. The EU encompasses both producer (Norway through EEA; Denmark, Romania) and consumer states.

### Energy Security vs. Climate

All jurisdictions face the tension between energy security (ensuring reliable, affordable supply) and climate mitigation (reducing fossil fuel consumption). The 2022 energy crisis demonstrated this tension acutely: Germany's emergency LNG infrastructure construction prioritized security over climate timelines; the U.S. IRA mandated continued fossil fuel leasing as the price of clean energy investment; South Korea reconsidered its nuclear and gas policies. The EU Methane Regulation's import standards represent an attempt to address both concerns simultaneously.

### Subsidy Reform

All four jurisdictions maintain fossil fuel subsidies of varying types and magnitudes. The OECD estimates global fossil fuel subsidies at over $1 trillion annually. South Korea's temporary fuel tax reductions, the U.S. intangible drilling cost deduction (26 U.S.C. Section 263), and Germany's energy-intensive industry exemptions from renewable energy surcharges all constitute forms of fossil fuel support that climate policy increasingly challenges.

### Strategic Reserves

IEA member states (including South Korea, the United States, and Germany) are obligated to maintain 90 days of net oil import equivalent in strategic reserves. The coordinated IEA release of 180 million barrels in 2022 in response to the energy crisis demonstrated the reserves system's function as a collective insurance mechanism.
