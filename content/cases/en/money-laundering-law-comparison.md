## Summary

Money laundering -- the process of concealing the origins of illegally obtained money to make it appear legitimate -- is a cornerstone offense in the global fight against financial crime. Every major jurisdiction criminalizes money laundering, yet the scope of predicate offenses, penalties, reporting obligations, and enforcement intensity vary significantly across legal systems. South Korea's Criminal Proceeds Regulation Act (범죄수익은닉규제법) punishes the concealment or disguise of criminal proceeds with up to five years imprisonment, with enhanced penalties for narcotics-related laundering. The United States employs a sweeping statutory framework anchored by the Bank Secrecy Act (BSA) and 18 U.S.C. section 1956, which criminalizes four distinct types of money laundering and carries penalties of up to 20 years imprisonment and fines of $500,000 or twice the value of the funds involved. Germany's reformed section 261 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB) adopted an all-crimes approach in 2021, abandoning its former enumeration of predicate offenses, with penalties of up to five years imprisonment or up to ten years in aggravated cases. At the supranational level, the European Union's Sixth Anti-Money Laundering Directive (AMLD6, Directive 2024/1640) harmonizes Member State criminal laws and establishes the Anti-Money Laundering Authority (AMLA), which commenced supervisory operations in July 2025. Overarching all national frameworks are the Financial Action Task Force (FATF) 40 Recommendations, which set the global standard for AML/CFT compliance and serve as the benchmark against which every country's anti-money laundering regime is evaluated.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's anti-money laundering regime rests on two principal statutes:

- **Criminal Proceeds Concealment Regulation Act (범죄수익은닉의 규제 및 처벌 등에 관한 법률):** The primary money laundering offense, criminalizing the concealment, disguise, or receipt of proceeds derived from predicate offenses.
- **Act on Reporting and Using Specified Financial Transaction Information (특정 금융거래정보의 보고 및 이용 등에 관한 법률):** Establishes the Korea Financial Intelligence Unit (KoFIU) and imposes reporting obligations on financial institutions, including suspicious transaction reports (STRs) and currency transaction reports (CTRs).

The Narcotics Control Act (마약류 관리에 관한 법률) provides separate and enhanced money laundering provisions for drug-related proceeds.

### Elements of the Offense

Under the Criminal Proceeds Concealment Regulation Act, money laundering is established by proving:

1. **Predicate Offense:** The funds must derive from a designated criminal offense. South Korea maintains a list-based approach to predicate offenses rather than an all-crimes model.
2. **Concealment or Disguise (은닉 또는 가장):** The accused must have concealed or disguised the nature, location, source, acquisition, or ownership of criminal proceeds.
3. **Knowledge (인식):** The accused must have known, or been in a position to know, that the property constituted criminal proceeds.

### Penalties

| Offense Type | Maximum Imprisonment | Maximum Fine |
|-------------|---------------------|-------------|
| Money laundering (general) | 5 years | 30 million won |
| Preparation or conspiracy to launder | 2 years | 10 million won |
| Narcotics-related laundering | 7 years | 30 million won (or both) |

### FATF Compliance

South Korea is a FATF member and was evaluated in its 2020 Mutual Evaluation Report. As of the most recent follow-up assessment, Korea is rated compliant on 13 Recommendations and largely compliant on 20, with 7 rated partially compliant. The country is substantially effective in 5 of 11 effectiveness areas but has been urged to strengthen its framework regarding tax crimes, designated non-financial businesses and professions (DNFBPs), and politically exposed persons (PEPs).

### Recent Enforcement

**Bithumb AML Suspension (2026):** In March 2026, South Korea's Financial Services Commission imposed a six-month partial business suspension on cryptocurrency exchange Bithumb for repeated and systemic failures in anti-money laundering controls and customer verification. The action reflected intensified regulatory scrutiny of the virtual asset sector following KoFIU's announcement of 2026 policy priorities focused on bolstering AML in the cryptocurrency industry.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States maintains one of the world's most expansive and aggressively enforced anti-money laundering regimes, built on several interlocking statutory pillars:

- **Bank Secrecy Act (BSA, 31 U.S.C. sections 5311-5332):** Enacted in 1970, the BSA requires financial institutions to maintain records and file reports that are useful in detecting and preventing money laundering. The Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN), a bureau of the Department of the Treasury, administers the BSA.
- **18 U.S.C. section 1956 (Laundering of Monetary Instruments):** The principal federal money laundering statute, enacted as part of the Money Laundering Control Act of 1986.
- **18 U.S.C. section 1957 (Engaging in Monetary Transactions in Property Derived from Specified Unlawful Activity):** A companion statute prohibiting monetary transactions exceeding $10,000 involving criminally derived property.
- **USA PATRIOT Act (2001):** Title III substantially expanded BSA requirements, imposing enhanced due diligence on correspondent banking and private banking accounts, and authorizing the Treasury to designate jurisdictions, institutions, or transactions as "primary money laundering concerns."
- **Anti-Money Laundering Act of 2020 (AMLA 2020):** The most comprehensive overhaul of U.S. AML law since the PATRIOT Act, establishing beneficial ownership reporting requirements through the Corporate Transparency Act, expanding FinCEN's subpoena authority, and enhancing whistleblower protections.

### Elements of the Offense

Section 1956 criminalizes four distinct types of money laundering:

1. **Promotional Laundering (section 1956(a)(1)(A)(i)):** Conducting a financial transaction with proceeds of specified unlawful activity with intent to promote the carrying on of that activity.
2. **Concealment Laundering (section 1956(a)(1)(B)(i)):** Conducting a financial transaction designed to conceal or disguise the nature, location, source, ownership, or control of proceeds of specified unlawful activity.
3. **Structuring Laundering (section 1956(a)(1)(B)(ii)):** Conducting a financial transaction designed to avoid a transaction reporting requirement under state or federal law.
4. **Tax Evasion Laundering (section 1956(a)(1)(A)(ii)):** Conducting a financial transaction with proceeds of specified unlawful activity with intent to engage in tax evasion or tax fraud.

Each type may be prosecuted under domestic (section 1956(a)(1)), international (section 1956(a)(2)), or sting (section 1956(a)(3)) jurisdictional provisions.

### Penalties

| Offense | Maximum Imprisonment | Maximum Fine |
|---------|---------------------|-------------|
| Section 1956 violation | 20 years | $500,000 or twice the value of the funds, whichever is greater |
| Section 1957 violation | 10 years | Twice the amount of the criminally derived property |
| BSA willful violation (individual) | 5 years | $250,000 |
| BSA willful violation (structuring) | 10 years | $500,000 |

Civil penalties, asset forfeiture, and restitution orders may also be imposed.

### Landmark Cases

**United States v. Approximately $4.5 Billion in Cryptocurrency -- Bitfinex Hack (2022-2023):** Ilya Lichtenstein and Heather Morgan were arrested in February 2022 for conspiring to launder approximately 120,000 Bitcoin stolen in the 2016 Bitfinex hack. The seizure, valued at approximately $3.6 billion at the time, was the largest single financial seizure in Department of Justice history. Lichtenstein pleaded guilty in August 2023.

**Samourai Wallet (2024-2025):** The co-founders of cryptocurrency mixing service Samourai Wallet were convicted for conspiracy to operate an unlicensed money transmitting business. The service allegedly processed over $2 billion in unlawful transactions, facilitating non-traceable private cryptocurrency transfers. The co-founders were sentenced to four- and five-year prison terms following their July 2025 guilty pleas.

### Recent Enforcement Trends

In late December 2025, FinCEN announced a "first-of-its-kind, data-driven enforcement operation" targeting more than 100 money services businesses along the Southwest U.S. border, resulting in six notices of investigation, dozens of examination referrals to the IRS, and more than 50 compliance outreach letters. In February 2026, FinCEN actions signaled an escalation of AML enforcement priorities under the current administration.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Money laundering (Geldwaesche) is criminalized under section 261 of the German Criminal Code (Strafgesetzbuch, StGB). The offense was substantially restructured by the Act to Improve Criminal Law Combating Money Laundering (Gesetz zur Verbesserung der strafrechtlichen Bekaempfung der Geldwaesche), which entered into force on March 18, 2021.

Germany's administrative AML framework is governed by the Money Laundering Act (Geldwaeschegesetz, GwG), which transposes EU Anti-Money Laundering Directives into German law and imposes compliance obligations on financial institutions, designated non-financial businesses and professions, and other obliged entities.

### Key 2021 Reform: The All-Crimes Approach

The 2021 reform to section 261 StGB introduced two fundamental changes:

1. **All-Crimes Approach:** Germany abandoned the former enumeration principle (Vortatenkatalog), which required the predicate offense to fall within a specific statutory list. Under the reformed provision, any criminal offense (Straftat) -- not merely those previously enumerated -- may serve as a predicate to money laundering. Tax offenses (Steuerstraftaten) are explicitly included.
2. **Elimination of Knowledge Requirement for Predicate Offense:** The perpetrator need no longer have a concrete idea of what the preceding predicate offense was. It is sufficient that the perpetrator acts recklessly (leichtfertig) with regard to the criminal origin of the asset.

### Elements of the Offense

Under reformed section 261 StGB, money laundering is committed by any person who:

1. Conceals an object originating from an unlawful act, disguises its origin, or obstructs or endangers the investigation of the origin, the finding, the forfeiture, the confiscation, or the seizure of such an object; or
2. Procures, keeps, or uses for themselves or a third party an object originating from an unlawful act committed by another person.

### Penalties

| Offense Type | Penalty Range |
|-------------|--------------|
| Basic offense (section 261(1)) | Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine |
| Aggravated case (section 261(4)) -- commercial or gang activity | 6 months to 10 years imprisonment |
| Reckless laundering (section 261(6)) | Up to 2 years imprisonment or fine |

### Financial Crime Combating Act (FKBG)

The German Federal Ministry of Finance has published a draft Financial Crime Combating Act (Finanzkriminalitaetsbekaempfungsgesetz, FKBG) establishing a new Federal Office for Combating Financial Crime (Bundesamt zur Bekaempfung von Finanzkriminalitaet, BBF). This authority will centralize AML intelligence, enforcement, and supervision functions currently distributed across multiple federal and state agencies.

## European Framework

### Evolution of EU Anti-Money Laundering Directives

The European Union has developed its AML framework through successive directives:

| Directive | Year | Key Innovation |
|-----------|------|---------------|
| 1st AMLD (91/308/EEC) | 1991 | First EU-wide AML obligations for credit and financial institutions |
| 2nd AMLD (2001/97/EC) | 2001 | Extended obligations to lawyers, accountants, real estate agents, and casinos |
| 3rd AMLD (2005/60/EC) | 2005 | Introduced risk-based approach and customer due diligence requirements |
| 4th AMLD (2015/849) | 2015 | Central beneficial ownership registers; enhanced PEP screening |
| 5th AMLD (2018/843) | 2018 | Public access to beneficial ownership registers; virtual currency providers included |
| 6th AMLD (2024/1640) | 2024 | Harmonized criminal law; AMLA establishment; comprehensive reform package |

### Sixth Anti-Money Laundering Directive (AMLD6)

AMLD6, adopted as Directive 2024/1640, represents the most comprehensive reform of the EU's AML/CFT framework. It replaces the Fourth AML Directive (as amended by the Fifth) and forms part of a broader legislative package that includes:

- **AML Regulation (AMLR):** A directly applicable EU regulation establishing uniform AML/CFT rules, replacing the directive-based approach for key provisions.
- **Anti-Money Laundering Authority (AMLA):** A new EU-level authority with direct and indirect supervisory powers over high-risk obliged entities in the financial sector, operational from July 1, 2025.
- **Recast Transfer of Funds Regulation:** Updated rules on information accompanying transfers of funds and crypto-assets.

### Key AMLD6 Provisions

1. **Harmonized Predicate Offenses:** AMLD6 establishes a minimum list of 22 predicate offenses, including tax crimes, cybercrime, and environmental crime.
2. **Enhanced Beneficial Ownership Registers:** Member States must ensure registers are accurate, adequate, and up-to-date, with provisions for verification by competent authorities. Related transposition deadline: July 10, 2026.
3. **Expanded Access:** Journalists, civil society organizations, and persons with a legitimate interest may access beneficial ownership registers.
4. **Strengthened FIU Cooperation:** National Financial Intelligence Units must cooperate more extensively, share data freely, and participate in joint analyses.
5. **Full Transposition Deadline:** Member States must transpose AMLD6 by July 10, 2027.

### Recent EU Enforcement

**De Volksbank Fine (2025):** In February 2025, the Dutch Central Bank (DNB) imposed a 2.5 million euro fine on De Volksbank N.V. for serious shortcomings in its AML compliance systems between 2018 and 2020, including failure to adequately assess money laundering and terrorist financing risks across its customer base.

## FATF Standards

### The 40 Recommendations

The Financial Action Task Force (FATF), established in 1989 by the G7, sets the global standard for combating money laundering, terrorist financing, and proliferation financing through its 40 Recommendations. These Recommendations provide a comprehensive framework that countries are expected to implement, covering:

- **AML/CFT Policies and Coordination (Recommendations 1-2):** Risk-based approach and national cooperation.
- **Money Laundering and Confiscation (Recommendations 3-4):** Criminalization of money laundering and provisional measures and confiscation.
- **Terrorist Financing and Proliferation (Recommendations 5-8):** Criminalization of terrorist financing and targeted financial sanctions.
- **Preventive Measures (Recommendations 9-23):** Customer due diligence, record-keeping, suspicious transaction reporting, and regulation of DNFBPs.
- **Transparency and Beneficial Ownership (Recommendations 24-25):** Legal persons and arrangements transparency.
- **Powers and Responsibilities of Competent Authorities (Recommendations 26-35):** FIU functions, law enforcement powers, and international cooperation.
- **International Cooperation (Recommendations 36-40):** Mutual legal assistance, extradition, and information exchange.

### 2025 Updates

In February 2025, the FATF Plenary approved amendments to Recommendation 1 and its Interpretive Note, along with corresponding amendments to Interpretive Notes to Recommendations 1, 10, and 15. These changes reinforce the expectation that AML/CFT/CPF controls must be implemented through a proportionate, risk-based approach and encourage countries to promote financial inclusion.

The FATF also published an updated Guidance on Financial Inclusion and AML/TF Measures, supporting countries in bringing more people into the formal financial sector through risk-proportionate approaches.

### Virtual Assets

The FATF's sixth targeted update on virtual assets and virtual asset service providers (VASPs), published in 2025, assesses global compliance with Recommendation 15 and its Interpretive Note. The FATF continues to press jurisdictions to implement the "travel rule" for virtual asset transfers and to register or license VASPs.

### Mutual Evaluation Process

The FATF conducts periodic mutual evaluations of member countries to assess both technical compliance (whether laws and regulations meet FATF standards) and effectiveness (whether AML/CFT systems produce the intended outcomes). Countries rated poorly face increased monitoring ("grey listing") or, in extreme cases, a call for countermeasures ("black listing").

As of October 2025, the FATF maintains lists of high-risk jurisdictions subject to a call for action and jurisdictions under increased monitoring, reflecting ongoing global concerns about AML/CFT deficiencies.

## Comparative Analysis

| Feature | South Korea | United States | Germany | EU (AMLD6) |
|---------|-------------|---------------|---------|------------|
| **Primary Statute** | Criminal Proceeds Concealment Regulation Act | 18 U.S.C. section 1956; BSA | Section 261 StGB; GwG | Directive 2024/1640 |
| **Predicate Offense Approach** | List-based | Specified unlawful activities (broad list) | All-crimes (since 2021) | Minimum 22 harmonized offenses |
| **Maximum Imprisonment** | 5 years (7 for narcotics) | 20 years | 5 years (10 aggravated) | Set by Member States (minimum 4 years for serious cases) |
| **Maximum Fine** | 30 million won (~$22,000) | $500,000 or 2x value | Varies by case | Set by Member States |
| **FIU** | KoFIU | FinCEN | FIU Germany (Zentralstelle fuer Finanztransaktionsuntersuchungen) | National FIUs coordinated by AMLA |
| **Beneficial Ownership Register** | Limited | Corporate Transparency Act (2024) | Transparency Register (Transparenzregister) | Mandatory, public access with safeguards |
| **Whistleblower Protections** | Limited | Extensive (AMLA 2020 enhancements) | EU Whistleblower Directive transposed | Directive 2019/1937 |

| **FATF Rating (Technical Compliance)** | 13 Compliant, 20 Largely Compliant | Largely compliant overall | Largely compliant overall | N/A (supranational) |
| **Self-Laundering** | Not explicitly criminalized | Criminalized | Criminalized (since 2021 reform) | Required to be criminalized |
| **Corporate Criminal Liability** | Limited | Extensive | Administrative (Ordnungswidrigkeitengesetz) | Required under AMLD6 |

## Career Timeline

The following timeline traces the development of the global anti-money laundering framework:

- **1970:** United States enacts the Bank Secrecy Act, the world's first comprehensive AML statute.
- **1986:** United States enacts the Money Laundering Control Act, creating 18 U.S.C. sections 1956 and 1957 as standalone federal money laundering offenses.
- **1989:** G7 establishes the Financial Action Task Force (FATF) at the Paris Summit.
- **1990:** FATF issues its initial 40 Recommendations on money laundering.
- **1991:** European Economic Community adopts the First Anti-Money Laundering Directive (91/308/EEC).
- **1992:** Germany introduces section 261 StGB, criminalizing money laundering with an enumerated list of predicate offenses.
- **2001:** South Korea enacts the Act on Reporting and Using Specified Financial Transaction Information and establishes KoFIU. United States enacts the USA PATRIOT Act, substantially expanding BSA obligations. EU adopts the Second AMLD.
- **2003:** FATF revises its Recommendations, expanding from 40 to 40+9 (adding Special Recommendations on terrorist financing).
- **2005:** EU adopts the Third AMLD, introducing the risk-based approach.
- **2012:** FATF consolidates and updates its Recommendations into the current 40 Recommendations framework.
- **2013:** South Korea enacts the Criminal Proceeds Concealment Regulation Act (범죄수익은닉규제법).
- **2015:** EU adopts the Fourth AMLD, mandating beneficial ownership registers.
- **2018:** EU adopts the Fifth AMLD, extending AML obligations to virtual currency exchanges and custodian wallet providers.
- **2020:** FATF publishes its Mutual Evaluation Report on South Korea. United States enacts the Anti-Money Laundering Act of 2020 and the Corporate Transparency Act.
- **2021:** Germany fundamentally reforms section 261 StGB, adopting the all-crimes approach and criminalizing self-laundering. South Korea amends its AML framework to regulate virtual asset service providers.
- **2024:** EU adopts the Sixth AMLD (Directive 2024/1640), the AML Regulation, and establishes the Anti-Money Laundering Authority (AMLA). United States begins enforcing Corporate Transparency Act beneficial ownership reporting requirements.
- **2025:** AMLA commences supervisory operations (July 1, 2025). FATF updates Recommendations 1, 10, and 15 to reinforce risk-based approach and financial inclusion. FinCEN launches data-driven enforcement operation targeting Southwest border money services businesses. Germany advances draft Financial Crime Combating Act (FKBG). Global AML fines total $3.8 billion.
- **2026:** South Korea's Financial Services Commission suspends Bithumb for AML failures (March 2026). EU beneficial ownership register provisions transposition deadline (July 10, 2026). KoFIU announces strengthened AML policy priorities for virtual asset industry.
