## Summary

Bankruptcy -- the legal process by which insolvent debtors obtain relief from debts and creditors receive equitable distribution of available assets -- is a cornerstone of modern market economies, balancing the fresh-start principle for debtors against the legitimate expectations of creditors while maintaining the stability of the broader financial system. South Korea operates a unified insolvency framework under the Debtor Rehabilitation and Bankruptcy Act (채무자 회생 및 파산에 관한 법률, DRBA), enacted in 2006 by consolidating four prior statutes, providing three principal procedures -- rehabilitation (회생), bankruptcy liquidation (파산), and individual debtor rehabilitation (개인회생) -- with the courts playing an active supervisory role and significant government influence through the Korea Deposit Insurance Corporation and the Financial Services Commission in financial institution insolvencies. The United States administers bankruptcy under Title 11 of the United States Code (Bankruptcy Code), offering six chapters of relief including Chapter 7 (liquidation), Chapter 11 (reorganization), and Chapter 13 (individual adjustment of debts), with the debtor-in-possession model as a distinctive feature of Chapter 11 and the automatic stay (Section 362) providing immediate protection upon filing, as refined through major reforms including the Bankruptcy Abuse Prevention and Consumer Protection Act (BAPCPA, 2005) and the Small Business Reorganization Act (SBRA, 2019). Germany regulates insolvency through the Insolvenzordnung (InsO), enacted in 1999 to replace the separate Western (Konkursordnung) and Eastern (Gesamtvollstreckungsordnung) German insolvency laws, providing a unified procedure that may result in either reorganization through an insolvency plan (Insolvenzplan) or liquidation, with the distinctive feature of a mandatory court-appointed insolvency administrator (Insolvenzverwalter) and the 2021 introduction of preventive restructuring under the Stabilisierungs- und Restrukturierungsrahmengesetz (StaRUG). At the EU level, the Regulation on Insolvency Proceedings (2015/848) coordinates cross-border insolvency within the EU through the COMI (center of main interests) principle, the Restructuring Directive (2019/1023) harmonizes preventive restructuring frameworks across member states, and the ECHR has recognized creditor rights as property rights protected under Protocol 1, Article 1.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's modern insolvency system was consolidated in the Debtor Rehabilitation and Bankruptcy Act (DRBA), which unified the Bankruptcy Act, Company Reorganization Act, Composition Act, and Individual Debtor Rehabilitation Act into a single statute effective April 1, 2006.

### Rehabilitation (회생)

The rehabilitation procedure is the primary corporate reorganization mechanism. Any debtor that is or is likely to become unable to pay debts as they fall due may file a rehabilitation petition. Key features include:

**Court-Appointed Administrator (관리인):** Unlike the American debtor-in-possession model, Korean rehabilitation proceedings are managed by a court-appointed administrator. In practice, the existing management often serves as administrator, but the court retains discretion to appoint an outside professional.

**Rehabilitation Plan:** The administrator must submit a rehabilitation plan within a court-specified period (typically four months, extendable). The plan must be approved by creditors voting in classes (secured creditors, unsecured creditors, shareholders) and confirmed by the court, which examines whether the plan is feasible, fair, and equitable.

**Absolute Priority Rule:** Korean law applies the absolute priority rule (절대우선의 원칙), requiring that senior creditors be paid in full before junior creditors receive any distribution, unless the junior class consents to different treatment.

### Significant Rehabilitation Cases

**Hanjin Shipping (2016):** The bankruptcy of Hanjin Shipping, then the world's seventh-largest container shipping line, was one of Korea's largest insolvency cases. The company filed for rehabilitation on August 31, 2016, with debts of approximately 6.1 trillion KRW. The case ultimately converted to liquidation in February 2017 after the rehabilitation plan failed, highlighting challenges in maritime insolvency and cross-border asset recovery.

**Ssangyong Motor Company (2009):** Ssangyong Motor entered rehabilitation proceedings in 2009 following the withdrawal of its Chinese parent SAIC Motor. The case involved contentious labor disputes, with approximately 2,600 workers laid off during restructuring. Ssangyong emerged from rehabilitation in 2015 after acquisition by Mahindra & Mahindra.

### Individual Debtor Rehabilitation (개인회생)

Individuals with regular income and debts below specified thresholds (currently 1 billion KRW for secured debt and 500 million KRW for unsecured debt) may file for individual rehabilitation. The debtor proposes a repayment plan of three to five years. Upon completion, remaining debts are discharged. The DRBA was amended in 2024 to increase accessibility and streamline the individual rehabilitation process.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States Bankruptcy Code (Title 11 U.S.C.), enacted in 1978 and substantially amended by BAPCPA in 2005, provides the world's most debtor-friendly insolvency system.

### Chapter 7 -- Liquidation

Chapter 7 provides for the appointment of a trustee who liquidates the debtor's non-exempt assets and distributes proceeds to creditors. Individual debtors receive a discharge of most debts upon completion. BAPCPA introduced a "means test" (Section 707(b)) requiring above-median-income filers to demonstrate inability to fund a Chapter 13 plan, diverting higher-income debtors from Chapter 7 liquidation to Chapter 13 repayment plans.

### Chapter 11 -- Reorganization

Chapter 11 permits business debtors to continue operating while reorganizing debts through a plan of reorganization:

**Debtor-in-Possession:** The debtor retains control of business operations absent appointment of a trustee for cause (Section 1104). This feature distinguishes American reorganization from most other systems and incentivizes early filing.

**Automatic Stay (Section 362):** Filing automatically stays all collection actions, lawsuits, foreclosures, and liens against the debtor and estate property. The automatic stay is one of the most powerful features of American bankruptcy law.

**Plan Confirmation:** The debtor has an exclusive period (typically 120 days, extendable) to propose a plan. Creditors vote by class, and the court confirms the plan if it meets statutory requirements including feasibility, good faith, and the "best interest of creditors" test. Section 1129(b) permits "cramdown" confirmation over objecting classes if the plan is fair and equitable.

### Subchapter V -- Small Business

The Small Business Reorganization Act of 2019 added Subchapter V to Chapter 11, providing a streamlined, faster, and less expensive reorganization process for small businesses with debts below $7.5 million (threshold increased temporarily during COVID-19 and made permanent by the Bankruptcy Threshold Adjustment and Technical Corrections Act of 2022).

### Chapter 15 -- Cross-Border Insolvency

Chapter 15 implements the UNCITRAL Model Law on Cross-Border Insolvency, providing mechanisms for recognition of foreign insolvency proceedings, cooperation between U.S. and foreign courts, and access for foreign representatives to U.S. courts.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's insolvency law underwent fundamental reform with the enactment of the Insolvenzordnung (InsO) in 1999, supplemented by the StaRUG in 2021.

### Insolvenzordnung (InsO)

The InsO provides a unified insolvency procedure that may result in either liquidation or reorganization:

**Insolvency Grounds:** Insolvency proceedings may be opened upon illiquidity (Zahlungsunfähigkeit, Section 17), imminent illiquidity (drohende Zahlungsunfähigkeit, Section 18), or over-indebtedness (Überschuldung, Section 19, applicable to legal entities only).

**Insolvenzverwalter (Insolvency Administrator):** The court appoints an insolvency administrator (Section 56) who takes control of the debtor's assets and management. The Insolvenzverwalter has broad powers to manage the estate, avoid preferential transfers, and decide whether to continue or liquidate the business.

**Eigenverwaltung (Self-Administration):** As an exception to the administrator model, the debtor may petition for self-administration under Sections 270-285 InsO, retaining management control under the supervision of a court-appointed custodian (Sachwalter). The ESUG (Act to Further Facilitate the Restructuring of Enterprises) of 2012 strengthened self-administration procedures.

### StaRUG (Stabilisierungs- und Restrukturierungsrahmengesetz)

The StaRUG, enacted on January 1, 2021, transposes the EU Restructuring Directive (2019/1023) into German law. It provides a preventive restructuring framework for debtors that are not yet insolvent but face imminent insolvency:

**Key Features:** The StaRUG enables out-of-court restructuring plans binding dissenting creditors through court confirmation, cross-class cramdown under the absolute priority rule, and moratorium on enforcement actions (up to four months). The procedure does not require public filing and preserves the debtor's management control.

### Consumer Insolvency (Verbraucherinsolvenz)

Sections 304-314 InsO provide a simplified procedure for consumer debtors. Following a mandatory out-of-court settlement attempt, the debtor enters simplified insolvency proceedings. Discharge of remaining debts (Restschuldbefreiung) is available after a good-conduct period, reduced from six years to three years by the 2020 reform, effective October 1, 2020.

### Notable Cases

**Wirecard AG (2020):** The insolvency of payment processor Wirecard AG following a massive accounting fraud (EUR 1.9 billion in fictitious bank balances) was Germany's largest corporate scandal. Insolvency proceedings were opened on August 25, 2020. The case prompted regulatory reforms, including strengthening the Federal Financial Supervisory Authority (BaFin) and amending auditing oversight rules.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Regulation on Insolvency Proceedings (2015/848)

The Recast Regulation replaces Regulation 1346/2000 and coordinates cross-border insolvency proceedings within the EU:

**COMI (Center of Main Interests):** Article 3(1) provides that courts of the member state where the debtor has its COMI have jurisdiction to open main insolvency proceedings. COMI is presumed to be the debtor's registered office unless proven otherwise.

**Universal Effect:** Main insolvency proceedings have universal scope, covering all of the debtor's assets across the EU. Secondary proceedings may be opened in member states where the debtor has an establishment.

**Insolvency Registers (Article 24):** Member states must establish insolvency registers and interconnect them through the European e-Justice Portal, enabling creditors and courts to access information about insolvency proceedings across the EU.

### Restructuring Directive (2019/1023)

Directive 2019/1023 requires member states to implement preventive restructuring frameworks with the following features:

**Stay of Individual Enforcement Actions:** Debtors must have access to stays of enforcement for a period sufficient to negotiate and adopt a restructuring plan (minimum four months, maximum twelve months).

**Cross-Class Cramdown:** Restructuring plans may be confirmed over dissenting creditor classes if the plan complies with the absolute priority rule (or, optionally, the relative priority rule).

**Discharge for Entrepreneurs:** Insolvent entrepreneurs must have access to full discharge within a maximum of three years, promoting a "second chance" culture.

### ECHR Creditor Rights

The ECHR has recognized creditor rights as property rights under Protocol 1, Article 1:

**Intersplav v. Ukraine (Application No. 803/02, Judgment of January 9, 2007):** The Court held that a legitimate expectation of receiving payment constitutes a "possession" and that delays in state-controlled insolvency proceedings violating creditor rights may breach Article 1, Protocol 1.

**Kotov v. Russia (Application No. 54522/00, Grand Chamber Judgment of April 3, 2012):** The Grand Chamber held that the right to receive a dividend in insolvency proceedings constitutes a possession, but the state has broad discretion in determining priority rules.

## Comparative Analysis

### Debtor-in-Possession vs. Administrator

The most significant structural difference is the American debtor-in-possession model versus the administrator-based systems in South Korea and Germany. The DIP model incentivizes early filing and reduces stigma, but may create conflicts of interest when existing management caused the insolvency. The administrator model provides independent oversight but may be more disruptive and costly.

### Fresh Start and Discharge

The United States provides the most generous discharge regime, with immediate discharge available in Chapter 7 for honest individual debtors. Germany and the EU have converged on a three-year discharge period. South Korea requires three-to-five-year repayment plans under individual rehabilitation. The trend across all jurisdictions is toward faster, more accessible discharge to promote economic recovery and entrepreneurship.

### Cross-Border Coordination

The EU Regulation provides the most advanced regional framework for cross-border insolvency through the COMI principle and automatic recognition. The UNCITRAL Model Law (implemented in the US as Chapter 15 and adopted by South Korea) provides a global framework but relies on bilateral recognition rather than automatic mutual recognition.
