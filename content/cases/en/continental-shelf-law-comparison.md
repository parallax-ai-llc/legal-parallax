## Summary

Continental shelf law -- the body of rules governing the sovereign rights of coastal states over the seabed and subsoil of submarine areas extending beyond the territorial sea to the outer edge of the continental margin or to a distance of 200 nautical miles, whichever is greater -- is a critical domain of international law with enormous economic implications for resource exploitation, including hydrocarbons, minerals, and sedentary species. The legal framework is established primarily by Part VI of the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS, 1982), which entered into force on November 16, 1994, and which built upon the earlier Convention on the Continental Shelf (Geneva, 1958). Article 76 of UNCLOS defines the continental shelf as comprising "the seabed and subsoil of the submarine areas that extend beyond its territorial sea throughout the natural prolongation of its land territory to the outer edge of the continental margin, or to a distance of 200 nautical miles from the baselines from which the breadth of the territorial sea is measured where the outer edge of the continental margin does not extend up to that distance." The Commission on the Limits of the Continental Shelf (CLCS), established under Annex II of UNCLOS, reviews submissions by coastal states seeking to establish the outer limits of their continental shelf beyond 200 nautical miles. South Korea has significant continental shelf interests in the Yellow Sea (서해/황해), the East China Sea (동중국해), and the East Sea/Sea of Japan (동해/일본해), governed by the Continental Shelf Act (대륙붕법, 1970), the Korea-Japan Joint Development Zone Agreement (한일대륙붕공동개발협정, 1974), and ongoing delimitation disputes with China and Japan, complicated by competing baselines, island sovereignty disputes (including Dokdo/Takeshima), and overlapping claims in areas where the equidistance line and natural prolongation produce divergent results. The United States, though not a party to UNCLOS, claims an extensive continental shelf under customary international law and the Outer Continental Shelf Lands Act (43 U.S.C. Sections 1331-1356b), with the Extended Continental Shelf Project mapping claims beyond 200 nautical miles in the Arctic, Atlantic, Pacific, and Gulf of Mexico. Germany, as a party to UNCLOS with limited continental shelf in the North Sea and Baltic Sea, was a party to the seminal North Sea Continental Shelf Cases (ICJ, 1969), which established that equidistance is not a mandatory rule of customary international law and that delimitation must achieve an equitable result. The ECHR and EU framework intersects with continental shelf law through EU energy policy, the EU Offshore Safety Directive (2013/30/EU), and the application of human rights principles to resource exploitation disputes.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's continental shelf regime is established by the Continental Shelf Act (대륙붕법), originally enacted in 1970 and subsequently amended, which declares sovereign rights over the continental shelf for the purpose of exploring and exploiting natural resources. Korea ratified UNCLOS on January 29, 1996, and its continental shelf claims are governed by UNCLOS Part VI as supplemented by domestic legislation.

Korea's continental shelf situation is complicated by the semi-enclosed sea geography of the Yellow Sea and East China Sea, where the continental shelves of Korea, China, and Japan overlap extensively. Korea has historically emphasized the natural prolongation principle, arguing that the continental shelf in the East China Sea constitutes a natural extension of the Korean landmass extending to the Okinawa Trough, which Korea contends represents a fundamental discontinuity separating the Korean/Chinese continental margin from the Japanese island arc.

### Korea-Japan Joint Development Zone

The Agreement between Japan and the Republic of Korea Concerning Joint Development of the Southern Part of the Continental Shelf Adjacent to the Two Countries (한일대륙붕공동개발협정), signed on January 30, 1974, and entering into force on June 22, 1978, established a Joint Development Zone (JDZ) in the East China Sea covering approximately 82,000 square kilometers. The agreement, with a 50-year initial term expiring in 2028, provides for joint exploration and exploitation of hydrocarbon resources with equal sharing of costs and revenues.

China has consistently protested the JDZ as encroaching on its continental shelf, arguing that the agreement was concluded without China's participation regarding areas over which China claims sovereign rights based on natural prolongation. The impending expiration of the JDZ agreement in 2028 has generated significant diplomatic activity, as the three states must determine whether to extend, renegotiate, or terminate the arrangement.

### Dokdo/Takeshima and Continental Shelf Delimitation

The sovereignty dispute over Dokdo (독도, Japanese: Takeshima/竹島) directly affects continental shelf delimitation in the East Sea/Sea of Japan. Korea maintains effective control over Dokdo and asserts that the features generate a full continental shelf entitlement under UNCLOS Article 76. Japan contests Korean sovereignty and argues that the features, even if sovereign, would generate limited maritime zones. The ICJ has not adjudicated the dispute, and Korea rejects Japan's proposals for referral to the Court.

### CLCS Submissions

Korea submitted preliminary information to the CLCS in December 2012 concerning areas of extended continental shelf beyond 200 nautical miles in the East China Sea, pursuant to the decision of the Meeting of States Parties extending the deadline for submissions. Korea had earlier made a joint submission with Japan in 2009 concerning an area in the southern East China Sea, though this submission was limited in scope.

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|

| Illegal resource extraction | Confiscation and criminal penalties under Continental Shelf Act |
| Environmental damage to continental shelf | Liability under Marine Environment Management Act (해양환경관리법) |

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has not ratified UNCLOS, making it the most significant non-party to the Convention. Nevertheless, the U.S. claims sovereign rights over the continental shelf under customary international law as reflected in the Truman Proclamation on the Continental Shelf (Proclamation No. 2667, September 28, 1945), which was the foundational act establishing the modern continental shelf doctrine. President Truman declared that "the Government of the United States regards the natural resources of the subsoil and sea bed of the continental shelf beneath the high seas but contiguous to the coasts of the United States as appertaining to the United States, subject to its jurisdiction and control."

The Outer Continental Shelf Lands Act (OCSLA, 43 U.S.C. Sections 1331-1356b), originally enacted in 1953 and substantially amended by the Outer Continental Shelf Lands Act Amendments of 1978, establishes federal jurisdiction over the outer continental shelf (beyond state waters, generally 3 nautical miles from shore) and provides the framework for mineral leasing, primarily for oil and gas exploration and production. The Bureau of Ocean Energy Management (BOEM), within the Department of the Interior, administers the OCS leasing program.

### Extended Continental Shelf Project

Despite non-ratification of UNCLOS, the United States has undertaken extensive mapping of its extended continental shelf (beyond 200 nautical miles) through the U.S. Extended Continental Shelf Project, a multi-agency effort involving NOAA, the Department of State, the U.S. Geological Survey, and other agencies. The U.S. has identified potential extended continental shelf areas in the Arctic (Chukchi Plateau, Beaufort Sea), the Atlantic margin, the Gulf of Mexico, the Bering Sea, and the Mariana Islands region, totaling approximately one million square kilometers of additional seabed.

However, because the U.S. is not a party to UNCLOS, it cannot submit claims to the CLCS, which has been cited as a significant disadvantage by proponents of ratification, including the Department of State, the Department of Defense, and the U.S. Navy.

### Maritime Boundary Agreements

The United States has concluded continental shelf delimitation agreements with several neighboring states:

- **U.S.-Mexico:** Treaty on Maritime Boundaries (1978); Western Gap Agreement (2000) concerning the "Doughnut Hole" in the Gulf of Mexico.
- **U.S.-Canada:** International Court of Justice judgment in the Gulf of Maine Case (ICJ Reports 1984, p. 246), delimiting the continental shelf and fisheries zones by a single maritime boundary.
- **U.S.-Russia:** Maritime Boundary Agreement (1990), establishing a boundary in the Bering Sea and Arctic Ocean. The agreement has been provisionally applied by both states, though the Russian Duma has not ratified it.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's continental shelf regime is governed by UNCLOS (ratified November 14, 1994), the Federal Mining Act (Bundesberggesetz, BBergG), and the Continental Shelf Mining Ordinance (Festlandsockel-Bergverordnung). Germany's continental shelf in the North Sea and Baltic Sea is relatively limited in extent compared to states with ocean-facing coastlines, but its legal significance is disproportionate due to Germany's role in the foundational ICJ case on continental shelf delimitation.

### North Sea Continental Shelf Cases (ICJ, 1969)

The North Sea Continental Shelf Cases (Federal Republic of Germany/Denmark; Federal Republic of Germany/Netherlands, ICJ Reports 1969, p. 3) represent the seminal judicial pronouncement on continental shelf delimitation. The cases arose from Germany's objection to the equidistance method prescribed by Article 6 of the 1958 Geneva Convention on the Continental Shelf, which would have disadvantaged Germany due to its concave coastline configuration.

The ICJ held, by 11 votes to 6, that the equidistance-special circumstances rule in Article 6 of the 1958 Convention had not become a rule of customary international law and that delimitation must be effected "by agreement in accordance with equitable principles, and taking account of all the relevant circumstances." The Court identified several factors relevant to achieving an equitable result, including the general configuration of the coasts, the physical and geological structure of the shelf areas, and the principle of natural prolongation.

The judgment established the foundation for the equitable principles/relevant circumstances approach that has dominated subsequent ICJ and arbitral jurisprudence on maritime delimitation, including the Libya/Malta Continental Shelf Case (ICJ, 1985), the Maritime Delimitation in the Black Sea Case (Romania v. Ukraine, ICJ, 2009), and the Bangladesh/Myanmar Continental Shelf Case (ITLOS, 2012).

### German Continental Shelf Boundaries

Following the ICJ's 1969 judgment, Germany concluded delimitation agreements with Denmark (Treaty of January 28, 1971) and the Netherlands (Treaty of January 28, 1971), both achieving results more favorable to Germany than strict equidistance would have produced. Germany also has continental shelf boundaries with the United Kingdom (Agreement of November 25, 1971) and Poland (Agreement of October 22, 2020, supplementing the 1989 agreement on maritime delimitation in the Baltic Sea).

### Offshore Activities and Environmental Regulation

Germany's continental shelf activities are primarily focused on offshore wind energy rather than hydrocarbon extraction. The Offshore Wind Energy Act (Windenergie-auf-See-Gesetz, WindSeeG) provides the regulatory framework for offshore wind farms in the German EEZ and continental shelf, with Germany's Maritimes Raumordnungsplan (Maritime Spatial Plan) designating priority areas for offshore wind development.

### Penalties

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| Unauthorized mineral extraction on continental shelf | Penalties under BBergG Section 145 et seq. |
| Environmental damage to continental shelf | Liability under Environmental Liability Act (UmweltHG) |
| Violation of offshore installation safety requirements | Administrative and criminal penalties under BBergG |
| Unauthorized construction on continental shelf | Administrative orders; penalties under maritime spatial planning law |

## ECHR & EU

### EU Legal Framework for Continental Shelf Activities

The European Union does not have direct competence over continental shelf delimitation, which remains a matter of international law between sovereign states. However, EU law significantly affects activities on the continental shelf through several regulatory instruments.

The EU Offshore Safety Directive (Directive 2013/30/EU), adopted in response to the Deepwater Horizon disaster (2010), establishes minimum safety requirements for offshore oil and gas operations within the EU. It requires member states to ensure that operators prepare Major Hazard Reports for installations on the continental shelf, establishes independent competent authorities for offshore safety, and mandates emergency preparedness and response plans. The Directive applies to all offshore oil and gas activities within the jurisdiction of EU member states, including their continental shelves and exclusive economic zones.

### EU Energy and Environmental Policy

The EU's maritime spatial planning framework, established by Directive 2014/89/EU, requires member states to develop maritime spatial plans covering their marine waters, including continental shelf areas. The EU Marine Strategy Framework Directive (2008/56/EC) requires member states to achieve "good environmental status" in their marine waters, including the seabed and subsoil of the continental shelf.

The EU's Renewable Energy Directive (EU) 2018/2001, as amended by the REPowerEU amendments, has accelerated offshore wind development on the continental shelves of EU member states, particularly in the North Sea, Baltic Sea, and Atlantic. The North Seas Energy Cooperation, involving nine EU member states and Norway, coordinates offshore wind development and grid interconnection across national continental shelf boundaries.

### ECHR and Resource Exploitation

The European Court of Human Rights has addressed property rights issues arising from continental shelf resource exploitation. In Beyeler v. Italy (Application No. 33202/96, Grand Chamber Judgment of January 5, 2000), the Court established principles on the protection of property under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 that are applicable to resource rights. While the Court has not directly adjudicated continental shelf delimitation disputes, its jurisprudence on environmental rights (under Articles 2 and 8 of the Convention) is relevant to the environmental impacts of continental shelf activities.

### EU External Relations and Continental Shelf

The EU has concluded fisheries agreements and partnership agreements with third states that intersect with continental shelf rights. The EU-Morocco Fisheries Partnership Agreement, challenged in Western Sahara Campaign UK v. Commissioners (Case C-266/16, 2018), raised questions about the application of EU agreements to the continental shelf of non-self-governing territories, with the CJEU ruling that Western Sahara's continental shelf was not covered by the agreement absent the consent of its people.

## Comparative Analysis

### Delimitation Methodology

The evolution of continental shelf delimitation methodology shows a clear trajectory from rigid rules toward flexible, equity-based approaches. The 1958 Geneva Convention's equidistance/special circumstances rule was rejected as customary law in the North Sea Continental Shelf Cases (1969), leading to the equitable principles/relevant circumstances approach. UNCLOS Article 83 requires delimitation "by agreement on the basis of international law, as referred to in Article 38 of the Statute of the International Court of Justice, in order to achieve an equitable solution," leaving the methodology open. Modern ICJ practice, exemplified by the three-stage approach in Romania v. Ukraine (2009), begins with a provisional equidistance line, then considers relevant circumstances justifying adjustment, and finally applies a proportionality check.

### Natural Prolongation vs. Distance

The tension between natural prolongation (the geological extension of the landmass) and distance-based criteria (200 nautical miles) is particularly acute in East Asia, where Korea's natural prolongation argument for extended continental shelf in the East China Sea conflicts with China's and Japan's distance-based claims. The ICJ's Libya/Malta decision (1985) diminished the role of natural prolongation within 200 nautical miles, but it retains significance for extended shelf claims beyond 200 nautical miles.

### UNCLOS Non-Ratification and Its Consequences

The United States' failure to ratify UNCLOS places it in an anomalous position: it claims customary law rights over the continental shelf but cannot submit extended continental shelf claims to the CLCS, potentially leaving significant seabed areas (particularly in the Arctic) without internationally recognized legal certainty. This contrasts sharply with South Korea's and Germany's ability to utilize UNCLOS institutions.

### Resource Exploitation Regimes

The four jurisdictions reflect different resource exploitation priorities. South Korea focuses on hydrocarbon exploration (Korea-Japan JDZ). The United States operates the world's largest offshore oil and gas leasing program through BOEM. Germany has shifted toward offshore wind energy, reflecting the Energiewende policy. The EU regulatory framework increasingly emphasizes environmental protection and renewable energy over hydrocarbon extraction.
