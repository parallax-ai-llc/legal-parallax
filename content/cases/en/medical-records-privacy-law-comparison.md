## Summary

Medical records privacy -- the legal protection of individually identifiable health information from unauthorized access, use, and disclosure -- has emerged as a critical area of regulation as healthcare systems increasingly digitize patient records and engage in cross-border data sharing. South Korea regulates medical records privacy through the Personal Information Protection Act (개인정보 보호법, PIPA) of 2011 as amended in 2023, the Medical Service Act (의료법) Articles 19 and 21, and the Act on the Protection of Health Information in Medical Institutions, creating a multi-layered regime that classifies health information as "sensitive information" (민감정보) requiring explicit consent for processing. The United States relies primarily on the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) of 1996 and its implementing regulations -- the Privacy Rule (45 C.F.R. Part 160 and Subparts A and E of Part 164) and the Security Rule (45 C.F.R. Part 160 and Subparts A and C of Part 164) -- supplemented by the HITECH Act of 2009, which expanded HIPAA's reach to business associates and introduced mandatory breach notification, as well as a patchwork of state laws that frequently provide greater protections. Germany applies both the EU General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) and national implementation through the Bundesdatenschutzgesetz (BDSG) of 2017, along with specific healthcare data protections in the Sozialgesetzbuch (Social Code) Book V and Book X, and professional secrecy obligations under Section 203 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB). At the European level, the GDPR Article 9 provides heightened protections for health data as a "special category" of personal data, the European Health Data Space (EHDS) Regulation adopted in 2025 establishes a framework for cross-border health data sharing, and the ECtHR has developed significant jurisprudence protecting medical data privacy under Article 8 of the European Convention on Human Rights.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's medical records privacy regime operates through several interlocking statutes. The Personal Information Protection Act (개인정보 보호법, PIPA), enacted in 2011 and substantially amended in 2023, serves as the general data protection law. The Medical Service Act (의료법) Articles 19 and 21 impose specific confidentiality obligations on healthcare providers. The 2023 PIPA amendments significantly strengthened the enforcement powers of the Personal Information Protection Commission (개인정보보호위원회, PIPC) and introduced revenue-based administrative fines.

### Classification and Consent

Under PIPA Article 23, health information constitutes "sensitive information" (민감정보) subject to heightened protections. Processing of sensitive information requires the explicit consent of the data subject, with the consent form clearly specifying the types of sensitive information to be processed, the purposes of processing, and the retention period. Exceptions are limited to situations required by law, necessary for the performance of a contract, or required for the vital interests of the data subject.

### Medical Service Act Provisions

Article 19 of the Medical Service Act prohibits medical personnel from disclosing patient information obtained in the course of their duties, except where required by other laws. Article 21 governs the provision of medical records, permitting disclosure to the patient, their legal representative, or specified third parties (such as insurers with patient consent). Violation of Article 19 carries penalties of up to three years imprisonment or a fine of up to 30 million KRW.

### Electronic Health Records

The Act on Promotion of the Use of Health Information and Technologies established the framework for electronic health records (전자건강기록) and health information exchanges. The Korea Health Information Service (KHIS) oversees the national health information infrastructure, and the My HealthWay (나의건강기록) platform launched in 2023 enables patients to access and control their health data through a centralized digital portal.

### Penalties

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| Unauthorized disclosure of medical records (Medical Service Act Art. 19) | Up to 3 years imprisonment or 30 million KRW fine |
| Processing sensitive information without consent (PIPA Art. 23) | Administrative fine up to 5% of related revenue |
| Failure to report data breach (PIPA) | Administrative fine up to 30 million KRW |
| Selling or providing health data for profit without authorization | Up to 5 years imprisonment or 50 million KRW fine |

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States regulates medical records privacy primarily through the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA), Public Law 104-191, enacted on August 21, 1996. The Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) promulgated the HIPAA Privacy Rule (45 C.F.R. Part 160 and Subparts A and E of Part 164), effective April 14, 2003, and the HIPAA Security Rule (45 C.F.R. Part 160 and Subparts A and C of Part 164), effective April 20, 2005.

**HITECH Act (2009):** Title XIII of the American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009, known as the Health Information Technology for Economic and Clinical Health (HITECH) Act, substantially expanded HIPAA's scope by making business associates directly subject to HIPAA requirements, introducing mandatory breach notification to HHS and affected individuals, increasing penalty amounts, and providing for state attorneys general enforcement authority.

### Protected Health Information

HIPAA protects "Protected Health Information" (PHI), defined as individually identifiable health information transmitted or maintained by a covered entity or business associate, in any form or medium. The Privacy Rule identifies 18 specific identifiers that must be removed for information to be considered "de-identified" under the Safe Harbor method.

### Permitted Uses and Disclosures

The Privacy Rule permits disclosure of PHI without patient authorization for: treatment, payment, and healthcare operations (TPO); public health activities; judicial and administrative proceedings; law enforcement purposes (with limitations); research (with IRB or Privacy Board waiver); and to avert serious threat to health or safety. All other uses require a signed authorization from the patient.

### State Law Preemption

HIPAA establishes a federal floor, not a ceiling; state laws that provide greater privacy protections are not preempted. Notable state laws include the California Confidentiality of Medical Information Act (CMIA), which provides broader protections than HIPAA and includes a private right of action; the New York SHIELD Act (2019); and the Texas Medical Records Privacy Act (Health and Safety Code Chapter 181). The 21st Century Cures Act (2016) and its implementing information blocking rule (effective April 5, 2021) added requirements preventing healthcare providers from unreasonably restricting patients' electronic access to their own health information.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany applies a layered approach to medical records privacy, combining the EU General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), the national Bundesdatenschutzgesetz (BDSG) of 2017, social security law provisions in the Sozialgesetzbuch (SGB), and criminal professional secrecy protections under the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB).

**GDPR Application:** Under Article 9(1) GDPR, "data concerning health" constitutes a special category of personal data, the processing of which is generally prohibited unless one of the specific legal bases in Article 9(2) applies. These include explicit consent (Art. 9(2)(a)), necessity for healthcare provision (Art. 9(2)(h)), and public health purposes (Art. 9(2)(i)).

**Section 22 BDSG:** Germany's national GDPR implementation law provides additional conditions for processing special categories of data, including health data. Section 22(1) BDSG specifies that processing is permitted for healthcare purposes where the data subject is a patient and the processing is carried out by healthcare professionals bound by professional secrecy.

### Professional Secrecy (Section 203 StGB)

Section 203 StGB criminalizes the unauthorized disclosure of another person's secrets, including health information, by persons bound by professional secrecy (Berufsgeheimnisträger), including physicians, dentists, pharmacists, psychotherapists, and other healthcare professionals. The provision was amended in 2017 to address disclosure to outsourced IT service providers and other external processors. Violations carry penalties of up to one year imprisonment or a fine; in particularly serious cases, up to two years imprisonment.

### Social Security Code Protections

SGB V (Statutory Health Insurance) and SGB X (Social Administration Procedure and Social Data Protection) contain specific provisions governing the processing of health data within the German statutory health insurance system. Section 284 SGB V restricts the purposes for which health insurance funds may process patient data. Section 67 SGB X defines "social data" (Sozialdaten) and establishes the permissible grounds for processing, transfer, and storage.

### Electronic Patient Record (ePA)

The Digitale-Versorgung-Gesetz (Digital Healthcare Act, DVG) of 2019 and the Patientendaten-Schutz-Gesetz (Patient Data Protection Act, PDSG) of 2020 established the legal framework for the elektronische Patientenakte (ePA), Germany's national electronic patient record system. Since January 2025, all statutory health insurance members have received an ePA on an opt-out basis, following the Digital Act (Digitalgesetz, DigiG) of 2024. Patients retain granular control over access permissions and may opt out entirely.

### Penalties

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| GDPR breach (administrative fine) | Up to 20 million EUR or 4% of annual global turnover |
| Section 203 StGB violation | Up to 1 year imprisonment or fine; serious cases up to 2 years |
| SGB data protection violation | Administrative fines under BDSG/GDPR |
| ePA unauthorized access | Penalties under PDSG and GDPR |

## ECHR & EU

### GDPR -- Health Data Protections

The General Data Protection Regulation (Regulation (EU) 2016/679) provides the foundational EU-wide framework for health data protection. Article 4(15) defines "data concerning health" as "personal data related to the physical or mental health of a natural person, including the provision of health care services, which reveal information about his or her health status."

**Article 9 -- Special Categories:** Processing of health data is prohibited under Article 9(1) unless one of the legal bases in Article 9(2) is satisfied. The most relevant bases for healthcare contexts include: explicit consent (Art. 9(2)(a)); necessity for preventive or occupational medicine, medical diagnosis, or healthcare treatment (Art. 9(2)(h)); and public interest in public health (Art. 9(2)(i)).

**Data Protection Impact Assessments:** Article 35(3)(b) GDPR requires a DPIA for processing on a large scale of special categories of data, including health data. National supervisory authorities have issued guidance specifying thresholds and methodologies.

### European Health Data Space (EHDS)

The European Health Data Space Regulation, proposed by the Commission on May 3, 2022, and formally adopted in 2025, establishes the first EU-wide framework for health data sharing. The EHDS creates two tiers of data use: primary use (patient access to and control over their electronic health data across member states) and secondary use (access to anonymized or pseudonymized health data for research, innovation, policy-making, and regulatory activities). The regulation establishes Health Data Access Bodies in each member state to manage secondary use applications.

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECtHR has developed significant jurisprudence on medical records privacy under Article 8 ECHR (right to respect for private and family life).

**Z v. Finland (Application No. 22009/93, Judgment of February 25, 1997):** The Court held that the protection of personal data, particularly medical data, is of fundamental importance to a person's enjoyment of the right to respect for private and family life. The disclosure of the applicant's HIV-positive status and identity in a criminal court judgment constituted a violation of Article 8. The Court established that "respecting the confidentiality of health data is a vital principle in the legal systems of all the Contracting Parties to the Convention."

**I v. Finland (Application No. 20511/03, Judgment of July 17, 2008):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where a hospital failed to implement adequate safeguards to prevent unauthorized access to the applicant's medical records by hospital staff. The case established that states have positive obligations to ensure that adequate data security measures are in place in healthcare settings.

**L.H. v. Latvia (Application No. 52019/07, Judgment of April 29, 2014):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where reproductive health data was collected and stored by the Health Inspectorate without adequate legal basis or sufficient safeguards.

### Key Principles Established

- Medical data protection is fundamental to Article 8 rights and requires "particularly cogent reasons" for any interference.
- States have positive obligations to establish effective medical data protection frameworks.
- Inadequate data security in healthcare settings may constitute an Article 8 violation.
- Even disclosure of medical information to judicial authorities must be strictly necessary and proportionate.

## Comparative Analysis

### Regulatory Architecture

The jurisdictions display three distinct regulatory architectures: South Korea and the EU apply general data protection laws with special provisions for health data; the United States has sector-specific health privacy legislation (HIPAA) that exists alongside general consumer privacy laws; and Germany combines the EU framework with national implementation, criminal professional secrecy, and social security-specific data protections. South Korea's 2023 PIPA amendments brought its framework closer to the GDPR model, with enhanced enforcement powers and revenue-based penalties.

### Consent Models

All jurisdictions require heightened consent for health data processing but differ in their exceptions. The United States permits disclosure for treatment, payment, and healthcare operations without specific patient authorization, a pragmatic approach that prioritizes care coordination. South Korea and the EU require explicit consent with narrow exceptions. Germany adds criminal professional secrecy obligations that exist independently of data protection law.

### Enforcement Mechanisms

The EU and South Korea feature strong centralized enforcement through data protection authorities (DPAs and PIPC, respectively) empowered to impose substantial administrative fines. The United States relies on HHS Office for Civil Rights enforcement for HIPAA, supplemented by state attorney general authority under the HITECH Act. Germany uniquely combines administrative enforcement under the GDPR with criminal prosecution under Section 203 StGB.

### Digital Health Records

All jurisdictions have established or are developing national electronic health record systems, each reflecting different privacy architectures. South Korea's My HealthWay platform operates on an opt-in consent model; Germany's ePA uses an opt-out model since 2025; and the EU's EHDS provides cross-border interoperability with tiered primary and secondary use frameworks.
