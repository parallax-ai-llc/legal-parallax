## Summary

Hydraulic fracturing -- the high-pressure injection of water, sand, and chemical additives into geological formations to fracture rock and release trapped oil and natural gas -- has transformed the global energy landscape since its large-scale commercialization in the early 2000s, while simultaneously generating intense legal and political controversy over groundwater contamination, induced seismicity, air pollution, and climate impacts. South Korea has no domestic hydraulic fracturing industry and no specific fracking legislation, though the Mining Industry Act (광업법) and the Framework Act on Environmental Policy (환경정책기본법) would govern any future unconventional extraction activities; South Korea's relevance lies in its position as a major importer of LNG produced through fracking in the United States and Australia. The United States is the global epicenter of hydraulic fracturing, with the technology responsible for approximately 79% of all U.S. natural gas production and 65% of oil production as of 2024; the Energy Policy Act of 2005 exempted hydraulic fracturing from the Safe Drinking Water Act's underground injection control program (the "Halliburton loophole," 42 U.S.C. Section 300h(d)), and regulation remains primarily a state function, with states like Texas, Pennsylvania, Colorado, and North Dakota maintaining comprehensive but varying frameworks; Vermont (2012), New York (2014), and Maryland (2017) have banned fracking entirely. Germany enacted an effective ban on hydraulic fracturing for unconventional shale gas extraction through the 2016 amendment to the Federal Water Resources Act (Wasserhaushaltsgesetz, WHG) Section 13a, while permitting limited fracking for conventional tight gas formations above 3,000 meters depth subject to environmental assessment; the ban was enacted despite estimates that Germany's shale gas reserves could supply decades of domestic consumption, reflecting strong public opposition and the precautionary principle embedded in German environmental law. At the European level, no EU-wide ban exists, but the European Commission's 2014 Recommendation on Minimum Principles for Shale Gas (2014/70/EU) established a non-binding framework for environmental protection; France (Loi Jacob, 2011) and Bulgaria (2012) imposed national bans, while Poland and Romania have authorized exploration; the European Court of Human Rights has addressed fracking-related complaints through the environmental rights framework of Articles 2 and 8 of the Convention, applying the principle that states must conduct adequate environmental impact assessments before authorizing activities that may affect residents' health and quality of life.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea has no domestic hydraulic fracturing industry and no specific legislation addressing the practice. The country's relevance to the global fracking debate is primarily as a consumer of fracked hydrocarbons.

**Mining Industry Act (광업법):** This statute governs the exploration and extraction of underground mineral resources in South Korea. While the Act does not specifically address hydraulic fracturing, any attempt to employ the technique for domestic extraction would require mining rights permits under this framework, subject to environmental impact assessment under the Environmental Impact Assessment Act (환경영향평가법).

**Framework Act on Environmental Policy (환경정책기본법):** This act establishes the precautionary principle and polluter-pays principle as foundational environmental policy principles. Article 7 states that the government shall take preventive measures when there is a risk of environmental harm, even in the absence of scientific certainty -- a standard that would likely apply to any proposal for domestic fracking operations.

### Import Dependence on Fracked Gas

South Korea is the world's third-largest LNG importer, purchasing approximately 45 million tonnes annually. A significant portion of this LNG originates from U.S. shale gas operations -- particularly from Gulf Coast liquefaction facilities connected to the Permian Basin, Eagle Ford, and Haynesville shale plays. The Korea Gas Corporation (한국가스공사, KOGAS) holds long-term purchase agreements with multiple U.S. LNG exporters.

The expansion of U.S. fracking has directly benefited South Korea by diversifying supply away from traditional Middle Eastern and Southeast Asian sources and exerting downward pressure on global LNG prices. However, environmental groups in South Korea have increasingly raised concerns about the climate and environmental impacts of imported fracked gas, arguing that lifecycle emissions analysis should inform energy import policy.

### Pohang Earthquake and Induced Seismicity

While unrelated to hydraulic fracturing specifically, the 2017 Pohang earthquake (magnitude 5.4) -- South Korea's second-strongest recorded earthquake -- was caused by an enhanced geothermal system (EGS) project that used high-pressure fluid injection techniques similar to fracking. A government investigation confirmed the causal link in 2019, leading to the suspension of the Pohang EGS project and heightened public awareness of induced seismicity risks associated with subsurface fluid injection. The incident has created significant public skepticism toward any technology involving high-pressure subsurface fluid injection.

### Legal Liability for Induced Seismicity

Following the Pohang earthquake, affected residents filed civil lawsuits against the government and the project operator. In 2022, the Daegu District Court (대구지방법원) awarded compensation to over 1,800 plaintiffs, finding that the state bore liability for authorizing the EGS project without adequate seismic risk assessment. The case established a precedent for government liability in connection with induced seismicity from subsurface energy operations.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States is the world's leading fracking nation, with hydraulic fracturing responsible for the "shale revolution" that transformed the U.S. from a declining oil and gas producer to the world's largest producer.

**Energy Policy Act of 2005 -- "Halliburton Loophole":** Section 322 of the Energy Policy Act of 2005 amended the Safe Drinking Water Act (SDWA) to exclude hydraulic fracturing operations (except those using diesel fuel) from the underground injection control (UIC) program administered by the EPA. The provision, 42 U.S.C. Section 300h(d)(1)(B)(ii), states that the term "underground injection" does not include the underground injection of fluids or propping agents (other than diesel fuels) pursuant to hydraulic fracturing operations related to oil, gas, or geothermal production activities. This exemption was advocated by the energy industry and is commonly attributed to then-Vice President Dick Cheney's Energy Task Force, earning the informal name "Halliburton loophole" after Cheney's former employer, the oilfield services company Halliburton.

**State Regulation:** In the absence of comprehensive federal oversight, state oil and gas commissions serve as primary regulators. Key regulatory frameworks include:

| State | Regulatory Body | Key Requirements |
|-------|----------------|-----------------|
| Texas | Railroad Commission of Texas | Well spacing; casing/cementing standards; Statewide Rule 13 (water protection); seismicity monitoring |
| Pennsylvania | Department of Environmental Protection | Act 13 (2012): setback requirements, chemical disclosure; Act 52 (2016): well construction standards |
| Colorado | Energy and Carbon Management Commission | SB 19-181: operator must demonstrate protection of public health; 2,000-ft setback from occupied buildings |
| Ohio | Department of Natural Resources | Injection well permits; seismicity traffic light protocol |
| Oklahoma | Corporation Commission | Induced seismicity traffic light protocol; saltwater disposal well restrictions |
| New York | Department of Environmental Conservation | Statewide ban (2014 executive order; codified 2020) |

**Chemical Disclosure -- FracFocus:** Launched in 2011, FracFocus (www.fracfocus.org) is a national hydraulic fracturing chemical registry where operators disclose the chemicals used in each fracking operation. As of 2026, over 230,000 chemical disclosure reports have been submitted. However, trade secret exemptions allow operators to withhold the identity of certain proprietary chemicals, and environmental groups argue that disclosure is insufficient without independent verification.

### Induced Seismicity

Oklahoma experienced a dramatic increase in earthquakes linked to the deep-well injection of wastewater produced during fracking operations. The state experienced 903 earthquakes of magnitude 3.0 or greater in 2015, compared to an average of two per year from 1978 to 2008. The Oklahoma Corporation Commission implemented volume restrictions on saltwater disposal wells in the state's Arbuckle formation, leading to a significant reduction in seismicity. The "traffic light protocol" -- green (continue), yellow (reduce), red (stop) -- based on seismic event magnitude has become the standard regulatory approach.

### State and Local Bans

Three states have banned fracking entirely: Vermont (Act 152, 2012), New York (2014 executive order, codified by legislation in 2020), and Maryland (HB 1325, 2017). Local fracking bans have faced mixed judicial reception. In Denton, Texas (2014), a voter-approved fracking ban was overridden by state legislation (HB 40, 2015), which preempted municipal regulation of oil and gas operations. In contrast, Colorado's SB 19-181 (2019) enhanced local government authority over oil and gas operations, including the power to regulate siting and surface impacts.

### Key Litigation

**EPA Hydraulic Fracturing Study (2016):** The EPA's comprehensive study on the relationship between hydraulic fracturing and drinking water resources found that fracking activities can impact drinking water resources under some circumstances, including water withdrawals in areas of low water availability, spills of hydraulic fracturing fluids, injection into wells with inadequate casing, and discharge of inadequately treated wastewater.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany enacted one of the world's most comprehensive fracking bans through the 2016 legislative package amending the Federal Water Resources Act and the Federal Mining Act.

**WHG Section 13a -- Prohibition of Hydraulic Fracturing:** The 2016 amendment to the Wasserhaushaltsgesetz (WHG) prohibits hydraulic fracturing for the extraction of natural gas, petroleum, or geothermal energy from shale formations, coal seams, clay stone, and marl (unconventional reservoirs). This prohibition applies regardless of depth.

**Limited Exception for Conventional Formations:** Fracking in conventional tight sandstone formations above 3,000 meters depth remains permissible subject to:
- Environmental impact assessment under the Environmental Impact Assessment Act (UVPG)
- Approval by the responsible water authority
- A ban on the use of environmentally hazardous substances in fracking fluids
- Mandatory baseline monitoring of groundwater quality before, during, and after operations

**Expert Commission (Expertenkommission Fracking):** The 2016 legislation established an expert commission to evaluate the environmental risks of unconventional fracking by 2021, with the possibility that the Bundestag could authorize limited scientific test drillings. The commission submitted its report in 2021 but the Bundestag has not acted to modify the ban.

### Political and Legal Context

The fracking ban reflects several factors specific to the German context:

**Precautionary Principle:** German environmental law is grounded in the Vorsorgeprinzip (precautionary principle), codified in Section 5 of the Federal Immission Control Act (Bundes-Immissionsschutzgesetz, BImSchG) and recognized as a constitutional principle through Article 20a of the Basic Law. The principle requires preventive action against potential environmental harm even in the absence of complete scientific certainty.

**Water Protection Priority:** Germany's dense population, intensive agriculture, and reliance on groundwater for drinking supply create acute vulnerability to potential groundwater contamination. Approximately 70% of Germany's drinking water comes from groundwater sources, making groundwater protection a paramount regulatory priority.

**Public Opposition:** Public opinion surveys consistently show that over 70% of Germans oppose hydraulic fracturing. The anti-fracking movement in Germany drew on the broader environmental movement's strength and organizational capacity, particularly in Lower Saxony and North Rhine-Westphalia, the states with the largest potential shale gas reserves.

### Energy Crisis Debate

The 2022 energy crisis, triggered by the cutoff of Russian gas supplies, reignited debate over whether Germany should develop its estimated 800 billion to 2.3 trillion cubic meters of technically recoverable shale gas reserves. The FDP (Free Democrats) advocated for lifting the fracking ban, while the Greens and SPD maintained their opposition. The Bundestag did not modify the ban, and the crisis was instead addressed through LNG imports, energy conservation measures, and accelerated renewable energy deployment.

## ECHR / EU

### EU Commission Recommendation 2014/70/EU

In January 2014, the European Commission issued a non-binding Recommendation on minimum principles for the exploration and production of hydrocarbons (such as shale gas) using high-volume hydraulic fracturing. Key principles include:

- Strategic environmental assessment before granting licenses for exploration or production
- Environmental impact assessment for each project
- Baseline monitoring of groundwater, air quality, seismicity, and biodiversity before operations begin
- Disclosure of chemical additives used in fracking fluids
- Well integrity assurance through proper casing and cementing
- Adequate financial guarantees to cover environmental liability
- Monitoring and reporting of methane emissions

The Recommendation was issued after the European Parliament's 2012 resolution calling for a EU-wide regulatory framework but failing to achieve the votes for a binding directive. The Commission committed to reviewing member state implementation but has not proposed binding legislation.

### ECHR Environmental Rights and Fracking

The ECHR has not decided a case directly addressing hydraulic fracturing, but its environmental rights jurisprudence provides an applicable framework.

**Tatar v. Romania (Application No. 67021/01, 2009):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where Romanian authorities failed to adequately assess and mitigate the environmental risks of a gold mining operation using sodium cyanide near the applicants' homes. The Court held that authorities have a duty to assess environmental risks before authorizing potentially hazardous activities and to provide the public with access to relevant environmental information.

**Dubetska v. Ukraine (Application No. 30499/03, 2011):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where long-term coal mining operations caused subsidence and groundwater contamination affecting residents' homes. The Court emphasized the state's positive obligation to take reasonable measures to protect individuals from environmental harm caused by industrial activities.

**Hardy and Maile v. United Kingdom (Application No. 31965/07, 2012):** The Court examined complaints about an LNG terminal's environmental impact, applying the framework that states must ensure a fair balance between the economic interests of the community and the effective enjoyment of individual rights under Article 8.

These cases establish that states authorizing fracking operations must:
- Conduct thorough environmental impact assessments
- Provide adequate information to affected communities
- Maintain effective regulatory oversight
- Ensure access to remedies for environmental damage

### Water Framework Directive

The EU Water Framework Directive (2000/60/EC) requires member states to achieve "good status" for all water bodies, including groundwater. Any fracking operation that risks compromising groundwater quality would need to demonstrate compliance with the Directive's environmental objectives, potentially limiting fracking activities even in member states that have not imposed explicit bans.

## Comparative Analysis

### Regulatory Philosophy

The jurisdictions represent three distinct regulatory approaches: permissive regulation (United States), precautionary prohibition (Germany, France), and subsidiary discretion (EU). The U.S. approach relies on market forces supplemented by state-level environmental regulation, exempting fracking from key federal water protection statutes. Germany and France apply the precautionary principle to prohibit the practice pending demonstration of safety. The EU defers to member states while providing non-binding guidance.

### Energy Independence vs. Environmental Protection

The United States treats fracking as a national security asset, with the shale revolution reducing dependence on Middle Eastern oil imports. Germany's fracking ban prioritizes environmental protection and groundwater safety despite the significant energy security benefits that domestic shale gas could provide -- a trade-off made stark by the 2022 energy crisis. South Korea's reliance on imported fracked gas illustrates how fracking bans in one jurisdiction shift environmental impacts to others.

### Induced Seismicity Governance

The U.S. experience with fracking-induced seismicity in Oklahoma has produced the most developed regulatory framework (traffic light protocols, disposal well restrictions), while South Korea's Pohang earthquake case has created significant legal precedent for government liability. Germany's ban eliminates the issue domestically, and the EU lacks harmonized seismicity standards.

### Local Democratic Participation

The tension between state/national authority and local democratic preferences runs through all jurisdictions. The U.S. experience -- with Texas preempting the Denton fracking ban while Colorado expanded local authority -- illustrates the spectrum. Germany's ban reflected broad public opposition channeled through national legislation. The EU's subsidiary approach allows both local and national preferences to prevail.
