## Summary

Noise pollution regulation -- the legal framework governing the emission, transmission, and impact of unwanted sound on human health, well-being, and the environment -- has evolved from rudimentary nuisance law into a sophisticated area of environmental and public health regulation, as evidence has mounted that chronic noise exposure contributes to cardiovascular disease, cognitive impairment in children, sleep disturbance, and mental health disorders, with the World Health Organization estimating that environmental noise causes 12,000 premature deaths and 48,000 new cases of ischaemic heart disease annually in Europe alone. South Korea regulates noise through the Noise and Vibration Control Act (소음·진동관리법), which establishes emission standards for industrial facilities, construction sites, and transportation, supplemented by the Environmental Dispute Coordination Act (환경분쟁 조정법) providing an administrative dispute resolution mechanism that has processed thousands of noise complaints, with particularly intense public concern over inter-floor noise (층간소음) in the country's predominantly apartment-based housing leading to the enactment of the Housing Act (주택법) provisions and dedicated management centers. The United States addresses noise primarily through the Noise Control Act of 1972 (42 U.S.C. Sections 4901-4918), which established the EPA's Office of Noise Abatement and Control, though the office was defunded in 1982 and federal noise regulation has largely been dormant since, leaving regulation to state and local governments, the Federal Aviation Administration for aircraft noise, and OSHA for occupational noise exposure. Germany provides comprehensive noise protection through the Bundes-Immissionsschutzgesetz (BImSchG) and its implementing ordinances, particularly the TA Lärm (Technische Anleitung zum Schutz gegen Lärm) establishing detailed industrial noise standards, the 16th BImSchV for traffic noise, and the Fluglärmschutzgesetz for aircraft noise, with extensive ECHR jurisprudence under Article 8 recognizing that severe noise pollution constitutes interference with the right to private and family life.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's noise regulation is governed by the Noise and Vibration Control Act (소음·진동관리법, Act No. 19953), the Airport Noise Prevention and Airport Surrounding Area Support Act (공항소음 방지 및 소음대책지역 지원에 관한 법률), and relevant provisions of the Housing Act (주택법) addressing inter-floor noise. The Ministry of Environment (환경부) administers the framework with support from the National Institute of Environmental Research (국립환경과학원).

### Emission Standards by Zone

| Zone Classification | Daytime (06:00-22:00) dB(A) | Nighttime (22:00-06:00) dB(A) |
|--------------------|------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Residential (전용주거지역) | 50 | 40 |
| General residential/commercial | 55 | 45 |
| Commercial (상업지역) | 65 | 55 |
| Industrial (공업지역) | 70 | 60 |

### Inter-Floor Noise (층간소음)

Korea's unique housing landscape -- approximately 60 percent of the population lives in apartment buildings (아파트) -- has made inter-floor noise one of the country's most pressing quality-of-life issues. In response:

- The Housing Act establishes inter-floor noise standards: impact noise (충격소음) must not exceed 43 dB(A) for lightweight impacts and 50 dB(A) for heavyweight impacts
- The Korea Environment Corporation operates the Inter-Floor Noise Neighborhood Center (층간소음 이웃사이센터), providing free mediation and measurement services
- Reports exceed 40,000 annually, with several high-profile criminal cases involving violence triggered by noise disputes

### Environmental Dispute Coordination

The Environmental Dispute Coordination Commission (환경분쟁조정위원회) under the Environmental Dispute Coordination Act (환경분쟁 조정법) provides a specialized administrative dispute resolution mechanism for noise complaints. The Commission handles mediation (조정), conciliation (알선), and adjudication (재정), offering a faster and less costly alternative to court litigation. Noise-related disputes constitute the largest category of cases processed.

### Construction Noise

Construction noise is regulated through specific provisions of the Noise and Vibration Control Act, with permitted construction hours typically limited to 06:00-18:00 in residential areas. Special permits may extend hours but require noise mitigation measures and community notification.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The federal Noise Control Act of 1972 (42 U.S.C. Sections 4901-4918) declared a national policy "to promote an environment for all Americans free from noise that jeopardizes their health or welfare." However, the EPA's Office of Noise Abatement and Control was defunded in 1982 under the Reagan administration, and the Act has been effectively dormant since. The statutory authority remains on the books but is not exercised.

### Current Regulatory Framework

| Source | Regulatory Authority | Standard |
|--------|---------------------|----------|
| Aircraft | FAA (14 CFR Part 36; Part 150) | Stage 3/4/5 aircraft noise standards; Airport Noise Compatibility Planning |
| Highway traffic | FHWA (23 CFR 772) | Noise abatement criteria for federally funded highway projects |
| Occupational | OSHA (29 CFR 1910.95) | 90 dB(A) TWA over 8 hours; hearing conservation program at 85 dB(A) |
| Product noise | EPA (42 U.S.C. 4905) | Authority to set noise emission standards for products (largely unused) |
| Railroad | FRA (49 CFR Part 210) | Railroad noise emission standards |

### State and Local Regulation

In the absence of active federal regulation, noise control is primarily a state and local matter. Most municipalities have noise ordinances establishing:
- Permitted noise levels by zone and time of day
- Construction hour restrictions
- Vehicle noise limits
- Nuisance noise provisions

New York City's Noise Code (Title 24, Chapter 2) is one of the most detailed, with specific decibel limits for commercial music, construction, and mechanical equipment.

### Airport Noise and Capacity Act of 1990

This Act preempts state and local regulation of aircraft noise, establishing federal authority over noise and access restrictions at airports. The Act requires the FAA to approve any airport noise restriction through a notice-and-comment process, preventing airports from unilaterally imposing curfews or fleet restrictions.

### Quiet Communities Act Proposals

The Quiet Communities Act has been introduced in multiple Congressional sessions, proposing to reauthorize and fund the EPA's noise control programs. It has not been enacted.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany has one of the world's most comprehensive noise protection frameworks, centered on the Bundes-Immissionsschutzgesetz (BImSchG) and its implementing regulations and technical guidance.

### TA Lärm (Technische Anleitung zum Schutz gegen Lärm)

The TA Lärm establishes binding noise immission values for industrial and commercial facilities:

| Area Category | Daytime dB(A) | Nighttime dB(A) |
|--------------|---------------|-----------------|
| Industrial areas | 70 | 70 |
| Commercial areas (Gewerbegebiete) | 65 | 50 |
| Core/mixed areas (Kern-/Mischgebiete) | 60 | 45 |
| General residential (Allgemeine Wohngebiete) | 55 | 40 |
| Pure residential (Reine Wohngebiete) | 50 | 35 |
| Health resorts/hospitals (Kurgebiete) | 45 | 35 |

### Traffic Noise (16th BImSchV)

The Verkehrslärmschutzverordnung (16th BImSchV) establishes noise limits triggered by the construction or significant modification of roads and railways:

| Area Category | Day dB(A) | Night dB(A) |
|--------------|-----------|-------------|
| Hospitals, schools | 57 | 47 |
| Pure/general residential | 59 | 49 |
| Core/mixed/village areas | 64 | 54 |
| Commercial areas | 69 | 59 |

When limits are exceeded, passive noise protection (sound insulation) or active measures (noise barriers) must be implemented at the road/rail authority's expense.

### Aircraft Noise (Fluglärmschutzgesetz)

The Fluglärmschutzgesetz (FlugLärmG), revised in 2007, establishes noise protection zones around airports with building restrictions and sound insulation requirements. The Act defines two protection zones with day and night criteria based on equivalent continuous sound levels.

### Noise Mapping

Germany implements the EU Environmental Noise Directive through the 34th BImSchV, requiring strategic noise maps and action plans for major agglomerations (>100,000 inhabitants), major roads (>3 million vehicles/year), major railways (>30,000 trains/year), and major airports (>50,000 movements/year).

## ECHR & EU

### Environmental Noise Directive (2002/49/EC)

The END establishes a common approach to avoiding, preventing, and reducing the harmful effects of environmental noise:

- **Noise Mapping:** Member states must produce strategic noise maps for major agglomerations, roads, railways, and airports using harmonized noise indicators (Lden for day-evening-night and Lnight for nighttime)
- **Action Plans:** Based on noise maps, authorities must develop noise action plans to manage noise issues and reduce exposure
- **Public Information:** Noise maps and action plans must be made publicly available
- **No Binding Limit Values:** The Directive does not establish EU-wide noise limit values, leaving this to member state discretion

The Commission has considered but not yet proposed binding EU noise limit values, which would represent a significant step beyond the current mapping and planning framework.

### EU Aircraft Noise Regulation (598/2014)

Regulation (EU) 598/2014 establishes rules for the introduction of noise-related operating restrictions at EU airports, implementing the ICAO Balanced Approach to Aircraft Noise Management (noise reduction at source, land-use planning, noise abatement procedures, operating restrictions as a last resort).

### ECHR Jurisprudence

Noise pollution has generated significant ECHR case law under Article 8:

**Powell and Rayner v. United Kingdom (Application No. 9310/81, 1990):** Early case recognizing that aircraft noise from Heathrow Airport constituted interference with Article 8 rights, though finding the interference justified given the economic importance of the airport.

**Hatton and Others v. United Kingdom (Grand Chamber, 2003):** Concerning night flights at Heathrow, the Grand Chamber held that Article 8 requires states to strike a fair balance between the economic interests of the airport and the rights of affected residents. The state's margin of appreciation was wide, but procedural requirements (including environmental assessment) must be met.

**Moreno Gómez v. Spain (Application No. 4143/02, 2004):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where Spanish authorities failed to enforce noise regulations in a residential area designated as an "acoustically saturated zone," allowing nightlife noise levels of 100+ dB(A).

**Deés v. Hungary (Application No. 2345/06, 2010):** Violation of Article 8 found where heavy traffic was diverted through a residential street causing excessive noise without adequate mitigation.

**Zammit Maempel v. Malta (Application No. 24202/10, 2011):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 due to the proximity of the applicant's home to a major fireworks factory, with noise from regular fireworks testing constituting interference with private life.

## Comparative Analysis

### Federal vs. Local Regulation

The most striking divergence is between the United States' effectively dormant federal noise program and the comprehensive approaches of other jurisdictions. Germany and the EU provide detailed, binding noise standards at the national and supranational levels. South Korea maintains a centralized framework with national standards. The U.S. relegation of noise control to local government has resulted in highly uneven protection.

### Source-Specific Approaches

All jurisdictions regulate specific noise sources differently. Aircraft noise is universally subject to the strictest regulation due to its intensity and geographic impact. Industrial and construction noise receive detailed treatment in Germany (TA Lärm) and South Korea. Traffic noise is most comprehensively addressed by Germany's 16th BImSchV.

### Unique Challenges

South Korea's inter-floor noise regulation addresses a challenge unique to its high-density apartment living culture, with no real parallel in the other jurisdictions. Germany's noise protection framework is the most technically detailed, reflecting its engineering tradition. The EU's mapping approach provides data-driven policy without setting binding limits.

### Health-Based Standards

The WHO Environmental Noise Guidelines (2018) recommend significantly lower noise exposure levels than most jurisdictions enforce (e.g., Lden of 53 dB(A) for road traffic noise), suggesting that current standards in all jurisdictions may be insufficiently protective of public health.
