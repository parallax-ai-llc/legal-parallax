## Summary

Bank fraud -- the deliberate use of deception, misrepresentation, or other unlawful means to obtain money, assets, or other property held by a financial institution, or to defraud a bank's customers through the bank's systems -- is a serious criminal offense in every major legal system, yet the scope of its definition, the severity of its penalties, and the procedural mechanisms for prosecution vary significantly across jurisdictions. South Korea criminalizes bank fraud primarily through the general fraud provision of Article 347 of the Criminal Act, supplemented by the Act on the Aggravated Punishment of Specific Economic Crimes, which imposes escalating penalties up to life imprisonment for fraud exceeding 5 billion won. The United States targets bank fraud through a dedicated federal statute, 18 U.S.C. section 1344, enacted in 1984, which carries penalties of up to 30 years imprisonment and $1,000,000 in fines per count. Germany prosecutes bank fraud under the general fraud provision of section 263 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), with penalties of up to 5 years for basic fraud and up to 10 years for aggravated or gang-related fraud. At the supranational level, the European Union has established a layered framework through the PIF Directive (Directive 2017/1371), the Non-Cash Payments Fraud Directive (Directive 2019/713), and the Anti-Money Laundering Directives, while the European Court of Human Rights enforces procedural safeguards through Article 6 (right to a fair trial) and Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (protection of property) that constrain how Member States may investigate and prosecute financial crime.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Bank fraud in South Korea is prosecuted primarily under the general fraud provision of the Criminal Act (형법), with enhanced penalties available under the Act on the Aggravated Punishment of Specific Economic Crimes (특정경제범죄 가중처벌 등에 관한 법률).

**Article 347 of the Criminal Act** provides that a person who defrauds another, thereby obtaining property or pecuniary advantage, shall be punished by imprisonment with labor for not more than 10 years or by a fine not exceeding 20 million won. Article 347-2 separately addresses computer fraud, covering cases where a person acquires property benefits by inputting false information or improper commands into a data-processing system, carrying the same maximum penalty of 10 years imprisonment or 20 million won fine.

**The Act on the Aggravated Punishment of Specific Economic Crimes (Article 3)** imposes escalating penalties based on the amount of illicit gain:

| Illicit Gain | Penalty |
|-------------|---------|
| Under 500 million won | Up to 10 years imprisonment (per Criminal Act Art. 347) |
| 500 million won to under 5 billion won | Imprisonment of at least 3 years |
| 5 billion won or more | Life imprisonment, or imprisonment of at least 5 years |

### Elements of the Offense

To establish bank fraud under Korean law, the prosecution must prove:

1. **Deception (기망행위):** The accused misled a financial institution or its customers through false representations, misleading conduct, or deliberate omission where a duty to disclose existed.
2. **Disposal of Property (재산처분행위):** The bank or victim disposed of funds, extended credit, or transferred other financial assets as a direct result of the deception.
3. **Obtaining Gain (재산취득):** The accused obtained money, property, or pecuniary advantage as a result of the disposal.
4. **Intent (고의):** The accused acted with the deliberate intention to defraud, with awareness that the representations were false.

### Financial Institution Employee Offenses

The Specific Economic Crimes Act also targets employees of financial institutions who accept, demand, or promise to receive money, valuables, or other benefits in connection with their duties. Executives and employees of financial services companies caught receiving or requesting 30 million won or more in job-related bribes are subject to aggravated punishment proportional to the amount received.

### Landmark Cases

**Woori Bank Embezzlement Case (2025):** The Supreme Court of Korea finalized criminal sentences in a case involving approximately 70 billion won in embezzlement from Woori Bank. The principal defendants were sentenced to 15 years and 12 years imprisonment respectively, both convicted under the Act on the Aggravated Punishment of Specific Economic Crimes.

**Daewoo Group Bank Loan Fraud (1999-2006):** Daewoo founder Kim Woo-choong was convicted of securing billions of dollars in fraudulent bank loans by fabricating accounts and falsifying approximately $43 billion in assets. He was sentenced to 10 years imprisonment and ordered to forfeit $22 billion, making it the largest financial fraud prosecution in Korean history at the time.

### Procedural Features

- The criminal statute of limitations for fraud is 10 years.
- Civil claims for damages arising from bank fraud may be pursued in parallel with criminal prosecution.
- The Financial Services Commission (금융위원회) and Financial Supervisory Service (금융감독원) play regulatory roles in detecting and referring bank fraud for criminal prosecution.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States is the only jurisdiction among the four compared that has a dedicated federal bank fraud statute. **18 U.S.C. section 1344**, enacted as part of the Comprehensive Crime Control Act of 1984, makes it a federal crime to knowingly execute or attempt to execute a scheme or artifice:

1. To defraud a financial institution; or
2. To obtain any of the moneys, funds, credits, assets, securities, or other property owned by, or under the custody or control of, a financial institution, by means of false or fraudulent pretenses, representations, or promises.

The statute applies to any scheme targeting a federally chartered or insured financial institution, giving it extremely broad reach across the American banking system.

### Elements of the Offense

Under subsection (1), the government must prove:
- The defendant knowingly executed or attempted to execute a scheme or artifice to defraud a financial institution.
- The financial institution was federally chartered or insured.

Under subsection (2), the government must prove:
- The defendant knowingly executed or attempted to execute a scheme to obtain bank property.
- The scheme involved false or fraudulent pretenses, representations, or promises.
- The property was owned by or under the custody or control of a federally insured financial institution.

Importantly, following the Supreme Court's rulings, subsection (2) does not require proof that the defendant intended to defraud the bank itself -- only that the defendant used false pretenses to obtain property held by the bank.

### Penalties

| Offense | Maximum Imprisonment | Maximum Fine |
|---------|---------------------|-------------|
| Bank fraud (section 1344) | 30 years | $1,000,000 |
| Conspiracy to commit bank fraud (section 1349) | 30 years | $1,000,000 |
| Bank fraud with identity theft enhancement | 30 years + 2 years consecutive | $1,000,000 |

In addition, courts may order restitution under the Mandatory Victims Restitution Act (18 U.S.C. section 3663A), disgorgement of profits, and forfeiture of proceeds under 18 U.S.C. section 981.

### Landmark Cases

**Loughrin v. United States, 573 U.S. 351 (2014):** The Supreme Court unanimously held that a conviction under section 1344(2) does not require the government to prove that the defendant intended to defraud a financial institution. The defendant had stolen checks from mailboxes, altered them, and used them to make purchases at Target stores, then returned the items for cash. The Court ruled that the "by means of" language in subsection (2) limits the statute's reach to cases where the misrepresentation has some real connection to a federally insured bank, satisfying the federal interest requirement.

**Shaw v. United States, 580 U.S. 63 (2016):** The Supreme Court clarified that a scheme to defraud a bank's customer is inherently a scheme to defraud the bank under section 1344(1), because the bank has a property interest in its customers' deposits. The defendant had obtained a customer's bank account information and transferred funds to accounts he controlled. The Court held that the defendant need not have specifically intended to harm the bank, as the bank's property interest was sufficient.

**United States v. Yates (9th Cir., 2021):** The Ninth Circuit rejected the government's theory that depriving a bank of "accurate information" or the continued payment of salaries and bonuses constituted a deprivation of "something of value" under the bank fraud statute, narrowing the scope of section 1344 in the circuit.

### Regulatory and Enforcement Framework

Bank fraud investigations are typically conducted by the Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI) and prosecuted by the U.S. Department of Justice (DOJ). The Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (OCC), the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC), and the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) serve as regulatory bodies that may refer cases for criminal prosecution. The Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN) monitors suspicious activity reports filed by banks under the Bank Secrecy Act.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany does not have a dedicated bank fraud statute. Bank fraud is prosecuted under the general fraud provision of **section 263 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB)**, which provides:

> Whosoever with the intention of obtaining an unlawful pecuniary advantage for themselves or a third party damages the assets of another by creating or maintaining an error by false pretences or by distorting or suppressing facts incurs a penalty of imprisonment for not more than five years or a fine.

Additional provisions may apply depending on the specific conduct:

- **Section 263a StGB (Computer Fraud / Computerbetrug):** Covers cases where the perpetrator influences the result of a data-processing operation through incorrect configuration of a program, use of incorrect or incomplete data, unauthorized use of data, or other unauthorized influence on the processing operation, to obtain an unlawful pecuniary advantage.
- **Section 264 StGB (Subsidy Fraud):** Applies where the fraud involves government subsidies channeled through banking institutions.
- **Section 265b StGB (Credit Fraud / Kreditbetrug):** Specifically criminalizes submitting false or incomplete documents or making false statements to obtain credit for a business or enterprise.

### Elements of the Offense

German fraud law requires a strict four-element causal chain:

1. **Deception (Tauschung):** The perpetrator must have created or maintained an error through false pretenses or by distorting or suppressing facts.
2. **Error (Irrtum):** The victim (bank employee, automated system under section 263a) must have developed or maintained an incorrect belief about the relevant facts.
3. **Property Disposition (Vermogensverfugung):** The victim must have disposed of property as a direct result of the error -- for example, approving a loan, transferring funds, or granting credit.
4. **Financial Loss (Vermogensschaden):** The property disposition must have caused an immediate financial loss to the victim or the institution they represent.

The mental element requires **intent (Vorsatz)** with respect to all objective elements plus the **intention to obtain an unlawful pecuniary advantage (Bereicherungsabsicht)** for the perpetrator or a third party.

### Penalties

| Category | Penalty Range |
|----------|-------------|
| Basic fraud (section 263(1)) | Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine |
| Aggravated fraud (section 263(3)) | 6 months to 10 years imprisonment |
| Commercial or gang-related fraud (section 263(5)) | 1 to 10 years imprisonment |
| Credit fraud (section 265b) | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine |

Aggravated cases under section 263(3) include fraud involving assets of great value, fraud that places the victim in economic hardship, fraud committed by a public official abusing their position, and fraud that simulates an insured event.

Fines in Germany are calculated under the daily rate system (Tagessatzsystem): the court determines a number of daily rates (up to 360) and a daily rate amount based on the offender's income, resulting in fines that are proportional to both the gravity of the offense and the offender's financial capacity.

### Landmark Cases

**Wirecard Scandal (2020-present):** Germany's largest post-war financial fraud case involved Wirecard AG, a Munich-based payment processing company that fabricated EUR 1.9 billion in assets. CEO Markus Braun was arrested in June 2020 and faced trial beginning in December 2022 on charges including organized fraud, falsification of financial statements, market manipulation, and defrauding banks of approximately EUR 1.75 billion. Former COO Jan Marsalek fled to Belarus and remains on Europol's most wanted list with an Interpol Red Notice. The case exposed severe regulatory failures at the Federal Financial Supervisory Authority (BaFin) and prompted sweeping reforms to German financial oversight.

**Flowtex Scandal (1994-2000):** Flowtex Technologie, based in Ettlingen, defrauded German banks of approximately EUR 1.5 billion through a leasing scheme involving fictitious horizontal drilling machines. Company founder Manfred Schmider was convicted of fraud and sentenced to over 11 years imprisonment. The case was one of the largest bank fraud prosecutions in German history prior to Wirecard.

### Procedural Features

- The statute of limitations for basic fraud is 5 years; for aggravated fraud, it is 10 years.
- Prosecution is carried out by the Staatsanwaltschaft (State Prosecutor's Office), with specialized economic crime divisions (Schwerpunktstaatsanwaltschaften fur Wirtschaftskriminalitat) handling complex bank fraud cases.
- The Federal Financial Supervisory Authority (BaFin) and the Deutsche Bundesbank provide regulatory oversight and may refer matters for criminal investigation.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Legislative Framework

The European Union addresses bank fraud through a multi-layered framework of directives and regulations that establish minimum standards for Member States:

**Directive (EU) 2017/1371 -- PIF Directive (Protection of EU Financial Interests):**
Adopted on 5 July 2017, this directive harmonizes definitions of criminal offenses affecting the EU's financial interests and establishes minimum penalties. It covers fraud (including cross-border VAT fraud involving total damage of at least EUR 10 million), corruption, money laundering, and misappropriation. Member States must provide for a maximum penalty of at least 4 years of imprisonment where the financial damage to the EU budget exceeds EUR 100,000.

**Directive (EU) 2019/713 -- Non-Cash Payments Fraud Directive:**
Adopted on 17 April 2019, this directive criminalizes fraud and counterfeiting of non-cash means of payment, including bank cards, electronic transfers, and virtual currencies. It requires Member States to ensure that:
- Fraudulent use of non-cash payment instruments is punishable by a maximum term of imprisonment of at least 2 years.
- Offenses committed within the framework of a criminal organization carry aggravated penalties.
- Both attempt and incitement are punishable.
- Legal persons can be held liable for offenses committed for their benefit.

**Anti-Money Laundering Directives (AMLD):**
The EU's successive Anti-Money Laundering Directives (most recently the 6th AMLD, effective December 2020) require Member States to criminalize money laundering -- which frequently accompanies bank fraud -- and impose obligations on financial institutions to detect and report suspicious transactions. The framework establishes know-your-customer (KYC) requirements, suspicious activity reporting, and beneficial ownership transparency.

### European Public Prosecutor's Office (EPPO)

Established by Regulation (EU) 2017/1939, the EPPO became operational on 1 June 2021 as the first EU body authorized to conduct criminal investigations and prosecute fraud affecting the EU's financial interests. The EPPO has competence over offenses defined in the PIF Directive, including fraud, money laundering of fraud proceeds, and corruption connected to EU funds. As of 2026, 24 EU Member States participate in the EPPO.

### ECHR Protections

The European Convention on Human Rights provides procedural safeguards that constrain how Member States investigate and prosecute bank fraud:

**Article 6 -- Right to a Fair Trial:**
Any person charged with a bank fraud offense is entitled to a fair and public hearing within a reasonable time by an independent and impartial tribunal. In the context of financial crime, Article 6 requires:
- The presumption of innocence (Art. 6(2)).
- Adequate time and facilities to prepare a defense, particularly important in complex financial cases involving voluminous evidence.
- The right to examine witnesses and obtain expert evidence.
- The right to legal assistance.

The ECHR has held that requirements of a fair hearing are stricter in criminal proceedings than in civil matters, and has applied Article 6's criminal head to penalties imposed by administrative authorities with powers in economic and financial spheres (Guisset v. France).

**Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 -- Protection of Property:**
This provision protects individuals and entities from arbitrary deprivation of property, constraining the use of asset freezing, confiscation, and forfeiture orders in bank fraud prosecutions. States must demonstrate that any interference with property rights is lawful, pursues a legitimate aim, and is proportionate.

**Ne Bis In Idem (Article 4 of Protocol No. 7):**
This principle prohibits double prosecution and punishment for the same offense. In the context of bank fraud, it is particularly relevant where both administrative penalties (imposed by financial regulators) and criminal sanctions may apply to the same conduct. The ECHR's Grand Chamber has developed criteria for assessing whether administrative and criminal proceedings are sufficiently connected to be treated as a single integrated response rather than prohibited double jeopardy.

### Key ECHR Case Law

**Salabiaku v. France (1988):** The ECHR established that presumptions of fact or law in fraud cases are permissible under Article 6(2) only if confined within reasonable limits that take into account the importance of what is at stake and maintain the rights of the defense.

**Guisset v. France (2000):** The Court confirmed that Article 6's criminal protections apply to proceedings before financial and budgetary courts imposing penalties for mismanagement of public funds, extending fair trial guarantees to a broader range of financial misconduct proceedings.

**Zolotukhin v. Russia (2009):** The Grand Chamber clarified the ne bis in idem principle under Protocol No. 7, holding that the prohibition applies to prosecution for an offense arising from identical facts or facts which are substantially the same, regardless of how the offense is legally classified -- directly relevant to cases where bank fraud conduct triggers both regulatory and criminal proceedings.

## Comparative Analysis

### Definitional Approaches

The four jurisdictions take distinctly different approaches to defining bank fraud. The **United States** stands alone in having a dedicated bank fraud statute (18 U.S.C. section 1344) that specifically targets schemes to defraud financial institutions, reflecting the federal government's particular interest in protecting the federally insured banking system. **South Korea** and **Germany** both rely on general fraud provisions in their criminal codes (Article 347 and section 263 StGB respectively), with supplementary legislation addressing aggravated economic crimes. The **EU** takes a harmonization approach, establishing minimum standards through directives that Member States must implement into national law, while the **ECHR** provides a rights-based framework that constrains prosecution methods rather than defining substantive offenses.

### Severity of Penalties

Penalty severity varies substantially across jurisdictions:

| Jurisdiction | Minimum for Major Cases | Maximum |
|-------------|------------------------|---------|
| South Korea | 3 years (over 500M won) | Life imprisonment |
| United States | Sentencing Guidelines dependent | 30 years per count |
| Germany | 6 months (aggravated) | 10 years |
| EU (PIF Directive) | N/A | At least 4 years maximum (where loss exceeds EUR 100,000) |

South Korea imposes the most severe potential penalties, with life imprisonment available for fraud exceeding 5 billion won. The United States provides for up to 30 years per count, meaning that defendants facing multiple counts can receive effectively life-equivalent sentences. Germany's penalties are notably more moderate, reflecting the continental European tradition of proportionality in sentencing. The EU's PIF Directive establishes only a floor for maximum penalties, leaving Member States to set higher limits.

### Procedural Differences

The American system relies heavily on plea bargaining, with the vast majority of federal bank fraud cases resolved through guilty pleas, often in exchange for cooperation agreements. South Korea's system, while also permitting plea negotiations, places greater emphasis on judicial fact-finding. Germany's inquisitorial system gives the presiding judge an active role in investigating the facts, and settlements (Verstandigungen) are possible but subject to strict procedural requirements. The EU framework introduces the unique institution of the EPPO for cross-border cases affecting EU financial interests, while the ECHR ensures that all proceedings comply with fundamental fair trial guarantees.

### Cross-Border Cooperation

Bank fraud increasingly involves cross-border elements, requiring international cooperation:

- **South Korea** participates in mutual legal assistance treaties (MLATs) and is a member of the Egmont Group of Financial Intelligence Units.
- **The United States** employs extraterritorial jurisdiction over any fraud involving federally insured institutions and maintains an extensive network of MLATs and extradition treaties.
- **Germany** cooperates through EU mechanisms (European Arrest Warrant, European Investigation Order) as well as bilateral treaties.
- **The EU** has established the EPPO, Eurojust, and Europol as institutional mechanisms for cross-border financial crime cooperation, while the ECHR ensures that cooperation mechanisms respect fundamental rights.
