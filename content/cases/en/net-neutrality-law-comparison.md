## Summary

Net neutrality -- the principle that internet service providers (ISPs) must treat all internet traffic equally, without blocking, throttling, or paid prioritization of specific content, applications, or services -- has been one of the most fiercely contested technology policy issues of the twenty-first century, touching on fundamental questions about internet freedom, market competition, investment incentives, and the role of telecommunications regulation in the digital economy. South Korea enshrined net neutrality principles in the Telecommunications Business Act (전기통신사업법) and the Guidelines on Net Neutrality and Internet Traffic Management (망 중립성 및 인터넷 트래픽 관리에 관한 가이드라인) issued by the Korea Communications Commission (방송통신위원회, KCC) in 2011 and revised in 2020, but enforcement has been complicated by the country's unique network usage fee disputes, particularly the conflict between content providers (notably Netflix) and ISPs (notably SK Broadband) that resulted in a landmark Seoul Central District Court ruling in 2021 that Netflix had an obligation to negotiate network usage fees with SK Broadband. The United States has experienced dramatic regulatory oscillation on net neutrality: the FCC adopted Open Internet rules in 2010 (partially struck down in Verizon v. FCC, 2014), reclassified broadband as a Title II common carrier service and adopted strong net neutrality rules in 2015, reversed course in 2017 under the Restoring Internet Freedom Order reclassifying broadband as a Title I information service, and restored net neutrality rules in 2024 by reclassifying broadband under Title II again, though this restoration faces judicial challenge. Germany and the EU adopted binding net neutrality rules through the Open Internet Regulation (Regulation 2015/2120), which prohibits blocking, throttling, and paid prioritization with limited exceptions, enforced by BEREC guidelines and national regulatory authorities, creating a more stable regulatory environment than the U.S. experience.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's net neutrality framework combines statutory provisions in the Telecommunications Business Act (전기통신사업법, Act No. 19953) with regulatory guidelines issued by the Korea Communications Commission (방송통신위원회, KCC).

### KCC Net Neutrality Guidelines

The KCC issued the Guidelines on Net Neutrality and Internet Traffic Management (망 중립성 및 인터넷 트래픽 관리에 관한 가이드라인) in December 2011, revised in 2020. Key principles:

| Principle | Content |
|-----------|---------|
| No blocking (차단 금지) | ISPs shall not block lawful content, applications, services, or non-harmful devices |
| No unreasonable discrimination (불합리한 차별 금지) | ISPs shall not unreasonably discriminate against or in favor of specific content |
| Transparency (투명성) | ISPs must disclose traffic management practices, network performance, and service conditions |
| Reasonable traffic management | ISPs may engage in reasonable network management for security, congestion relief, and legal compliance |

### Network Usage Fee Controversy

South Korea's net neutrality landscape has been uniquely shaped by the network usage fee (망 사용료) dispute:

**SK Broadband v. Netflix (Seoul Central District Court, 2021):** SK Broadband sued Netflix for network usage costs, arguing that Netflix's substantial traffic (estimated at 4.8% of total Korean internet traffic in 2020) imposed significant costs on SK Broadband's network. The Seoul Central District Court ruled in June 2021 that Netflix had a duty to negotiate network usage fees with SK Broadband, based on the principle that content providers benefiting from network infrastructure should contribute to its costs. The Seoul High Court upheld this ruling in December 2022.

This decision diverges sharply from the global consensus against content provider network fees and has been criticized as undermining net neutrality principles by effectively allowing ISPs to charge content providers for network access.

### Interconnection Regulation

Korea's interconnection framework under the Telecommunications Business Act distinguishes between:
- **Tier 1 ISPs:** Required to interconnect and establish interconnection terms
- **Content providers:** May be required to pay transit/peering fees
- **Over-the-top (OTT) providers:** Subject to increasing regulatory requirements

## United States

### Legal Framework

U.S. net neutrality has experienced a decade of regulatory instability driven by the classification question: whether broadband internet access service is a "telecommunications service" under Title II of the Communications Act (subject to common carrier regulation) or an "information service" under Title I (subject to lighter regulation).

### Regulatory History

| Year | Action | Classification | Key Features |
|------|--------|---------------|-------------|
| 2010 | Open Internet Order | Title I + ancillary authority | No blocking, no unreasonable discrimination, transparency |
| 2014 | Verizon v. FCC, 740 F.3d 623 (D.C. Cir.) | Title I authority insufficient | Court strikes down no-blocking and no-discrimination rules |
| 2015 | Open Internet Order | Title II (reclassification) | Strong no blocking, no throttling, no paid prioritization |
| 2016 | United States Telecom Assn v. FCC, 825 F.3d 674 | Title II upheld (en banc) | D.C. Circuit upholds 2015 rules |
| 2017 | Restoring Internet Freedom Order | Title I (reclassification back) | Eliminated net neutrality rules; transparency retained |
| 2024 | Safeguarding and Securing the Open Internet Order | Title II (reclassification) | Restored no blocking, no throttling, no paid prioritization |

### 2024 Restoration

The FCC adopted the Safeguarding and Securing the Open Internet Order on April 25, 2024, reclassifying broadband as a Title II service and restoring the three bright-line rules. The order was challenged in the Sixth Circuit (Broadband ISP Association v. FCC), with the court issuing a stay in August 2024. The case raises the question of whether the major questions doctrine (as articulated in West Virginia v. EPA) limits the FCC's authority to classify broadband.

### State-Level Actions

Several states enacted their own net neutrality laws during the 2017-2024 period when federal rules were absent:
- **California SB 822 (2018):** Comprehensive state net neutrality law; enforcement was initially deferred during federal litigation
- **Washington HB 2282 (2018):** First state to enact net neutrality legislation
- **Vermont, Oregon, Hawaii:** Executive orders or legislation addressing net neutrality

### Key Cases

**Verizon v. FCC, 740 F.3d 623 (D.C. Cir. 2014):** The court struck down the FCC's 2010 anti-blocking and anti-discrimination rules, holding that imposing common carrier-type obligations on broadband providers classified as information services exceeded the FCC's authority. This decision precipitated the 2015 Title II reclassification.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany applies the EU Open Internet Regulation (2015/2120) directly, enforced by the Bundesnetzagentur (BNetzA, Federal Network Agency). The German Telekommunikationsgesetz (TKG, Telecommunications Act), revised in 2021 to transpose the European Electronic Communications Code, provides supplementary provisions.

### Bundesnetzagentur Enforcement

The BNetzA has actively enforced net neutrality rules:

- Investigations of zero-rating practices by Deutsche Telekom (StreamOn) and Vodafone (Vodafone Pass), resulting in discontinuation of these services following CJEU rulings
- Monitoring of traffic management practices through regular transparency reports
- Measurement tools (Breitbandmessung) allowing consumers to verify whether their actual broadband speeds match advertised speeds

### Zero-Rating Enforcement

Deutsche Telekom's StreamOn service, which zero-rated (exempted from data caps) video and music streaming from participating providers, was challenged before German courts. Following the CJEU's Telenor decisions (see below), Deutsche Telekom discontinued StreamOn in April 2022, and Vodafone discontinued Vodafone Pass in the same period.

### Broadband as Essential Service

German law increasingly treats broadband as an essential service. The revised TKG (2021) establishes a right to a minimum broadband connection (Recht auf Versorgung mit Telekommunikationsdiensten) under Section 157, with minimum download speeds determined by the BNetzA.

### "Fair Share" Debate

The EU-wide debate about whether large content providers should contribute to network infrastructure costs ("fair share" or "sending party network pays") has been particularly active in Germany. Deutsche Telekom and other European incumbents have lobbied for content provider payments. The European Commission conducted a consultation in 2023 but has not proposed legislation, and the European Parliament expressed opposition to mandating content provider network fees.

## ECHR & EU

### Open Internet Regulation (2015/2120)

The EU Open Internet Regulation, adopted on November 25, 2015, establishes directly applicable net neutrality rules across all 27 member states:

**Article 3(1):** End-users have the right to access and distribute information and content, use and provide applications and services, and use terminal equipment of their choice.

**Article 3(3):** ISPs must treat all traffic equally "when providing internet access services, without discrimination, restriction or interference, and irrespective of the sender and receiver, the content accessed or distributed, the applications or services used or provided, or the terminal equipment used."

**Exceptions:** Traffic management measures are permitted for: compliance with legislation or court orders, preservation of network integrity and security, and prevention of impending congestion, provided they are transparent, non-discriminatory, proportionate, and not based on commercial considerations.

**Article 3(5) -- Specialized Services:** ISPs may offer services other than internet access that require a specific level of quality ("specialized services"), provided this does not impair the general quality of internet access.

### BEREC Guidelines

The Body of European Regulators for Electronic Communications (BEREC) published detailed guidelines on the implementation of the Open Internet Regulation, most recently updated in 2022. The guidelines provide national regulatory authorities with a common framework for assessing ISP practices including traffic management, zero-rating, specialized services, and quality of service.

### CJEU Zero-Rating Decisions

**Telenor Magyarország (Joined Cases C-807/18 and C-39/19, 2020):** The CJEU held that zero-rating practices (where ISPs exempt certain content from data caps) violate the Open Internet Regulation because they draw a distinction in traffic management based on commercial considerations. After data cap exhaustion, non-zero-rated traffic is blocked or throttled, which constitutes discrimination prohibited by Article 3(3).

**Deutsche Telekom (Joined Cases C-339/20 and C-340/20, 2022):** Confirmed that zero-rating combined with throttling of non-zero-rated traffic after data cap depletion violates the Regulation.

### Digital Single Market Context

Net neutrality regulation operates within the broader EU Digital Single Market strategy, alongside the European Electronic Communications Code (Directive 2018/1972), the Digital Services Act, the Digital Markets Act, and the Data Act.

### ECHR and Internet Access

While the ECHR has not directly addressed net neutrality, the Court has recognized the importance of internet access:

**Ahmet Yıldırım v. Turkey (Application No. 3111/10, 2012):** The Court found that Turkey's blanket blocking of Google Sites violated Article 10 (freedom of expression), establishing that internet blocking measures must be targeted and proportionate.

**Cengiz and Others v. Turkey (Application No. 48226/10, 2015):** The Court found that Turkey's blocking of YouTube violated Article 10, reinforcing that access to internet platforms is protected under freedom of expression.

## Comparative Analysis

### Regulatory Stability

The EU's approach through a directly applicable regulation has provided the most stable net neutrality framework, with clear rules since 2015. Germany benefits from this stability as an EU member state. South Korea's guideline-based approach provides flexibility but less legal certainty. The United States' experience of repeated reclassification and rule reversal has created the most unstable regulatory environment, undermining both ISP investment planning and content provider expectations.

### Network Usage Fees

The most significant policy divergence concerns network usage fees. South Korea's courts have effectively endorsed ISP claims for content provider payments, a position that no other major jurisdiction has adopted. The EU's "fair share" debate remains unresolved but has not resulted in legislation. The United States has not seriously considered mandating content provider fees.

### Zero-Rating

The CJEU's Telenor decisions created a clear prohibition on discriminatory zero-rating in the EU. South Korea assesses zero-rating on a case-by-case basis. The United States has not addressed zero-rating in its 2024 rules, leaving it to case-by-case enforcement.

### Enforcement Effectiveness

The EU's multi-layered enforcement (BEREC guidance, national regulatory authorities, CJEU) provides robust implementation. Germany's BNetzA has been among the most active national enforcers. South Korea's KCC enforcement has been complicated by the network usage fee controversy. U.S. enforcement has been disrupted by repeated regulatory changes.
