## Summary

Foreign direct investment (FDI) regulation across the Global South reveals fundamentally different approaches to balancing capital attraction with economic sovereignty, reflecting each region's colonial history, development model, and geopolitical positioning. China operates a sophisticated negative list system under the Foreign Investment Law (外商投资法, 2020) that progressively liberalizes market access while maintaining strategic sector control through Variable Interest Entity (VIE) structures and national security review. India employs a two-track FDI policy distinguishing automatic route approvals from government-scrutinized sectors, administered through the Department for Promotion of Industry and Internal Trade (DPIIT) under consolidated FDI Policy circulars, with sector-specific caps ranging from 26% to 100%. In Africa, Nigeria's Nigerian Investment Promotion Commission (NIPC) Act 1995 and the Companies and Allied Matters Act (CAMA) 2020 coexist with constitutional provisions reserving certain sectors for Nigerian citizens, while South Africa's Protection of Investment Act 22 of 2015 controversially replaced bilateral investment treaties (BITs) with a domestic statutory framework. Latin American neo-constitutionalism, particularly in Brazil, Mexico, and Colombia, layers FDI regulation within constitutional economic order provisions, with Brazil's notoriously complex regulatory environment requiring Central Bank registration under Resolution No. 4,373/2014 and CVM compliance. Southeast Asia's approach combines Indonesia's Daftar Negatif Investasi (DNI), now replaced by the Presidential Regulation 10/2021 Investment Priority List under the Omnibus Law on Job Creation, with the Philippines' amended Foreign Investments Act (Republic Act No. 7042 as amended by RA 11647) and its Regular Foreign Investment Negative List (RFINL).

## China

### Legal Framework

China's foreign investment regime underwent a fundamental transformation with the Foreign Investment Law of the People's Republic of China (中华人民共和国外商投资法), adopted on March 15, 2019 and effective January 1, 2020, replacing the trio of laws that had governed foreign investment since the 1979 opening: the Sino-Foreign Equity Joint Venture Law (中外合资经营企业法), the Sino-Foreign Cooperative Joint Venture Law (中外合作经营企业法), and the Wholly Foreign-Owned Enterprise Law (外资企业法).

Key statutory provisions include:

- **Article 4**: Establishes pre-establishment national treatment plus negative list management as the foundational regime. Foreign investors and their investments enjoy treatment no less favorable than domestic investors in the investment access phase, except for sectors on the negative list.
- **Article 28**: Creates the foreign investment national security review system, later detailed in the Measures for Security Review of Foreign Investment (Order No. 37, effective January 18, 2021), administered by a working mechanism led by the National Development and Reform Commission (NDRC) and the Ministry of Commerce (MOFCOM).
- **Article 35**: Mandates that the State protects the intellectual property rights of foreign investors and foreign-invested enterprises, prohibiting forced technology transfer through administrative means -- a direct response to longstanding US and EU complaints under the WTO framework.
- **Article 22**: Requires that foreign-invested enterprises in negative list sectors comply with equity ratio, senior management, and other conditions specified in the Negative List.

The 2024 Special Administrative Measures for Foreign Investment Access (Negative List) reduced restricted items to 29 for nationwide application and 27 for Free Trade Zones (FTZs), down from 93 items in 2017. Manufacturing was fully liberalized in 2022 with the removal of the final restrictions on automobile manufacturing equity caps. Remaining restricted sectors include traditional Chinese medicine, radio and television, basic telecommunications, and mining of rare earths.

### VIE Structures

The Variable Interest Entity (VIE) structure remains one of the most consequential and legally ambiguous mechanisms in Chinese FDI law. Developed initially by Sina Corporation for its 2000 NASDAQ listing, VIE structures allow foreign investors to gain economic exposure to sectors on the negative list -- particularly value-added telecommunications, media, and education -- through a series of contractual arrangements rather than equity ownership. The Wholly Foreign-Owned Enterprise (WFOE) enters into exclusive service agreements, exclusive technology consulting agreements, equity pledge agreements, loan agreements, and call option agreements with a domestic Chinese company that holds the required licenses.

The legal status of VIEs remains formally unresolved. The Foreign Investment Law (2020) Article 2 defines foreign investment to include "investment in any other manner stipulated by laws, administrative regulations, or provisions of the State Council," but implementing regulations have not explicitly addressed VIE structures. The landmark case of Chinachem Financial Services v. China Unicom (2012, Hong Kong Court of Final Appeal) highlighted enforcement risks when the domestic entity repudiated VIE agreements. In 2021-2022, during the regulatory crackdown on platform companies, the China Securities Regulatory Commission (CSRC) issued new overseas listing rules (Trial Administrative Measures of Overseas Securities Offering and Listing by Domestic Companies, effective March 31, 2023) requiring filing with the CSRC for VIE-structured companies, effectively bringing VIEs under regulatory supervision without formally legalizing or prohibiting them.

### Free Trade Zone Innovations

China's 22 Free Trade Zones, beginning with the Shanghai Pilot FTZ established in 2013, serve as laboratories for investment liberalization. The Hainan Free Trade Port Law (海南自由贸易港法, June 10, 2021) created a special regime with a shortened negative list (currently 27 items), zero-tariff on certain goods, and 15% corporate income tax for encouraged industries. Cross-border data transfer pilot programs in the FTZs have also addressed one of the most significant regulatory barriers to foreign investment under the Cybersecurity Law (2017), Data Security Law (2021), and Personal Information Protection Law (2021).

## India

### Legal Framework

India's FDI regime is governed by a layered regulatory architecture anchored in the Foreign Exchange Management Act, 1999 (FEMA), the Consolidated FDI Policy issued by the Department for Promotion of Industry and Internal Trade (DPIIT), and sector-specific regulations enforced by ministries and regulatory bodies including the Reserve Bank of India (RBI), the Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI), the Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority (IRDAI), and the Telecom Regulatory Authority of India (TRAI).

The FDI Policy designates two approval routes:

- **Automatic Route**: FDI up to specified sectoral caps requires no prior government approval. The foreign investor need only comply with post-facto notification to the RBI through the Single Master Form (SMF) filed on the Foreign Investment Reporting and Management System (FIRMS) portal. Sectors under the automatic route include construction development (100%), food processing (100%), IT/BPO (100%), pharmaceuticals (greenfield: 100%; brownfield: 74%), and single-brand retail (up to 100%, subject to local sourcing norms under 30% value addition for entities controlling more than 51%).
- **Government Route**: Sectors requiring prior approval include defence (beyond 74%), broadcasting (49% for news channels), print media (26%), multi-brand retail trading (51%, subject to conditions), and mining of titanium-bearing minerals (100%, with government approval).

### Press Note 3 (2020) and Geopolitical Screening

Following border tensions with China, DPIIT issued Press Note 3 of 2020 (April 17, 2020), amending Paragraph 3.1.1 of the FDI Policy to require prior government approval for FDI from entities in countries sharing a land border with India (China, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Nepal, Myanmar, Bhutan, Afghanistan). This measure specifically targeted Chinese investment, requiring that any "beneficial owner" of an investing entity situated in or a citizen of such countries must obtain government route approval. The provision also covers indirect transfers, meaning that a Singaporean or Cayman Islands entity with significant Chinese beneficial ownership triggers the screening requirement.

Press Note 3 was challenged in Tik Tok Technology India Private Limited v. Union of India but upheld as a valid exercise of sovereign regulatory authority under Article 19(6) of the Constitution.

### Sectoral Complexities

India's approach to multi-brand retail FDI illustrates the political economy of foreign investment regulation. Since 2012, 51% FDI in multi-brand retail has been permitted under the government route, subject to minimum investment of USD 100 million, 50% investment in backend infrastructure within three years, and mandatory 30% sourcing from Indian MSMEs. However, because retail is a State subject under the Seventh Schedule of the Constitution, implementation requires state government concurrence, and several states governed by opposition parties have declined to permit multi-brand retail FDI entry -- effectively creating a patchwork of sub-national FDI regimes.

The landmark case of Walmart's USD 16 billion acquisition of a 77% stake in Flipkart (2018) exposed gaps in FDI enforcement for e-commerce. Subsequent amendments to Press Note 2 of 2018 (FDI Policy on e-commerce) prohibited e-commerce entities from exercising ownership over inventory, mandated a marketplace model, and capped purchases from any single vendor at 25% of total sales -- rules that effectively constrained foreign-invested e-commerce platforms.

### India's Model BIT (2015)

Following its termination of 58 bilateral investment treaties beginning in 2016, India adopted a revised Model BIT text (December 2015) that significantly narrowed investor protections: eliminating the most-favored-nation clause, restricting fair and equitable treatment to a closed list of state obligations, requiring exhaustion of local remedies for five years before international arbitration, and excluding taxation measures from treaty coverage. India's experience in White Industries Australia Limited v. Republic of India (UNCITRAL, 2011), where a USD 4.08 million award was rendered against India under the India-Australia BIT, was a catalyst for this reassessment.

## Africa

### Nigeria

Nigeria's foreign investment framework operates through the Nigerian Investment Promotion Commission (NIPC) Act 1995 (Cap N117, LFN 2004), the Companies and Allied Matters Act 2020 (CAMA 2020), and the Foreign Exchange (Monitoring and Miscellaneous Provisions) Act 1995. The NIPC Act established the Nigerian Investment Promotion Commission as a one-stop agency for investment registration and provides guarantees of unconditional repatriation of capital and returns in freely convertible currency (Section 24), protection against nationalization and expropriation except in the national interest with fair compensation (Section 25), and access to dispute resolution under ICSID, UNCITRAL, or any bilateral or multilateral agreement (Section 26).

However, Section 17 of the NIPC Act and the Nigerian Oil and Gas Industry Content Development Act (NOGICD) 2010 impose local content requirements. The NOGICD mandates that Nigerian companies be given first consideration in oil and gas contracts, requires minimum Nigerian content levels (e.g., 80% for engineering design, 90% for fabrication and welding), and established the Nigerian Content Development and Monitoring Board (NCDMB).

The Petroleum Industry Act 2021 (PIA) reformed the upstream, midstream, and downstream oil and gas sector, creating the Nigerian Upstream Petroleum Regulatory Commission (NUPRC) and requiring Host Community Development Trusts funded by 3% of operating expenditure. Foreign participation in upstream petroleum requires operating through a Nigerian-incorporated company.

CAMA 2020 modernized corporate law, permitting single-member companies, limited liability partnerships, and electronic filing through the Corporate Affairs Commission (CAC) portal. Notably, CAMA 2020 Section 839 controversially empowered the Commission (and by extension the Minister) to suspend trustees of not-for-profit organizations, drawing criticism from civil society as potentially enabling political interference in NGO governance.

### South Africa

South Africa's Protection of Investment Act 22 of 2015 (PIA), effective July 13, 2018, marked a deliberate departure from BIT-based investment protection following the landmark case of Piero Foresti, Laura de Carli and Others v. Republic of South Africa (ICSID Case No. ARB(AF)/07/01), where Italian and Luxembourg investors challenged the Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act (MPRDA) 2002 and the Mining Charter's Black Economic Empowerment (BEE) requirements. Although the case was discontinued after a negotiated settlement, it prompted South Africa's comprehensive review and termination of its BIT program.

The PIA provides:

- **Section 6**: Protection against arbitrary deprivation of property, with expropriation only permitted "in the public interest" and "subject to compensation, the amount of which and the time and manner of payment of which have either been agreed to by those affected or decided or approved by a court" -- language drawn from Section 25 of the Constitution.
- **Section 7**: National treatment and no less favorable treatment than domestic investors "in like circumstances," but subject to qualification by development policy objectives.
- **Section 10**: Dispute resolution requiring exhaustion of domestic remedies, with international arbitration only through state-to-state mediation under bilateral agreements.
- **Section 13**: Right of the government to regulate in the public interest, explicitly preserving policy space for BEE, land reform, and other transformation objectives.

The Broad-Based Black Economic Empowerment Act 53 of 2003 (B-BBEE Act), as amended in 2013, and sector-specific transformation charters (Mining Charter III, Financial Sector Code, ICT Charter) create a parallel compliance regime for foreign investors, requiring minimum scores on ownership (25%+ black ownership), management control, skills development, enterprise and supplier development, and socio-economic development elements.

## Latin America

### Brazil

Brazil's FDI regime is characterized by extraordinary regulatory complexity distributed across multiple federal agencies. The foundational statute remains Law 4,131/1962 (Lei do Capital Estrangeiro), as amended, which requires registration of all foreign capital with the Central Bank of Brazil (Banco Central do Brasil, BCB) through the Electronic Declaratory Registration (RDE-IED) system for direct investment and the RDE-ROF system for financial operations. CVM Resolution 13/2020 governs foreign portfolio investment.

Constitutional constraints include:

- **Article 176** of the 1988 Federal Constitution: Mining activities require authorization or concession from the federal government, with participation restricted to Brazilian companies or cooperatives. Constitutional Amendment 6/1995 removed the requirement for majority Brazilian capital but the National Mining Agency (ANM) retains discretionary authority.
- **Article 222**: Media ownership is restricted to native-born or naturalized Brazilians (for at least 10 years) or legal entities constituted under Brazilian law with at least 70% of total and voting capital held by Brazilians.
- **Article 199(3)**: Foreign participation in healthcare is subject to specific legislative conditions, with direct or indirect foreign capital participation prohibited under Law 8,080/1990 (SUS Organic Law), subject to exceptions introduced by Law 13,097/2015 for hospital services, clinical activities, and related support services.

Brazil's "custo Brasil" (Brazil cost) -- the aggregate of tax complexity, labor regulation, infrastructure deficits, and bureaucratic barriers -- is quantified by the World Bank Doing Business methodology, which ranked Brazil 124th of 190 countries in its final 2020 edition. The federal, state, and municipal tax overlap (ICMS, ISS, PIS, COFINS, IPI, IRPJ, CSLL) creates cascading compliance obligations that multiply for foreign-invested entities.

### Mexico

Mexico's Foreign Investment Law (Ley de Inversión Extranjera, 1993), as amended, operates within the USMCA/T-MEC framework, OECD membership obligations, and bilateral investment treaties. The National Commission on Foreign Investment (Comisión Nacional de Inversiones Extranjeras, CNIE) administers sector-specific restrictions including: 10% in cooperative production societies; 25% in domestic air transport, air taxi, and specialized air transport; 49% in explosives manufacturing, firearms, printing of currency, port administration, and satellite communications; and various restrictions in energy and hydrocarbons following the 2013-2014 Energy Reform.

The 2023 amendments to the Mining Law (Ley Minera) introduced lithium as a strategic mineral reserved for the State through the national lithium company LitioMx, reflecting Latin American resource nationalism trends.

### Colombia

Colombia's foreign investment regime, governed by Decree 2080 of 2000 (as amended by Decree 119 of 2017) and administered by the Ministry of Commerce, Industry and Tourism, reflects a generally open stance consistent with its OECD accession (April 2020). FDI registration with the Central Bank (Banco de la República) through the exchange regime is mandatory. Colombia's Constitutional Court (Corte Constitucional) has been particularly active in conditioning extractive industry investment through the consulta previa (prior consultation) requirement for Indigenous and Afro-Colombian communities under ILO Convention 169, ratified by Law 21 of 1991. The landmark case of Sentencia SU-123/2018 established that prior consultation is a fundamental right that cannot be waived, affecting mining, hydrocarbon, and infrastructure FDI in territories with ethnic communities.

## Southeast Asia

### Indonesia

Indonesia's foreign investment regime was fundamentally restructured by the Omnibus Law on Job Creation (Undang-Undang Cipta Kerja, Law 11/2020, as revised by Government Regulation in Lieu of Law 2/2022 ratified by Law 6/2023). The traditional Daftar Negatif Investasi (DNI, Negative Investment List) was replaced by a Risk-Based Licensing system under Presidential Regulation 10/2021 (Peraturan Presiden tentang Bidang Usaha Penanaman Modal, amended by PR 49/2021), which classifies business sectors into Priority, Open, and Closed categories, with specific conditions for foreign ownership.

Key features include:

- **Priority Sectors**: Eligible for fiscal incentives (tax holidays of up to 20 years under MOF Regulation 130/PMK.010/2020, tax allowances, import duty exemptions) with 100% foreign ownership permitted.
- **Open Sectors with Conditions**: Foreign ownership caps ranging from 49% to 95% depending on the sector, with specific requirements for partnership with micro, small, and medium enterprises (MSMEs) in 46 designated sectors.
- **Closed Sectors**: Limited to six activities including narcotics cultivation, gambling, chemical weapons, ozone-depleting substances, and coral harvesting.
- **Online Single Submission (OSS) System**: Mandatory electronic licensing through the Risk-Based Approach (RBA) system administered by the Investment Coordinating Board (BKPM, now the Ministry of Investment/BKPM).

The Indonesian Constitutional Court (Mahkamah Konstitusi) Decision 91/PUU-XVIII/2020 declared the Omnibus Law formation process unconstitutional on procedural grounds, requiring legislative correction within two years -- a correction implemented through the Government Regulation in Lieu of Law (Perppu) 2/2022, which was itself controversial for bypassing normal legislative procedure.

### Philippines

The Philippines' foreign investment regime is anchored in the 1987 Constitution, which contains significant economic nationalism provisions. Article XII (National Economy and Patrimony) restricts foreign ownership in land (Section 7: 60% Filipino ownership for corporations), natural resources exploitation (Section 2: 60% Filipino ownership), public utilities (Section 11: formerly 60% Filipino, amended by Republic Act No. 11659 in 2022), educational institutions (Section 4(2): 60% Filipino), mass media (Section 11(1): 100% Filipino), and advertising (Section 11(2): 70% Filipino).

The Foreign Investments Act (Republic Act No. 7042, as amended by RA 11647 in 2022) establishes the Regular Foreign Investment Negative List (RFINL), updated every two years by the President. The 13th RFINL (Executive Order No. 175, 2022) lists activities where foreign ownership is restricted or prohibited. RA 11647 reduced the minimum paid-in capital requirement for foreign retail enterprises from USD 2.5 million to USD 500,000, and for foreign enterprises using advanced technology or employing at least 50 direct employees from USD 200,000 to USD 100,000.

The Public Utility Liberalization Act (RA 11659, 2022) amended the definition of "public utility" to cover only distribution and transmission of electricity, water pipeline distribution, and sewerage pipeline systems, allowing up to 100% foreign ownership in telecommunications, airlines, railways, expressways, and toll roads -- sectors previously capped at 40% foreign ownership. However, the implementing rules issued by the National Economic and Development Authority (NEDA) retain national security review authority.

The Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas (BSP) administers foreign exchange regulations under the Manual of Regulations on Foreign Exchange Transactions, requiring registration of foreign investments to ensure capital and earnings repatriation rights.

## Comparative Analysis

### Liberalization Trajectories

The Global South's FDI regimes reveal a common trajectory from protectionism toward selective liberalization, but at dramatically different paces and with distinct conditionalities. China's approach is the most systematic, using the negative list as a quantifiable tool -- reducing restricted items from 93 (2017) to 29 (2024) -- while maintaining control over strategic sectors. India's liberalization has been episodic and politically contingent, with reforms often introduced through DPIIT press notes rather than legislative action, creating a gap between de jure openness and de facto implementation. Indonesia's Omnibus Law represents the most ambitious single reform, but its constitutional challenges and implementation complexity illustrate the limits of top-down liberalization in democratic systems.

### Colonial Legacies and Economic Nationalism

Constitutional economic nationalism provisions in the Philippines (1987 Constitution), India (Directive Principles, Articles 38-39), Brazil (Article 170's economic order principles), and Nigeria (Section 16 of the 1999 Constitution) reflect post-colonial anxieties about foreign economic domination. South Africa's termination of its BIT program and adoption of the Protection of Investment Act represents the most explicit assertion of regulatory sovereignty in response to perceived constraints on developmental policy space. The Philippines' 2022 amendments to the Public Service Act and Foreign Investments Act mark a shift away from the most restrictive constitutional provisions, driven by the Philippine Economic Stimulus and Recovery Bill process.

### Digital Economy Challenges

FDI regulation across the Global South increasingly struggles with digital economy business models that transcend traditional sectoral boundaries. China's VIE structure exists precisely because of the mismatch between negative list restrictions on telecommunications and the reality of internet platform companies. India's e-commerce FDI policy amendments reveal similar tensions between marketplace and inventory models. Indonesia's 2021 Government Regulation 80 on e-commerce licensing requires foreign e-commerce platforms to establish local entities and use domestic payment gateways.

### South-South Investment Flows

Growing FDI flows between Global South countries -- Chinese investment in Africa through Belt and Road Initiative projects, Indian investment in Southeast Asia, and Brazilian investment in Lusophone Africa -- create novel regulatory challenges not fully addressed by existing frameworks designed primarily for North-South investment relationships. The China-Africa bilateral investment treaty network, China's BRI dispute resolution mechanisms (including the China International Commercial Court established in 2018), and the African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA) Investment Protocol (under negotiation as of 2026) represent emerging institutional responses.
