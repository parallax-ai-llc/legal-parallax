## Summary

Banking regulation -- the framework of laws, rules, and supervisory practices governing the establishment, operation, and dissolution of banking institutions -- is a cornerstone of modern financial systems, designed to protect depositors, maintain systemic stability, and prevent illicit financial activity. South Korea's banking sector is governed primarily by the Banking Act (은행법) of 1991 and the Act on the Structural Improvement of the Financial Industry (금융산업의 구조개선에 관한 법률), with supervision shared between the Financial Services Commission (금융위원회, FSC) and the Financial Supervisory Service (금융감독원, FSS), a dual structure established in the wake of the 1997 Asian Financial Crisis through the Act on the Establishment of Financial Supervisory Organizations (금융감독기구의 설치 등에 관한 법률). The United States maintains the most fragmented banking regulatory structure among major economies, with overlapping jurisdiction among the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (OCC), the Federal Reserve Board, the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC), and state banking departments, operating under a complex web of statutes including the National Bank Act of 1864 (12 U.S.C. §§ 21-216d), the Bank Holding Company Act of 1956 (12 U.S.C. §§ 1841-1850), the Federal Deposit Insurance Act (12 U.S.C. §§ 1811-1835a), and the Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act of 2010. Germany's banking supervision is conducted by the Federal Financial Supervisory Authority (Bundesanstalt für Finanzdienstleistungsaufsicht, BaFin) under the Banking Act (Kreditwesengesetz, KWG) of 1961, now operating within the European Banking Union framework, where the ECB's Single Supervisory Mechanism (SSM) under Council Regulation (EU) No 1024/2013 directly supervises significant institutions, while the European Court of Human Rights and the broader EU legal framework have established that banking regulation must comply with fundamental rights, particularly the protection of property under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 to the ECHR, while allowing member states a wide margin of appreciation in designing regulatory frameworks to ensure financial stability.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's banking regulatory framework was comprehensively restructured following the 1997 Asian Financial Crisis, which exposed fundamental weaknesses in supervision and corporate governance.

**Banking Act (은행법):** Originally enacted in 1991, the Banking Act governs the establishment, operation, and management of banking institutions. Article 8 requires FSC authorization for establishing a bank, with minimum capital requirements set at 100 billion KRW for nationwide banks and 25 billion KRW for regional banks. Article 15 imposes ownership restrictions: no single shareholder may hold more than 10 percent of a nationwide bank's voting shares without FSC approval, and the cap is 15 percent for regional banks.

**Financial Supervisory Architecture:** The Act on the Establishment of Financial Supervisory Organizations (금융감독기구의 설치 등에 관한 법률), enacted in 1997, created a unified supervisory structure:

| Body | Role |
|------|------|
| Financial Services Commission (금융위원회, FSC) | Policy-making and licensing authority |
| Financial Supervisory Service (금융감독원, FSS) | Examination and enforcement body |
| Securities and Futures Commission | Securities market oversight (within FSC) |

**Corporate Governance Requirements:** Following the chaebol-linked banking failures of the 1990s, the Banking Act imposes stringent governance requirements. Article 22-2 mandates that outside directors constitute a majority of the board. Article 22-4 requires the establishment of an audit committee comprising at least three directors, two-thirds of whom must be independent.

### Prudential Standards

South Korea adopted Basel III standards through FSC Financial Investment Services Regulations and related supervisory guidance. Korean banks must maintain:
- Common Equity Tier 1 (CET1) capital ratio: minimum 4.5 percent
- Tier 1 capital ratio: minimum 6 percent
- Total capital ratio: minimum 8 percent
- Countercyclical buffer: 0-2.5 percent (FSC determination)
- D-SIB surcharge: 1 percent for domestically systemically important banks

### Recent Developments

The Korean government has progressively relaxed ownership restrictions to attract investment, with the 2024 amendments permitting industrial capital to hold up to 34 percent of a bank's shares with FSC approval, subject to firewalls preventing conflicts of interest. The Internet-Only Bank Act (인터넷전문은행 설립 및 운영에 관한 특례법) of 2018 created a licensing framework for digital-only banks, enabling Kakao Bank and K-Bank to operate with relaxed ownership rules permitting ICT companies to hold up to 34 percent stakes.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States banking regulatory system is characterized by its dual banking system (federal and state charters), multiple overlapping regulators, and an extensive body of statute and regulation accumulated since the National Bank Act of 1864.

**Chartering and Licensing:**

| Charter Type | Licensing Authority | Primary Federal Regulator |
|-------------|-------------------|--------------------------|
| National bank | OCC | OCC |
| State member bank | State regulator | Federal Reserve |
| State non-member bank | State regulator | FDIC |
| Federal savings association | OCC | OCC |
| Bank holding company | N/A (formation) | Federal Reserve |

**Key Statutes:**

| Statute | Year | Primary Effect |
|---------|------|----------------|
| National Bank Act | 1864 | Federal chartering of banks |
| Federal Reserve Act | 1913 | Central bank; reserve requirements |
| Glass-Steagall Act | 1933 | Separation of commercial and investment banking (partially repealed 1999) |
| Bank Holding Company Act | 1956 | Federal Reserve oversight of bank holding companies |
| Community Reinvestment Act | 1977 | Requires banks to serve low- and moderate-income communities |
| Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act | 1999 | Repealed Glass-Steagall barriers; created financial holding companies |
| Dodd-Frank Act | 2010 | Post-2008 crisis reform; Volcker Rule; FSOC; OLA |
| Economic Growth Act | 2018 | Raised SIFI threshold from $50B to $250B in assets |

**Dodd-Frank Key Provisions:**
- **Title I:** Financial Stability Oversight Council (FSOC) designation of SIFIs
- **Title II:** Orderly Liquidation Authority (OLA) for systemically important financial institutions
- **Section 619 (Volcker Rule):** Prohibits proprietary trading by banking entities (12 U.S.C. § 1851)
- **Title VII:** Derivatives regulation
- **Title X:** Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB)

### Capital Requirements

The Federal Reserve, OCC, and FDIC jointly implemented Basel III through 12 C.F.R. Part 217 (Federal Reserve) and corresponding OCC and FDIC regulations. U.S. implementation includes the Collins Amendment floor, requiring U.S. standardized minimum capital requirements that may exceed Basel III international standards. Global Systemically Important Banks (G-SIBs) headquartered in the United States face additional surcharges of 1.0-4.5 percent of risk-weighted assets.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's banking regulation is governed by the Kreditwesengesetz (KWG), enacted on July 10, 1961, and extensively amended. Since November 2014, significant German banks have been directly supervised by the ECB under the Single Supervisory Mechanism.

**BaFin Authority:** The Bundesanstalt für Finanzdienstleistungsaufsicht (BaFin), established in 2002 through the merger of three predecessor agencies under the Finanzdienstleistungsaufsichtsgesetz (FinDAG), supervises approximately 2,400 banks, 800 financial services institutions, and 700 insurance companies in Germany.

**KWG Key Provisions:**

| Section | Content |
|---------|---------|
| § 1 | Definition of banking business and financial services |
| § 32 | Licensing requirement for banking business |
| § 33 | Conditions for granting a license |
| § 10 | Capital adequacy requirements (now largely superseded by CRR) |
| § 25a | Organizational duties; risk management requirements |
| § 25h | Internal safeguards against money laundering and terrorist financing |
| § 46 | Measures in cases of danger |

**Three-Pillar Banking System:** Germany's banking sector is uniquely structured into three pillars: private commercial banks, public-sector banks (Sparkassen and Landesbanken), and cooperative banks (Genossenschaftsbanken). Each pillar has its own institutional protection scheme (Sicherungseinrichtung), providing deposit protection beyond the statutory minimum.

### European Banking Union Integration

Since 2014, significant German banks (those with assets exceeding €30 billion or constituting more than 20 percent of national GDP) are directly supervised by the ECB under the Single Supervisory Mechanism. As of 2025, the ECB directly supervises 21 significant German banking groups. Less significant institutions remain under BaFin's direct supervision, with ECB oversight.

## ECHR / EU

### EU Banking Union Architecture

The EU Banking Union, established in response to the European sovereign debt crisis, rests on three pillars:

**Pillar 1 -- Single Supervisory Mechanism (SSM):** Council Regulation (EU) No 1024/2013 of October 15, 2013, confers specific supervisory tasks on the ECB. The ECB directly supervises approximately 110 significant institutions across the eurozone, covering roughly 82 percent of banking assets.

**Pillar 2 -- Single Resolution Mechanism (SRM):** Regulation (EU) No 806/2014 establishes the Single Resolution Board (SRB) with authority to resolve failing banks. The Single Resolution Fund, built from bank contributions, reached its target level of approximately €80 billion in 2024.

**Pillar 3 -- European Deposit Insurance Scheme (EDIS):** As of 2026, EDIS remains under negotiation. The existing framework relies on the Deposit Guarantee Schemes Directive 2014/49/EU, which harmonizes national deposit insurance at €100,000 per depositor per institution.

### Capital Requirements Regulation and Directive

The EU implements Basel III through:
- **CRR III (Regulation (EU) 2024/1623):** Directly applicable regulation setting prudential requirements, effective January 1, 2025
- **CRD VI (Directive (EU) 2024/1619):** Directive requiring national transposition, addressing governance, supervision, and sanctioning powers

### ECHR Jurisprudence on Banking Regulation

**Fruni v. Slovakia (Application No. 8014/07, ECtHR Judgment of June 21, 2011):** The Court held that the revocation of a banking license constitutes an interference with the right to peaceful enjoyment of possessions under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1, but found no violation as the measure pursued the legitimate aim of protecting depositors and maintaining financial stability, and was proportionate.

**Capital Bank AD v. Bulgaria (Application No. 49429/99, ECtHR Judgment of November 24, 2005):** The Court found a violation of Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 where a bank was denied the opportunity to contest its forced liquidation before an independent tribunal, establishing that even in the context of financial stability emergencies, procedural safeguards must be maintained.

**Credit and Industrial Bank v. Czech Republic (Application No. 29010/95, ECtHR Judgment of October 21, 2003):** The Court found a violation of Article 6(1) (right to a fair trial) in forced administration proceedings against a bank, holding that the bank was denied access to a court to challenge the supervisory authority's decision.

### Key Principles

The ECHR jurisprudence establishes that:
1. Banking licenses constitute "possessions" protected by Article 1 of Protocol No. 1
2. States enjoy a wide margin of appreciation in regulating banking to protect the public interest
3. Regulatory measures must be proportionate to the aim pursued
4. Banks must have access to judicial review of supervisory decisions (Article 6(1))
5. Emergency measures may justify expedited procedures but cannot eliminate judicial review entirely

## Comparative Analysis

### Regulatory Architecture Models

The four jurisdictions represent distinct regulatory architecture models:

**Integrated model (South Korea, Germany/BaFin):** A single or consolidated supervisor oversees banks alongside other financial institutions. This model promotes consistency but may create regulatory blind spots at the boundaries between financial sectors.

**Fragmented model (United States):** Multiple overlapping regulators supervise based on charter type and activity. This model creates regulatory competition and arbitrage opportunities but also provides checks and balances.

**Supranational model (EU Banking Union):** Supervision is shared between the ECB (significant institutions) and national authorities (less significant institutions), with harmonized rules. This model addresses the "financial trilemma" of integrating financial markets while maintaining national regulatory authority and financial stability.

### Ownership Restrictions

Jurisdictions diverge significantly on permissible ownership of banking institutions:

- **South Korea:** Historically strict separation of banking and commerce, now relaxing to 34 percent for qualifying shareholders
- **United States:** Bank Holding Company Act separates banking from commerce; Gramm-Leach-Bliley allows financial holding companies to engage in broader financial activities
- **Germany:** No formal banking-commerce separation; universal banking model
- **EU:** CRD VI harmonizes fit-and-proper assessments for qualifying shareholders

### Resolution Frameworks

Post-2008 crisis reforms have converged around the principle that failing banks should be resolved without taxpayer bailouts:

- **South Korea:** KDIC manages deposit insurance and resolution; Prompt Corrective Action framework
- **United States:** FDIC resolution authority; Dodd-Frank Orderly Liquidation Authority for SIFIs; living wills requirement
- **Germany/EU:** SRB under SRM Regulation; Bank Recovery and Resolution Directive (BRRD) bail-in tool; Single Resolution Fund
- All frameworks now incorporate total loss-absorbing capacity (TLAC) requirements for G-SIBs

### Digital Banking Challenges

All jurisdictions face emerging regulatory challenges from digital transformation:

**Neobanks and Digital-Only Institutions:**

| Jurisdiction | Digital Banking Framework | Notable Institutions |
|-------------|--------------------------|---------------------|
| South Korea | Internet-Only Bank Act (2018); dedicated licensing | Kakao Bank, K-Bank, Toss Bank |
| United States | OCC fintech charter (contested); state charters | Varo Bank, SoFi Bank |
| Germany/EU | Full banking license required; digital-only within existing framework | N26, Revolut (Lithuanian license) |

**Banking-as-a-Service (BaaS):** The growing separation of banking licenses from customer-facing services raises supervisory challenges across all jurisdictions. The 2023 failures of banks with significant BaaS relationships (Silvergate, Signature Bank in the U.S.) prompted regulatory re-evaluation of third-party risk management requirements.

**Big Tech in Banking:** The entry of large technology companies into financial services (Apple Savings, Google Pay, Samsung Pay, Kakao Pay) creates regulatory challenges around market concentration, data privacy, and systemic risk that existing banking regulation was not designed to address.

### Anti-Money Laundering Integration

Banking regulation increasingly integrates AML requirements:
- **South Korea:** FSS examination includes AML compliance assessment
- **United States:** BSA/AML compliance is a supervisory priority; failures can result in enforcement actions and consent orders
- **Germany/EU:** BaFin and ECB/SSM assess AML controls as part of prudential supervision; AMLA will provide centralized AML supervision for selected entities
- The Danske Bank scandal (2018-2022), involving approximately $230 billion in suspicious transactions through its Estonian branch, demonstrated the systemic risks of inadequate AML controls

### Cross-Border Banking Supervision

International coordination remains a persistent challenge:
- Basel Committee on Banking Supervision sets global standards
- Financial Stability Board (FSB) coordinates systemic risk oversight
- Supervisory colleges facilitate information sharing for cross-border banking groups
- The EU Banking Union represents the most advanced model of cross-border supervisory integration

### Macroprudential Regulation

Post-2008 reforms introduced macroprudential tools designed to address systemic risk rather than individual institution soundness:

- **South Korea:** BOK Financial Stability Board monitors systemic risk; countercyclical capital buffer framework adopted; loan-to-value (LTV) and debt-to-income (DTI) ratios actively managed for real estate market stability
- **United States:** Financial Stability Oversight Council (FSOC) designates systemically important financial institutions (SIFIs); stress testing through CCAR and DFAST frameworks
- **Germany/EU:** European Systemic Risk Board (ESRB) issues warnings and recommendations; national designated authorities set countercyclical capital buffers; O-SII and G-SII buffer requirements under CRD IV/V
- Macroprudential regulation represents a paradigm shift from microprudential focus on individual institution safety to system-wide stability monitoring

### Consumer Protection in Banking

The integration of consumer protection into banking regulation has intensified:

- **South Korea:** Financial Consumer Protection Act (금융소비자 보호에 관한 법률, 2020) established comprehensive suitability, appropriateness, and cooling-off requirements; FSS operates financial dispute mediation
- **United States:** Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) established by Dodd-Frank Title X; supervisory and enforcement authority over consumer financial products; constitutionality upheld in CFPB v. Community Financial Services Association (2024)
- **Germany/EU:** BaFin collective consumer protection mandate added in 2015; Markets in Financial Instruments Directive II (MiFID II) product governance and suitability requirements; Consumer Credit Directive (CCD II) adopted 2023
- All jurisdictions now require key information documents, complaint resolution mechanisms, and suitability assessments for complex financial products

### Prudential Standards Convergence

Basel III implementation timelines illustrate convergence and divergence in global banking standards:

| Standard | South Korea | United States | EU |
|---------|------------|---------------|-----|
| CET1 Minimum | 4.5% (2013) | 4.5% (2014) | 4.5% (2014) |
| Total Capital | 8.0% + buffers | 8.0% + buffers | 8.0% + buffers |
| Leverage Ratio | 3% (2018) | 5% (G-SIBs) | 3% (2021) |
| NSFR | 2018 | Proposed | 2021 |
| Basel III Final | 2025 | Delayed (2025+) | January 2025 (CRR III) |

The United States has notably delayed implementation of the Basel III "endgame" rules, with the Federal Reserve proposing significant modifications to the original proposal following industry pushback in 2023-2024.
