## Summary

Manslaughter -- the unlawful killing of another person without the malice aforethought or premeditation that characterizes murder -- is recognized across virtually all legal systems, yet its definition, classification, and punishment vary dramatically by jurisdiction. South Korea distinguishes between negligent homicide (과실치사, Article 267) and death resulting from bodily injury (Article 259), imposing comparatively lighter penalties than its murder provisions. The United States divides manslaughter into voluntary (heat of passion) and involuntary (criminal negligence or recklessness) categories, with federal penalties reaching 15 years for the former and 8 years for the latter. Germany classifies all intentional killings lacking specific "murder characteristics" as Totschlag (Section 212 StGB), carrying 5 to 15 years imprisonment, with a lesser category under Section 213 for provocation-based killings. The European Court of Human Rights, while not defining manslaughter as such, imposes positive obligations on states under Article 2 to prevent foreseeable deaths and to conduct effective investigations into negligent killings.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korean criminal law addresses manslaughter through several provisions of the Criminal Act (형법), each reflecting a different degree of culpability:

| Provision | Description | Penalty |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| Article 259 | Death resulting from bodily injury (상해치사) | 3 years to life imprisonment |
| Article 267 | Negligent homicide (과실치사) | Up to 2 years or fine up to 7 million won |
| Article 268 | Death by occupational or gross negligence (업무상과실치사) | Up to 5 years or fine up to 20 million won |

### Key Principles

**Distinction from murder:** Unlike Article 250 (murder), manslaughter provisions under Articles 267 and 268 do not require intent to kill. Article 259 occupies an intermediate position: the offender intends bodily harm, but the resulting death is unintended. This structure reflects a graduated culpability model where the offender's mental state at the time of the act determines classification.

**Occupational negligence (업무상과실):** Article 268 imposes heightened penalties when the offender's negligence occurs in the course of professional duties. This provision has been applied extensively to medical malpractice, industrial accidents, and transportation incidents.

### Key Cases

**Itaewon Crowd Crush (2022):** On October 29, 2022, a crowd crush in the Itaewon entertainment district of Seoul killed 159 people. The former chief of Yongsan Police Station, Lee Im-jae, was sentenced to three years imprisonment for professional negligence resulting in death (Article 268). The court held that the disaster was foreseeable and that Lee had failed to establish or implement an adequate safety management plan despite clear warning signs of dangerous overcrowding. However, the former Seoul Metropolitan Police Agency commissioner was acquitted, with the court finding insufficient evidence to establish a direct causal link between his conduct and the deaths.

**Sewol Ferry Negligence Charges (2014):** While the captain was ultimately convicted of murder by omission for failing to order evacuation, several crew members were convicted of the lesser charge of negligent homicide for their roles in the disaster that killed 304 people. The sentencing distinction between the captain and crew members illustrates the boundary between murder by omission and negligent manslaughter in Korean law.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Under federal law (18 U.S.C. Section 1112), manslaughter is defined as "the unlawful killing of a human being without malice" and is divided into two categories:

- **Voluntary manslaughter:** Killing upon a sudden quarrel or in the heat of passion. Federal penalty: imprisonment not more than 15 years. The U.S. Sentencing Guidelines set a base offense level of 25 (guideline range of 57-71 months at Criminal History Category I).
- **Involuntary manslaughter:** Killing during the commission of an unlawful act not amounting to a felony, or during the commission of a lawful act in an unlawful manner or without due caution. Federal penalty: imprisonment not more than 8 years.

State penalties vary considerably. For example, California imposes 3-11 years for voluntary manslaughter and 2-4 years for involuntary manslaughter, while New York classifies first-degree manslaughter as a Class B felony carrying 5-25 years.

### Landmark Cases

**Commonwealth v. Welansky (Massachusetts, 1944):** Barnett Welansky, owner of the Cocoanut Grove nightclub in Boston, was convicted of involuntary manslaughter after a fire killed 492 people in his establishment. Welansky knew the fire exits were inadequate and in violation of building codes. The Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court defined the standard for wanton or reckless conduct as "intentional conduct, by way either of commission or omission where there is a duty to act, which conduct involves a high degree of likelihood that substantial harm will result to another." This case remains a foundational precedent for involuntary manslaughter based on reckless omission.

**People v. Berry (California, 1976):** The California Supreme Court reduced a murder conviction to voluntary manslaughter, holding that prolonged provocation by the victim (verbal admission of infidelity) could sustain a heat-of-passion defense even when a traditional "cooling period" had elapsed. The court found that "the long course of provocatory conduct...reached its final culmination" at the time of killing, expanding the temporal scope of the heat-of-passion doctrine. This case is widely taught in American law schools for its controversial broadening of the provocation defense.

**State v. Williams (Washington, 1971):** A Native American couple was convicted of involuntary manslaughter after their 17-month-old child died from an abscessed tooth that developed into a fatal infection. The parents, who feared the child would be taken by welfare authorities, failed to seek medical attention. The court applied a simple negligence standard rather than the more common gross negligence or recklessness standard, generating lasting debate about the appropriate mens rea threshold for involuntary manslaughter.

### Vehicular Manslaughter

Many states have created separate vehicular manslaughter or vehicular homicide statutes to address deaths caused by reckless or impaired driving. Penalties for DUI-related vehicular manslaughter range from 1 year (misdemeanor in some states) to 15 years (felony in states like Florida and Georgia), with aggravating factors such as prior DUI convictions substantially increasing sentences.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German criminal law classifies intentional killing without the specific aggravating characteristics of murder (Mord, Section 211 StGB) as Totschlag under Section 212 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB):

**Section 212 -- Totschlag (Manslaughter)**
- Standard penalty: 5 to 15 years imprisonment
- In particularly severe cases (besonders schwerer Fall): life imprisonment

**Section 213 -- Lesser Case of Manslaughter (Minder schwerer Fall des Totschlags)**
- Penalty: 1 to 10 years imprisonment
- Applies when the offender was provoked by maltreatment or serious insult by the victim and was immediately driven to rage, or when other circumstances exist that make the case less severe

**Section 222 -- Negligent Killing (Fahrlassige Totung)**
- Penalty: up to 5 years imprisonment or fine
- This is the German equivalent of involuntary manslaughter and covers deaths caused by negligence without intent to kill or harm

### Key Distinctions

Unlike common law systems, the German distinction between Mord and Totschlag does not rest on premeditation. Instead, any intentional killing is classified as Totschlag unless one of the enumerated "murder characteristics" (Mordmerkmale) of Section 211 is present -- such as acting out of base motives (niedrige Beweggrunde), killing perfidiously (heimtuckisch), or killing to conceal another crime. The presence of even a single Mordmerkmal elevates the offense to Mord with a mandatory life sentence, leaving no room for judicial discretion.

### Notable Cases

**BGH, Armin Meiwes Case (2006):** In one of Germany's most unusual criminal cases, Armin Meiwes killed and consumed a willing victim who had responded to an internet advertisement. The trial court initially convicted Meiwes of Totschlag (manslaughter), reasoning that the victim's consent, while not a complete defense, mitigated the offense. On appeal, the Bundesgerichtshof (Federal Court of Justice) overturned the conviction and found Meiwes guilty of Mord (murder), holding that the motive of sexual gratification through cannibalism constituted a "base motive" under Section 211. The case illustrated the sharp consequences of the binary Mord/Totschlag distinction.

**Section 213 Provocation Defense:** German courts have applied the lesser case provision of Section 213 in domestic violence scenarios where the offender was subjected to sustained abuse or severe insult by the victim immediately prior to the killing. However, unlike the U.S. heat-of-passion doctrine, German law requires the provocation to have come from the victim specifically and to have immediately preceded the act.

### Reform Efforts

The rigid binary structure of Sections 211-213 has been subject to sustained criticism from German legal scholars and practitioners. A 2014 expert commission recommended replacing the system with a unified homicide provision that would allow courts to consider all circumstances in determining the appropriate sentence, rather than being locked into either the Totschlag range (5-15 years) or mandatory life imprisonment for Mord. As of 2026, no legislative reform has been enacted.

## European Framework

### Article 2: Right to Life and State Obligations

The European Court of Human Rights does not itself define or prosecute manslaughter, but its jurisprudence under Article 2 of the European Convention on Human Rights creates binding obligations on signatory states regarding how they prevent, investigate, and punish unlawful killings, including those arising from negligence.

Article 2 imposes three categories of obligation:

1. **Negative obligation:** States must refrain from intentionally taking life except in narrowly defined circumstances (self-defense, lawful arrest, quelling insurrection).
2. **Positive (substantive) obligation:** States must take appropriate steps to safeguard the lives of those within their jurisdiction, including through effective criminal law provisions deterring offenses against the person.
3. **Procedural obligation:** States must conduct an effective official investigation when individuals have been killed as a result of the use of force or through negligent acts.

### Landmark Cases

**Oneryildiz v. Turkey (2004):** In 1993, a methane explosion at a municipal waste site in Istanbul caused a landslide that buried a nearby slum settlement, killing 39 people including nine members of the applicant's family. The Grand Chamber held that Turkey violated both the substantive and procedural limbs of Article 2. The government had known of the risk of a methane explosion but failed to take measures to protect residents. Critically, the Court found that the criminal proceedings against the responsible officials -- who received minimal fines and suspended sentences -- were inadequate to satisfy the procedural obligation under Article 2, establishing that states must ensure meaningful criminal accountability for negligent deaths.

**Calvelli and Ciglio v. Italy (2002):** A newborn died due to the negligence of a doctor during delivery. The Grand Chamber held that Article 2 requires states to establish an effective judicial system capable of determining the cause of death in a medical context and holding responsible parties accountable. While the Court accepted that the obligation to investigate could in some cases be satisfied through civil or disciplinary proceedings (as opposed to criminal prosecution), it emphasized that the judicial system must in practice provide an effective remedy.

**Osman v. United Kingdom (1998):** Established the "Osman test" for state liability under Article 2: authorities violate the positive obligation to protect life when they knew or ought to have known of a real and immediate risk to the life of an identified individual and failed to take reasonable measures to avoid that risk. This test has been applied in numerous subsequent manslaughter-adjacent cases.

**Makaratzis v. Greece (2004):** Police officers opened fire on a fleeing vehicle, seriously injuring the driver. While the victim survived, the Court examined the applicable framework as if Article 2 had been engaged, holding that the absence of a clear legislative and administrative framework governing the use of firearms by police violated Article 2. This case extended Article 2 obligations to the regulatory infrastructure surrounding the use of lethal force.

### Protocol 13: Abolition of the Death Penalty

Protocol No. 13 to the Convention, which entered into force in 2003, requires the complete abolition of the death penalty in all circumstances among ratifying states. As of 2026, all Council of Europe member states except Russia (no longer a member) and Azerbaijan have ratified Protocol 13, making life imprisonment the maximum possible penalty for any form of homicide across Europe.
