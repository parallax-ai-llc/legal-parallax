## Summary

Insider trading -- the buying or selling of securities on the basis of material, non-public information in breach of a duty of trust or confidence -- is prohibited across all major financial markets, yet the legal foundations, enforcement mechanisms, and severity of sanctions vary considerably between jurisdictions. South Korea regulates insider trading through Articles 174 and 443 of the Financial Investment Services and Capital Markets Act (FSCMA), imposing criminal penalties of at least one year imprisonment and fines of three to five times illicit profits, with recent amendments introducing non-monetary sanctions such as account freezes and executive appointment bans. The United States, despite having no standalone federal insider trading statute, prosecutes the offense primarily under Section 10(b) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 and SEC Rule 10b-5, relying on a judicially developed body of law that imposes criminal penalties of up to 20 years imprisonment and $5 million in fines for individuals. Germany enforces insider trading prohibitions through the EU Market Abuse Regulation (MAR) as implemented by the Securities Trading Act (WpHG), with criminal sanctions of up to five years imprisonment -- or up to ten years in particularly serious cases -- and administrative fines reaching 15 percent of annual corporate revenues. At the supranational level, the EU Market Abuse Regulation (Regulation 596/2014) and the Criminal Sanctions Directive (Directive 2014/57/EU) establish a harmonized framework across Member States, while the European Court of Human Rights (ECHR) has shaped enforcement through landmark rulings on double jeopardy, the presumption of innocence, and the right against self-incrimination.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's insider trading regime is governed by the Financial Investment Services and Capital Markets Act (FSCMA, also known as the Capital Markets Act), enacted in 2009 as a comprehensive overhaul of the country's securities regulation. Article 174 of the FSCMA prohibits certain insiders of a company whose shares are listed on the Korea Exchange -- including officers, employees, major shareholders (holding 10 percent or more), and persons exercising regulatory authority -- from trading in the company's securities while in possession of undisclosed material information. The prohibition extends to any person who receives such information directly from an insider (first-generation tippees).

Material information is defined as information concerning the business or management of the issuer that has not been disclosed to the public and that would be likely to have a significant impact on the investment decisions of investors or the price of the securities.

### Criminal Penalties

Under Article 443 of the FSCMA, criminal penalties for insider trading and other unfair trading practices were significantly strengthened by amendments that took effect in June 2023:

- A person who gains illicit profits of 5 billion won or more faces imprisonment for life or a fixed term of not less than 5 years.
- A person who gains illicit profits of 500 million won or more faces imprisonment of not less than 3 years.
- Standard violations carry imprisonment of at least 1 year.
- Fines range from 3 to 5 times the amount of illicit profits obtained.

The Specific Economic Crimes Aggravated Punishment Act may also apply to large-scale insider trading offenses, further escalating penalties.

### Non-Monetary Sanctions (2025 Amendments)

The Financial Services Commission announced new enforcement mechanisms effective April 23, 2025, under revised FSCMA enforcement decrees:

- **Account freeze orders** preventing the disposal of proceeds from unfair trading.
- **Executive appointment restrictions** barring convicted persons from serving as officers of listed companies.
- **Administrative penalty system** supplementing criminal prosecution for certain market abuse violations.

### Prior Disclosure Regime (2024)

An amendment promulgated on January 23, 2024 and effective July 24, 2024, requires mandatory prior disclosure of insider transactions. If any executive or major shareholder of a listed company intends to buy or sell specified securities (including equity securities, convertible bonds, bonds with warrants, and related depository receipts) in an amount exceeding a prescribed threshold, they must disclose the planned transaction in advance of the scheduled trading date.

### Landmark Cases

**Celltrion Group Founder -- Seo Jung-jin (2022-2023):** Celltrion founder and chairman Seo Jung-jin was convicted of insider trading for selling shares ahead of negative corporate announcements. Despite the conviction, Seo's personal fortune grew substantially due to broader market trends in the electric vehicle and biotech sectors, highlighting tensions between criminal enforcement and market realities.

**LG Group Heiress -- Koo Yeon-kyung (2023-2026):** Koo Yeon-kyung, the eldest daughter of late LG Group chairman Koo Bon-moo, and her husband Yoon Kwan were prosecuted for allegedly purchasing approximately 650 million won worth of shares in biotech firm Mejion using advance knowledge of a 50 billion won capital increase. Prosecutors sought one year imprisonment for Koo and two years for Yoon. In February 2026, the couple was acquitted, underscoring the difficulty of proving the requisite causal link between possession of information and trading decisions.

**Stock Manipulation CFD Scheme (2023-2025):** In South Korea's largest stock manipulation prosecution, 25 individuals were convicted for a scheme worth approximately 730 billion won ($500 million) using contracts for difference (CFDs) to artificially inflate stock prices. Ra Deok-yeon, the scheme's architect, was sentenced to 25 years imprisonment.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States does not have a standalone federal statute that explicitly defines or prohibits insider trading. Instead, insider trading liability has been developed through judicial interpretation of the broad anti-fraud provisions of Section 10(b) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 and SEC Rule 10b-5, which prohibit any person from employing "any device, scheme, or artifice to defraud" or engaging in "any act, practice, or course of business which operates or would operate as a fraud or deceit upon any person" in connection with the purchase or sale of any security.

Additional statutory authority includes:

- **Insider Trading Sanctions Act of 1984 (ITSA):** Authorized the SEC to seek a civil penalty of up to three times the profits gained or losses avoided.
- **Insider Trading and Securities Fraud Enforcement Act of 1988 (ITSFEA):** Extended liability to controlling persons (employers) of inside traders and increased criminal penalties.
- **Securities Exchange Act Section 16(b):** Creates a strict-liability disgorgement remedy against statutory insiders (officers, directors, 10-percent shareholders) for short-swing profits -- profits from matching purchases and sales within a six-month period.

### Theories of Liability

Federal courts have developed two primary theories of insider trading liability:

1. **Classical Theory (Chiarella v. United States, 1980):** A corporate insider (officer, director, or employee) who trades in the corporation's securities while in possession of material, non-public information breaches a fiduciary duty owed to the corporation's shareholders and must either disclose the information or abstain from trading.

2. **Misappropriation Theory (United States v. O'Hagan, 1997):** A person who trades on material, non-public information in breach of a duty of trust or confidence owed to the source of the information -- even when that person is not a corporate insider -- commits securities fraud. This theory significantly expanded the reach of insider trading law to cover outsiders such as lawyers, accountants, and consultants.

### Elements of the Offense

To establish criminal insider trading under Rule 10b-5, prosecutors must prove:

1. **Breach of duty:** The defendant owed a fiduciary duty or other duty of trust and confidence (to shareholders under the classical theory, or to the source of information under the misappropriation theory).
2. **Material, non-public information:** The information was both material (a reasonable investor would consider it important) and not yet publicly available.
3. **Scienter:** The defendant acted with knowledge or reckless disregard of the breach.
4. **Connection to securities transaction:** The breach occurred in connection with the purchase or sale of a security.
5. **Personal benefit (for tipper liability):** Under Dirks v. SEC (1983), a tipper is liable only if they received a personal benefit -- pecuniary or reputational -- from disclosing the information.

### Penalties

| Category | Maximum Penalty |
|----------|----------------|
| Criminal imprisonment (individual) | 20 years |
| Criminal fine (individual) | $5 million |
| Criminal fine (entity) | $25 million |
| Civil penalty (SEC) | 3x profits gained or losses avoided |
| Disgorgement | Full amount of ill-gotten gains |
| Injunctive relief | Permanent bar from serving as officer or director |

### Landmark Cases

**SEC v. Texas Gulf Sulphur Co. (1968):** The seminal insider trading case established the "disclose or abstain" rule. Officers and employees of Texas Gulf Sulphur who traded on advance knowledge of a major mineral discovery in Ontario were found liable. The Second Circuit held that anyone in possession of material inside information must either disclose it to the public or refrain from trading.

**Chiarella v. United States (1980):** The Supreme Court reversed the conviction of a financial printer who deduced takeover targets from documents he was handling, holding that a duty to disclose arises only from a relationship of trust and confidence, not merely from possession of material, non-public information. This established the fiduciary duty requirement for classical insider trading liability.

**Dirks v. SEC (1983):** The Supreme Court held that a tippee's liability is derivative of the tipper's breach of duty, and a tipper breaches a fiduciary duty only when they receive a "personal benefit" from the disclosure. This personal benefit test remains central to tipping liability analysis.

**United States v. O'Hagan (1997):** The Supreme Court endorsed the misappropriation theory, upholding the conviction of a lawyer who traded on confidential information about a client's planned tender offer. The Court held that a person commits fraud "in connection with" a securities transaction when they misappropriate confidential information for trading purposes in breach of a duty owed to the source of the information.

**United States v. Raj Rajaratnam (2011):** The founder and manager of the Galleon Group hedge fund was convicted on all 14 counts of conspiracy and securities fraud for orchestrating one of the largest insider trading schemes in American history. Rajaratnam was sentenced to 11 years in prison -- the longest sentence ever imposed for insider trading -- fined $10 million, and ordered to forfeit $53.8 million. The SEC separately obtained a civil penalty of $92.8 million, the largest individual penalty in SEC insider trading history. The case was notable as the first use of wiretap evidence in an insider trading prosecution.

**SEC v. SAC Capital Advisors (2013-2014):** SAC Capital, the hedge fund founded by Steven A. Cohen, pleaded guilty to insider trading charges and agreed to pay $1.8 billion in penalties -- the largest insider trading penalty in history at the time. The firm was required to cease operating as an investment advisor to outside investors.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's insider trading regime is built on the directly applicable EU Market Abuse Regulation (MAR, Regulation 596/2014) and its domestic implementing legislation, the Securities Trading Act (Wertpapierhandelsgesetz, WpHG). Before the adoption of MAR, Germany regulated insider trading through the WpHG alone, which originally transposed the 2003 Market Abuse Directive. With MAR's entry into application on July 3, 2016, the substantive prohibitions are now set at the EU level, while the WpHG provides for criminal and administrative sanctions.

Article 14 of MAR prohibits:

1. **Insider dealing:** Engaging or attempting to engage in insider dealing (Article 14(a)).
2. **Recommending or inducing:** Recommending that another person engage in insider dealing, or inducing another person to engage in insider dealing (Article 14(b)).
3. **Unlawful disclosure:** Unlawfully disclosing inside information (Article 14(c)).

Inside information is defined under Article 7 of MAR as information of a precise nature, which has not been made public, relating directly or indirectly to one or more issuers or financial instruments, and which, if it were made public, would be likely to have a significant effect on the prices of those financial instruments or on related derivative financial instruments.

### Criminal Sanctions

Sections 119 and 120 of the WpHG impose criminal sanctions for intentional violations of MAR's insider trading prohibitions:

| Offense | Maximum Imprisonment |
|---------|---------------------|
| Insider dealing (intentional) | 5 years |
| Insider dealing (particularly serious cases) | 10 years |
| Unlawful disclosure of inside information | 5 years |
| Attempted insider dealing | Punishable (reduced sentence) |

A monetary fine may be imposed as an alternative to imprisonment. In addition, Section 73 et seq. of the German Criminal Code (StGB) provides for the confiscation of proceeds of crime (Einziehung) without any upper limit.

### Administrative Sanctions

BaFin may impose administrative fines for negligent or less serious violations:

- Up to 5 million euros for natural persons.
- Up to 15 million euros or 15 percent of total annual revenues (whichever is higher) for legal persons.
- Up to 15 million euros or 15 percent of total annual revenues for other intentional or negligent violations.
- BaFin publishes its Administrative Fine Guidelines (Bussgeldleitlinien) to ensure consistency in the assessment of fines.

### Enforcement Authority

The Federal Financial Supervisory Authority (Bundesanstalt fur Finanzdienstleistungsaufsicht, BaFin) is the primary enforcement authority. BaFin monitors securities markets, investigates suspected insider trading, and may refer cases to public prosecutors for criminal proceedings. Following the Wirecard scandal, the Financial Market Integrity Strengthening Act (FISG) of 2021 expanded BaFin's powers, granting it authority to conduct forensic audits of listed companies' financial statements.

### Landmark Cases

**Porsche-Volkswagen Takeover (2008-2015):** In one of Germany's most prominent market manipulation and insider trading investigations, BaFin investigated former Porsche CEO Wendelin Wiedeking and CFO Holger Harter for their roles in the attempted takeover of Volkswagen. Porsche had secretly accumulated 43 percent of VW's shares and held undisclosed options on an additional 32 percent, triggering a short squeeze that briefly made Volkswagen the world's most valuable company by market capitalization. Wiedeking and Harter were acquitted of market manipulation charges in 2016 by the Stuttgart Regional Court, which found insufficient evidence that they had intended to manipulate the market.

**Wirecard Scandal (2020-present):** The Wirecard fraud -- in which the company fabricated 1.9 billion euros in assets -- exposed serious failures in BaFin's oversight. Rather than investigating Wirecard, BaFin had filed criminal complaints against journalists and short sellers for alleged market manipulation based on their negative reporting about the company. BaFin subsequently filed a criminal complaint against one of its own employees for insider trading in Wirecard securities shortly before the scandal became public. The case prompted a comprehensive overhaul of German financial supervision through the FISG.

**Lafonta v. AMF (ECJ, 2015):** While a French case, the ECJ ruling in Lafonta (C-628/13) clarified the definition of "precise" information under MAR for all Member States, including Germany, holding that information need not enable the direction of a price movement to be determined in order to qualify as precise inside information.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Legal Framework

The EU's market abuse regime rests on two principal instruments adopted in 2014:

1. **Market Abuse Regulation (MAR, Regulation 596/2014):** A directly applicable regulation that entered into force on July 3, 2016, replacing the 2003 Market Abuse Directive. MAR establishes a uniform definition of insider dealing, market manipulation, and unlawful disclosure across all EU Member States, and sets minimum standards for administrative sanctions.

2. **Criminal Sanctions Directive (MAD II, Directive 2014/57/EU):** Requires Member States to establish criminal offenses and penalties for serious market abuse. It sets minimum maximum penalties:
   - Insider dealing and market manipulation: maximum imprisonment of at least 4 years.
   - Unlawful disclosure of inside information: maximum imprisonment of at least 2 years.
   - Member States must also provide for criminal liability of legal persons, with sanctions including criminal or non-criminal fines.

### Definition of Inside Information (MAR Article 7)

Inside information must satisfy four cumulative criteria:

1. **Precise nature:** The information must be specific enough to allow a conclusion to be drawn about its possible effect on prices. It need not indicate the direction of price movement (Lafonta v. AMF, C-628/13).
2. **Not publicly available:** The information must not have been made public.
3. **Direct or indirect relation:** The information must relate to one or more issuers or financial instruments.
4. **Price sensitivity:** If made public, the information would be likely to have a significant effect on prices (assessed by the "reasonable investor" test).

### The Spector Presumption

In Spector Photo Group NV v. CBFA (Case C-45/08, 2009), the Court of Justice of the European Union established a rebuttable presumption that a person who possesses inside information and trades in the relevant financial instruments has "used" that information. The CJEU held that this presumption does not violate the presumption of innocence under Article 6(2) of the European Convention on Human Rights, because the defendant retains the right to rebut the presumption and rights of the defence are guaranteed. This objective approach -- possession plus transaction equals presumed use -- significantly lowered the evidentiary burden on regulators compared to the subjective intent requirement in the United States.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

The European Court of Human Rights has profoundly influenced the enforcement architecture of market abuse laws across Europe:

**Grande Stevens and Others v. Italy (2014):** In this landmark decision, the ECHR held that Italy's dual-track enforcement system -- imposing both administrative sanctions and criminal penalties for the same market manipulation conduct -- violated the principle of ne bis in idem (prohibition of double jeopardy) under Article 4 of Protocol No. 7 to the European Convention. The Court found that the Italian CONSOB's administrative sanctions were "criminal" in nature under the Engel criteria (severity, classification under domestic law, and nature of the offense), meaning that subsequent criminal prosecution for the same conduct constituted impermissible double punishment. This ruling forced multiple EU Member States to restructure their dual-track enforcement systems.

**Right Against Self-Incrimination (2021-2023):** The European Court of Justice, in a ruling concerning the interaction between MAR's cooperation obligations and fundamental rights, clarified that market regulators in EU Member States cannot use their statutory powers to compel individuals to provide testimony that could incriminate them. This ruling reinforced the privilege against self-incrimination (nemo tenetur se ipsum accusare) as a fundamental right applicable in administrative proceedings that are "criminal" in character under the Engel criteria.

**A and B v. Norway (2016):** The ECHR Grand Chamber refined the ne bis in idem principle, holding that dual administrative and criminal proceedings for the same conduct do not necessarily violate Article 4 of Protocol No. 7, provided there is a "sufficiently close connection in substance and in time" between the two sets of proceedings, such that they form part of an integrated scheme of sanctions. This ruling partially tempered the impact of Grande Stevens and provided Member States with a framework for maintaining dual-track enforcement.

### ESMA Coordination

The European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) coordinates the consistent application of MAR across Member States through:

- Issuing technical standards, guidelines, and Q&As on MAR implementation.
- Maintaining the Financial Instruments Reference Data System (FIRDS) for identifying instruments subject to MAR.
- Publishing annual statistical reports on national enforcement activities.
- Facilitating cross-border cooperation between national competent authorities.

## Comparative Analysis

### Defining the Offense

The most fundamental divergence lies in how jurisdictions define insider trading. The United States relies on a fraud-based theory requiring proof of a breach of fiduciary duty (classical theory) or a duty of trust owed to the source of information (misappropriation theory). This approach means that not all trading on material, non-public information is illegal -- only trading that involves a breach of duty. By contrast, the EU/MAR framework and its implementation in Germany and other Member States adopt a possession-based approach: the Spector presumption means that trading while in possession of inside information is presumptively prohibited, regardless of whether a fiduciary relationship exists. South Korea occupies a middle position, prohibiting specific categories of insiders and their direct tippees from trading on undisclosed material information, without requiring proof of a fiduciary duty breach but limiting the scope of covered persons.

### Enforcement Architecture

The United States uniquely employs both civil enforcement (by the SEC) and criminal prosecution (by the DOJ) as parallel tracks, with the SEC maintaining one of the most active enforcement programs globally. Germany relies on BaFin for administrative enforcement and public prosecutors for criminal cases, though the Wirecard scandal exposed significant weaknesses in BaFin's willingness to pursue corporate misconduct. South Korea's FSC/FSS system has been supplemented by the 2025 introduction of non-monetary sanctions. At the EU level, the interaction between administrative and criminal sanctions has been the most contentious issue, with the ECHR's ne bis in idem jurisprudence requiring Member States to ensure that dual proceedings satisfy the "sufficiently close connection" test from A and B v. Norway.

### Severity of Penalties

South Korea now imposes the most severe maximum penalties, with life imprisonment available for illicit gains exceeding 5 billion won and mandatory minimum sentences of one year. The United States follows with a 20-year maximum and the ability to impose civil penalties of up to three times profits. Germany's maximum of 5 years (10 in serious cases) is among the lower criminal penalties, though its administrative fines -- up to 15 percent of annual revenues -- can be economically devastating for corporate offenders. The EU's MAD II harmonizes a minimum floor of 4 years maximum imprisonment across Member States.

### Evidentiary Standards and Presumptions

The Spector presumption in EU law represents the most prosecution-friendly approach, effectively shifting the burden to defendants to explain why their trading while in possession of inside information was legitimate. The United States requires affirmative proof of scienter (knowing or reckless conduct) and, for tipping cases, proof of personal benefit to the tipper. South Korea requires proof of knowing use but benefits from its more clearly defined categories of prohibited persons.

### Cross-Border Enforcement

The increasing globalization of securities markets has made cross-border insider trading a significant enforcement challenge. The EU's harmonized framework under MAR provides the most developed model for cross-border cooperation within a single regulatory zone. The United States relies on bilateral memoranda of understanding (MOUs) and the IOSCO Multilateral MOU for international cooperation. South Korea has entered into cooperation agreements with major financial regulators, including the SEC and BaFin.
