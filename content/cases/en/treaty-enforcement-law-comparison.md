## Summary

Treaty enforcement -- the mechanisms by which international agreements are incorporated into domestic legal systems and made binding upon states, individuals, and institutions -- presents one of the most fundamental questions in international and constitutional law, touching upon the relationship between international obligations and national sovereignty. South Korea adopts a monist approach under Article 6(1) of the Constitution (헌법), which provides that treaties duly concluded and promulgated in accordance with the Constitution shall have the same force as domestic law, though the Constitutional Court (헌법재판소) has developed jurisprudence clarifying the hierarchy between treaties and statutes, and the question of self-executing versus non-self-executing treaties remains an active area of judicial interpretation. The United States follows a dualist-influenced approach under Article VI of the Constitution (the Supremacy Clause), which declares treaties to be "the supreme Law of the Land," but the Supreme Court's distinction between self-executing and non-self-executing treaties -- articulated most recently in Medellín v. Texas, 552 U.S. 491 (2008) -- significantly limits the domestic enforceability of many international agreements. Germany occupies a nuanced position: Article 25 of the Grundgesetz (Basic Law) gives general rules of international law precedence over ordinary federal legislation, and Article 59(2) GG requires parliamentary approval for treaties relating to political relations or federal legislation, transforming approved treaties into federal law with the rank of ordinary statute, though the Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) has established in the Görgülü decision (2004) that treaties -- including the ECHR -- must be taken into account by German courts even where they have not been directly incorporated. At the European level, the EU legal order represents a unique supranational treaty enforcement system in which EU treaties (TEU, TFEU) have direct effect and supremacy over member state law, as established by the Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU) in Van Gend en Loos (1963) and Costa v. ENEL (1964), while the ECHR is enforced through the European Court of Human Rights with binding judgments supervised by the Committee of Ministers.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Article 6(1) of the Constitution of the Republic of Korea (대한민국 헌법) states: "Treaties duly concluded and promulgated under the Constitution and the generally recognized rules of international law shall have the same force as the domestic laws of the Republic of Korea." Article 60(1) provides that the National Assembly (국회) shall have the right to consent to the conclusion and ratification of treaties pertaining to mutual assistance or mutual security, treaties concerning important international organizations, treaties of friendship, commerce, and navigation, treaties pertaining to sovereignty restrictions, peace treaties, treaties imposing financial obligations on the state or the people, and treaties related to legislative matters.

### Constitutional Court Jurisprudence

The Constitutional Court (헌법재판소) has addressed the domestic status of treaties in several important decisions. In its decision on the constitutionality of the Korea-Chile Free Trade Agreement (2005헌마268, decided November 24, 2005), the Court held that while treaties have the force of domestic law, they remain subordinate to the Constitution and subject to constitutional review. The Court has also addressed the question of whether specific treaty provisions are self-executing, adopting a case-by-case analysis similar to but not identical with the US approach.

### Treaty Practice

South Korea is party to a substantial number of bilateral and multilateral treaties, including the ROK-US Mutual Defense Treaty (한미상호방위조약, 1953), numerous free trade agreements (the Korea-EU FTA entered into force in 2011; the KORUS FTA in 2012), and major multilateral conventions. The Ministry of Foreign Affairs (외교부) maintains the treaty database, and the Office of Government Legislation (법제처) reviews treaties for domestic legal compatibility.

### Enforcement Challenges

| Issue | Korean Approach |
|-------|----------------|
| Treaty-statute conflict | Later-in-time rule applies if treaty has statutory rank; constitution superior |
| Self-executing provisions | Courts apply directly if sufficiently specific and complete |
| Non-self-executing provisions | Require implementing legislation by National Assembly |
| Customary international law | Recognized under Art. 6(1) as having force of domestic law |

## United States

### Legal Framework

The US Constitution addresses treaties in two principal provisions. Article II, Section 2 grants the President the power to make treaties with the advice and consent of the Senate, provided two-thirds of the Senators present concur. Article VI, Clause 2 (the Supremacy Clause) provides that "all Treaties made, or which shall be made, under the Authority of the United States, shall be the supreme Law of the Land."

**Congressional-Executive Agreements:** In addition to Article II treaties, the United States enters into "congressional-executive agreements" (approved by simple majority of both houses) and "sole executive agreements" (entered into by the President alone under claimed inherent authority). The constitutional validity of congressional-executive agreements as a substitute for Article II treaties has been accepted by practice but remains debated by scholars.

### Self-Executing vs. Non-Self-Executing Treaties

The distinction between self-executing and non-self-executing treaties is the most critical doctrinal feature of US treaty enforcement.

**Foster v. Neilson, 27 U.S. (2 Pet.) 253 (1829):** Chief Justice Marshall established the foundational distinction, stating that a treaty is "equivalent to an act of the legislature" when it "operates of itself without the aid of any legislative provision," but when the treaty stipulates that either party shall perform a particular act, "the treaty addresses itself to the political, not the judicial department."

**Medellín v. Texas, 552 U.S. 491 (2008):** The Supreme Court held that neither the International Court of Justice's judgment in Avena (concerning consular notification rights under the VCCR) nor the President's executive memorandum directing state courts to comply with the ICJ judgment was directly enforceable federal law. The Court held that the ICJ judgment was not self-executing and that the President lacked authority to unilaterally convert a non-self-executing treaty obligation into binding domestic law.

### Treaty Termination

The question of which branch holds the power to terminate treaties remains constitutionally unsettled. In Goldwater v. Carter, 444 U.S. 996 (1979), the Supreme Court dismissed a challenge to President Carter's unilateral termination of the Mutual Defense Treaty with Taiwan, with a plurality holding the case presented a non-justiciable political question. Presidents have subsequently terminated treaties unilaterally (e.g., the US withdrawal from the INF Treaty in 2019 and the Open Skies Treaty in 2020).

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's Grundgesetz (Basic Law) addresses international law in several provisions. Article 25 GG provides: "The general rules of international law shall be an integral part of federal law. They shall take precedence over the laws and shall directly create rights and duties for the inhabitants of the federal territory." Article 59(2) GG provides that treaties relating to the political relations of the Federation or to matters of federal legislation require consent of the competent legislative bodies in the form of a federal law (Zustimmungsgesetz).

**Rank of Treaties:** Treaties approved under Article 59(2) GG acquire the rank of ordinary federal statute (einfaches Bundesgesetz). This means that, in principle, a later federal statute could override a treaty provision (lex posterior derogat legi priori). However, the Bundesverfassungsgericht has developed the principle of Völkerrechtsfreundlichkeit (friendliness toward international law), which requires German law to be interpreted, as far as possible, in conformity with international obligations.

### Görgülü Decision (2004)

The Bundesverfassungsgericht's landmark decision of October 14, 2004 (2 BvR 1481/04) -- the Görgülü case -- established that German courts are obligated to take the ECHR and the ECtHR's case law into account when interpreting domestic law. The Court held that while the ECHR has the rank of ordinary federal statute, the Völkerrechtsfreundlichkeit principle and the constitutional protection of fundamental rights require that ECHR standards be given interpretive weight. However, the BVerfG reserved the authority to depart from ECtHR judgments where their application would violate the Grundgesetz itself.

### EU Treaty Enforcement -- Lisbon Decision (2009)

In the Lissabon-Urteil (Lisbon Treaty judgment) of June 30, 2009 (2 BvE 2/08), the BVerfG upheld the constitutionality of the Treaty of Lisbon but established limits on the transfer of sovereign powers to the EU. The Court articulated the concept of "constitutional identity" (Verfassungsidentität) that cannot be surrendered through European integration and asserted its authority to review EU acts for ultra vires or violations of constitutional identity.

### Enforcement Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Application |
|-----------|-------------|
| Transformation through Zustimmungsgesetz | Treaties become federal statute through parliamentary approval |
| Völkerrechtsfreundlichkeit | Courts interpret domestic law in conformity with international obligations |
| Article 25 GG | General rules of international law have supra-statutory rank |
| EU law primacy | EU treaties and regulations have direct effect and supremacy |

## ECHR & EU

### EU Treaty Enforcement System

The EU represents the most developed supranational treaty enforcement system in the world, with treaties (TEU, TFEU) enforced through multiple mechanisms.

**Direct Effect -- Van Gend en Loos (Case 26/62, 1963):** The CJEU established that EU treaty provisions can create individual rights enforceable in national courts, provided the provision is clear, precise, unconditional, and requires no further legislative action.

**Supremacy -- Costa v. ENEL (Case 6/64, 1964):** The CJEU held that EU law takes precedence over conflicting national law, including subsequent national legislation. This principle has been affirmed and expanded in subsequent case law, though national constitutional courts (including the BVerfG) have asserted limits.

**Enforcement Actions (Art. 258-260 TFEU):** The European Commission may bring infringement proceedings against member states that fail to fulfill treaty obligations. Under Article 260(2) TFEU, the CJEU may impose lump sum or penalty payments on member states that fail to comply with its judgments.

### ECHR Enforcement

The ECHR enforcement system operates through the European Court of Human Rights, whose judgments are binding on the parties (Article 46(1) ECHR). The Committee of Ministers of the Council of Europe supervises the execution of judgments under Article 46(2).

**Pilot Judgments:** The ECtHR has developed the "pilot judgment" procedure to address systemic or structural problems in member states that generate large numbers of similar applications. In pilot judgments such as Broniowski v. Poland (Application No. 31443/96, Grand Chamber, 2004), the Court identifies the systemic problem and requires the state to implement general remedial measures.

**Non-Compliance Challenges:** Several states have faced persistent non-compliance issues. Russia's refusal to execute certain ECtHR judgments prior to its expulsion from the Council of Europe in 2022, Turkey's continued detention of Osman Kavala despite the ECtHR's Grand Chamber judgment in Kavala v. Turkey (2019), and Azerbaijan's non-compliance with Mammadov v. Azerbaijan judgments illustrate the enforcement challenges.

### Relationship Between EU Law and ECHR

The relationship between the EU legal order and the ECHR remains complex. The CJEU's Opinion 2/13 (December 18, 2014) rejected the draft agreement for EU accession to the ECHR, citing concerns about the autonomy of EU law and the CJEU's exclusive jurisdiction over questions of EU law. Negotiations on revised accession terms continue.

## Comparative Analysis

### Monism vs. Dualism

The four jurisdictions represent different positions on the monism-dualism spectrum. South Korea and the EU adopt monist approaches where international obligations have domestic legal force without requiring separate implementing legislation. The United States leans dualist through the self-executing treaty doctrine, effectively requiring congressional implementation for many treaties. Germany occupies a middle position, transforming treaties into domestic statute through parliamentary approval but also recognizing the direct applicability of general international law under Article 25 GG.

### Democratic Legitimacy

All jurisdictions require some form of parliamentary participation in treaty-making, reflecting the principle that international obligations should have democratic legitimacy. The US Senate's two-thirds requirement is the most demanding. South Korea and Germany require simple majority approval. The EU system involves both the European Parliament and national parliaments in treaty ratification processes.

### Judicial Enforcement

The role of courts varies significantly. US courts apply the self-executing doctrine to limit judicial enforcement of treaties. German courts are required to consider international law but may ultimately defer to the Grundgesetz. South Korean courts apply treaties directly when self-executing. The CJEU enforces EU treaties through the most robust judicial mechanism, with the power to impose financial penalties on non-compliant states.

### Treaty Hierarchy

The hierarchical position of treaties in the domestic legal order has practical consequences. In the United States and Germany, treaties rank below the constitution but at the level of ordinary statute, creating the possibility of override by later legislation. South Korea's position is similar. Only EU law claims supremacy over all national law, including constitutional provisions -- a claim that national constitutional courts have not universally accepted.
