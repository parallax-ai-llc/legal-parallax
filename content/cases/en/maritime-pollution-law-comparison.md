## Summary

Maritime pollution -- the introduction of harmful substances into the marine environment through vessel discharges, oil spills, dumping, or land-based sources -- represents one of the most heavily regulated areas of international environmental law, governed by a dense web of multilateral conventions administered primarily through the International Maritime Organization (IMO). South Korea, as a major shipbuilding and shipping nation, regulates maritime pollution through the Marine Environment Management Act (해양환경관리법), which implements MARPOL 73/78 standards and establishes strict liability for oil pollution damage, with the Hebei Spirit oil spill of December 2007 serving as a defining event that reshaped Korean marine environmental enforcement and compensation regimes. The United States operates under the Oil Pollution Act of 1990 (OPA 90), enacted in direct response to the Exxon Valdez disaster of March 1989, which established the Oil Spill Liability Trust Fund, imposed unlimited liability for gross negligence, and created the most stringent unilateral maritime pollution regime in the world; the Clean Water Act (33 U.S.C. Section 1251 et seq.) and the Act to Prevent Pollution from Ships (APPS, 33 U.S.C. Section 1901) provide additional enforcement mechanisms. Germany implements maritime pollution standards through the Federal Water Act (Wasserhaushaltsgesetz, WHG), the Ordinance on the Discharge of Waste Water into Waters (Abwasserverordnung), and specific North Sea and Baltic Sea protection regulations, operating within the framework of EU environmental directives and the OSPAR and HELCOM conventions. At the supranational European level, EU Directive 2005/35/EC on ship-source pollution (amended by Directive 2009/123/EC) imposes criminal penalties for illegal discharges, while the European Maritime Safety Agency (EMSA) provides satellite monitoring through CleanSeaNet, and the European Court of Human Rights has recognized environmental harm from marine pollution as engaging Article 8 rights to private and family life.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's maritime pollution regime is anchored in the Marine Environment Management Act (해양환경관리법), comprehensively revised in 2007 following the Hebei Spirit oil spill disaster. The Act consolidates provisions governing vessel-source pollution, ocean dumping, and land-based marine pollution into a unified statutory framework. The Ministry of Oceans and Fisheries (해양수산부) serves as the primary regulatory authority, while the Korea Coast Guard (해양경찰청) exercises enforcement jurisdiction over vessel operations and spill response.

The Act implements South Korea's obligations under the International Convention for the Prevention of Pollution from Ships (MARPOL 73/78), the International Convention on Civil Liability for Oil Pollution Damage (CLC 1992), the International Convention on the Establishment of an International Fund for Compensation for Oil Pollution Damage (Fund Convention 1992), and the International Convention for the Control and Management of Ships' Ballast Water and Sediments (BWM Convention 2004).

### Hebei Spirit Oil Spill (2007)

On December 7, 2007, the Hong Kong-registered oil tanker Hebei Spirit was struck by a crane barge owned by Samsung Heavy Industries while at anchor approximately 5 nautical miles off Taean, South Chungcheong Province. The collision ruptured three cargo tanks, releasing approximately 10,900 tonnes of crude oil into the Yellow Sea. The spill contaminated over 375 kilometers of coastline, devastated local fishing and aquaculture industries, and became the worst maritime environmental disaster in Korean history.

The aftermath prompted comprehensive legislative reform. The Marine Environment Management Act was substantially strengthened, response protocols were overhauled, and a domestic compensation regime supplementing the IOPC Fund was established. Criminal proceedings resulted in the conviction of both the Hebei Spirit's master and the Samsung Heavy Industries employees responsible for the barge. The Supreme Court of Korea (대법원) ultimately confirmed Samsung Heavy Industries' liability in 2015.

### Penalties

| Offense Category | Penalty |
|-----------------|---------|
| Illegal discharge of oil or noxious liquid substances | Up to 10 years imprisonment or fine up to 100 million KRW |
| Failure to report oil spill | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine up to 30 million KRW |
| Violation of ballast water management requirements | Up to 2 years imprisonment or fine up to 20 million KRW |
| Illegal ocean dumping | Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine up to 50 million KRW |
| Failure to maintain oil record book | Up to 1 year imprisonment or fine up to 10 million KRW |

### Marine Protected Areas

South Korea has designated over 30 marine protected areas under the Marine Ecosystem Conservation Act (해양생태계의 보전 및 관리에 관한 법률), imposing heightened pollution prevention requirements within these zones. Vessel traffic in sensitive areas is managed through the Korea Vessel Traffic Services system, operated by the Korea Coast Guard.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States maintains the world's most comprehensive unilateral maritime pollution regime, structured around three principal statutes.

**Oil Pollution Act of 1990 (OPA 90, 33 U.S.C. Section 2701 et seq.):** Enacted on August 18, 1990, in direct response to the Exxon Valdez oil spill of March 24, 1989, OPA 90 established a strict liability regime for oil discharges into navigable waters, shorelines, and the exclusive economic zone. Key provisions include mandatory double-hull requirements for tank vessels, creation of the Oil Spill Liability Trust Fund (currently funded at approximately $3.318 billion through a per-barrel tax on imported and domestically produced oil), mandatory oil spill response plans for vessels and facilities, and the elimination of liability caps for parties found to have acted with gross negligence or willful misconduct.

**Clean Water Act (CWA, 33 U.S.C. Section 1251 et seq.):** Section 311 of the CWA prohibits the discharge of oil or hazardous substances into navigable waters and establishes criminal penalties of up to $50,000 per day of violation and imprisonment of up to three years for knowing violations, or up to six years for repeat offenders.

**Act to Prevent Pollution from Ships (APPS, 33 U.S.C. Section 1901 et seq.):** APPS implements MARPOL 73/78 domestically, criminalizing improper discharges and record-keeping violations. APPS prosecutions have been particularly effective against foreign-flagged vessels calling at U.S. ports with falsified oil record books.

### Deepwater Horizon (2010)

The Deepwater Horizon explosion and oil spill of April 20, 2010, in the Gulf of Mexico resulted in the deaths of 11 workers and the release of approximately 4.9 million barrels of crude oil over 87 days, making it the largest marine oil spill in history. BP was found grossly negligent by the U.S. District Court for the Eastern District of Louisiana (In re: Oil Spill by the Oil Rig "Deepwater Horizon," MDL No. 2179). Total penalties and settlements exceeded $65 billion, including a $20.8 billion settlement with five Gulf states in 2016. The disaster led to the dissolution of the Minerals Management Service and creation of the Bureau of Safety and Environmental Enforcement (BSEE) and the Bureau of Ocean Energy Management (BOEM).

### Penalties

| Offense Level | Penalty |
|---------------|---------|
| CWA Section 311 -- negligent discharge | Up to $25,000/day and/or 1 year imprisonment |
| CWA Section 311 -- knowing discharge | Up to $50,000/day and/or 3 years imprisonment (6 years for repeat) |
| OPA 90 -- civil liability (tanker > 3,000 GT) | Greater of $2,200/GT or $18.8 million |
| OPA 90 -- civil liability (gross negligence) | Unlimited |
| APPS -- false record keeping | Up to $250,000 fine and/or 6 years imprisonment per count |

### State Regulation

Several states impose requirements exceeding federal standards. Washington State's Oil Spill Prevention Act mandates tug escorts for laden tank vessels transiting Puget Sound. California's Lempert-Keene-Seastrand Oil Spill Prevention and Response Act establishes a state-level oil spill response organization and imposes unlimited liability for oil spills.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany regulates maritime pollution within a multi-layered framework comprising federal legislation, EU directives, and regional sea conventions.

**Wasserhaushaltsgesetz (WHG -- Federal Water Act):** Section 324a StGB criminalizes the pollution of waters, including marine waters within German jurisdiction. Section 89 WHG establishes strict liability for water pollution, requiring any person who introduces or discharges substances into a body of water and thereby causes harm to compensate the injured party regardless of fault.

**Seeaufgabengesetz (Maritime Responsibilities Act):** This Act allocates maritime safety and environmental protection responsibilities among federal agencies. The Federal Maritime and Hydrographic Agency (Bundesamt für Seeschifffahrt und Hydrographie, BSH) exercises regulatory authority over vessel environmental compliance.

**Havariekommando (Joint Maritime Emergency Command):** Established in 2003 following the Pallas incident, the Havariekommando coordinates maritime emergency response among federal and state authorities, integrating resources from the Federal Ministry of Transport, the German Navy, and coastal state (Länder) emergency services.

### North Sea and Baltic Sea Protection

Germany participates in two regional marine protection regimes:

**OSPAR Convention (1992):** The Convention for the Protection of the Marine Environment of the North-East Atlantic governs pollution prevention, biodiversity protection, and offshore industry regulation in the North Sea. Germany has implemented OSPAR recommendations through domestic regulation, including restrictions on offshore chemical discharges and mandatory environmental impact assessments for offshore installations.

**HELCOM (Helsinki Commission):** The Baltic Marine Environment Protection Commission administers the Helsinki Convention (1992), addressing pollution prevention in the Baltic Sea. Germany's obligations include the designation of the Baltic Sea as a Special Area under MARPOL Annexes I, II, IV, and V, imposing the most restrictive discharge standards under MARPOL.

### Penalties

| Offense Category | Penalty |
|-----------------|---------|
| Water pollution (Section 324a StGB) | Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine |
| Negligent water pollution | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine |
| Violation of MARPOL discharge standards | Administrative fine up to 50,000 EUR |
| Failure to report pollution incident | Fine up to 25,000 EUR |
| Operation of vessel without required environmental certificates | Fine up to 50,000 EUR; vessel detention |

### Pallas Incident (1998)

The grounding of the Panamanian-flagged timber carrier Pallas off the coast of Amrum on October 25, 1998, exposed critical deficiencies in Germany's maritime emergency response capability. The vessel broke apart, releasing approximately 244 tonnes of heavy fuel oil that contaminated Wadden Sea coastline and killed an estimated 16,000 seabirds. The incident directly led to the establishment of the Havariekommando in 2003 and reforms to federal-state coordination protocols for maritime emergencies.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Ship-Source Pollution Directive

Directive 2005/35/EC of the European Parliament and of the Council of September 7, 2005, on ship-source pollution and the introduction of penalties for infringements, as amended by Directive 2009/123/EC, establishes a harmonized framework for criminal penalties for illegal discharges from ships.

**Scope:** The Directive applies to discharges of polluting substances from ships in the internal waters, territorial seas, straits used for international transit passage, exclusive economic zones, and high seas areas of member states. It covers MARPOL Annex I (oil) and Annex II (noxious liquid substances) violations.

**Criminal Liability:** The Directive requires member states to ensure that ship-source pollution committed with intent, recklessness, or serious negligence constitutes a criminal offense. Penalties must be "effective, proportionate and dissuasive" and may include imprisonment.

**CJEU Jurisprudence -- Intertanko (Case C-308/06, 2008):** The Court of Justice of the European Union ruled that the Directive's imposition of criminal liability for ship-source pollution was compatible with MARPOL and UNCLOS, confirming EU competence to establish criminal penalties for maritime pollution beyond MARPOL minimum standards.

### European Maritime Safety Agency (EMSA)

EMSA, established by Regulation (EC) No 1406/2002, provides technical assistance and operational support to member states on maritime safety and pollution prevention. Its CleanSeaNet satellite monitoring service provides near-real-time detection of oil spills and illegal discharges across European waters, processing approximately 2,500 satellite images per year.

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR has addressed marine pollution through the lens of Article 8 (right to respect for private and family life) and Article 2 (right to life).

**Tatar v. Romania (Application No. 67021/01, Judgment of January 27, 2009):** While primarily concerning freshwater contamination from the Baia Mare cyanide spill, the Court established that severe environmental pollution, including that affecting marine and aquatic ecosystems, can constitute interference with Article 8 rights, even absent proof of direct physical harm to the applicant.

**Cordella and Others v. Italy (Application No. 54414/13, Judgment of January 24, 2019):** The Court found Italy violated Articles 8 and 13 regarding industrial pollution from the ILVA steelworks in Taranto, which contaminated surrounding marine and terrestrial environments. The judgment affirmed states' positive obligation to adopt reasonable and appropriate measures to secure individuals' rights against environmental pollution.

### International Oil Pollution Compensation Fund

The IOPC Fund system, comprising the 1992 Civil Liability Convention and the 1992 Fund Convention, provides a two-tier compensation regime for oil pollution damage. The first tier imposes strict liability on the shipowner (limited to approximately 89.77 million SDR for ships exceeding 140,000 GT under the 2000 Protocol). The second tier provides supplementary compensation through the 1992 Fund (up to approximately 203 million SDR) and the Supplementary Fund Protocol (up to approximately 750.6 million SDR). All four jurisdictions under comparison are parties to the 1992 CLC and Fund Convention.

### Ballast Water Management

Ballast water -- seawater carried in ships' ballast tanks for stability and discharged at destination ports -- has been identified as a major vector for invasive species and marine pollution. Regulatory responses include:

**South Korea:** The Ballast Water Management Act (2017) implements the BWM Convention, requiring ships to install and operate approved ballast water management systems. The Korea Coast Guard conducts inspections at Korean ports to verify compliance. Ships entering Korean waters must submit ballast water reports.

**United States:** The Vessel Incidental Discharge Act (VIDA, 2018) replaced the EPA's Vessel General Permit system, transferring regulatory authority over ballast water discharges to the Coast Guard and EPA jointly. The Act establishes standards of performance for ballast water treatment that are among the world's most stringent, requiring treatment to a standard equivalent to the BWM Convention's D-2 standard.

**Germany:** Germany implements the BWM Convention through flag state regulations administered by the BSH. Ships flying the German flag must comply with BWM Convention requirements, and port state control inspections at German ports verify foreign-flagged vessel compliance.

### Underwater Noise Pollution

An emerging area of maritime environmental regulation concerns underwater noise pollution from shipping, which affects marine mammals, fish, and other marine organisms. The IMO has adopted voluntary guidelines for the reduction of underwater noise from commercial shipping (MEPC.1/Circ.833). The EU's Marine Strategy Framework Directive (2008/56/EC) includes underwater noise as a descriptor of good environmental status. South Korea and the United States have not yet adopted specific underwater noise regulations.

## Comparative Analysis

### Liability Standards

All four jurisdictions impose strict liability for oil pollution damage, reflecting the international consensus established by the CLC 1969/1992. However, the United States stands apart in its approach to liability caps. While the CLC/Fund system imposes calculable limits on shipowner and fund liability, OPA 90 eliminates all liability caps for parties found to have acted with gross negligence or willful misconduct -- a provision spectacularly applied in the Deepwater Horizon litigation. This U.S. exceptionalism extends to the country's refusal to ratify the CLC or Fund Conventions, maintaining instead its domestic Oil Spill Liability Trust Fund as the primary compensation mechanism.

### Criminal Enforcement

The United States has been the most aggressive in criminal enforcement of maritime pollution laws, particularly through APPS prosecutions targeting falsified oil record books. Between 2000 and 2025, the U.S. Department of Justice secured over 60 criminal convictions under APPS, with cumulative fines exceeding $500 million. Germany and South Korea maintain criminal penalties but have lower prosecution rates. The EU Directive 2005/35/EC harmonized criminal liability across member states but left penalty levels to national discretion.

### Response Infrastructure

Post-disaster institutional reforms are a common feature. The Exxon Valdez prompted OPA 90 and restructured U.S. Coast Guard response capabilities. The Hebei Spirit spill catalyzed Korean legislative reform. The Pallas incident led to Germany's Havariekommando. The Prestige disaster off Spain and France in 2002 accelerated EU maritime safety reforms and expanded EMSA's mandate.

### Emission Control Areas

All four jurisdictions participate in IMO-designated Emission Control Areas (ECAs) imposing stricter sulfur oxide, nitrogen oxide, and particulate matter emission standards. The North American ECA (effective August 2012) extends 200 nautical miles from the U.S. and Canadian coastlines. The North Sea and Baltic Sea ECAs apply to Germany and other European littoral states. South Korea participates in port-specific emission control measures and has advocated for an East Asian ECA.
