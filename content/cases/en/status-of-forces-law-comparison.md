## Summary

Status of forces agreements (SOFAs) -- bilateral or multilateral international agreements that define the legal status of military personnel and their dependents stationed in a foreign country -- regulate one of the most sensitive areas of international relations, balancing the sending state's interest in maintaining command authority over its forces with the host state's sovereignty and jurisdiction over its own territory. South Korea's SOFA with the United States (대한민국과 미합중국 간의 상호방위조약 제4조에 의한 시설과 구역 및 대한민국에서의 합중국군대의 지위에 관한 협정), signed on July 9, 1966, and significantly revised in 2001, governs the status of approximately 28,500 US military personnel stationed in South Korea under the ROK-US Mutual Defense Treaty, and has been the subject of sustained public controversy particularly regarding criminal jurisdiction, environmental remediation, and labor rights. The United States maintains SOFAs with over 100 countries and operates the largest network of overseas military installations in the world; the NATO SOFA of 1951 provides the foundational framework for US force status in Europe, supplemented by bilateral supplementary agreements with individual host nations. Germany hosts the largest US military presence in Europe (approximately 35,000 personnel) under the NATO SOFA and the Supplementary Agreement to the NATO SOFA with respect to Foreign Forces stationed in Germany (Zusatzabkommen zum NATO-Truppenstatut, 1959/1993), with the Residence Agreement (Aufenthaltsvertrag) of 1954/1990 and the Two Plus Four Treaty of 1990 having fundamentally reshaped the legal framework for foreign forces in Germany. At the European level, the NATO SOFA of June 19, 1951, provides the multilateral framework applicable to all NATO member states, while the ECHR has addressed SOFA-related issues through the lens of jurisdiction under Article 1 and state responsibility for acts of military personnel, and EU missions abroad (CSDP operations) operate under their own status of forces/status of mission agreements.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

The ROK-US SOFA was concluded pursuant to Article IV of the Mutual Defense Treaty between the Republic of Korea and the United States (한미상호방위조약, signed October 1, 1953). The original SOFA was signed on July 9, 1966, and entered into force on February 9, 1967. A significant revision was agreed in 2001, following years of public pressure catalyzed by incidents involving US military personnel.

### Criminal Jurisdiction (Article XXII)

Article XXII of the ROK-US SOFA establishes a concurrent jurisdiction framework:

**US Primary Jurisdiction:** The United States retains exclusive jurisdiction over offenses arising from official duty, offenses solely against US property or security, and offenses solely against the person or property of another member of the US forces. For offenses where both states have jurisdiction, the US has primary jurisdiction over offenses arising from any act or omission done in the performance of official duty.

**Korean Primary Jurisdiction:** Korea has primary jurisdiction over all other offenses, including off-duty crimes against Korean nationals. The 2001 revision significantly strengthened Korea's jurisdictional position by providing for earlier transfer of custody: Korean authorities may request custody of US personnel from the time of indictment (previously, the US retained custody until a final verdict). For 12 specified "heinous crimes" (including murder, rape, kidnapping, drug trafficking, and DUI resulting in death), Korea may request custody at the time of arrest.

### Environmental Provisions

The 2001 revision introduced environmental provisions (Memorandum of Special Understandings on Environmental Protection) requiring the US forces to adopt environmental standards consistent with Korean environmental law or US environmental standards, whichever is more protective. The agreement established a joint Environmental Governing Standards document. However, cleanup of contaminated US military bases (particularly Agent Orange contamination discovered at Camp Carroll in 2011) has been a persistent source of controversy, with disputes over the allocation of remediation costs.

### Labor Issues

Approximately 12,000 Korean nationals are employed by US Forces Korea (USFK) under the SOFA labor provisions. Working conditions, wages, and collective bargaining rights have been contentious, with the Korean Employees' Union (주한미군 한국인노동조합) advocating for improvements. A dispute in 2020 resulted in the furlough of approximately half of the Korean workforce when cost-sharing negotiations stalled.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States maintains SOFAs with over 100 countries, operating the world's largest network of overseas military installations (approximately 750 bases in about 80 countries). The principal multilateral SOFA is the NATO Status of Forces Agreement (Agreement between the Parties to the North Atlantic Treaty regarding the Status of their Forces, June 19, 1951).

**NATO SOFA Article VII (Criminal Jurisdiction):** The cornerstone provision establishes concurrent jurisdiction with priorities: the sending state has primary jurisdiction over offenses arising from official duty and offenses against the sending state's property, security, or personnel; the host state has primary jurisdiction in all other cases. Either state may request a waiver of its primary jurisdiction.

**Bilateral SOFAs:** The US negotiates bilateral SOFAs tailored to specific relationships. Key bilateral SOFAs include the ROK-US SOFA, the Japan-US SOFA (Agreement Under Article VI of the Treaty of Mutual Cooperation and Security, 1960), the Philippines Visiting Forces Agreement (VFA, 1998; Enhanced Defense Cooperation Agreement, 2014), and the Iraq-US Strategic Framework Agreement (2008).

### Congressional Oversight

The US Constitution does not explicitly address SOFAs. The executive branch typically concludes SOFAs as executive agreements rather than Article II treaties, avoiding the requirement of Senate advice and consent. This practice has been challenged by Congress. The Case-Zablocki Act (1 U.S.C. Section 112b) requires the Secretary of State to transmit all international agreements (other than treaties) to Congress within 60 days.

### Claims and Liability

The Military Claims Act (10 U.S.C. Section 2734) authorizes the settlement of claims by foreign nationals for damage, injury, or death arising from the actions of US military personnel outside the United States. Claims are processed through the military services' claims offices. The Foreign Claims Act (10 U.S.C. Section 2734a) provides additional authority.

### Key SOFA Provisions

| Element | Standard US SOFA Position |
|---------|--------------------------|
| Criminal jurisdiction | NATO SOFA Art. VII framework with variations |
| Civil claims | Joint claims processing; foreign claims act |
| Tax exemptions | Customs and tax exemptions for forces and their families |
| Driving licenses | Recognition of home state licenses |
| Entry/exit | Freedom of entry/exit for force members |
| Contractors | Varying status; Technical Agreements may cover |

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany hosts the largest US military presence in Europe, with approximately 35,000 US personnel at installations including Ramstein Air Base, Landstuhl Regional Medical Center, and the US Army Garrison Stuttgart. The legal framework for foreign forces in Germany operates through multiple layers.

**NATO SOFA (1951):** Germany acceded to the NATO SOFA upon joining NATO in 1955. The Agreement applies to all NATO forces stationed in Germany.

**Supplementary Agreement (Zusatzabkommen zum NATO-Truppenstatut, 1959/1993):** The Supplementary Agreement provides detailed implementing provisions specific to Germany. Originally signed on August 3, 1959, it was substantially revised on March 18, 1993, following German reunification and the changed security landscape. The 1993 revision gave Germany significantly greater jurisdiction over foreign forces and required environmental protection measures.

### Criminal Jurisdiction

Under the Supplementary Agreement, Germany exercises primary criminal jurisdiction over foreign military personnel for off-duty offenses more assertively than many other host states. German authorities may decline to exercise jurisdiction ("waiver") in minor cases, but for serious offenses -- particularly those involving German victims -- prosecution in German courts is the norm.

The Supplementary Agreement Article 19 provides for German court jurisdiction, with consultation procedures between German prosecutors and sending state military authorities. German procedural safeguards (including the right to counsel and the prohibition on cruel punishment) apply in all cases tried before German courts.

### Environmental Obligations

The 1993 revision of the Supplementary Agreement strengthened environmental protection requirements. Article 53 requires foreign forces to comply with German environmental law. Environmental impact assessments are required for new construction. Contamination remediation responsibilities were a significant issue during the post-Cold War base closure process, as numerous former military installations were found to have soil and groundwater contamination.

### Base Closures and Realignments

The end of the Cold War and subsequent US military realignments resulted in significant base closures in Germany. The number of US military installations declined from over 800 during the Cold War to approximately 40 as of 2025. The closure process raised complex legal questions about environmental remediation, property reversion, and community economic impact.

| Supplementary Agreement (1959/1993) | Germany-specific implementing provisions |

## ECHR & EU

### NATO SOFA Framework

The NATO SOFA of June 19, 1951, provides the foundational multilateral SOFA framework for Western military alliances. All NATO member states are parties. Key provisions include: Article I (definitions of "force," "civilian component," and "dependent"); Article VII (criminal jurisdiction); Article VIII (civil claims and damages); and Article IX (customs and tax exemptions).

### ECtHR Jurisprudence

The ECtHR has addressed SOFA-related issues through the lens of jurisdiction and state responsibility.

**Al-Skeini and Others v. United Kingdom (Application No. 55721/07, Grand Chamber Judgment of July 7, 2011):** While concerning military occupation rather than SOFA-based presence, the Court established that states exercising effective authority over individuals through their military forces abroad are bound by the ECHR, with significant implications for SOFA frameworks.

**Jaloud v. Netherlands (Application No. 47708/08, Grand Chamber Judgment of November 20, 2014):** The Court found that the Netherlands exercised jurisdiction under Article 1 ECHR when Dutch military personnel shot an Iraqi civilian at a checkpoint during the occupation of Iraq. The case has implications for the responsibility of SOFA-sending states for the actions of their military personnel abroad.

**Behrami and Behrami v. France and Saramati v. France, Germany and Norway (Application Nos. 71412/01 and 78166/01, Grand Chamber Decision of May 2, 2007):** The Court attributed actions of NATO-led KFOR forces in Kosovo to the United Nations rather than the contributing states, finding the applications inadmissible ratione personae. This attribution analysis has been debated and has implications for SOFA-based military operations.

### EU CSDP Mission SOFAs

EU Common Security and Defence Policy (CSDP) operations abroad require status of forces/status of mission agreements (SOFAs/SOMAs) with host states. The EU SOFA model, based on the NATO SOFA framework, establishes the legal status of EU military and civilian personnel deployed in CSDP missions such as EUFOR, EUNAVFOR, and civilian missions (EULEX Kosovo, EUMM Georgia).

### Visiting Forces and Jurisdiction

The ECHR system requires that SOFA-based immunity provisions be compatible with Convention rights. Where SOFA arrangements result in a gap in accountability -- for example, where neither the sending nor the host state exercises effective jurisdiction over a crime -- this may raise issues under Article 6 (right of access to a court) or Article 13 (right to an effective remedy).

## Comparative Analysis

### Jurisdictional Balance

The balance of criminal jurisdiction between sending and host states varies significantly across SOFAs. The NATO SOFA framework provides a default allocation that generally favors host state jurisdiction for off-duty offenses. The ROK-US SOFA has moved toward greater Korean jurisdiction through the 2001 revision but remains a source of tension. Germany exercises robust host state jurisdiction under the Supplementary Agreement.

### Custody Transfer

The timing of custody transfer from the sending state to the host state is a critical practical issue. The ROK-US SOFA's 2001 revision improved Korea's position by allowing custody from indictment (or arrest for heinous crimes). The Japan-US SOFA has been criticized for not providing early custody transfer, a significant political issue in Okinawa.

### Environmental Accountability

Environmental obligations represent a growing area of SOFA law. The ROK-US SOFA's 2001 environmental provisions and the German Supplementary Agreement's 1993 revision both addressed environmental concerns. The US has faced persistent criticism for inadequate environmental remediation at overseas bases.

### Democratic Accountability

SOFAs raise questions about democratic accountability: they are typically concluded as executive agreements without full legislative ratification, yet they significantly affect host state sovereignty. In South Korea, public protests have been the primary mechanism for driving SOFA reform. In Germany, the Bundestag exercises oversight through its parliamentary prerogatives. In the US, Congress has limited formal role in SOFA negotiations.
