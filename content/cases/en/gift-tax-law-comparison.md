## Summary

Gift tax -- levied on the transfer of property or assets from one person to another without full consideration in return -- serves as a backstop to inheritance and estate taxes, preventing individuals from circumventing wealth transfer taxation through lifetime gifts, while also raising revenue and addressing wealth concentration across generations. South Korea imposes a comprehensive gift tax (증여세) under the Inheritance Tax and Gift Tax Act (상속세 및 증여세법), with progressive rates ranging from 10 percent to 50 percent on cumulative gifts exceeding various thresholds, and includes a distinctive "deemed gift" regime (증여의제) that captures indirect economic benefits such as below-market transactions between related parties under Articles 33 through 42 of the Act. The United States imposes a federal gift tax under Internal Revenue Code Sections 2501-2524, unified with the estate tax through a single lifetime exemption of $13.61 million per individual (2024), with a flat 40 percent rate on taxable gifts exceeding the exemption, while annual exclusion gifts of $18,000 per donee (2024) escape both gift tax and reporting requirements entirely. Germany levies gift tax (Schenkungsteuer) under the Inheritance and Gift Tax Act (Erbschaftsteuer- und Schenkungsteuergesetz, ErbStG), with rates varying from 7 percent to 50 percent depending on the value of the gift and the relationship between donor and donee, categorized into three tax classes (Steuerklassen), with generous personal allowances that renew every 10 years. At the EU level, gift taxation remains exclusively within member state competence, though the European Commission has addressed obstacles to cross-border gifts through Recommendation 2011/856/EU on double taxation relief in inheritance and gift situations, and the ECHR has considered whether confiscatory gift taxation violates Protocol 1, Article 1 property rights, generally affording states a wide margin of appreciation in fiscal matters.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's gift tax is governed by the Inheritance Tax and Gift Tax Act (상속세 및 증여세법, Act No. 19934), administered by the National Tax Service (국세청). The Act imposes tax on the recipient (수증자) of a gift, in contrast to the donor-based U.S. system. The tax base is the value of property received by a donee from a donor over the preceding 10-year period, accumulated to determine the applicable progressive rate.

### Rate Structure

| Cumulative Gift Value (KRW) | Rate |
|------------------------------|------|
| Up to 100 million | 10% |
| 100 million - 500 million | 20% |
| 500 million - 1 billion | 30% |
| 1 billion - 3 billion | 40% |
| Over 3 billion | 50% |

A 30 percent surcharge (할증과세) applies when the donee is the donor's grandchild or more remote descendant, effectively adding a generation-skipping penalty. If the donee is a minor grandchild receiving more than 2 billion KRW, the surcharge increases to 40 percent.

### Exemption Thresholds

Gift tax exemptions (증여재산공제) are cumulative over a 10-year rolling period:

| Relationship | Exemption Amount (KRW) |
|-------------|----------------------|
| Spouse | 600 million |
| Adult children | 50 million |
| Minor children | 20 million |
| Other lineal descendants | 50 million |
| Other relatives (within 6th degree) | 10 million |

### Deemed Gift Regime

South Korea's most distinctive feature is the comprehensive "deemed gift" (증여의제) regime under Articles 33 through 42 of the Act. These provisions capture indirect economic benefits that do not constitute outright transfers:

- **Article 35:** Below-market-value transfers between related parties where the price differential exceeds 30 percent or 300 million KRW
- **Article 37:** Debt forgiveness or assumption by related parties
- **Article 38:** Gifts of corporate opportunities such as conversion rights and preemptive rights
- **Article 39:** Increase in share value resulting from capital contributions by related parties
- **Article 40:** Benefits from mergers and share exchanges at off-market ratios
- **Article 41-4:** Benefits from listed company shares where insiders have advance knowledge of value-affecting events
- **Article 42:** Residual catch-all provision for any transaction resulting in a transfer of economic benefit without adequate consideration

### Valuation of Unlisted Shares

For gifts of unlisted company shares, the Act prescribes a "supplementary valuation method" (보충적 평가방법) under Article 63, combining a weighted average of net asset value and earnings value in a 2:3 ratio. This frequently results in disputes between taxpayers and the NTS over the appropriate valuation methodology. The Supreme Court (대법원) in Decision 2017Du38959 (2018) confirmed that the supplementary method must be applied unless the taxpayer demonstrates that a different method produces a more accurate market value.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The federal gift tax is imposed under IRC Section 2501 on the transfer of property by gift by any individual. The tax is unified with the estate tax through a single applicable credit amount under Section 2010, currently sheltering $13.61 million (2024) of cumulative taxable gifts and bequests from tax. The flat rate of 40 percent applies to taxable gifts exceeding the exemption amount.

### Annual Exclusion

Section 2503(b) provides an annual exclusion of $18,000 per donee (2024, adjusted for inflation), allowing donors to give up to that amount to an unlimited number of recipients without gift tax consequences or reporting requirements. Married couples may elect gift-splitting under Section 2513, effectively doubling the annual exclusion to $36,000 per donee. The annual exclusion must be for gifts of a "present interest" -- the donee must have an immediate right to use, possess, or enjoy the property.

### Unified Credit and Lifetime Exemption

The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 doubled the basic exclusion amount from approximately $5.6 million to $11.18 million (2018), indexed for inflation. This elevated exemption is scheduled to sunset after December 31, 2025, reverting to approximately $7 million (inflation-adjusted). The IRS issued regulations (Treasury Decision 9884, 84 Fed. Reg. 64999) confirming that gifts made using the expanded exemption before the sunset will not be "clawed back" if the exemption decreases.

### Generation-Skipping Transfer Tax

The GST tax under IRC Sections 2601-2664 imposes an additional flat 40 percent tax on transfers that skip a generation, such as gifts from grandparents to grandchildren. Each transferor has a separate GST exemption equal to the basic exclusion amount ($13.61 million in 2024). The GST tax applies to direct skips, taxable distributions, and taxable terminations of trusts.

### Chapter 14 Special Valuation Rules

Sections 2701-2704 impose special valuation rules for transfers between family members involving interests in corporations and partnerships. Section 2701 values certain retained rights at zero unless they are "qualified payments," preventing the use of preferred equity structures to transfer value at a discount. Section 2702 applies similar rules to transfers of interests in trusts, effectively eliminating the grantor retained interest trust (GRIT) technique except for qualified personal residence trusts (QPRTs) and grantor retained annuity trusts (GRATs).

### Landmark Cases

**Commissioner v. Duberstein, 363 U.S. 278 (1960):** Established the standard for distinguishing gifts from compensation, holding that the critical determination is the transferor's "detached and disinterested generosity" rather than the recipient's expectations.

**Dickman v. Commissioner, 465 U.S. 330 (1984):** The Supreme Court held that interest-free demand loans between family members constitute taxable gifts of the foregone interest, establishing the principle that economic benefits need not involve a transfer of property title.

**Estate of Strangi v. Commissioner, 417 F.3d 468 (5th Cir. 2005):** Upheld the IRS's application of Section 2036(a) to disregard a family limited partnership formed shortly before death, finding that the decedent retained enjoyment of the transferred assets.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's gift tax is governed by the Erbschaftsteuer- und Schenkungsteuergesetz (ErbStG), most recently amended significantly by the Erbschaftsteuerreform 2016 following the Bundesverfassungsgericht's landmark decision of December 17, 2014 (1 BvL 21/12). Gift tax (Schenkungsteuer) and inheritance tax (Erbschaftsteuer) are treated as a single tax with identical rates and exemptions, the difference being merely the triggering event (inter vivos transfer versus transfer at death).

### Tax Classes and Rates

Rates depend on the donee's relationship to the donor:

| Gift Value | Tax Class I (spouse, children, grandchildren) | Tax Class II (siblings, nieces/nephews, in-laws) | Tax Class III (unrelated persons) |
|-----------|----------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------------|----------------------------------|
| Up to EUR 75,000 | 7% | 15% | 30% |
| EUR 75,001 - 300,000 | 11% | 20% | 30% |
| EUR 300,001 - 600,000 | 15% | 25% | 30% |
| EUR 600,001 - 6,000,000 | 19% | 30% | 30% |
| EUR 6,000,001 - 13,000,000 | 23% | 35% | 50% |
| EUR 13,000,001 - 26,000,000 | 27% | 40% | 50% |
| Over EUR 26,000,000 | 30% | 43% | 50% |

### Personal Allowances (Freibeträge)

| Relationship | Allowance (EUR) | Renewal Period |
|-------------|----------------|----------------|
| Spouse / registered partner | 500,000 | Every 10 years |
| Children | 400,000 | Every 10 years |
| Grandchildren | 200,000 | Every 10 years |
| Parents (for gifts) | 20,000 | Every 10 years |
| Siblings | 20,000 | Every 10 years |
| All other persons | 20,000 | Every 10 years |

The 10-year renewal cycle creates a powerful planning tool: parents can transfer EUR 400,000 to each child every decade tax-free, with both parents utilizing their respective allowances (EUR 800,000 per child per decade).

### Business Asset Privilege (Verschonungsregelung)

Following the BVerfG's 2014 decision requiring reform of the business asset exemption, the 2016 reform established two options under Sections 13a and 13b ErbStG:

- **Regelverschonung (85% exemption):** Available for qualifying business assets if the business maintains at least 400 percent of historical payroll over 5 years post-transfer and the administrative asset ratio does not exceed 90 percent.
- **Optionsverschonung (100% exemption):** Requires maintenance of 700 percent of historical payroll over 7 years and an administrative asset ratio below 20 percent.

For transfers exceeding EUR 26 million, an individual needs test (Abschmelzungsmodell) or a reduced exemption (Erlassmodell) applies, ensuring that very large business transfers cannot benefit from full exemption without demonstrating personal necessity.

### Anti-Avoidance Provisions

Section 7(1) ErbStG broadly defines taxable gifts as any enrichment of one person at the expense of another. Sections 7(5) and 7(6) specifically target indirect gifts through corporate structures, such as contributions to a company in which the donee holds shares, or disproportionate distributions. The Bundesfinanzhof in decision II R 9/11 (2013) held that a shareholder's waiver of a claim against the company constituted a gift to the other shareholders.

## ECHR & EU

### European Commission Recommendation on Double Taxation

Recommendation 2011/856/EU of December 15, 2011, addressed the problem of double taxation in cross-border gift and inheritance situations within the EU. The Commission found that double taxation of gifts arises when multiple member states claim taxing rights based on the donor's residence, the donee's residence, and the situs of gifted property. The Recommendation proposed that member states give priority to the member state with the strongest nexus and provide credit for tax paid in other member states, but this non-binding instrument has had limited practical impact.

### ECHR Property Rights and Gift Taxation

The ECHR has addressed gift tax claims under Protocol 1, Article 1:

**Jokela v. Finland (Application No. 28856/95, 2002):** While primarily an expropriation case, the Court confirmed that tax assessments based on property valuations must bear a reasonable relationship to actual market values, and arbitrary valuation methods may violate property rights.

**Burden v. United Kingdom (Application No. 13378/05, 2008):** The Grand Chamber held that cohabiting sisters who would face inheritance tax on the death of either (unlike married couples or civil partners who are exempt) did not suffer discrimination under Article 14 read with Protocol 1, Article 1, because their relationship was qualitatively different from marriage. While this case concerned inheritance tax, the same principle applies to gift tax exemptions for spouses.

**Stummer v. Austria (Application No. 37452/02, 2011):** The Court affirmed that states enjoy a wide margin of appreciation in designing tax systems, including the determination of exemptions and thresholds, provided the system does not impose an individual and excessive burden.

### EU Free Movement and Gift Taxation

The CJEU has scrutinized member state gift tax provisions that discriminate based on residence:

**Mattner v. Finanzamt Velbert (C-510/08, 2010):** The Court held that Germany's provision granting a EUR 400,000 allowance for gifts from parents to children resident in Germany, but only EUR 2,000 for non-resident donees, violated the free movement of capital under Article 63 TFEU. This led Germany to amend Section 2(3) ErbStG to allow non-residents to elect unlimited tax liability and claim the full personal allowance.

**Welte v. Finanzamt Velbert (C-181/12, 2013):** Extended the Mattner principle to gifts involving third-country (non-EU) residents, confirming that the free movement of capital also applies to transfers between EU and non-EU states.

## Comparative Analysis

### Donor vs. Donee Liability

A fundamental structural difference divides the jurisdictions: the United States imposes gift tax on the donor (the person making the gift), while South Korea and Germany impose the tax on the donee (the recipient). This distinction affects compliance obligations, enforcement mechanisms, and the practical economics of gift-giving. In the U.S., the donor's liability means that wealthy individuals must track cumulative lifetime gifts against their unified exemption. In South Korea and Germany, the donee bears the economic burden, though in practice donors often pay the tax on the donee's behalf, which itself constitutes an additional taxable gift.

### Exemption Generosity

The United States offers by far the most generous exemption, with $13.61 million (approximately 18 billion KRW or EUR 12.5 million) shielded from gift tax entirely. This exemption dwarfs South Korea's 50 million KRW (approximately $37,000) per adult child per decade and Germany's EUR 400,000 per child per decade. The planned sunset of the U.S. exemption to approximately $7 million will narrow but not eliminate this gap.

### Anti-Avoidance Sophistication

South Korea's deemed gift regime is the most comprehensive among the jurisdictions studied, capturing a wide range of indirect benefits including corporate opportunity transfers, below-market transactions, and share value increases resulting from related-party actions. The United States addresses similar concerns through Chapter 14 special valuation rules and the assignment-of-income doctrine. Germany relies on broader statutory definitions of enrichment combined with general anti-avoidance principles.

### Generation-Skipping Provisions

The United States is alone in imposing a separate generation-skipping transfer tax, reflecting a policy of ensuring that wealth is taxed at each generational level. South Korea achieves a similar result through the 30 percent surcharge on gifts to grandchildren. Germany has no specific generation-skipping provision, relying instead on lower personal allowances for grandchildren (EUR 200,000 versus EUR 400,000 for children) to partially address the issue.

### Business Transfer Relief

Germany provides the most favorable treatment for gifts of business assets through the Verschonungsregelung, allowing up to 100 percent exemption if employment levels are maintained. South Korea offers reduced valuation for family business transfers under Article 18-2 of the Inheritance Tax and Gift Tax Act. The United States does not provide a specific gift tax exemption for business transfers, though valuation discounts for minority interests and lack of marketability effectively reduce the taxable value.
