## Summary

Military conscription -- the compulsory enlistment of citizens for military service -- remains one of the most contentious intersections of state sovereignty, individual liberty, and national security. South Korea maintains one of the most demanding active conscription systems in the world, requiring all able-bodied men to serve 18-21 months under the Military Service Act, with a punitive 36-month alternative service regime for conscientious objectors introduced only after the Constitutional Court's landmark 2018 ruling. The United States ended active conscription in 1973 but retains the Selective Service System under the Military Selective Service Act (50 U.S.C. Chapter 49), requiring all males aged 18-25 to register, with failure to do so constituting a felony punishable by up to $250,000 fine and 5 years imprisonment. Germany suspended conscription (Wehrpflicht) in 2011 through amendment of the Wehrpflichtgesetz, though Article 12a of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz) retains the constitutional authority to reinstate it. The European Court of Human Rights, in its landmark Grand Chamber decision in Bayatyan v. Armenia (2011), established that the right to conscientious objection to military service is protected under Article 9 of the European Convention, fundamentally reshaping the obligations of member states that maintain conscription.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's conscription system is among the most comprehensive in the world, rooted in constitutional mandate and detailed statutory provisions. The legal foundation comprises:

**Constitution of the Republic of Korea:**

- **Article 39(1):** "All citizens shall have the duty of national defense under the conditions as prescribed by Act."
- **Article 39(2):** "No citizen shall be treated unfavorably on account of the fulfillment of his obligation of military service."

**Military Service Act (byeongyeogbeop):**

The Military Service Act is the primary statute governing conscription. Key provisions include:

| Provision | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Article 3** | Every male national shall faithfully perform military service as prescribed by the Constitution and this Act |
| **Article 5** | Classification of military service types: active duty, reserve, supplementary, wartime labor, and alternative service |
| **Article 8** | Physical examination grades (1-7) determining service assignment |
| **Article 18** | Active duty service periods: Army -- 18 months; Navy -- 20 months; Air Force -- 21 months; Marine Corps -- 18 months |
| **Article 25** | Postponement provisions for students, athletes, and those with special circumstances |
| **Article 88** | Penalties for evasion: imprisonment for up to 3 years |

**Act on Alternative Military Service (2019):**

Following the Constitutional Court's June 2018 decision (2011Hun-Ba379), the National Assembly enacted the Act on Alternative Military Service, which took effect on January 1, 2020. Key features:

- Alternative service personnel serve 36 months -- twice the standard military service duration
- Service is performed in correctional facilities (prisons and detention centers)
- Personnel must reside in the correctional facility during the service period
- The Alternative Service Commission evaluates applications based on the sincerity and consistency of the applicant's conscientious beliefs

### Key Principles

**Universality of Service:** South Korean law treats military service as a fundamental civic duty applicable to all able-bodied male citizens. The Constitutional Court has repeatedly upheld the constitutionality of mandatory service, noting the unique security situation posed by North Korea.

**K-Pop and Cultural Exemptions Debate:** The 2022 amendment to the Military Service Act (the so-called "BTS amendment") extended the enlistment deadline for recipients of the Order of Cultural Merit from age 28 to 30, allowing K-pop artists and other cultural figures to defer service. This amendment sparked intense public debate about equality of service obligations.

**Dual Citizenship Restrictions:** Under the Nationality Act, male citizens who acquire dual citizenship must complete military service before age 18 or renounce Korean nationality. Those who fail to do so are prohibited from renouncing Korean citizenship until they fulfill their military obligation or reach the age of exemption (currently 40 for most categories).

### Notable Cases

**Constitutional Court Decision 2011Hun-Ba379 (June 28, 2018):** The Constitutional Court ruled 6-3 that the absence of an alternative service option in the Military Service Act was unconstitutional, as it violated the freedom of conscience guaranteed by Article 19 of the Constitution. This reversed the court's 2004 and 2011 decisions upholding the constitutionality of the conscription system without alternative service.

**Supreme Court Decision 2018Do9781 (November 1, 2018):** Shortly after the Constitutional Court ruling, the Supreme Court held that conscientious objection constitutes a "justifiable cause" for refusing military service under Article 88 of the Military Service Act, overturning decades of precedent. This decision led to the acquittal of hundreds of conscientious objectors who had been convicted under the prior interpretation.

**Yoo Seung-jun (Steve Yoo) Case:** Korean-American entertainer Yoo Seung-jun renounced his Korean citizenship and obtained U.S. citizenship to avoid military service in 2002. He was subsequently permanently banned from entering South Korea under the Immigration Act. In 2020, a court upheld the entry ban, and the case remains a powerful symbol of the social stigma attached to draft evasion.

**Jehovah's Witnesses Cases:** Prior to the 2018 reforms, approximately 19,000 Jehovah's Witnesses were imprisoned in South Korea for refusing military service between 1953 and 2018, making South Korea one of the world's largest jailers of conscientious objectors. The UN Human Rights Committee found South Korea in violation of Article 18 of the ICCPR in multiple individual communications, including Min-Kyu Jeong et al. v. Republic of Korea (2011).

## United States

### Federal Framework

The United States transitioned from active conscription to an all-volunteer military force on January 27, 1973, following the end of the Vietnam War draft. However, the legal infrastructure for conscription remains intact:

**Military Selective Service Act (50 U.S.C. Chapter 49):**

- **Section 3802 (Registration):** Requires every male citizen and resident alien aged 18-25 to register with the Selective Service System within 30 days of their 18th birthday.
- **Section 3803 (Induction):** Authorizes the President to induct registered persons into the armed forces when authorized by Congress. This authority has not been exercised since 1973.
- **Section 3806 (Deferments and Exemptions):** Provides for deferments based on hardship, ministerial status, and other categories.
- **Section 3811 (Offenses and Penalties):** Failure to register is a felony punishable by a fine of up to $250,000 and/or imprisonment for up to 5 years.

**Selective Service Registration Requirements:**

| Category | Requirement |
|----------|-------------|
| Male U.S. citizens | Must register within 30 days of 18th birthday |
| Male immigrants (documented) | Must register within 30 days of arrival if aged 18-25 |
| Undocumented male immigrants | Required to register |
| Transgender individuals | Based on sex assigned at birth (policy under review) |
| Women | Not required to register (constitutional challenge ongoing) |

### State Variations

While conscription is a federal matter, states impose additional penalties for failure to register:

- Approximately 40 states link Selective Service registration to eligibility for state-funded financial aid, driver's licenses, or state employment
- The Workforce Innovation and Opportunity Act (WIOA) requires registration for eligibility for federal job training programs
- Federal employment with Executive Branch agencies requires registration (5 U.S.C. Section 3328)

**Gender Debate:** In *National Coalition for Men v. Selective Service System* (2019), a federal district court in Texas held that the male-only registration requirement violated the Equal Protection Clause. The Fifth Circuit reversed on standing grounds, and the Supreme Court declined certiorari in *National Coalition for Men v. Selective Service System*, 593 U.S. 2206 (2021), noting that Congress was actively considering the issue. The FY2022 and FY2025 NDAAs contained proposals to extend registration to women, but these provisions were not enacted.

### Notable Cases

**Rostker v. Goldberg, 453 U.S. 57 (1981):** The Supreme Court upheld the male-only registration requirement, reasoning that because women were excluded from combat positions, the exemption served a legitimate government purpose. This reasoning has been undermined by the Department of Defense's 2013 decision to open all combat roles to women.

**United States v. Seeger, 380 U.S. 163 (1965):** The Court broadened the definition of conscientious objection beyond traditional religious belief, holding that a sincere and meaningful belief occupying a place "parallel to that filled by the God of those admittedly qualifying for the exemption" was sufficient.

**Welsh v. United States, 398 U.S. 333 (1970):** Extended *Seeger* further, recognizing purely ethical or moral objection to war as a basis for conscientious objector status under the Universal Military Training and Service Act.

**Muhammad Ali (Clay v. United States), 403 U.S. 698 (1971):** The Supreme Court unanimously overturned the conviction of Muhammad Ali for draft evasion, finding that his claim of conscientious objection as a Muslim was improperly denied.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany has a complex constitutional and statutory framework for conscription that remains formally in place despite the suspension of compulsory service:

**Grundgesetz (Basic Law):**

- **Article 4(3):** "No person shall be compelled against his conscience to render military service involving the use of arms. Details shall be regulated by a federal law." This provision establishes an absolute constitutional right to conscientious objection -- stronger than the protections in most other constitutions.
- **Article 12a(1):** "Men who have attained the age of eighteen may be required to serve in the Armed Forces, in the Federal Border Guard, or in a civil defense organization."
- **Article 12a(2):** "Any person who, on grounds of conscience, refuses to render military service involving the use of arms may be required to render alternative service."

**Wehrpflichtgesetz (Compulsory Military Service Act):**

The Wehrpflichtgesetz provided the statutory framework for conscription. Key historical provisions:

| Provision | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Section 1** | Every German man is subject to compulsory military service from age 18 |
| **Section 5** | Duration of basic military service (reduced from 12 months to 9 months in 2002, then 6 months in 2010) |
| **Section 11** | Exemptions for clergy, physically unfit, and others |
| **Section 16** | Penalties for draft evasion: imprisonment up to 5 years |
| **Section 3 (as amended 2011)** | Suspension of compulsory service; obligation applies only in cases of tension or defense (Spannungs- oder Verteidigungsfall) |

**Suspension of Conscription (2011):**

On March 2, 2011, the Bundestag passed the Wehrrechtsanderungsgesetz 2011, suspending compulsory military service effective July 1, 2011. Minister of Defense Karl-Theodor zu Guttenberg initiated the reform, which was continued by his successor Thomas de Maiziere. The Bundeswehr transitioned to a fully professional volunteer force (Freiwilligenarmee), though the constitutional authority to reinstate conscription remains in Article 12a GG.

**Zivildienst (Civilian Service):** Germany's civilian alternative service system, established under the Zivildienstgesetz, was one of the most extensive in Europe. At its peak, approximately 90,000 men per year served in hospitals, nursing homes, and social service organizations. The suspension of conscription simultaneously ended mandatory civilian service, leading to significant staffing challenges in the healthcare and social services sectors.

### Key Principles

**Constitutional Right to Conscientious Objection:** Germany is unique in providing an absolute constitutional right to refuse armed military service (Article 4(3) GG). Unlike most systems, where conscientious objection must be individually justified and assessed, the German right requires no demonstration of religious or ethical motivation beyond the assertion of conscientious conviction. The Federal Constitutional Court confirmed in several decisions that the right is not subject to proportionality analysis and cannot be outweighed by competing state interests.

**Reinstatement Debate (2022-present):** Following Russia's invasion of Ukraine in February 2022, significant debate emerged about reinstating some form of compulsory service. Defense Minister Boris Pistorius proposed a "selective conscription" model (Auswahlwehrpflicht) in 2024, under which all young adults would complete a questionnaire, and a portion would be selected for mandatory service. As of early 2026, no legislation has been enacted.

### Notable Cases

**BVerfGE 12, 45 (1960):** The Federal Constitutional Court established that the right to conscientious objection under Article 4(3) GG is a fundamental right that may not be restricted by an assessment of the "reasonableness" of the applicant's convictions.

**BVerfGE 48, 127 (1978):** The Court upheld the constitutionality of compulsory military service, finding that Article 12a GG and the duty of national defense are not in conflict with Article 4(3) GG, as the Basic Law provides for both the general duty and the individual right of refusal.

**BVerfGE 69, 1 (1985):** The Court ruled on the scope of civilian alternative service, holding that it must be of comparable burden to military service but may not be designed punitively to discourage conscientious objection.

## ECHR / EU Framework

### Convention Articles

The European Convention on Human Rights addresses military service in several provisions:

- **Article 4(3)(b):** Exempts compulsory military service from the prohibition on forced labor, stating that "forced or compulsory labour" shall not include "any service of a military character or, in case of conscientious objectors in countries where they are recognised, service exacted instead of compulsory military service."
- **Article 9 (Freedom of thought, conscience and religion):** Protects the right to freedom of thought, conscience, and religion, including the right to manifest beliefs in practice and observance.
- **Article 14 (Prohibition of discrimination):** Prohibits discrimination in the enjoyment of Convention rights, relevant to gender-based conscription and differential treatment of conscientious objectors.

### Key Case Law

**Bayatyan v. Armenia, App. No. 23459/03 (Grand Chamber, July 7, 2011):** This landmark ruling fundamentally changed the ECHR's approach to conscientious objection. Vahan Bayatyan, a Jehovah's Witness, was imprisoned in Armenia for refusing military service. The Grand Chamber held, by a vote of 16 to 1, that the right to conscientious objection is protected under Article 9, overruling the prior Chamber decision and decades of Commission jurisprudence. The Court found that "opposition to military service, where it is motivated by a serious and insurmountable conflict between the obligation to serve in the army and a person's conscience or his deeply and genuinely held religious or other beliefs, constitutes a conviction or belief of sufficient cogency, seriousness, cohesion and importance to attract the guarantees of Article 9."

**Bukharatyan v. Armenia, App. No. 37819/03 (2012):** Following *Bayatyan*, the Court found a violation of Article 9 where Armenia had imprisoned a Jehovah's Witness for refusing military service, reaffirming that the right extends to situations where no alternative service was available.

**Savda v. Turkey, App. No. 42730/05 (2012):** The Court found Turkey in violation of Article 3 (prohibition of inhuman treatment) and Article 9 for repeatedly prosecuting and imprisoning a conscientious objector, describing the cycle of conviction and re-imprisonment as "civil death."

**Adyan and Others v. Armenia, App. No. 75604/11 (2017):** The Court held that alternative service must be genuinely civilian in nature and not punitive in duration or character. Armenia's alternative service, which was supervised by the military, was found to violate Article 9.

### Member State Obligations

Following *Bayatyan*, member states that maintain conscription must:

1. Recognize the right to conscientious objection on the basis of genuine religious or other beliefs
2. Provide a genuine alternative to military service
3. Ensure that alternative service is not punitive in nature or duration
4. Refrain from repeated prosecution of conscientious objectors

Among Council of Europe member states, conscription practices vary significantly. Israel (not a member) maintains universal conscription for both men and women. Turkey retains conscription with limited conscientious objection provisions despite ECHR rulings. Finland maintains conscription with a 347-day alternative service option. Switzerland holds regular referenda on conscription; the most recent (2013) voted to retain it. Russia reinstated elements of compulsory service in the context of the war in Ukraine. Outside Europe, countries such as Brazil maintain registration requirements similar to the U.S. Selective Service system, while Japan, India, and Australia rely on all-volunteer forces.

### Socioeconomic Impact of Conscription

The economic and social effects of mandatory military service extend far beyond national defense:

**Labor Market Effects:** Studies in South Korea estimate that the 18-month service requirement reduces lifetime earnings for male workers by approximately 3-5%, due to delayed entry into the workforce and lost educational and career development time. Germany's experience after suspending conscription showed labor market benefits but also created staffing shortages in the healthcare sector, where Zivildienst (civilian service) personnel had filled critical roles.

**Gender Equality Implications:** Male-only conscription creates structural inequalities that affect both military and civilian life. In South Korea, the mandatory service period is a major contributor to the gender wage gap and has fueled intense political debate about "military service compensation points" in civil service examinations -- a system ruled unconstitutional by the Constitutional Court in 1999 (98Hun-Ma363).

**Mental Health Considerations:** Research consistently identifies elevated rates of depression, anxiety, and suicide among conscripts. South Korea's military has faced particular scrutiny, with an average of approximately 80 military suicides per year, prompting reforms to conditions of service, hazing prevention, and mental health support.
