## Summary

Traffic violation law -- the legal framework governing the classification, prosecution, and punishment of breaches of road traffic rules, ranging from minor infractions like parking violations to serious criminal offenses like drunk driving resulting in death -- represents one of the most commonly encountered areas of law for ordinary citizens, directly affecting public safety, personal liberty, and the balance between regulatory efficiency and due process rights. South Korea regulates traffic violations through the Road Traffic Act (도로교통법), administered by the National Police Agency (경찰청) and local police departments, with a distinctive point-based license management system (벌점제도), extensive use of automated enforcement cameras, and a strong emphasis on drunk driving penalties that have been significantly strengthened through a series of amendments known collectively as the "Yoon Chang-ho Act" (윤창호법) following a tragic drunk-driving death in 2018. The United States has an entirely decentralized traffic enforcement system, with each state maintaining its own motor vehicle code, court system, and penalty structure, though federal standards apply to commercial motor vehicles through the Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration (FMCSA) and highway safety grants incentivize state compliance with national standards under the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) framework, including the national minimum drinking age of 21 established by the National Minimum Drinking Age Act of 1984. Germany applies a unified federal traffic law framework through the Straßenverkehrsgesetz (StVG, Road Traffic Act), the Straßenverkehrs-Ordnung (StVO, Road Traffic Regulation), and the Bußgeldkatalog (Fine Catalogue), with the Kraftfahrt-Bundesamt (KBA, Federal Motor Transport Authority) in Flensburg maintaining the centralized Fahreignungsregister (Fitness to Drive Register) point system, and the country's Autobahn speed limit debate representing one of the most culturally distinctive aspects of German traffic law. At the European level, the EU Cross-Border Enforcement Directive (2015/413) enables prosecution of traffic offenses committed in other member states, the Vienna Convention on Road Traffic (1968) provides international standardization of traffic rules and signs, and the ECtHR has addressed traffic enforcement issues under Articles 6 (fair trial) and Article 1 Protocol 1 (property) of the Convention.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's traffic violation law is governed primarily by the Road Traffic Act (도로교통법), which establishes rules of the road, driver licensing requirements, vehicle registration, and enforcement mechanisms. The Act is administered by the National Police Agency (경찰청) and supplemented by the Motor Vehicle Management Act (자동차관리법) and the Traffic Accident Processing Act (교통사고처리특례법).

### Point System (벌점제도)

The Korean demerit point system assigns point values to traffic violations, with accumulation leading to progressive license sanctions: 40 points within one year results in a one-year license suspension; accumulation of 121 points over three consecutive years results in license revocation. Points range from 10 (minor violations) to 100 (serious offenses like DUI causing injury). The system is managed by the police traffic division and tracked through the National Police Information System.

### DUI (음주운전) Penalties -- Yoon Chang-ho Act

The death of university student Yoon Chang-ho (윤창호), killed by a drunk driver in Busan on September 25, 2018, catalyzed a major tightening of drunk driving penalties. The subsequent amendments, collectively known as the Yoon Chang-ho Act, implemented in two phases (December 2018 and June 2019):

**Lowered BAC Thresholds:** The criminal DUI threshold was lowered from 0.05% to 0.03% BAC. The aggravated DUI threshold was lowered from 0.10% to 0.08%.

**Increased Penalties:**

| Offense | Pre-Reform Penalty | Post-Reform Penalty |
|---------|-------------------|---------------------|
| DUI (0.03-0.08% BAC) | Up to 6 months or 3 million KRW | Up to 1 year or 5 million KRW |
| Aggravated DUI (0.08%+ BAC) | Up to 1 year or 5 million KRW | Up to 2 years or 10 million KRW |
| Refusal of breathalyzer | Up to 3 years or 10 million KRW | Up to 5 years or 20 million KRW |
| DUI causing death | Up to 10 years | 3 years to life imprisonment |
| DUI causing injury | Up to 10 years | 1-15 years imprisonment |

### Automated Enforcement

South Korea operates one of the world's most extensive automated traffic enforcement networks. As of 2024, approximately 17,000 automated speed enforcement cameras, red-light cameras, and bus lane enforcement cameras operate nationwide. The system generates approximately 10 million violation notices annually. Fines from automated enforcement are processed administratively and may be challenged through the Administrative Appeals Commission (행정심판위원회).

## United States

### Legal Framework

Traffic violation law in the United States is almost entirely a matter of state law, with each of the 50 states, the District of Columbia, and US territories maintaining their own motor vehicle codes. Federal authority is limited to commercial motor vehicles (through the FMCSA and 49 C.F.R. Parts 383-399), highway safety grants (through NHTSA under 23 U.S.C. Chapter 4), and the establishment of minimum standards that states must meet to receive federal highway funding.

**National Minimum Drinking Age Act (23 U.S.C. Section 158):** This 1984 statute conditions 10% of federal highway funding on states maintaining a minimum legal drinking age of 21. Its constitutionality was upheld in South Dakota v. Dole, 483 U.S. 203 (1987).

**DUI Standards:** All states set the per se DUI limit at 0.08% BAC for adult drivers, following the 2000 federal highway funding incentive under 23 U.S.C. Section 163. Utah lowered its limit to 0.05% BAC in 2018, the first state to do so. Commercial motor vehicle drivers are subject to a 0.04% BAC limit. Zero tolerance laws apply to drivers under 21.

### State Variation

Traffic violation penalties vary enormously across states. DUI first-offense penalties range from no mandatory jail time (in some states) to mandatory minimum sentences (e.g., Arizona: 10 days for first offense). Speed limit enforcement approaches range from states that prohibit all automated enforcement (e.g., Mississippi, West Virginia) to states with extensive camera networks (e.g., Maryland, Virginia). License suspension and reinstatement procedures differ substantially.

### Implied Consent

All states have adopted implied consent laws, which provide that by operating a vehicle on public roads, a driver consents to chemical testing (breath, blood, or urine) for alcohol or drugs if lawfully arrested for DUI. However, the Supreme Court's decision in Birchfield v. North Dakota, 579 U.S. 438 (2016), held that while states may make it a crime to refuse a breath test incident to a lawful DUI arrest, they may not criminalize refusal of a blood test without a warrant, as blood draws constitute searches under the Fourth Amendment.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany applies a comprehensive federal traffic law framework through the Straßenverkehrsgesetz (StVG, Road Traffic Act), the Straßenverkehrs-Ordnung (StVO, Road Traffic Regulation), and the Bußgeldkatalog (Fine Catalogue). Criminal traffic offenses are prosecuted under the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), particularly Sections 315b-315d (dangerous interference with road traffic), Section 316 (drunken driving), and Section 222 (negligent homicide in traffic accidents).

### Fahreignungsregister (Fitness to Drive Register)

The Kraftfahrt-Bundesamt (KBA, Federal Motor Transport Authority) in Flensburg maintains the centralized Fahreignungsregister (formerly Verkehrszentralregister), commonly known as the "Flensburg Point System." The system assigns 1 to 3 points for traffic violations: 1 point for administrative offenses (Ordnungswidrigkeiten), 2 points for administrative offenses with driving ban or criminal offenses, and 3 points for criminal offenses with license revocation. At 4-5 points, a written warning is issued; at 6-7 points, mandatory participation in a driving aptitude seminar; at 8 or more points, license revocation (Entziehung der Fahrerlaubnis).

### DUI (Trunkenheit im Verkehr)

German DUI law operates on a tiered system:

| BAC Level | Classification | Consequence |
|-----------|---------------|-------------|
| 0.05-0.109% (first offense) | Administrative offense (Ordnungswidrigkeit) | Fine up to 3,000 EUR; 1-3 month driving ban; 2 points |
| 0.03%+ with driving impairment | Criminal offense (§ 316 StGB) | Fine or up to 1 year imprisonment; license revocation |
| 0.11%+ | Absolute unfitness (absolute Fahruntauglichkeit) | Criminal offense per se; fine or up to 1 year imprisonment; license revocation |
| DUI causing serious injury (§ 315c StGB) | Felony (Verbrechen equivalent) | Up to 5 years imprisonment |

### Autobahn Speed Limit Debate

Germany is unique among major nations in not having a general speed limit on the Autobahn highway system. The recommended advisory speed (Richtgeschwindigkeit) of 130 km/h is not legally binding. Approximately 30% of the Autobahn has permanent speed limits, and temporary limits apply in construction zones and during adverse weather. The Autobahn speed debate is culturally significant and politically charged, with environmental groups and the Green Party advocating for a general limit and the automotive industry and conservative parties opposing it. The environmental argument is supported by the Umweltbundesamt (Federal Environment Agency), which estimates that a 120 km/h limit would reduce CO2 emissions by approximately 6.7 million tons annually.

### Bußgeldkatalog (Fine Catalogue)

The Bußgeldkatalog provides standardized fines for traffic violations, ensuring consistent enforcement across the 16 Bundesländer. Notable fines include: speeding 21-25 km/h over limit within town: 115 EUR; speeding 41-50 km/h over limit outside town: 320 EUR + 1 month driving ban; mobile phone use while driving: 100 EUR + 1 point; running a red light: 200-360 EUR + 1-2 points + 1 month driving ban.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Cross-Border Enforcement Directive (2015/413)

Directive (EU) 2015/413 of the European Parliament and of the Council of March 11, 2015, facilitating cross-border exchange of information on road-safety-related traffic offenses, enables member states to identify owners of vehicles that commit specified traffic offenses in other member states. The directive covers eight offense categories: speeding; failure to use a seatbelt; failure to stop at a red traffic light; drink driving; driving under the influence of drugs; failure to wear a safety helmet; use of a forbidden lane; and illegal use of a mobile phone while driving.

The directive operates through the European Car and Driving Licence Information System (EUCARIS), which facilitates automated exchange of vehicle registration data between member states.

### Vienna Convention on Road Traffic (1968)

The Convention on Road Traffic, concluded in Vienna on November 8, 1968, provides international standardization of traffic rules and signs. The Convention has been ratified by 82 states, including South Korea and Germany. The United States ratified the earlier 1949 Geneva Convention on Road Traffic but not the 1968 Vienna Convention. The 1968 Convention establishes standards for vehicle requirements, driver licensing, and rules of the road that serve as the international baseline.

### ECtHR Jurisprudence

**Öztürk v. Germany (Application No. 8544/79, Judgment of February 21, 1984):** The Court held that a minor traffic offense (Ordnungswidrigkeit) reclassified from criminal to administrative fell within the "criminal" head of Article 6 ECHR for purposes of fair trial protections. This landmark decision established that administrative traffic penalties are subject to Article 6 safeguards regardless of their domestic classification.

**Lutz v. Germany (Application No. 9912/82, Judgment of August 25, 1987):** Confirmed the Öztürk approach for traffic regulatory offenses.

**O'Halloran and Francis v. United Kingdom (Applications Nos. 15809/02 and 25624/02, Grand Chamber Judgment of June 29, 2007):** The Grand Chamber held that the requirement for vehicle owners to disclose the identity of the driver in automated speed enforcement proceedings did not violate the privilege against self-incrimination under Article 6(1), as the regulatory regime was analogous to other compulsory disclosure obligations.

### EU Road Safety Policy

The EU Road Safety Policy Framework 2021-2030, building on the 2011-2020 framework, sets the target of reducing road deaths and serious injuries by 50% by 2030 (compared to 2019) and moving toward zero road fatalities by 2050 (Vision Zero). The framework encourages harmonization of enforcement standards, adoption of intelligent speed assistance (ISA) technology, and cross-border enforcement cooperation.

## Comparative Analysis

### DUI Enforcement

South Korea has the strictest DUI approach among the jurisdictions, with a 0.03% BAC criminal threshold and penalties up to life imprisonment for DUI causing death. Germany applies a tiered system distinguishing administrative offenses (0.05%+) from criminal offenses (0.03% with impairment or 0.11% per se). The US standard of 0.08% is higher than most developed nations, though Utah's 0.05% represents a trend toward lower thresholds.

### Speed Enforcement

Automated speed enforcement is ubiquitous in South Korea and Germany but controversial in the United States, where some states have banned it entirely. Germany's lack of a general Autobahn speed limit is globally unique and culturally significant.

### Point Systems

All jurisdictions use point-based license management systems, though the specifics differ. South Korea's 40-point annual threshold, Germany's 8-point cumulative system, and the various US state systems reflect different approaches to graduated sanctions.

### Cross-Border Enforcement

The EU's Cross-Border Enforcement Directive provides a mechanism for pursuing traffic offenses across borders that has no equivalent in East Asia or North America. This capability is particularly important for automated enforcement of speed and red-light violations by foreign-registered vehicles.
