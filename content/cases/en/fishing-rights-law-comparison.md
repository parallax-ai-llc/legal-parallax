## Summary

Fishing rights -- the legal entitlements governing who may harvest marine and freshwater resources, where, when, and in what quantities -- sit at the intersection of food security, environmental sustainability, indigenous rights, and international maritime law. South Korea regulates its fishing industry through the Fisheries Act (수산업법) and the Distant Water Fisheries Development Act (원양산업발전법), managing a fleet of approximately 66,000 registered vessels and a distant-water fishing fleet that operates across the Pacific, Atlantic, and Indian Oceans; Korea's illegal, unreported, and unregulated (IUU) fishing record has drawn international scrutiny, including a 2013 EU yellow card warning (lifted in 2015 after legislative reforms), and the country maintains complex bilateral fishing agreements with Japan under the 1998 Korea-Japan Fisheries Agreement (한일어업협정) governing shared stocks in overlapping exclusive economic zones. The United States manages fisheries primarily under the Magnuson-Stevens Fishery Conservation and Management Act (16 U.S.C. Sections 1801-1891d), which established eight regional fishery management councils, mandated science-based annual catch limits, and implemented a system of individual fishing quotas (catch shares) in several major fisheries; the Act was substantially strengthened by the 2006 reauthorization requiring an end to overfishing, and the 2022 America's Conservation Enhancement (ACE) Act provided additional tools for stock rebuilding; tribal and indigenous fishing rights, guaranteed by treaties and affirmed in United States v. Washington (the "Boldt Decision," 384 F. Supp. 312, 1974), remain a defining feature of U.S. fisheries law. Germany's commercial fishing industry is modest in scale but operates within the EU's Common Fisheries Policy (CFP, Regulation (EU) No 1380/2013), which establishes total allowable catches (TACs), fleet capacity limits, and the landing obligation (discard ban); German fisheries are managed under the Sea Fisheries Act (Seefischereigesetz) and regulated by the Federal Office for Agriculture and Food (Bundesanstalt für Landwirtschaft und Ernährung, BLE). At the international level, the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS, 1982) establishes the 200-nautical-mile exclusive economic zone framework and the obligation to conserve marine living resources; the UN Fish Stocks Agreement (1995), regional fisheries management organizations (RFMOs), and the 2009 FAO Agreement on Port State Measures to Prevent, Deter and Eliminate IUU Fishing provide the governance architecture for high seas and straddling stock management; the European Court of Human Rights has addressed fishing rights primarily through Protocol 1, Article 1 (protection of property), recognizing fishing licenses and quota entitlements as possessions protected against arbitrary deprivation.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's fishing industry is the sixth largest in Asia by volume, with annual marine capture production of approximately 1.3 million tonnes.

**Fisheries Act (수산업법):** This foundational statute, first enacted in 1953, governs the licensing of fishing operations, the designation of fishing grounds, the conservation and management of fishery resources, and the rights of fishing communities. The Act establishes several categories of fishing rights:

| Category | Description | Governance |
|----------|-------------|-----------|
| Fishing rights (어업권) | Exclusive rights to fish in designated coastal areas, typically held by fishing cooperatives | Provincial/municipal government |
| Licensed fishing (허가어업) | Government-issued licenses for specific fishing methods and areas | Ministry of Oceans and Fisheries |
| Reported fishing (신고어업) | Small-scale fishing requiring only notification | Local government |
| Distant-water fishing | Operations beyond Korean EEZ | Ministry of Oceans and Fisheries; RFMO regulations |

**Distant Water Fisheries Development Act (원양산업발전법):** Comprehensively reformed in 2015 following the EU IUU yellow card, this statute governs Korea's distant-water fishing fleet of approximately 200 vessels operating globally. The 2015 reform strengthened monitoring, control, and surveillance (MCS) requirements including mandatory vessel monitoring systems (VMS), electronic logbooks, and observer coverage.

### Korea-Japan Fisheries Relations

The 1998 Korea-Japan Fisheries Agreement (한일어업협정) replaced the 1965 agreement and established the framework for managing shared fish stocks in overlapping EEZ areas. The agreement designates provisional zones in the East Sea/Sea of Japan and the East China Sea where both countries' vessels may fish subject to bilateral management measures. The agreement has been a persistent source of diplomatic friction, particularly regarding Japanese restrictions on Korean fishing vessels and the unresolved Dokdo/Takeshima territorial dispute.

### Korea-China Fisheries Relations

The 2001 Korea-China Fisheries Agreement governs fishing in overlapping EEZ areas in the Yellow Sea. Chinese fishing vessels operating in Korean waters -- both legally under the agreement and illegally -- have been a major enforcement challenge. The Korean Coast Guard conducts regular patrols and has engaged in confrontations with Chinese vessels engaged in IUU fishing, including several incidents resulting in injuries and one Korean Coast Guard officer death in 2011.

### IUU Fishing Reform

The European Commission issued a yellow card to South Korea in November 2013 under the EU IUU Regulation (Regulation (EC) No 1005/2008), citing inadequate control over Korean distant-water fishing vessels. Korea responded with comprehensive legislative and institutional reforms:
- Comprehensive amendment of the Distant Water Fisheries Development Act (2015)
- Establishment of the Distant Water Fisheries Monitoring Center
- Mandatory VMS with real-time position reporting
- Increased observer coverage to 100% for large-scale tuna longliners
- Enhanced criminal penalties for IUU violations (up to 3 years imprisonment)

The EU lifted the yellow card in April 2015, recognizing Korea's reforms.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States possesses the world's largest exclusive economic zone (approximately 11.35 million km²) and manages one of the world's most complex fisheries governance systems.

**Magnuson-Stevens Fishery Conservation and Management Act (MSA, 16 U.S.C. Sections 1801-1891d):** Originally enacted in 1976 as the Fishery Conservation and Management Act, the MSA established federal jurisdiction over fisheries in the 200-mile EEZ (beyond the 3-mile state waters boundary) and created eight regional fishery management councils composed of federal and state officials, industry representatives, and scientists.

**2006 Reauthorization (Magnuson-Stevens Reauthorization Act):** The most significant amendment required all fishery management plans to establish annual catch limits (ACLs) and accountability measures to prevent overfishing, set science-based catch limits, and mandated the rebuilding of overfished stocks within specified timeframes (generally 10 years or less). The reauthorization is credited with dramatically improving U.S. fishery management: the number of stocks subject to overfishing declined from 72 in 2000 to 25 in 2024.

**Catch Share Programs:** Several U.S. fisheries operate under individual fishing quota (IFQ) or catch share systems, including:

| Fishery | Type | Year Implemented |
|---------|------|-----------------|
| Alaska halibut and sablefish | IFQ | 1995 |
| Pacific coast groundfish | Individual transferable quotas | 2011 |
| Gulf of Mexico red snapper | IFQ | 2007 |
| New England scallop | Days-at-sea and limited access | 1994/2010 |
| Northeast multispecies (groundfish) | Sectors (catch shares) | 2010 |

### Indigenous and Treaty Fishing Rights

**United States v. Washington ("Boldt Decision," 384 F. Supp. 312, W.D. Wash. 1974):** Judge George Boldt held that treaties between the United States and Pacific Northwest tribes guaranteed the tribes the right to take up to 50% of the harvestable salmon and steelhead passing through their usual and accustomed fishing grounds. The Ninth Circuit affirmed (520 F.2d 676, 1975), and the Supreme Court upheld the decision in Washington v. Washington State Commercial Passenger Fishing Vessel Association (443 U.S. 658, 1979).

**Culvert Case (United States v. Washington, 853 F.3d 946, 9th Cir. 2017):** The Ninth Circuit held that the treaty right to take fish implied a right to have fish available to take, requiring Washington State to replace road culverts that blocked salmon passage. The Supreme Court affirmed by an equally divided court (4-4, 584 U.S. ___, 2018).

### IUU Enforcement

The Lacey Act (16 U.S.C. Sections 3371-3378) prohibits trade in fish, wildlife, and plants taken in violation of any law, treaty, or regulation, including foreign law. The Seafood Import Monitoring Program (SIMP), established in 2018, requires importers to report catch data for thirteen species groups identified as particularly vulnerable to IUU fishing or seafood fraud.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's commercial fishing industry is relatively small, with approximately 1,400 fishing vessels and annual catches of approximately 200,000 tonnes. German fisheries operate almost entirely within the framework of the EU Common Fisheries Policy.

**Sea Fisheries Act (Seefischereigesetz):** This federal statute implements EU fisheries regulations at the national level, governs fishing licensing, and establishes enforcement mechanisms. The Federal Office for Agriculture and Food (BLE) administers fishing licenses, monitors compliance, and manages Germany's fish quota allocations under the CFP.

**EU Common Fisheries Policy (Regulation (EU) No 1380/2013):** The CFP governs all aspects of EU fisheries management:

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Total Allowable Catches (TACs)** | Annual catch limits set by the Council of Ministers based on scientific advice from ICES |
| **Relative Stability** | Historic allocation of national quota shares, fixed since 1983 |
| **Landing Obligation (Discard Ban)** | Progressive prohibition on discarding regulated species (fully implemented 2019) |
| **Fleet Capacity** | Limits on total fleet capacity (gross tonnage and engine power) per member state |
| **External Dimension** | EU negotiates fishing access in third-country waters and participates in RFMOs |

### Baltic and North Sea Fisheries

Germany's primary fishing grounds are the Baltic Sea and the North Sea. Key managed stocks include:
- **Baltic cod:** Subject to severe catch restrictions and even fishing moratoriums in recent years due to stock collapse; the Western Baltic cod TAC was reduced to near zero in 2022
- **North Sea herring and sprat:** Managed through ICES-advised TACs
- **North Sea plaice and sole:** Managed through the EU-Norway bilateral agreement

### Post-Brexit Fisheries

The EU-UK Trade and Cooperation Agreement (2020) fundamentally altered the fisheries landscape for German (and other EU) fleets. The agreement provided for a transition period (2021-2026) during which EU access to UK waters was gradually reduced, with a 25% reduction in EU fishing rights in UK waters by June 2026. The adjustment period has been particularly challenging for German vessels that historically fished in UK waters for species including mackerel, herring, and cod.

### Aquaculture

The German Aquaculture Act and EU aquaculture regulations govern freshwater and marine aquaculture production, which amounts to approximately 40,000 tonnes annually (primarily trout and carp). The Multiannual National Strategic Plan for Aquaculture sets development targets in alignment with the EU's Strategic Guidelines for the Sustainable Development of EU Aquaculture.

## ECHR / EU

### UNCLOS Framework

The United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (1982) establishes the foundational international framework for fishing rights:

**Exclusive Economic Zone (Part V):** Coastal states have sovereign rights over natural resources, including fisheries, within 200 nautical miles of their coastlines. States must promote the objective of optimum utilization of living resources (Article 62) and are obligated to conserve resources to prevent overexploitation (Article 61).

**High Seas Fisheries (Part VII):** All states have the right to fish on the high seas subject to their treaty obligations and the duty to cooperate in conservation and management through regional organizations.

**Straddling Stocks and Highly Migratory Species:** Articles 63 and 64 require cooperation for the management of stocks occurring both within and beyond EEZs.

### UN Fish Stocks Agreement (1995)

The Agreement for the Implementation of the Provisions of UNCLOS Relating to the Conservation and Management of Straddling Fish Stocks and Highly Migratory Fish Stocks strengthens UNCLOS by:
- Applying the precautionary approach to fisheries management
- Establishing enforcement mechanisms including the right to board and inspect fishing vessels on the high seas
- Requiring states to participate in RFMOs or abide by their conservation measures

### FAO Port State Measures Agreement (2009/2016)

The Agreement on Port State Measures to Prevent, Deter and Eliminate IUU Fishing (PSMA), which entered into force in 2016, requires port states to inspect foreign fishing vessels and deny port access or services to vessels engaged in IUU fishing. As of 2026, over 75 parties have ratified the PSMA.

### EU IUU Regulation (Regulation (EC) No 1005/2008)

The EU's IUU Regulation establishes a three-tier system for addressing non-EU countries' IUU fishing:
- **Yellow card:** Warning notification requiring the country to take corrective action
- **Red card:** Identification as a non-cooperating country, triggering import prohibitions
- **Green card:** Removal from the warning list following satisfactory reforms

Countries that have received yellow cards include South Korea (2013, lifted 2015), Thailand (2015, lifted 2019), Vietnam (2017, still yellow as of 2026), and Cameroon (2021).

### ECHR Property Rights and Fishing

**Posti and Rahko v. Finland (Application No. 27824/95, 2002):** The Court examined whether restrictions on fishing rights in Finland violated Protocol 1, Article 1 (protection of property). The Court recognized that long-standing fishing rights constitute "possessions" within the meaning of the Convention and that restrictions must be proportionate and in the public interest.

**Alatulkkila v. Finland (Application No. 33538/96, 2005):** The Court found that fishing rights established by historical privilege or long use are protected as possessions, and that the state's interference with these rights must satisfy the proportionality test.

**Brosset-Triboulet v. France (Grand Chamber, 2010):** While primarily a property case concerning structures in the maritime public domain, the judgment addressed the scope of property rights in coastal and maritime areas, with implications for fishing communities' customary rights.

## Comparative Analysis

### Management Paradigms

The jurisdictions represent different fisheries management paradigms. The United States has progressively moved toward rights-based management (catch shares), which creates quasi-property rights in fish stocks and has been associated with reduced overcapacity and improved stock health. South Korea relies primarily on input controls (vessel licensing, gear restrictions), supplemented by total catch limits. The EU's CFP combines TACs with relative stability (fixed national allocation shares), creating a hybrid system that balances conservation with political allocation.

### Indigenous and Traditional Rights

Indigenous fishing rights are most developed in the United States, where treaty rights guaranteed to Native American tribes represent judicially enforceable entitlements that preempt state regulation and command up to 50% of harvestable stocks. South Korea provides priority access to coastal fishing communities but not in the form of constitutionally protected rights. The EU's CFP does not specifically accommodate indigenous rights, though member states may do so domestically. The ECHR recognizes long-standing fishing rights as property under Protocol 1, Article 1.

### IUU Enforcement

The EU's yellow/red card system has proven remarkably effective as a trade-based enforcement mechanism, leveraging the EU's market power as the world's largest seafood importer. South Korea's successful response to its 2013 yellow card demonstrates the system's capacity to drive reform. The United States relies more on import monitoring (SIMP) and domestic law enforcement (Lacey Act). At the international level, the PSMA adds a port-state enforcement layer.

### Climate Change Adaptation

Climate change is shifting fish stock distributions, creating new management challenges. Warming ocean temperatures are pushing temperate species northward, creating winners and losers among fishing nations and communities. None of the examined jurisdictions has yet comprehensively adapted its fisheries management framework to climate-driven stock shifts, though the EU's updated CFP multiannual plans increasingly incorporate climate projections.
