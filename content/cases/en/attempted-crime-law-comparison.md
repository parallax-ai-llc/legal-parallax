## Summary

Attempted crime -- the act of taking substantial steps toward the commission of a criminal offense without completing it -- constitutes one of the most fundamental doctrines in criminal law and is recognized across virtually every legal system worldwide. The concept addresses the tension between punishing harmful intent and reserving criminal sanctions for completed harmful acts, and jurisdictions differ significantly in where they draw the line between mere preparation and punishable attempt. South Korea's Criminal Act (형법) addresses attempt under Articles 25 through 29, adopting a formal-objective approach that generally requires the commencement of the "execution of a crime" (범죄의 실행에 착수) and providing for mandatory or discretionary sentence reduction relative to the completed offense, while also recognizing the defense of voluntary abandonment (중지범) under Article 26. The United States employs multiple doctrinal tests across federal and state jurisdictions -- including the "substantial step" test adopted by the Model Penal Code Section 5.01, the "dangerous proximity" test derived from Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes's opinion in Commonwealth v. Peaslee (1901), and the "last act" test -- with federal attempt liability under 18 U.S.C. Section 1349 and various specific attempt provisions generally carrying penalties equal to or slightly less than the completed offense. Germany treats attempt (Versuch) under Sections 22 through 24 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), defining it as the point at which the offender "immediately commences the realization of the offense" (unmittelbares Ansetzen zur Tatbestandsverwirklichung), with attempt punishable for all felonies (Verbrechen) and only for misdemeanors (Vergehen) where expressly provided by statute. At the European level, the European Court of Human Rights has addressed attempt primarily through the lens of Article 7 (no punishment without law) and the principle of legality, while EU Framework Decision 2002/475/JHA on combating terrorism and subsequent Directive (EU) 2017/541 require member states to criminalize attempted terrorist offenses with effective, proportionate, and dissuasive penalties.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's Criminal Act (형법, Act No. 293 of 1953, as amended) addresses attempted crime in Chapter 4, Articles 25 through 29. The Korean approach reflects a continental European tradition that carefully distinguishes between preparation (예비), attempt (미수), and completion (기수) of criminal offenses.

**Article 25 (미수범 -- Attempted Crime):** Paragraph 1 provides that "when the execution of a crime has been commenced but not completed, or the intended result has not occurred, the offender shall be punished for attempted crime." Paragraph 2 states that "the punishment for an attempted crime may be mitigated compared to that for the completed crime." This establishes a discretionary -- not mandatory -- reduction in sentencing for attempt.

**Article 26 (중지범 -- Voluntary Abandonment):** "When an offender voluntarily abandons or prevents the result of the crime after commencing its execution, the punishment shall be reduced or exempted." This provision treats voluntary abandonment as a mandatory ground for either reduction or complete exemption from punishment, reflecting the policy of encouraging offenders to desist from criminal conduct.

**Article 27 (불능범 -- Impossible Attempt):** "Even though the means or object of a crime makes it impossible to produce a result, the punishment may be reduced or exempted if there was a danger." This provision addresses situations where the attempt could not possibly succeed -- such as attempting to poison someone with a harmless substance -- and permits courts to exercise discretion in sentencing.

**Article 28 (음모/예비 -- Conspiracy and Preparation):** Preparation and conspiracy to commit a crime are punishable only where expressly provided by specific provisions of the Criminal Act. This establishes a clear threshold: mere preparation is generally not punishable, while attempt -- defined by the commencement of execution -- triggers criminal liability.

### Determination of "Commencement of Execution"

Korean courts and scholars have developed several theories for determining when preparation crosses into attempt:

**Formal-Objective Theory (형식적 객관설):** The dominant approach requires that the offender has begun performing an act that constitutes part of the statutory elements (구성요건) of the offense. Under this view, an attempted murder requires at least the commencement of a killing act.

**Material-Objective Theory (실질적 객관설):** This approach, increasingly influential in Korean jurisprudence, holds that attempt begins when the offender's conduct creates a direct and immediate danger to the protected legal interest, even if the statutory elements have not yet been directly engaged.

The Supreme Court of Korea (대법원) has adopted a mixed approach, as illustrated in Decision 2008도3929 (2008), holding that commencement of execution is established when the offender performs an act that is closely connected to the constituent elements of the crime and poses an immediate danger to the legal interest protected by the criminal provision.

### Penalties for Specific Attempted Offenses

| Offense | Completed Penalty | Attempt Penalty |
|---------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Murder (Art. 250) | Death, life imprisonment, or 5+ years | Punishable (Art. 254); may be mitigated |
| Robbery (Art. 333) | 3+ years imprisonment | Punishable (Art. 342); may be mitigated |
| Rape (Art. 297) | 3+ years imprisonment | Punishable (Art. 300); may be mitigated |
| Arson (Art. 164) | Death, life imprisonment, or 3+ years | Punishable (Art. 174); may be mitigated |
| Theft (Art. 329) | 6 years or less, or fine up to 10 million KRW | Punishable (Art. 342); may be mitigated |

### Landmark Cases

**Supreme Court Decision 2003도3768 (2004):** The Court clarified the boundary between preparation and attempt in fraud cases, holding that the mere creation of false documents constitutes preparation, while their submission to the victim with intent to deceive constitutes commencement of execution.

**Supreme Court Decision 2019도16263 (2020):** In a case involving attempted murder, the Court applied the material-objective theory to find that the defendant's act of entering the victim's residence with a weapon constituted commencement of execution, even though no physical attack had yet been initiated.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States applies multiple doctrinal frameworks for attempt liability, varying significantly across federal and state jurisdictions. The evolution from common law tests to the Model Penal Code's "substantial step" approach represents one of the most significant developments in American criminal law theory.

**Model Penal Code Section 5.01 (1962):** The American Law Institute's Model Penal Code, drafted principally by Professor Herbert Wechsler of Columbia Law School, introduced the "substantial step" test, which has been adopted by a majority of federal circuits and approximately two-thirds of state jurisdictions. Under Section 5.01(1)(c), a person is guilty of attempt if they purposely engage in conduct that constitutes a "substantial step in a course of conduct planned to culminate in commission of the crime." Section 5.01(2) provides a non-exhaustive list of acts that may constitute a substantial step, including lying in wait, enticing the victim, reconnoitering the contemplated scene, unlawful entry, and possession of materials to be employed in the crime.

**Federal Attempt Statutes:** Unlike many countries that have a single general attempt provision, federal law criminalizes attempt through offense-specific statutes. For example, 18 U.S.C. Section 1349 criminalizes attempt to commit fraud offenses; 21 U.S.C. Section 846 criminalizes attempt to manufacture, distribute, or possess controlled substances; and 18 U.S.C. Section 2332a criminalizes attempted use of weapons of mass destruction.

### Common Law Tests

| Test | Origin | Standard | Status |
|------|--------|----------|--------|
| **Last Act Test** | Rex v. Eagleton (1855) | Attempt requires completion of all acts necessary; only the final result is lacking | Largely abandoned; too restrictive |
| **Dangerous Proximity Test** | Commonwealth v. Peaslee (1901) (Holmes, C.J.) | Whether the defendant was dangerously close to completing the crime | Used in some state jurisdictions |
| **Equivocality Test** | King v. Barker (1924, New Zealand) | Conduct must unambiguously manifest criminal intent | Minority approach |
| **Substantial Step Test** | Model Penal Code Section 5.01 (1962) | Substantial step strongly corroborative of criminal purpose | Majority approach in federal and state courts |

### Penalties

Federal and state law generally impose the same maximum penalty for attempt as for the completed offense, reflecting the view that the attempter's culpability is equivalent to that of the successful offender:

| Jurisdiction | Penalty Approach |
|-------------|-----------------|
| Federal (general) | Same penalty as completed offense (e.g., 18 U.S.C. Section 1349: same penalties as substantive fraud) |
| Federal (drug offenses) | Same penalties as completed offense under 21 U.S.C. Section 846 |
| New York | One grade lower than completed offense (N.Y. Penal Law Section 110.05) |
| California | Generally half the term of the completed offense (Cal. Penal Code Section 664) |
| Texas | One category lower than completed offense (Tex. Penal Code Section 15.01) |

### Key Federal Cases

**United States v. Mandujano, 499 F.2d 370 (5th Cir. 1974):** The Fifth Circuit adopted the MPC substantial step test, holding that the defendant's act of going to a location to sell heroin to an undercover agent, even though he failed to procure the drugs, constituted a substantial step toward distribution.

**United States v. Jackson, 560 F.2d 112 (2d Cir. 1977):** The Second Circuit held that entering a bank with a note, a gun, and a disguise constituted a substantial step toward bank robbery, even though the defendant fled before approaching a teller.

**United States v. Resendiz-Ponce, 549 U.S. 102 (2007):** The Supreme Court held that a federal indictment for attempted illegal reentry need not specifically allege a particular overt act, as the elements of attempt -- intent and a substantial step -- are sufficient.

### Impossibility Doctrine

American law distinguishes between "legal impossibility" (a valid defense) and "factual impossibility" (not a defense). Under the MPC Section 5.01(1)(a), factual impossibility is eliminated as a defense -- a person who engages in conduct that would constitute a crime if the attendant circumstances were as they believed them to be is guilty of attempt. Most modern jurisdictions have followed this approach, rejecting the common law distinction as "logically incoherent" (People v. Dlugash, 363 N.E.2d 1155 (N.Y. 1977)).

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German criminal law treats attempt (Versuch) under Sections 22 through 24 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB). The German approach combines subjective and objective elements in a nuanced framework that has influenced criminal law across continental Europe.

**Section 22 StGB -- Definition of Attempt (Begriffsbestimmung):** "An attempt to commit a criminal offense is made by a person who, according to his conception of the act, immediately commences the realization of the statutory definition of the offense" (Eine Straftat versucht, wer nach seiner Vorstellung von der Tat zur Verwirklichung des Tatbestandes unmittelbar ansetzt). This definition incorporates both a subjective element (the offender's conception of the act -- Vorstellung von der Tat) and an objective element (immediate commencement -- unmittelbares Ansetzen).

**Section 23 StGB -- Punishability of Attempt (Strafbarkeit des Versuchs):**
- Paragraph 1: Attempt to commit a felony (Verbrechen, offenses carrying a minimum of one year imprisonment) is always punishable; attempt to commit a misdemeanor (Vergehen) is punishable only where expressly provided by law.
- Paragraph 2: Attempt may be punished less severely than the completed offense (the court has discretion to mitigate under Section 49(1)).
- Paragraph 3: Where the offender, out of gross lack of understanding (grober Unverstand), fails to recognize that the attempt could not possibly lead to completion due to the nature of the object or means, the court may exempt from punishment or mitigate at its discretion.

**Section 24 StGB -- Voluntary Abandonment (Rücktritt):**
- Paragraph 1: A person who voluntarily abandons the further execution of the offense or voluntarily prevents its completion shall not be punished for attempt. If the offense would not have been completed regardless of the person's efforts, non-punishment applies if the person voluntarily and seriously endeavors to prevent completion.
- Paragraph 2: Where multiple persons participate, any person who voluntarily prevents the completion of the offense shall not be punished. A serious endeavor to prevent completion suffices if the offense is not completed regardless or is completed independently of the person's prior contribution.

### Determination of "Immediate Commencement"

The German Federal Court of Justice (Bundesgerichtshof -- BGH) has developed a comprehensive body of case law defining when preparation transitions to attempt:

**BGH, Judgment of September 16, 1975 (1 StR 264/75):** The Court established that "immediate commencement" requires that the offender's conduct, according to their plan, directly leads into the realization of the statutory elements without essential intermediate steps. The determination must consider both the offender's subjective plan and an objective assessment of temporal and spatial proximity to the completed offense.

**BGH, Judgment of January 26, 1982 (4 StR 631/81):** In a burglary case, the Court held that attempt begins when the offender begins to work on the obstacle that protects the targeted property -- for example, beginning to force open a locked door -- rather than merely approaching the premises.

### Penalties and Sentencing

| Offense Category | Attempt Punishable? | Sentencing Framework |
|-----------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| All felonies (Verbrechen, minimum 1 year) | Always (Section 23(1)) | Discretionary mitigation under Section 49(1) |
| Misdemeanors (Vergehen) | Only if expressly stated | Same discretionary mitigation |
| Impossible attempt (Section 23(3)) | Yes, but exemption possible | Court may exempt or mitigate (grober Unverstand) |
| Voluntary abandonment (Section 24) | Exemption from punishment | Mandatory non-punishment if requirements met |

### The "Rücktritt" Doctrine

Germany's voluntary abandonment provision (Section 24 StGB) is one of the most generous in comparative law, providing complete exemption from punishment rather than mere mitigation. The doctrine is grounded in the "golden bridge" theory (goldene Brücke) -- the idea that criminal law should incentivize offenders to abandon their criminal enterprise by offering a path to impunity.

The BGH distinguishes between the "unfinished attempt" (unbeendeter Versuch) and the "finished attempt" (beendeter Versuch):
- **Unfinished attempt:** The offender has not yet done everything they believe necessary for completion. Voluntary abandonment requires simply ceasing further action.
- **Finished attempt:** The offender believes they have done everything necessary. Voluntary abandonment requires actively preventing the result.

## ECHR & EU

### European Convention on Human Rights

The ECHR does not directly regulate attempt liability but addresses it through several fundamental principles:

**Article 7 -- No Punishment Without Law (Nulla Poena Sine Lege):** The principle of legality requires that criminal offenses, including attempt, be clearly defined by law. The European Court of Human Rights has emphasized that the definition of attempt must be sufficiently precise to enable individuals to foresee the consequences of their conduct.

**Kafkaris v. Cyprus (Application No. 21906/04, Grand Chamber Judgment of February 12, 2008):** While not directly concerning attempt, this case established key principles regarding the foreseeability of criminal penalties, holding that Article 7 requires that the law clearly define both the offense and the applicable penalty, including the conditions for sentence reduction.

**Coëme and Others v. Belgium (Application Nos. 32492/96 et al., Judgment of June 22, 2000):** The Court confirmed that criminal law provisions, including those governing attempt and inchoate offenses, must satisfy the accessibility and foreseeability requirements of Article 7.

### EU Framework on Attempt

**Framework Decision 2002/475/JHA on Combating Terrorism:** Article 4 required member states to ensure that attempting to commit terrorist offenses as defined in Articles 1 through 3 is punishable. This was one of the first EU instruments to mandate criminalization of attempt for specific offense categories.

**Directive (EU) 2017/541 on Combating Terrorism:** Replacing the 2002 Framework Decision, Article 14 requires member states to ensure that attempting to commit offenses referred to in Articles 3 through 8 (terrorist offenses, offenses related to terrorist groups, and offenses related to terrorist activities) is punishable with "effective, proportionate and dissuasive criminal penalties, which may entail surrender."

**Directive 2011/36/EU on Preventing and Combating Trafficking in Human Beings:** Article 3 requires the criminalization of attempt to commit trafficking offenses, recognizing that attempt at such serious crimes warrants punishment regardless of completion.

**Directive (EU) 2024/1226 on Environmental Crime:** Article 3(4) requires member states to criminalize attempt for the most serious environmental offenses, establishing minimum penalty thresholds for attempted environmental crimes.

### Harmonization Efforts

The EU has pursued harmonization of attempt doctrine primarily through sector-specific directives rather than a general harmonization instrument. This approach reflects the diversity of national traditions regarding:
- The threshold for punishable attempt (preparation vs. commencement of execution)
- The penalty differential between attempt and completed offenses
- The treatment of impossible attempts
- The scope and effect of voluntary abandonment defenses

The European Commission's 2004 Communication on the approximation of criminal law noted significant divergences among member states regarding attempt doctrine and recommended continued sector-specific harmonization rather than a general framework instrument.

## Comparative Analysis

### Theoretical Foundations

The four jurisdictions reflect two principal theoretical approaches to attempt liability:

**Subjective Theory:** Focuses on the offender's criminal intent or dangerousness. Under this approach, attempt is punishable because the offender has demonstrated a criminal will. Germany's inclusion of the offender's "conception of the act" (Vorstellung von der Tat) in Section 22 StGB reflects strong subjective elements, as does the MPC's focus on conduct "strongly corroborative of criminal purpose."

**Objective Theory:** Focuses on the danger created by the offender's conduct to legally protected interests. South Korea's formal-objective approach, requiring commencement of statutory elements, exemplifies this tradition. The objective approach generally sets a higher threshold for criminal liability and draws a clearer line between preparation and attempt.

### Penalty Models

Three distinct models emerge for the relationship between attempt and completed offense penalties:

**Equivalence Model (United States):** Federal law and many states impose the same maximum penalty for attempt as for the completed offense, reflecting the MPC's position that the attempter's culpability equals that of the successful offender.

**Discretionary Mitigation Model (Germany, South Korea):** Both jurisdictions permit -- but do not require -- courts to impose reduced sentences for attempt compared to the completed offense. This approach recognizes that incomplete offenses may warrant less punishment while preserving judicial flexibility.

**Mandatory Reduction Model:** Some jurisdictions (e.g., New York, California) require automatic penalty reductions for attempt. This approach provides greater certainty but less flexibility.

### Voluntary Abandonment

All four jurisdictions recognize voluntary abandonment as either a defense or a ground for penalty mitigation, but the consequences vary dramatically:

- **Germany:** Complete exemption from punishment (Section 24 StGB) -- the most generous approach
- **South Korea:** Mandatory reduction or exemption (Article 26) -- similarly generous
- **United States:** Affirmative defense eliminating liability (MPC Section 5.01(4)) -- available in most jurisdictions
- **ECHR/EU:** Recognized as a mitigating factor; specific treatment left to member states

### Impossible Attempts

The treatment of impossible attempts reveals fundamental philosophical differences:

- **South Korea (Art. 27):** Punishable but with discretionary reduction or exemption
- **United States (MPC):** Punishable; factual impossibility eliminated as a defense
- **Germany (Section 23(3)):** Punishable but court may exempt if based on "gross lack of understanding"
- **Common law (traditional):** Legal impossibility remains a defense; factual impossibility does not
