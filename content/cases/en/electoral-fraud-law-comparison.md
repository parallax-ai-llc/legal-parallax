## Summary

Electoral fraud -- the deliberate manipulation, falsification, or subversion of an election process -- is universally condemned across democratic legal systems, yet each jurisdiction employs distinct statutory frameworks, enforcement mechanisms, and penalty structures to protect electoral integrity. South Korea addresses election offenses primarily through its comprehensive Public Official Election Act (공직선거법), which criminalizes a wide spectrum of conduct from vote buying to deepfake dissemination, with penalties of up to 7 years imprisonment and automatic forfeiture of voting rights upon conviction. The United States prosecutes electoral fraud through an overlapping web of federal statutes -- principally 18 U.S.C. Chapter 29 (Elections and Political Activities) and 52 U.S.C. sections 10307 and 20511 -- with penalties of up to 5 years imprisonment for most offenses, enforced across federal and state jurisdictions. Germany criminalizes election offenses through a dedicated cluster of provisions in the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), sections 107 through 108d, which punish disruption of elections, falsification of results, forgery of election documents, and voter coercion with up to 5 years imprisonment, with enhanced penalties for public officials. At the supranational level, the European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR) guarantees the right to free elections under Article 3 of Protocol No. 1, enforced through the European Court of Human Rights, while the European Union has developed an increasingly robust election integrity framework through the Venice Commission's Code of Good Practice in Electoral Matters and the European Democracy Shield initiative.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's electoral fraud laws are anchored in the Public Official Election Act (공직선거법, Act No. 4739, originally enacted March 16, 1994, most recently amended 2024). This comprehensive statute governs all aspects of election conduct for presidential, National Assembly, and local government elections. The National Election Commission (중앙선거관리위원회), an independent constitutional body established under Articles 114-116 of the Constitution, oversees enforcement. The Political Parties Act (정당법) and the Political Fund Act (정치자금법) provide supplementary regulation of party financing and political donations.

### Key Offenses and Penalties

**Vote Buying and Bribery (Articles 230-231):** Any person who provides, offers, or promises money, goods, or other benefits to voters in connection with an election, or who accepts such benefits, faces imprisonment for up to 5 years or a fine of up to 30 million won. This prohibition extends to providing meals, entertainment, or transportation beyond nominal value.

**Intimidation and Coercion (Article 237):** Threatening, coercing, or obstructing voters, candidates, or election officials in connection with the exercise of electoral rights is punishable by up to 7 years imprisonment or a fine of up to 50 million won.

**Fraudulent Voting (Articles 232-236):** Casting a vote under a false identity, voting more than once, or tampering with ballots or ballot boxes carries imprisonment of up to 7 years. Election officials who falsify vote counts or election results face equivalent penalties.

**False Campaign Statements (Article 250):** Publishing false information about a candidate with the intent to influence an election is punishable by up to 5 years imprisonment or a fine of up to 30 million won. South Korea notably criminalizes both false factual statements and certain defamatory statements in the electoral context.

**Deepfake Prohibition (Article 82-8, added December 2023):** The creation, distribution, or use of AI-generated deepfake videos, photos, or audio that are realistic enough to mislead the public is prohibited during the 90 days prior to an election. Violations carry criminal penalties including imprisonment or fines.

### Collateral Consequences

Conviction for an election offense carries severe collateral consequences beyond the criminal sentence:

- A fine of 1 million won or more results in a **5-year ban on voting rights**.
- A sentence of imprisonment or suspended imprisonment results in a **10-year ban on voting rights** from the date the sentence is finalized.
- The Constitutional Court of Korea upheld the constitutionality of these voting bans in October 2025, ruling that they serve the legitimate purpose of protecting electoral integrity.
- Elected officials convicted of election offenses automatically forfeit their seats.

### Landmark Cases

**Lee Jae-myung Election Law Conviction (2024):** In November 2024, opposition leader Lee Jae-myung was convicted of violating the Public Official Election Act for making false statements during the 2022 presidential campaign regarding his role in a development scandal. The Seoul Central District Court found that his public statements about the Seongnam development project constituted false representations of material fact. The conviction placed his political future in jeopardy, as a confirmed fine of 1 million won or more would render him ineligible for public office.

**2020 General Election Fraud Allegations:** Following the April 2020 National Assembly elections, in which the ruling Democratic Party won a larger-than-expected majority, supporters of the opposition United Future Party alleged systematic rigging of early voting results. Over 182 formal complaints were filed with the National Election Commission. However, no legal challenge succeeded -- election rigging campaigners did not win a single court case in five years of litigation. The controversy nonetheless compelled the NEC to revert to hand counting of ballots in subsequent elections to restore public confidence.

**December 2024 Martial Law Incident:** On December 3, 2024, President Yoon Suk-yeol declared martial law, during which troops were deployed to the National Election Commission headquarters, reportedly to search for evidence of election fraud in the April 2024 parliamentary elections. The martial law was lifted within hours after the National Assembly voted to overturn it. Yoon was subsequently impeached, and the Constitutional Court upheld the impeachment in 2025. The NEC rejected all claims of electoral fraud as baseless.

### Enforcement Structure

The National Election Commission operates as an independent constitutional organ with quasi-judicial authority. Its staff can halt violations, issue warnings or corrective orders, and file criminal charges with prosecutorial authorities. The NEC maintains election monitoring teams at all levels -- central, metropolitan/provincial, and district -- and operates a real-time election monitoring system.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States employs a decentralized, multi-layered approach to electoral fraud prosecution, with both federal and state statutes applicable depending on the nature of the offense and the election involved. Federal law primarily addresses fraud in connection with federal elections (presidential, congressional, and senatorial), while states retain primary authority over the administration of elections and the prosecution of most election crimes.

The principal federal statutes are found in two titles:

- **Title 18, U.S.C. Chapter 29 (Elections and Political Activities):** Sections 594-597 address voter intimidation, interference by government employees, and vote buying.
- **Title 52, U.S.C. (Voting and Elections):** Section 10307 (derived from the Voting Rights Act of 1965) prohibits false voter registration information, conspiracy to encourage false registration, and payment for registration or voting. Section 20511 (derived from the National Voter Registration Act of 1993) criminalizes voter intimidation, coercion, and schemes to deprive residents of a fair election process.

Additional federal statutes include 18 U.S.C. section 241 (conspiracy against rights), section 242 (deprivation of rights under color of law), and section 1973i (now recodified at 52 U.S.C. section 10307).

### Key Offenses and Penalties

**Voter Intimidation (18 U.S.C. section 594):** Intimidating, threatening, or coercing any person for the purpose of interfering with the right to vote freely in a federal election is punishable by a fine and imprisonment for not more than 1 year.

**Interference by Government Officials (18 U.S.C. section 595):** Using official authority in connection with federally financed activity to interfere with or influence a federal election is punishable by a fine and imprisonment for not more than 1 year.

**Vote Buying (18 U.S.C. section 597):** Making or offering expenditures to any person to vote, or refrain from voting, for a candidate in a federal election is punishable by up to 1 year imprisonment (misdemeanor) or up to 2 years imprisonment if committed willfully (felony).

**Fraudulent Voter Registration and Ballot Fraud (52 U.S.C. section 20511):** Knowingly and willfully depriving residents of a fair and impartially conducted election process, including through fraudulent voter registration applications or ballots, is punishable by a fine and imprisonment for not more than 5 years.

**Conspiracy Against Rights (18 U.S.C. section 241):** Two or more persons conspiring to injure, oppress, threaten, or intimidate any person in the free exercise of any constitutional right -- including the right to vote -- face imprisonment for up to 10 years, or life imprisonment if the conspiracy results in death.

**False Voter Registration Information (52 U.S.C. section 10307):** Knowingly providing false information regarding name, address, or period of residence for voter registration purposes is punishable by a fine of up to $10,000 or imprisonment for not more than 5 years, or both.

### Statute of Limitations

Federal election crimes are generally subject to a **5-year statute of limitations** from the date of the violation.

### Landmark Cases

**Chicago Election Fraud (1982):** An estimated 100,000 fraudulent ballots were cast in Chicago elections, prompting a major Department of Justice investigation. Sixty-three individuals were ultimately convicted on charges including vote buying, impersonation fraud, fictitious voter registrations, phony absentee ballots, and voting by non-citizens. The case remains one of the largest-scale election fraud prosecutions in U.S. history.

**Reynolds v. Sims, 377 U.S. 533 (1964):** While not a fraud prosecution, this landmark Supreme Court decision established the "one person, one vote" principle under the Equal Protection Clause, holding that state legislative districts must be roughly equal in population. The decision created a constitutional foundation for challenging malapportionment as a structural form of electoral manipulation.

**Anderson v. United States, 417 U.S. 211 (1974):** The Supreme Court upheld federal convictions for vote fraud in a West Virginia primary election under 18 U.S.C. section 241, holding that the right to have one's vote counted honestly is a right secured by the Constitution. The Court affirmed that stuffing a ballot box with fraudulent ballots dilutes the value of legitimate votes and constitutes a conspiracy against rights.

**Crawford v. Marion County Election Board, 553 U.S. 181 (2008):** The Supreme Court upheld Indiana's voter photo identification law, finding that the state's interest in preventing voter fraud, modernizing elections, and safeguarding voter confidence justified the minimal burden on voters who lacked photo identification.

**Heritage Foundation Election Fraud Database:** As of 2025, the Heritage Foundation had documented over 1,400 proven instances of election fraud across the United States, including convictions for absentee ballot fraud, duplicate voting, illegal voter registration, ineligible voter participation, and vote buying across multiple states.

### Enforcement Structure

The Department of Justice's Public Integrity Section and the Election Crimes Branch within the Criminal Division coordinate federal election fraud prosecutions. Each of the 94 U.S. Attorney's offices designates a District Election Officer (DEO) to serve as the office's point of contact for election crime matters. State attorneys general and local district attorneys prosecute election crimes under state law. The Federal Election Commission (FEC) enforces campaign finance law but does not have criminal prosecution authority.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany addresses electoral fraud through a dedicated cluster of criminal provisions in the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), supplemented by the Federal Electoral Act (Bundeswahlgesetz, BWahlG) and the European Election Act (Europawahlgesetz, EuWG). The relevant criminal provisions are found in sections 107 through 108d of the StGB, located in the chapter on "Offenses Against Constitutional Organs and Elections" (Straftaten gegen Verfassungsorgane sowie bei Wahlen und Abstimmungen). These provisions protect the integrity of federal, state (Land), and local elections, as well as European Parliament elections.

### Key Offenses and Penalties

**Disruption of an Election (section 107 StGB):** Whoever unlawfully prevents or disrupts an election or the determination of its result by force, threat of force, or through trickery (List) faces imprisonment for up to 5 years or a fine. Attempt is punishable.

**Falsification of Election Results (section 107a StGB):** Whoever, without authorization, votes, falsifies the result of an election, or knowingly allows the result to be falsified faces imprisonment for up to 5 years or a fine. This provision targets both the active manipulation of votes and the passive allowance of falsified results by election officials. Attempt is punishable.

**Falsification of Election Documents (section 107b StGB):** Whoever registers falsely or otherwise manipulates the voter register, certification, or nomination documents, or makes incorrect entries regarding eligibility in such documents, faces imprisonment for up to 5 years or a fine. If the offender acts in an official capacity, the penalty is imprisonment from 1 to 10 years. Attempt is punishable.

**Violation of Election Secrecy (section 107c StGB):** Whoever unlawfully obtains information about, or communicates to third parties, the content of another person's vote faces imprisonment for up to 2 years or a fine.

**Voter Coercion (section 108 StGB):** Whoever unlawfully compels another person through force, threat of serious harm, or economic pressure to vote, to vote in a particular way, or to refrain from voting faces imprisonment for up to 5 years or a fine. Attempt is punishable.

**Deception of Voters (section 108a StGB):** Whoever deceives another person through falsehoods about the circumstances of the election in order to cause the person to refrain from voting or to vote invalidly faces imprisonment for up to 2 years or a fine.

**Bribery of Voters (section 108b StGB):** Whoever offers, promises, or grants gifts or other benefits to a voter in order to induce them to vote in a particular way or to refrain from voting, or whoever demands, accepts, or solicits such benefits in return for voting in a particular way, faces imprisonment for up to 5 years or a fine.

**Prohibition on Exerting Pressure on Subordinates (section 108d StGB):** It is unlawful for a person in a position of authority (employer, supervisor) to exert pressure on subordinates regarding how they vote. Penalties are subsumed under sections 108 and 108b.

### Aggravating Circumstances

Where the offender acts in an official capacity (als Amtstrager), courts may impose enhanced penalties. Additional charges may be brought under section 267 StGB (forgery of documents) or section 353b StGB (breach of official secrets). Conviction may result in professional consequences for civil servants, including disciplinary action and dismissal, as well as loss of eligibility for public office.

### Intent Requirement

All election fraud offenses under German law require criminal intent (Vorsatz) -- the knowledge and will to impermissibly influence an election. Negligent violations are not punishable under sections 107-108d.

### Landmark Cases

**2021 Berlin Election Rerun:** The September 2021 Berlin elections were marred by severe administrative failures -- including insufficient ballot papers, excessively long queues, and polling stations remaining open past official closing times -- that led the Berlin Constitutional Court (Verfassungsgerichtshof) to order a full rerun of the state parliament (Abgeordnetenhaus) election in February 2023. While classified as organizational failure rather than intentional fraud, the case demonstrated the stringent standards German courts apply to electoral integrity and highlighted the constitutional remedy of election annulment.

**1994 Bundesverfassungsgericht Election Review (BVerfGE 89, 243):** The Federal Constitutional Court established the principle that election errors justify annulment only where they are of sufficient magnitude to affect the seat allocation in the legislature. Minor irregularities that do not affect the overall result do not warrant invalidation of an election.

### Enforcement Structure

The Federal Returning Officer (Bundeswahlleiterin) oversees the administration of federal elections and certifies results. State returning officers (Landeswahlleiter) perform equivalent functions at the Land level. Criminal prosecution of election offenses falls to state prosecutors (Staatsanwaltschaften) under the general criminal jurisdiction of the Lander. The Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) adjudicates election challenges (Wahlprufung) regarding Bundestag elections under Article 41 of the Basic Law.

## ECHR & EU

### European Convention on Human Rights

**Article 3 of Protocol No. 1 -- Right to Free Elections:** The cornerstone of European electoral rights law provides: "The High Contracting Parties undertake to hold free elections at reasonable intervals by secret ballot, under conditions which will ensure the free expression of the opinion of the people in the choice of the legislature." Though phrased as a state obligation rather than an individual right, the European Court of Human Rights has interpreted it to encompass both the right to vote (active electoral right) and the right to stand for election (passive electoral right).

The Court applies a proportionality test to restrictions on electoral rights, requiring that any limitation: (a) pursues a legitimate aim, (b) is not arbitrary, and (c) does not impair the essence of the right. States enjoy a "margin of appreciation" in organizing their electoral systems, but this margin is not unlimited.

### Landmark ECHR Cases

**Mathieu-Mohin and Clerfayt v. Belgium (1987):** The Court established the foundational framework for interpreting Article 3 of Protocol No. 1, holding that the provision guarantees individual rights -- including the right to vote and stand for election -- and that restrictions must be proportionate to a legitimate aim.

**Hirst v. United Kingdom (No. 2) [GC] (2005):** The Grand Chamber held that the United Kingdom's blanket ban on prisoner voting violated Article 3 of Protocol No. 1. The Court ruled that a general, automatic, and indiscriminate disenfranchisement of all convicted prisoners, irrespective of the nature or gravity of their offense, was incompatible with the Convention. This case established that voting rights restrictions must be proportionate and individualized.

**Namat Aliyev v. Azerbaijan (2010):** The Court found a violation of Article 3 of Protocol No. 1 in respect of the applicant, an opposition candidate, in connection with the 2005 Azerbaijani parliamentary elections. The Court determined that widespread irregularities -- including ballot stuffing, falsification of protocols, and obstruction of election observers -- rendered the election process fundamentally flawed and deprived the applicant of his right to stand for election in conditions that ensured the free expression of the people.

**Davydov and Others v. Russia (2017):** The Court found a violation of Article 3 of Protocol No. 1 where 101 applicants alleged that the annulment of their ballot papers constituted a violation of their active electoral rights. The Court held that the arbitrary annulment of lawfully cast ballots amounted to a denial of the right to vote and that Russia had failed to meet its positive obligation to ensure the integrity of the electoral process.

**Mugemangango v. Belgium (2021):** The Grand Chamber established that states have a positive obligation under Article 3 of Protocol No. 1 to put in place a system for effective examination of individual complaints regarding electoral irregularities. This includes ensuring adequate procedural safeguards in the process of verifying election results.

**Bradshaw and Others v. United Kingdom (2025):** The Court addressed allegations that the UK government had failed to investigate Russian interference in domestic elections. The Court concluded that there was no violation of Article 3 of Protocol No. 1, finding that the state had not breached its positive obligations. The case was subsequently referred to the Grand Chamber.

### Venice Commission Standards

The European Commission for Democracy through Law (Venice Commission), the Council of Europe's advisory body on constitutional matters with 61 member states, has developed the most comprehensive international framework for electoral standards:

**Code of Good Practice in Electoral Matters (2002):** This foundational document establishes five principles of the European electoral heritage: (1) universal suffrage, (2) equal suffrage, (3) free suffrage, (4) secret suffrage, and (5) direct suffrage. It further requires that fundamental elements of election law -- including rules on seat allocation, electoral commission composition, and constituency boundaries -- must not be amended less than one year before an election.

**Guidelines on Electoral Fraud:** The Venice Commission holds that electoral fraud encompasses any deliberate act of manipulation that distorts the authentic expression of the will of voters, including falsification of voter registers, ballot stuffing, manipulation of vote counts, voter intimidation, and vote buying. States are required to establish effective criminal penalties and independent enforcement mechanisms.

**Stability Principle:** The Venice Commission has emphasized that the fundamental elements of electoral law should enjoy temporal stability, stating that modifications to core election rules within one year of an election create an inherent risk of partisan manipulation.

### EU Election Integrity Framework

**European Democracy Shield (2025):** Launched by the European Commission on November 12, 2025, this initiative addresses three priority areas: (1) reinforcing situational awareness and response capacity to safeguard the integrity of the information space; (2) strengthening democratic institutions, free and fair elections, and independent media; and (3) boosting societal resilience and citizen engagement.

**European Cooperation Network on Elections:** Established to coordinate Member States' election preparedness, this network developed a Checklist on the Integrity of Elections and a Risk-Management Matrix for Elections to support national authorities in identifying and mitigating threats to electoral processes, including cyber threats, disinformation campaigns, and foreign interference.

**Commission Recommendation (EU) 2023/2829:** This recommendation calls on Member States to enhance the resilience and integrity of electoral processes, including through measures to counter disinformation, ensure transparency in political advertising, and protect elections from foreign interference. It encourages states to adopt national action plans for election security.

**Regulation on Transparency and Targeting of Political Advertising (2024):** Requires transparency regarding the sponsors, funding, and targeting methods of political advertisements disseminated across the EU, particularly online. It mandates clear labeling of political advertising and restricts the use of sensitive personal data for political targeting.

## Comparative Analysis

### Structural Differences

The four jurisdictions reflect fundamentally different approaches to combating electoral fraud:

**Statutory architecture:** South Korea and Germany employ dedicated, comprehensive election crime statutes (the Public Official Election Act and StGB sections 107-108d, respectively), providing clear and specific definitions of prohibited conduct. The United States, by contrast, scatters its election fraud provisions across multiple titles of federal code and relies heavily on state law, creating a more fragmented enforcement landscape. The ECHR/EU framework operates at the highest level of abstraction, establishing rights and principles rather than specific criminal prohibitions.

**Enforcement centralization:** South Korea's NEC represents the most centralized enforcement model, functioning as a constitutionally independent body with investigative and quasi-prosecutorial powers. Germany occupies a middle position, with the Federal Returning Officer overseeing administration while criminal prosecution remains with state authorities. The United States is the most decentralized, dividing enforcement authority among federal prosecutors, state attorneys general, and local district attorneys. The ECHR relies entirely on state compliance with Convention obligations, with the Court serving as a post hoc supervisory mechanism.

**Collateral consequences:** South Korea imposes the most severe collateral consequences, with automatic voting bans of 5-10 years and mandatory forfeiture of elected office. Germany permits judicial imposition of voting restrictions on a case-by-case basis. The United States varies dramatically by state, with some states permanently disenfranchising convicted felons and others restoring voting rights upon completion of sentence. The ECHR, through Hirst v. UK, has established that blanket disenfranchisement is impermissible.

### Digital-Era Challenges

The rise of digital technologies has created new vectors for electoral manipulation that challenge traditional legal frameworks:

- **South Korea** leads in legislative specificity, having enacted an explicit prohibition on electoral deepfakes in its 2023 amendment to the Public Official Election Act.
- The **United States** lacks a unified federal statute addressing AI-generated electoral disinformation, relying on existing fraud and deception statutes of uncertain applicability.
- **Germany** applies its general criminal fraud and deception provisions to digital electoral manipulation but has not enacted election-specific digital legislation.
- The **EU** has responded with the European Democracy Shield and the Political Advertising Transparency Regulation, taking a regulatory rather than criminal approach.

### Evidentiary Standards

All four jurisdictions require proof of intent for criminal liability. South Korea and Germany demand specific intent to influence electoral outcomes. The United States requires proof of "knowing and willful" conduct for most federal election crimes. The ECHR assesses state conduct under a reasonableness standard, evaluating whether the state has met its positive obligation to ensure free elections rather than adjudicating individual criminal culpability.
