## Summary

Modern slavery and human trafficking laws address the criminalization, prevention, and remediation of forced labor, servitude, debt bondage, and trafficking in persons, with increasing emphasis on corporate supply chain transparency and mandatory due diligence obligations that require businesses to identify, prevent, and report risks of exploitation in their operations and supply chains. South Korea criminalizes human trafficking under the Criminal Act (형법) Articles 289-296, supplemented by the Act on the Prevention of Trafficking in Persons and Protection of Victims (인신매매등방지 및 피해자보호 등에 관한 법률, enacted 2023), which established a comprehensive framework for victim identification, protection, and support services, alongside the Act on the Punishment of Intermediating in the Sex Trade and Associated Acts (성매매알선 등 행위의 처벌에 관한 법률); the National Human Rights Commission of Korea (국가인권위원회) plays an oversight role, and Korea's 2023 legislation brought its legal framework into closer alignment with the UN Trafficking Protocol. The United States combats trafficking through the Trafficking Victims Protection Act (TVPA, 22 U.S.C. Section 7101 et seq., 2000) and its reauthorizations, the Federal Acquisition Regulation (FAR) anti-trafficking provisions, California's Transparency in Supply Chains Act (2010), and Section 307 of the Tariff Act of 1930 (19 U.S.C. Section 1307) prohibiting importation of goods produced by forced labor, enforced by U.S. Customs and Border Protection's Withhold Release Orders (WROs) and the Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act (UFLPA, 2021). Germany addresses modern slavery through Sections 232-233b of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB, Criminal Code) criminalizing human trafficking and forced labor, and the Lieferkettensorgfaltspflichtengesetz (LkSG, Supply Chain Due Diligence Act, 2023) requiring large enterprises to conduct human rights and environmental due diligence throughout their supply chains. The EU framework encompasses the Anti-Trafficking Directive 2011/36/EU, the proposed Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD), and the Regulation (EU) 2023/1115 on deforestation-free products, while the ECHR has developed extensive jurisprudence under Article 4 (prohibition of slavery and forced labor), with the European Court of Human Rights establishing positive obligations on states to prevent trafficking and protect victims.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's approach to modern slavery and human trafficking evolved significantly with the landmark enactment of the Act on the Prevention of Trafficking in Persons and Protection of Victims in 2023, following years of international criticism regarding gaps in Korean anti-trafficking legislation.

**Criminal Act (형법) Articles 289-296:** The Criminal Act criminalizes trafficking in persons, including buying and selling of persons (Article 289), kidnapping and abduction (Articles 287-288), and trafficking for sexual exploitation (Article 289-2). Penalties range from 2 to 15 years imprisonment depending on the purpose and circumstances of the trafficking.

**Trafficking Prevention Act (2023):** This comprehensive legislation, enacted after extensive advocacy by human rights organizations and pressure from the U.S. State Department's Trafficking in Persons (TIP) Report, establishes a unified legal definition of trafficking in persons consistent with the Palermo Protocol, creates a national coordination mechanism under the Ministry of Gender Equality and Family (여성가족부), mandates victim identification procedures for law enforcement, and provides for comprehensive victim support services including shelter, medical care, legal assistance, and vocational training.

### Victim Protection

The 2023 Act significantly strengthened victim protection measures:

**Victim Identification:** Law enforcement and immigration officials are trained in proactive victim identification procedures. The Act establishes indicators for identifying trafficking victims among vulnerable populations including migrant workers, particularly those employed under the Employment Permit System (고용허가제), and individuals in the entertainment visa (E-6) category.

**Residence Permits:** Foreign trafficking victims may be granted temporary residence permits (G-1 visa) under the Immigration Act (출입국관리법), allowing them to remain in Korea during criminal proceedings. The 2023 Act expanded access to these protections and reduced the evidentiary requirements for victim status.

**Non-Punishment Principle:** The Act establishes that trafficking victims shall not be punished for unlawful acts committed as a direct consequence of their trafficking situation, including immigration violations and acts committed under coercion.

### Labor Exploitation

South Korea faces ongoing challenges related to labor exploitation of migrant workers, particularly in agriculture, fishing, and manufacturing:

**Employment Permit System (고용허가제):** The EPS, governed by the Act on the Employment of Foreign Workers (외국인근로자의 고용 등에 관한 법률), has been criticized for tying workers to specific employers, creating conditions conducive to exploitation. The system's workplace change restrictions (limited to three changes within the employment period) have been identified as a structural risk factor for forced labor.

**Fishing Industry:** The Act on the Employment of Foreign Seafarers (선원법) and related regulations have been reformed to address severe exploitation of migrant fishers documented by the National Human Rights Commission and international organizations, including cases involving debt bondage, physical abuse, and confiscation of identity documents.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States operates one of the most comprehensive anti-trafficking legal frameworks globally, combining criminal prosecution, victim protection, and supply chain regulation.

**Trafficking Victims Protection Act (TVPA, 2000):** The TVPA, codified at 22 U.S.C. Section 7101 et seq. and reauthorized multiple times (most recently the Trafficking Victims Protection Reauthorization Act of 2022), establishes federal criminal offenses for sex trafficking (18 U.S.C. Section 1591), forced labor (18 U.S.C. Section 1589), trafficking with respect to peonage, slavery, involuntary servitude, or forced labor (18 U.S.C. Section 1590), and document servitude (18 U.S.C. Section 1592). Penalties range up to life imprisonment for trafficking resulting in death or involving kidnapping, aggravated sexual abuse, or an attempt to kill.

**TVPA Victim Services:** The TVPA mandates comprehensive services for trafficking victims, including certification by the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) for access to federal benefits, T-nonimmigrant status (T-visa) providing up to four years of residence and a pathway to permanent residence, and the Continued Presence mechanism allowing law enforcement to request temporary immigration relief for victims assisting investigations.

### Supply Chain Regulation

**Section 307 of the Tariff Act of 1930 (19 U.S.C. Section 1307):** This provision prohibits the importation of goods mined, produced, or manufactured wholly or in part by forced labor, including convict labor, forced child labor, and indentured labor. U.S. Customs and Border Protection (CBP) enforces this through Withhold Release Orders (WROs) and findings, which have increased dramatically since the closure of the "consumptive demand" exception by the Trade Facilitation and Trade Enforcement Act of 2015.

**Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act (UFLPA, 2021):** The UFLPA established a rebuttable presumption that goods mined, produced, or manufactured wholly or in part in the Xinjiang Uyghur Autonomous Region of China are produced by forced labor and prohibited from importation under Section 307. The Act created the Forced Labor Enforcement Task Force (FLETF) and required the development of an entity list and enforcement strategy.

**California Transparency in Supply Chains Act (2010):** Requires retail sellers and manufacturers doing business in California with annual worldwide gross receipts exceeding $100 million to disclose their efforts to eradicate slavery and human trafficking from their direct supply chains for tangible goods offered for sale.

### Federal Acquisition Regulation (FAR)

FAR Subpart 22.17 and 52.222-50 prohibit federal contractors and subcontractors from engaging in trafficking-related activities, including charging recruitment fees, destroying identity documents, and using misleading recruitment practices. Contractors with contracts exceeding $500,000 performed outside the United States must maintain compliance plans and certify compliance.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany addresses modern slavery through a combination of criminal law provisions and groundbreaking supply chain due diligence legislation.

**Strafgesetzbuch (StGB) Sections 232-233b:** The Criminal Code criminalizes human trafficking for sexual exploitation (Section 232), trafficking for labor exploitation (Section 232a), trafficking for the purpose of exploitation involving deprivation of liberty (Section 232b), and forced labor (Section 233). The 2016 reform (Act to Improve Combating Human Trafficking) significantly expanded the scope of criminal offenses, aligned definitions with the EU Anti-Trafficking Directive, and increased penalties to up to 10 years imprisonment for aggravated cases.

**Forced Prostitution (Section 232a StGB):** Separately criminalizes inducing a person to engage in prostitution under exploitative conditions, with penalties of 6 months to 10 years imprisonment.

### Supply Chain Due Diligence (LkSG)

The Lieferkettensorgfaltspflichtengesetz (Act on Corporate Due Diligence Obligations in Supply Chains), effective January 1, 2023, represents one of the world's most significant mandatory human rights due diligence laws:

**Scope:** Initially applicable to companies domiciled in Germany with at least 3,000 employees (from January 1, 2023) and extended to companies with at least 1,000 employees (from January 1, 2024). The Act covers a company's own business operations, the actions of contractual partners (direct suppliers), and, upon substantiated knowledge, indirect suppliers further down the supply chain.

**Due Diligence Obligations:** Companies must establish a risk management system, conduct regular and ad hoc risk analyses, adopt a policy statement, implement preventive measures for their own business area and direct suppliers, take remedial action when violations are identified, establish a complaint mechanism, and document and report on their compliance annually.

**Enforcement:** The Bundesamt für Wirtschaft und Ausfuhrkontrolle (BAFA, Federal Office for Economic Affairs and Export Control) is the competent enforcement authority, empowered to conduct investigations, issue orders for compliance, and impose fines of up to 2% of average annual worldwide turnover for companies with more than EUR 400 million in turnover. Non-compliance may also result in exclusion from public procurement for up to three years.

**Protected Interests:** The LkSG protects against human rights violations including forced labor, slavery, child labor, discrimination, unsafe working conditions, and environmental violations causing health impacts, referencing international instruments including the ILO Forced Labour Convention (No. 29), the ILO Abolition of Forced Labour Convention (No. 105), and the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child.

### Victim Protection

Germany provides trafficking victims with a reflection period of at least three months under the Residence Act (Aufenthaltsgesetz, AufenthG) Section 59(7), during which victims cannot be deported and may access counseling services. Section 25(4a) AufenthG provides for the grant of a residence permit to trafficking victims who cooperate with law enforcement. The Psychosoziale Prozessbegleitung (psychosocial process support) system provides specialized assistance to victims during criminal proceedings.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Article 4 Jurisprudence

The European Court of Human Rights has developed transformative jurisprudence under Article 4 ECHR (prohibition of slavery and forced labor):

**Siliadin v. France (Application No. 73316/01, 2005):** The landmark case in which the Court held that France violated Article 4 by failing to afford adequate criminal law protection to a Togolese national held in servitude as a domestic worker. The Court found that Article 4 requires states to adopt criminal legislation penalizing practices prohibited by Article 4 and to apply such legislation in practice, establishing positive obligations.

**Rantsev v. Cyprus and Russia (Application No. 25965/04, 2010):** The Court held that trafficking in human beings falls within the scope of Article 4 ECHR, finding violations by both Cyprus and Russia. The Court established that Article 4 entails a positive obligation to put in place a legislative and administrative framework to combat trafficking, to take operational measures to protect identified or potential victims, and to investigate potential trafficking situations.

**Chowdury and Others v. Greece (Application No. 21884/15, 2017):** The Court found a violation of Article 4 in the case of Bangladeshi strawberry pickers subjected to forced labor, holding that Greece had failed to fulfill its positive obligations to prevent trafficking and forced labor, to protect victims, and to conduct an effective investigation.

**S.M. v. Croatia (Application No. 60561/14, Grand Chamber, 2020):** The Grand Chamber clarified that Article 4 encompasses trafficking for the purpose of sexual exploitation and that states must establish comprehensive operational frameworks for victim identification and protection.

### EU Anti-Trafficking Directive 2011/36/EU

The Directive establishes minimum standards for the prevention and combating of trafficking in human beings and the protection of victims:

**Definition:** Trafficking is defined as the recruitment, transportation, transfer, harboring, or reception of persons through threat or use of force, coercion, abduction, fraud, deception, abuse of power or position of vulnerability, or giving or receiving payments to achieve consent of a controlling person, for the purpose of exploitation including sexual exploitation, forced labor, slavery, servitude, exploitation of criminal activities, or organ removal (Article 2).

**Penalties:** Member states must ensure maximum penalties of at least 5 years imprisonment, rising to at least 10 years for aggravated circumstances (Article 4).

**Victim Protection (Articles 11-17):** Mandatory provision of assistance and support before, during, and after criminal proceedings; access to legal counseling and representation; protection of privacy and identity; special protection measures for child victims including the appointment of guardians and a presumption of minority in cases of uncertainty.

**Non-Punishment (Article 8):** Member states must take measures to ensure that competent national authorities are entitled not to prosecute or impose penalties on victims of trafficking for their involvement in criminal activities that they have been compelled to commit.

### Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD)

The CSDDD, adopted in 2024 after extensive negotiations, requires large EU and non-EU companies meeting specified thresholds to conduct human rights and environmental due diligence across their value chains. The Directive mandates the identification, prevention, mitigation, and remediation of adverse human rights impacts, including forced labor and trafficking, and establishes civil liability for failures to comply with due diligence obligations. Companies must also adopt climate transition plans. The CSDDD goes beyond Germany's LkSG by covering the entire value chain (not just direct suppliers with limited indirect supplier coverage), establishing civil liability, and requiring climate transition plans.

### EU Forced Labour Regulation

Regulation (EU) 2024/3015, adopted in late 2024, prohibits the placing on the EU market, making available, or exporting from the EU of products made with forced labor. National competent authorities and the European Commission investigate and identify forced labor risks, with the power to order withdrawal of products from the market. This regulation mirrors the approach of the U.S. Tariff Act Section 307 but applies to products both imported into and produced within the EU.

## Comparative Analysis

### Criminal Law Approaches

All four jurisdictions criminalize human trafficking and forced labor, but with varying scope and severity. The United States provides the most severe penalties (up to life imprisonment), reflecting its aggressive prosecutorial approach. Germany's 2016 reform aligned its criminal provisions with EU standards, providing up to 10 years for aggravated cases. South Korea's 2023 Trafficking Prevention Act addressed long-standing definitional gaps. The ECHR's Article 4 jurisprudence has established that states bear positive obligations to criminalize, investigate, and prevent trafficking, providing a constitutional floor for member states.

### Supply Chain Transparency and Due Diligence

The most significant divergence is in corporate obligations. The EU/Germany approach has moved furthest toward mandatory due diligence, with Germany's LkSG (2023) and the EU CSDDD establishing binding obligations for companies to conduct human rights due diligence throughout their supply chains. The United States relies on a combination of import prohibitions (Section 307, UFLPA), disclosure requirements (California TISCA), and government contractor obligations (FAR). South Korea has not yet enacted mandatory supply chain legislation, relying on voluntary CSR frameworks.

### Victim Protection

All jurisdictions provide some form of immigration relief for trafficking victims, but the scope varies. The U.S. T-visa system provides the most comprehensive pathway to permanent residence. Germany and EU member states provide residence permits tied to cooperation with law enforcement, with the EU Directive establishing minimum standards. South Korea's 2023 Act expanded victim protections but implementation remains in early stages. The non-punishment principle, requiring that trafficking victims not be prosecuted for offenses committed as a direct result of their trafficking, is recognized across all jurisdictions but applied with varying consistency.

### Import Prohibitions

The United States has been the global leader in using trade measures to combat forced labor, particularly through the UFLPA's rebuttable presumption mechanism. The EU's Forced Labour Regulation (2024) represents a significant convergence toward the U.S. approach. Germany's LkSG adds procurement exclusion as an enforcement tool. South Korea has not adopted comparable trade-based forced labor measures.
