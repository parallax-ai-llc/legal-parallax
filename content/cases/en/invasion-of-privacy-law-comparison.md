## Summary

Invasion of privacy -- the tortious interference with an individual's right to be free from unwanted intrusion, disclosure, or exploitation of personal affairs -- has evolved from a scholarly concept proposed by Samuel Warren and Louis Brandeis in their seminal 1890 Harvard Law Review article into a complex, multi-layered area of law intersecting tort, constitutional, statutory, and increasingly data protection regimes across all major legal systems. South Korea protects privacy through the Constitution (헌법) Article 17 (right to privacy), the Civil Act (민법) Article 750 (general tort liability), the Personal Information Protection Act (개인정보 보호법, PIPA) of 2011, and the Act on Promotion of Information and Communications Network Utilization and Information Protection (정보통신망법), with the Constitutional Court recognizing the right to informational self-determination (개인정보자기결정권) as a fundamental right in its 2005 fingerprint collection decision. The United States recognizes four distinct privacy torts under the Restatement (Second) of Torts Section 652 -- intrusion upon seclusion, public disclosure of private facts, false light, and appropriation of name or likeness -- alongside constitutional privacy protections under the Fourth Amendment and the penumbral right of privacy established in Griswold v. Connecticut (1965), with recent developments including the Supreme Court's extension of Fourth Amendment protection to cell-site location information in Carpenter v. United States (2018) and a patchwork of state privacy statutes led by the California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA/CPRA). Germany protects privacy through the constitutionally grounded general personality right (allgemeines Persönlichkeitsrecht) derived from Articles 1 and 2 of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz), the right to informational self-determination (Recht auf informationelle Selbstbestimmung) established by the Federal Constitutional Court in the 1983 Census Decision, and the Bundesdatenschutzgesetz (BDSG) implementing the GDPR. At the European level, the ECHR guarantees the right to respect for private life under Article 8, the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights enshrines data protection as a fundamental right under Article 8, and the GDPR establishes the world's most comprehensive data protection framework with enforcement actions reaching billions of euros.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's privacy protection framework combines constitutional guarantees, comprehensive data protection legislation, and tort law remedies.

### Constitutional Foundation

Article 17 of the Constitution provides: "The privacy of no citizen shall be infringed" (모든 국민은 사생활의 비밀과 자유를 침해받지 아니한다). Article 18 guarantees the secrecy of correspondence. The Constitutional Court (헌법재판소) has interpreted these provisions expansively.

**Fingerprint Collection Decision (2005HunMa513, 2005):** The Constitutional Court recognized the "right to informational self-determination" (개인정보자기결정권) as a constitutionally protected right, holding that mandatory fingerprint collection for national ID cards required strict proportionality review.

**Internet Real-Name System Decision (2010HunMa47, 2012):** The Court struck down the mandatory internet real-name verification system as unconstitutional, holding that it disproportionately infringed the right to privacy and freedom of expression.

### Personal Information Protection Act (개인정보 보호법, PIPA)

PIPA, enacted in 2011 and comprehensively amended in 2023, is South Korea's omnibus data protection statute. The 2023 amendment introduced several significant changes:

**Revenue-Based Fines:** Maximum administrative fines increased to 3% of related revenue, aligning more closely with GDPR enforcement levels.

**Automated Decision-Making:** New provisions address the right to explanation and the right to refuse automated decision-making, paralleling GDPR Article 22.

**Cross-Border Transfers:** The amendment introduced adequacy determinations and standard contractual clauses for international data transfers, modeled on EU frameworks.

**Data Portability:** A new right to data portability was established, enabling individuals to obtain and reuse their personal data across services.

### Damages and Remedies

PIPA Article 39 provides for statutory damages of up to 3 million KRW per individual without proof of actual damage, available when the data handler's intentional or grossly negligent violation of PIPA results in a data breach. The Civil Act Article 750 provides the general tort remedy for privacy invasions, requiring proof of fault and damage. Courts have awarded compensation for emotional distress (정신적 손해) arising from privacy violations, with awards typically ranging from 100,000 to 5 million KRW per individual.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States employs a multi-layered privacy framework combining constitutional protections, common law torts, federal sectoral statutes, and increasingly comprehensive state privacy laws.

### Four Privacy Torts

William Prosser's 1960 systematization, adopted in the Restatement (Second) of Torts Section 652, identifies four distinct privacy torts:

| Tort | Elements | Notable Case |
|------|----------|-------------|
| **Intrusion upon seclusion** | Intentional intrusion on solitude/private affairs; highly offensive to reasonable person | Nader v. General Motors Corp., 25 N.Y.2d 560 (1970) |
| **Public disclosure of private facts** | Publicity given to private facts; highly offensive; not of legitimate public concern | Bartnicki v. Vopper, 532 U.S. 514 (2001) |
| **False light** | Publicity placing person in false light; highly offensive; defendant had knowledge/reckless disregard | Time, Inc. v. Hill, 385 U.S. 374 (1967) |
| **Appropriation** | Unauthorized use of name/likeness for commercial advantage | Zacchini v. Scripps-Howard Broadcasting Co., 433 U.S. 562 (1977) |

### Constitutional Privacy

**Fourth Amendment:** The Supreme Court has expanded Fourth Amendment protection to modern technologies. In Carpenter v. United States, 585 U.S. 296 (2018), the Court held that accessing seven days or more of historical cell-site location information constitutes a search requiring a warrant, recognizing that individuals maintain a reasonable expectation of privacy in their physical movements over time.

**Substantive Due Process:** The penumbral right of privacy, established in Griswold v. Connecticut, 381 U.S. 479 (1965), has been the subject of significant recent development. In Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization, 597 U.S. 215 (2022), the Court overruled Roe v. Wade and narrowed the scope of unenumerated constitutional privacy rights.

### State Privacy Legislation

The California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA, 2018), as amended by the California Privacy Rights Act (CPRA, 2020, effective January 2023), provides comprehensive consumer privacy rights including the right to know, delete, and opt-out of sale of personal information. As of 2026, at least 15 states have enacted comprehensive privacy laws, including Virginia (VCDPA), Colorado (CPA), Connecticut (CTDPA), and Texas (TDPSA), though no federal omnibus privacy law has been enacted.

### Illinois BIPA

The Illinois Biometric Information Privacy Act (BIPA, 740 ILCS 14), enacted in 2008, requires informed written consent before collection of biometric identifiers. BIPA has generated massive class action litigation, with notable settlements including Facebook's $650 million settlement in Patel v. Facebook, Inc. (2021) and TikTok's $92 million settlement in In re TikTok, Inc. Consumer Privacy Litigation (2021).

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany has one of the world's most developed privacy protection frameworks, rooted in constitutional personality rights and implemented through comprehensive legislation.

### General Personality Right (Allgemeines Persönlichkeitsrecht)

The Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) derived the general personality right from Articles 1(1) (human dignity) and 2(1) (free development of personality) of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz). This right encompasses several specific dimensions:

**Right to Informational Self-Determination (Recht auf informationelle Selbstbestimmung):** Established in the Census Decision (Volkszählungsurteil, BVerfGE 65, 1, December 15, 1983), this right guarantees the individual's authority to determine the disclosure and use of their personal data. The Federal Constitutional Court held that the free development of personality requires protection against unlimited collection, storage, use, and transmission of personal data.

**Right to Confidentiality and Integrity of IT Systems (Grundrecht auf Gewährleistung der Vertraulichkeit und Integrität informationstechnischer Systeme):** Established in the Federal Trojan Decision (BVerfGE 120, 274, February 27, 2008), this "computer basic right" protects against state infiltration of personal IT systems.

### Tort Protection

Privacy violations are actionable under BGB Section 823(1) through the "other right" (sonstiges Recht) category, which encompasses the general personality right. The BGH has developed a three-sphere theory (Sphärentheorie) distinguishing the intimate sphere (Intimsphäre), private sphere (Privatsphäre), and social sphere (Sozialsphäre), with the level of protection decreasing from intimate to social.

**Caroline von Monaco Decisions:** The BGH and BVerfG addressed media privacy in a series of decisions concerning Princess Caroline of Monaco, establishing that unauthorized photographs taken in clearly private settings violate the personality right even when the subject is a public figure. The ECHR confirmed this approach in Von Hannover v. Germany (No. 2) (2012).

### GDPR Implementation

Germany implements the GDPR through the Bundesdatenschutzgesetz (BDSG), as revised in 2018. Germany's data protection enforcement is decentralized, with the Federal Commissioner for Data Protection (Bundesbeauftragte für den Datenschutz und die Informationsfreiheit, BfDI) and 16 state data protection authorities (Landesdatenschutzbeauftragte) sharing enforcement responsibility.

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

Article 8 of the ECHR guarantees the right to respect for private and family life, home, and correspondence. The Court has developed extensive privacy jurisprudence.

**Von Hannover v. Germany (Application No. 59320/00, Judgment of June 24, 2004):** The Court held that Germany violated Article 8 by failing to protect Princess Caroline of Monaco from paparazzi photographs taken in public but private-nature settings, establishing that Article 8 protection extends beyond the home to activities of a clearly private nature.

**S. and Marper v. United Kingdom (Application No. 30562/04, Grand Chamber Judgment of December 4, 2008):** The Court found the UK's blanket and indiscriminate retention of DNA profiles and fingerprints of persons not convicted of any offense violated Article 8, establishing strict limits on biometric data retention.

**Big Brother Watch and Others v. United Kingdom (Application No. 58170/13, Grand Chamber Judgment of May 25, 2021):** The Court found that GCHQ's bulk interception regime violated Article 8, establishing minimum safeguards for mass surveillance programs including independent authorization, category-based selectors, and independent oversight.

### EU General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)

Regulation (EU) 2016/679, applicable since May 25, 2018, establishes comprehensive data protection rules across the EU and EEA. Key provisions include:

**Article 5 -- Principles:** Lawfulness, fairness, transparency; purpose limitation; data minimisation; accuracy; storage limitation; integrity and confidentiality; accountability.

**Article 17 -- Right to Erasure:** The "right to be forgotten," building on the CJEU's Google Spain SL v. AEPD (C-131/12, 2014) decision, which held that search engine operators must delist results that are inadequate, irrelevant, or excessive in relation to the purposes of processing.

**Article 82 -- Damages:** Any person who suffers material or non-material damage from a GDPR infringement has the right to compensation. The CJEU in Österreichische Post (C-300/21, 2023) confirmed that there is no de minimis threshold for non-material damages.

## Comparative Analysis

### Constitutional Foundations

Privacy protection demonstrates strikingly different constitutional architectures:

**Explicit Constitutional Right (South Korea, Germany):** Both jurisdictions explicitly protect privacy in their constitutions and have developed robust jurisprudence through their constitutional courts. The Korean right to informational self-determination and the German Recht auf informationelle Selbstbestimmung are conceptually parallel.

**Implied/Penumbral Right (United States):** The U.S. Constitution does not explicitly mention privacy. Constitutional privacy protection has been constructed through the Fourth Amendment, due process, and penumbral reasoning, resulting in a more contested and unstable doctrinal foundation.

**Treaty-Based Right (ECHR):** Article 8 provides a broad right to private life, interpreted dynamically by the Court to encompass modern privacy challenges including surveillance, data protection, and media intrusion.

### Comprehensive vs. Sectoral Regulation

The most significant structural difference is between comprehensive and sectoral approaches to privacy regulation:

**Comprehensive (South Korea, Germany, EU):** PIPA, the BDSG/GDPR, and the EU framework apply broadly across sectors, providing a unified set of data protection principles.

**Sectoral (United States):** The U.S. relies on sector-specific statutes (HIPAA for health, COPPA for children, FCRA for credit, FERPA for education) with no federal omnibus privacy law, creating significant gaps in coverage. State privacy laws are partially filling these gaps.

### Tort Remedies

The availability and scope of private tort remedies differ markedly. The U.S. four-tort framework provides the most detailed common law privacy protection. South Korea and Germany rely on general tort provisions applied to privacy contexts. The GDPR provides a standalone damages remedy, but courts across Europe have struggled with the valuation of non-material privacy damages.
