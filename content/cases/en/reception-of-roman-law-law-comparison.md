## Summary

The reception of Roman law -- the process by which Justinian's *Corpus Iuris Civilis* (compiled 529-534 CE) was rediscovered, studied, adapted, and incorporated into the living legal systems of medieval and early modern Europe -- is one of the most consequential developments in legal history. Beginning with the revival of systematic legal scholarship at the University of Bologna in the late eleventh century, Roman law gradually penetrated virtually every European legal system, forming the common intellectual foundation (ius commune) of continental European law and profoundly influencing the development of modern civil law systems from France to Japan to Brazil. The process occurred in several overlapping phases: the glossators (c. 1100-1260), led by Irnerius and Accursius, subjected the Justinianic texts to meticulous textual exegesis and produced the standard Glossa Ordinaria (c. 1230); the commentators or post-glossators (c. 1260-1500), led by Bartolus de Saxoferrato and Baldus de Ubaldis, adapted Roman law principles to contemporary conditions through sophisticated analogical and systematic reasoning; the legal humanists of the sixteenth century (Budé, Alciato, Cujas) applied Renaissance philological methods to recover the historical Roman law beneath the medieval glosses; and the usus modernus pandectarum (modern use of the Pandects) of the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries selectively applied Roman rules as living law alongside local custom and statutory law. In nineteenth-century Germany, the Historical School (Savigny, Puchta) and the Pandektenwissenschaft (Pandectist science) transformed Roman law into a comprehensive system of abstract legal concepts that became the basis of the German Civil Code (BGB, 1900) and influenced civil law codifications worldwide. Meanwhile, England -- alone among major European legal systems -- largely resisted the reception of Roman law, developing instead the distinctive common law tradition based on judicial precedent, writs, and jury trial. This document examines the reception process in comparative perspective, exploring why Roman law was received in some legal cultures and not others, and tracing its influence on East Asian and Islamic legal modernization.

## Ancient Greek and Roman Foundations

### The Original Roman Achievement

Before examining the reception, it is essential to appreciate the magnitude of the original Roman legal achievement. Roman law developed over approximately one thousand years, from the Twelve Tables (c. 450 BCE) through the classical period of jurisprudence (c. 100 BCE-250 CE) to Justinian's compilation (529-534 CE).

The classical Roman jurists -- Gaius, Papinian, Paul, Ulpian, Modestinus -- developed an analytical legal science of remarkable sophistication. Their method was casuistic (proceeding from case to case) rather than systematic, yet through their accumulated opinions (responsa) they articulated legal principles of extraordinary subtlety and generality. The concepts they developed -- obligation (obligatio), contract (contractus), delict (delictum), property (dominium, possessio), succession (hereditas), legal personality (persona) -- became the fundamental categories of Western private law.

### The Corpus Iuris Civilis

Emperor Justinian I (r. 527-565 CE) commissioned the compilation that would transmit Roman legal science to posterity. The *Corpus Iuris Civilis* comprised four parts:

1. **Codex Justinianus** (529, revised 534): A compilation of imperial constitutions (leges) organized by subject matter.
2. **Digesta (Pandectae)** (533): An anthology of excerpts from the writings of the classical jurists, organized into 50 books -- the most important and influential component.
3. **Institutiones** (533): An introductory textbook based on Gaius's second-century *Institutes*, intended for law students.
4. **Novellae** (534-565): New laws (constitutiones) promulgated by Justinian after the codification.

The *Digest* alone contained some 9,000 excerpts from 39 jurists spanning five centuries -- an intellectual achievement comparable in scope and influence to any compilation in world history.

### The Dark Ages and Survival

After the fall of the Western Roman Empire (476 CE), knowledge of the full *Corpus Iuris Civilis* was largely lost in Western Europe, though fragments survived. The *Lex Romana Visigothorum* (Breviary of Alaric, 506 CE) preserved a simplified version of the *Codex Theodosianus* and selected juristic writings for the Romanized population of the Visigothic kingdom. Isidore of Seville (c. 560-636) transmitted a simplified account of Roman legal concepts in his encyclopedic *Etymologiae*. The *Lex Romana Burgundionum* (c. 500 CE) similarly preserved Roman legal elements.

In the Eastern Roman (Byzantine) Empire, the *Corpus Iuris Civilis* remained in force but was progressively supplemented and eventually superseded by Greek-language compilations: the *Basilika* (c. 900 CE), a comprehensive Greek reworking of Justinianic law, and various later epitomes. Byzantine legal scholarship preserved the Roman legal tradition in a living form until the fall of Constantinople (1453).

## East Asian Legal Thought

### The Parallel Chinese Legal Tradition

While the reception of Roman law was a uniquely European phenomenon, it is instructive to compare it with the parallel process of legal diffusion in East Asia. The Tang Code (*Tanglü Shuyi* 唐律疏議, 653 CE), the product of a sophisticated legal tradition entirely independent of Rome, served as the basis for legal development throughout the Sinosphere -- much as the *Corpus Iuris Civilis* served for Europe.

The Tang Code was received and adapted by:

- **Japan**: The *Taihō Code* (701 CE) and *Yōrō Code* (718 CE) were modeled directly on Tang legislation, establishing the ritsuryō (律令) system that governed Japan for centuries.
- **Korea**: The Silla Kingdom (57 BCE-935 CE) and later the Goryeo (918-1392) and Joseon (1392-1897) dynasties adopted Chinese legal models, with the Joseon *Gyeongguk Daejeon* (1485) representing the most comprehensive Korean adaptation.
- **Vietnam**: The Lê Code (*Quốc triều hình luật*, 1428-1483) drew heavily on Tang and Ming Dynasty Chinese legal models.

This East Asian legal diffusion parallels the European reception of Roman law in several respects: a prestigious ancient legal tradition was transmitted through institutional channels (in this case, the examination system and diplomatic exchange rather than universities); local legal traditions were gradually harmonized with the received model while retaining distinctive elements; and the received law provided a common legal vocabulary and conceptual framework across a culturally related region.

### The Meiji Reception of Western Law

Japan's Meiji Restoration (1868) initiated a remarkable process of legal reception in which Japan deliberately adopted Western (primarily European) legal models -- models that were themselves products of the earlier reception of Roman law. The Japanese Civil Code (民法典, Minpō, 1898) was modeled primarily on the German BGB (itself the product of Pandektenwissenschaft), with significant French influence from the Code Napoléon. The criminal code followed the French model; the commercial code drew on German models; and the constitution (Meiji Constitution, 1889) was modeled on the Prussian constitution.

This "reception of a reception" -- Japan receiving European law that was itself based on received Roman law -- demonstrates the extraordinary reach of the Roman legal tradition. Through the Japanese model, Roman-derived legal concepts subsequently influenced the legal development of Korea (under Japanese colonial rule, 1910-1945), Taiwan, and other Asian legal systems.

## Islamic Jurisprudence

### Independent Parallel Development

Islamic jurisprudence (fiqh) developed a comprehensive system of private law -- contracts ('uqud), property (milk), succession (mirath), family law (ahwal shakhsiyyah) -- entirely independently of the Roman tradition. Yet the structural parallels are striking: both systems developed elaborate contract typologies, both distinguished between ownership and possession, both developed sophisticated rules of testamentary succession, and both grappled with the relationship between strict rules and equitable flexibility.

Whether Roman law directly influenced early Islamic jurisprudence has been debated since the early twentieth century. Patricia Crone and others have noted possible channels of transmission through the Roman-era legal practices surviving in the conquered provinces (Syria, Egypt, Iraq). However, the prevailing scholarly consensus, following the work of Noel Coulson and Wael Hallaq, is that while Islamic jurists were aware of Roman provincial practices, the systematic structure of fiqh was an independent development rooted in Quranic principles, prophetic practice (Sunnah), and the creative juristic reasoning (ijtihad) of the early Islamic scholars.

### Ottoman Modernization and the Mecelle

The Ottoman Empire's engagement with European (ultimately Roman-derived) law represents a distinctive form of legal reception. The *Mecelle* (*Majallat al-Ahkam al-'Adliyya*, 1869-1876), drafted by Ahmed Cevdet Pasha, codified Hanafi commercial and civil law in a European-style code of 1,851 articles. While the substantive content was drawn from Islamic jurisprudence, the form -- systematic codification in numbered articles -- was borrowed from European (particularly French) models that were themselves products of the Roman law tradition.

The subsequent adoption of European-style legal codes in the Ottoman successor states -- the Turkish Civil Code (1926, modeled on the Swiss ZGB), the Egyptian Civil Code (1948, drafted by 'Abd al-Razzaq al-Sanhuri with combined Islamic and French influences), and the Iraqi Civil Code (1951, also Sanhuri's work) -- represents a direct reception of Roman-derived legal concepts into Islamic legal systems.

## Medieval European Tradition

### The Revival at Bologna

The rediscovery and systematic study of the *Digest* at Bologna in the late eleventh century marks one of the most significant intellectual events in European history. The traditional founding figure is Irnerius (c. 1050-after 1125), who reportedly "lit the lamp of the law" (lucerna iuris) by beginning systematic lectures on the Justinianic texts. The University of Bologna, Europe's first university, grew directly out of the study of Roman law, and the law faculty remained its intellectual center.

The glossators developed a distinctive method of textual analysis: the glossa (gloss), a marginal or interlinear annotation explaining a word, phrase, or passage in the Justinianic text. Over more than a century of accumulated glossing, the Bolognese scholars produced a massive apparatus of commentary. Accursius (c. 1182-1263) compiled the definitive synthesis in his *Glossa Ordinaria* (c. 1230), which assembled and reconciled the glosses of all major predecessors into a standard commentary. The *Glossa Ordinaria* became so authoritative that the principle arose: "What the gloss does not recognize, the court does not recognize" (quidquid non agnoscit glossa, non agnoscit curia).

### The Commentators and the Ius Commune

The commentators (post-glossators), working primarily in the late thirteenth through fifteenth centuries, transformed Roman law from an object of academic study into a living legal system applicable to contemporary disputes. The decisive figure was Bartolus de Saxoferrato (1313-1357), whose influence was so great that the adage arose: "No one is a jurist who is not a Bartolist" (nemo jurista nisi Bartolista).

Bartolus's method was not mere textual exegesis but systematic reasoning from Roman principles to contemporary problems. He applied Roman law to questions unknown to the Romans -- the legal personality of corporations (universitates), conflict of laws (a field he virtually invented), the legal status of Italian city-states, and the legitimacy of tyranny. Baldus de Ubaldis (1327-1400), Bartolus's most brilliant student, extended this work further, producing a comprehensive body of commentary that addressed virtually every aspect of contemporary public and private law.

The product of the glossators' and commentators' work was the ius commune (common law) of continental Europe -- a body of learned legal doctrine, based primarily on the Roman *Corpus Iuris Civilis* as interpreted by the Bolognese tradition and supplemented by canon law, that served as the common intellectual framework for legal argument throughout the continent. The ius commune was not a code imposed by a sovereign but a shared legal culture transmitted through university education. Graduates of Bologna and its successor law schools carried the ius commune into their home jurisdictions, where it gradually penetrated local legal practice.

### The Humanist Critique

The Renaissance legal humanists of the sixteenth century, particularly in France, challenged the glossators' and commentators' approach. Guillaume Budé (1467-1540) and Andrea Alciato (1492-1550) applied humanist philological methods to the Roman texts, seeking to recover their original historical meaning rather than adapt them to contemporary purposes. Jacques Cujas (1522-1590), the greatest of the French humanists, produced magisterial historical commentaries that revealed the extent to which the glossators had misunderstood or distorted the classical texts.

The humanist movement (mos gallicus, the French method) stood in tension with the continuing practical application of Roman law (mos italicus, the Italian method). The humanists demonstrated that the *Corpus Iuris Civilis* was a historical document, produced in a specific context, and could not simply be treated as a timeless authority. This historicizing approach planted the seeds of the later Historical School of jurisprudence.

## Early Modern and Enlightenment Development

### Usus Modernus Pandectarum

The usus modernus pandectarum (modern use of the Pandects), a term coined by Samuel Stryk (1640-1710) in his work *Usus Modernus Pandectarum* (1690-1712), described the seventeenth- and eighteenth-century practice of selectively applying Roman law rules as living law alongside local customary and statutory law. In the Holy Roman Empire, Roman law had been officially "received" as subsidiary law (supplementing local law where it was silent) by the *Reichskammergerichtsordnung* (Imperial Chamber Court Ordinance) of 1495, which directed the court to decide cases according to "the common laws of the Empire" -- understood as the ius commune.

The usus modernus involved a pragmatic sorting of Roman rules into those still applicable and those that had become obsolete. It also involved creative adaptation: Roman principles were modified to accommodate Germanic customary institutions (such as communal property regimes and the German law of pledges) that had no Roman equivalent.

### The Natural Law Challenge

The seventeenth- and eighteenth-century natural law movement (Grotius, Pufendorf, Thomasius, Wolff) posed a fundamental challenge to the authority of Roman law. The natural lawyers argued that law should be based on rational principles derived from human nature, not on the historical authority of Roman texts. This challenge ultimately contributed to the codification movement: if law should be rationally ordered, why not replace the chaotic mass of Roman, canonical, customary, and statutory law with a comprehensive, rationally organized code?

### Pandektenwissenschaft

The nineteenth-century German Pandektenwissenschaft (science of the Pandects) represented the final and most sophisticated phase of the reception. Friedrich Carl von Savigny (1779-1861), founder of the Historical School of jurisprudence, argued in *Vom Beruf unserer Zeit für Gesetzgebung und Rechtswissenschaft* (Of the Vocation of Our Age for Legislation and Jurisprudence, 1814) that law was the organic product of the spirit of the people (Volksgeist), not the product of legislative will. Savigny advocated against premature codification, arguing instead for the continued development of law through scholarly analysis of the received Roman tradition.

Georg Friedrich Puchta (1798-1846) developed Savigny's approach into a formal "genealogy of concepts" (Begriffsgenealogie), systematizing Roman law into a hierarchy of abstract legal concepts. Bernhard Windscheid (1817-1892), in his monumental *Lehrbuch des Pandektenrechts* (Textbook of Pandect Law, 1862-70), produced the definitive systematic treatment of Roman private law as a living system of abstract principles. Windscheid's work directly influenced the drafting of the German Civil Code (BGB, 1900), whose five-book structure (General Part, Obligations, Property, Family, Succession) reflects the Pandectist system.

Rudolf von Jhering (1818-1892) provided a corrective to Pandectist formalism, arguing in *Der Zweck im Recht* (Law as a Means to an End, 1877-83) that legal concepts must be understood in terms of the social purposes they serve, not merely as abstract logical constructs. Jhering's purposive jurisprudence influenced the "free law" movement and later sociological jurisprudence.

## Common Law Tradition

### Why England Resisted

The English resistance to the reception of Roman law is one of the great puzzles of comparative legal history. Several factors explain why England developed its distinctive common law tradition while the rest of Europe received Roman law:

**Institutional Factors**: By the time the revival at Bologna began (c. 1100), England already possessed a relatively developed system of royal courts (established by Henry I and consolidated by Henry II) with their own forms of action (writs), their own procedural system (jury trial), and their own body of legal professionals trained at the Inns of Court rather than at universities. The common law was sufficiently sophisticated to resist displacement by the received Roman law.

**Professional Interests**: The English legal profession -- barristers and serjeants-at-law trained at the Inns of Court -- had a vested interest in maintaining the common law system in which they held expertise. Roman law was taught at Oxford and Cambridge but was excluded from the practical training at the Inns of Court. Sir Edward Coke (1552-1634) vigorously defended the common law against the encroachments of Roman-derived civil law in the ecclesiastical and admiralty courts.

**Constitutional Factors**: The English common law was closely identified with the liberties of English subjects and the limitation of royal power. The reception of Roman law was associated with absolutism (the maxim "what pleases the prince has the force of law" -- quod principi placuit legis habet vigorem -- from *Digest* 1.4.1) and was resisted as a threat to parliamentary supremacy and the rights of Englishmen.

### Limited Roman Influence

Despite the general resistance, Roman law did influence English law in several ways:

- **Bracton**: Henry de Bracton (c. 1210-1268), in *De Legibus et Consuetudinibus Angliae* (On the Laws and Customs of England), drew extensively on Roman law (particularly the *Institutes* and the work of the glossator Azo) to provide an analytical framework for the common law. Bracton's Romanist borrowings were, however, adapted to fit common law categories.
- **Ecclesiastical Courts**: The English ecclesiastical courts applied canon law, which was itself deeply influenced by Roman law. Matters of marriage, testamentary succession, and defamation fell within ecclesiastical jurisdiction and were governed by Romanist principles.
- **Admiralty**: The Court of Admiralty applied a form of civil (Roman-derived) law to maritime disputes.
- **Equity**: The Court of Chancery drew on canonist and Romanist equitable traditions, though it developed its own distinctive English equitable doctrines.

### Scottish Reception

Scotland, in contrast to England, experienced a substantial reception of Roman law. The "auld alliance" with France exposed Scottish lawyers to continental legal scholarship, and the Scottish court system (particularly the Court of Session, established 1532) applied a blend of Roman law, canon law, and Scottish custom. Scotland's mixed legal system -- combining civil law and common law elements -- survives to this day and represents a distinctive variant of the reception phenomenon.

## Historical Significance and Modern Legacy

The reception of Roman law has shaped the modern legal world in fundamental ways:

**The Civil Law Tradition**: The civil law systems of continental Europe, Latin America, much of Africa and Asia, and Quebec and Louisiana are all products, directly or indirectly, of the reception of Roman law. The conceptual framework of modern civil law -- the distinction between real and personal rights, the typology of contracts, the concept of legal personality, the systematic organization of private law into persons, things, and actions -- derives from the received Roman tradition.

**Legal Education**: The modern law school, with its systematic curriculum organized around abstract legal categories, is a product of the medieval university's engagement with Roman law. The case method developed by Christopher Columbus Langdell at Harvard (1870s), while distinctively common law in flavor, shares the scholastic aspiration to derive general principles from the analysis of particular authorities.

**Legal Science**: The aspiration to treat law as a science -- a systematic body of knowledge organized according to logical principles -- derives from the Pandektenwissenschaft. This aspiration continues to shape legal scholarship in the civil law tradition and has influenced common law legal thought through analytical jurisprudence.

**Comparative Law**: The shared Roman heritage of civil law systems provides the conceptual framework that makes comparative law possible. The functional similarities between civil law and common law institutions -- despite their different historical origins -- often reflect the influence of Roman concepts that permeated both traditions, albeit through different channels.
