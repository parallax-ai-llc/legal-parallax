## Summary

Genocide -- the deliberate destruction of a national, ethnic, racial, or religious group -- is universally condemned under international law, yet its domestic criminalization varies substantially across jurisdictions. South Korea implemented the Rome Statute through its 2007 Act on Punishment of Crimes Under Jurisdiction of the ICC, prescribing penalties up to capital punishment for genocidal killing. The United States enacted the Genocide Convention Implementation Act of 1987 (Proxmire Act) under 18 U.S.C. 1091, but has never secured a direct genocide conviction under the statute due in part to its narrow jurisdictional reach. Germany's Volkerstrafgesetzbuch (VStGB), enacted in 2002, provides for life imprisonment and has been used aggressively under universal jurisdiction principles to prosecute foreign perpetrators, most notably in the landmark Yazidi genocide trial. The European Court of Human Rights does not itself criminalize genocide but has developed significant jurisprudence on states' obligations to prosecute genocide and on the tension between genocide denial laws and freedom of expression.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea ratified the Rome Statute in 2002 and enacted the **Act on Punishment of Crimes Under the Jurisdiction of the International Criminal Court** (ICC Crimes Act) on November 23, 2007. Article 8 of the Act defines and criminalizes genocide, closely mirroring the Rome Statute's definition under Article 6.

The Act criminalizes acts committed with the intent to destroy, in whole or in part, a national, ethnical, racial, or religious group:

| Prohibited Act | Penalty |
|---------------|---------|
| Killing a member of the group | Death penalty, life imprisonment, or minimum 7 years |
| Causing serious bodily or mental harm | Life imprisonment or minimum 5 years |
| Inflicting destructive conditions of life | Life imprisonment or minimum 5 years |
| Imposing measures to prevent births | Life imprisonment or minimum 5 years |
| Forcibly transferring children to another group | Life imprisonment or minimum 5 years |

### Key Provisions

**No statute of limitations:** Genocide crimes are explicitly excluded from the prescription periods for both prosecution and execution of judgment under Article 6 of the Act. This means there is no time limit on bringing charges or enforcing sentences.

**Command responsibility:** Military commanders or civilian superiors who know or should know that subordinates are committing or about to commit genocide are criminally liable if they fail to take necessary and reasonable measures to prevent or suppress the acts. This follows the Rome Statute's command responsibility doctrine.

**Ex officio prosecution:** Genocide cases may be prosecuted without a formal complaint from the victim and even against the expressed wishes of the victim, reflecting the jus cogens nature of the prohibition.

**Death penalty status:** South Korea retains the death penalty in law but has not carried out an execution since December 1997. The country is classified by Amnesty International as a de facto abolitionist state. The availability of capital punishment for genocide thus remains theoretical.

### Practical Application

South Korea has not prosecuted any genocide case domestically. The Act was enacted primarily to satisfy treaty obligations under the Rome Statute. South Korea's geographic and historical context -- particularly the division of the Korean Peninsula -- makes the ICC framework relevant in the context of documented human rights abuses in North Korea. A 2014 UN Commission of Inquiry found that crimes against humanity had been committed in North Korea, though the characterization as genocide remains debated.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States ratified the Genocide Convention in 1988, forty years after its adoption, and implemented it domestically through the **Genocide Convention Implementation Act of 1987**, commonly known as the **Proxmire Act** (named after Senator William Proxmire, who delivered over 3,000 speeches urging ratification). The Act is codified at **18 U.S.C. 1091-1093**.

Section 1091 prohibits genocide whether committed in time of peace or war, defining it as specific acts committed with the intent to destroy, in whole or in substantial part, a national, ethnic, racial, or religious group:

| Prohibited Act | Penalty (if death results) | Penalty (no death) |
|---------------|---------------------------|---------------------|
| Killing members of the group | Death or life imprisonment + up to $1M fine | N/A |
| Causing serious bodily injury | Death or life imprisonment + up to $1M fine | Up to 20 years + up to $1M fine |
| Causing permanent mental impairment through drugs/torture | Death or life imprisonment + up to $1M fine | Up to 20 years + up to $1M fine |
| Subjecting to destructive conditions of life | Death or life imprisonment + up to $1M fine | Up to 20 years + up to $1M fine |
| Imposing measures to prevent births | Death or life imprisonment + up to $1M fine | Up to 20 years + up to $1M fine |
| Forcibly transferring children | Death or life imprisonment + up to $1M fine | Up to 20 years + up to $1M fine |
| Inciting genocide | Up to 5 years + up to $1M fine | Up to 5 years + up to $1M fine |

### Jurisdictional Limitations

A critical feature of the U.S. statute is its **narrow jurisdictional scope**. Originally, prosecution was limited to acts committed within the United States or by U.S. nationals. The **Genocide Accountability Act of 2007** expanded jurisdiction to cover persons who are "brought into, or found in" the United States, even if the genocide was committed abroad by a non-citizen. Despite this expansion, the statute remains significantly narrower than the universal jurisdiction exercised by Germany.

### Notable Cases and Enforcement Gap

The United States has **never secured a direct conviction under 18 U.S.C. 1091**. Instead, the government has used immigration fraud and denaturalization proceedings to address genocide perpetrators found in the U.S.:

**Beatrice Munyenyezi (2013):** A Rwandan national convicted of immigration fraud for concealing her role in the 1994 Rwandan genocide when applying for U.S. citizenship. She was sentenced to 10 years in prison and subsequently denaturalized and deported to Rwanda, where she was convicted of genocide and sentenced to life imprisonment in 2024. Notably, she was never charged under the Proxmire Act itself.

**Elizaphan Ntakirutimana (2000):** A Rwandan pastor residing in Texas was extradited to the International Criminal Tribunal for Rwanda (ICTR) rather than prosecuted domestically. He was convicted of aiding and abetting genocide in 2003 and sentenced to 10 years' imprisonment.

The enforcement gap underscores the practical limitations of the U.S. genocide statute. Prosecutors have consistently preferred immigration-based charges, which carry lower evidentiary burdens than proving specific genocidal intent.

### Genocide Denial

The United States has no federal prohibition on genocide denial. First Amendment protections for freedom of speech have prevented legislative efforts to criminalize denial of recognized genocides, placing the U.S. in stark contrast with European approaches.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany enacted the **Volkerstrafgesetzbuch** (Code of Crimes Against International Law, VStGB) on June 30, 2002, to bring domestic law into conformity with the Rome Statute. **Section 6 VStGB** defines genocide and prescribes penalties:

Genocide is committed when a person, with the intent to destroy as such, in whole or in part, a national, racial, religious, or ethnic group:

1. **Kills a member of the group** -- Life imprisonment
2. **Causes serious bodily or mental harm** (especially as defined in Section 226 StGB) -- Life imprisonment; minimum 5 years in less serious cases
3. **Inflicts conditions of life calculated to bring about physical destruction** -- Life imprisonment; minimum 5 years in less serious cases
4. **Imposes measures intended to prevent births** -- Life imprisonment; minimum 5 years in less serious cases
5. **Forcibly transfers a child to another group** -- Life imprisonment; minimum 5 years in less serious cases

### Universal Jurisdiction

Germany exercises **full universal jurisdiction** over genocide under Section 1 VStGB, meaning German courts may prosecute genocide regardless of where it was committed, the nationality of the perpetrator, or the nationality of the victims. The Federal Prosecutor General (Generalbundesanwalt) has used this authority more aggressively than any other country.

### Landmark Cases

**Nikola Jorgic (1997/2007):** A Bosnian Serb convicted by the Dusseldorf Higher Regional Court of genocide committed during the Bosnian War. He led a paramilitary group that terrorized Muslim civilians in the Doboj region. His conviction was upheld by the Federal Constitutional Court and, in *Jorgic v. Germany* (2007), by the European Court of Human Rights, which confirmed that Germany's exercise of universal jurisdiction did not violate Articles 5, 6, or 7 of the ECHR. This case established an important precedent for universal jurisdiction over genocide.

**Onesphore R. (2021):** A Rwandan national convicted by the Frankfurt Higher Regional Court as a co-perpetrator of genocide committed at the church of Kiziguro, Rwanda, in April 1994. He was found guilty of aiding in the killing of over 400 Tutsi civilians who had sought refuge in the church.

**Taha Al-Jumailly (2021):** An Iraqi national convicted by the Frankfurt Higher Regional Court of genocide against the Yazidi people, crimes against humanity, war crimes, and human trafficking. The court found him guilty of involvement in the enslavement and abuse of Yazidi women and girls by ISIS in 2014-2015. He was sentenced to life imprisonment and ordered to pay approximately EUR 50,000 in compensation. This was a landmark case as the **first-ever conviction worldwide for genocide against the Yazidis**.

### Genocide Denial

Under **Section 130(3) StGB** (Volksverhetzung / incitement to hatred), publicly approving, denying, or downplaying genocide, crimes against humanity, or war crimes committed under National Socialist rule is punishable by up to 5 years' imprisonment. A 2022 amendment broadened the provision to cover denial or gross trivialization of other internationally recognized genocides when done in a manner likely to incite hatred or disturb public peace.

### 2024 VStGB Reform

In October 2024, a significant reform of the VStGB entered into force, expanding the scope of international criminal law in Germany. The reform introduced additional provisions on sexual and gender-based violence as forms of genocide and crimes against humanity, further strengthening Germany's already comprehensive framework.

## ECHR & EU

### European Convention on Human Rights

The ECHR does not itself define or criminalize genocide -- it is a human rights treaty, not a criminal code. However, the European Court of Human Rights has developed significant jurisprudence that intersects with genocide law through several Convention articles:

**Article 2 (Right to Life):** Imposes positive obligations on states to protect life. In the context of genocide, this requires states to take preventive measures when they know or ought to know of a real and immediate risk of mass atrocity.

**Article 3 (Prohibition of Torture):** Many acts constituting genocide simultaneously violate Article 3, providing an additional basis for state accountability.

**Article 7 (No Punishment Without Law):** Central to challenges against genocide prosecutions. The Court examines whether the criminalization of conduct as genocide was foreseeable at the time it was committed, applying the principle of legality (nullum crimen sine lege).

### Landmark ECHR Cases

**Jorgic v. Germany (2007):** The Court upheld Germany's exercise of universal jurisdiction over genocide committed in Bosnia, finding no violation of Articles 5, 6, or 7 ECHR. The Court confirmed that the erga omnes obligation to punish genocide permitted states to exercise universal jurisdiction regardless of the nationality of the accused. The Court also endorsed a broader interpretation of "intent to destroy," accepting that destruction of a group need not mean exclusively physical or biological destruction.

**Perincek v. Switzerland (2015, Grand Chamber):** Dogu Perincek, a Turkish politician, was convicted by Swiss courts for publicly denying the Armenian genocide. The Grand Chamber ruled 10-7 that Switzerland violated Article 10 (freedom of expression), finding the conviction was not "necessary in a democratic society." Key reasoning included: (a) Perincek's statements, while offensive, did not constitute a call to hatred or violence; (b) there was no direct link between Switzerland and the events of 1915; (c) the Court distinguished Holocaust denial -- which may be lawfully criminalized given its direct connection to anti-democratic ideology -- from denial of other genocides. This decision created significant controversy for its perceived double standard between the Holocaust and other recognized genocides.

**Vasiliauskas v. Lithuania (2015, Grand Chamber):** The Court found a violation of Article 7 when Lithuania convicted the applicant of genocide of Lithuanian partisans, applying a domestic definition of genocide that included political and social groups -- broader than the international law definition. The Court held that the applicant could not have foreseen in 1953 that killing members of a political group would constitute genocide under Lithuanian law enacted decades later.

### EU Framework Decision 2008/913/JHA

The **Council Framework Decision of 28 November 2008 on combating certain forms and expressions of racism and xenophobia by means of criminal law** is the primary EU instrument addressing genocide denial. It requires all EU member states to criminalize:

- Public condoning, denial, or gross trivialization of crimes of genocide, crimes against humanity, and war crimes as defined in Articles 6, 7, and 8 of the Rome Statute;
- When directed against a group defined by race, colour, religion, descent, or national or ethnic origin;
- When carried out in a manner likely to incite violence or hatred.

**Minimum penalties:** Member states must ensure offenses are punishable by a maximum penalty of at least 1-3 years of imprisonment.

**Implementation challenges:** As of the European Commission's 2014 implementation report, multiple EU member states had not fully or correctly transposed the Framework Decision, particularly regarding genocide denial provisions. Some states apply the provision only to the Holocaust, while others extend it to all internationally recognized genocides.

### EU Charter of Fundamental Rights

Article 1 (human dignity), Article 2 (right to life), and Article 4 (prohibition of torture) of the EU Charter echo the ECHR provisions and provide additional grounds within EU law for addressing genocide-related conduct.

## Comparative Analysis

### Definition and Intent

All four jurisdictions follow the Genocide Convention's core definition requiring **specific intent** (dolus specialis) to destroy a protected group in whole or in part. However, they diverge on the interpretation of "destroy":

- **Germany** has applied a broader interpretation, accepting that "destruction" includes measures aimed at eliminating the group's social and cultural identity, not solely physical destruction. This was endorsed by the ECHR in *Jorgic*.
- **The United States** requires intent to destroy a "substantial part" of the group, a threshold derived from the legislative history of the Proxmire Act.
- **South Korea** closely mirrors the Rome Statute definition without significant judicial elaboration.

### Jurisdictional Reach

The most significant divergence lies in jurisdictional scope:

- **Germany** exercises full universal jurisdiction, requiring no territorial or nationality nexus.
- **South Korea** provides for universal jurisdiction under its ICC Crimes Act.
- **The United States** initially limited jurisdiction to acts committed on U.S. territory or by U.S. nationals; the 2007 Genocide Accountability Act expanded this but still requires the suspect to be physically present in the U.S.
- **ECHR/EU** framework does not prescribe jurisdictional rules but has endorsed universal jurisdiction as compatible with Convention rights.

### Enforcement Effectiveness

Germany stands alone in having secured multiple genocide convictions through domestic prosecution. The United States, despite having a comprehensive statute, has never used it to obtain a conviction, relying instead on immigration enforcement. South Korea has no domestic genocide prosecutions. The ECHR framework operates as a check on state action rather than a direct prosecution mechanism.

### Genocide Denial

This area reveals the sharpest policy divergence:

- **Germany and the EU** criminalize genocide denial.
- **The United States** protects denial as free speech under the First Amendment.
- **The ECHR** permits criminalization of Holocaust denial but has found that blanket criminalization of denial of other genocides may violate Article 10 (*Perincek*).
- **South Korea** has no specific genocide denial provision.
