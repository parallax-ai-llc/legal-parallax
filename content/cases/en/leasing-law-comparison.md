## Summary

Leasing law -- the body of rules governing agreements by which one party (the lessor) grants another party (the lessee) the right to use an asset for a specified period in exchange for periodic payments -- encompasses both commercial and consumer leasing and has been profoundly reshaped in recent years by international accounting standards convergence, particularly IFRS 16 and ASC 842, which have fundamentally altered the balance sheet treatment of lease obligations. South Korea regulates leasing through a combination of the Korean Civil Code (민법, Articles 618-654 covering the Imdae-cha contract), the Korean Commercial Code (상법), the Specialized Credit Finance Business Act (여신전문금융업법, SCFBA), and the Act on Consumer Protection in Electronic Commerce (전자상거래 등에서의 소비자보호에 관한 법률), with the Financial Services Commission (FSC) and Fair Trade Commission (KFTC) sharing regulatory oversight. The United States governs leasing through Article 2A of the Uniform Commercial Code (U.C.C. §§ 2A-101 to 2A-532), which provides a comprehensive framework for the leasing of goods, supplemented by the Consumer Leasing Act (15 U.S.C. §§ 1667-1667f) and Regulation M (12 C.F.R. Part 213) for consumer leases, while the federal tax treatment under the Internal Revenue Code and the accounting treatment under ASC 842 (effective for public companies from January 1, 2019) have dramatically affected lease structuring. Germany treats the Leasingvertrag as an atypical contract (atypischer Vertrag) not expressly regulated by the BGB, instead applying provisions of the Mietvertrag (rental contract, BGB §§ 535-580a) by analogy, with consumer leasing regulated by the Verbraucherdarlehensrecht (consumer credit law, BGB §§ 491-515) as transposed from EU Consumer Credit Directives, while the ECHR and EU framework addresses leasing through consumer protection directives, the Consumer Credit Directive 2008/48/EC (and its recast CCD II, Directive (EU) 2023/2225), and the property protection guarantees of Article 1, Protocol No. 1 to the ECHR.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Korean leasing law operates through a dual framework: general lease provisions under the Civil Code and specialized regulation for finance leases under the SCFBA.

**Civil Code Lease (Imdae-cha, 임대차):** Articles 618-654 of the Civil Code establish the basic lease framework. An Imdae-cha is a contract by which one party (임대인, lessor) provides the use and benefit of a thing to another party (임차인, lessee) in return for rent (차임, Article 618).

**Key Civil Code Provisions:**

| Article | Content |
|---------|---------|
| Art. 618 | Definition of lease contract |
| Art. 619 | Maximum lease term (20 years; renewable) |
| Art. 623 | Lessor's duty to maintain the leased property |
| Art. 624 | Lessee's obligation to use the property for its intended purpose |
| Art. 625 | Lessor's right to terminate for breach |
| Art. 626 | Notice period for termination of indefinite-term leases |
| Art. 628 | Lessee's obligation to return the property |
| Art. 640 | Lessee's liability for damage beyond normal wear |

### Finance Leasing (시설대여)

**Specialized Credit Finance Business Act (여신전문금융업법, SCFBA):** The SCFBA, enacted in 1997 and substantially amended in 2009, provides the regulatory framework for finance leasing companies (시설대여업자).

**Article 2, Paragraph 3:** Defines "facility leasing" (시설대여) as a transaction in which a leasing company acquires a facility designated by the lessee and makes it available to the lessee for use during a specified period, in return for periodic rental payments. This definition captures the essential elements of a finance lease.

**Licensing Requirements:** Under Article 3 of the SCFBA, a person engaging in the facility leasing business must register with the FSC. The registration requirements include:
- Minimum capital of 20 billion KRW
- Adequate risk management systems
- Fitness and propriety of major shareholders and executives

**Types of Finance Leases in Korean Practice:**

| Type | Korean Term | Characteristics |
|------|-----------|----------------|
| Finance lease | 금융리스 | Transfer of substantially all risks and rewards; lessee has purchase option |
| Operating lease | 운용리스 | Lessor retains risks; shorter term; return at end |
| Sale and leaseback | 환매조건부리스 | Lessee sells asset to lessor, then leases it back |
| Leveraged lease | 차입매입리스 | Lessor finances acquisition with third-party debt |

### Consumer Leasing Protection

**Fair Trade Commission (KFTC) Standard Terms Review:** The KFTC reviews and regulates standard lease terms under the Regulation of Standardized Contracts Act (약관의 규제에 관한 법률). The KFTC has issued corrective orders requiring modification of unfair lease terms, particularly regarding:
- Early termination penalties exceeding actual damages
- Automatic renewal clauses without adequate notice
- Disclaimers of warranty for leased goods

**Supreme Court Decision 2012다64253 (2013):** The Court held that a finance lease agreement was subject to the Standardized Contracts Act and struck down an early termination penalty clause as unfair under Article 6, finding that the penalty exceeded the lessor's actual loss and was unreasonably burdensome to the consumer lessee.

### Accounting Treatment: K-IFRS 1116

South Korea adopted K-IFRS 1116 (Korean International Financial Reporting Standards, aligned with IFRS 16) effective January 1, 2019, for all K-IFRS reporting entities. Under K-IFRS 1116:

- Lessees must recognize a right-of-use asset and a corresponding lease liability on the balance sheet for virtually all leases exceeding 12 months
- The distinction between finance and operating leases is eliminated for lessee accounting (but retained for lessors)
- Short-term leases (12 months or less) and leases of low-value assets may be exempted
- This standard significantly increased the reported liabilities of Korean companies, particularly in retail, aviation, and shipping sectors

### Tax Treatment

The National Tax Service (NTS) and FSC guidelines determine the tax classification of leases based on economic substance:

**Finance Lease (Tax):** A lease is classified as a finance lease for tax purposes if:
- The lease term exceeds 75 percent of the asset's useful life, or
- The present value of minimum lease payments exceeds 90 percent of the asset's fair value, or
- The lease contains a bargain purchase option
- Tax treatment: Lessee claims depreciation; interest element of payments is deductible

**Operating Lease (Tax):** All other leases. Tax treatment: Lessee deducts full lease payments as operating expenses; lessor claims depreciation.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States provides the most comprehensive statutory framework for goods leasing through U.C.C. Article 2A.

**U.C.C. Article 2A (Leases):** Promulgated in 1987 and revised in 1990, Article 2A has been adopted in all 50 states and the District of Columbia. It provides a comprehensive framework modeled on Article 2 (Sales) but adapted for lease transactions.

**Key Article 2A Provisions:**

| Section | Content |
|---------|---------|
| § 2A-103 | Definitions, including "finance lease," "consumer lease" |
| § 2A-201 | Statute of frauds (leases with payments of $1,000 or more) |
| § 2A-209 | Lessee under finance lease as beneficiary of supply contract |
| § 2A-212/213 | Implied warranty of merchantability and fitness |
| § 2A-303 | Alienability of interests |
| § 2A-407 | Irrevocability of finance lease; "hell or high water" clause |
| § 2A-508/509 | Lessee's remedies for default |
| § 2A-525 | Lessor's remedies for default |
| § 2A-532 | Lessor's rights to residual interest |

### Finance Lease Definition

**U.C.C. § 2A-103(1)(g):** A "finance lease" is defined as a lease in which:
1. The lessor does not select, manufacture, or supply the goods
2. The lessor acquires the goods in connection with the lease
3. The lessee receives a copy of the supply contract or specified information before signing the lease

**"Hell or High Water" Clause (§ 2A-407):** In a finance lease that is not a consumer lease, the lessee's promises become irrevocable and independent upon acceptance of the goods. This means the lessee must continue making payments regardless of defects in the goods, with recourse only against the supplier -- not the lessor.

### Consumer Leasing Act

**Consumer Leasing Act (15 U.S.C. §§ 1667-1667f):** Enacted as Title V of the Truth in Lending Act, the Consumer Leasing Act applies to leases of personal property to natural persons for personal, family, or household purposes, where the total contractual obligation does not exceed $50,000 (adjusted for inflation; currently approximately $69,500).

**Regulation M (12 C.F.R. Part 213):** Implements the Consumer Leasing Act, requiring lessors to disclose:
- Total amount due at lease signing or delivery
- Number, amount, and due dates of periodic payments
- Total of base periodic payments
- Charge for delinquent payment
- Description of lessee's liability at end of lease term
- Purchase option terms
- Statement of warranties available
- Early termination charges

### True Lease vs. Security Interest Distinction

The distinction between a "true lease" and a lease intended as a security interest (disguised sale) is critical in U.S. law:

**U.C.C. § 1-203 (Revised Article 1):** A transaction creates a security interest (not a true lease) if:
1. The lease is not subject to termination by the lessee, **and**
2. One of the following applies:
   - Original term equals or exceeds the remaining economic life of the goods
   - Lessee is bound to renew for the remaining economic life
   - Lessee has an option to become owner for no additional consideration or nominal consideration
   - Lessee has an option to renew for the remaining economic life for no additional or nominal consideration

**Significance:** If the transaction is recharacterized as a security interest:
- U.C.C. Article 9 (Secured Transactions) applies instead of Article 2A
- The "lessor" must perfect its security interest by filing a financing statement
- The "lessee's" property interest survives bankruptcy
- The "hell or high water" rule does not apply

### Accounting: ASC 842

**FASB Accounting Standards Codification Topic 842 (Leases):** Effective for public companies from January 1, 2019, and private companies from January 1, 2022, ASC 842 requires:

- Lessees must recognize a right-of-use asset and lease liability for virtually all leases (with exemptions for short-term leases of 12 months or less)
- Two categories for lessee accounting: **finance leases** (amortization of ROU asset + interest on liability) and **operating leases** (single lease expense on a straight-line basis)
- Lessor accounting remains substantially unchanged from prior standards (ASC 840)

**Impact:** ASC 842 brought approximately $2.8 trillion in previously off-balance-sheet lease obligations onto corporate balance sheets, fundamentally changing financial analysis for industries with significant lease portfolios (airlines, retail, real estate).

### Bankruptcy Treatment

**Bankruptcy Code § 365:** The treatment of leases in bankruptcy is one of the most litigated areas of U.S. commercial law:

- **Unexpired Leases (§ 365(a)):** The debtor-in-possession or trustee may assume or reject any unexpired lease
- **Assumption Requirements (§ 365(b)):** To assume a lease, the debtor must cure defaults, compensate for actual pecuniary loss, and provide adequate assurance of future performance
- **Rejection (§ 365(d)):** Rejected leases create breach of contract claims, which are general unsecured claims
- **Commercial Real Property (§ 365(d)(4)):** Nonresidential real property leases must be assumed within 120 days of the order for relief (with one 90-day extension)

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany does not have a dedicated leasing statute. The Leasingvertrag is classified as an atypical contract (atypischer Vertrag or Vertrag sui generis) whose legal treatment depends on its economic substance.

**BGH, November 9, 2005 (VIII ZR 339/04):** The BGH characterized the finance lease (Finanzierungsleasingvertrag) as an atypical contract combining elements of rental (Miete), purchase (Kauf), and credit (Darlehen), to which the provisions of the BGB apply by analogy depending on the specific legal question at issue.

### Classification of Lease Types

| Type | German Term | Legal Treatment |
|------|-----------|----------------|
| Operating lease | Operate-Leasing / Mietleasing | Treated primarily as Mietvertrag (BGB §§ 535 ff.) |
| Finance lease (full payout) | Vollamortisationsleasing | Atypical contract; Mietrecht by analogy with modifications |
| Finance lease (partial payout) | Teilamortisationsleasing | Atypical contract; residual value risk allocation critical |
| Sale and leaseback | Sale-and-lease-back | Recognized; subject to Sittenwidrigkeit review (§ 138 BGB) |
| Consumer lease | Verbraucherleasing | Subject to Verbraucherdarlehensrecht (§§ 491 ff. BGB) |

### Finance Leasing: Key Principles

**Risk Allocation:** The defining feature of a German finance lease is the full or partial transfer of investment risk from the lessor to the lessee. The BGH has established that in a finance lease:

- The lessor's duty to maintain the leased property (Instandhaltungspflicht, § 535 Abs. 1 S. 2 BGB) is typically excluded
- The lessee bears the risk of accidental destruction or deterioration (Sachgefahr) of the leased asset
- The lessee is responsible for maintenance, repair, and insurance
- The lessor's warranty obligations (Gewährleistung) are typically assigned to the lessee, who may pursue warranty claims directly against the supplier (Abwälzung der Gewährleistungsansprüche)

**BGH, December 15, 2010 (VIII ZR 86/10):** The Court confirmed that the assignment of the lessor's warranty claims against the supplier to the lessee (Abtretungskonstruktion) is a permissible and standard feature of finance leases, provided the lessee receives effective recourse against the supplier.

### Consumer Leasing Protection

**Verbraucherdarlehensrecht (§§ 491-515 BGB):** Since the implementation of the EU Consumer Credit Directive, German consumer leasing is subject to the consumer credit provisions of the BGB where the lease has financing character:

**§ 506 BGB (Finanzierungshilfen):** Applies consumer credit protections to:
- Installment delivery contracts (Teillieferungsverträge)
- Finance lease agreements where the lessee is a consumer
- The provisions apply regardless of whether the transaction is denominated as a "lease" or "rental"

**Key Consumer Protections:**
- **14-day withdrawal right (Widerrufsrecht, § 495 BGB):** Consumer lessees have a 14-day right of withdrawal from finance lease agreements
- **Pre-contractual information requirements (Art. 247 EGBGB):** Lessors must provide standardized information including the effective annual percentage rate, total amount payable, and early termination conditions
- **Early termination (§ 500 BGB):** Consumer lessees have the right to terminate the lease early, subject to compensation not exceeding the lessor's actual loss

### Tax Classification: BMF Leasing-Erlass

The Federal Ministry of Finance (Bundesministerium der Finanzen, BMF) Leasing-Erlass (leasing decrees) of 1971 (movable assets) and 1972 (immovable assets), as amended, establish the criteria for tax classification of leases:

**Economic Ownership (Wirtschaftliches Eigentum, § 39 Abs. 2 AO):** The tax treatment depends on which party -- lessor or lessee -- is the economic owner of the leased asset:

| Criterion | Assigned to Lessor | Assigned to Lessee |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Lease term relative to useful life | 40-90% of useful life | >90% or <40% with special circumstances |
| Purchase option price | At or above fair market value | Below book value or fair market value |
| Specialized asset | Non-specialized | Specifically designed for lessee's use only |
| Residual value guarantee | None or partial | Full residual value guarantee by lessee |

**Tax Consequences:**
- **Lessor is economic owner:** Lessor claims depreciation; lessee deducts full lease payments as Betriebsausgaben (business expenses)
- **Lessee is economic owner:** Lessee claims depreciation; lease payments split into interest and principal components

### Insolvency Treatment

**Insolvenzordnung (InsO):** The treatment of leases in German insolvency proceedings:

**§ 103 InsO (Election Right):** The insolvency administrator (Insolvenzverwalter) has the right to elect whether to perform or reject bilateral contracts not yet fully performed. For ongoing leases:
- If the administrator elects to perform: the lease continues; lease payments are administrative expenses (Masseverbindlichkeiten) entitled to priority
- If the administrator elects to reject: the lessor has a pre-petition claim for damages

**§ 108 InsO (Continuation of Certain Contracts):** Leases of real property (Mietverhältnisse über Grundstücke) continue in insolvency with effect for both sides. The administrator may terminate with the statutory notice period (§ 109 InsO).

**BGH, May 16, 2013 (IX ZR 198/12):** The Court held that the insolvency administrator's right of election under § 103 InsO applies to finance lease agreements, and that the administrator may choose to continue the lease if it benefits the insolvency estate.

## ECHR / EU

### EU Consumer Credit Framework

**Consumer Credit Directive (CCD, Directive 2008/48/EC):** The original CCD applied to credit agreements between EUR 200 and EUR 75,000, including leasing agreements that involve a purchase obligation. However, its scope regarding leases without a purchase obligation was limited.

**Consumer Credit Directive II (CCD II, Directive (EU) 2023/2225):** The recast CCD II, adopted on October 18, 2023, with member state transposition due by November 20, 2025, significantly expands the regulatory framework for consumer leasing:

- **Scope (Article 2):** Applies to credit agreements, including leasing agreements where the lease "provides for an option to purchase" or where the total amount payable under the lease exceeds the cash price of the goods
- **Pre-contractual Information (Article 10):** Standardized European Consumer Credit Information (SECCI) form required
- **Annual Percentage Rate (APR):** Mandatory disclosure using a harmonized calculation method
- **Creditworthiness Assessment (Article 18):** Lessors must assess the consumer's creditworthiness before concluding a lease agreement
- **Right of Withdrawal (Article 26):** 14-day withdrawal period for consumer lease agreements

### Unfair Contract Terms Directive

**Directive 93/13/EEC (Unfair Contract Terms Directive):** Standard lease terms in consumer contracts are subject to the fairness test under this Directive:

- Terms must be drafted in plain, intelligible language (Article 5)
- Terms causing a significant imbalance in the parties' rights and obligations to the detriment of the consumer are unfair and not binding (Article 3)
- Core terms (description of subject matter, price/remuneration) are exempt from fairness review only if drafted in plain, intelligible language (Article 4(2))

**CJEU, VB Penzugyi Lizing Zrt v. Ferenc Schneider (C-137/08, 2010):** The CJEU held that national courts must assess the unfairness of contract terms of their own motion (ex officio), including lease terms, even if the consumer has not raised the issue.

**CJEU, Constructora Principado SA v. Jose Ignacio Menendez Alvarez (C-226/12, 2014):** The Court addressed the transparency of contract terms in leasing agreements, holding that the transparency requirement under Article 4(2) extends beyond formal transparency to require that terms be comprehensible in their legal and economic effects.

### IFRS 16

**IFRS 16 (Leases):** Issued by the International Accounting Standards Board (IASB) in January 2016 and effective from January 1, 2019, IFRS 16 is mandatory for all companies reporting under IFRS in the EU (listed companies under Regulation (EC) No 1606/2002):

**Key Principles:**
- Single lessee accounting model: All leases result in the recognition of a right-of-use asset and a lease liability
- Eliminates the distinction between operating and finance leases for lessees
- Lessor accounting retained from IAS 17 (distinction between operating and finance leases maintained)
- Short-term leases (12 months or less) and low-value asset leases may be exempted

**European impact:** IFRS 16 adoption brought an estimated EUR 1.5 trillion in previously off-balance-sheet lease obligations onto the balance sheets of European listed companies. Industries most affected include airlines, retail, telecommunications, and hospitality.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Article 1, Protocol No. 1 (Protection of Property):** Lease interests -- both the lessor's proprietary interest in the leased asset and the lessee's contractual right to use and enjoy the asset -- constitute "possessions" protected by the Convention.

**Hutten-Czapska v. Poland (Application No. 35014/97, ECtHR Grand Chamber Judgment of June 19, 2006):** The Grand Chamber found that Polish rent control legislation constituted a systemic violation of Article 1 of Protocol No. 1. The Court held that while states have a wide margin of appreciation in regulating lease relationships in the general interest, the imposed restrictions must maintain a fair balance between the general interest and the protection of the landlord's property rights.

**Lindheim and Others v. Norway (Application No. 13221/08, ECtHR Judgment of June 12, 2012):** The Court found a violation of Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 in ground lease legislation that allowed lessees to extend leases indefinitely on the same terms, holding that the legislation failed to strike a fair balance between the competing interests of lessors and lessees.

**Statileo v. Croatia (Application No. 12027/10, ECtHR Judgment of April 10, 2014):** The Court found a violation where rent control regulations imposed a disproportionate burden on landlords, confirming the principle established in Hutten-Czapska.

## Comparative Analysis

### Finance Lease vs. Operating Lease: Declining Distinction

The distinction between finance and operating leases, historically central to lease classification and accounting treatment, is converging across jurisdictions:

- **Accounting:** Both IFRS 16 (applicable in Korea and the EU) and ASC 842 (U.S.) have eliminated the operating/finance lease distinction for lessee balance sheet recognition, though they retain it for income statement presentation and lessor accounting
- **Tax:** All jurisdictions maintain the distinction for tax purposes, as it determines which party claims depreciation and how lease payments are deducted
- **Legal:** The distinction remains critical for determining applicable law (e.g., German Mietrecht vs. atypical contract treatment; U.C.C. Article 2A finance lease rules)

### Consumer Protection

Consumer leasing protection has converged significantly, driven by the EU Consumer Credit Directive framework:

| Protection | South Korea | United States | Germany | EU Framework |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|---------|-------------|
| Disclosure requirements | KFTC standard terms; SCFBA | Consumer Leasing Act; Regulation M | Art. 247 EGBGB; § 491a BGB | CCD II Art. 10 |
| Withdrawal right | 14 days (E-Commerce Act) | Varies by state | 14 days (§ 495 BGB) | 14 days (CCD II Art. 26) |
| Early termination | Damages-based compensation | Varies; accelerated rent possible | § 500 BGB (capped compensation) | CCD II Art. 29 |
| APR disclosure | Required | Required (Regulation M) | Required (§ 491a BGB) | Required (CCD II Art. 10) |
| Unfair terms review | Standardized Contracts Act | Unconscionability (U.C.C. § 2A-108) | §§ 305-310 BGB (AGB-Kontrolle) | Directive 93/13/EEC |

### Hell or High Water Clauses

The enforceability of "hell or high water" clauses -- by which the lessee's obligation to pay rent is unconditional and independent of defects in the goods -- varies:

- **South Korea:** Recognized in commercial finance leases; subject to KFTC fairness review in consumer transactions
- **United States:** Codified in U.C.C. § 2A-407 for non-consumer finance leases; excluded for consumer leases
- **Germany:** Recognized through the Abtretungskonstruktion (assignment of warranty claims to lessee), effectively creating a hell or high water obligation with recourse against the supplier
- **EU:** Not harmonized; national law applies, subject to unfair terms review under Directive 93/13/EEC for consumer leases

### Insolvency Treatment

The treatment of leases in insolvency reveals significant divergence:

- **South Korea:** The Debtor Rehabilitation and Bankruptcy Act provides for assumption or rejection of leases, similar to U.S. Bankruptcy Code § 365
- **United States:** Bankruptcy Code § 365 gives the trustee/DIP broad powers to assume or reject unexpired leases, with specific rules for nonresidential real property
- **Germany:** InsO § 103 gives the insolvency administrator an election right, with special continuation rules for real property leases (§ 108)
- The treatment of finance leases in insolvency remains particularly complex, as courts must determine whether the lease is a "true lease" or a secured transaction

### Cross-Border Leasing

Cross-border leasing structures present unique legal challenges:

| Issue | Legal Framework |
|-------|----------------|
| Applicable law | Rome I Regulation (EU); choice of law provisions in U.C.C. Article 2A |
| Tax arbitrage | OECD Base Erosion and Profit Shifting (BEPS) Action 2 addresses hybrid mismatch arrangements |
| Regulatory arbitrage | Divergent classification standards create opportunities for tax-efficient structuring |
| IFRS 16 / ASC 842 divergence | Differences in short-term lease thresholds and low-value asset exemptions |
