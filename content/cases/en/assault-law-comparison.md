## Summary

Assault and battery — the unlawful application of force or the threat thereof against another person — represent some of the most commonly prosecuted criminal offenses worldwide, yet their definitions, grading, and penalties diverge sharply across jurisdictions. South Korea's Criminal Act distinguishes between "violence" (polkhaeng, Article 260) and "bodily injury" (sanghae, Article 257), with the former encompassing acts that need not cause physical harm. The United States fragments assault law across 50 state jurisdictions, with some maintaining the common-law distinction between assault (threat) and battery (contact) and others merging both under a single "assault" statute. Germany's Strafgesetzbuch employs a layered system of Koerperverletzung offenses from simple bodily harm (Section 223) through grievous bodily harm (Section 226) to bodily harm resulting in death (Section 227). The European Court of Human Rights addresses assault primarily through the absolute prohibition on torture and inhuman or degrading treatment under Article 3 of the Convention, imposing both negative and positive obligations on member states.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

The Korean Criminal Act (hyeongbeop) addresses assault-related offenses in two distinct categories: crimes of violence (polkhaeng-ui joe) under Articles 260-262 and crimes of injury (sanghae-ui joe) under Articles 257-259.

**Article 260 — Crime of Violence (Simple Assault):** A person who commits an act of violence against another shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than 2 years, a fine not exceeding 5 million won, detention, or a minor fine. Critically, this offense does not require physical contact — throwing an object near someone, approaching aggressively while waving fists, or spitting toward a person can all constitute violence under Korean law.

**Article 257 — Inflicting Bodily Injury:** A person who inflicts bodily injury upon another shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than 7 years, suspension of qualifications for not more than 10 years, or a fine not exceeding 10 million won. Injury is defined by visible or diagnosable harm such as bruises, fractures, loss of consciousness, or loss of hair.

| Offense | Provision | Penalty |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Simple violence | Art. 260(1) | Up to 2 years / 5 million won fine |
| Violence against lineal ascendant | Art. 260(2) | Up to 5 years / 10 million won fine |
| Special violence (group/weapon) | Art. 261 | Up to 5 years imprisonment |
| Bodily injury | Art. 257(1) | Up to 7 years / 10 million won fine |
| Bodily injury to lineal ascendant | Art. 257(2) | Up to 10 years / 15 million won fine |
| Aggravated bodily injury | Art. 258 | 1-10 years imprisonment |
| Death resulting from injury | Art. 259 | 3 years to life imprisonment |

### Key Principles

**Complainant-dependent prosecution (chingojooe):** Simple assault under Article 260 is an "offense unpunishable over objection," meaning the case cannot be prosecuted if the victim does not wish to press charges or withdraws the complaint. This procedural feature makes settlement (hapui) a central element of Korean assault defense, as reaching a financial agreement with the victim often leads to case dismissal.

**Violence without contact:** Korean courts have consistently held that the concept of "violence" under Article 260 extends beyond physical contact. In Supreme Court Decision 2000Do5716, the court affirmed that creating a threatening atmosphere through aggressive gestures constitutes assault, even absent any physical touching.

**Special violence (Article 261):** When violence is committed by displaying group power, by two or more persons acting jointly, or while carrying a dangerous object, the offense is elevated to "special violence" with enhanced penalties of up to 5 years imprisonment.

### Domestic Violence Framework

The Act on Special Cases Concerning the Punishment of Crimes of Domestic Violence (1997) provides additional penalties and protective measures for assault occurring within family relationships. Courts may issue restraining orders and require counseling as conditions of disposition.

## United States

### Legal Framework

U.S. assault and battery law operates across federal, state, and local jurisdictions with significant variation. The common-law tradition distinguishes between assault (an intentional act placing another in reasonable apprehension of imminent harmful or offensive contact) and battery (the intentional infliction of harmful or offensive bodily contact). However, many modern state codes have merged these offenses.

**Model Penal Code (Section 211.1):** The American Law Institute's Model Penal Code consolidates both crimes under "assault," defining simple assault as purposely, knowingly, or recklessly causing bodily injury, or negligently causing bodily injury with a deadly weapon. Simple assault is graded as a misdemeanor (or petty misdemeanor if arising from mutual combat). Aggravated assault — causing serious bodily injury or causing bodily injury with a deadly weapon — is graded as a felony of the second or third degree.

**State Variations:**

| State | Approach | Key Statute | Penalties (Simple) |
|-------|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| California | Separate assault and battery | PC 240 (assault), PC 242 (battery) | Misdemeanor, up to 6 months / $1,000 fine |
| Texas | Merged statute | Penal Code 22.01 | Class A or C misdemeanor |
| New York | Tiered assault only | Penal Law 120.00-120.10 | Class A misdemeanor to Class B felony |
| Florida | Separate offenses retained | 784.011 (assault), 784.03 (battery) | Misdemeanor, up to 1 year |
| Illinois | Separate offenses retained | 720 ILCS 5/12-1, 5/12-3 | Class C misdemeanor (assault), Class A misdemeanor (battery) |

### Landmark Cases

**Roper v. Simmons, 543 U.S. 551 (2005):** While primarily a death penalty case, the Supreme Court's analysis of juvenile culpability has influenced sentencing in aggravated assault cases involving minors, as courts increasingly consider diminished capacity of youth in violent offenses.

**State v. Norman, 378 S.E.2d 8 (N.C. 1989):** The North Carolina Supreme Court addressed battered woman syndrome in the context of an assault/homicide case, holding that the defendant could not claim self-defense based on imminent threat when killing her sleeping abuser. This case established important precedent about the imminence requirement in self-defense claims related to ongoing patterns of assault.

**People v. Goetz, 68 N.Y.2d 96 (1986):** The New York Court of Appeals clarified that self-defense against assault requires an objectively reasonable belief that force is necessary, rejecting a purely subjective standard. Bernhard Goetz shot four young men on a subway, and the court held that the "reasonable person" test must apply to self-defense claims in assault cases.

### Federal Assault Law

Under 18 U.S.C. Section 111, assaulting a federal officer carries penalties of up to 8 years imprisonment (or up to 20 years if a deadly weapon is used or serious bodily injury results). Federal assault on Indian reservations is governed by the Major Crimes Act (18 U.S.C. Section 1153).

## Germany

### Legal Framework

The German Strafgesetzbuch (StGB) addresses assault under Chapter Seventeen, "Offences Against the Person," through a graduated system of bodily harm offenses (Koerperverletzungsdelikte):

**Section 223 — Simple Bodily Harm (Koerperverletzung):** Whoever physically assaults another person or damages their health shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than 5 years or a fine. The attempt is punishable. Physical assault (koerperliche Misshandlung) is defined as any improper treatment that significantly impairs the victim's physical well-being or bodily integrity, and the impairment must exceed a trivial threshold.

**Section 224 — Dangerous Bodily Harm (Gefaehrliche Koerperverletzung):** Imprisonment from 6 months to 10 years when bodily harm is caused:
- By administering poison or other health-damaging substances
- By means of a weapon or other dangerous implement
- By means of a treacherous attack (hinterhaeltiger Ueberfall)
- Jointly with another participant
- By means of life-threatening treatment

**Section 225 — Abuse of Persons in the Care of the Offender (Misshandlung von Schutzbefohlenen):** Torturing or roughly mistreating a person under 18 or a person who is defenseless due to illness or disability and in the offender's care is punishable by 6 months to 10 years imprisonment.

**Section 226 — Grievous Bodily Harm (Schwere Koerperverletzung):** If the injury results in loss of sight, hearing, speech, or reproductive capacity, loss of an important limb, permanent significant disfigurement, or long-term illness, paralysis, or mental disability, the penalty is 1 to 10 years (or not less than 3 years if caused intentionally).

**Section 227 — Bodily Harm Resulting in Death (Koerperverletzung mit Todesfolge):** If the offender causes the death of the victim through the bodily harm, the penalty is imprisonment for not less than 3 years.

| Offense | StGB Section | Penalty Range |
|---------|-------------|---------------|
| Simple bodily harm | 223 | Up to 5 years / fine |
| Dangerous bodily harm | 224 | 6 months - 10 years |
| Abuse of dependents | 225 | 6 months - 10 years |
| Grievous bodily harm | 226 | 1-10 years (3-15 if intentional) |
| Bodily harm resulting in death | 227 | Not less than 3 years |
| Negligent bodily harm | 229 | Up to 3 years / fine |

### Key Principles

**Consent defense (Section 228):** Bodily harm committed with the consent of the injured person is not unlawful unless the act violates public morals (gute Sitten). This provision is particularly relevant in the context of sports injuries and consensual physical activities, though German courts have set limits — the Federal Court of Justice (Bundesgerichtshof) has held that consent to sadomasochistic acts causing serious injury is void if the acts offend public decency.

**Prosecution on complaint (Section 230):** Simple bodily harm under Section 223 and negligent bodily harm under Section 229 are generally prosecuted only upon filing of a criminal complaint by the victim, unless the prosecuting authority considers ex officio prosecution to be called for by a special public interest. This mirrors the Korean chingojooe mechanism.

**BGH, NStZ 2014, 569:** The Federal Court of Justice held that a single punch to the head constitutes dangerous bodily harm under Section 224(1)(5) — life-threatening treatment — when the victim falls and strikes their head on the ground, as the natural consequence of the initial blow encompasses the resulting secondary injuries.

## European Framework

### ECHR Article 3: Absolute Prohibition

Article 3 of the European Convention on Human Rights provides: "No one shall be subjected to torture or to inhuman or degrading treatment or punishment." This prohibition is absolute and admits of no exceptions or derogations, even in times of war or public emergency (Article 15(2)).

The European Court of Human Rights has developed a three-tier classification of prohibited treatment:

| Category | Threshold | Definition |
|----------|-----------|------------|
| Torture | Highest | Deliberate inhuman treatment causing very serious and cruel suffering |
| Inhuman treatment | Medium | Treatment causing intense physical or mental suffering |
| Degrading treatment | Lowest | Treatment arousing feelings of fear, anguish, and inferiority capable of humiliating and debasing |

### Landmark Cases

**Ireland v. United Kingdom (1978), App. No. 5310/71:** The Court examined five interrogation techniques used by British forces in Northern Ireland (wall-standing, hooding, subjection to noise, sleep deprivation, and food/drink deprivation). The Grand Chamber classified these as inhuman and degrading treatment but not torture — a distinction that proved highly controversial and was revisited decades later.

**Aksoy v. Turkey (1996), App. No. 21987/93:** The Court found that "Palestinian hanging" (suspension by the arms while hands were tied behind the back) constituted torture. This was the first time the Court explicitly found a state had committed torture under Article 3, establishing that acts of deliberate, severe cruelty by state agents meet the torture threshold.

**Selmouni v. France (1999), App. No. 25803/94:** The Grand Chamber held that repeated physical and sexual abuse of a detainee by police officers constituted torture, noting that the "increasingly high standard" required for classification as torture reflected growing European consensus on the absolute nature of the prohibition.

**Bouyid v. Belgium (2015), App. No. 23380/09:** The Grand Chamber held (14-3) that slaps administered by police officers to a minor and an adult in custody constituted degrading treatment violating Article 3. The court emphasized that when a person is deprived of liberty, "any recourse to physical force which has not been made strictly necessary by his own conduct diminishes human dignity" and is inherently a violation.

**MC v. Bulgaria (2003), App. No. 39272/98:** The Court held that Bulgaria's requirement that a sexual assault victim demonstrate physical resistance violated Articles 3 and 8, establishing that states have a positive obligation to penalize non-consensual sexual acts even absent physical resistance by the victim.

### Positive Obligations

The ECHR framework imposes both substantive and procedural positive obligations on member states regarding assault:

- **Substantive obligation:** States must establish criminal-law provisions that effectively deter the commission of offences against the person, backed by law-enforcement machinery for the prevention, suppression, and punishment of breaches.
- **Procedural obligation:** States must conduct an effective official investigation when an individual raises an arguable claim of ill-treatment. The investigation must be capable of leading to the identification and punishment of those responsible (Assenov v. Bulgaria, 1998).
- **Horizontal effect:** Article 3 obligations extend to assaults by private individuals — states must take reasonable steps to prevent ill-treatment of which authorities knew or ought to have known (Opuz v. Turkey, 2009, concerning domestic violence).

## Career Timeline

This section traces the historical development of assault and battery law across the compared jurisdictions.

- **1871:** Germany enacts the Reichsstrafgesetzbuch, codifying bodily harm offenses (Sections 223-233) that form the basis of modern German assault law.
- **1907:** The Model Penal Code's predecessor discussions begin in American legal scholarship, addressing the confusion between assault and battery in common law jurisdictions.
- **1950:** The European Convention on Human Rights is opened for signature, with Article 3 establishing the absolute prohibition on torture and inhuman or degrading treatment.
- **1953:** South Korea enacts its Criminal Act (Act No. 293), including Articles 257-262 on violence and injury offenses, heavily influenced by the Japanese Penal Code and German legal tradition.
- **1962:** The American Law Institute publishes the Model Penal Code, consolidating assault and battery under Section 211.1 and introducing the recklessness standard.
- **1978:** Ireland v. United Kingdom establishes the ECHR framework for distinguishing torture from inhuman and degrading treatment.
- **1986:** People v. Goetz establishes the objective reasonableness standard for self-defense claims in assault cases in New York.
- **1994:** The Violence Against Women Act (VAWA) is enacted in the United States, creating federal remedies for domestic assault.
- **1996:** Aksoy v. Turkey marks the first explicit finding of torture by the European Court of Human Rights.
- **1997:** South Korea enacts the Act on Special Cases Concerning the Punishment of Crimes of Domestic Violence, establishing specialized procedures for family assault cases.
- **1998:** Germany reforms its Gewaltschutzgesetz framework, enhancing civil protection orders for assault victims.
- **1999:** Selmouni v. France raises the standard for what constitutes torture under Article 3, reflecting evolving European norms.
- **2002:** Germany enacts the Gewaltschutzgesetz (Protection Against Violence Act), providing civil remedies for assault victims including exclusion orders.
- **2003:** MC v. Bulgaria establishes the ECHR positive obligation to criminalize non-consensual sexual contact without requiring proof of physical resistance.
- **2009:** Opuz v. Turkey extends Article 3 positive obligations to domestic violence by private actors, requiring states to take preventive operational measures.
- **2013:** The United States reauthorizes VAWA with expanded protections for assault victims, including coverage for same-sex partners and Native American tribal jurisdiction.
- **2015:** Bouyid v. Belgium Grand Chamber judgment holds that even a single slap by a police officer violates Article 3, lowering the severity threshold for state agents.
- **2020:** South Korea amends sentencing guidelines for assault offenses, increasing penalties for repeat offenders and offenses involving vulnerable victims.
