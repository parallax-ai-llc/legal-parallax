## Summary

Treason — the betrayal of one's sovereign or state through acts such as waging war against it, aiding its enemies, or attempting to overthrow its constitutional order — is among the oldest and most severely punished offenses in criminal law. Despite its antiquity, the crime remains actively prosecuted in the twenty-first century, as demonstrated by the 2026 life sentence imposed on former South Korean President Yoon Suk Yeol for insurrection and the ongoing Reichsbürger high treason trials in Germany. Each jurisdiction examined here approaches treason through a distinct legal and constitutional lens. South Korea criminalizes insurrection and related offenses under Articles 87 through 90 of the Criminal Act (형법) and supplements these provisions with the National Security Act (국가보안법), a Cold War-era statute that remains deeply controversial for its breadth. The United States is unique in constitutionally defining treason — the only crime so treated — restricting it under Article III, Section 3 of the Constitution to levying war against the nation or adhering to its enemies, with a stringent two-witness evidentiary requirement codified in 18 U.S.C. Section 2381. Germany distinguishes between Hochverrat (high treason against the state's constitutional order, Sections 81-83a of the Strafgesetzbuch) and Landesverrat (state treason or espionage against external security, Sections 93-101a StGB), reflecting a dual framework shaped by the experiences of the Weimar Republic, the Nazi era, and the Cold War division of Germany. The European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR) does not itself define treason but constrains how member states may prosecute it, requiring that restrictions on fundamental rights under Articles 5, 6, 7, 8, 10, and 11 satisfy the principles of legality, necessity, and proportionality — standards enforced by the European Court of Human Rights in cases such as Incal v. Turkey (1998) and Castells v. Spain (1992).

## South Korea

### Constitutional and Statutory Framework

The Constitution of the Republic of Korea (대한민국 헌법) does not define treason expressly but provides the constitutional architecture within which treason-related offenses operate. Article 1 declares the Republic's sovereignty to reside in the people, establishing popular sovereignty as the foundational principle that insurrection offenses are designed to protect. Article 84 provides the President with immunity from criminal prosecution during the term of office, except for charges of insurrection (내란) or treason (외환).

The Criminal Act (형법), originally enacted in 1953 and subsequently amended, contains two chapters directly relevant to treason:

**Chapter I of Part II — Crimes Concerning Insurrection (내란의 죄, Articles 87-90):**

| Article | Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Art. 87 | Insurrection — committing violence for the purpose of usurping the national territory or subverting the Constitution | Ringleader: death, life imprisonment with labor, or life imprisonment without labor |
| Art. 87 | Participation in essential tasks (commanding, plotting) | Death, life imprisonment, or minimum 5 years |
| Art. 87 | Persons who follow orders or merely join in violence | Up to 5 years imprisonment |
| Art. 88 | Conspiracy or instigation of insurrection not followed by the act | Minimum 3 years imprisonment (conspirators); minimum 5 years (instigators) |
| Art. 89 | Purpose of insurrection — acts auxiliary to insurrection such as disrupting transportation, seizing weapons | Minimum 1 year imprisonment |
| Art. 90 | Preparatory conspiracy for insurrection | Minimum 3 years imprisonment |

**Chapter II of Part II — Crimes Concerning Foreign Aggression (외환의 죄, Articles 92-104):**

These provisions address collaboration with foreign states against Korea, including inducing foreign aggression (Art. 92, penalty: death), providing military aid to foreign aggressors (Art. 93), and espionage for foreign states (Art. 98). Article 92 carries a mandatory death sentence — one of the few absolute mandatory death penalties remaining in Korean criminal law.

### The National Security Act (국가보안법)

Enacted on December 1, 1948, just three months after the founding of the Republic, the National Security Act (NSA) supplements the Criminal Act's treason provisions with offenses specific to the inter-Korean context. The Act was born from the existential security threat posed by North Korea and has been revised multiple times, most significantly in 1991.

**Key provisions:**

| Article | Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Art. 3 | Forming or joining an anti-state organization | Death, life imprisonment, or minimum 5 years |
| Art. 4 | Accepting instructions from anti-state organization | Death, life imprisonment, or minimum 5 years |
| Art. 6 | Infiltration and escape (unauthorized entry/exit to/from enemy territory) | Up to 10 years imprisonment |
| Art. 7 | Praising, encouraging, or propagandizing for anti-state organization | Up to 7 years imprisonment |
| Art. 9 | Espionage — gathering or leaking intelligence to benefit enemy | Death, life imprisonment, or minimum 5 years |
| Art. 10 | Failure to report knowledge of NSA offenses | Up to 5 years imprisonment |

**Criticism:** The NSA has been condemned by Amnesty International, Human Rights Watch, the UN Human Rights Committee, and the Korean Bar Association as incompatible with international human rights standards. Article 7 in particular has been used to prosecute individuals for possessing North Korean publications, expressing sympathetic views toward North Korea's political system, or engaging in academic research on North Korean ideology. The UN Human Rights Committee has repeatedly called for its repeal. In December 2025, a coalition of South Korean lawmakers introduced a bill to abolish the law, triggering fierce political debate.

### The Yoon Suk Yeol Insurrection Case (2024-2026)

On December 3, 2024, President Yoon Suk Yeol declared emergency martial law in a late-night televised address, claiming the existence of "anti-state forces" sympathetic to North Korea within the opposition Democratic Party. The declaration triggered the deployment of military and police units to the National Assembly compound, with orders to prevent legislators from entering to vote on lifting martial law. Yoon simultaneously ordered the arrest of opposition leaders, including Democratic Party leader Lee Jae-myung and ruling party leader Han Dong-hoon.

The martial law lasted approximately six hours before the National Assembly, in a dramatic session with legislators climbing walls and breaking through police cordons, convened a quorum and voted unanimously (190-0) to lift martial law. The Constitutional Court subsequently upheld the Assembly's decision, and impeachment proceedings began immediately.

On February 19, 2026, the Seoul Central District Court convicted Yoon of insurrection under Article 87 of the Criminal Act and sentenced him to life imprisonment. The court found that Yoon's declaration of martial law was not motivated by a genuine national security threat but rather by an intention to paralyze the National Assembly and eliminate political opposition. Former Defense Minister Kim Yong-hyun received a 30-year sentence, and former Prime Minister Han Duck-soo was sentenced to 23 years — the first sitting prime minister convicted of insurrection in South Korean history.

The case represents the first successful prosecution of a sitting or former president for insurrection in South Korean history, although Presidents Chun Doo-hwan and Roh Tae-woo were convicted in 1996 of mutiny and insurrection related to the December 12, 1979 coup and the May 18, 1980 Gwangju massacre. Both were subsequently pardoned.

## United States

### Constitutional Definition

Treason is the only crime expressly defined in the United States Constitution. Article III, Section 3, Clause 1 provides:

> "Treason against the United States, shall consist only in levying War against them, or in adhering to their Enemies, giving them Aid and Comfort. No Person shall be convicted of Treason unless on the testimony of two Witnesses to the same overt Act, or on Confession in open Court."

The Framers deliberately narrowed the definition to prevent the politically motivated treason prosecutions that had characterized English law under the Statute of Treasons (1351) and its expansive judicial interpretation. James Madison wrote in Federalist No. 43 that the constitutional definition was intended to guard against "new-fangled and artificial treasons" — a category that English courts had used to suppress political dissent. The word "only" was inserted specifically to prevent Congress from expanding the definition by statute.

**Two-witness rule:** The evidentiary requirement of two witnesses to the same overt act is unique in American constitutional law and constitutes one of the most demanding proof requirements in any criminal jurisdiction worldwide. In Cramer v. United States, 325 U.S. 1 (1945), the Supreme Court held that the two witnesses must testify to the same overt act, not merely to different acts within the same treasonous scheme, and that the overt act itself must be sufficient to demonstrate treasonous intent.

### Federal Statute: 18 U.S.C. Section 2381

The statutory codification provides:

> "Whoever, owing allegiance to the United States, levies war against them or adheres to their enemies, giving them aid and comfort within the United States or elsewhere, is guilty of treason and shall suffer death, or shall be imprisoned not less than five years and fined under this title but not less than $10,000; and shall be incapable of holding any office under the United States."

**Elements of the offense:**

1. **Allegiance:** The defendant must owe allegiance to the United States. This includes citizens (including dual nationals, as established in Kawakita v. United States, 343 U.S. 717 (1952)) and resident aliens.
2. **Actus reus:** Either (a) levying war against the United States or (b) adhering to enemies of the United States and giving them aid and comfort.
3. **Intent:** The defendant must act with intent to betray the United States. Mere negligence or recklessness is insufficient.

**Penalties:**

| Penalty Component | Provision |
|-------------------|-----------|
| Maximum | Death |
| Minimum imprisonment | 5 years |
| Minimum fine | $10,000 |
| Collateral consequence | Permanent disqualification from holding any federal office |

### Landmark Cases

**Cramer v. United States, 325 U.S. 1 (1945):** Anthony Cramer, a naturalized U.S. citizen of German origin, was convicted of treason for meeting with two German saboteurs who had landed by submarine on Long Island as part of Operation Pastorius. The Supreme Court reversed, holding that the overt act — meeting the saboteurs in public — was not sufficiently treasonous in character when viewed independently of the defendant's intent. The decision established that the two-witness requirement demands testimony to acts that are unambiguously treasonous.

**Haupt v. United States, 330 U.S. 631 (1947):** Hans Max Haupt, father of one of the German saboteurs in Operation Pastorius, was convicted of treason for sheltering his son, helping him purchase an automobile, and assisting him in obtaining employment at a factory manufacturing bombsights. The Supreme Court affirmed the conviction, holding that commonplace acts can constitute overt acts of treason when performed with treasonous intent and when two witnesses testify to the acts.

**Kawakita v. United States, 343 U.S. 717 (1952):** Tomoya Kawakita, a dual citizen of the United States and Japan, was convicted of treason for brutally mistreating American prisoners of war while serving as an interpreter at a mining camp in Japan during World War II. The Supreme Court affirmed, holding that a dual national owes allegiance to the United States and can commit treason even while in the territory of the other nation of citizenship. Kawakita was sentenced to death; President Eisenhower commuted the sentence to life imprisonment, and President Kennedy later ordered his release on condition of permanent deportation.

**Iva Toguri D'Aquino ("Tokyo Rose"), 1949:** D'Aquino was convicted of treason for broadcasting propaganda on Radio Tokyo during World War II. She was sentenced to ten years imprisonment and a $10,000 fine. The conviction was later widely recognized as a miscarriage of justice; key prosecution witnesses recanted their testimony, and President Gerald Ford granted her a full pardon on January 19, 1977 — the first presidential pardon for treason in U.S. history.

### Related Federal Offenses

Because the constitutional definition of treason is deliberately narrow and the evidentiary requirements are exceptionally demanding, the federal government has frequently relied on related statutes to prosecute conduct that might colloquially be described as treasonous:

| Offense | Statute | Maximum Penalty |
|---------|---------|-----------------|
| Seditious conspiracy | 18 U.S.C. § 2384 | 20 years imprisonment |
| Advocating overthrow of government | 18 U.S.C. § 2385 (Smith Act) | 20 years imprisonment |
| Espionage | 18 U.S.C. §§ 793-798 (Espionage Act) | Death (in certain circumstances) |
| Rebellion or insurrection | 18 U.S.C. § 2383 | 10 years imprisonment + disqualification from federal office |
| Material support for terrorism | 18 U.S.C. §§ 2339A-2339B | 20 years (life if death results) |

The Rosenbergs (1951) provide an instructive example: Julius and Ethel Rosenberg were charged with espionage under the Espionage Act — not treason — for passing nuclear weapons secrets to the Soviet Union, because the two-witness rule would likely have doomed a treason prosecution given that the case rested primarily on the testimony of a single witness, Ethel's brother David Greenglass.

After the January 6, 2021 Capitol breach, the Department of Justice charged numerous participants with seditious conspiracy under 18 U.S.C. Section 2384 rather than treason. The Oath Keepers leader Stewart Rhodes and Proud Boys leader Enrique Tarrio were convicted of seditious conspiracy and sentenced to 18 and 22 years respectively — the heaviest sentences imposed for the events of that day.

## Germany

### Constitutional Foundation

The Basic Law (Grundgesetz) of the Federal Republic of Germany establishes the constitutional framework within which treason offenses operate. Article 20 declares Germany to be a democratic and social federal state, with sovereignty vesting in the people through elections and specific organs of legislation, executive power, and jurisdiction. Article 20(4) — the so-called "right of resistance" (Widerstandsrecht) — provides that all Germans have the right to resist any person seeking to abolish the constitutional order, if no other remedy is available. Article 102 abolishes the death penalty absolutely.

### Hochverrat (High Treason) — StGB Sections 81-83a

German criminal law distinguishes sharply between Hochverrat (high treason), directed against the internal constitutional order, and Landesverrat (state treason), directed against external security.

**Section 81 — High treason against the Federation (Hochverrat gegen den Bund):**

Whoever undertakes, by force or through threat of force, to (1) undermine the continued existence of the Federal Republic of Germany, or (2) change the constitutional order based on the Basic Law, incurs a penalty of life imprisonment or imprisonment of not less than ten years.

**Section 82 — High treason against a Land (Hochverrat gegen ein Land):**

Whoever undertakes, by force or through threat of force, to (1) incorporate the territory of a Land in whole or in part into another Land, or (2) separate part of a Land's territory from that Land, incurs imprisonment of not less than one year and not more than ten years.

**Section 83 — Preparation of a treasonous undertaking:**

Whoever prepares a specific undertaking of high treason against the Federation is liable to imprisonment from one to ten years. The provision is notable for criminalizing preparatory acts — a stage of criminal conduct that many jurisdictions do not penalize.

| Offense | StGB Section | Penalty Range |
|---------|-------------|---------------|
| High treason against the Federation | § 81(1) | Life imprisonment or minimum 10 years |
| High treason against the Federation (less serious case) | § 81(2) | 1-10 years imprisonment |
| High treason against a Land | § 82 | 1-10 years imprisonment |
| Preparation of treasonous undertaking | § 83 | 1-10 years imprisonment |
| Preparation — voluntary abandonment | § 83a | Court may reduce penalty or dispense with punishment |

### Landesverrat (State Treason / Espionage) — StGB Sections 93-101a

State treason targets the external security of the Federal Republic. The central provision is Section 94:

**Section 94 — Treason (Landesverrat):**

Whoever communicates a state secret to a foreign power or one of its intermediaries, or otherwise allows it to reach an unauthorized person or become publicly known, in order to disadvantage the Federal Republic of Germany or to benefit a foreign power, and thereby creates a danger of serious disadvantage to the external security of the Federal Republic, incurs imprisonment of not less than one year. In especially serious cases, the penalty is imprisonment of not less than five years.

**Section 93 — Definition of state secrets:**

State secrets are facts, objects, or knowledge accessible only to a limited group of persons, which must be kept from foreign powers in order to avert the danger of serious disadvantage to the external security of the Federal Republic.

### Prosecution and Jurisdiction

Prosecution of treason offenses is the exclusive responsibility of the Federal Public Prosecutor (Generalbundesanwalt) at the Federal Court of Justice (Bundesgerichtshof) pursuant to Section 120 of the Courts Constitution Act (Gerichtsverfassungsgesetz, GVG). Investigations are conducted by the Federal Criminal Police Office (Bundeskriminalamt, BKA). First-instance jurisdiction lies with the Higher Regional Courts (Oberlandesgerichte) designated by the Federal Public Prosecutor.

### The Reichsbürger Coup Plot (2022-ongoing)

In December 2022, German federal police conducted one of the largest counter-terrorism raids in the country's history, arresting 25 individuals connected to the Reichsbürger movement — a far-right movement that denies the legitimacy of the modern German state and claims that the German Empire continues to exist under international law.

The alleged plot centered on Heinrich XIII, Prince Reuss, a 72-year-old aristocrat and real estate dealer whom the group planned to install as head of state following a violent overthrow. The conspirators allegedly planned to storm the Reichstag building in Berlin with armed support, arrest members of parliament, and broadcast images of a captive Chancellor Olaf Scholz on national television. Former military officers and a sitting judge — Birgit Malsack-Winkemann, a former AfD member of the Bundestag — were among the accused.

Twenty-six defendants are being tried in three separate proceedings in Frankfurt, Stuttgart, and Munich, beginning in April and May 2024. The primary charges are preparation of a treasonous undertaking under Section 83 StGB and membership in or support of a terrorist organization under Section 129a StGB. The trials are expected to continue into 2026 or beyond and represent the largest treason-related prosecution in the history of the Federal Republic.

### The Netzpolitik Affair (2015)

In 2015, the Federal Prosecutor General initiated proceedings against two journalists from netzpolitik.org under Section 94 StGB (Landesverrat) for publishing classified documents revealing that the Federal Office for the Protection of the Constitution (Bundesamt für Verfassungsschutz) had received 2.75 million euros from a secret government fund for internet surveillance operations. The investigation provoked national outrage over press freedom and was halted on August 10, 2015, after the Federal Minister of Justice criticized the prosecution and the Prosecutor General was dismissed. The affair became a landmark in German press freedom discourse and illustrated the tension between state secrecy and journalistic accountability.

## ECHR & EU

### The ECHR Framework

The European Convention on Human Rights (signed November 4, 1950; entered into force September 3, 1953) does not define or directly criminalize treason. Instead, it establishes a framework of fundamental rights that constrains how the 46 member states of the Council of Europe may investigate, prosecute, and punish treason and related offenses. The European Court of Human Rights (ECtHR) in Strasbourg serves as the enforcement mechanism, hearing individual applications from persons claiming that a member state has violated their Convention rights.

**Key Convention articles relevant to treason prosecutions:**

| Article | Right Protected | Relevance to Treason |
|---------|----------------|---------------------|
| Art. 2 | Right to life | Prohibits death penalty for treason (via Protocols 6 and 13) |
| Art. 3 | Prohibition of torture and inhuman treatment | Constrains interrogation methods in national security investigations |
| Art. 5 | Right to liberty and security | Governs pre-trial detention in treason cases; requires prompt judicial review |
| Art. 6 | Right to a fair trial | Guarantees procedural rights including presumption of innocence, legal representation, and public hearing |
| Art. 7 | No punishment without law (nulla poena sine lege) | Requires treason offenses to be defined with sufficient clarity and foreseeability |
| Art. 8 | Right to respect for private life | Limits surveillance powers in national security investigations |
| Art. 10 | Freedom of expression | Constrains prosecution of speech-based treason offenses (e.g., praising enemies) |
| Art. 11 | Freedom of assembly and association | Protects political party formation and membership against blanket criminalization |
| Art. 15 | Derogation in time of emergency | Permits limited suspension of certain rights during war or public emergency threatening the life of the nation |

### Abolition of the Death Penalty

The ECHR framework has progressively eliminated the death penalty across Council of Europe member states:

- **Protocol 6 (1983):** Abolished the death penalty in peacetime. Ratified by all Council of Europe member states.
- **Protocol 13 (2002):** Abolished the death penalty in all circumstances, including wartime and treason. Ratified by 44 of 46 member states (Russia and Azerbaijan have not ratified).

These protocols directly constrain the penalty available for treason. A Council of Europe member state may not impose the death penalty for treason regardless of how its domestic law is framed. This represents a significant departure from the historical position across European jurisdictions, where treason was traditionally a capital offense.

### Landmark ECtHR Case Law

**Incal v. Turkey, Application No. 22678/93 (1998):**

Ibrahim Incal, a member of the executive committee of the People's Labour Party (HEP), was convicted under Turkish national security legislation for preparing a leaflet criticizing government policy toward Kurds. The Grand Chamber held that Turkey violated Article 10 (freedom of expression), emphasizing that "interferences with the freedom of expression of a politician who is a member of an opposition party call for the closest scrutiny on the Court's part." The decision established that national security considerations cannot justify disproportionate restrictions on political speech.

**Castells v. Spain, Application No. 11798/85 (1992):**

Miguel Castells, a Basque senator, was convicted under Spain's Criminal Code for publishing an article criticizing the government's handling of violence in the Basque Country. The Court found a violation of Article 10, holding that a democratic government must tolerate criticism from elected representatives and that the criminal prosecution of political speech requires the most compelling justification.

**Lawless v. Ireland, Application No. 332/57 (1961):**

The first case decided on the merits by the ECtHR, arising from the detention without trial of an IRA member under Irish emergency legislation. The Court held that Ireland's derogation under Article 15 was valid given the IRA's campaign of violence but established the principle that derogation must be strictly proportionate to the exigencies of the situation and subject to judicial review.

**Aksoy v. Turkey, Application No. 21987/93 (1996):**

The Court examined Turkey's derogation from Article 5 in the context of its fight against PKK terrorism in southeast Turkey. While accepting the existence of a public emergency, the Court found that 14 days of incommunicado detention without judicial oversight constituted a violation of Article 5(3), even under the derogation regime.

### EU Legal Framework

The European Union supplements the ECHR through the Charter of Fundamental Rights of the European Union, which became legally binding with the entry into force of the Treaty of Lisbon on December 1, 2009. Article 2 of the Charter provides an absolute right to life, and Article 2(2) provides that no one shall be condemned to the death penalty or executed.

**EU Directive 2017/541 on combating terrorism** harmonizes terrorism-related offenses across EU member states, including offenses that overlap with traditional treason concepts such as attacks against the state and its institutions. The Directive requires member states to criminalize directing or participating in terrorist groups, while mandating respect for fundamental rights including the principle of legality and the right to a fair trial.

The EU itself does not define or prosecute treason. Criminal jurisdiction remains with member states, subject to the constraints of both the ECHR and EU law. However, the European Arrest Warrant Framework Decision (2002/584/JHA) facilitates the cross-border surrender of persons accused of treason-related offenses, and Eurojust coordinates prosecutorial cooperation in cases with transnational dimensions.

## Comparative Analysis

### Definition and Scope

The four jurisdictions reflect fundamentally different approaches to defining treason. The United States adopts the narrowest definition, constitutionally limited to two specific acts — levying war and adhering to enemies. This reflects the Framers' deliberate reaction against the expansive English common law of treason. South Korea's framework is broader, encompassing insurrection (usurping state authority through collective violence), foreign aggression, and under the National Security Act, expression of support for North Korea. Germany occupies an intermediate position, with the dual Hochverrat/Landesverrat framework covering both internal subversion and external espionage, while criminalizing preparatory acts that go well beyond what U.S. law would reach. The ECHR does not define treason but demands that however states define it, the definition must satisfy the principle of legality — meaning it must be sufficiently clear and foreseeable to enable individuals to regulate their conduct.

### Evidentiary Requirements

The U.S. two-witness requirement stands as the most demanding evidentiary standard among the jurisdictions examined. This constitutional safeguard has effectively rendered treason prosecutions nearly impossible in the modern era, pushing prosecutors toward alternative charges such as seditious conspiracy or espionage. No other jurisdiction imposes a comparable evidentiary threshold for treason. South Korea and Germany apply their general criminal evidentiary standards, and the ECHR requires only that member states satisfy Article 6 fair trial guarantees.

### Penalty Structures

The penalty structures reveal divergent approaches to capital punishment and proportionality:

- **South Korea** retains the death penalty as the maximum punishment for insurrection ringleaders under Article 87, but has maintained a de facto moratorium on executions since 1997, with the last executions carried out on December 30 of that year. Amnesty International classifies South Korea as "abolitionist in practice."
- **The United States** retains the death penalty as the statutory maximum for treason under 18 U.S.C. Section 2381, though no federal treason execution has occurred since the Civil War era.
- **Germany** abolished the death penalty absolutely under Article 102 of the Basic Law in 1949, making life imprisonment the maximum penalty for high treason.
- **The ECHR framework** has progressively abolished the death penalty through Protocols 6 and 13, prohibiting its use by member states even for treason.

### Political Dimensions

Treason law is inherently political. Each jurisdiction has confronted the tension between legitimate state security interests and the risk of weaponizing treason charges against political opponents:

- South Korea's National Security Act has been used to suppress left-leaning political expression and academic research sympathetic to North Korea, prompting sustained international criticism.
- The U.S. constitutional framework was specifically designed to prevent political abuse, with the two-witness rule serving as a structural safeguard.
- Germany's Netzpolitik affair demonstrated the risk of using Landesverrat provisions against journalistic activity, prompting the dismissal of the Prosecutor General.
- The ECHR has consistently held that political speech — even speech critical of the state or sympathetic to separatist movements — receives heightened protection under Article 10.
