## Summary

Pretrial detention -- the incarceration of accused persons between arrest and the conclusion of criminal proceedings -- represents one of the most consequential intersections of state authority and individual liberty in any legal system, as it restricts the freedom of persons who have not yet been convicted of any crime and are therefore presumed innocent. South Korea's pretrial detention (구속) is governed by Articles 70 through 92 of the Criminal Procedure Act (형사소송법), requiring judicial authorization through a warrant issued by a judge upon finding that the suspect poses a flight risk or danger of evidence destruction, with an initial detention period of ten days for prosecution investigations (extendable once to twenty days) and a complex system of renewal during trial proceedings governed by Supreme Court rules. The United States addresses pretrial detention through the Bail Reform Act of 1984 (18 U.S.C. Sections 3141-3156) at the federal level, which permits detention without bail upon a finding that no conditions of release will reasonably assure the appearance of the person and the safety of the community, while state bail systems vary dramatically -- with landmark reforms in New Jersey (2017), New York (2019), and Illinois (2023, the first state to eliminate cash bail entirely through the Pretrial Fairness Act). Germany regulates pretrial detention (Untersuchungshaft) through Sections 112 through 130 of the Strafprozessordnung (StPO), requiring both strong suspicion (dringender Tatverdacht) and a specific ground for detention (Haftgrund) such as flight risk, risk of evidence tampering, or risk of reoffending, with strict proportionality review and a presumptive six-month limit under Section 121 StPO beyond which continuation requires approval by the Higher Regional Court (Oberlandesgericht). The European Court of Human Rights has developed among its most extensive bodies of jurisprudence on pretrial detention under Article 5 of the Convention, establishing in cases such as Wemhoff v. Germany (1968), Letellier v. France (1991), and Buzadji v. Moldova (2016) that detention must be based on relevant and sufficient reasons, reviewed promptly and at reasonable intervals, and subject to strict proportionality -- with the reasonable time requirement of Article 5(3) generating more judgments finding violations than virtually any other Convention provision.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's pretrial detention system is governed primarily by Articles 70 through 92 of the Criminal Procedure Act (형사소송법) and supplemented by the Act on Criminal Procedure for the Protection of Human Rights (인권보호를 위한 형사절차법). The Constitution of the Republic of Korea (대한민국 헌법) provides foundational protections in Article 12 (personal liberty and freedom from arbitrary arrest) and Article 27 (right to trial), establishing that no person shall be arrested, detained, searched, or seized except as provided by law and pursuant to a warrant issued by a judge upon the request of a prosecutor.

### Warrant Requirement and Grounds

Under Article 70 of the Criminal Procedure Act, a court may issue an arrest warrant (구속영장) when there are reasonable grounds to believe the suspect has committed an offense and when one or more of the following conditions exist: the suspect has no fixed abode, there is a reasonable ground to believe the suspect may destroy evidence, or there is a reasonable ground to believe the suspect may flee. The prosecution must present the suspect before a judge within 48 hours of arrest. Article 201-2 establishes the suspect's right to a warrant review hearing (구속 전 피의자 심문), introduced by the 1995 amendment, during which the judge evaluates the necessity and proportionality of detention.

### Detention Periods

| Stage | Maximum Period |
|-------|---------------|
| Police investigation | 10 days (Article 202) |
| Prosecution investigation | 10 days, extendable by 10 days (Articles 203, 205) |
| Trial (first instance) | 6 months (Supreme Court Rules) |
| Trial (appellate) | 4 months per appeal stage |

### Bail System

South Korea's bail system operates through two mechanisms: bail with deposit (보증금납입조건부 석방) under Article 94, where the court sets a monetary amount that is deposited as a condition of release, and bail without deposit (보증서제출조건부 석방) under Article 95, where a surety pledges to ensure the accused's appearance. The 2019 amendments significantly expanded defendants' rights to apply for bail and strengthened the presumption in favor of release for non-violent offenses.

### Constitutional Court Decisions

The Constitutional Court has addressed pretrial detention on several occasions. In Decision 2015HeonBa77 (2016), the Court upheld the constitutionality of the maximum detention period during trial proceedings but emphasized that courts must exercise particular care in extending detention and that prolonged pretrial detention raises serious concerns under Articles 12 and 27 of the Constitution.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The U.S. pretrial detention framework operates at both federal and state levels, producing a patchwork of laws and practices that has generated significant controversy, particularly regarding the impact of cash bail on indigent defendants.

**Federal -- Bail Reform Act of 1984:** The Act (18 U.S.C. Sections 3141-3156) established a presumption of release with conditions, but permits pretrial detention upon a judicial finding, by clear and convincing evidence, that no condition or combination of conditions will reasonably assure the appearance of the person as required and the safety of any other person and the community. The Act creates a rebuttable presumption of detention for defendants charged with crimes of violence, offenses carrying maximum sentences of life imprisonment or death, serious drug offenses (maximum 10+ years), and certain repeat felony offenders.

**Constitutional Framework:** The Eighth Amendment prohibits excessive bail but does not guarantee an absolute right to bail. In United States v. Salerno, 481 U.S. 739 (1987), the Supreme Court upheld the Bail Reform Act's preventive detention provisions, finding that pretrial detention based on dangerousness is regulatory rather than punitive and does not violate the Due Process Clause or the Excessive Bail Clause.

### Cash Bail Reform Movement

The American cash bail system has faced intensifying criticism for effectively creating a two-tier system in which wealthy defendants secure release while indigent defendants remain incarcerated, regardless of the seriousness of the charges or the risk they pose:

**New Jersey (2017):** The Criminal Justice Reform Act, effective January 1, 2017, virtually eliminated cash bail, replacing it with a risk-assessment-based system using the Public Safety Assessment (PSA) tool. Pretrial jail population declined by approximately 44% in the first year.

**New York (2019-2020):** New York enacted bail reform eliminating cash bail for most misdemeanors and non-violent felonies, effective January 1, 2020. The law was subsequently amended in 2020 and 2022 to expand judicial discretion in setting bail, responding to public safety concerns.

**Illinois (2023):** The Pretrial Fairness Act, part of the SAFE-T Act, took effect on September 18, 2023 (following the Illinois Supreme Court's ruling in Rowe v. Raoul upholding constitutionality), making Illinois the first state to eliminate cash bail entirely. Detention is available only upon prosecution motion and judicial finding of specific statutory grounds.

### Scale of Pretrial Detention

Approximately 470,000 individuals are held in local jails pretrial on any given day in the United States, constituting roughly 74% of the total local jail population. This figure has increased substantially since the 1990s despite declining crime rates, driven largely by the expansion of pretrial detention practices and the inability of many defendants to post bail.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's pretrial detention (Untersuchungshaft) framework is widely regarded as among the most protective of defendants' rights in any legal system. Sections 112 through 130 of the Strafprozessordnung (StPO) establish a system that treats detention as a strictly exceptional measure, subject to continuous judicial oversight and rigorous proportionality review.

### Grounds for Detention

Section 112(1) StPO requires two cumulative conditions for pretrial detention: strong suspicion (dringender Tatverdacht) that the accused has committed the offense, and the existence of a specific ground for detention (Haftgrund). Section 112(2) StPO enumerates three grounds:

**Flight Risk (Fluchtgefahr):** Certain facts establish the danger that the accused will not appear for trial. The assessment considers the accused's personal ties, employment, family situation, citizenship, and the expected severity of the sentence.

**Risk of Evidence Tampering (Verdunkelungsgefahr):** The accused's behavior creates a strong suspicion that the accused will destroy, alter, suppress, or fabricate evidence, or improperly influence co-accused, witnesses, or experts.

**Risk of Reoffending (Wiederholungsgefahr):** Under Section 112a StPO, for specific enumerated offenses (sexual offenses, stalking, bodily harm), detention is permitted where the accused is strongly suspected of the offense and specific facts establish the danger of further offenses of the same kind.

### Proportionality and Time Limits

Section 112(1) sentence 2 StPO explicitly requires that detention be proportionate: it may not be ordered if it is disproportionate to the significance of the case and the expected sentence. Section 121(1) StPO establishes a presumptive six-month limit on pretrial detention, after which continuation requires a specific order by the Oberlandesgericht (Higher Regional Court) based on the particular difficulty or extraordinary scope of the investigation or another important reason justifying continuation.

### Judicial Oversight

The accused must be brought before a judge (Haftrichter) without delay following arrest, and no later than the end of the day following the arrest (Section 115 StPO). The detention order must state the offense, the facts supporting strong suspicion, the specific ground for detention, and the proportionality assessment. The accused may challenge the detention order at any time through a complaint (Beschwerde) to the Landgericht or through a motion for judicial review (Haftprüfung) under Section 117 StPO.

### Statistics

Germany's pretrial detention rate is among the lowest in Europe, with approximately 20% of the prison population held pretrial, corresponding to roughly 13,000 individuals nationwide in 2024. The Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) regularly reviews pretrial detention decisions, and has overturned detention orders in numerous cases for insufficient reasoning or disproportionate duration.

## ECHR & EU

### European Convention on Human Rights -- Article 5

Article 5 of the ECHR provides the most developed supranational framework for the regulation of pretrial detention. Article 5(1)(c) permits detention on reasonable suspicion for the purpose of bringing a person before the competent legal authority. Article 5(3) guarantees that everyone detained shall be brought promptly before a judge and shall be entitled to trial within a reasonable time or to release pending trial. Article 5(4) provides the right to challenge the lawfulness of detention before a court.

### Landmark ECHR Jurisprudence

**Wemhoff v. Germany, Application No. 2122/64, Judgment of June 27, 1968:** The Court's first major pretrial detention case established that the reasonable time guarantee in Article 5(3) runs from the date of arrest to the date of judgment at first instance (not the final judgment on appeal). The Court examined the complexity of the case, the accused's conduct, and the conduct of the authorities in assessing reasonableness.

**Letellier v. France, Application No. 12369/86, Judgment of June 26, 1991:** The Court established that the persistence of reasonable suspicion is a necessary but not sufficient condition for continued detention. National authorities must give relevant and sufficient reasons for continued detention, examining each case individually, and must consider whether alternative less restrictive measures could adequately address the grounds for detention.

**Buzadji v. Moldova [GC], Application No. 23755/07, Judgment of July 5, 2016:** The Grand Chamber clarified that a detained person must be released pending trial unless the state shows relevant and sufficient reasons for continued detention from the very first decision on detention, not only after a certain passage of time. Any system of mandatory detention without judicial assessment of individual circumstances is incompatible with Article 5(3).

**Kavala v. Turkey [GC], Application No. 28749/18, Judgment of December 11, 2019:** The Grand Chamber found that the pretrial detention of civil society leader Osman Kavala violated Article 5(1) because it was not based on reasonable suspicion and was instead motivated by an ulterior purpose -- to silence the applicant and dissuade other human rights defenders -- in violation of Article 18 taken in conjunction with Article 5.

### EU Directive 2016/343

Directive (EU) 2016/343 of the European Parliament and of the Council of March 9, 2016, on the strengthening of certain aspects of the presumption of innocence and of the right to be present at trial, establishes that member states must ensure that pretrial detention is treated as a measure of last resort and that alternatives to detention are considered. Article 5 provides that member states shall take appropriate measures to ensure that suspects and accused persons are not presented as being guilty, in court or in public, by use of measures of physical restraint.

### EU Directive 2013/48/EU

Directive 2013/48/EU on the right of access to a lawyer in criminal proceedings guarantees that suspects have access to a lawyer before police questioning and during detention decisions, including pretrial detention hearings. This right applies from the earliest stage of deprivation of liberty and cannot be waived unless done so voluntarily and unequivocally.

## Comparative Analysis

### Presumption of Liberty

All four jurisdictions formally recognize a presumption in favor of pretrial release, but the practical implementation varies dramatically. Germany's framework most consistently reflects this presumption through its explicit proportionality requirement, presumptive time limits, and low pretrial detention rate (approximately 20%). The United States presents the starkest contrast, with approximately 74% of local jail inmates held pretrial, reflecting the distorting effects of the cash bail system on the presumption of liberty.

### Grounds for Detention

The jurisdictions converge on two primary grounds: flight risk and danger to the community/risk of reoffending. Germany adds the specific ground of evidence tampering risk (Verdunkelungsgefahr), a ground that receives less systematic attention in other systems. South Korea's inclusion of "no fixed abode" as an independent ground for detention has been criticized as disproportionately affecting homeless and marginalized individuals.

### Time Limits

Germany's presumptive six-month limit with mandatory higher court review represents the most stringent time limitation. South Korea imposes specific limits at each procedural stage. The United States imposes no statutory maximum at the federal level, relying instead on the Speedy Trial Act (18 U.S.C. Section 3161) and constitutional due process protections. The ECHR applies a flexible "reasonable time" standard that depends on the circumstances of each case.

### Bail vs. Release on Conditions

The most significant structural divergence concerns the role of monetary bail. The United States is the only jurisdiction that historically relied heavily on cash bail as the primary mechanism for pretrial release, though this is rapidly changing through legislative reform. South Korea uses a deposit-based system but does not treat inability to pay as an automatic ground for continued detention. Germany does not use cash bail, relying instead on conditions of release such as reporting requirements, surrender of travel documents, and surety guarantees.
