## Summary

Broadcasting regulation -- the legal frameworks governing the licensing, content standards, ownership, and public service obligations of television, radio, and increasingly digital media services -- reflects fundamental choices about the role of the state in shaping the information environment, with each jurisdiction striking a different balance between market freedom, public service mandates, content regulation, and media pluralism. South Korea regulates broadcasting through the Broadcasting Act (방송법), the Telecommunications Business Act (전기통신사업법), and the oversight of the Korea Communications Commission (방송통신위원회, KCC) and the Korea Communications Standards Commission (방송통신심의위원회), with a mixed system of public broadcasters (KBS, EBS) funded through license fees and advertising, and commercial broadcasters subject to content standards, ownership restrictions, and local content quotas. The United States regulates broadcasting through the Federal Communications Commission (FCC), established by the Communications Act of 1934 (47 U.S.C. Section 151 et seq.) and substantially updated by the Telecommunications Act of 1996, operating under a lighter-touch regulatory philosophy than most other democracies, with the Supreme Court's decision in Red Lion Broadcasting Co. v. FCC (1969) upholding content regulation based on spectrum scarcity rationale while the elimination of the Fairness Doctrine in 1987 marked a decisive shift toward deregulation. Germany operates one of the world's most elaborate public broadcasting systems under the constitutional framework established by the Federal Constitutional Court's broadcasting decisions (Rundfunkurteile), with the dual broadcasting system (duales Rundfunksystem) comprising public broadcasters (ARD, ZDF) funded by the Rundfunkbeitrag (broadcasting contribution) and regulated by the Interstate Media Treaty (Medienstaatsvertrag, MStV, 2020), which also extends regulatory authority to digital intermediaries and video-sharing platforms. At the European level, the Audiovisual Media Services Directive (AVMSD, Directive 2018/1808/EU) harmonizes minimum standards for broadcasting and on-demand services across the EU, while the European Court of Human Rights has consistently held that broadcasting regulation, including licensing systems, is compatible with Article 10 (freedom of expression) provided it pursues legitimate aims and is proportionate.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's broadcasting regulation operates through a dual institutional structure: the Korea Communications Commission (방송통신위원회, KCC) and the Korea Communications Standards Commission (방송통신심의위원회, KCSC).

**Broadcasting Act (방송법):** Enacted in 2000, consolidating previous broadcasting legislation, the Act establishes the licensing framework for television and radio broadcasters, content standards, ownership restrictions, and public broadcasting obligations. The Act was significantly amended in 2009 to permit newspaper companies to own broadcasting stations, reversing the previous cross-ownership prohibition.

**Korea Communications Commission (KCC):** The KCC is an executive agency responsible for broadcasting licensing, spectrum management, and broadcasting policy. Its independence has been periodically questioned, particularly regarding political influence over public broadcaster appointments.

### Public Broadcasting

**KBS (한국방송공사):** South Korea's principal public broadcaster, established under the Korea Broadcasting System Act, operates two television channels (KBS1, KBS2) and multiple radio services. KBS1 is partially funded by the broadcasting license fee (수신료), currently 2,500 KRW per month collected through electricity bills. KBS2 is primarily advertising-funded.

**EBS (한국교육방송공사):** The Educational Broadcasting System focuses on educational programming and operates under the Educational Broadcasting System Act.

### Content Regulation

| Regulation | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| Content classification | All programs rated (All, 7+, 12+, 15+, 19+) |
| Korean content quota | Minimum percentage of domestic content required |
| Public interest programming | Minimum hours of news, educational, and cultural programming |
| Watershed rules | Adult content restricted to late-night hours |
| Political impartiality | Required for public broadcasters during elections |

### Media Independence Controversies

South Korea has experienced significant controversies regarding political influence over public broadcasting. During the administrations of Lee Myung-bak (2008-2013) and Park Geun-hye (2013-2017), journalists at KBS and MBC conducted extended strikes protesting alleged government interference in editorial decisions. The "blacklist" scandal under President Park, in which the government maintained lists of cultural figures and journalists to be excluded from support and opportunities, led to criminal prosecutions after her impeachment.

## United States

### Legal Framework

**Communications Act of 1934 (47 U.S.C. Section 151 et seq.):** Established the FCC and provided the foundational framework for broadcasting regulation. The Act requires broadcasters to operate in the "public interest, convenience, and necessity."

**Telecommunications Act of 1996:** The most comprehensive update to U.S. communications law, the Act significantly relaxed ownership limits, authorized spectrum auctions, and introduced provisions addressing media convergence. Section 202 raised the national television station ownership cap and relaxed local ownership restrictions.

### FCC Regulatory Authority

The FCC exercises regulatory authority over broadcasting through licensing, content regulation, and ownership rules:

**Licensing:** Broadcast licenses are issued for eight-year terms. License renewal is subject to public interest review. The FCC conducts spectrum auctions for new licenses.

**Content Regulation:** The FCC's authority to regulate broadcast content is narrower than in most other democracies:
- **Indecency (18 U.S.C. Section 1464):** The FCC may impose forfeiture penalties for "obscene, indecent, or profane language" broadcast between 6 AM and 10 PM. The Supreme Court upheld this authority in FCC v. Pacifica Foundation, 438 U.S. 726 (1978).
- **Children's Television Act (1990):** Requires broadcasters to serve the educational and informational needs of children.

### Fairness Doctrine

The FCC's Fairness Doctrine (1949-1987) required broadcasters to present contrasting viewpoints on controversial public issues. The Supreme Court upheld the Doctrine in Red Lion Broadcasting Co. v. FCC, 395 U.S. 367 (1969), based on the scarcity of broadcast frequencies. The FCC eliminated the Doctrine in 1987, concluding it chilled rather than encouraged robust debate. Subsequent legislative attempts to codify the Doctrine were vetoed by President Reagan (1987) and President George H.W. Bush (1991).

### Ownership Rules

The 1996 Act dramatically relaxed ownership limits. In the radio sector, national ownership caps were eliminated entirely, enabling the consolidation exemplified by iHeartMedia (currently over 850 stations). Television ownership rules have been progressively relaxed, with the FCC raising the national audience cap from 25% to 39% (codified by Congress in 2004). Cross-ownership rules (newspaper-broadcast) were eliminated by the FCC in 2017.

### Public Broadcasting

The Corporation for Public Broadcasting (CPB), established by the Public Broadcasting Act of 1967, provides federal funding to PBS (television) and NPR (radio). Total federal appropriations for CPB are approximately $535 million annually, a fraction of public broadcasting budgets in comparable democracies.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's broadcasting regulation is among the most elaborate in the world, reflecting the constitutional significance attached to broadcasting as a medium of public opinion formation (öffentliche Meinungsbildung).

**Federal Constitutional Court Broadcasting Decisions (Rundfunkurteile):** The BVerfG has issued a series of landmark decisions shaping broadcasting law:

| Decision | Year | Key Holding |
|----------|------|------------|
| First Broadcasting Decision (BVerfGE 12, 205) | 1961 | Federal government cannot establish a national television broadcaster; broadcasting is a state (Länder) competence |
| Second Broadcasting Decision (BVerfGE 31, 314) | 1971 | Broadcasting must serve "internal pluralism" (Binnenpluralismus) |
| Fourth Broadcasting Decision (BVerfGE 73, 118) | 1986 | Dual broadcasting system (public + private) is constitutional; public broadcasting receives "basic provision" (Grundversorgung) mandate |
| Rundfunkbeitrag Decision (BVerfGE 158, 389) | 2021 | State blocking of license fee increase violates broadcasting freedom; ARD/ZDF funding must be adequate |

### Medienstaatsvertrag (Interstate Media Treaty, 2020)

The MStV, effective November 7, 2020, replaced the Rundfunkstaatsvertrag (Interstate Broadcasting Treaty, 1991) and significantly expanded regulatory scope. Key innovations include:

- Extension of broadcasting-like regulation to video-sharing platforms and social media
- Requirements for media intermediaries (search engines, social networks) regarding transparency, non-discrimination, and discoverability of journalistic content
- Updated rules for media concentration control through the Kommission zur Ermittlung der Konzentration im Medienbereich (KEK)
- User interface regulation requiring prominence for public interest content

### Rundfunkbeitrag (Broadcasting Contribution)

The Rundfunkbeitrag, a per-household contribution of 18.36 EUR per month (as of 2024), funds ARD, ZDF, and Deutschlandradio. The system generates approximately 8.5 billion EUR annually, making German public broadcasting one of the best-funded in the world. The Federal Constitutional Court's 2021 decision confirmed that states cannot unilaterally block fee increases determined through the independent KEF (Commission for Determining the Financial Requirements of Broadcasters) process.

### Landesmedienanstalten

The 14 Landesmedienanstalten (state media authorities) regulate private broadcasting at the state level, issuing licenses, monitoring content compliance, and enforcing youth protection rules. They coordinate through the Direktorenkonferenz der Landesmedienanstalten (DLM).

## ECHR & EU

### Audiovisual Media Services Directive (AVMSD)

Directive 2018/1808/EU (revising Directive 2010/13/EU) provides the harmonized EU framework for audiovisual media services:

- **Country of Origin Principle:** Services are regulated by the member state where the provider is established, with limited exceptions
- **European Works Quotas:** On-demand services must ensure at least 30% European works in their catalogues and give prominence to such works
- **Advertising Limits:** Linear television services limited to 20% advertising per hour (between 6 AM and 6 PM, and between 6 PM and midnight)
- **Protection of Minors:** Enhanced requirements for content that may impair the development of minors
- **Video-Sharing Platforms:** Covered for the first time, with requirements for measures against harmful content

### European Media Freedom Act (EMFA, 2024)

Regulation (EU) 2024/1083, adopted on April 11, 2024, represents the first EU-wide regulation addressing media freedom and pluralism. Key provisions include:

- Protection of editorial independence from government and commercial interference
- Prohibition on compelled disclosure of journalistic sources through spyware
- Media pluralism monitoring through the European Board for Media Services (EBMS)
- Transparency requirements for media ownership and state advertising allocation
- Restrictions on audience measurement methodology manipulation

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Informationsverein Lentia v. Austria (Application No. 13914/88, Judgment of November 24, 1993):** The Court held that Austria's state broadcasting monopoly violated Article 10, as the technical restrictions on broadcasting frequencies do not justify an absolute state monopoly. However, the Court recognized that licensing systems are permitted under Article 10(1), third sentence.

**Centro Europa 7 S.r.l. and Di Stefano v. Italy (Application No. 38433/09, Grand Chamber Judgment of June 7, 2012):** The Grand Chamber found that Italy's failure to allocate broadcasting frequencies to a licensed operator for more than a decade violated Article 10 and Article 1 of Protocol No. 1. The judgment emphasized the state's positive obligation to ensure effective media pluralism.

**Manole and Others v. Moldova (Application No. 13936/02, Judgment of September 17, 2009):** The Court found that political control over Moldova's public broadcaster violated Article 10, establishing that states have a positive obligation to ensure the independence of public broadcasting from government interference.

### Streaming Services Regulation

The growth of streaming services (Netflix, Disney+, Amazon Prime) has challenged traditional broadcasting regulatory frameworks across all jurisdictions:

**South Korea:** Korea's content industry has benefited enormously from streaming platforms, with Korean dramas and films achieving global distribution. The Broadcasting Act does not directly regulate foreign streaming platforms, though the National Assembly has debated requiring streaming services to contribute to Korean content production funds and comply with local content quotas. Netflix invested over $2.5 billion in Korean content between 2015 and 2024.

**United States:** Streaming services are classified as "information services" rather than broadcasters, exempting them from FCC content regulation, public interest obligations, and ownership restrictions. The absence of regulatory parity between streaming and broadcast services has been a persistent concern for traditional broadcasters.

**Germany:** The Medienstaatsvertrag applies certain broadcasting-like obligations to "broadcasting-like telemedien" (rundfunkähnliche Telemedien), including registration and content standards. Streaming services operating in Germany must also comply with the AVMSD's European works quotas (30% catalogue requirement) and youth protection rules.

**EU:** The revised AVMSD (2018) brought on-demand audiovisual services, including streaming platforms, within the regulatory framework for the first time, imposing European works quotas, advertising standards, and youth protection obligations. The country-of-origin principle means that Netflix, headquartered in the Netherlands for EU purposes, is primarily regulated by the Dutch media authority.

### Media Concentration and Pluralism

Media ownership concentration raises distinct regulatory concerns across jurisdictions:

| Jurisdiction | Key Concentration Concern | Regulatory Response |
|-------------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| South Korea | Chaebol media ownership; political influence over public broadcasters | Cross-ownership restrictions (relaxed 2009) |
| United States | Radio consolidation (iHeartMedia); local media deserts | FCC ownership rules (progressively relaxed) |
| Germany | Regional media concentration; Axel Springer dominance in digital news | KEK concentration control; MStV intermediary regulation |
| EU | Cross-border platform dominance | EMFA (2024) media pluralism provisions |

## Comparative Analysis

### Public Broadcasting Models

The jurisdictions exhibit dramatically different public broadcasting models. Germany's system is the most generously funded and constitutionally protected, with the BVerfG effectively treating adequate public broadcasting as a constitutional requirement. South Korea maintains a significant public broadcasting presence but with persistent political independence challenges. The United States has the weakest public broadcasting infrastructure among developed democracies, with minimal federal funding and no constitutional or statutory guarantee of independence.

### Content Regulation Philosophy

The United States applies the lightest content regulation, limited primarily to indecency rules and children's programming requirements, reflecting First Amendment primacy. Germany imposes the most comprehensive content obligations, including internal pluralism requirements, youth protection, and European works quotas. South Korea maintains a middle position with content classification, domestic content quotas, and political impartiality requirements. The ECHR permits content regulation as a legitimate restriction on broadcasting freedom, provided it is proportionate and pursues legitimate aims.

### Digital Convergence

All jurisdictions face the challenge of extending broadcasting regulation to digital platforms. Germany's Medienstaatsvertrag is the most ambitious attempt, extending broadcasting-like obligations to media intermediaries. The EU's AVMSD extends coverage to video-sharing platforms. The United States has been the most reluctant to extend broadcasting regulation to the internet, with Section 230 CDA providing broad platform immunity. South Korea's KCSC exercises content oversight over online media but faces jurisdictional and enforcement challenges.
