## Summary

State succession -- the replacement of one state by another in the responsibility for the international relations of territory -- is among the most consequential and contested areas of international law, raising fundamental questions about the continuity of treaty obligations, the transfer of state property, archives, and debts, the nationality of inhabitants, and the legal status of territorial claims. The two principal codification instruments are the Vienna Convention on Succession of States in Respect of Treaties (1978), which entered into force on November 6, 1996, and the Vienna Convention on Succession of States in Respect of State Property, Archives and Debts (1983), which has not yet entered into force. The International Law Commission (ILC) has contributed substantially to this field, including through its 1999 study on nationality in relation to state succession. South Korea's legal framework is profoundly shaped by the division of the Korean Peninsula following the end of Japanese colonial rule in 1945, the establishment of two separate governments in 1948, and the ongoing constitutional claim under Article 3 of the Constitution of the Republic of Korea (대한민국 헌법) that the territory of the Republic encompasses the entire Korean Peninsula, creating a unique situation in which the Republic of Korea (ROK) claims to be the sole legitimate government of all Korea while simultaneously maintaining inter-Korean relations governed by special agreements such as the Inter-Korean Basic Agreement (남북기본합의서, 1991) and the Panmunjom Declaration (2018). Germany provides the paradigmatic modern example of state succession through reunification, as the German Democratic Republic (GDR) acceded to the Federal Republic of Germany (FRG) under Article 23 of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz) on October 3, 1990, pursuant to the Unification Treaty (Einigungsvertrag) of August 31, 1990, and the Treaty on the Final Settlement with Respect to Germany (Two-Plus-Four Treaty) of September 12, 1990, with the FRG treated as the continuing state rather than a successor state, thereby maintaining continuity of treaty obligations, NATO membership, and European Community membership. The United States, as a state born from revolutionary succession that has not itself experienced succession events since its founding, nevertheless engages extensively with state succession issues through recognition policy, treaty succession determinations, and its role in multilateral processes, including the breakup of the Soviet Union and Yugoslavia. The ECHR and EU framework addresses succession through the accession processes of successor states, the application of EU treaty obligations to reunified Germany, and the European Court of Human Rights' jurisdiction over successor states' obligations under the Convention, as demonstrated in cases such as Bijelić v. Montenegro and Serbia (Application No. 11890/05, 2009) and Silih v. Slovenia (Application No. 71463/01, 2009).

## South Korea

### Constitutional Framework and the Korean Division

The legal situation of the Korean Peninsula presents one of the most complex state succession problems in contemporary international law. Following Japan's surrender on August 15, 1945, Korea was divided along the 38th parallel into Soviet and American zones of occupation. In 1948, two separate governments were established: the Republic of Korea (대한민국, ROK) in the South on August 15, 1948, and the Democratic People's Republic of Korea (조선민주주의인민공화국, DPRK) in the North on September 9, 1948. Each government claimed (and continues to claim) sovereignty over the entire peninsula.

Article 3 of the ROK Constitution provides: "The territory of the Republic of Korea shall consist of the Korean peninsula and its adjacent islands." This territorial clause means that, as a matter of constitutional law, the ROK claims the territory administered by the DPRK, and the DPRK is not recognized as a sovereign state. The Constitutional Court has repeatedly upheld this position, including in Decision 92헌바6 (1993), where the Court held that North Korea constitutes an "anti-state organization" under the National Security Act (국가보안법), while simultaneously being a partner for dialogue in inter-Korean relations.

### Succession to Japanese Colonial-Era Treaties and Property

The transition from Japanese colonial rule (1910-1945) to Korean sovereignty raised critical succession questions regarding treaties, property, and claims. The Treaty on Basic Relations between Japan and the Republic of Korea (한일기본조약, June 22, 1965) established diplomatic relations and addressed some succession issues. The accompanying Agreement on the Settlement of Problems Concerning Property and Claims (한일청구권협정) provided $500 million in grants and loans from Japan, which Korea interpreted as economic cooperation rather than compensation for colonial-era wrongs.

The question of whether individual claims survived the 1965 Agreement became a major succession-related dispute. The Korean Supreme Court held in Decision 2013Da61381 (2018) that individuals' rights to claim compensation for forced labor during the colonial period were not extinguished by the 1965 Agreement, a determination with profound implications for post-colonial state succession and the treatment of individual claims.

### Inter-Korean Relations and Quasi-Succession

The Inter-Korean Basic Agreement (남북 사이의 화해와 불가침 및 교류·협력에 관한 합의서, December 13, 1991) acknowledged the "special relationship" between the two Koreas as a "temporary relationship formed in the process toward reunification" rather than an inter-state relationship. The Constitutional Court in Decision 2000헌바238 (2003) confirmed that inter-Korean agreements are not treaties under international law but constitute special domestic-type agreements.

The Panmunjom Declaration for Peace, Prosperity and Reunification of the Korean Peninsula (판문점선언, April 27, 2018) committed both Koreas to the goal of "complete denuclearization" and "establishing a permanent and solid peace regime on the Korean Peninsula," raising succession questions that would arise upon potential reunification, including the status of DPRK treaties, the disposition of state property, the treatment of debts, and the nationality of North Korean citizens.

### Penalties and Legal Consequences

| Issue | Legal Treatment |
|-------|----------------|
| North Korean residents entering the South | Treated as ROK nationals under Art. 3; eligible for citizenship under North Korean Refugees Protection Act (북한이탈주민법) |
| DPRK treaty obligations | Not recognized by ROK; potential renegotiation upon reunification |
| Colonial-era property claims | Subject to 1965 Agreement; individual claims survived per Supreme Court |
| Inter-Korean agreements violations | No international law enforcement mechanism; political/diplomatic consequences |

## United States

### Historical Succession and Recognition Policy

The United States was itself a product of revolutionary succession, with the thirteen colonies declaring independence from Great Britain on July 4, 1776, and achieving recognition through the Treaty of Paris (1783). The U.S. approach to state succession has been characterized by pragmatism and reliance on recognition policy rather than adherence to the Vienna Conventions, neither of which the United States has ratified.

The Restatement (Third) of the Foreign Relations Law of the United States (1987) Section 208-210 addresses state succession, generally recognizing that a successor state is bound by the treaty obligations of the predecessor state for the territory concerned (the "continuity" or "moving treaty frontiers" rule for territorial changes) but acknowledging that for new states emerging from decolonization or dissolution, the "clean slate" doctrine permits the new state to decide which predecessor treaties it will continue.

### Dissolution of the Soviet Union (1991)

The United States played a central role in managing the legal consequences of the Soviet Union's dissolution in December 1991. The U.S. recognized Russia as the continuing state of the Soviet Union, inheriting Soviet treaty obligations including the UN Security Council permanent seat, arms control agreements (START I Treaty, INF Treaty, ABM Treaty), and diplomatic relations. The Alma-Ata Declaration (December 21, 1991) and subsequent bilateral agreements established the framework for the transfer of nuclear weapons from Ukraine, Belarus, and Kazakhstan to Russia under the Budapest Memorandum on Security Assurances (December 5, 1994).

### Dissolution of Yugoslavia (1991-2006)

The breakup of Yugoslavia presented distinct succession challenges. The United States recognized Slovenia, Croatia, and Bosnia-Herzegovina in 1992 and supported the Badinter Arbitration Commission's opinions that Yugoslavia was in the process of dissolution rather than experiencing secession. The Federal Republic of Yugoslavia (Serbia and Montenegro) was denied automatic succession to the SFRY's UN seat, and was required to apply for UN membership anew (admitted in 2000). The Succession Agreement of June 29, 2001, among the five successor states addressed the division of assets, archives, and debts.

### Recognition and Its Legal Consequences

Under U.S. law, the President has exclusive authority to recognize foreign governments and states (Zivotofsky v. Kerry, 576 U.S. 1 (2015)). Recognition of a successor state triggers treaty succession determinations by the State Department, which evaluates on a case-by-case basis whether predecessor treaties continue in force. The United States recognized the independence of Kosovo in 2008, despite the ongoing dispute regarding whether Kosovo's declaration of independence constituted lawful secession or unilateral separation.

### Key Legal Framework

| Issue | U.S. Approach |
|-------|--------------|
| Continuing state | Recognized as inheritor of predecessor's obligations (e.g., Russia for USSR) |
| New state (clean slate) | May choose which predecessor treaties to continue |
| Territorial change | Moving treaty frontiers rule generally applied |
| State property/debt | Negotiated settlement among successor states preferred |
| Recognition | Executive prerogative; triggers legal consequences for treaties |
| UN membership | New states must apply; continuing states retain seat |

## Germany

### Reunification as State Succession

German reunification on October 3, 1990, constitutes the most significant and thoroughly documented case of state succession in the post-Cold War era. The process was accomplished through three interconnected legal instruments: the Treaty Establishing a Monetary, Economic and Social Union (Staatsvertrag, May 18, 1990), the Unification Treaty (Einigungsvertrag, August 31, 1990), and the Treaty on the Final Settlement with Respect to Germany (Two-Plus-Four Treaty, September 12, 1990).

The mechanism chosen -- accession of the GDR to the FRG under Article 23 of the Basic Law (in its pre-1990 version) -- meant that the FRG was treated as the continuing state and the GDR as a dissolved entity absorbed into the existing Federal Republic. This had profound consequences for treaty succession: FRG treaties continued to apply automatically to the enlarged territory, while GDR treaties were subject to review, renegotiation, or termination.

### Treaty Succession in Practice

Article 12 of the Unification Treaty addressed treaty succession comprehensively. FRG treaties were extended to the territory of the former GDR as a general rule, though certain treaties (particularly those with the European Communities) required specific arrangements. GDR treaties were categorized into three groups:

1. **Treaties to be maintained:** Those compatible with the FRG's legal order and international obligations, subject to consultation with treaty partners (Art. 12(2)).
2. **Treaties to be renegotiated:** Those requiring adaptation to the new circumstances.
3. **Treaties to be terminated:** Those incompatible with FRG obligations, particularly GDR treaties with Soviet bloc states that conflicted with NATO membership or EC obligations.

The Two-Plus-Four Treaty (involving the FRG, GDR, United States, United Kingdom, France, and the Soviet Union) resolved the remaining external aspects of German unity: the final settlement of borders (confirmation of the Oder-Neisse line with Poland), the withdrawal of Soviet forces, limitations on German military forces, and the termination of Four Power rights and responsibilities relating to Berlin and Germany as a whole. Article 7(2) declared: "The united Germany shall have accordingly full sovereignty over its internal and external affairs."

### State Property, Archives, and Debts

The Unification Treaty addressed the succession of GDR state property, which was transferred to the FRG or its constituent Lander. GDR state debts were assumed by the Kreditabwicklungsfonds (Credit Processing Fund), later integrated into the Erblastentilgungsfonds (Inherited Debt Redemption Fund). The disposition of property confiscated under GDR law or Soviet occupation (1945-1949) became one of the most contentious aspects of reunification, with the principle of "restitution before compensation" (Ruckgabe vor Entschadigung) adopted in the Joint Declaration of June 15, 1990, and subsequently challenged before the Federal Constitutional Court and the European Court of Human Rights.

### European Community Membership

The extension of EC/EU membership to the former GDR territory was accomplished without a formal accession process, treating reunification as a territorial enlargement of an existing member state. The European Council agreed at its special session on April 28, 1990, that the territory of the former GDR would be integrated into the Community under transitional arrangements. This precedent has been cited in discussions of potential future reunification scenarios, including the Korean Peninsula.

## ECHR & EU

### Successor State Obligations Under the ECHR

The European Court of Human Rights has addressed state succession in several important cases. In Bijelić v. Montenegro and Serbia (Application No. 11890/05, Judgment of June 28, 2009), the Court established that Montenegro, which declared independence from Serbia in June 2006, succeeded to Serbia and Montenegro's obligations under the Convention from the date of independence. The Court examined the declaration made by Montenegro upon notification of succession and held that the Convention applied to Montenegro from June 3, 2006.

In Silih v. Slovenia (Application No. 71463/01, Grand Chamber Judgment of April 9, 2009), the Court addressed the temporal jurisdiction question arising from Slovenia's succession to the SFRY and ratification of the ECHR on June 28, 1994. The Grand Chamber held that the procedural obligation under Article 2 (right to life) to investigate a death could be a detachable obligation binding the successor state even for deaths occurring before ratification, provided a significant proportion of the procedural steps were or ought to have been carried out after ratification.

### EU Enlargement and Succession

The EU accession process provides a structured framework for successor states to join the Union. Following the dissolution of Yugoslavia, Slovenia acceded to the EU in 2004, Croatia in 2013, and the remaining successor states are at various stages of the accession process. Each acceding state must adopt the entire acquis communautaire, effectively assuming the treaty obligations developed over decades.

The special case of German reunification demonstrated that EU law could be extended to new territory without formal accession. The European Commission's de Maiziere Report (1990) outlined the transitional measures for integrating the former GDR, including temporary derogations from EU environmental, product safety, and competition rules.

### State Succession and EU Treaty Law

The CJEU has not directly ruled on general state succession principles, but its jurisprudence on EU accession and treaty succession is relevant. In Kadi v. Council (Joined Cases C-402/05 P and C-415/05 P, 2008), the Court affirmed the autonomy of the EU legal order in relation to international law, with implications for how EU member states' succession obligations interact with EU law. The Brita case (C-386/08, 2010) addressed the application of EU trade agreements to disputed territories, touching on succession-related questions.

### Council of Europe Practice

The Council of Europe has developed a practice regarding successor states and the ECHR. Upon the dissolution of the Soviet Union, Russia acceded to the ECHR in 1998, while other former Soviet states acceded at various dates. The Parliamentary Assembly of the Council of Europe established criteria for admission, including commitments to democratic governance, rule of law, and human rights protection. Following Russia's expulsion from the Council of Europe on March 16, 2022, in response to the invasion of Ukraine, the ECHR ceased to apply to Russia from September 16, 2022, though cases concerning events before that date remain within the Court's jurisdiction.

### Succession Principles in European Practice

| Principle | Application |
|-----------|------------|
| Automatic succession | ECHR obligations transfer upon successor state notification |
| Clean slate with accession | EU membership requires formal accession and acquis adoption |
| Territorial extension | German reunification: EU law extended without new accession |
| Temporal jurisdiction | Silih: procedural obligations may extend before ratification date |
| Dissolution consequences | SFRY successor states required individual ECHR ratification |

## Comparative Analysis

### Continuity vs. Clean Slate

The fundamental theoretical debate in state succession law -- whether a successor state inherits the predecessor's treaty obligations (continuity) or starts fresh (clean slate) -- is resolved differently depending on the type of succession. Germany's reunification by absorption maintained full FRG treaty continuity. The dissolution of the Soviet Union and Yugoslavia produced a mixed approach: Russia was treated as a continuing state, while other successor states were given varying degrees of clean slate status. South Korea's unique situation, with both states claiming continuity from the pre-division Korean state, defies simple categorization.

### Practical vs. Doctrinal Approaches

The United States exemplifies a pragmatic approach, making case-by-case determinations based on political considerations and treaty-specific assessments. Germany followed a heavily negotiated, legally formalized process. South Korea exists in a state of constitutional ambiguity, where the legal claim to the entire peninsula coexists with practical engagement with the DPRK as a separate entity. The EU and ECHR systems impose structured frameworks that channel succession through accession processes.

### Unresolved Korean Succession Questions

The potential reunification of the Korean Peninsula would raise succession questions of extraordinary complexity, drawing on but extending beyond the German precedent. Key unresolved issues include: the status of DPRK treaties (particularly those with China and Russia); the assumption of DPRK state debts; the treatment of DPRK state-owned property; the application of international human rights obligations to former DPRK officials; and the extension of ROK's alliance obligations (including the US-ROK Mutual Defense Treaty) to the entire peninsula.

### The Vienna Conventions' Limited Impact

Neither the 1978 nor the 1983 Vienna Convention has achieved widespread ratification or practical influence. The 1978 Convention has only 23 parties, and the 1983 Convention has not entered into force. State practice, particularly in the post-Cold War period, has diverged significantly from these instruments, with negotiated solutions (as in the German and Yugoslav cases) proving more influential than codified rules.
