## Summary

Partnership law -- the body of rules governing the formation, operation, liability, and dissolution of business associations in which two or more persons carry on a business in common with a view to profit -- represents one of the oldest and most diverse areas of commercial law, with each jurisdiction developing distinctive forms of partnership organization and liability allocation. South Korea governs partnerships primarily through the Korean Commercial Code (상법, Act No. 1000, 1962), which recognizes the Hapmyeong Hoesa (합명회사, general partnership company), Hapja Hoesa (합자회사, limited partnership company), and Hapja Johap (합자조합, limited partnership association under the 2011 amendments), supplemented by the Civil Code (민법) provisions on Johap (조합, civil law associations) under Articles 703-724. The United States regulates partnerships through a patchwork of state-adopted uniform laws, principally the Revised Uniform Partnership Act (RUPA, 1997, last amended 2013) for general partnerships, the Uniform Limited Partnership Act (ULPA, 2001) for limited partnerships, and the Uniform Limited Liability Partnership provisions integrated into RUPA, each adopted with variations across the fifty states. Germany provides a sophisticated tripartite partnership structure under the Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB) for the Gesellschaft bürgerlichen Rechts (GbR, civil law partnership, §§ 705-740 BGB, substantially reformed effective January 1, 2024), the Handelsgesetzbuch (HGB) for the Offene Handelsgesellschaft (OHG, general commercial partnership, §§ 105-160 HGB) and the Kommanditgesellschaft (KG, limited partnership, §§ 161-177a HGB), while the European Union and ECHR framework addresses partnership law primarily through the freedom of establishment under Articles 49-55 TFEU and the protection of association rights under Article 11 ECHR.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's partnership law operates through two parallel tracks: commercial partnerships under the Commercial Code and civil law associations under the Civil Code.

**Civil Law Associations (조합, Johap):** Articles 703-724 of the Civil Code govern the Johap, the most basic form of partnership in Korean law. A Johap is formed when two or more persons mutually agree to combine their contributions to pursue a common enterprise (Article 703). The Johap does not have legal personality separate from its members; the partners hold partnership property as co-owners (합유, Article 704).

**Commercial Partnerships under the Commercial Code:**

| Type | Korean Term | Key Features |
|------|-----------|--------------|
| General Partnership Company | 합명회사 (Hapmyeong Hoesa) | All partners have unlimited liability; legal personality |
| Limited Partnership Company | 합자회사 (Hapja Hoesa) | General partners (unlimited) + limited partners (limited to contribution) |
| Limited Partnership Association | 합자조합 (Hapja Johap) | Introduced 2011; modeled on LP; no legal personality |
| Limited Liability Company | 유한책임회사 (Yuhan Chaegim Hoesa) | Introduced 2011; modeled on U.S. LLC |

### Hapmyeong Hoesa (General Partnership Company)

**Formation:** A Hapmyeong Hoesa is formed by the execution of articles of association (정관) by two or more partners and registration with the commercial registry (Article 178). The articles of association must include the partners' names, the company name, the purpose, the registered office, and the contributions of each partner (Article 179).

**Partner Liability:** Under Article 212, all partners are jointly and severally liable for the debts and obligations of the partnership without limitation. This liability is subsidiary (보충적 책임) -- creditors must first exhaust remedies against the partnership entity before pursuing individual partners (Article 212, paragraph 2), unless the partnership's assets are manifestly insufficient.

**Management and Representation:** Each partner has the right to manage the partnership's business (Article 200) and to represent the partnership (Article 207), unless the articles of association provide otherwise. Decisions on ordinary business matters may be made by any partner, while extraordinary matters require the consent of all partners (Article 201).

**Transfer of Partnership Interest:** A partner may not transfer their partnership interest without the unanimous consent of all other partners (Article 197). This restriction reflects the intuitu personae nature of the general partnership.

### Hapja Hoesa (Limited Partnership Company)

**Structure:** The Hapja Hoesa consists of one or more general partners (무한책임사원) with unlimited liability and one or more limited partners (유한책임사원) whose liability is limited to their agreed contribution (Article 268).

**Limited Partner Restrictions:** Limited partners may not participate in the management of the partnership (Article 278). A limited partner who interferes in management loses the protection of limited liability and becomes liable as a general partner (Article 278, paragraph 2). Limited partners have the right to inspect the partnership's books and financial statements (Article 277).

**Supreme Court Decision 2004다64482 (2005):** The Court held that a limited partner who actively participated in management decisions, including negotiating contracts on behalf of the partnership, lost the protection of limited liability under Article 278.

### 2011 Reform: New Business Forms

The 2011 amendments to the Commercial Code introduced two new business forms inspired by Anglo-American models:

**Hapja Johap (합자조합, Limited Partnership Association):** Modeled on the limited partnership, the Hapja Johap consists of a business executor (업무집행조합원) with unlimited liability and limited partners (유한책임조합원) whose liability is limited to their contributions. Unlike the Hapja Hoesa, the Hapja Johap does not have legal personality, making it a pass-through entity for tax purposes. This form has become popular for venture capital and private equity fund structures.

**Yuhan Chaegim Hoesa (유한책임회사, Limited Liability Company):** Modeled on the U.S. LLC, this form provides limited liability for all members while offering operational flexibility and pass-through taxation.

### Fiduciary Duties

Korean partnership law imposes fiduciary duties on partners through a combination of Commercial Code and Civil Code provisions:

- **Duty of Loyalty:** Article 198 of the Commercial Code prohibits partners from engaging in transactions that compete with the partnership's business or from acting as partners or directors of competing enterprises without the consent of all other partners.
- **Duty of Care:** The Civil Code duty of care (선관주의의무, Article 681) applies to partnership management by virtue of Article 195 of the Commercial Code.
- **Duty to Account:** Partners must account for profits and benefits derived from partnership opportunities (Article 198).

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States partnership law framework consists primarily of uniform laws adopted with variations by individual states.

**Revised Uniform Partnership Act (RUPA):** Originally promulgated in 1994, revised in 1997, and last amended in 2013, RUPA has been adopted in some form by 44 states and the District of Columbia. RUPA fundamentally changed U.S. partnership law by adopting the entity theory of partnership (§ 201), replacing the aggregate theory of the original Uniform Partnership Act (UPA, 1914).

**Key RUPA Provisions:**

| Section | Content |
|---------|---------|
| § 201 | Partnership as an entity distinct from partners |
| § 202 | Formation; association of two or more persons to carry on business for profit |
| § 301 | Partner as agent of partnership |
| § 306 | Partner's liability (joint and several) |
| § 401 | Partner's rights and duties |
| § 404 | Partner's duty of loyalty |
| § 409 | Standards of conduct for partners |
| § 601-603 | Dissociation |
| § 701-705 | Dissociation when business not wound up; buyout |
| § 801-807 | Dissolution and winding up |

### Formation

Under RUPA § 202, a partnership is formed when two or more persons associate to carry on a business as co-owners for profit, regardless of whether they intend to form a partnership. No filing is required for formation of a general partnership, though many states permit or require filing of a statement of partnership authority (RUPA § 303).

**Factors indicating partnership (§ 202(c)):**
- Sharing of profits creates a rebuttable presumption of partnership (§ 202(c)(3))
- Receipt of a share of profits as payment for debt, wages, rent, or other obligations does not create a presumption

### Partner Liability

**RUPA § 306:** All partners are jointly and severally liable for all obligations of the partnership. This represents a significant change from the original UPA, which imposed joint liability for contract claims and joint and several liability only for tort claims.

**Exhaustion Requirement:** RUPA § 307(d) provides that a judgment creditor of the partnership may not levy execution against the assets of a partner until a judgment against the partnership has been returned unsatisfied (the exhaustion rule).

### Limited Partnerships

**Uniform Limited Partnership Act (ULPA 2001):** ULPA 2001 governs limited partnerships, consisting of one or more general partners and one or more limited partners.

**Shield for Limited Partners (§ 303):** Under ULPA 2001, a limited partner is not personally liable for partnership obligations solely by reason of being a limited partner, even if the limited partner participates in management and control. This represents a complete abandonment of the "control rule" that characterized prior versions of the Act.

**General Partner Liability:** General partners in a limited partnership have the same unlimited liability as general partners in a general partnership, unless the limited partnership elects LLP status.

### Limited Liability Partnerships (LLPs)

LLP provisions, integrated into RUPA, allow general partnerships and limited partnerships to elect limited liability status by filing a statement of qualification:

- **Full Shield States:** Most states provide a "full shield," protecting partners from personal liability for all partnership obligations (contract and tort)
- **Partial Shield States:** A minority of states protect partners only from liability for the negligence or misconduct of other partners
- **Insurance/Financial Responsibility Requirements:** Many states require LLPs to maintain minimum levels of professional liability insurance (e.g., $1 million in many jurisdictions)

### Fiduciary Duties

**RUPA § 404 (1997) / § 409 (2013 amendments):** RUPA specifies partner fiduciary duties:

- **Duty of Loyalty (§ 404(b) / § 409(b)):** Accounting for profits; refraining from dealing adversely; refraining from competing
- **Duty of Care (§ 404(c) / § 409(c)):** Refraining from grossly negligent or reckless conduct, intentional misconduct, or knowing violation of law
- **Good Faith and Fair Dealing (§ 404(d) / § 409(d)):** Obligation of good faith in all partnership dealings

**Meinhard v. Salmon, 249 N.Y. 458, 164 N.E. 545 (1928):** Chief Judge Cardozo's landmark opinion established that partners owe each other "the duty of the finest loyalty" and that "not honesty alone, but the punctilio of an honor the most sensitive" is the standard of behavior required.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany provides the most elaborate typology of partnership forms among the jurisdictions examined, with a clear distinction between civil law partnerships and commercial partnerships.

### Gesellschaft bürgerlichen Rechts (GbR)

**BGB §§ 705-740 (Reformed January 1, 2024):** The GbR is the fundamental partnership form under German civil law, formed when two or more persons agree to pursue a common purpose through mutual contributions (§ 705 BGB).

**2024 Reform (Gesetz zur Modernisierung des Personengesellschaftsrechts, MoPeG):** The Modernization of Partnership Law Act, effective January 1, 2024, represents the most significant reform of German partnership law in over a century:

| Reform Element | Pre-2024 | Post-2024 |
|---------------|----------|-----------|
| Legal personality | Recognized by BGH case law (BGHZ 146, 341) but not by statute | Expressly codified (§ 705 para. 2 BGB) |
| Registration | No public register for GbR | Gesellschaftsregister (partnership register) established (§ 707 BGB) |
| Real property | Complex workarounds required | Registered GbR (eGbR) may hold real property directly |
| Partner liability | § 128 HGB applied analogously | § 721 BGB: joint and several liability codified |

**BGH, January 29, 2001 (II ZR 331/00, BGHZ 146, 341):** This landmark decision recognized the legal capacity (Rechtsfähigkeit) of the Außen-GbR (externally operating GbR), overturning more than a century of doctrine. The MoPeG reform codified this judicial development.

### Offene Handelsgesellschaft (OHG)

**HGB §§ 105-160:** The OHG is a general commercial partnership whose purpose is the operation of a commercial enterprise (Handelsgewerbe). All partners have unlimited joint and several liability for partnership debts (§ 128 HGB).

**Formation:** An OHG is formed by agreement and must be registered in the Handelsregister (commercial register) maintained by the Amtsgericht (local court) (§ 106 HGB). Registration is declaratory, not constitutive -- the OHG comes into existence upon commencement of business.

**Management (Geschäftsführung) and Representation (Vertretung):**
- Each partner is entitled and obligated to manage the partnership's business (§ 114 HGB)
- Each partner may individually represent the partnership in transactions with third parties (Einzelvertretungsbefugnis, § 125 HGB)
- The articles of association may restrict management or representation powers

**Partner Liability (§ 128 HGB):** Partners are personally, directly, jointly and severally liable for all partnership obligations. Unlike Korean law, German law does not require creditors to exhaust remedies against the partnership before pursuing individual partners (no Subsidiarität), though § 129 HGB allows a partner to raise defenses available to the partnership (Einreden der Gesellschaft).

### Kommanditgesellschaft (KG)

**HGB §§ 161-177a:** The KG consists of at least one Komplementär (general partner) with unlimited liability and at least one Kommanditist (limited partner) whose liability is limited to the Haftsumme (registered liability amount).

**Kommanditist Liability (§ 171 HGB):** The limited partner's liability is limited to the Haftsumme registered in the Handelsregister. Once the Kommanditist has made their contribution in full, personal liability is extinguished. If the contribution is returned, liability revives (§ 172 HGB).

**GmbH & Co. KG:** The most widely used German partnership form is the GmbH & Co. KG, in which a GmbH (limited liability company) serves as the sole Komplementär. This structure combines the tax advantages of a partnership (transparent taxation) with the limited liability of a corporation for the general partner position. The GmbH & Co. KG is used by approximately 170,000 enterprises in Germany, including many Mittelstand businesses.

### Partnerschaftsgesellschaft (PartG)

**Partnerschaftsgesellschaftsgesetz (PartGG, 1994):** The Partnerschaftsgesellschaft is a partnership form exclusively for members of the liberal professions (freie Berufe) -- lawyers, accountants, physicians, architects, and similar professionals.

**PartGmbB (2013):** The Partnerschaftsgesellschaft mit beschränkter Berufshaftung, introduced in 2013, allows professional partners to limit their liability for professional negligence, provided the partnership maintains adequate professional indemnity insurance. This form has been widely adopted by German law firms and accounting firms.

### Treuepflicht (Fiduciary Duty)

German partnership law imposes a comprehensive Treuepflicht (duty of loyalty) on partners, developed primarily through BGH jurisprudence:

**BGH, June 5, 1975 (II ZR 23/74, "ITT"):** The Court established that the Treuepflicht requires partners to act in the partnership's interest and to refrain from pursuing personal advantage at the partnership's expense. The duty extends to all aspects of partnership relations.

**Scope of Treuepflicht:**
- Prohibition on competition during the partnership's existence (§ 112 HGB for OHG)
- Duty to exercise voting rights in good faith
- Obligation to consent to necessary amendments to the partnership agreement
- Duty of disclosure regarding matters affecting the partnership

## ECHR / EU

### EU Framework

**Freedom of Establishment (Articles 49-55 TFEU):** The EU Treaty guarantees the right of establishment, enabling partnerships formed under the law of one member state to operate across the EU. However, partnership law has not been harmonized at the EU level, and partnerships formed in one member state are not automatically recognized in others.

**Centros, Uberseering, and Inspire Art:** The CJEU trilogy of decisions on freedom of establishment has significant implications for partnerships:

- **Centros Ltd v. Erhvervs- og Selskabsstyrelsen (C-212/97, 1999):** A company (or partnership) validly formed in one member state must be recognized by other member states
- **Uberseering BV v. Nordic Construction Company (C-208/00, 2002):** The real seat theory (Sitztheorie) cannot be used to deny legal capacity to an entity validly formed in another member state
- **Inspire Art Ltd (C-167/01, 2003):** Member states cannot impose additional requirements on foreign entities that would undermine freedom of establishment

**Impact on German Partnership Law:** These decisions have practical significance for German partnerships, as Germany traditionally applied the Sitztheorie (real seat doctrine), which determined the applicable law of an entity based on its actual headquarters rather than place of incorporation. The MoPeG reform of 2024 addressed this by introducing provisions on cross-border mobility for partnerships.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Freedom of Association (Article 11 ECHR):** The right to form associations, including business partnerships, is protected under Article 11 ECHR.

**Sidiropoulos and Others v. Greece (Application No. 26695/95, ECtHR Judgment of July 10, 1998):** The Court held that the right to form associations includes the right to form partnerships and other business organizations, and that restrictions on this right must be prescribed by law and necessary in a democratic society.

**Article 1, Protocol No. 1 (Protection of Property):** Partnership interests constitute possessions protected by the Convention. State interference with partnership interests -- through legislation altering partner rights, compulsory dissolution, or taxation -- must satisfy the proportionality requirement.

**Tre Traktorer Aktiebolag v. Sweden (Application No. 10873/84, ECtHR Judgment of July 7, 1989):** While concerning a company rather than a partnership, the Court's analysis of the revocation of business licenses as interference with possessions is directly applicable to partnerships.

### EU Anti-Money Laundering and Transparency

**Beneficial Ownership Disclosure:** EU anti-money laundering directives require partnerships to disclose their beneficial owners. The Sixth Anti-Money Laundering Directive (Directive (EU) 2024/1640) requires EU member states to maintain registers of beneficial owners of partnerships, with access available to competent authorities and, subject to legitimate interest, the public.

**CJEU, WM and Sovim SA (Joined Cases C-37/20 and C-601/20, 2022):** The CJEU struck down provisions of the Fifth Anti-Money Laundering Directive that required unlimited public access to beneficial ownership registers, finding that such access constituted a disproportionate interference with privacy rights under Articles 7 and 8 of the Charter of Fundamental Rights.

### European Economic Interest Grouping (EEIG)

**Council Regulation (EEC) No 2137/85:** The EEIG is the only EU-level partnership form, designed to facilitate cross-border cooperation between EU enterprises. Key features:
- Members have unlimited joint and several liability (Article 24)
- Cannot employ more than 500 persons
- Cannot be used to make profits for itself (only for its members)
- Must be registered in the member state of its official address

## Comparative Analysis

### Legal Personality

The treatment of partnership legal personality reveals fundamental doctrinal differences:

| Jurisdiction | General Partnership | Limited Partnership |
|-------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| South Korea | Yes (Commercial Code) | Yes (Commercial Code) |
| United States | Yes (RUPA § 201, entity theory) | Yes (ULPA § 104) |
| Germany | GbR: Yes (since 2024 MoPeG reform); OHG: Yes (HGB § 124) | KG: Yes (HGB § 161, § 124) |
| EU | No harmonization; follows national law | No harmonization |

### Liability Structures

Partner liability represents a core area of divergence:

- **South Korea:** Subsidiary liability for general partners (creditors must exhaust partnership assets first, Art. 212 para. 2)
- **United States:** Joint and several liability with exhaustion requirement (RUPA § 307(d))
- **Germany:** Direct joint and several liability without mandatory exhaustion (HGB § 128); partner may raise partnership defenses (§ 129)
- The German approach is the most creditor-friendly, allowing direct claims against partners without first pursuing the partnership entity

### Limited Partner Protection

The "control rule" -- whether limited partners who participate in management lose their liability protection -- has evolved differently:

- **South Korea:** Control rule retained; limited partners who participate in management are treated as general partners (Art. 278)
- **United States:** Control rule completely abolished under ULPA 2001 (§ 303); limited partners may participate in management without losing limited liability
- **Germany:** Kommanditist liability limited to Haftsumme regardless of management participation; separate prohibition on Kommanditist acting as manager
- The U.S. approach provides the most flexibility for limited partners

### Partnership Taxation

Tax treatment profoundly influences partnership form selection:

- **South Korea:** Hapmyeong Hoesa and Hapja Hoesa are taxed as corporations (entity-level taxation); Hapja Johap is treated as a pass-through
- **United States:** Partnerships are pass-through entities under Subchapter K of the Internal Revenue Code (26 U.S.C. §§ 701-777); no entity-level federal income tax
- **Germany:** OHG and KG are tax-transparent (Mitunternehmerschaft); income is attributed to partners and taxed at their individual rates; GmbH & Co. KG is especially popular for combining limited liability with partnership taxation
- The U.S. and German pass-through taxation models create strong incentives for partnership formation

### Modern Trends

Several convergence trends are observable:

1. **Limited Liability Partnerships:** All jurisdictions have introduced or expanded limited liability partnership forms to compete with LLCs and corporations
2. **Entity Theory:** The move from aggregate to entity theory is complete in the U.S. (RUPA) and has been codified in Germany (MoPeG 2024)
3. **Registration Requirements:** The German MoPeG reform establishing the Gesellschaftsregister for GbR represents convergence toward mandatory registration
4. **Beneficial Ownership Transparency:** EU requirements are pushing all member states toward greater partnership transparency
