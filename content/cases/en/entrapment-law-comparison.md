## Summary

Entrapment -- the practice by which law enforcement agents induce or encourage a person to commit a criminal offense that the person would not otherwise have committed, for the purpose of prosecuting that person -- raises fundamental questions about the proper limits of investigative authority, the integrity of the criminal justice system, and the fairness of convicting individuals whose criminal conduct was instigated by the very state apparatus tasked with preventing crime. South Korea addresses entrapment (함정수사) primarily through judicial interpretation rather than express statutory provision, with the Supreme Court (대법원) having developed a jurisprudential framework in decisions such as 2005Do1247 (2005) distinguishing between permissible "opportunity-providing" investigations (기회제공형 함정수사), where police merely provide an opportunity for a person already predisposed to commit a crime, and impermissible "crime-instigating" investigations (범의유발형 함정수사), where police actively create criminal intent in a person not otherwise disposed to offend -- with evidence obtained through the latter category excluded under Article 308-2 of the Criminal Procedure Act (형사소송법). The United States has developed the most extensive body of entrapment law of any jurisdiction, recognizing two competing approaches: the subjective test (majority federal approach, established in Sorrells v. United States, 287 U.S. 435 (1932) and Sherman v. United States, 356 U.S. 369 (1958)), which focuses on the defendant's predisposition to commit the offense, and the objective test (minority approach, advocated by Justice Roberts and later Justice Stewart in dissent), which focuses on whether government conduct would have induced a normally law-abiding person to commit the offense. Germany addresses entrapment under the concept of Tatprovokation (crime provocation) through the jurisprudence of the Federal Court of Justice (Bundesgerichtshof, BGH) and the Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht, BVerfG), requiring a substantial sentence reduction or, in extreme cases, a stay of proceedings where state agents have instigated an offense that the defendant would not otherwise have committed, with a critical 2014 Grand Chamber judgment by the ECHR in Furcht v. Germany establishing that mere sentence reduction is insufficient and that a procedural violation must result in exclusion of evidence or a comparable remedy. The European Court of Human Rights has developed the most stringent anti-entrapment framework of any system, holding in Teixeira de Castro v. Portugal (1998) and subsequent cases that police incitement resulting in a conviction violates the right to a fair trial under Article 6(1) of the Convention, regardless of the defendant's prior criminal history.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea lacks a statutory provision explicitly addressing entrapment, relying instead on the Supreme Court's jurisprudential distinction between permissible and impermissible forms of undercover investigation. The constitutional basis for entrapment claims rests on the due process principles of Article 12 of the Constitution (대한민국 헌법) and the right to a fair trial under Article 27.

### Supreme Court Framework

**Decision 2005Do1247 (2005):** The Supreme Court established the definitive framework for evaluating entrapment claims, distinguishing two categories:

**Opportunity-Providing Entrapment (기회제공형 함정수사):** Where the suspect already has criminal intent or predisposition and the police merely provide an opportunity or facilitate the commission of the planned offense, the investigation is lawful and evidence obtained is admissible. This form of entrapment is considered a legitimate investigative technique.

**Crime-Instigating Entrapment (범의유발형 함정수사):** Where the police actively create criminal intent in a person who had no prior disposition to commit the offense -- through persistent solicitation, exploitation of vulnerabilities, or deceptive inducements -- the investigation is unlawful. Evidence obtained through such methods is excluded under Article 308-2 of the Criminal Procedure Act (위법수집증거 배제법칙), and the defendant may be acquitted.

### Factors Considered

The Supreme Court has identified several factors relevant to the distinction:
- Whether the suspect was already engaged in or planning criminal activity
- The nature and intensity of police solicitation
- Whether the police exploited the suspect's personal vulnerabilities (poverty, addiction, relationship)
- The proportionality between the police conduct and the suspected criminal activity
- Whether the investigation was authorized by appropriate supervisory officials

### Application in Drug Cases

The majority of entrapment claims in South Korean courts arise in drug trafficking cases, where undercover officers or informants approach suspects offering to buy or sell controlled substances. The Supreme Court has generally upheld such operations where the suspect was already known to be involved in drug trafficking, but has found impermissible entrapment where police informants repeatedly contacted individuals with no prior drug involvement and gradually persuaded them to obtain drugs.

### Decision 2015Do2625 (2015)

In this case, the Supreme Court reversed a drug trafficking conviction where police informants had made over forty contacts with the defendant over a three-month period, progressively escalating their requests and offering increasingly large sums of money. The Court found that the persistence and intensity of the police solicitation crossed the line from opportunity-providing to crime-instigating entrapment.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has the most developed body of entrapment doctrine of any jurisdiction, with the defense recognized at both the federal and state levels. The entrapment defense is an affirmative defense that, if successfully established, results in acquittal.

### Subjective Test (Federal Majority Approach)

**Sorrells v. United States, 287 U.S. 435 (1932):** The Supreme Court first recognized the entrapment defense in a case involving a Prohibition-era defendant persuaded by an undercover officer to sell whiskey. Justice Hughes' majority opinion held that the defense turns on the defendant's predisposition: "the controlling question" is "whether the defendant is a person otherwise innocent whom the Government is seeking to punish for an alleged offense which is the product of the creative activity of its own officials."

**Sherman v. United States, 356 U.S. 369 (1958):** The Court reaffirmed the subjective test in a case where a government informant repeatedly asked the defendant, a recovering drug addict, to obtain narcotics for him. The Court reversed the conviction, finding that the government's conduct "played on the weaknesses of an innocent party and beguiled him into committing crimes which he otherwise would not have attempted."

**Jacobson v. United States, 503 U.S. 540 (1992):** The Court held that the government must prove predisposition that existed before the government's involvement, not predisposition created by the investigation itself. The case involved a 26-month undercover operation targeting the defendant for child pornography, and the Court found that the government had failed to prove predisposition independent of its own efforts.

### Objective Test (Minority Approach)

Justice Roberts' concurrence in Sorrells and Justice Stewart's concurrence in Sherman advocated an objective test focusing solely on whether government conduct would have induced a "normally law-abiding person" to commit the offense, regardless of the defendant's personal predisposition. This test is followed in several states, including California, and is endorsed by the Model Penal Code (Section 2.13).

### Sting Operations

The United States permits extensive sting operations, including:
- Undercover drug purchases (the most common context)
- Online child exploitation stings
- Public corruption investigations (e.g., Abscam)
- Terrorism sting operations (controversial post-9/11 FBI investigations)

The Supreme Court in United States v. Russell, 411 U.S. 423 (1973), held that the defense of entrapment is not available merely because the government supplied an ingredient or facilitated the offense, as long as the defendant was predisposed.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany addresses entrapment through the concept of Tatprovokation (crime provocation) or polizeiliche Tatprovokation (police crime provocation), developed through BGH case law and significantly influenced by the ECHR's Article 6 jurisprudence. German law also provides statutory regulation of undercover operations through Sections 110a-110e StPO.

### Undercover Agents (Verdeckte Ermittler) -- StPO Sections 110a-110e

Section 110a StPO authorizes the deployment of undercover agents (police officers operating under assumed identity) for the investigation of serious criminal offenses, including drug trafficking, organized crime, terrorism, and offenses against national security. The deployment requires authorization by the public prosecutor and, in certain cases, judicial approval. Undercover agents are expressly prohibited from "provoking" offenses that would not have been committed without their intervention.

### BGH Jurisprudence

The Federal Court of Justice (Bundesgerichtshof) has held that police-instigated offenses violate the principle of fair trial (faires Verfahren) derived from the rule of law (Rechtsstaatsprinzip) under Article 20(3) of the Basic Law:

**BGH, 1 StR 148/84 (1984):** The BGH established that police crime provocation constitutes a mitigating factor requiring a substantial reduction in sentence, but does not mandate acquittal or a stay of proceedings.

**BGH, 2 StR 97/14 (2015) (post-Furcht):** Following the ECHR's judgment in Furcht v. Germany, the BGH modified its approach to require that entrapment resulting from state provocation be remedied through exclusion of evidence obtained as a result of the provocation or, in exceptional cases, through a stay of proceedings, rather than merely a sentence reduction.

### Section 136a StPO -- Prohibited Methods of Examination

While not specifically addressing entrapment, Section 136a StPO prohibits obtaining statements through deception, duress, or threats, providing an additional basis for challenging evidence obtained through improper investigative methods.

### Proportionality Analysis

German courts assess entrapment through a proportionality analysis considering:
- Whether the suspect was already under reasonable suspicion before the undercover operation
- Whether the police conduct went beyond providing an opportunity to commit a crime already planned
- Whether the investigative methods were proportionate to the suspected offense
- Whether adequate supervision and authorization were in place

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Article 6(1) -- Right to a Fair Trial

The ECHR has developed the most protective anti-entrapment framework of any system, treating police incitement as a fundamental violation of the right to a fair trial:

**Teixeira de Castro v. Portugal, Application No. 25829/94, Judgment of June 9, 1998:** Two undercover police officers, without judicial authorization, approached the applicant through an intermediary and asked him to supply heroin. The applicant, who had no criminal record and was not under investigation, obtained the drugs from a third party and was arrested upon delivery. The Court unanimously found a violation of Article 6(1), holding that "the two police officers did not confine themselves to investigating Mr. Teixeira de Castro's criminal activity in an essentially passive manner but exercised an influence such as to incite the commission of the offence." The judgment established the foundational principle that police must not go beyond "essentially passive" investigation.

**Ramanauskas v. Lithuania [GC], Application No. 74420/01, Judgment of February 5, 2008:** The Grand Chamber found that an undercover operation targeting a prosecutor for corruption violated Article 6(1), establishing a two-stage test: (1) whether there were objective suspicions that the applicant had been involved in criminal activity or was predisposed to commit a criminal offense, and (2) whether the police merely "joined" the criminal activity or instigated it.

**Furcht v. Germany, Application No. 54648/09, Judgment of October 23, 2014:** The Court found that a German undercover operation targeting the applicant for drug trafficking constituted police incitement in violation of Article 6(1), and critically held that Germany's remedy of sentence reduction was insufficient. The Court required that the consequences of incitement be either exclusion of all evidence obtained as a result of the provocation or "a procedure with similar consequences," establishing that mere mitigation cannot cure a fundamental Article 6 violation.

**Bannikova v. Russia, Application No. 18757/06, Judgment of November 4, 2010:** The Court systematized the criteria for assessing police incitement: (1) reasons for the undercover operation, (2) conduct of the authorities, and (3) whether the applicant was subjected to pressure to commit the offense. The Court emphasized that the absence of a prior criminal record is a strong indicator of non-predisposition.

### Key ECHR Principles

- Police must act in an "essentially passive" manner
- Pre-existing objective suspicion of criminal activity is required before targeting an individual
- The suspect's prior criminal record does not by itself justify incitement
- Judicial or prosecutorial authorization of undercover operations is expected
- The remedy must effectively undo the prejudice caused by incitement (sentence reduction alone is insufficient)

## Comparative Analysis

### Test Focus

The jurisdictions reveal a fundamental divergence between subjective and objective approaches. The U.S. federal majority follows a subjective predisposition test focused on the defendant's character. South Korea applies a mixed approach examining both police conduct and defendant predisposition. Germany and the ECHR apply essentially objective tests focused on police conduct, with the ECHR framework being the most protective.

### Remedies

The remedies for proven entrapment vary dramatically. The United States provides a complete defense resulting in acquittal. South Korea provides evidence exclusion (which may result in acquittal). Germany historically provided only sentence reduction, but post-Furcht has moved toward evidence exclusion. The ECHR requires evidence exclusion or a comparable remedy.

### Undercover Regulation

Germany provides the most detailed statutory regulation of undercover operations through StPO Sections 110a-110e, including authorization requirements, duration limits, and reporting obligations. The United States regulates undercover operations primarily through Attorney General Guidelines rather than statute. South Korea and the ECHR provide framework principles without detailed operational regulation.

### Sting Operations

All jurisdictions permit some form of undercover operations, but the ECHR imposes the strictest limits, requiring pre-existing suspicion and essentially passive police conduct. The United States permits the most aggressive sting operations, including proactive targeting of individuals not previously suspected, subject only to the entrapment defense at trial.
