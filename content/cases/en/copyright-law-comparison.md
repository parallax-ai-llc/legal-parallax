## Summary

Copyright law protects the exclusive rights of authors and creators in their literary, artistic, musical, and other creative works, providing economic incentives for creation while balancing public access to cultural and educational materials through limitations and exceptions -- a balance that has been fundamentally challenged by digitalization, artificial intelligence, and the globalization of content distribution. South Korea's copyright system is governed by the Copyright Act (저작권법), administered by the Korea Copyright Commission (한국저작권위원회) and the Ministry of Culture, Sports and Tourism (문화체육관광부), protecting works for the life of the author plus 70 years and providing robust enforcement mechanisms including criminal penalties, statutory damages up to 50 million KRW per work infringed, and specialized online piracy countermeasures through the Korea Copyright Protection Agency (한국저작권보호원); the 2023 amendments addressed AI-related copyright issues and platform liability. The United States protects copyrights under Title 17 of the United States Code, with registration through the U.S. Copyright Office and enforcement through the federal courts, providing protection for the life of the author plus 70 years (or 95/120 years for works made for hire), with the fair use doctrine (17 U.S.C. Section 107) serving as the primary limitation on copyright, and the Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA, 1998) establishing the framework for online copyright enforcement including the notice-and-takedown system and anti-circumvention provisions. Germany protects copyright through the Urheberrechtsgesetz (UrhG, Copyright Act), which treats copyright as an inalienable personal right of the author (Urheberpersönlichkeitsrecht) that cannot be transferred -- only use rights (Nutzungsrechte) may be licensed -- and provides protection for the life of the author plus 70 years, with recent reforms including the Urheberrechts-Diensteanbieter-Gesetz (UrhDaG, 2021) transposing the EU Copyright in the Digital Single Market Directive. The EU has pursued comprehensive copyright harmonization through a series of directives, most recently Directive (EU) 2019/790 on copyright in the Digital Single Market, which introduced a press publishers' right (Article 15), platform liability for user-uploaded content (Article 17), and mandatory text and data mining exceptions for research (Article 3), while the Berne Convention (1886) and the WIPO Copyright Treaty (1996) provide the international framework.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's copyright system has undergone rapid development, driven by the country's emergence as a major content-producing nation through K-pop, K-drama, film, gaming, and digital content industries.

**Copyright Act (저작권법):** The Act, originally enacted in 1957 and comprehensively revised multiple times (most recently in 2023), protects literary, artistic, musical, dramatic, architectural, photographic, cinematographic, and graphic works, as well as computer programs (which were previously covered by a separate Computer Program Protection Act until 2009). Protection is automatic upon creation of a work in a fixed form, with no registration required.

### Neighboring Rights

The Act provides neighboring rights (저작인접권) for performers (실연자), phonogram producers (음반제작자), and broadcasting organizations (방송사업자). These rights, which last 70 years from publication or performance, are particularly significant in the Korean music industry, where the global success of K-pop has made neighboring rights licensing a major economic activity.

### Online Copyright Enforcement

South Korea has developed one of the world's most aggressive online copyright enforcement frameworks:

**Korea Copyright Protection Agency (한국저작권보호원):** This government agency monitors online platforms for copyright infringement, issues corrective recommendations, and supports criminal enforcement. The Agency operates an online monitoring system that identifies and tracks infringing content across Korean and international platforms.

**Three-Strikes System:** Article 133-2 and 133-3 of the Copyright Act authorize the Ministry of Culture to order online service providers to warn repeat infringers, suspend their accounts, and delete or disable access to infringing content. Upon three warnings, the infringer's account may be suspended for up to six months.

**Technical Protection Measures:** Articles 104-2 through 104-8 prohibit the circumvention of technological protection measures and the distribution of circumvention devices, analogous to the U.S. DMCA anti-circumvention provisions.

### AI and Copyright (2023 Amendments)

The 2023 amendments to the Copyright Act began addressing the intersection of AI and copyright, including provisions on the use of copyrighted works for AI training (text and data mining), the copyrightability of AI-generated outputs, and the liability of AI service providers for copyright infringement. The Korea Copyright Commission has published guidelines on AI-related copyright issues, and further legislative development is expected.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The U.S. copyright system provides extensive protection for original works of authorship while maintaining the broadest fair use doctrine among major jurisdictions.

**17 U.S.C. -- Copyright Act:** The Copyright Act of 1976 (effective January 1, 1978) provides the current statutory framework. Copyright protection subsists in original works of authorship fixed in a tangible medium of expression, including literary, musical, dramatic, choreographic, pictorial, graphic, sculptural, audiovisual, sound recording, and architectural works (Section 102). Ideas, procedures, processes, systems, methods of operation, concepts, principles, and discoveries are excluded from copyright protection (Section 102(b)).

### Fair Use (Section 107)

The fair use doctrine is the cornerstone of U.S. copyright limitations, providing a flexible, case-by-case defense to infringement claims based on four statutory factors:

| Factor | Consideration |
|--------|--------------|
| Purpose and character of use | Commercial vs. nonprofit/educational; transformative use (Campbell v. Acuff-Rose Music, 510 U.S. 569 (1994)) |
| Nature of the copyrighted work | Creative vs. factual; published vs. unpublished |
| Amount and substantiality of portion used | Quantitative and qualitative analysis |
| Effect on the market | Impact on the market or value of the copyrighted work |

The Supreme Court's decision in Andy Warhol Foundation v. Goldsmith, 598 U.S. 508 (2023), significantly recalibrated fair use analysis, holding that the commercial licensing of Warhol's silkscreen prints based on a photographer's portrait was not fair use, with Justice Sotomayor's majority opinion emphasizing that the first fair use factor must focus on whether the new use has a distinct purpose or character from the original rather than merely adding new expression.

### DMCA (Digital Millennium Copyright Act, 1998)

The DMCA provides two critical frameworks: Section 512 safe harbors for online service providers (OSPs) who comply with notice-and-takedown procedures, expeditiously remove infringing content upon receiving proper notices, and designate agents for receiving infringement notifications; and Section 1201 anti-circumvention provisions prohibiting circumvention of technological protection measures and trafficking in circumvention devices, subject to exemptions granted by the Copyright Office through triennial rulemaking.

### AI and Copyright

The U.S. Copyright Office has been actively engaged with AI-related copyright issues. In March 2023, the Office issued guidance stating that copyright protection requires human authorship, denying registration for AI-generated content that is not the product of human creative control. In the Thaler v. Perlmutter case (D.D.C. 2023), the court upheld this position, finding that human authorship is a prerequisite for copyright registration. The Copyright Office launched a study on AI and copyright in 2023, with a multi-part report expected to address AI training, AI-generated outputs, and the interaction between copyright and AI systems.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's copyright law reflects the continental European author's rights (Urheberrecht) tradition, which treats copyright as an inalienable personal right inseparable from the author.

**Urheberrechtsgesetz (UrhG):** The UrhG protects personal intellectual creations (persönliche geistige Schöpfungen, Section 2) in the areas of literature, science, and art. The critical distinction from Anglo-American copyright is the Schöpferprinzip (creator principle): only a natural person who creates the work can be the author (Section 7 UrhG). Legal entities cannot be original authors.

### Moral Rights (Urheberpersönlichkeitsrecht)

German copyright law provides the strongest moral rights protection among the jurisdictions compared:

**Right of Publication (Veröffentlichungsrecht, Section 12):** The author alone has the right to decide whether and how the work is published.

**Right of Attribution (Anerkennung der Urheberschaft, Section 13):** The author has the right to be recognized as the author and to determine whether the work bears a designation and what designation to use.

**Right of Integrity (Entstellungsverbot, Section 14):** The author has the right to prohibit any distortion or other impairment of the work that would jeopardize the author's legitimate intellectual or personal interests.

These moral rights are inalienable and survive the author's death, with the 70-year post-mortem protection period.

### Non-Transferability and Licensing

Under Section 29 UrhG, copyright as a whole cannot be transferred (Unübertragbarkeit des Urheberrechts). Authors can only grant use rights (Nutzungsrechte) under Sections 31-44 UrhG. Use rights may be exclusive (ausschließliche Nutzungsrechte) or non-exclusive (einfache Nutzungsrechte). This system protects authors from surrendering all rights in perpetuity, a common criticism of Anglo-American copyright assignment practices.

### UrhDaG (2021)

The Urheberrechts-Diensteanbieter-Gesetz (Copyright Service Provider Act), enacted on June 1, 2021, transposes Article 17 of the EU Digital Single Market Directive. The Act establishes that upload platform operators (Diensteanbieter) are directly liable for user-uploaded content and must obtain licenses or take measures to prevent the availability of unauthorized content. The Act includes provisions for pre-flagging of authorized uses, a complaint and redress mechanism, and limitations on the use of automated filtering to prevent over-blocking of lawful content.

### Text and Data Mining

Section 44b UrhG, transposing Article 4 of the DSM Directive, provides a general exception permitting the reproduction of lawfully accessible works for text and data mining purposes, provided that the rights holders have not reserved their rights through machine-readable means (opt-out). Section 60d provides a broader exception for scientific research text and data mining without an opt-out possibility.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Copyright Directive Framework

The EU has pursued progressive harmonization of copyright law through a series of directives:

**Information Society Directive 2001/29/EC:** The foundational EU copyright directive, establishing the exclusive rights of reproduction, communication to the public, and distribution, with an exhaustive list of permitted exceptions and limitations in Article 5.

**Digital Single Market Directive 2019/790:** The most significant recent EU copyright legislation, introducing:

**Article 3 (TDM for Research):** Mandatory exception for text and data mining carried out by research organizations and cultural heritage institutions for scientific research purposes.

**Article 4 (General TDM Exception):** Exception for text and data mining for any purpose, subject to rights holder opt-out.

**Article 15 (Press Publishers' Right):** A new two-year related right for press publishers covering the online use of press publications by information society service providers, excluding individual words, very short extracts, and private/non-commercial use.

**Article 17 (Platform Liability):** Online content-sharing service providers perform an act of communication to the public and must obtain authorization from rights holders. Providers that fail to obtain authorization are directly liable unless they demonstrate best efforts to obtain authorization, best efforts to ensure unavailability of specific works notified by rights holders, and expeditious action upon receiving notice to disable access and prevent future uploads.

### CJEU Copyright Jurisprudence

**Infopaq International A/S v. Danske Dagblades Forening (Case C-5/08, 2009):** The Court established the EU-autonomous standard of originality: a work is protected by copyright if it is the "author's own intellectual creation," reflecting the author's personality through free and creative choices.

**GS Media BV v. Sanoma Media Netherlands (Case C-160/15, 2016):** The Court held that posting a hyperlink to freely available content on another website does not constitute "communication to the public" unless the person who posted the link knew or ought to have known that the work was published without the copyright holder's consent. For links posted for profit, knowledge of the unlawful nature is presumed.

**Pelham GmbH v. Ralf Hütter (Case C-476/17, 2019):** The CJEU addressed the sampling of phonograms, holding that even very short samples constitute reproduction unless the sample is modified to such a degree as to be unrecognizable to the ear.

### ECHR and Copyright

The ECHR has addressed the intersection of copyright and freedom of expression under Article 10:

**Ashby Donald and Others v. France (Application No. 36769/08, 2013):** The Court held that the conviction of photographers for copyright infringement based on the unauthorized publication of fashion show photographs could constitute an interference with Article 10 rights but was justified as prescribed by law and necessary in a democratic society.

## Comparative Analysis

### Author's Rights versus Copyright

The fundamental philosophical divide is between the Anglo-American copyright tradition (United States, influenced in South Korea) and the continental European author's rights tradition (Germany, EU framework). The copyright tradition treats copyright primarily as an economic right that can be fully transferred, while the author's rights tradition treats it as an inalienable personal right of the author. This distinction has profound practical consequences for the entertainment and publishing industries, affecting the scope of rights that creators can transfer and the protections they retain.

### Fair Use versus Enumerated Exceptions

The limitation and exception framework represents a critical divergence. The United States provides the most flexible approach through the open-ended fair use doctrine, which adapts to new technologies and uses without legislative amendment. Germany and the EU rely on enumerated (closed-list) exceptions, providing greater legal certainty but less adaptability. South Korea follows the enumerated exception model but has introduced limited fair use-like provisions in recent amendments. The tension between flexibility and certainty has intensified with AI-related copyright issues, where the adaptability of fair use may provide advantages over closed-list systems.

### Platform Liability

The approach to platform liability for user-uploaded content has diverged significantly. The U.S. DMCA Section 512 provides a safe harbor based on notice-and-takedown, placing the burden primarily on rights holders to identify infringement. The EU's Article 17 (and Germany's UrhDaG) establish direct platform liability with licensing and filtering obligations, placing the burden primarily on platforms. South Korea's three-strikes system adds a punitive dimension targeting repeat infringers. These differences create significant compliance challenges for global platforms operating across jurisdictions.

### AI and Copyright

All jurisdictions are grappling with AI's impact on copyright law, but from different starting positions. The United States has the most developed case law and guidance requiring human authorship. Germany and the EU have established text and data mining exceptions that directly address AI training on copyrighted works. South Korea's 2023 amendments represent early legislative engagement with AI copyright issues. The absence of international harmonization on AI and copyright creates risks of regulatory fragmentation.
