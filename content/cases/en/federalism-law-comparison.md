## Summary

The choice between federalism and a unitary state structure -- whether governmental authority is constitutionally divided between a central government and constituent regional entities with autonomous powers, or concentrated in a central government that may delegate but retains ultimate authority -- represents one of the most consequential decisions in constitutional design, shaping everything from legislative competence and fiscal policy to cultural autonomy and crisis response. South Korea is a unitary state (단일국가) under Article 1 of the Constitution (대한민국 헌법), with local autonomy guaranteed under Chapter VIII (Articles 117-118) through a system of local self-government (지방자치) that was suppressed during authoritarian periods, revived with the first direct elections for local councils in 1991 and local executive heads in 1995, and significantly strengthened by the 2022 enactment of the Comprehensive Local Autonomy Act (지방자치분권 및 지역균형발전에 관한 특별법) and constitutional amendment proposals for stronger decentralization. The United States is the prototypical federal state, with the Constitution establishing a dual sovereignty structure in which the federal government exercises enumerated powers (Article I, Section 8) and states retain reserved powers (Tenth Amendment), a framework continuously reinterpreted by the Supreme Court from McCulloch v. Maryland, 17 U.S. 316 (1819) through National Federation of Independent Business v. Sebelius, 567 U.S. 519 (2012). Germany is a federal state (Bundesstaat) under Article 20(1) of the Grundgesetz, with sixteen Länder exercising significant autonomous legislative, executive, and judicial authority; the Grundgesetz elaborately allocates competences between the Federation (Bund) and the Länder through exclusive federal legislation (Articles 71, 73), concurrent legislation (Articles 72, 74), and exclusive Länder competence, with the Bundesrat providing Länder participation in federal legislation and the Basic Law's eternity clause (Article 79(3)) protecting the federal principle from constitutional amendment. At the European level, the EU operates on principles of conferral, subsidiarity, and proportionality (Article 5 TEU), creating a multi-level governance system that interacts with both federal and unitary member states, while the Council of Europe's European Charter of Local Self-Government (1985) establishes minimum standards for local and regional autonomy across all member states.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea is constitutionally defined as a unitary state. Article 1(1) declares the Republic of Korea to be a democratic republic, and the constitutional structure vests primary governmental authority in the central government. Chapter VIII (Articles 117-118) guarantees local self-government (지방자치):

**Article 117(1):** Local governments shall deal with matters pertaining to the welfare of local residents, manage properties, and may enact provisions relating to local autonomy within the limit of laws and regulations (법령의 범위 안에서 자치에 관한 규정을 제정할 수 있다).

**Article 118:** Local governments shall have councils (지방의회). The organization, powers, election of members, election procedures for heads of local governments, and other matters pertaining to the organization and operation of local governments shall be determined by law.

### Historical Development

| Period | Status of Local Autonomy |
|--------|------------------------|

| 1961-1987 | Local autonomy suspended under military governments; Local Autonomy Act provisions frozen |
| 1988-1990 | Constitutional restoration; preparation for implementation |
| 1991 | First direct elections for local councils (지방의회) |
| 1995 | First direct elections for local government heads (단체장) |
| 2006 | Jeju Special Self-Governing Province (제주특별자치도) established |
| 2022 | Comprehensive Local Autonomy Act enacted |

### Current Structure

South Korea has a two-tier local government system:

**Upper-Tier (광역자치단체):** 1 special city (Seoul), 6 metropolitan cities, 1 special self-governing city (Sejong), 8 provinces, and 1 special self-governing province (Jeju) -- 17 total.

**Lower-Tier (기초자치단체):** 226 cities (시), counties (군), and autonomous districts (자치구).

### Decentralization Reforms

The Local Autonomy Act (지방자치법) was comprehensively revised effective January 13, 2022. Key reforms included expanding the scope of local legislative authority, strengthening the local council's oversight powers, and introducing a local autonomy ombudsman system. The Comprehensive Decentralization and Regional Balanced Development Act (지방자치분권 및 지역균형발전에 관한 특별법, 2022) established a framework for transferring central government functions to local governments and promoting balanced regional development.

Despite these reforms, South Korea's fiscal structure remains heavily centralized. Local governments depend on central government transfers for approximately 60 percent of their revenue, and the scope of local taxation authority remains limited. The National Tax Service (국세청) collects approximately 78 percent of total government tax revenue, with local taxes accounting for only approximately 22 percent.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States Constitution establishes a federal system through the allocation of enumerated powers to the federal government (Article I, Section 8), the Supremacy Clause (Article VI), and the Tenth Amendment, which provides: "The powers not delegated to the United States by the Constitution, nor prohibited by it to the States, are reserved to the States respectively, or to the people."

### Key Constitutional Principles

**Enumerated Powers:** Congress may exercise only those powers specifically granted by the Constitution, including the power to regulate interstate commerce (Commerce Clause), to tax and spend for the general welfare (Taxing and Spending Clause), and to make all laws "necessary and proper" for carrying into execution the enumerated powers (Necessary and Proper Clause).

**Supremacy Clause:** Article VI establishes that the Constitution, federal statutes, and treaties constitute the "supreme Law of the Land," preempting conflicting state law.

**Anti-Commandeering:** The Tenth Amendment prohibits the federal government from compelling state legislatures to enact federal regulatory programs (New York v. United States, 505 U.S. 144 (1992)) or from conscripting state executive officers to enforce federal law (Printz v. United States, 521 U.S. 898 (1997); Murphy v. NCAA, 584 U.S. 453 (2018)).

### Commerce Clause Jurisprudence

| Case | Year | Holding | Impact on Federalism |

| NFIB v. Sebelius | 2012 | Individual mandate exceeds commerce power (but valid as tax) | New limits on commerce power |

### Cooperative Federalism

Despite the formal dual sovereignty structure, American federalism operates largely through cooperative mechanisms. Federal spending programs condition grants on state compliance with federal policy preferences, as upheld in South Dakota v. Dole, 483 U.S. 203 (1987). The Affordable Care Act's Medicaid expansion, however, was struck down as unconstitutionally coercive in NFIB v. Sebelius because it threatened to revoke all existing Medicaid funding rather than merely withholding new funding.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Article 20(1) of the Grundgesetz declares that the Federal Republic of Germany is a democratic and social federal state. Article 79(3) (Ewigkeitsklausel) protects this federal structure from constitutional amendment. The allocation of competences between the Federation (Bund) and the Länder is governed by Articles 30, 70-74, and 83-91.

### Legislative Competence Distribution

**Article 30 (Presumption):** The exercise of state powers and the discharge of state functions is a matter for the Länder insofar as the Grundgesetz does not otherwise provide or permit.

**Exclusive Federal Legislation (Articles 71, 73):** Areas including foreign affairs, defense, citizenship, currency, customs, air transport, postal and telecommunications, and nuclear energy.

**Concurrent Legislation (Articles 72, 74):** Areas including civil law, criminal law, labor law, economic regulation, environmental protection, and social welfare. The Federation may legislate in these areas, and the Länder may legislate only insofar as the Federation has not exercised its competence.

**Exclusive Länder Competence:** Education, cultural affairs, police, and local government -- areas where the Grundgesetz does not grant federal competence. Education is the most politically significant area of Länder autonomy, with each Land maintaining its own school system, curricula, and teacher training standards.

### Fiscal Federalism (Finanzausgleich)

Germany's fiscal equalization system (Finanzausgleich) is among the most elaborate in the world:

| Level | Mechanism |
|-------|-----------|
| Tax distribution | Shared taxes (Gemeinschaftsteuern) -- income tax, corporate tax, VAT -- distributed between Bund and Länder according to constitutional formulas |
| Horizontal equalization | Revenue redistribution among Länder to ensure roughly equal financial capacity |
| Federal supplementary grants | Additional transfers from the Bund to financially weaker Länder (Ergänzungszuweisungen) |

The 2017 reform of fiscal equalization (effective 2020) consolidated the system, replacing the previous three-step equalization (primary distribution, horizontal Länderfinanzausgleich, and federal supplementary grants) with a two-step system of enhanced VAT distribution and federal supplementary grants.

### The Bundesrat

The Bundesrat is composed of members of Land governments, with each Land receiving three to six votes based on population. Unlike the U.S. Senate, Bundesrat members are not elected but serve as delegates of their Land governments. Each Land must cast its votes as a block. The Bundesrat's consent is required for legislation affecting Land interests (Zustimmungsgesetze), and it may object to other legislation (Einspruchsgesetze), though such objections can be overridden by the Bundestag.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Principles of Competence

Article 5 TEU establishes three principles governing the exercise of EU competences:

**Conferral (Article 5(1)-(2)):** The EU may act only within the limits of the competences conferred upon it by the member states in the treaties.

**Subsidiarity (Article 5(3)):** In areas of non-exclusive competence, the EU shall act only if the objectives of the proposed action cannot be sufficiently achieved by the member states and can be better achieved at the EU level.

**Proportionality (Article 5(4)):** The content and form of EU action shall not exceed what is necessary to achieve the objectives of the treaties.

### Competence Categories

| Category | Treaty Basis | Examples |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Exclusive EU (Art. 3 TFEU) | Customs union, competition rules, monetary policy (eurozone), common commercial policy | |
| Shared (Art. 4 TFEU) | Internal market, social policy, environment, consumer protection, transport, energy | |
| Supporting (Art. 6 TFEU) | Culture, tourism, education, civil protection, industry | |

### European Charter of Local Self-Government

The Council of Europe's European Charter of Local Self-Government (CETS No. 122, 1985), ratified by all 46 Council of Europe member states, establishes minimum standards for local autonomy, including:

- Article 2: The principle of local self-government shall be recognized in domestic legislation and, where practicable, in the constitution.
- Article 3: Local self-government denotes the right and ability of local authorities to regulate and manage a substantial share of public affairs under their own responsibility and in the interests of the local population.
- Article 4: Local authorities shall have full discretion to exercise their initiative with regard to any matter not excluded from their competence.
- Article 9: Local authorities shall be entitled to adequate financial resources of their own.

South Korea is not a member of the Council of Europe but has observer status with the Congress of Local and Regional Authorities.

### Respect for National Identity

Article 4(2) TEU requires the EU to respect the national identities of member states, "inherent in their fundamental structures, political and constitutional, inclusive of regional and local self-government." This provision has been invoked by constitutional courts -- particularly the German BVerfG -- to assert limits on EU integration, arguing that the transfer of competences must not undermine the constitutional identity of the member state, including its federal structure.

## Comparative Analysis

### Unitary vs. Federal Trajectories

South Korea and Germany represent contrasting trajectories. South Korea has gradually decentralized from a highly centralized unitary state, reviving local autonomy suppressed during authoritarian periods and steadily expanding local government powers since 1991. Germany has oscillated between centralization and decentralization within its federal framework, with the Federalism Reforms of 2006 and 2009 seeking to disentangle Bund and Länder competences and enhance Länder autonomy after decades of creeping centralization. The United States has experienced long-term centralization within its federal framework, particularly since the New Deal era, though recent jurisprudence has imposed new limits on federal power.

### Fiscal Dimension

Fiscal autonomy is the clearest differentiator between federal and unitary systems. U.S. states possess broad independent taxation authority. German Länder share tax revenues through the Finanzausgleich but have limited independent tax-setting power. South Korean local governments rely heavily on central transfers and have narrow taxation authority. The EU collects no taxes directly, relying on member state contributions and customs duties.

### Subsidiarity as Organizing Principle

The EU's subsidiarity principle finds functional equivalents in all three national jurisdictions. The U.S. Tenth Amendment serves a similar purpose by reserving non-delegated powers to the states. Germany's Article 72(2) conditions federal concurrent legislation on necessity for uniform conditions. South Korea's Local Autonomy Act increasingly recognizes the principle that matters of local concern should be handled locally. However, enforcement of subsidiarity remains challenging across all systems: the EU's subsidiarity mechanism (Protocol No. 2) has produced limited results, the U.S. Tenth Amendment's judicially enforceable content remains contested, and South Korea's local autonomy provisions remain subject to broad central legislative override.
