## Summary

Illegal wiretapping -- the unauthorized interception of private communications by wire, electronic, or other means -- sits at the intersection of criminal law, privacy rights, and national security, raising fundamental questions about the proper balance between law enforcement needs and individual privacy in an era of rapidly evolving telecommunications technology. South Korea regulates wiretapping through the Protection of Communications Secrets Act (통신비밀보호법, enacted 1993), which prohibits the interception of communications without judicial authorization and prescribes penalties of up to 10 years imprisonment for illegal wiretapping, while permitting court-authorized surveillance for national security and criminal investigation purposes; the law gained renewed public attention following the 2005 exposure of the National Intelligence Service's (국가정보원) massive illegal wiretapping program targeting politicians, journalists, and businesspeople, which led to criminal prosecutions of senior intelligence officials and significant amendments strengthening oversight. The United States addresses wiretapping through a comprehensive federal framework anchored by Title III of the Omnibus Crime Control and Safe Streets Act of 1968 (18 U.S.C. Sections 2510-2522, commonly known as the "Wiretap Act"), the Electronic Communications Privacy Act of 1986 (ECPA), and the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act of 1978 (FISA), with the landmark Supreme Court decision in Katz v. United States, 389 U.S. 347 (1967) establishing that the Fourth Amendment protects "people, not places" and requiring a warrant for electronic surveillance; the post-9/11 expansion of surveillance authorities through the USA PATRIOT Act (2001) and subsequent revelations by Edward Snowden in 2013 of the NSA's mass surveillance programs triggered fundamental reassessment of surveillance law. Germany provides robust wiretapping protections through Article 10 of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz), which expressly protects the secrecy of telecommunications, supplemented by Section 201 StGB (violation of the confidentiality of the spoken word), Section 202a StGB (data espionage), and the comprehensive Telekommunikation-Telemedien-Datenschutz-Gesetz (TTDSG); the Federal Constitutional Court's landmark census decision (Volkszählungsurteil, 1983) established the fundamental right to informational self-determination (informationelle Selbstbestimmung), and its 2008 decision created a new fundamental right to the integrity and confidentiality of information technology systems. The ECHR has developed extensive surveillance jurisprudence through Article 8 (right to respect for private life and correspondence), with landmark cases from Klass and Others v. Germany (1978) through Big Brother Watch and Others v. United Kingdom (Grand Chamber, 2021) establishing detailed requirements for the lawfulness, necessity, and proportionality of state surveillance, while EU law addresses communications privacy through the ePrivacy Directive (2002/58/EC) and the landmark Court of Justice decisions in Digital Rights Ireland (2014) and La Quadrature du Net (2020) on data retention.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

**Protection of Communications Secrets Act (통신비밀보호법, Act No. 4650 of December 27, 1993, as amended):**

**Article 3 -- Prohibition of Wiretapping:** "No person shall intercept the communications of another, except as expressly provided by this Act. The secrecy of communications shall be guaranteed."

**Article 5 -- Permitted Interception for Criminal Investigation:** Interception is permitted when: (1) there is probable cause to believe that a crime punishable by imprisonment of 3+ years has been or is being committed; (2) other investigative methods have been exhausted or would be ineffective; and (3) a court warrant has been obtained.

**Article 7 -- National Security Surveillance:** The National Intelligence Service (국가정보원) may conduct surveillance for national security purposes with authorization from a High Court chief judge. For communications involving foreign states, the NIS director may authorize surveillance with post-facto notification to the court.

### Penalties

| Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|
| Illegal wiretapping (Art. 16) | Up to 10 years imprisonment |
| Illegal disclosure of intercepted communications (Art. 16) | Up to 10 years imprisonment |
| Unauthorized access to communications data (Art. 17) | Up to 5 years or fine up to 30 million KRW |
| Violation of notification requirements | Up to 3 years or fine up to 10 million KRW |

### The NIS Wiretapping Scandal (2005)

In July 2005, it was revealed that the National Intelligence Service (then the National Intelligence Service, 국가정보원) had conducted massive illegal wiretapping operations from 1998 to 2004:

**Scope:** The program targeted approximately 1,800 individuals, including politicians, journalists, businesspeople, and civic leaders. The surveillance was conducted without judicial authorization through direct connections to telecommunications networks.

**Criminal Proceedings:** Several senior NIS officials were prosecuted and convicted. In 2005, the Seoul Central District Court sentenced former NIS Deputy Director Shin Gun to imprisonment for illegal wiretapping.

**Reforms:** The scandal prompted significant amendments to the Protection of Communications Secrets Act in 2005:
- Enhanced judicial oversight requirements
- Mandatory notification to surveillance targets after investigation concludes
- Stricter limitations on the duration of surveillance warrants
- Creation of the Communications Standards Mediation Committee

### Recent Developments

**Constitutional Court Decision 2016헌마388 (2018):** The Court held that certain provisions of the Protection of Communications Secrets Act allowing broad metadata collection were unconstitutional, as they did not adequately limit the scope of data collection or provide sufficient oversight.

**2020 Amendment:** Further strengthened privacy protections by requiring enhanced notification to targets of surveillance and limiting the retention period for intercepted communications data.

## United States

### Legal Framework

**Title III -- Wiretap Act (18 U.S.C. Sections 2510-2522, 1968):** Enacted as Title III of the Omnibus Crime Control and Safe Streets Act, this statute establishes the primary framework for lawful interception of wire, oral, and electronic communications.

**Key Requirements for a Title III Warrant ("Super Warrant"):**
1. Probable cause that a specified predicate offense has been, is being, or is about to be committed
2. Probable cause that communications concerning the offense will be intercepted
3. Normal investigative procedures have failed, are reasonably unlikely to succeed, or are too dangerous
4. Probable cause that the targeted facility or place is used by the subject
5. Minimization procedures to limit interception of non-relevant communications

**Electronic Communications Privacy Act (ECPA, 1986):** Extended wiretap protections to electronic communications (email, digital transmissions) and enacted the Stored Communications Act (18 U.S.C. Sections 2701-2712).

**Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act (FISA, 1978):** Created the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court (FISC) to authorize surveillance for foreign intelligence purposes. Amended significantly by the USA PATRIOT Act (2001) and the FISA Amendments Act (2008).

### Constitutional Foundation

**Katz v. United States, 389 U.S. 347 (1967):** Justice Stewart's majority opinion established that the Fourth Amendment protects "people, not places" and applies wherever a person has a "reasonable expectation of privacy." Justice Harlan's concurrence articulated the two-pronged test: (1) the person exhibited an actual (subjective) expectation of privacy, and (2) the expectation is one that society is prepared to recognize as reasonable.

**Carpenter v. United States, 585 U.S. 296 (2018):** The Court held that the government's acquisition of cell-site location information (CSLI) constitutes a Fourth Amendment search requiring a warrant, recognizing that digital surveillance technologies raise unique privacy concerns.

### The Snowden Revelations (2013)

Edward Snowden's disclosure of classified NSA documents revealed:
- **PRISM:** Program collecting communications data directly from major technology companies
- **Section 215 Metadata Program:** Bulk collection of domestic telephone metadata
- **UPSTREAM:** Interception of internet communications from fiber-optic cables

**Legislative Response -- USA FREEDOM Act (2015):** Ended bulk metadata collection by requiring the government to obtain specific court orders for telecommunications metadata. Established the amicus curiae process for FISC proceedings.

### One-Party Consent

Federal law permits one party to a conversation to record or intercept the communication without the other party's knowledge (18 U.S.C. Section 2511(2)(c)). However, approximately 12 states require all-party consent:

| Consent Requirement | States |
|---------------------|--------|
| One-party consent | ~38 states + federal |
| All-party consent | California, Connecticut, Florida, Illinois, Maryland, Massachusetts, Montana, New Hampshire, Oregon, Pennsylvania, Washington, others |

## Germany

### Legal Framework

**Article 10 GG -- Secrecy of Telecommunications:** "(1) The privacy of correspondence, posts and telecommunications shall be inviolable. (2) Restrictions may only be ordered pursuant to a law." This constitutional provision provides express protection for telecommunications privacy.

**Section 201 StGB -- Violation of Confidentiality of the Spoken Word (Verletzung der Vertraulichkeit des Wortes):** Criminalizes the unauthorized recording of a person's spoken words not intended for the listener's ears (up to 3 years imprisonment or fine).

**Section 202a StGB -- Data Espionage (Ausspähen von Daten):** Criminalizes unauthorized access to data not intended for the person and specially secured against unauthorized access (up to 3 years or fine).

**Section 202b StGB -- Interception of Data (Abfangen von Daten):** Criminalizes the unauthorized interception of data not intended for the interceptor (up to 2 years or fine).

### G10 Act (Artikel 10-Gesetz)

The G10 Act regulates surveillance by intelligence services, implementing the constitutional requirement under Article 10(2) GG:

**Judicial and Parliamentary Oversight:**
- Strategic surveillance requires authorization by the G10 Commission (G10-Kommission), a parliamentary oversight body
- Individual surveillance measures require a judicial order
- The G10 Commission has authority to review and approve, modify, or reject surveillance requests

### Federal Constitutional Court Jurisprudence

**Volkszählungsurteil (Census Decision), BVerfG, December 15, 1983 (1 BvR 209/83):** Established the fundamental right to informational self-determination (informationelle Selbstbestimmung), derived from the general personality right (Article 2(1) in conjunction with Article 1(1) GG). This decision established that individuals have the right to determine the disclosure and use of their personal data.

**Online-Durchsuchung Decision, BVerfG, February 27, 2008 (1 BvR 370/07):** Created a new fundamental right to the integrity and confidentiality of information technology systems (Grundrecht auf Gewährleistung der Vertraulichkeit und Integrität informationstechnischer Systeme). This right protects against covert infiltration of computers and other information technology systems.

**Data Retention Decision, BVerfG, March 2, 2010 (1 BvR 256/08):** The Court declared the German Data Retention Act unconstitutional, holding that blanket retention of telecommunications metadata for six months violated Article 10 GG and the right to informational self-determination.

**BVerfG, May 19, 2020 (1 BvR 2835/17 -- BND Act):** The Court ruled that the German foreign intelligence service's (BND) surveillance of foreign nationals' telecommunications violated fundamental rights under the Basic Law, even when conducted outside German territory.

### Penalties

| Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|
| Section 201 (confidentiality of spoken word) | Up to 3 years or fine |
| Section 202a (data espionage) | Up to 3 years or fine |
| Section 202b (interception of data) | Up to 2 years or fine |
| Section 206 (violation of postal/telecom secrecy) | Up to 5 years or fine |
| Violation of G10 Act | Up to 2 years or fine |

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR has developed the most comprehensive surveillance jurisprudence of any international court:

**Klass and Others v. Germany (Application No. 5029/71, Judgment of September 6, 1978):** The first major surveillance case, in which the Court held that secret surveillance measures are compatible with Article 8 only if they are "strictly necessary" and subject to adequate safeguards. The Court recognized that the existence of legislation permitting surveillance may itself constitute an interference with Article 8 rights.

**Malone v. United Kingdom (Application No. 8691/79, Judgment of August 2, 1984):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 because UK law lacked a clear statutory framework governing communications interception. This decision prompted the UK to enact the Interception of Communications Act 1985.

**Weber and Saravia v. Germany (Application No. 54934/00, Decision of June 29, 2006):** The Court established six minimum safeguards for surveillance measures: (1) the nature of offenses that may give rise to interception; (2) a definition of the categories of people subject to interception; (3) a limit on the duration of interception; (4) procedure for examining, using, and storing data; (5) precautions for communicating data to other parties; and (6) circumstances for erasure or destruction of data.

**Big Brother Watch and Others v. United Kingdom (Application No. 58170/13, Grand Chamber Judgment of May 25, 2021):** The Grand Chamber assessed the UK's bulk interception regime revealed by the Snowden disclosures. The Court found violations of Articles 8 and 10, holding that while bulk interception per se does not violate the Convention, the UK regime lacked adequate safeguards, particularly regarding the selection of bearers for interception, search criteria, and the selection of intercepted material for examination.

### EU Framework

**ePrivacy Directive (2002/58/EC):** Article 5 requires member states to ensure the confidentiality of communications and related traffic data and prohibits listening, tapping, storage, or other kinds of interception without user consent, with exceptions for lawful interception.

**Digital Rights Ireland (CJEU, Joined Cases C-293/12 and C-594/12, April 8, 2014):** The Court of Justice declared the EU Data Retention Directive (2006/24/EC) invalid, holding that the blanket retention of communications metadata constituted a disproportionate interference with the fundamental rights to privacy and data protection under Articles 7 and 8 of the EU Charter.

**La Quadrature du Net (CJEU, Joined Cases C-511/18, C-512/18, C-520/18, October 6, 2020):** The Court held that general and indiscriminate retention of traffic and location data is precluded by EU law, but permitted targeted retention based on objective criteria defining categories of data, means of communication, and persons concerned.

## Comparative Analysis

### Scope of Protection

All four systems protect communications privacy but differ in scope:

- **Germany:** Broadest protection -- constitutional right covering all telecommunications; separate right for IT systems
- **ECHR:** Comprehensive Article 8 framework with detailed safeguards requirements
- **South Korea:** Strong statutory framework but challenged by national security exceptions
- **United States:** Fourth Amendment protection but significant national security carve-outs (FISA)

### Oversight Mechanisms

| Jurisdiction | Criminal Investigation | National Security |
|-------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| South Korea | Judicial warrant | High Court authorization; post-facto court notification |
| United States | Title III "super warrant" | FISA Court (secret proceedings) |
| Germany | Judicial order | G10 Commission (parliamentary oversight) |
| ECHR | Requires independent oversight | Independent authority required (Weber and Saravia) |

### Data Retention

The permissibility of mandatory data retention has been one of the most contentious issues:

- **EU:** Blanket retention invalidated (Digital Rights Ireland); targeted retention permitted (La Quadrature)
- **Germany:** Mandatory retention declared unconstitutional by BVerfG
- **United States:** No mandatory retention; warrant required for CSLI (Carpenter)
- **South Korea:** Metadata retention permitted under statute; partially struck down by Constitutional Court
