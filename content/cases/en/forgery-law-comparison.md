## Summary

Forgery -- the fraudulent creation, alteration, or use of a false document with intent to deceive -- is universally recognized as a serious criminal offense that undermines trust in written instruments, public records, and legal transactions. While counterfeiting targets currency specifically, forgery encompasses a far broader category of documentary fraud, including falsification of contracts, identity documents, official certificates, wills, deeds, and electronic records. South Korea addresses forgery through a detailed statutory scheme in Chapter XX of the Criminal Act (Articles 225-237), distinguishing sharply between public and private document forgery and imposing penalties of up to ten years' imprisonment for forgery of public documents. The United States criminalizes forgery at both the federal and state levels: federal statutes in 18 U.S.C. Chapter 25 (sections 471-514) cover forgery of government obligations, passports, and federal documents with penalties reaching 20-25 years, while state forgery laws -- often modeled on the Model Penal Code section 224.1 -- treat forgery as a felony with varying degrees of severity. Germany's Strafgesetzbuch (StGB) addresses forgery comprehensively in Chapter Twenty-Three (sections 267-282), anchored by section 267 (Urkundenfaelschung), which punishes the creation of false documents, falsification of genuine documents, and use of falsified documents with up to five years' imprisonment, escalating to ten years for serious cases. At the supranational level, the European Union has harmonized certain aspects of documentary fraud through Directive 2014/62/EU on currency counterfeiting and various framework decisions addressing identity document fraud, while the European Court of Human Rights (ECHR) shapes forgery prosecution through its jurisprudence on fair trial rights (Article 6), the principle of legality (Article 7), and the right to private life (Article 8) under the European Convention on Human Rights.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's forgery laws are codified in Chapter XX of the Criminal Act (hyeongbeop), spanning Articles 225 through 237-2. The statutory framework draws a fundamental distinction between forgery of public documents (Articles 225-230) and forgery of private documents (Articles 231-236), with public document forgery carrying substantially harsher penalties reflecting the greater harm to public trust and administrative integrity.

**Section 1 -- Forgery of Public Documents (Articles 225-230):**

- **Article 225 -- Forgery or Alteration of Public Documents:** A person who, for the purpose of uttering, counterfeits or alters a document or drawing of a public official or a public office shall be punished by imprisonment with labor for not more than ten years.
- **Article 226 -- Forgery of Public Document by Assuming False Capacity:** A person who, by falsely assuming the capacity of a public official, drafts a document or drawing of a public office shall be punished by imprisonment with labor for not more than seven years.
- **Article 227 -- False Preparation of Public Documents:** A public official who, in the course of duty, makes a false entry in a document or drawing with respect to an officially certified fact shall be punished by imprisonment with labor for not more than seven years.
- **Article 227-2 -- False Preparation or Alteration of Public Electromagnetic Records:** A person with the intention of disrupting official business who falsely prepares or alters electromagnetic records of public officials or public offices shall be punished by imprisonment with labor for not more than ten years.
- **Article 228 -- False Entry in Notarial Documents:** A person who causes a public official to make a false entry in a notarial deed, original copy of a register, or other authentic document by making a false declaration, with respect to a matter to be registered, shall be punished by imprisonment with labor for not more than five years or by a fine not exceeding ten million won.
- **Article 229 -- Uttering of Falsified Public Documents:** A person who utters any document, drawing, or electromagnetic record falsified under Articles 225-228 shall be subject to the same punishment as for the underlying forgery offense.
- **Article 230 -- Illegal Use of Public Documents:** A person who illegally uses a public document or drawing of another person shall be punished by imprisonment with or without labor for not more than two years or by a fine not exceeding five million won.

**Section 2 -- Forgery of Private Documents (Articles 231-236):**

- **Article 231 -- Forgery or Alteration of Private Documents:** A person who, for the purpose of uttering, counterfeits or alters a document or drawing concerning rights, obligations, or proof of facts shall be punished by imprisonment with labor for not more than five years or by a fine not exceeding ten million won.
- **Article 232 -- Drafting of Private Document by Assuming False Capacity:** A person who drafts a private document by falsely assuming another person's capacity or authority shall be punished by imprisonment with labor for not more than five years or by a fine not exceeding ten million won.
- **Article 232-2 -- Falsification or Alteration of Private Electromagnetic Records:** A person who, for the purpose of disrupting another person's business, falsely prepares or alters electromagnetic records concerning rights, obligations, or proof of facts shall be punished by imprisonment with labor for not more than five years or by a fine not exceeding ten million won.
- **Article 233 -- Preparation of False Medical Certificate:** A physician, dentist, oriental medical doctor, pharmacist, or midwife who issues a false certificate, examination report, or death certificate shall be punished by imprisonment with labor for not more than three years, suspension of qualifications for not more than seven years, or a fine not exceeding thirty million won.
- **Article 234 -- Uttering of Falsified Private Documents:** A person who utters any document, drawing, or electromagnetic record falsified under Articles 231-233 shall be subject to the same punishment as for the underlying forgery offense.
- **Article 237-2 -- Copying of Documents:** A copy of a document or drawing reproduced using an electronic photocopier, facsimile transmitter, or other similar device is treated as a document or drawing for the purposes of the forgery provisions.

### Enforcement and Practice

South Korean courts apply a strict interpretation of "uttering" (haengsa) -- the act of presenting a forged document as genuine to another person. The purpose of uttering is a required element for most forgery offenses: the prosecution must prove that the defendant created or altered the document with the intention that it would be used in legal or business transactions as though it were authentic.

Korean prosecutors maintain high conviction rates in forgery cases, and sentences tend toward the middle-to-upper range of statutory penalties for offenses involving public documents. The distinction between public and private documents is critically important: forgery of a public document such as a government-issued certificate, court order, or administrative permit is treated as a qualitatively more serious offense than forgery of a private contract or receipt.

### Landmark Cases

**Supreme Court Decision 2004Do5904 (2005) -- Scope of "Document":** The Korean Supreme Court clarified that the concept of a "document" in forgery provisions extends to any written instrument that embodies a statement of fact or expression of intent and is attributable to a specific issuer. This decision broadened the scope of forgery law to cover non-traditional written instruments, including certain electronic printouts, when they function as evidence in legal transactions.

**Seoul Central District Court -- Diploma Forgery Ring (2010s):** A major prosecution targeted a network that mass-produced counterfeit university diplomas and professional certifications. The organizers were convicted under Article 225 (forgery of public documents) because Korean university diplomas are considered documents of a public institution. Sentences ranged from three to seven years' imprisonment, reflecting the court's view that systematic diploma fraud undermines public confidence in the credentialing system.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States addresses forgery through a dual system of federal and state criminal statutes. At the federal level, forgery is primarily codified in Title 18 of the United States Code, Chapter 25 (Counterfeiting and Forgery, sections 471-514), with additional provisions in chapters covering passport fraud (sections 1541-1546), mail fraud (section 1341), and identity documents (section 1028). Each of the fifty states maintains its own forgery statute, many of which draw on the Model Penal Code section 224.1.

**Key Federal Statutes:**

- **18 U.S.C. section 471 -- Obligations or Securities of the United States:** Whoever, with intent to defraud, falsely makes, forges, counterfeits, or alters any obligation or other security of the United States shall be fined under this title or imprisoned not more than 20 years, or both.
- **18 U.S.C. section 495 -- Contracts, Deeds, and Powers of Attorney:** Whoever falsely makes, alters, forges, or counterfeits any deed, power of attorney, order, certificate, receipt, contract, or other writing for the purpose of obtaining or receiving any sum of money from the United States shall be fined or imprisoned not more than ten years, or both.
- **18 U.S.C. section 1028 -- Fraud and Related Activity in Connection with Identification Documents:** Criminalizes the production, transfer, or possession of false identification documents. Penalties range up to 15 years, increasing to 20 years if used to facilitate drug trafficking and 25 years if used to facilitate international terrorism.
- **18 U.S.C. section 1543 -- Forgery or False Use of Passport:** Whoever falsely makes, forges, counterfeits, mutilates, or alters any passport or instrument purporting to be a passport, with intent that the same may be used, or who willfully uses or furnishes such a document to another, shall be fined or imprisoned not more than 25 years (if related to international terrorism), 20 years (if related to drug trafficking), or 10 years (general cases).
- **18 U.S.C. section 1546 -- Fraud and Misuse of Visas, Permits, and Other Documents:** Criminalizes forging, counterfeiting, altering, or falsely making immigration documents, with penalties of up to 10 years (general), 15 years (terrorism-related), or 25 years (drug trafficking).
- **18 U.S.C. section 514 -- Fictitious Obligations:** Addresses the creation and trafficking of fictitious financial instruments, punishable by up to 25 years.

### Elements of the Offense

Under federal law and the prevailing common law tradition, the prosecution must establish:

1. **Making, altering, or uttering a false writing:** The defendant must have created a false document, altered a genuine one, or presented a forged document as authentic.
2. **The writing must be apparently capable of defrauding:** The forgery need not be perfect, but must be sufficiently convincing that it could deceive a reasonable person.
3. **Intent to defraud:** Specific intent to deceive another person and thereby obtain something of value or cause legal harm. The government need not prove that anyone was actually defrauded -- the intent alone suffices.

### Model Penal Code Approach

The Model Penal Code section 224.1 defines forgery as altering, making, completing, executing, authenticating, issuing, or transferring any writing so that it purports to be the act of another, to have been executed at a time or place or in a numbered sequence other than was in fact the case, or to be a copy of an original when no such original existed. The MPC grades forgery as a felony of the second degree if the writing is or purports to be a government-issued instrument, security, or other document typically relied upon in commercial transactions, and as a felony of the third degree in all other cases.

### Landmark Cases

**Gilbert v. United States, 370 U.S. 650 (1962):** The Supreme Court held that the word "forges" in 18 U.S.C. section 495 was intended to have its common law meaning. The Court clarified that a purported but unauthorized agency endorsement on a government check -- where the defendant signed the payee's name and then his own "as agent" without actual authority -- did not constitute forgery. This decision established an important boundary: forgery requires the creation of a document that is false in its making, not merely a document containing false statements.

**United States v. Hunt, 456 F.3d 1255 (10th Cir. 2006):** The Tenth Circuit addressed the distinction between forgery and fraud in the context of altered federal documents. The court upheld a conviction under 18 U.S.C. section 495 for altering genuine government checks, emphasizing that material alteration of a genuine instrument constitutes forgery when done with intent to defraud.

**State-Level Precedent -- People v. Bendit, 111 Cal.App.2d 1 (1952):** A California appellate court established the principle, widely adopted across jurisdictions, that a forged document need not be a perfect imitation to support a conviction -- it must only be "apparently genuine enough to be capable of deceiving persons of ordinary observation."

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany addresses forgery comprehensively in Chapter Twenty-Three of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), titled "Urkundenfalschung" (Forgery of Documents), spanning sections 267 through 282. The German approach is distinctive for its tripartite structure, which separately addresses traditional document forgery, forgery of technical records, and forgery of electronic data.

**Section 267 -- Forgery of Documents (Urkundenfalschung):**

The foundational offense is codified in section 267(1): whoever, for the purpose of deception in legal transactions, produces a false document, falsifies a genuine document, or uses a false or falsified document shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than five years or a fine.

The statute recognizes three distinct forms of forgery:

1. **Producing a false document (Herstellen einer unechten Urkunde):** Creating a document that gives the false appearance of having been issued by a person who did not in fact issue it.
2. **Falsifying a genuine document (Verfalschen einer echten Urkunde):** Altering the content of an existing authentic document without the issuer's authorization.
3. **Using a false or falsified document (Gebrauchen einer unechten oder verfalschten Urkunde):** Presenting such a document in legal transactions to deceive another party.

A critical principle of German forgery law is that the offense concerns the authenticity of the document's origin, not the truth of its contents. A document that is genuinely issued by its purported author but contains false statements is not a forgery under section 267 -- such conduct may instead be prosecuted as fraud (section 263) or, in specific contexts, as making false declarations (section 271).

**Aggravated Cases (Section 267(3)):**

A particularly serious case of forgery carries imprisonment from six months to ten years. Named examples of serious cases include:

- Acting on a commercial basis or as a member of a gang organized for continued commission of fraud or forgery offenses.
- Causing large-scale financial loss.
- Substantially endangering the security of legal transactions.
- Abusing one's powers or position as a public official.

**Gang-Based or Commercial Forgery (Section 267(4)):**

When forgery is committed commercially as a member of a gang organized for the continued commission of offenses under sections 263-264 or 267-269, the penalty is imprisonment from one to ten years, or in less serious cases from six months to five years.

### Landmark Cases

**BGH 1 StR 430/09 (2010) -- Digital Forgery and Section 269:** The Federal Court of Justice (Bundesgerichtshof) addressed the application of section 269 to manipulated digital records submitted in commercial disputes. The court held that electronically stored data that is functionally equivalent to a traditional document -- in that it serves as evidence in legal transactions and is attributable to a specific issuer -- falls within the scope of section 269. This decision confirmed that Germany's forgery framework extends fully to the digital domain.

**BGH 4 StR 480/12 (2013) -- Forged Employment References:** The Federal Court of Justice considered a case involving the systematic production of fraudulent employment reference letters. The defendant created false Arbeitszeugnisse (employment references) purporting to originate from companies that either did not exist or had not issued them. The court convicted under section 267(1) and emphasized that the key issue was whether the document appeared to originate from its purported issuer, not whether its substantive content was accurate.

**BGH 5 StR 318/19 (2020) -- Gang-Based Identity Document Forgery:** The court applied section 267(4) to a criminal network that produced and distributed forged identity documents across multiple EU Member States. The sentences ranged from three to eight years' imprisonment, reflecting the organized and commercial character of the operation and its threat to the security of cross-border legal transactions within the Schengen area.

## ECHR & EU

### European Convention on Human Rights Framework

The European Convention on Human Rights does not directly criminalize forgery; rather, it provides a framework of fundamental rights that constrains and shapes how Member States of the Council of Europe prosecute forgery offenses. Several Convention articles are directly relevant:

**Article 6 -- Right to a Fair Trial:**

Article 6 guarantees the right to a fair and public hearing within a reasonable time by an independent and impartial tribunal. In forgery prosecutions, this provision is frequently invoked in challenges to:

- The admissibility of expert evidence on document authenticity, where defendants argue that they were denied adequate opportunity to challenge forensic findings.
- The use of compelled statements obtained during regulatory investigations, as established in **Saunders v. United Kingdom (1996)**, where the Court held that the use at trial of statements obtained under statutory compulsion during a corporate fraud investigation violated the privilege against self-incrimination -- a principle applicable to forgery prosecutions involving compelled document production.
- The overall fairness of proceedings, including the right to effective legal assistance and the right to examine witnesses, as articulated in **Jalloh v. Germany (2006)**, where the Court held that the use of evidence obtained through treatment that violated Article 3 (prohibition of inhuman treatment) impaired the very essence of the privilege against self-incrimination.

**Article 7 -- No Punishment Without Law (Nulla Poena Sine Lege):**

Article 7 requires that criminal offenses must be clearly defined by law and that the law must not be applied retroactively. This principle constrains the expansion of forgery definitions to novel technologies: a Member State cannot retroactively criminalize forms of electronic document manipulation that were not covered by existing forgery statutes at the time the conduct occurred.

**Article 8 -- Right to Respect for Private Life:**

Forgery investigations frequently involve searches of private premises, seizure of personal documents, and analysis of personal electronic data. Article 8 requires that such investigative measures be prescribed by law, necessary in a democratic society, and proportionate to the legitimate aim pursued.

### ECHR Case Law on Forgery

**Spiteri v. Malta:** The European Court of Human Rights ruled on the pre-trial detention of a disbarred lawyer charged with fraud, misappropriation, and forgery of public documents. The Court examined whether the applicant's detention was "in accordance with a procedure prescribed by law" under Article 5(1), finding that the arrest following the applicant's return to Malta was lawful. This case illustrates how the ECHR's review of forgery-related detentions focuses on procedural legality rather than substantive criminal law.

**ECHR Forgery of Evidence Cases (Finland):** In a significant case involving deliberate submission of forged death certificates to the ECHR itself, the Court addressed the integrity of its own proceedings. Forged documents purporting to confirm a person's death were submitted to support an asylum claim. The Helsinki District Court subsequently convicted the perpetrators of aggravated fraud and aggravated forgery, sentencing them to approximately two years' imprisonment. The ECHR struck out the underlying application, underscoring that forgery directed at international judicial bodies carries severe consequences under both domestic law and the Convention system.

### EU Legislative Framework

The European Union has developed several instruments relevant to forgery, though it has not enacted a comprehensive EU-wide forgery directive:

**Directive 2014/62/EU -- Protection of the Euro and Other Currencies Against Counterfeiting:**

This directive replaced Council Framework Decision 2000/383/JHA and establishes minimum standards for the criminalization of currency counterfeiting across EU Member States. Key provisions include:

- **Article 3 -- Offenses:** Member States must criminalize the fraudulent making or altering of currency, fraudulent uttering of counterfeit currency, and importation, exportation, transportation, receiving, or obtaining of counterfeit currency for the purpose of uttering.
- **Article 5 -- Penalties:** The maximum penalty for manufacturing counterfeit currency must be at least eight years' imprisonment. The maximum penalty for distributing counterfeit currency must be at least five years' imprisonment.
- **Article 6 -- Attempt and participation:** Inciting, aiding, and abetting as well as attempting the offenses described in Article 3 must be punishable.

**Council Framework Decision 2001/413/JHA -- Combating Fraud and Counterfeiting of Non-Cash Means of Payment:**

This framework decision requires Member States to criminalize the forgery and fraudulent use of payment instruments, including credit cards, checks, and electronic payment methods. It mandates effective, proportionate, and dissuasive criminal penalties for document forgery connected to payment fraud.

**Directive 2013/40/EU -- Attacks Against Information Systems:**

While primarily focused on cybercrime, this directive is relevant to electronic document forgery, as it criminalizes illegal interference with data and system interference -- conduct that may overlap with digital forgery offenses under national law.

**EU Regulation 2019/1157 -- Strengthening Security of Identity Cards:**

This regulation establishes minimum security standards for identity cards issued by EU Member States, including biometric data requirements and tamper-resistant features, thereby raising the technical barriers to identity document forgery within the EU.

### Europol and Eurojust Coordination

Europol's European Serious and Organised Crime Centre (ESOCC) coordinates cross-border investigations into document forgery networks. Eurojust facilitates judicial cooperation in multi-jurisdictional forgery cases, particularly those involving the production or distribution of forged identity documents across the Schengen area. Annual threat assessments published by Europol consistently identify document forgery as a key enabler of organized crime, terrorism, and illegal immigration within the EU.

## Comparative Analysis

### Structural Differences

The four jurisdictions reflect fundamentally different approaches to structuring forgery offenses:

**South Korea** draws its sharpest line between public and private documents. The maximum penalty for forging a public document (ten years) is double that for a private document (five years), reflecting a Confucian-influenced emphasis on the sanctity of official instruments and public authority. This public-private distinction permeates the entire statutory scheme.

**The United States** structures forgery law primarily around the type of document and the jurisdictional level. The federal system addresses government obligations, passports, identity documents, and interstate fraud, while state laws cover the broader universe of forged instruments. The dual-sovereignty system means that a single act of forgery can potentially violate both federal and state law, exposing the defendant to prosecution in multiple forums.

**Germany** organizes forgery law around the medium and method of deception: traditional documents (section 267), technical records (section 268), and electronic data (section 269). This tripartite structure reflects a doctrinal commitment to precision in criminal definitions and has allowed Germany to address digital forgery without straining the traditional concept of a "document."

**The ECHR and EU** operate at a meta-level, setting minimum standards and fundamental rights constraints rather than defining forgery offenses directly. The EU's legislative instruments focus on specific high-priority areas -- currency, payment instruments, identity documents -- while leaving the general definition of forgery to national law.

### Penalty Severity

Penalty ranges vary significantly across jurisdictions:

| Offense Category | South Korea | United States (Federal) | Germany | EU Minimum |
|-----------------|------------|------------------------|---------|------------|
| Public document forgery | Up to 10 years | 10-25 years | Up to 10 years (aggravated) | Not harmonized |
| Private document forgery | Up to 5 years | Varies by instrument | Up to 5 years | Not harmonized |
| Identity document forgery | Up to 10 years | Up to 15-25 years | Up to 10 years (aggravated) | Not harmonized |
| Currency-related forgery | See counterfeiting provisions | Up to 20-25 years | Up to 15 years | At least 8 years max |
| Electronic/digital forgery | Up to 10 years (public) | Up to 20 years | Up to 5-10 years | Not specifically harmonized |

The United States imposes the most severe maximum penalties for federal forgery offenses, particularly those connected to terrorism or drug trafficking, where statutory maximums can reach 25 years. Germany and South Korea occupy a middle range, while EU minimum standards establish a floor rather than a ceiling.

### The Authenticity vs. Truthfulness Distinction

A fundamental conceptual divide runs through forgery law across all jurisdictions. German law draws the sharpest distinction between a document's authenticity (Echtheit) -- whether it genuinely originates from its purported issuer -- and its truthfulness (Wahrheit) -- whether its contents are accurate. Section 267 protects only authenticity; a genuine document containing false statements is not a forgery but may constitute fraud or another offense. South Korea and the United States adopt a somewhat broader view, encompassing certain forms of content falsification (such as false entries in public records) within the forgery framework, though both systems recognize the core concept of false making as central to forgery.

### Digital Adaptation

All four jurisdictions have adapted their forgery frameworks to address electronic documents, though through different mechanisms:

- **South Korea:** Articles 227-2 and 232-2, added by statutory amendment, explicitly criminalize falsification of electromagnetic records.
- **United States:** Federal computer fraud statutes (18 U.S.C. section 1030) supplement traditional forgery provisions, and courts have broadly interpreted existing forgery statutes to cover digitally produced documents.
- **Germany:** Section 269 provides a dedicated provision for forgery of data intended to provide proof, while section 270 extends the concept of deception to data processing contexts.
- **EU:** Directive 2013/40/EU addresses attacks on information systems, while national transpositions extend traditional forgery concepts to the digital realm.

## Timeline

- **1810:** French Code penal codifies modern forgery offenses, influencing civil law systems worldwide.
- **1871:** German Strafgesetzbuch enacted, including original forgery provisions in what is now section 267.
- **1929:** International Convention for the Suppression of Counterfeiting Currency signed in Geneva.
- **1948:** Republic of Korea established; colonial-era criminal law initially retained.
- **1950:** European Convention on Human Rights signed in Rome, establishing fair trial and legality principles.
- **1953:** South Korea enacts its own Criminal Act (Act No. 293), including Chapter XX on document forgery.
- **1962:** U.S. Supreme Court decides Gilbert v. United States, clarifying the federal definition of forgery.
- **1962:** Model Penal Code published, including section 224.1 on forgery, influencing state law reform.
- **1986:** United States enacts Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (18 U.S.C. section 1030), extending fraud and forgery concepts to electronic data.
- **1996:** ECHR decides Saunders v. United Kingdom, establishing limits on using compelled statements in fraud and forgery prosecutions.
- **1997:** Germany amends StGB to add section 269 (forgery of data intended to provide proof) and section 270 (deception in data processing).
- **2000:** EU Council Framework Decision 2000/383/JHA on counterfeiting and the euro.
- **2001:** EU Council Framework Decision 2001/413/JHA on fraud and counterfeiting of non-cash payment instruments.
- **2006:** ECHR decides Jalloh v. Germany, reinforcing limits on evidence obtained through coercive methods in criminal prosecutions.
- **2013:** EU Directive 2013/40/EU on attacks against information systems adopted.
- **2014:** EU Directive 2014/62/EU on protection of the euro against counterfeiting replaces Framework Decision 2000/383/JHA.
- **2019:** EU Regulation 2019/1157 on strengthening the security of identity cards of Union citizens adopted.
- **2020:** German Federal Court of Justice (BGH) confirms application of section 267(4) to gang-based identity document forgery networks operating across the EU.
