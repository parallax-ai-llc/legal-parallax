## Summary

Double jeopardy -- known in civil law traditions as *ne bis in idem* ("not twice for the same thing") -- is a procedural safeguard preventing a person from being tried or punished more than once for the same offense. While the principle enjoys near-universal recognition, its scope, exceptions, and practical application vary dramatically across jurisdictions. South Korea enshrines the protection in Article 13 of its Constitution yet permits prosecutors to appeal acquittals through three tiers of courts, effectively giving the state multiple opportunities to secure a conviction within a single proceeding. The United States applies the Fifth Amendment's Double Jeopardy Clause broadly but carves out a significant "separate sovereigns" exception allowing both federal and state prosecution for the same conduct. Germany provides one of the world's strongest protections under Article 103(3) of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz), with the Federal Constitutional Court striking down a 2021 legislative attempt to allow reopening of acquittals based on new evidence. The ECHR and EU framework, through Protocol 7, Article 4 and the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights Article 50, establishes a transnational floor of protection while permitting integrated dual-track (administrative and criminal) proceedings under certain conditions.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Double jeopardy protection in South Korea is rooted in **Article 13(1) of the Constitution of the Republic of Korea (1987)**, which states:

> "No citizen shall be prosecuted for an act which does not constitute a crime under the Act in force at the time it was committed, nor shall he/she be placed in double jeopardy."

The principle is further codified in the **Criminal Procedure Act** (hyeongsasosongbeop), which provides that a court shall dismiss prosecution where the defendant has already been finally adjudicated on the same matter.

### Prosecutor Appeals and the "Conclusion" Problem

South Korea's application of double jeopardy diverges sharply from common law systems. The prosecution is entitled to appeal an acquittal at every level of the judiciary:

1. **District Court** (1st instance): Either party may appeal
2. **High Court** (2nd instance): Either party may appeal to the Supreme Court
3. **Supreme Court** (daebeobwon): Final determination

A case is only deemed "concluded" -- triggering double jeopardy protection -- when the Supreme Court renders a final decision or the prosecutor declines to appeal. This means the prosecution effectively has three chances to obtain a guilty verdict within a single continuous proceeding. Korean constitutional law treats the entire appellate chain as one proceeding rather than successive trials.

### Key Principles

**Scope of "same offense":** Korean courts apply a fact-based approach, examining whether the underlying criminal conduct is identical rather than relying strictly on statutory elements. If the factual basis is the same, double jeopardy bars re-prosecution after a final judgment.

**Military and civilian courts:** A person acquitted by a military court may not be retried in a civilian court for the same offense, and vice versa.

**Administrative sanctions:** Double jeopardy protection does not extend to administrative penalties. A person may face both criminal prosecution and administrative sanctions (such as license revocation or tax penalties) for the same conduct.

### Landmark Decisions

- **Constitutional Court Decision 94Hun-Ka12 (1994):** Confirmed that the double jeopardy clause in Article 13(1) applies only after a judgment becomes final and that appellate proceedings constitute a continuation of the original trial, not a new prosecution.
- **Supreme Court Decision 2009Do14407 (2010):** Clarified that a retrial (jaesim) after a final conviction on grounds of newly discovered evidence favoring the defendant does not violate *ne bis in idem*, as it operates to benefit the accused.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The **Fifth Amendment** to the United States Constitution provides:

> "...nor shall any person be subject for the same offence to be twice put in jeopardy of life or limb."

This protection was originally applicable only to the federal government. In **Benton v. Maryland, 395 U.S. 784 (1969)**, the Supreme Court incorporated the Double Jeopardy Clause against the states through the Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause.

The Double Jeopardy Clause provides three distinct protections:
1. Protection against a second prosecution for the same offense after **acquittal**
2. Protection against a second prosecution for the same offense after **conviction**
3. Protection against **multiple punishments** for the same offense

### The Blockburger "Same Elements" Test

In **Blockburger v. United States, 284 U.S. 299 (1932)**, the Supreme Court established the definitive test for determining whether two offenses are "the same" for double jeopardy purposes:

> "Where the same act or transaction constitutes a violation of two distinct statutory provisions, the test to be applied to determine whether there are two offenses or only one, is whether each provision requires proof of a fact which the other does not."

Under this test, a defendant may be convicted of two offenses arising from the same criminal incident if each crime contains at least one element not found in the other. For example, armed robbery and assault with a deadly weapon arising from the same event may be separately prosecuted because each requires proof of an element the other does not.

### The Separate Sovereigns Doctrine

The most significant exception to double jeopardy protection in the United States is the **dual sovereignty doctrine**. Because federal and state governments are separate sovereigns, each may prosecute a defendant for the same conduct without violating the Double Jeopardy Clause.

In **Gamble v. United States, 587 U.S. ___ (2019)**, the Supreme Court reaffirmed this doctrine in a 7-2 decision. Terence Gamble was convicted under both Alabama state law and federal law for the same firearm possession. The Court held that since each sovereign's laws define distinct offenses, successive prosecutions by different sovereigns do not constitute double jeopardy.

In **Denezpi v. United States, 596 U.S. 591 (2022)**, the Court extended the doctrine further, holding that prosecution in a Court of Indian Offenses under tribal law followed by federal prosecution did not violate the Double Jeopardy Clause.

### When Jeopardy Attaches

- **Jury trial:** When the jury is empaneled and sworn
- **Bench trial:** When the first witness is sworn
- **Guilty plea:** When the court unconditionally accepts the plea

### Exceptions and Limitations

- **Mistrials:** If a mistrial is declared due to "manifest necessity" (e.g., hung jury), retrial is permitted. See **United States v. Perez, 22 U.S. 579 (1824)**.
- **Appeals by prosecution:** The government generally cannot appeal an acquittal, but may appeal dismissals on legal grounds that do not constitute acquittals on the merits.
- **Civil and criminal:** The same conduct may give rise to both civil liability and criminal prosecution. The acquittal of O.J. Simpson on murder charges did not bar the subsequent civil wrongful death suit.

| Case | Year | Holding |

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany provides one of the strongest double jeopardy protections in the world. **Article 103(3) of the Grundgesetz (Basic Law)** states:

> "*Niemand darf wegen derselben Tat auf Grund der allgemeinen Strafgesetze mehrmals bestraft werden.*"
> ("No person may be punished more than once for the same act under the general criminal laws.")

This protection has been elevated by the Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht, BVerfG) to the status of a fundamental right equivalent (*grundrechtsgleiches Recht*), meaning it is directly enforceable by individuals through constitutional complaints.

### Scope of Protection

**Protection of both convicted and acquitted persons:** Unlike some systems that focus only on preventing re-prosecution after conviction, Article 103(3) protects acquitted persons equally. Once a criminal court has rendered a final decision on an act, no further criminal proceedings may be initiated regarding the same set of facts.

**Definition of "same act" (*dieselbe Tat*):** German law uses a factual rather than legal definition. The concept of *Tat* in the procedural sense (as opposed to the substantive criminal law sense) encompasses the entire historical event or life occurrence (*Lebenssachverhalt*) that forms the subject of the prosecution. This is broader than the American Blockburger test, as it looks at the factual unity of the conduct rather than comparing statutory elements.

### The 2023 Constitutional Court Landmark Decision

In **BVerfG, Judgment of 31 October 2023 (2 BvR 900/22)**, the Federal Constitutional Court struck down **Section 362 No. 5 of the Code of Criminal Procedure (StPO)** as unconstitutional. This provision, added by the Bundestag in 2021, had permitted the reopening of criminal proceedings after a final acquittal when new evidence emerged in cases of murder, genocide, crimes against humanity, or war crimes.

The Court held that:
- Article 103(3) GG contains an absolute guarantee that prohibits the legislature from creating new grounds for reopening proceedings to the detriment of acquitted persons
- The protection of legitimate expectations (*Vertrauensschutz*) inherent in the rule of law (Art. 20(3) GG) bars retroactive application of such provisions
- Even in cases of the most serious crimes, the finality of acquittals must be respected
- The provision violated both the prohibition of double jeopardy and the principle of non-retroactivity

This decision reaffirmed that Germany's *ne bis in idem* protection is among the most absolute in the world, permitting no exceptions even for the gravest offenses.

### Reopening of Proceedings (Wiederaufnahme)

Under Sections 359-362 StPO, criminal proceedings may be reopened only in narrowly defined circumstances:

**In favor of the convicted (Section 359 StPO):**
- Documentary evidence was forged
- A witness or expert committed perjury
- A participating judge committed a criminal offense
- The ECHR found a Convention violation
- New facts or evidence that support acquittal

**To the detriment of the acquitted (Section 362 StPO):**
- A forged document was used (No. 1)
- A witness or expert committed perjury (No. 2)
- A participating judge was criminally complicit (No. 3)
- The acquitted person makes a credible confession (No. 4)
- ~~New evidence in murder/genocide/crimes against humanity cases (No. 5)~~ -- **struck down as unconstitutional in 2023**

### Relationship to Administrative Sanctions

The prohibition under Article 103(3) GG applies only to "general criminal laws" (*allgemeine Strafgesetze*). Disciplinary proceedings, administrative fines (*Ordnungswidrigkeiten*), and regulatory sanctions are not barred by a prior criminal acquittal or conviction for the same conduct, though courts apply proportionality principles to avoid excessive cumulative punishment.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR: Protocol 7, Article 4

**Article 4(1) of Protocol No. 7** to the European Convention on Human Rights provides:

> "No one shall be liable to be tried or punished again in criminal proceedings under the jurisdiction of the same State for an offence for which he or she has already been finally acquitted or convicted in accordance with the law and penal procedure of that State."

**Article 4(2)** provides an exception:

> "The provisions of the preceding paragraph shall not prevent the reopening of the case in accordance with the law and penal procedure of the State concerned, if there is evidence of new or newly discovered facts, or if there has been a fundamental defect in the previous proceedings, which could affect the outcome of the case."

### Key Elements of the ECHR Standard

Four cumulative conditions must be met for the *ne bis in idem* protection to apply:

1. **Two sets of proceedings criminal in nature (*bis*):** Assessed autonomously using the "Engel criteria" -- classification in domestic law, nature of the offense, and severity of the penalty. Administrative proceedings may qualify as "criminal" if they carry sufficiently punitive sanctions.
2. **Same facts (*idem*):** Following the **Zolotukhin test**, the inquiry focuses on identical or substantially identical facts, not on the legal classification of the offenses.
3. **Same offender:** The protection is personal and cannot be invoked by co-defendants.
4. **Final decision:** The first set of proceedings must have concluded with a final decision (*res judicata*).

### Landmark ECHR Cases

**Sergey Zolotukhin v. Russia (2009)** -- Application No. 14939/03

A Russian soldier was first sanctioned under the Administrative Offences Code for disorderly conduct and swearing, then prosecuted under the Criminal Code for the same behavior. The Grand Chamber established the definitive "same facts" test, holding that Article 4 of Protocol No. 7 prohibits prosecution for a second offense insofar as it arises from **identical facts or facts which are substantially the same**. This replaced the earlier inconsistent approach that sometimes looked at legal classification rather than factual identity.

**A and B v. Norway (2016)** -- Application Nos. 24130/11 and 29758/11

Norwegian taxpayers received both administrative tax surcharges (30% penalty) and criminal convictions for tax fraud based on the same omissions. The Grand Chamber held that **dual administrative and criminal proceedings do not violate Article 4 of Protocol No. 7** when:
- The proceedings are sufficiently connected in substance and time
- They form an "integrated" and foreseeable response to the conduct
- The combined sanctions are proportionate
- There is no duplication of evidence-gathering

This decision significantly qualified the Zolotukhin standard by permitting parallel tracks of sanctions within a single integrated system.

**Mihalache v. Romania (2019)** -- Application No. 54012/10

The Grand Chamber clarified the meaning of "final" decision, holding that a decision becomes final when it is irrevocable, meaning no further ordinary remedies are available.

### EU Charter of Fundamental Rights: Article 50

**Article 50** of the EU Charter provides:

> "No one shall be liable to be tried or punished again in criminal proceedings for an offence for which he or she has already been finally acquitted or convicted within the Union in accordance with the law."

This extends the *ne bis in idem* principle **across EU Member States**, unlike Protocol 7 which applies only within a single state. It is further implemented through:

- **Article 54 of the Convention Implementing the Schengen Agreement (CISA):** Prohibits prosecution in one Schengen state for the same acts for which a person has already been finally disposed of in another Schengen state, provided the sentence has been enforced or is currently being enforced.
- **Framework Decision 2009/948/JHA:** Establishes procedures for resolving conflicts of jurisdiction in criminal proceedings between Member States.

### CJEU Jurisprudence

The Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU) has developed its own body of *ne bis in idem* case law:

- **Menci (C-524/15, 2018):** Parallel criminal and administrative tax proceedings within the same Member State may be compatible with Article 50 of the Charter if the limitation on the right is proportionate and genuinely reflects complementary aims.
- **Spasic (C-129/14, 2014):** The enforcement condition in Article 54 CISA (requiring the penalty to have been enforced or currently being enforced) is compatible with Article 50 of the Charter.

## Comparative Analysis

### Defining the "Same Offense"

The four jurisdictions employ fundamentally different tests:

- **South Korea** uses a factual approach, examining whether the underlying criminal conduct is identical
- **United States** applies the Blockburger "same elements" test, focusing on whether each statutory offense requires proof of an element the other does not -- a narrower, more formalistic standard
- **Germany** defines *dieselbe Tat* as the entire historical life event forming the subject of the charge -- the broadest protection
- **ECHR** adopted the "same facts" test in Zolotukhin, examining factual identity regardless of legal classification

### Prosecution Appeals

The most striking divergence concerns the ability of the prosecution to appeal acquittals:

- **South Korea:** Prosecution may appeal acquittals through all three court levels, effectively giving the state three opportunities within a single proceeding
- **United States:** Prosecution generally cannot appeal acquittals (though it can appeal dismissals on legal grounds)
- **Germany:** Prosecution may appeal procedural issues but the underlying acquittal is protected by *ne bis in idem* once final
- **ECHR:** Does not prohibit prosecution appeals within a single proceeding but bars new proceedings after finality

### Multiple Sovereignty

- **United States** stands alone in its robust "separate sovereigns" doctrine, permitting both federal and state prosecution (and, after *Denezpi*, tribal prosecution) for the same conduct
- **South Korea** and **Germany**, as unitary states, do not face this issue domestically
- **EU/ECHR** addresses cross-border sovereignty through Article 54 CISA, which bars re-prosecution across Schengen states, but Protocol 7 applies only within a single state

### Strength of Protection (Ranked)

1. **Germany** -- Near-absolute constitutional protection; legislature cannot create exceptions even for the most serious crimes (2023 BVerfG decision)
2. **ECHR/EU** -- Strong baseline with the "integrated proceedings" exception from *A and B v. Norway*; cross-border protection via CISA
3. **United States** -- Broad protection within a single sovereignty but significantly undermined by the separate sovereigns doctrine
4. **South Korea** -- Constitutional protection exists but is practically weakened by permitting prosecution appeals of acquittals through the full appellate chain
