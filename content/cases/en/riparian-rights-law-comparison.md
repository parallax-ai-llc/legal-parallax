## Summary

Water rights law -- the legal framework governing the allocation, use, and protection of freshwater resources including surface water, groundwater, and transboundary watercourses -- reflects each jurisdiction's historical relationship with water scarcity, its property law traditions, and its evolving environmental policy, with the tension between treating water as a private resource subject to property rights and a public good requiring regulatory management representing the central doctrinal challenge across all systems. South Korea regulates water rights primarily through the River Act (하천법), the Groundwater Act (지하수법), the Water Environment Conservation Act (수질 및 수생태계 보전에 관한 법률), and the Framework Act on Water Management (물관리기본법, 2018), which collectively establish a permit-based system for water use, with the state exercising sovereign authority over rivers and other public waters, riparian landowners having limited customary use rights for domestic and agricultural purposes, and the Ministry of Environment serving as the coordinating authority for integrated water management since the 2018 reforms consolidated previously fragmented water governance. The United States employs two fundamentally different water rights doctrines -- the riparian rights system predominating in the eastern states, under which water use rights are tied to ownership of land bordering a watercourse, and the prior appropriation system predominating in the western states, under which water rights are acquired by actual diversion and beneficial use regardless of land ownership, with priority determined by the temporal sequence of appropriation ("first in time, first in right") -- creating a complex patchwork further complicated by federal reserved rights (the Winters doctrine), interstate compacts, the Clean Water Act's regulatory framework, and ongoing litigation over groundwater regulation, tribal water rights, and climate adaptation. Germany regulates water rights through the Federal Water Resources Act (Wasserhaushaltsgesetz, WHG), which establishes water as a public resource (kein Privateigentum) subject to a comprehensive permit system (Erlaubnis and Bewilligung), with limited exceptions for ordinary use (Gemeingebrauch) and landowner use (Eigentümergebrauch), supplemented by Länder water acts, and with German water law significantly shaped by the EU Water Framework Directive (2000/60/EC) and its requirements for integrated river basin management, good ecological status, and the polluter-pays principle. The ECHR addresses water rights through property protection (Protocol 1, Article 1), environmental rights (Article 8), and the right to life (Article 2), while the EU Water Framework Directive (2000/60/EC) represents the most ambitious transnational water governance framework, establishing binding objectives for water quality, river basin management plans, cost recovery for water services, and public participation, with significant influence extending beyond EU borders through its adoption by European Economic Area states and emulation by other jurisdictions.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's water law has undergone significant transformation from fragmented sectoral management to integrated water governance, culminating in the 2018 Framework Act on Water Management.

**Historical Development:** Korea's traditional water management drew on Chinese-influenced principles of state authority over rivers and irrigation systems. The modern legal framework evolved through Japanese colonial-era water legislation, post-independence river management acts, and progressive environmental protection statutes.

**River Act (하천법):** The foundational statute for surface water management, classifying rivers as national rivers (국가하천) and local rivers (지방하천) and establishing permit requirements for water use, construction in river areas, and discharge. Rivers are classified as public property (공물) under state management, and private ownership of riverbeds is not recognized.

**Groundwater Act (지하수법, 1993):** Establishes a permit system for groundwater development and use, requiring permits for wells exceeding specified depths and extraction volumes. The Act addresses groundwater contamination, aquifer protection, and sustainable yield management. Ground subsidence and groundwater depletion in urban areas (particularly Seoul's metropolitan region) have driven increasingly strict regulatory requirements.

**Framework Act on Water Management (물관리기본법, 2018):** A landmark statute that consolidated water governance authority under the Ministry of Environment (previously split among the Ministry of Environment, Ministry of Land, Infrastructure and Transport, and other agencies). The Act establishes principles of integrated water management, sustainable use, equitable access, and ecosystem protection, and created the National Water Management Committee (국가물관리위원회) as the coordinating body.

### Water Use Rights

**Permit System:** All significant water uses (industrial, agricultural, hydroelectric, and large-scale domestic) require a water use permit (취수허가) from the relevant river management authority. Permits specify the maximum volume, rate of extraction, purpose of use, and conditions for discharge.

**Customary Use Rights:** Riparian landowners retain limited customary rights (관행수리권) for traditional domestic and agricultural uses, recognized by the Supreme Court as pre-existing rights that may coexist with the modern permit system. However, the scope of customary rights has been progressively narrowed through legislative reform and judicial interpretation.

**Water Use Charges (물이용부담금):** Under the Water Management Fund Act (물관리기금법), users of water from designated river basins (particularly the Han River, Nakdong River, Geum River, and Yeongsan River basins) pay water use charges that fund watershed conservation, water quality improvement, and compensation for upstream communities whose development is restricted for water quality protection.

### Transboundary Issues

**Inter-Regional Water Transfers:** Korea's major water transfers -- including the Paldang Dam system supplying Seoul metropolitan area drinking water from the upper Han River basin -- have generated persistent conflicts between upstream and downstream regions, addressed through the water use charge system and regional compensation mechanisms.

**North-South Korean Water Issues:** The Imjin River, which flows from North Korea into South Korea, presents unresolved transboundary water management challenges. North Korea's unannounced releases from the Hwanggang Dam on the Imjin River have caused flooding in South Korea (most notably in 2009, killing six people), highlighting the absence of bilateral water management agreements.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States employs two fundamentally different water rights systems, with the geographical division generally following the 100th meridian.

### Eastern States: Riparian Rights

**Doctrine:** Under the riparian rights system, the right to use water is appurtenant to ownership of land bordering a natural watercourse. All riparian landowners share co-equal rights to make reasonable use of the water, with no priority based on timing of initial use.

**Reasonable Use Standard:** The modern riparian standard is "reasonable use," replacing the earlier "natural flow" doctrine that entitled each riparian to the continuation of the natural quantity and quality of flow. Under reasonable use, courts balance the utility of the claimant's use against the harm to other riparians, considering factors including the purpose of use, the suitability of the use to the watercourse, the economic value, the extent of harm, the practicality of adjustments, and the protection of existing investments. The Restatement (Second) of Torts §§ 850-857 articulates the balancing test for riparian disputes.

**Regulated Riparianism:** Many eastern states have adopted permit systems superimposed on the common law riparian framework. The American Society of Civil Engineers' Regulated Riparian Model Water Code (1997/2004) provides a model framework adopted in modified form by states including Florida (Water Resources Act), Mississippi, Iowa, and Virginia. Under regulated riparianism, significant water withdrawals require permits from a state water management agency, which allocates water based on statutory criteria rather than purely common law principles.

### Western States: Prior Appropriation

**Doctrine:** The prior appropriation doctrine, originating in the mining camps of the 1849 California Gold Rush, awards water rights based on temporal priority of appropriation and beneficial use. The fundamental principle -- "first in time, first in right" -- means that senior appropriators have priority over junior appropriators during periods of shortage, regardless of the location of use relative to the water source.

**Elements:** A valid appropriation requires: (1) intent to appropriate; (2) actual diversion of water from its natural source (though many states have relaxed this requirement for instream flow rights); (3) application to a beneficial use (including domestic, agricultural, industrial, municipal, mining, and increasingly recreational and environmental uses); and (4) in most states, compliance with the state's statutory appropriation procedure (typically filing an application with the state engineer or water board).

**Beneficial Use:** The measure of a water right, defining both the quantity and purpose of the appropriation. Water not applied to beneficial use is subject to forfeiture or abandonment. The doctrine of "use it or lose it" has been criticized for discouraging conservation, and several states have enacted statutes protecting water saved through conservation measures from forfeiture claims.

**Transfer:** Prior appropriation rights are generally transferable, both as to point of diversion and place or purpose of use, subject to the "no injury" rule -- a transfer may not injure other appropriators. Water marketing and transfers have become increasingly important in the western United States as growing urban demands compete with established agricultural uses.

### Federal Water Law

**Winters Doctrine (Federal Reserved Rights):** Under *Winters v. United States*, 207 U.S. 564 (1908), when the federal government reserves land from the public domain (including Indian reservations, national parks, military reservations, and national forests), it implicitly reserves sufficient water to fulfill the purposes of the reservation. Reserved rights are generally senior to all appropriative rights established after the reservation date. Tribal reserved water rights remain among the most significant and contested issues in western water law, with major quantification settlements concluded through the Arizona Water Settlements Act (2004), the Crow Tribe Water Rights Settlement Act (2010), and other congressional legislation.

**Clean Water Act (33 U.S.C. § 1251 et seq.):** The CWA regulates water quality through the National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System (NPDES) permit program but does not directly allocate water quantity. However, CWA requirements for maintaining water quality standards and designated uses indirectly affect water allocation by establishing minimum flow requirements for waste assimilation.

**Endangered Species Act:** The ESA's prohibition on taking listed species and its requirement to avoid jeopardizing their continued existence have profoundly affected water management in the western United States, particularly in the Sacramento-San Joaquin Delta (delta smelt, *Hypomestichus transpacificus*), the Columbia River basin (salmon), and the Rio Grande (silvery minnow).

### Groundwater

American groundwater law is characterized by extreme variation among states:

**Absolute Ownership (Rule of Capture):** A diminishing minority of states (most notably Texas, though modified by statute) hold that a landowner has an absolute right to pump groundwater beneath their land without liability for interference with neighboring wells.

**Reasonable Use:** Many states apply a reasonable use standard, allowing extraction for reasonable purposes on the overlying land but prohibiting waste and unreasonable interference with neighboring landowners.

**Correlative Rights:** Under this doctrine (applied in California per *Katz v. Walkinshaw*, 141 Cal. 116 (1903)), overlying landowners share the common supply and must limit extraction proportionally during periods of shortage.

**Prior Appropriation:** Several western states (including Colorado, Nevada, New Mexico, and Oregon) apply the prior appropriation doctrine to groundwater, with permits allocated based on temporal priority.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German water law is characterized by comprehensive federal legislation supplemented by Länder (state) implementation statutes, all within the overarching framework of the EU Water Framework Directive.

**Wasserhaushaltsgesetz (WHG, Federal Water Resources Act):** The primary federal statute, most recently comprehensively amended in 2009 to transpose the EU Water Framework Directive and again in 2017 (Hochwasserschutzgesetz II) to strengthen flood protection. The WHG establishes that waters (Gewässer) -- including surface waters (Oberflächengewässer), coastal waters (Küstengewässer), and groundwater (Grundwasser) -- are not subject to private ownership rights (kein Privateigentum, though the physical land beneath or adjacent to water bodies may be privately owned).

**Länder Water Acts (Landeswassergesetze):** Each of Germany's 16 Länder has enacted a water act implementing and supplementing the WHG, addressing local conditions, administrative procedures, and specific regional water management requirements.

### Water Use Permit System

**Erlaubnis (Permission, WHG § 8):** The standard water use authorization, granting a revocable right to use water for a specified purpose and duration. The Erlaubnis does not create a property right and may be modified or revoked in the public interest.

**Bewilligung (License, WHG § 10):** A stronger form of authorization conferring a right to use water that cannot be revoked except under specific statutory conditions. The Bewilligung provides greater legal certainty for investments requiring long-term water access (e.g., industrial cooling water, hydroelectric generation) but is subject to more rigorous application and review procedures.

**Gemeingebrauch (Common Use, WHG § 25):** Certain minor water uses -- including bathing, fishing (where permitted by fishery regulations), watering animals, and small-scale water extraction by hand -- are permitted without authorization. The scope of Gemeingebrauch is defined by Länder law and is generally limited to uses that do not significantly affect the water body.

**Eigentümergebrauch (Landowner Use, WHG § 26):** Landowners adjacent to surface waters and landowners overlying groundwater may extract small quantities for domestic and agricultural purposes without a permit, provided the extraction does not adversely affect the water body or other users. This limited riparian-type right is the closest German law comes to recognizing private water use rights.

### Groundwater Regulation

Groundwater is treated as a public resource under the WHG, with extraction requiring a permit (Erlaubnis or Bewilligung) except for de minimis landowner use. The WHG requires consideration of the quantitative and chemical status of groundwater bodies in all permitting decisions, reflecting EU Water Framework Directive objectives.

**Groundwater Protection Zones (Wasserschutzgebiete, WHG § 51):** Länder authorities may designate water protection zones around public water supply wells and spring catchments, imposing restrictions on agriculture, industry, and construction to protect groundwater quality. Approximately 13% of Germany's land area is designated as a water protection zone.

**Contamination and Remediation:** The Federal Soil Protection Act (Bundes-Bodenschutzgesetz) and WHG jointly address groundwater contamination, with extensive jurisprudence on the liability of polluters, landowners, and successor operators for remediation costs. The polluter-pays principle (Verursacherprinzip) is the primary allocation mechanism.

### Flood Protection

The 2002 Elbe flood and the 2013 Central European floods prompted major legislative reforms. The Hochwasserschutzgesetz II (2017) strengthened flood risk management by designating flood risk areas (Überschwemmungsgebiete, WHG § 76), restricting construction in flood plains, requiring municipal flood risk management plans, and authorizing compulsory property acquisition for flood retention areas. Climate change adaptation has become a central element of German water policy.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Framework

**Property Rights and Water:** The ECHR has addressed interference with water rights under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1. In *Fredin v. Sweden (No. 1)* (Application No. 12033/86, 1991), the Court examined the revocation of a gravel extraction permit that affected the applicant's ability to use groundwater, holding that states enjoy a wide margin of appreciation in regulating the use of natural resources. The Court requires that interference with water-related property rights serve a legitimate public interest and be proportionate.

**Environmental Protection and Right to Life:** In *Budayeva and Others v. Russia* (Application No. 15339/02, 2008), the Court held that Russia violated Article 2 (right to life) by failing to implement adequate mudslide and flood warning systems, establishing that states have positive obligations to protect against foreseeable water-related natural hazards. This reasoning extends to obligations regarding dam safety, flood protection, and water quality.

**Water Pollution and Article 8:** The Court has found that severe water pollution affecting residential areas may violate Article 8 (respect for private and family life). In *Dubetska and Others v. Ukraine* (Application No. 30499/03, 2011), the Court held that contamination of drinking water from coal mining operations violated Article 8, requiring the state to take reasonable measures to secure the applicants' rights.

### EU Water Framework Directive (2000/60/EC)

The WFD represents the cornerstone of EU water policy, establishing an integrated and participatory approach to water management.

**River Basin Management:** The WFD requires member states to manage water on the basis of river basin districts (Flussgebietseinheiten) rather than administrative boundaries, reflecting the hydrological reality that water does not respect political borders. For transboundary river basins, member states must coordinate their management plans through international river basin commissions (e.g., the International Commission for the Protection of the Rhine, the International Commission for the Protection of the Danube River).

**Environmental Objectives (Article 4):** The WFD establishes binding environmental objectives including: (1) preventing deterioration of the status of all water bodies (non-deterioration principle); (2) achieving "good ecological status" and "good chemical status" for surface waters by 2015 (with extensions possible to 2027); and (3) achieving "good quantitative status" and "good chemical status" for groundwater. The CJEU's landmark decision in *Weser Alliance* (Case C-461/13, 2015) confirmed that the non-deterioration obligation is directly applicable and constitutes a binding constraint on permitting decisions.

**Cost Recovery (Article 9):** The WFD requires member states to implement water pricing policies that provide adequate incentives for efficient water use and ensure an adequate contribution by water users to the recovery of costs of water services, including environmental and resource costs. Implementation of full cost recovery has been uneven across member states.

**Polluter Pays Principle:** The WFD incorporates the polluter-pays principle, requiring that the costs of pollution be borne by those responsible. This principle affects water use permitting, discharge regulation, and environmental remediation requirements.

### Groundwater Directive (2006/118/EC)

Supplementing the WFD, the Groundwater Directive establishes specific quality standards for groundwater, including threshold values for pollutants, criteria for identifying and reversing upward trends in contamination, and requirements for preventing or limiting the introduction of pollutants into groundwater.

### Floods Directive (2007/60/EC)

The EU Floods Directive requires member states to conduct preliminary flood risk assessments, prepare flood hazard and risk maps, and establish flood risk management plans on a river basin basis. The Directive complements the WFD by integrating flood risk management into river basin management planning.

## Comparative Analysis

### Property vs. Regulatory Models

The jurisdictions illustrate a spectrum from property-based to regulatory water rights models. The American prior appropriation system most closely treats water as a private property right, with established appropriations receiving legal protection against interference. The American riparian system occupies a middle position, connecting water rights to land ownership but subjecting them to reasonable use balancing. Korean and German law most fully embody the regulatory model, treating water as a public resource allocated through permits rather than property rights. The EU WFD reinforces the regulatory model by establishing binding environmental objectives that constrain national water allocation decisions.

### Scarcity and Allocation

The relationship between water scarcity and legal doctrine is starkly illustrated by the American east-west divide. The prior appropriation system developed in the arid West precisely because the riparian system's assumption of adequate water for all co-equal users was incompatible with conditions of chronic scarcity. Korea and Germany, both relatively water-abundant by global standards, have nonetheless adopted increasingly restrictive regulatory frameworks in response to growing demand pressure, pollution challenges, and climate change projections. The ECHR recognizes that the degree of regulatory restriction on water use may be justified by the level of scarcity and environmental need.

### Groundwater Governance

Groundwater regulation represents the most dynamic and contested area of water law across all jurisdictions. The United States' fragmented approach -- ranging from absolute ownership to prior appropriation -- reflects the historical difficulty of regulating an invisible and imperfectly understood resource. Germany and Korea have established comprehensive permit systems for groundwater, while the EU's Groundwater Directive establishes binding quality objectives. The global trend toward stricter groundwater regulation reflects growing scientific understanding of aquifer depletion, contamination, and the hydrological connection between surface water and groundwater.

### Transboundary Cooperation

Water governance increasingly requires international cooperation. The EU's WFD framework for transboundary river basin management represents the most advanced model, requiring coordinated management plans and shared environmental objectives. The UN Watercourses Convention (1997) and the UNECE Water Convention (1992) provide additional frameworks for transboundary cooperation. Korea's unresolved transboundary issues with North Korea illustrate the challenges of water management in divided or conflicting political contexts.
