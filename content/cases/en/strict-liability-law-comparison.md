## Summary

Strict liability -- the imposition of legal responsibility for harm without requiring proof of fault, negligence, or intent -- represents a fundamental departure from traditional fault-based tort principles and has expanded dramatically across legal systems over the past century in response to industrialization, mass production, and technological risk. South Korea applies strict liability primarily through the Product Liability Act (제조물 책임법) of 2002, the Nuclear Damage Compensation Act (원자력 손해배상법), and the Environmental Health Damage Relief Act (환경보건법), while retaining a general fault-based framework under Civil Act Article 750 supplemented by judicial presumptions that function as de facto strict liability in certain contexts. The United States developed strict liability in tort through the landmark Restatement (Second) of Torts Section 402A (1965), subsequently refined by the Restatement (Third) of Torts: Products Liability (1998), and applies strict liability to abnormally dangerous activities under the Rylands v. Fletcher (1868) lineage, product defect claims, and statutory environmental regimes such as CERCLA (42 U.S.C. Section 9601 et seq.). Germany codifies strict liability (Gefährdungshaftung) through numerous specialized statutes outside the BGB, including the Road Traffic Act (Straßenverkehrsgesetz, StVG) Section 7, the Product Liability Act (Produkthaftungsgesetz, ProdHaftG), the Environmental Liability Act (Umwelthaftungsgesetz), and the Air Traffic Act (Luftverkehrsgesetz), creating a patchwork of sector-specific regimes with varying liability caps and defenses. At the European level, EU Directive 85/374/EEC established product liability without fault across the single market, now replaced by Directive 2024/2853 extending strict liability to digital products and AI systems, while the ECHR has recognized that states bear a positive obligation under Article 2 to regulate dangerous activities through effective deterrence, regardless of fault.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's strict liability regime operates through several specialized statutes layered over the general fault-based tort framework of Civil Act (민법) Article 750. The most significant strict liability statute is the Product Liability Act (제조물 책임법), enacted in 2002 and substantially amended in 2017.

### Product Liability Act (제조물 책임법)

The Product Liability Act, Act No. 6109, imposes strict liability on manufacturers for damage caused by defective products. Article 3 provides that a manufacturer is liable for damage to life, body, or property caused by a defect in the product (제조물의 결함으로 인하여 생명·신체 또는 재산에 손해를 입은 경우). The Act recognizes three categories of defect: manufacturing defects (제조상의 결함), design defects (설계상의 결함), and warning/instruction defects (표시상의 결함).

The 2017 amendment introduced significant changes. Punitive damages of up to three times actual damages were authorized for cases where the manufacturer knew of the defect and failed to take appropriate action (Article 3(2)). A presumption of defect was established: if the damage arises during normal use and the damage is of a kind ordinarily caused by a defect, the product is presumed defective (Article 3-2).

### Nuclear Liability

The Nuclear Damage Compensation Act (원자력 손해배상법) imposes absolute liability on nuclear operators for damage arising from nuclear incidents. The operator is liable regardless of fault, and liability is channeled exclusively to the operator. The operator must maintain insurance or other financial security, and the government provides supplemental compensation beyond the operator's financial security.

### Environmental Strict Liability

The Environmental Health Damage Relief Act (환경보건법) and the Soil Environment Conservation Act (토양환경보전법) impose strict liability for environmental contamination from designated facilities. The Environmental Dispute Mediation Act (환경분쟁 조정법) establishes a causal presumption in environmental damage cases, providing that where a facility emits pollutants and damage occurs in the surrounding area, the pollution is presumed to have caused the damage.

### Animal Keeper Liability

Civil Act Article 759 establishes liability for animal keepers, providing that a person who keeps an animal is liable for damage caused by the animal to another person. However, this provision includes a due care defense: the keeper may escape liability by proving that they exercised due care in managing the animal. This contrasts with the strict liability approach in Germany.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States applies strict liability in three primary contexts: abnormally dangerous activities, defective products, and statutory environmental liability.

### Abnormally Dangerous Activities

The strict liability doctrine for abnormally dangerous activities traces to Rylands v. Fletcher [1868] UKHL 1, received into American law through early twentieth-century cases. The Restatement (Second) of Torts Sections 519-520 established six factors for determining whether an activity is abnormally dangerous: (1) existence of a high degree of risk of harm, (2) likelihood that the harm will be great, (3) inability to eliminate the risk by reasonable care, (4) extent to which the activity is not common, (5) inappropriateness to the location, and (6) extent to which the value to the community is outweighed by dangerous attributes. Classic examples include blasting, fumigation with toxic chemicals, and storage of large quantities of hazardous materials.

### Products Liability

Strict product liability emerged through Justice Roger Traynor's concurrence in Escola v. Coca Cola Bottling Co. of Fresno, 24 Cal.2d 453 (1944) and was formally adopted in Greenman v. Yuba Power Products, Inc., 59 Cal.2d 57 (1963), where the California Supreme Court held that a manufacturer is strictly liable when it places a product on the market knowing it will be used without inspection and the product proves defective and causes injury.

The Restatement (Second) of Torts Section 402A (1965) generalized strict product liability across jurisdictions. The Restatement (Third) of Torts: Products Liability (1998) refined the framework by distinguishing among:

| Defect Type | Liability Standard | Description |
|-------------|-------------------|-------------|
| Manufacturing defect | Strict liability | Product departs from intended design |
| Design defect | Risk-utility balancing (reasonable alternative design) | Product could have been designed more safely at reasonable cost |
| Warning defect | Reasonableness | Inadequate instructions or warnings about foreseeable risks |

### CERCLA Environmental Liability

The Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act (CERCLA, 42 U.S.C. Section 9601 et seq.), enacted in 1980, imposes strict, joint and several, and retroactive liability on potentially responsible parties (PRPs) for cleanup costs at contaminated sites. Four categories of PRPs face strict liability: current owners and operators, owners and operators at the time of disposal, generators who arranged for disposal, and transporters who selected the disposal site.

### Nuclear Liability

The Price-Anderson Nuclear Industries Indemnity Act (42 U.S.C. Section 2210) imposes strict liability on nuclear operators through a tiered financial protection system: operators must maintain the maximum amount of private insurance (currently $450 million per reactor), supplemented by a retrospective premium pool from all operators (totaling approximately $13.6 billion), with the federal government providing indemnification beyond these amounts.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German strict liability (Gefährdungshaftung) is based on the principle that those who create a source of particular danger through an activity that is socially tolerated because of its general utility should bear the resulting costs, regardless of fault. Strict liability is exclusively statutory; the BGB itself does not contain a general strict liability clause, and German courts have consistently refused to create strict liability through judicial development.

### Road Traffic Act (StVG Section 7)

Section 7 of the Straßenverkehrsgesetz establishes strict liability for the keeper (Halter) of a motor vehicle for damage caused during the operation of the vehicle. The keeper is liable for death, bodily injury, or property damage unless the damage was caused by unavoidable events (höhere Gewalt). Liability is capped at EUR 5 million for personal injury per accident and EUR 1 million for property damage.

### Product Liability Act (Produkthaftungsgesetz)

The Produkthaftungsgesetz (ProdHaftG), implementing EU Directive 85/374/EEC, imposes strict liability on producers for defective products. Total liability for personal injury from identical products with the same defect is capped at EUR 85 million. The Act provides several defenses under Section 1(2), including the development risk defense (Entwicklungsrisikoeinwand) -- that the state of scientific and technical knowledge at the time the product was put into circulation did not enable the defect to be discovered.

### Environmental Liability Act (Umwelthaftungsgesetz)

The Umwelthaftungsgesetz (UmweltHG) of 1990 imposes strict liability on operators of facilities listed in Annex 1 (approximately 96 types of facilities) for environmental damage. The Act establishes a causal presumption: if a listed facility is suited to causing the type of damage that occurred, causation is presumed unless the operator proves otherwise (Section 6). Liability is capped at EUR 85 million for personal injury and EUR 85 million for property damage.

### Animal Liability (BGB Section 833)

Section 833 BGB distinguishes between luxury animals (Luxustiere) and utility animals (Nutztiere). Keepers of luxury animals (pets, recreational horses) bear absolute strict liability. Keepers of utility animals (farm animals, working dogs, guard dogs) benefit from a rebuttable presumption of fault -- liability is presumed but can be rebutted by proof of due care in supervision.

### Nuclear Liability

The Atomic Energy Act (Atomgesetz, AtomG) Section 25 imposes absolute liability (without any force majeure defense) on nuclear facility operators for nuclear damage. Germany is party to the Paris Convention on Third Party Liability in the Field of Nuclear Energy (1960) and the Brussels Supplementary Convention (1963). Liability is unlimited under German law.

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR has addressed strict liability principles through the positive obligations doctrine under Article 2 (right to life).

**Öneryıldız v. Turkey (Application No. 48939/99, Grand Chamber Judgment of November 30, 2004):** The Court held that the state's positive obligation under Article 2 extends to industrial and other dangerous activities, requiring effective deterrence against threats to life through adequate regulation, licensing, and enforcement -- regardless of whether the activity is operated by state or private entities. The failure to regulate a waste dump that exploded, killing 39 people, constituted a violation even without proof of intentional state action.

**Budayeva and Others v. Russia (Application No. 15339/02, Judgment of March 20, 2008):** Concerning deaths from a mudslide in Tyrnauz, the Court found Russia violated Article 2 by failing to implement a known and effective risk mitigation policy, reinforcing that states bear a form of strict positive obligation in relation to natural disasters where the risk is known.

**Brincat and Others v. Malta (Application No. 60908/11, Judgment of July 24, 2014):** Former Malta Drydocks employees exposed to asbestos successfully argued that Malta violated Articles 2 and 8 by failing to protect workers from known health risks, extending the state's strict positive obligations to occupational health contexts.

### EU Product Liability Directive

Directive 85/374/EEC (1985) harmonized product liability across the EU on a no-fault basis. This was replaced by Directive 2024/2853, adopted October 23, 2024, which introduces several significant changes:

**Extended Scope:** The new directive covers software, AI systems, and digital manufacturing files alongside physical products. Online marketplaces may be liable as economic operators when no EU-based manufacturer or importer is identifiable.

**Presumption of Defect:** Article 9(3) introduces a rebuttable presumption that a product is defective where the claimant demonstrates non-compliance with mandatory safety requirements, or the damage was caused by an obvious malfunction of the product during normal use.

**Development Risk Defense Retained:** Article 11(e) preserves the development risk defense but allows member states to derogate from it for pharmaceutical products, food, and food materials.

### Environmental Liability Directive

Directive 2004/35/CE establishes an EU-wide framework based on the "polluter pays" principle. Operators of activities listed in Annex III (including waste management, chemical manufacturing, and GMO use) bear strict liability for environmental damage. Operators of other activities bear fault-based liability. The Directive covers damage to protected species and habitats, water damage, and land contamination.

## Comparative Analysis

### Scope of Strict Liability

The four jurisdictions exhibit markedly different approaches to the scope of strict liability:

**Germany** employs the most systematic approach, confining strict liability to enumerated statutory regimes. The numerus clausus principle means courts cannot create new categories of strict liability -- only the legislature can extend the doctrine to new activities or products.

**The United States** applies the broadest scope, with judicial authority to designate new activities as "abnormally dangerous" and subject to strict liability. The common law tradition permits ongoing doctrinal evolution.

**South Korea** occupies a middle position, with statutory strict liability regimes modeled on German and Japanese precedents, supplemented by judicially created presumptions that approach strict liability in effect.

**The EU** has pursued sectoral harmonization, imposing strict liability in specific domains (products, environment, nuclear) while leaving member states free to maintain fault-based frameworks in other areas.

### The Development Risk Defense

A critical doctrinal divergence concerns whether manufacturers can escape liability by proving that the state of knowledge at the time of production made the defect undiscoverable:

| Jurisdiction | Development Risk Defense |
|-------------|------------------------|
| South Korea | Available (Product Liability Act Art. 4(2)) |
| United States | Generally unavailable for manufacturing defects; some states recognize for design/warning |
| Germany | Available (ProdHaftG Section 1(2)(5)) |
| EU | Available (Directive 2024/2853 Art. 11(e)); member states may derogate for food and pharmaceuticals |

### Liability Caps

Germany and the EU permit or require liability caps in strict liability regimes, reflecting a policy judgment that unlimited strict liability could deter socially valuable activities. The United States generally does not impose caps on strict liability claims except in nuclear liability (Price-Anderson Act). South Korea takes a mixed approach, with caps in nuclear liability but no general caps in product liability.

### AI and Emerging Technologies

All four jurisdictions are grappling with the application of strict liability to artificial intelligence. The EU has taken the most concrete steps through Directive 2024/2853's extension to AI systems and the proposed AI Liability Directive. Germany's strict numerus clausus approach may require new legislation. The United States has yet to develop a coherent federal framework for AI liability.
