## Summary

Human cloning -- the creation of a genetically identical copy of a human being through either reproductive cloning (producing a live birth) or therapeutic cloning (creating cloned embryos for stem cell research) -- stands as one of the most legally and ethically contentious areas of biotechnology regulation worldwide. South Korea occupies a uniquely significant position in cloning jurisprudence following the Hwang Woo-suk scandal of 2005, in which a prominent scientist fabricated claims of successful human embryonic stem cell cloning; the subsequent regulatory overhaul resulted in the Bioethics and Safety Act (생명윤리 및 안전에 관한 법률), first enacted in 2004 and substantially revised in 2008 and 2012, which prohibits both reproductive and therapeutic cloning through somatic cell nuclear transfer (SCNT) while permitting limited embryonic research under strict oversight by the National Bioethics Committee (국가생명윤리심의위원회). The United States lacks a comprehensive federal ban on human cloning, relying instead on a patchwork of state laws -- approximately fifteen states have enacted cloning prohibitions of varying scope -- combined with FDA regulatory authority over clinical applications of cloning technology and congressional moratoriums on federal funding for human cloning research under the Dickey-Wicker Amendment. Germany maintains one of the strictest prohibitions through the Embryonenschutzgesetz (Embryo Protection Act) of 1990, which criminalizes all forms of human cloning under Section 6, with penalties of up to five years imprisonment, and the Stammzellgesetz (Stem Cell Act) of 2002, which severely restricts importation of embryonic stem cell lines. At the international level, the United Nations General Assembly adopted a non-binding Declaration on Human Cloning in 2005, the Council of Europe's Additional Protocol to the Convention on Human Rights and Biomedicine concerning the Prohibition of Cloning Human Beings (1998) provides a binding regional framework, and the European Union regulates cloning indirectly through Directive 98/44/EC on the Legal Protection of Biotechnological Inventions and the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's regulation of human cloning is governed primarily by the Bioethics and Safety Act (생명윤리 및 안전에 관한 법률, Act No. 7150), originally enacted on January 29, 2004, and substantially amended in 2008 (Act No. 8852) and 2012. The Act was a direct legislative response to the rapid advances in cloning technology demonstrated by Korean scientists, most notably the controversial research of Hwang Woo-suk at Seoul National University.

### Prohibited Activities

Article 20 of the Bioethics and Safety Act explicitly prohibits the following activities: (1) transferring a somatic cell nuclear transfer embryo (체세포복제배아) into a human or animal uterus for reproductive purposes; (2) fertilizing a cloned human embryo; (3) creating human embryos for purposes other than pregnancy in assisted reproduction; and (4) creating interspecies hybrid embryos. Article 21 further restricts the creation of SCNT embryos, permitting them only for specifically approved biomedical research purposes under the oversight of the Institutional Review Board (IRB) and the National Bioethics Committee.

### Penalties

| Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|
| Reproductive cloning (implanting SCNT embryo) | Up to 10 years imprisonment |
| Creating embryos for non-reproductive, non-approved purposes | Up to 5 years imprisonment or 50 million KRW fine |
| Conducting research without IRB approval | Up to 3 years imprisonment or 30 million KRW fine |
| Trading or selling embryos | Up to 5 years imprisonment or 50 million KRW fine |

### The Hwang Woo-suk Scandal

The most significant event in South Korean cloning law history was the exposure in December 2005 of fabricated research by Hwang Woo-suk, who had claimed in two landmark papers published in Science (2004 and 2005) to have successfully created human embryonic stem cells through SCNT. Seoul National University's investigation committee confirmed in January 2006 that both papers contained fabricated data. Hwang was convicted in 2009 of embezzlement and bioethics law violations, receiving a two-year suspended prison sentence. The scandal prompted the 2008 comprehensive revision of the Bioethics and Safety Act, strengthening IRB oversight requirements, increasing penalties, and establishing more rigorous research ethics protocols.

### National Bioethics Committee

The National Bioethics Committee (국가생명윤리심의위원회), established under Article 7 of the Act, serves as the supreme oversight body for all matters relating to human cloning and embryo research. The Committee comprises up to 20 members, including scientists, ethicists, legal scholars, religious representatives, and civic society members, appointed by the President of the Republic. The Committee reviews and approves all SCNT embryo research protocols, sets national bioethics policy, and advises the government on regulatory amendments.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has no comprehensive federal statute specifically banning human cloning, making it an outlier among developed nations. Multiple bills have been introduced in Congress since 1998 -- including the Human Cloning Prohibition Act (introduced repeatedly from 2001 to 2020) -- but none has been enacted into law due to persistent disagreements between those who would ban all forms of cloning and those who would permit therapeutic cloning while banning only reproductive cloning.

**FDA Regulatory Authority:** The Food and Drug Administration has asserted jurisdiction over human cloning as a form of clinical research involving human subjects. In 1998, the FDA issued a "Dear Colleague" letter stating that clinical research involving human cloning is subject to FDA regulation under the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act and the Public Health Service Act, requiring an Investigational New Drug (IND) application before any cloning-related clinical trial could proceed.

**Dickey-Wicker Amendment:** Since 1996, Congress has annually attached the Dickey-Wicker Amendment to appropriations bills for the Department of Health and Human Services, prohibiting the use of federal funds for the creation of human embryos for research purposes or for research in which human embryos are destroyed.

**NIH Guidelines on Human Stem Cell Research (2009):** Following Executive Order 13505 issued by President Obama on March 9, 2009, the National Institutes of Health issued guidelines permitting federal funding for research using human embryonic stem cell lines derived from embryos created for reproductive purposes through in vitro fertilization and no longer needed for that purpose, provided informed consent was obtained.

### Significant Federal Legislative Attempts

The Human Cloning Prohibition Act of 2001 (H.R. 2505), sponsored by Representative Dave Weldon, passed the House of Representatives on July 31, 2001, by a vote of 265-162, but failed in the Senate. The bill would have imposed criminal penalties of up to 10 years imprisonment and civil fines of at least $1 million for any attempt to create a human clone. The Senate alternative, the Human Cloning Ban and Stem Cell Research Protection Act (S. 2439, 2002), sponsored by Senators Dianne Feinstein and Edward Kennedy, would have banned reproductive cloning while permitting therapeutic cloning, but also failed to advance.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany maintains among the most restrictive human cloning regulations in the world, rooted in the Embryonenschutzgesetz (Embryo Protection Act, ESchG) of December 13, 1990, which entered into force on January 1, 1991. The Act was enacted prior to the successful cloning of Dolly the sheep in 1996, reflecting Germany's precautionary approach to embryo-related technologies shaped by historical sensitivity to eugenic practices.

**Section 6 ESchG -- Klonen (Cloning):** This provision makes it a criminal offense to artificially create a human embryo that is genetically identical to another embryo, fetus, or living or deceased person. The prohibition covers both reproductive cloning (implantation of a cloned embryo) and the creation of a cloned embryo for any purpose, effectively banning therapeutic cloning as well. The maximum penalty is five years imprisonment.

**Section 7 ESchG -- Chimären- und Hybridbildung (Chimera and Hybrid Formation):** This provision prohibits the creation of chimeras and hybrids by combining human and animal embryos or gametes.

### Stammzellgesetz (Stem Cell Act) of 2002

The Stammzellgesetz (StZG), enacted on June 28, 2002, addresses the importation and use of human embryonic stem cells for research purposes. The original Act permitted importation only of stem cell lines derived before January 1, 2002. Following extensive Bundestag debate in April 2008, the cutoff date was moved to May 1, 2007 (First Amendment to StZG, effective November 14, 2008). The Robert Koch Institute serves as the competent authority for granting import permits and maintaining the registry of approved stem cell lines. The Central Ethics Commission for Stem Cell Research (Zentrale Ethikkommission für Stammzellenforschung, ZES) provides expert opinions on applications.

### Grundgesetz Protections

Article 1(1) of the Grundgesetz (Basic Law) provides that "Human dignity shall be inviolable," and the Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) has interpreted this guarantee as extending to early forms of human life, providing constitutional support for strict cloning prohibitions. Article 5(3) GG protects freedom of research but is subject to limitation by competing constitutional values, including human dignity.

### Penalties

| Offense under ESchG | Penalty |
|---------------------|---------|
| Creating a genetically identical human embryo (Section 6(1)) | Up to 5 years imprisonment |
| Transferring a cloned embryo to a woman (Section 6(2)) | Up to 5 years imprisonment |
| Creating chimeras or hybrids (Section 7) | Up to 5 years imprisonment |
| Importing stem cells without authorization (StZG Section 13) | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine |

## ECHR & EU

### Council of Europe -- Additional Protocol on Cloning (1998)

The Additional Protocol to the Convention on Human Rights and Biomedicine (Oviedo Convention) concerning the Prohibition of Cloning Human Beings was opened for signature on January 12, 1998, in Paris. It is the first binding international legal instrument to prohibit human cloning.

**Article 1:** "Any intervention seeking to create a human being genetically identical to another human being, whether living or dead, is prohibited." The Explanatory Report clarifies that "genetically identical" means "sharing with another the same nuclear gene set."

The Protocol has been ratified by 24 states as of 2026. Notably, several major European states -- including the United Kingdom, Germany, and Russia -- have not ratified the Oviedo Convention itself, though they have enacted domestic legislation achieving equivalent or stricter prohibitions. South Korea is not a party.

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECtHR has addressed cloning and embryo-related issues through the lens of Articles 2 (right to life), 8 (right to respect for private and family life), and 10 (freedom of expression, encompassing scientific freedom).

**Parrillo v. Italy (Application No. 46470/11, Grand Chamber Judgment of August 27, 2015):** While not directly concerning cloning, this case addressed the donation of IVF embryos to scientific research under Italian Law No. 40/2004. The Grand Chamber held that Article 8 was applicable to the applicant's desire to donate her embryos to research, as this concerned her "private life" and personal autonomy. However, the Court found no violation, granting Italy a wide margin of appreciation in regulating sensitive ethical questions about the status of embryos.

**Costa and Pavan v. Italy (Application No. 54270/10, Judgment of August 28, 2012):** The Court addressed preimplantation genetic diagnosis restrictions, finding a violation of Article 8 where Italy prohibited a carrier couple from accessing PGD while permitting abortion for genetic abnormalities. The case underscored that states must maintain coherence in their regulation of reproductive technologies.

### EU Charter of Fundamental Rights

Article 3(2)(d) of the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights explicitly prohibits "the reproductive cloning of human beings" in the context of the right to integrity of the person. This is the only form of cloning expressly prohibited at the EU level; therapeutic cloning is left to member state regulation.

### Directive 98/44/EC -- Biotechnological Inventions

Directive 98/44/EC of the European Parliament and of the Council of July 6, 1998, on the Legal Protection of Biotechnological Inventions addresses cloning through patent exclusions. Article 6(2)(a) provides that "processes for cloning human beings" shall be considered unpatentable on grounds of ordre public or morality. The European Patent Office (EPO) implements this exclusion through Rule 28(a) of the Implementing Regulations to the European Patent Convention.

### Horizon Europe Framework

The EU's Horizon Europe research funding program (2021-2027), established by Regulation (EU) 2021/695, excludes from funding any research activity aimed at human cloning for reproductive purposes, as well as research intended to modify the genetic heritage of human beings that could make such modifications heritable.

## Comparative Analysis

### Scope of Prohibition

The jurisdictions differ most significantly in their treatment of therapeutic versus reproductive cloning. Germany and the Council of Europe Protocol do not distinguish between the two, prohibiting all creation of genetically identical human embryos regardless of purpose. South Korea prohibits both but theoretically permits narrowly scoped SCNT research under National Bioethics Committee approval. The United States has no federal prohibition on either form, with regulation fragmented across state legislatures and federal agencies. The EU Charter prohibits only reproductive cloning, leaving therapeutic cloning to member state discretion.

### Regulatory Models

Three distinct regulatory models emerge: (1) the criminal prohibition model (Germany, Council of Europe), which treats all human cloning as a criminal offense; (2) the regulatory oversight model (South Korea, FDA approach in the United States), which subjects cloning-related research to institutional review and governmental approval; and (3) the permissive model with funding restrictions (certain U.S. states like California and New Jersey), which permits therapeutic cloning and even provides public funding for stem cell research while maintaining a criminal ban on reproductive cloning.

### Enforcement Mechanisms

Germany relies primarily on criminal prosecution, with the Embryo Protection Act providing clear criminal sanctions. South Korea combines criminal penalties with institutional oversight through the National Bioethics Committee and IRBs. The United States relies on FDA regulatory enforcement (capable of seeking injunctions and criminal prosecutions for violations of IND requirements), state criminal statutes, and federal funding restrictions. The EU framework depends on member state implementation, with the ECHR providing a supranational judicial mechanism for addressing alleged violations of Convention rights.

### Impact of the Hwang Woo-suk Scandal

The 2005 exposure of fabricated cloning research in South Korea had global regulatory repercussions, prompting not only Korean legislative reform but also increased scrutiny of research ethics oversight worldwide. The scandal demonstrated that scientific misconduct in the cloning field could undermine public trust and impede legitimate research, reinforcing the importance of robust institutional review mechanisms.
