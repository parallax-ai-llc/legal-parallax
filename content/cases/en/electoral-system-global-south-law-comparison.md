## Summary

Electoral systems in the Global South operate under conditions of enormous demographic scale, deep social diversity, contested legitimacy, and institutional challenges that render direct comparisons with consolidated Western democracies misleading, yet these systems have also generated significant innovations in electoral administration, technology, dispute resolution, and inclusion mechanisms. China's People's Congress system (人民代表大会制度, renmin daibiao dahui zhidu) constitutes a fundamentally different model of political representation where elections occur at the grassroots level (direct election of deputies to county-level and township-level people's congresses under the Electoral Law of the National People's Congress and Local People's Congresses, 选举法, 1979, most recently revised 2020), with higher-level congresses elected indirectly by lower-level congresses, all under the organizational leadership of the CPC, reflecting a Leninist conception of democratic centralism (民主集中制) rather than competitive multi-party democracy. India conducts the world's largest democratic exercise, with the Election Commission of India (ECI, established under Article 324 of the Constitution) overseeing elections involving approximately 970 million eligible voters (2024 general election), using Electronic Voting Machines (EVMs) and Voter Verifiable Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT) systems across over one million polling stations, governed by the Representation of the People Act, 1950 (voter registration) and the Representation of the People Act, 1951 (conduct of elections, election offenses, and disputes). Nigeria's electoral system, administered by the Independent National Electoral Commission (INEC, established under Section 153 of the 1999 Constitution) and governed by the Electoral Act, 2022, has been marked by persistent challenges including vote-buying, ballot-box snatching, violence, and disputed results, though the introduction of the Bimodal Voter Accreditation System (BVAS) in 2023 represented a significant technological advance. Brazil has been a global pioneer in electronic voting, implementing the urna eletronica (electronic ballot box) nationwide since 2000, administered by the Tribunal Superior Eleitoral (TSE, Superior Electoral Court), which uniquely combines judicial and administrative electoral functions and exercises jurisdiction over both electoral administration and electoral disputes. The Philippines transitioned to automated elections through Republic Act No. 9369 (2007, amending the Automated Election System Act, RA 8436/1997), administered by the Commission on Elections (COMELEC, established under Article IX-C of the 1987 Constitution), though automation has not eliminated controversies over electoral integrity. Indonesia's Komisi Pemilihan Umum (KPU, General Elections Commission), an independent body under Article 22E of the amended Constitution, administers what is consistently ranked as one of the world's most complex single-day elections, with voters in the 2024 elections casting ballots simultaneously for the president, national legislature, provincial legislatures, and district/city legislatures.

## China (中国)

### The People's Congress Electoral System

China's electoral system operates on the principle of democratic centralism (民主集中制, minzhu jizhongzhi), where direct elections occur only at the county/district (县/区) and township (乡/镇) levels, with all higher-level people's congresses elected indirectly by the congress at the next lower level.

**Electoral Law of the NPC and Local People's Congresses (全国人民代表大会和地方各级人民代表大会选举法, first enacted 1953, current version 2020):**

- **Article 2:** Deputies to the NPC and provincial, prefectural, and city-level people's congresses are elected by the people's congress at the next lower level. Deputies to county-level and township-level people's congresses are directly elected by voters.
- **Article 30:** The number of candidates must exceed the number of seats by one-third to one hundred percent (差额选举, competitive election within limits). This "more candidates than seats" (差额选举) principle, introduced in 1979 and strengthened in subsequent revisions, distinguishes the Chinese system from purely single-candidate elections but does not constitute multi-party competition.
- **Article 34:** Election committees are established at the county and township levels to administer direct elections. These committees operate under the leadership of the Standing Committee of the people's congress at the next higher level.
- **Article 31:** Candidates for direct elections may be nominated by political parties, people's organizations, or jointly by ten or more voters. In practice, the CPC's organizational department plays a decisive role in vetting and recommending candidates.

**The 2016 Hunan Vote-Buying Scandal:** The NPC Standing Committee invalidated the election of 45 NPC deputies and punished 523 individuals for vote-buying in the Hunan provincial people's congress elections, the largest electoral fraud case in PRC history. This episode demonstrated both the existence of corruption within the people's congress system and the CPC's willingness to enforce electoral discipline.

**Villagers' Committee Elections:** At the sub-governmental level, the Organic Law of the Villagers' Committees (村民委员会组织法, 1998, revised 2010) provides for direct elections of village committees (village head, deputy head, and committee members) by village residents. These elections, covering approximately 600,000 villages, have been the subject of extensive scholarly attention as a form of grassroots democracy within the CPC-led system.

### Representation of Minority Nationalities

The Electoral Law contains specific provisions ensuring representation of minority nationalities. Article 18 provides that in areas with minority populations, each minority nationality is entitled to at least one deputy. Articles 19-20 establish that minority nationalities with particularly small populations receive favorable allocation of deputy quotas, ensuring representation even where their numbers would not otherwise warrant a seat.

## India (भारत)

### The Election Commission of India

The ECI, established under Article 324 of the Constitution, is one of the world's most powerful electoral management bodies. Since 1993, it has operated as a multi-member body (one Chief Election Commissioner and two Election Commissioners, per the Chief Election Commissioner and Other Election Commissioners (Appointment, Conditions of Service and Term of Office) Act, 2023).

**Constitutional Protections:** Article 324(5) provides that the Chief Election Commissioner cannot be removed from office except through impeachment (the same process as for a Supreme Court judge), guaranteeing functional independence. However, the Election Commissioners do not enjoy the same protection and can be removed on the CEC's recommendation.

**Model Code of Conduct (MCC):** The MCC, a non-statutory code that has evolved since the 1960s, governs the behavior of political parties and candidates during elections. Though lacking legislative backing, the ECI enforces the MCC through administrative orders, including the power to delay election schedules, transfer government officials, and restrict government announcements. The Supreme Court in Union of India v. Harbans Singh Jalal, (2001) 1 SCC 242, acknowledged the MCC's importance but noted its non-statutory nature.

### Electronic Voting Machines (EVMs) and VVPAT

India has used EVMs in all elections since 2004 and introduced VVPAT (Voter Verifiable Paper Audit Trail) through a phased process culminating in full deployment in the 2019 general election, following the Supreme Court's direction in Subramanian Swamy v. Election Commission of India, (2013) 10 SCC 500, which ordered the introduction of VVPAT.

The EVM system consists of a Control Unit (with the presiding officer) and a Ballot Unit (in the voting compartment). Each EVM records a maximum of 2,000 votes and can accommodate up to 384 candidates (using linked ballot units). The VVPAT unit prints a paper slip showing the voter's choice, visible through a window for seven seconds before being deposited in a sealed container. In 2019, the Supreme Court directed VVPAT verification of 5 randomly selected EVMs per constituency (Association for Democratic Reforms v. Union of India, unreported, April 8, 2019).

### Reserved Constituencies

Articles 330-332 provide for the reservation of seats in the Lok Sabha and state legislative assemblies for Scheduled Castes (SCs) and Scheduled Tribes (STs) in proportion to their population. The reservation, originally intended for ten years, has been extended repeatedly (most recently by the 104th Amendment Act, 2020, extending reservation until 2030). The 106th Amendment Act, 2023 (Nari Shakti Vandan Adhiniyam), provides for one-third reservation of seats for women in the Lok Sabha and state legislative assemblies, to be implemented after a delimitation exercise following the next census.

### Anti-Defection Law

The Tenth Schedule of the Constitution (added by the 52nd Amendment, 1985) provides for the disqualification of members who defect from their party. The Speaker of the Lok Sabha (or the Chairman of the state legislature) decides disqualification petitions, with the Supreme Court holding in Kihoto Hollohan v. Zachillhu, 1992 Supp (2) SCC 651, that the Speaker's decision is subject to judicial review.

## Africa (Nigeria and South Africa)

### Nigeria: Electoral Challenges and Reform

Nigeria's electoral history has been marked by widespread irregularities, violence, and contested outcomes, though the 2015 election (which saw the first democratic transfer of power from a ruling party to the opposition) and subsequent reforms have strengthened electoral institutions.

**Electoral Act, 2022:** This legislation, signed into law on February 25, 2022, introduced significant reforms:
- **Section 47:** Mandates the use of technology for voter accreditation, specifically the Bimodal Voter Accreditation System (BVAS), which uses fingerprint and facial recognition to verify voters.
- **Section 50:** Requires INEC to transmit election results from polling units to the INEC Result Viewing (IReV) portal for public access.
- **Section 84:** Establishes direct primaries or indirect primaries or consensus as methods for party candidate selection, with the original provision mandating direct primaries being removed after the President withheld assent and the provision was amended.
- **Section 134:** Regulates campaign finance, setting spending limits (presidential candidates: 5 billion naira; gubernatorial: 1 billion naira).

**2023 Presidential Election Dispute:** The election of Bola Ahmed Tinubu was challenged before the Presidential Election Petition Court (PEPC) by Atiku Abubakar (PDP) and Peter Obi (Labour Party) in *Atiku Abubakar v. INEC & Ors.* and *Peter Obi v. INEC & Ors.* (2023). The PEPC and subsequently the Supreme Court upheld Tinubu's election, though the dissenting opinions raised substantial questions about BVAS compliance and the validity of results from several states.

### South Africa: Proportional Representation

South Africa uses a closed-list proportional representation system for the National Assembly (400 members), with seats allocated from national and regional party lists. The Electoral Commission of South Africa (IEC), established under Section 190 of the Constitution and operationalized by the Electoral Commission Act 51 of 1996, has been consistently rated among the most trustworthy electoral bodies in Africa.

**The New Nation Movement Case:** In *New Nation Movement NPC v. President of the Republic of South Africa*, [2020] ZACC 11, the Constitutional Court held that the Electoral Act 73 of 1998, insofar as it required candidates for the National Assembly and provincial legislatures to be members of or nominated by political parties, was unconstitutional because it excluded independent candidates. The Electoral Amendment Act 1 of 2023 was enacted to comply with this ruling, allowing independent candidates to contest national and provincial elections alongside party-list candidates.

**Floor-Crossing:** South Africa experimented with permitting floor-crossing (allowing elected members to change parties without losing their seats) through the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa Amendment Act 18 of 2002 and the Loss or Retention of Membership of National and Provincial Legislatures Act 22 of 2002. These provisions were repealed by the Constitution Fifteenth Amendment Act 5 of 2008 and the Constitution Sixteenth Amendment Act of 2009, which abolished floor-crossing due to its destabilizing effects on the proportional representation system.

## Latin America (Brazil, Mexico, Colombia)

### Brazil: Electronic Voting Pioneer

Brazil has been a global leader in electronic voting, introducing the urna eletronica (electronic ballot box, a DRE -- Direct Recording Electronic -- system) in 1996 for municipal elections and deploying it nationwide for the 2000 elections.

**The Urna Eletronica:** Developed by the TSE in partnership with Brazilian technology firms, the urna is a standalone electronic device (not connected to the internet) with a numeric keypad. Voters enter the candidate's number, see the candidate's photo and party on screen, and confirm their vote. At the close of polls, each urna produces a printed summary (Boletim de Urna, BU) of results, which is publicly posted and transmitted to the TSE's totalization system.

**Electoral Justice:** Brazil's electoral judiciary is a constitutionally distinct branch (Article 118-121) comprising the TSE (7 members, including 3 STF ministers, 2 STJ ministers, and 2 lawyers appointed by the President), 27 Tribunais Regionais Eleitorais (TREs, one per state), electoral judges, and electoral juntas. This integrated system exercises both administrative (organizing elections) and judicial (resolving disputes) functions.

**Campaign Finance and Fundo Eleitoral:** Following the STF's ruling in ADI 4.650 (2015) that corporate campaign contributions violated the equality principle and the republican form of government, Brazil enacted Law 13.487/2017 creating the Fundo Especial de Financiamento de Campanha (Special Campaign Financing Fund, FEFC), a public fund distributed among parties based on their legislative representation.

**2022 Election and Institutional Stress:** The 2022 presidential election, in which President Lula da Silva defeated incumbent Jair Bolsonaro in a run-off (50.9% to 49.1%), was preceded by extensive questioning of the electronic voting system's integrity by Bolsonaro and supporters. The January 8, 2023 attacks on the Three Powers Square in Brasilia tested the electoral system's institutional resilience, leading to criminal proceedings (the TSE had previously barred Bolsonaro from running for office until 2030 for misuse of power and abuse of economic power related to his questioning of the electoral system).

### Mexico: The INE and Institutional Independence

Mexico's Instituto Nacional Electoral (INE, renamed from IFE in 2014) is an autonomous constitutional body (Article 41) with a General Council of 11 citizen councilors serving staggered nine-year terms, selected by the Chamber of Deputies through a two-thirds vote.

**Mixed Electoral System:** Mexico's Chamber of Deputies (500 members) uses a mixed system: 300 deputies elected by FPTP in single-member districts and 200 elected by closed-list PR in five multi-member regional constituencies. The Senate (128 members) uses a system combining majority, first minority, and PR seats.

**Gender Parity:** Mexico has been a global leader in gender parity requirements. Constitutional reforms (2014) and INE regulations require that parties nominate candidates on a 50/50 gender parity basis for both federal and state elections. The Supreme Court (SCJN) and the Electoral Tribunal (TEPJF) have enforced parity requirements rigorously, including requiring parties to nominate women for competitive (not merely token) districts.

### Colombia: Peace Process and Electoral Inclusion

Colombia's 2016 peace agreement with the FARC (Fuerzas Armadas Revolucionarias de Colombia) included provisions for political participation by former combatants, with the FARC-successor party Comunes guaranteed 5 seats each in the Senate and Chamber of Representatives for the 2018 and 2022 electoral periods (Legislative Act 03/2017). Additionally, 16 special peace constituencies (Circunscripciones Transitorias Especiales de Paz, CTEP) were created for conflict-affected areas, implemented for the first time in the 2022 elections.

## Southeast Asia (Indonesia and Philippines)

### Indonesia: The World's Most Complex Single-Day Election

Indonesia's 2024 elections involved approximately 204 million eligible voters casting ballots simultaneously for the president, 580 DPR (national legislature) seats, 136 DPD (Regional Representative Council) seats, and thousands of provincial and district/city legislative seats, all on the same day using paper ballots and manual counting.

**Open-List Proportional Representation:** Following the Constitutional Court's Decision No. 22-24/PUU-VI/2008, which struck down the closed-list system as violating voters' sovereignty, Indonesia uses open-list PR for the DPR. Voters can choose either a party or a specific candidate on the party list. A candidate who receives votes equal to or exceeding 100% of the BPP (Bilangan Pembagi Pemilih, seat quota calculated as total valid votes divided by available seats in the constituency) is elected regardless of list position.

**Presidential Threshold:** Article 222 of Law No. 7/2017 on General Elections requires presidential candidates to be nominated by political parties or coalitions holding at least 20% of DPR seats or 25% of valid votes in the previous election. The Constitutional Court upheld this threshold in Decision No. 53/PUU-XV/2017, finding it did not violate the constitutional right to participate in government.

**Election Dispute Resolution:** Presidential election disputes are decided exclusively by the Mahkamah Konstitusi (MK) under Article 24C(1) of the Constitution. The 2024 MK decision upholding the election of Prabowo Subianto (Decision No. 1/PHPU.PRES-XXII/2024) was controversial due to the prior involvement of then-Chief Justice Anwar Usman (who was Prabowo's relative by marriage) in the case regarding the age qualification of vice-presidential candidate Gibran Rakabuming Raka, leading to Anwar's removal as Chief Justice by the MK Ethics Council.

### Philippines: Automated Elections

The Philippines transitioned to automated elections through RA 8436 (1997) and RA 9369 (2007). The system uses Optical Mark Reader (OMR) technology: voters shade ovals on paper ballots, which are fed into Vote Counting Machines (VCMs, formerly PCOS -- Precinct Count Optical Scan -- machines) that read and tally votes electronically.

**COMELEC:** The Commission on Elections comprises a Chairman and six Commissioners, appointed by the President from a list prepared by the JBC, serving seven-year terms without reappointment. COMELEC has constitutional authority to enforce and administer all laws relating to elections, decide election cases (except contests involving the President, Vice-President, Senators, and Members of the House, which are decided by the Presidential Electoral Tribunal, Senate Electoral Tribunal, and House of Representatives Electoral Tribunal, respectively).

**The Party-List System:** Republic Act No. 7941 (Party-List System Act, 1995) allocates 20% of House seats (currently 59 of 316) to party-list organizations representing marginalized and underrepresented sectors. The Supreme Court in Veterans Federation of the Philippines v. COMELEC (G.R. No. 136781, 2000) initially limited party-list seats to three per organization and imposed a 2% threshold, but this was modified in Banat v. COMELEC (G.R. No. 179271, 2009), which removed the three-seat cap and revised the allocation formula.

## Comparative Analysis

### Electoral Technology and Trust

The Global South has been more willing to adopt electronic voting technology than most Western democracies, with Brazil, India, and the Philippines implementing various forms of automated elections. However, the relationship between technology and electoral trust is not straightforward. Brazil's urna eletronica faced its most serious legitimacy challenge in 2022 precisely because of the absence of a voter-verifiable paper trail (subsequently addressed by ongoing debates about VVPAT implementation). India's EVMs have been subject to persistent conspiracy theories despite multiple security audits. Indonesia's retention of paper ballots and manual counting -- at the cost of enormous logistical complexity and the deaths of over 500 election workers from exhaustion in 2019 -- reflects a deliberate choice to prioritize transparency and public trust over efficiency.

### Quotas, Reservations, and Substantive Representation

The Global South has experimented more extensively with mechanisms for ensuring descriptive representation than Western democracies. India's reservation system for SCs and STs (since 1950) and the 2023 women's reservation amendment, Mexico's 50/50 gender parity, Indonesia's 30% candidacy quota, Brazil's 30% candidacy quota (with the TSE ruling in 2018 that campaign fund distribution must proportionally reflect the gender composition of candidacies), and South Africa's voluntary party quotas (the ANC has maintained a 50% women quota since 2007) represent diverse approaches to the same fundamental challenge of ensuring that formal electoral equality translates into substantive representation.

### Electoral Authoritarianism and Competitive Elections

The distinction between "competitive authoritarian" regimes (Steven Levitsky and Lucan Way) and democracies with deficits is a persistent analytical challenge in the Global South. China's people's congress system, while containing elements of competition at the grassroots level, does not meet standard definitions of democratic elections due to CPC organizational control over candidate nomination and the absence of multi-party competition. Nigeria's elections, while competitive, have been marred by irregularities that raise questions about the integrity of outcomes. The challenge for comparative analysis is to assess electoral systems on their own terms while maintaining meaningful normative standards.
