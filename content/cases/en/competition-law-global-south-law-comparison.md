## Summary

Competition law across the Global South has undergone a dramatic expansion since the 2000s, with jurisdictions developing distinct enforcement philosophies that reflect their economic structures, development priorities, and political economies. China's Anti-Monopoly Law (反垄断法, AML), originally enacted in 2008 and substantially amended in 2022, served as the legal foundation for the unprecedented 2021 tech sector crackdown that culminated in the record RMB 18.228 billion (USD 2.8 billion) fine against Alibaba Group for abuse of market dominance through "choose one from two" (二选一) exclusive dealing practices. India's Competition Commission (CCI), established under the Competition Act, 2002 (as amended 2023), has become increasingly active in digital markets, imposing a landmark INR 1,337.76 crore (approximately USD 161 million) penalty on Google for abuse of dominance in Android (CCI Case No. 39/2018, affirmed by NCLAT in 2023). South Africa's Competition Tribunal and Competition Commission, operating under the Competition Act 89 of 1998 (as amended by the Competition Amendment Act 18 of 2018), uniquely integrate public interest considerations including BEE, employment, and industrial policy into merger review. Brazil's Administrative Council for Economic Defense (CADE), restructured by Law 12,529/2011, has established a mature enforcement regime with particular strength in cartel prosecution, having imposed its largest fine of BRL 3.1 billion on a cement cartel in 2014. The Philippine Competition Commission (PCC), created by the Philippine Competition Act (Republic Act No. 10667, 2015), represents one of the newest competition authorities in the Global South, still building institutional capacity and enforcement jurisprudence.

## China

### Legal Framework

China's Anti-Monopoly Law (中华人民共和国反垄断法), originally enacted on August 30, 2007 and effective August 1, 2008, was substantially amended on June 24, 2022 (effective August 1, 2022). The 2022 amendment strengthened the law by:

- **Article 56**: Increasing maximum fines for failure to notify concentrations from RMB 500,000 to up to 10% of previous year's turnover if the transaction has anti-competitive effects, or up to RMB 5 million if no such effects are found.
- **Article 63**: Introducing a "safe harbor" for vertical agreements where the undertaking's market share is below a threshold to be set by the State Council Anti-Monopoly Commission (currently being drafted).
- **Article 22, paragraph 2**: Adding a specific provision on undertakings with dominant positions using "data and algorithms, technology, capital advantages, or platform rules" to engage in abuse of dominance -- directly codifying the basis for platform economy enforcement.
- **Article 67**: Introducing personal criminal liability for concluding and implementing monopoly agreements, including bid-rigging, with penalties under the Criminal Law.

The State Administration for Market Regulation (SAMR), established in 2018 through a merger of the former three enforcement agencies (NDRC's Price Supervision Bureau, MOFCOM's Anti-Monopoly Bureau, and SAIC's Anti-Monopoly and Anti-Unfair Competition Bureau), serves as the unified enforcement authority through its Anti-Monopoly Bureau.

### Tech Sector Enforcement

The period 2020-2022 witnessed an unprecedented antitrust enforcement campaign targeting China's platform economy giants:

- **Alibaba Group (2021)**: SAMR imposed a record RMB 18.228 billion fine (4% of 2019 domestic revenue) for abuse of market dominance through exclusive dealing arrangements ("二选一," choose one from two), compelling merchants to deal exclusively on Alibaba's platforms. The decision (State Administration for Market Regulation Administrative Penalty Decision [2021] No. 28) found that Alibaba held a dominant position in China's online retail platform market (market share exceeding 60%) and abused it through contractual restrictions, search traffic demotion, and technical interference against merchants who also sold on rival platforms (principally JD.com and Pinduoduo).
- **Meituan (2022)**: SAMR fined Meituan RMB 3.442 billion (3% of 2020 domestic revenue) for similar "choose one from two" practices in the online food delivery market (Administrative Penalty Decision [2022] No. 11).
- **Didi Global (2022)**: The Cyberspace Administration of China (CAC) fined Didi RMB 8.026 billion under a combined cybersecurity, data security, and personal information protection enforcement action, while SAMR separately addressed its failure to notify the acquisition of Uber China in 2016 and other mergers.
- **Tencent (2021-2022)**: SAMR imposed administrative penalties on multiple Tencent subsidiaries for failure to notify prior concentrations (including investments in Sogou, DouYu, and others) and required Tencent to relinquish exclusive music licensing rights held by Tencent Music Entertainment.

The Anti-Monopoly Commission of the State Council issued the Guidelines on Anti-Monopoly in the Platform Economy (国务院反垄断委员会关于平台经济领域的反垄断指南, February 7, 2021), providing interpretive guidance on market definition for multi-sided platforms, hub-and-spoke arrangements, most-favored-nation clauses, self-preferencing, and algorithmic collusion.

### Merger Control

China's merger control regime, formalized by the Provisions on Thresholds for Notification of Concentrations of Undertakings (2024 revision), requires notification when: (a) combined worldwide turnover exceeds RMB 12 billion and at least two parties each have China turnover exceeding RMB 800 million; or (b) combined China turnover exceeds RMB 4 billion and at least two parties each have China turnover exceeding RMB 800 million. SAMR reviews approximately 700-800 notifications annually, with conditional clearances in approximately 5-10 cases per year. Notable conditional clearances include Broadcom/VMware (2023), Microsoft/Activision Blizzard (2023), and AMD/Xilinx (2022).

## India

### Legal Framework

The Competition Act, 2002, as substantially amended by the Competition (Amendment) Act, 2023, establishes the Competition Commission of India (CCI) as an expert body with quasi-judicial functions. The 2023 amendment introduced several significant reforms:

- **Section 5A**: Deal value threshold for merger notification -- combinations where the value of the transaction exceeds INR 2,000 crore (approximately USD 240 million) and the target has "substantial business operations in India" require notification, regardless of whether asset or turnover thresholds are met. This provision targets "killer acquisitions" in digital markets where targets have high valuations but low revenues.
- **Section 3(4)**: Expanded "hub-and-spoke" cartel provisions, explicitly bringing facilitators of horizontal agreements within the prohibition.
- **Section 18A**: Settlement and commitment mechanism allowing parties to offer remedies in abuse of dominance and vertical agreement cases, reducing the CCI's enforcement burden.
- **Section 46A**: Reduced penalties for enterprises with global turnover up to INR 300 crore, addressing concerns about disproportionate fines on smaller enterprises.

The CCI is composed of a Chairperson and not more than six Members (reduced from the original provision for ten members). Appeals lie to the National Company Law Appellate Tribunal (NCLAT) and thereafter to the Supreme Court of India.

### Digital Markets Enforcement

The CCI has been among the most active Global South competition authorities in digital markets:

- **Google Android (CCI Case No. 39/2018)**: The CCI imposed a penalty of INR 1,337.76 crore on Google for abuse of dominance through mandatory pre-installation of Google apps on Android devices, anti-fragmentation agreements restricting OEM freedom, and tying of Google Play Store with Google Mobile Services. The NCLAT largely upheld the CCI's decision in Google LLC v. CCI (2023), finding that Google's Android licensing practices constituted abuse of dominance under Section 4 of the Competition Act. The Supreme Court declined to stay the NCLAT's order.
- **Google Play Billing (CCI Case No. 07/2020)**: The CCI fined Google INR 936.44 crore for imposing mandatory use of Google Play's billing system for in-app purchases, constituting leveraging and unfair conditions under Section 4(2)(a)(i) and 4(2)(d).
- **Amazon/Future Retail**: The CCI's investigation into Amazon's 2019 investment in Future Coupons Pvt. Ltd. (later entangled in the Amazon-Future Group-Reliance dispute) raised questions about the interaction between FDI approval conditions and competition law. The CCI suspended its approval of the Amazon-Future Coupons combination after allegations that Amazon misrepresented the nature and purpose of the investment.
- **WhatsApp Privacy Policy**: The CCI's investigation into WhatsApp's 2021 privacy policy update (requiring data sharing with Facebook/Meta) under Section 4 (abuse of dominance) and Section 3(4) (vertical agreement) was upheld by the Supreme Court in WhatsApp LLC v. CCI (2024), rejecting Meta's argument that the matter fell exclusively within the remit of data protection legislation.

### Combination Regulation

India's merger control regime applies to combinations exceeding specified thresholds: joint assets exceeding INR 2,000 crore or joint turnover exceeding INR 6,000 crore for parties to the transaction; and joint assets exceeding INR 8,000 crore or joint turnover exceeding INR 24,000 crore for the group. The 2023 amendment added the deal value threshold of INR 2,000 crore. The CCI operates a voluntary pre-filing consultation mechanism and a "green channel" automatic approval for combinations with no horizontal or vertical overlaps (CCI Regulation 5A).

## Africa

### South Africa

South Africa's competition regime, established by the Competition Act 89 of 1998 (as amended by the Competition Amendment Acts of 2009 and 2018), is distinguished by its integration of public interest considerations into competition assessment:

- **Section 2**: The purpose of the Act includes promoting employment, advancing social and economic welfare, and ensuring equitable participation by historically disadvantaged persons (HDPs).
- **Section 12A(3)** (as amended 2018): Merger review requires consideration of public interest factors including impact on employment, the ability of national industries to compete internationally, the ability of SMEs or firms controlled by HDPs to become competitive, and the promotion of a greater spread of ownership particularly by HDPs and workers.
- **Section 8(1)(d)(ii)**: The 2018 amendment introduced an abuse of dominance provision specifically addressing excessive pricing of goods or services that are essential to participation in the economy by consumers.

The Competition Commission investigates and refers cases to the Competition Tribunal (for adjudication of large mergers and prohibited practices) and the Competition Appeal Court. The Commission is headed by a Commissioner and has approximately 200 staff members.

Notable enforcement actions include:

- **Pioneer Foods (2010)**: The Competition Tribunal confirmed a consent order with Pioneer Foods in the bread cartel case, imposing a record ZAR 195.7 million administrative penalty and establishing an investment and price reduction fund. The broader bread cartel investigation resulted in aggregate fines exceeding ZAR 500 million across multiple respondents (Tiger Brands, Foodcorp, Premier Foods).
- **Walmart/Massmart (2012)**: The Competition Tribunal approved Walmart's ZAR 16.5 billion acquisition of a majority stake in Massmart subject to conditions including a ZAR 240 million supplier development fund and a three-year moratorium on retrenchments, marking the first major application of public interest conditions in a cross-border merger. The Competition Appeal Court partially modified the conditions in Massmart Holdings Ltd v. Minister of Economic Development (2012).
- **Online Intermediation Platforms Inquiry (2021-2023)**: The Competition Commission conducted a market inquiry into online intermediation platforms under Section 43B, examining Google, Apple, Meta, Amazon, and local platforms. Provisional findings (March 2023) identified concerns regarding self-preferencing in search, app store gatekeeping, and algorithmic pricing.

### Nigeria

Nigeria's competition law is governed by the Federal Competition and Consumer Protection Act 2018 (FCCPA), which established the Federal Competition and Consumer Protection Commission (FCCPC) and the Competition and Consumer Protection Tribunal (CCPT). The FCCPA replaced the consumer protection mandate of the former Consumer Protection Council and introduced comprehensive competition provisions.

Key features include:

- **Section 59**: Prohibition of agreements, decisions, and concerted practices that prevent, restrict, or distort competition. The FCCPC has interpretive guidelines treating horizontal price-fixing, output restriction, and market allocation as per se violations.
- **Section 70**: Prohibition of abuse of dominant position, including imposing unfair purchase or selling prices, limiting production or markets, applying dissimilar conditions to equivalent transactions, and tying.
- **Section 92-95**: Merger control with mandatory notification for mergers meeting threshold requirements (FCCPC Merger Review Regulations, 2020: combined turnover exceeding NGN 1 billion or where the FCCPC determines that the merger may substantially prevent or lessen competition).
- **Section 43**: Leniency provisions allowing the FCCPC to grant immunity from penalties to the first applicant in cartel investigations who provides evidence of cartel conduct.

The FCCPC, chaired by a Chief Executive (the inaugural and current CEO being Babatunde Irukera, appointed 2017), has pursued several high-profile matters including investigations into alleged anti-competitive practices by telecommunications operators (MTN, Airtel, Globacom) and the pharmaceutical sector. However, the FCCPC's jurisdictional authority was challenged by sector-specific regulators; the Court of Appeal in NCC v. FCCPC (2022) clarified the concurrent jurisdiction of the FCCPC and sector regulators, holding that the FCCPA applies to all sectors unless expressly excluded by an Act of the National Assembly.

## Latin America

### Brazil

CADE (Conselho Administrativo de Defesa Econômica), restructured by Law 12,529/2011, operates through an Administrative Tribunal (with a President and six Commissioners) and a General Superintendence (the investigative and prosecutorial arm). Brazil's competition regime is notable for:

- **Pre-merger notification**: Mandatory and suspensory since Law 12,529/2011, with notification required when: (a) at least one of the economic groups involved had annual gross revenue in Brazil exceeding BRL 750 million; and (b) at least one other economic group had annual gross revenue in Brazil exceeding BRL 75 million. CADE reviews approximately 400-500 transactions annually.
- **Cartel enforcement**: CADE has established one of the most effective cartel enforcement programs in the Global South, utilizing a robust leniency program (Agreement of Leniency under Article 86 of Law 12,529) and cease-and-desist agreements (Termo de Compromisso de Cessação, TCC). The cement cartel case (2014) resulted in aggregate fines of approximately BRL 3.1 billion against Votorantim, InterCement, Nassau, Cimentos Liz, Itambé, and Ciplan for market allocation spanning over 20 years.
- **Gun-jumping enforcement**: CADE has been particularly aggressive in prosecuting premature implementation of notified transactions (gun-jumping), imposing fines in cases including JBS/Friboi, OI/Portugal Telecom, and Technicolor/Cisco.
- **Digital markets**: CADE's Working Paper on Digital Markets (2020) and subsequent investigations into Google's advertising practices, iFood's exclusivity clauses with restaurants (mirroring China's "choose one from two" enforcement), and Meta/WhatsApp data sharing demonstrate growing digital competition focus.

### Mexico

Mexico operates a unique dual-authority competition system: the Federal Economic Competition Commission (COFECE) has general competition jurisdiction, while the Federal Telecommunications Institute (IFT) exercises exclusive competition authority over the broadcasting and telecommunications sectors. This structure, established by the 2013 constitutional reform (Article 28 of the Mexican Constitution), was specifically designed to address the market dominance of América Móvil/Telmex (Carlos Slim) in telecommunications and Televisa in broadcasting.

The IFT designated Telmex/Telcel as a "preponderant economic agent" (agente económico preponderante) in 2014, finding it held over 70% of the telecommunications market, and imposed asymmetric regulation including interconnection obligations, infrastructure sharing requirements, and advertising restrictions. COFECE separately investigated Walmart Mexico for abuse of buying power vis-à-vis suppliers, resulting in a MXN 158.8 million fine in 2023.

## Southeast Asia

### Indonesia

Indonesia's competition law is governed by Law 5/1999 concerning the Prohibition of Monopolistic Practices and Unfair Business Competition, enforced by the Business Competition Supervisory Commission (Komisi Pengawas Persaingan Usaha, KPPU). The KPPU is a quasi-judicial body with authority to investigate, adjudicate, and impose sanctions for competition violations.

Key features include:

- **Articles 4-16**: Prohibited agreements including oligopoly (Article 4), price-fixing (Article 5), price discrimination (Article 6), predatory pricing (Article 7), resale price maintenance (Article 8), territorial division (Article 9), boycotts (Article 10), cartels (Article 11), trusts (Article 12), oligopsony (Article 13), vertical integration (Article 14), closed agreements (Article 15), and agreements with foreign parties (Article 16).
- **Articles 17-19**: Prohibition of monopoly (Article 17), monopsony (Article 18), and market dominance (Article 19), with a dominant position presumed at 50% market share for a single undertaking or 75% for two or three undertakings jointly.
- **Articles 28-29**: Merger control with post-merger notification within 30 working days for transactions exceeding IDR 2.5 trillion in combined assets or IDR 5 trillion in combined turnover. Voluntary pre-merger consultation is available under KPPU Regulation 3/2023.

The KPPU has adjudicated numerous cases across sectors including construction bidding cartels, aviation fuel pricing, motorcycle distribution, and telecommunications. The Supreme Court of Indonesia (Mahkamah Agung) hears appeals from KPPU decisions through the district court. A draft amendment to Law 5/1999 has been under legislative consideration since 2020, proposing increased penalties (up to 25% of net profit or 5% of turnover), leniency program formalization, and pre-merger notification.

### Philippines

The Philippine Competition Act (Republic Act No. 10667, 2015) established the Philippine Competition Commission (PCC) as an independent quasi-judicial body with five Commissioners. The PCC exercises jurisdiction over anti-competitive agreements (Section 14), abuse of dominant position (Section 15), and merger review (Section 16-23).

Key provisions include:

- **Section 14**: Prohibits agreements that substantially prevent, restrict, or lessen competition, with per se prohibition of price-fixing, bid-rigging, output restriction, and market allocation among competitors.
- **Section 15**: Prohibits abuse of dominant position, defined as an entity's ability to control prices, restrict competition, or exclude competitors. Specific abuses include imposing unfair prices, limiting production, applying discriminatory conditions, tying, and predatory pricing.
- **Section 17**: Compulsory merger notification for transactions where the aggregate value of assets or revenue exceeds PHP 7 billion (adjusted annually by the PCC, current threshold for 2024-2025) and both acquiring and acquired entities have assets or revenue exceeding PHP 2.8 billion.
- **Section 29**: Maximum administrative fines of PHP 100 million for first offense and PHP 250 million for second and subsequent offenses for anti-competitive agreements and abuse of dominance.

The PCC's notable enforcement actions include the review of the Globe-PLDT tower asset sale to shared tower companies, investigation of price coordination in the cement industry, and challenges to vertical restraints in the pharmaceutical distribution chain. The PCC has issued Merger Review Guidelines (2017), Leniency Program Guidelines (2017), and a Framework for Competition Assessment of Government Policies (2018).

## Comparative Analysis

### Institutional Design

Competition authorities in the Global South reflect different institutional models: China's SAMR Anti-Monopoly Bureau operates within the executive branch with direct State Council oversight; India's CCI is an independent statutory body with quasi-judicial functions; South Africa's separation of investigation (Commission) and adjudication (Tribunal) creates procedural independence; Brazil's CADE combines both functions with an internal separation between General Superintendence and Administrative Tribunal; and the Philippines' PCC mirrors the US FTC model with a multi-member commission. Nigeria's FCCPC uniquely combines competition and consumer protection mandates.

### Development Policy Integration

South Africa's explicit incorporation of public interest considerations (employment, HDP participation, SME competitiveness) into competition assessment is the most developed model of integrating competition policy with development objectives. India's 2023 amendment reducing penalties for smaller enterprises and the CCI's advocacy function under Section 49 reflect similar concerns. China's selective enforcement -- pursuing platform antitrust while tolerating SOE market power -- reveals the political economy of competition enforcement in state-capitalist systems.

### Digital Markets Convergence

Despite different legal traditions, competition authorities across the Global South are converging on similar concerns about digital platform power: exclusive dealing (China's "choose one from two," Brazil's iFood investigation), tying and bundling (India's Google Android case), self-preferencing (South Africa's online intermediation inquiry), and algorithmic pricing. This convergence suggests potential for South-South regulatory cooperation, evidenced by the BRICS Competition Law and Policy Centre established in 2016 and the ICN (International Competition Network) engagement of all surveyed jurisdictions.
