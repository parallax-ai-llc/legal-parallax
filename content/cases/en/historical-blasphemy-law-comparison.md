## Summary

Blasphemy -- speech or conduct showing contempt or irreverence toward the divine, the sacred, or established religious authority -- has been punished across virtually all known legal systems throughout history, reflecting the intimate connection between political authority and religious legitimacy in pre-modern societies. Ancient Near Eastern civilizations, where kings ruled as agents or representatives of the gods, treated offenses against divine order as direct threats to the cosmic and social fabric, punishing sacrilege, profanation of temples, and violation of religious taboos with death, mutilation, or divine curse. Roman law developed the concept of sacrilegium (theft from a temple, later expanded to include any offense against divine or imperial sanctity), which evolved under the Christian Empire into a comprehensive prohibition of heresy, apostasy, and religious dissent enforced through the machinery of state coercion. East Asian legal traditions, while not centered on theistic blasphemy in the Abrahamic sense, severely punished offenses against imperial sanctity, ancestral rites, and Confucian orthodoxy, with the Tang Code's "Great Disrespect" (大不敬) category encompassing acts ranging from mishandling imperial objects to performing unauthorized sacrifices. Islamic law distinguished between blasphemy against God or the Prophet (sabb Allah/sabb al-rasul), apostasy (riddah), and heresy (zandaqa), developing elaborate jurisprudence on the conditions under which each offense warranted the death penalty, with significant disagreement among the schools about the availability of repentance as a defense. Germanic and medieval European law treated blasphemy, heresy, and witchcraft as overlapping offenses against the divine order, culminating in the systematic persecution of heretics through the Inquisition (established 1231) and the witch trials of the 15th-17th centuries. English common law recognized blasphemy as a criminal offense from at least the 17th century, with Lord Chief Justice Hale declaring in Taylor's Case (1676) that "Christianity is parcel of the laws of England" and that attacks upon it were punishable at common law, a doctrine that persisted in increasingly attenuated form until the abolition of the common law offense of blasphemy by the Criminal Justice and Immigration Act 2008.

## Ancient Near East

### Mesopotamian Sacred Order

In ancient Mesopotamia, the distinction between "religious" and "secular" law was essentially nonexistent. All law derived from divine authority, and offenses against the gods were simultaneously offenses against the social order.

**Temple Sacrilege:** The Code of Hammurabi (Law 6) prescribed death for stealing property of a god or palace, treating temple theft as the most severe form of larceny. This severity reflected the understanding that temple property was divine property, and its theft constituted a direct offense against the deity.

**False Oath and Perjury:** Swearing falsely by a god was treated as an offense against the divine order. The Laws of Eshnunna (Section 37) and the Code of Hammurabi (multiple provisions) prescribed severe penalties for false oaths. The oath (niš ilim, "the life of the god") invoked divine witness, and perjury was understood as simultaneously deceiving the court and profaning the god's name.

**Ritual Impurity and Prohibited Practices:** Mesopotamian texts record extensive prohibitions on improper ritual conduct:
- The surpu incantation series (c. 1000 BCE) catalogued offenses that could provoke divine wrath, including: entering a temple in a state of impurity, eating taboo foods, performing unauthorized rituals, and revealing divine secrets.
- The šurpu texts also condemned sorcery and malevolent magic (kišpu), treated as an offense against the divine order. The Code of Hammurabi (Law 2) addressed false accusations of sorcery: "If a man has charged another with sorcery and cannot prove it, the one who was charged shall go to the river [ordeal]; if the river overcomes him, the accuser shall take his house."

### Egyptian Religious Offenses

Egypt's theocratic system treated offenses against the gods and the pharaoh (himself divine) as the gravest possible crimes:

- **Tomb Desecration:** The systematic desecration of tombs was treated not merely as theft but as sacrilege, threatening the deceased's survival in the afterlife. The tomb robbery trials of the 20th Dynasty (c. 1150-1070 BCE) record severe punishments including impalement for those who violated royal and noble tombs.
- **The Amarna Revolution:** Pharaoh Akhenaten's (r. 1353-1336 BCE) attempted suppression of the Amun cult and the subsequent restoration under Tutankhamun illustrate how religious heterodoxy could be treated as a form of sacrilege by the restored orthodoxy, with Akhenaten's name being erased from monuments (damnatio memoriae).

## Roman Law

### Sacrilegium and Its Expansion

Roman sacrilege law evolved from a narrow concept of temple theft into a comprehensive framework for enforcing religious orthodoxy under the Christian Empire.

**Republican and Early Imperial Period:**

- **Sacrilegium (Temple Robbery):** Originally, sacrilegium meant literally "stealing sacred things" -- theft from a temple. The Twelve Tables (Table VIII) addressed this offense, and the lex Julia peculatus (c. 46 BCE) established jurisdiction over theft of sacred, public, and religious property.
- **Extension to Imperial Worship:** As the imperial cult developed, sacrilegium was extended to cover offenses against the emperor's divine or quasi-divine status. Under Theodosius I (Edict of Thessalonica, 380 CE), sacrilegium was redefined to encompass deviation from Nicene Christian orthodoxy: "those foolish madmen who dissent...shall be branded with the ignominious name of heretics" and shall be subject to divine and imperial punishment.

**The Christian Empire:**

The Theodosian Code (438 CE) and the Justinian Code (534 CE) systematized the criminal prosecution of religious dissent:

- **CTh. 16.1.2 (380 CE, Edict of Thessalonica):** Declared Nicene Christianity the only lawful religion. Dissenters were declared guilty of sacrilegium and subject to "the punishment which our authority, guided by heavenly wisdom, shall think fit to inflict."
- **CTh. 16.5 (De haereticis):** A comprehensive title containing over sixty laws against various heresies. Penalties ranged from fines and exile to confiscation of property and, for relapsed heretics and Manichaeans, death.
- **CTh. 16.7 (De apostatis):** Addressed apostasy from Christianity. Apostates lost the right to make wills, testify in court, or hold office. Their property was subject to confiscation.
- **CTh. 16.10 (De paganis):** Prohibited pagan worship, sacrifice, and temple access. Theodosius ordered the closing of temples (391 CE); Justinian closed the Academy of Athens (529 CE), ending the last institutional center of pagan philosophy.

**Justinian's Legislation:**

- **Novel 77 (535 CE):** "Concerning blasphemers" -- prescribed death for those who blasphemed God, Christ, the Virgin Mary, or the saints. Justinian explicitly linked blasphemy to natural disasters: "Because of such crimes there are famines, earthquakes, and pestilences, wherefore we admonish men to abstain from the aforesaid unlawful acts."
- **CJ 1.5 (De haereticis):** Consolidated anti-heresy legislation. Manichaeanism was punished with particular severity -- Manichaeans were subject to death even for their first offense, without the opportunity for repentance offered to other heretics.

## East Asian Law

### Imperial Sacrilege and Confucian Orthodoxy

East Asian legal systems did not have a concept of "blasphemy" equivalent to the Abrahamic traditions, but severely punished offenses against imperial sanctity, ancestral worship, and ritual propriety.

**Tang Code (唐律疏議, 653 CE):**

**Great Disrespect (大不敬) -- The Sixth of the Ten Abominations:**

Article 261 of the Tang Code defined "Great Disrespect" as encompassing:

1. **Stealing objects used in imperial sacrifices (盜大祀神御之物):** Objects used in the great state sacrifices to Heaven, Earth, and the imperial ancestors were sacred; their theft constituted not mere larceny but a violation of cosmic order.
2. **Stealing or forging the imperial seal (盜制書及假御寶):** The imperial seal (璽, xi) represented the emperor's authority. Its misuse was equivalent to usurping sovereign power.
3. **Preparing imperial medicine incorrectly (合和御藥有誤):** An error in preparing the emperor's medicine was treated as Great Disrespect regardless of intent, reflecting the sacred status of the imperial person.
4. **Making errors in imperial food (御膳誤犯食禁):** Similarly, violating dietary prohibitions in the emperor's meals was classified as Great Disrespect.
5. **Constructing imperial vessels incorrectly (造御舟船, with error):** Defects in imperial conveyances endangered the sacred person and constituted Great Disrespect.
6. **Cursing the emperor (指斥乘輿):** Direct verbal attacks on the emperor's person, a provision most closely approximating blasphemy in the Abrahamic sense.

Penalties for Great Disrespect ranged from two years of penal servitude to death by strangulation, depending on the specific offense. Critically, offenses classified as Great Disrespect were excluded from the ordinary privileges of rank, the Eight Deliberations (八議), and seasonal amnesties -- demonstrating their gravity within the legal system.

**Heterodox Teachings (左道, Zuodao):**

- **Article 166:** "All who practice heterodox magic (左道亂政)," including shamanic practices, divination intended to influence politics, and teachings contrary to Confucian orthodoxy, were punished by strangulation. This provision served a function analogous to European anti-heresy legislation, though the "orthodoxy" being protected was Confucian statecraft rather than Christian theology.
- **Unauthorized Worship:** The Tang Code prohibited unauthorized performance of state sacrifices. Only the emperor could perform the feng (封) and shan (禪) sacrifices to Heaven and Earth. Usurping these ritual prerogatives was treated as rebellion.

### Korean Application

**Joseon Dynasty (1392-1897):**

- **Neo-Confucian Orthodoxy:** Joseon Korea adopted Neo-Confucianism (Zhu Xi school) as state orthodoxy with particular intensity. Buddhism, which had been the dominant religion under the preceding Goryeo dynasty, was systematically marginalized. Buddhist monks were prohibited from entering the capital; monasteries were confiscated; and the examination system was purged of Buddhist influence.
- **Catholic Persecutions (天主教迫害):** The most dramatic application of anti-heterodoxy laws in Korean history occurred during the Catholic persecutions of 1791 (辛亥迫害), 1801 (辛酉迫害), 1839 (己亥迫害), and 1866 (丙寅迫害). Korean Catholics were prosecuted under existing laws against heterodox practice (左道) and, in the case of the 1791 Jinsan Incident (珍山事件), for destroying ancestral tablets -- an act treated as the ultimate violation of filial piety and Confucian social order. Thousands of Korean Catholics were executed, including 103 who were canonized by Pope Francis in 2014.
- **Abuse of Imperial/Royal Prerogatives:** Acts constituting "Great Disrespect" (大不敬) under the Chinese model were equally criminal in Korea. The Annals of the Joseon Dynasty record numerous cases of officials punished for improper handling of royal documents, unauthorized use of royal symbols, or disrespectful language about the king.

## Islamic Law

### Blasphemy, Apostasy, and Heresy

Islamic jurisprudence distinguishes several categories of religious offense, each with distinct definitions, evidentiary requirements, and penalties.

**Sabb Allah / Sabb al-Rasul (Blasphemy against God or the Prophet):**

Insulting or reviling God (sabb Allah) or the Prophet Muhammad (sabb al-rasul) is treated with extreme gravity by all schools of Islamic jurisprudence, though the specific legal treatment varies:

- **The Hanafi School:** Abu Hanifa distinguished between Muslim and non-Muslim blasphemers. A Muslim who blasphemed against God or the Prophet was treated as an apostate (murtadd), subject to the apostasy penalty (death) but with the opportunity for repentance (istitaba). A dhimmi (non-Muslim subject) who blasphemed violated the terms of the dhimma (protection compact) and could be punished, though Abu Hanifa held that repentance was also available.
- **The Maliki School:** Imam Malik (d. 795) held that the blasphemer against the Prophet must be killed without the opportunity for repentance, whether Muslim or dhimmi. This position was also adopted by Ibn Taymiyyah (d. 1328) in his influential treatise Al-Sarim al-Maslul 'ala Shatim al-Rasul ("The Drawn Sword against Those Who Insult the Messenger").
- **The Shafi'i School:** Al-Shafi'i (d. 820) generally treated blasphemy by a Muslim as apostasy with the opportunity for repentance. For a dhimmi, blasphemy violated the dhimma but did not necessarily warrant death if the dhimma terms did not explicitly prohibit it.
- **The Hanbali School:** Ahmad ibn Hanbal (d. 855) held that the blasphemer against the Prophet must be killed, and the majority of Hanbali scholars followed Ibn Taymiyyah in denying the availability of repentance for this specific offense.

**Riddah (Apostasy):**

Apostasy -- the renunciation of Islam by a Muslim -- is treated as a capital offense by the majority of classical jurists, though the basis and conditions are debated:

- **Hadith Basis:** "Whoever changes his religion, kill him" (man baddala dinahu faqtuluhu, Sahih al-Bukhari 6922). This hadith, attributed to the Prophet, is the primary textual basis for the apostasy penalty.
- **Conditions:** The apostate must be an adult of sound mind who voluntarily renounces Islam. Apostasy under duress is not punishable (Quran 16:106). The majority of jurists required that the apostate be given an opportunity to repent (istitaba), typically three days, before execution.
- **Hanafi Position:** Abu Hanifa held that a female apostate should not be executed but imprisoned until she repented or died, drawing an analogy to the prohibition on killing women in warfare.
- **Modern Debates:** Contemporary scholars including Abdullah Saeed, Taha Jabir al-Alwani, and others have argued that the classical apostasy penalty was rooted in political betrayal (treason/defection to enemies) rather than religious belief per se, and that freedom of conscience is protected by the Quranic principle "There is no compulsion in religion" (la ikraha fi al-din, Quran 2:256).

**Zandaqa (Heresy/Hypocrisy):**

The zindiq -- a heretic who professed Islam outwardly while holding heterodox beliefs secretly -- posed a particular jurisprudential challenge. The Abbasid caliphs conducted systematic persecutions of alleged zandiqa in the 8th-9th centuries, primarily targeting Manichaeans and dualists. The jurists debated whether the zindiq's repentance could be accepted, since by definition his beliefs were concealed. The Maliki school held that the zindiq's repentance was not acceptable because it could never be verified as sincere.

## Medieval European Law

### Heresy and the Inquisition

Medieval European blasphemy and heresy law developed into the most systematic apparatus for the prosecution of religious dissent in human history.

**Early Medieval Period:**

- **Charlemagne's Capitularies (c. 789):** The Admonitio Generalis required all subjects to maintain orthodox belief and prohibited pagan practices. Penalties included fines, corporal punishment, and exile.
- **The Peace and Truce of God (10th-11th centuries):** While primarily directed at limiting violence, these movements also reinforced the authority of the Church to define and punish offenses against the faith.

**The Inquisition (1231 onwards):**

- **Ad Extirpanda (1252):** Pope Innocent IV's bull authorized the use of torture to extract confessions from suspected heretics, subject to certain conditions (no shedding of blood, no permanent injury, confession must be repeated freely afterward). This bull was reissued and confirmed by Alexander IV (1259) and Clement IV (1265).
- **Procedure:** The inquisitorial process differed fundamentally from the accusatorial procedure of common law. The inquisitor acted as investigator, prosecutor, and judge. The accused was not informed of the specific charges or the identity of witnesses. Defense counsel was permitted but under severe restrictions. The "question" (torture) was authorized to extract confessions.
- **Penalties:** Graduated from minor penances (fasting, pilgrimage, wearing crosses) for repentant first offenders to imprisonment, confiscation of property, and "relaxation to the secular arm" (handing over to secular authorities for execution, typically by burning at the stake) for obstinate or relapsed heretics.

**Notable Cases:**
- **The Cathars (1209-1229):** The Albigensian Crusade and subsequent Inquisition in Languedoc resulted in the systematic destruction of the Cathar heresy, with thousands burned at stake. The mass burning at Montségur (1244) -- over 200 Cathars -- was among the most notorious episodes.
- **The Templars (1307-1314):** Accusations of heresy, blasphemy, and idolatry were used by Philip IV of France to destroy the Knights Templar. Jacques de Molay, the last Grand Master, was burned at the stake in 1314 after retracting his confession.
- **Joan of Arc (1431):** Tried and burned for heresy by an ecclesiastical court at Rouen; posthumously rehabilitated in 1456.
- **Jan Hus (1415):** Burned at the Council of Constance despite a safe-conduct guarantee, for doctrines declared heretical.

### Witchcraft

The witch trials of the 15th-17th centuries represented the confluence of blasphemy, heresy, and demonological theology:

- **Malleus Maleficarum (1487):** The Dominican inquisitors Kramer and Sprenger's treatise provided systematic guidance for identifying, prosecuting, and punishing witches. Witchcraft was treated as heresy compounded by a pact with the Devil.
- **Constitutio Criminalis Carolina (1532):** Article 109 prescribed death by burning for harmful sorcery (Zauberei). Article 44 authorized the use of torture in witchcraft investigations.

## Common Law Origins

### Blasphemous Libel

English common law recognized blasphemy as a criminal offense, though its scope and rationale evolved significantly over time.

**Early Development:**

- **De Heretico Comburendo (1401):** The statute authorizing the burning of heretics in England, enacted to suppress Lollard heresy. John Badby (1410) and Sir John Oldcastle (1417) were among those executed under this statute.
- **Act of Supremacy (1534) and Subsequent Legislation:** The English Reformation created new categories of religious offense: denial of royal supremacy was treason; adherence to papal authority was praemunire; and Catholic worship became criminal under the penal laws.
- **The Blasphemy Act 1697 (9 & 10 Will. 3, c. 32):** Prohibited denial of any person of the Trinity, denial of the truth of Christianity, or denial of the authority of Scripture. First offense: incapacity to hold office; second offense: incapacity to bring legal actions and three years' imprisonment.

**Taylor's Case (1676):**

The foundational common law authority on blasphemy. John Taylor was convicted before Lord Chief Justice Hale for publishing blasphemous statements. Hale declared: "Such kind of wicked blasphemous words were not only an offence to God and religion, but a crime against the laws, State and Government, and therefore punishable in this Court. For to say, religion is a cheat, is to dissolve all those obligations whereby the civil societies are preserved... And Christianity is parcel of the laws of England; and therefore to reproach the Christian religion is to speak in subversion of the law."

This rationale -- that Christianity was part of English law and that blasphemy therefore threatened the civil order -- became the standard justification for common law blasphemy prosecutions.

**Subsequent Development:**

- **R v. Woolston (1729):** Thomas Woolston was convicted for publishing allegorical interpretations of Christ's miracles that the court deemed blasphemous. The sentence was one year's imprisonment and a fine of £25 per libel.
- **Bowman v. Secular Society [1917] AC 406:** The House of Lords significantly narrowed the scope of blasphemy, holding that it was not blasphemous to deny the truth of Christianity per se, but only to do so in a manner that was "contemptuous, reviling, scurrilous or ludicrous." This shifted the focus from content to manner of expression.
- **R v. Lemon [1979] AC 617 (Gay News case):** The House of Lords upheld the conviction of Gay News for publishing a poem depicting homosexual acts with Christ. The case confirmed that blasphemous libel remained a common law offense, but the narrow 3-2 majority and widespread criticism signaled the offense's obsolescence.
- **Criminal Justice and Immigration Act 2008, s. 79:** Abolished the common law offenses of blasphemy and blasphemous libel in England and Wales.

## Historical Significance and Modern Legacy

The history of blasphemy law illuminates fundamental questions about the relationship between religious authority, political power, and individual conscience:

**The Theocratic Foundation:** All pre-modern blasphemy laws rest on the premise that the political order depends on religious order -- that an offense against God or the gods is simultaneously an offense against society. Justinian's linking of blasphemy to earthquakes, Hale's assertion that Christianity is "parcel of the laws of England," and the Confucian equation of ritual propriety with good governance all express this foundational assumption. The Enlightenment separation of church and state represented a direct challenge to this premise.

**From Content to Manner:** The evolution of English blasphemy law from Taylor's Case (where the content of anti-Christian speech was itself criminal) to Bowman v. Secular Society (where only the "scurrilous" manner of expression was criminal) traces the gradual acceptance of religious pluralism and freedom of conscience within the common law tradition.

**Blasphemy and Human Rights:** The tension between blasphemy laws and freedom of expression remains unresolved in modern international law. Article 19 of the ICCPR protects freedom of expression subject to limitations, while Article 20(2) requires prohibition of "advocacy of...religious hatred that constitutes incitement." The balance between these provisions continues to generate controversy.
