## Summary

Workplace sexual harassment law -- the legal framework prohibiting unwelcome sexual conduct in employment settings and establishing employer liability, prevention obligations, and victim remedies -- has undergone transformative expansion across developed legal systems, particularly following the global #MeToo movement beginning in 2017. South Korea addresses workplace sexual harassment through the Act on Equal Employment and Support for Work-Family Reconciliation (남녀고용평등과 일·가정 양립 지원에 관한 법률, originally 1987, substantially amended in 1999, 2017, and 2021), the National Human Rights Commission Act (국가인권위원회법, 2001), and the Act on the Prevention of Sexual Harassment and Protection of Victims in Public Institutions (공공기관의 성희롱 예방 등에 관한 법률), with a landmark 2018 amendment making employer liability more robust following the An Hee-jung sexual assault case involving a provincial governor and his secretary; enforcement has been significantly strengthened since the Korean #MeToo movement, with the Supreme Court establishing in 2018 that sexual harassment claims must be assessed from the victim's perspective (피해자 관점). The United States developed the legal framework for sexual harassment as a form of sex discrimination under Title VII through the EEOC's 1980 guidelines and the Supreme Court's decisions in Meritor Savings Bank v. Vinson, 477 U.S. 57 (1986), which recognized hostile work environment harassment, and Faragher v. City of Boca Raton, 524 U.S. 775 (1998) and Burlington Industries v. Ellerth, 524 U.S. 742 (1998), which established the employer liability framework; the Ending Forced Arbitration of Sexual Assault and Sexual Harassment Act of 2021 subsequently prohibited mandatory pre-dispute arbitration agreements for sexual harassment claims. Germany addresses sexual harassment through Section 3(4) of the Allgemeines Gleichbehandlungsgesetz (AGG, 2006), which defines it as unwanted sexual conduct including comments, gestures, display of pornographic material, and physical contact creating a hostile environment, with employer obligations under Section 12 including prevention, training, and appropriate response measures. At the European level, the EU has progressively strengthened the legal framework through the recast Gender Equality Directive 2006/54/EC defining sexual harassment as sex discrimination, the Directive (EU) 2024/1385 on combating violence against women which addresses sexual harassment in the digital sphere, and the ILO Convention No. 190 on Violence and Harassment in the World of Work (2019), the first international treaty dedicated to workplace violence and harassment.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Sexual harassment in the workplace is addressed through multiple legislative instruments reflecting South Korea's fragmented anti-discrimination architecture.

### Equal Employment Act Provisions

Article 12 of the Act on Equal Employment prohibits workplace sexual harassment, defined as conduct by an employer, superior, or co-worker that causes sexual humiliation or disgust through sexual comments, demands, or other sexual conduct, using a position of power within the employment relationship. The definition encompasses:

| Category | Examples |
|----------|---------|
| Verbal harassment | Sexual jokes, comments about appearance/body, sexual rumors, unwanted romantic solicitations |
| Visual harassment | Display of sexual images, sending sexually explicit messages or images |
| Physical harassment | Unwanted touching, hugging, kissing, or physical contact of a sexual nature |
| Conditional harassment | Linking employment benefits/detriment to acceptance/rejection of sexual advances |

### Employer Obligations

**Prevention (Article 13):** All employers must conduct annual sexual harassment prevention education for all employees. The Ministry of Employment and Labor prescribes the content, duration (minimum one hour), and method of training. Failure to conduct training results in a fine of up to 5 million KRW.

**Investigation (Article 14):** Upon receiving a sexual harassment complaint, the employer must immediately investigate. During investigation, the employer may separate the victim from the alleged perpetrator. The employer may not take adverse action against the complainant (retaliation prohibition).

**Remedial Action (Article 14-2):** Following investigation, the employer must take appropriate corrective measures including disciplinary action against the perpetrator, workplace reassignment, and measures to prevent recurrence. Failure to take appropriate action results in a fine of up to 5 million KRW.

### 2018 Reforms and #MeToo

South Korea's #MeToo movement, which gained momentum in January 2018 when prosecutor Seo Ji-hyeon publicly accused a senior prosecutor of sexual assault on live television, led to significant legal reforms:

**An Hee-jung Case (2018):** The governor of South Chungcheong Province was convicted of repeatedly sexually assaulting his secretary, Kim Ji-eun. The Supreme Court affirmed his conviction in 2019, applying the principle that consent must be assessed in light of the power dynamics inherent in the employment relationship. The case catalyzed amendments strengthening protections for employees in subordinate positions.

**2018 Amendment:** Expanded the definition of employer to include independent contractors and irregular workers in certain circumstances. Enhanced penalties for employer failure to investigate and address sexual harassment complaints. Prohibited secondary victimization (2차 피해) of complainants.

**2021 Amendment:** Further strengthened employer investigation duties and extended protections to include harassment by clients, customers, or third parties encountered in the course of employment.

### Landmark Supreme Court Decision (2018)

The Supreme Court Decision 2018Da18869 established that sexual harassment claims must be assessed from the perspective of a reasonable victim in the complainant's position (피해자 관점), considering the overall context of the employment relationship, power dynamics, and workplace culture, rather than from the perspective of the alleged harasser or an abstract "reasonable person."

## United States

### Legal Framework

Sexual harassment is treated as a form of sex discrimination under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964. The legal framework was developed through EEOC guidelines and Supreme Court jurisprudence rather than explicit statutory text.

### EEOC Guidelines (1980)

The EEOC's 1980 Guidelines on Sexual Harassment (29 CFR Section 1604.11) established two forms of actionable sexual harassment:

**Quid Pro Quo:** Submission to or rejection of unwelcome sexual conduct is made a term or condition of employment, or is used as the basis for employment decisions.

**Hostile Work Environment:** Unwelcome sexual conduct has the purpose or effect of unreasonably interfering with work performance or creating an intimidating, hostile, or offensive working environment.

### Supreme Court Framework

| Case | Year | Holding |
|------|------|---------|
| Meritor Savings Bank v. Vinson, 477 U.S. 57 | 1986 | Hostile work environment sexual harassment is actionable under Title VII |
| Harris v. Forklift Systems, 510 U.S. 17 | 1993 | No requirement to prove severe psychological injury; totality of circumstances test |
| Oncale v. Sundowner Offshore, 523 U.S. 75 | 1998 | Same-sex sexual harassment is actionable |
| Faragher v. City of Boca Raton, 524 U.S. 775 | 1998 | Employer vicariously liable for supervisor harassment; affirmative defense available |
| Burlington Industries v. Ellerth, 524 U.S. 742 | 1998 | Same as Faragher; defense requires employer had anti-harassment policy and victim unreasonably failed to use it |

### Faragher/Ellerth Affirmative Defense

When a supervisory employee's harassment does not result in a tangible employment action (hiring, firing, demotion, reassignment), the employer may avoid liability by proving two elements: (1) the employer exercised reasonable care to prevent and correct promptly any harassing behavior, and (2) the employee unreasonably failed to take advantage of any preventive or corrective opportunities provided by the employer. This defense is not available when the harassment culminates in a tangible employment action.

### Ending Forced Arbitration Act (2021)

The Ending Forced Arbitration of Sexual Assault and Sexual Harassment Act (9 U.S.C. Section 402), signed on March 3, 2022, invalidates pre-dispute arbitration agreements and class/collective action waivers for claims of sexual assault or sexual harassment. This legislation responded to widespread criticism that mandatory arbitration concealed serial harassers by preventing public adjudication.

### State Variations

Several states have enacted stronger protections than federal law, particularly following #MeToo:

- **New York (2019):** Eliminated the "severe or pervasive" standard; lowered threshold to conduct that subjects an individual to "inferior terms, conditions, or privileges of employment"
- **California (2018):** SB 1300 lowered harassment standard; AB 3080 prohibited mandatory arbitration for harassment claims (partially preempted)
- **Illinois (2019):** Workplace Transparency Act: anti-harassment training required; restrictions on NDAs

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Workplace sexual harassment is addressed through the Allgemeines Gleichbehandlungsgesetz (AGG) Section 3(4), which defines sexual harassment as unwanted sexual conduct including unwanted sexual acts and requests for such acts, sexually determined physical contact, remarks of a sexual nature, and the unwanted display or distribution of pornographic material. The conduct must be intended to or does violate the dignity of the person affected, particularly when it creates an intimidating, hostile, degrading, humiliating, or offensive environment.

### Employer Obligations (Section 12 AGG)

**Prevention (§ 12(1)):** Employers must take necessary measures to protect employees from discrimination, including sexual harassment. The statutory text does not specify particular prevention measures, but case law and administrative guidance require anti-harassment policies and complaint mechanisms.

**Training (§ 12(2)):** Employers must provide appropriate training on anti-discrimination obligations, including prevention of sexual harassment. While the statute does not specify frequency, the Antidiskriminierungsstelle recommends regular training for all employees.

**Response (§ 12(3)):** When an employee is harassed by another employee, the employer must take appropriate measures including warnings (Abmahnung), reassignment, or dismissal of the harasser. Failure to act may result in employer liability under Section 15 AGG.

**Third-Party Harassment (§ 12(4)):** If an employee is harassed by a third party (client, customer), the employer must take appropriate protective measures.

### Criminal Law

German criminal law addresses sexual harassment through Section 184i StGB (Sexuelle Belästigung), introduced by the Gesetz zur Verbesserung des Schutzes der sexuellen Selbstbestimmung (Act to Improve Protection of Sexual Self-Determination) of November 10, 2016, enacted following the 2015/2016 Cologne New Year's Eve sexual assaults. The provision criminalizes physical touching of another person in a sexually determined manner against the person's recognizable will, carrying a penalty of up to two years' imprisonment or a fine.

### Cologne New Year's Eve (2015/2016)

The mass sexual assaults and robberies in Cologne on New Year's Eve 2015/2016, involving over 1,200 reported victims, exposed significant gaps in Germany's criminal law regarding sexual harassment and sexual assault. The subsequent reform of sexual criminal law in 2016 (the "Nein heißt Nein" or "No means No" reform) amended Section 177 StGB to adopt a consent-based model and introduced the new Section 184i StGB specifically targeting sexual harassment.

### Remedies

Section 15(1) AGG provides for material damages (with fault requirement), and Section 15(2) provides for immaterial damages not exceeding three months' salary in hiring discrimination cases. The damages available for sexual harassment are not subject to the three-month cap applicable to hiring discrimination, but German courts have traditionally awarded relatively modest amounts by U.S. standards.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Directive Framework

**Recast Gender Equality Directive 2006/54/EC:** Article 2(1)(d) defines sexual harassment as "any form of unwanted verbal, non-verbal or physical conduct of a sexual nature" with the purpose or effect of violating the dignity of a person or creating an intimidating, hostile, degrading, humiliating, or offensive environment. Article 2(2) establishes that sexual harassment constitutes discrimination on grounds of sex.

**Directive (EU) 2024/1385 on Combating Violence against Women:** Adopted May 14, 2024, this Directive addresses sexual harassment in the digital sphere, criminalizing the non-consensual sharing of intimate images and cyber harassment with sexual content. Member states must transpose by June 14, 2027.

### ILO Convention No. 190

The ILO Violence and Harassment Convention, adopted on June 21, 2019, is the first international treaty dedicated to addressing violence and harassment in the world of work. Key provisions include:

- A broad definition of violence and harassment covering physical, psychological, sexual, and economic harm
- Protection covering all workers regardless of contractual status, including interns, volunteers, and job seekers
- Coverage of violence and harassment occurring in the workplace, during work-related travel, in work-related communications, in employer-provided accommodation, and during commuting
- Requirement for a comprehensive approach including prevention, protection, enforcement, and remedies

As of 2025, 46 states have ratified Convention 190. Germany ratified in June 2023. South Korea and the United States have not ratified.

### ECHR

The European Court of Human Rights has addressed workplace sexual harassment primarily through Article 8 (right to respect for private life) and Article 3 (prohibition of inhuman or degrading treatment).

**C. v. Romania (Application No. 47358/20, Judgment of 2023):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where Romanian authorities failed to effectively investigate a workplace sexual harassment complaint, establishing that states have positive obligations to provide adequate legal protection against workplace sexual harassment.

**M.C. v. Bulgaria (Application No. 39272/98, Judgment of December 4, 2003):** While concerning sexual assault rather than workplace harassment specifically, this case established that states have positive obligations under Articles 3 and 8 to enact and apply criminal law provisions effectively against sexual violence, including adopting a consent-based rather than resistance-based approach.

### European Social Charter

The European Committee of Social Rights has interpreted Article 26 of the Revised European Social Charter (the right to dignity at work) to require states to promote awareness and prevent sexual harassment, provide legal protection for employees, and ensure effective remedies. In its 2020 conclusions, the Committee found several states in non-conformity for inadequate sexual harassment legislation or enforcement.

## Comparative Analysis

### Definition and Scope

All jurisdictions define sexual harassment broadly to include verbal, physical, and visual conduct. The United States uniquely distinguishes between quid pro quo and hostile work environment harassment, a framework that influences the employer liability analysis. South Korea and Germany define harassment by its effect on the victim's dignity and working environment without this formal distinction. The ILO Convention 190 provides the broadest definition, encompassing economic harm and extending beyond the workplace to work-related contexts.

### Employer Liability

The United States' Faragher/Ellerth framework provides a structured analysis of employer vicarious liability with an affirmative defense. South Korea imposes more direct employer liability, particularly following the 2018 amendments. Germany requires affirmative employer prevention and response but limits damages. The EU Directives require effective employer liability without prescribing a specific framework.

### Mandatory Prevention

South Korea stands out for requiring annual mandatory sexual harassment prevention training for all workplaces. German law requires training but does not specify frequency. The United States has no federal training mandate, though several states require it. This divergence reflects different approaches to the balance between prevention and response.

### Criminal Dimension

Germany's introduction of Section 184i StGB following the Cologne events represents the most explicit criminal response to sexual harassment as distinct from sexual assault. South Korea applies general criminal provisions for sexual assault. The United States treats sexual harassment primarily as a civil employment matter under Title VII, with criminal law applying separately for conduct that rises to the level of sexual assault.
