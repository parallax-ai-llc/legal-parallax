## Summary

The law of the sea -- the body of international law governing the rights and responsibilities of states with respect to the world's oceans, including maritime zones, navigation, resource exploitation, environmental protection, and dispute settlement -- is anchored in the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS, 1982), which entered into force on November 16, 1994, and has been ratified by 169 states, establishing what is often described as the "constitution for the oceans." South Korea ratified UNCLOS on January 29, 1996, and implements maritime law through the Maritime Safety Act (해사안전법), the Marine Environment Management Act (해양환경관리법), and the Exclusive Economic Zone Act (배타적 경제수역법), with overlapping maritime boundary claims with Japan and China in the East China Sea and the Sea of Japan/East Sea, and the strategically significant Northern Limit Line (NLL, 북방한계선) in the West Sea/Yellow Sea creating ongoing legal tensions. The United States, despite being the world's dominant naval power, has not ratified UNCLOS, instead recognizing most of its provisions as reflective of customary international law; the US implements the law of the sea through domestic statutes including the Outer Continental Shelf Lands Act (43 U.S.C. Section 1331 et seq.), the Magnuson-Stevens Fishery Conservation and Management Act (16 U.S.C. Section 1801 et seq.), and Presidential Proclamations establishing maritime zones, while the US Navy conducts Freedom of Navigation (FON) operations to challenge excessive maritime claims. Germany ratified UNCLOS on October 14, 1994, and implements it through the Seeaufgabengesetz (Maritime Tasks Act), the Seerechtsübereinkommen-Ausführungsgesetz, and regulations governing the North Sea and Baltic Sea maritime zones, with particular focus on offshore renewable energy regulation and environmental protection. At the European level, the EU is itself a party to UNCLOS as an international organization, CJEU jurisprudence has addressed the intersection of EU law and the law of the sea, and the International Tribunal for the Law of the Sea (ITLOS) in Hamburg provides the primary dispute resolution mechanism alongside ICJ and Annex VII arbitration.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea ratified UNCLOS on January 29, 1996, and declared its EEZ through the Exclusive Economic Zone Act (배타적 경제수역법), effective August 1996. The territorial sea is fixed at 12 nautical miles under the Territorial Sea and Contiguous Zone Act (영해 및 접속수역법). South Korea's maritime legal framework also includes the Maritime Safety Act (해사안전법), the Marine Environment Management Act (해양환경관리법), and the Fisheries Act (수산업법).

### Maritime Boundary Disputes

South Korea faces overlapping maritime claims with three neighbors: Japan, China, and North Korea.

**Korea-Japan:** The EEZ overlap in the East Sea/Sea of Japan and the East China Sea has not been delimited by agreement. The 1974 Korea-Japan Joint Development Zone Agreement established a joint development area for continental shelf hydrocarbon resources in the southern part of the overlap zone, but the agreement expires in 2028 and its renewal is uncertain. The Dokdo/Takeshima dispute complicates maritime delimitation because the sovereignty of the islands affects basepoint calculations for EEZ and continental shelf claims.

**Korea-China:** The EEZ overlap in the Yellow Sea and East China Sea involves disputes over the equidistance/relevant circumstances method of delimitation. The Ieodo/Suyan Rock (이어도/蘇岩礁) -- a submerged rock 4.6 meters below sea level -- lies within the overlap zone. South Korea built the Ieodo Ocean Research Station on the rock in 2003. Under UNCLOS Article 121(3), rocks that cannot sustain human habitation or economic life do not generate EEZ or continental shelf entitlements; as a submerged feature, Ieodo cannot serve as a territorial claim basis, but the research station serves as an assertion of jurisdiction.

**Northern Limit Line (NLL):** The NLL, a maritime boundary in the West Sea/Yellow Sea established unilaterally by the UN Command on August 30, 1953, following the armistice, has been a persistent source of tension. North Korea rejects the NLL, claiming a more southerly maritime boundary. Naval clashes along the NLL occurred in 1999 (First Battle of Yeonpyeong), 2002 (Second Battle of Yeonpyeong), 2009 (Battle of Daecheong), and 2010 (sinking of ROKS Cheonan and Yeonpyeong Island bombardment).

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has not ratified UNCLOS, making it the most significant non-party. The Senate Foreign Relations Committee reported the treaty favorably in 2004 and 2007, but the full Senate has not consented to ratification, primarily due to opposition from senators concerned about sovereignty, the International Seabed Authority, and mandatory dispute settlement provisions.

Despite non-ratification, the US recognizes most UNCLOS provisions as reflective of customary international law. Maritime zones are established through Presidential Proclamations: Proclamation 5928 (December 27, 1988) extended the territorial sea to 12 nautical miles; Proclamation 5030 (March 10, 1983) declared the EEZ of 200 nautical miles; and Proclamation 7219 (September 2, 1999) established the contiguous zone.

**Freedom of Navigation Program:** The US Navy conducts Freedom of Navigation (FON) operations worldwide to challenge what the US considers excessive maritime claims inconsistent with international law. These operations are particularly prominent in the South China Sea, where the US contests China's extensive claims based on the "nine-dash line." In 2016, the Arbitral Tribunal in Philippines v. China (PCA Case No. 2013-19) found that China's nine-dash line claims had no legal basis under UNCLOS.

### Key Domestic Statutes

**Outer Continental Shelf Lands Act (OCSLA, 43 U.S.C. Section 1331 et seq.):** Establishes federal jurisdiction over submerged lands beyond state waters and governs mineral resource development on the outer continental shelf.

**Magnuson-Stevens Fishery Conservation and Management Act (16 U.S.C. Section 1801 et seq.):** Establishes the framework for fisheries management within the US EEZ, administered by NOAA through regional fishery management councils.

**Oil Pollution Act of 1990 (OPA, 33 U.S.C. Section 2701 et seq.):** Enacted following the Exxon Valdez spill, establishes liability, compensation, and prevention requirements for oil pollution.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany ratified UNCLOS on October 14, 1994, just days before the Convention's entry into force on November 16, 1994. Implementation is accomplished through the Seeaufgabengesetz (Maritime Tasks Act) and the Seerechtsübereinkommen-Ausführungsgesetz (UNCLOS Implementation Act). Germany's maritime zones in the North Sea and Baltic Sea have been delimited through bilateral agreements with neighboring states (Denmark, Netherlands, Poland, Sweden).

### North Sea Continental Shelf Cases (1969)

The ICJ's judgment in the North Sea Continental Shelf Cases (Federal Republic of Germany v. Denmark; Federal Republic of Germany v. Netherlands, February 20, 1969) remains a foundational precedent in the law of the sea. The Court rejected the application of the equidistance principle as a mandatory rule of customary international law for continental shelf delimitation, holding instead that delimitation should achieve an equitable result. The judgment established the principle that continental shelf delimitation is governed by equitable principles rather than rigid geometrical methods.

### Offshore Renewable Energy

Germany has become a world leader in offshore wind energy regulation, with significant implications for the law of the sea. The Seeanlagengesetz (Marine Facilities Act) and the Windenergie-auf-See-Gesetz (Offshore Wind Energy Act, WindSeeG, 2017) govern the construction and operation of offshore wind farms in the German EEZ. Germany's 2023 offshore wind energy strategy targets 30 GW of installed capacity by 2030 and 70 GW by 2045, requiring extensive spatial planning of maritime zones.

### Environmental Protection

Germany has been a strong advocate for marine environmental protection within the UNCLOS framework. The implementation of EU Marine Strategy Framework Directive 2008/56/EC through the Meeresstrategirahmenrichtlinie national provisions establishes environmental targets for the North Sea and Baltic Sea marine environments.

## ECHR & EU

### EU as UNCLOS Party

The European Union is a party to UNCLOS in its own right, having formally confirmed its participation on April 1, 1998, pursuant to Annex IX of the Convention, which allows international organizations to become parties. The EU's competence under UNCLOS extends to areas falling within EU exclusive or shared competence, including fisheries management, environmental protection, and maritime transport. Upon confirmation, the EU made a declaration specifying its areas of competence.

### Common Fisheries Policy (CFP)

The EU's Common Fisheries Policy, established by Regulation (EU) No 1380/2013, governs fisheries management within the EEZ of EU member states. The CJEU has adjudicated numerous cases on the intersection of the CFP and the law of the sea, including questions of member state competence within their EEZ.

### ITLOS

The International Tribunal for the Law of the Sea, established under UNCLOS Part XV and headquartered in Hamburg, Germany, provides the primary judicial mechanism for law of the sea disputes. Notable cases include: the M/V "Saiga" (No. 2) Case (Saint Vincent and the Grenadines v. Guinea, 1999), establishing principles on prompt release of vessels; the Southern Bluefin Tuna Cases (New Zealand v. Japan; Australia v. Japan, 1999), addressing fisheries conservation obligations; and the Advisory Opinion on Responsibilities and Obligations of States Sponsoring Persons and Entities with Respect to Activities in the Area (2011).

**ITLOS Advisory Opinion on Climate Change (2024):** In a landmark advisory opinion requested by the Commission of Small Island States on Climate Change and International Law, ITLOS held on May 21, 2024, that greenhouse gas emissions absorbed by the marine environment constitute "pollution of the marine environment" under UNCLOS Article 1(1)(4), and that states parties have specific obligations under Part XII of UNCLOS to take all necessary measures to prevent, reduce, and control marine pollution from greenhouse gas emissions.

### Philippines v. China (2016)

The Annex VII Arbitral Tribunal's award in the South China Sea Arbitration (Philippines v. China, PCA Case No. 2013-19, Award of July 12, 2016) was one of the most significant law of the sea decisions. The Tribunal found that China's claim to historic rights within the nine-dash line was without legal foundation under UNCLOS, that certain features in the Spratly Islands were not entitled to EEZ or continental shelf under Article 121(3), and that China had violated Philippines' sovereign rights within its EEZ.

### EU Marine Strategy Framework Directive

Directive 2008/56/EC establishes a framework for EU member states to achieve "good environmental status" in the marine environment by 2020 (subsequently extended). It covers all European marine waters and addresses aspects including biodiversity, marine litter, underwater noise, and eutrophication.

## Comparative Analysis

### UNCLOS Participation

The most fundamental divergence is the US non-ratification of UNCLOS, contrasting with universal participation by the other jurisdictions and the EU. While the US recognizes most UNCLOS provisions as customary law, non-ratification excludes the US from the Convention's dispute settlement mechanisms, the International Seabed Authority, and the Commission on the Limits of the Continental Shelf.

### Maritime Dispute Resolution

South Korea and Germany participate in UNCLOS dispute settlement mechanisms (ITLOS, ICJ, Annex VII arbitration). The United States relies on diplomatic negotiation, bilateral agreements, and, controversially, Freedom of Navigation operations to assert its maritime interests. South Korea has not formally submitted its maritime boundary disputes to international adjudication, preferring bilateral negotiation.

### Environmental Protection

All jurisdictions implement marine environmental protection obligations, but through different frameworks. The EU's Marine Strategy Framework Directive provides the most comprehensive regional approach. South Korea's Marine Environment Management Act addresses domestic maritime environmental concerns. The US relies on a patchwork of domestic statutes (Clean Water Act, OPA). Germany combines EU directives with national offshore energy regulation.

### Emerging Issues

Climate change, deep seabed mining, and autonomous vessel regulation represent emerging law of the sea challenges. ITLOS's 2024 advisory opinion on climate change and the marine environment represents a significant development. The ISA's ongoing negotiations for a deep seabed mining exploitation code raise questions about the US's absence from the UNCLOS framework.
