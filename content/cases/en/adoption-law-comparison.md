## Summary

Adoption -- the legal process by which a person assumes the parenting rights and responsibilities for a child who is not their biological offspring, creating a permanent parent-child relationship recognized by law -- operates through diverse legal frameworks reflecting distinct cultural attitudes toward kinship, blood ties, and the state's role in child welfare. South Korea has undergone a dramatic transformation in its adoption regime, from being one of the world's largest sources of intercountry adoption during the post-Korean War era, with over 200,000 children adopted internationally between 1953 and 2023, to enacting the Special Adoption Act (입양특례법) in 2012 requiring all adoptions to be processed through the family court system rather than private agencies, mandating birth parent counseling and a seven-day post-birth consent period, and imposing a principle of domestic adoption priority; the 2012 Act significantly reduced intercountry adoptions but also led to a rise in unreported births and child abandonment, prompting ongoing legislative debate. The United States is both the world's largest receiving country for intercountry adoption and has a complex domestic adoption system governed by state law for domestic adoptions, the Intercountry Adoption Act of 2000 (IAA, 42 U.S.C. Section 14901 et seq.) implementing the Hague Adoption Convention for international adoptions, the Indian Child Welfare Act (ICWA, 25 U.S.C. Section 1901 et seq.) providing specific protections for Native American children, and the Multiethnic Placement Act (42 U.S.C. Section 1996b) prohibiting racial discrimination in adoption placement; the Supreme Court's 2023 decision in Haaland v. Brackeen, 599 U.S. 255, upheld the constitutionality of ICWA. Germany regulates adoption through BGB Sections 1741-1772, requiring family court approval (Adoptionsbeschluss) and distinguishing between full adoption (Volladoption) -- which severs all legal ties to the biological family -- and adult adoption (Erwachsenenadoption), which maintains existing family relationships; the 2021 Adoptionshilfegesetz (Adoption Assistance Act) strengthened counseling requirements and established a right to information about biological origins. At the European level, the Hague Convention on Intercountry Adoption (1993) establishes international standards, the European Convention on the Adoption of Children (revised 2008) sets regional standards including opening adoption to unmarried couples, and the ECHR has developed extensive Article 8 jurisprudence on adoption, including the landmark Strand Lobben v. Norway (2019) on the preservation of biological family ties and the requirement of particularly compelling reasons for dispensing with parental consent.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korean adoption law operates under two regimes: general adoption (일반입양) under Articles 878-908 of the Civil Act (민법), which functions more like a guardianship transfer and does not fully sever ties with the biological family, and special adoption (입양특례법에 의한 입양) under the Special Adoption Act, which creates a full parent-child relationship equivalent to biological parentage.

### Historical Context: Intercountry Adoption

South Korea's adoption history is inseparable from the Korean War (1950-1953). Following the war, thousands of orphaned and mixed-race children were adopted internationally, initially through the efforts of Harry Holt and his wife Bertha, who founded Holt International Children's Services. Key statistics:

| Period | Approximate Intercountry Adoptions |
|--------|-----------------------------------|
| 1953-1970 | ~50,000 |
| 1971-1990 | ~100,000 |
| 1991-2010 | ~45,000 |
| 2011-2023 | ~10,000 |
| **Total** | **~205,000** |

South Korea has been the fourth-largest source country for intercountry adoption globally, behind China, Russia, and Guatemala.

### Special Adoption Act (2012)

The Special Adoption Act (입양특례법), comprehensively revised in August 2012 (effective August 5, 2012), introduced sweeping reforms:

**Court-Centered Process:** All adoptions must be approved by the family court, replacing the previous system in which private adoption agencies processed adoptions with minimal judicial oversight.

**Birth Parent Protections:**
- Mandatory counseling for birth parents before consent
- Seven-day post-birth waiting period before consent can be given
- Written informed consent required
- Birth parents may revoke consent until the court adoption decree

**Domestic Adoption Priority:** The Act establishes a principle that domestic adoption efforts must be exhausted before intercountry adoption is pursued.

**Birth Registration Requirement:** The Act requires that children be registered in the national family register (가족관계등록부) before they can be adopted, ensuring official documentation of the birth.

### Impact and Controversies

The 2012 Act's requirements, particularly the birth registration mandate, have had unintended consequences. Reports indicate that some birth mothers, unwilling to register births in their name due to social stigma, have abandoned children or chosen illegal abortions. A 2019 study by the Korean Institute of Criminology found a correlation between the Act's implementation and increased baby box (베이비박스) usage. Legislative proposals to address these issues, including anonymous birth provisions (익명출산제), have been debated but not enacted.

### Adoptee Rights Movement

Korean adoptees have been increasingly vocal in advocating for access to adoption records and biological family information. The Truth and Reconciliation Commission on Overseas Korean Adoptions was proposed in 2022 to investigate historical adoption practices, including allegations of falsified records and coerced relinquishments during the 1970s-1980s peak adoption period.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Adoption in the United States is primarily governed by state law, with federal law addressing intercountry adoption, Native American child welfare, and anti-discrimination in foster care placement.

### State Law Variation

| Element | Typical Provisions | Variation |
|---------|-------------------|-----------|
| Consent waiting period | 48-72 hours post-birth | Alabama: immediately; Utah: 24 hours |
| Revocation period | Varies: irrevocable to 30 days | Some states: no revocation after consent |
| Home study | Required in all states | Standards vary |
| Post-placement supervision | 3-12 months | Varies by state and type |
| Open adoption agreements | Enforceable in ~28 states | Not enforceable in remaining states |

### Indian Child Welfare Act (ICWA, 1978)

ICWA, 25 U.S.C. Section 1901 et seq., was enacted to address the disproportionate removal of Native American children from their families and tribes. Key provisions:

- Exclusive tribal jurisdiction for children domiciled on reservations
- Transfer jurisdiction to tribal courts for off-reservation children (upon petition)
- Placement preferences: extended family, tribal members, other Indian families
- Higher evidentiary standard (beyond a reasonable doubt) for termination of parental rights
- Right of tribe to intervene in state adoption proceedings

**Haaland v. Brackeen, 599 U.S. 255 (2023):** The Supreme Court upheld ICWA's constitutionality, rejecting challenges under the equal protection, anti-commandeering, and nondelegation doctrines. Justice Barrett's majority opinion held that the challengers lacked standing for some claims and that ICWA's provisions were a valid exercise of Congress's power over Indian affairs.

### Intercountry Adoption Act (2000)

The IAA implements the Hague Convention on Intercountry Adoption in the United States. The State Department serves as the Central Authority, and all intercountry adoption service providers must be accredited. The Act establishes safeguards against trafficking and ensures that intercountry adoption is conducted in the best interests of the child.

### Foster Care Adoption

The Adoption and Safe Families Act of 1997 (ASFA, P.L. 105-89) reformed the federal foster care system to prioritize timely permanency. States must file a petition to terminate parental rights for children who have been in foster care for 15 of the most recent 22 months, with exceptions. In fiscal year 2022, approximately 54,000 children were adopted from foster care in the United States.

### Same-Sex Couple Adoption

Following Obergefell v. Hodges, 576 U.S. 644 (2015), same-sex married couples have equal adoption rights in all states. The Supreme Court in Fulton v. City of Philadelphia, 593 U.S. 522 (2021), addressed whether religious foster care agencies receiving government funding could refuse to certify same-sex couples, holding on narrow grounds that Philadelphia's refusal to contract with Catholic Social Services violated the Free Exercise Clause.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German adoption law is governed by BGB Sections 1741-1772, the Adoption Placement Act (Adoptionsvermittlungsgesetz, AdVermiG), and the Adoptionshilfegesetz of 2021.

### Types of Adoption

**Full Adoption of Minors (Minderjährigenadoption, Volladoption):** Under BGB Section 1755, full adoption severs all legal relationships between the child and the biological family. The child becomes legally the child of the adoptive parents with all attendant rights (inheritance, maintenance, name). This is the standard form for minor adoption.

**Adult Adoption (Erwachsenenadoption):** Under BGB Section 1770, adoption of adults does not sever ties to the biological family. The adopted adult acquires additional family relationships rather than replacement ones. Adult adoption requires that a parent-child relationship has already developed between the parties.

### Requirements for Adoption

| Requirement | Provision |
|-------------|-----------|
| Adopter age | Minimum 25 years (one spouse) and 21 years (other spouse) (Section 1743) |
| Consent | Both biological parents; child if 14+ (Sections 1746-1748) |
| Trial period | Appropriate trial period of foster care (Adoptionspflegezeit) (Section 1744) |
| Home study | Mandatory assessment by Jugendamt (Youth Office) |
| Court approval | Family court decree (Adoptionsbeschluss) (Section 1752) |
| Best interests | Adoption must serve the child's best interests (Section 1741) |

### Dispensing with Parental Consent

BGB Section 1748 allows the family court to substitute consent where:
- The parent's whereabouts are unknown for a prolonged period
- The parent is permanently unable to consent
- The parent has persistently and gravely violated parental duties or demonstrated indifference to the child
- Failure to adopt would cause disproportionate disadvantage to the child

### Adoptionshilfegesetz (2021)

The Adoption Assistance Act (Adoptionshilfegesetz), effective April 1, 2021, introduced several reforms:

- **Right to Information:** Adoptees have a right to access their adoption files and learn about their biological origins. The Jugendamt must counsel adoptees seeking information.
- **Mandatory Counseling:** All parties to an adoption (birth parents, adoptive parents, and the child if age-appropriate) must receive professional counseling before and after the adoption.
- **Open Adoption Promotion:** The Act promotes agreements on contact and information exchange between birth and adoptive families, though these agreements are not legally enforceable.
- **Domestic Intercountry Coordination:** Enhanced reporting requirements for intercountry adoption agencies.

### Same-Sex Couple Adoption

Following the enactment of the Gesetz zur Einführung des Rechts auf Eheschließung für Personen gleichen Geschlechts (Marriage for All Act) on October 1, 2017, same-sex married couples may jointly adopt. Previously, only stepchild adoption by a same-sex partner in a registered civil partnership was permitted, following the BVerfG's 2013 decision (1 BvL 1/11) finding the exclusion of civil partners from stepchild adoption unconstitutional.

## ECHR & EU

### Hague Convention on Intercountry Adoption (1993)

The Convention on Protection of Children and Co-operation in Respect of Intercountry Adoption, concluded on May 29, 1993, establishes:

- **Subsidiarity Principle:** Intercountry adoption should be considered only after domestic options have been adequately explored (Article 4(b)).
- **Central Authority System:** Each contracting state must designate a Central Authority to coordinate intercountry adoptions (Article 6).
- **Safeguards:** The state of origin must establish that the child is adoptable, that intercountry adoption is in the child's best interests, that consents have been freely given, and that no improper financial gain has been involved (Article 4).
- **Automatic Recognition:** Adoptions certified by the competent authority of the state where the adoption was made are recognized by operation of law in all contracting states (Article 23).

As of 2026, 104 states are parties to the Convention.

### European Convention on the Adoption of Children (Revised 2008)

The revised European Convention (CETS No. 202), opened for signature on November 27, 2008, updates the original 1967 Convention. Key provisions:

- Article 7: Adoption open to married couples, registered partners where national law permits, and single persons.
- Article 9: Minimum age for adopters of 18, with a minimum age difference of 16 years between adopter and child.
- Article 5: Consent of the child must be obtained if the child has sufficient understanding.
- Article 22: Right to know identity of biological parents, subject to restrictions in the child's best interests.

### ECHR Article 8 Jurisprudence

**Strand Lobben v. Norway (Application No. 37283/13, Grand Chamber, September 10, 2019):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where Norway authorized the adoption of a child against the biological mother's wishes without sufficiently considering the possibility of reunification. The Grand Chamber emphasized that national authorities must demonstrate that they have considered all viable alternatives to adoption and that adoption is supported by "particularly weighty reasons."

**Johansen v. Norway (Application No. 17383/90, August 7, 1996):** The Court established that measures depriving a parent of custody and authorizing adoption must be supported by "sufficiently sound and weighty" reasons and must be regarded as a "last resort."

**X and Others v. Austria (Application No. 19010/07, Grand Chamber, February 19, 2013):** The Court found a violation of Article 14 read with Article 8 where Austrian law permitted unmarried different-sex couples but not same-sex couples to apply for second-parent adoption.

**Harroudj v. France (Application No. 43631/09, October 4, 2012):** The Court found no violation of Article 8 where France refused to convert an Algerian kafala (guardianship) arrangement into a full adoption, respecting the Algerian prohibition on adoption.

### EU Framework

The EU has no harmonized adoption law but addresses adoption indirectly through:
- Brussels II ter Regulation (EU 2019/1111): jurisdiction and recognition of parental responsibility decisions, which may include adoption-related orders.
- EU Charter of Fundamental Rights, Article 24: best interests of the child as primary consideration.
- EU Anti-Trafficking Directive 2011/36: addresses adoption-related trafficking.

## Comparative Analysis

### Intercountry Adoption Trajectories

South Korea's evolution from one of the world's largest sending countries to a state actively restricting intercountry adoption reflects broader global trends. The Hague Convention's subsidiarity principle has contributed to a worldwide decline in intercountry adoption, from a peak of approximately 45,000 annually in 2004 to fewer than 10,000 in 2023. The United States, as the largest receiving country, has experienced the sharpest decline, from approximately 23,000 in 2004 to fewer than 2,000 in 2023.

### Consent and Revocation

The jurisdictions differ significantly in consent timing and revocability. South Korea's seven-day post-birth waiting period is more protective of birth parents than most U.S. states (typically 48-72 hours). Germany requires consent but does not specify a minimum waiting period. The ECHR's emphasis on "particularly weighty reasons" for dispensing with consent (Strand Lobben) has pushed European jurisdictions toward stronger protections for biological parents.

### Right to Know Origins

All jurisdictions increasingly recognize adoptees' right to information about their biological origins, but implementation varies. Germany's 2021 Adoptionshilfegesetz establishes a clear right. U.S. states are split between sealed and open record systems, with a trend toward greater access. South Korea is grappling with historical record integrity issues. The ECHR has recognized the right to identity under Article 8 but balances it against biological parents' privacy interests.

### Same-Sex Adoption

Germany and the United States both permit joint adoption by same-sex couples (since 2017 and 2015 respectively). South Korea does not recognize same-sex marriage and does not permit joint adoption by same-sex couples. The ECHR has found discrimination where same-sex couples are excluded from second-parent adoption (X v. Austria) but has not required joint adoption access.
