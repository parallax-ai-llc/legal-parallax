## Summary

Climate-induced displacement represents one of the most pressing legal and humanitarian challenges of the twenty-first century, yet the international legal framework remains fundamentally inadequate to address it. The 1951 Convention Relating to the Status of Refugees (CSR) and its 1967 Protocol define refugees narrowly as persons fleeing persecution on grounds of race, religion, nationality, membership in a particular social group, or political opinion -- a definition that excludes persons displaced by the effects of climate change such as rising sea levels, desertification, extreme weather events, and environmental degradation. The UN Human Rights Committee's landmark decision in Teitiota v. New Zealand (CCPR/C/127/D/2728/2016, 2020) established that climate change may engage non-refoulement obligations under Article 6 (right to life) of the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights, though it found no violation on the specific facts. South Korea has no specific legal provisions for climate-displaced persons, though its Refugee Act (난민법, Act No. 11298) incorporates international standards. The United States has used Temporary Protected Status (TPS) under INA § 244 (8 U.S.C. § 1254a) for nationals of countries affected by environmental disasters but lacks any permanent framework for climate displacement. Germany and the EU have established the Temporary Protection Directive (2001/55/EC, activated for the first time in 2022 for Ukraine) and subsidiary protection under the Qualification Directive (2011/95/EU), but neither explicitly covers climate displacement.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's Refugee Act (난민법, Act No. 11298, enacted 2012, effective 2013) was the first standalone refugee law in Asia. However, it does not explicitly address climate displacement:

- **Article 2 -- Definition of Refugee:** Adopts the 1951 Convention definition verbatim, requiring persecution on the five enumerated grounds. Climate-induced displacement does not fit within this framework unless connected to persecution -- for example, where a government deliberately withholds disaster relief from a particular ethnic group.
- **Article 3 -- Non-Refoulement:** Prohibits the return of refugees to territories where their life or freedom would be threatened. The scope is limited to Convention refugees and does not expressly extend to persons facing climate-related threats to life.
- **Article 2(3) -- Humanitarian Status Holder (인도적 체류허가):** Provides a complementary protection status for persons who do not qualify as Convention refugees but whom the Minister of Justice determines should not be returned for humanitarian reasons. This is the most plausible legal basis for protecting climate-displaced persons, though it has not been applied in this context. Humanitarian status holders receive a G-1 visa permitting residence and employment but lacking the full rights of recognized refugees (no family reunification, no travel document, no path to permanent residence).
- **Immigration Control Act (출입국관리법):** Article 46 governs deportation proceedings. Article 61 allows the Minister of Justice to grant special permission to stay for humanitarian reasons, providing an additional discretionary mechanism.

### Institutional Context

South Korea's geographic position makes it relatively insulated from direct climate displacement flows compared to Pacific Island nations or South Asian countries. However, the issue has gained policy attention through:

- **Korean National Human Rights Commission (국가인권위원회) Recommendation (2023):** The NHRC issued a recommendation urging the government to develop a policy framework for climate displacement, citing South Korea's obligations under the Paris Agreement and the Global Compact on Refugees.
- **National Assembly Research Service Report (2024):** A legislative research report examined the legal adequacy of existing frameworks for addressing climate displacement, concluding that amendments to the Refugee Act or new legislation would be necessary to provide systematic protection.
- **Climate Change Adaptation Framework Act (기후변화대응 기본법):** While primarily focused on domestic climate adaptation, Article 38 addresses climate vulnerability and disaster response, potentially providing a policy basis for engaging with international climate displacement issues.

### Notable Cases

- **Bangladesh Flood Displacement Cases (2022-2023):** Several Bangladeshi nationals in South Korea whose homes were destroyed in the 2022 monsoon floods sought extensions of their humanitarian stay permits, arguing they could not safely return. The Immigration Office granted extensions on a case-by-case basis under discretionary humanitarian provisions, without establishing a precedent for climate-based protection.
- **Myanmar Climate-Conflict Nexus Cases (2024):** Rohingya asylum seekers argued that climate-driven resource scarcity in Cox's Bazar (Bangladesh, their country of first asylum) compounded their persecution claim, representing an emerging "climate-plus" argument in Korean refugee adjudication.

## United States

### Federal Framework

The United States has no permanent legal framework for climate displacement but possesses several mechanisms that have been applied to environmental disaster situations:

- **INA § 101(a)(42) (8 U.S.C. § 1101(a)(42)) -- Refugee Definition:** Incorporates the 1951 Convention definition, requiring persecution on enumerated grounds. The Board of Immigration Appeals (BIA) and federal courts have consistently held that generalized harm from natural disasters does not constitute persecution. See Matter of Acosta, 19 I&N Dec. 211 (BIA 1985), establishing the "immutable characteristic" test for particular social group claims.
- **INA § 244 (8 U.S.C. § 1254a) -- Temporary Protected Status (TPS):** Authorizes the Secretary of Homeland Security to designate a foreign state for TPS based on: (A) ongoing armed conflict; (B) environmental disaster (including earthquake, flood, drought, epidemic, or other environmental disaster) resulting in substantial but temporary disruption of living conditions, when the state is temporarily unable to handle the return of its nationals; or (C) extraordinary and temporary conditions. TPS provides protection from deportation and work authorization for a designated period (6-18 months, renewable).
- **Convention Against Torture (CAT) Protection:** Under 8 C.F.R. § 1208.16-18, implementing the CAT, a person may be protected from removal if they establish it is "more likely than not" they would be tortured by or with the acquiescence of a government official upon return. Climate-related CAT claims are theoretically possible where government action or inaction in the face of climate disasters constitutes acquiescence to torture-level harm.

### TPS for Environmental Disasters

TPS has been the primary US response to environmental displacement:

- **Haiti:** Designated following the 2010 earthquake; redesignated multiple times. As of 2025, approximately 150,000 Haitian TPS holders remain in the US, many having lived in the country for over 15 years without a path to permanent status.
- **Honduras and Nicaragua:** Designated following Hurricane Mitch (1998); TPS has been continuously extended.
- **Nepal:** Designated following the 2015 earthquake.
- **Somalia:** Designated in part due to environmental disaster (drought, famine) combined with armed conflict.

Critical limitations of TPS for climate displacement include: (1) the designation requires a specific, identifiable environmental disaster, not gradual climate degradation; (2) the "temporary" nature of TPS assumes conditions will improve, which is inconsistent with irreversible climate impacts such as sea-level rise; (3) TPS provides no path to permanent residence or citizenship; and (4) designation and termination are discretionary executive decisions subject to political change.

### Legislative Proposals

- **Climate Displaced Persons Act (H.R. 4732, 117th Congress):** Introduced by Representative Nydia Velazquez, would have created a dedicated visa category for persons displaced by climate change, with an annual cap of 50,000 admissions. The bill was not enacted.
- **Central American and Caribbean Climate Resilience Act (S. 2112, 118th Congress):** Proposed integrating climate displacement considerations into US foreign aid and immigration policy for the Western Hemisphere.

### Notable Cases

- **Teitiota v. New Zealand (CCPR/C/127/D/2728/2016, 2020):** While a UNHRC decision rather than a US case, Teitiota has been cited in US immigration proceedings. Ioane Teitiota, a national of Kiribati, sought asylum in New Zealand arguing that sea-level rise made conditions in Kiribati life-threatening. The UNHRC found no violation of the ICCPR on the facts but established the principle that climate change may engage non-refoulement obligations where it exposes individuals to life-threatening risks.
- **In re S-E-G-, 24 I&N Dec. 579 (BIA 2008):** While not a climate case, established the framework for "particular social group" analysis under which climate-displaced groups would need to demonstrate social distinction and particularity -- a standard that climate-based groups have struggled to meet.
- **Torres v. Barr (9th Cir. 2020):** Guatemalan asylum seeker argued that drought-induced crop failure forcing rural-to-urban migration constituted a form of persecution. The Ninth Circuit rejected the claim, finding no nexus to a protected ground.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's asylum and protection framework, implementing EU directives, provides multiple layers of protection potentially applicable to climate-displaced persons:

- **Asylum Act (Asylgesetz, AsylG):** Section 3 defines refugee status in accordance with the 1951 Convention and the EU Qualification Directive. Climate displacement alone does not satisfy the persecution requirement. Section 4 provides subsidiary protection for persons facing "serious harm" including the death penalty, torture or inhuman or degrading treatment, or serious and individual threat to life by reason of indiscriminate violence in armed conflict.
- **Residence Act (Aufenthaltsgesetz, AufenthG) § 60:** Implements non-refoulement obligations. Paragraph 5 prohibits deportation where it would violate the ECHR, and paragraph 7 prohibits deportation where there is a "concrete danger to life, limb, or freedom" in the destination country. Federal Administrative Court jurisprudence has interpreted § 60(7) to cover extreme humanitarian situations, including environmental catastrophes, under the doctrine of "Abschiebungsverbot aus humanitären Gründen" (humanitarian deportation prohibition).
- **Duldung (Toleration, AufenthG § 60a):** Where deportation is temporarily impossible (including due to disaster conditions in the destination country), authorities issue a Duldung -- a formal toleration of the person's presence that suspends deportation without conferring any positive immigration status. Duldung holders have limited rights and live in precarious legal limbo.
- **Humanitarian Admission Programs (AufenthG § 22, § 23):** The federal government or individual Bundesländer may establish humanitarian admission programs for persons from specific countries or situations. These have been used for conflict situations (Syria, Afghanistan) but could theoretically be applied to climate displacement.

### Administrative Practice

The Federal Office for Migration and Refugees (Bundesamt für Migration und Flüchtlinge, BAMF) processes asylum applications under a two-step procedure: first assessing Convention refugee status, then subsidiary protection, and finally national deportation prohibitions. Climate displacement claims typically fail at the first two stages but may succeed under § 60(5) or (7) AufenthG:

- **§ 60(5) AufenthG (ECHR-based prohibition):** Where return would violate ECHR Article 3 due to extreme humanitarian conditions linked to climate change. Following the ECtHR's decision in M.S.S. v. Belgium and Greece (2011, App. No. 30696/09), Article 3 can be engaged by extreme material deprivation.
- **§ 60(7) AufenthG (Individual danger):** Applied where the individual faces a "concrete danger" to life from environmental conditions. The Federal Administrative Court (BVerwG) has held that generalized dangers (allgemeine Gefahren) affecting the entire population of a country do not suffice -- only "extreme danger" (extreme Gefahrenlage) exceeding the general risk qualifies. This threshold has been met in cases involving severe drought or famine (e.g., BVerwG, Urteil vom 8. September 2011, 10 C 14.10, concerning conditions in Afghanistan).

### Notable Cases

- **BVerwG, Urteil vom 29. Juni 2010, 10 C 10.09:** The Federal Administrative Court established that conditions of extreme poverty and deprivation in a destination country can trigger the deportation prohibition under § 60(7) AufenthG, even without persecution by a specific actor. This framework has been applied in cases involving environmental degradation.
- **VG Düsseldorf, Urteil vom 22. November 2023, 6 K 5679/23.A:** An administrative court granted a Somali national a deportation prohibition under § 60(5) AufenthG, citing the combined effects of drought, famine, and clan-based persecution, representing an emerging "climate-plus" approach in German jurisprudence.
- **Sachverständigenrat für Integration und Migration (Expert Council on Integration and Migration) Report (2024):** The Expert Council recommended that Germany develop a proactive legal framework for climate displacement, including consideration of humanitarian visa programs and expanded subsidiary protection criteria.

## ECHR / EU Framework

### Convention Articles

The European Convention on Human Rights provides the most developed regional human rights framework applicable to climate displacement:

- **Article 3 -- Prohibition of Torture and Inhuman or Degrading Treatment:** The absolute prohibition on return to conditions amounting to inhuman or degrading treatment is the most robust non-refoulement protection in the ECHR system. The ECtHR has progressively expanded Article 3's scope to cover socioeconomic deprivation in the destination country (M.S.S. v. Belgium and Greece, 2011), severe illness without access to medical treatment (Paposhvili v. Belgium, Grand Chamber, 2016, App. No. 41738/10), and conditions in reception facilities (Tarakhel v. Switzerland, Grand Chamber, 2014, App. No. 29217/12). Climate-induced extreme deprivation could engage Article 3.
- **Article 2 -- Right to Life:** States have positive obligations to protect life from foreseeable risks, including environmental risks. The ECtHR's jurisprudence on environmental disasters (Budayeva v. Russia, 2008, App. No. 15339/02, mudslide deaths) and industrial hazards (Oneryildiz v. Turkey, Grand Chamber, 2004, App. No. 48939/99, methane explosion at waste site) establishes that failure to take reasonable protective measures against known environmental threats violates Article 2.
- **Article 8 -- Right to Respect for Private and Family Life:** The ECtHR recognized in Verein KlimaSeniorinnen Schweiz v. Switzerland (Grand Chamber, 2024, App. No. 53600/20) that states have positive obligations under Article 8 to take adequate measures to combat climate change. This ruling has implications for climate displacement, as failure to mitigate climate change that renders territory uninhabitable could engage state responsibility.

### Key International Instruments

- **1951 Convention Relating to the Status of Refugees:** Does not cover climate displacement. Efforts to amend the Convention to include climate refugees have been resisted by states concerned about opening the Convention to renegotiation, which could result in weakened protections.
- **Global Compact on Refugees (2018):** Paragraph 63 acknowledges that "climate, environmental degradation and natural disasters increasingly interact with the drivers of refugee movements" and encourages states to develop practices related to displacement caused by natural disasters and climate change. However, the Compact is non-binding.
- **Nansen Initiative and Platform on Disaster Displacement (PDD):** Established in 2012, the Nansen Initiative produced the Protection Agenda (2015), endorsed by 109 states, which outlines principles for the protection of persons displaced across borders by natural disasters and climate change. The PDD continues this work, promoting effective practices but without binding legal authority.
- **Paris Agreement (2015) -- Warsaw International Mechanism (WIM):** The WIM on Loss and Damage, established at COP 19 (2013) and referenced in Paris Agreement Article 8, includes a Task Force on Displacement that has issued recommendations on integrated approaches to avert, minimize, and address displacement related to climate change. COP 28 (2023) operationalized the Loss and Damage Fund, which could indirectly support displacement response.
- **UNHRC, Teitiota v. New Zealand (CCPR/C/127/D/2728/2016, 7 January 2020):** The Human Rights Committee found that while Kiribati had not yet reached the threshold of an uninhabitable territory, states may not deport individuals to countries where climate change has created conditions that violate the right to life. The Committee stated: "without robust national and international efforts, the effects of climate change in receiving states may expose individuals to a violation of their rights under articles 6 or 7 of the Covenant, thereby triggering the non-refoulement obligations of sending states."

### EU Qualification Directive

Directive 2011/95/EU (recast) establishes two forms of international protection:

- **Refugee Status (Article 2(d)):** Requires persecution on Convention grounds. Climate displacement does not qualify unless linked to discriminatory persecution.
- **Subsidiary Protection (Article 2(f), Article 15):** Available to persons facing "serious harm" defined as: (a) death penalty; (b) torture or inhuman or degrading treatment; or (c) serious and individual threat to life by reason of indiscriminate violence in armed conflict. None of these categories explicitly covers climate displacement, though category (b) could potentially be interpreted to include severe climate-induced deprivation, following the ECtHR's Article 3 jurisprudence.

### Temporary Protection Directive

Directive 2001/55/EC on temporary protection was activated for the first time in March 2022 for persons displaced from Ukraine (Council Implementing Decision (EU) 2022/382). The Directive could theoretically be activated for mass climate displacement, as Article 2(c) defines "mass influx" broadly. However, activation requires a qualified majority vote in the Council, and political consensus for climate displacement has not materialized.
