## Summary

War crimes — serious violations of the laws and customs of armed conflict that entail individual criminal responsibility — occupy a unique position at the intersection of domestic criminal law and public international law. The legal obligation to prosecute war crimes derives from the Geneva Conventions of 1949, which impose upon their 196 States Parties the duty to search for, prosecute, or extradite persons alleged to have committed grave breaches. The Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court (1998), which entered into force on 1 July 2002, codified war crimes in Article 8 and established the principle of complementarity: the ICC exercises jurisdiction only where national courts are unwilling or genuinely unable to prosecute. This framework places primary responsibility on domestic legal systems, and the manner in which states have discharged that responsibility varies considerably. South Korea enacted the Act on the Punishment of Crimes within the Jurisdiction of the International Criminal Court (국제형사재판소 관할 범죄의 처벌 등에 관한 법률) in 2007, incorporating Rome Statute crimes into domestic law with penalties including capital punishment. The United States, which is not a party to the Rome Statute, relies principally on the War Crimes Act of 1996 (18 U.S.C. Section 2441) and the Uniform Code of Military Justice (UCMJ) to prosecute war crimes, with jurisdiction limited to offenses involving U.S. nationals or members of the Armed Forces. Germany's Völkerstrafgesetzbuch (VStGB, Code of Crimes Against International Law), enacted in 2002 and substantially reformed in 2024, establishes pure universal jurisdiction over genocide, crimes against humanity, and war crimes, making Germany one of the most active states in international criminal law enforcement. The European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR) does not itself criminalize war crimes but shapes their prosecution through Article 7 (principle of legality) and Article 3 (prohibition of torture), while the European Union's institutional framework — including Council Decision 2003/335/JHA and the EU Genocide Network coordinated by Eurojust — facilitates cross-border cooperation in the investigation and prosecution of international crimes.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's war crimes legal architecture rests on the Act on the Punishment of Crimes within the Jurisdiction of the International Criminal Court (국제형사재판소 관할 범죄의 처벌 등에 관한 법률, Act No. 8719), enacted on 21 December 2007 and entering into force on 22 June 2008. The Act was adopted to implement South Korea's obligations under the Rome Statute, which the Republic of Korea ratified on 13 November 2002, becoming the first state in Northeast Asia to do so. The Act supplements the existing Criminal Act (형법, Act No. 293 of 1953) and the Military Criminal Act (군형법), which address certain war-related offenses but lack the specificity required by modern international criminal law.

**Purpose:** The Act states its objective as punishing crimes within ICC jurisdiction and establishing procedures for cooperation between the Republic of Korea and the ICC, "so as to respect the dignity and value of mankind and realize justice in the international community."

### Structure and Substantive Offenses

The ICC Crimes Act is organized into chapters addressing genocide (Article 8), crimes against humanity (Article 9), and war crimes (Article 10 and following). Unlike the Rome Statute, the Act does not distinguish between war crimes committed in international armed conflict and those committed in non-international armed conflict; it applies a unified framework.

| Offense | Article | Penalty |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Genocide — killing members of a protected group | Art. 8(1) | Death, life imprisonment, or minimum 7 years |
| Genocide — causing serious bodily or mental harm | Art. 8(2) | Imprisonment for life or minimum 5 years |
| Crimes against humanity — murder | Art. 9(1) | Death, life imprisonment, or minimum 7 years |
| War crimes — killing protected persons | Art. 10(1) | Death, life imprisonment, or minimum 7 years |
| War crimes — prohibited methods of warfare | Art. 11 | Life imprisonment or minimum 3 years |
| War crimes — prohibited weapons | Art. 12 | Life imprisonment or minimum 3 years |
| War crimes — resulting in death | Art. 10(1) | Death, life imprisonment, or minimum 7 years |
| Command responsibility — failure to prevent or punish | Art. 5 | Penalty equivalent to principal offender (may be reduced) |

### Statute of Limitations

Genocide and crimes against humanity are not subject to any statute of limitations for public prosecution or execution of judgment. Certain war crimes offenses may be subject to prescription periods under the general Criminal Act, although the most serious — those resulting in death — are treated analogously to the no-limitations regime.

### Landmark Cases and Historical Context

South Korea's record of war crimes adjudication is marked more by historical controversy than domestic prosecution.

**Korean War Atrocities:** During the Korean War (1950–1953), numerous atrocities were committed by forces on both sides. The No Gun Ri massacre (July 26–29, 1950) — in which U.S. Army units killed an estimated 250–300 South Korean refugees, predominantly women and children — was investigated only after an Associated Press investigation in 1999. A South Korean government inquest in 2005 certified 163 dead or missing and 55 wounded. No criminal prosecutions resulted.

**Vietnam War Conduct:** Between 1964 and 1973, South Korea deployed approximately 350,000 troops to South Vietnam. Korean forces are alleged to have perpetrated massacres in more than 45 incidents, with an estimated 9,000 civilian casualties across villages including Binh Tai, Binh An, Binh Hoa, and Ha My. In February 2023, a South Korean court recognized for the first time at the national level that South Korean military forces had massacred civilians during the Vietnam War, ordering the government to pay compensation to a Vietnamese survivor. The South Korean government has not officially acknowledged or apologized for these incidents.

**People's Tribunal (2018):** In 2018, a People's Tribunal on War Crimes by South Korean Troops during the Vietnam War was convened in Seoul by civil society organizations, although it lacked binding legal authority.

### Institutional Framework

Prosecution of war crimes under the ICC Crimes Act falls within the jurisdiction of ordinary courts, with military courts retaining jurisdiction over offenses committed by members of the armed forces. Korea maintains a National Human Rights Commission (국가인권위원회), although its mandate does not extend to criminal prosecution of international crimes. Cooperation with the ICC is administered through the Ministry of Justice.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has not ratified the Rome Statute and withdrew its signature in 2002. U.S. war crimes prosecution operates through a complex overlay of federal statute and military law.

**War Crimes Act of 1996 (18 U.S.C. Section 2441):** Enacted on 21 August 1996 and amended substantively in 1997 and 2006, this is the principal federal criminal statute addressing war crimes. The Act criminalizes conduct that constitutes a "grave breach" of the Geneva Conventions, violations of Common Article 3, and certain violations of the Annex to the Hague Convention IV of 1907.

**Jurisdictional Requirement:** The statute applies only when the person committing the war crime or the victim is a member of the Armed Forces of the United States or a national of the United States. This nationality-based jurisdictional nexus, combined with the absence of universal jurisdiction provisions, significantly limits the statute's reach compared to German or other European frameworks.

### Penalties

| Offense Category | Penalty |
|-----------------|---------|
| War crime (general) | Fine and/or imprisonment for life or any term of years |
| War crime resulting in death | Death penalty or life imprisonment |
| Grave breaches of Common Article 3 (torture, cruel treatment, murder) | Fine and/or imprisonment for life or any term of years; death if death results |

### Statute of Limitations

For grave breaches of Common Article 3 in non-international armed conflicts, there is no statute of limitations. For other war crimes, a five-year statute of limitations applies from the date of commission, unless death results, in which case there is no limitation period.

### The Uniform Code of Military Justice (UCMJ)

The UCMJ (10 U.S.C. Chapter 47) provides the military criminal justice system through which the majority of U.S. war crimes prosecutions have been conducted. Relevant articles include:

- **Article 118 (Murder):** Applicable to unlawful killings during armed conflict.
- **Article 119 (Manslaughter):** Applicable to negligent or reckless killings.
- **Article 128 (Assault):** Applicable to physical mistreatment of detainees or civilians.
- **Article 134 (General Article):** The catch-all provision for "disorders and neglects to the prejudice of good order and discipline."
- **Article 77 (Principals):** Establishes liability for those who aid, abet, or command the commission of offenses, forming the basis for command responsibility.

### Military Extraterritorial Jurisdiction Act (MEJA)

The Military Extraterritorial Jurisdiction Act (18 U.S.C. Chapter 212) extends federal criminal jurisdiction to certain offenses committed outside the United States by persons employed by or accompanying the Armed Forces. MEJA was enacted partly to address the jurisdictional gap for civilian contractors and Defense Department employees.

### Landmark Cases

**United States v. William Calley (1971) — My Lai Massacre:** On 16 March 1968, soldiers of Charlie Company, 1st Battalion, 20th Infantry Regiment massacred between 347 and 504 unarmed South Vietnamese civilians in the hamlet of My Lai. Of the 26 soldiers initially charged, only Lieutenant William Calley was convicted — of premeditated murder of 22 civilians. He was sentenced to life imprisonment at hard labor, which was subsequently reduced to 20 years, then 10 years, before his parole in 1974 after serving approximately three and a half years of house arrest. The case exposed systemic failures in military accountability and prompted the Department of Defense in 1974 to require centralized reporting of all war crimes allegations.

**Haditha Killings (2005):** On 19 November 2005, U.S. Marines of Kilo Company, 3rd Battalion, 1st Marines killed 24 Iraqi civilians, including women and children, in Haditha following a roadside IED attack. Eight Marines were initially charged. Six had their charges dismissed, one was acquitted, and Staff Sergeant Frank Wuterich — the last defendant — pleaded guilty to a single count of negligent dereliction of duty and received no prison time. The case represented the longest-running criminal prosecution of military personnel in the Iraq War.

**Abu Ghraib Prisoner Abuse (2003–2004):** Photographs documenting the systematic abuse and torture of detainees at the Abu Ghraib prison facility in Iraq prompted global outrage. Twelve soldiers were convicted of various charges, with sentences ranging from reprimand to ten years imprisonment. Specialist Charles Graner received the longest sentence of ten years. No senior officers or policymakers were criminally prosecuted, despite investigations by Major General Antonio Taguba and others suggesting institutional responsibility.

**Clint Lorance (2013):** First Lieutenant Clint Lorance was convicted by court-martial of two counts of murder for ordering his platoon to fire on unarmed Afghan villagers in 2012. He was sentenced to 20 years imprisonment but was pardoned by President Donald Trump on 15 November 2019, along with other servicemembers convicted or accused of war crimes, sparking debate over the erosion of military justice norms.

### The American Service-Members' Protection Act (ASPA)

Enacted in 2002, ASPA (22 U.S.C. Section 7421 et seq.) — colloquially known as the "Hague Invasion Act" — authorizes the President to use "all means necessary and appropriate" to free any U.S. or allied personnel detained by or on behalf of the ICC. It also prohibits U.S. cooperation with the ICC, including the provision of classified national security information and law enforcement cooperation. ASPA reflects the U.S. position that the ICC lacks jurisdiction over nationals of non-party states.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's Völkerstrafgesetzbuch (VStGB, Code of Crimes Against International Law) entered into force on 30 June 2002, the same day as the Rome Statute. It represents the most comprehensive domestic implementation of Rome Statute crimes in any national legal system and establishes pure universal jurisdiction — German courts may prosecute genocide, crimes against humanity, and war crimes regardless of where they were committed and regardless of the nationality of the perpetrator or victim.

**Statutory Structure:**

|---------|---------|
| Section 1 | Scope of application (universal jurisdiction) |
| Section 2 | Application of general criminal law |
| Section 3 | Acting on superior orders |
| Section 4 | Responsibility of military commanders and other superiors |
| Section 5 | Imprescriptibility (no statute of limitations) |
| Section 6 | Genocide |
| Section 7 | Crimes against humanity |
| Section 8 | War crimes against persons |
| Section 9 | War crimes against property and other rights |
| Section 10 | War crimes against humanitarian operations and emblems |
| Section 11 | War crimes involving prohibited methods of warfare |
| Section 12 | War crimes involving prohibited means of warfare |

### Penalties for War Crimes

| Offense | Section | Penalty |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Killing a protected person | § 8(1)(1) | Life imprisonment |
| Taking hostages | § 8(1)(2) | Minimum 5 years |
| Cruel or degrading treatment | § 8(1)(3) | Minimum 3 years |
| Sexual violence / forced prostitution | § 8(1)(4) | Minimum 2 years |
| Conscription of child soldiers (under 15) | § 8(1)(5) | Minimum 3 years |
| Deportation / unlawful transfer | § 8(1)(6) | Minimum 5 years |
| Sentencing without fair trial | § 8(1)(7) | Minimum 3 years |
| Unlawful confinement | § 8(1)(8) | Minimum 1 year |
| Attacks on civilian objects / property destruction | § 9(1) | Minimum 1 year |
| Attacks on humanitarian operations or emblems | § 10 | Minimum 3 years (minimum 1 year in less serious cases) |
| Use of prohibited methods (starvation, human shields, treacherous killing) | § 11 | Minimum 3 years |
| Use of prohibited weapons (poison, biological, expanding bullets) | § 12 | Minimum 3 years |
| Death resulting from § 8(1)(3)–(8) offenses | § 8(2) | Life imprisonment or minimum 10 years |
| Death resulting from § 9, 10, 11, 12 offenses | Various | Minimum 5 years to life imprisonment |

### Key Legal Principles

**Universal Jurisdiction (Section 1):** Section 1 VStGB provides that the Code applies to all offenses designated therein "even when the offence was committed abroad and bears no relation to Germany." This is the broadest universal jurisdiction provision of any national legal system. The Federal Prosecutor General (Generalbundesanwalt, GBA) exercises prosecutorial discretion under Section 153f of the Code of Criminal Procedure (StPO), which permits the GBA to decline prosecution where the suspect is not present in Germany and such presence is not expected.

**Command Responsibility (Section 4):** A military commander or civilian superior who fails to prevent subordinates from committing VStGB offenses is punishable. Where the superior intentionally or negligently fails to exercise proper supervision, and the subordinate commits an offense that the superior could have prevented, the superior is criminally liable.

**No Statute of Limitations (Section 5):** All VStGB offenses are imprescriptible. This contrasts with the general German criminal law, which imposes varying prescription periods for most offenses under the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB).

**Superior Orders (Section 3):** Acting on superior orders does not exculpate a perpetrator unless the subordinate did not realize the order was unlawful and it was not manifestly unlawful. For genocide, crimes against humanity, and war crimes resulting in killing, the defense of superior orders is effectively excluded, as such orders are always manifestly unlawful.

### The 2024 Reform

On 3 August 2024, the Law on the Further Development of International Criminal Law (Gesetz zur Fortentwicklung des Völkerstrafrechts) entered into force, substantially amending the VStGB. Key reforms include:

- **Expanded weapons prohibitions (Section 12):** Criminalization of the use of weapons primarily injuring by fragments non-detectable by X-rays and permanently blinding laser weapons.
- **Sexual and gender-based violence:** Explicit inclusion of sexual assault, sexual slavery, and forced abortion as war crimes and crimes against humanity, closing gaps in the previous codification.
- **Victims' rights:** Victims of war crimes and crimes against humanity may now join criminal proceedings as private accessory prosecutors (Nebenklage), a right previously available only for certain domestic offenses.
- **Functional immunity:** The reform clarifies that foreign state officials cannot invoke functional immunity when charged with international crimes under the VStGB, resolving prior jurisprudential uncertainty.
- **Data retention:** Review periods for storing investigative data on international crimes extended from 10 to 15 years for adults and from 5 to 10 years for juveniles.

### Landmark Cases

**Anwar Raslan — Koblenz Trial (2020–2022):** In the world's first criminal trial for state-sponsored torture in Syria, the Higher Regional Court of Koblenz convicted Anwar Raslan, a former colonel in Syria's General Intelligence Directorate and head of the Investigation Department at Branch 251, of crimes against humanity. Raslan was found guilty of co-perpetrating torture, 27 murders, sexual violence, and deprivation of liberty, and was sentenced to life imprisonment on 13 January 2022. The trial was conducted under universal jurisdiction, with Raslan having been arrested in Germany in 2019 after fleeing Syria. The Koblenz trial established Germany as the leading forum for accountability for atrocities committed in Syria.

**Eyad al-Gharib — Koblenz (2021):** In a related proceeding, Eyad al-Gharib, a former Syrian intelligence officer, was convicted of aiding and abetting crimes against humanity and sentenced to four years and six months imprisonment on 24 February 2021.

**FDLR Trial — Stuttgart (2015):** Ignace Murwanashyaka and Straton Musoni, leaders of the Forces Démocratiques de Libération du Rwanda (FDLR), were convicted by the Higher Regional Court of Stuttgart. Murwanashyaka received 13 years for aiding and abetting war crimes and commanding FDLR forces responsible for atrocities in the Democratic Republic of the Congo. Musoni received 8 years.

**Structural Investigations:** The GBA has opened structural investigations into crimes committed in Syria, Iraq, Ukraine, and other conflict zones. These investigations collect evidence and build case files even where specific suspects have not yet been identified, facilitating future prosecutions as suspects become accessible.

### Institutional Framework

The Federal Prosecutor General (GBA) has exclusive jurisdiction over VStGB offenses. Within the GBA, specialized units handle international criminal law cases. Germany's Federal Criminal Police Office (Bundeskriminalamt, BKA) maintains a dedicated unit — the Central Office for Combating War Crimes (Zentralstelle für die Bekämpfung von Kriegsverbrechen, ZBKV) — which conducts investigations. Germany also actively participates in the EU Genocide Network and cooperates with international investigative mechanisms, including the International, Impartial and Independent Mechanism for Syria (IIIM).

## ECHR & EU

### The European Convention on Human Rights

The ECHR does not itself define or criminalize war crimes. Its relevance to war crimes adjudication is indirect but consequential, operating primarily through three provisions:

**Article 7 — No Punishment Without Law (Nullum Crimen Sine Lege):**

Article 7(1) provides that "no one shall be held guilty of any criminal offence on account of any act or omission which did not constitute a criminal offence under national or international law at the time when it was committed." This provision has generated the most extensive jurisprudence in the context of war crimes prosecutions, particularly concerning the retroactive application of domestic criminal law to historical atrocities.

Article 7(2) provides an exception: "This Article shall not prejudice the trial and punishment of any person for any act or omission which, at the time when it was committed, was criminal according to the general principles of law recognised by civilised nations." This provision was originally drafted to ensure that the Nuremberg and Tokyo prosecutions were not deemed Convention violations.

**Article 3 — Prohibition of Torture:**

Article 3's absolute prohibition on torture and inhuman or degrading treatment has been invoked in the context of extradition and expulsion cases where individuals face war crimes prosecution in states with inadequate procedural safeguards or where treatment in detention would violate Convention standards.

**Article 6 — Right to a Fair Trial:**

War crimes defendants before domestic courts of Convention States are entitled to the full panoply of Article 6 protections, including the presumption of innocence, the right to examine witnesses, and the right to adequate time and facilities for the preparation of a defense.

### Landmark ECHR Cases

**Kononov v. Latvia (Grand Chamber, 2010):** Vassili Kononov, a former Soviet Red Partisan, was convicted by Latvian courts for war crimes committed in 1944 — specifically, leading a punitive expedition against the village of Mazie Bati in which nine villagers, including a pregnant woman, were killed. He challenged his conviction under Article 7, arguing that his acts did not constitute war crimes under applicable law in 1944. The Chamber initially found a violation of Article 7 by four votes to three (2008), but the Grand Chamber reversed by 14 votes to three, holding that the laws and customs of war as applicable in 1944, including the 1907 Hague Regulations and the principle of distinction between combatants and civilians, provided a sufficiently clear legal basis for the conviction. The Court emphasized that the villagers killed were civilians and that their execution constituted a war crime under international law at the time.

**Vasiliauskas v. Lithuania (Grand Chamber, 2015):** Vytautas Vasiliauskas was convicted in 2004 of genocide for participating in the killing of two Lithuanian partisans during a Soviet military operation in 1953. He challenged the conviction under Article 7, arguing that the partisans did not constitute a "national, ethnical, racial or religious group" as required by the definition of genocide under the 1948 Genocide Convention. The Grand Chamber found, by nine votes to eight, a violation of Article 7, holding that the Lithuanian courts had expanded the definition of genocide beyond its scope under international law as it stood in 1953, thereby violating the principle of legality.

**Streletz, Kessler and Krenz v. Germany (Grand Chamber, 2001):** Former senior officials of the German Democratic Republic were convicted for their roles in the "shoot-to-kill" policy at the Berlin Wall. The Court found no violation of Article 7, holding that the applicants could reasonably have foreseen that their acts would be considered criminal under international law, even though the GDR domestic law appeared to authorize them.

**Janowiec and Others v. Russia (Grand Chamber, 2013):** Concerning the Katyn massacre of 1940 and Russia's procedural obligations, the Court addressed the obligation to investigate potential war crimes, finding that the events fell outside the Court's temporal jurisdiction but engaging with the principle of effective investigation.

### EU Legal and Institutional Framework

**Council Decision 2003/335/JHA (8 May 2003):** This Decision, on the investigation and prosecution of genocide, crimes against humanity, and war crimes, requires EU Member States to ensure that national law enforcement authorities can effectively cooperate in the investigation and prosecution of alleged perpetrators of core international crimes. Member States must inform law enforcement when an applicant for a residence permit is suspected of having committed such crimes, enabling criminal proceedings domestically or referral to international courts.

**The EU Genocide Network:** Established pursuant to Council Decision 2002/494/JHA and coordinated by Eurojust (the EU Agency for Criminal Justice Cooperation), the Genocide Network brings together national contact points from all EU Member States responsible for investigating and prosecuting genocide, crimes against humanity, and war crimes. The Network facilitates the exchange of information, coordinates cross-border investigations, and publishes operational guidance and case-law digests.

**Eurojust's Role:** Eurojust supports national authorities in coordinating complex cross-border investigations into core international crimes. It has facilitated cooperation in cases arising from the conflicts in Syria, Iraq, and Ukraine, including supporting the use of joint investigation teams (JITs). The EU established a Joint Investigation Team with Ukraine and the ICC in March 2022 to investigate war crimes committed in connection with Russia's invasion.

**Directive 2012/29/EU (Victims' Rights Directive):** While not specific to war crimes, this Directive establishes minimum standards for the rights, support, and protection of victims of crime across the EU, including victims of international crimes prosecuted under universal jurisdiction in EU Member States.

**European Parliament Resolution of 4 July 2017:** The European Parliament adopted a resolution on addressing human rights violations in the context of war crimes and crimes against humanity, including genocide, calling on Member States to strengthen their capacity for investigation and prosecution and encouraging the use of universal jurisdiction.

## Comparative Analysis

### Jurisdictional Approaches

The most fundamental divergence among the four frameworks concerns the scope of jurisdiction. Germany's pure universal jurisdiction under Section 1 VStGB stands at one extreme: any person, anywhere, committing any VStGB offense is subject to German criminal jurisdiction, regardless of any connection to Germany. South Korea's ICC Crimes Act operates on a territorial and nationality basis — offenses must have a nexus to Korean territory or nationals. The United States occupies a similar position, with 18 U.S.C. Section 2441 explicitly requiring that the perpetrator or victim be a U.S. national or Armed Forces member. The ECHR/EU framework operates on a different plane entirely, providing the normative architecture (legality, fair trial, cooperation obligations) within which Member States exercise their own jurisdictional choices.

This jurisdictional divergence has practical consequences. Germany has emerged as the global leader in universal jurisdiction prosecutions of war crimes. The Koblenz trial of Anwar Raslan — a Syrian national, tried for crimes committed in Syria, against Syrian victims — would have been jurisdictionally impossible under either U.S. or South Korean law.

### Penalties and Sentencing

All four jurisdictions impose severe penalties for the most serious war crimes. South Korea and the United States retain the death penalty for war crimes resulting in death, while Germany, consistent with Article 102 of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz) and Article 1 of Protocol No. 13 to the ECHR, has abolished capital punishment. The maximum penalty under the VStGB is life imprisonment. The ECHR does not prescribe penalties but, through Protocol No. 13, prohibits the death penalty in all circumstances for Convention States that have ratified it, which includes all EU Member States.

A notable discrepancy exists in actual sentencing outcomes. U.S. military justice has historically imposed lenient sentences relative to the gravity of war crimes — the Haditha case resulted in no prison time, and the My Lai case yielded only three and a half years of house arrest for the mass murder of civilians. Germany's sentences in universal jurisdiction cases have generally been more proportionate, with life imprisonment in the Raslan case.

### Accountability Gaps

Each jurisdiction exhibits characteristic accountability gaps:

- **South Korea:** Despite extensive evidence of atrocities committed by Korean forces in Vietnam, no criminal prosecutions have been initiated. The 2023 civil court recognition of massacres has not been accompanied by criminal proceedings.
- **United States:** The combination of military self-policing, presidential pardon power (as exercised in the Lorance case), and the absence of ICC jurisdiction creates structural incentives against accountability. Research has identified 572 alleged perpetrators across 151 cases, with only 130 convictions and few lengthy sentences.
- **Germany:** The prosecutorial discretion afforded by Section 153f StPO means that, despite universal jurisdiction, the GBA may decline to prosecute where suspects are not present in Germany. Resource constraints limit the scope of structural investigations.
- **ECHR/EU:** The absence of direct enforcement power means the system relies on Member State willingness to investigate and prosecute, with the Court's role limited to reviewing the legality and fairness of proceedings after the fact.

### Rome Statute Relationship

The relationship between domestic law and the Rome Statute varies markedly. South Korea and Germany, as States Parties, have implemented the Rome Statute into domestic law (South Korea through the ICC Crimes Act, Germany through the VStGB). The United States not only refuses to ratify the Rome Statute but has enacted legislation (ASPA) actively opposing ICC jurisdiction over U.S. nationals. The ECHR framework is Rome Statute-neutral, although most ECHR States Parties are also Rome Statute parties.

### Command Responsibility

All four frameworks recognize command responsibility in some form, but the doctrinal articulation differs. Germany's Section 4 VStGB provides the most detailed statutory framework, distinguishing between intentional and negligent failure to prevent subordinate crimes. South Korea's Article 5 of the ICC Crimes Act establishes command responsibility with the possibility of penalty reduction. The United States relies primarily on the UCMJ and case law (United States v. Yamashita, 1946; United States v. Medina, 1971), without a comprehensive statutory codification comparable to the VStGB. The ECHR has not directly addressed command responsibility in its jurisprudence but applies its general principles of individual criminal liability and fair trial protections to cases involving superior responsibility.
