## Summary

Anti-corruption legal frameworks across the Global South reveal profoundly different institutional architectures, enforcement philosophies, and political dynamics. China's anti-corruption campaign (反腐败, fanfubai) under President Xi Jinping since 2012 represents arguably the most sweeping anti-graft operation in modern history, transitioning from the Communist Party's internal shuanggui (双规) detention system to the constitutionally established National Supervisory Commission (国家监察委员会, NSC) and its liuzhi (留置) detention power under the Supervision Law (监察法, 2018). India's anti-corruption architecture spans the Prevention of Corruption Act 1988 (amended 2018), the Lokpal and Lokayuktas Act 2013 -- realized after decades of advocacy and Anna Hazare's 2011 anti-corruption movement -- the Central Vigilance Commission (CVC), and the Central Bureau of Investigation (CBI). Nigeria's Economic and Financial Crimes Commission (EFCC), established by the EFCC Act 2004, and the Independent Corrupt Practices and Other Related Offences Commission (ICPC), established by the Corrupt Practices and Other Related Offences Act 2000, operate within a system where corruption is both endemic and a central political discourse. South Africa's post-apartheid anti-corruption framework centers on the Prevention and Combating of Corrupt Activities Act 12 of 2004 (PRECCA), the Public Protector established under Section 182 of the Constitution, and the National Prosecuting Authority (NPA), though the "state capture" era under President Jacob Zuma (documented by the Zondo Commission, 2018-2022) exposed systemic institutional compromise. Brazil's Operation Lava Jato (Car Wash, 2014-2021) prosecuted hundreds of politicians and executives using the Anti-Organized Crime Law (Law 12.850/2013) collaboration agreements, but itself became mired in controversy when leaked messages revealed prosecutorial misconduct. Colombia's anti-corruption framework operates through the Procuraduria General de la Nacion and the Contraloria General de la Republica, supplemented by the 2018 Anti-Corruption Consultation (consulta anticorrupcion) that narrowly failed to meet the participation threshold. Indonesia's Corruption Eradication Commission (Komisi Pemberantasan Korupsi, KPK), established by Law No. 30 of 2002, was once lauded as a model anti-corruption agency but has been progressively weakened by legislative amendments, particularly the controversial Law No. 19 of 2019. The Philippines' anti-corruption architecture includes the Sandiganbayan (special anti-graft court), the Office of the Ombudsman, and Republic Act 3019 (Anti-Graft and Corrupt Practices Act, 1960).

## China

### Institutional Architecture

China's anti-corruption system is unique in merging Communist Party discipline with state law. The Central Commission for Discipline Inspection (中央纪律检查委员会, CCDI) exercises party discipline over CPC members, while the National Supervisory Commission (NSC), established by the Supervision Law of the People's Republic of China (中华人民共和国监察法, March 2018) and constitutionalized through the 2018 Constitutional Amendment, exercises state supervisory power over all public officials. The NSC chair (currently Li Xi, preceded by Yang Xiaochao and Liu Jinguo, with Zhao Leji as founding chair in the constitutional role) ranks alongside the SPC and SPP heads. In practice, the CCDI and NSC operate as one integrated body with two nameplates (一套人马两块牌子, yitao renma liangkuai paizi).

### From Shuanggui to Liuzhi

The transition from shuanggui (双规, "double designation" -- designated time, designated place -- an extralegal party detention measure) to liuzhi (留置, retention in custody) under the Supervision Law represented a significant legalization effort. Shuanggui, practiced since the 1990s, lacked legal basis and was associated with coerced confessions, torture, and deaths in custody. Liuzhi, authorized by Supervision Law Article 22, permits detention of up to three months (extendable once to six months) with approval of the supervisory authority at the next higher level. Detainees must be held in designated locations with video monitoring (Article 44), and confessions obtained through torture are inadmissible (Article 40). However, access to legal counsel during liuzhi remains severely restricted, and the process lacks meaningful judicial oversight.

### Xi Jinping's Anti-Corruption Campaign

Since the 18th Party Congress (November 2012), the campaign has investigated over 4.7 million party members and punished over 500 officials at the provincial/ministerial level or above. Notable cases include:

- Former Politburo Standing Committee member Zhou Yongkang, sentenced to life imprisonment in 2015
- Former Central Military Commission Vice-Chairman Xu Caihou, who died before trial in 2015
- Former Vice Chairman of the Chinese People's Political Consultative Conference Su Rong
- Former Public Security Minister Sun Lijun, sentenced to death with two-year reprieve in 2022
- Former Justice Minister Fu Zhenghua, sentenced to death with two-year reprieve in 2022

The campaign extended internationally through "Operation Sky Net" (天网行动) and "Operation Fox Hunt" (猎狐行动), targeting corrupt officials who fled abroad, resulting in the repatriation of over 12,000 individuals and recovery of over RMB 40 billion between 2014 and 2023.

The campaign's scope expanded beyond individual prosecutions to institutional reform. The CPC's "eight-point regulation" (八项规定, baxiang guiding, December 2012) targeted official extravagance -- luxury banquets, gift-giving, and misuse of official vehicles. Over 700,000 party members were disciplined for violations of the eight-point regulation by 2023. The Central Inspection Groups (中央巡视组, zhongyang xunshi zu), dispatched by the CCDI to inspect provincial and ministerial-level units, conducted multiple rounds of inspections covering all provinces and central state organs.

### Criminal Law Provisions

The Criminal Law Articles 382-396 address corruption and bribery offenses. Article 382 defines corruption (贪污罪, tanwu zui) as misappropriation of public funds or property. Article 383 prescribes graduated penalties: life imprisonment or death for amounts of RMB 3 million or more (per the 2016 SPC/SPP Interpretation), 3-10+ years for RMB 200,000-3 million, and lesser terms for smaller amounts. Article 385 defines bribery (受贿罪, shouhui zui), and Article 390 addresses bribe-giving. The 2015 Ninth Amendment to the Criminal Law introduced "life imprisonment without commutation or parole" (终身监禁, zhongshen jianjin) as a penalty specifically for corruption, applied first to former Hebei provincial secretary Bai Enpei.

## India

### Prevention of Corruption Act 1988

The Prevention of Corruption Act 1988 (PCA), as amended by the Prevention of Corruption (Amendment) Act 2018, is India's primary anti-corruption statute. The 2018 Amendment introduced significant changes: Section 7 was restructured to criminalize obtaining an undue advantage by a public servant (replacing the earlier "gratification" language); a new Section 7A addressed taking undue advantage to influence a public servant; and Section 9 criminalized giving a bribe (previously only giving gratification to a public servant who demanded it was an offense). Section 17A introduced a requirement of prior approval for investigation of serving officials, criticized by anti-corruption advocates as a protective shield.

### Lokpal and Lokayuktas

The Lokpal and Lokayuktas Act 2013 established the Lokpal as a national anti-corruption ombudsman, fulfilling a demand first articulated in the Administrative Reforms Commission (1966). The Act was catalyzed by the 2011 India Against Corruption movement led by Anna Hazare and legal activist Arvind Kejriwal. The Lokpal's jurisdiction extends to the Prime Minister (with exceptions under Section 14 for matters relating to international relations, external and internal security, public order, atomic energy, and space), Union Ministers, Members of Parliament, and Group A-D central government officials. Justice Pinaki Chandra Ghose was appointed as the first Lokpal chairperson in March 2019. However, the Lokpal's operational capacity has been limited by staffing constraints and institutional resistance.

### CBI and Institutional Challenges

The Central Bureau of Investigation (CBI), established by resolution of the Ministry of Home Affairs in 1963 and deriving its investigative authority from the Delhi Special Police Establishment Act 1946, is the primary investigating agency for corruption cases. The Supreme Court in Vineet Narain v. Union of India (1998) 1 SCC 226 directed the establishment of a CBI Director selection committee and fixed tenure to ensure independence. However, the CBI's credibility has been repeatedly questioned -- the Supreme Court itself described it as a "caged parrot" in the coal allocation scam case (Manohar Lal Sharma v. Principal Secretary, 2013). The "general consent" controversy -- whereby states can withdraw consent for CBI operations on their territory under Section 6 of the DSPE Act -- has seen multiple opposition-ruled states (Kerala, West Bengal, Rajasthan, Maharashtra, Punjab, Chhattisgarh) withdrawing consent.

### Benami Transactions

The Benami Transactions (Prohibition) Amendment Act 2016 strengthened India's capacity to address hidden asset ownership. "Benami" property -- held in a fictitious name or in the name of a person who did not pay consideration -- is liable to confiscation under the Act. The Initiating Officer (IO) and Approving Authority under the Act can provisionally attach property pending adjudication before the Adjudicating Authority. The Supreme Court in Union of India v. Ganpati Dealcom Pvt. Ltd. (2022) upheld the constitutional validity of the amended Act's retrospective provisions.

## Africa

### Nigeria: EFCC and ICPC

The Economic and Financial Crimes Commission (EFCC), established by the EFCC (Establishment) Act 2004, has jurisdiction over economic and financial crimes including money laundering, advance fee fraud (419), and misappropriation of public funds. The EFCC is headed by a Chairman (currently Ola Olukoyede, succeeding Abdulrasheed Bawa). The Commission has prosecuted high-profile cases including former Governors James Ibori (convicted in UK, 2012), Diepreye Alamieyeseigha (convicted 2005, pardoned 2013), and former Petroleum Minister Diezani Alison-Madueke (facing proceedings in UK and Nigeria). The EFCC's interim forfeiture powers under the EFCC Act and the Administration of Criminal Justice Act 2015 Section 330 allow temporary forfeiture of assets pending trial.

The Independent Corrupt Practices and Other Related Offences Commission (ICPC), established by the Corrupt Practices and Other Related Offences Act 2000, has overlapping jurisdiction with the EFCC, creating institutional rivalry. The ICPC focuses on public-sector corruption, with powers to receive and investigate complaints (Section 6), prosecute (Section 61), and examine government practices (Section 6(b)-(f)).

The Supreme Court of Nigeria in Attorney General of Ondo State v. Attorney General of the Federation (2002) 9 NWLR (Pt. 772) 222 upheld the constitutionality of the ICPC Act, finding that the National Assembly had legislative competence under the residual legislative list.

### South Africa: State Capture and the Zondo Commission

South Africa's anti-corruption landscape is defined by the "state capture" phenomenon -- the systematic diversion of state resources and subversion of state institutions by private interests aligned with political power. The Judicial Commission of Inquiry into Allegations of State Capture (Zondo Commission), established by Presidential Proclamation in January 2018 and chaired by then-Deputy Chief Justice Raymond Zondo, heard testimony over four years and produced a six-part report (2022) documenting the Gupta family's capture of state-owned enterprises including Eskom, Transnet, and Denel, with the complicity of former President Jacob Zuma and senior officials.

The Prevention and Combating of Corrupt Activities Act 12 of 2004 (PRECCA) criminalizes corruption in both the public and private sectors, with a maximum penalty of life imprisonment. Section 34 imposes a duty on persons in positions of authority to report corruption exceeding R100,000 to SAPS. The Directorate for Priority Crime Investigation (DPCI, "Hawks"), established under the South African Police Service Amendment Act 57 of 2008 following the disbandment of the Scorpions, investigates serious corruption. The Constitutional Court in Glenister v. President of the Republic of South Africa 2011 (3) SA 347 (CC) held that the Constitution requires an independent anti-corruption unit with adequate structural and operational independence.

The Public Protector, established under Section 182 of the Constitution, gained prominence under Advocate Thuli Madonsela, whose "State of Capture" report (2016) triggered the Zondo Commission. However, Madonsela's successor, Busisiwe Mkhwebane, was impeached by the National Assembly in September 2023 for misconduct and incompetence.

## Latin America

### Brazil: Lava Jato and Its Legacy

Operation Lava Jato (Car Wash), launched in March 2014 from the 13th Federal Criminal Court in Curitiba under Judge Sergio Moro, became the largest anti-corruption investigation in Latin American history. Using colaboracao premiada (rewarded collaboration) plea agreements authorized by the Anti-Organized Crime Law (Law 12.850/2013), prosecutors secured testimony from over 170 cooperating defendants, uncovering a multi-billion dollar bribery scheme involving Petrobras, construction conglomerates (Odebrecht, now Novonesis; Andrade Gutierrez; Camargo Correa), and hundreds of politicians across multiple parties.

The operation resulted in the conviction and imprisonment of former President Luiz Inacio Lula da Silva (convicted in 2017, imprisoned April 2018 - November 2019), though the STF subsequently annulled his convictions in March 2021 on jurisdictional grounds (HC 193.726) and declared Judge Moro biased (habeas corpus 164.493) based on leaked Telegram messages published by The Intercept Brazil in 2019 (the "Vaza Jato" leaks). These messages revealed extensive coordination between Judge Moro and lead prosecutor Deltan Dallagnol, violating the accusatorial system's separation of functions. Moro subsequently served as Minister of Justice under President Bolsonaro (2019-2020) and was elected Senator for Parana in 2022.

Brazil's broader anti-corruption framework includes the Administrative Improbity Law (Law 8.429/1992, reformed by Law 14.230/2021), the Clean Company Act (Law 12.846/2013) establishing corporate liability for corruption, and the Lei de Acesso a Informacao (Freedom of Information Law, 12.527/2011). The Controladoria-Geral da Uniao (CGU) serves as the internal control body of the federal executive.

### Mexico: National Anti-Corruption System

Mexico's National Anti-Corruption System (Sistema Nacional Anticorrupcion, SNA), established by constitutional amendment in 2015 and implemented through the General Law of the National Anti-Corruption System (2016) and six associated laws, represents a comprehensive institutional architecture. The SNA comprises the Coordinating Committee (chaired by the Citizens' Participation Committee), the Superior Audit Office (Auditoria Superior de la Federacion, ASF), the Specialized Anti-Corruption Prosecutor (Fiscalia Especializada en Combate a la Corrupcion), the Federal Tribunal of Administrative Justice, and the National Institute of Transparency (INAI).

However, the SNA has been progressively undermined. The Specialized Anti-Corruption Prosecutor position remained vacant for years. President Andres Manuel Lopez Obrador (2018-2024) criticized the SNA as a product of the previous administration and relied instead on the Secretaria de la Funcion Publica and his personal anti-corruption narrative ("no puede haber gobierno rico con pueblo pobre"). The 2024 judicial reform under President Claudia Sheinbaum, which introduced popular election of judges, has raised concerns about the independence of courts handling corruption cases.

## Southeast Asia

### Indonesia: KPK Rise and Decline

The Komisi Pemberantasan Korupsi (KPK), established by Law No. 30 of 2002, was designed as an independent, powerful anti-corruption commission with authority to wiretap, conduct sting operations (operasi tangkap tangan, OTT), and prosecute without police or prosecutorial intermediation. The KPK achieved a remarkable 100% conviction rate in its first decade, prosecuting ministers, governors, judges, legislators, and police generals. High-profile cases include the conviction of former Democratic Party chairman Anas Urbaningrum (2014), former House Speaker Setya Novanto (2018), and former Social Affairs Minister Juliari Batubara (2021, for COVID-19 social aid corruption).

However, Law No. 19 of 2019 (the KPK Amendment Law) significantly weakened the institution by: converting KPK investigators and prosecutors to civil servants (subject to government control), establishing a Supervisory Board (Dewan Pengawas) with authority to approve wiretaps (previously requiring only internal approval), restricting wiretap duration, and removing the KPK's authority to issue travel bans independently. The Constitutional Court in Decision No. 70/PUU-XVII/2019 upheld the constitutionality of the amendments. A subsequent "civics test" (tes wawasan kebangsaan) resulted in the dismissal of 75 KPK employees in 2021, including prominent investigators, widely viewed as a purge.

Indonesia's Corruption Court (Pengadilan Tindak Pidana Korupsi, Tipikor), established by Law No. 46 of 2009, has exclusive jurisdiction over corruption cases. The court employs a mixed bench of career judges and ad hoc judges, the latter recruited from legal academics and practitioners to counter perceived judicial corruption.

### Philippines: Sandiganbayan and Ombudsman

The Philippines' anti-corruption institutional framework rests on the Sandiganbayan (established by Presidential Decree No. 1606 of 1978, reorganized by RA 8249 of 1997), a special court with jurisdiction over criminal and civil cases involving graft and corruption by public officials (Salary Grade 27 and above), and the Office of the Ombudsman (established under the 1987 Constitution, Article XI, implemented by RA 6770 of 1989), with authority to investigate and prosecute graft cases. Republic Act 3019 (Anti-Graft and Corrupt Practices Act, 1960) prohibits specific corrupt practices including persuading, inducing, or influencing another public officer to perform an act constituting a violation of rules (Section 3(a)), directly or indirectly requesting or receiving gifts in connection with government contracts (Section 3(b)), and causing undue injury to any party or giving any private party unwarranted benefits (Section 3(e)).

The plunder law (RA 7080 of 1991, as amended by RA 7659) criminalizes the accumulation of ill-gotten wealth amounting to at least PHP 50 million by a public officer through a series or combination of criminal acts.

Notable Philippine corruption cases include:

- Former President Joseph Estrada, convicted of plunder in 2007 (Estrada v. Sandiganbayan), pardoned by President Arroyo
- Former President Gloria Macapagal Arroyo, charged with plunder over the Philippine Charity Sweepstakes Office (PCSO) fund misuse, acquitted in 2016
- Former Senator Juan Ponce Enrile, charged in the Priority Development Assistance Fund (PDAF/"pork barrel") scam
- Janet Lim-Napoles, convicted as the mastermind of the PDAF scam involving PHP 10 billion in diverted public funds

The recovery of the Marcos ill-gotten wealth through the Presidential Commission on Good Government (PCGG), established in 1986 by Executive Order No. 1, remains ongoing four decades later. The PCGG has recovered approximately PHP 170 billion in cash, assets, and properties, but estimates of total Marcos wealth range from $5-10 billion.

## Comparative Analysis

### Institutional Design

The Global South exhibits three broad models of anti-corruption institutional design. First, the integrated party-state model (China), where anti-corruption enforcement is fused with ruling party discipline, achieving efficiency at the cost of political independence. Second, the independent commission model (Indonesia's KPK, Nigeria's EFCC, the Philippines' Ombudsman), inspired by Hong Kong's ICAC, which aims for structural independence but is vulnerable to political capture and legislative weakening. Third, the dispersed institutional model (India, Brazil, South Africa, Mexico), distributing anti-corruption functions across multiple bodies -- prosecutors, ombudsmen, audit institutions, specialized courts -- which creates redundancy but also fragmentation and inter-institutional rivalry.

### Political Will and Selectivity

A persistent critique across all Global South jurisdictions is that anti-corruption enforcement is politically selective. China's campaign has overwhelmingly targeted officials outside Xi Jinping's factional network. India's CBI is perceived as a tool of the ruling party ("caged parrot"). Nigeria's EFCC prosecutions disproportionately target opposition figures. Brazil's Lava Jato was criticized for targeting the Workers' Party while initially sparing figures from other parties. This selectivity undermines both the rule of law and public trust in anti-corruption institutions.

### Asset Recovery

Asset recovery represents a critical Global South priority. The Stolen Asset Recovery (StAR) Initiative estimates that developing countries lose $20-40 billion annually to corruption. China's Operation Sky Net has recovered billions; Nigeria recovered $322 million in Abacha loot from Switzerland, Jersey, and the United States through the World Bank's StAR framework; and the Philippines continues pursuing Marcos wealth estimated at $5-10 billion. The mechanisms vary: civil forfeiture (South Africa's POCA Chapter 5-6; Nigeria's ACJA Section 330), criminal confiscation (China's Criminal Law Art. 64; India's PCA Section 18), and international mutual legal assistance under UNCAC Chapter IV.

### Whistleblower Protection

Whistleblower protection varies dramatically across Global South jurisdictions and represents a critical gap in anti-corruption infrastructure. South Africa's Protected Disclosures Act 26 of 2000 (amended 2017) provides the most comprehensive framework, protecting employees who make disclosures of improper conduct to specified bodies. India's Whistle Blowers Protection Act 2014 received presidential assent but has not been brought into force due to pending amendments that critics argue would eviscerate its protections. Brazil's anti-corruption framework lacks dedicated whistleblower legislation, though the colaboracao premiada mechanism provides incentives for insider testimony. China's system relies on reporting to disciplinary inspection commissions, with limited formal protection for complainants -- the 2022 Anti-Organized Crime Law Article 53 provides some witness protection but not specifically for corruption whistleblowers. In Nigeria, the Whistleblower Policy of 2016 (a Ministry of Finance initiative) offers financial rewards of 2.5-5% of recovered assets but lacks statutory backing. Indonesia's KPK has a witness protection mechanism, but the assassination of anti-corruption witnesses remains a concern -- the 2004 acid attack on KPK investigator Novel Baswedan demonstrated the risks. The Philippines' Witness Protection, Security and Benefit Act (RA 6981 of 1991) provides for relocation and financial assistance but has been criticized as inadequate.

### UNCAC Implementation and Peer Review

The UN Convention against Corruption (UNCAC), ratified by all comparator states, establishes the global anti-corruption normative framework. The UNCAC Implementation Review Mechanism (IRM), launched in 2010, conducts peer reviews of state compliance. First-cycle reviews (covering UNCAC Chapters III and IV, criminalization and international cooperation) have been completed for all comparator states. Second-cycle reviews (covering Chapters II and V, preventive measures and asset recovery) are ongoing. Key findings include: China's lack of an independent judiciary reviewing corruption cases; India's failure to enact the Lokpal and bring it to full operational capacity (prior to 2019); Nigeria's overlapping institutional mandates; South Africa's weakening of the NPA and Hawks under state capture; Brazil's vulnerability to political instrumentalization of anti-corruption tools; Indonesia's legislative weakening of the KPK; and the Philippines' inadequate enforcement of asset declaration requirements. These reviews demonstrate that even where robust legal frameworks exist, implementation gaps undermine effectiveness.
