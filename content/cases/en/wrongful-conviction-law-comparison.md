## Summary

Wrongful conviction compensation -- the legal mechanisms by which individuals who have been erroneously convicted and imprisoned receive financial and non-financial redress from the state -- represents a fundamental test of each legal system's commitment to justice, accountability, and human dignity. South Korea provides compensation through the Criminal Compensation Act (형사보상법), which guarantees compensation for acquitted defendants who were detained during criminal proceedings, with per diem rates set by the Supreme Court and additional claims available under the State Compensation Act (국가배상법) Article 2 for cases involving prosecutorial or judicial misconduct, as exemplified by the landmark Yoon Seong-yeo Case (윤성여 사건) and systematic compensation for democracy activists wrongfully convicted under authoritarian military regimes. The United States has no federal constitutional right to wrongful conviction compensation, relying instead on a patchwork of state compensation statutes (38 states plus the District of Columbia as of 2026), federal compensation under 28 U.S.C. Section 2513 for federal wrongful convictions, and civil rights litigation under 42 U.S.C. Section 1983 for prosecutorial and police misconduct, with the Innocence Project identifying over 3,500 exonerations since 1989 through DNA evidence and other means. Germany guarantees compensation through the Act on Compensation for Criminal Prosecution Measures (Gesetz über die Entschädigung für Strafverfolgungsmaßnahmen, StrEG), providing mandatory compensation for acquittal, dismissal, or non-prosecution after detention, with the Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) holding that the right to compensation derives from the Rechtsstaatsprinzip (rule of law principle). At the European level, Article 3 of Protocol No. 7 to the ECHR establishes a right to compensation for wrongful conviction, the CJEU has recognized state liability for judicial errors under the Köbler doctrine, and the EU has promoted harmonization of victims' rights through Directive 2012/29/EU, while the UN Human Rights Committee has addressed wrongful conviction compensation under Article 14(6) of the ICCPR.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's wrongful conviction compensation system reflects both the Constitutional guarantee of Article 28 and the nation's particular history of political persecution under authoritarian regimes.

### Constitutional Foundation

Article 28 of the Constitution provides: "In the event that a criminal suspect or defendant who has been detained is not indicted or is acquitted, such person may claim compensation from the State in accordance with the conditions as prescribed by Act" (형사피의자 또는 형사피고인으로서 구금되었던 자가 법률이 정하는 불기소처분을 받거나 무죄판결을 받은 때에는 법률이 정하는 바에 의하여 국가에 정당한 보상을 청구할 수 있다).

### Criminal Compensation Act (형사보상법)

The Criminal Compensation Act provides per diem compensation for persons who were detained and subsequently acquitted. The Supreme Court sets minimum and maximum per diem rates, currently ranging from 100,000 KRW to 400,000 KRW per day of detention (approximately $85 to $340). The compensation is calculated based on the total days of detention multiplied by the per diem rate, with the court considering factors such as the nature of the charges, the conditions of detention, lost income opportunities, and mental distress.

### State Compensation Act (국가배상법)

For wrongful convictions attributable to official misconduct -- such as fabricated evidence, coerced confessions, or negligent investigation -- victims may file tort claims against the state under Article 2 of the State Compensation Act, which establishes state liability for damages caused by public officials in the execution of their duties through intentional misconduct or negligence. These claims are not limited to per diem rates and may include compensation for lost income, property damage, and non-pecuniary damages.

### Historical Wrongful Convictions: Democratization Cases

South Korea's most significant wrongful conviction cases involve political dissidents convicted under the authoritarian regimes of Park Chung-hee and Chun Doo-hwan:

**People's Revolutionary Party Incident (인혁당 사건, 1975):** Eight individuals were executed after being convicted of espionage in a trial widely regarded as politically motivated. In 2007, the Seoul Central District Court posthumously acquitted all eight defendants, and the court awarded approximately 63.1 billion KRW (approximately $70 million) in compensation to the families.

**Samcheong Education Camp (삼청교육대):** Following the 1980 military coup, an estimated 60,000 persons were forcibly interned in "purification camps" without trial. Survivors and families have received compensation through a combination of the State Compensation Act and special legislation.

### Truth and Reconciliation Commission

The Truth and Reconciliation Commission (진실·화해를 위한 과거사정리위원회), established in 2005 and reconstituted in 2020, has investigated historical wrongful convictions and recommended compensation. The Commission's findings have supported numerous retrial applications and compensation claims.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States lacks a uniform national framework for wrongful conviction compensation, resulting in substantial variation across jurisdictions.

### Federal Compensation

**28 U.S.C. Section 2513:** Provides compensation of $50,000 per year of imprisonment ($100,000 per year if the sentence included death) for persons unjustly convicted of federal offenses. The statute requires a certificate of innocence from the sentencing court and filing of a claim with the U.S. Court of Federal Claims within six years of conviction reversal.

### State Compensation Statutes

As of 2026, 38 states and the District of Columbia have enacted wrongful conviction compensation statutes, though the terms vary dramatically:

| State | Compensation per Year | Additional Benefits |
|-------|----------------------|-------------------|
| Texas | $80,000/year + $25,000/year on parole | Health insurance, tuition, annuity |
| California | $140/day (approximately $51,100/year) | Lost wages claim available |
| New York | No fixed amount; court discretion | All consequential damages |
| Florida | $50,000/year (capped at $2 million) | Tuition, expedited sealing |
| Illinois | Per diem rate set by court | Attorney fees, certificates |

### Civil Rights Litigation (42 U.S.C. Section 1983)

Section 1983 provides a federal cause of action against state officials who, acting under color of state law, deprive individuals of constitutional rights. In wrongful conviction cases, plaintiffs typically allege violations of the Fourth Amendment (fabricated evidence, false arrest), the Fifth/Fourteenth Amendment (due process -- Brady violations, coerced confessions), and the Sixth Amendment (ineffective assistance of counsel).

**Brady v. Maryland, 373 U.S. 83 (1963):** Established that the prosecution's suppression of evidence favorable to the defense violates due process, forming the basis for many wrongful conviction claims.

**Connick v. Thompson, 563 U.S. 51 (2011):** The Supreme Court held that a district attorney's office cannot be held liable under Section 1983 for a single Brady violation based on a failure-to-train theory absent a pattern of similar violations, significantly limiting municipal liability for prosecutorial misconduct.

### The Innocence Movement

The Innocence Project, founded in 1992 by Barry Scheck and Peter Neufeld at the Benjamin N. Cardozo School of Law, has been instrumental in securing exonerations through DNA testing. The National Registry of Exonerations has documented over 3,500 exonerations in the United States since 1989. Leading causes of wrongful conviction include mistaken eyewitness identification (contributing to approximately 69% of DNA exonerations), false confessions (29%), forensic science errors (24%), and jailhouse informant testimony.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany provides comprehensive wrongful conviction compensation through the StrEG, grounded in the constitutional Rechtsstaatsprinzip.

### StrEG (Gesetz über die Entschädigung für Strafverfolgungsmaßnahmen)

The StrEG, originally enacted in 1971 and most recently amended in 2021, provides mandatory compensation for:

**Section 1 -- Acquittal in Criminal Proceedings:** Compensation is owed when a defendant is acquitted, the proceedings are dismissed, or the court declines to open main proceedings (Hauptverfahren). The right arises automatically upon acquittal without requiring proof of state fault.

**Section 2 -- Pre-Trial Detention and Other Measures:** Compensation covers pre-trial detention (Untersuchungshaft), provisional arrest (vorläufige Festnahme), seizure (Beschlagnahme), search (Durchsuchung), and other coercive measures.

### Compensation Rates

The StrEG provides compensation for material and non-material damages:

**Material Damages:** Lost income, property damage, and other quantifiable financial losses are compensated in full. The claimant must prove the specific losses resulting from the criminal prosecution measures.

**Non-Material Damages (Immaterielle Schäden):** Section 7(3) StrEG provides a per diem rate for non-material damages arising from deprivation of liberty. The rate was increased to EUR 75 per day in 2021, up from EUR 25 per day (the rate that had been unchanged since 1971). Legal scholars and practitioners have criticized even the increased rate as inadequate.

### Exclusion Grounds

Compensation may be reduced or denied under Section 5 StrEG if the accused caused the criminal prosecution measures through intentional or grossly negligent conduct -- for example, by making a false confession or providing misleading information. The Bundesgerichtshof has held that the exclusion must be applied narrowly, and mere exercise of the right to silence does not constitute contributory fault.

### Wiederaufnahmeverfahren (Retrial Procedure)

The Strafprozessordnung (StPO) Sections 359-373 govern the retrial procedure, which is the primary mechanism for challenging wrongful convictions. A retrial may be initiated based on new evidence (neue Tatsachen oder Beweismittel), proven falsity of evidence used at trial, or judicial/prosecutorial misconduct. Germany amended Section 362 StPO in 2021 to allow retrials to the detriment of acquitted defendants in murder cases when new, compelling evidence of guilt emerges -- a controversial change that the Bundesverfassungsgericht is reviewing for compatibility with the ne bis in idem principle.

### Notable Cases

**Gustl Mollath Case (2006-2014):** Mollath was committed to a psychiatric institution in 2006 after being convicted of assault and found mentally incompetent. In 2014, he was acquitted in a retrial after evidence emerged that the psychiatric evaluation was flawed and that Mollath's allegations of financial crimes by bank employees (which had been dismissed as delusions) were substantially accurate. The case prompted reforms to forensic psychiatric procedures.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Protocol No. 7, Article 3

Article 3 of Protocol No. 7 to the ECHR provides: "When a person has by a final decision been convicted of a criminal offence and when subsequently his conviction has been reversed, or he has been pardoned, on the ground that a new or newly discovered fact shows conclusively that there has been a miscarriage of justice, the person who has suffered punishment as a result of such conviction shall be compensated according to the law or the practice of the State concerned, unless it is proved that the non-disclosure of the unknown fact in time is wholly or partly attributable to him."

**Allen v. United Kingdom (Application No. 25424/09, Grand Chamber Judgment of July 12, 2013):** The Grand Chamber addressed the relationship between the presumption of innocence (Article 6(2)) and compensation for wrongful conviction, holding that statements made by courts when refusing compensation must not cast doubt on the acquitted person's innocence. The reasoning in compensation proceedings must not suggest that the applicant was in fact guilty.

### ECHR Article 5(5) -- Unlawful Detention

Article 5(5) provides an enforceable right to compensation for detention in contravention of Article 5. This provision has been applied in wrongful conviction cases where pre-trial detention exceeded reasonable limits or was not based on reasonable suspicion.

**Assanidze v. Georgia (Application No. 71503/01, Grand Chamber Judgment of April 8, 2004):** The Court ordered the immediate release of a Georgian political figure held in detention despite presidential pardon and court acquittal, awarding EUR 150,000 in non-pecuniary damages under Article 41.

### EU Framework

The EU has addressed wrongful conviction compensation indirectly through several instruments:

**Directive 2012/29/EU (Victims' Rights Directive):** While primarily focused on crime victims, the Directive's principles of dignity, respect, and non-discrimination apply to the treatment of wrongfully convicted persons within the criminal justice system.

**Köbler Doctrine (C-224/01, 2003):** The CJEU established that member states may be liable for damages caused by judicial decisions that manifestly infringe EU law, providing a potential remedy for wrongful convictions resulting from judicial misapplication of EU law.

**European Investigation Order Directive 2014/41/EU:** Facilitates cross-border evidence gathering that may support wrongful conviction claims, including DNA evidence and witness testimony in other member states.

## Comparative Analysis

### Right to Compensation

The four jurisdictions demonstrate a spectrum from constitutional guarantee to discretionary remedy:

**Constitutional Right (South Korea):** Article 28 explicitly guarantees compensation for acquitted detainees, providing the strongest textual foundation.

**Statutory Right with Constitutional Underpinning (Germany):** The StrEG creates a statutory right, but the Bundesverfassungsgericht has grounded it in the Rechtsstaatsprinzip, giving it quasi-constitutional status.

**Treaty Right (ECHR):** Protocol 7, Article 3 creates an international legal obligation, though not all ECHR member states have ratified Protocol 7.

**No Constitutional Right (United States):** There is no federal constitutional right to wrongful conviction compensation, and the Supreme Court has not recognized one. Compensation depends entirely on state legislation and civil rights litigation.

### Compensation Adequacy

Compensation amounts vary dramatically. Texas provides $80,000 per year of imprisonment plus supplemental benefits. Germany's EUR 75 per day (approximately EUR 27,375 per year) is widely criticized as inadequate. South Korea's per diem rates (100,000-400,000 KRW per day) fall in the middle range. The ECHR's just satisfaction awards are case-specific and have ranged from modest sums to substantial awards exceeding EUR 100,000.

### Procedural Barriers

The United States imposes the most significant procedural barriers, with many states requiring proof of "actual innocence" (as opposed to mere acquittal or reversal), barring compensation for persons who contributed to their own conviction (including through false confessions), and imposing short filing deadlines. Germany and South Korea impose fewer barriers, requiring only acquittal or equivalent disposition. The ECHR's Protocol 7 standard requires that the conviction was reversed based on "a new or newly discovered fact" showing "conclusively" a miscarriage of justice.
