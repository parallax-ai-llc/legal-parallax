## Summary

The right to internet access -- whether as a standalone fundamental right, a component of existing rights to information and communication, or a universal service obligation -- has become a defining legal question of the digital era. As governments, courts, and international bodies increasingly recognize that meaningful participation in modern society requires internet connectivity, legal frameworks have diverged significantly in their approaches to enshrining, implementing, and enforcing this right. South Korea has positioned itself as a global leader in broadband infrastructure through the Framework Act on Telecommunications (전기통신기본법) and the National Information Society Agency Act, achieving near-universal connectivity but stopping short of constitutionalizing internet access as a fundamental right. The United States treats broadband access primarily as a market-driven service regulated by the Federal Communications Commission under the Communications Act of 1934 (as amended by the Telecommunications Act of 1996), with recent legislative interventions including the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act of 2021 (Pub. L. 117-58) allocating $65 billion for broadband expansion. Germany recognized internet access as functionally essential through the Bundesverfassungsgericht's jurisprudence on the Existenzminimum (subsistence minimum) and enacted the Telecommunications Act 2021 (TKG 2021) establishing a legal right to adequate broadband. The European Union has affirmed internet access as a component of the right to education and information through the European Electronic Communications Code (Directive (EU) 2018/1972), while the Council of Europe and the ECHR framework protect internet access as an element of Article 10 freedom of expression.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's approach to internet access combines extensive state-directed infrastructure investment with regulatory frameworks that treat connectivity as a core public utility:

- **Framework Act on Telecommunications (전기통신기본법):** Establishes the foundational principle that telecommunications services, including broadband internet, constitute essential infrastructure for national economic and social development. Article 3 declares the government's obligation to promote universal access to telecommunications services. Article 37 designates certain telecommunications services as "universal services" (보편적 역무) that must be provided throughout the national territory.

- **Telecommunications Business Act (전기통신사업법) Article 4:** Imposes universal service obligations on designated telecommunications carriers. The Korea Communications Commission (KCC) designates KT (Korea Telecom) as the universal service provider, requiring nationwide broadband availability. Since 2012, broadband internet has been explicitly included in the scope of universal service obligations, expanding beyond traditional telephony.

- **Framework Act on Intelligent Informatization (지능정보화 기본법, 2020):** Replaced the earlier Framework Act on Informatization Promotion. Article 42 mandates that the government implement measures to bridge the digital divide (정보격차 해소), including providing internet access devices, digital literacy education, and subsidized connectivity for vulnerable populations (the elderly, persons with disabilities, rural residents, and low-income households). Article 46 establishes the National Information Society Agency (NIA) as the implementing body.

- **National Informatization Basic Plan:** South Korea's cyclical national plans have systematically prioritized broadband deployment. The Korean New Deal Digital Transformation Initiative (2020) allocated KRW 58.2 trillion for digital infrastructure including nationwide 5G coverage, smart village broadband expansion, and public WiFi in underserved areas. The plan targeted 100% broadband coverage in rural areas by 2025.

- **Constitution of the Republic of Korea, Article 21 (Freedom of Expression):** While the Constitution does not explicitly mention internet access, the Constitutional Court has interpreted Article 21's guarantee of freedom of speech and the press to encompass digital expression. In Decision 2018헌마730 (2020), the Court stated that restrictions on internet access "fundamentally constrain the exercise of the freedom of expression guaranteed by Article 21."

### Key Principles

South Korea's framework is characterized by a "build first, regulate later" approach, with massive public investment creating one of the world's most connected societies (97.6% broadband penetration as of 2024). Rather than constitutionalizing internet access as an explicit right, South Korea has achieved near-universal connectivity through infrastructure mandates and universal service obligations, rendering the rights question somewhat academic in practice.

A distinctive feature is South Korea's "information gap index" (정보격차지수), annually measured by the NIA to track disparities in access, skills, and usage across demographic groups. The 2024 index showed the gap narrowing to 7.4% overall, with the greatest remaining disparities among elderly populations (65+) and persons with disabilities. This data-driven approach enables targeted policy interventions.

South Korea's approach to internet shutdowns is also notable. While no explicit statutory prohibition exists, the Constitutional Court's jurisprudence on Article 21 effectively precludes government-ordered general internet shutdowns. During the 2016-2017 candlelight protests and the COVID-19 pandemic, the government maintained full internet access, even as neighboring jurisdictions in Asia implemented shutdowns during political crises.

### Notable Cases

- **Constitutional Court, Decision 2012헌마191 (Internet Real-Name System, 2012):** The Court struck down the Internet Real-Name Verification System (인터넷 실명제), which required identity verification before posting on large platforms, as an unconstitutional restriction on freedom of expression. The decision affirmed that anonymous internet access is protected under Article 21.

- **Constitutional Court, Decision 2018헌마730 (Internet Access Restriction, 2020):** The Court reviewed a case where a prisoner's internet access was completely denied. While upholding the restriction under the specific facts (security concerns), the Court recognized that internet access is "closely connected to the exercise of fundamental rights including freedom of expression, right to know, and right to education," signaling that blanket internet denial may be unconstitutional outside the prison context.

- **Seoul Administrative Court, Digital Divide and Elderly Access (2023):** The court held that the government's transition to digital-only service delivery for certain social welfare benefits, without adequate accommodations for elderly citizens lacking internet access or digital literacy, violated the Framework Act on Intelligent Informatization Article 42's digital divide bridging obligation.

## United States

### Federal Framework

The United States treats internet access primarily as a commercial service rather than a fundamental right, with regulatory authority centered on the FCC and significant recent legislative investment:

- **Communications Act of 1934 (47 U.S.C.) as amended by the Telecommunications Act of 1996:** The foundational federal telecommunications law. Section 254 establishes the universal service framework, requiring that telecommunications services be available at "just, reasonable, and affordable rates" and creating the Universal Service Fund (USF), which collects approximately $9 billion annually from telecommunications providers. The USF finances four programs: (a) the High-Cost Program supporting rural broadband, (b) Lifeline providing discounted service to low-income consumers, (c) E-Rate connecting schools and libraries, and (d) the Rural Health Care Program.

- **Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA, Pub. L. 117-58, 2021):** Allocated $65 billion for broadband infrastructure, the largest single investment in connectivity in U.S. history. Key programs include: (a) the Broadband Equity, Access, and Deployment (BEAD) Program ($42.45 billion for state broadband grants); (b) the Affordable Connectivity Program (ACP, $14.2 billion for household subsidies of $30/month, extended but ultimately expired June 2024); (c) the Digital Equity Act ($2.75 billion for digital inclusion activities); and (d) the Middle Mile Broadband Infrastructure Grant Program ($1 billion).

- **FCC Broadband Definition (2024 Update):** The FCC revised its benchmark for adequate broadband from 25/3 Mbps (download/upload) to 100/20 Mbps in March 2024, with a long-range goal of 1000/500 Mbps. While this definition does not create an enforceable right, it informs universal service determinations and broadband subsidy eligibility.

- **Net Neutrality / Open Internet Orders:** The FCC's authority over internet service has fluctuated dramatically. The 2015 Open Internet Order reclassified broadband as a Title II telecommunications service; the 2017 Restoring Internet Freedom Order reversed this classification; the FCC's 2024 reinstatement of Title II classification was vacated by the Sixth Circuit in 2025 (Ohio Telecom Ass'n v. FCC), leaving broadband classified as an information service under Title I with limited federal regulatory authority.

### State Variations

- **Vermont (Act 71, 2019):** Established the Vermont Community Broadband Board and created a statewide broadband plan targeting 100% coverage. Vermont also authorized municipalities to build and operate their own broadband networks, overriding industry opposition.

- **California (AB 14, 2021):** Declared broadband a public utility and established that every Californian has the right to "affordable, reliable high-speed broadband" -- one of the few state-level declarations of a right to internet access.

- **New York (Affordable Broadband Act, 2021):** Required ISPs to offer a $15/month plan to qualifying low-income households, though the law was struck down in ACA Connects v. Bonta (2023, reversed and remanded) on federal preemption grounds, with the question of state authority over broadband pricing remaining unsettled.

- **Municipal Broadband:** Approximately 20 states have passed laws restricting or prohibiting municipalities from offering broadband service, while others (Vermont, Colorado, Washington) have authorized and encouraged municipal networks. The FCC's 2015 attempt to preempt state municipal broadband bans was reversed by the Sixth Circuit in Tennessee v. FCC (2016).

### Notable Cases

- **Packingham v. North Carolina (582 U.S. 98, 2017):** The Supreme Court struck down a North Carolina law prohibiting registered sex offenders from accessing social media, calling the internet "the modern public square" and stating that access to it is "important for speaking and listening" under the First Amendment. While not establishing a right to internet access per se, the decision recognized the internet's fundamental role in democratic participation.

- **Ohio Telecom Association v. FCC (6th Cir., 2025):** The Sixth Circuit vacated the FCC's 2024 reinstatement of net neutrality rules and Title II classification of broadband, holding that the FCC lacked authority to reclassify broadband without clear congressional authorization under the "major questions doctrine" established in West Virginia v. EPA (2022). This decision leaves broadband regulation in a state of legal uncertainty.

- **Biden v. Knight First Amendment Institute (2021, cert. denied):** The Supreme Court declined to address whether public access to internet platforms is protected by the First Amendment, with Justice Thomas's concurrence suggesting that dominant platforms might be treated as common carriers with access obligations.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany has established the most robust legal framework for internet access as a right in Europe, combining constitutional principles with specific statutory provisions:

- **Basic Law (Grundgesetz) Derived Rights:** While the Grundgesetz does not explicitly mention internet access, the Bundesverfassungsgericht (BVerfG) has derived a functional right to internet access from several constitutional principles: (a) Article 1(1) (human dignity, Menschenwürde) in conjunction with Article 20(1) (Sozialstaatsprinzip, social state principle) establishes the right to an Existenzminimum (subsistence minimum) that, in the digital age, encompasses internet connectivity; (b) Article 2(1) (free development of personality) requires access to the informational infrastructure necessary for personal development; (c) Article 5(1) (freedom of information) protects the right to access "generally accessible sources" of information, which includes the internet.

- **Telecommunications Act 2021 (Telekommunikationsgesetz, TKG 2021) § 157:** Establishes a legal right to "adequate broadband internet access service" (Recht auf Versorgung mit Telekommunikationsdiensten). This provision entitles every household and business in Germany to demand connection to a broadband internet service meeting minimum quality parameters set by the Bundesnetzagentur (BNetzA). This is one of the most explicit statutory rights to internet access in the world.

- **BNetzA Minimum Broadband Determination (June 2023):** Pursuant to TKG § 157(3), the BNetzA determined minimum broadband parameters: 10 Mbps download speed, 1.7 Mbps upload speed, and 150ms latency. Citizens who cannot obtain service meeting these parameters may file a complaint with the BNetzA, which can order designated providers to supply service. These thresholds are subject to annual review and upward revision.

- **Social Code Book XII (SGB XII) § 27a(1):** The Existenzminimum guaranteed under constitutional and social welfare law includes "socio-cultural participation" costs, which the Federal Social Court (Bundessozialgericht) has interpreted to encompass basic internet access and an internet-capable device. The 2024 standard benefit rates (Regelbedarfe) include an allowance for telecommunications and internet services.

- **Gigabit Strategy (Gigabitstrategie, 2022):** The Federal Government's infrastructure plan targeting nationwide coverage of at least 50% of households with fibre-to-the-premises (FTTP) by 2025 and 100% by 2030. The strategy allocates EUR 12 billion in federal subsidies for areas where market-driven deployment is insufficient.

### Key Principles

Germany's approach treats internet access as a necessary component of dignified existence and social participation in the digital age, consistent with the social state principle. The statutory right under TKG § 157 is unique in Europe for its enforceability: citizens can initiate administrative proceedings to compel provision of adequate broadband, shifting the burden from the individual to designated telecommunications providers backed by state subsidy.

The BNetzA's role as both the telecommunications regulator and the enforcement body for the right to broadband ensures administrative capacity for rights enforcement. As of 2025, the BNetzA had received approximately 1,200 complaints under § 157, resulting in over 400 orders to providers to extend service to underserved locations.

Germany also addresses internet access during emergencies through the Civil Protection and Disaster Assistance Act (ZSKG), which mandates the maintenance of critical telecommunications infrastructure during crises, and the BSI Act (BSI-Gesetz), which requires operators of critical infrastructure to maintain cybersecurity and service continuity.

### Notable Cases

- **BVerfG, Hartz IV Decision (1 BvL 1/09, 2010):** The Constitutional Court held that the human dignity guarantee in Article 1(1) GG, combined with the social state principle in Article 20(1), establishes a fundamental right to a dignified subsistence minimum that evolves with societal conditions. While the case predated the internet access right, subsequent academic commentary and government policy explicitly extend this principle to internet connectivity as an essential component of modern existence.

- **BVerfG, Computer Grundrecht (1 BvR 370/07, 2008):** The Court recognized a new fundamental right to the "confidentiality and integrity of information technology systems" (Grundrecht auf Gewährleistung der Vertraulichkeit und Integrität informationstechnischer Systeme). While focused on state surveillance, this decision implicitly affirmed the constitutional significance of digital infrastructure access.

- **BSG, Internet Costs in Social Assistance (B 14 AS 6/18 R, 2020):** The Federal Social Court held that internet access costs are part of the standard benefit needs covered by SGB XII, confirming that welfare recipients have a legal entitlement to assistance covering internet connectivity.

- **VG Köln, BNetzA Broadband Obligation Enforcement (2024):** The Verwaltungsgericht Köln upheld the BNetzA's order requiring Deutsche Telekom to provide broadband service to a rural household in the Eifel region that could not obtain the minimum 10 Mbps download speed, confirming the justiciability and enforceability of the TKG § 157 right to adequate broadband.

## ECHR / EU Framework

### Convention Articles

- **Article 10 -- Freedom of Expression and Information:** The ECtHR has increasingly recognized that internet access is essential to the exercise of freedom of expression. In Yıldırım v. Turkey (2012, App. No. 3111/10), the Court held that blocking access to the entirety of Google Sites to prevent access to a single offensive page violated Article 10, finding that wholesale internet blocking "disregards the effects on Internet access which does not concern the illegal content." In Cengiz and Others v. Turkey (2015, App. No. 48226/10), the Court found that blanket blocking of YouTube violated Article 10.

- **Article 2 of Protocol No. 1 -- Right to Education:** As education increasingly depends on internet access, this provision creates a positive obligation on states to ensure that educational resources are accessible. The COVID-19 pandemic amplified this dimension, as the shift to online education highlighted that students without internet access were effectively denied their right to education.

- **Article 14 -- Prohibition of Discrimination (read with Article 10 or Protocol 1, Article 2):** Internet access disparities that disproportionately affect particular groups (rural populations, ethnic minorities, persons with disabilities) may engage Article 14 when read in conjunction with substantive Convention rights.

### EU Legislative Framework

- **European Electronic Communications Code (EECC, Directive (EU) 2018/1972):** Articles 84-92 establish the EU universal service framework. Article 84(1) requires member states to ensure that "adequate broadband internet access service" is available to all consumers at an affordable price. Article 84(2) specifies that adequacy is determined by national circumstances including the speed necessary for "a minimum set of services including those in Annex V" (email, search engines, basic e-government services, online education tools). Article 85 provides for affordable tariffs and Article 86 allows special tariffs for low-income or special needs consumers.

- **EU Charter of Fundamental Rights, Article 11 (Freedom of Expression and Information):** Corresponding to ECHR Article 10, this provision applies to EU institutions and member states when implementing EU law, providing an additional legal basis for internet access as a component of the right to receive and impart information.

- **European Declaration on Digital Rights and Principles (2023):** The European Parliament, Council, and Commission adopted this non-binding declaration committing to "universal access to the internet" as a core digital principle. Chapter II ("Solidarity and Inclusion") states that "everyone should have access to an affordable, high-speed digital connectivity wherever they are" and that "no one should be left offline."

- **Connecting Europe Facility (CEF) Digital Program:** Provides EU co-financing for gigabit network deployment, with a budget of EUR 2.07 billion for 2021-2027. Member states additionally invest through National Recovery and Resilience Plans, with broadband infrastructure receiving approximately EUR 22 billion in total EU recovery funding.

### Key Case Law

- **Yıldırım v. Turkey (ECtHR, 2012, App. No. 3111/10):** Found a violation of Article 10 where Turkey blocked all access to Google Sites based on a court order targeting a single page. The Court stated that "the Internet has now become one of the principal means by which individuals exercise their right to freedom of expression and information."

- **Cengiz and Others v. Turkey (ECtHR, 2015, App. No. 48226/10):** Found a violation of Article 10 in the blanket blocking of YouTube in Turkey. The Court emphasized that YouTube had become "an important platform for exercising the freedom of expression" and that blanket blocking was disproportionate.

- **Kharitonov v. Russia (ECtHR, 2020, App. No. 10795/14):** Found a violation of Article 10 where Russia's IP-based website blocking system resulted in collateral blocking of unrelated websites sharing the same IP address, demonstrating the Article 10 implications of technically imprecise internet restrictions.

- **OOO Flavus and Others v. Russia (ECtHR, 2020, App. No. 12468/15):** The Court found Article 10 violations in Russia's blocking of several websites without adequate procedural safeguards, reinforcing that internet restrictions require legal basis, legitimate aim, and proportionality.
