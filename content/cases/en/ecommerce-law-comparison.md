## Summary

E-commerce regulation (전자상거래 규제) encompasses the legal frameworks governing commercial transactions conducted through electronic means, including online retail, digital services, electronic payments, and cross-border digital trade. South Korea, one of the world's most digitally connected economies, regulates e-commerce primarily through the Act on Consumer Protection in Electronic Commerce (전자상거래 등에서의 소비자보호에 관한 법률), first enacted in 2002 and comprehensively amended multiple times, most recently in 2024 to address platform-mediated transactions, live commerce, and subscription-based services; the Electronic Commerce Basic Act (전자거래기본법) provides the foundational legal infrastructure for electronic transactions. The United States relies on a combination of federal statutes -- including the Electronic Signatures in Global and National Commerce Act (E-SIGN Act, 15 U.S.C. Sections 7001-7031), the Uniform Electronic Transactions Act (UETA) adopted by 49 states, and the Federal Trade Commission Act (15 U.S.C. Section 45) enforced against unfair or deceptive online practices -- supplemented by state consumer protection laws and sector-specific regulations. Germany regulates e-commerce through the implementation of EU directives, principally the Telemediengesetz (TMG, Telemedia Act) which was substantially reformed in 2024, the Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB) provisions on distance contracts (Fernabsatzverträge, Sections 312b-312h), and the comprehensive Digitale-Dienste-Gesetz (DDG, Digital Services Act Implementation) effective since May 2024. At the European Union level, the E-Commerce Directive 2000/31/EC established foundational principles for the internal market in electronic services, now substantially supplemented by the Digital Services Act (Regulation (EU) 2022/2065) and the Digital Markets Act (Regulation (EU) 2022/1925), while the Consumer Rights Directive 2011/83/EU harmonizes distance selling rules, and the General Data Protection Regulation constrains data processing in commercial contexts across all member states.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's e-commerce regulatory regime reflects the nation's position as a global leader in digital commerce, with internet penetration exceeding 97 percent and e-commerce sales comprising approximately 30 percent of total retail by 2025. The regulatory architecture rests on two primary statutes supplemented by sector-specific rules.

**Act on Consumer Protection in Electronic Commerce (전자상거래 등에서의 소비자보호에 관한 법률):** Originally enacted in 2002, this statute governs consumer-facing online transactions. It imposes information disclosure requirements on online sellers, establishes withdrawal (cooling-off) rights, regulates electronic payment methods, and provides enforcement mechanisms through the Korea Fair Trade Commission (KFTC, 공정거래위원회).

**Electronic Commerce Basic Act (전자거래기본법):** Enacted in 1999, this statute provides the legal foundation for electronic transactions, including the legal validity of electronic documents, electronic signatures, and automated transaction systems.

### 2024 Comprehensive Amendment

The Act on Consumer Protection in Electronic Commerce underwent a landmark amendment in 2024, driven by the explosive growth of platform-mediated commerce, live commerce streaming, and subscription-based digital services. Key reforms include:

**Platform Operator Obligations:** Online platform operators (온라인 플랫폼 사업자) are now subject to explicit statutory obligations, including the duty to verify seller identity, display transaction terms transparently, and maintain dispute resolution mechanisms. Platform operators bear joint and several liability with sellers for consumer damages arising from defective information disclosure.

**Live Commerce Regulation:** The amendment introduced specific provisions for live commerce (라이브커머스), requiring real-time disclosure of product information, seller identity, and return policies during live-streamed sales events.

**Subscription Economy Rules:** New provisions address subscription-based services, requiring clear disclosure of recurring charges, simplified cancellation procedures, and mandatory notification before automatic renewal.

### Consumer Withdrawal Rights

Korean law grants consumers a seven-day withdrawal period (청약철회) from the date of receipt of goods or from the date when product information becomes available, whichever is later. Exceptions include perishable goods, customized products, digital content where the seal has been broken, and goods whose value has been substantially diminished due to consumer use.

### Enforcement

The KFTC exercises primary enforcement authority, with powers to issue corrective orders, impose administrative fines (과징금) of up to 2 percent of relevant revenue, and refer criminal violations for prosecution. In 2024, the KFTC imposed approximately 45 billion KRW in e-commerce-related penalties, with a focus on platform transparency and misleading advertising.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States lacks a single comprehensive e-commerce statute, instead relying on a layered system of federal and state laws, regulatory guidance, and industry self-regulation.

**Electronic Signatures in Global and National Commerce Act (E-SIGN Act, 2000):** Enacted as 15 U.S.C. Sections 7001-7031, the E-SIGN Act establishes the legal equivalence of electronic signatures and records with their paper counterparts in interstate and foreign commerce. It provides that contracts, signatures, and records may not be denied legal effect solely because they are in electronic form.

**Uniform Electronic Transactions Act (UETA):** Promulgated by the Uniform Law Commission in 1999 and adopted by 49 states (all except New York, which has its own Electronic Signatures and Records Act), UETA provides a consistent state-level framework for electronic transactions, complementing the federal E-SIGN Act.

**Federal Trade Commission Act, Section 5 (15 U.S.C. Section 45):** The FTC's broad authority to prohibit "unfair or deceptive acts or practices" in commerce serves as the primary federal enforcement tool for e-commerce consumer protection. The FTC has applied Section 5 to online advertising disclosures, dark patterns, subscription traps, negative option marketing, and data security failures.

### FTC Enforcement Actions

The FTC has pursued aggressive enforcement against e-commerce violations:

| Action | Year | Subject | Outcome |
|--------|------|---------|---------|
| FTC v. Amazon.com (Prime subscription) | 2023 | Dark patterns in Prime enrollment and cancellation | Ongoing litigation; alleged billions in consumer harm |
| FTC Negative Option Rule (revised) | 2024 | Click-to-cancel requirements for subscriptions | Final rule effective 2025; requires simple cancellation |
| FTC v. Fortnite (Epic Games) | 2022 | Children's privacy and dark patterns | $520 million settlement |
| Telemarketing Sales Rule amendments | 2024 | Expanded to cover additional e-commerce practices | Updated disclosure requirements |

### State Consumer Protection

All 50 states maintain consumer protection statutes (often called "Little FTC Acts" or UDAP statutes) that apply to e-commerce transactions. California's Online Privacy Protection Act (CalOPPA, Business and Professions Code Sections 22575-22579) was the first state law requiring commercial websites to post a privacy policy. The California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA/CPRA) imposes comprehensive data protection obligations on businesses conducting e-commerce in California.

### No Federal Cooling-Off Period for Online Sales

Unlike South Korea and the EU, the United States does not mandate a general withdrawal period for online purchases. The FTC's Cooling-Off Rule (16 C.F.R. Part 429) applies only to door-to-door sales and certain off-premises transactions, not to online or mail-order purchases. Return policies remain a matter of individual retailer discretion, subject to state laws requiring disclosure of return policies.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's e-commerce regulation operates within the EU harmonization framework, implementing EU directives while maintaining distinctive national provisions.

**Telemediengesetz (TMG, Telemedia Act):** Originally enacted in 2007 to consolidate earlier legislation, the TMG governs information society services, including identification requirements for website operators (Impressumspflicht), liability provisions for intermediaries, and information obligations. The TMG was substantially amended by the Digitale-Dienste-Gesetz (DDG) in May 2024, which implemented the EU Digital Services Act.

**Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB) -- Distance Contracts:** Sections 312b through 312h BGB implement the EU Consumer Rights Directive's distance selling provisions, including the 14-day withdrawal right (Widerrufsrecht), pre-contractual information requirements, and form requirements for distance contracts.

### Withdrawal Right (Widerrufsrecht)

German law, implementing EU Directive 2011/83/EU, grants consumers a 14-day right of withdrawal from distance contracts without needing to give any reason. The withdrawal period begins upon receipt of goods or, for service contracts, from the date of contract conclusion. The trader must provide a model withdrawal form. Failure to properly inform the consumer of the withdrawal right extends the period to 12 months and 14 days.

### Button Solution (Button-Lösung)

Section 312j BGB implements the EU requirement that online orders involving payment obligations must include a clearly labeled confirmation button. The button must contain only the words "zahlungspflichtig bestellen" (order with obligation to pay) or equivalent unambiguous language. Orders placed without such a button are not binding on the consumer.

### Impressumspflicht (Identification Obligation)

Under Section 5 TMG (now DDG), every commercial website must display readily accessible information identifying the operator, including full legal name and address, contact details, commercial register entry, VAT identification number, and supervisory authority where applicable. Non-compliance is punishable by fines up to 50,000 euros and constitutes a competition law violation actionable by competitors under the Gesetz gegen den unlauteren Wettbewerb (UWG, Unfair Competition Act).

### Digital Content Contracts

Sections 327 through 327u BGB, implementing EU Directive 2019/770, establish specific rules for contracts involving digital content and digital services. These provisions impose conformity requirements on digital products, require updates to maintain conformity throughout a relevant period, and provide consumer remedies including repair, replacement, price reduction, and contract termination.

## ECHR & EU

### E-Commerce Directive 2000/31/EC

The foundational EU instrument for e-commerce, Directive 2000/31/EC on electronic commerce, established the internal market framework for information society services. Key principles include:

**Country of Origin Principle (Article 3):** Information society services are subject to the law of the member state in which the service provider is established, facilitating cross-border provision of services without requiring compliance with the laws of each receiving state.

**Information Requirements (Articles 5-6):** Service providers must make specified information easily accessible, including name, address, email, trade register details, and VAT number. Commercial communications must be clearly identifiable as such.

**Electronic Contracts (Articles 9-11):** Member states must ensure their legal systems allow contracts to be concluded by electronic means. Service providers must acknowledge receipt of orders electronically and provide technical means to identify and correct input errors.

**Intermediary Liability (Articles 12-15):** The Directive established conditional liability exemptions for mere conduit, caching, and hosting services, provided they meet specified conditions. Article 15 prohibited general monitoring obligations.

### Digital Services Act (Regulation (EU) 2022/2065)

The DSA, which became fully applicable on February 17, 2024, substantially reformed the intermediary liability framework established by the E-Commerce Directive. It introduces tiered due diligence obligations based on the size and nature of the platform:

**Very Large Online Platforms (VLOPs) and Very Large Online Search Engines (VLOSEs):** Platforms with more than 45 million monthly active users in the EU must conduct systemic risk assessments, undergo independent audits, provide data access to researchers, and appoint compliance officers. The Commission designated 19 VLOPs and 2 VLOSEs in April 2023.

**Transparency and Consumer Protection:** All online platforms must provide clear terms of service, establish complaint mechanisms, disclose advertising parameters, and design interfaces without dark patterns (Article 25).

**Enforcement:** The European Commission has direct enforcement authority over VLOPs and VLOSEs, with power to impose fines of up to 6 percent of global annual turnover.

### Digital Markets Act (Regulation (EU) 2022/1925)

The DMA, applicable since May 2, 2023, targets designated "gatekeeper" platforms that exercise significant influence over the digital marketplace. Gatekeepers must comply with obligations including interoperability requirements, data portability, prohibition on self-preferencing, and restrictions on combining personal data across services. Non-compliance penalties reach up to 10 percent of worldwide annual turnover.

### Consumer Rights Directive 2011/83/EU

This directive harmonized distance selling rules across the EU, establishing the 14-day withdrawal right, pre-contractual information requirements, delivery time limits (maximum 30 days unless agreed otherwise), and risk of loss rules for delivered goods.

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR's involvement in e-commerce regulation is primarily indirect, through the protection of expression (Article 10) and privacy (Article 8) in the digital commercial sphere. In Delfi AS v. Estonia (Application No. 64569/09, Grand Chamber Judgment of June 16, 2015), the Court held that requiring a commercial news portal to remove defamatory user comments did not violate Article 10, recognizing states' latitude in balancing commercial expression with personality rights in the online environment.

## Comparative Analysis

### Regulatory Philosophy

The four jurisdictions exhibit distinct regulatory philosophies. South Korea employs a prescriptive, statute-driven approach with detailed behavioral rules enforced by the KFTC. The United States relies on a principles-based approach through the FTC's broad "unfair or deceptive" standard, supplemented by sector-specific rules. Germany and the EU combine harmonized directive-based regulation with detailed implementing legislation, creating a layered system that seeks to balance market integration with consumer protection.

### Consumer Withdrawal Rights

The most significant divergence concerns cooling-off periods: the EU and its member states (including Germany) mandate a 14-day withdrawal right; South Korea provides 7 days; and the United States imposes no general federal withdrawal period for e-commerce transactions. This reflects fundamentally different views on the appropriate balance between consumer protection and freedom of contract.

### Platform Regulation

The regulation of online platforms represents the fastest-evolving area of e-commerce law. The EU's DSA and DMA represent the most comprehensive approach, imposing tiered obligations based on platform size and market power. South Korea's 2024 amendments introduced joint liability for platform operators. The United States continues to rely primarily on Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act, which broadly shields platforms from liability for user-generated content, though legislative reform proposals are pending.

### Cross-Border Commerce

E-commerce is inherently cross-border, creating jurisdictional challenges. The EU internal market framework is the most developed, combining country-of-origin principles with harmonized consumer protection rules. South Korea and the United States address cross-border issues primarily through enforcement cooperation agreements and mutual recognition arrangements rather than harmonized substantive law.
