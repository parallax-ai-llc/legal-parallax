## Summary

Medical debt -- financial obligations arising from healthcare services that patients are unable to pay -- represents a uniquely acute legal and social problem that varies dramatically in severity across jurisdictions depending on the design of their healthcare financing systems. The four jurisdictions examined here illustrate profoundly different approaches ranging from systems where medical debt is virtually nonexistent as a social phenomenon to the United States, where it constitutes the leading cause of personal bankruptcy. South Korea operates a universal National Health Insurance (NHI) system under the National Health Insurance Act (국민건강보험법) that covers all residents, but relatively high out-of-pocket costs (approximately 33% of total health expenditure) combined with limited catastrophic expense protections create significant medical debt burdens, addressed in part by the Medical Aid Program (의료급여) and the Ceiling System for Out-of-Pocket Expenses (본인부담상한제). The United States is the only developed nation without universal health coverage, and medical debt affects an estimated 100 million Americans; the legal framework involves federal protections under the No Surprises Act (Pub. L. 116-260, 2021), Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (15 U.S.C. Section 1692 et seq.), and the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau's 2023 rule removing medical debt from credit reports, alongside a patchwork of state medical debt protection laws. Germany's statutory health insurance system (gesetzliche Krankenversicherung, GKV) under SGB V covers approximately 90% of the population with co-payments capped at 2% of gross annual income (1% for chronically ill patients), making medical debt a marginal phenomenon. The ECHR framework protects access to healthcare under Articles 2, 3, and 8, while EU law addresses cross-border healthcare costs through Directive 2011/24/EU and Regulation 883/2004, and the European Pillar of Social Rights declares healthcare a fundamental entitlement.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's healthcare system is built on the **National Health Insurance Act** (국민건강보험법, NHIA), which establishes mandatory universal health insurance covering all residents. Despite this universal coverage, medical debt remains a significant problem due to structural features of the system.

### National Health Insurance System

The NHI, administered by the **National Health Insurance Service (NHIS, 국민건강보험공단)**, covers approximately 97% of the population, with the remaining 3% covered by the **Medical Aid Program** (의료급여제도) for low-income residents. Key features:

- **Coverage Scope:** The NHI covers a defined benefit package including physician consultations, hospitalization, prescription medications, and diagnostic procedures. However, many services are classified as "non-covered" (비급여), including advanced diagnostic imaging, premium hospital room charges, and certain specialized treatments.
- **Cost-Sharing:** Patients pay co-payments ranging from 20% (hospitalization) to 30-60% (outpatient visits, depending on facility type). For non-covered services, patients bear 100% of costs.

### Out-of-Pocket Cost Structure

South Korea's out-of-pocket spending as a share of total health expenditure was approximately 33% in 2023, significantly higher than the OECD average of 20%. This creates vulnerability to medical debt:

| Expense Category | Patient Cost Share | NHI Coverage |
|-----------------|-------------------|--------------|
| Inpatient (tertiary hospital) | 20% of covered costs | 80% |
| Outpatient (general hospital) | 40% of covered costs | 60% |
| Outpatient (clinic) | 30% of covered costs | 70% |
| Prescription drugs | 30-50% depending on drug | 50-70% |
| Non-covered services | 100% | 0% |
| Advanced imaging (MRI, PET-CT) | Varies; partially covered since 2018 | Partial |

### Ceiling System for Out-of-Pocket Expenses (본인부담상한제)

Article 44 of the NHIA establishes a ceiling on annual out-of-pocket expenses, adjusted by income decile:

| Income Decile | Annual OOP Cap (KRW, 2025) | Approximate USD |
|---------------|---------------------------|-----------------|
| 1st (lowest) | 810,000 | $600 |
| 2nd-3rd | 1,000,000 | $750 |
| 4th-5th | 1,500,000 | $1,125 |
| 6th-7th | 2,400,000 | $1,800 |
| 8th | 3,600,000 | $2,700 |
| 9th | 4,400,000 | $3,300 |
| 10th (highest) | 5,800,000 | $4,350 |

Excess amounts are reimbursed by the NHIS. However, the ceiling applies only to covered services; non-covered service costs are excluded, creating a significant gap.

### Catastrophic Health Expenditure

The **Catastrophic Health Expenditure Support Program** (재난적의료비 지원사업), expanded in 2018, provides financial assistance to households where medical expenses exceed a specified threshold of household income. Eligible households receive support for both covered and non-covered expenses up to KRW 20 million per year.

Despite these protections, a 2023 Korea Health Panel Survey found that approximately 12% of Korean households experienced medical debt, with the average outstanding medical debt among affected households being KRW 4.8 million (approximately $3,600). Cancer treatment, advanced diagnostic procedures, and dental care were the most common sources of medical debt.

### Debt Collection for Medical Expenses

Medical debt collection in South Korea is governed by the **Civil Execution Act** (민사집행법):

- **Wage Garnishment:** Article 246 exempts wages up to 150% of the minimum cost of living (최저생계비) from garnishment. For a single-person household in 2025, this exempts approximately KRW 2.3 million per month.
- **Property Seizure:** Essential household goods, tools of the trade, and social welfare benefits are exempt from seizure.
- **Hospital Liens:** Hospitals may not refuse emergency treatment due to inability to pay under the Emergency Medical Service Act (응급의료에 관한 법률, Article 6).

### Medical Aid Program (의료급여)

The Medical Aid Program, governed by the **Medical Aid Act** (의료급여법), provides coverage for approximately 1.5 million low-income residents, including Basic Livelihood Security recipients, homeless individuals, and disaster victims. Type 1 beneficiaries pay no cost-sharing for most services; Type 2 beneficiaries pay reduced co-payments (10% for inpatient, KRW 1,000-1,500 for outpatient).

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States stands alone among developed nations in the severity of its medical debt crisis. An estimated 100 million Americans -- approximately 41% of adults -- carry some form of medical debt, and medical bills are the leading cause of personal bankruptcy filings, cited in approximately 66% of all bankruptcy cases (American Journal of Public Health, 2019).

### The Scale of the Problem

The unique severity of medical debt in the United States stems from structural features of the healthcare system:

- **No Universal Coverage:** Approximately 27 million Americans (8.3% of the population) were uninsured in 2024 (Census Bureau).
- **High Deductibles:** Among employer-sponsored plans, the average individual deductible was $1,735 in 2024 (KFF Employer Health Benefits Survey). High-deductible health plans (HDHPs) with deductibles of $3,000+ cover a growing share of workers.
- **Out-of-Network Billing:** Before the No Surprises Act, patients routinely received surprise bills from out-of-network providers, particularly anesthesiologists, emergency physicians, and pathologists practicing at in-network facilities.
- **Price Opacity:** Hospital chargemaster prices bear little relationship to actual costs, and patients historically had no ability to ascertain prices before receiving care.

### No Surprises Act (Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2021, Pub. L. 116-260, Division BB)

Effective January 1, 2022, the No Surprises Act established the most significant federal protection against medical debt:

- **Emergency Services (Section 2799A-1):** Patients may not be balance-billed for emergency services, regardless of whether the provider or facility is in-network. Cost-sharing is calculated at in-network rates.
- **Non-Emergency Out-of-Network Services at In-Network Facilities (Section 2799A-2):** Patients may not be balance-billed by out-of-network providers at in-network facilities (e.g., an out-of-network anesthesiologist at an in-network hospital) unless the provider gives notice 72 hours before the service and obtains written consent.
- **Independent Dispute Resolution (IDR, Section 2799A-1(c)):** Disputed payment amounts between providers and insurers are resolved through a federal arbitration process. In **Texas Medical Association v. HHS** (No. 6:22-cv-372, E.D. Tex., 2023), the court struck down portions of the IDR rule that created a presumption in favor of the qualifying payment amount (QPA).
- **Good Faith Estimates (Section 2799B-6):** Uninsured or self-pay patients have the right to receive a good faith estimate of charges before receiving scheduled services. If the actual charges exceed the estimate by more than $400, the patient may initiate a dispute resolution process.

### Hospital Price Transparency Rule (45 CFR Section 180)

Effective January 1, 2021, CMS requires all hospitals to make public:

- A machine-readable file of all standard charges (gross charges, discounted cash prices, payer-specific negotiated charges, and de-identified minimum and maximum negotiated charges) for all items and services.
- A consumer-friendly display of shoppable services showing the charges for at least 300 common services.

Compliance has been limited: a 2024 JAMA study found that only 36% of hospitals were fully compliant. CMS increased penalties in 2022 to a maximum of $5,500 per day for non-compliance (adjusted by bed count).

### Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA, 15 U.S.C. Section 1692 et seq.)

The FDCPA governs third-party medical debt collection:

- Collectors must send a written validation notice within five days of initial contact.
- Collectors may not contact debtors before 8 a.m. or after 9 p.m.
- Collectors may not use threats, harassment, or deceptive practices.
- Debtors have 30 days to dispute the debt in writing after receiving the validation notice.

### CFPB Medical Debt Credit Reporting Rule (2023-2025)

In September 2023, the **Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB)** proposed a rule to prohibit medical debt from appearing on consumer credit reports. Key developments:

- The three major credit bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion) voluntarily removed medical debts under $500 from credit reports in 2023.
- In January 2025, the CFPB finalized a rule prohibiting creditors from using medical debt information in credit decisions under the Equal Credit Opportunity Act and the Fair Credit Reporting Act. The rule was projected to remove approximately $49 billion in medical debt from the credit system, affecting approximately 15 million Americans.
- The rule faced immediate legal challenges from the debt collection industry, with ACA International v. CFPB (filed February 2025) seeking to vacate the rule.

### State Protections

States have increasingly enacted medical debt protection laws:

- **Colorado (Fair Health Care Billing Act, 2024):** Prohibits medical debt collection during the first 60 days after billing; limits interest on medical debt to 3%; requires hospitals to screen uninsured patients for financial assistance eligibility.
- **California (SB 1061, 2022):** Prohibits hospitals from reporting medical debt to credit agencies or selling medical debt if the patient is eligible for charity care or financial assistance.
- **New York (Medical Debt Protection Act, 2024):** Exempts primary residences from liens for medical debt; prohibits wage garnishment for medical debts under $2,000.
- **Minnesota (Health Care Affordability Act, 2023):** Requires hospitals to provide free care to patients with incomes below 300% of the federal poverty level.

### Bankruptcy and Medical Debt

Medical debt is dischargeable in both **Chapter 7** (liquidation) and **Chapter 13** (reorganization) bankruptcy under 11 U.S.C. Sections 727 and 1328. Unlike student loans, medical debt does not require a showing of "undue hardship" for discharge. The **Bankruptcy Abuse Prevention and Consumer Protection Act of 2005** (BAPCPA) imposed a means test for Chapter 7 eligibility, but medical expenses are deductible in the means test calculation.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's system of statutory health insurance (GKV) effectively prevents the accumulation of significant medical debt for the vast majority of the population. The legal framework is rooted in **Book V of the Social Code (Sozialgesetzbuch V, SGB V)**, which governs statutory health insurance.

### Universal Coverage

Germany maintains mandatory health insurance coverage for all residents:

- **Statutory Health Insurance (GKV):** Covers approximately 90% of the population. Membership is mandatory for employees with gross annual income below the insurance threshold (Versicherungspflichtgrenze, EUR 69,300 in 2025). Contributions are income-based (average total rate: 15.9% of gross wages, split between employer and employee).
- **Private Health Insurance (PKV):** Covers approximately 10% of the population, including self-employed individuals, civil servants, and employees above the income threshold who opt out of GKV. PKV operates on a reimbursement model, creating a potential (but limited) gap for medical debt.

### Co-Payment Structure and Cap (SGB V Section 62)

GKV co-payments (Zuzahlungen) are strictly limited:

- **Prescription Drugs:** EUR 5-10 per prescription, depending on pack price (Section 61 SGB V).
- **Hospital Stay:** EUR 10 per day for a maximum of 28 days per year (Section 39(4) SGB V).
- **Medical Devices:** 10% of cost, minimum EUR 5, maximum EUR 10 per item (Section 33 SGB V).
- **Dental Prosthetics:** Patient pays a share varying from 0-50% depending on Bonus-Heft (documentation of regular dental check-ups) and financial status.

**Section 62 SGB V -- Hardship Exemption (Zuzahlungsbefreiung):** Co-payments are capped at **2% of gross household income per calendar year** (reduced to **1%** for persons with a serious chronic condition as defined by the Chronic Illness Guideline (Chroniker-Richtlinie)). Once the cap is reached, the insured person receives an exemption certificate (Befreiungsausweis) for the remainder of the year.

| Household Gross Income | Annual Co-Payment Cap | Chronic Illness Cap |
|------------------------|-----------------------|---------------------|
| EUR 20,000 | EUR 400 | EUR 200 |
| EUR 40,000 | EUR 800 | EUR 400 |
| EUR 60,000 | EUR 1,200 | EUR 600 |

### Medical Debt in Germany

Medical debt is a marginal phenomenon in Germany due to the universal coverage and co-payment cap structure. However, limited situations exist:

- **Uninsured Residents:** While health insurance is legally mandatory since 2009 (Versicherungspflicht under Section 193 VVG for PKV, Section 5(1) SGB V for GKV), some individuals fall through gaps -- estimated at 60,000-80,000 persons, primarily undocumented immigrants and homeless individuals. The Gesundheitsaemter (public health offices) provide emergency care.
- **PKV Patients:** Private insurance operates on a reimbursement model (Kostenerstattungsprinzip). Patients pay providers directly and seek reimbursement from their insurer. If reimbursement is denied or partial, the patient bears the difference. This creates a limited medical debt risk for PKV members.
- **Non-Covered Services (Individuelle Gesundheitsleistungen, IGeL):** Elective services not covered by GKV (e.g., certain preventive screenings, cosmetic procedures) are paid out-of-pocket. Providers must obtain written consent and provide cost information before performing IGeL services (Section 630c BGB).

### Debt Collection and Insolvency

For the limited medical debt that arises:

- **Zivilprozessordnung (ZPO), Sections 850-850k:** Establishes wage garnishment exemptions. The debtor's wage is protected up to the **garnishment-free amount** (Pfaendungsfreigrenze), currently EUR 1,402.28/month for a single person, increasing with dependents.
- **Social Welfare Exemption:** Social welfare benefits (Sozialleistungen under SGB II and SGB XII) are completely exempt from garnishment.
- **Insolvenzordnung (InsO):** The 2020 reform reduced the good-conduct period for consumer insolvency from six years to **three years**, after which remaining debts (including medical debt) are discharged (Restschuldbefreiung, Section 287 InsO).

### Hospital Funding and Billing

German hospitals are predominantly financed through a **Diagnosis-Related Group (DRG)** system for inpatient care, with the GKV paying hospitals directly. Patients receive no bill for covered services beyond the EUR 10/day co-payment. This structural feature eliminates the surprise billing problem that plagues the U.S. system.

## ECHR & EU

### European Convention on Human Rights

The ECHR addresses medical debt indirectly through the right to healthcare and dignity:

**Article 2 (Right to Life):** The positive obligation to protect life includes ensuring access to emergency medical care. In **Cyprus v. Turkey** (Application No. 25781/94, Grand Chamber, May 10, 2001), the Court found a violation of Article 2 based on the failure to provide adequate medical treatment. A healthcare system that conditions emergency treatment on ability to pay could engage Article 2.

**Article 3 (Prohibition of Inhuman or Degrading Treatment):** Denial of necessary medical treatment that causes severe suffering may constitute a violation. In **N. v. United Kingdom** (Application No. 26565/05, Grand Chamber, May 27, 2008), the Court addressed whether deportation to a country without adequate healthcare violated Article 3. While the threshold is high, a state that permits aggressive medical debt collection causing severe psychological distress could face Article 3 scrutiny.

**Article 8 (Right to Respect for Private Life):** Encompasses the right to physical and psychological integrity. Medical debt that prevents access to follow-up care, or debt collection practices that expose sensitive medical information, may engage Article 8. In **Bensaid v. United Kingdom** (Application No. 44599/98, February 6, 2001), the Court recognized that the right to mental health falls within Article 8's scope.

### European Social Charter

The **European Social Charter** (Revised, 1996) provides more direct protections:

- **Article 11 (Right to Protection of Health):** Requires states to take measures to remove causes of ill-health and ensure access to healthcare.
- **Article 13 (Right to Social and Medical Assistance):** Requires states to ensure that any person without adequate resources be granted adequate assistance in case of sickness.
- **Article 30 (Right to Protection Against Poverty and Social Exclusion):** Requires states to take measures to promote effective access to healthcare for persons living in or at risk of social exclusion.

The **European Committee of Social Rights** has found violations of these provisions in several cases involving inadequate healthcare access, including **International Federation of Human Rights Leagues (FIDH) v. France** (Complaint No. 14/2003), concerning healthcare access for undocumented migrants.

### EU Framework

**Directive 2011/24/EU (Cross-Border Healthcare):** Requires member states to provide transparent pricing information (Article 4(2)(b)) and to ensure that complaints procedures and mechanisms for seeking remedies exist for patients who suffer harm (Article 4(2)(c)).

**Regulation 883/2004 (Social Security Coordination):** Coordinates healthcare entitlements for persons moving between member states, ensuring that insured persons can access necessary healthcare in other member states without facing disproportionate costs.

**European Pillar of Social Rights (Principle 16):** Declares that "everyone has the right to timely access to affordable, preventive and curative health care of good quality." While not legally binding, it establishes a political commitment to healthcare affordability across the EU.

## Comparative Analysis

### Structural Determinants of Medical Debt

The comparison reveals that medical debt is primarily a function of healthcare system design rather than legal protections:

- **Germany** virtually eliminates medical debt through universal coverage, the 2% income cap on co-payments, and direct hospital payment.
- **South Korea** generates moderate medical debt despite universal coverage due to high out-of-pocket costs (33% of expenditure) and extensive non-covered service categories.
- **The United States** produces severe medical debt through the combination of incomplete coverage, high deductibles, price opacity, and out-of-network billing.
- **The EU/ECHR** framework establishes aspirational standards but lacks enforcement mechanisms to prevent medical debt at the member-state level.

### Legal Protections: Prevention vs. Mitigation

The jurisdictions differ in whether their legal frameworks primarily prevent medical debt or mitigate its consequences after accumulation:

- Germany focuses on prevention through the co-payment cap structure.
- South Korea provides both prevention (ceiling system) and mitigation (catastrophic health expenditure support).
- The United States primarily focuses on mitigation through debt collection regulation, credit reporting restrictions, and bankruptcy discharge -- addressing the consequences rather than the causes.
- The ECHR framework emphasizes structural obligations to ensure healthcare access, representing a preventive approach at the constitutional level.

### Credit Reporting and Financial Consequences

The impact of medical debt on financial status varies dramatically:

- In Germany, medical debt (if it arises) is subject to the same rules as other consumer debt; the SCHUFA credit system does not separately categorize medical debt.
- In South Korea, medical debt is reported to the Korea Credit Information Services but is not separately identified.
- In the United States, the CFPB's 2025 rule represents a major shift toward recognizing that medical debt is involuntary and should not bear the same credit consequences as commercial debt.
