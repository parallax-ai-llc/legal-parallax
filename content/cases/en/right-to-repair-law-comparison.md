## Summary

Right to repair legislation addresses the ability of consumers and independent repair providers to access the parts, tools, documentation, and software necessary to repair products they own, spanning sectors from consumer electronics and appliances to agricultural equipment and medical devices. South Korea has taken an indirect approach to repair rights, relying on existing consumer protection statutes, warranty frameworks under the Consumer Fundamental Act, and the Fair Trade Commission's oversight of anticompetitive repair restrictions, without enacting a standalone right to repair law. The United States has seen rapid legislative momentum at the state level, with California's SB 244 (Digital Fair Repair Act, effective July 2024), Oregon's landmark anti-parts-pairing law (effective January 2025), and similar statutes in New York, Minnesota, and Colorado, complemented by federal enforcement through the FTC's antitrust actions targeting manufacturers who restrict independent repair. Germany, as an EU Member State, must transpose Directive (EU) 2024/1799 (the EU Right to Repair Directive) into national law by 31 July 2026, extending repair obligations beyond the legal guarantee period and building on existing Ecodesign Regulation requirements for spare parts availability. The EU framework, anchored by the Right to Repair Directive and the Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation (EU) 2024/1781, represents the most comprehensive approach globally, mandating manufacturer repair obligations, standardized repair information, and an online platform to connect consumers with repair services. Countries including Australia, Japan, India, and Brazil are at varying stages of developing their own repair rights frameworks, reflecting a global trend toward empowering consumers and independent repair providers.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea does not have a standalone right to repair statute. Instead, repair-related rights are derived from several overlapping consumer protection and competition laws.

| Statute | Relevance to Repair Rights |
|---------|---------------------------|
| Consumer Fundamental Act (소비자기본법) | Establishes consumers' basic right to safety, information, and choice; mandates after-sales service standards |
| Fair Trade Act (공정거래법) | Prohibits anticompetitive practices including tying arrangements that restrict independent repair |
| Act on Fair Labeling and Advertising (표시광고법) | Requires accurate product information that may include repairability information |
| Framework Act on Resources Circulation (자원순환기본법) | Promotes resource circulation including product longevity and repairability |
| Electrical Appliances and Consumer Products Safety Control Act | Sets safety standards for electronic products including requirements for repair components |

**After-Sales Service Standards:** The Korea Consumer Agency (KCA) publishes compensation standards for consumer disputes (소비자분쟁해결기준) that specify mandatory after-sales service periods for major product categories. Manufacturers must provide repair parts for designated periods after discontinuation of production:

| Product Category | Mandatory Parts Availability Period |
|-----------------|-------------------------------------|
| Refrigerators | 7 years after production discontinuation |
| Washing machines | 6 years after production discontinuation |
| Air conditioners | 7 years after production discontinuation |
| Televisions | 7 years after production discontinuation |
| Mobile phones | 3-4 years after production discontinuation |
| Personal computers | 4 years after production discontinuation |

### Key Principles

**Fair Trade Commission Enforcement:** The Korea Fair Trade Commission (KFTC) has the authority to investigate and penalize manufacturers who restrict independent repair through anticompetitive practices such as tying repair services to original parts, refusing to supply spare parts to independent repair shops, or voiding warranties for third-party repairs. However, enforcement actions specifically targeting repair restrictions have been limited.

**Samsung and LG Repair Ecosystems:** South Korea's two dominant electronics manufacturers, Samsung Electronics and LG Electronics, maintain extensive authorized repair networks domestically while gradually expanding parts availability to independent providers in other markets. Samsung's Self-Repair program, launched in 2022 in partnership with iFixit in the United States, has not been fully implemented in the Korean domestic market, creating an asymmetry where Korean consumers have fewer self-repair options than their American counterparts.

**Agricultural Equipment:** South Korea's agricultural machinery sector, dominated by domestic manufacturers such as Daedong (KIOTI) and TYM, has faced fewer repair restriction controversies than the United States, partly due to smaller-scale farming operations and established dealer networks.

### Notable Cases

**KFTC v. Apple Korea (2016):** The KFTC fined Apple Korea approximately 2.73 billion won (approximately $2.3 million) for unfair business practices related to repair policies, including requiring Korean telecommunications carriers to bear repair costs and restricting consumers' ability to seek independent repairs. This represented one of the earliest regulatory actions in the Asia-Pacific region addressing manufacturer repair restrictions.

**KCA Consumer Dispute Resolution Rulings:** The Korea Consumer Agency has issued numerous mediation rulings requiring manufacturers to provide replacement products or full refunds when repair parts were unavailable within the mandated after-sales service period, establishing a body of quasi-judicial precedent supporting consumers' right to functional repair options.

**Hyundai/Kia Automotive Repair Access (2023):** Consumer advocacy groups raised concerns about Hyundai Motor Group's increasing use of software-dependent systems in vehicles that limited independent repair access. While South Korea's Motor Vehicle Management Act requires manufacturers to provide technical information for safety-related repairs, the expansion of software-locked systems in electric vehicles and advanced driver-assistance systems (ADAS) has created new barriers to independent automotive repair that the existing regulatory framework was not designed to address.

**E-Waste and Circular Economy Policy:** South Korea's Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) system under the Framework Act on Resources Circulation requires manufacturers to collect and recycle a certain percentage of products sold. Advocates have argued that right to repair provisions would complement EPR obligations by extending product lifespans and reducing waste generation, with estimates suggesting that improved repairability could reduce electronic waste by 15-20% annually.

## United States

### Federal Framework

The United States lacks a comprehensive federal right to repair statute, but federal agencies and state legislatures have increasingly addressed repair restrictions through competition law, warranty regulation, and dedicated repair legislation.

| Federal Authority / Statute | Relevance to Repair |
|----------------------------|---------------------|
| FTC Act (15 U.S.C. sections 41-58) | Authority to prosecute unfair and deceptive practices restricting repair |
| Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act (15 U.S.C. sections 2301-2312) | Prohibits conditioning warranty on use of authorized repair services or branded parts |
| Executive Order 14036 (July 2021) | Directed FTC and USDA to address repair restrictions across sectors |
| Sherman Antitrust Act (15 U.S.C. sections 1-7) | Prohibits monopolistic practices including those restricting repair markets |
| Copyright Act section 1201 (DMCA) | Anti-circumvention provisions can restrict software-related repairs; Librarian of Congress has granted limited repair exemptions |

**FTC Enforcement:** The FTC has taken significant enforcement actions:

- In May 2021, the FTC issued a report, "Nixing the Fix," documenting how manufacturers restrict repair through design choices, limited parts availability, repair restrictions in End User License Agreements, and application of copyright law against independent repair.
- In July 2021, the FTC voted unanimously to ramp up enforcement against illegal repair restrictions.
- In 2024, the FTC, joined by the attorneys general of Illinois and Minnesota, filed a federal antitrust complaint against John Deere (Deere & Company) in the U.S. District Court for the Northern District of Illinois, alleging that the company monopolized repair services for its agricultural equipment by imposing software locks and restricting access to diagnostic tools.

**DMCA Exemptions:** The Copyright Office has granted triennial exemptions under section 1201 of the DMCA for circumventing technological protection measures (TPMs) for the purpose of diagnosis, maintenance, or repair of certain classes of devices, including motorized land vehicles, smartphones, home appliances, and medical devices.

### State Variations

As of 2025, over 20 states have enacted or are considering right to repair legislation. Key enacted laws include:

| State | Law | Effective Date | Key Provisions |
|-------|-----|---------------|----------------|
| **New York** | Digital Fair Repair Act (S.4104) | July 1, 2023 | OEMs must make parts, tools, and documentation available; limited to devices manufactured after July 2023 |
| **California** | SB 244 (Right to Repair Act) | July 1, 2024 | Parts, tools, and documentation for products over $50; 7-year availability for products over $100; penalties up to $5,000/day |
| **Minnesota** | Digital Fair Repair Act (HF 1337) | July 1, 2024 | Broad coverage including agricultural equipment; independent repair provider access |
| **Oregon** | SB 1596 | January 1, 2025 | First state to ban parts pairing; covers electronics sold since 2015; enforcement from 2027 |
| **Colorado** | HB 24-1121 (Consumer Right to Repair) | January 1, 2025 | Covers powered wheelchairs and agricultural equipment in addition to electronics |

**California SB 244 Details:** California's law requires electronics and appliance manufacturers to provide parts, tools, software, and documentation to consumers and independent repair providers on fair and reasonable terms. For products costing more than $100, these resources must be available for up to seven years after the device is first placed on the market. For products between $50 and $99.99, resources must be available for three years. Violations are enforceable by the Attorney General with penalties of $1,000 per day for the first violation, $2,000 per day for the second, and $5,000 per day for subsequent violations.

**Oregon Parts Pairing Ban:** Oregon's law is notable for being the first U.S. state to prohibit parts pairing, the practice of using software to restrict the functionality of replacement parts not sourced from the original manufacturer. The ban applies to covered devices manufactured after January 1, 2025.

### Notable Cases

**FTC v. John Deere (2024):** The FTC's antitrust complaint against Deere & Company alleged that John Deere illegally monopolized repair services for its agricultural equipment by requiring farmers and independent repair shops to use dealer-only diagnostic software to complete many repairs, even when the physical repair itself could be performed independently. This case represents the most significant federal enforcement action in the right to repair space.

**FTC v. Harley-Davidson, Westinghouse, and Weber (2022):** The FTC issued orders against three companies for illegally conditioning warranties on the use of authorized repair services or branded parts, in violation of the Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act, putting the industry on notice regarding warranty-voiding repair restrictions.

***Apple Inc. v. Psystar Corp.* (658 F.3d 1150, 9th Cir. 2011):** While predating the modern right to repair movement, this case established that manufacturer use of DMCA section 1201 to prevent hardware modification could prevail over repair-related defenses, illustrating the tension between intellectual property protection and repair rights that continues to shape the legal landscape.

**Medical Device Right to Repair:** The Critical Medical Infrastructure Right-to-Repair Act (proposed federal legislation) would require medical device manufacturers to provide hospitals and independent repair organizations with access to service manuals, diagnostic tools, and replacement parts. The COVID-19 pandemic highlighted the urgency of this issue, as hospitals faced critical delays in repairing ventilators and other essential equipment when manufacturer-authorized technicians were unavailable.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's right to repair framework derives primarily from EU law, complemented by domestic consumer protection and competition statutes.

| Legal Source | Relevance |
|-------------|-----------|
| Directive (EU) 2024/1799 (Right to Repair Directive) | Must be transposed by 31 July 2026; extends repair obligations beyond warranty period |
| Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation (EU) 2024/1781 | Sets repairability and spare parts requirements for product categories |
| BGB sections 437-442 (Buergerliches Gesetzbuch) | Warranty rights including repair as first remedy for defective goods |
| GWB (Gesetz gegen Wettbewerbsbeschraenkungen) | Competition law prohibiting anticompetitive repair restrictions |
| ElektroG (Elektro- und Elektronikgeraetegesetz) | Implements WEEE Directive; waste reduction through extended product life |
| ProdHaftG (Produkthaftungsgesetz) | Product liability considerations for repairs |

**EU Right to Repair Directive (2024/1799):** Adopted in June 2024 and entering into force on 30 July 2024, the Directive requires Member States to transpose its provisions by 31 July 2026. Key requirements include:

- **Post-Warranty Repair Obligation:** Manufacturers must repair products covered by EU product legislation (initially household appliances, consumer electronics, and future product categories) even after the legal guarantee period has expired, at a reasonable price.
- **Repair Information Form:** Before committing to a repair, consumers have the right to receive a standardized European Repair Information Form detailing the conditions and price of repair.
- **Prohibition of Anti-Repair Practices:** Manufacturers may not use contractual, hardware, or software techniques to impede independent repair, prohibit the use of compatible spare parts, or refuse to provide repair services for economic reasons alone.
- **Online Repair Platform:** Member States must establish national online platforms connecting consumers with local repair services, refurbished goods sellers, and community repair initiatives.

### Key Principles

**Warranty Hierarchy:** Under BGB section 439, consumers have the right to demand supplementary performance (Nacherfuellung) for defective products, with repair (Nachbesserung) as one of two options alongside replacement. The seller may refuse the chosen form of supplementary performance only if it would impose disproportionate costs. The statutory warranty period is two years from delivery under section 438(1) BGB, aligned with the EU Sale of Goods Directive.

**Ecodesign Spare Parts Requirements:** Under the existing Ecodesign Directive (2009/125/EC) and its successor Regulation (EU) 2024/1781, Germany has implemented spare parts availability requirements for specific product categories:

| Product Category | Spare Parts Availability | Delivery Timeframe |
|-----------------|-------------------------|-------------------|
| Washing machines | 10 years after last unit placed on market | 15 working days |
| Dishwashers | 10 years | 15 working days |
| Refrigerators | 7-10 years | 15 working days |
| Electronic displays | 7 years | 15 working days |
| Welding equipment | 10 years | 15 working days |
| Smartphones and tablets | 7 years (from 2025 under new Ecodesign Regulation) | 5-10 working days |

**Planned Obsolescence Debate:** Germany's consumer protection organizations, particularly the Verbraucherzentrale Bundesverband (vzbv), have advocated for stronger measures against planned obsolescence, including mandatory product lifetime labeling, extended minimum warranty periods, and a "repairability index" modeled on France's indice de reparabilite, which has been mandatory since January 2021.

### Notable Cases

**BGH Ruling on Printer Cartridge DRM (BGH, I ZR 23/19, 2022):** The Federal Court of Justice addressed the use of digital rights management to prevent the use of third-party printer cartridges, finding that such practices may constitute unfair commercial practices under the UWG (Unfair Competition Act) when consumers are not adequately informed prior to purchase.

**Bundeskartellamt Investigation of Agricultural Equipment Manufacturers (2023-2024):** The Federal Cartel Office investigated practices by agricultural equipment manufacturers restricting access to diagnostic software and repair tools, signaling increased competition law scrutiny of repair restrictions in the agricultural sector.

**Repair Cafe Movement in Germany:** Germany hosts over 1,000 community repair cafes (Reparatur-Cafes), volunteer-operated workshops where citizens bring broken items for free guided repair. The repair cafe movement has been a significant force in German civil society advocacy for right to repair legislation, and the EU Right to Repair Directive's requirement for national online repair platforms was influenced in part by the German and Austrian repair cafe networks.

**German Automotive Sector:** The German Association of the Automotive Industry (VDA) has engaged extensively with EU right to repair discussions, as the automotive aftermarket represents a significant economic sector. The EU Type Approval Regulation (EU) 2018/858 already requires vehicle manufacturers to provide independent operators with access to vehicle repair and maintenance information, and the Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation may extend similar requirements to battery-electric vehicles and their high-voltage components.

## ECHR / EU Framework

### Convention Articles

The ECHR does not directly address right to repair. However, several Convention rights provide an indirect framework:

- **Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (Protection of Property):** The right to peaceful enjoyment of possessions includes the ability to use, maintain, and repair one's property. Manufacturer restrictions that render a product unusable or unrepairable may, in extreme circumstances, engage property rights concerns.
- **Article 8 (Right to Respect for Private Life):** Software restrictions requiring consumers to submit devices to manufacturer-controlled repair processes, potentially exposing personal data, may raise privacy concerns.
- **Article 10 (Freedom of Expression):** The dissemination of repair manuals, instructional content, and diagnostic information by independent repair communities is protected expression. Manufacturer use of copyright or trade secret law to suppress such information raises freedom of expression concerns.

### Key Case Law

***GS Media BV v. Sanoma Media Netherlands BV* (C-160/15, CJEU 2016):** While not a repair case, this CJEU decision on hyperlinking to copyrighted content established principles relevant to the sharing of repair documentation, distinguishing between commercial and non-commercial actors and the knowledge requirement regarding unlawful publication.

***Husqvarna v. Commission* (concerning aftermarket restrictions, ongoing):** EU competition law increasingly scrutinizes manufacturers who restrict access to aftermarket repair markets, building on the CJEU's established doctrine that aftermarket monopolization may constitute abuse of a dominant position under Article 102 TFEU.

**European Commission Ecodesign Enforcement Actions:** The Commission has pursued infringement proceedings against Member States that have failed to adequately implement spare parts availability requirements under delegated Ecodesign Regulations, establishing that repair-related obligations carry binding force.

### Agricultural Equipment Repair in the EU

The EU's approach to agricultural equipment repair has been shaped by several factors:

**Type Approval Regulation (EU) 2018/858:** Requires manufacturers of motor vehicles, including agricultural and forestry vehicles covered under Regulation (EU) 167/2013, to provide independent operators with access to vehicle on-board diagnostic (OBD) systems, repair and maintenance information, and related software updates.

**Common Agricultural Policy (CAP) Considerations:** The European Commission has recognized that repair restrictions on agricultural equipment directly affect the economic viability of farming operations, particularly for small and medium-sized farms that cannot afford manufacturer-authorized repair services. The European Parliament has called for specific measures to ensure that farmers can repair their equipment independently without losing warranty coverage.

**Environmental Justification:** The European Commission's Circular Economy Action Plan identifies product longevity and repairability as central to achieving climate neutrality by 2050. Studies commissioned by the Commission estimate that extending the average lifespan of consumer electronics by one year across the EU would save approximately 4 million tonnes of CO2 annually, equivalent to taking 2 million cars off the road.

### Intellectual Property Tensions

A fundamental tension in right to repair law involves the intersection between repair rights and intellectual property protections:

**Patent Law:** Repair of patented products is generally permissible under patent exhaustion doctrine, which provides that a patent holder's rights are exhausted upon the first authorized sale of a patented article. However, the boundary between permissible "repair" and impermissible "reconstruction" of patented products remains contested, as illustrated by the CJEU's approach in *Hewlett-Packard v. Repain* and related cases involving refilled printer cartridges.

**Copyright and Software Locks:** Manufacturers increasingly use software to restrict repair by pairing components to specific device serial numbers, requiring authentication for replacement parts to function. The DMCA's anti-circumvention provisions (17 U.S.C. section 1201) in the United States, and the EU's Computer Programs Directive (2009/24/EC), create potential legal barriers to circumventing these software locks for repair purposes. The Oregon and EU approaches to banning parts pairing represent legislative efforts to resolve this tension in favor of repair rights.

**Trade Secrets:** Manufacturers may claim that diagnostic software, error codes, and repair procedures constitute trade secrets, restricting access by independent repair providers. The EU Right to Repair Directive addresses this by requiring manufacturers to provide repair information on fair, reasonable, and non-discriminatory terms, though the precise boundaries of this obligation in relation to trade secret protection remain to be tested in litigation.

**Design Rights:** Visible spare parts such as car body panels, phone screens, and appliance housings are often protected by design rights (registered designs or community designs in the EU). Several jurisdictions have adopted "repair clauses" exempting the use of protected designs in spare parts for repair purposes. The EU Design Directive includes an optional repair clause, and Germany has advocated for a mandatory EU-wide repair clause to harmonize this area.

### Member State Obligations

**EU Right to Repair Directive (2024/1799):** Member States must transpose the Directive by 31 July 2026, establishing:

1. **Manufacturer Repair Obligation:** For products covered by Union harmonization legislation containing repairability requirements, manufacturers must carry out repairs even after the legal guarantee period, subject to reasonable pricing.
2. **Spare Parts and Information Access:** Manufacturers must provide independent repairers and consumers with spare parts, tools, and repair-related information at fair, reasonable, and non-discriminatory terms.
3. **European Repair Information Form:** A standardized form enabling consumers to compare repair offers, including estimated costs, expected repair time, and availability of refurbished or second-hand parts.
4. **Online Repair Platforms:** Each Member State must establish a national online platform connecting consumers with repair services and community repair initiatives.
5. **Defect Liability Extension:** Where a consumer opts for repair instead of replacement during the legal guarantee period, the guarantee is extended by 12 months from the date the repaired goods are returned.

**Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation (EU) 2024/1781:** This regulation establishes a framework for setting ecodesign requirements including:

- Mandatory product passports containing repairability information
- Spare parts availability periods for product categories
- Design requirements facilitating disassembly and repair
- Prohibition of software practices that prevent repair or mandate the use of specific spare parts

**France's Repairability Index:** France pioneered the EU-wide approach with its indice de reparabilite, mandatory since January 2021 for smartphones, laptops, televisions, washing machines, and lawnmowers, rating products on a scale of 1-10 based on documentation availability, disassembly ease, spare parts availability and pricing, and other criteria. This model is being adopted at the EU level.

### Additional Jurisdictions

- **Australia** addressed right to repair through the Productivity Commission's 2021 report, "Right to Repair," which recommended strengthening the Australian Consumer Law to better support repair rights, though comprehensive legislation has not yet been enacted. The Competition and Consumer Act 2010 provides consumer guarantees including the right to a repair remedy.
- **Japan** has no standalone right to repair law, but the Ministry of Economy, Trade and Industry (METI) has implemented resource efficiency standards that include spare parts availability requirements for certain appliance categories.
- **India** has emerging right to repair considerations driven by the Bureau of Indian Standards and growing civil society advocacy, with the Ministry of Consumer Affairs establishing a Right to Repair portal in 2022 covering sectors including farming equipment, mobile phones, and electronics.
- **Brazil** addresses repair through the Consumer Defense Code (Codigo de Defesa do Consumidor, Law No. 8,078/1990), which guarantees consumers access to spare parts during a product's useful life, with the National Consumer Secretariat (SENACON) overseeing enforcement.

### Economic Impact and Market Analysis

The economic implications of right to repair legislation are substantial across all jurisdictions:

**Global Repair Market Size:** The global electronics repair and maintenance market was valued at approximately $120 billion in 2024, with independent repair shops accounting for roughly 40% of the market by revenue. Right to repair legislation is projected to increase the independent repair market share by 15-25% in jurisdictions with comprehensive laws, while reducing the overall cost of repairs for consumers by an estimated 20-40%.

**Employment Effects:** The EU estimates that the Right to Repair Directive and Ecodesign Regulation together will create approximately 270,000 additional jobs in the EU repair and refurbishment sector by 2030, primarily in small and medium enterprises.

**Manufacturer Opposition Arguments:** Industry groups, including the Information Technology Industry Council (ITI) and the Association of Home Appliance Manufacturers (AHAM), have argued that mandatory repair access could compromise product safety, expose trade secrets, undermine cybersecurity, and increase product costs. These arguments have been partially addressed by statutory carve-outs for security-critical components, trade secret protections for non-essential repair documentation, and safety certification requirements for independent repair providers.

**Consumer Savings:** Studies in the United States suggest that right to repair could save American consumers $40-50 billion annually in reduced repair costs, avoided premature replacement purchases, and lower insurance premiums. In the agricultural sector, the FTC's complaint against John Deere alleged that repair restrictions cost U.S. farmers thousands of dollars per year in unnecessary dealer charges and equipment downtime during critical growing seasons.
