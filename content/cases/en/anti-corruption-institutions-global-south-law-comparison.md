## Summary

Anti-corruption institutions in the Global South have developed distinctive models that reflect the particular corruption challenges facing post-colonial, post-revolutionary, and transitioning states, operating within institutional environments where the rule of law is often contested, patronage networks are deeply embedded, and the political will for anti-corruption enforcement is cyclical and selective. China operates the world's most organizationally extensive anti-corruption apparatus, combining the CPC's Central Commission for Discipline Inspection (CCDI, 中央纪律检查委员会, established 1949, reconstituted 1978) with the National Supervisory Commission (NSC, 国家监察委员会, established by constitutional amendment in 2018 and the Supervision Law, 监察法), creating a system that merges Party discipline and state law enforcement under Xi Jinping's comprehensive anti-corruption campaign that has sanctioned over 4.7 million officials since 2012, including senior leaders such as Zhou Yongkang (former Politburo Standing Committee member, sentenced 2015), Sun Zhengcai (former Chongqing Party Secretary, sentenced 2018), and over 500 officials at the provincial/ministerial level and above. India's anti-corruption framework is fragmented across multiple bodies: the Central Bureau of Investigation (CBI, established 1963 under the Delhi Special Police Establishment Act, 1946), the Central Vigilance Commission (CVC, established 1964, granted statutory status by the Central Vigilance Commission Act, 2003), the Lokpal (established by the Lokpal and Lokayuktas Act, 2013, following the anti-corruption movement led by Anna Hazare and the India Against Corruption movement), and the Enforcement Directorate (ED, operating under the Prevention of Money Laundering Act, 2002, PMLA), alongside the Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988 (amended 2018), as the primary substantive criminal statute. South Africa's anti-corruption architecture was shaped by the Constitutional Court's ruling in *Glenister v. President of the Republic of South Africa*, 2011 (3) SA 347 (CC), which held that the Constitution requires an adequately independent anti-corruption entity, leading to the creation of the Directorate for Priority Crime Investigation (DPCI/Hawks) under the South African Police Service Amendment Act 57 of 2008 and the appointment of the Judicial Commission of Inquiry into Allegations of State Capture (Zondo Commission, 2018-2022), which produced a comprehensive report on state capture during the Jacob Zuma presidency. Brazil's anti-corruption system comprises the Ministerio Publico Federal (MPF, Federal Public Ministry, with constitutional independence under Article 127), the Controladoria-Geral da Uniao (CGU, Office of the Comptroller General, established 2003), and the Federal Police (Policia Federal), which together drove the Lava Jato (Car Wash) investigation (2014-2021) that resulted in convictions of senior political and business figures, the recovery of over R$4 billion, and the imprisonment (later reversed) of former President Lula da Silva. The Philippine anti-corruption framework centers on the Office of the Ombudsman (established under Article XI of the 1987 Constitution and Republic Act No. 6770, the Ombudsman Act of 1989) and the Sandiganbayan (anti-graft court, established under Presidential Decree No. 1606, 1978, as amended by Republic Act No. 8249, 1997), operating under the Anti-Graft and Corrupt Practices Act (Republic Act No. 3019, 1960) and Republic Act No. 1379 (forfeiture of unlawfully acquired property). Indonesia's Komisi Pemberantasan Korupsi (KPK, Corruption Eradication Commission, established by Law No. 30/2002) emerged as one of the most effective anti-corruption bodies in the Global South, with a conviction rate exceeding 99%, though the controversial 2019 amendments to the KPK Law (Law No. 19/2019) restructured the Commission by converting its investigators to civil servants, establishing a Supervisory Board (Dewan Pengawas), and limiting wiretapping authority, changes widely criticized as weakening the institution.

## China (中国)

### The CCDI-NSC System

China's anti-corruption architecture is the product of the merger of Party discipline inspection and state supervisory functions, creating a unified system under CPC leadership.

**Central Commission for Discipline Inspection (CCDI, 中央纪律检查委员会):** The CCDI, established in the CPC Constitution, exercises supervisory authority over CPC members (approximately 99 million as of 2024). The CCDI Secretary is a member of the Politburo Standing Committee (currently Li Xi, since 2022), ensuring that anti-corruption policy is made at the highest level of political authority. The CCDI's local counterparts (Discipline Inspection Commissions at provincial, prefectural, and county levels) exercise shuanggui (双规, later replaced by liuzhi, 留置) -- the power to detain and interrogate Party members suspected of discipline violations, initially without formal legal authority and later formalized through the Supervision Law.

**National Supervisory Commission (NSC, 国家监察委员会):** The NSC, established by the 2018 constitutional amendment as a state organ of equal rank to the State Council, the Supreme People's Court, and the Supreme People's Procuratorate, exercises supervisory authority over all persons exercising public power (approximately 90 million), not only CPC members. The NSC merged the functions of the Ministry of Supervision (行政监察), the National Bureau of Corruption Prevention (国家预防腐败局), and the procuratorate's duty crime investigation function.

**Supervision Law (监察法, 2018):**
- **Article 3:** Supervisory commissions at all levels are the state's specialized organs for conducting supervision against duty-related malfeasance.
- **Article 11:** Supervisory commissions exercise the powers of supervision, investigation, and disposition, including the authority to question suspects, search premises, seize property, and freeze bank accounts.
- **Article 22:** Supervisory commissions may take a suspect into liuzhi (留置, retention in custody) for investigation, for an initial period of three months extendable to six months. Liuzhi replaced the extralegal shuanggui system, bringing detention within a statutory framework, though concerns about access to counsel and conditions of detention persist.
- **Article 33:** Evidence collected by supervisory commissions may be used in criminal proceedings, bridging the gap between Party discipline investigation and criminal prosecution.

**Scale of the Anti-Corruption Campaign:** Between November 2012 and December 2024, the CCDI/NSC system investigated and sanctioned over 4.7 million officials, including over 500 at the provincial/ministerial level and above. The campaign targeted both "tigers" (高级干部, senior officials) and "flies" (基层干部, grassroots officials), with high-profile cases including:
- **Zhou Yongkang:** Former member of the Politburo Standing Committee and Secretary of the Political-Legal Committee, sentenced to life imprisonment in 2015 for bribery, abuse of power, and intentional disclosure of state secrets.
- **Bo Xilai:** Former Chongqing Party Secretary, sentenced to life imprisonment in 2013 for bribery, embezzlement, and abuse of power.
- **Ling Jihua:** Former head of the CPC United Front Work Department, sentenced to life imprisonment in 2016.

### International Cooperation: Operation Sky Net and Fox Hunt

China operates international anti-corruption cooperation programs including "Sky Net" (天网, Tianwang, coordinated by the Central Anti-Corruption Coordination Group) and "Fox Hunt" (猎狐, Liehu, operated by the Ministry of Public Security). Between 2014 and 2024, these operations repatriated over 12,000 fugitives and recovered over 40 billion yuan in illicitly transferred assets. However, these operations have raised concerns in target countries about due process, extraterritorial application of Chinese law, and coercive methods of persuading suspects to return.

## India (भारत)

### The Fragmented Anti-Corruption Architecture

India's anti-corruption framework is characterized by institutional fragmentation, overlapping jurisdictions, and persistent concerns about political interference.

**Central Bureau of Investigation (CBI):** Established in 1963 by a Home Ministry resolution (not by statute) under the Delhi Special Police Establishment Act, 1946, the CBI is India's primary federal investigative agency for corruption, economic crimes, and special crimes. The Supreme Court in *Vineet Narain v. Union of India*, AIR 1998 SC 889, directed the creation of an independent selection process for the CBI Director (now appointed by a committee comprising the Prime Minister, Leader of Opposition/largest opposition party leader, and the Chief Justice of India, per the Lokpal and Lokayuktas Act, 2013). Despite this reform, the Court memorably described the CBI as a "caged parrot speaking in its master's voice" in the 2013 coal allocation case (*Centre for Public Interest Litigation v. Union of India*, 2013 SCC Online SC 332), reflecting pervasive concerns about executive manipulation.

**Central Vigilance Commission (CVC):** The CVC, granted statutory status by the Central Vigilance Commission Act, 2003, exercises superintendence over vigilance administration in central government organizations, including the CBI. The CVC is headed by a Central Vigilance Commissioner appointed by the President on the recommendation of a committee comprising the Prime Minister, Home Minister, and Leader of Opposition. However, the CVC functions primarily as an advisory body, issuing vigilance guidance rather than conducting criminal investigations.

**Lokpal and Lokayuktas Act, 2013:** The Lokpal institution was the product of decades of advocacy, catalyzed by Anna Hazare's 2011 anti-corruption movement (India Against Corruption). The Lokpal consists of a Chairperson and up to eight members (of whom 50% must be from SC, ST, OBC, minority, or women categories). The Lokpal has jurisdiction over the Prime Minister (with certain restrictions), Union Ministers, Members of Parliament, and officers and employees of the central government (Groups A, B, C, and D). The first Lokpal (Justice Pinaki Chandra Ghose) was appointed only in 2019, six years after the Act's passage.

**Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988 (PCA, amended 2018):** The principal substantive anti-corruption statute:
- **Section 7:** Criminalizes public servants obtaining undue advantage (replacing the former "gratification" standard) with punishment of 3-7 years imprisonment.
- **Section 8:** Criminalizes giving undue advantage to a public servant (bribe-giving, previously not explicitly criminalized).
- **Section 17A (added 2018):** Requires prior approval of an "authority competent to remove" the public servant before investigation can commence against officials of a certain rank, raising concerns about creating barriers to investigation.

### The Enforcement Directorate and PMLA

The Enforcement Directorate (ED), operating under the Prevention of Money Laundering Act, 2002 (PMLA), has become an increasingly prominent anti-corruption instrument. The Supreme Court in *Vijay Madanlal Choudhary v. Union of India*, (2022) 10 SCC 1, upheld the constitutionality of the PMLA, including the reverse burden of proof (Section 24), stringent bail conditions (Section 45), and the ED's powers of attachment and confiscation. Critics argue that the PMLA's broad definition of "proceeds of crime" and the ED's selective enforcement create an instrument of political targeting.

## Africa (Nigeria and South Africa)

### Nigeria: EFCC and ICPC

Nigeria has established two primary anti-corruption agencies:

**Economic and Financial Crimes Commission (EFCC):** Established by the Economic and Financial Crimes Commission (Establishment) Act, 2004, the EFCC investigates and prosecutes economic and financial crimes including money laundering, advance fee fraud, and corruption. The EFCC Chairman is appointed by the President and confirmed by the Senate. The EFCC's conviction record includes former state governors (Diepreye Alamieyeseigha, Bayelsa Governor, convicted 2007; Joshua Dariye, Plateau Governor, convicted 2018; Jolly Nyame, Taraba Governor, convicted 2018), though critics point to selective prosecution targeting political opponents of the incumbent administration.

**Independent Corrupt Practices and Other Related Offences Commission (ICPC):** Established by the Corrupt Practices and Other Related Offences Act, 2000, the ICPC has a broader anti-corruption mandate including investigation, prosecution, prevention, and public education. The ICPC focuses on public sector corruption, including contract fraud, misappropriation of public funds, and abuse of office.

**Constitutional Provisions:** Section 15(5) directs the state to abolish all corrupt practices and abuse of power, while the Fifth Schedule (Code of Conduct for Public Officers) and the Code of Conduct Bureau and Tribunal (Sections 153(1)(a) and Part I of the Fifth Schedule) require public officers to declare their assets, prohibit the maintenance of foreign accounts (except for diplomats), and restrict outside employment. The controversy over Chief Justice Onnoghen's alleged failure to declare assets under the Code of Conduct was the ostensible basis for his 2019 suspension.

### South Africa: State Capture and Institutional Response

South Africa's anti-corruption framework was tested by the phenomenon of "state capture" during the Jacob Zuma presidency (2009-2018), involving the systematic diversion of state resources through the Gupta family's influence over government appointments and state-owned enterprise procurement.

**The Glenister Jurisprudence:** In *Glenister v. President of the Republic of South Africa* (Glenister II), 2011 (3) SA 347 (CC), the Constitutional Court held that the Constitution, read with South Africa's international obligations under the UN Convention against Corruption and the AU Convention on Preventing and Combating Corruption, requires the existence of an independent anti-corruption entity. The dissolution of the Directorate of Special Operations (DSO/"Scorpions") and its replacement with the DPCI ("Hawks") -- a unit within the South African Police Service -- did not meet the independence requirement. The Court ordered structural amendments to enhance the Hawks' independence, leading to the South African Police Service Amendment Act 10 of 2012.

**The Zondo Commission (Judicial Commission of Inquiry into Allegations of State Capture, 2018-2022):** Chaired by Deputy Chief Justice Raymond Zondo (later Chief Justice), the Commission heard testimony from over 300 witnesses across 430 hearing days, producing a six-part report documenting state capture at Eskom (national electricity utility), Transnet (national freight logistics company), Denel (defence company), the South African Revenue Service (SARS), and multiple government departments. The Commission's recommendations included criminal prosecution of identified individuals (including former President Zuma, who was convicted of contempt of court for refusing to testify: *Secretary of the Judicial Commission of Inquiry into Allegations of State Capture v. Zuma*, [2021] ZACC 18), institutional reforms to prevent future capture, and strengthened oversight of state-owned enterprises.

**Public Protector:** The Public Protector (Section 182 of the Constitution) is a Chapter 9 institution empowered to investigate any conduct in state affairs that is alleged or suspected to be improper or to result in impropriety or prejudice. Former Public Protector Thuli Madonsela's "State of Capture" report (Report No. 6 of 2016/17) was instrumental in triggering the Zondo Commission, though the subsequent Public Protector Busisiwe Mkhwebane was impeached in 2023 for misconduct and incompetence, highlighting the vulnerability of anti-corruption institutions to political capture.

## Latin America (Brazil, Mexico, Colombia)

### Brazil: The MPF and Lava Jato

Brazil's anti-corruption system is anchored by the constitutional independence of the Ministerio Publico (Public Ministry), which Article 127 of the 1988 Constitution describes as "a permanent institution, essential to the jurisdictional function of the State, responsible for the defense of the legal order, the democratic regime, and indisponible social and individual interests."

**Lava Jato (Car Wash, 2014-2021):** The largest anti-corruption investigation in Brazilian history began as a money laundering investigation at a gas station car wash in Curitiba, Parana, and expanded to reveal a massive corruption scheme centered on the state oil company Petrobras, involving political parties, construction companies, and senior government officials across multiple administrations. Key features:

- **Plea bargaining (colaboracao premiada):** Law 12.850/2013 (organized crime law) formalized plea agreements, which became the primary tool for Lava Jato prosecutors. The STF upheld the constitutionality of plea agreements in Plenario 5.952 (2015) but imposed procedural safeguards.
- **Judicial activism:** Judge Sergio Moro (13th Federal Court of Curitiba) became the most prominent judicial figure in the investigation, convicting former President Lula da Silva in 2017 (upheld by the TRF-4 appeals court in 2018). However, the STF later found Moro biased (HC 193.726, 2021), annulling Lula's convictions.
- **Results:** Over 270 criminal cases, more than 170 convictions, recovery of over R$4 billion in plea agreements, and criminal charges against senior political figures including former Presidents Lula and Temer.
- **Criticism:** The investigation was criticized for due process violations (including the leaked conversations between Judge Moro and prosecutors that formed the basis for the bias finding), selective prosecution, and destabilizing effects on the political system.

**Law 12.846/2013 (Anti-Corruption Law/Clean Company Act):** This law establishes strict (objective) civil and administrative liability for legal entities that commit acts against the public administration, including bribery, bid-rigging, and fraud in public procurement. Penalties include fines of 0.1% to 20% of gross revenue, publication of the decision, and debarment. The law also provides for leniency agreements (acordos de leniencia) negotiated with the CGU.

### Colombia: Anti-Corruption Within Armed Conflict

Colombia's anti-corruption framework operates within the context of decades of armed conflict, drug trafficking, and the intermingling of illegal and legal economies.

**Procuraduria General de la Nacion:** The Procurador General, elected by the Senate from a terna presented by the President, Supreme Court, and Council of State, exercises disciplinary authority over all public officials. The Procuraduria can impose sanctions including dismissal and disqualification from holding public office, as controversially applied in *Petro v. Colombia* (IACtHR, 2020), where the Inter-American Court found that the Procuraduria's removal of then-Mayor of Bogota Gustavo Petro violated the American Convention's requirement that political rights can only be restricted by criminal conviction by a competent judge.

**Contraloria General de la Republica:** The Contralor General, elected by Congress, exercises fiscal control over the management of public resources, with the power to declare fiscal responsibility (responsabilidad fiscal) and order restitution from officials who cause patrimonial damage to the state.

### Mexico: The National Anti-Corruption System

Mexico's 2015 constitutional reform established the Sistema Nacional Anticorrupcion (SNA, National Anti-Corruption System), comprising the Comite Coordinador (Coordinating Committee), the Comite de Participacion Ciudadana (Citizen Participation Committee), and the Tribunal Federal de Justicia Administrativa (Federal Court of Administrative Justice). The SNA coordinates the actions of the Auditoria Superior de la Federacion (ASF, Federal Audit Office), the Secretaria de la Funcion Publica (SFP, Ministry of Public Administration), the Fiscalia General de la Republica (FGR, Attorney General's Office), and the Instituto Nacional de Transparencia, Acceso a la Informacion y Proteccion de Datos Personales (INAI, transparency institute).

## Southeast Asia (Indonesia and Philippines)

### Indonesia: The KPK

The Komisi Pemberantasan Korupsi (KPK), established by Law No. 30/2002 on the Corruption Eradication Commission, was designed as an independent, superbody anti-corruption commission with extraordinary powers:

- **Wiretapping authority:** The KPK could conduct wiretaps without prior judicial authorization (a power unique among Indonesian law enforcement agencies).
- **Prosecution authority:** The KPK has its own prosecutors, independent of the Attorney General's Office.
- **No authority to dismiss cases:** Article 6(e) of Law 30/2002 originally provided that the KPK could not dismiss cases (wajib menindaklanjuti), creating a structural incentive for thorough investigation before case initiation.
- **Trigger mechanism:** The KPK can take over cases from the police or prosecutor's office under specified circumstances.

**The 2019 KPK Law Amendments (Law No. 19/2019):** These amendments, enacted despite widespread protests (the "Reformasi Dikorupsi" movement), introduced several changes widely viewed as weakening the KPK:
- **Dewan Pengawas (Supervisory Board):** A five-member board with the power to authorize wiretapping, searches, and seizures, adding a layer of oversight previously absent.
- **Civil servant conversion:** KPK investigators and employees were converted to civil servants (Aparatur Sipil Negara, ASN), subjecting them to civil service regulations and the National Insight Test (Tes Wawasan Kebangsaan, TWK), which was used in 2021 to terminate 57 KPK employees who "failed" the test.
- **The Constitutional Court** in Decision No. 70/PUU-XVII/2019 upheld most provisions of the amended law, though dissenting opinions argued the amendments undermined the KPK's constitutional mandate.

**Notable Cases:**
- **e-KTP (electronic national identity card) scandal:** A corruption case involving the procurement of electronic identity cards, resulting in losses of approximately Rp 2.3 trillion (approximately USD 170 million), with convictions of multiple legislators including former House Speaker Setya Novanto (sentenced to 15 years, 2018).
- **Akil Mochtar:** Former Chief Justice of the Constitutional Court, arrested by the KPK in 2013 for accepting bribes in election dispute cases, sentenced to life imprisonment in 2014.
- **Juliari Batubara:** Former Social Affairs Minister, arrested by the KPK in 2020 for corruption in COVID-19 social assistance distribution, sentenced to 12 years in 2021.

### Philippines: Ombudsman and Sandiganbayan

The Philippine anti-corruption framework centers on two constitutionally mandated institutions:

**Office of the Ombudsman (Article XI, Sections 5-14; RA 6770):** The Ombudsman is appointed by the President from a list prepared by the Judicial and Bar Council, serving a seven-year term without reappointment. The Ombudsman has jurisdiction over all government officials and employees (except impeachable officers, members of Congress, and the judiciary), with powers to:
- Investigate and prosecute any act or omission of any public officer that appears to be illegal, unjust, improper, or inefficient.
- Direct any government agency to perform and expedite any act or duty required by law.
- Direct any government agency to produce documents relating to complaints filed.
- Request assistance from any government agency.

**Sandiganbayan (PD 1606, as amended by RA 8249 and RA 10660):** The Sandiganbayan is a special anti-graft court with jurisdiction over criminal and civil cases involving graft and corrupt practices and other offenses committed by public officers and employees in relation to their office, including those with Salary Grade 27 and above (or equivalent). The Sandiganbayan comprises seven divisions of three justices each.

**The PDAF/DAP Scam:** The Priority Development Assistance Fund (PDAF, also known as "pork barrel") scandal, which erupted in 2013 based on whistleblower Janet Lim-Napoles' testimony, revealed the systematic diversion of congressional funds through fictitious non-governmental organizations. The Supreme Court in *Belgica v. Ochoa* (G.R. No. 208566, 2013) declared the PDAF unconstitutional for violating the separation of powers and the principle that appropriation must be for a specific public purpose. The related Disbursement Acceleration Program (DAP) was also partially struck down in *Araullo v. Aquino* (G.R. No. 209287, 2014). Plunder charges were filed against several senators, including Senators Revilla, Estrada, and Enrile.

**Republic Act No. 3019 (Anti-Graft and Corrupt Practices Act, 1960):** Section 3 defines corrupt practices of public officers, including persuading, inducing, or influencing another public officer to perform an act constituting a violation of rules and regulations (Section 3(a)), directly or indirectly requesting or receiving any gift, present, share, percentage, or benefit in connection with government contracts or transactions (Section 3(b)), and causing undue injury to any party or giving unwarranted benefits through manifest partiality, evident bad faith, or gross inexcusable negligence (Section 3(e)). Section 3(e) is the most commonly prosecuted provision.

## Comparative Analysis

### Independence vs. Effectiveness Tradeoffs

The Global South experience reveals a persistent tension between institutional independence and anti-corruption effectiveness. China's CCDI-NSC system is the most effective in quantitative terms (millions of officials sanctioned) but operates under CPC leadership, raising concerns about selective enforcement and political instrumentalization. India's fragmented system provides multiple checks but also creates coordination failures and opportunities for institutional arbitrage. Brazil's constitutionally independent MPF drove Lava Jato's successes but also generated concerns about prosecutorial overreach. Indonesia's KPK demonstrated that an independent commission can achieve extraordinary results (99% conviction rate) but also showed that independence invites political backlash (the 2019 amendments). South Africa's Glenister jurisprudence articulates the constitutional requirement for independence but the practical implementation of that requirement remains contested.

### Anti-Corruption as Political Weapon

In all five regions, anti-corruption enforcement has been used as a tool of political competition: China's campaign disproportionately targeted political rivals of Xi Jinping; India's CBI and ED have been deployed against opposition politicians; Nigeria's EFCC has faced allegations of selective prosecution; Brazil's Lava Jato was criticized for targeting left-wing politicians while initially sparing others; and the Philippines' Ombudsman investigations have followed political cycles. The challenge of ensuring that anti-corruption institutions serve the rule of law rather than political power is the central dilemma of anti-corruption governance in the Global South.

### International Frameworks and Domestic Implementation

All states examined have ratified the UN Convention against Corruption (UNCAC, 2003). However, the domestic implementation of UNCAC provisions varies dramatically. China's asset declaration system for officials (领导干部个人有关事项报告制度) lacks public transparency, unlike South Africa's Code of Conduct requirements. India's whistleblower protection (Whistle Blowers Protection Act, 2014) has not been implemented effectively. Brazil's access to information law (Law 12.527/2011) is among the strongest in the Global South. The gap between international commitments and domestic implementation reflects both structural constraints and political choices.
