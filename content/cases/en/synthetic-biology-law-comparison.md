## Summary

Synthetic biology -- the interdisciplinary field that applies engineering principles to biology, encompassing the design and construction of new biological parts, devices, and systems as well as the redesign of existing natural biological systems for useful purposes, including gene synthesis, genome editing (CRISPR-Cas9), metabolic engineering, xenobiology, and the creation of minimal or synthetic genomes -- presents novel and rapidly evolving regulatory challenges that existing biosafety, biosecurity, and intellectual property frameworks were not designed to address, as the technology enables the creation of organisms and biological components with no natural analog, the synthesis of dangerous pathogens from publicly available sequence data, and the democratization of powerful biological engineering tools to a widening circle of practitioners including community biology laboratories. South Korea regulates synthetic biology primarily through the Living Modified Organisms Act (유전자변형생물체의 국가간 이동 등에 관한 법률, the LMO Act), enacted in 2001 to implement the Cartagena Protocol on Biosafety, the Bioethics and Safety Act (생명윤리 및 안전에 관한 법률), and the Industrial Technology Protection Act (산업기술의 유출방지 및 보호에 관한 법률), supplemented by the Ministry of Science and ICT's biosafety guidelines for gene editing research; Korea's BioHealth Innovation Strategy announced in 2023 identified synthetic biology as a priority area. The United States regulates synthetic biology through a fragmented multi-agency framework known as the Coordinated Framework for the Regulation of Biotechnology (1986, updated 2017), with jurisdiction shared among the EPA (Toxic Substances Control Act, 15 U.S.C. Sections 2601-2629), FDA (Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act, 21 U.S.C. Sections 301-399), and USDA-APHIS (Plant Protection Act, 7 U.S.C. Sections 7701-7772); Executive Order 14081 on Advancing Biotechnology and Biomanufacturing Innovation (2022) directed agencies to modernize regulatory frameworks for biotechnology; the IBBEA (International Biosafety and Biosecurity Enhancement Act) and SECURE Rule (2020) represent recent regulatory developments. Germany regulates synthetic biology within the EU framework, primarily through the Gentechnikgesetz (GenTG, Genetic Engineering Act) implementing EU Directive 2001/18/EC on deliberate release of GMOs and Directive 2009/41/EC on contained use of genetically modified micro-organisms, with the BVL (Bundesamt fuer Verbraucherschutz und Lebensmittelsicherheit) and the ZKBS (Zentrale Kommission fuer die Biologische Sicherheit) serving as key regulatory bodies. The EU and international framework includes the Cartagena Protocol on Biosafety (2000), the Nagoya Protocol on Access and Benefit-Sharing (2010), EU GMO Directive 2001/18/EC, and the CJEU's landmark ruling in Confedération paysanne v. Premier ministre (Case C-528/16, 2018) holding that organisms obtained by mutagenesis techniques (including directed mutagenesis/gene editing) are in principle subject to the GMO Directive.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's regulation of synthetic biology operates through a combination of biosafety legislation originally designed for conventional GMOs and a developing set of guidelines and strategies addressing newer technologies.

**Living Modified Organisms Act (유전자변형생물체의 국가간 이동 등에 관한 법률, LMO Act, 2001):** Enacted to implement the Cartagena Protocol on Biosafety, the LMO Act is the primary statute governing biosafety in South Korea. Key provisions:

- **Article 2 (Definitions):** Defines "living modified organism" as any living organism that possesses a novel combination of genetic material obtained through the use of modern biotechnology. The definition's scope regarding synthetic biology products -- particularly synthetic organisms, cell-free systems, and xenobiological systems -- remains uncertain.
- **Article 8 (Import Approval):** Importation of LMOs requires approval from the relevant competent authority (Ministry of Science and ICT for research use; Ministry of Agriculture for agricultural use; Ministry of Environment for environmental release).
- **Article 12 (Environmental Release):** Environmental release of LMOs requires risk assessment and approval, including assessment of potential effects on biological diversity, human health, and the environment.
- **Article 22 (Contained Use):** Research facilities using LMOs must establish Institutional Biosafety Committees (IBCs, 기관생물안전위원회) and comply with biosafety guidelines.

**Bioethics and Safety Act (생명윤리 및 안전에 관한 법률):** Regulates research involving human embryos, embryonic stem cells, and human gene therapy. Article 47 prohibits human cloning and the creation of human-animal chimeric embryos for reproductive purposes. The Act's application to synthetic biology research involving human genetic material or human-derived cells is increasingly relevant.

**Gene Editing Oversight:** South Korea's Korea Disease Control and Prevention Agency (KDCA, 질병관리청) has issued guidelines on gene editing research that apply the LMO Act framework to CRISPR and other gene editing technologies on a case-by-case basis. For gene-edited organisms that do not contain foreign DNA (SDN-1 and SDN-2 modifications), the regulatory status remains under review, with the Ministry of Agriculture, Food and Rural Affairs (MAFRA) announcing in 2022 a potential exemption pathway for certain gene-edited agricultural products that are indistinguishable from conventionally bred varieties.

### Biosecurity Framework

**Industrial Technology Protection Act (산업기술의 유출방지 및 보호에 관한 법률):** Designates certain biotechnologies as "national core technologies" (국가핵심기술) subject to export control and technology transfer restrictions. Synthetic biology techniques with dual-use potential may be classified under this framework.

**Infectious Disease Control and Prevention Act (감염병의 예방 및 관리에 관한 법률):** Article 21 establishes a classification system for high-risk pathogens, and Article 22 requires facilities working with such pathogens to obtain authorization from the KDCA. The synthesis or reconstruction of scheduled pathogens falls within this regulatory framework.

### BioHealth Innovation Strategy (2023)

The Korean government's BioHealth Innovation Strategy, announced by the Ministry of Science and ICT in 2023, identified synthetic biology as one of six priority technology areas for national investment and regulatory modernization. The strategy called for establishing a dedicated regulatory pathway for synthetic biology products distinct from the LMO framework and for developing biosecurity guidelines for gene synthesis providers.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States regulates synthetic biology through a multi-agency framework that has struggled to keep pace with rapid technological advances.

**Coordinated Framework for the Regulation of Biotechnology (1986, Updated 2017):** Established in 1986, the Coordinated Framework allocates regulatory jurisdiction over biotechnology products among three agencies based on the product's intended use:

- **EPA (Environmental Protection Agency):** Regulates new chemical substances (including certain microorganisms) under TSCA Section 5 (15 U.S.C. Section 2604), requiring premanufacture notification for new microorganisms intended for commercial use. EPA also regulates biopesticides under FIFRA (7 U.S.C. Sections 136-136y).
- **FDA (Food and Drug Administration):** Regulates food, drugs, and medical devices derived from biotechnology under the FFDCA. Genetically engineered animals are regulated as "new animal drugs" under 21 U.S.C. Section 360b. The FDA approved the first genetically engineered animal for food use, AquAdvantage salmon, in 2015.
- **USDA-APHIS (Animal and Plant Health Inspection Service):** Regulates the importation, interstate movement, and environmental release of organisms that may pose plant pest risks. The SECURE Rule (Sustainable, Ecological, Consistent, Uniform, Responsible, Efficient), finalized in 2020, modernized USDA's biotechnology regulations under the Plant Protection Act, establishing exemptions for certain gene-edited plants that could otherwise have been produced through conventional breeding.

**Executive Order 14081 -- Advancing Biotechnology and Biomanufacturing Innovation (September 12, 2022):** Directed federal agencies to modernize regulatory systems for biotechnology, including synthetic biology, and to develop a plan for reducing regulatory uncertainty. The Order explicitly recognized synthetic biology's economic and national security significance.

### Biosecurity Framework

**Select Agent Regulations (42 CFR Part 73; 7 CFR Part 331; 9 CFR Part 121):** The Federal Select Agent Program, jointly administered by the CDC and USDA-APHIS, regulates the possession, use, and transfer of biological agents and toxins that could pose a severe threat to public health and safety. Synthetic reconstruction of select agents (e.g., the synthesis of variola virus or 1918 influenza virus genetic material) is subject to these regulations.

**HHS Screening Framework Guidance for Providers and Users of Synthetic Double-Stranded DNA (Updated 2024):** Originally published in 2010 and updated in 2024, this guidance establishes voluntary screening standards for gene synthesis companies, recommending that providers screen orders of synthetic nucleic acids against sequences of concern (including select agents and toxins). The 2024 update, pursuant to Executive Order 14081, strengthened screening requirements and extended them to all federally funded research involving synthetic nucleic acids.

**Dual Use Research of Concern (DURC) Policy:** The 2024 update to the U.S. Government Policy for Oversight of DURC expanded the list of agents and categories of experiments subject to institutional review and federal oversight. Research involving the creation of enhanced potential pandemic pathogens (ePPPs) is subject to the HHS P3CO (Potential Pandemic Pathogen Care and Oversight) Framework.

**National Science Advisory Board for Biosecurity (NSABB):** Advises the federal government on biosecurity issues, including those arising from synthetic biology. The NSABB's 2023 recommendations on oversight of dual-use research and gain-of-function research significantly influenced the 2024 DURC policy update.

### Intellectual Property

Synthetic biology products raise novel patent issues. In Association for Molecular Pathology v. Myriad Genetics, 569 U.S. 576 (2013), the Supreme Court held that naturally occurring DNA sequences are not patent-eligible, but that complementary DNA (cDNA) created in the laboratory is patent-eligible under 35 U.S.C. Section 101. The decision's implications for synthetic biology -- where the distinction between "natural" and "synthetic" may be blurred -- remain actively debated.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany regulates synthetic biology within the EU's process-based GMO regulatory framework, with additional domestic provisions addressing biosafety and biosecurity.

**Gentechnikgesetz (GenTG -- Genetic Engineering Act, 1990, as amended):** The GenTG implements EU Directives 2001/18/EC and 2009/41/EC, establishing a comprehensive regulatory framework for contained use, deliberate release, and placing on the market of genetically modified organisms.

- **Section 3 No. 3 (Definition of GMO):** An organism whose genetic material has been altered in a way that does not occur naturally by mating and/or natural recombination. The scope of this definition for synthetic biology products is a central regulatory question.
- **Part 2 (Sections 7-12, Contained Use -- Gentechnische Arbeiten in gentechnischen Anlagen):** Research with GMOs must be conducted in registered or approved facilities (gentechnische Anlagen) with appropriate biosafety levels (Sicherheitsstufen S1-S4). Institutional Biological Safety Officers (Beauftragte fuer Biologische Sicherheit, BBS) must be appointed.
- **Part 3 (Sections 14-16, Deliberate Release -- Freisetzung):** Environmental release of GMOs requires authorization from the BVL, preceded by a comprehensive risk assessment and public consultation.

**ZKBS (Zentrale Kommission fuer die Biologische Sicherheit):** The Central Committee on Biological Safety, established under GenTG Section 4, provides scientific advice on the classification of genetic engineering operations and the appropriate biosafety measures. The ZKBS has issued opinions on the regulatory classification of organisms produced by various gene editing techniques, including CRISPR-Cas9.

**BVL (Bundesamt fuer Verbraucherschutz und Lebensmittelsicherheit):** The Federal Office of Consumer Protection and Food Safety is the competent authority for GenTG matters, responsible for authorizing contained use (above S1), deliberate release, and marketing of GMOs.

### Gene Editing Regulation Post-C-528/16

Following the CJEU's 2018 decision in Confedération paysanne (see EU section below), Germany applies the full GenTG regulatory framework to organisms produced by directed mutagenesis/gene editing techniques, even where no foreign DNA is inserted. This has significant implications for synthetic biology applications that use CRISPR and similar tools.

The German government, along with other member states, has supported the European Commission's proposal for deregulation of certain New Genomic Techniques (NGTs) products (COM(2023) 411), which would create exemptions for certain gene-edited plants that are equivalent to conventionally bred varieties (Category 1 NGT plants). As of 2026, legislative negotiations continue.

### Biosecurity Framework

**Biostoffverordnung (BioStoffV -- Biological Agents Ordinance, 2013):** Implements EU Directive 2000/54/EC on the protection of workers from biological agents. Classifies biological agents into four risk groups and establishes containment and worker protection measures for each level.

**Infektionsschutzgesetz (IfSG -- Infection Protection Act, 2000):** Article 44 requires notification and authorization for laboratories working with pathogens listed in Section 7. The reconstruction or synthesis of listed pathogens using synthetic biology techniques falls within this framework.

**Kriegswaffenkontrollgesetz (KrWaffKontrG -- War Weapons Control Act):** The Act's Annex lists biological weapons agents, and the possession, production, or transfer of such agents is prohibited except under specific authorizations. Synthetic production of listed agents is covered.

**Export Control (Dual-Use Regulation):** Synthetic biology equipment and technology may be subject to export controls under EU Regulation 2021/821 (Dual-Use Regulation) implementing the Australia Group and Wassenaar Arrangement controls on biological equipment and related technology.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Regulatory Framework

**Directive 2001/18/EC (Deliberate Release of GMOs):** The principal EU legislation governing the environmental release and marketing of GMOs. Article 2(2) defines a GMO as "an organism, with the exception of human beings, in which the genetic material has been altered in a way that does not occur naturally by mating and/or natural recombination." Annex I A lists techniques giving rise to genetic modification (including recombinant nucleic acid techniques), while Annex I B lists techniques excluded from the Directive (including mutagenesis and cell fusion of plant cells).

**CJEU: Confedération paysanne v. Premier ministre (Case C-528/16, Grand Chamber, July 25, 2018):** The Grand Chamber held that organisms obtained by mutagenesis techniques, including directed mutagenesis techniques such as CRISPR-Cas9 that have emerged since the adoption of Directive 2001/18/EC, are in principle "genetically modified organisms" within the meaning of the Directive, and that the mutagenesis exemption in Annex I B applies only to techniques with a long safety record (conventional mutagenesis). This landmark decision placed gene-edited organisms squarely within the EU's GMO regulatory framework, in contrast to the approach taken by the United States and several other major agricultural producers.

**Proposed NGT Regulation (COM(2023) 411):** The European Commission proposed in July 2023 a regulation on plants obtained by new genomic techniques, which would create two categories: Category 1 NGT plants (equivalent to conventional plants) would be exempt from GMO rules, while Category 2 NGT plants would remain subject to modified requirements. The proposal represents a potential paradigm shift in EU biotechnology regulation.

### Cartagena Protocol on Biosafety (2000)

The Cartagena Protocol, adopted under the Convention on Biological Diversity, establishes an international regulatory framework for the transboundary movement of living modified organisms (LMOs). Key provisions:

- **Article 7-10 (Advance Informed Agreement):** First-time intentional transboundary movements of LMOs for introduction into the environment require advance informed agreement from the importing Party.
- **Article 11 (LMOs for Food, Feed, or Processing):** A separate, less stringent procedure for LMOs intended for direct use as food, feed, or for processing.
- **Article 15 (Risk Assessment):** Risk assessments must be conducted on a case-by-case basis following Annex III criteria.

The Protocol's application to synthetic biology products that may not fit the traditional LMO definition (e.g., cell-free systems, synthetic organisms with no natural counterpart) is a developing area of international law. The Ad Hoc Technical Expert Group on Synthetic Biology, established under the CBD, has addressed these questions in multiple reports.

### Nagoya Protocol on Access and Benefit-Sharing (2010)

The Nagoya Protocol establishes obligations regarding access to genetic resources and the fair and equitable sharing of benefits arising from their utilization. Synthetic biology raises significant issues under the Protocol, as digital sequence information (DSI) derived from genetic resources may be used to synthesize biological materials without physical access to the original resource, potentially circumventing the Protocol's access and benefit-sharing requirements.

**CBD Decision 15/9 (2022):** The fifteenth Conference of the Parties to the CBD established a multilateral mechanism for benefit-sharing from the use of digital sequence information on genetic resources, acknowledging that DSI is essential for synthetic biology and other research while requiring fair benefit-sharing arrangements.

### EU Biosecurity

The EU CBRN (Chemical, Biological, Radiological, Nuclear) Action Plan, updated in 2023, addresses biosecurity risks from synthetic biology, including the potential for bioterrorism using synthetically produced pathogens. The Action Plan calls for strengthened screening of gene synthesis orders, enhanced laboratory security, and international cooperation on biosafety norms.

## Comparative Analysis

### Process-Based vs. Product-Based Regulation

The fundamental regulatory divergence concerns whether biotechnology regulation should focus on the process used (genetic modification techniques) or the characteristics of the final product. The EU and South Korea follow a process-based approach: if modern biotechnology techniques were used, the organism is subject to GMO/LMO regulations regardless of the outcome. The United States follows a product-based approach: the organism is regulated based on its characteristics and intended use, not the process used to create it. This divergence has widened with the CJEU's Confedération paysanne decision and the U.S. SECURE Rule.

### Gene Editing Treatment

The treatment of gene-edited organisms (particularly SDN-1 edits that could also occur naturally) is the most consequential current divergence. The United States (SECURE Rule) and several other countries have exempted gene-edited organisms without foreign DNA from GMO regulation. The EU (post-C-528/16) applies full GMO rules, though the proposed NGT Regulation would partially align the EU with the U.S. approach for certain categories. South Korea is in a transitional period, with potential exemptions under consideration.

### Gene Synthesis Oversight

The United States has developed the most comprehensive gene synthesis screening framework (HHS Screening Framework), though it remains largely voluntary except for federally funded research. Germany and South Korea rely on general biosafety and biosecurity laws without gene-synthesis-specific oversight. The EU lacks a harmonized gene synthesis screening framework, though individual member states are developing national approaches.

### Biosecurity Governance

The United States has the most developed dual-use research governance system (NSABB, DURC policy, P3CO Framework), reflecting both the breadth of U.S. biological research and the national security emphasis in U.S. science policy. Germany's biosecurity governance is integrated into the EU CBRN framework and domestic biosafety law. South Korea's biosecurity framework is developing but less mature.

### International Coordination

The Cartagena Protocol and Nagoya Protocol provide the primary international frameworks, but their application to synthetic biology remains uncertain. The divergence between process-based (EU) and product-based (US) regulatory philosophies creates significant challenges for international harmonization and trade in synthetic biology products.
