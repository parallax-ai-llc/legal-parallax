## Summary

Endangered species protection law -- the regulatory framework governing the identification, conservation, and recovery of species at risk of extinction -- has become increasingly urgent as the global biodiversity crisis intensifies, with the Intergovernmental Science-Policy Platform on Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services (IPBES) estimating that approximately one million species face extinction within decades. South Korea protects endangered species through the Wildlife Protection and Management Act (야생생물 보호 및 관리에 관한 법률), which classifies endangered species into two categories -- Class I (멸종위기 야생생물 I급, 68 species critically threatened) and Class II (멸종위기 야생생물 II급, 199 species vulnerable), administered by the Ministry of Environment, supplemented by the Natural Environment Conservation Act (자연환경보전법) establishing ecological corridors and the Act on Conservation and Use of Biodiversity (생물다양성 보전 및 이용에 관한 법률) implementing the Convention on Biological Diversity. The United States enacted the Endangered Species Act of 1973 (ESA, 16 U.S.C. Sections 1531-1544), widely regarded as the world's strongest species protection statute, which prohibits the "take" of any listed species (Section 9), requires federal agencies to ensure their actions do not jeopardize listed species or destroy critical habitat (Section 7), and mandates recovery plans for all listed species, currently protecting over 1,680 domestic species. Germany protects endangered species through the Bundesnaturschutzgesetz (BNatSchG, Federal Nature Conservation Act) implementing the EU Habitats Directive (92/43/EEC) and Birds Directive (2009/147/EC), which together establish the Natura 2000 network covering approximately 18 percent of the EU's land area, with strict species protection provisions under Section 44 BNatSchG and the CJEU actively enforcing member state obligations through infringement proceedings.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's endangered species protection is governed primarily by the Wildlife Protection and Management Act (야생생물 보호 및 관리에 관한 법률, Act No. 19953), which establishes the classification system, protection prohibitions, and conservation measures. The Act is supplemented by the Natural Environment Conservation Act (자연환경보전법) governing habitat protection and the Act on Conservation and Use of Biodiversity (생물다양성 보전 및 이용에 관한 법률, 2012) implementing the Nagoya Protocol on access and benefit-sharing.

### Classification System

| Category | Criteria | Species Count | Examples |
|----------|---------|---------------|----------|
| Class I (멸종위기 야생생물 I급) | Species facing imminent risk of extinction | 68 | Korean tiger (한국호랑이), Amur leopard (아무르표범), white-naped crane |
| Class II (멸종위기 야생생물 II급) | Species with significantly declining populations | 199 | Asiatic black bear (반달가슴곰), narrow-mouthed toad, Korean goral |

The Ministry of Environment reviews and updates the list every five years based on population surveys, habitat assessments, and expert evaluation.

### Protection Measures

- **Prohibition on take:** Capture, killing, collecting, or transporting endangered species is prohibited (Article 14)
- **Habitat protection:** Wildlife protection areas (야생생물 보호구역) may be designated by the Minister of Environment or local government heads
- **Restoration programs:** Active species restoration programs include the Asiatic black bear reintroduction in Jirisan National Park (since 2004, population exceeding 70 individuals by 2023) and the restoration of the crested ibis (따오기) breeding program in Upo Wetland

### CITES Implementation

South Korea implements the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species (CITES) through the Wildlife Protection and Management Act, controlling import, export, and trade of species listed in the three CITES Appendices. Korea Customs Service conducts border enforcement.

### Challenges

Korea's rapid urbanization and infrastructure development create persistent tension with species protection. The construction of the Four Major Rivers Project damaged riparian habitats. Ongoing highway and rail projects require environmental impact assessments under the Environmental Impact Assessment Act (환경영향평가법), but critics argue that mitigation measures are often insufficient and poorly enforced.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The Endangered Species Act of 1973 (ESA) is administered jointly by the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service (FWS, Department of the Interior) for terrestrial and freshwater species and the National Marine Fisheries Service (NMFS, Department of Commerce) for marine and anadromous species.

### Listing Process

Species are listed as either "endangered" (in danger of extinction throughout all or a significant portion of its range) or "threatened" (likely to become endangered in the foreseeable future). Listing decisions must be based "solely on the basis of the best scientific and commercial data available" (Section 4(b)(1)(A)), without consideration of economic impacts. Critical habitat must be designated concurrently with listing to the maximum extent "prudent and determinable."

### Section 7 -- Interagency Consultation

Federal agencies must consult with FWS/NMFS to ensure that any action they authorize, fund, or carry out is not likely to "jeopardize the continued existence" of a listed species or result in "destruction or adverse modification" of critical habitat. The consultation produces a Biological Opinion (BiOp) that may include an Incidental Take Statement authorizing limited take that is incidental to otherwise lawful activity.

### Section 9 -- Take Prohibition

The ESA defines "take" broadly to include "harass, harm, pursue, hunt, shoot, wound, kill, trap, capture, or collect, or to attempt to engage in any such conduct." The FWS regulation further defines "harm" to include significant habitat modification that kills or injures wildlife (50 C.F.R. 17.3).

### Landmark Cases

**Tennessee Valley Authority v. Hill, 437 U.S. 153 (1978):** The Supreme Court halted construction of the nearly completed Tellico Dam to protect the snail darter, holding that the ESA's language was "the most comprehensive legislation for the preservation of endangered species ever enacted" and that courts had no discretion to balance economic costs against species preservation.

**Babbitt v. Sweet Home Chapter of Communities for a Great Oregon, 515 U.S. 687 (1995):** The Court upheld the FWS definition of "harm" as including significant habitat modification, affirming the broad scope of the take prohibition.

### Habitat Conservation Plans

Section 10 allows non-federal landowners to obtain Incidental Take Permits (ITPs) through Habitat Conservation Plans (HCPs), which require the applicant to minimize and mitigate the impacts of the taking "to the maximum extent practicable." Over 1,000 HCPs have been approved since the program's inception.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany protects endangered species through the Bundesnaturschutzgesetz (BNatSchG), which implements the EU Habitats Directive (92/43/EEC) and Birds Directive (2009/147/EC). Section 44 BNatSchG establishes the strict species protection regime (strenger Artenschutz), prohibiting the killing, capture, or disturbance of specially protected species and the destruction of their breeding sites and resting places.

### Natura 2000 in Germany

Germany has designated approximately 5,200 Natura 2000 sites covering about 15.5 percent of its land area and significant marine areas. These include:

| Network | Directive | Number of Sites |
|---------|-----------|----------------|
| Special Areas of Conservation (SAC / FFH-Gebiete) | Habitats Directive | ~4,600 |
| Special Protection Areas (SPA / Vogelschutzgebiete) | Birds Directive | ~740 |

### Section 44 BNatSchG -- Strict Protection

The strict species protection provisions prohibit:
1. Killing, injuring, or capturing specially protected animals
2. Significant disturbance of strictly protected species during breeding, rearing, or migration
3. Removing, damaging, or destroying breeding sites or resting places
4. Removing, damaging, or destroying specially protected plants

### Renewable Energy and Species Protection Conflict

The tension between renewable energy expansion (particularly wind energy) and species protection has been one of Germany's most contentious environmental law debates. In 2022, the Bundestag enacted Section 45b BNatSchG, specifically addressing wind energy and species protection, establishing a standardized methodology for assessing collision risks to birds and bats, with defined significance thresholds and mitigation measures. This legislation was accompanied by the designation of wind energy as a matter of "overriding public interest" (überragendes öffentliches Interesse) under Section 2 EEG 2023.

### Red List

The Rote Liste (Red List) of threatened species in Germany, compiled by the Federal Agency for Nature Conservation (Bundesamt für Naturschutz, BfN), provides the scientific basis for conservation priorities. The latest assessment (2020-2021) found that approximately 31 percent of evaluated animal species and 27 percent of plant species are threatened.

## ECHR & EU

### Habitats Directive (92/43/EEC)

The Habitats Directive is the cornerstone of EU biodiversity protection:

**Annex II:** Lists approximately 900 species whose conservation requires the designation of Special Areas of Conservation.

**Annex IV:** Lists approximately 500 species requiring strict protection throughout their natural range under Article 12 (animals) and Article 13 (plants).

**Article 6 -- Appropriate Assessment:** Any plan or project likely to have a significant effect on a Natura 2000 site must undergo an appropriate assessment of its implications for the site's conservation objectives. The plan may only be approved if it will not adversely affect the integrity of the site, unless there are imperative reasons of overriding public interest (IROPI) and compensatory measures are secured.

### Birds Directive (2009/147/EC)

The Birds Directive protects all wild bird species naturally occurring in the EU (approximately 500 species):
- **Article 5:** Prohibits deliberate killing, capture, destruction of nests and eggs, disturbance, and keeping
- **Article 4:** Requires designation of Special Protection Areas for listed species

### CJEU Enforcement

The CJEU has actively enforced the nature directives:

**Commission v. Ireland (C-418/04, 2007):** Ireland found in breach of the Habitats Directive for systematic failure to designate sufficient SACs and implement appropriate assessment requirements.

**Commission v. Poland (C-441/17, 2018) (Białowieża Forest):** The Court issued interim measures requiring Poland to immediately cease logging in the Białowieża Forest, finding that the logging operations likely violated the Habitats and Birds Directives. Poland was fined EUR 100,000 per day for initial non-compliance.

**Sweetman (C-258/11, 2013):** Established that the appropriate assessment must exclude all reasonable scientific doubt as to the absence of adverse effects on the site's integrity.

### EU Biodiversity Strategy 2030

The EU Biodiversity Strategy for 2030 sets targets including legally protecting at least 30 percent of EU land and 30 percent of EU seas, with one-third of protected areas under strict protection. The Nature Restoration Regulation (EU) 2024/1991, adopted on June 17, 2024, establishes legally binding restoration targets for degraded ecosystems.

### Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework

The GBF, adopted at COP 15 in December 2022, establishes global targets including the "30x30" target (30 percent of land and ocean under effective conservation by 2030). The EU was instrumental in its adoption and committed to implementing all targets.

### ECHR and Biodiversity

While no ECHR case has directly addressed endangered species, the Court has recognized environmental degradation as affecting rights under Articles 2 and 8, and the evolving positive obligations doctrine could potentially extend to biodiversity loss that significantly affects human well-being.

## Comparative Analysis

### Strength of Protections

The U.S. ESA provides the strongest individual species protections, with its absolute prohibition on federal actions that jeopardize listed species (TVA v. Hill established there is no economic balancing). The EU Habitats Directive provides strong but not absolute protection, allowing derogations for imperative reasons of overriding public interest. South Korea's protections are substantial on paper but enforcement has been criticized as inadequate.

### Habitat Protection Approaches

The EU's Natura 2000 network provides the most systematic approach to habitat protection through site designation. The U.S. critical habitat system is species-specific rather than site-based, potentially leaving gaps. South Korea's wildlife protection areas are smaller and less systematically designated.

### Development-Conservation Balance

The tension between development and conservation is managed differently. Germany's 2022 legislation explicitly balancing wind energy against species protection represents a legislative resolution. The U.S. Section 7 consultation process and Section 10 HCPs provide project-level mechanisms. South Korea relies primarily on environmental impact assessment, which critics argue favors development.

### International Framework

CITES provides the primary international framework for trade-related species protection, implemented by all four jurisdictions. The Kunming-Montreal GBF establishes ambitious targets but lacks binding enforcement mechanisms, unlike the EU's Nature Restoration Regulation which creates binding domestic obligations.
