## Summary

Impeachment -- the constitutional mechanism for removing a head of state or other senior public official from office for serious misconduct, typically through a legislative vote of accusation followed by a trial -- represents one of the most consequential checks on executive power, operating at the intersection of constitutional law, criminal law, and democratic governance. South Korea's impeachment process, established under Articles 65 and 111 of the Constitution and the Constitutional Court Act (헌법재판소법), requires a motion supported by a majority of National Assembly members (two-thirds for the President), adjudicated by the Constitutional Court (헌법재판소) with a six-justice supermajority required for removal, and has been exercised twice against sitting presidents: the unsuccessful impeachment of President Roh Moo-hyun in 2004 (Constitutional Court Decision 2004HunNa1) and the successful impeachment and removal of President Park Geun-hye in 2017 (Constitutional Court Decision 2016HunNa1), with the latter case becoming a landmark in global constitutional law for its demonstration that a sitting president can be peacefully removed through institutional mechanisms. The United States Constitution establishes impeachment in Articles I and II, granting the House of Representatives the sole power of impeachment (Article I, Section 2, Clause 5) and the Senate the sole power to try impeachments (Article I, Section 3, Clauses 6-7), with a two-thirds Senate supermajority required for conviction, applicable for "Treason, Bribery, or other high Crimes and Misdemeanors" (Article II, Section 4), and exercised against presidents Andrew Johnson (1868, acquitted), Bill Clinton (1998, acquitted), and Donald Trump twice (2019, acquitted; 2021, acquitted). Germany does not use the term "impeachment" but provides for presidential removal through the Präsidentenanklage under Article 61 of the Grundgesetz, with the Bundestag or Bundesrat bringing charges before the Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht, BVerfG) for willful violation of the Basic Law or other federal law, a mechanism never used in the Federal Republic's history due to the ceremonial nature of the German presidency, while the EU framework addresses the accountability of EU institution leaders through removal procedures in the Treaties, and the ECHR's jurisprudence on fair trial rights (Article 6), freedom of expression (Article 10), and electoral rights (Article 3 of Protocol No. 1) has established that impeachment proceedings must respect fundamental procedural guarantees.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's impeachment mechanism is distinctive for vesting adjudicatory power in the Constitutional Court rather than the legislature itself.

**Article 65 Constitution (Impeachment):**

| Provision | Content |
|-----------|---------|
| Article 65(1) | Eligible officials: President, Prime Minister, members of the State Council, heads of Executive Ministries, justices of the Constitutional Court, judges, members of the NEC, Chairman and members of the Board of Audit and Inspection, and other officials designated by law |
| Article 65(2) | Motion: proposed by 1/3+ of National Assembly members; adopted by majority (President: proposed by majority; adopted by 2/3) |
| Article 65(3) | Suspension: person against whom impeachment motion is adopted is suspended from exercising power until judgment |
| Article 65(4) | Effect: removal from office; does not exempt from civil or criminal liability |

**Constitutional Court Act (헌법재판소법):**
- Articles 48-54 govern impeachment trial procedures
- Adjudication by full bench (9 justices); 6+ votes required for removal
- Oral arguments; evidence examination; witness testimony
- No time limit prescribed, but the Court has consistently prioritized impeachment cases

### Roh Moo-hyun Impeachment (2004)

**Constitutional Court Decision 2004HunNa1 (May 14, 2004):**

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Date of NA vote | March 12, 2004 (193-2, with opposition majority) |
| Grounds alleged | Illegal electioneering; violations of Political Funds Act; incompetence |
| Court decision | Impeachment dismissed; President reinstated |
| Reasoning | While the President violated election law by making partisan statements, the violations were not sufficiently grave to warrant removal; proportionality required that removal be reserved for "grave" violations |
| Significance | Established the "gravity" standard: not all legal violations warrant removal; the seriousness of the violation must be proportionate to the extreme consequence of removal |

### Park Geun-hye Impeachment (2016-2017)

**Constitutional Court Decision 2016HunNa1 (March 10, 2017):**

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Date of NA vote | December 9, 2016 (234-56) |
| Grounds | Abuse of presidential authority; violation of Constitution; corruption involving Choi Soon-sil |
| Court decision | Impeachment upheld; President removed from office |
| Vote | 8-0 (unanimous; 9th seat vacant) |
| Key findings | President violated duty of confidentiality by sharing classified documents with Choi Soon-sil; abused presidential authority by coercing corporations to donate to foundations controlled by Choi; failed to protect citizens' right to life during Sewol ferry disaster; concealed facts from the public and public officials |
| Significance | First successful presidential impeachment in Korean history; demonstrated peaceful democratic transfer of power through constitutional mechanisms |

**Aftermath:**
- Snap presidential election held May 9, 2017 (Moon Jae-in elected)
- Park Geun-hye subsequently convicted of bribery, abuse of power, and coercion; sentenced to 20 years (later increased to 25 years on appeal; pardoned December 31, 2021)
- Choi Soon-sil convicted and sentenced to 20 years (reduced to 18 on appeal)

### Yoon Suk-yeol Impeachment (2024)

President Yoon Suk-yeol was impeached by the National Assembly on December 14, 2024, following his brief declaration of martial law on December 3, 2024. The Constitutional Court proceedings were ongoing as of early 2026, with the President suspended from duties and the Prime Minister serving as acting President.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The U.S. Constitution establishes a two-stage impeachment process: House impeachment (accusation) followed by Senate trial.

**Constitutional Provisions:**

| Provision | Content |
|-----------|---------|
| Art. I, § 2, cl. 5 | "The House of Representatives . . . shall have the sole Power of Impeachment" |
| Art. I, § 3, cl. 6 | "The Senate shall have the sole Power to try all Impeachments . . . no Person shall be convicted without the Concurrence of two thirds of the Members present" |
| Art. I, § 3, cl. 7 | Judgment: removal from office + possible disqualification from future office; no further legal consequences |
| Art. II, § 4 | "The President, Vice President and all civil Officers of the United States, shall be removed from Office on Impeachment for, and Conviction of, Treason, Bribery, or other high Crimes and Misdemeanors" |
| Art. II, § 2, cl. 1 | President's pardon power does not extend to cases of impeachment |

### Presidential Impeachments

**Andrew Johnson (1868):**
- Impeached by the House (February 24, 1868) for violating the Tenure of Office Act by removing Secretary of War Edwin Stanton
- Senate trial: acquitted 35-19 (one vote short of 2/3 majority)
- Significance: Established limits on Congressional power to control executive personnel

**Richard Nixon (1974):**
- House Judiciary Committee approved three articles of impeachment (July 1974) for obstruction of justice, abuse of power, and contempt of Congress (Watergate)
- Nixon resigned August 9, 1974, before full House vote
- Significance: Demonstrated that impeachment threat can achieve accountability without formal trial

**Bill Clinton (1998-1999):**
- Impeached by the House (December 19, 1998) on two articles: perjury before a grand jury and obstruction of justice (related to Monica Lewinsky scandal)
- Senate trial: acquitted on both articles (45-55 on perjury; 50-50 on obstruction; 67 required)
- Significance: Highlighted debate over whether personal misconduct constitutes "high Crimes and Misdemeanors"

**Donald Trump -- First Impeachment (2019-2020):**
- Impeached by the House (December 18, 2019) on two articles: abuse of power and obstruction of Congress (related to Ukraine pressure campaign)
- Senate trial: acquitted on both articles (48-52 on abuse of power; 47-53 on obstruction)
- Senator Mitt Romney voted to convict on abuse of power -- the first time in history a senator voted to convict a president of their own party

**Donald Trump -- Second Impeachment (2021):**
- Impeached by the House (January 13, 2021) on one article: incitement of insurrection (January 6, 2021 Capitol attack)
- Senate trial: acquitted 57-43 (10 votes short of 67 required)
- 7 Republican senators voted to convict
- Significance: First president impeached twice; raised question of whether a former president can be tried (Senate determined yes, 56-44)

### "High Crimes and Misdemeanors"

The meaning of "high Crimes and Misdemeanors" remains debated:
- **Broad view (Hamilton, Federalist No. 65):** "Those offences which proceed from the misconduct of public men, or, in other words, from the abuse or violation of some public trust"
- **Narrow view:** Requires indictable criminal conduct
- **Historical practice:** Both criminal conduct (Trump: incitement of insurrection) and non-criminal abuse of power (Trump: Ukraine; Johnson: Tenure of Office Act violation) have been charged

### Justiciability

**Nixon v. United States, 506 U.S. 224 (1993):** The Supreme Court held that the Senate's authority to "try" impeachments is a textually demonstrable constitutional commitment to the Senate, making challenges to Senate trial procedures nonjusticiable political questions.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's presidential impeachment mechanism (Präsidentenanklage) has never been used, reflecting the largely ceremonial role of the German presidency.

**Article 61 Grundgesetz:**

| Provision | Content |
|-----------|---------|
| Article 61(1) | "The Bundestag or the Bundesrat may impeach the Federal President before the Federal Constitutional Court for willful violation of this Basic Law or of any other federal law" |
| Article 61(1) | Motion requires 1/4 of Bundestag or Bundesrat members; resolution requires 2/3 majority |
| Article 61(2) | BVerfG may issue interim order suspending the President from duties |
| Article 61(2) | If found guilty, BVerfG may declare the President forfeited of office |

**Article 98(2) GG -- Judicial Impeachment:**
Federal judges who infringe upon the principles of the Basic Law or the constitutional order of a Land may be transferred or retired upon application of the Bundestag to the BVerfG. This provision has been used once (1950s, regarding a judge with Nazi-era background).

### Chancellor Accountability

The German system addresses executive accountability primarily through the constructive vote of no confidence (Article 67 GG) rather than impeachment:
- The Bundestag may express its lack of confidence in the Federal Chancellor only by electing a successor by majority vote and requesting the President to dismiss the incumbent Chancellor
- This mechanism was successfully used once: Helmut Kohl replaced Helmut Schmidt through a constructive vote of no confidence on October 1, 1982
- The constructive mechanism prevents purely negative votes of no confidence that could create governmental instability

### Ministerial Accountability

Federal ministers are not subject to impeachment. They serve at the Chancellor's pleasure (Article 64 GG) and can be dismissed by the President at the Chancellor's request. Parliamentary accountability operates through:
- Question time (Fragestunde)
- Investigative committees (Untersuchungsausschüsse, Article 44 GG)
- Vote of no confidence in the Chancellor (which removes the entire government)

### Comparison with Parliamentary Systems

Germany's approach reflects the broader pattern in parliamentary systems:
- **Parliamentary systems:** Executive accountability primarily through confidence votes; impeachment reserved for heads of state
- **Presidential systems (South Korea, United States):** Impeachment is the primary accountability mechanism for the executive, as the president cannot be removed through a vote of no confidence

## ECHR / EU

### EU Accountability Mechanisms

**European Commission:**
- **Motion of censure (Article 234 TFEU):** The European Parliament may adopt a motion of censure against the Commission by two-thirds of votes cast representing a majority of MEPs. If adopted, all Commissioners must resign. Never successfully adopted (motion against Santer Commission in 1999 failed, but Commission resigned preemptively).
- **Individual Commissioner removal (Article 247 TFEU):** A Commissioner who "no longer fulfils the conditions required for the performance of his duties or if he has been guilty of serious misconduct" may be compulsorily retired by the CJEU on application of the Council (acting by simple majority) or the Commission. Used against Edith Cresson in 2006 (Case C-432/04, CJEU found misconduct but declined to deprive pension rights due to elapsed time).

**CJEU Judges and Advocates General:**
- May be removed by unanimous decision of other judges and advocates general that they "no longer fulfil the requisite conditions or meet the obligations arising from their office" (Article 6 of the Statute of the CJEU). Never used.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

The ECHR has addressed impeachment-related issues in several important cases:

**Paksas v. Lithuania (Application No. 34932/04, Grand Chamber, January 6, 2011):** The Court found a violation of Article 3 of Protocol No. 1 (right to stand for election) where Lithuanian law permanently barred former President Paksas from standing for election following his impeachment and removal for serious constitutional violations. The Court held that the permanent and irreversible nature of the disqualification was disproportionate, noting that even the gravity of the constitutional violations did not justify a lifelong ban on electoral participation.

**Selahattin Demirtaş v. Turkey (No. 2) (Application No. 14305/17, Grand Chamber, December 22, 2020):** While not technically an impeachment case, the Court addressed the lifting of parliamentary immunity and criminal prosecution of a political leader (HDP co-chair), finding violations of Articles 5, 10, and 18 of the Convention and Article 3 of Protocol No. 1. The judgment established that the use of criminal proceedings to silence political opposition undermines democratic pluralism.

**Merabishvili v. Georgia (Application No. 72508/13, Grand Chamber, November 28, 2017):** The Court addressed the detention of a former prime minister, finding a violation of Article 18 (limitation on use of restrictions on rights) taken in conjunction with Article 5, establishing the framework for assessing whether criminal proceedings against political figures serve ulterior political purposes.

### Standards for Impeachment Proceedings

The ECHR jurisprudence establishes several principles applicable to impeachment:

1. **Fair trial (Article 6):** While impeachment is primarily a political process, where it involves determination of civil rights or criminal charges, Article 6 guarantees apply. The Court has held that parliamentary proceedings with quasi-judicial character must afford adequate procedural safeguards.

2. **Freedom of expression (Article 10):** Impeachment of officials for protected political speech raises Article 10 concerns. The boundary between legitimate political accountability and punishment for expression must be carefully drawn.

3. **Electoral rights (Article 3, Protocol No. 1):** Post-impeachment disqualification from office must be proportionate (Paksas v. Lithuania). Permanent, irrevocable bans are presumptively disproportionate.

4. **Non-abuse (Article 18):** Impeachment used as a tool to eliminate political opponents rather than to address genuine misconduct may violate Article 18.

## Comparative Analysis

### Institutional Design

The four jurisdictions reflect three distinct models of impeachment adjudication:

**Constitutional Court model (South Korea, Germany):** A specialized constitutional court adjudicates impeachment, applying constitutional and legal standards. This model separates the political act of accusation from the legal act of adjudication, providing the greatest procedural independence.

**Legislative trial model (United States):** The Senate serves as both political body and trier of fact, with the Chief Justice presiding in presidential trials. This model makes impeachment inherently political, as senators are both jurors and political actors.

**EU hybrid model:** Different mechanisms for different officials (parliamentary censure for Commission; CJEU adjudication for individual Commissioners and judges).

### Grounds for Removal

| Jurisdiction | Standard | Breadth |
|-------------|----------|---------|
| South Korea | "Violation of Constitution or law in performance of duties" | Broad; any legal violation (but "gravity" required per 2004 precedent) |
| United States | "Treason, Bribery, or other high Crimes and Misdemeanors" | Debated; includes both criminal and political misconduct |
| Germany | "Willful violation of the Basic Law or other federal law" | Narrow; requires willfulness (Vorsatz) |
| ECHR | Proportionality of removal and consequences | Framework for evaluating state action |

### Suspension Upon Impeachment

A critical procedural difference:
- **South Korea:** Automatic suspension upon National Assembly vote (before trial)
- **United States:** No automatic suspension; President remains in office during trial
- **Germany:** Court may order suspension (discretionary)

This difference has profound practical implications: in South Korea, the impeachment vote itself removes the President from daily governance, while in the United States, the President continues to exercise full powers during the trial.

### Frequency and Effectiveness

| Jurisdiction | Uses (Head of State) | Successful Removals | Assessment |
|-------------|---------------------|---------------------|------------|
| South Korea | 3 (2004, 2017, 2024) | 1 (2017; 2024 pending) | Active; functional accountability mechanism |
| United States | 4 formal + 1 resignation | 0 convictions | Partisan polarization prevents conviction |
| Germany | 0 | 0 | Constructive no-confidence serves primary function |
| EU | 0 (Commission censure) | 0 (Santer resigned preemptively) | Institutional design limits use |

### Post-Impeachment Legal Consequences

The legal aftermath of impeachment varies significantly across jurisdictions:

- **South Korea:** A successful impeachment by the Constitutional Court results in immediate removal from office; the removed president may face criminal prosecution for underlying offenses. President Park Geun-hye was sentenced to 25 years imprisonment (later reduced to 22 years) on corruption charges; President Yoon Suk-yeol faces criminal prosecution for insurrection
- **United States:** Senate conviction results in removal and potential disqualification from future office (by separate vote); criminal prosecution in regular courts remains possible per the Double Jeopardy clause analysis; no former president has been criminally prosecuted following impeachment (Nixon received a presidential pardon)
- **Germany:** The BVerfG may declare forfeiture of presidential office; no criminal immunity after removal
- **ECHR:** In Paksas v. Lithuania (2011), the Grand Chamber held that a permanent and irreversible ban on holding parliamentary office following impeachment violated Article 3 of Protocol No. 1 (right to free elections), establishing proportionality limits on post-impeachment disqualification

### Impeachment and Democratic Stability

Impeachment mechanisms interact with democratic stability in complex ways:

- Frequent or partisan use of impeachment can destabilize government and undermine democratic legitimacy
- Insufficient impeachment mechanisms may allow authoritarian consolidation
- South Korea's experience demonstrates that a functional impeachment mechanism can strengthen democratic accountability while maintaining constitutional order
- The U.S. experience illustrates the risk that partisan polarization can render impeachment effectively inoperative as an accountability mechanism, regardless of the underlying conduct
