## Summary

Land reform is among the most consequential and contested areas of law in the Global South, where colonial and feudal land tenure systems have left enduring legacies of inequality, landlessness, and competing claims between state sovereignty, communal ownership, and individual title. China's collective land system, rooted in the revolutionary land reforms of 1949-1953 and the collectivization campaigns of the 1950s, now operates through a distinctive dual-track structure: urban land is state-owned (国有), while rural land is collectively owned (集体所有) by village collectives, with individual farmers holding contractual management rights (承包经营权) under the Rural Land Contract Law (2018 revision) and the Civil Code (2021, Book Two: Property Rights); the 2019 revision of the Land Administration Law (土地管理法) permitted the first direct market entry of collectively-owned construction land, a historic reform that partially broke the state monopoly on land supply. India's land reform history encompasses the zamindari abolition of the 1950s, tenancy reforms, land ceiling legislation (now enacted in virtually every state under the Land Acquisition, Rehabilitation and Resettlement Act 2013, or LARR), and the ongoing struggle over the Right to Fair Compensation and Transparency in Land Acquisition Act 2013, which replaced the colonial Land Acquisition Act of 1894 after decades of conflict over forced acquisition for industrial and infrastructure projects -- the failed attempt by the Modi government to amend LARR through ordinance in 2014-2015 demonstrated the political centrality of land rights. South Africa's post-apartheid land reform program, constitutionally mandated under section 25 of the 1996 Constitution (the "property clause"), operates through three pillars -- restitution (Restitution of Land Rights Act 22 of 1994), redistribution (various programs), and tenure reform (Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997) -- but has delivered only approximately 10% of white-owned commercial farmland to Black South Africans after three decades, prompting the ANC to pursue constitutional amendment to allow expropriation without compensation (the Expropriation Act 13 of 2024, which codifies expropriation at nil compensation in specified circumstances). Brazil's agrarian reform framework, centered on the Land Statute (Estatuto da Terra, Lei No. 4.504/1964) and Articles 184-186 of the 1988 Constitution, ties land reform to the "social function of property" (função social da propriedade) -- productive land that fulfills its social function is constitutionally protected from expropriation, while unproductive latifúndios may be expropriated with compensation in agrarian debt bonds; the Movimento dos Trabalhadores Rurais Sem Terra (MST) has used direct land occupation as a strategy to pressure redistribution, with the STF addressing the constitutionality of these occupations in multiple decisions. The Philippines' Comprehensive Agrarian Reform Program (CARP), established by Republic Act No. 6657 (1988) and extended by RA 9700 (2009), distributes agricultural land to tenant farmers and landless workers through a combination of voluntary offer to sell, compulsory acquisition, and voluntary land transfer, with the Department of Agrarian Reform administering the program and the Supreme Court adjudicating valuation disputes -- as of 2025, the DAR reports over 4.7 million hectares distributed to approximately 2.8 million agrarian reform beneficiaries; Indonesia's Basic Agrarian Law (Undang-Undang Pokok Agraria, UUPA No. 5 of 1960) sought to unify the colonial dual land system (Western/adat) and establish "social function" (fungsi sosial) as a constraint on property rights, while mandating land redistribution -- however, implementation was interrupted by the political upheaval of 1965-1966 and has never been fully realized, with land inequality persisting particularly in Java, Sumatra, and Kalimantan where plantation concessions (Hak Guna Usaha) cover millions of hectares.

## China

### The Collective Land System

China's land tenure system is the product of revolutionary land reform (1949-1953), collectivization (1953-1958), communization (1958-1978), and the post-Mao reforms (1978-present).

**Constitutional Foundation:**
- **Article 6:** Socialist public ownership of the means of production, including state ownership (全民所有制) and collective ownership (集体所有制).
- **Article 8:** Rural collective economic organizations practice a dual-tier management system combining unified and separate operations on the basis of the household contract responsibility system (家庭联产承包责任制).
- **Article 10:** Urban land belongs to the state. Rural and suburban land belongs to collectives, except for portions that belong to the state as provided by law. Homestead sites and private plots belong to collectives.

**The Household Responsibility System (家庭联产承包责任制):** Introduced in 1978 beginning in Xiaogang Village, Anhui Province, this system allocated collective farmland to individual households under contracts. The system revolutionized agricultural productivity while maintaining collective ownership. The Rural Land Contract Law (农村土地承包法, originally 2002, revised 2018) codifies the system:

- **Article 5:** Rural collective economic organizations exercise ownership of collectively-owned land as members of the collective.
- **Article 22:** Contract periods: 30 years for arable land (耕地), 30-50 years for grassland (草地), 30-70 years for forest land (林地).
- **2018 Revision (Article 24):** The state protects the long-term stability of rural land contractual relations. Upon expiration of arable land contracts, the contract shall be extended for another 30 years (guaranteeing rights through approximately 2057).

**The "Three Rights Separation" Reform (三权分置):** In 2016, the CPC Central Committee and State Council issued the "Opinion on Improving the Separation of Ownership Rights, Contract Rights, and Management Rights for Rural Land" (完善农村土地所有权承包权经营权分置办法的意见). This policy distinguishes:
1. **Ownership (所有权):** Held by the village collective
2. **Contract Right (承包权):** Held by the contracted household (cannot be transferred outside the collective)
3. **Management Right (经营权):** Can be transferred, leased, or used as collateral for financing, enabling land to be consolidated for more efficient farming

The Civil Code (2021, Book Two, Chapter 11) codifies this framework, with Article 339 permitting the management right holder to engage in agricultural production within the contract period.

### Land Administration Law Reform (2019)

The 2019 revision of the Land Administration Law (土地管理法) was a watershed:

**Collectively-Owned Construction Land Market Entry (集体经营性建设用地入市):** Article 63 (revised) permits collectively-owned construction land (集体经营性建设用地) to enter the market directly through sale, lease, or equity participation, provided the land conforms to the land use plan and has been registered. Previously, all land for construction purposes had to be first converted to state-owned land through requisition -- creating a monopoly that generated massive local government revenue and widespread farmer grievances over inadequate compensation.

**Compensation for Land Requisition (征地补偿):** Article 48 (revised) requires comprehensive compensation that ensures the living standards of land-requisitioned farmers are not lowered and their long-term livelihood is secured. Compensation includes: land compensation fees (土地补偿费), resettlement subsidies (安置补助费), compensation for above-ground attachments and young crops, and social security costs for land-requisitioned farmers.

### Conflict: Urbanization and Forced Requisition

China's urbanization process has generated enormous conflict over collective land requisition. Between 2000 and 2020, approximately 70 million rural residents lost land to urbanization. Compensation disputes, "nail households" (钉子户) who refuse to relocate, and mass incidents (群体性事件) related to land requisition have been a major source of social instability. The 2019 reforms were explicitly designed to address these grievances, though implementation remains uneven.

## India

### Historical Land Reform

India's land reform program, the most legislatively ambitious in any democracy, unfolded in three phases:

**Zamindari Abolition (1950s):** The First Amendment to the Constitution (1951) inserted Articles 31A and 31B, protecting land reform legislation from challenge under fundamental rights. Every state enacted zamindari abolition laws, eliminating the feudal intermediary system where zamindars (landlords) collected revenue on behalf of the colonial state. The Supreme Court in **Kameshwar Singh v. State of Bihar, AIR 1952 SC 252**, upheld zamindari abolition, establishing the principle that land reform was a constitutionally valid objective.

**Tenancy Reforms (1950s-1970s):** States enacted legislation to protect tenant cultivators, regulate rents, and in some cases vest ownership in tenants. The most successful was Kerala's Land Reforms Act 1963 (as amended 1969), which abolished landlordism and vested ownership in over 1.5 million tenants -- Kerala's act was placed in the Ninth Schedule to protect it from judicial review.

**Land Ceiling Legislation:** Every state enacted ceiling laws limiting individual landholdings. The ceiling varied by state and land type (irrigated, non-irrigated, etc.). Surplus land was distributed to landless laborers and marginal farmers. However, enforcement was systematically undermined by: benami (fictitious name) transfers to family members and associates, reclassification of land, litigation delays, and political resistance. The P.S. Appu Committee (1972) and subsequent studies found that only a small fraction of potential surplus land was actually redistributed.

### Land Acquisition: From 1894 to 2013

The colonial **Land Acquisition Act, 1894** (LAA) allowed the state to acquire any land for "public purpose" with compensation at market value. The Act's vague "public purpose" definition and inadequate compensation provisions enabled what critics termed "accumulation by dispossession" -- acquisition of farmland at agricultural rates for industrial, mining, and infrastructure projects that generated enormous profits for developers.

Landmark conflicts included:
- **Singur (West Bengal, 2006-2008):** Acquisition of 997 acres of multi-crop agricultural land for a Tata Motors Nano factory. The Supreme Court in **Singur Land Rehabilitation and Development Act case (2016)** upheld West Bengal's retrospective law returning land to farmers.
- **Nandigram (West Bengal, 2007):** Violent conflict over proposed acquisition for a special economic zone, resulting in deaths and political upheaval.
- **Posco (Odisha, 2005-2017):** A 12-year struggle over land acquisition for a Korean steel company's $12 billion project, which was ultimately abandoned.

**The Right to Fair Compensation and Transparency in Land Acquisition, Rehabilitation and Resettlement Act, 2013 (LARR):** Replaced the 1894 Act with significantly enhanced protections:
- **Social Impact Assessment (SIA):** Mandatory SIA before any acquisition, reviewed by an independent expert group
- **Consent Requirements:** 80% consent of affected families required for private projects; 70% for public-private partnerships (no consent required for pure public purpose projects)
- **Enhanced Compensation:** Market value multiplied by a factor of 1-2 (urban) or 2-4 (rural), plus a 100% solatium; total compensation typically 2-4 times market value
- **Food Security Safeguard:** Multi-crop irrigated land cannot be acquired except as a last resort, limited to 5% of such land in a district

**2014-2015 Ordinance Controversy:** The Modi government issued an ordinance in December 2014 amending LARR to: remove consent requirements for five categories of projects, exempt them from SIA, and dilute food security safeguards. After massive political opposition (including from within the BJP's coalition), the ordinance was allowed to lapse in 2015 -- a significant political defeat and affirmation of the LARR's protective framework.

### Forest Rights Act, 2006

The Scheduled Tribes and Other Traditional Forest Dwellers (Recognition of Forest Rights) Act, 2006 (FRA) recognized the rights of forest-dwelling communities to land they had occupied for generations but which had been classified as government forest land under colonial legislation. The Act provides individual forest rights (up to 4 hectares) and community forest rights (including the right to manage community forest resources). The Supreme Court's controversial 2019 order directing eviction of rejected claimants was stayed after widespread protest.

## Africa

### Nigeria: The Land Use Act

The **Land Use Act, 1978** (Cap L5, LFN 2004), enacted as a decree during military rule and subsequently entrenched in the 1999 Constitution (section 315), fundamentally restructured land tenure:

**Section 1:** All land comprised in the territory of each state is vested in the Governor of that state, who holds it in trust for the people.

**Section 5:** The Governor may grant statutory rights of occupancy (for urban land) and customary rights of occupancy (for rural land). Rights of occupancy are granted for a definite term (typically 99 years for statutory rights).

**Section 28:** The Governor may revoke a right of occupancy for overriding public interest, including: for public purposes, for mining, or for required roads, drainage, and other public infrastructure. Compensation is payable only for improvements on the land, not for the land itself -- a provision that has generated significant grievance.

**Critique:** The Land Use Act has been widely criticized for:
- Failing to resolve the tenure security of millions of customary landholders who have not obtained certificates of occupancy
- Enabling corruption and patronage in land allocation by governors
- Creating a cumbersome consent requirement (Governor's consent for land transactions) that increases costs and delays
- Undermining customary land rights while failing to deliver the intended benefits of state ownership (equitable distribution, efficient land use)

The Presidential Technical Committee on Land Reform (2009) recommended comprehensive reform, but constitutional entrenchment of the Land Use Act makes amendment politically extremely difficult.

### South Africa: The Property Clause and Land Reform

**Section 25 of the Constitution (Property Clause):**
- (1) No one may be deprived of property except in terms of law of general application, and no law may permit arbitrary deprivation.
- (2) Property may be expropriated only for a public purpose or in the public interest, and subject to compensation.
- (3) The amount of compensation must be just and equitable, reflecting an equitable balance between the public interest and the interests of those affected, with regard to: current use, history of acquisition, market value, extent of direct state investment, and purpose of expropriation.
- (5) The state must take reasonable legislative and other measures to enable equitable access to land.
- (6) A person or community dispossessed of property after 19 June 1913 as a result of past racially discriminatory laws or practices is entitled to restitution or equitable redress.

**Three Pillars of Land Reform:**

1. **Restitution (Restitution of Land Rights Act 22 of 1994):** Persons dispossessed of property after June 19, 1913 (the date of the Natives Land Act) due to racially discriminatory laws may lodge claims with the Land Claims Court. The Commission on Restitution of Land Rights processes claims, offering either restoration of the land or equitable compensation. Over 80,000 claims were lodged by the original 1998 deadline. The Restitution of Land Rights Amendment Act 15 of 2014 attempted to reopen the claims process, but the Constitutional Court in **Land Access Movement of South Africa v. Chairperson of the National Council of Provinces [2016] ZACC 22** declared the amendment invalid for insufficient public participation.

2. **Redistribution:** Various programs (Settlement/Land Acquisition Grant, Land Redistribution for Agricultural Development, Proactive Land Acquisition Strategy) aimed at transferring 30% of white-owned agricultural land to Black South Africans by 2014. By 2025, only approximately 10% had been transferred -- a widely acknowledged failure attributed to budget constraints, institutional incapacity, willing-buyer/willing-seller constraints, and lack of post-settlement support.

3. **Tenure Reform (ESTA 62 of 1997):** Protects occupiers of rural land (primarily farmworkers and labor tenants) from arbitrary eviction. The Interim Protection of Informal Land Rights Act 31 of 1996 (IPILRA) protects informal rights to land, including those held under customary law, from deprivation without consent -- IPILRA has been renewed annually since 1996, reflecting the failure to enact permanent tenure reform legislation.

**Expropriation Without Compensation:** Following the ANC's 2017 Nasrec conference resolution, Parliament initiated a process to amend section 25 to explicitly permit expropriation without compensation. The Constitutional Eighteenth Amendment Bill was not passed in 2021. Subsequently, the **Expropriation Act 13 of 2024** was enacted, which provides for expropriation at nil compensation in specified circumstances (section 12(3)), including: land held for speculation, land abandoned by the owner, land where the market value is equivalent to or less than direct state investment, and property occupied or used by labor tenants or persons occupying land for more than 10 years. The Act's constitutionality under the existing section 25 (without amendment) is likely to be tested before the Constitutional Court.

## Latin America

### Brazil: Social Function and Agrarian Reform

**Constitutional Framework:**
- **Article 5(XXII-XXIII):** The right of property is guaranteed; property shall fulfill its social function.
- **Article 184:** The Union may expropriate for agrarian reform purposes rural property that does not fulfill its social function, with prior and fair compensation in agrarian debt bonds (Títulos da Dívida Agrária, TDAs) redeemable in up to 20 years.
- **Article 185:** The following are not subject to expropriation: small and medium rural properties (provided the owner has no other) and productive property.
- **Article 186:** Social function is fulfilled when the rural property simultaneously meets: (1) rational and adequate use, (2) adequate use of natural resources and environmental preservation, (3) compliance with labor regulations, (4) exploitation that favors the well-being of owners and workers.

**Land Statute (Estatuto da Terra, Lei No. 4.504/1964):** The foundational agrarian reform law, defining concepts of latifúndio (large unproductive estate), minifúndio (subdivision below economically viable size), and rural modules (módulos rurais). INCRA (National Institute of Colonization and Agrarian Reform) administers the program.

**The MST (Movimento dos Trabalhadores Rurais Sem Terra):** Founded in 1984, the MST is arguably the largest social movement in Latin America. Its strategy centers on organized occupation (ocupação) of unproductive land to pressure expropriation. The MST has settled over 370,000 families on approximately 7.5 million hectares. The constitutional and legal status of occupations is contested:
- The STF has generally held that land occupation is not a legitimate exercise of rights, but prosecution is selective.
- The Inter-American Commission on Human Rights has addressed Brazil's agrarian conflicts in multiple reports, noting the pattern of violence against rural workers and landless movements.
- The 2017 "Legal Framework for Land Regularization" (Lei No. 13.465/2017) was criticized by agrarian reform advocates for prioritizing land titling (regularização fundiária) over redistribution, potentially benefiting large-scale land grabbers (grileiros).

### Mexico: The Ejido System

**Article 27 of the Constitution (1917):** Established the revolutionary principle that the nation has original ownership of all land and water, with the right to impose conditions on private property as dictated by the public interest. The ejido system granted communal land rights to peasant communities.

**1992 Reform (Ley Agraria):** Amended Article 27 to permit privatization of ejido land -- ejidatarios could vote to dissolve the ejido and receive individual titles. The reform, associated with NAFTA and neoliberal restructuring, was intended to modernize agriculture but has been criticized for accelerating land concentration and displacement of indigenous and peasant communities.

**PROCEDE/FANAR Programs:** Certified ejido boundaries and individual parcels, issuing over 9 million certificates. By 2025, approximately 70% of ejido land has been certified.

### Colombia: Conflict, Peace, and Land

The 2016 Peace Agreement between the Colombian government and FARC included a Comprehensive Rural Reform (Reforma Rural Integral) as its first point, committing to: creation of a Land Fund with 3 million hectares for landless and land-poor farmers, formalization of 7 million hectares of smallholder land, territorial development programs (PDETs) in 170 conflict-affected municipalities, and a rural land cadaster. Implementation has been slow -- the Comprehensive Land Reform Law (Ley de Tierras) was enacted in 2023, but the Land Fund has acquired only a fraction of its 3 million hectare target.

The Victims and Land Restitution Law (Ley 1448 de 2011) established a process for restoring land to victims of the armed conflict, administered by the Land Restitution Unit. Over 130,000 claims have been filed, covering approximately 6 million hectares.

## Southeast Asia

### Indonesia: UUPA and Unfinished Land Reform

**Basic Agrarian Law (UUPA No. 5 of 1960):** Enacted to: (1) unify the colonial dual land system, (2) recognize adat rights, (3) establish the social function of all land rights (fungsi sosial, Article 6), and (4) implement land reform. Key provisions:

- **Article 2:** The earth, water, and airspace, including the natural resources contained therein, are controlled by the state (hak menguasai negara) at the highest level for the greatest prosperity of the people.
- **Article 3:** Adat rights (hak ulayat) of indigenous communities (masyarakat hukum adat) are recognized insofar as they still exist and do not conflict with national and state interests.
- **Article 5:** Agrarian law applicable to land, water, and airspace is adat law (hukum adat), provided it does not conflict with national interests, Indonesian socialism, and the regulations of this law.
- **Articles 7, 17:** Excessive land ownership is prohibited; maximum and minimum sizes shall be fixed by government regulation.

**Government Regulation No. 224/1961:** Implemented the landreform program, fixing ceilings at 5-20 hectares depending on population density and soil type, and mandating redistribution of excess and former colonial estate land. However, the program was interrupted by the political upheaval of 1965-1966 (linked to the mass killings of Communist Party members and supporters, who had been active in land reform advocacy). Land reform effectively stalled.

**Current Conflicts:**
- **Plantation Concessions (Hak Guna Usaha/HGU):** Palm oil, rubber, and timber concessions covering approximately 16 million hectares in Sumatra and Kalimantan frequently overlap with adat land claims. The Constitutional Court in **Decision No. 35/PUU-X/2012** held that customary forests (hutan adat) are not state forests, potentially unlocking millions of hectares from state control for indigenous communities -- but implementation through the Agrarian Reform Program and Social Forestry Program has been slow.
- **Agrarian Reform Presidential Regulation (Perpres 86/2018):** Targeted redistribution of 9 million hectares through asset legalization (4.5 million hectares of land titling) and asset redistribution (4.5 million hectares of land transfer). Progress has focused heavily on titling (regularization) rather than genuine redistribution.
- **Job Creation Law (Undang-Undang Cipta Kerja, Law No. 6/2023):** Consolidated various land-related provisions, including streamlining land procurement for strategic projects and establishing a "land bank" (bank tanah). Critics argue it prioritizes investment over agrarian reform.

### Philippines: CARP

**Republic Act No. 6657 (Comprehensive Agrarian Reform Law, 1988):** Enacted under President Corazon Aquino as a cornerstone of post-Marcos democratic reform.

**Scope:** Covers all public and private agricultural lands regardless of tenurial arrangement and commodity produced. The law applies to: tenanted rice and corn lands, all other alienable and disposable public lands, all private agricultural lands (subject to retention limits), and other lands suitable for agriculture.

**Retention Limits:** The landowner may retain up to 5 hectares, plus 3 hectares for each child over 15 years of age who is actually tilling the land.

**Modes of Acquisition:**
- **Voluntary Offer to Sell (VOS):** Landowner offers land to DAR at a price within valuation guidelines.
- **Compulsory Acquisition (CA):** DAR identifies land for acquisition, determines just compensation, and acquires it.
- **Voluntary Land Transfer/Direct Payment Scheme (VLT/DPS):** Landowner and beneficiaries negotiate directly.

**Just Compensation:** Determined under DAR Administrative Order No. 5, Series of 1998, using a formula incorporating capitalized net income, comparable sales, market value per tax declaration, and government assessment. The Supreme Court in **Land Bank of the Philippines v. Banal (G.R. No. 143276, 2004)** and numerous subsequent cases has reviewed DAR/LBP valuation determinations, generally applying the statutory formula but allowing deviation for "just" results.

**Extension and Completion:** RA 9700 (2009) extended CARP with reforms (CARPER) and allocated additional funding. By 2025, DAR reports: 4.78 million hectares distributed, 2.84 million agrarian reform beneficiaries, and a remaining balance of approximately 500,000 hectares.

**Persistent Issues:**
- **Landlord Resistance:** The Hacienda Luisita case (**Hacienda Luisita Inc. v. Presidential Agrarian Reform Council, G.R. No. 171101, 2011**) -- involving the estate of the Cojuangco family (relatives of President Aquino) -- became emblematic of elite resistance to CARP. The Supreme Court ruled that the Stock Distribution Option granted to Hacienda Luisita was invalid and ordered actual land distribution.
- **Beneficiary Attrition:** Significant numbers of beneficiaries have sold or abandoned their awarded lands, often due to lack of support services, credit access, and irrigation.
- **Conversion and Exemptions:** Agricultural land conversion to non-agricultural uses (residential, commercial, industrial) has removed significant areas from CARP coverage.

## Comparative Analysis

### State Ownership vs. Private Property

The Global South presents a spectrum: China maintains state/collective ownership of all land; Nigeria vests all land in state governors; Indonesia asserts state control (hak menguasai negara) while recognizing adat rights; Brazil and the Philippines protect private property but subject it to social function requirements; South Africa protects private property under section 25 while pursuing redistributive reform.

### Social Function Doctrine

The "social function of property" is a distinctively Global South legal innovation. Brazil's constitutional formulation (Article 186) with its four-part test is the most developed. Indonesia's UUPA Article 6 establishes the principle. The Philippines' CARP implements it through coverage of all agricultural lands. This doctrine contrasts sharply with the Anglo-American tradition of property as a near-absolute right.

### Compensation and Expropriation

The debate over expropriation compensation ranges from South Africa's "nil compensation" provisions (Expropriation Act 2024) to India's LARR multiplied-market-value approach. China's 2019 reforms increased farmer compensation but within a collective ownership framework. Brazil's agrarian debt bonds (TDAs) for unproductive land represent a middle ground. The Philippines' just compensation formula has generated thousands of Supreme Court cases.

### Implementation Gap

A common theme across the Global South is the gap between ambitious legislative frameworks and actual implementation. South Africa's 10% land transfer (vs. 30% target), Indonesia's stalled landreform, Brazil's ongoing MST occupations, and the Philippines' CARP beneficiary attrition all demonstrate that legal reform alone is insufficient without political will, institutional capacity, and sustained public investment.
