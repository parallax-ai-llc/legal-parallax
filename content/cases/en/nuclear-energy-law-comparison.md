## Summary

Nuclear energy regulation represents one of the most complex areas of international law, involving safety standards, environmental protection, liability frameworks, and non-proliferation obligations that intersect across multiple legal regimes. South Korea, which operates 26 commercial reactors generating approximately 30% of its electricity, maintains a comprehensive regulatory framework anchored by the Nuclear Safety Act and the Nuclear Liability Act, with unlimited operator liability channeled through Korea Hydro and Nuclear Power Corporation (KHNP). The United States, the world's largest producer of nuclear energy, regulates the sector through the Atomic Energy Act of 1954 and the Price-Anderson Nuclear Industries Indemnity Act, which establishes a two-tier insurance system providing over $13 billion in coverage. Germany, once a significant nuclear power producer, completed its nuclear phase-out on 15 April 2023 under the Atomgesetz (AtG), but remains bound by decommissioning obligations and the Paris Convention on Third Party Liability. At the international level, the IAEA facilitates cooperation through the Convention on Nuclear Safety (1994), the Vienna Convention on Civil Liability for Nuclear Damage (1963), and the Paris Convention (1960), while the Joint Protocol (1988) bridges these two liability regimes. Countries including Japan, India, France, Brazil, and the United Arab Emirates each maintain distinct regulatory approaches reflecting their varied nuclear ambitions and energy needs.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's nuclear regulatory architecture rests on several principal statutes administered by the Nuclear Safety and Security Commission (NSSC), an independent body established in October 2011 and reporting directly to the President.

| Statute | Purpose | Key Provisions |
|---------|---------|----------------|
| Nuclear Safety Act (원자력안전법) | Safety regulation of nuclear facilities | Licensing, inspection, operational safety standards |
| Nuclear Liability Act (원자력손해배상법) | Civil liability for nuclear damage | Operator liability, mandatory financial security, compensation procedures |
| Act on Physical Protection and Radiological Emergency (방사선비상조치법) | Security and emergency preparedness | Physical protection of nuclear materials, emergency response protocols |
| Atomic Energy Promotion Act (원자력진흥법) | Promotion and development of nuclear energy | R&D support, international cooperation, workforce development |
| Radioactive Waste Management Act (방사성폐기물관리법) | Disposal of nuclear waste | Waste classification, disposal facility siting, long-term management |

The Nuclear Safety Act requires all nuclear installations to obtain construction permits, operating licenses, and periodic safety reviews every 10 years. The NSSC may order operators to suspend operations, remodel, repair, or transfer reactors when facilities fail to conform to prescribed technical criteria.

### Key Principles

**Operator Liability:** Under the Nuclear Liability Act, the nuclear business operator bears strict liability for all nuclear damage resulting from reactor operations. Operators must deposit financial security with the district court having jurisdiction over their principal office, in cash or securities prescribed by Presidential Decree.

**Financial Security Requirements:** Nuclear operators are required to maintain liability coverage of 500 billion won (approximately $375 million) per installation. The government provides supplementary compensation above this threshold through the state indemnity system.

**Channeling Principle:** All liability is channeled exclusively to the nuclear operator, shielding suppliers, contractors, and transporters from direct claims. This mirrors international conventions and provides legal certainty for the nuclear supply chain.

**Regulatory Oversight:** The NSSC conducts comprehensive regulatory oversight throughout the lifecycle of nuclear installations. This includes pre-construction site evaluation, construction permit review, pre-operational inspection, operating license issuance, periodic safety reviews every 10 years, and decommissioning approval. The Korean Institute of Nuclear Safety (KINS) serves as the technical support organization to the NSSC, conducting safety assessments, inspections, and research on behalf of the commission.

**Emergency Preparedness:** The Act on Physical Protection and Radiological Emergency mandates that nuclear operators maintain comprehensive emergency response plans, conduct regular emergency drills (at least once annually), and coordinate with local and national emergency management agencies. Emergency planning zones extend to a radius of 20-30 km from each nuclear installation, within which evacuation plans must be maintained and periodically tested.

**Spent Fuel Management:** South Korea faces a significant spent fuel storage challenge, with on-site storage pools at several reactor sites approaching capacity. The Korea Radioactive Waste Agency (KORAD) manages radioactive waste disposal, including the Gyeongju Low- and Intermediate-Level Waste (LILW) disposal facility. A permanent high-level waste repository site has not yet been selected, and the issue remains one of the most politically sensitive aspects of Korean nuclear policy.

**Non-Proliferation:** South Korea is party to the Treaty on the Non-Proliferation of Nuclear Weapons (NPT) and maintains a comprehensive safeguards agreement with the IAEA. The Korea-U.S. Agreement for Peaceful Nuclear Cooperation (commonly known as the "123 Agreement"), renewed in 2015 and amended in 2025 to permit enrichment and reprocessing activities, governs bilateral nuclear cooperation.

### Notable Cases

**Shin Kori Units 5 and 6 Deliberative Poll (2017):** President Moon Jae-in's government convened a 471-member citizens' jury to decide whether to resume construction of Shin Kori Units 5 and 6, which had been suspended as part of an energy transition policy. The jury voted 59.5% in favor of resumption, demonstrating a novel approach to nuclear governance through deliberative democracy.

**Nuclear Parts Scandal (2012-2013):** The discovery that thousands of components in Korean nuclear reactors had been supplied with forged quality certificates led to the temporary shutdown of multiple reactors. Eight people were convicted, including a former senior official at KHNP, resulting in prison sentences of up to five years. The scandal prompted comprehensive reforms to the nuclear supply chain oversight system and the creation of the NSSC's enhanced inspection regime.

**Wolsong Unit 1 Early Closure (2019):** The decision to permanently shut down Wolsong Unit 1 before the end of its extended operating license generated significant legal and political controversy, with the Board of Audit and Inspection later concluding that KHNP had undervalued the reactor's economic viability, leading to ongoing litigation.

## United States

### Federal Framework

The United States regulatory framework for nuclear energy is anchored by the Atomic Energy Act of 1954 (AEA, 42 U.S.C. sections 2011-2297), as amended, which grants the Nuclear Regulatory Commission (NRC) authority over civilian uses of nuclear materials. The Energy Reorganization Act of 1974 separated promotional and regulatory functions, creating the NRC as an independent agency.

| Statute / Regulation | Citation | Purpose |
|----------------------|----------|---------|
| Atomic Energy Act of 1954 | 42 U.S.C. sections 2011-2297 | Comprehensive framework for nuclear energy regulation |
| Energy Reorganization Act of 1974 | 42 U.S.C. sections 5801-5891 | Created the NRC as independent regulator |
| Price-Anderson Nuclear Industries Indemnity Act | 42 U.S.C. section 2210 | Nuclear liability and insurance framework |
| Nuclear Waste Policy Act of 1982 | 42 U.S.C. sections 10101-10270 | Radioactive waste disposal policy |
| 10 C.F.R. Part 50 | NRC Regulations | Licensing of production and utilization facilities |
| 10 C.F.R. Part 52 | NRC Regulations | Combined licenses, early site permits, standard design certifications |

**Price-Anderson Liability Framework:** The Price-Anderson Act, first enacted in 1957 and most recently extended in 2024 through December 31, 2065, establishes a two-tier insurance system:

- **Primary Layer:** Each reactor site must maintain $500 million in private liability insurance (increased from $450 million effective January 1, 2024).
- **Secondary Layer:** If damages exceed the primary layer, all licensed reactor operators are assessed retrospective premiums of up to approximately $158 million per reactor, per incident, creating a pooled fund exceeding $13 billion.
- **Government Indemnity:** If the combined primary and secondary layers are exhausted, Congress must consider additional relief measures.
- **DOE Contractor Coverage:** Congress increased liability coverage for DOE contractors for nuclear incidents occurring outside the United States to $2 billion from $500 million.

### State Variations

While the NRC maintains exclusive federal jurisdiction over radiological safety, states retain authority over financial qualifications, rate-setting, need for power determinations, and environmental matters beyond radiological concerns. California's Warren-Alquist Act effectively imposed a moratorium on new nuclear construction by requiring a federally approved waste disposal solution before new plants could be approved. Vermont attempted to shut down the Vermont Yankee Nuclear Power Station through state legislation, but in *Entergy Nuclear Vermont Yankee, LLC v. Shumlin* (733 F.3d 393, 2d Cir. 2013), the court found that state action motivated by radiological safety concerns was preempted by federal law.

### Notable Cases

***Pacific Gas & Electric Co. v. State Energy Resources Conservation & Development Commission* (461 U.S. 190, 1983):** The Supreme Court upheld California's moratorium on new nuclear construction, finding that states may regulate nuclear power for economic (but not safety) reasons, establishing the distinction between federal safety authority and state economic regulation.

***Silkwood v. Kerr-McGee Corp.* (464 U.S. 238, 1984):** The Supreme Court held that a state-authorized punitive damages award for plutonium contamination was not preempted by federal nuclear regulation, even though the NRC exclusively regulated safety matters.

**Three Mile Island (1979):** The partial meltdown at TMI-2 in Pennsylvania led to the NRC's most comprehensive regulatory overhaul, resulting in billions of dollars in cleanup costs over 14 years and generating approximately $71 million in insurance claim payments under the Price-Anderson framework.

**Fukushima-Related U.S. Regulatory Response:** Following the 2011 Fukushima Daiichi disaster, the NRC established a Near-Term Task Force that issued 12 safety recommendations, leading to orders requiring enhanced spent fuel pool instrumentation, hardened containment venting systems for boiling water reactors with Mark I and Mark II containments, and updated seismic and flooding hazard analyses for all operating reactors. These post-Fukushima requirements represented the most significant expansion of U.S. nuclear safety regulation since the TMI reforms.

**NRC Combined License (COL) Process:** The 10 C.F.R. Part 52 combined license process, developed to streamline new reactor construction, allows the NRC to issue a single license authorizing both construction and operation, subject to inspections, tests, analyses, and acceptance criteria (ITAAC) verification prior to fuel loading. Vogtle Units 3 and 4 in Georgia, the first new U.S. nuclear reactors in a generation, were licensed under this process, with Unit 3 entering commercial operation in July 2023 and Unit 4 in April 2024.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's nuclear regulatory framework is governed by the Atomgesetz (Atomic Energy Act, AtG), first promulgated on 23 December 1959. Following the 2002 amendment and subsequent revisions, the Act's primary purpose was reoriented from promotion to the orderly phase-out of nuclear energy.

| Statute / Regulation | Reference | Purpose |
|----------------------|-----------|---------|
| Atomgesetz (AtG) | BGBl. I S. 3053 | Core nuclear energy regulation, phase-out framework |
| Strahlenschutzgesetz (StrlSchG) | BGBl. I S. 1966 | Radiation protection |
| Atomrechtliche Verfahrensverordnung (AtVfV) | | Licensing procedures for nuclear installations |
| Atomrechtliche Deckungsvorsorge-Verordnung (AtDeckV) | | Financial security requirements for nuclear operators |
| StGB section 307 | Strafgesetzbuch | Causing a nuclear explosion (up to 5 years, or life imprisonment if death results) |
| StGB section 311 | Strafgesetzbuch | Releasing ionizing radiation (1-10 years imprisonment) |

**Unlimited Liability:** Under Section 31(1) of the AtG, Germany introduced unlimited operator liability in 1985, stating that the liability of the operator of a nuclear installation shall be unlimited. The Nuclear Financial Security Ordinance (AtDeckV) requires operators to maintain financial security of EUR 2.5 billion per operating installation, the highest mandatory financial security level of any country worldwide.

**Phase-Out Timeline:** The 13th Amendment to the AtG (2011), enacted after the Fukushima Daiichi disaster, set firm shutdown dates for all German reactors. The 19th Amendment (December 2022) extended the operating life of the final three reactors (Isar 2, Neckarwestheim 2, Emsland) until 15 April 2023, after which Germany completed its nuclear phase-out.

### Key Principles

**Decommissioning Obligations:** Despite the phase-out, operators remain responsible for decommissioning, dismantling, and waste management. The Public Fund for Nuclear Waste Management (KENFO), established in 2017, received approximately EUR 24 billion from reactor operators to finance interim and final storage of radioactive waste.

**Constitutional Review:** In its 2016 decision (BVerfG, 1 BvR 2821/11), the Federal Constitutional Court found the 13th Amendment largely compatible with the Basic Law but held that certain provisions inadequately compensated operators for investments made in reliance on previously granted operating periods, ordering the legislature to provide remediation by June 2018.

**Paris Convention Compliance:** Germany is party to the Paris Convention on Third Party Liability in the Field of Nuclear Energy (1960) and the Brussels Supplementary Convention (1963), which together provide a three-tier liability and compensation system shared among the operator, the installation state, and all contracting parties collectively.

### Notable Cases

**Vattenfall AB v. Federal Republic of Germany (ICSID Case No. ARB/12/12):** Swedish energy company Vattenfall filed an investment arbitration claim seeking EUR 6.1 billion in compensation for the accelerated phase-out of its Krummel and Brunsbuttel nuclear plants. The case was settled in 2021 with Germany agreeing to pay approximately EUR 1.425 billion in compensation.

**Biblis Nuclear Power Plant Shutdown (2011):** The immediate suspension of older reactors following Fukushima led to legal challenges by operators RWE and E.ON. RWE won a ruling from the Hessian Administrative Court (VGH Kassel) finding the temporary moratorium unlawful, though the subsequent legislative phase-out superseded this judgment.

**E.ON, RWE, EnBW, and Vattenfall Constitutional Complaints (2016):** The four major nuclear operators jointly challenged the accelerated phase-out before the Federal Constitutional Court, arguing that the cancellation of previously granted electricity production quotas constituted an expropriation requiring full compensation. The Court's nuanced ruling acknowledged that the operators had legitimate expectations based on the 2010 lifetime extensions but upheld the government's authority to reverse course, requiring only targeted compensation for stranded investments specifically attributable to the 2010 amendments.

**Asse II Mine Radioactive Waste Retrieval:** The Asse II salt mine in Lower Saxony, used as a repository for approximately 126,000 barrels of low- and intermediate-level radioactive waste between 1967 and 1978, has been the subject of decades of legal and environmental controversy due to brine intrusion threatening the integrity of the storage chambers. The Federal Office for the Safety of Nuclear Waste Management (BASE) ordered the retrieval of all waste from the mine, a process expected to take decades and cost billions of euros, raising complex questions about historical liability allocation among operators, the federal government, and the state of Lower Saxony.

## ECHR / EU Framework

### Convention Articles

The ECHR does not contain provisions specifically addressing nuclear energy. However, several Convention rights are engaged by nuclear activities:

- **Article 2 (Right to Life):** Imposes positive obligations on states to protect life from environmental hazards, including nuclear risks. In *L.C.B. v. United Kingdom* (App. No. 23413/94, 1998), the ECtHR examined whether the UK had failed to protect the applicant from exposure to radiation from nuclear testing.
- **Article 8 (Right to Respect for Private and Family Life):** Environmental pollution, including radiation exposure, may constitute an interference with private and family life. *Balmer-Schafroth v. Switzerland* (App. No. 22110/93, 1997) involved residents near a nuclear plant alleging that continued operation violated Article 8.
- **Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (Protection of Property):** Nuclear regulatory decisions affecting property rights must strike a fair balance between public interest and individual rights.

### Key Case Law

***Balmer-Schafroth and Others v. Switzerland* (1997):** Swiss residents near the Muhleberg nuclear power plant challenged the extension of its operating license, claiming a violation of Article 6 (fair trial). The Grand Chamber found no violation, holding that the applicants had failed to establish a sufficiently direct link between the operating conditions and their right to protection of life, though a strong dissent argued that proximity to a nuclear plant created a sufficient threat.

***Athanassoglou and Others v. Switzerland* (App. No. 27644/95, 2000):** Similarly concerning the Beznau nuclear plant, the Grand Chamber held that Swiss law did not violate Article 6 by denying residents standing to challenge operating license extensions, though again the decision attracted vigorous dissent.

***Tatar v. Romania* (App. No. 67021/01, 2009):** While concerning mining rather than nuclear energy, this case established important precedent regarding state obligations to protect citizens from environmental hazards involving dangerous substances, applying the precautionary principle under Article 8.

### Member State Obligations

EU Member States retain sovereign authority over their energy mix under Article 194(2) TFEU, which explicitly preserves each state's right to determine the conditions for exploiting its energy resources and to choose between different energy sources. However, EU-wide frameworks apply in several areas:

- **Euratom Treaty (1957):** Establishes the European Atomic Energy Community, providing for nuclear safety standards, radiation protection, and nuclear safeguards. The Euratom Treaty remains in force as primary EU law.
- **Nuclear Safety Directive (2009/71/Euratom, as amended by 2014/87/Euratom):** Establishes a Community framework for the nuclear safety of nuclear installations, requiring peer reviews every six years.
- **Radioactive Waste Directive (2011/70/Euratom):** Requires Member States to establish national programs for managing spent fuel and radioactive waste.
- **Basic Safety Standards Directive (2013/59/Euratom):** Sets uniform radiation protection standards across the EU.

### IAEA Conventions and Instruments

The international nuclear liability and safety regime is built on several IAEA-administered instruments:

**Convention on Nuclear Safety (1994):** Adopted in the wake of the Chernobyl disaster, the Convention commits states parties to maintaining high levels of nuclear safety by establishing fundamental safety principles for land-based civil nuclear power plants. Contracting parties undergo peer review at triennial review meetings, where each state presents a national report detailing compliance with Convention obligations. As of 2025, there are 91 contracting parties.

**Joint Convention on the Safety of Spent Fuel Management and on the Safety of Radioactive Waste Management (1997):** This Convention extends the peer review mechanism to spent fuel and radioactive waste management, requiring states to maintain national inventories and demonstrate safe management practices.

**Convention on Early Notification of a Nuclear Accident (1986) and Convention on Assistance in the Case of a Nuclear Accident or Radiological Emergency (1986):** Adopted immediately following Chernobyl, these twin conventions establish obligations for prompt notification and mutual assistance in the event of a nuclear emergency, forming the core of the international nuclear emergency response framework.

**Paris-Vienna Liability Bridge:** The Joint Protocol Relating to the Application of the Vienna Convention and the Paris Convention (1988) acts as a bridge between the two principal liability regimes, ensuring that victims in a Paris Convention state can claim compensation from operators in a Vienna Convention state, and vice versa. As of 2025, the Joint Protocol has 29 contracting parties.

**Convention on Supplementary Compensation (CSC, 1997):** The CSC establishes an additional tier of compensation funded by contributions from all contracting parties, supplementing the amounts available under the national liability regime. The United States ratified the CSC in 2008, and its entry into force in 2015 created a global supplementary fund, though participation remains limited.

### Additional Jurisdictions

- **Japan** operates under the Act on the Regulation of Nuclear Source Material, Nuclear Fuel Material and Reactors (Reactor Regulation Act), reformed after the 2011 Fukushima disaster to create the Nuclear Regulation Authority (NRA). Japan's operator liability under the Act on Compensation for Nuclear Damage is unlimited, similar to Germany.
- **France**, the world's most nuclear-dependent country (~70% of electricity), operates under the Transparency and Security in the Nuclear Field Act of 2006 (TSN Act) and is party to the Paris Convention.
- **India** enacted the Civil Liability for Nuclear Damage Act, 2010, which controversially includes a right of recourse against suppliers, deviating from the channeling principle common in other jurisdictions.
- **Brazil** regulates nuclear energy through the National Nuclear Energy Commission (CNEN) under Law No. 6,189/1974 and is party to the Vienna Convention.
- **United Arab Emirates** established the Federal Authority for Nuclear Regulation (FANR) under Federal Law No. 6 of 2009 and operates the Barakah Nuclear Energy Plant, the Arab world's first commercial nuclear power station.
- **Australia** prohibited nuclear power generation under the Australian Radiation Protection and Nuclear Safety Act 1998 (ARPANS Act, section 10), though political debate about lifting this prohibition intensified in 2024-2025 as part of energy transition discussions.
- **South Africa** operates the Koeberg Nuclear Power Station, the only commercial nuclear power plant on the African continent, under the National Nuclear Regulator Act (No. 47 of 1999), and has considered expanding its nuclear fleet under the Integrated Resource Plan.

### Emerging Regulatory Challenges

**Small Modular Reactors (SMRs):** The development of small modular reactors has raised novel regulatory questions across all jurisdictions. The United States NRC has certified NuScale Power's SMR design (the first SMR design certification globally), while South Korea's SMART reactor design has received Standard Design Approval from the NSSC. The IAEA's International Expert Group on Nuclear Liability (INLEX) has been specifically examining liability issues related to SMRs, including questions about factory fabrication and transportability that do not fit neatly within existing frameworks designed for large, site-built reactors.

**Nuclear-Powered Maritime Vessels:** The growing interest in nuclear-powered civil merchant ships raises liability questions that span nuclear law and maritime law. INLEX has examined whether existing nuclear liability conventions adequately address the unique risks posed by mobile nuclear installations, including the determination of the competent court, applicable law, and the responsibility of flag states versus port states.

**Fusion Energy Regulation:** Several jurisdictions are considering how to regulate fusion energy, which does not fit within traditional fission-focused nuclear regulatory frameworks. The United States has proposed regulating fusion reactors as machines at the state level rather than under NRC jurisdiction. The United Kingdom has adopted a similar approach, regulating fusion under the Health and Safety at Work Act rather than the Nuclear Installations Act. The EU is examining whether the Euratom Treaty's provisions apply to fusion energy or whether new legislative instruments are needed.

### Liability Regime Comparison Detail

The international nuclear liability framework comprises two distinct but parallel regimes:

**Paris/Brussels Regime:** Primarily adhered to by Western European countries. The Paris Convention (1960) establishes the operator's strict liability with a minimum amount of 5 million SDR (originally), later increased by the 2004 Protocol (not yet in force for all parties) to EUR 700 million. The Brussels Supplementary Convention (1963) adds two additional tiers: the installation state provides a second tier up to 175 million SDR, and all contracting parties collectively provide a third tier up to 300 million SDR.

**Vienna Regime:** Primarily adhered to by Eastern European and developing countries. The original Vienna Convention (1963) established a minimum liability of $5 million. The 1997 Protocol to Amend the Vienna Convention raised the minimum to 300 million SDR and expanded the definition of nuclear damage to include environmental damage and preventive measures.

### Non-Proliferation and Safeguards

The relationship between nuclear energy regulation and non-proliferation remains a central concern:

**IAEA Safeguards:** All non-nuclear-weapon states operating nuclear power programs are subject to IAEA safeguards agreements, which allow IAEA inspectors to verify that nuclear material is not diverted from peaceful to military purposes. South Korea maintains a Comprehensive Safeguards Agreement (INFCIRC/236) and an Additional Protocol, providing the IAEA with enhanced inspection access.

**Nuclear Suppliers Group (NSG):** South Korea, the United States, and Germany are all members of the NSG, which establishes export control guidelines for nuclear materials, equipment, and technology. The 2025 amendment to the U.S.-South Korea 123 Agreement, permitting Korean enrichment and reprocessing activities under agreed conditions, represents a significant evolution in the bilateral non-proliferation relationship.

**Dual-Use Technology Controls:** All four jurisdictions maintain export control regimes for dual-use nuclear technology, though the specific scope and licensing requirements vary. The Wassenaar Arrangement, the Zangger Committee, and the NSG collectively provide the multilateral framework, supplemented by national legislation such as the U.S. Export Control Reform Act of 2018 and Germany's Aussenwirtschaftsgesetz (Foreign Trade and Payments Act).

**Iran and North Korea:** The international nuclear non-proliferation regime remains strained by the unresolved nuclear programs of Iran and the DPRK (North Korea). South Korea's geographic proximity to the DPRK makes non-proliferation a particularly acute national security concern, directly influencing the regulatory framework governing its civilian nuclear program and the terms of the U.S.-ROK 123 Agreement.

### Climate Change and Nuclear Renaissance

The global debate over nuclear energy's role in climate change mitigation has intensified:

- **COP28 Declaration (2023):** At the 28th Conference of the Parties in Dubai, over 20 countries, including the United States, South Korea, France, and the United Kingdom, signed a declaration pledging to triple global nuclear energy capacity by 2050, reflecting a significant shift in international climate diplomacy.
- **EU Taxonomy Regulation:** The EU Complementary Delegated Act (2022) included nuclear energy in the EU's sustainable finance taxonomy as a "transitional activity" under strict conditions, including the use of advanced fuel technologies and compliance with radioactive waste disposal plans. This inclusion was controversial, with Germany and Austria among the most vocal opponents.
- **South Korea's Carbon Neutrality Strategy:** South Korea's 10th Basic Plan for Electricity Supply and Demand (2023) reversed the previous administration's nuclear phase-down policy, increasing the target share of nuclear power to approximately 33% of the electricity mix by 2036 and planning new reactor construction.
