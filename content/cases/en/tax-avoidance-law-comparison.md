## Summary

Tax avoidance -- the use of legal means to minimize tax liability, as distinguished from illegal tax evasion -- has become one of the most intensely contested areas of fiscal policy, particularly following revelations from the LuxLeaks (2014), Panama Papers (2016), and Pandora Papers (2021) investigations that exposed the scale of aggressive tax planning by multinational enterprises and wealthy individuals. South Korea addresses tax avoidance through a statutory General Anti-Avoidance Rule (GAAR) under Article 14 of the Framework Act on National Taxes (국세기본법 제14조), the substance-over-form doctrine (실질과세 원칙), specific anti-avoidance provisions throughout the tax code, and the International Tax Coordination Act (국제조세조정에 관한 법률) targeting international avoidance structures including CFC rules, thin capitalization rules, and transfer pricing adjustments. The United States combats tax avoidance through a combination of judicial doctrines (economic substance, business purpose, step transaction, sham transaction), codified economic substance doctrine under Section 7701(o) of the Internal Revenue Code since the Health Care and Education Reconciliation Act of 2010, specific anti-avoidance statutes (GILTI, BEAT, Section 269), and extensive regulatory and administrative guidance including listed transaction reporting requirements and promoter penalties. Germany applies the general anti-abuse rule (Missbrauchsvorschrift) under Section 42 of the Abgabenordnung (AO), supplemented by specific anti-avoidance provisions including the Außensteuergesetz (AStG) CFC rules, the earnings stripping rule of Section 4h EStG, and the substance-over-form principle (wirtschaftliche Betrachtungsweise). The EU has established the most comprehensive supranational anti-avoidance framework through ATAD I (Directive 2016/1164), ATAD II (Directive 2017/952), the Unshell Directive proposal targeting shell entities, and the landmark Pillar Two global minimum tax directive, while the CJEU has developed the abuse of rights doctrine constraining the use of EU law for tax avoidance purposes.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's anti-avoidance framework centers on the substance-over-form principle (실질과세 원칙) codified in Article 14 of the Framework Act on National Taxes (국세기본법). This provision states that where the nominal form of a transaction differs from its economic substance, taxation shall be based on the substance rather than the form. The Supreme Court of Korea has interpreted this provision as South Korea's GAAR, applying it to recharacterize transactions structured to avoid tax.

### Specific Anti-Avoidance Measures

| Measure | Statutory Basis | Application |
|---------|----------------|-------------|
| CFC rules | International Tax Coordination Act, Articles 17-22 | Attributes income of controlled foreign corporations in low-tax jurisdictions (effective rate below 15%) to Korean parent |
| Thin capitalization | International Tax Coordination Act, Article 14 | Denies interest deduction on debt from foreign related parties exceeding 2:1 debt-to-equity ratio (6:1 for financial institutions) |
| Transfer pricing | International Tax Coordination Act, Articles 5-11 | Arm's length pricing for related-party transactions |
| Deemed gift taxation | Inheritance Tax and Gift Tax Act, Articles 33-45 | Taxes indirect wealth transfers through corporate transactions as gifts |

### Judicial Development

The Korean Supreme Court has applied the substance-over-form principle in numerous landmark cases:

**Supreme Court Decision 2008Du8499 (2012):** The Court held that a series of share transactions structured to avoid capital gains tax through a tax-exempt entity constituted an abuse of form and recharacterized the transaction based on economic substance.

**Supreme Court Decision 2015Du45700 (2017):** Applied the substance-over-form doctrine to an international transaction structure involving a conduit entity in a treaty jurisdiction, denying treaty benefits where the conduit had no genuine economic substance.

### Enhanced Disclosure

Korea has progressively expanded mandatory disclosure requirements for cross-border transactions, requiring detailed reporting of transactions with entities in designated low-tax jurisdictions and periodic reporting of foreign financial accounts through the Overseas Financial Account Reporting System (해외금융계좌 신고제도).

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States employs a multi-layered approach to combating tax avoidance, combining judicial doctrines, statutory rules, and administrative enforcement.

### Economic Substance Doctrine

The economic substance doctrine, codified in Section 7701(o) of the IRC by the Health Care and Education Reconciliation Act of 2010, requires that a transaction have both: (1) a meaningful change in the taxpayer's economic position apart from tax effects (objective test), and (2) a substantial non-tax business purpose (subjective test). Both prongs must be satisfied (conjunctive test). A strict 20 percent penalty (40 percent if not disclosed) applies to any underpayment attributable to a transaction lacking economic substance.

### Judicial Anti-Avoidance Doctrines

| Doctrine | Key Case | Application |
|----------|----------|-------------|
| Business purpose | Gregory v. Helvering, 293 U.S. 465 (1935) | Transaction must serve a genuine business purpose beyond tax avoidance |
| Step transaction | Commissioner v. Clark, 489 U.S. 726 (1989) | Series of formally separate steps treated as single transaction |
| Sham transaction | Knetsch v. United States, 364 U.S. 361 (1960) | Transactions lacking genuine economic activity disregarded |
| Substance over form | Commissioner v. Court Holding Co., 324 U.S. 331 (1945) | Substance of transaction governs over nominal form |
| Assignment of income | Lucas v. Earl, 281 U.S. 111 (1930) | Income taxed to person who earns it, not assignee |

### Listed Transaction Reporting

The IRS maintains a list of "listed transactions" -- specific avoidance structures identified as potentially abusive (Notice 2004-67 et seq.). Taxpayers participating in listed transactions must disclose them on Form 8886. Material advisors must report under Section 6111. Failure to disclose carries significant penalties: $10,000 per failure for individuals, $50,000 for corporations, per tax year.

### Corporate Transparency Act

The Corporate Transparency Act (CTA), enacted as part of the Anti-Money Laundering Act of 2020, requires most companies to report beneficial ownership information to the Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN), targeting the use of anonymous shell companies for tax evasion and money laundering.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's GAAR is found in Section 42 of the Abgabenordnung (AO). The provision states that tax law may not be circumvented through the abuse of forms provided by law (Missbrauch von Gestaltungsmöglichkeiten des Rechts). An abuse is deemed to exist where an inappropriate legal arrangement is chosen that leads to a tax advantage not intended by the law, and the taxpayer cannot demonstrate non-tax reasons for the arrangement that are significant given the overall circumstances.

### Application of Section 42 AO

The BFH has established several key principles in applying Section 42 AO:

- The taxpayer bears the burden of demonstrating non-tax reasons for an arrangement that appears abusive.
- The provision applies as a residual rule: specific anti-avoidance provisions take precedence.
- The "ungewöhnlich" (unusual) nature of the arrangement is a key indicator of abuse.
- Legitimate tax planning remains permissible; only arrangements that are "inappropriate" and produce "unintended" tax advantages are caught.

### Specific Anti-Avoidance Provisions

**CFC Rules (Hinzurechnungsbesteuerung):** Sections 7-14 AStG attribute the passive income of controlled foreign companies in low-tax jurisdictions (effective rate below 25 percent) to German shareholders. The 2022 AStG reform lowered the CFC threshold from one-third of the income being low-taxed passive income to any low-taxed passive income.

**Earnings Stripping (Zinsschranke):** Section 4h EStG limits net interest expense deductions to 30 percent of EBITDA, with a EUR 3 million de minimis threshold and escape clauses for standalone entities and entities meeting the equity ratio test.

**Exit Taxation (Wegzugsbesteuerung):** Section 6 AStG imposes a deemed disposition on unrealized gains when a taxpayer emigrates from Germany, with installment payment options for transfers within the EU/EEA.

### DAC 6 Implementation

Germany implemented the EU mandatory disclosure directive (DAC 6, Directive 2018/822) through Sections 138d-138k AO, requiring intermediaries and, in some cases, taxpayers to report cross-border arrangements bearing certain hallmarks of potential aggressiveness. Reports are submitted to the Federal Central Tax Office (BZSt) and automatically exchanged with other EU tax administrations.

## ECHR & EU

### ATAD I (Directive 2016/1164)

The Anti-Tax Avoidance Directive, adopted on July 12, 2016, requires all EU member states to implement five key anti-avoidance measures:

| Measure | ATAD Article | Requirement |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| Interest limitation | Article 4 | 30% EBITDA limit (EUR 3 million de minimis) |
| Exit taxation | Article 5 | Tax unrealized gains on asset/residence transfer |
| GAAR | Article 6 | Disregard artificial arrangements lacking genuine economic substance |
| CFC rules | Articles 7-8 | Attribute low-taxed passive income of CFCs to parent |
| Hybrid mismatch rules | Article 9 | Prevent deduction/no-inclusion outcomes from hybrid instruments |

### ATAD II (Directive 2017/952)

Extended the hybrid mismatch rules to cover arrangements with third countries and reverse hybrid mismatches.

### DAC 6 -- Mandatory Disclosure

Directive 2018/822 (DAC 6) requires intermediaries and taxpayers to report cross-border arrangements containing specified "hallmarks" suggesting potential aggressive tax planning. Hallmarks include: main benefit test arrangements, cross-border payments to zero/near-zero tax jurisdictions, transfer pricing arrangements involving hard-to-value intangibles, and arrangements circumventing CRS reporting.

### EU List of Non-Cooperative Jurisdictions

The EU maintains a list of non-cooperative tax jurisdictions, updated periodically by the Council. As of 2025, the list includes jurisdictions that fail to meet standards of tax transparency, fair taxation, and implementation of BEPS measures. Defensive measures against listed jurisdictions include enhanced withholding taxes, non-deductibility of payments, and enhanced CFC rules.

### CJEU Abuse of Rights Doctrine

**Cadbury Schweppes (C-196/04, 2006):** The Court held that CFC legislation is compatible with freedom of establishment only where it targets "wholly artificial arrangements" that do not reflect economic reality. Genuine economic activities, even if tax-motivated, are protected by EU fundamental freedoms.

**Danish Beneficial Ownership Cases (Joined Cases C-116/16 and C-117/16, 2019):** The Court held that the prohibition of abuse of rights is a general principle of EU law, allowing member states to deny withholding tax exemptions under the Parent-Subsidiary and Interest and Royalties Directives where the recipient is a conduit entity without genuine economic substance.

### Unshell Directive Proposal

In December 2021, the European Commission proposed a Directive laying down rules to prevent the misuse of shell entities for tax purposes (ATAD 3/Unshell). The proposal would require EU entities meeting certain "gateway" indicators (cross-border income, outsourced administration, lack of own premises) to demonstrate substance through a minimum substance test.

## Comparative Analysis

### GAAR Design and Application

All four systems include a GAAR, but their design reflects different legal traditions. South Korea's substance-over-form principle is the broadest, giving courts wide discretion. Germany's Section 42 AO requires an "inappropriate" arrangement producing "unintended" tax advantages -- a more structured test. The U.S. economic substance doctrine requires both objective and subjective elements. The ATAD GAAR focuses on "arrangements not genuine" that lack "valid commercial reasons reflecting economic reality."

### Specific vs. General Rules Balance

The United States relies most heavily on specific anti-avoidance rules (Subpart F, GILTI, BEAT, PFIC), with judicial doctrines as a backstop. Germany and the EU maintain a balanced approach with both specific rules (CFC, interest limitation) and a residual GAAR. South Korea increasingly supplements its broad GAAR with targeted specific provisions.

### International Coordination

The OECD BEPS project has driven unprecedented convergence in anti-avoidance measures. The EU has been the most effective implementor, converting BEPS recommendations into binding directives with fixed transposition deadlines. South Korea and Germany have actively participated in BEPS implementation. The United States, while a BEPS participant, has implemented its own versions of anti-avoidance measures (GILTI rather than OECD-model CFC rules) that do not always align with the OECD consensus.

### Transparency and Disclosure

The EU's DAC framework (DAC 1-8) represents the most comprehensive mandatory disclosure and exchange system globally. The U.S. listed transaction reporting is more targeted but narrower. South Korea has expanded disclosure requirements but lacks the breadth of the EU system.
