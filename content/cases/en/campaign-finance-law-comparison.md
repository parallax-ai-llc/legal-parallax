## Summary

Campaign finance regulation -- the legal framework governing the raising, spending, and disclosure of money used to influence elections -- represents one of the most politically contentious areas of law, directly implicating the tension between preventing corruption and protecting political expression. South Korea regulates campaign finance through the Political Funds Act (정치자금법) of 1965, as extensively amended, and the Public Official Election Act (공직선거법) of 1994, administered by the National Election Commission (중앙선거관리위원회, NEC), imposing strict contribution limits, campaign expenditure ceilings, public campaign subsidies, and criminal penalties for violations including imprisonment for candidates who exceed spending limits or accept illegal donations. The United States has the most permissive campaign finance regime among established democracies, shaped fundamentally by the Supreme Court's decision in Buckley v. Valeo, 424 U.S. 1 (1976), equating campaign spending with protected speech under the First Amendment, and further liberalized by Citizens United v. FEC, 558 U.S. 310 (2010), which struck down prohibitions on independent corporate and union expenditures, resulting in a system governed by the Federal Election Campaign Act (FECA, 52 U.S.C. §§ 30101-30146) that permits unlimited independent expenditures through Super PACs and dark money organizations while maintaining individual contribution limits and disclosure requirements for direct campaign contributions. Germany finances political parties primarily through a combination of public funding under the Parteiengesetz (PartG, Political Parties Act) of 1967, membership dues, and private donations with strict disclosure requirements and an absolute upper limit (absolute Obergrenze) on total state funding indexed to inflation, while the EU framework, including the European Parliament's Regulation (EU, Euratom) No 1141/2014 on European political parties and the ECHR's jurisprudence under Articles 10 (freedom of expression) and 11 (freedom of association) and Article 3 of Protocol No. 1 (right to free elections), establishes that states enjoy a wide margin of appreciation in regulating campaign finance but may not impose restrictions that impair the essence of free electoral participation.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's campaign finance system is among the most restrictive in democratic countries, reflecting a history of political corruption scandals and the strong authority of the National Election Commission.

**Political Funds Act (정치자금법):**

| Provision | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| Article 11 | Individual donation limit: 5 million KRW per donation; 20 million KRW annual aggregate |
| Article 12 | Donation to political parties: up to 10 million KRW per year (reduced from 20 million) |
| Article 31 | Corporate and organizational donations prohibited (since 2004) |
| Article 40 | Annual financial reporting to NEC |
| Article 45 | Criminal penalties: imprisonment up to 5 years for illegal donations |

**Public Official Election Act (공직선거법) -- Spending Limits:**

| Office | Spending Ceiling (Approximate, 2024) |
|--------|--------------------------------------|
| Presidential | ~50 billion KRW (~$38 million) |
| National Assembly (constituency) | ~200-400 million KRW (~$150,000-$300,000) |
| Metropolitan/provincial governor | ~5-15 billion KRW |
| Municipal mayor/council | Varies by population |

Exceeding the spending ceiling by more than 1/200th results in automatic invalidation of the election and criminal prosecution of the candidate.

### Public Funding System

South Korea provides substantial public campaign subsidies:
- **Campaign subsidies:** Distributed based on seats in the National Assembly and previous election results
- **Party operating subsidies:** Annual subsidies to registered political parties proportional to seats and votes
- **Policy research subsidies:** Funding for party policy institutes
- **Women candidate subsidies:** Additional funding for parties nominating women candidates above minimum thresholds

### Corporate Donation Ban

The 2004 amendment to the Political Funds Act prohibiting corporate donations was upheld by the Constitutional Court in Decision 2004HunMa554 (2010). The Court held that the ban was a proportionate means of preventing corporate influence over politics and did not violate corporations' freedom of expression or property rights. This stands in sharp contrast to the U.S. Supreme Court's approach in Citizens United.

### Notable Scandals

Korean political history is marked by major campaign finance scandals:
- **1997 Presidential election:** Allegations of illegal chaebol contributions to multiple candidates
- **2002 Presidential election:** Illegal fundraising scandal involving both major party candidates; President Roh Moo-hyun's party fined; opposition Grand National Party leader indicted
- **2016-2017:** Park Geun-hye impeachment, partly triggered by revelations of coerced corporate donations to foundations controlled by confidante Choi Soon-sil
- **2024:** Multiple National Assembly candidates prosecuted for exceeding spending limits

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States campaign finance system is the most permissive among established democracies, shaped by First Amendment jurisprudence equating spending with speech.

**Federal Election Campaign Act (FECA, 52 U.S.C. §§ 30101-30146):**

**Contribution Limits (2024 election cycle):**

| Donor | To Candidate | To PAC | To Party (National) | To Party (State/Local) |
|-------|-------------|--------|---------------------|----------------------|
| Individual | $3,300/election | $5,000/year | $41,300/year | $10,000/year (combined) |
| PAC (multicandidate) | $5,000/election | $5,000/year | $15,000/year | $5,000/year |
| Party committee | $5,000/election | $5,000/year | Unlimited transfers | Unlimited transfers |

**Post-Citizens United Framework:**

| Entity | Direct Contributions | Independent Expenditures | Disclosure |
|--------|---------------------|------------------------|------------|
| Candidates/campaigns | Limited | N/A | Full |
| PACs | Limited receipts | Limited | Full |
| Super PACs | Cannot contribute to candidates | Unlimited | Full |
| 501(c)(4) social welfare organizations | Cannot contribute | Unlimited | Minimal (donors not disclosed) |
| 501(c)(6) trade associations | Cannot contribute | Unlimited | Minimal |

### Key Supreme Court Decisions

**Buckley v. Valeo, 424 U.S. 1 (1976):** The foundational campaign finance case established that:
- Contribution limits are constitutional (preventing quid pro quo corruption)
- Expenditure limits are unconstitutional (impermissible burden on political speech)
- Disclosure requirements are constitutional (informing electorate; deterring corruption)
- Public financing may be conditioned on spending limits (voluntary acceptance)

**Citizens United v. FEC, 558 U.S. 310 (2010):** The Court held that the First Amendment prohibits the government from restricting independent political expenditures by corporations, labor unions, and other associations. The decision:
- Overruled Austin v. Michigan Chamber of Commerce (1990)
- Overruled relevant portions of McConnell v. FEC (2003)
- Enabled the creation of Super PACs (through the subsequent SpeechNow.org v. FEC, D.C. Cir. 2010)
- Did not address disclosure requirements or direct contribution limits

**McCutcheon v. FEC, 572 U.S. 185 (2014):** Struck down aggregate contribution limits (the total amount an individual could contribute to all candidates and parties combined), while leaving individual per-candidate limits intact.

**FEC v. Cruz, 596 U.S. 289 (2022):** Struck down the FECA provision limiting candidates' use of post-election contributions to repay personal campaign loans exceeding $250,000.

### Campaign Finance Scale

Total spending on federal elections has escalated dramatically:
- 2020 presidential election: approximately $14.4 billion (all federal races combined)
- 2024 election cycle: estimated to exceed $16 billion
- Outside spending (Super PACs and dark money): approximately $4.5 billion in 2024 cycle

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's party finance system emphasizes public funding, membership dues, and regulated private donations, with strong disclosure requirements.

**Parteiengesetz (PartG, Political Parties Act, 1967):**

**Public Funding (§§ 18-22 PartG):**

| Funding Source | Amount/Calculation |
|---------------|-------------------|
| Vote subsidy (Wahlkampfkostenerstattung) | €1.13 per valid vote (first 4 million votes); €0.93 per vote thereafter |
| Donation/dues matching | €0.45 per €1 of member dues and eligible donations (up to €3,300 per donor) |
| Absolute upper limit (absolute Obergrenze) | Total state funding may not exceed party's self-generated income; indexed to inflation (~€200 million in 2024 across all parties) |

**Private Donations (§§ 25-27 PartG):**
- No per-donation limit for individuals
- Donations exceeding €10,000: named in annual report
- Donations exceeding €50,000: immediately published by Bundestag President
- Donations from abroad: generally prohibited (EU citizen exceptions)
- Anonymous donations above €500: prohibited
- Corporate donations: permitted (unlike South Korea and direct U.S. contributions)

**Sanctions:**
- Failure to disclose: penalty of three times the undisclosed amount (§ 31c PartG)
- Illegal acceptance: return of donation plus equivalent penalty payment to Bundestag President
- CDU donations scandal (1999-2000): Former Chancellor Kohl's undisclosed donations led to party losing approximately €41 million in public funding

### BVerfG Jurisprudence

**BVerfGE 20, 56 (1966, Party Finance I):** The Court held that direct state funding of parties' general activities was unconstitutional, permitting only reimbursement of campaign costs. This decision fundamentally shaped the structure of German party finance.

**BVerfGE 85, 264 (1992, Party Finance II):** The Court revised its position, permitting general state funding of parties but establishing the relative upper limit (state funding may not exceed self-generated income) and the absolute upper limit, ensuring parties remain rooted in society rather than becoming state-dependent.

**BVerfGE 111, 54 (2004):** The Court addressed the interaction between party finance law and the right to equal electoral participation, holding that the threshold for public funding eligibility (0.5 percent of votes) was constitutional.

### AfD Finance Controversies

The Alternative für Deutschland (AfD) has faced multiple party finance investigations, including allegations of illegal donations from Swiss sources and failure to properly report advertising expenditure. The Bundestag administration has imposed penalties exceeding €400,000 against the AfD for campaign finance violations.

## ECHR / EU

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Bowman v. United Kingdom (Application No. 24839/94, February 19, 1998):** The Court found a violation of Article 10 (freedom of expression) where British electoral law imposed a £5 spending limit on third-party expenditure in support of or against a candidate during an election period. The Court held that while the aim of equalizing the financial means of candidates was legitimate, the spending limit was so low as to constitute a disproportionate restriction on political expression.

**Parti nationaliste basque -- Organisation régionale d'Iparralde v. France (Application No. 71251/01, June 7, 2007):** The Court upheld France's ban on foreign donations to political parties, finding that the aim of preventing foreign interference in elections justified the restriction under Article 11 (freedom of association).

**Animal Defenders International v. United Kingdom (Application No. 48876/08, Grand Chamber, April 22, 2013):** The Court upheld the UK's blanket ban on political advertising in broadcast media, finding no violation of Article 10. The decision endorsed a leveling-the-playing-field rationale, holding that the general measure was necessary in a democratic society to prevent wealthy groups from gaining undue political influence through media.

**Article 3, Protocol No. 1 (Right to Free Elections):** While this provision primarily concerns voting rights, the Court has recognized that campaign finance regulation affects the fairness of elections and thus falls within its scope. States enjoy a wide margin of appreciation in this area.

### EU Political Party Regulation

**Regulation (EU, Euratom) No 1141/2014:** Establishes the Authority for European Political Parties and European Political Foundations (APPF), governing:

| Provision | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| Registration | European political parties must be registered with the APPF |
| Funding | EU budget funding based on EP representation (85%) and equal share (15%) |
| Donations | Maximum €18,000 per donor per year from EU citizens; anonymous donations prohibited above €1,500 |
| Transparency | Annual accounts and donor lists published |
| Sanctions | Deregistration; financial penalties; recovery of unduly paid amounts |

**2024 Reform (Regulation (EU, Euratom) 2024/2504):** Strengthened transparency requirements, expanded the ban on foreign donations, and introduced additional sanctions for non-compliance.

### Venice Commission Standards

The Council of Europe's Venice Commission (European Commission for Democracy through Law) has issued comprehensive guidelines on political party financing:
- **CDL-AD(2010)024:** Guidelines on Political Party Regulation, recommending balanced public and private funding, contribution limits, transparency, independent oversight, and proportionate sanctions
- **CDL-AD(2020)033:** Updated guidelines addressing digital campaigning, online advertising transparency, and micro-targeting

## Comparative Analysis

### Regulatory Philosophy

The jurisdictions reflect fundamentally different approaches to the relationship between money and politics:

**Equality-oriented (South Korea, Germany):** Campaign finance regulation is designed to ensure equal opportunity in political competition. Spending limits (South Korea), public funding (both), and corporate donation bans (South Korea) reflect a commitment to preventing wealth-based political inequality.

**Liberty-oriented (United States):** Political spending is treated as protected expression under the First Amendment. Restrictions are permissible only to prevent quid pro quo corruption, not to equalize political influence. This approach permits vast spending inequalities.

**Subsidiarity-based (EU/ECHR):** States enjoy wide discretion in choosing their regulatory model, with supranational law establishing minimum standards for transparency and limits on foreign interference.

### Public Funding

| Jurisdiction | Public Funding Model | Significance |
|-------------|---------------------|-------------|
| South Korea | Substantial; based on seats and votes | Major funding source for parties |
| United States | Presidential fund (optional; declined since 2012) | Negligible |
| Germany | Substantial; matching + vote subsidy | Major funding source (~30-40% of party income) |
| EU | Budget funding for EU-level parties | Primary funding source for European parties |

### Enforcement

Enforcement effectiveness varies dramatically:
- **South Korea:** NEC with criminal prosecution authority; high-profile prosecutions; candidates disqualified
- **Germany:** Bundestag President oversees annual reports; penalties up to 3x undisclosed amount; BVerfG enforcement
- **United States:** FEC hampered by partisan gridlock (3 Republican, 3 Democratic commissioners); frequent deadlock on enforcement
- **EU:** APPF with limited enforcement capacity

### Digital Political Advertising

The rise of online political advertising has created significant regulatory gaps:

- **South Korea:** Public Official Election Act Article 82-7 regulates internet political advertising; real-name verification required for election-related online communications
- **United States:** No comprehensive federal law governing online political advertising; the Honest Ads Act (proposed but never enacted) would have extended broadcast transparency requirements to digital platforms; FEC has not updated regulations to adequately address digital spending
- **Germany/EU:** Digital Services Act (DSA) Article 39 requires platforms to label political advertising and provide transparency on targeting; Germany's Medienstaatsvertrag (2020) imposes transparency obligations on political advertising across digital platforms
- **AI-generated content:** The use of deepfakes and AI-generated campaign materials raises urgent questions about disclosure requirements; the EU AI Act (2024) requires disclosure of AI-generated political content; South Korea amended the Public Official Election Act in 2024 to prohibit deepfake political advertisements within 90 days of an election

### Foreign Interference

Preventing foreign influence in domestic elections has become a priority across all jurisdictions:

- **South Korea:** Political Funds Act prohibits foreign contributions; NEC monitors foreign interference in elections
- **United States:** FECA § 30121 prohibits foreign national contributions; concerns persist about enforcement gaps regarding dark money and digital advertising; Foreign Agents Registration Act (FARA) requires registration of agents acting on behalf of foreign principals
- **Germany:** PartG § 25(2) prohibits donations from foreign nationals outside the EU exceeding €1,000; BfV (domestic intelligence) monitors foreign election interference
- **EU:** Regulation 2024/900 on European political advertising transparency requires disclosure of funding sources for political advertising; proposed Defence of Democracy package addresses foreign covert influence

### Super PACs and Independent Expenditures

The U.S. model of unlimited independent expenditures through Super PACs has no direct parallel in other jurisdictions:

- Post-Citizens United, independent expenditure groups spent over $2.1 billion in the 2024 election cycle
- South Korea, Germany, and the EU all impose spending limits that effectively prevent the emergence of Super PAC equivalents
- The ECHR has held that spending limits can be justified to protect "the equality of the struggle between candidates" (Bowman v. UK), a rationale explicitly rejected by the U.S. Supreme Court in Buckley and Citizens United
