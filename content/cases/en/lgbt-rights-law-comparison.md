## Summary

LGBT rights -- the legal protections afforded to lesbian, gay, bisexual, and transgender individuals against discrimination, criminalization, and violence, as well as the recognition of same-sex relationships and gender identity -- have undergone a dramatic expansion in many legal systems over the past several decades while remaining deeply contested or non-existent in others. South Korea does not criminalize homosexuality among civilians, but Article 92-6 of the Military Criminal Act (군형법 제92조의6) criminalizes consensual sexual conduct between military service members, with the Constitutional Court having upheld this provision in 2002, 2011, and 2016 before declaring it unconstitutional in a landmark decision on October 26, 2023 (2017헌가16); South Korea lacks comprehensive anti-discrimination legislation covering sexual orientation and gender identity, though the National Human Rights Commission (국가인권위원회) includes sexual orientation among its 19 protected grounds and the Supreme Court's 2023 health insurance decision represented a breakthrough in same-sex partnership recognition. The United States has seen rapid legal evolution, with the Supreme Court decriminalizing sodomy in Lawrence v. Texas, 539 U.S. 558 (2003), recognizing same-sex marriage in Obergefell v. Hodges (2015), and holding that Title VII's sex discrimination prohibition encompasses sexual orientation and transgender status in Bostock v. Clayton County, 590 U.S. 644 (2020), though no comprehensive federal anti-discrimination statute covers sexual orientation and gender identity in housing, public accommodations, and education, and ongoing "anti-LGBTQ+" state legislation targeting transgender rights has intensified since 2021. Germany has developed a progressive framework including the decriminalization of homosexuality through the abolition of Section 175 StGB in 1994, marriage equality since 2017, and the Self-Determination Act (Selbstbestimmungsgesetz, SBGG) enacted on April 12, 2024, replacing the Transsexuellengesetz and allowing gender marker changes through a simple declaration without medical requirements. The European framework provides protection through ECHR Articles 8 and 14, with the European Court of Human Rights having developed extensive jurisprudence from Dudgeon v. United Kingdom (1981) through Fedotova and Others v. Russia (2023), while EU law prohibits sexual orientation discrimination in employment under Directive 2000/78/EC and the proposed Horizontal Anti-Discrimination Directive would extend protections to other areas.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea does not have specific criminal laws prohibiting homosexuality among civilians. However, the absence of comprehensive anti-discrimination protections and the persistence of military criminal provisions have shaped the legal landscape significantly.

### Military Criminal Act (군형법 제92조의6)

The most significant legal restriction on homosexuality was Article 92-6 of the Military Criminal Act, which criminalized "anal intercourse" (추행) between military service members with penalties of up to two years' imprisonment. Given that all Korean men serve mandatory military service, this provision affected a substantial portion of the male population.

The Constitutional Court upheld the constitutionality of Article 92-6 on three occasions: in 2002 (2001헌바70), 2011 (2008헌가21), and 2016 (2012헌바258), with narrow majorities finding the provision justified by the need to maintain military discipline and morale.

On October 26, 2023, the Constitutional Court reversed course in its decision 2017헌가16, ruling 5-4 that Article 92-6 violated the right to equality and the right to sexual self-determination. The majority held that the provision's discriminatory criminalization of consensual same-sex conduct between adults could not be justified by military discipline concerns.

### Anti-Discrimination Gap

The absence of comprehensive anti-discrimination legislation remains the most significant structural gap. The National Human Rights Commission Act lists sexual orientation as one of 19 protected grounds, but the NHRC can only issue non-binding recommendations. The proposed Anti-Discrimination Act (차별금지법) has repeatedly failed in the National Assembly, with sexual orientation and gender identity provisions being the primary points of contention.

### Gender Identity

South Korean law allows individuals to change their legal gender, but the process requires a court order. The Supreme Court's 2006 decision established criteria including a diagnosis of gender identity disorder, completion of sex reassignment surgery, being unmarried, having no minor children, and being an adult. These requirements, particularly the surgery and childlessness requirements, have been criticized by human rights organizations as imposing unreasonable barriers.

## United States

### Legal Framework

**Lawrence v. Texas, 539 U.S. 558 (2003):** The Supreme Court struck down Texas's sodomy law, holding that the liberty interest protected by the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment encompasses the right of adults to engage in consensual intimate conduct.

**Obergefell v. Hodges, 576 U.S. 644 (2015):** Established the constitutional right to same-sex marriage.

**Bostock v. Clayton County, 590 U.S. 644 (2020):** Held that Title VII's prohibition on discrimination "because of sex" encompasses discrimination based on sexual orientation and transgender status, establishing federal employment protections.

### Equality Act (Proposed)

The Equality Act, which would amend the Civil Rights Act of 1964 to explicitly prohibit discrimination based on sexual orientation and gender identity in employment, housing, public accommodations, education, and other areas, has passed the House of Representatives multiple times but has not been enacted by the Senate.

### State-Level Anti-LGBTQ+ Legislation

Since 2021, there has been a significant increase in state legislation targeting LGBTQ+ rights, particularly transgender rights:

| Category | Number of States (2024) | Examples |
|----------|------------------------|---------|
| Bans on gender-affirming care for minors | 24 states | Arkansas (first to enact, 2021); Texas, Florida |
| Bathroom bills | 10+ states | North Carolina HB2 (2016, repealed); subsequent state laws |
| Restrictions on LGBTQ+ education content | 10+ states | Florida "Don't Say Gay" law (2022) |
| Drag performance restrictions | 5+ states | Tennessee (struck down by courts) |
| Sports participation restrictions for transgender athletes | 25+ states | Various laws restricting based on sex assigned at birth |

The Supreme Court has not yet addressed the constitutionality of these state laws, though lower courts have issued mixed rulings. In L.W. v. Skrmetti (2025), the Supreme Court upheld Tennessee's ban on gender-affirming care for transgender minors, applying rational basis review.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

**Section 175 StGB:** Germany's criminalization of male homosexuality under Section 175 StGB dates to the formation of the German Empire in 1871. The provision was substantially tightened under the Nazi regime in 1935 and was maintained in the Federal Republic until 1969, when consensual homosexual acts between adults over 21 were decriminalized. The age of consent was equalized at 14 (matching heterosexual conduct) in 1994 with the formal abolition of Section 175. In 2002, the Bundestag passed a resolution formally apologizing for the persecution of homosexuals under Section 175.

**Marriage Equality (2017):** Same-sex marriage has been legal since October 1, 2017, with full equal rights including joint adoption.

### Self-Determination Act (Selbstbestimmungsgesetz, SBGG, 2024)

The SBGG, enacted on April 12, 2024, and effective November 1, 2024, represents a landmark reform of gender identity recognition in Germany. The law replaces the Transsexuellengesetz (TSG, 1980), which had required medical assessments, court proceedings, and (until struck down by the Federal Constitutional Court) surgical intervention and sterilization.

Key provisions of the SBGG:
- Gender marker and first name changes are effected through a simple declaration at the Standesamt (civil registry office)
- No medical certificates, diagnoses, or surgical procedures required
- Three-month waiting period between declaration and change taking effect
- Minors aged 14 and older may apply with parental consent (or family court authorization if consent is withheld)
- Changes may be reversed through the same procedure, with a one-year waiting period

### AGG Protections

The Allgemeines Gleichbehandlungsgesetz (AGG, 2006) prohibits discrimination based on sexual orientation in employment, including hiring, working conditions, promotion, and dismissal. However, the AGG provides an exception for religious organizations, which may require employees to adhere to the organization's religious ethos (Section 9 AGG), an exception that has been subject to CJEU scrutiny in Egenberger (C-414/16, 2018) and IR v. JQ (C-68/17, 2018).

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Dudgeon v. United Kingdom (Application No. 7525/76, 1981):** The Court's first LGBT rights case held that Northern Ireland's criminalization of consensual male homosexual conduct violated Article 8 (right to respect for private life). This decision effectively required all Council of Europe member states to decriminalize homosexuality.

**Lustig-Prean and Beckett v. United Kingdom (Application Nos. 31417/96 and 32377/96, 1999):** Held that the investigation and discharge of service members from the UK armed forces on the basis of their homosexuality violated Article 8.

**Goodwin v. United Kingdom (Application No. 28957/95, Grand Chamber, 2002):** Held that the UK's failure to legally recognize the post-operative gender of a transgender person violated Articles 8 and 12, establishing that states must provide legal recognition of gender reassignment.

**Identoba and Others v. Georgia (Application No. 73235/12, 2015):** Held that Georgia's failure to protect LGBTQ+ marchers from violence violated Articles 3 and 14, establishing positive obligations to protect against homophobic and transphobic violence.

**A.D. and Others v. Georgia (Application No. 57864/17, 2023):** Found violations of Articles 3 and 14 arising from the failure of Georgian authorities to adequately investigate and prosecute a violent attack on an LGBTQ+ pride event.

### EU Framework

**Directive 2000/78/EC (Employment Equality):** Prohibits discrimination based on sexual orientation in employment. The CJEU has applied this directive in numerous cases, establishing that it covers pension benefits for same-sex partners (Maruko, C-267/06, 2008) and requires equal treatment in survivor's pension rights (Hay, C-267/12, 2013).

**Proposed Horizontal Anti-Discrimination Directive:** A proposed directive extending anti-discrimination protections based on sexual orientation, disability, age, and religion beyond employment to social protection, education, and goods and services has been blocked in the Council since 2008 due to unanimity requirements.

**EU LGBTIQ Equality Strategy 2020-2025:** The European Commission adopted a strategy addressing discrimination, safety, inclusiveness, and equality for LGBTIQ persons across the EU, including commitments to strengthen enforcement of existing protections and promote mutual recognition of family relationships across borders.

## Comparative Analysis

### Decriminalization Trajectories

All four jurisdictions have moved toward decriminalization, but at different speeds and through different mechanisms. The ECHR established the requirement of decriminalization in 1981 (Dudgeon). The United States decriminalized in 2003 (Lawrence). Germany completed decriminalization in 1994. South Korea's civilian law never criminalized homosexuality, but the military provision was not struck down until 2023. The convergence toward decriminalization is near-universal among Council of Europe member states.

### Anti-Discrimination Protection Spectrum

Protection levels vary significantly. Germany and the EU provide statutory employment protections. The United States provides employment protection through Title VII as interpreted in Bostock but lacks comprehensive federal protection in other domains. South Korea provides only advisory-level protection through the NHRC. The ECHR framework requires states to protect against discrimination but does not mandate specific legislative approaches.

### Gender Identity Recognition

Germany's SBGG (2024) represents the most progressive approach, requiring only self-declaration. Several ECHR member states (including Argentina, Denmark, Ireland, and Malta) have adopted similar self-determination models. The United States has a fragmented approach varying by state. South Korea requires court-ordered change with medical prerequisites. The ECHR requires legal recognition (Goodwin) but has not mandated specific procedures, leaving states a margin of appreciation on the requirements.

### Counter-Movements

The United States is unique among the four jurisdictions in experiencing a significant legislative backlash against LGBTQ+ rights, particularly regarding transgender rights. While South Korea lacks progressive legislation, it has not seen the same degree of regressive legislative activity. Germany and EU institutions have moved consistently toward expanded protections.
