## Summary

Drug decriminalization -- the reduction or elimination of criminal penalties for personal possession or use of controlled substances while typically maintaining prohibitions on manufacture, trafficking, and distribution -- represents one of the most contested areas of contemporary legal reform, challenging traditional punitive approaches to drug control with public health-oriented alternatives. South Korea maintains one of the strictest anti-drug regimes among developed nations, with the Act on the Control of Narcotics (마약류 관리에 관한 법률) imposing penalties of up to life imprisonment for drug trafficking and up to 10 years for simple possession; there is no decriminalization of any controlled substance, and the country's near-zero-tolerance approach is reinforced by mandatory drug testing in certain professions and aggressive prosecution of Korean nationals who consume drugs abroad under the extraterritorial jurisdiction provisions of Article 4. The United States maintains federal criminal prohibition of all Schedule I substances under the Controlled Substances Act (21 U.S.C. Section 801 et seq.), but state-level reforms have created a complex patchwork: Oregon became the first state to decriminalize personal possession of all drugs through Measure 110 in 2020 (though this was substantially reversed by HB 4002 in 2024), 24 states and the District of Columbia have legalized recreational marijuana, and numerous jurisdictions have adopted harm reduction measures including needle exchanges and naloxone access laws. Germany has traditionally classified drug possession for personal use as a criminal offense under the Betäubungsmittelgesetz (BtMG), but Section 31a BtMG grants prosecutors discretion to dismiss cases involving small quantities for personal consumption; the landmark Cannabisgesetz (Cannabis Act, KCanG), which entered into force on April 1, 2024, legalized possession of up to 25 grams of cannabis for personal use by adults, making Germany the largest EU country to legalize recreational cannabis. At the European level, the European Monitoring Centre for Drugs and Drug Addiction (EMCDDA, renamed EUDA in 2024) tracks member state drug policies, the EU Drugs Strategy 2021-2025 and EU Drugs Action Plan emphasize harm reduction alongside supply reduction, and the European Court of Human Rights has recognized a wide margin of appreciation for states in drug policy while holding that states bear positive obligations to protect drug users' right to life and health under Articles 2 and 3 ECHR.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea maintains one of the most prohibitive drug control regimes among OECD nations, reflecting deep cultural stigma against drug use and a law enforcement approach rooted in deterrence.

**Act on the Control of Narcotics (마약류 관리에 관한 법률):** This comprehensive statute, originally enacted in 2000 by consolidating three prior drug control laws, classifies controlled substances into three categories: narcotics (마약), psychotropic substances (향정신성의약품), and hemp/cannabis (대마). Key penalty provisions include:

- **Article 58:** Manufacturing, importing, or trafficking narcotics: imprisonment of 5 years or more, up to life imprisonment.
- **Article 59:** Possession of narcotics for non-medical purposes: imprisonment of up to 10 years.
- **Article 60:** Use or administration of narcotics: imprisonment of up to 10 years.
- **Article 61:** Possession or use of psychotropic substances: imprisonment of up to 5 years.
- **Article 4 (Extraterritorial Application):** Korean nationals who use or possess controlled substances abroad may be prosecuted upon return to South Korea. This provision is actively enforced through drug testing at airports and border crossings.

**Juvenile Drug Offenses:** Under the Juvenile Act (소년법), drug offenders under 19 may be subject to protective dispositions including institutional treatment rather than criminal prosecution, though criminal prosecution remains available for serious offenses.

### Enforcement and Cultural Context

South Korea's aggressive anti-drug stance is reflected in enforcement statistics: approximately 18,000 drug arrests were recorded in 2023, a significant increase from approximately 11,000 in 2018. The Supreme Prosecutors' Office operates a dedicated Narcotics Crime Division, and the Korea Customs Service conducts extensive border screening including package interception and canine units.

Korean celebrities and public figures who are discovered to have used drugs face severe professional and social consequences, often resulting in permanent career termination. The cultural stigma against drug use is significantly stronger than in Western nations, with drug use widely perceived as a moral failure rather than a health issue.

### Medical Marijuana

South Korea legalized limited medical cannabis use in March 2019 through amendments to the Act on the Control of Narcotics, becoming the first East Asian country to do so. However, the framework is extremely restrictive: patients must obtain individual import approval from the Korea Orphan Drug Center for specific FDA-approved cannabis-derived medications (such as Epidiolex for epilepsy), and no domestic cultivation or dispensary system exists.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States presents the most internally contradictory drug policy framework among developed nations, with a strict federal prohibition regime coexisting alongside rapidly expanding state-level legalization and decriminalization.

**Controlled Substances Act (CSA), 21 U.S.C. Section 801 et seq.:** Enacted as Title II of the Comprehensive Drug Abuse Prevention and Control Act of 1970, the CSA establishes five schedules of controlled substances. Cannabis remains classified as Schedule I (high potential for abuse, no currently accepted medical use), alongside heroin, LSD, and MDMA. The Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA) administers scheduling decisions, subject to judicial review.

**Federal Penalties (21 U.S.C. Section 844):** Simple possession of any controlled substance for personal use carries a penalty of up to 1 year imprisonment and/or $1,000 fine for a first offense, 15 days to 2 years for a second offense, and 90 days to 3 years for subsequent offenses.

**State Cannabis Legalization:** As of 2025, 24 states and the District of Columbia have legalized recreational cannabis for adults 21 and over, beginning with Colorado and Washington in 2012 (ballot initiatives). An additional 14 states have legalized medical cannabis only. The federal-state conflict remains unresolved; the Cole Memorandum (2013) directed federal prosecutors to exercise restraint in states with robust regulatory frameworks, was rescinded by Attorney General Jeff Sessions in 2018 (Sessions Memorandum), and the Biden administration restored prosecutorial discretion guidelines in 2022.

**Oregon Measure 110 (2020):** Oregon became the first state to decriminalize personal possession of all drugs through a ballot measure approved on November 3, 2020, effective February 1, 2021. The measure reclassified possession of small amounts of any controlled substance from a criminal misdemeanor to a Class E violation (maximum $100 fine, waivable by calling a treatment hotline). However, facing criticism regarding increased public drug use, inadequate treatment infrastructure, and rising overdose deaths, the Oregon legislature enacted HB 4002 on March 1, 2024, partially recriminalizing possession as a misdemeanor while expanding treatment mandates and drug courts.

**Drug Courts:** Over 3,000 drug courts operate across the United States, offering treatment-based alternatives to incarceration for nonviolent drug offenders. The first drug court was established in Miami-Dade County, Florida, in 1989 by Judge Herbert Klein. Drug courts have been shown to reduce recidivism by 8-14 percent compared to traditional criminal adjudication.

### Harm Reduction

The legal landscape for harm reduction has expanded significantly. As of 2025, 42 states have enacted naloxone access laws removing barriers to the distribution and administration of the opioid overdose reversal medication. Syringe service programs (needle exchanges) operate in 43 states. Supervised consumption sites (SCS) remain legally controversial: New York City opened the first sanctioned SCS in the United States (OnPoint NYC) in November 2021, operating under a prosecutorial non-enforcement agreement rather than explicit statutory authorization.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's drug policy has evolved from a strictly prohibitive stance under the Betäubungsmittelgesetz to a pragmatic harm reduction approach, culminating in the landmark legalization of recreational cannabis in 2024.

**Betäubungsmittelgesetz (BtMG) -- Narcotics Act (1981):** The BtMG classifies controlled substances into three Anlagen (schedules) and criminalizes the unauthorized acquisition, possession, manufacture, and trafficking of listed substances.

- **Section 29(1):** Unauthorized possession, acquisition, or cultivation of narcotics is punishable by imprisonment of up to 5 years or a fine.
- **Section 29(3):** Trafficking in narcotics is punishable by imprisonment of 1 to 15 years.
- **Section 29a:** Trafficking involving not-insignificant quantities is punishable by imprisonment of at least 1 year (Verbrechen).
- **Section 30:** Large-scale trafficking or trafficking as a member of a criminal organization is punishable by imprisonment of 2 to 15 years.
- **Section 30a:** Armed trafficking or trafficking by organized groups is punishable by imprisonment of at least 5 years.

**Section 31a BtMG -- Prosecutorial Discretion:** This crucial provision allows prosecutors to dismiss proceedings for offenses involving small quantities of narcotics intended for personal consumption when the offender's guilt is considered minor and there is no public interest in prosecution. The Bundesverfassungsgericht upheld this provision in its landmark Cannabis Decision of March 9, 1994 (BVerfGE 90, 145), holding that while the prohibition of cannabis was constitutional, the principle of proportionality required that prosecution of possession of small quantities for personal use be subject to prosecutorial discretion. The definition of "small quantity" (geringe Menge) varies by Land, ranging from 6 grams in Bavaria to 15 grams in Berlin for cannabis.

**Cannabisgesetz (KCanG) -- Cannabis Act (2024):** On April 1, 2024, Germany's Cannabisgesetz entered into force, making Germany the largest EU country to legalize recreational cannabis. Key provisions include:

- **Personal Possession:** Adults 18 and over may possess up to 25 grams of cannabis in public and up to 50 grams at home.
- **Home Cultivation:** Adults may cultivate up to three cannabis plants for personal use.
- **Cannabis Social Clubs (Anbauvereinigungen):** Non-profit associations with a maximum of 500 members may collectively cultivate and distribute cannabis to members, with monthly distribution limited to 25 grams per member (50 grams for ages 18-20).
- **Proximity Restrictions:** Cannabis consumption is prohibited within 200 meters of schools, playgrounds, sports facilities, and within the areas of Cannabis Social Clubs.
- **Driving:** No specific THC limit was initially set in the KCanG; the Straßenverkehrsgesetz was subsequently amended in August 2024 to establish a THC blood serum limit of 3.5 nanograms per milliliter.

**Therapy statt Strafe (Therapy Instead of Punishment) -- Section 35 BtMG:** This provision allows courts to suspend the execution of prison sentences of up to 2 years for drug-dependent offenders who agree to undergo drug treatment. The treatment period is credited against the sentence. If the offender completes treatment successfully, the remaining sentence may be remitted.

### Drug Consumption Rooms

Germany operates approximately 30 drug consumption rooms (Drogenkonsumräume) in cities including Frankfurt, Hamburg, Berlin, and Düsseldorf, authorized under Section 10a BtMG (introduced by amendment in 2000). These facilities provide supervised spaces for drug use, sterile equipment, overdose prevention, and referrals to treatment services. The Frankfurt model, dating to the early 1990s, is internationally recognized as a pioneering harm reduction approach.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Drug Policy Framework

**EU Drugs Strategy 2021-2025:** Adopted by the Council on December 18, 2020, the Strategy identifies three priority areas: (1) reducing drug supply, (2) reducing drug demand, and (3) addressing drug-related harms. The Strategy explicitly endorses harm reduction as a core component of drug policy, including needle and syringe programs, opioid substitution treatment, naloxone distribution, and drug checking services.

**EU Drugs Action Plan 2021-2025:** This implementing document translates the Strategy into specific actions, including strengthening early warning systems for new psychoactive substances, promoting alternatives to coercive sanctions for drug-using offenders, and enhancing cooperation on drug precursor control.

**Council Framework Decision 2004/757/JHA:** This Framework Decision establishes minimum provisions on the constituent elements of criminal acts and penalties in the field of illicit drug trafficking. Article 2 requires member states to criminalize the production, manufacture, extraction, preparation, offering, distribution, sale, and delivery of drugs. Article 5 sets minimum maximum penalties of 1-3 years for basic trafficking offenses. Notably, the Decision does not require criminalization of personal possession for use, leaving this to member state discretion.

### European Monitoring Centre for Drugs and Drug Addiction (EMCDDA/EUDA)

The EMCDDA, established by Regulation (EEC) No 302/93 and recast as the EU Drugs Agency (EUDA) by Regulation (EU) 2023/1322 effective July 2, 2024, monitors drug trends, collects data, and provides evidence-based analysis to inform EU and member state drug policies. The Agency publishes the annual European Drug Report and operates the EU Early Warning System for new psychoactive substances.

### European Court of Human Rights

**Wenner v. Germany (Application No. 62303/13, Judgment of September 1, 2016):** The Court found a violation of Article 3 ECHR where German prison authorities refused to provide opioid substitution treatment (methadone) to a heroin-dependent prisoner with chronic pain. The Court held that the refusal to provide medically indicated drug treatment in detention constituted inhuman treatment, establishing that drug-dependent detainees have a right to adequate medical care including substitution therapy.

**Klauz v. Croatia (Application No. 28963/10, Judgment of July 18, 2013):** The Court held that Croatia's criminal prosecution and conviction of an individual for possession of 0.21 grams of heroin for personal use did not violate Article 8, recognizing the state's wide margin of appreciation in drug policy. The Court noted that drug policy falls within the sphere of public health and social policy where states enjoy significant discretion.

**Pham v. Italy (Application No. 5765/04, Decision of June 17, 2007):** The Court reiterated that states have no obligation under the Convention to decriminalize drug possession, and that the choice between punitive and therapeutic approaches to drug policy falls within the state's margin of appreciation.

### Portugal Model

While not within the four core jurisdictions, Portugal's decriminalization framework (Law 30/2000, effective July 1, 2001) warrants mention as the most influential model in European drug policy. Portugal decriminalized personal possession of all drugs (defined as amounts not exceeding a 10-day personal supply), replacing criminal penalties with administrative proceedings before Commissions for the Dissuasion of Drug Addiction (Comissões para a Dissuasão da Toxicodependência). The model has been associated with reductions in drug-related deaths, HIV infections among drug users, and incarceration rates, and has influenced reform discussions in Germany and other EU member states.

## Comparative Analysis

### Philosophical Approaches

The four jurisdictions represent distinct philosophical approaches to drug control. South Korea exemplifies the deterrence model, maintaining severe penalties and cultural stigma as primary tools. The United States exhibits an evolving and internally contradictory approach, combining federal prohibition with state-level experimentation ranging from legalization to harm reduction. Germany has transitioned from a modified prohibition model to a regulated legalization approach for cannabis while maintaining prohibition with harm reduction for other substances. The EU framework endorses a balanced approach combining supply reduction, demand reduction, and harm reduction.

### Cannabis Policy Divergence

Cannabis policy represents the sharpest divergence: South Korea criminalizes any possession with penalties of up to 10 years; the United States maintains federal prohibition while 24 states have legalized; Germany has legalized possession of up to 25 grams nationally; and EU member states span the full spectrum from prohibition to decriminalization to regulated sale. This divergence illustrates how the same substance can be treated as a dangerous narcotic or a regulated consumer product depending on jurisdictional context.

### Harm Reduction

Germany and the EU framework lead in institutionalizing harm reduction through drug consumption rooms, needle exchange programs, and opioid substitution therapy. South Korea has minimal harm reduction infrastructure. The United States has rapidly expanded naloxone access and syringe programs but remains cautious about supervised consumption sites.

### Extraterritoriality

South Korea's extraterritorial drug jurisdiction (Article 4) is unique among the four jurisdictions and reflects the intensity of Korea's anti-drug stance. Korean nationals traveling abroad face prosecution upon return if found to have consumed drugs, a provision actively enforced through airport drug testing.
