## Summary

Emotional distress -- tortious liability for causing severe psychological harm through outrageous conduct or negligent action -- represents one of the most rapidly evolving and contentious areas of tort law, balancing the legitimate need to compensate genuine psychological injury against concerns about fraudulent claims, unbounded liability, and the chilling effect on freedom of expression. South Korea recognizes claims for mental anguish (정신적 고통) under the general tort provision of Civil Act (민법) Article 750 and the specific non-pecuniary damages provision of Article 751, with the Supreme Court awarding consolation money (위자료) for emotional harm caused by both intentional and negligent conduct, including workplace harassment codified under the Labor Standards Act (근로기준법) Article 76-2 as amended in 2019. The United States recognizes two distinct torts -- intentional infliction of emotional distress (IIED), requiring "outrageous" conduct as defined in the Restatement (Second) of Torts Section 46, and negligent infliction of emotional distress (NIED), with jurisdictions divided among the impact rule, zone of danger test, and bystander recovery under Dillon v. Legg (1968) and Thing v. La Chusa (1989) -- with First Amendment constraints limiting emotional distress claims by public figures following Hustler Magazine v. Falwell (1988) and Snyder v. Phelps (2011). Germany compensates non-material damage (immaterieller Schaden) through Schmerzensgeld under BGB Section 253(2), limited to enumerated categories of injury (body, health, freedom, sexual self-determination), with the Bundesgerichtshof developing the "shock damage" (Schockschaden) doctrine for bystander emotional harm. At the European level, the ECHR has recognized claims for non-pecuniary damage under Article 41 for violations causing psychological suffering, the Court of Justice has interpreted EU anti-discrimination directives as requiring compensation for emotional distress caused by discrimination, and the GDPR Article 82 expressly covers non-material damage from data protection violations.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's approach to emotional distress compensation is integrated into the general tort framework rather than treated as a separate cause of action.

### Consolation Money (위자료)

Article 751 of the Civil Act provides: "A person who is liable for damages for unlawful act shall also compensate for non-pecuniary damages, even if such damages are to body, liberty, or reputation" (타인의 신체, 자유 또는 명예를 해하거나 기타 정신상 고통을 가한 자는 재산 이외의 손해에 대하여도 배상할 책임이 있다). Korean courts award 위자료 (consolation money) for emotional distress across a wide range of contexts.

The Supreme Court has established that consolation money awards are determined considering all circumstances, including the nature and severity of the wrongful act, the degree of emotional suffering, the defendant's financial capacity, and the social status of the parties (Supreme Court Decision 2009Da72910, 2012).

### Workplace Harassment

The 2019 amendment to the Labor Standards Act (근로기준법) inserted Article 76-2, explicitly prohibiting workplace harassment (직장 내 괴롭힘): conduct by an employer or employee that causes physical or mental distress to another employee or degrades the work environment by exploiting a superior position in the workplace beyond the appropriate scope of work. Victims may seek consolation money under the Civil Act and may file complaints with the Ministry of Employment and Labor.

### Sexual Harassment Emotional Distress

The Equal Employment Opportunity and Work-Family Balance Assistance Act (남녀고용평등과 일·가정 양립 지원에 관한 법률) addresses sexual harassment in the workplace. The Supreme Court has awarded substantial consolation money in sexual harassment cases, with amounts increasing significantly in recent years. In Supreme Court Decision 2017Da270934 (2018), the Court emphasized that sexual harassment causes dignity harm (인격권 침해) warranting enhanced compensation.

### Death-Related Emotional Distress

Article 752 of the Civil Act establishes a statutory right to consolation money for family members in cases of death caused by tortious conduct. The statute enumerates specific relatives entitled to claim (spouse, lineal ascendants and descendants, and siblings in some circumstances), though courts have expanded standing through Article 751.

## United States

### Legal Framework

American law recognizes emotional distress as an independent basis for tort recovery through two distinct causes of action.

### Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress (IIED)

The Restatement (Second) of Torts Section 46 establishes the elements of IIED: (1) the defendant acted intentionally or recklessly; (2) the conduct was extreme and outrageous; (3) the conduct caused emotional distress; and (4) the emotional distress was severe.

The "extreme and outrageous" standard is the primary gatekeeper. Comment d provides that "the conduct must be so outrageous in character, and so extreme in degree, as to go beyond all possible bounds of decency, and to be regarded as atrocious, and utterly intolerable in a civilized community." Courts have consistently held that mere insults, annoyances, and petty oppressions are insufficient.

### First Amendment Limitations

**Hustler Magazine, Inc. v. Falwell, 485 U.S. 46 (1988):** The Supreme Court unanimously held that a public figure cannot recover for IIED based on a parody that could not reasonably be interpreted as stating actual facts. The decision established that the First Amendment requires public figure IIED plaintiffs to prove that the publication contained a false statement of fact made with "actual malice" (knowledge of falsity or reckless disregard for truth).

**Snyder v. Phelps, 562 U.S. 443 (2011):** The Court held that the Westboro Baptist Church's picketing near a military funeral was protected speech under the First Amendment, precluding an IIED claim by the deceased soldier's father. The decision reinforced that speech on matters of public concern is entitled to special protection against emotional distress claims.

### Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress (NIED)

Jurisdictions apply one of three principal approaches to NIED:

| Approach | Rule | Jurisdictions |
|----------|------|---------------|
| **Impact rule** | Physical impact required; emotional distress recoverable as consequential damage | Indiana, Florida (modified), Georgia |
| **Zone of danger** | Plaintiff must be in zone of physical danger from defendant's negligence | Federal courts (Consolidated Rail Corp. v. Gottshall, 512 U.S. 532 (1994)) |
| **Bystander recovery** | Plaintiff may recover for emotional distress from witnessing injury to a close family member | California (Thing v. La Chusa), majority of states |

**Thing v. La Chusa, 48 Cal.3d 644 (1989):** The California Supreme Court refined the bystander recovery standard established in Dillon v. Legg, 68 Cal.2d 728 (1968), requiring that the plaintiff: (1) is closely related to the victim, (2) is present at the scene of the injury-producing event and aware that it is causing injury to the victim, and (3) suffers serious emotional distress beyond what would be anticipated in a disinterested witness.

### Damages

Emotional distress damages in the United States can be substantial. In workplace harassment cases under Title VII, compensatory damages (including emotional distress) are capped based on employer size: $50,000 (15-100 employees) to $300,000 (500+ employees) under 42 U.S.C. Section 1981a. State law claims are generally uncapped.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German law takes a restrictive approach to emotional distress compensation, limiting recovery to enumerated categories under BGB Section 253(2) and developing the Schockschaden doctrine for bystander emotional harm.

### BGB Section 253(2) -- Schmerzensgeld

Section 253(2) provides that reasonable compensation in money (eine billige Entschädigung in Geld) may be claimed for non-material damage in cases of injury to body, health, freedom, or sexual self-determination. Notably, this list does not include a general category of "emotional distress" -- recovery is available only when emotional suffering manifests as a recognized injury to health (Gesundheitsverletzung) or falls within one of the other enumerated categories.

The Bundesgerichtshof determines Schmerzensgeld amounts by reference to comparable cases, considering the severity and duration of the injury, the degree of pain and suffering, the impact on quality of life, and the defendant's degree of fault. Published Schmerzensgeld tables (Schmerzensgeldtabellen) provide guidance on typical awards.

### Schockschaden (Shock Damage)

The Schockschaden doctrine permits bystander recovery for emotional harm suffered upon learning of or witnessing the death or serious injury of a close relative. The BGH requires:

**Close Relationship:** The claimant must have a close personal relationship (nahestehende Person) with the primary victim -- typically family members, though the BGH has extended this to life partners.

**Medically Diagnosable Condition:** The emotional reaction must go beyond normal grief and constitute a medically diagnosable health impairment (medizinisch fassbare Gesundheitsbeschädigung). Ordinary grief, sadness, and shock, however severe subjectively, are not compensable under the Schockschaden doctrine.

**BGH Decision of May 27, 2014 (VI ZR 141/13):** The Court confirmed that Schockschaden claims require proof of a health injury exceeding the normal grief reaction. The decision emphasized that the threshold is designed to prevent unlimited expansion of liability while protecting persons who suffer genuine psychiatric injury.

### Personality Right Damages

Violations of the general personality right (allgemeines Persönlichkeitsrecht) may give rise to monetary compensation under a separate doctrinal basis. The BGH has held that serious violations of personality rights -- such as unauthorized publication of intimate photographs or sustained harassment -- may warrant compensation even absent physical health impairment, under the Geldentschädigung für Persönlichkeitsrechtsverletzungen doctrine developed in the "Caroline von Monaco" line of cases.

### Workplace Emotional Distress

The Allgemeines Gleichbehandlungsgesetz (AGG, General Equal Treatment Act), implementing EU anti-discrimination directives, provides compensation for non-material damage resulting from workplace discrimination or harassment (Section 15(2) AGG). The Bundesarbeitsgericht (Federal Labor Court) has established that harassment-related emotional distress constitutes compensable non-material damage under the AGG regardless of physical manifestation.

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR awards non-pecuniary damages under Article 41 for psychological suffering caused by Convention violations.

**Selmouni v. France (Application No. 25803/94, Grand Chamber Judgment of July 28, 1999):** The Court awarded FRF 500,000 in non-pecuniary damages for severe psychological harm resulting from police torture violating Article 3, recognizing that the emotional and psychological impact of torture warrants substantial monetary compensation independent of physical injury.

**Varnava and Others v. Turkey (Application No. 16064/90, Grand Chamber Judgment of September 18, 2009):** The Court awarded non-pecuniary damages to family members of disappeared persons, recognizing the prolonged anguish and uncertainty suffered by relatives as a distinct form of emotional harm.

**Centre for Legal Resources on behalf of Valentin Câmpeanu v. Romania (Application No. 47848/08, Grand Chamber Judgment of July 17, 2014):** The Court awarded non-pecuniary damages for the psychological suffering of a vulnerable individual resulting from institutional neglect, extending emotional distress recognition to particularly vulnerable persons.

### EU Anti-Discrimination Framework

EU anti-discrimination directives require "effective, proportionate, and dissuasive" remedies for discrimination, which courts have interpreted as including compensation for emotional distress:

**Directive 2000/78/EC (Employment Equality Directive):** Article 17 requires member states to provide effective sanctions for discrimination, which the CJEU has interpreted as including non-material damages.

**Directive 2006/54/EC (Recast Gender Equality Directive):** Article 18 requires compensation that is "real and effective" and acts as a "deterrent," which includes emotional distress damages without requiring proof of physical manifestation.

### GDPR Non-Material Damages

GDPR Article 82(1) explicitly covers "non-material damage," which has been interpreted to include emotional distress from privacy violations. The CJEU in Österreichische Post (C-300/21, 2023) confirmed that there is no minimum threshold of seriousness for non-material GDPR damages, opening the door to claims for anxiety, distress, and worry resulting from data breaches. German courts have been cautious, typically awarding between EUR 100 and EUR 5,000 for GDPR-related emotional distress.

## Comparative Analysis

### Threshold Requirements

The four jurisdictions impose markedly different thresholds for emotional distress recovery:

**Lowest Threshold (South Korea):** Korean law imposes no specific severity threshold for consolation money. Any tortious act causing identifiable emotional suffering is potentially compensable, though awards for minor distress are modest.

**Moderate Threshold (ECHR/EU):** The ECHR requires that the violation be sufficiently serious to warrant non-pecuniary damages, but does not require physical manifestation. GDPR claims require no minimum severity threshold.

**High Threshold (United States -- IIED):** The "extreme and outrageous" conduct requirement and "severe emotional distress" standard create the highest threshold for intentional claims. NIED claims vary but generally require some form of physical manifestation, impact, or proximity.

**Physical Manifestation Required (Germany):** The Schockschaden doctrine's requirement of a "medically diagnosable health impairment" beyond normal grief creates the most restrictive standard for bystander emotional distress claims.

### Bystander Recovery

All four jurisdictions permit some form of bystander emotional distress recovery but impose different limitations. The American Thing v. La Chusa test requires contemporaneous sensory perception. The German Schockschaden doctrine requires medical diagnosis beyond normal grief. Korean law applies the general tort framework without specific bystander limitations. The ECHR recognizes family member suffering as compensable non-pecuniary damage.

### Workplace Context

All four jurisdictions have expanded emotional distress protections in the workplace context, reflecting growing recognition that workplace harassment and discrimination cause serious psychological harm. South Korea's 2019 workplace harassment legislation, American Title VII, the German AGG, and EU anti-discrimination directives converge on requiring effective remedies for workplace-caused emotional distress.
