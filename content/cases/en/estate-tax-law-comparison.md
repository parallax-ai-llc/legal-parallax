## Summary

Estate and gift taxes -- levied on the transfer of wealth upon death or during a person's lifetime -- serve dual purposes of revenue generation and redistribution of concentrated wealth, though their scope and rates vary dramatically across jurisdictions. South Korea imposes one of the world's highest inheritance and gift tax rates under the Inheritance Tax and Gift Tax Act (상속세 및 증여세법), with a top marginal rate of 50 percent on transfers exceeding 3 billion KRW, plus an additional 20 percent surcharge when the largest shareholder of a corporation transfers shares to family members, creating an effective maximum rate of 60 percent -- a rate that drew global attention in the Samsung Group succession following Chairman Lee Kun-hee's death in October 2020, with the Lee family paying approximately 12 trillion KRW (roughly $10 billion) in inheritance tax, the largest such payment in history. The United States imposes a unified estate and gift tax under the Internal Revenue Code (26 U.S.C. Sections 2001-2704) at a flat rate of 40 percent on transfers exceeding a historically high exemption amount of $13.61 million per individual (2024), doubled for married couples through portability, though this exemption is scheduled to revert to approximately $7 million (adjusted for inflation) on January 1, 2026, upon expiration of the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act provisions. Germany levies inheritance and gift tax (Erbschaftsteuer und Schenkungsteuer) under the Erbschaftsteuer- und Schenkungsteuergesetz (ErbStG), with rates ranging from 7 percent to 50 percent depending on the value transferred and the relationship between the transferor and transferee, divided into three tax classes (Steuerklassen). The European Court of Human Rights has generally upheld inheritance taxation as within the state's margin of appreciation under Protocol 1, Article 1, while the CJEU has intervened where inheritance tax provisions discriminate against cross-border transfers in violation of the free movement of capital guaranteed by Article 63 TFEU.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's inheritance and gift tax system is governed by the Inheritance Tax and Gift Tax Act (상속세 및 증여세법, Act No. 19931), administered by the National Tax Service (국세청). The system is estate-based, meaning the total value of the decedent's estate is taxed before distribution to heirs, unlike the inheritance-based systems used in Germany and many other civil law jurisdictions.

### Rate Structure

| Taxable Base (KRW) | Marginal Rate |
|-------------------|---------------|
| Up to 100 million | 10% |
| 100 million - 500 million | 20% |
| 500 million - 1 billion | 30% |
| 1 billion - 3 billion | 40% |
| Over 3 billion | 50% |

An additional surcharge of 20 percent of the standard tax applies when the largest shareholder (최대주주) of a corporation or a related party inherits or receives shares by gift, creating an effective top rate of 60 percent. The government proposed reducing this surcharge in 2024, but the proposal faced significant legislative opposition.

### Deductions and Exemptions

| Deduction Category | Amount |
|-------------------|--------|
| Basic deduction (기본공제) | 200 million KRW per heir |
| Spousal deduction (배우자 공제) | Up to 3 billion KRW (minimum 500 million) |
| Lump-sum deduction (일괄공제) | 500 million KRW |
| Financial assets deduction | 20% of financial assets (max 200 million KRW) |
| Family business deduction (가업상속공제) | Up to 50 billion KRW for qualifying businesses operated 10+ years |

### Samsung Succession Case

The death of Samsung Group Chairman Lee Kun-hee on October 25, 2020, with an estimated estate valued at over 26 trillion KRW, resulted in the largest inheritance tax assessment in history. The Lee family -- including heir apparent Lee Jae-yong -- was assessed approximately 12 trillion KRW (roughly $10 billion) in inheritance tax, payable in installments over five years. To fund the payment, the family donated significant art collections to national museums and pledged Samsung shares as collateral. The case reignited national debate about whether South Korea's inheritance tax rates are excessively high and discourage business succession planning.

### 2024 Reform Proposals

The government proposed significant reforms in 2024, including reducing the top rate from 50 percent to 40 percent and eliminating the 20 percent major shareholder surcharge. These proposals were partially adopted, with the surcharge reduced from 20 percent to 10 percent effective 2025, but the rate reduction was blocked in the National Assembly.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States imposes a unified transfer tax system encompassing the estate tax (26 U.S.C. Sections 2001-2210), gift tax (26 U.S.C. Sections 2501-2524), and generation-skipping transfer tax (26 U.S.C. Sections 2601-2664). The three taxes share a single unified credit, currently equivalent to an exemption of $13.61 million per individual (2024, indexed for inflation).

### Estate Tax

The estate tax applies to the worldwide assets of U.S. citizens and residents and to U.S.-situs assets of nonresidents. The taxable estate comprises the gross estate (all property in which the decedent had an interest at death, including life insurance, retirement accounts, and jointly held property) minus deductions for debts, expenses, charitable bequests (unlimited), and marital transfers (unlimited marital deduction).

### Rate and Exemption

| Year | Exemption Amount | Top Rate |
|------|-----------------|----------|
| 2017 (pre-TCJA) | $5.49 million | 40% |
| 2018 (TCJA) | $11.18 million | 40% |
| 2024 | $13.61 million | 40% |
| 2026 (post-TCJA sunset) | ~$7 million (projected) | 40% |

The TCJA doubled the exemption amount from approximately $5.6 million to $11.2 million, indexed for inflation. This provision expires on December 31, 2025, creating significant estate planning urgency for high-net-worth individuals.

### Portability

Since the American Taxpayer Relief Act of 2012, the deceased spousal unused exclusion amount (DSUE) may be transferred to the surviving spouse ("portability"), effectively allowing married couples to shelter up to $27.22 million (2024) from estate and gift tax without traditional bypass trust planning.

### Generation-Skipping Transfer Tax

The GST tax imposes a flat 40 percent tax on transfers that skip a generation (e.g., grandparent to grandchild), designed to prevent families from avoiding one layer of transfer tax. The GST exemption mirrors the estate tax exemption at $13.61 million (2024).

### Landmark Cases

**United States v. Windsor, 570 U.S. 744 (2013):** While primarily a constitutional equal protection case, Windsor arose from a $363,053 estate tax refund claim. The Court's decision striking down Section 3 of the Defense of Marriage Act ensured that same-sex married couples could claim the unlimited marital deduction.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's inheritance and gift tax is governed by the Erbschaftsteuer- und Schenkungsteuergesetz (ErbStG). Unlike the U.S. estate tax, Germany uses an inheritance-based system where each individual recipient is taxed separately based on the value received and their relationship to the transferor.

### Tax Classes and Rates

**Tax Class I** (spouse, children, grandchildren, parents on inheritance):

| Taxable Amount (EUR) | Rate |
|---------------------|------|
| Up to 75,000 | 7% |
| 75,001 - 300,000 | 11% |
| 300,001 - 600,000 | 15% |
| 600,001 - 6,000,000 | 19% |
| 6,000,001 - 13,000,000 | 23% |
| 13,000,001 - 26,000,000 | 27% |
| Over 26,000,000 | 30% |

**Tax Class II** (siblings, nieces/nephews, in-laws, divorced spouse): Rates range from 15% to 43%.

**Tax Class III** (all others, including unrelated persons): Rates range from 30% to 50%.

### Personal Exemptions (Freibeträge)

| Relationship | Exemption (EUR) |
|-------------|-----------------|
| Spouse/registered life partner | 500,000 |
| Children | 400,000 |
| Grandchildren | 200,000 |
| Parents (inheritance only) | 100,000 |
| Siblings, nieces, nephews | 20,000 |
| All others | 20,000 |

Exemptions renew every 10 years for gift tax purposes, enabling significant tax-free wealth transfers through lifetime giving strategies.

### Business Property Relief (Sections 13a-13c ErbStG)

Germany provides generous relief for business property transfers:

**Standard Relief (Regelverschonung):** 85 percent exemption for qualifying business assets, subject to a five-year holding period and maintenance of aggregate payroll at 400 percent of the average over the preceding five years.

**Option Relief (Optionsverschonung):** 100 percent exemption, subject to a seven-year holding period and maintenance of aggregate payroll at 700 percent of the preceding average.

The BVerfG (Federal Constitutional Court) ruled in its landmark decision of December 17, 2014 (1 BvL 21/12) that the business property exemption in its then-existing form was unconstitutional because it was overly broad, leading to the 2016 reform that introduced the current system with payroll and holding period requirements.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Free Movement Constraints

While the EU has not harmonized inheritance or gift taxation, the CJEU has developed significant case law constraining member state inheritance tax provisions that impede the free movement of capital (Article 63 TFEU):

**Mattner (C-510/08, 2010):** Germany's provision granting a EUR 400,000 exemption to residents but only EUR 2,000 to non-residents for gift tax purposes was held to violate free movement of capital.

**Welte (C-181/12, 2013):** The Court held that Germany's lower inheritance tax exemption for non-EU residents (compared to residents) violated free movement of capital, extending the Mattner principle to third-country nationals.

**Commission v. Germany (C-211/13, 2014):** The CJEU ruled that Germany's inheritance tax provisions, which applied reduced exemptions to non-residents, constituted an unjustified restriction on the free movement of capital.

In response, Germany amended the ErbStG to allow non-residents to opt for the higher resident exemptions, subject to declaring their worldwide inheritance for German tax purposes.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

The European Court of Human Rights has generally afforded states a wide margin of appreciation in inheritance taxation under Protocol 1, Article 1:

**Burden v. United Kingdom (Application No. 13378/05, Grand Chamber, 2008):** Two elderly sisters challenged the UK's refusal to apply the spousal inheritance tax exemption to sibling cohabitants. The Grand Chamber held by 15 votes to 2 that the difference in treatment between spouses and siblings was justified, as marriage constitutes a fundamentally different legal relationship.

**Jahn and Others v. Germany (Application No. 46720/99, Grand Chamber, 2005):** The Court held that expropriation of inherited land in post-reunification Germany did not violate Protocol 1, Article 1, given the exceptional historical circumstances.

### EU Succession Regulation

Regulation (EU) No 650/2012 on succession matters harmonizes private international law rules for cross-border successions within the EU, establishing that the law of the deceased's habitual residence governs the succession (with the option to choose the law of nationality). While the Regulation does not address tax matters directly, it significantly impacts estate planning and the determination of which jurisdiction's inheritance tax applies.

## Comparative Analysis

### Estate-Based vs. Inheritance-Based Systems

South Korea and the United States tax the total estate before distribution, while Germany taxes each individual inheritor separately. The inheritance-based approach typically results in lower effective tax rates when the estate is divided among multiple heirs, as each heir benefits from separate exemptions and lower marginal rate brackets.

### Rate Levels and Revenue

South Korea's effective top rate of 60 percent (including the major shareholder surcharge) is among the highest in the world. The United States' 40 percent rate is substantial but mitigated by the historically high exemption. Germany's rates range widely depending on the transferee's relationship to the deceased. Despite high rates, inheritance taxes generally generate modest revenue: approximately 1.5 percent of total tax revenue in South Korea, 0.5 percent in the United States, and 1.1 percent in Germany.

### Business Succession

All three jurisdictions provide relief for business property transfers but through different mechanisms. Germany's 85-100 percent exemption with employment maintenance requirements is the most generous. South Korea's family business deduction of up to 50 billion KRW is substantial but limited. The United States provides various planning vehicles (GRATs, family limited partnerships, qualified small business deductions) but no single comprehensive business exemption comparable to Germany's.

### Wealth Inequality Implications

The effectiveness of estate taxes in reducing wealth concentration is debated. The extremely high U.S. exemption means that fewer than 0.1 percent of estates owe any federal estate tax. South Korea's lower exemptions mean a larger share of estates are subject to tax, but the high rates are criticized for forcing asset liquidation and discouraging domestic wealth retention. Germany's inheritance-based system with generous business exemptions is criticized for enabling dynastic business empires to pass between generations with minimal taxation.
