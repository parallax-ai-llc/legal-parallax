## Summary

Self-defense -- the right to use force to protect oneself or others from unlawful aggression -- is recognized in virtually every legal system, yet its scope, limitations, and consequences vary dramatically across jurisdictions. South Korea's Criminal Act (Article 21) establishes a narrow right of self-defense (jeongdangbangwi) that courts have interpreted restrictively, with only a handful of successful murder-level self-defense claims in decades of jurisprudence. The United States presents a fragmented landscape: the common-law duty to retreat has been supplanted in 38 states by "Stand Your Ground" statutes, while the Castle Doctrine provides broad protections for defensive force within the home. Germany's Strafgesetzbuch (Section 32) grants one of the most robust self-defense rights in Western legal systems, permitting the use of deadly force to protect any legally recognized interest -- including property -- without any duty to retreat, though socio-ethical limitations (sozialethische Einschraenkungen) temper this breadth in practice. The European Court of Human Rights approaches self-defense through the lens of Article 2 (right to life), requiring that any lethal force be "absolutely necessary" -- a standard stricter than mere proportionality -- and evaluating not only the conduct of individuals but the operational planning and control of state authorities.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Self-defense in South Korea is governed by Article 21 of the Criminal Act (hyeongbeop), located in Part I (General Provisions). The provision establishes three distinct rules:

**Article 21(1) -- Self-Defense (jeongdangbangwi):** An act which is performed in order to prevent impending and unjust infringement of one's own or another person's legal interest shall not be punishable if there are reasonable grounds for that act.

**Article 21(2) -- Excessive Self-Defense (gwajang bangwi):** When a preventive act has exceeded normal limits, the punishment may be mitigated or remitted according to the extenuating circumstances.

**Article 21(3) -- Night and Emergency Circumstances:** An act performed through fear, surprise, excitement, or confusion in the night or under other extraordinary circumstances shall not be punishable.

| Element | Requirement |
|---------|-------------|
| Attack nature | Must be "impending and unjust" (geupbakhan bujeonghawi) |
| Protected interests | One's own or another's legal interests |
| Defensive act | Must have "reasonable grounds" (sangdanghan iyu) |
| Proportionality | Act must not exceed "normal limits" |
| Timing | Defense must be contemporaneous with the attack |

### Narrow Judicial Interpretation

Korean courts have historically interpreted self-defense extremely narrowly. Over the past 25-plus years, only one reported case involving a murder charge resulted in a successful self-defense claim. Courts impose a de facto duty to retreat, frequently holding that defendants should have attempted to flee or seek help before resorting to force.

**Key factors courts examine:**
- Whether the defendant attempted to avoid the confrontation before using force
- Whether lesser means of defense were available
- The relative severity of the attack versus the defensive response
- Whether the defensive act was contemporaneous with the threat (not preemptive or retaliatory)

### Penalties When Self-Defense Fails

If the court rejects a self-defense claim, the defendant faces full punishment for the underlying offense. For homicide, this means:

| Offense | Provision | Penalty |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Murder | Art. 250(1) | Death, life imprisonment, or 5+ years |
| Murder of lineal ascendant | Art. 250(2) | Death, life imprisonment, or 7+ years |
| Manslaughter (excessive defense recognized) | Art. 21(2) + underlying offense | Mitigated or remitted |
| Injury resulting in death | Art. 259 | 3 years to life imprisonment |

### Landmark Cases

**Supreme Court Decision 84Do1611 (September 25, 1984) -- The Chestnut Tree Case:** A property owner beat and injured a woman who attempted to steal chestnuts from his tree. The Supreme Court held that the owner's actions did not constitute self-defense because the response lacked "reasonable grounds" -- the force used was grossly disproportionate to the minor property threat.

**Supreme Court Decision 92Do2540 (1992) -- The Kim Bo-eun Case:** A 21-year-old woman who had been habitually raped by her stepfather for over 12 years had her boyfriend stab and kill the stepfather while he slept. The court rejected the self-defense claim, ruling that while the infringement was ongoing and unjust, the method (stabbing a sleeping person to death) lacked reasonable grounds and that less extreme alternatives existed. Both defendants were convicted of murder. This case provoked widespread public debate about the narrowness of Korean self-defense law, particularly for victims of sustained domestic abuse.

**Supreme Court Decision 96Do241 (April 9, 1996) -- Pursuit of Fleeing Burglar:** When a homeowner chased and assaulted a burglar who had already broken a window and was fleeing, the court held this was not self-defense. Once the attacker was retreating, the "impending" requirement was no longer satisfied, and pursuing the burglar transformed the homeowner into the aggressor.

**Supreme Court Decision 99Do4341 (July 4, 2000) -- Resisting Unlawful Arrest:** The court recognized that using force to resist a clearly unjust arrest by a police officer could constitute self-defense, as an unlawful arrest is itself an "unjust infringement" of personal liberty. However, force used against a lawful arrest receives no such protection.

**Police Shooting Case:** A police officer shot a knife-wielding suspect in the chest, killing him instantly. Self-defense was rejected because alternative means existed -- the officer could have used a gas gun, aimed for the legs, or employed other non-lethal methods. This case illustrates the extreme proportionality standard Korean courts demand.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Self-defense in the United States operates across a complex, multi-layered system of federal, state, and local laws. The foundational common-law principles have evolved into two major doctrinal frameworks: the traditional duty-to-retreat approach and the modern Stand Your Ground expansion.

**Model Penal Code Section 3.04 -- Use of Force in Self-Protection:** The use of force upon or toward another person is justifiable when the actor believes that such force is immediately necessary for the purpose of protecting himself against the use of unlawful force by such other person on the present occasion.

**Deadly Force (MPC Section 3.04(2)(b)):** Deadly force is not justifiable unless the actor believes that such force is necessary to protect himself against death, serious bodily harm, kidnapping, or sexual intercourse compelled by force or threat. The MPC also imposes a duty to retreat: deadly force is not justifiable if the actor knows he can avoid the necessity of using such force with complete safety by retreating. An exception exists for the home -- no retreat is required from one's dwelling.

**MPC Section 3.09(2) -- Reckless/Negligent Belief:** If the defendant was reckless or negligent in forming the belief that force was necessary, the defense is unavailable for offenses requiring only recklessness or negligence as the culpable mental state.

### Stand Your Ground vs. Duty to Retreat

| Approach | States | Key Principle |
|----------|--------|---------------|
| Stand Your Ground (by statute) | 38 states, including FL, TX, GA, OH, IN, AL, AZ, KY, MO, SC, TN | No duty to retreat in any place where one is lawfully present |
| Duty to retreat | 12 states, including CT, DE, HI, ME, MD, MA, MN, NE, NJ, NY, RI, WI | Must retreat if safely possible before using deadly force (except in the home) |
| Castle Doctrine (universal) | All 50 states (varying strength) | No duty to retreat within one's home |

**Florida Statute 776.013.3 (2005):** Florida was the first state to enact a modern Stand Your Ground law, eliminating the duty to retreat and establishing a presumption that a person who unlawfully and forcefully enters a dwelling is doing so with intent to commit a violent act. This legislation became the model for similar statutes nationwide.

### Castle Doctrine

The Castle Doctrine, rooted in the English common-law maxim "a man's home is his castle" (formalized by Sir Edward Coke in 1628), permits the use of deadly force against an intruder in the home without any duty to retreat. Key features across states include:

- **Presumption of threat:** Many states presume that an intruder in the home poses a deadly threat
- **No duty to retreat:** Universal within the home; some states extend to vehicles and workplaces
- **Scope of "dwelling":** Varies -- some states include attached porches, garages, and yards

### Penalties and Outcomes

| Outcome | Effect |
|---------|--------|
| Successful self-defense | Complete acquittal; no criminal liability |
| Imperfect self-defense | Murder reduced to voluntary manslaughter (recognized in ~20 states) |
| Self-defense rejected | Full punishment for underlying offense (murder: 15 years to life/death) |
| Stand Your Ground immunity | Pre-trial immunity hearing; charges dismissed if successful |
| Civil immunity | Many states bar civil suits when criminal self-defense is established |

### Landmark Cases

**Beard v. United States, 158 U.S. 550 (1895):** The Supreme Court overturned a manslaughter conviction where Mr. Babe Beard had shot Will Jones, who was approaching him menacingly with a concealed knife following prior threats. The Court held that there was no requirement for Beard to retreat from his own property and that the jury had improperly failed to consider self-defense. This decision established the foundational principle that a person lawfully present in a place has no obligation to retreat before using defensive force.

**Brown v. United States, 256 U.S. 335 (1921):** Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes wrote that "detached reflection cannot be demanded in the presence of an uplifted knife," establishing that self-defense must be evaluated in the context of the urgent circumstances the defendant faced, not through calm after-the-fact analysis.

**People v. Goetz, 68 N.Y.2d 96 (1986):** Bernhard Goetz shot four young men on a New York City subway, claiming self-defense. The New York Court of Appeals held that self-defense requires an objectively reasonable belief in the necessity of force, rejecting a purely subjective standard. This case shaped the modern "reasonable person" test applied in most jurisdictions.

**State of Florida v. George Zimmerman (2013):** The shooting death of 17-year-old Trayvon Martin by neighborhood watch volunteer George Zimmerman in 2012 brought Stand Your Ground laws to national prominence. Zimmerman was acquitted of second-degree murder and manslaughter after invoking Florida's self-defense provisions. The case sparked nationwide debate over racial disparities in the application of Stand Your Ground laws and led to legislative review in multiple states.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany possesses one of the broadest self-defense rights (Notwehr) among Western legal systems, codified in the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB):

**Section 32 -- Self-Defense (Notwehr):**
(1) A person who commits an act that is required by self-defense does not act unlawfully.
(2) Self-defense means any defensive action that is necessary to avert a present unlawful attack on oneself or another person.

**Section 33 -- Excessive Self-Defense (Ueberschreitung der Notwehr):**
A person who exceeds the limits of self-defense out of confusion, fear, or fright (Verwirrung, Furcht oder Schrecken) shall not be punished.

| Element | Requirement |
|---------|-------------|
| Attack | Present (gegenwaertig), unlawful (rechtswidrig), on any legal interest |
| Defense | Necessary (erforderlich) -- the mildest effective means |
| No proportionality | No formal requirement that defense be proportionate to attack |
| No duty to retreat | Defender need not flee; "right need not yield to wrong" (das Recht braucht dem Unrecht nicht zu weichen) |

### Breadth of the German Right

The German Notwehr right is notably expansive in several respects:

**Protection of all legal interests:** Unlike most jurisdictions that limit deadly force to threats against life or serious bodily harm, German law permits defensive force to protect any legally recognized interest -- including property. A homeowner may, in principle, use lethal force against a burglar if no lesser means would suffice, even when the threat is only to property.

**No formal proportionality:** German law does not require formal proportionality between the attack and the defense. The defender is entitled to use the mildest effective means (mildestes wirksames Mittel), but if that mildest effective means happens to be lethal force, it is in principle justified.

**No duty to retreat:** The fundamental principle "das Recht braucht dem Unrecht nicht zu weichen" (right need not yield to wrong) means the defender has no obligation to flee, even when retreat would be safely possible. This principle reflects the dual justification of Notwehr: individual protection (Individualschutz) and affirmation of the legal order (Rechtsbewaeehrung).

### Socio-Ethical Limitations (Sozialethische Einschraenkungen)

German courts have developed judicially created limitations that restrict the otherwise broad Notwehr right in specific situations:

**Grossly disproportionate defense (krasses Missverhältnis):** When the defensive force would result in harm grossly disproportionate to the threatened interest, self-defense may be limited. Example: shooting a child who is stealing apples would not be justified despite being technically "necessary."

**Attacks by non-culpable persons:** The right of self-defense is restricted when the attacker is a child, mentally ill, intoxicated to the point of incapacity, or otherwise blameless. In these cases, the defender must attempt to evade, then warn, and only use force as a last resort.

**Attacks within close relationships:** When the attack arises within a close personal relationship (e.g., between spouses), the defender must exercise greater restraint and exhaust alternatives before resorting to force.

**Provoked attacks (Notwehrprovokation):** If the defender deliberately provoked the attack, the right of self-defense is significantly curtailed -- in cases of intentional provocation (Absichtsprovokation), the defender may lose the right to Notwehr entirely.

### Section 33: The Fear/Confusion Exemption

Section 33 provides a unique exculpation for excessive self-defense: if the defender exceeded the necessary limits of force out of confusion, fear, or fright (asthenic affects), the defender is not punished. This provision recognizes that individuals under attack cannot always calibrate their response with precision. Importantly, Section 33 does not justify the act (it remains unlawful) but rather excuses the actor from punishment -- a distinction with significant implications for civil liability and third-party intervention.

### Penalties When Self-Defense Fails

| Outcome | Legal Effect |
|---------|-------------|
| Notwehr recognized (Section 32) | Act is lawful; no criminal liability, no civil liability |
| Excessive Notwehr excused (Section 33) | Act is unlawful but unpunished; civil liability may remain |
| Notwehr rejected | Full punishment for underlying offense |
| Murder (Mord, Section 211) | Life imprisonment (lebenslange Freiheitsstrafe) |
| Manslaughter (Totschlag, Section 212) | 5-15 years imprisonment |
| Bodily harm resulting in death (Section 227) | Not less than 3 years imprisonment |

### Notable Jurisprudence

German courts have addressed Notwehr in numerous significant decisions by the Federal Court of Justice (Bundesgerichtshof, BGH):

**BGH, NJW 1975, 62 -- The Fence Post Case:** The BGH held that a farmer who struck an aggressive trespasser with a fence post acted within Notwehr, as the post was the mildest effective means available at the time, despite the serious injuries inflicted.

**BGH, NStZ 2003, 599 -- Spousal Abuse and Notwehr:** A woman who killed her chronically abusive husband was denied a full Notwehr defense because the attack was not "present" at the time of the killing (the husband was asleep). However, the court considered a Section 33 excuse and mitigating circumstances. This parallels limitations seen in the Korean Kim Bo-eun case and U.S. battered spouse cases.

**BGH, NJW 1989, 3027 -- Burglar Shot Dead:** The court upheld a self-defense claim where a homeowner shot and killed a burglar, applying the principle that even property interests can be defended with deadly force when no lesser means would be effective. The decision drew criticism and renewed debate about whether deadly force should be available to protect mere property.

## ECHR & EU

### Legal Framework

The European Convention on Human Rights addresses self-defense primarily through Article 2 (Right to Life), which establishes the strictest standard among the compared jurisdictions:

**Article 2(1):** Everyone's right to life shall be protected by law. No one shall be deprived of his life intentionally save in the execution of a sentence of a court following his conviction of a crime for which this penalty is provided by law.

**Article 2(2):** Deprivation of life shall not be regarded as inflicted in contravention of this Article when it results from the use of force which is no more than absolutely necessary:
- (a) in defence of any person from unlawful violence;
- (b) in order to effect a lawful arrest or to prevent the escape of a person lawfully detained;
- (c) in action lawfully taken for the purpose of quelling a riot or insurrection.

| Element | ECHR Standard |
|---------|---------------|
| Test | "Absolutely necessary" (stricter than "necessary" or "proportionate") |
| Scope | Primarily applies to state agents; influences interpretation of domestic self-defense law |
| Review | Ex post facto review by the Court considering all circumstances |
| Operational duty | State must plan and control operations to minimize lethal force |
| Investigation | Mandatory effective investigation of all deaths involving state force |

### The "Absolutely Necessary" Standard

The ECHR standard is notably more demanding than domestic self-defense standards in most member states. In McCann v. United Kingdom, the Court established that "absolutely necessary" indicates a stricter and more compelling test of necessity than the "necessary in a democratic society" standard applied under Articles 8-11 of the Convention. The force used must be strictly proportionate to the achievement of the permitted aims.

### Three Pillars of Article 2 State Obligations

1. **Negative obligation:** States must refrain from unlawful deprivation of life.
2. **Procedural obligation:** States must conduct effective, independent investigations into deaths caused by state agents or occurring in state custody.
3. **Positive obligation:** States must take appropriate steps to safeguard the lives of those within their jurisdiction, including through adequate legal frameworks for self-defense.

### Landmark Cases

**McCann and Others v. United Kingdom (1995), App. No. 18984/91 -- The Gibraltar SAS Shooting:** Three IRA members (Daniel McCann, Sean Savage, and Mairead Farrell) suspected of planning a car bombing in Gibraltar were shot dead by SAS soldiers. The Grand Chamber found a violation of Article 2 by a vote of 10-9 -- not because the individual soldiers acted unreasonably (they honestly believed the suspects were about to detonate a bomb), but because the authorities had failed at the operational level: they did not prevent the suspects from entering Gibraltar, and they failed to consider that intelligence about a remote detonation device might be wrong. This landmark ruling established that Article 2 requires evaluation of the entire operation, not merely the final act of lethal force.

**Nachova and Others v. Bulgaria (2005), App. Nos. 43577/98 and 43579/98 -- Shooting of Unarmed Fugitives:** Military police shot and killed two unarmed Romani men who had escaped from a military detention facility. The Grand Chamber held that the use of lethal force was not "absolutely necessary" because the fugitives posed no threat to life and were not suspected of violent offenses. The Court established that the legitimate aim of effecting a lawful arrest can only justify putting human life at risk in circumstances of absolute necessity, and that in principle no such necessity exists where the person to be arrested poses no threat to life or limb.

**Armani Da Silva v. United Kingdom (2016), App. No. 5878/08 -- The Jean Charles de Menezes Case:** Following the 2005 London bombings, police shot and killed Jean Charles de Menezes, a Brazilian electrician mistakenly identified as a suicide bomber. The Grand Chamber held (by 13 votes to 4) that the United Kingdom's legal framework -- which assessed self-defense based on an honest belief in the necessity of force -- was not incompatible with Article 2. However, the Court reiterated that Article 2 compliance requires evaluation of both individual actions and the systemic planning of security operations.

**Giuliani and Gaggio v. Italy (2011), App. No. 23458/02 -- The Genoa G8 Summit:** During the 2001 G8 summit protests in Genoa, a police officer shot and killed demonstrator Carlo Giuliani. The Grand Chamber found no violation of Article 2, accepting that the officer had acted in self-defense when surrounded by an aggressive crowd and about to be struck by a fire extinguisher. The Court held that the force used was not disproportionate given the genuine threat to life the officer faced.

### EU Charter of Fundamental Rights

Article 2 of the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights mirrors ECHR Article 2: "Everyone has the right to life. No one shall be condemned to the death penalty, or executed." While the Charter applies to EU institutions and member states when implementing EU law, it is interpreted consistently with ECHR case law pursuant to Article 52(3) of the Charter.

### Impact on Domestic Self-Defense Laws

The ECHR framework indirectly shapes domestic self-defense law in all 46 member states of the Council of Europe:

- Domestic self-defense laws that permit lethal force based on unreasonable beliefs may be incompatible with Article 2 (see academic criticism of English law following the Criminal Justice and Immigration Act 2008, Section 76)
- States must ensure their legal frameworks for self-defense minimize the risk of arbitrary deprivation of life
- Law enforcement training, rules of engagement, and operational planning must satisfy Article 2 requirements

## Comparative Analysis

### Philosophical Foundations

The four jurisdictions reflect fundamentally different philosophical approaches to self-defense:

**South Korea -- Communal Harmony Model:** Korean self-defense law reflects Confucian values emphasizing social harmony and conflict avoidance. The strong de facto duty to retreat and the requirement of "reasonable grounds" reflect a societal preference for non-violent resolution. The extraordinarily low recognition rate suggests courts view self-defense as a truly last resort.

**United States -- Individual Rights Model:** The American approach, particularly in Stand Your Ground states, emphasizes individual autonomy and the right to hold one's ground. The absence of a duty to retreat reflects the philosophical position that a law-abiding person should not be forced to flee by a wrongdoer. The Castle Doctrine embodies the sanctity of the home as a zone of absolute protection.

**Germany -- Legal Order Model:** The German Notwehr right serves a dual purpose: protecting the individual (Individualschutz) and affirming the legal order (Rechtsbewaehrung). The principle that "right need not yield to wrong" makes Notwehr broader in scope than many comparable systems, though socio-ethical limitations introduce necessary flexibility.

**ECHR -- Human Rights Model:** The ECHR approach subordinates individual self-defense to the overarching protection of the right to life. The "absolutely necessary" standard reflects the position that human life is the supreme value, and any taking of life -- even in defense -- must be subjected to the most rigorous scrutiny.

### Key Divergences

| Issue | Most Permissive | Most Restrictive |
|-------|----------------|------------------|
| Deadly force for property | Germany | ECHR / South Korea |
| Duty to retreat | Germany / US (SYG states) | South Korea |
| Subjective vs. objective standard | US (some states allow subjective belief) | ECHR ("absolutely necessary" objective test) |
| Excessive defense treatment | Germany (Section 33: unpunished) | South Korea (merely mitigated) |
| Pre-emptive defense | None permit | All require present/imminent threat |
| Battered spouse defense | US (some states recognize) | South Korea / Germany (rejected when attacker asleep) |

### Convergences

Despite their differences, all four jurisdictions share certain common principles:
- Self-defense requires an unlawful attack or threat
- The threat must be present or imminent (no preemptive strikes)
- Some form of necessity is required (force must be needed)
- Self-defense is a complete defense when fully established
- Excessive self-defense receives more lenient treatment than unprovoked violence
- Defense of third parties is generally recognized alongside self-defense

## Career Timeline

- **1628:** Sir Edward Coke articulates the Castle Doctrine in English common law: "For a man's house is his castle, et domus sua cuique est tutissimum refugium."
- **1871:** Germany's Reichsstrafgesetzbuch codifies Notwehr, establishing the framework that continues in modern Section 32 StGB.
- **1895:** The U.S. Supreme Court decides Beard v. United States, affirming that there is no duty to retreat on one's own property.
- **1921:** Justice Holmes writes in Brown v. United States that "detached reflection cannot be demanded in the presence of an uplifted knife."
- **1950:** The European Convention on Human Rights is signed, with Article 2(2) establishing the "absolutely necessary" standard for lawful use of lethal force.
- **1953:** South Korea enacts its Criminal Act (Act No. 293), including Article 21 on self-defense, influenced by Japanese and German legal traditions.
- **1962:** The American Law Institute publishes the Model Penal Code, with Section 3.04 establishing a framework for self-defense including a duty to retreat for deadly force.
- **1975:** The German BGH decides the Fence Post Case (NJW 1975, 62), affirming broad Notwehr rights in defense of property.
- **1984:** The Korean Supreme Court rejects self-defense in the Chestnut Tree Case (84Do1611), reinforcing strict proportionality requirements.
- **1986:** People v. Goetz establishes the objective reasonableness standard for self-defense in New York.
- **1992:** The Kim Bo-eun case in South Korea sparks national debate on the narrowness of self-defense law for abuse victims.
- **1995:** McCann and Others v. United Kingdom becomes the first ECHR case to find a violation of Article 2 in a self-defense/use-of-force context.
- **2000:** Korean Supreme Court (99Do4341) recognizes self-defense against unlawful police arrest.
- **2005:** Florida enacts the first modern Stand Your Ground law (Statute 776.013.3), eliminating the duty to retreat in public spaces. Nachova v. Bulgaria establishes that lethal force to arrest non-violent fugitives violates Article 2.
- **2012:** The shooting of Trayvon Martin by George Zimmerman in Florida ignites national debate over Stand Your Ground laws and racial disparities in self-defense claims.
- **2013:** Zimmerman is acquitted of murder charges in Florida, prompting legislative review of Stand Your Ground laws in multiple states.
- **2016:** Armani Da Silva v. United Kingdom (Grand Chamber) addresses the de Menezes killing, holding that honest-belief self-defense standards are not per se incompatible with Article 2 ECHR.
- **2020-2025:** Multiple U.S. states expand Stand Your Ground protections; South Korea sees continued academic and public pressure to broaden Article 21 interpretation; ECHR continues to refine the operational duties of states under Article 2.
