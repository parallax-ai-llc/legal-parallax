## Summary

Corporate governance across the Global South exhibits remarkable diversity, ranging from China's unique integration of Communist Party committee oversight into state-owned enterprise (SOE) governance, to India's prescriptive statutory framework under the Companies Act, 2013, to South Africa's pioneering stakeholder-oriented King Code model, to Brazil's market-driven Novo Mercado premium listing standards, to Indonesia's dual-board system influenced by Dutch colonial law. Each jurisdiction navigates the fundamental tension between shareholder primacy and stakeholder capitalism through distinct legal and institutional mechanisms shaped by ownership structures, political economies, and colonial legacies. China's mixed ownership reform (混合所有制改革) seeks to introduce market discipline into SOEs while maintaining Party control through the "Three Important and One Large" decision-making framework (三重一大). India's Companies Act, 2013, one of the most prescriptive corporate statutes globally, mandates Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) spending (Section 135), woman directors (Section 149), independent director requirements, and a revolutionary class action mechanism (Section 245). Nigeria's Companies and Allied Matters Act (CAMA) 2020 modernized a colonial-era framework, while South Africa's King IV Report on Corporate Governance (2016) introduced the "apply and explain" principle that has influenced governance codes across Africa. Brazil's B3 stock exchange created the Novo Mercado, Nível 2, and Nível 1 listing segments requiring governance standards beyond statutory minima, and Indonesia's dual-board structure (Board of Commissioners and Board of Directors) under the Company Law (Undang-Undang Perseroan Terbatas, Law 40/2007) reflects its civil law heritage.

## China

### Legal Framework

China's corporate governance regime is anchored in the Company Law of the People's Republic of China (中华人民共和国公司法), which underwent its most significant revision since 1993 when the Standing Committee of the National People's Congress adopted the 2023 revision (effective July 1, 2024). The revised Company Law introduced several transformative provisions:

- **Article 137**: Mandates that at least one-third of board members of listed companies be independent directors, codifying a requirement previously established only through CSRC rules. Article 139 permits companies to establish an audit committee of the board as an alternative to a board of supervisors (监事会), marking a historic shift toward allowing monistic board structures.
- **Article 47**: Introduces a five-year capital contribution period for registered capital, replacing the prior system that allowed indefinite contribution periods, with a three-year transition period for existing companies.
- **Article 21**: Strengthens the duty of loyalty (忠实义务) and duty of diligence (勤勉义务) owed by directors, supervisors, and senior management, and extends fiduciary duties to controlling shareholders and de facto controllers.
- **Article 89**: Creates a simplified deregistration procedure for companies, addressing China's historically cumbersome process.
- **Article 180-181**: Introduces a horizontal piercing of the corporate veil (横向法人人格否认), allowing courts to disregard the separate legal personality of affiliated companies where assets are commingled.

The Securities Law (证券法), revised in 2019 and effective March 1, 2020, shifted to a registration-based IPO system (replacing the approval-based system) and enhanced investor protection through representative litigation (Article 95), allowing investor protection agencies to bring "opt-out" class action suits.

### Party Committee Integration

The integration of Communist Party committees (党委) into corporate governance represents China's most distinctive contribution to global corporate governance discourse. The Party's role was formalized through:

- **General Secretary Xi Jinping's speech at the National SOE Party Building Conference** (October 10-11, 2016): Established that Party leadership and modern corporate governance are "organically unified" (有机统一) and that Party committee deliberation should be a "pre-procedure" (前置程序) for major corporate decisions.
- **SASAC Guidance on Further Improving the Corporate Governance of SOEs** (2017): Required SOE articles of association to include provisions on the Party committee's role, specifically the "Three Important and One Large" (三重一大) framework: important decisions (重大决策), important personnel appointments (重要人事任免), important project investments (重大项目安排), and large expenditures (大额资金运作).
- **CSRC Guidelines on Corporate Governance of Listed Companies** (2018 revision): Required listed companies with party organizations to embed Party committee provisions in their articles of association and provide for "cross-appointment" (交叉任职) between Party committee members and board/management positions.

In practice, the Party Secretary typically serves concurrently as the Chairman of the Board in SOEs. The Party committee reviews and provides opinions on major decisions before they are submitted to the Board of Directors for formal approval, creating a parallel governance channel. For privately controlled listed companies, the requirements are less prescriptive but Party building obligations still exist.

### Mixed Ownership Reform

China's mixed ownership reform (混合所有制改革, hunhe suoyouzhi gaige) aims to improve SOE efficiency by introducing private capital, market mechanisms, and professional management while maintaining state control over strategic sectors. Key milestones include:

- **Guiding Opinions on Deepening SOE Reform** (中共中央、国务院关于深化国有企业改革的指导意见, September 2015): The foundational document classifying SOEs into commercial (competitive) and public welfare categories, with different reform pathways.
- **SASAC Action Plan for Improving Governance of SOE Subsidiaries** (2022-2025): Targets board effectiveness, independent director roles, and professional manager recruitment.
- **Pilot Programs**: China Unicom's 2017 mixed ownership reform raised RMB 78 billion from strategic investors including Alibaba, Tencent, Baidu, and JD.com, while granting employee stock ownership plans covering 7,800 employees. China National Salt Industry Corporation and Dongfang Electric Corporation completed similar reforms.

State-owned Assets Supervision and Administration Commission (SASAC) directly supervises 97 central SOEs (央企) and indirectly oversees thousands of subsidiaries, with combined assets exceeding RMB 80 trillion (USD 11 trillion). Provincial SASACs supervise local SOEs with assets of approximately RMB 200 trillion.

## India

### Legal Framework

The Companies Act, 2013 (replacing the Companies Act, 1956) is one of the most comprehensive and prescriptive corporate governance statutes in the Global South, with 470 sections and 29 schedules. Key governance provisions include:

- **Section 149(1)**: Minimum 3 directors for public companies, with at least one-third independent directors for listed companies. Section 149(1) proviso mandates at least one woman director for listed companies and specified classes of public companies.
- **Section 149(6)**: Defines "independent director" with detailed negative criteria, including a prohibition on material pecuniary relationships with the company, a maximum of two consecutive terms of five years, and a three-year cooling-off period before re-appointment.
- **Section 135 (CSR)**: Companies with net worth exceeding INR 500 crore, turnover exceeding INR 1,000 crore, or net profit exceeding INR 5 crore during the immediately preceding financial year must constitute a CSR Committee and spend at least 2% of average net profits of the preceding three financial years on CSR activities listed in Schedule VII. The Companies (Amendment) Act, 2019 amended Section 135 to require transfer of unspent CSR amounts to a Fund specified in Schedule VII within six months.
- **Section 166**: Codified directors' duties including: duty to act in good faith in the interest of the company, its employees, shareholders, community, and environment (Section 166(2)); duty to exercise independent judgment (Section 166(3)); duty to exercise due care and diligence (Section 166(4)); and prohibition on achieving any undue gain (Section 166(5)).
- **Sections 241-245**: Oppression and mismanagement provisions, with Section 245 introducing a class action mechanism allowing a specified number of members (100 or 10% of total members for companies with share capital; 1/5 of total members for companies without share capital) or depositors to file class action suits before the National Company Law Tribunal (NCLT).

### SEBI Regulatory Framework

The Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI) LODR (Listing Obligations and Disclosure Requirements) Regulations, 2015 impose additional governance requirements on listed companies:

- **Regulation 17**: Board composition requirements, including at least six directors for the top 2,000 listed companies (by market capitalization), with not less than one-half being independent directors where the chairperson is a non-executive director, and two-thirds independent directors where the chairperson is an executive or promoter director.
- **Regulation 17A**: Maximum number of directorships -- a person cannot serve as independent director in more than seven listed companies, or three listed companies if serving as whole-time director in any listed company.
- **Regulation 18**: Audit committee with at least two-thirds independent directors, including members with financial literacy and at least one member with accounting or financial management expertise.
- **Regulation 27**: Corporate governance compliance report to be submitted quarterly to the stock exchange.

### Landmark Cases

Indian corporate governance jurisprudence has been shaped by several significant disputes:

- **Cyrus Mistry v. Tata Sons**: The Supreme Court in Tata Consultancy Services Ltd. v. Cyrus Investments Pvt. Ltd. (2021) 9 SCC 449 reversed the NCLAT's order restoring Cyrus Mistry as Executive Chairman of Tata Sons, holding that the NCLAT exceeded its jurisdiction by substituting its judgment on a business decision. However, the Court observed that Tata Sons' conversion from a public company to a private company may have been "oppressive" to minority shareholders under Section 241.
- **Satyam Scandal (2009)**: The confession by Satyam Computer Services Chairman B. Ramalinga Raju of a INR 7,000 crore accounting fraud led directly to the strengthening of independent director requirements, mandatory audit committee powers, and enhanced disclosure norms in the Companies Act, 2013.

## Africa

### South Africa

South Africa's corporate governance framework operates on a dual-track system: the Companies Act 71 of 2008 (as amended) provides the statutory foundation, while the King Report on Corporate Governance supplies the soft-law principles and practices that have made South Africa a global leader in stakeholder governance.

**King IV Report on Corporate Governance (2016)**:

The King IV Report, issued by the Institute of Directors in Southern Africa (IoDSA), introduced the "apply and explain" regime (replacing King III's "apply or explain"), meaning that all listed companies on the Johannesburg Stock Exchange (JSE) are expected to apply all principles and explain how they have been applied, rather than choosing whether to apply. Key principles include:

- **Principle 1**: The governing body should lead ethically and effectively.
- **Principle 3**: The governing body should ensure the organization is a responsible corporate citizen, treating stakeholder relationships as a strategic asset.
- **Principle 5**: The governing body should ensure that reports enable stakeholders to make informed assessments of the organization's performance and its short-, medium-, and long-term prospects, embracing integrated reporting under the International Integrated Reporting Framework.
- **Principle 8**: The governing body should ensure that its composition reflects competence, diversity (including race, gender, age, and field of knowledge), and independence.
- **Principle 15**: The governing body should ensure that assurance services and functions enable an effective control environment supporting the integrity of information for internal decision-making and external reporting.

The JSE Listings Requirements (Section 3.84) mandate compliance with King IV. The Companies Act 71 of 2008 provides statutory governance requirements including: mandatory audit committee for public and state-owned companies (Section 94); social and ethics committee for listed companies and companies exceeding prescribed thresholds (Section 72(4) and Regulation 43); and business rescue proceedings as an alternative to liquidation (Chapter 6).

### Nigeria

CAMA 2020 modernized Nigerian corporate governance by:

- **Section 275-277**: Requiring public companies to have at least three independent directors, with independence defined by reference to financial, familial, and business relationships.
- **Section 330**: Mandating audit committees with equal representation of directors and shareholders (maximum six members, three from each group), reflecting Nigeria's unique shareholder-elected audit committee model.
- **Section 46**: Introducing limited liability partnerships (LLPs) into Nigerian law for the first time.
- **Section 868-872**: Modernizing company administration procedures, including mandatory electronic filing and digital records.

The Nigerian Code of Corporate Governance 2018, issued by the Financial Reporting Council of Nigeria (FRCN), applies to all public companies and regulates board composition, board evaluation, related party transactions, and sustainability reporting. The Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) Nigerian Code of Corporate Governance for publicly listed companies provides additional market-specific requirements.

## Latin America

### Brazil

Brazilian corporate governance is governed by the Corporation Law (Lei das Sociedades por Ações, Law 6,404/1976, as amended by Law 10,303/2001 and subsequent amendments) and the CVM (Comissão de Valores Mobiliários) regulations. The Corporation Law provides:

- **Article 116**: Defines controlling shareholder and imposes duties to exercise control power in the company's interest and social function, with liability for damages to minority shareholders (Article 117, abuse of control).
- **Article 161-163**: Establishes the Fiscal Council (Conselho Fiscal) as a shareholder-elected body (distinct from the Board of Directors) with authority to oversee management activities, review financial statements, and report irregularities. The Fiscal Council is mandatory when requested by shareholders holding at least 10% of voting shares.
- **Article 141**: Minority shareholder rights including separate board election by holders of preferred shares (without voting rights) and minority common shareholders (Article 141, paragraphs 4-5).

**B3 Novo Mercado**:

B3 (Brasil, Bolsa, Balcão) operates differentiated governance listing segments:

- **Novo Mercado**: The premium segment requiring 100% common shares (one-share-one-vote), minimum 20% free float, board with at least five members and 20% independent directors, audit committee, 100% tag-along rights, and mandatory arbitration through the Market Arbitration Chamber (Câmara de Arbitragem do Mercado, CAM). As of 2025, approximately 190 companies listed on Novo Mercado representing over 70% of B3's market capitalization.
- **Nível 2**: Permits preferred shares but requires 80% tag-along for common shares and 100% for preferred shares, independent board members, and arbitration.
- **Nível 1**: Minimum 25% free float and enhanced disclosure requirements.

The Novo Mercado has served as a model for governance-tiered listing segments globally, including India's SEBI proposal for similar differentiated compliance frameworks.

### Colombia and Mexico

Colombia's Financial Superintendence (Superintendencia Financiera de Colombia) oversees corporate governance through the Country Code (Código País) of best governance practices, updated in 2014, which operates on a "comply or explain" basis for listed companies. Mexico's General Business Corporations Law (Ley General de Sociedades Mercantiles, 2014 amendments) and the Securities Market Law (Ley del Mercado de Valores, 2005) establish governance requirements including mandatory audit and corporate practices committees for listed companies, with at least 25% independent directors.

## Southeast Asia

### Indonesia

Indonesia's corporate governance is characterized by its mandatory dual-board structure (two-tier system) under Company Law 40/2007:

- **Board of Commissioners (Dewan Komisaris)**: Supervisory function, including overseeing the Board of Directors and approving certain material transactions. At least 30% independent commissioners required for listed companies (OJK Regulation 33/POJK.04/2014).
- **Board of Directors (Direksi)**: Management function, responsible for day-to-day operations. Listed companies must have at least two directors.
- **Article 74**: Mandates that companies conducting business activities in the natural resources sector implement environmental and social responsibility, with sanctions under implementing regulations.

OJK Regulation 73/POJK.04/2017 on Indonesian Corporate Governance Principles sets out five principles: transparency, accountability, responsibility, independence, and fairness. For state-owned enterprises (BUMN), the Ministry of State-Owned Enterprises Regulation 2/2023 imposes additional governance requirements including independent commissioner ratios, related party transaction controls, and performance-based management contracts.

Indonesia's SOE sector (BUMN) includes significant enterprises such as Bank Mandiri, Bank Rakyat Indonesia, Pertamina (oil and gas), PLN (electricity), Telkom Indonesia (telecommunications), and Garuda Indonesia (airlines). The 2023 formation of the Indonesia Investment Authority (INA) as a sovereign wealth fund and the creation of holding company structures (e.g., mining holding company MIND ID, pharmaceutical holding company Bio Farma) represent ongoing governance reforms.

### Philippines

The Revised Corporation Code (Republic Act No. 11232, 2019), replacing the 40-year-old Corporation Code (Batas Pambansa Blg. 68, 1980), modernized Philippine corporate governance:

- **Section 22**: Introduced the one-person corporation (OPC), allowing a single stockholder to incorporate.
- **Section 23**: At least two independent directors for corporations vested with public interest (listed companies, banks, insurance companies, and other publicly held companies), with independence defined by the SEC.
- **Section 29**: Enhanced the right of inspection and broadened access to corporate records.
- **Section 177**: Perpetual corporate existence as the default, replacing the previous 50-year term requiring renewal.
- **Section 91-92**: Codified appraisal rights for dissenting stockholders in cases of amendments restricting their rights, mergers, and asset sales.

The SEC Revised Code of Corporate Governance for Public Companies and Registered Issuers (SEC Memorandum Circular No. 19, Series of 2016) requires: a board with at least two independent directors or such number as constitutes 20% of the board, whichever is higher; mandatory board committees (audit, nomination, remuneration, corporate governance); and compliance officer designation.

## Comparative Analysis

### Ownership Concentration and Minority Protection

All five regions exhibit high ownership concentration compared to Anglo-American dispersed shareholding models. China's state ownership of SOEs, India's promoter-dominated family business groups (the top 10 business groups account for approximately 20% of BSE market capitalization), South Africa's BEE-driven ownership transformation, Brazil's controlling family shareholders, and Indonesia/Philippines' conglomerate structures (Indonesian Chinese-Indonesian conglomerates and Philippine family conglomerates like Ayala, SM, and JG Summit) all create principal-principal agency problems distinct from the principal-agent framework underlying Anglo-American governance models. India's Section 245 class action and Brazil's CVM arbitration mechanisms represent the most developed minority protection tools, while China's 2019 Securities Law representative litigation provision is emerging as a significant enforcement mechanism.

### Stakeholder Governance Models

South Africa's King Code represents the most developed stakeholder governance model in the Global South, predating and influencing the EU's ESG reporting directives and the UK's Section 172 stakeholder duty. India's Section 135 mandatory CSR spending is the world's most prescriptive CSR mandate. Indonesia's Article 74 environmental and social responsibility requirement for natural resource companies represents a sector-specific stakeholder approach. China's integration of Party committee oversight introduces political stakeholders into governance in a manner without parallel elsewhere.

### Enforcement Challenges

Across all jurisdictions, the gap between law-on-the-books and law-in-action remains significant. China's enforcement priorities are politically determined, with anti-corruption campaigns driving governance improvements in SOEs. India's NCLT system faces significant case backlogs (over 50,000 pending cases as of 2024). Nigeria's CAC enforcement capacity is constrained by resources. South Africa's Companies and Intellectual Property Commission (CIPC) handles over 900,000 annual filings with limited enforcement capacity. The Philippines' SEC prioritizes investor protection but has limited staff for proactive governance monitoring.
