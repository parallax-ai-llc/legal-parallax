## Summary

Cooperation with the International Criminal Court (ICC) -- the permanent international tribunal established by the Rome Statute of 1998 to prosecute individuals for genocide, crimes against humanity, war crimes, and the crime of aggression -- presents complex questions at the intersection of international criminal law, constitutional law, and sovereignty, as the Court lacks its own enforcement apparatus and depends entirely on state cooperation for the execution of arrest warrants, the collection of evidence, the protection of witnesses, and the enforcement of sentences. South Korea ratified the Rome Statute on November 13, 2002, and enacted the Act on Punishment of Crimes under Jurisdiction of the International Criminal Court (국제형사재판소 관할 범죄의 처벌 등에 관한 법률, ICC Crimes Act) in 2007, establishing both implementing criminal offenses and cooperation procedures, including provisions for the arrest and surrender of suspects, mutual legal assistance, and the enforcement of ICC sentences; however, constitutional questions remain regarding the relationship between the ICC's complementarity principle and Korean constitutional protections, particularly the prohibition on extradition of nationals in certain circumstances. The United States has not ratified the Rome Statute and has actively opposed ICC jurisdiction over US nationals through the American Service-Members' Protection Act of 2002 (ASPA, colloquially known as "The Hague Invasion Act"), bilateral Article 98 agreements, and executive sanctions against ICC personnel, though the US has also cooperated selectively with the ICC in specific situations, reflecting an ambivalent and evolving relationship. Germany ratified the Rome Statute on December 11, 2000, and enacted the Völkerstrafgesetzbuch (VStGB, 2002) and the ICC Cooperation Act (IStGH-Zusammenarbeitsgesetz, 2002) providing comprehensive implementing legislation; Germany has been among the most active ICC supporters, contributing personnel, funding, and political support. At the European level, EU common positions and Council decisions have established frameworks for member state cooperation with the ICC, the ECtHR has addressed the tension between ICC cooperation obligations and ECHR rights (particularly regarding surrender and life imprisonment), and the Assembly of States Parties continues to address cooperation challenges, including the controversial non-arrest of Sudanese President Omar al-Bashir by multiple states parties.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea ratified the Rome Statute on November 13, 2002, becoming the 84th state party. The implementing legislation, the Act on Punishment of Crimes under Jurisdiction of the International Criminal Court (국제형사재판소 관할 범죄의 처벌 등에 관한 법률), was enacted on December 21, 2007, after a five-year legislative process. The Act serves a dual purpose: it domestically criminalizes genocide, crimes against humanity, and war crimes (implementing the complementarity principle), and it establishes procedures for cooperation with the ICC.

### Cooperation Procedures

Chapter 3 of the ICC Crimes Act establishes the cooperation framework. The Minister of Justice (법무부장관) serves as the central authority for ICC cooperation requests. Procedures include: arrest and surrender of suspects (Articles 18-23); assistance with investigation and evidence collection (Articles 24-26); interim measures and freezing of assets (Articles 27-28); and enforcement of ICC sentences (Articles 29-30).

### Constitutional Considerations

Article 6(2) of the Korean Constitution provides that the status of aliens shall be guaranteed as prescribed by international law and treaties. More critically, Article 12(1) guarantees that no person shall be arrested, detained, searched, or interrogated except as provided by law, and Article 27 guarantees the right to trial by judges qualified under the Constitution and law. The surrender of Korean nationals to the ICC raises potential tensions with these provisions, though the Constitutional Court has not yet directly addressed this question.

### Korean ICC Engagement

South Korea has played an active but low-profile role in ICC affairs. Korean nationals have served in significant ICC positions, including Judge Song Sang-Hyun (송상현), who served as President of the ICC from 2009 to 2015. South Korea contributes regular assessed contributions to the ICC budget and has participated in Assembly of States Parties proceedings.

### Penalties for Non-Cooperation

| Violation | Consequence |
|-----------|------------|
| Failure to comply with ICC cooperation request | Diplomatic consequences; potential ASP referral |
| Harboring ICC fugitive | Potential criminal liability under ICC Crimes Act |
| Obstruction of ICC investigation | Potential prosecution under ICC Crimes Act or Criminal Act |

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States signed the Rome Statute on December 31, 2000, during the final days of the Clinton administration, but the signature was "unsigned" by the Bush administration on May 6, 2002, through a letter from Under Secretary of State John Bolton to UN Secretary-General Kofi Annan. The US has not ratified the Rome Statute and is not an ICC state party.

**American Service-Members' Protection Act (ASPA, 2002):** Enacted as Title II of the Supplemental Appropriations Act for Further Recovery From and Response to Terrorist Attacks on the United States, FY 2002, ASPA (22 U.S.C. Section 7421 et seq.) contains several provisions opposing ICC jurisdiction over US nationals: Section 2004 prohibits cooperation with the ICC, including the arrest or surrender of US persons to the ICC; Section 2007 authorizes the President to use "all means necessary and appropriate" to release US personnel detained by the ICC (the provision giving rise to the "Hague Invasion Act" nickname); and Section 2008 restricts military assistance to states parties to the Rome Statute (though this provision has been waived for most countries).

**Article 98 Agreements:** The US negotiated bilateral agreements with approximately 100 countries under Article 98(2) of the Rome Statute, in which those countries agreed not to surrender US nationals to the ICC without US consent. These agreements have been controversial, with critics arguing they undermine the ICC's effectiveness.

### Selective Cooperation

Despite its opposition to ICC jurisdiction over US nationals, the United States has cooperated with the ICC in specific situations: the US voted in the UN Security Council to refer the situation in Darfur, Sudan, to the ICC (UNSC Resolution 1593, 2005); the US voted for the referral of the situation in Libya (UNSC Resolution 1970, 2011); and the Obama administration engaged more constructively with the ICC, with the State Department establishing a position for an ambassador-at-large for global criminal justice.

### ICC Investigation into US Actions

The ICC Prosecutor's investigation into the situation in Afghanistan, authorized by the Appeals Chamber in March 2020, included allegations against US military and CIA personnel for torture and other war crimes. This investigation prompted the Trump administration's Executive Order 13928 (June 11, 2020), imposing sanctions on ICC personnel, including travel bans and asset freezes on Prosecutor Fatou Bensouda and senior ICC officials. The Biden administration revoked these sanctions on April 2, 2021.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany ratified the Rome Statute on December 11, 2000, among the first states to do so. Germany enacted comprehensive implementing legislation through two statutes on June 26, 2002: the Völkerstrafgesetzbuch (VStGB, Code of Crimes against International Law) and the Gesetz über die Zusammenarbeit mit dem Internationalen Strafgerichtshof (IStGH-Zusammenarbeitsgesetz, ICC Cooperation Act).

**Constitutional Amendment:** To enable the surrender of German nationals to the ICC, Germany amended Article 16(2) of the Grundgesetz in 2000. The original provision prohibited the extradition of German nationals entirely. The amendment permits extradition or surrender of German nationals to an EU member state or to an international court, provided that the rule of law is observed. This amendment also facilitated compliance with the European Arrest Warrant.

### ICC Cooperation Act (IStGH-Zusammenarbeitsgesetz)

The Act establishes detailed procedures for all forms of cooperation with the ICC: arrest and surrender of suspects (Sections 2-18); other forms of judicial assistance including evidence gathering, witness protection, and asset freezing (Sections 19-40); and enforcement of ICC sentences in German facilities (Sections 41-47). The Federal Ministry of Justice (Bundesministerium der Justiz) serves as the central authority.

### German Support for the ICC

Germany is consistently among the largest financial contributors to the ICC, contributing approximately 11% of the Court's annual budget (the second-largest share after Japan). Germany has also provided significant operational support, including: secondment of personnel to the Office of the Prosecutor; hosting of ICC evidence storage facilities; and diplomatic support for ICC cooperation resolutions in multilateral forums.

### Complementarity in Practice

The VStGB's comprehensive universal jurisdiction provisions (Section 1: no nexus requirement) are designed to ensure that Germany can always exercise complementarity, preventing the ICC from needing to assert jurisdiction over situations where German courts are willing and able to prosecute. The landmark Koblenz trials of Syrian officials demonstrate Germany's commitment to domestic prosecution of international crimes.

### Key Provisions

| IStGH-Zusammenarbeitsgesetz Provision | Function |
|----------------------------------------|----------|
| Sections 2-18 | Arrest and surrender procedures |
| Sections 19-40 | Mutual legal assistance |
| Sections 41-47 | Enforcement of ICC sentences |
| VStGB Section 1 | Universal jurisdiction (ensures complementarity) |

## ECHR & EU

### EU Common Positions and Council Decisions

The EU has adopted multiple common positions and decisions supporting ICC cooperation: Common Position 2003/444/CFSP (June 16, 2003) committed EU member states to supporting the effective functioning of the ICC and promoting universal ratification of the Rome Statute. Council Decision 2011/168/CFSP (March 21, 2011) updated and strengthened the EU's ICC cooperation framework.

The EU has also included ICC cooperation clauses in agreements with third countries, requiring partners to cooperate with the ICC and to consider ratifying the Rome Statute.

### ECHR and ICC Cooperation

The ECtHR has addressed potential conflicts between ICC cooperation obligations and ECHR rights.

**Nait-Liman v. Switzerland (Application No. 51357/07, Grand Chamber Judgment of March 15, 2018):** While not directly an ICC case, the Grand Chamber addressed the scope of states' obligations to exercise universal jurisdiction for civil claims arising from international crimes. The Court found no violation of Article 6 where Switzerland declined jurisdiction over a civil claim for torture committed in Tunisia.

**Life Imprisonment Question:** The Rome Statute permits life imprisonment (Article 77(1)(b)), while Protocol No. 13 to the ECHR (abolishing the death penalty in all circumstances) and ECtHR jurisprudence (Vinter and Others v. United Kingdom, 2013) require that life sentences be reducible. The Rome Statute addresses this through Article 110, which provides for mandatory review of sentences after 25 years. States parties that have constitutional prohibitions on life imprisonment (e.g., Portugal, which limits sentences to 25 years) have addressed this through declarations upon ratification.

### Al-Bashir Non-Arrest Controversy

The failure of multiple states parties to arrest Sudanese President Omar al-Bashir when he visited their territory, despite ICC arrest warrants issued in 2009 and 2010, highlighted the enforcement challenges of ICC cooperation. The ICC Appeals Chamber addressed South Africa's non-arrest of al-Bashir (Judgment of May 6, 2019) and Jordan's non-arrest (Judgment of May 6, 2019), finding both in non-compliance with their Rome Statute obligations. The Appeals Chamber held that sitting heads of state of non-party states do not enjoy immunity before the ICC when the situation has been referred by the UN Security Council.

### Assembly of States Parties

The Assembly of States Parties (ASP) to the Rome Statute, which meets annually, addresses cooperation challenges through its working groups and resolutions. The ASP adopted a resolution on cooperation at its 21st session (December 2022) reaffirming states' obligations and establishing review mechanisms for non-compliance.

## Comparative Analysis

### Ratification and Implementation

The three state jurisdictions represent three models of engagement with the ICC: full ratification with comprehensive implementing legislation (Germany, South Korea); non-ratification with active opposition (United States). Germany's approach is the most robust, combining constitutional amendment, substantive criminal law (VStGB), and procedural cooperation law. South Korea's implementation was delayed but eventually comprehensive. The US's non-ratification creates a significant gap in the global ICC cooperation framework, particularly given US military operations worldwide.

### Constitutional Adaptation

Both Germany and South Korea faced constitutional challenges in implementing the Rome Statute, particularly regarding the surrender of nationals to the ICC. Germany resolved this through constitutional amendment (Art. 16(2) GG). South Korea's approach relies on the constitutional hierarchy of treaties but has not been tested through judicial challenge.

### Complementarity

Germany most fully implements the complementarity principle, with the VStGB providing universal jurisdiction that ensures German courts can prosecute all crimes within the ICC's jurisdiction. South Korea's ICC Crimes Act similarly provides domestic prosecution capacity. The US, as a non-party, operates outside the complementarity framework but prosecutes war crimes through military justice and federal criminal law.

### Political Will

Political will remains the decisive factor in ICC cooperation effectiveness. Germany demonstrates consistent strong political will. South Korea is supportive but less active. The United States oscillates between hostility and selective cooperation depending on political administration. The EU's collective position provides important institutional support but cannot compensate for individual state defections on specific cooperation obligations.
