## Summary

Pesticide regulation -- the legal frameworks governing the registration, sale, use, and residue limits of chemical and biological substances used to control agricultural pests, weeds, and diseases -- is a critical intersection of agricultural productivity, environmental protection, and public health. South Korea regulates pesticides through the Pesticide Control Act (농약관리법) and the Agricultural Chemicals Control Act, administered by the Rural Development Administration (농촌진흥청, RDA), which conducts safety evaluations and establishes maximum residue limits (MRLs); South Korea adopted a Positive List System (PLS, 허용물질목록관리제도) in 2019 that sets a default MRL of 0.01 mg/kg for any pesticide-crop combination not explicitly registered, effectively banning the use of unregistered pesticides and significantly tightening import standards. The United States regulates pesticides under the Federal Insecticide, Fungicide, and Rodenticide Act (FIFRA, 7 U.S.C. Sections 136-136y), which requires EPA registration of all pesticide products and periodic reregistration, and the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (FFDCA), under which the EPA sets tolerances (MRLs) for pesticide residues in food; the Food Quality Protection Act of 1996 (FQPA) strengthened protections for children and infants and required consideration of aggregate and cumulative exposure; the 2024 EPA decision to revoke remaining food tolerances for chlorpyrifos following the Ninth Circuit's order in LULAC v. EPA (No. 19-71979, 2021) represented one of the most significant individual pesticide regulatory actions in decades. Germany, operating within the EU framework, regulates pesticides under Regulation (EC) No 1107/2009 (plant protection products) and Regulation (EC) No 396/2005 (MRLs), with the Federal Office of Consumer Protection and Food Safety (BVL) serving as the national competent authority for pesticide authorization; the EU's approach is distinguished by its hazard-based assessment criteria (substances with inherently dangerous properties may be banned regardless of exposure levels), the precautionary principle, and recent high-profile regulatory controversies including the contentious 2023 reauthorization of glyphosate for ten years despite significant opposition from several member states and the European Citizens' Initiative "Ban Glyphosate." At the European level, the European Commission's proposed Sustainable Use of Plant Protection Products Regulation (SUR, COM(2022) 305), which would have mandated a 50% reduction in pesticide use and risk by 2030 as part of the Farm to Fork Strategy, was withdrawn in February 2024 following intense opposition from agricultural interests and member states; the ECHR has addressed pesticide-related harm through Articles 2 (right to life) and 8 (right to private life), with the Court finding in Tatar v. Romania (2009) and Fadeyeva v. Russia (2005) that states have positive obligations to protect individuals from environmental pollution including agricultural chemicals.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

**Pesticide Control Act (농약관리법):** This statute governs the registration, manufacturing, import, sale, and use of pesticides in South Korea. The Rural Development Administration (RDA) conducts safety evaluations including toxicological assessment, environmental fate studies, residue trials, and efficacy testing. All pesticides must be registered before they can be manufactured, imported, or sold.

**Positive List System (PLS, 허용물질목록관리제도, 2019):** Effective January 1, 2019, the PLS establishes a default maximum residue limit of 0.01 mg/kg (essentially zero tolerance) for any pesticide-crop combination not explicitly registered with the Korean MRL system. This means that only pesticides with established Korean MRLs may be present in food; all others must be at or below the default limit. The PLS has significant implications for imported food, as pesticides registered in exporting countries but not in Korea trigger violations if detected above 0.01 mg/kg.

**Impact of PLS:** The system has caused trade friction with major agricultural exporters including the United States, Australia, and Brazil, who argue that the 0.01 mg/kg default is unnecessarily restrictive for pesticides with established international safety profiles (Codex MRLs). South Korea has responded by accelerating the establishment of import tolerance MRLs and accepting Codex MRLs as interim standards for some pesticide-crop combinations.

### Pesticide Use Patterns

South Korea uses approximately 12-13 kg of active ingredient per hectare of cropland annually -- among the highest rates in the OECD, reflecting intensive agricultural practices on small, fragmented plots. The Ministry of Agriculture, Food and Rural Affairs (농림축산식품부) has set targets for pesticide use reduction through the promotion of integrated pest management (IPM) and environmentally friendly farming practices (친환경농업).

### Environmentally Friendly Agriculture

The Act on the Promotion of Environmentally Friendly Agriculture and Fisheries and the Management and Support of Organic Foods (친환경농어업 육성 및 유기식품 등의 관리·지원에 관한 법률) establishes the Korean organic and low-pesticide certification system. Certified organic farming area has increased from approximately 20,000 hectares in 2010 to approximately 30,000 hectares in 2024, representing approximately 1.8% of total agricultural area.

## United States

### Legal Framework

**FIFRA (7 U.S.C. Sections 136-136y):** The primary federal statute governing pesticide registration and use. Key provisions:
- All pesticides must be registered by the EPA before sale or distribution
- Registration requires demonstration that the pesticide will not cause "unreasonable adverse effects on the environment" considering economic, social, and environmental costs and benefits
- EPA conducts periodic registration review (every 15 years) for all registered pesticides
- FIFRA preempts state registration requirements but allows states to impose additional use restrictions

**Food Quality Protection Act (FQPA, 1996):** This landmark amendment to FIFRA and FFDCA:
- Established a single, health-based safety standard for pesticide residues in food: "reasonable certainty that no harm will result"
- Required consideration of aggregate exposure (all dietary and non-dietary sources)
- Required consideration of cumulative effects of pesticides with common mechanisms of toxicity
- Mandated an additional 10-fold safety factor for children and infants unless adequate data demonstrate that a lesser factor is protective
- Required expedited reassessment of existing tolerances

**Chlorpyrifos Regulatory Action:**

The organophosphate insecticide chlorpyrifos became the most contested individual pesticide in U.S. regulatory history:
- 2007: EPA proposed to revoke food tolerances based on neurodevelopmental risk to children
- 2017: EPA reversed course under the Trump administration, denying the petition to revoke tolerances
- 2021: Ninth Circuit in LULAC v. EPA (No. 19-71979) ordered EPA to either grant the petition or modify the tolerance to a safe level within 60 days
- 2021: EPA revoked all food tolerances for chlorpyrifos (effective February 2022)
- 2024: EPA finalized cancellation of most remaining agricultural uses

### State-Level Regulation

States may impose use restrictions more stringent than federal requirements. California's Department of Pesticide Regulation (DPR) operates the most aggressive state pesticide regulatory program, maintaining its own registration review process, pesticide illness surveillance, and environmental monitoring. Hawaii passed Act 45 (2018) restricting the use of restricted-use pesticides near schools.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany regulates pesticides within the EU framework, which operates through a two-stage process: active substance approval at the EU level, and product authorization at the national (zonal) level.

**EU Regulation (EC) No 1107/2009:** Governs the placing of plant protection products on the market. Key features:
- **Hazard-based cut-off criteria:** Substances classified as carcinogenic (category 1A or 1B), mutagenic, toxic for reproduction, or as endocrine disruptors may not be approved, regardless of exposure levels. This hazard-based approach differs fundamentally from the U.S. risk-based approach, which considers both hazard and exposure.
- **Zonal authorization:** The EU is divided into three zones (North, Central, South); a product authorized in one member state of a zone can seek mutual recognition in other zone members, though member states retain the right to impose additional conditions.
- **Substitution principle:** Where a safer alternative exists, member states may restrict approval of more hazardous substances through comparative assessment.

**BVL and BfR:** The Federal Office of Consumer Protection and Food Safety (BVL, Bundesamt für Verbraucherschutz und Lebensmittelsicherheit) serves as the national competent authority for pesticide product authorization. The Federal Institute for Risk Assessment (BfR, Bundesinstitut für Risikobewertung) conducts health risk assessments and has been centrally involved in the glyphosate controversy as the rapporteur member state for EFSA's assessment.

### Glyphosate Controversy

Germany served as the EU Rapporteur Member State for the 2017 and 2023 glyphosate reauthorization assessments, placing the BfR at the center of one of the most polarized regulatory debates in European history:

- **2017 reauthorization:** The European Commission reauthorized glyphosate for 5 years after EFSA's assessment concluded it was unlikely to be carcinogenic, despite IARC's 2015 classification as "probably carcinogenic to humans" (Group 2A). Germany abstained in the final Council vote.
- **2023 reauthorization:** EFSA completed its updated assessment in 2023, again finding no critical areas of concern. The Commission reauthorized glyphosate for 10 years (until December 2033). Germany voted against reauthorization in the Council, but the required qualified majority to block was not achieved.
- **National restrictions:** Germany adopted a national plan to phase out glyphosate use in specific contexts (parks, private gardens, pre-harvest applications) through the Plant Protection Application Ordinance (Pflanzenschutz-Anwendungsverordnung) amendments, while stopping short of a national ban.

### Neonicotinoid Ban

The EU banned the outdoor use of three neonicotinoid insecticides (clothianidin, imidacloprid, thiamethoxam) in 2018 through Commission Implementing Regulations 2018/783, 2018/784, and 2018/785, based on EFSA's assessment of risks to bees and other pollinators. Germany supported the ban and has implemented it through national enforcement measures. The ban was upheld by the General Court in Bayer CropScience v. Commission (T-429/18, 2021).

## ECHR / EU

### EU Regulatory Framework

**Maximum Residue Limits (Regulation (EC) No 396/2005):** Harmonized EU-wide MRLs for pesticide residues in food and feed. The default MRL for any substance-crop combination without a specific MRL is 0.01 mg/kg. EFSA coordinates MRL assessments, and the European Commission adopts MRLs through comitology procedures.

**Sustainable Use Directive (2009/128/EC):** Requires member states to adopt National Action Plans for the sustainable use of pesticides, promote integrated pest management (IPM), and protect water bodies and sensitive areas from pesticide contamination.

**Proposed SUR (COM(2022) 305 -- withdrawn 2024):** The European Commission proposed a regulation to replace the Sustainable Use Directive, mandating a legally binding 50% reduction in pesticide use and risk by 2030. Key provisions included:
- Ban on all pesticide use in sensitive areas (parks, schools, playgrounds, hospitals)
- Electronic record-keeping for all professional pesticide use
- Mandatory IPM implementation with verification
- Member state national reduction targets based on current use intensity

The proposal was withdrawn in February 2024 following protests by European farmers, opposition from several member states, and rejection in the European Parliament's plenary vote. The withdrawal was widely seen as a political concession amid the 2024 European farmer protests.

### ECHR and Pesticide Exposure

**Tatar v. Romania (Application No. 67021/01, 2009):** While addressing gold mining with sodium cyanide rather than agricultural pesticides, the Court's findings on state obligations to assess and mitigate environmental health risks apply directly to pesticide regulation. The Court held that Article 8 requires states to conduct adequate environmental and health impact assessments before authorizing potentially hazardous activities.

**Fadeyeva v. Russia (Application No. 55723/00, 2005):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where industrial pollution (including chemical exposure) near the applicant's home exceeded safe levels. The principles established -- that states have a positive obligation to protect individuals from serious environmental pollution -- apply to agricultural pesticide exposure scenarios, particularly for communities near intensive agricultural operations.

**Lopez Ostra v. Spain (Application No. 16798/90, 1994):** The foundational case establishing that environmental pollution can constitute an interference with the right to private life under Article 8, even without direct physical harm, if it seriously affects a person's quality of life.

### Codex Alimentarius

The Codex Committee on Pesticide Residues (CCPR) establishes international MRLs that serve as reference standards for international trade. Codex MRLs are recognized by the WTO SPS Agreement as the international benchmark, meaning that national MRLs more restrictive than Codex standards must be scientifically justified.

## Comparative Analysis

### Hazard vs. Risk Assessment

The most fundamental regulatory divergence is between the EU's hazard-based cut-off criteria and the U.S. risk-based approach. The EU may ban a substance based on its inherent properties (e.g., carcinogenicity classification) regardless of actual human exposure levels. The U.S. considers both hazard and exposure, allowing continued use of hazardous substances if exposure can be managed to "reasonable certainty of no harm." South Korea's approach is risk-based but with precautionary elements (particularly the PLS default MRL).

### Default MRLs

The EU and South Korea both apply a 0.01 mg/kg default MRL for unregistered pesticide-crop combinations, creating effectively zero-tolerance standards for any pesticide not specifically approved for a particular crop. The U.S. does not apply a universal default MRL, establishing substance-specific tolerances instead. The 0.01 mg/kg default has significant trade implications, particularly for developing countries whose pesticide use patterns may not align with EU or Korean registration priorities.

### Pesticide Reduction Policies

The EU's Farm to Fork Strategy set the most ambitious reduction target (50% by 2030), but the SUR's withdrawal demonstrated the political difficulty of implementing mandatory reduction. South Korea and the United States do not have comparable binding reduction targets, though both promote IPM and environmentally friendly agriculture.

### Pollinator Protection

The EU's neonicotinoid ban represents the most significant pollinator protection measure globally. The U.S. has not imposed comparable restrictions, though the EPA has strengthened label requirements for pollinator protection. South Korea has restricted some neonicotinoid uses but has not implemented a comprehensive outdoor ban.
