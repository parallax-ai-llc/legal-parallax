## Summary

Trademark law protects distinctive signs used in commerce to identify the source of goods or services, preventing consumer confusion and safeguarding the goodwill that businesses build through consistent quality and reputation, while balancing these interests against the public's need for a rich vocabulary of commercial expression and fair competition. South Korea's trademark system is governed by the Trademark Act (상표법), administered by the Korean Intellectual Property Office (특허청, KIPO), providing protection through registration for renewable 10-year terms, with the Act comprehensively revised in 2016 to adopt a multi-class application system, strengthen protection for non-traditional marks (sounds, colors, scents, holographic marks), and enhance enforcement against counterfeit goods; South Korea's specialized IP courts and the KIPO trial division (특허심판원) provide efficient adjudication of trademark disputes. The United States protects trademarks under the Lanham Act (15 U.S.C. Sections 1051-1141n), administered by the United States Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO), with trademark rights arising from use in commerce (the common law priority principle) rather than registration alone, though federal registration on the Principal Register provides significant benefits including nationwide constructive notice, prima facie evidence of validity, and the possibility of incontestable status after five years of continuous use; the Supreme Court's decision in Jack Daniel's Properties, Inc. v. VIP Products LLC, 599 U.S. 140 (2023), clarified the interaction between trademark infringement and the First Amendment, and the Trademark Modernization Act of 2020 introduced new mechanisms for canceling unused trademark registrations. Germany protects trademarks through the Markengesetz (MarkenG, Trademark Act), which implements the EU Trade Mark Directive 2015/2436, alongside the EU Trade Mark Regulation (EU) 2017/1001 providing unitary protection through registration at the European Union Intellectual Property Office (EUIPO); Germany's courts, particularly the specialized trademark chambers, and the Bundespatentgericht (Federal Patent Court) provide sophisticated adjudication. The EU has achieved extensive trademark harmonization through the Trade Mark Directive and Regulation, with the CJEU developing detailed jurisprudence on likelihood of confusion, distinctiveness, dilution, and the interface between trademarks and fundamental rights.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's trademark system has undergone significant modernization to align with international standards and address the challenges of a globalized digital marketplace.

**Trademark Act (상표법):** The Act defines a trademark as any sign used to distinguish goods or services of one business from those of others (Article 2). Registrable marks include words, figures, three-dimensional shapes, colors, sounds, scents, holograms, motion marks, and any combination thereof. The 2016 comprehensive revision introduced multi-class applications, reduced the opposition period, and expanded protectable mark types.

### Registration Process

KIPO examines trademark applications for absolute grounds (distinctiveness, descriptiveness, deceptiveness) and relative grounds (likelihood of confusion with prior registrations). The examination process typically takes 10-12 months from filing to registration. Key examination criteria include:

| Ground | Provision | Standard |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| Absolute distinctiveness | Article 33 | Generic, descriptive, and customary marks refused |
| Deceptiveness | Article 34(1)(11) | Marks likely to deceive consumers about quality, origin, or characteristics |
| Public order | Article 34(1)(4) | Marks contrary to public order or morality |
| Likelihood of confusion | Article 34(1)(7) | Similarity to prior registered marks for identical/similar goods |
| Well-known marks | Article 34(1)(9)-(13) | Enhanced protection for well-known and famous marks |

### Well-Known Mark Protection

South Korea provides multi-tiered protection for well-known marks. Article 34(1)(9) refuses registration of marks identical or similar to well-known marks in Korea, even for dissimilar goods where confusion as to source is likely. Article 34(1)(11) refuses marks that are likely to cause confusion with well-known foreign marks. The Supreme Court has developed a comprehensive framework for assessing whether a mark qualifies as "well-known" based on factors including duration and extent of use, geographic scope, advertising expenditure, and degree of public recognition.

### Anti-Counterfeiting Enforcement

South Korea maintains aggressive anti-counterfeiting enforcement through customs border measures (Korea Customs Service trademark recordation system), specialized IP police units, and the Trademark Act's criminal provisions (Articles 93-97), which provide for imprisonment of up to 7 years or fines up to 100 million KRW for trademark counterfeiting. The KIPO Special Judicial Police for Intellectual Property conducts investigations and raids on counterfeit goods operations.

### Korean Wave (한류) and Brand Protection

The global expansion of Korean cultural products (K-pop, K-drama, Korean cuisine, cosmetics) has created unique trademark protection challenges. Korean entertainment companies have adopted aggressive global trademark filing strategies to protect artist names, group names, lightstick designs, and associated merchandise brands. The KIPO has established specialized examination guidelines for entertainment and character marks.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States is unique among major jurisdictions in grounding trademark rights in use rather than registration, reflecting common law tradition.

**Lanham Act (15 U.S.C. Sections 1051-1141n):** Enacted in 1946, the Lanham Act provides for federal trademark registration, enforcement of both registered and unregistered marks, and remedies for unfair competition. The Act distinguishes between the Principal Register (for marks that are distinctive or have acquired distinctiveness) and the Supplemental Register (for descriptive marks that have not yet acquired secondary meaning but may do so).

### Distinctiveness Spectrum

American trademark law classifies marks on a spectrum of distinctiveness:

| Category | Protectability | Example |
|----------|---------------|---------|
| Generic | Never protectable | "Computer" for computers |
| Descriptive | Protectable only with secondary meaning | "Sharp" for televisions |
| Suggestive | Inherently distinctive | "Coppertone" for suntan lotion |
| Arbitrary | Inherently distinctive | "Apple" for computers |
| Fanciful | Inherently distinctive | "Kodak" for cameras |

### Key Recent Developments

**Jack Daniel's Properties, Inc. v. VIP Products LLC, 599 U.S. 140 (2023):** The Supreme Court held that when a mark is used as a source identifier (rather than as expressive commentary), the standard likelihood-of-confusion test applies rather than the more speech-protective Rogers v. Grimaldi test. The Court clarified that the First Amendment does not require departure from traditional trademark analysis when a challenged use functions as a trademark.

**Trademark Modernization Act of 2020 (TMA):** Enacted on December 27, 2020, the TMA introduced two new ex parte procedures for challenging trademark registrations: ex parte expungement (for marks never used in commerce) and ex parte reexamination (for marks not in use at the relevant date). These provisions address the growing problem of "deadwood" registrations and bad-faith filings.

### Dilution (Section 43(c))

The Lanham Act provides federal protection against dilution of famous marks through blurring (impairment of distinctiveness) and tarnishment (harm to reputation), regardless of the absence of likelihood of confusion and regardless of competition between the parties. Following the Trademark Dilution Revision Act of 2006, dilution claims require proof that the defendant's mark is likely to cause dilution, overriding the prior requirement of actual dilution established in Moseley v. V Secret Catalogue, Inc., 537 U.S. 418 (2003).

### Anti-Counterfeiting

The Trademark Counterfeiting Act of 1984 (18 U.S.C. Section 2320) provides criminal penalties of up to 10 years imprisonment and fines up to $2 million for individuals (and $5 million for organizations) for trafficking in counterfeit goods. U.S. Customs and Border Protection (CBP) records over 25,000 seizures of counterfeit goods annually.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany operates a dual trademark system comprising national trademarks (MarkenG) and European Union trade marks (EUTM Regulation).

**Markengesetz (MarkenG):** The German Trademark Act, enacted in 1995 to implement the original EU Trade Mark Directive and substantially amended to implement Directive 2015/2436, provides for registration-based trademark protection administered by the Deutsches Patent- und Markenamt (DPMA).

### Sources of Trademark Rights

German law recognizes three bases for trademark protection (Section 4 MarkenG):

| Source | Provision | Requirements |
|--------|-----------|-------------|
| Registration | Section 4(1) | Registration in the DPMA register |
| Use-based rights (Benutzungsmarke) | Section 4(2) | Acquisition of recognition as a trademark through use in commerce (Verkehrsgeltung) |
| Well-known mark (notorisch bekannte Marke) | Section 4(3) | Well-known mark within the meaning of Paris Convention Article 6bis |

### EU Trade Mark (EUTM)

German businesses frequently seek EU-wide protection through EUTM registration at EUIPO (Alicante). A single EUTM registration provides unitary protection across all 27 EU member states, with priority over subsequently filed national marks. The EUTM system coexists with national trademark systems, and trademark holders may maintain both national and EU registrations.

### Infringement Standards

Section 14 MarkenG defines trademark infringement in three tiers:

**Double Identity (Section 14(2)(1)):** Use of a sign identical to a registered mark for identical goods or services. Infringement is presumed without need to prove likelihood of confusion.

**Likelihood of Confusion (Section 14(2)(2)):** Use of a sign identical or similar to a registered mark for identical or similar goods or services where there exists a likelihood of confusion on the part of the public, including the likelihood of association.

**Dilution/Free-Riding (Section 14(2)(3)):** Use of a sign identical or similar to a mark with a reputation (bekannte Marke) where such use takes unfair advantage of, or is detrimental to, the distinctive character or the repute of the trade mark, regardless of whether the goods or services are similar.

### Enforcement Practice

German trademark litigation is characterized by efficient procedures and specialized courts. The Landgerichte (District Courts) of Hamburg, Munich, Düsseldorf, Cologne, and Frankfurt host specialized trademark chambers. The einstweilige Verfügung (preliminary injunction) procedure provides rapid interim relief, with courts frequently granting injunctions within days or weeks of filing. The Abmahnung (cease-and-desist letter) system provides a pre-litigation enforcement mechanism, though the 2020 reform restricted abusive Abmahnungen.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Trade Mark Directive 2015/2436

Directive (EU) 2015/2436, adopted on December 16, 2015, recast the original Trade Mark Directive 89/104/EEC, modernizing and harmonizing national trademark law across member states.

**Key Innovations:** Elimination of the graphical representation requirement (enabling registration of non-traditional marks through other means of representation); harmonization of administrative procedures for invalidity and revocation; introduction of certification marks; and alignment of national trademark procedures with EUTM practice.

### EUTM Regulation 2017/1001

The European Union Trade Mark Regulation, as amended, provides for unitary trademark protection through registration at EUIPO. Key features include a single application covering all 27 member states, a centralized opposition procedure, a unified cancellation system, and enforcement through national courts designated as EU trade mark courts.

### CJEU Landmark Decisions

**SABEL BV v. Puma AG (Case C-251/95, 1997):** The Court established the framework for assessing likelihood of confusion, holding that it must be assessed globally, taking into account all factors relevant to the circumstances, including the similarity of the marks, the similarity of the goods, and the distinctiveness of the earlier mark.

**L'Oréal SA v. Bellure NV (Case C-487/07, 2009):** The Court held that use of a sign similar to a mark with a reputation that takes unfair advantage of the mark's distinctive character or repute constitutes infringement, even where there is no likelihood of confusion and no detriment to the mark.

**Google France SARL v. Louis Vuitton Malletier SA (Joined Cases C-236/08 to C-238/08, 2010):** The Court addressed the use of trademarks as keywords in internet advertising (AdWords), holding that the advertiser's use of a trademark as a keyword constitutes "use in the course of trade" but that the search engine operator does not itself "use" the trademark within the meaning of the Directive.

**Louboutin v. Van Haren (Case C-163/16, 2018):** The Court held that a mark consisting of a color applied to the sole of a shoe (Christian Louboutin's red sole) does not consist of a "shape" within the meaning of the exclusion from registration, but rather consists of a color applied to a specific part of a product.

### ECHR and Trademarks

In Anheuser-Busch Inc. v. Portugal (Application No. 73049/01, Grand Chamber, 2007), the Court held that a trademark application constitutes a "possession" within the meaning of Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 where the applicant has a legitimate expectation of registration. The Court found no violation where Portugal refused registration based on a prior bilateral treaty obligation, holding that the interference pursued a legitimate aim and was proportionate.

## Comparative Analysis

### Rights Basis

The most fundamental difference across jurisdictions is the basis for trademark rights. The United States uniquely grants priority based on use in commerce rather than registration, meaning that a common law user may have rights superior to a subsequent registrant. South Korea, Germany, and the EU follow the first-to-file principle, granting priority to the first applicant to register. This divergence has significant practical implications for global trademark strategy, requiring businesses to adopt different approaches in use-based versus registration-based jurisdictions.

### Non-Traditional Marks

The scope of registrable mark types has expanded substantially across all jurisdictions. The EU's elimination of the graphical representation requirement in Directive 2015/2436 has been the most significant reform, enabling registration of sound marks through audio files, motion marks through video files, and other non-traditional signs without the need for visual representation. South Korea's 2016 reform similarly expanded non-traditional mark registration. The United States has long recognized non-traditional marks but requires proof of acquired distinctiveness for product design trade dress (Wal-Mart Stores v. Samara Brothers, 529 U.S. 205 (2000)).

### Dilution Protection

All four systems provide protection for famous/reputable marks beyond the likelihood of confusion standard, but with varying scope. The EU and German systems protect marks "with a reputation," a lower threshold than the U.S. "famous mark" requirement. South Korea provides tiered protection for well-known and famous marks. The U.S. dilution standard was clarified by the 2006 Trademark Dilution Revision Act to require only likelihood of dilution rather than actual dilution.

### Counterfeiting Enforcement

All jurisdictions maintain robust anti-counterfeiting frameworks, but enforcement intensity and mechanisms differ. South Korea's specialized IP police and customs enforcement, the U.S. criminal counterfeiting statute and CBP border measures, and the EU's customs regulation on counterfeit goods (Regulation (EU) 608/2013) reflect different institutional approaches to addressing the global counterfeit goods trade.
