## Summary

Refugee and asylum law encompasses the legal frameworks governing international protection for individuals fleeing persecution, armed conflict, and other serious threats, grounded in the 1951 Convention Relating to the Status of Refugees and its 1967 Protocol. South Korea, which enacted Asia's first standalone refugee act in 2013, maintains a notably restrictive system with a recognition rate of only 2.7% against an OECD average of 25%, reflecting persistent tensions between international obligations and domestic political considerations. The United States provides asylum through Section 208 of the Immigration and Nationality Act (INA), derived from the Refugee Act of 1980 which codified the 1951 Convention definition, though the system has undergone dramatic policy oscillations across successive administrations. Germany, which received over one million asylum applications during the 2015-2016 refugee crisis, provides constitutional protection for the politically persecuted under Article 16a of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz) and processes claims through the Federal Office for Migration and Refugees (BAMF) under the Asylum Act (Asylgesetz, AsylG). The European Court of Human Rights has developed extensive jurisprudence on non-refoulement under Article 3 ECHR, while the EU's Common European Asylum System (CEAS) establishes harmonized procedures across Member States, most recently reformed through the New Pact on Migration and Asylum adopted in 2024. Globally, countries including Japan, Brazil, India, Australia, South Africa, and Canada each maintain distinctive approaches to refugee protection that reflect diverse legal traditions, geographic realities, and political contexts.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea acceded to the 1951 Refugee Convention in 1992 and its 1967 Protocol in 1993. The Refugee Act (난민법, Act No. 11298), enacted on 10 February 2012 and effective from 1 July 2013, is Asia's first comprehensive standalone refugee statute.

| Provision | Content |
|-----------|---------|
| **Article 1** | Purpose: to prescribe matters concerning refugee status determination and treatment of refugees in accordance with the 1951 Convention and the 1967 Protocol |
| **Article 2** | Definition: adopts the 1951 Convention definition of a refugee |
| **Article 5** | Application: any foreigner in the Republic of Korea may apply for refugee recognition |
| **Article 8** | Interview: the Minister of Justice must interview applicants and allow legal representation |
| **Article 18** | Recognition: the Minister of Justice shall recognize refugee status if criteria are met |
| **Article 21** | Humanitarian status: persons not meeting refugee criteria may receive humanitarian stay permits if return would pose serious threat |
| **Article 30-31** | Rights of recognized refugees: social security, basic livelihood security, education equal to nationals |
| **Article 44** | Non-refoulement: prohibition of return to a country where life or liberty would be threatened |

**Institutional Framework:** The Ministry of Justice administers the refugee status determination (RSD) process through the Korea Immigration Service. The Refugee Committee (난민위원회), composed of 15 members including legal scholars, lawyers, and civil society representatives, reviews appeals from negative first-instance decisions.

### Key Principles

**Restrictive Recognition Rate:** By the end of 2024, cumulative asylum applications reached 122,095, yet only 1,544 individuals had been granted refugee status since 1994, yielding a recognition rate of just 2.7%. An additional 2,696 applicants received humanitarian stay permits, providing temporary residence without full refugee rights.

**Processing Delays:** The average processing time for asylum claims is 13.4 months, with 18.5% of cases exceeding two years. Advocacy organizations have criticized the system for insufficient numbers of trained asylum officers, inadequate interpretation services, and the absence of an independent appeal body.

**Airport Procedure:** South Korea operates a pre-screening system at ports of entry, where immigration officials may deny entry to asylum seekers who fail an initial screening, confining them to airport transit zones while appeals are processed. This practice has drawn criticism from UNHCR and civil society groups.

**Jeju Island Yemeni Refugee Crisis (2018):** The arrival of approximately 500 Yemeni nationals on Jeju Island in 2018, facilitated by the island's visa-free entry policy, sparked intense public debate and a petition with over 700,000 signatures opposing refugee acceptance. Only 2 of the 484 applicants received refugee status, though 412 received humanitarian stay permits.

### Notable Cases

**Egyptian Refugee Recognition Case (Seoul Administrative Court, 2014):** A landmark ruling in which the court overturned the Ministry of Justice's rejection of an Egyptian national's asylum claim, finding that membership in a political opposition group constituted a valid ground for persecution under the 1951 Convention, and criticizing the Ministry for applying an excessively narrow interpretation.

**Myanmar Nationals Mass Recognition (2021-2022):** Following the February 2021 military coup in Myanmar, South Korea saw a significant increase in humanitarian status grants to Myanmar nationals, though full refugee recognition remained limited, highlighting the preference for discretionary humanitarian protection over Convention-based status.

**Syrian Refugee Resettlement (2015-2016):** In contrast to the large-scale European response, South Korea accepted only a small number of Syrian refugees through a pilot resettlement program coordinated with UNHCR, resettling approximately 30 individuals in 2015-2016. The limited scope of this program reflected both geographic distance and domestic political resistance to refugee admissions.

**Constitutional Court on Immigration Detention (2018, 2014Hun-Ka253):** The Constitutional Court ruled that the indefinite detention of asylum seekers pending removal without judicial review was unconstitutional, requiring legislative reform to ensure that immigration detention is subject to periodic judicial oversight. This decision strengthened procedural protections for asylum seekers, though implementation has been criticized as slow.

## United States

### Federal Framework

The United States acceded to the 1967 Protocol on 1 November 1968, binding itself to the substantive provisions of the 1951 Convention. The Refugee Act of 1980 (Pub. L. 96-212) codified the Convention's refugee definition into domestic law and created the modern asylum system.

| Statute / Provision | Citation | Purpose |
|---------------------|----------|---------|
| Immigration and Nationality Act section 208 | 8 U.S.C. section 1158 | Asylum eligibility and procedures |
| INA section 101(a)(42) | 8 U.S.C. section 1101(a)(42) | Definition of "refugee" |
| INA section 241(b)(3) | 8 U.S.C. section 1231(b)(3) | Withholding of removal (mandatory non-refoulement) |
| Convention Against Torture implementing regulations | 8 C.F.R. section 208.16-18 | Protection from removal to countries where torture is likely |
| INA section 235(b)(1) | 8 U.S.C. section 1225(b)(1) | Expedited removal and credible fear screening |

**Affirmative Asylum:** Applicants not in removal proceedings may file Form I-589 with USCIS within one year of arrival. An asylum officer conducts a non-adversarial interview to determine eligibility.

**Defensive Asylum:** Applicants in removal proceedings before an Immigration Judge raise asylum as a defense against deportation. The proceeding is adversarial, with the applicant bearing the burden of proof.

**Credible Fear Standard:** Under INA section 235(b)(1)(B)(v), individuals subject to expedited removal who express a fear of persecution are screened for a "credible fear of persecution," defined as a "significant possibility" of establishing asylum eligibility -- a lower threshold than the well-founded fear standard required for full asylum.

### State Variations

While immigration law is exclusively federal, state and local governments affect asylum seekers through:

- **Sanctuary policies:** Some jurisdictions limit cooperation with federal immigration enforcement, providing de facto protection for asylum seekers.
- **State-funded legal representation:** New York, California, and several other states fund legal aid programs for asylum seekers, significantly improving grant rates.
- **Public benefits:** State eligibility rules for Medicaid, SNAP, and other programs vary widely, affecting asylum seekers' access to social services during often years-long adjudication periods.

### Notable Cases

***INS v. Cardoza-Fonseca* (480 U.S. 421, 1987):** The Supreme Court held that the "well-founded fear of persecution" standard for asylum requires only a reasonable possibility, not a probability, of persecution, establishing a more generous standard than the "clear probability" required for withholding of removal.

***Matter of Acosta* (19 I&N Dec. 211, BIA 1985):** The Board of Immigration Appeals defined "particular social group" as a group sharing a common, immutable characteristic that members either cannot change or should not be required to change because it is fundamental to their identities. This formulation has shaped asylum law worldwide.

***Sessions v. Dimaya* (584 U.S. 148, 2018) and *Barton v. Barr* (590 U.S. 222, 2020):** These decisions addressed the "particularly serious crime" bar to asylum, illustrating ongoing judicial efforts to define the boundaries of criminal bars to protection.

***East Bay Sanctuary Covenant v. Biden* (993 F.3d 640, 9th Cir. 2021):** Litigation challenging the transit ban rule that barred asylum for individuals who transited through a third country without first applying for protection there, illustrating the ongoing legal battles over asylum restrictions.

***Matter of A-B-* (27 I&N Dec. 316, A.G. 2018) and *Matter of L-E-A-* (27 I&N Dec. 581, A.G. 2019):** These Attorney General certification decisions narrowed the scope of "particular social group" claims, particularly for individuals fleeing domestic violence and gang violence. *Matter of A-B-* held that claims based on membership in a "particular social group" defined by victimization in private criminal activity (such as domestic violence) generally would not qualify for asylum. These decisions were subsequently vacated by Attorney General Garland in June 2021, but the underlying legal questions remain contested.

**Asylum Backlog Crisis:** As of fiscal year 2024, the U.S. asylum system faced a backlog of approximately 3.5 million pending immigration court cases and over 1 million pending affirmative asylum applications before USCIS. Average wait times for an Immigration Court hearing exceeded four years in many jurisdictions, creating prolonged legal uncertainty for applicants and straining the capacity of legal aid organizations, immigration courts, and federal agencies.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's asylum framework combines constitutional, statutory, and EU law. Article 16a(1) of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz, GG) declares: "Persons persecuted on political grounds shall have the right of asylum." This constitutional guarantee makes Germany one of the few countries worldwide to enshrine asylum as a fundamental right.

| Source of Law | Provision | Content |
|---------------|-----------|---------|
| Grundgesetz Art. 16a | Constitutional law | Right to asylum for politically persecuted persons |
| Asylgesetz (AsylG) | Federal statute | Procedural rules for asylum applications |
| Aufenthaltsgesetz (AufenthG) | Federal statute | Residence permits, subsidiary protection, toleration (Duldung) |
| Dublin III Regulation (EU) No 604/2013 | EU law | Determines responsible Member State for asylum claims |
| Qualification Directive 2011/95/EU | EU law | Standards for refugee status and subsidiary protection |
| Asylum Procedures Directive 2013/32/EU | EU law | Common procedures for granting and withdrawing international protection |

**The BAMF Process:** The Federal Office for Migration and Refugees (Bundesamt fuer Migration und Fluechtlinge, BAMF) processes all asylum applications through a structured procedure:

1. **Registration:** Asylum seekers are registered and assigned to reception centers through the EASY distribution system.
2. **Dublin Check:** BAMF first determines whether Germany is responsible under the Dublin III Regulation; if another EU state is responsible, the applicant must be transferred.
3. **Personal Interview:** Applicants attend an in-person interview conducted by a BAMF case worker with an interpreter. The interview is recorded in writing and translated into the applicant's language.
4. **Decision:** BAMF may grant refugee status (section 3 AsylG), subsidiary protection (section 4 AsylG), a national ban on deportation (section 60(5) or (7) AufenthG), or reject the application.

### Key Principles

**Safe Country of Origin (section 29a AsylG):** Applications from nationals of designated safe countries of origin are presumed manifestly unfounded unless the applicant presents evidence overcoming this presumption. Designated safe countries include EU Member States, Ghana, Senegal, and the Western Balkan states (Albania, Bosnia and Herzegovina, Kosovo, North Macedonia, Montenegro, Serbia).

**Safe Third Country (Article 16a(2) GG):** A person entering Germany from an EU Member State or another designated safe third country cannot invoke the constitutional right to asylum, as they could have sought protection in that country. This "safe third country" rule effectively restricts the constitutional right to asylum to persons arriving by air or sea directly from the persecuting state.

**Subsidiary Protection (section 4 AsylG):** Applicants who do not qualify as refugees under the 1951 Convention may receive subsidiary protection if they face a real risk of serious harm, including the death penalty, torture, or serious individual threat from indiscriminate violence in armed conflict.

**Toleration (Duldung, section 60a AufenthG):** Persons whose removal is temporarily impossible may receive a "toleration" status, which suspends deportation without granting a residence permit. The Duldung system has been criticized for creating long-term legal limbo for hundreds of thousands of individuals.

### Notable Cases

**2015-2016 Refugee Crisis:** Germany received over 890,000 asylum seekers in 2015 alone, prompting Chancellor Merkel's "Wir schaffen das" ("We can do it") declaration. BAMF processed over 695,000 decisions in 2016, granting protection in approximately 62% of cases. The crisis led to the EU-Turkey Statement of March 2016 and significant amendments to German asylum law, including the Asylum Packages I (October 2015) and II (March 2016).

**Administrative Court Rulings on Syria:** German administrative courts have issued divergent rulings on whether Syrian military-age men face persecution by the Assad regime, with some courts granting full refugee status based on political opinion imputed by the regime and others limiting protection to subsidiary status. The divergence among the higher administrative courts (OVG/VGH) highlighted the need for the Federal Administrative Court (BVerwG) to provide authoritative guidance on country conditions assessment and the legal standard for imputed political opinion persecution.

**2023-2024 Asylum Reforms:** Germany enacted further asylum reforms in response to rising application numbers, including expanded use of accelerated procedures for applications from safe countries of origin, increased capacity at reception centers, and measures to reduce processing times at BAMF. The "return improvement law" (Rueckfuehrungsverbesserungsgesetz, effective February 2024) expanded police powers for enforcement of removal orders, extended pre-deportation detention from 10 to 28 days, and introduced stricter penalties for those who obstruct deportation.

**Integration and Residence:** Recognized refugees in Germany receive a three-year residence permit (section 25(2) AufenthG), access to integration courses (including 600 hours of German language instruction and 100 hours of orientation), and after five years of residence may apply for a settlement permit (Niederlassungserlaubnis) granting permanent residence, provided they meet language proficiency (B1 level) and self-sufficiency requirements.

## ECHR / EU Framework

### Convention Articles

The ECHR does not contain an express right to asylum, but the European Court of Human Rights has developed extensive protection for refugees and asylum seekers through interpretation of existing Convention rights:

- **Article 3 (Prohibition of Torture and Inhuman or Degrading Treatment):** The absolute prohibition against refoulement under Article 3 is the cornerstone of refugee protection under the ECHR. A state may not remove an individual to a country where there are substantial grounds for believing they would face a real risk of treatment contrary to Article 3.
- **Article 4 of Protocol No. 4 (Prohibition of Collective Expulsion):** Prohibits the collective expulsion of aliens, requiring individualized assessment of each person's circumstances before removal.
- **Article 13 (Right to an Effective Remedy):** Asylum seekers must have access to an effective remedy with automatic suspensive effect to challenge removal decisions that raise arguable claims under Article 3.
- **Article 5 (Right to Liberty and Security):** Regulates the detention of asylum seekers, requiring that detention be lawful, necessary, and proportionate, with access to judicial review.

### Key Case Law

***Soering v. United Kingdom* (App. No. 14038/88, 1989):** The foundational case establishing that Article 3 ECHR prohibits removal of a person to a state where there are substantial grounds for believing they would face a real risk of torture or inhuman or degrading treatment. Though not an asylum case, Soering established the extraterritorial application of Article 3 that underpins refugee protection.

***M.S.S. v. Belgium and Greece* (App. No. 30696/09, 2011):** The Grand Chamber held that Belgium violated Article 3 by transferring an Afghan asylum seeker to Greece under the Dublin Regulation, where deficient asylum procedures and degrading reception conditions amounted to inhuman treatment. Greece also violated Article 3 and Article 13. This landmark ruling effectively suspended Dublin transfers to Greece and established that EU Member States cannot assume other Member States comply with Convention obligations.

***Hirsi Jamaa and Others v. Italy* (App. No. 27765/09, 2012):** The Grand Chamber found that Italy violated Article 3 ECHR and Article 4 of Protocol No. 4 by intercepting migrants at sea and returning them to Libya without individual assessment. The ruling established that the Convention applies extraterritorially when a state exercises effective control over individuals, even on the high seas, and that the prohibition of collective expulsion applies beyond territorial borders.

***Ilias and Ahmed v. Hungary* (App. No. 47287/15, 2019):** The Grand Chamber held that confining asylum seekers in a transit zone at the Hungarian-Serbian border constituted a deprivation of liberty under Article 5, even though the applicants could theoretically leave toward Serbia.

***N.D. and N.T. v. Spain* (App. Nos. 8675/15 and 8697/15, 2020):** The Grand Chamber found no violation of the prohibition of collective expulsion where Sub-Saharan African migrants attempted to scale the fence surrounding the Spanish enclave of Melilla, reasoning that Spain had provided genuine and effective access to legal entry through a nearby border crossing.

### Member State Obligations

**Common European Asylum System (CEAS):** The EU has established a harmonized asylum framework through several instruments:

- **Qualification Directive (2011/95/EU):** Establishes uniform standards for refugee status and subsidiary protection.
- **Asylum Procedures Directive (2013/32/EU):** Sets common procedures for granting and withdrawing international protection.
- **Reception Conditions Directive (2013/33/EU):** Establishes standards for the reception of asylum applicants, including housing, food, health care, and employment access.
- **Dublin III Regulation (EU) No 604/2013:** Determines the Member State responsible for examining an asylum application, generally the state of first entry.
- **New Pact on Migration and Asylum (2024):** Adopted in May 2024, this comprehensive reform overhauls the CEAS, introducing mandatory solidarity mechanisms, a screening regulation at external borders, faster border procedures for unfounded claims, and a crisis regulation for situations of mass arrivals. Member States must transpose the Pact by June 2026.

### EU Asylum and Migration Pact (2024)

The New Pact on Migration and Asylum, adopted by the European Parliament and Council in May 2024, represents the most comprehensive overhaul of the EU asylum system since the CEAS was first established:

**Screening Regulation:** Introduces mandatory screening of all third-country nationals arriving irregularly at EU external borders, including identity checks, health and security screenings, and fingerprinting within the Eurodac database, to be completed within seven days of apprehension.

**Asylum and Migration Management Regulation:** Replaces the Dublin III Regulation with a reformed responsibility-sharing system. The new rules maintain the principle that the Member State of first irregular entry is generally responsible, but introduce a mandatory solidarity mechanism under which all Member States must contribute to burden-sharing through relocation of asylum seekers, financial contributions, or operational support.

**Asylum Procedures Regulation:** Establishes a mandatory border procedure for applicants from countries with recognition rates below 20%, to be completed within 12 weeks. Applicants in the border procedure are deemed not to have entered the territory of the Member State.

**Crisis and Force Majeure Regulation:** Allows Member States facing a mass influx or situations of force majeure to request activation of crisis measures, including extended registration timelines, longer border procedures, and broader application of the safe country of origin concept.

Member States must transpose and implement the Pact's provisions by June 2026.

### Additional Jurisdictions

- **Japan** maintains an extremely low recognition rate (historically under 1%), though reforms to the Immigration Control and Refugee Recognition Act in 2023 created a complementary protection system.
- **Australia** operates a mandatory detention policy for unauthorized maritime arrivals and has implemented offshore processing in Nauru and Papua New Guinea, drawing significant international criticism.
- **Brazil** has been recognized for progressive refugee legislation under Law No. 9,474/1997, which includes a broader definition of refugees encompassing those fleeing "serious and generalized human rights violations."
- **South Africa** is party to both the 1951 Convention and the 1969 OAU Convention, and its Refugees Act 130 of 1998 adopts an expanded refugee definition, though the system faces significant implementation challenges.
- **India** is notably not a party to the 1951 Convention or 1967 Protocol and has no domestic refugee legislation, managing refugees through administrative discretion and ad hoc policies.
- **Canada** operates the Immigration and Refugee Board (IRB) as an independent tribunal and maintains one of the highest resettlement rates globally, with both its in-country asylum system and Government-Assisted Refugees Program.
- **Turkey** hosts the world's largest refugee population (approximately 3.6 million registered Syrians under temporary protection), regulated under the Law on Foreigners and International Protection (No. 6458, 2013), which provides a "temporary protection" regime distinct from individual refugee status determination.
- **Kenya** has hosted large refugee populations at the Dadaab and Kakuma camps for decades, regulated under the Refugees Act of 2006, operating alongside UNHCR in a hybrid management model that has faced persistent challenges in funding, rights protection, and integration.

### Climate Refugees and Emerging Protection Gaps

A growing area of legal debate concerns the protection of persons displaced by climate change, who generally do not meet the 1951 Convention's persecution-based definition. The UN Human Rights Committee's decision in *Teitiota v. New Zealand* (CCPR/C/127/D/2728/2016, 2020) held that while the applicant from Kiribati had not demonstrated an immediate risk sufficient to trigger non-refoulement protections, states may not return individuals to countries where climate change effects create life-threatening conditions. The case established that environmental degradation can, in principle, engage non-refoulement obligations, opening a pathway for future claims while setting a high threshold.

The Global Compact on Refugees (2018), while not legally binding, acknowledges climate change and environmental degradation as drivers of displacement and encourages states to develop protection tools for affected populations. Regional frameworks such as the Kampala Convention (African Union Convention for the Protection and Assistance of Internally Displaced Persons in Africa, 2009) and the Nansen Initiative's Protection Agenda (2015) provide additional normative guidance on cross-border displacement in the context of natural disasters and climate change.

### Resettlement and Complementary Pathways

Beyond territorial asylum, several jurisdictions operate refugee resettlement programs:

| Country | Annual Resettlement Quota (approximate) | Program Type |
|---------|----------------------------------------|-------------|
| United States | 125,000 (FY 2024 ceiling) | Presidential Determination; USRAP |
| Canada | 40,000+ | Government-Assisted Refugees (GAR); Private Sponsorship (PSR); Blended Visa Office-Referred (BVOR) |
| Germany | 5,500 (2024) | Federal resettlement program via BAMF |
| Australia | 13,750 (2023-24) | Humanitarian Programme (offshore component) |
| South Korea | Very limited (pilot programs only) | Ad hoc; UNHCR coordination |
| Japan | ~120-200 (since 2010 pilot) | Third-country resettlement from Asia |

**Complementary pathways** -- legal avenues for refugee protection beyond asylum and resettlement -- include humanitarian visas, family reunification, labor mobility schemes, and community sponsorship programs. Canada's Private Sponsorship of Refugees Program, established in 1979, is the world's oldest and largest community sponsorship model, enabling groups of five or more citizens to sponsor refugees for a minimum of one year. This model has been replicated in the United Kingdom (Community Sponsorship Scheme, 2016), Ireland, Argentina, and New Zealand, with UNHCR's Global Refugee Sponsorship Initiative promoting further expansion.
