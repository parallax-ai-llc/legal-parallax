## Summary

Water rights law governs the allocation, use, and protection of freshwater resources, addressing fundamental questions about whether water is public or private property, how competing uses are prioritized, what limits apply to extraction and consumption, and how environmental flows are maintained -- questions that have become increasingly urgent as climate change, population growth, and economic development intensify pressure on finite water supplies worldwide. South Korea's water law framework operates through the River Act (하천법), the Underground Water Act (지하수법), the Water Supply and Waterworks Installation Act (수도법), and the Water Environment Conservation Act (물환경보전법), administered primarily by the Ministry of Environment (환경부) and the Ministry of Land, Infrastructure and Transport, under a public ownership model where rivers and major water bodies are classified as national or local public property (국가하천/지방하천) and water use requires permits from the relevant authority; the Framework Act on Water Management (물관리기본법), enacted in 2018, consolidated fragmented water governance by designating the Ministry of Environment as the lead ministry for water management and establishing the National Water Management Commission (국가물관리위원회). The United States employs two fundamentally different water rights systems depending on geographic region: the riparian rights doctrine in eastern states (allowing reasonable use by landowners whose property borders a waterway) and the prior appropriation doctrine in western states ("first in time, first in right," permitting diversion of water regardless of land ownership based on priority of beneficial use), with interstate water disputes adjudicated by the Supreme Court under its original jurisdiction and groundwater governed by varying state doctrines. Germany regulates water through the Wasserhaushaltsgesetz (WHG, Federal Water Resources Act), which treats all water bodies as public goods subject to state management, requiring permits (Erlaubnis or Bewilligung) for any use beyond the limited Gemeingebrauch (common use) allowance, with implementation delegated to the 16 federal states (Länder) under their respective state water laws (Landeswassergesetze). The EU provides the most comprehensive supranational water regulation framework through the Water Framework Directive 2000/60/EC, which requires member states to achieve "good status" for all water bodies by establishing river basin management plans, the Groundwater Directive 2006/118/EC, the Environmental Quality Standards Directive 2008/105/EC, and the recently adopted Drinking Water Directive (EU) 2020/2184, while the ECHR has addressed water access as an aspect of the right to private and family life under Article 8.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's water law reflects the country's geographic and climatic characteristics: approximately 65 percent of annual precipitation falls during the monsoon season (June-September), creating seasonal water management challenges, while rapid industrialization and urbanization have intensified demand for reliable water supply.

**Framework Act on Water Management (물관리기본법, 2018):** This landmark Act consolidated South Korea's previously fragmented water governance structure, which had been divided between the Ministry of Land, Infrastructure and Transport (dams, flood control, water supply infrastructure) and the Ministry of Environment (water quality, wastewater treatment). The Act designated the Ministry of Environment as the lead ministry for national water management and established the National Water Management Commission (국가물관리위원회) to coordinate water policy across government.

### Surface Water (River Act)

The River Act (하천법) classifies rivers into national rivers (국가하천) and local rivers (지방하천). All rivers are classified as public property, and the use of river water beyond basic domestic use requires a permit from the relevant river management authority. The Act establishes a permit system for water extraction, diversion, and use, with permits assessed based on available water quantity, impact on downstream users, environmental flow requirements, and the applicant's intended use.

### Groundwater (Underground Water Act)

The Underground Water Act (지하수법) requires permits for groundwater extraction, with exemptions for small-scale domestic use wells below specified depth and yield thresholds. The Act addresses overexploitation through designation of groundwater conservation areas (지하수보전구역), mandatory metering and reporting for permitted wells, groundwater quality monitoring, and restrictions on new well development in areas of depletion.

### Water Quality and Environmental Protection

The Water Environment Conservation Act (물환경보전법) establishes water quality standards, discharge permits for pollutants, and total pollution load management systems for major river basins. The Act implements the "polluter pays" principle through water use charges (물이용부담금) imposed on water users in downstream areas, with revenue used to compensate upstream communities for land use restrictions that protect water quality.

### Dam Management and Multi-Purpose Use

Korea Water Resources Corporation (한국수자원공사, K-water), a government-owned enterprise, manages 37 multi-purpose dams and 12 water supply dams, providing approximately 56 percent of national water supply. K-water also operates the National Water Management Information System (국가수자원관리종합정보시스템), providing real-time data on dam operations, river flows, and water quality.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States employs two fundamentally different water rights systems, a distinction rooted in the climatic and geographic differences between the water-abundant East and the arid West.

### Riparian Rights (Eastern States)

The riparian rights doctrine, derived from English common law, grants landowners whose property adjoins a natural waterway the right to make reasonable use of the water. Key principles include: only riparian landowners (those with property touching the waterway) hold water rights; each riparian owner may make "reasonable use" of the water, assessed relative to the uses of other riparians; water may generally not be transported outside the watershed of origin; and in times of scarcity, all riparian owners must reduce use proportionally.

### Prior Appropriation (Western States)

The prior appropriation doctrine, developed during the western expansion and gold rush era, governs water rights in the 17 western states. Key principles include:

| Principle | Application |
|-----------|------------|
| First in time, first in right | Senior appropriators have priority over junior appropriators |
| Beneficial use | Water must be put to beneficial use (agricultural, industrial, municipal, environmental) |
| Use it or lose it | Rights may be lost through non-use (abandonment or forfeiture) |
| Transferability | Water rights are generally transferable, separate from land ownership |
| No riparian requirement | Rights are not tied to land ownership adjacent to the waterway |

### Groundwater Doctrines

Groundwater law in the United States varies significantly by state:

| Doctrine | States | Principle |
|----------|--------|-----------|
| Absolute ownership | Texas (modified), select others | Landowner may pump unlimited groundwater regardless of impact on neighbors |
| Reasonable use | Most eastern states | Landowner may use groundwater for reasonable purposes on overlying land |
| Correlative rights | California | Overlying landowners share proportionally in times of shortage |
| Prior appropriation | Western states | Priority-based allocation same as surface water |
| Regulated permits | Many states trending toward | State permit required for significant groundwater extraction |

### Colorado River Crisis

The ongoing water crisis in the Colorado River Basin exemplifies the challenges of prior appropriation under climate change. The 1922 Colorado River Compact divided the river's water between the Upper Basin (Wyoming, Colorado, Utah, New Mexico) and Lower Basin (Arizona, Nevada, California) states, allocating a total of 15 million acre-feet per year based on what proved to be an unusually wet period in the river's history. Chronic over-allocation, prolonged drought, and climate change have reduced Lake Mead and Lake Powell to historically low levels, prompting emergency curtailment measures, the 2023 Lower Basin Supplemental Environmental Impact Statement, and ongoing negotiations for post-2026 operating guidelines.

### Federal Water Law

Federal water law overlays state water rights through the Clean Water Act (33 U.S.C. Sections 1251-1387), the Safe Drinking Water Act (42 U.S.C. Sections 300f-300j), the Endangered Species Act (which may require maintenance of minimum flows for protected species), and federal reserved water rights (the Winters doctrine, establishing that the creation of federal reservations impliedly reserves water sufficient for the reservation's purposes).

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany treats all water resources as public goods requiring state management, reflecting the continental European tradition of public water governance.

**Wasserhaushaltsgesetz (WHG, Federal Water Resources Act):** The WHG, originally enacted in 1957 and comprehensively revised in 2009 to implement the EU Water Framework Directive, establishes the federal framework for water management. Article 72(3) of the Grundgesetz grants the federal government concurrent legislative competence for water resource management, while the Länder (federal states) implement and supplement federal law through their Landeswassergesetze (state water laws).

### Water Use Permits

The WHG requires permits for virtually all water uses beyond minimal Gemeingebrauch (common use):

| Permit Type | Duration | Uses |
|-------------|----------|------|
| Erlaubnis (permission, Section 8 WHG) | Revocable; typically limited term | Non-permanent water use; extraction, discharge, diversion |
| Bewilligung (grant, Section 8 WHG) | Fixed term; greater legal security | Long-term water use; significant investment-backed uses |
| Gehobene Erlaubnis (enhanced permission) | Intermediate security | Uses requiring greater certainty but not qualifying for Bewilligung |

**Gemeingebrauch (Common Use, Section 25 WHG):** Limited water uses are permitted without a permit under the Gemeingebrauch provision. Common use typically includes bathing, watering livestock, small-scale domestic garden use, and limited withdrawal for personal needs. The precise scope of Gemeingebrauch is defined by the Landeswassergesetze.

### Water Framework Directive Implementation

Germany implemented the EU WFD through the 2009 WHG revision, establishing 10 river basin districts (Flussgebietseinheiten) covering Germany's entire territory. River basin management plans (Bewirtschaftungspläne) and programs of measures (Maßnahmenprogramme) are prepared on a six-year cycle, with the current cycle covering 2022-2027. Germany has struggled to achieve the WFD's "good status" objectives, particularly for groundwater (nitrate contamination from agriculture) and surface water (morphological degradation).

### Groundwater Protection

The WHG classifies groundwater as a component of the water cycle requiring particular protection. Section 47 WHG establishes the objective of maintaining or achieving good groundwater status, consistent with the WFD and Groundwater Directive. Groundwater extraction requires a permit (Erlaubnis or Bewilligung), and water supply areas are protected through Wasserschutzgebiete (water protection zones) that restrict land use activities posing contamination risks.

### Climate Adaptation

Germany's National Water Strategy (Nationale Wasserstrategie), adopted by the Federal Cabinet on March 15, 2023, addresses long-term water management challenges including climate change impacts on water availability, heat stress on aquatic ecosystems, drought management, and the equitable allocation of water during scarcity. The Strategy envisions a hierarchy of water uses during shortage, with drinking water supply receiving the highest priority, followed by public infrastructure, food production, and other uses.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Water Framework Directive (2000/60/EC)

The Water Framework Directive (WFD), adopted on October 23, 2000, is the cornerstone of EU water policy, establishing a comprehensive framework for the protection and management of all water resources.

**Key Objectives:** Achievement of "good status" for all surface waters and groundwaters; prevention of deterioration of water body status; promotion of sustainable water use; progressive reduction of pollution; mitigation of floods and droughts; and protection of terrestrial and aquatic ecosystems.

**River Basin Management:** The WFD requires member states to manage water resources on the basis of river basin districts rather than administrative or political boundaries, recognizing the hydrological unity of river systems. Member states must prepare river basin management plans (Article 13) and programs of measures (Article 11) on a six-year cycle.

### CJEU Water Law Jurisprudence

**Weser Case (Case C-461/13, 2015):** The Court held that member states must refuse authorization for individual projects that may cause deterioration of the status of a body of water, establishing a strict non-deterioration obligation. "Deterioration" means any deterioration of the status of a water body element by one class, even if the overall status classification does not change.

**Commission v. Germany (Nitrates) (Case C-543/16, 2018):** The Court found that Germany failed to fulfil its obligations under the Nitrates Directive 91/676/EEC by not taking sufficient measures to prevent nitrate pollution of waters from agricultural sources.

### Groundwater Directive (2006/118/EC)

The Groundwater Directive establishes criteria for assessing good groundwater chemical status, criteria for identifying significant upward trends in pollutant concentrations, and measures for preventing or limiting inputs of pollutants into groundwater. Member states must establish threshold values for groundwater pollutants and monitor trends in groundwater quality.

### Drinking Water Directive (EU) 2020/2184

The recast Drinking Water Directive, adopted on December 16, 2020, establishes updated quality standards for water intended for human consumption, requires risk-based approaches to water safety, mandates transparency on water quality through public reporting, and requires member states to improve or maintain access to drinking water for all, particularly vulnerable groups.

### ECHR and Water Access

The ECHR has addressed water-related issues under several Convention provisions:

**Dubetska and Others v. Ukraine (Application No. 30499/03, 2011):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where the state failed to protect the applicants' home environment from industrial water pollution, holding that the state's positive obligation under Article 8 extends to ensuring effective protection against environmental pollution that affects the quality of life and the enjoyment of the home.

**Hudorovič and Others v. Slovenia (Application Nos. 24816/14 and 25140/14, 2020):** The Court addressed the question of access to drinking water and sanitation under Article 8, holding that while there is no explicit right to water under the Convention, the lack of access to safe drinking water and sanitation may engage Article 8 where it affects the enjoyment of private and family life.

## Comparative Analysis

### Water Ownership Models

The jurisdictions reveal three distinct approaches to water ownership. South Korea and Germany treat water as a public good subject to state management and permit-based allocation. The United States employs a hybrid system where eastern states retain elements of private riparian rights while western states treat appropriated water rights as usufructuary property rights that are transferable but subject to state regulation. The EU WFD does not prescribe an ownership model but requires all member states to achieve ecological objectives regardless of the underlying property rights regime.

### Allocation Mechanisms

Water allocation mechanisms range from permit-based administrative systems (South Korea, Germany) to property-rights-based systems (U.S. prior appropriation) to reasonable-use balancing (U.S. riparian rights). The permit-based systems provide greater administrative flexibility in responding to changing conditions but may be less responsive to economic signals. The prior appropriation system provides greater certainty and transferability but creates rigidities that complicate adaptation to drought and climate change. Water markets, most developed in the western United States and Australia, represent an emerging mechanism for reallocation that remains undeveloped in Asian and European contexts.

### Climate Adaptation

All four systems face increasing pressure from climate change, but their adaptation strategies differ. Germany's National Water Strategy represents the most comprehensive proactive planning effort. The United States is grappling with acute climate-driven crises, particularly in the Colorado River Basin. South Korea faces challenges from intensifying monsoon variability and increasing urban water demand. The EU WFD provides a framework for long-term water management but has struggled with implementation timelines and member state compliance.

### Environmental Protection

Environmental flow requirements differ in scope and enforcement. The EU WFD establishes the most ambitious environmental objectives through the "good status" requirement. Germany implements these objectives through river basin management plans but has faced criticism for insufficient enforcement. The U.S. Endangered Species Act provides powerful but species-specific environmental flow protections. South Korea has established ecological flow requirements but faces challenges in enforcement against competing agricultural and industrial demands.
