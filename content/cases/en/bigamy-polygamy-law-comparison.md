## Summary

Bigamy and polygamy -- the practices of entering into a marriage while already legally married (bigamy) or maintaining multiple simultaneous marriages (polygamy) -- remain criminal offenses across most Western legal systems, though enforcement patterns, constitutional challenges, and the interplay with religious freedom and cultural practices create dramatically different legal landscapes across jurisdictions. South Korea criminalizes bigamy under Article 241 of the Criminal Act (형법), which originally prohibited both bigamy and adultery, but underwent a transformative shift when the Constitutional Court (헌법재판소) struck down the adultery provision as unconstitutional in 2015 (Decision 2009헌바17) while leaving the bigamy prohibition intact; Article 241's bigamy provision carries a penalty of up to two years imprisonment, and the Family Registration Act (가족관계의 등록 등에 관한 법률) makes a second marriage legally void. The United States criminalizes bigamy in all 50 states, with the Supreme Court's foundational decision in Reynolds v. United States, 98 U.S. 145 (1878) holding that the Free Exercise Clause of the First Amendment does not protect religiously motivated polygamy, a decision rendered in the context of the federal government's campaign against Mormon polygamy in the Utah Territory; more recently, Brown v. Buhman, 947 F. Supp. 2d 1170 (D. Utah 2013) struck down portions of Utah's anti-bigamy statute as applied to religious cohabitation, though this was subsequently vacated as moot, and the Utah legislature partially decriminalized polygamy in 2020 by reducing it from a felony to an infraction. Germany criminalizes bigamy under Section 172 StGB (Doppelehe/Bigamie), punishing with imprisonment of up to three years any person who enters a marriage while already married or who marries a person they know to be married, and the provision has gained renewed significance in the context of migration from countries where polygamous marriages are legally recognized, prompting legislative debate about the recognition of foreign polygamous marriages. The ECHR has addressed polygamy primarily through Article 12 (right to marry) and Article 8 (respect for private and family life), with the Commission's early decision in Bibi v. United Kingdom (1992) holding that the prohibition of polygamy falls within the state's margin of appreciation, while the European Court has more recently grappled with the immigration and social security implications of polygamous marriages contracted abroad.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

**Article 241 -- Bigamy:** The Criminal Act's Article 241 originally addressed both adultery and bigamy. Following the Constitutional Court's 2015 decision striking down the adultery provision, the bigamy prohibition remains in force.

The provision criminalizes: (1) a married person who enters into another marriage, and (2) an unmarried person who knowingly marries a person they know to be already married. Both parties face imprisonment of up to two years.

**Family Registration Act (가족관계의 등록 등에 관한 법률):** Under this act, a second marriage entered into while the first marriage subsists is legally void (무효). The family court may annul the second marriage upon application by either spouse of the first marriage, the bigamous spouse, or a prosecutor.

### The 2015 Adultery Decriminalization

**Constitutional Court Decision 2009헌바17 (February 26, 2015):** In a landmark 7-2 decision, the Constitutional Court struck down the criminalization of adultery (간통죄) under Article 241, holding that it violated the right to sexual self-determination and the right to privacy. The Court reasoned that criminal punishment was a disproportionate means of protecting marital fidelity in modern society, particularly given changes in gender equality and social attitudes.

**Impact on Bigamy:** The Court's decision specifically preserved the bigamy prohibition, distinguishing between the state's interest in protecting the institution of monogamous marriage (which justifies criminal sanctions) and the state's interest in enforcing sexual fidelity within marriage (which does not).

### Historical Context

Korea's monogamy requirement has roots in both traditional law and modern reform:
- The Joseon Dynasty (조선, 1392-1897) permitted concubinage (첩) but distinguished it from legal marriage
- Japanese colonial law (1910-1945) imposed strict monogamy
- The post-independence Civil Act (민법, 1958) established monogamy as a legal principle
- The 1991 revision of the Civil Act eliminated the family head system (호주제) and strengthened monogamy protections

### Supreme Court Jurisprudence

**Supreme Court Decision 96도1554 (1996):** The Court held that for bigamy liability, the prosecution must prove that the defendant's first marriage was legally valid and subsisting at the time of the second marriage. A void or annulled first marriage does not support a bigamy conviction.

**Supreme Court Decision 2001도3006 (2002):** The Court addressed the temporal element, holding that a person who enters a second marriage after filing for divorce but before the divorce is finalized may be convicted of bigamy.

## United States

### Legal Framework

All 50 states criminalize bigamy, though penalties and enforcement vary enormously:

**Reynolds v. United States, 98 U.S. 145 (1878):** The Supreme Court's foundational polygamy decision upheld the federal Anti-Bigamy Act of 1862 (Morrill Act) against a Free Exercise Clause challenge by George Reynolds, a member of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints. Chief Justice Waite's opinion established the "belief-action" distinction: "Laws are made for the government of actions, and while they cannot interfere with mere religious belief and opinions, they may with practices."

### Modern Developments

**Brown v. Buhman, 947 F. Supp. 2d 1170 (D. Utah 2013):** Federal Judge Clark Waddoups ruled in a case brought by Kody Brown (star of the TLC reality show "Sister Wives") that Utah's anti-bigamy statute, as applied to religious cohabitation (where only one marriage is legally registered), violated the Free Exercise, Due Process, and Equal Protection Clauses. The Tenth Circuit vacated this decision in 2016 as moot after the Browns moved to Nevada.

**Utah SB 102 (2020):** Utah reduced polygamy from a third-degree felony (up to 5 years) to an infraction (comparable to a traffic ticket) with a maximum fine of $750, unless accompanied by other crimes such as fraud, abuse, or human trafficking. This reform reflected recognition that felony penalties drove polygamous communities underground and hindered reporting of abuse.

### Constitutional Issues

**Lawrence v. Texas, 539 U.S. 558 (2003):** While striking down sodomy laws, Justice Kennedy's majority opinion explicitly stated: "The present case does not involve... whether the government must give formal recognition to any relationship that homosexual persons seek to enter." Dissenting justices warned the decision's logic could extend to polygamy.

**Obergefell v. Hodges, 576 U.S. 644 (2015):** The marriage equality decision's emphasis on the "two-person union" suggested the Court was not extending constitutional protection to polygamous marriages. Chief Justice Roberts's dissent argued the majority's reasoning logically supported polygamy.

### Immigration Context

8 U.S.C. Section 1182(a)(10)(A) renders inadmissible any immigrant "coming to the United States to practice polygamy." The Immigration and Nationality Act also prohibits naturalization of persons practicing or advocating polygamy.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

**Section 172 StGB -- Bigamy (Doppelehe):** "Whoever enters into a marriage while already married, or whoever enters into a marriage with a married person, shall be punished with imprisonment for a term not exceeding three years or a fine. The attempt is punishable."

### Elements

**Existing Valid Marriage:** The prosecution must prove that the defendant's first marriage was legally valid under the applicable law (German law or foreign law as determined by private international law rules).

**Conclusion of a Second Marriage:** The defendant must have formally entered into a second marriage -- mere cohabitation does not constitute bigamy.

**Knowledge:** For the knowing new spouse, the prosecution must prove knowledge of the existing marriage. For the already-married person, the fact of the existing marriage is itself the actus reus; no additional mens rea is required.

### Migration and Polygamy

Germany's large immigrant population has created complex legal questions about polygamous marriages:

**Recognition of Foreign Polygamous Marriages:** German private international law (EGBGB -- Einführungsgesetz zum Bürgerlichen Gesetzbuch) generally applies the national law of each spouse to determine the validity of their marriage. However, Article 6 EGBGB (ordre public clause) provides that foreign law shall not be applied if the result would be manifestly incompatible with essential principles of German law. Courts have held that polygamous marriages contracted abroad violate German ordre public and are generally not recognized.

**Social Security and Benefits:** German courts have addressed whether a second wife from a polygamous marriage contracted abroad may receive social security benefits:

**BVerwG, Judgment of January 30, 2013 (10 C 7.12):** The Federal Administrative Court held that social benefits (Sozialleistungen) may not be denied solely because the marriage was polygamous if the marriage was valid under the law of the country where it was contracted.

### Legislative Debate

In 2018, the CDU/CSU parliamentary group proposed legislation explicitly barring recognition of polygamous marriages for all legal purposes. The debate centered on:
- Integration policy considerations
- Protection of women in polygamous marriages
- The distinction between non-recognition and criminalization of existing relationships

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

**Article 12 -- Right to Marry:** "Men and women of marriageable age have the right to marry and to found a family, according to the national laws governing the exercise of this right." The phrase "according to national laws" grants states a wide margin of appreciation in defining marriage.

**Bibi v. United Kingdom (Application No. 19628/92, Commission Decision of June 29, 1992):** The European Commission of Human Rights held that the prohibition of polygamy did not violate Article 12 or Article 8, as the right to marry does not include the right to contract a polygamous marriage where domestic law prohibits it.

**Article 8 -- Family Life:** The Court has recognized that de facto family relationships arising from polygamous marriages may engage Article 8 protections:

**Biao v. Denmark (Application No. 38590/10, Grand Chamber Judgment of May 24, 2016):** While not directly about polygamy, the Grand Chamber addressed family reunification rules that differentially affected persons from countries where polygamy is practiced, finding a violation of Article 14 in conjunction with Article 8.

### Immigration and Polygamy in Europe

Most European states address polygamous marriages through immigration law rather than criminal prosecution:

| Approach | Countries | Key Feature |
|----------|-----------|-------------|
| Non-recognition + deportation | France, Belgium | Second marriages not recognized; family reunification denied |
| Non-recognition + limited rights | UK, Netherlands | Some social welfare rights for additional spouses |
| Criminal prosecution | Germany (Section 172 StGB) | Bigamy prosecutable upon contracting marriage in Germany |
| Administrative penalties | Various | Denial of residence permits for polygamous spouses |

### EU Framework

**Council Directive 2003/86/EC on Family Reunification:** Article 4(4): "In the event of a polygamous marriage, where the sponsor already has a spouse living with him in the territory of a Member State, the Member State concerned shall not authorise the family reunification of a further spouse." This provision effectively prevents the use of EU family reunification rights to facilitate polygamy.

## Comparative Analysis

### Enforcement Patterns

Despite universal criminalization across the studied jurisdictions, enforcement patterns vary dramatically:

- **South Korea:** Rare prosecution; family registration system prevents most bigamous marriages from occurring
- **United States:** Historically aggressive enforcement (especially in Utah); modern trend toward reduced penalties
- **Germany:** Prosecuted when discovered, particularly in immigration context
- **ECHR/EU:** Addressed primarily through immigration controls rather than criminal prosecution

### Religious Freedom

The jurisdictions uniformly reject religious freedom as a defense to bigamy, but the reasoning differs:

- **United States:** Belief-action distinction (Reynolds); laws of general applicability (Employment Division v. Smith)
- **Germany:** Article 4 GG protects religious freedom but Section 172 is a law of general application
- **South Korea:** Constitution protects religious freedom but bigamy prohibition serves compelling interest
- **ECHR:** States have margin of appreciation to define marriage; Article 9 does not extend to polygamy

### Cultural and Immigration Dimensions

The question of recognizing polygamous marriages contracted abroad has become increasingly important:

- **Germany and France:** Most active in addressing the integration dimensions
- **United States:** Immigration law explicitly bars polygamy
- **ECHR:** Provides limited guidance; margin of appreciation for non-recognition
