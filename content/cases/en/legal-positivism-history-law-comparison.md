## Summary

Legal positivism -- the thesis that the existence and content of law depends on social facts rather than on moral merit -- represents one of the most significant developments in Western legal philosophy, yet the underlying insight that law is fundamentally a human creation, separable from moral evaluation, has ancient and cross-cultural roots. The Chinese Legalist school (法家, Fajia), flourishing in the Warring States period (475-221 BCE), produced what may be the earliest systematic positivist legal philosophy, with Han Feizi (韓非子) and Shang Yang (商鞅) arguing that law is the command of the sovereign, that its authority derives from promulgation and enforcement rather than moral content, and that the ruler's fa (法, law) must be clear, public, and uniformly applied regardless of considerations of virtue or social status. In the Western tradition, the explicit separation of law as it is from law as it ought to be emerged gradually from the natural law tradition, finding its first systematic articulation in Jeremy Bentham's (1748-1832) utilitarian critique of William Blackstone and his distinction between expositional and censorial jurisprudence. John Austin (1790-1859) crystallized the command theory in *The Province of Jurisprudence Determined* (1832), defining law as the command of the sovereign backed by the threat of sanction. Hans Kelsen (1881-1973) developed the "pure theory of law" (*Reine Rechtslehre*, 1934/1960), seeking to establish jurisprudence as a science free from both natural law metaphysics and sociological reductionism, grounding legal validity in a hypothetical basic norm (Grundnorm). H.L.A. Hart (1907-1992), in *The Concept of Law* (1961), offered the most sophisticated positivist account, replacing Austin's command theory with a rule-based analysis distinguishing primary rules of obligation from secondary rules of recognition, change, and adjudication. This document traces the positivist impulse across legal civilizations, examining its parallels in Islamic jurisprudence (the Ash'arite divine command tradition), medieval European voluntarism, and the modern codification movements, while exploring the fundamental debate over whether law and morality are conceptually separable.

## Ancient Greek and Roman Foundations

### Early Positivist Currents

While ancient Greek and Roman thought is predominantly associated with natural law, positivist currents existed from the earliest period. The Sophists, particularly Thrasymachus as portrayed in Plato's *Republic* (I.338c), argued that "justice is nothing other than the advantage of the stronger" -- that law reflects the interests of those in power rather than any objective moral order. Callicles, in Plato's *Gorgias* (482c-486d), similarly argued that conventional law (nomos) is an artificial constraint imposed by the weak majority to restrain the naturally superior.

The Epicurean tradition offered a more nuanced proto-positivism. Epicurus (341-270 BCE) argued that justice is nothing more than a social contract: "Natural justice is a pledge of mutual advantage, to prevent one from harming or being harmed" (Principal Doctrines, XXXI-XXXIII). Law, on this view, is a human convention that derives its validity from its utility, not from nature. Where laws cease to be useful, they cease to be just.

### Roman Legal Science

Roman jurists developed a sophisticated practical legal science that, while nominally incorporating natural law categories, was in practice highly positivist in its methodology. The ius civile -- the law peculiar to the Roman people -- was understood as a human creation, the product of legislation (leges), senatorial decrees (senatus consulta), imperial constitutions (constitutiones), magisterial edicts (edicta), and juristic opinion (responsa prudentium). Gaius's systematic classification of legal sources in his *Institutes* (c. 161 CE) provided a descriptive, non-moralized account of what counted as law in Rome.

The concept of auctoritas (authority) in Roman constitutional thought also contained positivist elements. The validity of law depended on its proper enactment by an authorized body, not on its conformity to any higher moral standard. While Roman jurists like Papinian could declare that a law contrary to good morals (contra bonos mores) was void, this was the exception rather than the rule in practical jurisprudence.

## East Asian Legal Thought

### The Legalist School (法家)

Chinese Legalism represents the most fully developed pre-modern legal positivism anywhere in the world. Emerging during the Warring States period (475-221 BCE) as a direct challenge to Confucian governance through virtue and ritual, the Legalists argued that effective government required clear, publicly promulgated laws (fa 法) backed by severe and certain punishments.

**Shang Yang (商鞅, c. 390-338 BCE)**, chief minister of the state of Qin, articulated the foundational Legalist principles in the *Book of Lord Shang* (*Shangjun Shu* 商君書). Shang Yang insisted on several proto-positivist principles: (1) law must be written, public, and accessible to all; (2) law must apply equally to all persons regardless of social rank; (3) law derives its authority from the sovereign's command, not from Confucian moral teachings; (4) severe and certain punishment is essential for legal efficacy; and (5) the content of law should be determined by what makes the state powerful, not by what traditional morality dictates.

**Shen Buhai (申不害, c. 400-337 BCE)** contributed the concept of shu (術, techniques or methods of governance), arguing that the ruler must employ administrative techniques to control officials and ensure that laws are properly executed. This emphasis on bureaucratic procedure and institutional design anticipates modern concerns with the rule of law as institutional practice.

**Han Feizi (韓非子, c. 280-233 BCE)** produced the most philosophically sophisticated Legalist synthesis, combining Shang Yang's emphasis on fa (法, law), Shen Buhai's emphasis on shu (術, technique), and Shen Dao's (慎到) emphasis on shi (勢, positional power or authority). In the *Han Feizi* text, he argued:

- Law must be based on the actual conditions of the present, not on the idealized models of ancient sage-kings (a direct attack on Confucian appeals to antiquity).
- Human nature is fundamentally self-interested (a parallel to Hobbes's later anthropology), making moral governance futile and legal governance necessary.
- The ruler must rely on objective, impersonal standards (fa) rather than on personal virtue (de 德) or relationships (ren 仁).
- Rewards and punishments are the "two handles" (er bing 二柄) by which the ruler controls his subjects.

Han Feizi's system shares remarkable structural parallels with Austin's command theory: law is defined by its source (the sovereign), its form (a general command), and its sanction (punishment for noncompliance). The key difference is that Han Feizi situated his theory within a broader framework of political strategy (statecraft), whereas Austin presented his as a purely analytical jurisprudential project.

### The Qin Unification and Its Legacy

The Legalist philosophy was implemented most thoroughly during the Qin Dynasty (221-206 BCE) under the First Emperor, Qin Shi Huang, with Li Si (李斯, c. 280-208 BCE) as chief minister. The Qin state promulgated detailed legal codes, standardized weights and measures, and -- most notoriously -- ordered the burning of Confucian texts and the execution of scholars (焚書坑儒). The harshness and ultimate collapse of the Qin Dynasty discredited pure Legalism in Chinese political thought, but its institutional innovations -- centralized bureaucracy, written legal codes, uniform administration -- were quietly retained by the succeeding Han Dynasty (206 BCE-220 CE) under a Confucian ideological veneer.

This pattern -- Confucian rhetoric (外儒, wairu) concealing Legalist practice (内法, neifa) -- characterized Chinese governance for over two millennia and represents one of the most striking examples of the tension between natural law idealism and positivist pragmatism in world legal history.

### Japanese Reception

The Legalist-Confucian synthesis influenced Japanese legal development through the *Ritsuryō* (律令) system, adopted during the Taika Reforms (645 CE) and codified in the *Taihō Code* (701 CE) and *Yōrō Code* (718 CE). The ritsu (律, penal law) and ryō (令, administrative law) were modeled on Tang Dynasty Chinese codes, which themselves reflected the Legalist-Confucian synthesis. The later Tokugawa period (1603-1868) saw an explicit revival of Legalist-influenced governance through the extensive *buke shohatto* (武家諸法度, Laws for the Military Houses) and other detailed regulatory codes.

## Islamic Jurisprudence

### The Ash'arite Divine Command Theory

Islamic jurisprudence contains a powerful voluntarist tradition that parallels legal positivism in crucial respects. The Ash'arite school of theology, founded by Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari (873-935) and developed by Al-Ghazali (1058-1111), held that moral values are entirely dependent on God's command. Nothing is inherently good or bad; things become obligatory, permissible, or forbidden solely because God has so decreed. This divine command theory (voluntarism) mirrors positivism's insistence that the validity of law depends on its source (the sovereign's command), not on its moral content.

Al-Ghazali, in *Al-Iqtisad fi al-I'tiqad* (Moderation in Belief) and *Al-Mustasfa min 'Ilm al-Usul* (The Essentials of Legal Theory), argued that reason alone cannot determine moral obligation. Without revelation, human reason can identify what is beneficial or harmful, but it cannot establish that something is morally obligatory or prohibited. Only God's command creates true moral and legal obligation. This position places Al-Ghazali closer to Austin's command theory than to natural law theory, though with a divine rather than human sovereign.

### The Zahiri School

Ibn Hazm (994-1064), the great Andalusian jurist of the Zahiri (literalist) school, developed what may be the most explicitly positivist methodology within Islamic jurisprudence. In his monumental *Al-Muhalla* (The Adorned), Ibn Hazm insisted on strict adherence to the apparent (zahir) meaning of the Quran and Hadith, rejecting analogical reasoning (qiyas), juristic preference (istihsan), and all forms of interpretive discretion. For Ibn Hazm, legal rules are to be found in the text of the divine command, not derived from rational speculation about purposes or principles. This textual positivism -- the insistence that law is what the authoritative text says, not what reason or equity might suggest -- parallels the most austere forms of Western legal positivism.

### Codification in the Ottoman Period

The Ottoman *Mecelle* (*Majallat al-Ahkam al-'Adliyya*, 1869-1876), drafted under the direction of Ahmed Cevdet Pasha, represented a landmark positivist project within the Islamic legal tradition. For the first time, rules of Islamic commercial and civil law were extracted from the Hanafi school's juristic literature and codified into a systematic code of 1,851 articles organized along European lines. The Mecelle acknowledged its sources in traditional fiqh but its form was distinctly positivist: law as enacted text rather than living scholarly tradition.

## Medieval European Tradition

### Voluntarism versus Intellectualism

The medieval debate between voluntarism and intellectualism directly prefigured the modern positivism-natural law controversy. The intellectualist position, represented by Aquinas, held that God commands what is good because it is good -- moral truths are grounded in the rational nature of things, and God's will follows His intellect. The voluntarist position, developed by Duns Scotus (c. 1265-1308) and William of Ockham (c. 1287-1347), held that moral obligation derives from God's will alone. Ockham, in his *Quodlibeta Septem*, argued that God could have commanded the opposite of the Decalogue, and it would then have been obligatory. This voluntarism removed the rational constraint on the lawgiver's will -- a crucial step toward the positivist conception of law as the product of sovereign will.

### Marsilius of Padua

Marsilius of Padua (c. 1275-1342), in *Defensor Pacis* (1324), offered one of the most explicitly positivist theories of law in medieval thought. Marsilius defined law as a coercive command issued by the human legislator (the people or their representatives) and enforced by the ruler. He rejected the identification of law with justice or reason, arguing that what makes a rule a law is not its conformity to moral principles but its enactment by legitimate authority and its backing by coercive sanction. Marsilius's theory anticipates Austin's command theory by five centuries.

### The Euthyphro Dilemma in Medieval Dress

The voluntarism-intellectualism debate recapitulated, in theological terms, the dilemma first posed by Plato in the *Euthyphro* (10a): Is something pious because the gods love it, or do the gods love it because it is pious? Applied to law: Does the sovereign's command create legal obligation, or does the sovereign command what is already obligatory by reason? The voluntarist answer -- command creates obligation -- is the essence of legal positivism. The intellectualist answer -- obligation exists independently of command -- is the essence of natural law. This structural parallel demonstrates that the positivism-natural law debate is not merely a modern Western phenomenon but a recurrent philosophical problem arising wherever legal authority is exercised.

### Late Medieval Legal Science

The post-glossator (commentator) tradition in medieval Roman law, represented by Bartolus de Saxoferrato (1313-1357) and Baldus de Ubaldis (1327-1400), developed an increasingly positivist methodology. While nominally acknowledging natural law principles, these jurists focused overwhelmingly on the interpretation of authoritative texts -- the Justinianic *Corpus Iuris Civilis* -- and on the reconciliation of conflicting textual authorities. Their methodology was fundamentally one of textual positivism: law is what the authoritative texts say, and the jurist's task is interpretation and systematization rather than moral evaluation.

## Early Modern and Enlightenment Development

### Hobbes as Proto-Positivist

Thomas Hobbes (1588-1679) provides the crucial bridge between pre-modern voluntarism and modern legal positivism. In *Leviathan* (1651), Hobbes argued that in the state of nature there is no law, no justice, and no property -- only the war of all against all. Law comes into existence only through the sovereign's command, and its validity depends entirely on sovereign authority, not on its moral content. "Auctoritas, non veritas, facit legem" -- "Authority, not truth, makes law" (*Leviathan*, Chapter 26). While Hobbes retained a minimal natural law framework (the laws of nature as theorems of reason directing toward peace), his practical conclusion was thoroughly positivist: the sovereign's command is the measure of right and wrong within the commonwealth.

### Bentham's Expositional Jurisprudence

Jeremy Bentham (1748-1832) launched the modern positivist tradition with his withering critique of Blackstone's natural law assumptions. In *A Fragment on Government* (1776), Bentham attacked Blackstone's assertion that no human law contrary to natural law could be valid, arguing that this confused what law is with what law ought to be. In the posthumously published *Of Laws in General* (composed c. 1782), Bentham developed a systematic analysis of law as the expression of the sovereign's will, distinguishing between "expositional jurisprudence" (describing the law as it is) and "censorial jurisprudence" (evaluating the law as it ought to be by the standard of utility). Bentham's denunciation of natural rights as "nonsense upon stilts" (*Anarchical Fallacies*, 1796) remains the most memorable positivist salvo against the natural law tradition.

### Austin's Command Theory

John Austin (1790-1859) systematized Bentham's insights into the first comprehensive positivist jurisprudence in *The Province of Jurisprudence Determined* (1832). Austin defined law (properly so called) as: (1) a command (2) issued by a sovereign (3) to a person or persons in a state of subjection (4) backed by the threat of a sanction. The sovereign is defined as a determinate human superior who receives habitual obedience from the bulk of a given society but does not habitually obey any other human superior. Austin sharply distinguished positive law from positive morality, divine law, and customary rules, arguing that only sovereign commands constitute law in the strict sense.

### Kelsen's Pure Theory

Hans Kelsen (1881-1973) developed the most rigorous and systematic positivist legal theory in the twentieth century. In *Reine Rechtslehre* (Pure Theory of Law, 1934; 2nd ed. 1960), Kelsen sought to establish a science of law purified of all political, sociological, and moral considerations. For Kelsen, law is a system of norms (Sollen, "ought" propositions) hierarchically organized: each norm derives its validity from a higher norm that authorizes its creation, tracing upward to a hypothetical basic norm (Grundnorm) that is presupposed, not enacted. Kelsen rejected both natural law (as confusing law and morality) and Austin's command theory (as reducing law to facts of power), insisting that the normativity of law -- its "ought" character -- must be taken seriously as an irreducible category.

### Hart's Rule-Based Positivism

H.L.A. Hart (1907-1992) transformed legal positivism in *The Concept of Law* (1961), replacing Austin's command model with a sophisticated analysis of law as a system of social rules. Hart distinguished between primary rules (rules imposing duties on citizens) and secondary rules (rules empowering officials to create, modify, and apply primary rules). The most important secondary rule is the rule of recognition -- a social practice among officials that identifies the criteria for legal validity. Hart's positivism made three central claims: (1) the separability thesis (law and morality are conceptually distinct); (2) the social fact thesis (legal validity is determined by social facts); and (3) the discretion thesis (in "hard cases" where rules are indeterminate, judges exercise discretion). The Hart-Fuller debate (1958) and the Hart-Dworkin debate (1967-1986) remain the defining controversies of modern analytical jurisprudence.

## Common Law Tradition

### Positivist Strands in Common Law

The English common law, despite its deep natural law roots, also harbors significant positivist elements. The doctrine of parliamentary sovereignty -- that Parliament can enact any law whatsoever and no court may declare an Act of Parliament invalid -- is a quintessentially positivist principle. A.V. Dicey (1835-1922), in *Introduction to the Study of the Law of the Constitution* (1885), articulated this doctrine in its classic form: "Parliament has the right to make or unmake any law whatever; and no person or body is recognized by the law as having a right to override or set aside the legislation of Parliament."

### Austin and the Common Law

Austin's command theory was, ironically, poorly suited to explain the common law itself. Austin struggled to account for customary law and judge-made law within his framework, ultimately arguing that custom becomes law only when recognized by the sovereign (tacitly, through non-interference with judicial enforcement). This awkward accommodation revealed a fundamental tension: legal positivism arose largely within the common law tradition, yet the common law -- with its reliance on custom, precedent, and judicial reasoning rather than sovereign command -- seems to resist positivist analysis.

### Hart's Resolution

Hart's rule-based positivism provided a much more satisfactory account of common law. On Hart's analysis, the common law is law because it satisfies the criteria of the rule of recognition -- that is, because English legal officials in practice treat judicial precedent as a source of law. The validity of a common law rule depends not on its being commanded by a sovereign but on its satisfying the social practice of recognition that constitutes the legal system's ultimate criterion of validity.

### Contemporary Developments

Joseph Raz (b. 1939), in *The Authority of Law* (1979) and *The Morality of Freedom* (1986), developed "exclusive legal positivism," arguing that the identification of law can never depend on moral considerations. This "sources thesis" holds that all law is source-based: its existence and content can be determined by reference to social facts alone, without resort to moral argument. By contrast, Jules Coleman and W.J. Waluchow developed "inclusive legal positivism," allowing that a legal system's rule of recognition may incorporate moral criteria as conditions of legal validity.

## Historical Significance and Modern Legacy

Legal positivism has profoundly shaped modern legal systems and legal thought in several ways:

**Codification Movements:** The positivist emphasis on law as enacted text encouraged the great codification movements of the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries. The Prussian *Allgemeines Landrecht* (1794), the French *Code Civil* (1804), and the German *Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch* (1900) all reflect the positivist aspiration to reduce law to a comprehensive, systematically organized body of written rules.

**Separation of Powers:** The positivist distinction between making law and applying law reinforced the constitutional principle of separation of powers, clearly delineating the legislative, executive, and judicial functions.

**Legal Education and Scholarship:** Positivism transformed legal education from a practice-oriented apprenticeship into an academic discipline focused on the systematic analysis of legal concepts, rules, and institutions.

**Critiques and Limitations:** The experience of totalitarianism in the twentieth century -- where formally valid legal systems authorized genocide and systematic human rights violations -- posed the most serious challenge to legal positivism. While positivists like Hart argued that one could condemn Nazi law as morally iniquitous while still recognizing it as law, critics contended that this sterile recognition stripped jurisprudence of its critical function.

**The Ongoing Debate:** The positivism-natural law debate remains the central axis of jurisprudential inquiry. Contemporary legal positivists (Raz, Shapiro, Marmor) and natural lawyers (Finnis, George, Murphy) continue to refine and develop their respective positions, while new approaches -- legal realism, critical legal studies, feminist jurisprudence, law and economics -- have complicated the traditional binary.

**Legal Realism as Post-Positivist Development:** American Legal Realism, developed by Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr. (1841-1935), Karl Llewellyn (1893-1962), and Jerome Frank (1889-1957), pushed beyond classical positivism by arguing that law in action often diverges from law in books. Holmes's famous aphorism -- "The prophecies of what the courts will do in fact, and nothing more pretentious, are what I mean by the law" (*The Path of the Law*, 1897) -- represents a radical empiricist extension of positivism that collapses the distinction between legal norms and social facts. The Scandinavian Legal Realists (Axel Hägerström, Alf Ross, Karl Olivecrona) similarly argued that legal concepts like "rights" and "obligations" are metaphysical fictions; what exists are patterns of judicial behavior and the psychological attitudes of legal officials. Ross, in *On Law and Justice* (1958), proposed a predictive theory of legal validity that synthesized Kelsenian normativism with empirical observation.

**The Challenge of International Law:** Legal positivism faces distinctive challenges in the domain of international law, where no sovereign in Austin's sense exists, no Grundnorm in Kelsen's sense commands universal presupposition, and no rule of recognition in Hart's sense is shared by all states. Kelsen himself addressed this problem by positing the principle *pacta sunt servanda* (agreements must be kept) as the Grundnorm of the international legal order. The persistence of natural law reasoning in international human rights law, international humanitarian law, and the jurisprudence of jus cogens (peremptory norms) suggests that positivism may be unable to provide a complete account of the legal order in the absence of a centralized sovereign authority.
