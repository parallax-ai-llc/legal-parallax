## Summary

Indigenous rights -- the collective and individual rights of indigenous peoples to maintain their cultures, identities, languages, and traditional lands, and to participate in decisions affecting their lives and territories -- have gained increasing recognition in international and domestic law, though the degree of legal protection varies enormously depending on the historical relationship between indigenous communities and the state. South Korea presents a unique case as a relatively ethnically homogeneous nation without a significant indigenous population in the conventional sense, though the Jeju islanders (제주도민) have a distinct cultural identity and the question of minority rights arises in relation to mixed-heritage communities (다문화가정) and the treatment of ethnic Korean minorities from China (조선족); the Framework Act on the Treatment of Foreigners Residing in the Republic of Korea (재한외국인 처우 기본법) and the Multicultural Families Support Act (다문화가족지원법) address some aspects of cultural minority protection. The United States has the most complex indigenous rights framework, rooted in the unique sovereign-to-sovereign relationship between the federal government and 574 federally recognized tribal nations, with the legal doctrine of tribal sovereignty, the trust responsibility, and the Indian Commerce Clause (Article I, Section 8, Clause 3) forming the constitutional foundation; key statutes include the Indian Reorganization Act (1934), the Indian Civil Rights Act (1968), the Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act (1975), and the Indian Child Welfare Act (1978), while the Supreme Court's McGirt v. Oklahoma (2020) decision reaffirmed the continuing existence of Native American reservations. Germany does not have indigenous peoples within its borders but has engaged with indigenous rights through its development policy, reparations for colonial-era atrocities (particularly the 2021 recognition of the Herero and Nama genocide), and the implementation of EU and international standards. The European and international framework is anchored by the UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP, 2007), ILO Convention 169 (1989), and the European Court of Human Rights jurisprudence addressing the rights of the Sámi people and other indigenous groups, while the EU has integrated indigenous rights into its trade, development, and environmental policies.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea does not have indigenous peoples in the international legal sense as defined by ILO Convention 169 or UNDRIP. The Korean population is predominantly ethnically homogeneous, though this is rapidly changing due to immigration and international marriage. The primary relevant legal frameworks address cultural minorities and multicultural families.

### Jeju Special Self-Governing Province (제주특별자치도)

The Jeju Special Self-Governing Province, established by the Special Act on the Establishment of Jeju Special Self-Governing Province (제주특별자치도 설치 및 국제자유도시 조성을 위한 특별법) in 2006, represents the closest analogue to indigenous self-governance in South Korea. Jeju has a distinct cultural identity, dialect, and historical experience, including the Jeju April 3rd Incident (제주 4.3 사건) of 1948, in which an estimated 25,000-30,000 civilians were killed during an anti-communist suppression campaign. The Special Act on Discovering the Truth about the Jeju 4.3 Incident and the Restoration of Honor of Victims (제주4.3사건 진상규명 및 희생자명예회복에 관한 특별법), enacted in 2000, established a truth commission and reparations program.

### Multicultural Families (다문화가정)

The Multicultural Families Support Act (다문화가족지원법), enacted in 2008, provides support services for families formed through international marriage, including language education, cultural adaptation programs, and anti-discrimination measures. The Framework Act on the Treatment of Foreigners Residing in the Republic of Korea (재한외국인 처우 기본법, 2007) establishes basic principles for the treatment of foreign residents, including protection against discrimination.

### Ethnic Korean Minorities (조선족)

Ethnic Koreans from China (조선족, Joseonjok) represent a significant minority community in South Korea, numbering approximately 700,000. While they share ethnic Korean heritage, they face discrimination in employment, social services, and legal status. The Act on the Immigration and Legal Status of Overseas Koreans (재외동포의 출입국과 법적 지위에 관한 법률) provides a framework for overseas Korean communities but has been criticized for differential treatment between overseas Koreans from developed versus developing countries.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The relationship between the federal government and Native American tribes is rooted in the Constitution, treaties, federal statutes, and Supreme Court decisions. The Indian Commerce Clause (Article I, Section 8, Clause 3) grants Congress the power to "regulate Commerce with foreign Nations, and among the several States, and with the Indian Tribes," establishing tribes as distinct political entities.

### Tribal Sovereignty Doctrine

The Supreme Court's "Marshall Trilogy" established the foundational principles of federal Indian law:

| Case | Year | Principle |
|------|------|-----------|
| Johnson v. M'Intosh | 1823 | Discovery doctrine; tribes have right of occupancy |
| Cherokee Nation v. Georgia | 1831 | Tribes as "domestic dependent nations" |
| Worcester v. Georgia | 1832 | State laws have no force within Indian territory |

Tribal sovereignty means that tribes possess inherent powers of self-governance, including the authority to establish their own form of government, determine tribal membership, administer justice through tribal courts, regulate domestic relations, and manage tribal lands and resources.

### Key Federal Statutes

**Indian Reorganization Act (IRA, 1934):** Ended the disastrous allotment policy of the Dawes Act (1887) and encouraged tribal self-governance and the adoption of tribal constitutions.

**Indian Civil Rights Act (ICRA, 1968):** Extended most Bill of Rights protections to tribal members in dealings with tribal governments.

**Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act (ISDEAA, 1975):** Authorized tribes to contract with the federal government to administer programs previously managed by the Bureau of Indian Affairs.

**Indian Child Welfare Act (ICWA, 1978):** Established federal standards for the removal and placement of Native American children in foster or adoptive homes, giving tribal courts jurisdiction over child welfare proceedings and establishing placement preferences for Native American families. The Supreme Court upheld ICWA's constitutionality in Haaland v. Brackeen, 599 U.S. 255 (2023).

**Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act (NAGPRA, 1990):** Requires federal agencies and institutions receiving federal funding to repatriate human remains, funerary objects, sacred objects, and objects of cultural patrimony to affiliated tribes.

### McGirt v. Oklahoma, 591 U.S. 894 (2020)

The Supreme Court held 5-4 that Congress had never disestablished the Muscogee (Creek) Nation's reservation in Oklahoma, meaning that much of eastern Oklahoma remains "Indian country" for purposes of federal criminal law. The decision had significant implications for criminal jurisdiction, tribal authority, and the recognition of treaty-based rights.

## Germany

### Colonial History and Reparations

Germany does not have indigenous peoples within its current borders but has engaged with indigenous rights through its colonial history. Germany colonized parts of Africa (including German South West Africa, now Namibia; German East Africa, now Tanzania; and others) and the Pacific from 1884 to 1919.

### Herero and Nama Genocide Recognition (2021)

On May 28, 2021, Germany officially recognized the 1904-1908 massacres of the Herero and Nama peoples in German South West Africa as genocide. Germany committed to providing 1.1 billion euros over 30 years for development projects in affected communities in Namibia. The agreement was negotiated between the German and Namibian governments, though Herero and Nama representatives criticized their exclusion from negotiations and the characterization of the payments as "development aid" rather than reparations.

### Development Policy

Germany's Federal Ministry for Economic Cooperation and Development (BMZ) has adopted guidelines on indigenous peoples' rights in development cooperation, committing to respecting FPIC, supporting indigenous land rights, and ensuring that development projects do not adversely affect indigenous communities. Germany's position in international forums has generally supported the recognition of indigenous rights, including voting in favor of UNDRIP in 2007.

### Colonial-Era Artifacts

German museums hold significant collections of artifacts acquired during the colonial period, including the Benin Bronzes. In 2022, Germany transferred ownership of over 1,100 Benin Bronzes to Nigeria, one of the largest repatriations of colonial-era artifacts in history. The German Museum Association's guidelines on handling collections from colonial contexts (2018, revised 2021) provide a framework for identifying, researching, and potentially repatriating colonial-era holdings.

## ECHR & EU / International

### UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP, 2007)

UNDRIP, adopted by the UN General Assembly on September 13, 2007, with 144 states voting in favor (the United States, Canada, Australia, and New Zealand initially voted against but subsequently endorsed it), establishes comprehensive standards including:

- Right to self-determination (Articles 3-4)
- Right to nationality and to belong to an indigenous community (Article 6, 9)
- Right to practice and revitalize cultural traditions (Article 11)
- Right to traditional lands, territories, and resources (Articles 25-27)
- Free, prior, and informed consent (FPIC) before adoption of legislation or administrative measures affecting indigenous peoples (Article 19)
- Right to redress for lands, territories, and resources confiscated or taken (Article 28)

### ILO Convention 169 (1989)

ILO Convention 169 on Indigenous and Tribal Peoples, adopted in 1989, is the only binding international treaty specifically addressing indigenous rights. Key provisions include the right of consultation and participation in decision-making (Article 6), the right to determine priorities for development (Article 7), and protection of land rights (Articles 13-19). As of 2026, 24 states have ratified the Convention. Neither South Korea, the United States, nor Germany has ratified it.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

The European Court of Human Rights has addressed indigenous rights primarily in the context of the Sámi people in Nordic countries:

**Handölsdalen Sami Village and Others v. Sweden (Application No. 39013/04, 2010):** The Court found a violation of Article 6(1) (fair trial) due to excessive litigation costs imposed on a Sámi village seeking to establish reindeer grazing rights, holding that the financial burden constituted an impairment of access to court.

**Johtti Sapmelazzat ry and Others v. Finland (Application No. 42969/98, admissibility, 2005):** Addressed Sámi representation and voting rights in the Sámi Parliament.

The Court has also addressed Roma rights extensively, recognizing the Roma as a minority requiring special protection in cases such as D.H. and Others v. Czech Republic (2007) and Oršuš and Others v. Croatia (2010).

### EU Framework

The EU has integrated indigenous rights into multiple policy areas:

- **Trade Policy:** Free trade agreements (FTAs) include sustainability chapters addressing indigenous rights, including the EU-Canada CETA and the EU-New Zealand FTA
- **Environmental Policy:** The EU Deforestation Regulation (2023) requires companies to ensure that commodities imported into the EU were produced on land not subject to deforestation after December 31, 2020, with specific provisions protecting indigenous land rights
- **Development Policy:** The European Consensus on Development (2017) commits the EU to supporting indigenous peoples' rights in partner countries

## Comparative Analysis

### Structural Differences

The fundamental structural difference is that only the United States has a substantial indigenous population with a constitutional relationship to the federal government. Germany lacks indigenous peoples domestically but engages with indigenous rights through foreign policy and colonial reparations. South Korea addresses cultural minority rights through multicultural frameworks rather than indigenous rights instruments. The ECHR/EU framework addresses indigenous issues primarily through minority rights frameworks (Sámi, Roma).

### Sovereignty and Self-Governance

The United States' tribal sovereignty model represents the most developed framework for indigenous self-governance in the world, granting tribes inherent governmental authority subject to Congressional plenary power. This model has no equivalent in the other jurisdictions. South Korea's Jeju Special Self-Governing Province provides limited autonomy but is not grounded in indigenous rights.

### Land Rights

Indigenous land rights are most developed in the United States (trust lands, reservations, treaty rights) and in countries that have adopted FPIC requirements. The International framework through UNDRIP and ILO 169 provides aspirational standards that most states have not fully implemented domestically.

### Truth and Reconciliation

All jurisdictions engage with historical justice in different ways. South Korea's Jeju 4.3 truth commission, Germany's Herero and Nama genocide recognition, and the United States' various settlement acts and truth-telling initiatives reflect different approaches to addressing historical wrongs against indigenous or marginalized communities.
