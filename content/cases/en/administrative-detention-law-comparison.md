## Summary

Administrative detention -- the deprivation of liberty by executive or administrative authorities without criminal charge or trial -- encompasses immigration detention, preventive detention, and security-related internment. Its legality, duration limits, and judicial review requirements vary enormously across jurisdictions. South Korea authorizes immigration detention under the Immigration Act (출입국관리법), permitting detention of foreign nationals for deportation purposes with initial periods of up to three months, extendable by the Ministry of Justice, though the Constitutional Court in 2023 (2020헌가1) declared indefinite detention without judicial review unconstitutional. The United States permits both immigration detention under the Immigration and Nationality Act (INA, 8 U.S.C. §§ 1225-1231) -- including mandatory detention for certain categories with no statutory time limit for arriving aliens under 8 U.S.C. § 1225(b) -- and preventive/military detention under the Authorization for Use of Military Force (AUMF), with the Supreme Court in Zadvydas v. Davis, 533 U.S. 678 (2001) imposing a presumptive six-month limit on post-removal-order detention. Germany regulates immigration detention (Abschiebungshaft) under §§ 62-62b of the Residence Act (Aufenthaltsgesetz, AufenthG), requiring judicial authorization by a judge within 48 hours, imposing a maximum detention period of six months (extendable to 18 months in exceptional circumstances), and incorporating EU Return Directive standards. The ECHR provides the overarching framework through Article 5 (right to liberty), with the Court's extensive jurisprudence establishing that detention must be lawful, necessary, proportionate, subject to prompt and periodic judicial review, and must not become arbitrary through excessive duration, as articulated in Saadi v. United Kingdom (2008) and Amuur v. France (1996).

## South Korea

### Constitutional Framework

Article 12 of the Constitution of the Republic of Korea guarantees personal liberty and security:

**Article 12(1):** "All citizens shall enjoy personal liberty. No person shall be arrested, detained, seized, searched, or interrogated except as provided by Act. No person shall be punished, placed under preventive restrictions, or subjected to involuntary labor except as provided by Act and through lawful procedures."

**Article 12(3):** "For arrest, detention, seizure, or search, a warrant issued by a judge through due procedures upon the request of a prosecutor shall be required." However, this warrant requirement has traditionally been interpreted to apply to criminal proceedings and not to immigration detention, a distinction the Constitutional Court addressed in its 2023 landmark decision.

**Article 12(6):** "Any person who is arrested or detained shall have the right to request the court to review the legality of the arrest or detention."

### Immigration Detention

**Immigration Act (출입국관리법):** Article 63 provides the primary basis for immigration detention. The head of a regional immigration office or the head of a detention facility may detain a foreign national subject to a deportation order if they cannot be immediately deported. Historically, this provision imposed no maximum detention period.

**Detention Process:**
1. **Protective Custody (보호):** Immigration officers may issue a "protective custody" order (보호명령서) to detain foreign nationals who are suspected of violating immigration laws or who are subject to deportation proceedings.
2. **Emergency Custody:** In urgent cases, officers may detain individuals without a written order for up to 48 hours.
3. **Extension:** Detention was historically extendable in three-month increments by approval of the Minister of Justice, with no upper limit.

### 2023 Constitutional Court Decision (2020헌가1)

In a watershed ruling, the Constitutional Court of Korea declared that the absence of any maximum detention period and the lack of mandatory judicial review in Article 63 of the Immigration Act violated the constitutional guarantee of personal liberty (Article 12 GG) and the prohibition of excessive restrictions on fundamental rights (Article 37(2)):

The Court held that:
- Indefinite detention without judicial review is incompatible with the Constitution's guarantee of personal liberty.
- Administrative convenience cannot justify prolonged deprivation of liberty without independent judicial oversight.
- The National Assembly must enact legislation providing for maximum detention periods and mandatory judicial review.

The decision ordered legislative reform by a specified deadline, requiring the introduction of judicial authorization and periodic review mechanisms for immigration detention.

### Detention Conditions and Oversight

**Immigration Detention Facilities:** South Korea operates dedicated immigration detention centers (외국인보호소) in Hwaseong and Cheongju, as well as temporary detention rooms at ports of entry. Conditions have been criticized by the National Human Rights Commission of Korea (NHRCK) in multiple reports, including concerns about overcrowding, inadequate medical care, lack of legal assistance, and prolonged detention of asylum seekers.

**NHRCK Monitoring:** The NHRCK conducts regular inspections of immigration detention facilities and has issued recommendations addressing the rights of detained migrants, including access to legal representation, interpretation services, and medical care.

### Preventive Detention

South Korea's National Security Act (국가보안법) historically authorized preventive detention of individuals deemed threats to national security, though its application has been significantly curtailed. The Act remains in force but preventive detention provisions are rarely invoked in contemporary practice. The Constitutional Court has upheld the Act's constitutionality in multiple decisions while emphasizing the need for narrow interpretation.

### Landmark Cases

**Constitutional Court 2014헌마253 (2018):** The Court held that prolonged immigration detention of a stateless person for over three years without effective judicial review violated the right to personal liberty, contributing to the framework that led to the 2023 landmark decision.

**Supreme Court 2013두14238 (2015):** The Court held that immigration authorities must consider the proportionality of continued detention, particularly where deportation cannot be reasonably expected to occur within a foreseeable timeframe.

## United States

### Constitutional Framework

The Fifth Amendment provides that no person shall "be deprived of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law." The Due Process Clause applies to all "persons" within the United States, including non-citizens. However, the extent of due process protections available to non-citizens -- particularly those at the border or seeking admission -- has been a subject of ongoing constitutional debate.

### Immigration Detention Statutory Framework

The Immigration and Nationality Act (INA) establishes multiple detention categories with varying legal standards:

**Mandatory Detention (8 U.S.C. § 1226(c)):** Certain categories of non-citizens must be detained during removal proceedings without the possibility of bond, including those convicted of aggravated felonies, drug offenses, firearms offenses, and certain crimes of moral turpitude. The Supreme Court in Jennings v. Rodriguez, 583 U.S. 281 (2018) held that § 1226(c) does not contain an implicit time limitation, though the Court remanded on the constitutional question.

**Discretionary Detention (8 U.S.C. § 1226(a)):** Non-citizens in removal proceedings who do not fall into mandatory detention categories may be detained or released on bond. Immigration judges may conduct bond hearings and set bond conditions.

**Arriving Aliens (8 U.S.C. § 1225(b)):** Non-citizens who arrive at a port of entry and are found inadmissible are subject to detention with no statutory time limit. In Shaughnessy v. United States ex rel. Mezei, 345 U.S. 206 (1953), the Supreme Court upheld the indefinite detention of an arriving alien who could not be removed, applying the "entry fiction" doctrine -- though this precedent has been subject to increasing criticism.

**Post-Removal-Order Detention (8 U.S.C. § 1231):** After a final order of removal, the government has a 90-day removal period during which detention is mandatory. Beyond this period, the government may continue to detain individuals deemed a flight risk or danger to the community.

### Key Supreme Court Decisions

**Zadvydas v. Davis, 533 U.S. 678 (2001):** The Court held that due process constrains the duration of post-removal-order detention, establishing a presumptive six-month limit. If removal is not reasonably foreseeable, continued detention becomes constitutionally suspect. The government bears the burden of demonstrating that removal remains reasonably foreseeable.

**Clark v. Martinez, 543 U.S. 371 (2005):** Extended the Zadvydas presumptive time limit to inadmissible aliens, not just deportable aliens.

**Demore v. Kim, 538 U.S. 510 (2003):** Upheld mandatory detention under § 1226(c) during removal proceedings for aliens convicted of specified crimes, reasoning that such detention was typically brief and supported by the government's interest in ensuring appearance at removal proceedings.

**Jennings v. Rodriguez, 583 U.S. 281 (2018):** Held that neither § 1225(b) nor § 1226(c) contains an implicit requirement for periodic bond hearings, reversing the Ninth Circuit's canon-of-avoidance reading of the statutes.

**Johnson v. Arteaga-Martinez, 596 U.S. 573 (2022):** Held that § 1231(a)(6) does not require the government to provide bond hearings for detained noncitizens after six months.

### Preventive and Military Detention

**Authorization for Use of Military Force (AUMF, 2001):** The Supreme Court in Hamdi v. Rumsfeld, 542 U.S. 507 (2004) held that the AUMF authorizes military detention of enemy combatants, including U.S. citizens, but that due process requires meaningful opportunity to contest the factual basis for detention before a neutral decisionmaker.

**Boumediene v. Bush, 553 U.S. 723 (2008):** Aliens detained as enemy combatants at Guantanamo Bay have a constitutional right to habeas corpus, and the procedures provided by the Detainee Treatment Act were not an adequate substitute.

**Material Witness Detention (18 U.S.C. § 3144):** Permits detention of material witnesses whose testimony is material in criminal proceedings and who may be unable to be secured by subpoena. Post-9/11 use of the material witness statute for investigative purposes has been criticized as a form of de facto preventive detention.

### Detention Conditions

Immigration detention in the United States is administered by Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE), which operates a network of dedicated detention centers, contract facilities, and county jails. As of 2025, ICE detained approximately 37,000 individuals daily. The DHS Office of Inspector General and civil rights organizations have documented systemic concerns including inadequate medical care, sexual abuse, deaths in custody, and the use of solitary confinement.

**Flores Settlement Agreement (1997):** Establishes standards for the detention, release, and treatment of minors in immigration custody, requiring that minors be held in the "least restrictive setting" appropriate to their age and circumstances and released to qualified sponsors without unnecessary delay. The settlement generally limits detention of accompanied minors to 20 days.

## Germany

### Constitutional Framework

The Basic Law (Grundgesetz) provides robust protections against arbitrary detention:

**Article 2(2):** "Every person shall have the right to life and physical integrity. Freedom of the person shall be inviolable."

**Article 104 (Deprivation of Liberty):**
- (1) "The liberty of the person may be restricted only pursuant to a formal law and only in compliance with the procedures prescribed therein."
- (2) "Only a judge may rule upon the permissibility or continuation of any deprivation of liberty. If such deprivation is not based on a judicial order, a judicial decision shall be obtained without delay."
- (3) "Any person provisionally detained on suspicion of having committed a criminal offense shall be brought before a judge no later than the day following the day of apprehension."
- (4) "A relative or a person enjoying the confidence of the person detained shall be notified without delay of any judicial order imposing or continuing a deprivation of liberty."

These constitutional provisions impose an absolute requirement of judicial authorization for detention, with no exception for immigration or administrative detention.

### Immigration Detention (Abschiebungshaft)

**Aufenthaltsgesetz (Residence Act) §§ 62-62b:** German immigration detention law distinguishes between several forms of detention:

**Preparatory Detention (Vorbereitungshaft, § 62(2)):** Detention to secure the preparation of an expulsion decision, permitted only where a deportation order has not yet been issued but is expected. Limited to six weeks.

**Security Detention (Sicherungshaft, § 62(3)):** The primary form of immigration detention, permitted when there is a risk that the individual will abscond or obstruct deportation. Specific grounds include:
- The individual has been convicted of a criminal offense.
- The individual has provided false identity information.
- The individual has previously failed to comply with a deportation order.
- The individual has clearly expressed an intent to abscond.
- Deportation was unsuccessful due to the individual's conduct.

**Duration Limits:** Initial detention may not exceed six months. Extension up to a total of 18 months is permitted only in exceptional circumstances where the individual has deliberately obstructed deportation or where the required travel documents cannot be obtained from the country of origin despite timely efforts.

**Judicial Authorization:** Under Article 104(2) GG, all immigration detention requires prior judicial authorization (except in emergency situations where judicial review must follow within 48 hours). Detention orders are issued by the local court (Amtsgericht) following a hearing at which the detainee has the right to legal representation. The court must review continued detention at least monthly (§ 425 FamFG).

**Proportionality and Alternatives:** § 62(1) establishes that detention may only be ordered as a last resort (ultima ratio). Less restrictive alternatives -- including reporting obligations, deposit of travel documents, electronic monitoring, and designated residence requirements -- must be considered and found insufficient before detention can be ordered.

### Separation from Criminal Detention

German law requires that immigration detainees be held in specialized removal detention facilities (Abschiebungshafteinrichtungen) separate from criminal prisoners. This requirement, derived from the EU Return Directive and reinforced by the Federal Constitutional Court, ensures that administrative detention does not carry the punitive character of criminal imprisonment. Several German Länder have established dedicated facilities, though capacity constraints have been a recurring challenge.

### Preventive Detention (Sicherungsverwahrung)

German criminal law permits preventive detention (Sicherungsverwahrung) of dangerous offenders under StGB §§ 66-66c, distinct from immigration detention:

- Preventive detention may be imposed alongside a prison sentence for offenders convicted of serious crimes who are deemed likely to commit further serious offenses.
- The detention is not punishment but a measure of correction and prevention (Maßregel der Besserung und Sicherung).
- It is subject to annual judicial review and must be accompanied by therapeutic treatment opportunities.

**M. v. Germany, ECHR App. No. 19359/04 (2009):** The European Court of Human Rights found that Germany's retrospective extension of preventive detention beyond the former 10-year maximum violated Article 5(1) (right to liberty) and Article 7(1) (no retrospective punishment) of the Convention. This landmark ruling forced Germany to fundamentally reform its preventive detention system.

**Legislative Reform (2013):** In response to M. v. Germany and subsequent ECHR and Constitutional Court decisions, Germany enacted the Act on the Implementation of Distance Requirements in Preventive Detention Law (Gesetz zur bundesrechtlichen Umsetzung des Abstandsgebots im Recht der Sicherungsverwahrung, 2013), requiring that preventive detention be clearly distinguished from imprisonment in its conditions and treatment orientation.

### Police Preventive Detention (Unterbindungsgewahrsam)

German Länder police laws permit short-term preventive police detention (Unterbindungsgewahrsam or Präventivgewahrsam) to prevent imminent offenses. Duration limits vary by Land but are typically 48 hours, extendable to up to 14 days in some Länder (e.g., Bavaria under Art. 17 PAG). Judicial authorization is required within the first day.

## ECHR/EU Framework

### Article 5 ECHR (Right to Liberty)

Article 5 of the European Convention on Human Rights provides the foundational framework for evaluating the legality of all forms of administrative detention:

**Article 5(1):** "Everyone has the right to liberty and security of person. No one shall be deprived of his liberty save in the following cases and in accordance with a procedure prescribed by law:" The exhaustive list of permissible grounds includes:
- (c) Lawful arrest or detention for the purpose of bringing a person before a competent legal authority on reasonable suspicion of having committed an offence, or for the prevention of an offence or flight after commission.
- (f) Lawful arrest or detention of a person to prevent his effecting an unauthorized entry into the country or of a person against whom action is being taken with a view to deportation or extradition.

**Article 5(2):** Right to be promptly informed of the reasons for detention.

**Article 5(3):** Right to be brought promptly before a judge (applicable to criminal detention under Art. 5(1)(c)).

**Article 5(4):** "Everyone who is deprived of his liberty by arrest or detention shall be entitled to take proceedings by which the lawfulness of his detention shall be decided speedily by a court and his release ordered if the detention is not lawful." This habeas corpus provision applies to all forms of detention, including immigration and administrative detention.

**Article 5(5):** Right to compensation for unlawful detention.

### Landmark ECHR Jurisprudence on Immigration Detention

**Saadi v. United Kingdom, App. No. 13229/03 (2008):** The Grand Chamber held that detention under Article 5(1)(f) for the purpose of preventing unauthorized entry does not require that detention be necessary to prevent absconding or offending. However, detention must not be arbitrary, meaning it must be carried out in good faith, closely connected to the ground of detention relied upon, the place and conditions of detention must be appropriate, and the length of detention must not exceed that reasonably required for the purpose pursued.

**Amuur v. France, App. No. 19776/92 (1996):** The Court held that holding asylum seekers in the international transit zone of Paris-Orly airport constituted a deprivation of liberty requiring the protections of Article 5. The mere fact that asylum seekers were theoretically free to leave the country did not prevent their confinement from constituting detention.

**Chahal v. United Kingdom, App. No. 22414/93 (1996):** The Grand Chamber held that immigration detention pending deportation under Article 5(1)(f) is permissible only as long as deportation proceedings are being conducted with "due diligence." If the state fails to pursue deportation with sufficient speed, continued detention becomes arbitrary.

**A. and Others v. United Kingdom, App. No. 3455/05 (2009):** The Grand Chamber found that the indefinite detention without charge of suspected international terrorists under the Anti-terrorism, Crime and Security Act 2001 violated Article 5(1), as the detention was disproportionate and discriminatory (applying only to foreign nationals).

**Khlaifia and Others v. Italy, App. No. 16483/12 (2016):** The Grand Chamber held that the detention of Tunisian migrants in a reception center on Lampedusa violated Article 5(1) due to the absence of a clear and accessible legal basis, lack of formal detention orders, and failure to provide effective judicial review.

### EU Return Directive (2008/115/EC)

The Return Directive establishes minimum standards for the detention of third-country nationals subject to return procedures:

**Article 15 (Detention):**
- Detention may be ordered only where other sufficient but less coercive measures cannot be applied effectively.
- Detention must be for as short a period as possible and maintained only as long as removal arrangements are in progress and executed with due diligence.
- Maximum period: 6 months, extendable to a maximum of 18 months where the individual fails to cooperate or there are delays in obtaining travel documents from third countries.
- Detention must be reviewed at reasonable intervals of time either on the application of the third-country national concerned or ex officio (on the authority's own initiative).

**Article 16 (Conditions of Detention):**
- Detention shall as a rule take place in specialized detention facilities.
- Where a Member State cannot provide accommodation in a specialized facility and is obliged to resort to prison accommodation, the detained third-country nationals shall be kept separately from ordinary prisoners.

**Article 17 (Detention of Minors and Families):**
- Unaccompanied minors and families with minors shall be detained only as a measure of last resort and for the shortest appropriate period of time.
- Minors in detention shall have the possibility to engage in leisure activities, including play and recreational activities appropriate to their age.

### Reception Conditions Directive (2013/33/EU)

For asylum seekers, the Reception Conditions Directive provides additional protections:

**Article 8:** Member States shall not hold a person in detention for the sole reason that they have applied for international protection. Detention may be ordered only on specified grounds, including verification of identity or nationality, determination of elements of the application, and in cases of risk of absconding.

**Article 9:** Detention shall be ordered in writing with reasons in fact and in law. It shall be subject to judicial review. Detained asylum seekers shall have access to free legal assistance.

**Article 11:** Special detention needs of vulnerable persons -- including minors, unaccompanied minors, pregnant women, persons with disabilities, victims of torture, and persons with mental health disorders -- must be addressed.

## Comparative Analysis

### Maximum Detention Periods

The permissible duration of administrative detention reveals fundamental differences in the weight assigned to personal liberty:

| Jurisdiction | Immigration Detention Maximum | Preventive Detention Maximum |
|-------------|------------------------------|------------------------------|
| **South Korea** | Previously unlimited; now constitutionally constrained (reform pending) | Rarely used; National Security Act provisions largely dormant |
| **United States** | No statutory maximum for arriving aliens; 6-month presumptive limit post-removal order (Zadvydas) | Indefinite under AUMF (with habeas review per Boumediene) |
| **Germany** | 6 months; extendable to 18 months in exceptional cases | Indefinite Sicherungsverwahrung (with annual review) |
| **ECHR/EU** | Return Directive: 6 months + 18 months maximum | Must be proportionate; M. v. Germany established limits on retrospective extension |

The United States permits the longest immigration detention periods, with no statutory ceiling for arriving aliens. Germany's strictly limited framework, requiring judicial authorization and ultima ratio consideration, represents the most protective model for immigration detainees.

### Judicial Review Requirements

| Jurisdiction | Prior Judicial Authorization | Periodic Review | Habeas Corpus Access |
|-------------|----------------------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| **South Korea** | Now required (post-2023 ruling) | Every 3 months (administrative) | Article 12(6) Constitution |
| **United States** | Not required for immigration detention | Not automatically guaranteed for all categories | Available but scope contested (Boumediene) |
| **Germany** | Required within 48 hours (Art. 104(2) GG) | Monthly (§ 425 FamFG) | Guaranteed under Art. 104 GG |
| **ECHR** | Art. 5(4) requires access to court | At "reasonable intervals" | Art. 5(4) -- core guarantee |

Germany's constitutional requirement of judicial authorization within 48 hours for all deprivations of liberty -- with no exception for immigration detention -- stands in stark contrast to the U.S. system, where immigration detention may proceed without any prior judicial authorization.

### Treatment of Vulnerable Populations

All jurisdictions have developed special protections for vulnerable detainees, though their scope and enforceability differ:

- **Minors:** The Flores Settlement (U.S.), Article 17 of the Return Directive (EU), and § 62(1) of the Aufenthaltsgesetz (Germany) all provide enhanced protections. South Korea's framework is less developed.
- **Asylum seekers:** The EU Reception Conditions Directive provides the most comprehensive framework, prohibiting detention solely for seeking asylum. The U.S. permits detention of asylum seekers, including under the expedited removal framework.
- **Stateless persons:** All jurisdictions struggle with the detention of stateless persons who cannot be removed. Zadvydas (U.S.) and Chahal (ECHR) address this through proportionality and due diligence requirements.

### Alternatives to Detention

There is increasing convergence toward the use of alternatives to detention (ATD):

- **South Korea:** Bail, designated residence, and reporting requirements are available but underutilized.
- **United States:** ICE operates an ATD program including GPS ankle monitors, smartphone-based check-ins (SmartLINK), and community supervision. However, expansion of ATD has been inconsistent.
- **Germany:** The ultima ratio principle requires consideration of alternatives (reporting obligations, depositing travel documents, electronic monitoring) before detention can be ordered.
- **EU:** Article 15(1) of the Return Directive mandates consideration of less coercive measures before detention.
