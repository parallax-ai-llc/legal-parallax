## Summary

State responsibility -- the international legal doctrine that holds states accountable for internationally wrongful acts, requiring them to make reparation for the injury caused by breaches of their international obligations -- forms a cornerstone of the international legal order, bridging the gap between states' international obligations and the consequences of their violation. The International Law Commission's Articles on Responsibility of States for Internationally Wrongful Acts (ARSIWA, 2001), adopted by the ILC and taken note of by the UN General Assembly in Resolution 56/83, provide the authoritative codification of the customary international law rules governing state responsibility, addressing attribution, circumstances precluding wrongfulness, content of responsibility, and implementation. South Korea's engagement with state responsibility doctrine is shaped by its unique geopolitical circumstances, including unresolved historical claims against Japan regarding wartime forced labor and "comfort women" (위안부), the ongoing legal status of the Korean War (which ended in armistice rather than peace treaty), and domestic implementation through the State Compensation Act (국가배상법) and Constitutional Court jurisprudence. The United States has contributed significantly to the development of state responsibility doctrine through ICJ litigation, claims settlement practice (including the Iran-US Claims Tribunal), and domestic legislation such as the Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act (FSIA, 28 U.S.C. Sections 1602-1611) and the state-sponsored terrorism exception, while simultaneously resisting certain expansions of the doctrine, particularly regarding environmental and human rights obligations. Germany's engagement with state responsibility is profoundly shaped by its post-World War II reparations experience, its extensive practice before the International Court of Justice, and the Federal Constitutional Court's landmark rulings on the domestic effects of international responsibility, including the Distomo massacre cases and the ICJ's Jurisdictional Immunities judgment (2012). At the European level, the ECHR provides the most developed regional system for holding states responsible for human rights violations, the EU has developed its own regime of member state liability for breaches of EU law through the Francovich doctrine, and the ECtHR's pilot judgment procedure addresses systemic state responsibility failures.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

The domestic framework for state responsibility in South Korea is anchored in Article 29 of the Constitution (헌법), which provides: "In case a person has sustained damages by an unlawful act committed by a public official in the course of official duties, he may claim just compensation from the State or public organization under the conditions as prescribed by Act." The implementing legislation is the State Compensation Act (국가배상법), first enacted in 1967.

### State Compensation Act (국가배상법)

The State Compensation Act establishes a fault-based liability regime. Article 2 provides that the State or a local government shall compensate for damage caused by a public official's intentional or negligent commission of an unlawful act in the course of performing official duties. Article 5 provides for liability for defects in the construction or management of public facilities (strict liability for dangerous conditions).

Claims are initially heard by the State Compensation Council (국가배상심의회), with judicial review available in the courts. The statute of limitations is five years from when the victim knew of the damage and the identity of the responsible party.

### Historical Claims Against Japan

The most significant state responsibility issues for South Korea concern unresolved claims against Japan arising from the colonial period (1910-1945).

**Comfort Women (위안부):** The Constitutional Court rendered a landmark decision on August 30, 2011 (2006헌마788), holding that the Korean government's failure to take diplomatic action to resolve the comfort women claims against Japan constituted an unconstitutional omission violating the victims' fundamental rights. The 2015 Korea-Japan agreement, in which Japan provided 1 billion yen to a foundation for surviving victims, was controversial and criticized by many survivors as inadequate.

**Forced Labor (강제동원):** In a series of decisions beginning with Supreme Court Decision 2009다22549 (May 24, 2012), the Supreme Court of Korea held that individual Korean victims of forced labor during the Japanese colonial period retained their right to claim compensation from Japanese companies, finding that the 1965 Korea-Japan Claims Settlement Agreement did not extinguish individual claims for illegal acts. This jurisprudence culminated in the October 30, 2018 Supreme Court decision ordering Nippon Steel (formerly Shin Nippon Seitetsu) to pay 100 million KRW to each of four surviving victims.

### Penalties and Remedies

| Claim Type | Remedy |
|-----------|--------|
| State Compensation Act claim (Art. 2) | Monetary compensation for damages |
| Public facility defect (Art. 5) | Strict liability compensation |
| Constitutional omission (comfort women model) | Government obligated to take diplomatic action |
| Historical claims against foreign states | Subject to sovereign immunity limitations; diplomatic resolution |

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States addresses state responsibility through multiple legal frameworks. Domestically, the Federal Tort Claims Act (FTCA, 28 U.S.C. Sections 1346(b), 2671-2680) waives sovereign immunity for tort claims against the federal government, subject to numerous exceptions. The Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act (FSIA, 28 U.S.C. Sections 1602-1611) governs suits against foreign states in US courts.

**FSIA State-Sponsored Terrorism Exception:** Section 1605A, added by the 2008 amendments, creates an exception to foreign sovereign immunity for claims arising from acts of torture, extrajudicial killing, aircraft sabotage, or hostage taking by designated state sponsors of terrorism. This provision has been used to obtain multi-billion-dollar default judgments against Iran, Syria, and Sudan.

**Iran-US Claims Tribunal:** Established in 1981 following the Iran hostage crisis through the Algiers Accords, the Tribunal in The Hague has adjudicated thousands of claims between Iranian and American nationals and the two governments, developing significant jurisprudence on state responsibility, attribution, and compensation standards.

### Notable Cases

**Nicaragua v. United States (ICJ, 1986):** The ICJ held the United States responsible for violations of international law through its support of Contra rebels in Nicaragua and the mining of Nicaraguan harbors. The Court applied the "effective control" test for attribution of non-state actor conduct to states. The US refused to participate in the merits phase and declined to comply with the judgment.

**Iran v. United States (Oil Platforms, ICJ, 2003):** The ICJ addressed US attacks on Iranian oil platforms in the Persian Gulf, examining the law of state responsibility in the context of the use of force and the bilateral Treaty of Amity.

### Remedies

| Framework | Remedy Available |
|-----------|-----------------|
| FTCA claims against US government | Monetary compensation (with exceptions) |
| FSIA terrorism exception (§ 1605A) | Compensatory and punitive damages |
| Iran-US Claims Tribunal | Monetary awards |
| ICJ proceedings | Declarations; reparation orders (compliance voluntary) |

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's state responsibility framework operates on multiple levels. Domestically, state liability (Staatshaftung) is governed by Article 34 of the Grundgesetz, which provides: "Where any person, in the exercise of a public office entrusted to him, violates his official duty to a third party, liability shall rest principally with the State or the public body which employs him." This constitutional provision is implemented through Section 839 BGB (Amtshaftung, official liability).

### Post-War Reparations

Germany's experience with state responsibility is uniquely shaped by the post-World War II reparations regime. The Federal Compensation Act (Bundesentschädigungsgesetz, BEG) of 1953 and the Federal Restitution Act (Bundesrückerstattungsgesetz, BRüG) of 1957 established comprehensive compensation frameworks for victims of Nazi persecution. Germany has paid over 80 billion euros in reparations and compensation to Holocaust survivors and their heirs. The German Foundation "Remembrance, Responsibility and Future" (Stiftung "Erinnerung, Verantwortung und Zukunft," EVZ), established in 2000, provided 4.4 billion euros in payments to former forced laborers.

### Distomo and Sovereign Immunity

The Distomo massacre case arose from the killing of 218 Greek civilians by Waffen-SS troops in the village of Distomo on June 10, 1944. Greek courts awarded damages, and Italian courts (in the Ferrini decision, Corte di Cassazione No. 5044/2004) denied Germany sovereign immunity for wartime atrocities. The dispute reached the ICJ in Jurisdictional Immunities of the State (Germany v. Italy: Greece Intervening), where the Court ruled on February 3, 2012, that Italy had violated Germany's sovereign immunity by allowing civil claims for wartime atrocities. The judgment was controversial, as it prioritized sovereign immunity over access to justice for victims of serious international law violations.

### Kunduz Airstrike

The September 4, 2009 airstrike near Kunduz, Afghanistan, ordered by German Colonel Georg Klein, killed approximately 142 people. The case raised state responsibility issues in multiple forums: German criminal proceedings (Klein was not charged), domestic administrative proceedings, and civil claims. The Bundesgerichtshof (Federal Court of Justice) ruled in 2016 (III ZR 140/15) that the Amtshaftung claim was properly before German courts but ultimately dismissed the claim on the merits, finding no breach of duty under the applicable rules of international humanitarian law.

### Penalties and Remedies

| Claim Type | Remedy |
|-----------|--------|
| Amtshaftung (Art. 34 GG / § 839 BGB) | Monetary compensation |
| Post-WWII reparations | Structured compensation programs |
| ICJ proceedings | Declaratory judgments; reparation orders |
| EU law breaches (Francovich) | State liable for individual damages caused by non-transposition |

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR State Responsibility

The ECHR provides the most developed regional system for holding states responsible for human rights violations. Under Article 34, any person, non-governmental organization, or group of individuals claiming to be the victim of a violation may file an application against a state party. Article 41 provides that the Court shall afford "just satisfaction" to the injured party where it finds a violation.

**Interstate Applications (Article 33):** States parties may refer alleged breaches by other states to the Court. Recent notable interstate cases include Ukraine v. Russia (applications concerning the conflict in eastern Ukraine and Crimea) and the Netherlands and Ukraine v. Russia (regarding the downing of Malaysia Airlines Flight MH17).

**Pilot Judgments:** The pilot judgment procedure, formalized by Rule 61 of the Rules of Court, addresses systemic state responsibility failures. In Broniowski v. Poland (2004), the Court identified a systemic property rights violation affecting approximately 80,000 people and required Poland to implement general remedial measures.

**Just Satisfaction (Article 41):** The Court awards pecuniary and non-pecuniary damages. Awards have ranged from symbolic amounts to millions of euros in interstate cases. In Georgia v. Russia (II) (2021), the Court awarded 10 million euros in non-pecuniary damages for Russia's actions during the 2008 armed conflict.

### EU Member State Liability -- Francovich Doctrine

The CJEU established in Francovich and Bonifaci v. Italy (Joined Cases C-6/90 and C-9/90, Judgment of November 19, 1991) that member states are liable to individuals for damage caused by the state's failure to transpose EU directives. The doctrine was refined in Brasserie du Pêcheur/Factortame III (Joined Cases C-46/93 and C-48/93, 1996), establishing a three-part test: the rule of EU law breached must confer rights on individuals; the breach must be sufficiently serious; and there must be a direct causal link between the breach and the damage.

### International Law Commission -- ARSIWA

The ILC's Articles on Responsibility of States for Internationally Wrongful Acts (2001), drafted under the leadership of Special Rapporteur James Crawford, provide the authoritative codification of state responsibility rules. Key provisions include: Article 1 (every internationally wrongful act entails responsibility); Article 2 (elements: conduct attributable to the state and breach of an international obligation); Articles 4-11 (attribution rules); Articles 20-27 (circumstances precluding wrongfulness); and Articles 28-41 (content of responsibility, including cessation, reparation, and countermeasures).

## Comparative Analysis

### Domestic State Liability

All jurisdictions provide domestic frameworks for holding their own state responsible for wrongful acts, but with different structures. South Korea uses a fault-based system. The United States waives sovereign immunity through the FTCA with significant exceptions. Germany applies the Amtshaftung system through constitutional and civil law provisions. The ECHR provides a supranational layer of state accountability.

### Historical Claims

State responsibility for historical wrongs remains one of the most contentious areas. South Korea's forced labor and comfort women claims against Japan, Germany's WWII reparations program, and the US's claims settlement practice (Iran-US Claims Tribunal) illustrate different approaches to historical accountability. The tension between sovereign immunity and access to justice, highlighted by the ICJ's Jurisdictional Immunities judgment, remains unresolved.

### Attribution

The standard for attributing non-state actor conduct to states differs. The ICJ's "effective control" test (Nicaragua) sets a high threshold. The ICTY's "overall control" test (Tadić) is broader. The ARSIWA codify attribution rules but leave room for judicial interpretation. The ECtHR applies its own attribution analysis under the Convention.

### Enforcement

The fundamental challenge of state responsibility remains enforcement. ICJ judgments lack effective enforcement mechanisms. ECHR judgments are supervised by the Committee of Ministers but compliance challenges persist. The EU's Francovich doctrine is enforced through national courts under the primacy of EU law. Diplomatic means, countermeasures, and political pressure remain the primary enforcement tools at the international level.
