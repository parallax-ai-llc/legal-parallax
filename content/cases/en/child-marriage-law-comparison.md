## Summary

Child marriage laws reveal one of the starkest divergences in legal standards among developed nations, with some jurisdictions maintaining absolute minimum age requirements while others permit exceptions that enable marriage of children as young as 12 or 13 under certain circumstances. South Korea amended its Civil Act (민법) in 2007 to establish an absolute minimum marriage age of 18 with no exceptions, eliminating the prior provision that permitted marriage at 16 (females) and 18 (males) with parental consent. Germany enacted the Act to Combat Child Marriages (Gesetz zur Bekämpfung von Kinderehen, 2017) setting an absolute minimum marriage age of 18, with marriages involving persons under 16 declared void ab initio and those between 16-18 subject to annulment, though the Federal Constitutional Court (BVerfG) partially struck down this framework in 2023 for failing to address the consequences for child spouses from abroad. The United States presents the most fragmented landscape among developed nations: while the general marriage age is 18, as of 2025 only 16 states have established a true absolute minimum age of 18 with no exceptions, while 34 states continue to permit child marriage under varying conditions of parental consent, judicial authorization, or both, with some states setting statutory minimums as low as 14 or imposing no minimum at all. The ECHR framework, through Article 12 and the Council of Europe's Istanbul Convention, has established that the right to marry presupposes free and full consent, providing a human rights basis for member states' increasing movement toward absolute minimum age requirements.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's prohibition on child marriage is codified in the Civil Act (민법, Minbeop), the foundational private law statute governing personal and family relationships:

- **Article 807 -- Marriageable Age (혼인적령):** "No man or woman under the age of 18 shall marry." This provision, amended in 2007 (effective 2008), replaced the prior gender-differentiated system that set the minimum age at 18 for males and 16 for females with parental consent.
- **Article 808 -- Consent of Persons with Parental Authority (동의):** Prior to the 2007 amendment, this article required parental consent for minors aged 16-17 (females) or exactly 18 (males) to marry. The amendment rendered this provision inapplicable by raising the absolute minimum to 18 for all persons, effectively eliminating any parental consent pathway to child marriage.
- **Article 816 -- Grounds for Annulment:** A marriage entered into in violation of Article 807 (i.e., by a person under 18) is voidable and may be annulled by the parties, their legal representatives, or the prosecutor.
- **Article 820 -- Parental Authority:** Parents retain authority over unmarried minor children, and any attempt to consent to a minor's marriage is legally ineffective.

The Family Relations Registration Act (가족관계의 등록 등에 관한 법률) provides additional procedural safeguards by requiring age verification at the time of marriage registration. Municipal offices (구청, 시청) will not accept marriage registration applications where either party is under 18.

**Related Criminal Provisions:**

While child marriage itself is not separately criminalized, sexual offenses against minors are governed by:

- **Criminal Act (형법) Article 305:** Sexual intercourse with a person under the age of 13 constitutes statutory rape regardless of consent, punishable by imprisonment of not less than three years.
- **Act on Special Cases Concerning the Punishment of Sexual Crimes (성폭력범죄의 처벌 등에 관한 특례법) Article 7:** Sexual intercourse with a person under 16 (raised from 13 in 2020) by means of deception or authority carries imprisonment of not less than two years.

### Key Principles

South Korea's absolute prohibition reflects a cultural shift driven by feminist advocacy, child rights organizations, and alignment with international standards under the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child (CRC), which South Korea ratified in 1991. The prevalence of child marriage in South Korea is now negligible: according to Statistics Korea, fewer than 0.01% of marriages involve a party under 18. However, advocates note that informal unions and unregistered cohabitation involving minors remain a concern, particularly among North Korean defector communities and in rural areas.

The 2007 amendment also eliminated the gender-based age differential, which had reflected traditional patriarchal norms in Korean family law. The equalization of the minimum age to 18 for all genders was part of a broader modernization of Korean family law, including the abolition of the hoju (male household head) system in 2005.

### Notable Cases

- **Constitutional Court Decision 97Hun-Ka12 (1997):** The Constitutional Court upheld the prior gender-differentiated marriage age system (18/16), finding it did not violate the equality principle given then-prevailing social conditions. This decision was effectively superseded by the 2007 legislative amendment.
- **Seoul Family Court, 2012Beu-XXXX (anonymized):** The court annulled a marriage where a 17-year-old was married using falsified age documents, reaffirming the non-negotiable nature of the age requirement and imposing penalties for document fraud.
- **NHRCK Recommendation on Child Marriage Recognition (2019):** The National Human Rights Commission of Korea recommended that the government adopt a policy of non-recognition of foreign child marriages for immigration purposes, strengthening protections against cross-border child marriage.

## United States

### Federal Framework

The United States has no federal minimum marriage age. Marriage law is constitutionally reserved to the states under the Tenth Amendment, resulting in a patchwork of statutes with widely varying minimum ages and exception mechanisms.

**Federal Constitutional Framework:**

- **Due Process Clause (14th Amendment):** The Supreme Court recognized marriage as a fundamental right in Loving v. Virginia, 388 U.S. 1 (1967), and Obergefell v. Hodges, 576 U.S. 644 (2015). However, these rulings address the right of adults to marry and do not establish a right to child marriage.
- **State Action Doctrine:** Federal courts have generally declined to review state minimum marriage age laws under rational basis scrutiny, treating them as within the traditional domain of state family law.

**Federal Legislation:**

- **Preventing Forced Child Marriage Act (proposed, not enacted as of 2026):** Multiple bills have been introduced in Congress to set a federal minimum marriage age of 18 for purposes of immigration and federal recognition, but none have been enacted.
- **Violence Against Women Act (VAWA, 34 U.S.C. § 12291):** While not directly addressing minimum marriage age, VAWA provides protections for victims of forced marriage, including immigration relief for victims of domestic violence.

### State Variations

As of 2025, the landscape of state child marriage laws is as follows:

**States with Absolute Minimum Age of 18 (No Exceptions) -- 16 States:**

Delaware (2016, first state), New Jersey (2018), Pennsylvania (2020), Minnesota (2020), Massachusetts (2022), New York (2021), Connecticut (2023), Michigan (2024), Virginia (2016 -- raised to 18 from 16), and others including Illinois, Vermont, Rhode Island, and Washington.

**States Permitting Marriage Under 18 with Exceptions -- 34 States:**

These states employ various combinations of:

- **Parental consent alone:** Some states permit marriage at 16 or 17 with parental or guardian consent.
- **Judicial authorization:** Some states require a judge to approve a minor's marriage, often requiring a finding that the marriage is in the minor's best interest.
- **Combined parental consent and judicial authorization:** Some states require both.
- **Pregnancy exception:** Some states historically allowed minors of any age to marry if pregnant, though this exception has been narrowing.

**Notably Problematic Provisions (as of 2025):**

- **California:** Has no statutory minimum marriage age; minors of any age may marry with parental consent and a court order. Legislative efforts to set a minimum age have repeatedly failed.
- **Mississippi:** General marriage age is 21; with parental consent, males may marry at 17 and females at 15. No judicial authorization required.
- **West Virginia:** Permits marriage at 16 with parental consent; no judicial review required.

**Scale of the Issue:**

Between 2000 and 2018, an estimated 300,000 minors were legally married in the United States, according to Unchained at Last. The vast majority (over 86%) were girls married to adult men. In many cases, the age gap between the child and the adult spouse would have constituted statutory rape had the marriage not provided a legal exception.

### Notable Cases

- **Abolish Child Marriage Campaign -- Unchained at Last (2015-present):** The advocacy organization founded by Fraidy Reiss, herself a survivor of a forced marriage at age 19, has led the legislative campaign to ban child marriage state by state, achieving bans in 16 states as of 2025.
- **Heather Strawn v. Indiana (2023):** An Indiana court case that drew national attention after a 16-year-old was denied the right to divorce because she was too young to file a lawsuit, highlighting the paradox that minors old enough to marry may lack the legal capacity to initiate divorce proceedings.
- **UNICEF USA Campaign on California (2024):** UNICEF USA publicly advocated for California to set an absolute minimum marriage age of 18, citing the state's failure to pass such legislation despite multiple attempts, including AB 2924 (2024) which died in committee.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's child marriage law underwent a dramatic transformation beginning in 2017, driven by the arrival of significant numbers of refugees from countries where child marriage is prevalent:

**Civil Code (Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch, BGB):**

- **§ 1303 -- Marriageable Age (Ehemündigkeit):** Since the 2017 amendment, marriage requires that both parties have reached the age of 18. The prior provision (§ 1303(2) old version) had allowed marriage at 16 with family court approval if the other party was of full age and the court found the minor sufficiently mature. This exception was abolished.

**Act to Combat Child Marriages (Gesetz zur Bekämpfung von Kinderehen, 2017):**

- **§ 1303 sentence 2 BGB (new):** A marriage is void (nichtig) if one party was under 16 at the time of the marriage.
- **§ 1314(1) No. 1 BGB:** A marriage where one party was between 16 and 18 at the time of the marriage is annullable (aufhebbar). The annulment can be sought by the minor spouse, the legal representative, or the administrative authority.
- **Article 13(3) EGBGB (Introductory Act to the BGB):** Foreign marriages involving a party under 16 are not recognized in Germany. Foreign marriages involving a party between 16 and 18 are subject to annulment proceedings.

**Federal Constitutional Court Decision (BVerfG, 1 BvL 7/18, 1 February 2023):**

The Bundesverfassungsgericht ruled that the 2017 Act was partially unconstitutional. The Court found that:

1. The blanket invalidation of marriages involving parties under 16 was constitutionally permissible as a matter of child protection.
2. However, the law was incompatible with the Basic Law (Articles 1(1), 2(1), and 6(1) GG) insofar as it failed to provide any mechanism to address the legal consequences of invalidation for the child spouse, particularly regarding maintenance, property rights, and the status of children born during the marriage.
3. The law also unconstitutionally failed to provide a mechanism for recognizing marriages concluded abroad by parties who were under 16 at the time but had since reached majority.

The Court ordered the legislature to enact a new provision by 30 June 2024. In response, the Bundestag amended the BGB (effective 1 July 2024) to maintain the absolute prohibition while creating provisions for the legal consequences of annulled child marriages, including maintenance claims, property adjustments, and the option for former child spouses who have reached 18 to apply for recognition of the marriage.

**Criminal Code (Strafgesetzbuch, StGB):**

- **§ 237 -- Forced Marriage (Zwangsheirat):** Criminalizes forcing a person to enter into a marriage through threats or violence, punishable by imprisonment of six months to five years. This provision targets forced marriage specifically, regardless of the ages involved.
- **§ 176 -- Sexual Abuse of Children (Sexueller Missbrauch von Kindern):** Sexual acts with a person under 14 are punishable by imprisonment of one to fifteen years. Marriage does not constitute a defense.
- **§ 182 -- Sexual Abuse of Juveniles (Sexueller Missbrauch von Jugendlichen):** Criminalizes sexual acts with persons between 14 and 18 under specified circumstances of exploitation.

### Key Principles

Germany's approach reflects the constitutional principle that the protection of children (Article 6(2) GG) takes precedence over the freedom to marry, while also recognizing the complex reality of cross-border marriages. The 2023 BVerfG decision exemplifies the German legal tradition of balancing competing constitutional values: the Court upheld the policy goal of prohibiting child marriage while requiring the legislature to address the practical consequences for child spouses who had entered into marriages in good faith in their countries of origin.

The 2017 Act was directly prompted by the European refugee crisis: German authorities reported approximately 1,475 registered married minors in Germany in 2016, the majority from Syria, Afghanistan, and Iraq. The legislative response was politically contentious, with critics arguing that blanket non-recognition of foreign marriages denied agency to adolescents who had entered marriages voluntarily under their home country's laws.

### Notable Cases

- **BVerfG, 1 BvL 7/18 (1 February 2023):** The landmark ruling finding the 2017 Act partially unconstitutional, discussed above. The case arose from a referral by the Wiesbaden District Court concerning the marriage of a Syrian couple where the wife was 14 at the time of marriage in Syria.
- **BGH, XII ZB 292/16 (14 November 2018):** The Federal Court of Justice referred the question of the constitutionality of § 1303 BGB to the Federal Constitutional Court, noting concerns about the retroactive application of the 2017 Act to marriages validly concluded abroad.
- **OLG Bamberg, 2 UF 58/17 (12 May 2016):** Prior to the 2017 Act, the Bamberg Higher Regional Court recognized a marriage between a Syrian man and his 14-year-old cousin, applying Syrian family law under German private international law. This decision provoked public outrage and was a direct catalyst for the 2017 legislation.

## ECHR / EU Framework

### Convention Articles

- **Article 12 -- Right to Marry:** "Men and women of marriageable age have the right to marry and to found a family, according to the national laws governing the exercise of this right." The phrase "of marriageable age" expressly contemplates a minimum age for marriage, but the Convention defers to national law to set the specific age threshold.
- **Article 8 -- Right to Respect for Private and Family Life:** Provides protection for family life, including marriages, but does not prevent states from refusing to recognize child marriages concluded abroad when doing so serves the legitimate aim of protecting children.
- **Article 14 -- Prohibition of Discrimination:** Applicable to gender-based differences in minimum marriage ages, which the Court has increasingly viewed with skepticism.

### Key Case Law

- **Z.H. and R.H. v. Switzerland (2015, App. No. 60119/12):** The applicants, an Afghan couple married in Iran when the wife was 14, challenged Switzerland's refusal to recognize their marriage. The Court held that neither Article 8 nor Article 12 imposed an obligation on states to recognize marriages contracted by 14-year-olds, emphasizing the "sensitive moral choices" involved and the importance of child protection. The Court gave states a wide margin of appreciation in setting minimum marriage ages.
- **Muñoz Díaz v. Spain (2009, App. No. 49151/07):** While not directly about child marriage, the Court addressed the recognition of Roma customary marriages, holding that Spain violated Article 14 in conjunction with Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 by refusing to recognize a Roma marriage for social security purposes. The case illustrates the intersection of cultural practices and marriage recognition.
- **Khan v. United Kingdom (2018, App. No. 47486/06, admissibility decision):** The Court considered the forced marriage of a young woman and the UK's obligations under Article 4 (prohibition of servitude) and Article 8 to protect victims of forced marriage.

**Council of Europe Instruments:**

- **Istanbul Convention (CETS No. 210), Article 37 -- Forced Marriage:** States parties must criminalize forced marriage and ensure that marriages concluded under force can be voidable, annullable, or dissolved. Article 37(2) specifically addresses the luring of a person abroad for forced marriage.
- **Parliamentary Assembly Resolution 1468 (2005):** Called on member states to set the minimum legal age for marriage at 18 and to ensure that all exceptions are subject to judicial authorization.
- **Committee of Ministers Recommendation CM/Rec(2002)5:** Recommended that member states take measures to combat violence against women, including setting an appropriate minimum marriage age.

### Member State Obligations

Under the evolving ECHR framework and Council of Europe standards, member states are expected to:

- Establish a clear minimum marriage age in national law, with the trend strongly favoring 18 as the absolute minimum.
- Ensure free and full consent of both parties as a prerequisite for marriage.
- Criminalize forced marriage (Istanbul Convention, Article 37).
- Provide legal and social support for victims of forced and child marriages.
- Address the recognition of foreign child marriages through a framework that balances child protection with the rights of persons already in such marriages.

## Global Perspectives

- **Japan:** The Civil Code was amended in 2022, effective 1 April 2022, to raise the marriage age for women from 16 to 18, equalizing it with the male marriage age of 18. This reform was part of Japan's broader reduction of the age of majority from 20 to 18.
- **India:** The Prohibition of Child Marriage Act, 2006, sets the minimum age at 18 for women and 21 for men. In 2021, the government proposed raising the minimum age for women to 21, but the Prohibition of Child Marriage (Amendment) Bill remained pending. Despite legal prohibitions, India accounts for approximately one-third of global child marriages, with significant regional variation.
- **Brazil:** The Civil Code (Article 1520) previously allowed marriage at 16 with parental consent, with exceptions at any age in cases of pregnancy. In 2019, Law No. 13,811 abolished the pregnancy exception, and the minimum age is now 16 with parental consent and 18 without.
- **Nigeria:** The Child Rights Act (2003) sets a minimum marriage age of 18 in its adopting states, but only 26 of 36 states have adopted the Act, and customary and Islamic law in northern states frequently permits marriage below 18. Nigeria has one of the highest rates of child marriage globally.
- **Australia:** The Marriage Act 1961 (Cth) sets the minimum marriage age at 18, with a narrow exception allowing marriage at 16 with parental consent and court authorization in "exceptional and unusual" circumstances.
- **Saudi Arabia:** Has no codified minimum marriage age, though a 2019 regulation requires court approval for marriages involving persons under 18. In practice, marriages of girls as young as 8-10 have been documented.

## Enforcement Challenges and Implementation Gaps

### Cultural and Social Barriers

Across all jurisdictions, the effectiveness of child marriage laws depends on enforcement capacity and cultural context:

**South Korea:** While formal child marriage is virtually nonexistent due to the registration requirement, advocates have identified concerns about informal unions and cohabitation involving minors, particularly in marginalized communities. The government's focus has shifted to addressing online grooming and exploitation of minors, which may lead to informal coercive relationships even without formal marriage.

**United States:** The persistence of child marriage in 34 states reflects a combination of cultural, religious, and political factors. In some communities, particularly certain religious groups and rural populations, marriage of minors with parental consent is viewed as a culturally acceptable practice. Legislative efforts to ban child marriage have faced opposition from religious liberty advocates, parental rights groups, and legislators who argue that exceptions are necessary for pregnant minors. The Tahirih Justice Center has documented cases where child marriage is used to avoid statutory rape charges, with the marriage effectively providing legal cover for what would otherwise be a criminal sexual relationship.

**Germany:** The primary enforcement challenge relates to foreign marriages: German authorities must adjudicate the legal status of marriages concluded in countries where child marriage is legal or customary. The influx of refugees from Syria, Afghanistan, and other countries where child marriage is prevalent has created complex legal and humanitarian situations. Social workers report that some young women in child marriages resist annulment, viewing the marriage as a source of stability and legal status, complicating a purely rights-based response.

**ECHR Framework:** The margin of appreciation doctrine means that enforcement varies significantly among Council of Europe member states. While Western European states have largely converged on 18 as the minimum age, some member states retain lower minimums or broader exceptions. The Roma community across Europe presents particular challenges, with traditional marriages sometimes conducted outside the state registration system.

### Intersections with Immigration Law

Child marriage intersects critically with immigration and asylum law across all jurisdictions:

- **United States:** No federal policy prohibits the issuance of spousal visas where the beneficiary is a minor married abroad. The Unchained at Last organization has documented cases where the US government approved marriage-based visa petitions involving child spouses.
- **Germany:** The 2017 Act and subsequent BVerfG ruling specifically addressed the recognition of foreign child marriages for immigration purposes. The current framework provides for annulment of marriages involving parties between 16-18, with the possibility of post-majority validation under the 2024 amendments.
- **South Korea:** Foreign marriages involving a party under 18 are not recognized for immigration purposes. The Immigration Control Act (출입국관리법) requires age verification for spousal visa applications.
- **EU/ECHR:** The absence of a harmonized European approach means that a child marriage recognized in one EU member state may not be recognized in another, creating legal uncertainty for families who move within the EU.

## Legal Consequences of Child Marriage

### Capacity and Legal Paradoxes

Child marriage creates significant legal paradoxes across jurisdictions:

- **Contractual Capacity:** In most jurisdictions, minors lack full contractual capacity, yet marriage -- one of the most consequential legal acts -- may be permitted for minors. Married minors may gain emancipation (legal capacity to act as adults) in some US states, while in others they remain unable to file for divorce, retain an attorney, or access domestic violence shelters independently.
- **Sexual Consent:** In many US states, marriage creates an exception to statutory rape laws. If a 15-year-old marries a 25-year-old with parental consent, the sexual relationship becomes legal by virtue of the marriage, even though the same relationship would constitute a criminal offense outside marriage.
- **Education:** Child marriage is strongly correlated with school dropout. South Korea's compulsory education system (mandatory through middle school, age 15) creates a structural safeguard, while Germany's compulsory education laws (typically to age 18) similarly protect against early marriage. In the US, married minors may be exempt from compulsory education requirements in some states.
- **Divorce Rights:** Several US states have documented that minors who are legally married cannot independently petition for divorce because they lack the legal capacity to file a lawsuit. This creates a trap where a minor can enter marriage but cannot exit it without parental assistance -- the same parents who may have consented to the marriage in the first place.

### International Law Standards

International instruments establish a framework for child marriage prohibition:

| Instrument | Year | Key Provision |
|-----------|------|---------------|
| Universal Declaration of Human Rights | 1948 | Article 16(2): free and full consent required |
| Convention on Consent to Marriage | 1962 | Article 2: states shall specify a minimum marriage age |
| CEDAW | 1979 | Article 16(2): child marriages "shall have no legal effect" |
| Convention on the Rights of the Child | 1989 | Article 1: child defined as under 18; Article 3: best interests standard |
| Istanbul Convention | 2011 | Article 37: criminalize forced marriage; Article 32: prohibit marriage of children |
| SDG Target 5.3 | 2015 | Eliminate child, early, and forced marriage by 2030 |

Despite these instruments, enforcement mechanisms remain weak. The CRC Committee has repeatedly called on the United States (which signed but never ratified the CRC) to establish a federal minimum marriage age of 18 with no exceptions.
