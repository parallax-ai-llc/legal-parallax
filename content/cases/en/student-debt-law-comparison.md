## Summary

Student debt regulation -- the legal framework governing student loans, repayment obligations, forgiveness programs, and borrower protections -- has become one of the most pressing policy issues in advanced economies, as the cost of higher education has risen dramatically and student debt burdens have reached historic levels with profound implications for economic mobility, housing markets, and generational wealth. South Korea's student loan system is administered primarily through the Korea Student Aid Foundation (한국장학재단, KOSAF), which provides government-guaranteed income-contingent loans (취업 후 학자금 상환 특별법, ICL) under the Special Act on Repayment of Student Loans After Employment enacted in 2010, alongside direct loans (일반 상환 학자금 대출), with repayment commencing when the borrower's annual income exceeds a threshold (currently approximately 26.8 million KRW), and the government has progressively expanded loan forgiveness for public service workers and reduced interest rates to ease the burden. The United States faces a student debt crisis of unparalleled scale, with outstanding federal student loan debt exceeding $1.7 trillion held by approximately 43 million borrowers, regulated through Title IV of the Higher Education Act and administered by the Department of Education; the Supreme Court's decision in Biden v. Nebraska (2023) struck down the Biden administration's broad student loan forgiveness plan under the HEROES Act, while the SAVE (Saving on a Valuable Education) repayment plan has been challenged in litigation, and the bankruptcy treatment of student loans under 11 U.S.C. Section 523(a)(8) requiring proof of "undue hardship" remains one of the most criticized provisions in American consumer law. Germany maintains a distinctive hybrid system through the Federal Training Assistance Act (Bundesausbildungsförderungsgesetz, BAföG), which provides need-based grants and interest-free loans to students, with the loan portion capped at EUR 10,010 and repayable in income-adjusted installments beginning five years after the maximum funding period, creating a fundamentally different student debt landscape than the American or Korean models. At the European level, the EU does not harmonize student loan systems but supports cross-border student mobility through the Erasmus+ program and the European Student Loan Guarantee Facility (discontinued after a pilot phase), while the European Court of Human Rights has addressed education financing through the right to education under Protocol 1, Article 2, and several member states have adopted income-contingent loan systems modeled in part on the Australian and UK experiences.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's student loan system is governed by the Special Act on Repayment of Student Loans After Employment (취업 후 학자금 상환 특별법), enacted in 2010, and administered by the Korea Student Aid Foundation (한국장학재단, KOSAF), established in 2009 under the Korea Student Aid Foundation Act.

### Loan Types

| Type | Description | Interest Rate (2024) | Repayment |
|------|-------------|---------------------|-----------|
| Income-Contingent Loan (ICL, 취업 후 상환 학자금 대출) | Repayment linked to income after employment | 1.7% | Begins when annual income exceeds threshold (~26.8M KRW) |
| Direct Loan (일반 상환 학자금 대출) | Standard loan with fixed repayment schedule | 1.7% | Begins after graduation or withdrawal |
| Living Expense Loan (생활비 대출) | Supplementary loan for living costs | 1.7% | Follows associated loan terms |

### Income-Contingent Loan (ICL) System

The ICL system, modeled in part on the Australian HECS-HELP system and the UK system, is the centerpiece of Korean student aid policy:
- **Eligibility:** Students from families below the income decile 8 threshold attending accredited higher education institutions.
- **Repayment Threshold:** Repayment obligations begin when the borrower's annual income exceeds the statutory threshold, which is adjusted annually (approximately 26.8 million KRW in 2024, roughly $20,000).
- **Repayment Rate:** Progressive rates from 3% to 10% of income above the threshold, collected through the National Tax Service.
- **Forgiveness:** Remaining balances may be forgiven after an extended repayment period, and specific forgiveness programs exist for public service workers, military service members, and persons with disabilities.

### Recent Reforms

The Korean government has progressively expanded student aid and reduced loan burdens:
- **Interest Rate Reductions:** The ICL interest rate was reduced from 2.7% in 2018 to 1.7% in 2024.
- **Tuition Freeze:** The government has maintained an effective tuition freeze at public universities since 2010 through funding agreements with universities.
- **National Scholarship Program (국가장학금):** Provides means-tested grants covering up to full tuition for students from lower-income families, significantly reducing the need for loans. In 2024, the National Scholarship covered approximately 1.8 million students.
- **Half-Price Tuition Policy (반값등록금):** The government's longstanding policy goal of reducing tuition to half of 2011 levels has been partially achieved through the National Scholarship expansion.

### Debt Burden

South Korea's student debt burden, while significant domestically, is substantially smaller than the American crisis. Total outstanding student loan debt is approximately 14 trillion KRW ($10.5 billion), held by roughly 1.5 million borrowers. The average debt per borrower is approximately 9 million KRW ($6,750). Default rates have remained below 3%, aided by the ICL system's income-contingent design.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The American student loan system is governed primarily by Title IV of the Higher Education Act of 1965 (20 U.S.C. §§1070-1099e) and administered by the Department of Education through the Federal Student Aid office.

### Federal Loan Programs

| Program | Description | Annual Limit (Dependent Undergrad) | Interest Rate (2024-25) |
|---------|-------------|-----------------------------------|------------------------|
| Direct Subsidized | Need-based; no interest during enrollment | $3,500-5,500 | 6.53% |
| Direct Unsubsidized | Non-need-based | $2,000-7,000 | 6.53% (undergrad); 8.08% (grad) |
| Direct PLUS | For parents and graduate students | Cost of attendance minus other aid | 9.08% |
| Direct Consolidation | Combines existing federal loans | N/A | Weighted average of consolidated loans |

### Repayment Plans

| Plan | Monthly Payment | Forgiveness Timeline |
|------|----------------|---------------------|
| Standard | Fixed over 10 years | None |
| Graduated | Starts low, increases every 2 years | None |
| Income-Based Repayment (IBR) | 10-15% of discretionary income | 20-25 years |
| Pay As You Earn (PAYE) | 10% of discretionary income | 20 years |
| SAVE | 5% (undergrad) or 10% (grad) of income above 225% FPL | 20 years (undergrad) or 25 years (grad) |
| Income-Contingent (ICR) | 20% of discretionary income or fixed 12-year payment | 25 years |

### Biden v. Nebraska, 600 U.S. 477 (2023)

The Supreme Court struck down the Biden administration's student loan forgiveness plan, which would have cancelled up to $10,000 in federal student debt for borrowers earning under $125,000 and up to $20,000 for Pell Grant recipients. The 6-3 decision held that the HEROES Act of 2003 did not authorize the Secretary of Education to cancel approximately $430 billion in student debt, applying the major questions doctrine to require clear congressional authorization for such economically significant action.

### Bankruptcy Treatment

Section 523(a)(8) of the Bankruptcy Code provides that student loans are non-dischargeable in bankruptcy unless the debtor demonstrates "undue hardship." The majority of circuits apply the Brunner test (Brunner v. New York State Higher Education Services Corp., 1987), requiring proof that: (1) the debtor cannot maintain a minimal standard of living while repaying; (2) additional circumstances exist indicating this state will persist; and (3) the debtor has made good faith efforts to repay. The Department of Justice issued guidance in 2022 directing U.S. Attorneys to more readily consent to discharge where appropriate, but the statutory standard remains unchanged.

### Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF)

The PSLF program, established in 2007, forgives remaining federal loan balances after 120 qualifying monthly payments while working full-time for a qualifying public service employer. The program was plagued by administrative failures in its early years, with a 98% rejection rate for initial applications. The Biden administration's temporary PSLF waiver (October 2021-October 2022) and subsequent regulatory reforms significantly expanded approvals, with over 900,000 borrowers receiving forgiveness totaling more than $62 billion through 2024.

### Scale of the Crisis

The U.S. student debt crisis is unprecedented in scale: approximately $1.77 trillion in outstanding federal student loan debt held by 43.5 million borrowers, with an average balance of approximately $37,000. Student debt exceeds credit card debt and auto loan debt, making it the second-largest category of consumer debt after mortgages. The crisis disproportionately affects Black borrowers, who owe an average of $25,000 more than white borrowers four years after graduation.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's approach to student financing is fundamentally different from the American and Korean models, reflecting the principle that higher education should be accessible regardless of financial means.

**Bundesausbildungsförderungsgesetz (BAföG):** Enacted in 1971, BAföG provides need-based financial support to students. The key feature is its structure as a 50% grant and 50% interest-free loan, with the loan portion capped at EUR 10,010 (regardless of how much BAföG was received).

### BAföG Structure

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Eligibility | Need-based; dependent on parental income and assets |
| Maximum Monthly Rate | EUR 934 (2024, living independently) |
| Grant Portion | 50% of total support |
| Loan Portion | 50% of total support; capped at EUR 10,010 |
| Interest Rate | 0% (interest-free) |
| Repayment Start | 5 years after end of maximum funding period |
| Repayment Amount | EUR 130/month (minimum installment) |
| Forgiveness | Automatic discharge of remaining debt 20 years after repayment start (since 2020 reform) |
| Early Repayment Discount | Lump-sum early repayment reduces total amount owed |

### 2022 BAföG Reform (27. BAföG-Änderungsgesetz)

The 27th BAföG Amendment, effective August 2022, represented the most significant reform in years:
- **Increased Rates:** Maximum monthly support raised to EUR 934 for students living independently.
- **Expanded Eligibility:** Income thresholds for parental income raised, expanding the number of eligible students.
- **Housing Allowance:** Increased to EUR 360/month.
- **Age Limit Relaxed:** The previous age limit of 30 (35 for master's programs) was relaxed for students with children or other qualifying circumstances.

### Tuition-Free Higher Education

The most fundamental difference between Germany and the United States/Korea is the near-absence of tuition at public universities. Between 2006 and 2014, several Länder experimented with tuition fees of approximately EUR 500 per semester, but all subsequently abolished them following student protests and political opposition. As of 2026, no German Land charges general tuition for first-degree programs at public universities. Students pay only a semester contribution (Semesterbeitrag) of approximately EUR 150-400, covering student union services and public transportation.

This means that the German "student debt problem" is fundamentally different in character: it consists almost entirely of BAföG loans for living expenses, capped at EUR 10,010, interest-free, and automatically forgiven after 20 years. There is no equivalent to the American phenomenon of six-figure educational debt.

### KfW Studienkredit

The Kreditanstalt für Wiederaufbau (KfW) offers a supplementary student loan (Studienkredit) for living expenses, providing up to EUR 650/month regardless of parental income. Unlike BAföG, the KfW loan is not need-based and charges interest (variable rate). During the COVID-19 pandemic, the interest rate was temporarily set to 0%. The KfW Studienkredit functions as a safety net for students who do not qualify for BAföG or need additional support.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Framework

The EU does not harmonize student loan systems. Article 165 TFEU limits EU competence in education to supporting and supplementing member state action. However, several EU-level initiatives are relevant:

**Erasmus+ Program:** The primary EU education mobility program provides grants (not loans) for study abroad. The program budget for 2021-2027 is EUR 26.2 billion, supporting approximately 10 million participants.

**European Student Loan Guarantee Facility:** A pilot program under Erasmus+ (2015-2020) provided guarantees for cross-border master's degree student loans up to EUR 12,000 (one year) or EUR 18,000 (two years). The facility was discontinued after the pilot phase due to limited uptake and high administrative costs.

**European Pillar of Social Rights (Principle 1):** "Everyone has the right to quality and inclusive education, training and life-long learning." While aspirational, this principle supports member state efforts to ensure affordable higher education.

### Comparative European Models

European member states employ diverse student financing models:

| Country | Model | Key Features |
|---------|-------|-------------|
| United Kingdom | Income-contingent loans | Tuition up to £9,250/year; repayment at 9% above threshold; forgiveness after 40 years (Plan 5) |
| Netherlands | Grant-based (since 2023) | Returned to basic grant system after abandoning loans; supplementary loans available |
| Sweden | Combination grant/loan | CSN provides grants and low-interest loans; income-contingent repayment |
| France | Near-free tuition | Public university fees of ~€170-380/year; means-tested grants (bourses sur critères sociaux) |
| Denmark | Grant-based (SU) | Generous state education grant; supplementary loans available |

### ECHR Jurisprudence

The ECHR has not directly addressed student debt but has established relevant principles:

**Protocol 1, Article 2:** The right to education does not require free higher education, but states must ensure that financial barriers do not effectively deny access. The Court has recognized states' wide margin of appreciation in setting tuition fees and designing financial aid systems.

**Article 1, Protocol 1 (Property):** Student loan obligations constitute "possessions" within the meaning of this provision, and disproportionate repayment requirements or retroactive changes to loan terms could potentially engage property rights.

## Comparative Analysis

### Scale and Structure

The most striking comparative finding is the vast difference in scale. The United States' $1.77 trillion student debt burden dwarfs South Korea's $10.5 billion and Germany's minimal capped-loan system. This disparity reflects fundamental structural differences: German tuition-free education eliminates the need for tuition-related borrowing; South Korea's government-controlled tuition freeze and generous scholarship program limit borrowing; American deregulation of tuition at both public and private institutions has created a debt spiral.

### Income Contingency

All three loan-based systems have moved toward income-contingent repayment, but with different designs. South Korea's ICL makes income-contingent repayment the default for qualifying borrowers. The United States offers income-driven repayment as an option among multiple plans, creating complexity. Germany's BAföG repayment is effectively income-contingent through hardship deferral provisions, though the cap at EUR 10,010 makes income contingency less critical.

### Bankruptcy Treatment

The United States is a global outlier in its treatment of student loans in bankruptcy. Both South Korea and Germany allow student loan discharge through standard insolvency proceedings. The "undue hardship" standard under 11 U.S.C. §523(a)(8) has been criticized as arbitrary, inconsistent across circuits, and a barrier to fresh starts for financially distressed borrowers.

### Forgiveness and Moral Hazard

All three systems incorporate forgiveness mechanisms, but with different approaches to moral hazard. Germany's automatic 20-year forgiveness combined with the EUR 10,010 cap creates minimal moral hazard risk. South Korea's ICL includes forgiveness provisions tied to public service and extended repayment. The United States' multiple forgiveness programs (PSLF, IDR forgiveness) have created both opportunities and administrative complexity, with ongoing political debate about the fairness and fiscal responsibility of broad forgiveness.
