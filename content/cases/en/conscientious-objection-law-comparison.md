## Summary

Conscientious objection -- the refusal to perform military service or bear arms on the grounds of freedom of thought, conscience, or religion -- stands at the intersection of individual liberty and state security, generating profoundly different legal responses across jurisdictions depending on whether a country maintains conscription and how it balances military necessity against fundamental rights. South Korea, which maintains universal male conscription under the Military Service Act (병역법), criminalized conscientious objection for decades until the Constitutional Court (헌법재판소) ruled in 2018 (2011헌바379 등) that the absence of an alternative service option violated the Constitution; the Alternative Service Act (대체역의 편입 및 복무 등에 관한 법률) took effect in January 2020, providing for 36 months of service in correctional facilities, though critics argue the excessive length and restrictive placement constitute a punitive rather than genuine alternative. The United States has recognized conscientious objector status since the colonial era, with the current framework under the Military Selective Service Act (50 U.S.C. Section 3806(j)) exempting from combatant training and service persons who, by reason of religious training and belief, are conscientiously opposed to participation in war in any form, as interpreted expansively by the Supreme Court in United States v. Seeger (1965) and Welsh v. United States (1970) to include deeply held moral and ethical beliefs not rooted in traditional religious faith. Germany enshrined the right to conscientious objection in Article 4(3) of the Grundgesetz (Basic Law) from its founding in 1949, providing that "no person shall be compelled against his conscience to render military service involving the use of arms," and operated one of the world's most generous alternative service (Zivildienst) systems until conscription was suspended in 2011. The European Court of Human Rights recognized conscientious objection as protected under Article 9 (freedom of thought, conscience, and religion) in the Grand Chamber judgment Bayatyan v. Armenia (2011), reversing decades of prior jurisprudence, while the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights explicitly protects the right in Article 10(2).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's treatment of conscientious objection represents one of the most dramatic legal transformations in comparative military law. For nearly seven decades, the Military Service Act (병역법) imposed criminal penalties on all persons refusing to perform compulsory military service, with no provision for alternative service. Article 88(1) of the Military Service Act prescribed imprisonment of up to three years for failure to comply with enlistment orders.

Between the 1950s and 2018, an estimated 19,000 conscientious objectors were imprisoned, the vast majority being Jehovah's Witnesses. South Korea consistently ranked as the world's largest incarcerator of conscientious objectors, accounting at times for over 90 percent of the world's imprisoned COs.

### Constitutional Court Decision (2018)

On June 28, 2018, the Constitutional Court ruled in Decision 2011헌바379 등 that the absence of an alternative service provision was unconstitutional. The Court found that while the state has a legitimate interest in national defense, the complete denial of any alternative to military service disproportionately infringed upon the freedom of conscience guaranteed by Article 19 of the Constitution (양심의 자유). The Court ordered the National Assembly to enact alternative service legislation by December 31, 2019.

Key elements of the decision:
- The Court explicitly recognized that conscientious objection based on sincere religious or personal conviction constitutes a protected exercise of freedom of conscience
- The absence of alternative service failed the proportionality test, as less restrictive means were available to serve national defense interests
- The decision was rendered by a 6-3 vote, with three justices dissenting on national security grounds

### Supreme Court Decision (2018)

On November 1, 2018, the Supreme Court of Korea (대법원) issued its en banc decision in Case 2016Do10912, reversing decades of precedent by holding that sincere conscientious objection constitutes a "justifiable cause" (정당한 사유) for refusing military service under Article 88(1) of the Military Service Act. The Court acquitted the defendant, a Jehovah's Witness who had refused enlistment.

### Alternative Service Act (2019)

The National Assembly enacted the Act on the Assignment and Performance of Alternative Service (대체역의 편입 및 복무 등에 관한 법률) on December 27, 2019, effective January 1, 2020.

| Feature | Provision |
|---------|-----------|
| Duration | 36 months (approximately double the standard military service of 18-21 months) |
| Placement | Correctional facilities operated by the Ministry of Justice |
| Eligibility | Persons whose sincere conscience makes military service impossible |
| Review Body | Alternative Service Review Committee (대체역심사위원회) |
| Living Conditions | Required to reside at the correctional facility for the service period |

### Criticisms

The UN Human Rights Committee, in its 2023 Concluding Observations on South Korea (CCPR/C/KOR/CO/5), expressed concern that the alternative service regime may be punitive in nature, noting the 36-month duration (nearly double military service), the restriction to correctional facility placement, and the residential requirement as factors that may deter genuine conscientious objectors and constitute de facto punishment.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has a long history of recognizing conscientious objection, predating independence. Colonial-era exemptions for Quakers, Mennonites, and other peace churches were incorporated into early militia laws. The current statutory framework derives from the Military Selective Service Act.

**50 U.S.C. Section 3806(j):** "Nothing contained in this title shall be construed to require any person to be subject to combatant training and service in the armed forces of the United States who, by reason of religious training and belief, is conscientiously opposed to participation in war in any form."

The statute defines "religious training and belief" as "an individual's belief in a relation to a Supreme Being involving duties superior to those arising from any human relation, but does not include essentially political, sociological, or philosophical views or a merely personal moral code."

### Supreme Court Expansion

**United States v. Seeger, 380 U.S. 163 (1965):** The Supreme Court unanimously broadened the statutory definition of "religious training and belief," holding that the test is "whether a given belief that is sincere and meaningful occupies a place in the life of its possessor parallel to that filled by the orthodox belief in God of one who clearly qualifies for the exemption." The Court granted CO status to Daniel Seeger, who expressed a "skepticism or disbelief in the existence of God" but held deep conscientious scruples against war.

**Welsh v. United States, 398 U.S. 333 (1970):** The Court extended CO eligibility further, holding that deeply held moral, ethical, or religious beliefs that function as religion in the registrant's life qualify for exemption, even if the registrant does not characterize his beliefs as "religious." The plurality opinion effectively eliminated the requirement that opposition to war derive from a belief in a Supreme Being.

**Gillette v. United States, 401 U.S. 437 (1971):** The Court upheld the requirement that conscientious objection must extend to participation in war "in any form," rejecting selective objection to particular wars. Guy Gillette's objection specifically to the Vietnam War did not qualify.

### CO Classification System

| Classification | Description |
|---------------|-------------|
| 1-O | Conscientious objector opposed to both combatant and noncombatant military service |
| 1-A-O | Conscientious objector opposed to combatant service but available for noncombatant military service |

During the Vietnam War era (1964-1973), approximately 170,000 registrants received CO classifications, while an estimated 570,000 evaded the draft through other means. Since the end of conscription in 1973, CO status remains available to active-duty service members who develop conscientious objections during service, governed by DoD Directive 1300.06.

### Historical Prosecution

During World War I, approximately 450 COs were court-martialed and imprisoned. During World War II, approximately 6,000 COs performed alternative service in Civilian Public Service (CPS) camps, while approximately 6,000 more served prison sentences for refusing both military and alternative service. The most notable prosecution involved the "Fort Hood Three" in 1966, three soldiers who refused deployment to Vietnam.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany provides the strongest constitutional protection for conscientious objection among the jurisdictions compared. Article 4(3) of the Grundgesetz states: "No person shall be compelled against his conscience to render military service involving the use of arms. Details shall be regulated by a federal law."

This provision, included in the Basic Law from its adoption in 1949, reflected the drafters' determination to prevent a recurrence of the compulsory military service that had been instrumentalized by the Nazi regime. The right is absolute and unconditional -- it does not require proof of religious belief, membership in a peace church, or opposition to all wars.

### Zivildienst (Civilian Alternative Service)

When conscription was active (1956-2011), conscientious objectors were required to perform Zivildienst (civilian alternative service) under the Zivildienstgesetz (Civilian Service Act). The system evolved significantly:

| Period | Duration of Zivildienst | Comparison to Military Service |
|--------|------------------------|-------------------------------|
| 1961-1983 | Same as military service | Equal duration |
| 1983-1990 | Military service + 3 months | Slightly longer |
| 1990-2004 | 13 months (military: 10 months) | 3 months longer |
| 2004-2011 | 9 months (military: 9 months) | Equal duration |

At its peak, the Zivildienst system employed approximately 130,000 young men annually in hospitals, nursing homes, ambulance services, and social welfare organizations. The system became so integral to Germany's healthcare and social service infrastructure that its suspension in 2011 (alongside conscription) necessitated the creation of the Bundesfreiwilligendienst (Federal Voluntary Service) to fill the gap.

### Recognition Procedure

Unlike most conscription-based systems, Germany's recognition procedure evolved from requiring applicants to demonstrate their sincerity before an examination committee (including oral hearings) to a simplified written application process. The Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) held in several decisions that the examination procedure must not impose excessive burdens on the applicant and that the state bears the burden of demonstrating that an application is not genuine (BVerfGE 12, 45; BVerfGE 69, 1).

### Suspension of Conscription

On March 24, 2011, the Bundestag voted to suspend compulsory military service (Aussetzung der Wehrpflicht), effective July 1, 2011. The suspension also ended the Zivildienst. The constitutional provisions on conscientious objection (Article 4(3) GG) and conscription (Article 12a GG) remain in force and could be reactivated by parliamentary decision. As of 2026, policy discussions regarding potential reinstatement of conscription in light of the changed European security environment have intensified, with implications for the dormant conscientious objection framework.

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

**Bayatyan v. Armenia (Application No. 23459/03, Grand Chamber Judgment of July 7, 2011):** In a historic reversal of prior jurisprudence, the Grand Chamber held by 16 votes to 1 that Armenia's conviction and imprisonment of a Jehovah's Witness for refusing military service, in the absence of genuine alternative civilian service, violated Article 9 of the Convention (freedom of thought, conscience, and religion). The Court overruled its previous approach, exemplified by decisions such as Autio v. Finland (1991), which had held that Article 9 did not guarantee a right to conscientious objection.

Key findings:
- Opposition to military service, where motivated by a serious and insurmountable conflict between the obligation to serve and a person's conscience or deeply held beliefs, constitutes a conviction of sufficient cogency, seriousness, cohesion, and importance to attract the guarantees of Article 9
- The right to conscientious objection is not an absolute right to exemption from all service; states may impose alternative service of a genuinely civilian nature
- Alternative service must not be punitive or deterrent in nature

**Savda v. Turkey (Application No. 42730/05, Judgment of June 12, 2012):** The Court found violations of Articles 3 and 9 in Turkey's treatment of a conscientious objector who was repeatedly prosecuted and imprisoned for refusing military service. Turkey had no alternative service provision.

**Adyan and Others v. Armenia (Application No. 75604/11, Judgment of October 12, 2017):** The Court found that Armenia's alternative service regime violated Article 9 because alternative service was supervised by the military authorities rather than being genuinely civilian in nature.

### EU Charter of Fundamental Rights

Article 10(2) of the Charter of Fundamental Rights of the European Union explicitly provides: "The right to conscientious objection is recognised, in accordance with the national laws governing the exercise of this right." This provision, which has no equivalent in the ECHR text itself, represents a codification of the right at EU primary law level.

### UN Human Rights Committee

The UN Human Rights Committee has interpreted Article 18 of the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR) as encompassing a right to conscientious objection. In General Comment No. 22 (1993), the Committee stated that the right to freedom of thought, conscience, and religion under Article 18 includes the right to conscientious objection to military service. In Yoon and Choi v. Republic of Korea (Communications Nos. 1321-1322/2004, 2006), the Committee found South Korea in violation of Article 18 for imprisoning Jehovah's Witnesses who refused military service.

### Conscientious Objection During Service

A distinct issue from pre-service conscientious objection is the development of conscientious beliefs during active military service:

**South Korea:** Service members who develop conscientious objections after entering military service face particular difficulty. The Alternative Service Act applies primarily to persons who object before enlisting. In-service objectors may face disciplinary action and criminal prosecution for refusal to obey orders.

**United States:** Department of Defense Directive 1300.06 provides a process for active-duty service members who develop conscientious objections during service. Applicants must demonstrate a sincere belief that developed after entry into service. If approved, the service member may be discharged or reassigned to noncombatant duties. Between 2002 and 2024, several hundred applications were filed, with approval rates varying by service and era.

**Germany:** Since conscription is suspended, the issue primarily arises for professional soldiers (Berufssoldaten) and fixed-term soldiers (Soldaten auf Zeit). The Grundgesetz Article 4(3) right applies equally to those who develop objections during service. A soldier who successfully claims conscientious objection must be released from service.

### Total Objection

"Total objectors" -- persons who refuse both military service and any form of alternative civilian service -- present a particularly challenging case:

- **South Korea:** Total objectors are not accommodated; refusal of alternative service would constitute a criminal offense
- **United States:** Not applicable (no conscription), but historically, total objectors during draft eras were prosecuted
- **Germany:** During the Zivildienst era, total objectors were subject to criminal prosecution. The Federal Constitutional Court did not recognize a right to refuse alternative service
- **ECHR:** In Bayatyan v. Armenia, the Court did not address total objection. In Savda v. Turkey (2012), the repeated prosecution of a total objector was found to violate Article 3 (degrading treatment) due to the cumulative effect of multiple criminal proceedings and imprisonment

## Comparative Analysis

### Constitutional Protection

Germany provides the strongest constitutional protection, with an express and unconditional right to refuse armed military service. South Korea's Constitutional Court derived the right from the general freedom of conscience provision (Article 19) only in 2018. The U.S. treats conscientious objection as a statutory entitlement rather than a constitutional right -- Congress could theoretically eliminate the exemption. The ECHR recognizes it under Article 9 jurisprudence since 2011.

### Grounds for Recognition

Germany has the broadest grounds, recognizing any conscience-based objection without requiring religious motivation or opposition to all wars. The U.S. statute references "religious training and belief" but Supreme Court interpretation has expanded this to include deeply held moral and ethical beliefs. South Korea recognizes both religious and personal conscience-based objections. The ECHR requires beliefs of "sufficient cogency, seriousness, cohesion and importance."

### Alternative Service Design

The quality and design of alternative service varies dramatically. Germany's Zivildienst was widely regarded as a model system -- equal in duration to military service, genuinely civilian in nature, and integrated into the national social service infrastructure. South Korea's alternative service has been criticized as punitive: 36 months (nearly double military service), restricted to correctional facility work, with mandatory residence at the facility. The ECHR requires that alternative service be neither punitive nor deterrent.

### Selective Objection

No jurisdiction fully recognizes selective conscientious objection -- opposition to a specific war rather than all wars. The U.S. Supreme Court explicitly rejected it in Gillette. The ECHR has not directly addressed the question. This remains a significant doctrinal gap, as many potential objectors hold views that are war-specific rather than pacifist in nature.
