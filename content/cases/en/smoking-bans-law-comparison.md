## Summary

Smoking bans -- legal prohibitions on tobacco smoking in designated public spaces, workplaces, and other environments -- have proliferated globally since the early 2000s as scientific evidence of the health harms of secondhand smoke exposure solidified, evolving from voluntary courtesy measures to comprehensive statutory mandates backed by significant penalties. South Korea has progressively expanded its smoking restrictions through the National Health Promotion Act (국민건강증진법), originally enacted in 1995, with major amendments in 2003, 2010, 2012, and 2015 that extended indoor smoking bans from large public buildings to virtually all enclosed public spaces, restaurants, bars, and cafes regardless of size; outdoor smoking is prohibited within 10 meters of building entrances under Article 9(5), and designated smoking areas are mandated in public parks, bus stops, and pedestrian zones under local government ordinances. The United States lacks a single federal indoor smoking ban for private workplaces, instead relying on a patchwork of state and local laws, with 28 states and the District of Columbia having enacted comprehensive smoke-free workplace, restaurant, and bar laws as of 2025; federal regulation operates through the Pro-Children Act of 1994 (20 U.S.C. Section 6083), prohibiting smoking in federally funded children's facilities, and the Family Smoking Prevention and Tobacco Control Act of 2009 (21 U.S.C. Section 387 et seq.) granting the FDA authority to regulate tobacco products. Germany enacted a federal ban on smoking in federal government buildings and public transportation through the Bundesnichtraucherschutzgesetz (Federal Non-Smokers Protection Act) of 2007, but comprehensive workplace and hospitality venue bans are determined by the sixteen Länder, resulting in significant regional variation; Bavaria enacted the strictest ban following a 2010 referendum (Volksentscheid), while other Länder permit designated smoking rooms. At the European level, the Council Recommendation 2009/C 296/02 on smoke-free environments urged member states to adopt comprehensive smoking bans, the revised Tobacco Products Directive 2014/40/EU regulates product standards and packaging, and the European Court of Human Rights has upheld the legitimacy of smoking bans as proportionate restrictions on private life under the state's margin of appreciation in public health matters.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea has implemented one of the most comprehensive and progressively expanding smoking ban frameworks in Asia, driven primarily by the National Health Promotion Act (국민건강증진법).

**National Health Promotion Act (국민건강증진법) -- Article 9:** This central provision designates smoke-free zones (금연구역) and has been amended repeatedly to expand coverage:

- **1995 (Original):** Smoking restricted in large public buildings (government offices, hospitals, schools) exceeding 1,000 square meters.
- **2003 Amendment:** Extended coverage to public transportation, taxis, and smaller public buildings.
- **2010 Amendment:** All public buildings regardless of size designated as smoke-free.
- **2012 Amendment:** Restaurants and cafes larger than 150 square meters required to be smoke-free or provide designated smoking rooms.
- **2015 Amendment:** All restaurants, cafes, bars, and entertainment venues designated smoke-free regardless of size; designated smoking rooms abolished in restaurants.

**Article 9(5) -- Outdoor Smoking Restrictions:** Smoking is prohibited within 10 meters of entrances to smoke-free buildings. Local governments are authorized to designate additional outdoor smoke-free zones.

**Article 9(6) -- Penalties:** Individuals who smoke in designated smoke-free zones face fines of KRW 100,000 (approximately $85). Building owners or operators who fail to post no-smoking signs, install required signage, or allow smoking face fines of KRW 1.7 million to KRW 5 million.

**Tobacco Business Act (담배사업법):** This statute governs tobacco product manufacturing, importation, and sales. The 2016 amendment brought electronic cigarettes (전자담배) and heated tobacco products under the regulatory framework, subjecting them to the same smoke-free zone requirements.

### Local Government Ordinances

Metropolitan and provincial governments have exercised their authority under Article 9-2 of the National Health Promotion Act to designate additional outdoor smoke-free zones. Seoul Metropolitan Government has designated smoke-free zones in major pedestrian areas including Gwanghwamun Square, Myeongdong, and areas surrounding subway station entrances. Busan, Daegu, and Incheon have implemented similar ordinances covering tourist districts, public parks, and beach areas. As of 2024, over 120,000 outdoor smoke-free zones have been designated nationwide.

### Tobacco Taxation and Warning Labels

South Korea sharply increased the tobacco tax in January 2015, raising the price of a standard pack of cigarettes from KRW 2,500 to KRW 4,500 -- an 80 percent increase. Graphic health warnings covering 50 percent of the cigarette pack surface area became mandatory in December 2016 under amended provisions of the National Health Promotion Act. The graphic warnings were expanded to cover 75 percent of the pack surface in 2021.

### Smoking Prevalence

The adult male smoking rate in South Korea has declined from approximately 66.3 percent in 1998 to 29.4 percent in 2023, according to the Korea Disease Control and Prevention Agency. The female smoking rate has remained relatively stable at approximately 6-7 percent, though health officials believe actual female smoking rates are higher due to social stigma affecting self-reporting.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States employs a decentralized regulatory model for smoking bans, with federal law addressing specific contexts and state/local governments implementing comprehensive workplace and public space restrictions.

**Federal Provisions:**

**Pro-Children Act of 1994 (20 U.S.C. Section 6083):** Prohibits smoking within any indoor facility that provides federally funded services to children, including schools, libraries, and day care centers.

**Family Smoking Prevention and Tobacco Control Act of 2009 (21 U.S.C. Section 387 et seq.):** Grants the FDA authority to regulate the manufacture, distribution, and marketing of tobacco products. While the Act does not directly impose smoking bans, it authorizes the FDA to set product standards, require warning labels, and restrict marketing to youth. The Act created the Center for Tobacco Products within the FDA.

**Federal Aviation Administration regulations (14 C.F.R. Section 252):** Smoking has been prohibited on all domestic airline flights since 1990 and all international flights by U.S. carriers since 2000.

**Executive Order 13058 (1997):** Prohibits smoking in all interior spaces owned, rented, or leased by the federal executive branch, covering approximately 1.8 million federal employees.

**State and Local Laws:** As of 2025, 28 states and the District of Columbia have enacted comprehensive smoke-free laws covering workplaces, restaurants, and bars. Notable state laws include:

| State | Year | Key Features |
|-------|------|-------------|
| California | 1998 (bars added) | First state to ban smoking in bars; Health and Safety Code Section 118875 |
| New York | 2003 | Clean Indoor Air Act covers all workplaces, restaurants, bars |
| Ohio | 2006 | Smoke Free Workplace Act (ballot initiative) |
| Illinois | 2008 | Smoke-Free Illinois Act (410 ILCS 82) |

**Preemption Issues:** Several states have enacted preemption laws prohibiting local governments from enacting smoking restrictions stronger than state law, creating regulatory gaps. As of 2025, approximately 14 states preempt local smoking ordinances to varying degrees.

### Landmark Litigation

**EPA v. Philip Morris (D.C. Cir., 2000):** The federal court upheld the EPA's 1993 classification of secondhand smoke as a Group A carcinogen, establishing the scientific basis for workplace smoking bans.

**United States v. Philip Morris USA (D.D.C. 2006):** Judge Gladys Kessler found major tobacco companies liable for racketeering under RICO, establishing that the tobacco industry had engaged in a decades-long conspiracy to deceive the public about the health effects of smoking and secondhand smoke.

### E-Cigarette Regulation

The FDA asserted jurisdiction over e-cigarettes through its 2016 Deeming Rule (81 Fed. Reg. 28974), bringing all products meeting the statutory definition of "tobacco product" under the Tobacco Control Act. As of 2025, 19 states include e-cigarettes in their clean indoor air laws.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's smoking ban framework reflects its federal structure, with a national baseline law supplemented by diverse Länder-level regulations.

**Bundesnichtraucherschutzgesetz (BNichtrSchG) -- Federal Non-Smokers Protection Act (2007):** This federal statute, which entered into force on September 1, 2007, prohibits smoking in federal government buildings and on public transportation (trains, buses, trams, and stations). The Act does not extend to private workplaces, restaurants, or bars, leaving these to Länder regulation.

**Länder Nichtraucherschutzgesetze (State Non-Smokers Protection Acts):** Each of the sixteen Länder has enacted its own non-smoker protection law, resulting in significant regulatory variation:

| Land | Strictness | Key Features |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| Bayern (Bavaria) | Strictest | Complete ban in all enclosed public spaces including bars and beer tents (following 2010 Volksentscheid) |
| Nordrhein-Westfalen (NRW) | Strict | Comprehensive ban with limited exceptions for owner-operated single-room bars |
| Saarland | Strict | Near-complete ban since 2010 |
| Berlin | Moderate | Ban in restaurants; smoking rooms permitted in bars with separate ventilation |
| Sachsen (Saxony) | Moderate | Designated smoking rooms permitted in hospitality venues |

**Bavarian Volksentscheid (2010):** On July 4, 2010, Bavarian voters approved a referendum (Volksentscheid) establishing Germany's strictest smoking ban. The measure, driven by a citizens' initiative (Volksbegehren) that collected over 1.3 million signatures, imposed a complete indoor smoking ban with no exceptions for Nebenräume (separate smoking rooms), beer tents (Bierzelte), or owner-operated bars. The result -- 61 percent in favor -- was binding and could not be modified by the Bavarian parliament for a period of one year.

### Constitutional Challenges

**BVerfG, Decision of July 30, 2008 (1 BvR 3262/07, 1 BvR 402/08, 1 BvR 906/08):** The Federal Constitutional Court ruled on the constitutionality of Länder smoking bans, holding that comprehensive bans on smoking in hospitality venues were compatible with the Basic Law's protections of occupational freedom (Article 12(1) GG) and property rights (Article 14(1) GG). However, the Court found that bans affecting single-room bars without permitting designated smoking rooms were disproportionate to smaller establishments, requiring either a complete ban for all or exceptions for small owner-operated bars. This decision prompted several Länder to amend their laws to include size-based exceptions.

### Arbeitsstättenverordnung (Workplace Ordinance)

Section 5 of the Arbeitsstättenverordnung (ArbStättV) requires employers to take effective measures to protect non-smoking employees from tobacco smoke exposure, constituting a de facto workplace smoking ban. The ordinance does not apply to hospitality venues, which are regulated by Länder laws.

### Smoking Prevalence

Germany's adult smoking rate was approximately 26.4 percent in 2023, one of the highest in Western Europe. The German Cancer Research Center (Deutsches Krebsforschungszentrum, DKFZ) has been a leading advocate for comprehensive smoking bans and regularly publishes Tabakatlas Deutschland documenting smoking-related health impacts.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Tobacco Products Directive 2014/40/EU

The revised Tobacco Products Directive (TPD), adopted on April 3, 2014, and transposed by member states by May 20, 2016, establishes harmonized rules for tobacco product manufacture, presentation, and sale:

**Health Warnings:** Articles 8-12 require combined (text and picture) health warnings covering 65 percent of both the front and back of cigarette and roll-your-own tobacco packages. Member states may require plain/standardized packaging under Article 24(2).

**E-Cigarettes:** Articles 20 regulates electronic cigarettes, requiring notification to competent authorities, setting maximum nicotine concentration at 20 mg/mL, limiting e-liquid container size to 10 mL and tank capacity to 2 mL, and mandating health warnings and ingredient labeling.

**Traceability:** Articles 15-16 establish a tracking and tracing system for tobacco products to combat illicit trade, fully operational since May 2019 for cigarettes and roll-your-own tobacco.

### Council Recommendation 2009/C 296/02

The Council Recommendation of November 30, 2009, on smoke-free environments invited member states to adopt and implement laws providing effective protection from exposure to tobacco smoke in enclosed workplaces, enclosed public places, public transport, and as appropriate other public places. While non-binding, the Recommendation served as a catalyst for member state action, and by 2025, 24 of 27 EU member states had enacted comprehensive smoke-free legislation.

### WHO Framework Convention on Tobacco Control (FCTC)

The FCTC, adopted on May 21, 2003, and entered into force on February 27, 2005, is the first international treaty negotiated under the auspices of the World Health Organization. Article 8 requires parties to adopt and implement effective measures providing protection from exposure to tobacco smoke in indoor workplaces, indoor public places, public transport, and other appropriate public places. The EU and all EU member states are parties to the FCTC.

### European Court of Human Rights

**Rampogna and Murgia v. Italy (Application No. 40753/04, Decision of May 11, 2010):** The Court declared inadmissible a challenge to Italy's comprehensive indoor smoking ban (Law No. 3 of January 16, 2003), holding that the ban fell within the state's margin of appreciation in protecting public health and did not constitute a disproportionate interference with private life under Article 8 ECHR.

**S.P. and Others v. Russia (Application No. 36463/18, 2022):** The Court addressed a complaint regarding inadequate enforcement of smoking bans in residential buildings, noting that while states have positive obligations to protect individuals from secondhand smoke in shared living spaces, the specific measures adopted fall within the state's margin of appreciation.

## Comparative Analysis

### Scope of Bans

South Korea has achieved the most comprehensive regulatory coverage, banning smoking in virtually all enclosed public spaces, all restaurants and bars regardless of size, and extending restrictions to outdoor areas within 10 meters of building entrances. The United States and Germany exhibit significant internal variation due to their federal structures. The EU provides a framework for harmonization but does not mandate specific smoking bans.

### Enforcement Models

South Korea imposes fixed fines on individual smokers (KRW 100,000) through a decentralized enforcement system involving local government inspectors and citizen reporting mechanisms. The United States relies primarily on venue-level enforcement, with fines directed at establishments rather than individual smokers in most jurisdictions. Germany's enforcement varies by Land but generally involves lower fines (EUR 5-250 for individuals) with inconsistent enforcement.

### E-Cigarette Integration

The jurisdictions differ significantly in their treatment of electronic cigarettes and heated tobacco products. South Korea integrated e-cigarettes into its existing smoke-free zone framework in 2016. The United States treats e-cigarettes under some but not all state clean indoor air laws. Germany's Länder laws vary in their treatment of e-cigarettes, and the EU's TPD regulates product standards but does not mandate indoor use restrictions.

### Cultural Resistance

Smoking bans have faced varying degrees of cultural resistance. Germany's difficulty in implementing a comprehensive national ban reflects the cultural significance of beer gardens and Kneipen (pubs) where smoking has been historically ingrained. South Korea's rapid implementation was facilitated by strong government authority and the collectivist cultural framework that prioritizes public health. The United States' decentralized approach reflects the strong tradition of individual liberty and state autonomy.
