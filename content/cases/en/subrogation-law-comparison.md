## Summary

Subrogation -- the legal mechanism by which one party who has satisfied the obligation of another succeeds to the rights and remedies of the original creditor against the debtor or third-party wrongdoer -- is a foundational doctrine that operates across insurance law, suretyship, guarantor relationships, and the law of obligations generally, serving to prevent unjust enrichment, ensure that the ultimate loss falls on the party responsible, and maintain the integrity of risk allocation in contractual and statutory frameworks. South Korea recognizes subrogation (대위) under multiple provisions of the Civil Act (민법), including Article 481 (legal subrogation upon satisfaction of an obligation, 변제자의 대위), Article 482 (priority among subrogees), and the Commercial Act (상법) Article 682 (insurer's subrogation rights after indemnification), with the Supreme Court of Korea (대법원) developing a substantial body of case law addressing conflicts between original creditors and subrogees, the scope of subrogation in guarantor relationships, and the interaction between subrogation and security interests. The United States treats subrogation as both an equitable doctrine and a contractual right, with the Restatement (Third) of Restitution and Unjust Enrichment (2011) Sections 57-62 providing the most comprehensive modern treatment; conventional (contractual) subrogation is governed by insurance policy terms subject to state regulation, while equitable subrogation is a court-created remedy with significant variation among jurisdictions on issues such as the "volunteer" doctrine, the "made whole" rule, and federal preemption under ERISA (29 U.S.C. Section 1132); the Supreme Court's decision in US Airways, Inc. v. McCutchen, 569 U.S. 88 (2013) clarified that ERISA plan terms govern subrogation in employer-sponsored health plans. Germany provides a comprehensive statutory framework for subrogation through the BGB (Buergerliches Gesetzbuch) Sections 268, 774 (surety's subrogation), 1143 (mortgage debtor), and 1225 (pledgor), as well as the VVG (Versicherungsvertragsgesetz) Section 86 (insurer's subrogation after payment of insurance claims), with the BGH (Bundesgerichtshof) developing principles governing the relationship between the original creditor's priority (Vorrang des Glaeubigers) and the subrogee's acquired rights. The ECHR and EU framework address subrogation primarily through the Brussels I Recast Regulation (Regulation (EU) No 1215/2012) governing jurisdiction for subrogation claims across member states, the Rome II Regulation (Regulation (EC) No 864/2007) Article 19 on subrogation in non-contractual obligations, and the Motor Insurance Directive (Directive 2009/103/EC) establishing cross-border subrogation mechanisms for motor vehicle insurers.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's subrogation regime is anchored in the Civil Act (민법) and the Commercial Act (상법), reflecting a continental legal tradition that treats subrogation as a statutory right arising by operation of law upon satisfaction of another's obligation.

**Civil Act Article 481 -- Legal Subrogation (변제자의 대위):** "A person who has performed an obligation on behalf of the debtor shall be subrogated to the rights of the creditor to the extent of the performance." This provision establishes the fundamental principle that any person who satisfies another's debt acquires the creditor's rights against the debtor by operation of law. The subrogee steps into the shoes of the original creditor, acquiring not only the principal claim but also accessory rights including security interests (담보권), priority rights (우선권), and related guarantees.

**Civil Act Article 482 -- Priority Among Subrogees and Original Creditors:** Article 482 addresses the critical conflict between the original creditor and the subrogee when the obligation has been only partially satisfied. Paragraph 1 provides that a subrogee who has satisfied only part of the obligation may not exercise subrogation rights to the detriment of the original creditor. Paragraph 2 establishes detailed priority rules for multiple subrogees, including:

- A surety (보증인) who pays is subrogated against co-sureties proportionally.
- A third-party provider of collateral and a surety share subrogation rights according to their respective numbers.
- A person who holds collateral in real property has priority over the surety with respect to that property.

**Commercial Act Article 682 -- Insurer's Subrogation:** Article 682(1) provides: "Where an insurer has paid insurance money, the insurer shall be subrogated to the rights of the insured against the third party who caused the loss, to the extent of the amount paid." This right arises automatically upon payment and does not require assignment by the insured. Article 682(2) establishes an anti-subrogation rule: the insurer may not exercise subrogation rights against the insured's household members (가족) or employees, except in cases of intentional conduct.

### Key Case Law

**Supreme Court Decision 2015Da235882 (July 14, 2016):** The Court held that an insurer who has paid under a property insurance policy acquires subrogation rights against the negligent third party under Article 682, and that these rights are limited to the amount actually paid, not the full amount of the insured's loss. Where the insured has not been made whole, the insured's direct claim against the tortfeasor takes priority over the insurer's subrogation claim.

**Supreme Court Decision 2012Da89399 (March 27, 2014):** Addressed the scope of subrogation for guarantors under the Credit Guarantee Fund Act (신용보증기금법). The Court held that a guarantor who satisfies the guaranteed obligation is subrogated not only to the principal claim but to all security interests and accessory rights, including mortgage rights and rights under other guarantee agreements.

**Supreme Court Decision 2018Da248855 (February 13, 2020):** Clarified that the anti-subrogation rule under Article 682(2) applies to employees acting within the scope of their employment, even if the conduct constituting the insured event involved gross negligence, but does not extend to intentional wrongdoing (고의).

### National Health Insurance Subrogation

Under the National Health Insurance Act (국민건강보험법) Article 58, the National Health Insurance Service (NHIS, 국민건강보험공단) has subrogation rights against third parties responsible for injuries that the NHIS has covered. This right has generated significant litigation, particularly in motor vehicle accident cases where the NHIS seeks to recover medical costs from the at-fault driver's insurer.

## United States

### Legal Framework

U.S. subrogation law operates through a dual framework of equitable subrogation (created by courts of equity) and conventional subrogation (created by contract), with significant variation among the fifty states and federal preemption adding further complexity.

**Equitable Subrogation:** The Restatement (Third) of Restitution and Unjust Enrichment (2011) Section 57 provides: "If the claimant's payment of an obligation owed by the defendant to a third person has the effect of discharging or reducing the debt, the claimant is entitled to a lien on property of the defendant to the extent that a lien on the same property previously secured the obligation in question." Equitable subrogation requires that: (1) the subrogee paid the obligation of another; (2) the subrogee was not a volunteer; (3) the subrogee was not primarily liable for the debt; (4) the payment extinguished the obligation; and (5) justice requires the remedy to prevent unjust enrichment.

**The "Made Whole" Doctrine:** Many states follow the common law "made whole" doctrine, which provides that an insurer may not exercise subrogation rights until the insured has been fully compensated for all losses. This doctrine has been codified in some states (e.g., Georgia Code Section 33-24-56.1) and rejected in others. The doctrine is particularly significant in personal injury litigation where insurance recoveries may be inadequate to cover the insured's total damages.

**ERISA Preemption -- US Airways, Inc. v. McCutchen, 569 U.S. 88 (2013):** The Supreme Court held that ERISA plan terms govern subrogation and reimbursement rights in employer-sponsored health plans, but equitable principles apply to fill gaps in plan language. Where plan terms clearly authorize subrogation from personal injury recoveries, state anti-subrogation laws are preempted. However, the "common fund" doctrine applies by default: if the plan does not address attorney's fees, the subrogating plan must share proportionally in the litigation costs incurred by the beneficiary.

**Montanile v. Board of Trustees, 577 U.S. 136 (2016):** The Court held that ERISA fiduciaries seeking subrogation/reimbursement under Section 502(a)(3) may only recover specifically identifiable funds in the defendant's possession, limiting the remedy to situations where the beneficiary has not dissipated the personal injury recovery.

### Anti-Subrogation Rules

**Workers' Compensation:** The employer's workers' compensation insurer typically has subrogation rights against negligent third parties (not the employer), with the specific mechanism varying by state. Some states require the insurer to share in attorney's fees and litigation costs.

**Family Member Exclusion:** Many jurisdictions apply an anti-subrogation rule preventing automobile insurers from exercising subrogation against the insured's family members or household residents.

**Medical Payments Coverage:** Courts are divided on whether medical payments (MedPay) coverage triggers subrogation rights, with some states treating MedPay as first-party coverage without subrogation rights.

### Key Jurisdictional Variations

**Comparative Fault and Subrogation:** In comparative fault jurisdictions, the subrogation claim is reduced proportionally by the insured's own negligence, creating complex allocation issues when combined with the "made whole" doctrine.

**No-Fault Insurance:** States with no-fault automobile insurance systems (Michigan, New York, Florida, among others) limit subrogation by insurers for personal injury protection (PIP) benefits, though the scope varies significantly.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's subrogation regime is rooted in the BGB's law of obligations and supplemented by the VVG for insurance-specific subrogation, reflecting a systematic civil-law approach to the transfer of creditor rights upon satisfaction by a third party.

**BGB Section 268 -- Right of Third Party to Satisfy Obligation (Abloesungsrecht):** A third party who has a right in the debtor's property that would be lost upon enforcement by the creditor may satisfy the creditor's claim. Upon satisfaction, the claim passes to the third party by operation of law (gesetzlicher Forderungsuebergang). This provision protects junior lienholders and other parties with interests in the debtor's property.

**BGB Section 774 -- Surety's Right of Recourse (Rueckgriffsrecht des Buergen):** Paragraph 1 provides: "To the extent that the surety satisfies the creditor, the creditor's claim against the principal debtor passes to the surety." This is the central subrogation provision for suretyship, supplemented by Section 774(2), which provides that the surety may not exercise acquired rights to the detriment of the creditor. The surety also has an independent right of recourse (Aufwendungsersatz) under Sections 670, 683 BGB from the mandate or negotiorum gestio relationship with the principal debtor.

**BGB Section 426 -- Contribution Among Joint Debtors (Gesamtschuldnerausgleich):** While primarily a contribution provision, Section 426(2) provides for statutory subrogation: "To the extent that a joint debtor satisfies the creditor, the creditor's claim against the other joint debtors passes to the satisfying debtor." This creates a dual basis for recourse: an independent contribution claim under Section 426(1) and a subrogated claim under Section 426(2).

**VVG Section 86 -- Subrogation of Insurer (Uebergang von Ersatzanspruechen):** Paragraph 1 provides: "If the policyholder has a claim for compensation against a third party, the claim passes to the insurer upon indemnification of the policyholder, to the extent that the insurer compensates the damage." Key features:

- **Automatic Transfer (Legalzession):** The claim transfers by operation of law; no assignment is required.
- **Paragraph 2 (Original Creditor Priority):** The policyholder's remaining claim takes priority over the insurer's subrogated claim if the indemnification does not fully cover the loss.
- **Paragraph 3 (Anti-Subrogation -- Household Members):** The insurer's subrogation right does not extend to claims against persons who live in the same household as the policyholder (mit dem Versicherungsnehmer in haeuslicher Gemeinschaft leben), unless those persons acted intentionally.

### BGH Case Law

**BGH, Judgment of February 12, 2014 (IV ZR 284/13):** The BGH confirmed that VVG Section 86(3)'s anti-subrogation protection for household members extends to registered life partners (eingetragene Lebenspartner) and applies regardless of whether the household member is named in the policy.

**BGH, Judgment of November 13, 2013 (IV ZR 224/13):** Addressed the Vorrang des Glaeubigers (priority of the original creditor) principle under VVG Section 86(2). The Court held that where the insurer has partially indemnified the policyholder, the policyholder retains priority for the uncompensated portion, and the insurer may not reduce the policyholder's recovery by exercising subrogation rights.

**BGH, Judgment of October 18, 2017 (IV ZR 188/16):** Clarified that VVG Section 86 applies to all forms of indemnity insurance (Schadensversicherung) but not to fixed-sum insurance (Summenversicherung) such as life insurance or accident insurance paying fixed benefits.

### Social Insurance Subrogation

Under the Social Code Book X (SGB X) Section 116, social insurance carriers (including health insurance funds, pension carriers, and accident insurance carriers) are subrogated to the insured person's claims against third-party tortfeasors. This provision is particularly important in personal injury cases, where the social insurance carrier's subrogated claim may substantially reduce the injured person's direct recovery from the tortfeasor.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Private International Law

**Rome II Regulation (Regulation (EC) No 864/2007) Article 19 -- Subrogation:** "Where a person (the creditor) has a non-contractual claim upon another (the debtor), and a third person has a duty to satisfy the creditor, or has in fact satisfied the creditor in discharge of that duty, the law which governs the third person's duty to satisfy the creditor shall determine whether, and the extent to which, the third person is entitled to exercise against the debtor the rights which the creditor had against the debtor under the law governing their relationship." This provision establishes that the law governing the subrogee's obligation to pay (typically the insurance contract or guarantee) determines the existence and extent of subrogation, while the subrogated claim retains the characteristics of the original claim.

**Rome I Regulation (Regulation (EC) No 593/2008) Article 15 -- Legal Subrogation:** Provides the equivalent rule for contractual obligations: where a third person satisfies the creditor, the law governing the third person's obligation determines subrogation rights.

**Brussels I Recast (Regulation (EU) No 1215/2012):** Subrogation claims must be brought in accordance with the Regulation's jurisdictional rules. The CJEU in Vorarlberger Gebietskrankenkasse v. WGV-Schwäbische Allgemeine Versicherungs AG (Case C-347/08, 2009) held that a social security institution exercising subrogated rights against a tortfeasor's insurer in another member state may invoke the special jurisdiction for tort claims under Article 7(2).

### Motor Insurance Directive

**Directive 2009/103/EC (Codified Motor Insurance Directive):** Article 24 establishes a system of claims representatives and green card bureaux facilitating cross-border motor insurance claims. Insurers who have indemnified their policyholders may exercise subrogation rights against the at-fault driver's insurer in another member state. The Fourth Motor Insurance Directive introduced provisions enabling injured parties to bring claims directly against the insurer, which affects the practical operation of subrogation in cross-border motor accidents.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Stec and Others v. United Kingdom (Application Nos. 65731/01 and 65900/01, Grand Chamber, 2006):** While not directly addressing subrogation, the Grand Chamber held that social security benefits constitute possessions within Article 1 of Protocol No. 1, implying that subrogation rights acquired by social insurers may also constitute protected property rights under the Convention.

**Pressos Compania Naviera S.A. v. Belgium (Application No. 17849/91, 1995):** The Court held that claims for compensation constitute "possessions" under Protocol No. 1 once they have a sufficient basis in national law, protecting subrogated claims from retroactive legislative extinguishment.

## Comparative Analysis

### Structural Approaches to Subrogation

South Korea and Germany, as civil-law jurisdictions, treat legal subrogation as a statutory transfer of claims (Legalzession/법정대위) occurring automatically by operation of law upon satisfaction of another's obligation. The United States, rooted in equity, distinguishes between equitable subrogation (court-imposed) and conventional subrogation (contract-based), creating a more fragmented framework with significant interstate variation. The EU regime focuses on conflict-of-laws rules rather than substantive harmonization.

### Original Creditor Priority

All jurisdictions recognize some form of the principle that the original creditor should not be prejudiced by subrogation. South Korea (Article 482), Germany (VVG Section 86(2)), and U.S. "made whole" states protect the insured's right to full compensation before the insurer exercises subrogation. However, the U.S. Supreme Court's decision in US Airways v. McCutchen permits ERISA plans to override the "made whole" doctrine through explicit plan language, creating a significant exception in the employer-sponsored insurance context.

### Anti-Subrogation Protections

Germany's VVG Section 86(3) provides a clear statutory anti-subrogation rule for household members; South Korea's Commercial Act Article 682(2) similarly protects household members and employees; the United States applies family-member exclusions through judicial doctrine and policy interpretation, with less uniformity. All jurisdictions carve out exceptions for intentional conduct.

### Insurance vs. Social Insurance Subrogation

All four systems distinguish between private insurance subrogation and social insurance subrogation. Social insurers (NHIS in South Korea, social insurance carriers under SGB X in Germany, Medicaid/Medicare in the United States) exercise subrogation rights under specific statutory provisions that may differ from private insurance rules in scope, priority, and procedural requirements.

### ERISA and Federal Preemption

The U.S. system is uniquely complicated by ERISA preemption, which creates a parallel subrogation regime for employer-sponsored health plans governed by federal law rather than state insurance law. This has no equivalent in the other jurisdictions studied.
