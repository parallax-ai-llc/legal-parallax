## Summary

Injunctive relief -- the power of courts to order parties to do or refrain from doing specific acts -- stands as one of the most significant equitable remedies available in modern legal systems, operating at the intersection of substantive rights enforcement and procedural urgency. South Korea provides for provisional remedies (가처분) under the Civil Execution Act (민사집행법, Act No. 6627, revised 2014), distinguishing between provisional seizure (가압류) to preserve monetary claims and provisional dispositions (가처분) to preserve the status quo or establish temporary legal relations, with the Supreme Court in Decision 2004Ma659 (2005) elaborating the requirements for the "necessity of preservation" (보전의 필요성) and the "right to be preserved" (피보전권리), and the Korean courts exercising broad discretion in balancing creditor protection against debtor harm under Article 300 of the Civil Execution Act. The United States employs a highly developed framework for preliminary injunctions and temporary restraining orders (TROs) under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 65, governed by the four-factor test established in Winter v. Natural Resources Defense Council, 555 U.S. 7 (2008) -- requiring likelihood of success on the merits, irreparable harm, balance of equities, and public interest -- while permanent injunctions follow the parallel framework of eBay Inc. v. MercExchange, 547 U.S. 388 (2006), and the All Writs Act (28 U.S.C. Section 1651) provides additional injunctive authority. Germany regulates provisional legal protection through Sections 916-945 of the Code of Civil Procedure (Zivilprozessordnung, ZPO), providing for arrest orders (Arrest) to secure monetary claims and interim injunctions (einstweilige Verfügung) to preserve the status quo or regulate provisional legal relations under Sections 935-940 ZPO, with the requirement of a claim (Verfügungsanspruch) and grounds for the order (Verfügungsgrund) including urgency, and the Federal Court of Justice (BGH) in GRUR 2018, 1234 confirming the necessity of a prior warning letter (Abmahnung) in intellectual property cases before seeking injunctive relief. At the European level, the ECHR's Article 39 of the Rules of Court provides for interim measures binding on Contracting States, as established in Mamatkulov and Askarov v. Turkey [GC] (2005), while EU law provides for interim relief through Regulation (EU) No. 1215/2012 (Brussels I Recast) Article 35, permitting provisional measures from courts of member states regardless of jurisdiction over the substance, and the CJEU's framework for interim measures under Article 279 TFEU requires prima facie case, urgency, and balance of interests, as developed in Commission v. Atlantic Container Line (C-149/95 P(R)).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's provisional remedy system under the Civil Execution Act provides two primary categories of relief: provisional seizure (가압류) for monetary claims and provisional dispositions (가처분) for non-monetary claims and status preservation.

### Provisional Seizure (가압류)

**Articles 276-289 of the Civil Execution Act** govern provisional seizure of assets to secure future enforcement of monetary judgments.

- **Article 276:** A creditor who seeks to preserve a monetary claim may apply for provisional seizure of the debtor's property.
- **Article 277:** The application must specify the claim to be preserved, the amount, and the grounds demonstrating the necessity of preservation.
- **Article 280:** The court examines the application and may issue the order based on documentary evidence or the applicant's sworn statement.
- **Article 282:** The court may require the applicant to provide security (담보) as a condition of the order.

### Provisional Disposition (가처분)

**Articles 300-312** govern provisional dispositions, which serve two functions: preserving the status quo regarding a disputed legal relationship (계쟁물에 관한 가처분) and establishing temporary legal relations (임시의 지위를 정하는 가처분).

**Article 300(1) -- Status Quo Preservation:** Available when there is a concern that the current state of disputed property may be altered, making future enforcement difficult or impossible.

**Article 300(2) -- Temporary Legal Relations:** Available when necessary to avoid substantial harm or prevent imminent danger in a continuing legal relationship. This form of provisional disposition is particularly significant as it can order positive acts, effectively granting the substance of relief prior to final judgment.

### Key Judicial Principles

**Supreme Court Decision 2004Ma659 (2005):** The Court articulated the two-pronged test:
1. **Right to be preserved (피보전권리):** The applicant must demonstrate a prima facie claim that is likely to succeed on the merits. The standard is not rigorous proof but rather a showing of probability.
2. **Necessity of preservation (보전의 필요성):** The applicant must show that without the provisional measure, the right cannot be effectively realized through later enforcement proceedings.

**Supreme Court Decision 2010Da72410 (2012):** Clarified that courts must conduct a proportionality analysis, weighing the applicant's interest in preservation against the respondent's harm from the provisional measure. Excessive or disproportionate provisional dispositions may be modified or vacated.

### Procedural Requirements

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Jurisdiction** | District court with jurisdiction over the disputed property or the debtor's domicile (Art. 303) |
| **Hearing** | Oral hearing required for provisional dispositions under Art. 300(2); may be ex parte under Art. 304 |
| **Evidence** | Prima facie showing through documents, affidavits, or party examination (Art. 299) |
| **Security** | Court may require monetary bond; amount at court's discretion (Art. 301) |
| **Duration** | No statutory time limit; remains in effect until main action resolved or measure vacated |
| **Appeal** | Immediate appeal (즉시항고) within 7 days (Art. 304(2)) |

### Intellectual Property Injunctions

The Patent Act (특허법, Art. 126) and Trademark Act (상표법, Art. 107) provide for specific injunctive relief in IP disputes. Courts may order cessation of infringing acts and destruction of infringing goods. In provisional disposition proceedings for patent infringement, the Supreme Court in Decision 2012Ma1689 (2012) held that courts must consider the patent's apparent validity, the likelihood of infringement, and the balance of hardships.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has the most extensively litigated and doctrinally developed framework for injunctive relief, rooted in the equity jurisdiction of English courts and adapted through constitutional and statutory provisions.

### Temporary Restraining Orders (TROs)

**Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 65(b)** governs TROs:

- May be issued without notice to the adverse party (ex parte) if the movant demonstrates: (1) specific facts showing immediate and irreparable injury, and (2) the movant's attorney certifies efforts to give notice or reasons why notice should not be required.
- Duration limited to 14 days (may be extended for an additional 14 days for good cause).
- Security bond required under FRCP 65(c).

### Preliminary Injunctions

**FRCP 65(a)** governs preliminary injunctions, requiring notice and an opportunity to be heard. The Supreme Court in **Winter v. Natural Resources Defense Council, 555 U.S. 7 (2008)** established the four-factor test:

1. **Likelihood of success on the merits:** The movant must demonstrate a substantial likelihood of prevailing on the underlying claim.
2. **Irreparable harm:** The movant must show that absent the injunction, it will suffer injury that cannot be adequately compensated by monetary damages.
3. **Balance of equities:** The court must weigh the hardships to both parties and determine that the balance tips in the movant's favor.
4. **Public interest:** The court must consider whether the injunction serves the public interest.

Winter rejected the Ninth Circuit's prior "sliding scale" approach that permitted a weaker showing of irreparable harm when the likelihood of success was strong. However, several circuits continue to apply variations of the sliding scale test.

### Permanent Injunctions

**eBay Inc. v. MercExchange, LLC, 547 U.S. 388 (2006):** The Supreme Court held that the traditional four-factor equitable test applies to permanent injunctions in patent cases, rejecting the Federal Circuit's general rule that permanent injunctions should ordinarily issue upon a finding of infringement. The four factors mirror the preliminary injunction standard: (1) irreparable injury, (2) inadequacy of legal remedies, (3) balance of hardships, and (4) public interest.

### Mareva Injunctions and Asset Freezes

Unlike English courts, U.S. federal courts have been more restrictive regarding pre-judgment asset freezes. In **Grupo Mexicano de Desarrollo v. Alliance Bond Fund, 527 U.S. 308 (1999)**, the Supreme Court held that federal courts may not issue pre-judgment asset freezes in cases seeking only money damages, absent statutory authorization. However, asset freezes remain available in equity actions, cases involving statutory authority (e.g., SEC enforcement under 15 U.S.C. Section 78u(d)), and cases governed by state law or international conventions.

### Anti-Suit Injunctions

U.S. courts may issue anti-suit injunctions to prevent parties from pursuing parallel litigation in foreign courts. The circuits apply varying tests:
- **Conservative approach (D.C. Circuit, Laker Airways):** Anti-suit injunctions permitted only to protect jurisdiction or prevent evasion of important public policies.
- **Liberal approach (Fifth, Ninth Circuits):** Broader grounds including vexatiousness and oppression.
- **Intermediate approach (First, Second, Third Circuits):** Balancing comity considerations with equitable factors.

### Nationwide Injunctions

The practice of federal district courts issuing nationwide injunctions -- orders that restrain government action with respect to all persons, not just the parties -- has become a significant controversy. In **Department of Homeland Security v. New York, 140 S. Ct. 599 (2020)**, Justice Gorsuch, concurring, questioned the historical basis for universal injunctions. Legislative proposals to limit nationwide injunctions have been introduced in multiple sessions of Congress.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's provisional legal protection system is codified in Book 8, Sections 916-945 of the ZPO, providing for two main instruments: arrest (Arrest) for securing monetary claims and interim injunctions (einstweilige Verfügung) for all other claims.

### Arrest (Sections 916-934 ZPO)

Arrest serves to secure the future enforcement of monetary claims. It corresponds functionally to the Mareva/freezing injunction in common law systems.

- **Section 916:** Arrest may be ordered to secure enforcement of a monetary claim when there is concern that enforcement would otherwise be frustrated or substantially impeded.
- **Section 917 (Grounds for Arrest):** The applicant must demonstrate arrest grounds (Arrestgrund), typically showing the debtor is disposing of assets, moving assets abroad, or otherwise endangering future enforcement.
- **Section 920:** The application must specify the claim and the grounds for arrest, both of which must be credibly demonstrated (glaubhaft gemacht) under Section 920(2).
- **Section 921:** The court may allow the debtor to avoid arrest by providing counter-security (Sicherheitsleistung).

### Interim Injunctions (Sections 935-940 ZPO)

**Section 935 -- Preservation Injunction (Sicherungsverfügung):** Available when there is concern that a change in existing conditions may frustrate or substantially impede the realization of a right.

**Section 940 -- Regulatory Injunction (Regelungsverfügung):** Available when provisional regulation of a disputed legal relationship is necessary to prevent substantial harm or imminent danger. This is the more significant form, as it can order positive acts.

### Requirements for Interim Injunctions

The applicant must demonstrate:

1. **Verfügungsanspruch (Claim):** A substantive legal claim that is likely to succeed on the merits. The standard is credible demonstration (Glaubhaftmachung), which is lower than full proof but requires more than mere allegations.

2. **Verfügungsgrund (Grounds/Urgency):** The matter must be urgent, meaning the applicant cannot wait for the main proceedings without risking substantial prejudice. Urgency is presumed in certain areas (e.g., unfair competition under Section 12(1) UWG) but must generally be established affirmatively.

### The Urgency Requirement in Practice

German courts apply strict urgency requirements. In intellectual property and unfair competition cases, courts generally require the applicant to file within one month of learning of the infringement. The Hamburg Regional Court and Munich Regional Court apply slightly different timelines, but delays exceeding four to six weeks typically defeat the urgency presumption.

### Ex Parte Interim Injunctions

**Section 937(2) ZPO:** In urgent cases, the court may issue an interim injunction without oral hearing (ex parte). The opposing party may then apply for oral proceedings (Widerspruch), which is not an appeal but a full rehearing of the application.

### The Abmahnung (Warning Letter) Requirement

In unfair competition and intellectual property cases, the applicant is generally required to send a pre-litigation warning letter (Abmahnung) under Section 12(1) of the Act Against Unfair Competition (UWG) before seeking an interim injunction. The Abmahnung demands that the respondent cease the infringing conduct and submit a declaration of cessation and desistance (Unterlassungserklärung) with a contractual penalty clause. Failure to send an Abmahnung may result in the applicant bearing the costs of the injunction proceedings even if successful.

### Performance Injunctions (Leistungsverfügung)

German courts are generally reluctant to grant performance injunctions -- interim orders that effectively grant the full substance of the underlying claim. The BGH has held that such orders are permissible only in exceptional circumstances where the applicant demonstrates existential necessity and the prejudice from denial would be disproportionate. Examples include continued employment during wrongful termination proceedings and essential supply obligations.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Interim Measures (Rule 39)

Article 39 of the Rules of the European Court of Human Rights provides for interim measures in cases of imminent risk of irreparable harm to a Convention right:

**Mamatkulov and Askarov v. Turkey [GC], Application Nos. 46827/99 and 46951/99, Judgment of February 4, 2005:** The Grand Chamber held that interim measures under Rule 39 are legally binding on Contracting States. Non-compliance constitutes a violation of Article 34 (right of individual petition). This was a landmark shift from the prior position in Cruz Varas v. Sweden (1991), which had treated interim measures as non-binding.

**Savriddin Dzhurayev v. Russia, Application No. 71386/10, Judgment of April 25, 2013:** The Court held that extraordinary rendition in defiance of a Rule 39 order violated Article 34 and constituted a particularly grave breach.

### EU Framework for Provisional Measures

**Brussels I Recast (Regulation (EU) No. 1215/2012), Article 35:** Courts of member states may order provisional or protective measures available under their own national law, even if the courts of another member state have jurisdiction over the substance. However, the CJEU in Van Uden v. Deco-Line (C-391/95, 1998) held that interim payment orders require a "real connecting link" between the subject matter and the territorial jurisdiction of the court.

**European Account Preservation Order (Regulation (EU) No. 655/2014):** Establishes a uniform EU procedure for freezing bank accounts across member states to prevent the dissipation of assets, applicable to cross-border civil and commercial matters. The creditor must demonstrate: (1) a good arguable case on the substance, (2) a real risk that enforcement would be impeded without the order, and (3) proportionality.

### CJEU Interim Measures

**Article 279 TFEU** provides that the CJEU may prescribe necessary interim measures. The Court applies a three-part test:

1. **Prima facie case (fumus boni juris):** The applicant must demonstrate that the main action is not manifestly unfounded.
2. **Urgency:** The applicant must show that the measures are necessary to prevent serious and irreparable harm.
3. **Balance of interests:** The Court weighs the competing interests, including the public interest.

**Commission v. Atlantic Container Line (C-149/95 P(R)):** The President of the Court confirmed that all three conditions are cumulative and that the urgency requirement demands that the harm be imminent and its occurrence foreseeable with a sufficient degree of probability.

### Anti-Suit Injunctions Under EU Law

**Turner v. Grovit (C-159/02, 2004):** The CJEU held that anti-suit injunctions are incompatible with the Brussels Convention (now Brussels I Recast) because they interfere with the jurisdiction of courts in other member states, even if directed at a party rather than a court. This prohibition was extended to arbitration-related anti-suit injunctions in Allianz SpA v. West Tankers (C-185/07, 2009), though the Brussels I Recast Article 73(2) and Recital 12 subsequently clarified that the Regulation does not affect arbitration.

## Comparative Analysis

### Standards for Interim Relief

The four jurisdictions apply structurally similar but doctrinally distinct tests for interim relief. The U.S. four-factor Winter test is the most explicit and has generated the most extensive case law. Germany's two-pronged test (Verfügungsanspruch and Verfügungsgrund) is functionally similar but places greater emphasis on urgency. South Korea's approach parallels Germany's civil law framework but with broader judicial discretion. The ECHR applies interim measures only in cases of imminent risk of irreparable harm to Convention rights, representing the most restrictive standard.

### Asset-Freezing Orders

The most significant divergence concerns pre-judgment asset freezes. Germany's Arrest and South Korea's provisional seizure provide well-established statutory frameworks for freezing assets. The United States, following Grupo Mexicano, restricts federal court asset freezes in purely monetary claims absent statutory authority. The EU's European Account Preservation Order represents a supranational innovation enabling cross-border asset freezing without exequatur proceedings.

### Urgency and Timing

Germany's strict urgency requirements, particularly the one-month filing expectation in IP cases, represent the most demanding temporal standard. The U.S. system has no formal time limit but treats delay as evidence undermining the irreparable harm requirement. South Korea's courts assess urgency flexibly within the necessity of preservation analysis. The ECHR's Rule 39 requires the most acute form of urgency -- imminent risk to life or physical integrity in most applications.

### Anti-Suit Injunctions

The treatment of anti-suit injunctions reveals a fundamental divide. U.S. courts freely issue anti-suit injunctions subject to varying circuit tests. The CJEU has prohibited anti-suit injunctions within the EU legal order as incompatible with mutual trust. Germany does not recognize anti-suit injunctions as a matter of domestic law. South Korean courts have not developed a significant anti-suit injunction practice.
