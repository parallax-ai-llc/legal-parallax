## Summary

Adverse possession -- the legal doctrine by which a person who possesses another's property for a statutory period without permission may acquire legal title to that property -- reflects a tension between protecting established ownership rights and recognizing the social value of productive land use, with its scope, requirements, and underlying justifications differing substantially across legal systems. South Korea recognizes adverse possession through the Korean Civil Code (민법) under the concept of acquisitive prescription (취득시효), codified in Articles 245-248, which provides for acquisition of ownership through 20 years of peaceful, public, and continuous possession with the intent to possess as owner (자주점유), or through a shortened 10-year period for possessors who register the property based on a legally insufficient cause and possess in good faith and without negligence; the Supreme Court of Korea (대법원) has developed extensive jurisprudence on the requirements and effects of acquisitive prescription, particularly in the context of unregistered land, boundary disputes, and publicly owned property. The United States recognizes adverse possession as a common law doctrine, with specific requirements and statutory periods set by each state, generally requiring that possession be actual, open and notorious, exclusive, continuous, hostile (without permission), and maintained for the statutory period, which ranges from 5 years (California) to 40 years (Ohio), with some states additionally requiring payment of property taxes or color of title. Germany codifies acquisitive prescription (Ersitzung) in BGB Section 900 for immovable property (requiring 30 years of proprietary possession and registration as owner in the land register) and Section 937 for movable property (requiring 10 years of good-faith possession), while the land registration system (Grundbuch) creates a strong presumption of ownership that significantly limits the practical importance of adverse possession for real property. The ECHR has addressed adverse possession under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (property protection), with the European Court of Human Rights examining whether domestic adverse possession regimes achieve a fair balance between the possessor's acquired rights and the original owner's property rights, while EU law does not harmonize adverse possession but member state regimes must comply with Convention standards.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's acquisitive prescription (취득시효) doctrine is codified in the Civil Code, reflecting the civil law tradition's systematic approach to prescription.

**Article 245 (Acquisitive Prescription of Immovable Property):**

Paragraph (1) provides that a person who has possessed immovable property peacefully and publicly for 20 years with the intent to possess as owner (소유의 의사로 평온, 공연하게) acquires ownership by registering the property. This is the ordinary acquisitive prescription, which does not require good faith or a legal basis for the initial possession.

Paragraph (2) provides that a person who has been registered as owner of immovable property based on a legally insufficient cause and has possessed the property peacefully, publicly, and in good faith without negligence for 10 years acquires ownership. This shortened period applies only where the possessor has registration based on a defective but colorable legal basis.

### Key Requirements

**Intent to Possess as Owner (자주점유):** The Supreme Court of Korea has established a presumption of self-possession (자주점유의 추정) under Article 197(1) of the Civil Code, meaning that possession is presumed to be with the intent to possess as owner unless proven otherwise. This presumption can be rebutted by showing that the possessor entered possession under circumstances indicating recognition of another's ownership (e.g., as a tenant, borrower, or custodian).

**Peaceful and Public Possession (평온·공연한 점유):** The possession must not have been acquired or maintained through violence or stealth. "Public" means that the possession is not concealed from the true owner.

**Continuity (계속):** The possession must be continuous throughout the statutory period. Article 198 provides a presumption of continuity where possession is established at the beginning and end of the statutory period.

### Registration Requirement

A critical feature of Korean acquisitive prescription is that completion of the statutory period does not automatically transfer ownership. Article 245 requires the possessor to register the property in order to acquire ownership. The possessor who has completed the statutory period has a claim against the registered owner for cooperation in registration (등기이전청구권). This claim is itself subject to a 10-year statute of limitations.

### Supreme Court Jurisprudence

The Supreme Court has addressed numerous contentious issues:

**Government Property:** The Court has distinguished between administrative property (행정재산) used for public purposes, which cannot be acquired by prescription, and fiscal property (일반재산) held by the government in its proprietary capacity, which may be subject to acquisitive prescription. This distinction has generated significant litigation, particularly regarding unregistered state-owned land in rural areas.

**Boundary Disputes:** Acquisitive prescription is frequently invoked in boundary disputes where adjacent landowners have mistakenly occupied strips of land beyond their registered boundaries for extended periods. The Supreme Court has held that boundary encroachment for 20 years, with peaceful and public possession and intent to possess as owner, can establish ownership through acquisitive prescription.

**Multiple Successive Possessors:** Article 199 permits a current possessor to add the predecessor's possession period to their own (점유의 승계), provided the predecessor's possession also met the statutory requirements. The Supreme Court has held that the current possessor may selectively choose whether to add the predecessor's possession or rely solely on their own.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Adverse possession in the United States is a state-law doctrine with common law origins, meaning requirements and statutory periods vary across all 50 states and the District of Columbia.

### Standard Requirements

Despite state variations, five common elements are generally required:

**Actual Possession:** The claimant must physically occupy and use the property in a manner consistent with how a reasonable owner would use property of that type. Seasonal use may satisfy this requirement for property appropriate for seasonal use (e.g., vacation property, agricultural land).

**Open and Notorious:** The possession must be visible and apparent, such that a reasonable owner inspecting the property would notice the occupation. This requirement ensures the true owner has an opportunity to discover the adverse use and take action to protect their rights.

**Exclusive:** The claimant must possess the property to the exclusion of others, including the true owner. Shared use with the true owner generally defeats exclusivity, though co-tenants may adversely possess against each other through ouster.

**Continuous:** The possession must be uninterrupted for the entire statutory period. The claimant need not be physically present at every moment; seasonal or periodic use consistent with the property's nature may suffice. Tacking (adding the periods of successive possessors in privity) is generally permitted.

**Hostile/Adverse:** The possession must be without the true owner's permission. States differ on whether hostile intent requires the possessor to know they are trespassing or merely to act inconsistently with the true owner's rights.

### Color of Title and Tax Payment

Several states provide shortened statutory periods for possessors who have "color of title" (a document purporting to transfer ownership that is legally defective) and/or who pay property taxes during the possession period. California's adverse possession statute (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. Section 325) requires payment of all property taxes during the five-year period, making it among the most demanding requirements nationally.

### Government Property

Under the common law doctrine of nullum tempus occurrit regi (time does not run against the king), government property is generally immune from adverse possession claims. Most states have codified this immunity, though some permit adverse possession of certain categories of government-owned land, and others have specific statutes of limitation for claims against government property.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's approach to adverse possession is significantly shaped by the Grundbuch (land register) system, which provides a highly reliable record of property ownership and substantially reduces the practical need for adverse possession of real property.

**BGB Section 900 -- Tabular Prescription (Tabularersitzung):** A person who is registered in the land register as the owner of a plot of land without actually being the owner acquires ownership if 30 years have elapsed since the registration was made and the person has been in proprietary possession (Eigenbesitz) of the plot during this period. The claim of the true owner to rectification of the land register (Section 894 BGB) is then extinguished by prescription.

This provision has several distinctive features. First, it requires registration -- the possessor must actually be registered as owner in the Grundbuch, meaning pure factual possession without registration cannot ripen into ownership of real property. Second, it requires proprietary possession (Eigenbesitz, Section 872 BGB) -- the possessor must exercise actual control over the property with the intent to possess as owner. Third, the 30-year period is among the longest in comparative law.

**BGB Section 937 -- Acquisitive Prescription of Movable Property (Ersitzung):** A person who has had a movable thing in proprietary possession for 10 years acquires ownership (Ersitzung). Acquisitive prescription is excluded if the possessor is not in good faith at the time of acquisition or later learns that ownership does not belong to them.

### Practical Significance

Due to the reliability of the Grundbuch system, adverse possession of real property under Section 900 BGB has minimal practical significance in modern Germany. The Grundbuch provides a near-conclusive record of ownership, and discrepancies between registered and actual ownership are rare. Adverse possession cases in German practice are almost exclusively limited to movable property under Section 937 or to historical situations where land register entries became inaccurate due to wartime destruction, post-war property reforms, or German reunification.

### Prescription of Claims (Verjährung)

Separate from acquisitive prescription, the BGB provides for extinctive prescription (Verjährung) of the owner's claims. Under Section 197(1) No. 2, the owner's claim for return of property (Herausgabeanspruch, Section 985 BGB) prescribes after 30 years. This extinctive prescription operates alongside the acquisitive prescription under Section 900 but has independent significance where the possessor is not registered in the Grundbuch.

### Post-Reunification Issues

German reunification in 1990 created unique adverse possession challenges. Property in the former East Germany had been subject to confiscation, collectivization, and redistribution under the GDR regime. The Property Act (Vermögensgesetz) of 1990 and the Property Reconciliation Act (Sachenrechtsbereinigungsgesetz) of 1994 addressed competing claims between former owners, current possessors, and the state, creating specialized restitution and compensation procedures that partially displaced general adverse possession principles.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Proportionality Analysis

The European Court of Human Rights has examined adverse possession under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1, assessing whether domestic adverse possession regimes achieve a fair balance between the interests of the possessor and the original owner.

**JA Pye (Oxford) Ltd v. United Kingdom (Application No. 44302/02, Grand Chamber, 2007):** The most significant ECHR decision on adverse possession. The applicants, who owned 23 hectares of agricultural land near Oxford, lost their property through adverse possession under the English Limitation Act 1980 after the possessor (a farmer) had occupied the land without permission for 12 years. The Grand Chamber held, by a vote of 10 to 7, that the English adverse possession regime did not violate Article 1 of Protocol No. 1. The majority reasoned that adverse possession was a "control of use" of property rather than a deprivation, and that the statutory limitation period of 12 years, with the possibility for the owner to interrupt prescription by taking action, achieved a fair balance between competing interests. However, seven dissenting judges argued that the regime imposed a disproportionate burden on the original owner.

### Impact on Domestic Reform

The Pye litigation (including the initial Chamber judgment in 2005, which found a violation, subsequently reversed by the Grand Chamber) contributed to significant reform in England and Wales through the Land Registration Act 2002, which substantially modified the adverse possession regime for registered land. Under the new system, after 10 years of adverse possession, the possessor may apply for registration, but the registered owner is notified and given two years to object and recover possession; only if the owner fails to act during this period can the possessor reapply and obtain title after a total of 12 years.

### EU Law

The EU has not harmonized adverse possession law, which remains a matter of national property law. However, adverse possession intersects with EU law in several contexts: the free movement of capital (Article 63 TFEU) may be implicated where adverse possession rules discriminate against non-resident or cross-border property owners; EU environmental law may interact with adverse possession where land subject to conservation designations is adversely possessed; and the European Land Registry Association (ELRA) has promoted convergence of land registration systems, indirectly affecting the practical operation of adverse possession.

### Council of Europe Property Law Convergence

The Council of Europe has not adopted binding instruments on adverse possession but has, through the European Commission for the Efficiency of Justice (CEPEJ), conducted comparative studies of property registration and dispute resolution systems that highlight the divergent approaches to adverse possession across member states. These studies note a general trend toward strengthening land registration systems, which reduces the practical relevance of adverse possession as a title acquisition mechanism.

## Comparative Analysis

### Underlying Justifications

The theoretical justifications for adverse possession differ across jurisdictions, though several themes recur. The quieting titles rationale emphasizes the social value of resolving stale property claims and ensuring marketable title. The productive use rationale holds that property should be owned by those who actively use it rather than by those who neglect it. The evidence preservation rationale recognizes that evidence of ownership deteriorates over time, making older claims increasingly unreliable. The reliance interest rationale protects the possessor's investments and expectations in property they have occupied and improved over an extended period.

### Registration Systems and Practical Relevance

The practical importance of adverse possession is inversely related to the reliability of the jurisdiction's property registration system. Germany's highly reliable Grundbuch system renders adverse possession nearly irrelevant for registered real property. South Korea's registration system is less conclusive, leaving greater scope for adverse possession claims, particularly for unregistered land and boundary disputes. The United States, with its recording-based (rather than registration-based) system, provides significant scope for adverse possession to resolve title uncertainties. The ECHR has recognized that well-functioning registration systems may justify stricter adverse possession requirements.

### Good Faith and Intent

The role of good faith varies markedly. Germany requires good faith for adverse possession of movable property but not for real property (where registration substitutes). South Korea does not require good faith for the 20-year ordinary prescription but does for the 10-year shortened period. Most U.S. states do not require good faith, accepting adverse possession even by knowing trespassers (though some states, particularly in the Connecticut doctrine states, require the possessor to believe they have a right to the property). The ECHR has treated the presence or absence of good faith as relevant to the proportionality analysis but not as a determinative factor.

### Government Property

All four systems generally protect government property from adverse possession, though with varying scope. South Korea distinguishes between administrative and fiscal government property. Most U.S. states provide blanket immunity for government property. Germany generally excludes publicly owned property from adverse possession. The ECHR permits states to protect public property from adverse possession as a legitimate policy choice.
