## Summary

Social media platform regulation represents one of the most rapidly evolving areas of technology law, as governments worldwide grapple with balancing free expression, user safety, platform accountability, and democratic governance in the digital age. South Korea regulates social media through the Act on Promotion of Information and Communications Network Utilization and Information Protection (commonly known as the Network Act), enforced by the Korea Communications Standards Commission (KCSC), which may order removal of illegal content and impose penalties of up to two years imprisonment or fines of up to 20 million won for non-compliance. The United States maintains a largely permissive regime anchored by Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act (47 U.S.C. section 230), which provides broad immunity to platforms for user-generated content, though this framework faces growing bipartisan pressure for reform. Germany pioneered platform content regulation with the Network Enforcement Act (Netzwerkdurchsetzungsgesetz, NetzDG) of 2017, requiring social media platforms with over two million German users to remove "clearly illegal" content within 24 hours, with fines of up to EUR 50 million for systematic non-compliance, though the NetzDG has been substantially superseded by the EU Digital Services Act. The European Union's Digital Services Act (Regulation (EU) 2022/2065), fully applicable since February 2024, establishes the world's most comprehensive platform regulation framework, imposing tiered obligations based on platform size, mandatory risk assessments for very large online platforms (VLOPs), and fines of up to 6% of global annual turnover. Countries including Australia, India, Japan, Brazil, and Singapore have adopted divergent approaches reflecting distinct priorities regarding content moderation, platform accountability, and online harms.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's social media regulation rests on several interlocking statutes and an active administrative enforcement body.

| Statute / Body | Purpose |
|----------------|---------|
| Act on Promotion of Information and Communications Network Utilization and Information Protection (정보통신망법, Network Act) | Core platform regulation; prohibits distribution of illegal content; governs intermediary liability |
| Telecommunications Business Act (전기통신사업법) | Regulates telecommunications operators including platform services |
| Korea Communications Standards Commission (KCSC, 방송통신심의위원회) | Administrative body that reviews and orders removal of illegal online content |
| Korea Communications Commission (KCC, 방송통신위원회) | Regulates broadcasting and telecommunications policy |
| Personal Information Protection Act (개인정보보호법, PIPA) | Governs platform data collection and processing obligations |
| Act on Special Cases Concerning the Punishment of Sexual Crimes | Criminalizes distribution of deepfake sexual content and non-consensual intimate imagery |

**Article 44-7 of the Network Act** prohibits the distribution of the following categories of content through information and communications networks:

1. Content that violates the Criminal Act or other laws (obscenity, defamation, national security threats)
2. Content that infringes on intellectual property rights
3. Content that constitutes cyber harassment or cyberstalking
4. Content that distributes personal information without consent
5. Content that constitutes false information intended to harm the public interest

**KCSC Enforcement Powers:** The Korea Communications Standards Commission reviews complaints and may issue corrective orders requiring information and communications service providers to:

- Delete or block specific content
- Restrict access to specific online resources
- Suspend user accounts distributing illegal content

Non-compliance with KCSC orders carries penalties of up to two years imprisonment or a fine of up to 20 million won (approximately $15,000) under Article 73 of the Network Act.

### Key Principles

**Real-Name Verification (Legacy):** South Korea was one of the first countries to require real-name identity verification for posting on major online platforms, under a 2007 amendment to the Network Act. However, the Constitutional Court struck down the real-name verification requirement in 2012 (2010Hun-Ma47), finding it an unconstitutional restriction on freedom of expression and privacy. Despite the ruling, platforms in South Korea continue to require identity verification for account creation through mobile phone or i-PIN authentication.

**Defamation Framework:** South Korea's defamation laws are particularly relevant to social media regulation. Article 70 of the Network Act criminalizes online defamation with enhanced penalties compared to offline defamation under the Criminal Act:

| Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|
| Online defamation using true facts (Art. 70(1)) | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine up to 30 million won |
| Online defamation using false facts (Art. 70(2)) | Up to 7 years imprisonment or fine up to 50 million won |
| Offline defamation using true facts (Criminal Act Art. 307(1)) | Up to 2 years imprisonment or fine up to 5 million won |
| Offline defamation using false facts (Criminal Act Art. 307(2)) | Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine up to 50 million won |

**AI Content Regulation:** Planned amendments to the Network Act would introduce mandatory labeling of AI-generated content posted online, impose management obligations on platform operators regarding AI-generated misinformation, and introduce punitive damages for platforms that fail to address AI-generated harmful content.

**Deepfake Content:** Following the September 2024 amendments to the Act on Special Cases Concerning the Punishment of Sexual Crimes, creation or dissemination of deepfake sexual content carries up to seven years imprisonment, while possession or viewing of such content is punishable by up to three years imprisonment or a fine of up to 30 million won.

### Notable Cases

**Cho Ju-bin "Nth Room" Case (2020):** The operator of Telegram chatrooms distributing sexually exploitative content, including deepfake material, was sentenced to 42 years imprisonment. The case catalyzed South Korea's legislative response to digital sex crimes and deepfakes and led to amendments requiring platforms to proactively detect and remove such content.

**Constitutional Court Real-Name Verification Decision (2012, 2010Hun-Ma47):** The Court struck down the mandatory internet real-name verification system, finding it disproportionately restricted freedom of expression while providing minimal benefit in reducing harmful content. The decision remains a landmark in Asian digital rights jurisprudence.

**Kakao Platform Regulation Disputes (2022-2024):** Ongoing regulatory proceedings addressing the dominance of Kakao Corporation across messaging (KakaoTalk), search, commerce, and content platforms, raising questions about platform self-preferencing, algorithmic transparency, and the intersection of competition and content regulation.

**Naver News Algorithm Controversy (2023):** The Korea Communications Commission investigated Naver, South Korea's dominant search and news platform, over allegations that its news recommendation algorithm was biased in favor of certain political viewpoints and media outlets. The investigation raised fundamental questions about algorithmic transparency obligations, the distinction between editorial curation and automated recommendation, and whether platform algorithms constitute a form of speech subject to regulation.

**Sulli Act Proposal (2019-2020):** Following the death of K-pop star Sulli (Choi Jin-ri), widely attributed to cyberbullying, the National Assembly considered legislation that would restrict anonymous online commenting and strengthen penalties for malicious online speech. While a comprehensive "Sulli Act" was not enacted, the debate contributed to amendments strengthening the KCSC's enforcement powers and expanding criminal penalties for persistent cyberbullying and online harassment.

## United States

### Federal Framework

The United States maintains a predominantly permissive approach to platform regulation, grounded in strong First Amendment protections and statutory immunity for intermediaries.

| Statute / Provision | Citation | Purpose |
|---------------------|----------|---------|
| Section 230, Communications Decency Act | 47 U.S.C. section 230 | Broad platform immunity for user-generated content; safe harbor for good-faith content moderation |
| First Amendment | U.S. Constitution | Constrains government regulation of online speech |
| COPPA (Children's Online Privacy Protection Act) | 15 U.S.C. sections 6501-6506 | Protects children under 13 from online data collection |
| FOSTA-SESTA (2018) | Pub. L. 115-164 | Carves out Section 230 immunity for sex trafficking content |
| FTC Act section 5 | 15 U.S.C. section 45 | Authority over unfair and deceptive platform practices |

**Section 230 Framework:** Section 230(c)(1) provides that "[n]o provider or user of an interactive computer service shall be treated as the publisher or speaker of any information provided by another information content provider." This provision has been interpreted to provide broad immunity from civil liability for hosting, distributing, or moderating user-generated content. Section 230(c)(2) provides additional protection for good-faith content moderation, stating that platforms shall not be liable for voluntarily restricting access to material the provider considers "obscene, lewd, lascivious, filthy, excessively violent, harassing, or otherwise objectionable."

**Reform Proposals:** Section 230 faces bipartisan but divergent pressure for reform:

- **Conservative proposals** (e.g., EARN IT Act, various state laws) aim to condition Section 230 immunity on platforms maintaining content neutrality or meeting specific safety obligations.
- **Progressive proposals** focus on requiring algorithmic transparency, prohibiting targeted advertising to minors, and imposing duty-of-care obligations.
- **State-level laws** in Texas (HB 20) and Florida (SB 7072) attempted to prohibit platforms from censoring political speech, leading to the Supreme Court's decision in *Moody v. NetChoice* (2024).

### State Variations

| State Law | Year | Key Provisions |
|-----------|------|----------------|
| **California Age-Appropriate Design Code** (AB 2273) | 2022 | Data protection impact assessments for services likely accessed by children; enjoined by courts |
| **Texas HB 20** | 2021 | Prohibited platforms with 50M+ users from censoring based on viewpoint; subject of Supreme Court review |
| **Florida SB 7072** | 2021 | Required platforms to disclose content moderation standards and provide notice before moderation actions |
| **Utah Social Media Regulation Act** | 2024 | Parental consent requirements for minors; curfew features; age verification |
| **New York SAFE for Kids Act** | 2024 | Restricts addictive algorithmic feeds for minors without parental consent |

### Notable Cases

***Moody v. NetChoice, LLC* (144 S. Ct. 2383, 2024):** The Supreme Court addressed the constitutionality of Texas and Florida laws restricting platforms' content moderation practices. The Court vacated lower court decisions and remanded for further analysis, holding that the First Amendment protects platforms' editorial discretion in content curation but that the facial challenges to the laws required more granular analysis of their applications across different platform functions.

***Gonzalez v. Google LLC* (598 U.S. 617, 2023):** The Supreme Court declined to narrow Section 230's scope in a case alleging that YouTube's recommendation algorithms aided ISIS recruitment, issuing a narrow opinion that left Section 230's broad immunity largely intact.

***Twitter, Inc. v. Taamneh* (598 U.S. 471, 2023):** Decided alongside *Gonzalez*, the Court unanimously held that social media platforms did not "aid and abet" terrorism under the Anti-Terrorism Act merely by providing services used by terrorists, setting a high bar for platform liability for user content.

***Reno v. ACLU* (521 U.S. 844, 1997):** The foundational case establishing that internet speech receives full First Amendment protection, striking down the Communications Decency Act's indecency provisions while leaving Section 230's immunity intact.

**Children's Online Safety Legislation:** The bipartisan Kids Online Safety Act (KOSA), passed by the Senate in 2024, would impose a duty of care on platforms to prevent harm to minors, including requirements to disable addictive design features for users under 17 and provide parental tools. KOSA represents the most significant proposed expansion of platform obligations for child safety at the federal level since COPPA in 1998. Separately, the COPPA 2.0 Act would extend protections from children under 13 to minors under 17 and restrict targeted advertising to minors.

**Social Media and Elections:** The Federal Election Commission (FEC) has addressed social media's role in elections through advisory opinions on paid political advertising, disclosure requirements for online ads, and the use of AI-generated content in campaign communications. Section 230 does not shield platforms from liability for campaign finance violations, creating an important carve-out in the platform immunity framework.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's platform regulation framework comprises the NetzDG, EU Digital Services Act obligations, and domestic criminal and competition law.

| Legal Source | Purpose |
|-------------|---------|
| Netzwerkdurchsetzungsgesetz (NetzDG, 2017) | Platform content removal obligations for illegal content; transparency reporting |
| Digital Services Act (Regulation (EU) 2022/2065) | Comprehensive platform regulation; largely supersedes NetzDG |
| StGB sections 86, 86a, 130, 131, 185-187 | Criminal offenses forming basis for NetzDG content removal (incitement, hate speech, defamation) |
| Telemediengesetz (TMG) | Telemedia/intermediary liability (being superseded by DSA) |
| Medienstaatsvertrag (MStV) | Interstate media treaty; media plurality and platform regulation |
| Jugendschutzgesetz (JuSchG) | Youth protection obligations for online platforms |
| GWB section 19a | Competition law provisions for platforms with paramount cross-market significance |

**NetzDG Key Requirements:**

- **Scope:** Applies to social media platforms with two million or more registered users in Germany.
- **Content Removal Timelines:** Platforms must remove or block "clearly illegal" content within 24 hours of receiving a complaint. Content that is illegal but not "clearly" so must be assessed and, if necessary, removed within seven days. Complex cases may be referred to a recognized self-regulatory body.
- **Transparency Reports:** Platforms must publish semi-annual reports detailing the volume and handling of complaints, response times, and content removal decisions.
- **Complaint Mechanism:** Platforms must maintain an easily recognizable, directly accessible, and always available procedure for submitting complaints about illegal content.
- **Storage Obligation:** Deleted content must be preserved for at least 10 weeks for evidentiary purposes.
- **Penalties:** Systematic failures to maintain an adequate complaints management system may result in fines of up to EUR 50 million against the platform. Individual regulatory fines for specific violations can reach EUR 5 million.

**2021 NetzDG Amendments:** Key amendments included:
- Mandatory reporting of certain criminal content (incitement to violence, death threats, child sexual abuse material) directly to the Federal Criminal Police Office (BKA), along with the user's IP address and port number.
- A counter-notification procedure allowing users whose content was removed to contest the decision within two weeks.
- Requirement for platforms to appoint a domestic authorized agent for service of process.

### Key Principles

**Relationship with the DSA:** The EU Digital Services Act, fully applicable since 17 February 2024, has substantially superseded the NetzDG. The DSA provides a harmonized EU-wide framework that largely replaces national platform regulation laws. Germany enacted the Digitale-Dienste-Gesetz (DDG) in May 2024 to implement DSA-related domestic provisions, including designating the Federal Network Agency (Bundesnetzagentur) as the Digital Services Coordinator.

**Bundeskartellamt Platform Regulation:** The Federal Cartel Office has used section 19a GWB (amended in 2021) to designate platforms of "paramount cross-market significance" and prohibit specific anticompetitive practices. Designations have been issued against Alphabet/Google (January 2022), Meta/Facebook (May 2022), Amazon (July 2022), Apple (April 2023), and Microsoft (September 2023).

### Notable Cases

**BVerfG Facebook Real-Name Decision (1 BvR 1073/20, 2022):** The Federal Constitutional Court ruled on whether Facebook's real-name policy violated users' fundamental rights, balancing contractual freedom with personality rights and freedom of expression.

**Bundeskartellamt v. Meta/Facebook (BGH, KVR 69/19, 2020):** The Federal Court of Justice upheld the Bundeskartellamt's order prohibiting Facebook from combining user data from its various services (Facebook, WhatsApp, Instagram) and third-party websites without voluntary consent, finding that the practice constituted an exploitative abuse of dominance under section 19 GWB.

**NetzDG Transparency Report Enforcement (2018-2023):** The Federal Office of Justice (Bundesamt fuer Justiz) issued fines against Facebook (EUR 2 million in 2019) for incomplete transparency reports under NetzDG, establishing that reporting obligations carry real enforcement consequences.

**Telegram Regulation Challenge (2021-2024):** German authorities struggled to enforce NetzDG obligations against Telegram, which refused to appoint a domestic authorized agent or comply with content removal orders, particularly regarding far-right extremist and COVID-19 conspiracy content. The Bundesamt fuer Justiz imposed fines totaling EUR 5.125 million against Telegram in 2022, but enforcement proved difficult given the platform's lack of physical presence in Germany. The case highlighted the jurisdictional challenges of regulating platforms operated from outside the EU, a gap that the DSA's enhanced enforcement mechanisms seek to address.

**German Federal Court on Facebook Terms of Service (BGH, III ZR 179/20, 2021):** The Federal Court of Justice ruled that Facebook's deletion of a user's post and temporary account suspension without prior notice violated the platform's contractual obligations under its terms of service, finding that the terms granting Facebook unilateral content removal authority were invalid standard business terms (AGB) under BGB section 307. The ruling established that platforms must provide adequate notice and opportunity to respond before taking content moderation actions, influencing the development of due process requirements in the DSA.

## ECHR / EU Framework

### Convention Articles

- **Article 10 (Freedom of Expression):** The foundational provision for online speech, protecting both the right to impart and receive information. Restrictions on expression must be "prescribed by law," pursue a legitimate aim, and be "necessary in a democratic society." Platform content moderation mandated by law must satisfy this three-part test.
- **Article 8 (Right to Respect for Private Life):** Protects users' privacy, including their online communications and personal data. State-mandated data retention or content monitoring obligations must be proportionate and accompanied by adequate safeguards.
- **Article 13 (Right to an Effective Remedy):** Users whose content is removed or whose accounts are restricted must have access to effective complaint and redress mechanisms.
- **Article 14 (Prohibition of Discrimination):** Content moderation practices that discriminate on protected grounds may engage Article 14.

### Key Case Law

***Delfi AS v. Estonia* (App. No. 64569/09, Grand Chamber, 2015):** The Grand Chamber held that imposing liability on a commercial online news portal for anonymous defamatory user comments did not violate Article 10 ECHR. The Court emphasized that the portal derived commercial benefit from user comments, the comments were unlawful hate speech, and the portal had failed to prevent or promptly remove them. This decision established that platforms may bear liability for user content in certain circumstances without violating freedom of expression.

***Magyar Tartalomszolgaltatok Egyesulete (MTE) and Index.hu Zrt v. Hungary* (App. No. 22947/13, 2016):** The Court distinguished *Delfi*, finding that imposing liability on a self-regulatory body and a news portal for user comments that were offensive but not hate speech violated Article 10. The decision emphasized the need for context-specific assessment and the distinction between clearly unlawful content and merely offensive expression.

***Cengiz and Others v. Turkey* (App. Nos. 48226/10 and 14027/11, 2015):** The Court found that Turkey's blanket blocking of YouTube violated Article 10, holding that a wholesale ban on accessing a major platform was a disproportionate restriction on the right to receive information.

***Podchasov v. Russia* (App. No. 33696/19, 2024):** The Court found that Russian legislation requiring messaging services to provide decryption keys to security services violated Article 8, holding that weakening encryption for all users was disproportionate. This ruling has significant implications for platform regulation that mandates content scanning or encryption backdoors.

***Big Brother Watch and Others v. United Kingdom* (App. Nos. 58170/13 et al., Grand Chamber, 2021):** The Grand Chamber addressed bulk interception of communications and intelligence sharing, establishing detailed minimum safeguards that apply to state surveillance of online platforms, including independent authorization, limits on search criteria, and oversight mechanisms.

### Member State Obligations

**Digital Services Act (Regulation (EU) 2022/2065):** The DSA establishes a tiered framework for platform regulation:

| Platform Category | Definition | Key Obligations |
|------------------|------------|-----------------|
| **Intermediary Services** | All online intermediaries | Transparency reports; cooperation with authorities; terms of service |
| **Hosting Services** | Services storing information | Notice-and-action mechanisms; statement of reasons for content removal |
| **Online Platforms** | Hosting services making information public | Complaint and redress mechanisms; trusted flaggers; online advertising transparency |
| **Very Large Online Platforms (VLOPs)** | 45M+ monthly active EU users | Systemic risk assessments; independent audits; data access for researchers; crisis response protocols |
| **Very Large Online Search Engines (VLOSEs)** | 45M+ monthly active EU users | Same as VLOPs |

**Risk Assessment and Mitigation (VLOPs/VLOSEs):** Articles 34 and 35 of the DSA require very large online platforms and search engines to conduct annual assessments of systemic risks arising from their services, including risks to public discourse, electoral processes, gender-based violence, and the protection of minors. Based on these assessments, platforms must implement reasonable, proportionate, and effective mitigation measures, which are subject to independent annual audits under Article 37.

**Trusted Flaggers (Article 22):** The DSA establishes a system of "trusted flaggers" -- organizations with demonstrated expertise in detecting illegal content -- whose notices must be processed by platforms with priority. National Digital Services Coordinators award trusted flagger status, and platforms must report on their handling of trusted flagger notices.

**Data Access for Researchers (Article 40):** VLOPs and VLOSEs must provide access to data to vetted independent researchers for the purpose of conducting research on systemic risks. This provision addresses a longstanding demand from the academic community for meaningful access to platform data, though the implementation modalities remain subject to a delegated act by the European Commission.

**Enforcement:** Fines for non-compliance can reach up to 6% of global annual turnover. The European Commission directly supervises VLOPs and VLOSEs, while national Digital Services Coordinators oversee other platforms. In December 2025, the Commission fined X (formerly Twitter) EUR 120 million for DSA violations related to lack of transparency and deceptive design practices.

**Digital Markets Act (Regulation (EU) 2022/1925):** Complements the DSA by regulating "gatekeepers" (large platforms with entrenched market positions), prohibiting self-preferencing, mandating interoperability, and restricting data combination practices.

## Comparative Analysis

| Aspect | South Korea | United States | Germany | EU (DSA) |
|--------|-------------|---------------|---------|----------|
| **Primary Framework** | Network Act; KCSC enforcement | Section 230 immunity | NetzDG (superseded by DSA); DDG | Digital Services Act (2022/2065) |
| **Platform Liability Model** | Conditional immunity with active removal obligations | Broad immunity (Section 230) | NetzDG: strict removal timelines; DSA: notice-and-action | Conditional exemption with due diligence obligations |
| **Content Removal Timeline** | KCSC order-based (no fixed statutory timeline) | No federal requirement | NetzDG: 24 hours (clearly illegal); 7 days (other) | DSA: "expeditious" (no fixed timeline) |
| **Fines / Penalties** | Up to 20 million won (~$15,000) + imprisonment | No federal platform fines; state laws vary | NetzDG: up to EUR 50M; DSA: up to 6% global turnover | Up to 6% of global annual turnover |
| **Transparency Reporting** | Limited mandatory reporting | Voluntary; some state requirements | NetzDG: semi-annual; DSA: annual | Annual for platforms; additional for VLOPs |
| **Child Protection** | Network Act; youth protection regulations | COPPA (under 13); state laws (Utah, NY) | JuSchG; DSA Art. 28 | DSA Art. 28: enhanced protection for minors |
| **Algorithmic Transparency** | Not mandated | Proposed in legislation; state laws emerging | DSA requirement for VLOPs | Mandatory for VLOPs; user opt-out of profiling-based recommendations |
| **Enforcement Body** | KCSC; KCC | FTC; state AGs; courts | Bundesnetzagentur (DSC); BfJ | European Commission (VLOPs); national DSCs |

### Additional Jurisdictions

- **Australia** enacted the Online Safety Act 2021, empowering the eSafety Commissioner to issue removal notices for cyber-abuse, image-based abuse, and content harmful to children, with fines of up to AUD 555,000 per day for non-compliance. In 2024, Australia passed the Online Safety Amendment (Social Media Minimum Age) Act, setting a minimum age of 16 for social media use.
- **India** regulates platforms through the Information Technology (Intermediary Guidelines and Digital Media Ethics Code) Rules, 2021, requiring significant social media intermediaries (5 million+ users) to appoint compliance officers, enable traceability of first originators of harmful content, and remove content within 36 hours of a government order.
- **Japan** amended the Act on the Limitation of Liability for Damages of Specified Telecommunications Service Providers in 2024, requiring large platforms to establish transparent content moderation procedures and provide notice to users before removing content.
- **Brazil** passed the "Fake News Bill" (PL 2630/2020, the Brazilian Internet Freedom, Responsibility, and Transparency Act) in 2024, imposing content moderation obligations, algorithmic transparency, and platform liability for failure to remove court-ordered content.
- **Singapore** enacted the Protection from Online Falsehoods and Manipulation Act (POFMA) in 2019, giving ministers broad authority to order corrections or removal of online falsehoods deemed contrary to the public interest.
- **South Africa** regulates online content through the Films and Publications Amendment Act (2019), which extends the Film and Publications Board's classification mandate to certain online content, and the Cybercrimes Act (2020), which criminalizes the distribution of data messages inciting damage to property or violence.
- **Canada** introduced the Online Harms Act (Bill C-63) in 2024, proposing a Digital Safety Commission to oversee platform compliance with a duty-of-care standard, mandatory removal of child sexual exploitation material within 24 hours, and new Criminal Code offenses for online hate speech.

### Algorithmic Accountability and AI

The regulation of algorithmic recommendation systems represents a frontier issue in social media law:

**Recommender System Transparency:** The EU DSA (Article 27) requires platforms to disclose the main parameters of their recommender systems and provide users with the option to use a recommendation system not based on profiling. This represents the most advanced mandatory algorithmic transparency requirement in any jurisdiction.

**AI-Generated Content:** The EU AI Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689), which entered into force in August 2024 with phased implementation through 2027, classifies AI systems that generate synthetic media (deepfakes) as limited-risk systems subject to transparency obligations. Platforms deploying generative AI for content creation or recommendation must label AI-generated content and maintain technical documentation.

**Amplification Liability:** An emerging legal theory, advanced in several U.S. and European contexts, holds that platforms should be liable not merely for hosting illegal content but for algorithmically amplifying it to maximize engagement. While *Gonzalez v. Google* declined to resolve this question definitively, the DSA's systemic risk assessment requirements and mitigation obligations implicitly address amplification-related harms for VLOPs.

### International Cooperation and Governance

Several international initiatives address cross-border social media regulation challenges:

**OECD Principles on AI (2019, updated 2024):** Provide a framework for trustworthy AI that applies to social media recommendation algorithms, including principles of transparency, accountability, and human oversight.

**G7 and G20 Statements:** The G7 Hiroshima Leaders' Communique (2023) called for platform regulation consistent with democratic values, while the G20 Digital Economy Ministerial Declaration has addressed cross-border content moderation cooperation.

**Internet Governance Forum (IGF):** The UN-mandated multi-stakeholder forum continues to serve as the primary international venue for discussing social media governance, platform regulation, and the intersection of free expression with online safety.

**Christchurch Call (2019):** Following the Christchurch mosque shootings in New Zealand, 51 countries and major tech companies committed to addressing terrorist and violent extremist content online, establishing a framework for crisis response protocols and content-sharing among platforms and governments.
