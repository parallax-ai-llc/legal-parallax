## Summary

Small claims courts -- simplified judicial forums designed to resolve low-value civil disputes quickly, affordably, and without mandatory legal representation -- serve as a critical gateway to justice for individuals and small businesses in every modern legal system. South Korea operates a small claims procedure (소액사건심판) under the Small Claims Trial Act (소액사건심판법, Act No. 2547, 1973), which applies to civil claims not exceeding 30 million KRW (approximately $22,500), with simplified procedural rules including relaxed evidence requirements, single-judge adjudication, oral argument preference, and accelerated timelines, administered by all district courts and branch courts across the country. The United States has no federal small claims court but all 50 states and the District of Columbia maintain small claims divisions within their state court systems, with jurisdictional limits varying dramatically from $2,500 (Kentucky) to $25,000 (Tennessee), and procedural features ranging from mandatory non-attorney representation (some California courts) to full attorney participation, with increasing adoption of online small claims procedures. Germany processes small-value disputes through the regular Amtsgerichte (local courts) under simplified procedures established in the Code of Civil Procedure (Zivilprozessordnung, ZPO), where claims up to EUR 5,000 are heard by single judges under Section 23 of the Courts Constitution Act (Gerichtsverfassungsgesetz, GVG), and disputes up to EUR 600 benefit from the further simplified procedure under ZPO Section 495a permitting determination without oral hearing. The EU has established the European Small Claims Procedure (ESCP) under Regulation (EC) No. 861/2007 (as amended by Regulation (EU) 2015/2421), applicable to cross-border disputes up to EUR 5,000, providing a standardized written procedure across all member states with automatic recognition and enforcement of judgments without the need for a declaration of enforceability, while the ECHR's Article 6 right to a fair trial imposes minimum procedural standards even in simplified proceedings.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's small claims procedure is governed by the Small Claims Trial Act (소액사건심판법), enacted on February 24, 1973, and most recently amended in 2017 to increase the jurisdictional threshold.

**Article 2 (Scope / 적용범위):** The Act applies to civil cases where the amount in dispute does not exceed the amount prescribed by the Supreme Court Regulations. The current threshold, set by Supreme Court Regulation, is 30 million KRW (approximately $22,500).

**Article 4 (Single Judge / 단독판사):** Small claims cases are heard by a single judge rather than a panel, reducing judicial resources required per case.

**Article 7 (Simplified Procedure / 간이절차):** The Act provides for simplified procedural rules:
- Claims may be filed orally at the court clerk's office (Article 5-2)
- The court may conduct proceedings based on written submissions without oral argument (Article 7)
- Evidence rules are relaxed; the court may rely on any materials it deems appropriate (Article 10)
- The court may issue a judgment immediately after the first hearing (Article 7)

**Article 10 (Relaxed Evidence Rules / 증거조사의 간이화):** The court may examine evidence through simplified methods and is not bound by the strict evidence rules of the Civil Procedure Act. The court may rely on telephone testimony, written statements, and other informal evidence.

### Appeals

Small claims judgments are subject to limited appellate review. Under Article 3 of the Small Claims Trial Act, appeal to the Supreme Court (상고) is permitted only on limited grounds: violation of the Constitution, violation of statutes affecting the judgment, or conflict with Supreme Court precedent. This restriction significantly reduces the appellate burden and promotes finality.

### Payment Orders (지급명령)

For undisputed monetary claims, creditors may alternatively seek a payment order (지급명령) under Civil Procedure Act Articles 462-471. The court issues the order without a hearing; if the debtor does not object within two weeks, the order becomes enforceable. This procedure is frequently used for small debts and operates alongside the small claims procedure.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has no unified small claims system; each state, the District of Columbia, and most territories operate their own small claims courts or divisions with varying rules, jurisdictional limits, and procedures.

### Jurisdictional Limits (Selected States)

| State | Limit | Notable Features |
|-------|-------|-----------------|
| California | $10,000 ($5,000 for corporations) | Attorneys generally prohibited; mandatory in-person hearings |
| New York | $10,000 (NYC); $5,000 (elsewhere) | Arbitrators may hear cases with consent; evening sessions |
| Texas | $20,000 | Attorneys permitted; jury trial available on request |
| Tennessee | $25,000 | Highest jurisdictional limit in the nation |
| Kentucky | $2,500 | Lowest jurisdictional limit |
| Florida | $8,000 | Mandatory pre-trial mediation |
| Michigan | $6,500 | No attorneys permitted |

### Procedural Features

**Simplified Filing:** Most states permit filing by completing a standard form; many now offer online filing. Filing fees are nominal, typically $30-$75.

**No-Attorney Rules:** Several states (California, Michigan) prohibit or restrict attorney representation in small claims proceedings, reflecting the courts' origin as forums for self-represented litigants. Other states permit full attorney participation.

**Default Judgments:** If the defendant fails to appear, the court may enter a default judgment. Default judgments constitute a significant percentage of small claims dispositions (estimated 30-40 percent nationally).

**Jury Trial:** A few states (Texas, Massachusetts) permit jury trials in small claims cases upon request by either party, though this is rarely exercised due to increased cost and delay.

### Online Dispute Resolution

Several states have implemented or piloted online small claims resolution:

- **Utah:** The Online Dispute Resolution pilot program, launched in 2018, allows parties to small claims disputes to negotiate and mediate online before appearing in court.
- **Ohio (Franklin County):** Implemented an online small claims resolution platform in 2019.
- **Michigan (various counties):** Expanded online dispute resolution during the COVID-19 pandemic and retained it post-pandemic.

### Enforcement Challenges

A persistent weakness of U.S. small claims courts is enforcement. Judgment creditors are generally responsible for collecting on judgments themselves, with tools including wage garnishment, bank account levy, and property liens varying by state. Studies estimate that 25-50 percent of small claims judgments go uncollected.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany does not maintain separate small claims courts but processes low-value disputes through the regular Amtsgerichte (local courts) under simplified procedural rules.

**GVG Section 23 (Amtsgericht Jurisdiction):** The Amtsgericht has jurisdiction over civil disputes where the amount in controversy does not exceed EUR 5,000. Cases are heard by a single judge (Einzelrichter).

**ZPO Section 495a (Simplified Procedure / Vereinfachtes Verfahren):** For disputes where the amount in controversy does not exceed EUR 600, the court may determine the dispute through any procedure it deems appropriate, including written proceedings without oral hearing. The court retains discretion to schedule an oral hearing if requested by a party. This provision was introduced in 1991 to facilitate resolution of small disputes.

**ZPO Section 128a (Video Hearing):** Since 2013, courts may conduct hearings by video conference with the consent of the parties, a provision increasingly used for small-value disputes.

**Mahnverfahren (Payment Order Procedure, ZPO Sections 688-703d):** For undisputed monetary claims of any amount, creditors may file for a Mahnbescheid (payment order) through the centralized online system (Online-Mahnantrag). If the debtor does not object within two weeks, the creditor may obtain a Vollstreckungsbescheid (enforcement order). The system is highly automated and processes millions of applications annually, with filing fees as low as EUR 36 for claims up to EUR 1,000.

### Attorney Representation

Before the Amtsgericht, parties are not required to be represented by attorneys (no Anwaltszwang). However, before the Landgericht (regional court, handling claims above EUR 5,000) and higher courts, attorney representation is mandatory. This distinction effectively ensures that small claims litigants can proceed without the cost of legal representation.

### Court Fees

Court fees are governed by the Gerichtskostengesetz (Court Fees Act) and are proportional to the amount in dispute:

| Amount in Dispute | Court Fee (approximate) |
|-------------------|------------------------|
| Up to EUR 500 | EUR 38 |
| Up to EUR 1,000 | EUR 62 |
| Up to EUR 2,000 | EUR 100 |
| Up to EUR 5,000 | EUR 168 |

The losing party typically bears all court fees and the prevailing party's attorney fees (if any), in accordance with the German "loser pays" principle (ZPO Section 91).

### Enforcement

Enforcement of small claims judgments is handled through the standard civil enforcement system (Zwangsvollstreckung, ZPO Sections 704-945). Creditors may seek enforcement through Gerichtsvollzieher (court bailiffs) for seizure of movable property, court orders for wage garnishment (Lohnpfändung), and attachment of bank accounts (Kontopfändung). The enforcement system is considered more effective than its U.S. counterpart due to centralized mechanisms and the court bailiff system.

## ECHR / EU

### European Small Claims Procedure (ESCP)

Regulation (EC) No. 861/2007, as amended by Regulation (EU) 2015/2421, establishes a uniform procedure for cross-border small claims within the EU.

**Scope (Article 2):** The ESCP applies to cross-border civil and commercial matters where the value of the claim does not exceed EUR 5,000 (increased from EUR 2,000 by the 2015 amendment), excluding revenue, customs, or administrative matters and certain excluded areas (status of natural persons, matrimonial property, insolvency, social security, employment, tenancies of immovable property).

**Procedure (Articles 4-8):** The claimant initiates the procedure by completing Standard Form A and submitting it to the competent court. The court serves the form on the defendant, who has 30 days to respond. The court may request further written information, take evidence through written statements, and hold an oral hearing only if it considers one necessary. The procedure is primarily written.

**Oral Hearings (Article 8):** The court shall hold an oral hearing only if it considers it necessary or if a party so requests. The court may refuse the request if it considers that an oral hearing is not necessary for the fair conduct of the proceedings. Oral hearings may be held by videoconference or other communication technology.

**Judgments (Article 7):** The court shall give judgment within 30 days of receiving all necessary information. The judgment is enforceable notwithstanding any possible appeal.

**Cross-Border Enforcement (Article 21):** Judgments given under the ESCP are recognized and enforceable in all other member states without the need for a declaration of enforceability (exequatur) and without the possibility of opposing recognition. This automatic enforcement mechanism distinguishes the ESCP from traditional cross-border enforcement procedures.

### ECHR Article 6 -- Fair Trial in Small Claims

The ECHR's right to a fair trial (Article 6) applies to all civil proceedings, including simplified small claims procedures:

**Jussila v. Finland (Application No. 73053/01, Grand Chamber, November 23, 2006):** The Grand Chamber confirmed that Article 6 guarantees are not absolute and that simplified procedures may satisfy fair trial requirements, provided the essence of the right is preserved. The Court recognized that "there are clearly 'criminal charges' of differing weight" and by analogy, civil proceedings of varying complexity may justify differing procedural safeguards.

**Pönka v. Estonia (Application No. 64160/11, November 8, 2016):** The Court found a violation of Article 6 where a party was denied an oral hearing in civil proceedings without adequate justification, establishing that while simplified procedures may limit oral hearings, the right to be heard must be meaningfully protected.

### EU Consumer ADR and Small Claims

The EU's consumer ADR framework (Directive 2013/11/EU) complements the ESCP by providing non-judicial dispute resolution for consumer disputes of any value, including those below the ESCP threshold. The ADR system is particularly relevant for very small claims (under EUR 100) that may not justify even the simplified ESCP procedure.

## Comparative Analysis

### Access to Justice

All four systems aim to provide accessible dispute resolution for small claims, but their approaches differ markedly. South Korea and the United States maintain dedicated small claims procedures/courts with specific simplification rules. Germany integrates small claims into the regular court system with procedural simplifications. The EU ESCP provides a cross-border layer that complements domestic systems.

### Jurisdictional Limits

The range of jurisdictional limits is substantial: from EUR 600 (Germany's Section 495a most simplified procedure) to $25,000 (Tennessee). South Korea's 30 million KRW threshold is comparatively generous. The EU ESCP's EUR 5,000 limit represents a compromise suitable for cross-border consumer disputes.

### Attorney Involvement

The approaches to attorney representation reflect different philosophies. Several U.S. states prohibit attorneys to maintain the informal, pro se nature of small claims proceedings. South Korea, Germany, and the ESCP permit but do not require attorney representation. Germany's lack of attorney requirement before the Amtsgericht, combined with the "loser pays" fee structure, creates a distinctive incentive structure.

### Enforcement

Germany's centralized enforcement system through court bailiffs is the most effective among the four systems. The EU ESCP's automatic cross-border enforcement without exequatur is groundbreaking for international disputes. U.S. small claims enforcement remains the weakest link, with significant uncollected judgments. South Korea's enforcement mechanisms are effective but require creditor initiative through standard civil enforcement procedures.
