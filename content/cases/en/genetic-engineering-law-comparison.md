## Summary

Genetic engineering law -- encompassing the regulation of genetically modified organisms (GMOs), gene editing technologies such as CRISPR-Cas9, and human genetic modification -- represents one of the most scientifically complex and politically contested areas of comparative law. South Korea regulates genetic engineering through the Bioethics and Biosafety Act (생명윤리 및 안전에 관한 법률), the Living Modified Organisms Act (유전자변형생물체의 국가간 이동 등에 관한 법률), and the Food Sanitation Act, prohibiting genetic modification of human embryos while maintaining a mandatory labeling regime for GMO foods and gradually developing a separate framework for gene-edited organisms. The United States employs a product-based regulatory approach through the Coordinated Framework for the Regulation of Biotechnology (1986, updated 2017), distributing oversight across the EPA, USDA, and FDA without requiring mandatory GMO labeling at the federal level until the National Bioengineered Food Disclosure Standard (2022), and generally does not regulate gene-edited organisms lacking foreign DNA as GMOs. Germany applies some of the strictest genetic engineering regulations in the world through the Genetic Engineering Act (Gentechnikgesetz, GenTG) of 1990 and the EU GMO framework, treating gene-edited organisms as GMOs following the European Court of Justice ruling in Case C-528/16 (2018), with criminal penalties of up to five years imprisonment for unauthorized release of GMOs. The EU framework, anchored in Directive 2001/18/EC on the deliberate release of GMOs and Regulation (EC) No. 1829/2003 on GM food and feed, is undergoing potential reform following a 2023 European Commission proposal to create a differentiated regulatory pathway for certain gene-edited plants.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's genetic engineering regulatory regime involves multiple agencies and statutes:

| Legislation | Scope | Enforcing Authority |
|------------|-------|-------------------|
| Bioethics and Biosafety Act (생명윤리 및 안전에 관한 법률) | Human genetic research, embryo research, gene therapy, biobanking | Ministry of Health and Welfare; National Bioethics Committee |
| Living Modified Organisms Act (LMO Act, 유전자변형생물체의 국가간 이동 등에 관한 법률) | Import, export, and domestic movement of LMOs; implements the Cartagena Protocol | Ministry of Trade, Industry and Energy (coordinating); MFDS, MAFRA, MOE |
| Food Sanitation Act (식품위생법) | GMO food safety assessment and labeling | Ministry of Food and Drug Safety (MFDS) |
| Seed Industry Act (종자산업법) | Registration and cultivation of GM crop varieties | Ministry of Agriculture, Food and Rural Affairs (MAFRA) |
| Act on Transboundary Movement of Living Modified Organisms | Implementing legislation for the Cartagena Protocol on Biosafety | Multiple ministries |

Key provisions of the Bioethics and Biosafety Act:

- **Article 47 (Prohibition of Human Cloning):** No person shall create a human clone by somatic cell nuclear transfer or other methods. Violation: imprisonment of up to 10 years.
- **Article 48 (Prohibition of Interspecies Hybridization):** No person shall create a hybrid by combining human and animal embryos or gametes. Violation: imprisonment of up to 10 years.
- **Article 49 (Prohibition of Human Embryo Gene Editing for Reproduction):** No person shall perform genetic modification on a human embryo for the purpose of reproduction. Gene editing of human embryos is permitted only for limited research purposes approved by the Institutional Review Board and the National Bioethics Committee.
- **Article 50 (Gene Therapy Regulation):** Somatic cell gene therapy is permitted subject to regulatory approval. Germline gene therapy (modifications that can be inherited) is prohibited.

### Key Principles

1. **Mandatory GMO Food Labeling:** South Korea requires mandatory labeling of foods containing genetically modified ingredients above a threshold of 3% per ingredient. The labeling requirement applies to 27 categories of processed foods. However, products derived from GMOs where the modified DNA or protein is no longer detectable (e.g., refined oils, sugars) are exempt from labeling.

2. **Safety Assessment for LMOs:** All living modified organisms intended for food, feed, or environmental release must undergo a risk assessment conducted by the relevant ministry. The assessment evaluates potential adverse effects on human health and the environment, including gene flow, allergenicity, toxicity, and nutritional composition.

3. **Gene Editing Regulatory Status:** South Korea has been developing a regulatory framework specifically for gene-edited organisms, recognizing that organisms modified through site-directed nuclease (SDN) technologies like CRISPR-Cas9 may not contain foreign DNA and may be functionally indistinguishable from naturally occurring variants. As of 2025, the government has signaled an intention to establish a differentiated regulatory pathway for gene-edited organisms that do not contain transgenic DNA (SDN-1 and SDN-2 modifications), though formal regulations have not yet been finalized.

### Notable Cases

**Hwang Woo-suk Stem Cell Fraud (2004-2006):** Professor Hwang Woo-suk of Seoul National University published two landmark papers in Science claiming to have created human embryonic stem cells through somatic cell nuclear transfer. Both papers were found to be fabricated. Hwang was convicted of embezzlement and bioethics violations in 2009 and sentenced to two years imprisonment (suspended). The scandal led to significant strengthening of the Bioethics and Biosafety Act and the establishment of more rigorous research oversight mechanisms.

**GM Rice Field Trials Controversy (2011-2014):** Field trials of genetically modified rice varieties developed by the Rural Development Administration (RDA) faced strong opposition from environmental groups and organic farmers. The controversy contributed to South Korea's cautious approach to GM crop cultivation -- as of 2025, no GM crops are commercially cultivated in South Korea, though the country is one of the world's largest importers of GM soybeans, corn, and canola for food and feed use.

## United States

### Federal Framework

The United States regulates genetic engineering through the Coordinated Framework for the Regulation of Biotechnology, first established in 1986 and updated in 2017. This framework distributes regulatory authority across three agencies based on the intended use of the product:

| Agency | Authority | Scope |
|--------|----------|-------|
| USDA-APHIS | Plant Protection Act (7 U.S.C. section 7701 et seq.) | Plant pests, GM crops (field trials, deregulation) |
| EPA | Federal Insecticide, Fungicide, and Rodenticide Act (FIFRA); Toxic Substances Control Act (TSCA) | Pesticide-producing GM plants (e.g., Bt crops); microbial biotechnology products |
| FDA | Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (FDCA, 21 U.S.C. section 301 et seq.) | Food and feed safety of GM products; new animal drugs (including GM animals) |

**Key Regulatory Features:**

- **Product-Based (Not Process-Based) Approach:** The U.S. regulates the end product rather than the method by which it was produced. An organism is regulated based on whether it poses a plant pest risk, contains a pesticidal substance, or raises food safety concerns -- not simply because it was created through genetic engineering.

- **USDA SECURE Rule (2020):** The Sustainable, Ecological, Consistent, Uniform, Responsible, Efficient (SECURE) rule revised USDA-APHIS regulations under 7 CFR Part 340, exempting certain gene-edited plants from regulation if they could have been developed through conventional breeding. Specifically, organisms modified through SDN-1 (simple gene knockouts) or SDN-2 (small targeted changes) without introducing foreign DNA are generally exempt.

- **National Bioengineered Food Disclosure Standard (7 U.S.C. section 1639-1639c):** Enacted in 2016 and fully implemented January 1, 2022, this federal standard requires disclosure of bioengineered food ingredients. Disclosure may be made through text, symbol, electronic/digital link (QR code), or phone number. The standard preempts state labeling laws (notably Vermont's GMO labeling law, Act 120 of 2014). The threshold is 5% per ingredient for inadvertent or technically unavoidable GMO presence.

- **Human Gene Editing:** The FDA regulates human gene therapy as a biological product under the Public Health Service Act (42 U.S.C. section 262) and the FDCA. Germline editing that would produce heritable changes is not explicitly banned by federal statute but is effectively prohibited by a congressional rider (first attached in the Consolidated Appropriations Act of 2016) that prohibits the FDA from accepting or reviewing any application involving "a human embryo [that] is intentionally created or modified to include a heritable genetic modification."

### State Variations

- States generally cannot regulate GMO labeling in a manner that conflicts with the federal National Bioengineered Food Disclosure Standard.
- Several states have enacted restrictions on GM crop cultivation in specific areas (e.g., county-level bans in Oregon, Hawaii, and California's Mendocino, Marin, and Humboldt counties).
- Vermont enacted the first mandatory state GMO labeling law (Act 120, 2014), but it was preempted by the 2016 federal standard.

### Notable Cases

**Diamond v. Chakrabarty, 447 U.S. 303 (1980):** The Supreme Court held 5-4 that a genetically modified bacterium capable of breaking down crude oil was patentable subject matter under 35 U.S.C. section 101. Chief Justice Burger wrote that Congress intended patentable subject matter to include "anything under the sun that is made by man." This landmark decision opened the door to patenting of genetically modified organisms and was foundational to the commercial biotechnology industry.

**Association of Molecular Pathology v. Myriad Genetics, 569 U.S. 576 (2013):** The Supreme Court held unanimously that naturally occurring DNA segments are products of nature and not patent-eligible merely because they have been isolated. However, complementary DNA (cDNA), which is synthetically created, remains patent-eligible. The decision established important boundaries for genetic engineering intellectual property.

**Monsanto Co. v. Geertson Seed Farms, 561 U.S. 139 (2010):** The Supreme Court addressed the scope of environmental review required for USDA deregulation of GM crops (specifically, Roundup Ready alfalfa). The Court held that the district court had exceeded its authority by imposing a nationwide injunction against planting, but acknowledged that NEPA environmental review was required before deregulation.

**He Jiankui CRISPR Baby Experiment (International Implications, 2018):** While this case occurred in China (He Jiankui was sentenced to three years imprisonment by a Chinese court in 2019 for "illegal medical practice"), it had profound implications for U.S. policy. The NIH Director condemned the experiment, and the congressional prohibition on FDA review of heritable gene editing applications was reinforced in subsequent appropriations acts.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's genetic engineering law is among the most restrictive in the world. The primary legislation is:

- **Genetic Engineering Act (Gentechnikgesetz, GenTG):** Enacted on June 20, 1990, and substantially amended multiple times. The GenTG governs all activities involving genetically modified organisms, including contained use, deliberate release, and placing on the market.

- **Genetic Engineering Safety Ordinance (Gentechnik-Sicherheitsverordnung, GenTSV):** Establishes technical safety requirements for genetic engineering facilities, including containment levels (S1 through S4).

- **Novel Food and Feed Regulation (Neuartige Lebensmittel- und Lebensmittelzutaten-Verordnung):** Implements EU novel food regulations at the national level.

- **Embryo Protection Act (Embryonenschutzgesetz, ESchG):** Enacted in 1990, this criminal statute prohibits genetic modification of human germ cells (section 5) and human cloning (section 6). Violations carry imprisonment of up to five years.

Key GenTG provisions:

| Provision | Subject | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| section 1 | Purpose: protect human health, environment, and animals | -- |
| section 3 | Definitions: GMO, genetic engineering operation, release, marketing | -- |
| section 6-12 | Contained use: notification, registration, and authorization requirements | -- |
| section 14-16 | Deliberate release: authorization required from Federal Office for Consumer Protection and Food Safety (BVL) | -- |
| section 16a-16d | Co-existence provisions: buffer zones between GM and conventional/organic crops | -- |
| section 32 | Strict liability (Gefaehrdungshaftung) for damages from GMO properties | Unlimited |
| section 36a-36c | Liability for gene flow to neighboring properties | Strict liability; joint and several liability |
| section 38 | Administrative offenses (Ordnungswidrigkeiten) | Fines up to EUR 50,000 |
| section 39 | Criminal offenses | Imprisonment up to 3 years or fine; up to 5 years for endangering health or life |

### Key Principles

1. **Process-Based Regulation:** Unlike the United States, Germany (and the EU) regulates based on the process of genetic modification, not the characteristics of the final product. Any organism produced through genetic engineering techniques is classified as a GMO, regardless of whether the final product contains foreign DNA or is distinguishable from a naturally occurring variant.

2. **ECJ Ruling on Gene Editing (Case C-528/16, 2018):** The European Court of Justice ruled in Confedration paysanne and Others v. Premier ministre that organisms obtained by mutagenesis techniques that have not conventionally been used and do not have a long safety record -- including CRISPR-Cas9 and other site-directed nuclease technologies -- are GMOs within the meaning of Directive 2001/18/EC and are therefore subject to the full EU GMO regulatory framework. Germany implements this ruling, meaning all CRISPR-edited organisms must undergo the same authorization process as traditional transgenic GMOs.

3. **Opt-Out from GM Crop Cultivation:** Under Directive (EU) 2015/412 (amending Directive 2001/18/EC), member states may restrict or prohibit the cultivation of EU-authorized GM crops on their territory. Germany exercised this opt-out for MON 810 maize (the only GM crop authorized for cultivation in the EU as of 2025), effectively banning all GM crop cultivation in the country.

4. **Strict Liability for GMO Damage:** Section 32 GenTG establishes strict liability (Gefaehrdungshaftung) for damages arising from the specific properties of a GMO or from genetic engineering operations. The liability is unlimited in amount and attaches to the operator of the genetic engineering facility or the person who released the GMO. Sections 36a-36c extend strict liability to cases of gene flow from GM crops to neighboring conventional or organic fields, including joint and several liability where multiple GMO operators are involved.

5. **National Opt-Out and Laender Bans:** In addition to the federal opt-out under Directive (EU) 2015/412, several German states have adopted their own resolutions against GM crop cultivation, and Germany's coalition agreements have consistently included commitments to maintain GM-free agriculture.

### Notable Cases

**BVerfG, 1 BvF 2/05 (November 24, 2010):** The Federal Constitutional Court upheld the constitutionality of the GenTG, including the co-existence provisions (sections 16a-16d) and the strict liability regime (sections 32, 36a-36c). The applicant (the state of Saxony-Anhalt) argued that the liability provisions disproportionately restricted the freedom of agricultural practice. The Court found the provisions justified by the legislature's legitimate aim of protecting organic and conventional farming from GMO contamination, and consistent with the precautionary principle.

**BVL Suspension of MON 810 (2009):** The Federal Office for Consumer Protection and Food Safety (BVL) invoked the safeguard clause under Directive 2001/18/EC to suspend the cultivation of MON 810 maize in Germany, citing new scientific evidence of potential environmental risks to certain butterfly species and aquatic organisms. The decision was upheld by the European General Court.

**Prosecution of Unauthorized GMO Release:** German authorities have prosecuted several cases of unauthorized release of GMOs. Penalties have included fines and, in more serious cases involving deliberate circumvention of containment requirements, criminal prosecution under section 39 GenTG with sentences of up to three years imprisonment.

## ECHR / EU Framework

### Convention Articles

While the ECHR does not specifically address genetic engineering, several articles are relevant:

- **Article 2 (Right to Life):** States have a positive obligation to protect life, which may extend to ensuring adequate regulation of genetic engineering activities that could pose risks to human health.

- **Article 8 (Right to Respect for Private and Family Life):** The right to physical integrity under Article 8 is relevant to human gene therapy and genetic testing. Additionally, the environmental dimension of Article 8 (as developed in Lopez Ostra v. Spain, 1994, and Hatton v. United Kingdom, 2003) may apply to environmental contamination from GMO releases.

- **Article 10 (Freedom of Expression) and Article 11 (Freedom of Assembly):** The right to protest against genetic engineering activities has been addressed in the context of direct action against GM field trials.

- **Oviedo Convention (Convention on Human Rights and Biomedicine, 1997):** Though not part of the ECHR system, this Council of Europe treaty is relevant. Article 13 provides: "An intervention seeking to modify the human genome may only be undertaken for preventive, diagnostic or therapeutic purposes and only if its aim is not to introduce any modification in the genome of any descendants." This effectively prohibits heritable human germline editing.

### Key EU Regulatory Framework

**Directive 2001/18/EC (Deliberate Release of GMOs):**

| Provision | Requirement |
|-----------|------------|
| Art. 2(2) | Definition of GMO: "an organism in which the genetic material has been altered in a way that does not occur naturally" |
| Art. 4 | General obligations: environmental risk assessment, monitoring, traceability, labeling |
| Art. 6-11 | Part B: deliberate release for experimental purposes (field trials) -- requires authorization from national competent authority |
| Art. 12-24 | Part C: placing on the market -- requires EU-level authorization through EFSA risk assessment and comitology |
| Art. 26a | Co-existence: member states may adopt measures to prevent unintended presence of GMOs in conventional and organic crops |
| Art. 26b (added by Dir. 2015/412) | Member state opt-out from cultivation of EU-authorized GMOs |
| Annex I B | Exemptions: organisms produced by mutagenesis techniques "conventionally used" with a "long safety record" |

**Regulation (EC) No. 1829/2003 (GM Food and Feed):**

Authorization procedure for placing GM food and feed on the EU market. Requires EFSA risk assessment, labeling of all food and feed containing, consisting of, or produced from GMOs (threshold: 0.9% per ingredient for adventitious presence).

**Regulation (EC) No. 1830/2003 (Traceability and Labeling of GMOs):**

Establishes traceability requirements throughout the food and feed chain. Operators must transmit and retain information about GMO content at each stage of production and distribution.

### Key Case Law

**CJEU, Case C-528/16, Confedration paysanne v. Premier ministre (July 25, 2018):** The landmark ruling establishing that organisms obtained through new mutagenesis techniques (including CRISPR-Cas9) are GMOs under Directive 2001/18/EC. The Court held that the mutagenesis exemption in Annex I B applies only to techniques with a "long safety record" that have been "conventionally used in a number of applications." New gene editing techniques do not meet these criteria and are therefore subject to full GMO regulation.

**CJEU, Case C-442/09, Bablok v. Freistaat Bayern (September 6, 2011):** The Court held that honey containing pollen from GM maize MON 810 constitutes a food "produced from GMOs" within the meaning of Regulation (EC) No. 1829/2003 and must therefore be authorized before it can be placed on the market. This decision had significant implications for beekeepers near GM crop fields and strengthened the case for strict co-existence measures.

**European Commission Proposal COM(2023) 411 (July 5, 2023):** The Commission proposed a regulation on new genomic techniques (NGTs), creating a two-tier system. "Category 1 NGT plants" -- those with modifications that could also occur naturally or through conventional breeding (up to 20 genetic modifications) -- would be exempt from GMO authorization, labeling, and traceability requirements. "Category 2 NGT plants" -- those with more extensive modifications -- would remain subject to a modified version of the GMO framework. As of early 2026, the legislative process is ongoing, with the European Parliament and Council considering amendments.

### Member State Obligations

EU member states must:

1. Transpose Directive 2001/18/EC into national law and designate a competent authority for GMO authorizations.
2. Conduct environmental risk assessments for deliberate releases.
3. Establish public registers of GM crop cultivation locations.
4. Ensure labeling compliance under Regulations 1829/2003 and 1830/2003.
5. Participate in post-market environmental monitoring of authorized GMOs.
6. Maintain biosafety inspection and enforcement capacity.

### International Comparisons

- **Japan:** Japan's Cartagena Act (カルタヘナ法, 2003) implements the Cartagena Protocol. In 2019, the Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare announced that gene-edited foods without foreign DNA insertion do not require safety review under the Food Sanitation Act, but must be registered through a notification system. This places Japan closer to the U.S. product-based approach for gene-edited organisms.
- **Argentina:** Argentina was the first country to establish a regulatory framework specifically for gene-edited organisms (Resolution 173/2015 of the Ministry of Agriculture). Gene-edited organisms that do not contain transgenic DNA are evaluated on a case-by-case basis and may be exempted from GMO regulation.
- **Brazil:** The National Biosafety Technical Commission (CTNBio) adopted Resolution No. 16 (2018), establishing that organisms developed through innovative precision improvement techniques (including gene editing) that do not contain foreign genetic material are not considered GMOs under the National Biosafety Law (Law No. 11.105/2005).
- **China:** China's Biosafety Law (2020) and the Regulations on the Safety of Agricultural Genetically Modified Organisms govern GMOs. In 2022, China began issuing biosafety certificates for gene-edited crop varieties, and in 2023 it approved the commercial cultivation of gene-edited soybeans, signaling a potential shift toward a more permissive approach to gene editing.
- **India:** The Environment Protection Act (1986) and the Rules for the Manufacture, Use/Import/Export and Storage of Hazardous Micro Organisms/Genetically Engineered Organisms or Cells (1989) govern GMO regulation. India has approved only one GM crop for commercial cultivation (Bt cotton), while the moratorium on GM food crops (including Bt brinjal/eggplant) continues.
- **Australia:** The Gene Technology Act 2000 (Cth) and the Gene Technology Regulator govern GMOs. In 2019, Australia amended the Gene Technology Regulations to exempt organisms modified by SDN-1 (gene knockouts without template DNA) from GMO regulation, adopting an approach similar to the United States.

## Emerging Issues and Future Directions

### Gene Drives and Environmental Release

Gene drives -- genetic engineering systems designed to spread modified genes through wild populations -- represent a frontier regulatory challenge:

- **International Framework:** The Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD) has debated gene drive governance at multiple Conferences of the Parties (COP). COP15 (2022) called for a precautionary approach but stopped short of a moratorium.
- **United States:** DARPA's Safe Genes program has funded gene drive research, while EPA and USDA evaluate environmental release under existing authority. No gene drive organism has been approved for environmental release.
- **EU:** Gene drives fall under Directive 2001/18/EC's deliberate release provisions. The European Group on Ethics in Science and New Technologies (EGE) has called for enhanced governance frameworks before any environmental release is authorized.
- **Germany:** The German Environment Agency (Umweltbundesamt, UBA) has published position papers opposing environmental release of gene drive organisms until comprehensive risk assessment methodologies are developed.

### Synthetic Biology and Regulatory Gaps

Synthetic biology -- the design and construction of new biological parts, devices, and systems -- challenges existing regulatory categories:

- Fully synthetic organisms or minimal genomes may not fit neatly within existing GMO definitions in any jurisdiction.
- The United States addresses synthetic biology through the existing Coordinated Framework, treating synthetic organisms based on their characteristics and intended use.
- The EU's GMO definition in Directive 2001/18/EC arguably encompasses synthetic organisms, but the regulatory framework was not designed with synthetic biology in mind.
- South Korea's Bioethics and Biosafety Act primarily addresses human genetic research and may require amendment to comprehensively address synthetic biology applications.

### Agricultural Biotechnology and Global Trade

Divergent regulatory approaches to GMOs and gene-edited organisms create significant international trade friction:

- The EU's strict regulation of gene-edited organisms (following Case C-528/16) puts it at odds with major agricultural exporters including the United States, Canada, Argentina, Brazil, and Australia, all of which have adopted more permissive approaches to gene-edited organisms that do not contain foreign DNA.
- South Korea, as a major food importer, faces practical challenges in distinguishing between gene-edited and conventionally bred products in imported commodities, particularly given that gene-edited organisms may be molecularly indistinguishable from naturally occurring variants.
- The Codex Alimentarius Commission has not yet established international standards specifically for gene-edited foods, creating a gap in the WTO-recognized food safety standard-setting framework.
- The WTO Agreements on Sanitary and Phytosanitary Measures (SPS) and Technical Barriers to Trade (TBT) are relevant to disputes over divergent GMO regulations. The WTO panel in EC -- Biotech Products (DS291/292/293, 2006) found that the EU's de facto moratorium on GM crop approvals violated its SPS obligations.

### Human Gene Therapy and Clinical Applications

As gene therapy moves from research to clinical application, regulatory frameworks must balance innovation and safety:

- **Approved Gene Therapies:** Several gene therapies have been approved across jurisdictions, including Luxturna (voretigene neparvovec, for inherited retinal dystrophy), Zolgensma (onasemnogene abeparvovec, for spinal muscular atrophy), and various CAR-T cell therapies for cancer. These are regulated as biological products (US FDA), advanced therapy medicinal products (EU EMA), or biological products (South Korea MFDS, Germany through EU framework).
- **CRISPR-Based Therapies:** In December 2023, the UK's MHRA and the US FDA approved Casgevy (exagamglogene autotemcel), the first CRISPR-based gene therapy, for sickle cell disease and transfusion-dependent beta thalassemia. The EU EMA followed in early 2024. This milestone marks the clinical maturation of CRISPR technology.
- **Germline Editing Governance:** The International Commission on the Clinical Use of Human Germline Genome Editing (convened by the U.S. National Academies, the UK Royal Society, and others) published a report in 2020 recommending that heritable human genome editing should not proceed unless strict criteria are met, including established safety and efficacy standards. No jurisdiction currently permits heritable human germline editing.
