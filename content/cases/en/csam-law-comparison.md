## Summary

Child sexual exploitation material (CSAM), also referred to as child sexual abuse material, encompasses any visual depiction of sexually explicit conduct involving a minor. The production, distribution, possession, and access to such material constitutes a grave criminal offense in virtually every jurisdiction worldwide, reflecting a universal consensus that children require absolute protection from sexual exploitation. Despite this shared objective, the legal frameworks governing CSAM offenses differ substantially across jurisdictions in their definitions, penalty structures, treatment of virtual or AI-generated material, platform liability regimes, and enforcement mechanisms. South Korea addresses CSAM primarily through the Act on the Protection of Children and Youth Against Sex Offenses (adonq-cheongsonyeonui seongbohoe gwanhan beomnyul), which underwent significant amendments following the "Nth Room" scandal of 2018-2020 and the deepfake crisis of 2024. The United States maintains one of the most extensive federal frameworks through 18 U.S.C. Sections 2251-2260, the PROTECT Act of 2003, and the EARN IT Act, supported by landmark Supreme Court jurisprudence establishing that CSAM falls outside First Amendment protection. Germany criminalizes CSAM under Section 184b of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), though the controversial June 2024 amendment reclassified certain possession offenses from felonies (Verbrechen) back to misdemeanors (Vergehen), sparking significant public debate. At the supranational level, the Council of Europe's Lanzarote Convention (CETS No. 201, 2007) and EU Directive 2011/93/EU establish harmonized minimum standards for criminalization, victim protection, and cross-border cooperation across European states, with an ongoing revision process initiated in 2024 to address technological developments including AI-generated material and livestreamed abuse.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's legal regime for combating child sexual exploitation material is anchored in the Act on the Protection of Children and Youth Against Sex Offenses (adonq-cheongsonyeonui seongbohoe gwanhan beomnyul), first enacted in 2000 and substantially amended on multiple occasions, most recently in October 2024 (Act No. 20462). The Act defines "child or youth pornography" broadly to encompass any depiction -- whether video, film, photograph, or other visual medium -- that presents a person under 19 years of age (or a person who appears to be under 19) engaged in sexual conduct. This definition is notably expansive compared to many jurisdictions, as it extends protection to all persons under 19 and covers depictions of apparent minors.

**Article 11 -- Core CSAM Offenses:**

| Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|
| Production, import, or export of child/youth pornography | Imprisonment for not less than 5 years up to life |
| Selling, lending, distributing for commercial purposes; possession or transport for such purposes | Imprisonment for not more than 10 years |
| Distribution, provision, or public exhibition (non-commercial) | Imprisonment for not more than 7 years or fine not exceeding 50 million KRW |
| Possession with knowledge | Imprisonment for not more than 1 year or fine not exceeding 20 million KRW |
| Procuring a child for a producer | Imprisonment for not less than 3 years |

**Supplementary Legislation:** The Act on Special Cases Concerning the Punishment of Sexual Crimes provides additional mechanisms for enhanced sentencing, electronic monitoring, and public disclosure of offender information. The Telecommunications Business Act and the Act on Promotion of Information and Communications Network Utilization and Information Protection impose obligations on internet service providers and platform operators to remove CSAM upon detection or notification.

### Landmark Case: The Nth Room Scandal (2018-2020)

The "Nth Room" case represents South Korea's most significant CSAM prosecution and a catalyst for sweeping legislative reform. Between 2018 and 2020, a network of perpetrators operated encrypted chatrooms on the Telegram messaging platform through which they blackmailed victims -- at least 103 confirmed, including 26 minors -- into producing sexually exploitative material. The material was then distributed to paying subscribers.

**Cho Ju-bin**, the principal organizer, was convicted of violating the Act on the Protection of Children and Youth Against Sex Offenses and operating a criminal enterprise. He received an initial sentence of 40 years' imprisonment in November 2020 from the Seoul Central District Court, later increased to 42 years following appellate proceedings in October 2021, and subsequently to 47 years after the Supreme Court upheld an additional conviction for sexually assaulting a minor in 2019. His sentence includes 30 years of electronic ankle monitoring upon release and a fine of 10.64 million KRW.

**Moon Hyung-wook** ("GodGod"), the original creator of the Nth Room chatrooms, was sentenced to 34 years in prison, upheld by the Supreme Court in April 2022.

The case prompted the National Assembly to strengthen penalties for digital sex crimes, expand the definition of exploitation material, and enhance law enforcement investigative powers for encrypted platforms.

### 2024 Deepfake Amendments

In September 2024, South Korea amended the Act on Special Cases Relating to Punishments for Sexual Offences to address the proliferation of deepfake pornography. The amendments impose a maximum penalty of seven years' imprisonment for creating or disseminating deepfake sexual content, and up to three years' imprisonment or a fine of up to 30 million KRW for possessing or viewing such material. These provisions apply regardless of whether the depicted individual is a minor, though penalties are enhanced when victims are children or youth.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States maintains one of the world's most comprehensive federal frameworks for combating CSAM, built upon a series of statutes codified primarily in Title 18 of the United States Code, supported by robust judicial precedent that has carved CSAM entirely out of First Amendment protection.

**Core Federal Statutes:**

| Statute | Offense | Penalty (First Offense) | Penalty (Repeat Offense) |
|---------|---------|------------------------|--------------------------|
| 18 U.S.C. Section 2251 | Sexual exploitation of children (production) | 15-30 years | 25-50 years |
| 18 U.S.C. Section 2252(a)(1)-(3) | Transportation, receipt, or distribution | 5-20 years | 15-40 years |
| 18 U.S.C. Section 2252(a)(4) | Possession | Up to 10 years (20 years if victim under 12) | 10-20 years |
| 18 U.S.C. Section 2252A | Material involving exploitation of minors (broader scope) | Varies by subsection | Enhanced penalties |
| 18 U.S.C. Section 2260 | Production outside the United States | 15-30 years | 25-50 years |

**Definitions (18 U.S.C. Section 2256):** "Child pornography" is defined as any visual depiction involving the use of a minor engaging in sexually explicit conduct, including photographs, film, video, digital images, and computer-generated imagery that is indistinguishable from an actual minor. The EARN IT Act modernized federal statutory language, replacing "child pornography" with "child sexual abuse material" to reflect the exploitative nature of the content.

**PROTECT Act of 2003:** The Prosecutorial Remedies and Other Tools to End the Exploitation of Children Today Act expanded federal jurisdiction to cover virtual child pornography, criminalizing obscene computer-generated imagery depicting minors in sexually explicit situations. This legislation responded directly to the Supreme Court's ruling in Ashcroft v. Free Speech Coalition (2002) by targeting obscene virtual depictions rather than relying on the broader category struck down in that case.

**EARN IT Act:** The Eliminating Abusive and Rampant Neglect of Interactive Technologies Act modified Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act to permit state-law claims against online platforms relating to CSAM distribution. It also enhanced reporting obligations to the National Center for Missing and Exploited Children (NCMEC) CyberTipline and strengthened criminal enforcement mechanisms.

### Landmark Cases

**New York v. Ferber, 458 U.S. 747 (1982):** The Supreme Court unanimously held that child pornography is not protected speech under the First Amendment, establishing a new category of unprotected expression distinct from obscenity. The Court identified five reasons justifying this exclusion: the compelling government interest in preventing child exploitation; the intrinsic connection between distribution and abuse; the economic incentive that distribution creates for production; the negligible value of such material; and the consistency of this categorization with prior First Amendment principles. Paul Ferber, a Manhattan bookstore owner, had been convicted under New York Penal Law for selling films depicting young boys engaged in sexual conduct.

**Osborne v. Ohio, 495 U.S. 103 (1990):** The Court extended Ferber to uphold the constitutionality of criminalizing the mere possession of child pornography, reasoning that the state's interest in protecting child victims justified regulation at every level of the distribution chain. The Court held that banning possession serves to "dry up the market" for exploitative material and removes the permanent record of abuse from circulation.

**Ashcroft v. Free Speech Coalition, 535 U.S. 234 (2002):** The Court struck down provisions of the Child Pornography Prevention Act of 1996 (CPPA) that criminalized virtual child pornography produced without real children. Justice Kennedy, writing for the majority, held that the CPPA was overbroad because it banned "a significant universe of speech that is neither obscene under Miller nor child pornography under Ferber." This ruling clarified that the Ferber exception applies specifically because real children are harmed in the production process, not because of the content's subject matter alone. Congress responded with the PROTECT Act, which narrowed the prohibition to obscene virtual depictions.

**United States v. Paroline, 572 U.S. 434 (2014):** The Supreme Court addressed the question of restitution in CSAM cases, holding that each possessor of child exploitation material may be ordered to pay restitution to the depicted victim in an amount reflecting the individual defendant's relative contribution to the victim's losses, rather than the full amount of the victim's total damages.

### Enforcement Architecture

The United States employs a multi-agency enforcement framework: the Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI) Internet Crime Against Children (ICAC) Task Force Program, the Department of Homeland Security Investigations (HSI) Cyber Crimes Center, and the Department of Justice's Child Exploitation and Obscenity Section (CEOS) coordinate federal investigations. The NCMEC CyberTipline processes millions of reports annually from electronic service providers pursuant to mandatory reporting requirements under 18 U.S.C. Section 2258A.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany criminalizes CSAM offenses under Section 184b of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), which addresses the dissemination, acquisition, and possession of child pornographic content (Verbreitung, Erwerb und Besitz kinderpornographischer Inhalte). The provision distinguishes between material involving children under 14 years of age (Section 184b) and material involving juveniles aged 14 to 17 (Section 184c, Jugendpornographie), with the latter carrying lower penalties.

**Section 184b StGB -- Current Penalties (since 28 June 2024):**

| Offense | Penalty Range |
|---------|--------------|
| Dissemination of child pornographic content | 6 months to 5 years imprisonment |
| Acquisition and possession of child pornographic content | 3 months to 5 years imprisonment |
| Production of child pornographic content | 3 months to 5 years imprisonment |
| Commercial dissemination or gang-related offenses involving actual/realistic conduct | 6 months to 10 years imprisonment |
| Possession of content depicting actual abuse (aggravated) | 1 year to 10 years imprisonment |

**Definition:** Section 184b defines child pornographic content (kinderpornographische Inhalte) as content relating to sexual acts performed by, on, or in the presence of a child (person under 14), the reproduction of a child's genitalia in a sexually provocative manner, or a realistic depiction of a child's body engaged in sexual activity.

### The 2024 Amendment Controversy

The most significant recent development in German CSAM law is the amendment that came into force on 28 June 2024, which reclassified possession and distribution offenses under Section 184b from Verbrechen (crimes/felonies carrying a minimum sentence of one year) back to Vergehen (misdemeanors). This reversal of a 2021 reform generated substantial public and political controversy.

**Background of the 2021 Reform:** In June 2021, the German legislature elevated all Section 184b offenses to felony status (Verbrechen) with a mandatory minimum sentence of one year imprisonment. While intended to signal the severity of these offenses, the reform produced unintended consequences: prosecutors lost the discretion to dismiss minor or borderline cases (such as parents forwarding material to police, or teenagers sharing images within peer relationships), judges could no longer impose suspended sentences for first-time offenders, and a conviction automatically resulted in entry in the Federal Central Criminal Register with severe collateral consequences including loss of public employment.

**Constitutional Concerns:** The Bundesverfassungsgericht (Federal Constitutional Court) addressed these issues in a September 2024 decision (2 BvR 618/24), finding that Section 184b(3) Alt. 3 StGB in its pre-amendment form -- with a mandatory minimum of one year and no provision for less serious cases (minder schwerer Fall) -- was incompatible with the principle of proportionality (Verhaeltnismaessigkeitsgrundsatz) and the prohibition of excess. This constitutional ruling vindicated the legislature's decision to reclassify the offense.

**Current Classification:** Since June 2024, basic possession carries a minimum of three months' imprisonment, and distribution carries a minimum of six months'. These penalties remain significantly higher than pre-2021 levels but restore prosecutorial and judicial flexibility. Aggravated offenses involving commercial activity, organized groups, or material depicting actual abuse retain enhanced penalty ranges.

### Enforcement

Germany's Federal Criminal Police Office (Bundeskriminalamt, BKA) operates specialized units for combating online child sexual exploitation. The BKA cooperates with Europol's European Cybercrime Centre (EC3) and participates in international operations. The Network Enforcement Act (Netzwerkdurchsetzungsgesetz, NetzDG) and the EU Digital Services Act (DSA) impose content moderation and reporting obligations on platforms operating in Germany.

## ECHR and EU Framework

### Council of Europe: Lanzarote Convention (CETS No. 201)

The Convention on the Protection of Children against Sexual Exploitation and Sexual Abuse, adopted on 25 October 2007 in Lanzarote, Spain, and entering into force on 1 July 2010, is the first international treaty to criminalize all forms of sexual abuse against children, including offenses committed within the family or home environment. As of 2026, it has been ratified by all 46 Council of Europe member states and signed by several non-member states.

**Key Provisions:**

| Article | Subject | Requirement |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| Art. 20 | Child pornography offenses | Criminalize production, offering, distribution, transmission, procurement, and possession |
| Art. 20(3) | Virtual material | Parties may reserve the right not to apply provisions to realistic images not involving real children |
| Art. 21 | Solicitation (grooming) | Criminalize intentional solicitation of a child for sexual purposes |
| Art. 23 | Solicitation through ICT | Criminalize solicitation through information and communication technologies |
| Art. 25 | National jurisdiction | Establish jurisdiction over offenses committed on territory, by nationals, or against nationals |
| Art. 30 | Principles of prosecution | Ensure effective prosecution in the best interests of the child |
| Art. 31 | General protective measures | Ensure that investigations and proceedings do not aggravate the trauma experienced by the child |

**Lanzarote Committee:** The Committee of the Parties to the Convention (the Lanzarote Committee) monitors implementation through thematic rounds of monitoring. The Committee has issued opinions on the protection of children against sexual exploitation through ICT, the protection of children in the circle of trust, and the management of sexual offenders.

### European Union: Directive 2011/93/EU

Directive 2011/93/EU of the European Parliament and of the Council of 13 December 2011 on combating the sexual abuse and sexual exploitation of children and child pornography establishes minimum rules for the definition of criminal offenses and sanctions across EU member states, replacing the earlier Framework Decision 2004/68/JHA.

**Minimum Maximum Penalties Required:**

| Offense | Minimum Maximum Penalty |
|---------|------------------------|
| Production of child pornography (Art. 5(4)) | 3 years imprisonment |
| Distribution, dissemination, or transmission (Art. 5(4)) | 2 years imprisonment |
| Offering, supplying, or making available (Art. 5(4)) | 2 years imprisonment |
| Acquisition or possession (Art. 5(2)) | 1 year imprisonment |
| Knowingly obtaining access via ICT (Art. 5(3)) | 1 year imprisonment |

These are minimum harmonization standards -- member states must provide for at least these maximum penalties but may impose higher ones. The Directive also addresses grooming (Art. 6), coerced participation in pornographic performances (Art. 4), and establishes provisions on extraterritorial jurisdiction (Art. 17), victim assistance (Art. 18-19), and preventive measures including intervention programs for offenders (Art. 24).

**Definition of Child Pornography (Art. 2(c)):** The Directive defines child pornography as any material visually depicting a child engaged in real or simulated sexually explicit conduct, or any depiction of a child's sexual organs for primarily sexual purposes, or any realistic depiction of a child engaged in sexually explicit conduct. Member states retain discretion on whether to criminalize realistic depictions not involving real children.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**K.U. v. Finland (Application No. 2872/02, 2008):** The European Court of Human Rights held that Finland violated Article 8 (right to respect for private and family life) of the European Convention by failing to provide a legal framework enabling the identification of a person who posted a sexual advertisement about a 12-year-old boy on an internet dating site. The Court emphasized that states bear a positive obligation to ensure effective criminal law protection against sexual exploitation, stating that "practical and effective protection of the applicant required that effective steps be taken to identify and prosecute the perpetrator." This ruling established that privacy protections for internet users cannot be absolute when they obstruct the investigation of sexual offenses against children.

**M.C. v. Bulgaria (Application No. 39272/98, 2003):** While primarily a sexual assault case, the Court's reasoning -- that states must penalize and effectively prosecute any non-consensual sexual act, including where the victim has not physically resisted -- has been applied in the broader context of child sexual exploitation to reinforce the principle of positive state obligations in protecting minors.

### Ongoing Reform: Revision of Directive 2011/93/EU

In March 2024, the European Commission proposed a comprehensive revision of Directive 2011/93/EU to address technological developments and emerging threats. Key elements of the proposed revision include:

- Expanded definitions to cover livestreamed sexual abuse and AI-generated CSAM
- Strengthened provisions on grooming and self-generated material obtained through coercion
- Enhanced obligations for cross-border cooperation and information sharing
- Alignment with the proposed EU Regulation on preventing and combating child sexual abuse (the "CSAM Regulation"), which would establish mandatory detection obligations for online service providers
- Updated minimum penalty thresholds reflecting the increased severity and scale of online exploitation

## Comparative Analysis

### Definitional Scope

The jurisdictions examined exhibit significant variation in their definitional approaches. South Korea's framework is notably broad, protecting all persons under 19 and extending to depictions of apparent minors, reflecting a protective stance that encompasses late adolescence. The United States protects persons under 18, consistent with international standards, while Germany's Section 184b applies specifically to children under 14, with a separate and less severe provision (Section 184c) covering juveniles aged 14-17. The EU Directive sets the standard at under 18, aligning with the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child.

### Penalty Severity

The United States imposes the most severe penalties among the jurisdictions studied, with mandatory minimum sentences of 15 years for production and 5 years for distribution. South Korea's penalty structure is also severe for production (minimum 5 years to life), though possession penalties remain comparatively mild (maximum 1 year or fine). Germany's June 2024 reclassification lowered the practical severity of its penalties, prompting criticism from child protection organizations while being upheld as constitutionally necessary by the Federal Constitutional Court. The EU Directive establishes floor requirements that member states must meet or exceed.

### Treatment of Virtual and AI-Generated Material

All four jurisdictions address virtual or computer-generated CSAM, though with differing approaches. The United States, following Ashcroft v. Free Speech Coalition, criminalizes only obscene virtual depictions under the PROTECT Act, preserving a constitutional distinction between material produced through actual child abuse and artificially generated content. South Korea and Germany criminalize realistic depictions regardless of whether real children were involved. The EU Directive and Lanzarote Convention cover "realistic images" but allow member states to reserve application regarding material not involving real children.

### Platform Accountability

The regulatory burden on online platforms varies considerably. The United States has moved toward greater platform accountability through the EARN IT Act's modification of Section 230 immunity, while maintaining mandatory reporting obligations through 18 U.S.C. Section 2258A. The EU's Digital Services Act (DSA) and the proposed CSAM Regulation would impose the most comprehensive platform obligations globally, including potential mandatory detection requirements. South Korea requires immediate removal upon detection or notification. Germany applies NetzDG and DSA obligations, including reporting and content moderation requirements.

### Enforcement Challenges

Common challenges across jurisdictions include the proliferation of encrypted platforms, the growing volume of AI-generated material that complicates detection and verification, jurisdictional conflicts in cross-border investigations, the increasing use of cryptocurrency for payment, and the tension between privacy rights and investigative necessity. The Nth Room case in South Korea and ongoing debates around the EU's proposed CSAM Regulation illustrate the difficulty of balancing effective enforcement against encrypted communications with fundamental rights protections.
