## Summary

Organic certification -- the regulatory process by which agricultural products are verified as meeting defined standards for organic production, processing, and labeling -- has become a major area of international agricultural law as consumer demand for organic products has grown into a global market exceeding $200 billion annually. South Korea regulates organic certification through the Environmentally-Friendly Agriculture Promotion Act (친환경농어업 육성 및 유기식품 등의 관리·지원에 관한 법률), first enacted in 1997 and comprehensively amended in 2013 to align with international standards, administered by the National Agricultural Products Quality Management Service (국립농산물품질관리원, NAQS) under the Ministry of Agriculture, Food, and Rural Affairs. The United States governs organic certification through the Organic Foods Production Act of 1990 (7 U.S.C. Section 6501 et seq.) and the USDA National Organic Program (NOP) regulations (7 C.F.R. Part 205), administered by the Agricultural Marketing Service, which establishes a comprehensive framework of production standards, accredited certifying agents, and the "USDA Organic" seal. Germany, as an EU member state, operates under Regulation (EU) 2018/848 on organic production and labelling of organic products, which replaced the prior Regulation (EC) No. 834/2007 effective January 1, 2022, and additionally maintains its own national Bio-Siegel (organic seal) under the Öko-Kennzeichengesetz of 2001 and the stricter private standards of associations such as Bioland, Naturland, and Demeter. At the EU level, the organic regulatory framework harmonizes production rules, control systems, and trade provisions across all 27 member states, with the European Court of Justice interpreting organic labeling as a form of consumer protection under the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union, while international equivalence agreements between the EU, US, South Korea, and other jurisdictions facilitate cross-border organic trade.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's organic certification system is governed by the Environmentally-Friendly Agriculture Promotion Act (친환경농어업 육성 및 유기식품 등의 관리·지원에 관한 법률), originally enacted in 1997 and significantly restructured by amendment in 2013 to create a standalone organic certification framework aligned with Codex Alimentarius guidelines and international equivalence requirements.

**Article 19 (Organic Certification / 유기인증):** Persons wishing to produce, process, or handle organic agricultural products must obtain certification from a certifying body accredited by the National Agricultural Products Quality Management Service (NAQS, 국립농산물품질관리원). The NAQS accredits both domestic and foreign certifying bodies.

**Article 20 (Certification Standards / 인증기준):** Organic production standards prohibit the use of synthetic chemical pesticides, synthetic fertilizers, genetically modified organisms, and growth regulators. Land must undergo a minimum conversion period of three years for crops and one year for livestock before products may be certified as organic.

**Article 23 (Labeling Requirements / 표시기준):** Certified organic products must bear the official organic mark (유기농산물 or 유기가공식품) and display the name and certification number of the certifying body. Products containing at least 95 percent organic ingredients may be labeled as "organic"; products containing 70-95 percent organic ingredients may use the designation "made with organic [ingredient]."

### Categories of Certification

| Category | Requirements | Label |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| Organic (유기) | Full organic standards; no synthetic chemicals; 3-year conversion | 유기농산물 |
| Non-Pesticide (무농약) | No chemical pesticides; limited chemical fertilizer permitted | 무농약농산물 |
| Organic Processed Food (유기가공식품) | 95%+ organic ingredients; approved processing aids only | 유기가공식품 |
| Organic Livestock (유기축산물) | Organic feed; animal welfare standards; outdoor access | 유기축산물 |

### Penalties

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| False organic labeling | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine up to 30 million KRW |
| Use of organic label without certification | Fine up to 10 million KRW; product seizure |
| Certifying body negligence | Accreditation suspension (6-12 months) or revocation |
| Failure to maintain records | Fine up to 5 million KRW |

### Equivalence Agreements

South Korea has established organic equivalence agreements with the United States (2014), the European Union (2015), and other jurisdictions. Under the Korea-US equivalence arrangement, products certified as organic under either country's system may be sold as organic in the other market without recertification, subject to compliance with specific residue testing requirements.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States established its organic regulatory framework through the Organic Foods Production Act of 1990 (OFPA, 7 U.S.C. Section 6501 et seq.), enacted as Title XXI of the 1990 Farm Bill. The USDA's National Organic Program (NOP), codified at 7 C.F.R. Part 205, implements the OFPA and has been in effect since October 21, 2002.

**National Organic Standards Board (NOSB):** The OFPA establishes a 15-member advisory board composed of organic farmers, handlers, retailers, consumer advocates, environmentalists, and scientists. The NOSB makes recommendations to the Secretary of Agriculture regarding the National List of Allowed and Prohibited Substances, which specifies the limited synthetic substances permitted in organic production and the natural substances prohibited.

**Accredited Certifying Agents:** The NOP accredits public and private entities as certifying agents. As of 2025, approximately 80 certifying agents are accredited, including domestic organizations such as Oregon Tilth, California Certified Organic Farmers (CCOF), and Quality Assurance International, as well as foreign certifying agents operating in more than 120 countries.

### Labeling Categories

| Category | Organic Content | Label Permitted |
|----------|----------------|-----------------|
| "100% Organic" | 100% organic ingredients | USDA Organic seal; "100% Organic" |
| "Organic" | 95-100% organic ingredients | USDA Organic seal; "Organic" |
| "Made with Organic [Ingredients]" | 70-95% organic ingredients | May list up to 3 organic ingredients; no USDA seal |
| Less than 70% organic | Below 70% organic ingredients | May list organic ingredients in information panel only |

### Strengthening Organic Enforcement Rule (2024)

The USDA published the Strengthening Organic Enforcement (SOE) final rule on January 19, 2023, with full compliance required by March 19, 2024. This rule represents the most significant update to organic regulations since the NOP's inception and addresses fraud vulnerabilities identified in the organic supply chain:

- **Import Certificates:** All organic imports must be accompanied by a NOP Import Certificate issued through the USDA's Organic Integrity Database.
- **Supply Chain Traceability:** All certified operations in the supply chain must maintain records sufficient to verify organic integrity from farm to final sale.
- **Unannounced Inspections:** Certifying agents must conduct unannounced inspections of at least 5 percent of their certified operations annually.
- **Fraud Investigation Authority:** The NOP's authority to investigate and take enforcement action against uncertified entities making fraudulent organic claims was strengthened.

### Penalties

The OFPA authorizes civil penalties of up to $11,000 per violation for any person who knowingly sells or labels a product as organic when it does not meet NOP standards. Criminal penalties for organic fraud may be pursued under 18 U.S.C. Section 1001 (false statements) and 18 U.S.C. Section 1341 (mail fraud). In United States v. Constant (2019), a Missouri farmer was sentenced to 10 years in prison for a $142 million organic grain fraud scheme in which conventionally grown crops were sold as certified organic.

### Landmark Cases

**Harvey v. Veneman, 396 F.3d 28 (1st Cir. 2005):** The First Circuit ruled that the USDA had improperly allowed synthetic substances in organic processed foods, leading to a Congressional amendment (Section 771 of the 2006 Agricultural Appropriations Act) that preserved the NOP's approach to the National List while adding additional oversight requirements.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's organic certification operates within a dual framework: the mandatory EU organic regulation and additional national and private standards.

**EU Regulation 2018/848:** As an EU member state, Germany applies Regulation (EU) 2018/848 on organic production and labelling of organic products, effective January 1, 2022. This regulation establishes harmonized rules for organic production, processing, labeling, control, and trade across all EU member states.

**Öko-Kennzeichengesetz (Organic Label Act, 2001):** Germany's national organic label, the Bio-Siegel, was introduced under this statute. The hexagonal Bio-Siegel, featuring green lettering on a white background, certifies compliance with EU organic standards and is used alongside the mandatory EU organic logo. The Bio-Siegel remains one of the most recognized organic labels in Europe, with consumer recognition exceeding 90 percent in Germany.

**Öko-Landbaugesetz (Organic Farming Act, 2002):** This statute designates the competent authorities for organic control in Germany, assigns enforcement responsibilities to the federal states (Länder), and establishes the framework for cooperation between federal and state authorities.

### Private Organic Standards

Germany is home to several prominent private organic certification associations whose standards exceed the EU minimum requirements:

| Association | Founded | Key Differences from EU Standard |
|-------------|---------|----------------------------------|
| Bioland | 1971 | 100% organic farm required; stricter fertilizer limits; no hydroponic production |
| Naturland | 1982 | Social standards; aquaculture certification; forestry certification |
| Demeter | 1928 | Biodynamic agriculture; strictest animal welfare standards; mandatory composting |
| Biokreis | 1979 | Regional focus; stricter slaughter transport limits |

### Control System

Organic control in Germany is performed by approximately 20 state-approved private control bodies (Kontrollstellen), supervised by the Bundesanstalt für Landwirtschaft und Ernährung (BLE, Federal Office for Agriculture and Food) and the state-level competent authorities. Each certified operator receives an annual inspection, with additional risk-based and unannounced inspections.

### Penalties

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| Unauthorized use of Bio-Siegel | Fine up to EUR 30,000 (Öko-Kennzeichengesetz Section 4) |
| Fraudulent organic labeling | Criminal prosecution under StGB Section 263 (fraud); fine or imprisonment up to 5 years |
| Violation of EU organic production rules | Withdrawal of organic certification; marketing prohibition |
| Control body negligence | Suspension or withdrawal of approval |

### Market Context

Germany has the largest organic market in Europe, with retail sales exceeding EUR 15 billion in 2023. The German federal government's agricultural policy strategy targets 30 percent organic farming by 2030, up from approximately 14 percent of agricultural land in 2024. This target is supported through the CAP strategic plan, which provides area-based payments for organic conversion and maintenance.

## ECHR / EU

### EU Regulation 2018/848

Regulation (EU) 2018/848 of the European Parliament and of the Council of May 30, 2018, on organic production and labelling of organic products, replaced the prior framework (Regulation (EC) No. 834/2007) effective January 1, 2022. Key provisions include:

**Article 3 (Definitions):** Defines "organic production" as a sustainable management system for agriculture that combines best environmental and climate action practices, a high level of biodiversity, and preservation of natural resources.

**Article 5-12 (Production Rules):** Establishes detailed rules for organic plant production (prohibiting synthetic pesticides, mineral nitrogen fertilizers, and GMOs), organic livestock production (requiring organic feed, outdoor access, restrictions on antibiotics, and species-specific animal welfare standards), and organic aquaculture.

**Article 30 (Organic Logo):** The EU organic logo ("Euro-leaf") is mandatory on all pre-packaged organic food products produced in the EU. Products must also display the code number of the control body and an indication of the place of farming (EU Agriculture, non-EU Agriculture, or specific country).

**Article 45-46 (Trade / Equivalence):** The regulation provides for recognition of third-country organic standards as equivalent to EU standards, enabling import of organic products from recognized countries without recertification. The EU has granted equivalence recognition to organic standards in the United States, South Korea, Japan, Switzerland, and several other jurisdictions.

### Group Certification

A significant innovation of Regulation 2018/848 is the introduction of group certification for small farmers (Article 36), allowing groups of small operators to be certified collectively. This provision is particularly relevant for developing-country producers exporting to the EU market and reduces the per-farmer cost of certification.

### Official Controls -- Regulation (EU) 2017/625

Organic control is integrated into the broader EU framework for official controls on food and feed under Regulation (EU) 2017/625. Control bodies must be accredited under ISO/IEC 17065 and approved by the competent authority of the member state. The European Commission conducts audits of member state control systems and third-country organic control authorities.

### Court of Justice of the European Union

**Case C-497/17, Aldi (2019):** The CJEU ruled that products labeled as organic that are found to contain pesticide residues above certain thresholds cannot continue to be marketed as organic, even if the contamination was unintentional, unless the operator can demonstrate that the contamination was adventitious and technically unavoidable.

**Case C-137/13, Herbaria Kräuterparadies (2014):** The Court held that organic processed food products may not contain vitamins and minerals that are not derived from organic agricultural ingredients, unless their addition is directly required by law. The ruling strengthened the integrity of organic labeling by preventing the routine addition of synthetic nutrients to organic products.

### Fraud Prevention

The European Anti-Fraud Office (OLAF) and Europol have conducted multiple investigations into organic food fraud, including Operation OPSON (annual), which targets fraudulent organic claims among other food fraud. The European Commission's 2023 Action Plan for organic production emphasized strengthening traceability and control mechanisms, particularly for imported organic products.

## Comparative Analysis

### Regulatory Architecture

South Korea and the United States operate primarily national organic certification systems with centralized standard-setting (NAQS and USDA NOP, respectively). Germany operates within a multi-level governance structure where EU regulations set the baseline, the federal Bio-Siegel provides a national complement, and private associations impose stricter standards. The EU framework harmonizes minimum standards across 27 member states while permitting supplementary national and private standards.

### Standard Stringency

The private German organic associations (Bioland, Demeter, Naturland) maintain the strictest organic standards globally, requiring whole-farm conversion, prohibiting certain EU-permitted practices, and imposing social and environmental standards beyond the organic production rules. The EU, US, and Korean government standards are broadly comparable, which has facilitated mutual equivalence recognition for trade purposes.

### Fraud Enforcement

The United States has taken the most aggressive enforcement approach, with criminal prosecutions and multi-million-dollar penalty cases. The 2024 Strengthening Organic Enforcement rule significantly enhanced import controls and supply chain traceability. South Korea and the EU have also strengthened fraud enforcement but rely more heavily on administrative penalties and certification withdrawal.

### Equivalence and Trade

The trilateral web of equivalence agreements between the US, EU, and South Korea facilitates approximately $5 billion in annual cross-border organic trade. However, differences in specific standards -- particularly regarding permitted substances, contamination thresholds, and livestock standards -- create ongoing technical challenges and require periodic negotiation and review.
