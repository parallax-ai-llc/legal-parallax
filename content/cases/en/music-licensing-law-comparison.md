## Summary

Music licensing -- the legal framework governing the authorization and compensation for the use of musical compositions and sound recordings in public performance, broadcasting, streaming, synchronization, and mechanical reproduction -- is among the most complex areas of intellectual property law, reflecting the layered nature of rights in musical works and the rapid evolution of digital distribution technologies. South Korea's Copyright Act (저작권법) establishes a comprehensive licensing framework administered primarily through collective management organizations including the Korea Music Copyright Association (한국음악저작권협회, KOMCA) for musical compositions and the Korea Phonogram Producers Association for sound recordings; South Korea's music market, the sixth largest globally driven by the K-pop phenomenon, has prompted significant legal developments including the 2020 Copyright Act amendments addressing online music platform obligations and the establishment of the Korea Copyright Commission (한국저작권위원회) as the principal regulatory and dispute resolution body. The United States operates one of the world's most complex music licensing regimes, with separate rights and licensing mechanisms for musical compositions (administered by ASCAP, BMI, and SESAC under antitrust consent decrees) and sound recordings (administered by SoundExchange for digital performance); the Music Modernization Act of 2018 (Pub. L. 115-264) represented the most significant reform in decades, creating the Mechanical Licensing Collective (MLC) to administer blanket mechanical licenses for digital music services and establishing a federal right in pre-1972 sound recordings. Germany's music licensing framework operates through the collecting society GEMA (Gesellschaft für musikalische Aufführungs- und mechanische Vervielfältigungsrechte) for musical compositions and GVL (Gesellschaft zur Verwertung von Leistungsschutzrechten) for sound recording performers' rights, regulated under the Collecting Societies Act (Verwertungsgesellschaftengesetz, VGG) implementing EU Directive 2014/26/EU on collective rights management; the 2021 amendment to the Copyright Act (Urheberrechtsgesetz, UrhG) implementing the EU Copyright Directive (2019/790) introduced significant new provisions on platform liability and fair remuneration for creators. At the European level, the EU Copyright Directive (Directive (EU) 2019/790) fundamentally reformed the digital music licensing landscape through Article 17 (platform liability for user-uploaded content), Article 18 (appropriate and proportionate remuneration for authors and performers), and the mandatory collective licensing provisions, while the ECHR has addressed music copyright through Article 1 of Protocol 1 (protection of property) and Article 10 (freedom of expression), balancing creators' rights against users' interests in cases such as Ashby Donald v. France (2013).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's music market generated approximately $800 million in recorded music revenue in 2024, making it the sixth-largest music market globally, driven primarily by the global success of K-pop.

**Copyright Act (저작권법):** The Act protects musical works (compositions and lyrics) as copyrightable works and sound recordings as neighboring rights. Key provisions include:
- **Section 2:** Defines musical works, sound recordings, and performances as protected subject matter
- **Section 46-50:** Establish licensing mechanisms including compulsory licenses and collective management
- **Section 102-104:** Online service provider liability provisions, establishing notice-and-takedown procedures

**KOMCA (한국음악저작권협회):** Korea's primary performing rights organization, KOMCA manages the public performance, broadcasting, and digital transmission rights of approximately 35,000 member songwriters and composers. KOMCA collects and distributes royalties from broadcasters, streaming platforms, venues, and businesses using music publicly. Total KOMCA collections exceeded 400 billion KRW (approximately $300 million) in 2024.

**Streaming Platform Licensing:** South Korea's digital music market is dominated by domestic platforms including Melon, Genie Music, Bugs, and FLO, alongside global platforms (Spotify, Apple Music, YouTube Music). Platforms obtain licenses from KOMCA for composition rights and from record labels (individually or through the Korea Phonogram Producers Association) for sound recording rights.

### 2020 Copyright Act Amendments

The 2020 amendments introduced several significant changes:
- **Fair remuneration for creators:** New provisions ensuring that songwriters and performers receive equitable compensation from exploitation of their works, regardless of contractual assignments
- **Extended collective licensing:** Broader authority for collective management organizations to issue licenses covering non-member works in certain contexts
- **Platform transparency:** Requirements for digital music platforms to provide detailed usage data and accounting to rights holders

### K-pop and Global Licensing

The global expansion of K-pop has created new licensing challenges and opportunities. Korean entertainment companies (HYBE, SM Entertainment, JYP Entertainment, YG Entertainment) increasingly negotiate global rights deals directly with international platforms and sub-publishers. The Korea Copyright Commission (한국저작권위원회) has played an active role in establishing bilateral copyright cooperation agreements with China, Japan, Southeast Asian countries, and others to facilitate cross-border licensing and enforcement.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The U.S. music licensing system is distinguished by the separation of composition rights and sound recording rights into distinct licensing streams, each with different statutory frameworks and collecting entities.

**Composition Rights (Musical Works):**

| License Type | What It Covers | Who Administers | Rate-Setting |
|-------------|---------------|----------------|-------------|
| Public performance | Live, broadcast, and digital performance of compositions | ASCAP, BMI, SESAC, GMR | Consent decree rate courts (ASCAP, BMI); free market (SESAC, GMR) |
| Mechanical reproduction | Physical reproduction (CDs, vinyl); digital delivery (downloads, streaming) | MLC (blanket for digital); Harry Fox Agency (physical) | Copyright Royalty Board (statutory rate) |
| Synchronization | Use of composition in audiovisual works (film, TV, advertising) | Publishers directly | Free market negotiation |

**Sound Recording Rights:**

| License Type | What It Covers | Who Administers | Rate-Setting |
|-------------|---------------|----------------|-------------|
| Digital performance (non-interactive) | Internet radio, satellite radio | SoundExchange | Copyright Royalty Board (statutory rate) |
| Interactive streaming | On-demand streaming (Spotify, Apple Music) | Record labels directly | Free market negotiation |
| Synchronization | Use of specific recording in audiovisual works | Record labels directly | Free market negotiation |

**Music Modernization Act of 2018 (Pub. L. 115-264):** The MMA reformed U.S. music licensing in three significant ways:
- **Title I (Musical Works Modernization Act):** Created the Mechanical Licensing Collective (MLC) to administer a blanket mechanical license for digital music services, replacing the previous song-by-song compulsory licensing system. The MLC distributes royalties to songwriters and publishers based on usage data.
- **Title II (Classics Protection and Access Act):** Extended federal copyright protection to pre-1972 sound recordings, which had previously been governed only by state law.
- **Title III (Allocation for Music Producers Act):** Established a process for music producers, mixers, and sound engineers to receive royalties from SoundExchange.

### Consent Decree Framework

ASCAP and BMI operate under antitrust consent decrees entered with the Department of Justice (ASCAP: 1941, last modified 2001; BMI: 1941, last modified 1994). These decrees require the PROs to offer blanket licenses to all users, prevent them from granting exclusive rights, and establish rate court proceedings before designated federal judges in the Southern District of New York to resolve rate disputes. The DOJ completed a review of the consent decrees in 2016 and declined to modify them, though reform proposals continue to be debated.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

**Urheberrechtsgesetz (UrhG):** Germany's Copyright Act protects musical works (compositions) as works of authorship (Section 2) and sound recordings as neighboring rights (Sections 85-86). The UrhG provides for both individual licensing and collective rights management.

**GEMA:** Germany's performing rights organization and mechanical rights society, GEMA administers the rights of approximately 90,000 member composers, lyricists, and music publishers. GEMA is one of the world's largest and most influential collecting societies, with annual collections exceeding 1.2 billion euros. GEMA establishes tariff schedules for all categories of music use, from concert performances to background music in retail establishments to digital streaming.

**GVL (Gesellschaft zur Verwertung von Leistungsschutzrechten):** GVL administers the neighboring rights of performing artists and record producers, collecting remuneration for broadcasting, public performance, and making available of sound recordings.

**Verwertungsgesellschaftengesetz (VGG):** The Collecting Societies Act, implementing EU Directive 2014/26/EU, regulates the governance, transparency, and accountability of collecting societies. The German Patent and Trade Mark Office (DPMA) supervises collecting societies' compliance with the VGG.

### GEMA-YouTube Dispute

The protracted dispute between GEMA and YouTube (2009-2016) was one of the most significant music licensing conflicts globally. GEMA claimed that YouTube owed licensing fees for music videos uploaded by users. YouTube argued that it was a mere hosting platform protected by safe harbor provisions. The dispute resulted in millions of music videos being blocked in Germany with the message "This video is not available in your country." A licensing agreement was finally reached in 2016, the terms of which were not publicly disclosed. The dispute informed the development of Article 17 of the EU Copyright Directive.

### UrhG Reform (2021) -- EU Copyright Directive Implementation

The 2021 reform implemented the EU Copyright Directive through the Urheberrechts-Diensteanbieter-Gesetz (UrhDaG), establishing new rules for online content-sharing platforms:
- Platforms are directly liable for unauthorized user-uploaded content (Article 17 implementation)
- Platforms must obtain licenses from rights holders or demonstrate best efforts to obtain authorization
- Automated content recognition tools ("upload filters") may be used but must include safeguards against over-blocking
- Users have the right to upload content for quotation, criticism, review, caricature, parody, and pastiche
- A complaint and redress mechanism must be available for users whose content is blocked

### Fair Remuneration (UrhG Section 32)

UrhG Section 32 provides authors (including songwriters and composers) with a legally enforceable right to fair remuneration for the exploitation of their works. If the contractually agreed remuneration is not fair, the author may demand that the contract be modified to provide fair remuneration. Joint remuneration agreements (gemeinsame Vergütungsregeln) negotiated between creator associations and user associations establish benchmarks for fair remuneration across sectors.

## ECHR / EU

### EU Copyright Directive (Directive (EU) 2019/790)

The most significant recent reform of EU music licensing:

**Article 17 (Online Content-Sharing Service Providers):** Establishes that platforms that store and give public access to large amounts of user-uploaded copyrighted works perform an act of communication to the public and must obtain authorization from rights holders. This provision reversed the hosting safe harbor that platforms had relied upon and created a new licensing obligation specifically for music and other copyrighted content on platforms like YouTube.

**Article 18 (Fair Remuneration):** Authors and performers are entitled to receive appropriate and proportionate remuneration for the exploitation of their works, with member states required to ensure that contractual mechanisms achieve this objective.

**Article 19 (Transparency):** Authors and performers have the right to receive regular and comprehensive information from their contractual counterparts regarding the exploitation of their works and the remuneration due.

**Article 20 (Contract Adjustment):** Authors and performers may claim additional remuneration when the originally agreed remuneration is disproportionately low compared to the revenues derived from exploitation.

### Collective Rights Management Directive (2014/26/EU)

This directive harmonizes rules on the governance, transparency, and multi-territorial licensing of collecting societies across the EU. Key provisions for music licensing include:
- Multi-territorial licensing of online music rights by collecting societies
- Minimum governance standards for collecting societies
- Transparency and reporting obligations to members and licensees
- Cross-border licensing facilitation

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Ashby Donald and Others v. France (Application No. 36769/08, 2013):** The Court recognized that copyright enforcement constitutes an interference with the right to freedom of expression under Article 10, requiring a proportionality assessment. The Court upheld the French courts' copyright infringement finding against fashion photographers, concluding that the interference was proportionate.

**Neij and Sunde Kolmisoppi v. Sweden (Application No. 40397/12, 2013, admissibility decision):** The Court declared inadmissible the complaint by Pirate Bay founders challenging their criminal conviction for facilitating copyright infringement, finding that copyright protection serves the legitimate aim of protecting the rights of others under Article 10(2).

**Pendov v. Bulgaria (Application No. 44229/11, 2022):** The Court addressed a dispute between a collecting society and a music venue operator, finding that the enforcement of collective licensing obligations did not violate the applicant's rights under Article 1 of Protocol 1.

## Comparative Analysis

### Collective Management Models

All four jurisdictions rely on collective management organizations for music licensing, but their structures differ significantly. The U.S. operates multiple competing PROs for composition performance rights, subject to antitrust consent decrees. South Korea and Germany each maintain a single dominant CMO (KOMCA and GEMA respectively), which simplifies licensing but creates monopoly concerns. The EU's CRM Directive aims to promote competition and efficiency among European collecting societies while facilitating multi-territorial licensing.

### Platform Liability

The treatment of online platform liability for user-uploaded music represents the most significant divergence. The EU's Article 17 imposes direct licensing obligations on platforms, reversing the safe harbor. The U.S. DMCA Section 512 maintains the safe harbor approach with notice-and-takedown. South Korea's OSP liability provisions occupy a middle ground. Germany's experience with the GEMA-YouTube dispute directly informed the EU's decision to impose platform licensing obligations.

### Fair Remuneration

Germany and the EU lead in statutory fair remuneration protections for creators, with enforceable rights to equitable compensation and contract adjustment. South Korea's 2020 amendments moved in a similar direction. The United States relies primarily on market mechanisms and statutory rate-setting by the Copyright Royalty Board, without a general statutory right to fair remuneration.

### Digital Transition

The transition from physical to digital music distribution has challenged licensing frameworks everywhere. The U.S. Music Modernization Act addressed the mechanical licensing crisis created by streaming services' difficulty in identifying and licensing millions of compositions. The EU's multi-territorial licensing provisions address the fragmentation of online rights across 27 member states. South Korea's concentrated platform market (dominated by domestic services) has facilitated centralized licensing.
