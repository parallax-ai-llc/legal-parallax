## Summary

Digital inheritance -- the transmission of digital assets, online accounts, and virtual property upon death -- represents one of the most rapidly evolving areas of succession law, challenging legal frameworks designed for tangible property. The legal landscape is marked by fundamental tensions between privacy rights of the deceased, property rights of heirs, contractual rights of platform operators, and the technical realities of digital asset management. Germany established a landmark precedent through the Bundesgerichtshof's 2018 Facebook inheritance decision (BGH III ZB 183/17), holding that digital accounts are inheritable like physical correspondence. South Korea's Civil Act (민법) inheritance provisions apply to digital assets, but the absence of specific digital inheritance legislation has created uncertainty, prompting the Ministry of Justice to publish interpretive guidelines in 2024. The United States has adopted the Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act (RUFADAA), enacted in 49 states and the District of Columbia by 2025, which provides a three-tiered priority system for fiduciary access to digital assets. The European Union lacks harmonized digital inheritance legislation, but the Succession Regulation (EU) No. 650/2012 provides conflict-of-laws rules that determine which member state's inheritance law applies to digital assets in cross-border successions, while GDPR provisions on data of deceased persons are left to member state discretion.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's approach to digital inheritance relies primarily on the general inheritance provisions of the Civil Act (민법), supplemented by sector-specific legislation and administrative guidance:

- **Civil Act (민법) Article 1005 (Universal Succession):** Upon death, the decedent's property rights and obligations transfer to the heir(s) as a comprehensive unit. Korean courts have interpreted "property rights" (재산권) broadly to include contractual rights arising from online service agreements, digital content licenses, and virtual asset holdings. However, the scope of "rights that are extinguished by the death of the person" (일신전속적 권리) -- personality rights excluded from inheritance -- remains contested in the digital context.

- **Virtual Asset User Protection Act (가상자산이용자보호법, effective 19 July 2024):** This landmark legislation regulates virtual asset service providers (VASPs) and establishes that virtual assets (including cryptocurrency) constitute "property" for purposes of Korean law. Article 8 requires VASPs to segregate customer assets, and Article 15 mandates cooperation with lawful requests from heirs and estate administrators. The Financial Services Commission (FSC) implementing regulations specify documentation requirements for heirs claiming cryptocurrency holdings.

- **Personal Information Protection Act (PIPA, 개인정보 보호법):** PIPA does not explicitly address post-mortem data rights. However, Article 39-3 (as amended) allows surviving family members to request deletion or cessation of processing of a deceased person's personal information. The PIPC issued interpretive guidance in 2023 clarifying that heirs' rights to access a decedent's digital accounts may be limited by the deceased's express privacy preferences recorded during their lifetime.

- **Ministry of Justice Interpretive Guidelines on Digital Inheritance (2024):** Recognizing the inadequacy of existing law, the Ministry of Justice published comprehensive guidelines addressing: (a) classification of digital assets (distinguishing between economically valuable digital property and personality-linked accounts); (b) procedures for heirs to access digital accounts; (c) interaction between platform terms of service and statutory inheritance rights; and (d) recommended estate planning practices for digital assets.

### Key Principles

South Korea's legal framework treats digital inheritance as an extension of general succession principles, applying the universal succession doctrine to digital assets. However, courts have recognized that certain digital accounts may be "inherently personal" (일신전속적) and therefore extinguished upon death, analogous to how the right to receive alimony terminates. The boundary between inheritable digital property and non-inheritable personal digital rights remains the central doctrinal question.

Korean law gives significant weight to platform terms of service, unlike the German approach. Courts have generally held that ToS provisions regarding account termination upon death are valid contractual terms, unless they unreasonably prejudice the heir's statutory inheritance rights under the Consumer Contract Fairness Act (약관의 규제에 관한 법률, Standard Terms Regulation Act). The Ministry of Justice guidelines recommend that platforms provide a "digital legacy contact" designation mechanism similar to Apple's Digital Legacy program and Meta's Legacy Contact feature.

### Notable Cases

- **Seoul Central District Court, Online Game Account Inheritance (2022):** The court held that virtual items and in-game currency in a massively multiplayer online game constituted inheritable property under Civil Act Article 1005, ordering the game operator to transfer account access to the decedent's heir. The court distinguished between the "account relationship" (contractual, potentially personal) and the "economic value stored within the account" (property, inheritable).

- **Seoul Family Court, Cryptocurrency Inheritance Proceeding (2023):** In one of the first Korean cases addressing cryptocurrency succession, the court ordered Upbit (operated by Dunamu Inc.) to disclose the decedent's cryptocurrency holdings to the court-appointed estate administrator, rejecting the exchange's claim that privacy policies prevented disclosure. The court held that the heir's statutory right to universal succession under Article 1005 overrides platform privacy policies.

- **Suwon District Court, KakaoTalk Account Access (2024):** The court denied heirs' request for access to a deceased person's KakaoTalk messaging history, holding that private communications are "inherently personal" and extinguished upon death, distinguishing the case from economically valuable digital assets. The decision has been criticized by legal scholars who argue it is inconsistent with the German BGH Facebook approach.

## United States

### Federal Framework

The United States addresses digital inheritance primarily through state law, with limited federal provisions:

- **Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act (RUFADAA, 2015):** Drafted by the Uniform Law Commission, RUFADAA has been enacted in 49 states, the District of Columbia, and the U.S. Virgin Islands (all except Louisiana as of 2025). RUFADAA establishes a three-tiered priority system for determining fiduciary access to digital assets: (1) the user's online tool designation (e.g., Google Inactive Account Manager, Apple Digital Legacy) takes highest priority; (2) the user's will, trust, or power of attorney takes second priority; (3) the platform's terms of service apply only as a default if neither of the above exists.

- **Electronic Communications Privacy Act (18 U.S.C. §§ 2701-2712, Stored Communications Act):** The SCA historically prevented service providers from disclosing the contents of stored electronic communications, creating a barrier to fiduciary access. RUFADAA addresses this by providing that lawful fiduciary access constitutes "lawful consent" under the SCA, though the interaction between federal and state law in this area remains contested.

- **Copyright Act (17 U.S.C.):** Copyrighted works created by the decedent (digital photographs, writings, software code) are inheritable property under 17 U.S.C. § 201(d)(1), with copyright duration extending 70 years after the author's death (§ 302(a)). However, this transfers the copyright itself, not necessarily access to the digital platform where works are stored.

- **Internal Revenue Service Notice 2014-21:** Established that virtual currency (cryptocurrency) is treated as property for federal tax purposes, meaning inherited cryptocurrency receives a stepped-up basis under I.R.C. § 1014, potentially eliminating capital gains accrued during the decedent's lifetime.

### State Variations

While RUFADAA provides a uniform framework, significant state-level variations exist:

- **Delaware (first enactee, 2014):** Delaware was the first state to enact fiduciary access legislation (before RUFADAA was finalized), adopting the original Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act (2014) which provided broader fiduciary access than RUFADAA. Delaware subsequently updated its law to conform to RUFADAA.

- **New York (EPTL § 13-A):** New York enacted RUFADAA effective September 2019 with modifications, including requiring that fiduciaries demonstrate a "legitimate interest" in accessing digital assets beyond mere curiosity.

- **California (Probate Code §§ 870-884):** California enacted RUFADAA in 2016, adding provisions that specifically address digital music, film, and other entertainment content licenses, reflecting the state's entertainment industry significance.

- **Louisiana:** As the only state without RUFADAA as of 2025, Louisiana relies on its Civil Code forced heirship provisions (La. C.C. Art. 1493ff) and general succession rules, creating potential conflicts with platform ToS.

- **Cryptocurrency-Specific Provisions:** Several states have enacted supplementary legislation addressing cryptocurrency inheritance specifically. Wyoming's Digital Asset Law (W.S. § 34-29-101ff) classifies digital assets into three categories (digital consumer assets, digital securities, and virtual currency) and provides specific inheritance and custodial rules. Texas enacted HB 1666 (2023) explicitly including virtual currency in the definition of "property" for probate purposes.

### Notable Cases

- **In re Ellsworth (2005, Oakland County, Michigan):** One of the earliest digital inheritance cases, where the family of U.S. Marine Lance Cpl. Justin Ellsworth killed in Iraq sought access to his Yahoo! email account. The probate court ordered Yahoo! to provide the contents to the family, though Yahoo! provided only a CD of emails rather than account access, highlighting the limitations of judicial remedies.

- **Ajemian v. Yahoo!, Inc. (474 Mass. 169, 2016):** The Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court held that Yahoo!'s terms of service provision terminating accounts upon death was unenforceable as unconscionable, allowing the personal representative to access the deceased brother's email. The court distinguished between the user agreement (which could be modified post-mortem) and the SCA (which required authorized consent).

- **In re Facebook (Sahar Daftary, 2012, UK/US):** A UK inquest into the death of a model required Facebook to disclose account information. While a UK proceeding, it demonstrated the cross-jurisdictional challenges of digital inheritance when platforms are headquartered in the US but users are abroad.

- **Cardwell v. Borders (2024, Texas):** A Texas probate court applied RUFADAA to order a cryptocurrency exchange to provide full transaction history and access credentials to the estate administrator, establishing that RUFADAA's fiduciary access provisions apply to decentralized finance (DeFi) wallet interfaces as well as centralized exchanges.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany possesses the most developed judicial framework for digital inheritance in the world, anchored by the landmark Bundesgerichtshof Facebook decision:

- **BGB § 1922 (Universal Succession):** German inheritance law follows the principle of Universalsukzession -- upon death, the entire estate passes as a whole to the heir(s). The BGH has interpreted this to encompass all contractual relationships, including those with digital service providers.

- **BGH, Judgment of 12 July 2018, III ZB 183/17 (Facebook Inheritance Case):** In this landmark decision, the BGH held that a Facebook account and its contents (including private messages) are fully inheritable. The court rejected Facebook's arguments that (a) telecommunications secrecy (Fernmeldegeheimnis) under Grundgesetz Article 10 prevented disclosure of communications to heirs, (b) privacy rights of the deceased's communication partners precluded inheritance of messages, and (c) Facebook's terms of service providing for account "memorialization" upon death could override statutory inheritance rights. The BGH reasoned that digital accounts are analogous to physical letters and diaries, which have always been inheritable, and that a platform's ToS cannot derogate from mandatory inheritance law.

- **BGB § 1922 in conjunction with BGB § 399 (Non-Transferable Claims):** The BGH explicitly rejected the argument that social media accounts are "non-transferable" under § 399 because they are inherently personal. The court held that while the creation of an account requires personal attributes, the contractual relationship itself and its associated data are transferable property rights.

- **Erbschaftsteuer- und Schenkungsteuergesetz (ErbStG, Inheritance and Gift Tax Act):** Digital assets are subject to German inheritance tax. Cryptocurrency holdings are valued at fair market value on the date of death (§ 11 ErbStG). The Federal Finance Ministry (BMF) issued guidance in 2022 clarifying tax treatment of inherited digital assets.

### Key Principles

The German approach is distinguished by its robust application of universal succession to digital assets, treating them identically to physical property. Key principles include:

1. **ToS Cannot Override Statutory Inheritance:** Platform terms of service that purport to terminate accounts, prevent inheritance, or limit heir access are unenforceable insofar as they contradict mandatory inheritance law provisions (BGB §§ 1922ff). This represents the strongest position globally against platform contractual overrides.

2. **Communications Privacy Does Not Prevent Inheritance:** The BGH held that telecommunications secrecy (protected under GG Art. 10 and the Telecommunications Act) is a right held against the state, not against private heirs, and that the heir steps into the decedent's position vis-à-vis the platform. The communication partners' expectation of confidentiality yields to the heir's statutory right.

3. **Full Content Access:** Unlike jurisdictions that distinguish between "catalogue" information (metadata) and "content" (message bodies), German law provides heirs access to all account contents without distinction.

4. **Cryptocurrency as Inheritable Property:** German courts and tax authorities treat cryptocurrency as "other property rights" (sonstige Vermögensrechte) under BGB § 1922, fully subject to inheritance. The practical challenge of accessing cryptocurrency wallets (particularly where private keys are unknown) remains, but this is a factual rather than legal obstacle.

### Notable Cases

- **BGH III ZB 183/17 (Facebook Inheritance, 2018):** The mother of a deceased 15-year-old sought access to her daughter's Facebook account to determine whether cyberbullying had contributed to her death. After lower courts split -- the Landgericht Berlin granted access, the Kammergericht reversed -- the BGH definitively held that the account was inheritable. Facebook was required to grant the heirs full access to the account, including all messages and content.

- **AG München, Digital Music Library Inheritance (2019):** The Munich Local Court held that iTunes music purchases made by the decedent constituted inheritable "usage rights" that transferred to heirs, rejecting Apple's argument that music licenses were personal and non-transferable. The court ordered Apple to transfer the iTunes account to the heir.

- **LG Münster, Email Account Access (2023):** The Landgericht Münster ordered a German email provider to grant an estate executor access to the decedent's email account, applying the BGH Facebook principles to email services. The court rejected the provider's argument that email communications were distinguishable from social media messages.

- **OLG Frankfurt, Cryptocurrency Inheritance Dispute (2024):** The Oberlandesgericht Frankfurt addressed a dispute between co-heirs over a Bitcoin wallet where the deceased had not recorded the private key. The court held that the Bitcoin constituted estate property requiring equal division among heirs, and ordered a forensic digital investigation of the decedent's devices to locate the private key, allocating investigation costs to the estate.

## ECHR / EU Framework

### Convention Articles

The European Convention on Human Rights provides principles relevant to digital inheritance through several provisions:

- **Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 -- Protection of Property:** Digital assets that constitute "possessions" within the autonomous meaning of Article 1 Protocol No. 1 are protected. The ECtHR has adopted a broad interpretation of "possessions" encompassing economic interests and contractual rights, which plausibly extends to valuable digital accounts, cryptocurrency holdings, and digital content licenses.

- **Article 8 -- Right to Respect for Private Life:** Engages competing interests in digital inheritance: the deceased's privacy interest in digital communications (which may survive death to a limited extent under national law), the heirs' interest in accessing the estate, and third parties' privacy interests in communications with the deceased. The ECtHR balances these under the proportionality framework.

- **Article 14 -- Prohibition of Discrimination:** Relevant where digital inheritance rules treat digital and physical assets differently without objective justification, potentially discriminating against heirs whose inheritance consists primarily of digital assets.

### EU Legislative Framework

The EU has not enacted specific digital inheritance legislation, but several instruments are relevant:

- **Succession Regulation (EU) No. 650/2012 (Brussels IV):** Determines which member state's inheritance law applies in cross-border successions. Article 21 establishes habitual residence of the deceased as the general connecting factor. For digital assets stored on servers in different jurisdictions, the Regulation applies to determine the applicable substantive inheritance law, though the practical enforcement of inheritance rights against non-EU platforms remains challenging.

- **GDPR (Regulation (EU) 2016/679) Recital 27:** States that the GDPR "does not apply to the personal data of deceased persons" and that "Member States may provide for rules regarding the processing of personal data of deceased persons." This has created a patchwork of approaches: France's Loi pour une République numérique (2016) grants individuals the right to issue advance directives regarding their digital data after death; Italy's Privacy Code Article 2-terdecies allows heirs to exercise data subject rights on behalf of the deceased; Spain's LOPDGDD Article 96 provides for digital testament rights.

- **Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA, Regulation (EU) 2023/1114):** While primarily focused on regulation of crypto-asset service providers, MiCA's requirements for asset segregation (Article 70) and customer protection facilitate inheritance claims by ensuring that customer crypto assets are identifiable and separable from the provider's own assets.

- **Digital Content Directive (Directive (EU) 2019/770):** Governs contracts for digital content and digital services, but does not address the transferability of digital content rights upon death, leaving this to member state inheritance law.

### Key Case Law

- **Bărbulescu v. Romania (Grand Chamber, 2017, App. No. 61496/08):** While not a digital inheritance case, the Grand Chamber's analysis of privacy expectations in digital communications informs the balance between heir access and communication partner privacy in digital inheritance contexts.

- **Delfi AS v. Estonia (Grand Chamber, 2015, App. No. 64569/09):** The Court's analysis of intermediary liability for user-generated content has implications for platforms' obligations to preserve or provide access to digital content of deceased users.
