## Summary

Judicial review -- the power of courts to examine legislative and executive acts for conformity with a higher law, typically the constitution, and to invalidate those found to be in conflict -- constitutes one of the most fundamental mechanisms of constitutional governance, yet its institutional design, scope, and procedural mechanisms vary dramatically across legal systems. South Korea established a dedicated Constitutional Court (헌법재판소) under the 1987 Constitution, vested with exclusive jurisdiction over constitutional review under Articles 107 and 111, including the power to adjudicate the constitutionality of statutes upon referral from ordinary courts (위헌법률심판), decide constitutional complaints filed directly by individuals (헌법소원심판), resolve competence disputes between state organs, and adjudicate impeachments and party dissolution proceedings; the Court has emerged as one of the most active and assertive constitutional courts in Asia, issuing over 40,000 decisions and declaring more than 700 statutory provisions unconstitutional or nonconforming since its establishment in September 1988. The United States pioneered the modern concept of judicial review through the Supreme Court's decision in Marbury v. Madison, 5 U.S. 137 (1803), establishing the principle that the judiciary has the authority to declare laws unconstitutional, within a system of diffuse review where all federal and state courts may exercise judicial review with the Supreme Court as the final arbiter. Germany created the Bundesverfassungsgericht (Federal Constitutional Court) in 1951 under Article 93 of the Grundgesetz, establishing a concentrated model of constitutional review with both abstract and concrete normative control (abstrakte and konkrete Normenkontrolle) and individual constitutional complaint (Verfassungsbeschwerde), which has profoundly influenced constitutional court design worldwide, particularly through its development of proportionality analysis. At the European level, the European Court of Human Rights exercises a form of supranational judicial review by examining whether member state laws and actions conform to the European Convention on Human Rights, while the Court of Justice of the European Union ensures the uniform interpretation and application of EU law and has established the doctrines of direct effect and supremacy that effectively subject national legislation to review for EU law conformity.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

The Constitutional Court of Korea (헌법재판소) was established under the 1987 Constitution and began operations on September 1, 1988. Its jurisdiction is defined by Article 111 of the Constitution and further detailed in the Constitutional Court Act (헌법재판소법).

**Article 111(1) Jurisdiction:**
1. Constitutionality of statutes upon request of courts (위헌법률심판)
2. Impeachment proceedings (탄핵심판)
3. Dissolution of political parties (정당해산심판)
4. Competence disputes between state organs, between state organs and local governments, and between local governments (권한쟁의심판)
5. Constitutional complaints (헌법소원심판)

### Constitutional Review of Statutes (위헌법률심판)

Under Article 107(1), when the constitutionality of a statute is at issue in a trial, the court shall request a decision of the Constitutional Court and adjudge according to its decision. This system requires referral from an ordinary court -- litigants cannot directly challenge a statute's constitutionality before the Constitutional Court through this procedure but may file a separate constitutional complaint if the referring court declines to make the referral (Constitutional Court Act Article 68(2)).

### Constitutional Complaint (헌법소원심판)

Two types of constitutional complaints exist:

**Article 68(1) Complaint:** Any person whose fundamental rights guaranteed by the Constitution have been violated by an exercise or non-exercise of governmental power may file a constitutional complaint, provided all other available remedies have been exhausted. Notably, court judgments are excluded from the scope of this complaint, distinguishing the Korean system from Germany's Verfassungsbeschwerde.

**Article 68(2) Complaint:** When a court rejects a party's motion to request constitutional review, the party may directly file a constitutional complaint with the Constitutional Court challenging the statute's constitutionality.

### Nonconformity Decisions (헌법불합치)

A distinctive feature of the Korean Constitutional Court is its frequent use of nonconformity decisions (헌법불합치결정), which declare a statute incompatible with the Constitution without immediately invalidating it, instead ordering the legislature to amend the law within a specified period. This mechanism, borrowed from German practice, allows the Court to avoid legislative vacuums while still compelling reform.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Judicial review in the United States is not explicitly enumerated in the Constitution but was established as a constitutional principle by Chief Justice John Marshall in Marbury v. Madison, 5 U.S. (1 Cranch) 137 (1803). The American system employs diffuse judicial review: all courts -- federal and state, trial and appellate -- may determine the constitutionality of laws within the context of cases and controversies before them, with the Supreme Court serving as the final arbiter.

### Marbury v. Madison (1803)

Chief Justice Marshall's opinion established several foundational principles: the Constitution is the supreme law; an act of the legislature repugnant to the Constitution is void; and it is emphatically the province and duty of the judicial department to say what the law is. The case arose when William Marbury sought a writ of mandamus compelling Secretary of State James Madison to deliver his judicial commission. Marshall held that while Marbury had a right to the commission, the Judiciary Act of 1789's grant of original jurisdiction to the Supreme Court for writs of mandamus exceeded the Constitution's definition of the Court's original jurisdiction, thus establishing the power of judicial review by invalidating a portion of a federal statute.

### Standing and Justiciability

The American system imposes significant limitations on access to judicial review through justiciability doctrines:

| Doctrine | Requirement | Key Case |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Standing | Injury in fact, causation, redressability | Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife, 504 U.S. 555 (1992) |
| Ripeness | Controversy must be sufficiently developed | Abbott Laboratories v. Gardner, 387 U.S. 136 (1967) |
| Mootness | Live controversy must persist | DeFunis v. Odegaard, 416 U.S. 312 (1974) |
| Political question | Some issues committed to political branches | Baker v. Carr, 369 U.S. 186 (1962) |

### Standards of Review

| Standard | Application | Burden |
|----------|-------------|--------|
| Strict scrutiny | Fundamental rights; suspect classifications | Government must show compelling interest and narrow tailoring |
| Intermediate scrutiny | Quasi-suspect classifications (gender, illegitimacy) | Government must show important interest and substantial relation |
| Rational basis | Economic regulation; non-suspect classifications | Challenger must show no rational relationship to legitimate purpose |

### Recent Developments

The Supreme Court's exercise of judicial review has generated significant controversy in recent terms. Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization, 597 U.S. 215 (2022), overruled Roe v. Wade and Planned Parenthood v. Casey, eliminating the federal constitutional right to abortion. This decision reignited debates about judicial legitimacy, stare decisis, and the counter-majoritarian difficulty inherent in judicial review.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

The Bundesverfassungsgericht (Federal Constitutional Court) was established in 1951 under Articles 93 and 94 of the Grundgesetz. It is organized into two Senates of eight justices each, with justices serving twelve-year non-renewable terms. Half are elected by the Bundestag and half by the Bundesrat, each requiring a two-thirds majority.

### Procedures

**Abstract Normative Control (abstrakte Normenkontrolle, Art. 93(1)(2)):** The Federal Government, a Land government, or one-quarter of the members of the Bundestag may request the Court to review the constitutionality of any federal or state law, regardless of whether a concrete case exists. This procedure allows for preventive constitutional review.

**Concrete Normative Control (konkrete Normenkontrolle, Art. 100(1)):** When a court considers a statute on which its decision depends to be unconstitutional, it must suspend proceedings and refer the question to the Bundesverfassungsgericht. This is analogous to South Korea's Article 107(1) referral system.

**Constitutional Complaint (Verfassungsbeschwerde, Art. 93(1)(4a)):** Any person may file a constitutional complaint alleging that one of their basic rights has been violated by public authority, including court judgments. This is the most frequently used procedure, accounting for approximately 96 percent of the Court's caseload. Unlike the Korean system, German constitutional complaints may challenge court judgments.

### Proportionality Analysis

Germany's most significant contribution to constitutional methodology is the development of structured proportionality analysis (Verhältnismäßigkeitsprinzip), which assesses whether a restriction on a fundamental right is: (1) pursuing a legitimate aim (legitimer Zweck), (2) suitable for achieving that aim (Geeignetheit), (3) necessary -- no less restrictive means available (Erforderlichkeit), and (4) proportionate in the strict sense -- the burden on the right is not disproportionate to the aim pursued (Angemessenheit). This framework has been adopted by constitutional courts worldwide, including South Korea.

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECtHR exercises a form of supranational judicial review, examining whether member state laws, administrative acts, and court judgments conform to the European Convention on Human Rights. Under Article 34, any person, NGO, or group of individuals claiming to be a victim of a violation may file an individual application.

**Key Principles:**

- **Subsidiarity:** The Court acts as a subsidiary mechanism; domestic remedies must be exhausted before filing an application (Art. 35(1)).
- **Margin of Appreciation:** States enjoy a certain margin of discretion in how they implement Convention rights, the breadth of which varies depending on the right at issue and the existence of a European consensus.
- **Living Instrument Doctrine:** The Convention is interpreted as a living instrument in light of present-day conditions (Tyrer v. United Kingdom, 1978).
- **Pilot Judgment Procedure:** For systemic or structural problems, the Court may issue a pilot judgment identifying the underlying deficiency and requiring general remedial measures (Broniowski v. Poland, 2004).

### Court of Justice of the European Union

The CJEU ensures the uniform interpretation and application of EU law through several procedures:

| Procedure | Legal Basis | Function |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| Preliminary reference | Art. 267 TFEU | National courts refer questions of EU law interpretation |
| Infringement proceedings | Arts. 258-260 TFEU | Commission or member state challenges non-compliance |
| Annulment action | Art. 263 TFEU | Review of legality of EU institution acts |
| Failure to act | Art. 265 TFEU | Challenge EU institution's unlawful inaction |

**Foundational Doctrines:**

- **Direct Effect:** EU law creates rights enforceable by individuals in national courts (Van Gend en Loos, Case 26/62, 1963).
- **Supremacy:** EU law takes precedence over conflicting national law (Costa v. ENEL, Case 6/64, 1964).
- **State Liability:** Member states may be liable in damages for breaches of EU law (Francovich, Joined Cases C-6/90 and C-9/90, 1991).

### Tension Between Courts

The relationship between the BVerfG and the CJEU has produced significant jurisprudential tension. The BVerfG's PSPP decision (2020), declaring the ECB's bond-buying program ultra vires and the CJEU's prior approval of it methodologically deficient, represented an unprecedented direct challenge by a national constitutional court to the CJEU's authority. The European Commission initiated infringement proceedings against Germany, though these were not pursued to judgment. This episode illustrates the unresolved structural tension between national constitutional identity and EU legal supremacy.

## Comparative Analysis

### Concentrated vs. Diffuse Models

The fundamental structural divide lies between concentrated review (South Korea, Germany) and diffuse review (United States). In the concentrated model, a specialized court exercises monopoly jurisdiction over constitutional questions, providing uniformity and expertise at the cost of limiting access. In the diffuse model, any court may address constitutional questions, providing broader access but creating the possibility of inconsistent interpretations until the supreme court resolves disagreements. The European system adds a supranational layer, with the ECtHR and CJEU exercising review functions that complement and sometimes conflict with national systems.

### Access Mechanisms

Germany provides the broadest access through its constitutional complaint procedure, which permits challenges to court judgments and accounts for the overwhelming majority of cases. South Korea provides significant access through its dual constitutional complaint system but excludes challenges to court judgments. The United States imposes the most restrictive access requirements through its standing, ripeness, and mootness doctrines. The ECHR provides individual access but requires exhaustion of domestic remedies.

### Remedial Powers

The three jurisdictions differ in their approach to remedies when a law is found unconstitutional. The United States simply declares the law void, though courts may prospectively delay the effect. Germany has developed a range of remedial options including nullity (Nichtigkeit), incompatibility without nullity (Unvereinbarkeitserklärung), and directive decisions ordering specific legislative action. South Korea has adopted the German approach of nonconformity decisions, allowing legislative correction within a specified timeframe.

### Counter-Majoritarian Legitimacy

All systems grapple with the counter-majoritarian difficulty: how to justify unelected judges overriding democratically enacted legislation. Germany addresses this through the supermajority requirement for selecting justices and non-renewable twelve-year terms. South Korea divides appointment power among the President, National Assembly, and Chief Justice. The United States relies on life tenure and Senate confirmation. The ECtHR derives legitimacy from the international treaty framework and the principle of subsidiarity.
