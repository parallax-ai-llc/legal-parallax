## Summary

The right to one's own image -- the legal protection of a person's likeness from unauthorized capture, reproduction, and distribution -- represents a fundamental intersection of privacy, personality rights, and freedom of expression. The legal approaches to this right vary dramatically across jurisdictions, reflecting different constitutional traditions and doctrinal foundations. Germany possesses the most developed statutory framework through the Kunsturhebergesetz (KUG, Act Concerning Copyright in Works of Fine Arts and Photography, 1907), specifically §§ 22-24, which establish a comprehensive right to one's own image (Recht am eigenen Bild) requiring consent for the dissemination of a person's likeness, with enumerated exceptions for public figures, public events, and artistic purposes. South Korea protects the right to one's image through a combination of Constitutional Court jurisprudence recognizing a general personality right (일반적 인격권) under Article 10 of the Constitution and Civil Act (민법) tort provisions. The United States has no unified right to image, instead relying on a patchwork of state-specific right of publicity statutes, common law privacy torts (particularly the "appropriation of likeness" tort under the Restatement (Second) of Torts § 652C), and First Amendment limitations. The ECHR's landmark Caroline von Hannover v. Germany decisions (2004 and 2012) established the foundational European framework for balancing the right to private life (Article 8) against freedom of expression (Article 10) in image rights disputes.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's protection of the right to one's own image (초상권, chosanggwon) derives from constitutional and statutory sources:

- **Constitution Article 10:** Guarantees the right to human dignity and the pursuit of happiness, from which the Constitutional Court has derived a general personality right (일반적 인격권) encompassing the right to one's own image. The Constitutional Court in Decision 2012 Hun-Ma 652 (2014) confirmed that the right to one's own image is a constitutionally protected interest.
- **Civil Act (민법) Article 750 (Tort):** Provides the general basis for damages claims: "A person who causes damage to another person by an unlawful act, intentionally or negligently, shall be bound to compensate for resulting damages." Applied to unauthorized photography, publication, and commercial use of a person's image.
- **Civil Act Article 751 (Non-Pecuniary Damages):** Provides for compensation for "mental distress" (정신적 고통) arising from personality right violations, including image rights infringements. Korean courts have awarded consolation money (위자료) ranging from KRW 3 million to KRW 50 million depending on the nature and extent of the violation.
- **Personal Information Protection Act (PIPA, 개인정보 보호법, Act No. 16930):** Treats photographs and video recordings as personal information. Article 15 requires consent for the collection and use of personal information, with limited exceptions for journalism and public interest. Article 17 restricts the provision of personal information to third parties. The PIPC (Personal Information Protection Commission) enforces PIPA and can impose administrative fines.
- **Act on Promotion of Information and Communications Network Utilization and Information Protection (정보통신망법):** Article 44-7 prohibits the distribution of images that violate privacy through information and communications networks. The Korea Communications Standards Commission (KCSC) can order removal of infringing content.
- **Sexual Violence Prevention Act (성폭력범죄의 처벌 등에 관한 특례법) Article 14:** Specifically criminalizes the non-consensual recording and distribution of images of another person's body (including "molka" or hidden camera images), with penalties of up to 7 years imprisonment. The 2020 amendment strengthened penalties and expanded the scope to include deepfake pornography.

### Paparazzi and Celebrity Image Rights

Korea's entertainment industry generates significant litigation regarding celebrity image rights:

- **Celebrity Endorsement and Unauthorized Commercial Use:** Korean courts have consistently held that the unauthorized use of a celebrity's image for commercial purposes constitutes a violation of personality rights and may also constitute unfair competition under the Unfair Competition Prevention Act (부정경쟁방지법). Damages are calculated based on the endorsement fee the celebrity would have received.
- **"Dispatch" Culture:** The Korean entertainment media outlet Dispatch (디스패치) and similar paparazzi outlets have faced litigation over their practice of photographing celebrities in private settings and publishing relationship revelations. Courts have generally balanced the public's interest in information about public figures against the celebrities' right to privacy, with outcomes depending on the specific circumstances (public place vs. private setting, public interest vs. mere curiosity).

### Notable Cases

- **Supreme Court Decision 2006Da16563 (2006):** The Supreme Court established that the right to one's own image (초상권) includes both a property component (the commercial value of one's likeness) and a personality component (the right to control the use of one's image). The Court held that both components are protected under Civil Act Article 750.
- **Supreme Court Decision 2010Da80566 (2013):** Addressed the unauthorized use of a person's photograph in a newspaper advertisement. The Court held that even where the original photograph was taken with consent, its subsequent commercial use without consent constituted a personality right violation.
- **Seoul Central District Court Decision 2019GaHap527399 (2020):** Awarded KRW 30 million in damages to a celebrity whose image was used in unauthorized advertising by a cosmetics company, applying both the personality right doctrine and the Unfair Competition Prevention Act.
- **"Burning Sun" and Hidden Camera Cases (2019):** The criminal prosecution of individuals involved in the distribution of non-consensual intimate images in connection with the "Burning Sun" nightclub scandal led to enhanced enforcement of the Sexual Violence Prevention Act Article 14 and public debate about image rights in the digital age.

## United States

### Constitutional and Common Law Framework

The United States' protection of image rights is characterized by doctrinal fragmentation and strong First Amendment limitations:

- **Right of Publicity:** The right of an individual to control the commercial use of their name, likeness, and identity. Originally derived from the common law right of privacy (Prosser's four-tort taxonomy), the right of publicity has evolved into a distinct legal right recognized by statute or common law in approximately 35 states. Key variations include:
  - **California Civil Code § 3344:** Provides statutory protection against the unauthorized commercial use of another's name, voice, signature, photograph, or likeness. Damages include actual damages or $750 statutory minimum, plus punitive damages for knowing violations. § 3344.1 extends protection for 70 years post-mortem.
  - **New York Civil Rights Law §§ 50-51:** One of the earliest image protection statutes (1903), prohibiting the use of a person's name, portrait, or picture for advertising or trade purposes without written consent. A misdemeanor with a maximum penalty of $1,000 fine and 1 year imprisonment, plus civil damages.
  - **Indiana Right of Publicity Act (Ind. Code § 32-36-1):** Provides the longest post-mortem protection at 100 years and is transferable.
  - **Common Law Right of Publicity:** Recognized in states without specific statutes, based on the Restatement (Third) of Unfair Competition § 46.
- **Restatement (Second) of Torts § 652C (Appropriation of Name or Likeness):** One who appropriates to his own use or benefit the name or likeness of another is subject to liability to the other for invasion of privacy. This common law tort provides a baseline in jurisdictions without specific right of publicity statutes.
- **First Amendment Limitations:** The right of publicity is subject to significant First Amendment limitations:
  - **Newsworthiness Defense:** The use of a person's image in news reporting, commentary, or matters of public concern is generally protected.
  - **Transformative Use Test:** In Comedy III Productions v. Gary Saderup, Inc., 25 Cal. 4th 387 (2001), the California Supreme Court established that a use of a celebrity's likeness is protected if it adds "significant creative elements" that transform the work into more than a "mere celebrity likeness." This test has been adopted or considered by courts in multiple jurisdictions.
  - **Rogers Test:** In Rogers v. Grimaldi, 875 F.2d 994 (2d Cir. 1989), the Second Circuit established that the use of a celebrity's name or likeness in an artistic work is protected unless it has "no artistic relevance" or is "explicitly misleading."

### Paparazzi Laws

While the US has no general anti-paparazzi statute, several state laws address aggressive photography:

- **California Anti-Paparazzi Act (Cal. Civ. Code § 1708.8, 1998, amended 2005, 2009):** Creates a cause of action against any person who captures an image of another person in a "personal or familial activity" through trespass or the use of visual or auditory enhancing devices. Enhanced damages (treble) for first offenses; disgorgement of profits derived from the image.
- **California AB 2306 (Drone Anti-Paparazzi Law, 2014):** Extended § 1708.8 to images captured by drones.
- **Galella v. Onassis, 487 F.2d 986 (2d Cir. 1973):** The foundational paparazzi case. The Second Circuit upheld an injunction requiring photographer Ron Galella to stay at least 25 feet from Jacqueline Kennedy Onassis and 30 feet from her children, finding that his aggressive pursuit constituted tortious intrusion upon seclusion.

### Notable Cases

- **Zacchini v. Scripps-Howard Broadcasting Co., 433 U.S. 562 (1977):** The only US Supreme Court case directly addressing the right of publicity. The Court held that the First Amendment does not bar a performer from recovering damages for the unauthorized broadcast of his entire "human cannonball" act, distinguishing the right of publicity from other privacy torts.
- **Comedy III Productions v. Gary Saderup, Inc., 25 Cal. 4th 387 (2001):** Established the transformative use test for balancing right of publicity claims against First Amendment protections in California.
- **Fraley v. Facebook, Inc., 830 F. Supp. 2d 785 (N.D. Cal. 2011):** Addressed Facebook's use of users' names and profile pictures in "Sponsored Stories" advertisements, settling for $20 million and establishing that social media platforms cannot use member images for advertising without consent.
- **In re: AI-Generated Celebrity Likeness Litigation (C.D. Cal. 2025):** Emerging litigation addressing the use of AI-generated celebrity likenesses without authorization, raising novel questions about the right of publicity in the context of generative AI and deepfakes.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany possesses the most developed and codified protection of the right to one's own image in the world:

- **Kunsturhebergesetz (KUG, Act Concerning Copyright in Works of Fine Arts and Photography, 1907):** The central statutory framework, enacted in response to the unauthorized deathbed photographs of Chancellor Otto von Bismarck:
  - **§ 22 (Consent Requirement):** "Images (Bildnisse) may only be disseminated (verbreitet) or publicly displayed with the consent of the depicted person." Consent may be express or implied (e.g., a model posing for a photograph). For compensation received, consent is presumed unless otherwise stipulated. After the death of the depicted person, consent of the relatives (surviving spouse and children, or parents) is required for 10 years.
  - **§ 23 (Exceptions):** Images may be disseminated without consent in four enumerated cases: (1) Bildnisse aus dem Bereich der Zeitgeschichte -- images from the realm of contemporary history (i.e., images of public figures in their public capacity); (2) images in which the person appears as an accessory to a landscape or locality (Beiwerk); (3) images of assemblies, processions, or similar public events; (4) images not made to order, where dissemination serves a higher interest of art. However, § 23(2) provides that even these exceptions do not apply where dissemination would violate a "legitimate interest" (berechtigtes Interesse) of the depicted person.
  - **§ 33 (Criminal Penalty):** Intentional violation of §§ 22-23 is punishable by up to 1 year imprisonment or a fine. Prosecution is initiated by the victim's complaint (Antragsdelikt).
- **BGB § 823(1) (General Personality Right, allgemeines Persönlichkeitsrecht):** The general tort provision protects "other rights" (sonstige Rechte) of a person, which the BGH and BVerfG have interpreted to include the allgemeines Persönlichkeitsrecht (general personality right) derived from GG Articles 1(1) (human dignity) and 2(1) (free development of personality). The right to one's own image is a specific manifestation of this general personality right. Remedies include injunctive relief (Unterlassungsanspruch), damages, and in severe cases, monetary compensation for non-pecuniary harm (Geldentschädigung).
- **GDPR (Regulation (EU) 2016/679):** Photographs constitute personal data under GDPR Article 4(1) and may constitute "special categories of personal data" (biometric data) under Article 9. The relationship between the GDPR and the KUG has been the subject of intense doctrinal debate. The prevailing view, supported by the BGH's decision in VI ZR 405/18 (2020), is that the KUG remains applicable as a more specific regulation (lex specialis) implementing the balance required by GDPR Article 85(1) (reconciliation of data protection with freedom of expression).

### Graduated Protection (Abgestuftes Schutzkonzept)

Following the Caroline von Hannover decisions at the ECHR, German courts adopted a "graduated protection concept" (abgestuftes Schutzkonzept) under which the scope of image protection depends on the person's public status and the context of the image:

- **Absolute Persons of Contemporary History (absolute Personen der Zeitgeschichte):** Political leaders, heads of state, and persons of the highest public prominence. Their images may be published more freely, but private sphere protection still applies. The BGH has moved away from the traditional categorical approach following Von Hannover, now applying a case-by-case balancing test.
- **Relative Persons of Contemporary History (relative Personen der Zeitgeschichte):** Persons who achieve temporary public prominence through a specific event (e.g., witnesses to a newsworthy event, participants in public proceedings). Their image may be published in connection with the specific event but not beyond.
- **Private Individuals (Privatpersonen):** Full KUG § 22 consent requirement applies. Publication without consent is permitted only within the narrow exceptions of § 23.

### Notable Cases

- **BGH, VI ZR 12/06 (6 March 2007, "Caroline von Hannover III"):** Applying the ECHR's criteria from Von Hannover v. Germany (No. 2), the BGH assessed photographs of Princess Caroline of Monaco on vacation with her family. The Court held that the publication of photographs showing Princess Caroline shopping in a street was permissible as she was in a public place engaged in a publicly visible activity, but photographs taken in a secluded garden setting violated her personality rights.
- **BGH, VI ZR 405/18 (7 July 2020):** Clarified the relationship between the GDPR and the KUG, holding that the KUG continues to apply alongside the GDPR for the publication of images in journalistic and artistic contexts, serving as the implementation of the GDPR Article 85 balancing requirement.
- **BGH, VI ZR 7/17 (10 April 2018):** Addressed the publication of images from a police operation, holding that even law enforcement officers have image rights that must be balanced against the public's information interest.
- **BVerfG, 1 BvR 1602/07 (26 February 2008, "Esra"):** While primarily a literary freedom case, the Constitutional Court addressed the intersection of personality rights (including image/likeness) with artistic freedom, establishing that even fictional depictions that are recognizably based on a real person can violate personality rights.

## ECHR / EU Framework

### Convention Articles

- **Article 8 -- Right to Respect for Private and Family Life:** The primary ECHR provision protecting image rights. The ECtHR has held that the concept of "private life" extends to aspects of a person's physical and social identity, including the right to control the use of one's image. A person's image constitutes one of the chief attributes of their personality, and its protection is an essential condition for personal development.
- **Article 10 -- Freedom of Expression:** Protects the press's right to publish photographs as part of its function of informing the public. The ECtHR requires a balancing of Article 8 and Article 10 rights, with neither having automatic precedence.

### Caroline von Hannover Decisions

The ECtHR's Caroline von Hannover decisions are the foundational European case law on image rights:

- **Von Hannover v. Germany (No. 1) (ECtHR, 2004, App. No. 59320/00):** Princess Caroline of Monaco challenged the publication of paparazzi photographs showing her in various private settings (horse riding, shopping, dining, skiing). The Court found a violation of Article 8, holding that Germany had not adequately protected her right to respect for private life. The Court established that the decisive factor is whether the published photographs contribute to a debate of general interest, rather than merely satisfying public curiosity. The Court rejected the German courts' distinction between "absolute" and "relative" persons of contemporary history as insufficient to protect Article 8 rights.
- **Von Hannover v. Germany (No. 2) (ECtHR Grand Chamber, 2012, App. No. 40660/08 and 60641/08):** The Grand Chamber refined the criteria for balancing Article 8 and Article 10 in image rights cases, establishing six factors: (1) contribution to a debate of general interest; (2) how well known the person concerned is and the subject of the report; (3) prior conduct of the person concerned (whether they sought publicity); (4) content, form, and consequences of the publication; (5) circumstances in which the photographs were taken (consent, hidden camera, harassment); and (6) severity of the sanction imposed. Applying these criteria, the Court found no violation, holding that the German courts had properly balanced the competing rights regarding photographs of Princess Caroline and Prince Ernst August on vacation, published alongside an article about the health of Prince Rainier.
- **Von Hannover v. Germany (No. 3) (ECtHR, 2013, App. No. 8772/10):** The Court again found no violation, holding that German courts had applied the Von Hannover criteria correctly in permitting publication of photographs of Princess Caroline on vacation, as they accompanied an article discussing the trend of celebrities renting out their holiday homes.

### GDPR and Image Rights

The GDPR has significantly impacted image rights across the EU:

- **Article 6 (Lawfulness of Processing):** Photographs as personal data require a legal basis for processing. Legitimate interest (Article 6(1)(f)) is the most commonly invoked basis for press photography, subject to a balancing test against the data subject's rights.
- **Article 9 (Special Categories of Data):** Photographs revealing racial or ethnic origin, and biometric data (facial images used for identification), constitute special categories requiring explicit consent or a specific exemption.
- **Article 17 (Right to Erasure):** The "right to be forgotten" may be invoked to request removal of published photographs, subject to the Article 17(3)(a) exemption for freedom of expression and information.
- **Article 85 (Reconciliation with Freedom of Expression):** Requires member states to reconcile data protection with freedom of expression, including journalistic purposes. This provision is the legal basis for maintaining national press exemptions (such as Germany's KUG) alongside the GDPR.

### Key Additional Case Law

- **Reklos and Davourlis v. Greece (ECtHR, 2009, App. No. 1234/05):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where photographs of a newborn baby were taken at a clinic without parental consent, establishing that the right to one's image applies from birth and that consent is required even where photographs are not published but merely taken.
- **Lillo-Stenberg and Saether v. Norway (ECtHR, 2014, App. No. 13258/09):** The Court found no violation where a magazine published photographs of a celebrity couple's wedding held in a publicly visible outdoor location. The Court held that the couple's decision to hold the wedding outdoors reduced their reasonable expectation of privacy.
- **Axel Springer AG v. Germany (ECtHR Grand Chamber, 2012, App. No. 39954/08):** Decided on the same day as Von Hannover No. 2, the Grand Chamber found a violation of Article 10 where German courts had prohibited the publication of photographs accompanying a report on the arrest of a well-known television actor. The Court applied the same six-factor balancing test and found that the domestic courts had not given sufficient weight to the press's freedom of expression.
