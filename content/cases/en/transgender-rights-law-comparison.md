## Summary

Transgender rights -- the legal protections ensuring that transgender, non-binary, and gender diverse individuals can live authentically, obtain legal recognition of their gender identity, access healthcare, and be protected from discrimination and violence -- represent one of the most rapidly evolving and politically contested areas of law worldwide. South Korea permits legal gender change through a court-ordered process established by a 2006 Supreme Court (대법원) decision and refined by the Supreme Court's 2022 guidelines, but requires a diagnosis of gender identity disorder, psychiatric evaluation, and historically has required surgical intervention and sterilization, though the Supreme Court is reconsidering the surgery requirement; no comprehensive anti-discrimination statute protects gender identity, and the Military Criminal Act's former sodomy provision, struck down in 2023, had particular impact on transgender service members. The United States provides federal employment protection for transgender individuals under Title VII as interpreted in Bostock v. Clayton County (2020) but faces a complex and rapidly shifting landscape where the Biden administration extended protections through executive action and regulatory interpretation while a majority of states have enacted laws restricting transgender healthcare for minors, bathroom access, and sports participation, with the Supreme Court upholding Tennessee's ban on gender-affirming care for minors in L.W. v. Skrmetti (2025). Germany enacted the Selbstbestimmungsgesetz (Self-Determination Act, SBGG) on April 12, 2024, effective November 1, 2024, replacing the 1980 Transsexuellengesetz (TSG) with a self-declaration model requiring no medical diagnosis, no surgical intervention, and no court proceedings, representing one of the most progressive gender recognition frameworks globally; the Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) had already struck down the TSG's surgery and sterilization requirements in 2011 (1 BvR 3295/07). The European Court of Human Rights established in Goodwin v. United Kingdom (2002) that states must provide legal recognition of gender reassignment under Article 8 ECHR, and the Court has continued to develop jurisprudence requiring states to balance transgender rights against other interests, while the Council of Europe's Committee of Ministers Resolution CM/Rec(2010)5 recommends that member states establish quick, transparent, and accessible procedures for gender recognition based on self-determination.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea has no comprehensive legislation governing gender recognition or transgender rights. The legal framework is primarily judge-made, with the Supreme Court and lower courts establishing procedures and criteria through case law.

### Gender Recognition Process

The Supreme Court's 2006 decision established that legal gender change is permissible through a family court petition. The Supreme Court's Internal Guidelines for Gender Registration Change Cases (성전환자의 성별정정허가신청사건 등 사무처리지침), most recently revised in 2022, set forth the criteria:

- Applicant must be an adult (18 or older)
- Diagnosed with gender identity disorder (성별불일치) by a qualified psychiatrist
- Must have lived in the affirmed gender for a period
- Historically required sex reassignment surgery, though courts have increasingly waived this in individual cases
- Must not have minor children (this requirement has been applied inconsistently)
- Must be unmarried or have divorced

### Byun Hui-su Case

Staff Sergeant Byun Hui-su (변희수) became the first known transgender service member in the South Korean military when she publicly came out and underwent gender reassignment surgery in 2019. In January 2020, the Army forcibly discharged her, finding that her surgery constituted a "mental or physical disability" rendering her unfit for service. Byun filed a lawsuit challenging the discharge and an appeal with the National Human Rights Commission, which ruled the discharge constituted discrimination. Tragically, Byun was found dead in her home on March 3, 2021. In October 2021, a court ruled her discharge illegal. The case drew international attention to the treatment of transgender individuals in South Korea's military.

### Healthcare Access

Gender-affirming healthcare is not covered by the National Health Insurance Service (국민건강보험) as a standard benefit. Transgender individuals must bear the cost of hormonal therapy and surgical procedures out of pocket. There are a limited number of medical providers offering gender-affirming care in South Korea, and there are no standardized clinical guidelines comparable to those in Western countries.

## United States

### Legal Framework

**Bostock v. Clayton County, 590 U.S. 644 (2020):** The Supreme Court held that discrimination based on transgender status constitutes discrimination "because of sex" under Title VII. This provides federal employment protection but does not extend to housing, public accommodations, or education under Title VII.

**Title IX:** The Biden administration issued regulations in 2024 interpreting Title IX's prohibition on sex discrimination in education to encompass gender identity. These regulations faced legal challenges and injunctions in multiple states. The regulations did not address transgender participation in sports, deferring that issue.

### L.W. v. Skrmetti (2025)

In its most significant transgender rights decision, the Supreme Court upheld Tennessee's ban on puberty blockers and hormones for transgender minors in L.W. v. Skrmetti, holding that the law did not trigger heightened scrutiny because it regulated medical procedures rather than classifying by transgender status per se. The decision applied rational basis review, finding a rational relationship between the ban and the state's interest in protecting minors from experimental medical procedures.

### State Legislation

The landscape of state legislation affecting transgender rights has shifted dramatically since 2021:

**Gender-Affirming Care for Minors:** As of 2026, at least 24 states have enacted laws restricting or banning puberty blockers, hormones, and/or surgical procedures for minors with gender dysphoria. Several laws have been challenged in court with mixed results.

**Bathroom and Facilities:** Multiple states have enacted laws requiring individuals to use facilities corresponding to their sex assigned at birth in schools, government buildings, and other settings.

**Identity Documents:** Federal passport policy allows gender marker X (non-binary) since 2022. State policies for driver's licenses and birth certificates vary widely, with some states offering X markers and self-attestation and others requiring surgical documentation.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

### Transsexuellengesetz (TSG, 1980-2024)

The Transsexuellengesetz, enacted in 1980, was the original framework for legal gender recognition. It established two pathways: a "small solution" (name change) and a "large solution" (legal gender change). Both required psychiatric assessments and court proceedings. The "large solution" originally required sex reassignment surgery, sterilization, permanent inability to reproduce, and divorce.

The Federal Constitutional Court systematically struck down the most restrictive TSG requirements:

| Decision | Year | Provision Struck Down |
|----------|------|-----------------------|
| BVerfGE 60, 123 | 1982 | Minimum age of 25 |
| BVerfGE 88, 87 | 1993 | Requirement to be German citizen or have German residence |
| BVerfGE 116, 243 | 2006 | Requirement to be unmarried (partial) |
| 1 BvR 3295/07 | 2011 | Surgery and sterilization requirements |
| 1 BvR 747/17 | 2017 | Divorce requirement |

### Selbstbestimmungsgesetz (SBGG, 2024)

The Self-Determination Act replaced the TSG effective November 1, 2024. Key provisions:

**Declaration Procedure:** Legal gender and first name changes are accomplished through a declaration at the Standesamt (civil registry office). No court proceedings, medical assessments, diagnoses, or surgical interventions are required.

**Three-Month Reflection Period:** Applicants must register their intention at least three months before the declaration takes effect.

**Options:** Available gender markers are male (männlich), female (weiblich), diverse (divers), and no entry (keine Angabe). Germany thus recognizes four options for gender markers.

**Minors:** Persons aged 14 and older may apply with parental consent. If parents withhold consent, the family court may authorize the change if it serves the minor's best interests. Children under 14 must be represented by their legal guardian.

**Waiting Period for Reversal:** A subsequent change requires a one-year waiting period.

**Special Provisions:** The SBGG includes provisions addressing parental status (a transgender man who gives birth is registered as "mother"), military service obligations, and access to gender-segregated facilities.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Goodwin v. United Kingdom (Application No. 28957/95, Grand Chamber, 2002):** The Grand Chamber held that the UK's failure to legally recognize the post-operative gender of a transgender person violated Articles 8 and 12, establishing that states have a positive obligation to provide legal gender recognition.

**A.P., Garçon and Nicot v. France (Application No. 79885/12, 2017):** The Court held that France's requirement that transgender individuals undergo sterilization or medical treatment likely to cause sterilization as a precondition for legal gender recognition violated Article 8. This decision effectively prohibited surgery and sterilization requirements across Council of Europe member states.

**X and Y v. Romania (Application No. 2145/16, 2021):** The Court found that Romania's lack of clear, foreseeable, and accessible legal procedures for gender recognition violated Article 8, requiring states to establish transparent legal frameworks.

**O.H. and G.H. v. Germany (Application No. 53568/18, 2023):** The Court addressed the registration of a transgender man as the legal mother of his biological child, finding no violation of Article 8 and noting that Germany's registration of the biological mother role, regardless of legal gender, fell within the margin of appreciation.

### EU Framework

**P v S and Cornwall County Council (C-13/94, 1996):** The CJEU held that dismissal of a transgender employee constitutes sex discrimination under the Equal Treatment Directive, establishing EU-level protection for transgender individuals in employment.

**Directive 2006/54/EC (Gender Equality Recast):** The recast Gender Equality Directive in employment incorporates the P v S principle, covering discrimination arising from gender reassignment.

**Council of Europe Resolution CM/Rec(2010)5:** Recommends that member states establish "quick, transparent and accessible procedures, based on self-determination" for legal gender recognition.

**EU LGBTIQ Equality Strategy 2020-2025:** Commits the Commission to supporting member states in adopting best practices on legal gender recognition and to funding transgender-inclusive health research.

## Comparative Analysis

### Gender Recognition Models

The jurisdictions represent three distinct models. Germany's SBGG (2024) establishes the self-declaration model, requiring no medical or judicial gatekeeping. The United States presents a fragmented approach, with some states allowing self-attestation and others requiring surgery. South Korea maintains the most restrictive approach among the four, requiring judicial proceedings and psychiatric diagnosis, though the surgery requirement is under review. The ECHR has prohibited surgery and sterilization requirements (A.P., Garçon and Nicot) but has not yet mandated self-declaration, leaving states a margin of appreciation on the procedural pathway.

### Healthcare Access for Minors

This is the most contentious area. The United States is experiencing the most significant backlash, with a majority of states banning or restricting gender-affirming care for minors and the Supreme Court upholding such bans. Germany provides access through established clinical guidelines. South Korea has limited access due to a small number of providers and no insurance coverage. The ECHR has not yet addressed this issue directly.

### Military Service

All four jurisdictions now formally permit transgender military service, though implementation varies. Germany has the most comprehensive framework. The U.S. policy was reinstated by executive order in 2021 after the Trump administration's ban. South Korea's de facto approach has been hostile, as demonstrated by the Byun Hui-su case, though no formal ban exists.

### Non-Binary Recognition

Germany recognizes four gender marker options (male, female, diverse, no entry) since 2018 (following BVerfG 1 BvR 2019/16) and the SBGG. The United States allows X markers on federal passports and in some states. South Korea does not recognize non-binary gender markers. The ECHR has not yet addressed non-binary recognition.
