## Summary

Cannibalism -- the act of consuming human flesh or tissue -- presents one of the most unusual challenges in comparative criminal law, as the overwhelming majority of legal systems do not specifically criminalize the act of consumption itself, instead relying on adjacent offenses such as murder, mutilation of remains, desecration of corpses, and abuse of a corpse to prosecute conduct associated with cannibalism. South Korea does not have a specific statutory prohibition on cannibalism but addresses related conduct through Articles 159 and 161 of the Criminal Act (형법 제159조, 제161조), which criminalize the excavation of human remains (시체발굴) and the concealment, destruction, abandonment, or seizure of corpses (시체손괴 등), with penalties of up to seven years' imprisonment; additionally, cases involving homicide committed for the purpose of cannibalism would be prosecuted under the murder provisions (형법 제250조) with potential sentences of death or life imprisonment. The United States has no federal prohibition on cannibalism, and only Idaho explicitly criminalizes the act of consuming human flesh through Idaho Code Section 18-5003, carrying a penalty of up to fourteen years' imprisonment; all other states rely on murder, desecration of remains, and related statutes to address cannibalism-related conduct. Germany famously confronted the issue in the Armin Meiwes case (BGH 2 StR 35/05, 2006), where the Bundesgerichtshof convicted Meiwes of murder (Mord, Section 211 StGB) after initially being convicted only of manslaughter (Totschlag, Section 212 StGB), despite the victim's apparent consent to being killed and consumed; Germany has no specific anti-cannibalism statute and relies on the inviolability of human dignity (Menschenwürde) under Article 1 of the Grundgesetz as the foundational principle preventing consent from validating the killing. At the European level, the ECHR's jurisprudence on the absolute nature of Article 2 (right to life) and Article 3 (prohibition of inhuman and degrading treatment) provides the framework through which cannibalism cases implicate human rights norms, while no EU directive or regulation specifically addresses cannibalism as a distinct offense.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea has no specific statutory prohibition against cannibalism. The Korean legal system addresses cannibalism-related conduct through existing criminal provisions, primarily those governing offenses against the dead and homicide.

### Applicable Provisions

| Offense | Provision | Penalty |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Murder | Art. 250(1) (살인죄) | Death, life imprisonment, or 5+ years |
| Murder of lineal ascendant | Art. 250(2) | Death, life imprisonment, or 7+ years |
| Concealment/destruction of corpse | Art. 161(1) (시체손괴/유기/은닉) | Up to 7 years imprisonment |
| Excavation of human remains | Art. 159 | Up to 5 years or fine up to 5 million KRW |
| Desecration of grave to access remains | Art. 160 (분묘발굴) | Up to 5 years imprisonment |

### Consent in Korean Criminal Law

Korean criminal law does not recognize consent as a defense to homicide. Article 252 of the Criminal Act addresses murder upon request or with consent (촉탁·승낙에 의한 살인), providing reduced penalties (1-10 years imprisonment) when a person kills another at the victim's earnest request or with their consent. However, even this provision does not provide a defense -- it merely reduces the penalty. Cannibalism involving a willing victim would thus be prosecuted as murder upon request at minimum.

### Necessity Defense

Article 22 of the Criminal Act provides a necessity defense (긴급피난): "An act which is done in order to avert a present and imminent danger to one's own or another person's legal interest shall not be punishable, provided it does not exceed a reasonable limit." In survival situations, this defense could theoretically apply to cannibalism of an already-deceased person, though no Korean court has directly addressed this scenario.

### Cultural and Historical Context

Korean historical sources document instances of survival cannibalism during severe famines, particularly during the Joseon Dynasty. The annals record government responses to famine-related cannibalism as matters of welfare policy rather than criminal prosecution. In modern Korea, no significant cannibalism prosecution has been reported, and public discourse on the topic remains extremely limited.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States is notable for the near-complete absence of specific anti-cannibalism legislation. Only one state -- Idaho -- has enacted a statute directly criminalizing the consumption of human flesh.

**Idaho Code Section 18-5003:** "Any person who willfully ingests the flesh or blood of a human being is guilty of cannibalism. Cannibalism is punishable by imprisonment in the state prison not exceeding fourteen years." The statute includes an exception for survival situations: "It shall be an affirmative defense to a charge of cannibalism that the action was taken under extreme life-threatening conditions as the only apparent means of survival."

### Prosecution Through Adjacent Offenses

In the absence of specific cannibalism statutes, U.S. jurisdictions rely on the following:

| Offense Category | Typical Statutes | Application to Cannibalism |
|-----------------|-----------------|---------------------------|
| Murder/homicide | State murder statutes | Killing for purpose of consumption |
| Abuse of corpse | E.g., California H&S Code § 7052 | Mutilation or consumption of remains |
| Desecration of remains | E.g., New York PL § 145.23 | Disturbing remains for consumption |
| Mayhem | Common law and statutory | Removing body parts from living victim |

### Influential Case Law

**R v. Dudley and Stephens (1884):** While a British case, this decision profoundly influenced American law on survival cannibalism. After their yacht sank, sailors Thomas Dudley and Edwin Stephens killed and ate cabin boy Richard Parker after 20 days adrift at sea. The Queen's Bench Division rejected the necessity defense, holding that taking an innocent life can never be justified by necessity. This principle was adopted by American courts and remains the prevailing view.

**Jeffrey Dahmer (Wisconsin, 1992):** Perhaps the most notorious American cannibalism case, Jeffrey Dahmer murdered 17 men and boys between 1978 and 1991, consuming portions of several victims. Dahmer was prosecuted for murder rather than cannibalism, as Wisconsin has no specific cannibalism statute. He was convicted of 15 counts of murder and sentenced to fifteen consecutive life terms.

**Albert Fish (New York, 1935):** Fish was convicted of murder and executed for kidnapping and killing 10-year-old Grace Budd, whose remains he partially consumed. His trial raised significant questions about the insanity defense in cases involving cannibalistic behavior.

### Constitutional Considerations

The absence of specific cannibalism laws in most states has not been subjected to significant constitutional challenge. Legal scholars have debated whether the right to personal autonomy, as articulated in cases like Lawrence v. Texas, 539 U.S. 558 (2003), could theoretically protect consensual cannibalism of tissue voluntarily provided by a living person. However, no court has addressed this question, and public health regulations regarding human tissue would likely provide independent grounds for prohibition.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany has no specific statute criminalizing cannibalism. The legal framework relies on general criminal provisions, primarily the homicide and corpse desecration statutes.

### The Armin Meiwes Case

The Armin Meiwes case is the most legally significant cannibalism case in modern European jurisprudence. In 2001, Meiwes posted an advertisement on the internet seeking a willing victim to be killed and consumed. Bernd Jürgen Brandes responded, and on March 9, 2001, traveled to Meiwes's home in Rotenburg, where Meiwes killed him with his consent and consumed portions of his body over the following months.

**First Trial (LG Kassel, 2004):** The Landgericht Kassel convicted Meiwes of Totschlag (manslaughter, Section 212 StGB) rather than Mord (murder, Section 211 StGB), sentencing him to eight years and six months' imprisonment. The court reasoned that the victim's consent, while not a defense, negated the "base motives" (niedrige Beweggründe) element required for murder under Section 211.

**BGH Appeal (BGH 2 StR 35/05, 2005):** The Bundesgerichtshof reversed the manslaughter conviction and remanded for retrial, holding that the lower court had erred in its analysis of the murder elements. The BGH held that killing for the purpose of sexual gratification (the court found that Meiwes was sexually motivated) constitutes killing "to satisfy sexual desire" (zur Befriedigung des Geschlechtstriebs), which is an enumerated murder element under Section 211.

**Second Trial (LG Frankfurt, 2006):** The Landgericht Frankfurt convicted Meiwes of Mord and sentenced him to life imprisonment. The court found that the killing was motivated by sexual gratification and that the victim's consent could not negate the murder elements.

### Legal Analysis

| Issue | Court's Resolution |
|-------|-------------------|
| Effect of victim's consent | Consent does not negate murder elements; human dignity (Art. 1 GG) prevents consent to killing |
| Sexual motivation | Killing to satisfy sexual desire constitutes Mord under Section 211 |
| Cannibalism as motive | Not separately analyzed; subsumed under sexual motivation |
| Section 216 (killing on request) | Court rejected application; victim's "request" to be killed does not reduce to Tötung auf Verlangen |

### Killing on Request (Tötung auf Verlangen)

Section 216 StGB provides for reduced penalties (6 months to 5 years) when a person kills another at the victim's express and earnest request. In the Meiwes case, the courts debated but ultimately rejected the application of Section 216, finding that Meiwes's primary motivation was his own gratification rather than compliance with the victim's wish.

### Human Dignity (Menschenwürde)

Article 1(1) of the Grundgesetz states: "Human dignity shall be inviolable. To respect and protect it shall be the duty of all state authority." German legal scholarship regards human dignity as an objective value that cannot be waived by the individual. This principle underpins the legal position that consent to being killed and consumed cannot validate the act, regardless of how freely and deliberately the consent was given.

### Störung der Totenruhe

In addition to homicide charges, the desecration or abuse of a corpse -- including consumption of human remains -- is independently punishable under Section 168 StGB (Störung der Totenruhe), carrying up to three years' imprisonment. This provision would apply to cannibalism of remains even in the absence of a homicide charge.

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

While no ECHR case has directly addressed cannibalism, the Court's jurisprudence establishes several principles relevant to the legal treatment of cannibalism:

**Article 2 -- Right to Life:** The right to life under Article 2 imposes both negative obligations (states must not arbitrarily take life) and positive obligations (states must protect life). In Pretty v. United Kingdom (Application No. 2346/02, 2002), the Court held that Article 2 cannot be interpreted as conferring a right to die, and that states are not obligated to permit assisted suicide. By extension, consent to being killed for the purpose of cannibalism would not create a right under Article 2 that states must respect.

**Article 3 -- Prohibition of Inhuman or Degrading Treatment:** The absolute prohibition of inhuman and degrading treatment under Article 3 has been interpreted to impose positive obligations on states to prevent and punish severe forms of physical abuse. The consumption of human remains could engage Article 3 obligations, particularly where the victim was subjected to suffering.

**Article 8 -- Right to Private Life and Autonomy:** In cases involving personal autonomy, such as Laskey, Jaggard and Brown v. United Kingdom (Application No. 21627/93, 1997), the Court has held that states may criminalize consensual acts that cause bodily harm, rejecting the argument that personal autonomy under Article 8 precludes criminal sanction for sadomasochistic activities resulting in injury. This reasoning would apply a fortiori to consensual cannibalism.

### EU Regulatory Framework

No EU directive or regulation specifically addresses cannibalism. However, the following instruments are relevant:

**Regulation (EC) No 1069/2009 (Animal By-Products Regulation):** This regulation governs the handling of animal by-products and derived products not intended for human consumption. While primarily directed at animal remains, the regulation's underlying public health principles inform the treatment of human tissue disposal.

**Directive 2004/23/EC (Tissues and Cells Directive):** This Directive establishes quality and safety standards for the donation, procurement, testing, processing, preservation, storage, and distribution of human tissues and cells. While directed at medical applications, the Directive reinforces the principle that human tissue is subject to rigorous regulatory control.

### Comparative Human Dignity Frameworks

The concept of human dignity, which plays a central role in Germany's approach to cannibalism cases, is reflected in EU law through Article 1 of the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights: "Human dignity is inviolable. It must be respected and protected." The CJEU has invoked this provision in various contexts, though not directly in relation to cannibalism.

## Comparative Analysis

### Specific Criminalization vs. General Provisions

The overwhelming trend across jurisdictions is to address cannibalism through general criminal provisions rather than specific anti-cannibalism statutes. Idaho remains the sole notable exception among the jurisdictions studied. This approach reflects several factors: the extreme rarity of cannibalism cases, the difficulty of defining the offense precisely, and the adequacy of existing murder and corpse desecration provisions.

### The Consent Problem

The treatment of victim consent represents the most legally challenging aspect of cannibalism cases. All four jurisdictions agree that consent to being killed is legally invalid -- consent cannot transform homicide into a lawful act. However, the reasoning differs:

- **South Korea:** Consent reduces murder to a lower offense (Art. 252) but does not eliminate criminal liability.
- **United States:** Consent is not a defense to homicide; the Dudley and Stephens principle (adopted from English law) rejects necessity as justification.
- **Germany:** Human dignity (Menschenwürde) as an objective value prevents consent from validating killing, as the Meiwes case established.
- **ECHR:** The right to life (Art. 2) does not include a right to die (Pretty v. UK), and states may criminalize consensual harm (Laskey v. UK).

### Survival Cannibalism

All jurisdictions recognize, to varying degrees, the moral and legal complexity of survival cannibalism -- consuming the flesh of a person who has already died in order to survive extreme conditions. Idaho explicitly provides a statutory defense for survival cannibalism. Other jurisdictions would analyze such cases through general necessity or emergency doctrines, with outcomes depending on the specific circumstances.

### Penalty Severity

Where cannibalism involves homicide, penalties are severe across all jurisdictions: potential death penalty in South Korea, life imprisonment in the United States and Germany. Where cannibalism involves only the consumption of remains (without homicide), penalties are comparatively modest: up to seven years in South Korea, up to three years in Germany, and varying widely in the United States.
