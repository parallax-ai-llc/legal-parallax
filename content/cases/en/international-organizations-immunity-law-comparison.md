## Summary

The immunity of international organizations -- the legal doctrine shielding international organizations from the jurisdiction of domestic courts, protecting their premises from search and seizure, and granting privileges and immunities to their staff -- is a critical but contested area of international law that balances the functional necessity of organizational independence against the right of individuals to access justice. Unlike state immunity, which derives from sovereign equality (par in parem non habet imperium), the immunity of international organizations is rooted in functional necessity: organizations require immunity to perform their functions independently of host state or member state interference. The foundational instruments include the Convention on the Privileges and Immunities of the United Nations (General Convention, February 13, 1946), the Convention on the Privileges and Immunities of the Specialized Agencies (Specialized Agencies Convention, November 21, 1947), and the myriad headquarters agreements and bilateral arrangements between organizations and their host or member states. South Korea, as host to several international organizations and a major contributor to multilateral institutions, addresses organizational immunity through the Act on the Privileges, Immunities, etc. of International Organizations (국제기구의 특권·면제 등에 관한 법률, 2013), the Act on Privileges and Immunities of Diplomatic and Consular Missions (외교관 등의 특권·면제에 관한 법률), and specific headquarters agreements with organizations such as the Green Climate Fund (GCF) headquartered in Songdo, Incheon; Korean courts have had limited but growing engagement with organizational immunity questions. The United States, as host to the United Nations headquarters in New York and numerous other international organizations, governs immunity through the International Organizations Immunities Act (IOIA, 22 U.S.C. Section 288 et seq., 1945), the UN Headquarters Agreement (1947), and an extensive body of case law including Jam v. International Finance Corporation, 586 U.S. 199 (2019), in which the Supreme Court held that international organizations enjoy the same immunity as foreign governments under the Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act (FSIA), including its restrictive immunity exceptions for commercial activity. Germany, as host to significant international organizations including the European Central Bank (ECB) in Frankfurt, the European Patent Office (EPO) in Munich, and multiple UN agencies in Bonn, applies organizational immunity through the Gaststaatgesetz (Host State Act, 2019), individual headquarters agreements, and the general principles of the General Convention; German courts have actively addressed the tension between organizational immunity and the right of access to courts under Article 19(4) of the Basic Law and Article 6 of the ECHR. The ECHR and EU framework has produced the most significant jurisprudence on the limits of organizational immunity, with the European Court of Human Rights addressing the compatibility of immunity with Article 6(1) of the Convention (right of access to a court) in landmark cases including Waite and Kennedy v. Germany (Application No. 26083/94, Grand Chamber Judgment of February 18, 1999) and Stichting Mothers of Srebrenica v. Netherlands (Application No. 65542/12, Decision of June 11, 2013), while the CJEU has addressed the immunity of EU institutions and the relationship between EU law and international organization immunity.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's legal framework for organizational immunity is relatively recent and continues to develop. The Act on the Privileges, Immunities, etc. of International Organizations (국제기구의 특권·면제 등에 관한 법률, Act No. 11598, 2013) provides the general statutory basis, supplemented by specific headquarters agreements concluded with individual organizations. Korea is a party to the Convention on the Privileges and Immunities of the United Nations (General Convention, acceded June 9, 1992) and the Convention on the Privileges and Immunities of the Specialized Agencies (acceded December 13, 1977).

Article 3 of the 2013 Act establishes that international organizations designated by presidential decree shall enjoy juridical personality, immunity from jurisdiction, inviolability of premises, and exemption from taxation, in accordance with the applicable treaties and headquarters agreements. The scope of immunity is determined by the specific terms of each organization's agreement with Korea.

### Green Climate Fund (GCF)

The Green Climate Fund, the principal multilateral climate finance mechanism established under the UNFCCC, is headquartered in Songdo International City, Incheon, South Korea. The Agreement between the Republic of Korea and the Green Climate Fund Regarding the Headquarters of the Green Climate Fund (GCF Headquarters Agreement, 2013) grants the GCF immunity from jurisdiction and legal process (Article 4), inviolability of premises, archives, and communications (Articles 5-7), fiscal privileges including tax exemptions (Article 8), and privileges and immunities for GCF officials including immunity from legal process for acts performed in their official capacity (Article 10).

The GCF Headquarters Agreement follows the standard model for international organization headquarters agreements but includes specific provisions reflecting Korean legal requirements, including arrangements for employment of local staff, social security, and coordination with Korean immigration authorities for staff and delegates.

### Global Green Growth Institute (GGGI)

The Global Green Growth Institute, established as a treaty-based international organization by the Agreement on the Establishment of the Global Green Growth Institute (October 2012), is also headquartered in Seoul. Korea concluded a separate headquarters agreement with the GGGI granting privileges and immunities comparable to those of the GCF.

### Korean Court Practice

Korean courts have had limited engagement with international organization immunity issues. The Seoul Central District Court has addressed employment disputes involving staff of international organizations based in Korea, generally applying the immunity provisions of the relevant headquarters agreements. The Korean legal system's approach to organizational immunity is still developing, with scholars noting the tension between the constitutional right of access to courts (Article 27 of the Constitution: "All citizens shall have the right to trial in conformity with law") and the immunity of organizations from domestic jurisdiction.

### Penalties and Enforcement

| Issue | Legal Treatment |
|-------|----------------|
| Violation of organizational premises inviolability | Korean authorities may not enter without consent; diplomatic consequences |
| Employment disputes with international organizations | Generally immune from Korean labor courts; internal grievance mechanisms |
| Tax disputes involving exempt organizations | Resolved through headquarters agreement consultation mechanisms |
| Criminal acts by immune staff | Immunity may be waived by organization; functional immunity limits |
| Enforcement of judgments against organizations | Immunity from execution; no garnishment of organizational assets |

## United States

### Legal Framework

The International Organizations Immunities Act (IOIA, 22 U.S.C. Section 288 et seq.), enacted on December 29, 1945, is the primary statute governing the immunity of international organizations in the United States. The IOIA provides that international organizations designated by executive order shall enjoy "the same immunity from suit and every form of judicial process as is enjoyed by foreign governments" (Section 288a(b)), immunity of property and assets from search and seizure (Section 288a(c)), exemption from federal, state, and local taxation (Section 288a(d)), and customs privileges (Section 288a(e)).

The IOIA currently applies to over 80 international organizations designated by executive order, including the United Nations, the World Bank Group (IBRD, IDA, IFC, MIGA), the International Monetary Fund, the Organization of American States, NATO, and numerous specialized agencies and regional organizations.

### Jam v. International Finance Corporation (2019)

The Supreme Court's decision in Jam v. International Finance Corporation, 586 U.S. 199 (2019), fundamentally reshaped the law of international organization immunity in the United States. The case arose from claims by Indian fishermen and farmers alleging environmental and social harm caused by a coal-fired power plant in Gujarat, India, financed by the IFC (a World Bank Group institution).

The central question was whether the IOIA's grant of "the same immunity from suit... as is enjoyed by foreign governments" incorporated the restrictive immunity regime of the Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act (FSIA, 28 U.S.C. Section 1602 et seq.), including its exceptions for commercial activity (Section 1605(a)(2)), torts (Section 1605(a)(5)), and other categories. In a 7-1 decision authored by Chief Justice Roberts, the Court held that the IOIA's reference to foreign government immunity is a reference to the immunity that foreign governments currently enjoy, not to the absolute immunity they enjoyed in 1945 when the IOIA was enacted. Because foreign government immunity shifted from absolute to restrictive with the passage of the FSIA in 1976, international organizations now enjoy the same restrictive immunity.

The Jam decision means that international organizations designated under the IOIA may be sued in U.S. courts for claims arising from commercial activity (FSIA Section 1605(a)(2)), torts committed in the United States (Section 1605(a)(5)), and other excepted categories. The decision has generated significant concern among international organizations and their member states about the erosion of organizational immunity.

### UN Headquarters Agreement

The Agreement Between the United Nations and the United States Regarding the Headquarters of the United Nations (UN Headquarters Agreement, June 26, 1947, 11 UNTS 11) establishes the legal framework for the UN's presence in New York. The agreement grants the UN headquarters district inviolability (Section 9), guarantees freedom of transit for representatives and staff (Sections 11-13), and provides that the UN shall not be impediment to the administration of justice (Section 22). The interpretation and application of the Headquarters Agreement has generated disputes, including the 1988 attempt by Congress to close the Palestine Liberation Organization (PLO) Mission to the UN, which the U.S. District Court for the Southern District of New York held was precluded by the Headquarters Agreement (United States v. Palestine Liberation Organization, 695 F. Supp. 1456 (S.D.N.Y. 1988)).

### Staff Immunities

IOIA Section 288d provides that officers and employees of designated international organizations are immune from suit and legal process for acts performed in their official capacity. Representatives of member states to international organizations enjoy similar immunity under the IOIA and applicable conventions. The scope of "official capacity" immunity has been litigated in cases involving employment disputes, personal injury claims, and allegations of sexual harassment or misconduct by international organization staff.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany enacted the Gaststaatgesetz (Host State Act, Gesetz uber den Aufenthalt, die Erwerbstatigkeit und die Integration von Auslandern im Bundesgebiet -- specifically the Act on the Legal Status of International Organizations in Germany, 2019) to provide a comprehensive statutory framework for the privileges and immunities of international organizations headquartered or operating in Germany. Prior to the Gaststaatgesetz, organizational immunity was governed exclusively by individual headquarters agreements and the general principles of international law.

Germany is a party to the General Convention on the Privileges and Immunities of the United Nations (acceded November 5, 1980, with a reservation regarding German nationals employed by the UN) and hosts significant international organizations including the European Central Bank (ECB) in Frankfurt am Main, the European Patent Office (EPO) in Munich, and the UN Campus in Bonn (hosting UNFCCC, UNCCD, UNV, and other agencies).

### European Patent Office and Labor Disputes

The European Patent Office, headquartered in Munich with branches in The Hague, Berlin, and Vienna, has been the focus of some of the most contentious litigation regarding organizational immunity and employee rights. The EPO's internal governance and staff management practices have generated numerous disputes, including challenges to disciplinary proceedings, salary adjustments, and labor relations policies.

The EPO is governed by the European Patent Convention (EPC) and enjoys immunity from domestic jurisdiction under its Headquarters Agreement with Germany (Protocol on Privileges and Immunities, 1977). The Administrative Tribunal of the International Labour Organization (ILOAT) serves as the EPO's external administrative tribunal for staff disputes. German courts have generally upheld the EPO's immunity, but the Bundesverfassungsgericht has indicated that immunity cannot be absolute where no reasonable alternative remedy exists.

In the landmark decision of the Bundesverfassungsgericht of November 13, 2020 (2 BvR 1845/18 and 2 BvR 2100/18), the Court addressed the immunity of the EPO in the context of labor protests by EPO staff. The Court held that the immunity of international organizations under international law does not automatically preclude German courts from exercising jurisdiction over claims arising from the organization's activities on German territory when fundamental rights are at stake. The Court emphasized that the right to effective legal protection under Article 19(4) of the Basic Law requires that adequate alternative dispute resolution mechanisms exist within the organization.

### European Central Bank

The ECB, headquartered in Frankfurt am Main, enjoys privileges and immunities under the Protocol on the Privileges and Immunities of the European Union (Protocol No. 7 to the Treaties). As an EU institution, the ECB's immunity is governed by EU law rather than the general international law framework applicable to other international organizations. The CJEU has jurisdiction over disputes involving the ECB, including staff disputes (under the EU Civil Service Tribunal, now the General Court) and challenges to ECB regulatory decisions.

### Access to Justice Requirements

German courts have been at the forefront of developing the requirement that organizational immunity must be balanced against access to justice rights. The Bundesverfassungsgericht has held that the constitutional guarantee of effective legal protection (Rechtsschutzgarantie, Article 19(4) GG) and the right to a lawful judge (Article 101(1) GG) impose limits on the recognition of organizational immunity. Where an international organization provides no adequate alternative remedy for individuals whose rights are affected, German courts may decline to recognize immunity.

This approach is consistent with, but more explicitly developed than, the ECtHR's Waite and Kennedy framework (discussed below). German courts have applied this analysis in cases involving the EPO, UN agencies in Bonn, and other organizations, creating a body of jurisprudence that influences the broader European approach to organizational immunity.

| Issue | Legal Treatment |
|-------|----------------|

| Tax and customs disputes | Resolved under headquarters agreements; fiscal privileges |
| Criminal acts by immune personnel | Waiver request to organization; functional immunity limits |
| Environmental/tort claims | Immunity may yield to access to justice requirements |

## ECHR & EU

### Waite and Kennedy v. Germany (1999)

The Grand Chamber's judgment in Waite and Kennedy v. Germany (Application No. 26083/94, February 18, 1999) is the foundational ECHR case on the compatibility of international organization immunity with the right of access to a court under Article 6(1). The applicants were former temporary employees of the European Space Agency (ESA) who brought employment claims against ESA before German labor courts. The German courts dismissed the claims on the basis of ESA's immunity under its Convention and Headquarters Agreement.

The Grand Chamber held that the grant of immunities to international organizations was a legitimate aim pursuing the proper functioning of such organizations. However, the Court established a crucial proportionality test: "a material factor in determining whether granting ESA immunity from German jurisdiction is permissible under the Convention is whether the applicants had available to them reasonable alternative means to protect effectively their rights under the Convention" (paragraph 68). Because ESA had an internal appeals process (the Appeals Board) that the applicants had not utilized, the Court found no violation of Article 6(1).

The Waite and Kennedy test -- requiring "reasonable alternative means" of legal protection as a condition for the compatibility of organizational immunity with Article 6(1) -- has become the standard framework applied in subsequent cases, including Beer and Regan v. Germany (Application No. 28934/95, 1999, companion case decided on the same day), Mothers of Srebrenica (see below), and Klausecker v. Germany (Application No. 415/07, 2015, concerning the EPO).

### Stichting Mothers of Srebrenica v. Netherlands (2013)

In Stichting Mothers of Srebrenica v. Netherlands (Application No. 65542/12, Decision of June 11, 2013), the Court considered whether the Netherlands' recognition of the United Nations' immunity in a case brought by relatives of victims of the Srebrenica massacre (July 1995) violated Article 6(1). The applicants argued that the UN's absolute immunity, combined with the absence of any effective alternative remedy for their claims, rendered the immunity incompatible with their right of access to a court.

The Court declared the application inadmissible, holding that the recognition of UN immunity served the legitimate aim of ensuring the proper functioning of the organization and did not disproportionately restrict the applicants' right of access to a court. The Court emphasized the special position of the UN as the preeminent universal international organization and stated that "the attribution of immunity to the UN served a legitimate aim and was not disproportionate" even in the absence of an alternative remedy. This decision has been criticized by scholars who argue that it creates a gap in legal protection for victims of serious human rights violations by UN forces.

### EU Institutions and Immunity

The Protocol on the Privileges and Immunities of the European Union (Protocol No. 7 to the TEU and TFEU) grants the EU, its institutions, assets, and property immunity from jurisdiction and execution, with important exceptions. The CJEU has exclusive jurisdiction to interpret and apply EU law, providing a comprehensive alternative remedy system for disputes involving EU institutions. EU officials enjoy functional immunity under Article 11 of the Protocol, which may only be waived by the institution concerned.

The CJEU has addressed the immunity of EU institutions in employment disputes (under the EU Civil Service framework) and contractual disputes (under Article 272 TFEU jurisdiction clauses). The General Court and the Court of Justice provide a robust adjudicative system that largely satisfies the Waite and Kennedy "reasonable alternative means" test.

### Evolving Standards and Access to Justice

The tension between organizational immunity and access to justice continues to evolve. The ILC's ongoing work on the immunity of international organizations may eventually produce a comprehensive codification. The Institut de Droit International adopted a resolution in 2017 (Session of Hyderabad) on "The Immunity from Jurisdiction of International Organizations and of Their Agents" recommending that organizations should ensure access to appropriate modes of settlement of disputes. The International Law Association's Committee on Accountability of International Organizations has similarly advocated for enhanced accountability mechanisms.

## Comparative Analysis

### Scope of Immunity

The four jurisdictions reflect different approaches to the scope of organizational immunity. The United States, post-Jam v. IFC, applies restrictive immunity (with commercial activity and tort exceptions), representing the most significant judicial limitation on organizational immunity. Germany conditions immunity on the availability of adequate alternative remedies, reflecting its constitutional emphasis on effective legal protection. South Korea provides immunity based on specific headquarters agreements without yet developing significant limitations through case law. The ECHR framework establishes a proportionality test but has applied it deferentially, particularly regarding the UN.

### Access to Justice

The right of access to a court is the principal mechanism through which domestic and supranational courts limit organizational immunity. Germany's Article 19(4) GG and the ECHR's Article 6(1) both require that individuals have access to effective legal protection, but differ in stringency. The German approach is more demanding, potentially declining to recognize immunity where no adequate internal remedy exists. The ECHR approach, particularly after Mothers of Srebrenica, is more deferential, recognizing that the UN's special position may justify immunity even without alternative remedies. The U.S. approach bypasses the access to justice framework entirely, instead incorporating foreign sovereign immunity exceptions into organizational immunity through statutory interpretation.

### Staff Immunities and Employment Disputes

Employment disputes represent the most frequent source of litigation involving organizational immunity. All jurisdictions generally recognize that organizations are immune from domestic labor courts, but the availability and adequacy of internal dispute resolution mechanisms (administrative tribunals, appeals boards, ombudsmen) varies dramatically. The ILOAT (with jurisdiction over approximately 60 organizations), the World Bank Administrative Tribunal, the UN Dispute Tribunal, and various organization-specific mechanisms provide varying levels of due process protection.

### Future Directions

The ILC's work on organizational immunity, ongoing scholarly debate, and the practical consequences of the Jam decision suggest continued evolution in this area. The expansion of international organizations' activities into areas that directly affect individuals (development finance, peacekeeping, environmental regulation) increases the pressure for accountability mechanisms that balance functional independence with access to justice.
