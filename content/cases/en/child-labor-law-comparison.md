## Summary

Child labor law -- the legal framework establishing minimum ages for employment, regulating the types and conditions of work permitted for minors, and prohibiting hazardous or exploitative child labor -- reflects both historical labor movement achievements and contemporary challenges including the growth of social media child influencers and global supply chain accountability. South Korea regulates child labor through Articles 64 through 70 of the Labor Standards Act (근로기준법), which set the minimum employment age at 15 (with work permits available from age 13 for light work), limit working hours for minors aged 15-17 to 7 hours per day and 35 hours per week (extendable to 40 hours with consent), and prohibit minors from performing hazardous work designated by Presidential Decree; the Act on the Protection of Children and Youth from Sex Offenses (아동·청소년의 성보호에 관한 법률) provides additional protections against sexual exploitation in employment contexts. The United States regulates child labor under the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) of 1938 (29 U.S.C. Section 212), which establishes a general minimum age of 14 for non-agricultural employment, 16 for non-hazardous work during school hours, and 18 for hazardous occupations, while providing a broad agricultural exemption that has drawn sustained criticism; a disturbing resurgence of child labor violations in 2022-2023, with the DOL finding over 5,800 children employed in violation of federal law in fiscal year 2023, prompted renewed enforcement and several states controversially moved to weaken protections. Germany prohibits employment of children under 15 through the Jugendarbeitsschutzgesetz (JArbSchG, Youth Labor Protection Act) of 1976, permits limited light work from age 13 with parental consent, restricts working hours for adolescents aged 15-17 to 8 hours per day and 40 hours per week, and prohibits hazardous work, night work, and Sunday/holiday work for all minors. At the European level, EU Directive 94/33/EC on the protection of young people at work establishes minimum standards including a prohibition on employment under age 15 (or the end of compulsory schooling), ILO Convention No. 138 on Minimum Age (1973) and Convention No. 182 on Worst Forms of Child Labour (1999) provide the international framework, and the EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD) 2024/1760 requires large companies to identify and address child labor in their supply chains.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Child labor regulation in South Korea is governed by Chapter V of the Labor Standards Act (근로기준법), supplemented by the Enforcement Decree of the Labor Standards Act, which specifies hazardous occupations prohibited for minors.

### Age Requirements and Work Permits

**Under 13:** Employment generally prohibited. The Ministry of Employment and Labor may issue work permits for children aged 13-14 for light work (경미한 근로) in the entertainment industry or other fields that do not interfere with the child's education or health. A work permit (취직인허증) must be obtained from the labor office, with written parental consent and school authorization.

**Ages 15-17:** May work subject to Labor Standards Act protections. Employers must verify age through family relationship certificates and obtain written parental consent.

**Under 18 -- Hazardous Work Prohibition:** Article 65 prohibits the employment of persons under 18 in hazardous or morally dangerous work, including mining, work involving toxic substances, work with heavy machinery, adult entertainment establishments, and other occupations designated by Presidential Decree.

### Working Hour Restrictions

| Category | Daily Maximum | Weekly Maximum | Notes |
|----------|--------------|----------------|-------|
| Ages 13-14 (with permit) | 7 hours | 35 hours | Cannot exceed 40 hours even with consent |
| Ages 15-17 | 7 hours | 35 hours | Extendable to 1 additional hour/day (8 hrs) and 5 additional hours/week (40 hrs) with the minor's consent |
| Night work prohibition | Prohibited 10 PM - 6 AM | -- | Exceptions possible with Ministry approval and minor's consent |

### Entertainment Industry

The entertainment industry -- encompassing K-pop trainee programs, television, film, and advertising -- presents unique child labor challenges in South Korea. The Act on the Promotion of the Motion Pictures and Video Products Industry and the Broadcasting Act regulate minors' participation, with requirements including parental consent, working hour limitations, educational accommodation, and prohibitions on exploitative contract terms. The 2019 amendment to the Popular Culture and Arts Industry Development Act (대중문화예술산업발전법) strengthened protections for trainee minors, including the right to terminate unfair contracts and protections against abusive training practices.

### Child Influencer Regulation

In response to the growing phenomenon of child YouTube and social media influencers, the Ministry of Employment and Labor issued guidelines in 2020 clarifying that commercially motivated content creation by minors constitutes employment subject to Labor Standards Act protections, including age requirements, working hour limitations, and parental consent obligations.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Child labor in the United States is regulated under the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) of 1938, with additional protections provided by state laws. The FLSA applies different standards to agricultural and non-agricultural employment, creating a significant gap in protection for agricultural child workers.

### Non-Agricultural Employment

| Age | Permitted Work | Hour Restrictions |
|-----|---------------|-------------------|
| Under 14 | Limited exceptions (newspaper delivery, family business, acting) | Varies by exception |
| 14-15 | Non-manufacturing, non-mining, non-hazardous work | 3 hrs/school day, 8 hrs/non-school day, 18 hrs/school week, 40 hrs/non-school week; 7 AM - 7 PM (9 PM June-Labor Day) |
| 16-17 | Any non-hazardous work | No federal hour limits |
| 18+ | Any work | Full FLSA protections |

### Agricultural Exemption

The FLSA provides significantly relaxed standards for agricultural employment:
- Age 12+: May work with parental consent or on a farm where parents are employed
- Age 10-11: May work in hand harvesting under specified waiver conditions
- Age 14+: May perform any non-hazardous agricultural work
- Age 16+: May perform hazardous agricultural work
- Family farm exemption: Children of any age may work on their parents' farm

This agricultural exemption has been criticized by the ILO, Human Rights Watch, and numerous child welfare organizations as creating a two-tier system that disproportionately affects Latino and immigrant children.

### 2022-2023 Child Labor Resurgence

The DOL's Wage and Hour Division documented a dramatic increase in child labor violations:

| Fiscal Year | Children Found in Violation | Percent Increase |
|-------------|---------------------------|-----------------|
| 2019 | ~3,800 | -- |
| 2022 | ~3,800 | ~0% |
| 2023 | ~5,800 | ~53% |

High-profile cases included: Packers Sanitation Services Inc. (PSSI) -- 102 children aged 13-17 found working overnight shifts cleaning meatpacking equipment in eight states (February 2023, $1.5 million penalty); Hyundai and Kia supplier companies in Alabama -- children as young as 12 found working in auto parts manufacturing (2022 Reuters investigation); and multiple fast-food franchise violations across the Midwest.

### State-Level Weakening

Controversially, several states moved to weaken child labor protections during the same period:

| State | Year | Reform |
|-------|------|--------|
| Arkansas | 2023 | Eliminated work permit verification requirements for under-16 |
| Iowa | 2023 | Expanded allowable work for 14-15 year-olds; extended working hours |
| New Hampshire | 2024 | Allowed 16-17 year-olds to serve alcohol |
| Missouri | 2023 | Proposed eliminating work hour restrictions for 14-15 year-olds |

These state actions drew criticism from the DOL, child welfare organizations, and the ILO.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's Jugendarbeitsschutzgesetz (JArbSchG), enacted on April 12, 1976, provides comprehensive protections for young workers. The JArbSchG distinguishes between Kinder (children under 15 or those still in compulsory education) and Jugendliche (adolescents aged 15-17 who have completed compulsory education).

### Prohibitions and Exceptions for Children (Under 15)

**General Prohibition (§ 5(1) JArbSchG):** Employment of children is prohibited.

**Limited Exceptions (§ 5(2)-(4)):** Children aged 13 and above may, with parental consent, perform light work (leichte Arbeit) for up to 2 hours per day (3 hours for agricultural work) that does not affect the child's health, development, or school performance. Examples include newspaper delivery, babysitting, garden work, and running errands.

**Performance and Media (§ 6):** Children aged 6+ may participate in theatrical performances, musical events, film/television recordings, and advertising with prior approval from the Gewerbeaufsichtsamt (trade supervisory authority), parental consent, and medical certification.

### Adolescents (15-17) -- Working Conditions

| Provision | Standard |
|-----------|---------|
| Maximum daily hours (§ 8) | 8 hours (8.5 hours if compensated by shorter day elsewhere) |
| Maximum weekly hours (§ 8) | 40 hours, 5-day week |
| Rest breaks (§ 11) | 30 minutes for 4.5-6 hours; 60 minutes for over 6 hours |
| Daily rest period (§ 13) | 12 consecutive hours minimum |
| Night work prohibition (§ 14) | 8 PM to 6 AM (exceptions for bakeries from 5 AM, agriculture from 5 AM, hospitality until 10 PM for 16+) |
| Saturday work (§ 16) | Generally prohibited; exceptions for retail, hospitality, healthcare, agriculture |
| Sunday/holiday work (§ 17-18) | Generally prohibited; limited exceptions for specific sectors |
| Annual leave (§ 19) | 30 working days (under 16), 27 (under 17), 25 (under 18) |

### Hazardous Work Prohibition (§ 22 JArbSchG)

Adolescents may not be employed in work that exceeds their physical or mental capacity, exposes them to moral danger, involves accident risks they cannot recognize or avoid due to insufficient experience, exposes them to harmful effects of heat, cold, noise, vibration, radiation, or hazardous substances, or requires work in dangerous conditions (e.g., mining, construction at height).

### Vocational Training (Berufsausbildung)

Germany's dual vocational training system (Duales Ausbildungssystem) involves adolescents working in firms while attending vocational school. Trainees (Auszubildende) are subject to the JArbSchG's protections, with working time at the firm and vocational school hours combined for the purpose of maximum hour calculations. The Berufsbildungsgesetz (BBiG) governs vocational training contracts, including provisions on training remuneration, leave, and examination requirements.

### Enforcement

The Gewerbeaufsichtsämter (trade supervisory authorities) of each Land enforce the JArbSchG through inspections. Violations carry fines of up to EUR 15,000 for individual offenses and imprisonment of up to one year for repeated or intentional violations. Employers are required to display JArbSchG provisions prominently in the workplace.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Directive 94/33/EC

Adopted on June 22, 1994, Directive 94/33/EC on the protection of young people at work establishes minimum standards across the EU:

**Minimum Age (Article 4):** Member states must prohibit the employment of children (under 15 or the age of compulsory schooling). Light work may be permitted from age 13 (12 for cultural, artistic, sporting, or advertising activities under strict conditions).

**Working Time (Article 8):** Children performing light work: maximum 2 hours on school days, 12 hours per week during school term, 7 hours per day (35 per week) outside school. Adolescents: maximum 8 hours per day, 40 hours per week.

**Night Work (Article 9):** Prohibited for children and adolescents, with the definition varying by age (generally 8 PM-6 AM for children, 10 PM-6 AM or 11 PM-7 AM for adolescents).

**Rest Periods (Article 10):** Minimum 12 consecutive hours daily rest for children; 14 hours for children under 15 performing light work. Minimum 2 consecutive days' weekly rest (including Sunday where possible).

### ILO Conventions

**Convention No. 138 on Minimum Age (1973):** Establishes that the minimum age for employment shall not be less than the age of completion of compulsory schooling and not less than 15 (13 for light work; 18 for hazardous work). Developing countries may set the general minimum at 14. Ratified by 175 states including all three jurisdictions.

**Convention No. 182 on Worst Forms of Child Labour (1999):** Requires immediate and effective measures to secure the prohibition and elimination of the worst forms of child labor, including slavery, forced labor, trafficking, prostitution, pornography, illicit activities (drug production/trafficking), and hazardous work. This Convention achieved universal ratification in 2020 when Tonga became the 187th ILO member state to ratify.

### EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD) 2024/1760

Adopted on July 5, 2024, the CSDDD requires large EU and non-EU companies to identify, prevent, mitigate, and account for adverse human rights impacts -- including child labor -- in their own operations, subsidiaries, and value chains. Companies must conduct human rights due diligence covering ILO core conventions, including Convention 182 on worst forms of child labor. Non-compliance may result in administrative fines of up to 5 percent of worldwide net turnover.

### ECHR

The ECHR addresses child labor through Article 4 (prohibition of slavery and forced labor) and Article 8 (private life). In C.N. v. United Kingdom (Application No. 4239/08, 2012), the Court found that the UK had failed to establish adequate legal provisions to protect against servitude and forced labor, including of minors. The case contributed to the enactment of the UK Modern Slavery Act 2015.

## Comparative Analysis

### Agricultural Exemption

The most significant divergence is the U.S. agricultural exemption, which permits children as young as 12 to perform agricultural work and allows 16-year-olds to perform hazardous agricultural work. Neither South Korea, Germany, nor the EU framework provides comparable agricultural exemptions. This gap has been consistently flagged by the ILO and human rights organizations.

### Enforcement Trends

The 2022-2023 resurgence of child labor violations in the United States, combined with state-level efforts to weaken protections, represents a counter-trend to the general global trajectory of strengthening child labor regulation. Germany and South Korea have maintained or strengthened protections during the same period.

### Digital Age Challenges

All jurisdictions face emerging challenges from child social media influencers, online content creation, and entertainment industry participation. South Korea has been most proactive in extending labor protections to child influencers, consistent with its broader experience regulating minors in the entertainment industry. France enacted specific child influencer legislation in 2020, which may serve as a model for other EU member states.

### Supply Chain Accountability

The EU CSDDD represents the most significant development in combating child labor through supply chain regulation. The U.S. addresses supply chain child labor through customs enforcement (the Tariff Act prohibition on goods made with forced or child labor) and the Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act of 2021, but lacks a comprehensive due diligence mandate comparable to the CSDDD.
