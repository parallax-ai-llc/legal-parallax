## Summary

Patent law grants inventors exclusive rights to their inventions for a limited period in exchange for public disclosure of the invention, creating a balance between incentivizing innovation and ensuring public access to technological knowledge that is calibrated differently across legal systems through variations in patentability requirements, examination procedures, enforcement mechanisms, and exceptions to patent rights. South Korea's patent system is governed by the Patent Act (특허법), administered by the Korean Intellectual Property Office (특허청, KIPO), and provides 20-year patent protection from the filing date for inventions that are novel, involve an inventive step (진보성), and have industrial applicability (산업상 이용가능성); South Korea has become one of the world's most active patent jurisdictions, ranking fourth globally in patent applications with approximately 240,000 applications filed in 2024, and hosts the Patent Court (특허법원) and the Intellectual Property Trial and Appeal Board (특허심판원) as specialized adjudicatory bodies. The United States grants patents under Title 35 of the United States Code, administered by the United States Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO), with patentability requirements of novelty (35 U.S.C. Section 102), non-obviousness (Section 103), utility (Section 101), and adequate written description and enablement (Section 112); the America Invents Act of 2011 transitioned the U.S. from a first-to-invent to a first-inventor-to-file system and established inter partes review as an administrative patent validity challenge mechanism. Germany, operating within the European patent system, provides patent protection through the German Patent Act (Patentgesetz, PatG) and the European Patent Convention (EPC), with the German Federal Patent Court (Bundespatentgericht) handling validity challenges and ordinary courts (particularly the Mannheim, Düsseldorf, and Munich district courts) handling infringement; the Unified Patent Court (UPC), which commenced operations on June 1, 2023, now provides an additional adjudication forum for European patents with unitary effect. At the international level, the Paris Convention for the Protection of Industrial Property (1883), the Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT, 1970), the TRIPS Agreement (1994), and the European Patent Convention (1973, revised 2000) provide the foundational framework for international patent protection.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's patent system has evolved from a developing country framework to one of the world's most sophisticated and active intellectual property systems.

**Patent Act (특허법):** The Act provides for patent protection of inventions defined as "highly advanced creations of technical ideas utilizing the laws of nature" (Article 2). Patentable subject matter includes products, processes, and uses, subject to exclusions for inventions contrary to public order or morality and inventions of medical treatment methods for humans.

### Patentability Requirements

| Requirement | Standard | Key Provisions |
|-------------|----------|---------------|
| Novelty (신규성) | Not identical to prior art available before filing date | Article 29(1); absolute novelty with 12-month grace period |
| Inventive step (진보성) | Not easily derivable from prior art by person skilled in the art | Article 29(2) |
| Industrial applicability (산업상 이용가능성) | Capable of being made or used in industry | Article 29(1) |
| Disclosure (명세서 기재요건) | Specification must enable person skilled in the art to practice invention | Article 42(3)-(4) |

### KIPO Examination

KIPO conducts substantive examination of patent applications, with an average pendency of approximately 15-16 months from request for examination to first action -- among the fastest in the world. The accelerated examination system (우선심사) provides even faster processing for designated technology areas including green technology, national defense, and export-related inventions. KIPO's Patent-KIPO-US Highway (PPH) agreements with over 50 patent offices facilitate mutual recognition of search and examination results.

### IP Trial and Appeal Board (특허심판원)

The Intellectual Property Trial and Appeal Board (IPTAB) adjudicates patent validity challenges (invalidation trials, 무효심판), correction trials, and appeals from KIPO examination decisions. IPTAB decisions are appealable to the Patent Court (특허법원), a specialized court established in 1998, and ultimately to the Supreme Court (대법원).

### Patent Infringement Remedies

Korean courts may grant injunctive relief (Article 126), damages calculated through lost profits, infringer's profits, or reasonable royalty methods (Articles 128-130), and orders for destruction of infringing articles. The 2019 amendment to the Patent Act introduced enhanced damages (up to three times actual damages) for intentional infringement, aligning Korea with the U.S. treble damages framework.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States patent system, authorized by Article I, Section 8, Clause 8 of the Constitution ("to promote the Progress of Science and useful Arts"), provides one of the most extensive and litigated patent frameworks in the world.

**35 U.S.C. -- Patent Act:** The patent statute establishes patentability requirements, prosecution procedures, patent rights, and enforcement mechanisms. The America Invents Act (AIA, Pub. L. 112-29, signed September 16, 2011) represented the most significant reform since the Patent Act of 1952, transitioning from first-to-invent to first-inventor-to-file, creating inter partes review (IPR) and other administrative validity challenges, and eliminating interference proceedings.

### Subject Matter Eligibility (Section 101)

The Supreme Court's decision in Alice Corp. v. CLS Bank International, 573 U.S. 208 (2014), established a two-step test for patent eligibility under Section 101: (1) determine whether the patent claims are directed to a patent-ineligible concept (abstract idea, law of nature, or natural phenomenon); and (2) if so, determine whether the claim elements, individually or as an ordered combination, transform the nature of the claim into a patent-eligible application. The Alice decision has significantly restricted software and business method patents, generating ongoing uncertainty and substantial litigation.

### Inter Partes Review (IPR)

The AIA created the Patent Trial and Appeal Board (PTAB) to conduct inter partes review proceedings, which allow third parties to challenge patent validity on grounds of anticipation or obviousness based on prior art patents and printed publications. IPR has become a major feature of patent litigation strategy, with petitioners prevailing in finding at least some claims unpatentable in approximately 60-65 percent of instituted proceedings.

### Patent Litigation

The United States has the most active patent litigation system in the world, with approximately 4,000-5,000 new patent infringement actions filed annually in federal district courts. The Federal Circuit (U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit) has exclusive appellate jurisdiction over patent cases, providing national uniformity in patent law interpretation.

**Remedies:** Patent holders may obtain injunctive relief (though the Supreme Court's decision in eBay Inc. v. MercExchange, L.L.C., 547 U.S. 388 (2006) replaced the prior presumption of injunction with a four-factor equitable test), compensatory damages (reasonable royalty as a floor), enhanced damages up to three times for willful infringement (35 U.S.C. Section 284), and attorney's fees in exceptional cases (Section 285).

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany operates a dual patent system comprising national patents (Patentgesetz, PatG) and European patents (European Patent Convention, EPC), with the Unified Patent Court adding a third layer since 2023.

**German Patent Act (Patentgesetz):** The PatG provides for national patent protection administered by the Deutsches Patent- und Markenamt (DPMA). Patentability requirements align with the EPC: novelty (Neuheit), inventive step (erfinderische Tätigkeit), industrial applicability (gewerbliche Anwendbarkeit), and sufficient disclosure (Offenbarung).

### Bifurcated System

Germany's distinctive bifurcated system separates infringement and validity proceedings:

**Infringement Courts (Verletzungsgerichte):** Civil courts -- primarily the Landgerichte (District Courts) of Mannheim, Düsseldorf, and Munich -- adjudicate patent infringement claims. These courts may not invalidate patents but may stay infringement proceedings pending the outcome of parallel validity challenges. The Mannheim and Düsseldorf courts have developed particular expertise and attract a disproportionate share of European patent litigation.

**Federal Patent Court (Bundespatentgericht):** Located in Munich, the Bundespatentgericht has exclusive jurisdiction over patent validity challenges (Nichtigkeitsklagen). The Court's technically qualified judges (including judges with scientific or engineering backgrounds) conduct thorough examination of validity issues. Decisions are appealable to the Bundesgerichtshof (Federal Court of Justice).

### Unified Patent Court (UPC)

The UPC, which commenced operations on June 1, 2023, under the Agreement on a Unified Patent Court, provides an integrated forum for both infringement and validity proceedings for European patents with unitary effect (unitary patents) and classical European patents (unless opted out). The UPC comprises a Court of First Instance (with central, local, and regional divisions), a Court of Appeal (Luxembourg), and a Registry. The UPC's jurisdiction extends across all participating EU member states (currently 17, with notable absences including Spain and Poland).

### Patent Infringement Practice

German patent litigation is characterized by rapid proceedings (typically 9-12 months to first instance judgment), relatively low costs compared to U.S. litigation, high injunction rates (German courts have traditionally granted injunctions almost automatically upon finding infringement, though the 2021 amendment to Section 139 PatG introduced a proportionality assessment), and the absence of jury trials and discovery.

### Employee Inventions

The Gesetz über Arbeitnehmererfindungen (Act on Employee Inventions, ArbnErfG) provides a comprehensive framework for inventions made by employees in the course of their employment. The Act requires employees to report service inventions to their employers, gives employers four months to claim the invention, and entitles employees to reasonable compensation (angemessene Vergütung) calculated according to detailed guidelines issued by the Federal Ministry of Justice.

## ECHR & EU

### European Patent Convention (EPC)

The European Patent Convention, signed on October 5, 1973, and substantially revised in 2000 (EPC 2000), established the European Patent Organisation and the European Patent Office (EPO). The EPC provides for centralized patent examination and grant through a single application to the EPO, which, upon grant, produces a bundle of national patents in the designated contracting states.

**Patentability (Articles 52-57):** Article 52(1) provides that European patents shall be granted for inventions in all fields of technology that are new, involve an inventive step, and are susceptible of industrial application. Article 52(2)-(3) excludes from patentability (to the extent they relate to such subject matter "as such") discoveries, scientific theories, mathematical methods, aesthetic creations, computer programs, and presentations of information.

### Unitary Patent and UPC

**Regulation (EU) 1257/2012 (Unitary Patent):** Establishes the European patent with unitary effect, providing uniform protection across participating member states through a single patent granted by the EPO. The unitary patent eliminates the need for individual national validation and renewal, reducing costs and administrative complexity.

**Agreement on a Unified Patent Court:** Establishes the UPC with jurisdiction over infringement and validity of both unitary patents and classical European patents. Key features include a common set of procedural rules, integrated remedies (injunctions, damages, product recall, destruction), a bifurcation option (allowing the court to refer validity counterclaims to the central division while proceeding with infringement), and a transitional opt-out mechanism allowing holders of classical European patents to opt out of UPC jurisdiction.

### TRIPS Agreement

The Agreement on Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights (TRIPS), Annex 1C of the WTO Agreement, establishes minimum standards for patent protection that all WTO members must provide:

| TRIPS Provision | Requirement |
|----------------|-------------|
| Article 27 | Patents available for inventions in all fields of technology; product and process patents required |
| Article 28 | Patent rights: right to prevent making, using, selling, offering for sale, and importing |
| Article 29 | Disclosure requirement: sufficient disclosure to enable practice |
| Article 30 | Limited exceptions: research use, prior user rights |
| Article 31 | Compulsory licensing: permitted under specified conditions |
| Article 33 | Minimum patent term: 20 years from filing date |

### ECHR and Patent Rights

The ECHR has addressed patent-related issues primarily through the lens of Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (property) and Article 6 (fair trial). Patent rights constitute "possessions" within the meaning of Protocol 1, and their revocation or limitation must comply with the proportionality requirements of that provision. In Anheuser-Busch Inc. v. Portugal (Application No. 73049/01, Grand Chamber, 2007), while addressing trademarks, the Court established principles applicable to all intellectual property rights regarding the scope of protection under Protocol 1.

## Comparative Analysis

### Examination Quality and Speed

Patent examination quality and speed vary significantly across jurisdictions. KIPO provides the fastest examination (approximately 15-16 months average) with high technical quality. The USPTO processes applications in approximately 23-24 months on average. The EPO takes approximately 24-27 months. The DPMA processes applications in approximately 24-36 months. These differences in examination speed have practical implications for patent prosecution strategy and timing of commercial exploitation.

### Software and AI Patentability

The patentability of software and AI-related inventions represents one of the most significant areas of divergence. The United States, following Alice, applies the most restrictive standard, invalidating many software patents as directed to "abstract ideas." The EPO and KIPO are more permissive, allowing software patents where the invention makes a "technical contribution" or has "technical character." Germany's national practice aligns with the EPO approach. The increasing importance of AI-generated and AI-assisted inventions presents new challenges that all jurisdictions are actively addressing.

### Infringement Proceedings

The structure of infringement proceedings reveals fundamental differences. The United States provides the most adversarial and expensive litigation process, with broad discovery, jury trials, and the possibility of treble damages. Germany's bifurcated system provides rapid, expert-driven adjudication at lower cost but with the potential for injunctions based on patents later found invalid. South Korea's unified system provides a middle ground, with specialized courts and recent introduction of enhanced damages. The UPC aims to combine the efficiency of the German model with the unity of a single proceeding.

### Injunctive Relief

The availability of injunctive relief has been a major divergence point. Germany traditionally granted injunctions almost automatically upon finding infringement, though the 2021 PatG amendment introduced proportionality analysis. The United States requires a four-factor equitable analysis since eBay (2006), making injunctions less automatic, particularly for non-practicing entities. South Korea generally grants injunctions but considers the equities. The UPC rules provide for injunctions subject to proportionality and the legitimate interests of third parties.
