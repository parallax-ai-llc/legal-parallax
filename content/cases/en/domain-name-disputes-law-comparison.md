## Summary

Domain name disputes (도메인 분쟁) -- conflicts arising from the registration and use of internet domain names that are identical or confusingly similar to trademarks, trade names, or other protected identifiers -- have generated a substantial body of international, national, and administrative law since the commercialization of the internet in the 1990s. South Korea established the Internet Address Dispute Resolution Committee (인터넷주소분쟁조정위원회) under the Internet Address Resources Act (인터넷주소자원에 관한 법률, 2004) to adjudicate disputes over .kr domain names through a mandatory administrative process modeled on ICANN's UDRP; Korean courts have additionally developed significant case law applying the Unfair Competition Prevention Act (부정경쟁방지 및 영업비밀보호에 관한 법률) and the Trademark Act (상표법) to domain name cybersquatting. The United States enacted the Anticybersquatting Consumer Protection Act (ACPA, 15 U.S.C. Section 1125(d)) in 1999, providing trademark holders with a federal cause of action against bad-faith registration of domain names, including in rem jurisdiction against the domain name itself; the ACPA supplements the Lanham Act's trademark protection framework and operates alongside ICANN's Uniform Domain-Name Dispute-Resolution Policy (UDRP). Germany addresses domain name disputes through trademark law (Markengesetz, MarkenG), unfair competition law (Gesetz gegen den unlauteren Wettbewerb, UWG), and the general civil law protection of name rights under Section 12 BGB, with the Bundesgerichtshof (BGH) having developed extensive case law including the foundational shell.de (2001) and weltonline.de (2007) decisions establishing the framework for balancing domain name registration against prior rights. At the international level, ICANN's UDRP, adopted in 1999, provides the primary global mechanism for resolving domain name disputes involving generic top-level domains (gTLDs), with over 60,000 cases filed since its inception, while the WIPO Arbitration and Mediation Center serves as the leading UDRP dispute resolution provider and WIPO has extended its domain name policy recommendations through the Second WIPO Internet Domain Name Process.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's domain name dispute resolution system combines specialized administrative proceedings with judicial remedies under trademark and unfair competition law.

**Internet Address Resources Act (인터넷주소자원에 관한 법률):** Enacted in 2004, this Act provides the legal framework for the management and allocation of internet address resources in Korea, including domain names under the .kr country-code top-level domain. The Act established the Internet Address Dispute Resolution Committee (인터넷주소분쟁조정위원회, IADRC) as the administrative body for resolving .kr domain name disputes.

**Internet Address Dispute Resolution Committee (IADRC):** The IADRC, operating under the Korea Internet & Security Agency (KISA, 한국인터넷진흥원), adjudicates disputes over .kr domain names. The Committee applies standards modeled on ICANN's UDRP, requiring complainants to demonstrate that:
- The domain name is identical or confusingly similar to a trademark, trade name, or other protected identifier in which the complainant has rights.
- The registrant has no legitimate interest in the domain name.
- The domain name was registered or is being used in bad faith.

The IADRC can order transfer or cancellation of the domain name. Its decisions are not binding; either party may file a court action within the prescribed period.

**Unfair Competition Prevention Act (부정경쟁방지 및 영업비밀보호에 관한 법률):** Article 2(1)(a) defines as unfair competition the registration and use of a domain name identical or similar to a well-known mark with the intent to profit, cause harm, or transfer for consideration. This provision, added by amendment in 2004, provides a dedicated statutory basis for cybersquatting claims beyond general trademark infringement.

### Landmark Cases

**Samsung v. Cybersquatter (.kr domain):** The Seoul Central District Court (서울중앙지방법원) ordered transfer of multiple Samsung-related .kr domain names, holding that registration by a party with no connection to the Samsung brand, combined with offers to sell the domains, constituted unfair competition under Article 2(1)(a).

**Naver Corp. v. Domain Registrant:** The IADRC ordered transfer of naver-related domain variations, applying the three-element test and finding bad faith registration based on the registrant's pattern of registering domains incorporating well-known Korean brand names.

### Enforcement Statistics

The IADRC processes approximately 200-300 disputes annually, with complainants prevailing in approximately 70 percent of cases. Korean entities also actively participate in UDRP proceedings for gTLD disputes, with Korean trademark holders filing over 500 UDRP complaints between 2015 and 2024.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States provides both statutory and administrative mechanisms for resolving domain name disputes.

**Anticybersquatting Consumer Protection Act (ACPA, 15 U.S.C. Section 1125(d)):** Enacted on November 29, 1999, the ACPA creates a federal cause of action for the bad-faith registration, trafficking in, or use of a domain name that is identical or confusingly similar to a distinctive or famous mark. The ACPA is codified within the Lanham Act, the primary federal trademark statute.

**Bad Faith Factors (Section 1125(d)(1)(B)):** The ACPA enumerates nine non-exclusive factors for determining bad faith:

| Factor | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| (I) | Registrant's IP rights in the domain name |
| (II) | Whether the domain name is the registrant's legal name or common identifier |
| (III) | Registrant's prior use of the domain in connection with bona fide goods/services |
| (IV) | Registrant's bona fide noncommercial or fair use |
| (V) | Registrant's intent to divert consumers from the mark owner's site |
| (VI) | Registrant's offer to sell the domain to the mark owner for profit |
| (VII) | Registrant's provision of misleading false contact information |
| (VIII) | Registrant's registration of multiple marks as domain names |
| (IX) | Distinctiveness and fame of the mark |

**Remedies:** The ACPA provides for injunctive relief, actual damages or statutory damages of $1,000-$100,000 per domain name (at the court's discretion), and forfeiture, cancellation, or transfer of the domain name.

**In Rem Jurisdiction:** Section 1125(d)(2) provides a unique in rem action against the domain name itself where the registrant is not subject to personal jurisdiction. The action is filed in the judicial district where the domain name registrar or registry is located.

### Landmark Cases

**Sporty's Farm L.L.C. v. Sportsman's Market, Inc. (202 F.3d 489, 2d Cir. 2000):** The first appellate decision under the ACPA, establishing that the statutory bad faith factors are not exhaustive and that courts should consider the totality of circumstances.

**Lamparello v. Falwell (420 F.3d 309, 4th Cir. 2005):** The court held that registration of a domain name for a criticism/commentary website ("fallwell.com" for a site criticizing Jerry Falwell) did not constitute bad faith under the ACPA, protecting First Amendment interests.

**Bosley Medical Institute, Inc. v. Kremer (403 F.3d 672, 9th Cir. 2005):** The court held that a complaint website ("bosleymedical.com" for consumer complaints) was not actionable under the Lanham Act because it did not constitute commercial use of the mark.

### UDRP Usage

The UDRP remains widely used by U.S. trademark holders for gTLD disputes, offering faster and less expensive resolution than federal litigation. However, UDRP decisions are not binding -- either party may file a court action within 10 business days of the decision.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany addresses domain name disputes through a combination of trademark law, unfair competition law, civil name protection, and case law developed by the Bundesgerichtshof.

**Markengesetz (MarkenG, Trademark Act):** Section 14 provides trademark holders with claims against use of identical or confusingly similar signs in commercial transactions. Domain name registrations that constitute trademark use in commercial contexts are actionable under Section 14. Section 15 extends protection to commercial designations (Geschäftsbezeichnungen) and company names.

**BGB Section 12 (Name Protection):** The general civil law protection of personal and business names under Section 12 BGB has proven particularly important for domain name disputes. German courts have held that registering a domain name identical to another person's name constitutes a "name dispute" (Namensstreit) actionable under Section 12, even without commercial use.

**UWG (Unfair Competition Act):** The catch-all provision of Section 3 UWG (prohibition of unfair commercial practices) supplements trademark claims where the domain name registration constitutes unfair competitive behavior.

### Landmark BGH Decisions

**shell.de (BGH, Judgment of November 22, 2001, I ZR 138/99):** A landmark decision in which the BGH addressed the conflict between Shell AG's trademark rights and a private individual named "Shell" who had registered shell.de. The Court held that the individual had a legitimate right to the domain name based on his personal name (Section 12 BGB) but was required to add a distinguishing element to avoid confusion with the well-known Shell trademark. This "priority principle with modification obligation" (Prioritätsprinzip mit Abwandlungspflicht) became a foundational doctrine.

**weltonline.de (BGH, Judgment of September 13, 2007, I ZR 33/05):** The BGH held that the publisher of "Die Welt" newspaper could claim the domain weltonline.de based on its trademark rights, even against a registrant who had registered the domain before the publisher sought it. The decision established that subsequent trademark rights can prevail over prior domain registration where there is bad faith or the registrant lacks legitimate interests.

**ambiente.de (BGH, Judgment of May 19, 2005, I ZR 269/02):** The BGH addressed the conflict between a generic word as a domain name and a trademark for the same word, holding that generic domains do not inherently infringe trademarks where the domain is used for its generic meaning.

### DENIC and .de Domains

DENIC eG, the registry for the .de country-code top-level domain, operates a dispute resolution process through its terms and conditions. Unlike ICANN's UDRP, DENIC's process is not a full administrative adjudication; rather, DENIC will suspend a domain name upon receipt of a court order. Disputes over .de domains are therefore primarily resolved through German courts.

## ECHR & EU

### ICANN Uniform Domain-Name Dispute-Resolution Policy (UDRP)

The UDRP, adopted by ICANN on August 26, 1999, and implemented on December 1, 1999, is the primary global mechanism for resolving domain name disputes involving gTLDs. The UDRP was developed based on recommendations from the WIPO First Internet Domain Name Process (1999).

**Three-Element Test:** A complainant must prove all three elements:
1. The domain name is identical or confusingly similar to a trademark or service mark in which the complainant has rights.
2. The registrant has no rights or legitimate interests in the domain name.
3. The domain name has been registered and is being used in bad faith.

**Bad Faith Indicators (UDRP Paragraph 4(b)):** Registration to sell to the mark owner; registration to prevent the mark owner from using the domain; registration to disrupt a competitor; and registration to attract users through confusion with the mark.

**Dispute Resolution Providers:** WIPO Arbitration and Mediation Center (the leading provider, handling approximately 60 percent of all UDRP cases), National Arbitration Forum (NAF), Asian Domain Name Dispute Resolution Centre (ADNDRC), and Czech Arbitration Court (CAC).

**Statistics:** As of 2025, over 65,000 UDRP cases have been filed with WIPO alone, involving over 120,000 domain names. Complainants prevail in approximately 85 percent of decided cases.

### WIPO Second Internet Domain Name Process

The Second WIPO Internet Domain Name Process (2001) examined domain name issues beyond trademarks, including personal names, geographical indications, international nonproprietary names (INNs) for pharmaceutical substances, and names of international organizations. While its recommendations have not been fully implemented by ICANN, they have influenced ccTLD dispute resolution policies and national legislation.

### EU Trademark Regulation and Domain Names

The EU Trade Mark Regulation (Regulation (EU) 2017/1001) provides EU trademark holders with rights enforceable across all member states, including against domain name registrations. The .eu top-level domain, administered by EURID under Regulation (EU) 2019/517, has its own alternative dispute resolution procedure based on the UDRP model, administered by the Czech Arbitration Court.

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR's involvement in domain name disputes is indirect but relevant through its protection of property rights (Article 1 of Protocol 1) and freedom of expression (Article 10). In Paeffgen GmbH v. Germany (Applications Nos. 25379/04, 21688/05, 21722/05, and 21770/05, Admissibility Decision of September 18, 2007), the Court declared inadmissible claims that German court orders transferring domain names violated property rights, holding that domain name transfers pursuant to trademark law constituted justified interference with property under Article 1 of Protocol 1.

## Comparative Analysis

### Administrative vs. Judicial Resolution

The UDRP provides a streamlined administrative process available globally for gTLD disputes, but its remedies are limited to transfer or cancellation -- no damages are available. The United States complements the UDRP with the ACPA's judicial remedies including substantial statutory damages. South Korea's IADRC provides an administrative process for .kr domains. Germany relies primarily on court litigation, with no dedicated administrative dispute resolution for .de domains.

### Bad Faith Standards

While all systems require bad faith, the standards differ. The ACPA enumerates nine factors; the UDRP identifies four bad faith indicators; Korean law and German case law employ case-by-case analysis without enumerated factors. The UDRP requires both bad faith registration **and** use, while the ACPA requires registration, trafficking, **or** use in bad faith.

### Name Rights vs. Trademark Rights

German law uniquely recognizes domain name disputes based on personal name rights (Section 12 BGB), not merely trademark rights. The shell.de decision's accommodation of both name rights and trademark rights through the modification obligation represents a distinctive approach. The UDRP and ACPA are trademark-centric, though the UDRP's legitimate interest analysis can accommodate some name-based defenses.

### Remedial Options

The United States provides the most extensive remedies, including statutory damages up to $100,000 per domain, actual damages, and in rem jurisdiction. Germany and South Korea provide injunctive relief and compensatory damages through court proceedings. The UDRP provides only transfer or cancellation, making it a complement rather than a substitute for national litigation where damages are sought.
