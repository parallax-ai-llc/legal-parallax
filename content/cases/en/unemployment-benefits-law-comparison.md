## Summary

Unemployment benefits law -- the legal framework providing temporary income support to workers who have lost employment through no fault of their own -- is a cornerstone of social insurance systems that varies dramatically in eligibility criteria, benefit levels, duration, and conditionality across developed economies. South Korea's Employment Insurance Act (고용보험법), enacted in 1993 and effective July 1, 1995, provides job-seeking allowance (구직급여) at 60 percent of the worker's average daily wage (capped and floored) for 120 to 270 days depending on age and contribution period, administered by the Korea Employment Information Service (한국고용정보원) under the Ministry of Employment and Labor; the system was significantly expanded during the COVID-19 pandemic through emergency employment stability subsidies and extended eligibility to artists, special-type workers, and platform workers between 2020 and 2022. The United States operates a federal-state partnership system established by Title III and Title IX of the Social Security Act of 1935 and the Federal Unemployment Tax Act (FUTA, 26 U.S.C. Section 3301), under which 53 separate state programs administer benefits with wide variation in replacement rates (typically 40-50 percent of prior wages), duration (generally 26 weeks, reduced in some states), and eligibility criteria; the CARES Act of 2020 created unprecedented temporary expansions including Pandemic Unemployment Assistance (PUA) for gig workers, the $600/$300 weekly Federal Pandemic Unemployment Compensation (FPUC) supplement, and Pandemic Emergency Unemployment Compensation (PEUC) extending benefits to 79 weeks. Germany provides Arbeitslosengeld I (ALG I) under Sozialgesetzbuch III (SGB III) at 60 percent of net pay (67 percent for claimants with children) for 6 to 24 months depending on age and contribution history, followed by Bürgergeld (formerly Arbeitslosengeld II/Hartz IV) under SGB II as a means-tested basic income support; the 2023 Bürgergeld reform replaced the controversial Hartz IV system with enhanced benefits and reduced sanctions. At the European level, there is no common EU unemployment insurance system, though Regulation (EC) No 883/2004 coordinates benefits for cross-border workers, the European Commission's 2020 SURE instrument provided EUR 98.4 billion in temporary support for short-time work schemes during COVID-19, and proposals for a European Unemployment Reinsurance Scheme (EURS) remain under discussion.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's Employment Insurance system, established by the Employment Insurance Act (고용보험법, Law No. 4644), was enacted on December 27, 1993, and became effective on July 1, 1995. Initially covering workplaces with 30 or more employees, it was progressively expanded and has covered all workplaces with one or more employees since 2004.

### Benefit Structure

**Job-Seeking Allowance (구직급여):**

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Replacement rate | 60% of average daily wage |
| Daily minimum | 64,192 KRW (2025) -- effectively the minimum wage equivalent |
| Daily maximum | 66,000 KRW (2025) |
| Duration | 120-270 days based on age and contribution period |

Duration by age and contribution period:

| Contribution Period | Under 50 | 50+ / Disabled |
|--------------------|----------|---------------|
| Less than 1 year | 120 days | 120 days |
| 1-3 years | 150 days | 180 days |
| 3-5 years | 180 days | 210 days |
| 5-10 years | 210 days | 240 days |
| 10+ years | 240 days | 270 days |

### Eligibility Requirements

To qualify for job-seeking allowance, a worker must have been enrolled in Employment Insurance for at least 180 days during the 18 months prior to separation, have lost employment involuntarily (through dismissal, contract expiration, or constructive dismissal), be capable of and available for work, and be actively seeking employment through the Employment Security Office (고용센터).

### COVID-19 Expansions (2020-2022)

The pandemic prompted the most significant expansion of Korea's unemployment protection system since its establishment:

**Employment Retention Subsidy (고용유지지원금):** The government dramatically expanded subsidies to employers retaining workers through reduced hours or temporary shutdown, modeled partly on Germany's Kurzarbeit system. Subsidies covered up to 90 percent of wages for small businesses.

**Emergency Employment Stability Fund:** Provided cash assistance to workers not covered by Employment Insurance, including freelancers, special-type workers, and informal sector workers.

**Expansion to New Categories:** Employment Insurance coverage was extended to artists and cultural workers (2020), special-type workers including delivery riders and insurance solicitors (2021), and platform workers in designated categories (2022).

### Reform Debates

The Korean system faces ongoing debates about benefit adequacy (the effective replacement rate is often lower than 60 percent due to the cap), coverage gaps for self-employed and irregular workers, and the appropriate balance between benefit generosity and work incentives.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The U.S. unemployment insurance system is a federal-state partnership established by the Social Security Act of 1935 and financed through the Federal Unemployment Tax Act (FUTA). The federal government sets broad parameters and provides administrative funding, while states design and administer their own programs with significant variation.

### Federal Framework

**FUTA (26 U.S.C. § 3301):** Employers pay a federal payroll tax of 6.0 percent on the first $7,000 of each employee's annual wages. Employers in states with conforming programs receive a credit of up to 5.4 percent, resulting in an effective federal rate of 0.6 percent. State payroll taxes are experience-rated, with rates varying based on the employer's layoff history.

### State Variation

| Feature | Range Across States |
|---------|-------------------|
| Weekly benefit amount (max) | $235 (Mississippi) to $1,033 (Massachusetts) |
| Replacement rate | ~27% to ~57% of average wages |
| Maximum duration | 12 weeks (North Carolina, post-reform) to 26 weeks (most states) |
| Base period | Generally first 4 of last 5 completed calendar quarters |
| Waiting period | 0-1 week |

### COVID-19 Programs (CARES Act and Extensions)

| Program | Benefit | Duration | Dates |
|---------|---------|----------|-------|
| FPUC (Federal Pandemic Unemployment Compensation) | Additional $600/week (March-July 2020); $300/week (January-September 2021) | Through September 6, 2021 | March 2020 - September 2021 |
| PUA (Pandemic Unemployment Assistance) | Extended eligibility to gig workers, self-employed, and others normally ineligible | Up to 79 weeks | March 2020 - September 2021 |
| PEUC (Pandemic Emergency Unemployment Compensation) | Extended benefits beyond state maximum | Up to 53 additional weeks | March 2020 - September 2021 |

At peak pandemic unemployment in May 2020, approximately 33 million Americans were receiving some form of unemployment compensation. Total UI expenditures in fiscal years 2020-2021 exceeded $870 billion, compared to approximately $30 billion in a typical year.

### Post-Pandemic Retrenchment

Several states reduced regular benefit duration and tightened eligibility after the pandemic. North Carolina reduced maximum duration from 26 to 12 weeks (subsequently increased to 16). Georgia, Florida, and others shortened duration or imposed stricter work-search requirements.

### Structural Weaknesses

The U.S. system has been criticized for low recipiency rates (the proportion of unemployed workers actually receiving benefits), which fell to approximately 28 percent in 2023 -- meaning approximately 72 percent of unemployed workers receive no unemployment compensation. Contributing factors include restrictive eligibility criteria, exclusion of part-time and gig workers, waiting periods, and administrative burdens.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany operates a two-tier unemployment protection system: Arbeitslosengeld I (ALG I), a contributory insurance benefit under Sozialgesetzbuch III (SGB III), and Bürgergeld (citizens' income), a means-tested basic income support under Sozialgesetzbuch II (SGB II), which replaced Arbeitslosengeld II (Hartz IV) on January 1, 2023.

### Arbeitslosengeld I (ALG I)

**Eligibility:** 12 months of insurable employment during the last 30 months (Anwartschaftszeit). Under the short qualifying period (kurze Anwartschaftszeit), 6 months suffice if predominantly short-term employment contracts were held.

**Benefit Rate:** 60 percent of the claimant's net pay (Leistungsentgelt); 67 percent for claimants with at least one child.

**Duration:** Based on age and contribution history:

| Age | Contribution Period | Duration |
|-----|-------------------|----------|
| All ages | 12 months | 6 months |
| All ages | 16 months | 8 months |
| All ages | 20 months | 10 months |
| All ages | 24 months | 12 months |
| 50+ | 30 months | 15 months |
| 55+ | 36 months | 18 months |
| 58+ | 48 months | 24 months |

### Bürgergeld (Citizens' Income)

The Bürgergeld-Gesetz, enacted on November 25, 2022, and effective January 1, 2023, replaced the controversial Hartz IV system (Arbeitslosengeld II) introduced in 2005. Key changes include:

**Increased Standard Rates (Regelsätze):** The standard rate for a single adult is EUR 563/month (2025), increased through a new mechanism indexing rates to current cost-of-living data rather than historical data with a two-year lag.

**Reduced Sanctions:** The first six months (Karenzzeit) are largely sanction-free to allow claimants to stabilize. Maximum sanctions are capped at 30 percent of the standard rate (reduced from up to 100 percent under Hartz IV). Total elimination of benefits is prohibited following the Federal Constitutional Court's decision in BVerfGE 152, 68 (2019), which held that a human-dignity minimum (menschenwürdiges Existenzminimum) must be maintained.

**Asset Protection:** During the 12-month Karenzzeit, claimants retain up to EUR 40,000 in assets (EUR 15,000 for each additional household member). After the Karenzzeit, the asset threshold is EUR 15,000 per person.

**Housing Costs:** Actual housing costs (Kosten der Unterkunft) are covered in full during the Karenzzeit, without the "appropriateness" (Angemessenheit) test that previously forced claimants to move to cheaper accommodation.

### Kurzarbeitergeld (Short-Time Work Benefits)

Germany's Kurzarbeit system, codified in §§ 95-111 SGB III, allows employers to temporarily reduce working hours during economic downturns while the Bundesagentur für Arbeit partially compensates workers for lost wages. During COVID-19, the system was dramatically expanded: access requirements were relaxed, the replacement rate was increased to 70/77 percent (after four months) and 80/87 percent (after seven months), and employer social insurance contributions were reimbursed. At peak usage in April 2020, approximately 6 million workers were on Kurzarbeit, compared to approximately 50,000 in normal times. The system is widely credited with preventing mass unemployment during the pandemic.

### Federal Constitutional Court -- Hartz IV Decision (2019)

In BVerfGE 152, 68 (November 5, 2019), the Federal Constitutional Court held that complete elimination of benefits (100 percent sanction) under Hartz IV violated the constitutional guarantee of a dignified minimum existence (menschenwürdiges Existenzminimum) derived from Article 1(1) GG (human dignity) in conjunction with the social state principle (Sozialstaatsprinzip, Article 20(1) GG). The Court ruled that sanctions exceeding 30 percent are constitutionally suspect and that total benefit withdrawal is categorically unconstitutional.

## ECHR & EU

### Social Security Coordination (Regulation 883/2004)

EU Regulation 883/2004 coordinates unemployment benefits for cross-border workers. Key rules include: the principle of aggregation (insurance periods completed in different member states are combined for eligibility), the principle that the last state of employment typically pays benefits, and special rules for frontier workers (who may choose to register with either the state of residence or the state of last employment).

### SURE Instrument (2020)

The European instrument for temporary Support to mitigate Unemployment Risks in an Emergency (SURE), adopted on May 19, 2020, under Article 122 TFEU, provided up to EUR 100 billion in loans to member states to finance short-time work schemes and similar measures. By its closure, SURE had disbursed EUR 98.4 billion to 19 member states. The instrument was widely regarded as successful and contributed to discussions about permanent EU-level stabilization mechanisms.

### European Unemployment Reinsurance Scheme (Proposed)

The European Commission has repeatedly proposed a European Unemployment Reinsurance Scheme that would provide temporary financial support to national unemployment systems during severe economic shocks. While included in the Commission's 2020 proposal and supported by the European Parliament, the scheme has faced opposition from fiscal-conservative member states and remains under discussion.

### ECHR

The European Court of Human Rights has addressed unemployment benefits primarily under Article 1 of Protocol 1 (protection of property). In Stec and Others v. United Kingdom (Application Nos. 65731/01 and 65900/01, Grand Chamber, 2006), the Court confirmed that social security benefits, including those funded by contributions, constitute "possessions" within the meaning of Article 1 Protocol 1. Interference with benefit entitlements must therefore satisfy the proportionality test.

### ILO Convention No. 168

ILO Convention No. 168 on Employment Promotion and Protection against Unemployment (1988) establishes minimum standards including coverage of not less than 85 percent of all employees, a replacement rate of at least 50 percent of previous earnings for an initial period, minimum benefit duration, and active labor market policy integration. Only 8 states have ratified the Convention; none of the three jurisdictions examined here has done so.

## Comparative Analysis

### System Architecture

Germany's two-tier system (contributory ALG I followed by means-tested Bürgergeld) provides the most comprehensive safety net, ensuring that no unemployed person falls below a constitutionally guaranteed minimum. South Korea operates a single contributory tier with limited means-tested assistance. The United States operates solely a contributory system with no federal means-tested supplement for those who exhaust benefits, resulting in the largest coverage gap among the three jurisdictions.

### Benefit Adequacy

Germany provides the highest effective replacement (60-67 percent of net pay, with indefinite means-tested support), followed by South Korea (60 percent of average wage, time-limited), and the United States (40-50 percent with significant state variation and relatively short duration). The OECD's net replacement rate calculations consistently rank the U.S. at or near the bottom among member states.

### Short-Time Work

Germany's Kurzarbeit system is the most highly developed and was widely credited with preserving employment during the COVID-19 crisis. South Korea adapted a similar model with its Employment Retention Subsidy. The United States lacked a comparable system, contributing to the dramatic initial spike in unemployment claims (6.9 million in a single week in March 2020).

### Coverage of Non-Standard Workers

All three jurisdictions have confronted the challenge of extending unemployment protection to gig workers, freelancers, and other non-standard workers. South Korea's phased extension of Employment Insurance to special-type and platform workers (2020-2022) represents the most systematic approach. The U.S. PUA provided temporary pandemic-era coverage but expired without a permanent successor. Germany's system remains largely tied to traditional employment relationships.
