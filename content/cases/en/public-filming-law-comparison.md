## Summary

Public filming -- the legal regulation of photography, videography, and live streaming in public spaces, including streets, parks, government buildings, and commercial areas -- raises complex questions at the intersection of freedom of expression, privacy rights, security concerns, and the protection of personal image, with jurisdictions reaching dramatically different conclusions about whether individuals have a reasonable expectation of privacy in public and whether the state may restrict photographic documentation of public life. South Korea regulates public filming primarily through the Act on Special Cases Concerning the Punishment of Sexual Crimes (성폭력범죄의 처벌 등에 관한 특별법) Article 14, which criminalizes the non-consensual filming of another person's body with a sexual purpose, the Personal Information Protection Act (개인정보 보호법, PIPA) provisions on image data, and the Act on the Use and Protection of CCTV (영상정보처리기기의 운영 등에 관한 법률), with the "molka" (몰카) epidemic of hidden camera crimes having driven particularly strict enforcement of non-consensual filming laws. The United States provides the broadest right to photograph in public, grounded in the First Amendment, with federal courts consistently holding that there is no reasonable expectation of privacy in public places, as established in the photography context through cases like Glik v. Cunniffe (2011) recognizing a First Amendment right to film police officers performing their duties in public; restrictions are limited to "time, place, and manner" regulations and specific statutory prohibitions such as voyeurism laws. Germany applies the Kunsturhebergesetz (KUG) Section 22 requirement of consent for the publication of a person's image, the Allgemeines Persönlichkeitsrecht (general personality right) protecting against unwanted photography that intrudes on personal autonomy, and the GDPR's data protection requirements for identifiable images, creating a significantly more restrictive framework than the United States. The European Court of Human Rights has balanced Article 8 (right to respect for private life) against Article 10 (freedom of expression) in public filming contexts, establishing in the Von Hannover cases and Peck v. United Kingdom (2003) that the concept of "private life" extends beyond the home to encompass certain activities in public spaces, particularly where systematic or targeted recording occurs.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's public filming laws have been significantly shaped by the "molka" (몰카) crisis -- the widespread problem of non-consensual hidden camera filming, particularly targeting women in restrooms, public transportation, and changing areas.

**Act on Special Cases Concerning the Punishment of Sexual Crimes (성폭력범죄의 처벌 등에 관한 특별법) Article 14:** Criminalizes the filming of another person's body against their will using a camera or similar device in a manner that could cause sexual shame. The provision also criminalizes the distribution, sale, or possession of such images.

| Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|
| Non-consensual filming of body (Article 14(1)) | Up to 7 years imprisonment or fine up to 50 million KRW |
| Distribution of non-consensually filmed images (Article 14(2)) | Up to 7 years imprisonment or fine up to 50 million KRW |
| Possession/purchase of such images (Article 14(4)) | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine up to 30 million KRW |
| Commercial distribution (Article 14(3)) | Up to 10 years imprisonment or fine up to 100 million KRW |

### Molka Crisis and Legal Response

The hidden camera epidemic prompted massive public protests in 2018, with demonstrations of up to 70,000 participants demanding stronger enforcement. Key responses included:

- Enhanced penalties under the 2018 amendment to the Sexual Crimes Act
- Establishment of dedicated "digital sex crime" investigation units in the National Police Agency
- The "Nth Room" case (2020), involving the production and distribution of sexual exploitation content through Telegram chatrooms, led to further legal reforms including the Act on the Prevention of Distribution of Sexual Exploitation Images
- Mandatory inspection of public restrooms for hidden cameras by local governments

### Personal Information Protection Act (PIPA, 개인정보 보호법)

PIPA, comprehensively amended in 2023, treats identifiable images as personal data. Public CCTV operators must comply with data protection requirements including purpose limitation, notice, retention limits, and security measures. The Personal Information Protection Commission (개인정보보호위원회) enforces these requirements.

### Street Photography

While Korean law does not specifically prohibit street photography, cultural norms and PIPA's broad definition of personal data create a more restrictive environment than in the United States. Identifiable photographs used for commercial purposes require consent under PIPA. Journalistic and artistic use enjoys a narrower exemption than in Western jurisdictions.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States provides the broadest right to photograph in public spaces, grounded in the First Amendment.

**No Expectation of Privacy in Public:** The Supreme Court has established that individuals do not have a reasonable expectation of privacy in public places for purposes of the Fourth Amendment. This principle extends to photography: what is visible to the naked eye in public is generally photographable without restriction.

**Glik v. Cunniffe, 655 F.3d 78 (1st Cir. 2011):** The First Circuit held that there is a "clearly established" First Amendment right to film police officers carrying out their duties in a public space. Simon Glik had been arrested for filming a police encounter on Boston Common with his cell phone. The court held the arrest violated both the First and Fourth Amendments.

**Turner v. Driver, 848 F.3d 678 (5th Cir. 2017):** The Fifth Circuit recognized a First Amendment right to record police activity, extending the principle beyond the First Circuit.

**ACLU v. Alvarez, 679 F.3d 583 (7th Cir. 2012):** The Seventh Circuit held that an Illinois eavesdropping law criminalizing audio recording of police officers in public violated the First Amendment, finding that audio recording is protected speech.

### Statutory Restrictions

Despite the broad right to photograph in public, specific statutory restrictions exist:

**Video Voyeurism Prevention Act (18 U.S.C. Section 1801):** Criminalizes the intentional capture of an image of a private area of an individual without consent in circumstances where the individual has a reasonable expectation of privacy. Applies in federal facilities and to images taken with intent to distribute.

**State Voyeurism Laws:** All 50 states have enacted voyeurism or "Peeping Tom" statutes criminalizing non-consensual recording of individuals in private settings (changing rooms, restrooms, bedrooms).

**Wiretapping/Recording Laws:** State laws governing audio recording vary between "one-party consent" (approximately 38 states) and "all-party consent" (approximately 12 states). All-party consent states require the consent of all parties to a conversation before audio recording.

### Government Photography

Photographing government buildings from public spaces is generally protected by the First Amendment. The Department of Homeland Security has confirmed that there are "no federal statutes that prohibit photographing government buildings from public spaces." However, security measures at specific facilities (military installations, nuclear plants) may restrict photography within controlled areas.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany applies the most restrictive framework for public photography among the jurisdictions compared, reflecting the primacy of personality rights in German law.

**Kunsturhebergesetz (KUG) Section 22:** "Images may be disseminated or publicly displayed only with the consent of the person depicted." The consent requirement applies to publication and distribution, not to the act of taking the photograph itself. Consent is presumed where the person received compensation for being photographed.

**Section 23 KUG (Exceptions):** Publication without consent is permitted for:
1. Images from the domain of contemporary history (zeitgeschichtliche Bilder)
2. Images where persons appear merely as accessories to a landscape or other setting
3. Images of assemblies, processions, or similar events in which the depicted person participated
4. Images that serve a higher interest of art (provided they do not violate the legitimate interests of the person depicted)

### Recht am eigenen Bild (Right to One's Own Image)

The right to one's own image (Recht am eigenen Bild) is a core component of the general personality right. It protects not only against publication but, in certain circumstances, against the act of being photographed:

**BGH, Judgment of May 25, 1954 (Leserbrief):** The Federal Court of Justice established that the personality right includes the right to determine what aspects of one's person are disclosed to the public.

**Photographing in Public:** While taking photographs of people in public spaces is generally not prohibited per se, German courts have held that persistent, targeted, or harassing photography can violate the personality right even without publication. The act of pointing a camera at a specific individual can constitute an intrusion on their personal autonomy.

### GDPR and Photography

The GDPR (Regulation 2016/679) treats identifiable photographs as personal data. Processing (including capture, storage, and publication) requires a legal basis. For public photography, the primary legal bases are:

- **Article 6(1)(f) -- Legitimate interest:** The photographer's interest in creative expression must be balanced against the data subject's rights
- **Article 85 -- Journalistic and artistic expression:** The GDPR requires member states to provide exemptions for journalistic, academic, artistic, and literary purposes

Germany implemented the GDPR photography provisions through the Bundesdatenschutzgesetz (BDSG) Section 23, which preserves the KUG framework for photography as a lex specialis within the GDPR.

### StGB Section 201a -- Violation of Intimate Sphere by Images

Enacted in 2004 and significantly strengthened in 2015, Section 201a criminalizes:
- Producing or transmitting images of a person in their dwelling or a space protected from view without consent (up to 2 years imprisonment)
- Producing or transmitting images that expose the helplessness of another person (up to 2 years)
- Making images accessible to third parties or the public that could significantly damage the reputation of the depicted person (up to 2 years)

### Demonstration Photography

Photographing at demonstrations is protected as both a press freedom and assembly right. However, Section 12a of the Versammlungsgesetz (Assembly Act) prohibits recordings at demonstrations that could be used to identify participants and deter them from exercising their right of assembly. Police body cameras at demonstrations are subject to specific regulations varying by state.

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

**Peck v. United Kingdom (Application No. 44647/98, Judgment of January 28, 2003):** A local council released CCTV footage of the applicant attempting suicide on a public street to the media for crime-prevention publicity purposes. The Court found a violation of Article 8, establishing that even in public spaces, the disclosure of CCTV footage showing a person in a vulnerable state engages the right to private life.

**Von Hannover v. Germany (No. 1) (Application No. 59320/00, Judgment of June 24, 2004):** The Court found that the publication of photographs of Princess Caroline in public settings (restaurants, markets, horse riding) violated Article 8 because they did not contribute to a debate of general interest. The judgment established that public figures retain Article 8 protections in their private activities even when conducted in public.

**Haldimann and Others v. Switzerland (Application No. 21830/09, Judgment of February 24, 2015):** The Court found that Switzerland's conviction of journalists who used a hidden camera to record an insurance broker for a consumer protection program violated Article 10, establishing that hidden camera recordings for journalistic purposes can be protected when they address matters of public interest and the less intrusive means principle is respected.

**López Ribalda and Others v. Spain (Application Nos. 1874/13, 8567/13, Grand Chamber Judgment of October 17, 2019):** The Grand Chamber held that covert video surveillance of employees by an employer did not violate Article 8 where the employer had reasonable suspicion of theft and the surveillance was proportionate. The judgment establishes that privacy expectations are lower in workplace contexts.

### GDPR and Public Photography

The GDPR's application to public photography has generated significant debate across the EU. The Article 29 Working Party (now EDPB) has provided guidance indicating that:

- Casual photography in public spaces for personal/household purposes is exempt under Article 2(2)(c) GDPR
- Street photography for artistic or journalistic purposes benefits from the Article 85 exemption
- Systematic or large-scale photography (e.g., Google Street View) requires GDPR compliance including Data Protection Impact Assessment

### EU Drone Regulation

Regulation (EU) 2019/947 on the rules and procedures for the operation of unmanned aircraft establishes EU-wide rules for drone operations, including filming. Operators must comply with privacy requirements, and flights over assemblies of people are restricted. National data protection authorities provide guidance on drone-based filming and GDPR compliance.

### Facial Recognition and Public Photography

The intersection of public filming and automated facial recognition technology represents the most significant emerging issue across all jurisdictions:

**South Korea:** PIPA regulates biometric data processing, including facial recognition. The Personal Information Protection Commission has issued guidance restricting the use of facial recognition technology in public spaces without explicit consent, except for law enforcement purposes authorized by law.

**United States:** No comprehensive federal regulation of facial recognition exists. Several cities (San Francisco, Portland, Boston) have banned government use of facial recognition. The Illinois Biometric Information Privacy Act (BIPA) requires consent for biometric data collection and has generated substantial litigation. The FTC has taken enforcement action against companies using facial recognition without consent (e.g., Rite Aid, 2023).

**Germany:** The GDPR's special category data protections (Article 9) apply to biometric data, including facial recognition from photographs. The use of automated facial recognition in public spaces by law enforcement is subject to strict proportionality requirements under both the GDPR and police law. The Hamburg data protection authority fined the company Clearview AI for scraping publicly available facial images.

**EU:** The AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689) prohibits real-time remote biometric identification in publicly accessible spaces for law enforcement purposes, with limited exceptions for specific serious offenses. The prohibition reflects European concerns about mass surveillance through facial recognition technology in public spaces.

### Live Streaming and Real-Time Broadcasting

The proliferation of live streaming on platforms (YouTube Live, TikTok Live, Instagram Live, Twitch) has created new challenges:

- **Bystander Privacy:** Live streaming captures bystanders in real-time without the possibility of pre-publication consent or blurring
- **Crime Documentation:** Live streams have documented crimes in progress, raising questions about filming obligations and obstruction
- **Emergency Situations:** First responder operations may be complicated by live-streaming bystanders
- **Monetization:** Commercial live streams in public spaces may trigger different regulatory considerations than casual recording

No jurisdiction has enacted specific live-streaming regulation, but existing frameworks (PIPA in South Korea, GDPR in Germany, state privacy laws in the U.S.) apply to the extent that live streams capture identifiable personal information.

## Comparative Analysis

### Right vs. Privacy Spectrum

The jurisdictions represent a clear spectrum from broad filming rights (United States) to strong privacy protection (Germany), with South Korea and the ECHR occupying intermediate positions shaped by specific cultural and legal concerns. The United States treats the right to photograph in public as essentially unrestricted, viewing privacy in public as a contradiction in terms. Germany extends personality right protection into public spaces, treating the right to one's own image as a core aspect of human dignity. South Korea's framework is primarily driven by the specific problem of sexual voyeurism rather than a general privacy philosophy. The ECHR balances case by case, considering the purpose, context, and impact of photography.

### Voyeurism and Hidden Cameras

All four jurisdictions criminalize voyeuristic photography and hidden cameras, but the intensity of enforcement and the driving concerns differ. South Korea's strict penalties reflect the molka crisis -- a specific social phenomenon that made hidden camera filming a major public safety and gender equality issue. The United States relies on a patchwork of state voyeurism statutes. Germany's Section 201a StGB covers the intimate sphere. The ECHR addresses the issue through Article 8 positive obligations.

### Police Filming

The right to film police in public has been most firmly established in the United States, where multiple circuit courts have recognized a First Amendment right to film officers performing public duties. Germany generally permits filming police but with Assembly Act restrictions at demonstrations. South Korea has no specific right to film police. The ECHR has not directly addressed the right to film police but the Article 10 framework supports documentation of police conduct as journalistic and watchdog activity.

### Digital and Drone Challenges

All jurisdictions face evolving challenges from drones, live streaming, facial recognition, and AI-enhanced image analysis. The EU's comprehensive drone regulations and GDPR framework provide the most detailed response. The United States relies primarily on FAA regulations with limited privacy provisions. South Korea applies PIPA to drone-captured personal data. The rapid expansion of real-time facial recognition technology in public spaces represents the next frontier of public filming regulation.
