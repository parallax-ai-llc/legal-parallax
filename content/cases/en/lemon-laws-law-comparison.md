## Summary

Lemon laws -- consumer protection statutes that provide remedies to purchasers of vehicles and other products that fail to meet quality and performance standards after repeated repair attempts -- represent a targeted response to one of the most common and economically significant consumer grievances: the purchase of a defective automobile. South Korea addresses defective vehicle complaints through the Automobile Management Act (자동차관리법) and the Framework Act on Consumers (소비자기본법), supplemented by the Korea Consumer Agency's (한국소비자원) automobile dispute resolution mechanism and the Ministry of Land, Infrastructure and Transport's recall authority; while South Korea does not have a standalone "lemon law" statute, the 2019 amendment to the Motor Vehicle Management Act (결함차 반환법) introduced a vehicle exchange and refund system (자동차 교환·환불 제도) for new vehicles with serious defects that cannot be repaired after multiple attempts, colloquially known as the "Korean Lemon Law," which took effect on January 1, 2019. The United States has the most developed lemon law framework in the world, with all 50 states and the District of Columbia having enacted specific lemon law statutes (beginning with Connecticut in 1982), typically requiring the manufacturer to replace or refund the purchase price of a new vehicle that cannot be repaired after a reasonable number of attempts, supplemented by the Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act (15 U.S.C. §§2301-2312) at the federal level. Germany addresses defective vehicles primarily through the BGB's sales law provisions on warranty (Gewährleistung, Sections 434-442), the Kaufrecht reforms of the 2002 Schuldrechtsmodernisierung, and the specific right to withdraw from the contract (Rücktritt) after failed repair attempts, providing a robust remedial framework without a dedicated lemon law statute. At the European level, the Consumer Sales Directive (EU) 2019/771 on the sale of goods establishes a harmonized two-year conformity guarantee across all member states, requiring sellers to repair or replace non-conforming goods and permitting price reduction or contract termination if repair or replacement is impossible or disproportionate, while the CJEU's jurisprudence in Weber and Putz (C-65/09 and C-87/09, 2011) and Quelle AG v. Bundesverband (C-404/06, 2008) has clarified the scope of consumer remedies for defective goods.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's "lemon law" system was introduced through amendments to the Motor Vehicle Management Act (자동차관리법), effective January 1, 2019. The system is commonly referred to as the 결함차 반환법 (defective vehicle return law) or Korean Lemon Law.

### Vehicle Exchange and Refund System (자동차 교환·환불 제도)

**Eligibility:** New vehicles within the first year of purchase or within 20,000 km (whichever comes first).

**Qualifying Defects:**
| Category | Criteria |
|----------|---------|
| Safety-critical defects | Defects affecting braking, steering, or fuel system that cause sudden failure |
| Repeated repair failures | Same defect repaired 3 or more times without resolution |
| Cumulative repair days | Vehicle in repair for 30 or more cumulative days |
| Different defects | 2 or more different defects, each repaired 2 or more times |

**Remedies:** The consumer may demand either:
- Vehicle exchange: Replacement with a new vehicle of the same model and specifications
- Refund: Return of the purchase price minus a deduction for usage (calculated by mileage and depreciation)

### Dispute Resolution

When manufacturer and consumer disagree, the dispute is referred to the Automobile Dispute Settlement Committee (자동차분쟁심의위원회), established under the Motor Vehicle Management Act. The Committee includes technical experts, consumer advocates, and industry representatives. Decisions are advisory but carry significant practical weight.

The Korean Consumer Agency (한국소비자원) also handles vehicle complaints through its general consumer dispute mediation process. Vehicle complaints consistently rank among the top complaint categories, with approximately 8,000 automobile-related complaints filed annually.

### Recall System

The Ministry of Land, Infrastructure and Transport (국토교통부) administers the vehicle recall system. Manufacturers must report known defects and implement recalls. The Korea Transportation Safety Authority (한국교통안전공단) conducts defect investigations. In 2024, approximately 7.2 million vehicles were subject to recall notices, a figure that includes both manufacturer-initiated and government-ordered recalls.

### Landmark Case: BMW Engine Fire Crisis (2018)

The 2018 BMW engine fire crisis was a catalyzing event for Korean lemon law reform. Over 40 BMW vehicles (primarily the 520d model) caught fire due to defective EGR (exhaust gas recirculation) valves. The incidents caused widespread public alarm and led to a government ban on unrecalled BMW vehicles from road use -- an unprecedented measure. BMW Korea was fined 11.2 billion KRW, and the crisis accelerated implementation of the vehicle exchange and refund system.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has the most developed and varied lemon law framework in the world. Connecticut enacted the first modern lemon law in 1982, and by 1993 all 50 states and the District of Columbia had enacted lemon law statutes.

### Typical State Lemon Law Structure

| Element | Common Provision |
|---------|-----------------|
| Coverage | New motor vehicles purchased or leased in the state |
| Lemon Period | 1-2 years from delivery or 12,000-24,000 miles |
| Repair Attempts | 3-4 attempts for same nonconformity |
| Days Out of Service | 30 cumulative business days |
| Manufacturer's Duty | Replace vehicle or refund purchase price |
| Usage Deduction | Mileage-based formula for refund offset |
| Arbitration | Many states require participation in manufacturer arbitration before litigation |
| Attorney's Fees | Available to prevailing consumer in most states |

### State Variations

**California (Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act, Cal. Civ. Code §§1790-1795.8):** One of the strongest lemon laws. Covers new and used vehicles with manufacturer warranties. Provides for civil penalty of up to two times actual damages for willful violations. Does not require arbitration before litigation. The "Tanner Consumer Protection Act" (Cal. Civ. Code §1793.22) creates a presumption that reasonable repair attempts have been made after 4 repair attempts for the same problem or 30 days out of service within the first 18 months or 18,000 miles.

**New York (New Car Lemon Law, General Business Law §198-a):** Covers new cars within 2 years or 18,000 miles. Requires 4 repair attempts or 30 days out of service. Manufacturer must offer arbitration through the New York New Car Arbitration Program.

**Texas (Lemon Law, Tex. Occ. Code §§2301.601-2301.613):** Administered by the Texas Department of Motor Vehicles. Covers new vehicles within 24 months or 24,000 miles. Requires 4 repair attempts, 2 attempts for serious safety defects, or 30 days out of service. No requirement for prior arbitration.

### Federal Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act

The Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act (15 U.S.C. §§2301-2312) provides a federal layer of warranty protection applicable to consumer products including vehicles. Key features:
- Requires written warranties to be designated as "full" or "limited"
- Full warranties must provide repair or replacement within a reasonable time at no cost; if repair fails, consumer may elect refund or replacement
- Permits consumer class actions for warranty violations
- Provides attorney's fees to prevailing consumers
- Does not preempt state lemon laws; consumers may pursue both federal and state remedies

### Used Vehicle Lemon Laws

Several states have enacted lemon laws covering used vehicles, though protections are generally weaker:
- **New York:** Used Car Lemon Law (GBL §198-b) covers used vehicles with 18,000-100,000 miles
- **Massachusetts:** Covers used vehicles under 125,000 miles
- **Connecticut:** Covers used vehicles from licensed dealers

### Lemon Law Buyback Branding

When a vehicle is repurchased under a lemon law, most states require it to be branded with a "lemon law buyback" title notation before resale. This protects subsequent purchasers by providing notice of the vehicle's defective history.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany does not have a dedicated lemon law statute. Instead, defective vehicles are addressed through the BGB's general sales law provisions (Kaufrecht), which were substantially reformed by the Schuldrechtsmodernisierung (2002) to implement the EU Consumer Sales Directive.

### BGB Warranty (Gewährleistung) Framework

**Section 434 BGB (Material Defect):** A thing is free from material defects if, upon the passing of risk, it has the agreed quality, is fit for the purpose stipulated in the contract, and is fit for ordinary use and has a quality usual for things of the same type and which the buyer may expect.

**Section 437 BGB (Buyer's Rights in Case of Defects):** The buyer may demand:
1. Supplementary performance (Nacherfüllung, §439) -- repair or replacement
2. Withdrawal from contract (Rücktritt, §440, 323) -- after failed supplementary performance
3. Reduction of purchase price (Minderung, §441)
4. Damages (Schadensersatz, §440, 280, 281)

### Remedial Hierarchy

German law establishes a strict remedial hierarchy for defective vehicles:

**First: Nacherfüllung (Supplementary Performance):** The buyer must first give the seller the opportunity to cure the defect through repair or delivery of a defect-free replacement (§439). The seller has the right to choose between repair and replacement, unless the chosen method is disproportionate (§439(4)).

**Second: Rücktritt or Minderung:** If Nacherfüllung fails -- defined by case law as generally two unsuccessful repair attempts for the same defect -- the buyer may withdraw from the contract (Rücktritt) or reduce the purchase price (Minderung). The buyer must set a reasonable Nachfrist (additional period) for performance before exercising these secondary remedies (§323).

**Compensation for Use (Nutzungsentschädigung):** Upon withdrawal, the buyer must compensate the seller for use of the vehicle, calculated by a formula considering the purchase price and expected lifespan of the vehicle.

### BGH Jurisprudence on Vehicle Defects

The Bundesgerichtshof (BGH) has developed extensive case law on vehicle warranty:

**BGH, VIII ZR 94/13 (2015):** Held that a vehicle with a defective diesel particulate filter that cannot be repaired after two attempts entitles the buyer to withdraw from the contract.

**BGH, VIII ZR 234/15 (2016):** Established that in cases of fraudulent concealment of defects by the seller, the buyer may withdraw immediately without granting a Nachfrist.

**Diesel Emissions Scandal Cases:** The VW Dieselgate scandal (2015) generated thousands of German court cases. The BGH held in VIII ZR 225/17 (2020) that VW's use of illegal defeat devices constituted deliberate unethical conduct (sittenwidrige vorsätzliche Schädigung) under Section 826 BGB, entitling buyers to damages without the usual Gewährleistung framework.

### Reversed Burden of Proof

Section 477 BGB (implementing EU Directive 2019/771) provides that if a defect manifests within one year of delivery, it is presumed to have existed at the time of delivery. This was extended from six months to one year by the 2022 reform implementing the updated EU Consumer Sales Directive.

### Limitation Period

Warranty claims for new vehicles are subject to a two-year limitation period from delivery (Section 438(1)(3) BGB). For used vehicles, the parties may reduce this to one year by agreement. Many manufacturers provide voluntary manufacturer warranties (Herstellergarantie) extending beyond the statutory period.

## ECHR & EU

### Consumer Sales Directive (EU) 2019/771

Directive (EU) 2019/771 on certain aspects concerning contracts for the sale of goods, applicable from January 1, 2022, replaced the earlier Directive 1999/44/EC and provides the foundational EU framework for defective goods remedies, including vehicles.

**Conformity Guarantee (Articles 5-7):** The seller is liable for any lack of conformity existing at the time of delivery and which becomes apparent within two years of delivery. Conformity requirements include: compliance with the contract description, fitness for purpose, possession of qualities of goods of the same type, and delivery with all accessories and instructions.

**Remedial Hierarchy (Articles 13-16):**
1. Repair or replacement (consumer's choice, unless disproportionate)
2. Price reduction or contract termination (if repair/replacement impossible, disproportionate, not provided in reasonable time, or would cause significant inconvenience)

**Reversed Burden of Proof (Article 11):** Any lack of conformity becoming apparent within one year of delivery is presumed to have existed at the time of delivery. Member states may extend this to two years.

### CJEU Jurisprudence

**Quelle AG v. Bundesverband der Verbraucherzentralen (C-404/06, 2008):** The Court held that where a consumer good is replaced under the conformity guarantee, the seller cannot charge the consumer for use of the original defective good during the period before replacement. This decision was significant for vehicle warranty because it eliminated the usage compensation that German law previously required.

**Weber and Putz (Joined Cases C-65/09 and C-87/09, 2011):** The Court held that the seller must bear all costs of replacement, including the costs of removing the defective good and installing the replacement. The seller cannot refuse replacement solely because of disproportionate cost; instead, the seller may contribute an appropriate amount.

**Faber v. Autobedrijf Hazet Ochten (C-497/13, 2015):** The Court held that the reversed burden of proof (defect presumed pre-existing if manifesting within six months) applies broadly and that the consumer need only prove the existence of the lack of conformity, not its cause.

### General Product Safety Regulation and Vehicle Type-Approval

The EU vehicle type-approval framework (Regulation (EU) 2018/858) establishes safety and environmental standards for vehicles sold in the EU. Following the Dieselgate scandal, the regulation was strengthened to include enhanced market surveillance, independent testing, and penalties for non-compliance. This framework complements the consumer sales remedy by addressing systemic vehicle defects at the regulatory level.

## Comparative Analysis

### Dedicated vs. General Framework

The United States is the only jurisdiction with dedicated lemon law statutes specifically designed for defective vehicles. South Korea introduced a vehicle-specific exchange and refund system in 2019 that functions as a lemon law within the Motor Vehicle Management Act. Germany and the EU rely on general consumer sales law to address vehicle defects, without vehicle-specific legislation. The general framework approach has the advantage of covering all consumer goods uniformly but may not adequately address the unique characteristics of automobile purchases.

### Repair Opportunity Thresholds

The number of repair attempts required before triggering replacement or refund rights varies:
- United States: Typically 3-4 attempts (varies by state)
- South Korea: 3 attempts for same defect
- Germany: Generally 2 attempts (BGH case law)
- EU Directive: "Reasonable time" without specifying a number

Germany's lower threshold is more consumer-friendly in this specific dimension, though the overall process (including Nachfrist requirements) may be more procedurally complex.

### Usage Deduction

A critical practical issue is whether the manufacturer can deduct for the consumer's use of the defective vehicle before return:
- United States: Yes (mileage-based formula in all state lemon laws)
- South Korea: Yes (mileage and depreciation formula)
- Germany: Initially yes, but the CJEU's Quelle decision prohibited usage charges for replacement; for Rücktritt, BGH still permits usage compensation
- EU: CJEU prohibits usage charges for replacement; termination compensation varies

### Arbitration Requirements

Many U.S. states require the consumer to participate in manufacturer-sponsored arbitration before pursuing litigation. This requirement is intended to promote efficient dispute resolution but has been criticized as favoring manufacturers. South Korea's dispute settlement committee serves a similar function. Germany and the EU do not generally require arbitration, though voluntary arbitration boards exist.
