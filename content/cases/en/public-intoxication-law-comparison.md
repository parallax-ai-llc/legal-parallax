## Summary

Public intoxication -- the state of being visibly impaired by alcohol or other substances in a public place -- occupies a contested position between criminal law and public health policy, with jurisdictions increasingly debating whether criminalizing the condition of being intoxicated in public is an effective or just approach to addressing alcohol-related harm. South Korea, which has one of the highest rates of alcohol consumption per capita in the world, addresses public intoxication through the Minor Offenses Act (경범죄처벌법), which classifies "drunken misbehavior" (음주소란) as a minor offense punishable by a fine of up to 600,000 KRW or detention of up to 30 days, while the Act on the Prevention of Alcohol-Related Harm (국민건강증진법 and the 2018 주류에 의한 피해 예방 등에 관한 법률) takes a public health approach; Korean law has grappled with the unique issue of alcohol-related violence through the controversial "alcohol blackout defense" (음주감경), which was effectively curtailed by the 2018 amendment to Article 10(3) of the Criminal Act (형법) following the Yoon Chang-ho case. The United States treats public intoxication inconsistently across jurisdictions, with some states criminalizing it as a misdemeanor while others have decriminalized it following the landmark case of Powell v. Texas, 392 U.S. 514 (1968), in which the Supreme Court narrowly declined to extend the Eighth Amendment's prohibition on cruel and unusual punishment to public intoxication statutes, and Robinson v. California, 370 U.S. 660 (1962), which held that criminalizing the status of being a drug addict violates the Eighth Amendment; a growing number of states have adopted the Uniform Alcoholism and Intoxication Treatment Act, which treats public intoxication as a public health matter rather than a criminal offense. Germany does not criminalize simple public intoxication but addresses alcohol-related disturbances through general public order provisions and the Ordnungswidrigkeitengesetz, while Section 323a StGB (Vollrausch, full intoxication) creates a unique offense of placing oneself in a state of intoxication and then committing an unlawful act while incapable of criminal responsibility, punishable by up to five years imprisonment. The ECHR has addressed the intersection of intoxication and criminal responsibility through Article 5 (right to liberty), with the Court recognizing in Witold Litwa v. Poland (Application No. 26629/95, 2000) that the detention of intoxicated persons under Article 5(1)(e) requires procedural safeguards and must be proportionate.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

**Minor Offenses Act (경범죄처벌법), Article 3, Paragraph 1, No. 21 -- Drunken Misbehavior (음주소란):** Classifies as a minor offense the act of causing a disturbance through drunken behavior in a public place. The offense requires not merely being intoxicated but engaging in disorderly conduct while intoxicated.

**Police Protection Measures (보호조치):** Under the Police Officers' Duties Act (경찰관 직무집행법), Article 4, police officers may take protective measures for persons who are intoxicated to the degree that they pose a danger to themselves or others. This includes temporary detention for up to 12 hours in a "protection room" (보호실) at a police station.

### The Alcohol Blackout Defense (음주감경) Controversy

The most significant Korean legal development concerning public intoxication relates to the "alcohol blackout defense" -- the use of voluntary intoxication to argue for reduced criminal responsibility:

**Article 10 of the Criminal Act:**
- Paragraph 1: A person whose mental capacity is impaired and who lacks the ability to distinguish right from wrong or to make decisions shall not be punished.
- Paragraph 2: A person whose above capacity is diminished shall have their punishment reduced.
- Paragraph 3 (added 2018): "The provisions of paragraphs 1 and 2 shall not apply to a person who foresees the commission of a crime and brings about a state of mental impairment by his own volition through the consumption of alcohol or drugs."

### The Yoon Chang-ho Case (2018)

The amendment to Article 10(3) was prompted by the case of Yoon Chang-ho, a college student killed in a drunk driving accident in Busan on September 25, 2018. The driver, who had a blood alcohol content of 0.171%, received what was widely perceived as a lenient sentence, generating a public petition with over 300,000 signatures demanding reform. The resulting amendment, commonly known as the "Yoon Chang-ho Law," addressed the broader practice of using voluntary intoxication as a defense:

**Before Amendment:** Defendants could argue that voluntary intoxication reduced their mental capacity, potentially receiving lighter sentences even for the most serious crimes.

**After Amendment:** Voluntary intoxication through alcohol or drugs cannot be used to argue diminished capacity if the person foresaw the possibility of committing a crime.

### Alcohol Consumption Culture

South Korea's alcohol consumption patterns provide important context:
- South Korea ranks among the highest in OECD countries for alcohol consumption per capita
- Soju is the world's best-selling spirit brand, with over 90 million cases sold annually
- The drinking culture (회식, hweshik -- company drinking gatherings) has been identified as contributing to alcohol-related harm
- The Act on the Prevention of Alcohol-Related Harm (주류에 의한 피해 예방 등에 관한 법률, 2018) established a comprehensive framework for addressing alcohol harm through education, treatment, and restrictions on advertising

## United States

### Legal Framework

Public intoxication laws in the United States vary dramatically by jurisdiction:

**Criminalization States:** Approximately 31 states maintain criminal penalties for public intoxication, typically as a misdemeanor or infraction.

**Decriminalization States:** Approximately 19 states and the District of Columbia have decriminalized public intoxication, treating it as a public health matter rather than a criminal offense.

### Constitutional Framework

**Robinson v. California, 370 U.S. 660 (1962):** The Supreme Court held that a California statute making it a criminal offense to "be addicted to the use of narcotics" violated the Eighth Amendment's prohibition on cruel and unusual punishment. The Court ruled that criminalizing a status or condition, rather than an act, constitutes cruel and unusual punishment.

**Powell v. Texas, 392 U.S. 514 (1968):** The Court declined to extend Robinson to public intoxication. In a 5-4 decision, the plurality (Justice Marshall) held that the defendant was not being punished for the "status" of being an alcoholic but for the "act" of appearing in public while intoxicated. However, Justice White's concurrence, which provided the fifth vote, narrowly limited the holding and left open the possibility that prosecuting a homeless alcoholic who had no choice but to be in public might violate the Eighth Amendment.

### Uniform Alcoholism and Intoxication Treatment Act (1971)

The National Conference of Commissioners on Uniform State Laws adopted this act to promote the decriminalization of public intoxication:

**Key Provisions:**
- Section 12: "No county, municipality, or other political subdivision may adopt or enforce a local law, ordinance, resolution, or regulation having the force of law that includes drinking, being a common drunkard, or being found in an intoxicated condition as one of the elements of the offense..."
- The Act establishes a system of detoxification centers, treatment facilities, and aftercare programs as alternatives to criminal prosecution

Approximately 35 states have adopted some version of this Act.

### State Approaches

| Approach | States | Key Features |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| Criminal offense | Texas, Florida, Virginia, others | Misdemeanor; fine and/or jail time |
| Decriminalized | Alaska, Minnesota, Connecticut, others | Civil protective custody; referral to treatment |
| No specific statute | Nevada, Missouri, Montana | General disorderly conduct may apply |
| Civil infraction | Various | Fine only; no criminal record |

### Protective Custody

Most states authorize police to take intoxicated persons into protective custody without criminal arrest:

**Typical Protective Custody Provisions:**
- Duration: 12-24 hours or until sober
- No criminal charge filed
- Person may be transported to a detoxification facility
- No right to refuse custody if danger to self or others

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany does not criminalize simple public intoxication. Being drunk in public, without more, is neither a criminal offense nor an administrative infraction.

### Section 323a StGB -- Full Intoxication (Vollrausch)

Germany's most distinctive provision on intoxication and criminal law is Section 323a:

"(1) Whoever intentionally or negligently places themselves in a state of intoxication through the consumption of alcoholic beverages or other intoxicating substances shall be punished with imprisonment for a term not exceeding five years or a fine if they commit an unlawful act while in this condition and cannot be punished for the act because they lacked criminal responsibility due to the intoxication or because this cannot be excluded."

**Purpose:** Section 323a addresses a gap in German criminal law created by the combination of: (1) the principle that persons without criminal responsibility (Section 20 StGB) cannot be punished for their acts, and (2) the recognition that persons who are profoundly intoxicated may lack criminal responsibility. Without Section 323a, persons who committed serious crimes while in a state of self-induced profound intoxication could escape all punishment.

**Elements:**
- The offender intentionally or negligently became profoundly intoxicated
- While intoxicated, the offender committed an unlawful act
- The offender cannot be punished for the unlawful act because of intoxication-induced lack of criminal responsibility
- The penalty for Vollrausch may not exceed the maximum penalty for the underlying unlawful act

### Intoxication and Criminal Responsibility

**Section 20 StGB -- Incapacity Due to Mental Disorder (Schuldunfähigkeit):** A person who, at the time of the act, is incapable of appreciating the wrongfulness of the act or of acting in accordance with that appreciation due to a pathological mental disorder, a profound consciousness disorder, mental deficiency, or any other serious mental abnormality, acts without guilt. Profound intoxication may constitute a "profound consciousness disorder."

**Section 21 StGB -- Diminished Capacity (Verminderte Schuldfähigkeit):** If the capacity described in Section 20 is "substantially diminished" at the time of the act, the sentence may be mitigated under Section 49(1). This provision is frequently applied in cases involving significant but not complete intoxication.

### Actio Libera in Causa

German courts apply the doctrine of actio libera in causa (act free in its cause) to hold defendants fully responsible when they intentionally become intoxicated in order to commit a crime or while foreseeing the commission of a crime:

**BGH, Judgment of September 22, 2004 (5 StR 296/04):** The Court reaffirmed the actio libera in causa doctrine, holding that a defendant who consumed alcohol with the knowledge that they become violent when intoxicated could be held fully responsible for subsequent violent acts.

### Public Order Provisions

Public intoxication-related disturbances are addressed through:
- **OWiG provisions:** Minor disturbances (noise, nuisance) may result in administrative fines
- **Section 118 OWiG -- Grober Unfug (Gross Misconduct):** Fines for conduct that disturbs public order
- **Police law (Polizeirecht):** Officers may take intoxicated persons into protective custody (Gewahrsam) if they pose a danger

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

**Article 5(1)(e) -- Detention of "Alcoholics":** The ECHR permits the lawful detention of "alcoholics" as one of the specified grounds for deprivation of liberty. However, the Court has imposed significant safeguards:

**Witold Litwa v. Poland (Application No. 26629/95, Judgment of March 21, 2000):** The Court established that "alcoholics" in Article 5(1)(e) is not limited to persons in a clinical state of alcoholism but extends to persons whose behavior under the influence of alcohol poses a threat to public order or to themselves. However, the detention must be proportionate and take place in an appropriate facility -- detention in a "sobering-up centre" rather than a police cell is preferred.

**Hilda Hafsteinsdóttir v. Iceland (Application No. 40905/98, Judgment of June 8, 2004):** The Court found a violation of Article 5(1) where the applicant was detained at a police station for public intoxication without adequate procedural safeguards, emphasizing that detention must be in an appropriate facility and for no longer than necessary.

**Kharin v. Russia (Application No. 37345/03, Judgment of February 3, 2011):** The Court found a violation of Article 5 where the applicant was detained in a sobering-up center for public intoxication, holding that the conditions and duration of detention were disproportionate.

### EU Framework

**EU Alcohol Strategy (2006):** The European Commission's Communication on an EU strategy to support Member States in reducing alcohol-related harm emphasized public health approaches over criminalization.

**Council Conclusions on Alcohol and Health (2017):** Called for member states to implement evidence-based policies including treatment-oriented approaches to alcohol-related public order issues.

**EU Drug Strategy 2021-2025:** While focused on drugs, the strategy's emphasis on health-oriented approaches has influenced alcohol policy discussions.

## Comparative Analysis

### Criminal vs. Public Health Model

The jurisdictions reflect a spectrum from criminalization to medicalization:

- **South Korea:** Hybrid -- criminal sanctions for disruptive behavior, public health measures for prevention and treatment
- **United States:** Mixed -- criminal in ~31 states, public health in ~19 states; trend toward decriminalization
- **Germany:** Predominantly public health -- no criminalization of simple intoxication; Vollrausch addresses only cases involving unlawful acts
- **ECHR/EU:** Strong preference for public health approach; detention only with safeguards

### Intoxication as Defense or Mitigation

The treatment of voluntary intoxication as a criminal defense varies dramatically:

- **South Korea (post-2018):** Cannot reduce liability if crime was foreseeable
- **United States:** Voluntary intoxication not a defense to general intent crimes; may negate specific intent in some jurisdictions
- **Germany:** May reduce or eliminate liability (Sections 20-21); actio libera in causa prevents abuse; Section 323a provides safety net
- **ECHR:** No harmonized approach; leaves to member states

### Cultural Context

Alcohol policy cannot be separated from cultural context:
- South Korea's drinking culture (회식) creates unique pressures
- The American experience reflects tensions between morality-based criminalization and evidence-based public health
- Germany's approach reflects the Central European tradition of relatively permissive alcohol regulation
- The ECHR balances individual autonomy with public safety
