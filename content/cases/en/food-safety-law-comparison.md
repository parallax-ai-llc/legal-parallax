## Summary

Food safety and adulteration law represents a critical intersection of public health, criminal law, and regulatory enforcement, with all major jurisdictions imposing both administrative and criminal penalties for violations. South Korea regulates food safety primarily through the Food Sanitation Act (식품위생법), the Special Act on Imported Food Safety Control, and the Livestock Products Sanitary Control Act, imposing penalties of up to five years imprisonment or fines of up to 50 million won for serious violations, with the Ministry of Food and Drug Safety (MFDS) serving as the central enforcement authority. The United States relies on the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (FDCA, 21 U.S.C. sections 301-399i), as substantially amended by the FDA Food Safety Modernization Act (FSMA) of 2011, with criminal penalties ranging from one year imprisonment for misdemeanor violations to life imprisonment under aggravated circumstances involving intent to defraud or cause death. Germany's food safety framework is anchored in the Food, Consumer Goods, and Feed Code (Lebensmittel- und Futtermittelgesetzbuch, LFGB), supplemented by directly applicable EU regulations, particularly the General Food Law Regulation (EC) No. 178/2002, with criminal penalties of up to five years imprisonment for serious violations under LFGB sections 58-62. At the EU level, the General Food Law Regulation establishes uniform principles of risk analysis, traceability, and the precautionary principle, while criminal enforcement remains the responsibility of individual member states.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's food safety regulatory system is managed primarily by the Ministry of Food and Drug Safety (식품의약품안전처, MFDS), established as an independent ministry-level agency in 2013. The statutory framework includes:

| Legislation | Primary Focus | Enforcement Authority |
|------------|--------------|----------------------|
| Food Sanitation Act (식품위생법) | Domestic food production, processing, sale, and food service | MFDS; local governments |
| Special Act on Imported Food Safety Control (수입식품안전관리 특별법) | Imported food, food additives, and food containers | MFDS |
| Livestock Products Sanitary Control Act (축산물 위생관리법) | Meat, poultry, eggs, and dairy products | Ministry of Agriculture, Food and Rural Affairs (MAFRA) |
| Health Functional Foods Act (건강기능식품에 관한 법률) | Dietary supplements and health functional foods | MFDS |
| Food Labeling and Advertising Act (식품 등의 표시·광고에 관한 법률) | Food labeling accuracy and advertising standards | MFDS |

Key provisions of the Food Sanitation Act:

- **Article 4 (Prohibition of Hazardous Foods):** No person shall sell, manufacture, process, use, cook, store, transport, or display any food or food additive that is rotten, deteriorated, or immature, or that contains or is contaminated with toxic or harmful substances.
- **Article 5 (Prohibition of Diseased Meat):** No person shall sell or use the flesh of animals that have died from disease or unknown causes.
- **Article 6 (Standards for Chemical Residues):** The MFDS establishes maximum residue limits (MRLs) for pesticides, heavy metals, and other chemical contaminants. South Korea operates a Positive List System (PLS) since 2019, under which any pesticide residue not specifically assigned an MRL is subject to a uniform default limit of 0.01 mg/kg.
- **Article 7 (Food Standards and Specifications):** The MFDS publishes the Korean Food Code (식품공전) and the Korean Food Additives Code (식품첨가물공전), which establish compositional standards, processing requirements, and test methods.

### Key Principles

1. **Criminal Penalties:** Violations of the Food Sanitation Act carry substantial criminal penalties:
   - Selling or manufacturing hazardous food (Art. 93): imprisonment of up to five years or fine of up to 50 million won (approximately $38,000), or both.
   - Aggravated violations causing harm: imprisonment of up to seven years or fine of up to 100 million won.
   - Operating without a license or permit: imprisonment of up to three years or fine of up to 30 million won.
   - Repeat offenders face doubled penalties.

2. **Administrative Sanctions:** The MFDS and local authorities may impose administrative sanctions including business suspension (up to six months), business closure orders, product recalls, and monetary penalties (과징금) of up to three times the revenue from the violating product.

3. **Positive List System (PLS):** Since January 1, 2019, South Korea applies a Positive List System for pesticide residue management in all agricultural products. Under this system, only pesticides with specifically established MRLs are permitted; all others are subject to a uniform default limit of 0.01 mg/kg, one of the strictest approaches globally.

### Notable Cases

**Melamine Contamination Scandal (2008):** Following the Chinese melamine-tainted milk powder crisis, South Korean authorities conducted emergency testing of imported dairy products and infant formula. Several Chinese-origin products were found to contain melamine above safe limits. The incident led to strengthened import inspection protocols and contributed to the enactment of the Special Act on Imported Food Safety Control.

**Oxy Reckitt Benckiser Humidifier Disinfectant Case (2011-2020):** While primarily a consumer product safety case, the humidifier disinfectant scandal (가습기 살균제 사건) had significant food safety regulatory implications. The toxic chemical PHMG in humidifier disinfectants caused over 1,500 deaths. The former CEO of Oxy Reckitt Benckiser Korea was convicted and sentenced to seven years imprisonment. The case prompted comprehensive reform of chemical safety regulation, including food contact materials.

**Egg Contamination Crisis (2017):** South Korean authorities detected the insecticide fipronil in eggs from multiple farms, leading to a nationwide suspension of egg distribution and testing of all egg-producing farms. The crisis exposed enforcement gaps in the livestock products safety system and led to enhanced monitoring requirements and the expansion of the PLS to animal products.

## United States

### Federal Framework

The United States food safety system is governed by a complex web of federal statutes administered primarily by the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) and the United States Department of Agriculture (USDA):

- **Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (FDCA), 21 U.S.C. sections 301-399i:** The primary statute governing the safety, labeling, and adulteration of food products. First enacted in 1938, it replaced the Pure Food and Drug Act of 1906.

- **FDA Food Safety Modernization Act (FSMA), Pub. L. 111-353 (2011):** The most significant reform of U.S. food safety law in over 70 years. FSMA shifted the regulatory paradigm from reactive (responding to contamination events) to preventive (requiring food facilities to implement hazard analysis and preventive controls).

- **Federal Meat Inspection Act (FMIA), 21 U.S.C. sections 601-695:** Governs the inspection and safety of meat and meat products, administered by USDA's Food Safety and Inspection Service (FSIS).

- **Poultry Products Inspection Act (PPIA), 21 U.S.C. sections 451-472:** Governs the inspection and safety of poultry and poultry products.

- **Egg Products Inspection Act (EPIA), 21 U.S.C. sections 1031-1056:** Governs egg product safety.

### Prohibited Acts and Penalties

The FDCA's enforcement provisions are structured as follows:

**Prohibited Acts (21 U.S.C. section 331):**

The introduction or delivery for introduction into interstate commerce of any food that is adulterated (section 342) or misbranded (section 343) is a prohibited act. Additional prohibited acts include the adulteration or misbranding of food while in interstate commerce, the receipt and delivery of adulterated or misbranded food, and the refusal to permit inspections.

**Criminal Penalties (21 U.S.C. section 333):**

| Violation Type | Penalty |
|---------------|---------|
| First offense (misdemeanor, no intent) | Up to 1 year imprisonment and/or fine up to $1,000 |
| Repeat offense or violation with intent to defraud/mislead | Up to 3 years imprisonment and/or fine up to $10,000 |
| Knowing and willful violation causing death (18 U.S.C. section 1365) | Up to life imprisonment |
| Tampering with consumer products (18 U.S.C. section 1365) | Up to 10 years imprisonment (20 years if death results) |

**Civil Penalties and Administrative Actions:**

- Product seizure (21 U.S.C. section 334): FDA may seek judicial seizure of adulterated or misbranded food.
- Injunction (21 U.S.C. section 332): FDA may seek court orders to halt violations.
- Mandatory recall authority (21 U.S.C. section 350l, added by FSMA): FDA can order a recall if a company fails to do so voluntarily.
- Import detention (21 U.S.C. section 381): FDA may detain imported food that appears to be adulterated or misbranded.
- Debarment (21 U.S.C. section 335a): Individuals convicted of felony food safety offenses may be permanently barred from the food industry.

### State Variations

States maintain concurrent authority over food safety within their borders:

- All 50 states have food safety statutes that generally parallel federal law.
- The FDA Food Code serves as a model for state and local food service regulations; adoption is voluntary but widespread.
- State departments of agriculture and health conduct inspections of restaurants, retail food establishments, and intrastate food producers.
- Some states impose stricter standards than federal law (e.g., California's Proposition 65 warning requirements for foods containing listed chemicals).

### Notable Cases

**United States v. Park, 421 U.S. 658 (1975):** The Supreme Court upheld the "responsible corporate officer" doctrine under the FDCA, holding that corporate officers can be held criminally liable for food safety violations even without personal participation in or knowledge of the specific violation. This landmark case established that persons in positions of authority within food companies have an affirmative duty to prevent violations.

**Peanut Corporation of America / United States v. Parnell (2014-2015):** Stewart Parnell, CEO of the Peanut Corporation of America, was convicted of federal conspiracy, fraud, and other charges related to a nationwide Salmonella outbreak linked to contaminated peanut butter that killed nine people and sickened over 700. Parnell was sentenced to 28 years in federal prison -- the most severe sentence ever imposed in a U.S. food safety case. The case was prosecuted under 18 U.S.C. section 1001 (false statements) and other federal fraud statutes.

**Jensen Farms / United States v. Jensen (2013):** Eric and Ryan Jensen, owners of Jensen Farms in Colorado, pleaded guilty to misdemeanor adulteration charges under the FDCA in connection with a Listeria monocytogenes outbreak in cantaloupe that killed 33 people -- the deadliest U.S. foodborne illness outbreak in nearly a century. Each was sentenced to five years of probation and ordered to pay restitution. The relatively light sentences drew criticism and contributed to calls for enhanced criminal penalties.

**Blue Bell Creameries / United States v. Kruse (2020):** Former Blue Bell Creameries president Paul Kruse was indicted on federal charges including conspiracy and wire fraud related to a 2015 Listeria outbreak that killed three people. He was accused of orchestrating a scheme to conceal the contamination from regulators and the public. Kruse was convicted and sentenced to federal prison, reinforcing the application of fraud statutes to food safety concealment.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's food safety law operates within the broader EU regulatory framework, with national legislation supplementing directly applicable EU regulations:

**Primary National Legislation:**

- **Food, Consumer Goods, and Feed Code (Lebensmittel- und Futtermittelgesetzbuch, LFGB):** Entered into force September 7, 2005, replacing the former Food and Consumer Goods Act (Lebensmittel- und Bedarfsgegenstaendegesetz, LMBG). The LFGB is the central national statute governing food safety, labeling, and enforcement.

- **Food Hygiene Regulation (Lebensmittelhygiene-Verordnung, LMHV):** Implements EU hygiene regulations at the national level.

- **Food Information Regulation (Lebensmittelinformations-Durchfuehrungsverordnung, LMIDV):** National implementation of EU Regulation No. 1169/2011 on food information to consumers.

**Directly Applicable EU Regulations:**

| EU Regulation | Subject |
|--------------|---------|
| Regulation (EC) No. 178/2002 (General Food Law) | General principles, risk analysis, traceability, precautionary principle |
| Regulation (EC) No. 852/2004 | Hygiene of foodstuffs (HACCP requirements) |
| Regulation (EC) No. 853/2004 | Specific hygiene rules for food of animal origin |
| Regulation (EC) No. 854/2004 | Official controls on products of animal origin |
| Regulation (EU) 2017/625 | Official controls and enforcement activities |
| Regulation (EC) No. 1935/2004 | Food contact materials |
| Regulation (EC) No. 396/2005 | Maximum residue levels for pesticides |

### Criminal and Administrative Penalties

The LFGB contains detailed penalty provisions:

| LFGB Provision | Offense | Penalty |
|---------------|---------|---------|
| section 58(1) | Placing dangerous food on the market (intentional) | Imprisonment up to 3 years or fine |
| section 58(2) | Placing dangerous food on the market resulting in health damage | Imprisonment up to 5 years or fine |
| section 58(3) | Negligent commission of the above | Imprisonment up to 1 year or fine |
| section 59(1) | Violations of labeling, traceability, or hygiene requirements | Imprisonment up to 1 year or fine |
| section 59(2) | Negligent commission of section 59(1) offenses | Fine only |
| section 60 | Administrative offenses (Ordnungswidrigkeiten) | Fines up to EUR 100,000 |

Additional criminal provisions under the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB):

- **StGB section 314 (Contamination of Water or Food):** Whoever contaminates water sources or food intended for public consumption with substances harmful to health is punished with imprisonment from one to ten years. In particularly serious cases involving danger to life, the penalty is imprisonment of not less than five years.
- **StGB section 319 (Endangerment Through Construction Defects):** Can apply to food facility construction and safety.

### Key Principles

1. **Shared Federal-State Enforcement (Foederalismus):** Food safety enforcement in Germany is the responsibility of the 16 federal states (Laender), not the federal government. Each state maintains its own food inspection authority (Lebensmittelueberwachung). The Federal Office for Consumer Protection and Food Safety (Bundesamt fuer Verbraucherschutz und Lebensmittelsicherheit, BVL) coordinates between states and the EU but does not conduct inspections directly.

2. **Precautionary Principle:** German and EU food law embodies the precautionary principle (Vorsorgeprinzip). Under Article 7 of Regulation (EC) No. 178/2002, where scientific uncertainty exists regarding a potential risk to health, provisional risk management measures may be adopted pending further scientific assessment.

3. **Traceability (Rueckverfolgbarkeit):** Under Article 18 of Regulation (EC) No. 178/2002, food business operators must be able to identify from whom they received food, food-producing animals, or any substance intended for incorporation into food, and to whom they supplied such products. This "one step back, one step forward" traceability requirement enables rapid identification of contamination sources.

4. **Public Naming (Oeffentliche Nennung):** Under LFGB section 40, authorities may publicly disclose the names of businesses and products involved in food safety violations, even before criminal proceedings are concluded, if certain conditions are met. This "naming and shaming" provision has been controversial and was the subject of a Federal Constitutional Court decision in 2018.

### Notable Cases

**Dioxin Scandal (2010-2011):** Industrial fatty acids contaminated with dioxin were incorporated into animal feed, leading to elevated dioxin levels in eggs, poultry, and pork from over 4,700 farms. The scandal led to temporary closures of approximately 1,000 farms. Criminal investigations resulted in convictions of responsible parties at the feed company Harles und Jentzsch. The incident exposed weaknesses in the feed-to-food traceability system and led to stricter controls on feed fat suppliers.

**Wilke Wurst Listeria Outbreak (2019):** The Hessian sausage manufacturer Wilke Waldecker Fleisch- und Wurstwaren was linked to a Listeria monocytogenes outbreak that caused at least three deaths and 37 illnesses. The company was shut down, and criminal investigations were launched against company officials. The case raised questions about the adequacy of state-level inspection frequency and inter-state coordination, as the company's products were distributed throughout Germany and to other EU member states.

**Horse Meat Scandal (2013):** While originating in other EU member states, the discovery of undeclared horse meat in products labeled as beef had significant ramifications in Germany. German authorities conducted extensive testing and identified fraudulently labeled products in the German supply chain. The scandal led to enhanced EU-wide controls under Regulation (EU) 2017/625 and the establishment of the EU Food Fraud Network.

## ECHR / EU Framework

### Convention Articles

The ECHR does not contain specific food safety provisions, but several articles are relevant:

- **Article 2 (Right to Life):** States have a positive obligation to protect life, which extends to ensuring adequate food safety regulation. Systematic failure to enforce food safety standards leading to deaths could engage Article 2.

- **Article 8 (Right to Respect for Private Life):** The right to physical and psychological integrity falls within Article 8. Exposure to harmful food contaminants can constitute a violation.

- **Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (Protection of Property):** Food safety enforcement measures (seizures, business closures, recalls) must be proportionate and lawful to avoid violating property rights.

- **Article 6 (Right to a Fair Trial):** Food businesses subject to criminal prosecution or administrative sanctions are entitled to fair trial guarantees.

### Key EU Framework

**General Food Law Regulation (EC) No. 178/2002:**

This foundational regulation establishes:

| Principle | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Risk Analysis (Art. 6) | Food law must be based on risk analysis comprising risk assessment, risk management, and risk communication |
| Precautionary Principle (Art. 7) | Provisional protective measures may be adopted where scientific uncertainty exists |
| Traceability (Art. 18) | "One step back, one step forward" traceability throughout the food chain |
| Primary Responsibility (Art. 17) | Food business operators bear primary responsibility for food safety |
| Rapid Alert System (Art. 50) | RASFF (Rapid Alert System for Food and Feed) enables rapid exchange of food safety information among member states |
| European Food Safety Authority (Art. 22) | EFSA provides independent scientific advice on food-related risks |

**Regulation (EU) 2017/625 (Official Controls Regulation):**

Replaced the previous official controls framework, establishing harmonized rules for:
- Frequency and scope of official controls
- Methods and techniques of official control
- Actions in case of non-compliance
- Import controls
- Financing of official controls

**Directive 2008/99/EC (Environmental Crime Directive):**

Requires member states to ensure that serious violations of EU environmental law, including illegal handling of food waste and contamination of water or soil affecting the food chain, are punishable as criminal offenses.

### Key Case Law

**CJEU, Berger v. Freistaat Bayern, C-636/11 (2013):** The Court addressed the scope of "food business operator" under Regulation (EC) No. 178/2002, holding that the definition encompasses all stages of production, processing, and distribution, including retail. The case clarified the extent of traceability and safety obligations throughout the food chain.

**CJEU, Lidl Italia, C-134/15 (2016):** The Court addressed the labeling requirements for food products under Regulation (EU) No. 1169/2011, including requirements for allergen declarations and country of origin labeling. The decision clarified that member states may impose additional national labeling requirements only where not in conflict with EU harmonized rules.

**BVerfG, 1 BvR 1826/18 (2018):** The German Federal Constitutional Court addressed the constitutionality of public disclosure of food safety violations under LFGB section 40. The Court held that the provision was generally compatible with the Basic Law's protection of professional freedom (Art. 12 GG) and property (Art. 14 GG), but required that disclosure be limited to cases where there is a sufficient factual basis and the public interest outweighs the business's interest in confidentiality.

### Member State Obligations

EU member states are required to:

1. Designate competent authorities for official food controls (Art. 4, Reg. 2017/625).
2. Prepare and implement multi-annual national control plans (Art. 109, Reg. 2017/625).
3. Ensure adequate penalties -- including criminal penalties -- for food law violations (Art. 139, Reg. 2017/625).
4. Participate in the Rapid Alert System for Food and Feed (RASFF).
5. Cooperate with EFSA in food safety risk assessments.

### International Comparisons

- **Japan:** The Food Sanitation Act (食品衛生法) and Food Safety Basic Act (食品安全基本法, 2003) govern food safety. The Food Safety Commission of Japan conducts independent risk assessments. Penalties for food safety violations include imprisonment of up to three years or fines of up to 3 million yen. Japan has strict import controls, particularly for agricultural chemicals and additives not approved under Japanese law.
- **China:** The Food Safety Law of the People's Republic of China (2015 revision) imposes some of the world's strictest penalties, including fines of up to 30 times the value of the violating product, lifetime industry bans, and criminal penalties including the death penalty for food safety violations causing death in particularly egregious circumstances.
- **Brazil:** The National Health Surveillance Agency (ANVISA) enforces food safety under Law No. 6.437/1977 and Law No. 9.782/1999. Criminal penalties for food adulteration are established under the Penal Code (Articles 272-278), with imprisonment of up to eight years for adulterating food intended for consumption.
- **India:** The Food Safety and Standards Act, 2006 (FSSA) established the Food Safety and Standards Authority of India (FSSAI). Criminal penalties include imprisonment of up to life for food adulteration causing death (Section 59). The Act replaced the Prevention of Food Adulteration Act, 1954.
- **Australia:** Food Standards Australia New Zealand (FSANZ) develops food standards under the Food Standards Australia New Zealand Act 1991. Enforcement is conducted by state and territory authorities. Criminal penalties vary by jurisdiction but can include imprisonment of up to 10 years for serious food safety offenses in some states.
