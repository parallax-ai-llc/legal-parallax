## Summary

Blockchain and smart contracts (블록체인/스마트계약) -- distributed ledger technologies that enable trustless, immutable record-keeping and self-executing contractual agreements encoded in software -- have generated a rapidly evolving body of law spanning financial regulation, contract law, securities law, data protection, and intellectual property. South Korea regulates blockchain-based activities primarily through the Act on Reporting and Use of Specific Financial Transaction Information (특정 금융거래정보의 보고 및 이용 등에 관한 법률, commonly known as the Special Financial Transactions Act), amended in 2021 to require virtual asset service providers (VASPs) to register with the Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU), and the Virtual Asset User Protection Act (가상자산 이용자 보호법, enacted 2023, effective July 2024), which established comprehensive investor protection rules for virtual assets including market manipulation prohibitions, custodial requirements, and disclosure obligations. The United States applies a fragmented regulatory framework where the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) classifies many tokens as securities under the Howey test, the Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC) asserts jurisdiction over cryptocurrency derivatives and certain spot markets, state money transmitter laws apply to cryptocurrency exchanges, and individual states including Wyoming have enacted blockchain-specific legislation recognizing smart contracts and decentralized autonomous organizations (DAOs); the landmark SEC v. Ripple Labs (2023) decision provided the first major judicial framework for determining when digital assets constitute securities. Germany has positioned itself as a blockchain-friendly jurisdiction through the Gesetz über elektronische Wertpapiere (eWpG, Electronic Securities Act, 2021) permitting the issuance of securities on distributed ledger systems, the Kryptowertetransferverordnung (KryptoWTransfV) implementing FATF travel rule requirements, and BaFin's licensing regime for crypto custody and trading. At the EU level, the Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA, Regulation (EU) 2023/1114), fully applicable since December 30, 2024, creates the world's first comprehensive regulatory framework for crypto-assets across all member states, while the DLT Pilot Regime Regulation (Regulation (EU) 2022/858) enables experimentation with DLT-based market infrastructures, and the eIDAS 2.0 Regulation recognizes qualified electronic ledgers as a trust service.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea has evolved from a near-ban on initial coin offerings (ICOs) in 2017 to one of the more developed virtual asset regulatory frameworks in Asia.

**Virtual Asset User Protection Act (가상자산 이용자 보호법):** Enacted by the National Assembly in July 2023 and effective July 19, 2024, this Act establishes comprehensive investor protection for virtual asset transactions:

- **Market Manipulation Prohibition:** The Act prohibits unfair trading practices including market manipulation, insider trading, and price manipulation of virtual assets, mirroring securities law prohibitions. Violators face criminal penalties of up to life imprisonment for gains exceeding 5 billion KRW.
- **Custodial Requirements:** Virtual asset service providers must segregate customer assets from proprietary assets, maintain cold storage for at least 80 percent of customer deposits, and carry insurance or maintain reserves to cover potential losses.
- **Disclosure Obligations:** VASPs must disclose material information to users including trading volumes, listing/delisting criteria, and security incident reports.
- **Financial Services Commission (FSC) Authority:** The FSC (금융위원회) exercises regulatory oversight, with authority to investigate market violations, impose sanctions, and coordinate with law enforcement.

**Special Financial Transactions Act (특정금융거래정보법):** Amended in 2021 to incorporate FATF Recommendation 15, this Act requires all VASPs operating in Korea to register with the Korea Financial Intelligence Unit (KoFIU), implement AML/CFT procedures, and comply with the travel rule for transfers exceeding 1 million KRW.

### ICO Ban and Evolution

South Korea's Financial Services Commission issued a blanket ban on ICOs in September 2017, prohibiting all forms of token issuance for fundraising purposes. While this ban has not been formally lifted, the regulatory landscape has shifted toward permitting security token offerings (STOs) under regulated conditions, with the FSC announcing STO guidelines in February 2023 and pilot programs for tokenized securities through Korea Exchange (KRX) launching in 2024.

### Smart Contracts Under Korean Law

Korean law does not have specific smart contract legislation but recognizes smart contracts under general contract law principles of the Civil Code (민법). The Korean courts apply standard offer-and-acceptance analysis, treating code execution as performance of contractual obligations where the parties have agreed to be bound by the smart contract's terms.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States' regulatory approach to blockchain and crypto-assets is characterized by overlapping agency jurisdictions and ongoing legislative efforts to create a comprehensive framework.

**SEC Jurisdiction -- Securities Classification:** The SEC applies the Howey test (SEC v. W.J. Howey Co., 328 U.S. 293, 1946) to determine whether a digital asset constitutes an "investment contract" (and thus a security). The test asks whether there is an investment of money in a common enterprise with an expectation of profits derived primarily from the efforts of others.

**SEC v. Ripple Labs (S.D.N.Y., 2023):** Judge Torres's ruling distinguished between institutional sales of XRP (which constituted securities offerings under Howey) and programmatic sales on secondary exchanges (which did not). This bifurcated analysis became the most influential judicial framework for digital asset classification, though its precedential value remains debated as an appeal was pending.

**CFTC Jurisdiction:** The CFTC classifies Bitcoin and Ethereum as commodities and asserts jurisdiction over cryptocurrency derivatives markets and, following the CFTC Reauthorization Act discussions, potentially over certain spot crypto markets.

### State-Level Innovation

Several states have enacted blockchain-specific legislation:

| State | Legislation | Key Provision |
|-------|------------|---------------|
| Wyoming | SF 125 (2019); DAO LLC Act (2021) | Smart contracts legally enforceable; DAOs recognized as LLCs |
| Tennessee | Blockchain Technology Act (2018) | Smart contracts recognized; electronic signatures on blockchain valid |
| Arizona | HB 2417 (2017) | Smart contracts recognized as valid under electronic transactions law |
| Vermont | Blockchain Enabling Act (2018) | Blockchain records admissible as evidence |
| Delaware | Blockchain Amendments to DGCL (2017) | Corporations may maintain records on blockchain |

### Stablecoin and Pending Legislation

Federal stablecoin legislation has been a priority, with the Clarity for Payment Stablecoins Act and competing proposals advancing through Congress. These proposals would establish federal licensing for stablecoin issuers, reserve requirements, and redemption rights. The comprehensive FIT21 Act (Financial Innovation and Technology for the 21st Century Act) passed the House in 2024, proposing to divide crypto-asset regulatory authority between the SEC (securities) and CFTC (digital commodities).

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany has adopted a proactive, blockchain-friendly regulatory approach within the EU framework.

**Gesetz über elektronische Wertpapiere (eWpG, Electronic Securities Act, 2021):** Enacted on June 10, 2021, the eWpG allows the issuance of bearer bonds and fund units as electronic securities (elektronische Wertpapiere) registered on a crypto securities register (Kryptowertpapierregister) maintained on a distributed ledger. This eliminates the requirement for a physical securities certificate (Wertpapierurkunde) and enables fully digital securities issuance. The eWpG was the first major European legislation permitting DLT-based securities issuance.

**BaFin Crypto Licensing:** Germany was among the first EU jurisdictions to establish a dedicated licensing regime for crypto-related activities. The Kreditwesengesetz (KWG, Banking Act) was amended in 2020 to include crypto custody (Kryptoverwahrgeschäft) as a regulated financial service requiring BaFin authorization. By 2025, BaFin had granted over 30 crypto-related licenses.

**Kryptowertetransferverordnung (KryptoWTransfV):** Implementing the FATF travel rule, this regulation requires obliged entities to collect and transmit originator and beneficiary information for crypto-asset transfers, regardless of amount. Germany's implementation is among the strictest globally, with no de minimis threshold.

### Smart Contracts Under German Law

German contract law (BGB) applies general principles of offer, acceptance, and declaration of intent (Willenserklärung) to smart contracts. The legal validity of automated contract execution is recognized under Section 116 et seq. BGB, with the smart contract code constituting the parties' agreed terms. Academic commentary and emerging case law address challenges including error correction (Anfechtung, Section 119 BGB), impossibility of performance (Section 275 BGB), and the application of consumer protection rules to smart contract transactions.

### Transition to MiCA

With MiCA's full applicability from December 30, 2024, Germany transitioned its national crypto regulatory framework to the harmonized EU system. BaFin serves as the national competent authority for MiCA implementation, and existing German-licensed crypto firms were granted transitional arrangements to obtain MiCA authorization.

## ECHR & EU

### Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA, Regulation (EU) 2023/1114)

MiCA, adopted on June 9, 2023, and fully applicable from December 30, 2024, creates the world's first comprehensive regulatory framework for crypto-assets:

**Scope:** MiCA covers crypto-assets not already regulated as financial instruments, including utility tokens, asset-referenced tokens (ARTs), and electronic money tokens (EMTs). It does not cover NFTs (unless they meet the definition of crypto-assets), DeFi protocols (to be addressed in future regulation), or crypto-assets that qualify as financial instruments under MiFID II.

**Crypto-Asset Service Provider (CASP) Authorization:** CASPs must obtain authorization from their home member state's competent authority. Authorization requirements include governance arrangements, prudential safeguards, custody obligations, complaint handling procedures, and conflicts of interest management.

**Stablecoin Regulation (Titles III and IV):** ARTs must maintain adequate reserves, redemption rights, and disclosure requirements. Significant ARTs face enhanced requirements supervised by the EBA. EMTs must be issued by authorized credit institutions or e-money institutions, backed 1:1 by funds denominated in the referenced currency.

**Market Abuse (Title VI):** MiCA prohibits insider dealing, market manipulation, and unlawful disclosure of inside information regarding crypto-assets, mirroring the Market Abuse Regulation (MAR) framework for traditional securities.

### DLT Pilot Regime (Regulation (EU) 2022/858)

Effective March 23, 2023, the DLT Pilot Regime enables market participants to operate DLT-based market infrastructures (multilateral trading facilities and securities settlement systems) under relaxed regulatory requirements for a limited period. The Regime allows experimentation with tokenized securities while maintaining investor protection.

### eIDAS 2.0 and Electronic Ledgers

Regulation (EU) 2024/1183 (eIDAS 2.0) introduces qualified electronic ledgers as a new trust service category, providing legal recognition and cross-border validity for records maintained on distributed ledger systems that meet qualified trust service requirements.

### Transfer of Funds Regulation (Regulation (EU) 2023/1113)

The recast Transfer of Funds Regulation extends the FATF travel rule to crypto-asset transfers, requiring CASPs to collect and transmit originator and beneficiary information for all crypto-asset transfers without a de minimis threshold, effective from December 30, 2024, alongside MiCA.

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR's jurisprudence on property rights (Article 1 of Protocol 1) is relevant to crypto-asset regulation. While no crypto-specific ECHR case has been decided, the Court's broad interpretation of "possessions" (including contractual claims and legitimate expectations) in cases such as Beyeler v. Italy (Application No. 33202/96, Grand Chamber Judgment of January 5, 2000) suggests that crypto-assets may be protected as property under the Convention, constraining regulatory confiscation or disproportionate interference.

## Comparative Analysis

### Regulatory Comprehensiveness

MiCA represents the most comprehensive regulatory framework, establishing uniform rules across 27 member states. South Korea's Virtual Asset User Protection Act provides focused investor protection. The United States' fragmented approach creates regulatory uncertainty, with competing agency jurisdictions and ongoing legislative efforts. Germany has bridged national innovation (eWpG) with EU harmonization (MiCA).

### Securities Classification

The most contentious regulatory question remains when a digital asset constitutes a security. The US Howey test, as interpreted in SEC v. Ripple, creates a fact-intensive, case-by-case analysis. MiCA largely avoids this issue by creating separate crypto-asset categories distinct from MiFID II financial instruments. South Korea and Germany apply case-by-case classification approaches aligned with their respective securities law frameworks.

### Smart Contract Legal Status

Wyoming represents the most advanced explicit statutory recognition of smart contracts. Germany and South Korea recognize smart contracts under general contract law principles without dedicated legislation. The EU's eIDAS 2.0 electronic ledger trust service provides indirect recognition. The fundamental challenge across all jurisdictions is reconciling immutable code execution with legal concepts of error correction, changed circumstances, and consumer protection.

### Stablecoin Regulation

MiCA's ART/EMT framework is the most developed stablecoin regulatory regime. The US lacks comprehensive federal stablecoin legislation despite multiple proposals. South Korea is developing stablecoin-specific rules. The collapse of TerraUSD/Luna in May 2022 (which particularly devastated Korean retail investors) catalyzed regulatory urgency across all jurisdictions.
