## Summary

Police brutality and excessive force — the use of force by law enforcement officers that exceeds what is reasonably necessary under the circumstances — remain among the most contested areas of criminal and constitutional law worldwide. The legal frameworks governing police use of force differ dramatically across jurisdictions in their definitions, accountability mechanisms, available remedies, and practical enforcement. South Korea criminalizes violence and cruel acts by public officials under Articles 124-125 of the Criminal Act, with the National Human Rights Commission of Korea serving as a non-binding oversight body. The United States relies on a dual civil-criminal framework, with 42 U.S.C. Section 1983 providing civil remedies and 18 U.S.C. Section 242 establishing federal criminal liability, though the judicially created doctrine of qualified immunity significantly limits officer accountability. Germany addresses the issue through Section 340 of the Strafgesetzbuch (Koerperverletzung im Amt), which imposes heightened penalties on public officials who commit assault while exercising their duties, and established a federal police ombudsperson (Polizeibeauftragte des Bundes) in 2024. The European Court of Human Rights enforces the absolute prohibition on torture and inhuman or degrading treatment under Article 3 of the European Convention, imposing both substantive and procedural obligations on member states to prevent, investigate, and punish police violence.

## South Korea

### Constitutional Foundation

Article 12 of the Constitution of the Republic of Korea guarantees personal liberty and provides that no person shall be tortured or compelled to testify against themselves in criminal cases. Article 10 protects human dignity and the right to pursue happiness, forming the constitutional basis for protection against police violence.

### Criminal Act Provisions

The Korean Criminal Act (hyeongbeop) addresses police brutality through Chapter VII (Crimes Concerning the Duties of Public Officials), with several key provisions:

**Article 123 — Abuse of Authority:** A public official who, by abusing his or her official authority, causes another person to perform an act the person is not legally obligated to perform, or obstructs the exercise of a right of another person, shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than five years and suspension of qualifications for not more than ten years.

**Article 124 — Unlawful Arrest and Confinement:** If a person who performs or assists in activities concerning judgment, prosecution, police, or other functions involving the restraint of the human body arrests or imprisons another by abusing his or her official authority, he or she shall be punished by imprisonment with labor for not more than seven years and suspension of qualifications for not more than ten years.

**Article 125 — Violence and Cruel Act:** A person who, in performing or assisting in activities concerning judgment, prosecution, police, or other functions involving the restraint of the human body, commits an act of violence or cruelty against a criminal suspect or against another person while performing his or her duties shall be punished by imprisonment with labor for not more than five years and suspension of qualifications for not more than ten years.

| Offense | Provision | Penalty |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Abuse of authority | Art. 123 | Up to 5 years / suspension of qualifications up to 10 years |
| Unlawful arrest / confinement | Art. 124(1) | Up to 7 years / suspension of qualifications up to 10 years |
| Unlawful arrest causing injury/death | Art. 124(2) | Enhanced penalties |
| Violence or cruel act by official | Art. 125 | Up to 5 years / suspension of qualifications up to 10 years |
| Violence causing injury/death | Art. 125(2) | Enhanced penalties |

### Police Officer Duty Execution Act

The Act on the Performance of Duties by Police Officers (gyeongchalgwan jigmu jiphaeng-e gwanhan beomnyul) regulates the conditions under which Korean police may use force. Key provisions include:

- A police officer may use a weapon only when "under reasonable assessment of the situation, there are considerable causes" to believe it is necessary.
- Force may be used only if the situation has been rationally assessed, if it absolutely calls for physical force, and there is no possible alternative.
- Any person who inflicts harm on others by violating police duties or abusing official authority under the Act shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than one year.

In 2022, the National Assembly revised the Act to allow reduced or eliminated punishment for officers who use force in situations where a crime has been committed or is likely to occur, reflecting a policy shift toward greater operational discretion for police.

### Oversight and Accountability

**National Human Rights Commission of Korea (NHRCK):** Established as an independent government body, the NHRCK investigates complaints of excessive or indiscriminate use of force by police. However, its recommendations and decisions are non-binding. The UN Committee against Torture expressed concern in 2017 about the Commission's "low rate of acceptance of complaints relating to torture or ill-treatment."

**Korean National Police Agency Human Rights Protection Division:** This internal body investigates reported allegations of human rights abuses by police. Cases involving alleged police violations of human rights are handled by a committee of nine members, including six representatives of human rights organizations.

**State Compensation Act:** Victims of police violence may seek civil compensation from the state under the State Compensation Act (gukga baesang beop), which provides for government liability when public officials cause damage through intentional or negligent illegal acts in the course of their duties.

### Key Developments

South Korea ratified the Convention against Torture and Other Cruel, Inhuman or Degrading Treatment or Punishment on January 9, 1995. The Criminal Procedure Act provides that confessions extracted by torture, violence, threat, or after prolonged arrest or detention shall not be admitted as evidence of guilt.

## United States

### Constitutional Framework

The U.S. Constitution provides the primary legal basis for excessive force claims through two amendments:

**Fourth Amendment:** Protects against "unreasonable searches and seizures." The Supreme Court held in Graham v. Connor (1989) that all claims of excessive force during arrest, investigatory stop, or other seizure must be analyzed under the Fourth Amendment's "objective reasonableness" standard. This fact-specific inquiry considers the severity of the crime, whether the suspect poses an immediate threat to the safety of officers or others, and whether the suspect is actively resisting or attempting to flee.

**Fourteenth Amendment (Due Process Clause):** Governs excessive force claims by pretrial detainees and in non-seizure contexts. The substantive due process "shocks the conscience" standard applies when the Fourth Amendment does not.

### Civil Liability: 42 U.S.C. Section 1983

Section 1983 of Title 42 of the United States Code is the principal federal civil remedy for victims of police excessive force. Enacted as part of the Civil Rights Act of 1871 (the "Ku Klux Klan Act"), it allows citizens to hold state or local government officials personally liable for civil rights violations committed while acting under color of law. Plaintiffs may recover compensatory damages, punitive damages, and attorneys' fees.

**Municipal Liability (Monell Doctrine):** Under Monell v. Department of Social Services (1978), municipalities may be held liable under Section 1983 only when a constitutional violation results from the implementation of an official policy or custom. Respondeat superior liability — holding municipalities automatically liable for employee misconduct — is not available under Section 1983.

### Criminal Liability: 18 U.S.C. Section 242

Section 242 of Title 18 makes it a federal crime for any person acting under color of law to willfully deprive another of rights protected by the Constitution or federal law. Penalties range up to one year in prison for basic violations, up to ten years if bodily injury results, and up to life imprisonment (or the death penalty) if the violation results in death or involves kidnapping, attempted kidnapping, or sexual abuse.

The mens rea requirement of "willfulness" has been a significant barrier to prosecution, requiring proof that the officer acted with the specific intent to deprive the victim of a constitutional right. The proposed George Floyd Justice in Policing Act (reintroduced in 2025 as H.R. 5361) would amend this standard from "willfully" to "knowingly or recklessly."

### Qualified Immunity

Qualified immunity is a judicially created doctrine shielding government officials from civil liability unless their conduct violated "clearly established" statutory or constitutional rights of which a reasonable person would have known (Harlow v. Fitzgerald, 1982). In practice, this doctrine has proven to be a formidable obstacle for plaintiffs in excessive force cases, as courts often require a prior case with nearly identical facts to find that a right was "clearly established."

**Pearson v. Callahan (2009):** The Supreme Court modified the procedural framework for qualified immunity analysis, allowing courts to skip the question of whether a constitutional violation occurred and proceed directly to whether the right was clearly established. Critics argue this has stunted the development of constitutional law by allowing courts to avoid ruling on the merits of excessive force claims.

### Pattern-or-Practice Investigations

Under 34 U.S.C. Section 12601 (formerly 42 U.S.C. Section 14141), the U.S. Department of Justice may investigate and bring civil actions against law enforcement agencies engaged in a "pattern or practice" of conduct that deprives persons of constitutional rights. These investigations can result in consent decrees requiring systemic reforms.

### State-Level Reforms

Several states have enacted legislation limiting or eliminating qualified immunity at the state level, including Colorado (2020), New Mexico (2021), and New York City (through local legislation). Some states have also established independent oversight bodies, such as civilian review boards and inspectors general with subpoena power.

### Landmark Cases

| Case | Year | Holding |
|------|------|---------|
| Tennessee v. Garner | 1985 | Deadly force against an unarmed fleeing felon violates the Fourth Amendment unless the officer has probable cause to believe the suspect poses a significant threat of death or serious physical injury |
| Graham v. Connor | 1989 | All excessive force claims during seizure analyzed under Fourth Amendment objective reasonableness standard; factors include crime severity, immediate threat, and active resistance |
| Monell v. Dept. of Social Services | 1978 | Municipalities are "persons" under Section 1983 but liable only for unconstitutional policies or customs, not respondeat superior |
| Harlow v. Fitzgerald | 1982 | Established the modern qualified immunity test: liability only if conduct violates clearly established rights |
| Pearson v. Callahan | 2009 | Courts may address qualified immunity in any order, need not decide whether constitutional violation occurred |
| Kingsley v. Hendrickson | 2015 | Pretrial detainee excessive force claims require only objective unreasonableness, not subjective intent to harm |

## Germany

### Constitutional Foundation

Article 2(2) of the Grundgesetz (Basic Law) guarantees the right to life and physical integrity (koerperliche Unversehrtheit). Article 1(1) declares human dignity inviolable and imposes a duty on all state authority to respect and protect it. Police use of force must satisfy the principle of proportionality (Verhaeltnismaessigkeitsgrundsatz), which requires that any state interference with fundamental rights be suitable, necessary, and proportionate in the narrow sense.

### Criminal Law: Strafgesetzbuch (StGB)

**Section 340 — Koerperverletzung im Amt (Assault in Office):** Introduced into the StGB in 1998 in response to increasing concerns about police violence, this provision imposes heightened penalties on public officials who commit assault while exercising their duties:

- Paragraph 1: A public official who, during the exercise of his duties or in relation to his duties, commits an assault or allows an assault to be committed shall be punished with imprisonment from three months to five years.
- Paragraph 2: In less serious cases (minder schwerer Fall), the penalty is imprisonment of up to five years or a fine.

Section 340 StGB is an Offizialdelikt (official offense), meaning prosecution is initiated automatically by the state prosecutor (Staatsanwaltschaft) rather than requiring a complaint from the victim. This is a significant distinction from ordinary assault under Section 223 StGB, which in some contexts is a Antragsdelikt (complaint offense).

| Offense | Provision | Penalty |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Assault in office | Section 340(1) | 3 months to 5 years imprisonment |
| Assault in office (less serious case) | Section 340(2) | Up to 5 years or fine |
| Simple assault (comparison) | Section 223 | Up to 5 years or fine |
| Dangerous assault by official | Section 340 in conjunction with Section 224 | 6 months to 10 years imprisonment |
| Grievous bodily harm by official | Section 340 in conjunction with Section 226 | 3 to 15 years imprisonment |

### Civil Liability: Amtshaftung

Under Article 34 of the Grundgesetz, in conjunction with Section 839 of the Buergerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB), the state assumes liability for damage caused by public officials in the exercise of their duties. Unlike the U.S. system, individual officers are generally not personally liable to the victim — instead, the state bears direct liability. The state may, however, seek recourse against the officer in cases of intentional misconduct or gross negligence.

### Police Law (Polizeirecht)

Police use of force is regulated by both federal and state (Laender) police statutes. The principle of proportionality pervades all police law, requiring that:

- **Suitability (Geeignetheit):** The measure must be capable of achieving its intended purpose.
- **Necessity (Erforderlichkeit):** No less intrusive means is available to achieve the same purpose.
- **Proportionality in the narrow sense (Angemessenheit):** The severity of the interference must not be disproportionate to the importance of the objective pursued.

Deadly force (Schusswaffengebrauch) is regulated as an ultima ratio — a last resort — and each state police law specifies the narrow conditions under which firearms may be discharged.

### Oversight and Accountability

**Polizeibeauftragte des Bundes (Federal Police Commissioner):** On March 15, 2024, Germany established a federal police ombudsperson, with former MP and police officer Uli Groetsch appointed as the first officeholder. This commissioner oversees the Bundespolizei (federal police), Bundeskriminalamt (BKA), and Polizei beim Deutschen Bundestag (parliamentary police). The ombudsperson may analyze patterns, investigate structures, collect data, and open channels for whistleblowers.

**State-Level Ombudspersons:** Several German Laender have established their own police ombudspersons. A distinctive feature of the German system is that these ombudspersons also receive complaints from within the police itself.

**Structural Challenges:** A central dilemma in oversight is limited jurisdiction when parallel criminal or disciplinary proceedings are running against officers, which can prevent ombudspersons from dealing with the most serious misconduct cases.

### Research and Reform

The KviAPol project (Koerperverletzung im Amt durch Polizeibeamt*innen) at Goethe University Frankfurt has conducted systematic academic research into assault in office by police officers, publishing findings on the prevalence, patterns, and prosecution of police violence in Germany. This research has contributed to public discourse about the gap between reported incidents and successful prosecutions.

## ECHR & EU

### Article 3: Absolute Prohibition

Article 3 of the European Convention on Human Rights provides: "No one shall be subjected to torture or to inhuman or degrading treatment or punishment." This prohibition is absolute — it permits no exceptions, no derogations, and no balancing against other interests, even in times of war or public emergency (Article 15(2) ECHR).

The European Court of Human Rights has developed a rich body of case law applying Article 3 to police conduct, establishing a three-tier classification of prohibited treatment:

- **Torture:** Deliberate inhuman treatment causing very serious and cruel suffering, with a purposive element.
- **Inhuman treatment:** Treatment causing intense physical or mental suffering.
- **Degrading treatment:** Treatment arousing feelings of fear, anguish, and inferiority capable of humiliating and debasing the victim.

### Article 2: Right to Life

Where police use of force results in death, the analysis falls under Article 2, which permits the use of lethal force only when it is "absolutely necessary" — a stricter standard than the "reasonable" or "proportionate" tests applied in many domestic jurisdictions.

### Substantive Obligations

States must ensure that:

1. Law enforcement officers do not subject individuals to treatment prohibited by Article 3.
2. Domestic law adequately regulates the use of force by police, including clear rules on the deployment of weapons, crowd-control devices, and restraint techniques.
3. Officers receive adequate training on the permissible limits of force.

### Procedural Obligations

When an individual raises an arguable claim that they have been seriously ill-treated by police in breach of Article 3, the state has a procedural obligation to conduct an effective official investigation. This investigation must be:

- **Independent:** Investigators must have no institutional or hierarchical connection to the officers under investigation.
- **Adequate:** The investigation must be capable of leading to the identification and punishment of those responsible.
- **Prompt:** The investigation must be conducted with reasonable expedition.
- **Subject to public scrutiny:** The victim must have effective access to the investigative procedure.

Failure to meet these procedural requirements constitutes a separate and independent violation of the Convention.

### Landmark Cases

| Case | Year | Key Holding |
|------|------|-------------|
| Ireland v. United Kingdom | 1978 | Defined the distinction between torture and inhuman/degrading treatment; five interrogation techniques constituted inhuman treatment |
| Selmouni v. France | 1999 | Repeated beatings and sexual humiliation during police custody constituted torture; the Court signaled an evolving standard raising the threshold classification |
| Nachova and Others v. Bulgaria | 2005 | States have a duty to investigate possible racist motives behind police use of lethal force (Art. 14 in conjunction with Art. 2); Bulgaria violated right to life through failure to regulate use of firearms by military police |
| Cestaro v. Italy | 2015 | Brutal police raid on school during G8 summit constituted torture under Article 3; Italy's failure to criminalize torture as a specific offense contributed to the violation |
| Rehbock v. Slovenia | 2000 | Excessive force during arrest (broken jaw) violated Article 3; burden shifts to government to provide plausible explanation when individual is injured in police custody |
| Bouyid v. Belgium | 2015 | A single police slap to a detainee constitutes degrading treatment violating Article 3, regardless of limited physical impact |
| Makaratzis v. Greece | 2004 | Unregulated police shooting during car chase violated Article 2; states must have adequate legal and administrative framework regulating use of lethal force |

### EU Charter of Fundamental Rights

Article 4 of the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights mirrors Article 3 ECHR, providing that "No one shall be subjected to torture or to inhuman or degrading treatment or punishment." The Charter applies to EU institutions and to member states when implementing EU law.

### Committee for the Prevention of Torture (CPT)

The European Committee for the Prevention of Torture and Inhuman or Degrading Treatment or Punishment (CPT), established under the 1987 European Convention for the Prevention of Torture, conducts periodic and ad hoc visits to places of detention in Council of Europe member states. CPT reports on police treatment constitute a key accountability mechanism, and the Committee has developed detailed standards on permissible police conduct, including guidelines on the use of electrical discharge weapons, restraint techniques, and conditions during police custody.

## Comparative Analysis

### Defining Excessive Force

The jurisdictions compared employ different standards to determine when police force becomes unlawful:

- **South Korea** applies a "reasonable assessment" standard with an emphasis on necessity and the absence of alternatives, though the 2022 reform expanded police discretion.
- **The United States** applies the "objective reasonableness" standard from Graham v. Connor, focusing on the perspective of a reasonable officer at the scene.
- **Germany** applies the constitutional principle of proportionality (Verhaeltnismaessigkeit), requiring suitability, necessity, and proportionality in the narrow sense.
- **The ECHR** applies the strictest standard: force must be "absolutely necessary" under Article 2 (deadly force) and is absolutely prohibited if it amounts to torture, inhuman, or degrading treatment under Article 3.

### Accountability Gaps

Each jurisdiction exhibits structural accountability gaps:

- **South Korea:** The NHRCK's non-binding recommendations limit its effectiveness. Internal police investigation mechanisms raise independence concerns.
- **United States:** Qualified immunity shields officers from civil liability unless existing case law with nearly identical facts establishes the right. The "willfulness" requirement under 18 U.S.C. Section 242 creates a high criminal prosecution threshold.
- **Germany:** Despite mandatory prosecution under the Legalitaetsprinzip, studies (including the KviAPol project) have documented a significant gap between reported incidents and successful prosecutions of police violence. The new federal ombudsperson's limited jurisdiction in cases involving parallel criminal proceedings is a structural weakness.
- **ECHR:** The Court can only award monetary compensation and cannot directly compel criminal prosecution. Enforcement of judgments depends on the Committee of Ministers and political will of member states.

### Remedies Available to Victims

| Remedy | South Korea | United States | Germany | ECHR |
|--------|-------------|---------------|---------|------|
| Criminal prosecution of officer | Yes (Arts. 123-125) | Yes (18 U.S.C. Section 242) | Yes (Section 340 StGB) | Via domestic systems |
| Civil damages from officer | Limited | Yes (Section 1983) | No (state assumes liability) | N/A |
| Civil damages from state | Yes (State Compensation Act) | Limited (Monell doctrine) | Yes (Amtshaftung) | Just satisfaction (Art. 41) |
| Injunctive relief | Limited | Yes (consent decrees) | Yes (administrative courts) | Binding judgments (Art. 46) |
| Disciplinary action | Yes (internal) | Yes (varies by jurisdiction) | Yes (Disziplinarrecht) | Via domestic systems |
| Ombudsperson complaint | Yes (NHRCK) | Varies by jurisdiction | Yes (Polizeibeauftragte) | CPT visits |

### Structural Differences

The fundamental philosophical divergence lies in who bears liability. Germany and South Korea channel liability primarily through the state, reflecting a public-law tradition in which the individual officer is a manifestation of state authority. The United States, by contrast, permits (and primarily relies upon) personal liability of the individual officer under Section 1983, though qualified immunity significantly narrows this avenue. The ECHR holds the state responsible as a matter of international law, regardless of domestic liability frameworks.
