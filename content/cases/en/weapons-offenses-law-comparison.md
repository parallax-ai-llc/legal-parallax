## Summary

Weapons offenses -- the unlawful manufacture, acquisition, possession, transfer, or use of firearms, explosives, and other regulated weapons -- represent one of the most ideologically divisive areas of criminal law worldwide. South Korea enforces one of the strictest civilian firearms regimes in the world through the Act on the Safety Management of Guns, Swords, and Explosives (chonghpodobomhwayangnyudeungesangjeonggwanhaenbeomnyul), which essentially prohibits all civilian firearm ownership outside narrow categories of licensed hunters and sport shooters, with mandatory police storage of all firearms when not in authorized use. The United States stands as a dramatic outlier, where the Second Amendment to the Constitution has been interpreted by the Supreme Court as protecting an individual right to keep and bear arms, creating a complex interplay between federal statutes such as 18 U.S.C. Sections 922 and 924 and a patchwork of state laws that range from near-total permissiveness to restrictive licensing schemes. Germany occupies a middle ground through its Weapons Act (Waffengesetz, WaffG) of 2002, which permits civilian ownership under a strict licensing regime requiring demonstrated need, reliability, personal aptitude, and technical knowledge, with criminal penalties under Sections 51-52 WaffG for violations. The European framework operates through the EU Firearms Directive (Directive 2021/555), which establishes minimum harmonized standards for weapons classification and acquisition across member states, while the European Convention on Human Rights contains no right to bear arms, instead addressing weapons issues through the lens of the right to life (Article 2) and state obligations to protect public safety.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea maintains one of the world's most restrictive firearms regimes. The primary statute is the Act on the Safety Management of Guns, Swords, and Explosives (ASMGSE), originally enacted as the Gun Control Act of 1962 and substantially amended multiple times, most recently to expand its scope to include electronic shock devices and crossbows. The Act's stated purpose is to "contribute to the maintenance of public safety by preventing in advance any danger and accident likely to be caused by guns, swords, powders, gas sprayers, electronic shock machines and crossbows."

**Scope of Regulated Items:** The Act covers not only conventional firearms but also swords, explosives, gas sprayers, electronic shock devices, crossbows, and even imitation firearms that resemble real weapons. The breadth of this coverage reflects a comprehensive approach to weapons control that extends far beyond firearms alone.

**Authorized Possession:** Civilian firearm ownership is limited to:
- Presidential bodyguards and security personnel for foreign heads of state
- Licensed hunters who have passed physical and mental examinations
- Competitive sport shooters (e.g., Olympic athletes)
- Industrial workers at mining or construction sites requiring blasting equipment

All licensed firearms must be stored at the nearest police station when not in active authorized use. Hunters must retrieve their weapons from the police station before each hunting trip and return them immediately afterward.

### Criminal Offenses and Penalties

| Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|
| Illegal manufacture of firearms | Up to 15 years imprisonment / up to 100 million won fine |
| Illegal sale or purchase of firearms | Up to 10-15 years imprisonment |
| Illegal possession of firearms | Up to 5-15 years imprisonment |
| Possession of imitation firearms resembling real weapons | Up to 3 years imprisonment / up to 7 million won fine |
| Failure to store firearm at police station | License revocation and criminal penalties |
| Carrying a firearm without authorization | Up to 5 years imprisonment |
| Illegal modification of firearms | Up to 7 years imprisonment |

### Key Principles

**Historical context:** South Korea's strict weapons control traces its origins to the Japanese colonial period (1910-1945), when colonial authorities confiscated civilian weapons. Following the Korean War (1950-1953), the government enacted comprehensive legislation to prevent firearms proliferation in a post-conflict society. The Gun Control Act of 1958 laid initial groundwork, and the 1962 Act established the modern framework.

**Police storage mandate:** The requirement that all civilian firearms be stored at police stations when not in active use is among the most restrictive storage provisions in the world. This effectively ensures that no civilian has unsupervised access to a firearm outside of specific licensed activities.

**Broad definition of prohibited items:** Korean law prohibits not only actual firearms but also toy guns and imitation weapons that resemble real firearms. This expansive definition reflects the legislature's concern with even the appearance of weapons in civilian life.

**Comprehensive amendments:** The legislation has been amended in 1984, 1986, 1989, 1991, 1995, 1996, 1997, 1999, 2001, 2003, 2005, 2006, 2008, and 2010, demonstrating continuous legislative refinement. The 1984 amendment significantly increased penalties and banned the manufacture, sale, and possession of toy guns.

### Landmark Cases and Enforcement

Due to the near-total prohibition on civilian firearms, weapons cases in South Korea are relatively rare and typically involve organized crime, defectors, or military personnel. The Korean National Police Agency maintains strict oversight, conducting regular inspections of licensed firearms holders and maintaining a comprehensive registry. South Korea's gun homicide rate is among the lowest in the world, with fewer than 10 firearm deaths per year in a population of over 51 million.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States presents a unique case in global weapons law due to the Second Amendment to the Constitution, which states: "A well regulated Militia, being necessary to the security of a free State, the right of the people to keep and bear Arms, shall not be infringed." This constitutional provision creates a fundamental tension between individual rights and public safety regulation that does not exist in any of the other jurisdictions examined.

**Federal Statutory Framework:**

The primary federal statutes governing weapons offenses are the Gun Control Act of 1968 (GCA), codified principally at 18 U.S.C. Sections 921-931, and the National Firearms Act of 1934 (NFA), codified at 26 U.S.C. Sections 5801-5872.

**18 U.S.C. Section 922 -- Unlawful Acts:** This section establishes the core federal prohibitions, including:
- Section 922(a): Restrictions on interstate firearms commerce, requiring federal firearms licenses (FFLs) for dealers
- Section 922(b): Prohibitions on sales to certain persons (minors, out-of-state residents for handguns)
- Section 922(d): Prohibition on selling to known prohibited persons
- Section 922(g): The "prohibited persons" provision, barring firearm possession by convicted felons, fugitives, unlawful drug users, persons adjudicated mentally defective, undocumented immigrants, persons dishonorably discharged, those who have renounced citizenship, persons under domestic violence restraining orders, and persons convicted of misdemeanor domestic violence
- Section 922(o): Prohibition on possession of machine guns manufactured after May 19, 1986

**18 U.S.C. Section 924 -- Penalties:**

| Offense | Provision | Penalty |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Prohibited person in possession | Section 922(g) | Up to 10 years / $250,000 fine |
| Armed career criminal (3+ prior violent felonies/drug crimes) | Section 924(e) (ACCA) | 15-year mandatory minimum |
| Use of firearm during crime of violence or drug trafficking | Section 924(c)(1)(A) | 5-year mandatory minimum (consecutive) |
| Brandishing firearm during crime of violence | Section 924(c)(1)(A)(ii) | 7-year mandatory minimum |
| Discharging firearm during crime of violence | Section 924(c)(1)(A)(iii) | 10-year mandatory minimum |
| Short-barreled rifle/shotgun, machine gun during crime of violence | Section 924(c)(1)(B)(ii) | 30-year mandatory minimum |
| Firearms trafficking | Section 922(a) | Up to 10-20 years |

**National Firearms Act (NFA):** Regulates certain categories of weapons through a registration and tax system: machine guns, short-barreled rifles (barrel under 16 inches), short-barreled shotguns (barrel under 18 inches), silencers/suppressors, destructive devices, and "any other weapons" (AOWs). Possession of an unregistered NFA item carries penalties of up to 10 years imprisonment and a $250,000 fine under 26 U.S.C. Section 5871.

### Landmark Cases

**District of Columbia v. Heller, 554 U.S. 570 (2008):** The Supreme Court held 5-4 that the Second Amendment protects an individual's right to keep and bear arms unconnected with service in a militia, for traditionally lawful purposes such as self-defense within the home. The Court struck down Washington D.C.'s handgun ban and requirement that lawfully owned rifles and shotguns be kept unloaded and disassembled. Justice Scalia's majority opinion established that the right is not unlimited and does not extend to "dangerous and unusual weapons," preserving the validity of longstanding prohibitions on possession by felons and the mentally ill, and laws forbidding firearms in sensitive places.

**McDonald v. City of Chicago, 561 U.S. 742 (2010):** The Court incorporated the Second Amendment right recognized in Heller against state and local governments through the Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause, striking down Chicago's handgun ban. This decision established that the individual right to bear arms applies not only against the federal government but against all levels of government.

**New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen, 597 U.S. 1 (2022):** In a 6-3 decision, the Court struck down New York's Sullivan Act requirement that concealed carry applicants demonstrate "proper cause" (a special need distinguishable from the general public). The Court established a new framework for evaluating firearms regulations: when the Second Amendment's plain text covers an individual's conduct, the Constitution presumptively protects that conduct, and the government must demonstrate that any regulation is "consistent with this Nation's historical tradition of firearm regulation." This test rejected the means-end scrutiny (intermediate or strict scrutiny) approach previously used by lower courts.

**United States v. Rahimi, 602 U.S. 680 (2024):** The Court ruled 8-1 that 18 U.S.C. Section 922(g)(8) -- prohibiting firearm possession by persons subject to domestic violence restraining orders -- is consistent with the Second Amendment. Chief Justice Roberts, writing for the majority, held that temporary disarmament of individuals found by a court to pose a credible threat to the physical safety of another is supported by the Nation's historical tradition of firearm regulation. The decision refined the Bruen framework, clarifying that historical analogues need not be identical but must share similar principles.

### Federal Background Check System

The National Instant Criminal Background Check System (NICS), administered by the FBI, is required for all sales through federally licensed dealers. However, private sales between individuals are not subject to federal background check requirements in many states, creating what critics call the "private sale loophole." Some states have enacted universal background check laws to close this gap.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany regulates weapons through two principal statutes: the Weapons Act (Waffengesetz, WaffG) of 2002 and the War Weapons Control Act (Kriegswaffenkontrollgesetz, KrWaffKontrG) of 1961. The WaffG governs civilian weapons possession, while the KrWaffKontrG controls military-grade weapons and implements Germany's obligations under international arms control treaties.

**Weapons Act (Waffengesetz) Structure:**

The WaffG classifies weapons into categories based on the degree of regulation required:
- **Prohibited weapons (Annex 2, Section 1):** Fully automatic firearms, disguised firearms, weapons designed to circumvent detection, certain knives (e.g., butterfly knives, spring-loaded knives with blades over 8.5 cm), brass knuckles, and other items
- **Weapons requiring a license (Annex 2, Section 2):** Semi-automatic firearms, revolvers, pistols, rifles, shotguns -- possession requires a Waffenbesitzkarte (weapons possession card); carrying in public requires a Waffenschein (weapons carry permit)
- **Weapons requiring notification:** Certain air guns, gas signal weapons, and others that must be reported but do not require a full license

**Licensing Requirements (Sections 4-10 WaffG):**

To obtain a Waffenbesitzkarte, an applicant must demonstrate:
1. **Age:** At least 18 years (21 for certain categories)
2. **Reliability (Zuverlaessigkeit, Section 5):** No criminal convictions in the prior 10 years for offenses carrying penalties over 60 daily rates or 2 months imprisonment; no membership in organizations pursuing unconstitutional aims
3. **Personal aptitude (Persoenliche Eignung, Section 6):** Physical and mental fitness, including no evidence of drug or alcohol dependency
4. **Technical knowledge (Sachkunde, Section 7):** Demonstrated competence in safe handling, legal requirements, and ballistics
5. **Need (Beduerfnis, Section 8):** A recognized legitimate purpose such as hunting (Jagd), sport shooting (Sportschieszen), collecting, or professional need

### Criminal Offenses and Penalties

| Offense | WaffG Section | Penalty |
|---------|---------------|---------|
| Illegal manufacture, possession, or transfer of prohibited weapons | Section 51(1) | 1-5 years imprisonment |
| Illegal manufacture, possession, or transfer of firearms without license | Section 52(1) | Up to 5 years or fine |
| Particularly serious cases (commercial trafficking, organized crime, gang-related) | Section 51(2) | 1-10 years imprisonment |
| Illegal import/export of firearms | Section 52(1) | Up to 5 years or fine |
| Particularly serious import/export cases | Section 52(2) | 1-10 years imprisonment |
| Carrying weapons in public without Waffenschein | Section 52(3) | Up to 3 years or fine |
| Administrative violations (improper storage, failure to report) | Section 53 | Fine up to 10,000 euros |
| War weapons violations (KrWaffKontrG) | Sections 19-22a KrWaffKontrG | 1-5 years (up to 10 for serious cases) |

### Storage Requirements (Section 36 WaffG)

Firearms must be stored in approved gun safes meeting European standard EN 1143-1 (security level 0 or higher for up to 10 long guns; level A or higher if short guns or more than 10 firearms are stored). Ammunition must be stored separately or in the same safe if it meets a higher security level. Police may conduct unannounced inspections to verify compliance.

### Landmark Cases and Developments

**Winnenden school shooting (2009):** On March 11, 2009, a 17-year-old killed 15 people and himself using his father's legally owned Beretta 92FS pistol, which had not been properly secured. The tragedy led to the 2009 amendment of the Weapons Act, which introduced mandatory unannounced police inspections of firearms storage (Section 36(3) WaffG), raised the minimum age for large-caliber sport shooting to 18, established a national weapons registry (Nationales Waffenregister), and banned paintball guns resembling real firearms.

**Duesseldorf Administrative Court ruling on AfD membership (2024):** The Verwaltungsgericht Duesseldorf ruled that membership in the Alternative fuer Deutschland (AfD) party -- classified as a suspected case of anti-constitutional activity by the Federal Office for the Protection of the Constitution (Bundesamt fuer Verfassungsschutz) -- constitutes grounds for finding a person unreliable (unzuverlaessig) under Section 5 WaffG. The court held that this interpretation does not violate party privilege under Article 21 of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz). This ruling established that political affiliations deemed constitutionally suspect can disqualify an individual from firearms ownership.

**Federal Administrative Court, BVerwG 6 C 24/06:** The Bundesverwaltungsgericht addressed the scope of the reliability requirement under the Weapons Act, establishing that weapons authorities have broad discretion in assessing whether an applicant poses risks to public safety, and that doubt is to be resolved against the applicant.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Firearms Directive Framework

The European Union's approach to weapons regulation operates through harmonization directives that establish minimum standards while leaving implementation and specific penalties to member states.

**Directive (EU) 2021/555 (codified Firearms Directive):**

This directive, which consolidated and replaced earlier directives (91/477/EEC and 2008/51/EC as amended by 2017/853), establishes minimum common rules on the acquisition and possession of civilian firearms within the EU. It applies since April 26, 2021.

**Weapons Classification:**

| Category | Description | Regulatory Status |
|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| Category A (Prohibited) | Explosive military missiles/launchers; automatic firearms; firearms disguised as other objects; ammunition with penetrating, explosive, or incendiary projectiles; semi-automatic firearms converted from automatic; certain high-capacity semi-automatics | Prohibited for civilians (limited exceptions for museums, dealers, national defense) |
| Category B (Subject to authorization) | Semi-automatic or repeating short firearms; single-shot short firearms with center-fire percussion; single-shot short firearms with rimfire percussion (overall length < 28 cm); semi-automatic long firearms with magazines >3 rounds; repeating and semi-automatic long firearms with smooth-bore barrels not exceeding 60 cm | Acquisition and possession require authorization |
| Category C (Subject to declaration) | Repeating long firearms other than Category B; single-shot long firearms with rifled barrels; semi-automatic long firearms not in Category A or B; deactivated firearms; other long firearms | Must be declared to competent authorities |

**2017 Amendment (Directive 2017/853):** Following terrorist attacks in Paris (2015) and other European cities, the EU tightened the Firearms Directive to:
- Reclassify certain semi-automatic firearms (those converted from automatic, or with high-capacity magazines) into Category A (prohibited)
- Strengthen controls on deactivated firearms to prevent reactivation
- Improve marking and traceability requirements
- Enhance information exchange between member states through the European Firearms Information System
- Require regular medical assessments for firearms holders

**Proposed Firearms Trafficking Directive (February 2026):** The European Commission proposed a new directive to combat firearms trafficking and related offenses as part of the Protect EU European Internal Security Strategy. Key provisions include:

| Offense | Proposed Minimum Maximum Penalty |
|---------|----------------------------------|
| Trafficking and manufacturing of illicit firearms | At least 8 years imprisonment |
| Possession of illicit firearms, essential components, and ammunition | At least 5 years imprisonment |
| Marking tampering (falsifying, removing, or altering firearm markings) | At least 4 years imprisonment |
| 3D printing of firearms (illicit creation, acquisition, possession, dissemination of blueprints) | At least 2 years imprisonment |

### ECHR Framework

The European Convention on Human Rights does not contain any right to bear arms. Unlike the U.S. Second Amendment, the Convention treats weapons regulation as squarely within the state's margin of appreciation regarding public safety. Weapons issues before the European Court of Human Rights arise primarily through:

**Article 2 -- Right to Life:** States have a positive obligation to take appropriate steps to safeguard the lives of those within their jurisdiction. This includes regulatory frameworks to prevent foreseeable threats, which courts have interpreted as supporting robust weapons regulation. The obligation extends to both preventing unlawful use of weapons by private actors and regulating the use of lethal force by state agents.

**Article 8 -- Right to Private and Family Life:** While Article 8 protects a broad right to private life, the ECHR has never held that it encompasses a right to possess weapons. Restrictions on firearms possession have been upheld as justified interference with private life in the interests of public safety under Article 8(2).

**Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 -- Protection of Property:** Confiscation or revocation of weapons licenses may engage property rights, but such measures are consistently upheld as lawful controls on the use of property in the general interest.

**Margin of Appreciation:** The Court affords states a wide margin of appreciation in firearms regulation, recognizing that national authorities are better placed to assess the specific public safety needs of their jurisdictions. No ECHR case has ever established a Convention right to civilian firearm ownership.

### CJEU Firearms Directive Jurisprudence

**Czech Republic v. European Parliament and Council of the European Union (Case C-482/17, 2019):** The Czech Republic challenged the legality of Directive 2017/853, arguing that the EU lacked competence to impose the restrictions and that the measures were disproportionate. The Court of Justice of the European Union dismissed the action in its entirety, upholding the directive as a valid internal market measure under Article 114 TFEU. The Court held that the EU legislature had wide discretion in pursuing the objective of combating the illicit trafficking of weapons and that the restrictions on civilian firearms were proportionate to the legitimate aim of improving public safety.

## Comparative Analysis

### Philosophical Foundations

The four jurisdictions reflect fundamentally different philosophical approaches to the relationship between citizens and weapons:

**South Korea** operates from a premise of near-total state monopoly on armed force, rooted in historical experiences of colonial occupation, civil war, and the ongoing military standoff with North Korea. Civilian disarmament is treated as essential to internal security and social order.

**The United States** uniquely treats civilian arms possession as a constitutional right, rooted in English common law, colonial experience, and revolutionary-era distrust of standing armies. The Supreme Court's decisions in Heller, McDonald, and Bruen have progressively expanded the recognized scope of this right while acknowledging that it is not unlimited.

**Germany** adopts a regulatory middle ground, recognizing that civilian firearms ownership can serve legitimate purposes (hunting, sport, collecting) while maintaining that access must be carefully controlled through licensing. The concept of Zuverlaessigkeit (reliability) places the burden on the applicant to demonstrate fitness for weapons ownership, reflecting a presumption that weapons possession is a privilege rather than a right.

**The ECHR and EU** framework treats weapons regulation as a matter of public safety policy within the sovereign discretion of states, subject only to general human rights constraints. The EU's harmonization efforts focus on internal market consistency and cross-border security rather than individual rights.

### Regulatory Intensity Spectrum

From most to least restrictive:
1. **South Korea:** Near-total prohibition; mandatory police storage; extremely limited licensing
2. **Germany:** Strict licensing with demonstrated need; mandatory secure storage; regular inspections
3. **EU framework:** Minimum harmonized standards with category-based restrictions; member states may exceed minimum requirements
4. **United States:** Constitutional right; federal prohibited-persons framework; highly variable state-level regulation

### Enforcement Approaches

**Proactive vs. reactive:** South Korea and Germany emphasize proactive prevention through licensing, storage requirements, and inspections. The United States relies more heavily on reactive enforcement -- punishing possession by prohibited persons after the fact rather than preventing initial acquisition through universal licensing.

**Penalty severity:** South Korea imposes the most severe penalties (up to 15 years for illegal manufacture or possession). The United States federal system provides up to 10 years for prohibited-person possession, with significant enhancements for use during violent crimes. Germany's penalties are moderate by comparison (up to 5 years for standard violations, up to 10 for serious cases). The proposed EU Trafficking Directive would require member states to set maximum penalties of at least 8 years for trafficking.

### Impact on Public Safety Outcomes

The divergence in regulatory approaches correlates with dramatically different outcomes in firearms-related violence. South Korea records fewer than 10 gun deaths per year. Germany experiences approximately 700-800 firearms deaths annually (largely suicides). The United States records over 45,000 firearms deaths annually (including approximately 20,000 suicides and 20,000 homicides). While correlation does not establish causation and numerous social, cultural, and economic factors contribute to these outcomes, the data illustrates the practical significance of the regulatory choices examined.
