## Summary

Change of venue -- the transfer of legal proceedings from one court or jurisdiction to another, whether within a single legal system or across borders -- implicates fundamental questions of judicial efficiency, party convenience, jurisdictional authority, and the appropriate allocation of disputes among courts, with different legal systems employing markedly different doctrines and mechanisms to resolve venue challenges. South Korea addresses venue and jurisdictional transfer through the Code of Civil Procedure (민사소송법), which establishes general venue rules based on the defendant's domicile (Articles 2-7), special venue provisions for particular types of claims (Articles 8-24), and mechanisms for transfer of venue (이송) under Articles 34-36 where the court finds that another court would be more appropriate; Korean law does not recognize the common law doctrine of forum non conveniens, but achieves similar results through its statutory transfer provisions and the Supreme Court's jurisprudence on international jurisdiction (국제재판관할권). The United States employs a complex, multi-layered venue system, with venue governed by 28 U.S.C. § 1391 in federal courts and analogous state statutes in state courts; transfer of venue is authorized by 28 U.S.C. § 1404(a) for cases filed in a proper venue ("for the convenience of parties and witnesses, in the interest of justice") and by 28 U.S.C. § 1406(a) for cases filed in an improper venue; the common law doctrine of forum non conveniens, as articulated in Gulf Oil Corp. v. Gilbert (330 U.S. 501, 1947) and Piper Aircraft Co. v. Reyno (454 U.S. 235, 1981), permits dismissal in favor of a more appropriate foreign forum. Germany governs venue (Zuständigkeit) through the ZPO and specialized legislation, distinguishing between subject-matter jurisdiction (sachliche Zuständigkeit), territorial jurisdiction (örtliche Zuständigkeit, ZPO §§ 12-37), and international jurisdiction (internationale Zuständigkeit); transfer of venue (Verweisung) is governed by ZPO § 281 when the court to which a case was brought is incompetent, and German law does not recognize forum non conveniens, treating jurisdiction as either existing or not under the applicable rules. The ECHR examines venue and jurisdictional allocation under Article 6(1) (right to a fair trial and access to court), requiring that venue rules not impose disproportionate barriers to justice, while EU Regulation 1215/2012 (Brussels I Recast) establishes comprehensive jurisdiction rules for civil and commercial matters within the EU, including the actor sequitur forum rei principle (Article 4), special jurisdiction provisions (Articles 7-9), exclusive jurisdiction rules (Article 24), and the lis pendens mechanism (Articles 29-34) for resolving parallel proceedings in multiple member states.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Korean venue law is primarily codified in the Code of Civil Procedure (민사소송법), supplemented by the Court Organization Act (법원조직법) for subject-matter jurisdiction and by Supreme Court precedent for international jurisdiction.

### General Venue Rules

**Article 2 (General Venue):** The general venue for lawsuits is the court having jurisdiction over the defendant's domicile (보통재판적). This reflects the fundamental principle of actor sequitur forum rei -- the plaintiff must sue where the defendant is located -- which protects defendants from being forced to litigate in inconvenient or distant locations.

**Article 3 (Domicile of Legal Persons):** For legal persons, the general venue is the location of their principal office or business establishment.

### Special Venue Provisions

The Code provides special venue rules for specific types of claims, giving the plaintiff additional venue options:

**Article 8 (Property Claims):** Claims relating to property may be filed at the location of the property.

**Article 11 (Tort Claims):** Tort claims may be filed at the place where the tortious act occurred (불법행위지).

**Article 12 (Contract Claims):** Claims arising from contractual obligations may be filed at the place of performance (의무이행지).

**Article 14 (Consumer Claims):** Consumer protection claims may be filed at the consumer's domicile, providing protective venue for consumers.

### Transfer of Venue (이송)

**Article 34 (Transfer for Lack of Jurisdiction):** When a court determines it lacks subject-matter or territorial jurisdiction, it must transfer the case to the competent court by decision (결정). The transfer is binding on the receiving court.

**Article 35 (Discretionary Transfer):** Even when a court has jurisdiction, it may transfer the case to another court with jurisdiction if the transfer would serve the interests of justice, considering factors including: convenience of the parties and witnesses; location of evidence; the connection between the dispute and the proposed venue; and the efficient administration of justice. This provision serves a function analogous to the U.S. § 1404(a) transfer.

**Article 36 (Transfer by Agreement):** When parties agree on a venue, the court may transfer the case to the agreed venue. Conversely, when parties have agreed on an exclusive venue, a court that lacks the agreed jurisdiction must transfer the case.

### Forum Selection Agreements

**Article 29 (Jurisdiction by Agreement):** Parties may agree in advance on the court that will have jurisdiction over disputes arising from a specific legal relationship. The agreement must specify the legal relationship and may be exclusive or non-exclusive. However, jurisdiction agreements are subject to restrictions: they must be in writing (or recorded electronically); they cannot contravene exclusive jurisdiction rules; and agreements in adhesion contracts (약관) are subject to review under the Act on the Regulation of Terms and Conditions (약관의 규제에 관한 법률).

### International Jurisdiction

Korean law does not have a comprehensive statute on international jurisdiction. The Supreme Court has developed rules through case law, applying the principle that Korean courts have international jurisdiction when there is a "substantial connection" (실질적 관련성) between the dispute and Korea. In Decision 2009Da22549 (2012), the Supreme Court established a framework for analyzing international jurisdiction that considers: the defendant's domicile or place of business in Korea; the place of performance of the obligation; the location of the tortious act; and the reasonable expectations of the parties.

The Korean Supreme Court does not recognize forum non conveniens as a basis for declining jurisdiction. In Decision 2005Da75071 (2008), the Court held that when Korean courts have jurisdiction under applicable rules, they may not decline to exercise it on the ground that a foreign forum would be more convenient. This position reflects the civil law tradition's view of jurisdiction as a legal right rather than a discretionary allocation.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The U.S. venue system operates at three levels: constitutional limitations on personal jurisdiction (Due Process Clause); statutory venue provisions (28 U.S.C. § 1391 and state analogues); and discretionary transfer or dismissal doctrines.

### Federal Venue Statute (28 U.S.C. § 1391)

As amended in 2011, the general venue statute provides that a civil action may be brought in:

**(b)(1):** A judicial district in which any defendant resides, if all defendants reside in the same state.

**(b)(2):** A judicial district in which a substantial part of the events or omissions giving rise to the claim occurred, or a substantial part of property that is the subject of the action is situated.

**(b)(3):** If there is no district in which the action may otherwise be brought, any judicial district in which any defendant is subject to personal jurisdiction.

### Transfer of Venue

**28 U.S.C. § 1404(a) (Transfer from Proper Venue):** "For the convenience of parties and witnesses, in the interest of justice, a district court may transfer any civil action to any other district or division where it might have been brought or to any district or division to which all parties have consented."

The Supreme Court in Atlantic Marine Construction Co. v. U.S. District Court, 571 U.S. 49 (2013), clarified that when a valid forum-selection clause exists, the calculus under § 1404(a) changes: the plaintiff's choice of forum merits no weight, and the party acting in violation of the clause bears the burden of showing that public-interest factors overwhelmingly disfavor transfer.

**28 U.S.C. § 1406(a) (Transfer from Improper Venue):** When a case is filed in the wrong venue, the court may dismiss or, in the interest of justice, transfer it to a proper venue.

### Forum Non Conveniens

**Gulf Oil Corp. v. Gilbert, 330 U.S. 501 (1947):** The Supreme Court established the modern doctrine of forum non conveniens, permitting dismissal of cases where a foreign court would be a substantially more convenient forum. The Court identified private-interest factors (ease of access to evidence, availability of compulsory process for witnesses, cost of obtaining witnesses, practical problems) and public-interest factors (administrative difficulties, local interest in the controversy, applicable law, burden on the community).

**Piper Aircraft Co. v. Reyno, 454 U.S. 235 (1981):** The Court held that a change in applicable law following dismissal on forum non conveniens grounds should not be given conclusive or even substantial weight. The Court also established that a foreign plaintiff's choice of forum is entitled to less deference than a domestic plaintiff's choice.

**Sinochem International Co. v. Malaysia International Shipping Corp., 549 U.S. 422 (2007):** The Court held that a federal court may dismiss on forum non conveniens grounds without first determining whether it has subject-matter or personal jurisdiction.

### Forum Selection Clauses

**The Bremen v. Zapata Off-Shore Co. (M/S Bremen), 407 U.S. 1 (1972):** The Supreme Court held that forum selection clauses are "prima facie valid and should be enforced unless enforcement is shown by the resisting party to be 'unreasonable' under the circumstances." This decision marked a shift from the historical American hostility toward forum selection clauses.

**Carnival Cruise Lines, Inc. v. Shute, 499 U.S. 585 (1991):** The Court enforced a forum selection clause in a consumer contract (cruise ticket), though the decision has been partially constrained by subsequent legislation and state consumer protection laws.

### Multidistrict Litigation (MDL)

**28 U.S.C. § 1407:** The Judicial Panel on Multidistrict Litigation may transfer civil actions involving one or more common questions of fact pending in different districts to a single district for coordinated or consolidated pretrial proceedings. MDL has become the predominant mechanism for managing mass tort and complex litigation in federal courts, with over 50% of the federal civil docket now comprised of MDL cases.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German venue law (Zuständigkeitsrecht) is primarily governed by the ZPO, the Court Organization Act (Gerichtsverfassungsgesetz, GVG), and for cross-border disputes involving EU-domiciled defendants, the Brussels I Recast Regulation.

### Territorial Jurisdiction (Örtliche Zuständigkeit)

**ZPO § 12 (General Venue):** The general venue is determined by the defendant's domicile (Wohnsitz). For legal persons, § 17 provides that the general venue is at the seat (Sitz) of the entity.

**ZPO § 13 (Domicile):** A person's domicile for venue purposes is the place where they have established their permanent residence.

### Special Venue Rules

The ZPO provides numerous special venue provisions:

**ZPO § 20 (Property Venue):** Claims relating to property located in Germany may be brought at the location of the property.

**ZPO § 29 (Place of Performance):** Contract claims may be brought at the place of performance of the disputed obligation.

**ZPO § 29c (Consumer Venue):** Actions against consumers on claims arising from contracts within the scope of the consumer protection provisions must be brought at the consumer's domicile. This protective provision cannot be derogated by agreement.

**ZPO § 32 (Tort Venue):** Tort claims may be brought at the place where the tortious act was committed (Begehungsort), which includes both the place of the wrongful act and the place where the injury was suffered.

**ZPO § 32b (Capital Markets Venue):** Claims arising from false or misleading capital market information may be brought at the seat of the issuer, providing a centralized venue for securities litigation.

### Verweisung (Transfer of Venue, ZPO § 281)

When a court determines that it lacks territorial or subject-matter jurisdiction, it must, upon the plaintiff's motion, transfer the case to the competent court by binding order (bindender Verweisungsbeschluss). Key features:

**Binding Effect:** The transfer order is binding on the receiving court, which may not refuse to accept the case or transfer it back. This principle, established in BGH decisions, prevents jurisdictional ping-pong between courts.

**No Discretionary Transfer:** Unlike U.S. law, German law does not provide for discretionary transfer of cases from a court that has jurisdiction to another court that would be more convenient. The German view is that venue rules already embody the legislature's determination of the appropriate court, and individual judicial discretion in venue allocation would undermine legal certainty.

### Prorogation (Jurisdiction Agreements, ZPO § 38)

**ZPO § 38(1):** A court that would otherwise lack territorial jurisdiction may acquire it through the parties' agreement (Gerichtsstandsvereinbarung), provided that: the agreement refers to a specific legal relationship; it is concluded after the dispute has arisen, or the party against whom the agreement is invoked is a merchant (Kaufmann), a legal person under public law, or a special fund under public law.

**ZPO § 38(3):** Jurisdiction agreements must be in writing or, if concluded orally, must be confirmed in writing.

**Restrictions:** Jurisdiction agreements may not derogate from exclusive jurisdiction rules (ausschließliche Zuständigkeit), such as those for real property claims (ZPO § 24) or certain corporate law matters. Consumer and employment jurisdiction protections cannot be overridden by prorogation.

### Rejection of Forum Non Conveniens

German law does not recognize the doctrine of forum non conveniens. The Bundesgerichtshof (BGH) has consistently held that when a German court has jurisdiction under applicable rules, it must exercise that jurisdiction and may not decline to do so on grounds of convenience. This position was confirmed in BGH Decision XII ZR 99/06 (2007), where the Court rejected the argument that German courts should decline jurisdiction over an international family law dispute in favor of courts in another country.

The theoretical justification for this position is that jurisdiction in the German system is a matter of law, not discretion. Venue rules reflect objective criteria established by the legislature, and judicial refusal to exercise jurisdiction would introduce unacceptable legal uncertainty.

### International Jurisdiction

For defendants domiciled in EU member states, international jurisdiction is governed by the Brussels I Recast Regulation. For defendants domiciled outside the EU, German courts apply the ZPO's venue rules by analogy (doppelfunktionale Zuständigkeitsnormen), treating the ZPO's territorial jurisdiction provisions as simultaneously governing international jurisdiction.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Article 6(1) and Venue

The European Court of Human Rights has examined venue rules under the right to a fair trial and the right of access to court.

**Golder v. United Kingdom (Application No. 4451/70, 1975):** The foundational decision establishing that Article 6(1) guarantees not merely the right to a fair hearing in pending proceedings but the right of access to court itself. Venue rules that effectively prevent a party from accessing any court may violate this right.

**Markovic and Others v. Italy (Application No. 1398/03, Grand Chamber, 2006):** The Court held that while Article 6(1) does not guarantee access to a court that can annul or override legislation, jurisdictional rules that categorically exclude judicial review of certain types of claims may violate the right of access to court.

**Tserkva Sela Sosulivky v. Ukraine (Application No. 37878/02, 2008):** The Court examined whether territorial jurisdiction rules that required the plaintiff to litigate in a distant court imposed disproportionate barriers to justice, emphasizing that venue rules must be applied in a manner consistent with effective access to court.

### EU Regulation 1215/2012 (Brussels I Recast)

The Brussels I Recast is the cornerstone of EU jurisdiction and enforcement law in civil and commercial matters. Its venue provisions are mandatory and directly applicable in all EU member states (except Denmark, which participates through a parallel agreement).

**Article 4 (General Jurisdiction):** Persons domiciled in a member state must be sued in the courts of that member state, regardless of nationality. This codifies the actor sequitur forum rei principle.

**Articles 7-9 (Special Jurisdiction):** The plaintiff may also sue in: the place of performance of the obligation (Art. 7(1)); the place where the harmful event occurred for tort claims (Art. 7(2)); the place of the branch or agency for disputes arising from its operations (Art. 7(5)).

**Article 24 (Exclusive Jurisdiction):** Certain matters are subject to exclusive jurisdiction regardless of domicile, including: proceedings concerning rights in rem in immovable property (Art. 24(1)); proceedings concerning the validity of constitution, nullity, or dissolution of companies (Art. 24(2)); and proceedings concerning the validity of entries in public registers (Art. 24(3)).

**Article 25 (Prorogation):** Parties may agree on the jurisdiction of courts of a member state, provided the agreement is in writing (or evidenced in writing), consistent with established practices between the parties, or in international trade, consistent with a usage of which the parties are or ought to be aware. The chosen court has exclusive jurisdiction unless the parties have agreed otherwise.

### Lis Pendens and Related Actions (Articles 29-34)

**Article 29 (Lis Pendens):** When proceedings involving the same cause of action and between the same parties are brought in the courts of different member states, the court second seised must stay its proceedings until the court first seised has established jurisdiction. If the court first seised establishes jurisdiction, the court second seised must decline jurisdiction.

**Article 30 (Related Actions):** Where related actions are pending in different member states, any court other than the court first seised may stay its proceedings or, upon application of one party, decline jurisdiction if the court first seised has jurisdiction and its law permits consolidation.

**Torpedo Actions:** The lis pendens rule has been criticized for enabling "torpedo actions" -- tactical proceedings filed in slow-moving jurisdictions to block litigation in other member states. The Brussels I Recast partially addressed this problem through Article 31(2), which provides that when an exclusive choice-of-court agreement designates a particular court, that court has priority regardless of the order in which proceedings were commenced.

### Protective Jurisdiction (Insurance, Consumer, Employment)

The Brussels I Recast provides special jurisdiction rules protecting weaker parties:

**Articles 10-16 (Insurance):** Policyholders, insured persons, and beneficiaries may sue insurers in the courts of their own domicile.

**Articles 17-19 (Consumer Contracts):** Consumers may sue the other party in the consumer's domicile, and may only be sued in their own domicile. These protections apply when the professional directs activities to the consumer's member state.

**Articles 20-23 (Employment Contracts):** Employees may sue employers in the courts of the place where they habitually carry out their work, and may only be sued in their domicile.

## Comparative Analysis

### Forum Non Conveniens: The Great Divide

The most striking divergence between the systems is the treatment of forum non conveniens. The United States embraces the doctrine as a flexible tool for managing international litigation. Germany and South Korea reject it entirely, treating jurisdiction as a binary legal determination. The EU Brussels I Recast regime effectively eliminates forum non conveniens within the EU, as jurisdiction rules are mandatory and do not permit courts to decline jurisdiction on convenience grounds. The CJEU confirmed in Owusu v. Jackson (C-281/02, 2005) that the Brussels Convention (predecessor to the Recast) precludes the application of forum non conveniens doctrine.

### Mandatory vs. Discretionary Venue Rules

The systems differ in the degree of judicial discretion permitted in venue determinations. The U.S. system, with its § 1404(a) transfer provision and forum non conveniens doctrine, gives judges substantial discretion. The German system minimizes discretion through binding, legislatively determined venue rules. The EU Brussels I Recast similarly limits discretion through mandatory rules. Korea occupies an intermediate position, providing discretionary transfer but not forum non conveniens.

### Protection of Weaker Parties

All four systems recognize the need to protect weaker parties (consumers, employees, policyholders) from unfavorable venue choices imposed by stronger contracting parties. The EU Brussels I Recast provides the most comprehensive protective regime, with specific sections devoted to insurance, consumer, and employment jurisdiction. Korean law provides consumer-protective venue rules. U.S. law achieves similar protection through a combination of statutory provisions and judicial hostility toward unconscionable forum selection clauses in consumer contracts.

### Parallel Proceedings and Jurisdictional Conflicts

The problem of parallel proceedings in multiple jurisdictions is addressed differently. The EU's lis pendens mechanism provides a clear, priority-based rule (first seised prevails). The U.S. relies on a combination of forum non conveniens, anti-suit injunctions, and comity principles. Korea and Germany, lacking forum non conveniens, face particular challenges when parallel proceedings arise in non-EU international contexts.
