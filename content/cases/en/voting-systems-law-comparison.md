## Summary

Voting systems -- the methods by which votes are cast, counted, and translated into seats or outcomes -- form the structural foundation of representative democracy, profoundly shaping party systems, legislative composition, and government formation. South Korea employs a mixed-member system for its National Assembly (국회) under the Public Official Election Act (공직선거법), combining 253 single-member constituency seats elected by first-past-the-post with 46 proportional representation seats allocated through a parallel system modified in 2020 by the introduction of a semi-linked mixed-member proportional (준연동형 비례대표제) mechanism that was intended to benefit smaller parties but was effectively circumvented by major parties creating satellite parties; the President is elected by a simple plurality national vote under Article 67 of the Constitution. The United States relies predominantly on first-past-the-post (FPTP) plurality voting for nearly all federal and state elections, with the Electoral College providing an additional structural layer for presidential elections under Article II and the Twelfth Amendment; however, ranked-choice voting (RCV) has been adopted for federal elections in Alaska and Maine, and for local elections in over fifty U.S. cities as of 2026, representing the most significant shift in American voting methodology in a century. Germany pioneered the mixed-member proportional (MMP) system (personalisierte Verhältniswahl) under the Federal Election Act (Bundeswahlgesetz), combining 299 constituency seats with proportional representation seats to ensure that each party's total Bundestag representation reflects its share of second votes (Zweitstimmen), subject to a 5% threshold or three constituency wins; the 2023 electoral reform (Wahlrechtsreform) eliminated overhang mandates (Überhangmandate) and capped Bundestag membership at 630 seats, prompting a Federal Constitutional Court challenge. At the European level, the European Parliament is elected under a proportional representation framework mandated by the 1976 Direct Elections Act as amended, with member states retaining significant discretion over specific methods; the European Court of Human Rights has established through Protocol 1, Article 3 that states enjoy a wide margin of appreciation in designing electoral systems but must ensure that the chosen system does not impair the essence of the right to free elections.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's electoral system is governed primarily by the Public Official Election Act (공직선거법), the Political Parties Act (정당법), and the Political Funds Act (정치자금법), with constitutional foundations in Articles 41 (National Assembly composition) and 67 (presidential election).

**National Assembly Elections:** The National Assembly consists of 300 members, of which 253 are elected from single-member constituencies by first-past-the-post plurality and 46 are elected through proportional representation from closed party lists. The 2020 reform introduced a semi-linked mixed-member proportional system (준연동형 비례대표제), which was designed to allocate a portion of proportional seats in a compensatory manner to correct disproportionality arising from constituency results.

**Presidential Elections:** The President is elected by a simple plurality of the national popular vote under Article 67(1) of the Constitution. There is no runoff provision; the candidate receiving the most votes wins regardless of the margin. This system has produced presidents with relatively low vote shares -- Roh Tae-woo won the 1987 election with just 36.6% of the vote in a four-candidate field.

### 2020 Electoral Reform and Satellite Parties

The 2020 introduction of the semi-linked MMP system represented a major structural change. Under the new formula, 30 of the 46 proportional seats were allocated using a compensation mechanism: parties that won fewer constituency seats than their proportional vote share warranted would receive additional proportional seats to partially close the gap.

However, the reform was effectively neutralized by the creation of "satellite parties" (위성정당). Both the ruling Democratic Party and the opposition People Power Party created affiliated proportional representation-only parties -- the Platform Party (더불어시민당) and the Future Korea Party (미래한국당) respectively -- that existed solely to capture proportional seats without constituency victories diluting their compensatory allocation. The National Election Commission condemned the practice, and the Constitutional Court considered but did not invalidate it in a 2020 decision, finding no explicit constitutional prohibition.

### Election Administration

The National Election Commission (중앙선거관리위원회) is a constitutionally independent body established under Chapter VII of the Constitution (Articles 114-116). It administers all elections and referendums, regulates political parties and campaign finance, and adjudicates election disputes at the administrative level. The NEC has been recognized internationally for its efficient election administration, including same-day vote counting and rapid results reporting.

### Voting Technology

South Korea uses paper ballots marked manually and counted by hand at polling stations, with electronic optical scanners used for verification in some locations. The NEC has conducted pilot programs for electronic voting in local elections and organizational elections but has not implemented electronic voting for national elections, citing security and transparency concerns.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States employs a patchwork of voting systems reflecting the constitutional delegation of election administration to the states under Article I, Section 4 (the Elections Clause) and the Tenth Amendment.

**Federal Elections:**
- **Presidential:** The Electoral College system, established by Article II and modified by the Twelfth Amendment (1804), allocates 538 electors among the states based on their congressional representation. All states except Maine and Nebraska use winner-take-all allocation; Maine and Nebraska allocate electors by congressional district. A candidate must receive 270 electoral votes to win. The system has produced five presidents who lost the popular vote, most recently Donald Trump in 2016.
- **Senate:** Two senators per state, elected by statewide plurality vote under the Seventeenth Amendment (1913).
- **House of Representatives:** 435 members elected from single-member districts by plurality vote. Districts are reapportioned following each decennial census under 2 U.S.C. Section 2a.

**Help America Vote Act (HAVA, 2002):** Enacted in response to the 2000 presidential election controversy, HAVA established the Election Assistance Commission, mandated statewide voter registration databases, required provisional balloting, and set accessibility standards for voting systems. HAVA allocated $3.9 billion for election administration improvements.

### Ranked-Choice Voting Movement

The most significant voting system innovation in the United States is the adoption of ranked-choice voting (also called instant-runoff voting):

| Jurisdiction | Year Adopted | Scope | Method |
|-------------|-------------|-------|--------|
| Maine | 2016 (referendum) | Federal and state primary/general elections | Instant-runoff (sequential elimination) |
| Alaska | 2020 (referendum) | Federal and state general elections (with top-four open primary) | Instant-runoff following open primary |
| New York City | 2019 (referendum) | Municipal primary and special elections | Instant-runoff |
| 50+ cities | Various | Municipal elections | Instant-runoff |

**Alaska's Top-Four System:** Alaska's 2020 Ballot Measure 2 introduced a unique combination: an open top-four primary in which all candidates regardless of party appear on a single ballot and the top four advance, followed by a ranked-choice general election. The system produced notable results in 2022 when Democrat Mary Peltola won Alaska's at-large House seat, defeating former governor Sarah Palin.

**Constitutional Challenges:** In Baber v. Dunlap (D. Me. 2018), a federal district court upheld Maine's RCV system against a challenge under Article II of the Constitution, finding that the Constitution does not mandate plurality voting. The First Circuit declined to issue an injunction.

### National Popular Vote Interstate Compact

The National Popular Vote Interstate Compact (NPVIC) is an agreement among states to award all their electoral votes to the presidential candidate who wins the national popular vote. As of 2026, jurisdictions representing 209 of the required 270 electoral votes have adopted the compact. The compact would take effect only when states controlling a majority of electoral votes have joined. Constitutional challenges are anticipated under the Compact Clause (Article I, Section 10) and the Electoral College provisions.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's mixed-member proportional representation system (personalisierte Verhältniswahl) has been influential worldwide, serving as a model for electoral systems in New Zealand, Scotland, and other jurisdictions.

**Federal Election Act (Bundeswahlgesetz, BWahlG):** Each voter casts two votes: a first vote (Erststimme) for a constituency candidate elected by plurality, and a second vote (Zweitstimme) for a party list used to determine proportional representation. The second vote is the primary determinant of each party's total share of Bundestag seats.

**5% Threshold (Sperrklausel):** A party must receive at least 5% of valid second votes nationwide or win at least three constituency seats to qualify for proportional representation allocation. The Federal Constitutional Court has upheld the 5% threshold as constitutionally permissible to prevent parliamentary fragmentation, most recently in BVerfGE 82, 322 (1990).

### 2023 Electoral Reform (Wahlrechtsreform)

The 2023 reform, enacted as the Act to Reduce the Size of the Bundestag (Gesetz zur Verkleinerung des Bundestages), represented the most significant change to German electoral law in decades.

**Problem:** The Bundestag had grown from its notional 598-seat size to 736 seats in 2021 due to overhang mandates (Überhangmandate) -- seats awarded when a party wins more constituency seats than its proportional entitlement -- and compensatory leveling mandates (Ausgleichsmandate) added to restore proportionality for other parties.

**Solution:** The reform:
- Fixed the Bundestag at 630 seats (299 constituency + 331 proportional)
- Eliminated overhang mandates entirely: constituency victories exceeding a party's proportional entitlement are no longer honored
- Applied a "second vote coverage" (Zweitstimmendeckung) requirement: constituency winners are seated only if their party has sufficient proportional entitlement
- Maintained the 5% threshold but eliminated the alternative three-constituency-win route

**Constitutional Challenge:** The CSU (Bavaria's Christian Social Union) and the Left Party (Die Linke) filed complaints with the Federal Constitutional Court (BVerfG), arguing that the elimination of the three-constituency exception and the non-seating of constituency winners violated the principles of direct election and equal suffrage under Articles 28 and 38 GG. In January 2024, the BVerfG issued a partial ruling (BVerfG, 2 BvF 1/23) upholding the core reform but requiring the restoration of the three-constituency-win alternative to the 5% threshold, finding its elimination to be disproportionate.

### European Parliament Elections

Germany elects its 96 MEPs through closed-list proportional representation. Following the BVerfG's 2014 ruling (BVerfGE 135, 259) striking down a 3% threshold for European Parliament elections as unconstitutional, Germany had no threshold for EP elections until the EU's 2018 amendment to the Direct Elections Act mandated a minimum threshold of between 2% and 5% for member states electing more than 35 MEPs. Germany subsequently enacted a 2% threshold for EP elections.

## ECHR / EU

### EU Electoral Framework

The legal framework for European Parliament elections is established by the 1976 Act Concerning the Election of Representatives to the European Parliament (Direct Elections Act) as amended by Council Decision 2018/994.

**Key Requirements:**
- Elections must use a proportional representation system (list-based or single transferable vote)
- Member states may establish a minimum threshold not exceeding 5% of votes cast
- Member states with more than 35 seats must establish a minimum threshold of between 2% and 5%
- Member states may divide their territory into electoral constituencies
- Member states may permit preferential voting

**Member State Variation:** Despite the harmonized PR requirement, significant variation exists:

| Member State | Method | Threshold | Constituencies |
|-------------|--------|-----------|---------------|
| Germany | Closed list PR | 2% (since 2024) | Single national |
| France | Closed list PR | 5% | Single national (since 2019) |
| Ireland | STV | None | 4 regional |
| Italy | Open list PR | 4% | 5 macro-regional |
| Netherlands | Open list PR | Effective threshold via seat allocation | Single national |
| Poland | Open list PR | 5% | 13 regional |

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Protocol 1, Article 3:** "The High Contracting Parties undertake to hold free elections at reasonable intervals by secret ballot, under conditions which will ensure the free expression of the opinion of the people in the choice of the legislature."

**Mathieu-Mohin and Clerfayt v. Belgium (1987):** The Court established that Article 3 of Protocol 1 implies subjective rights, including the right to vote and the right to stand for election, but that these rights are not absolute and may be subject to limitations. States enjoy a wide margin of appreciation in choosing their electoral system.

**Yumak and Sadak v. Turkey (Grand Chamber, 2008):** The Grand Chamber upheld Turkey's 10% national electoral threshold -- the highest in Europe -- finding it within the state's margin of appreciation, though noting that its effects were "striking." The Court emphasized that the assessment of an electoral system must take account of the political and historical context of the country and that no obligation exists under the Convention to introduce a specific electoral system.

**Ekoglasnost v. Bulgaria (2012):** The Court found a violation of Protocol 1, Article 3 where Bulgaria's electoral registration requirements effectively prevented a political party from participating in elections, holding that while states have broad discretion in designing electoral systems, they must not impose conditions that impair the very essence of the right.

### Venice Commission Standards

The Code of Good Practice in Electoral Matters (CDL-AD(2002)023rev2-cor) establishes principles including:
- Universal, equal, free, secret, and direct suffrage
- Proportionality between votes and seats should be a primary objective
- Electoral thresholds should not exceed 5%
- Electoral system changes should not take effect within one year of an election
- Electoral boundaries should be drawn by an independent body

## Comparative Analysis

### System Design Philosophy

Each jurisdiction's voting system reflects distinct design priorities. South Korea's mixed system attempts to combine local representation with proportionality but has been undermined by strategic party creation. The United States prioritizes simplicity and local representation through FPTP but produces significant disproportionality and two-party dominance. Germany's MMP system prioritizes proportionality while preserving constituency representation, producing multi-party coalition governments. The EU mandates proportional representation to ensure representativeness across diverse member states.

### Reform Trajectories

All four jurisdictions are experiencing active reform pressures. South Korea is debating the elimination of satellite party exploitation and the expansion of proportional seats. The United States is witnessing the most dynamic reform period through the ranked-choice voting movement. Germany's 2023 reform addresses the chronic Bundestag size problem. The EU's 2018 threshold mandate represents the most significant harmonization step since the original Direct Elections Act.

### Threshold Effects

Electoral thresholds vary from none (U.S. FPTP effectively operates as a de facto threshold of approximately 50% in each constituency) to 5% (Germany, several EU member states). South Korea's 3% threshold is relatively permissive. The ECHR has approved thresholds up to 10% (Turkey) while the Venice Commission recommends a maximum of 5%. The tension between preventing parliamentary fragmentation and ensuring minority representation runs through all jurisdictions.

### Technology and Security

Voting technology approaches range from South Korea's manual paper ballots to the variety of electronic and paper systems in U.S. jurisdictions. The Help America Vote Act's push toward electronic voting machines has been partially reversed by a return to paper ballots with optical scanners following cybersecurity concerns post-2016. Germany uses exclusively paper ballots counted by hand. The EU has no harmonized voting technology standards.
