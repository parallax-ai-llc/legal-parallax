## Summary

Forced labor import bans -- the legal mechanisms prohibiting the importation of goods produced wholly or in part with forced labor, compulsory labor, or exploitative labor practices -- have emerged as a primary tool in the global fight against modern slavery and labor exploitation, using trade policy as a lever to enforce international labor standards extraterritorially by denying market access to goods tainted by forced labor. South Korea does not have a dedicated forced labor import ban but addresses related concerns through the Customs Act (관세법), the Foreign Trade Act (대외무역법), and the Act on the Protection of North Korean Defectors (북한이탈주민의 보호 및 정착지원에 관한 법률), with the Korea Customs Service (관세청) having authority to restrict imports on public order grounds; the National Human Rights Commission (국가인권위원회) has recommended legislation creating a forced labor import prohibition, and Korea's substantial trade relationships with countries identified as high-risk for forced labor (particularly China) create significant supply chain exposure for Korean industries including electronics, textiles, solar energy, and agriculture. The United States operates the world's most established and actively enforced forced labor import ban under Section 307 of the Tariff Act of 1930 (19 U.S.C. Section 1307), which prohibits importation of goods "mined, produced, or manufactured wholly or in part" by forced or indentured labor including forced child labor, enforced by U.S. Customs and Border Protection (CBP) through Withhold Release Orders (WROs) and Findings, dramatically strengthened by the repeal of the "consumptive demand" exception in the Trade Facilitation and Trade Enforcement Act of 2015 and the enactment of the Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act (UFLPA, Pub. L. 117-78, effective June 21, 2022) establishing a rebuttable presumption that goods from China's Xinjiang region are produced with forced labor; between 2016 and 2024, CBP issued 56 WROs and detained or denied entry to over 10,000 shipments valued at more than $3.5 billion. Germany, as the EU's largest trading economy, implements EU trade law provisions and is subject to the forthcoming EU Forced Labor Regulation (Regulation 2024/3015), while German domestic law addresses forced labor primarily through criminal law (Strafgesetzbuch Sections 232-233a, human trafficking and forced labor offenses) and the Lieferkettensorgfaltspflichtengesetz (LkSG) supply chain due diligence obligations. The EU adopted the Forced Labour Products Regulation (Regulation 2024/3015) on November 19, 2024, establishing a prohibition on placing, making available on the EU market, or exporting from the EU products made with forced labor, enforced through a risk-based investigation system by member state competent authorities and the European Commission, with products found to involve forced labor subject to withdrawal from the market and prohibition at EU borders, complementing the CSDDD's broader due diligence obligations and reflecting the EU's commitment to implementing the ILO Forced Labour Convention (No. 29) and its 2014 Protocol.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea does not have a dedicated forced labor import ban comparable to US Section 307 or the EU Forced Labour Regulation. The regulatory framework addresses forced labor concerns through general customs authority, labor law, and international treaty obligations.

### Customs Act (관세법)

The Korea Customs Service (KCS, 관세청) has general authority under the Customs Act to restrict imports that violate public order or morality (Article 234) and to require documentation of goods' origin and production conditions. However, the Customs Act does not contain a specific prohibition on imports of forced labor goods, and the KCS has not established a systematic enforcement mechanism comparable to CBP's WRO system.

### International Obligations

South Korea ratified ILO Convention No. 29 (Forced Labour Convention, 1930) in 2021, along with ILO Conventions No. 87 (Freedom of Association) and No. 98 (Right to Organise and Collective Bargaining), fulfilling the requirements for core ILO convention ratification. This ratification creates international legal obligations to suppress forced labor but does not, by itself, establish a domestic import ban mechanism.

### North Korea Dimension

Korea's unique geopolitical situation adds a distinctive dimension to forced labor import concerns. Products from North Korea are subject to comprehensive import bans under UN Security Council sanctions (Resolutions 2270, 2321, 2356, 2371, 2375, and 2397) and South Korea's Inter-Korean Exchange and Cooperation Act (남북교류협력에 관한 법률). The Kaesong Industrial Complex, which had operated from 2004 to 2016 with North Korean workers under South Korean management, was permanently closed following North Korea's nuclear test, partly due to concerns about labor conditions and wage diversion.

### Legislative Proposals

The National Human Rights Commission recommended in 2023 that the National Assembly enact legislation establishing:
- A forced labor import prohibition mechanism
- A forced labor risk assessment framework for imports
- Enhanced customs screening procedures for goods from high-risk origins
- Corporate due diligence obligations regarding forced labor in supply chains

A draft bill was prepared in 2024 by the Ministry of Trade, Industry and Energy (MOTIE) but has not yet been formally introduced.

### Supply Chain Exposure

Korean industries face significant forced labor supply chain risks:

| Industry | Risk Area | Minerals/Products |
|----------|-----------|------------------|
| Electronics | Xinjiang polysilicon; DRC cobalt mining | Solar panels, semiconductors, batteries |
| Textiles and Apparel | Xinjiang cotton; Southeast Asian garment factories | Cotton products, clothing, footwear |
| Agriculture and Food | Migrant labor exploitation; fishing industry forced labor | Seafood, agricultural products |
| Construction | Migrant worker exploitation in Gulf states | Materials sourced from forced-labor-affected regions |
| Automotive | Xinjiang aluminum; various mineral extraction | Aluminum components, wiring, electronic parts |

Korean companies with US operations or customers face indirect enforcement through UFLPA compliance requirements, creating a de facto forced labor screening obligation for supply chains connected to the US market.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has the world's oldest and most actively enforced forced labor import ban, rooted in Section 307 of the Tariff Act of 1930. The enforcement infrastructure has been dramatically strengthened since 2016, making the US the global leader in using trade law to combat forced labor.

### Section 307 of the Tariff Act (19 U.S.C. Section 1307)

Section 307 provides: "All goods, wares, articles, and merchandise mined, produced, or manufactured wholly or in part in any foreign country by convict labor or/and forced labor or/and indentured labor under penal sanctions shall not be entitled to entry at any of the ports of the United States."

**Consumptive Demand Exception (Repealed 2016):** Until 2016, Section 307 contained a critical loophole: the "consumptive demand" exception exempted forced labor goods if domestic production was insufficient to meet US demand. The Trade Facilitation and Trade Enforcement Act (TFTEA, Pub. L. 114-125, Section 910), signed February 24, 2016, repealed this exception, dramatically expanding enforcement.

### Enforcement Mechanisms

CBP enforces Section 307 through two primary instruments:

**Withhold Release Orders (WROs):**
- Issued when CBP has information reasonably indicating that goods were produced with forced labor
- Detain shipments at the port of entry for up to 90 days (extendable)
- Importer may provide proof that goods were not produced with forced labor
- If importer fails to rebut, goods are excluded or re-exported

**Findings:**
- Issued after a full investigation confirms forced labor in production
- Permanently prohibit importation of specific goods from specific entities/regions
- Stronger than WROs; require formal rulemaking process

### Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act (UFLPA)

The UFLPA, effective June 21, 2022, represents the most aggressive forced labor trade measure globally:

**Rebuttable Presumption:** All goods mined, produced, or manufactured wholly or in part in the Xinjiang Uyghur Autonomous Region (XUAR) of China, or by entities on the UFLPA Entity List, are presumed to be products of forced labor and are prohibited from importation.

**UFLPA Entity List:** Maintained by the Forced Labor Enforcement Task Force (FLETF), chaired by DHS and including the Departments of State, Treasury, Commerce, Justice, Labor, and the Office of the US Trade Representative. As of 2024, the Entity List includes:
- Cotton and textile entities (e.g., Xinjiang Production and Construction Corps, XPCC/Bingtuan)
- Polysilicon manufacturers (e.g., Hoshine Silicon Industry, Xinjiang Daqo New Energy, Xinjiang GCL New Energy Materials, East Hope Group)
- Tomato product entities
- Government labor transfer program participants

**Rebutting the Presumption:** An importer may apply for an exception by demonstrating through "clear and convincing evidence" that goods were not produced with forced labor. CBP has published enforcement guidance specifying the types of evidence required:
- Complete supply chain mapping from raw materials to finished goods
- Independent third-party audits of labor conditions at each production stage
- Worker interviews conducted by qualified auditors
- Payment records, employment contracts, and evidence of voluntary employment
- Documentation that goods did not originate in Xinjiang or from Entity List entities

As of 2024, very few exception requests have been granted, reflecting the extremely high evidentiary standard.

**UFLPA Enforcement Data (June 2022-December 2024):**

| Metric | Cumulative Total |
|--------|-----------------|
| Shipments reviewed | 9,600+ |
| Shipments detained | 5,200+ |
| Shipments denied entry | 2,800+ |
| Shipments released after review | 4,200+ |
| Value of reviewed shipments | $4.2+ billion |
| Top sectors | Electronics/solar (polysilicon), apparel/textiles (cotton), consumer goods |

### Private Sector Litigation

Section 307 does not provide a private right of action, but affected parties have sought judicial review of CBP enforcement decisions. In Heliogen Inc. v. U.S. Customs and Border Protection (Ct. Int'l Trade, 2023), the Court of International Trade confirmed CBP's broad discretion in UFLPA enforcement while establishing that importers have procedural due process rights in the exception process.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany addresses forced labor through a combination of criminal law, supply chain due diligence obligations, and the forthcoming EU Forced Labour Regulation. Germany does not have an independent national forced labor import ban but will implement the EU-wide prohibition when the Forced Labour Regulation becomes fully applicable.

### Criminal Law Provisions

The Strafgesetzbuch (StGB) addresses forced labor through several criminal offenses:

| Section | Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|---------|
| **Section 232** | Human trafficking for labor exploitation (Menschenhandel zum Zweck der Arbeitsausbeutung) | 6 months to 10 years imprisonment |
| **Section 232a** | Forced labor (Zwangsarbeit) | 1 to 15 years imprisonment |
| **Section 232b** | Exploitation of labor (Ausbeutung der Arbeitskraft) | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine |
| **Section 233** | Exploitation while illegally employing foreigners (Ausbeutung unter Ausnutzung einer Freiheitsberaubung) | Up to 3 years or fine |
| **Section 233a** | Assisting trafficking/forced labor | Same as principal offense |

These criminal provisions apply extraterritorially in certain circumstances and can reach corporate officers responsible for supply chain decisions.

### LkSG and Forced Labor

The Lieferkettensorgfaltspflichtengesetz (LkSG) specifically identifies forced labor as a protected human rights interest, referencing ILO Conventions No. 29 and No. 105. Companies with 1,000+ employees must:
- Assess forced labor risks in their own operations and direct suppliers' operations
- Implement preventive measures against forced labor, including contractual assurances
- Establish complaint mechanisms accessible to workers alleging forced labor
- Take remedial measures upon discovering forced labor in the supply chain
- Report annually on forced labor due diligence measures

BAFA has identified forced labor as a priority risk area and conducts targeted monitoring of sectors with elevated forced labor exposure (textiles, electronics, agriculture, construction).

### German Customs Authority

The German Zollverwaltung (customs administration), particularly the Zollkriminalamt (ZKA, Customs Criminal Investigation Office), can intercept goods where there is evidence of criminal forced labor involvement. However, this authority is exercised within the criminal law framework rather than as a standalone trade enforcement mechanism.

### EU Forced Labour Regulation Implementation

Germany will be a key implementer of the EU Forced Labour Regulation given its position as the EU's largest importing economy. The German government is preparing national implementation legislation to:
- Designate competent authorities for investigation and enforcement (likely BAFA, building on its LkSG expertise)
- Establish procedures for market surveillance and border enforcement coordination
- Define national penalty provisions consistent with the Regulation's requirements
- Create information sharing mechanisms between customs, BAFA, and other enforcement agencies

## ECHR & EU

### EU Forced Labour Products Regulation (Regulation 2024/3015)

The Regulation on prohibiting products made with forced labour on the Union market, adopted on November 19, 2024, establishes the EU's first dedicated instrument for banning forced labor goods from the single market. The Regulation complements existing instruments (CSDDD, EU Conflict Minerals Regulation) by creating an enforcement mechanism specifically targeting products rather than imposing corporate due diligence obligations.

### Prohibition

Article 3 establishes a comprehensive prohibition: economic operators shall not place or make available on the EU market, or export from the EU, products that have been made with forced labor. The prohibition covers:
- Products made with forced labor at any stage of production, manufacture, harvest, or extraction
- Both imported goods and goods produced within the EU
- Finished products and components/raw materials incorporated into finished products

### Definition of Forced Labor

The Regulation adopts the ILO Convention No. 29 definition: "all work or service which is exacted from any person under the menace of any penalty and for which the said person has not offered himself voluntarily." This includes forced labor imposed by state authorities (state-imposed forced labor, referencing situations such as Xinjiang).

### Investigation and Enforcement Process

The Regulation establishes a phased investigation process:

**Phase 1 -- Preliminary Assessment:**
- Competent authorities conduct risk-based assessments, using a database of forced labor risk areas/products maintained by the Commission
- Information sources include submissions from natural or legal persons, international organizations, civil society, trade unions
- Authorities assess the probability and scale of forced labor

**Phase 2 -- Investigation:**

| Lead Authority | Scope |
|----------------|-------|
| **Member state competent authorities** | Products made within the EU or where forced labor occurs within a member state's jurisdiction |
| **European Commission** | Products involving forced labor outside the EU (including state-imposed forced labor); ensures uniform enforcement |

- Authorities may request information from economic operators, conduct inspections, and cooperate with third-country authorities
- Economic operators must respond to information requests within 30 days (extendable)
- Investigations must conclude within a reasonable timeframe (guidelines to be established by implementing acts)

**Phase 3 -- Decision:**
- If forced labor is established, the competent authority issues a decision prohibiting the product from being placed or made available on the market
- Products already on the market must be withdrawn
- Products at EU borders must be refused entry or re-exported
- Decisions are recognized across all member states (mutual recognition)

**Phase 4 -- Remediation:**
- Operators may request review of the decision if they demonstrate that forced labor has been eliminated from the supply chain
- The decision is withdrawn once compliance is established

### Forced Labor Single Portal

The Commission will establish a Forced Labour Single Portal providing:
- Database of forced labor risk areas, products, and economic operators
- Guidance documents for economic operators
- Information on due diligence standards and verification mechanisms
- Decisions and their outcomes (anonymized where necessary)

### Coordination with Customs

The Regulation integrates with the EU customs framework. Customs authorities will:
- Receive alerts on products subject to prohibition decisions
- Suspend release of goods matching product descriptions in decisions
- Detain goods pending competent authority assessment
- Refuse entry, order destruction, or require re-export of prohibited goods

The EU Customs Authority will coordinate cross-border enforcement, ensuring that goods banned in one member state are not diverted to another.

### Relationship with Other Instruments

| Instrument | Relationship |
|------------|-------------|
| **CSDDD** | Complementary: CSDDD imposes due diligence obligations; Forced Labour Regulation provides product-specific enforcement. Compliance with CSDDD does not immunize from product prohibition. |
| **EU Conflict Minerals Regulation** | Parallel: addresses mineral supply chains; Forced Labour Regulation covers all products |
| **LkSG (Germany)** | Overlapping: LkSG due diligence can inform Forced Labour Regulation investigations |
| **US UFLPA** | Comparable purpose; different mechanism (rebuttable presumption vs. investigation-based prohibition) |

### ILO Forced Labour Convention and Protocol

The Regulation implements the EU's commitments under ILO Convention No. 29 and its 2014 Protocol (P029), which strengthened the international framework by requiring states to take effective measures to prevent forced labor, protect victims, and ensure access to remedies. The Protocol entered into force on November 9, 2016, and has been ratified by all EU member states.

### ECHR Forced Labor Provisions

Article 4 of the ECHR prohibits slavery and forced labor. The ECtHR has interpreted this provision broadly:

**Siliadin v. France (2005):** The ECtHR held that France violated Article 4 by failing to provide adequate criminal law protection against domestic servitude, establishing positive obligations to criminalize and effectively prosecute forced labor.

**Chowdury v. Greece (2017):** The ECtHR found Greece violated Article 4 in a case involving Bangladeshi strawberry pickers subjected to forced labor, establishing that states must identify victims and investigate situations of potential trafficking and forced labor.

**S.M. v. Croatia (2020, Grand Chamber):** Clarified that Article 4 encompasses trafficking for labor exploitation and imposes positive obligations on states to: (1) adopt a legislative and administrative framework to prohibit and punish forced labor and trafficking; (2) take operational measures to protect victims; and (3) investigate credible allegations.

These ECtHR decisions provide the human rights law foundation supporting both domestic criminal enforcement and trade-based measures against forced labor products.

## Comparative Analysis

### Enforcement Models

The jurisdictions demonstrate three distinct enforcement models. The United States uses a customs-driven model with rebuttable presumptions (UFLPA) and unilateral CBP determinations (WROs), providing the fastest and most aggressive enforcement. The EU uses an investigation-based model requiring competent authority findings before prohibition, which is more procedurally protective but potentially slower. Germany relies primarily on criminal law enforcement and supply chain due diligence (LkSG), which addresses systemic risk but lacks a product-specific enforcement mechanism until the EU Regulation becomes applicable. South Korea has no dedicated enforcement mechanism, relying on general customs authority.

### Burden of Proof

The burden of proof allocation differs fundamentally. Under the UFLPA, the burden shifts to the importer to prove goods are not made with forced labor (rebuttable presumption), a highly importer-unfavorable standard. Under the EU Forced Labour Regulation, the competent authority bears the burden of establishing forced labor through investigation, a more due-process-oriented but potentially less effective approach. The EU model provides greater protection for economic operators but may allow more forced labor products to enter the market during lengthy investigation processes.

### Geographic Targeting

The UFLPA's Xinjiang-specific rebuttable presumption is the most geographically targeted measure, effectively creating a regional import ban absent exceptional evidence. The EU Regulation adopts a product-based approach without geographic presumptions, though the Commission database will identify high-risk areas. This difference reflects distinct strategic calculations: the US approach maximizes enforcement impact on the primary target (Xinjiang forced labor) while the EU approach aims for universal application.

### State-Imposed vs. Private Forced Labor

The EU Regulation explicitly addresses state-imposed forced labor, with the Commission taking the lead on investigations involving state-imposed forced labor outside the EU. This is directly relevant to situations like Xinjiang (where the Chinese government's labor transfer programs have been documented by the UN, OHCHR, and numerous investigations) and Uzbekistan and Turkmenistan cotton harvests (where state-imposed forced labor has historically been used). The US addresses state-imposed forced labor through both Section 307 and the UFLPA. South Korea and Germany have not developed specific mechanisms for state-imposed forced labor.

### Market Impact and Trade Implications

The proliferation of forced labor import bans is reshaping global trade patterns. Companies are investing in supply chain mapping and traceability technology, diversifying away from high-risk regions, and developing "forced labor-free" certification systems. However, concerns exist about protectionist misuse, particularly in the context of US-China trade tensions, and about the impact on developing country workers who may lose employment when companies exit high-risk regions rather than remediate labor conditions.

### Convergence Trajectory

Despite different mechanisms, the US and EU approaches are converging toward a global norm against forced labor imports. The adoption of the EU Forced Labour Regulation in 2024 significantly narrows the enforcement gap between the US and EU. Future developments likely include: enhanced US-EU cooperation on forced labor investigations; expansion of product categories subject to heightened scrutiny; and pressure on jurisdictions without forced labor import bans (including South Korea, Japan, and Australia) to adopt comparable measures.
