## Summary

Mortgage and foreclosure law -- the legal framework governing security interests in real property and the procedures by which creditors may enforce those interests upon debtor default -- lies at the intersection of property law, contract law, and consumer protection, with each jurisdiction reflecting distinct historical traditions and policy choices about the balance between creditor remedies and borrower protections. South Korea regulates real property security primarily through the mortgage (저당권, jeodanggwon) provisions of the Civil Code (민법) Articles 356-372, supplemented by the Housing Finance Credit Guarantee Act, the Act on Registration of Mortgage Bonds, and the Debtor Rehabilitation and Bankruptcy Act, with foreclosure accomplished through court-administered auction (경매) under the Civil Execution Act (민사집행법), which provides a unified judicial foreclosure framework with borrower protections including mandatory appraisal, public auction requirements, and surplus distribution rules. The United States employs a dual system of judicial and non-judicial foreclosure procedures varying by state, with mortgage law shaped by the distinction between title theory and lien theory states, and borrower protections established through federal legislation including the Truth in Lending Act (TILA), Real Estate Settlement Procedures Act (RESPA), Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform Act, and the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau's (CFPB) Regulation X, alongside state-level anti-deficiency statutes, redemption rights, and homestead exemptions. Germany distinguishes between the Hypothek (accessory mortgage, BGB §§ 1113-1190) and the Grundschuld (non-accessory land charge, BGB §§ 1191-1198), with the Grundschuld predominating in practice, and foreclosure accomplished through compulsory auction (Zwangsversteigerung) under the Gesetz über die Zwangsversteigerung und die Zwangsverwaltung (ZVG), which provides a rigorous judicial procedure with significant debtor protections including minimum bid requirements, temporary suspension for hardship, and the possibility of compulsory administration (Zwangsverwaltung) as an alternative to auction. The ECHR protects borrowers' property and housing rights under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 and Article 8, requiring that foreclosure procedures satisfy proportionality requirements and provide adequate procedural safeguards, while EU law addresses mortgage lending through the Mortgage Credit Directive (2014/17/EU), which establishes harmonized standards for responsible lending, pre-contractual information, and borrower protections in foreclosure.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's mortgage law reflects its civil law tradition with a comprehensive statutory framework.

**Mortgage (저당권, Civil Code Articles 356-372):** A non-possessory security interest in real property that entitles the mortgagee (저당권자) to satisfy their claim from the proceeds of the property's sale in priority over unsecured creditors. The mortgage is accessory (부종성) -- it exists only to secure an obligation and is extinguished when the underlying obligation is satisfied. The mortgage must be registered in the real property register (등기부) to be effective against third parties.

**Cheonse Right (전세권, Articles 303-319):** A distinctive Korean security/use right under which the cheonse holder pays a lump-sum deposit (전세금) and obtains the right to use the property; upon termination, the cheonse holder has a preferential right to recover the deposit from the property's auction proceeds. The cheonse system has been under significant pressure due to the "jeonse fraud crisis" (전세사기) that emerged in 2022-2023, leading to substantial legislative reforms.

**Right of Retention (유치권, Articles 320-328):** A possessory security right allowing a person who has expended resources to improve or maintain property to retain possession until compensated. The intersection of retention rights with mortgages has generated extensive litigation, particularly in construction contexts where contractors assert retention rights against mortgagees.

### Foreclosure Procedure (경매)

All mortgage foreclosures in South Korea proceed through judicial auction under the Civil Execution Act (민사집행법):

**Application:** The mortgagee files an application for compulsory auction (임의경매) with the competent district court, submitting the mortgage registration certificate, evidence of the secured obligation, and evidence of default.

**Seizure and Appraisal:** The court issues a seizure order (압류) prohibiting the debtor from disposing of the property. A court-appointed appraiser conducts a professional valuation, which establishes the minimum bid price (typically 80% of appraised value for the first sale attempt).

**Sale Procedure:** The court conducts a public auction (매각기일). If no bidder meets the minimum price, the court may reduce the minimum by 20% and schedule a second sale. This process may be repeated, with further reductions, until a buyer is found. Internet-based auction systems have been implemented to increase transparency and participation.

**Distribution:** Sale proceeds are distributed according to legally established priorities: court costs, tax liens, secured creditors (in order of registration priority), unsecured creditors, and finally any surplus to the debtor. The distribution procedure (배당절차) is subject to objection by any interested party.

### Borrower Protections

**Minimum Living Space Exemption:** The Civil Execution Act provides protections for tenants occupying the foreclosed property, including a minimum deposit protection (소액임차인보호) ensuring that small residential tenants receive priority recovery of a portion of their deposits.

**Housing Stability Measures:** In response to the jeonse fraud crisis, the government enacted the Special Act on Support for Victims of Housing Rental Fraud (전세사기피해자지원특별법, 2023), providing emergency housing assistance, priority purchase rights for victims at auction, and special recovery procedures.

**Debtor Rehabilitation:** The Debtor Rehabilitation and Bankruptcy Act (채무자회생법) provides rehabilitation procedures that can stay foreclosure proceedings, allowing individual debtors to restructure their debts while retaining their homes under court-approved repayment plans.

### Priority Disputes

Korean foreclosure law generates complex priority disputes, particularly involving:

**Retention Rights vs. Mortgages:** The Supreme Court has addressed whether a contractor's retention right (유치권) over a building takes priority over a previously registered mortgage. The Court has held that a retention right arising after the mortgage registration cannot be asserted against the purchaser at a mortgage foreclosure auction (Supreme Court Decision 2011Da84298), but the issue remains contentious.

**Unpaid Taxes:** Tax liens generally take priority over private security interests, though the timing and nature of the tax obligation affect the priority analysis. The Supreme Court has developed detailed rules for the interaction between tax priorities and mortgage priorities.

## United States

### Legal Framework

American mortgage law is characterized by significant variation among states and a layered federal-state regulatory framework.

**Mortgage vs. Deed of Trust:** In approximately 30 states, the standard security instrument is a deed of trust, under which the borrower (trustor) conveys legal title to a trustee who holds it for the benefit of the lender (beneficiary). In the remaining states, a traditional mortgage is used, creating a lien on the property without transferring title. The practical significance lies in the foreclosure process: deeds of trust typically authorize non-judicial foreclosure through the trustee's power of sale, while mortgages generally require judicial foreclosure.

**Title Theory vs. Lien Theory:** In title theory states (a diminishing minority), the mortgage conveys legal title to the mortgagee, who holds it until the debt is satisfied. In lien theory states (the majority), the mortgage creates only a lien, with the mortgagor retaining legal title throughout. Intermediate theory states treat the mortgage as creating a lien until default, at which point title or the right to possession transfers to the mortgagee.

### Foreclosure Procedures

**Judicial Foreclosure:** Required in approximately 22 states (including New York, New Jersey, Florida, Illinois, and Ohio). The lender files a lawsuit, the borrower receives formal notice and the opportunity to answer and raise defenses, the court enters a judgment of foreclosure, and the property is sold at a court-supervised public auction. Judicial foreclosure provides significant procedural protections but is typically slower and more expensive.

**Non-Judicial Foreclosure (Power of Sale):** Available in approximately 28 states (including California, Texas, Georgia, and Virginia). The foreclosure is conducted outside the court system, typically by a trustee or other designated party, following statutory notice and publication requirements. Non-judicial foreclosure is generally faster and less expensive but provides fewer procedural protections for the borrower.

**Strict Foreclosure:** Available in a handful of states (Connecticut, Vermont), allowing the court to vest title directly in the mortgagee without a sale, provided the property's value does not substantially exceed the debt.

### Anti-Deficiency Protections

Approximately 12 states have anti-deficiency statutes that limit or prohibit the lender from obtaining a deficiency judgment (a personal judgment against the borrower for the difference between the debt and the foreclosure sale price):

**California:** Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §§ 580b, 580d prohibit deficiency judgments for purchase money mortgages on owner-occupied dwellings (1-4 units) and for any debt where the property is sold through non-judicial foreclosure.

**Arizona:** A.R.S. § 33-814 prohibits deficiency judgments for purchase money deeds of trust on residential property of 2.5 acres or less.

**Oregon:** ORS § 86.770 prohibits deficiency judgments following non-judicial foreclosure.

These protections reflect legislative determinations that the lender's security interest in the property should bear the risk of property value decline, particularly for owner-occupied homes purchased with the lender's financing.

### Federal Consumer Protections

**Truth in Lending Act (TILA, 15 U.S.C. § 1601 et seq.):** Requires disclosure of loan terms, including APR, total finance charges, and payment schedules. TILA's rescission right allows borrowers to cancel certain home equity transactions within three business days, or up to three years if required disclosures were not provided.

**RESPA (12 U.S.C. § 2601 et seq.):** Prohibits kickbacks and unearned fees in settlement transactions, requires good faith estimates of settlement costs, and limits escrow account requirements.

**Dodd-Frank Act (2010) and CFPB Regulation X:** Established the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, which promulgated Regulation X (12 C.F.R. Part 1024) requiring mortgage servicers to provide early intervention for delinquent borrowers, evaluate borrowers for loss mitigation options before proceeding with foreclosure, and maintain adequate staffing and procedures for handling borrower communications. Regulation X prohibits dual tracking -- simultaneously pursuing foreclosure while a loss mitigation application is pending.

**Homeowner Assistance Fund (HAF, 2021):** Established under the American Rescue Plan Act, providing $9.961 billion to states for mortgage payment assistance, utility payment assistance, and other housing-related expenses for homeowners experiencing pandemic-related financial hardship.

### Redemption Rights

**Equity of Redemption:** All states recognize the borrower's equitable right to redeem the property by paying the full debt before the foreclosure sale. The equity of redemption cannot be waived in the mortgage instrument (the prohibition against "clogging" the equity of redemption).

**Statutory Redemption:** Approximately 30 states provide a post-sale statutory redemption period (ranging from 30 days to 2 years) during which the borrower or junior lienholders may redeem the property by paying the foreclosure sale price plus statutory interest and costs.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German real property security law is structured around two fundamentally different instruments registered in the Grundbuch (land register).

**Hypothek (BGB §§ 1113-1190):** An accessory (akzessorisch) security right in real property, meaning its existence and enforceability depend on the existence and enforceability of the underlying obligation. If the debt is satisfied, the Hypothek is automatically extinguished. Despite its detailed regulation in the BGB, the Hypothek has been largely supplanted in practice by the Grundschuld.

**Grundschuld (BGB §§ 1191-1198):** A non-accessory (abstrakt) security right in real property, meaning its validity and enforceability are independent of the underlying obligation. The Grundschuld encumbers the property with the obligation to pay a specified sum of money from the property. The relationship between the Grundschuld and the underlying debt is governed by a separate security agreement (Sicherungsvertrag or Zweckerklärung) under the law of obligations. The Grundschuld's non-accessory nature provides significant advantages in commercial lending: it can be transferred without reference to the underlying debt, reused for new obligations, and retained as an Eigentümergrundschuld (owner's land charge) after the debt is satisfied.

**Practical Dominance of Grundschuld:** Approximately 95% of real property security in Germany takes the form of a Grundschuld rather than a Hypothek. The Grundschuld's flexibility, transferability, and reusability have made it the preferred instrument for both commercial and residential lending.

### Consumer Protections for Grundschuld

The abstract nature of the Grundschuld historically created risks for borrowers, particularly where the Grundschuld was transferred to a third party who might enforce it without regard to the borrower's defenses against the original lender. Legislative reforms have progressively strengthened consumer protections:

**Risikobegrenzungsgesetz (2008):** Enacted in response to concerns about "mass loan transfers" (Kreditverkäufe), this statute: (1) requires that the borrower be notified of any transfer of the loan or the Grundschuld; (2) prevents the new Grundschuld holder from enforcing the security without the underlying claim; (3) limits the circumstances under which defenses can be cut off by transfer; and (4) protects the borrower's right to invoke personal defenses (Einreden) against any Grundschuld holder.

**BGB § 1192 Abs. 1a (added 2008):** Provides that for Sicherungsgrundschulden (security land charges securing a monetary obligation), the borrower may assert defenses arising from the security agreement against any assignee of the Grundschuld, effectively limiting the abstract character of the Grundschuld in consumer lending contexts.

### Foreclosure: Zwangsversteigerung

Foreclosure on real property in Germany is exclusively judicial, governed by the Gesetz über die Zwangsversteigerung und die Zwangsverwaltung (ZVG, Act on Compulsory Auction and Compulsory Administration) of 1897.

**Application and Court Order:** The creditor applies to the competent Amtsgericht (local court) for a compulsory auction order (Anordnung der Zwangsversteigerung). The court verifies the creditor's entitlement and the enforceability of the security right, and issues an order that is registered in the Grundbuch.

**Valuation:** The court appoints an expert to determine the property's market value (Verkehrswert). The valuation is the basis for minimum bid requirements and is communicated to all parties.

**Auction Procedure:** The auction is conducted by the court at a public hearing (Versteigerungstermin). The procedure includes a bidding period of at least 30 minutes, during which any person may submit bids. The highest bidder is awarded the property by court order (Zuschlag), subject to minimum bid requirements.

**Minimum Bid Requirements (ZVG §§ 74a, 85a):** The ZVG imposes two critical minimum bid thresholds. Under § 74a, if the highest bid does not reach 5/10 (50%) of the Verkehrswert, the court must refuse the award upon application by any secured creditor whose claim would not be fully satisfied. Under § 85a, if the highest bid does not reach 7/10 (70%) of the Verkehrswert, the court must refuse the award upon application by the debtor. These thresholds protect against grossly inadequate sale prices but may be invoked only once (the thresholds do not apply at a subsequent auction).

**Compulsory Administration (Zwangsverwaltung):** As an alternative or supplement to compulsory auction, a creditor may apply for compulsory administration (ZVG §§ 146-161), under which a court-appointed administrator (Zwangsverwalter) manages the property and applies the income (rents, profits) to satisfy the creditor's claim. This procedure preserves the debtor's ownership while providing the creditor with an income stream from the property.

### Hardship Protections

**Temporary Suspension (Einstweilige Einstellung, ZVG § 30a):** The court may temporarily suspend the compulsory auction for up to six months upon the debtor's application if the debtor demonstrates a credible prospect of satisfying the creditor's claim within a reasonable period. This suspension may be granted up to twice, for a total maximum suspension of twelve months.

**Protection Against Loss of Housing:** German social welfare law and court practice provide additional protections for residential debtors, including the court's obligation to consider the debtor's housing situation when deciding on suspension, and the debtor's rights under residential tenancy law if they remain as a tenant after the auction sale.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Proportionality Framework

The European Court of Human Rights has developed significant jurisprudence on the intersection of mortgage enforcement and fundamental rights.

**Article 1, Protocol No. 1 (Property):** Foreclosure constitutes a deprivation of property that must serve a legitimate aim and be proportionate. The Court examines whether the foreclosure procedure provides adequate procedural safeguards, whether alternatives to sale were considered, and whether the sale price bears a reasonable relationship to the property's market value.

**Article 8 (Home):** The loss of one's home through foreclosure engages Article 8, requiring that any interference be in accordance with law, pursue a legitimate aim, and be necessary in a democratic society. In *Rousk v. Sweden* (Application No. 27183/04, 2013), the Court found a violation of Article 8 where the applicant's home was sold at a compulsory auction for tax debts of approximately €6,000, without adequate consideration of proportionality or the debtor's personal circumstances. The Court emphasized that the loss of one's home is one of the most extreme forms of interference with Article 8 rights and requires heightened scrutiny.

**Proportionality in Summary Procedures:** In *Zehentner v. Austria* (Application No. 20082/02, 2009), the Court held that compulsory auction of the home of a mentally incapacitated person, conducted without adequate legal representation, violated Article 8. The Court emphasized that procedural safeguards must be particularly robust where the debtor is vulnerable.

### Mortgage Credit Directive (2014/17/EU)

The MCD represents the EU's primary legislative instrument for harmonizing mortgage lending standards across the internal market.

**Responsible Lending:** The MCD requires creditors to conduct thorough creditworthiness assessments before granting mortgage credit, based on information from the consumer and from relevant databases. Mortgage credit may not be granted where the assessment indicates the consumer is unlikely to meet their obligations.

**Pre-Contractual Information:** The MCD mandates the European Standardised Information Sheet (ESIS) providing consumers with standardized information about the key features of the mortgage product, enabling comparison between products and lenders.

**Right of Early Repayment (Article 25):** Consumers have the right to repay mortgage credit early, in full or in part. Member states may permit creditors to charge fair and objectively justified compensation for early repayment, but the compensation must not exceed the creditor's financial loss.

**Forbearance and Foreclosure (Article 28):** The MCD requires member states to adopt measures encouraging creditors to exercise reasonable forbearance before initiating foreclosure proceedings. Where the sale price achieved in foreclosure affects the amount owed by the consumer, member states must ensure reasonable efforts to obtain the best price.

**Foreign Currency Mortgages (Article 23):** Where a mortgage is denominated in a foreign currency, the consumer has the right to convert the currency of the credit agreement, or the creditor must ensure that the exchange rate risk is limited, addressing the severe distress caused by Swiss franc-denominated mortgages in Central and Eastern European countries.

### EBA Guidelines

The European Banking Authority (EBA) has issued guidelines on mortgage arrears and foreclosure that complement the MCD, emphasizing early intervention, individualized assessments, and the exploration of sustainable alternatives to foreclosure.

## Comparative Analysis

### Judicial vs. Non-Judicial Foreclosure

The jurisdictions divide sharply on whether foreclosure requires judicial supervision. Germany and South Korea require judicial foreclosure exclusively, reflecting civil law traditions emphasizing judicial protection of property rights and orderly debt enforcement. The United States uniquely offers non-judicial foreclosure in most states, reflecting a market-oriented approach that prioritizes enforcement efficiency, though at the cost of reduced procedural protections for borrowers. The ECHR does not mandate judicial foreclosure but requires that whatever procedure is used provide adequate safeguards against disproportionate outcomes.

### Minimum Price Protections

Germany's ZVG minimum bid thresholds (50% and 70% of appraised value) represent the strongest protection against grossly inadequate sale prices. South Korea's court-set minimum prices serve a similar function. The United States generally lacks minimum bid requirements in non-judicial foreclosure, though some states require "commercially reasonable" sale procedures and permit challenges to inadequate sale prices. The ECHR has expressed concern about foreclosure sales at prices grossly below market value, as in *Rousk v. Sweden*.

### Deficiency Liability

The jurisdictions diverge significantly on whether the debtor remains personally liable for any shortfall between the foreclosure sale price and the outstanding debt. Germany and South Korea generally permit deficiency judgments, placing the risk of inadequate sale prices partly on the borrower. American anti-deficiency statutes in select states (notably California and Arizona) eliminate this risk for purchase money mortgages on owner-occupied homes, reflecting a policy judgment that the lender's security interest should internalize the risk of property value decline.

### Abstract vs. Accessory Security

Germany's distinction between the accessory Hypothek and the abstract Grundschuld has no direct parallel in the other jurisdictions. The Grundschuld's abstract character -- separation of the security right from the underlying obligation -- provides unique flexibility but also creates risks addressed by consumer protection legislation (particularly the 2008 Risikobegrenzungsgesetz). Korean and American mortgages are fundamentally accessory, and their extinguishment upon satisfaction of the debt occurs automatically.
