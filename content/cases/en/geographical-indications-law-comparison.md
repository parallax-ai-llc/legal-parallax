## Summary

Geographical indications (GIs) are signs used on products that have a specific geographical origin and possess qualities, reputation, or characteristics essentially attributable to that place of origin, with protection systems ranging from comprehensive sui generis registration frameworks to certification and collective trademark approaches, reflecting fundamentally different philosophical orientations regarding the relationship between terroir, tradition, and trade -- tensions that have produced some of the most contentious disputes in international intellectual property negotiations. South Korea protects geographical indications through the Agricultural and Fishery Products Quality Control Act (농수산물 품질관리법) administered by the National Agricultural Products Quality Management Service (국립농산물품질관리원) for agricultural products, the Liquor Tax Act (주세법) for traditional Korean liquors, and the Trademark Act (상표법) providing for collective marks and geographical collective marks; the Korean Intellectual Property Office (KIPO, 특허청) registers geographical indication collective marks, and bilateral agreements with the EU have expanded mutual GI recognition. The United States does not provide a sui generis GI protection system, instead relying on the trademark system under the Lanham Act (15 U.S.C. Section 1051 et seq.) through certification marks (15 U.S.C. Section 1054) and collective marks, with the U.S. Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO) examining GI-based trademark applications; the U.S. approach treats GIs as a subset of trademark law, leading to fundamental conflicts with the EU's sui generis approach in WTO and bilateral trade negotiations. Germany, as a member of the EU, participates in the comprehensive EU GI protection framework under Regulation (EU) 2024/1143 on geographical indications for wine, spirit drinks, and agricultural products (replacing the previous separate regulations), providing for Protected Designations of Origin (PDO, geschützte Ursprungsbezeichnung/g.U.), Protected Geographical Indications (PGI, geschützte geografische Angabe/g.g.A.), and Traditional Specialities Guaranteed (TSG, garantiert traditionelle Spezialität/g.t.S.), with Germany registering significant GIs including Bayerisches Bier, Nürnberger Bratwürste, and Dresdner Christstollen. The EU framework represents the most developed GI protection system globally, with over 3,500 registered GIs, strong enforcement through ex officio protection against misuse, evocation, and misleading indications, and active promotion of the GI model through bilateral trade agreements and the TRIPS Agreement negotiations.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea has developed a dual-track GI protection system combining agricultural sui generis protection with trademark-based geographical collective marks.

**Agricultural and Fishery Products Quality Control Act (농수산물 품질관리법):** This Act, administered by the National Agricultural Products Quality Management Service (NAQS, 국립농산물품질관리원) under the Ministry of Agriculture, Food and Rural Affairs (농림축산식품부), provides for the registration of geographical indications for agricultural and fishery products. The Act requires that the product's quality, reputation, or other characteristics be essentially attributable to the geographical environment, including natural and human factors.

**Registration Process:** Applicants (producer organizations or local government entities) must demonstrate that the product has a specific quality, reputation, or other characteristic linked to the defined geographical area; that the production, processing, or preparation occurs within the area; and that quality control measures are in place. The registration process includes public notification and an opposition period.

### Trademark-Based Protection

**Trademark Act (상표법) Geographical Collective Marks:** The 2004 amendment to the Trademark Act introduced geographical collective marks (지리적 표시 단체표장), allowing producer groups to register marks that identify goods originating from a specific region where a quality, reputation, or other characteristic of the goods is essentially attributable to their geographical origin.

**KIPO Registration:** The Korean Intellectual Property Office registers geographical collective marks under the standard trademark examination procedures, including distinctiveness assessment and likelihood of confusion analysis. As of 2025, KIPO has registered over 200 geographical collective marks covering products from Boseong Green Tea (보성녹차) to Icheon Rice (이천쌀) and Yeongdeok Snow Crab (영덕대게).

### EU-Korea FTA

The Korea-EU Free Trade Agreement (2011) includes provisions on GI protection (Chapter 10, Section C), requiring Korea to protect EU GIs listed in an annex and vice versa. Korea agreed to provide enhanced protection for EU GIs, including protection against evocation, and to address conflicts between prior Korean trademarks and EU GIs through coexistence arrangements. This agreement significantly expanded the scope of GI protection in Korea, covering over 160 EU GIs including Champagne, Prosciutto di Parma, and Feta.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States fundamentally differs from most other major economies in treating GIs as a subset of trademark law rather than providing sui generis protection.

**Lanham Act (15 U.S.C. Section 1051 et seq.):** GIs in the United States may be protected through:

**Certification Marks (15 U.S.C. Section 1054):** A certification mark certifies that goods or services meet certain standards, including regional origin, material, mode of manufacture, quality, accuracy, or other characteristics. Examples include ROQUEFORT (registered by the Comite Interprofessionnel du Fromage de Roquefort), DARJEELING (registered by the Tea Board of India), and COLOMBIAN (registered by the Federacion Nacional de Cafeteros de Colombia).

**Collective Marks:** Marks used by members of a cooperative, association, or collective group to identify goods produced by members, potentially including geographical collective membership marks indicating membership in an organization of producers from a particular region.

**Geographic Terms as Trademarks:** Under Section 2(e)(2) of the Lanham Act (15 U.S.C. Section 1052(e)(2)), a mark that is primarily geographically descriptive of the goods is refused registration unless it has acquired distinctiveness (secondary meaning) under Section 2(f). A mark that is primarily geographically deceptively misdescriptive (15 U.S.C. Section 1052(a)) is absolutely barred from registration.

### Semi-Generic Wine Designations

The United States has historically permitted the use of certain European wine terms as "semi-generic" designations, a practice that has been a major source of transatlantic friction:

**27 C.F.R. Section 4.24:** The Alcohol and Tobacco Tax and Trade Bureau (TTB) historically permitted the use of terms such as "Burgundy," "Champagne," "Chablis," "Chianti," and "Port" as semi-generic designations for American wines, provided the wine's actual origin is disclosed. The 2006 TTB rulemaking (following the U.S.-EU Wine Agreement of 2006) prohibited new uses of these terms but grandfathered existing uses, creating a transitional regime that continues to frustrate EU producers.

### WTO Disputes

**WTO Panel Report, European Communities -- Protection of Trademarks and Geographical Indications for Agricultural Products and Foodstuffs (DS174/DS290, 2005):** The U.S. and Australia successfully challenged certain aspects of the EU's GI Regulation (then Regulation (EC) 2081/92), including the requirement that non-EU countries provide reciprocal GI protection and the regulation's coexistence provisions with prior trademarks. The Panel found that the EU regulation's conditions for registration of non-EU GIs violated TRIPS national treatment obligations. However, the Panel upheld the basic structure of EU GI protection as consistent with TRIPS.

### Genericness Doctrine

U.S. trademark law permits any party to petition for cancellation of a registered mark on the ground that it has become the generic name for the goods (genericide). This applies to GI-based marks as well. European producers view the U.S. genericness doctrine as a fundamental threat to GI protection, as it theoretically permits the cancellation of geographical terms that have become common names in U.S. commerce. The U.S. position on certain terms that the EU considers protected GIs (e.g., "parmesan," "feta," "gruyere") has been a persistent source of bilateral tension.

**Gruyere -- USPTO TTAB and Federal Circuit:** In Interprofession du Gruyere v. U.S. Dairy Export Council (2022), the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit held that "GRUYERE" is a generic term in the United States for a type of cheese, reversing the TTAB and denying certification mark registration. The decision relied on evidence of U.S. consumer understanding, FDA standards of identity, and widespread use of "gruyere" by non-Swiss producers.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany participates in the comprehensive EU geographical indication protection framework and has a deep tradition of protecting regional food and beverage designations.

**German GI Registrations:** Germany has secured EU registration for numerous products, reflecting its strong regional food and beverage traditions:

| Product | Type | Region |
|---------|------|--------|
| Bayerisches Bier | PGI | Bavaria |
| Nürnberger Bratwürste/Nürnberger Rostbratwürste | PGI | Nuremberg, Bavaria |
| Dresdner Christstollen/Dresdner Stollen | PGI | Dresden, Saxony |
| Schwäbische Spätzle/Schwäbische Knöpfle | PGI | Swabia (Baden-Württemberg, Bavaria) |
| Lübecker Marzipan | PGI | Lübeck, Schleswig-Holstein |
| Thüringer Rostbratwurst | PGI | Thuringia |
| Schwarzwälder Schinken | PGI | Black Forest, Baden-Württemberg |
| Allgäuer Bergkäse | PDO | Allgäu, Bavaria |
| Spreewälder Gurken | PGI | Spreewald, Brandenburg |

**German Wine Law (Weingesetz):** German wine classifications include the Prädikatswein system with origin designations such as Mosel, Rheingau, Pfalz, and Rheinhessen, protected at both the EU and national level. The German Wine Act (Weingesetz) and the Wine Regulation (Weinverordnung) govern the use of geographical designations for wine, aligned with EU Regulation (EU) 2024/1143 and Regulation (EU) No 1308/2013 (CMO Regulation).

### National Enforcement

**Lebensmittel- und Futtermittelgesetzbuch (LFGB, Food and Feed Code):** German authorities enforce GI protection through food safety and labeling regulations, with the Bundesamt für Verbraucherschutz und Lebensmittelsicherheit (BVL, Federal Office of Consumer Protection and Food Safety) and Länder (state) food inspection authorities responsible for market surveillance and enforcement. Violations may result in administrative fines and criminal penalties under the LFGB.

**Gesetz gegen den unlauteren Wettbewerb (UWG, Unfair Competition Act):** GI misuse may also constitute unfair commercial practices under the UWG, particularly through misleading geographical indications (Section 5 UWG) or imitation of protected designations (Section 4 No. 3 UWG), providing additional enforcement mechanisms through private litigation by competitor associations and consumer protection organizations.

### German Beer Purity Law and GI Interaction

The Bayerisches Reinheitsgebot (Bavarian Purity Law of 1516) and the national Vorläufiges Biergesetz (Provisional Beer Act) interact with GI protection for German beer. The Bayerisches Bier PGI registration requires compliance with the Reinheitsgebot's ingredient restrictions (water, malt, hops, and yeast), creating a synergy between traditional quality regulation and geographical indication protection. The PGI specification also requires that the beer be brewed entirely in Bavaria.

## ECHR & EU

### EU GI Regulation Framework

**Regulation (EU) 2024/1143:** The unified GI regulation, replacing the previously separate regulations for agricultural products (Regulation (EU) No 1151/2012), wine (elements of Regulation (EU) No 1308/2013), and spirit drinks (Regulation (EU) 2019/787), establishes a comprehensive framework for GI protection:

**Protected Designation of Origin (PDO):** Identifies a product originating in a specific place, region, or country whose quality or characteristics are essentially or exclusively due to a particular geographical environment with its inherent natural and human factors, and whose production steps all take place in the defined geographical area.

**Protected Geographical Indication (PGI):** Identifies a product originating in a specific place, region, or country, a given quality, reputation, or other characteristic of which is essentially attributable to its geographical origin, and at least one of the production steps of which takes place in the defined geographical area.

**Traditional Specialities Guaranteed (TSG):** Identifies a product resulting from a mode of production, processing, or composition corresponding to traditional practice, without a necessary link to a geographical area. Examples include Mozzarella TSG and Jamón Serrano TSG.

### Scope of Protection (Article 27)

The EU provides extensive protection for registered GIs against:

1. **Direct misuse:** Any use of a registered name for comparable products not compliant with the product specification
2. **Evocation:** Any misuse, imitation, or evocation, even if the true origin is indicated, the name is translated, or accompanied by expressions such as "style," "type," "method," "as produced in," "imitation," or similar
3. **Misleading indications:** Any false or misleading indication as to the provenance, origin, nature, or essential qualities on packaging, advertising, or documents
4. **Deceptive practices:** Any other practice liable to mislead the consumer as to the true origin of the product

### CJEU GI Jurisprudence

The CJEU has developed extensive case law interpreting the scope of GI protection:

**Consorzio del Prosciutto di Parma v. Asda Stores (Case C-108/01, 2003):** The Court held that a PDO designation may require that the product be sliced and packaged in the region of production if this is specified in the product specification and is justified by the need to preserve quality and ensure traceability.

**Feta -- Commission v. Germany and Denmark (Joined Cases C-465/02 and C-466/02, 2005):** The landmark case in which the CJEU upheld the registration of "Feta" as a PDO for Greek cheese, rejecting Germany and Denmark's arguments that "feta" had become a generic term. The Court deferred to the Commission's assessment based on consumption patterns, labeling practices, and the strength of the association between "Feta" and Greek origin.

**Scotch Whisky Association v. Klotz (Case C-44/17 -- Queso Manchego, 2019):** Actually decided as Queso Manchego (Case C-614/17), where the Court held that evocation of a PDO may occur through the use of figurative signs (images), not only verbal references, if the imagery is capable of triggering in the mind of a reasonably circumspect European consumer a direct link with the product bearing the PDO.

**Czech Republic v. Commission -- Champagne (Case T-495/18, 2020):** The General Court addressed the interaction between the Champagne PDO and the use of "Champagner Sorbet" as a designation for a food product containing Champagne as an ingredient, applying the criteria developed in the CJEU's case law on evocation.

### TRIPS Agreement (Articles 22-24)

The WTO TRIPS Agreement provides the international framework for GI protection:

**Article 22 (General Protection):** Requires WTO members to provide legal means to prevent the use of designations that mislead the public as to the geographical origin of goods and to prevent acts of unfair competition.

**Article 23 (Enhanced Protection for Wines and Spirits):** Provides enhanced protection for geographical indications identifying wines and spirits, prohibiting use even where the true origin is indicated or the geographical indication is accompanied by expressions such as "kind," "type," "style," or "imitation." This higher level of protection does not require proof of consumer confusion.

**Article 24 (Exceptions):** Permits continued use of geographical indications that have become generic or that were used in good faith prior to the TRIPS Agreement. The so-called "TRIPS claw-back" negotiations at the Doha Round sought to extend Article 23 enhanced protection to products beyond wines and spirits but have remained stalled since the mid-2000s.

### Bilateral Agreements

The EU has pursued an aggressive bilateral strategy to secure GI protection through trade agreements, including comprehensive GI chapters in agreements with:

- **Japan (EPA, 2019):** Mutual recognition of over 200 GIs
- **South Korea (FTA, 2011):** Mutual recognition of over 160 EU GIs
- **Canada (CETA, 2017):** Recognition of over 140 EU GIs, with exceptions for certain terms used in Canada
- **China (GI Agreement, 2021):** Mutual recognition of 200+ GIs (expanding in phases)

These agreements have been a major tool for extending the EU's sui generis model of GI protection beyond its borders, often generating resistance from countries with trademark-based approaches.

## Comparative Analysis

### Sui Generis versus Trademark Approaches

The fundamental philosophical divide is between the EU's sui generis GI system and the U.S. trademark approach. The EU system treats GIs as collective public rights belonging to all producers in a region who comply with the product specification, providing indefinite protection that cannot become generic. The U.S. trademark system treats GIs as private rights subject to the same rules as other marks, including vulnerability to genericide. South Korea's dual-track approach bridges these systems, while Germany's participation in the EU framework reflects the European tradition.

### Genericness and Evocation

The treatment of genericness represents the most politically contentious divergence. The EU's position that registered GIs can never become generic (upheld in Feta and subsequent cases) conflicts fundamentally with the U.S. genericness doctrine (affirmed in the Gruyere decision). The EU's broad evocation doctrine, which prohibits even indirect references to registered GIs, goes far beyond U.S. trademark confusion analysis. These differences have been a persistent obstacle in transatlantic trade negotiations.

### International Harmonization

The TRIPS Agreement provides minimum standards but fails to resolve the fundamental divergence between sui generis and trademark approaches. The stalled Doha Round negotiations on extending enhanced protection beyond wines and spirits reflect the depth of disagreement between the EU (seeking broader protection) and the United States, Australia, and other "new world" producers (defending the trademark approach and generic use of certain terms). Bilateral agreements have become the primary mechanism for extending GI protection, with the EU successfully embedding its sui generis model in FTAs worldwide.

### Economic Significance

GIs represent significant economic value across all jurisdictions. EU GIs generate an estimated annual sales value exceeding EUR 80 billion, with a price premium averaging 2.1 times that of comparable non-GI products. Korean GIs support rural economies and traditional food production. The U.S. certification mark system, while less comprehensive, protects valuable designations including Idaho potatoes, Vidalia onions, Florida oranges, and Napa Valley wine.
