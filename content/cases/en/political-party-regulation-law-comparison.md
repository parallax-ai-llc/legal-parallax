## Summary

Political party regulation -- the legal framework governing the formation, registration, internal governance, financing, and dissolution of political parties -- reflects fundamental choices about the relationship between organized political activity, democratic governance, and state authority. South Korea regulates political parties under the Political Parties Act (정당법) of 1962, as extensively amended, which imposes significant organizational requirements including mandatory establishment of metropolitan/provincial chapters in at least five regions, minimum membership thresholds, and registration with the National Election Commission (중앙선거관리위원회), while the Constitutional Court retains exclusive authority to dissolve parties that threaten the democratic basic order under Article 8(4) of the Constitution, a power exercised for the first and only time in the dissolution of the Unified Progressive Party (통합진보당) in 2014. The United States, uniquely among established democracies, has no comprehensive federal party regulation statute, with parties regulated primarily through state ballot access laws, the Federal Election Campaign Act's (FECA) campaign finance provisions, and constitutional jurisprudence protecting parties' First Amendment association rights, with the Supreme Court striking down numerous state regulations as infringements on freedom of association in decisions including California Democratic Party v. Jones, 530 U.S. 567 (2000), and Tashjian v. Republican Party of Connecticut, 479 U.S. 208 (1986). Germany's political party regulation is constitutionally anchored in Article 21 of the Grundgesetz, which uniquely recognizes parties as constitutionally necessary institutions of democratic governance, with the Parteiengesetz (PartG, Political Parties Act) of 1967 implementing detailed requirements for internal democracy (innerparteiliche Demokratie), financial transparency, and the Federal Constitutional Court's (BVerfG) exclusive jurisdiction to ban parties deemed unconstitutional, while the EU framework, including the Regulation on European political parties and the Venice Commission's Guidelines on Political Party Regulation (CDL-AD(2010)024), and the ECHR's jurisprudence under Articles 11 (freedom of association) and 3 of Protocol No. 1 (right to free elections), establishes that party dissolution is a last resort measure requiring compelling evidence of an imminent threat to democracy and must be proportionate to the aim pursued.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's political party regulation is among the most restrictive in democratic countries, reflecting the historical experience of authoritarian misuse of political parties and the ongoing security threat from North Korea.

**Political Parties Act (정당법):**

| Article | Requirement |
|---------|-------------|
| Article 3 | Definition: voluntary organization of citizens for participation in political will-formation |
| Article 4 | Registration with NEC; detailed organizational information |
| Article 17 | Must establish chapters in 5+ metropolitan cities/provinces |
| Article 18 | Each chapter must have 1,000+ members |
| Article 25 | General meeting of party members or delegates must be held at least annually |
| Article 29 | Internal democracy: leadership selection through democratic procedures |
| Article 38 | Dissolution by NEC for failure to meet registration requirements |
| Article 44 | Forced dissolution by Constitutional Court for threats to democratic basic order |

**Constitutional Framework:**

**Article 8 of the Constitution:**
1. "The establishment of political parties shall be free, and the plural party system shall be guaranteed."
2. "Political parties shall be democratic in their objectives, organization and activities, and shall have the necessary organizational arrangements for the people to participate in the formation of the political will."
3. "Political parties shall enjoy the protection of the State as prescribed by law, and may be provided with operational funds by the State under the conditions as prescribed by law."
4. "If the purposes or activities of a political party are contrary to the democratic basic order, the Government may bring an action against it in the Constitutional Court for its dissolution, and the political party shall be dissolved in accordance with the decision of the Constitutional Court."

### Unified Progressive Party Dissolution (2014)

**Constitutional Court Decision 2013HunDa1 (December 19, 2014):**

The dissolution of the Unified Progressive Party (통합진보당, UPP) was the first and only party dissolution in South Korea's constitutional history:

| Element | Finding |
|---------|---------|
| Grounds | UPP's objectives and activities contrary to democratic basic order |
| Evidence | Secret meeting discussing insurrection in the event of war; promotion of North Korean ideology (주체사상, Juche) |
| Vote | 8-1 (Justice Kim Yi-su dissenting) |
| Effect | Party dissolved; 5 National Assembly members lost seats |
| Proportionality | Court found dissolution proportionate given existential threat to democracy |

The decision was controversial both domestically and internationally:
- Venice Commission expressed concern about the scope of evidence and the dissolution's proportionality
- Amnesty International criticized the decision as disproportionate
- Supporters argued it was necessary to protect democracy from internal subversion

### Party System

South Korea's party system is characterized by frequent party creation, merger, and dissolution:
- Major parties regularly dissolve and reform under new names
- Personal faction-based politics rather than ideological permanence
- The ruling party has changed its name multiple times (e.g., Saenuri Party -> Liberty Korea Party -> People Power Party)
- This fluidity reflects the relatively weak institutionalization of Korean political parties compared to Western democracies

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States is distinctive for treating political parties primarily as private voluntary associations rather than public institutions requiring detailed regulation.

**Constitutional Framework:**
- **First Amendment:** "Congress shall make no law . . . abridging . . . the right of the people peaceably to assemble"
- Political parties enjoy robust First Amendment protection as associational organizations
- No federal statutory framework governing party formation, structure, or internal governance

**State Regulation:**
States regulate parties primarily through ballot access laws:

| Requirement | Typical Provisions |
|------------|-------------------|
| Party recognition | Meeting vote threshold in prior election (typically 2-5%) |
| New party qualification | Petition signatures (0.5-3% of registered voters) |
| Primary elections | State-mandated primary elections for candidate selection |
| Internal governance | Minimal state requirements; parties set own rules |

### Supreme Court Jurisprudence

**California Democratic Party v. Jones, 530 U.S. 567 (2000):** The Court struck down California's blanket primary system (allowing any voter to participate in any party's primary), holding that it violated parties' First Amendment right to choose their own candidates.

**Tashjian v. Republican Party of Connecticut, 479 U.S. 208 (1986):** The Court struck down a state law prohibiting parties from opening their primaries to independent voters, holding that parties have a constitutional right to define their own membership and participation criteria.

**Timmons v. Twin Cities Area New Party, 520 U.S. 351 (1997):** The Court upheld state bans on fusion candidacies (multiple parties nominating the same candidate), giving states latitude to maintain the two-party system's stability.

**Arkansas Educational Television Commission v. Forbes, 523 U.S. 666 (1998):** The Court upheld a public broadcaster's exclusion of a minor party candidate from a televised debate, finding no First Amendment violation.

### Two-Party System

The United States has a deeply entrenched two-party system, maintained through:
- Single-member plurality (FPTP) elections (Duverger's Law)
- State ballot access barriers for third parties
- Commission on Presidential Debates' threshold (15% in national polls)
- Campaign finance laws favoring major party candidates
- Gerrymandering reinforcing safe seats for the two major parties

**No federal dissolution authority:** The United States has never dissolved or attempted to dissolve a political party. The concept is regarded as fundamentally inconsistent with First Amendment values.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's party regulation is the most constitutionally developed in the world, reflecting the Grundgesetz's deliberate institutional design to prevent the failures of the Weimar Republic.

**Article 21 Grundgesetz:**
1. "Political parties shall participate in the formation of the political will of the people. They may be freely established. Their internal organization must conform to democratic principles. They must publicly account for their assets and for the sources and use of their funds and assets."
2. "Parties that, by reason of their aims or the behavior of their adherents, seek to undermine or abolish the free democratic basic order or to endanger the existence of the Federal Republic of Germany shall be unconstitutional."
3. [Added 2017] "Parties that, by reason of their aims or the behavior of their adherents, are oriented towards undermining or abolishing the free democratic basic order or endangering the existence of the Federal Republic of Germany shall be excluded from state financing."
4. "The Federal Constitutional Court shall rule on the questions of unconstitutionality within the meaning of paragraph (2) and on exclusion from state financing within the meaning of paragraph (3)."

**Parteiengesetz (PartG):**

| Section | Requirement |
|---------|-------------|
| § 1 | Constitutional status and function of parties |
| § 2 | Definition: associations of citizens that influence political will-formation on a permanent basis |
| § 6 | Party statutes and program required |
| § 7 | Organization: must be organized from bottom up; territorial structure |
| § 9 | General assembly (Mitglieder-/Parteitag) as supreme organ |
| § 15 | Equal voting rights for members in internal decisions |
| § 17 | Intra-party arbitration (Parteischiedsgerichte) required |
| § 23 | Annual financial report (Rechenschaftsbericht) to Bundestag President |
| § 31c | Sanctions for financial reporting violations |

### Internal Democracy (Innerparteiliche Demokratie)

The requirement for internal democratic organization is uniquely German:
- **Member participation:** Party congresses must be the supreme decision-making body
- **Secret ballot:** Leadership elections must be conducted by secret ballot
- **Equal rights:** All members must have equal voting rights
- **Minority protection:** Minorities within the party have procedural rights
- **Arbitration:** Internal disputes must be adjudicated by party arbitration courts

### Party Ban and Exclusion from Funding

**NPD Ban Proceedings:**

| Proceeding | Year | Outcome |
|-----------|------|---------|
| First NPD ban attempt | 2003 | Discontinued due to extensive use of government informants (V-Leute) in NPD leadership (BVerfGE 107, 339) |
| Second NPD ban attempt | 2017 | Application denied: NPD found to pursue unconstitutional goals but lacked the "potential" to achieve them (BVerfGE 144, 20) |
| NPD exclusion from state financing | 2024 | BVerfG excluded NPD (renamed "Die Heimat") from state financing under Art. 21(3) GG for 6 years |

**BVerfGE 144, 20 (2017, NPD II):** The Court introduced the "potentiality" criterion, holding that a party ban requires not only unconstitutional aims but also concrete evidence that the party has the capacity to achieve those aims. Since the NPD had declined to approximately 1 percent of the vote and held no significant political positions, the Court found it lacked the potentiality to endanger democracy. However, the Court explicitly found the NPD's aims to be unconstitutional, paving the way for the 2024 exclusion from state financing under the newly added Article 21(3) GG.

### AfD and the Verfassungsschutz

The Federal Office for the Protection of the Constitution (Bundesamt für Verfassungsschutz, BfV) has classified portions of the AfD as "suspected right-wing extremist" (Verdachtsfall) and certain state organizations as "confirmed right-wing extremist" (gesichert rechtsextremistisch). Several Länder Verfassungsschutz agencies have imposed similar classifications. As of 2026, political discussions about a potential AfD ban application continue, though no formal application to the BVerfG has been filed.

## ECHR / EU

### ECHR Jurisprudence on Party Dissolution

**Refah Partisi (The Welfare Party) and Others v. Turkey (Application Nos. 41340/98, 41342/98, 41343/98, 41344/98, Grand Chamber, February 13, 2003):** The landmark ECHR case on party dissolution. The Court upheld Turkey's dissolution of the Welfare Party (then the country's largest party), finding no violation of Article 11:

| Element | Court's Finding |
|---------|----------------|
| Interference | Yes, dissolution constituted interference with freedom of association |
| Prescribed by law | Yes, Turkish Constitutional Court had legal authority |
| Legitimate aim | Yes, protection of democracy and rights of others |
| Necessary in a democratic society | Yes, given leaders' statements advocating Sharia law, plurality of legal systems, and use of force |
| Proportionality | Yes, dissolution was proportionate given the imminent threat |

The Court introduced the "democracy capable of defending itself" (militant democracy) principle, holding that states may dissolve parties that threaten democratic values even before they have the opportunity to implement their anti-democratic program.

**United Communist Party of Turkey and Others v. Turkey (Application No. 19392/92, January 30, 1998):** The Court found a violation of Article 11 where Turkey dissolved a political party based solely on its name and program, without evidence that the party had engaged in or advocated any activity threatening democratic order.

**Herri Batasuna and Batasuna v. Spain (Application No. 25803/04, June 30, 2009):** The Court found no violation of Article 11 in the dissolution of two Basque political parties linked to ETA, holding that the dissolution was justified by the parties' support for terrorism.

**Republican Party of Russia v. Russia (Application No. 12976/07, April 12, 2011):** The Court found a violation of Article 11 where Russia dissolved a party for allegedly violating registration requirements (minimum regional chapters and membership), finding that the requirements were disproportionate and the dissolution was not necessary in a democratic society.

### Venice Commission Guidelines

CDL-AD(2010)024 (Guidelines on Political Party Regulation) establishes comprehensive standards:

| Principle | Standard |
|-----------|----------|
| Formation | Free establishment; minimal registration requirements |
| Internal democracy | Encouraged but not mandated; state regulation should be proportionate |
| Financing | Balanced public and private funding; transparency; independent oversight |
| Dissolution | Only by judicial decision; last resort; proportionate; compelling evidence |
| Equal treatment | State must treat all parties equally; no discrimination |

### EU Political Party Regulation

**Regulation (EU, Euratom) No 1141/2014:**
- European political parties must be registered with the Authority for European Political Parties (APPF)
- Requirements: legal personality in a member state; representation in at least 7 member states; respect for EU values
- Deregistration: APPF may deregister parties that manifestly and seriously breach EU values (Art. 2 TEU)
- The 2024 reform strengthened transparency and anti-foreign interference provisions

## Comparative Analysis

### Constitutional Status of Parties

The constitutional treatment of parties varies dramatically:
- **Germany:** Explicitly recognized as constitutionally necessary institutions (Art. 21 GG) with detailed constitutional requirements
- **South Korea:** Constitutional recognition with freedom of formation and dissolution authority (Art. 8)
- **United States:** Treated as private associations with First Amendment protections; no constitutional mention
- **ECHR:** Protected under freedom of association (Art. 11); states have wide margin of appreciation

### Militant Democracy

The concept of "militant democracy" (streitbare Demokratie / wehrhafte Demokratie) -- the principle that democracies may restrict anti-democratic forces to protect themselves -- is reflected differently:
- **Germany:** Explicit constitutional framework for party bans (Art. 21(2) GG); most developed militant democracy doctrine
- **South Korea:** Constitutional dissolution authority (Art. 8(4)); exercised in 2014
- **ECHR:** Endorsed in Refah Partisi; dissolution as last resort with strict proportionality
- **United States:** No militant democracy doctrine; parties cannot be dissolved; reliance on electoral competition and marketplace of ideas

### Internal Democracy Requirements

| Jurisdiction | Internal Democracy Required | Enforcement |
|-------------|---------------------------|-------------|
| Germany | Yes (constitutional requirement) | BVerfG; party arbitration courts |
| South Korea | Yes (statutory requirement) | NEC oversight |
| United States | No (parties set own rules) | First Amendment limits state regulation |
| ECHR/Venice Commission | Recommended but not required | State discretion |

### Digital Era Challenges for Party Regulation

Social media and digital campaigning have created new challenges for party regulation:

- **Disinformation:** All jurisdictions face difficulties regulating party-affiliated disinformation networks; Germany's NetzDG (Netzwerkdurchsetzungsgesetz, 2017) and the EU's Digital Services Act impose platform obligations, but parties themselves face limited direct regulation of online speech
- **Microtargeting:** Political advertising through algorithmically targeted social media raises transparency and manipulation concerns; the EU's proposed Political Advertising Regulation (2024) would require disclosure of targeting criteria and funding sources for all online political advertising
- **Deepfakes and AI-generated content:** The EU AI Act (2024) classifies AI systems generating political content as high-risk; South Korea amended the Public Official Election Act to prohibit AI-generated deepfakes within 90 days of elections; the United States lacks comprehensive federal regulation
- **Cryptocurrency donations:** The FEC permits Bitcoin and other cryptocurrency contributions subject to standard contribution limits; most other jurisdictions have not explicitly addressed cryptocurrency political donations

### Extremist Parties and the Limits of Tolerance

The treatment of extremist parties illustrates the tension between democratic pluralism and democratic self-defense:

- **Germany:** The BVerfG's 2017 NPD decision (2 BvB 1/13) created a middle ground between dissolution and inaction by confirming the party's unconstitutional aims while denying a ban due to lack of "potentiality" (Potentialität) to achieve those aims; the subsequent Article 21(3) GG amendment allowing exclusion from state financing represented a calibrated response
- **South Korea:** The 2014 dissolution of the Unified Progressive Party (2013HunDa1) was the first and only party dissolution in Korean democratic history; the Constitutional Court required evidence that the party posed a concrete danger to the democratic order
- **ECHR:** The Court has developed a graduated proportionality framework: dissolution is permissible only as a last resort, where the party poses a "plausible" threat and less restrictive measures would be insufficient (Herri Batasuna v. Spain, 2009)
