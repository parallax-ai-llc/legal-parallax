## Summary

The right to healthcare and the legal architecture of health systems in the Global South reveal starkly different models of state obligation, financing, and delivery. China's socialist legal system frames healthcare as a state responsibility implemented through the Basic Medical Insurance (BMI) system, which since the 2009 New Healthcare Reform has achieved near-universal coverage through three interlocking schemes: the Urban Employee Basic Medical Insurance (UEBMI, established 1998), the Urban Resident Basic Medical Insurance (URBMI, 2007), and the New Rural Cooperative Medical Scheme (NRCMS, 2003), the latter two merged into the Urban-Rural Resident Basic Medical Insurance (URRBMI) in 2016. India's hybrid common law system embeds the right to health within the constitutional right to life under Article 21, judicially expanded through landmark decisions such as Paschim Banga Khet Mazdoor Samity v. State of West Bengal (1996 AIR SC 2426) and operationalized since 2018 through the Ayushman Bharat Pradhan Mantri Jan Arogya Yojana (AB-PMJAY), the world's largest government-funded health insurance scheme covering 500 million beneficiaries. South Africa's Constitution of 1996 uniquely enshrines the right to healthcare in Section 27, enforceable through the Constitutional Court as demonstrated in Minister of Health v. Treatment Action Campaign (2002 (5) SA 721 (CC)), with the National Health Insurance Bill (NHI Bill, signed into law in May 2024) promising a single-payer universal system. Brazil's 1988 Constitution created the Unified Health System (Sistema Unico de Saude, SUS) under Articles 196-200, establishing health as a right of all and a duty of the state, financed through social security contributions and general taxation. The Philippines operates PhilHealth (Philippine Health Insurance Corporation), established under Republic Act No. 7875 (1995) and expanded dramatically by the Universal Health Care Act (Republic Act No. 11223, 2019), which automatically enrolls all Filipino citizens in the National Health Insurance Program. Indonesia's Jaminan Kesehatan Nasional (JKN), launched in 2014 under Law No. 24 of 2011 on Social Security Administering Bodies (BPJS), now covers over 200 million people through a single-payer model administered by BPJS Kesehatan. These systems reflect profoundly different legal traditions yet share common challenges of financing adequacy, quality assurance, rural-urban disparities, and the justiciability of health rights.

## China

### Constitutional and Legal Framework

China's approach to healthcare law is rooted in its socialist legal tradition, which treats social welfare as an integral function of the state-party apparatus rather than an individual justiciable right. Article 45 of the Constitution of the People's Republic of China (1982, as amended) provides that citizens have the right to material assistance from the state and society when old, ill, or disabled, and that the state develops social insurance, social relief, and medical and health services required for citizens to enjoy this right. However, unlike justiciable constitutional rights in liberal democracies, this provision operates primarily as a programmatic directive guiding state policy rather than as a basis for individual litigation.

The primary legislative framework consists of the Social Insurance Law of the People's Republic of China (enacted October 28, 2010, effective July 1, 2011), which consolidated previously disparate regulations into a unified statutory scheme. Chapter 3 (Articles 23-32) governs Basic Medical Insurance, mandating employer and employee contributions for urban workers and establishing the framework for government-subsidized insurance for non-employed urban and rural residents.

### The Three-Tier Insurance Structure

China's BMI system evolved through three separate schemes that have been progressively consolidated:

1. **Urban Employee Basic Medical Insurance (UEBMI):** Established by State Council Decision No. 44 of 1998 (Guofa [1998] No. 44, "Decision on Establishing the Basic Medical Insurance System for Urban Workers"), UEBMI requires mandatory participation by all urban employers and employees. Employers contribute approximately 6% of total payroll, while employees contribute 2% of their wages. The scheme operates through municipal-level pooling funds and individual medical savings accounts (MSAs). As of 2023, UEBMI covered approximately 370 million participants.

2. **New Rural Cooperative Medical Scheme (NRCMS):** Launched in 2003 under State Council guidelines, the NRCMS was a voluntary scheme for rural residents, financed through individual contributions (originally 10 RMB annually), local government subsidies, and central government transfers. By 2008, coverage had expanded from pilot counties to near-universal rural enrollment.

3. **Urban Resident Basic Medical Insurance (URBMI):** Introduced in 2007 for urban residents not covered by UEBMI (children, students, elderly, unemployed), the URBMI was financed primarily through government subsidies supplemented by individual contributions.

In 2016, the State Council issued the "Opinions on Integrating the Basic Medical Insurance Systems for Urban and Rural Residents" (Guofa [2016] No. 3), directing the merger of NRCMS and URBMI into the Urban-Rural Resident Basic Medical Insurance (URRBMI). This consolidation, largely completed by 2019, eliminated the urban-rural coverage disparity and standardized benefits. The per capita government subsidy for URRBMI reached 640 RMB in 2023.

### The National Healthcare Security Administration (NHSA)

The 2018 State Council institutional reform created the National Healthcare Security Administration (guojia yiliao baozhang ju), a vice-ministerial-level agency directly under the State Council. The NHSA consolidated functions previously dispersed across the Ministry of Human Resources and Social Security, the National Health Commission, the National Development and Reform Commission, and the Ministry of Civil Affairs. The NHSA administers BMI funds, negotiates drug prices (achieving dramatic reductions through centralized bulk procurement), manages the National Reimbursement Drug List (NRDL), and oversees the Medical Assistance program for low-income populations.

### 2009 New Healthcare Reform

The landmark "Opinions of the CPC Central Committee and the State Council on Deepening the Healthcare System Reform" (Zhongfa [2009] No. 6), issued on March 17, 2009, launched the most comprehensive healthcare reform since the founding of the PRC. The reform committed 850 billion RMB (approximately USD 124 billion) over three years and established five priorities: expanding insurance coverage, establishing a national essential medicines system, strengthening primary care, equalizing basic public health services, and piloting public hospital reform. By 2011, coverage had expanded from approximately 45% to over 95% of the population.

### Challenges and Limitations

Despite near-universal enrollment, China's healthcare system faces persistent structural challenges. Reimbursement rates remain uneven, with UEBMI offering substantially higher benefits than URRBMI. The hukou (household registration) system restricts cross-provincial medical insurance portability, disadvantaging the approximately 376 million internal migrants (2023 National Bureau of Statistics data). Out-of-pocket expenditure still constituted approximately 27.6% of total health expenditure in 2022 (National Health Commission statistical bulletin). The dispute resolution mechanism remains predominantly administrative rather than judicial, with patients filing complaints through healthcare security hotlines (12393) and administrative review processes rather than through courts.

## India

### Constitutional Foundation

India's approach to healthcare law exemplifies the creative judicial expansion of constitutional rights within a common law framework. The Constitution of India does not explicitly guarantee a right to healthcare as a fundamental right. Instead, healthcare is addressed through the Directive Principles of State Policy (DPSP): Article 39(e) directs the state to ensure that the health and strength of workers are not abused; Article 41 provides for the right to public assistance in cases of sickness and disablement; and Article 47 imposes a duty on the state to raise the level of nutrition and the standard of living and to improve public health.

However, the Supreme Court of India has progressively read the right to health into the enforceable fundamental right to life under Article 21 through a series of landmark decisions:

- **Paschim Banga Khet Mazdoor Samity v. State of West Bengal (1996 AIR SC 2426):** The Supreme Court held that failure of a government hospital to provide timely medical treatment to a person in need constitutes a violation of the right to life under Article 21. Justice S.B. Majmudar, writing for the bench, declared: "The Constitution envisages the establishment of a welfare state at the federal level as well as at the state level. In a welfare state, the primary duty of the government is to secure the welfare of the people."

- **Consumer Education and Research Centre v. Union of India (1995 AIR SC 922):** The Court held that the right to health and medical care is a fundamental right under Article 21 read with Articles 39(e), 41, and 43 of the DPSP.

- **Mohd. Ahmed (Minor) v. Union of India (2014 (3) SCALE 651):** Reinforced the state's obligation to provide healthcare facilities to all citizens, particularly children.

### Ayushman Bharat Pradhan Mantri Jan Arogya Yojana (AB-PMJAY)

Launched on September 23, 2018, by Prime Minister Narendra Modi, AB-PMJAY is the world's largest government-funded health insurance scheme. The program provides health coverage of up to INR 5 lakh (approximately USD 6,000) per family per year for secondary and tertiary care hospitalization to approximately 120 million families (500 million individuals) identified through the Socio-Economic Caste Census (SECC) 2011 data in rural areas and occupational criteria in urban areas.

The scheme operates without a dedicated statute; it was established through executive order and is administered by the National Health Authority (NHA), an autonomous body under the Ministry of Health and Family Welfare constituted through a Government of India order dated January 2, 2019. Beneficiaries are entitled to cashless and paperless treatment at any empanelled hospital (public or private) across India, with portability across state lines.

As of 2024, AB-PMJAY had facilitated over 60 million hospital admissions, with over 28,000 empanelled hospitals. State co-financing (60:40 ratio for most states, 90:10 for northeastern and special category states) has enabled variable implementation across India's 28 states and 8 union territories. Several states, including Rajasthan (Chiranjeevi scheme), Tamil Nadu (Chief Minister's Comprehensive Health Insurance Scheme), and Kerala (Karunya Arogya Suraksha Padhathi), operate their own schemes that top up or parallel AB-PMJAY.

### Judicial Enforcement and the Judicializacao Problem

India's Public Interest Litigation (PIL) mechanism, established through the relaxation of locus standi rules in S.P. Gupta v. Union of India (1981 Supp SCC 87), has enabled extensive judicial intervention in healthcare policy. Notable examples include:

- **Laxmi Mandal v. Deen Dayal Harinagar Hospital (2010 Delhi HC):** The Delhi High Court held that denial of maternity care to a woman from a marginalized community violated her right to life and reproductive rights.
- **Common Cause v. Union of India (2018 (5) SCC 1):** The Supreme Court recognized the right to die with dignity, establishing the legal framework for advance medical directives and passive euthanasia.

### Regulatory Framework

The Clinical Establishments (Registration and Regulation) Act, 2010, provides for registration and regulation of clinical establishments (hospitals, clinics, laboratories) to prescribe minimum standards of facilities and services. However, as health is a concurrent subject under the Seventh Schedule (Entry 6, List III), adoption requires state ratification, and as of 2025, only 14 states and all Union Territories have adopted the Act. The National Medical Commission Act, 2019 (replacing the Indian Medical Council Act, 1956), restructured medical education regulation through the National Medical Commission (NMC), addressing chronic issues of corruption and regulatory capture that plagued the Medical Council of India.

## Africa

### South Africa: Constitutional Right to Healthcare

South Africa's Constitution of 1996 provides the most robust justiciable right to healthcare on the African continent. Section 27 states:

> (1) Everyone has the right to have access to -- (a) health care services, including reproductive health care; (b) sufficient food and water; and (c) social security...
> (2) The state must take reasonable legislative and other measures, within its available resources, to achieve the progressive realisation of each of these rights.

The Constitutional Court has given teeth to this provision through several landmark rulings:

- **Soobramoney v. Minister of Health, KwaZulu-Natal (1998 (1) SA 765 (CC)):** While upholding the state's rationing decision to deny renal dialysis to a terminally ill patient, the Court established the framework for reviewing healthcare allocation decisions under Section 27, applying a reasonableness standard rather than a minimum core obligation approach.

- **Minister of Health v. Treatment Action Campaign (TAC) (2002 (5) SA 721 (CC)):** In what remains the most consequential health rights case in the Global South, the Constitutional Court ordered the government to provide nevirapine to HIV-positive pregnant women to prevent mother-to-child transmission. The Court held that the government's refusal to make the drug available at public hospitals outside pilot sites was unreasonable and violated Section 27. Justice Yacoob, writing for a unanimous Court, held that the government was constitutionally obligated to devise and implement a comprehensive program to progressively realize the right of access to healthcare.

- **Madala Mapasa Mankayi v. AngloGold Ashanti Ltd (2011 (3) SA 237 (CC)):** The Court allowed silicosis claims by mineworkers against their employer, opening the door to massive class-action litigation (the 2018 Tshiamiso Trust settlement of approximately ZAR 5 billion for affected gold miners).

### South Africa's National Health Insurance Act (2024)

President Cyril Ramaphosa signed the National Health Insurance Act (Act No. 20 of 2024) on May 15, 2024, establishing the legal framework for a single-payer, single-purchaser universal healthcare system. Key provisions include:

- Establishment of the NHI Fund as a single national purchaser of health services (Chapter 4)
- Universal coverage for all South African citizens, permanent residents, and certain categories of refugees and asylum seekers (Section 4)
- Mandatory registration of all users and healthcare providers with the NHI Fund (Sections 5-6)
- The NHI Fund will purchase personal healthcare services on behalf of users from accredited and contracted health establishments and providers (Section 11)
- Phased implementation over approximately 15 years, with full implementation contingent on financial sustainability assessments

The Act has faced immediate legal challenges, with the South African Medical Association (SAMA), Business Unity South Africa (BUSA), and the Democratic Alliance (DA) filing constitutional challenges arguing violations of Section 27 (right to access healthcare), Section 22 (freedom of trade), and Section 36 (limitation of rights). Implementation regulations remain pending as of early 2026.

### Nigeria: Fragmented Healthcare Governance

Nigeria's Constitution (1999, as amended) addresses healthcare through Section 17(3)(d), which provides that the state shall direct its policy towards ensuring that "the health, safety and welfare of all persons in employment are safeguarded and not endangered or abused." However, Section 6(6)(c) explicitly excludes Chapter II (Fundamental Objectives and Directive Principles of State Policy) from judicial enforcement, rendering the right to health non-justiciable.

The National Health Act (2014) established the Basic Health Care Provision Fund (BHCPF), financed through not less than 1% of the Consolidated Revenue Fund, grants from international donors, and other sources. The National Health Insurance Authority Act (2022, replacing the NHIS Act 1999) reconstituted the National Health Insurance Authority (NHIA) and introduced mandatory health insurance for all Nigerians, though implementation remains severely limited. The Formal Sector Social Health Insurance Programme (FSSHIP) covers federal civil servants, while state-level schemes and the Vulnerable Group Fund target the poor.

Nigeria's federal structure compounds challenges: the Constitution places healthcare on the Concurrent Legislative List, and with 36 states plus the FCT operating semi-autonomous health systems, regulatory coherence is elusive. As of 2024, the National Health Insurance Authority reported approximately 15 million enrollees out of a population of over 220 million.

## Latin America

### Brazil: The Unified Health System (SUS)

Brazil's 1988 Constitution, drafted during the democratic transition after 21 years of military dictatorship, contains the most expansive constitutional right to health in the Americas. Article 196 declares:

> "Health is a right of all and a duty of the state, guaranteed through social and economic policies aimed at reducing the risk of disease and other health hazards and at universal and equal access to actions and services for the promotion, protection, and recovery of health."

Articles 197-200 establish the Unified Health System (Sistema Unico de Saude, SUS), organized around three principles: universality (universalidade), comprehensiveness (integralidade), and equity (equidade). The Organic Health Law (Lei No. 8.080 of September 19, 1990) and the Social Participation Law (Lei No. 8.142 of December 28, 1990) operationalize the constitutional framework, establishing decentralized management with three levels (federal, state, municipal), social participation through health councils (conselhos de saude), and financing through the social security budget.

### Judicialization of Healthcare (Judicializacao da Saude)

Brazil has experienced the most extensive judicialization of health rights in the world. Brazilian courts receive hundreds of thousands of health-related lawsuits annually, with individual litigants seeking judicial orders (tutela antecipada) compelling the state to provide specific medications, treatments, or procedures not included in SUS protocols. The Federal Supreme Court (Supremo Tribunal Federal, STF) has addressed this phenomenon in several landmark decisions:

- **STA-RE 657.718 (2019):** The STF established criteria for judicial orders requiring provision of medicines not registered with ANVISA (National Health Surveillance Agency), holding that courts may order provision only where: (1) there is no registered substitute; (2) there is a pending registration application with ANVISA; and (3) the medicine is registered in reputable international regulatory agencies.

- **Recurso Extraordinario 566.471 (Theme 6, 2020):** The STF ruled on the state's obligation to provide high-cost medications, establishing that the state must supply medicines incorporated into SUS protocols and may be ordered to supply others in exceptional circumstances based on medical evidence.

The National Justice Council (CNJ) created specialized health courts (Varas Especializadas em Saude) and the e-NatJus system (National Technological Assistance Nucleus for the Judiciary) to provide technical support to judges deciding health cases. In 2023, the CNJ reported over 670,000 pending health-related lawsuits in Brazilian courts.

### Mexico and Colombia

Mexico's 2003 General Health Law reform created the System of Social Protection in Health (Seguro Popular), which by 2018 covered approximately 53 million previously uninsured Mexicans. In 2020, President Lopez Obrador replaced Seguro Popular with the Institute of Health for Well-Being (INSABI), and in 2023 further restructured the system through IMSS-Bienestar, transferring administration to the Mexican Social Security Institute. Mexico's constitutional right to health protection (Article 4) has been enforced through amparo proceedings before federal courts.

Colombia's Constitutional Court (Corte Constitucional) has been the most activist health rights court in Latin America. The landmark Sentencia T-760 of 2008 ordered comprehensive reform of the health system, directing the government to unify the contributory (Regimen Contributivo) and subsidized (Regimen Subsidiado) benefit plans, ensure portability of coverage, and eliminate barriers to access. Colombia's accion de tutela mechanism, a rapid constitutional remedy, generates tens of thousands of health-related judicial orders annually.

## Southeast Asia

### Indonesia: Jaminan Kesehatan Nasional (JKN)

Indonesia launched the Jaminan Kesehatan Nasional (National Health Insurance, JKN) on January 1, 2014, operationalizing the mandate of Law No. 40 of 2004 on the National Social Security System (Sistem Jaminan Sosial Nasional, SJSN) and Law No. 24 of 2011 on Social Security Administering Bodies (Badan Penyelenggara Jaminan Sosial, BPJS). BPJS Kesehatan (BPJS Health) serves as the single-payer entity administering the JKN program.

The constitutional basis lies in Article 28H(1) of the 1945 Constitution (as amended in 2000): "Every person has the right to live in physical and mental well-being, to have a place to live, to enjoy a good and healthy environment, and to receive medical care." Article 34(3) further provides: "The state is responsible for the provision of adequate health care facilities and public service facilities."

JKN operates through three participant categories: (1) Penerima Bantuan Iuran (PBI) -- premium assistance beneficiaries whose contributions are paid by the government (covering approximately 96.8 million poor and near-poor individuals as of 2024); (2) Pekerja Penerima Upah (PPU) -- salaried workers whose premiums are shared between employer and employee; and (3) Pekerja Bukan Penerima Upah (PBPU) -- independent workers who pay their own premiums.

### Indonesian Constitutional Court Review

The Indonesian Constitutional Court (Mahkamah Konstitusi, MK) has played an important role in shaping JKN policy. In Decision No. 7/PUU-XVIII/2020, the MK reviewed the constitutionality of Presidential Regulation No. 75 of 2019, which had increased JKN premiums by 100% for most participant categories. While upholding the government's authority to adjust premiums, the MK held that the premium increase process must comply with principles of transparency, participation, and accountability, and struck down the premium increase for PBI participants as disproportionately burdening the poor.

BPJS Kesehatan has faced chronic deficits since inception, with cumulative losses exceeding IDR 28 trillion (approximately USD 1.8 billion) by 2019. Government interventions have included premium increases, benefit package adjustments, and fiscal transfers from the general budget (APBN injection of IDR 26.7 trillion in 2024).

### Indonesia: Adat and Health

Indonesia's legal pluralism intersects with healthcare through adat (customary) practices. Presidential Regulation No. 72 of 2012 on the National Health System recognizes traditional health services (pelayanan kesehatan tradisional) alongside modern medicine. Law No. 36 of 2009 on Health (Articles 59-61) regulates traditional medicine practices, distinguishing between empirical traditional medicine (based on experience, such as jamu herbal medicine) and complementary traditional medicine (using biomedical technology). The integration of traditional and modern medicine remains contested, with the Indonesian Ulema Council (MUI) issuing fatwa on the halal status of medicines and vaccines, notably influencing public health campaigns.

### Philippines: PhilHealth and the Universal Health Care Act

The Philippines' Universal Health Care Act (Republic Act No. 11223, signed February 20, 2019) represents the most ambitious health law reform in Southeast Asian history. The Act mandates:

- Automatic enrollment of all Filipino citizens in the National Health Insurance Program (Section 5)
- Immediate eligibility without prior premium payment for indigent, senior citizen, and other vulnerable populations (Section 6)
- Integration of PhilHealth with primary care provider networks and health care provider networks (Sections 14-21)
- Population-based funding allocation through province-wide and city-wide health systems (Section 23)

PhilHealth (Philippine Health Insurance Corporation), established under Republic Act No. 7875 (National Health Insurance Act of 1995), serves as the implementing agency. The UHC Act increased the premium rate from 2.75% to a target of 5% of monthly income by 2025 (Section 10), with premium subsidies for indigent members funded through sin taxes on tobacco and alcohol (Republic Act No. 10351, Sin Tax Reform Law of 2012, and RA 11346, 2019 amendment).

The Philippine Supreme Court has addressed health rights through several mechanisms, including the Writ of Amparo (A.M. No. 07-9-12-SC, 2007) for threats to life and security, and the Writ of Kalikasan (A.M. No. 09-6-8-SC, 2010) for environmental health. In Pharmaceutical and Health Care Association of the Philippines v. Duque (2007, G.R. No. 173034), the Supreme Court upheld the validity of Executive Order No. 51 implementing the International Code of Marketing of Breast-Milk Substitutes, affirming the state's authority to regulate health-related commercial practices.

## Comparative Analysis

### Convergences

Despite fundamentally different legal traditions, Global South healthcare systems exhibit remarkable convergences:

1. **Universal Coverage Aspiration:** All five regions have committed to universal health coverage (UHC), reflecting the influence of WHO frameworks and SDG Target 3.8. The methods differ (social insurance in China and Indonesia, tax-funded in Brazil and South Africa, hybrid in India and the Philippines), but the commitment to expanding coverage is shared.

2. **Constitutional Entrenchment:** Every system except China has constitutionalized the right to health, though enforceability varies dramatically. The spectrum runs from fully justiciable (South Africa, Brazil, Colombia) to partially justiciable (India through Article 21 interpretation, Indonesia, Philippines) to non-justiciable (Nigeria, China).

3. **Judicialization Pressures:** Brazil, India, Colombia, and South Africa all face significant judicialization of health rights, where courts order the provision of specific treatments, medications, or services. This creates tension between judicial enforcement of individual rights and the executive's allocation of scarce resources.

4. **Fragmented Governance:** Federal or decentralized systems (India, Nigeria, Brazil, Indonesia, Philippines) face coordination challenges across multiple levels of government, leading to disparities in coverage quality and access.

### Divergences

1. **Financing Models:** China and Indonesia rely primarily on contributory social insurance; Brazil and South Africa use general taxation; India uses targeted government-funded insurance for the poor; the Philippines employs a hybrid of earmarked taxes and contributory premiums. These choices reflect different political economies and state capacity.

2. **Role of the Private Sector:** South Africa and India have large private healthcare sectors serving the wealthy, creating two-tier systems. Brazil's SUS coexists with a substantial supplementary private insurance market. China's public hospital system retains dominance despite growing private provision. The Philippines and Indonesia maintain mixed systems where public and private providers participate in the national insurance schemes.

3. **Traditional Medicine Integration:** Indonesia, India, China, and several African countries formally integrate traditional medicine into their healthcare legal frameworks, while Brazil and the Philippines take more cautious regulatory approaches. India's AYUSH (Ayurveda, Yoga, Unani, Siddha, Homeopathy) ministry, established in 2014, and China's Traditional Chinese Medicine Law (2017) represent the most institutionalized approaches.

4. **State Accountability Mechanisms:** The enforceability spectrum reflects deeper differences in legal culture. China's administrative complaint mechanisms operate within a system where judicial independence from the party-state is structurally limited. India and Brazil's PIL/acao civil publica mechanisms empower civil society organizations and individuals to challenge state inaction. South Africa's Constitutional Court serves as the ultimate arbiter of state compliance with health obligations.
