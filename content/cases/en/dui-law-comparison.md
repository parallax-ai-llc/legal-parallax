## Summary

Driving under the influence of alcohol remains one of the most significant public safety challenges worldwide, yet the legal frameworks governing its prohibition, detection, and punishment vary substantially across jurisdictions. South Korea applies one of the strictest blood alcohol concentration (BAC) thresholds in the world at 0.03%, enacted through the landmark "Yoon Chang-ho Act" of 2018-2019, with penalties escalating to life imprisonment for fatal DUI accidents. The United States sets its standard BAC limit at 0.08% (0.05% in Utah) but fragments enforcement across 50 state jurisdictions, each with distinct penalty structures, implied consent laws, and administrative sanction regimes. Germany employs a distinctive three-tier system distinguishing between administrative infractions (0.05% BAC / 0.5 per mille), relative unfitness to drive (0.03% BAC / 0.3 per mille with evidence of impairment), and absolute unfitness to drive (0.11% BAC / 1.1 per mille), the latter established through landmark BGH jurisprudence. The European Court of Human Rights and the EU framework address DUI primarily through recommendations on BAC limits, cross-border enforcement directives, and the evolving recognition that states bear positive obligations under the European Convention to protect life through effective road safety regulation.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's DUI laws are principally governed by the Road Traffic Act (dorogyotongbeop), with the key provisions found in Article 44 (prohibition of drunk driving) and Article 148-2 (penalties for violations). The framework underwent a dramatic transformation following the Yoon Chang-ho incident of 2018, which resulted in two rounds of legislative amendments collectively known as the "Yoon Chang-ho Act."

**Article 44 -- Prohibition of Drunk Driving:** No person shall drive a motor vehicle or a bicycle while under the influence of alcohol. The legal threshold for intoxication is a blood alcohol concentration of 0.03% or higher, lowered from the previous 0.05% standard by the first Yoon Chang-ho Act amendment effective December 18, 2018.

**Article 148-2 -- Penalties for Drunk Driving:**

| BAC Level | First Offense | Repeat Offense (3rd+) |
|-----------|---------------|----------------------|
| 0.03% - 0.20% | 1-5 years imprisonment or 5-20 million KRW fine | 1-3 years imprisonment or 5-10 million KRW fine |
| 0.20% or above | 2-5 years imprisonment or 10-20 million KRW fine | Enhanced penalties apply |
| Test refusal | 1-5 years imprisonment or 5-20 million KRW fine | Same as above |

**Special Act on Traffic Accidents (Teukjeongbeomjoega-jung-cheobeolle-gwanhan-beopyul):** When a DUI accident causes injury, the driver faces 1 to 15 years imprisonment or a fine of 10 to 30 million KRW. When a DUI accident causes death, the driver faces life imprisonment or a minimum of 3 years imprisonment.

### The Yoon Chang-ho Act (2018-2019)

On September 25, 2018, Yoon Chang-ho, a 22-year-old army conscript and college student, was struck by a drunk driver while walking in Busan. After spending 46 days on life support, Yoon was pronounced brain dead and died. The public outrage that followed drove the Korean National Assembly to enact the most significant reform of DUI law in the country's history.

**First Yoon Chang-ho Act (December 18, 2018):** Lowered the legal BAC limit from 0.05% to 0.03%, making South Korea's standard among the strictest in the world. Enhanced criminal penalties across all BAC tiers.

**Second Yoon Chang-ho Act (June 25, 2019):** Introduced the possibility of life imprisonment for DUI accidents causing death. Established the "three strikes" rule for permanent licence revocation after three DUI convictions.

The drunk driver who struck Yoon Chang-ho was sentenced to six years in prison, a result that Yoon's father publicly criticized as insufficient, stating his intention to appeal.

### Administrative Consequences

- **Licence suspension:** BAC of 0.03% to 0.08% triggers a 100-day licence suspension.
- **Licence revocation:** BAC of 0.08% or above triggers immediate licence revocation.
- **Permanent revocation:** Three DUI convictions result in permanent licence revocation with no possibility of reinstatement.
- **Insurance surcharges:** First DUI conviction triggers a 10% insurance surcharge for 2 years; second or subsequent convictions trigger a 20% surcharge for 2 years.

### Constitutional Review

In December 2025, the Constitutional Court of Korea upheld the constitutionality of the aggravated penalties for third-offense drunk driving, rejecting arguments that the mandatory minimum sentences violated the principle of proportionality. The court held that the severity of the penalties was justified by the grave danger posed by habitual drunk drivers and the state's compelling interest in road safety.

### 2026 Legislative Changes

Effective April 2, 2026, South Korea will implement sweeping amendments specifically targeting drug-impaired driving. Under revised Article 45(2), police officers will have authority to stop and test drivers suspected of drug impairment using saliva swabs, granting unprecedented enforcement tools for substance-impaired driving beyond alcohol.

## United States

### Legal Framework

DUI law in the United States operates across federal, state, and local jurisdictions, producing a patchwork of statutes, penalties, and enforcement mechanisms. There is no single federal DUI statute governing all drivers; instead, the federal government influences state law through fiscal incentives, most notably 23 U.S.C. Section 158, which conditions federal highway funding on states adopting a minimum legal drinking age of 21, and the subsequent legislative push through the Department of Transportation Act of 2000 that encouraged states to adopt 0.08% BAC as the per se intoxication standard.

**Per Se BAC Limits:**

| Category | BAC Limit | Basis |
|----------|-----------|-------|
| General drivers (age 21+) | 0.08% (all states except Utah) | State statutes; federal incentive under 23 U.S.C. Section 164 |
| General drivers in Utah | 0.05% | Utah Code Section 41-6a-502 (effective December 30, 2018) |
| Commercial vehicle operators | 0.04% | 49 CFR Section 382.201 |
| Drivers under 21 | 0.00%-0.02% | Zero tolerance laws in all 50 states |

**Enhanced Penalty Thresholds:** Most states impose aggravated or "extreme" DUI charges at BAC levels of 0.15% or 0.16%, triggering mandatory minimum sentences, higher fines, and extended licence suspensions.

### State Variation in Penalties

| State | First Offense | Aggravated DUI | Felony DUI Threshold |
|-------|---------------|----------------|---------------------|
| Arizona | Mandatory 10-day jail minimum, $1,250+ fine | 30-day jail minimum (.15%+ BAC) | 3rd offense within 7 years |
| California | 48 hours - 6 months jail, $390-$1,000 fine | Enhanced penalties at .15%+ | 4th offense within 10 years |
| Texas | Up to 180 days jail, $2,000 fine | Enhanced at .15%+ | 3rd offense |
| New York | Up to 1 year jail, $500-$1,000 fine | Aggravated at .18%+ | Multiple offenses within 10 years |
| Alaska | Mandatory 72-hour minimum, $1,500 fine | Enhanced penalties apply | 2nd felony DUI or DUI causing serious injury |

### Landmark Cases

**Birchfield v. North Dakota, 579 U.S. 438 (2016):** The Supreme Court held that the search-incident-to-arrest doctrine permits warrantless breath tests but not warrantless blood tests on suspected drunk drivers. The 7-1 majority opinion, authored by Justice Alito, established that breath tests are minimally invasive and fall within the search-incident-to-arrest exception to the Fourth Amendment warrant requirement, while blood tests involve a more significant intrusion into bodily integrity and therefore require a warrant. The court also held that states may not impose criminal penalties for refusal to submit to a warrantless blood test, while affirming that criminal penalties for refusing a breath test remain constitutionally permissible.

**South Dakota v. Neville, 459 U.S. 553 (1983):** The Supreme Court held that admitting evidence of a suspect's refusal to submit to a blood-alcohol test does not violate the Fifth Amendment privilege against self-incrimination. The Court reasoned that the right to refuse a blood test is not a constitutional right but rather a matter of state grace under implied consent statutes.

**Michigan Department of State Police v. Sitz, 496 U.S. 444 (1990):** The Supreme Court upheld the constitutionality of sobriety checkpoints under the Fourth Amendment, holding that the brief seizure involved in a checkpoint stop is reasonable given the state's substantial interest in preventing drunk driving. The Court applied a balancing test weighing the gravity of the public concern, the degree to which the checkpoint advances that interest, and the severity of the interference with individual liberty.

**Missouri v. McNeely, 569 U.S. 141 (2013):** The Supreme Court rejected the argument that the natural dissipation of alcohol in the bloodstream constitutes a per se exigent circumstance justifying warrantless blood draws in every DUI case. The Court held that the totality of the circumstances must be considered in determining whether exigency exists.

### Implied Consent Laws

All 50 states have enacted implied consent statutes providing that any person who operates a motor vehicle on public roads has impliedly consented to chemical testing for intoxication. However, the consequences for refusal vary significantly:

- **Administrative consequences:** All states impose licence suspension for refusal, typically ranging from 90 days to 1 year for a first refusal.
- **Criminal penalties:** Some states criminalize refusal itself, though the constitutionality of criminalizing blood test refusal was curtailed by Birchfield.
- **Evidentiary consequences:** Most states allow the fact of refusal to be admitted as evidence of consciousness of guilt at trial.

### Ignition Interlock Devices

As of 2025, 34 states require ignition interlock devices (IIDs) for all DUI convictions, including first offenses. IIDs require the driver to provide a breath sample below a preset BAC threshold before the vehicle will start. The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) has been developing passive alcohol detection technology under the DADSS (Driver Alcohol Detection System for Safety) program, which could make BAC testing automatic in all new vehicles by 2026-2027.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany employs a sophisticated three-tier system for addressing alcohol-impaired driving, distinguishing between administrative infractions (Ordnungswidrigkeiten), criminal offenses requiring evidence of impairment (relative Fahruntuechtigkeit), and criminal offenses with irrebuttable presumption of impairment (absolute Fahruntuechtigkeit).

**Tier 1 -- Administrative Offense (Section 24a StVG):**

Operating a motor vehicle with a BAC between 0.05% and 0.109% (0.5 to 1.09 per mille) without exhibiting signs of impairment constitutes an administrative infraction (Ordnungswidrigkeit) under Section 24a of the Road Traffic Act (Strassenverkehrsgesetz, StVG).

| Occurrence | Fine | Driving Ban | Points (Flensburg) |
|-----------|------|-------------|---------------------|
| First offense | 500 EUR | 1 month | 2 points |
| Second offense | 1,000 EUR | 3 months | 2 points |
| Third offense | 1,500 EUR | 3 months | 2 points |

**Tier 2 -- Relative Unfitness to Drive (Section 316 StGB):**

A driver with a BAC of 0.03% or above (0.3 per mille) who exhibits alcohol-related driving errors or impairment symptoms commits a criminal offense under Section 316 of the Criminal Code (Strafgesetzbuch, StGB). The prosecution must prove both the BAC level and concrete evidence of impaired driving ability, such as swerving, running red lights, or involvement in an accident. Penalties include imprisonment of up to 1 year or a fine, along with licence revocation.

**Tier 3 -- Absolute Unfitness to Drive (Sections 315c, 316 StGB):**

At a BAC of 0.11% or above (1.1 per mille), the driver is irrebuttably presumed to be unfit to drive, regardless of whether any signs of impairment are observed. This threshold was established through landmark jurisprudence of the Bundesgerichtshof (Federal Court of Justice, BGH), which lowered the threshold from 1.3 per mille to 1.1 per mille in a seminal 1990 decision.

- **Section 316 StGB (Drunkenness in traffic):** Up to 1 year imprisonment or fine for operating a vehicle while unfit due to alcohol.
- **Section 315c StGB (Endangering road traffic):** Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine when the intoxicated driver endangers the life, limb, or property of another person.

### Special Rules for Novice and Young Drivers

Under Section 24c StVG, a strict zero-tolerance policy (0.00% BAC) applies to all drivers under the age of 21 and to all drivers during their probationary period (Probezeit, typically 2 years after obtaining a licence). Any detectable alcohol constitutes an infraction punishable by a 250 EUR fine, 1 point in the Flensburg register, and extension of the probationary period.

### The MPU Requirement

A distinctive feature of German DUI law is the Medizinisch-Psychologische Untersuchung (MPU), colloquially known as the "Idiotentest." Drivers whose licences have been revoked due to alcohol offenses must pass this medical-psychological examination before reinstatement. The MPU is required in the following circumstances:

- Any DUI with BAC of 0.16% (1.6 per mille) or above
- Repeat DUI offenses
- DUI in combination with other traffic offenses
- Drug-impaired driving

The MPU has a failure rate of approximately 35-40%, making licence reinstatement a significant practical hurdle. The examination assesses both medical fitness (including liver function tests and abstinence markers) and psychological readiness to drive responsibly.

### Landmark Jurisprudence

**BGH Decision on the 1.1 Per Mille Threshold (1990):** The Bundesgerichtshof lowered the threshold for absolute unfitness to drive from 1.3 per mille to 1.1 per mille, following scientific evidence that virtually all drivers are significantly impaired at this level. The decision of the fourth criminal panel (4. Strafsenat) established that at 1.1 per mille, the presumption of unfitness is irrebuttable (unwiderlegbare Vermutung), meaning no evidence of actual driving competence can overcome the legal conclusion of impairment.

**BGH Decision on the 0.3 Per Mille Threshold:** The Federal Court confirmed that even at BAC levels as low as 0.3 per mille, a driver can be criminally liable under Section 316 StGB if concrete evidence of impaired driving ability exists. This established the lower boundary of potential criminal responsibility for alcohol-impaired driving.

### E-Scooter and Bicycle Rules

German courts have extended drunk driving rules to e-scooters, treating them identically to motor vehicles for purposes of BAC limits and criminal liability. Bicycles, however, are subject to a higher absolute unfitness threshold of 0.16% (1.6 per mille), established by BGH jurisprudence.

## ECHR & EU

### European Convention on Human Rights

The European Court of Human Rights (ECtHR) does not directly legislate DUI offenses but addresses drunk driving through several Convention articles that impose obligations on member states and protect the rights of accused persons.

**Article 2 -- Right to Life:** The ECtHR has interpreted Article 2 as imposing positive obligations on states to take appropriate steps to safeguard the lives of those within their jurisdiction. In the road safety context, this includes the duty to establish and enforce adequate criminal law provisions to deter dangerous driving, including DUI. States that fail to adequately investigate or prosecute fatal DUI accidents may face Article 2 liability.

**Article 6 -- Right to a Fair Trial:** DUI proceedings must comply with fair trial guarantees, including the right to be informed of charges, the right to legal representation, the presumption of innocence, and the right to examine witnesses. The ECtHR has addressed whether administrative proceedings for driving disqualification constitute "criminal" charges within the autonomous meaning of Article 6. In **Nilsson v. Sweden (Application No. 73661/01, 2005)**, the Court examined whether a lengthy driving licence revocation following a drunk driving conviction constituted a second "criminal" penalty in violation of the ne bis in idem principle. The Court applied the Engel criteria to determine whether the licence revocation constituted a criminal sanction.

**Article 8 -- Right to Private Life:** Mandatory blood testing for BAC can engage Article 8 protections regarding physical integrity and private life. The Court has generally held that such interferences are justified under Article 8(2) as necessary in a democratic society for the prevention of crime and the protection of public safety, provided they are conducted in accordance with law and are proportionate.

**Article 1 of Protocol 4 -- Prohibition of Imprisonment for Debt:** This provision is potentially relevant where states impose custodial sentences for failure to pay DUI-related fines, though the Court has generally distinguished between punitive fines and civil debt.

### EU Legislative Framework

The European Union addresses drunk driving through a combination of recommendations, directives, and vehicle safety regulations, while recognizing that criminal law penalties remain primarily a matter for member states.

**Commission Recommendation 2001/115/EC:** The Commission recommended that all member states adopt a maximum BAC limit of 0.05% (0.5 g/L) for the general driver population, with a limit of 0.00% or near-zero for novice drivers, professional drivers, and riders of two-wheeled vehicles.

**Current BAC Limits Across EU Member States:**

| BAC Limit | Countries |
|-----------|-----------|
| 0.00% | Czech Republic, Hungary, Romania, Slovakia |
| 0.02% | Estonia, Poland, Sweden |
| 0.03% | -- |
| 0.05% | Austria, Belgium, Bulgaria, Croatia, Denmark, Finland, France, Germany, Greece, Ireland, Italy, Latvia, Lithuania, Netherlands, Portugal, Slovenia, Spain |
| 0.08% | England & Wales (no longer EU), Malta |

### EU Road Safety Package (March 2023)

In March 2023, the European Commission adopted a comprehensive road safety package containing three interdependent legislative proposals:

**1. Revision of the Driving Licence Directive:** Proposed an EU-wide probationary period for novice drivers with stricter rules on drink driving. The directive aims to harmonize aspects of licence acquisition and maintenance across member states.

**2. Directive on EU-Wide Driving Disqualifications:** Under current rules, a driving disqualification imposed by one member state for a serious offense (including DUI) committed on its territory cannot be enforced EU-wide if the driver holds a licence from another member state. The new directive establishes a system allowing for mutual recognition of driving disqualifications across all member states. A provisional political agreement was reached in trilogue in May 2025, the European Parliament formally approved the rules on October 21, 2025, and the directives entered into force on November 25, 2025. Member states have three years to transpose the new rules, plus one additional year for implementation preparation.

**3. Revision of the Cross-Border Enforcement Directive:** Strengthens cooperation between national authorities to enforce traffic offenses committed by non-resident drivers, including DUI, ensuring that drivers cannot evade penalties simply by crossing borders.

### Vehicle Safety Regulations

**Regulation (EU) 2019/2144 (General Safety Regulation):** Since July 2022, all new vehicle types sold in the EU must be technically prepared for the installation of alcohol ignition interlocks ("alcolock-ready"), with standardized electrical and technical interfaces for easy retrofitting. This does not mandate the installation of interlocks but ensures that vehicles can accommodate them when required by national law or court order.

**Advanced Driver Distraction Warning (ADDW):** As part of the broader vehicle safety framework, the EU requires new vehicles to be equipped with driver monitoring systems that can detect impaired driving behavior, though these systems are not specifically calibrated for alcohol detection.

### European Transport Safety Council (ETSC) Recommendations

The ETSC, an independent advisory body, has consistently advocated for:
- A harmonized EU-wide BAC limit of 0.02% for all drivers
- Mandatory alcohol interlock programs for repeat DUI offenders
- Standardized rehabilitation programs across member states
- Zero-tolerance policies for all professional and novice drivers

## Comparative Analysis

### BAC Thresholds and Legal Philosophy

The divergence in BAC thresholds reflects fundamentally different approaches to the balance between individual liberty and public safety. South Korea's 0.03% limit embodies a near-zero-tolerance philosophy rooted in the cultural and political response to high-profile DUI fatalities, particularly the Yoon Chang-ho case. The United States' 0.08% standard reflects a more permissive approach, balancing public safety against individual freedom and the political power of the alcohol industry. Germany's tiered system represents a middle path, recognizing that impairment exists on a spectrum and calibrating legal consequences accordingly. The EU's recommended 0.05% standard attempts to harmonize approaches across diverse legal traditions while respecting member state sovereignty in criminal matters.

### Penalty Structures

South Korea stands out for the severity of its penalties, with even first-offense DUI carrying a potential 1-5 year prison sentence -- a range that would typically apply only to repeat or aggravated offenders in Western jurisdictions. The United States relies heavily on administrative sanctions (licence suspension, ignition interlocks) alongside criminal penalties, with significant variation producing disparate outcomes depending on geography. Germany's emphasis on the MPU requirement creates a uniquely burdensome path to licence reinstatement that goes beyond mere punishment to assess genuine fitness to drive. The EU framework focuses on cross-border enforcement mechanisms rather than harmonizing penalties, recognizing the political impracticability of standardizing criminal sanctions across 27 member states.

### Constitutional and Human Rights Dimensions

Each jurisdiction grapples with the tension between effective DUI enforcement and individual rights. In the United States, the Fourth Amendment has produced the most extensive jurisprudence, with the Supreme Court drawing careful distinctions between breath and blood testing, checkpoint stops, and exigent circumstances. Germany's Basic Law (Grundgesetz) protections are mediated through the proportionality principle that the Federal Constitutional Court applies to all criminal sanctions. South Korea's Constitutional Court has upheld the severity of DUI penalties against proportionality challenges. The ECHR provides a supranational rights framework that operates as a floor rather than a ceiling, with the ne bis in idem principle and fair trial guarantees most frequently implicated in DUI cases.

### Enforcement Innovations

Technological convergence is beginning to reduce jurisdictional divergence. The EU's alcolock-ready mandate, the U.S. DADSS program for passive alcohol detection, and South Korea's 2026 saliva-swab provisions for drug testing all point toward a future in which enforcement is increasingly automated and detection occurs before or at the point of vehicle operation rather than during traffic stops. These developments raise new legal questions about privacy, consent, and the scope of state power that will require resolution across all four legal frameworks.
