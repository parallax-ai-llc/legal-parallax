## Summary

Naturalization -- the legal process by which a non-citizen acquires citizenship of a state through application and fulfillment of prescribed requirements -- is one of the most consequential acts in immigration law, conferring the full panoply of political and civil rights upon the naturalized individual while simultaneously reflecting a state's fundamental conception of membership and belonging. South Korea's naturalization framework, governed by the Nationality Act (국적법) of 1997 and implemented by the Ministry of Justice (법무부), requires a minimum of five years of continuous domicile in Korea, with reduced requirements for spouses of Korean nationals (2 years) and persons of Korean descent (귀화 요건), alongside Korean language proficiency, knowledge of Korean customs and culture, and sufficient financial means, with naturalization rates remaining among the lowest in the OECD due to historically restrictive attitudes and the requirement, relaxed only in 2010 for certain categories, to renounce prior nationality. The United States naturalization process, codified in the Immigration and Nationality Act (INA, 8 U.S.C. §§ 1421-1459), requires five years of lawful permanent residence (three years for spouses of U.S. citizens), continuous physical presence, good moral character, English language proficiency, knowledge of U.S. civics and history, and an oath of allegiance, with the process administered by U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services (USCIS) and historically naturalizing approximately 800,000-900,000 persons annually. Germany's naturalization requirements under the Staatsangehörigkeitsgesetz (StAG) were significantly reformed in June 2024, reducing the standard residence requirement from eight to five years (three years with exceptional integration), introducing acceptance of dual nationality for all applicants, and requiring a commitment to the free democratic basic order (freiheitliche demokratische Grundordnung), while the EU framework, particularly the ECHR's jurisprudence under Article 8 and the CJEU's proportionality review of nationality decisions affecting EU citizenship, increasingly constrains member state discretion in naturalization and denaturalization decisions.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea distinguishes between three types of naturalization under the Nationality Act (국적법):

**General Naturalization (일반귀화, Article 5):**

| Requirement | Detail |
|-------------|--------|
| Domicile | 5+ years continuous domicile in Korea |
| Age | Adult under Korean law (19+) |
| Character | Good conduct; no serious criminal record |
| Financial capacity | Sufficient assets or skills to support self |
| Language and culture | Pass the Korea Immigration and Naturalization Aptitude Test (KINAT, 사회통합프로그램) |
| Nationality renunciation | Must renounce prior nationality (subject to exceptions) |

**Simplified Naturalization (간이귀화, Article 6):**

| Category | Residence Requirement |
|----------|---------------------|
| Spouse of Korean national | 2 years of marriage + 1 year domicile; OR 3 years of marriage if divorced/widowed |
| Person with Korean parent (by birth) | 3 years continuous domicile |
| Person born in Korea to former Korean national parent | 3 years continuous domicile |
| Person adopted by Korean national | 3 years continuous domicile |

**Special Naturalization (특별귀화, Article 7):** Available to persons who have rendered exceptional service to Korea or who possess outstanding abilities contributing to the national interest (e.g., elite athletes, scholars). No residence requirement applies.

### Korea Immigration and Naturalization Aptitude Test (KINAT)

The Social Integration Program (사회통합프로그램, KIIP) provides a standardized pathway to meeting the language and culture requirements:
- 5 levels of Korean language instruction (0-4)
- Level 5: Understanding Korean Society (한국사회이해)
- Final assessment includes Korean language proficiency and knowledge of Korean history, culture, and political system
- Completion of KIIP exempts applicants from separate naturalization testing

### Naturalization Statistics

South Korea's naturalization numbers remain modest relative to its population:

| Year | Naturalizations | Primary Source Countries |
|------|----------------|------------------------|
| 2019 | 10,237 | China (ethnic Korean), Vietnam, Philippines |
| 2020 | 8,892 | China (ethnic Korean), Vietnam, Philippines |
| 2022 | 12,431 | China (ethnic Korean), Vietnam, Philippines, Thailand |
| 2024 | ~14,500 | China (ethnic Korean), Vietnam, Uzbekistan |

The majority of naturalizations are through simplified naturalization (marriage to Korean nationals), with marriage migrants (결혼이주민) constituting approximately 70 percent of naturalizing persons.

### Constitutional Court Jurisprudence

**Constitutional Court Decision 2014Hun-Ma1440 (2015):** The Court upheld the requirement to renounce prior nationality as constitutional, finding it did not violate the right to pursue happiness or equality, though noting the legislature's discretion to modify the requirement.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States naturalizes more persons annually than any other country, with a well-established statutory and administrative framework.

**INA Section 316 (8 U.S.C. § 1427) -- General Requirements:**

| Requirement | Detail |
|-------------|--------|
| LPR status | 5 years (3 years if married to and living with USC) |
| Continuous residence | 5 years (3 years for spouse of USC) |
| Physical presence | 30 months of physical presence in U.S. during 5-year period |
| State residence | 3 months in state where application filed |
| Age | 18+ |
| Good moral character | During statutory period |
| English | Read, write, speak ordinary English (exceptions for age/disability) |
| Civics | Knowledge of U.S. history and government |
| Oath | Oath of allegiance to the United States |

**English and Civics Exemptions:**

| Exemption | Eligibility |
|-----------|-------------|
| 50/20 rule | Age 50+ with 20+ years as LPR: civics test in native language; English waived |
| 55/15 rule | Age 55+ with 15+ years as LPR: civics test in native language; English waived |
| 65/20 rule | Age 65+ with 20+ years as LPR: simplified civics test (20 questions) in native language |
| Disability | Physical or mental disability: form N-648 medical certification |

**Military Service Naturalization (INA Section 328-329, 8 U.S.C. §§ 1439-1440):** Active-duty military members may naturalize after one year of service; wartime service permits immediate naturalization without prior LPR status.

### Oath of Allegiance

The naturalization oath (8 U.S.C. § 1448) includes language renouncing "allegiance and fidelity to any foreign prince, potentate, state, or sovereignty," but the United States does not enforce this as a legal obligation to surrender prior nationality. The State Department recognizes that many countries do not permit renunciation, and U.S. law does not penalize retention of prior nationality.

### Good Moral Character

The good moral character requirement (INA § 101(f), 8 U.S.C. § 1101(f)) is evaluated during the statutory period and includes bars for:
- Murder (permanent bar)
- Aggravated felony (permanent bar, post-November 29, 1990)
- Drug trafficking
- Prostitution
- Illegal gambling
- Habitual drunkard
- Failure to pay court-ordered child support
- Unlawful voting

### Processing and Backlogs

USCIS processing times for Form N-400 (Application for Naturalization) vary significantly by field office, ranging from 5 to 20 months. As of 2025, approximately 3 million pending naturalization cases existed. The Biden administration expanded naturalization access through additional testing sites and fee waiver programs, while the Trump administration (2025) proposed increased fees and additional screening requirements.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's naturalization law underwent its most significant reform in decades with the Staatsangehörigkeitsgesetz amendments effective June 27, 2024.

**StAG § 10 -- Entitlement Naturalization (Anspruchseinbürgerung):**

| Requirement | Pre-2024 | Post-2024 |
|-------------|----------|-----------|
| Lawful residence | 8 years | 5 years |
| Accelerated track | 7 years (integration course); 6 years (special integration) | 3 years (exceptional integration: C1 German, civic engagement, professional achievement) |
| Permanent residence permit | Required | Required |
| Subsistence | Self-sufficient (no social assistance) | Self-sufficient (expanded hardship exceptions for gastarbeiter generation) |
| Language | B1 German | B1 German (C1 for 3-year track) |
| Civics | Einbürgerungstest | Einbürgerungstest (unchanged) |
| Criminal record | No significant convictions | No significant convictions; antisemitic or racist acts bar |
| Dual nationality | Must renounce prior nationality (exceptions for EU/Swiss nationals and hardship cases) | Permitted for all |
| Commitment to FDGO | Required | Required; explicit commitment to special historical responsibility toward Nazi persecution |

**Einbürgerungstest (Naturalization Test):** Administered by the Bundesamt für Migration und Flüchtlinge (BAMF), the test comprises 33 questions (30 general + 3 state-specific) drawn from a catalogue of 310 questions. Applicants must answer at least 17 correctly. Topics include German democratic institutions, rule of law, equality, freedom of religion, and historical events.

### 2024 Reform: Key Changes

The 2024 reform represented a paradigm shift in German nationality law:

**Dual Nationality:** The most significant change was the general acceptance of dual nationality (Hinnahme von Mehrstaatigkeit) for all naturalizing persons. Previously, applicants were required to renounce their prior nationality before naturalization (with exceptions for EU nationals and hardship cases). This change was driven by recognition that the renunciation requirement deterred naturalization among long-term residents, particularly Turkish-German communities.

**Historical Responsibility Clause:** The 2024 reform added a requirement for applicants to commit to "the special historical responsibility of Germany for the National Socialist injustice and its consequences, in particular the protection of Jewish life." This provision was prompted by rising antisemitism and the desire to embed historical consciousness in the naturalization process.

**Gastarbeiter Exception:** Recognizing that elderly members of the guest worker generation (Gastarbeiter) who arrived in Germany in the 1960s-1970s may struggle with language testing, the reform provides simplified naturalization for long-term residents who can demonstrate oral German proficiency without a formal B1 certificate.

### Statistics

Germany has seen a significant increase in naturalization following the 2024 reform:

| Year | Naturalizations | Change |
|------|----------------|--------|
| 2021 | 131,600 | +20% |
| 2022 | 168,500 | +28% |
| 2023 | 200,100 | +19% |
| 2024 (estimate) | ~250,000 | +25% (post-reform surge) |

The largest source countries for naturalization include Syria, Turkey, Iraq, Romania, and Afghanistan.

## ECHR / EU

### European Convention on Nationality (ECN)

**Article 6(3) -- Naturalization:** States parties shall provide for naturalization of persons lawfully and habitually resident on their territory, with a residence period not exceeding 10 years.

**Article 6(4):** States shall facilitate naturalization for: spouses of nationals, children of nationals, persons born in the territory, long-term residents, stateless persons, and refugees.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Genovese v. Malta (2011):** Nationality falls within the ambit of Article 8 (private life); discriminatory nationality rules violate Article 14 read with Article 8.

**Ramadan v. Malta (2016):** Withdrawal of nationality rendering a person stateless requires particularly compelling justification under Article 8.

**K2 v. United Kingdom (Application No. 42387/13, Decision of March 7, 2017):** The Court held that the deprivation of British citizenship on national security grounds, while the applicant was abroad, fell within the margin of appreciation where the applicant retained another nationality. The decision confirms that states have broad discretion in nationality matters but must respect Convention rights.

**Usmanov v. Russia (Application No. 43936/18, ECtHR, December 22, 2020):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where Russian authorities annulled naturalization on the basis that the applicant had provided false information, without adequate procedural safeguards or proportionality assessment.

### CJEU and EU Citizenship

The CJEU has developed a body of case law establishing that member state nationality decisions affecting EU citizenship are subject to EU law:

**Rottmann v. Freistaat Bayern (Case C-135/08, 2010):** Withdrawal of naturalization obtained by fraud that results in loss of EU citizenship must be proportionate.

**Tjebbes (Case C-221/17, 2019):** Automatic loss of nationality for long-term residence abroad must allow individual proportionality review.

**JY v. Wiener Landesregierung (Case C-118/20, 2022):** Denial of naturalization after applicant renounced prior nationality must be subject to proportionality review where it results in de facto statelessness and loss of prospect of EU citizenship.

### EU Long-Term Residents

Directive 2003/109/EC (Long-Term Residents Directive), amended by Directive (EU) 2024/1233, grants third-country nationals who have legally resided in a member state for five continuous years a right to long-term resident status. While this does not confer nationality, it provides:
- Right to equal treatment with nationals in most areas
- Right to reside in other EU member states
- Enhanced protection against expulsion
- A pathway that facilitates subsequent naturalization in many member states

## Comparative Analysis

### Residence Requirements

The trend across all jurisdictions is toward shorter residence requirements:
- **South Korea:** 5 years (2 years for spousal naturalization)
- **United States:** 5 years (3 years for spousal naturalization)
- **Germany:** 5 years (3 years accelerated track) -- reduced from 8 years in 2024
- **ECN recommendation:** Maximum 10 years

### Dual Nationality

The global trend strongly favors acceptance of dual nationality:
- **United States:** De facto acceptance; no enforcement of renunciation oath
- **Germany:** Full acceptance since 2024 (historic shift)
- **South Korea:** Restrictive; qualified acceptance since 2010 with ongoing limitations
- As of 2025, over 75 percent of countries worldwide permit some form of dual nationality

### Integration Requirements

All jurisdictions require demonstration of integration through language and civics testing, but the rigor and format vary:
- **United States:** Relatively straightforward English and civics test; generous exemptions for elderly/disabled
- **Germany:** B1 German (B2/C1 for accelerated track); Einbürgerungstest with published question catalogue
- **South Korea:** KINAT test encompassing Korean language, history, and culture; Social Integration Program

### Philosophical Approaches

- **United States:** Civic integration model: naturalization as commitment to constitutional values
- **Germany:** Post-2024: civic-historical model: integration plus commitment to democratic order and historical responsibility
- **South Korea:** Cultural assimilation model: emphasis on Korean language, customs, and cultural knowledge

### Naturalization Rates and Backlogs

Processing efficiency varies substantially across jurisdictions:

| Jurisdiction | Annual Naturalizations (2024 est.) | Average Processing Time | Backlog |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|------------------------|---------|
| South Korea | ~15,000 | 12-24 months | Moderate |
| United States | ~870,000 | 8-14 months (N-400) | ~1 million pending |
| Germany | ~200,000+ (post-2024 reform) | 6-18 months | Significant (varies by state) |

The United States naturalization backlog has been a persistent challenge, with USCIS processing times fluctuating significantly based on staffing, funding (the agency is fee-funded), and policy priorities. The German 2024 reform is expected to increase naturalization applications substantially, potentially creating processing bottlenecks in already overstretched local Einbürgerungsbehörden.

### Military Service and Naturalization

Military service provides alternative naturalization pathways in some jurisdictions:

- **United States:** INA § 328-329 permits naturalization through military service with reduced residence and physical presence requirements; expedited naturalization available during periods of armed conflict
- **South Korea:** Military service is mandatory for male citizens but does not create a pathway to naturalization for non-citizens; conscription applies only after citizenship is acquired
- **Germany:** Bundeswehr service does not create a special naturalization pathway, though integration through military service may support demonstrating Eingliederung in deutsche Lebensverhältnisse
