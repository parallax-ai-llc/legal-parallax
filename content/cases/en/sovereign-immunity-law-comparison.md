## Summary

Sovereign immunity -- the legal doctrine that the state and its instrumentalities cannot be sued without their consent, derived from the historical principle that "the king can do no wrong" -- has undergone fundamental transformation across legal systems over the past century, evolving from an absolute bar on litigation against the state to a system of qualified immunity that balances the state's need for efficient governance and protection from unmeritorious claims against the individual's right to an effective remedy for wrongs committed by state actors. South Korea's state liability framework is primarily governed by Article 29 of the Constitution (대한민국 헌법), which provides that citizens may claim just compensation for damages suffered through unlawful acts of public officials performed in the course of official duties, implemented through the State Compensation Act (국가배상법, originally enacted in 1951 and substantially revised in 1971 and 2008) -- with a distinctive feature being the dual-track system that channels most state liability claims through the State Compensation Council (국가배상심의회) before court proceedings, while the Constitutional Court has addressed the constitutionality of various immunity provisions, including the military immunity clause in Decision 96HeonBa7 (1998). The United States has a uniquely complex sovereign immunity landscape, with the Eleventh Amendment providing states with constitutional immunity from suit in federal court, the federal government's sovereign immunity waived in defined circumstances by the Federal Tort Claims Act (FTCA, 28 U.S.C. Sections 1346(b), 2671-2680) and the Tucker Act (28 U.S.C. Section 1491), and individual government officials protected by qualified immunity under the doctrine established in Harlow v. Fitzgerald, 457 U.S. 800 (1982) -- a doctrine that has generated intense controversy for effectively shielding police officers and other officials from civil liability for constitutional violations. Germany has abolished sovereign immunity for liability purposes through Article 34 of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz), which provides that when a public official violates their official duty toward a third party, liability falls on the state, implemented through Section 839 of the Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB -- Civil Code) in conjunction with Article 34 GG, creating a comprehensive state liability system in which individuals may sue the state directly for damages caused by official wrongdoing. The European framework addresses state liability through the ECHR's right to an effective remedy under Article 13, the CJEU's Francovich doctrine establishing member state liability for failure to implement EU directives (Francovich v. Italy, 1991), and the European Convention on State Immunity (1972).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's state liability system rests on the constitutional guarantee of Article 29, which establishes both the right to compensation and the framework for official liability.

### Constitutional Provisions

**Article 29(1):** "In case a person has sustained damages by an unlawful act committed by a public official in the course of official duties, he may claim just compensation from the State or public organization under the conditions as prescribed by Act. In this case, the public official concerned shall not be immune from liabilities."

**Article 29(2):** "In case a person on active military service or an employee of the military forces, a police official or others as designated by Act sustains damages in connection with the performance of official duties such as combat action, drill and so forth, he shall not be entitled to a claim against the State or public organization on the grounds of unlawful acts committed by public officials in the course of official duties, but shall only be entitled to compensation as prescribed by Act."

### State Compensation Act (국가배상법)

The Act provides the procedural and substantive framework for state liability claims:

**Elements of Liability:**
1. Act by a public official (공무원의 행위)
2. In the course of official duties (직무를 집행하면서)
3. Unlawfulness (위법성) -- violation of law or duty
4. Fault (고의 또는 과실) -- intent or negligence
5. Damage (손해) -- actual harm suffered
6. Causation -- causal link between act and damage

### State Compensation Council (국가배상심의회)

The Council provides a pre-litigation administrative review mechanism:

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Composition | Judges, prosecutors, lawyers, and government officials |
| Jurisdiction | Claims against the state for damages by public officials |
| Process | Review of claim; investigation; recommended settlement |
| Timeline | Decision within 3 months of claim filing |
| Binding effect | Non-binding; claimant may proceed to court |
| Mandatory | Optional since 2008 amendment (previously mandatory) |

### Constitutional Court Jurisprudence

**Decision 96HeonBa7 (1998):** The Constitutional Court upheld the constitutionality of Article 29(2) restricting military personnel's right to sue the state for service-related injuries, finding that the provision pursues legitimate objectives of military discipline and operational efficiency, though noting that the restriction must be narrowly construed.

**Decision 2008HeonBa141 (2014):** The Court examined the constitutionality of the dual liability bar for military personnel, holding that the bar applies only to injuries directly connected to military service activities and does not extend to ordinary negligence unrelated to military operations.

### Reform Developments

The 2008 amendment to the State Compensation Act made the Council review process optional rather than mandatory, enabling claimants to file directly in court. The 2019 amendment expanded the scope of compensable damages to include psychological harm and improved the efficiency of the administrative review process.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has the most complex sovereign immunity landscape of any jurisdiction examined, with multiple overlapping doctrines at the federal and state levels.

### Federal Government -- Federal Tort Claims Act (FTCA)

The FTCA (28 U.S.C. Sections 1346(b), 2671-2680) waives sovereign immunity for tort claims against the federal government, subject to significant exceptions:

**Exceptions to FTCA Waiver:**

| Exception | Effect |
|-----------|--------|
| Discretionary function (Section 2680(a)) | No liability for acts involving discretionary policy judgments |
| Intentional torts (Section 2680(h)) | No liability for assault, battery, false imprisonment (with law enforcement exception) |
| Foreign country exception (Section 2680(k)) | No liability for claims arising in foreign countries |
| Combatant activities (Section 2680(j)) | No liability for combatant activities during wartime |
| Postal matters (Section 2680(b)) | No liability for loss of postal matter |

**Feres Doctrine:** In Feres v. United States, 340 U.S. 135 (1950), the Supreme Court held that the FTCA does not authorize claims by military personnel for injuries "incident to service." Despite sustained criticism, the doctrine was reaffirmed in United States v. Johnson, 481 U.S. 681 (1987). The 2022 National Defense Authorization Act created an administrative claims process for medical malpractice claims by service members, partially circumventing Feres.

### State Sovereign Immunity -- Eleventh Amendment

The Eleventh Amendment provides: "The Judicial power of the United States shall not be construed to extend to any suit in law or equity, commenced or prosecuted against one of the United States by Citizens of another State, or by Citizens or Subjects of any Foreign State."

The Supreme Court has interpreted this provision broadly in cases such as Seminole Tribe v. Florida, 517 U.S. 44 (1996) (Congress cannot abrogate state immunity under Article I powers) and Alden v. Maine, 527 U.S. 706 (1999) (state sovereign immunity extends to suits in state court).

### Qualified Immunity

**Harlow v. Fitzgerald, 457 U.S. 800 (1982):** The Court established that government officials performing discretionary functions are shielded from civil liability insofar as their conduct does not violate "clearly established" statutory or constitutional rights of which a reasonable person would have known.

The qualified immunity doctrine has generated intense controversy:
- Critics argue it effectively immunizes police officers from accountability for constitutional violations
- The "clearly established" requirement demands that prior cases have addressed the specific factual scenario, creating a "catch-22" in which new types of violations can never be clearly established because prior victims could not obtain rulings on the merits
- Multiple Supreme Court justices (Thomas, Sotomayor) have expressed skepticism about the doctrine's foundations
- Legislative reform proposals (George Floyd Justice in Policing Act) have sought to eliminate or limit qualified immunity

### Section 1983 -- Civil Rights Claims

42 U.S.C. Section 1983 provides a cause of action against state and local officials who violate constitutional rights "under color of" state law. Section 1983 does not apply to the federal government (Bivens v. Six Unknown Named Agents, 1971, provides a limited federal analog) and does not abrogate Eleventh Amendment immunity of states (though it applies to local governments and officials acting in their personal capacity).

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany has effectively abolished sovereign immunity for liability purposes, creating one of the most comprehensive state liability systems in the world through the combination of Article 34 GG and Section 839 BGB.

### Article 34 GG -- State Liability Substitution

Article 34 provides: "Where any person, in the exercise of a public office entrusted to him, violates his official duty to a third party, liability shall rest principally with the state or the public body that employs him. In the event of intentional wrongdoing or gross negligence, the right of recourse against the individual official shall be reserved."

This provision establishes two critical principles:
1. **Liability substitution:** The state assumes liability for the wrongful acts of its officials, and the individual official is not personally liable to the injured party
2. **Right of recourse:** The state may seek reimbursement from the official in cases of intentional or grossly negligent conduct

### Section 839 BGB -- Official Liability

Section 839(1) BGB provides: "If an official intentionally or negligently breaches the official duty incumbent upon him in relation to a third party, he must compensate the third party for any damage arising from this." Read in conjunction with Article 34 GG, this provision creates the substantive basis for state liability claims.

### Elements of State Liability

| Element | Requirement |
|---------|-------------|
| Official (Beamter) | Broadly defined to include all persons exercising public authority |
| Official duty (Amtspflicht) | Duty owed to a third party (not merely to the state) |
| Breach | Violation of the duty through act or omission |
| Fault | Intent (Vorsatz) or negligence (Fahrlässigkeit) |
| Third-party relatedness | The breached duty must be owed to the specific claimant |
| Damage | Actual pecuniary or non-pecuniary harm |
| Causation | Causal link between breach and damage |

### No Discretionary Function Exception

Critically, German law does not have an equivalent to the U.S. FTCA's discretionary function exception. The state is liable for wrongful exercise of discretion, including legislative and judicial acts in certain circumstances. However, Section 839(2) BGB provides that officials whose wrongful act consists in a judicial decision are liable only if the breach constitutes a criminal offense.

### Staatshaftungsrecht Reform

The Federal Constitutional Court in BVerfGE 61, 149 (1982) struck down an attempt to enact comprehensive state liability legislation (Staatshaftungsgesetz), finding that the federal government lacked legislative competence under the existing constitutional framework. This decision left state liability governed by the somewhat awkward combination of Article 34 GG and Section 839 BGB, with reform proposals periodically advanced but not yet enacted.

### European State Liability (Francovich)

German courts apply the CJEU's Francovich doctrine, holding the state liable for damages caused by failure to properly implement EU directives or other violations of EU law. This creates an additional layer of state liability beyond the domestic Article 34/Section 839 framework.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR -- Right to an Effective Remedy (Article 13)

Article 13 provides: "Everyone whose rights and freedoms as set forth in this Convention are violated shall have an effective remedy before a national authority." This provision constrains sovereign immunity by requiring that states provide accessible and effective mechanisms for individuals to obtain redress for Convention violations.

**Kudła v. Poland [GC], Application No. 30210/96, Judgment of October 26, 2000:** The Grand Chamber held that Article 13 requires a remedy that is effective both in practice and in law, enabling the competent authority to deal with the substance of the complaint and to grant appropriate relief.

**Al-Adsani v. United Kingdom [GC], Application No. 35763/97, Judgment of November 21, 2001:** The Grand Chamber held that the grant of state immunity from civil proceedings for alleged torture did not violate Article 6(1) (right of access to court), finding that sovereign immunity serves legitimate aims in international law and that the restriction was proportionate. This controversial decision has been criticized for effectively prioritizing state immunity over the prohibition of torture.

### CJEU -- Francovich Doctrine

**Francovich and Bonifaci v. Italy, Joined Cases C-6/90 and C-9/90, Judgment of November 19, 1991:** The CJEU established that member states are liable to compensate individuals for damages caused by failure to implement EU directives, creating a supranational state liability framework. The conditions for liability are: (1) the directive must confer rights on individuals, (2) the content of those rights must be identifiable from the directive, and (3) there must be a causal link between the failure to implement and the damage suffered.

**Brasserie du Pêcheur and Factortame, Joined Cases C-46/93 and C-48/93, Judgment of March 5, 1996:** The CJEU extended the Francovich doctrine to all breaches of EU law by member states, establishing a three-part test: (1) the rule infringed must be intended to confer rights on individuals, (2) the breach must be sufficiently serious, and (3) there must be a direct causal link between the breach and the damage.

### European Convention on State Immunity (1972)

The European Convention on State Immunity (ETS No. 074), opened for signature on May 16, 1972, codifies the restrictive theory of sovereign immunity in international law, providing that a state is not immune in proceedings relating to commercial activities, employment, personal injury occurring on the territory of the forum state, and immovable property. The Convention has been ratified by only eight Council of Europe states, reflecting the trend toward domestic statutory waivers rather than treaty-based solutions.

## Comparative Analysis

### Degree of Waiver

Germany has most comprehensively abolished sovereign immunity, with no discretionary function exception and state liability for virtually all wrongful official acts. South Korea provides broad waiver with limited military exceptions. The United States retains the most extensive immunity, with multiple exceptions to the FTCA, Eleventh Amendment state immunity, and the qualified immunity doctrine for individual officials.

### Employee vs. State Liability

Germany and South Korea channel liability to the state, protecting individual officials from personal liability (though the state may seek recourse). The United States uniquely exposes individual officials to personal liability through Section 1983 and Bivens claims, while simultaneously protecting them through qualified immunity -- creating a system that theoretically permits individual accountability but practically shields most officials.

### Qualified Immunity

The U.S. qualified immunity doctrine has no equivalent in the other jurisdictions. German law provides official immunity only as liability substitution (the state pays, not the official), not as a defense against liability altogether. South Korea similarly channels liability to the state. The ECHR requires effective remedies without recognizing qualified immunity as a defense.

### Access to Courts

Germany provides the broadest access to courts for state liability claims, with no mandatory pre-suit administrative process and comprehensive judicial review. South Korea's system provides administrative review (now optional) followed by court access. The U.S. FTCA requires mandatory administrative claims before suit, and the PLRA imposes additional barriers for prisoner claims. The ECHR requires exhaustion of domestic remedies before application to Strasbourg.
