## Summary

Double taxation -- the imposition of comparable taxes by two or more jurisdictions on the same taxpayer in respect of the same income or capital for the same period -- poses one of the most significant obstacles to international trade, investment, and labor mobility, and its avoidance has spawned an extensive network of bilateral tax treaties, multilateral instruments, and domestic unilateral relief mechanisms. South Korea has concluded over 90 bilateral tax treaties (조세조약) for the avoidance of double taxation, modeled primarily on the OECD Model Tax Convention, and provides unilateral relief through foreign tax credits under the International Tax Coordination Act (국제조세조정에 관한 법률) and the Income Tax Act (소득세법); the country ratified the OECD Multilateral Convention to Implement Tax Treaty Related Measures to Prevent Base Erosion and Profit Shifting (MLI) in 2020, modifying its existing treaty network to incorporate anti-abuse provisions. The United States maintains the world's most extensive treaty network with over 60 income tax treaties and provides unilateral relief through the foreign tax credit mechanism (26 U.S.C. Sections 901-909), though its unique citizenship-based taxation system creates double taxation risks not present in residence-based systems, addressed through the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (Section 911) and detailed treaty provisions including saving clauses that preserve U.S. taxing rights over its citizens. Germany has concluded over 95 double taxation agreements (Doppelbesteuerungsabkommen, DBA) and provides unilateral relief through the Außensteuergesetz (AStG) and Section 34c EStG, while the EU has addressed double taxation through the Parent-Subsidiary Directive, the Interest and Royalties Directive, the Arbitration Convention (90/436/EEC), and the EU Dispute Resolution Directive (2017/1852) which provides binding resolution mechanisms for cross-border tax disputes between member states.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's double taxation relief framework rests on three pillars: bilateral tax treaties (조세조약), the International Tax Coordination Act (국제조세조정에 관한 법률, Act No. 19929), and domestic provisions in the Income Tax Act and Corporate Tax Act providing foreign tax credits.

### Treaty Network

South Korea maintains over 90 bilateral tax treaties, covering all major trading partners. Treaties with developing countries frequently include tax sparing credit provisions, reflecting Korea's own historical transition from a capital-importing to a capital-exporting economy. Key treaty provisions typically include:

| Provision | Standard Korean Treaty Position |
|-----------|-------------------------------|
| Dividend withholding | 5% (qualifying participation); 15% (portfolio) |
| Interest withholding | 10-15% |
| Royalty withholding | 10-15% |
| Permanent establishment threshold | Generally follows OECD Model; some treaties include service PE |
| Capital gains on shares | Taxable in residence state; real property-rich company exception |

### Foreign Tax Credit

Under Article 57 of the Income Tax Act (소득세법 제57조) and Article 57 of the Corporate Tax Act (법인세법 제57조), resident taxpayers may claim a credit for foreign income taxes paid, limited to the Korean tax attributable to the foreign-source income (per-country limitation method or overall limitation method at the taxpayer's election). Excess credits may be carried forward for five years.

### MLI Implementation

South Korea deposited its instrument of ratification of the OECD MLI on September 13, 2020, with the convention entering into force on January 1, 2021. South Korea adopted the Principal Purpose Test (PPT) as the minimum standard for treaty abuse prevention and elected to apply the simplified limitation on benefits (LOB) provision. The MLI modifies South Korea's covered tax agreements to incorporate anti-abuse and dispute resolution provisions without the need for bilateral renegotiation.

## United States

### Legal Framework

U.S. double taxation relief derives from bilateral tax treaties (negotiated under the U.S. Model Tax Convention, most recently updated in 2016), the foreign tax credit under Sections 901-909 of the Internal Revenue Code, and the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion under Section 911.

### Citizenship-Based Taxation and Double Taxation

The United States' unique citizenship-based taxation system creates inherent double taxation risks. All U.S. citizens and permanent residents are taxed on worldwide income regardless of residence. Treaty provisions address this through the "saving clause," which preserves the U.S. right to tax its citizens and residents as if the treaty had not come into force, with specific exceptions carved out for certain benefits (typically foreign tax credits, non-discrimination, and specific exemptions).

### Foreign Tax Credit (Sections 901-909)

The foreign tax credit is the primary relief mechanism:

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Eligible taxes | Income, war profits, and excess profits taxes paid or accrued to foreign governments |
| Limitation | Cannot exceed U.S. tax on foreign-source income (Section 904 limitation) |
| Baskets | Separate limitation categories: general, passive, GILTI, foreign branch |
| Carryback/forward | 1-year carryback, 10-year carryforward |
| Election | Credit or deduction (Section 164); credit is generally more beneficial |

### U.S. Model Tax Convention

The U.S. Model diverges from the OECD Model in several respects:

- **Saving clause:** Preserves U.S. taxing rights over citizens and residents
- **Limitation on Benefits (LOB):** Detailed anti-treaty-shopping provision (not present in OECD Model)
- **Zero withholding:** Seeks elimination of source-country withholding on dividends, interest, and royalties in many cases
- **Arbitration:** Includes mandatory binding arbitration in the MAP process

### Non-Participation in MLI

The United States has not signed the MLI, preferring to implement BEPS measures through bilateral treaty renegotiation. This reflects concerns about the MLI's compatibility with the U.S. treaty network's unique features, including the saving clause and detailed LOB provisions.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's double taxation relief system is based on bilateral Doppelbesteuerungsabkommen (DBA), the Außensteuergesetz (AStG) for international tax coordination, and Section 34c EStG providing unilateral foreign tax credits.

### Treaty Network and Methods

Germany maintains over 95 bilateral tax treaties, one of the most extensive networks globally. Germany's treaties predominantly employ the exemption method (Freistellungsmethode) for active business income and employment income, while applying the credit method (Anrechnungsmethode) for passive income (dividends, interest, royalties).

| Income Type | Typical German Treaty Method |
|-------------|------------------------------|
| Business profits (PE) | Exemption with progression |
| Employment income | Exemption with progression |
| Dividends | Credit method; reduced withholding (typically 5-15%) |
| Interest | Credit method; often zero withholding |
| Royalties | Credit method; often zero withholding |
| Real property income | Exemption (taxable in situs state) |

The exemption with progression (Freistellung mit Progressionsvorbehalt) means that exempt foreign income is excluded from the German tax base but is considered in determining the applicable tax rate for remaining German-source income.

### Unilateral Relief (Section 34c EStG)

Where no treaty exists or the treaty does not fully eliminate double taxation, Section 34c EStG provides a unilateral foreign tax credit limited to the German tax attributable to the foreign income. Alternatively, the taxpayer may elect to deduct the foreign tax as a business expense.

### Switch-Over and Subject-to-Tax Clauses

Under Section 50d(9) EStG and corresponding treaty provisions, Germany applies switch-over clauses (Umschaltklauseln) that change from the exemption method to the credit method where the other state does not tax the income or taxes it at a reduced rate. This prevents "double non-taxation" (Keinmalbesteuerung) where income escapes taxation in both states.

### MLI Implementation

Germany deposited its instrument of ratification of the MLI on March 17, 2020, with entry into force on July 1, 2020. Germany adopted the PPT as the minimum standard and elected to apply arbitration provisions for covered tax agreements.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Mechanisms for Eliminating Double Taxation

The EU has developed multiple instruments addressing double taxation within the single market:

**Parent-Subsidiary Directive (2011/96/EU):** Eliminates withholding taxes on dividend distributions between parent and subsidiary companies in different member states (minimum 10 percent holding) and requires the parent state to either exempt or credit the subsidiary's underlying tax.

**Interest and Royalties Directive (2003/49/EC):** Eliminates withholding taxes on interest and royalty payments between associated companies in different member states (25 percent minimum holding).

**Arbitration Convention (90/436/EEC):** Provides a binding dispute resolution mechanism for transfer pricing disputes between member states. If mutual agreement is not reached within two years, an advisory commission must be established to deliver a binding opinion.

**EU Dispute Resolution Directive (2017/1852):** Adopted on October 10, 2017, the Directive establishes a comprehensive binding dispute resolution mechanism for tax disputes between member states, extending beyond transfer pricing to all double taxation situations arising under bilateral tax treaties or the Arbitration Convention. Member states had to transpose the Directive by June 30, 2019.

### CJEU Case Law

The CJEU has addressed double taxation in numerous cases, though it has held that the EU fundamental freedoms do not themselves require the elimination of double taxation where both member states exercise legitimate taxing jurisdiction:

**Kerckhaert-Morres (C-513/04, 2006):** The Court held that the free movement of capital does not require Belgium to eliminate juridical double taxation of dividends received from a French company, where the double taxation resulted from the parallel exercise of legitimate taxing competences by both states.

**Block (C-67/08, 2009):** Confirmed that EU law does not require member states to eliminate double taxation of inheritances, even within the single market.

**Damseaux (C-128/08, 2009):** Reiterated that the residence state is not required under EU law to credit or exempt foreign-source income merely because it is also taxed at source, absent a treaty obligation.

### ECHR and Double Taxation

The ECHR has rarely addressed double taxation directly, but the property rights protection under Protocol 1, Article 1, theoretically constrains the cumulative tax burden:

**Imbert de Trémiolles v. France (Application Nos. 25834/05 and 27815/05, 2008):** The Court noted that the cumulative effect of multiple taxes could constitute a disproportionate interference with property rights, though it found no violation on the facts.

## Comparative Analysis

### Relief Methods: Credit vs. Exemption

The fundamental policy choice in double taxation relief is between the credit method (taxing worldwide income but allowing a credit for foreign taxes) and the exemption method (excluding foreign income from the domestic tax base). The United States and South Korea primarily use the credit method, ensuring that income is taxed at least at the domestic rate. Germany primarily uses the exemption method for active income, which is simpler to administer and more favorable to taxpayers when domestic rates exceed foreign rates but may result in lower overall taxation if foreign rates are lower.

### Treaty Shopping Prevention

The approaches to preventing treaty abuse differ significantly. The United States relies on detailed Limitation on Benefits (LOB) provisions in each treaty, reflecting a specific, rules-based approach. The OECD MLI (adopted by South Korea and Germany but not the United States) introduces the Principal Purpose Test (PPT), a broader, purpose-based anti-abuse rule. The EU's Anti-Tax Avoidance Directive adds a general anti-abuse rule (GAAR) applicable regardless of treaty provisions.

### Dispute Resolution

The EU Dispute Resolution Directive provides the most robust mechanism, with binding arbitration as a backstop when mutual agreement fails. The OECD MLI's arbitration provision offers a similar mechanism for MLI signatories who opt in. U.S. bilateral treaties increasingly include mandatory arbitration, but the U.S.'s non-participation in the MLI means this must be negotiated treaty by treaty.

### Double Non-Taxation

An emerging concern is the inverse problem: structures that achieve "double non-taxation" through treaty mismatches. Germany's switch-over clauses and the OECD/EU anti-hybrid rules (ATAD II) address this by ensuring that exemption is denied where the other state does not exercise its taxing right.
