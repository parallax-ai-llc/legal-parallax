## Summary

The right to counsel -- the legal guarantee that persons accused of criminal offenses may be assisted by a lawyer in their defense, and that those who cannot afford representation shall have counsel provided at public expense -- constitutes one of the most fundamental procedural safeguards in criminal justice systems worldwide, serving as a gateway right that enables the effective exercise of all other defense rights. South Korea enshrines the right to counsel in Article 12(4) of the Constitution (헌법 제12조 제4항), which guarantees the right to assistance of counsel (변호인의 조력을 받을 권리) from the moment of arrest, supplemented by the Criminal Procedure Act (형사소송법) provisions on mandatory defense and the Legal Aid Act (법률구조법) establishing the Korea Legal Aid Corporation (대한법률구조공단); the Constitutional Court has significantly expanded the right through landmark decisions including the 2004 ruling requiring attorney presence during police interrogation. The United States provides the right to counsel under the Sixth Amendment, with the Supreme Court's landmark decision in Gideon v. Wainwright, 372 U.S. 335 (1963), establishing that states must provide counsel to indigent defendants in felony cases, subsequently extended to all cases involving potential incarceration by Argersinger v. Hamlin (1972) and to the initial appellate stage by Douglas v. California (1963); the right attaches at all "critical stages" of prosecution as defined by Rothgery v. Gillespie County (2008). Germany guarantees the right to defense counsel under Article 6(3) ECHR and Sections 137-149 of the Strafprozessordnung (StPO, Code of Criminal Procedure), with mandatory defense (notwendige Verteidigung) required in serious cases and the system of Pflichtverteidiger (appointed defense counsel) providing representation at state expense; the 2019 reform implementing EU Directive 2013/48/EU significantly strengthened early-stage access. The ECHR has developed extensive jurisprudence on the right to counsel under Article 6(3)(c), with the Grand Chamber's Salduz v. Turkey decision (2008) establishing that access to a lawyer must be provided from the first police interrogation, and EU Directive 2013/48/EU harmonizing the right of access to a lawyer across all member states.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

**Constitution Article 12(4):** "Any person who is arrested or detained shall have the right to prompt assistance of counsel. When a criminal defendant is unable to secure counsel by his or her own efforts, the State shall assign counsel for the defendant as provided by Act."

### Criminal Procedure Act Provisions

| Provision | Content |
|-----------|---------|
| Art. 30 | Defendant may retain counsel at any stage |
| Art. 33 | Mandatory defense for death penalty or life imprisonment cases |
| Art. 33(2) | Court shall appoint counsel when defendant is indigent |
| Art. 33(3) | Court shall appoint counsel when defendant is a minor |
| Art. 34 | Defendant's family members may retain counsel |
| Art. 243-2 | Right to have counsel present during prosecution interrogation |

### Constitutional Court Expansion of Rights

**Constitutional Court Decision 2003헌마282 (2004):** The court ruled that the right to assistance of counsel encompasses the right to have an attorney physically present during police interrogation, not merely the right to consult with counsel before or after questioning. This decision was groundbreaking in Korean criminal procedure, as police had previously conducted interrogations without counsel present.

**Constitutional Court Decision 2008헌마550 (2011):** The court held that restricting a detained suspect's meetings with counsel (접견교통권) to specific hours or imposing unreasonable conditions violated the constitutional right to counsel. The court emphasized that the right to counsel is not merely a procedural formality but a substantive guarantee essential to the fairness of criminal proceedings.

### Korea Legal Aid Corporation (대한법률구조공단)

Established under the Legal Aid Act (법률구조법), the Corporation provides free legal representation to persons who cannot afford counsel. The Corporation employs approximately 300 staff attorneys and contracts with private attorneys to handle the overflow. As of 2024, the Corporation handled over 100,000 cases annually.

### Mandatory Defense

The Criminal Procedure Act requires mandatory defense (국선변호인) in the following cases:

| Circumstance | Provision |
|-------------|-----------|
| Charges carrying death or life imprisonment | Art. 33(1) No. 1 |
| Defendant is a minor | Art. 33(1) No. 2 |
| Defendant is 70+ years old | Art. 33(1) No. 3 |
| Defendant is deaf, mute, or has mental disability | Art. 33(1) No. 4 |
| Defendant is suspected of mental incompetence | Art. 33(1) No. 5 |
| Defendant requests counsel and is indigent | Art. 33(2) |

### Challenges

Despite constitutional protections, the quality of appointed counsel has been a persistent concern. Studies by the Korean Bar Association have found that court-appointed attorneys in some cases spend minimal time with clients and provide perfunctory representation. The compensation rates for appointed counsel -- significantly lower than private rates -- have been cited as a contributing factor.

## United States

### Legal Framework

**Sixth Amendment:** "In all criminal prosecutions, the accused shall enjoy the right... to have the Assistance of Counsel for his defence."

### Landmark Cases

| Case | Year | Holding |
|------|------|---------|
| Powell v. Alabama | 1932 | Right to counsel in capital cases under due process |
| Gideon v. Wainwright | 1963 | Right to appointed counsel for indigent felony defendants |
| Douglas v. California | 1963 | Right to counsel on first appeal as of right |
| Miranda v. Arizona | 1966 | Right to counsel during custodial interrogation |
| Argersinger v. Hamlin | 1972 | Right extends to all cases involving potential incarceration |
| Strickland v. Washington | 1984 | Standard for ineffective assistance of counsel claims |
| Rothgery v. Gillespie Cnty. | 2008 | Right attaches at initial appearance before judicial officer |
| Lafler v. Cooper | 2012 | Right extends to plea bargaining process |

### Gideon v. Wainwright (1963)

The Supreme Court's unanimous decision in Gideon established that the Sixth Amendment right to counsel is a fundamental right applicable to the states through the Fourteenth Amendment. Clarence Earl Gideon, charged with felony breaking and entering in Florida, was denied appointed counsel because Florida law provided free counsel only in capital cases. Gideon's handwritten petition to the Supreme Court led to one of the most consequential decisions in American criminal justice history.

### Indigent Defense Systems

The United States employs three primary models for providing counsel to indigent defendants:

| Model | Description | States Using |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| Public defender offices | Government-funded offices with full-time defense attorneys | ~28 states (including California, New York) |
| Assigned counsel | Private attorneys appointed by courts on a case-by-case basis | ~15 states |
| Contract systems | Government contracts with private attorneys or firms | ~7 states |
| Mixed systems | Combination of above models | Multiple states |

### Crisis in Indigent Defense

Despite the Gideon guarantee, indigent defense in the United States faces a systemic crisis. The American Bar Association has documented excessive caseloads, inadequate funding, and systemic failures in multiple states. In 2024, Louisiana's public defender system faced such severe underfunding that courts declared a systemic violation of the right to counsel, with individual public defenders carrying caseloads exceeding 300 felony cases per year.

### Strickland v. Washington (1984)

The two-prong test for ineffective assistance of counsel requires the defendant to demonstrate: (1) counsel's performance fell below an objective standard of reasonableness ("deficient performance"), and (2) there is a reasonable probability that, but for counsel's unprofessional errors, the result of the proceeding would have been different ("prejudice"). This standard has been criticized as excessively deferential to defense counsel, with successful Strickland claims being relatively rare.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

**StPO Sections 137-149:** These provisions establish the comprehensive framework for defense counsel rights in German criminal procedure.

### Key Provisions

| Section | Content |
|---------|---------|
| § 137 | Right to retain defense counsel at any stage |
| § 138 | Qualifications for defense counsel |
| § 140 | Cases of mandatory defense (notwendige Verteidigung) |
| § 141 | Appointment of Pflichtverteidiger (mandatory defense counsel) |
| § 141a | Right to defense counsel during police interrogation |
| § 148 | Unrestricted communication between counsel and accused |
| § 148a | Limitations on communication in terrorism cases |

### Mandatory Defense (Notwendige Verteidigung, § 140)

German law requires the appointment of defense counsel in specified circumstances:

| Circumstance | Provision |
|-------------|-----------|
| Proceedings before the Oberlandesgericht or Landgericht | § 140(1) No. 1 |
| Defendant accused of a Verbrechen (serious offense) | § 140(1) No. 2 |
| Proceedings may result in professional disqualification | § 140(1) No. 3 |
| Defendant is in pretrial detention | § 140(1) No. 4 |
| Defendant was detained for more than 3 months | § 140(1) No. 5 |
| Defendant may be committed to psychiatric hospital | § 140(1) No. 7 |
| Previous defense counsel excluded from proceedings | § 140(1) No. 8 |
| Complexity of case requires counsel | § 140(2) |

### 2019 Reform: Implementation of EU Directive 2013/48/EU

The Gesetz zur Neuregelung des Rechts der notwendigen Verteidigung (Act on the Reform of Mandatory Defense), effective December 13, 2019, implemented EU Directive 2013/48/EU and significantly strengthened early-stage access to counsel:

- **Right to counsel from first interrogation:** Section 141a StPO now explicitly guarantees the right to have defense counsel present during the first police interrogation, not merely upon formal arrest.
- **Mandatory defense triggered earlier:** The appointment of counsel is now required before the first interrogation in cases where mandatory defense applies.
- **Enhanced notification:** Suspects must be informed of their right to counsel before any questioning begins.

### Pflichtverteidiger System

The Pflichtverteidiger (appointed defense counsel) is selected by the court from the local bar. Unlike the U.S. public defender model, Germany does not maintain government-employed defense attorneys. Instead, private attorneys are appointed and compensated according to the Rechtsanwaltsvergütungsgesetz (Lawyer Compensation Act). The compensation, while regulated, is generally adequate to ensure competent representation.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Article 6(3)(c)

"Everyone charged with a criminal offence has the following minimum rights: ... (c) to defend himself in person or through legal assistance of his own choosing or, if he has not sufficient means to pay for legal assistance, to be given it free when the interests of justice so require."

### Salduz v. Turkey [GC] (Application No. 36391/02, 2008)

The Grand Chamber held that access to a lawyer should be provided from the first interrogation by police, unless there are compelling reasons to restrict the right. The Court established that the right of access to a lawyer is not merely an abstract guarantee but a practical and effective requirement. Statements obtained without access to counsel may not be used as the basis for a conviction.

### Ibrahim and Others v. United Kingdom [GC] (Application No. 50541/08, 2016)

The Grand Chamber addressed whether temporary restrictions on the right to counsel during police interrogation could be justified. The Court held that such restrictions require compelling reasons, must be temporary, and the overall fairness of the proceedings must be maintained. The Court applied a two-stage test: (1) were there compelling reasons for the restriction, and (2) did the restriction irretrievably prejudice the overall fairness of the trial.

### EU Directive 2013/48/EU

The Directive on the right of access to a lawyer in criminal proceedings establishes minimum standards:

- **Article 3:** Right of access to a lawyer without undue delay, and in any event from the earliest of the following: before questioning by police, upon carrying out investigative acts, or without undue delay after deprivation of liberty.
- **Article 3(3):** The right entitles the suspect to have their lawyer present and participate during questioning.
- **Article 4:** Right to have a third party informed upon deprivation of liberty.
- **Article 9:** Waiver must be voluntary and unequivocal, given after adequate information, and documented.

### EU Directive 2016/1919 (Legal Aid)

This Directive establishes minimum standards for legal aid in criminal proceedings, ensuring that suspects and accused persons who lack sufficient resources have the right to provisional legal aid and that a merits-and-means test is applied fairly.

## Comparative Analysis

### Timing of Attachment

The timing of when the right to counsel attaches differs across jurisdictions. South Korea guarantees the right from arrest. The United States has developed the "critical stages" doctrine, with the right attaching at the first formal judicial proceeding. Germany and the ECHR guarantee access from the first police interrogation.

### Quality of Representation

All jurisdictions struggle with ensuring the quality of publicly funded defense. The United States has the most developed (though controversial) standard through Strickland. Germany relies on professional bar standards and the Pflichtverteidiger system. South Korea has identified quality gaps in court-appointed representation. The ECHR assesses quality under the overall fairness standard.

### Waiver Standards

All jurisdictions permit waiver of the right to counsel, but with varying safeguards. The ECHR (following Salduz) requires that waivers be voluntary and unequivocal, given after the suspect has been adequately informed. U.S. law requires a "knowing, intelligent, and voluntary" waiver (Johnson v. Zerbst, 1938). German law requires that the accused be informed of the right before any waiver.
