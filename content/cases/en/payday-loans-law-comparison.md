## Summary

Payday loan regulation -- the legal framework governing short-term, high-interest consumer credit products typically repayable on the borrower's next payday -- represents one of the most contentious areas of consumer finance law, pitting access to emergency credit for financially vulnerable populations against concerns about predatory lending practices and debt traps. South Korea regulates small-amount, high-interest lending through the Act on Registration of Credit Business and Protection of Finance Users (대부업 등의 등록 및 금융이용자 보호에 관한 법률, commonly known as the Moneylending Business Act), which imposes a statutory maximum interest rate cap (법정최고금리) progressively reduced from 66 percent per annum in 2002 to 20 percent per annum effective July 2021, alongside the Interest Limitation Act (이자제한법) capping contractual interest at 20 percent per annum, enforced by the Financial Services Commission (금융위원회) and local governments. The United States regulates payday lending through a patchwork of state laws, with approaches ranging from outright prohibition (17 states and the District of Columbia) to permissive regulation allowing annualized percentage rates (APR) exceeding 400 percent, supplemented by federal protections including the Military Lending Act (10 U.S.C. § 987, capping rates at 36 percent APR for active-duty military) and the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau's (CFPB) Payday Lending Rule (12 C.F.R. Part 1041). Germany effectively prohibits payday-style lending through the Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB) Section 138 unconscionability doctrine (sittenwidrige Darlehen), which renders loan agreements void where the interest rate exceeds approximately double the market rate, and the Preisangabenverordnung (PAngV) requiring APR disclosure, while the EU's Consumer Credit Directive (Directive 2008/48/EC, replaced by Directive (EU) 2023/2225 effective November 2026) establishes disclosure requirements and member state authority to impose interest rate caps, and the ECHR has addressed the tension between contractual freedom and state obligations to protect vulnerable borrowers from exploitative practices.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's regulation of high-interest lending has undergone dramatic tightening over two decades, reflecting growing social concern about household debt and lending to vulnerable populations.

**Moneylending Business Act (대부업 등의 등록 및 금융이용자 보호에 관한 법률):** Originally enacted in 2002, this Act requires all moneylending businesses to register with local governments and comply with conduct of business requirements:

| Provision | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| Article 3 | Registration with metropolitan/provincial government |
| Article 8 | Prohibition on false or misleading advertising |
| Article 9 | Written contract requirement; disclosure of terms |
| Article 9-2 | Pre-contractual creditworthiness assessment |
| Article 11 | Prohibition on unfair debt collection practices |
| Article 15 | Establishment of supervisory system |

**Interest Rate Cap Evolution:**

| Period | Maximum Rate | Legal Basis |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| Before 2002 | Unregulated | No statutory cap for moneylenders |
| 2002-2007 | 66% per annum | Moneylending Business Act |
| 2007-2010 | 49% per annum | Amendment |
| 2010-2014 | 39% per annum | Amendment |
| 2014-2016 | 34.9% per annum | Amendment |
| 2016-2018 | 27.9% per annum | Amendment |
| 2018-2021 | 24% per annum | Amendment |
| July 2021-present | 20% per annum | Amendment; Interest Limitation Act alignment |

**Interest Limitation Act (이자제한법):** Revived in 2007 after having been abolished in 1998 during IMF-mandated deregulation, this Act caps all contractual interest rates at the statutory maximum (currently 20 percent per annum, aligned with the Moneylending Business Act cap). Interest charged in excess of the statutory cap is void (Article 2(4)), and amounts already paid may be offset against principal (Article 2(3)).

### Household Debt Crisis Context

South Korea's household debt-to-GDP ratio has risen to approximately 105 percent as of 2025, among the highest in the OECD. The progressive reduction of the interest rate cap has been accompanied by concerns that overly restrictive caps push vulnerable borrowers toward illegal private lending (사채) markets operating outside regulatory oversight. The Korean Institute of Finance estimated that the 2021 rate reduction to 20 percent excluded approximately 2.3 million borrowers from the regulated lending market.

### Government Alternatives

To mitigate the exclusion effect of rate caps, the Korean government has expanded public microfinance programs:
- **Sunshine Loan (햇살론):** Government-guaranteed small loans for low-income borrowers at reduced rates
- **New Hope Loan (새희망홀씨):** Bank-provided loans for borrowers with low credit scores
- **Smile Microcredit (미소금융):** Non-profit microfinance for self-employed and low-income individuals

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has the most fragmented payday lending regulatory landscape among major economies, with regulation primarily at the state level.

**State Regulatory Approaches:**

| Approach | States | Typical Provisions |
|----------|--------|-------------------|
| Prohibition | 17 states + D.C. (including New York, New Jersey, Maryland, Massachusetts) | Payday lending illegal; usury laws apply |
| Rate cap (36% or less) | ~8 states (including Colorado, Illinois, Virginia) | APR capped at 36% (many reformed 2020-2024) |
| Permissive regulation | ~25 states (including Texas, Mississippi, Utah) | Specific payday lending statutes; APR may exceed 400% |

**Federal Regulation:**

**Military Lending Act (MLA, 10 U.S.C. § 987):** Enacted in 2006, amended in 2015, the MLA caps interest rates on consumer credit to active-duty servicemembers and their dependents at 36 percent Military Annual Percentage Rate (MAPR). The MAPR includes fees that would be excluded from a standard APR calculation, making it a more comprehensive measure.

**Truth in Lending Act (TILA, 15 U.S.C. § 1601 et seq.):** Requires disclosure of APR, finance charges, and total payment amounts for all consumer credit, including payday loans. Regulation Z (12 C.F.R. Part 226) implements TILA.

**CFPB Payday Lending Rule (12 C.F.R. Part 1041):** The CFPB finalized its "Payday, Vehicle Title, and Certain High-Cost Installment Loans" rule in October 2017, requiring lenders to conduct ability-to-repay assessments before making covered short-term and longer-term balloon-payment loans. The rule was substantially weakened in July 2020 when the CFPB under the Trump administration rescinded the mandatory underwriting provisions, retaining only the payment withdrawal protections. The rule's status has remained contested through subsequent administrations.

### Industry Scale

The payday lending industry in the United States originated approximately $29 billion in loans annually as of 2023, with approximately 14,000 storefront locations operating nationwide. The average payday loan is approximately $375, with a typical fee of $55 per $375 (equivalent to approximately 391 percent APR for a two-week loan). Approximately 12 million Americans use payday loans annually.

### Tribal Lending and Online Lenders

Tribal lending operations -- payday lending entities affiliated with Native American tribes asserting tribal sovereign immunity from state regulation -- have complicated the regulatory landscape. The Supreme Court's decision in Yellen v. Confederated Tribes of the Chehalis Reservation, 594 U.S. 338 (2021), while not directly addressing tribal lending, clarified aspects of tribal entity status. Multiple states have brought enforcement actions against tribal lenders, with mixed results in federal and state courts.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany effectively prohibits payday-style lending through its general civil law framework rather than through specific payday lending legislation.

**BGB § 138 -- Unconscionability (Sittenwidrigkeit):** Under Section 138(2) BGB, a legal transaction through which a person exploits another's distress, inexperience, lack of judgment, or considerable weakness of will to obtain pecuniary advantages that are clearly disproportionate to the performance is void. German courts apply this provision to consumer lending through the "double rate" threshold (Doppelte des marktüblichen Zinses): a loan agreement is presumed unconscionable when the agreed interest rate exceeds approximately double the prevailing market rate for comparable credit.

**Application to High-Interest Lending:**

| Market Indicator | Approximate Rate (2025) | Unconscionability Threshold |
|-----------------|------------------------|---------------------------|
| Consumer installment credit (average) | ~8-9% per annum | ~16-18% per annum |
| Overdraft facilities (Dispo) | ~10-12% per annum | ~20-24% per annum |
| Credit card revolving credit | ~15-18% per annum | ~30-36% per annum |

Since typical payday loan APRs (300-400+ percent) vastly exceed any plausible market rate threshold, payday lending as practiced in the United States or parts of Asia is effectively impossible under German law.

**Verbraucherdarlehensrecht (BGB §§ 491-505e):** Consumer credit provisions, transposing the EU Consumer Credit Directive, require:
- Pre-contractual information disclosure (§ 491a)
- Creditworthiness assessment (§ 505a)
- Right of withdrawal within 14 days (§ 495)
- APR disclosure using the EU standardized methodology (§ 492)
- Prohibition on tied selling (§ 492a)

**Preisangabenverordnung (PAngV):** Requires disclosure of the effective annual interest rate (effektiver Jahreszins) in all consumer credit advertising and contracts.

### Alternative Access to Credit

Germany's banking system provides alternatives that reduce demand for payday-style credit:
- **Dispo credit (Dispositionskredit):** Overdraft facilities attached to current accounts, typically at 8-12 percent per annum, available to most account holders
- **Sparkassen mission:** Public savings banks (Sparkassen) have a public mandate to provide banking services to all residents, including basic credit facilities
- **Social lending (Sozialkredit):** Welfare organizations and municipal institutions provide interest-free or low-interest emergency loans
- **Grundsicherung (basic income support):** Germany's social welfare system reduces the need for emergency borrowing for basic necessities

## ECHR / EU

### Consumer Credit Directive (CCD)

The EU has regulated consumer credit through successive directives:

**Directive 2008/48/EC (CCD I):** Established harmonized rules for consumer credit between €200 and €75,000, including standardized APR calculation, pre-contractual information (Standard European Consumer Credit Information, SECCI), right of withdrawal (14 days), and creditworthiness assessment obligations. CCD I did not impose interest rate caps but permitted member states to maintain or introduce national caps.

**Directive (EU) 2023/2225 (CCD II, adopted November 2023):** Replacing CCD I, with transposition deadline of November 20, 2026, CCD II significantly strengthens consumer protections:

| CCD II Provision | Description |
|-----------------|-------------|
| Article 10 | Enhanced creditworthiness assessment obligations |
| Article 11 | Prohibition on lending where assessment indicates likely inability to repay |
| Article 12 | Personalized explanations of credit terms |
| Article 14 | Right of member states to impose interest rate caps, total cost caps, or APR caps |
| Article 26 | 14-day right of withdrawal |
| Article 36 | Extended scope to cover credit under €200 and interest-free credit |
| Article 44 | Advisory services: member states must ensure access to debt advisory services |
| Recital 67 | Recognition that member state interest rate caps are effective consumer protection tools |

### Member State Interest Rate Caps

CCD II explicitly affirms member states' authority to impose interest rate caps. As of 2025, significant variation exists:

| Member State | Cap Mechanism |
|-------------|---------------|
| France | Taux d'usure: 4/3 of average market rate per loan category |
| Germany | BGB § 138 unconscionability (approximately 2x market rate) |
| Netherlands | Maximum credit compensation: statutory interest rate + 12 percentage points |
| Poland | Maximum non-interest costs: 25% of credit value + 30% per annum |
| Belgium | APR caps by loan size and duration (set by royal decree) |
| Spain | Supreme Court case law on revolving credit (2020) |

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Vaskrsić v. Slovenia (Application No. 31371/12, ECtHR, April 25, 2017):** The Court found a violation of Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 where a disproportionate enforcement measure (sale of the applicant's home) was used to recover a small debt. The case established that states have a positive obligation to ensure proportionate debt enforcement procedures, relevant to aggressive payday loan collection practices.

**Zolotas v. Greece (No. 2) (Application No. 66610/09, ECtHR, June 29, 2013):** The Court addressed the charging of interest on dormant bank accounts, finding a violation of Article 1 of Protocol No. 1. The judgment's principles regarding disproportionate financial charges are relevant to the cumulative cost of payday loan rollovers.

**Bäck v. Finland (Application No. 37598/97, ECtHR, July 20, 2004):** The Court addressed debt adjustment proceedings and the balance between creditor rights and debtor protection, establishing that states enjoy a wide margin of appreciation in designing mechanisms to protect over-indebted individuals.

### EU High-Level Initiatives

The European Commission has addressed high-cost credit through several non-legislative initiatives:
- **Responsible Lending and Borrowing (2011):** Commission staff working document on responsible lending practices
- **Financial Literacy Strategy:** EU-wide financial education initiatives targeting vulnerable consumers
- **European Social Pillar:** Principle 15 recognizes the right to adequate social protection, relevant to reducing dependence on high-cost credit

## Comparative Analysis

### Regulatory Philosophy

The four jurisdictions reflect fundamentally different philosophies regarding payday lending:

**Prohibition through general law (Germany):** The unconscionability doctrine effectively prevents payday lending without requiring specific legislation, reflecting a civil law tradition that treats grossly unfair contracts as legally void.

**Progressive rate cap (South Korea):** Korea has steadily reduced its maximum interest rate over 20 years, reflecting a policy choice to accept some credit exclusion in exchange for borrower protection, supplemented by public microfinance alternatives.

**Market-based with targeted protections (United States):** The US approach allows significant state variation, from prohibition to permissive regulation, reflecting federalism and tension between consumer protection and credit access.

**Minimum harmonization with member state discretion (EU):** The EU framework establishes disclosure and assessment requirements while permitting member states to impose interest rate caps.

### Effectiveness Assessment

| Criterion | South Korea | United States | Germany | EU |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|---------|-----|
| Prevents excessive interest | Yes (20% cap) | Inconsistent (state variation) | Yes (unconscionability) | Depends on member state |
| Maintains credit access | Partial (public alternatives) | Yes (where legal) | Yes (banking system) | Depends on member state |
| Prevents illegal lending | Challenging (사채 market) | Tribal/online lending circumvention | Effective | Varies |
| Protects most vulnerable | Moderate | Weak (in permissive states) | Strong | Varies |

### Emerging Trends

All jurisdictions face new challenges from technology-driven high-cost credit:
- **Buy Now, Pay Later (BNPL):** Increasingly regulated as consumer credit (CCD II extends scope; CFPB interpretive rule in the US)
- **Earned Wage Access (EWA):** Products allowing workers to access earned but unpaid wages; regulatory classification debated
- **Digital lending platforms:** Online lending circumventing traditional regulatory perimeters

### Buy Now, Pay Later (BNPL) Regulation

BNPL products have emerged as a significant regulatory concern:

| Jurisdiction | BNPL Status | Regulatory Approach |
|-------------|-------------|-------------------|
| South Korea | Rapidly growing (Kakao Pay, Naver Pay BNPL) | FSC guidance treating BNPL as installment sales or credit; regulatory framework under development |
| United States | Major market ($30+ billion annual volume) | CFPB interpretive rule (2024) treating BNPL as "credit card" under TILA; state-level regulation varies |
| Germany/EU | Growing market (Klarna, PayPal Pay Later) | CCD II (2023/2225) extends scope to cover interest-free credit including BNPL |

Key BNPL regulatory concerns include:
- Absence of creditworthiness assessment requirements (addressed by CCD II)
- Multiple concurrent BNPL obligations not visible to credit bureaus
- Potential for over-indebtedness among younger consumers
- Late fee structures that may function similarly to high-interest credit

### Behavioral Economics and Regulatory Design

Payday loan regulation increasingly reflects behavioral economics insights:
- **Disclosure design:** Standard APR disclosure has limited effectiveness because borrowers focus on fee amounts rather than annualized rates. California's "dollar cost disclosure" requirement and the UK FCA's price cap experiments reflect behavioral approaches.
- **Cooling-off periods:** Mandatory waiting periods between consecutive payday loans (required in some U.S. states) address impulsive borrowing behavior
- **Default options:** Automatic enrollment in repayment plans (rather than rollovers) can reduce debt trap effects
- **Debt counseling mandates:** Germany's Schuldnerberatung (debt counseling) system provides free counseling services, and CCD II Article 44 requires member states to ensure access to advisory services

### Social Impact Assessment

The impact of payday lending regulation extends beyond individual borrowers:

| Metric | Rate Cap (South Korea Model) | Market Approach (U.S. Permissive States) |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Access to emergency credit | Reduced (some borrowers excluded) | Broad access |
| Debt trap incidence | Lower | Higher |
| Illegal lending market | May increase (사채 in Korea) | Lower (legal alternatives available) |
| Borrower outcomes | Generally better for those with access | Mixed; some benefit, many harmed |
| Overall welfare | Debated | Debated |

### Earned Wage Access and Salary Advances

Earned wage access (EWA) products, which allow workers to access a portion of their earned but unpaid wages before payday, represent a growing alternative to payday loans:

- **United States:** EWA providers (Earnin, DailyPay, Payactiv) argue their products are not "credit" because workers have already earned the wages; the CFPB issued an advisory opinion (2020) stating that certain EWA products are not "credit" under TILA, but several states have challenged this interpretation; California's Earned Wage Access Services Act (2024) established a dedicated regulatory framework
- **South Korea:** Salary advance services have grown rapidly through fintech platforms; the FSC has issued guidance classifying some EWA products as lending under the Moneylending Business Act, requiring registration and interest rate cap compliance
- **EU:** EWA products are generally classified as credit under the Consumer Credit Directive if they involve any charge or fee; the CCD II (2023) does not create a specific EWA exemption
