## Summary

Emergency powers -- the legal authority of the state to suspend or restrict ordinary constitutional protections, centralize decision-making, and mobilize resources in response to existential threats including war, natural disasters, public health crises, and internal disturbances -- represent one of the most sensitive areas of constitutional law, as they involve the tension between the imperative to protect the state and the risk that emergency measures may be abused to undermine democratic governance. South Korea's Constitution contains detailed emergency power provisions under Articles 76 and 77 (대한민국 헌법 제76조, 제77조), authorizing presidential emergency decrees and the declaration of martial law (계엄), with historical experience of emergency power abuse by authoritarian regimes -- including President Park Chung-hee's Emergency Measures (긴급조치) of 1972-1979 and the martial law declarations surrounding the 1980 Gwangju Uprising -- profoundly shaping constitutional safeguards; the December 3, 2024 martial law declaration by President Yoon Suk-yeol, which lasted approximately six hours before being lifted by the National Assembly, demonstrated that these safeguards retain vitality. The United States distributes emergency powers across the federal and state levels, with the President exercising powers under the National Emergencies Act of 1976 (50 U.S.C. Section 1601), the Insurrection Act (10 U.S.C. Section 251-255), and the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA, 50 U.S.C. Section 1701), while the Supreme Court has imposed constitutional limits most notably in Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co. v. Sawyer, 343 U.S. 579 (1952), establishing Justice Jackson's three-tier framework for analyzing presidential power. Germany provides for states of emergency under Articles 35, 80a, 87a, and 91 of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz), with the Emergency Constitution (Notstandsverfassung), enacted in 1968 after heated debate, establishing detailed safeguards including the Joint Committee (Gemeinsamer Ausschuss) as a mini-parliament for situations where the Bundestag cannot convene, and a prohibition on amending the Basic Law during a state of defense (Verteidigungsfall); Germany's experience with the Weimar Republic's Article 48, whose abuse facilitated Hitler's rise to power, deeply informed the Basic Law's restrictive approach. The European framework addresses emergency powers through ECHR Article 15, which permits derogation from certain Convention rights "in time of war or other public emergency threatening the life of the nation," subject to strict conditions including notification to the Secretary General of the Council of Europe, proportionality, and the non-derogable character of certain rights including the right to life (Article 2), the prohibition of torture (Article 3), the prohibition of slavery (Article 4), and the principle of no punishment without law (Article 7).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Article 76 of the Constitution authorizes presidential emergency decrees in two scenarios:

"(1) In time of internal turmoil, external menace, natural calamity, or a grave financial or economic crisis, the President may take minimum necessary financial and economic actions or issue orders having the effect of Act when it is required to take urgent measures for the maintenance of national security or public peace and order, and there is no time to await the convocation of the National Assembly.

(2) In case of major hostility affecting national security, the President may issue orders having the effect of Act when it is required to preserve the integrity of the territory of the nation or to protect the nation, and when it is impossible to convene the National Assembly."

Article 77 authorizes the declaration of martial law (계엄):

"(1) When it is required to cope with a military necessity or to maintain the public safety and order by mobilization of the military forces in time of war, armed conflict, or similar national emergency, the President may proclaim martial law under the conditions as prescribed by Act."

Article 77(3) distinguishes between extraordinary martial law (비상계엄), under which warrants, the right to speech, press, assembly, and association, and the powers of the government or courts may be subject to special measures, and precautionary martial law (경비계엄), which involves a lesser degree of military authority.

### Historical Abuse

**Emergency Measures (긴급조치, 1972-1979):** Under the Yushin Constitution of 1972, President Park Chung-hee issued nine Emergency Measures that severely curtailed civil liberties, prohibiting criticism of the Constitution, banning specific organizations, and subjecting violators to military tribunals. The Constitutional Court retroactively declared Emergency Measures 1, 2, 4, and 9 unconstitutional in a series of decisions from 2010 to 2013.

**Gwangju Uprising (1980):** Following President Park's assassination in 1979, General Chun Doo-hwan declared martial law on May 17, 1980, extending it nationwide and deploying special forces to suppress pro-democracy protests in Gwangju. An estimated 200-2,000 civilians were killed during the suppression. The Special Act on the May 18 Democratization Movement (5.18민주화운동 등에 관한 특별법, 1995) provided for prosecution of those responsible.

### December 3, 2024 Martial Law

On December 3, 2024, President Yoon Suk-yeol declared emergency martial law, citing threats from "anti-state forces." Military personnel were deployed to the National Assembly and the National Election Commission. The National Assembly, however, convened and voted 190-0 to demand the lifting of martial law, exercising its constitutional authority under Article 77(5). President Yoon lifted the martial law declaration approximately six hours after it was imposed. The incident led to President Yoon's impeachment by the National Assembly on December 14, 2024, and subsequent arrest. The Constitutional Court is adjudicating the impeachment as of early 2026.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The Constitution does not contain a comprehensive emergency powers clause but distributes relevant authority between Congress and the President:

**Suspension Clause (Article I, Section 9, Clause 2):** "The Privilege of the Writ of Habeas Corpus shall not be suspended, unless when in Cases of Rebellion or Invasion the public Safety may require it."

**Commander-in-Chief Clause (Article II, Section 2):** The President serves as Commander-in-Chief of the armed forces.

**National Emergencies Act (NEA, 1976):** Establishes procedures for presidential emergency declarations, requiring the President to specify the statutory authorities activated by the emergency, to report to Congress on expenditures, and to renew the declaration annually. Congress may terminate a declaration by joint resolution.

### Youngstown Framework

**Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co. v. Sawyer, 343 U.S. 579 (1952):** President Truman's seizure of steel mills during the Korean War was struck down. Justice Jackson's concurrence established a three-tier framework:

| Category | Situation | Presidential Power |
|----------|-----------|-------------------|
| Category One | President acts pursuant to Congressional authorization | Maximum presidential power; presumptively valid |
| Category Two | President acts in absence of Congressional grant or denial | "Zone of twilight"; concurrent authority; depends on circumstances |
| Category Three | President acts contrary to Congressional will | Lowest ebb of presidential power; constitutional only if exclusive presidential authority |

### Post-9/11 Emergency Powers

The Authorization for Use of Military Force (AUMF, 2001) and subsequent executive actions raised fundamental questions about emergency powers:

**Hamdi v. Rumsfeld, 542 U.S. 507 (2004):** Held that a citizen detained as an enemy combatant must be given a meaningful opportunity to contest detention before a neutral decision-maker.

**Boumediene v. Bush, 553 U.S. 723 (2008):** Held that Guantanamo detainees have a constitutional right to habeas corpus, striking down provisions of the Military Commissions Act that stripped federal courts of jurisdiction.

### Insurrection Act (10 U.S.C. Section 251-255)

The Insurrection Act authorizes the President to deploy the armed forces domestically in limited circumstances, including when requested by a state legislature or governor, when federal law is obstructed, or when the Constitutional rights of citizens are being denied. The Act has been invoked on numerous occasions, most recently during civil unrest in 1992 (Los Angeles) and was considered but not invoked during the 2020 protests.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

The Basic Law's emergency provisions were enacted through the Notstandsgesetze (Emergency Acts) of 1968, adopted only after extensive public debate and protest. The provisions reflect deep concern about the abuse of emergency powers under the Weimar Republic:

**Article 91 (Internal Emergency):** The Federation may assist a Land in combating a danger to the free democratic basic order if the Land is unable to do so itself.

**Article 80a (State of Tension, Spannungsfall):** Permits the application of certain defense-related laws when the Bundestag determines that a state of tension exists.

**Articles 115a-115l (State of Defense, Verteidigungsfall):** Provide for the declaration of a state of defense when the federal territory is under armed attack or imminent threat. The declaration requires a two-thirds majority of the Bundestag and consent of the Bundesrat. During a state of defense:

- The Joint Committee (Gemeinsamer Ausschuss, Art. 53a) may assume legislative functions if the Bundestag cannot convene
- The Federal Government may issue legislative orders (Art. 115f)
- The term of the Bundestag and state parliaments is extended; no elections during the emergency
- The Basic Law may not be amended (Art. 115g references Art. 79(3))
- The Federal Constitutional Court continues to function (Art. 115g)
- The state of defense must be terminated when the conditions no longer exist; the Bundestag may demand termination at any time

### Article 48 Weimar Constitution (Historical)

The Basic Law's restrictive approach to emergency powers was a direct response to the abuse of Article 48 of the Weimar Constitution, which authorized the Reich President to take measures necessary to restore public security and order, including the suspension of fundamental rights. President Hindenburg invoked Article 48 approximately 250 times between 1919 and 1933, and it was used to bypass the Reichstag and pave the way for the Nazi Enabling Act of March 23, 1933.

### COVID-19 Emergency Measures

Germany's response to COVID-19 tested the emergency framework in a new context. Rather than declaring a formal state of emergency under the Basic Law, the government relied on the Infektionsschutzgesetz (Infection Protection Act, IfSG), which was amended multiple times to provide legal bases for pandemic restrictions. The Federal Constitutional Court upheld the Bundesnotbremse (federal emergency brake) measures, including curfews and contact restrictions, in its November 2021 decision (1 BvR 781/21).

## ECHR & EU

### Article 15 ECHR (Derogation in Time of Emergency)

"(1) In time of war or other public emergency threatening the life of the nation, any High Contracting Party may take measures derogating from its obligations under this Convention to the extent strictly required by the exigencies of the situation, provided that such measures are not inconsistent with its other obligations under international law."

Article 15(2) prohibits derogation from: Article 2 (right to life, except lawful acts of war), Article 3 (torture), Article 4(1) (slavery), and Article 7 (no punishment without law).

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Lawless v. Ireland (No. 3) (Application No. 332/57, 1961):** The Court's first derogation case upheld Ireland's detention without trial of an IRA member, establishing that a "public emergency threatening the life of the nation" requires an actual or imminent exceptional crisis affecting the whole population and constituting a threat to the organized life of the community.

**Ireland v. United Kingdom (Application No. 5310/71, 1978):** Addressed the UK's derogation during the Northern Ireland conflict, finding that the "five techniques" of interrogation constituted inhuman and degrading treatment (Article 3) but not torture. Crucially, the Court confirmed that Article 3 is non-derogable.

**A and Others v. United Kingdom (Application No. 3455/05, Grand Chamber, 2009):** The Grand Chamber found that the UK's indefinite detention of foreign terror suspects under the Anti-Terrorism, Crime and Security Act 2001 violated Article 5(1) (right to liberty) read with Article 14 (non-discrimination), as the measures disproportionately targeted non-nationals.

**Alparslan Altan v. Turkey (Application No. 12778/17, 2019):** The Court found that Turkey's detention of a Constitutional Court judge following the 2016 coup attempt was not justified under its Article 15 derogation, as the detention lacked a plausible basis of reasonable suspicion.

Notably, most states did not invoke Article 15 during the COVID-19 pandemic, relying instead on ordinary restrictions under the limitation clauses of individual Convention rights. Only ten states submitted derogation notices related to COVID-19.

### Venice Commission Standards

The European Commission for Democracy through Law (Venice Commission) has issued detailed guidance on emergency powers, including the "Respect for Democracy, Human Rights and the Rule of Law during States of Emergency" compilation (CDL-PI(2020)003). Key principles include: emergency measures must be proportionate, time-limited, subject to parliamentary oversight, subject to judicial review, and must not be used to suppress opposition or undermine democratic processes.

## Comparative Analysis

### Constitutional Design

Germany's Basic Law provides the most detailed and restrictive emergency framework, with multiple categories of emergency, specific procedural requirements, and explicit prohibitions on constitutional amendment. The United States lacks a comprehensive emergency clause, distributing power between branches and relying on the Youngstown framework for judicial review. South Korea has detailed constitutional provisions that have been strengthened by historical experience of abuse. The ECHR framework provides a supranational check on national emergency measures.

### Legislative Checks

All four systems provide for legislative oversight of emergency powers, but the mechanisms differ. The National Assembly of Korea can lift martial law by majority vote. The U.S. Congress may terminate national emergencies by joint resolution. The German Bundestag may demand termination of a state of defense at any time. The ECHR requires notification to the Council of Europe but no legislative approval.

### Historical Lessons

Each jurisdiction's emergency power framework reflects its historical experience. Germany's restrictive approach responds to the Weimar/Nazi abuse. South Korea's safeguards respond to the authoritarian use of emergency measures in the 1970s-1980s. The United States' framework reflects the Founders' distrust of concentrated executive power. The ECHR's Article 15 regime reflects the European experience of wartime and authoritarian governance.

### Judicial Review

All four systems maintain judicial review during emergencies, though the scope varies. Germany explicitly preserves Federal Constitutional Court jurisdiction. The United States has generally maintained judicial review, as demonstrated in Hamdi and Boumediene. South Korea's Constitutional Court exercises post-hoc review. The ECtHR reviews derogation measures but applies a margin of appreciation.
