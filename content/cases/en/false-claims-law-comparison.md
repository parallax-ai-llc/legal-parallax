## Summary

False claims and government fraud law -- the body of regulation targeting fraudulent claims for payment, procurement fraud, and misrepresentation in dealings with the state -- reflects fundamentally different enforcement philosophies across jurisdictions. South Korea addresses government fraud primarily through the Act on the Prevention of Corruption and the Establishment and Management of the Anti-Corruption and Civil Rights Commission (부패방지법) and the Criminal Act (형법) Articles 347 (fraud), 355 (embezzlement/breach of trust), and 356 (aggravated embezzlement by fiduciaries), with supplementary regulation under the Act on Contracts to Which the State Is a Party (국가를 당사자로 하는 계약에 관한 법률) governing public procurement integrity. The United States possesses the world's most powerful government fraud statute in the False Claims Act (FCA, 31 U.S.C. §§ 3729-3733), uniquely combining treble damages, per-claim civil penalties of $13,946-$27,894 (2025 figures), and a qui tam mechanism allowing private relators to file suit on behalf of the government and receive 15-30% of recovered proceeds -- a system that has recovered over $75 billion since 1986. Germany prosecutes government fraud through Strafgesetzbuch (StGB) § 263 (Betrug/fraud) and § 264 (Subventionsbetrug/subsidy fraud), supplemented by the Act against Restraints of Competition (GWB) §§ 97 ff. governing public procurement, while EU anti-fraud measures are coordinated through the European Anti-Fraud Office (OLAF), the European Public Prosecutor's Office (EPPO), and the PIF Directive (Directive (EU) 2017/1371) protecting the EU's financial interests through harmonized criminal offenses.

## South Korea

### Criminal Law Framework

South Korean law addresses government fraud primarily through general criminal provisions in the Criminal Act (형법):

**Article 347 (Fraud):** "A person who defrauds another person and thereby obtains or causes a third person to obtain property or pecuniary advantage shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than ten years or by a fine not exceeding twenty million won." This general fraud provision applies to all forms of fraudulent misrepresentation to the government, including false claims for payment, fraudulent invoicing, and misrepresentation in grant applications.

**Article 355 (Embezzlement and Breach of Trust):** (1) "A person who administers another's property and embezzles or disposes of it for his/her own benefit or for the benefit of a third person shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than five years or by a fine not exceeding fifteen million won." (2) "A person who, administering another's business, obtains a pecuniary advantage for himself/herself or a third person, or causes damage to the principal, in violation of his/her duty, shall be subject to the same punishment."

**Article 356 (Aggravated Embezzlement):** Applies when the offender acts in the capacity of a fiduciary managing another's property. Penalties are increased to imprisonment of up to ten years or fines not exceeding thirty million won.

**Special Economic Crimes Act (특정경제범죄 가중처벌 등에 관한 법률):** For large-scale government fraud, penalties are significantly enhanced:
- Fraud involving 500 million KRW or more: imprisonment for not less than 3 years.
- Fraud involving 5 billion KRW or more: imprisonment for not less than 5 years or life imprisonment.

### Public Procurement Framework

**Act on Contracts to Which the State Is a Party (국가를 당사자로 하는 계약에 관한 법률):** This Act and its Enforcement Decree govern all government contracts, establishing procedures for competitive bidding, contract execution, and fraud prevention:

- **Competitive bidding requirements:** Government contracts above threshold values must be awarded through open competitive bidding, limited competitive bidding, or negotiated contracts.
- **Integrity pact system:** Government procurement agencies may require bidders to sign integrity pacts pledging not to engage in bribery, collusion, or fraudulent claims.
- **Electronic procurement:** The Government e-Procurement System (GePS/나라장터) operated by the Public Procurement Service (PPS) provides transparency in government contracting.

**Debarment:** Companies convicted of fraud, bid rigging, or corruption in government contracts face administrative debarment (부정당업자 제재) for periods of one month to two years, during which they are barred from participating in government procurement.

### Board of Audit and Inspection (BAI)

The Board of Audit and Inspection (감사원), a constitutional institution under Article 97 of the Constitution, audits government expenditures and investigates waste, fraud, and abuse. BAI audit findings may be referred to prosecution authorities for criminal investigation.

### ACRC Anti-Corruption Measures

The Anti-Corruption and Civil Rights Commission (ACRC) plays a central role in preventing and detecting government fraud:

- **Code of Conduct for Public Officials:** Establishes standards prohibiting conflicts of interest, improper solicitation, and acceptance of unlawful advantages.
- **Improper Solicitation and Graft Act (청탁금지법, "Kim Young-ran Act," 2016):** Prohibits improper solicitations to public officials and limits the value of gifts, meals, and congratulatory/condolence money that public officials may accept. Violations are punishable by criminal penalties or administrative fines.
- **Whistleblower rewards:** The ACRC administers financial rewards for individuals who report corruption and fraud against the government, with rewards up to 3 billion KRW.

### Landmark Cases

**Defense Procurement Fraud (2014-2018):** The Defense Acquisition Program Administration (DAPA) uncovered systematic fraud by defense contractors submitting inflated cost claims and fraudulent test data for military equipment. Criminal prosecutions resulted in imprisonment of corporate executives and debarment of companies.

**National Health Insurance Fraud Cases (ongoing):** The National Health Insurance Service (NHIS) regularly prosecutes healthcare providers for submitting false claims for reimbursement, including billing for services not rendered, upcoding, and phantom patients. The NHIS operates a specialized fraud investigation unit.

## United States

### False Claims Act (31 U.S.C. §§ 3729-3733)

The False Claims Act, originally enacted in 1863 during the Civil War to combat defense contractor fraud, is the most powerful government fraud statute in the world and the primary vehicle for recovering losses from fraud against the federal government:

**Prohibited Conduct (§ 3729(a)(1)):**
- (A) Knowingly presenting or causing to be presented a false or fraudulent claim for payment or approval.
- (B) Knowingly making or using a false record or statement material to a false or fraudulent claim.
- (C) Conspiring to commit a violation of the FCA.
- (D) Knowingly making or using a false record or statement material to an obligation to pay or transmit money or property to the Government (the "reverse false claims" provision).
- (G) Knowingly concealing or knowingly and improperly avoiding or decreasing an obligation to pay or transmit money or property to the Government.

**Scienter Standard (§ 3729(b)(1)):** The FCA defines "knowingly" to include:
- Actual knowledge of the information.
- Deliberate ignorance of the truth or falsity of the information.
- Reckless disregard of the truth or falsity of the information.

Crucially, no specific intent to defraud is required. This expansive scienter standard distinguishes the FCA from criminal fraud statutes.

**Damages and Penalties (§ 3729(a)):**
- **Treble damages:** The violator is liable for three times the amount of damages the Government sustains.
- **Civil penalties:** Per-claim civil penalties adjusted annually for inflation -- currently $13,946 to $27,894 per false claim (2025 figures).
- **Costs:** Recovery of the costs of the civil action.

### Qui Tam Mechanism (§ 3730)

The FCA's qui tam provision is its most distinctive feature, empowering private citizens (relators) to file civil actions on behalf of the United States:

**Filing Procedure (§ 3730(b)):**
1. The relator files a complaint under seal in federal court.
2. The complaint remains under seal for at least 60 days (routinely extended) while the DOJ investigates.
3. The government decides whether to intervene and take over the prosecution or decline intervention.

**Relator's Share (§ 3730(d)):**
- **If the government intervenes:** The relator receives 15-25% of the proceeds of the action or settlement.
- **If the government declines to intervene:** The relator receives 25-30% of the proceeds.
- The court may increase the percentage where the action is based primarily on publicly disclosed information, or decrease it where the relator planned and initiated the violation.

**Government Intervention Decision:** In practice, the DOJ intervenes in approximately 20-25% of qui tam cases. However, intervened cases account for the overwhelming majority of recoveries, as government participation substantially increases the likelihood of a favorable outcome.

### Landmark FCA Cases and Recoveries

**Recovery Statistics:** Since the 1986 amendments that strengthened the FCA, the Department of Justice has recovered over $75 billion. In fiscal year 2024, the DOJ reported over $2.9 billion in FCA settlements and judgments, with qui tam actions accounting for the vast majority.

**United States ex rel. Escobar v. Universal Health Services, Inc., 579 U.S. 176 (2016):** The Supreme Court held that the FCA's materiality requirement is "demanding" and cannot be satisfied merely by showing that the government would have had the right to decline payment had it known of the violation. The government's actual knowledge and continued payment is strong evidence of immateriality. This decision significantly tightened FCA enforcement.

**United States v. Brookdale Senior Living Communities, Inc. (2021):** The DOJ recovered $56.1 million in a settlement involving false claims for Medicare therapy services and hospice services, one of numerous large healthcare fraud FCA recoveries.

**Healthcare Fraud:** The healthcare sector consistently generates the largest FCA recoveries, accounting for approximately 80% of annual FCA recoveries. Common theories include billing for medically unnecessary services, upcoding, kickbacks (Anti-Kickback Statute/Stark Law violations presented as FCA claims), and failure to meet quality standards.

**Defense Procurement Fraud:** The second-largest area of FCA enforcement involves defense contractor fraud, including defective product claims, cost mischarging, false testing and quality assurance certifications, and small business set-aside fraud.

### Criminal False Claims

**18 U.S.C. § 287 (False, Fictitious, or Fraudulent Claims):** Makes it a criminal offense to knowingly make or present a false, fictitious, or fraudulent claim upon or against the United States. Penalties include imprisonment of up to 5 years and fines.

**18 U.S.C. § 1001 (False Statements):** Criminalizes making materially false statements to the federal government in any matter within its jurisdiction. Penalties include imprisonment of up to 5 years and fines, increased to 8 years for offenses involving international or domestic terrorism.

**18 U.S.C. § 1341-1343 (Mail and Wire Fraud):** The federal mail and wire fraud statutes -- with penalties of up to 20 years imprisonment (30 years if affecting a financial institution) -- are frequently used to prosecute government fraud schemes.

### Anti-Kickback Statute and Stark Law

**Anti-Kickback Statute (42 U.S.C. § 1320a-7b(b)):** Prohibits offering, paying, soliciting, or receiving anything of value to induce or reward referrals for services covered by federal healthcare programs. Violations are felonies punishable by up to 10 years imprisonment. Since 2010, a claim resulting from a kickback arrangement is automatically deemed a false claim under the FCA.

**Physician Self-Referral Law (Stark Law, 42 U.S.C. § 1395nn):** Prohibits physicians from referring Medicare patients for designated health services to entities with which the physician has a financial relationship, unless an exception applies. Stark Law violations carry civil penalties and FCA liability.

## Germany

### Criminal Fraud (StGB § 263)

German law addresses government fraud primarily through the general criminal fraud provision:

**StGB § 263 (Betrug):** "(1) Whoever, with the intention of obtaining an unlawful pecuniary advantage for himself or a third person, damages the assets of another by causing or maintaining an error by pretending false facts or by distorting or suppressing true facts, shall be punished with imprisonment for not more than five years or a fine."

Key elements for government fraud prosecution:
- **Deception (Täuschung):** Active misrepresentation of facts or failure to disclose material information where there is a duty to do so.
- **Error (Irrtum):** The government official processing the claim must be actually deceived.
- **Disposition (Vermögensverfügung):** The deceived official must make a disposition of government assets based on the error.
- **Damage (Vermögensschaden):** The government must suffer actual financial loss.

**Aggravated fraud (§ 263(3)):** In particularly serious cases -- including professional or gang fraud, causing large-scale losses, or exploiting a fiduciary position -- penalties increase to imprisonment of 6 months to 10 years.

**Computer fraud (§ 263a):** Addresses manipulation of data processing to obtain unauthorized financial advantages, covering automated government payment systems where no human official is deceived.

### Subsidy Fraud (StGB § 264)

Germany has a specific criminal provision for subsidy fraud, uniquely tailored to fraud against government financial assistance programs:

**StGB § 264 (Subventionsbetrug):** "(1) Whoever, in connection with a subsidy:
1. provides a grantor or another agency or person involved in the subsidy proceedings with incorrect or incomplete information that is material to the subsidy application, for himself or another,
2. uses an object or a pecuniary advantage obtained through a subsidy contrary to the conditions attached to the subsidy,
3. fails to notify the grantor of material facts, contrary to the law on subsidies..."

Key features:
- **Lower scienter threshold:** Unlike § 263, subsidy fraud does not require that the government be actually deceived -- the mere submission of incorrect or incomplete information in subsidy proceedings suffices.
- **Broad definition of subsidy (§ 264(8)):** Includes any financial benefit from public funds to enterprises or businesses that is at least partly provided without market-rate consideration.
- **Recklessness (Leichtfertigkeit, § 264(5)):** Reckless commission is punishable, with penalties of up to 3 years imprisonment or a fine.
- **Penalties:** Imprisonment of up to 5 years or fine (standard); up to 10 years for particularly serious cases (§ 264(2)).

### Public Procurement Law

**GWB (Act against Restraints of Competition) §§ 97 ff.:** Germany's public procurement law, implementing the EU Public Procurement Directives, establishes comprehensive rules for government contracting:

**Mandatory Exclusion (§ 123 GWB):** Contracting authorities must exclude bidders convicted of specified offenses including:
- Participation in a criminal organization (§ 129 StGB)
- Terrorism financing (§ 89c StGB)
- Money laundering (§ 261 StGB)
- Fraud (§ 263 StGB) and subsidy fraud (§ 264 StGB)
- Bribery and corruption offenses (§§ 299, 332-334 StGB)
- Tax evasion (§ 370 Abgabenordnung)

**Discretionary Exclusion (§ 124 GWB):** Contracting authorities may exclude bidders who have demonstrated:
- Violations of environmental, social, or labor law obligations
- Insolvency or equivalent proceedings
- Professional misconduct that calls into question their integrity
- Agreements with other bidders aimed at distorting competition
- Significant deficiencies in performing prior public contracts

**Self-Cleaning (Selbstreinigung, § 125 GWB):** Bidders subject to exclusion may demonstrate that they have taken sufficient corrective measures, including compensation of damages, cooperation with investigating authorities, and implementation of compliance systems.

### Bid Rigging (StGB § 298)

**StGB § 298 (Wettbewerbsbeschränkende Absprachen bei Ausschreibungen):** Specifically criminalizes agreements aimed at causing a particular bid to be accepted in a procurement procedure. Penalties include imprisonment of up to 5 years or a fine. This provision addresses the intersection of procurement fraud and cartel law.

### Federal Audit Office (Bundesrechnungshof)

The Bundesrechnungshof, Germany's supreme audit institution, audits the expenditure and revenue of the federal government under Articles 114 and 117 of the Basic Law. While the Bundesrechnungshof cannot prosecute fraud directly, its audit findings frequently lead to referrals to prosecution authorities and legislative reforms.

### Landmark Cases

**Toll Collect GmbH (2018):** After years of litigation, the federal government settled a major fraud and damage claim against the operators of the German motorway toll system for EUR 3.2 billion, arising from the delayed and flawed implementation of the toll collection system. The case involved allegations of deliberate misrepresentation of project readiness.

**European Emissions Scandal (Dieselgate, 2015-ongoing):** German prosecution authorities charged Volkswagen executives with market manipulation and fraud in connection with the diesel emissions cheating scandal. The Braunschweig Regional Court convicted former VW CEO Martin Winterkorn-related proceedings involved allegations of fraud against regulators and consumers.

## ECHR/EU Framework

### Protection of EU Financial Interests (PIF)

**PIF Directive (Directive (EU) 2017/1371):** The Directive on the fight against fraud to the Union's financial interests through criminal law harmonizes the definition of offenses affecting the EU budget:

**Fraud affecting expenditure (Article 3(2)(a)):** Use or presentation of false, incorrect, or incomplete statements or documents resulting in misappropriation or wrongful retention of EU funds.

**Fraud affecting revenue (Article 3(2)(b)):** Use or presentation of false, incorrect, or incomplete statements or documents resulting in the illegal diminution of EU resources (e.g., customs duty fraud, VAT fraud).

**Money laundering (Article 4(1)):** As defined in Directive (EU) 2015/849, when connected to PIF offenses.

**Corruption (Article 4(2)):** Active and passive corruption of public officials affecting the EU's financial interests.

**Misappropriation (Article 4(3)):** The intentional commitment of funds for purposes other than those for which they were granted.

**Minimum Penalties (Article 7):**
- Offenses involving damage or advantage exceeding EUR 100,000: maximum penalty of at least 4 years imprisonment.
- Offenses involving damage or advantage exceeding EUR 10,000 (or involving other specified elements): criminal penalties, which must include imprisonment.

### European Anti-Fraud Office (OLAF)

OLAF (Office Européen de Lutte Antifraude), operating under Regulation (EU, Euratom) No 883/2013, conducts administrative investigations into fraud, corruption, and other illegal activities affecting the EU's financial interests:

- **Internal investigations:** Investigating fraud, corruption, and serious misconduct within EU institutions, bodies, offices, and agencies.
- **External investigations:** Investigating fraud by economic operators (e.g., fraudulent claims for EU structural funds, agricultural subsidies, or customs duty evasion).
- **Coordination:** Assisting Member State authorities in coordinating cross-border fraud investigations.

OLAF has no power to prosecute -- it transmits its findings and recommendations to national prosecution authorities or the EPPO.

### European Public Prosecutor's Office (EPPO)

The EPPO, established by Council Regulation (EU) 2017/1939 under enhanced cooperation (currently 24 participating Member States), represents a paradigm shift in EU criminal enforcement:

- **Competence:** Exclusive competence for investigating and prosecuting criminal offenses affecting the EU's financial interests (PIF offenses) and inextricably linked offenses.
- **Structure:** A central office in Luxembourg and European Delegated Prosecutors (EDPs) in each participating Member State, operating within national judicial systems.
- **Cross-border powers:** The EPPO can conduct cross-border investigations without the need for mutual legal assistance requests, dramatically expediting transnational fraud investigations.
- **Prosecutorial authority:** The EPPO brings cases before national courts, applying national procedural law but operating under a unified European mandate.

Since becoming operational in June 2021, the EPPO has opened thousands of investigations and recovered significant sums. Notable areas of activity include VAT carousel fraud, structural fund fraud, and agricultural subsidy fraud.

### EU Procurement Directives

**Directive 2014/24/EU (Public Procurement):** Article 57 establishes mandatory and discretionary grounds for excluding economic operators from procurement procedures:

**Mandatory exclusion (Art. 57(1)):** Contracting authorities must exclude operators convicted of:
- Participation in a criminal organization (Framework Decision 2008/841/JHA)
- Corruption (Convention on the fight against corruption involving officials of the European Communities)
- Fraud (Convention on the protection of the European Communities' financial interests)
- Terrorist offenses
- Money laundering or terrorist financing
- Child labor and trafficking

**Discretionary exclusion (Art. 57(4)):** May exclude operators that have violated environmental, social, or labor law obligations; are bankrupt; have committed professional misconduct; have entered into agreements to distort competition; or have shown significant deficiencies in performing prior contracts.

**Self-cleaning (Art. 57(6)):** Operators may demonstrate that measures taken are sufficient to establish reliability despite the existence of exclusion grounds.

### ECHR Dimensions

**Article 6 (Fair Trial):** The ECHR's fair trial protections apply to criminal fraud prosecutions. In Salabiaku v. France, App. No. 10519/83 (1988), the Court held that presumptions of fact or law in criminal cases must be confined within reasonable limits and must not entirely shift the burden of proof to the defendant.

**Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (Protection of Property):** Government recovery of fraudulently obtained funds engages property protections. In Agosi v. United Kingdom, App. No. 9118/80 (1986), the Court established that confiscation of property used in fraud must be proportionate.

**Article 7 (No Punishment without Law):** The principle of legality requires that fraud offenses be defined with sufficient precision. The ECHR has examined whether expansive interpretations of fraud statutes violate the foreseeability requirement.

## Comparative Analysis

### Qui Tam: The American Exceptionalism

The FCA's qui tam mechanism is the most distinctive feature of U.S. government fraud law and has no true equivalent in other jurisdictions:

| Jurisdiction | Private Enforcement Mechanism | Private Financial Incentive |
|-------------|------------------------------|---------------------------|
| **United States** | Qui tam (FCA): private relator files suit on behalf of government | 15-30% of recovery (billions in total awards) |
| **South Korea** | ACRC whistleblower rewards (administrative, not litigation-based) | Up to 3 billion KRW per report |
| **Germany** | No private enforcement mechanism for government fraud | None |
| **EU** | No EU-level qui tam; EPPO has exclusive prosecution authority | None at EU level |

The qui tam system's effectiveness is demonstrated by the data: qui tam actions account for over 70% of all FCA recoveries. However, the system has been criticized for generating marginal or parasitic lawsuits and imposing substantial defense costs on innocent parties.

### Scienter Standards

The knowledge requirement for government fraud liability varies significantly:

- **United States (FCA):** "Knows or should know" -- including deliberate ignorance and reckless disregard. No specific intent to defraud required. This is the lowest scienter standard among the four jurisdictions.
- **Germany (§ 263):** Full intent (Vorsatz) required for standard fraud; recklessness (Leichtfertigkeit) sufficient only for subsidy fraud (§ 264(5)).
- **South Korea:** Criminal intent required for fraud (Article 347); negligence may support administrative sanctions.
- **EU (PIF Directive):** Intentional commission required; Member States may add negligent offenses for certain categories.

The FCA's expansive scienter standard is its most powerful enforcement feature, as it captures not only deliberate fraud but also willful blindness and reckless billing practices -- a standard that would not meet the intent requirements of German or Korean criminal law.

### Penalty Structures

| Jurisdiction | Maximum Criminal Penalty | Maximum Civil/Administrative Penalty | Treble/Multiple Damages |
|-------------|------------------------|-------------------------------------|------------------------|
| **United States** | 20 years (mail/wire fraud); 10 years (AKS) | Treble damages + $13,946-$27,894 per claim | Yes -- 3x damages |
| **South Korea** | Life imprisonment (large-scale fraud) | Debarment; contract cancellation | No |
| **Germany** | 10 years (aggravated fraud) | Debarment (GWB §§ 123-124) | No |
| **EU** | 4 years minimum max (PIF Directive, serious cases) | OLAF recommendations; procurement exclusion | No |

The U.S. treble damages provision, combined with per-claim penalties that can multiply astronomically in cases involving thousands of individual claims (e.g., healthcare billing fraud), creates by far the most financially punitive regime.

### Procurement Fraud and Debarment

All four jurisdictions employ debarment/exclusion from government contracts as a sanction for procurement fraud, though the frameworks differ:

- **EU:** Most harmonized approach through Directive 2014/24/EU's mandatory and discretionary exclusion grounds, with self-cleaning provisions.
- **Germany:** Transposes EU framework through GWB §§ 123-125.
- **United States:** FAR Subpart 9.4 provides both suspension (temporary) and debarment (up to 3 years), with a government-wide effect.
- **South Korea:** Administrative debarment of 1 month to 2 years administered by the PPS.
