## Summary

Human trafficking — the recruitment, transportation, or harboring of persons through force, fraud, or coercion for the purpose of exploitation — is among the most severe transnational crimes addressed by modern legal systems. The international framework rests upon the Protocol to Prevent, Suppress and Punish Trafficking in Persons, Especially Women and Children (the "Palermo Protocol"), adopted in 2000 as a supplement to the United Nations Convention against Transnational Organized Crime (UNTOC) and entering into force on 25 December 2003. Article 3(a) of the Palermo Protocol defines trafficking through three constituent elements: the act (recruitment, transportation, transfer, harbouring, or receipt of persons), the means (threat or use of force, coercion, abduction, fraud, deception, abuse of power, or abuse of a position of vulnerability), and the purpose (exploitation, including sexual exploitation, forced labour, slavery, servitude, or organ removal). This internationally recognized definition has shaped domestic legislation worldwide, though national implementations diverge considerably in scope, penalties, victim protections, and enforcement mechanisms. South Korea enacted its landmark Act on Prevention of Human Trafficking and Protection of and Support for Victims (인신매매등방지 및 피해자보호 등에 관한 법률) effective 1 January 2023, bringing its legal framework into closer alignment with the Palermo Protocol. The United States addresses trafficking primarily through the Trafficking Victims Protection Act (TVPA) of 2000 and its successive reauthorizations, establishing a global monitoring framework through the annual Trafficking in Persons (TIP) Report. Germany criminalizes trafficking under Sections 232 through 233a of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), while the European Union established a harmonized minimum standard through Directive 2011/36/EU, recently amended by Directive (EU) 2024/1712.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's approach to human trafficking underwent a fundamental transformation with the enactment of the Act on Prevention of Human Trafficking and Protection of and Support for Victims (인신매매등방지 및 피해자보호 등에 관한 법률, commonly referred to as the 인신매매방지법), which came into force on 1 January 2023. Prior to this legislation, trafficking offenses were addressed through a patchwork of provisions scattered across the Criminal Act, the Act on the Punishment of Intermediating in the Sex Trade, and immigration statutes — an arrangement that left significant gaps in coverage and victim protection.

**Definition of Trafficking:** Under the new act, human trafficking is defined as the recruitment, transportation, transfer, harboring, or receipt of persons by means of coercion, fraud, or other improper methods for the purpose of exploitation, including prostitution, sexual exploitation, forced labor, organ harvesting, and other forms of servitude. This definition closely mirrors the Palermo Protocol's three-element structure.

**Scope of Protection:** Both foreign nationals in Korea and Korean citizens overseas fall within the law's protective ambit. This extraterritorial dimension addresses the transnational nature of trafficking networks that exploit migrant workers in sectors such as fishing, agriculture, and manufacturing.

| Feature | Provision | Detail |
|---------|-----------|--------|
| Victim rescue rights | Art. 10 | Right to rescue, emergency shelter, and safety |
| Interpretation services | Art. 14 | Right to interpretation during legal proceedings |
| Compensation and support | Art. 16 | Financial support for medical expenses and rehabilitation |
| Non-punishment of sex trafficking victims | Art. 21 | Victims of sex trafficking shall not be criminally punished |
| Comprehensive Plan | Art. 5 | Five-year national strategy (2023-2027) |

**Institutional Framework:** The Ministry of Gender Equality and Family oversees implementation, and the government adopted the first Comprehensive Plan for Prevention of Human Trafficking (2023-2027), setting out a five-year strategy encompassing prevention, protection, and prosecution. In January 2023, an expert seminar was held at the Police Human Resources Development Institute to establish operational protocols for law enforcement.

### Key Developments

The 2023 act represented a significant legislative milestone that contributed to South Korea's improved standing in international assessments. Prior to this law, Korea had been criticized for lacking a comprehensive, standalone anti-trafficking statute. The new framework addresses forced labor in the fishing industry, sexual exploitation, and digital sex crimes — areas where enforcement had previously been fragmented.

**TIP Report Status:** South Korea was placed on Tier 1 in the 2025 TIP Report, indicating that the government fully meets the minimum standards for the elimination of trafficking. The government increased trafficking investigations, prosecuted more suspects, convicted more traffickers, updated the victim identification index to include labor trafficking indicators, and established a specialized fund for trafficking victims. However, concerns remain regarding the low number of labor trafficking prosecutions involving migrant workers, particularly in the distant-water fishing sector.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States addresses human trafficking primarily through the Trafficking Victims Protection Act (TVPA) of 2000, codified at 22 U.S.C. Chapter 78, which established trafficking in persons as a federal crime and created the institutional architecture for domestic and international anti-trafficking efforts.

**Criminal Provisions:** The TVPA introduced new federal offenses prohibiting:

- **Forced labor** (18 U.S.C. § 1589): Obtaining labor or services through threats of serious harm, physical restraint, or abuse of legal process. Penalties of up to 20 years imprisonment, or life imprisonment if the offense involves kidnapping, sexual abuse, or death.
- **Trafficking with respect to peonage, slavery, or involuntary servitude** (18 U.S.C. § 1590): Knowingly recruiting, harboring, transporting, or obtaining persons for labor or services in violation of forced labor statutes. Penalties of up to 20 years imprisonment.
- **Sex trafficking of children or by force, fraud, or coercion** (18 U.S.C. § 1591): Recruiting, enticing, or maintaining a person for a commercial sex act through force, fraud, or coercion, or involving a minor under 18. Penalties of 15 years to life imprisonment for offenses involving minors under 14, and up to 20 years for other violations.

| Offense | Statute | Maximum Penalty |
|---------|---------|-----------------|
| Forced labor | 18 U.S.C. § 1589 | 20 years (life if death/kidnapping) |
| Trafficking for peonage/slavery | 18 U.S.C. § 1590 | 20 years |
| Sex trafficking by force/fraud/coercion | 18 U.S.C. § 1591 | 20 years (life if victim under 14) |
| Document servitude | 18 U.S.C. § 1592 | 5 years |
| Benefiting from trafficking | 18 U.S.C. § 1593A | Same as underlying offense |

**Victim Protections:** The TVPA mandates that traffickers pay restitution to victims and provides immigration relief through the T Visa, which grants temporary legal status to trafficking victims who assist law enforcement. Victims may also access federal benefits and services regardless of immigration status.

### Reauthorizations

The TVPA has been reauthorized and expanded through successive legislation:

- **TVPRA 2003:** Established the right of trafficking victims to bring civil actions against their traffickers and created the Senior Policy Operating Group.
- **TVPRA 2005:** Extended protections to U.S. citizens and lawful permanent residents trafficked within the United States.
- **TVPRA 2008:** Strengthened penalties, expanded the definition of trafficking, and enhanced protections for unaccompanied minors.
- **TVPRA 2013:** Improved data collection, enhanced the TIP Report methodology, and strengthened supply chain transparency requirements.
- **TVPRA 2017/2018:** Updated grant programs, expanded training requirements for federal agencies, and strengthened accountability measures.
- **TVPRA 2022:** Enacted as Pub. L. 117-348 in January 2023, further refining victim services, international cooperation frameworks, and criminal enforcement mechanisms.

### TIP Report and Global Monitoring

The TVPA established the Office to Monitor and Combat Trafficking in Persons within the U.S. Department of State, which publishes the annual Trafficking in Persons (TIP) Report — the world's most comprehensive assessment of government anti-trafficking efforts. The report ranks countries into tiers:

| Tier | Standard | Consequence |
|------|----------|-------------|
| Tier 1 | Fully meets TVPA minimum standards | No sanctions |
| Tier 2 | Does not fully meet standards but making significant efforts | Monitoring |
| Tier 2 Watch List | Significant efforts but severe/increasing trafficking, failure to show progress | Potential downgrade |
| Tier 3 | Does not fully meet standards and not making significant efforts | Subject to sanctions and aid restrictions |

The United States evaluates its own anti-trafficking efforts in the TIP Report and has maintained a Tier 1 ranking, though it has faced criticism for potential conflicts of interest in self-assessment.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany criminalizes human trafficking under Sections 232 through 233a of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB, Criminal Code). The framework was significantly reformed in 2016 to implement EU Directive 2011/36/EU, restructuring the trafficking provisions into a more differentiated system that addresses the full spectrum of exploitative conduct.

**Section 232 — Human Trafficking (Menschenhandel):** This section penalizes preparatory acts including solicitation, recruitment, transportation, transfer, harboring, or receipt of persons for the purpose of exploitation. It addresses the recruitment phase before actual exploitation commences and covers both sexual exploitation and labor trafficking. The penalty ranges from six months to five years' imprisonment.

**Section 232a — Forced Prostitution (Zwangsprostitution):** Criminalizes inducing or forcing another person to engage in prostitution. The penalty is six months to ten years' imprisonment.

**Section 232b — Forced Labor (Zwangsarbeit):** Criminalizes inducing or forcing another person into labor exploitation, slavery-like conditions, or debt bondage. The penalty mirrors that of Section 232a at six months to ten years.

**Section 233 — Exploitation of Labor (Ausbeutung der Arbeitskraft):** Punishes employers who recognize and exploit the vulnerable situation of individuals by employing them under exploitative conditions, including wages significantly below market rate, excessive working hours, or dangerous working conditions. The penalty is up to three years' imprisonment or a fine.

**Section 233a — Exploitation Involving Deprivation of Liberty (Ausbeutung unter Ausnutzung einer Freiheitsberaubung):** Addresses the most severe form of exploitation involving unlawful imprisonment. The penalty is one to ten years' imprisonment, or three months to five years in less serious cases.

| Section | Offense | Penalty Range |
|---------|---------|---------------|
| § 232 | Human trafficking (recruitment/transport) | 6 months – 5 years |
| § 232a | Forced prostitution | 6 months – 10 years |
| § 232b | Forced labor | 6 months – 10 years |
| § 233 | Exploitation of labor | Up to 3 years or fine |
| § 233a | Exploitation with deprivation of liberty | 1 – 10 years |

### Enforcement and Challenges

**TIP Report Status:** Germany has maintained a Tier 1 ranking in the TIP Report, most recently confirmed in the 2024 report. The government fully meets minimum standards through increased investigations, enhanced victim assistance funding, and improved identification of labor trafficking victims. However, the report highlights a persistent sentencing problem: 63 percent of convicted traffickers received fully suspended sentences, fines, or less than one year of imprisonment — outcomes widely criticized as insufficiently deterrent.

**Federal Structure Challenges:** Germany's federal system creates coordination difficulties, as policing is primarily a state (Laender) competency. Anti-trafficking efforts vary considerably across the 16 federal states, with some maintaining specialized units and victim support structures while others rely on general criminal investigation departments.

**Victim Protection:** Germany provides a minimum 30-day reflection period for identified trafficking victims, during which they may access shelter, medical care, and legal advice regardless of immigration status. Victims who cooperate with law enforcement may receive a temporary residence permit under Section 25(4a) of the Residence Act (Aufenthaltsgesetz).

## European Framework

### EU Directive 2011/36/EU

The European Union established its comprehensive anti-trafficking framework through Directive 2011/36/EU of the European Parliament and of the Council of 5 April 2011 on preventing and combating trafficking in human beings and protecting its victims, replacing the earlier Council Framework Decision 2002/629/JHA.

**Criminal Offenses and Sanctions:** The Directive requires all EU Member States to establish maximum penalties of at least five years' imprisonment for trafficking offenses, increased to at least ten years where aggravating circumstances are present. Aggravating circumstances include offenses committed against particularly vulnerable victims (including children), offenses committed within the framework of a criminal organization, offenses that deliberately or by gross negligence endangered the life of the victim, and offenses committed by the use of serious violence.

**Victim Protection Standards:** The Directive mandates that Member States provide:

- Immediate access to assistance and support, including accommodation, material assistance, medical treatment, interpretation and translation services, counseling, and legal representation
- A presumption of minority when the age of the victim is uncertain but there are reasons to believe the person is a child
- Individual risk assessments to determine appropriate protection measures
- Protection during criminal investigations and proceedings, including the possibility of testimony without direct confrontation with the defendant

**Non-prosecution Principle:** Article 8 establishes that Member States shall take measures to ensure that competent national authorities are entitled not to prosecute or impose penalties on victims of trafficking for their involvement in criminal activities that they were compelled to commit.

**Investigation Independence:** Article 9 provides that investigation and prosecution of trafficking offenses shall not depend on the victim's report or accusation, and criminal proceedings may continue even if the victim withdraws their statement.

### Amendment: Directive (EU) 2024/1712

Directive (EU) 2024/1712, which entered into force on 14 July 2024, amends the original 2011 Directive to expand its scope. Key changes include:

- Expansion of forms of exploitation to explicitly include forced marriage, illegal adoption, and exploitation of surrogacy
- Strengthened provisions on the use of online platforms for recruitment
- Enhanced obligations for Member States regarding demand reduction
- Member States must transpose the amendments into national law by 15 July 2026

### EU Anti-Trafficking Coordinator

The EU Anti-Trafficking Coordinator ensures strategic coherence across EU institutions, Member States, and external partners. The Coordinator works with national anti-trafficking coordinators or equivalent mechanisms, independent monitoring bodies, EU agencies (including Europol, Eurojust, and the European Border and Coast Guard Agency), and relevant civil society organizations.

## Comparative Analysis

### Definitional Comparison

| Element | Palermo Protocol | South Korea (2023 Act) | United States (TVPA) | Germany (StGB) | EU Directive 2011/36/EU |
|---------|------------------|------------------------|---------------------|----------------|------------------------|
| Act | Recruitment, transport, transfer, harboring, receipt | Recruitment, transport, transfer, harboring, receipt | Recruiting, harboring, transporting, providing, obtaining | Recruitment, transport, transfer, harboring, receipt | Recruitment, transport, transfer, harboring, receipt |
| Means | Force, coercion, fraud, deception, abuse of power/vulnerability | Coercion, fraud, improper methods | Force, fraud, coercion | Force, threats, deception, abuse of vulnerability | Threat/use of force, coercion, fraud, deception, abuse of power/vulnerability |
| Purpose | Sexual exploitation, forced labor, slavery, servitude, organ removal | Sexual exploitation, forced labor, organ harvesting, servitude | Forced labor, involuntary servitude, sex trafficking | Sexual exploitation, forced labor, slavery, debt bondage | Sexual exploitation, forced labor, slavery, organ removal, forced begging, criminal activities |
| Consent relevance | Irrelevant when means established | Irrelevant when means established | Irrelevant for minors in sex trafficking; irrelevant when force/fraud/coercion present | Irrelevant when exploitation of vulnerability established | Irrelevant when any means established |
