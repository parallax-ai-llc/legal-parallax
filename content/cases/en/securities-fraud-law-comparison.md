## Summary

Securities fraud -- the deliberate misrepresentation, omission, or manipulation of information in connection with the purchase, sale, or trading of securities -- is a cornerstone offense in financial criminal law across all major jurisdictions, yet its statutory definitions, enforcement architectures, and penalty structures differ markedly. South Korea addresses securities fraud primarily through the Financial Investment Services and Capital Markets Act (FSCMA), which prohibits unfair trading practices including market manipulation, fraudulent transactions, and dissemination of false information, with criminal penalties reaching life imprisonment for large-scale offenses and recent 2025 amendments introducing penalty surcharges of up to twice the illicit gains and non-monetary sanctions such as account freezes and executive appointment bans. The United States prosecutes securities fraud under multiple overlapping statutes -- Section 10(b) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, SEC Rule 10b-5, the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 (18 U.S.C. Section 1348), and the Dodd-Frank Act of 2010 -- with criminal penalties of up to 25 years imprisonment and individual fines of up to $5 million, though the Supreme Court's 2024 ruling in SEC v. Jarkesy fundamentally reshaped enforcement by requiring jury trials for SEC civil penalty actions. Germany prosecutes securities fraud through the Securities Trading Act (Wertpapierhandelsgesetz, WpHG) implementing the EU Market Abuse Regulation (MAR), with criminal penalties of up to five years imprisonment for market manipulation and insider trading, administrative fines reaching 15 percent of annual corporate revenues, and the Wirecard scandal having triggered a comprehensive overhaul of the supervisory framework through the Financial Market Integrity Strengthening Act (FISG). At the European level, the Market Abuse Regulation (Regulation 596/2014) and the Criminal Sanctions Directive (Directive 2014/57/EU) establish a harmonized enforcement framework, while the European Court of Human Rights has profoundly influenced securities enforcement through landmark rulings on the ne bis in idem principle, fair trial guarantees, and the classification of administrative sanctions as criminal in nature.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea regulates securities fraud through the Financial Investment Services and Capital Markets Act (FSCMA), enacted in 2009 to consolidate and modernize the country's fragmented securities regulatory framework. The FSCMA addresses securities fraud through several key provisions:

- **Article 176 (Market Manipulation):** Prohibits schemes to artificially fix, stabilize, or manipulate the market price of listed securities through wash sales, matched orders, or other deceptive trading patterns.
- **Article 178 (Unfair Trading):** A broad anti-fraud provision prohibiting the use of deceptive schemes, dissemination of false or misleading information, and the use of any fraudulent device in connection with securities transactions. This article serves as South Korea's functional equivalent of SEC Rule 10b-5.
- **Article 179 (Civil Liability):** Provides a private right of action for investors who suffer damages as a result of unfair trading practices.

The Financial Services Commission (FSC) serves as the primary policymaking body, while the Financial Supervisory Service (FSS) carries out front-line surveillance and enforcement. The Korea Exchange also conducts market surveillance and refers suspected violations to the FSS.

### Criminal Penalties

Under Article 443 of the FSCMA, as substantially strengthened by June 2023 amendments:

- Illicit profits of **5 billion won or more**: imprisonment for life or a fixed term of not less than 5 years.
- Illicit profits of **500 million won or more**: imprisonment of not less than 3 years.
- Standard violations: imprisonment of at least 1 year.
- Fines: 3 to 5 times the amount of illicit profits obtained.

Article 444 provides supplementary criminal penalties, including fines for corporate entities whose officers or employees commit securities fraud. The Specific Economic Crimes Aggravated Punishment Act may also apply to large-scale fraud offenses, further escalating potential sentences.

### Non-Monetary Sanctions (2025 Amendments)

The revised FSCMA and its Enforcement Decree, effective April 23, 2025, introduced significant new enforcement mechanisms beyond traditional criminal prosecution:

- **Penalty surcharge system:** Administrative penalties of up to twice the amount of illegally earned profits, with a maximum of KRW 4 billion when profits cannot be calculated.
- **Account freeze orders:** The FSC may restrict rule-breakers from engaging in transactions of financial investment products.
- **Executive appointment bans:** Convicted persons may be barred from being appointed or serving as executive officers at listed companies for up to five years.
- **Mandatory disposal orders:** If a rule-breaker subject to transaction restrictions acquires a financial investment product in violation of the order, the FSC may order disposal within six months.

### Landmark Cases

**Stock Manipulation CFD Scheme (2023-2025):** In South Korea's largest stock manipulation prosecution, 25 individuals were convicted for operating a scheme worth approximately 730 billion won ($500 million) using contracts for difference (CFDs) to artificially inflate stock prices of multiple listed companies. The scheme's mastermind, Ra Deok-yeon, received a sentence of 25 years imprisonment. The case prompted accelerated legislative reform and the introduction of the 2025 penalty surcharge system.

**SPC Group Unfair Trading (2021):** SPC Group was fined a combined 64.7 billion won ($54 million) for unfair trading between affiliates designed to facilitate a leadership succession. The group's affiliates, including Paris Croissant, SPC Samlip, and BR Korea, engaged in inter-affiliate transactions worth 41.4 billion won over seven years (2011-2018) to inflate the value of SPC Samlip shares held by the chairman's sons. Chairman Hur was found to have orchestrated the scheme through weekly executive meetings.

**Samsung BioLogics Accounting Fraud (2018-2020):** Samsung BioLogics was accused by the Securities and Futures Commission of inflating its value by approximately 4.5 trillion won through fraudulent accounting of its stake in Samsung Bioepis ahead of its 2016 IPO. The case raised questions about the adequacy of FSS oversight of major conglomerates (chaebol) and prompted calls for enhanced auditor independence requirements.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States prosecutes securities fraud under a layered statutory framework developed over nine decades:

- **Securities Act of 1933, Section 17(a):** Prohibits fraud in the offer or sale of securities. Unlike Rule 10b-5, Section 17(a) does not require a purchase or sale by the plaintiff and applies only to sellers.
- **Securities Exchange Act of 1934, Section 10(b) and Rule 10b-5:** The broadest federal anti-fraud provision, prohibiting any manipulative or deceptive device in connection with the purchase or sale of a security. Rule 10b-5 requires: (1) a material misrepresentation or omission; (2) scienter (intent to deceive); (3) in connection with the purchase or sale of a security; (4) reliance; (5) economic loss; and (6) loss causation.
- **Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 (18 U.S.C. Section 1348):** Enacted in response to the Enron and WorldCom scandals, this provision broadly criminalizes any scheme to defraud in connection with securities of publicly traded companies, carrying penalties of up to 25 years imprisonment.
- **Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform Act of 2010:** Enhanced SEC enforcement authority, established the Office of the Whistleblower, and provided new tools for addressing systemic risk.

### Criminal Penalties

- **18 U.S.C. Section 1348 (SOX securities fraud):** Up to 25 years imprisonment.
- **Securities Exchange Act Section 32(a):** Up to 20 years imprisonment and fines of up to $5 million for individuals ($25 million for entities).
- **Securities Act Section 24:** Up to 5 years imprisonment.
- **Wire fraud (18 U.S.C. Section 1343):** Frequently charged alongside securities fraud; up to 20 years imprisonment.
- **Mail fraud (18 U.S.C. Section 1341):** Up to 20 years imprisonment.

### Civil and Administrative Penalties

The SEC pursues civil enforcement through:

- **Civil monetary penalties:** Up to $725,000 per violation for individuals, even absent investor losses. Tiered penalties escalate based on the severity of misconduct.
- **Disgorgement:** Ill-gotten gains must be returned, subject to a five-year limitations period as established by the Supreme Court in Liu v. SEC (2020) and Kokesh v. SEC (2017).
- **Injunctions and bars:** The SEC may obtain court orders barring individuals from serving as officers or directors of public companies, or from associating with broker-dealers or investment advisers.

### SEC v. Jarkesy (2024): A Landmark Shift

In Securities and Exchange Commission v. Jarkesy, 603 U.S. ___ (2024), the Supreme Court held 6-3 that the Seventh Amendment requires a jury trial when the SEC seeks civil penalties for securities fraud. The ruling effectively ended the SEC's decades-long practice of using in-house administrative law judges (ALJs) to adjudicate fraud cases and impose civil penalties. George Jarkesy and his firm Patriot28 had been accused of defrauding over 100 investors in two hedge funds holding around $24 million. The SEC had imposed a $300,000 civil penalty and ordered $685,000 in disgorgement through an administrative proceeding. Chief Justice Roberts, writing for the majority, held that securities fraud actions seeking penalties are suits at common law triggering the right to a jury trial. The decision has profound implications for SEC enforcement efficiency and resource allocation.

### Landmark Cases

**SEC v. Enron (2001-2006):** The collapse of Enron Corporation due to systematic accounting fraud, off-balance-sheet entities, and misrepresentation of financial condition resulted in over $60 billion in shareholder losses. CEO Jeffrey Skilling was sentenced to 24 years imprisonment (later reduced to 14 years). CFO Andrew Fastow received 6 years. Chairman Kenneth Lay was convicted but died before sentencing. The scandal led directly to the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002.

**United States v. Madoff (2009):** Bernard Madoff operated the largest known Ponzi scheme in history, defrauding thousands of investors of approximately $65 billion in stated account values (approximately $17.5 billion in actual principal losses). Madoff was sentenced to 150 years imprisonment. The case exposed significant failures in SEC oversight and examination procedures.

**SEC v. WorldCom (2002-2005):** WorldCom's $11 billion accounting fraud, involving the capitalization of operating expenses and inflation of revenues, led to the largest bankruptcy filing in U.S. history at the time. CEO Bernard Ebbers was sentenced to 25 years imprisonment. CFO Scott Sullivan received 5 years after cooperating with prosecutors.

**SEC v. Goldman Sachs -- Abacus CDO (2010):** Goldman Sachs settled SEC charges for $550 million over its marketing of a synthetic collateralized debt obligation (CDO) called Abacus 2007-AC1, in which Goldman failed to disclose that hedge fund Paulson & Co. had selected the portfolio's mortgage-backed securities and was betting against it.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's securities fraud regime operates within the dual framework of EU law and national legislation:

- **Securities Trading Act (WpHG -- Wertpapierhandelsgesetz):** The primary national statute implementing the EU Market Abuse Regulation. Sections 119 and 120 of the WpHG establish criminal and administrative sanctions for violations of MAR provisions on insider dealing (Article 14 MAR) and market manipulation (Article 15 MAR).
- **German Criminal Code (StGB), Sections 263-264a:** Section 263 (Betrug, fraud) and Section 264a (Kapitalanlagebetrug, investment fraud) provide general criminal fraud provisions applicable to securities. Section 264a specifically targets the dissemination of incorrect favorable statements in connection with the sale of securities, carrying penalties of up to three years imprisonment.
- **Stock Corporation Act (AktG):** Imposes disclosure obligations and liability on corporate officers and boards.
- **Financial Market Integrity Strengthening Act (FISG, 2021):** Enacted in response to the Wirecard scandal, this legislation significantly expanded BaFin's investigative powers, including broader rights of access to information, enhanced search and seizure authority, and the ability to directly question company executives and auditors.

### Criminal Penalties

- **Market manipulation (WpHG Section 119):** Intentional manipulation that influences the exchange or market price of a financial instrument is punishable by imprisonment of up to five years or a fine. In particularly serious cases, the sentence may be increased to up to ten years.
- **Insider dealing (WpHG Section 119):** Intentional insider trading is punishable by imprisonment of up to five years or a fine.
- **Investment fraud (StGB Section 264a):** Imprisonment of up to three years or a fine for making incorrect favorable statements to the public in connection with securities offerings.
- **General fraud (StGB Section 263):** Imprisonment of up to five years, or up to ten years in particularly serious cases.

### Administrative Fines

BaFin may impose administrative fines under the WpHG as follows:

- **Legal persons:** Up to EUR 15 million or 15 percent of total annual revenues, whichever is higher, for violations of MAR provisions.
- **Natural persons:** Up to EUR 5 million for MAR-related violations; up to EUR 2 million for other WpHG violations.
- Under the German Regulatory Offences Act (OWiG), fines on companies can reach up to EUR 10 million or, in certain cases under the WpHG, 15 percent of annual revenues.

BaFin initiated 195 administrative offence proceedings in 2024 alone, reflecting an increasingly active enforcement posture.

### Landmark Cases

**Wirecard Scandal (2020-present):** The collapse of payment processor Wirecard AG in June 2020 -- after the company admitted that EUR 1.9 billion in purported trust account balances did not exist -- represents Germany's largest securities fraud case. Former CEO Markus Braun was arrested and charged in March 2022 with fraud, breach of trust, and accounting manipulation, facing up to 15 years imprisonment. The trial commenced in Munich in December 2022. Prosecutors allege that Wirecard executives systematically inflated earnings starting at least from 2015 by fabricating revenue streams from fictitious third-party acquirer (TPA) businesses in Dubai, the Philippines, and Singapore. Former COO Jan Marsalek fled Germany and remains a fugitive. The scandal exposed critical failures in BaFin's supervisory approach -- BaFin had only supervised Wirecard's subsidiary bank, not its core payment processing business -- and prompted the passage of the FISG and a comprehensive restructuring of BaFin.

**Porsche-Volkswagen Market Manipulation (2008-2012):** During the 2008 financial crisis, Porsche SE secretly accumulated options on Volkswagen shares, ultimately disclosing that it effectively controlled 74.1 percent of VW's voting shares. The disclosure triggered a massive short squeeze that briefly made Volkswagen the world's most valuable company by market capitalization. Stuttgart prosecutors investigated two former Porsche board members for market manipulation. Civil suits were filed by hedge funds alleging losses of billions of euros. The case raised fundamental questions about disclosure obligations and the line between legitimate corporate strategy and market manipulation.

**NBSH Acquisition LLC Fine (2023):** BaFin imposed a EUR 1.6 million fine on NBSH Acquisition LLC for failing to comply with voting rights notification requirements under the WpHG, illustrating BaFin's enforcement of disclosure obligations.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Legal Framework

The EU's securities fraud regime rests on two complementary instruments adopted in 2014 and fully applicable since July 2016:

- **Market Abuse Regulation (MAR -- Regulation 596/2014):** A directly applicable regulation that prohibits insider dealing (Article 14), market manipulation (Article 15), and unlawful disclosure of inside information (Article 10) across all EU Member States. MAR replaced the original Market Abuse Directive (2003/6/EC) and provides a unified framework for administrative enforcement, including harmonized investigative powers and minimum sanctioning levels for national competent authorities.
- **Criminal Sanctions for Market Abuse Directive (MAD II -- Directive 2014/57/EU):** Requires all Member States to ensure that insider dealing, market manipulation, and unlawful disclosure of inside information constitute criminal offenses at least in serious cases committed intentionally. MAD II establishes minimum maximum penalties:
  - **Insider dealing and market manipulation:** At least 4 years maximum imprisonment.
  - **Unlawful disclosure of inside information:** At least 2 years maximum imprisonment.
  - **Legal persons:** Effective, proportionate, and dissuasive criminal or non-criminal fines.

### Administrative Sanctions under MAR

MAR establishes minimum harmonized sanctions that national competent authorities must have available:

- **Natural persons:** Maximum administrative fine of at least EUR 5 million.
- **Legal persons (insider dealing/market manipulation):** Maximum fine of at least EUR 15 million or 15 percent of total annual turnover.
- **Legal persons (unlawful disclosure):** Maximum fine of at least EUR 2.5 million or 2 percent of total annual turnover.
- **Disgorgement:** Authorities must be able to require the disgorgement of profits gained or losses avoided.
- **Additional measures:** Public statements identifying the person and the violation, temporary or permanent bans on management functions, and suspension of trading.

Member States may set higher penalties than the MAR minimums. ESMA publishes annual reports on the application of sanctions across Member States, revealing significant disparities in enforcement intensity.

### ECHR Framework and Fundamental Rights

The European Convention on Human Rights constrains the enforcement of securities fraud laws across all 46 Council of Europe member states (which include all EU members). Key principles:

- **Article 6 (Right to a Fair Trial):** Securities fraud proceedings, whether classified as criminal or administrative under national law, must comply with fair trial guarantees if the sanctions are "criminal" in nature under the autonomous ECHR criteria established in Engel v. Netherlands (1976): (1) the domestic classification; (2) the nature of the offense; and (3) the severity of the penalty.
- **Article 7 (No Punishment without Law):** Securities fraud offenses must be defined with sufficient precision and foreseeability.
- **Article 1, Protocol 1 (Protection of Property):** Pecuniary sanctions must be proportionate.
- **Article 4, Protocol 7 (Ne Bis in Idem):** Prohibits double jeopardy -- being tried or punished twice for the same offense.

### Landmark Cases

**Grande Stevens and Others v. Italy (ECtHR, 2014):** The foundational ECHR decision on securities fraud enforcement. The applicants -- including lawyer Franzo Grande Stevens and companies associated with the Agnelli family -- were sanctioned by Italy's market regulator (Consob) for market manipulation relating to a 2005 equity swap involving Fiat shares. Subsequently, parallel criminal proceedings were initiated for the same conduct. The ECtHR held:
- That Consob's administrative sanctions were "criminal" in nature under the Engel criteria, given their severity (fines of up to EUR 5 million and temporary market bans).
- That Article 6 ECHR was violated because the Consob proceedings lacked independence and impartiality, as the investigation and adjudication functions were not sufficiently separated.
- That Article 4 of Protocol 7 was violated by the cumulation of criminal and administrative proceedings for the same facts, establishing that the ne bis in idem principle applies to dual-track market abuse enforcement.

The decision forced multiple EU Member States to restructure their market abuse enforcement procedures to avoid dual prosecution.

**Spector Photo Group v. CBFA (CJEU, C-45/08, 2009):** The Court of Justice of the European Union held that a presumption of insider dealing arising from the mere possession of inside information at the time of a transaction is compatible with EU law, provided the rights of the defense are respected and the presumption is rebuttable. This decision significantly lowered the enforcement threshold across the EU.

**A and B v. Norway (ECtHR, 2016):** The Grand Chamber partially retreated from Grande Stevens by holding that parallel administrative and criminal proceedings for the same facts do not necessarily violate the ne bis in idem principle, provided the proceedings are sufficiently connected in substance and time and form an integrated scheme of sanctions. This ruling gave Member States more flexibility in maintaining dual-track enforcement, though it requires demonstrating a "sufficiently close connection" between proceedings.

**Garlsson Real Estate SA v. Consob (CJEU, C-537/16, 2018):** The CJEU held that the principle of ne bis in idem in Article 50 of the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights precludes national legislation permitting proceedings for an administrative fine of a criminal nature to be brought against a person for unlawful conduct for which they have already been finally convicted or acquitted, unless the restriction is proportionate and strictly necessary.

## Comparative Analysis

### Definitional Approaches

The four jurisdictions define securities fraud with varying degrees of specificity. South Korea's FSCMA employs a relatively broad prohibition in Article 178, capturing fraudulent schemes, false information, and deceptive devices without a single comprehensive definition. The United States similarly lacks a statutory definition of securities fraud, relying instead on the judicially developed elements of Rule 10b-5 and the broad language of 18 U.S.C. Section 1348. Germany implements the EU-defined categories of insider dealing, market manipulation, and unlawful disclosure through the WpHG, incorporating MAR's detailed definitions. The EU framework under MAR provides the most granular definitional structure, with specific provisions addressing information-based manipulation, transaction-based manipulation, and technology-enabled manipulation including algorithmic and high-frequency trading strategies.

### Enforcement Architecture

A fundamental divergence exists in how these jurisdictions allocate enforcement authority. South Korea and the United States concentrate enforcement in specialized agencies (FSC/FSS and SEC, respectively) that possess both investigative and sanctioning powers, though criminal prosecution requires referral to public prosecutors. Germany assigns administrative enforcement to BaFin but relies on state prosecutors for criminal cases, a division that the Wirecard scandal exposed as insufficiently coordinated. The EU framework is inherently decentralized, with ESMA exercising coordination and supervisory convergence functions while enforcement remains with 27 national competent authorities, producing significant disparities in enforcement intensity across Member States.

### Penalty Severity

South Korea imposes the most severe criminal penalties among the four jurisdictions, with the possibility of life imprisonment for securities fraud yielding illicit profits exceeding 5 billion won. The United States follows with maximum sentences of 25 years under the Sarbanes-Oxley Act. Germany and the EU framework impose comparatively lighter maximum sentences, with 5 years as the standard maximum under the WpHG (though up to 10 years in particularly serious cases). However, the EU's administrative fine regime -- reaching 15 percent of annual turnover -- can impose the largest absolute monetary penalties on corporate entities.

### Human Rights Constraints

The ECHR's influence on securities fraud enforcement is most directly felt in EU Member States, where the Grande Stevens and A and B v. Norway line of cases has forced restructuring of dual-track enforcement systems. South Korea and the United States are not subject to ECHR jurisdiction, though both face domestic constitutional constraints on enforcement procedures. The U.S. Supreme Court's Jarkesy decision -- requiring jury trials for SEC civil penalty actions -- represents a parallel development constraining administrative enforcement, albeit grounded in the Seventh Amendment rather than international human rights law.

### Whistleblower Protections

All four jurisdictions now provide some form of whistleblower protection, reflecting a global trend toward incentivizing internal reporting. The United States leads with the SEC Whistleblower Program under the Dodd-Frank Act, which has awarded over $2 billion in bounties since inception. The EU's Whistleblower Protection Directive (2019/1937) requires all Member States to establish reporting channels and prohibit retaliation. Germany transposed this directive through the Hinweisgeberschutzgesetz (HinSchG) in 2023. South Korea has incorporated whistleblower protections into the FSCMA framework, though the bounty system is less developed than in the United States.
