## Summary

Co-ownership of property -- the legal situation in which two or more persons simultaneously hold ownership interests in the same property -- presents fundamental questions about the nature of property rights, the relationship between co-owners, and the mechanisms for resolving disputes over shared resources, with each legal system developing distinctive doctrinal frameworks reflecting its broader property law traditions. South Korea regulates co-ownership (공유, gongyu) primarily through the Civil Code (민법) Articles 262-270, which establish a fractional co-ownership (공유) system as the default form of shared ownership, alongside community ownership (합유, hapyu) under Articles 271-274 for partnership-type arrangements and collective ownership (총유, chongyu) under Articles 275-277 for unincorporated associations, with the Supreme Court of Korea (대법원) developing extensive case law on partition rights, management of co-owned property, and the protection of minority co-owners. The United States recognizes multiple concurrent estates -- tenancy in common, joint tenancy with right of survivorship, and tenancy by the entirety -- each carrying distinct legal incidents regarding alienability, survivorship, and partition, with state statutes and common law governing the rights and obligations of co-tenants including contribution, accounting, ouster, and partition actions. Germany distinguishes between fractional co-ownership (Miteigentum nach Bruchteilen) under BGB Sections 1008-1011 and joint ownership in undivided hand (Gesamthandseigentum) arising in the context of civil law partnerships (GbR), marital property communities, and communities of heirs, each carrying fundamentally different legal consequences for disposition, management, and dissolution. The ECHR protects co-ownership interests under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1, requiring that interference with co-owners' rights -- including forced partition, restrictions on disposal, and majority decision-making -- satisfy proportionality requirements, while the EU has not harmonized co-ownership law but addresses cross-border co-ownership through private international law instruments such as the Succession Regulation (EU) No. 650/2012 and the Matrimonial Property Regulation (EU) 2016/1103.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korean law recognizes three forms of co-ownership, each governed by distinct provisions of the Civil Code (민법) and reflecting different relationships among the co-owners.

**Fractional Co-Ownership (공유, Articles 262-270):** The default and most common form of co-ownership, where each co-owner holds a quantified fractional share (지분) of the property. Each share is independently alienable, inheritable, and subject to encumbrance. Where the shares are not specified, co-owners are presumed to hold equal shares (Article 262, Paragraph 2).

**Community Ownership (합유, Articles 271-274):** A form of shared ownership arising among members of a partnership-type entity (조합), where the co-owners hold undivided shares but cannot freely dispose of their individual shares without the consent of all other co-owners. Partition requires dissolution of the underlying partnership relationship.

**Collective Ownership (총유, Articles 275-277):** A form of shared ownership applicable to unincorporated associations (비법인사단), where ownership belongs to the association as a whole rather than to individual members. Members have no individual shares that can be separately alienated, and management and disposition are governed by the association's rules.

### Management and Use of Co-Owned Property

The Civil Code establishes a tiered decision-making framework for fractional co-ownership:

**Preservation Acts (보존행위, Article 265):** Any co-owner may independently undertake acts necessary to preserve the property, such as emergency repairs, defensive litigation, or measures to prevent deterioration. The Supreme Court has broadly interpreted preservation acts to include filing lawsuits to remove illegal occupants or cancel fraudulent registrations.

**Management Acts (관리행위, Article 265):** Acts relating to the ordinary management of the property require the agreement of co-owners holding a majority of the shares by value. This includes decisions about leasing the property, ordinary maintenance, and collection of rents.

**Disposition and Alteration Acts (처분·변경행위, Article 264):** Acts that dispose of or substantially alter the entire property require the unanimous consent of all co-owners. The Supreme Court has held that constructing a new building on co-owned land, changing the property's designated use, or granting a long-term lease constitutes a disposition or alteration requiring unanimity.

### Partition (분할)

Article 268 guarantees each co-owner the right to demand partition at any time, subject to an agreement not to partition for a period not exceeding five years (renewable). Partition may be accomplished through:

**Partition by Agreement (협의분할):** The co-owners voluntarily agree on the method and terms of partition.

**Judicial Partition (재판분할, Article 269):** Where agreement cannot be reached, any co-owner may file a partition action. The court may order partition in kind (현물분할) by physically dividing the property, partition by sale (경매분할) through court auction with distribution of proceeds, or a combination thereof. The Supreme Court has held that partition by price compensation (가액분할) -- where one co-owner acquires full ownership by compensating the others -- is also permissible under certain circumstances (Supreme Court Decision 2004Da30583).

### Supreme Court Jurisprudence

**Exclusive Use by One Co-Owner:** The Supreme Court has held that where one co-owner exclusively uses the entire co-owned property beyond their share, the other co-owners may claim compensation equivalent to rent for the portion corresponding to their shares (Supreme Court Decision 2001Da17633). This right exists regardless of whether there is a formal use agreement.

**Pre-emption and Third-Party Sales:** Unlike some civil law systems, Korean law does not grant co-owners a statutory right of pre-emption (우선매수권) when another co-owner sells their share to a third party. However, in agricultural land, the Farmland Act may impose separate transfer restrictions.

**Co-Ownership and Registration:** The Supreme Court has extensively addressed situations where registered ownership does not accurately reflect co-ownership arrangements, particularly in cases of title trust (명의신탁) where property is registered in one party's name but actually co-owned. The Real Name Registration Act (부동산실권리자명의등기에관한법률) of 1995 significantly restricted title trust arrangements.

## United States

### Legal Framework

American property law recognizes three principal forms of concurrent ownership, each defined by common law principles as modified by state statutes.

**Tenancy in Common (TIC):** The default form of co-ownership in all states, where each co-tenant holds an undivided fractional interest with no right of survivorship. Each interest is freely alienable, devisable, and inheritable. Co-tenants may hold unequal shares. Upon one co-tenant's death, their interest passes through their estate rather than to the surviving co-tenants.

**Joint Tenancy with Right of Survivorship (JTWROS):** A form of co-ownership requiring the four unities -- time, title, interest, and possession -- where each joint tenant holds an equal, undivided share with an automatic right of survivorship. Upon one joint tenant's death, their interest automatically passes to the surviving joint tenants by operation of law, outside the probate process. A joint tenant may sever the joint tenancy by conveying their interest to a third party, converting it to a tenancy in common.

**Tenancy by the Entirety (TBE):** A form of co-ownership available only to married couples in approximately 25 states, carrying a right of survivorship and the additional feature that neither spouse can unilaterally alienate or encumber their interest, and the property is generally immune from the creditors of one spouse alone. Divorce converts TBE to tenancy in common.

### Partition Actions

The right to partition is considered a fundamental incident of co-ownership in American law, though equity courts retain broad discretion in implementing partition.

**Partition in Kind:** The traditional preference, involving physical division of the property among the co-tenants. Courts historically favored partition in kind to preserve the co-tenants' connection to the land.

**Partition by Sale:** Where physical division is impractical or would result in a significant diminution of value, courts may order the property sold and the proceeds distributed. The Uniform Partition of Heirs Property Act (UPHPA), adopted by over 20 states since 2010, provides significant protections against forced sales of family-owned property, including rights of first refusal, independent appraisals, and a preference for partition in kind. This legislation was prompted by concerns that partition-by-sale actions were disproportionately used to dispossess African-American families of "heirs property" passed down through generations without formal estate planning.

### Co-Tenant Rights and Obligations

**Right of Possession:** Each co-tenant has the right to possess and use the entire property, regardless of their fractional share. This concurrent possessory right means that one co-tenant's use of the property does not, standing alone, constitute ouster of other co-tenants.

**Ouster:** Where one co-tenant excludes another from possession or makes an unequivocal claim of exclusive ownership, the excluded co-tenant may bring an action for ouster and recover their proportionate share of the property's rental value. Ouster also triggers the running of the statute of limitations for adverse possession by one co-tenant against another.

**Contribution:** A co-tenant who pays more than their proportionate share of necessary expenses -- including mortgage payments, property taxes, and essential repairs -- may seek contribution from the other co-tenants. However, most states do not allow contribution claims for improvements unless the improvements increase the property's value and are made in connection with a partition action.

**Accounting:** A co-tenant who receives rents or profits from the co-owned property must account to the other co-tenants for their proportionate shares. This obligation is governed by state accounting statutes and equitable principles.

### Severance of Joint Tenancy

Joint tenancy may be severed by unilateral conveyance, mutual agreement, operation of law (such as bankruptcy), or, in some states, by recording a declaration of severance. The question of whether a mortgage constitutes a severance depends on whether the state follows the title theory (severance occurs) or lien theory (no severance) of mortgages. California, a lien theory state, held in *Riddle v. Harmon* (1980) 102 Cal.App.3d 524 that a joint tenant may sever by conveying to themselves, eliminating the common law straw-man requirement.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German law establishes a fundamental distinction between two forms of co-ownership that reflects deep structural differences in how shared property rights are conceptualized.

**Fractional Co-Ownership (Bruchteilsgemeinschaft, BGB §§ 741-758, 1008-1011):** Each co-owner holds a quantified fractional share (Bruchteil) of the property, which may be freely disposed of (BGB § 747, sentence 1). The BGB provides detailed rules for management, use, expenses, and partition of fractionally co-owned property. This form arises by operation of law or by agreement whenever multiple persons acquire ownership shares in the same property without forming a Gesamthand relationship.

**Joint Ownership in Undivided Hand (Gesamthandseigentum):** A fundamentally different conceptual model in which property belongs to a community of persons collectively, and no individual co-owner holds a separately disposable share. Gesamthand ownership arises in three specific legal contexts: the civil law partnership (Gesellschaft bürgerlichen Rechts, BGB §§ 705 ff.), the community of heirs (Erbengemeinschaft, BGB §§ 2032 ff.), and the marital property community (Gütergemeinschaft, BGB §§ 1415 ff.). The 2024 reform of GbR law (Personengesellschaftsrechtsmodernisierungsgesetz, MoPeG) modernized partnership property rules while preserving the Gesamthand principle.

### Management of Fractionally Co-Owned Property

The BGB establishes a multi-tier decision-making system:

**Ordinary Administration (ordentliche Verwaltung, § 745):** Decisions relating to ordinary management of the property are made by majority vote, calculated by share values (Anteilsmehrheit). Ordinary administration includes entering into standard lease agreements, routine maintenance, and insurance.

**Extraordinary Measures (§ 745, Abs. 3):** Each co-owner may demand measures necessary for the property's preservation (Erhaltung). This right cannot be excluded by majority decision and extends to urgent repairs, fire protection, and defensive legal actions.

**Disposition of the Entire Property (§ 747):** Disposition of the entire co-owned property requires the consent of all co-owners. A single co-owner may freely dispose of their own fractional share but cannot dispose of the whole property or bind the other co-owners' shares.

**Use Regulation (Benutzungsregelung, § 745, Abs. 2):** Each co-owner is entitled to use the co-owned property insofar as use does not impair the co-use of the other co-owners (§ 743, Abs. 2). Co-owners may establish a use regulation by majority decision, and a court may establish one upon application where the co-owners cannot agree.

### Partition (Aufhebung der Gemeinschaft)

Under BGB § 749, each co-owner in a Bruchteilsgemeinschaft may demand partition (Aufhebung) at any time. Partition may not be demanded at an inopportune time (zur Unzeit), and co-owners may agree to exclude the right of partition, though such agreements are not binding beyond 30 years for immovable property.

**Methods of Partition:** The BGB provides for partition by physical division (Teilung in Natur, § 752) where the property can be divided into proportionate parts without significant diminution of value. Where physical division is impracticable, partition by sale (Teilungsversteigerung, § 753) is accomplished through public auction under the Zwangsversteigerungsgesetz (ZVG). Co-owners may bid at the auction.

**Community of Heirs (Erbengemeinschaft):** Partition of a community of heirs follows special rules under BGB §§ 2042-2057, requiring settlement of estate debts before distribution and providing for in-kind partition, sale, or compensatory partition. Any co-heir may demand partition at any time (§ 2042), and the testator cannot permanently exclude partition (though they may delay it for up to 30 years under § 2044).

### The Gesamthand Principle

The Gesamthand (joint hand or undivided hand) concept is distinctive to German law and has no direct equivalent in common law systems. Under Gesamthand ownership, the property belongs to the community as such rather than to the individual members in divisible shares. Key consequences include: (1) individual members cannot dispose of their share in specific Gesamthand assets (though they may transfer their membership interest in the Gesamthand community itself); (2) creditors of individual members cannot attach specific Gesamthand assets; and (3) partition requires dissolution of the underlying legal relationship (partnership, inheritance, or marriage).

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Proportionality Analysis

The European Court of Human Rights has addressed co-ownership disputes under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1, recognizing that co-ownership interests constitute "possessions" entitled to Convention protection.

**Forced Partition and Sale:** The Court has examined whether forced partition or sale of co-owned property constitutes a justified interference with the co-owner's property rights. In *Brosset-Triboulet and Others v. France* (Application No. 34078/02, 2010), the Grand Chamber considered the proportionality of demolition orders affecting property constructed on publicly owned coastal land, addressing the balance between individual property expectations and public interest in coastal preservation. The Court has recognized that states enjoy a wide margin of appreciation in establishing co-ownership management rules, provided that adequate procedural safeguards protect minority co-owners' interests.

**Marital Property Division:** The ECHR has also addressed co-ownership issues arising from divorce proceedings. In *Pla and Puncernau v. Andorra* (Application No. 69498/01, 2004), the Court examined discriminatory inheritance and property rules, while other cases have addressed the proportionality of forced property sales in the context of marital dissolution.

**Minority Co-Owner Protections:** The Court requires that national co-ownership regimes provide meaningful avenues for minority co-owners to challenge majority decisions that disproportionately affect their property interests. Procedural fairness, access to courts, and availability of compensation are key factors in the proportionality assessment.

### EU Cross-Border Co-Ownership

While the EU has not harmonized substantive co-ownership law, several EU instruments address cross-border co-ownership issues:

**Succession Regulation (EU) No. 650/2012:** Establishes uniform choice-of-law rules for cross-border succession, determining which national law governs the inheritance and partition of co-owned property when the deceased co-owner had cross-border connections. The default connecting factor is the deceased's habitual residence at death, though the deceased may elect their nationality's law.

**Matrimonial Property Regulation (EU) 2016/1103:** Provides choice-of-law rules for matrimonial property regimes in participating member states (enhanced cooperation procedure), determining which national law governs co-ownership interests arising from marriage. This regulation directly affects the characterization and division of marital co-ownership in cross-border marriages.

**Brussels Ia Regulation (EU) No. 1215/2012:** Provides jurisdictional rules for civil disputes, including exclusive jurisdiction for proceedings relating to rights in rem in immovable property (Article 24(1)), which directly affects litigation over co-owned real property located in EU member states.

### Council of Europe Standards

The Council of Europe has addressed co-ownership indirectly through recommendations on family law (including matrimonial property) and housing rights. The European Commission for Democracy through Law (Venice Commission) has examined property law reforms in transitional democracies, including issues of co-ownership arising from privatization of formerly state-owned housing.

## Comparative Analysis

### Structural Approaches to Co-Ownership

The four jurisdictions reveal two fundamentally different conceptual models of co-ownership. The fractional model (Korean 공유, American tenancy in common, German Bruchteilsgemeinschaft) treats co-ownership as individual ownership of quantified shares that happen to coexist in the same property. The collective model (Korean 합유/총유, American joint tenancy/TBE, German Gesamthand) treats the co-owned property as belonging to a relationship or community rather than to individuals separately. Each jurisdiction's co-ownership law is shaped by which model it treats as primary and how it manages the tension between individual autonomy and collective governance.

### Partition Rights

All four systems recognize a fundamental right to demand partition, reflecting the policy against perpetual co-ownership. However, the restrictions on this right vary significantly. Korean law permits agreements to exclude partition for up to five years. German law permits exclusion agreements but limits them to 30 years for immovable property. American courts exercise broad equitable discretion in partition proceedings, and the UPHPA has introduced significant new protections against forced sales. The ECHR requires that any forced partition or restriction on partition satisfy proportionality, with particular attention to the impact on minority co-owners and vulnerable populations.

### Management and Decision-Making

The majority-rule principle for ordinary management is common to Korean, German, and many American jurisdictions, but its implementation differs. Korean and German law calculate majority by share value, while American law is less systematic, often relying on concurrent possessory rights rather than formal majority decision-making. The ECHR's proportionality framework requires that majority rule not be used to effectively expropriate minority co-owners' interests.

### Survivorship and Inheritance

The right of survivorship represents a fundamental dividing line among co-ownership forms. American joint tenancy's automatic survivorship has no exact parallel in Korean fractional co-ownership but functionally resembles Korean community ownership (합유) and German Gesamthand forms. The interaction between co-ownership and inheritance law is particularly complex in cross-border situations, where the EU Succession Regulation attempts to provide predictability by establishing uniform choice-of-law rules.
