## Summary

Cybercrime -- criminal activity targeting or using computers, networks, and digital systems -- has emerged as one of the fastest-growing areas of criminal law worldwide, yet national legal frameworks vary significantly in scope, penalties, and enforcement mechanisms. South Korea addresses cybercrime primarily through the Act on Promotion of Information and Communications Network Utilization and Information Protection (정보통신망 이용촉진 및 정보보호 등에 관한 법률, commonly known as the Network Act or 정보통신망법), imposing penalties of up to seven years imprisonment for malware distribution and up to five years for unauthorized system intrusion. The United States relies on the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA, 18 U.S.C. section 1030), enacted in 1986 and amended multiple times, which carries penalties ranging from one year to life imprisonment depending on the severity of harm caused. Germany criminalizes cybercrime through several sections of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), particularly sections 202a through 202c (data espionage and interception) and sections 303a through 303b (data alteration and computer sabotage), with penalties of up to ten years for serious cases of computer sabotage. At the international level, the Budapest Convention on Cybercrime (2001), the first multilateral treaty on cybercrime, provides a harmonized framework for criminalizing cyber offenses and facilitating cross-border cooperation, with 81 states parties as of 2025. All four jurisdictions are grappling with emerging challenges posed by ransomware, deepfake technology, and AI-enabled criminal activity, prompting legislative updates across the board.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's cybercrime framework rests on several interlocking statutes. The primary vehicle is the Act on Promotion of Information and Communications Network Utilization and Information Protection (정보통신망법), which criminalizes unauthorized access, data theft, malware distribution, and system disruption. The Criminal Act (형법) provides supplementary provisions, and specialized statutes address particular categories of cybercrime.

Key provisions of the Network Act include:

- **Article 48(1) -- Unauthorized Intrusion:** No person shall intrude upon an information and communications network without legitimate access authority. Violations carry imprisonment for up to five years or a fine of up to 50 million won (approximately $38,000).
- **Article 48(2) -- Malware Distribution:** No person shall transmit or spread malicious programs that may cause disruption to, or destruction of, an information and communications system. Violations carry imprisonment for up to seven years or a fine of up to 70 million won (approximately $53,000).
- **Article 48(3) -- System Disruption:** No person shall damage another person's information or disrupt the operation of another person's information and communications network. Violations carry imprisonment for up to five years or a fine of up to 50 million won.
- **Article 49 -- Data Misappropriation:** No person shall damage, destroy, alter, forge, or leak information processed, stored, or transmitted through an information and communications network. Violations carry imprisonment for up to five years or a fine of up to 50 million won.

### Deepfake and AI-Related Crime

South Korea has taken an aggressive stance on deepfake crime. In September 2024, the National Assembly amended the Act on Special Cases Concerning the Punishment of Sexual Crimes to specifically address AI-generated deepfake pornography:

- **Creation or dissemination** of deepfake sexual content: up to seven years imprisonment.
- **Possession or viewing** of such content: up to three years imprisonment or a fine of up to 30 million won (approximately $22,600).

The government launched a dedicated crackdown campaign running through October 2026, targeting creators, distributors, and consumers of illegal deepfake content. Additionally, the AI Act (scheduled to take effect January 2026) requires mandatory AI-generated content labeling: any person who creates or edits AI-generated content and posts it online must clearly indicate its synthetic origin.

### Ransomware

While South Korea does not have a standalone ransomware statute, ransomware attacks are prosecuted under Articles 48 and 49 of the Network Act (malware distribution and system disruption), as well as extortion provisions under the Criminal Act. The Korea Internet and Security Agency (KISA) coordinates incident response and supports law enforcement investigations.

### Landmark Cases

**Operation "Nth Room" (2019-2020):** Cho Ju-bin operated a network of Telegram chatrooms distributing sexually exploitative deepfake and real content, coercing victims through digital blackmail. He was sentenced to 42 years imprisonment in 2022. The case catalyzed South Korea's legislative response to digital sex crimes and deepfakes.

**Upbit Cryptocurrency Exchange Hack (2019):** Hackers stole approximately 342,000 Ether (valued at approximately $50 million at the time) from South Korea's largest cryptocurrency exchange. South Korean and international investigators attributed the attack to the North Korean Lazarus Group. The case highlighted cross-border enforcement challenges in cybercrime.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA), codified at 18 U.S.C. section 1030, is the primary federal statute for prosecuting cybercrime. Enacted in 1986 as an amendment to the Comprehensive Crime Control Act of 1984, it has been amended multiple times, most recently in 2008, to address evolving threats. The CFAA criminalizes seven categories of conduct:

1. **Section 1030(a)(1):** Unauthorized access to a computer to obtain national security information (espionage).
2. **Section 1030(a)(2):** Unauthorized access to obtain information from financial institutions, government computers, or any protected computer.
3. **Section 1030(a)(3):** Unauthorized access to a nonpublic government computer.
4. **Section 1030(a)(4):** Knowingly accessing a protected computer with intent to defraud and obtaining something of value.
5. **Section 1030(a)(5):** Knowingly causing damage to a protected computer through transmission of code, programs, or commands (covers malware, ransomware, and DDoS attacks).
6. **Section 1030(a)(6):** Trafficking in computer passwords or similar access credentials.
7. **Section 1030(a)(7):** Extortion involving threats to damage a protected computer or release stolen data (directly applicable to ransomware).

### Penalties

| Offense | First Offense | Repeat Offense |
|---------|--------------|----------------|
| Unauthorized access for information (a)(2) | Up to 1 year (misdemeanor) or 5 years (felony) | Up to 10 years |
| Computer fraud (a)(4) | Up to 5 years | Up to 10 years |
| Intentional damage to protected computer (a)(5)(A) | Up to 10 years | Up to 20 years |
| Reckless damage causing serious bodily injury (a)(5)(A) | Up to 20 years | Up to 20 years |
| Damage resulting in death | Up to life imprisonment | Up to life imprisonment |
| Extortion via computer threats (a)(7) | Up to 5 years | Up to 10 years |
| Password trafficking (a)(6) | Up to 1 year or 10 years | Up to 20 years |

Fines under the CFAA can reach up to $250,000 per count, or twice the gain or loss resulting from the offense.

### Ransomware

Ransomware is prosecuted under multiple federal statutes, including CFAA section 1030(a)(5) (damage to protected computers), section 1030(a)(7) (extortion), the Economic Espionage Act (18 U.S.C. sections 1831-1839), and wire fraud (18 U.S.C. section 1343). The Department of Justice established a dedicated Ransomware and Digital Extortion Task Force in 2021 to coordinate federal enforcement. Executive Order 14028, signed in May 2021, strengthened federal cybersecurity requirements for government contractors and software supply chains.

### Deepfake and AI-Related Crime

The United States lacks a comprehensive federal AI-cybercrime statute, though several targeted measures exist:

- **TAKE IT DOWN Act (2025):** Signed into law on May 19, 2025, it criminalizes the knowing publication of non-consensual intimate visual depictions, including AI-generated deepfakes. Penalties include up to two years imprisonment for offenses involving adults and enhanced penalties for offenses involving minors.
- **State Laws:** Over 40 states have enacted or proposed deepfake-related legislation, covering election disinformation, non-consensual intimate imagery, and fraud.
- **Existing Federal Tools:** Prosecutors use wire fraud (18 U.S.C. section 1343), identity theft (18 U.S.C. section 1028), and the CFAA to prosecute AI-enabled cybercrime pending comprehensive federal legislation.

### Landmark Cases

**United States v. Van Buren (2021):** The Supreme Court held in a 6-3 decision that the CFAA's "exceeds authorized access" provision applies only to those who access information in areas of a computer to which their access does not extend, not to those who misuse information they are otherwise entitled to access. The decision narrowed the scope of the CFAA and resolved a longstanding circuit split.

**Colonial Pipeline Ransomware Attack (2021):** The DarkSide ransomware group attacked Colonial Pipeline, the largest fuel pipeline in the eastern United States, forcing a six-day shutdown and causing widespread fuel shortages. Colonial paid a $4.4 million ransom in Bitcoin; the DOJ subsequently recovered approximately $2.3 million of the payment. The attack accelerated federal ransomware enforcement and policy development.

**United States v. Astamirov and Vasiliev (Lockbit, 2023-2024):** Federal authorities disrupted the LockBit ransomware operation, one of the most prolific ransomware-as-a-service groups globally, and charged multiple individuals. The operation involved coordinated action across the US, UK, and Europol, demonstrating the importance of international cooperation in combating ransomware.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany addresses cybercrime through several provisions of the Strafgesetzbuch (Criminal Code, StGB), organized into two principal groups:

**Data Espionage and Interception (sections 202a-202d):**

- **Section 202a -- Data Espionage (Ausspähen von Daten):** Whoever unlawfully obtains data for themselves or another that was not intended for them and was especially protected against unauthorized access, by circumventing the protection, shall be liable to imprisonment not exceeding three years or a fine.
- **Section 202b -- Data Interception (Abfangen von Daten):** Whoever intercepts data not intended for them, by technical means from non-public data transmissions or electromagnetic emissions, shall be liable to imprisonment not exceeding two years or a fine.
- **Section 202c -- Preparation of Data Espionage and Interception (Vorbereiten des Ausspähens und Abfangens von Daten):** Whoever prepares the commission of an offense under sections 202a or 202b by producing, acquiring, selling, or distributing passwords, security codes, or computer programs whose purpose is the commission of such offenses, shall be liable to imprisonment not exceeding two years or a fine.
- **Section 202d -- Handling of Stolen Data (Datenhehlerei):** Introduced in 2015, it criminalizes the acquisition or transfer of data not generally accessible that another person has obtained through an unlawful act, with imprisonment not exceeding three years or a fine.

**Data Alteration and Computer Sabotage (sections 303a-303b):**

- **Section 303a -- Data Alteration (Datenveränderung):** Whoever unlawfully deletes, suppresses, renders unusable, or alters data shall be liable to imprisonment not exceeding two years or a fine.
- **Section 303b -- Computer Sabotage (Computersabotage):** Whoever interferes with data processing of substantial importance to another person by committing an offense under section 303a, entering or transmitting data with the intent to cause harm, or destroying, damaging, rendering unusable, removing, or altering a data processing system, shall be liable to imprisonment not exceeding three years or a fine. In especially serious cases -- particularly those involving commercial operations, government agencies, or essential public services -- the penalty is imprisonment from six months to ten years.

### Deepfake and AI-Related Crime

Germany is in the process of creating specific criminal provisions for deepfakes:

- **Proposed Section 201b StGB -- Violation of Personality Rights Through Digital Forgery:** The Bundesrat submitted a draft bill to the Bundestag on August 27, 2025, proposing a new criminal offense covering the misuse of AI-generated or manipulated audio-visual media that infringe personality rights. The proposed penalties are up to two years imprisonment in standard cases and up to five years in serious cases. Exceptions are provided for legitimate interests such as art, science, and satire.
- **Existing Gaps:** Current sections 201 (violation of the confidentiality of the spoken word) and 201a (violation of intimate privacy through images) require real recordings and do not cover AI-generated content, creating a legal gap that the proposed section 201b is designed to fill.
- **EU AI Act Implementation:** Germany is implementing the EU Artificial Intelligence Act (Regulation 2024/1689), which reaches full enforcement on August 2, 2026. The Act imposes transparency obligations on deepfake content and classifies certain AI applications by risk level.

### Ransomware

Ransomware attacks are prosecuted under section 303a (data alteration), section 303b (computer sabotage), and section 253 StGB (extortion/Erpressung). The Bundeskriminalamt (BKA, Federal Criminal Police) leads ransomware investigations, coordinating with Europol and the FBI through established mutual legal assistance channels.

### Security Research Protection

In 2024, the German government drafted legislation to protect security researchers from criminal prosecution under sections 202a-202c when they responsibly disclose vulnerabilities. The draft aims to address criticism that the broad language of section 202c has had a chilling effect on legitimate cybersecurity research.

### Landmark Cases

**Bundestag Hack (2015):** The German federal parliament suffered a major cyberattack attributed to the Russian APT28 (Fancy Bear) group. Attackers exfiltrated approximately 16 gigabytes of data, including emails from members of parliament. Germany issued an arrest warrant for Dmitri Badin, a GRU officer, in 2020, though he has not been apprehended. The case underscored the challenge of attributing and prosecuting state-sponsored cyber operations.

**Düsseldorf University Hospital Attack (2020):** A ransomware attack on the Düsseldorf University Hospital forced the diversion of emergency patients, and a patient died after being redirected to a hospital 30 kilometers away. German prosecutors initially investigated the death as negligent homicide linked to the cyberattack, though the investigation was later closed due to difficulty establishing direct causation. The case was one of the first globally to raise the prospect of criminal liability for death caused by ransomware.

## European Framework

### Budapest Convention on Cybercrime (2001)

The Convention on Cybercrime, opened for signature in Budapest on November 23, 2001, and entering into force on July 1, 2004, is the first and most widely adopted international treaty addressing cybercrime. As of 2025, 81 states have ratified it and an additional two have signed without ratification.

**Substantive Provisions:** The Convention requires parties to criminalize:

- Illegal access to computer systems (Article 2)
- Illegal interception of non-public transmissions (Article 3)
- Data interference -- damaging, deleting, or altering data (Article 4)
- System interference -- hindering the functioning of a computer system (Article 5)
- Misuse of devices -- production, sale, or distribution of hacking tools (Article 6)
- Computer-related forgery and fraud (Articles 7-8)
- Content-related offenses including child exploitation material (Article 9)
- Copyright infringement (Article 10)

**Procedural Provisions:** The Convention establishes procedures for:

- Expedited preservation of stored computer data (Article 16)
- Production orders compelling disclosure of subscriber data (Article 18)
- Search and seizure of stored computer data (Article 19)
- Real-time collection of traffic data (Article 20)
- Interception of content data (Article 21)

**Mutual Assistance:** Parties must establish a 24/7 point of contact for urgent cross-border requests and cooperate in the collection of electronic evidence.

### Second Additional Protocol (2022)

The Second Additional Protocol, opened for signature on May 12, 2022, enhances cross-border cooperation and access to electronic evidence. Key provisions include:

- **Article 6:** Direct requests to domain name registrars in other parties' territories for registration information.
- **Article 7:** Disclosure of subscriber information by service providers across borders.
- **Article 8:** Expedited production orders for subscriber and traffic data across jurisdictions.
- **Article 10:** Emergency mutual legal assistance for situations involving significant risk to life or safety.

The Protocol includes robust data protection safeguards, including limitations on automated decisions, data retention requirements, and restrictions on onward transfers.

### EU Directive on Attacks Against Information Systems (2013/40/EU)

This Directive requires EU Member States to criminalize illegal access to information systems, illegal system interference, illegal data interference, and illegal interception. It mandates minimum maximum penalties of at least two years imprisonment for most offenses and at least five years for aggravated cases involving organized crime, critical infrastructure, or large-scale attacks.

## Comparative Analysis

### Penalty Comparison Table

| Offense | South Korea | United States | Germany |
|---------|------------|---------------|---------|
| **Unauthorized access / hacking** | Up to 5 years / 50M won fine | Up to 1-5 years (first); 10 years (repeat) | Up to 3 years / fine (section 202a) |
| **Malware distribution** | Up to 7 years / 70M won fine | Up to 10 years (first); 20 years (repeat) | Up to 2 years / fine (section 303a) |
| **Computer sabotage / system disruption** | Up to 5 years / 50M won fine | Up to 10-20 years | 6 months to 10 years (serious cases, section 303b) |
| **Data interception** | Up to 5 years / 50M won fine | Up to 5 years (first) | Up to 2 years / fine (section 202b) |
| **Ransomware / cyber extortion** | Up to 7 years (combined offenses) | Up to 5-20 years (CFAA + extortion) | Up to 10 years (sections 303b + 253) |
| **Deepfake sexual content (creation)** | Up to 7 years | Up to 2 years (TAKE IT DOWN Act) | Up to 5 years (proposed section 201b) |
| **Deepfake sexual content (possession)** | Up to 3 years / 30M won fine | Varies by state | Up to 2 years (proposed section 201b) |
| **Hacking tools / preparation** | Prosecuted under Network Act | Up to 1-10 years (password trafficking) | Up to 2 years (section 202c) |
| **Death resulting from cyberattack** | Criminal Act provisions apply | Up to life imprisonment | Negligent homicide (section 222) applies |

### Structural Differences

| Feature | South Korea | United States | Germany | Budapest Convention |
|---------|------------|---------------|---------|-------------------|
| **Primary statute** | Network Act (정보통신망법) | CFAA (18 U.S.C. section 1030) | StGB sections 202a-d, 303a-b | Treaty (ETS 185) |
| **Legislative approach** | Comprehensive single statute | Multiple overlapping federal statutes | Integrated into general criminal code | Harmonization framework |
| **Deepfake-specific law** | Yes (since 2024) | Partial (TAKE IT DOWN Act, 2025) | Proposed (section 201b, pending) | Not yet addressed |
| **AI regulation** | AI Act (effective Jan 2026) | No comprehensive federal law | EU AI Act implementation (Aug 2026) | Not yet addressed |
| **Security researcher protection** | Limited | Narrowed by Van Buren (2021) | Draft legislation (2024) | Not specifically addressed |
| **Ransomware-specific provision** | No (uses existing offenses) | Yes (section 1030(a)(7)) | No (uses existing offenses) | No (uses existing framework) |
| **Extraterritorial jurisdiction** | Yes, for attacks on Korean systems | Yes, for "protected computers" | Yes, for attacks on German systems | Facilitated via mutual assistance |
| **24/7 contact point** | Yes (KISA) | Yes (DOJ/FBI) | Yes (BKA) | Required (Article 35) |

### Key Trends

1. **Penalty escalation:** All jurisdictions have increased cybercrime penalties over the past decade, reflecting growing recognition of economic and national security impacts.
2. **Deepfake criminalization:** South Korea leads with comprehensive deepfake legislation; the US has enacted targeted measures; Germany has proposed but not yet enacted specific provisions.
3. **Ransomware as priority:** All jurisdictions treat ransomware as a high-priority enforcement area, using combinations of existing statutes rather than standalone ransomware laws (with the partial exception of the CFAA's extortion provision).
4. **AI governance gap:** No jurisdiction has enacted a comprehensive AI-cybercrime statute, though all are developing regulatory frameworks that will affect criminal liability.
5. **International cooperation:** The Budapest Convention and its Second Additional Protocol provide the primary mechanism for cross-border cooperation, though enforcement remains challenging when attackers operate from non-party states.
