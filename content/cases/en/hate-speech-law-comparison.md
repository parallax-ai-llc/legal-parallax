## Summary

Hate speech -- expression that attacks, demeans, or incites violence or discrimination against individuals or groups based on protected characteristics such as race, ethnicity, religion, gender, sexual orientation, or disability -- represents one of the most contested areas of comparative law, with jurisdictions reflecting fundamentally irreconcilable philosophies about the relationship between free expression and human dignity. South Korea lacks a comprehensive anti-hate speech law, with protection against discriminatory expression scattered across the National Human Rights Commission Act (국가인권위원회법), limited provisions in the Information and Communications Network Act (정보통신망법), and the Broadcasting Act's content standards; multiple comprehensive anti-discrimination bills including hate speech provisions have been introduced in the National Assembly since 2007 but none have been enacted, facing opposition from conservative religious organizations and concerns about free speech implications. The United States provides the broadest protection for hate speech among the jurisdictions compared, with the Supreme Court consistently holding that the government may not prohibit speech solely on the basis of its hateful or offensive content, as established in R.A.V. v. City of St. Paul (1992) and reaffirmed in Matal v. Tam (2017), with narrow exceptions for true threats (Virginia v. Black, 2003), incitement to imminent lawless action (Brandenburg v. Ohio, 1969), and fighting words (Chaplinsky v. New Hampshire, 1942). Germany maintains one of the world's most comprehensive hate speech frameworks, anchored in Section 130 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB) on Volksverhetzung (incitement to hatred), the prohibition of Holocaust denial under Section 130(3) StGB, and the Netzwerkdurchsetzungsgesetz (NetzDG, 2017) requiring social media platforms to remove manifestly unlawful hate speech within 24 hours, with the historical experience of Nazi propaganda providing the constitutional and moral foundation for robust speech restrictions. At the European level, the EU Framework Decision 2008/913/JHA on combating certain forms and expressions of racism and xenophobia requires member states to criminalize public incitement to violence or hatred against groups defined by race, color, religion, descent, or national or ethnic origin, and the denial, condoning, or gross trivialization of genocide and crimes against humanity, while the ECHR has held that hate speech may be excluded from Article 10 protection entirely under Article 17 (prohibition of abuse of rights) or restricted under Article 10(2) as necessary in a democratic society.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's approach to hate speech is characterized by the absence of comprehensive legislation despite persistent advocacy and multiple legislative proposals.

**National Human Rights Commission Act (국가인권위원회법):** The NHRC (국가인권위원회) has authority to investigate discrimination cases and issue recommendations, including cases involving discriminatory expression. However, the Commission's powers are non-binding, and it cannot impose penalties. The NHRC has repeatedly called for comprehensive anti-discrimination legislation including hate speech provisions.

**Information and Communications Network Act (정보통신망법):** Article 44-7 prohibits the distribution of information that "defames or insults" others through information and communication networks, and the KCSC may order removal of such content. This provision is not specifically designed for hate speech but has been applied in some cases involving discriminatory online expression.

### Failed Legislative Efforts

| Year | Bill | Status |
|------|------|--------|
| 2007 | Comprehensive Anti-Discrimination Act | Failed (religious organization opposition) |
| 2013 | Anti-Discrimination Act (revised) | Failed to advance from committee |
| 2020 | Equality Act (proposed by Justice Party) | Failed |
| 2021 | Comprehensive Anti-Discrimination Act | Failed (21st National Assembly) |
| 2024 | Anti-Discrimination and Hate Speech Prevention Act | Pending (22nd National Assembly) |

The primary opposition has come from conservative Christian organizations, which argue that hate speech prohibitions covering sexual orientation would restrict religious expression and advance a "homosexuality normalization" agenda. Political parties, reluctant to alienate religious voting blocs, have repeatedly declined to advance the legislation.

### Existing Protections

Despite the absence of comprehensive legislation, limited protections exist:

- **Election Act:** Prohibits discriminatory expression targeting candidates based on gender, religion, or disability
- **Broadcasting Act:** Prohibits broadcasting content that discriminates based on race, gender, age, occupation, religion, or disability
- **Criminal Act Article 311 (모욕):** The criminal insult provision has been applied in some hate speech contexts, though it is designed for individual rather than group-based insults
- **Jeju 4·3 Special Act and May 18 Special Act:** Prohibit denial or distortion of specific historical atrocities

### Hate Crime Data

The NHRC's annual reports document increasing complaints regarding discriminatory expression. In 2023, online hate speech targeting women, LGBTQ+ individuals, persons with disabilities, and migrants constituted the largest categories of discrimination complaints.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States stands alone among mature democracies in providing constitutional protection for hate speech. The First Amendment has been consistently interpreted to prevent government restriction of expression based solely on its hateful or offensive content.

**Brandenburg v. Ohio, 395 U.S. 444 (1969):** The Supreme Court established that the government may not punish advocacy of illegal conduct unless the advocacy is "directed to inciting or producing imminent lawless action and is likely to incite or produce such action." This narrow incitement standard replaced the broader "clear and present danger" test and effectively immunizes most hate speech from criminal punishment.

**R.A.V. v. City of St. Paul, 505 U.S. 377 (1992):** The Court unanimously struck down a city ordinance prohibiting the display of symbols that "arouse anger, alarm or resentment in others on the basis of race, color, creed, religion or gender," holding that the government may not engage in viewpoint-based or content-based discrimination even within categories of unprotected speech (fighting words).

**Virginia v. Black, 538 U.S. 343 (2003):** The Court upheld Virginia's cross-burning statute to the extent it prohibited cross burning with the intent to intimidate, recognizing true threats as unprotected speech. However, the Court struck down the statutory provision that treated cross burning as prima facie evidence of intimidating intent.

**Matal v. Tam, 582 U.S. 218 (2017):** The Court unanimously struck down the Lanham Act's "disparagement clause" prohibiting registration of trademarks that disparage persons, affirming that "speech may not be banned on the ground that it expresses ideas that offend."

### Hate Crimes vs. Hate Speech

While hate speech is constitutionally protected, hate crimes receive enhanced penalties:

**Matthew Shepard and James Byrd Jr. Hate Crimes Prevention Act (2009, 18 U.S.C. Section 249):** Federalizes hate crimes committed because of actual or perceived race, color, religion, national origin, gender, sexual orientation, gender identity, or disability. The Act addresses conduct, not speech.

**Wisconsin v. Mitchell, 508 U.S. 476 (1993):** The Supreme Court unanimously upheld hate crime penalty enhancement statutes, holding that the First Amendment does not prohibit states from imposing enhanced penalties for crimes motivated by bias, distinguishing motive-based sentencing enhancement from punishment of speech.

### Campus Speech Codes

University hate speech codes have been consistently struck down by courts. In Doe v. University of Michigan, 721 F. Supp. 852 (E.D. Mich. 1989), the court invalidated the university's anti-harassment policy as unconstitutionally vague and overbroad.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany maintains arguably the most comprehensive hate speech regime among democracies, reflecting the constitutional commitment to human dignity (Article 1 GG) and the historical lessons of Nazi propaganda.

**Section 130 StGB -- Volksverhetzung (Incitement to Hatred):**

| Subsection | Offense | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| Section 130(1) | Inciting hatred against a national, racial, religious, or ethnic group; calling for violence against such groups; attacking human dignity by insulting, maliciously disparaging, or defaming such groups | 3 months to 5 years imprisonment |
| Section 130(2) | Dissemination of written materials inciting hatred | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine |
| Section 130(3) | Denial or gross trivialization of Holocaust (genocide under National Socialism) | Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine |
| Section 130(4) | Glorification of Nazi rule in a manner likely to disturb public peace | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine |
| Section 130(5) | Added 2022: denial, approval, or gross trivialization of genocide, crimes against humanity, or war crimes directed against identified groups | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine |

### Holocaust Denial

Section 130(3) StGB specifically criminalizes the denial or gross trivialization of acts committed under National Socialist rule that constitute genocide within the meaning of Section 6 of the Völkerstrafgesetzbuch (Code of Crimes Against International Law). The Federal Constitutional Court has repeatedly upheld this provision, holding in BVerfGE 90, 241 (1994) that the assertion of the Holocaust as a historical fact is not a protected opinion but a provably false factual claim, and that its denial constitutes an attack on the dignity of Holocaust victims.

### NetzDG (Netzwerkdurchsetzungsgesetz, 2017)

The NetzDG, enacted on October 1, 2017, required social media platforms with more than 2 million German users to:

- Maintain an accessible complaint mechanism for unlawful content
- Remove "manifestly unlawful" content within 24 hours of notification
- Remove other unlawful content within 7 days of notification
- Publish semi-annual transparency reports on content complaints and removals
- Designate a domestic agent for legal service

Violations were punishable by fines up to 50 million EUR. The NetzDG has been largely superseded by the EU Digital Services Act since 2024 but served as an influential model for platform content regulation worldwide.

### Enforcement Statistics

German authorities prosecuted approximately 8,500 cases of Volksverhetzung in 2023, reflecting significant enforcement commitment. Online hate speech cases have increased substantially since the NetzDG's enactment, aided by improved platform reporting mechanisms.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Framework Decision 2008/913/JHA

The Council Framework Decision of November 28, 2008, on combating certain forms and expressions of racism and xenophobia by means of criminal law requires all EU member states to criminalize:

- Public incitement to violence or hatred against a group defined by reference to race, color, descent, religion, or national or ethnic origin
- Public dissemination or distribution of material inciting such violence or hatred
- Public condoning, denial, or gross trivialization of crimes of genocide, crimes against humanity, and war crimes (as defined in the Statute of the ICC) directed against such groups

Member states must ensure that these offenses are punishable by effective, proportionate, and dissuasive criminal penalties with a maximum of at least 1-3 years imprisonment.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

The ECHR applies two approaches to hate speech:

**Article 17 Exclusion (Abuse of Rights):** The Court may declare hate speech entirely outside Article 10 protection under Article 17, which provides that nothing in the Convention may be interpreted as granting "any right to engage in any activity or perform any act aimed at the destruction of any of the rights and freedoms set forth herein."

- **Garaudy v. France (2003, inadmissibility):** Holocaust denial excluded from Article 10 protection under Article 17
- **Norwood v. United Kingdom (2004, inadmissibility):** Display of anti-Islamic poster excluded under Article 17

**Article 10(2) Restriction:** Where the Court does not apply Article 17, it analyzes hate speech restrictions under the Article 10(2) framework (prescribed by law, legitimate aim, necessary in a democratic society).

- **Jersild v. Denmark (Application No. 15890/89, Judgment of September 23, 1994):** The Court found that Denmark's conviction of a journalist for aiding and abetting the dissemination of racist statements violated Article 10. The journalist had broadcast a news program featuring interviews with racist individuals and had not endorsed their views.
- **Féret v. Belgium (Application No. 15615/07, Judgment of July 16, 2009):** The Court upheld Belgium's conviction of a political party leader for distributing racist leaflets during election campaigns, finding no violation of Article 10.

### EU Code of Conduct on Illegal Hate Speech Online (2016)

The European Commission and major technology companies (Facebook, Microsoft, Twitter, YouTube) agreed to a voluntary Code of Conduct in May 2016, committing companies to review the majority of notified hate speech within 24 hours and remove or disable access to content determined to be illegal. By 2023, participating platforms reported reviewing over 90% of notifications within 24 hours, with removal rates averaging approximately 63%.

### Digital Services Act

The DSA (Regulation 2022/2065) provides the comprehensive framework for addressing illegal content online, including hate speech. The DSA does not define what constitutes illegal hate speech (this remains a matter of national law under the Framework Decision), but establishes obligations for platforms to act on illegal content notifications, maintain transparent content moderation policies, and provide effective complaint mechanisms.

### Gender-Based Hate Speech

Gender-based hate speech and online misogyny have become prominent issues, particularly in South Korea:

**South Korea:** Online misogyny and gender-based hate speech have intensified since the late 2010s, with particularly virulent expression targeting feminists and women in public life. The absence of a comprehensive anti-discrimination law means gender-based hate speech has no specific legal prohibition. The "Nth Room" case (2020) and related digital sex crime scandals highlighted the intersection of gender-based hate speech and sexual exploitation.

**United States:** Gender is not a covered category under the Matthew Shepard Hate Crimes Prevention Act for hate speech purposes (though it is covered for hate crimes). Title VII and Title IX prohibit hostile environment harassment in employment and education contexts but do not address public hate speech.

**Germany:** Section 130 StGB does not specifically enumerate gender as a protected category, though the provision has been applied in some gender-based cases. The 2021 strengthening of Section 140 StGB (rewarding and approving of crimes) was partly motivated by the need to address online threats against female politicians.

**EU:** The European Commission's 2022 proposal to add hate speech and hate crime to the list of EU crimes under Article 83(1) TFEU would create an EU-wide framework covering gender-based hate speech. As of 2026, negotiations continue among member states.

### Anti-Semitic and Islamophobic Expression

Anti-Semitic and Islamophobic expression receives distinct treatment reflecting historical and contemporary concerns:

**Germany:** Anti-Semitic expression is subject to the most comprehensive legal prohibition, including Section 130(3) (Holocaust denial), Section 130(4) (glorification of Nazi rule), and the broader Section 130 framework. The 2022 addition of Section 130(5) extended denial prohibitions to genocides beyond the Holocaust. Anti-Semitic motivation is treated as an aggravating factor in sentencing. Islamophobic expression is addressed through the general Volksverhetzung framework.

**United States:** Both anti-Semitic and Islamophobic speech are constitutionally protected under the First Amendment. The Noahide Principles resolution (1991) and various congressional resolutions have condemned anti-Semitism, but no legislative prohibition exists. Universities and employers may restrict discriminatory expression within their own contexts.

## Comparative Analysis

### Philosophical Foundations

The four jurisdictions reflect three distinct philosophical orientations. The United States treats free speech as a near-absolute right, viewing government as the primary threat to expression and the "marketplace of ideas" as the optimal mechanism for countering hateful speech. Germany treats human dignity as the paramount constitutional value, viewing hate speech as an attack on the foundations of democratic society that the state has an affirmative obligation to suppress. The ECHR occupies a middle position, applying proportionality analysis rather than categorical rules. South Korea's absence of comprehensive legislation reflects not a libertarian philosophy but political deadlock between competing social forces.

### Online Regulation Divergence

The digital context has amplified divergences. Germany's NetzDG pioneered mandatory platform content moderation. The EU's DSA provides the most comprehensive platform regulation framework. The United States' Section 230 regime provides platform immunity that effectively prevents regulation of online hate speech at the platform level. South Korea's KCSC operates within a narrower mandate. The global nature of online platforms means that content legal in the United States may trigger removal obligations in Germany and vice versa, creating persistent jurisdictional tensions.

### Protected Categories

The categories protected by hate speech laws vary. The EU Framework Decision covers race, color, descent, religion, and national or ethnic origin but not gender, sexual orientation, disability, or gender identity. Germany's Section 130 covers national, racial, religious, and ethnic groups. The ECHR has not established a fixed list. South Korea's proposed but unenacted legislation would cover the broadest categories, including gender, sexual orientation, disability, age, and migrant status.
