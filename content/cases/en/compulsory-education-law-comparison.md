## Summary

Compulsory education -- the legal obligation imposed on parents, guardians, or the state to ensure that children receive a minimum level of formal education during a specified age range -- reflects each society's fundamental commitments to child welfare, equal opportunity, social cohesion, and economic development, with significant variation in the age of compulsory attendance, permissible alternatives to public schooling, enforcement mechanisms, and the philosophical balance between state authority and parental rights. South Korea mandates compulsory education for nine years (six years of elementary school and three years of middle school) under Article 31 of the Constitution (교육을 받을 권리) and the Elementary and Secondary Education Act (초·중등교육법), with the Education Act (교육기본법) establishing the framework; South Korea's compulsory education system has been credited as a central driver of the country's rapid economic development, with near-universal attendance rates and consistently top-tier performance in international assessments such as PISA, though debates continue regarding the extension of compulsory education to high school and the intense pressure of the examination-driven education culture. The United States operates a decentralized compulsory education system governed primarily by state law, with all 50 states requiring attendance generally between ages 5-8 and 16-18, with the Supreme Court's decisions in Pierce v. Society of Sisters (1925) protecting the right to choose private education and Wisconsin v. Yoder (1972) recognizing limited religious exemptions for the Amish; there is no federal compulsory education statute, and significant variation exists among states regarding exemptions, homeschooling regulations, and enforcement. Germany has one of the most strictly enforced compulsory education systems in the world, with Schulpflicht (compulsory school attendance) mandated under state (Länder) education laws for 9-10 years of full-time schooling followed by part-time vocational school attendance until age 18, with the critical distinction that Germany requires school attendance (Schulbesuchspflicht) rather than merely education, effectively prohibiting homeschooling -- a position upheld by the Federal Constitutional Court and which led to the high-profile grant of asylum to the Romeike family by a U.S. immigration court in 2010 (later reversed). The European Court of Human Rights has addressed compulsory education under Article 2 of Protocol No. 1 (right to education) and Article 8 (right to respect for private and family life), establishing in Konrad v. Germany (2006) that the requirement of compulsory school attendance does not violate the Convention, while the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child (Article 28) requires states parties to make primary education compulsory and free.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's compulsory education system is anchored in the Constitution and multiple education statutes.

**Constitution Article 31:**
1. All citizens have the right to receive education according to their ability.
2. All citizens have the duty to provide compulsory education for children under their protection. Compulsory education shall be free.
3. The state shall promote lifelong education.

**Education Act (교육기본법):** Establishes the fundamental principles of education including the obligation of compulsory education, the principles of equal educational opportunity, and the responsibility of parents and guardians.

**Elementary and Secondary Education Act (초·중등교육법):** Provides the detailed framework for compulsory education, including school establishment, curriculum standards, teacher qualifications, and enforcement mechanisms.

### Structure

| Level | Duration | Age | Status |
|-------|----------|-----|--------|
| Elementary school (초등학교) | 6 years | 6-12 | Compulsory |
| Middle school (중학교) | 3 years | 12-15 | Compulsory (since 2004 nationally) |
| High school (고등학교) | 3 years | 15-18 | Not compulsory (but 99%+ attendance) |

Middle school education became universally compulsory in 2004, having been progressively extended from rural areas (1985) to urban areas. High school education is not compulsory but has been effectively free since 2021, when the government implemented free high school education (고교 무상교육) nationwide.

### Historical Development

South Korea's compulsory education system has been one of the most dramatic educational success stories in modern history:

| Year | Milestone |
|------|-----------|
| 1948 | Constitution establishes right to education; 6-year compulsory elementary education |
| 1954 | Six-Year Plan for Compulsory Education implementation begins |
| 1959 | Elementary enrollment reaches 96% (from approximately 54% in 1945) |
| 1985 | Free middle school education begins in rural/fishing areas |
| 2002 | Middle school education becomes compulsory in most regions |
| 2004 | Universal compulsory middle school education nationwide |
| 2021 | Free high school education implemented nationally |

### Enforcement

Parents who fail to enroll children in compulsory education may be subject to fines of up to 1 million KRW per semester under the Elementary and Secondary Education Act. In practice, enforcement is rarely necessary due to near-universal compliance -- South Korea's elementary and middle school enrollment rate has been consistently above 99%.

### Education Pressure and Reform

Despite high enrollment and academic achievement, South Korea's education system faces criticism for the intense pressure it places on students. The private tutoring (학원, hakwon) industry generates over 20 trillion KRW annually. The suicide rate among Korean youth has been linked to academic pressure, prompting reforms including the designation of "no-hakwon days" and efforts to reduce the emphasis on college entrance examinations.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has no federal compulsory education statute. Compulsory education is governed entirely by state law, with all 50 states and the District of Columbia mandating school attendance.

### State Variation

| Element | Range |
|---------|-------|
| Compulsory start age | 5 (various) to 8 (Pennsylvania, Washington) |
| Compulsory end age | 16 (various) to 18 (California, Texas, Oregon, others) |
| Total compulsory years | 8-13 years |
| Homeschool regulation level | None (11 states) to high (New York, Pennsylvania) |

### Supreme Court Jurisprudence

**Pierce v. Society of Sisters, 268 U.S. 510 (1925):** The Supreme Court struck down an Oregon law requiring all children to attend public schools, holding that parents have a liberty interest under the Fourteenth Amendment in directing the education of their children. The decision established that states may require education but cannot require that it occur in public schools.

**Wisconsin v. Yoder, 406 U.S. 205 (1972):** The Court held that the Free Exercise Clause required Wisconsin to exempt Amish families from compulsory education beyond the eighth grade (approximately age 14). The Court found that the additional one or two years of formal schooling would not serve a compelling state interest sufficient to override the Amish community's centuries-old religious practice.

**Plyler v. Doe, 457 U.S. 202 (1982):** The Court held that the Equal Protection Clause prohibits states from denying free public education to undocumented immigrant children. The decision established that all children within a state's jurisdiction, regardless of immigration status, are entitled to compulsory education.

### Truancy Enforcement

State truancy laws vary significantly in their enforcement mechanisms:

| Mechanism | States |
|-----------|--------|
| Parental fine | Most states; amounts range from $25 to $2,500 |
| Parental criminal penalty (misdemeanor) | Approximately 30 states |
| Juvenile court referral | Most states for habitual truancy |
| Community service | Various states |
| Driver's license suspension | Approximately 20 states link attendance to license eligibility |

### Homeschooling

All 50 states permit homeschooling, though regulatory requirements vary enormously:

- **Low regulation (11 states):** No notification, assessment, or curriculum requirements (e.g., Idaho, Texas, Alaska)
- **Moderate regulation:** Notification and/or standardized assessment required (e.g., Colorado, Florida)
- **High regulation:** Notification, assessment, curriculum approval, and/or teacher qualifications required (e.g., New York, Pennsylvania, Massachusetts)

Approximately 3.3 million children (5.6% of school-age children) were homeschooled in the 2023-2024 school year, a significant increase from pre-pandemic levels.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany maintains the strictest compulsory school attendance regime among the jurisdictions compared, with a critical distinction that sets it apart from most other democracies: Germany requires Schulbesuchspflicht (compulsory school attendance) rather than merely Bildungspflicht (compulsory education).

**Grundgesetz Article 7(1):** "The entire school system shall be under the supervision of the state." This provision establishes the state's authority over education, which the Federal Constitutional Court has interpreted as supporting the compulsory school attendance requirement.

**Länder Education Laws:** Each of Germany's 16 states enacts its own school attendance law (Schulgesetz). While details vary, all states require:
- Full-time school attendance (Vollzeitschulpflicht) for 9 years (10 years in some states) beginning at age 6
- Part-time vocational school attendance (Berufsschulpflicht) until age 18 for students not attending full-time secondary school

### Prohibition of Homeschooling

Germany is one of a very small number of European countries (along with Sweden) that effectively prohibits homeschooling. The prohibition is justified on multiple grounds:

1. **Social integration (soziale Integration):** The state has a legitimate interest in ensuring children learn to interact with persons from diverse backgrounds and develop social competence
2. **Democratic citizenship:** Schools serve a function in preparing children for participation in democratic society
3. **Parallel societies prevention:** Compulsory attendance prevents the formation of isolated religious or ideological communities that might undermine social cohesion
4. **Child protection:** School attendance enables teachers and staff to detect signs of abuse or neglect

### Federal Constitutional Court Decisions

**BVerfG, 1 BvR 436/03 (May 29, 2006):** The Court declined to accept a constitutional complaint by Christian parents seeking to homeschool their children, holding that compulsory school attendance does not violate the parents' right to education (Article 6(2) GG) or freedom of religion (Article 4 GG). The Court emphasized the state's legitimate interest in social integration.

**BVerfG, 2 BvR 1693/04 (2007):** The Court affirmed that compulsory attendance serves the prevention of parallel societies and the integration of minorities into the democratic community.

### Enforcement

Germany enforces compulsory school attendance through an escalating system of sanctions:

| Stage | Measure |
|-------|---------|
| 1. School notification | School contacts parents; offers support |
| 2. Ordnungswidrigkeitsverfahren | Administrative fine (Bußgeld): typically 500-2,500 EUR per violation |
| 3. Zwangsgeld | Coercive fine designed to compel compliance |
| 4. Criminal prosecution | In some states, repeated violation can lead to criminal penalties (fine or imprisonment up to 6 months) |
| 5. Schulzwang | Physical enforcement: police escort of child to school (last resort) |
| 6. Custody measures | Family court intervention; possible partial withdrawal of parental custody |

### Romeike Case

The Romeike family, evangelical Christians from Baden-Württemberg who sought to homeschool their children on religious grounds, fled Germany in 2008 after facing escalating fines and the threat of criminal prosecution. A U.S. immigration judge granted them asylum in 2010, finding that Germany's enforcement of compulsory attendance constituted persecution of a "particular social group" (homeschoolers). The Board of Immigration Appeals reversed in 2013, finding that German law was neutral and generally applicable. The Supreme Court declined to hear the case, though the family was ultimately granted indefinite deferred action status in the United States.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR -- Right to Education

**Protocol No. 1, Article 2:** "No person shall be denied the right to education. In the exercise of any functions which it assumes in relation to education and to teaching, the State shall respect the right of parents to ensure such education and teaching in conformity with their own religious and philosophical convictions."

**Konrad v. Germany (Application No. 35504/03, Inadmissibility Decision of September 11, 2006):** The Court declared inadmissible the application of German parents who sought to homeschool their children, finding that Germany's compulsory school attendance requirement did not violate Article 2 of Protocol No. 1 or Article 8 ECHR. The Court accepted Germany's argument that compulsory attendance serves the legitimate aim of social integration and the prevention of parallel societies.

**Wunderlich v. Germany (Application No. 18925/15, Judgment of January 10, 2019):** The Court found no violation of Article 8 in Germany's temporary removal of four children from a family that had been homeschooling in defiance of compulsory attendance laws. The Court held that while the removal was a serious interference with family life, it was proportionate given the parents' sustained non-compliance and the state's interest in enforcing compulsory education.

**Kjeldsen, Busk Madsen and Pedersen v. Denmark (Application No. 5095/71, Judgment of December 7, 1976):** The foundational case on parental rights in education. The Court held that compulsory sex education in Danish public schools did not violate Article 2 of Protocol No. 1, establishing that the state may include subjects in the curriculum that parents find objectionable, provided the instruction is "conveyed in an objective, critical and pluralistic manner."

### UN Convention on the Rights of the Child

Article 28 CRC requires states parties to:
1. Make primary education compulsory and available free to all
2. Encourage the development of different forms of secondary education, make them accessible to every child, and take appropriate measures such as free education and financial assistance
3. Make higher education accessible to all on the basis of capacity
4. Take measures to encourage regular attendance at schools and reduce drop-out rates

Article 29 CRC establishes the aims of education, including the development of the child's personality, talents, and mental and physical abilities; respect for human rights; and preparation for responsible life in a free society.

### EU Framework

Education is primarily a member state competence under the EU treaties. However, the EU contributes through:

- **Article 14 EU Charter:** Right to education, including the possibility to receive free compulsory education
- **Erasmus+ Programme:** EU mobility programme covering all levels of education
- **European Pillar of Social Rights:** Principle 1 affirms that everyone has the right to quality and inclusive education, training, and life-long learning
- **Council Recommendation on Early Childhood Education and Care (2019):** Promotes access to quality ECEC for all children

## Comparative Analysis

### Compulsory Attendance vs. Compulsory Education

The fundamental structural distinction is between systems that require school attendance (Germany) and systems that require education (United States, South Korea). Germany's Schulbesuchspflicht mandates physical presence in an approved school, effectively prohibiting homeschooling and ensuring state oversight of the educational environment. The United States and South Korea mandate education but allow various delivery mechanisms including private schools and (in the U.S.) homeschooling. The ECHR has validated both approaches, finding Germany's attendance requirement compatible with the Convention in Konrad.

### Philosophical Foundations

Germany's strict attendance requirement reflects a communitarian philosophy: the school is not merely an institution for knowledge transmission but a vehicle for social integration, democratic citizenship, and the prevention of parallel societies. The U.S. approach reflects a more individualist philosophy, with Pierce and Yoder establishing robust parental rights that constrain state authority over educational choice. South Korea's system reflects a developmental state philosophy, with education viewed primarily as an engine of national economic growth and social mobility.

### Enforcement Intensity

Germany enforces compulsory attendance most vigorously, with criminal penalties and even physical enforcement (Schulzwang) as last-resort measures. The United States' enforcement is highly variable, with some states pursuing truancy aggressively (including parental imprisonment in extreme cases) and others applying minimal consequences. South Korea's near-universal compliance makes enforcement largely unnecessary.

### Quality and Outcomes

All three national jurisdictions achieve high levels of educational attainment, but through different mechanisms. South Korea achieves top PISA rankings through a combination of compulsory education, intensive private tutoring, and cultural emphasis on academic achievement. Germany's dual system (academic and vocational tracks) produces strong vocational outcomes. The United States shows the greatest internal variation in outcomes, with significant disparities by state, district, and socioeconomic status.
