## Summary

Insurance fraud -- the deliberate deception of an insurance company or insured party for financial gain -- is a pervasive form of financial crime that costs the global economy hundreds of billions of dollars annually. Despite universal condemnation, the legal treatment of insurance fraud varies considerably across jurisdictions. South Korea addresses insurance fraud through both the general fraud provision of Article 347 of the Criminal Act and the dedicated Special Act on the Prevention of Insurance Fraud, which imposes penalties of up to 10 years imprisonment or life imprisonment for large-scale offenses exceeding KRW 5 billion. The United States combats insurance fraud through a layered federal and state framework, with 18 U.S.C. section 1347 targeting health care fraud (up to 10 years, or life if death results) and nearly all 50 states maintaining dedicated insurance fraud statutes with their own penalty structures. Germany prosecutes insurance fraud as a species of general fraud (Betrug) under section 263 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), carrying up to 5 years imprisonment in standard cases and up to 10 years for aggravated forms, supplemented by section 265 StGB which separately criminalizes the deliberate destruction of insured property (Versicherungsmissbrauch). At the supranational level, the European Union's PIF Directive (Directive 2017/1371) harmonizes minimum criminal standards for fraud against the EU's financial interests, while the ECHR's Article 6 fair trial guarantees impose procedural constraints on how Member States may investigate and prosecute insurance fraud cases.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea addresses insurance fraud through two principal legal instruments. The general fraud provision, Article 347 of the Criminal Act, punishes any person who deceives another to obtain property or pecuniary advantage with imprisonment for not more than 10 years or a fine not exceeding KRW 20 million. In addition, the Special Act on the Prevention of Insurance Fraud, originally enacted in 2016 and significantly amended thereafter, provides a dedicated statutory framework specifically targeting insurance fraud with enhanced penalties and investigative mechanisms.

The Special Act defines insurance fraud as any act of intentionally causing an insured event, or making a false claim for insurance benefits by exaggerating or fabricating losses. Critically, the Act extends criminal liability not only to direct perpetrators but also to those who solicit, broker, entice, or advertise insurance fraud, closing a loophole that previously allowed intermediaries to escape punishment.

### Penalties

| Offense Category | Penalty |
|-----------------|---------|
| Standard insurance fraud | Up to 10 years imprisonment or fine up to KRW 50 million |
| Fraud profits KRW 500 million to KRW 5 billion | Imprisonment of at least 3 years |
| Fraud profits exceeding KRW 5 billion | Life imprisonment or imprisonment of at least 5 years |
| Habitual offenders | Penalty aggravated by up to one half |
| Soliciting or advertising insurance fraud | Same penalties as direct perpetrators |
| Non-compliance with investigation requests | Fine up to KRW 5 million |

Where profits from insurance fraud are at least KRW 500 million, fines corresponding to not more than the full profits from the insurance fraud may be imposed concurrently with imprisonment. Additionally, convicted individuals whose fraud profits exceed KRW 500 million face employment restrictions: they may not be employed for up to one year by financial companies, State-invested or State-assisted institutions, or enterprises closely related to the convicted offense.

### Elements of the Offense

To establish insurance fraud under Korean law, the prosecution must prove:

1. **Deception or Fabrication:** The accused intentionally caused an insured event, fabricated a non-existent insured event, or exaggerated the scope of an actual insured event.
2. **Claim for Benefits:** The accused made a claim for insurance benefits based on the fraudulent event.
3. **Intent:** The accused acted with the specific intention of obtaining insurance proceeds to which they were not entitled.

### Landmark Cases

**Supreme Court Decision on Electric Kickboard Accident (2025):** On September 25, 2025, the Korean Supreme Court's 3rd Criminal Division reversed an appellate court acquittal for a defendant charged with violating the Special Act on the Prevention of Insurance Fraud. The defendant had been involved in an electric kickboard accident but concealed the full circumstances of the incident when filing an insurance claim. The Supreme Court held that concealing material details of an accident -- even where a genuine accident occurred -- constitutes insurance fraud, significantly broadening the scope of actionable conduct under the Special Act.

**Rising Auto Insurance Fraud (2025):** Despite the strengthened penalties under the amended Special Act, detected auto insurance fraud in South Korea reached KRW 570 billion (approximately $388 million) in 2024, continuing an upward trend that has prompted calls for further legislative reform, including mandatory reimbursement of fraudulent payouts upon conviction and harsher penalties for insurance industry insiders who participate in or facilitate fraud schemes.

### Statute of Limitations

The criminal limitation period for fraud offenses in South Korea is 10 years from the date of the offense. If the perpetrator flees abroad for the purpose of avoiding punishment, the statute of limitations is suspended during the period of absence and resumes upon their return to Korea.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States addresses insurance fraud through a dual federal-state framework. At the federal level, the primary statute is 18 U.S.C. section 1347, which criminalizes health care fraud -- the most commonly prosecuted form of insurance fraud at the federal level. Additionally, the general federal mail fraud (18 U.S.C. section 1341) and wire fraud (18 U.S.C. section 1343) statutes serve as powerful tools for prosecuting insurance fraud schemes that involve use of the mails or interstate communications.

At the state level, 48 of the 50 states have enacted dedicated insurance fraud statutes. Notable examples include:

- **New York Penal Law Article 176:** Establishes five degrees of insurance fraud based on the value of the fraudulently obtained property, ranging from a Class A misdemeanor (fifth degree, under $1,000) to a Class B felony (first degree, exceeding $1 million).
- **California Penal Code section 550:** Criminalizes the knowing presentation or preparation of false or fraudulent insurance claims, with penalties including fines up to $150,000 and imprisonment for up to 5 years.

### Penalties

**Federal Penalties (18 U.S.C. section 1347):**

| Circumstance | Maximum Imprisonment | Maximum Fine |
|-------------|---------------------|-------------|
| Standard health care fraud | 10 years | $250,000 (individual) / $500,000 (organization) |
| Fraud resulting in serious bodily injury | 20 years | $250,000 |
| Fraud resulting in death | Life imprisonment | $250,000 |
| Conspiracy to commit health care fraud (section 1349) | Same as underlying offense | Same as underlying offense |

**New York State Penalties (Penal Law Art. 176):**

| Degree | Value Threshold | Classification | Maximum Imprisonment |
|--------|----------------|----------------|---------------------|
| Fifth degree | Under $1,000 | Class A misdemeanor | 1 year |
| Fourth degree | $1,000 - $3,000 | Class E felony | 4 years |
| Third degree | $3,000 - $50,000 | Class D felony | 7 years |
| Second degree | $50,000 - $1,000,000 | Class C felony | 15 years |
| First degree | Over $1,000,000 | Class B felony | 25 years |

### Elements of the Offense

Under 18 U.S.C. section 1347, the prosecution must prove beyond a reasonable doubt:

1. **Scheme to Defraud:** The defendant knowingly devised or participated in a scheme or artifice to defraud a health care benefit program.
2. **Material Misrepresentation:** The scheme involved the use of false or fraudulent pretenses, representations, or promises regarding a material fact.
3. **Knowledge and Intent:** The defendant acted knowingly and willfully, with the specific intent to defraud.
4. **Connection to Health Care Program:** The scheme was directed at a health care benefit program, including Medicare, Medicaid, private health insurance, or any other public or private plan affecting commerce.

### Landmark Cases

**Martin Frankel Insurance Fraud Scheme (1991-1999):** Martin Frankel orchestrated one of the largest insurance fraud schemes in American history, involving the acquisition and systematic looting of insurance companies across multiple states. Frankel gained control of a small securities firm and created a fraudulent trust to purchase insurers, then converted their actuarial reserves to cash -- ostensibly for Treasury bond trading -- while actually stealing and laundering the proceeds through secret accounts. He ultimately acquired and looted seven insurance companies before fleeing to Germany in 1999. After extradition, Frankel pleaded guilty in May 2002 to 24 federal counts including securities fraud, wire fraud, racketeering, and conspiracy. He was sentenced to approximately 17 years (200 months) in federal prison, with proven losses exceeding $200 million.

**State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Co. v. Campbell (2003):** In this landmark U.S. Supreme Court case (538 U.S. 408), the Court addressed the constitutional limits of punitive damages in insurance bad faith cases. The Utah Supreme Court had reinstated a $145 million punitive damages award against State Farm for its fraudulent claims handling practices. The U.S. Supreme Court reversed, holding that a punitive damages award of 145 times the compensatory damages violated the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment, establishing that single-digit multipliers are more likely to comport with due process.

### Enforcement

The U.S. Department of Justice, the FBI, and the Department of Health and Human Services Office of Inspector General jointly enforce federal insurance fraud laws. The annual Health Care Fraud and Abuse Control Program has recovered billions of dollars -- over $2.2 billion in fiscal year 2023 alone. State-level enforcement is conducted by state attorneys general and dedicated insurance fraud bureaus.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany does not have a dedicated insurance fraud statute. Instead, insurance fraud (Versicherungsbetrug) is prosecuted as a special form of the general fraud offense under section 263 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB). Additionally, section 265 StGB (Versicherungsmissbrauch, or insurance abuse) separately criminalizes the deliberate destruction, damaging, or concealment of insured property with the intent to obtain insurance benefits -- even before any fraudulent claim is actually filed.

**Section 263 StGB (Betrug / Fraud):** A person who, with the intent to obtain an unlawful material advantage for themselves or a third party, causes damage to the property of another by creating or maintaining an error through the pretense of false facts or the distortion or suppression of true facts, shall be punished with imprisonment for not more than 5 years or a fine.

**Section 265 StGB (Versicherungsmissbrauch / Insurance Abuse):** A person who destroys, damages, renders unusable, makes away with, or transfers to another a thing insured against destruction, damage, loss of utility, loss, or theft shall be punished with imprisonment for not more than 3 years or a fine. This offense is complete upon the destruction or disposal of the insured property, regardless of whether an insurance claim is subsequently filed.

### Elements of Insurance Fraud Under Section 263 StGB

German law requires proof of a strict four-element causal chain:

1. **Deception (Taeuschung):** The accused must have created or maintained a false belief -- for example, by reporting a fabricated or exaggerated insurance claim.
2. **Error (Irrtum):** The insurance company must have been misled by the deception into believing the reported claim was genuine and covered by the policy.
3. **Property Disposition (Vermoegensverfuegung):** The insurance company must have made a payment (or other property disposition) based on its erroneous belief.
4. **Financial Loss (Vermoegensnachteil):** The insurance company must have suffered an actual financial loss as a result of the disposition.

Each element must causally follow from the preceding one. A failure to establish any link in the chain results in acquittal on the fraud charge.

### Penalties

| Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|
| Standard fraud (section 263 I StGB) | Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine |
| Aggravated fraud (section 263 III StGB) -- including commercial fraud, gang-related fraud, or faking an insured event | 6 months to 10 years imprisonment |
| Insurance abuse (section 265 StGB) | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine |
| Attempted fraud | Punishable (section 263 II StGB) |

Aggravated cases under section 263 III StGB include situations where the perpetrator acts on a commercial basis (gewerbsmaessig), as a member of a criminal gang (bandenmassig), or causes a property loss of great extent (Vermoegensverlust grossen Ausmasses). The deliberate staging of a fake insured event (Vortaeuschen eines Versicherungsfalles) is specifically listed as a named example of a particularly serious case.

### Additional Consequences

Beyond criminal penalties, individuals convicted of insurance fraud in Germany face:

- Entry in the Federal Central Criminal Register (Bundeszentralregister)
- Civil liability for repayment of the fraudulently obtained insurance sum plus interest
- Immediate termination of the insurance contract by the insurer
- Placement on industry-shared fraud registries, making it difficult to obtain future insurance coverage
- Professional consequences, including potential loss of professional licenses

### Statute of Limitations

The statute of limitations for standard fraud under section 263 StGB is 5 years from completion of the offense. For aggravated fraud under section 263 III StGB, the limitation period extends to 10 years. Insurance abuse under section 265 StGB carries a 5-year limitation period.

## ECHR & EU

### European Union Framework

The European Union does not have a dedicated insurance fraud directive. Instead, the EU's approach to insurance fraud operates through three distinct but overlapping regulatory layers:

**1. The PIF Directive (Directive (EU) 2017/1371):**
The Directive on the fight against fraud to the Union's financial interests by means of criminal law -- known as the PIF Directive (Protection des Interets Financiers) -- was adopted on July 5, 2017, and is binding on all EU Member States except Denmark. While primarily aimed at fraud against the EU budget rather than private insurance fraud per se, it establishes important minimum standards for fraud prosecution across the EU:

- Member States must impose a maximum criminal penalty of at least 4 years imprisonment for fraud causing damage exceeding EUR 100,000 to the EU's financial interests, or in serious cases involving the common VAT system.
- For offenses involving damage below EUR 10,000, Member States may provide for non-criminal sanctions.
- Legal persons must be subject to "effective, proportionate and dissuasive" sanctions.
- Member States must establish a minimum limitation period of at least 5 years from the time the offense was committed (with a permissible derogation down to 3 years in certain circumstances, provided interruption or suspension mechanisms exist).

**2. The Solvency II Directive (Directive 2009/138/EU):**
This directive establishes the regulatory framework for insurance and reinsurance undertakings across the EU. While not a criminal law instrument, Solvency II requires insurers to maintain robust governance systems including internal controls, risk management, and compliance functions that serve as preventive measures against insurance fraud. Implementing Regulation (EU) 2015/2450 further specifies reporting requirements.

**3. The Insurance Distribution Directive (Directive (EU) 2016/97):**
Adopted on January 20, 2016 and required to be transposed by Member States by October 1, 2018, the IDD establishes minimum standards for insurance distribution, including transparency and disclosure obligations that indirectly combat fraudulent practices in insurance sales and claims handling.

### European Convention on Human Rights

The ECHR does not directly address insurance fraud, but its provisions impose critical procedural constraints on how Member States of the Council of Europe may investigate and prosecute insurance fraud cases:

**Article 6 -- Right to a Fair Trial:**
Any person charged with insurance fraud is entitled to the full panoply of Article 6 protections, including:

- The right to a fair and public hearing within a reasonable time by an independent and impartial tribunal.
- The presumption of innocence until proven guilty according to law.
- The right to be informed promptly of the nature and cause of the accusation.
- Adequate time and facilities for the preparation of a defense.
- The right to legal assistance, including free legal aid where the interests of justice so require.
- The right to examine witnesses and to obtain the attendance and examination of defense witnesses.

**Article 8 -- Right to Respect for Private Life:**
Investigative measures in insurance fraud cases -- such as surveillance, interception of communications, or access to personal financial records -- must be proportionate and prescribed by law. The ECHR has consistently held that interference with private life for law enforcement purposes must be "necessary in a democratic society."

**Article 1 of Protocol 1 -- Protection of Property:**
Where insurance fraud proceedings involve asset seizure, confiscation, or forfeiture, the state's actions must comply with the principle that no one shall be deprived of possessions except in the public interest and subject to conditions provided for by law.

### Enforcement Institutions

- **OLAF (European Anti-Fraud Office):** Investigates fraud, corruption, and serious misconduct within EU institutions and coordinates with national authorities on fraud affecting the EU budget.
- **EPPO (European Public Prosecutor's Office):** Established by Regulation (EU) 2017/1939, the EPPO has the authority to investigate and prosecute crimes against the EU's financial interests, including fraud, in participating Member States.
- **EIOPA (European Insurance and Occupational Pensions Authority):** While primarily a regulatory rather than criminal enforcement body, EIOPA coordinates supervisory activities across Member States and can issue warnings, recommendations, and guidelines related to insurance sector integrity.

### Landmark ECHR Jurisprudence Relevant to Fraud Prosecution

**Salabiaku v. France (1988):** The ECHR held that while legal presumptions of fact or law (such as a presumption of fraudulent intent) are not prohibited by the Convention, they must be confined within reasonable limits that take into account the importance of what is at stake and maintain the rights of the defense.

**Golder v. United Kingdom (1975):** Established the foundational principle that Article 6 encompasses not only the right to a fair hearing in existing proceedings but also the right of access to a court, which is directly relevant to defendants' ability to challenge insurance fraud allegations.

## Comparative Analysis

### Definitional Approaches

The four jurisdictions reflect fundamentally different approaches to defining insurance fraud as a criminal offense. South Korea and most U.S. states treat insurance fraud as a distinct statutory offense with its own elements and penalty structures, reflecting a legislative judgment that insurance fraud poses unique societal harms warranting specialized treatment. Germany, by contrast, subsumes insurance fraud within its general fraud statute (section 263 StGB), treating it as merely one application of the universal prohibition on deceptive acquisition of property -- though section 265 StGB carves out the preparatory act of destroying insured property as a separate offense. The EU operates at a meta-level, establishing minimum standards that Member States must implement through national legislation.

### Penalty Severity

South Korea imposes the most severe penalties for large-scale insurance fraud, with life imprisonment possible for schemes exceeding KRW 5 billion (approximately $3.7 million). The United States follows closely, with life imprisonment available under 18 U.S.C. section 1347 when health care fraud results in a patient's death, and state-level penalties reaching 25 years in New York for first-degree insurance fraud exceeding $1 million. Germany's penalties are comparatively moderate, with a 10-year maximum even in aggravated cases. The EU's PIF Directive sets only a floor of 4 years maximum imprisonment for serious fraud.

### Scope of Liability

South Korea's Special Act is notable for its broad scope of secondary liability, expressly criminalizing the solicitation, brokerage, enticement, and advertisement of insurance fraud at the same penalty level as direct perpetration. The United States achieves a similar result through conspiracy statutes (18 U.S.C. section 1349) and aiding and abetting provisions (18 U.S.C. section 2). Germany's approach is more restrained, relying on general complicity principles (sections 25-27 StGB) rather than specialized provisions.

### Investigative Powers

All jurisdictions grant substantial investigative powers for insurance fraud cases, but they differ in the degree of judicial oversight required. The ECHR imposes the most stringent proportionality requirements through its Article 8 jurisprudence, requiring that surveillance and financial investigation measures be both prescribed by law and necessary in a democratic society. South Korea's Special Act grants investigators specific powers to request information from insurance companies and related entities, with fines for non-compliance. The United States provides the broadest investigative toolkit, including grand jury subpoenas, administrative subpoenas, and the extensive discovery powers of federal regulatory agencies.

### Civil vs. Criminal Enforcement

A distinctive feature of U.S. insurance fraud enforcement is the extensive use of civil remedies alongside criminal prosecution. The False Claims Act (31 U.S.C. sections 3729-3733) allows both the government and private whistleblowers (qui tam relators) to bring civil actions for fraudulent insurance claims submitted to government programs, with treble damages and per-claim penalties. No comparable qui tam mechanism exists in South Korea, Germany, or the EU framework. Germany relies primarily on criminal prosecution and private civil suits for damages, while the EU leaves the balance between civil and criminal enforcement to Member State discretion.
