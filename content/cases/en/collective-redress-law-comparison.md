## Summary

Collective redress -- the procedural mechanisms through which groups of persons who have suffered harm from the same or similar conduct may seek judicial relief collectively rather than through individual proceedings -- represents one of the most significant areas of divergence in comparative civil procedure. The fundamental design choice between opt-out systems (where all affected persons are automatically included unless they affirmatively exclude themselves) and opt-in systems (where affected persons must affirmatively join the proceedings) shapes the effectiveness, fairness, and economic dynamics of collective litigation. South Korea operates a bifurcated system, with the Securities-Related Class Action Act (증권관련 집단소송법, 2005) providing opt-out class actions for securities fraud and the Consumer Fundamental Act (소비자기본법) enabling consumer group actions through designated organizations, while the pending General Class Action Act (일반 집단소송법) would expand opt-out class actions to additional subject areas. The United States maintains the world's most developed opt-out class action system under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23, supported by the contingency fee structure, opt-out mechanisms, and the availability of punitive and treble damages, generating annual class action settlements estimated at $3-5 billion. Germany has developed a multi-layered system including the Capital Markets Model Case Act (KapMuG, 2005), the Model Declaratory Action (Musterfeststellungsklage, 2018), and the implementation of the EU Representative Actions Directive through amendments effective in 2023. At the EU level, Directive 2020/1828 on representative actions for the protection of the collective interests of consumers, with a transposition deadline of June 25, 2023, represents the most significant supranational effort to harmonize collective redress, while the ECHR addresses collective access to justice through Article 6 (right to a fair trial) and Article 13 (right to an effective remedy).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's collective redress landscape reflects a cautious, sector-specific approach to group litigation, with the securities class action serving as the sole true opt-out mechanism while consumer protection relies on organizational representative actions.

### Securities-Related Class Action Act (증권관련 집단소송법)

**Standing and Certification:** To initiate a securities class action, the class must consist of at least 50 members, and the named plaintiffs must hold at least 0.01% of the outstanding shares of a listed company. The court evaluates commonality, typicality, adequacy of representation, and superiority of the class action mechanism before granting permission (허가결정).

**Opt-Out Mechanism:** Once certified, all persons falling within the class definition are automatically included unless they file an exclusion request (제외신고) within the period designated by the court. This opt-out structure follows the American model and contrasts with the opt-in approach prevailing in most civil law jurisdictions.

**Settlement Approval:** Court approval is required for class action settlements. The court evaluates fairness, adequacy, and reasonableness, considering the strength of the claims, the amount of the settlement, the views of class members, and the recommendation of the lead counsel. Notice of proposed settlement must be provided to all class members.

**Attorney Fees:** Class counsel's fees are determined by the court, typically based on a percentage of the total recovery (generally lower than US contingency fees) or a reasonable hourly rate. Success fee arrangements are permitted but subject to court approval and are less generous than US contingency fee norms.

### Consumer Group Actions

**Consumer Fundamental Act (소비자기본법):** Designated consumer organizations and the Korea Consumer Agency (한국소비자원) may bring group actions (단체소송) on behalf of affected consumers. These are injunctive in nature -- consumer organizations may seek cessation of unfair practices but cannot directly recover damages on behalf of individual consumers.

**Korea Consumer Agency Dispute Resolution:** The KCA operates a dispute resolution mechanism (소비자분쟁조정위원회) that processes over 30,000 consumer disputes annually. When the KCA's Consumer Dispute Resolution Committee issues a mediation decision and both parties accept, it has the binding effect of a judicial settlement.

### Proposed General Class Action Act (일반 집단소송법)

The General Class Action Act, proposed in various forms since 2020, would establish an opt-out class action mechanism applicable to civil claims generally, including consumer protection, environmental harm, data privacy breaches, and corporate misconduct. Key features of the latest proposal include:

- Lowered class size threshold (from 50 to a less restrictive standard);
- Expanded subject matter scope beyond securities;
- Enhanced attorney fee provisions, including greater acceptance of success fees;
- Mandatory opt-out notice through multiple channels;
- Court-supervised settlement approval process.

The legislation has faced sustained opposition from the Korea Employers Federation (한국경영자총협회) and the Federation of Korean Industries (전국경제인연합회), which argue it would encourage frivolous litigation, increase corporate compliance costs, and transplant American-style litigation culture into Korea's civil law system.

### Key Developments

**Seoul Central District Court, 2022GaHap500123 (Samsung BioLogics Accounting Fraud):** One of the largest securities class actions in Korean history, involving allegations that Samsung BioLogics Co. manipulated accounting valuations ahead of its subsidiary Samsung Bioepis's IPO. The case, with estimated damages exceeding KRW 1 trillion, has become a test case for the effectiveness of Korea's securities class action system.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States operates the world's most extensive and consequential class action system. The combination of opt-out class certification, contingency fees, broad discovery, jury trials, and the availability of punitive and treble damages creates uniquely powerful incentives for collective litigation.

### FRCP Rule 23 Framework

**Rule 23(a) Prerequisites:** (1) Numerosity -- the class is so numerous that joinder is impracticable; (2) Commonality -- there are questions of law or fact common to the class; (3) Typicality -- the claims of the representative parties are typical of the class; (4) Adequacy -- the representative parties will fairly and adequately protect the interests of the class.

**Rule 23(b) Categories:** (b)(1) -- individual adjudication would create inconsistent obligations or impair non-parties; (b)(2) -- injunctive or declaratory relief appropriate for the class as a whole; (b)(3) -- common questions predominate and class action is superior.

**Rule 23(b)(3) and the Opt-Out Right:** For damages class actions under (b)(3), class members must receive the best notice practicable and must be given the opportunity to opt out. Absent class members who do not opt out are bound by the judgment. The opt-out mechanism is the signature feature of the American class action system.

### Class Action Fairness Act of 2005 (CAFA)

CAFA expanded federal jurisdiction over class actions by establishing: minimal diversity (any class member diverse from any defendant); at least 100 class members; and aggregate amount in controversy exceeding $5 million. CAFA also regulates settlements, requiring additional scrutiny of coupon settlements and those where class counsel receive disproportionate fees relative to class recovery.

### Key Supreme Court Decisions

**Wal-Mart Stores, Inc. v. Dukes, 564 U.S. 338 (2011):** The Court heightened the commonality standard, requiring that class claims "depend upon a common contention" capable of classwide resolution. The Court decertified a class of 1.5 million female Wal-Mart employees alleging sex discrimination, finding that discretionary employment decisions across thousands of stores did not present sufficient common questions.

**Epic Systems Corp. v. Lewis, 584 U.S. 497 (2018):** The Court held that arbitration agreements requiring individual proceedings are enforceable under the Federal Arbitration Act, even when they effectively prevent class action participation. This decision has substantially reduced class action availability in employment and consumer contexts.

**TransUnion LLC v. Ramirez, 594 U.S. 413 (2021):** Only class members who suffered concrete harm have Article III standing. The Court reduced a certified class from 8,185 to 1,853 members, reinforcing the requirement for individualized injury analysis even within certified classes.

**Amgen Inc. v. Connecticut Retirement Plans and Trust Funds, 568 U.S. 455 (2013):** In the securities fraud context, the Court held that proof of materiality is not required at the class certification stage because it is a common question that will be resolved on a class-wide basis at trial or summary judgment.

### Multidistrict Litigation (MDL)

The Judicial Panel on Multidistrict Litigation (JPML) may transfer civil actions involving one or more common questions of fact to a single district for coordinated pretrial proceedings under 28 U.S.C. Section 1407. MDL consolidation has become the dominant mechanism for mass tort litigation, with MDLs accounting for over 70% of the federal civil caseload. Notable MDLs include the National Prescription Opiate Litigation (MDL 2804), the 3M Combat Arms Earplug Litigation (MDL 2885, with over 300,000 claims), and various pharmaceutical product liability MDLs.

### Settlement Dynamics

The vast majority of class actions settle rather than proceed to trial. The Class Action Fairness Act and Rule 23(e) (2018 amendment) impose enhanced settlement review requirements, including evaluation of whether the settlement is fair, reasonable, and adequate, and whether it treats class members equitably. Courts scrutinize the relationship between class recovery and attorney fees, cy pres distributions, and reversion clauses.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany has traditionally rejected American-style class actions, viewing them as incompatible with the civil law emphasis on individual legal autonomy (Dispositionsmaxime), the loser-pays cost system, and the absence of contingency fees. Instead, Germany has developed alternative collective redress mechanisms.

### Capital Markets Model Case Act (KapMuG)

The Kapitalanleger-Musterverfahrensgesetz (KapMuG), enacted in 2005, provides a model case procedure specifically for capital markets disputes. When at least ten individual plaintiffs file claims raising the same factual or legal issues, the court may select a model case (Musterverfahren) for initial determination of common questions.

**Procedure:** A model claimant (Musterklager) is selected from among the plaintiffs. The model case decision on common questions binds all pending individual cases (Aussetzungsbeschlüsse). After the model case decision, individual cases resume for determination of individual issues (causation, damages).

**Deutsche Telekom Model Case (2005-2024):** Approximately 17,000 individual investor claims were consolidated under KapMuG. After nearly two decades of litigation through multiple court levels, the case illustrated both the advantages (efficient resolution of common questions) and disadvantages (extreme duration) of the model case approach.

### Model Declaratory Action (Musterfeststellungsklage)

**ZPO Sections 606-614:** Effective November 1, 2018, the Musterfeststellungsklage permits qualified consumer organizations (qualifizierte Einrichtungen listed in the UKlaG register) to seek a declaratory judgment on common factual and legal questions affecting at least 50 consumers who register in a publicly accessible claims register (Klageregister) maintained by the Bundesamt für Justiz (Federal Office of Justice).

**Key Features:**
- **Organizational Standing:** Only qualified consumer organizations may bring the action; individual consumers register as affected parties.
- **Opt-In Registration:** Consumers must affirmatively register within the designated period to benefit from the declaratory judgment. Registration suspends the limitation period.
- **Declaratory Effect:** The judgment establishes binding findings on common questions but does not directly award damages. Individual consumers must pursue separate enforcement proceedings or negotiate settlements based on the declaratory findings.
- **Settlement:** The court may approve a settlement between the qualified entity and the defendant, binding on all registered consumers who do not opt out within one month of settlement publication.

**Volkswagen Dieselgate Musterfeststellungsklage:** The first and most prominent Musterfeststellungsklage was brought by the Verbraucherzentrale Bundesverband (vzbv) against Volkswagen AG in November 2018. Approximately 460,000 consumers registered. In February 2020, the parties reached a settlement of approximately EUR 830 million, with individual payments averaging EUR 1,350-6,257 depending on vehicle model and age. Approximately 260,000 consumers accepted the settlement; those who rejected it retained their individual claims.

### EU Representative Actions Directive Implementation

Germany transposed Directive 2020/1828 through amendments to the UKlaG and the introduction of new provisions in the ZPO, effective in 2023. The German implementation:

- **Redress Actions:** Qualified entities may seek redress measures (Abhilfemaßnahmen) on behalf of consumers, including damages, price reductions, and contract termination.
- **Opt-In Model:** Germany adopted an opt-in model for redress actions, requiring individual consumers to register or authorize the qualified entity to act on their behalf.
- **Cross-Border Actions:** Qualified entities designated in other EU member states may bring representative actions in German courts.
- **Litigation Funding:** Third-party litigation funding is permitted but subject to safeguards, including prohibition of funder influence on procedural decisions and transparency requirements.

### Fee Structure and Economic Incentives

Germany's loser-pays cost system (Kostentragungspflicht) and prohibition of contingency fees (Erfolgshonorar, with limited exceptions under Section 4a RVG) create fundamentally different economic incentives for collective litigation compared to the US. Third-party litigation funding (Prozessfinanzierung) has partially filled the gap, with specialized funders (Burford Capital, Therium, Fortress) financing large-scale collective claims in exchange for a share of the recovery (typically 20-35%).

## ECHR & EU

### EU Directive 2020/1828 on Representative Actions

Adopted on November 25, 2020, with a transposition deadline of June 25, 2023 (applicable from June 25, 2023), the Directive represents the EU's most comprehensive effort to establish a minimum standard for collective redress across all member states.

**Scope (Article 2):** The Directive covers representative actions for the protection of the collective interests of consumers in areas including consumer protection, data protection (GDPR), financial services, travel and tourism, energy, telecommunications, environment, health, and passenger rights. The scope is defined by reference to a list of EU instruments in Annex I, which may be updated by the Commission.

**Qualified Entities (Articles 4-5):** Only designated qualified entities may bring representative actions. For domestic actions, member states designate entities meeting criteria of public interest purpose, non-profit character, and organizational capacity. For cross-border actions, entities must additionally demonstrate 12 months of prior activity, financial capacity, independence from third-party funders, and transparency regarding funding and governance.

**Types of Measures (Article 7-9):**
- **Injunctive Measures (Article 8):** Qualified entities may seek orders requiring the trader to cease or prohibit infringing practices, including interim measures. No individual consumer participation is required.
- **Redress Measures (Article 9):** Qualified entities may seek orders requiring the trader to provide redress, including compensation, repair, replacement, price reduction, contract termination, or reimbursement. Member states determine whether redress actions follow an opt-in or opt-out model.

**Safeguards (Articles 10-12):**
- **Loser-Pays Rule (Article 12):** The Directive does not require member states to depart from their general cost allocation rules.
- **No Punitive Damages (Article 10):** Punitive, exemplary, or other damages exceeding compensation for actual harm are prohibited.
- **Litigation Funding (Article 10(3)):** Member states must ensure that funded representative actions are not influenced by the funder in ways detrimental to consumers, and must prevent conflicts of interest.

### Member State Implementation Approaches

**Netherlands -- WAMCA (2020):** The Netherlands adopted the Wet Afwikkeling Massaschade in Collectieve Actie (WAMCA) in January 2020, establishing an opt-out collective damages action. Under WAMCA, representative organizations may bring collective damages claims before the Amsterdam District Court, which appoints an exclusive representative for the class. The opt-out applies to persons domiciled in the Netherlands; those domiciled elsewhere must opt in.

**France -- Action de Groupe:** France introduced the action de groupe in 2014 (initially for consumer and competition matters, expanded to environmental, health, data protection, and discrimination in 2016). The French system uses a distinctive two-phase procedure: first, the court establishes liability and defines the group; second, individual group members opt in to claim compensation.

**Portugal -- Popular Action (Ação Popular):** Portugal maintains a broad opt-out popular action (Lei No. 83/95), which permits any citizen, association, or foundation to bring claims in the public interest, including for consumer protection, environmental protection, and cultural heritage.

**Italy -- Class Action (Azione di Classe):** Italy reformed its class action framework effective May 19, 2021, expanding standing beyond consumer organizations to individual class members and adopting an opt-in mechanism with judicial evaluation of admissibility.

### ECHR Jurisprudence on Collective Access to Justice

**Article 6 (Right to a Fair Trial):** The ECHR has recognized that collective litigation mechanisms serve the right of access to court, particularly for low-value claims where individual litigation would be economically irrational.

**Gorraiz Lizarraga and Others v. Spain (Application No. 62543/00, April 27, 2004):** The Court held that associations formed to defend the collective interests of their members have standing under Article 6 to bring claims, even when individual members could theoretically bring their own claims.

**Lithgow and Others v. United Kingdom (Application No. 9006/80, July 8, 1986):** The Court recognized that grouping claims with common issues for efficient resolution does not violate the right to a fair hearing, provided individual rights are adequately protected.

**Collectif Stop Mines de Charbon v. France (Decision of December 12, 2023):** The Court addressed whether environmental associations have victim status under Article 34 for environmental harm affecting their members, holding that associations may claim to be "victims" when the environmental harm directly affects their organizational mission and their members' rights.

### ECHR and Due Process in Opt-Out Systems

The compatibility of opt-out collective litigation with ECHR Article 6 remains debated. Critics argue that binding absent parties who did not consent to the proceedings violates the principle of party autonomy. Proponents argue that adequate notice and an opportunity to opt out satisfy due process requirements, and that the alternative -- allowing defendants to harm large numbers of people with impunity because individual claims are too small to litigate -- would itself constitute a denial of access to justice.

## Comparative Analysis

### Opt-In vs. Opt-Out: The Fundamental Design Choice

The opt-in/opt-out distinction is the single most consequential structural choice in collective redress design. Opt-out systems (US, Netherlands, Korean securities class actions) maximize participation and deterrent effect but raise concerns about binding absent parties. Opt-in systems (Germany's Musterfeststellungsklage, most EU implementations, France's action de groupe) respect individual autonomy but consistently achieve low participation rates (often below 10% of affected persons), limiting both compensation coverage and deterrent effect.

The Volkswagen Dieselgate experience illustrates this dynamic: approximately 460,000 of an estimated 2.4 million affected consumers registered for the Musterfeststellungsklage (approximately 19%), and ultimately approximately 260,000 accepted the settlement. An opt-out system would have covered all 2.4 million consumers automatically.

### Economic Incentives and Fee Structures

The American contingency fee system eliminates financial risk for plaintiffs and creates strong incentives for attorneys to pursue class actions. This system has no equivalent in civil law jurisdictions, where loser-pays rules and prohibitions on contingency fees create significant financial barriers. Third-party litigation funding has partially addressed this gap in Europe, but funding is typically available only for large, high-value claims with strong prospects of success, leaving smaller consumer claims under-served.

### Qualified Entity Gatekeeping vs. Private Attorney General

The EU's qualified entity model restricts standing to designated organizations meeting specific criteria, creating a gatekeeping function intended to prevent abusive litigation. The US model, where any class member with standing may serve as class representative (with adequacy reviewed by the court), democratizes access but has been criticized for generating attorney-driven litigation. South Korea occupies an intermediate position, with organizational standing for consumer actions and individual standing (with minimum thresholds) for securities class actions.

### Cross-Border Collective Redress

Cross-border collective litigation remains underdeveloped in all jurisdictions. The EU Directive's cross-border representative action mechanism represents the most advanced effort, enabling qualified entities from one member state to act in other member states. The US has limited extraterritorial class action reach following Morrison v. National Australia Bank, 561 U.S. 247 (2010), which restricted the application of US securities laws to domestic transactions. South Korea's class action system has no cross-border mechanism.

### Adequacy of Existing Mechanisms

The effectiveness gap between the US system and all other jurisdictions remains substantial. Annual class action settlements in the US exceed $3-5 billion, while collective redress recoveries in Europe and Korea are orders of magnitude smaller. This gap reflects not only procedural differences but also substantive differences in damages availability (treble damages, punitive damages), fee structures, and judicial and cultural attitudes toward collective litigation.
