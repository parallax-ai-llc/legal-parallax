## Summary

Constitutional frameworks in the Global South represent radically different philosophical foundations from the Euro-American liberal constitutionalist tradition, reflecting distinct histories of colonialism, revolution, authoritarian rule, and communal governance that have produced constitutional texts and practices with their own internal logic and legitimacy claims. China's Constitution of the People's Republic of China (中华人民共和国宪法, 1982, as amended through 2018) establishes a socialist constitutional order under the leadership of the Communist Party of China (CPC), enshrining the "Four Cardinal Principles" (四项基本原则) -- the socialist road, the people's democratic dictatorship, CPC leadership, and Marxism-Leninism-Mao Zedong Thought -- as the foundational political framework, with constitutional review exercised not by courts but by the National People's Congress Standing Committee (NPCSC) under Articles 62 and 67. India's Constitution (1950), the world's longest written constitution with 470 articles, 25 parts, and 12 schedules (as amended through 2024), creates a unique hybrid of British parliamentary democracy, American judicial review, Irish directive principles, and indigenous concepts, with the Supreme Court asserting the "basic structure doctrine" in Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973) to limit Parliament's amendment power. South Africa's Constitution (1996) embodies "transformative constitutionalism" -- a concept articulated by Karl Klare and developed by the Constitutional Court under justices such as Pius Langa and Albie Sachs -- that obligates the state to actively dismantle the legacy of apartheid through substantive equality, socio-economic rights (Sections 26-29), and the recognition of customary law (Section 211). Brazil's Constituicao Federal (1988), known as the "Citizen Constitution" (Constituicao Cidada), was drafted through extensive popular participation after 21 years of military dictatorship (1964-1985), containing 250 articles in the permanent text plus 97 articles of transitional provisions (Ato das Disposicoes Constitucionais Transitorias, ADCT), and has been amended 132 times through 2025. The Philippine Constitution (1987) was drafted after the People Power Revolution that ousted Ferdinand Marcos, incorporating extensive social justice provisions, a ban on political dynasties (Article II, Section 26), and the writ of amparo, while the Bangsamoro Organic Law (Republic Act No. 11054, 2018) created asymmetric constitutional autonomy for the Bangsamoro region. Indonesia's Undang-Undang Dasar 1945 (UUD 1945), originally a brief revolutionary document, was substantially amended (1999-2002) to create the Mahkamah Konstitusi (Constitutional Court), entrench Pancasila as the state philosophy, and establish direct presidential elections, while navigating the relationship between civil law, adat (customary law), and Islamic law (hukum Islam).

## China (中国)

### Constitutional Framework

China's constitutional system operates within a fundamentally different paradigm from liberal constitutionalism, one that scholars such as Jiang Shigong and Qiang Shigong have termed "Party-State constitutionalism" (党国宪政), where the CPC's leadership role is not merely political convention but a constitutional norm enshrined in Article 1, paragraph 2 (as amended in 2018): "The defining feature of socialism with Chinese characteristics is the leadership of the Communist Party of China."

**Constitution of the People's Republic of China (中华人民共和国宪法, 1982):** The current constitution, the fourth since 1949, was adopted on December 4, 1982, and has been amended five times (1988, 1993, 1999, 2004, 2018). Key structural features include:

- **Preamble:** The lengthy preamble (approximately 1,800 characters) establishes the historical narrative of Chinese revolution and development, names the Four Cardinal Principles, and since 2018 incorporates Xi Jinping Thought on Socialism with Chinese Characteristics for a New Era (习近平新时代中国特色社会主义思想).
- **Article 1:** Declares the PRC a "socialist state under the people's democratic dictatorship" (人民民主专政). The 2018 amendment added CPC leadership as a "defining feature" of Chinese socialism.
- **Article 2:** Establishes the National People's Congress as the highest organ of state power, through which the people exercise state power.
- **Article 5:** Establishes the principle of "governing the country according to law" (依法治国) and that no organization or individual may enjoy the privilege of being above the Constitution. However, the 2018 amendment to Article 1 creates an interpretive tension regarding the CPC's constitutional status.
- **Chapter II (Articles 33-56):** Enumerates fundamental rights and duties, including equality before the law (Article 33), freedom of speech, press, assembly, association, procession, and demonstration (Article 35), freedom of religious belief (Article 36), and the right to education (Article 46). Notably, these rights are paired with corresponding duties (义务), reflecting the socialist conception of rights as relational rather than purely individual.

### Constitutional Review

China does not practice judicial review of legislation. Constitutional review (合宪性审查, hexianxing shencha) is exercised by the NPCSC under Article 67, which empowers it to "interpret the Constitution and supervise its enforcement." The Legislation Law (立法法, 2000, revised 2015 and 2023) establishes a recording and review system (备案审查制度, bei'an shencha zhidu) under Articles 99-104, allowing citizens to petition the NPCSC to review the constitutionality of administrative regulations and local legislation.

In practice, the NPCSC has been reluctant to issue formal constitutional interpretations. The landmark 2003 Sun Zhigang incident (孙志刚事件) -- where a migrant worker died in custody under the Custody and Repatriation system -- prompted citizens' constitutional review petitions to the NPCSC, ultimately leading the State Council to abolish the regulation through administrative rather than constitutional channels (State Council Decree No. 381, 2003). The 2017 establishment of the Constitution and Law Committee (宪法和法律委员会) of the NPC, replacing the former Law Committee, signals a modest institutional strengthening of constitutional review.

### Relationship with International Law

Article 142 of the General Principles of Civil Law (1986) and subsequent practice establish that international treaties to which China is a party prevail over conflicting domestic law, except where China has declared reservations. China has ratified the International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights (ICESCR, 2001) but has signed without ratifying the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR, signed 1998).

## India (भारत)

### Constitutional Framework

India's Constitution, which came into effect on January 26, 1950, is the product of a Constituent Assembly that sat from 1946 to 1949 under the chairmanship of Dr. B.R. Ambedkar. It represents what Granville Austin termed a "seamless web" of national unity, social revolution, and democratic governance.

**Key Structural Features:**

- **Preamble:** Declares India a "sovereign socialist secular democratic republic" (the words "socialist" and "secular" were added by the 42nd Amendment, 1976), committed to securing justice, liberty, equality, and fraternity.
- **Part III (Fundamental Rights, Articles 12-35):** Enforceable through writ jurisdiction under Article 32 (Supreme Court) and Article 226 (High Courts). Includes equality before the law (Article 14), prohibition of discrimination on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth (Article 15), abolition of untouchability (Article 17), and freedoms under Article 19 (speech, assembly, association, movement, residence, profession), subject to "reasonable restrictions."
- **Part IV (Directive Principles of State Policy, Articles 36-51):** Non-justiciable but "fundamental in the governance of the country." Key directives include equal pay for equal work (Article 39(d)), right to work (Article 41), uniform civil code (Article 44, never implemented due to personal law controversies), and protection of the environment (Article 48A, added by 42nd Amendment).
- **Part IVA (Fundamental Duties, Articles 51A):** Added by the 42nd Amendment (1976), establishes 11 fundamental duties of citizens, including respecting the Constitution, national flag, and national anthem, and promoting harmony transcending religious, linguistic, and regional diversities.

### The Basic Structure Doctrine

The Supreme Court's decision in Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala, AIR 1973 SC 1461 (decided by a 13-judge bench, 7-6 majority), established that Parliament's amendment power under Article 368 is subject to an implied limitation: amendments cannot destroy the "basic structure" of the Constitution. This doctrine -- which has no textual basis in the Constitution itself -- has been described by scholars such as Sudhir Krishnaswamy as the "most important constitutional development in postcolonial legal history."

The basic structure includes (as elaborated in subsequent cases): supremacy of the Constitution, republican and democratic form of government, secular character, separation of powers, federal character, sovereignty and integrity of India, dignity of the individual (Minerva Mills v. Union of India, AIR 1980 SC 1789), unity and integrity of the nation, judicial review (L. Chandra Kumar v. Union of India, AIR 1997 SC 1125), and rule of law.

### Public Interest Litigation (PIL)

Indian constitutional jurisprudence pioneered the liberalization of standing requirements through PIL, beginning with S.P. Gupta v. Union of India, AIR 1982 SC 149, where Justice P.N. Bhagwati held that any member of the public acting bona fide could approach the court on behalf of those unable to access justice. This epistolary jurisdiction allowed letters addressed to the court to be treated as writ petitions, dramatically expanding access to constitutional remedies for marginalized populations.

### Personal Law and Constitutional Tensions

The coexistence of constitutionally guaranteed equality (Articles 14-15) with religion-based personal laws governing marriage, divorce, inheritance, and adoption creates ongoing constitutional tension. The Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Marriage) Act, 2019, which criminalized triple talaq (instant divorce), followed the Supreme Court's decision in Shayara Bano v. Union of India (2017) 9 SCC 1, where a 3-2 majority struck down triple talaq under Article 14, though the judges differed on their reasoning.

## Africa (Nigeria and South Africa)

### South Africa: Transformative Constitutionalism

The Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996 (Act 108 of 1996), represents what Karl Klare described in his seminal 1998 article as "transformative constitutionalism" -- a long-term project of constitutional enactment, interpretation, and enforcement committed to transforming a country's political and social institutions and power relationships in a democratic, participatory, and egalitarian direction.

**Bill of Rights (Chapter 2):** Sections 7-39 enumerate extensive rights, notable for including justiciable socio-economic rights:

- **Section 26 (Housing):** "Everyone has the right to have access to adequate housing." In Government of the Republic of South Africa v. Grootboom, 2001 (1) SA 46 (CC), the Constitutional Court (per Yacoob J) held that the state must take reasonable legislative and other measures, within its available resources, to achieve the progressive realization of this right, establishing the "reasonableness review" standard.
- **Section 27 (Health care, food, water, social security):** Applied in Minister of Health v. Treatment Action Campaign, 2002 (5) SA 721 (CC), where the Constitutional Court ordered the government to provide nevirapine to prevent mother-to-child transmission of HIV.
- **Section 9 (Equality):** Prohibits unfair discrimination on listed and analogous grounds. The Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act 4 of 2000 (PEPUDA) implements this section.
- **Section 211 (Customary Law):** Recognizes the institution, status, and role of traditional leadership according to customary law, subject to the Constitution. This creates a dynamic tension between customary law norms (including those regarding chieftainship, land allocation, and gender roles) and constitutional rights, addressed in cases such as Bhe v. Khayelitsha Magistrate, 2005 (1) SA 580 (CC), which struck down the customary law rule of male primogeniture in inheritance.

### Nigeria: Federalism and Constitutional Complexity

The Constitution of the Federal Republic of Nigeria, 1999 (as amended) establishes a presidential federal republic with 36 states and the Federal Capital Territory.

**Chapter II (Fundamental Objectives and Directive Principles of State Policy):** Section 6(6)(c) explicitly renders these provisions non-justiciable, unlike the South African approach. This limitation was confirmed in Attorney-General of Ondo State v. Attorney-General of the Federation [2002] 9 NWLR (Pt 772) 222.

**Sharia and Legal Pluralism:** Following the return to civilian rule in 1999, twelve northern states (Zamfara, Kano, Sokoto, Katsina, Niger, Bauchi, Borno, Jigawa, Kebbi, Kaduna, Yobe, and Gombe) adopted or expanded Sharia criminal law through state legislation, raising constitutional questions under Section 10 (prohibition of state religion) and Section 38 (freedom of thought, conscience, and religion). The case of Safiya Hussaini Tungar-Tudu (Sokoto Sharia Court of Appeal, 2002), involving a stoning sentence for adultery later overturned on appeal, drew international attention to the constitutional tension between state-level Sharia implementation and federal constitutional rights.

**Customary Law Courts:** The Constitution recognizes customary courts of appeal at the state level (Section 280), operating alongside the formal court system. The relationship between customary law, Islamic law, and the received English common law creates what scholars such as A.O. Obilade have described as a "tripartite legal system."

## Latin America (Brazil, Mexico, Colombia)

### Brazil: The Citizen Constitution

The Constituicao Federal of October 5, 1988, was promulgated after a constituent assembly process involving significant popular participation, with over 60,000 proposed amendments submitted by civil society. Ulysses Guimaraes, president of the Constituent Assembly, called it the "Citizen Constitution" (Constituicao Cidada).

**Key Features:**

- **Title II (Fundamental Rights and Guarantees):** Article 5 alone contains 78 items (incisos) enumerating individual and collective rights, including habeas corpus (LXVIII), habeas data (LXXII, the right to access personal data held by the government), mandado de seguranca (LXIX, a remedy against unlawful acts by public authorities), and the mandado de injuncao (LXXI, a remedy when the absence of regulatory legislation makes the exercise of constitutional rights unviable).
- **Article 6 (Social Rights):** Enumerates education, health, food, work, housing, transportation, leisure, security, social security, protection of motherhood and childhood, and assistance to the destitute as social rights.
- **Article 225 (Environment):** Establishes the right to an ecologically balanced environment, imposing on the government and the community the duty to defend and preserve it for present and future generations.
- **Clausulas Petreas (Entrenchment Clauses, Article 60, Section 4):** Constitutional amendments cannot abolish the federal form of state, the direct, secret, universal, and periodic vote, the separation of powers, or individual rights and guarantees. This provision reflects the Brazilian constituent assembly's concern about preventing a return to authoritarianism.

### Colombia: The 1991 Constitution and the Tutela

The Constitucion Politica de Colombia (1991), replacing the 1886 Constitution, was drafted by a constituent assembly that included former guerrilla members, indigenous representatives, and civil society activists. It established Colombia as a "social state of law" (Estado social de derecho, Article 1).

**The Accion de Tutela (Article 86):** Any person may claim before a judge, at any time and place, through a preferential and summary procedure, the immediate protection of fundamental constitutional rights when these are violated or threatened by the action or omission of any public authority. The Corte Constitucional has interpreted tutela expansively, connecting socio-economic rights to the right to a dignified life (vida digna) in cases such as Sentencia T-760 de 2008, which ordered systemic reform of the health system to guarantee the right to health.

### Mexico: The 1917 Constitution

The Constitucion Politica de los Estados Unidos Mexicanos (1917), born from revolution, was the first constitution globally to enshrine social rights. Article 27 established the social function of property and the basis for agrarian reform (ejido system), while Article 123 guaranteed labor rights including the eight-hour workday, minimum wage, and the right to strike. The 2011 constitutional reform (reforma de derechos humanos) fundamentally transformed Mexican constitutional law by requiring all authorities to interpret rights in conformity with the Constitution and international human rights treaties under the principle of pro persona (most favorable interpretation to the individual).

### The Bloque de Constitucionalidad

A distinctive feature of Latin American constitutionalism is the "constitutionality block" (bloque de constitucionalidad), under which international human rights treaties are incorporated at the constitutional level. In Colombia, Article 93 provides that international treaties ratified by Congress that recognize human rights "prevail in the internal order." In Argentina, the 1994 constitutional reform (Article 75, inciso 22) granted constitutional hierarchy to eleven named human rights instruments. In Brazil, the 2004 amendment (Article 5, Section 3) provides that international human rights treaties approved by both houses of Congress with a three-fifths majority in two rounds have the status of constitutional amendments, while the STF in RE 466.343 (2008) held that other human rights treaties have "supra-legal" status above ordinary legislation but below the Constitution.

## Southeast Asia (Indonesia and Philippines)

### Indonesia: Pancasila Constitutionalism

The Undang-Undang Dasar Negara Republik Indonesia Tahun 1945 (UUD 1945) was originally drafted as a brief revolutionary document but underwent four rounds of amendment (1999-2002) that fundamentally restructured the state.

**Pancasila (Five Principles):** The state philosophy, enshrined in the Preamble and operationalized throughout the constitutional text, comprises: (1) Belief in the One and Only God (Ketuhanan Yang Maha Esa), (2) Just and Civilized Humanity (Kemanusiaan yang Adil dan Beradab), (3) Unity of Indonesia (Persatuan Indonesia), (4) Democracy Led by Wisdom in Deliberation/Representation (Kerakyatan yang Dipimpin oleh Hikmat Kebijaksanaan dalam Permusyawaratan/Perwakilan), and (5) Social Justice for All the People of Indonesia (Keadilan Sosial bagi Seluruh Rakyat Indonesia).

**Constitutional Court (Mahkamah Konstitusi, MK):** Established by the Third Amendment (2001) and operational from 2003, the MK has jurisdiction over judicial review of legislation against the Constitution, disputes between state institutions, dissolution of political parties, disputes regarding election results, and opinions regarding presidential impeachment. Under Chief Justice Jimly Asshiddiqie (2003-2008) and subsequent leadership, the MK rapidly established itself as an assertive constitutional tribunal, issuing conditionally constitutional decisions (konstitusional bersyarat) that effectively rewrote legislation.

**Legal Pluralism:** Indonesia's legal system is characterized by the coexistence of civil law (inherited from Dutch colonial law and codified in the Burgerlijk Wetboek/Kitab Undang-Undang Hukum Perdata), adat (customary law, recognized by Article 18B(2) of the amended Constitution, which states: "The State recognizes and respects customary law communities and their traditional rights"), and Islamic law (particularly in family law matters and fully implemented in Aceh province under Law No. 11 of 2006 on the Governing of Aceh).

### Philippines: Post-Marcos Constitutionalism

The 1987 Constitution of the Republic of the Philippines was ratified by plebiscite on February 2, 1987, following the People Power Revolution of February 1986 that ended the Marcos dictatorship.

**Key Features:**

- **Article II (Declaration of Principles and State Policies):** Section 26 mandates that "The State shall guarantee equal access to opportunities for public service, and prohibit political dynasties as may be defined by law." Despite this mandate, no anti-political dynasty law has been enacted, illustrating the gap between constitutional aspiration and legislative implementation.
- **Article III (Bill of Rights):** Section 1 provides that no person shall be deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process of law. Section 18(1) mandates that no person shall be detained solely by reason of political beliefs and aspirations, a direct response to Marcos-era political detention.
- **Article VIII (Judicial Department):** Section 1 vests judicial power in the Supreme Court and lower courts, explicitly including "the duty to determine whether or not there has been a grave abuse of discretion amounting to lack or excess of jurisdiction on the part of any branch or instrumentality of the Government." This expanded certiorari power has been interpreted as a mandate for robust judicial review.
- **Article X (Local Government):** Section 15-21 provide for autonomous regions in Muslim Mindanao and the Cordilleras. The Bangsamoro Organic Law (Republic Act No. 11054, 2018) implemented this mandate for Muslim Mindanao, establishing the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao (BARMM) with its own parliamentary form of government, fiscal autonomy, and authority over Sharia courts.

**Writ of Amparo:** Adopted by the Supreme Court through A.M. No. 07-9-12-SC (effective October 24, 2007), the writ of amparo is a remedy available to any person whose right to life, liberty, and security is violated or threatened by an unlawful act or omission of a public official or employee, or of a private individual or entity. Modeled partly on the Latin American amparo tradition, it was a direct response to extrajudicial killings and enforced disappearances. The companion Writ of Habeas Data (A.M. No. 08-1-16-SC, 2008) protects the right to informational privacy.

## Comparative Analysis

### Constitutional Identity and State Ideology

A fundamental distinction among Global South constitutions concerns the relationship between state ideology and constitutional text. China's Constitution explicitly enshrines CPC leadership and Marxism-Leninism as constitutional norms, creating what Chen Duanhong has called a "political constitution" (政治宪法) where political authority precedes and shapes legal authority. Indonesia's Pancasila occupies an analogous (though substantively different) position as a compulsory state philosophy, with the Constitutional Court in Decision No. 140/PUU-VII/2009 striking down the Blasphemy Law challenge partly on the basis that Pancasila's first principle (Belief in God) requires the state to protect religion. By contrast, India's constitutional secularism (upheld as part of the basic structure in S.R. Bommai v. Union of India, 1994 AIR SCW 2946) and South Africa's non-racialism/non-sexism represent constitutional identities defined by negation of prior injustice rather than affirmative ideological commitment.

### Socio-Economic Rights Justiciability

The Global South has pioneered the constitutionalization and judicial enforcement of socio-economic rights in ways that challenge the dominant Western constitutional paradigm of negative rights. South Africa's "reasonableness review" (Grootboom, Treatment Action Campaign) directly engages courts in assessing government policy choices regarding resource allocation. India's Supreme Court, through PIL jurisprudence, has read the right to life under Article 21 expansively to include the right to livelihood (Olga Tellis v. Bombay Municipal Corporation, AIR 1986 SC 180), the right to food (PUCL v. Union of India, Writ Petition (Civil) No. 196 of 2001), and the right to a clean environment (M.C. Mehta v. Union of India, AIR 1987 SC 1086). Brazil's STF has enforced the right to health by ordering governments to provide medications and treatments (RE 271.286, 2000), creating a phenomenon scholars call the "judicialization of health" (judicializacao da saude). Colombia's tutela has generated what Rodrigo Uprimny calls "a jurisprudence of daily life," with over 7 million tutela actions filed since 1991.

### Customary Law and Constitutional Supremacy

The relationship between customary law and constitutional rights is a persistent challenge. South Africa's Constitution explicitly subordinates customary law to the Bill of Rights (Section 211(3): "The courts must apply customary law when that law is applicable, subject to the Constitution and any legislation that specifically deals with customary law"), requiring courts to develop customary law in line with the spirit, purport, and objects of the Bill of Rights. Nigeria's approach is more fragmented, with customary law operating through state-level customary courts without a clear constitutional framework for reconciling conflicting norms. Indonesia's recognition of adat under Article 18B(2) is qualified by the requirement that such communities and rights "still exist" and are "in accordance with the development of the community and the principles of the Unitary State of the Republic of Indonesia," a qualification that the Constitutional Court has applied to require affirmative recognition in MK Decision No. 35/PUU-X/2012, which recognized indigenous peoples' rights over customary forests.

### Amendment Culture and Constitutional Rigidity

The frequency and nature of constitutional amendment varies dramatically. Brazil's Constitution has been amended 132 times (through 2025), reflecting what scholars call a "hyperconstitutionalization" of policy matters that in other systems would be addressed by ordinary legislation. India's 106 amendments since 1950 have been filtered through the basic structure doctrine, creating a constitutional system that is formally flexible but substantively rigid on core principles. China's five amendments in 42 years reflect a system where constitutional change is less important than policy change through CPC decisions and administrative regulation. South Africa's relatively stable text (18 amendment acts through 2025) reflects both constitutional rigidity (two-thirds majority requirement) and the Constitutional Court's active role in constitutional interpretation.
