## Summary

University governance -- the structures and processes through which universities are directed, controlled, and held accountable -- reflects fundamental choices about the relationship between academic self-governance, state oversight, and managerial efficiency, with each jurisdiction striking a distinctive balance among these competing principles. South Korea operates a dual governance system in which national universities are governed under the National University Corporation Act (국립대학법인법, applicable to Seoul National University and others that have adopted corporate status) or directly by the Ministry of Education, while private universities -- constituting approximately 80% of Korean higher education -- are governed by school foundations (학교법인) under the Private Schools Act (사립학교법), a structure that has generated persistent conflicts between foundation boards and faculty over academic governance, transparency, and the misuse of university assets. The United States employs a Board of Trustees model in which governing boards -- elected, appointed, or self-perpetuating depending on the institution type -- exercise ultimate fiduciary and strategic authority over public and private universities, with the American Association of University Professors (AAUP) advocating since 1920 for meaningful shared governance giving faculty primary responsibility for curriculum, instruction, and research; recent tensions over board composition, presidential authority, and political interference have intensified debates about governance effectiveness. Germany constitutionally guarantees academic self-governance (akademische Selbstverwaltung) as an element of Wissenschaftsfreiheit under Article 5(3) of the Grundgesetz, with the Federal Constitutional Court establishing in its landmark Hochschulurteil (BVerfGE 35, 79, 1973) that professors must retain decisive influence over matters directly affecting research and teaching; the subsequent introduction of Hochschulräte (university councils) modeled on corporate boards through Länder reforms has created a new layer of external governance that the Court has scrutinized for compatibility with academic self-governance requirements. The European framework addresses university governance through the Magna Charta Universitatum (1988), the Bologna Process's emphasis on institutional autonomy, the European University Association's Autonomy Scorecard, and CJEU jurisprudence including the landmark Commission v. Hungary (C-66/18, 2020) decision striking down Hungary's restrictions on the Central European University.

## South Korea

### Constitutional Framework

**Article 31(4) Constitution:** "The autonomy of institutions of higher education and the professionalism of education shall be guaranteed under the conditions as prescribed by Act (대학의 자율성은 법률이 정하는 바에 의하여 보장된다)." The Constitutional Court of Korea has interpreted this provision as establishing a constitutional guarantee of university autonomy that protects against both state interference and the domination of private foundations.

**Constitutional Court Decision 2006Hun-Ba23 (2008):** The Court held that university autonomy under Article 31(4) encompasses the freedom of universities to determine their own internal governance structures, academic programs, faculty appointments, and student admissions, subject to reasonable legislative regulation.

### National University Governance

**Ministry of Education Direct Governance (Traditional Model):** Most national universities historically operated as government agencies under direct Ministry of Education control, with the university president appointed by the President of the Republic upon recommendation of the Minister of Education.

**National University Corporation Act (국립대학법인법):** Seoul National University became the first national university to adopt corporate status in 2011 under the Seoul National University Act (서울대학교 설립·운영에 관한 법률). Key governance features include:

| Governance Body | Composition | Authority |
|----------------|-------------|-----------|
| Board of Trustees (이사회) | 15 members: president, 7 appointed by president, 7 external (including government, alumni, faculty representatives) | Strategic decisions; budget approval; bylaw amendments |
| University Council (평의원회) | Faculty, staff, student, alumni representatives | Advisory; deliberative on academic matters |
| President (총장) | Elected by direct vote of faculty and staff; confirmed by Board | Chief executive; academic and administrative leadership |

The corporatization of Seoul National University was controversial, with critics arguing that it introduced market-oriented governance that could undermine academic autonomy, while supporters contended that it freed the university from bureaucratic constraints.

### Private University Governance

Private universities, which educate approximately 80% of Korean university students, are governed by school foundations (학교법인) under the Private Schools Act:

**Foundation Board (이사회):** The board of directors controls the university's finances, appoints the president, and approves major decisions. A minimum of seven directors (이사) and two auditors (감사) is required.

**The Chaebol University Problem:** Many private universities were established by chaebol (재벌) families or other wealthy founders who continue to exercise dynastic control through foundation board seats. Scandals involving the misuse of university funds, nepotistic appointments, and suppression of faculty dissent have led to repeated calls for governance reform:

- **Konkuk University (2005):** The founder's family was investigated for embezzling university funds through related-party transactions.
- **Sangji University (2014):** The founder's son, convicted of embezzlement, was barred from re-assuming the chairmanship, leading to a prolonged governance crisis.
- **Keimyung University (2019):** Faculty protests over foundation interference in academic appointments and financial transparency.

**Open Board Requirement (개방이사제):** The 2005 amendment to the Private Schools Act introduced a requirement that private university foundations include open directors (개방이사) -- directors recommended by a committee including faculty, alumni, and other stakeholders -- constituting at least one-quarter of the board. This reform aimed to reduce dynastic control, but implementation has been uneven, and many foundations have found ways to maintain effective founder-family dominance.

### Faculty Participation

**Faculty Council (교수회의/교수협의회):** While most Korean universities have faculty councils, their formal authority varies dramatically. At some universities, the faculty council has significant influence over academic matters including curriculum, appointment procedures, and presidential selection. At many private universities, the faculty council serves only an advisory function, with the foundation board retaining ultimate authority.

The Presidential Election System (총장직선제), under which university presidents are elected by direct vote of the faculty (and sometimes staff and students), was widely adopted in the 1990s following democratization. However, a trend toward indirect presidential selection through search committees has emerged at some institutions, with proponents arguing that direct election produces populist rather than visionary leadership.

## United States

### Legal Framework

American university governance reflects the dual principles of trusteeship (corporate governance by lay boards) and shared governance (faculty participation in academic decision-making).

### Board of Trustees Model

**Public Universities:** Governing boards of public universities are established by state statute or constitutional provision. Board members may be:
- Elected by popular vote (Michigan, Colorado, Nebraska)
- Appointed by the governor (most states)
- Ex officio (governor, superintendent of education serving on the board)

**Private Universities:** Boards are typically self-perpetuating (existing members select new members) and exercise full corporate authority under state nonprofit corporation law.

**Key Board Functions:**
- Fiduciary responsibility for institutional assets
- Appointment and evaluation of the president
- Approval of budgets, tuition, and major contracts
- Strategic planning and policy oversight
- Degree conferral authority

### Shared Governance

**AAUP 1920 Report:** The initial AAUP framework on governance established the principle that faculty should participate in university decision-making, particularly on academic matters.

**AAUP/ACE/AGB Statement on Government of Colleges and Universities (1966):** This joint statement by the AAUP, the American Council on Education, and the Association of Governing Boards established the shared governance framework:

| Domain | Primary Authority |
|--------|------------------|
| Curriculum, instruction, research | Faculty |
| Faculty status (appointment, promotion, tenure) | Faculty recommendation; board approval |
| Student life and activities | Student affairs administration; student participation |
| Budget and finances | Administration and board; faculty consultation |
| Strategic direction | Board with presidential leadership; faculty and student input |
| Degree requirements | Faculty; board conferral |

### Recent Governance Controversies

**Political Interference in Public Universities:** State legislators and governors have increasingly sought to influence public university governance through:
- Board appointments of politically aligned members (Florida under Governor DeSantis's overhaul of New College of Florida's board in 2023)
- Legislative mandates on curriculum (anti-CRT legislation affecting university programs)
- Funding conditions tied to governance changes
- Removal of DEI offices and staff

**Presidential Authority and Faculty Relations:** High-profile governance crises have highlighted tensions between board authority, presidential leadership, and faculty governance:
- **University of North Carolina (2021):** The board's initial denial of tenure to journalist Nikole Hannah-Jones generated national controversy over political interference in academic appointment decisions.
- **Harvard University (2024):** President Claudine Gay's resignation following congressional testimony on antisemitism raised questions about external political pressure on university leadership.
- **Stanford University (2023):** President Marc Tessier-Lavigne's resignation following an investigation into research misconduct allegations illustrated the accountability function of board governance.

### Accreditation as Governance

Regional accreditation agencies (now consolidated into the Higher Learning Commission, Middle States, and others) serve as external governance mechanisms, establishing standards for institutional quality that include governance requirements. The accreditation system effectively delegates significant quality assurance authority to private, non-governmental bodies, a distinctive feature of the American system.

## Germany

### Constitutional Framework

German university governance is uniquely shaped by the constitutional guarantee of Wissenschaftsfreiheit and its institutional dimension:

**Article 5(3) Grundgesetz:** "Kunst und Wissenschaft, Forschung und Lehre sind frei." The Federal Constitutional Court has derived from this provision an institutional guarantee of academic self-governance (Selbstverwaltungsgarantie), requiring that university governance structures ensure scholars' participation in decisions affecting research and teaching.

### Key Federal Constitutional Court Decisions

**BVerfGE 35, 79 (Hochschulurteil, 1973):** The foundational university governance decision established the Gruppenhochschule (group university) model. The Court held that:
- Article 5(3) GG guarantees not only individual academic freedom but also an institutional framework for its exercise.
- In matters directly affecting research and teaching, the "group of university teachers" (Hochschullehrergruppe) must possess at least half the votes on the relevant decision-making body.
- In matters of decisive significance for research or teaching (such as professorial appointments), professors must possess a majority.

**BVerfGE 136, 338 (2014):** The Court struck down provisions of the Brandenburg Higher Education Act that granted the university president the power to veto faculty appointment decisions, holding that this violated the requirement of adequate faculty participation (hinreichende Teilhabe) in academic matters.

**BVerfG, 1 BvR 911/18 (2021):** The Hamburg University decision reaffirmed that governance reforms must preserve meaningful faculty influence over appointment processes and that administrative efficiency cannot justify excluding academic self-governance bodies from decisions with significant impact on research and teaching.

### Governance Bodies

German universities typically have a tripartite governance structure:

| Body | Composition | Authority |
|------|-------------|-----------|
| **Senat (Academic Senate)** | Professors (majority in academic matters), academic staff, students, non-academic staff | Academic affairs; curriculum; research policies; faculty appointment recommendations; university bylaws |
| **Präsidium/Rektorat (Executive Board)** | President/Rector (Präsident/Rektor), Vice-Presidents (Vizepräsidenten), Chancellor (Kanzler) | Executive leadership; budget execution; strategic planning; external representation |
| **Hochschulrat (University Council)** | External members (majority in most Länder); some internal members | Strategic oversight; presidential appointment; budget approval; structural decisions |

### The Hochschulrat Debate

The introduction of Hochschulräte (university councils) through Länder higher education reforms beginning in the 2000s represents the most significant governance innovation and controversy:

**Proponents argue** that external governance brings strategic perspective, accountability, and connection to society and the economy, similar to supervisory boards in corporate governance (Aufsichtsrat model).

**Critics contend** that external governance threatens academic self-governance by transferring decision-making authority from scholars to individuals without academic expertise, potentially subjecting universities to political and economic influence incompatible with Wissenschaftsfreiheit.

**Nordrhein-Westfalen Hochschulfreiheitsgesetz (2006):** This pioneering law gave universities corporate legal personality and established Hochschulräte with significant authority including presidential selection, budget approval, and strategic planning. The subsequent SPD/Green government rolled back some of these provisions through the Hochschulzukunftsgesetz (2014), restoring greater authority to the Senat and reducing the Hochschulrat to an advisory role.

### Student Participation (Gruppenhochschule)

The German Gruppenhochschule model guarantees student representation in university governance through elected student members of the Academic Senate and university committees. The BVerfGE 35, 79 decision established that students must be represented in governance bodies, though professors must retain majority voting power in academic matters.

The Allgemeiner Studierendenausschuss (AStA, General Students' Committee) serves as the executive body of the student self-governance (Verfasste Studierendenschaft) in most Länder, exercising authority over student affairs, cultural programming, and political representation. In Bayern, the verfasste Studierendenschaft was historically not recognized, with student representation limited to Studentischer Konvent, though reforms have expanded student governance rights.

## ECHR & EU

### Magna Charta Universitatum (1988)

Signed in Bologna by 388 rectors and subsequently endorsed by over 900 universities worldwide, the Magna Charta Universitatum establishes foundational principles of university governance:
- "The university is an autonomous institution at the heart of societies differently organized because of geography and historical heritage."
- "Teaching and research in universities must be inseparable."
- "Freedom in research and training is the fundamental principle of university life."
- "A university must be intellectually and morally independent of all political authority and economic power."

The Magna Charta 2020 (renewed version) added emphasis on sustainability, social responsibility, and civic engagement while reaffirming autonomy and academic freedom.

### Bologna Process and Governance

The Bologna Process has influenced university governance through its emphasis on:
- **Quality Assurance:** The European Standards and Guidelines (ESG, 2015) require institutional quality assurance systems involving faculty, students, and external stakeholders.
- **Student Participation:** The Berlin Communiqué (2003) recognized students as "full partners" in European Higher Education Area governance.
- **Institutional Autonomy:** The Yerevan Communiqué (2015) reaffirmed the "centrality of academic freedom and institutional autonomy" to the EHEA.

### EUA Autonomy Scorecard

The European University Association's Autonomy Scorecard assesses institutional autonomy across four dimensions:

| Dimension | Top-Scoring Countries | Low-Scoring Countries |
|-----------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Organizational Autonomy | United Kingdom, Denmark, Finland | Turkey, Greece, Cyprus |
| Financial Autonomy | United Kingdom, Luxembourg, Estonia | Greece, France, Croatia |
| Staffing Autonomy | United Kingdom, Sweden, Estonia | Turkey, Greece, France |
| Academic Autonomy | Ireland, United Kingdom, Finland | Turkey, France, Greece |

### Commission v. Hungary (C-66/18, 2020)

The CJEU Grand Chamber's landmark decision addressed Hungary's 2017 amendments to the Higher Education Act (Lex CEU):
- Hungary imposed requirements that foreign-accredited universities operate a campus in their home country and enter into an international agreement between Hungary and the home state.
- The Court held that these requirements violated GATS commitments, Directive 2006/123/EC, EU Charter Article 13 (academic freedom), and Article 14 (right to education).
- The decision established that member states cannot impose disproportionate restrictions on university operations that undermine institutional autonomy and academic freedom.

### EU Rule of Law Mechanism and University Governance

Regulation (EU) 2020/2092 on a general regime of conditionality for the protection of the EU budget has indirect implications for university governance, as breaches of the rule of law -- including threats to academic freedom and institutional autonomy -- may trigger conditionality measures affecting EU funding to member states. The European Parliament has repeatedly called for application of the mechanism in connection with academic freedom concerns in Hungary and other member states.

## Comparative Analysis

### Models of Governance Authority

The four jurisdictions represent three distinct governance models. The United States employs a trusteeship model in which lay governing boards hold ultimate authority, tempered by shared governance norms. Germany constitutionally mandates academic self-governance with the evolving addition of external council oversight. South Korea presents a hybrid system where governance authority differs fundamentally between public and private institutions, with the foundation board model for private universities creating the most acute governance tensions.

### Faculty Power and Academic Self-Governance

Faculty governance authority is strongest in Germany, where the Gruppenhochschule model and Federal Constitutional Court jurisprudence guarantee professors a constitutionally protected majority in academic decision-making. American shared governance, while widely endorsed in principle, is not legally mandated and varies significantly in practice. Korean faculty councils are the weakest among the three national systems, particularly at private universities where foundation boards may override faculty preferences.

### External Accountability vs. Academic Autonomy

All jurisdictions face the tension between external accountability and academic autonomy. Germany's Hochschulrat reforms explicitly attempt to introduce external accountability while preserving constitutional self-governance requirements. American accreditation agencies provide external quality assurance through a voluntary, peer-based system. South Korea's Ministry of Education exercises direct oversight through accreditation and funding conditionality. The EU framework promotes quality assurance through the European Standards and Guidelines while respecting institutional autonomy.

### Student Participation

Student participation in governance ranges from constitutionally guaranteed representation in Germany's Gruppenhochschule model to limited formal roles in Korean and American institutions. The Bologna Process has promoted student participation as a governance principle across European systems, though implementation varies significantly.
