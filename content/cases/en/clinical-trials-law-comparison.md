## Summary

Clinical trials regulation (임상시험 규제) -- the legal framework governing the design, conduct, oversight, and reporting of experimental research involving human participants to evaluate the safety and efficacy of medical interventions -- represents one of the most internationally harmonized yet jurisdictionally nuanced areas of healthcare law. South Korea regulates clinical trials primarily through the Pharmaceutical Affairs Act (약사법) and the Bioethics and Safety Act (생명윤리 및 안전에 관한 법률), with the Ministry of Food and Drug Safety (식품의약품안전처, MFDS) serving as the regulatory authority; Korea has emerged as a major clinical trial hub in Asia, ranking among the top 10 countries globally for clinical trial activity, with over 700 new trials approved annually and the Korean Good Clinical Practice (KGCP) standards harmonized with ICH-GCP guidelines. The United States operates the world's most extensive clinical trial regulatory framework through the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (FDCA, 21 U.S.C.) as administered by the Food and Drug Administration (FDA), with human subject protections governed by the Common Rule (45 C.F.R. Part 46) and FDA regulations (21 C.F.R. Parts 50, 56), Institutional Review Board (IRB) oversight, and mandatory trial registration on ClinicalTrials.gov under the FDA Amendments Act of 2007 (FDAAA Section 801). Germany's clinical trial framework is now governed primarily by the EU Clinical Trials Regulation (Regulation (EU) No 536/2014), which became applicable on January 31, 2022, replacing the previous Clinical Trials Directive 2001/20/EC, with national implementation through the Arzneimittelgesetz (AMG, Medicinal Products Act) and oversight by the Bundesinstitut für Arzneimittel und Medizinprodukte (BfArM) and the Paul-Ehrlich-Institut (PEI) for biologicals; the Federal Ethics Committee (Bundesethikkommission) and state ethics committees provide ethical review. At the EU level, Regulation 536/2014 creates a unified clinical trial authorization process through the EU Clinical Trials Information System (CTIS), the European Medicines Agency (EMA) coordinates multinational trial oversight, and the European Court of Human Rights has addressed research ethics through its Article 3 and Article 8 jurisprudence.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea has developed a robust clinical trial regulatory infrastructure that has made it a preferred site for multinational clinical research.

**Pharmaceutical Affairs Act (약사법):** The primary statutory basis for clinical trial regulation. Article 34 governs clinical trial authorization, requiring approval from the MFDS before initiating any clinical trial involving investigational drugs. The Act specifies requirements for the investigational new drug (IND) application, including preclinical data, manufacturing information, and the clinical protocol.

**Bioethics and Safety Act (생명윤리 및 안전에 관한 법률):** This Act establishes the ethical framework for human subjects research, requiring all clinical trials to obtain approval from an Institutional Review Board (IRB, 기관생명윤리위원회) before enrollment begins. The Act implements the principles of the Declaration of Helsinki and ICH-GCP guidelines.

### MFDS Oversight

The Ministry of Food and Drug Safety (식품의약품안전처, MFDS) exercises comprehensive regulatory authority:

- **IND Approval:** All clinical trials require MFDS approval. The review process takes approximately 30 working days for standard applications. Korea participates in the international simultaneous submission program for multinational trials.
- **GCP Inspections:** MFDS conducts regular GCP inspections of clinical trial sites, sponsors, and contract research organizations (CROs). Korea's inspection program is recognized by the FDA and EMA.
- **Clinical Trial Phases:** Korea recognizes the standard four-phase clinical trial structure (Phase I-IV), with Phase I trials conducted at designated clinical pharmacology centers.

### Korean Good Clinical Practice (KGCP)

KGCP, published by MFDS, is harmonized with ICH E6(R2) Good Clinical Practice guidelines. Key requirements include:

| Requirement | KGCP Provision |
|------------|----------------|
| Protocol review | IRB review and approval before enrollment |
| Informed consent | Written consent; additional protections for minors, pregnant women, cognitively impaired |
| Adverse event reporting | Serious adverse events reported within 15 days; fatal/life-threatening within 7 days |
| Data management | Electronic data capture systems must be validated and auditable |
| Monitoring | Risk-based monitoring approach consistent with ICH E6(R2) |

### Clinical Trial Infrastructure

Korea's clinical trial ecosystem includes over 180 MFDS-certified clinical trial institutions, 14 designated clinical pharmacology centers for Phase I studies, and a growing network of CROs. The Korea National Institute of Health (KNIH, 국립보건연구원) operates the Clinical Research Information Service (CRIS), Korea's clinical trial registry recognized by the WHO International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (ICTRP).

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States operates the world's largest clinical trial regulatory system, with the FDA overseeing more clinical trials than any other national authority.

**Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (FDCA):** The FDCA provides the statutory foundation for FDA regulation of clinical trials. Key provisions include the IND application process (21 C.F.R. Part 312), requirements for adequate and well-controlled studies for drug approval (21 U.S.C. Section 355(d)), and post-marketing study requirements.

**Common Rule (45 C.F.R. Part 46):** The Federal Policy for the Protection of Human Subjects, adopted by 20 federal departments and agencies, establishes baseline human subject protections including IRB review, informed consent, and additional protections for vulnerable populations (subparts B-D: pregnant women, prisoners, children). The Common Rule was significantly revised in 2018 (effective 2019).

### FDA Clinical Trial Oversight

| Phase | Purpose | Typical Design | Duration |
|-------|---------|---------------|----------|
| Phase I | Safety, dosing, pharmacokinetics | 20-100 healthy volunteers | Several months |
| Phase II | Efficacy, side effects | 100-300 patients with condition | Several months to 2 years |
| Phase III | Efficacy confirmation, monitoring | 300-3,000+ patients | 1-4 years |
| Phase IV | Post-marketing surveillance | Varies | Ongoing |

### Informed Consent (21 C.F.R. Part 50)

FDA informed consent requirements include disclosure of the experimental nature of the study, the purpose and expected duration, procedures involved, reasonably foreseeable risks and discomforts, potential benefits, alternative treatments available, confidentiality measures, compensation and medical treatments available for injury, and the right to withdraw at any time without penalty.

### Trial Registration and Results Reporting

The FDA Amendments Act of 2007 (FDAAA) Section 801 mandates registration of applicable clinical trials on ClinicalTrials.gov and submission of results within one year of the primary completion date. The Final Rule (42 C.F.R. Part 11, effective 2017) specifies detailed requirements for trial registration, including protocol information, recruitment status, and results data. Non-compliance may result in civil monetary penalties of up to $10,000 per day.

### 2018 Common Rule Revisions

The revised Common Rule introduced several significant changes: exemption categories expanded for low-risk research; a single IRB requirement for multi-site cooperative research; broad consent for secondary use of identifiable biospecimens; and elimination of continuing review for minimal-risk studies approved by expedited review.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's clinical trial framework has been fundamentally reshaped by the EU Clinical Trials Regulation.

**EU Clinical Trials Regulation (Regulation (EU) No 536/2014):** Directly applicable since January 31, 2022, this Regulation replaced the Clinical Trials Directive 2001/20/EC. It establishes a harmonized authorization procedure across all EU member states through the EU Clinical Trials Information System (CTIS). Germany adapted its national law through amendments to the Arzneimittelgesetz (AMG).

**Arzneimittelgesetz (AMG, Medicinal Products Act):** AMG Section 40 et seq. provides the national framework for clinical trial conduct, supplementing the EU Regulation with Germany-specific provisions including insurance requirements, ethics committee structures, and enforcement mechanisms.

### Clinical Trial Authorization

Under the EU CTR, clinical trial applications are submitted through CTIS and assessed jointly by the reporting member state (for the scientific/medical aspects) and each concerned member state (for national aspects including ethics and patient rights). Germany's process:

- **Part I Assessment (Scientific):** Conducted by BfArM (for medicinal products) or PEI (for biological products including vaccines). Assessment period: 45 days (extendable to 91 for complex cases).
- **Part II Assessment (National):** Conducted by the responsible ethics committee, addressing informed consent, participant recruitment, investigator qualifications, and site suitability. Assessment period: 45 days.

### Ethics Committees (Ethik-Kommissionen)

Germany's ethics committee system is decentralized, with ethics committees established at medical faculties, state medical associations (Landesärztekammern), and independent bodies. For multi-site trials within Germany, the lead ethics committee provides the binding opinion, with other concerned ethics committees contributing within the Part II assessment framework.

### Insurance Requirement

AMG Section 40(1)(8) mandates that the sponsor maintain insurance coverage for trial participants to cover potential harm arising from the clinical trial. This mandatory insurance requirement exceeds the protections in many other jurisdictions and reflects Germany's emphasis on participant safety.

### GCP Inspections

The Landesüberwachungsbehörden (state surveillance authorities) conduct GCP inspections of clinical trial sites, sponsors, and CROs in Germany. Germany is also subject to EMA-coordinated GCP inspections for trials supporting marketing authorization applications submitted through the centralized EU procedure.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Clinical Trials Regulation (Regulation (EU) No 536/2014)

The CTR represents the most comprehensive supranational clinical trial framework:

**Unified Application Process:** A single application through CTIS covers all participating EU member states, dramatically reducing administrative burden for multinational trials.

**Transparency:** CTIS provides unprecedented public access to clinical trial information, including protocols, summaries of results, and clinical study reports (with redaction of commercially confidential information and personal data). This transparency requirement, effective from January 2023, addresses longstanding concerns about selective reporting and publication bias.

**Informed Consent (Articles 28-35):**
- Consent must be written, dated, and signed by the participant (or legal representative).
- Prior information must include the nature, objectives, benefits, risks, and alternatives of the trial.
- Participants must be informed of their right to withdraw at any time without detriment.
- Special protections for minors (Article 32), incapacitated adults (Article 31), and emergency situations (Article 35).
- Clinical trials in emergency situations may proceed without prior consent under strict conditions, including independent confirmation by a physician not involved in the trial.

**Damage Compensation (Article 76):** Member states must ensure a system for compensation of damage suffered by participants attributable to participation in a clinical trial. This may be through mandatory insurance, national guarantee schemes, or other appropriate arrangements.

### European Medicines Agency (EMA)

EMA coordinates clinical trial oversight across the EU, maintaining the EudraCT database (now integrated into CTIS), conducting GCP inspections, and providing scientific advice to sponsors. EMA's Committee for Medicinal Products for Human Use (CHMP) evaluates marketing authorization applications based on clinical trial evidence.

### Council of Europe -- Oviedo Convention

**Article 16 -- Protection of Persons Undergoing Research:** Research on a person may only be undertaken if there is no alternative of comparable effectiveness to research on humans, the risks are not disproportionate to potential benefits, the research project has been approved by a competent body, the person has given informed consent specifically, and withdrawal from the research is possible at any time.

**Additional Protocol on Biomedical Research (2005):** This Protocol to the Oviedo Convention establishes detailed requirements for research ethics review, informed consent, and participant protection, providing a Council of Europe-wide framework that extends beyond the EU.

### European Court of Human Rights

**Hristozov and Others v. Bulgaria (Applications Nos. 47039/11 and 358/12, Judgment of November 13, 2012):** Terminally ill cancer patients challenged Bulgaria's refusal to permit access to unauthorized experimental medicines. The Court found no violation of Articles 2 or 8, holding that states enjoy a wide margin of appreciation in regulating access to experimental treatments and that the regulatory framework (including clinical trial requirements) serves the legitimate aim of protecting health.

The Court acknowledged the tension between individual autonomy (the right of terminally ill patients to try experimental treatments) and regulatory protection (the requirement for evidence-based evaluation through controlled clinical trials). This tension remains central to ongoing debates about compassionate use, right-to-try legislation, and adaptive trial designs.

## Comparative Analysis

### Regulatory Harmonization

Clinical trials represent one of the most internationally harmonized areas of healthcare law, primarily through the ICH-GCP guidelines adopted by the US, EU, Japan, and numerous other jurisdictions. However, significant differences remain in authorization timelines, ethics review structures, insurance requirements, and transparency obligations. The EU CTR's unified application process is the most streamlined for multinational trials.

### Ethics Review Structures

The US system uses institution-based IRBs with a recent shift toward single-IRB review for multi-site studies. Germany employs a combination of institutional and medical association-based ethics committees. South Korea uses institution-based IRBs modeled on the US system. The EU CTR's Part I/Part II division separates scientific from ethical assessment across member states.

### Transparency and Registration

The US ClinicalTrials.gov system, with mandatory registration and results reporting under FDAAA 801, set the global standard for trial transparency. The EU CTIS now provides comparable or greater transparency within the EU. South Korea's CRIS participates in the WHO ICTRP network. Despite these requirements, registration and reporting gaps persist across all jurisdictions.

### Participant Compensation

Germany and the EU mandate insurance coverage for trial participants, providing guaranteed compensation for research-related injuries. The US has no federal insurance mandate, relying instead on IRB-required informed consent disclosures about compensation availability and sponsor-specific policies. South Korea requires mandatory insurance. This divergence reflects different approaches to distributing the risks of clinical research.
