## Summary

Libel and slander -- the tort and criminal law doctrines addressing false statements that damage a person's reputation, with libel traditionally referring to written or published defamation and slander to spoken defamation -- present one of the sharpest divergences in comparative law, reflecting fundamentally different philosophical orientations toward the balance between reputation protection and freedom of expression. South Korea maintains one of the most expansive defamation regimes in the world, criminally punishing not only false statements but also true statements that damage reputation under Article 307 of the Criminal Act (형법), with the Act on Promotion of Information and Communications Network Utilization and Information Protection (정보통신망법) imposing enhanced penalties for online defamation; the breadth of Korean defamation law has drawn persistent criticism from international human rights bodies, particularly the UN Human Rights Committee, which found South Korea in violation of Article 19 of the ICCPR in Park v. Republic of Korea (2011). The United States provides the narrowest defamation liability among the jurisdictions compared, with the Supreme Court's landmark decision in New York Times Co. v. Sullivan (1964) constitutionalizing defamation law by requiring public officials to prove "actual malice" -- knowledge of falsity or reckless disregard for the truth -- and with truth serving as an absolute defense, reflecting the First Amendment's paramount protection of free expression. Germany addresses defamation through Sections 185-200 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), distinguishing between Beleidigung (insult), Üble Nachrede (malicious gossip), and Verleumdung (slander/defamation with knowledge of falsity), with the Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) engaging in extensive case-by-case balancing between personality rights (Allgemeines Persönlichkeitsrecht) derived from Articles 1 and 2 of the Grundgesetz and freedom of expression under Article 5 GG. The European Court of Human Rights has developed extensive jurisprudence balancing Article 10 (freedom of expression) against Article 8 (right to respect for private life), consistently holding that criminal defamation sanctions, particularly imprisonment, must meet strict proportionality requirements, and that public figures must tolerate a greater degree of criticism than private individuals.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's defamation law is distinctive in criminalizing both false and true statements that damage reputation, creating one of the broadest defamation regimes among democracies.

**Criminal Act (형법) Articles 307-312:**

| Provision | Offense | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| Article 307(1) | Defamation by publicly stating facts | Up to 2 years imprisonment or fine up to 5 million KRW |
| Article 307(2) | Defamation by publicly stating false facts | Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine up to 10 million KRW |
| Article 309(1) | Defamation through publication (facts) | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine up to 15 million KRW |
| Article 309(2) | Defamation through publication (false facts) | Up to 7 years imprisonment or fine up to 15 million KRW |
| Article 310 | Defense of truth | Not punishable if true and solely in the public interest |
| Article 311 | Insult (모욕) | Up to 1 year imprisonment or fine up to 2 million KRW |

**Critical Distinction:** Article 310 provides a defense for true statements but only if the statement relates "solely to the public interest" (오로지 공공의 이익에 관한 때). This standard is interpreted narrowly, meaning that truthful statements made for mixed motives (including personal animus alongside public interest) can still be criminally punished.

### Online Defamation

The Act on Promotion of Information and Communications Network Utilization and Information Protection (정보통신망법) Article 70 imposes enhanced penalties for online defamation:
- Defamation by true facts online: up to 3 years imprisonment or fine up to 30 million KRW
- Defamation by false facts online: up to 7 years imprisonment or fine up to 50 million KRW

### International Criticism

The UN Human Rights Committee, in **Park v. Republic of Korea (Communication No. 1628/2007, 2011)**, found that South Korea's criminal defamation law violated Article 19 of the ICCPR (freedom of expression), noting that imprisonment for defamation is a disproportionate penalty that chills legitimate expression. The Committee recommended that South Korea decriminalize defamation.

The Committee's Concluding Observations on South Korea (CCPR/C/KOR/CO/4, 2015) reiterated concerns about criminal defamation and recommended that "defamation be addressed through civil rather than criminal proceedings."

### Landmark Cases

**Supreme Court Decision 2008Do8812 (2011):** The Supreme Court held that the "public interest" defense under Article 310 requires the defendant to demonstrate that the primary purpose of the statement was serving the public interest, not merely that the statement was factually true.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States provides the broadest protection for expression in defamation cases, primarily through the First Amendment as interpreted by the Supreme Court.

**New York Times Co. v. Sullivan, 376 U.S. 254 (1964):** The foundational case constitutionalized defamation law. L.B. Sullivan, a Montgomery, Alabama city commissioner, sued the New York Times over an advertisement containing minor factual inaccuracies about civil rights protests. The Supreme Court held that the First Amendment requires public officials suing for defamation to prove "actual malice" -- that the defendant published the statement "with knowledge that it was false or with reckless disregard of whether it was false or not."

**Curtis Publishing Co. v. Butts and Associated Press v. Walker, 388 U.S. 130 (1967):** Extended the actual malice standard to "public figures" who are not government officials but have assumed prominence in public controversies.

**Gertz v. Robert Welch, Inc., 418 U.S. 323 (1974):** The Court held that private individuals need not prove actual malice but that states may not impose strict liability for defamation. Private plaintiffs must prove at least negligence and may recover presumed or punitive damages only upon a showing of actual malice.

### Elements of Defamation

| Element | Public Official/Figure | Private Individual |
|---------|----------------------|-------------------|
| False statement of fact | Required | Required |
| Publication to third party | Required | Required |
| Fault standard | Actual malice (knowledge or reckless disregard) | Negligence (varies by state) |
| Damages | Proven actual damages required (absent actual malice) | Varies by state; some presume damages |
| Truth | Absolute defense | Absolute defense |

### Section 230 Immunity

Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act (47 U.S.C. Section 230) provides broad immunity to internet platforms for third-party content, stating that "No provider or user of an interactive computer service shall be treated as the publisher or speaker of any information provided by another information content provider." This provision has been critically important in shielding platforms from defamation liability for user-generated content, though its scope has been increasingly debated.

### SPEECH Act (2010)

The Securing the Protection of our Enduring and Established Constitutional Heritage Act (28 U.S.C. Sections 4101-4105) prohibits U.S. courts from enforcing foreign defamation judgments unless the foreign law provides at least as much protection for freedom of speech and press as the First Amendment. The Act was prompted by "libel tourism" -- plaintiffs suing in foreign jurisdictions with more plaintiff-friendly defamation laws.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German defamation law distinguishes among three categories of offense under the Strafgesetzbuch:

**Beleidigung (Insult, Section 185 StGB):** Punishes the expression of disrespect or contempt toward another person, whether through words, images, or gestures. Penalty: fine or imprisonment up to two years. Beleidigung is the broadest category and can include true statements or value judgments that are insulting in form.

**Üble Nachrede (Malicious Gossip, Section 186 StGB):** Punishes the assertion or dissemination of a fact about another person that is capable of degrading that person or negatively affecting public opinion, unless the fact can be proven true. Penalty: fine or imprisonment up to one year; up to two years if committed publicly or through dissemination of content.

**Verleumdung (Defamation/Slander, Section 187 StGB):** The most serious form; punishes the intentional assertion or dissemination of an untrue fact about another person. The defendant must know the statement is false. Penalty: imprisonment up to two years; up to five years if committed publicly.

### Constitutional Balancing

The Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) has developed extensive jurisprudence balancing the Allgemeines Persönlichkeitsrecht (general personality right, derived from Articles 1(1) and 2(1) GG) against Meinungsfreiheit (freedom of expression, Article 5(1) GG):

**Lüth Decision (BVerfGE 7, 198 (1958)):** The foundational case establishing that fundamental rights have "radiating effect" (Ausstrahlungswirkung) on all areas of law, including private law. The Court held that freedom of expression must be given particular weight in cases involving public debate.

**Schmid-Spiegel Decision (BVerfGE 12, 113 (1961)):** Established that statements of opinion (Werturteile) receive greater protection than assertions of fact (Tatsachenbehauptungen), and that criticism of public figures in their public capacity is entitled to broader protection.

**Caroline von Hannover decisions (BVerfG, various):** The Court has engaged in repeated balancing of press freedom and personality rights in cases involving public figures, particularly the Princess Caroline of Monaco/Hannover cases that also reached the ECHR.

### NetzDG and Online Defamation

The Netzwerkdurchsetzungsgesetz (NetzDG, 2017) required social media platforms with more than 2 million German users to remove "manifestly unlawful" content, including defamatory content under Sections 185-187 StGB, within 24 hours of notification. The NetzDG has been largely superseded by the EU Digital Services Act (Regulation (EU) 2022/2065), which establishes a more comprehensive framework for platform content moderation obligations.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Jurisprudence

The ECtHR has developed extensive case law balancing Article 10 (freedom of expression) and Article 8 (right to respect for private life) in defamation cases.

**Lingens v. Austria (Application No. 9815/82, Judgment of July 8, 1986):** The Court found that Austria's conviction of a journalist for defaming the Chancellor violated Article 10, establishing that politicians must tolerate a greater degree of criticism than private citizens and that criminal sanctions for political commentary require strict justification.

**Castells v. Spain (Application No. 11798/85, Judgment of April 23, 1992):** The Court found Spain's criminal defamation conviction of a senator who accused the government of complicity in Basque Country killings violated Article 10, emphasizing the importance of political expression in a democratic society.

**Von Hannover v. Germany (No. 2) (Application Nos. 40660/08, 60641/08, Grand Chamber Judgment of February 7, 2012):** The Grand Chamber established criteria for balancing Articles 8 and 10: contribution to a debate of general interest, the degree of public notoriety of the person affected, the subject of the news report, the prior conduct of the person concerned, the content and form of the publication, and the circumstances in which the photographs were taken.

**Perinçek v. Switzerland (Application No. 27510/08, Grand Chamber Judgment of October 15, 2015):** The Grand Chamber found that Switzerland's criminal conviction for denying the Armenian genocide violated Article 10, applying a broad proportionality analysis.

### Key Principles

- Criminal defamation is not per se incompatible with the Convention, but imprisonment for defamation will be compatible with Article 10 only in exceptional circumstances, particularly where other fundamental rights have been seriously impaired (as in hate speech or incitement to violence)
- Public figures, especially politicians, must tolerate a wider degree of criticism
- Value judgments (opinions) cannot require proof of truth; but they must have a sufficient factual basis
- Journalists acting in good faith are entitled to rely on official reports without independent verification (Bladet Tromsø v. Norway, 1999)

### EU Digital Services Act

Regulation (EU) 2022/2065 (Digital Services Act, effective February 17, 2024) establishes a comprehensive framework for online platform liability and content moderation across the EU. While not directly addressing defamation standards, the DSA affects defamation enforcement by establishing notice-and-action mechanisms for illegal content (which includes defamatory content under national laws), requiring platforms to publish transparency reports on content moderation, and creating a trusted flagger mechanism for priority notice processing.

## Comparative Analysis

### Truth as a Defense

The most striking divergence concerns the treatment of truth. The United States treats truth as an absolute defense -- true statements can never be defamatory regardless of motive. Germany's Üble Nachrede provides a truth defense, but Beleidigung (insult) can be committed with true statements if the form of expression is sufficiently degrading. South Korea uniquely criminalizes true statements that damage reputation unless made solely in the public interest. The ECHR position requires proportionality analysis rather than a categorical rule.

### Criminal vs. Civil Enforcement

South Korea and Germany maintain active criminal defamation regimes, while the United States has effectively decriminalized defamation through desuetude and First Amendment constraints. The ECHR permits criminal defamation but increasingly restricts the use of imprisonment as a sanction, especially for political speech. The trend in international human rights law, articulated by the UN Human Rights Committee and the UN Special Rapporteur on Freedom of Expression, strongly favors civil rather than criminal remedies.

### Protection of Public Discourse

All jurisdictions provide enhanced protection for commentary on public figures and matters of public interest, but the mechanisms differ fundamentally. The U.S. actual malice standard is the most protective, requiring public-figure plaintiffs to prove knowing or reckless falsity by clear and convincing evidence. Germany and the ECHR apply case-by-case proportionality balancing. South Korea's "solely in the public interest" standard offers comparatively narrow protection.

### Insult Law (모욕죄) in South Korea

South Korea's Criminal Act Article 311 criminalizes insult (모욕), defined as publicly demeaning another person through expression that does not involve the assertion of specific facts. Unlike defamation, which requires a factual assertion, insult encompasses value judgments, vulgar language, and contemptuous expression directed at a specific individual in the presence of others. The maximum penalty is one year imprisonment or a fine up to 2 million KRW.

The insult provision has been increasingly applied to online expression. In 2022, approximately 12,500 cases of criminal insult were referred to prosecutors. The Constitutional Court upheld the constitutionality of Article 311 in Decision 2013헌바121 (2016), finding that the provision serves the legitimate purpose of protecting individual dignity and social order, though dissenting justices argued that criminal punishment for mere insults is disproportionate and incompatible with freedom of expression.

### Defamation Reform Proposals in South Korea

Several proposals to reform South Korea's criminal defamation system have been advanced:

| Year | Proposal | Status |
|------|----------|--------|
| 2013 | Abolition of criminal defamation for true statements | Failed |
| 2016 | Reduction of penalties; removal of imprisonment for online defamation | Failed |
| 2021 | Decriminalization of defamation (civil remedies only) | Failed |
| 2023 | Amendment to narrow "public interest" defense | Pending |

The persistence of criminal defamation has been linked to its use by powerful individuals and corporations to silence critics and journalists, a phenomenon termed "strategic litigation against public participation" (SLAPP) in other jurisdictions.

### German Civil Defamation (Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch)

In addition to criminal defamation, German civil law provides remedies through Section 823(1) BGB (damages for violation of rights, including the personality right), Section 823(2) BGB (damages for violation of a protective statute, including Sections 185-187 StGB), and Section 1004 BGB analogically applied (injunctive relief against continuing violations). The Geldentschädigung (monetary compensation for non-material damage) is available for serious personality right violations, though German law does not provide for punitive damages.

The Landgericht Hamburg has been a preferred forum for defamation plaintiffs due to its historically plaintiff-friendly jurisprudence, leading to "forum shopping" criticism. The Hamburg courts have issued numerous preliminary injunctions (einstweilige Verfügungen) against media publications, sometimes based on ex parte proceedings.

### Defamation Tourism and Cross-Border Issues

The global nature of online publication creates significant cross-border defamation challenges. The U.S. SPEECH Act (2010) prevents enforcement of foreign defamation judgments that do not meet First Amendment standards. The EU's Brussels I Regulation (recast) allows defamation plaintiffs to sue in any member state where the defamatory content was accessible (eDate Advertising, CJEU C-509/09, 2011), creating potential for forum shopping. South Korea's courts have asserted jurisdiction over online defamation accessible within Korean territory regardless of where the content was published.
