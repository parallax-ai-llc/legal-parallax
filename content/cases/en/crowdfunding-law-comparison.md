## Summary

Crowdfunding regulation -- the legal framework governing the solicitation of small financial contributions from large numbers of individuals, typically via internet platforms, for business ventures, creative projects, or social causes -- has evolved rapidly since the early 2010s as regulators have sought to balance investor protection with capital formation for startups and small businesses. South Korea regulates equity crowdfunding through the Financial Investment Services and Capital Markets Act (자본시장과 금융투자업에 관한 법률, commonly known as the Capital Markets Act) as amended in 2015 and its implementing regulations, administered by the Financial Services Commission (금융위원회, FSC), which permits licensed crowdfunding intermediaries (크라우드펀딩 중개업자) to facilitate securities offerings with individual investment limits and issuer caps. The United States created a comprehensive federal crowdfunding framework through Title III of the Jumpstart Our Business Startups (JOBS) Act of 2012, codified as Section 4(a)(6) of the Securities Act of 1933 (15 U.S.C. § 77d(a)(6)) and implemented through SEC Regulation Crowdfunding (17 C.F.R. §§ 227.100-227.503), which was significantly liberalized in March 2021 to raise the offering limit from $1.07 million to $5 million. Germany initially regulated crowdfunding through the Kleinanlegerschutzgesetz (Small Investor Protection Act) of 2015 and the Vermögensanlagengesetz (Asset Investment Act), but has since transitioned to the EU's harmonized framework under Regulation (EU) 2020/1503, the European Crowdfunding Service Providers Regulation (ECSPR), which became fully applicable on November 10, 2023, establishing a single EU-wide licensing regime for lending-based and investment-based crowdfunding platforms with a maximum offering threshold of €5 million calculated over a 12-month period, while the European Court of Human Rights has addressed the tension between securities regulation and the fundamental right to property and freedom of enterprise.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's equity crowdfunding framework was introduced through the 2015 amendment to the Capital Markets Act (자본시장과 금융투자업에 관한 법률), effective January 25, 2016. The framework was designed to create an alternative financing channel for startups and SMEs while maintaining investor protection.

**Eligible Issuers:** Companies eligible for crowdfunding must be unlisted SMEs that have been in existence for less than 7 years (extended to 10 years for certain technology companies). Issuers in specific excluded sectors (financial services, real estate speculation) are not eligible.

**Crowdfunding Intermediaries (크라우드펀딩 중개업자):** Platforms must register with the FSC as crowdfunding intermediaries and are subject to minimum capital requirements of 500 million KRW. As of 2025, approximately 15 registered platforms operate in Korea, including Wadiz (와디즈), Open Trade, and Crowdy.

### Investment Limits

The framework employs tiered investment limits based on investor sophistication:

| Investor Category | Per-Issuer Limit | Annual Aggregate Limit |
|------------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| General investors (일반투자자) | 5 million KRW | 10 million KRW |
| Income-qualified investors (소득적격투자자) | 10 million KRW | 20 million KRW |
| Professional investors (전문투자자) | No limit | No limit |

**Income-qualified investors** must demonstrate annual income of at least 100 million KRW or financial assets of at least 200 million KRW.

### Disclosure and Investor Protection

Issuers must file a standardized offering document with the FSC containing information about the company, its management, financial condition, business plan, and risk factors. The offering document must be made available to investors through the platform for at least 10 business days before the offering closes.

**Cooling-Off Period:** Investors may withdraw their investment commitments within 7 business days of making the investment without penalty.

**Lock-Up Period:** Securities purchased through crowdfunding are subject to a 6-month transfer restriction from the offering closing date, after which they may be traded on the platform's bulletin board or through other permitted channels.

### Reward and Donation Crowdfunding

Reward-based and donation-based crowdfunding are not specifically regulated under securities law in Korea, as they do not involve the issuance of financial instruments. However, platforms facilitating reward crowdfunding are subject to general consumer protection laws, including the Act on Consumer Protection in Electronic Commerce (전자상거래 등에서의 소비자보호에 관한 법률) and the Electronic Financial Transactions Act (전자금융거래법).

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States established the first comprehensive federal crowdfunding framework through Title III of the JOBS Act of 2012, which added Section 4(a)(6) to the Securities Act of 1933, creating an exemption from securities registration for certain crowdfunding transactions.

**Regulation Crowdfunding (17 C.F.R. Part 227):** The SEC's implementing rules, initially adopted on October 30, 2015 (effective May 16, 2016), and significantly amended on March 15, 2021, establish the operational framework:

**Offering Limits (post-March 2021 amendment):**
- Maximum aggregate amount raised: $5 million in a 12-month period (increased from $1.07 million)
- The 2021 amendment represented a nearly five-fold increase, dramatically expanding the utility of the exemption

**Investment Limits:**

| Investor Annual Income & Net Worth | Maximum Investment (12-month aggregate across all Reg CF offerings) |
|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Either income or net worth below $124,000 | Greater of $2,500 or 5% of the lesser of income or net worth |
| Both income and net worth $124,000+ | 10% of the lesser of income or net worth, up to $124,000 |
| Accredited investors | No limit |

**Funding Portals:** Intermediaries may register with the SEC as "funding portals" under Section 4A(a) of the Securities Act (15 U.S.C. § 77d-1(a)), a lighter regulatory category than full broker-dealer registration. Funding portals must also register with FINRA. Major platforms include Wefunder, StartEngine, and Republic.

### Disclosure Requirements

| Offering Size | Financial Statement Requirement |
|--------------|-------------------------------|
| Up to $124,000 | Financial statements certified by principal executive officer |
| $124,001 - $618,000 | Financial statements reviewed by independent public accountant |
| $618,001 - $1,235,000 | Reviewed financial statements (audited if previously offered >$1.235M) |
| Over $1,235,000 | Audited financial statements |

Issuers file Form C with the SEC, including business description, officers and directors, use of proceeds, target amount, deadline, ownership and capital structure, and risk factors. Annual reports on Form C-AR are required until the issuer becomes an Exchange Act reporting company, has fewer than 300 holders of record, or repurchases all securities sold under Regulation CF.

### Regulation A+ and Regulation D Comparisons

Regulation Crowdfunding exists alongside other capital-raising exemptions:

| Feature | Reg CF | Reg A+ Tier 1 | Reg A+ Tier 2 | Reg D 506(c) |
|---------|--------|---------------|---------------|--------------|
| Max Offering | $5M | $20M | $75M | Unlimited |
| Investor Limits | Yes | No | Yes (10% for non-accredited) | Accredited only |
| SEC Filing | Form C | Form 1-A | Form 1-A | Form D |
| General Solicitation | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Resale Restrictions | 12 months | None | None | 6-12 months |

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's crowdfunding regulation has undergone a significant transition from a national framework to the EU's harmonized regime.

**Pre-ECSPR Framework (2015-2023):** The Kleinanlegerschutzgesetz (Small Investor Protection Act) of 2015 amended the Vermögensanlagengesetz (VermAnlG, Asset Investment Act) to create a specific framework for crowdfunding. Key provisions included:
- Prospectus exemption for offerings up to €6 million (VermAnlG § 2a)
- Individual investment limit of €10,000 per offering
- Self-declaration requirement for investments exceeding €1,000
- Vermögensanlagen-Informationsblatt (VIB) -- simplified investment information sheet of maximum 3 pages

**ECSPR Transition:** Regulation (EU) 2020/1503 became applicable on November 10, 2021, with a transitional period for existing platforms until November 10, 2023. BaFin now licenses crowdfunding service providers under the ECSPR framework.

**Key ECSPR Provisions (as applied in Germany):**

| Provision | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| Article 3 | Authorization required from national competent authority (BaFin) |
| Article 7 | Minimum prudential safeguards (€25,000 or 0.2% of outstanding loans) |
| Article 21 | Key Investment Information Sheet (KIIS) -- standardized 6-page format |
| Article 22 | Entry knowledge test and simulation of loss-bearing ability for non-sophisticated investors |
| Article 23 | €1,000 minimum threshold for investor knowledge test |
| Article 24 | Platform may offer bulletin board for secondary trading |
| Article 25 | Cross-border passport: single authorization valid across all EU/EEA states |

### Market Data

Germany has one of Europe's largest crowdfunding markets. According to the Cambridge Centre for Alternative Finance, Germany's crowdfunding and peer-to-peer lending market reached approximately €1.8 billion in 2023. Major platforms include Seedmatch (equity), Exporo (real estate), and Companisto (equity).

### Real Estate Crowdfunding

Real estate crowdfunding has been particularly popular in Germany, typically structured as subordinated profit-participating loans (partiarische Nachrangdarlehen). Several high-profile platform failures, including the collapse of Exporo-backed projects and Zinsbaustein, have prompted BaFin to increase scrutiny of real estate crowdfunding platforms and their due diligence practices.

## ECHR / EU

### European Crowdfunding Service Providers Regulation (ECSPR)

Regulation (EU) 2020/1503 of the European Parliament and of the Council of October 7, 2020, establishes a harmonized framework for crowdfunding service providers across the EU:

**Scope:** The ECSPR covers lending-based crowdfunding (peer-to-peer lending) and investment-based crowdfunding (equity and debt securities). Reward-based and donation-based crowdfunding remain outside its scope.

**Authorization and Passporting:** Crowdfunding service providers must obtain authorization from their home member state's national competent authority under Article 3. Once authorized, they receive a European passport allowing cross-border services in all EU/EEA member states under Article 18, without requiring additional authorization in host member states.

**Investor Protection Framework:**

| Protection Measure | ECSPR Provision | Description |
|-------------------|-----------------|-------------|
| Knowledge test | Article 21 | Assessment of investor's understanding of crowdfunding risks |
| Loss simulation | Article 21 | Investors must demonstrate ability to bear potential losses |
| Cooling-off period | Article 22 | 4 calendar days to withdraw offer or expression of interest |
| KIIS | Article 23 | Standardized 6-page Key Investment Information Sheet per project |
| Individual portfolio management | Article 6 | Optional; platform manages diversified portfolio on investor's behalf |

**Offering Threshold:** Article 1(2)(c) limits the ECSPR's scope to offers not exceeding €5 million calculated over a 12-month period per project owner. Offerings above this threshold require a full prospectus under the Prospectus Regulation (EU) 2017/1129.

### ESMA Regulatory Technical Standards

The European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) has adopted extensive regulatory technical standards (RTS) and implementing technical standards (ITS) under the ECSPR:
- RTS on business continuity plans (Article 12(16))
- RTS on individual portfolio management of loans (Article 6(7))
- RTS on credit risk assessment framework (Article 19(7))
- RTS on entry knowledge test (Article 21(8))
- ITS on Key Investment Information Sheet format (Article 23(16))

### ECHR Considerations

While the ECHR has not directly addressed crowdfunding regulation, relevant principles emerge from broader financial regulation jurisprudence:

**Article 1, Protocol No. 1 (Protection of Property):** Securities regulation that restricts the ability of individuals to invest or businesses to raise capital constitutes an interference with property rights, requiring proportionality. The wide margin of appreciation granted to states in regulating financial markets means that crowdfunding restrictions are unlikely to violate the Convention unless they are manifestly disproportionate.

**Article 10 (Freedom of Expression):** Advertising restrictions on crowdfunding offerings have potential implications for freedom of commercial expression. However, the ECHR has consistently upheld proportionate restrictions on commercial speech in the financial services context.

**Joined Cases C-370/19 and C-37/20, Commission v. United Kingdom (CJEU):** While concerning post-Brexit regulatory divergence rather than crowdfunding specifically, this case illustrates the CJEU's approach to financial regulation as a legitimate exercise of the EU's internal market competence.

## Comparative Analysis

### Regulatory Philosophy

The four jurisdictions reflect distinct regulatory philosophies:

**South Korea:** Conservative approach with relatively low offering and investment limits, reflecting concern about investor protection in a market with high retail participation and limited investor sophistication in alternative investments.

**United States:** Initially conservative (the original $1.07 million cap made Reg CF largely impractical), the 2021 reforms adopted a more permissive approach that has significantly increased market activity. The exemption-based approach (Section 4(a)(6) exemption from registration) allows flexibility without full securities registration.

**Germany/EU:** The ECSPR represents a middle ground, balancing investor access with standardized protections. The single passport eliminates the fragmentation that previously characterized European crowdfunding regulation.

### Market Impact

Crowdfunding market development correlates closely with regulatory design:

- **United States:** The post-2021 reform market has seen explosive growth, with annual Reg CF offerings exceeding $700 million in 2023, up from approximately $239 million in 2020
- **South Korea:** Market remains modest at approximately 50 billion KRW annually, constrained by low offering limits
- **EU/Germany:** The ECSPR is expected to increase cross-border activity, though early implementation has been marked by platform compliance challenges

### Outstanding Issues

All jurisdictions face common challenges:
1. **Secondary market liquidity:** Crowdfunded securities remain largely illiquid despite bulletin board provisions
2. **Information asymmetry:** Simplified disclosure requirements may not adequately inform investors about startup risks
3. **Platform failure risk:** The insolvency of crowdfunding platforms raises investor protection concerns
4. **Cross-border enforcement:** Different regulatory standards create regulatory arbitrage opportunities

### Tokenization and Blockchain-Based Crowdfunding

The intersection of crowdfunding and blockchain technology is creating new regulatory challenges:

**Security Token Offerings (STOs):** Tokenized securities issued through crowdfunding exemptions combine the benefits of crowdfunding (broader investor access) with blockchain features (programmable securities, automated compliance, fractional ownership):
- **United States:** STOs may qualify under Regulation Crowdfunding, Regulation A+, or Regulation D; SEC has not created a separate STO framework
- **EU:** MiCA does not cover securities tokens; existing securities regulation (MiFID II, Prospectus Regulation) applies; DLT Pilot Regime allows experimentation
- **South Korea:** FSC has issued guidelines treating security tokens as regulated securities under the Capital Markets Act
- **Germany:** Elektronische Wertpapiergesetz (eWpG, 2021) allows issuance of electronic and crypto securities

**Real Estate Tokenization:** Fractional ownership of real estate through tokenized crowdfunding has become a significant market segment:
- Germany's real estate crowdfunding market exceeds €1 billion annually
- South Korea permits real estate investment through securities-type crowdfunding
- United States platforms like RealT and Republic offer tokenized real estate through Regulation D/Regulation A+ exemptions

### Social Impact and Community-Based Crowdfunding

Beyond investment returns, crowdfunding increasingly serves social policy objectives:

| Type | Characteristics | Regulatory Treatment |
|------|----------------|---------------------|
| Community share offerings | Local community investment in social enterprises | UK: FCA-regulated; Germany: cooperative law; US: Regulation CF |
| Social bonds | Impact-linked financial returns | Generally regulated as securities |
| Civic crowdfunding | Citizen funding of public projects | Generally unregulated (donation-based) |
| Scientific crowdfunding | Research funding from public supporters | Varies; typically donation/reward-based |

### Cross-Border Crowdfunding

Cross-border crowdfunding remains challenging despite regulatory harmonization efforts:

- **EU:** The ECSPR creates a single passport allowing crowdfunding service providers authorized in one member state to operate across the EU; however, practical barriers remain, including language requirements, local payment infrastructure, and varying investor expectations
- **South Korea:** Cross-border crowdfunding is effectively limited by the Capital Markets Act's licensing requirements and Korean-language disclosure obligations
- **United States:** Regulation Crowdfunding and Regulation A+ offerings are generally limited to domestic issuers; foreign private issuers face significant compliance burdens
- The IOSCO report on crowdfunding regulation (2015, updated 2023) identified regulatory fragmentation as the primary obstacle to cross-border crowdfunding growth

### Investor Protection Outcomes

Data on investor outcomes in crowdfunding investments remains limited, but available evidence suggests:
- **United States:** Approximately 50 percent of Regulation CF offerings since 2016 have either failed or ceased operations, though early-stage investment inherently carries high failure rates
- **Germany:** Real estate crowdfunding has experienced notable defaults, including several Exporo-backed projects
- **South Korea:** KDIC does not cover crowdfunding investments; investor losses are borne entirely by investors
- All jurisdictions emphasize the importance of portfolio diversification and risk disclosure in crowdfunding investment

### Secondary Markets for Crowdfunding Instruments

The lack of liquidity for crowdfunding investments has prompted regulatory attention to secondary market development:

- **EU:** The ECSPR allows crowdfunding service providers to operate a bulletin board for buying and selling crowdfunded instruments, though this stops short of a full secondary market; the DLT Pilot Regime enables tokenized crowdfunding instruments to trade on distributed ledger-based platforms
- **United States:** Regulation CF instruments are subject to a 12-month resale restriction; the SEC has approved Alternative Trading Systems (ATS) for secondary trading of Regulation CF and Regulation A+ securities; platforms like StartEngine Secondary and Securitize Markets offer limited secondary liquidity
- **South Korea:** No dedicated secondary market for crowdfunding securities exists; FSC has explored the possibility of linking crowdfunding instruments to the K-OTC market for unlisted securities
- **Germany:** BaFin has authorized several platforms for secondary trading of crowdfunding instruments under MiFID II investment firm licenses
