## Summary

Involuntary commitment (강제 입원) -- the legal process by which individuals with mental disorders are confined to psychiatric facilities without their consent, typically on grounds that they pose a danger to themselves or others, or are unable to care for themselves due to mental illness -- is one of the most contested areas of healthcare law, involving fundamental tensions between individual liberty, the state's duty to protect vulnerable persons, and the right to health. South Korea fundamentally reformed its involuntary commitment framework through the comprehensive revision of the Mental Health and Welfare Act (정신건강복지법, renamed from the previous Mental Health Act in 2017), following the Constitutional Court's landmark 2016 decision (2014헌가9) declaring portions of the prior law unconstitutional for insufficient procedural protections; the reformed Act requires independent psychiatric evaluation by two psychiatrists from different institutions, mandatory review by a Mental Health Review Committee (정신건강심사위원회) within one month, and periodic judicial review. The United States regulates involuntary commitment through state law, with the constitutional framework established by the Supreme Court in O'Connor v. Donaldson (422 U.S. 563, 1975, holding that non-dangerous mentally ill persons cannot be confined against their will) and Addington v. Texas (441 U.S. 418, 1979, requiring clear and convincing evidence for civil commitment); all states now require either dangerousness to self or others or grave disability as the basis for involuntary commitment, with procedural protections including judicial hearings, right to counsel, and periodic review. Germany regulates involuntary commitment through the Psychisch-Kranken-Gesetze (PsychKG, Mental Health Acts) enacted by each of the 16 Bundesländer (federal states), supplemented by the Betreuungsrecht (guardianship law, BGB Sections 1814 et seq., reformed 2023) and constrained by the Bundesverfassungsgericht's (Federal Constitutional Court) significant jurisprudence establishing strict proportionality requirements; the 2011 and 2018 BVerfG decisions on forced medication fundamentally reshaped the conditions under which involuntary psychiatric treatment may be administered. At the European level, the European Court of Human Rights has developed extensive Article 5(1)(e) jurisprudence on the detention of persons of "unsound mind," establishing in Winterwerp v. Netherlands (1979) the foundational requirements that mental disorder must be established by objective medical expertise, the disorder must be of a kind or degree warranting compulsory confinement, and continued confinement depends on the persistence of the disorder; the ECHR has additionally addressed forced medication under Article 3 (prohibition of inhuman treatment) and Article 8 (respect for private life).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's involuntary commitment system was fundamentally transformed following the 2016 Constitutional Court decision and the enactment of the reformed Mental Health and Welfare Act.

**Constitutional Court Decision (2014헌가9, September 29, 2016):** The Constitutional Court declared unconstitutional the provisions of the former Mental Health Act that permitted involuntary hospitalization based solely on the consent of a family member and the diagnosis of a single psychiatrist from the admitting institution. The Court held that these provisions violated the constitutional right to physical freedom (Article 12, Constitution) and the right to dignity (Article 10) by failing to provide adequate procedural safeguards against unjustified deprivation of liberty. The Court gave the National Assembly until September 30, 2018, to enact reformed legislation.

**Mental Health and Welfare Act (정신건강복지법, 2017):** The reformed Act, effective May 30, 2017, introduced substantive and procedural safeguards:

### Commitment Procedures

**Voluntary Admission (자의입원):** The Act prioritizes voluntary admission, requiring psychiatric facilities to inform patients of their right to voluntary treatment and to respect discharge requests.

**Involuntary Commitment (비자의입원):** The Act establishes three types of non-voluntary admission:

| Type | Requirement | Duration |
|------|------------|----------|
| Consent-based admission (동의입원) | Patient's agreement + guardian consent; less restrictive than involuntary | Up to 2 months; renewable |
| Administrative involuntary admission (행정입원) | Local government head's order based on danger assessment | Up to 3 months; extendable with review |
| Family-requested admission (보호입원) | Two psychiatrists from different institutions + family request | Up to 3 months; committee review required |

### Key Safeguards

**Two-Physician Requirement:** Involuntary commitment now requires evaluation by two psychiatrists from different medical institutions (서로 다른 의료기관), eliminating the conflict of interest inherent in having the admitting institution's own psychiatrist as the sole evaluator.

**Mental Health Review Committee (정신건강심사위원회):** Each city, county, and district must establish a Mental Health Review Committee to review all involuntary commitments within one month of admission. The Committee includes psychiatrists, lawyers, and representatives of mental health service users.

**Periodic Review:** Continued involuntary commitment beyond the initial period requires periodic review at intervals not exceeding six months, with independent psychiatric re-evaluation.

### Impact of Reform

Following the 2017 reform, the number of involuntary psychiatric admissions in South Korea decreased by approximately 40 percent between 2016 and 2020. However, concerns have been raised about inadequate community mental health resources to support patients discharged or diverted from inpatient treatment.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States' involuntary commitment framework is shaped by constitutional jurisprudence and state statutory law.

**O'Connor v. Donaldson (422 U.S. 563, 1975):** The Supreme Court held that a state cannot constitutionally confine a non-dangerous individual who is capable of surviving safely in freedom by himself or with the help of willing and responsible family members or friends. This decision effectively established that dangerousness is a constitutional prerequisite for involuntary commitment.

**Addington v. Texas (441 U.S. 418, 1979):** The Court held that the standard of proof in civil commitment proceedings must be at least "clear and convincing evidence" -- higher than the preponderance standard used in ordinary civil cases but lower than the beyond-a-reasonable-doubt standard used in criminal cases.

### State Commitment Standards

All 50 states require at least one of the following criteria for involuntary commitment:

| Standard | Description | States |
|----------|------------|--------|
| Danger to self | Substantial risk of physical harm to oneself | All 50 states |
| Danger to others | Substantial risk of physical harm to others | All 50 states |
| Grave disability | Unable to provide for basic needs (food, clothing, shelter) | Approximately 45 states |
| Need for treatment | Deterioration without treatment (broader standard) | Approximately 12 states (controversial) |

### Procedural Requirements

**Emergency Commitment (72-hour hold):** Most states authorize temporary emergency detention for 48-72 hours based on certification by a physician, psychologist, or law enforcement officer that an individual meets commitment criteria. This detention triggers judicial review.

**Judicial Hearing:** A formal hearing before a judge or judicial officer must occur within the period specified by state law (typically 5-14 days). The individual has the right to counsel (appointed if indigent), the right to present evidence and cross-examine witnesses, and the right to an independent psychiatric evaluation.

**Outpatient Commitment (Assisted Outpatient Treatment):** Following the advocacy of Kendra's Law in New York (1999) and Laura's Law in California (2002), approximately 47 states now authorize court-ordered outpatient treatment as an alternative to inpatient commitment for individuals who meet specified criteria.

### Forced Medication

**Sell v. United States (539 U.S. 166, 2003):** The Supreme Court held that the government may involuntarily administer medication to render a mentally ill criminal defendant competent to stand trial, but only if (1) an important governmental interest is at stake, (2) forced medication will significantly further that interest, (3) involuntary medication is necessary to further that interest, and (4) the medication is medically appropriate.

**Washington v. Harper (494 U.S. 210, 1990):** The Court held that a prison inmate has a significant liberty interest in avoiding unwanted administration of antipsychotic drugs, but that interest may be overridden by a finding that the inmate is dangerous to himself or others and that the medication is in the inmate's medical interest.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's involuntary commitment framework is characterized by strong constitutional constraints and a federal structure that delegates primary responsibility to the Länder.

**Psychisch-Kranken-Gesetze (PsychKG):** Each of Germany's 16 federal states has enacted its own Mental Health Act (Psychisch-Kranken-Gesetz or Psychisch-Kranken-Hilfe-Gesetz), establishing the substantive and procedural requirements for involuntary commitment. While specific provisions vary, all state laws require:
- An acute mental disorder (including psychosis, severe depression with suicidal risk, or substance-induced crisis).
- A danger to the person's own life or health, or to the life, health, or essential interests of others.
- No less restrictive alternative (Verhältnismäßigkeit -- proportionality).
- Judicial authorization (richterliche Genehmigung).

**Betreuungsrecht (Guardianship Law, BGB Sections 1814 et seq.):** For persons under legal guardianship (Betreuung), commitment to a closed psychiatric facility requires court authorization under BGB Section 1831. The 2023 reform of guardianship law strengthened the supported decision-making approach, requiring that guardianship measures respect the wishes and preferences of the person concerned to the maximum extent possible.

### Bundesverfassungsgericht (Federal Constitutional Court) Jurisprudence

**Forced Medication Decisions:**

**BVerfG, Order of March 23, 2011 (2 BvR 882/09):** The Court struck down provisions of the Rhineland-Palatinate PsychKG authorizing forced medication, holding that forced psychiatric medication constitutes a serious interference with physical integrity (Article 2(2) Grundgesetz) and the right to self-determination. The Court established that forced medication is permissible only as an ultima ratio (last resort), requires a specific legal basis, and demands enhanced procedural safeguards.

**BVerfG, Order of October 12, 2011 (2 BvR 633/11):** Applying the same principles to the guardianship context, the Court held that forced medication under guardianship law requires: (1) the patient lacks the capacity to consent to or refuse treatment, (2) the patient has been given adequate opportunity for a personal explanation, (3) a prior attempt at persuasion has been made, (4) the treatment is necessary to avert a serious health danger, (5) no less intrusive alternative exists, and (6) the expected benefit clearly outweighs the expected burden.

### Fixation (Physical Restraint) Decision

**BVerfG, Judgment of July 24, 2018 (2 BvR 309/15 and 2 BvR 502/16):** The Court held that five-point fixation (Fünf-Punkt-Fixierung) of psychiatric patients constitutes a deprivation of liberty requiring prior judicial authorization (or, in emergency cases, subsequent judicial review without delay). This landmark decision extended judicial oversight requirements to mechanical restraint, previously considered a medical decision within institutional discretion.

### Judicial Authorization

All involuntary commitments require judicial authorization by the Betreuungsgericht (guardianship court). In emergencies, temporary commitment without judicial authorization is permitted for a limited period (typically 24 hours to the end of the following day), after which judicial confirmation must be obtained.

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights -- Article 5(1)(e)

Article 5(1)(e) ECHR permits the lawful detention of "persons of unsound mind" as an exception to the right to liberty. The ECHR has developed detailed requirements through extensive case law:

**Winterwerp v. Netherlands (Application No. 6301/73, Judgment of October 24, 1979):** The foundational judgment established three conditions for lawful detention of persons of unsound mind:
1. The individual must be reliably shown to be of "unsound mind" -- established by objective medical expertise.
2. The mental disorder must be of a kind or degree warranting compulsory confinement.
3. The validity of continued confinement depends on the persistence of the disorder.

**Stanev v. Bulgaria (Application No. 36760/06, Grand Chamber Judgment of January 17, 2012):** The Grand Chamber addressed the placement of an adult with limited legal capacity in a social care institution. The Court found violations of Articles 3, 5, and 13, holding that the conditions of the institution constituted degrading treatment and that the applicant had been deprived of his liberty without a procedure prescribed by law.

**Ilnseher v. Germany (Applications Nos. 10211/12 and 27505/14, Grand Chamber Judgment of December 4, 2018):** The Grand Chamber found no violation of Article 5 or 7 regarding the retrospective imposition of preventive detention on a mentally disordered offender. The Court confirmed that preventive detention must be accompanied by a genuine therapeutic purpose and appropriate treatment.

### Forced Medication Under the ECHR

**Herczegfalvy v. Austria (Application No. 10533/83, Judgment of September 24, 1992):** The Court held that a measure constituting therapeutic necessity from a medical perspective (here, forced medication and mechanical restraint) cannot in principle be regarded as inhuman or degrading treatment under Article 3. However, the Court must satisfy itself that the medical necessity has been convincingly shown and that procedural guarantees exist.

**X v. Finland (Application No. 34806/04, Judgment of July 3, 2012):** The Court found violations of Articles 5 and 8 where Finnish law permitted involuntary confinement and forced medication without adequate procedural safeguards. The Court emphasized that forced medication requires specific legal authority, medical justification, and judicial oversight.

### UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD)

The CRPD, ratified by South Korea (2008), the EU (2010), Germany (2009), and the United States (signed but not ratified), challenges involuntary commitment through Article 14 (liberty and security of the person), which the CRPD Committee interprets as prohibiting deprivation of liberty based on disability, including psychosocial disability. This interpretation creates tension with the ECHR Article 5(1)(e) framework and domestic commitment laws, and jurisdictions have responded with varying degrees of accommodation.

## Comparative Analysis

### Commitment Standards

All jurisdictions require mental disorder plus dangerousness (to self or others) or inability to care for oneself. The US Addington standard (clear and convincing evidence) provides the most explicit evidentiary threshold. Germany's proportionality principle requires demonstration that no less restrictive alternative exists. South Korea's two-physician requirement from different institutions provides unique safeguards against institutional conflicts of interest.

### Procedural Protections

Germany provides the strongest constitutional constraints through the BVerfG's detailed jurisprudence on forced medication and physical restraint. South Korea's 2017 reform significantly strengthened procedural protections through the two-physician requirement and Mental Health Review Committee. The US provides robust procedural protections through judicial hearings and right to counsel.

### Forced Medication

The conditions for forced psychiatric medication have converged across jurisdictions toward requiring: (1) incapacity to consent, (2) medical necessity, (3) least restrictive alternative analysis, and (4) judicial or quasi-judicial authorization. Germany's BVerfG and the ECHR have established the strictest proportionality requirements. The US Supreme Court's Sell framework applies primarily in the criminal competency context.

### Community Mental Health

The shift toward community-based care and away from institutionalization is a global trend, but implementation varies significantly. The US's outpatient commitment laws (Kendra's Law model) represent one approach. South Korea faces challenges in developing community mental health infrastructure following its reform. Germany's system benefits from robust social welfare infrastructure but faces capacity constraints.
