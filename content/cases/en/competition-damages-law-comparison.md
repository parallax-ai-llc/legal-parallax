## Summary

Private competition damages actions -- civil claims by victims of anticompetitive conduct for compensation of harm suffered -- represent one of the most dynamically evolving areas of comparative law. The interplay between public enforcement (fines and behavioral remedies imposed by competition authorities) and private enforcement (damages claims by harmed parties) varies dramatically across jurisdictions, reflecting different policy choices about deterrence, compensation, and the proper role of private litigation in competition law enforcement. South Korea provides for private damages actions under Article 56 of the Monopoly Regulation and Fair Trade Act (독점규제 및 공정거래에 관한 법률, MRFTA), supplemented by recent reforms expanding the scope of treble damages and introducing presumptions of harm. The United States has the world's most developed private antitrust enforcement regime, with Section 4 of the Clayton Act (15 U.S.C. Section 15) providing for treble damages, and class action litigation serving as a primary enforcement mechanism, generating billions of dollars in settlements annually. Germany has implemented the EU Antitrust Damages Directive (2014/104/EU) through the 9th Amendment to the Act against Restraints of Competition (Gesetz gegen Wettbewerbsbeschränkungen, GWB), establishing a comprehensive framework for follow-on and standalone damages actions. At the EU level, the Damages Directive establishes harmonized rules on access to evidence, limitation periods, the effect of national competition authority decisions, the passing-on defense, and the quantification of harm, while the ECHR has recognized the right to compensation for competition law violations under Article 6 and Article 1 of Protocol No. 1.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's private competition damages framework has undergone significant expansion since 2019, reflecting a policy shift toward strengthening private enforcement alongside the Korean Fair Trade Commission's (KFTC, 공정거래위원회) dominant public enforcement role.

### MRFTA Damages Provisions

**Article 56 (Damages Liability):** Any person who has suffered loss as a result of a violation of the MRFTA may claim damages from the violator. The 2020 comprehensive amendment (effective December 30, 2021) substantially reformed the damages provisions, introducing presumptions of harm and expanding treble damages.

**Article 56-2 (Presumption of Harm for Cartels):** A cartel infringement is presumed to have caused harm to the persons affected, shifting the burden to the defendant to rebut the presumption. This provision addresses the historically high evidentiary burden faced by private plaintiffs in proving causation and harm.

**Article 56-3 (Treble Damages):** Introduced in 2019 for cartel violations and expanded in the 2020 amendment to cover abuse of market dominance and unfair trade practices, this provision permits courts to award up to three times the actual damages when the violation is intentional. In determining the multiplier, courts consider the degree of intent, the scale and duration of the violation, the economic benefit derived, and the number of times the violation has occurred.

### Interaction with KFTC Proceedings

The KFTC's findings and corrective orders serve as powerful evidence in subsequent private damages actions. Under Article 56-2, KFTC final decisions on cartel violations create a rebuttable presumption of the existence of the infringement and the harm caused. However, KFTC consent orders (동의의결) do not carry this presumptive effect.

### Key Case Law

**Supreme Court Decision 2012Da11775 (2014) -- Flour Cartel Case:** The Court held that plaintiff purchasers could recover damages based on the difference between the actual price paid during the cartel period and the "but-for" price that would have prevailed absent the cartel. The Court accepted econometric regression analysis as a valid method for establishing the overcharge.

**Supreme Court Decision 2017Da241304 (2019) -- Military Fuel Supply Cartel:** The Court clarified the limitation period for competition damages, holding that the 3-year limitation runs from when the plaintiff had actual or constructive knowledge of both the violation and the resulting harm. Knowledge of the KFTC's corrective order was held to constitute constructive knowledge.

**Seoul High Court Decision 2019Na2049379 (2021) -- First Treble Damages Award:** The Seoul High Court issued the first treble damages award under Article 56-3, finding that the defendant's participation in a bid-rigging cartel was intentional and warranted enhanced damages of approximately KRW 15 billion.

### Quantification of Harm

Korean courts have accepted various methods of damages quantification, including the before-and-after method (comparing prices during and outside the cartel period), the benchmark method (comparing prices in affected and unaffected markets), and econometric regression analysis. Expert economic evidence has become increasingly sophisticated, with courts appointing court experts alongside party-retained experts.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has by far the most developed private antitrust enforcement system globally, with private treble damages actions generating an estimated $5-10 billion in settlements annually and serving as a complement to DOJ criminal enforcement and FTC civil enforcement.

### Clayton Act Section 4

**15 U.S.C. Section 15:** "Any person who shall be injured in his business or property by reason of anything forbidden in the antitrust laws may sue therefor in any district court of the United States... and shall recover threefold the damages by him sustained, and the cost of suit, including a reasonable attorney's fee."

The treble damages provision is mandatory -- courts have no discretion to award single or double damages once liability is established. This provision, dating to the original Clayton Act of 1914, reflects Congress's intent that private enforcement serve as a critical supplement to public enforcement, with the treble multiplier serving both compensatory and deterrent functions.

### Illinois Brick and Direct Purchaser Rule

**Illinois Brick Co. v. Illinois, 431 U.S. 720 (1977):** The Supreme Court held that only direct purchasers from the antitrust violator have standing to sue for damages under federal antitrust law. Indirect purchasers (those who purchased from intermediaries who passed on the overcharge) are barred from federal claims.

**State Illinois Brick Repealer Statutes:** In response, 37 states and the District of Columbia have enacted "Illinois Brick repealer" statutes permitting indirect purchaser damages claims under state antitrust or consumer protection laws. This creates a complex dual-track system where direct purchasers sue federally while indirect purchasers sue in state court, sometimes leading to overlapping recovery.

**Apple Inc. v. Pepper, 587 U.S. 27 (2019):** The Supreme Court held that iPhone users are direct purchasers from Apple for purposes of antitrust standing when they buy apps through the App Store, even though independent developers set app prices. This decision clarified the direct purchaser rule in platform markets.

### Passing-On Defense

**Hanover Shoe, Inc. v. United Shoe Machinery Corp., 392 U.S. 481 (1968):** The Supreme Court held that a defendant may not defend against a direct purchaser's damages claim by arguing that the plaintiff passed on the overcharge to its own customers. This rule, paired with Illinois Brick, creates a system where direct purchasers recover the entire overcharge regardless of whether they absorbed or passed on the harm.

### Class Action Antitrust Litigation

Class actions under FRCP Rule 23 are the primary vehicle for private antitrust damages claims involving numerous claimants. Antitrust class certification requires demonstration that common questions predominate, typically focusing on whether class-wide proof of impact and damages is feasible.

**In re Hydrogen Peroxide Antitrust Litigation, 552 F.3d 305 (3d Cir. 2008):** The Third Circuit held that class certification requires a rigorous analysis of the evidence and that common proof of antitrust impact must be capable of showing class-wide injury.

**Comcast Corp. v. Behrend, 569 U.S. 27 (2013):** The Supreme Court held that a class-wide damages model must be consistent with the theory of liability and must be capable of calculating damages on a class-wide basis.

### Criminal-Civil Interface

DOJ criminal antitrust prosecutions are followed by a wave of private "follow-on" damages actions. A guilty plea or conviction in a criminal antitrust case is admissible as prima facie evidence of the violation in subsequent civil proceedings under 15 U.S.C. Section 16(a). Nolo contendere pleas, however, are not admissible.

**Leniency Program Implications:** To incentivize leniency applications, the Antitrust Criminal Penalty Enhancement and Reform Act of 2004 (ACPERA, renewed in 2020) reduces the civil damages exposure of leniency applicants from treble to single damages, provided the applicant cooperates with private plaintiffs.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany has transformed from a jurisdiction with negligible private antitrust enforcement to one of Europe's most active, driven by the EU Damages Directive transposition and the Bundeskartellamt's (Federal Cartel Office) prolific enforcement record.

### GWB 9th Amendment (2017)

The 9th Amendment to the GWB, effective June 9, 2017, transposed Directive 2014/104/EU into German law, introducing comprehensive provisions on private damages in Sections 33a through 33h.

**Section 33a (Claim for Damages):** Any person who has suffered harm as a result of a competition law infringement may claim damages from the infringer. The provision explicitly grants standing to both direct and indirect purchasers, resolving a prior ambiguity in German law.

**Section 33a(2) (Presumption of Harm for Cartels):** There is a rebuttable presumption that cartel infringements cause harm. This shifts the burden to the defendant to demonstrate that the cartel did not result in a price increase or other competitive harm.

**Section 33b (Binding Effect of NCA Decisions):** Final decisions by the Bundeskartellamt, the European Commission, or other EU national competition authorities finding a competition law infringement are binding on German civil courts in subsequent damages actions. This provision is one of the most powerful facilitators of follow-on damages actions in Europe.

**Section 33c (Passing-On Defense):** The defendant may raise the defense that the claimant passed on the overcharge to its own customers. The burden of proving passing-on lies with the defendant. Indirect purchasers may claim damages for overcharges passed on to them, with a rebuttable presumption that overcharges are passed on in a supply chain.

**Section 33h (Limitation Period):** The limitation period is 5 years, running from the end of the infringement and the claimant's knowledge (or duty to know) of the infringement and the harm. The limitation period is suspended during proceedings before a competition authority and during consensual dispute resolution.

### Key German Cases

**LKW-Kartell (Truck Cartel) Litigation:** Following the European Commission's decisions fining truck manufacturers (Daimler, MAN, Volvo/Renault, DAF, Iveco) a total of approximately EUR 3.8 billion for price-fixing, thousands of follow-on damages claims have been filed in German courts. The Landgericht Stuttgart and other courts have issued important rulings on damages quantification, the binding effect of Commission decisions, and the scope of the passing-on defense.

**BGH, KZR 19/20 (Rail Cartel, 2022):** The BGH addressed the quantification of cartel damages, holding that courts should estimate damages under Section 287 ZPO (judicial estimation) when precise calculation is impossible, and that the but-for price must reflect the competitive conditions that would have prevailed absent the cartel.

**BGH, KZR 35/17 (Schienenkartell II, 2020):** The BGH confirmed that the binding effect of NCA decisions under Section 33b GWB extends to factual findings relevant to the determination of the infringement, not merely the legal characterization.

### Damages Quantification

German courts have developed sophisticated approaches to damages quantification. The Bundeskartellamt has published guidance on damages quantification, and courts increasingly rely on economic expert evidence. The "but-for" method (Differenzmethode) compares actual prices with hypothetical competitive prices, using econometric techniques including regression analysis, benchmark comparisons, and simulation models.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Antitrust Damages Directive (2014/104/EU)

Adopted on November 26, 2014, with a transposition deadline of December 27, 2016, the Directive establishes harmonized rules facilitating private damages actions for competition law infringements across the EU.

**Background -- Courage v. Crehan (C-453/99, 2001):** The CJEU established that EU law confers on any individual the right to claim damages for harm caused by a breach of EU competition law. This foundational principle was reaffirmed in Manfredi v. Lloyd Adriatico Assicurazioni (C-295/04, 2006), where the Court held that national courts must ensure the full effectiveness of EU competition law, including by permitting damages claims and ensuring effective remedies.

**Article 3 (Right to Full Compensation):** Any natural or legal person who has suffered harm caused by an infringement of competition law has the right to claim and obtain full compensation. Full compensation covers actual loss (damnum emergens) and loss of profit (lucrum cessans), plus interest. The Directive prohibits overcompensation through punitive, multiple, or other damages.

**Article 5 (Disclosure of Evidence):** Courts may order the disclosure of specific items of evidence or categories of evidence from the opposing party or third parties. Proportionality requirements apply, and the Directive protects leniency statements and settlement submissions from disclosure (Article 6).

**Article 9 (Effect of National Decisions):** A final decision of a national competition authority or review court finding an infringement constitutes irrebuttable proof of the infringement in subsequent damages actions before courts of the same member state. Decisions of NCAs of other member states constitute at least prima facie evidence.

**Article 12-16 (Passing-On):** The Directive establishes a comprehensive framework for the passing-on defense and indirect purchaser claims. Defendants may raise the defense that claimants passed on overcharges. Indirect purchasers may claim damages with a rebuttable presumption of pass-on. Courts must take reasonable steps to avoid multiple recovery.

**Article 17 (Quantification of Harm):** National courts may estimate the amount of harm if quantification is practically impossible or disproportionately difficult on the basis of evidence available. A rebuttable presumption establishes that cartel infringements cause harm.

### European Commission Practical Guide on Quantification

The Commission published a Practical Guide on Quantifying Harm in Actions for Damages based on Breaches of Articles 101 and 102 TFEU (SWD(2013) 205), providing guidance on economic and econometric methods for calculating overcharges, including comparison-based methods, financial analysis, and market simulation models.

### Collective Redress for Competition Damages

**Directive 2020/1828 on Representative Actions:** This directive complements the Damages Directive by providing a collective redress mechanism for consumer competition damages, enabling qualified entities to bring representative actions on behalf of affected consumers.

**National Collective Redress Mechanisms:** The Netherlands has become a preferred jurisdiction for collective competition damages actions through its WCAM settlement procedure (Wet Collectieve Afwikkeling Massaschade) and, since 2020, the WAMCA (Wet Afwikkeling Massaschade in Collectieve Actie) class action legislation. The UK Competition Appeal Tribunal (CAT) administers an opt-out collective proceedings regime under the Consumer Rights Act 2015.

### ECHR Considerations

**Article 6 (Right to a Fair Trial):** Access to judicial determination of competition damages claims is protected under Article 6. The ECHR has recognized that excessive procedural barriers to damages claims may violate the right of access to court.

**Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (Protection of Property):** Competition damages claims constitute "possessions" or at least legitimate expectations of obtaining assets. State measures that undermine or extinguish existing damages claims may engage property rights protections.

**Menarini Diagnostics v. Italy (Application No. 43509/08, September 27, 2011):** While primarily addressing public enforcement, the ECHR held that competition law proceedings resulting in substantial fines must comply with Article 6 fair trial guarantees, including judicial review with full jurisdiction. This principle extends to ensuring that private damages proceedings provide adequate procedural protections.

## Comparative Analysis

### Damages Multiplier and Deterrence

The most fundamental divergence is the US mandatory treble damages versus the EU/German compensatory-only approach. South Korea's 2019 introduction of discretionary treble damages represents an intermediate position. The US treble damages system generates far greater deterrence and incentivizes private enforcement, but critics argue it encourages over-litigation and disproportionate settlements. The EU model prioritizes compensation over deterrence, relying primarily on public enforcement fines for deterrence.

### Passing-On and Indirect Purchaser Claims

The Hanover Shoe/Illinois Brick framework creates a uniquely American system where direct purchasers recover the entire overcharge (even if passed on) while indirect purchasers have no federal claim. The EU and South Korea permit both the passing-on defense and indirect purchaser claims, creating a more complex but arguably fairer allocation of recovery. The challenge in all jurisdictions is avoiding under-compensation (where no one recovers) or over-compensation (where both direct and indirect purchasers recover the full overcharge).

### Follow-On vs. Standalone Actions

Most private competition damages actions worldwide are follow-on claims based on prior public enforcement decisions. The binding effect of NCA decisions under the EU Directive (Article 9) and German law (Section 33b GWB) powerfully facilitates follow-on actions. South Korea similarly gives KFTC decisions evidential weight. The US system, where guilty pleas are prima facie evidence but consent decrees are not, takes an intermediate approach. Standalone actions (without a prior public enforcement decision) remain relatively rare in all jurisdictions due to the evidentiary burden.

### Collective Redress Gaps

While the US provides comprehensive class action capability for antitrust claims, collective redress for competition damages remains underdeveloped in most other jurisdictions. Germany's Musterfeststellungsklage and the EU Representative Actions Directive are steps forward, but participation rates in opt-in systems are typically low, limiting their effectiveness for diffuse consumer harm from competition infringements.
