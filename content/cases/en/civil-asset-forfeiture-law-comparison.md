## Summary

Civil asset forfeiture -- the legal process by which the state seizes and permanently confiscates property alleged to be connected to criminal activity, often without requiring a criminal conviction of the property owner -- represents one of the most controversial areas of criminal procedure, raising fundamental questions about due process, property rights, the presumption of innocence, and the potential for abuse when law enforcement agencies have financial incentives to seize assets. South Korea's forfeiture system operates primarily through criminal forfeiture provisions in Article 48 of the Criminal Act (형법) and specialized statutes including the Act on Regulation and Punishment of Criminal Proceeds Concealment (범죄수익은닉의 규제 및 처벌 등에 관한 법률, 2001) and the Act on Special Cases Concerning the Prevention of Illegal Trafficking in Narcotics (마약류 불법거래 방지에 관한 특례법), requiring a criminal conviction as a prerequisite for forfeiture in most cases and providing for the confiscation of proceeds and instrumentalities of crime through court orders. The United States operates the most extensive civil asset forfeiture system in the world, with federal civil forfeiture governed by 18 U.S.C. Section 981 and 21 U.S.C. Section 881 (narcotics), permitting the government to bring in rem proceedings against property itself without charging the owner with a crime -- a system that has generated billions of dollars in revenue for federal, state, and local law enforcement agencies through the Department of Justice's Asset Forfeiture Program and the Equitable Sharing Program, while facing growing bipartisan criticism culminating in the Supreme Court's landmark ruling in Timbs v. Indiana, 586 U.S. 149 (2019) incorporating the Excessive Fines Clause against the states. Germany distinguishes between Einziehung (confiscation) under Sections 73-76b StGB, reformed comprehensively in 2017 to permit non-conviction-based confiscation of proceeds of crime in limited circumstances, and Verfall (forfeiture of proceeds), with the Federal Constitutional Court requiring strict proportionality and adequate procedural safeguards. The European framework addresses asset confiscation through EU Directive 2014/42/EU on the freezing and confiscation of instrumentalities and proceeds of crime, which establishes minimum rules for member states while requiring extended confiscation and permitting non-conviction-based confiscation in limited circumstances, and the ECHR's jurisprudence under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (protection of property), which has upheld forfeiture schemes while requiring proportionality and procedural fairness.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's asset forfeiture system is primarily conviction-based, operating through several statutory frameworks that require a criminal judgment before property may be confiscated.

### Criminal Act Provisions

Article 48 of the Criminal Act (형법) authorizes the confiscation (몰수) of:
- Objects that were used or intended for use in committing a crime (instrumentalities)
- Objects that were acquired through or as a result of a crime (proceeds)
- Objects that were received as consideration for a crime

Confiscation under Article 48 is ordered as part of the criminal sentence and requires a conviction. Where confiscation of the original object is not possible, the court may order forfeiture of an equivalent value (추징) under Article 48(2).

### Act on Regulation and Punishment of Criminal Proceeds Concealment (범죄수익은닉의 규제 및 처벌 등에 관한 법률)

Enacted in 2001 to comply with the Financial Action Task Force (FATF) recommendations, this Act provides enhanced forfeiture powers for proceeds of specified serious offenses including drug trafficking, fraud, corruption, and organized crime. Key features include:

- **Presumption of criminal proceeds:** For specified offenses, property acquired during or after the criminal period may be presumed to constitute criminal proceeds unless the defendant demonstrates lawful origin
- **Third-party confiscation:** Property transferred to third parties may be confiscated if the third party knew or should have known of the criminal origin
- **Provisional seizure:** The prosecution may apply for court orders freezing suspected criminal proceeds pending trial

### Narcotics Forfeiture

The Act on Special Cases Concerning the Prevention of Illegal Trafficking in Narcotics provides for enhanced forfeiture of drug trafficking proceeds, including the confiscation of property that cannot be traced directly to specific transactions but is found to be disproportionate to the defendant's lawful income.

### Constitutional Limitations

The Constitutional Court has held that forfeiture must comply with the proportionality principle (비례의 원칙) derived from Article 37(2) of the Constitution. In Decision 2012HeonBa168 (2014), the Court struck down a provision permitting forfeiture of a vehicle used in a single minor drug offense, finding it disproportionate to the gravity of the offense.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States operates the most extensive asset forfeiture system in the world, with two distinct procedural tracks: civil forfeiture (in rem proceedings against the property) and criminal forfeiture (in personam proceedings against the defendant).

### Civil Forfeiture

Civil forfeiture proceeds against the property itself, treating the property as the defendant in an in rem action. The government need not charge or convict the property owner with any crime. The legal fiction that property can be "guilty" derives from English admiralty law and was established in American law as early as The Palmyra, 25 U.S. (12 Wheat.) 1 (1827).

**Federal Statutes:**
- 18 U.S.C. Section 981: Civil forfeiture of property involved in specified federal offenses
- 21 U.S.C. Section 881: Civil forfeiture of property related to drug offenses
- 31 U.S.C. Section 5317: Civil forfeiture of currency involved in structuring or reporting violations

### Civil Asset Forfeiture Reform Act (CAFRA) of 2000

CAFRA (Pub. L. 106-185) enacted several reforms:
- Shifted the burden of proof from the property owner to the government
- Required the government to prove by a preponderance of evidence that the property is subject to forfeiture
- Codified an innocent owner defense (18 U.S.C. Section 983(d))
- Provided for court-appointed counsel for indigent claimants in certain cases
- Established a requirement for prompt post-seizure notice

### Scale of Forfeiture

The scope of U.S. asset forfeiture is staggering:

| Metric | Amount |
|--------|--------|
| DOJ Assets Forfeiture Fund balance (FY 2023) | Approximately $4.7 billion |
| Annual deposits to DOJ Fund | Approximately $1.5-2 billion |
| Equitable Sharing payments to state/local agencies (2019) | Approximately $440 million |
| Total forfeiture since program inception (1985) | Over $36 billion |

### Equitable Sharing Program

The DOJ Equitable Sharing Program permits state and local law enforcement agencies to transfer seized assets to federal authorities for forfeiture under federal law, with the seizing agency receiving up to 80% of the forfeited proceeds. Critics argue this program incentivizes seizures and allows state agencies to circumvent more restrictive state forfeiture laws.

### Constitutional Limitations

**Timbs v. Indiana, 586 U.S. 149 (2019):** The Supreme Court unanimously held that the Excessive Fines Clause of the Eighth Amendment is incorporated against the states through the Fourteenth Amendment, applying to civil forfeiture proceedings. The case involved the seizure of Tyson Timbs' $42,000 Land Rover after a drug conviction carrying a maximum fine of $10,000.

**Austin v. United States, 509 U.S. 602 (1993):** The Court held that civil in rem forfeiture constitutes "punishment" for purposes of the Excessive Fines Clause, subjecting it to proportionality review.

**United States v. Bajakajian, 524 U.S. 321 (1998):** The Court established that a forfeiture is "grossly disproportional" to the gravity of the offense if the amount forfeited bears no relationship to the harm caused.

### State Reforms

Growing criticism of civil forfeiture has prompted state-level reforms. As of 2025:
- Seven states (Nebraska, New Mexico, North Carolina, Maine, Connecticut, Minnesota, and Vermont) have abolished civil forfeiture entirely
- Numerous states have raised the burden of proof requirement
- Several states have restricted or prohibited the Equitable Sharing workaround

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's confiscation system underwent comprehensive reform through the Act Reforming Criminal Asset Recovery (Gesetz zur Reform der strafrechtlichen Vermögensabschöpfung), effective July 1, 2017. The reform restructured Sections 73-76b StGB, replacing the previous distinction between Verfall (forfeiture of proceeds) and Einziehung (confiscation of instrumentalities) with a unified system of Einziehung covering both categories.

### Einziehung von Taterträgen (Confiscation of Proceeds) -- Section 73 StGB

Section 73 StGB provides that the court shall order confiscation of anything that the perpetrator or participant obtained through or for the commission of an unlawful act. The confiscation order covers the "gross principle" (Bruttoprinzip), meaning the total value obtained without deduction of expenses incurred in committing the offense.

### Independent Confiscation (Selbständige Einziehung) -- Section 76a StGB

The 2017 reform introduced non-conviction-based confiscation in two circumstances:
- **Section 76a(1) StGB:** Where criminal proceedings cannot be conducted or concluded (e.g., death of suspect, statute of limitations)
- **Section 76a(4) StGB (Erweiterte Einziehung von unklarer Herkunft):** Extended confiscation of assets of unclear origin, applicable where an object was seized in connection with an unlawful act and the court is convinced that it was obtained through or for an unlawful act -- even without conviction for a specific offense

### Proportionality

The Federal Constitutional Court has consistently held that confiscation must comply with the Übermaßverbot (prohibition of excess) under the proportionality principle. In BVerfG, 2 BvR 1437/14 (2018), the Court upheld the constitutionality of the gross principle but emphasized that courts must conduct an individualized proportionality assessment, particularly where confiscation would cause severe hardship to the offender or their family.

### Third-Party Rights

Section 73b StGB permits confiscation from third parties who obtained the proceeds gratuitously or without legal justification, while Section 73e StGB protects bona fide third-party acquisitions.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Directive 2014/42/EU

Directive 2014/42/EU of the European Parliament and of the Council of April 3, 2014, on the freezing and confiscation of instrumentalities and proceeds of crime in the European Union establishes minimum rules:

**Article 4 -- Confiscation:** Requires member states to take measures enabling confiscation of instrumentalities and proceeds, or property of equivalent value, following a final conviction.

**Article 5 -- Extended Confiscation:** Requires member states to enable confiscation of property belonging to a person convicted of a criminal offense that is liable to give rise directly or indirectly to economic benefit, where the court is satisfied that the property is derived from criminal conduct.

**Article 6 -- Non-Conviction-Based Confiscation:** Permits member states to take measures enabling confiscation where criminal proceedings have been initiated but cannot be continued due to illness, absconding, or death of the suspect.

### ECHR -- Article 1 of Protocol No. 1

The ECHR has addressed forfeiture under the protection of property guarantee:

**Raimondo v. Italy, Application No. 12954/87, Judgment of February 22, 1994:** The Court accepted that confiscation of assets suspected to be of criminal origin pursues a legitimate aim in the general interest and is not per se disproportionate, even when applied preventively without a conviction.

**Silickienė v. Lithuania, Application No. 20496/02, Judgment of April 10, 2012:** The Court found a violation of Article 1 Protocol 1 where civil confiscation proceedings did not provide adequate procedural safeguards to the property owner, particularly regarding the opportunity to effectively challenge the seizure.

**Todorov and Others v. Bulgaria, Applications Nos. 50705/11 et al., Judgment of July 13, 2021:** The Court found no violation where Bulgaria's civil forfeiture scheme, based on a finding of unexplained wealth, was applied with adequate procedural safeguards and proportionality review.

### EU Anti-Money Laundering Framework

Directive (EU) 2024/1640 of the European Parliament and of the Council (the Sixth Anti-Money Laundering Directive), adopted in 2024, strengthens the framework for confiscation of criminal proceeds by enhancing beneficial ownership transparency and extending the scope of predicate offenses for money laundering confiscation.

## Comparative Analysis

### Conviction Requirement

The most fundamental divergence concerns whether a criminal conviction is required. The United States permits civil forfeiture without any criminal charge, making it uniquely aggressive among the jurisdictions examined. South Korea and Germany (traditionally) require a conviction. The 2017 German reform and EU Directive 2014/42/EU permit non-conviction-based confiscation in limited circumstances (death, flight, illness of suspect).

### Financial Incentives

The U.S. Equitable Sharing Program creates a direct financial incentive for law enforcement agencies to pursue forfeiture, a feature absent in the other jurisdictions where forfeited assets flow to the general treasury. This structural incentive has been identified by scholars and reformers as a primary driver of abusive forfeiture practices.

### Burden of Proof

The United States applies a preponderance of evidence standard in civil forfeiture -- the lowest standard of any jurisdiction for permanent deprivation of property. South Korea requires proof beyond a reasonable doubt as part of a criminal conviction. Germany requires judicial conviction or, for independent confiscation, judicial satisfaction that the property derives from criminal conduct.

### Proportionality

Germany and the ECHR apply the most rigorous proportionality review. The United States historically had minimal proportionality constraints until Timbs v. Indiana (2019) incorporated the Excessive Fines Clause against the states, but the scope of this protection remains underdeveloped.
