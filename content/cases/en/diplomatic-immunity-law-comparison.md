## Summary

Diplomatic immunity -- the principle of international law that grants foreign diplomatic agents protection from the jurisdiction of the host state's courts and enforcement authorities -- is one of the oldest and most universally recognized doctrines in international relations, codified primarily in the Vienna Convention on Diplomatic Relations (VCDR) of 1961 and the Vienna Convention on Consular Relations (VCCR) of 1963. South Korea implements diplomatic immunity through the Diplomatic Relations Act (외교관계에 관한 법률) and related provisions of the Criminal Act (형법) and Civil Act (민법), while facing unique challenges related to the status of the United States Forces Korea (USFK) under the ROK-US Status of Forces Agreement (SOFA, 주한미군 지위협정), which has generated significant public debate when crimes committed by foreign military personnel have been perceived as inadequately prosecuted. The United States implements the VCDR through the Diplomatic Relations Act of 1978 (22 U.S.C. Section 254a-254e) and manages one of the world's largest diplomatic communities in Washington, D.C., with the State Department's Office of Foreign Missions administering privileges and immunities for approximately 50,000 persons in the United States; the Supreme Court's landmark decision in Republic of Austria v. Altmann (2004) and cases involving diplomatic personnel accused of crimes have shaped the scope and limits of immunity. Germany, hosting a substantial diplomatic corps in Berlin and as the seat of numerous international organizations, implements the VCDR through the Gesetz über die Vorrechte und Immunitäten der internationalen Organisationen and applies immunity through provisions of the Gerichtsverfassungsgesetz (Courts Constitution Act) Section 18-20, with German courts having developed significant jurisprudence on the boundaries between diplomatic and state immunity. At the European level, the ECHR has confronted the tension between diplomatic immunity and access to justice under Article 6 (right to a fair trial), with the European Court of Human Rights establishing in cases such as Cudak v. Lithuania (2010) and Sabeh El Leil v. France (2011) that blanket grants of immunity to diplomatic missions in employment disputes may violate the Convention.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea ratified the Vienna Convention on Diplomatic Relations on December 28, 1970, and the Vienna Convention on Consular Relations on January 10, 1977. Domestic implementation is accomplished through the Diplomatic Relations Act (외교관계에 관한 법률) and related provisions. Under the Korean Constitution (헌법) Article 6(1), treaties duly concluded and promulgated have the same force as domestic law, giving the VCDR direct applicability in Korean courts.

### ROK-US Status of Forces Agreement (SOFA)

The most contentious diplomatic immunity issue in South Korea concerns the ROK-US SOFA (대한민국과 미합중국 간의 상호방위조약 제4조에 의한 시설과 구역 및 대한민국에서의 합중국군대의 지위에 관한 협정), originally signed on July 9, 1966, and revised in 2001. The SOFA grants United States military personnel, civilian employees, and their dependents a modified form of jurisdictional immunity.

**Article XXII (Criminal Jurisdiction):** The SOFA establishes concurrent jurisdiction, with the United States retaining primary jurisdiction over offenses arising from official duty and offenses committed solely against US property or personnel. South Korea retains primary jurisdiction over all other offenses. However, the exercise of Korean jurisdiction has historically been contested, with several high-profile incidents generating public outrage.

### Notable Incidents

**The Yun Geum-i Case (1992):** Private Kenneth Markle, a US soldier, was convicted by a Korean court for the murder of sex worker Yun Geum-i in Dongducheon. The case became a rallying point for advocates of SOFA revision and broader anti-base movements.

**The Yangju Highway Incident (2002):** Two Korean middle school students, Shin Hyo-sun (신효순) and Shim Mi-seon (심미선), were killed by a US military armored vehicle during a training exercise on June 13, 2002. The two soldiers were tried by US military courts-martial under SOFA provisions and acquitted, sparking massive anti-American protests and demands for SOFA revision. The incident was a major factor in the 2001 SOFA revision negotiations.

### Penalties for Violations of Diplomatic Immunity

| Violation | Consequence |
|-----------|------------|
| Criminal offense by diplomat | Request for waiver of immunity; PNG declaration; criminal prosecution if waiver granted |
| SOFA-covered offenses (Korean primary jurisdiction) | Korean criminal prosecution; subject to SOFA Article XXII procedures |
| SOFA-covered offenses (US primary jurisdiction) | US military court-martial; Korean observers permitted |
| Civil claims against diplomatic personnel | Generally barred; insurance or ex gratia payments negotiated diplomatically |

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States ratified the VCDR on November 13, 1972, and implements it domestically through the Diplomatic Relations Act of 1978 (22 U.S.C. Sections 254a-254e). The Act provides that any action or proceeding brought against a diplomatic agent who is entitled to immunity shall be dismissed, and the courts shall grant immunity in accordance with the VCDR.

**Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act (FSIA, 1976):** While primarily concerning state immunity rather than diplomatic immunity, the FSIA (28 U.S.C. Sections 1602-1611) governs suits against foreign states and their agencies in US courts. The Supreme Court clarified the relationship between the FSIA and diplomatic immunity in Republic of Austria v. Altmann, 541 U.S. 677 (2004), holding that the FSIA applies retroactively to conduct predating its enactment.

**State Department Office of Foreign Missions:** The Office of Foreign Missions (OFM), established under the Foreign Missions Act of 1982 (22 U.S.C. Section 4301 et seq.), administers the privileges and immunities of foreign missions and their personnel. The OFM processes approximately 50,000 identity cards for diplomatic and consular personnel and their families.

### Notable Cases

**Gueorgui Makharadze Incident (1997):** Georgian Ambassador Gueorgui Makharadze caused a fatal car accident in Washington, D.C., killing a 16-year-old girl while driving under the influence. Georgia waived Makharadze's diplomatic immunity at the request of the State Department. He was convicted of involuntary manslaughter and sentenced to 7-21 years imprisonment, serving three years before being transferred to Georgian custody.

**Dominique Strauss-Kahn (2011):** While the IMF Managing Director's arrest in New York involved international organization immunity rather than diplomatic immunity, the case highlighted the distinction between different immunity regimes. Strauss-Kahn did not claim immunity, and the IMF did not assert it on his behalf.

**Anne Sacoolas Case (2019):** The wife of a US intelligence operative stationed at RAF Croughton in the United Kingdom struck and killed British teenager Harry Dunn while driving on the wrong side of the road. The US initially asserted diplomatic immunity on Sacoolas's behalf, causing a major diplomatic incident. The UK charged Sacoolas with causing death by dangerous driving, and she eventually pled guilty in a UK court in 2022 to causing death by careless driving, receiving a conditional discharge.

### Penalties and Enforcement

| Situation | US Response |
|-----------|-------------|
| Criminal offense by foreign diplomat in US | Request waiver; declare PNG; prosecution if waiver granted |
| Traffic violations by diplomats | Diplomatic notes; parking tickets often unpaid (NYC reports millions in unpaid fines) |
| Civil claims against foreign missions (employment) | FSIA commercial activity exception may apply |
| Espionage by diplomatic personnel | PNG declaration; mass expulsions (e.g., 35 Russian diplomats expelled December 2016) |

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany ratified the VCDR on November 11, 1964, and implements diplomatic immunity through the Gerichtsverfassungsgesetz (GVG, Courts Constitution Act) Sections 18-20. Section 18 GVG provides that members of diplomatic missions accredited to Germany are exempt from German jurisdiction in accordance with international law and treaties. Section 19 extends this to other persons exempt from German jurisdiction under international agreements. Section 20 addresses the immunity of international organization officials.

**Berlin Diplomatic Quarter:** As Germany's capital and the seat of a large diplomatic community, Berlin faces unique challenges related to diplomatic immunity. The Auswärtiges Amt (Federal Foreign Office) manages diplomatic privileges and immunities through its Protocol Division (Protokoll).

### Notable Cases

**Tiergarten Murder Case (2019):** On August 23, 2019, Zelimkhan Khangoshvili, a Georgian citizen of Chechen origin, was assassinated in Berlin's Kleiner Tiergarten park by Vadim Krasikov, a Russian national traveling on a false identity. While not directly a diplomatic immunity case, the German government's expulsion of two Russian diplomats in response to the assassination and the subsequent trial of Krasikov (convicted of murder in December 2021) highlighted the intersection of diplomatic relations and criminal accountability. The Kammergericht (Berlin Court of Appeals) found that the murder was ordered by Russian state authorities.

**Libyan Embassy Siege Parallel:** Germany has dealt with incidents involving diplomatic premises, applying the VCDR principle of inviolability of mission premises under Article 22.

### Employment Disputes and ECHR

German labor courts (Arbeitsgerichte) have grappled with the question of diplomatic immunity in employment disputes brought by local staff of foreign embassies. Following the ECtHR's decisions in Cudak v. Lithuania and Sabeh El Leil v. France, German courts have increasingly recognized that blanket immunity in employment matters may be inconsistent with Article 6 ECHR. The Bundesarbeitsgericht (Federal Labor Court) has applied a restrictive interpretation of immunity in employment cases involving non-diplomatic functions.

### Penalties and Mechanisms

| Situation | German Response |
|-----------|----------------|
| Criminal offense by diplomat | Diplomatic note requesting waiver; PNG declaration |
| Traffic violations | Diplomatic notes; enforcement limited by immunity |
| Employment disputes (local staff) | Courts may exercise jurisdiction following ECHR case law |
| Espionage | PNG declaration; potential criminal investigation if immunity waived |

## ECHR & EU

### Vienna Convention Framework

The VCDR (1961) and VCCR (1963) form the primary international legal framework for diplomatic immunity. Key VCDR provisions include: Article 22 (inviolability of mission premises), Article 29 (inviolability of the diplomatic agent's person), Article 31 (immunity from criminal, civil, and administrative jurisdiction), Article 32 (waiver of immunity by the sending state), and Article 41 (obligation of diplomats to respect the host state's laws).

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECtHR has developed significant jurisprudence on the relationship between diplomatic immunity and access to justice under Article 6 ECHR.

**Cudak v. Lithuania (Application No. 15869/02, Grand Chamber Judgment of March 23, 2010):** The Grand Chamber found a violation of Article 6(1) where Lithuanian courts had declined jurisdiction over an employment dispute brought by a locally recruited employee of the Polish Embassy, citing state immunity. The Court held that employment contracts with foreign missions are generally covered by the territorial tort/employment exception under the UN Convention on Jurisdictional Immunities of States and that denying access to court was disproportionate.

**Sabeh El Leil v. France (Application No. 34869/05, Grand Chamber Judgment of June 29, 2011):** The Grand Chamber found a violation of Article 6(1) where French courts denied jurisdiction to hear an employment claim by the former head accountant of the Kuwaiti Embassy in Paris. The Court reiterated that employment disputes involving locally recruited embassy staff performing non-diplomatic functions should generally not be barred by immunity.

**Radunović and Others v. Montenegro (Applications Nos. 45197/13, ## 53000/13, and 73404/13, Judgment of October 25, 2016):** The Court confirmed the Cudak and Sabeh El Leil approach, finding a violation where Montenegrin courts dismissed employment claims of local embassy staff on immunity grounds.

### EU Privileges and Immunities

The Protocol on the Privileges and Immunities of the European Union (Protocol No. 7 to the TFEU) grants EU officials immunity from jurisdiction in respect of acts performed in their official capacity. This regime is distinct from diplomatic immunity but raises analogous issues regarding accountability and access to justice.

### International Law Commission

The International Law Commission's Draft Articles on Jurisdictional Immunities of States and Their Property (2004), which formed the basis for the UN Convention on Jurisdictional Immunities of States and Their Property (adopted 2004, not yet in force), codified exceptions to state immunity including the employment exception (Article 11) and the personal injuries exception (Article 12).

## Comparative Analysis

### Scope of Protection

All jurisdictions apply the VCDR framework consistently for diplomatic agents (full immunity from criminal, civil, and administrative jurisdiction). The divergences appear in three areas: the treatment of consular officers (limited to official acts), employment disputes with locally hired staff, and the status of military forces under SOFAs.

### Accountability Mechanisms

When diplomatic personnel commit crimes, the available responses include: waiver of immunity by the sending state (rare in practice), declaration of persona non grata (the most common response), criminal prosecution in the sending state (often difficult to verify), and insurance-based compensation for civil claims. South Korea's SOFA framework provides a structured mechanism for allocating criminal jurisdiction but has been criticized for insufficient accountability.

### Employment Rights

The ECHR's Cudak and Sabeh El Leil line of cases has significantly shifted the balance in Europe toward permitting employment claims against diplomatic missions, a development that has influenced German and other European courts. The United States applies the FSIA framework, which includes a commercial activity exception that may reach some employment relationships. South Korea's approach to embassy employment disputes is less developed in the case law.

### Political Sensitivity

Diplomatic immunity controversies are inherently politically charged, as they implicate bilateral relations between sovereign states. South Korea's SOFA disputes are particularly sensitive due to the security alliance context. The Anne Sacoolas case demonstrated how immunity disputes can cause significant diplomatic damage even between close allies.
