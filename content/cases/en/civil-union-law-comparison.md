## Summary

Civil unions and domestic partnerships -- legal relationship statuses that provide some or all of the rights and obligations of marriage without the designation of marriage itself -- have played a pivotal role in the evolution of relationship recognition law, serving in many jurisdictions as either a stepping stone toward full marriage equality for same-sex couples or as a permanent alternative to marriage for both same-sex and different-sex couples who choose not to marry. South Korea does not legally recognize civil unions, domestic partnerships, or same-sex marriage, though the Supreme Court's landmark 2023 decision (대법원 2022두36728, July 21, 2023) recognized a same-sex partner's right to spousal health insurance benefits under the National Health Insurance Act, marking the first judicial recognition of same-sex couple rights in Korean law; the Constitutional Court is currently reviewing the constitutionality of the marriage provisions (Civil Act Article 812) that implicitly limit marriage to different-sex couples, and several municipal ordinances in Seoul and other cities have recognized "life partnerships" for anti-discrimination purposes. The United States went through a significant civil union phase: Vermont created the first civil union in 2000 (Baker v. State, 744 A.2d 864 (Vt. 1999)), followed by Connecticut, New Hampshire, and other states, before the Supreme Court's decisions in United States v. Windsor, 570 U.S. 744 (2013) (striking down DOMA Section 3) and Obergefell v. Hodges, 576 U.S. 644 (2015) (requiring all states to license and recognize same-sex marriages) rendered civil unions largely obsolete as a marriage substitute, though several states retain them as an option; the Respect for Marriage Act (2022) provides federal statutory protection for same-sex and interracial marriages. Germany established the Eingetragene Lebenspartnerschaft (registered life partnership) in 2001 under the Lebenspartnerschaftsgesetz (LPartG), which was progressively expanded through a series of Federal Constitutional Court decisions to provide rights nearly identical to marriage, before the Bundestag enacted the Gesetz zur Einführung des Rechts auf Eheschließung für Personen gleichen Geschlechts (Ehe für alle -- Marriage for All) on October 1, 2017, opening marriage to same-sex couples and allowing existing life partnerships to be converted to marriages. The European framework includes ECHR jurisprudence requiring states to provide some form of legal recognition for same-sex couples under Article 8 (Oliari v. Italy, 2015) while not requiring marriage equality under Article 12, EU Directive 2004/38/EC on free movement of EU citizens and their family members (including registered partners), and the significant variation among Council of Europe member states, with 35 providing some form of legal recognition for same-sex relationships as of 2026.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea does not have legislation recognizing civil unions, domestic partnerships, or same-sex marriage. The Civil Act (민법) Article 812 requires marriage registration (혼인신고) and has been interpreted as implicitly limiting marriage to different-sex couples, though the statute does not explicitly contain gendered language.

### Supreme Court 2023 Decision

**Supreme Court Decision 2022두36728 (July 21, 2023):** In a landmark ruling, the Supreme Court held that a same-sex partner was entitled to dependent coverage under the National Health Insurance Act (국민건강보험법). The case involved So Seong-uk and Kim Yong-min, a same-sex couple who had held a public wedding ceremony in 2019. When So registered Kim as a dependent for health insurance purposes, the National Health Insurance Service (NHIS) initially accepted the registration but later revoked it, arguing that same-sex partners did not qualify as dependents. The Supreme Court reversed the revocation, holding that the NHIS's interpretation was too narrow and that the statute's reference to "spouses" (배우자) should be interpreted to include same-sex partners for health insurance purposes.

This decision did not legalize same-sex marriage or establish a civil union framework but marked the first judicial recognition of same-sex couple rights in South Korean law.

### Constitutional Court Review

As of early 2026, the Constitutional Court (헌법재판소) is reviewing the constitutionality of the marriage provisions, including whether the limitation of marriage to different-sex couples violates the constitutional rights to equality (Article 11), dignity and pursuit of happiness (Article 10), and freedom of marriage (Article 36(1)). Multiple constitutional complaints have been consolidated for review.

### Municipal Initiatives

Several municipalities have adopted ordinances recognizing "life partnerships" or providing anti-discrimination protections:

| Municipality | Year | Scope |
|-------------|------|-------|
| Seoul (서울특별시) | 2012 | Student rights ordinance (includes sexual orientation) |
| Various districts | 2014-2020 | Anti-discrimination ordinances |
| Seoul (proposed) | 2023 | Life partnership registration (not enacted) |

### Public Opinion

Public attitudes toward same-sex relationships have shifted significantly. According to a 2024 Korea Research survey, approximately 52 percent of respondents supported legal recognition of same-sex partnerships (including marriage or civil unions), up from approximately 30 percent in 2014. Support is strongest among younger demographics (ages 18-29: approximately 75 percent).

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States' experience with civil unions illustrates a rapid legal evolution from complete non-recognition to full marriage equality within two decades.

### Civil Union Timeline

| 2005 | Connecticut | Civil union enacted (subsequently converted to marriage in 2008) |
| 2007 | New Hampshire | Civil union enacted (converted to marriage in 2010) |
| 2007 | Oregon | Domestic partnership enacted |

| 2009 | Nevada | Domestic partnership enacted |
| 2011 | Illinois | Civil union enacted (marriage 2014) |
| 2011 | Hawaii | Civil union enacted (marriage 2013) |
| 2011 | Delaware | Civil union enacted (converted to marriage 2013) |

### From Civil Union to Marriage Equality

**United States v. Windsor, 570 U.S. 744 (2013):** The Supreme Court struck down Section 3 of the Defense of Marriage Act (DOMA), which had defined marriage as between one man and one woman for federal purposes. Justice Kennedy's majority opinion held that DOMA violated the Due Process and Equal Protection principles of the Fifth Amendment by treating legally married same-sex couples differently from different-sex couples for over 1,000 federal statutes and programs.

**Obergefell v. Hodges, 576 U.S. 644 (2015):** The Supreme Court held that the Fourteenth Amendment requires states to license and recognize same-sex marriages. Justice Kennedy's majority opinion grounded the right in both the Due Process Clause (fundamental right to marry) and the Equal Protection Clause (discrimination based on sexual orientation).

### Respect for Marriage Act (2022)

Following concerns about the stability of Obergefell after Dobbs v. Jackson, Congress enacted the Respect for Marriage Act (RMA), signed into law on December 13, 2022. The RMA:

- Requires the federal government to recognize any marriage valid in the state where performed
- Requires states to give full faith and credit to marriages from other states
- Repeals the remaining provisions of DOMA
- Provides religious liberty protections for religious organizations

### Remaining Civil Unions and Domestic Partnerships

Several states continue to offer civil unions or domestic partnerships, primarily as an option for couples (including different-sex couples) who prefer an alternative to marriage. Colorado, Hawaii, Illinois, and New Jersey retain civil union options. California's domestic partnership remains available for all couples.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's path to marriage equality passed through the Eingetragene Lebenspartnerschaft (registered life partnership), a parallel institution created specifically for same-sex couples.

### Lebenspartnerschaftsgesetz (LPartG, 2001)

The Life Partnership Act, enacted on February 16, 2001, and effective August 1, 2001, created a registered partnership framework for same-sex couples. Initial rights were significantly limited compared to marriage:

| Right | LPartG (2001) | After BVerfG Decisions | Marriage (2017) |
|-------|---------------|----------------------|-----------------|
| Maintenance obligations | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Inheritance | Limited | Equalized (2004 amendment) | Full |
| Tax benefits (Ehegattensplitting) | No | Yes (BVerfG 2013) | Yes |
| Joint adoption | No | Stepchild adoption (BVerfG 2013); successive adoption (BVerfG 2013) | Full joint adoption |
| Pension benefits | Limited | Equalized | Full |

### Constitutional Court Equalization

The Bundesverfassungsgericht systematically eliminated differences between life partnerships and marriage through a series of decisions:

**BVerfG 1 BvF 1/01 (2002):** The Court upheld the LPartG's constitutionality, holding that creating a registered partnership for same-sex couples did not violate Article 6(1) GG (protection of marriage and family), because the life partnership did not diminish marriage.

**BVerfG 1 BvR 1164/07 (2009):** The Court struck down the exclusion of registered partners from survivors' pension benefits in the public service pension scheme, finding a violation of Article 3(1) (equality).

**BVerfG 1 BvL 1/11 (2013):** The Court declared the exclusion of registered partners from stepchild adoption (Stiefkindadoption) unconstitutional, requiring equal treatment.

**BVerfG 2 BvR 909/06 (2013):** The Court held that the exclusion of registered partners from the income tax splitting benefit (Ehegattensplitting) violated the equality principle, requiring retroactive equalization.

### Ehe für alle (Marriage for All, 2017)

On June 30, 2017, the Bundestag voted 393-226 to open marriage to same-sex couples. Chancellor Angela Merkel voted against but allowed a free vote. The law entered into force on October 1, 2017. Key provisions:

- Same-sex couples may marry with all rights and obligations of marriage
- Existing life partnerships may be converted to marriages upon application
- No new life partnerships may be registered after October 1, 2017
- Existing unconverted life partnerships continue with their existing rights

As of 2024, approximately 65,000 same-sex couples had married in Germany, and approximately 34,000 existing life partnerships had been converted to marriages.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Schalk and Kopf v. Austria (Application No. 30141/04, June 24, 2010):** The Grand Chamber held that Article 12 ECHR does not require states to open marriage to same-sex couples. However, the Court recognized for the first time that same-sex couples living in a stable relationship fall within the notion of "family life" under Article 8.

**Oliari and Others v. Italy (Application No. 18766/11, July 21, 2015):** The Court found a violation of Article 8, holding that Italy's failure to provide any form of legal recognition for same-sex couples (neither marriage nor civil union) violated the right to respect for private and family life. The Court held that Italy had failed to strike a fair balance between the competing interests, noting that a majority of Council of Europe member states already provided some form of recognition.

**Fedotova and Others v. Russia (Application No. 40792/10, Grand Chamber, January 17, 2023):** The Grand Chamber found a violation of Article 8, holding that Russia's blanket refusal to provide any form of legal recognition for same-sex couples, including civil unions or registered partnerships, violated the right to respect for private and family life.

### EU Free Movement

**Coman v. Romania (Case C-673/16, CJEU, June 5, 2018):** The CJEU held that a member state must recognize a same-sex marriage concluded in another member state for the purpose of granting a derived right of residence under Directive 2004/38/EC, even if the member state does not allow same-sex marriage domestically. The term "spouse" in the Directive is gender-neutral and includes same-sex spouses.

**Directive 2004/38/EC (Free Movement Directive):** Article 2(2)(b) includes the registered partner as a family member if the legislation of the host member state treats registered partnerships as equivalent to marriage.

### Variation Across Council of Europe Member States

### EU Charter of Fundamental Rights

Article 9: "The right to marry and the right to found a family shall be guaranteed in accordance with the national laws governing the exercise of these rights." The Explanations to the Charter note that Article 9 was deliberately drafted without reference to "men and women," leaving the question of same-sex marriage to national law. This formulation is broader than Article 12 ECHR but does not itself mandate marriage equality.

## Comparative Analysis

### Evolutionary Pathways

The jurisdictions illustrate three distinct pathways toward relationship recognition. Germany and the United States followed a sequential model: civil unions first, then marriage equality. South Korea is in the earliest stages, with judicial recognition of specific rights but no legislative framework. The ECHR has established a minimum floor (some form of recognition required) without mandating marriage equality.

### Civil Union as Transitional or Permanent Institution

A key question is whether civil unions are inherently transitional (a stepping stone to marriage equality) or a permanent feature of relationship law. In Germany and most U.S. states, civil unions proved transitional, giving way to full marriage equality. However, in some jurisdictions, civil unions persist as an option for couples who prefer an alternative to marriage -- France's PACS remains extremely popular, with over 200,000 PACS concluded annually, and is available to both same-sex and different-sex couples.

### Judicial vs. Legislative Leadership

The role of courts vs. legislatures varies significantly. The U.S. pathway was primarily judicially driven (Baker, Windsor, Obergefell). Germany's pathway was a combination of legislative action (LPartG 2001, Ehe für alle 2017) and constitutional court equalization. South Korea's trajectory appears to be court-led, with the 2023 Supreme Court decision and pending Constitutional Court review. The ECHR has played a catalyzing role, with Oliari and Fedotova establishing positive obligations that compel legislative action.

### Cultural and Religious Dimensions

Cultural and religious factors significantly influence the pace of reform. South Korea's strong Confucian heritage and the political influence of Christian evangelical churches have slowed recognition. In the United States, religious liberty concerns prompted the religious exemptions in the Respect for Marriage Act. Germany's reform was facilitated by broad social acceptance despite the CDU's initial opposition. The ECHR's margin of appreciation doctrine acknowledges these cultural differences while setting a minimum floor of recognition.
