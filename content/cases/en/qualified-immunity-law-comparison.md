## Summary

Qualified immunity and analogous doctrines of official immunity shield government officers -- particularly law enforcement -- from personal civil liability for conduct performed in the course of their duties, unless they violate clearly established rights. The doctrine varies dramatically across jurisdictions. South Korea provides state liability for unlawful official acts under Article 2 of the State Compensation Act (국가배상법), while individual officers generally enjoy immunity unless they acted with intentional misconduct or gross negligence, in which case the state may exercise a right of recourse against them. The United States has developed the most expansive immunity doctrine through judicially created qualified immunity under 42 U.S.C. § 1983, shielding officers from personal liability unless their conduct violated "clearly established" statutory or constitutional rights of which a reasonable person would have known, as articulated in Harlow v. Fitzgerald, 457 U.S. 800 (1982). Germany operates under a state liability model rooted in Article 34 of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz) and § 839 of the Civil Code (BGB), channeling all claims against the state rather than the individual officer (Amtshaftung), while preserving a right of recourse against officers who acted with intent or gross negligence. The European Court of Human Rights (ECHR) addresses immunity through the lens of Article 6 (right of access to court) and Article 13 (right to an effective remedy), holding that immunities must be proportionate and must not entirely deprive individuals of access to justice, as established in Osman v. United Kingdom (1998) and Al-Adsani v. United Kingdom (2001).

## South Korea

### Constitutional Foundation

Article 29(1) of the Constitution of the Republic of Korea establishes the right to state compensation: "In case a person has sustained damages by an unlawful act committed by a public official in the course of official duties, he/she may claim just compensation from the State or public organization under the conditions as prescribed by Act." Article 29(2) provides that military personnel, police officers, and certain other public servants may have their right to claim state compensation restricted by special legislation, though this provision has been narrowly interpreted by the Constitutional Court.

### State Compensation Act (국가배상법)

The State Compensation Act, first enacted in 1951 and substantially revised in 1967, establishes the framework for liability arising from official acts:

**Article 2 (Liability for Illegal Acts):** The State or a local government shall be liable to compensate for damages when a public official intentionally or negligently causes damage to another person by an illegal act in the course of performing official duties. The claimant need not identify the specific officer responsible -- establishing that the harmful act was performed by a government officer in the course of duty suffices.

**Article 2(2) (Right of Recourse):** When a public official has caused damage through intentional misconduct or gross negligence, the State or local government may exercise a right of recourse (구상권) against that officer after compensating the victim. This recourse right effectively creates an indirect accountability mechanism for individual officers.

**Article 5 (Liability for Defective Public Facilities):** The State or local government is liable for damages caused by defects in the installation or management of roads, rivers, and other public facilities -- a form of strict liability that does not require proof of officer fault.

### Police Accountability

South Korean police accountability operates through multiple channels:

**National Human Rights Commission of Korea (NHRCK):** Established by the National Human Rights Commission Act (2001), the NHRCK investigates complaints of human rights violations by state agencies, including police misconduct. While its recommendations are not legally binding, they carry significant persuasive authority and are published publicly.

**Police Disciplinary System:** The Police Officers Act (경찰공무원법) establishes internal disciplinary proceedings for officers who violate laws or regulations. Disciplinary measures range from reprimand to dismissal. The 2022 Police Reform Act restructured the disciplinary system to include external civilian participation in disciplinary committees.

**Administrative Litigation:** Under the Administrative Litigation Act (행정소송법), individuals may challenge unlawful police actions through administrative courts, seeking annulment of administrative dispositions, confirmation of illegality, or injunctive relief.

### Judicial Interpretation

The Supreme Court of Korea has interpreted the State Compensation Act to impose a relatively claimant-friendly standard. In Supreme Court Decision 2001Da53950 (2003), the Court held that "illegality" for purposes of state compensation encompasses not only violations of specific statutory provisions but also conduct that violates objective standards of care expected of public officials. The Court has also held that emotional distress (위자료) is compensable under the Act.

In Supreme Court Decision 2012Da201041 (2014), the Court addressed police liability for failure to protect, holding that the State may be liable when police officers with knowledge of a specific and imminent threat to an identifiable individual fail to take reasonable protective measures.

### Landmark Cases

**Sewol Ferry Disaster Litigation (2014-2020):** Following the Sewol ferry disaster that killed 304 people (mostly high school students), courts held the State liable under the State Compensation Act for the negligent conduct of Coast Guard officials who failed to order timely evacuation. The Seoul Central District Court awarded substantial damages to victims' families, finding that the government's rescue response constituted gross negligence.

**Yongsan Disaster State Compensation (2014):** The Seoul Central District Court awarded state compensation to families of protesters killed during a police operation at a building in Yongsan, Seoul in 2009, finding that the police use of force was disproportionate and constituted an illegal act under Article 2 of the State Compensation Act.

## United States

### Constitutional and Statutory Framework

The United States has developed the most elaborate -- and most controversial -- system of official immunity in the comparative landscape. The framework rests on the interaction between constitutional rights, statutory causes of action, and judicially created immunity doctrines.

**42 U.S.C. § 1983 (Civil Rights Act of 1871):** The primary vehicle for civil rights litigation against state and local officials: "Every person who, under color of any statute, ordinance, regulation, custom, or usage, of any State or Territory or the District of Columbia, subjects, or causes to be subjected, any citizen of the United States or other person within the jurisdiction thereof to the deprivation of any rights, privileges, or immunities secured by the Constitution and laws, shall be liable to the party injured in an action at law."

**Bivens v. Six Unknown Named Agents, 403 U.S. 388 (1971):** The Supreme Court recognized an implied cause of action for damages against federal officers who violate constitutional rights. However, the Court has dramatically curtailed Bivens actions in recent decades, declining to extend the remedy to new contexts in Ziglar v. Abbasi, 582 U.S. 120 (2017) and Egbert v. Boule, 596 U.S. 482 (2022).

### Qualified Immunity Doctrine

Qualified immunity is a judicially created doctrine with no statutory basis, developed through a series of Supreme Court decisions:

**Pierson v. Ray, 386 U.S. 547 (1967):** The Court first recognized a good-faith defense for police officers under § 1983, rooted in common law immunity for officers acting in good faith and with probable cause.

**Harlow v. Fitzgerald, 457 U.S. 800 (1982):** The Court transformed the doctrine from a subjective good-faith standard to a purely objective "clearly established law" test: government officials are shielded from liability unless their conduct violates "clearly established statutory or constitutional rights of which a reasonable person would have known." The subjective component was eliminated to allow early resolution of insubstantial claims.

**Anderson v. Creighton, 487 U.S. 635 (1987):** The Court held that the "clearly established" inquiry must be undertaken at a high level of specificity -- the right allegedly violated must be defined with sufficient particularity that a reasonable official would understand that what they were doing violated that right.

**Ashcroft v. al-Kidd, 563 U.S. 731 (2011):** The Court reiterated that existing precedent must place the statutory or constitutional question "beyond debate" for a right to be clearly established.

**Taylor v. Riojas, 592 U.S. 7 (2020):** In a rare per curiam reversal, the Court held that no reasonable officer could have concluded it was constitutionally permissible to house a prisoner in cells covered with massive amounts of feces for six days, illustrating the "obvious clarity" exception to the specificity requirement.

### Absolute Immunity

Certain officials enjoy absolute immunity that cannot be overcome regardless of the egregiousness of the conduct:

- **Judges:** Absolute immunity for judicial acts, even if performed maliciously (Stump v. Sparkman, 435 U.S. 349 (1978); Mireles v. Waco, 502 U.S. 9 (1991)).
- **Prosecutors:** Absolute immunity for conduct "intimately associated with the judicial phase of the criminal process" (Imbler v. Pachtman, 424 U.S. 409 (1976)), including decisions to prosecute and courtroom advocacy, but not investigative or administrative functions (Buckley v. Fitzsimmons, 509 U.S. 259 (1993)).
- **Legislators:** Absolute immunity for legislative acts under the Speech or Debate Clause (Gravel v. United States, 408 U.S. 606 (1972)).
- **The President:** Absolute immunity from civil damages for official acts (Nixon v. Fitzgerald, 457 U.S. 731 (1982); Trump v. United States, 603 U.S. 593 (2024)).

### Municipal Liability

Municipalities do not enjoy qualified immunity (Owen v. City of Independence, 445 U.S. 622 (1980)) but can only be held liable under § 1983 when the violation results from an official policy or custom (Monell v. Department of Social Services, 436 U.S. 658 (1978)). This creates a significant practical barrier, as plaintiffs must prove that their injury resulted from a municipal "policy or custom" rather than the ad hoc act of a single officer.

### Federal Pattern-or-Practice Authority

Under 34 U.S.C. § 12601 (formerly 42 U.S.C. § 14141), the Department of Justice may bring civil actions against state and local law enforcement agencies that engage in a "pattern or practice" of conduct that deprives persons of constitutional rights. These actions typically result in consent decrees imposing comprehensive reforms. Notable investigations have targeted police departments in Ferguson, Missouri; Baltimore, Maryland; Chicago, Illinois; Minneapolis, Minnesota; and Louisville, Kentucky.

### Reform Movements

Qualified immunity has become one of the most debated legal doctrines in the United States:

- **Federal proposals:** The Ending Qualified Immunity Act has been introduced in multiple sessions of Congress but has not passed.
- **State-level reforms:** Colorado (SB 20-217, 2020), New Mexico (HB 4, 2021), and New York City (Intro. 2220, 2021) have enacted laws eliminating or restricting qualified immunity for state law claims against officers.
- **Academic criticism:** Justice Sonia Sotomayor has repeatedly dissented from qualified immunity decisions, and scholars including Joanna Schwartz and William Baude have argued that the doctrine lacks historical foundation and impedes accountability.

## Germany

### Constitutional Framework

German official liability law is rooted in Article 34 of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz, GG): "If any person, in the exercise of a public office entrusted to him, violates his official duty to a third party, liability shall rest in principle on the State or on the public body which employs him. In the event of intentional or grossly negligent conduct, the right of recourse against the individual officer shall be preserved."

This provision establishes the fundamental principle of state liability channeling (Haftungskanalisierung): the state assumes primary liability for the tortious acts of its officers, and the injured party sues the state rather than the individual. This approach reflects the ordoliberal conviction that the state bears institutional responsibility for the lawful exercise of public authority.

### Civil Code Framework (Amtshaftung)

**BGB § 839 (Liability for Breach of Official Duty):** "If an official intentionally or negligently breaches the official duty incumbent upon him in relation to a third party, he shall compensate the third party for any damage arising therefrom." Through the operation of Article 34 GG, this liability is channeled to the state.

Key elements of an Amtshaftung claim:

1. **Official (Beamter im haftungsrechtlichen Sinne):** Broadly interpreted to include all persons exercising public authority, not limited to formally appointed civil servants (Beamte). Police officers, judges, prosecutors, teachers, and even private parties exercising delegated public functions are covered.

2. **Breach of official duty (Amtspflichtverletzung):** The officer must have violated a duty owed specifically to the claimant (Drittbezogenheit). The duty must relate to the protection of the individual's interests, not merely to the general public interest.

3. **Third-party protection (Drittschutz):** The official duty violated must have been intended to protect the specific claimant or a class of persons to which the claimant belongs. This requirement serves a gatekeeping function analogous to the "clearly established law" standard in U.S. qualified immunity, though it operates differently.

4. **Fault (Verschulden):** The officer must have acted intentionally or negligently. An objective standard of care applies -- the question is whether a diligent officer in the same position would have acted differently.

5. **Subsidiarity (Subsidiarität):** Under BGB § 839(1) sentence 2, if the officer acted only negligently, liability is excluded if the injured party can obtain compensation by other means (e.g., from a third-party tortfeasor). This "subsidiarity clause" significantly limits state liability in negligence cases.

### Police Liability

German police liability follows the Amtshaftung framework. Claims against police officers are brought against the relevant Land (state) or the Federal Republic (for federal police), not against the individual officer:

**Bundesgerichtshof (BGH), III ZR 60/16 (2017):** The Federal Court of Justice held that disproportionate police use of force during a lawful assembly constitutes a breach of official duty giving rise to state liability under Article 34 GG and BGB § 839, regardless of whether the officers acted in good faith.

**BVerfG, 1 BvR 1783/17 (2019):** The Federal Constitutional Court held that victims of unlawful police violence have a constitutional right to an effective investigation under Article 2(2) GG (right to physical integrity) read in conjunction with Article 1(1) GG (human dignity), establishing a duty to investigate police misconduct analogous to the ECHR's procedural obligations under Articles 2 and 3.

### Judicial Immunity (Spruchrichterprivileg)

BGB § 839(2) provides a special immunity for judges: "If an official breaches his official duty in giving a judgment in a legal matter, he is only responsible for any damage arising therefrom if the breach of duty consists in a criminal offense." This "judge's privilege" (Spruchrichterprivileg) effectively grants judges absolute immunity from civil liability for judicial decisions, unless the decision constitutes a criminal act. The State may still be liable under Article 34 GG for egregious denials of justice, but the standard is extremely high.

### Right of Recourse (Rückgriffsanspruch)

Article 34 sentence 2 GG preserves the state's right of recourse against officers who acted with intent or gross negligence. In practice, recourse is rarely exercised, as public employers tend to absorb liability rather than pursue claims against their own officers. However, the theoretical availability of recourse serves as an accountability mechanism and deterrent.

### Comparative Advantage

Germany's state liability model has been praised for its efficiency and claimant-friendliness compared to the U.S. system. Because claims are directed against the state rather than individual officers, claimants avoid the qualified immunity defense entirely. The state's deeper pockets and institutional interest in settling meritorious claims facilitate compensation. Critics note, however, that the channeling approach may reduce individual officer accountability and that the subsidiarity clause creates gaps in protection.

## ECHR/EU Framework

### Right of Access to Court (Article 6)

The European Court of Human Rights has developed a substantial body of case law addressing the compatibility of official immunities with the right of access to court under Article 6(1) of the Convention:

**Osman v. United Kingdom, App. No. 23452/94 (1998):** In this landmark Grand Chamber judgment, the Court considered whether the blanket immunity from negligence claims enjoyed by British police (the "Hill immunity" from Hill v. Chief Constable of West Yorkshire [1989] AC 53) violated Article 6. The Court held that the automatic application of immunity without any consideration of the competing interests at stake constituted a disproportionate restriction on the right of access to court. This decision fundamentally reshaped the relationship between official immunity and Convention rights.

**Z and Others v. United Kingdom, App. No. 29392/95 (2001):** The Grand Chamber refined its approach, holding that the issue was more properly analyzed under the substantive right (Article 3 -- prohibition of inhuman treatment) rather than the procedural right of access to court, and found a violation of Article 13 (right to effective remedy) where domestic law provided no mechanism to hold social services accountable for failing to protect children from severe abuse.

**Al-Adsani v. United Kingdom, App. No. 35763/97 (2001):** The Grand Chamber held that the grant of sovereign immunity to Kuwait in a torture claim brought in UK courts did not violate Article 6(1), as state immunity pursued the legitimate aim of compliance with international law and was proportionate. However, the narrow 9-8 majority and vigorous dissents signaled the Court's evolving approach to immunity in cases involving fundamental rights.

### State Obligation to Investigate (Articles 2 and 3)

The ECHR has established robust procedural obligations requiring states to conduct effective investigations into deaths and serious injuries at the hands of state agents:

**McCann and Others v. United Kingdom, App. No. 18984/91 (1995):** The Grand Chamber held that Article 2 requires not only that states refrain from intentional and unlawful killing, but also that they conduct an effective official investigation when individuals have been killed by use of force.

**Nachova and Others v. Bulgaria, App. No. 43577/98 (2005):** The Grand Chamber established that when state agents use lethal force, the burden shifts to the government to provide a satisfactory and convincing explanation, and that a failure to investigate adequately may itself constitute a violation of Article 2.

**Ramsahai and Others v. The Netherlands, App. No. 52391/99 (2007):** The Grand Chamber specified the requirements for an effective investigation into police killings: it must be independent, adequate, prompt, subject to public scrutiny, and involve the next of kin to the extent necessary to safeguard their legitimate interests.

### Police Accountability Under the Convention

**Makaratzis v. Greece, App. No. 50385/99 (2004):** The Grand Chamber held that a police car chase resulting in the shooting of a fleeing motorist violated Article 2, emphasizing that the use of potentially lethal force must be regulated by a framework of adequate and effective safeguards against arbitrariness and abuse.

**Giuliani and Gaggio v. Italy, App. No. 23458/02 (2011):** The Grand Chamber established that domestic laws and regulations governing police operations must afford adequate protection against arbitrary deprivation of life, including clear guidelines on the use of force, effective training, and post-incident accountability mechanisms.

**Cestaro v. Italy, App. No. 6884/11 (2015):** The Court found that the brutal police assault on the Diaz School during the 2001 Genoa G8 summit constituted torture under Article 3, and that Italy's failure to have adequate legal mechanisms to punish those responsible -- including the absence of a specific crime of torture in Italian law at the time -- constituted a systemic violation.

### EU Framework on Official Liability

Within the European Union legal order, the Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU) has established the principle of state liability for breaches of EU law in Francovich v. Italy, Cases C-6/90 and C-9/90 (1991), and refined the conditions in Brasserie du Pêcheur/Factortame III, Cases C-46/93 and C-48/93 (1996). A Member State is liable for damages when: (1) the rule of EU law infringed confers rights on individuals; (2) the breach is sufficiently serious; and (3) there is a direct causal link between the breach and the damage.

The EU Charter of Fundamental Rights, Article 41 (right to good administration) and Article 47 (right to an effective remedy and to a fair trial), provide additional accountability frameworks. Article 340 TFEU establishes the EU's own non-contractual liability for damage caused by its institutions.

### Proportionality Standard

The ECHR's approach to official immunity is characterized by a proportionality analysis rather than categorical rules. The Court examines:

1. Whether the immunity pursues a legitimate aim (e.g., maintaining the independence of the judiciary, ensuring the effective functioning of government).
2. Whether the restriction on the right of access to court is proportionate to the aim pursued.
3. Whether alternative mechanisms exist for obtaining redress.
4. Whether the immunity is narrowly tailored to the function it serves.

This proportionality framework contrasts sharply with the categorical immunities in U.S. law (absolute immunity for judges, prosecutors, legislators) and provides a more flexible mechanism for balancing accountability with the need to protect official functions.

## Comparative Analysis

### Structural Divergence: Individual vs. State Liability

The most fundamental divergence in this area concerns whether liability is channeled to the state or imposed on the individual officer:

| Jurisdiction | Primary Liability Target | Individual Officer Exposure |
|-------------|------------------------|---------------------------|
| **South Korea** | State (국가/지방자치단체) | Recourse for intent/gross negligence only |
| **United States** | Individual officer (under § 1983) | Full personal liability unless qualified immunity applies |
| **Germany** | State (via Amtshaftung channeling) | No personal liability to claimant; recourse for intent/gross negligence |
| **ECHR/EU** | State (Convention responsibility) | Indirect -- state must provide effective domestic remedies |

The United States stands alone in primarily targeting individual officers for civil rights violations. Germany and South Korea channel liability to the state, shielding individual officers from direct suit while preserving recourse mechanisms. The ECHR supervises the adequacy of domestic accountability frameworks without itself imposing individual liability.

### The "Clearly Established Law" Problem

The U.S. qualified immunity doctrine has been criticized for creating perverse incentives. Because courts may resolve qualified immunity on either prong of the analysis -- finding that the right was not clearly established without deciding whether it was violated -- constitutional law may fail to develop, creating a feedback loop in which rights remain perpetually "not clearly established" because courts decline to establish them. The Supreme Court's decision in Pearson v. Callahan, 555 U.S. 223 (2009), which made the two-step sequencing of Saucier v. Katz optional, exacerbated this problem.

Neither South Korea, Germany, nor the ECHR employs a comparable specificity requirement. South Korea applies a standard negligence analysis; Germany requires breach of an official duty owed to a third party; the ECHR applies proportionality review.

### Police Accountability Mechanisms

All four jurisdictions have developed mechanisms for police accountability, though they differ significantly in effectiveness:

- **South Korea:** The 2022 Police Reform Act restructured police governance, separating the police from direct executive control and establishing enhanced civilian oversight. The NHRCK provides an independent complaint mechanism.
- **United States:** A fragmented system of internal affairs investigations, civilian review boards, DOJ pattern-or-practice suits, and private § 1983 litigation. The qualified immunity doctrine significantly constrains the effectiveness of private litigation.
- **Germany:** The Amtshaftung framework ensures compensation for victims of police misconduct, while administrative courts and disciplinary proceedings provide additional accountability. Some Länder have established independent police complaint bodies.
- **ECHR:** The Court's procedural obligations under Articles 2 and 3 require effective, independent investigations into police use of force, providing a supranational accountability backstop.

### Judicial and Prosecutorial Immunity

All jurisdictions grant enhanced protection to judges and prosecutors, recognizing the need to protect adjudicative independence:

- **United States:** Absolute immunity for judges and prosecutors acting in their judicial/advocacy capacities -- the most protective standard.
- **Germany:** Spruchrichterprivileg limits judicial liability to criminal misconduct -- nearly as protective as the U.S. standard.
- **South Korea:** State liability applies to wrongful judicial acts, but the standard for establishing liability is high.
- **ECHR:** Judicial immunity must be proportionate under Article 6, but the Court has generally accepted broad judicial immunities as necessary for the independence of the judiciary.
