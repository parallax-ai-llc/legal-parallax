## Summary

Habeas corpus -- the ancient writ requiring that a person under detention be brought before a court to determine the lawfulness of their confinement -- stands as one of the most fundamental safeguards against arbitrary state power in the history of legal civilization, originating in English common law as early as the Magna Carta of 1215 and codified through the Habeas Corpus Act of 1679. South Korea enshrines the right to petition for release from unlawful detention in the Habeas Corpus Act (인신보호법), enacted on June 22, 2007 and effective June 22, 2008, which extended habeas corpus protection beyond criminal detention to encompass involuntary commitment in mental health facilities, immigration detention, and other forms of state or private confinement, administered through the district courts with appeal to the High Courts and the Supreme Court. The United States provides the most extensive habeas corpus framework of any jurisdiction, rooted in Article I, Section 9 of the Constitution (the Suspension Clause), implemented through 28 U.S.C. Sections 2241-2255, and profoundly shaped by the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA), which imposed significant restrictions on the filing of habeas petitions by state prisoners in federal courts -- restrictions upheld by the Supreme Court in Felker v. Turpin (1996) but subject to ongoing constitutional scrutiny, particularly regarding the rights of detainees held at Guantanamo Bay as established in Boumediene v. Bush (2008). Germany does not use the term "habeas corpus" but provides equivalent protection through Article 104 of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz), which requires judicial authorization for any deprivation of liberty exceeding the end of the day following arrest, supplemented by the right of constitutional complaint (Verfassungsbeschwerde) to the Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) under Article 93(1)(4a) Grundgesetz. At the European level, Article 5(4) of the European Convention on Human Rights guarantees the right to challenge the lawfulness of detention before a court, and the European Court of Human Rights has developed extensive jurisprudence on the scope, speed, and procedural requirements of such review, notably in cases such as De Wilde, Ooms and Versyp v. Belgium (1971) and A. and Others v. United Kingdom (2009).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's Habeas Corpus Act (인신보호법, Act No. 8724) was enacted on June 22, 2007, and entered into force on June 22, 2008. The Act represented a landmark development in Korean constitutional law, creating for the first time a comprehensive statutory mechanism for challenging the lawfulness of any form of detention or confinement, whether imposed by the state or by private entities. Prior to the Act's enactment, individuals challenging unlawful detention could rely only on the general provisions of the Criminal Procedure Act for criminal detention, or on civil injunctive remedies for non-criminal confinement, a fragmented approach that left significant protection gaps.

### Scope of Protection

Article 2 of the Habeas Corpus Act defines its scope broadly to encompass any person "confined in a facility by an administrative agency, a juristic person, or an individual against his or her will, or confined in a facility under circumstances in which his or her free will cannot be deemed to have been considered." This definition extends protection to persons in mental health facilities (정신건강증진 및 정신질환자 복지서비스 지원에 관한 법률 -- the Mental Health Welfare Act), immigration detention centers (외국인보호소), care facilities for persons with disabilities, and other institutional settings where individuals may be deprived of liberty without adequate legal process.

### Procedure

A habeas corpus petition (인신보호 청구) may be filed by the detainee, a relative, a legal representative, or any person with a legitimate interest. The petition is filed with the district court (지방법원) having jurisdiction over the place of detention. Upon receipt, the court must promptly issue an order requiring the detaining authority to present the detainee before the court and to submit a written explanation of the grounds for detention. The court conducts a hearing within 72 hours and issues a decision. If the court finds the detention unlawful, it orders immediate release.

### Constitutional Foundation

The Habeas Corpus Act implements the constitutional guarantees of Article 12 of the Constitution (personal liberty), which provides that "no person shall be arrested, detained, seized, searched, or interrogated except as provided by Act" and that "no person shall be punished, placed under preventive restrictions, or subjected to involuntary labor except as provided by Act and through lawful procedures." The Constitutional Court in Decision 2008HeonMa222 (2012) confirmed that the right to challenge the lawfulness of detention is a core constitutional right under Article 12.

### Landmark Cases

**Supreme Court Decision 2009Choga4 (2009):** The Supreme Court's first major habeas corpus case under the new Act involved the involuntary confinement of a person with a mental disability in a residential care facility. The Court granted the petition, finding that the confinement lacked the consent required under applicable legislation and that the facility had failed to demonstrate adequate legal grounds for continued confinement.

**Supreme Court Decision 2014InBo2 (2015):** This case addressed the detention of a foreign national in an immigration detention center beyond the permissible period. The Supreme Court held that the detention had become unlawful upon expiration of the authorized period and ordered immediate release, establishing that administrative convenience does not justify prolonged immigration detention.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States possesses the most elaborate habeas corpus framework of any jurisdiction, reflecting both the historical centrality of the writ in Anglo-American legal tradition and the unique challenges posed by American federalism. The Suspension Clause of Article I, Section 9 of the Constitution provides that "The Privilege of the Writ of Habeas Corpus shall not be suspended, unless when in Cases of Rebellion or Invasion the public Safety may require it."

### Statutory Framework

**28 U.S.C. Section 2241:** The general federal habeas corpus statute, authorizing federal courts to grant writs of habeas corpus for prisoners held "in custody in violation of the Constitution or laws or treaties of the United States."

**28 U.S.C. Section 2254:** Governs habeas petitions by state prisoners in federal court. As amended by AEDPA, this section requires deference to state court adjudications and permits relief only if the state court decision "resulted in a decision that was contrary to, or involved an unreasonable application of, clearly established Federal law" or "resulted in a decision that was based on an unreasonable determination of the facts."

**28 U.S.C. Section 2255:** Provides the remedy for federal prisoners seeking to vacate, set aside, or correct sentences imposed in violation of the Constitution or federal law.

### AEDPA Restrictions

The Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA) imposed the most significant restrictions on federal habeas review in modern American legal history:

| AEDPA Restriction | Effect |
|-------------------|--------|
| One-year statute of limitations | Petitions must be filed within one year of final state court judgment |
| Deference to state courts | Federal courts may not grant relief unless state court decision was "contrary to" or "unreasonable application of" clearly established federal law |
| Successive petition bar | Second or successive petitions require certification by the court of appeals |
| Exhaustion requirement | State remedies must be fully exhausted before federal habeas |
| Factual development limitations | Federal courts generally may not hold evidentiary hearings if the petitioner failed to develop facts in state court |

### Landmark Cases

**Boumediene v. Bush, 553 U.S. 723 (2008):** The Supreme Court held that the Suspension Clause applies to non-citizen detainees held at Guantanamo Bay, Cuba, and that the Detainee Treatment Act of 2005 and the Military Commissions Act of 2006 did not provide an adequate substitute for habeas corpus review. Justice Kennedy's majority opinion established that the habeas privilege extends to territories under de facto U.S. sovereignty.

**Brown v. Allen, 344 U.S. 443 (1953):** Established the principle that federal habeas courts may reexamine constitutional claims decided by state courts, providing the foundation for federal habeas review of state criminal convictions.

**Harrington v. Richter, 562 U.S. 86 (2011):** The Court clarified the AEDPA standard of review, holding that a state court decision is "unreasonable" only if "there is no possibility fairminded jurists could disagree" with the state court's conclusion.

### Historical Suspensions

The writ has been suspended on limited occasions: by President Abraham Lincoln during the Civil War (later authorized by Congress in 1863), and effectively by the Military Commissions Act of 2006 for certain non-citizen enemy combatants (struck down in Boumediene).

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany provides habeas corpus protection through constitutional provisions rather than a dedicated habeas corpus statute. Article 104 of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz) establishes the fundamental requirements for lawful deprivation of liberty:

**Article 104(1):** Personal liberty may be restricted only pursuant to a formal law and only in compliance with the procedural forms prescribed therein.

**Article 104(2):** Only a judge may decide on the permissibility and continuation of any deprivation of liberty. Where such deprivation is not based on a judicial order, a judicial decision must be obtained without delay. The police may not hold anyone on their own authority in their custody beyond the end of the day following the arrest.

**Article 104(3):** Any person provisionally detained on suspicion of having committed a criminal offense must be brought before a judge no later than the day following the arrest; the judge must inform the detainee of the reasons for the arrest, interrogate the detainee, and give the detainee an opportunity to raise objections.

### Verfassungsbeschwerde (Constitutional Complaint)

The most powerful mechanism for challenging unlawful detention in Germany is the constitutional complaint (Verfassungsbeschwerde) to the Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht), available under Article 93(1)(4a) of the Basic Law and Sections 90-95 of the Federal Constitutional Court Act (Bundesverfassungsgerichtsgesetz). Any person who claims that their fundamental rights, including the right to personal liberty under Article 2(2) and Article 104, have been violated by public authority may file a constitutional complaint after exhausting ordinary legal remedies.

### Ordinary Court Remedies

Before reaching the Federal Constitutional Court, individuals challenging detention may seek relief through:

- **Haftprüfung (detention review):** Under Section 117 StPO, a detained person may apply at any time for judicial review of the detention order.
- **Beschwerde (complaint):** An appeal against the detention order to the next higher court (Landgericht).
- **Weitere Beschwerde (further complaint):** In some cases, a further appeal to the Oberlandesgericht.

### Federal Constitutional Court Jurisprudence

The Federal Constitutional Court has developed extensive case law on the right to liberty under Article 2(2) and Article 104, consistently emphasizing that:

- Deprivation of liberty is among the most serious intrusions upon a fundamental right and requires strict proportionality review (BVerfGE 22, 180 (1967)).
- Extended pretrial detention must be justified by particularly weighty reasons, and the state bears the burden of demonstrating that less restrictive measures are insufficient (BVerfGE 53, 152 (1980)).
- Courts must act with particular expedition in proceedings involving deprivation of liberty; unjustified delays violate Article 104(2) (BVerfG, 2 BvR 1675/16 (2018)).

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Article 5(4)

Article 5(4) of the European Convention on Human Rights provides: "Everyone who is deprived of his liberty by arrest or detention shall be entitled to take proceedings by which the lawfulness of his detention shall be decided speedily by a court and his release ordered if the detention is not lawful." This provision is the functional equivalent of habeas corpus in the Council of Europe system.

### Key ECHR Principles

The European Court of Human Rights has developed extensive jurisprudence on Article 5(4):

**De Wilde, Ooms and Versyp v. Belgium, Application Nos. 2832/66, 2835/66, 2899/66, Judgment of June 18, 1971:** The Court established that Article 5(4) requires access to a "court" -- a body with judicial character that provides procedural guarantees appropriate to the kind of deprivation of liberty at issue. The reviewing body must have the competence to decide the lawfulness of the detention and, if appropriate, to order release.

**Weeks v. United Kingdom, Application No. 9787/82, Judgment of March 2, 1987:** The Court held that where the basis for detention may change over time (as with indeterminate sentences), Article 5(4) requires periodic review of the lawfulness of detention at reasonable intervals.

**A. and Others v. United Kingdom [GC], Application No. 3455/05, Judgment of February 19, 2009:** In a case challenging the indefinite detention without charge of foreign terrorism suspects under the Anti-terrorism, Crime and Security Act 2001, the Grand Chamber found violations of Article 5(1) (the detention was disproportionate and discriminatory) and Article 5(4) (the detainees were denied sufficient information about the case against them to effectively challenge their detention). The judgment had profound implications for the balance between national security and individual liberty across Council of Europe states.

**Idalov v. Russia [GC], Application No. 5826/03, Judgment of May 22, 2012:** The Grand Chamber held that excessive length of pretrial detention violates Article 5(3) and that the requirement of speediness under Article 5(4) means that a habeas-type application must be decided within weeks rather than months.

### EU Charter of Fundamental Rights

Article 6 of the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights guarantees the right to liberty and security, while Article 47 guarantees the right to an effective remedy and a fair trial. These provisions apply when EU member states implement EU law and are interpreted in light of the corresponding ECHR provisions.

### Council of Europe Recommendations

Recommendation CM/Rec(2006)13 of the Committee of Ministers on the use of remand in custody, the conditions in which it takes place, and the provision of safeguards against abuse, calls on member states to ensure effective habeas corpus remedies, prompt judicial review, and access to legal representation for all detained persons.

## Comparative Analysis

### Scope of Protection

The jurisdictions diverge significantly in the scope of habeas corpus protection. South Korea's 2007 Act is notable for explicitly extending protection to non-criminal detention, including mental health commitment and private institutional confinement. The United States provides the broadest coverage in terms of military and extraterritorial detention (following Boumediene). Germany's constitutional framework covers all forms of state-imposed deprivation of liberty. The ECHR applies to all forms of detention within the jurisdiction of a Contracting State.

### Deference to Prior Decisions

The most significant divergence concerns the standard of review. The United States, through AEDPA, imposes a uniquely deferential standard on federal courts reviewing state court habeas decisions, requiring a showing that the state court's decision was not merely incorrect but "unreasonable." Germany applies full constitutional review, South Korea applies de novo review of lawfulness, and the ECHR conducts a subsidiary but independent assessment of Convention compliance.

### Speed of Review

Germany imposes the strictest time requirements, with Article 104(2) requiring judicial review by the end of the day following arrest. The ECHR requires "speedy" review, interpreted as weeks rather than months. South Korea requires a hearing within 72 hours of petition filing. The U.S. system is notably slower, with federal habeas proceedings frequently taking years from filing to resolution.

### Suspension and Derogation

The United States is unique in providing an explicit constitutional mechanism for suspending habeas corpus. Germany does not permit suspension of Article 104 protections under any circumstances. The ECHR permits derogation from Article 5 in time of war or public emergency under Article 15, but the Court has interpreted this narrowly and requires strict proportionality.
