## Summary

Animal legal personhood -- the proposition that non-human animals should be recognized as legal persons possessing enforceable rights rather than being classified solely as property or objects of human obligation -- represents one of the most transformative ongoing debates in comparative legal theory, challenging centuries of jurisprudential tradition rooted in the Roman law distinction between personae (legal subjects) and res (legal objects). South Korea's Animal Protection Act (동물보호법), originally enacted in 1991 and substantially amended in 2007, 2011, 2017, and 2023, has progressively strengthened animal welfare protections including criminalizing animal cruelty with penalties up to 3 years imprisonment or KRW 30 million fine (Article 46), mandating companion animal registration (Article 12), and establishing the Animal Welfare Committee (동물복지위원회); the 2023 amendment introduced the concept of animal sentience and imposed a duty of care on animal guardians (보호자), though the Act does not recognize animals as legal persons and the Civil Act (민법) continues to classify animals as "things" (물건) under Article 98. The United States classifies animals as personal property under state and federal law, with animal welfare regulated through the Animal Welfare Act (7 U.S.C. Section 2131 et seq.) covering research and exhibition animals but exempting farm animals and pets, while the animal rights movement has pursued legal personhood through habeas corpus petitions, most notably in the Nonhuman Rights Project's (NhRP) cases involving chimpanzees Happy, Tommy, and Kiko in New York courts; the New York Court of Appeals denied habeas corpus relief for Happy the elephant in In re Nonhuman Rights Project, Inc. v. Breheny, 38 N.Y.3d 555 (2022), by a 5-2 vote. Germany became the first European country to constitutionally protect animals through the 2002 amendment to Article 20a of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz), which establishes state protection of the natural foundations of life "and the animals" (und die Tiere), while the BGB was amended in 1990 to state that animals are not things (Section 90a BGB: "Tiere sind keine Sachen"), though they remain subject to the provisions concerning things unless otherwise provided. At the European level, the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union (TFEU) Article 13 recognizes animals as "sentient beings" and requires the EU and member states to "pay full regard to the welfare requirements of animals," while the European Court of Human Rights has addressed animal welfare as a legitimate aim justifying restrictions on Convention rights and the CJEU has interpreted EU animal welfare legislation including Regulation (EC) No 1099/2009 on the protection of animals at the time of killing.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's animal law has evolved significantly from minimal protections to an increasingly comprehensive welfare framework, though it has not embraced the legal personhood paradigm.

**Animal Protection Act (동물보호법):** Originally enacted in 1991 as a basic anti-cruelty statute with minimal enforcement provisions, the Act has been substantially strengthened through successive amendments:

- **2007 Amendment:** Expanded the definition of animal cruelty; established the Animal Protection Management System.
- **2011 Amendment:** Criminalized abandonment of companion animals; introduced companion animal registration requirements.
- **2017 Amendment:** Increased maximum penalties for animal cruelty; prohibited killing animals in cruel manners including hanging and drowning.
- **2023 Amendment (effective 2024):** Introduced the concept of animal sentience (동물의 감각능력); imposed a legal duty of care (보호의무) on animal guardians (보호자, replacing the prior term "owner"); expanded the definition of cruelty to include neglect; established the Korea Animal Welfare Institute (한국동물복지연구원).

**Article 3 (Basic Principles):** "Animals are sentient beings capable of feeling pain and suffering. All citizens and the State have a duty to protect the life, safety, and welfare of animals."

**Article 8 (Prohibition of Animal Cruelty):** Prohibits killing, injuring, or causing unnecessary suffering to animals. Specific prohibited acts include: beating animals using tools; using animals for baiting or fighting; abandoning animals in a manner that causes suffering; and killing animals by methods causing excessive pain.

**Article 46 (Penalties):** Animal cruelty is punishable by imprisonment of up to 3 years or a fine of up to KRW 30 million. Animal killing by cruel methods carries imprisonment of up to 2 years or a fine of up to KRW 20 million. Abandonment carries a fine of up to KRW 3 million.

**Civil Act (민법) Article 98:** Defines "things" (물건) as "tangible objects and natural forces manageable for human use." Animals remain classified as things under this provision. There is no equivalent to Germany's BGB Section 90a declaring animals "not things." Legal scholars and animal welfare advocates have called for an amendment to Article 98, and several bills to this effect have been introduced in the National Assembly.

### Dog Meat Industry

The legal status of dogs raised for meat consumption has been a contentious issue. The Dog Meat Prohibition Act (개식용 종식에 관한 특별법), enacted on January 9, 2024, bans the slaughter and distribution of dogs for human consumption effective 2027, with a three-year transition period. The Act imposes penalties of up to 3 years imprisonment or KRW 30 million fine for violations after the transition period.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States classifies animals as personal property under common law, with welfare protections provided through a patchwork of federal and state statutes.

**Animal Welfare Act (AWA, 7 U.S.C. Section 2131 et seq.):** Enacted in 1966 and administered by the USDA, the AWA establishes minimum standards of care for animals used in research, exhibition, transport, and by dealers. The Act specifically excludes farm animals used for food, fiber, or other agricultural purposes, as well as birds, rats, and mice used in research (under a regulatory exclusion). Violations carry civil penalties of up to $10,000 per violation and potential criminal penalties of up to $2,500 and/or one year imprisonment for willful violations.

**Endangered Species Act (ESA, 16 U.S.C. Section 1531 et seq.):** Provides protections for listed species, including prohibitions on "take" (harass, harm, pursue, hunt, shoot, wound, kill, trap, capture, or collect). In Babbitt v. Sweet Home Chapter of Communities for a Great Oregon, 515 U.S. 687 (1995), the Supreme Court upheld the broad interpretation of "harm" to include significant habitat modification.

**State Anti-Cruelty Laws:** All 50 states have enacted animal cruelty statutes, with 46 states classifying certain forms of cruelty as felonies. The PAWS (Preventing Animal Cruelty and Torture) Act of 2019 (18 U.S.C. Section 48) established a federal felony for animal crushing offenses, filling a jurisdictional gap for interstate conduct.

### Legal Personhood Litigation

**In re Nonhuman Rights Project, Inc. v. Breheny (Happy the Elephant), 38 N.Y.3d 555 (2022):** The New York Court of Appeals, in a 5-2 decision, denied habeas corpus relief for Happy, an elephant held at the Bronx Zoo. The majority held that "habeas corpus is a procedural vehicle intended to secure the liberty rights of human beings" and that extending it to animals "would have an enormous destabilizing impact on modern society." The dissent (Judge Rowan Wilson, joined by Judge Jenny Rivera) argued that habeas corpus should not be categorically denied to non-human beings capable of autonomous decision-making and that the writ historically had been used expansively to address unjust confinement.

**People ex rel. NhRP v. Lavery (Tommy the Chimpanzee), 31 N.Y.3d 1054 (2018):** The Court of Appeals denied leave to appeal, affirming the lower court's denial of habeas corpus for a captive chimpanzee. Justice Eugene Fahey issued a notable concurrence stating that the denial should not be understood as a finding that Tommy lacks entitlement to habeas corpus, calling the issue "profound and far-reaching."

**Naruto v. Slater, 888 F.3d 418 (9th Cir. 2018):** The Ninth Circuit held that a crested macaque monkey that took selfies using a photographer's camera could not hold a copyright under the Copyright Act, as the statute does not authorize non-human animal authorship. The ruling, while narrow, reinforced the general principle that animals lack standing under federal statutory frameworks.

### Standing Doctrine

The Supreme Court addressed animal-related standing in Sierra Club v. Morton, 405 U.S. 727 (1972), where Justice Douglas's dissent proposed that natural objects (and by extension animals) should have standing to sue in their own name. In Cetacean Community v. Bush, 386 F.3d 1169 (9th Cir. 2004), the Ninth Circuit held that the world's cetaceans lacked statutory standing under the ESA, MMPA, and NEPA.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany has achieved some of the most significant legal advancements in animal protection among the four jurisdictions, including constitutional protection and a statutory declaration that animals are not things.

**Article 20a Grundgesetz (Basic Law):** Amended in 2002 to add "and the animals" (und die Tiere) to the existing state objective of protecting the natural foundations of life: "Mindful also of its responsibility towards future generations, the state shall protect the natural foundations of life and the animals by legislation and, in accordance with law and justice, by executive and judicial action." Germany became the first EU country to incorporate animal protection into its constitution. The provision creates an objective state duty (Staatszielbestimmung) rather than a subjective right that animals could enforce.

**BGB Section 90a:** Inserted by the Animal Protection Improvement Act of 1990: "Animals are not things (Tiere sind keine Sachen). They are protected by special legislation. The provisions applicable to things apply to them correspondingly, unless otherwise provided." This provision has symbolic significance but limited practical effect, as animals remain subject to property law rules in the absence of specific overriding legislation.

**Tierschutzgesetz (TierSchG) -- Animal Welfare Act (1972, as amended):** Section 1 establishes the purpose of the Act: "to protect the life and well-being of the animal from the responsibility of human beings for the animal as a fellow creature (Mitgeschöpf). No one may cause an animal pain, suffering, or harm without reasonable cause." Key penalty provisions:

- **Section 17:** Killing a vertebrate without reasonable cause or causing vertebrate animals considerable suffering is punishable by imprisonment of up to 3 years or a fine.
- **Section 18:** Administrative offenses (Ordnungswidrigkeiten) include violations of housing, transport, and care standards, punishable by fines up to EUR 25,000.

### Constitutional Balancing

The BVerfG has addressed the relationship between animal protection under Article 20a GG and other fundamental rights:

**BVerfG, Decision of January 15, 2002 (1 BvR 1783/99) -- Ritual Slaughter (Schächten):** Before the 2002 amendment, the Court held that a Muslim butcher's freedom of religion (Article 4(1) GG) and occupational freedom (Article 12(1) GG) were infringed by a blanket ban on slaughter without stunning, as the ban lacked a countervailing constitutional animal protection norm. This decision catalyzed the 2002 constitutional amendment.

**BVerfG, Order of February 12, 2019 (2 BvR 1322/12, 2 BvR 1337/12):** Post-amendment, the Court considered whether the constitutional animal protection norm in Article 20a GG could justify restrictions on the freedom of religion. The Court recognized that Article 20a GG now provides a constitutional basis for balancing animal welfare against other fundamental rights.

### Academic Debate

German legal scholarship has been at the forefront of the animal legal personhood debate. Scholars including Peter Singer (who lectured extensively in Germany), Gary Francione, and German academics such as Anne Peters (Max Planck Institute) have argued for recognition of partial legal subjectivity (Teilrechtsfähigkeit) for sentient animals. Peters' concept of "zoopolis" envisions a legal framework in which animals hold basic rights proportionate to their cognitive and emotional capacities.

## ECHR & EU

### TFEU Article 13

Article 13 of the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union, introduced by the Treaty of Lisbon (2007, effective 2009), provides: "In formulating and implementing the Union's agriculture, fisheries, transport, internal market, research and technological development and space policies, the Union and the Member States shall, since animals are sentient beings, pay full regard to the welfare requirements of animals, while respecting the legislative or administrative provisions and customs of the Member States relating in particular to religious rites, cultural traditions and regional heritage."

This provision recognizes animal sentience as a matter of EU primary law but operates as a cross-cutting requirement rather than granting rights to animals. It has been invoked by the CJEU in interpreting animal welfare regulations.

### EU Animal Welfare Legislation

**Regulation (EC) No 1099/2009 on the Protection of Animals at the Time of Killing:** Requires that animals be stunned before slaughter, with exceptions for religious rites (Article 4(4)). In Liga van Moskeeën en Islamitische Organisaties Provincie Antwerpen (Case C-426/16, CJEU Judgment of May 29, 2018), the Court held that member states may require that ritual slaughter take place only in approved slaughterhouses, balancing animal welfare under TFEU Article 13 against religious freedom.

**Directive 2010/63/EU on the Protection of Animals Used for Scientific Purposes:** Establishes minimum standards for the use of animals in research, including requirements for ethical review, the 3Rs principle (replacement, reduction, refinement), and prohibitions on the use of great apes in research (Article 8).

**Proposed EU Animal Welfare Regulation (2023):** The European Commission proposed a comprehensive revision of EU animal welfare legislation in 2023, including updated transport rules, an EU-wide ban on caged farming by 2027, and enhanced enforcement mechanisms. The proposal followed the successful European Citizens' Initiative "End the Cage Age" (2018-2020), which collected over 1.4 million signatures.

### European Court of Human Rights

**Herrmann v. Germany (Application No. 9300/07, Grand Chamber Judgment of June 26, 2012):** While primarily addressing hunting rights and property rights, the Grand Chamber acknowledged animal welfare as a legitimate aim under the Convention that can justify restrictions on the peaceful enjoyment of property (Article 1 Protocol 1).

**Friend and Others v. United Kingdom (Application No. 16072/06, Decision of November 24, 2009):** The Court declared inadmissible a challenge to the Hunting Act 2004 (which banned hunting with dogs in England and Wales), holding that the ban served the legitimate aim of animal protection and did not violate Articles 8, 11, or Article 1 Protocol 1.

### International Developments

**Universal Declaration on Animal Welfare (proposed):** A proposed intergovernmental agreement recognizing that animals are sentient beings, that their welfare is an issue of international importance, and that states should take measures to prevent animal cruelty. Despite support from over 50 governments, the Declaration has not been adopted by the UN General Assembly.

**Argentina -- Cecilia the Chimpanzee (2016):** While outside the four core jurisdictions, the Mendoza court's recognition of chimpanzee Cecilia as a "non-human legal person" entitled to habeas corpus relief represents the most significant judicial recognition of animal legal personhood globally.

## Comparative Analysis

### Legal Classification Spectrum

The jurisdictions occupy different positions on a spectrum from treating animals purely as property (United States, South Korea's Civil Act) to recognizing intermediate status (Germany's "not things" declaration, EU's "sentient beings" recognition). None has fully recognized animal legal personhood with enforceable rights.

### Constitutional vs. Statutory Protection

Germany's constitutional animal protection (Article 20a GG) is unique among the four jurisdictions and provides a basis for balancing animal welfare against other constitutional rights. The EU's TFEU Article 13 operates at the treaty level but as a cross-cutting principle rather than a fundamental right. Neither South Korea nor the United States provides constitutional-level animal protection.

### Enforcement Gaps

All jurisdictions exhibit significant enforcement gaps between statutory protections and actual outcomes. The United States' AWA excludes the vast majority of animals (farm animals, pets) from its scope. South Korea's Animal Protection Act has been strengthened but enforcement remains inconsistent, particularly regarding the dog meat industry (partially addressed by the 2024 ban). Germany's relatively strong enforcement is supported by state veterinary authorities and civil society organizations.

### Future Trajectory

The trajectory across all jurisdictions points toward increasing recognition of animal sentience and welfare requirements without full legal personhood. The EU's "sentient beings" language, Germany's "not things" declaration, and South Korea's 2023 sentience recognition represent a gradual shift in legal status that may eventually support more robust protections, though the gap between sentience recognition and legal personhood remains substantial.
