## Summary

Director liability -- the legal responsibility imposed on corporate directors for breaches of their duties to the company, its shareholders, and in some jurisdictions its stakeholders -- defines the boundaries of managerial authority and accountability and is central to the functioning of corporate governance. South Korea imposes liability on directors through the Commercial Code (상법) Articles 382-2 (duty of care/선관주의의무), 382-3 (duty of loyalty/충실의무), and 399 (joint and several liability for unlawful acts), with the Supreme Court developing an expanding body of jurisprudence on director accountability for chaebol-related misconduct, and the 2020 amendment introducing liability for de facto directors and extending derivative suit standing to parent company shareholders. The United States, primarily through Delaware law, applies the fiduciary duties of care and loyalty as refined through landmark Court of Chancery and Supreme Court decisions, with the business judgment rule providing a powerful presumption of director good faith (Aronson v. Lewis, 1984), the Caremark duty of oversight requiring good-faith monitoring systems (In re Caremark International Inc. Derivative Litigation, 1996), and the exculpation provisions of DGCL Section 102(b)(7) protecting directors from monetary liability for duty-of-care breaches. Germany imposes strict personal liability on Vorstand members under AktG Section 93 (Sorgfaltspflicht eines ordentlichen und gewissenhaften Geschäftsleiters) with a reversed burden of proof requiring directors to demonstrate they acted with the care of a diligent and conscientious manager, and on GmbH managing directors (Geschäftsführer) under GmbHG Section 43, with the business judgment rule (unternehmerische Ermessensentscheidung) codified in AktG Section 93(1) sentence 2 since the UMAG reform of 2005. At the EU level, the CSDDD (Directive 2024/1760) introduces director duties related to sustainability due diligence, the Insolvency Directive addresses directors' duties in the vicinity of insolvency, and the ECHR has recognized that directors' liability regimes must comply with fair trial guarantees under Article 6.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Korean director liability law reflects the country's civil law tradition supplemented by governance reforms targeting chaebol misconduct.

### Duties

**Duty of Care (선관주의의무):** Article 382(2) requires directors to perform their duties with the care of a good manager (선량한 관리자의 주의). The Supreme Court has interpreted this as an objective standard requiring directors to make informed decisions based on reasonable investigation and deliberation.

**Duty of Loyalty (충실의무):** Article 382-3 requires directors to perform their duties faithfully in the best interest of the company. The Supreme Court in Supreme Court Decision 2003Da49221 (2005) held that the duty of loyalty encompasses the prohibition of self-dealing, the prohibition of usurping corporate opportunities, and the obligation to avoid conflicts of interest.

### Business Judgment Rule

Although not codified, the Supreme Court has recognized a business judgment rule. In Supreme Court Decision 2002Da60467 (2004), the Court held that directors are not liable for business decisions that turn out unfavorably if, at the time of the decision, the director: (1) acted on the basis of adequate information, (2) acted in good faith, (3) had no personal interest in the transaction, and (4) reasonably believed the decision was in the company's best interest.

### Liability to Company (Article 399)

Article 399 provides that directors who violate laws or regulations, the articles of incorporation, or resolutions of the shareholders' meeting are jointly and severally liable to the company for resulting damages. This provision is the basis for derivative suits under Article 403.

### Liability to Third Parties (Article 401)

Article 401 establishes personal liability of directors toward third parties (including creditors and other external parties) for damages caused by intentional misconduct or gross negligence in the performance of their duties. The Supreme Court has interpreted this provision to cover situations where directors cause the company's insolvency through reckless management or misappropriation.

### De Facto Director Liability

Article 401-2, strengthened in the 2020 amendment, imposes director-equivalent liability on de facto directors (사실상의 이사) -- persons who exercise significant influence over the company's management without holding a formal director position. This provision targets chaebol controlling shareholders who exercise management control through informal channels.

## United States

### Legal Framework

American director liability law, centered on Delaware, is the most developed body of fiduciary duty jurisprudence in the world.

### Fiduciary Duties

**Duty of Care:** Directors must make informed business decisions, acting with the care that an ordinarily careful and prudent person would use in similar circumstances. The standard is process-oriented: did the board inform itself of all material information reasonably available before making a decision?

**Duty of Loyalty:** The "most fundamental" fiduciary obligation (Guth v. Loft, Inc., 5 A.2d 503 (Del. 1939)) requires that directors act in good faith in the best interest of the corporation, rather than in their personal interest. Loyalty violations include self-dealing transactions, usurpation of corporate opportunities, and acting in bad faith.

**Duty of Good Faith:** Following In re Walt Disney Co. Derivative Litigation, 906 A.2d 27 (Del. 2006), good faith is understood as a component of the duty of loyalty rather than an independent duty. Bad faith includes intentional dereliction of duty, a conscious disregard for one's responsibilities, and acting for purposes other than advancing the corporation's best interests.

### Business Judgment Rule and Exculpation

The business judgment rule creates a powerful presumption protecting directors from liability for honest mistakes of judgment. DGCL Section 102(b)(7), enacted in response to Smith v. Van Gorkom, 488 A.2d 858 (Del. 1985), permits corporations to include charter provisions eliminating director personal liability for monetary damages for breaches of the duty of care (but not loyalty or good faith).

### Caremark Duty of Oversight

**In re Caremark International Inc. Derivative Litigation, 698 A.2d 959 (Del. Ch. 1996):** Chancellor Allen held that directors have a duty to ensure that adequate information and reporting systems exist to keep them reasonably informed of the corporation's compliance with law.

**Marchand v. Barnhill, 212 A.3d 805 (Del. 2019):** The Delaware Supreme Court reinvigorated Caremark, holding that directors of Blue Bell Creameries could face liability for failing to establish any board-level system for monitoring food safety compliance, resulting in a listeria outbreak.

### Officer Liability

In McDonald's Corp. Stockholder Derivative Litigation (Del. Ch. 2023), the Court of Chancery held that corporate officers do not benefit from Section 102(b)(7) exculpation and may face personal liability for duty-of-care breaches. The 2022 amendment to DGCL Section 102(b)(7) extended exculpation to officers, but only for direct claims (not derivative claims).

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German director liability is notably stricter than its American counterpart, with a reversed burden of proof that places the onus on directors to demonstrate they acted properly.

### AktG Section 93 -- Vorstand Liability

Section 93(1) sentence 1: "Vorstand members must exercise the care of a diligent and conscientious manager in conducting business" (Die Vorstandsmitglieder haben bei ihrer Geschäftsführung die Sorgfalt eines ordentlichen und gewissenhaften Geschäftsleiters anzuwenden).

Section 93(2) sentence 1: "Vorstand members who breach their duties are jointly and severally liable to the company for resulting damages."

Section 93(2) sentence 2: **Reversed Burden of Proof:** "If it is disputed whether they applied the care of a diligent and conscientious manager, they bear the burden of proof" (Ist streitig, ob sie die Sorgfalt eines ordentlichen und gewissenhaften Geschäftsleiters angewandt haben, so trifft sie die Beweislast). This reversal is one of the strictest features of German director liability.

### Business Judgment Rule (Section 93(1) Sentence 2)

Codified by the UMAG reform of 2005: "A breach of duty does not exist if the board member, in making a business decision, could reasonably assume that he was acting on the basis of adequate information for the benefit of the company." This provision, inspired by the American business judgment rule, provides a safe harbor for honest business decisions but does not reverse the burden of proof on compliance with law.

### Mandatory D&O Insurance Deductible

AktG Section 93(2) sentence 3, enacted in 2009, requires that D&O insurance for Vorstand members include a deductible of at least 10% of the damage, up to 1.5 times the member's fixed annual compensation. This provision ensures that directors retain personal financial exposure even when insured.

### GmbH Managing Director Liability

GmbHG Section 43 imposes similar duties on GmbH managing directors (Geschäftsführer), requiring the care of a prudent businessperson. The reversed burden of proof under Section 93(2) AktG does not directly apply to GmbH Geschäftsführer, but courts have applied analogous principles.

### Insolvent Trading (InsO Section 15a)

Managing directors of GmbHs and Vorstand members of AGs must file for insolvency within three weeks (reduced from six weeks in certain circumstances) of becoming aware of insolvency (Zahlungsunfähigkeit) or over-indebtedness (Überschuldung). Failure to file timely exposes directors to criminal liability (up to three years imprisonment) and personal civil liability for all payments made after the insolvency filing obligation arose (GmbHG Section 64; AktG Section 92(2)).

## ECHR & EU

### Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD)

Directive 2024/1760 introduces director duties related to sustainability:

**Article 26:** Directors of in-scope companies must integrate sustainability due diligence into their corporate strategy. The Directive does not create a separate director liability regime but requires member states to ensure that directors' existing duties of care encompass sustainability considerations.

### Restructuring Directive (2019/1023)

Article 19 requires member states to ensure that, where there is a likelihood of insolvency, directors have due regard to the interests of creditors, equity holders, and other stakeholders, take steps to avoid insolvency, and avoid deliberate or grossly negligent conduct that threatens the viability of the business.

### ECHR Fair Trial Guarantees

The ECHR has addressed director liability proceedings under Article 6:

**Kress v. France (Application No. 39594/98, Grand Chamber Judgment of June 7, 2001):** The Court established that proceedings involving substantial financial penalties against directors must comply with fair trial guarantees, including the right to an independent and impartial tribunal and the right to adversarial proceedings.

**Västberga Taxi Aktiebolag and Vulic v. Sweden (Application No. 36985/97, Judgment of July 23, 2002):** The Court found that personal liability imposed on a managing director for the company's tax debts constituted a determination of "civil rights" under Article 6, requiring full procedural protections.

### Company Law Directive (2017/1132)

The Directive does not harmonize director liability standards but addresses formation and capital maintenance requirements that indirectly affect director obligations. Article 58 addresses board reporting obligations in cross-border mergers, and Articles 86b-86s establish director responsibilities in cross-border conversions.

## Comparative Analysis

### Burden of Proof

The most significant practical difference in director liability regimes is the allocation of the burden of proof:

**Director Bears Burden (Germany):** AktG Section 93(2) sentence 2 requires the director to prove they acted with the required care. This creates a presumption of liability once the company demonstrates damage and a potential duty breach.

**Plaintiff Bears Burden (United States, South Korea):** The business judgment rule creates a presumption of proper conduct that the plaintiff must rebut. This strongly favors directors and is widely regarded as essential to encouraging qualified individuals to serve on boards.

### Scope of Protection

American exculpation provisions (DGCL Section 102(b)(7)) provide the broadest protection, eliminating monetary liability for duty-of-care breaches entirely. Germany's mandatory D&O deductible ensures personal financial exposure. South Korea does not offer statutory exculpation, though the judicially recognized business judgment rule provides meaningful protection.

### Insolvent Trading

Germany and South Korea impose the strictest obligations in the vicinity of insolvency, with German law requiring insolvency filing within three weeks and imposing criminal sanctions for failure. American law has no federal insolvent trading prohibition, though some states recognize "deepening insolvency" theories. The EU Restructuring Directive aims to harmonize duties in the zone of insolvency.
