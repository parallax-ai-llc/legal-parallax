## Summary

Sports betting regulation reflects deeply divergent cultural, moral, and economic attitudes toward gambling, ranging from strict prohibition to comprehensive legalization with consumer protection frameworks. South Korea permits limited sports betting through the state-operated Sports Toto (스포츠토토) system established under the National Sports Promotion Act (국민체육진흥법), with revenues directed to sports development; all other forms of sports gambling, including online betting with foreign operators, are illegal under the National Gambling Control Commission Act (사행산업통합감독위원회법), with violators facing up to 5 years imprisonment. The United States underwent a dramatic transformation following the Supreme Court's decision in Murphy v. National Collegiate Athletic Association (584 U.S. 453, 2018), which struck down the Professional and Amateur Sports Protection Act of 1992 (PASPA) as unconstitutional federal commandeering of state legislatures; as of 2026, 38 states and the District of Columbia have legalized sports betting in some form, generating over $11 billion in annual gross gaming revenue. Germany legalized online sports betting through the Interstate Treaty on Gambling (Glücksspielstaatsvertrag, GlüStV 2021), effective July 1, 2021, establishing a national licensing system administered by the Joint Gambling Authority of the Länder (Gemeinsame Glücksspielbehörde der Länder, GGL) in Saxony-Anhalt; operators must obtain a federal license, comply with monthly deposit limits of 1,000 euros, and observe a one-second minimum interval between bets to prevent impulse wagering. At the European level, the EU has not harmonized gambling regulation, recognizing member states' discretion under the principle of subsidiarity, though the CJEU has developed extensive case law on the compatibility of national gambling restrictions with the freedom to provide services (Article 56 TFEU) in cases including Gambelli (C-243/01, 2003), Placanica (C-338/04, 2007), and Liga Portuguesa (C-42/07, 2009); the Council of Europe's Convention on the Manipulation of Sports Competitions (Macolin Convention, 2014) provides the international framework for combating match-fixing, and the ECHR has addressed gambling restrictions primarily through Protocol 1, Article 1 (protection of property) and Article 10 (freedom of expression regarding advertising).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea maintains one of the more restrictive sports betting regimes among developed economies, permitting only state-operated gambling within tightly controlled parameters.

**National Sports Promotion Act (국민체육진흥법):** This statute authorizes the Korea Sports Promotion Foundation (국민체육진흥공단) to operate sports betting through the Sports Toto (스포츠토토) system. Sports Toto offers fixed-odds and pool betting on domestic and international football, baseball, basketball, volleyball, golf, and other sports. Betting is available through authorized retail outlets and a regulated online platform (betman.co.kr). Individual bet limits are capped at 100,000 KRW (approximately $75) per ticket, with daily purchase limits.

**Revenue Allocation:** Sports Toto generates approximately 4.5-5 trillion KRW (approximately $3.4-3.8 billion) annually in total sales. Net revenues are allocated to the National Sports Promotion Fund, which supports elite athlete training, grassroots sports programs, facility construction, and bidding for international sporting events.

**Illegal Gambling Enforcement:** The National Gambling Control Commission (사행산업통합감독위원회) coordinates enforcement against illegal gambling, including unlicensed sports betting operations. The Criminal Act (형법) Article 246 prohibits gambling and Article 247 prohibits facilitating gambling, with penalties of up to 5 years imprisonment. The Act on the Regulation of Specific Economic Crimes (특정경제범죄 가중처벌 등에 관한 법률) provides enhanced penalties for large-scale gambling operations.

### Illegal Online Betting

Despite strict prohibition, illegal online sports betting through offshore platforms is estimated to be a multi-trillion-won industry in South Korea. The Korean National Police Agency conducts regular enforcement operations targeting illegal gambling websites and their domestic agents. In 2023, police dismantled over 1,200 illegal gambling sites. The Act on Promotion of Information and Communications Network Utilization (정보통신망법) authorizes the Korea Communications Standards Commission (방송통신심의위원회) to block access to illegal gambling websites, with over 100,000 URLs blocked annually.

### Match-Fixing Cases

South Korea has experienced several major match-fixing scandals:
- **2011 K League match-fixing scandal:** Over 60 players and coaches were charged with match-fixing in connection with illegal gambling rings. Several players received prison sentences and lifetime bans.
- **2016 Korean baseball match-fixing:** KBO players were found to have accepted payments from illegal gambling operators to manipulate game outcomes.

## United States

### Legal Framework

**Murphy v. National Collegiate Athletic Association (584 U.S. 453, 2018):** The Supreme Court struck down PASPA (which had effectively prohibited sports betting nationwide except in Nevada and a few grandfathered states) as violating the anti-commandeering doctrine of the Tenth Amendment. Justice Alito, writing for the 7-2 majority, held that Congress cannot compel states to prohibit sports gambling. The decision opened the door for state-by-state legalization.

**Post-Murphy Legalization Wave:**

| Category | Number of States (as of 2026) |
|----------|------------------------------|
| Retail and online sports betting | ~30 states |
| Retail only | ~8 states |
| Legal but not yet operational | ~3 states |
| Not legal | ~9 states |

**Federal Law (Remaining):**
- **Wire Act (18 U.S.C. Section 1084):** Prohibits the use of wire communications for interstate or foreign sports betting. The DOJ's 2019 opinion narrowing the Wire Act to sports gambling only (reversing a 2011 opinion) was upheld by the First Circuit in New Hampshire Lottery Commission v. Rosen (2021).
- **Indian Gaming Regulatory Act (IGRA, 25 U.S.C. Section 2701):** Governs gaming on tribal lands, requiring tribal-state compacts for Class III gaming (which includes sports betting).

**Market Scale:** U.S. legal sports betting generated approximately $11.5 billion in gross gaming revenue in 2024, with handle (total amount wagered) exceeding $120 billion. The online/mobile channel accounts for approximately 85% of total handle in states offering online betting.

### Regulatory Models

States have adopted various regulatory approaches:
- **Competitive licensing (New Jersey, Colorado):** Multiple online and retail operators licensed competitively
- **Lottery model (New Hampshire, Oregon):** Sports betting operated through or under the state lottery
- **Tribal exclusivity (Connecticut, Arizona):** Tribal nations granted primary or exclusive sports betting rights
- **Tax rates:** Range from 6.75% of GGR (Nevada) to 51% of GGR (New York online)

### Integrity Agreements

Professional and collegiate sports organizations have negotiated integrity agreements with state regulators and operators, including requirements for operators to use official league data for in-play betting and to share suspicious betting information with sports governing bodies.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

**Glücksspielstaatsvertrag 2021 (GlüStV):** The Interstate Treaty on Gambling, effective July 1, 2021, represents Germany's first comprehensive regulatory framework for online gambling, including sports betting.

**Key Provisions:**
- **Licensing:** Operators must obtain a license from the GGL to offer sports betting in Germany. As of 2025, approximately 30 sports betting licenses have been granted.
- **Deposit limits:** Monthly deposit limit of 1,000 euros across all licensed operators, enforced through a centralized OASIS self-exclusion system
- **Bet pacing:** Minimum one-second interval between bet placements
- **In-play betting restrictions:** Only certain types of in-play bets permitted (e.g., next goal, final result); no bets on individual events like corners, throw-ins, or yellow cards
- **Advertising restrictions:** No advertising during live sports broadcasts before 9 PM; no targeted advertising to minors; health warning requirements

**Tax Structure:** Sports betting is subject to a 5.3% tax on stakes (Sportwettsteuer), significantly higher than the gross gaming revenue-based taxation used in most other European jurisdictions. Operators have argued that the stake-based tax makes it difficult to offer competitive odds, driving consumers to unlicensed offshore operators.

### GGL Enforcement

The Gemeinsame Glücksspielbehörde der Länder (GGL), based in Halle (Saale), Saxony-Anhalt, began operations in January 2023 as the national gambling regulatory authority. The GGL's enforcement powers include:
- Ordering internet service providers to block unlicensed gambling websites (Websperre)
- Ordering payment service providers to block transactions with unlicensed operators (Zahlungssperre)
- Administrative fines up to 500,000 euros for regulatory violations
- License revocation for serious or repeated violations

### Match-Fixing Criminal Law

Germany enacted specific match-fixing criminal provisions through the 2017 amendment to the Criminal Code:
- **Section 265c StGB (Sports Betting Fraud):** Criminalizes agreements to manipulate professional sports competitions in connection with sports betting, with penalties of up to 3 years imprisonment
- **Section 265d StGB (Manipulation of Professional Sports Competitions):** Criminalizes manipulation of professional sports competitions even without a betting connection, with penalties of up to 3 years imprisonment

## ECHR / EU

### EU Free Movement and Gambling

The EU has not harmonized gambling regulation, but the CJEU has developed extensive case law on member states' restrictions on cross-border gambling services:

**Gambelli (C-243/01, 2003):** Italian restrictions on cross-border sports betting constituted a restriction on the freedom to provide services (Article 56 TFEU). Such restrictions may be justified by overriding reasons of public interest (prevention of fraud, crime, gambling addiction), but must be proportionate and applied consistently.

**Placanica (C-338/04, 2007):** The CJEU held that Italy could not criminally sanction operators excluded from the Italian licensing process when the licensing system itself was discriminatory. National restrictions must be applied in a non-discriminatory and proportionate manner.

**Liga Portuguesa (C-42/07, 2009):** The CJEU upheld Portugal's online gambling monopoly, recognizing that the internet's specific characteristics (accessibility, volume, anonymity) justify more restrictive regulatory approaches than those applied to traditional gambling.

**Ince (C-336/14, 2016):** The CJEU held that Germany's previous gambling regulatory framework (transitional period between the old and new interstate treaties) was inconsistent with EU free movement law because it maintained a de facto prohibition on private sports betting while simultaneously tolerating unlicensed operators.

### Macolin Convention (2014)

The Council of Europe Convention on the Manipulation of Sports Competitions (CETS No. 215), opened for signature in Magglingen/Macolin on September 18, 2014, provides the international framework for combating match-fixing:
- Requires states parties to criminalize the manipulation of sports competitions
- Establishes national platforms for information exchange between sports organizations, gambling regulators, and law enforcement
- Creates a follow-up committee to monitor implementation
- Promotes cooperation between states, sports organizations, and gambling operators

As of 2026, the Convention has been signed by 32 states and ratified by 9, with the EU's accession complicated by competence questions between EU and member state authority over gambling regulation.

### ECHR Gambling Jurisprudence

**Derwick Associates v. Sweden (admissibility decision):** The Court has recognized that gambling licenses may constitute "possessions" under Protocol 1, Article 1, such that revocation must satisfy the proportionality test.

**Hachette Filipacchi Presse Automobile v. France (2009):** The Court addressed gambling advertising restrictions under Article 10, finding that states have a margin of appreciation to restrict gambling advertising in pursuit of health and consumer protection objectives.

## Comparative Analysis

### Monopoly vs. Competitive Licensing

South Korea maintains a strict state monopoly model, with all sports betting channeled through Sports Toto. Germany and most EU member states operate licensing models permitting multiple private operators under regulatory supervision. U.S. states have adopted varying competitive licensing frameworks. The monopoly model maximizes state revenue capture and regulatory control but may drive demand to illegal markets if the product is insufficiently attractive. Competitive licensing models may improve consumer experience but create greater regulatory complexity.

### Online Regulation

The treatment of online sports betting is the most dynamically evolving regulatory area. The United States moved from effective prohibition to widespread legalization in under five years following Murphy. Germany established its first comprehensive online framework in 2021. South Korea maintains strict online prohibition (except for Sports Toto's limited platform) despite massive illegal online gambling activity. The CJEU's Liga Portuguesa decision provides legal support for more restrictive online regulation, recognizing the internet's unique risk characteristics.

### Match-Fixing Response

Germany leads in criminal law specificity with dedicated match-fixing provisions (Sections 265c-d StGB). South Korea relies on general criminal fraud provisions. The United States uses federal wire fraud and state-specific laws. The Macolin Convention provides an international coordination framework but has been slow to achieve ratification.

### Revenue and Taxation

Tax design varies dramatically: South Korea captures revenue through the state monopoly; U.S. states impose GGR-based taxes ranging from 6.75% to 51%; Germany taxes stakes at 5.3%. These different approaches reflect competing priorities: maximizing state revenue, maintaining competitive odds to channel demand away from illegal markets, and ensuring operator viability.
