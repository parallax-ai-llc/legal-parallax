## Summary

Anti-boycott laws -- statutory provisions prohibiting or restricting participation in, compliance with, or support for foreign-sponsored boycotts of allied nations, or conversely laws restricting domestic boycott activities targeting specific commercial or political actors -- occupy a uniquely contentious intersection of trade regulation, foreign policy, free speech, and domestic political activism, raising fundamental questions about the boundaries between protected expression and regulable commercial conduct. South Korea has no specific anti-boycott legislation comparable to the U.S. framework, but addresses boycott-related conduct through the Monopoly Regulation and Fair Trade Act (독점규제 및 공정거래에 관한 법률), which prohibits coordinated refusals to deal (공동의 거래거절) under Article 26 when they constitute unfair trade practices, and through informal government responses to foreign economic pressure, most notably during the 2017 THAAD dispute (사드 갈등) when China imposed extensive unofficial economic boycotts against Korean businesses, cultural products, and tourism in retaliation for Korea's deployment of the Terminal High Altitude Area Defense system. The United States operates the most extensive anti-boycott legal framework globally through two overlapping statutes: the Export Administration Regulations (EAR, 15 C.F.R. Part 760), administered by the Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS) within the Department of Commerce, which prohibit U.S. persons from complying with or supporting the Arab League boycott of Israel, and the Ribicoff Amendment to the Tax Reform Act of 1976 (26 U.S.C. Section 999), which denies tax benefits to persons participating in international boycotts; additionally, 38 states have enacted anti-BDS (Boycott, Divestment, Sanctions) laws restricting state contractors and investment funds from boycotting Israel, sparking intense First Amendment litigation. Germany addresses boycotts primarily through competition law (Gesetz gegen Wettbewerbsbeschränkungen, GWB) prohibiting concerted refusals to deal (Section 1 GWB) and through historical criminal law provisions; Germany's complex relationship with boycotts is shaped by the historical memory of the Nazi-era boycott of Jewish businesses (April 1, 1933) and the post-war commitment to anti-discrimination. At the European level, the EU maintains its own sanctions and restrictive measures framework under Article 215 TFEU, has enacted Regulation (EC) No 2271/96 (the EU Blocking Statute) to prohibit EU persons from complying with certain extraterritorial sanctions imposed by third countries, and the ECHR has addressed boycott-related expression under Article 10 (freedom of expression), with the European Court of Human Rights ruling in Baldassi and Others v. France (Application No. 15271/16, 2020) that criminal conviction for promoting a consumer boycott of Israeli products violated Article 10.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea lacks specific anti-boycott legislation but addresses boycott-related conduct through competition law and diplomatic responses.

**Monopoly Regulation and Fair Trade Act (독점규제 및 공정거래에 관한 법률, MRFTA):** Article 26 prohibits "unfair trade practices" including concerted refusals to deal (공동의 거래거절) where businesses collectively refuse to transact with a specific party to their detriment. The Korea Fair Trade Commission (공정거래위원회, KFTC) enforces these provisions with administrative fines and corrective orders. However, the MRFTA targets anticompetitive commercial conduct rather than political boycotts.

**Consumer-Organized Boycotts:** Korean consumer boycotts (불매운동) have been a significant feature of civil society activism. The 2019 boycott of Japanese products (일본 불매운동, "No Japan" movement) following Japan's export restrictions on semiconductor materials was one of the most widespread consumer boycotts in Korean history, with sales of Japanese beer declining by over 90 percent and Japanese automobile sales dropping by 60 percent. This boycott was entirely consumer-driven with no government mandate, and no legal restrictions were imposed on participants.

### THAAD Dispute (2017)

The most significant boycott-related legal and economic issue in South Korean history was China's unofficial economic retaliation for Korea's deployment of the THAAD missile defense system. Chinese measures included: banning organized tour groups to South Korea, restricting distribution of Korean cultural content (한류, Hallyu) including K-pop concerts and K-drama broadcasts, forcing closure of Lotte Group's 87 China-based supermarkets (Lotte had provided the land for THAAD deployment), and restricting imports of Korean cosmetics and electronics. South Korea estimated economic losses of approximately KRW 8.5 trillion (USD 7.5 billion). Korea filed no WTO complaint but pursued diplomatic resolution, culminating in the October 2017 bilateral agreement. The episode demonstrated the vulnerability of export-dependent economies to informal boycott measures and the limitations of international trade law in addressing state-sponsored informal boycotts.

### Anti-Discrimination Framework

South Korea does not have a comprehensive anti-discrimination act. The National Human Rights Commission Act (국가인권위원회법, 2001) establishes the NHRC and defines discriminatory acts but does not specifically address boycotts motivated by discriminatory intent.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States maintains the most extensive anti-boycott legal framework globally, combining federal anti-foreign-boycott regulations with a rapidly expanding network of state anti-BDS laws.

**Export Administration Regulations (EAR), 15 C.F.R. Part 760:** Originally enacted through the Export Administration Act of 1979 (50 U.S.C. Section 4607) and maintained through executive orders since the Act's expiration, these regulations prohibit U.S. persons from:

- Refusing to do business with boycotted countries or nationals of boycotted countries (Section 760.2(a))
- Discriminating against any U.S. person on the basis of race, religion, sex, or national origin (Section 760.2(b))
- Furnishing information about business relationships with boycotted countries (Section 760.2(c))
- Furnishing information about the race, religion, sex, or national origin of any U.S. person (Section 760.2(d))
- Implementing letters of credit containing boycott-related conditions (Section 760.2(e))

The regulations were originally designed to counter the Arab League boycott of Israel. Violations carry civil penalties of up to $328,259 per violation and criminal penalties of up to $1 million and 20 years imprisonment.

**Ribicoff Amendment (26 U.S.C. Section 999):** This tax provision denies foreign tax credits, deferral of foreign income, and DISC benefits to persons participating in or cooperating with an international boycott. Taxpayers must report boycott-related requests on IRS Form 5713.

**State Anti-BDS Laws:** As of 2025, 38 states have enacted laws restricting boycotts of Israel, taking several forms:

| Type | Number of States | Mechanism |
|------|-----------------|-----------|
| State contractor certification | 25 states | Contractors must certify they do not boycott Israel |
| State divestment from boycotting companies | 18 states | State pension/investment funds must divest from BDS companies |
| Prohibition on state contracts with boycotting entities | 15 states | State may not contract with entities boycotting Israel |

### First Amendment Litigation

**Arkansas Times LP v. Waldrip, 37 F.4th 1386 (8th Cir. 2022), cert. denied 600 U.S. ___ (2023):** The Eighth Circuit upheld Arkansas's anti-BDS law (Act 710), holding that the law regulated commercial conduct (refusal to deal) rather than protected speech and therefore did not violate the First Amendment. The Supreme Court denied certiorari, leaving the constitutional question unresolved at the federal level.

**Koontz v. Watson, No. 5:17-cv-4099 (D. Kan. 2018):** A federal district court enjoined enforcement of Kansas's anti-BDS law against a schoolteacher who refused to sign a certification that she was not boycotting Israel, finding a likelihood of First Amendment violation. The case settled after Kansas amended its law to exempt sole proprietors.

**Amawi v. Pflugerville ISD, No. 1:18-cv-1091 (W.D. Tex. 2019):** A federal court enjoined enforcement of Texas's anti-BDS law (HB 89) against a speech pathologist who refused to certify she would not boycott Israel. Texas subsequently amended the law to apply only to companies with 10 or more employees.

**NAACP v. Claiborne Hardware Co., 458 U.S. 886 (1982):** The Supreme Court held that a civil rights boycott of white-owned businesses in Mississippi was protected expression under the First Amendment. This landmark ruling established that politically motivated boycotts constitute protected speech, though the Court distinguished between peaceful boycotts and those involving violence or intimidation.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's approach to boycotts is shaped by the historical trauma of the Nazi-era boycotts and a commitment to preventing discriminatory economic exclusion.

**GWB Section 1 (Prohibition of Anticompetitive Agreements):** The Gesetz gegen Wettbewerbsbeschränkungen prohibits agreements and concerted practices between undertakings that restrict competition. Coordinated commercial boycotts -- where competitors collectively refuse to deal with a specific entity -- may constitute violations of Section 1 GWB, subject to fines of up to 10 percent of annual turnover.

**Boycott as Unfair Competition:** Under the Gesetz gegen den unlauteren Wettbewerb (UWG, Act Against Unfair Competition), commercially motivated boycott calls may constitute unfair competitive practices if they involve misleading claims, undue pressure, or exploitation of market power.

**Historical Context -- 1933 Boycott:** The April 1, 1933, boycott of Jewish businesses organized by the Nazi party (Boykott jüdischer Geschäfte) remains a defining reference point in German legal and political discourse on boycotts. SA members stood outside Jewish-owned shops, law offices, and medical practices to prevent customers from entering. This historical experience profoundly shapes German sensitivity to boycotts that target businesses based on national, ethnic, or religious identity.

**Bundestag BDS Resolution (2019):** On May 17, 2019, the Bundestag adopted a non-binding resolution (Drucksache 19/10191) declaring the BDS movement anti-Semitic and comparing its boycott tactics to the Nazi-era "Don't buy from Jews" campaign. The resolution called on the federal government and Länder to withhold public funding and facilities from organizations supporting BDS. While legally non-binding, the resolution has had significant practical effects, with numerous municipalities refusing to rent public spaces for BDS-related events.

**Constitutional Dimensions:** The relationship between the Bundestag BDS resolution and Article 5 GG (freedom of expression) remains contested. In BVerfG, Decision of January 8, 2020 (1 BvR 2620/19), a complainant challenged the denial of a public room for a BDS-related event. The Constitutional Court did not reach the merits, but the case highlighted tensions between anti-discrimination commitments and expressive freedom.

### Criminal Law Dimensions

**Section 130 StGB (Volksverhetzung, Incitement to Hatred):** In extreme cases, boycott calls targeting specific ethnic, religious, or national groups may constitute incitement to hatred under Section 130 StGB, punishable by imprisonment of 3 months to 5 years. However, standard commercial or political boycotts do not typically meet the high threshold of Volksverhetzung.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Blocking Statute -- Regulation (EC) No 2271/96

The EU Blocking Statute, adopted on November 22, 1996, and updated by Delegated Regulation (EU) 2018/1100, prohibits EU persons from complying with certain specified extraterritorial sanctions or boycott measures imposed by third countries (specifically targeting U.S. secondary sanctions on Iran and Cuba). Key provisions:

- **Article 5:** EU persons shall not comply with the listed extraterritorial sanctions requirements, whether directly or through a subsidiary or intermediary.
- **Article 6:** EU persons who suffer damage from compliance with extraterritorial sanctions may seek compensation from the imposing state in EU courts.

In Bank Melli Iran v. Telekom Deutschland (Case C-124/20, CJEU Judgment of December 21, 2021), the CJEU held that the Blocking Statute does not require EU operators to maintain business relationships with sanctioned entities but prohibits termination of contracts solely to comply with U.S. secondary sanctions.

### European Court of Human Rights

**Baldassi and Others v. France (Application No. 15271/16, Judgment of June 11, 2020):** In this landmark decision, the Court found that the criminal conviction of BDS activists for incitement to economic discrimination violated Article 10 (freedom of expression). The applicants had been convicted under French law (Article 225-2 of the Penal Code) for promoting a boycott of Israeli products in a supermarket in Mulhouse. The Court held that the convictions constituted a disproportionate interference with the right to freedom of expression, emphasizing that calls for boycotts fall within the scope of political expression entitled to heightened protection. The judgment significantly constrained the ability of Council of Europe member states to criminalize boycott advocacy.

**Willem v. France (Application No. 10883/05, Judgment of July 16, 2009):** The Court found no violation of Article 10 in the conviction of a French mayor who issued an official municipal statement calling for a boycott of Israeli products, distinguishing between individual political expression (protected) and official acts of a public authority (subject to broader regulation).

### EU Sanctions Framework

The EU maintains its own autonomous sanctions regime under Article 215 TFEU, distinct from anti-boycott legislation. EU restrictive measures may include asset freezes, travel bans, and trade restrictions targeting specific countries, entities, or individuals. The comprehensive sanctions against Russia following the 2022 invasion of Ukraine represent the most extensive EU sanctions program, raising complex compliance questions for EU businesses.

## Comparative Analysis

### Scope and Purpose

The jurisdictions address fundamentally different boycott concerns. The U.S. anti-boycott framework targets compliance with foreign-imposed boycotts (specifically the Arab League boycott of Israel) and has expanded to encompass domestic anti-BDS legislation. The EU Blocking Statute addresses the reverse problem: prohibiting compliance with U.S. extraterritorial sanctions that the EU considers unlawful. Germany addresses boycotts primarily through the lens of historical anti-Semitism and competition law. South Korea has no specific anti-boycott framework, having experienced boycotts primarily as a target rather than as a regulator.

### Free Expression Tensions

The central legal tension across all jurisdictions concerns the relationship between boycotts and protected expression. The ECHR's Baldassi decision establishes the strongest protection for boycott advocacy, treating it as political expression entitled to heightened protection. U.S. courts are divided, with the Eighth Circuit treating anti-BDS laws as commercial regulation and district courts finding First Amendment violations. Germany's Bundestag BDS resolution has practical effects on expressive freedom despite its non-binding character.

### Economic vs. Political Boycotts

All four jurisdictions distinguish, explicitly or implicitly, between economically motivated boycotts (which may violate competition law) and politically motivated boycotts (which may be protected expression). The ECHR draws this distinction most clearly in Baldassi, treating boycott advocacy as political expression entitled to heightened protection under Article 10. U.S. law blurs the distinction through anti-BDS laws that regulate politically motivated commercial conduct -- proponents characterize these laws as regulating commercial discrimination, while opponents characterize them as suppressing political expression. South Korea's competition law addresses only the economic dimension, leaving political boycotts in a largely unregulated space where social pressure and market dynamics operate without legal constraint.

### Effectiveness and Enforcement

The effectiveness of anti-boycott laws varies significantly across jurisdictions. The U.S. EAR anti-boycott provisions have been largely successful in preventing American companies from complying with the Arab League boycott of Israel, with BIS reporting declining numbers of boycott-related requests. However, the EU Blocking Statute's effectiveness has been questioned, as many EU companies have quietly reduced business with sanctioned entities to avoid U.S. secondary sanctions exposure, despite the Blocking Statute's prohibition on such compliance. South Korea's experience as a target of China's THAAD-related boycott demonstrated the limitations of international trade law in addressing informal, state-sponsored economic coercion that operates through non-transparent mechanisms.

### Diplomatic and Trade Law Dimensions

The intersection of anti-boycott laws and international trade obligations creates complex legal tensions. State-sponsored boycotts may violate WTO principles of most-favored-nation treatment and national treatment. However, WTO Article XXI provides a security exception that states frequently invoke to justify trade restrictions. South Korea's decision not to file a WTO complaint against China's THAAD-related measures reflected both the difficulty of proving informal boycotts and the diplomatic calculation that litigation would further damage bilateral relations. The EU's approach of using the Blocking Statute rather than WTO dispute settlement to counter U.S. extraterritorial sanctions reflects a preference for unilateral protective measures over multilateral adjudication.

### Civil Society and Consumer Activism

Consumer-organized boycotts remain a powerful tool of civil society expression across all jurisdictions. South Korea's "No Japan" movement (2019) demonstrated the potential for consumer boycotts to achieve significant economic impact without government mandate or coordination. In the United States, consumer boycotts organized through social media platforms have targeted companies ranging from fast-food chains to social media platforms over political and social issues, with limited legal constraints. Germany's historical experience with the Nazi-era boycott creates particular sensitivity around boycotts targeting businesses based on national, ethnic, or religious identity, informing the Bundestag's BDS resolution even as the resolution's legal status remains non-binding.
