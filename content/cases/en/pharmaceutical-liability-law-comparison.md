## Summary

Pharmaceutical liability law (의약품 책임법) -- the legal framework governing the civil liability of drug manufacturers, distributors, prescribers, and regulatory authorities for injuries caused by pharmaceutical products -- represents a critical intersection of product liability, tort law, regulatory compliance, and public health policy across all major jurisdictions. South Korea addresses pharmaceutical liability through a combination of the Product Liability Act (제조물 책임법, Act No. 6109, 2000, substantially amended 2017), the Pharmaceutical Affairs Act (약사법), and general tort provisions under the Civil Code (민법, Article 750 et seq.), with the Supreme Court of Korea (대법원) having established important precedents on the burden of proof for pharmaceutical defects, including the landmark Decision 2008Da17517 (2011) which recognized a relaxed causation standard for vaccine injury claims. The United States has the most extensively developed pharmaceutical liability jurisprudence in the world, governed by state products liability law as informed by the Restatement (Third) of Torts: Products Liability (1998), the learned intermediary doctrine, federal preemption principles established in Wyeth v. Levine, 555 U.S. 555 (2009) and PLIVA, Inc. v. Mensing, 564 U.S. 604 (2011), the National Childhood Vaccine Injury Act of 1986 (42 U.S.C. Section 300aa-1 et seq.), and massive multidistrict litigation (MDL) proceedings that have produced some of the largest product liability settlements in history. Germany provides one of the most claimant-friendly pharmaceutical liability frameworks through the Arzneimittelgesetz (AMG, Medicinal Products Act), specifically Section 84 et seq., which establishes a strict liability regime for pharmaceutical manufacturers with a rebuttable presumption of causation under Section 84a AMG, supplemented by general product liability under the Produkthaftungsgesetz (ProdHaftG, implementing the EU Product Liability Directive 85/374/EEC) and tort liability under BGB Section 823. At the EU level, the Product Liability Directive 85/374/EEC (currently being revised as the proposed new Product Liability Directive COM/2022/495) establishes a harmonized strict liability framework for defective products including pharmaceuticals, while the ECtHR has addressed pharmaceutical liability through the lens of Article 2 (right to life) and Article 8 (right to respect for private life), notably in cases involving regulatory failure and access to medicines.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's pharmaceutical liability framework combines product liability legislation with pharmaceutical-specific regulation and evolving judicial doctrine.

**Product Liability Act (제조물책임법, PLA):** Enacted in 2000 and substantially amended in 2017, the PLA establishes strict liability for manufacturers of defective products, including pharmaceuticals. Article 3 imposes liability on the manufacturer when a product defect causes injury, without requiring proof of fault. Article 2 defines three types of defects: manufacturing defects, design defects, and warning/instruction defects (failure to warn). The 2017 amendment shifted the burden of proof, requiring the manufacturer to prove the absence of a defect rather than requiring the plaintiff to prove its existence, when the injury occurs during normal use.

**Pharmaceutical Affairs Act (약사법):** Regulates the manufacturing, importation, distribution, and safety of pharmaceuticals. Article 68-10 et seq. establish the drug safety management system, including adverse drug reaction reporting requirements and post-marketing surveillance obligations.

### Judicial Developments

**Supreme Court Decision 2008Da17517 (2011):** In a hepatitis B vaccine injury case, the Supreme Court recognized that strict proof of pharmacological causation may be relaxed where (1) the plaintiff was a healthy individual before receiving the pharmaceutical, (2) the injury occurred within a medically plausible timeframe after administration, and (3) no other cause for the injury can be identified. This epidemiological-inference approach significantly eased the plaintiff's burden in pharmaceutical cases.

**Supreme Court Decision 2011Da22092 (2013):** The Court addressed the scope of the duty to warn, holding that pharmaceutical manufacturers must provide warnings that are adequate for the medical profession and that reflect the current state of scientific knowledge, including risks identified through post-marketing surveillance.

### Drug Injury Relief Program

The Korea Institute of Drug Safety and Risk Management (한국의약품안전관리원, KIDS) administers the Drug Injury Relief Program (의약품부작용 피해구제제도), established under the Pharmaceutical Affairs Act. This no-fault compensation program provides relief for patients who suffer serious adverse drug reactions from properly manufactured and properly used pharmaceuticals. Compensation covers medical expenses, disability benefits, and death benefits, funded by contributions from pharmaceutical manufacturers.

### Humidifier Disinfectant Disaster

The humidifier disinfectant tragedy (가습기살균제 사건) of 2011, in which toxic humidifier sanitizer products caused over 1,600 deaths and thousands of lung injuries, catalyzed major reforms to South Korea's product liability and consumer safety frameworks. While technically involving household chemicals rather than pharmaceuticals, the disaster led to the enactment of the Special Act on Remedy for Damage Caused by Humidifier Disinfectants (가습기살균제 피해구제를 위한 특별법, 2017) and broader strengthening of product safety regulation.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has produced the world's most extensive body of pharmaceutical liability jurisprudence, driven by its litigation-oriented legal culture, contingency fee system, class action and MDL mechanisms, and punitive damages availability.

**Products Liability Framework:** Pharmaceutical liability is governed primarily by state tort law, with the Restatement (Third) of Torts: Products Liability (1998) providing influential guidance. Section 6 specifically addresses prescription drug and medical device liability, establishing that a prescription drug or device is defective if it contains a manufacturing defect, is not accompanied by adequate instructions or warnings (Section 6(d)), or has a design defect where the foreseeable risks of harm are sufficiently great that no reasonable healthcare provider would prescribe the drug for any class of patients (Section 6(c)).

**Learned Intermediary Doctrine:** A majority of US states recognize this doctrine, which holds that the pharmaceutical manufacturer's duty to warn runs to the prescribing physician rather than directly to the patient, because the physician serves as a "learned intermediary" who evaluates the drug's risks and benefits for the individual patient. Exceptions exist in some states for direct-to-consumer (DTC) advertising, mass immunization programs, and oral contraceptives.

### Federal Preemption

The interaction between federal FDA regulation and state tort liability has produced a complex preemption landscape:

| Case | Holding | Impact |
|------|---------|--------|
| Wyeth v. Levine, 555 U.S. 555 (2009) | FDA approval of a brand-name drug's label does not preempt state failure-to-warn claims | Brand-name manufacturers remain subject to state tort liability |
| PLIVA, Inc. v. Mensing, 564 U.S. 604 (2011) | Federal law preempts state failure-to-warn claims against generic drug manufacturers | Generic manufacturers shielded from failure-to-warn claims due to federal duty of sameness |
| Mutual Pharm. Co. v. Bartlett, 570 U.S. 472 (2013) | Extended Mensing preemption to design defect claims against generic manufacturers | Broad generic manufacturer protection |
| Merck Sharp & Dohme Corp. v. Albrecht, 587 U.S. 299 (2019) | "Clear evidence" preemption defense is a question for the judge, not the jury | Procedural clarification of brand-name preemption defense |

### National Childhood Vaccine Injury Act (NCVIA, 1986)

The NCVIA (42 U.S.C. Section 300aa-1 et seq.) created the National Vaccine Injury Compensation Program (VICP), a no-fault administrative compensation system for vaccine injuries. Petitioners file claims in the U.S. Court of Federal Claims before "special masters." The Vaccine Injury Table lists specific injuries and timeframes that create a presumption of causation. In Bruesewitz v. Wyeth LLC, 562 U.S. 223 (2011), the Supreme Court held that the NCVIA preempts all design defect claims against vaccine manufacturers.

### Major Pharmaceutical MDLs

Multidistrict litigation has become the primary mechanism for resolving mass pharmaceutical injury claims. Notable proceedings include the Vioxx litigation (Merck, $4.85 billion settlement, 2007), opioid litigation (MDL No. 2804, resulting in multi-billion dollar settlements with manufacturers, distributors, and pharmacy chains), and Zantac/ranitidine litigation (MDL No. 2924, Daubert challenges to plaintiff expert testimony).

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany provides one of the most comprehensive and claimant-favorable pharmaceutical liability regimes in the world through the AMG's specialized provisions.

**AMG Section 84 -- Strict Liability:** The Arzneimittelgesetz establishes strict liability (Gefaehrdungshaftung) for pharmaceutical manufacturers when a pharmaceutical product, used in accordance with its intended purpose, has harmful effects that exceed what is acceptable in light of the current state of medical science (Section 84(1)(1) -- development defect), or when the injury is caused by labeling, information for specialists, or instructions for use that do not correspond to the current state of medical knowledge (Section 84(1)(2) -- information defect).

**AMG Section 84a -- Presumption of Causation:** This provision creates a rebuttable presumption of causation where the pharmaceutical is "suitable" (geeignet) to cause the damage that occurred, considering the product's composition, dosage, duration of use, temporal proximity, the patient's state of health, and all other circumstances that support or contradict causation. This presumption substantially shifts the burden to the manufacturer, who must rebut it with evidence that the pharmaceutical did not cause the injury.

### The Contergan/Thalidomide Case

The Contergan (thalidomide) catastrophe of the late 1950s and early 1960s, in which the sedative Contergan manufactured by Chemie Gruenenthal caused severe birth defects (phocomelia) in approximately 10,000 children worldwide (including approximately 5,000 in Germany), was the defining event in the development of modern pharmaceutical liability law. The criminal trial (LG Aachen, 1968-1970, Az. 4 KMs 1/68) ended in a settlement without a conviction, but the case directly led to the enactment of AMG Section 84 in the 1976 reform of the Arzneimittelgesetz and the establishment of the Conterganstiftung (Contergan Foundation) to compensate victims. The Conterganstiftungsgesetz provides ongoing financial support to survivors.

### AMG vs. ProdHaftG Liability

| Feature | AMG Section 84 | ProdHaftG (Product Liability Act) |
|---------|----------------|----------------------------------|
| Scope | Pharmaceuticals specifically | All products including pharmaceuticals |
| Liability type | Strict (Gefaehrdungshaftung) | Strict (no-fault for defective products) |
| Causation presumption | Yes (Section 84a) | No; plaintiff bears burden |
| Development risk defense | Not available | Available (Section 1(2)(5)) |
| Damages cap | EUR 600,000 per person; EUR 120 million aggregate | EUR 85 million aggregate for personal injury |
| Immaterial damages | Available (pain and suffering) | Available |

### Pharmapool

German pharmaceutical manufacturers participate in Pharmapool, an industry consortium that provides pooled insurance coverage for AMG Section 84 liability. AMG Section 94 requires manufacturers to maintain adequate financial security (insurance or guarantee) to cover potential liability claims, ensuring that injured patients can obtain compensation even if the manufacturer becomes insolvent.

### Information Disclosure (AMG Section 84a(2))

The manufacturer is required to disclose to the claimant all information and data known to it regarding the effects, side effects, and interactions of the pharmaceutical, as well as any suspected cases of harmful effects reported to it. This discovery-like disclosure obligation, unique among European jurisdictions, facilitates claimants' ability to establish their claims.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Product Liability Directive (85/374/EEC)

The Product Liability Directive establishes a harmonized strict liability framework across EU member states for damage caused by defective products, including pharmaceuticals.

**Defect Standard (Article 6):** A product is defective when it does not provide the safety which a person is entitled to expect, taking all circumstances into account, including the presentation of the product, the use to which the product could reasonably be expected to be put, and the time when the product was put into circulation.

**CJEU Pharmaceutical Jurisprudence:**

**Sanofi Pasteur (C-621/15, Judgment of June 21, 2017):** The Court of Justice held that Article 4 of the Directive does not preclude national evidentiary rules that allow a court to find that a vaccine is defective and that there is a causal link between the defect and the injury on the basis of serious, specific, and consistent presumptions (indices graves, precis et concordants), even in the absence of definitive scientific proof. The Court specified that such presumptions must be drawn from evidence including the temporal proximity between vaccination and onset of disease, the absence of personal or family history of the disease, and a significant number of reported cases of the disease occurring after vaccination.

**Boston Scientific Medizintechnik (C-503/13 and C-504/13, Judgment of March 5, 2015):** The Court held that where products belonging to the same group or forming part of the same production series have a potential defect, such a product may be classified as defective without there being any need to establish that the specific product is defective.

### Proposed New Product Liability Directive (COM/2022/495)

The European Commission proposed a revised Product Liability Directive in September 2022 to modernize the framework. Key changes relevant to pharmaceutical liability include: extension of liability to online platforms and importers; facilitation of the burden of proof through disclosure of evidence and rebuttable presumptions of defectiveness or causation where the claimant faces excessive difficulty; removal of the EUR 500 threshold for property damage claims; and retention of the development risk defense as a member state option.

### Representative Actions Directive (2020/1828/EU)

Effective from June 25, 2023, this Directive requires member states to establish representative action mechanisms allowing qualified entities to bring collective redress actions on behalf of consumers, including in pharmaceutical injury cases. This harmonizes access to collective litigation for pharmaceutical harm across the EU.

### ECtHR Jurisprudence

**Tatar v. Romania (Application No. 67021/01, Judgment of January 27, 2009):** While not directly a pharmaceutical case, the Court established that Article 8 ECHR imposes positive obligations on states to protect individuals from serious environmental and health risks, including those arising from industrial activities. This principle extends to pharmaceutical regulatory obligations.

**Vasileva v. Bulgaria (Application No. 23796/10, Judgment of March 17, 2016):** The Court found a violation of Article 2 (right to life) where the state's regulatory framework failed to ensure adequate pharmacovigilance and timely response to reports of serious adverse drug reactions.

## Comparative Analysis

### Liability Standards

All four jurisdictions impose some form of strict liability for pharmaceutical defects, but the scope and application differ substantially. Germany's AMG Section 84 provides the broadest strict liability with the most favorable causation presumption for claimants. The US applies strict liability but carves out significant protections for prescription drugs under the Restatement (Third) Section 6(c), making design defect claims exceptionally difficult. South Korea's PLA provides strict liability but with a development risk defense. The EU Directive establishes a harmonized strict liability baseline but leaves significant procedural aspects to member states.

### Causation and Proof

The most significant practical difference across jurisdictions lies in the approach to proving causation. Germany's AMG Section 84a presumption of causation effectively reverses the burden, requiring manufacturers to disprove causation once the plaintiff establishes suitability. The CJEU's Sanofi Pasteur decision permits presumption-based evidence in the absence of definitive scientific proof. South Korea's Supreme Court has adopted a relaxed epidemiological-inference approach. The US maintains the plaintiff's burden but applies the Daubert standard to expert testimony, creating a significant gatekeeping function that has excluded plaintiff claims in several major MDLs.

### Learned Intermediary Doctrine

The learned intermediary doctrine represents a uniquely American approach, channeling the manufacturer's duty to warn through the prescribing physician. Neither Germany, South Korea, nor the EU recognize this doctrine. Under AMG Section 84, the manufacturer is directly liable for information defects regardless of the prescriber's role. This divergence has practical significance for direct-to-consumer pharmaceutical advertising, which is permitted in the US but prohibited in the EU, South Korea, and most other jurisdictions.

### Collective Litigation

The US MDL system provides the most developed mechanism for mass pharmaceutical litigation, with dedicated judicial infrastructure and experienced transferee judges. Germany's Musterfeststellungsklage (model declaratory action), introduced in 2018, and the EU's Representative Actions Directive are developing but remain less powerful tools for pharmaceutical mass litigation. South Korea's class action framework under the Civil Procedure Act has been used in pharmaceutical cases but is less frequently employed than US mechanisms.

### No-Fault Compensation

South Korea's Drug Injury Relief Program and the US VICP represent alternative approaches to pharmaceutical injury compensation that bypass the tort system. Germany's Pharmapool provides pooled insurance rather than a separate compensation scheme. The EU lacks a harmonized no-fault pharmaceutical compensation mechanism, though several member states have national schemes.
