## Summary

Nuclear weapons policy -- the legal frameworks governing the development, possession, deployment, testing, use, and threat of use of nuclear weapons -- represents the highest-stakes intersection of international law, national security, and existential risk, with profoundly different legal positions depending on whether a state possesses nuclear weapons, shelters under a nuclear umbrella, or pursues denuclearization. South Korea is a non-nuclear-weapon state party to the Treaty on the Non-Proliferation of Nuclear Weapons (NPT) and subject to International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) safeguards under a Comprehensive Safeguards Agreement, while relying on the United States nuclear umbrella through the Mutual Defense Treaty (한미상호방위조약, 1953) and the Washington Declaration of April 2023, which established the Nuclear Consultative Group (NCG) to enhance extended deterrence coordination; South Korea's domestic framework is governed by the Atomic Energy Act (원자력법) and the Nuclear Safety Act (원자력안전법), with the periodic domestic debate about indigenous nuclear weapons development constrained by NPT obligations and alliance commitments. The United States, as one of the five NPT-recognized nuclear-weapon states, maintains a nuclear arsenal of approximately 5,500 warheads governed by the Atomic Energy Act of 1954 (42 U.S.C. Section 2011 et seq.), with nuclear weapons employment authority vested solely in the President under the nuclear command structure, and arms control commitments shaped by the New START Treaty (2010, expired 2026), the Nuclear Posture Review process, and domestic statutory constraints including the prohibition on nuclear testing under the Comprehensive Nuclear-Test-Ban Treaty (CTBT, signed but not ratified). Germany, as a non-nuclear-weapon state, hosts approximately 20 U.S. B61 nuclear gravity bombs at Büchel Air Base under NATO nuclear sharing arrangements, a practice that has generated sustained legal controversy regarding compatibility with the NPT, while German law prohibits domestic nuclear weapons development under Article 26 of the Grundgesetz and the obligations assumed under the Treaty on the Final Settlement with Respect to Germany (Two Plus Four Treaty, 1990). At the international level, the International Court of Justice's 1996 Advisory Opinion on the Legality of the Threat or Use of Nuclear Weapons established that international humanitarian law applies to nuclear weapons while controversially declining to find their use categorically illegal, and the Treaty on the Prohibition of Nuclear Weapons (TPNW, 2021) represents the most ambitious attempt to establish a comprehensive ban, though no nuclear-weapon state or NATO member has joined.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's nuclear policy operates within a framework defined by international treaty obligations, alliance commitments, and domestic legislation.

**NPT Obligations:** South Korea ratified the NPT on April 23, 1975, as a non-nuclear-weapon state. Under Article II, South Korea undertakes not to receive, manufacture, or otherwise acquire nuclear weapons or nuclear explosive devices. South Korea concluded a Comprehensive Safeguards Agreement with the IAEA (INFCIRC/236) in 1975 and an Additional Protocol in 2004, enabling enhanced IAEA inspection authority.

**Atomic Energy Act (원자력법):** The Act regulates the peaceful use of nuclear energy, including the operation of South Korea's 26 commercial nuclear reactors and nuclear fuel cycle activities. The Nuclear Safety Act (원자력안전법), separated from the Atomic Energy Act in 2011, governs safety regulation through the Nuclear Safety and Security Commission (원자력안전위원회).

### Extended Deterrence: Washington Declaration (2023)

On April 26, 2023, President Yoon Suk-yeol and President Biden issued the Washington Declaration, establishing the bilateral Nuclear Consultative Group (NCG) to strengthen extended deterrence. Key elements include:

- Regular visits of U.S. strategic nuclear submarines (SSBNs) to Korean ports
- Enhanced information sharing on nuclear planning and strategy
- South Korean conventional support for U.S. nuclear operations in contingency
- Reaffirmation of U.S. extended deterrence commitment

### Domestic Nuclear Debate

Public polling consistently shows significant support for indigenous nuclear weapons development in South Korea, driven by North Korea's advancing nuclear and missile capabilities. A February 2023 survey indicated approximately 76 percent support for developing South Korea's own nuclear weapons. However, this option faces multiple legal and political constraints:

| Constraint | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| NPT obligations | Withdrawal requires 3-month notice citing "extraordinary events" jeopardizing supreme interests |
| U.S. alliance | Nuclear weapons development would likely undermine the alliance framework |
| IAEA safeguards | All nuclear facilities under comprehensive safeguards |
| 123 Agreement | U.S.-ROK nuclear cooperation agreement restricts enrichment and reprocessing |
| Economic risk | Potential sanctions and trade consequences |

### Historical Nuclear Activities

South Korea pursued a covert nuclear weapons program in the 1970s under President Park Chung-hee, which was terminated under U.S. pressure in 1975. In 2004, South Korea disclosed previously unreported nuclear experiments conducted between 1982 and 2000, including uranium enrichment and plutonium separation experiments. The IAEA investigated and concluded that the activities did not constitute a weapons program but represented safeguards violations.

## United States

### Legal Framework

**Atomic Energy Act of 1954 (42 U.S.C. Section 2011 et seq.):** The foundational statute governing both civilian and military nuclear activities. The Act establishes the Department of Energy's (successor to the Atomic Energy Commission) authority over nuclear weapons research, development, testing, and production. Section 91 restricts the transfer of nuclear weapons to foreign nations.

**Nuclear Command Authority:** The President of the United States has sole authority to order the use of nuclear weapons. There is no legal requirement for congressional authorization, judicial review, or concurrence from the Secretary of Defense, although the military chain of command verifies the authenticity of the order. This arrangement has generated persistent debate about the adequacy of checks on nuclear use authority.

### Arms Control Framework

| Treaty/Agreement | Status | Key Provision |
|-----------------|--------|---------------|
| NPT (1968/1970) | Ratified | Recognized nuclear-weapon state; Article VI disarmament obligation |
| ABM Treaty (1972) | Withdrew 2002 | Limited anti-ballistic missile systems |
| INF Treaty (1987) | Withdrew 2019 | Eliminated intermediate-range nuclear forces |

| New START (2010) | Expired Feb 2026 | Limited deployed strategic warheads to 1,550 |

### Nuclear Modernization

The United States is undertaking a comprehensive nuclear modernization program estimated at $1.5-2 trillion over 30 years, including:
- Columbia-class ballistic missile submarines (replacing Ohio-class)
- B-21 Raider strategic bomber
- LGM-35A Sentinel ICBM (replacing Minuteman III)
- W93 warhead for submarine-launched ballistic missiles
- B61-12 and B61-13 gravity bombs

### Key Legal Debates

**No First Use:** Multiple legislative proposals have sought to establish a "no first use" nuclear policy as a matter of law, including the No First Use Act introduced by Senator Elizabeth Warren and Representative Adam Smith. No such legislation has been enacted.

**Sole Authority:** The Restricting First Use of Nuclear Weapons Act, proposed repeatedly since 2017, would prohibit the use of nuclear weapons without a prior declaration of war by Congress. The proposal has not advanced beyond committee hearings.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's non-nuclear status is anchored in multiple legal instruments:

**Two Plus Four Treaty (1990):** Article 3 of the Treaty on the Final Settlement with Respect to Germany provides that "The Federal Republic of Germany reaffirms its renunciation of the manufacture and possession of and control over nuclear, biological and chemical weapons." This international legal obligation, considered binding under international law, effectively prohibits indigenous German nuclear weapons development.

**Grundgesetz Article 26:** The Basic Law's prohibition on preparations for aggressive war and requirement for government authorization of weapons-of-warfare production provides a constitutional basis for nuclear weapons prohibition.

**NPT Obligations:** Germany ratified the NPT in 1975 as a non-nuclear-weapon state and is subject to IAEA safeguards and Euratom verification under the trilateral safeguards agreement (INFCIRC/193).

### NATO Nuclear Sharing

Germany participates in NATO's nuclear sharing arrangements, hosting approximately 20 U.S. B61 nuclear gravity bombs at Fliegerhorst Büchel in Rhineland-Palatinate. Under nuclear sharing, German Tornado (and future successor) aircraft are certified to deliver U.S. nuclear weapons in wartime, with release authorization from the U.S. President and NATO's Nuclear Planning Group.

**Legal Controversy:** Critics argue that NATO nuclear sharing violates Articles I and II of the NPT, which prohibit nuclear-weapon states from transferring nuclear weapons to non-nuclear-weapon states and vice versa. NATO and participating states maintain that nuclear sharing was an existing practice when the NPT was negotiated and that transfer of control occurs only in wartime, when the NPT would no longer apply. This interpretation remains contested by non-aligned states and disarmament advocates.

### Policy Debate

Germany's nuclear policy debate has intensified since Russia's invasion of Ukraine:

- The 2022 NATO Strategic Concept reaffirmed nuclear deterrence as the "supreme guarantee of the Allies' security"
- Chancellor Scholz announced procurement of F-35 aircraft as the Tornado replacement, ensuring continued nuclear delivery capability
- The Bundestag debated but rejected a 2023 motion to withdraw from nuclear sharing
- Public opinion remains divided, with significant support for both nuclear sharing and disarmament

## ECHR & EU / International

### ICJ Advisory Opinion on Nuclear Weapons (1996)

On July 8, 1996, the International Court of Justice delivered its Advisory Opinion on the Legality of the Threat or Use of Nuclear Weapons, requested by the UN General Assembly. The Court's findings:

- The threat or use of nuclear weapons would "generally be contrary to the rules of international law applicable in armed conflict, and in particular the principles and rules of humanitarian law"
- The Court could not definitively conclude "whether the threat or use of nuclear weapons would be lawful or unlawful in an extreme circumstance of self-defence, in which the very survival of a State would be at stake"
- There exists an obligation to pursue in good faith and bring to a conclusion negotiations leading to nuclear disarmament (unanimously)

The split decision (7-7 with President Bedjaoui's casting vote on the self-defense exception) remains the most significant and debated international judicial pronouncement on nuclear weapons.

### Treaty on the Prohibition of Nuclear Weapons (TPNW)

The TPNW, adopted on July 7, 2017, and entering into force on January 22, 2021, is the first multilateral treaty to comprehensively prohibit nuclear weapons. Article 1 prohibits states parties from developing, testing, producing, manufacturing, acquiring, possessing, stockpiling, using, or threatening to use nuclear weapons.

As of 2026, 94 states have ratified the TPNW. No nuclear-weapon state, NATO member (including Germany), or U.S. ally under a nuclear umbrella (including South Korea and Japan) has signed or ratified the treaty. The absence of nuclear-armed and nuclear-dependent states limits the TPNW's practical disarmament impact but proponents argue it establishes a normative framework that will progressively delegitimize nuclear weapons.

### Nuclear-Weapon-Free Zones

Five regional nuclear-weapon-free zone treaties establish denuclearized areas:

| Treaty | Region | Year |
|--------|--------|------|
| Treaty of Tlatelolco | Latin America and Caribbean | 1967 |
| Treaty of Rarotonga | South Pacific | 1985 |
| Treaty of Bangkok | Southeast Asia | 1995 |
| Treaty of Pelindaba | Africa | 1996 |
| Treaty of Semipalatinsk | Central Asia | 2006 |

Northeast Asia, despite hosting the Korean Peninsula crisis, has no nuclear-weapon-free zone, though proposals have been periodically advanced.

### ECHR Dimension

While the ECHR does not directly address nuclear weapons, the Court has addressed related issues:

**CND v. United Kingdom (2002, inadmissibility decision):** The Campaign for Nuclear Disarmament challenged the legality of the UK's nuclear weapons policy under the ECHR. The Court declared the application inadmissible, finding that questions of national defense policy fall within the margin of appreciation of member states.

### Tactical Nuclear Weapons

The renewed focus on tactical (non-strategic) nuclear weapons has distinct implications for each jurisdiction:

**South Korea:** The deployment of U.S. tactical nuclear weapons on the Korean Peninsula (withdrawn in 1991 under the Presidential Nuclear Initiative) has been periodically debated as a response to North Korea's nuclear program. The Washington Declaration (2023) enhanced extended deterrence short of redeployment. Some Korean policymakers have advocated for the return of tactical nuclear weapons, though this would face significant legal and diplomatic obstacles.

**United States:** The B61-12 tactical nuclear gravity bomb remains the primary U.S. tactical nuclear weapon. Deployed in Europe under NATO nuclear sharing, the B61-12 is also planned for deployment on the new B-21 Raider bomber and F-35 aircraft. The U.S. has approximately 200 tactical nuclear weapons deployed in Europe.

**Germany:** The approximately 20 B61 bombs at Büchel represent the entirety of Germany's nuclear sharing commitment. The transition from Tornado to F-35 aircraft for the nuclear delivery mission has been politically controversial, with some coalition partners favoring withdrawal from nuclear sharing.

### North Korea's Nuclear Program

North Korea's nuclear weapons program, estimated at 50-70 warheads as of 2026, provides the critical security context for South Korea's nuclear policy and for U.S. extended deterrence:

## Comparative Analysis

### Nuclear Status and Legal Obligations

The legal frameworks are fundamentally shaped by nuclear status. The United States, as a nuclear-weapon state, faces Article VI NPT obligations to pursue disarmament but faces no prohibition on possession. South Korea and Germany, as non-nuclear-weapon states, are prohibited from acquiring nuclear weapons under the NPT, though each engages with nuclear deterrence through alliance arrangements -- South Korea through extended deterrence and Germany through nuclear sharing. This creates a legal tension between non-proliferation obligations and deterrence dependence.

### Extended Deterrence Mechanisms

South Korea and Germany rely on U.S. nuclear weapons for their ultimate security guarantee but through different mechanisms. South Korea's Washington Declaration (2023) establishes a consultative framework without physical nuclear weapon presence on Korean territory. Germany physically hosts U.S. nuclear weapons and trains its air force for nuclear delivery missions. Both arrangements face legitimacy challenges -- South Korea's from the credibility of extended deterrence across the Pacific, Germany's from NPT compatibility concerns.

### Disarmament Versus Deterrence

The fundamental tension in nuclear weapons law is between the disarmament obligation (NPT Article VI, ICJ Advisory Opinion) and the continued reliance on nuclear deterrence. The TPNW represents the most ambitious attempt to resolve this tension through comprehensive prohibition, but its effectiveness is limited by the non-participation of nuclear-armed states and their allies. The ICJ's inability to rule definitively on the legality of nuclear weapons use in extreme self-defense continues to provide legal ambiguity that nuclear-armed states exploit to justify their arsenals.
