## Summary

Ride-sharing regulation -- the legal framework governing transportation network companies (TNCs) such as Uber, Lyft, and their local equivalents that use digital platforms to connect passengers with drivers using personal vehicles -- represents one of the most disruptive intersections of technology, transportation policy, and labor law in the 21st century, challenging established taxi and livery regulatory frameworks worldwide. South Korea has maintained one of the most restrictive approaches among developed nations, with the Passenger Transport Service Act (여객자동차 운수사업법) effectively prohibiting most ride-hailing services using private vehicles, leading to the dramatic shutdown of Uber's Seoul operations in 2015 after the CEO was indicted for operating an unlicensed taxi service; subsequent legislative compromises led to the Act on the Improvement of Shared Transportation (여객자동차 운수사업법 개정안, commonly known as the "Tada Act" or 타다 금지법) of 2020, which restricted platform-based transportation services while carving out limited exceptions, and the death by self-immolation of taxi driver Choi Ung (최웅) in December 2018 in protest against the Tada ride-sharing service underscored the intensity of the regulatory debate. The United States has adopted the most permissive approach, with California's AB 2293 (2014) creating the first statutory framework for TNCs and the California Public Utilities Commission establishing regulations that have been replicated across most states, though the worker classification debate remains fiercely contested, as illustrated by California's Proposition 22 (2020) and its subsequent partial invalidation by the California Supreme Court's ongoing litigation. Germany has largely maintained its restrictive approach, with the Personenbeförderungsgesetz (Passenger Transport Act, PBefG) requiring all commercial passenger transport to operate under licensed categories, though the 2021 PBefG reform created a new category of "gebündelter Bedarfsverkehr" (pooled on-demand transport) that provides a limited regulatory pathway for platform-based services. At the European level, the CJEU's landmark Asociación Profesional Elite Taxi v. Uber Systems Spain (Case C-434/15, 2017) held that Uber constitutes a transport service rather than an information society service, subjecting it to member state transport regulation, and the proposed EU Platform Workers Directive addresses the classification of platform workers across the gig economy.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's ride-sharing regulation is governed by the Passenger Transport Service Act (여객자동차 운수사업법), which classifies passenger transport into licensed categories: taxi (택시), bus (버스), charter (전세), and rental car (렌터카) services. The Act effectively prohibits the use of private vehicles for commercial passenger transport outside these licensed categories, creating a near-total regulatory barrier for Western-style ride-hailing platforms.

### Uber's Failed Entry

Uber launched its UberX service in Seoul in August 2014. In December 2014, Seoul Mayor Park Won-soon (박원순) filed a criminal complaint against Uber CEO Travis Kalanick for operating an unlicensed taxi service in violation of the Passenger Transport Service Act. The Seoul Central District Court issued an arrest warrant, and Uber suspended its UberX operations in Seoul in March 2015. Uber subsequently pivoted to a model operating through licensed taxi companies and licensed rental car partnerships, offering Uber Taxi (a dispatch service for licensed taxis) and Uber Black (using licensed corporate vehicles).

### The Tada (타다) Controversy

VCNC (subsidiary of Socar) launched the Tada mobility platform in October 2018, operating 11-15 seat vans with employed drivers under a rental car license exemption. The service rapidly gained popularity, reaching 1.7 million registered users within a year.

**Taxi Driver Protests and Self-Immolation:** On December 10, 2018, taxi driver Choi Ung (최웅) died by self-immolation in front of the National Assembly in protest against Tada, which taxi drivers viewed as an illegal ride-sharing service undermining their livelihoods. His death galvanized the taxi industry's opposition and intensified the political debate.

**Tada Act (2020):** The National Assembly enacted amendments to the Passenger Transport Service Act in March 2020 (commonly known as the "Tada Act" or 타다 금지법), which prohibited the operation of 11-15 seat rental vehicles through platform dispatch for more than six hours per trip -- effectively banning Tada's business model. The legislation carved out exceptions for premium taxi services, late-night services, and rural transportation. VCNC had been acquitted in January 2020 of operating an illegal taxi service under the previous law, but the new legislation effectively shut down the service.

### Current Status

As of 2025, ride-sharing in South Korea operates primarily through: Kakao T (카카오 T), which dispatches licensed taxis; Uber Taxi, operating through licensed taxis; and limited premium transportation services. The regulatory framework continues to prioritize the protection of the licensed taxi industry.

### Penalties

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| Operating unlicensed passenger transport | Up to 2 years imprisonment or 20 million KRW fine |
| Platform facilitating unlicensed transport | Criminal prosecution of platform operators |
| Violation of Tada Act provisions | Administrative sanctions; license revocation |

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has adopted the most permissive approach to ride-sharing regulation, with most states having enacted TNC-specific legislation or regulations that create a new regulatory category distinct from taxis and limousines.

**California AB 2293 (2014) and CPUC Framework:** California was the first jurisdiction to create a formal regulatory framework for TNCs. The California Public Utilities Commission (CPUC) issued Decision 13-09-045 in September 2013, creating the TNC category and establishing requirements for insurance, driver background checks, vehicle inspections, and data reporting. AB 2293 (effective January 1, 2015) codified TNC insurance requirements, establishing a three-tier insurance model: Period 1 (app on, awaiting request: minimum $50,000/$100,000/$30,000); Period 2 (en route to pickup: $1 million commercial liability); Period 3 (passenger in vehicle: $1 million commercial liability).

### Worker Classification

The classification of ride-sharing drivers as independent contractors or employees is the most contentious legal issue in US ride-sharing regulation.

**California AB 5 (2019):** Codified the ABC test from Dynamex Operations West, Inc. v. Superior Court, 4 Cal.5th 903 (2018), presuming workers are employees unless the hiring entity proves the worker is (A) free from control and direction, (B) performing work outside the usual course of the hiring entity's business, and (C) customarily engaged in an independently established trade.

**Proposition 22 (2020):** California voters approved Proposition 22 in November 2020, exempting app-based drivers from AB 5 by classifying them as independent contractors with certain benefits (minimum earnings guarantee, healthcare subsidy, accident insurance). In 2021, an Alameda County Superior Court ruled portions of Proposition 22 unconstitutional. In 2024, the California Court of Appeal largely upheld Proposition 22 but struck down the provision requiring a 7/8 supermajority to amend it.

**Federal Level:** The DOL has issued guidance and rules on worker classification under the Fair Labor Standards Act. The Biden administration's 2024 final rule on independent contractor classification applies a totality-of-the-circumstances economic reality test.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany regulates ride-sharing through the Personenbeförderungsgesetz (PBefG, Passenger Transport Act), which was substantially reformed in 2021 to address platform-based mobility services while maintaining the traditional regulatory framework for taxis and rental cars (Mietwagen).

### PBefG Category System

The PBefG establishes distinct licensing categories for passenger transport: Linienverkehr (scheduled service -- buses, trams); Gelegenheitsverkehr (occasional transport -- taxis, Mietwagen); and, since the 2021 reform, gebündelter Bedarfsverkehr (pooled on-demand transport) and Linienbedarfsverkehr (on-demand scheduled service).

**Taxi (§ 47 PBefG):** Taxis are obligated to serve all passengers (Beförderungspflicht), may be hailed on the street, and are subject to regulated fares (Tarifpflicht). Taxi licenses are subject to numerical quotas in most German cities.

**Mietwagen (§ 49 PBefG):** Rental cars with drivers operate under a distinct license. Critically, the Rückkehrpflicht (return obligation) requires that Mietwagen return to their operating base after completing each trip, rather than cruising for the next passenger -- a provision designed to prevent Mietwagen from operating as de facto taxis. This provision has been central to the Uber regulatory debate in Germany.

### 2021 PBefG Reform

The 2021 PBefG reform (effective August 1, 2021) introduced several innovations: the new category of gebündelter Bedarfsverkehr (pooled on-demand transport), allowing platform-based pooling services; relaxation (not elimination) of the Rückkehrpflicht for Mietwagen, permitting them to accept new rides without returning to base under certain conditions; introduction of minimum fare regulations to prevent predatory pricing; and requirements for platform operators to provide data to regulatory authorities.

### Uber in Germany

Uber has faced persistent regulatory challenges in Germany. In 2014, a Frankfurt court issued an injunction against UberPOP (using private drivers) for violating the PBefG. Uber subsequently shifted to operating through licensed Mietwagen drivers. Several German courts have addressed Uber's operations, generally holding that the Rückkehrpflicht and other PBefG requirements apply to Uber-dispatched Mietwagen. As of 2025, Uber operates in several German cities (Berlin, Munich, Frankfurt, Düsseldorf) through licensed Mietwagen and, in some cities, through taxi dispatch partnerships.

### Penalties

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| Operating unlicensed passenger transport (PBefG § 61) | Fine up to 20,000 EUR; criminal prosecution possible |
| Violation of Rückkehrpflicht | Administrative fine; license revocation risk |
| Operating without proper insurance | Fine; license suspension |

## ECHR & EU

### CJEU -- Asociación Profesional Elite Taxi v. Uber Systems Spain (Case C-434/15)

The CJEU's Grand Chamber judgment of December 20, 2017, was the most significant EU-level decision on ride-sharing regulation. The case was referred by a Barcelona commercial court in the context of a complaint by a taxi association against Uber's unlicensed operations in Barcelona.

The Court held that Uber's intermediation service "must be regarded as forming an integral part of an overall service whose main component is a transport service and, accordingly, must be classified not as 'an information society service' within the meaning of Article 1(2) of Directive 98/34, but as 'a service in the field of transport' within the meaning of Article 2(2)(d) of Directive 2006/123." This classification meant that Uber could not invoke the E-Commerce Directive or the Services Directive to challenge member state transport regulations.

### EU Platform Workers Directive

The Directive on improving working conditions in platform work, politically agreed in March 2024 and formally adopted in October 2024, introduces a rebuttable presumption of employment for platform workers (including ride-sharing drivers) where certain indicators of control are present. Member states must transpose the directive by 2026. The directive requires platforms to: provide transparency on algorithmic management; allow human review of automated decisions; and inform workers and their representatives about automated monitoring and decision-making systems.

### Digital Services Act (DSA)

Regulation (EU) 2022/2065 (Digital Services Act), applicable from February 17, 2024, imposes obligations on digital platforms including ride-sharing platforms regarding transparency, illegal content, and algorithmic accountability. Very large online platforms (VLOPs) with over 45 million monthly active users in the EU are subject to enhanced obligations.

### EU Competition Law

The European Commission has addressed ride-sharing through competition policy. The Commission's investigation into taxi and ride-hailing markets has focused on anti-competitive regulations that unduly restrict entry while acknowledging legitimate regulatory objectives (safety, consumer protection, urban planning).

## Comparative Analysis

### Regulatory Philosophy

The jurisdictions represent a spectrum of regulatory approaches. The United States prioritizes market entry and innovation, creating a new regulatory category for TNCs. Germany maintains the primacy of its traditional transport licensing system, adapting it incrementally for digital platforms. South Korea takes the most protective approach toward incumbent taxi operators, effectively prohibiting most ride-hailing services.

### Worker Classification

Worker classification represents the most significant ongoing legal issue. The EU Platform Workers Directive establishes a presumption of employment that would fundamentally change the business model of ride-sharing platforms. The US remains divided, with California's Proposition 22 creating a hybrid "independent contractor with benefits" status. South Korea's effective ban on private vehicle ride-hailing means the issue is largely moot domestically.

### Taxi Industry Protection

All jurisdictions to some degree protect incumbent taxi operators, but the intensity varies dramatically. South Korea's Tada Act, driven by a taxi driver's self-immolation and sustained industry protests, represents the strongest protectionist response. Germany's PBefG framework protects taxis through the Rückkehrpflicht and fare regulation. The US has largely allowed market forces to determine the taxi-TNC balance, resulting in significant disruption of the traditional taxi industry.

### Innovation vs. Regulation Balance

The CJEU's classification of Uber as a transport service rather than a technology platform was a defining moment for global ride-sharing regulation, establishing that jurisdictions may apply traditional transport rules to digital platforms. This contrasts with the US approach, which generally classifies TNCs as technology platforms subject to lighter-touch regulation.
