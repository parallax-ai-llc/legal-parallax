## Summary

Voting rights -- the right of citizens to participate in the selection of their government through free and fair elections -- constitute the foundational mechanism of democratic self-governance, yet the conditions of eligibility, methods of voter registration, electoral systems, and protections against disenfranchisement vary substantially across legal systems. South Korea guarantees universal suffrage under Article 24 of the Constitution (대한민국 헌법 제24조) and conducts elections under the Public Official Election Act (공직선거법), with the National Election Commission (중앙선거관리위원회) serving as an independent constitutional body under Article 114; the voting age was lowered from 19 to 18 in 2019, voter registration is automatic based on resident registration, and the Constitutional Court has struck down electoral district malapportionment exceeding a 3:1 population ratio. The United States protects voting rights through the Fifteenth Amendment (race), Nineteenth Amendment (sex), Twenty-Fourth Amendment (poll taxes), and Twenty-Sixth Amendment (age 18), supplemented by the Voting Rights Act of 1965, the most significant federal voting rights statute, which was substantially weakened by the Supreme Court's decisions in Shelby County v. Holder (2013), striking down the preclearance coverage formula, and Brnovich v. Democratic National Committee (2021), narrowing the scope of Section 2 challenges; the U.S. remains distinctive for its decentralized, state-administered election system with no automatic voter registration and ongoing debates over voter identification requirements, gerrymandering, and felon disenfranchisement. Germany guarantees universal, direct, free, equal, and secret suffrage under Article 38 of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz) and employs a mixed-member proportional representation system (personalisierte Verhältniswahl) for Bundestag elections, with the Federal Constitutional Court having intervened to require electoral reform addressing the phenomenon of Überhangmandate (overhang seats) that distorted proportionality; Germany's 2023 electoral reform reduced the Bundestag from an inflated 736 seats to a fixed 630. The European framework protects the right to free elections under Article 3 of Protocol No. 1 to the ECHR, the right to vote in European Parliament elections under the EU Treaties, and the European Court of Human Rights has developed jurisprudence requiring that restrictions on voting rights pursue a legitimate aim and be proportionate, notably in Hirst v. United Kingdom (2005) regarding prisoner voting rights.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Article 24 of the Constitution provides: "All citizens shall have the right to vote under the conditions as prescribed by Act." Article 41 establishes that the National Assembly (국회) is composed of members elected by universal, equal, direct, and secret ballot. Article 67 provides for direct presidential elections. The National Election Commission (중앙선거관리위원회), established under Article 114 of the Constitution, is one of South Korea's independent constitutional organs, with the authority to manage elections, regulate political parties, and adjudicate electoral disputes.

### Public Official Election Act (공직선거법)

The Public Official Election Act consolidates the rules governing presidential, National Assembly, and local elections. Key provisions include:

**Voting Age:** Reduced from 20 to 19 in 2005 and from 19 to 18 in December 2019, effective for the 2020 general election. The reduction to 18 aligned South Korea with the majority of OECD countries.

**Automatic Registration:** All citizens aged 18 and older who are registered in the resident registration system (주민등록) are automatically registered to vote. This eliminates the registration barrier that exists in the United States.

**Election Day:** Election day for major elections is designated as a national holiday, facilitating voter participation. Early voting (사전투표) was introduced in 2013, allowing voters to cast ballots at any early voting station nationwide during a two-day period before election day.

### Electoral System

South Korea uses a parallel mixed system for National Assembly elections. Of 300 seats, 253 are elected from single-member constituencies using first-past-the-post, and 47 are allocated through proportional representation from party lists. The proportional seats are partially linked to constituency results through a "semi-mixed member proportional" formula introduced in 2019.

### Constitutional Court Jurisprudence

**Malapportionment (2014헌마189, 2014):** The Constitutional Court ruled that the population deviation between the largest and smallest electoral districts must not exceed 2:1 (previously 3:1), requiring redistricting before the 2016 general election. This decision significantly reduced urban-rural representational disparities.

**Overseas Voting (2007헌마1083, 2007):** The Court declared the denial of absentee voting rights to overseas Korean citizens unconstitutional, leading to the 2009 amendment enabling overseas voting beginning with the 2012 presidential election.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The Constitution addresses voting rights through multiple amendments:

| Amendment | Year | Provision |
|-----------|------|-----------|
| 15th | 1870 | Prohibits denial of vote based on race, color, or previous condition of servitude |
| 19th | 1920 | Prohibits denial of vote based on sex |
| 24th | 1964 | Prohibits poll taxes in federal elections |
| 26th | 1971 | Sets voting age at 18 for all elections |

### Voting Rights Act of 1965

The VRA, signed by President Lyndon B. Johnson on August 6, 1965, is the most significant piece of voting rights legislation in U.S. history. Key provisions include:

**Section 2:** Prohibits voting practices or procedures that discriminate on the basis of race, color, or membership in a language minority group. Applies nationwide.

**Section 5 (Preclearance):** Required covered jurisdictions (primarily in the South) to obtain federal approval before changing voting practices. Effectively invalidated by the Supreme Court's decision in Shelby County v. Holder, 570 U.S. 529 (2013), which struck down the coverage formula of Section 4(b) as based on outdated data.

**Brnovich v. Democratic National Committee, 594 U.S. 647 (2021):** The Court adopted a restrictive interpretation of Section 2, identifying multiple "guideposts" for evaluating challenges to voting restrictions and making it substantially more difficult to establish vote dilution claims.

### Contemporary Controversies

**Voter Identification:** As of 2026, 36 states require some form of identification to vote, with strict photo ID requirements in 15 states. Proponents argue ID requirements prevent fraud; opponents argue they disproportionately burden minority, elderly, and low-income voters.

**Gerrymandering:** In Rucho v. Common Cause, 588 U.S. 684 (2019), the Supreme Court held that partisan gerrymandering claims present political questions beyond the reach of federal courts. Several states have responded by establishing independent redistricting commissions.

**Felon Disenfranchisement:** Approximately 4.6 million Americans are disenfranchised due to felony convictions, with significant racial disparities. Laws vary dramatically by state, from permanent disenfranchisement (in some states) to no disenfranchisement at all (Maine and Vermont).

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Article 38(1) of the Basic Law provides: "Members of the German Bundestag shall be elected in general, direct, free, equal, and secret elections." These five principles -- universality, directness, freedom, equality, and secrecy -- are considered inviolable and cannot be amended even through constitutional revision (protected by Article 79(3) GG, the "eternity clause").

### Electoral System (Personalisierte Verhältniswahl)

Germany's mixed-member proportional (MMP) system combines single-member constituency elections (Erststimme, first vote) with proportional representation through party lists (Zweitstimme, second vote). The system is designed to produce a Bundestag that accurately reflects the parties' share of the second vote while maintaining the constituency link of the first vote.

### 2023 Electoral Reform

The Federal Electoral Act (Bundeswahlgesetz) was comprehensively reformed in March 2023 to address the problem of Überhangmandate (overhang seats) and Ausgleichsmandate (compensatory seats) that had inflated the Bundestag to 736 members in 2021 (far exceeding the nominal size of 598). The reform establishes a fixed Bundestag size of 630 seats and eliminates overhang mandates by awarding constituency seats to parties only up to their proportional entitlement, with the Federal Constitutional Court upholding the core of the reform in its July 2024 decision while striking down the elimination of the basic mandate clause (Grundmandatsklausel).

### Five-Percent Threshold (Fünf-Prozent-Hürde)

Section 6(3) of the Bundeswahlgesetz requires parties to receive at least five percent of valid second votes (or win at least three direct constituency seats under the basic mandate clause) to enter the Bundestag. The Federal Constitutional Court has upheld this threshold as necessary to prevent fragmentation and ensure a functional parliament, most recently in BVerfGE 82, 322 (1990).

### Prisoner Voting

Unlike many U.S. states, Germany does not automatically disenfranchise prisoners. Under Section 45 of the Criminal Code (StGB), loss of the right to vote may be imposed only as a specific accessory penalty (Nebenstrafe) for certain political offenses such as treason, and the deprivation must be ordered by a court. Ordinary criminal convictions do not affect voting rights.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Protocol 1, Article 3

Article 3 of Protocol No. 1 provides: "The High Contracting Parties undertake to hold free elections at reasonable intervals by secret ballot, under conditions which will ensure the free expression of the opinion of the people in the choice of the legislature." This provision creates both individual rights and institutional obligations.

### Key ECHR Jurisprudence

**Mathieu-Mohin and Clerfayt v. Belgium (Application No. 9267/81, 1987):** The Court established that Article 3 of Protocol 1 guarantees individual subjective rights, including the right to vote and the right to stand for election, not merely institutional obligations on states.

**Hirst v. United Kingdom (No. 2) (Application No. 74025/01, Grand Chamber, 2005):** The Grand Chamber held that the UK's blanket ban on prisoner voting violated Article 3 of Protocol 1, finding that a general, automatic, and indiscriminate restriction on a vitally important Convention right was disproportionate. The UK's protracted non-compliance with this judgment became a major point of contention between the UK and the Council of Europe.

**Scoppola v. Italy (No. 3) (Application No. 126/05, Grand Chamber, 2012):** The Grand Chamber distinguished Hirst, upholding Italy's system of disenfranchisement for prisoners convicted of specific serious offenses carrying sentences of three years or more, finding it was not a blanket ban and was proportionate.

**Oran v. Turkey (Application No. 28881/07, Grand Chamber, 2014):** The Grand Chamber addressed electoral threshold issues, upholding Turkey's ten-percent electoral threshold while emphasizing that the margin of appreciation is narrower where restrictions on voting rights are at issue.

### EU Electoral Framework

EU citizens have the right to vote and stand as candidates in European Parliament elections and municipal elections in their member state of residence, regardless of nationality (Articles 20 and 22 TFEU). The European Parliament is elected by direct universal suffrage under conditions determined by each member state, subject to common principles including proportional representation (for EP elections) and a maximum threshold of five percent.

## Comparative Analysis

### Voter Registration

The most significant structural difference affecting voter participation is the registration system. South Korea and Germany employ automatic registration based on civil registration systems, eliminating registration as a barrier to participation. The United States places the burden of registration on the individual voter, contributing to lower registration and turnout rates. The National Voter Registration Act of 1993 (Motor Voter Act) and state-level automatic voter registration initiatives have partially addressed this gap.

### Electoral Integrity Institutions

South Korea's constitutionally established National Election Commission represents the most robust institutional model. Germany's federal and state electoral offices provide effective administration within a decentralized structure. The United States' highly decentralized system, with over 10,000 electoral jurisdictions, creates inconsistencies in administration and has been the subject of sustained controversy regarding election security and confidence.

### Proportional Representation vs. Plurality

Germany's MMP system and the EU's proportional representation requirement for EP elections produce legislatures that more accurately reflect the popular vote distribution. South Korea's mixed system provides partial proportionality. The United States' FPTP system, combined with the Electoral College for presidential elections, can produce significant deviations between popular vote shares and seat allocation.
