## Summary

Treaty interpretation -- the process by which courts, tribunals, and states determine the meaning and legal effects of international agreements -- lies at the heart of the international legal order and reflects fundamental differences in how jurisdictions receive, apply, and enforce international law. The Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties (VCLT, 1969), which entered into force on January 27, 1980, codifies the authoritative rules of treaty interpretation in Articles 31-33, establishing a general rule based on good faith interpretation in accordance with the ordinary meaning of terms in their context and in light of the treaty's object and purpose (Article 31(1)), supplementary means of interpretation including preparatory work and circumstances of conclusion (Article 32), and rules for treaties authenticated in multiple languages (Article 33). South Korea, as a party to the VCLT since 1977 and operating under a monist constitutional framework established by Article 6(1) of the Constitution of the Republic of Korea (대한민국 헌법), which provides that treaties duly concluded and promulgated have the same force as domestic laws, applies treaty interpretation through its Constitutional Court (헌법재판소) and Supreme Court (대법원), with significant jurisprudence on the status of treaties vis-a-vis domestic statutes, including the landmark decisions concerning the Korea-Japan Agreement on the Settlement of Problems Concerning Property and Claims (1965) and the Comfort Women Agreement (2015). The United States, though a signatory but not a party to the VCLT, applies the Convention's interpretive principles as reflecting customary international law, but operates under a dualist framework in which treaties must be either self-executing or implemented by Congress to have domestic legal effect, as established in Foster v. Neilson, 27 U.S. (2 Pet.) 253 (1829), and Medellin v. Texas, 552 U.S. 491 (2008), with the Supremacy Clause of Article VI making treaties the "supreme Law of the Land" but the last-in-time rule permitting subsequent federal statutes to override earlier treaties as a matter of domestic law. Germany operates under a moderately monist system in which treaties, once approved by the Bundestag and Bundesrat under Article 59(2) of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz) and published in the Federal Law Gazette (Bundesgesetzblatt), have the rank of federal statute but are interpreted in conformity with the VCLT and subject to the principle of Volkerrechtsfreundlichkeit (friendliness toward international law) established by the Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) in the Gorgulu decision (2 BvR 1481/04, 2004). The ECHR and EU legal order represent distinct supranational approaches: the European Court of Human Rights employs dynamic and teleological interpretation of the European Convention on Human Rights as a "living instrument" (Tyrer v. United Kingdom, 1978), while the Court of Justice of the European Union applies autonomous interpretation of EU treaties, with EU law enjoying primacy over national law (Costa v. ENEL, Case 6/64, 1964) and treaties concluded by the EU forming an integral part of the EU legal order under Article 216(2) TFEU.

## South Korea

### Constitutional Framework

Article 6(1) of the Constitution of the Republic of Korea (대한민국 헌법 제6조 제1항) establishes the monist principle: "Treaties duly concluded and promulgated under the Constitution and the generally recognized rules of international law shall have the same effect as the domestic laws of the Republic of Korea." This provision, retained since the 1948 Constitution, means that treaties ratified and promulgated through the Official Gazette (관보) automatically become part of domestic law without the need for separate implementing legislation.

The treaty-making power is vested in the President under Article 73, but treaties involving mutual assistance or mutual security, treaties concerning important international organizations, treaties of friendship, commerce and navigation, treaties relating to sovereignty restrictions, peace treaties, treaties imposing financial obligations on the state, and treaties related to legislative matters require the consent of the National Assembly (국회) under Article 60(1).

### Treaty Interpretation by Korean Courts

The Constitutional Court (헌법재판소) has addressed treaty interpretation in several landmark cases. In the decision concerning the Korea-Japan Claims Agreement of 1965 (헌재 2011. 8. 30. 2006헌마788), the Court held that the government's failure to pursue diplomatic remedies to resolve the dispute over compensation for former comfort women and forced laborers constituted an unconstitutional omission. The Court engaged in detailed interpretation of Article III of the Claims Agreement, which provided for dispute resolution through diplomatic channels and, failing that, arbitration. The Court determined that the agreement's scope was disputed between Korea and Japan and that the government had a constitutional duty to take diplomatic action.

The Supreme Court (대법원) has also addressed treaty hierarchy. In the Supreme Court Decision 2013Da208388 (2018), concerning individual claims by Korean forced labor victims against Japanese corporations, the Court held that the 1965 Claims Agreement did not extinguish individuals' rights to claim damages and that claims based on illegal colonial rule and forced mobilization were not covered by the agreement. This interpretation diverged significantly from Japan's position and illustrated the contentious nature of bilateral treaty interpretation.

### Relationship Between Treaties and Domestic Law

Under the prevailing interpretation, treaties occupy the same hierarchical rank as domestic statutes, meaning that the lex posterior (later in time) and lex specialis (more specific) principles govern conflicts. However, the Constitutional Court has indicated that certain treaties, particularly human rights treaties, may enjoy a quasi-constitutional status. In Decision 2001Hun-Ka25 (2003), the Court referenced the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR) and the Universal Declaration of Human Rights as interpretive aids for constitutional rights provisions.

| Violation | Consequence |
|-----------|------------|

| Violation of treaty obligations by state organs | Administrative liability; potential constitutional complaint |
| Failure to implement treaty obligations domestically | Legislative omission subject to Constitutional Court review |
| Unauthorized treaty conclusion bypassing National Assembly | Constitutionally invalid under Article 60(1) |

## United States

### Constitutional Framework

The Supremacy Clause of Article VI, Clause 2 of the U.S. Constitution provides that "all Treaties made, or which shall be made, under the Authority of the United States, shall be the supreme Law of the Land; and the Judges in every State shall be bound thereby." The Treaty Clause (Article II, Section 2, Clause 2) vests the treaty-making power in the President, with the advice and consent of the Senate by a two-thirds majority. In addition, congressional-executive agreements, approved by simple majorities in both chambers, and sole executive agreements have become increasingly common instruments of international obligation.

### Self-Executing vs. Non-Self-Executing Treaties

The distinction between self-executing and non-self-executing treaties, first articulated by Chief Justice Marshall in Foster v. Neilson, 27 U.S. (2 Pet.) 253 (1829), determines whether a treaty provision may be directly enforced in domestic courts without implementing legislation. In Foster, the Court held that a treaty is "equivalent to an act of the legislature" when it "operates of itself without the aid of any legislative provision," but when "the terms of the stipulation import a contract" requiring legislative action, it is non-self-executing.

The Supreme Court's decision in Medellin v. Texas, 552 U.S. 491 (2008), dramatically reinforced this distinction. The Court held that neither the ICJ's Avena judgment (concerning consular notification rights under the Vienna Convention on Consular Relations) nor the UN Charter's obligation to comply with ICJ judgments was self-executing, and that President George W. Bush's memorandum directing state courts to give effect to the Avena decision exceeded executive authority. Justice Stevens, concurring, and Justice Breyer, dissenting, offered competing visions of treaty enforcement.

### VCLT and Interpretive Methods

Although the United States has not ratified the VCLT, the Supreme Court recognizes its interpretive provisions as reflecting customary international law. In Sanchez-Llamas v. Oregon, 548 U.S. 331 (2006), the Court applied VCLT Article 31 principles while emphasizing that U.S. courts retain the ultimate authority to interpret treaties. The Restatement (Fourth) of the Foreign Relations Law of the United States (2018) Section 306 confirms that courts "give effect to the text of treaties, interpreted in good faith in accordance with their ordinary meaning, in their context, and in light of their object and purpose."

In practice, U.S. courts employ a textual approach tempered by reference to the Senate's ratification history (comparable to travaux preparatoires), executive branch interpretations (which receive "great weight" under Sumitomo Shoji America, Inc. v. Avagliano, 457 U.S. 176 (1982)), and the Charming Betsy canon (Murray v. The Schooner Charming Betsy, 6 U.S. (2 Cranch) 64 (1804)), which requires courts to construe federal statutes to avoid violating international law where possible.

### Last-in-Time Rule

Under Whitney v. Robertson, 120 U.S. 190 (1888), when a treaty and a federal statute conflict, the later-enacted provision prevails as a matter of domestic law. This means Congress may effectively override treaty obligations through subsequent legislation, as occurred with the Breard v. Greene, 523 U.S. 371 (1998), case concerning the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act's restrictions on habeas corpus and the Vienna Convention on Consular Relations.

## Germany

### Constitutional Framework

Article 25 of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz) provides that "the general rules of international law shall be an integral part of federal law" and "shall take precedence over the laws and directly create rights and duties for the inhabitants of the federal territory." Article 59(2) requires that treaties regulating the political relations of the Federation or relating to matters of federal legislation receive legislative approval through a federal statute (Zustimmungsgesetz), thereby transforming the treaty into domestic law with the rank of an ordinary federal statute.

### Volkerrechtsfreundlichkeit

The Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht, BVerfG) has developed the principle of Volkerrechtsfreundlichkeit (friendliness toward international law) as a constitutional interpretive principle. In the landmark Gorgulu decision (BVerfG, 2 BvR 1481/04, October 14, 2004), the Court held that German courts must take the European Convention on Human Rights and the case law of the European Court of Human Rights into account when interpreting domestic law, though the ECHR has the rank of a federal statute rather than constitutional law. The Court stated that the Basic Law is oriented toward the international legal order (Offenheit fur die internationale Zusammenarbeit) and that German courts must interpret domestic law in conformity with international obligations insofar as the applicable methodological principles permit.

### VCLT Application

Germany, as a party to the VCLT since 1987, directly applies its interpretive rules. German courts apply Article 31's general rule with particular emphasis on the systematic and teleological elements. The Bundesgerichtshof (Federal Court of Justice) regularly applies VCLT interpretation rules to bilateral and multilateral treaties, as demonstrated in its jurisprudence on the Convention on the International Sale of Goods (CISG), where German courts have been among the most prolific in applying VCLT-based interpretation to the CISG as an international uniform law instrument.

### Treaty Rank and Constitutional Review

The BVerfG has held that treaties, upon transformation through Article 59(2), occupy the rank of federal statutory law and may therefore be overridden by subsequent legislation (lex posterior). However, the principle of Volkerrechtsfreundlichkeit means that courts must interpret domestic statutes in conformity with international obligations wherever possible. In the Alteigentumer decision (BVerfG, 2 BvR 955/00, 2004), the Court held that the Basic Law does not require that international treaty law always prevails over domestic statutory law but that the legislature should be presumed not to have intended to deviate from treaty obligations without clear indication.

### EU Treaty Interpretation and German Law

As an EU member state, Germany is bound by the CJEU's interpretations of EU treaties. The BVerfG has recognized EU law primacy with certain constitutional limits, as articulated in the Solange decisions (Solange I, BVerfGE 37, 271 (1974); Solange II, BVerfGE 73, 339 (1986)), the Maastricht decision (BVerfGE 89, 155 (1993)), the Lisbon decision (BVerfGE 123, 267 (2009)), and the PSPP decision (BVerfG, 2 BvR 859/15, May 5, 2020), in which the Court declared for the first time that a CJEU judgment was ultra vires.

| Violation | Consequence |
|-----------|------------|
| State organ acting contrary to treaty obligations | State liability under international law; potential ECHR claim |
| Legislative override of treaty without clear intent | Presumption of conformity under Volkerrechtsfreundlichkeit |
| Failure to implement EU treaty obligations | EU infringement proceedings (Art. 258 TFEU); financial penalties |
| Ultra vires application of EU law | Constitutional review by BVerfG (PSPP framework) |

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR: The Living Instrument Doctrine

The European Court of Human Rights has developed a distinctive teleological approach to treaty interpretation, treating the European Convention on Human Rights as a "living instrument which must be interpreted in the light of present-day conditions" (Tyrer v. United Kingdom, Application No. 5856/72, Judgment of April 25, 1978). This dynamic interpretive method permits the evolution of Convention rights beyond what the original drafters contemplated. In Golder v. United Kingdom (Application No. 4451/70, 1975), the Court confirmed its reliance on VCLT Articles 31-33, while emphasizing the Convention's "special character as a treaty for the collective enforcement of human rights."

The autonomous concepts doctrine means that terms used in the Convention (such as "civil rights and obligations" in Article 6) have independent European meanings not determined by any single national legal system (Engel v. Netherlands, 1976). The margin of appreciation doctrine, developed in Handyside v. United Kingdom (Application No. 5493/72, 1976), allows states a degree of discretion in implementing Convention obligations, varying with the nature of the right and the existence or absence of European consensus.

### EU Treaty Interpretation

The Court of Justice of the European Union applies a predominantly teleological approach to interpreting EU founding treaties, emphasizing the effet utile (useful effect) of treaty provisions. In Van Gend en Loos (Case 26/62, 1963), the Court established that the EEC Treaty created a "new legal order of international law" for the benefit of which states limited their sovereign rights, and that treaty provisions could create direct rights for individuals. Costa v. ENEL (Case 6/64, 1964) established the primacy of EU law over conflicting national law, including subsequent national legislation.

### Mixed Agreements and Treaty Interpretation

The EU concludes many international treaties as "mixed agreements" alongside its member states. The CJEU has exclusive jurisdiction to interpret provisions of such agreements falling within EU competence (Ruling 1/78; Opinion 1/94). The Court's approach to interpreting external agreements generally follows VCLT principles but is influenced by the internal logic of EU law, as demonstrated in Brita GmbH v. Hauptzollamt Hamburg-Hafen (Case C-386/08, 2010), where the Court interpreted the EC-Israel Association Agreement in light of the EU's obligation not to recognize unlawful territorial situations.

### International Agreements in EU Law

Under Article 216(2) TFEU, international agreements concluded by the EU "are binding upon the institutions of the Union and on its Member States." Such agreements form an integral part of the EU legal order and rank between primary law (treaties) and secondary law (regulations, directives). The CJEU may review the validity of EU secondary legislation for conformity with international agreements, as established in International Fruit Company (Joined Cases 21-24/72, 1972) for GATT and refined in Intertanko (Case C-308/06, 2008) for UNCLOS.

## Comparative Analysis

### Monism vs. Dualism

The most fundamental divergence among the four jurisdictions concerns the relationship between international and domestic law. South Korea's monist system automatically incorporates ratified treaties into domestic law. Germany's moderately monist system requires legislative transformation but gives treaties direct domestic effect upon approval. The United States' dualist approach requires treaties to be self-executing or implemented by legislation to have domestic legal effect. The EU represents a sui generis system in which EU treaties create an autonomous legal order with direct effect and primacy, while the ECHR creates binding obligations that the ECtHR interprets dynamically.

### Interpretive Methodology

All jurisdictions formally accept the VCLT framework as the starting point for treaty interpretation, but significant divergences exist in emphasis. U.S. courts place heavy weight on text and ratification history (akin to original intent), reflecting the domestic constitutional tradition of statutory interpretation. German courts emphasize systematic and teleological interpretation, consistent with their civilian legal tradition. The ECtHR's living instrument doctrine represents the most dynamic approach, permitting evolutionary interpretation detached from original intent. South Korean courts, while applying VCLT principles, have shown willingness to adopt expansive interpretations in cases involving historical justice, as demonstrated in the forced labor decisions.

### Self-Execution and Direct Effect

The self-executing treaty doctrine is peculiarly American, arising from the Constitution's dual character as both a domestic law instrument and the foundation for international engagement. Neither South Korea nor Germany employs the concept in the same way. The EU's direct effect doctrine, though functionally similar, derives from a different theoretical basis -- the autonomous nature of the EU legal order. The practical consequence is that individuals in the United States face the greatest obstacles in invoking treaty rights in domestic courts.

### Treaty Override

All jurisdictions except the EU permit some form of legislative override of treaty obligations as a matter of domestic law. The U.S. last-in-time rule is the most explicit. Germany's Volkerrechtsfreundlichkeit principle creates a strong presumption against override but does not absolutely prohibit it. South Korea's lex posterior principle similarly permits override. EU law's primacy doctrine categorically prohibits member states from overriding EU treaty obligations through domestic legislation.
