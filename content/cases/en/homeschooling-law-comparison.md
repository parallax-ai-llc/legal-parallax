## Summary

Homeschooling -- the practice of educating children at home or in non-institutional settings rather than in public or private schools, typically directed by parents or private tutors -- represents one of the most polarizing intersections of parental rights, state educational authority, and children's welfare, with legal frameworks ranging from near-total freedom to criminal prohibition. South Korea occupies a legally ambiguous position on homeschooling, with no explicit statutory authorization or prohibition; the Elementary and Secondary Education Act (초·중등교육법) mandates school enrollment for compulsory education years, but enforcement mechanisms are weak and a small but growing homeschooling community (estimated at 2,000-3,000 families) operates in a legal gray zone, with advocacy groups such as the Korean Homeschool Association (한국홈스쿨링연합) seeking formal recognition and regulatory accommodation. The United States is the world's most permissive major jurisdiction for homeschooling, with legal recognition in all 50 states since 1993 (Mississippi being the last state to legalize), approximately 3.3 million homeschooled children as of the 2023-2024 school year (5.6% of the school-age population), and a regulatory spectrum ranging from states with no notification requirements (Texas, Idaho, Alaska) to states requiring curriculum approval, standardized testing, and professional evaluation (New York, Pennsylvania); the legal foundation rests on the Supreme Court's recognition of parental educational liberty in Pierce v. Society of Sisters (1925) and Meyer v. Nebraska (1923), reinforced by the Free Exercise Clause protection in Wisconsin v. Yoder (1972). Germany effectively prohibits homeschooling through its Schulbesuchspflicht (compulsory school attendance) requirement, enforced through escalating administrative fines, criminal penalties, and family court intervention, with the Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) having repeatedly upheld the prohibition on the grounds that compulsory school attendance serves the state's legitimate interest in social integration, democratic citizenship, and the prevention of parallel societies; Germany's position makes it one of the most restrictive jurisdictions in Europe alongside Sweden. The European Court of Human Rights has addressed homeschooling primarily through Konrad v. Germany (2006, inadmissibility) and Wunderlich v. Germany (2019), holding that compulsory school attendance requirements do not violate Article 2 of Protocol No. 1 (right to education) or Article 8 (right to respect for family life), while leaving open the question of whether a complete prohibition of homeschooling in all circumstances could violate Convention rights.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's legal framework for homeschooling is characterized by ambiguity. The Elementary and Secondary Education Act (초·중등교육법) requires parents and guardians to enroll children in school during compulsory education years (ages 6-15), and Article 68 authorizes fines of up to 1 million KRW for violations. However, the Act does not explicitly address homeschooling, and enforcement against homeschooling families has been minimal to non-existent.

### Constitutional Context

The Constitution provides competing bases for both state educational authority and parental rights:

**Article 31(1):** "All citizens have the right to receive education according to their ability." This right is interpreted as imposing an obligation on the state to provide education and on parents to ensure children receive it.

**Article 31(2):** "All citizens who have children to protect have the duty of providing at least an elementary education and such other education as may be prescribed by law."

**Article 36(1):** Marriage and family life are protected, which includes parental authority over child-rearing decisions.

### Homeschooling Community

Despite the legal ambiguity, a small homeschooling community has developed in South Korea:

| Characteristic | Description |
|---------------|-------------|
| Estimated families | 2,000-3,000 (as of 2024) |
| Primary motivations | Religious conviction; dissatisfaction with examination-driven system; special needs; international mobility |
| Organizational support | Korean Homeschool Association (한국홈스쿨링연합); Christian homeschool networks |
| Legal status | Technically in violation of enrollment obligation; no reported prosecutions |
| Re-entry challenges | Homeschooled children may sit for the middle school equivalency exam (검정고시) to obtain recognized credentials |

### 검정고시 (Qualification Examination System)

South Korea's 검정고시 system provides an alternative pathway for persons who did not complete formal schooling. Qualification examinations are available at elementary, middle school, high school, and university entrance levels. Homeschooled students can sit for these examinations to obtain recognized educational credentials, effectively providing a back-door mechanism for homeschooling despite the absence of formal legal authorization.

### Policy Debate

The South Korean homeschooling debate reflects broader tensions in the education system:

- **Advocates** argue that homeschooling provides relief from the extreme academic pressure of the Korean education system, enables religious or philosophical education aligned with family values, and supports families with internationally mobile careers
- **Opponents** emphasize the importance of socialization, concern about potential educational neglect, and the risk of undermining the universal compulsory education system that has been central to Korea's development

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States provides the world's most permissive and well-developed legal framework for homeschooling, with recognition in all 50 states and the District of Columbia.

### Historical Development

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1925 | Pierce v. Society of Sisters: right to choose private education |
| 1972 | Wisconsin v. Yoder: Amish exemption from compulsory attendance |
| 1980s | Modern homeschooling movement emerges; legal battles in multiple states |
| 1983 | Home School Legal Defense Association (HSLDA) founded |
| 1985-1993 | Progressive legalization across all states |
| 1993 | Mississippi becomes last state to legalize homeschooling |
| 2008 | California Court of Appeal, In re Rachel L.: initially requires teaching credential, reversed |
| 2020 | COVID-19 pandemic drives massive surge in homeschooling |

### Regulatory Spectrum

The Home School Legal Defense Association classifies states into four regulatory categories:

**No Notification Required (11 states):**
Idaho, Texas, Alaska, Connecticut, Indiana, Michigan, Missouri, New Jersey, Oklahoma, Illinois, Iowa. Parents need not notify the state that they are homeschooling.

**Notification Only (15 states):**
Parents must notify the school district or state education agency of their intent to homeschool. Examples: Alabama, Arizona, California (as a private school filing), Florida, Kentucky.

**Notification with Assessment (17 states):**
Parents must provide notification and submit to some form of educational assessment (standardized testing, portfolio review, or professional evaluation). Examples: Colorado, Oregon, South Carolina, Virginia, Washington.

**High Regulation (7 states + DC):**
Parents must provide notification, submit to assessment, and meet additional requirements such as curriculum approval, teacher qualifications, or home visits. Examples: New York, Pennsylvania, Massachusetts, Rhode Island, Vermont.

### Legal Challenges and Issues

**Parental Rights vs. Child Welfare:** The tension between parental homeschooling rights and child welfare concerns has generated significant debate, particularly following cases of severe abuse discovered in homeschooling families (e.g., the Turpin case in California, 2018, where 13 children were held captive by parents who claimed to be homeschooling). These cases have prompted legislative proposals for enhanced oversight in several states.

**Special Education:** Homeschooled children may be eligible for some public school special education services under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), but eligibility varies by state and district. No federal requirement exists for school districts to provide a complete free appropriate public education (FAPE) to voluntarily homeschooled children.

**Higher Education Access:** Most colleges and universities have developed admissions policies for homeschooled applicants, typically accepting SAT/ACT scores, portfolios, and homeschool transcripts. The Higher Education Opportunity Act of 2008 requires institutions to treat homeschool credentials comparably to public and private school credentials for federal financial aid purposes.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany effectively prohibits homeschooling through its Schulbesuchspflicht (compulsory school attendance) requirement, distinguishing it from most other democracies that require only Bildungspflicht (compulsory education).

### Constitutional Basis

**Grundgesetz Article 7(1):** "The entire school system shall be under the supervision of the state." The Federal Constitutional Court interprets this as establishing a comprehensive state educational mandate that encompasses not only content but the institutional form of education delivery.

**Article 6(2) GG:** "The care and upbringing of children is the natural right of parents and a duty primarily incumbent upon them. The state shall watch over them in the performance of this duty." The Court has held that this parental right does not encompass a right to homeschool, as the state's supervisory interest under Article 7 takes precedence in the context of compulsory education.

### Federal Constitutional Court Jurisprudence

**BVerfG, 1 BvR 436/03 (May 29, 2006):** The Court declined to accept a constitutional complaint by Christian parents seeking to homeschool, holding:
- Compulsory school attendance serves the legitimate aims of educating children to be responsible citizens and integrating minorities into society
- The state's interest in preventing "parallel societies" (Parallelgesellschaften) justifies the restriction on parental educational freedom
- Schools provide not only academic education but essential social experiences (encounters with different worldviews, learning to engage with dissent) that cannot be replicated at home
- The interference with parental rights is proportionate given the important public interests served

**BVerfG, 2 BvR 1693/04 (2007):** Affirmed the constitutionality of criminal penalties for compulsory attendance violations, including fines imposed on parents who refused to send their children to school on religious grounds.

### Enforcement in Practice

| State (Land) | Enforcement Approach |
|--------------|---------------------|
| Baden-Württemberg | Administrative fines (500-1,500 EUR per child per semester); criminal referral for persistent violations |
| Bayern (Bavaria) | Fines; Schulzwang (physical enforcement via police escort); family court referral |
| Nordrhein-Westfalen | Progressive fines; potential custody measures |
| Hessen | Fines up to 2,500 EUR; criminal prosecution (fine or up to 6 months imprisonment) |
| Sachsen | Administrative fines; family court intervention |

### Notable Cases

**Romeike Family (2008):** Evangelical Christian family from Baden-Württemberg who faced fines exceeding 7,000 EUR and threats of criminal prosecution and custody proceedings. They fled to the United States in 2008, where an immigration judge granted asylum in 2010 (Romeike v. Holder). The Board of Immigration Appeals reversed in 2013, finding Germany's enforcement was a neutral law of general applicability that did not constitute persecution. The family received indefinite deferred action status.

**Wunderlich Family (2013):** Authorities removed four children from the Wunderlich family after years of non-compliance with compulsory attendance. The children were placed in temporary state care before being returned under conditions requiring school attendance. The family's application to the ECHR resulted in the Wunderlich v. Germany (2019) judgment.

### Exceptions

Germany provides extremely narrow exceptions to compulsory school attendance:

- **Illness:** Children too ill to attend school may receive Hausunterricht (home instruction) provided by the school
- **Diplomatic families:** Children of diplomatic personnel may be exempt under international agreements
- **Temporary absence:** Families temporarily abroad may be exempt from attendance during the absence
- **Disability:** Children with severe disabilities may receive instruction at home if no appropriate school placement is available, but this is provided by the state, not parents

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

**Konrad v. Germany (Application No. 35504/03, Inadmissibility Decision of September 11, 2006):** The Court declared inadmissible the complaint of German parents who sought to homeschool their children, finding that compulsory school attendance did not violate Article 2 of Protocol No. 1 or Article 8 ECHR. Key reasoning:

- Article 2 of Protocol No. 1 does not guarantee a right to be educated at home
- The state has a legitimate interest in ensuring education through school attendance
- The social integration aim served by compulsory attendance is a valid basis for restricting parental educational freedom
- The restriction is proportionate given the state's important interest in a democratic and pluralistic society

**Wunderlich v. Germany (Application No. 18925/15, Judgment of January 10, 2019):** The Court found no violation of Article 8 in the temporary removal of four children from a family that had refused to comply with compulsory attendance laws for several years. The Court held:

- The removal was a serious interference with family life
- However, it was justified by the children's need for education and the parents' persistent defiance of court orders
- The authorities' response was proportionate, particularly as the children were returned relatively quickly and the parents were given an opportunity to comply
- The margin of appreciation on educational matters is wide

**Campbell and Cosans v. United Kingdom (Application No. 7511/76, Judgment of February 25, 1982):** While addressing corporal punishment, the Court established the principle that the state must respect parents' philosophical convictions in education, but this does not extend to exemption from the compulsory education system itself.

### UN Committee on the Rights of the Child

The CRC Committee has not taken a definitive position on homeschooling per se but has emphasized:
- The right of all children to education (Article 28 CRC)
- The aims of education including socialization and preparation for democratic life (Article 29 CRC)
- The state's obligation to ensure the quality of education regardless of where it occurs
- The importance of monitoring home education where it is permitted to prevent educational neglect

### EU Framework

Education remains primarily a member state competence. The EU has no position on homeschooling. The EU Charter of Fundamental Rights (Article 14) guarantees the right to education and the freedom to found educational establishments, and recognizes the right of parents to ensure education in conformity with their convictions, but does not address the specific question of home-based instruction.

## Comparative Analysis

### Philosophical Divergence

The homeschooling debate reveals a fundamental philosophical divergence about the purpose of education. Germany views schooling as a state function that serves social cohesion and democratic integration -- education cannot be separated from the institutional setting of the school. The United States views education as primarily a parental responsibility that the state supports but cannot monopolize -- parents retain the fundamental liberty to direct their children's education. South Korea's ambiguous position reflects a system where near-universal school attendance is achieved through cultural consensus rather than strict enforcement, making the legal question largely academic.

### Rights Framework

The jurisdictions assign different priorities to competing rights. Germany prioritizes the state's educational mandate (Article 7 GG) over parental educational freedom (Article 6 GG). The United States prioritizes parental liberty (Pierce, Yoder) over state regulatory authority. The ECHR assigns a wide margin of appreciation to states, accepting both permissive and restrictive approaches as compatible with Convention rights.

### Quality Control

The most significant practical challenge is ensuring educational quality in homeschooling. The United States demonstrates the widest variation, with some states requiring no assessment at all and others demanding annual standardized testing. Research on homeschooling outcomes is limited and methodologically contested, with homeschooled students generally performing at or above public school averages on standardized tests, though selection bias (motivated parents self-selecting into homeschooling) complicates interpretation. Germany avoids the quality control problem entirely by prohibiting the practice.

### Child Welfare

The intersection of homeschooling and child welfare presents unique challenges. The isolation of homeschooled children from mandatory reporters (teachers, school counselors) creates potential monitoring gaps. High-profile abuse cases in homeschooling families have prompted calls for enhanced oversight, though homeschooling advocates argue that abuse occurs across all educational settings and that additional regulation disproportionately burdens law-abiding families.
