## Summary

Marriage law across the Global South reveals an extraordinary diversity of legal traditions that resist reduction to a single "developing world" model. In China, the Marriage Law of 1950 was the first major piece of legislation enacted by the People's Republic, abolishing the feudal marriage system and establishing free choice, monogamy, and gender equality as foundational principles; the current Marriage Law (2001 revision) and the Civil Code (2021, Book Five: Marriage and Family) maintain state registration as the sole path to legal recognition, rejecting religious or customary ceremonies as legally insufficient. India operates a pluralistic marriage system in which the Hindu Marriage Act 1955, the Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act 1937, the Indian Christian Marriage Act 1872, the Parsi Marriage and Divorce Act 1936, and the Special Marriage Act 1954 create parallel tracks governed by religious identity, with the Supreme Court repeatedly grappling with the tension between personal law autonomy and constitutional guarantees of equality under Articles 14-15. In Nigeria and South Africa, dual systems overlay statutory marriage acts with customary marriage recognition -- Nigeria's Marriage Act (Cap M6, LFN 2004) governs statutory marriages while the Matrimonial Causes Act 1970 addresses dissolution, and customary marriages remain governed by ethnic group-specific rules recognized under the Constitution; South Africa's Recognition of Customary Marriages Act 120 of 1998 brought customary unions, including potentially polygynous unions, under statutory protection following the Constitutional Court's engagement with section 9 equality rights. In Latin America, Brazil's recognition of the "stable union" (união estável) as a constitutionally protected family unit under Article 226(3) of the 1988 Constitution, expanded by the Supreme Federal Tribunal (STF) in ADPF 132 and ADI 4277 (2011) to include same-sex couples, represents a distinctive departure from formalist marriage models; Mexico's gradual state-by-state legalization of same-sex marriage culminated in a 2022 Supreme Court (SCJN) ruling that all states must comply; Colombia's Constitutional Court recognized same-sex marriage in Sentencia SU-214/16. In Southeast Asia, Indonesia's Marriage Law No. 1 of 1974 requires that marriages be performed according to the religious law of the parties, creating a system where inter-religious marriage is legally fraught -- the Constitutional Court's Decision No. 68/PUU-XII/2014 declined to strike down the religious requirement, while the Supreme Court's Circular Letter (SEMA) No. 2 of 2023 attempted to clarify registration pathways; the Philippines, governed by the Family Code of 1987 (Executive Order No. 209), remains the only country alongside Vatican City where divorce is unavailable for the general population, with marriage dissolution limited to annulment and recognition of foreign divorces for Filipino Muslims under the Code of Muslim Personal Laws (Presidential Decree No. 1083).

## China

### Historical Evolution

Marriage reform was a revolutionary priority in China. The Marriage Law of 1950 (中华人民共和国婚姻法) was promulgated on May 1, 1950, making it one of the first laws of the newly established People's Republic. Article 1 proclaimed the abolition of the "feudal marriage system" (封建主义婚姻制度), which encompassed arranged marriages, bride-price (彩礼), child betrothal, and concubinage. The law established the "New Democratic marriage system" based on free choice of partners, monogamy, and equal rights for both sexes.

The 1950 law was revised in 1980 and again in 2001. The 2001 revision introduced provisions on domestic violence (家庭暴力), prohibited cohabitation of a married person with a third party (Article 3), and established a fault-based compensation system for divorce (Article 46). The law set minimum marriage ages at 22 for men and 20 for women -- the highest mandatory minimums in the world -- reflecting population control policies.

### Civil Code Era (2021-Present)

On January 1, 2021, the Civil Code of the People's Republic of China (民法典) took effect, incorporating marriage and family law as Book Five (婚姻家庭编, Articles 1040-1118). Key provisions include:

- **Article 1041:** Marriage freedom, monogamy, and equality between men and women are fundamental principles. Arranged marriages, mercenary marriages, and bigamy are prohibited.
- **Article 1046:** Marriage must be based on the complete willingness of both parties; neither party may compel the other, and no third party may interfere.
- **Article 1047:** Marriage age requirements remain 22 (male) and 20 (female).
- **Article 1049:** Parties must appear in person at the marriage registration authority to apply for registration. Marriages meeting legal conditions are registered and a marriage certificate is issued; marriage is established upon registration.
- **Article 1051:** Marriages are void (无效) if contracted by persons who lack capacity, are within prohibited degrees of kinship, or are already married.

The bride-price (彩礼) remains culturally pervasive despite legal prohibition of "mercenary marriage." In 2024, the Supreme People's Court issued the "Provisions on Several Issues Concerning the Application of Law in the Trial of Cases Involving Disputes over Bride Price" (关于审理涉彩礼纠纷案件适用法律若干问题的规定), effective February 1, 2024, establishing clearer rules for bride-price return upon dissolution.

### Marriage Registration and Practical Issues

Marriage registration is administered by the Civil Affairs Bureau (民政局) at the county or district level under the Marriage Registration Regulation (婚姻登记条例, State Council Decree No. 387, 2003). Both parties must present household registration booklets (户口簿), identification cards, and declarations of unmarried status. Foreign nationals must present passport and certificate of marriageability.

A significant practical issue involves the hukou (户籍) system: registration typically occurs at the location of one party's household registration, creating barriers for internal migrants. The State Council announced in 2023 that marriage registration would be expanded to allow registration at any location nationwide on a trial basis, moving away from the hukou-linked restriction.

China does not recognize common-law marriage. Relationships established before February 1, 1994 that met the "factual marriage" (事实婚姻) criteria were grandfathered, but no new factual marriages are recognized after that date. Cohabitation without registration creates no marital rights.

## India

### The Pluralistic Marriage System

India's marriage law is structured around religious personal law, a legacy of colonial administration that was constitutionally preserved. The Indian Constitution under Article 44 directs the state to endeavor to secure a Uniform Civil Code (UCC), but this Directive Principle remains unimplemented at the national level as of 2026 -- though Uttarakhand enacted the Uniform Civil Code, Uttarakhand, 2024 (Act No. 2 of 2024), becoming the first state to do so.

**Hindu Marriage Act, 1955 (HMA):** Governs marriages among Hindus, Buddhists, Jains, and Sikhs. Key provisions include:
- Section 5: Conditions for valid Hindu marriage -- neither party may have a living spouse, both must be of sound mind, the groom must be 21 and the bride 18, and parties must not be within prohibited degrees of relationship or sapinda relationship.
- Section 7: Ceremonies -- a Hindu marriage may be solemnized in accordance with the customary rites of either party; where the ceremony includes saptapadi (seven steps around the sacred fire), the marriage becomes complete upon the seventh step.
- Section 8: Registration -- marriages may be registered under state-specific rules; registration is not a condition of validity but serves as evidence.

**Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act, 1937:** Muslim marriages (nikah) are governed by uncodified personal law as recognized by this Act. A Muslim marriage is a civil contract requiring offer (ijab) and acceptance (qubul) in the presence of witnesses, and mahr (dower) from the husband to the wife. Polygyny is permitted for Muslim men (up to four wives), though subject to growing judicial scrutiny.

**Indian Christian Marriage Act, 1872:** Governs marriages where one or both parties are Christian. Requires solemnization by a licensed minister or marriage registrar with at least two witnesses.

**Special Marriage Act, 1954 (SMA):** Provides a secular marriage option available to any Indian citizen regardless of religion. Requires a 30-day notice period under Section 5, during which objections may be filed. The notice requirement has been criticized as effectively creating an anti-conversion and anti-inter-caste marriage surveillance mechanism -- the Allahabad High Court in Safiya Sultana v. State of U.P. (2021) and the Supreme Court in Shafin Jahan v. Asokan K.M. (2018) addressed related issues of interfaith marriage autonomy.

### Recent Developments

The Supreme Court of India in **Shilpa Sailesh v. Varun Sreenivasan (2023)** exercised its extraordinary power under Article 142 to directly grant divorce by mutual consent, bypassing the mandatory 6-month cooling-off period under Section 13B(2) HMA, marking an important liberalization.

The **Prohibition of Child Marriage (Amendment) Act, 2021** proposed raising the minimum marriage age for women from 18 to 21 (matching men), following the Jaya Jaitly Committee recommendation. The bill was referred to a parliamentary standing committee and as of 2026 awaits passage.

The Uttarakhand UCC (2024) establishes a uniform framework for marriage registration, prescribes monogamy for all communities, and standardizes age and consent requirements -- serving as a potential model or cautionary example for national implementation.

## Africa

### Nigeria: Triple Legal System

Nigeria operates a tripartite legal system for marriage: statutory law (based on English common law), customary law (varying among ethnic groups), and Islamic law (applicable in northern states).

**Statutory Marriage:** The Marriage Act (Cap M6, Laws of the Federation of Nigeria 2004) governs monogamous marriages. Requirements include notice to the registrar (28 days), publication of banns, consent of both parties, minimum ages of 21 (male) and 16 (female, with parental consent), and celebration before a recognized minister or registrar. A statutory marriage precludes subsequent customary or Islamic marriages during its subsistence (section 35, Marriage Act; section 370, Criminal Code).

**Customary Marriage:** Validity is determined by the customs of the parties' ethnic groups. Common requirements across many groups include: consent of both families, payment of bride-price, performance of traditional ceremonies, and capacity (not being under a subsisting statutory marriage). Customary marriages are potentially polygamous. The Nigerian courts have recognized customary marriage validity in numerous decisions including Okonkwo v. Okagbue [1994] 9 NWLR (Pt. 368) 301 and Jadesimi v. Okotie-Eboh [1986] 1 NWLR (Pt. 16) 264.

**Islamic Marriage:** In the twelve northern states that have adopted Sharia penal codes since 1999, Islamic marriage law applies to Muslims. The marriage contract (nikah) requires consent of both parties, presence of witnesses (two male or one male and two female), a marriage guardian (wali) for the bride, and specification of mahr.

A critical issue is the **Child Rights Act 2003**, which sets the minimum marriage age at 18 for both sexes. However, only 26 of 36 states have domesticated this act -- many northern states have refused, citing incompatibility with Islamic law, which uses puberty rather than a fixed age as the criterion.

### South Africa: Post-Apartheid Marriage Reform

South Africa's Constitution (1996) guarantees equality (section 9), human dignity (section 10), and freedom of religion (section 15), providing the framework for marriage law reform.

**Marriage Act 25 of 1961:** Governs civil marriages. Post-apartheid amendments removed racial restrictions. The Act requires both parties to be 18 or older (as amended by the Marriage Amendment Act, 2022, which removed the provision allowing marriage at 15 with parental or ministerial consent).

**Recognition of Customary Marriages Act 120 of 1998 (RCMA):** Enacted to address the historical non-recognition of African customary marriages. Requirements for validity (section 3): both parties must be above 18, both must consent, the marriage must be negotiated and entered into in accordance with customary law. Registration is required (section 4) but failure to register does not invalidate the marriage.

The Constitutional Court addressed polygynous customary marriages in **Gumede v. President of the Republic of South Africa [2009] ZACC 1**, striking down KwaZulu-Natal and Natal legislation that gave husbands exclusive ownership and control of customary marriage property, finding it violated section 9 equality rights. The court ordered that customary marriages be subject to the community of property default regime.

**Civil Union Act 17 of 2006:** Enacted following the Constitutional Court's decision in **Minister of Home Affairs v. Fourie [2005] ZACC 19**, which held that the common-law definition of marriage as between a man and a woman violated the equality clause. South Africa became the first African country -- and fifth globally -- to legalize same-sex marriage.

The **Recognition of Muslim Marriages Bill**, debated for over two decades, has not been enacted. The Western Cape High Court in **Women's Legal Centre Trust v. President of the Republic of South Africa [2018] ZAWCHC 109** declared the non-recognition of Muslim marriages unconstitutional, but the Supreme Court of Appeal in 2022 held the declaration premature, deferring to the legislature.

## Latin America

### Brazil: Constitutional Family Law

Brazil's 1988 Constitution transformed family law by broadening the concept of "family" beyond formal marriage. Article 226 recognizes three forms of family: marriage (§1-2), stable union (§3), and single-parent families (§4). This pluralistic approach distinguishes Brazilian law from formalist traditions.

**Marriage (Casamento):** Regulated by the Civil Code (Lei No. 10.406/2002, Articles 1511-1582). Key features:
- Article 1514: Marriage is established through the free consent of the parties before a civil authority.
- Article 1517: Both parties must be 18 or older. The 2019 amendment (Lei No. 13.811/2019) eliminated the possibility of marriage under 16, which had previously been allowed in cases of pregnancy -- a significant anti-child marriage reform.
- Article 1521: Impediments include existing marriage, affinity within prohibited degrees, and having committed homicide or attempted homicide against the former spouse.
- Property regime defaults to partial community of property (comunhão parcial de bens) under Article 1640.

**Stable Union (União Estável):** Recognized as a family entity when a continuous, public, and lasting relationship is established with the objective of constituting a family (Article 1723, Civil Code). No formal registration is required, though registration at a notary office (cartório) provides evidentiary certainty. The STF in **ADPF 132 and ADI 4277 (2011)** extended stable union recognition to same-sex couples, interpreting Article 1723 in light of constitutional principles of equality and human dignity. The National Council of Justice (CNJ) issued **Resolution 175 of 2013**, prohibiting notary offices and civil registrars from refusing to perform or convert same-sex stable unions and marriages.

### Mexico: Federalized Marriage Law

Marriage in Mexico is governed by state civil codes, creating significant variation. The Federal Civil Code and the Mexico City Civil Code (reformed in 2009) were among the first to permit same-sex marriage. The SCJN in **Jurisprudencia 43/2015** (Acción de Inconstitucionalidad 28/2015) held that state laws defining marriage exclusively as between a man and a woman are unconstitutional, effectively requiring all states to permit same-sex marriage. By 2022, all 32 states had complied.

Indigenous communities' marriage practices are recognized under Article 2 of the Mexican Constitution, which guarantees the right of indigenous peoples to apply their own normative systems (usos y costumbres) in internal matters, including family organization.

### Colombia: Judicial Constitutionalism

Colombia's Constitutional Court (Corte Constitucional) has been a driving force in marriage law reform. In **Sentencia C-577/2011**, the Court recognized that same-sex couples constituted a family under the Constitution and directed Congress to legislate by June 2013 -- when Congress failed to act, the Court in **Sentencia SU-214/2016** directly ordered civil registrars to perform same-sex marriages.

The Colombian Constitution (1991) Article 42 defines the family as the fundamental unit of society, formed "by natural or legal bonds, by the free decision of a man and a woman to enter into marriage, or by the responsible will to form one." The Constitutional Court interpreted "responsible will" (voluntad responsable) to encompass diverse family structures including same-sex partnerships.

## Southeast Asia

### Indonesia: The Religious Requirement

Indonesia's Marriage Law No. 1 of 1974 (Undang-Undang Perkawinan) is the primary statute governing marriage. The most consequential provision is **Article 2(1):** "A marriage is valid if it is performed according to the law of the respective religion and belief of the parties." This means the state does not recognize purely civil marriage -- every marriage must conform to religious requirements.

For the six officially recognized religions (Islam, Protestantism, Catholicism, Hinduism, Buddhism, Confucianism), this creates parallel marriage systems administered by different authorities:
- **Muslim marriages:** Registered at the Office of Religious Affairs (Kantor Urusan Agama, KUA) under the Ministry of Religious Affairs. Governed additionally by the Compilation of Islamic Law (Kompilasi Hukum Islam, KHI, Presidential Instruction No. 1/1991).
- **Non-Muslim marriages:** Registered at the Civil Registration Office (Kantor Catatan Sipil) under the Ministry of Home Affairs.

**Inter-Religious Marriage:** Article 2(1) creates a near-prohibition on inter-religious marriage, as no recognized religious authority will typically solemnize a marriage between adherents of different faiths. The Constitutional Court in **Decision No. 68/PUU-XII/2014** declined to declare Article 2(1) unconstitutional, holding that the religious marriage requirement did not violate the Constitution (UUD 1945) and that marriage was not solely a civil matter but carried sacred religious dimensions. Couples seeking inter-religious marriage have historically resorted to: (a) one party converting (at least formally), (b) marrying abroad and registering the foreign marriage in Indonesia, or (c) seeking a court determination under Article 35(a) of Law No. 23/2006 on Civil Administration.

The **Supreme Court Circular Letter (SEMA) No. 2 of 2023** instructed lower courts to process inter-religious marriage registrations through court determination (penetapan pengadilan), but this was met with significant controversy and was informally suspended in some jurisdictions.

**Marriage Age Reform:** Law No. 16 of 2019 amended Article 7 of the Marriage Law to raise the minimum marriage age for women from 16 to 19 (matching men), following Constitutional Court Decision No. 22/PUU-XV/2017, which declared the differential age provision discriminatory. However, the amendment retained the possibility of court dispensation (dispensasi kawin) for marriages below 19, and data from the Religious Courts shows that dispensation requests surged from approximately 23,700 in 2019 to over 65,000 in 2022.

### Philippines: No Divorce Jurisdiction

The Philippines is the only sovereign nation other than Vatican City where divorce is not available to the general population. The Family Code of the Philippines (Executive Order No. 209, 1987) governs marriage for the non-Muslim population.

**Valid Marriage Requirements (Article 2-4):**
- Legal capacity (18 years or older; parental consent required for 18-21; parental advice required for 21-25)
- Consent freely given before a solemnizing officer
- Marriage license (issued after 10-day posting of notice)
- Marriage ceremony with at least two witnesses

**Marriage Dissolution Options:**
- **Annulment (Articles 35-54):** Grounds include lack of parental consent (voidable), fraud, force, impotence, sexually transmissible disease, and psychological incapacity (Article 36). The Supreme Court in **Republic v. Molina (G.R. No. 108763, 1997)** established stringent guidelines for psychological incapacity that effectively required expert psychiatric testimony and proof of a pre-existing, permanent, and grave condition -- though the Court substantially relaxed these requirements in **Tan-Andal v. Andal (G.R. No. 196359, 2021)**.
- **Legal Separation (Articles 55-67):** Allows separation of property and persons but does not dissolve the marriage bond -- neither party may remarry.
- **Recognition of Foreign Divorce (Article 26, paragraph 2):** Where a Filipino is married to a foreign national who obtains a valid divorce abroad, the Filipino spouse may have the foreign divorce recognized to restore their capacity to remarry. The Supreme Court in **Republic v. Manalo (G.R. No. 221029, 2018)** extended this to Filipinos who initiate the foreign divorce.

**Muslim Filipinos:** The Code of Muslim Personal Laws (Presidential Decree No. 1083, 1977) allows divorce through talaq (husband-initiated) and faskh (judicial dissolution) for Muslims, administered by Sharia Circuit and District Courts.

**Divorce Legislation Efforts:** House Bill No. 1979 (Absolute Divorce Act) was approved by the House of Representatives on its third reading in 2024, but the Senate has not acted on a companion bill. President Marcos Jr. has expressed openness but not active support.

## Comparative Analysis

### Registration vs. Ceremony as Source of Validity

A fundamental axis of comparison is whether legal validity derives from state registration or religious/customary ceremony. China represents the pure registration model -- no ceremony, religious or otherwise, creates a legally valid marriage without state registration. India and Indonesia represent the religious ceremony model, where religious rites are constitutive of validity and registration is secondary. Nigeria and South Africa maintain dual tracks. Brazil and Mexico employ the civil registration model for formal marriage but recognize informal unions (stable union / concubinato) that arise from factual circumstances.

### Pluralism and Personal Law

India and Indonesia share a structural similarity: both allocate family law jurisdiction based on religious identity. However, the mechanisms differ -- India uses separate statutory codes (HMA, CMPA, etc.) while Indonesia routes all marriages through a single statute (Marriage Law 1974) that delegates substantive requirements to religious authorities. Nigeria adds a third tier with ethnically-specific customary law.

### Age of Marriage

The Global South has seen a marked trend toward equalizing and raising minimum marriage ages. Indonesia's 2019 amendment, India's proposed (but stalled) amendment, Nigeria's Child Rights Act, and Brazil's 2019 elimination of under-16 marriage all reflect this trajectory. However, enforcement remains inconsistent -- Indonesia's dispensation system and Nigeria's incomplete domestication of the Child Rights Act demonstrate the gap between statutory reform and practice.

### Same-Sex Marriage

The Global South presents a fragmented picture. South Africa (2006) and Brazil (2013) were early global leaders in same-sex marriage recognition, while India, Indonesia, Nigeria, and the Philippines maintain non-recognition or active prohibition. The judicial route was decisive in South Africa, Brazil, Mexico, and Colombia, where constitutional courts interpreted equality guarantees expansively -- contrasting with legislative inaction or resistance.
