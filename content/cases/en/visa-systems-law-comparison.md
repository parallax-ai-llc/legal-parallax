## Summary

Visa systems -- the legal and administrative frameworks through which states regulate the entry, stay, and activities of foreign nationals within their territories -- serve as the primary mechanism for controlling immigration, facilitating international travel, and protecting national security and labor markets. South Korea operates a comprehensive visa system under the Immigration Act (출입국관리법) and the Enforcement Decree of the Immigration Act (출입국관리법 시행령), administered by the Korea Immigration Service (출입국관리사무소) within the Ministry of Justice (법무부), classifying visas into 36 categories across work, study, family, and diplomatic purposes, with the country's visa-free agreements covering nationals of approximately 112 countries for short-term stays and the Korean Electronic Travel Authorization (K-ETA) system introduced in 2021 for visa-exempt travelers. The United States maintains one of the most complex visa systems globally, governed by the Immigration and Nationality Act (INA, 8 U.S.C. §§ 1101-1537), with over 80 visa categories divided between nonimmigrant visas (temporary stay, INA § 101(a)(15)) and immigrant visas (permanent residence, INA §§ 201-203), administered by the Department of State (consular processing) and USCIS (change/adjustment of status), with the system processing approximately 10 million nonimmigrant visa applications annually. Germany, as part of the EU's common visa policy, issues Schengen visas for short stays (up to 90 days) under the EU Visa Code (Regulation (EC) No 810/2009, amended by Regulation (EU) 2019/1155) and national visas for longer stays under the Aufenthaltsgesetz (AufenthG), while the EU's visa policy framework -- encompassing the Visa Code, the Visa Information System (VIS, Regulation (EU) 2021/1134), the European Travel Information and Authorisation System (ETIAS, Regulation (EU) 2018/1240), and the Schengen Borders Code (Regulation (EU) 2016/399) -- represents the most integrated supranational visa regime in the world, and the ECHR's jurisprudence ensures that visa refusals with implications for family life or protection from persecution comply with Convention rights.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's visa system is structured around status of sojourn (체류자격) categories defined in the Enforcement Decree of the Immigration Act:

**Visa Categories:**

| Group | Status Codes | Purpose |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| Diplomatic/Official | A-1 to A-3 | Diplomatic, official, international agreements |
| Short-term | B-1, B-2, C-1 to C-4 | Transit, tourism, short-term business/employment |
| Study | D-2, D-4 | Academic programs, language training |
| Work | E-1 to E-10 | Teaching, research, technology, professional, arts, entertainment, specific activities, non-professional employment, vessel crew, working holiday |
| Family | F-1 to F-6 | Visiting family, residential, accompanying, overseas Korean (F-4), permanent resident (F-5), marriage migrant (F-6) |
| Other | G-1, H-2 | Miscellaneous (humanitarian status), working visit (ethnic Korean) |

**Employment Permit System (고용허가제, EPS):** Established by the Act on the Employment of Foreign Workers (외국인근로자의 고용 등에 관한 법률) of 2003, the EPS is Korea's primary framework for low-skilled labor migration. Workers from 16 countries with bilateral MOUs receive E-9 non-professional employment visas, with maximum stays of 4 years and 10 months (extendable under certain conditions).

**Overseas Korean (F-4) Visa:** A unique category for ethnic Koreans with foreign nationality, providing long-term residence and employment rights. Approximately 700,000 F-4 visa holders reside in Korea.

### K-ETA and Visa-Free Entry

The Korea Electronic Travel Authorization (K-ETA), launched September 1, 2021, required nationals of visa-exempt countries to obtain electronic authorization before travel. However, the system was suspended for nationals of 22 countries in April 2023, and subsequently for all K-ETA-eligible countries through December 2025, as part of tourism promotion efforts.

### Recent Reforms

- **2023:** Introduction of digital nomad visa (F-1-D, 워케이션 비자) for remote workers earning above a minimum threshold
- **2024:** Expansion of E-7-4 skilled worker points-based visa; relaxation of E-9 to E-7 transition requirements
- **2025:** Proposed comprehensive Immigration and Integration Act (이민통합기본법) to replace the Immigration Act framework

## United States

### Legal Framework

The U.S. visa system is the most complex in the world, with over 80 distinct visa categories.

**Nonimmigrant Visa Categories (INA § 101(a)(15)):**

| Category | Purpose | Annual Limit |
|----------|---------|-------------|
| B-1/B-2 | Business visitors / tourists | No cap |
| F-1 | Academic students | No cap |
| H-1B | Specialty occupation workers | 65,000 + 20,000 (Master's) |
| H-2A | Temporary agricultural workers | No cap |
| H-2B | Temporary non-agricultural workers | 66,000 |
| L-1 | Intracompany transferees | No cap |
| O-1 | Extraordinary ability | No cap |
| J-1 | Exchange visitors | No cap (program limits) |
| TN | USMCA professionals (Canada/Mexico) | No cap |
| E-1/E-2 | Treaty traders / investors | No cap |

**Immigrant Visa Categories (INA §§ 201-203):**

| Category | Basis | Annual Limit |
|----------|-------|-------------|
| IR/CR | Immediate relatives of USC | Unlimited |
| F-1 to F-4 | Family preference | ~226,000 total |
| EB-1 to EB-5 | Employment-based preference | ~140,000 total |
| DV | Diversity lottery | 55,000 |

**Per-Country Limit:** No single country may receive more than 7 percent of total family or employment-based immigrant visas, creating massive backlogs for nationals of high-demand countries (India, China, Mexico, Philippines).

### Visa Waiver Program and ESTA

The Visa Waiver Program (VWP, INA § 217) allows nationals of 41 designated countries to enter the United States for tourism or business for up to 90 days without a visa, subject to:
- Electronic System for Travel Authorization (ESTA) approval
- Machine-readable passport with biometric identifiers
- No prior visa refusal or removal order
- Waiver of right to contest removal (except asylum claims)

### H-1B Visa System

The H-1B specialty occupation visa is the most debated element of U.S. immigration policy:
- Annual cap: 65,000 regular + 20,000 Master's exemption
- Demand consistently exceeds supply: FY2025 lottery received approximately 780,000 registrations for 85,000 slots
- Labor Condition Application (LCA) requires attestation of prevailing wage and working conditions
- H-1B portability (AC21 § 105): workers may begin new H-1B employment upon filing of petition
- Reform proposals include wage-based allocation replacing lottery, increased caps, and elimination of per-country limits

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany operates a dual visa system: Schengen visas for short stays and national visas for longer stays.

**Schengen Visa (Type C):** Issued under the EU Visa Code for stays of up to 90 days within any 180-day period. Valid for travel throughout the 27-state Schengen Area.

**National Visa (Type D):** Issued for stays exceeding 90 days. Upon arrival, the holder applies for a residence permit (Aufenthaltserlaubnis) from the local foreigners authority (Ausländerbehörde).

**Work-Related Residence Permits (AufenthG §§ 18-21):**

| Section | Category | Requirements |
|---------|----------|-------------|
| § 18a | Skilled workers with vocational training | Recognized qualification; job offer; Federal Employment Agency approval |
| § 18b | Skilled workers with academic training | Recognized degree; job offer; no labor market test for qualified professions |
| § 18c | Job-seeker visa | Academic degree; sufficient funds; 6-month duration |
| § 18d | Researchers | Hosting agreement with recognized research institution |
| § 18g | EU Blue Card | Academic degree; salary threshold (€45,300 or €41,042 for shortage occupations, 2024) |
| § 19c | Skilled employment regulation (BeschV) | Various; includes IT specialists without formal degree (3 years experience + €49,830 salary) |
| § 20 | Job-seeker visa for IT specialists | No formal degree required; 3 years IT experience |
| § 21 | Self-employed / freelance | Business plan approval; economic interest; assured livelihood |

### Skilled Immigration Act (Fachkräfteeinwanderungsgesetz)

The 2020 Skilled Immigration Act and its 2023 amendment fundamentally reformed Germany's labor migration framework:
- Opened labor migration to all qualified occupations (previously limited to shortage occupations)
- Recognition partnerships (Anerkennungspartnerschaft): allows entry for qualification recognition in Germany
- Experience-based immigration: workers with 2+ years of experience and state-recognized qualification from their home country may enter without German qualification recognition
- Opportunity Card (Chancenkarte, § 20a AufenthG): points-based system for job-seekers (qualification, language, experience, age, connection to Germany)

### EU Blue Card Reform

The revised EU Blue Card Directive (Directive (EU) 2021/1883), transposed by Germany effective November 18, 2023, significantly improved the Blue Card:
- Lower salary threshold
- Extended to beneficiaries of international protection
- Intra-EU mobility after 12 months (previously 18)
- Family reunification without waiting period

## ECHR / EU

### EU Common Visa Policy

The EU's common visa policy is one of the most integrated aspects of the Area of Freedom, Security and Justice:

**Visa Code (Regulation (EC) No 810/2009, as amended):**
- Uniform rules for Schengen visa issuance
- Standard processing time: 15 calendar days (extendable to 45)
- Visa fee: €80 (reduced to €40 for children 6-12; free for children under 6)
- Multiple-entry visas: preference for long-validity MEVs for regular travelers
- Visa leverage mechanism (2019 amendment): visa processing conditions may be adjusted based on third countries' cooperation on readmission

**Visa Lists (Regulation (EU) 2018/1806):** Establishes two lists:
- Annex I: countries whose nationals require a visa (visa-required list)
- Annex II: countries whose nationals are exempt from visa requirements (visa-free list)
- Suspension mechanism: temporary reintroduction of visa requirements for visa-free countries

### ETIAS (European Travel Information and Authorisation System)

Regulation (EU) 2018/1240 establishes ETIAS, an electronic travel authorization system for visa-exempt third-country nationals:
- Pre-travel screening through automated checks against security databases
- Fee: €7 (free for travelers under 18 or over 70)
- Valid for 3 years or until passport expiry
- Launch repeatedly delayed; expected 2025-2026

### Entry/Exit System (EES)

Regulation (EU) 2017/2226 establishes an automated system replacing manual passport stamping:
- Records biometric data (fingerprints, facial image) of third-country nationals crossing Schengen external borders
- Calculates authorized stay automatically
- Alerts overstay situations
- Launch repeatedly delayed; linked to ETIAS launch

### CJEU Jurisprudence

**Case C-84/12, Koushkaki v. Bundesrepublik Deutschland (2013):** The Court held that Schengen visa applicants have a right to obtain a visa when all admission conditions are met, and that member states may refuse visas only on the grounds listed in the Visa Code. Consular discretion is constrained by EU law.

**Case C-403/16, El Hassani v. Minister Spraw Zagranicznych (2017):** The Court held that member states must provide an appeal procedure against Schengen visa refusals that guarantees, at some stage, a judicial remedy, in compliance with Article 47 of the EU Charter (right to effective judicial protection).

### ECHR Considerations

**Article 8 (Private and Family Life):** Visa refusals that prevent family reunification may violate Article 8 if disproportionate. The ECHR has held that while states have no general obligation to accept family reunification on their territory, refusals must be proportionate (Gül v. Switzerland, 1996; Ahmut v. Netherlands, 1996).

**Article 3 (Non-Refoulement):** Refusal to grant a visa to a person seeking protection from persecution must comply with non-refoulement obligations, though the Court has not imposed a general obligation on states to grant visas for the purpose of seeking asylum (M.N. and Others v. Belgium, Grand Chamber, 2020).

## Comparative Analysis

### System Complexity

The United States operates the most complex visa system (80+ categories), followed by South Korea (36 categories) and Germany/EU (simplified structure with modular residence permits). Complexity creates both precision in managing different migration streams and significant barriers for applicants and employers.

### Labor Migration Approaches

- **South Korea (EPS):** Government-managed bilateral labor migration for low-skilled workers; limited high-skilled pathways expanding
- **United States:** Employer-driven with numerical caps; significant backlogs and lottery systems
- **Germany:** Skills-based with qualification recognition requirements; 2023 reforms significantly liberalized access
- **EU Blue Card:** Harmonized high-skilled migration pathway across EU

### Digital Transformation

All jurisdictions are moving toward electronic travel authorization:
- **United States:** ESTA operational since 2009 (most mature system)
- **South Korea:** K-ETA operational since 2021 (currently suspended for promotion purposes)
- **EU:** ETIAS under development (launch expected 2025-2026)

### Visa-Free Mobility

Visa-free access reflects geopolitical relationships and reciprocity:
- EU Schengen area: largest visa-free zone (27 countries)
- South Korea: extensive visa-free agreements (~112 countries)
- United States VWP: 41 countries; stricter requirements than other programs

### Visa Processing and Backlogs

Visa processing times and backlogs significantly affect the practical accessibility of visa systems:

| Jurisdiction | Average Processing Time (Nonimmigrant) | Key Backlogs |
|-------------|---------------------------------------|-------------|
| South Korea | 5-10 business days | E-9 EPS: limited by annual quotas |
| United States | 1-6 months (varies dramatically by post) | B1/B2 wait times exceeding 900 days at some posts (2023-2024); immigrant visa backlogs exceeding 20 years for some categories/countries |
| Germany | 4-12 weeks (national visa) | Ausländerbehörde appointment backlogs of 3-6 months in major cities (Berlin, Munich) |
| EU (Schengen) | 15 calendar days (standard); 45 days maximum | Processing delays during peak travel seasons |

The United States immigrant visa backlog is particularly severe: as of 2025, approximately 8.7 million persons are waiting in immigrant visa queues, with family-based categories for Mexico and Philippines extending beyond 20 years and employment-based EB-2/EB-3 categories for India extending beyond 50 years under current per-country limits.

### Remote Work and Digital Nomad Visas

The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated the adoption of digital nomad and remote work visa categories:

| Jurisdiction | Visa Category | Requirements |
|-------------|---------------|-------------|
| South Korea | F-1-D (워케이션) (2023) | Income threshold; health insurance; remote work documentation |
| Germany | § 21(5) AufenthG (freelance visa) | Adaptable for remote workers; financial self-sufficiency |
| EU (Estonia) | Digital Nomad Visa (2020) | First national EU implementation; income threshold €3,504/month |
| United States | No dedicated category | B-1/B-2 with remote work: legally uncertain; no USCIS guidance |

Over 50 countries now offer some form of digital nomad or remote work visa, reflecting a structural shift in the relationship between work location and immigration status.

### Visa Reciprocity and Geopolitics

Visa requirements increasingly reflect geopolitical relationships:
- **EU-US visa reciprocity:** Despite Schengen visa-free access for US citizens, some EU nationalities (Poland until 2024, Bulgaria, Romania, Cyprus) did not have VWP access to the US, creating reciprocity tensions
- **South Korea-China:** Complex visa arrangements reflecting economic interdependence and political tensions
- **EU visa leverage mechanism:** The 2019 Visa Code amendment allows the EU to adjust visa processing conditions based on third countries' cooperation on readmission of irregular migrants

### Visa Fraud and Document Security

All jurisdictions face ongoing challenges with visa fraud and document integrity:

- **Biometric integration:** All jurisdictions now require biometric data (fingerprints, facial images) for visa applications; the EU's Visa Information System (VIS) stores biometric data from all Schengen visa applicants
- **South Korea:** The Korea Immigration & Integration System (KIIS) incorporates biometric verification at ports of entry; the K-ETA system provides pre-screening for visa-exempt travelers
- **United States:** The National Visa Center and consular officers utilize the Consular Consolidated Database (CCD) and Automated Biometric Identification System (IDENT/HART) for fraud detection
- **Document fraud trends:** AI-generated supporting documents (bank statements, employment letters) pose emerging challenges for all consular services; the EU's anti-fraud network (FADO) coordinates document authentication across member states
