## Summary

Informed consent (의료 동의) -- the legal and ethical doctrine requiring healthcare providers to disclose material information about proposed treatments, risks, alternatives, and prognosis to patients who must voluntarily agree to or refuse medical interventions based on adequate understanding -- is a foundational principle of modern medical law and bioethics across all major legal systems. South Korea's informed consent framework operates through the Medical Service Act (의료법), which establishes physicians' duty to explain medical procedures, and the patient rights provisions of the Bioethics and Safety Act (생명윤리 및 안전에 관한 법률); the Supreme Court of Korea (대법원) has developed significant case law establishing that failure to obtain informed consent constitutes an independent tort even where the medical treatment itself was performed without negligence, with the landmark 1994 decision (94다27151) establishing the physician's duty to explain (설명의무). The United States developed the informed consent doctrine through landmark judicial decisions including Schloendorff v. Society of New York Hospital (1914, establishing the right to self-determination), Canterbury v. Spence (464 F.2d 772, D.C. Cir. 1972, establishing the "reasonable patient" standard of disclosure), and the distinction between physician-based and patient-based disclosure standards that varies by state; all 50 states now recognize informed consent requirements through statute, common law, or both. Germany's informed consent law (Einwilligung nach Aufklärung) is codified in Sections 630d-630e of the Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB), enacted through the Patientenrechtegesetz (Patient Rights Act) of 2013, which codified decades of judicial development by the Bundesgerichtshof (BGH) establishing strict Aufklärungspflicht (duty to inform) requirements including disclosure in oral form, in a timely manner allowing the patient adequate reflection, and covering the diagnosis, proposed treatment, risks, alternatives, and prognosis. At the European level, the Council of Europe's Convention on Human Rights and Biomedicine (Oviedo Convention, 1997) establishes informed consent as a fundamental principle of biomedical law under Article 5, and the European Court of Human Rights has recognized that the failure to obtain informed consent may violate Article 8 (respect for private life) of the ECHR in cases including Shtukaturov v. Russia (2008) and Fernandes de Oliveira v. Portugal (2019).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's informed consent doctrine has developed through a combination of statutory law and landmark Supreme Court decisions that have progressively strengthened patient autonomy rights.

**Medical Service Act (의료법):** Article 24-2 establishes the physician's duty to explain (설명의무, seolmyeong-uimu) medical procedures to patients. The provision requires physicians to explain the diagnosis, the general details of the proposed treatment, potential risks and side effects, and alternative treatments before proceeding with medical interventions.

**Supreme Court Doctrine:** The Supreme Court has developed detailed requirements for informed consent through a series of decisions:

**대법원 1994.4.15. 선고 93다60953 판결:** The Court established that the physician's duty to explain is an independent legal obligation, breach of which constitutes a tort actionable for damages regardless of whether the underlying medical treatment was negligently performed. This dual-track approach allows patients to recover damages for violation of informed consent even when the medical outcome was not caused by malpractice.

**대법원 1994.11.25. 선고 94다27151 판결:** This landmark decision defined the scope of the duty to explain, requiring disclosure of the patient's condition, the nature of the proposed treatment, expected outcomes, potential risks including rare but serious complications, and available alternatives. The Court adopted a hybrid standard, considering both what a reasonable physician would disclose and what a reasonable patient would want to know.

### Scope of Disclosure

The Supreme Court has refined disclosure requirements across multiple decisions:

| Category | Required Disclosure |
|----------|-------------------|
| Diagnosis | Current condition and its significance |
| Treatment | Nature, purpose, and procedure of proposed treatment |
| Risks | Significant risks including rare but serious complications; frequency where known |
| Alternatives | Available alternative treatments and their comparative risks/benefits |
| Prognosis | Expected outcomes with and without treatment |
| Non-treatment | Consequences of refusing treatment |

### Special Contexts

**Clinical Trials:** The Bioethics and Safety Act (생명윤리 및 안전에 관한 법률) imposes enhanced informed consent requirements for research participants, including disclosure of the experimental nature of the intervention, the right to withdraw at any time, and compensation for research-related injuries.

**Mental Health:** The Mental Health and Welfare Act (정신건강복지법) establishes specific consent procedures for psychiatric treatment, including requirements for voluntary admission and limitations on involuntary treatment without informed consent.

### Damages

Korean courts award damages for informed consent violations in two categories: compensation for physical harm that would not have occurred had the patient been properly informed and refused the procedure (causation-based damages), and compensation for the violation of the right to self-determination itself (autonomy-based damages), even where the patient cannot prove they would have refused treatment.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States developed the informed consent doctrine through common law evolution and subsequent statutory codification, with significant variation across states.

**Schloendorff v. Society of New York Hospital (211 N.Y. 125, 1914):** Justice Cardozo's foundational statement: "Every human being of adult years and sound mind has a right to determine what shall be done with his own body; and a surgeon who performs an operation without his patient's consent commits an assault." This established informed consent as rooted in bodily autonomy and self-determination.

**Two Disclosure Standards:**

**Physician-Based Standard (Majority Rule):** Applied in approximately 25 states, this standard measures disclosure against what a reasonable physician in the same specialty would disclose under similar circumstances. The standard is established through expert testimony. Representative cases: Natanson v. Kline (186 Kan. 393, 1960).

**Patient-Based Standard (Canterbury Standard):** Applied in approximately 25 states and the District of Columbia, this standard requires disclosure of information that a reasonable patient would consider material to the decision. The standard does not require expert testimony. Canterbury v. Spence (464 F.2d 772, D.C. Cir. 1972) articulated this standard, holding that "the patient's right of self-decision shapes the boundaries of the duty to reveal."

### Exceptions to Informed Consent

| Exception | Application |
|-----------|------------|
| Emergency | Implied consent when patient is incapacitated and delay would endanger life |
| Therapeutic privilege | Physician may withhold information likely to cause serious psychological harm |
| Patient waiver | Patient may voluntarily waive the right to information |
| Common knowledge | Risks that are common knowledge need not be specifically disclosed |

### Causation Requirement

Most states require the plaintiff to prove that a reasonable patient in the plaintiff's position would have declined the procedure had proper disclosure been made (objective causation standard). A minority of states apply a subjective standard (whether this particular patient would have declined). The causation requirement has been described as the "primary barrier" to successful informed consent claims.

### Federal Requirements

Federal law imposes specific informed consent requirements in regulated contexts: the FDA requires informed consent for clinical trials (21 C.F.R. Part 50), the Common Rule (45 C.F.R. Part 46) governs consent for federally funded human subjects research, and CMS Conditions of Participation require hospitals to have informed consent policies.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's informed consent law represents one of the most patient-protective frameworks globally, codified in 2013 after decades of judicial development.

**Patientenrechtegesetz (Patient Rights Act, 2013):** Effective February 26, 2013, this Act codified the physician-patient relationship in the BGB (Sections 630a-630h), including comprehensive informed consent provisions:

**Section 630d BGB (Consent):** Treatment may only be carried out with the patient's consent, unless obtained consent would be unreasonable given the particular urgency. Consent may be revoked at any time without stating reasons. Where the patient is incapable of consent, the consent of a person authorized to give consent is required.

**Section 630e BGB (Duty to Inform -- Aufklärungspflicht):** The treating person must inform the patient of all circumstances essential to consent, in particular the type, scope, implementation, expected consequences and risks of the measure, as well as its necessity, urgency, suitability, and prospects of success with regard to diagnosis or treatment. The treating person must also refer to alternatives to the measure, if several medically equally indicated measures may lead to substantially different burdens, risks, or chances of recovery.

### Strict Form and Timing Requirements

German law imposes specific requirements that distinguish it from other jurisdictions:

**Oral Disclosure:** Section 630e(2)(1)(1) BGB requires that informed consent be provided orally (mündlich). Written information may supplement but not replace oral discussion. This requirement reflects the Bundesgerichtshof's long-standing position that a meaningful consent conversation requires personal interaction.

**Timely Disclosure:** Information must be provided in sufficient time for the patient to weigh the decision. For elective procedures, the BGH has held that disclosure on the day of surgery is generally insufficient (BGH, Judgment of November 17, 1970, VI ZR 68/69). For non-urgent procedures, disclosure should occur at least one day before the intervention.

**Comprehensible Language:** Information must be given in language comprehensible to the specific patient, taking into account educational level, language barriers, and emotional state. For patients with limited German proficiency, translation assistance is required.

### Burden of Proof

Section 630h(2) BGB places the burden of proof for proper informed consent on the treating person (physician/hospital). If the physician cannot prove that informed consent was properly obtained, consent is presumed absent, and the treatment constitutes a battery (Körperverletzung) regardless of whether it was medically indicated and properly performed. This burden allocation is among the most patient-favorable globally.

### Hypothetical Consent (Hypothetische Einwilligung)

The BGH has developed the doctrine of hypothetical consent (hypothetische Einwilligung) as a defense. Where informed consent was deficient, the physician may argue that the patient would have consented even with complete disclosure. However, the patient need only establish a "genuine decisional conflict" (echter Entscheidungskonflikt) -- plausible reasons for potentially declining -- to defeat this defense, a deliberately low threshold.

## ECHR & EU

### Council of Europe -- Oviedo Convention (1997)

The Convention on Human Rights and Biomedicine (Oviedo Convention), opened for signature on April 4, 1997, establishes informed consent as a fundamental principle of biomedical law:

**Article 5 -- General Rule:** "An intervention in the health field may only be carried out after the person concerned has given free and informed consent to it. This person shall beforehand be given appropriate information as to the purpose and nature of the intervention as well as on its consequences and risks. The person concerned may freely withdraw consent at any time."

**Article 6 -- Protection of Persons Not Able to Consent:** Provides safeguards for minors and adults with incapacity, requiring authorization by a representative, body, or authority provided for by law, with the individual's participation in the authorization procedure to the greatest extent possible.

**Article 8 -- Emergency Situation:** "When because of an emergency situation the appropriate consent cannot be obtained, any medically necessary intervention may be carried out immediately for the benefit of the health of the individual concerned."

The Oviedo Convention has been ratified by 29 states (notably not by Germany, the UK, or Russia), but its principles inform EU member state law and ECHR jurisprudence.

### European Court of Human Rights

**Shtukaturov v. Russia (Application No. 44009/05, Judgment of March 27, 2008):** The Court found violations of Articles 8 and 6 where a legally incapacitated person was subjected to psychiatric treatment without informed consent and without access to a court to challenge his incapacitation. The judgment established that medical treatment without informed consent engages Article 8 and requires legal safeguards.

**Fernandes de Oliveira v. Portugal (Application No. 78103/14, Grand Chamber Judgment of January 31, 2019):** The Grand Chamber addressed the duty to protect psychiatric patients, holding that states have a positive obligation under Article 2 to ensure adequate informed consent procedures in psychiatric institutions.

**Y.F. v. Turkey (Application No. 24209/94, Judgment of July 22, 2003):** The Court held that a gynecological examination performed on a detained person without her consent violated Article 8. The judgment confirmed that medical interventions without consent constitute interference with physical integrity protected by Article 8.

**Csoma v. Romania (Application No. 8759/05, Judgment of January 15, 2013):** The Court found an Article 8 violation where a pregnant woman was subjected to an invasive procedure without adequate informed consent. The judgment emphasized that pregnant women retain full informed consent rights regarding medical interventions during pregnancy.

## Comparative Analysis

### Disclosure Standards

The most fundamental divergence concerns what must be disclosed. Germany's Aufklärungspflicht is the most comprehensive, requiring oral disclosure of all circumstances essential to consent. South Korea's hybrid standard requires both what a reasonable physician would disclose and what a reasonable patient would want to know. The US split between physician-based and patient-based standards creates inter-state variation. The Oviedo Convention's "appropriate information" standard provides flexibility.

### Burden of Proof

Germany's allocation of the burden of proof to the physician is the most patient-protective approach, effectively creating a presumption that consent was deficient unless the physician proves otherwise. South Korea similarly places the burden on the physician. US states vary, with most placing the initial burden on the patient. This divergence significantly affects litigation outcomes.

### Form Requirements

Germany's oral disclosure requirement is unique, reflecting a philosophy that meaningful consent requires personal dialogue rather than form-signing. The US emphasizes written consent forms, particularly for surgical procedures. South Korea does not prescribe strict form but recommends written consent for invasive procedures. The Oviedo Convention is silent on form.

### Damages Without Causation

South Korea's recognition of autonomy-based damages (compensation for violation of self-determination even without proof that the patient would have refused treatment) is distinctive. Germany's hypothetical consent defense similarly reduces the causation barrier. The US generally requires proof of causation, making the causation requirement the primary obstacle to recovery.
