## Summary

The right to education across the Global South reveals a spectrum of constitutional commitments, legislative frameworks, and implementation realities shaped by colonial educational legacies, post-independence nation-building imperatives, resource constraints, and competing visions of education's role in social transformation. China's education system, governed by the Compulsory Education Law (yiwu jiaoyu fa, 1986, revised 2006) mandating nine years of free compulsory education, operates within a highly centralized framework where the gaokao (National College Entrance Examination) functions as the primary mechanism for social mobility and resource allocation, while the recent "double reduction" (shuangjian) policy (2021) targeting the private tutoring industry represents an unprecedented intervention in the shadow education sector; regional disparities between coastal and interior provinces, and between urban and rural areas, remain the system's defining challenge. India's Right to Education Act (RTE, 2009), implementing the constitutional right established by the 86th Amendment (2002) adding Article 21A, mandates free and compulsory education for children aged 6 to 14 in neighborhood schools with minimum quality standards, including the controversial 25% reservation for economically weaker and disadvantaged sections in private unaided schools -- upheld by the Supreme Court in Society for Un-Aided Private Schools of Rajasthan v. Union of India (2012) but excluded for minority-run institutions in Pramati Educational and Cultural Trust v. Union of India (2014). South Africa's Constitution (Section 29) guarantees "the right to a basic education, including adult basic education" and "the right to further education, which the state, through reasonable measures, must make progressively available and accessible," with the Constitutional Court in Governing Body of the Juma Musjid Primary School v. Essay NO (2011) holding that the right to basic education is "immediately realisable" rather than subject to progressive realization. Nigeria's Universal Basic Education (UBE) Act (2004) mandates free, compulsory, and universal basic education for the first nine years of schooling, financed partly through the Universal Basic Education Commission (UBEC) and a 2% education tax on company profits, but out-of-school children in Nigeria number approximately 18.3 million (UNICEF 2023), the highest in the world. Brazil's Constitution mandates minimum education spending -- 18% of federal tax revenue and 25% of state and municipal tax revenue (Article 212) -- and the 2014 National Education Plan (PNE, Law No. 13,005/2014) set targets including spending 10% of GDP on education by 2024, while the FUNDEB (Fundo de Manutencao e Desenvolvimento da Educacao Basica) mechanism, made permanent by Constitutional Amendment No. 108/2020, redistributes education funding to reduce regional inequalities. Indonesia's Constitution (Article 31) mandates that "every citizen shall have the right to education" and that "the Government shall establish and conduct a national education system," with the National Education System Law (UU Sisdiknas, Law No. 20/2003) requiring 20% of national and regional budgets for education (mirroring the constitutional mandate in Article 31(4)). The Philippines' Enhanced Basic Education Act (Republic Act No. 10533, 2013) implemented the K-12 reform adding senior high school (Grades 11-12) to the previously 10-year basic education cycle, aligning the Philippines with international standards and addressing the country's status as one of the last Asian nations without a 12-year basic education system.

## China

### Constitutional and Legislative Framework

Article 46 of the PRC Constitution provides: "Citizens of the People's Republic of China have the duty as well as the right to receive education." Article 19 mandates that "the State develops socialist educational undertakings and works to raise the scientific and cultural level of the whole nation." Unlike many Global South constitutions, the Chinese provision frames education as both a right and a duty, reflecting the socialist conception of education as serving collective rather than purely individual goals.

The Compulsory Education Law (yiwu jiaoyu fa), originally enacted June 25, 1986, and comprehensively revised June 29, 2006, mandates nine years of free compulsory education (six years of primary school and three years of junior secondary school). Key provisions include: Article 2 (the state implements a system of compulsory education of nine years); Article 4 (all school-age children and adolescents of appropriate age shall enjoy the equal right to receive compulsory education regardless of sex, nationality, race, family financial conditions, religious belief, etc.); Article 12 (local people's governments at and above the county level shall guarantee that school-age children attend schools nearest to their residences); and Article 25 (the state encourages the establishment of schools in poverty-stricken areas and ethnic minority areas).

The Education Law of the People's Republic of China (jiaoyu fa, 1995, revised 2021) provides the overarching framework, establishing: the state education examination system (Article 20); the system of academic certificates (Article 21); the system of degree conferral (Article 22); and provisions for education funding (Chapter VII).

### The Gaokao System

The National College Entrance Examination (gaokao, literally "high examination"), administered annually over two to four days in June, is the primary gateway to higher education and one of the most consequential examinations in the world, taken by approximately 12.91 million students in 2024. The gaokao operates on a provincial quota system, with each university allocating admission slots to different provinces, creating significant regional disparities. Students in Beijing and Shanghai, where elite universities are concentrated, face lower admission thresholds than students from populous provinces like Henan or Shandong.

Gaokao reform has been ongoing since 2014, when the State Council issued the Opinions on Deepening Reform of the Examination and Enrollment System. The "3+3" model (adopted in Shanghai and Zhejiang from 2017, subsequently in other provinces) allows students to choose three subjects beyond the mandatory Chinese, mathematics, and English, replacing the traditional arts/science track division. The "3+1+2" model (adopted in Guangdong, Hebei, and other provinces from 2021) requires physics or history as one mandatory choice.

The gaokao's role in social mobility is contested. Research by Peking University's China Institute for Educational Finance Research found that students from rural areas constituted only 16.3% of admissions at elite (C9 League) universities in 2022, despite comprising approximately 36% of the population. The "special admission plans" (zhuanxiang jihua), including the National Special Plan for Rural and Impoverished Areas (guojia zhuanxiang jihua) introduced in 2012, have allocated approximately 100,000 additional elite university places annually for students from designated counties, partially addressing but not resolving the rural-urban gap.

### The Double Reduction Policy

The Opinions on Further Reducing the Burden of Homework and Off-Campus Training for Students in Compulsory Education (shuangjian yijian), jointly issued by the CPC Central Committee General Office and the State Council General Office on July 24, 2021, represented one of the most dramatic regulatory interventions in education globally. Key provisions include: prohibition of for-profit companies from providing curriculum-based tutoring (xueke lei peixun) for students in compulsory education (Grades 1-9); prohibition of tutoring on weekends, holidays, and winter/summer vacations; requirement that existing tutoring companies re-register as non-profit organizations; prohibition of foreign investment in curriculum tutoring institutions; and limitations on homework volume (no written homework for Grade 1-2, no more than 60 minutes for Grades 3-6, no more than 90 minutes for Grades 7-9).

The policy devastated China's estimated $100 billion private tutoring industry. Companies including New Oriental (XDF), TAL Education (formerly Xueersi), and Gaotu Techedu saw market capitalizations decline by 90% or more. New Oriental pivoted to live-streaming e-commerce, while others restructured around non-academic subjects (sports, arts, STEM enrichment) that remained permitted.

The stated rationale combined educational equity (reducing the advantage of wealthy families who could afford tutoring), demographic policy (reducing the financial burden of child-rearing to encourage higher birth rates), and ideological concerns (reasserting public education's primacy). Critics argued the policy would not eliminate demand but drive tutoring underground, benefiting wealthy families who could afford private one-on-one instruction while disadvantaging middle-class families who relied on affordable group tutoring.

### Rural-Urban Disparities

China's education system exhibits stark rural-urban disparities despite the compulsory education guarantee. Per-pupil expenditure in Beijing was approximately 7 times that of the lowest-spending provinces (Guizhou, Yunnan) in 2022. The "left-behind children" (liushou ertong) phenomenon, caused by parents migrating to cities while children remain in rural areas due to hukou restrictions on urban school enrollment, affects millions of children's educational outcomes.

The "rural teacher support plan" (xiangcun jiaoshi zhichi jihua, 2015) attempted to address the quality gap through salary supplements, training, and recruitment incentives. The "Internet+ education" initiative expanded online learning resources to rural schools, with 98% of primary and secondary schools connected to the internet by 2023 (Ministry of Education data). However, digital access does not translate directly to educational quality, and research by Stanford University's Rural Education Action Program (REAP) found persistent gaps in cognitive development and educational attainment for rural children.

## India

### From Directive Principle to Fundamental Right

India's journey toward recognizing education as a fundamental right spans five decades. The original Constitution (1950) placed education in the Directive Principles of State Policy: Article 45 directed the state to "endeavour to provide, within a period of ten years from the commencement of this Constitution, for free and compulsory education for all children until they complete the age of fourteen years." Article 41 directed the state to make "effective provision for securing the right to work, to education and to public assistance in cases of unemployment, old age, sickness and disablement."

The Supreme Court's evolution on education rights began with Mohini Jain v. State of Karnataka (1992 (3) SCC 666), where Justice Kuldip Singh held that "the right to education flows directly from the right to life" under Article 21. In Unni Krishnan v. State of Andhra Pradesh ((1993) 1 SCC 645), the Court affirmed that the right to education was a fundamental right for children up to age 14, derived from Article 21 read with Article 45, though not unlimited beyond that age.

The 86th Constitutional Amendment (2002) inserted Article 21A: "The State shall provide free and compulsory education to all children of the age of six to fourteen years in such manner as the State may, by law, determine." This was supplemented by the revised Article 45 (now directing the state to provide early childhood care and education for children below six years) and Article 51A(k) (imposing a fundamental duty on parents/guardians to provide educational opportunities to children aged 6-14).

### The RTE Act, 2009

The Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education Act, 2009 (RTE Act), effective April 1, 2010, operationalizes Article 21A. Key provisions include: Section 3 (every child aged 6-14 has the right to free and compulsory education in a neighborhood school); Section 4 (special provision for children not admitted to or who have not completed elementary education); Section 6 (duty of local authorities to ensure enrollment, attendance, and completion); Section 8-9 (duties of the central and state governments including providing schools, ensuring quality, providing infrastructure); Section 12(1)(c) (the controversial 25% reservation for children belonging to weaker sections and disadvantaged groups in private unaided schools, with government reimbursement of per-child expenditure); Section 13 (prohibition of capitation fees and screening procedures for admission); Section 16 (prohibition on detention/expulsion, amended in 2019 to permit detention at Grades 5 and 8); Section 17 (prohibition of physical punishment and mental harassment); and the Schedule specifying norms and standards including pupil-teacher ratios (30:1 for primary, 35:1 for upper primary), building requirements, and teacher qualifications.

In Society for Un-Aided Private Schools of Rajasthan v. Union of India ((2012) 6 SCC 1), a five-judge bench upheld the constitutionality of Section 12(1)(c) by a 3:2 majority. Chief Justice Kapadia's majority opinion held that the provision constituted a "reasonable restriction" on the right to carry on occupation (Article 19(1)(g)) and fell within the state's regulatory power over education. However, in Pramati Educational and Cultural Trust v. Union of India ((2014) 8 SCC 1), the Court held that the RTE Act could not apply to minority-run educational institutions protected under Article 30(1) (right of minorities to establish and administer educational institutions), exempting these schools from the 25% reservation.

### National Education Policy 2020

The National Education Policy 2020 (NEP 2020), approved by the Union Cabinet on July 29, 2020, is the first comprehensive education policy since 1986. Key proposals include: restructuring the 10+2 school system into a 5+3+3+4 structure (Foundational: ages 3-8; Preparatory: 8-11; Middle: 11-14; Secondary: 14-18); multilingual education with instruction in the mother tongue or regional language until at least Grade 5; integration of vocational education from Grade 6; multidisciplinary undergraduate education with multiple entry and exit points; establishment of a Higher Education Commission of India (HECI) replacing the UGC and AICTE; a target of increasing the Gross Enrollment Ratio in higher education from 26.3% (2018) to 50% by 2035; and a commitment to increasing public expenditure on education to 6% of GDP.

The learning outcomes crisis remains India's central education challenge. The Annual Status of Education Report (ASER) 2022 found that 57.3% of children in Grade 3 could not read a Grade 2 level text, and 74.1% of Grade 3 students could not solve a two-digit subtraction problem. These findings suggest that while the RTE Act has been largely successful in achieving near-universal enrollment (97.2% net enrollment ratio for primary education in 2022-23), it has not translated into adequate learning outcomes.

## Africa

### South Africa: The Right to Basic Education

Section 29 of the South African Constitution establishes a tiered education right: Section 29(1)(a) provides "the right to a basic education, including adult basic education" (immediately realizable); Section 29(1)(b) provides "the right to further education, which the state, through reasonable measures, must make progressively available and accessible" (progressively realizable); and Section 29(2) guarantees the right to instruction in the language of choice "where that education is reasonably practicable."

The Constitutional Court's treatment of Section 29(1)(a) as immediately realizable -- unlike most socio-economic rights in the Constitution, which are subject to "progressive realization" under Section 27(2) -- was confirmed in Governing Body of the Juma Musjid Primary School v. Essay NO (2011 (8) BCLR 761 (CC)). Chief Justice Ngcobo held: "Unlike some of the other socio-economic rights, this right is immediately realisable. There is no internal limitation requiring that the right be 'progressively realised' within 'available resources' subject to 'reasonable legislative measures.'"

In Madzodzo v. Minister of Basic Education (2014 (3) SA 441 (ECM)), the Eastern Cape High Court ordered the provincial government to provide furniture to all public schools in the province, establishing that the right to basic education encompasses adequate physical learning conditions. In Minister of Basic Education v. Basic Education for All (2016 (4) SA 63 (SCA)), the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the right to textbook delivery as part of the right to basic education.

The South African Schools Act (SASA, 84 of 1996) establishes the framework for school governance, including: compulsory attendance from age 7 to 15 or completion of Grade 9 (Section 3); School Governing Bodies (SGBs) with parent-majority membership (Section 23); quintile-based school funding with no-fee schools for the poorest three quintiles (Section 39); and provision for instruction in the language of choice (Section 6), which has generated significant litigation including Head of Department, Mpumalanga v. Hoerskool Ermelo (2010 (2) SA 415 (CC)), where the Constitutional Court addressed the tension between Afrikaans-medium instruction and access to education.

South Africa's education spending of approximately 6.6% of GDP and 20% of government expenditure is among the highest in the world, yet learning outcomes remain poor. The Progress in International Reading Literacy Study (PIRLS) 2021 found that 81% of South African Grade 4 learners could not read for meaning, placing South Africa last among 57 participating countries.

### Nigeria: Universal Basic Education

Section 18(1) of the Nigerian Constitution provides: "Government shall direct its policy towards ensuring that there are equal and adequate educational opportunities at all levels." This is a Directive Principle of State Policy, non-justiciable under Section 6(6)(c).

The Compulsory, Free Universal Basic Education Act (UBE Act, 2004) mandates nine years of free, compulsory basic education (six years of primary and three years of junior secondary). The Universal Basic Education Commission (UBEC) administers the program, funded by: 2% of the Consolidated Revenue Fund; proceeds of a 2% education tax on assessable profits of companies registered in Nigeria (Education Tax Act, 1993, now Tertiary Education Trust Fund Act, 2011); and bilateral and multilateral development assistance.

Nigeria's education crisis is defined by the world's highest number of out-of-school children -- estimated at 18.3 million by UNICEF (2023), predominantly in the northern states. Factors include: poverty and the direct/indirect costs of schooling; cultural norms particularly affecting girls' enrollment in the North; the Boko Haram insurgency (which has destroyed over 2,000 schools and displaced more than 800,000 students in the Northeast since 2009, per UNICEF data); the tension between secular education and Islamic education (almajiri system); and inadequate school infrastructure and teacher quality.

The almajiri education system, a traditional Quranic education model involving approximately 10 million children (predominantly boys) in northern Nigeria, operates largely outside the formal education system. The Almajiri Education Programme (launched in 2012) attempted to integrate Quranic and Western education through 157 model schools, but implementation has been criticized as insufficient.

## Latin America

### Brazilian Education Architecture

Brazil's Constitution of 1988 dedicates an entire chapter to education (Articles 205-214) with unusual specificity. Article 205 declares education "a right of all and a duty of the State and of the family." Article 206 establishes principles including equality of access, pluralism of pedagogical approaches, free public education, democratic management, and a national minimum standard of educational quality. Article 208 specifies state obligations including: mandatory and free basic education for ages 4-17 (expanded from 7-14 by Constitutional Amendment No. 59/2009); progressive universalization of secondary education; specialized services for disabled students; early childhood education in nurseries and pre-schools; and university access based on individual ability.

Article 212 establishes constitutionally mandated minimum education spending: 18% of federal tax revenue and 25% of state and municipal tax revenue. This spending mandate -- one of the most specific in any constitution globally -- has been enforced by the courts. The STF in ADPF 292 (2018) addressed challenges to manipulation of the spending base by state governments seeking to reduce their education obligations.

The FUNDEB (Fundo de Manutencao e Desenvolvimento da Educacao Basica e de Valorizacao dos Profissionais da Educacao), originally established as a transitional fund (2007-2020), was made permanent by Constitutional Amendment No. 108/2020 with enhanced federal complementation. The new FUNDEB increased the federal government's contribution from 10% to 23% of the total fund by 2026, with a new "cost-quality indicator" (Custo Aluno-Qualidade, CAQ) designed to ensure that per-pupil spending meets minimum quality standards. FUNDEB redistributes education funding from wealthier to poorer states and municipalities, functioning as the primary equalization mechanism in Brazil's deeply unequal education system.

The National Education Plan 2014-2024 (PNE, Law No. 13,005/2014) established 20 goals and 254 strategies, including: universalization of preschool education for ages 4-5 (Goal 1); universalization of primary education (Goal 2); universalization of secondary education (Goal 3); inclusion of people with disabilities (Goal 4); literacy of all children by the end of the 3rd year of primary education (Goal 5); full-time education in 50% of public schools (Goal 6); quality targets linked to IDEB (Basic Education Development Index) scores (Goal 7); and education spending of 10% of GDP by 2024 (Goal 20). Evaluation at the plan's conclusion showed significant progress on enrollment goals but underachievement on quality and spending targets.

### Colombian and Mexican Frameworks

Colombia's Constitution (Article 67) declares education "a public service function" that is compulsory for ages 5-15 and free in state institutions. The Constitutional Court has developed extensive education rights jurisprudence through tutela (individual constitutional complaint) decisions, including establishing the right of pregnant students to continue their education (Sentencia T-656/98) and the right of children with disabilities to inclusive education (Sentencia T-523/16).

Mexico's Article 3 of the Constitution -- one of the most amended provisions in the document -- guarantees the right to education from initial (preschool) through upper secondary (bachillerato) levels, with the state obligated to provide free public education at all these levels. The 2019 educational reform under President Lopez Obrador replaced the 2013 reform's teacher evaluation system (which had generated fierce opposition from the CNTE teachers' union) with the Ley General de Educacion (General Education Law) establishing the System for Career Improvement of Teachers (USICAMM) and the National Commission for Continuous Improvement of Education (MEJOREDU).

## Southeast Asia

### Indonesian Education Budget Mandate

Article 31(4) of the Indonesian Constitution (as amended in 2002) provides: "The state shall give priority to the education budget allocation of a minimum 20 percent of the state budget as well as of the regional budgets to fulfill the needs of the implementation of national education." This provision -- one of the few constitutionally mandated education spending floors globally -- was the subject of Constitutional Court Decision No. 012/PUU-III/2005, where the Court found that the government's failure to meet the 20% threshold violated the Constitution.

The National Education System Law (UU Sisdiknas, Law No. 20 of 2003) establishes: nine years of compulsory education (Article 6, extended to 12 years by Ministerial Regulation in 2015); the right of every citizen to quality education (Article 5); the obligation of the central and regional governments to guarantee the availability of funds for national education (Article 46); and the national curriculum framework (Article 36-38).

The pesantren (Islamic boarding school) system, one of the oldest educational traditions in Indonesia, enrolls approximately 4.5 million students across 27,000 pesantren (Ministry of Religious Affairs data, 2023). The Pesantren Law (Law No. 18 of 2019) formalized government recognition and support for pesantren, including funding for development, teacher certification, and curriculum integration with national standards. The relationship between pesantren education and the formal national curriculum has been a persistent policy challenge; some pesantren focus exclusively on religious education, while others (pesantren modern) integrate secular subjects.

Indonesia's education system faces several structural challenges: the quality gap between Java/Bali and eastern Indonesia (Papua, NTT, Maluku); the adequacy of teacher qualifications (the 2005 Teacher Law required all teachers to hold bachelor's degrees and professional certificates, but implementation has been uneven); and the 2022 introduction of the Merdeka Curriculum (Kurikulum Merdeka), which replaced the previous national curriculum with a more flexible, competency-based framework emphasizing project-based learning.

### Philippine K-12 Reform

The Enhanced Basic Education Act of 2013 (Republic Act No. 10533) was the most significant structural reform in Philippine education since the American colonial period. The law added two years of senior high school (Grades 11-12) to the basic education cycle, extending it from 10 years (6 years elementary + 4 years high school) to 13 years (1 year kindergarten + 6 years elementary + 4 years junior high school + 2 years senior high school). The reform was phased in beginning 2012, with the first cohort of K-12 graduates completing in 2018.

The rationale for K-12 included: aligning the Philippines with the international standard of 12 years of basic education (the Philippines and Djibouti were the last countries with 10-year cycles); improving the quality of graduates entering the workforce or higher education; introducing tracks in senior high school (Academic, Technical-Vocational-Livelihood, Sports, Arts and Design) to diversify post-secondary pathways; and enhancing international competitiveness.

Implementation challenges have been significant. The Department of Education estimated a shortage of 159,000 classrooms, 98,000 teachers, and millions of textbooks at the reform's inception. The COVID-19 pandemic disrupted implementation from 2020-2022, with prolonged school closures (among the longest in the world) and the shift to blended/distance learning exacerbating existing resource gaps. The Philippines' performance on the Programme for International Student Assessment (PISA) 2022 -- ranking near the bottom in reading, mathematics, and science among 81 participating countries -- highlighted persistent learning quality challenges.

Article XIV of the Philippine Constitution addresses education extensively. Section 1 directs the state to "protect and promote the right of all citizens to quality education at all levels and shall take appropriate steps to make such education accessible to all." Section 2 mandates the establishment and maintenance of "a complete, adequate, and integrated system of education relevant to the needs of the people and society." Section 5(5) provides for "optional religious instruction" in public elementary and high schools. The Free Public Higher Education Act (Republic Act No. 10931, 2017) eliminated tuition in state universities and colleges (SUCs), funded by an increase in the tobacco and alcohol excise tax under the TRAIN Law (RA 10963, 2017).

## Comparative Analysis

### Justiciability of Education Rights

The comparison reveals three distinct approaches to the justiciability of education rights. South Africa treats basic education as an immediately realizable right, enforceable through the courts without deference to resource constraints. India treats education for ages 6-14 as a fundamental right enforceable through Article 32 and 226 writ jurisdiction, but with significant latitude in implementation. Brazil combines constitutional spending mandates with justiciable individual claims (the STF has ordered municipalities to provide daycare placements). China, Nigeria, and Indonesia place education in constitutional provisions that lack effective judicial enforcement mechanisms -- China due to the absence of constitutional review, Nigeria due to the non-justiciable status of Directive Principles, and Indonesia due to the limited scope of Constitutional Court review.

### Spending Mandates as Constitutional Mechanisms

Brazil and Indonesia are distinctive in constitutionalizing minimum education spending. Brazil's 18%/25% formula and Indonesia's 20% mandate create legally enforceable floors that have been upheld by their respective constitutional courts. This approach -- treating education funding as a constitutional obligation rather than a discretionary budget allocation -- represents a Global South innovation in education rights enforcement. Nigeria's UBE funding through the education tax provides a dedicated revenue stream but at a lower level. South Africa achieves high spending through political commitment rather than constitutional mandate.

### Quality vs. Access

Every jurisdiction examined has made significant progress on enrollment and access, but the "learning crisis" (World Bank terminology) is universal. India's near-universal enrollment coexists with ASER findings that most Grade 3 students cannot read at Grade 2 level. South Africa's high spending produces PIRLS results ranking it last among participating countries. Brazil's IDEB (Basic Education Development Index) has shown improvement but remains below PNE targets. The Global South's education challenge has shifted from access to quality, requiring a different set of policy instruments including teacher quality, curriculum reform, assessment systems, and accountability mechanisms.

### Private Education and Equity

The role of private education varies dramatically: from approximately 50% of urban enrollment in India to 5% in South Africa, with Brazil, Indonesia, and the Philippines at intermediate levels. India's RTE Act 25% reservation represents a unique attempt to use private schools as instruments of social integration. Indonesia's pesantren system and the Philippines' large private tertiary sector raise questions about quality assurance and regulatory coherence. The growth of low-fee private schools across the Global South -- documented by researchers including James Tooley and Pauline Dixon -- has generated debate about whether market-based education extends or undermines the right to education.
