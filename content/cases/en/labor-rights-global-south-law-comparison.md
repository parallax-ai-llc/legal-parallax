## Summary

Labor rights across the Global South present a paradox of formal protection and structural informality, shaped by the legacies of socialist command economies, colonial labor extraction, post-independence developmentalism, and contemporary globalization pressures. China's labor framework operates under the tension between a nominally socialist system that claims to represent workers' interests and the practical demands of export-oriented industrialization; the Labor Law (1994), Labor Contract Law (2007), and Trade Union Law (2001) establish formal protections that coexist with the hukou (household registration) system's creation of a two-tier labor market affecting approximately 290 million internal migrant workers, and the "996" work culture (9am to 9pm, 6 days a week) that the Supreme People's Court declared illegal in 2021 but which remains widespread in the technology sector. India's labor law landscape has undergone its most significant transformation since independence through the consolidation of 29 central labor statutes into four Labor Codes -- the Code on Wages (2019), Industrial Relations Code (2020), Social Security Code (2020), and Occupational Safety, Health and Working Conditions Code (2020) -- though implementation has been delayed by the need for state-level rules and political opposition, while approximately 90% of India's workforce remains in the informal sector beyond the effective reach of labor regulation. South Africa's post-apartheid labor framework, centered on the Labour Relations Act (66 of 1995), Basic Conditions of Employment Act (75 of 1997), and Employment Equity Act (55 of 1998), includes the world's most ambitious affirmative action program through the Broad-Based Black Economic Empowerment Act (53 of 2003, amended 2013), yet confronts official unemployment exceeding 32% and youth unemployment above 60%. Nigeria's labor law, primarily governed by the Labour Act (Cap L1 LFN 2004) and the Trade Unions Act (Cap T14 LFN 2004), remains largely unamended since the 1970s and covers only the formal sector. Brazil's Consolidacao das Leis do Trabalho (CLT, Decree-Law No. 5,452/1943), the foundational labor statute created during the Getulio Vargas era, was significantly reformed by the 2017 Labor Reform (Law No. 13,467/2017) under President Temer, which introduced more than 100 changes including legitimizing outsourcing of core activities, creating new forms of flexible employment, and restricting labor court access; Colombia's labor framework under the Codigo Sustantivo del Trabajo (1950, extensively amended) faces the challenge of 60% labor informality. Indonesia's controversial Job Creation Law (UU Cipta Kerja, Law No. 11 of 2020) -- the "Omnibus Law" -- was declared unconstitutionally enacted by the Constitutional Court in Decision No. 91/PUU-XVIII/2020, subsequently re-enacted through the controversial Government Regulation in Lieu of Law (Perppu) No. 2 of 2022 and ratified as Law No. 6 of 2023, introducing significant deregulation of labor protections including reduced severance, expanded fixed-term contracts, and modified minimum wage formulas. The Philippines' labor framework under the Labor Code (Presidential Decree No. 442, 1974, extensively amended) and the constitutional provisions on social justice (Article XIII) confronts widespread contractualization ("endo" or end-of-contract practices) that the DOLE Department Order No. 174 (2017) and subsequent Executive Order No. 51 (2018) sought to address.

## China

### Constitutional and Legislative Framework

Articles 42 through 44 of the PRC Constitution address labor rights. Article 42 provides: "Citizens of the People's Republic of China have the right as well as the duty to work." Article 43 guarantees the right to rest, requiring the state to "prescribe working hours and vacations for working people." Article 44 addresses retirement systems.

The Labor Law (laodong fa, 1994, effective January 1, 1995) establishes basic protections including: the eight-hour workday and 44-hour workweek (Article 36, later reduced to 40 hours by State Council regulations); overtime limits of one hour per day and 36 hours per month (Articles 41-42); minimum wage determined by provincial governments (Article 48); and prohibitions on child labor (Article 15, below age 16).

The Labor Contract Law (laodong hetong fa, 2007, amended 2012) strengthened employment security by requiring written contracts within one month of commencing work (Article 10), limiting fixed-term contracts to two consecutive terms before an open-ended contract must be offered (Article 14), requiring economic compensation for contract termination (Articles 46-47), and imposing restrictions on labor dispatch (outsourced) workers (Articles 57-67, strengthened in 2012 to limit dispatch to "temporary, auxiliary, or substitutive" positions).

### The Hukou System and Migrant Labor

The hukou (household registration) system, established by the Regulations on Household Registration of the People's Republic of China (1958), creates a two-tier labor market by tying access to public services -- education, healthcare, housing subsidies, social security -- to a person's registered permanent residence. The approximately 290 million rural-to-urban migrant workers (nongmingong), as counted in the National Bureau of Statistics' 2023 Migrant Worker Monitoring Survey, typically hold rural hukou but work in urban areas, placing them outside the urban social security system.

Hukou reform has been gradual and incomplete. The State Council's Opinions on Further Promoting the Reform of the Household Registration System (2014) eliminated the distinction between "agricultural" and "non-agricultural" hukou but maintained the residence-based service access framework. Small and medium cities have largely liberalized settlement, but megacities (Beijing, Shanghai, Guangzhou, Shenzhen) maintain strict point-based systems that effectively exclude most low-skilled migrant workers.

The consequences are profound: migrant workers' children face barriers to urban public school enrollment, requiring many to remain as "left-behind children" (liushou ertong) in rural areas -- estimated at 9 million in 2020, down from 61 million in 2010 due to partial reforms and declining birth rates. Migrant workers receive lower social insurance coverage, are more vulnerable to wage theft, and have limited access to workplace injury compensation under the Work-Related Injury Insurance Regulations (2010).

### 996 Culture and Overwork

The "996" work schedule (9am to 9pm, six days per week, totaling 72 hours weekly) became a subject of public debate after the "996.ICU" GitHub repository launched in March 2019, documenting technology companies enforcing such schedules. Prominent tech founders including Jack Ma (Alibaba) publicly endorsed 996, calling it "a huge blessing." The movement highlighted the gap between statutory working hour limits (40 hours/week with limited overtime) and industry practice.

In August 2021, the Supreme People's Court and the Ministry of Human Resources and Social Security jointly issued ten typical labor dispute cases, explicitly declaring 996 schedules illegal under the Labor Law. The case of Zhang v. Zhongtian Company stated that work hour arrangements "seriously violating the legal provisions on the extension of working hours" rendered relevant contract clauses void. However, enforcement mechanisms remain weak, and the practice of "voluntary" overtime continues through algorithmic management, performance targets, and organizational culture.

The rise of platform/gig economy work has created new challenges. Food delivery riders (waimai qishou), numbering approximately 7 million on Meituan and Ele.me alone, are typically classified as independent contractors. The Ministry of Human Resources and Social Security's Guiding Opinions on Safeguarding the Labor Rights and Interests of Workers in New Employment Forms (2021) required platform companies to establish labor relations with workers who meet certain criteria, but implementation remains uneven.

### Trade Unions: The ACFTU Monopoly

The All-China Federation of Trade Unions (ACFTU, Zhonghua Quanguo Zonggonghui) is the sole legal trade union organization under Article 10 of the Trade Union Law (gonghui fa, 2001): "The establishment of trade unions in enterprises, institutions, and government organs with 25 or more members shall be subject to reporting to the trade union at the next higher level for approval." Independent unions are prohibited. The ACFTU claims approximately 302 million members across 2.8 million primary-level unions (2022 data), making it the world's largest labor organization by membership but one with limited worker representation capacity given its structural subordination to the CPC.

Wildcat strikes, while technically in a legal grey zone (Chinese law neither explicitly permits nor prohibits strikes), occur with some frequency -- the China Labour Bulletin Strike Map documented over 1,000 collective actions annually in the mid-2010s, declining after increased repression of labor NGOs from 2015 onward. The arrest and conviction of labor rights advocates, including the Jasic workers' support group in Shenzhen (2018), demonstrated the limits of tolerated labor activism.

## India

### The Four Labor Codes

India's labor law consolidation represents one of the most significant regulatory reforms in its post-independence history. Twenty-nine central labor statutes were consolidated into four codes:

The **Code on Wages, 2019** (effective date pending state rule notification) consolidates the Payment of Wages Act (1936), Minimum Wages Act (1948), Payment of Bonus Act (1965), and Equal Remuneration Act (1976). Key provisions include: a national minimum wage floor (Section 9); prohibition of gender discrimination in wages (Section 3); and simplified compliance through a single definition of "wages" (Section 2(y)) replacing the varying definitions across replaced statutes.

The **Industrial Relations Code, 2020** consolidates the Industrial Disputes Act (1947), Trade Unions Act (1926), and Industrial Employment (Standing Orders) Act (1946). Controversial provisions include: the increase in the threshold for requiring government permission before layoff, retrenchment, or closure from 100 workers to 300 workers (Section 77); the requirement for a strike or lockout notice 60 days in advance (Section 62); and the introduction of a "negotiating union" or "negotiating council" framework for collective bargaining (Section 14).

The **Social Security Code, 2020** consolidates nine statutes including the Employees' Provident Funds Act (1952), Employees' State Insurance Act (1948), and Maternity Benefit Act (1961). A significant innovation is the extension of social security coverage to gig workers and platform workers (Section 2(35), 2(60)), defined to include ride-hailing drivers, food delivery riders, and other workers in the platform economy.

The **Occupational Safety, Health and Working Conditions Code, 2020** consolidates 13 statutes including the Factories Act (1948), Mines Act (1952), and Building and Other Construction Workers Act (1996).

As of early 2026, implementation remains incomplete. The codes require state governments to frame rules (labor being a concurrent subject under the Seventh Schedule), and political opposition -- particularly from trade unions affiliated with opposition parties and the Left -- has slowed state-level adoption. States governed by the Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) have generally moved faster on rule-making, while others have delayed or challenged specific provisions.

### Informal Sector and Unorganized Workers

India's defining labor challenge is the overwhelming informality of its workforce. The Periodic Labour Force Survey (PLFS) 2022-23 estimated that approximately 89% of workers are in informal employment (either in the informal sector or informally employed in the formal sector). The Unorganised Workers' Social Security Act (2008) created a framework for extending social security to unorganized workers, but implementation has been limited.

The e-Shram portal, launched in August 2021, aimed to create a national database of unorganized workers. By 2024, approximately 300 million workers had registered, providing the first comprehensive data on the unorganized workforce. However, registration alone does not confer benefits; the portal serves primarily as a targeting mechanism for future social security extensions.

Platform workers in India -- estimated at 7.7 million by NITI Aayog (2022) -- face classification challenges similar to those worldwide. The Social Security Code's provisions for gig and platform workers were a positive step, but the absence of implementing rules has left them in legal limbo.

## Africa

### South Africa: Post-Apartheid Labor Architecture

South Africa's post-apartheid labor framework is among the most comprehensive in the Global South. Section 23 of the Constitution guarantees: "Everyone has the right to fair labour practices" (Section 23(1)); "Every worker has the right to form and join a trade union; to participate in the activities and programmes of a trade union; and to strike" (Section 23(2)); and "Every employer has the right to form and join an employers' organisation" and "to engage in collective bargaining" (Section 23(3-5)).

The Labour Relations Act (LRA, 66 of 1995) establishes the framework for collective bargaining, unfair dismissal protection, and dispute resolution through the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA). The CCMA has become one of the most efficient labor dispute resolution bodies in the developing world, handling over 200,000 referrals annually.

The Basic Conditions of Employment Act (BCEA, 75 of 1997) establishes minimum conditions including: a 45-hour ordinary workweek (Section 9); regulation of overtime at 1.5 times the normal rate (Section 10); 21 consecutive days of annual leave (Section 20); four consecutive months of maternity leave (Section 25); and a national minimum wage, established by the National Minimum Wage Act (9 of 2018) at R27.58/hour (2024), covering all workers except those in expanded public works programs.

### BBBEE: Affirmative Action in Employment and Enterprise

The Broad-Based Black Economic Empowerment Act (53 of 2003, amended by Act 46 of 2013) and its implementing Codes of Good Practice establish the world's most comprehensive affirmative action framework extending beyond employment to ownership, management, enterprise development, and socio-economic development. The generic scorecard measures: ownership (25 points), management control (19 points), skills development (20 points), enterprise and supplier development (40 points), and socio-economic development (5 points) -- totaling 109 points, with certification levels from Level 1 (100+ points, 135% procurement recognition) to Level 8 (40-54 points, 10% recognition).

BBBEE compliance is enforced through procurement requirements (government and state-owned enterprises must consider BBBEE levels in tender evaluations) and sector-specific charters (mining, financial services, ICT, etc.). The Constitutional Court addressed the framework in South African Police Service v. Solidarity obo Barnard (2014 (6) SA 123 (CC)), holding that the SAPS Employment Equity Plan constituted a valid affirmative action measure under Section 9(2) of the Constitution.

Critics, including the Congress of South African Trade Unions (COSATU), have argued that BBBEE has primarily benefited a small politically connected elite rather than achieving broad-based transformation. The phenomenon of "fronting" -- false or superficial compliance arrangements -- led to the BBBEE Amendment Act's inclusion of fronting as a criminal offense (Section 13O, penalties up to 10 years imprisonment).

### Nigeria: Formal Framework, Informal Reality

Nigeria's Labour Act (Cap L1 LFN 2004), largely unchanged since the 1970s, applies only to workers employed under "a contract of service" (Section 91), effectively excluding the vast informal sector. The Act establishes: a 40-hour workweek (Section 13); overtime at 1.5 times the hourly rate (Section 13(3)); 6 days of annual leave after 12 months (Section 18); and 12 weeks of maternity leave at 50% of wages (Section 54).

The minimum wage, established by the National Minimum Wage Act (2019, amended 2024), was increased to NGN 70,000/month in 2024 following extended negotiations between the Federal Government and the Nigeria Labour Congress (NLC). Enforcement remains a major challenge; many state governments and private employers, particularly in the informal sector, do not comply.

The Petroleum Industry Act (2021) included provisions for "host communities" affected by oil extraction, including a requirement for operating companies to contribute 3% of operating expenditure to host community development trusts (Section 235). While not a traditional labor provision, this addresses the specific labor and livelihoods context of the Niger Delta.

## Latin America

### Brazilian CLT and the 2017 Reform

The Consolidacao das Leis do Trabalho (CLT), enacted by Decree-Law No. 5,452 on May 1, 1943, during the Estado Novo of Getulio Vargas, remains the foundational framework for Brazilian labor law. Modeled on Italian corporatist labor law, the CLT established a comprehensive system of individual employment rights, collective bargaining through the unicidade sindical (single union) structure, and specialized labor courts (Justica do Trabalho).

The 2017 Labor Reform (Reforma Trabalhista, Law No. 13,467/2017), enacted under President Temer, introduced more than 100 amendments to the CLT, representing the most significant deregulation of Brazilian labor law since the CLT's creation. Key changes include: permitting negotiated agreements to prevail over statutory provisions in specified areas (Article 611-A, the "prevalence of the negotiated over the legislated"); legitimizing outsourcing of core activities (Article 4-A, Law No. 6,019/1974 as amended); creating the "intermittent work" contract category (Article 443, para. 3 CLT); eliminating the mandatory union contribution (contribuicao sindical, previously equivalent to one day's wages annually); restricting access to free legal aid (justica gratuita) for labor claims; and establishing that agreements upon termination (Article 484-A) constitute full discharge of claims.

The STF upheld key provisions of the reform, including in ADI 5794 (2018) (elimination of mandatory union dues), while the TST (Superior Labor Court) has developed extensive case law interpreting the reformed provisions. Critics, including the Central Unica dos Trabalhadores (CUT, Brazil's largest trade union federation), argue that the reform increased precarity, reduced real wages, and weakened collective bargaining. Data from PNAD Continua (IBGE) showed that informal employment increased from 40.8% in 2017 to 43.4% in 2019 before the pandemic disrupted trends.

Article 7 of the Brazilian Constitution enumerates 34 workers' rights, including: protection against arbitrary dismissal (Item I); unemployment insurance (Item II); FGTS severance indemnity fund (Item III); minimum wage (Item IV); reducibility of wages through collective agreement (Item VI); 13th salary (Item VIII); overtime at minimum 50% premium (Item XVI); annual vacation with 1/3 premium (Item XVII); maternity leave of 120 days (Item XVIII); paternity leave (Item XIX); and retirement pension (Item XXIV).

### Mexican Labor Reform and Union Democracy

Mexico's 2019 labor reform, implementing obligations under the United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement (USMCA/T-MEC) labor chapter, represented a paradigm shift in Mexican labor relations. The reform amended the Federal Labor Law (Ley Federal del Trabajo, LFT) to: require secret ballot elections for union leadership and collective bargaining agreement ratification (Article 390 Bis-Ter); replace the tripartite Conciliation and Arbitration Boards (Juntas de Conciliacion y Arbitraje) with independent labor courts under the judicial branch; create the Federal Center for Conciliation and Labor Registration (CFCRL) to verify union elections and register collective bargaining agreements; and establish the "legitimation" process requiring existing unions to verify their contracts through worker votes by May 2023.

The USMCA Rapid Response Labor Mechanism, used by the United States to address specific labor rights violations at individual facilities, was invoked over 20 times between 2021 and 2025, including against General Motors' Silao plant, Tridonex auto parts manufacturer, and multiple maquiladora operations, demonstrating the intersection of trade policy and labor rights enforcement.

## Southeast Asia

### Indonesia's Omnibus Law Controversy

Indonesia's Job Creation Law (UU Cipta Kerja, Law No. 11 of 2020) -- commonly called the "Omnibus Law" -- amended approximately 79 existing laws across 11 regulatory clusters, with labor provisions among the most controversial. Key labor changes include: modified severance pay calculations reducing maximum severance from 32 months to 19 months of wages (amending Article 156 of Manpower Law No. 13/2003); elimination of the cap on fixed-term contract duration (previously limited to 3 years under the Manpower Law); modification of the minimum wage formula to be based on economic growth and inflation (removing the component based on "decent living needs" surveys); and expanded outsourcing of previously restricted activities.

The Constitutional Court in Decision No. 91/PUU-XVIII/2020 (November 25, 2021) declared the law "conditionally unconstitutional" due to deficiencies in the legislative process, including inadequate public participation and transparency. The Court ordered the government to remedy these defects within two years or the law would become "permanently unconstitutional." Rather than follow the legislative process, President Jokowi issued Government Regulation in Lieu of Law (Perppu) No. 2 of 2022 on December 30, 2022, subsequently ratified by the DPR as Law No. 6 of 2023. This controversial approach was challenged before the Constitutional Court, which in Decision No. 54/PUU-XXI/2023 upheld the constitutionality of the Perppu/Law.

Mass protests against the Omnibus Law in October 2020 -- the largest labor mobilizations in Indonesia since the 1998 reformasi -- involved millions of workers organized by confederations including KSPI (Confederation of Indonesian Trade Unions) and KSBSI (Indonesian Prosperous Trade Union Confederation). The Omnibus Law also established a "job loss guarantee" (Jaminan Kehilangan Pekerjaan, JKP) program providing cash benefits, training access, and job placement services for terminated workers.

### Philippine Contractualization and Labor Protection

The Philippines' labor framework is rooted in Article XIII, Section 3 of the Constitution: "The State shall afford full protection to labor, local and overseas, organized and unorganized, and promote full employment and equality of employment opportunities for all." The Labor Code (Presidential Decree No. 442, 1974) establishes protections including security of tenure (Article 294), prohibition of unfair labor practices (Articles 258-259), and regulation of collective bargaining (Articles 261-275).

The practice of "contractualization" or "endo" (end-of-contract, referring to the termination of workers before the six-month period after which they would achieve regular status and security of tenure under Article 295) has been the most contentious labor issue. DOLE Department Order No. 174, Series of 2017, tightened restrictions on labor-only contracting but did not prohibit all forms of contracting. Executive Order No. 51 (2018), issued by President Duterte, strengthened protections against illegal contracting but fell short of the comprehensive ban on contractualization that labor groups demanded.

The Overseas Filipino Workers (OFW) framework represents a unique dimension of Philippine labor law. The Migrant Workers and Overseas Filipinos Act (Republic Act No. 8042, 1995, as amended by RA 10022, 2010) and the Department of Migrant Workers Act (RA 11641, 2022) establish protections for the approximately 1.96 million OFWs (2022 PSA data) whose remittances constituted approximately 8.9% of GDP. The Overseas Workers Welfare Administration (OWWA) provides insurance, repatriation assistance, and welfare services.

## Comparative Analysis

### The Informality Challenge

The defining feature of Global South labor markets is the overwhelming scale of informal employment -- approximately 90% in India, 80% in Nigeria, 60% in Indonesia and Colombia, and 39% in Brazil. Formal labor law frameworks, however sophisticated, reach only a fraction of workers. China's hukou system creates a distinctive form of "internal informality" where workers are formally employed but excluded from local social protections. The Global South's labor law challenge is thus fundamentally different from the Western model: not simply regulating the employment relationship, but extending protections to workers outside traditional employment relationships entirely.

### Trade Union Independence

The spectrum of trade union freedom across these jurisdictions reveals starkly different models. China's ACFTU monopoly represents one extreme -- institutional subordination to the ruling party. Brazil's unicidade sindical (constitutionally mandated single union per category per territory) represents a corporatist middle ground, though unions retain genuine bargaining power. India's multiplicity of politically affiliated union federations creates fragmentation that weakens collective bargaining despite formal freedom. South Africa's COSATU, embedded in the ANC-SACP tripartite alliance, demonstrates both the power and constraints of unions aligned with governing parties. Mexico's 2019 reform, mandating democratic union elections and dismantling the protection contract system, represents the most significant recent expansion of union democracy in the Global South.

### Labor Deregulation Trends

The 2017 Brazilian reform, the Indonesian Omnibus Law, and India's Industrial Relations Code share a common deregulatory logic: reducing employment protection to increase labor market flexibility and attract investment. The counter-trend is visible in Mexico's 2019 reform (strengthening union democracy), Indonesia's Constitutional Court decisions, and South Africa's ongoing BBBEE framework. The tension between labor flexibility and worker protection remains the central axis of labor law contestation across the Global South.
