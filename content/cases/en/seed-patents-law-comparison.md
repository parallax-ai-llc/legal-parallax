## Summary

Seed patents and plant variety protection (PVP) represent one of the most contentious intersections of intellectual property law, agricultural policy, and human rights. The legal frameworks governing who may own, reproduce, and sell plant genetic material vary significantly across jurisdictions, reflecting deep tensions between incentivizing innovation in plant breeding, ensuring food security, and protecting traditional farmer practices of seed saving and exchange. South Korea regulates plant variety protection through the Seed Industry Act (종자산업법) and the Plant Variety Protection Act (식물신품종 보호법), implementing the 1991 Act of the UPOV Convention (International Union for the Protection of New Varieties of Plants) while maintaining restrictions on patent protection for plant varieties under the Patent Act (특허법). The United States provides dual protection through the Plant Patent Act of 1930 (35 U.S.C. Sections 161-164), the Plant Variety Protection Act of 1970 (7 U.S.C. Sections 2321-2583), and utility patents on plants following Diamond v. Chakrabarty, 447 U.S. 303 (1980) and J.E.M. Ag Supply, Inc. v. Pioneer Hi-Bred International, Inc., 534 U.S. 124 (2001). Germany implements the EU Community Plant Variety Rights (CPVR) Regulation (EC) No. 2100/94 alongside the German Plant Variety Protection Act (Sortenschutzgesetz, SortSchG) and the Patent Act (Patentgesetz, PatG), while the Directive 98/44/EC on the legal protection of biotechnological inventions establishes the EU framework for biotech patents. At the international level, the UPOV Convention (1961, revised 1972, 1978, 1991), the Agreement on Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights (TRIPS), and the International Treaty on Plant Genetic Resources for Food and Agriculture (ITPGRFA) establish the multilateral framework, while the European Patent Convention (EPC) and decisions of the Enlarged Board of Appeal of the European Patent Office shape patent eligibility for plants.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's plant variety protection system reflects a relatively recent modernization driven by UPOV accession and international trade commitments, particularly the Korea-US Free Trade Agreement (KORUS FTA) and WTO/TRIPS obligations.

### Plant Variety Protection Act (식물신품종 보호법)

Enacted in 1995 and substantially revised in 2001 to comply with the UPOV 1991 Act, the Plant Variety Protection Act grants breeders exclusive rights over new plant varieties that satisfy the criteria of novelty, distinctness, uniformity, and stability (DUS). The Korea Seed & Variety Service (국립종자원) under the Ministry of Agriculture, Food and Rural Affairs administers the registration system.

**Scope of Protection:** The breeder's right covers production, reproduction, conditioning, sale, export, import, and stocking of propagating material. Since Korea's accession to UPOV 1991, protection extends to harvested material if the breeder had no reasonable opportunity to exercise rights over the propagating material.

**Farmer's Privilege:** Article 56 of the Act provides a limited farmer's privilege permitting subsistence farmers to save seed from protected varieties for replanting on their own holdings. However, this exemption does not extend to commercial seed production, and its scope has been narrowed by implementing regulations that restrict the exemption to specific crop species designated by presidential decree.

### Seed Industry Act (종자산업법)

The Seed Industry Act regulates seed certification, quality standards, and the commercial seed market. It establishes a mandatory seed certification system for major crops and provides the regulatory framework for the National Seed Master Plan (종자산업 육성 기본계획), which sets strategic priorities for Korea's seed industry development.

### Patent Protection for Plants

Under Article 32 of the Korean Patent Act (특허법), plant varieties per se are excluded from patent protection. However, biotechnological inventions, including genetic engineering techniques applied to plants and transgenic plants defined by their genetic construct rather than as a variety, may be patentable. The Korean Intellectual Property Office (KIPO) has issued examination guidelines clarifying the boundary between patentable biotechnological inventions and non-patentable plant varieties.

### Key Developments

**Korea Seed & Variety Service Registration Statistics:** As of 2025, over 12,000 plant variety rights have been granted in Korea, with significant applications in rice, pepper, chrysanthemum, and rose varieties. Korean breeders have increasingly sought protection abroad, particularly through the Community Plant Variety Office (CPVO) in the EU.

**GMO Regulation:** South Korea's Living Modified Organisms Act (유전자변형생물체의 국가간 이동 등에 관한 법률) implements the Cartagena Protocol on Biosafety. Korea imports significant quantities of GM soybeans, corn, and cotton but does not permit commercial cultivation of GM crops, creating tension with seed patent holders seeking to enforce utility patents on GM seeds.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States provides the world's most expansive intellectual property protection for plants, offering three overlapping forms of protection: plant patents, plant variety protection certificates, and utility patents.

### Plant Patent Act of 1930

The Plant Patent Act (35 U.S.C. Sections 161-164) was the first legislation worldwide to grant patent protection for plants. It covers asexually reproduced plants (excluding tuber-propagated plants) that are distinct and new. Plant patents grant the right to exclude others from asexually reproducing the plant, selling it, or using it.

### Plant Variety Protection Act of 1970

The PVPA (7 U.S.C. Sections 2321-2583) provides certificate-based protection for sexually reproduced and tuber-propagated plant varieties meeting the DUS criteria, administered by the Plant Variety Protection Office within the USDA. The 1994 amendments brought the PVPA into closer alignment with UPOV 1991.

**Saved Seed Exemption:** Section 2543 permits farmers to save seed from protected varieties for replanting on their own farm. The Supreme Court in Bowman v. Monsanto Co., 569 U.S. 278 (2013), however, held that patent exhaustion does not permit a farmer to reproduce patented seeds through planting and harvesting, effectively limiting the saved seed exemption to PVPA-protected varieties not also covered by utility patents.

**Breeder's Exemption:** Section 2544 permits use of protected varieties for research and development of new varieties, a critical distinction from utility patent protection.

### Utility Patents on Plants

**Diamond v. Chakrabarty, 447 U.S. 303 (1980):** The Supreme Court held that living organisms are patentable subject matter under 35 U.S.C. Section 101 when they are the product of human ingenuity, opening the door to plant patents.

**J.E.M. Ag Supply, Inc. v. Pioneer Hi-Bred International, Inc., 534 U.S. 124 (2001):** The Court held that newly developed plant breeds are eligible for utility patent protection, even though the PVPA and Plant Patent Act also exist. This created the most powerful form of plant IP protection, as utility patents have no saved seed exemption and no breeder's exemption.

**Monsanto Co. v. Schmeiser, [2004] 1 SCR 902 (Canadian Supreme Court):** Although a Canadian case, Schmeiser profoundly influenced global debate on seed patents. Percy Schmeiser, a Saskatchewan canola farmer, was found to have infringed Monsanto's patent on Roundup Ready canola even though wind-blown seed contaminated his field. The court held that growing patented plants constitutes "use" of the invention under Canadian patent law, though it declined to award damages because Schmeiser had not sprayed Roundup herbicide and thus derived no benefit from the patented trait.

**Bowman v. Monsanto Co., 569 U.S. 278 (2013):** The Supreme Court unanimously held that patent exhaustion does not extend to the self-replicating nature of seeds. Farmer Vernon Bowman purchased commodity soybeans from a grain elevator and planted them, knowing they likely contained Roundup Ready genetics. The Court ruled this constituted unauthorized "making" of a new patented article.

### Farmer Seed Saving Litigation

The consolidation of the seed industry has generated extensive litigation. Monsanto (now Bayer) filed over 140 seed patent infringement lawsuits against American farmers between 1997 and 2016. The Center for Food Safety organized a preemptive challenge in Organic Seed Growers and Trade Association v. Monsanto Co., 718 F.3d 1350 (Fed. Cir. 2013), where the Federal Circuit declined to provide a declaratory judgment that organic farmers would not infringe, though Monsanto's covenant not to sue farmers with trace contamination was deemed binding.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany provides plant variety protection through the Sortenschutzgesetz (SortSchG) implementing the UPOV Convention, while patent protection is governed by the Patentgesetz (PatG) and the European Patent Convention (EPC). The interaction between these systems reflects broader European debates about the boundaries of patent protection for biological material.

### Sortenschutzgesetz (SortSchG)

The German Plant Variety Protection Act, administered by the Bundessortenamt (Federal Plant Variety Office), grants breeders exclusive rights over protected varieties meeting the DUS criteria plus novelty. Protection lasts 25 years (30 years for hops, potatoes, vines, and tree species).

**Breeder's Exemption:** Section 10a SortSchG provides a robust breeder's exemption, permitting the use of protected varieties for the development of new varieties without authorization from the right holder. This exemption is considered fundamental to the European PVP system and distinguishes it from the utility patent regime.

**Farm Saved Seed:** Section 10a(3) SortSchG, implementing Article 14 of the CPVR Regulation, permits farmers to save seed of listed agricultural species for replanting on their own holdings, subject to payment of equitable remuneration to the right holder. Small farmers (as defined by EU regulation) are exempt from remuneration. The practical enforcement of this provision has generated significant litigation.

### Patent Protection and the Essentially Biological Processes Debate

**Directive 98/44/EC on Biotechnological Inventions:** Transposed into German law through amendments to the PatG, this directive permits patents on biological material that is isolated from its natural environment or produced by means of a technical process. However, Article 4 excludes plant and animal varieties and essentially biological processes for producing plants or animals from patent eligibility.

**Broccoli/Tomatoes Cases (G 2/12 and G 2/13):** The Enlarged Board of Appeal of the European Patent Office held in 2015 that plants obtained by essentially biological processes (crossing and selection) are not patentable, even if the claims are directed to the product rather than the process. However, this left open whether products defined by their genetic characteristics (without reference to the process) could be patented.

**G 3/19 (Pepper Case):** In May 2020, the Enlarged Board confirmed that under amended Rule 28(2) EPC, plants and animals exclusively obtained by essentially biological processes are excluded from patentability even when claimed as products. This decision resolved a major controversy and significantly limited the scope of plant patents in Europe.

### German Case Law

**Bundesgerichtshof (BGH), X ZR 136/13 (2016) -- Tomaten II:** The BGH referred questions to the Enlarged Board regarding the patentability of plants obtained through essentially biological processes, contributing to the G 3/19 decision.

**Enforcement of Farm Saved Seed Obligations:** The Saatgut-Treuhandverwaltungs-GmbH (STV), acting on behalf of plant breeders, regularly pursues claims against farmers for unpaid farm saved seed remuneration. German courts have upheld the obligation of farmers to provide information about their use of protected varieties and to pay reasonable compensation.

## ECHR & EU

### UPOV Convention Framework

The International Union for the Protection of New Varieties of Plants (UPOV) establishes the international standard for plant variety protection. The 1991 Act, to which 78 states and the EU are party, requires members to provide breeders with exclusive rights over protected varieties, subject to mandatory exceptions for private/non-commercial use and experimental use, and optional exceptions for farm saved seed.

**UPOV 1978 vs. 1991 Act:** The 1978 Act provided a broader farmer's privilege (automatic right to save seed) and did not extend protection to harvested material. The 1991 Act narrowed the farmer's privilege to an optional exception, extended protection to harvested material and essentially derived varieties, and strengthened the breeder's rights.

### EU Community Plant Variety Rights (CPVR) Regulation

Regulation (EC) No. 2100/94, as amended, establishes a unitary Community plant variety right valid throughout the EU, administered by the Community Plant Variety Office (CPVO) in Angers, France.

**Article 13 -- Scope of Rights:** The CPVR holder has exclusive rights over production, reproduction, conditioning, sale, export, import, and stocking of propagating material. Protection extends to harvested material and products made directly from harvested material under certain conditions.

**Article 14 -- Farm Saved Seed:** The regulation permits farmers to use the product of harvest for propagation on their own holding, subject to the payment of equitable remuneration to the right holder. Only varieties of listed agricultural species (including wheat, barley, oats, rye, triticale, rice, potatoes, rapeseed, and field peas) qualify for this exception. Small farmers are exempted from the remuneration obligation.

**Article 15 -- Breeder's Exemption:** Acts done for breeding and exploiting new varieties are not subject to the CPVR holder's authorization, unless the new variety is an essentially derived variety (EDV). The concept of EDV, introduced by UPOV 1991, prevents cosmetic breeding that produces varieties predominantly derived from the protected variety while retaining its essential characteristics.

### Directive 98/44/EC on Biotechnological Inventions

This directive harmonizes the patentability of biotechnological inventions across EU member states while establishing exclusions for plant and animal varieties and essentially biological processes.

**Article 4(1)(a):** Plant and animal varieties are not patentable.

**Article 4(1)(b):** Essentially biological processes for the production of plants or animals are not patentable. A process is essentially biological if it consists entirely of natural phenomena such as crossing or selection.

**Article 4(2):** Inventions concerning plants or animals are patentable if the technical feasibility of the invention is not confined to a particular plant or animal variety.

**Court of Justice of the EU -- Spain v. Council (C-377/98, 2001):** The CJEU upheld the validity of Directive 98/44/EC against challenges that it violated fundamental rights, finding that it appropriately balanced innovation incentives with ethical concerns and access to biological material.

### ITPGRFA and Multilateral System

The International Treaty on Plant Genetic Resources for Food and Agriculture (ITPGRFA, 2004) establishes a multilateral system for access and benefit-sharing for 64 crops and forages listed in Annex I. The treaty recognizes farmers' rights (Article 9), including the right to save, use, exchange, and sell farm-saved seed, though implementation is left to national legislation.

### ECHR Considerations

**Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (Protection of Property):** Plant variety rights and patents constitute "possessions" protected under the Convention. The ECHR has recognized in cases such as Anheuser-Busch Inc. v. Portugal (Application No. 73049/01, Grand Chamber, January 11, 2007) that intellectual property rights, including applications for registration, fall within the scope of property protection.

**Article 8 (Right to Private and Family Life):** Environmental degradation, including from industrial agriculture practices associated with monoculture seed systems, may engage Article 8 rights. The Court has held in Hatton v. United Kingdom (Application No. 36022/97, 2003) that states must strike a fair balance between economic interests and environmental protection.

## Comparative Analysis

### Patent vs. PVP Protection

The most fundamental divergence among the examined jurisdictions concerns whether plants may receive patent protection in addition to or instead of plant variety protection. The United States uniquely permits utility patents on plant varieties, providing the strongest protection with no saved seed exemption and no breeder's exemption. The EU and its member states explicitly exclude plant varieties from patent protection, confining IP rights for plant varieties to the PVP system with its mandatory breeder's exemption and optional farmer's privilege. South Korea follows the European approach of excluding plant varieties from patents while permitting patents on biotechnological processes and transgenic constructs.

### Farmer Seed Saving Rights

The treatment of farmer seed saving illustrates the tension between breeder incentives and agricultural autonomy. Under the US utility patent system, farmer seed saving of patented varieties is categorically prohibited, as confirmed by Bowman v. Monsanto. Under the EU CPVR and German SortSchG, seed saving is permitted for listed crop species subject to remuneration obligations. South Korea provides a narrower farmer's privilege limited to subsistence farmers. Developing countries and civil society organizations have criticized the UPOV 1991 framework for restricting traditional seed saving practices that are fundamental to smallholder agriculture.

### Essentially Biological Processes

The European exclusion of essentially biological processes and their products from patent protection (following G 3/19) represents a significant limitation on plant patents that has no counterpart in US law. This exclusion reflects concerns that patents on conventionally bred plants would grant rights far broader than those available under the PVP system, undermining the breeder's exemption and farmer's privilege that are foundational to the PVP framework.

### Corporate Concentration and Access

The global seed industry has undergone extreme consolidation, with four companies (Bayer/Monsanto, Corteva, BASF, and Syngenta/ChemChina) controlling approximately 60% of the global proprietary seed market. This concentration raises competition concerns across all jurisdictions and has prompted regulatory responses including merger conditions imposed by the European Commission in Bayer/Monsanto (Case M.8084, 2018) and the US Department of Justice.

### Indigenous and Traditional Knowledge

The intersection of seed patents with traditional knowledge and indigenous rights remains inadequately addressed across all jurisdictions. The Nagoya Protocol on Access and Benefit-Sharing (2010) and the ITPGRFA provide frameworks for benefit-sharing but have been criticized as insufficient to prevent biopiracy -- the appropriation of traditional plant genetic resources through IP systems.
