## Summary

Loitering and vagrancy laws -- which criminalize the act of remaining in a place without apparent purpose or the condition of lacking a settled home and visible means of support -- have a long and contested history across legal systems, serving alternatively as tools for public order maintenance, mechanisms of social control over marginalized populations, and targets of constitutional and human rights challenges. South Korea addresses loitering through the Minor Offenses Act (경범죄처벌법), which penalizes behaviors such as obstruction of passage (통행방해) and causing unease by loitering without apparent reason (불안감 조성), while vagrancy per se is not criminalized; Korean law has historically addressed homelessness and vagrancy through social welfare legislation rather than criminal sanctions, though the legacy of the Brothers Home (형제복지원) scandal of the 1970s-1980s, in which thousands of vagrants, homeless persons, and others were forcibly detained and subjected to forced labor and abuse, continues to inform Korean approaches to vagrancy policy. The United States has seen the most dramatic constitutional confrontation with loitering and vagrancy laws, with the Supreme Court striking down traditional vagrancy statutes as unconstitutionally vague in Papachristou v. City of Jacksonville, 405 U.S. 156 (1972) and invalidating an anti-loitering ordinance targeting suspected gang members in City of Chicago v. Morales, 527 U.S. 41 (1999); despite these constraints, cities continue to adopt anti-loitering ordinances targeting specific behaviors such as drug-related loitering, prostitution-related loitering, and aggressive panhandling, while the Supreme Court's 2024 decision in City of Grants Pass v. Johnson significantly expanded governmental authority to enforce anti-camping ordinances against homeless persons. Germany does not criminalize vagrancy or simple loitering, having historically addressed these issues through the now-reformed Landstreichereitatbestand (vagrancy offense); contemporary German law treats homelessness as a social welfare issue, with municipalities bearing obligations under social welfare codes (Sozialgesetzbuch) to provide shelter, and the Federal Constitutional Court having established a right to a dignified minimum existence (menschenwürdiges Existenzminimum) under Article 1(1) in conjunction with Article 20(1) GG. The ECHR has addressed vagrancy through Article 5(1)(e), which expressly permits the detention of "vagrants," though the Court has interpreted this provision narrowly in cases such as De Wilde, Ooms and Versyp v. Belgium (1971) and subsequent jurisprudence has raised questions about whether the term "vagrant" retains contemporary applicability.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea does not criminalize vagrancy as a status offense. Loitering-related conduct is addressed through specific behavioral prohibitions in the Minor Offenses Act:

**Minor Offenses Act (경범죄처벌법), Relevant Provisions:**
- Article 3, No. 19: Obstruction of passage (통행방해)
- Article 3, No. 20: Causing unease by loitering without apparent reason near another person's home or business (불안감 조성)
- Article 3, No. 40: Persistent following or accosting of another person (지속적 괴롭힘)

These provisions target specific behaviors rather than the status of being homeless or idle.

### The Brothers Home Scandal (형제복지원)

The Brothers Home (형제복지원) in Busan represents one of the darkest chapters in Korean vagrancy policy. Operating from the 1960s to 1987:

**Facts:** Under the Park Chung-hee and Chun Doo-hwan military dictatorships, the government pursued "purification" (정화) campaigns that included rounding up vagrants, homeless persons, street children, and disabled individuals. Many were forcibly sent to the Brothers Home, a nominally charitable institution that functioned as a forced labor camp.

**Scale:** An estimated 3,000-4,000 inmates were held at any given time. Government records indicate at least 513 deaths at the facility. Former inmates and investigations have documented systematic physical abuse, sexual violence, and forced labor.

**2018-2022 Truth and Reconciliation Commission:** The Truth and Reconciliation Commission (진실·화해를위한과거사정리위원회) investigated the Brothers Home and determined that the government bore responsibility for the systematic human rights violations. In 2022, the Commission recommended that the government apologize and provide compensation to survivors.

**Legislative Response:** The scandal has contributed to South Korea's commitment to treating homelessness and vagrancy through social welfare rather than criminal enforcement. The Act on Welfare of Homeless Persons (노숙인 등의 복지 및 자립지원에 관한 법률, 2011) established a comprehensive framework for shelters, services, and rehabilitation.

### Current Homelessness Policy

The Ministry of Health and Welfare maintains a network of shelters and services:

| Facility Type | Number (approx.) | Function |
|--------------|-------------------|----------|
| Emergency shelters (긴급쉼터) | ~100 | Immediate overnight shelter |
| Transitional housing (자활시설) | ~50 | Medium-term housing with support |
| Rehabilitation centers (재활시설) | ~30 | Addiction and mental health treatment |
| Day centers (주간보호시설) | ~40 | Daytime services, meals, medical care |

## United States

### Constitutional Framework

**Papachristou v. City of Jacksonville, 405 U.S. 156 (1972):** Justice Douglas, writing for a unanimous Court, struck down Jacksonville's vagrancy ordinance, which criminalized "rogues and vagabonds," "common drunkards," "persons wandering or strolling around from place to place without any lawful purpose," and other vaguely defined categories. The Court held that the ordinance was void for vagueness because it "fails to give a person of ordinary intelligence fair notice that his contemplated conduct is forbidden" and "encourages arbitrary and erratic arrests and convictions."

**City of Chicago v. Morales, 527 U.S. 41 (1999):** The Court struck down Chicago's Gang Congregation Ordinance, which prohibited "criminal street gang members" from loitering in public places. The plurality held that the ordinance was unconstitutionally vague because it provided insufficient guidance for determining when loitering had occurred and gave police excessive discretion.

**City of Grants Pass v. Johnson, 603 U.S. ___ (2024):** In a 6-3 decision, the Supreme Court held that enforcement of public camping ordinances against homeless persons does not constitute cruel and unusual punishment under the Eighth Amendment, even when insufficient shelter is available. This overturned the Ninth Circuit's holding in Martin v. City of Boise, 920 F.3d 584 (9th Cir. 2019), which had held that the Eighth Amendment prohibits enforcement of anti-camping ordinances when no adequate shelter is available. The Grants Pass decision significantly expanded local governments' authority to criminalize sleeping in public spaces.

### Surviving Anti-Loitering Ordinances

Despite Papachristou and Morales, many jurisdictions maintain targeted anti-loitering ordinances that survive constitutional challenge by incorporating specific behavioral elements:

| Type | Typical Elements | Constitutional Status |
|------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Drug-related loitering | Loitering + specified drug-related behavior | Generally upheld |
| Prostitution-related loitering | Loitering + specific solicitation indicators | Split; some struck down |
| Curfew ordinances | Minor present in public during specified hours | Generally upheld with exceptions |
| Anti-panhandling | Aggressive solicitation in specific locations | Split; passive panhandling protected |

### Homelessness and Criminalization

The criminalization of homelessness through loitering, camping, and public sleeping ordinances has generated significant controversy:

**National Homelessness Law Center (2023):** Reported that cities are increasingly adopting anti-camping, anti-sleeping, and anti-sitting ordinances, with enforcement disproportionately affecting communities of color and persons with mental illness.

**Housing First Model:** Jurisdictions including Houston, Virginia, and Salt Lake City have adopted Housing First approaches that prioritize providing permanent housing without preconditions, demonstrating that decriminalization combined with housing services reduces both homelessness and related public order issues.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany does not criminalize vagrancy or simple loitering. The historical vagrancy offense (Landstreicherei) was abolished through criminal law reform.

### Historical Context

**Landstreicherei (Vagrancy):** The historical German vagrancy offense, originally included in the Reichsstrafgesetzbuch of 1871, was progressively reformed:
- The 1871 provision criminalized "persons who have no permanent residence and do not pursue a trade, and who cannot demonstrate that they have lawful means of support"
- Under the Nazi regime (1933-1945), vagrancy was among the grounds for indefinite preventive detention and concentration camp internment
- Post-war reforms progressively narrowed the offense
- The offense was effectively abolished through the 1974 criminal law reform

### Constitutional Framework

**Article 1(1) GG -- Human Dignity:** "Human dignity shall be inviolable." Combined with Article 20(1) GG (social state principle), the Federal Constitutional Court has derived a constitutional right to a dignified minimum existence (menschenwürdiges Existenzminimum).

**BVerfG, Judgment of February 9, 2010 (1 BvL 1/09 -- Hartz IV):** The Court held that the legislature is constitutionally required to ensure a minimum standard of living that corresponds to human dignity. While this case addressed welfare benefits rather than vagrancy directly, it established the principle that the state bears a constitutional obligation to prevent destitution.

**Article 2(1) GG -- General Freedom of Action:** Protects the right to remain in public places as an aspect of general freedom of action.

**Article 11 GG -- Freedom of Movement:** Guarantees freedom of movement throughout the federal territory.

### Social Welfare Framework

German law addresses homelessness through the Sozialgesetzbuch (Social Code):

**SGB XII -- Social Assistance (Sozialhilfe):** Municipalities are obligated to provide emergency shelter to persons who are homeless and at risk of exposure. This obligation derives from the right to human dignity and the social state principle.

**SGB II -- Basic Provision for Jobseekers (Grundsicherung für Arbeitsuchende):** Provides benefits including housing costs for unemployed persons.

### Municipal Police Law

While vagrancy is not criminalized, municipal police law (Polizeirecht) allows authorities to address public order concerns:
- **Platzverweis (Place Removal Order):** Police may order persons to leave specific locations if their presence creates a concrete danger to public safety
- **Aufenthaltsverbot (Residence Ban):** More extensive prohibition on entering specific areas, used primarily for repeat offenders; subject to strict proportionality review

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

**Article 5(1)(e) -- Detention of Vagrants:** The ECHR expressly lists "vagrants" among the categories of persons who may be lawfully detained. This provision reflects the Convention's 1950 drafting context, when vagrancy laws were common across Europe.

**De Wilde, Ooms and Versyp v. Belgium (Application Nos. 2832/66, 2835/66, 2899/66, Judgment of June 18, 1971):** The Court addressed the detention of vagrants under Belgian law. While finding no violation of Article 5(1), the Court found violations of Article 5(4) (right to challenge detention) because the applicants had no effective means of challenging their detention before a court. The judgment established important procedural safeguards for the detention of vagrants.

**Lacatus v. Switzerland (Application No. 14065/15, Judgment of January 19, 2021):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 (right to private life) where the applicant, a homeless Romanian Roma woman, was repeatedly fined for begging in Geneva. The Court held that begging constitutes a means of survival for persons in extreme poverty and that criminal sanctions for begging interfere with the right to private life, requiring justification under Article 8(2).

**Article 3 -- Prohibition of Torture and Inhuman Treatment:** Relevant where conditions of detention for vagrants or treatment of homeless persons reaches the Article 3 threshold.

**Article 14 -- Prohibition of Discrimination:** Enforcement of loitering and vagrancy laws may violate Article 14 if disproportionately applied against particular groups (Roma, ethnic minorities, persons with disabilities).

### EU Framework

**European Parliament Resolution on Homelessness (2020):** Called on member states to adopt strategies for ending homelessness by 2030, emphasizing housing-led approaches and the decriminalization of homelessness.

**European Platform on Combating Homelessness (Lisbon Declaration, 2021):** All 27 EU member states committed to working toward ending homelessness by 2030, endorsing the principles that homelessness should never be criminalized and that Housing First approaches should be prioritized.

**EU Charter of Fundamental Rights:**
- Article 1: Human dignity is inviolable
- Article 34(3): The Union recognizes and respects the right to social and housing assistance

## Comparative Analysis

### Status vs. Conduct

The fundamental question across all jurisdictions is whether the law targets a status (being homeless, idle, or without means) or specific conduct (obstructing passage, aggressive panhandling, camping in prohibited areas):

- **South Korea:** Conduct-based; specific behaviors criminalized
- **United States:** Post-Papachristou, must be conduct-based; Grants Pass allows broader enforcement
- **Germany:** No status-based offenses; conduct addressed through proportionate police measures
- **ECHR:** Article 5(1)(e) permits status-based detention of "vagrants" but narrowly interpreted

### Social Welfare vs. Criminal Justice

The jurisdictions represent different points on the welfare-punishment spectrum:

- **Germany (most welfare-oriented):** Constitutional right to minimum existence; municipal shelter obligations
- **South Korea (welfare-oriented with historical scars):** Social welfare approach driven partly by Brothers Home scandal
- **ECHR/EU (aspirational):** Lisbon Declaration commitment to ending homelessness by 2030
- **United States (most punitive):** Grants Pass expands criminalization; significant variation by locality

### Historical Accountability

Both South Korea and Germany have confronted historical abuses of vagrancy laws:
- South Korea: Brothers Home investigation and truth commission findings
- Germany: Reckoning with Nazi-era persecution of "asocials" including vagrants
