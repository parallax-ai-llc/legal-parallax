## Summary

Prosecutorial discretion -- the authority of prosecutors to decide whether to initiate, continue, or terminate criminal proceedings, what charges to bring, what plea agreements to accept, and what sentences to recommend -- is one of the most powerful and least visible forces in criminal justice systems worldwide, directly determining the outcomes of individual cases while shaping the broader patterns of enforcement, punishment, and justice across entire societies. South Korea operates under a hybrid system in which the prosecution (검찰) traditionally held a monopoly on the authority to indict (기소독점주의) and exercised broad discretion over charging decisions (기소편의주의), as codified in Article 247 of the Criminal Procedure Act (형사소송법), but this monopoly has been substantially curtailed by the establishment of the Corruption Investigation Office for High-ranking Officials (고위공직자범죄수사처, CIO) in 2021 and the transfer of primary investigative authority from prosecutors to police under the 2022 reform of the Prosecutors' Office Act (검찰청법) and the Criminal Procedure Act -- reforms that have fundamentally restructured the balance of power in Korean criminal justice. The United States vests the broadest prosecutorial discretion of any jurisdiction in both federal and state prosecutors, with the Supreme Court recognizing virtually unreviewable charging discretion in cases such as United States v. Armstrong, 517 U.S. 456 (1996) and Wayte v. United States, 470 U.S. 598 (1985), while plea bargaining -- in which approximately 97% of federal criminal cases and 94% of state cases are resolved without trial -- concentrates extraordinary power in the hands of prosecutors. Germany operates under the principle of mandatory prosecution (Legalitätsprinzip) codified in Section 152(2) StPO, which obligates prosecutors to prosecute all prosecutable offenses where sufficient evidence exists, though significant exceptions under Sections 153-154e StPO (particularly Section 153a permitting conditional dismissal) create substantial de facto discretion despite the formal commitment to mandatory prosecution. The ECHR has addressed prosecutorial discretion primarily through the positive obligations doctrine under Articles 2 and 3, requiring effective investigation and prosecution of serious offenses, and through Article 6 principles on equality of arms and disclosure obligations.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's prosecution system has undergone the most dramatic structural transformation of any of the jurisdictions examined over the past five years, shifting from a prosecutor-dominated system to one with significantly distributed authority.

### Traditional Prosecutorial Power

Historically, Korean prosecutors held three distinctive powers:
1. **Monopoly on indictment (기소독점주의):** Only prosecutors could initiate criminal proceedings (Article 246 CPA)
2. **Discretion over prosecution (기소편의주의):** Prosecutors could decline to prosecute even when evidence was sufficient (Article 247 CPA)
3. **Direction of investigations (수사지휘권):** Prosecutors directed police investigations and had independent investigative authority

### 2020-2022 Reforms

**Corruption Investigation Office for High-ranking Officials (고위공직자범죄수사처, CIO):** Established by the Act on the Establishment and Operation of the CIO (고위공직자범죄수사처 설치 및 운영에 관한 법률, 2020), effective January 2021. The CIO has independent authority to investigate and prosecute corruption by high-ranking public officials, breaking the prosecution's monopoly for the first time.

**Transfer of Investigative Authority (2022):** Amendments to the Prosecutors' Office Act and the Criminal Procedure Act, effective September 2022, designated police as primary investigators and restricted prosecutors' investigative authority to six categories of offenses (corruption, economic crimes, election crimes, public safety crimes, drug crimes, and crimes by defense industry officials). This reform fundamentally restructured the prosecutor-police relationship.

### Checks on Prosecutorial Discretion

**Article 260 Complaint (재정신청):** Victims may challenge a prosecutor's decision not to prosecute by filing an application with the High Court, which reviews whether the non-prosecution decision was justified. If the court finds the decision unjustified, it orders the case to proceed to trial. This mechanism provides the most direct judicial check on non-prosecution decisions of any jurisdiction examined.

**Prosecutorial Review Committee (검찰시민위원회):** Established in 2018, this committee of external members reviews prosecutorial decisions in cases of public interest, providing a form of civilian oversight over charging discretion.

### Plea Bargaining

South Korea does not have a formal plea bargaining system comparable to the United States. However, informal negotiations between defense counsel and prosecutors regarding charges and sentencing recommendations occur, and the 2020 reform of the Criminal Procedure Act introduced provisions for expedited proceedings in cases where the defendant admits the charges.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States vests the broadest prosecutorial discretion of any developed legal system, reflecting both the adversarial tradition and the unique feature of elected state prosecutors.

### Constitutional Framework

The prosecution function is rooted in Article II of the Constitution (executive power), with federal prosecution authority delegated to U.S. Attorneys under 28 U.S.C. Section 547 and to the Attorney General under 28 U.S.C. Section 515. State prosecution authority derives from state constitutions and statutes.

### Scope of Discretion

**United States v. Armstrong, 517 U.S. 456 (1996):** The Supreme Court reaffirmed the "broad discretion" of prosecutors in charging decisions, holding that a defendant challenging selective prosecution must demonstrate both discriminatory effect and discriminatory purpose -- a standard that courts have found nearly impossible to meet.

**Wayte v. United States, 470 U.S. 598 (1985):** The Court held that the decision to prosecute is "particularly ill-suited to judicial review" and that courts should not scrutinize the reasons behind prosecution decisions unless there is evidence of constitutional violation.

### Plea Bargaining

Plea bargaining dominates the American criminal justice system:

| Metric | Percentage |
|--------|-----------|
| Federal cases resolved by guilty plea | Approximately 97% |
| State cases resolved by guilty plea | Approximately 94% |
| Federal cases proceeding to trial | Approximately 3% |

**Types of plea agreements:**
- Charge bargaining: agreement to plead guilty to a lesser offense
- Sentence bargaining: agreement on a recommended sentence
- Fact bargaining: agreement on which facts to present to the court
- Cooperation agreements: reduced charges or sentences in exchange for testimony

**Brady v. United States, 397 U.S. 742 (1970):** The Supreme Court held that guilty pleas induced by the threat of more severe punishment at trial are not per se involuntary, establishing the constitutional foundation for plea bargaining.

**Bordenkircher v. Hayes, 434 U.S. 357 (1978):** The Court upheld the practice of threatening to charge a defendant as a habitual offender to induce a guilty plea, holding that prosecutorial vindictiveness does not apply in the plea bargaining context.

### Progressive Prosecution Movement

Since approximately 2016, a growing number of "progressive prosecutors" (including Larry Krasner in Philadelphia, Kim Foxx in Chicago, and George Gascón in Los Angeles) have used prosecutorial discretion to decline prosecution for minor drug offenses, reduce reliance on cash bail, and limit the use of mandatory minimums -- demonstrating the enormous policy-shaping power of prosecutorial discretion.

### Accountability Concerns

The United States lacks effective external oversight of prosecutorial discretion:
- Judicial review of charging decisions is virtually nonexistent
- Internal DOJ guidelines are not enforceable by defendants
- Elected state prosecutors face accountability only through elections
- Prosecutorial misconduct, including Brady violations, is rarely sanctioned

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's Legalitätsprinzip (legality principle or mandatory prosecution principle) represents the conceptual opposite of the American opportunity principle, requiring prosecutors to prosecute all offenses for which sufficient evidence exists, though significant statutory exceptions create substantial practical discretion.

### Section 152(2) StPO -- Legality Principle

Section 152(2) StPO provides: "Except as otherwise provided by law, [the prosecution] is obliged to take action in the case of all prosecutable criminal offenses, provided that sufficient factual indications exist." This mandatory prosecution duty extends to all Verbrechen (felonies, offenses with a minimum of one year imprisonment) and Vergehen (misdemeanors).

### Exceptions Creating Discretion

**Section 153 StPO:** The prosecution may dismiss proceedings for minor offenses (Vergehen) if the offender's guilt is minor and there is no public interest in prosecution, with court approval.

**Section 153a StPO:** The prosecution may conditionally dismiss proceedings for Vergehen if the accused fulfills conditions or instructions (such as payment of a sum to the treasury, community service, or participation in a program), with court approval. This is the most widely used discretionary dismissal, processing hundreds of thousands of cases annually.

**Section 154 StPO:** The prosecution may dismiss individual charges where multiple offenses are charged and the remaining charges will result in an adequate sentence.

**Section 154a StPO:** Parts of an offense may be excluded from prosecution where they are of minor significance compared to the remaining charges.

### Verständigung (Understanding) -- Section 257c StPO

Germany introduced a statutory framework for negotiated agreements in 2009, following the Federal Constitutional Court's acknowledgment that informal negotiations had become widespread. Section 257c StPO permits the court to indicate a sentencing range contingent on the defendant's confession, with the following safeguards:
- The agreement must be disclosed in open court
- The defendant retains the right to appeal
- The court is not bound if new facts emerge
- The defendant's confession must be voluntary

In BVerfGE 133, 168 (2013), the Federal Constitutional Court upheld the constitutionality of Section 257c but imposed strict conditions, finding that the widespread deviation from statutory requirements in practice (particularly off-the-record agreements) was unconstitutional.

### Klageerzwingungsverfahren (Compelled Prosecution Procedure)

Under Section 172(2) StPO, a victim may challenge a prosecutor's dismissal decision by applying to the Oberlandesgericht (Higher Regional Court) to compel prosecution. The court reviews whether sufficient evidence exists and whether the dismissal was justified under the applicable legal standards. This mechanism provides the most robust judicial check on non-prosecution decisions among the jurisdictions examined.

### Prosecutor's Role as Master of Investigation

The German prosecutor (Staatsanwalt) serves as the "Herr des Ermittlungsverfahrens" (master of the investigation), directing police investigations and determining the scope and direction of the inquiry. Unlike the U.S. system, where prosecutors and police operate with significant independence from each other, the German system places the prosecutor in a supervisory role over investigative activities.

## ECHR & EU

### Positive Obligations Doctrine

The ECHR has addressed prosecutorial discretion primarily through the positive obligations to investigate and prosecute serious offenses:

**Osman v. United Kingdom, Application No. 23452/94, Judgment of October 28, 1998:** The Court established that Article 2 (right to life) may require effective prosecution of perpetrators, though it does not require prosecution in every individual case.

**Nachova and Others v. Bulgaria [GC], Applications Nos. 43577/98, 43579/98, Judgment of July 6, 2005:** The Court held that the procedural obligation under Article 2 requires an effective official investigation capable of leading to the identification and punishment of those responsible.

**M.C. v. Bulgaria, Application No. 39272/98, Judgment of December 4, 2003:** The Court found that the failure to effectively investigate and prosecute a sexual assault violated Article 3, establishing that prosecutorial inaction in cases of serious violence may engage Convention obligations.

### Equality of Arms and Disclosure

**Rowe and Davis v. United Kingdom [GC], Application No. 28901/95, Judgment of February 16, 2000:** The Court held that the prosecution's failure to disclose material evidence to the defense violated Article 6(1), establishing that prosecutorial discretion over disclosure is constrained by the right to a fair trial.

**Edwards and Lewis v. United Kingdom [GC], Applications Nos. 39647/98, 40461/98, Judgment of October 27, 2004:** The Court further developed disclosure obligations, holding that the prosecution must disclose evidence that might undermine the prosecution case or strengthen the defense, with any restriction on disclosure subject to strict proportionality.

### EU Framework

Regulation (EU) 2017/1939 establishing the European Public Prosecutor's Office (EPPO) created the first supranational prosecution authority in the EU, with competence over offenses affecting the financial interests of the Union. The EPPO operates under the legality principle, with obligations to investigate and prosecute where sufficient evidence exists.

## Comparative Analysis

### Discretion Models

The jurisdictions represent a spectrum from maximum discretion (United States) through substantial but checked discretion (South Korea) to formally mandatory prosecution with significant exceptions (Germany). The ECHR imposes a minimum floor through positive obligations but does not prescribe a specific model.

### Accountability Mechanisms

Germany provides the strongest accountability through the Klageerzwingungsverfahren, enabling judicial review of non-prosecution decisions. South Korea's Article 260 complaint provides a similar but somewhat less developed mechanism. The United States provides virtually no accountability, with prosecutorial discretion largely immune from judicial review.

### Plea Bargaining

The United States dominates in plea bargaining, with 94-97% of cases resolved without trial. Germany has reluctantly accepted negotiated agreements since 2009, with constitutional safeguards. South Korea has not formally adopted plea bargaining. The ECHR requires that any plea agreement process comply with Article 6 fair trial standards.

### Independence vs. Accountability

The tension between prosecutorial independence (necessary to prevent political interference) and accountability (necessary to prevent abuse) is resolved differently across jurisdictions. U.S. elected prosecutors face electoral but not judicial accountability. Korean and German career prosecutors face institutional oversight but not electoral pressure. The recent Korean reforms reflect an attempt to enhance accountability through structural redistribution of power.
