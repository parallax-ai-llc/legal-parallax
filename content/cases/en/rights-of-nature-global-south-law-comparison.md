## Summary

The legal concept of nature as a rights-bearing subject -- rather than merely an object of human property and regulatory management -- has emerged as one of the most transformative legal innovations of the 21st century, with the Global South serving as its primary laboratory. This movement challenges the foundational Western legal distinction between legal persons (subjects of rights) and things (objects of rights), drawing instead on indigenous cosmologies, ecological science, and the philosophical insight that functioning ecosystems are preconditions for all human rights. China's "ecological civilization" (shengtai wenming) doctrine, elevated to constitutional status in 2018 and operationalized through the Civil Code (2020), the Yangtze River Protection Law (2020), and the Yellow River Protection Law (2022), represents a state-directed environmental governance model that emphasizes systemic ecosystem protection without granting nature independent legal personality. India's judiciary has pushed the boundaries through landmark decisions granting legal personhood to the Ganges and Yamuna rivers (Mohd. Salim v. State of Uttarakhand, 2017, Uttarakhand High Court, later stayed by the Supreme Court) and recognizing the entire animal kingdom as a legal entity (Animal Welfare Board of India v. A. Nagaraja, 2014). South Africa's Constitution enshrines environmental rights in Section 24, with the National Environmental Management Act 107 of 1998 (NEMA) and extensive Constitutional Court jurisprudence establishing one of the strongest environmental constitutional frameworks in the world, though stopping short of granting nature independent rights. Ecuador and Bolivia represent the most radical legislative approaches: Ecuador's 2008 Constitution (Articles 71-74) is the first in the world to grant constitutional rights to Nature (Pachamama), while Bolivia's Law of the Rights of Mother Earth (Ley No. 071, 2010) and Framework Law of Mother Earth and Integral Development for Living Well (Ley No. 300, 2012) establish nature as a collective subject of public interest with enumerated rights. Colombia's Constitutional Court has developed rights-of-nature jurisprudence through the landmark Atrato River decision (Sentencia T-622/2016), which declared the river a subject of rights. The Philippines' Writ of Kalikasan (A.M. No. 09-6-8-SC, 2010), while not granting nature legal personality per se, creates a powerful procedural remedy for environmental protection that is unique in Southeast Asian law, complemented by Indonesia's growing environmental constitutionalism and the recognition of hutan adat (customary forests) through Constitutional Court Decision No. 35/PUU-X/2012.

## China

### Ecological Civilization (Shengtai Wenming)

China's environmental legal framework is distinguished by its top-down, party-state-driven character, anchored in the concept of "ecological civilization" (生态文明, shengtai wenming) -- a term first appearing in the CPC's 17th National Congress Report (2007) and progressively elevated to the highest policy level. The 2018 Constitutional amendment inserted "ecological civilization" into the Preamble, establishing it as a foundational state objective alongside material, political, cultural, and social civilization.

The ecological civilization concept, as articulated by Xi Jinping's thought on ecological civilization (习近平生态文明思想), integrates environmental protection with economic development under party leadership. Unlike the rights-of-nature approach, it does not grant nature independent legal standing but treats ecosystem protection as a state duty and collective interest administered through the party-state apparatus.

### Yangtze River Protection Law (2020)

The Yangtze River Protection Law (长江保护法), adopted by the NPC Standing Committee on December 26, 2020 (effective March 1, 2021), is China's first legislation devoted to the ecological protection of a specific river basin. Key provisions include:

- **Article 1:** Purpose: "to strengthen the ecological environment protection and restoration of the Yangtze River basin, promote rational and efficient use of resources, ensure ecological security, and realize harmonious coexistence between human and nature."
- **Article 3:** Comprehensive coordination principle -- establishing the Yangtze River basin coordination mechanism (changjiang liuyu xietiao jizhi) under the State Council.
- **Article 26:** Prohibition of new chemical industry parks and expansion of existing ones within one kilometer of the Yangtze River main stem and major tributaries (the "one-kilometer withdrawal").
- **Article 53:** Ten-year fishing ban on the main stem of the Yangtze and designated tributaries and lakes, prohibiting all natural fish resource extraction (the "ten-year fishing moratorium," commenced January 1, 2021).
- **Article 87-94:** Legal liability provisions including fines of up to 5 million RMB for illegal discharge and criminal referral for serious violations.

The Yellow River Protection Law (黄河保护法), adopted on October 30, 2022 (effective April 1, 2023), followed a similar model for the Yellow River basin, emphasizing water conservation, sediment management, and ecological restoration.

### Civil Code and Environmental Liability

The Civil Code of the PRC (2020), Book VII ("Tort Liability"), Chapter VII ("Environmental Pollution and Ecological Damage Liability"), establishes:

- **Article 1229:** Polluters who cause damage through environmental pollution or ecological destruction bear tort liability.
- **Article 1230:** Reversal of burden of proof -- the polluter bears the burden of proving that there is no causal relationship between the polluting/destructive conduct and the damage.
- **Article 1232:** Punitive damages for environmental pollution or ecological damage caused intentionally.
- **Article 1234-1235:** Ecological damage liability -- where ecological environment is damaged, the state-designated authority or organization may seek compensation for ecological restoration costs, loss of ecological service functions during the restoration period, and reasonable investigation and assessment costs. This codifies the ecological damage compensation system (生态环境损害赔偿制度) piloted since 2017.

### Environmental Public Interest Litigation

Since 2014, China has established over 2,000 specialized environmental courts and tribunals (环境资源审判庭). The Environmental Protection Law (2014 revision) established public interest environmental litigation (环境公益诉讼) standing for qualified social organizations (Article 58) and for the People's Procuratorate as part of its public interest litigation mandate (established by the 2017 amendment to the Civil Procedure Law, Article 55(2), and the Administrative Litigation Law). In 2023, procuratorial organs filed over 100,000 environmental public interest litigation cases, making China's procuratorial system the most active public interest environmental litigator in the world by volume.

## India

### Constitutional Environmental Framework

India's environmental rights framework rests on two constitutional provisions and extensive judicial interpretation:

- **Article 48A (DPSP, added by the 42nd Amendment, 1976):** "The State shall endeavour to protect and improve the environment and to safeguard the forests and wild life of the country."
- **Article 51A(g) (Fundamental Duties, added by the 42nd Amendment, 1976):** "It shall be the duty of every citizen of India to protect and improve the natural environment including forests, lakes, rivers and wild life, and to have compassion for living creatures."

The Supreme Court has read the right to a healthy environment into the right to life under Article 21 through a series of landmark decisions:

- **M.C. Mehta v. Union of India (Oleum Gas Leak Case, 1987 (1) SCC 395):** Established the doctrine of absolute liability for hazardous industries, going beyond the English Rylands v. Fletcher strict liability standard.
- **Vellore Citizens Welfare Forum v. Union of India (1996 (5) SCC 647):** The Court constitutionalized the precautionary principle and the polluter-pays principle as part of Indian environmental law.
- **T.N. Godavarman Thirumulpad v. Union of India (1997):** Initiated continuing mandamus jurisdiction over forest protection, with the Supreme Court issuing hundreds of orders over two decades directing forest policy nationwide.

### Ganges and Yamuna River Personhood

In **Mohd. Salim v. State of Uttarakhand (2017, Uttarakhand High Court, Writ Petition (PIL) No. 126 of 2014)**, a Division Bench of the Uttarakhand High Court declared the rivers Ganga and Yamuna, their tributaries, and associated natural features to be "legal persons/living entities having the status of a legal person with all corresponding rights, duties and liabilities." The Court appointed the Director of NAMAMI Gange, the Chief Secretary of Uttarakhand, and the Advocate General of Uttarakhand as "persons in loco parentis" (guardians) of the rivers.

The Court drew on New Zealand's Te Awa Tupua (Whanganui River Claims Settlement) Act 2017, which granted legal personhood to the Whanganui River, and on Ecuadorian and Colombian jurisprudence. The ruling was grounded in the Hindu religious significance of the Ganges as well as in the constitutional duty to protect the environment.

However, the Supreme Court of India **stayed the Uttarakhand High Court's order** in July 2017 following an appeal by the Uttarakhand state government, which argued that the practical implications were unworkable -- including potential tort liability for the rivers' guardians for downstream flooding damage. The appeal remains pending before the Supreme Court, leaving the legal personhood status of the Ganges and Yamuna in constitutional limbo.

### Animal Rights Jurisprudence

In **Animal Welfare Board of India v. A. Nagaraja (2014 (7) SCC 547)**, the Supreme Court banned Jallikattu (bull-taming) in Tamil Nadu, holding that the practice violated the Prevention of Cruelty to Animals Act, 1960, and recognizing that animals have a right to life under Article 21 (through a wide interpretation). Justice Radhakrishnan, writing for the bench, stated: "Every species has an inherent right to live and shall be protected by law. Animal has also honour and dignity which cannot be arbitrarily deprived of." The judgment effectively recognized the animal kingdom as having quasi-legal personhood.

Parliament subsequently enacted the Constitution (One Hundred and Nineteenth Amendment) Bill, which became the Prevention of Cruelty to Animals (Tamil Nadu Amendment) Act, 2017, specifically permitting Jallikattu as a cultural practice. The Supreme Court upheld this amendment in **A. Nagaraja v. Union of India (2023)**, creating a complex tension between animal rights, cultural rights, and legislative authority.

### National Green Tribunal

The National Green Tribunal Act, 2010, established the NGT as a specialized environmental court with jurisdiction over "substantial questions relating to the environment" arising under seven statutes (Environment Protection Act, Water Act, Air Act, Forest Conservation Act, Biodiversity Act, and the Public Liability Insurance Act). The NGT has original and appellate jurisdiction and applies the principles of sustainable development, the precautionary principle, and the polluter-pays principle (Section 20). The NGT has been one of the most active environmental tribunals in the world, issuing thousands of orders addressing air pollution (particularly in the National Capital Region), river pollution, waste management, and forest protection.

## South Africa

### Constitutional Environmental Right

South Africa's Section 24 is one of the most comprehensive environmental rights provisions in any constitution:

> "Everyone has the right -- (a) to an environment that is not harmful to their health or well-being; and (b) to have the environment protected, for the benefit of present and future generations, through reasonable legislative and other measures that -- (i) prevent pollution and ecological degradation; (ii) promote conservation; and (iii) secure ecologically sustainable development and use of natural resources while promoting justifiable economic and social development."

The Constitutional Court has interpreted Section 24 through several landmark decisions:

- **Fuel Retailers Association of Southern Africa v. Director-General: Environmental Management (2007 (6) SA 4 (CC)):** The Court held that Section 24 imposes a duty on the state to balance environmental and socio-economic development considerations, and that environmental impact assessments (EIAs) must be conducted before authorizing potentially harmful activities. Justice Ngcobo stated: "The Constitution recognises the interrelationship between the environment and development."

- **Bengwenyama Minerals (Pty) Ltd v. Genorah Resources (Pty) Ltd (2011 (4) SA 113 (CC)):** The Court emphasized the duty to consult affected communities before granting mining rights, reinforcing the connection between environmental rights and the rights of vulnerable communities.

- **Earthlife Africa Johannesburg v. Minister of Environmental Affairs (2017 (2) All SA 519 (GP)):** The Gauteng High Court (Full Bench) held that the environmental authorization for the proposed Thabametsi coal-fired power station was deficient because it failed to consider climate change impacts, establishing that Section 24 requires consideration of climate change in EIAs.

### National Environmental Management Act (NEMA)

NEMA (Act 107 of 1998) provides the overarching framework for environmental governance, establishing principles including:

- **Section 2(4)(a):** Sustainable development as the integration of social, economic, and environmental factors.
- **Section 2(4)(r):** Sensitive, vulnerable, and highly dynamic or stressed ecosystems require specific attention in management and planning.
- **Section 32:** Right to access environmental information.
- **Section 33:** Private prosecution for environmental offenses.

NEMA does not grant nature legal personality, but its expansive standing provisions (Section 32) and the constitutional right (Section 24) create a robust framework for environmental protection through human-centered rights.

### South Africa and Rights of Nature

South Africa has not embraced the rights-of-nature paradigm, though legal scholars have argued that Section 24's reference to protecting the environment "for the benefit of present and future generations" and the intrinsic value implied in biodiversity protection legislation (National Environmental Management: Biodiversity Act 10 of 2004) create a foundation for ecocentric rather than purely anthropocentric environmental law. The concept has been discussed in the South African Law Reform Commission but has not resulted in legislative proposals.

## Latin America

### Ecuador: Constitutional Rights of Nature

Ecuador's 2008 Constitution (Constitucion de Montecristi), drafted by a Constituent Assembly under President Rafael Correa, contains the world's first constitutional rights of nature:

- **Article 71:** "Nature, or Pachamama, where life is reproduced and occurs, has the right to integral respect for its existence and for the maintenance and regeneration of its life cycles, structure, functions and evolutionary processes. Any person, community, people or nationality may demand that the public authorities respect the rights of nature."

- **Article 72:** "Nature has the right to restoration... In the case of severe or permanent environmental impact, including those caused by the exploitation of non-renewable natural resources, the State shall establish the most effective mechanisms to achieve restoration and shall adopt adequate measures to eliminate or mitigate harmful environmental consequences."

- **Article 73:** "The State shall apply precautionary and restrictive measures on activities that may lead to the extinction of species, the destruction of ecosystems, or the permanent alteration of natural cycles."

- **Article 74:** "Persons, communities, peoples and nationalities shall have the right to benefit from the environment and the natural resources that enable them to live well (buen vivir)."

### Landmark Cases in Ecuador

The first judicial application of rights of nature globally occurred in **Wheeler v. Director de la Procuraduria General Del Estado (Vilcabamba River case, 2011)**. The Provincial Court of Loja ruled that the government had violated the rights of the Vilcabamba River by allowing a road construction project to dump debris into the river, causing flooding and contamination. The Court ordered remediation and payment of damages.

In **Los Cedros Case (Constitutional Court of Ecuador, Sentencia No. 1149-19-JP/21, 2021)**, the Court ruled that the constitutional rights of nature precluded mining activities in the Los Cedros Protected Forest, holding that the potential environmental damage from mining was incompatible with nature's right to the maintenance of its life cycles, structure, and evolutionary processes. The Court applied the precautionary principle and required that the interests of nature be balanced against economic development.

In the **Machángara River case (2023)**, the Constitutional Court affirmed the duty of local governments to protect the rights of rivers and ordered comprehensive ecological restoration of the Machángara River in Quito.

### Bolivia: Law of the Rights of Mother Earth

Bolivia's **Law of the Rights of Mother Earth (Ley de Derechos de la Madre Tierra, Ley No. 071, December 21, 2010)** establishes Mother Earth as a collective subject of public interest, with seven enumerated rights:

1. **Right to life:** The right to the maintenance of the integrity of living systems and natural processes.
2. **Right to diversity of life:** The right to the preservation of differentiation and variety of beings.
3. **Right to water:** The right to the preservation of water sources and water cycles.
4. **Right to clean air:** The right to the preservation of air quality.
5. **Right to equilibrium:** The right to the maintenance and restoration of inter-relationships, interdependence, complementarity, and functionality.
6. **Right to restoration:** The right to timely and effective restoration of living systems affected by human activities.
7. **Right to pollution-free living:** The right to the preservation of any part of Mother Earth from contamination.

The **Framework Law of Mother Earth and Integral Development for Living Well (Ley Marco de la Madre Tierra y Desarrollo Integral para Vivir Bien, Ley No. 300, October 15, 2012)** provides implementing mechanisms, establishing the Ombudsperson of Mother Earth (Defensora de la Madre Tierra) and requiring the integration of rights of nature into development planning.

However, Bolivia's rights-of-nature framework has faced sharp criticism for contradicting the government's aggressive extractivist policies. The construction of a highway through the TIPNIS (Isiboro Secure National Park and Indigenous Territory) and continued expansion of mining, natural gas extraction, and soy cultivation have led indigenous organizations and environmental groups to characterize the laws as greenwashing.

### Colombia: Judicial River Personhood

Colombia's Constitutional Court decision in **Sentencia T-622/2016 (Atrato River)** declared the Atrato River and its basin an "entity subject to rights to protection, conservation, maintenance, and restoration" (entidad sujeto de derechos a la proteccion, conservacion, mantenimiento y restauracion). The Court appointed the Colombian government and the ethnic communities of the river basin as joint guardians. The decision was grounded in the constitutional right to a healthy environment (Article 79), biocultural rights of ethnic communities, and the ecocentric principle.

Subsequent Colombian judicial decisions have extended the rights-of-nature approach:

- **STC4360-2018 (Colombian Amazon, Supreme Court of Justice):** The Court declared the Colombian Amazon an "entity subject of rights" and ordered the government to create an intergenerational pact to reduce deforestation to zero and to develop an action plan for addressing climate change.
- **Sentencia SU-095/2018:** The Constitutional Court recognized the right of local communities to participate in decisions about extractive activities affecting their environment, connecting environmental rights with participatory democracy.

## Southeast Asia

### Philippines: Writ of Kalikasan

The Philippines has developed the most innovative procedural environmental remedies in Southeast Asia through the **Rules of Procedure for Environmental Cases (A.M. No. 09-6-8-SC, effective April 29, 2010)**, promulgated by the Supreme Court. The Rules establish two special writs:

**Writ of Kalikasan (Nature):** A remedy available when the constitutional right to a balanced and healthful ecology (Article II, Section 16 of the 1987 Constitution) is violated or threatened by an unlawful act or omission of a public official, employee, or private entity involving environmental damage of such magnitude as to prejudice the life, health, or property of inhabitants in two or more cities or provinces. The Writ may be filed by any Filipino citizen, juridical entity, or entity authorized by law (Rule 7, Section 1), and is accompanied by a Temporary Environmental Protection Order (TEPO) and, upon final judgment, a Permanent Environmental Protection Order (PEPO). The Writ includes the power of continuing mandamus, requiring ongoing compliance monitoring.

**Writ of Continuing Mandamus:** Allows the court to issue an order directing a government agency or officer to perform a duty specifically enjoined by law and to monitor continued compliance.

### Resident Marine Mammals Case

In **Resident Marine Mammals of the Protected Seascape Tanon Strait v. Secretary of the Department of Energy (2015, G.R. No. 180771)**, the Supreme Court recognized the legal standing of marine mammals (dolphins, whales, and other species) as represented by their stewards (a group of environmental lawyers) to file a case challenging the legality of an oil exploration permit in the Tanon Strait Protected Seascape. While the Court ultimately dismissed the petition on procedural grounds, Justice Villarama's ponencia stated: "The need to give the Resident Marine Mammals legal standing has been eliminated by our Rules, which allow any Filipino citizen to file a petition for the Writ of Kalikasan." The case is significant as the first instance in Philippine jurisprudence where animals were named as petitioners.

### Oposa v. Factoran

The foundational Philippine environmental case is **Oposa v. Factoran (G.R. No. 101083, 1993)**, where the Supreme Court recognized the standing of minors to sue on behalf of their generation and future generations to stop logging licenses that threatened the remaining old-growth forests. Justice Davide's landmark opinion established the doctrine of intergenerational equity in Philippine law, holding that the right to a balanced and healthful ecology carries with it a correlative duty to refrain from impairing the environment, and that this right belongs to all future generations.

### Indonesia: Environmental Law and Adat Forests

Indonesia's environmental framework operates through **Law No. 32 of 2009 on Environmental Protection and Management** (replacing Law No. 23 of 1997). The 2009 Law introduced several significant innovations:

- **Article 66:** Protection for persons fighting for the right to a good and healthy environment from criminal and civil suits ("anti-SLAPP" provision).
- **Article 84:** Right to file environmental lawsuits including class actions (gugatan kelompok), organizational lawsuits (gugatan organisasi), and government lawsuits.
- **Article 90:** Environmental organizations' right to file lawsuits for the public interest (hak gugat organisasi lingkungan).

While Indonesia has not adopted rights-of-nature legislation, the Constitutional Court's **Decision No. 35/PUU-X/2012** on customary forests effectively recognizes that indigenous communities' relationship with their forests constitutes a legal bond that the state must respect. By separating hutan adat (customary forests) from hutan negara (state forests), the MK created a framework in which forest ecosystems are protected through the rights of adat communities rather than through independent rights of nature -- an approach scholars have termed "relational environmental rights."

The **Job Creation Law (Omnibus Law)** controversies of 2020-2023 highlighted tensions between environmental protection and economic deregulation, with environmental organizations successfully challenging provisions that weakened environmental impact assessment requirements before the Constitutional Court (MK Decision No. 91/PUU-XVIII/2020, which declared the Law conditionally unconstitutional and required revision within two years).

## Comparative Analysis

### Three Models of Nature's Legal Status

The Global South has generated three distinct models for addressing nature's legal status:

1. **Constitutional/Statutory Rights of Nature (Ecuador, Bolivia):** Nature is a constitutional or statutory rights-bearer with enumerated rights enforceable by any person or community. This is the most radical departure from Western legal anthropocentrism, though enforcement remains the critical challenge.

2. **Judicial Personhood (Colombia, India):** Courts grant specific natural entities (rivers, ecosystems) legal personhood through judicial decisions, typically appointing guardians. This is more targeted than the Ecuadorian model but depends on judicial willingness and faces implementation challenges (as demonstrated by the Supreme Court of India's stay of the Ganges/Yamuna personhood decision).

3. **Procedural Innovation without Personhood (Philippines, South Africa, China):** Rather than granting nature independent legal status, these jurisdictions create powerful procedural mechanisms (Writ of Kalikasan, environmental public interest litigation, constitutional environmental rights) that achieve protective outcomes without changing nature's ontological legal status.

### Indigenous Cosmology and Legal Innovation

The rights-of-nature movement draws heavily on indigenous cosmologies:

- **Ecuador and Bolivia's** Pachamama/Mother Earth frameworks are explicitly rooted in Andean indigenous worldviews that understand nature as a living, interconnected whole rather than a collection of resources.
- **Colombia's** biocultural rights doctrine links Afro-Colombian and indigenous communities' cultural identity to their river ecosystems.
- **India's** Ganges personhood draws on Hindu religious traditions treating the river as a living goddess (Ganga Ma).
- **Indonesia's** adat forest recognition reflects indigenous customary relationships with forest ecosystems.
- **China's** ecological civilization concept, while not indigenous in origin, draws on Daoist and Confucian philosophical traditions of harmony between humans and nature (天人合一, tian ren he yi).

### Enforcement Gap

Across all jurisdictions, the primary challenge is enforcement. Ecuador's constitutional rights of nature coexist with ongoing petroleum extraction in the Amazon (though the 2023 Yasuni ITT referendum, in which Ecuadorians voted to halt oil extraction in the Yasuni National Park, represents a significant democratic expression of rights-of-nature principles). Bolivia's rights of Mother Earth have not prevented accelerating deforestation. Colombia's river personhood orders face implementation challenges from weak state presence in conflict-affected territories. China's environmental enforcement has improved dramatically since 2015 but remains constrained by local government growth imperatives.
