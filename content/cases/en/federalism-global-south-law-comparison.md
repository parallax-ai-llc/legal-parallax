## Summary

Federal and unitary systems in the Global South reflect profoundly different approaches to managing ethnic, linguistic, religious, and regional diversity within post-colonial and post-revolutionary states, operating on logics that often diverge sharply from the Euro-American federal models (United States, Germany, Switzerland) that dominate comparative law scholarship. China operates as a centralized unitary state under Article 3 of the Constitution, which establishes the principle that "the division of functions and powers between the central and local state organs is guided by the principle of giving full play to the initiative and enthusiasm of the local authorities under the unified leadership of the central authorities," supplemented by the Regional Ethnic Autonomy Law (民族区域自治法, 1984, revised 2001) that establishes five autonomous regions (自治区), thirty autonomous prefectures (自治州), and 120 autonomous counties (自治县) for minority nationalities, as well as the Special Administrative Regions of Hong Kong and Macau operating under the "One Country, Two Systems" (一国两制) framework per Articles 31 and 62(14) of the Constitution. India's Constitution establishes what K.C. Wheare characterized as "quasi-federal" -- a federal structure with strong unitary features including the Seventh Schedule's division of legislative powers into Union List (97 entries), State List (66 entries), and Concurrent List (47 entries), the President's power to declare a state of emergency under Articles 352-360 (transforming the federation into a unitary state), and the Governor's power to reserve state bills for presidential consideration under Article 200. Nigeria, Africa's most populous country, operates a three-tier federal system (federal, 36 states, and 774 local government areas) under the 1999 Constitution, with fiscal federalism structured through the Revenue Mobilisation Allocation and Fiscal Responsibility Commission and the Federation Account (Section 162), though in practice the federal government dominates revenue collection and allocation, creating what scholars call "feeding bottle federalism." Brazil is a federation of 26 states, the Federal District, and 5,570 municipios (municipalities), with the 1988 Constitution uniquely recognizing municipios as constituent units of the federation (Article 18), though the fiscal relationship between the federal government and subnational entities remains contentious, with disputes over the Fundo de Participacao dos Estados (State Participation Fund) and the ICMS (state-level value-added tax). The Philippines, a unitary republic, has addressed demands for self-determination through asymmetric autonomy, most notably the Bangsamoro Organic Law (Republic Act No. 11054, 2018) establishing the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao (BARMM) with its own parliamentary government, fiscal autonomy, and authority over Sharia courts, complemented by the broader decentralization under the Local Government Code (Republic Act No. 7160, 1991). Indonesia's transition from Suharto's centralized unitary state to the "Big Bang" decentralization of 1999-2004, implemented through Laws No. 22/1999 and No. 25/1999 (later replaced by Laws No. 23/2014 and No. 33/2004), transferred extensive administrative authority to over 500 kabupaten (districts) and kota (cities), while maintaining asymmetric autonomy arrangements for Aceh (Law No. 11/2006), Papua (Law No. 21/2001, amended 2021), and the Special Region of Yogyakarta (Law No. 13/2012).

## China (中国)

### The Unitary System with Chinese Characteristics

China's territorial governance system is formally unitary but encompasses remarkable diversity in institutional arrangements, operating under what scholars have termed "fragmented authoritarianism" (Kenneth Lieberthal and Michel Oksenberg) or "decentralized authoritarianism" (Pierre Landry).

**The Tax-Sharing System (分税制, fenshuizhi):** The 1994 fiscal reform fundamentally restructured central-local fiscal relations by dividing taxes into central taxes (e.g., customs duties, consumption tax), local taxes (e.g., urban land-use tax, farmland occupation tax), and shared taxes (e.g., value-added tax, initially 75% central/25% local, later adjusted; corporate and individual income tax, 60% central/40% local after 2002). This reform dramatically increased the central government's share of fiscal revenue (from approximately 22% in 1993 to over 55% by 2002) while expenditure responsibilities remained primarily with local governments, creating the "vertical fiscal imbalance" (纵向财政不平衡) that drives local government debt and land finance dependency.

**Regional Ethnic Autonomy Law (民族区域自治法, REAL, 1984, revised 2001):** The REAL implements the Constitution's provisions (Articles 4, 112-122) on ethnic regional autonomy. Key provisions include:

- **Article 4:** Autonomous areas are classified as autonomous regions (自治区), autonomous prefectures (自治州), and autonomous counties (自治县).
- **Article 15:** The head of an autonomous region, prefecture, or county must be a citizen of the ethnic group exercising regional autonomy.
- **Article 19:** The people's congress of an autonomous area has the power to enact "autonomous regulations" (自治条例) and "separate regulations" (单行条例) in light of local political, economic, and cultural characteristics.
- **Article 20:** An autonomous area may, in accordance with local conditions, modify or halt the implementation of resolutions, decisions, orders, and instructions of higher-level state organs that do not suit the area's conditions, subject to approval by the next higher-level state organ.

In practice, the autonomous powers under the REAL are significantly constrained by CPC organizational principles, including the CPC's control over the appointment of key officials (the Party Secretary of an autonomous region is typically not from the titular nationality), and the overriding authority of central government policies.

**Special Administrative Regions (SARs):** Hong Kong SAR (established July 1, 1997) and Macau SAR (established December 20, 1999) operate under Basic Laws enacted by the NPC pursuant to Article 31 of the Constitution. The Hong Kong Basic Law (香港特别行政区基本法) guarantees that the SAR's capitalist system and way of life shall remain unchanged for 50 years (Article 5), with the SAR exercising a high degree of autonomy and enjoying executive, legislative, and independent judicial power, including that of final adjudication (Article 2). The enactment of the Law of the People's Republic of China on Safeguarding National Security in the Hong Kong Special Administrative Region (香港国安法, June 30, 2020) by the NPCSC, and its application by Hong Kong courts, has significantly reshaped the autonomy framework.

### The Tiao-Kuai System

Chinese governance is organized along two intersecting axes: the vertical functional system (条, tiao, "lines") of ministries and agencies extending from central to local levels, and the horizontal territorial system (块, kuai, "blocks") of provincial, prefectural, county, and township governments. This tiao-kuai (条块) matrix creates complex accountability relationships where local officials report both to their functional superiors and to local Party and government leaders. The resulting dynamic -- termed "fragmented authoritarianism" -- means that central directives are mediated through multiple layers of negotiation and adaptation.

## India (भारत)

### Constitutional Federalism

India's federal structure was influenced by the Government of India Act 1935 (which introduced provincial autonomy), the Canadian Constitution (strong centre), and the framers' concerns about national unity in the context of Partition and princely states integration.

**The Seventh Schedule:** The heart of Indian federalism is the three-list division of legislative competence:
- **Union List (List I):** 97 entries including defense, foreign affairs, banking, telecommunications, and income tax.
- **State List (List II):** 66 entries including public order, police, public health, agriculture, and land revenue.
- **Concurrent List (List III):** 47 entries including criminal law, criminal procedure, marriage and divorce, forests, and education (added by the 42nd Amendment).

Article 246 establishes that in case of conflict between Union and state legislation on a concurrent subject, the Union law prevails. Article 248 vests residuary power in Parliament, unlike the U.S. model where residual powers rest with the states.

**Cooperative Federalism and the GST:** The Goods and Services Tax (GST), introduced by the 101st Constitutional Amendment (2016), represents a landmark in cooperative federalism, creating a unified indirect tax regime through the GST Council (Article 279A), which comprises the Union Finance Minister (chair), the Union Minister of State for Finance, and the Finance Ministers of all states. The Council's weighted voting system (one-third weight to the Centre, two-thirds to the states, with decisions requiring a three-fourths majority) creates a federal negotiation forum. The Supreme Court in Union of India v. Mohit Minerals Pvt. Ltd., 2022 SCC OnLine SC 657, held that GST Council recommendations are not binding on state legislatures, affirming that "cooperative federalism" does not mean "coerced federalism."

**President's Rule (Article 356):** This provision, allowing the President (on the advice of the Council of Ministers) to assume the government of a state on the ground that "the government of the State cannot be carried on in accordance with the provisions of this Constitution," has been invoked over 130 times since 1950. The Supreme Court in S.R. Bommai v. Union of India, 1994 AIR SCW 2946, imposed significant constraints: the President's satisfaction must be based on objective material, is subject to judicial review, and the state assembly can only be dissolved after parliamentary approval.

**Article 370 and Jammu & Kashmir:** The abrogation of Article 370 (which granted special autonomous status to J&K) and the reorganization of the state into two Union Territories by the Jammu and Kashmir Reorganisation Act, 2019, represented the most significant alteration of India's federal architecture since independence. The Supreme Court in *In Re: Article 370 of the Constitution* (2023) upheld the abrogation, holding that Article 370 was a temporary provision and that the President had the power to issue the order abrogating it.

### Linguistic States and Federal Identity

India's internal boundaries were reorganized along linguistic lines following the States Reorganisation Act, 1956, responding to the recommendations of the States Reorganisation Commission and the political movement catalyzed by Potti Sriramulu's fatal fast for a Telugu-speaking state (Andhra Pradesh) in 1952. This linguistic reorganization has been described as one of India's greatest democratic achievements, accommodating linguistic diversity within the federal framework. Subsequent reorganizations have continued: Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, and Uttarakhand (2000), Telangana (2014).

## Africa (Nigeria and South Africa)

### Nigeria: Oil, Revenue, and "Feeding Bottle Federalism"

Nigeria's federal system, established by the 1999 Constitution, is shaped by the dominance of oil revenue and the political dynamics of its approximately 250 ethnic groups.

**Revenue Allocation (Section 162):** All revenue collected by the federal government (except the proceeds of the personal income tax of the personnel of the armed forces, the police force, the foreign affairs ministry, and residents of the FCT) is paid into the Federation Account. The Revenue Mobilisation Allocation and Fiscal Responsibility Commission recommends the formula for distribution among the three tiers (federal, state, local). As of 2024, the allocation is approximately 52.68% federal, 26.72% state, and 20.60% local, with additional provisions for "derivation" (Section 162(2): not less than 13% of revenue derived from any natural resource to the state of derivation).

The 13% derivation principle, applicable primarily to oil-producing states in the Niger Delta (Rivers, Delta, Bayelsa, Akwa Ibom, Edo, Ondo, Imo, Abia, Cross River), has been a persistent source of litigation. In *Attorney-General of the Federation v. Attorney-General of Abia State* [2002] 6 NWLR (Pt 764) 542, the Supreme Court addressed the geographic definition of derivation revenue, including offshore resources, holding that the southern boundary of littoral states extends to the low-water mark and not further, a decision subsequently modified by the Allocation of Revenue (Abolition of Dichotomy in the Application of the Principle of Derivation) Act, 2004.

**State Creation and the "National Question":** Nigeria has experienced successive rounds of state creation (from 3 regions in 1963, to 12 states in 1967, 19 in 1976, 21 in 1987, 30 in 1991, and 36 in 1996), each reflecting attempts to balance ethnic and regional claims within the federal structure. The continued agitation for additional states and the calls for restructuring (ranging from fiscal federalism reforms to confederation proposals from groups such as the Movement for the Actualization of the Sovereign State of Biafra, MASSOB) demonstrate the unresolved nature of Nigerian federalism.

### South Africa: Cooperative Government

South Africa's 1996 Constitution establishes a unitary state with a strong decentralization component, organized into national government, nine provinces, and 278 municipalities. Chapter 3 (Cooperative Government) establishes principles of cooperative government and intergovernmental relations.

**Provincial Legislative Competence:** Schedule 4 (functional areas of concurrent national and provincial legislative competence, including agriculture, education, environment, health services, and housing) and Schedule 5 (functional areas of exclusive provincial legislative competence, including abattoirs, ambulance services, and liquor licenses) define provincial powers. However, provinces have limited fiscal autonomy, receiving approximately 96% of their revenue from national transfers (equitable share under Section 214 and conditional grants).

**The National Council of Provinces (NCOP):** The upper house of Parliament (Section 60) represents provincial interests in national legislation, with 10 delegates per province. Special bills affecting provinces require the support of at least five of the nine provincial delegations.

## Latin America (Brazil, Mexico, Colombia)

### Brazil: The Three-Tier Federation

Brazil's 1988 Constitution uniquely elevates municipios to constituent units of the federation alongside the Union, states, and the Federal District (Article 18: "The political-administrative organization of the Federative Republic of Brazil comprises the Union, the States, the Federal District, and the Municipalities, all of them autonomous").

**Fiscal Federalism:** Brazil's fiscal system involves complex intergovernmental transfers:
- **Fundo de Participacao dos Estados (FPE):** 21.5% of federal income tax and IPI (industrialized products tax) revenue is distributed among states based on population and income criteria.
- **Fundo de Participacao dos Municipios (FPM):** 24.5% of the same revenues is distributed among municipalities.
- **ICMS (Imposto sobre Circulacao de Mercadorias e Servicos):** The state-level value-added tax, which is the most important subnational revenue source, has been the subject of "fiscal wars" (guerra fiscal) as states compete for investment through tax incentives. The 2023 tax reform (Constitutional Amendment 132/2023) replaced the ICMS and federal ISS with a dual VAT system (CBS at the federal level and IBS at the subnational level) to be phased in by 2033.

**Indigenous Territories (Article 231):** The Constitution recognizes indigenous peoples' original rights (direitos originarios) over lands they traditionally occupy, which are inalienable and indisposable. The demarcation process involves FUNAI (National Indigenous Peoples Foundation) identification, administrative procedures, and presidential homologation. The STF's 2023 decision in RE 1.017.365 (the "marco temporal" or "time frame" thesis) held that indigenous land claims must relate to lands occupied or under dispute on October 5, 1988, sparking significant controversy and subsequent legislative action (Law No. 14.701/2023).

### Colombia: Decentralization Within a Unitary State

Colombia's 1991 Constitution defines the state as a "unitary, decentralized Republic with autonomy of its territorial entities" (Article 1). Territorial entities include departments (32), districts, municipalities (over 1,100), and indigenous territories (territorios indigenas, Article 286).

**The Sistema General de Participaciones (SGP):** Articles 356-357 (as amended by Legislative Acts 01/2001 and 04/2007) establish the system of fiscal transfers from the national government to territorial entities, earmarked primarily for education, health, water supply, and sanitation. The SGP replaced the earlier situado fiscal and participaciones municipales systems.

**Indigenous Territorial Entities (ETIs):** Article 329 provides for the creation of indigenous territorial entities, governed by councils formed and regulated according to the customs of the respective communities. Despite the constitutional mandate, the organic law creating ETIs had not been enacted as of 2025, leaving indigenous governance operating through the resguardo system under transitional arrangements.

## Southeast Asia (Indonesia and Philippines)

### Indonesia: Big Bang Decentralization

Indonesia's post-Suharto decentralization has been described as one of the most rapid and comprehensive decentralization processes in modern history, transferring authority over most government functions (except defense, foreign affairs, fiscal policy, justice, religion, and monetary policy) to over 500 districts and cities.

**Law No. 23/2014 on Regional Government:** This law, replacing the earlier Laws No. 22/1999 and No. 32/2004, classifies government affairs into absolute affairs (central government), concurrent affairs (shared between central and regional), and general government affairs. Concurrent affairs are further divided into mandatory (with and without minimum service standards) and optional affairs.

**Asymmetric Autonomy:**
- **Aceh (Law No. 11/2006, Law on the Governing of Aceh):** Grants Aceh authority to implement Sharia for Muslims, establish Sharia courts (Mahkamah Syar'iyah), retain 70% of oil and gas revenue for the first eight years (declining thereafter), and form local political parties (a unique exception to the national political party requirement).
- **Papua (Law No. 21/2001 on Special Autonomy for Papua, amended by Law No. 2/2021):** Provides for the Majelis Rakyat Papua (Papuan People's Assembly), 80% revenue sharing from mining, forestry, and fisheries, and affirmative action for indigenous Papuans. The 2022 division of Papua province into multiple new provinces (Papua, Papua Pegunungan, Papua Selatan, Papua Tengah, Papua Barat Daya) has generated debate about whether proliferation of provinces strengthens or weakens autonomy.
- **Yogyakarta (Law No. 13/2012):** The Sultan of Yogyakarta (currently Sultan Hamengkubuwono X) automatically becomes the Governor, a unique monarchical exception within the republican system.

### Philippines: Asymmetric Autonomy

The Philippines, a unitary state, has pursued asymmetric autonomy primarily to address the Bangsamoro conflict in Mindanao.

**Bangsamoro Organic Law (Republic Act No. 11054, 2018):** Replacing the failed Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao (ARMM), the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao (BARMM) was established following the 2014 Comprehensive Agreement on the Bangsamoro between the Government and the Moro Islamic Liberation Front (MILF). Key features:
- **Parliamentary Government:** A Chief Minister heading a cabinet, responsible to the Bangsamoro Parliament (80 members, including reserved seats for non-Moro indigenous peoples, settler communities, women, and youth).
- **Fiscal Autonomy:** The BARMM receives a block grant from the national government plus its share of the Internal Revenue Allotment, and has taxing powers subject to certain limitations.
- **Sharia Courts:** The BARMM has authority over Sharia courts with jurisdiction over personal law matters for Muslims, with the Supreme Court retaining appellate jurisdiction.

**The Cordillera Question:** Article X, Section 15 of the Constitution also mandates an autonomous region in the Cordilleras, but two plebiscites (1990 and 1998) failed. The Cordillera Administrative Region (CAR) functions as a regular administrative region without autonomous powers, though legislative proposals for Cordillera autonomy continue.

## Comparative Analysis

### Holding-Together vs. Coming-Together Federalism

Alfred Stepan's distinction between "coming-together" federalism (where previously independent units aggregate, as in the United States and Switzerland) and "holding-together" federalism (where a unitary state devolves power to prevent disintegration) is particularly relevant in the Global South. India, Nigeria, and Indonesia all represent holding-together arrangements where federalism or decentralization serves as a mechanism for managing diversity within post-colonial borders that rarely correspond to pre-colonial political units. The asymmetric arrangements in Aceh, BARMM, and formerly J&K reflect the most intense holding-together dynamics, where special autonomy is offered as an alternative to secession.

### Fiscal Federalism and Resource Dependence

The distribution of natural resource revenue is the most contentious dimension of federalism across the Global South. Nigeria's derivation principle, Indonesia's special fiscal arrangements for Aceh and Papua, and Brazil's pre-salt oil revenue distribution (Law No. 12.351/2010) all demonstrate how resource wealth both enables and destabilizes federal arrangements. The "resource curse" manifests federally as competition among subnational units for resource revenue, corruption in revenue-sharing mechanisms, and the disconnect between revenue-generating and expenditure-responsible levels of government.
