## Summary

Deportation -- the formal removal of a non-citizen from a state's territory, typically following a determination that the individual's presence is unlawful or contrary to the public interest -- represents one of the most consequential exercises of sovereign power, directly affecting individual liberty, family unity, and the right to private life. South Korea's deportation framework is governed by the Immigration Act (출입국관리법) of 1963, as extensively amended, which authorizes the Ministry of Justice (법무부) and its Korea Immigration Service (출입국관리사무소) to issue deportation orders (강제퇴거명령) against foreign nationals who violate immigration conditions, overstay visas, commit criminal offenses, or engage in activities detrimental to public interest, with detention in immigration detention facilities (외국인보호소) pending removal and limited but expanding judicial review mechanisms. The United States operates one of the world's largest deportation systems, with removal proceedings governed by the Immigration and Nationality Act (INA, 8 U.S.C. §§ 1221-1232), administered by Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) within the Department of Homeland Security and adjudicated by immigration judges in the Executive Office for Immigration Review (EOIR), with the system annually removing approximately 300,000-500,000 individuals through formal removal orders, reinstatement of removal, and voluntary departure. Germany's deportation law, codified in the Aufenthaltsgesetz (AufenthG, Residence Act) of 2004 (§§ 50-62b), operates within a framework that includes significant constitutional protections under the Grundgesetz, particularly Article 16a (right of asylum) and Article 19(4) (guarantee of judicial review), alongside EU law obligations under the Return Directive 2008/115/EC, while the ECHR's extensive jurisprudence under Articles 3 (prohibition of torture and inhuman treatment), 5 (right to liberty), 8 (right to private and family life), and 13 (right to an effective remedy) has created a robust supranational framework constraining state deportation powers, with the European Court of Human Rights establishing that deportation to a country where an individual faces a real risk of treatment contrary to Article 3 is absolutely prohibited regardless of the individual's conduct or national security considerations.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's deportation system is governed by the Immigration Act (출입국관리법), originally enacted in 1963 and extensively amended to address the country's transformation from an emigration to an immigration country.

**Grounds for Deportation (Article 46):** Deportation orders may be issued against foreign nationals who:

| Ground | Article |
|--------|---------|
| Enter without valid documents or inspection | Art. 46(1)(i) |
| Violate conditions of stay | Art. 46(1)(ii) |
| Overstay authorized period | Art. 46(1)(iii) |
| Engage in unauthorized employment | Art. 46(1)(iv) |
| Commit crimes (sentences exceeding specified thresholds) | Art. 46(1)(viii) |
| Engage in activities threatening public interest or security | Art. 46(1)(xiii) |

**Deportation Procedure:**
1. Investigation by immigration officers (출입국관리공무원)
2. Detention in immigration detention facility (외국인보호소) -- Article 51
3. Hearing before immigration officer -- Article 55
4. Deportation order issued by regional immigration office chief -- Article 59
5. Execution of deportation

### Immigration Detention

Korea operates two dedicated immigration detention facilities: Hwaseong Immigration Detention Center and Cheongju Immigration Detention Center, with combined capacity of approximately 700 persons.

**Duration:** The Immigration Act authorizes detention without a fixed statutory maximum, though amendments have introduced some procedural safeguards:
- Initial detention period: up to 10 days (extendable by 10 days)
- Extended detention for deportation execution: no explicit maximum (maximum 3 years under practice guidelines)
- UNHCR and domestic civil society organizations have criticized the lack of a statutory maximum and limited judicial oversight

**Conditions and Oversight:** Following a series of deaths in detention (including the widely publicized cases at Hwaseong in 2018-2021), the National Human Rights Commission of Korea (국가인권위원회) issued recommendations for improved medical care, mental health services, and independent monitoring of detention facilities.

### Judicial Review

Historically, judicial review of deportation orders was limited to administrative courts reviewing the legality (not proportionality) of deportation decisions. Recent developments include:
- Constitutional Court Decision 2018HunMa447 (2020): affirmed that immigration detention must comply with constitutional due process requirements
- Expansion of administrative court review to include proportionality assessment considering family ties, duration of residence, and integration

### Undocumented Population

Korea's undocumented population is estimated at approximately 400,000 persons (2025), primarily visa overstayers from China, Vietnam, Thailand, and Mongolia. Periodic voluntary departure programs (자진출국 프로그램) offer reduced penalties for voluntary compliance.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States operates one of the largest and most complex deportation systems in the world.

**INA Section 237 (8 U.S.C. § 1227) -- Deportable Aliens:**

| Category | Key Grounds |
|----------|-------------|
| Immigration violations | Inadmissible at entry; present in violation of law; failure to maintain status |
| Criminal grounds | Crimes involving moral turpitude; aggravated felonies; controlled substance offenses; firearms offenses; domestic violence |
| Security grounds | Espionage; sabotage; terrorism; Nazi persecution |
| Public charge | Becoming a public charge within 5 years of entry |
| Unlawful voting | Voting in violation of law |

**Removal Proceedings (INA § 240):** Immigration judges in the Executive Office for Immigration Review (EOIR) conduct removal hearings. Appeals go to the Board of Immigration Appeals (BIA) and then to federal circuit courts of appeals (subject to significant jurisdictional limitations under the REAL ID Act of 2005).

### Forms of Removal

| Type | Description | Due Process |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| Formal removal (§ 240) | Full removal hearing before immigration judge | Full hearing; right to counsel (not government-provided); appeal rights |
| Expedited removal (§ 235(b)(1)) | Summary removal of arriving aliens and certain others | Limited; credible fear interview for asylum seekers |
| Reinstatement of removal (§ 241(a)(5)) | Reinstated prior removal order for re-entrants | No hearing; limited review |
| Voluntary departure (§ 240B) | Voluntary departure in lieu of removal order | Avoids formal removal order and re-entry bar |
| Administrative removal (§ 238(b)) | Non-LPR aggravated felons | Limited hearing rights |

### Relief from Removal

| Relief | Statutory Basis | Key Requirements |
|--------|----------------|------------------|
| Asylum | INA § 208 | Well-founded fear of persecution; filed within 1 year |
| Withholding of removal | INA § 241(b)(3) | More likely than not to face persecution |
| Convention Against Torture (CAT) | 8 C.F.R. § 1208.16-18 | More likely than not to be tortured by or with acquiescence of government |
| Cancellation of removal (LPR) | INA § 240A(a) | 7 years continuous residence; 5 years LPR status; no aggravated felony |
| Cancellation of removal (non-LPR) | INA § 240A(b) | 10 years continuous physical presence; good moral character; exceptional hardship to USC/LPR spouse, parent, or child |

### Immigration Detention

ICE detains approximately 30,000-40,000 individuals daily in a network of federal facilities, private detention centers, and county jails. The Supreme Court has addressed detention authority in several landmark cases:
- **Zadvydas v. Davis, 533 U.S. 678 (2001):** Post-removal-order detention is limited to a "reasonably foreseeable" period (presumptively 6 months)
- **Jennings v. Rodriguez, 583 U.S. 281 (2018):** No statutory right to periodic bond hearings during prolonged detention
- **Johnson v. Arteaga-Martinez, 596 U.S. 573 (2022):** INA does not require bond hearings for individuals detained under withholding-only proceedings

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's deportation law operates within a comprehensive framework of constitutional, EU, and international law protections.

**Aufenthaltsgesetz (AufenthG) -- Deportation Provisions:**

| Section | Content |
|---------|---------|
| § 50 | Obligation to leave (Ausreisepflicht) following denial/revocation of residence title |
| § 53 | Deportation order (Abschiebungsanordnung): balancing of departure interest vs. stay interest |
| § 54 | Serious departure interest (schwerwiegendes Ausweisungsinteresse): criminal convictions; terrorism; public safety |
| § 55 | Serious stay interest (schwerwiegendes Bleibeinteresse): long-term residence; family ties; born in Germany |
| § 58 | Deportation (Abschiebung): enforcement of obligation to leave |
| § 60 | Prohibition of deportation: non-refoulement; life/freedom threatened; ECHR Art. 3 |
| § 60a | Temporary suspension of deportation (Duldung) |
| § 62 | Detention pending deportation (Abschiebungshaft): maximum 18 months |

### Balancing Test (§§ 53-55 AufenthG)

The 2016 reform of German deportation law replaced the previous categorical approach with a comprehensive balancing test:

**Deportation interest factors (§ 54):**
- Terrorism-related activity (particularly serious)
- Sentenced to imprisonment of at least 2 years
- Criminal conviction for drug offenses
- Threat to public safety, order, or free democratic basic order

**Stay interest factors (§ 55):**
- Holds a permanent residence permit (Niederlassungserlaubnis) and has resided in Germany for 5+ years
- Has minor German national children
- Lives in a family unit with a German national or long-term resident spouse
- Born and raised in Germany
- Is a recognized refugee or beneficiary of subsidiary protection

### Duldung (Toleration Status)

The Duldung (§ 60a AufenthG) is a uniquely German legal instrument suspending deportation without conferring a residence title. Grounds include:
- Legal impossibility (e.g., non-refoulement obligations)
- Factual impossibility (e.g., no travel documents; no flights; medical unfitness)
- Humanitarian reasons

As of 2025, approximately 230,000 persons held Duldung status in Germany. The Chancen-Aufenthaltsrecht (Opportunity Residence Right) introduced in January 2023 created a pathway to a residence permit for long-term Duldung holders (5+ years) who demonstrate integration, addressing the criticism that Duldung creates a permanent state of legal limbo (Kettenduldung).

### Constitutional Protections

**Article 19(4) GG:** Guarantees judicial review of all administrative decisions, including deportation orders. German administrative courts (Verwaltungsgerichte) provide full review of fact and law, including proportionality.

**Article 16a GG:** Constitutional right to asylum (significantly limited by the 1993 "asylum compromise" amendment).

## ECHR / EU

### ECHR Jurisprudence

The European Court of Human Rights has developed the most comprehensive body of case law constraining state deportation powers.

**Article 3 -- Absolute Prohibition:**

**Soering v. United Kingdom (Application No. 14038/88, July 7, 1989):** The landmark case establishing that extradition/deportation to a country where an individual faces a real risk of treatment contrary to Article 3 violates the Convention. The prohibition is absolute and cannot be balanced against national security or public interest.

**Saadi v. Italy (Application No. 37201/06, Grand Chamber, February 28, 2008):** The Court unanimously rejected the argument that the risk of ill-treatment could be balanced against the danger posed by the individual to the receiving state. Even where an individual poses a national security threat, deportation is prohibited if there is a real risk of Article 3 treatment.

**Chahal v. United Kingdom (Application No. 22414/93, Grand Chamber, November 15, 1996):** Confirmed the absolute nature of Article 3 in deportation cases; diplomatic assurances insufficient to eliminate the risk of torture.

**Article 8 -- Private and Family Life:**

**Üner v. Netherlands (Application No. 46410/99, Grand Chamber, October 18, 2006):** Established criteria for assessing proportionality of deportation under Article 8:
1. Nature and seriousness of the offense
2. Duration of residence in the host country
3. Time elapsed since the offense and conduct during that period
4. Nationalities of the persons concerned
5. Family situation (spouse, children)
6. Whether the spouse knew about the offense at time of family relationship
7. Ages of children and their interest
8. Difficulties the spouse and children would face in the country of deportation
9. Strength of social, cultural, and family ties with host country and destination country
10. Immigration status at time of family relationship

**Maslov v. Austria (Application No. 1638/03, Grand Chamber, June 23, 2008):** The Court held that deportation of a long-term resident who committed offenses as a juvenile required particularly strong justification. "Settled migrants" who have spent most of their lives in the host country enjoy enhanced protection.

### EU Return Directive 2008/115/EC

The Return Directive establishes common standards for the return of illegally staying third-country nationals:

| Article | Requirement |
|---------|-------------|
| Art. 6 | Return decision required for illegally staying third-country nationals |
| Art. 7 | Voluntary departure period: 7-30 days (generally) |
| Art. 9 | Postponement of removal (non-refoulement; best interest of child; medical) |
| Art. 11 | Entry ban: maximum 5 years (longer for serious public security threats) |
| Art. 13 | Effective remedy: right to appeal before judicial/administrative authority |
| Art. 15 | Detention: last resort; maximum 6 months (extendable to 18 months) |
| Art. 16 | Conditions of detention: specialized facilities; separation from ordinary prisoners |

**CJEU Jurisprudence on Return Directive:**

**Case C-61/11 PPU, El Dridi (2011):** The Court held that member states may not impose criminal penalties for illegal stay that are liable to jeopardize the attainment of the Return Directive's objectives. The criminalization of illegal stay through imprisonment is incompatible with the Directive's graduated approach.

**Case C-357/09 PPU, Kadzoev (2009):** The Court held that the 18-month maximum detention period under Article 15 cannot be circumvented by re-detaining individuals after release. Once the maximum period is reached, the person must be released immediately.

## Comparative Analysis

### Due Process Protections

Due process protections vary dramatically:
- **Germany:** Full judicial review by administrative courts with proportionality assessment; constitutional complaint to BVerfG
- **ECHR:** Supranational review with binding judgments; individual petition mechanism
- **United States:** Immigration courts (non-Article III); limited federal court review; no right to government-funded counsel
- **South Korea:** Expanding but still limited administrative court review; minimal procedural protections during detention

### Detention Safeguards

- **EU/Germany:** Maximum 18 months; detention as last resort; specialized facilities
- **United States:** No statutory maximum for certain categories; routine detention; significant use of private facilities
- **South Korea:** No statutory maximum (3-year practice guideline); limited judicial oversight

### Non-Refoulement

All jurisdictions recognize non-refoulement, but implementation varies:
- **ECHR:** Absolute prohibition under Article 3; no balancing against national security
- **Germany:** Statutory prohibition (§ 60 AufenthG) implementing both ECHR and EU Charter obligations
- **United States:** Statutory withholding and CAT protection; but expedited removal may limit access to these protections
- **South Korea:** Limited statutory framework; reliance on UNHCR and administrative discretion

### Immigration Detention Conditions

Detention conditions during deportation proceedings have generated significant litigation:

- **ECHR:** Khlaifia and Others v. Italy (Grand Chamber, 2016) established that conditions of immigration detention must comply with Article 3 (prohibition of inhuman treatment) and Article 5(1)(f) (lawful detention); MSS v. Belgium and Greece (Grand Chamber, 2011) held that reception conditions in Greece violated Article 3, effectively halting Dublin returns to Greece
- **United States:** Flores Settlement Agreement (1997) limits detention of minors to 20 days; conditions at immigration detention facilities have been subject to ongoing litigation; Hernandez v. Mesa (2020) addresses cross-border use of force
- **Germany:** Abschiebungshaft (detention pending deportation) under AufenthG §§ 62-62a; maximum duration 18 months; must be ordered by a judge; conditions subject to EU Return Directive requirements; separation from criminal detainees required
- **South Korea:** Immigration detention centers operated by the Ministry of Justice; maximum detention period not fixed by statute; the Constitutional Court in 2018 held that indefinite detention without judicial review raises constitutional concerns (2014HunGa8)

### Voluntary Departure Programs

All jurisdictions offer alternatives to forced removal:

- **EU Return Directive Article 7:** Requires member states to offer voluntary departure periods of 7-30 days before issuing removal orders; voluntary departure preferred over forced removal
- **United States:** Voluntary departure under INA § 240B; available at both pre-hearing and post-hearing stages; failure to depart within the specified period triggers civil penalties and bars from future relief
- **Germany:** Assisted voluntary return programs (REAG/GARP) provide financial and logistical support; in 2023, voluntary departures exceeded forced removals
- **South Korea:** Voluntary departure orders under Immigration Act Article 68; shorter processing and no entry ban compared to deportation orders
