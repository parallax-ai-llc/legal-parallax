## Summary

Victim rights -- the legal entitlements of persons harmed by criminal conduct to information, participation, protection, and compensation within the criminal justice process -- have undergone a dramatic transformation over the past four decades, shifting from a paradigm in which victims were treated primarily as witnesses for the prosecution to one in which they are recognized as independent participants with enforceable rights. South Korea established a comprehensive victim rights framework through the Crime Victim Protection Act (범죄피해자 보호법, 2005), the Crime Victim Compensation Act (범죄피해자구조법, originally enacted in 1987 and comprehensively revised in 2010), and constitutional protections under Article 27(5) of the Constitution (대한민국 헌법), which guarantees crime victims the right to make statements during criminal proceedings -- making South Korea one of the few countries to constitutionalize victim participation rights. The United States developed victim rights through the Victims' Rights and Restitution Act of 1990, the Violence Against Women Act (VAWA, first enacted 1994), and most significantly the Crime Victims' Rights Act of 2004 (18 U.S.C. Section 3771), which established eight enforceable federal rights including the right to be reasonably protected, to reasonable notice of proceedings, to be present and heard at public proceedings, and to restitution -- while at the state level, 37 states have adopted constitutional amendments (commonly known as "Marsy's Law" amendments after the 2008 California ballot initiative) guaranteeing victim rights. Germany's victim rights framework has evolved through successive reforms, including the Victim Protection Act (Opferschutzgesetz) of 1986, the Victim Rights Reform Act (Opferrechtsreformgesetz) of 2004, the Third Victim Rights Reform Act of 2015, and the unique institution of Nebenklage (accessory prosecution) under Sections 395-402 of the Strafprozessordnung (StPO), which grants victims of serious offenses the right to join criminal proceedings as a party with independent procedural rights. At the European level, EU Directive 2012/29/EU establishing minimum standards on the rights, support, and protection of victims of crime represents the most comprehensive supranational victim rights instrument, while the European Court of Human Rights has developed positive obligation jurisprudence requiring states to conduct effective investigations and protect victims under Articles 2, 3, and 8 of the Convention.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's victim rights framework rests on three pillars: constitutional protection, statutory rights, and institutional support services. Article 27(5) of the Constitution provides that "crime victims shall be entitled to make a statement during the course of the trial under the conditions as prescribed by Act," establishing victim participation as a constitutional right rather than merely a statutory privilege.

### Crime Victim Protection Act (범죄피해자 보호법)

Enacted in 2005 and substantially amended in 2010, 2014, and 2020, the Act establishes comprehensive rights for crime victims:

**Right to Information (알 권리):** Victims have the right to be informed of the progress and outcome of criminal proceedings, including charging decisions, trial dates, verdicts, and sentencing. The prosecution is required to notify victims at each stage of proceedings.

**Right to Participate (참여할 권리):** Beyond the constitutional right to make statements, the Act provides for victim impact statements at sentencing, the right to attend trial proceedings, and the right to review investigation records with certain restrictions.

**Right to Protection (보호받을 권리):** The Act establishes victim protection measures including confidentiality of identity, testimony via closed-circuit television, courtroom screens, and accompaniment by support persons during testimony.

**Right to Support (지원받을 권리):** The Act created the Crime Victim Support Centers (범죄피해자지원센터), operating nationwide through 61 centers providing counseling, legal assistance, emergency financial aid, and referral services.

### Crime Victim Compensation Act (범죄피해자구조법)

Originally enacted in 1987 and comprehensively revised in 2010, the Act establishes a state-funded compensation system for victims of violent crime who cannot obtain adequate compensation from the offender. Compensation covers:

| Category | Maximum Amount |
|----------|---------------|
| Death of victim | Up to 60 million KRW (approximately $50,000) |
| Serious disability | Up to 40 million KRW |
| Serious injury | Up to 20 million KRW |
| Medical expenses | Actual costs up to prescribed limits |

### Victim-Offender Mediation

The Criminal Mediation Act (형사조정법, 2010) established a formal framework for victim-offender mediation (형사조정), administered through prosecutor's offices. Participation is voluntary, and agreements reached through mediation may be considered by the court in sentencing.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has developed victim rights through a combination of federal legislation, state constitutional amendments, and institutional innovation, though the absence of a federal constitutional amendment remains a significant gap.

### Crime Victims' Rights Act (CVRA) -- 18 U.S.C. Section 3771

Enacted as part of the Justice for All Act of 2004, the CVRA established eight enforceable rights for victims of federal crimes:

1. The right to be reasonably protected from the accused
2. The right to reasonable, accurate, and timely notice of any public court proceeding or parole proceeding
3. The right not to be excluded from any such public proceeding unless the court determines testimony would be materially altered
4. The right to be reasonably heard at any public proceeding in the district court involving release, plea, sentencing, or parole
5. The reasonable right to confer with the attorney for the government
6. The right to full and timely restitution as provided in law
7. The right to proceedings free from unreasonable delay
8. The right to be treated with fairness and with respect for the victim's dignity and privacy

### Enforcement

The CVRA includes an enforcement mechanism through 18 U.S.C. Section 3771(d)(3), permitting victims to file a petition for mandamus in the court of appeals if their rights are denied. This provision was invoked in the landmark case In re Dean, 527 F.3d 391 (5th Cir. 2008), where the Fifth Circuit granted mandamus relief ordering the district court to allow victim impact testimony.

### Marsy's Law

Named after Marsalee Nicholas, who was murdered in 1983 by her ex-boyfriend, Marsy's Law refers to state constitutional amendments guaranteeing victim rights. California adopted the first Marsy's Law in 2008 (Proposition 9), and as of 2025, similar amendments have been adopted in 37 states, typically guaranteeing rights to notification, protection, participation, restitution, and speedy proceedings.

### Violence Against Women Act (VAWA)

First enacted in 1994 and reauthorized in 2000, 2005, 2013, and 2022, VAWA established federal programs addressing domestic violence, sexual assault, and stalking, including funding for victim services, the creation of the National Domestic Violence Hotline, provisions for immigrant victim protections (U-visa), and tribal jurisdiction provisions expanded significantly in the 2022 reauthorization.

### Restitution

Federal restitution is governed by the Mandatory Victims Restitution Act (18 U.S.C. Sections 3663A, 3664), which requires courts to order restitution for victims of specified offenses including crimes of violence, certain property offenses, and offenses under the sexual exploitation and abuse of children statutes. The Supreme Court in Lagos v. United States, 584 U.S. 169 (2018), limited restitution to losses directly caused by the offense of conviction.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany provides one of the most robust victim participation mechanisms in any legal system through the institution of Nebenklage (accessory prosecution), which grants victims of serious offenses the status of a party to criminal proceedings with independent procedural rights.

### Nebenklage (Accessory Prosecution) -- StPO Sections 395-402

Under Section 395 StPO, victims of specified serious offenses may join the criminal proceedings as accessory prosecutors (Nebenkläger). Eligible offenses include attempted homicide, sexual offenses, serious bodily harm, offenses against personal liberty, stalking, and offenses under various specialized statutes. The Nebenkläger has the right to:

- Attend all court proceedings, including those from which the public is excluded
- Ask questions of witnesses, experts, and the defendant
- Object to questions and procedural orders
- Make statements on evidence
- Present closing arguments
- File appeals against the judgment

This is a significantly stronger participatory role than any equivalent in the other jurisdictions examined, as the Nebenkläger functions essentially as a second prosecutor with independent procedural standing.

### Legal Representation for Victims

Under Section 397a StPO, victims who qualify as Nebenkläger in cases involving sexual offenses, attempted homicide, and other serious offenses are entitled to court-appointed legal representation at state expense, regardless of their financial means. This right was expanded by the Third Victim Rights Reform Act of 2015 (Drittes Opferrechtsreformgesetz), which also extended legal aid eligibility to victims of hate crimes and forced marriage.

### Adhesion Procedure (Adhäsionsverfahren)

Sections 403-406c StPO provide an adhesion procedure enabling victims to pursue civil compensation claims within the criminal proceedings rather than filing separate civil litigation. The court may award damages for physical injury, pain and suffering, property damage, and other losses directly caused by the offense.

### Victim Protection Reforms

Germany has enacted three major victim rights reform acts:

| Reform | Year | Key Provisions |
|--------|------|---------------|
| Victim Protection Act (Opferschutzgesetz) | 1986 | Introduced victim's right to join as Nebenkläger; victim examination protections |
| Victim Rights Reform Act (Opferrechtsreformgesetz) | 2004 | Expanded Nebenklage eligibility; enhanced information rights; improved video testimony |
| Third Victim Rights Reform Act (Drittes Opferrechtsreformgesetz) | 2015 | Extended legal aid to additional categories; psychosocial support during proceedings; enhanced protection for child witnesses |

### Psychosocial Support (Psychosoziale Prozessbegleitung)

The Third Victim Rights Reform Act of 2015 introduced the right to psychosocial support during criminal proceedings (psychosoziale Prozessbegleitung), implemented through Section 406g StPO. Specially trained professionals accompany victims through the criminal justice process, providing emotional support, explaining procedures, and helping victims exercise their rights. This form of support is distinct from legal representation and is available to particularly vulnerable victims, especially children, victims of sexual violence, and victims of serious violent crime.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Directive 2012/29/EU (Victims' Rights Directive)

Adopted on October 25, 2012, and required to be transposed by November 16, 2015, the Victims' Rights Directive establishes minimum standards on the rights, support, and protection of victims of crime across all EU member states. Key provisions include:

**Right to Information (Articles 4-6):** Victims must be provided with information about their rights, available support services, how to access legal advice, and how to participate in proceedings from first contact with a competent authority.

**Right to Understand and Be Understood (Article 3):** All communications with victims must be in simple and accessible language, taking into account personal characteristics including disability, age, and maturity.

**Right to Be Heard (Article 10):** Victims must have the right to be heard during criminal proceedings and to provide evidence, though the procedural rules are determined by national law.

**Individual Assessment of Protection Needs (Article 22):** All victims must receive a timely individual assessment to identify specific protection needs and determine whether they would benefit from special measures during proceedings.

**Right to Protection (Articles 18-24):** Victims must be protected from secondary victimization, intimidation, and retaliation, with special measures available for vulnerable victims including the use of communication technology for testimony, screening measures, and avoidance of unnecessary contact with the offender.

### ECHR Positive Obligations

The European Court of Human Rights has developed extensive jurisprudence requiring states to protect victims through effective investigation and prosecution:

**Osman v. United Kingdom, Application No. 23452/94, Judgment of October 28, 1998:** The Court established that Article 2 imposes a positive obligation on states to take preventive operational measures to protect an individual whose life is at risk from the criminal acts of another, provided the authorities knew or ought to have known of a real and immediate risk.

**M.C. v. Bulgaria, Application No. 39272/98, Judgment of December 4, 2003:** The Court held that Article 3 requires states to penalize and effectively prosecute any non-consensual sexual act, and that the investigation and prosecution of sexual offenses must not be dependent on proof of physical resistance by the victim.

**Opuz v. Turkey, Application No. 33401/02, Judgment of June 9, 2009:** The Court found violations of Articles 2, 3, and 14 where Turkish authorities had failed to protect the applicant and her mother from repeated domestic violence that ultimately resulted in the mother's murder. The judgment established that systematic failure to protect domestic violence victims can constitute gender-based discrimination.

### EU Compensation Directive

Council Directive 2004/80/EC relating to compensation to crime victims requires each member state to ensure that its national rules provide for a scheme of compensation to victims of violent intentional crime committed in its territory, and establishes a system for cross-border access to compensation within the EU.

## Comparative Analysis

### Level of Participation

Germany provides the strongest victim participation rights through the Nebenklage system, granting victims quasi-party status with the right to question witnesses, make statements, and appeal. South Korea's constitutional right to make statements and the United States' CVRA right to be heard are more limited, typically restricted to specific proceedings such as sentencing. The EU Directive sets a minimum floor of the right to be heard without specifying the precise mechanism.

### Compensation Systems

All four jurisdictions provide for victim compensation, but through different mechanisms. Germany's adhesion procedure integrates civil claims into criminal proceedings. The United States emphasizes mandatory restitution orders by the sentencing court. South Korea maintains a state-funded compensation fund for violent crime victims. The EU Directive requires member states to ensure access to compensation schemes, with cross-border access facilitated by Directive 2004/80/EC.

### Enforcement

The United States provides the most explicit enforcement mechanism through the CVRA's mandamus provision. Germany's enforcement relies on the general rights of procedural parties, including the right to appeal. South Korea's enforcement mechanisms are primarily administrative. The EU Directive's enforcement depends on national transposition and EU infringement proceedings.

### Constitutional Status

South Korea is unique in constitutionalizing the right of victim participation in Article 27(5). In the United States, 37 states have adopted constitutional victim rights amendments, but no federal constitutional amendment exists. Germany derives victim protections from the general dignity clause of Article 1 GG rather than a specific victim rights provision.
