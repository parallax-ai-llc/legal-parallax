## Summary

Divorce law across the Global South demonstrates the deep imprint of cultural, religious, and ideological commitments on the legal regulation of marriage dissolution. China's Civil Code (2021, Book Five) introduced a mandatory 30-day "cooling-off period" (离婚冷静期, Article 1077) for consensual divorces, a measure that provoked intense public debate about state paternalism versus individual autonomy, particularly among women's rights advocates who argued it would trap victims in abusive marriages; alongside this, China maintains a dual-track system of administrative divorce (协议离婚) by mutual consent at the Civil Affairs Bureau and judicial divorce (诉讼离婚) through the People's Courts. India's divorce landscape was transformed by the Supreme Court's landmark decision in Shayara Bano v. Union of India (2017) 9 SCC 1, which struck down the practice of instantaneous triple talaq (talaq-e-biddat) as unconstitutional, followed by the Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Marriage) Act, 2019, which criminalized the practice; Hindu divorce under section 13 of the Hindu Marriage Act 1955 requires proof of specific grounds unless proceeding by mutual consent under section 13B. South Africa's Recognition of Customary Marriages Act 120 of 1998 brought customary marriage dissolution within the statutory framework, though the intersection between living customary law and statutory requirements remains contested, as demonstrated in Mayelane v. Ngwenyama [2013] ZACC 14 and Ramuhovhi v. President of the Republic of South Africa [2018] ZACC 7. Brazil underwent a paradigm shift with Constitutional Amendment No. 66 of 2010 (Emenda Constitucional 66/2010), which eliminated all fault requirements and waiting periods for divorce, establishing what is arguably the most liberal divorce regime in the Global South -- a direct divorce (divórcio direto) can now be obtained at a notary office (cartório) within days if consensual and without minor children. The Philippines stands alone as the only country in the world (apart from Vatican City) that does not permit divorce for the general population, forcing citizens to pursue the costly and time-consuming remedy of annulment under the Family Code or, for Muslim Filipinos, divorce under the Code of Muslim Personal Laws (Presidential Decree No. 1083); Indonesia's Religious Courts (Pengadilan Agama) handle Muslim divorce through distinct procedures for husband-initiated talaq (cerai talak) and wife-initiated khuluk/gugatan (cerai gugat), with the striking trend that wife-initiated divorces now account for approximately 70% of all divorce filings in Religious Courts.

## China

### Dual-Track Divorce System

China maintains two pathways for divorce, both codified in the Civil Code (2021):

**Administrative Divorce (协议离婚, Articles 1076-1078):** Available when both spouses agree to divorce, they have reached agreement on child custody and property division, and both appear in person at the marriage registration authority. The process involves:

1. **Application:** Both parties submit a divorce agreement to the Civil Affairs Bureau.
2. **Cooling-Off Period (Article 1077):** Within 30 days of application, either party may withdraw the application. This "divorce cooling-off period" (离婚冷静期) was the most controversial innovation of the 2021 Civil Code. During legislative deliberation, the provision received over 100,000 public comments, the most for any Civil Code provision.
3. **Confirmation Period:** After the 30-day cooling-off period, both parties must appear within the next 30 days to confirm the application. Failure to appear is deemed withdrawal.
4. **Certificate Issuance:** The registration authority issues divorce certificates upon confirmation.

The cooling-off period was criticized by feminist scholars and domestic violence advocates, including those affiliated with the Equality Beijing organization, who argued it could endanger victims by delaying exit from abusive marriages. The NPC Standing Committee responded that the cooling-off period applies only to administrative divorce by mutual consent, not to judicial divorce, and that victims of domestic violence could proceed directly to litigation. However, critics note that judicial divorce is more expensive, time-consuming, and emotionally burdensome.

**Judicial Divorce (诉讼离婚, Articles 1079-1081):** Either spouse may file for divorce in the People's Court. Article 1079 establishes the framework:

- The court must first attempt mediation (调解). If mediation fails and the relationship has irretrievably broken down (感情确已破裂), the court shall grant the divorce.
- Specific circumstances constituting breakdown include: bigamy or cohabitation with a third party, domestic violence or abuse, gambling or drug addiction, and separation for two continuous years.
- **The "first rejection" problem:** Chinese courts frequently reject first-time divorce petitions, particularly where the non-filing spouse objects. After a petition is rejected, the filing spouse must wait six months before refiling (Article 1079, paragraph 5, added in 2021, provides that if the court rejects a divorce petition and the parties have been separated for a further year, the court shall grant the divorce upon refiling -- addressing the notorious "three-petition" divorce problem).

### Property Division

The Civil Code distinguishes between personal property (个人财产, Article 1063) and community property (共同财产, Article 1062). The Supreme People's Court has issued multiple judicial interpretations addressing contentious issues:
- **Housing:** SPC Interpretation I of the Marriage and Family Book (2021) addresses situations where one spouse's parents contributed to a home purchase, codifying rules that generated public outcry in the earlier Marriage Law Interpretation III (2011) for their perceived disadvantage to women.
- **Debts:** Article 1064 establishes that debts incurred by both spouses or by one spouse for family needs are community debts; debts incurred by one spouse individually for non-family purposes are personal debts, shifting the burden from the approach that previously presumed community liability.

## India

### Hindu Divorce

Section 13 of the Hindu Marriage Act, 1955, provides grounds for divorce applicable to Hindus, Buddhists, Jains, and Sikhs:

1. Adultery (extra-marital sexual intercourse)
2. Cruelty (mental or physical)
3. Desertion for a continuous period of not less than two years
4. Conversion to another religion
5. Unsoundness of mind or mental disorder
6. Virulent and incurable leprosy (this ground has been criticized as discriminatory)
7. Venereal disease in communicable form
8. Renunciation of the world by entering a religious order
9. Not heard of as being alive for seven years

Section 13B provides for divorce by mutual consent, requiring that the parties have lived separately for at least one year, have agreed that the marriage should be dissolved, and file a joint petition. A six-month cooling-off period (second motion) was mandated under section 13B(2), though the Supreme Court in **Amardeep Singh v. Harveen Kaur (2017) 8 SCC 746** held that courts may waive this period in appropriate cases. The Court in **Shilpa Sailesh v. Varun Sreenivasan (2023)** went further, invoking Article 142 powers to directly grant mutual consent divorce.

### The Triple Talaq Revolution

Instantaneous triple talaq (talaq-e-biddat) -- whereby a Muslim husband could irrevocably divorce his wife by pronouncing "talaq" three times in a single sitting -- was a practice with deep roots in Hanafi jurisprudence but disputed by other Islamic schools and reformists.

In **Shayara Bano v. Union of India (2017) 9 SCC 1**, a five-judge Constitution Bench delivered a 3:2 majority striking down triple talaq. The majority opinions diverged in reasoning:
- Justice Nariman (joined by Justice Lalit) held that triple talaq was "manifestly arbitrary" and violated Article 14 (equality before law), applying Part III fundamental rights directly to personal law.
- Justice Joseph (for himself, Chief Justice Khehar, and Justice Nazeer) relied on the Quran itself, holding that triple talaq is not integral to Islam and therefore not protected under Article 25 (freedom of religion).
- Chief Justice Khehar and Justice Nazeer dissented, holding that personal law was beyond judicial review under Part III but recommended that Parliament legislate.

The **Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Marriage) Act, 2019** followed, making the pronouncement of triple talaq a criminal offense punishable by up to three years' imprisonment. The Act has been criticized for criminalizing a civil matter and for the provision allowing the wife to be the complainant while the husband's bail requires the wife's consent.

### Maintenance

Section 125 of the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 (now Section 144 of the Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita, 2023), provides a secular maintenance remedy available to wives, children, and parents of all religions. The Supreme Court in **Mohd. Ahmed Khan v. Shah Bano Begum (1985) 2 SCC 556** held that Muslim women were entitled to maintenance under Section 125 CrPC, triggering a major political crisis that led to the Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Divorce) Act, 1986 -- which the Supreme Court subsequently interpreted in **Danial Latifi v. Union of India (2001) 7 SCC 740** to require reasonable provision during the iddat period and beyond.

## Africa

### Nigeria: Tripartite Divorce System

**Statutory Divorce:** The Matrimonial Causes Act 1970 governs dissolution of statutory marriages. Section 15(1) provides that the sole ground for dissolution is that the marriage has broken down irretrievably. Section 15(2) specifies facts from which breakdown may be inferred: adultery, unreasonable behavior, desertion for at least one year, living apart for at least two years (with respondent's consent) or three years (without consent).

Before filing a petition, the parties must have attempted reconciliation, and the court has discretion to adjourn proceedings for reconciliation (section 11). Nigerian courts have elaborated on these provisions in cases including Akinbuwa v. Akinbuwa [1998] 7 NWLR (Pt. 559) 661 and Damulak v. Damulak [2004] 8 NWLR (Pt. 874) 151.

**Customary Divorce:** Dissolution of customary marriages follows the customs of the parties' ethnic group. Common elements include: return of bride-price, consent or involvement of extended families, and (in some traditions) a public declaration. Courts have recognized the validity of customary divorce in Adekunle v. Adekunle [2002] 14 NWLR (Pt. 788) 427.

**Islamic Divorce:** In Sharia courts operating in northern states, divorce may be effected through talaq (husband's unilateral pronouncement), khul' (wife-initiated dissolution upon return of mahr or other consideration), or faskh (judicial dissolution on specified grounds such as cruelty, desertion, or impotence).

### South Africa: Divorce Act and Customary Law

The **Divorce Act 70 of 1979** governs dissolution of civil marriages and, after the RCMA, extends to registered customary marriages. The sole ground is irretrievable breakdown of the marriage (section 4), demonstrated by: continuous unconscionability, habitual criminality, drug/alcohol addiction, or the parties having lived apart for a continuous period.

**Customary Marriage Dissolution:** Before the RCMA, customary marriages were dissolved according to living customary law, typically involving family negotiation and return of lobola (bride-price). The RCMA brought customary marriages within the Divorce Act framework, but the Constitutional Court has addressed complications:

- **Mayelane v. Ngwenyama [2013] ZACC 14:** The Court held that the consent of the first wife is a requirement for the validity of a subsequent customary marriage, overturning the SCA's finding that Tsonga customary law did not require such consent. This affected the status of women in polygynous marriages and their rights upon divorce.
- **Ramuhovhi v. President of the Republic of South Africa [2018] ZACC 7:** The Court declared section 7(1) of the RCMA unconstitutional insofar as it subjected old customary marriages (entered before the RCMA) to the husband's exclusive ownership of marital property, finding this violated section 9 equality rights.

**Maintenance:** The Maintenance Act 99 of 1998 provides a mechanism for claiming maintenance, while the Divorce Act section 7(2) allows the court to order a redistribution of assets on divorce from a marriage out of community of property, considering each party's contributions and future needs.

## Latin America

### Brazil: The No-Fault Revolution

Brazil's divorce law underwent a radical transformation in stages:

1. **Pre-1977:** Divorce was prohibited under the 1891 and 1946 Constitutions, which declared marriage indissoluble. Only desquite (legal separation) was available.
2. **1977:** The Divorce Law (Lei No. 6.515/1977, the "Divórcio Lei") introduced divorce for the first time, requiring prior legal separation of one year or de facto separation of five years.
3. **1988 Constitution:** Article 226, §6 reduced the waiting period (one year of legal separation or two years of de facto separation).
4. **Constitutional Amendment 66/2010 (EC 66/2010):** Eliminated all prior separation requirements and fault considerations. The amended Article 226, §6 now reads simply: "Civil marriage may be dissolved by divorce." This removed legal separation (separação judicial) as a prerequisite and made divorce available on demand.

**Extrajudicial Divorce (Divórcio Extrajudicial):** Law No. 11.441/2007 authorized consensual divorce at a notary office (cartório) when the parties agree, have no minor or incapacitated children, and are represented by a lawyer. The process takes as little as one day and costs a fraction of judicial proceedings. Resolution CNJ 35/2007 regulates the procedure.

**Judicial Divorce:** Required when there are minor children, when the parties disagree, or when one party is incapacitated. Filed in the Family Court (Vara de Família). Since EC 66/2010, no grounds need be alleged -- the mere expression of the desire to divorce suffices.

### Mexico: State-by-State Reform

Mexico's divorce law has been substantially reformed state by state. The Mexico City Civil Code was amended in 2008 to introduce "incausado" (no-cause) divorce, eliminating the requirement to allege specific grounds. The SCJN upheld this reform and in subsequent jurisprudencia extended the principle, finding that requiring fault grounds for divorce violated the right to free development of personality (libre desarrollo de la personalidad), a right derived from Article 1 of the Constitution and Article 11 of the American Convention on Human Rights. By 2025, the majority of Mexican states have adopted no-fault divorce.

### Colombia: Mixed System

Colombia permits divorce by mutual consent before a notary (Law 962 of 2005) or by judicial petition on specified grounds (Civil Code Art. 154), including extramarital relations, domestic violence, drug/alcohol addiction, and mutual consent. The Constitutional Court in Sentencia C-985/2010 upheld the constitutionality of fault-based grounds while emphasizing that the right to divorce is protected under the right to free development of personality (Article 16, Constitution).

## Southeast Asia

### Indonesia: The Religious Court System

Indonesia's divorce system is bifurcated by religion:

**Muslim Divorce (Religious Courts):** Governed by Law No. 7 of 1989 on Religious Courts (as amended), the Compilation of Islamic Law (KHI), and Law No. 1 of 1974.

- **Cerai Talak:** The husband files a petition at the Religious Court requesting permission to pronounce talaq. The court examines the grounds, attempts reconciliation (per Supreme Court Regulation/PERMA No. 1 of 2016 on mediation), and if reconciliation fails and grounds are established, grants permission. The husband then pronounces talaq before the court. Grounds include: adultery, alcoholism/drug addiction, gambling, desertion, imprisonment for five or more years, domestic violence, disability preventing marital duties, and continuous disputes (KHI Article 116).
- **Cerai Gugat:** Wife-initiated divorce through a lawsuit (gugatan). The wife may seek fasakh (judicial dissolution), khuluk (dissolution with compensation to husband), or divorce on the same grounds as cerai talak. Notably, cerai gugat has grown from approximately 50% of Religious Court divorce cases in the early 2000s to approximately 70% by 2023, reflecting increased female agency and economic independence.
- **Reconciliation Requirement:** PERMA No. 1 of 2016 requires mediation in all divorce cases. A certified mediator (from the court or accredited) facilitates reconciliation efforts over a maximum of 30 days (extendable by 30 days). Failure to attempt mediation is grounds for appeal.

**Non-Muslim Divorce (District Courts):** Governed by the Civil Code (BW/Burgerlijk Wetboek) and Government Regulation No. 9 of 1975. Grounds include adultery, malicious abandonment, imprisonment for five or more years, severe assault, disability, and continuous disputes.

**Property Division:** Joint marital property (harta bersama) acquired during the marriage is divided equally upon divorce (Article 37, Marriage Law; Article 97, KHI). Pre-marital property (harta bawaan) returns to the original owner. The Religious Courts apply these principles, though local adat (customary law) may influence the characterization of property.

### Philippines: Life Without Divorce

The absence of divorce in the Philippines creates a distinctive legal landscape:

**Annulment vs. Declaration of Nullity:** The Family Code distinguishes between:
- **Voidable Marriages (Articles 45-46):** Grounds include lack of parental consent (18-21), fraud, force/intimidation/undue influence, impotence, sexually transmissible disease. Must be filed within specified prescriptive periods. Results in annulment.
- **Void Marriages (Articles 35-36):** Marriages void from the beginning due to: absence of essential/formal requisites, bigamous marriages, and -- most significantly -- **psychological incapacity** under Article 36.

**Psychological Incapacity (Article 36):** This has become the primary vehicle for marriage dissolution. The Supreme Court in Republic v. Molina (1997) established eight stringent guidelines requiring that the incapacity be: (a) grave, (b) juridically antecedent (existing before marriage), and (c) incurable. Expert psychiatric/psychological testimony was effectively required.

The landmark decision in **Tan-Andal v. Andal (G.R. No. 196359, April 27, 2021)** substantially reformed Article 36 jurisprudence:
- Expert testimony is no longer mandatory -- "ordinary witnesses who have been present in the life of the spouses" may testify.
- Psychological incapacity need not be a clinical disorder -- it is a legal, not medical, concept.
- Incurability is not required in a medical sense -- it suffices that the condition is "enduring" in the legal sense.
- The "clear and convincing evidence" standard was maintained, but the Court acknowledged that Molina's guidelines had been applied too rigidly.

**Cost and Access:** Annulment proceedings in the Philippines typically cost PHP 150,000-500,000 (USD 2,700-9,000) in legal fees and take 2-5 years, making them inaccessible to most Filipinos. This has led to widespread informal separation without legal recognition, creating issues of property division, support, and remarriage capacity.

**The Absolute Divorce Bill:** House Bill No. 1979 (19th Congress, filed 2022, approved on third reading May 2024) proposes to allow absolute divorce on grounds including: irreconcilable differences after separation for at least five years, psychological incapacity, domestic violence, sexual infidelity, and other grounds. The bill would allow both judicial and administrative divorce pathways. Senate action remains pending, with opposition from the Catholic Bishops' Conference of the Philippines (CBCP) constituting the primary political obstacle.

## Comparative Analysis

### State Control vs. Individual Autonomy

China's cooling-off period and the Philippines' divorce prohibition represent the strongest forms of state intervention in marriage dissolution, though from entirely different ideological foundations -- China's intervention is paternalistic-pragmatic (reducing "impulsive" divorces) while the Philippines' prohibition is religious-constitutional (marriage as indissoluble sacrament). Brazil's EC 66/2010 represents the opposite pole: maximum individual autonomy with no waiting period, no fault requirement, and an extrajudicial pathway.

### Religious Law and Divorce

India and Indonesia demonstrate contrasting approaches to religious divorce. India has moved toward limiting religious divorce prerogatives -- banning triple talaq (2017/2019) and subjecting Muslim divorce to constitutional standards. Indonesia has formalized religious divorce through the Religious Court system, creating a state-administered but religiously-grounded process. Nigeria maintains a parallel Sharia court system with less integration into the general legal framework.

### Gender and Divorce Access

A striking trend across the Global South is the increase in wife-initiated divorce: Indonesia's cerai gugat surge (70% of divorce filings), India's growing use of Section 13B mutual consent and Section 13 cruelty grounds, and Brazil's no-barrier divorce system all reflect increased female agency. Conversely, the Philippines' annulment-only system disproportionately affects women of lower socioeconomic status who cannot afford the process.

### Property Division Regimes

The default marital property regime varies significantly: China and Indonesia divide community property equally; Brazil defaults to partial community of property; South Africa applies full community of property to civil marriages and (after Gumede) to customary marriages; India has no community property by default under Hindu law, with the wife claiming maintenance and streedhan (her personal property) but no automatic share of the husband's estate -- a gap that the Hindu Succession (Amendment) Act 2005 partially addressed for ancestral property.
