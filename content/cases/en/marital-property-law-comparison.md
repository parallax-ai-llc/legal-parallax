## Summary

Marital property law -- the body of rules governing the ownership, management, and division of assets acquired during marriage and upon its dissolution -- represents one of the most consequential areas of family law, directly affecting the economic security and autonomy of spouses both during and after marriage. South Korea's default matrimonial property regime under the Civil Act (민법) is separate property (별산제, Article 830), under which each spouse retains ownership of property acquired in their own name during marriage, supplemented by a property division claim (재산분할청구권, Article 839-2) upon divorce that allows the court to equitably divide property by considering each spouse's contributions to its acquisition and maintenance; the Supreme Court (대법원) has progressively expanded the recognition of homemaking and childcare contributions, moving toward a presumptive equal division in long-term marriages, and has held that the property division claim is an independent right that cannot be waived in advance. The United States is divided between community property states (nine states, predominantly in the West and South, following Spanish and French legal traditions) where property acquired during marriage is presumptively owned equally by both spouses, and equitable distribution states (the remaining forty-one states and the District of Columbia) where marital property is divided "equitably" by the court based on statutory factors; the Supreme Court in United States v. Windsor, 570 U.S. 744 (2013) and Obergefell v. Hodges, 576 U.S. 644 (2015) extended marital property rights to same-sex couples. Germany's default matrimonial property regime is the Zugewinngemeinschaft (community of accrued gains, BGB Sections 1363-1390), a sophisticated hybrid system under which spouses maintain separate property during the marriage but upon dissolution equalize the gains (Zugewinn) each has accumulated, with the spouse having the lesser gain receiving a compensatory claim for half the difference; alternative regimes include contractual community of property (Gütergemeinschaft, BGB Sections 1415-1518) and separation of property (Gütertrennung, BGB Section 1414). The European framework includes the EU Matrimonial Property Regulation (No 2016/1103), adopted through enhanced cooperation and applicable since January 29, 2019, which establishes uniform conflict-of-law rules for matrimonial property matters in cross-border cases, the ECHR's Article 1 Protocol 1 jurisprudence on property rights in the context of marriage dissolution, and comparative perspectives from the Commission on European Family Law's Principles of European Family Law.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's matrimonial property regime is governed by Chapter 3, Section 1 of the Civil Act (민법 제3편 제1장).

### Separate Property System (별산제)

**Article 830(1):** Property owned by one spouse prior to marriage and property acquired in one's own name during marriage belongs to that spouse individually (특유재산).

**Article 830(2):** Property whose ownership cannot be established to belong to either spouse is presumed to be jointly owned.

**Article 831:** Each spouse manages their own separate property independently.

### Property Division Upon Divorce (재산분할청구권)

**Article 839-2:** Upon divorce, either party may claim division of property against the other party. The family court determines the division considering:

| Factor | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| Amount and nature of property | All property acquired during the marriage |
| Contributions to acquisition | Financial contributions of each spouse |
| Homemaking contributions | Childcare, household management, support of other spouse's career |
| Future livelihood | Post-divorce economic prospects of each spouse |
| Duration of marriage | Longer marriages tend toward more equal division |
| Other circumstances | All relevant circumstances |

### Supreme Court Jurisprudence

**Property Division Ratio:** The Supreme Court has gradually increased recognition of the non-earning spouse's contributions. In long-term marriages (generally 20+ years), courts typically award 40-50 percent to the non-earning spouse, with some decisions reaching 50 percent. The specific ratio depends on the totality of circumstances.

**Supreme Court Decision 93므1002 (1993):** Established that homemaking contributions constitute a genuine contribution to property formation, eligible for recognition in property division.

**Supreme Court Decision 2012므4130 (2014):** Held that the property division claim under Article 839-2 is an independent right arising from divorce, distinct from consolation money (위자료), and encompasses all types of marital property including real estate, financial assets, and retirement benefits.

**Supreme Court Decision 2013므568 (2013):** Recognized the "support element" (부양적 요소) in property division, allowing courts to increase the division ratio for the economically disadvantaged spouse beyond what contribution alone would justify.

### Retirement Benefits and Pensions

The National Pension Act (국민연금법) Article 64 provides for division of pension rights. The non-contributing spouse who was married for at least five years during the contribution period is entitled to a division of the contributing spouse's old-age pension upon divorce. This was enacted in 1999 and applies to marriages of sufficient duration overlapping with pension contribution periods.

### Hidden Assets

The discovery and valuation of hidden assets is a significant practical challenge. Korean courts may issue financial investigation orders (재산조사명령), and the 2020 amendments to the Family Litigation Act strengthened courts' powers to investigate and discover marital assets, including bank accounts, real property, and securities holdings.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has two fundamentally different marital property systems:

### Community Property States

Nine states follow community property principles: Arizona, California, Idaho, Louisiana, Nevada, New Mexico, Texas, Washington, and Wisconsin (via the Uniform Marital Property Act).

**Key Principles:**
- Property acquired during marriage through spousal labor or skill is community property, owned equally by both spouses.
- Property acquired before marriage, or during marriage by gift or inheritance, is separate property.
- Upon divorce, community property is divided equally (some states permit equitable departure).
- Debts incurred during marriage are generally community obligations.

**California Example (Cal. Fam. Code Section 2550):** "The court shall divide the community estate of the parties equally."

### Equitable Distribution States

The remaining forty-one states and the District of Columbia follow equitable distribution principles.

**Key Principles:**
- Marital property is divided "equitably" (fairly), which may or may not mean equally.
- Courts consider statutory factors including duration of marriage, each spouse's contributions (financial and homemaking), age and health of parties, earning capacity, and tax consequences.
- Separate property (pre-marital, inherited, gifted) is typically excluded from division.

**New York Example (DRL Section 236(B)(5)(d)):** Lists thirteen factors for equitable distribution.

### Significant Assets

| Asset Type | Treatment |
|-----------|-----------|
| Real estate | Divisible marital property if acquired during marriage |
| Retirement accounts | Divided by Qualified Domestic Relations Order (QDRO) under ERISA |
| Business interests | Valued and divided; may require expert appraisal |
| Professional goodwill | Varies by state: some include, some exclude |
| Stock options | Generally divisible if earned during marriage |
| Digital assets / cryptocurrency | Increasingly addressed; valuation challenges |

### Uniform Premarital and Marital Agreements Act (UPMAA)

The UPMAA (2012) superseded the Uniform Premarital Agreement Act (UPAA, 1983) and provides a framework for both premarital and postmarital agreements. Key provisions include requirements for voluntary execution, fair disclosure of assets, and the agreement not being unconscionable at the time of enforcement. Not all states have adopted the UPMAA.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German matrimonial property law offers three regimes:

### Zugewinngemeinschaft (Default Regime)

**BGB Section 1363:** If the spouses do not agree on a different regime by marital agreement, the Zugewinngemeinschaft applies. Despite its name ("community of accrued gains"), this is not a community property system during the marriage. Each spouse retains separate ownership and management of their property.

**Equalization of Gains (Zugewinnausgleich):** Upon termination of the regime (divorce, death, or contractual termination), each spouse's Zugewinn (gain) during the marriage is calculated:

| Step | Calculation |
|------|-------------|
| 1. Initial assets (Anfangsvermögen) | Assets at marriage commencement (BGB Section 1374) |
| 2. Final assets (Endvermögen) | Assets at regime termination (BGB Section 1375) |
| 3. Gain (Zugewinn) | Final assets minus initial assets |
| 4. Equalization claim | Spouse with lesser gain receives half the difference |

**Example:** If Spouse A has a gain of 200,000 EUR and Spouse B has a gain of 80,000 EUR, the equalization claim is (200,000 - 80,000) / 2 = 60,000 EUR, payable by A to B.

### Alternative Regimes

**Gütertrennung (Separation of Property, BGB Section 1414):** No equalization of gains. Each spouse retains all property upon dissolution. Must be agreed in a notarized marital agreement (Ehevertrag).

**Gütergemeinschaft (Community of Property, BGB Sections 1415-1518):** True community ownership of a common fund (Gesamtgut). Rarely chosen in practice due to complexity.

**Wahl-Zugewinngemeinschaft (Optional Community of Accrued Gains):** Introduced by the German-French Agreement of 2010, this transnational option is available to Franco-German and other cross-border couples, based on common principles of both systems.

### Marital Agreement (Ehevertrag)

**BGB Section 1408:** Spouses may modify their property regime through a notarized marital agreement (Ehevertrag). The agreement may also address maintenance and pension equalization. However, the Bundesgerichtshof (BGH) has established limits on marital agreements:

**BGH Decision XII ZR 265/99 (2004):** The Court established a core area (Kernbereich) of post-divorce protection that cannot be completely waived by marital agreement. Agreements that cumulatively eliminate all financial protections (property equalization, maintenance, and pension equalization) may be subject to judicial control for unconscionability.

### Negative Gains

A significant feature of the Zugewinngemeinschaft is that negative gains (where a spouse's final assets are less than initial assets) are treated as zero for purposes of the equalization calculation. One spouse's losses are not imposed on the other. This was modified by the 2009 reform (Gesetz zur Änderung des Zugewinnausgleichs), which eliminated the previous rule that gains could not exceed the actual value of final assets, and permitted negative initial assets to be counted.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Matrimonial Property Regulation (No 2016/1103)

Council Regulation (EU) 2016/1103 of June 24, 2016, implementing enhanced cooperation in the area of jurisdiction, applicable law, and recognition and enforcement of decisions in matters of matrimonial property regimes, entered into application on January 29, 2019. As of 2026, 18 member states participate.

**Applicable Law:** In the absence of a choice by the spouses, the applicable law is determined by a cascade:
1. Law of the spouses' first common habitual residence after the marriage
2. Law of the spouses' common nationality at the time of marriage
3. Law with which the spouses jointly have the closest connection at the time of marriage

**Choice of Law (Article 22):** Spouses may choose the law of a state of habitual residence or nationality of either spouse.

**Recognition and Enforcement:** Decisions on matrimonial property rendered in one participating member state are recognized in others without special procedure (Article 36).

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Pla and Puncernau v. Andorra (Application No. 69498/01, July 13, 2004):** The Court found a violation of Article 14 with Article 8 in the discriminatory application of succession rules distinguishing between children born in and out of wedlock. While primarily a succession case, it has implications for marital property rights of children.

**Belli and Arquier-Martinez v. Switzerland (Application No. 65550/13, December 11, 2018):** The Court addressed a claim that matrimonial property settlement was unfair but declared it inadmissible, confirming that Article 1 Protocol 1 may apply to property division claims but states enjoy a wide margin of appreciation in designing their property regimes.

### Commission on European Family Law (CEFL)

The CEFL has published Principles of European Family Law Regarding Property Relations Between Spouses (2013), proposing:
- Recognition of both community property and separate property with equalization as valid models
- Equal sharing of gains during marriage as the default rule
- Recognition of homemaking contributions
- Limitations on agreements that produce manifestly unfair results

These Principles are not binding but serve as a comparative reference for national reforms.

### Property Rights of Unmarried Partners

The EU framework does not address property rights of unmarried partners. The ECHR has addressed this in Burden v. United Kingdom (2008), finding no discrimination in excluding cohabiting siblings from the tax treatment available to married couples, while acknowledging that the property rights of unmarried partners remain a matter for national law.

## Comparative Analysis

### Regime Design

The three jurisdictions employ fundamentally different default regimes. South Korea's separate property system is the simplest during marriage but creates significant issues at dissolution. Germany's Zugewinngemeinschaft elegantly combines marital autonomy during the marriage with equitable sharing at dissolution. The U.S. community property system vests concurrent ownership during the marriage, while equitable distribution states use a separate property default with equitable division at dissolution. Each system reflects different assumptions about the nature of marriage as an economic partnership.

### Equal vs. Equitable Division

A key divergence is between equal division (German Zugewinnausgleich; U.S. community property states) and equitable/discretionary division (South Korea; U.S. equitable distribution states). Equal division provides predictability and consistency but may produce unfair results in atypical circumstances. Equitable division provides flexibility but creates uncertainty and litigation incentive.

### Recognition of Non-Financial Contributions

All jurisdictions recognize homemaking and childcare contributions, but the mechanism differs. Germany's Zugewinngemeinschaft inherently recognizes non-financial contributions because the stay-at-home spouse's gain is equalized against the earning spouse's gain. U.S. courts explicitly list homemaking as a factor. South Korea has progressively expanded recognition through Supreme Court jurisprudence, moving from low recognition in earlier decades to near-equal treatment in long-term marriages.

### Pension and Retirement Benefits

Germany's separate Versorgungsausgleich system for pension division is the most comprehensive, treating pension equalization as a distinct legal mechanism separate from property division. The United States handles pension division through QDROs within the property division framework. South Korea provides for pension division under the National Pension Act but with less comprehensive coverage.
