## Summary

Special education law -- the legal framework governing the education of children with disabilities, including the right to appropriate educational services, inclusive placement, and reasonable accommodations -- reflects a global trend toward recognizing education as a fundamental right that must be adapted to the needs of all learners, though the scope of obligations, enforcement mechanisms, and inclusion philosophies vary substantially across jurisdictions. South Korea enacted the Act on Special Education for Persons with Disabilities and Others (장애인 등에 대한 특수교육법) in 2007 (effective May 2008), replacing the earlier 1994 Special Education Promotion Act, establishing the right of children with disabilities to free and appropriate special education from birth through high school, mandating Individualized Education Programs (개별화교육계획, IEP), and requiring the progressive expansion of inclusive education (통합교육) in regular schools alongside maintaining special schools (특수학교) for students with severe disabilities. The United States provides the most extensively litigated special education framework through the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA, originally enacted as the Education for All Handicapped Children Act of 1975, most recently reauthorized in 2004), which guarantees a free appropriate public education (FAPE) in the least restrictive environment (LRE), requires individualized education programs developed with parental participation, and provides robust procedural safeguards including due process hearings and judicial review; the Supreme Court's decision in Endrew F. v. Douglas County School District (2017) raised the substantive standard from "merely more than de minimis" to "reasonably calculated to enable a child to make progress appropriate in light of the child's circumstances." Germany implements special education through the Länder school laws under the framework of the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD), which Germany ratified in 2009, triggering a shift from the traditional Förderschule (special school) system toward greater inclusion (Inklusion) in regular schools, though the transition has been contentious and implementation varies dramatically across the sixteen Länder. At the European level, the CRPD (ratified by the EU in 2011) and the European Court of Human Rights' jurisprudence under Protocol 1, Article 2 (right to education) read together with Article 14 (non-discrimination) have established that segregation of children with disabilities into special schools without adequate justification may violate Convention rights, while EU Directive 2000/78/EC on equal treatment in employment applies to educational staff accommodations and the European Agency for Special Needs and Inclusive Education coordinates policy across member states.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

The Act on Special Education for Persons with Disabilities and Others (장애인 등에 대한 특수교육법), enacted on May 25, 2007, provides the comprehensive framework for special education in South Korea. The Act replaced the 1994 Special Education Promotion Act and significantly expanded the scope and quality of special education services.

**Right to Education (Article 3):** The state and local governments are obligated to provide free special education to eligible persons with disabilities. The right covers tuition, textbooks, and educational materials.

**Eligible Disabilities (Article 15):** The Act covers 11 disability categories: visual impairment, hearing impairment, intellectual disability, physical disability, emotional and behavioral disorders, autism spectrum disorders, communication disorders, learning disabilities, health impairments, developmental delay, and other disabilities specified by presidential decree.

### Individualized Education Programs (개별화교육계획)

Article 22 requires that an individualized education program (IEP) be developed for each student receiving special education. The IEP must include:
- Assessment of the student's current performance level
- Educational goals and objectives
- Educational content, methods, and necessary support services
- Evaluation criteria and schedule

An IEP team (개별화교육지원팀) consisting of the school principal, special education teacher, general education teacher, parents, and relevant specialists develops the IEP within 30 days of placement.

### Inclusive Education (통합교육)

Article 21 establishes the principle of inclusive education, requiring that students with disabilities be educated with non-disabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate. Schools are required to provide necessary support services, including:
- Assistive technology and devices
- Special education aides (특수교육 보조인력)
- Curriculum modifications
- Accessible facilities

Despite the legislative mandate, implementation of inclusion varies significantly. As of 2024, approximately 72 percent of students receiving special education services were placed in general schools (including special classes within general schools), while 28 percent attended special schools (특수학교). The special school system remains robust, with 192 special schools nationwide serving students with severe disabilities.

### Early Intervention and Transition Services

**Early Intervention (Article 18):** Free early intervention services are available for infants and toddlers (birth through age 2) with disabilities or developmental delays through special education support centers (특수교육지원센터).

**Transition Services (Article 23):** Schools must develop transition plans for students aged 14 and older, addressing post-secondary education, vocational training, and employment preparation.

### Enforcement and Remedies

Students and parents may file complaints with the local education office or the National Human Rights Commission of Korea (국가인권위원회). Administrative appeals through the education office's special education support committee are available. The Act prohibits discrimination against students with disabilities and imposes penalties on educational institutions that fail to provide required accommodations.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States provides the most comprehensive and most litigated special education framework in the world through a network of federal statutes.

**Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA, 20 U.S.C. §§1400-1482):** Originally enacted as the Education for All Handicapped Children Act of 1975 (P.L. 94-142), IDEA guarantees a free appropriate public education (FAPE) to all children with disabilities from birth through age 21.

**Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act (29 U.S.C. §794):** Prohibits disability discrimination in federally funded programs, including schools. Students who do not qualify under IDEA may receive accommodations under Section 504 plans.

**Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA, 42 U.S.C. §§12101-12213):** Title II prohibits disability discrimination by public entities, including public schools. Title III covers private schools that are places of public accommodation.

### IDEA Framework

| Component | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| Child Find | Schools must identify, locate, and evaluate all children with suspected disabilities |
| Evaluation | Comprehensive, nondiscriminatory evaluation using multiple assessment tools |
| IEP | Individualized Education Program developed by team including parents, teachers, LEA representative |
| FAPE | Free appropriate public education tailored to the child's unique needs |
| LRE | Least restrictive environment: education with non-disabled peers to maximum extent appropriate |
| Procedural Safeguards | Prior written notice; consent; due process hearings; mediation |
| Discipline | Manifestation determination required before change in placement for more than 10 days |

### Key Supreme Court Decisions

**Board of Education v. Rowley, 458 U.S. 176 (1982):** The first IDEA case to reach the Supreme Court. The Court held that FAPE requires an IEP "reasonably calculated to enable the child to receive educational benefits" but does not require the school to maximize the child's potential.

**Endrew F. v. Douglas County School District RE-1, 580 U.S. 386 (2017):** The Court raised the substantive standard for FAPE, holding that an IEP must be "reasonably calculated to enable a child to make progress appropriate in light of the child's circumstances." For children in regular education, the IEP should aim for grade-level advancement; for children not in regular education, the IEP must be "appropriately ambitious."

**Perez v. Sturgis Public Schools, 598 U.S. 142 (2023):** The Court held that IDEA's exhaustion requirement does not bar ADA damages claims that are not available under IDEA, expanding the remedial options for families.

### Due Process and Enforcement

IDEA provides the most robust procedural protections in any jurisdiction:
- Parents may request an independent educational evaluation at public expense
- Mediation is available at no cost to parents
- Due process hearings before an impartial hearing officer
- Appeal to state review officer or federal/state court
- Attorney's fees available to prevailing parents under 20 U.S.C. §1415(i)(3)
- Compensatory education as a remedy for past FAPE denials

### Funding

IDEA Part B authorizes federal grants to states, originally intended to cover 40 percent of the excess cost of educating children with disabilities. In practice, federal funding has never approached this level, typically covering approximately 13-15 percent, with state and local funding bearing the remainder.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Special education in Germany is primarily a matter of Länder competence under the Basic Law's allocation of education to the states (Article 70 GG). The 16 Länder have their own school laws governing special education, creating significant variation in approach.

### Pre-CRPD System: Förderschulen

Germany's traditional special education system was built around Förderschulen (support schools, formerly Sonderschulen), which provided specialized instruction for students with different types of disabilities (Förderschwerpunkte):

| Förderschwerpunkt | Focus |
|-------------------|-------|
| Lernen | Learning difficulties |
| Geistige Entwicklung | Intellectual development |
| Emotionale und soziale Entwicklung | Emotional and social development |
| Sprache | Speech and language |
| Hören | Hearing |
| Sehen | Vision |
| Körperliche und motorische Entwicklung | Physical and motor development |

### CRPD Impact and Inclusion Debate

Germany ratified the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD) on February 24, 2009. Article 24 CRPD requires states parties to ensure an inclusive education system at all levels. The CRPD triggered a fundamental debate about Germany's Förderschule system:

**Kultusministerkonferenz (KMK) Recommendation (2011):** The Standing Conference of the Ministers of Education adopted recommendations for inclusive education, urging Länder to expand inclusive placement options while maintaining Förderschulen as part of a continuum of services.

**Implementation by Länder:** Progress toward inclusion varies dramatically:

| Land | Inclusion Rate (approx., 2024) | Approach |
|------|-------------------------------|----------|
| Bremen | ~90% | Most inclusive; nearly eliminated Förderschulen |
| Schleswig-Holstein | ~75% | Strong inclusion policy |
| North Rhine-Westphalia | ~50% | Mixed system with declining Förderschulen enrollment |
| Bavaria | ~30% | Conservative approach; maintains Förderschulen |
| Saxony | ~35% | Förderschule system largely maintained |

### Sozialgesetzbuch IX (SGB IX)

The SGB IX (Social Code Book IX) governs rehabilitation and participation of persons with disabilities, including educational support services. Schools must work with the Eingliederungshilfe (integration assistance) system under SGB IX to provide necessary therapeutic and support services.

### Federal Constitutional Court

The BVerfG has not issued a comprehensive ruling on the right to inclusive education, but has recognized the right of children with disabilities to education under Article 2(1) GG (free development of personality) and the prohibition of disability discrimination under Article 3(3) sentence 2 GG ("No person shall be disfavored because of disability").

### Monitoring and Enforcement

The UN Committee on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities has repeatedly expressed concern about Germany's slow progress toward inclusion. In its 2023 Concluding Observations, the Committee urged Germany to develop a comprehensive national strategy for inclusive education with clear timelines and to allocate sufficient resources for the transition from segregated to inclusive settings.

## ECHR & EU

### UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD)

**Article 24:** States parties recognize the right of persons with disabilities to education and shall ensure an inclusive education system at all levels. This includes:
- Non-exclusion from general education on the basis of disability
- Access to inclusive, quality, and free primary and secondary education
- Reasonable accommodation of individual needs
- Individualized support within the general education system

The CRPD was ratified by the EU as a whole in 2011 -- the first human rights treaty ratified by the EU.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Protocol 1, Article 2 + Article 14:** The ECHR has addressed special education primarily through the right to education read in conjunction with the non-discrimination provision.

**Çam v. Turkey (Application No. 51500/08, 2016):** The Court found a violation of Article 14 taken together with Protocol 1, Article 2, where a blind student was denied admission to a music academy solely on the basis of her visual impairment, without any individual assessment of her ability to participate. The Court held that the failure to make reasonable accommodation constituted discrimination.

**Enver Şahin v. Turkey (Application No. 23065/12, 2018):** The Court found a violation where a university failed to provide wheelchair accessibility and other accommodations to a paralyzed student. The Court emphasized that the right to education under Protocol 1, Article 2 requires reasonable accommodation for students with disabilities.

### EU Framework

**Directive 2000/78/EC (Equal Treatment in Employment):** While primarily an employment directive, it applies to vocational training and education, requiring reasonable accommodation for persons with disabilities.

**European Pillar of Social Rights (Principle 1):** "Everyone has the right to quality and inclusive education, training and life-long learning in order to maintain and acquire skills that enable them to participate fully in society and manage successfully transitions in the labour market."

**European Agency for Special Needs and Inclusive Education:** This organization, funded by member states and the European Commission, coordinates research and policy development on inclusive education. Its Country Policy Reviews and thematic analyses provide comparative data on special education systems across 31 member states and partner countries.

### EU Disability Strategy 2021-2030

The EU Strategy for the Rights of Persons with Disabilities 2021-2030 includes education as a priority area, committing to support member states in implementing CRPD Article 24, developing guidelines for inclusive education, and investing in accessible digital education tools through the Digital Education Action Plan.

## Comparative Analysis

### Inclusion Spectrum

The jurisdictions occupy different positions on the inclusion spectrum. The CRPD mandates inclusive education as a right, but implementation varies. The United States' IDEA LRE requirement establishes a strong preference for inclusion but maintains a continuum of placements. South Korea similarly favors inclusion while maintaining special schools. Germany, despite its CRPD ratification, has been slowest to transition from a segregated system, with some Länder maintaining robust Förderschule networks. Bremen's near-complete inclusion contrasts sharply with Bavaria's conservative approach, illustrating how federalism affects disability rights implementation.

### Substantive Standards

The United States' Endrew F. decision provides the clearest judicial articulation of the substantive standard for special education: education must be "reasonably calculated to enable progress appropriate in light of the child's circumstances." Neither South Korea nor Germany has an equivalent judicial standard. The CRPD establishes broad aspirational goals without specifying measurable educational outcomes.

### Procedural Protections

The United States provides the most extensive procedural protections through IDEA's due process framework, including independent evaluation, mediation, hearings, and judicial review with available attorney's fees. South Korea provides administrative complaints and National Human Rights Commission recourse. Germany relies on administrative court review through the Verwaltungsgerichte. The ECHR provides a supranational backstop through Article 13 (right to effective remedy).

### Funding and Resources

All jurisdictions face challenges in adequately funding special education. The United States' chronic underfunding of IDEA relative to its authorization level is a persistent concern. South Korea's centralized funding model provides more uniform resources across regions. Germany's Länder-based system creates significant resource disparities. The EU's structural funds provide supplementary support but cannot replace national investment.
