## Summary

Algorithmic pricing -- the use of automated systems to dynamically set, adjust, or personalize prices based on real-time data, demand signals, competitor behavior, and individual consumer characteristics -- has emerged as a critical regulatory challenge at the intersection of competition law, consumer protection, and digital market regulation. The practice encompasses dynamic pricing (real-time price fluctuation based on demand), personalized pricing (individualized prices based on consumer profiles), and algorithmic collusion (where competing firms' pricing algorithms tacitly coordinate to maintain supracompetitive prices without explicit human agreement). South Korea has addressed algorithmic pricing primarily through its Fair Trade Act (독점규제 및 공정거래에 관한 법률) and the Act on the Regulation of Terms and Conditions (약관의 규제에 관한 법률), with the Korea Fair Trade Commission (KFTC) investigating algorithmic collusion in the online retail sector. The United States relies on existing antitrust law under the Sherman Act (15 U.S.C. §§ 1-7) and FTC Act (15 U.S.C. § 45), with recent DOJ enforcement actions targeting algorithmic price-fixing in the rental housing market. Germany enforces EU competition law while applying its strengthened national competition framework under the GWB Digitalization Act (10th Amendment to the Act against Restraints of Competition), and the Bundeskartellamt has been at the forefront of investigating algorithmic pricing in e-commerce. The European Union addresses algorithmic pricing through the Digital Markets Act (Regulation (EU) 2022/1925), the Platform-to-Business Regulation (Regulation (EU) 2019/1150), and evolving interpretation of TFEU Articles 101 and 102.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea regulates algorithmic pricing through overlapping competition, consumer protection, and e-commerce frameworks:

- **Monopoly Regulation and Fair Trade Act (독점규제 및 공정거래에 관한 법률, Fair Trade Act):** Article 19 prohibits "unfair concerted practices" (부당한 공동행위), including agreements to fix, maintain, or change prices. The KFTC has interpreted "agreement" broadly to encompass tacit coordination facilitated by algorithmic pricing tools. Article 23 prohibits "unfair business practices" including discriminatory pricing without reasonable justification and exploitation of superior bargaining positions through algorithmically optimized pricing.

- **Act on the Regulation of Terms and Conditions (약관의 규제에 관한 법률):** Article 6 invalidates unfair standard terms, which courts have applied to opaque algorithmic pricing terms in online platform contracts. Where a platform's terms reserve the right to change prices algorithmically without clear criteria, courts have found such terms unreasonably disadvantageous to consumers.

- **Act on the Consumer Protection in Electronic Commerce (전자상거래법):** Article 13-2 (added by 2023 amendment) requires e-commerce operators to clearly indicate the basis for price calculations and to provide consumers with a clear and verifiable final price before purchase confirmation. This provision was specifically enacted to address algorithmic pricing opacity in online marketplaces.

- **Price Stabilization Act (물가안정에 관한 법률):** Empowers the Ministry of Economy and Finance to designate goods and services subject to price controls during inflationary emergencies. While not specifically targeting algorithmic pricing, this Act provides authority to restrict algorithmic surge pricing for essential goods and services during crisis periods.

- **Personal Information Protection Act (PIPA):** Article 37-2 (amended 2023) grants individuals the right to refuse automated decision-making that produces "significant effects" including pricing decisions. Where algorithmic pricing relies on personal data profiling, PIPA's consent requirements and the right to explanation apply.

### Key Principles

The KFTC's approach to algorithmic pricing has evolved through several phases. Initially, the Commission viewed dynamic pricing as generally pro-competitive, enabling efficient price discovery. However, growing evidence of algorithmic coordination among competing sellers on platforms like Coupang, 11st (11번가), and Gmarket prompted investigations beginning in 2022.

A distinctive feature of South Korea's approach is the KFTC's focus on "platform-facilitated algorithmic collusion," where marketplace platforms' recommended pricing algorithms effectively coordinate prices among competing sellers. The KFTC published its "Guidelines on Competition Issues in the Platform Economy" in 2023, specifically addressing the risk that pricing algorithms recommended or mandated by platforms may constitute facilitated coordination under Article 19.

South Korea also regulates surge pricing more aggressively than most jurisdictions in the ride-hailing sector. The Passenger Transport Service Act (여객자동차 운수사업법) limits fare surcharges for mobility platforms, capping surge multipliers at 1.5x during peak demand, a provision enacted in direct response to complaints about Kakao Mobility's dynamic pricing practices.

### Notable Cases

- **KFTC v. Online Travel Agencies (2023):** The KFTC investigated and fined three major online travel agencies a combined KRW 45.6 billion for using algorithmic rate parity agreements that effectively prevented hotels from offering lower prices on their own websites. The Commission found that the platforms' pricing algorithms, which automatically matched or undercut competitors' prices, constituted concerted practices under Article 19 of the Fair Trade Act.

- **KFTC Investigation of E-Commerce Pricing Algorithms (2024):** The KFTC opened formal investigations into whether pricing recommendation algorithms on major Korean e-commerce platforms functioned as "hub-and-spoke" price coordination mechanisms, where the platform's algorithm served as the hub facilitating parallel pricing by competing sellers (the spokes).

- **Seoul Central District Court, Personalized Pricing Discrimination (2023):** A consumer class action challenged a food delivery platform's practice of showing different delivery fees to different users based on algorithmically assessed willingness to pay. The court held that the practice constituted "unfair discrimination" under the E-Commerce Act Article 21 and ordered the platform to apply uniform pricing for identical delivery services.

## United States

### Federal Framework

The United States relies on century-old antitrust statutes to address algorithmic pricing, supplemented by FTC consumer protection authority:

- **Sherman Antitrust Act § 1 (15 U.S.C. § 1):** Prohibits "every contract, combination... or conspiracy, in restraint of trade." The DOJ has taken the position that agreements implemented through pricing algorithms are no less illegal than agreements implemented through handshakes. The core legal question is whether algorithmic parallelism -- where competing firms use similar pricing algorithms that converge on supracompetitive prices -- constitutes an "agreement" or merely lawful conscious parallelism. The DOJ's 2023 prosecution of RealPage represents the first major federal enforcement action directly targeting algorithmic collusion.

- **Sherman Act § 2 (15 U.S.C. § 2):** Prohibits monopolization and attempted monopolization. A dominant firm's use of pricing algorithms to engage in predatory pricing (below-cost pricing to eliminate competitors) or exploitative pricing (supracompetitive pricing enabled by market power) may violate § 2. The Brooke Group v. Brown & Williamson (509 U.S. 209, 1993) predatory pricing framework applies to algorithmically implemented predatory strategies.

- **FTC Act § 5 (15 U.S.C. § 45):** The FTC's authority over "unfair or deceptive acts or practices" reaches algorithmic pricing practices that are deceptive (e.g., false scarcity signals, fake countdown timers driving urgency pricing) or unfair (e.g., discriminatory pricing that exploits consumer vulnerabilities). The FTC issued a 2024 policy statement warning that algorithmic pricing can constitute an unfair practice when it "facilitates pricing that would not occur in a competitive market."

- **Robinson-Patman Act (15 U.S.C. § 13):** Prohibits price discrimination between purchasers of commodities of like grade and quality where the effect may be to substantially lessen competition. While historically under-enforced, the FTC has signaled renewed interest in the Robinson-Patman Act in the context of algorithmic personalized pricing.

### State Variations

- **Price Gouging Laws:** Approximately 37 states and the District of Columbia have price gouging statutes that are triggered during declared emergencies. These laws typically cap price increases at 10-25% above pre-emergency levels and apply regardless of whether the increase is implemented by algorithm or by human decision. For example, California Penal Code § 396 prohibits price increases exceeding 10% during a state of emergency.

- **Live Event Ticketing:** Multiple states have enacted laws addressing algorithmic ticket pricing. New York's Arts and Cultural Affairs Law § 25.24 prohibits the use of bots to circumvent ticket purchase limits, addressing a form of algorithmic market manipulation. Colorado's Ticket Sales Act (C.R.S. § 6-1-718) requires disclosure when ticket prices are dynamically adjusted.

- **Rental Housing Algorithmic Pricing:** In response to the DOJ's RealPage prosecution, several states and municipalities have proposed or enacted legislation targeting algorithmic rent-setting. The Minnesota Residential Rent Transparency Act (2024) requires disclosure when landlords use algorithmic pricing tools, and Oregon's SB 611 (2025) prohibits landlords from using third-party algorithms that aggregate competitor pricing data.

### Notable Cases

- **United States v. RealPage, Inc. (D. Ariz., filed August 2024):** The DOJ filed a civil antitrust lawsuit alleging that RealPage's revenue management software, used by landlords controlling millions of rental units, functioned as an algorithmic price-fixing mechanism. The software collected non-public pricing data from competing landlords and recommended specific rent levels to each, effectively coordinating prices among competitors. The DOJ alleges this constitutes a "combination... in restraint of trade" under Sherman Act § 1, even without a traditional explicit agreement. The case is the most significant federal test of algorithmic collusion theory.

- **Duffy v. Yardi Systems (2024, N.D. Cal.):** A companion private class action to the RealPage litigation, alleging that rental property management software facilitated algorithmic collusion among landlords, seeking treble damages under the Clayton Act § 4 (15 U.S.C. § 15).

- **FTC v. Amazon (Antitrust, W.D. Wash., 2023):** The FTC's monopolization case against Amazon alleges, among other claims, that Amazon's pricing algorithms punish sellers who offer lower prices on competing platforms by suppressing their listings, effectively maintaining supracompetitive prices across the e-commerce market through algorithmic monitoring and enforcement of price parity.

- **Meyer v. Uber Technologies (2d Cir., 2017):** The Second Circuit upheld Uber's arbitration clause, but the underlying complaint alleged that Uber's surge pricing algorithm constituted a price-fixing agreement among drivers coordinated through the platform -- the classic "hub-and-spoke" algorithmic collusion theory. While the merits were not reached, the theory remains legally viable.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany has developed one of the most sophisticated regulatory approaches to algorithmic pricing through its strengthened competition law framework and active enforcement agency:

- **Act against Restraints of Competition (Gesetz gegen Wettbewerbsbeschränkungen, GWB):** The 10th Amendment (GWB Digitalization Act, effective 19 January 2021) introduced several provisions directly relevant to algorithmic pricing:
  - **GWB § 1:** Prohibits agreements and concerted practices that restrict competition, interpreted to encompass algorithmic coordination.
  - **GWB § 19(1)-(2):** Prohibits abuse of a dominant position, including exploitative pricing. The Bundeskartellamt has investigated whether algorithmic pricing by dominant platforms constitutes exploitative abuse.
  - **GWB § 19a:** A groundbreaking provision empowering the Bundeskartellamt to designate companies of "paramount significance for competition across markets" (überragende marktübergreifende Bedeutung) and to impose specific behavioral obligations, including prohibitions on self-preferencing in pricing algorithms.
  - **GWB § 20(3a):** Specifically addresses "intermediary power" (Intermediationsmacht) of platforms, enabling intervention against algorithmic pricing practices that exploit dependent platform sellers.

- **Price Indication Ordinance (Preisangabenverordnung, PAngV):** Requires transparent price disclosure for goods and services. The 2022 amendments require that where prices have been reduced, the "lowest price in the last 30 days" must be displayed alongside the reduced price (implementing the Omnibus Directive 2019/2161). This directly targets algorithmic practices of inflating prices before "discounting" them.

- **Unfair Competition Act (UWG) § 5:** Prohibits misleading commercial practices including deceptive pricing representations. Algorithmically generated fake urgency signals ("only 2 left in stock" when supply is ample) or fake countdown timers constitute misleading practices under § 5.

### Key Principles

The Bundeskartellamt has positioned itself as a global thought leader on algorithmic competition issues. Its 2019 study "Algorithms and Competition" (Algorithmen und Wettbewerb) identified four categories of algorithmic collusion risk: (1) algorithms as instruments of existing human conspiracies; (2) hub-and-spoke arrangements where a shared algorithm coordinates competitors; (3) predictable agent situations where algorithms learn to coordinate without explicit design; and (4) autonomous machine collusion where self-learning algorithms independently develop coordinated strategies.

Germany's enforcement approach recognizes that traditional competition law concepts of "agreement" and "concerted practice" require adaptation for the algorithmic context. The Bundeskartellamt takes the position that even absent explicit human coordination, the use of algorithms that inherently facilitate tacit coordination can constitute a "concerted practice" under GWB § 1, particularly where the algorithm's design makes parallel pricing behavior foreseeable and rational.

The GWB § 19a designation mechanism has proven particularly effective: the Bundeskartellamt designated Meta (January 2023), Amazon (July 2023), Apple (April 2023), Alphabet/Google (December 2023), and Microsoft (September 2024) as companies of paramount cross-market significance, enabling targeted intervention in their pricing algorithms and self-preferencing practices.

### Notable Cases

- **Bundeskartellamt, Amazon Marketplace Investigation (2018-2024):** The Bundeskartellamt conducted a multi-year investigation into Amazon's dual role as marketplace operator and competing seller, examining whether Amazon's pricing algorithm systematically disadvantaged third-party sellers. In 2024, following the § 19a designation, the Bundeskartellamt imposed obligations requiring Amazon to ensure that its pricing algorithms do not discriminate in favor of Amazon's own products in the "Buy Box" allocation.

- **Bundeskartellamt, Online Fuel Price Monitoring (Market Transparency Unit):** Germany's Markttransparenzstelle für Kraftstoffe, operated by the Bundeskartellamt since 2013, collects real-time fuel pricing data from all filling stations and makes it publicly available. This addressed concerns about algorithmic pricing coordination among petrol stations by creating countervailing transparency. Studies have shown a measurable reduction in price coordination following the introduction of the transparency mechanism.

- **Bundeskartellamt, Hotel Booking Platform Price Parity (2015-2024):** The Bundeskartellamt prohibited Booking.com's "best price" clauses (both wide and narrow parity) that algorithmically enforced price uniformity across booking channels. The decision was upheld by the Oberlandesgericht Düsseldorf (2019) and subsequently influenced EU-wide legislation through the DMA's prohibition on price parity requirements by gatekeepers.

- **OLG Düsseldorf, Algorithmic Price Surveillance (2023):** The Oberlandesgericht Düsseldorf upheld fines against an electronics retailer whose pricing algorithm systematically monitored and matched competitors' prices within minutes, finding that the algorithm's design -- which made competitive pricing responses effectively automatic and predictable -- facilitated tacit coordination constituting a concerted practice under GWB § 1.

## ECHR / EU Framework

### Convention Articles

- **Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 -- Protection of Property:** Algorithmic pricing practices that effectively expropriate consumer surplus through exploitative or discriminatory pricing may engage property rights protections, establishing positive obligations on states to regulate market abuses.

- **Article 14 -- Prohibition of Discrimination:** Personalized pricing that systematically charges higher prices to consumers based on protected characteristics (race, ethnicity, national origin, as detectable through proxy data) engages Article 14 when combined with economic rights.

- **Article 8 -- Right to Respect for Private Life:** Personalized pricing necessarily involves extensive consumer profiling, engaging privacy rights. The collection and analysis of browsing history, location data, purchase patterns, and device information to determine individualized prices constitutes processing of personal data with significant privacy implications.

### EU Legislative Framework

- **TFEU Article 101 (Concerted Practices):** The European Commission has signaled that algorithmic collusion falls within Article 101's prohibition. Commissioner Vestager stated in 2017 that "companies can't escape responsibility for collusion by hiding behind a computer program." The Commission's evolving enforcement position distinguishes between algorithms used to implement existing human agreements (clearly prohibited), algorithms designed to facilitate coordination (likely prohibited), and purely autonomous algorithmic coordination (legally uncertain).

- **TFEU Article 102 (Abuse of Dominant Position):** Algorithmic pricing by dominant firms may constitute exploitative abuse (excessive pricing) or exclusionary abuse (predatory pricing, margin squeeze). The Commission's investigation into Amazon's use of non-public marketplace data to inform its own retail pricing decisions resulted in commitments requiring Amazon to refrain from using third-party seller data for its own pricing algorithms (December 2022).

- **Digital Markets Act (Regulation (EU) 2022/1925):** Article 6(1)(a) prohibits designated gatekeepers from using non-public data of business users (including pricing data) to compete against those business users. Article 5(3) prohibits gatekeepers from imposing price parity obligations on business users. These provisions directly target the most harmful algorithmic pricing practices of dominant platforms.

- **Platform-to-Business Regulation (Regulation (EU) 2019/1150):** Article 5 requires platforms to disclose the main parameters determining ranking, including how pricing affects visibility. Article 7 requires disclosure where platforms treat their own goods or services differently from those of business users in pricing or ranking algorithms.

- **Unfair Commercial Practices Directive (2005/29/EC):** The European Commission's 2021 Guidance specifically addresses algorithmic pricing practices that constitute unfair commercial practices, including: (a) drip pricing (revealing additional charges only at checkout); (b) reference price manipulation (algorithmically inflating pre-discount prices); (c) personalized pricing without disclosure; and (d) algorithmic fake urgency or scarcity signals.

### Key Case Law

- **CJEU, Eturas (C-74/14, 2016):** The Court held that a platform's unilateral imposition of a maximum discount cap through its booking system, communicated to all connected travel agents, constituted a "concerted practice" under Article 101 TFEU, even though the travel agents did not explicitly agree. This case established that platform-facilitated algorithmic pricing coordination can constitute antitrust infringement.

- **European Commission v. Amazon (AT.40703, Commitments Decision 2022):** The Commission accepted Amazon's commitments to refrain from using non-public marketplace seller data (including pricing data) to inform its own retail decisions, and to treat Amazon's offers and third-party seller offers equally in the "Buy Box" algorithm.

- **European Commission, E-Commerce Sector Inquiry (COM(2017) 229):** The Commission's sector inquiry found that 53% of online retailers used automated software to monitor competitors' prices and that 67% of those subsequently adjusted their own prices based on algorithmic analysis. The inquiry identified algorithmic pricing as a significant competition concern.
