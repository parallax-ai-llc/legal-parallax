## Summary

Racial discrimination -- the differential treatment of individuals or groups based on race, ethnicity, color, descent, or national origin -- is prohibited across virtually all major legal systems and international human rights instruments, yet the conceptual frameworks, enforcement mechanisms, and practical effectiveness of anti-racial discrimination law vary substantially, reflecting different historical experiences, demographic compositions, and political priorities. South Korea, historically characterized by relative ethnic homogeneity, has confronted growing racial discrimination challenges as immigration and multicultural families have increased; Article 11 of the Constitution (대한민국 헌법 제11조) prohibits discrimination on grounds including "social status" (which has been interpreted to potentially encompass race), but the absence of a comprehensive anti-discrimination act, the reliance on the non-binding National Human Rights Commission (국가인권위원회), and documented discrimination against migrant workers, mixed-heritage children, and ethnic Korean minorities from China (조선족) represent significant gaps, with the UN Committee on the Elimination of Racial Discrimination (CERD) repeatedly recommending that South Korea enact comprehensive anti-racial discrimination legislation. The United States has the most extensive body of racial discrimination law, rooted in the Reconstruction Amendments (Thirteenth, Fourteenth, and Fifteenth), the Civil Rights Act of 1964, the Voting Rights Act of 1965, and the Fair Housing Act of 1968, with the Supreme Court having developed doctrines of strict scrutiny for racial classifications, disparate impact theory, and constitutional color-blindness that have shifted the landscape from affirmative remedial action to formal equality, most dramatically in Students for Fair Admissions v. Harvard (2023). Germany prohibits racial discrimination under Article 3(3) of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz), the Allgemeines Gleichbehandlungsgesetz (AGG, 2006), and criminal law provisions against Volksverhetzung (incitement to hatred, Section 130 StGB), with particular attention to anti-Semitism and discrimination against Roma, Sinti, and persons of African descent; Germany's ongoing engagement with its Nazi-era legacy informs its approach to racial hatred, including the criminalization of Holocaust denial. The European framework provides the most comprehensive supranational anti-racial discrimination regime through ECHR Article 14, the International Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Racial Discrimination (ICERD, 1965), and the EU Racial Equality Directive (2000/43/EC), which prohibits racial discrimination in employment, education, social protection, and access to goods and services, with the European Court of Human Rights having developed landmark jurisprudence on Roma discrimination, racial profiling, and the positive obligations of states to investigate racially motivated violence.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Article 11 of the Constitution provides that "all citizens shall be equal before the law, and there shall be no discrimination in political, economic, social, or cultural life on account of sex, religion, or social status." Race and ethnicity are not explicitly enumerated as prohibited grounds of discrimination in the Constitution, creating a textual gap that the proposed Anti-Discrimination Act would address.

### Discrimination Against Migrant Workers

South Korea's Employment Permit System (고용허가제, EPS), established by the Act on Employment of Foreign Workers (외국인근로자의 고용 등에 관한 법률, 2003), permits employers to hire foreign workers in designated industries including manufacturing, agriculture, fisheries, and construction. Migrant workers under the EPS face documented discrimination including:

- Workplace abuse and non-payment of wages
- Restrictions on changing employers (limited to three employer changes under EPS)
- Exclusion from certain labor protections
- Housing discrimination and inadequate accommodations
- Social discrimination and xenophobic attitudes

The National Human Rights Commission has investigated numerous complaints of racial discrimination against migrant workers and issued recommendations to strengthen protections. The UN CERD Committee, in its 2018 Concluding Observations on South Korea, expressed concern about the insufficient legal protections for migrant workers and recommended comprehensive anti-discrimination legislation.

### Multicultural Families and Mixed-Heritage Children

The Multicultural Families Support Act (다문화가족지원법, 2008) provides support for families formed through international marriage, primarily marriages between Korean men and Southeast Asian women. However, mixed-heritage children (다문화 자녀) face discrimination in schools and social settings. A 2022 NHRC survey found that approximately 26 percent of multicultural family members had experienced discrimination.

### Ethnic Korean Minorities (조선족)

Ethnic Koreans from China (조선족, Joseonjok), numbering approximately 700,000 in South Korea, face employment discrimination, social stigma, and negative media portrayals despite sharing ethnic Korean heritage. The Act on the Immigration and Legal Status of Overseas Koreans has been criticized for treating overseas Koreans from developed countries (particularly the United States and Japan) more favorably than those from China and the former Soviet Union.

### Yemeni Refugee Crisis (2018)

In 2018, approximately 550 Yemeni nationals arrived on Jeju Island seeking asylum, triggering a significant public backlash. A petition opposing their acceptance garnered over 714,000 signatures on the Blue House petition platform. The incident exposed deep-seated racial and xenophobic attitudes and prompted debate about South Korea's refugee policies. Ultimately, two individuals were granted refugee status, 412 received humanitarian stay permits, and 56 were denied protection.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has the most extensive body of racial discrimination law, developed through constitutional amendments, federal legislation, and Supreme Court jurisprudence.

**Reconstruction Amendments:**
- Thirteenth Amendment (1865): Abolishes slavery and involuntary servitude
- Fourteenth Amendment (1868): Equal Protection Clause; Privileges or Immunities Clause; Due Process Clause
- Fifteenth Amendment (1870): Prohibits denial of vote based on race

### Federal Civil Rights Statutes

| Statute | Year | Coverage |
|---------|------|----------|
| Civil Rights Act of 1866 (42 U.S.C. Section 1981) | 1866 | Right to make and enforce contracts without racial discrimination |
| Civil Rights Act of 1964, Title VII | 1964 | Employment discrimination based on race, color, national origin |
| Voting Rights Act of 1965 | 1965 | Prohibits racial discrimination in voting |
| Fair Housing Act (Title VIII) | 1968 | Housing discrimination based on race, color, national origin |
| Section 1983 (42 U.S.C.) | 1871 | Civil action for deprivation of rights under color of state law |

### Supreme Court Jurisprudence

The Supreme Court's approach to race has evolved significantly:

**Brown v. Board of Education, 347 U.S. 483 (1954):** Struck down racial segregation in public schools, overruling Plessy v. Ferguson (1896) and the "separate but equal" doctrine.

**Griggs v. Duke Power Co., 401 U.S. 424 (1971):** Established disparate impact theory under Title VII, holding that facially neutral employment practices that disproportionately affect racial minorities are unlawful absent business necessity.

**Washington v. Davis, 426 U.S. 229 (1976):** Held that the Equal Protection Clause requires proof of discriminatory intent, not merely disparate impact, creating a higher bar for constitutional (as opposed to statutory) racial discrimination claims.

**Students for Fair Admissions v. Harvard, 600 U.S. 181 (2023):** Ended race-conscious admissions at universities, holding that Harvard's and UNC's admissions programs violated the Equal Protection Clause. The decision reflected a constitutional color-blindness principle, with the majority holding that the Equal Protection Clause applies "without regard to any differences of race."

### Racial Profiling

Racial profiling by law enforcement remains a significant issue. The Supreme Court in Whren v. United States, 517 U.S. 806 (1996), held that the subjective motivations of officers are irrelevant to the legality of traffic stops if there is objective probable cause, making it difficult to challenge pretextual stops. The Department of Justice Guidance Regarding the Use of Race by Federal Law Enforcement Agencies (2003, updated 2014) prohibits racial profiling but is not legally binding and contains national security exceptions.

### Hate Crime Legislation

The Matthew Shepard and James Byrd, Jr. Hate Crimes Prevention Act of 2009 (18 U.S.C. Section 249) provides federal jurisdiction over hate crimes motivated by race, color, religion, national origin, gender, sexual orientation, gender identity, or disability. The Supreme Court in Wisconsin v. Mitchell, 508 U.S. 476 (1993), upheld penalty enhancement for racially motivated crimes.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Article 3(3) of the Basic Law provides: "No person shall be favoured or disfavoured because of sex, parentage, race, language, homeland and origin, faith, or religious or political opinions." The inclusion of "race" in the Basic Law has itself been debated, with some legal scholars and politicians arguing that the term should be replaced because it implies the existence of distinct human races. A 2020 proposal to amend Article 3(3) to replace "race" with "racial motivation" (rassistisch) or "racist" has not yet been enacted.

### AGG Racial Discrimination Provisions

The Allgemeines Gleichbehandlungsgesetz (AGG, 2006) prohibits discrimination based on race and ethnic origin across the broadest scope of any protected ground, covering employment, social protection, education, and access to goods and services, including housing. This broader scope (compared to religion, disability, age, and sexual orientation, which are protected primarily in employment) reflects the implementation of the EU Racial Equality Directive.

### Volksverhetzung and Hate Crime

Section 130 StGB (Volksverhetzung) criminalizes:
- Inciting hatred against segments of the population (Paragraph 1): 3 months to 5 years imprisonment
- Attacking the human dignity of segments of the population (Paragraph 1): same penalty
- Publicly approving of, denying, or downplaying the genocide under National Socialist rule (Paragraph 3): up to 5 years imprisonment
- Publicly approving, glorifying, or justifying National Socialist rule of violence and tyranny (Paragraph 4): up to 3 years imprisonment

Section 46(2) StGB provides for sentence enhancement where an offense is motivated by racist, xenophobic, anti-Semitic, or other contempt-related motives.

### Anti-Semitism

Germany maintains a uniquely intensive focus on combating anti-Semitism, reflecting its historical responsibility:

- The Federal Government Commissioner for Jewish Life in Germany and the Fight against Anti-Semitism (Antisemitismusbeauftragter) was established in 2018
- All 16 Länder have appointed anti-Semitism commissioners
- The Bundestag adopted the IHRA Working Definition of Anti-Semitism in 2017
- The Federal Strategy to Combat Anti-Semitism (2022) provides a comprehensive action plan

### Discrimination Against Roma and Sinti

Roma and Sinti communities in Germany face persistent discrimination in housing, education, and employment. The Central Council of German Sinti and Roma (Zentralrat Deutscher Sinti und Roma) advocates for the community's rights. Germany formally recognized its responsibility for the Porajmos (Roma genocide during the Nazi era) in 1982.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Racial Equality Directive (2000/43/EC)

Directive 2000/43/EC, adopted on June 29, 2000, provides the most comprehensive supranational framework for combating racial discrimination. Key provisions:

- **Scope:** Employment, social protection, education, access to goods and services, including housing
- **Prohibited conduct:** Direct discrimination, indirect discrimination, harassment, instruction to discriminate
- **Burden of proof:** Shifts to respondent once complainant establishes facts from which discrimination may be presumed (Article 8)
- **Equality bodies:** Member states must designate independent bodies to promote equal treatment (Article 13)
- **Remedies:** Must be effective, proportionate, and dissuasive (Article 15)

### Key ECHR Jurisprudence

**Nachova and Others v. Bulgaria (Application Nos. 43577/98 and 43579/98, Grand Chamber, 2005):** The Grand Chamber held that when a person is killed by a state agent and there is evidence suggesting racial motivation, the state bears the burden of establishing that the use of force was not racially motivated. The case involved the killing of two Roma men by military police.

**D.H. and Others v. Czech Republic (Application No. 57325/00, Grand Chamber, 2007):** Found that the disproportionate placement of Roma children in special schools constituted indirect racial discrimination, establishing that statistical evidence is sufficient to establish a prima facie case of indirect discrimination.

**Timishev v. Russia (Application No. 55762/00, 2005):** Found that the refusal to admit a person of Chechen ethnicity through a checkpoint violated Article 14 read with Protocol 4 Article 2 (freedom of movement), holding that racial discrimination constitutes a particularly egregious form of discrimination requiring special vigilance and vigorous response.

**Budinova and Chaprazov v. Bulgaria (Application No. 12567/13, 2021):** Addressed media hate speech against Roma, finding that Bulgaria's failure to adequately protect Roma from racist media stereotyping violated Article 8 read with Article 14.

**Lingurar v. Romania (Application No. 48474/14, 2019):** Found that a violent police raid on a Roma settlement constituted inhuman and degrading treatment (Article 3) with a racial dimension (Article 14), as the excessive force was motivated in part by anti-Roma prejudice.

### International Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Racial Discrimination (ICERD)

ICERD, adopted in 1965 and entered into force in 1969, is the primary international treaty addressing racial discrimination. Key provisions include:

- Definition of racial discrimination (Article 1)
- Obligation to condemn racial discrimination and pursue policies of eliminating it (Article 2)
- Prohibition of racial segregation and apartheid (Article 3)
- Condemnation of racist propaganda and organizations (Article 4)
- Guarantee of equality before the law (Article 5)
- Effective remedies (Article 6)

The Committee on the Elimination of Racial Discrimination (CERD) monitors implementation and issues Concluding Observations on state reports. CERD's General Recommendation No. 36 (2020) on Preventing and Combating Racial Profiling by Law Enforcement Officials provides detailed guidance on the prohibition of racial profiling.

### EU Anti-Racism Action Plan 2020-2025

The European Commission's Anti-Racism Action Plan commits to strengthening enforcement of existing anti-discrimination legislation, addressing structural racism, promoting diversity in EU institutions, and ensuring that racial equality is mainstreamed across EU policies. The Plan includes a commitment to proposing strengthened equality body standards and to funding anti-racism initiatives through EU programs.

## Comparative Analysis

### Constitutional Frameworks

The United States has the most textually explicit racial equality provisions through the Reconstruction Amendments, but the Supreme Court's interpretation has evolved from a remedial understanding (permitting race-conscious measures to address historical discrimination) to a formal color-blindness principle (treating all racial classifications with equal suspicion). Germany's Article 3(3) explicitly prohibits racial disfavor but has generated debate about whether the term "race" itself should be removed. South Korea's Constitution does not explicitly mention race, leaving a significant textual gap. The ECHR framework addresses race through Article 14's non-discrimination principle.

### Hate Speech Regulation

The most significant divergence is between the U.S. approach (hate speech generally protected under the First Amendment unless it constitutes incitement to imminent lawless action) and the European approach (comprehensive criminalization of hate speech, including Holocaust denial in Germany). South Korea lacks specific racial hate speech legislation. The EU Framework Decision 2008/913/JHA requires member states to criminalize public incitement to violence or hatred against groups defined by race, color, descent, religion, or ethnic origin.

### Indirect/Structural Discrimination

The concept of indirect or structural discrimination is most developed in EU law (Racial Equality Directive) and U.S. law (Title VII disparate impact). Germany's AGG incorporates the EU concept. South Korea lacks a developed indirect discrimination framework, though the NHRC has applied the concept in advisory opinions. The ECHR's D.H. decision significantly advanced the recognition of indirect discrimination at the European level.

### Enforcement Effectiveness

The United States provides the strongest individual enforcement through private litigation with compensatory and punitive damages. The EU framework requires "effective, proportionate, and dissuasive" remedies. Germany's AGG provides compensation but imposes a two-month filing deadline. South Korea's reliance on the non-binding NHRC represents the weakest enforcement mechanism among the four systems.
