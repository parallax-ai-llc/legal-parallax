## Summary

Zoning and land use regulation -- the system by which governments divide territory into zones and regulate the types of activities, building forms, and densities permitted within each zone -- represents one of the most significant exercises of state power over private property, shaping urban form, housing availability, environmental quality, and economic development patterns across every jurisdiction. South Korea's land use planning system is governed by the National Land Planning and Utilization Act (국토의 계획 및 이용에 관한 법률, commonly known as the National Land Planning Act), enacted in 2002 by consolidating the Urban Planning Act and the National Land Use Management Act, which establishes a hierarchical zoning system dividing all national territory into four broad use zones (urban, management, agricultural/forest, and natural environment), each subdivided into detailed use districts (용도지역), use areas (용도지구), and use zones (용도구역), administered by the Ministry of Land, Infrastructure and Transport (국토교통부) and local governments. The United States employs a primarily local zoning system derived from the police power, validated by the Supreme Court in Village of Euclid v. Ambler Realty Co., 272 U.S. 365 (1926), with approximately 30,000 local governments exercising zoning authority under state enabling legislation, creating enormous variation in zoning codes, development standards, and land use procedures across the country; recent reform efforts have challenged exclusionary zoning practices, with states including Oregon, California, Minnesota, and Montana enacting legislation to override local single-family zoning restrictions. Germany employs a two-tier planning system under the Baugesetzbuch (Federal Building Code, BauGB), with municipalities preparing the Flächennutzungsplan (preparatory land use plan) and the Bebauungsplan (binding land use plan), supplemented by the Baunutzungsverordnung (BauNVO, Federal Land Use Ordinance) which defines standard land use categories and development standards. The EU does not harmonize land use planning but significantly affects it through the Environmental Impact Assessment Directive 2011/92/EU, the Strategic Environmental Assessment Directive 2001/42/EC, the Habitats Directive 92/43/EEC, and the European Spatial Development Perspective (ESDP), while the ECHR has addressed zoning under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (property) and Article 8 (home and private life).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's comprehensive land use planning system reflects decades of rapid industrialization, urbanization, and government-directed economic development.

**National Land Planning and Utilization Act (국토의 계획 및 이용에 관한 법률):** This Act, enacted in 2002, establishes a unified national land use planning framework that covers all territory, eliminating the previous dual system that applied different regulations to urban and non-urban areas. The Act establishes a hierarchical planning system: the Comprehensive National Territorial Plan (국토종합계획), Provincial Plans (도종합계획), Metropolitan Area Plans (광역도시계획), and Municipal Plans (도시·군기본계획/관리계획).

### Zoning Structure

The Act divides all national territory into four primary use zones (용도지역):

| Primary Zone | Subdivisions | Permitted Uses |
|-------------|-------------|----------------|
| Urban Area (도시지역) | Residential, Commercial, Industrial, Green Belt | Defined by detailed residential density categories (exclusive, general, semi-residential) |
| Management Area (관리지역) | Conservation Management, Production Management, Planned Management | Transitional areas between urban and rural; varying development permissions |
| Agricultural/Forest Area (농림지역) | N/A (single category) | Agriculture, forestry; very limited development |
| Natural Environment Conservation Area (자연환경보전지역) | N/A (single category) | Environmental conservation; minimal development permitted |

### Development Activity Permits

The Act requires Development Activity Permits (개발행위허가, Article 56) for construction, land formation, land division, placement of objects, and similar activities. Local governments review permit applications for conformity with the applicable use zone regulations, infrastructure capacity, environmental impact, and consistency with the municipal plan. Permits may be conditioned on infrastructure contributions, environmental mitigation, and design standards.

### Green Belt System

South Korea's Development Restriction Zones (개발제한구역), commonly known as "Green Belts," were established in 1971 around major cities to prevent urban sprawl. Modeled on the British Green Belt concept, these zones severely restrict development in designated peri-urban areas. The Green Belt around Seoul is the largest, covering approximately 1,566 square kilometers. While Green Belt restrictions have effectively prevented uncontrolled sprawl, they have also been criticized for inflating land prices within urban areas and creating development pressure on unprotected areas.

### Recent Reforms

Recent reforms have focused on increasing housing supply and flexibility. The 2024 amendments to the National Land Planning Act introduced expedited rezoning procedures for housing supply zones, increased density allowances for transit-oriented development areas, and streamlined the environmental review process for designated housing supply projects. The government has also designated "Special Districts for Housing Supply" (주택공급 특별구역) where normal zoning restrictions are relaxed to accelerate housing construction.

## United States

### Legal Framework

American zoning law originated in the early 20th century as cities sought to separate incompatible land uses and is characterized by extreme local variation and fragmentation.

**Constitutional Foundation -- Village of Euclid v. Ambler Realty Co., 272 U.S. 365 (1926):** The Supreme Court upheld the constitutionality of comprehensive zoning as a valid exercise of the police power, establishing that zoning regulations bear a rational relation to the public health, safety, morals, and general welfare. The Court endorsed the separation of land uses into zones as a reasonable means of promoting community welfare.

**Standard State Zoning Enabling Act (1926):** The U.S. Department of Commerce published the Standard State Zoning Enabling Act, which served as a model for state zoning enabling legislation across the country. Most states adopted enabling legislation based on this model, delegating zoning authority to municipalities.

### Zoning Reform Movement

Recent years have seen an unprecedented movement to reform exclusionary zoning practices:

| State | Reform | Key Provisions |
|-------|--------|----------------|
| Oregon (HB 2001, 2019) | Statewide elimination of single-family zoning | Cities over 25,000 must allow fourplexes in residential zones; smaller cities must allow duplexes |
| California (SB 9, 2021) | Statewide duplex and lot-split authorization | All residential parcels may be split and developed with two units per parcel |
| California (SB 35, 2017) | Streamlined housing approval | Mandatory ministerial approval for qualifying housing projects in jurisdictions failing to meet housing element goals |
| Minnesota (2023) | Statewide legalization of triplexes | Cities over 10,000 must allow triplexes; smaller cities must allow duplexes |
| Montana (HB 908, 2023) | Statewide elimination of single-family zoning | All cities must allow at least a duplex on any residential lot; up to fourplexes in larger cities |

### Exclusionary Zoning Litigation

Courts have addressed the exclusionary effects of zoning in landmark decisions. In Southern Burlington County NAACP v. Township of Mount Laurel, 67 N.J. 151 (1975) ("Mount Laurel I"), the New Jersey Supreme Court held that developing municipalities have a constitutional obligation to provide a realistic opportunity for the construction of their fair share of regional affordable housing, striking down zoning that excluded low- and moderate-income housing. The Mount Laurel doctrine remains one of the most significant judicial challenges to exclusionary zoning in American law.

### Environmental Review

The National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA, 42 U.S.C. Sections 4321-4347) requires federal agencies to prepare environmental impact statements for major federal actions significantly affecting the environment. Most states have enacted analogous state environmental policy acts (SEPAs) that apply environmental review requirements to local land use decisions.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's planning system is characterized by a clear hierarchical structure, strong municipal planning authority (kommunale Planungshoheit), and extensive public participation requirements.

**Baugesetzbuch (BauGB, Federal Building Code):** The BauGB provides the comprehensive federal framework for urban land use planning. Two principal types of plans are established:

**Flächennutzungsplan (Preparatory Land Use Plan, Sections 5-7 BauGB):** A comprehensive plan covering the entire municipal territory, depicting the intended land uses for the foreseeable future. The Flächennutzungsplan is binding only on public authorities, not on individual property owners. It establishes the general framework within which more detailed Bebauungspläne are developed.

**Bebauungsplan (Binding Land Use Plan, Sections 8-10 BauGB):** A legally binding plan for specific areas within the municipality, establishing detailed regulations including land use type (Art der baulichen Nutzung), intensity of use (Maß der baulichen Nutzung), building lines and setbacks, and other design requirements. The Bebauungsplan is enacted as a municipal ordinance (Satzung) and is directly binding on property owners.

### Baunutzungsverordnung (Federal Land Use Ordinance)

The BauNVO defines standard land use categories that municipalities must use in their Bebauungspläne:

| Category | German Term | Typical Permitted Uses |
|----------|------------|----------------------|
| Small settlement area | Kleinsiedlungsgebiet (WS) | Allotment gardens, small settlements |
| Pure residential area | Reines Wohngebiet (WR) | Dwellings only; limited exceptions |
| General residential area | Allgemeines Wohngebiet (WA) | Dwellings; non-disruptive commercial; social infrastructure |
| Mixed-use area | Mischgebiet (MI) | Dwellings and compatible commercial |
| Urban area | Urbanes Gebiet (MU) | High-density mixed use (added 2017) |
| Commercial area | Gewerbegebiet (GE) | Non-polluting commercial and industrial |
| Industrial area | Industriegebiet (GI) | All industrial uses including polluting industries |
| Special area | Sondergebiet (SO) | Uses defined by specific plan (shopping centers, leisure, military) |

### Section 34 BGB -- Unplanned Interior Areas

For built-up areas without a Bebauungsplan, Section 34 BauGB provides that development is permissible if it fits into (sich einfügen) the surrounding development in terms of use type, intensity, building form, and plot coverage, and if infrastructure is secured. This provision functions as a de facto zoning tool for areas that have developed without formal planning, relying on existing development patterns as the reference standard.

### Public Participation

The BauGB requires a two-stage public participation process for the preparation of Bebauungspläne. Section 3(1) provides for early public participation (frühzeitige Öffentlichkeitsbeteiligung), and Section 3(2) requires formal public display (öffentliche Auslegung) of the draft plan for at least one month. Section 4 requires consultation with public authorities and agencies (Behörden und Träger öffentlicher Belange). Environmental assessment is integrated into the planning process under Section 2(4) BauGB.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Environmental Planning Directives

The EU significantly affects national land use planning through environmental directives:

**Strategic Environmental Assessment Directive 2001/42/EC:** Requires environmental assessment of plans and programs, including land use plans, that set the framework for future development consent. Member states must assess the likely significant environmental effects of plans before their adoption, prepare an environmental report, consult the public and environmental authorities, and take the environmental report into account in decision-making.

**Environmental Impact Assessment Directive 2011/92/EU (as amended by 2014/52/EU):** Requires environmental assessment of individual projects likely to have significant environmental effects. The Directive establishes a two-tier system: Annex I projects (mandatory assessment) and Annex II projects (screening to determine whether assessment is required).

### ECHR Property and Planning

The European Court of Human Rights has addressed land use regulation under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1:

**Pine Valley Developments Ltd v. Ireland (Application No. 12742/87, 1991):** The Court accepted that planning restrictions serve a legitimate public interest but found a violation of Article 14 (non-discrimination) in conjunction with Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 where the applicant company was denied planning permission while other similar companies were granted permission.

**Sporrong and Lönnroth v. Sweden (1982):** The Court found that prolonged prohibitions on construction imposed through expropriation permits violated Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 where the restrictions were maintained for extended periods without certainty about the eventual outcome.

**Hamer v. Belgium (Application No. 21861/03, 2007):** The Court held that a demolition order for a building constructed without planning permission in a forest zone did not violate Article 8 or Article 1 of Protocol No. 1, recognizing the importance of environmental protection as a legitimate planning objective.

### Aarhus Convention

The UNECE Convention on Access to Information, Public Participation in Decision-Making and Access to Justice in Environmental Matters (Aarhus Convention, 1998), ratified by all EU member states and implemented through Directive 2003/35/EC, requires states to provide public participation in environmental decision-making, including land use planning decisions with significant environmental effects.

### European Spatial Development Perspective (ESDP)

The ESDP, adopted by informal meetings of EU ministers responsible for spatial planning in 1999, provides a non-binding framework for coordination of national spatial planning policies. The ESDP promotes polycentric urban development, parity of access to infrastructure, and wise management of the natural and cultural heritage, influencing national planning policies across the EU without creating binding legal obligations.

## Comparative Analysis

### Planning Authority Distribution

The distribution of planning authority differs fundamentally across jurisdictions. The United States fragments zoning authority among approximately 30,000 local governments, creating significant inter-jurisdictional coordination problems and enabling exclusionary practices. Germany vests primary planning authority in municipalities but within a strong federal and state framework that constrains local discretion through the BauGB and BauNVO. South Korea employs a centralized hierarchical system with the Ministry of Land, Infrastructure and Transport setting national policy that cascades through provincial and municipal plans. The EU lacks planning authority but constrains national planning through environmental directives.

### Housing and Zoning Reform

The relationship between zoning and housing affordability has become a central policy concern in all jurisdictions. The United States has seen the most dramatic recent reform movement, with multiple states overriding local single-family zoning restrictions to increase housing supply. South Korea has pursued supply-side reforms through expedited rezoning and density increases. Germany added the "Urban Area" (Urbanes Gebiet) category in 2017 to facilitate higher-density mixed-use development. All jurisdictions grapple with the tension between preserving existing neighborhood character and accommodating growth.

### Environmental Integration

The integration of environmental considerations into land use planning varies in approach and depth. Germany provides the most integrated system, with strategic environmental assessment built directly into the BauGB planning process. The EU mandates environmental assessment through the SEA and EIA Directives. The United States requires environmental review under NEPA and state equivalents but applies it primarily to individual projects rather than comprehensive plans. South Korea requires environmental impact assessment for major plans and projects but has been criticized for insufficient integration of environmental considerations into routine planning decisions.
