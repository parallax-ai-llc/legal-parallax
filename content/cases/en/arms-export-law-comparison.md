## Summary

Arms export control -- the regulation of the transfer of weapons, military equipment, and defense-related technology across national borders -- reflects a complex intersection of national security, foreign policy, human rights, and commercial interests, with each jurisdiction balancing these competing considerations through distinct legal frameworks. South Korea regulates defense exports through the Defense Acquisition Program Act (방위사업법) and the Foreign Trade Act (대외무역법), administered by the Defense Acquisition Program Administration (방위사업청, DAPA), and has dramatically expanded its arms export program since the early 2010s, reaching a record $17.3 billion in defense export contracts in 2022 following landmark deals with Poland and other nations. The United States operates the world's most extensive arms export control system through the Arms Export Control Act (AECA, 22 U.S.C. Section 2751 et seq.) and the International Traffic in Arms Regulations (ITAR, 22 C.F.R. Parts 120-130), administered by the State Department's Directorate of Defense Trade Controls (DDTC), alongside the Export Administration Regulations (EAR) administered by the Commerce Department's Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS) for dual-use items, with total authorized arms transfers exceeding $238 billion in fiscal year 2023. Germany controls arms exports through the War Weapons Control Act (Kriegswaffenkontrollgesetz, KrWaffKontrG) and the Foreign Trade and Payments Act (Außenwirtschaftsgesetz, AWG), applying the Political Principles for the Export of War Weapons and Other Military Equipment (Politische Grundsätze) adopted by the Federal Security Council (Bundessicherheitsrat), which have been significantly liberalized since Russia's invasion of Ukraine in 2022. At the supranational level, the EU Common Position 2008/944/CFSP establishes eight criteria for arms export assessment, the Arms Trade Treaty (ATT, 2014) creates the first binding international framework requiring risk assessments of arms transfers, and the European Court of Human Rights has addressed state responsibility for arms transfers contributing to human rights violations.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's arms export control system operates under the Defense Acquisition Program Act (방위사업법) and the Foreign Trade Act (대외무역법). The Defense Acquisition Program Administration (방위사업청, DAPA), established in 2006, serves as the primary regulatory and promotional agency for defense exports. The Ministry of Trade, Industry and Energy oversees dual-use export controls through the Strategic Trade Control system.

South Korea has transformed from an arms importer to a major global exporter within two decades. Defense exports grew from approximately $250 million in 2006 to $17.3 billion in contracted agreements in 2022, driven by competitive pricing, technology transfer packages, and diplomatic engagement.

### Export Control Process

| Stage | Authority | Requirement |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| Pre-marketing | DAPA | Export feasibility review; government-to-government approval |
| License application | DAPA | Technical assessment; foreign policy review |
| End-use verification | Ministry of National Defense | End-use certificate from recipient government |
| Post-shipment monitoring | DAPA | Compliance verification; re-transfer restrictions |
| Offset obligations | DAPA | Industrial offset agreements negotiated per contract |

### Major Recent Exports

South Korea's defense export surge has been concentrated in several high-profile programs:

- **Poland:** In 2022, Poland signed contracts for 1,000 K2 Black Panther main battle tanks, 672 K9 Thunder self-propelled howitzers, and 48 FA-50 light combat aircraft, with total contract value exceeding $14.5 billion. Initial deliveries began in 2022 under accelerated schedules prompted by security concerns following Russia's invasion of Ukraine.
- **Australia:** The Hanwha Defense consortium was selected for Australia's LAND 8116 program to supply 129 Redback infantry fighting vehicles, valued at approximately A$7 billion.
- **UAE and Saudi Arabia:** South Korea has supplied the Chunmoo multiple launch rocket system and other systems to Middle Eastern partners.

### Penalties

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| Unauthorized export of defense articles | Up to 7 years imprisonment or fine up to 50 million KRW |
| Violation of end-use conditions | Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine up to 30 million KRW |
| Unauthorized technology transfer | Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine up to 30 million KRW |
| False statements in export application | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine up to 20 million KRW |

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States maintains the world's most complex and far-reaching arms export control regime, structured around two parallel systems.

**Arms Export Control Act (AECA, 22 U.S.C. Section 2751 et seq.) and ITAR:** The AECA authorizes the President to control the export and import of defense articles and defense services. The International Traffic in Arms Regulations (ITAR, 22 C.F.R. Parts 120-130) implement the AECA, establishing the United States Munitions List (USML) and licensing procedures administered by the State Department's Directorate of Defense Trade Controls (DDTC).

**Export Administration Regulations (EAR):** The Commerce Department's Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS) administers controls on dual-use items under the Export Control Reform Act of 2018 (ECRA, 50 U.S.C. Section 4811 et seq.). The Commerce Control List (CCL) identifies controlled items and applicable reasons for control (national security, foreign policy, nonproliferation, anti-terrorism).

### Congressional Oversight

Section 36 of the AECA requires the executive branch to notify Congress of proposed arms sales exceeding specified thresholds:
- Major defense equipment: $14 million (NATO/allies); $14 million (others)
- Defense articles and services: $100 million (NATO/allies); $100 million (others)
- Design and construction services: $200 million

Congress has a 30-day review period (15 days for NATO members and certain allies) during which it may pass a joint resolution of disapproval to block the sale. While Congress has never successfully blocked a sale through this mechanism, the notification process provides significant leverage for informal congressional influence.

### Key Enforcement Cases

**United States v. Turi (2014):** Marc Turi was charged with violating the AECA for allegedly arranging arms transfers to Libyan rebels during the 2011 civil war. The case was ultimately dismissed amid controversy over classified information.

**ITAR Consent Agreements:** Major defense contractors have paid substantial penalties for ITAR violations, including Raytheon ($20 million in 2022), L3 Technologies ($13 million in 2020), and Honeywell ($13 million in 2023).

### Penalties

| Violation | Criminal Penalty | Civil Penalty |
|-----------|-----------------|---------------|
| AECA/ITAR violation | Up to 20 years imprisonment and/or $1 million fine per violation | Up to $500,000 per violation |
| EAR violation | Up to 20 years imprisonment and/or $1 million fine | Up to $300,000 per violation or twice the transaction value |
| Failure to register as manufacturer/exporter | Up to $500,000 civil penalty | DDTC debarment |

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's arms export control system reflects the country's historical sensitivity to military exports and its constitutional commitment to peace (Article 26 GG, which declares preparations for aggressive war unconstitutional).

**Kriegswaffenkontrollgesetz (KrWaffKontrG -- War Weapons Control Act):** This Act implements Article 26(2) of the Grundgesetz, which provides that weapons designed for warfare may be manufactured, transported, or marketed only with permission from the Federal Government. The Act establishes a licensing requirement for the production, acquisition, transfer, and transport of war weapons listed in the War Weapons List (Kriegswaffenliste).

**Außenwirtschaftsgesetz (AWG -- Foreign Trade and Payments Act):** The AWG and its implementing regulation, the Außenwirtschaftsverordnung (AWV), provide the broader legal framework for export controls on all military equipment (including items not classified as war weapons) and dual-use goods.

### Bundessicherheitsrat (Federal Security Council)

The Bundessicherheitsrat, a cabinet committee chaired by the Federal Chancellor, makes the most politically sensitive arms export decisions. Its deliberations are classified, and its decisions are not subject to judicial review -- a feature that has drawn persistent criticism from transparency advocates and opposition parliamentarians. The Federal Government publishes an annual Arms Export Report (Rüstungsexportbericht) disclosing aggregate data on approved and denied export licenses.

### Political Principles (Politische Grundsätze)

The Political Principles for the Export of War Weapons and Other Military Equipment, adopted in 2000 and informally revised thereafter, establish policy guidelines (not legally binding statutes) for arms export decisions. Key principles include:

- Restrictive approach to exports to non-NATO/EU states
- Human rights situation in the recipient country as a significant consideration
- Internal and regional stability assessment
- Risk of diversion to unauthorized end-users
- NATO/EU allies and equivalent countries generally approved

### Post-2022 Liberalization

Russia's invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 triggered a fundamental shift (Zeitenwende) in German arms export policy. Chancellor Olaf Scholz announced a historic reversal of the longstanding policy against supplying weapons to conflict zones, approving direct arms deliveries to Ukraine. Germany's arms exports surged to approximately 12.2 billion EUR in approved licenses in 2024, reflecting both Ukraine-related transfers and broader recalibration of defense export policy.

### Penalties

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| Unauthorized production/transfer of war weapons (Section 22a KrWaffKontrG) | 1-5 years imprisonment; aggravated cases up to 15 years |
| Unauthorized export of military equipment (AWG) | Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine |
| Violation of end-use commitments | License revocation; criminal prosecution |
| Negligent violations | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine |

## ECHR & EU

### EU Common Position 2008/944/CFSP

Council Common Position 2008/944/CFSP of December 8, 2008, defines common rules governing the control of exports of military technology and equipment. It establishes eight criteria that member states must apply when assessing export license applications:

1. Respect for international obligations and commitments (UN sanctions, embargoes)
2. Respect for human rights in the country of final destination
3. Internal situation in the country of final destination (armed conflict, tensions)
4. Preservation of regional peace, security, and stability
5. National security of member states and allies
6. Behavior of the buyer country regarding the international community
7. Risk of diversion or re-export
8. Compatibility with technical and economic capacity of the recipient

### Arms Trade Treaty (ATT)

The ATT, adopted by the UN General Assembly on April 2, 2013, and entering into force on December 24, 2014, is the first legally binding international agreement regulating the conventional arms trade. Key provisions include:

- **Article 6 (Prohibitions):** No transfer if it would violate UN Security Council arms embargoes or if the state has knowledge that the arms would be used to commit genocide, crimes against humanity, or war crimes
- **Article 7 (Export Assessment):** Exporting states must assess the potential that transferred arms could be used to commit or facilitate serious violations of international humanitarian law or international human rights law
- **Ratification Status:** 113 states parties as of 2026; the United States signed but has not ratified; South Korea ratified in 2014; Germany ratified in 2014

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR has addressed arms export responsibility in limited but significant contexts:

**Stichting Mothers of Srebrenica v. Netherlands (Application No. 65542/12, 2013):** While primarily addressing UN immunity, the case raised questions about state responsibility for failing to prevent arms reaching parties in armed conflicts. The Court declared the application inadmissible but the broader issue of state complicity through arms transfers remains an evolving area of jurisprudence.

National courts in EU member states have increasingly been asked to review arms export decisions. The UK Court of Appeal in Campaign Against Arms Trade v. Secretary of State for International Trade [2019] EWCA Civ 1020 ruled that the UK government had acted unlawfully by failing to properly assess whether Saudi Arabia had committed violations of international humanitarian law in Yemen before approving arms export licenses.

### EU Dual-Use Regulation

Regulation (EU) 2021/821 of the European Parliament and of the Council, adopted May 20, 2021, updates the EU's dual-use export control regime. The Regulation introduces controls on cyber-surveillance items that could be used for internal repression or human rights violations, establishes an EU-level coordination mechanism for catch-all controls, and requires member states to publish annual reports on dual-use export licensing.

### Technology Transfer Controls

Technology transfer -- the sharing of defense-related knowledge, data, and technical assistance -- is often more strategically sensitive than physical arms transfers:

**South Korea:** Technology transfer agreements are integral to Korean defense export contracts. DAPA negotiates offset and technology sharing requirements, with Korean firms increasingly offering technology transfer packages to buyer nations. The Technology Security Act (기술보호법) governs protection of defense-related technologies.

**United States:** ITAR controls on "technical data" and "defense services" extend to intangible technology transfers, including verbal briefings, training, and electronic data sharing. The Deemed Export Rule treats the disclosure of controlled technical data to foreign nationals within the United States as an export requiring a license. This provision has significant implications for the defense industry workforce and for universities conducting defense-related research.

**Germany:** The AWG and AWV control technology transfers related to military items. The Bundesnachrichtendienst (BND -- Federal Intelligence Service) monitors for unauthorized technology transfers. Germany participates in the multilateral export control regimes (Wassenaar Arrangement, Nuclear Suppliers Group, Australia Group, MTCR) that coordinate technology transfer controls.

**EU:** Regulation (EU) 2021/821 on dual-use items imposes controls on technology transfers, including intangible transfers (email, cloud storage, oral communication) of controlled dual-use technology. The "catch-all" clause permits member states to control unlisted items when there is concern about proliferation or human rights end-uses.

### Emerging Technology Concerns

New categories of defense-relevant technology create evolving export control challenges:

| Technology | Export Control Status | Key Concern |
|-----------|---------------------|-------------|
| Artificial intelligence (military applications) | Increasingly controlled; entity listings | Autonomous weapons; surveillance |
| Quantum computing | Under review across jurisdictions | Cryptographic implications |
| Hypersonic technology | Heavily controlled (MTCR, ITAR) | Missile delivery systems |
| Semiconductor manufacturing equipment | ITAR/EAR; multilateral controls (2022-2024) | China advanced chip restrictions |
| Cyber surveillance tools | EU dual-use regulation; Wassenaar | Human rights implications |

## Comparative Analysis

### Restrictiveness Spectrum

The four jurisdictions range from relatively permissive (United States, prioritizing commercial competitiveness and alliance relationships) to traditionally restrictive (Germany, constrained by historical memory and constitutional peace commitments), with South Korea occupying a rapidly evolving middle position. The EU framework attempts to harmonize standards but allows significant national discretion, resulting in inconsistent application of the eight criteria across member states.

### Human Rights Integration

All four jurisdictions formally incorporate human rights considerations into arms export decisions, but the weight given to these considerations varies significantly. The U.S. Leahy Law prohibits assistance to specific military units credibly alleged to have committed gross human rights violations but does not prevent arms sales to the government. The EU Common Position Criterion 2 requires assessment of human rights but does not impose an absolute prohibition. Germany's Political Principles treat human rights as a "significant" factor, but Bundessicherheitsrat decisions are opaque. South Korea's framework includes human rights review but prioritizes commercial and diplomatic objectives.

### Parliamentary Oversight

Congressional oversight in the U.S. is the most structured, with mandatory notification and review periods. German parliamentary oversight is weakened by the classified nature of Bundessicherheitsrat deliberations. South Korea's National Assembly has review authority but limited blocking mechanisms. The European Parliament can issue resolutions on arms exports but has no binding authority over member state decisions.

### Post-Ukraine Realignment

Russia's invasion of Ukraine has triggered a fundamental reassessment of arms export policies across all jurisdictions. Germany's Zeitenwende dramatically loosened traditional restrictions. South Korea accelerated exports to NATO allies. The United States authorized unprecedented transfers. The EU relaxed export criteria for Ukraine-bound transfers. This convergence suggests that geopolitical crisis can rapidly override long-standing policy constraints.
