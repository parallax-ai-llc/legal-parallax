## Summary

Drone regulation (드론 규제) -- the legal framework governing the manufacture, registration, operation, and use of unmanned aircraft systems (UAS) for commercial, recreational, and governmental purposes -- has rapidly evolved as drone technology has proliferated from military applications to commercial delivery, infrastructure inspection, agriculture, journalism, law enforcement, and recreational use. South Korea enacted the Drone Utilization Promotion and Foundation Establishment Act (드론 활용 촉진 및 기반조성에 관한 법률, commonly known as the Drone Act) in 2020, establishing a comprehensive framework for drone industry promotion and safety regulation supplemented by the Aviation Safety Act (항공안전법) which governs UAS operational requirements including registration, pilot certification, flight restrictions near airports and military facilities, and payload limitations; the Korean government launched K-Drone System as its national unmanned traffic management (UTM) platform. The United States regulates drones primarily through the Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) under 14 C.F.R. Part 107 (Small Unmanned Aircraft Systems, effective 2016), which established the regulatory framework for commercial drone operations under 55 pounds, supplemented by the FAA Reauthorization Act of 2024 expanding beyond visual line of sight (BVLOS) operations and Remote ID requirements (effective 2024); state and local governments exercise concurrent jurisdiction over privacy, trespass, and nuisance issues related to drone use. Germany applies the EU's comprehensive drone regulation framework under Commission Implementing Regulation (EU) 2019/947 and Delegated Regulation (EU) 2019/945, which established a risk-based categorization system (open, specific, certified) effective since January 2021, supplemented by national airspace restrictions through the Luftverkehrs-Ordnung (LuftVO, Air Traffic Order) and the Drohnenverordnung (drone ordinance). At the EU level, the harmonized drone regulation framework creates uniform rules for drone operations across all member states through the European Union Aviation Safety Agency (EASA), while the European Court of Human Rights has addressed drone surveillance under Article 8 privacy protections.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea has adopted an ambitious regulatory approach balancing drone industry promotion with safety and security requirements.

**Drone Utilization Promotion and Foundation Establishment Act (드론 활용 촉진 및 기반조성에 관한 법률, 2020):** This Act provides the strategic framework for Korea's drone industry development, establishing the National Drone Committee, promoting drone-based services, and facilitating regulatory sandboxes for innovative drone applications. It mandates five-year national drone development plans and designates drone special zones (드론 특별자유화구역) where relaxed operational rules encourage testing and commercial deployment.

**Aviation Safety Act (항공안전법):** The Aviation Safety Act provides the operational regulatory framework for UAS:

- **Registration:** All UAS over 250g must be registered with the Korea Transportation Safety Authority (한국교통안전공단). As of 2024, over 120,000 drones are registered in Korea.
- **Pilot Certification:** Commercial drone operators must obtain an ultralight vehicle pilot certificate (초경량비행장치 조종자 증명) by passing written and practical examinations. Different certificate classes apply based on drone weight and operational complexity.
- **Flight Restrictions:** Flights are prohibited in controlled airspace around airports (9.3 km radius), military installations, government buildings, and densely populated areas without specific authorization from the Regional Aviation Administration (지방항공청).
- **Altitude Limits:** Maximum altitude of 150 meters above ground level for general operations; higher altitudes require specific authorization.

### K-Drone System

South Korea developed the K-Drone System as its national UTM (Unmanned Traffic Management) platform, operated by the Korea Institute of Aviation Safety Technology. The system provides real-time airspace management, flight plan filing and approval, conflict detection, and integration with manned aviation traffic management. The K-Drone System is being expanded to support urban air mobility (UAM) operations in preparation for commercial air taxi services planned for launch at the 2028 pilot phase.

### Urban Air Mobility (UAM)

The Korean government has designated UAM as a national strategic industry, with plans for commercial air taxi services in Seoul and major cities. The UAM Special Act (도심항공교통 활성화 및 지원에 관한 특별법, 2024) establishes the regulatory framework for passenger-carrying electric vertical takeoff and landing (eVTOL) aircraft operations.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has developed a comprehensive federal drone regulatory framework while navigating federalism tensions regarding state and local authority.

**14 C.F.R. Part 107 (Small UAS Rule, 2016):** Part 107, effective August 29, 2016, establishes the primary regulatory framework for commercial drone operations involving small UAS (under 55 pounds):

- **Remote Pilot Certificate:** Commercial operators must obtain a Remote Pilot Certificate by passing an aeronautical knowledge test at an FAA-approved testing center. Certificate holders must pass recurrent knowledge tests every 24 months.
- **Operational Limitations:** Visual line of sight (VLOS) operations; maximum altitude of 400 feet AGL; daylight operations (extended to civil twilight with anti-collision lighting); maximum groundspeed of 100 mph; no operations over people (with waiver or compliance with subcategories).
- **Waivers:** The FAA may issue waivers for operations beyond Part 107 limitations, including nighttime operations, operations over people, and BVLOS flights.

### FAA Reauthorization Act of 2024

The FAA Reauthorization Act of 2024 significantly expanded the drone regulatory framework:

**BVLOS Framework:** The Act directed the FAA to establish regulations permitting routine BVLOS operations for commercial drones, a critical enabler for package delivery, infrastructure inspection, and agricultural applications. The framework requires detect-and-avoid technology, ground-based radar systems, or other approved mitigation measures.

**Remote Identification:** The Remote ID rule (14 C.F.R. Part 89), effective March 16, 2024, requires all drones operating in US airspace to broadcast identification and location information in real time. Remote ID enables law enforcement, airspace management, and accountability for drone operations.

**Counter-UAS Authority:** The Act expanded federal authority to detect and mitigate threatening drones near airports, critical infrastructure, and mass gatherings, while restricting state and local counter-drone authority to prevent interference with federal airspace management.

### State and Local Authority

The relationship between federal and state/local drone authority remains contested. The FAA asserts exclusive jurisdiction over navigable airspace. However, states and localities have enacted regulations addressing:

| Area | Examples |
|------|---------|
| Privacy | 38+ states have enacted drone privacy laws restricting surveillance |
| Trespass | State laws addressing low-altitude drone flights over private property |
| Law enforcement | 22+ states require warrants for law enforcement drone surveillance |
| Hunting/fishing | Multiple states prohibit using drones for hunting or fishing |
| Critical infrastructure | State laws restricting drone flights near prisons, power plants |

### Landmark Cases

**National Geospatial-Intelligence Agency v. Causby Legacy:** The Supreme Court's foundational airspace property rights decision in United States v. Causby (328 U.S. 256, 1946) established that property owners have rights in the immediate reaches of airspace above their property. This doctrine is being reinterpreted in the drone context, with courts and commentators debating the altitude at which drone flights constitute trespass.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany implements the EU's harmonized drone regulation framework through national measures, with some of the most restrictive operational requirements in Europe.

**EU Regulation 2019/947 (Implementing Regulation on UAS Operations):** Directly applicable in Germany since January 1, 2021, this regulation establishes three operational categories:

**Open Category:** Low-risk operations not requiring prior authorization. Subcategories A1 (fly over people, <250g), A2 (fly close to people, <4kg, with certificate), and A3 (fly far from people, <25kg). Operators must register and complete online training or competency examinations.

**Specific Category:** Medium-risk operations requiring operational authorization from the Luftfahrt-Bundesamt (LBA, Federal Aviation Authority) or use of a standard scenario. Operators must conduct risk assessments using the SORA (Specific Operations Risk Assessment) methodology developed by JARUS (Joint Authorities for Rulemaking on Unmanned Systems).

**Certified Category:** High-risk operations (large drones, operations over assemblies of people, cargo or passenger transport) requiring type certification, operator licensing, and pilot licensing analogous to manned aviation.

### National Restrictions (LuftVO)

The Luftverkehrs-Ordnung (LuftVO, Air Traffic Order) and the Drohnenverordnung impose additional national restrictions:

- **No-Fly Zones:** Federal and state government buildings, embassies, military installations, industrial areas, nature conservation areas, hospitals, prisons, motorways and railways (within 100m), and assemblies of people.
- **Height Restrictions:** 120 meters maximum in the open category (EU-harmonized).
- **Insurance:** Mandatory third-party liability insurance for all drone operations, as required by EU Regulation 785/2004 as applied to UAS.

### U-Space Implementation

Germany is implementing the U-space framework (Commission Implementing Regulation (EU) 2021/664) for managing drone traffic in designated urban airspace. U-space services include electronic identification, geo-awareness, flight authorization, and traffic management. Pilot projects in Hamburg, Ingolstadt, and other cities are testing U-space services for commercial drone delivery and urban air mobility.

### Privacy and Data Protection

Drone operations involving cameras or sensors that capture personal data are subject to GDPR requirements. The data protection conference of German state DPAs (Datenschutzkonferenz, DSK) issued guidance in 2023 specifying that drone operators processing personal data through aerial photography must comply with GDPR principles including purpose limitation, data minimization, and, in many cases, consent requirements.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Drone Regulation Framework

The EU has established the most comprehensive supranational drone regulatory framework:

**Regulation (EU) 2019/945 (Delegated Regulation on UAS):** Establishes technical requirements for UAS placed on the EU market, including class identification labels (C0-C6), noise limits, direct remote identification capability, and geo-awareness functionality. Manufacturers must ensure compliance through CE marking and EU declaration of conformity.

**Regulation (EU) 2019/947 (Implementing Regulation on UAS Operations):** Establishes operational rules for UAS in the three-category system (open, specific, certified). It requires operator registration, pilot competency, and operational procedures scaled to the risk level.

**U-Space Regulation (Commission Implementing Regulation (EU) 2021/664):** Establishes the framework for U-space airspace -- designated airspace volumes where drone operations are managed through digital services. U-space service providers must offer network identification, geo-awareness, flight authorization, and traffic information services.

### EASA (European Union Aviation Safety Agency)

EASA serves as the central regulatory authority for EU drone policy, developing implementing rules, guidance material, and acceptable means of compliance. EASA conducts type certification for certified-category drones and approves standard scenarios for specific-category operations. The Agency published its "Drone Strategy 2.0" in 2023, outlining the pathway to routine BVLOS operations and urban air mobility by 2030.

### European Court of Human Rights

While the ECHR has not issued a drone-specific judgment, its surveillance and privacy jurisprudence applies directly:

**Big Brother Watch v. United Kingdom (Applications Nos. 58170/13, 62322/14, and 24960/15, Grand Chamber Judgment of May 25, 2021):** The Grand Chamber addressed bulk surveillance, establishing principles on necessity, proportionality, and independent oversight that apply to governmental drone surveillance programs. The judgment requires that surveillance regimes include clear legal basis, purpose limitation, independent authorization, and effective oversight.

**Uzun v. Germany (Application No. 35623/05, Judgment of September 2, 2010):** The Court addressed GPS surveillance by law enforcement, finding that systematic GPS tracking constitutes interference with Article 8 rights but may be justified under Article 8(2) where provided by law, necessary in a democratic society, and subject to adequate safeguards. This framework applies directly to drone-based tracking and surveillance.

### Warsaw Convention and International Framework

ICAO (International Civil Aviation Organization) has adopted Standards and Recommended Practices (SARPs) for UAS through Annex 8 (Airworthiness) amendments and is developing further SARPs for unmanned aircraft traffic management (UTM). These international standards inform national and regional regulatory frameworks.

## Comparative Analysis

### Regulatory Architecture

The EU's supranational harmonized framework (applied by Germany) is the most comprehensive, creating uniform rules across 27 member states. South Korea's centralized national framework integrates industry promotion with safety regulation. The United States' federal framework addresses airspace safety but faces ongoing tensions with state and local authority over privacy and property rights. The fragmented US approach contrasts with the EU's unified system.

### Risk-Based Categorization

The EU's three-category system (open, specific, certified) represents the most developed risk-based approach, proportioning regulatory requirements to operational risk. The US Part 107 system is evolving toward risk-based differentiation through waivers and the BVLOS framework. South Korea applies graduated requirements based on drone weight and operational context.

### BVLOS Operations

BVLOS capability is the key enabler for commercial drone services (delivery, inspection, agriculture). The US FAA Reauthorization Act of 2024 provides the most advanced statutory framework for routine BVLOS operations. The EU's specific-category SORA methodology provides a structured pathway. South Korea permits BVLOS with special authorization through K-Drone System integration.

### Privacy Protection

The US has the most fragmented privacy framework, with 38+ state drone privacy laws. The EU's GDPR provides uniform data protection requirements for drone-captured data. South Korea's PIPA applies to drone surveillance data. The ECHR's surveillance jurisprudence provides an overarching human rights framework applicable across Council of Europe member states.
