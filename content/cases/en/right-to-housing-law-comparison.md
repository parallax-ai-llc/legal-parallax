## Summary

The right to housing -- encompassing access to adequate, affordable, and secure shelter as well as protection against forced eviction, homelessness, and housing discrimination -- is recognized in numerous international human rights instruments and national constitutions, yet its justiciability and practical implementation remain deeply contested across legal systems. South Korea addresses housing through Article 35(3) of the Constitution (대한민국 헌법 제35조 제3항), which provides that "the State shall endeavor to ensure comfortable housing for all citizens through housing development policies and the like," supplemented by the Housing Act (주택법), the Housing Lease Protection Act (주택임대차보호법), and extensive public housing programs; the Korean housing market has been characterized by unique features including the jeonse (전세) deposit-based rental system, extreme price volatility in the Seoul metropolitan area, and aggressive government intervention through dozens of housing stabilization measures under successive administrations. The United States does not recognize a constitutional right to housing, with housing policy operating primarily through federal programs including Section 8 Housing Choice Vouchers, public housing, the Low-Income Housing Tax Credit (LIHTC), and fair housing protections under the Fair Housing Act of 1968; the Supreme Court addressed the intersection of homelessness and constitutional rights in Grants Pass v. Johnson (2024), holding that enforcement of anti-camping ordinances against homeless individuals does not constitute cruel and unusual punishment under the Eighth Amendment. Germany derives housing protections from the Sozialstaatsprinzip (social state principle) under Article 20(1) of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz), with robust tenant protections under the Civil Code (Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch, BGB) including rent control provisions (Mietpreisbremse) introduced in 2015 and a landmark Berlin rent cap (Mietendeckel) that was struck down by the Federal Constitutional Court in 2021 on federalism grounds; several Länder constitutions explicitly guarantee the right to housing. The European framework protects housing through Article 31 of the European Social Charter (revised), Article 7 of the EU Charter (respect for home), and ECHR Article 8 jurisprudence, with the European Court of Human Rights having addressed forced evictions, Roma housing rights, and the positive obligations of states to provide housing for vulnerable groups.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Article 35(3) of the Constitution provides: "The State shall endeavor to ensure comfortable housing for all citizens through housing development policies and the like." While this is framed as a state duty rather than an individual right, the Constitutional Court has held that it creates an obligation on the state to pursue housing policies that secure minimum living conditions.

### Housing Lease Protection Act (주택임대차보호법)

The Housing Lease Protection Act, originally enacted in 1981, is the primary statute protecting tenants in South Korea. Key provisions include:

**Minimum Lease Term:** The Act guarantees a minimum lease period of two years, even if the contract specifies a shorter term. Tenants have the right to request renewal for an additional two years under the 2020 amendment (계약갱신청구권), creating a de facto four-year minimum tenure.

**Deposit Protection:** The Act establishes a priority lien (우선변제권) for tenant deposits (including jeonse deposits) against the landlord's property, providing security in the event of the landlord's bankruptcy or foreclosure.

**Rent Increase Cap:** The 2020 amendment introduced a five percent cap on rent increases during lease renewal, the first statutory rent control mechanism in South Korea's modern history.

### Jeonse System (전세)

South Korea's unique jeonse rental system involves a tenant providing a large lump-sum deposit (typically 50-80 percent of the property's value) to the landlord, in lieu of monthly rent. The landlord invests the deposit and returns it at the end of the lease. This system, which has no direct equivalent in other countries, has faced a crisis since 2022 as declining property values have left some landlords unable to return deposits, leading to the "jeonse fraud" (전세사기) crisis. The government enacted emergency measures including the Special Act on Support for Victims of Housing Lease Fraud (전세사기 피해자 지원 특별법, 2023).

### Public Housing

The Korea Land and Housing Corporation (한국토지주택공사, LH) is the primary provider of public housing. Public rental housing programs include:

| Program | Target Population | Key Features |
|---------|-------------------|--------------|
| Permanent public rental (영구임대주택) | Lowest-income households | 50-year lease; heavily subsidized rent |
| National public rental (국민임대주택) | Low-to-moderate income | 30-year lease; below-market rent |
| Happy Housing (행복주택) | Young adults, newlyweds, elderly | Near transit; 6-20 year lease |
| Long-term jeonse rental (장기전세주택) | Moderate-income households | Jeonse at 80% of market rate |

### Housing Price Interventions

The Seoul metropolitan area housing market has been subject to intensive government intervention. The Moon Jae-in administration (2017-2022) introduced over 25 housing stabilization packages, including multiple property tax surcharges, loan-to-value ratio restrictions, comprehensive real estate taxes, and designation of "overheated speculation zones" (과열지구). Despite these measures, Seoul apartment prices approximately doubled during that period. The Yoon Suk-yeol administration (2022-) has relaxed many restrictions, emphasizing housing supply expansion.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States does not recognize a federal constitutional right to housing. The Supreme Court in Lindsey v. Normet, 405 U.S. 56 (1972), held that the Constitution does not provide judicial remedies for every social and economic ill, and that "the assurance of adequate housing... [is] a legislative and not a judicial function."

### Fair Housing Act (42 U.S.C. Section 3601)

The Fair Housing Act of 1968, enacted as Title VIII of the Civil Rights Act of 1968, prohibits discrimination in the sale, rental, and financing of housing based on race, color, religion, sex, national origin, familial status, and disability. The Supreme Court in Texas Department of Housing and Community Affairs v. Inclusive Communities Project, 576 U.S. 519 (2015), held that disparate impact claims are cognizable under the Fair Housing Act.

### Federal Housing Programs

**Public Housing:** Administered by approximately 3,300 local public housing authorities (PHAs), public housing serves approximately 900,000 households. The public housing stock has declined significantly since its peak, with the HOPE VI and Rental Assistance Demonstration (RAD) programs converting public housing to mixed-income developments.

**Section 8 Housing Choice Vouchers:** The largest federal rental assistance program, serving approximately 2.3 million households. Vouchers subsidize the difference between 30 percent of household income and a fair market rent. Demand far exceeds supply, with waitlists of years in many jurisdictions.

**Low-Income Housing Tax Credit (LIHTC):** Enacted in 1986, the LIHTC is the largest source of affordable housing production in the United States, financing approximately 110,000 units annually through tax credits to developers who commit to affordability restrictions.

### Grants Pass v. Johnson (2024)

In City of Grants Pass v. Johnson, 603 U.S. ___ (2024), the Supreme Court held 6-3 that the enforcement of generally applicable public camping ordinances against homeless persons does not constitute cruel and unusual punishment under the Eighth Amendment. The decision reversed the Ninth Circuit's ruling in Martin v. Boise (2018), which had held that criminalizing sleeping outdoors when no shelter is available violates the Eighth Amendment. The Grants Pass decision removed constitutional constraints on municipalities' ability to enforce anti-camping laws, prompting renewed debate over approaches to homelessness.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

The Basic Law does not contain an explicit right to housing, but several Länder constitutions do. Bavaria's Constitution (Article 106) provides that "every resident of Bavaria has a right to adequate housing." The Federal Constitutional Court has derived minimum housing protections from the Sozialstaatsprinzip and the right to human dignity (Article 1(1) GG).

### Tenant Protection under BGB

German tenancy law, codified in Sections 535-580a of the BGB, provides some of the strongest tenant protections in the world:

**Termination Protection:** Landlords may only terminate residential leases for cause (Section 573 BGB), including breach of contract, own-use need (Eigenbedarf), or substantial economic disadvantage. Even where cause exists, tenants may invoke the social hardship clause (Sozialklausel, Section 574 BGB) to obtain continuation of the lease if termination would constitute undue hardship.

**Rent Increase Limitations:** Section 558 BGB limits rent increases to the local comparative rent (ortsübliche Vergleichsmiete) and imposes a Kappungsgrenze (cap) of 20 percent over three years (15 percent in areas with tight housing markets).

### Mietpreisbremse (Rent Brake, 2015)

The Mietenspiegel-Reform and Mietpreisbremse legislation, enacted in 2015 and strengthened in 2019 and 2020, limits new-lease rents to no more than 10 percent above the local comparative rent (ortsübliche Vergleichsmiete) in designated areas with tight housing markets (angespannte Wohnungsmärkte). Exceptions apply to new construction, comprehensive modernization, and apartments where the previous tenant already paid above the limit.

### Berlin Mietendeckel (2020-2021)

The Berlin state parliament enacted the Gesetz zur Mietenbegrenzung im Wohnungswesen in Berlin (Mietendeckel) in February 2020, which froze and in some cases reduced residential rents across Berlin. The Federal Constitutional Court struck down the law on April 15, 2021 (2 BvF 1/20), holding that the regulation of tenancy law (including rent levels) falls within the federal government's exclusive concurrent legislative competence under Article 74(1)(1) GG, and that the federal Mietpreisbremse had exhaustively occupied this field, precluding state-level rent control.

### Social Housing

Germany's social housing stock (Sozialer Wohnungsbau) has declined significantly from approximately 4 million units in the 1980s to roughly 1.1 million in 2024, as affordability restrictions on subsidized units have expired. The federal government launched a construction offensive (Bau-Offensive) targeting 400,000 new housing units per year, including 100,000 social housing units, but construction figures have fallen short of these targets.

## ECHR & EU

### European Social Charter

Article 31 of the European Social Charter (revised) provides the most explicit international guarantee of housing rights: "With a view to ensuring the effective exercise of the right to housing, the Parties undertake to take measures designed: (1) to promote access to housing of an adequate standard; (2) to prevent and reduce homelessness with a view to its gradual elimination; (3) to make the price of housing accessible to those without adequate resources."

The European Committee of Social Rights (ECSR) monitors compliance and has found violations in numerous cases, particularly regarding Roma housing rights and homelessness.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Connors v. United Kingdom (Application No. 66746/01, 2004):** The Court held that the eviction of a Roma family from a local authority caravan site violated Article 8, as the lack of procedural safeguards and the failure to consider the family's circumstances rendered the interference disproportionate.

**McCann v. United Kingdom (Application No. 19009/04, 2008):** The Grand Chamber held that the loss of a person's home is a "most extreme form of interference" with Article 8 rights and requires that the proportionality of the eviction be determined by an independent tribunal.

**Yordanova and Others v. Bulgaria (Application No. 25446/06, 2012):** The Court held that the planned eviction of Roma families from an informal settlement in Sofia would violate Article 8, requiring the authorities to consider the applicants' vulnerability and the availability of alternative housing.

**FEX v. Russia (Application No. 24405/09, 2022):** The Court addressed the right to adequate housing in the context of environmental contamination, finding that Russia's failure to resettle families living in contaminated housing violated Article 8.

### EU Framework

The EU does not have specific competence over housing policy, which remains a member state responsibility. However, EU law affects housing through:

- **Mortgage Credit Directive (2014/17/EU):** Harmonizes consumer protection standards for mortgage lending
- **Energy Performance of Buildings Directive (EPBD, recast 2024):** Requires member states to establish renovation strategies to achieve highly energy-efficient building stock by 2050
- **European Pillar of Social Rights (Principle 19):** "Housing and assistance for the homeless: (a) Access to social housing or housing assistance of good quality shall be provided for those in need. (b) Vulnerable people have the right to appropriate assistance and protection against forced eviction."

## Comparative Analysis

### Constitutional Status

The four jurisdictions reflect a spectrum of constitutional recognition. South Korea's Constitution imposes a state duty to ensure housing. Germany derives housing protections from the social state principle and Länder constitutions. The ECHR protects the "home" under Article 8 and housing rights through the Social Charter. The United States provides no constitutional right to housing.

### Tenant Protection

Germany provides the strongest tenant protections, with cause-based termination, rent control mechanisms, and social hardship defenses. South Korea has strengthened tenant protections significantly since 2020 with renewal rights and rent increase caps. The United States provides minimal federal tenant protections, with regulation largely at the state and local level. The ECHR requires proportionality assessments for evictions.

### Rent Control Approaches

Germany's Mietpreisbremse and Kappungsgrenze represent the most developed rent control framework among the four systems. South Korea's five percent renewal cap is more recent and narrower. The United States has no federal rent control, with regulation existing only in select states and cities (New York, California, Oregon). The Berlin Mietendeckel case illustrates the constitutional limits on rent regulation within federal systems.

### Homelessness

All four jurisdictions face homelessness challenges but approach them differently. The United States has the largest absolute homeless population among the four and the Grants Pass decision has expanded municipalities' enforcement powers. Germany's social assistance system (SGB XII) includes emergency shelter obligations. South Korea provides shelter programs through the Homeless Welfare Act. The European Social Charter explicitly commits states to reducing and eliminating homelessness.
