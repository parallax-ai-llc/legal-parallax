## Summary

Conflict minerals regulation -- the legal frameworks designed to prevent the extraction and trade of minerals from funding armed conflict, human rights abuses, and exploitation in politically unstable regions -- has developed into a significant area of international regulatory convergence, centered on the sourcing of tin, tantalum, tungsten, and gold (collectively "3TG") from the Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC) and adjoining countries, but increasingly extending to other minerals and conflict-affected regions globally. South Korea addresses conflict mineral sourcing primarily through voluntary corporate responsibility frameworks and the Korea Exchange (KRX, 한국거래소) disclosure requirements for listed companies, supplemented by the Act on the Promotion of Purchase of Products from Social Enterprises (사회적기업 육성법) and sector-specific guidelines issued by the Ministry of Trade, Industry and Energy (산업통상자원부, MOTIE); Korea is a major consumer of 3TG minerals through its electronics and automotive industries (Samsung Electronics, LG, Hyundai, SK Hynix), creating significant supply chain exposure to conflict mineral risks, and Korean companies listed on US exchanges are additionally subject to Dodd-Frank Section 1502. The United States pioneered mandatory conflict minerals disclosure through Section 1502 of the Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act (15 U.S.C. Section 78m(p)), enacted on July 21, 2010, requiring SEC-reporting companies that manufacture or contract to manufacture products for which 3TG minerals are necessary to the functionality or production to conduct due diligence on the source and chain of custody of those minerals and file annual conflict minerals reports (Form SD) with the SEC; the implementing SEC Rule 13p-1 (17 C.F.R. Section 240.13p-1) survived legal challenges in National Association of Manufacturers v. SEC (D.C. Cir. 2014) except for the compelled speech requirement to label products as "not been found to be DRC conflict free." Germany, as a major manufacturing economy and significant importer of mineral resources, implements the EU Conflict Minerals Regulation (Regulation 2017/821) through the Bundesanstalt für Geowissenschaften und Rohstoffe (BGR, Federal Institute for Geosciences and Natural Resources) which serves as the competent authority for monitoring importers' due diligence obligations. The EU adopted Regulation 2017/821 on May 17, 2017, applicable from January 1, 2021, establishing mandatory supply chain due diligence obligations for EU importers of tin, tantalum, tungsten, and gold from conflict-affected and high-risk areas (CAHRAs) globally -- not limited to the DRC region -- requiring importers above specified volume thresholds to identify and assess supply chain risks, implement risk mitigation strategies, carry out independent third-party audits of supply chain due diligence, and report publicly on their policies and practices, complemented by the broader Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD) framework.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea lacks dedicated conflict minerals legislation but addresses the issue through a combination of US regulatory compliance (for companies listed on American exchanges), voluntary corporate responsibility commitments, and evolving KRX disclosure expectations.

### Exposure Through Global Supply Chains

Korea is one of the world's largest consumers of 3TG minerals through its dominant electronics and semiconductor industries. Key Korean companies with significant conflict mineral supply chain exposure include:

| Company | Sector | 3TG Relevance |
|---------|--------|---------------|
| Samsung Electronics | Semiconductors, consumer electronics | Major consumer of tantalum (capacitors), tin (solder), tungsten (wiring), gold (connectors) |
| SK Hynix | Semiconductors | Tantalum, tungsten, gold in chip manufacturing |
| LG Electronics | Consumer electronics, batteries | 3TG in electronic components |
| Hyundai Motor | Automotive | Tin (solder, coatings), tungsten (tooling), gold (electronics) |
| POSCO | Steel and materials | Tungsten in specialty steel alloys |

### Dodd-Frank Compliance by Korean Companies

Korean companies listed on US exchanges (through ADRs or direct listings) are subject to SEC conflict minerals reporting requirements. Samsung Electronics, LG Display, POSCO, and others file annual Form SD reports with the SEC, conducting supply chain due diligence and identifying smelters and refiners in their supply chains. This US regulatory requirement has had a cascading effect throughout Korean supply chains, as listed companies impose due diligence requirements on their Korean suppliers.

### Korea Exchange Disclosure

The KRX has incorporated sustainability disclosure requirements into its listing rules, and conflict minerals due diligence is addressed within the broader ESG reporting framework. The KRX ESG Information Portal, operational since 2021, encourages listed companies to disclose their approaches to responsible mineral sourcing.

### OECD Guidance Adoption

Korean industry associations, particularly the Korea Electronics Association (한국전자정보통신산업진흥회, KEA), have promoted adoption of the OECD Due Diligence Guidance for Responsible Supply Chains of Minerals from Conflict-Affected and High-Risk Areas among member companies. The KEA published a Korean-language implementation guide and conducts training programs for member companies on conflict mineral due diligence.

### Responsible Minerals Initiative (RMI) Participation

Korean smelters and refiners participate in the Responsible Minerals Initiative's (formerly Conflict-Free Smelter Program) audit program. As of 2024, several Korean tungsten processors and tin smelters had achieved Responsible Minerals Assurance Process (RMAP) conformance, facilitating their inclusion in downstream companies' responsible sourcing programs.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States established the first mandatory conflict minerals disclosure regime globally through Section 1502 of the Dodd-Frank Act, reflecting Congressional concern that the extraction and trade of minerals from the DRC and adjoining countries was funding armed groups responsible for widespread human rights abuses.

### Dodd-Frank Section 1502 (15 U.S.C. Section 78m(p))

Section 1502 requires every SEC-reporting company (issuer) that manufactures or contracts to manufacture products for which conflict minerals are "necessary to the functionality or production" to:

1. **Reasonable Country of Origin Inquiry (RCOI):** Conduct a reasonable inquiry to determine whether its conflict minerals originated in the DRC or an adjoining country, or are from recycled or scrap sources
2. **Due Diligence:** If the RCOI does not establish that minerals are from recycled/scrap sources or did not originate in covered countries, conduct due diligence on the source and chain of custody conforming to a nationally or internationally recognized due diligence framework (effectively the OECD Guidance)
3. **Form SD Filing:** File an annual Specialized Disclosure Report (Form SD) with the SEC describing the due diligence measures undertaken and results
4. **Conflict Minerals Report (CMR):** If the company cannot determine its minerals are DRC conflict-free, file a CMR as an exhibit to the Form SD describing products manufactured or contracted to be manufactured that have not been found to be DRC conflict free, the facilities used to process minerals, the country of origin, and the efforts to determine the mine or location of origin

### Adjoining Countries

The statute defines "covered countries" as the DRC and its nine adjoining countries: Angola, Burundi, Central African Republic, Republic of the Congo, Rwanda, South Sudan, Tanzania, Uganda, and Zambia.

### SEC Rule 13p-1 and Form SD

The SEC adopted final implementing rules on August 22, 2012 (Release No. 34-67716). Key provisions:

| Requirement | Details |
|-------------|---------|
| **Filing Deadline** | May 31 of each year (covering prior calendar year) |
| **Reasonable Country of Origin Inquiry** | Must be reasonably designed to determine whether minerals originated in covered countries or from recycled/scrap sources |
| **Due Diligence Framework** | Must conform to a nationally or internationally recognized framework (OECD Guidance) |
| **Independent Private Sector Audit (IPSA)** | Originally required for CMR; SEC has not enforced this requirement following NAM v. SEC decision |
| **Product Description** | Description of products manufactured or contracted that may contain covered-country minerals |
| **Smelter/Refiner Identification** | Identification of processing facilities; country of origin information |

### National Association of Manufacturers v. SEC (D.C. Cir. 2014)

In NAM v. SEC, the D.C. Circuit upheld most of Section 1502 and Rule 13p-1 against statutory authority and Administrative Procedure Act challenges but struck down the requirement that companies describe their products as "not been found to be DRC conflict free" as compelled speech violating the First Amendment. Following this ruling and subsequent SEC guidance, the SEC has not required companies to label their products with conflict status descriptions, and the independent private sector audit requirement has been held in abeyance.

### Filing Statistics

| Year | Form SD Filings | Companies Reporting "DRC Conflict-Free" | Companies Unable to Determine |
|------|----------------|----------------------------------------|------------------------------|
| 2014 | 1,321 | ~2% | ~80% |
| 2018 | 1,157 | ~12% | ~55% |
| 2022 | 1,098 | ~20% | ~45% |
| 2024 | ~1,050 | ~25% | ~40% |

The data shows gradual improvement in supply chain traceability, though a significant proportion of companies still cannot determine the conflict status of all minerals in their products.

### Criticisms and Proposed Reforms

Dodd-Frank Section 1502 has been criticized from multiple perspectives:
- **Effectiveness:** Critics argue the law has not significantly reduced conflict in the DRC and may have harmed artisanal miners by creating a de facto embargo
- **Compliance Burden:** Industry groups cite high compliance costs ($3-4 billion initially, reduced to $600 million-$1 billion annually) relative to uncertain benefits
- **Scope Limitations:** Only covers 3TG from DRC and adjoining countries, missing other minerals (cobalt, lithium) and other conflict regions
- **Enforcement Gaps:** The SEC has brought no enforcement actions specifically for conflict minerals violations

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany implements the EU Conflict Minerals Regulation (Regulation 2017/821) through the Bundesanstalt für Geowissenschaften und Rohstoffe (BGR), which serves as the national competent authority for monitoring and enforcing conflict mineral due diligence obligations for German importers.

### EU Regulation Implementation

Germany transposed the implementation requirements of Regulation 2017/821 through administrative arrangements designating BGR as the competent authority and establishing monitoring procedures. BGR has particular expertise as Germany's federal geological survey, with longstanding programs on mineral resource governance and certification, including the Certified Trading Chains (CTC) initiative for mineral supply chains in the Great Lakes Region of Africa.

### Covered German Importers

Germany is a significant importer of 3TG minerals and metals, particularly:
- **Tin:** Used in electronics, automotive, and chemical industries
- **Tungsten:** Germany is a major tungsten consumer for tooling, mining, and specialty steel (Wolfram Bergbau und Hütten, though Austrian, supplies German industry)
- **Tantalum:** Used in capacitors and superalloys
- **Gold:** Frankfurt is a major European gold trading center; several LBMA-accredited refineries

BGR maintains a register of covered importers and conducts annual monitoring to verify compliance with due diligence obligations.

### BGR Monitoring Activities

BGR's monitoring approach includes:

| Activity | Frequency | Details |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| Self-assessment questionnaires | Annual | All covered importers must complete questionnaires on supply chain due diligence practices |
| Documentary review | Annual (risk-based) | Review of supply chain policies, risk assessments, smelter/refiner lists, audit reports |
| On-site inspections | Risk-based | Physical inspection of importer records and due diligence systems |
| Smelter/refiner verification | Ongoing | Cross-referencing reported smelters against RMAP-conformant and EU responsible smelter lists |

### BGR Analytical Fingerprinting

Germany has made a unique contribution to conflict mineral traceability through BGR's Analytical Fingerprint (AFP) method, which uses geochemical and mineralogical analysis to determine the geographic origin of mineral samples. This scientific method provides forensic-level verification of mineral provenance, complementing documentary due diligence.

### Integration with LkSG

The LkSG (Supply Chain Due Diligence Act) creates additional obligations for German companies sourcing minerals from conflict-affected areas. Companies with 1,000+ employees must conduct human rights due diligence across their supply chains, which includes conflict mineral sourcing risks. The LkSG's broader scope (covering all human rights and environmental risks, not just 3TG from specific regions) means that conflict mineral due diligence is subsumed within the general supply chain due diligence framework for covered companies.

### German Industry Initiatives

German industry associations have developed sector-specific conflict mineral guidance:
- **Bundesverband der Deutschen Industrie (BDI):** Published guidelines on responsible mineral sourcing
- **ZVEI (Electrical Industry Association):** Conflict minerals compliance toolkit for the electronics sector
- **VDA (Association of the German Automotive Industry):** Responsible sourcing requirements within the automotive supply chain, addressing 3TG and extending to cobalt and mica

## ECHR & EU

### EU Conflict Minerals Regulation (Regulation 2017/821)

The EU Conflict Minerals Regulation, adopted on May 17, 2017, and applicable from January 1, 2021, establishes mandatory supply chain due diligence for EU importers of 3TG minerals and metals. The Regulation reflects the EU's implementation of the OECD Due Diligence Guidance and represents a different regulatory approach from Dodd-Frank Section 1502.

### Key Differences from Dodd-Frank

| Feature | EU Regulation 2017/821 | Dodd-Frank Section 1502 |
|---------|----------------------|------------------------|
| **Covered Entities** | EU importers of minerals/metals (upstream focus) | SEC-reporting manufacturers/contractors (downstream focus) |
| **Geographic Scope** | Global conflict-affected and high-risk areas (CAHRAs) | DRC and 9 adjoining countries only |
| **Volume Thresholds** | Annual import volumes below specified thresholds exempt | No volume threshold; all SEC filers manufacturing with 3TG covered |
| **Due Diligence Approach** | Mandatory conformity with OECD Guidance 5-step framework | Due diligence conforming to nationally/internationally recognized framework |
| **Third-Party Audit** | Mandatory for smelters/refiners and large importers | Recommended but not enforced (post-NAM v. SEC) |
| **Enforcement** | Member state competent authorities with inspection powers | SEC (disclosure-based; no specific enforcement actions) |

### OECD Five-Step Due Diligence Framework

The Regulation mandates compliance with the OECD Due Diligence Guidance five-step framework:

**Step 1:** Establish strong company management systems (supply chain policy, internal management structure, traceability system, supplier engagement mechanism, grievance mechanism)

**Step 2:** Identify and assess risks in the supply chain (map the supply chain, identify red flags, assess risks against OECD Annex II adverse impacts including armed group financing, forced labor, serious human rights abuses)

**Step 3:** Design and implement a strategy to respond to identified risks (report findings to senior management, devise risk mitigation measures, implement risk management plan, monitor implementation)

**Step 4:** Carry out independent third-party audit of supply chain due diligence (scope of audit, auditor qualifications, audit cycle)

**Step 5:** Report annually on supply chain due diligence (public reporting on policies, risks identified, and risk mitigation measures)

### Conflict-Affected and High-Risk Areas (CAHRAs)

The EU maintains a non-exhaustive, indicative list of CAHRAs, published and regularly updated by the Commission's Joint Research Centre (JRC) and the European External Action Service (EEAS). Unlike Dodd-Frank's fixed geographic scope, the CAHRA concept is dynamic, encompassing:
- Areas in armed conflict or post-conflict fragile situations
- Areas with weak or non-existent governance and widespread human rights violations
- Areas where armed groups exploit natural resources
- Areas affected by widespread violence, including gender-based violence

As of 2024, the CAHRA list includes regions across sub-Saharan Africa, Southeast Asia, Central America, and parts of the former Soviet Union.

### EU Responsible Smelter and Refiner Lists

The European Commission publishes lists of responsible smelters and refiners that comply with Regulation 2017/821 or equivalent due diligence standards (including RMAP, LBMA Good Delivery, and other recognized audit programs). Importers sourcing from listed smelters/refiners benefit from a simplified due diligence process.

### Accompanying Measures

The EU's approach includes "accompanying measures" to support responsible sourcing without creating de facto embargoes:
- Technical assistance to CAHRAs for governance and mining sector reform
- EU Mineral Governance Partnerships with producing countries (including DRC, Rwanda, Colombia, Peru)
- Support for artisanal and small-scale mining (ASM) formalization
- Capacity building for SME importers

### Extension Debates: Cobalt, Lithium, and Critical Raw Materials

The EU has debated extending conflict mineral-type due diligence to other minerals, particularly:
- **Cobalt:** Critical for battery production; significant artisanal mining in the DRC with documented child labor (addressed partly by the EU Battery Regulation, Regulation 2023/1542, which requires battery manufacturers to conduct due diligence on cobalt, lithium, nickel, and graphite supply chains)
- **Lithium:** Growing demand for electrification; environmental and indigenous rights concerns in South America's "lithium triangle"
- **Critical Raw Materials Act (Regulation 2024/1252):** Includes strategic partnerships and supply chain diversification but stops short of mandatory due diligence for all critical raw materials

### ECHR Dimensions

The ECHR has addressed resource-related human rights violations in cases such as Ilaşcu v. Moldova and Russia (2004) and decisions concerning extractive industry impacts on community rights under Article 8. The ECtHR's expanding environmental jurisprudence, particularly the KlimaSeniorinnen v. Switzerland (2024) decision establishing positive obligations for environmental protection, provides a human rights law foundation supporting conflict mineral regulation as a mechanism to prevent corporate complicity in human rights abuses.

## Comparative Analysis

### Upstream vs. Downstream Focus

The most fundamental regulatory design difference is between upstream and downstream approaches. The EU Regulation targets importers of raw minerals and metals (upstream), aiming to break the link between mineral extraction and conflict at the point of entry into European supply chains. Dodd-Frank Section 1502 targets manufacturers and product companies (downstream), requiring them to trace minerals back through their supply chains to the point of extraction. Both approaches have limitations: upstream regulation misses minerals processed outside the EU before being incorporated into products; downstream regulation creates enormous supply chain mapping challenges for complex manufactured goods with thousands of components.

### Geographic Scope

The EU's global CAHRA approach is more comprehensive than Dodd-Frank's DRC-plus-nine limitation, which fails to address conflict minerals from other regions. However, the CAHRA approach introduces uncertainty about which areas are covered and requires ongoing geographic risk assessment. South Korea's lack of dedicated legislation means geographic scope depends entirely on which foreign regulations apply to Korean companies through cross-border compliance requirements.

### Effectiveness Debate

Empirical evidence on the effectiveness of conflict minerals regulation is mixed. Studies suggest that Dodd-Frank Section 1502 increased transparency and reduced some armed group financing in eastern DRC, but also contributed to an artisanal mining crisis by creating a de facto embargo effect as risk-averse companies sourced away from the region entirely. The EU's accompanying measures approach attempts to mitigate this "unintended consequences" problem by supporting responsible sourcing from CAHRAs rather than encouraging blanket avoidance.

### Beyond 3TG: The Cobalt Challenge

The limitation of current conflict mineral regulations to 3TG is increasingly anachronistic given the surge in demand for cobalt, lithium, and nickel for the energy transition. The EU Battery Regulation's due diligence requirements for battery minerals represent the most significant extension beyond 3TG. The United States has not enacted comparable legislation for battery minerals, though the UFLPA addresses forced labor in any supply chain. This regulatory gap for non-3TG minerals is a significant area of ongoing policy development across all jurisdictions.

### Technology and Traceability

All jurisdictions are grappling with the technical challenge of mineral traceability through complex, multi-tier supply chains. Blockchain-based traceability systems, bag-and-tag programs at mine sites, analytical fingerprinting (BGR's AFP method), and consolidated industry databases (RMI's Responsible Minerals Assurance Process) are all being deployed, but no single technology provides end-to-end traceability with full certainty.
