## Summary

Vandalism -- the willful destruction, damage, or defacement of property belonging to another -- is universally criminalized but regulated through markedly different legal frameworks across jurisdictions. South Korea addresses vandalism primarily through Article 366 of its Criminal Act (property damage) and the Cultural Heritage Protection Act for offences against designated heritage sites, with penalties reaching up to five years for cultural property damage. The United States prosecutes vandalism at both federal and state levels, with 18 U.S.C. section 1361 governing destruction of federal property and individual states maintaining their own statutes under labels such as "criminal mischief," "malicious mischief," or "vandalism." Germany employs a structured approach under Sections 303-305a of the Strafgesetzbuch, notably including a 2005 "graffiti clause" (Section 303(2)) that criminalizes unauthorized alteration of property appearance even without physical damage. The ECHR does not directly criminalize vandalism but imposes positive obligations on member states under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (protection of property) and Article 8 (respect for private life) to maintain adequate criminal frameworks protecting property rights.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Vandalism and property damage in South Korea are governed primarily by Chapter XLII of the Criminal Act ("Crimes of Destruction and Damage"), with additional provisions in the Cultural Heritage Protection Act and the Punishment of Minor Offenses Act.

| Statute | Offence | Penalty |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Criminal Act, Art. 366 | Destroying, damaging, or concealing another's property, documents, or special media records, or by any other means reducing their utility | Imprisonment with prison labor for not more than 3 years or fine not exceeding 7 million won |
| Criminal Act, Art. 367 | Destruction or damage of property by occupant or custodian | Imprisonment for not more than 3 years, fine not exceeding 7 million won, or misdemeanor imprisonment |
| Criminal Act, Art. 368(1) | Special aggravated property damage (dangerous methods such as explosives or flooding) | Imprisonment for 1-10 years |
| Criminal Act, Art. 368(2) | Property damage causing danger to public safety | Imprisonment for 1-10 years |
| Criminal Act, Art. 369 | Property damage resulting from a special relationship (e.g., spouse or lineal relative) | Prosecution only upon complaint |
| Cultural Heritage Protection Act, Art. 82-3 | Writing, drawing, or carving on designated cultural heritage | Prison sentence up to 5 years or fine up to 50 million won |
| Cultural Heritage Protection Act, Art. 91(1) | Damaging or destroying designated cultural heritage | Imprisonment of not less than 3 years |
| Punishment of Minor Offenses Act | Minor property defacement (e.g., posting unauthorized notices) | Fine or administrative penalty |

### Key Principles

**Complaint Offence Status (Art. 369):** Property damage involving family members (spouses, lineal relatives, cohabitants) is classified as a complaint offence (chingojeui), meaning prosecution can only proceed upon formal complaint by the victim. This reflects Korean criminal law's deference to family harmony in minor property disputes.

**Utility Reduction Standard:** Article 366 does not require physical destruction -- it is sufficient that the property's "utility" (hyoyong) has been reduced. Korean courts have interpreted this broadly to include rendering property unusable, concealing it from its owner, or altering it in ways that impair its function, even without visible physical damage.

**Cultural Heritage Enhancement:** The Cultural Heritage Protection Act imposes dramatically harsher penalties than the general Criminal Act. While basic property damage under Article 366 carries a maximum of 3 years, damage to designated cultural heritage under Article 91(1) carries a minimum of 3 years, reflecting the irreplaceable value placed on national cultural assets.

### Landmark Cases

**Gyeongbokgung Palace Graffiti Incident (2023):** In December 2023, an 18-year-old man and a 17-year-old woman spray-painted approximately 30 meters of graffiti on Yeongchumun Gate and surrounding walls of Gyeongbokgung Palace, South Korea's main Joseon Dynasty palace and the nation's foremost national treasure. The following day, a separate individual left an additional 4-meter graffiti marking on the palace walls. The suspects were investigated under Article 82-3 of the Cultural Heritage Protection Act, which prohibits writing, drawing, or carving on designated cultural heritage. The Korea Heritage Service filed civil charges seeking compensation for restoration costs, which ultimately amounted to approximately 150 million won (roughly $115,000 USD). The case prompted nationwide debate about heritage site security and the adequacy of penalties for cultural property vandalism.[^1]

**Graffiti and Art Debate (2019):** A significant legal and cultural controversy arose in South Korea over the boundary between graffiti as vandalism and graffiti as artistic expression. The case highlighted tensions between Article 366 of the Criminal Act and constitutional protections for artistic freedom, with courts generally holding that unauthorized application of graffiti to another's property constitutes criminal damage regardless of artistic merit.[^2]

**Namdaemun Gate Vandalism (2006):** Before the devastating 2008 arson that destroyed the gate, Chae Jong-gi had been previously charged with damaging Changgyeonggung Palace in 2006. The earlier vandalism charge under the Cultural Heritage Protection Act demonstrated the escalation pattern that ultimately led to the complete destruction of National Treasure No. 1, prompting reforms in both cultural property security and repeat-offender monitoring.[^3]

## United States

### Legal Framework

Vandalism in the United States is prosecuted at both the federal and state levels, with significant variation in terminology, classification, and penalties across jurisdictions.

#### Federal Law

**18 U.S.C. section 1361 -- Destruction of Government Property:**
- Applies to willful depredation of any property of the United States or any department or agency thereof, or any property being manufactured or constructed for the United States
- Damage under $1,000: fine and up to 1 year imprisonment
- Damage of $1,000 or more: fine up to $250,000 and up to 10 years imprisonment

**36 C.F.R. section 2.31 -- Trespassing, Tampering, and Vandalism:**
- Applies specifically within National Park Service lands
- Prohibits tampering with, destroying, or vandalizing property, structures, signs, or markers
- Violations are federal misdemeanors punishable by fines and up to 6 months imprisonment

**18 U.S.C. section 1369 -- Destruction of Veterans' Memorials:**
- Specifically criminalizes willful destruction or injury to any structure, plaque, statue, or other monument on public property commemorating military service
- Fine and imprisonment up to 10 years

#### State Law

Most states classify vandalism by the dollar value of damage caused:

| State | Statute | Misdemeanor Threshold | Felony Threshold | Maximum Felony Penalty |
|-------|---------|----------------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| California | Penal Code section 594 | Damage under $400 | Damage $400 or more | Up to 3 years state prison; fine up to $50,000 |
| New York | Penal Law Art. 145 (Criminal Mischief) | 4th degree: damage to property | 1st degree: damage by explosive (Class B felony) | Up to 25 years |
| Texas | Penal Code section 28.03 (Criminal Mischief) | Damage under $2,500 | Damage $2,500 or more | 2-10 years (2nd degree felony for $300,000+) |
| Florida | section 806.13 (Criminal Mischief) | Damage under $1,000 | Damage $1,000 or more | Up to 5 years |
| Tennessee | Code section 39-14-408 | Damage under $1,000 | Damage $1,000 or more | Up to 12 years (Class B felony for $250,000+) |

**California Penal Code section 594 -- Notable Provisions:**
- "Wobbler" offence: damages of $400 or more can be charged as either misdemeanor or felony
- Mandatory graffiti cleanup: courts must order defendants convicted of graffiti to clean, repair, or replace damaged property
- Parental liability: parents or guardians of minor vandals may be ordered to keep property free of graffiti for up to one year
- Prior conviction enhancement: second or subsequent vandalism convictions allow increased fines up to $50,000

**New York Penal Law Article 145 -- Criminal Mischief Degrees:**
- Fourth degree (Class A misdemeanor): intentional damage to another's property
- Third degree (Class E felony): damage exceeding $250
- Second degree (Class D felony): damage exceeding $1,500
- First degree (Class B felony): damage by means of an explosive

### Key Principles

**Malicious Intent Requirement:** Most U.S. vandalism statutes require proof of willful or malicious intent. Accidental damage, even if negligent, is typically addressed through civil liability rather than criminal prosecution. Some states, such as New York, also recognize reckless damage as criminal mischief.

**Restitution as Mandatory Component:** Beyond fines and imprisonment, U.S. courts routinely order restitution -- requiring defendants to pay for the repair or replacement of damaged property. This compensatory element supplements the punitive function of criminal penalties.

**Community Service and Graffiti Abatement:** Many jurisdictions, particularly California, mandate community service specifically related to graffiti removal as part of sentencing. This restorative justice approach aims to connect the punishment to the offence.

### Landmark Cases

**United States v. Wilson (D.C. Cir., 2020):** Federal prosecution under 18 U.S.C. section 1361 for vandalism of federal buildings during protests in Washington, D.C. The case tested the boundaries of First Amendment protections in the context of property destruction during political demonstrations, with courts holding that the destruction of government property is not constitutionally protected expressive conduct.[^4]

**People v. Mahoney (Cal. App., 2013):** A California appellate court upheld a felony vandalism conviction under Penal Code section 594 for graffiti tagging that caused $75,000 in damages across multiple properties. The court affirmed the aggregation of damages from a single spree for purposes of determining felony versus misdemeanor classification.[^5]

**In re Nicholas Y. (Cal. App. 4th Dist., 2000):** The court addressed juvenile vandalism, holding that a minor's spray-painting of graffiti on public and private property constituted vandalism under section 594 regardless of whether the minor considered the markings "art." The decision reinforced that aesthetic intent does not negate the malicious damage element.[^6]

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German vandalism law is codified in Chapter Twenty-Seven of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), covering "Property Damage" (Sachbeschaedigung) under Sections 303 through 305a. The framework was significantly expanded in 2005 to address graffiti and unauthorized appearance modification.

#### Section 303 -- Property Damage (Sachbeschaedigung)

**(1)** Whoever unlawfully damages or destroys property belonging to another shall be punished with imprisonment for not more than two years or a fine.

**(2)** Whoever unauthorizedly alters the appearance of property belonging to another in a manner that is not merely insignificant and not merely temporary shall be punished likewise. This subsection, introduced by the 39th Criminal Law Amendment Act of 2005, was specifically designed to address graffiti -- it does not require physical damage to the substance of the property, only an unauthorized, non-trivial, non-temporary alteration of appearance (e.g., spray paint, permanent markers, adhesive stickers).

**(3)** Attempt is punishable.

#### Section 303a -- Alteration of Data (Datenveraenderung)

Whoever unlawfully deletes, suppresses, renders unusable, or alters data shall be punished with imprisonment for not more than two years or a fine. This provision extends vandalism concepts to digital property.

#### Section 303b -- Computer Sabotage (Computersabotage)

Data processing operations of substantial importance to another are protected from interference through deletion, suppression, or alteration of data or destruction of data processing equipment. Penalties range up to 3 years (basic) or up to 5 years (essential infrastructure).

#### Section 304 -- Damage to Public Property (Gemeinschaedliche Sachbeschaedigung)

Whoever unlawfully damages or destroys the following categories of property shall be punished with imprisonment for not more than three years or a fine:

1. Objects of religious veneration of a domestic religious community or items devoted to religious services
2. Gravestones, public monuments, or nature monuments
3. Objects of art, science, or commerce placed in public collections or publicly displayed
4. Objects serving public utility or the beautification of public ways, places, or facilities

**(2)** Unauthorized alteration of appearance (same as 303(2)) applies to Section 304 objects as well.

#### Section 305 -- Destruction of Buildings (Zerstoerung von Bauwerken)

Whoever unlawfully wholly or partially destroys a building belonging to another shall be punished with imprisonment for not more than five years or a fine. This elevated provision reflects the heightened social value and economic significance of buildings.

#### Section 305a -- Destruction of Important Means of Work (Zerstoerung wichtiger Arbeitsmittel)

Whoever unlawfully wholly or partially destroys technical means of work of significant value that are of substantial importance to another's economic enterprise or undertaking shall face imprisonment for up to five years or a fine.

### Key Principles

**The Graffiti Clause (Section 303(2)):** Introduced effective September 8, 2005, through the 39th Criminal Law Amendment Act (Neununddreissigstes Strafrechtsaenderungsgesetz), this provision was a direct legislative response to the widespread graffiti problem in German cities. It criminalizes unauthorized changes to a property's appearance even when no physical substance damage occurs. For liability to attach, the alteration must be (a) unauthorized, (b) not merely insignificant, and (c) not merely temporary. Chalk drawings that wash away with rain are excluded; spray paint, etching, and permanent adhesive applications are covered.

**Complaint Requirement (Antragsdelikt):** Basic property damage under Section 303 is generally prosecuted only upon private complaint (Strafantrag) by the victim, unless the prosecution authority deems prosecution to be in the special public interest (besonderes oeffentliches Interesse). The complaint must be filed within three months of the victim learning of the offence and the identity of the offender. Section 304 offences against public property are prosecuted ex officio.

**Attempt Liability:** Section 303(3) explicitly makes attempted property damage punishable -- an offender who begins to deface a wall but is interrupted before completing the act can still be held criminally liable.

### Landmark Cases

**BVerfG, Graffiti Legislation Decision (2005-2006):** The constitutionality of the newly enacted Section 303(2) was challenged on the grounds that it violated the principle of legal certainty (Bestimmtheitsgrundsatz) under Article 103(2) of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz). Critics argued that terms such as "not merely insignificant" and "not merely temporary" were impermissibly vague. The legislation survived constitutional scrutiny, with courts holding that the terms were sufficiently defined by reference to established jurisprudence on property damage.[^7]

**KG Berlin, Decision of 28 April 2014 ((4) 161 Ss 47/14):** The Kammergericht (Berlin Court of Appeal) addressed graffiti on public transit vehicles under Section 304 StGB. The court held that spray-painting public transit vehicles constitutes damage to objects serving public utility, as the cleaning requirements can necessitate removal of the vehicle from service beyond normal maintenance schedules, thereby impairing its public function.[^8]

**BGH, Judgment on Monument Defacement (Section 304):** The Federal Court of Justice addressed the spray-painting of a public monument, holding that even partial coating of a monument's surface with graffiti constitutes an unauthorized alteration under Section 304(2), with the court emphasizing that the provision protects the communal interest in preserving the aesthetic integrity and memorial function of public monuments.[^9]

## ECHR & EU Framework

### European Convention on Human Rights

The ECHR does not contain a specific provision criminalizing vandalism. The Court addresses property destruction through positive obligations derived from substantive Convention rights.

#### Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 -- Protection of Property

The foundational property protection provision in the Convention system, Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 guarantees the right to peaceful enjoyment of possessions. The European Court of Human Rights has developed jurisprudence establishing that states bear positive obligations to protect property from criminal destruction:

1. **Legislative Obligation:** States must maintain an adequate legal framework criminalizing property damage and providing effective deterrence.
2. **Enforcement Obligation:** States must take reasonable steps to enforce property protection laws, including effective investigation and prosecution of vandalism offences.
3. **Environmental Context:** The Court has recognized that severe environmental degradation can constitute a violation of property rights when it significantly impairs a property's value, usability, or the owner's control.

#### Article 8 -- Right to Respect for Private and Family Life

Vandalism targeting homes engages Article 8 obligations. States must take reasonable measures to protect individuals' homes from criminal damage, including maintaining effective law enforcement responses to vandalism.

#### Article 13 -- Right to an Effective Remedy

When property is damaged through criminal conduct, Article 13 requires that states provide victims with an effective domestic remedy, encompassing both criminal prosecution mechanisms and civil compensation procedures.

### EU Framework

#### Council Framework Decision 2002/584/JHA (European Arrest Warrant)

Property damage offences, when punishable by a custodial sentence of at least three years in the issuing member state, can trigger the European Arrest Warrant mechanism. Under Article 2(2), the list of offence categories that do not require verification of double criminality includes "damage to property" (Sachbeschaedigung/dommages) -- enabling cross-border surrender of vandalism suspects without the executing state needing to verify that the specific conduct constitutes vandalism under its own law.

#### Directive 2012/29/EU (Victims' Rights Directive)

This directive establishes minimum standards for the rights, support, and protection of victims of crime across all EU member states. Property crime victims, including victims of vandalism, are guaranteed:

- The right to information about their case
- The right to interpretation and translation
- The right to be heard in criminal proceedings
- The right to compensation through the criminal process
- Protection from secondary victimization

#### EU Directive 2024/1260 (Asset Recovery and Confiscation)

Replacing several earlier instruments, this directive (to be transposed by November 2026) establishes minimum rules on tracing, identification, freezing, confiscation, and management of criminal property, applicable to proceeds from property crimes including serious or organized vandalism.

#### Organised Property Crime -- European Parliament Study (2020)

A 2020 European Parliament study on "Organised Property Crime in the EU" documented the cross-border dimension of property crimes and recommended enhanced cooperation mechanisms, information sharing, and harmonized minimum penalties. While primarily focused on burglary and theft, the study's recommendations extend to organized vandalism affecting multiple member states.

### Landmark Cases

**Oneryildiz v. Turkey, App. No. 48939/99 (Grand Chamber, 2004):** Though arising from an explosion rather than deliberate vandalism, this Grand Chamber judgment established foundational principles on state obligations to protect property. The Court held that Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 imposes a positive obligation on states to take appropriate steps to safeguard property, including through preventive measures and regulatory frameworks. The Court found Turkey violated Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 by failing to protect the applicant's dwelling from a foreseeable methane explosion at a municipal waste site. The decision established that illegal constructions can constitute "possessions" protected by the Convention.[^10]

**Blecic v. Croatia, App. No. 59532/00 (Grand Chamber, 2006):** The Grand Chamber addressed the scope of "possessions" under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1, confirming that the concept encompasses a wide range of property interests beyond formal legal ownership. The decision reinforced that states must provide adequate protection for property interests, including protection from criminal interference.[^11]

**Hamer v. Belgium, App. No. 21861/03 (2007):** The Court examined the proportionality of state measures to enforce building and environmental regulations that resulted in demolition of the applicant's property. While upholding the state's right to regulate property use, the Court emphasized that any interference with property must be proportionate and accompanied by adequate procedural safeguards. The case is relevant to vandalism jurisprudence because it clarifies the balance between state property regulation powers and individual property rights.[^12]

**Farmanyan and Others v. Armenia, App. No. 68699/14 (2024):** In this recent judgment, the Court examined state responsibility for property destruction during civil unrest, reinforcing the obligation of Convention states to protect private property from criminal damage by third parties and to conduct effective investigations when such damage occurs.[^13]

## Comparative Analysis

### Structural Approaches

The four jurisdictions reveal fundamentally different approaches to structuring vandalism offences:

- **South Korea** employs a dual-track system: general property damage under the Criminal Act (Art. 366) is treated as a relatively minor offence, while cultural heritage vandalism under the Cultural Heritage Protection Act triggers dramatically enhanced penalties, reflecting the nation's prioritization of historical preservation.
- **Germany** uses a property-function classification: basic property damage (Section 303), property serving public functions (Section 304), and buildings (Section 305) receive progressively higher penalties. The 2005 graffiti amendment (Section 303(2)) represents the most innovative legislative response among the four systems, criminalizing appearance alteration without physical damage.
- **The United States** operates a damage-threshold model at the state level, with the dollar value of damage determining misdemeanor versus felony classification. Federal jurisdiction is triggered only when government property or specially protected sites (veterans' memorials, national parks) are involved.
- **The ECHR/EU framework** functions as a meta-regulatory system, setting minimum standards for victim protection and cross-border cooperation without directly defining vandalism offences, while the ECtHR's positive obligations jurisprudence effectively imposes floor requirements on domestic criminal property protection regimes.

### Notable Distinctions

**Graffiti-Specific Legislation:** Germany stands alone in having enacted a specific statutory provision (Section 303(2)) targeting unauthorized appearance alteration without physical substance damage. South Korea and the United States address graffiti through general vandalism provisions, while California has developed the most detailed graffiti-specific sentencing provisions (mandatory cleanup, parental liability, enhanced fines for repeat offenders).

**Cultural Heritage Protection:** South Korea maintains the most severe penalties for cultural property vandalism among the compared jurisdictions, with a minimum of 3 years imprisonment for damaging designated cultural heritage under Article 91(1) of the Cultural Heritage Protection Act. This reflects both the concentration of irreplaceable historical sites and the cultural importance placed on national heritage preservation.

**Complaint Requirement:** Both South Korea (Art. 369, for family-related damage) and Germany (Section 303, as a general rule) classify basic property damage as a complaint offence requiring the victim to initiate prosecution. The United States and the ECHR framework generally treat vandalism as a public offence prosecutable without victim complaint.

**Restitution and Restorative Justice:** The United States places the greatest emphasis on restitution -- mandatory repair, replacement, or cleanup by the offender -- as a sentencing component. Germany and South Korea also permit restitution orders but do not emphasize them as heavily in statutory text.

**Digital Vandalism:** Germany's framework is the most developed regarding digital property destruction, with Section 303a (data alteration) and Section 303b (computer sabotage) extending vandalism concepts to digital assets. South Korea's Article 366 covers "special media records, such as electromagnetic records," providing some digital coverage. U.S. digital vandalism is addressed primarily through the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (18 U.S.C. section 1030) rather than traditional vandalism statutes.
