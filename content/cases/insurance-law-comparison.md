## Summary

Insurance contract law governs one of the world's most critical financial services sectors, with global insurance premiums exceeding $7 trillion annually. The legal frameworks governing the formation, interpretation, and performance of insurance contracts, the duties of disclosure, the doctrine of insurable interest, subrogation rights, and regulatory oversight vary significantly across jurisdictions, reflecting different legal traditions and policy choices regarding policyholder protection. South Korea regulates insurance through the Commercial Act (상법) Part IV on Insurance (보험편, Articles 638-739), the Insurance Business Act (보험업법), and supervision by the Financial Services Commission (금융위원회) and Financial Supervisory Service (금융감독원). The United States governs insurance primarily through state law, with each of the 50 states maintaining its own insurance code, regulatory department, and judicial doctrines, though federal law applies to specific areas including the McCarran-Ferguson Act (15 U.S.C. Sections 1011-1015) and the Affordable Care Act. Germany regulates insurance contracts through the Insurance Contract Act (Versicherungsvertragsgesetz, VVG), comprehensively reformed in 2008, and insurance supervision through the Insurance Supervision Act (Versicherungsaufsichtsgesetz, VAG) implementing the EU Solvency II framework. At the EU and ECHR level, the Solvency II Directive (2009/138/EC) establishes the prudential framework for insurance undertakings, while the Insurance Distribution Directive (2016/97/EU) regulates insurance intermediation, and the ECHR has addressed insurance-related rights under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 and Article 6.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's insurance market is among the largest in Asia, with a high insurance penetration rate reflecting cultural emphasis on savings-oriented life insurance products and comprehensive health insurance supplementation.

### Commercial Act Insurance Provisions (상법 보험편)

The Commercial Act (상법) Part IV (Articles 638-739) provides the foundational contract law framework for insurance in Korea, divided into chapters on general provisions, property insurance (손해보험), and personal insurance (인보험, including life insurance and accident/health insurance).

**Article 638 (Definition):** An insurance contract is defined as a contract whereby one party (the insurer) agrees to compensate the other party (the policyholder or insured) for loss caused by a specified contingency, in exchange for premium payment.

**Article 651 (Duty of Disclosure):** The policyholder and the insured must disclose material facts known to them at the time of contract formation. If there is a breach of the duty of disclosure due to fraud or gross negligence, the insurer may rescind the contract within one month of learning of the breach and within three years of contract inception.

**Article 655 (Insurer's Duty of Explanation):** The insurer must explain the important contents of the insurance contract to the policyholder. Failure to explain excludes the insurer from relying on terms unfavorable to the policyholder. This provision, introduced to address information asymmetry in insurance sales, has generated extensive litigation.

**Article 659 (Moral Hazard Exclusion):** Insurance contracts covering intentional acts of the insured are void. The insurer bears the burden of proving intentional causation of loss.

**Article 682 (Subrogation):** Upon payment of insurance proceeds for property insurance, the insurer is subrogated to the insured's rights against third parties responsible for the loss, to the extent of the payment. The insured must cooperate with the insurer's exercise of subrogation rights and must not prejudice those rights.

### Insurance Business Act (보험업법)

The Insurance Business Act provides the regulatory framework for insurance companies, including licensing requirements, capital adequacy, governance, and market conduct. The Financial Services Commission (금융위원회) and Financial Supervisory Service (금융감독원) exercise supervisory authority.

**Solvency Regime:** Korea transitioned to a risk-based capital (RBC) regime in 2011, later moving toward the Korean Insurance Capital Standard (K-ICS), aligned with the International Association of Insurance Supervisors (IAIS) Insurance Capital Standard (ICS). K-ICS became fully effective in 2023, requiring insurers to value liabilities on a market-consistent basis.

### Key Case Law

**Supreme Court Decision 2017Da229462 (2019):** The Court addressed the scope of the insurer's duty of explanation under Article 655, holding that the duty extends to all terms that are objectively important and would influence a reasonable policyholder's decision, not merely terms that the particular policyholder subjectively considered important.

**Supreme Court Decision 2015Da243347 (2018):** The Court clarified the burden of proof in insurance fraud cases, holding that while the insurer bears the ultimate burden of proving intentional causation under Article 659, circumstantial evidence including the timing, circumstances, and financial motivation of the insured may establish a prima facie case shifting the evidential burden.

**Supreme Court Decision 2019Da292530 (2021):** The Court addressed subrogation rights in automobile insurance, holding that the insurer's subrogation right under Article 682 includes the right to pursue claims under the Guarantee of Automobile Accident Compensation Act (자동차손해배상 보장법) against uninsured drivers.

### Consumer Protection

The Korea Life Insurance Association (생명보험협회) and the General Insurance Association of Korea (손해보험협회) operate dispute resolution mechanisms. The Financial Supervisory Service also operates the Financial Dispute Mediation Committee (금융분쟁조정위원회), which handles insurance disputes. The Korea Insurance Guarantee Corporation (under the Korea Deposit Insurance Corporation, 예금보험공사) provides policyholder protection in the event of insurer insolvency.

## United States

### Legal Framework

US insurance law is uniquely characterized by state-based regulation, with the McCarran-Ferguson Act (1945) affirming state primacy in insurance regulation. Each state maintains its own insurance code, insurance department, and body of judicial precedent, creating a fragmented but deeply developed legal landscape.

### Duty of Disclosure and Representations

The duty of disclosure in US insurance law varies significantly by state. Traditional common law imposed a strict duty of utmost good faith (uberrima fides), requiring disclosure of all material facts. Modern statutes in most states have moved toward a representations-based framework, where the insurer asks specific questions on the application and the applicant's answers must be substantially true.

**Representations vs. Warranties:** The distinction between representations (statements that need only be substantially true) and warranties (statements that must be literally true) has been substantially eroded in most states by statute. Many states have enacted anti-technical statutes providing that misrepresentations are grounds for avoidance only if they are material (would have affected the insurer's decision to issue the policy or the terms thereof) and were made with intent to deceive or if the fact misrepresented actually contributed to the loss.

### Insurable Interest

**Property Insurance:** An insurable interest must exist at the time of loss. Any lawful and substantial economic interest in the preservation of the insured property suffices. Absent insurable interest, the contract is void as a wager.

**Life Insurance:** Insurable interest must exist at the inception of the policy but need not exist at the time of death. Every person has an insurable interest in their own life without limitation. Close family relationships (spouse, parent-child) create presumptive insurable interest. Commercial relationships (creditor-debtor, business partners) may create insurable interest to the extent of the economic value.

**Stranger-Originated Life Insurance (STOLI):** Courts and legislatures have addressed STOLI arrangements, where investors procure life insurance on elderly individuals with no pre-existing insurable interest, structured to circumvent insurable interest requirements through initial policyholder ownership followed by immediate transfer. Most states now treat STOLI arrangements as void ab initio.

### Subrogation

US insurance subrogation operates through both equitable subrogation (arising by operation of law upon payment of an insured loss) and conventional subrogation (arising from contractual provisions in the insurance policy).

**Made Whole Doctrine:** Under the equitable made whole doctrine, recognized in most states, an insurer's subrogation right does not attach until the insured has been fully compensated for the loss. Some states permit contractual override of the made whole doctrine. The anti-subrogation rule prevents an insurer from pursuing subrogation against its own insured.

### State Regulation and NAIC

The National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC) promotes regulatory uniformity through model laws and regulations adopted (with modifications) by individual states. Key NAIC model laws include the Unfair Trade Practices Act, the Life and Health Insurance Guaranty Association Model Act, and the Insurance Holding Company System Regulatory Act.

**State Guaranty Associations:** All states have established insurance guaranty associations that protect policyholders when an insurer becomes insolvent. Guaranty associations are funded by post-insolvency assessments on remaining solvent insurers and typically provide coverage up to $300,000-$500,000 per claim.

### Key Case Law

**Garamendi v. Executive Life Insurance Co. of New York, 17 Cal. App. 4th 504 (1993):** Addressed the obligations of guaranty associations in large-scale insurer insolvencies.

**UNUM Life Insurance Co. of America v. Ward, 526 U.S. 358 (1999):** The Supreme Court addressed the interaction between ERISA preemption and state insurance law, holding that California's notice-prejudice rule (requiring an insurer to show prejudice from late notice before denying a claim) was a law regulating insurance saved from ERISA preemption.

**Montrose Chemical Corp. v. Admiral Insurance Co., 10 Cal. 4th 645 (1995):** The California Supreme Court established the "continuous trigger" theory for long-tail environmental and toxic tort claims, holding that all policies on the risk during the period of continuous damage are triggered.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's insurance contract law was comprehensively modernized by the VVG reform of 2008, replacing the century-old 1908 VVG with a modern, policyholder-protective statute.

### Versicherungsvertragsgesetz (VVG)

The VVG, effective January 1, 2008, governs all aspects of insurance contract law in Germany. The reform was motivated by the need to modernize outdated provisions, strengthen policyholder protection, and address EU requirements.

**Section 19 (Pre-Contractual Duty of Disclosure -- Vorvertragliche Anzeigepflicht):** The policyholder must truthfully answer the insurer's questions in text form regarding risk-relevant circumstances before contract conclusion. The insurer's right to ask questions limits the duty of disclosure to matters the insurer specifically inquires about, a significant departure from the former "spontaneous disclosure" requirement.

**Section 19(2)-(4) (Proportionality Remedy -- Quotenregelung):** If the policyholder breaches the duty of disclosure through simple (non-fraudulent) negligence, the insurer may not rescind the contract but must pay benefits reduced in proportion to the ratio of the agreed premium to the premium that would have been charged had the insurer known the true facts. This proportionality rule was a landmark innovation of the 2008 reform, replacing the former "all-or-nothing" approach.

**Example:** If the policyholder negligently failed to disclose a medical condition, and the insurer would have charged a 50% higher premium had it known, the insurer must pay 67% of the claim (100/150 = 66.7%). Only in cases of intentional or fraudulently negligent (arglistig) misrepresentation may the insurer fully rescind.

**Section 28 (Breach of Obligations After Contract Formation):** A parallel proportionality rule applies to breaches of policy conditions (Obliegenheiten) occurring after contract inception. The insurer's right to deny coverage is proportional to the policyholder's degree of fault.

**Section 86 (Subrogation -- Übergang von Ersatzansprüchen):** Claims of the policyholder against third parties pass to the insurer upon payment, to the extent of the payment. The insurer may not exercise subrogation rights to the detriment of the policyholder (the policyholder's claims take priority). Subrogation against family members living in the same household (Familienprivileg) is excluded unless the family member acted intentionally.

### Insurable Interest and Indemnity Principle

German insurance law adheres strictly to the indemnity principle (Bereicherungsverbot) for property insurance (Schadensversicherung): the insured may not profit from a loss. VVG Section 55 provides that the insurer is obligated to compensate the insured for the loss actually suffered, up to the insured amount. Overinsurance (Überversicherung, Section 74) and double insurance (Doppelversicherung, Section 78) are addressed through mandatory provisions preventing unjust enrichment.

For life insurance (Lebensversicherung) and personal accident insurance (Unfallversicherung), the sum insured is fixed (Summenversicherung), and the indemnity principle does not apply.

### Regulatory Framework (VAG and Solvency II)

The Insurance Supervision Act (Versicherungsaufsichtsgesetz, VAG) implements the Solvency II Directive (2009/138/EC) in German law. The Bundesanstalt für Finanzdienstleistungsaufsicht (BaFin) exercises prudential supervision, including authorization, ongoing solvency monitoring, governance requirements, and own risk and solvency assessment (ORSA).

**Solvency II Three-Pillar System:** Pillar 1 (quantitative requirements: Solvency Capital Requirement (SCR) and Minimum Capital Requirement (MCR)); Pillar 2 (qualitative requirements: governance, risk management, ORSA); Pillar 3 (reporting and disclosure requirements).

### Key Case Law

**BGH, IV ZR 273/14 (2016):** The BGH clarified the scope of the proportionality remedy under VVG Section 19, holding that the insurer bears the burden of proving the premium that would have been charged had the risk been correctly disclosed.

**BGH, IV ZR 9/21 (2022):** The Court addressed the enforceability of insurance exclusion clauses for pandemic-related business interruption losses during COVID-19, interpreting standard infectious disease exclusion clauses narrowly in favor of policyholders under the transparency and surprise clause controls of BGB Sections 305c and 307.

**BGH, IV ZR 421/18 (2020):** The Court held that an insurer's internal claims handling guidelines do not create enforceable rights for policyholders, but the insurer's duty of good faith (Treu und Glauben) requires consistent treatment of comparable claims.

## ECHR & EU

### Solvency II Directive (2009/138/EC)

The Solvency II Directive, effective January 1, 2016, establishes a comprehensive EU-wide prudential framework for insurance and reinsurance undertakings, replacing the previous Solvency I regime with a risk-based capital system.

**Scope:** The directive applies to all insurance and reinsurance undertakings established in the EU with gross premium income exceeding EUR 5 million or gross technical provisions exceeding EUR 25 million. Smaller undertakings may be subject to national regimes.

**Single Passport:** Insurance undertakings authorized in one member state may operate throughout the EU under the single passport (freedom of establishment and freedom to provide services), supervised primarily by the home member state supervisor.

**EIOPA:** The European Insurance and Occupational Pensions Authority (EIOPA) promotes supervisory convergence, develops regulatory technical standards, and provides opinions and recommendations. EIOPA conducts EU-wide insurance stress tests and participates in the European System of Financial Supervision.

### Solvency II Review (2024)

The 2024 Solvency II review (Directive 2024/XXX amending 2009/138/EC) introduced amendments including: a macroprudential framework for insurance; enhanced proportionality for smaller insurers; sustainability considerations in risk assessment; and Insurance Recovery and Resolution Directive (IRRD) establishing resolution frameworks for failing insurers.

### Insurance Distribution Directive (IDD, 2016/97/EU)

The IDD, effective October 1, 2018, regulates the distribution of insurance products across the EU, establishing conduct of business rules for insurance distributors (agents, brokers, and direct sales by insurers).

**Product Oversight and Governance (POG):** Insurers must maintain product approval processes ensuring that products are designed to meet the needs of an identified target market, and distributors must ensure products are distributed to appropriate customers.

**Insurance-Based Investment Products (IBIPs):** Enhanced duties apply to the distribution of IBIPs, including suitability assessments, cost disclosure, and transparency requirements modeled on MiFID II.

### Towards EU Insurance Contract Law Harmonization

The Principles of European Insurance Contract Law (PEICL), developed by the Project Group on a Restatement of European Insurance Contract Law, provides an academic model for harmonized insurance contract law. While not binding, the PEICL has influenced national reforms and may serve as the basis for future EU harmonization.

**European Commission Initiatives:** The Commission has periodically explored harmonization of insurance contract law to support cross-border insurance provision, most recently in the context of the Capital Markets Union action plan. The 2024 EU retail investment strategy indirectly addresses insurance product regulation through enhanced disclosure and value-for-money requirements.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Pressos Compania Naviera S.A. v. Belgium (Application No. 17849/91, Judgment of November 20, 1995):** The Court held that retrospective legislation extinguishing existing insurance-related tort claims violated Article 1 of Protocol No. 1. The case arose from Belgian legislation retroactively eliminating the liability of the Belgian state pilotage service for shipping accidents, effectively destroying policyholders' and insurers' subrogation claims.

**Stec and Others v. United Kingdom (Application Nos. 65731/01 and 65900/01, Grand Chamber, April 12, 2006):** The Court addressed the relationship between social insurance contributions and benefit entitlements, holding that where a state establishes a contributory social insurance scheme, the entitlements arising from such contributions constitute "possessions" within Article 1 of Protocol No. 1.

**Aon Consulting Czech Republic v. Czech Republic (Application No. 21776/08, Decision of November 20, 2012):** The Court addressed the state's regulatory authority over insurance intermediation, holding that regulatory requirements for insurance brokers do not violate Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 when they serve legitimate public interest objectives and are proportionate.

## Comparative Analysis

### Duty of Disclosure Reform

The treatment of pre-contractual disclosure reveals a clear trend toward policyholder protection. Germany's 2008 VVG reform introduced the proportionality remedy, replacing the all-or-nothing approach. The UK Insurance Act 2015 imposed a duty of "fair presentation" replacing the strict uberrima fides standard. South Korea and most US states retain more traditional frameworks but have enacted statutory modifications softening the harshness of outright rescission. The trend across all jurisdictions is away from strict insurer-favoring disclosure duties toward more balanced approaches that account for the information asymmetry in insurance markets.

### Subrogation and the Family Privilege

All jurisdictions recognize the insurer's subrogation right, but the scope and limitations differ. Germany's Familienprivileg (exclusion of subrogation against household family members) reflects social policy considerations absent in US and Korean law. The US made whole doctrine provides additional policyholder protection by deferring subrogation until the insured is fully compensated. South Korea's statutory subrogation framework most closely resembles the German model.

### Regulatory Architecture

The most significant structural difference is the US state-based regulatory system versus the EU's single-market approach. The EU's Solvency II framework creates harmonized prudential standards and enables cross-border operation through the single passport. The US system fragments regulation across 50+ jurisdictions, though NAIC model laws and federal-state cooperation promote some uniformity. South Korea's centralized FSC/FSS supervision resembles the EU single-regulator model at the national level, with K-ICS aligned with international solvency standards.

### Policyholder Protection in Insolvency

All jurisdictions provide safety net mechanisms for policyholder protection when insurers fail. The US state guaranty association system, Germany's Protektor and Medicator entities, and South Korea's deposit insurance system represent different approaches. The EU has not yet harmonized policyholder protection schemes, creating potential gaps in cross-border situations.
