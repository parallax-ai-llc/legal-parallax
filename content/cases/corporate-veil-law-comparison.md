## Summary

Piercing the corporate veil -- the judicial doctrine permitting courts to disregard the separate legal personality of a corporation and hold its shareholders, parent companies, or other controlling persons personally liable for corporate obligations -- represents one of the most frequently litigated yet doctrinally uncertain areas of corporate law, as courts in every jurisdiction struggle to balance the foundational principle of limited liability against the need to prevent abuse of the corporate form. South Korea recognizes veil piercing (법인격 부인론) as a judicial doctrine applied under the good faith principle (신의성실의 원칙) of Civil Act Article 2, with the Supreme Court establishing in landmark decisions that the corporate form may be disregarded when it is used as a mere instrument (도구) for the controlling shareholder's personal affairs or to achieve an unjust purpose, a doctrine of particular importance in the chaebol context where complex corporate structures may be used to shield controlling families from liability. The United States is the global leader in veil-piercing litigation, with thousands of reported decisions applying various multi-factor tests -- including the alter ego doctrine, the instrumentality rule, and the single enterprise theory -- with studies showing veil piercing succeeds in approximately 33-50% of cases depending on the jurisdiction and entity type, and significant variation between state approaches as evidenced by the contrast between California's two-prong test and New York's totality-of-circumstances approach. Germany applies the doctrine of Durchgriffshaftung (piercing liability) sparingly, recognizing only narrowly defined categories -- Vermögensvermischung (commingling of assets), existenzvernichtender Eingriff (existence-destroying interference, as reformulated in the Trihotel decision of 2007), and Unterkapitalisierung (undercapitalization) -- while primarily protecting creditors through capital maintenance rules and managing director personal liability provisions. At the EU level, the CJEU has addressed veil piercing in the context of competition law (holding parent companies liable for subsidiaries' antitrust violations through the "single economic unit" doctrine established in Akzo Nobel v. Commission (2009)), while the ECHR has recognized the corporate veil as relevant to the determination of Convention rights under Articles 6 and 1 of Protocol 1.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's veil-piercing doctrine (법인격 부인론) is a judicially developed principle grounded in the good faith (신의성실) and abuse of rights (권리남용 금지) provisions of Civil Act Article 2.

### Supreme Court Framework

The Supreme Court has established two principal grounds for veil piercing:

**Instrumentality Ground (형해화 사유):** The corporate form may be pierced when the corporation is a mere instrument (형해) of the controlling shareholder, lacking any independent existence separate from its owner. Factors include: the corporation has no independent decision-making process, the controlling shareholder treats corporate assets as personal property, corporate formalities are not observed, and the corporation exists solely to serve the controller's purposes.

**Abuse of Purpose Ground (남용 사유):** The corporate form may be pierced when it is used to achieve an unjust purpose (부당한 목적), such as evading legal obligations, defrauding creditors, or circumventing contractual restrictions. This ground does not require that the corporation lacks independent existence but focuses on the abusive use of the corporate form in the specific transaction.

### Landmark Case: Supreme Court Decision 2001Da24655 (2004)

The Supreme Court established the foundational framework for veil piercing, holding that when a person completely controls a corporation and uses it as a mere instrument for personal affairs, or when the corporate form is abused to achieve unjust results, the corporation's separate legal personality may be disregarded and obligations may be imposed directly on the controlling person. The Court emphasized that veil piercing should be applied cautiously and only in exceptional cases.

### Chaebol Context

Veil piercing in the chaebol context presents unique challenges. Korean courts have been asked to pierce the veil between chaebol group companies to hold the controlling family or parent company liable for subsidiary obligations. In Supreme Court Decision 2006Da24438 (2008), the Court applied veil piercing to hold a controlling shareholder personally liable for a subsidiary's labor obligations, finding that the shareholder had used the subsidiary as a mere tool to avoid labor law obligations.

### Reverse Veil Piercing

Korean courts have recognized reverse veil piercing in limited circumstances -- allowing a creditor of the controlling shareholder to reach corporate assets, or treating the shareholder's personal liabilities as corporate liabilities. The Supreme Court has applied this doctrine sparingly, primarily in cases where the corporation was formed solely to shield the shareholder's personal assets from creditors.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has the world's most extensive body of veil-piercing case law, with the doctrine applied across all state jurisdictions through varying multi-factor tests.

### Alter Ego Doctrine

The alter ego doctrine, predominant in California and many other jurisdictions, requires proof of two elements: (1) such unity of interest and ownership between the corporation and its controlling person that the separate personalities no longer exist in fact (unity of interest), and (2) that adherence to the fiction of separate existence would sanction fraud or promote injustice (inequitable result). Associated Vendors, Inc. v. Oakland Meat Co., 210 Cal.App.2d 825 (1962).

Factors courts consider include:

| Factor | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| Commingling of funds | Personal and corporate funds mixed |
| Failure to observe formalities | No board meetings, minutes, or corporate records |
| Undercapitalization | Corporation has inadequate capital for its operations |
| Treatment as alter ego | Controller treats corporation as personal property |
| Identical ownership/control | Same persons as officers, directors, shareholders |
| Siphoning of funds | Controller extracts corporate assets |
| Use of corporate form to promote fraud | Corporation used to mislead creditors or evade obligations |

### Thompson Empirical Study

Professor Robert Thompson's landmark empirical study of veil-piercing cases (1991) found that courts pierced the veil in approximately 40% of cases overall, with a higher success rate in contract claims (42%) than tort claims (31%), higher success rates against closely held corporations than public companies, and higher success rates against individual shareholders than corporate parents. A follow-up study found that veil piercing succeeded in approximately 48% of LLC cases, higher than for corporations (33%).

### Single Enterprise Theory

Some jurisdictions recognize the "single enterprise" or "enterprise entity" theory, treating multiple affiliated corporations as a single entity when they operate as an integrated enterprise. This theory is particularly relevant in parent-subsidiary relationships and has been applied in cases involving construction companies, restaurant chains, and other businesses operating through multiple entities.

### Reverse Veil Piercing

Several jurisdictions recognize "reverse veil piercing" -- disregarding the corporate form to reach corporate assets to satisfy a shareholder's personal obligations, or treating corporate property as the shareholder's property for other purposes. In Postal Instant Press, Inc. v. Kaswa Corp., 162 Cal.App.4th 1510 (2008), the California Court of Appeal applied reverse veil piercing where the corporation was the alter ego of the individual debtor.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German law applies veil piercing (Durchgriffshaftung) far more restrictively than American or Korean law, consistent with the civil law tradition's emphasis on statutory predictability.

### Categories of Durchgriffshaftung

German courts and scholars recognize only narrow, defined categories of veil piercing:

**Vermögensvermischung (Asset Commingling):** When the shareholder's personal assets are so thoroughly commingled with corporate assets that separation is impossible, the separate identity of the corporation is disregarded. The BGH has held that commingling need not be intentional; inadequate bookkeeping that makes separation impossible suffices.

**Existenzvernichtender Eingriff (Existence-Destroying Interference):** Reformulated by the BGH in the Trihotel decision (BGH, July 16, 2007, II ZR 3/04), this doctrine holds that a shareholder who deliberately withdraws assets from a company, thereby destroying its ability to satisfy creditors, is personally liable under BGB Section 826 (intentional damage contra bonos mores). The Trihotel decision shifted the doctrinal basis from veil piercing to direct tort liability, making it technically an internal liability claim by the corporation against its shareholder rather than a creditor's direct claim.

**Unterkapitalisierung (Undercapitalization):** While discussed extensively in legal scholarship, German courts have rarely applied undercapitalization as an independent basis for veil piercing. The prevailing view is that inadequate capitalization alone, absent other factors, does not justify disregarding the corporate form.

### Alternative Creditor Protections

Germany's reluctance to pierce the corporate veil is compensated by robust alternative mechanisms for creditor protection:

| Mechanism | Provision |
|-----------|----------|
| Capital maintenance rules | GmbHG Sections 30-31; AktG Sections 57-58 |
| Managing director personal liability | GmbHG Section 43; AktG Section 93 |
| Insolvent trading liability | InsO Section 15a; GmbHG Section 64 |
| Shareholder loan subordination | InsO Section 39(1)(5) -- shareholder loans treated as subordinated claims |
| Konzernrecht (group law) | AktG Sections 291-328 -- parent company liability in corporate groups |

### Konzernrecht (Group Law)

Germany's unique Konzernrecht provides an alternative to veil piercing for corporate groups. The AktG distinguishes between contractual groups (Vertragskonzern, based on a formal domination agreement under Section 291) and de facto groups (faktischer Konzern). In contractual groups, the parent company is directly liable for subsidiary losses (Section 302) and creditor claims (Section 303). In de facto groups, the parent must compensate the subsidiary for any disadvantage caused by its influence (Section 311).

## ECHR & EU

### EU Competition Law: Single Economic Unit

The CJEU has developed a robust doctrine of parent company liability in competition law through the "single economic unit" concept:

**Akzo Nobel NV v. Commission (C-97/08 P, Grand Chamber Judgment of September 10, 2009):** The Court established a rebuttable presumption that a parent company exercises decisive influence over a wholly-owned subsidiary, making the parent jointly and severally liable for the subsidiary's competition law infringements. The parent can rebut this presumption only by demonstrating that the subsidiary acted autonomously in the market.

**Eni SpA v. Commission (C-508/11 P, 2013):** The Court confirmed that the presumption applies even when the parent is a holding company with no direct involvement in the subsidiary's day-to-day operations or anticompetitive conduct.

**Sumal SL v. Mercedes Benz Trucks España SL (C-882/19, Judgment of October 6, 2021):** The Court extended the single economic unit doctrine to allow victims of antitrust infringements to bring damages claims against subsidiaries of the infringing parent company, establishing a form of "downward" veil piercing in competition law.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

The ECHR has addressed the corporate veil in the context of access to justice and property rights:

**CDI Holding Aktiengesellschaft and Others v. Slovakia (Application No. 37398/97, Judgment of October 18, 2001):** The Court recognized that in determining whether shareholders may bring claims in respect of company property, the corporate veil must normally be respected, but may be disregarded in exceptional circumstances where the company is merely a vehicle for the shareholder's rights.

**Agrotexim and Others v. Greece (Application No. 14807/89, Judgment of October 24, 1995):** The Court held that shareholders generally cannot claim to be victims of measures affecting the company's property, respecting the corporate veil as a barrier to shareholder standing. The corporate form may be disregarded only when it is clearly established that it is impossible for the company to bring the claim itself.

## Comparative Analysis

### Frequency and Predictability

The four jurisdictions demonstrate markedly different approaches to the frequency and predictability of veil piercing:

**Frequent but Unpredictable (United States):** American courts pierce the veil more frequently than any other jurisdiction but apply varying multi-factor tests that produce inconsistent results. The doctrine has been criticized as "among the most confusing in corporate law."

**Infrequent and Principled (Germany):** German law applies veil piercing rarely and only within defined categories, providing greater predictability but potentially leaving some creditors unprotected.

**Moderate and Cautious (South Korea):** Korean courts apply veil piercing more readily than German courts but more cautiously than American courts, focusing on the instrumentality and abuse-of-purpose grounds.

### Alternative Mechanisms

Germany's comprehensive alternative creditor protection mechanisms (capital maintenance, managing director liability, Konzernrecht) reduce the need for veil piercing. The United States' more permissive approach to veil piercing may reflect the relative weakness of alternative creditor protections, including the absence of minimum capital requirements for most entities and limited managing director personal liability.

### Corporate Groups

The treatment of corporate groups represents the most significant structural divergence. Germany's Konzernrecht provides a statutory framework for parent-subsidiary liability that largely obviates veil piercing in the group context. The EU competition law single economic unit doctrine provides a functional equivalent in the antitrust context. American and Korean law rely primarily on general veil-piercing doctrine applied to corporate groups.
