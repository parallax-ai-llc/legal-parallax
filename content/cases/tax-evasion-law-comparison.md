## Summary

Tax evasion -- the deliberate and unlawful failure to pay taxes owed through fraud, concealment, or misrepresentation -- is a criminal offense in every major jurisdiction, yet the statutory frameworks, burden of proof, sentencing regimes, and enforcement philosophies diverge considerably. South Korea criminalizes tax evasion primarily through the Punishment of Tax Offenses Act (조세범 처벌법), with penalties scaling from fines to life imprisonment for evasion exceeding 1 billion won, and requires a formal accusation by the National Tax Service before prosecution can proceed. The United States treats tax evasion as a federal felony under 26 U.S.C. section 7201 of the Internal Revenue Code, carrying up to 5 years imprisonment and fines of up to $250,000 for individuals, with the requirement that the government prove willfulness -- a voluntary, intentional violation of a known legal duty -- beyond a reasonable doubt. Germany prosecutes tax evasion (Steuerhinterziehung) under Section 370 of the Abgabenordnung (Fiscal Code), imposing up to 5 years imprisonment for standard cases and up to 10 years for particularly serious cases involving sums exceeding 50,000 euros, while offering a voluntary disclosure mechanism (Selbstanzeige) that can grant immunity from prosecution if certain conditions are met. At the supranational level, the European Union combats tax evasion through the Anti-Tax Avoidance Directive (ATAD, Directive 2016/1164) and mandatory disclosure rules under DAC6 (Directive 2018/822), while the European Court of Human Rights has developed significant jurisprudence on the applicability of fair trial rights (Article 6 ECHR) and the ne bis in idem principle (Article 4 Protocol 7) to tax penalty proceedings.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Tax evasion in South Korea is governed by the Punishment of Tax Offenses Act (조세범 처벌법, also known as the Punishment of Tax Evaders Act), which serves as the primary criminal statute for tax-related offenses. The Act defines tax evasion as the act of refusing to pay taxes or securing a tax refund or credit by fraud or other unlawful means, including producing false documents, destroying financial books and records, concealing assets, and fabricating or concealing income, profits, acts, or transactions.

The Framework Act on National Taxes (국세기본법) and the individual tax statutes (Income Tax Act, Corporate Tax Act, Value-Added Tax Act) provide the substantive tax rules, while the Punishment of Tax Offenses Act prescribes the criminal sanctions for their violation.

### Elements of the Offense

To establish criminal tax evasion under Korean law, the prosecution must demonstrate:

1. **Tax Obligation:** A legally imposed tax obligation existed.
2. **Fraudulent or Unlawful Means (사기 기타 부정한 행위):** The taxpayer used fraud or other unlawful means to evade payment, secure an improper refund, or obtain an improper credit. Simple non-payment without active deception does not constitute criminal evasion.
3. **Intent (故意):** The taxpayer acted with the deliberate intent to evade taxes. Negligent underreporting, while subject to administrative penalties, does not give rise to criminal liability.

### Penalties

The Punishment of Tax Offenses Act establishes a graduated penalty structure:

| Offense Category | Imprisonment | Fine |
|-----------------|-------------|------|
| General tax evasion | Up to 2 years | Up to 20 million won |
| Special Taxation Act violations | Up to 3 years | Up to 30 million won |
| Counterfeit petroleum product evasion | Up to 5 years | Up to 5x the evaded tax |
| Evasion exceeding 500 million won | 3 years to life | Proportional to evaded amount |
| Evasion exceeding 1 billion won | 5 years to life | Proportional to evaded amount |

Under the Specific Economic Crimes Aggravated Punishment Act (특정경제범죄 가중처벌 등에 관한 법률), tax evasion involving particularly large sums can attract enhanced penalties, including life imprisonment. Suspended sentences are generally unavailable where the sentence exceeds three years.

### Prosecution Procedure

A distinctive feature of Korean tax enforcement is the prosecution gatekeeping mechanism: unless the Commissioner of the National Tax Service (NTS), the head of a regional tax office, or the head of a tax office formally accuses the taxpayer, no public prosecutor may institute criminal proceedings for offenses under the Punishment of Tax Offenses Act. The statute of limitations for prosecution of tax offenses expires after seven years.

For tax assessment purposes, the right to assess tax may be exercised for 10 years where the taxpayer has evaded tax or fraudulently claimed deductions or refunds. For offshore transactions, this period extends to 15 years.

### Landmark Cases

**Chaebol Tax Enforcement:** South Korea's large family-run conglomerates (chaebols) have been subject to sustained tax enforcement campaigns. Choi Tae-won of SK Group, Chung Mong-koo of Hyundai, Kim Seung-youn of Hanwha, and Shin Dong-bin of Lotte have all faced charges, convictions, or imprisonment for white-collar crimes including tax evasion and embezzlement. These prosecutions reflect the Korean government's periodic crackdowns on corporate malfeasance.

**Cha Eun-woo Tax Dispute (2026):** In early 2026, the NTS issued an additional tax assessment reportedly exceeding 20 billion won (approximately US$13.7 million) against K-pop star and actor Cha Eun-woo (real name Lee Dong-min). The reassessment followed an audit into a "one-person agency" established by his mother and placed between the entertainer and his management agency Fantagio. Investigators concluded that the company functioned as a paper entity, allowing entertainment income to be taxed at the lower corporate rate rather than the higher personal income tax rate. The case, representing the largest celebrity tax assessment in Korean history, prompted legislative proposals to curb tax evasion through solo entertainment agencies.

**Jang Geun-suk Case:** Prior to the Cha Eun-woo controversy, the largest entertainment-related tax case involved actor Jang Geun-suk's mother, who paid approximately 5 billion won and received a suspended prison sentence for a similar structure-based tax evasion scheme.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Federal tax evasion in the United States is principally prosecuted under 26 U.S.C. section 7201 of the Internal Revenue Code, which provides: "Any person who willfully attempts in any manner to evade or defeat any tax imposed by this title or the payment thereof shall, in addition to other penalties provided by law, be guilty of a felony." Additional criminal tax provisions include tax fraud (section 7206), failure to file a return (section 7203), and failure to pay tax (section 7202).

The Internal Revenue Service Criminal Investigation Division (IRS-CI) conducts tax crime investigations, but prosecution authority rests with the Department of Justice Tax Division, which must authorize all federal tax prosecutions.

### Elements of the Offense

Under section 7201, the government must prove three elements beyond a reasonable doubt:

1. **Tax Due and Owing:** A substantial tax deficiency existed for the relevant tax year.
2. **Affirmative Act of Evasion:** The defendant committed at least one affirmative act constituting an attempt to evade or defeat a tax. As established in Spies v. United States (1943), mere passive failure to file a return is insufficient; there must be a willful commission of an affirmative act, such as keeping a double set of books, making false entries or alterations, destroying records, concealing assets or income sources, or conducting transactions to disguise income.
3. **Willfulness:** The defendant acted willfully, meaning with the voluntary, intentional violation of a known legal duty. As clarified in Cheek v. United States (1991), a good-faith misunderstanding of the law or a good-faith belief that one is not violating the law negates willfulness, whether or not the claimed belief is objectively reasonable.

### Penalties

| Offense | Maximum Imprisonment | Maximum Fine (Individual) | Maximum Fine (Corporation) |
|---------|---------------------|--------------------------|---------------------------|
| Tax evasion (section 7201) | 5 years | $250,000 | $500,000 |
| Tax fraud / false statements (section 7206) | 3 years | $250,000 | $500,000 |
| Failure to file a return (section 7203) | 1 year (misdemeanor) | $100,000 | $200,000 |
| Failure to collect or pay tax (section 7202) | 5 years | $250,000 | $500,000 |

Note: Under 18 U.S.C. section 3571, the fine for any felony may be increased to twice the gross gain derived from the offense or twice the gross loss caused to victims, whichever is greater. Courts routinely order restitution for unpaid taxes, interest, and penalties. A felony conviction under section 7201 results in a permanent criminal record.

Actual sentencing is governed by the U.S. Sentencing Guidelines, with the tax loss amount serving as the primary driver. Once the tax loss exceeds approximately $550,000, sentences typically reach the statutory maximum of 5 years regardless of criminal history.

### Voluntary Disclosure

The IRS maintains a Voluntary Disclosure Practice that allows non-compliant taxpayers to come forward and disclose unreported income or unfiled returns. While voluntary disclosure does not provide guaranteed immunity from prosecution, the IRS has historically exercised its discretion not to recommend criminal prosecution where a taxpayer makes a timely, voluntary, and complete disclosure before the IRS has initiated an investigation. The program requires full payment of all taxes, interest, and penalties.

### Landmark Cases

**Al Capone (1931):** Perhaps the most famous tax evasion prosecution in history, the case against Chicago organized crime boss Alphonse "Al" Capone demonstrated the power of tax law as an enforcement tool. Despite his leadership of a massive criminal enterprise involving bootlegging, gambling, and murder during Prohibition, Capone proved nearly impossible to convict on racketeering charges. Federal investigators, led by Special Agent Frank Wilson of the IRS Intelligence Unit, instead built a case around Capone's failure to file income tax returns despite his lavish lifestyle. On October 18, 1931, Capone was found guilty on five counts of tax evasion spanning the years 1925 through 1929. He was sentenced to 11 years in federal prison, fined $50,000, charged $7,692 in court costs, and ordered to pay his back taxes with interest. The case established the paradigm of using tax enforcement against organized crime figures.

**Spies v. United States, 317 U.S. 492 (1943):** The Supreme Court established the foundational distinction between passive failure to file a tax return (a misdemeanor) and active tax evasion (a felony). The Court held that mere non-filing does not constitute attempted evasion under section 7201; the government must prove an affirmative act of evasion. The Court enumerated examples of qualifying affirmative acts, including keeping a double set of books, making false entries or alterations, destroying books or records, concealing assets or covering up sources of income, and handling transactions to avoid the usual records. This decision remains the controlling authority on the affirmative act requirement.

**Cheek v. United States, 498 U.S. 192 (1991):** John Cheek, an airline pilot and tax protester, stopped filing returns and paying taxes based on his belief that the tax laws were unconstitutional. The Supreme Court held that the term "willfully" in criminal tax statutes requires the government to prove that the defendant was aware of his legal duty and voluntarily and intentionally violated it. A good-faith misunderstanding of the law or a good-faith belief that one is not violating the law negates willfulness, even if the belief is objectively unreasonable. However, the Court distinguished between beliefs about the law's requirements (which can negate willfulness) and beliefs about the law's constitutionality (which cannot). This decision established the subjective standard for willfulness in tax prosecutions.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Tax evasion (Steuerhinterziehung) in Germany is criminalized under Section 370 of the Abgabenordnung (AO, Fiscal Code). The provision applies to any person who:

1. Provides incorrect or incomplete information to the tax authorities regarding tax-relevant facts;
2. Fails to disclose tax-relevant facts to the tax authorities in breach of a legal duty; or
3. Fails to use tax stamps or tax meters in breach of a legal duty;

and thereby causes a reduction in taxes or obtains an unjustified tax advantage for themselves or another person.

Additional tax offenses include reckless tax reduction (leichtfertige Steuerverkuerzung) under Section 378 AO (an administrative offense rather than a criminal one), smuggling under Section 373 AO, and organized or commercial tax fraud under Section 374 AO.

### Mental Element

Section 370 requires intent (Vorsatz), which under German criminal law includes conditional intent (bedingter Vorsatz or dolus eventualis). This means that a taxpayer who considers it possible that the information provided to tax authorities is incorrect or incomplete and could lead to an understatement of tax, but acts regardless, satisfies the mental element. This is a lower threshold than the "willfulness" requirement under U.S. law.

Reckless (leichtfertig) tax reduction under Section 378 AO is treated as a less serious administrative offense (Ordnungswidrigkeit), punishable by a fine of up to 50,000 euros rather than imprisonment.

### Penalties

| Evaded Tax Amount | Typical Penalty |
|-------------------|----------------|
| Under 50,000 euros | Fine (Geldstrafe) |
| 50,000 to 100,000 euros | Fine or suspended custodial sentence |
| 100,000 euros to 1 million euros | Suspended custodial sentence |
| Over 1 million euros | Custodial sentence without suspension |

Under Section 370(1) AO, the standard penalty is imprisonment for up to 5 years or a fine. For particularly serious cases (besonders schwerer Fall) under Section 370(3) AO -- including large-scale evasion, abuse of official authority, or use of falsified documents -- the penalty increases to imprisonment from 6 months to 10 years. Smuggling under Section 373 AO and organized or commercial tax offenses under Section 374(2) AO also carry up to 10 years imprisonment.

German courts have established through case law that evaded tax amounts exceeding 50,000 euros constitute large-scale tax evasion (grosse Ausmasse), generally precluding the imposition of a mere fine.

### Statute of Limitations

The statute of limitations for prosecuting simple tax evasion under Section 370(1) AO is 5 years. For particularly serious cases under Section 370(3) AO, the limitation period was extended to 15 years following legislative reforms prompted by high-profile offshore tax evasion cases.

### Voluntary Disclosure (Selbstanzeige)

Germany's Selbstanzeige (voluntary disclosure) mechanism under Section 371 AO is one of the most distinctive features of its tax enforcement regime. A taxpayer who has committed tax evasion can avoid criminal prosecution entirely by filing a corrected and complete tax return with the authorities, provided that:

1. The disclosure covers all unreported income for a period of at least 10 years;
2. The taxpayer pays all outstanding taxes, interest, and a surcharge within a specified deadline;
3. The tax authorities have not yet initiated an investigation or audit;
4. The evasion has not already been discovered.

For evaded amounts exceeding 25,000 euros, a surcharge of 10 to 20 percent on the evaded tax must be paid in addition to the tax itself. The Selbstanzeige mechanism has been repeatedly reformed and its conditions tightened, particularly following the Uli Hoeness case.

### Landmark Cases

**Uli Hoeness (2014):** The tax evasion trial of Bayern Munich president Uli Hoeness became one of the most prominent white-collar criminal cases in German legal history. Hoeness admitted to evading approximately 27.2 million euros ($37.6 million) in taxes by hiding funds in Swiss bank accounts. He had filed a voluntary disclosure (Selbstanzeige), but the court ruled that the disclosure was incomplete and therefore did not meet the requirements for immunity under Section 371 AO. Hoeness was sentenced to three and a half years imprisonment. The case shocked the German public and prompted thousands of other tax evaders to file voluntary disclosures. It also led to legislative reforms tightening the conditions for valid Selbstanzeige filings.

**CumEx Scandal (2012-present):** The CumEx-Files represent the largest tax fraud case in German and European history. The scheme involved the rapid trading of dividend-bearing shares around the dividend date (cum/ex trading), creating the appearance of multiple owners and enabling multiple refund claims for dividend withholding tax that was paid only once -- or not at all. The estimated total loss to European treasuries exceeds 55 billion euros, with Germany as the hardest-hit country at approximately $36.2 billion. German authorities have recovered approximately 3.4 billion euros through convictions and settlements. In a landmark ruling, former Freshfields Bruckhaus Deringer tax partner Ulf Johannemann was convicted of aiding serious tax evasion and sentenced to three and a half years imprisonment, a sentence upheld by the Federal Court of Justice (Bundesgerichtshof). The scandal also ensnared former Chancellor Olaf Scholz, who faced parliamentary inquiries regarding his role as mayor of Hamburg when Warburg Bank received questionable CumEx-related tax refunds.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Legislative Framework

The European Union has established a comprehensive legislative framework to combat tax evasion and avoidance across Member States, though it lacks a unified criminal tax law. Key instruments include:

**Anti-Tax Avoidance Directive (ATAD, Directive 2016/1164):** Adopted on July 12, 2016, the ATAD sets out legally binding anti-abuse measures that all Member States must apply against common forms of aggressive tax planning. The directive establishes five key provisions functioning as minimum standards:

1. **Interest Limitation Rule (Article 4):** Limits deductibility of exceeding borrowing costs to 30 percent of a taxpayer's EBITDA.
2. **Exit Taxation (Article 5):** Subjects unrealized capital gains to taxation when assets, tax residence, or a permanent establishment are transferred out of a Member State's tax jurisdiction.
3. **General Anti-Abuse Rule (GAAR, Article 6):** Allows Member States to disregard arrangements that are not genuine and have been put in place for the essential purpose of obtaining a tax advantage.
4. **Controlled Foreign Company Rules (Article 7-8):** Re-attribute income of a low-taxed subsidiary to its parent company to prevent profit shifting.
5. **Hybrid Mismatch Rules (Article 9, amended by ATAD II, Directive 2017/952):** Address arrangements exploiting differences in the tax treatment of entities or instruments between jurisdictions.

**DAC6 -- Mandatory Disclosure Rules (Directive 2018/822):** Obliges intermediaries -- tax advisors, accountants, and lawyers -- to report cross-border tax arrangements containing specified "hallmarks" of potential aggressive tax planning to the tax authorities. Information is then automatically exchanged between Member States. In Case C-623/22 (Belgian Bar Association), the Court of Justice of the European Union ruled on the validity of DAC6 in light of fundamental rights, including legal professional privilege.

**European Public Prosecutor's Office (EPPO):** Established under Regulation 2017/1939, the EPPO has competence to investigate and prosecute crimes affecting the EU's financial interests, including VAT fraud exceeding 10 million euros. As of 2026, 24 EU Member States participate in the EPPO.

### ECHR Jurisprudence on Taxation

While the European Convention on Human Rights does not specifically address taxation, the European Court of Human Rights (ECtHR) has developed a substantial body of case law affecting how Member States may enforce their tax laws.

**Article 6 (Right to a Fair Trial):**

*Ferrazzini v. Italy (2001):* The Grand Chamber held, by 11 votes to 6, that Article 6(1) does not apply to pure tax disputes under its "civil rights and obligations" limb, reasoning that tax matters form part of the hard core of public authority prerogatives and that the obligation to pay tax does not involve a "civil" right or obligation. However, this exclusion applies only to purely fiscal proceedings; Article 6 does apply to tax proceedings under its criminal head where tax surcharges or penalties are imposed.

*Jussila v. Finland (2006):* The Grand Chamber held that the imposition of a 10 percent tax surcharge was "criminal" for the purposes of Article 6, even though Finnish domestic law classified it as administrative. The Court applied the Engel criteria -- the legal classification of the offense under national law, the nature of the offense, and the severity of the potential penalty -- and concluded that tax surcharges of a punitive nature engage Article 6's criminal guarantees. This decision extended fair trial protections, including the right to an oral hearing and the presumption of innocence, to administrative tax penalty proceedings across Convention states.

**Article 4 Protocol 7 (Ne Bis in Idem / Double Jeopardy):**

The ne bis in idem principle has become a critical issue in tax enforcement, where many states impose both administrative penalties (tax surcharges) and criminal sanctions (prosecution for tax fraud) based on the same underlying facts. Under Article 4 of Protocol 7, four elements must be satisfied to trigger the prohibition:

1. Two sets of proceedings of a criminal nature (bis);
2. Concerning the same facts (idem);
3. Against the same offender;
4. A final decision in one of the proceedings.

*A and B v. Norway (2016):* The Grand Chamber held that dual proceedings -- administrative tax surcharges and criminal prosecution -- based on the same facts do not violate the ne bis in idem principle provided there is a "sufficiently close connection in substance and in time" between the two sets of proceedings. This ruling gave Member States significant latitude to maintain parallel administrative and criminal enforcement tracks for tax offenses.

**Article 1 Protocol 1 (Protection of Property):**

The ECtHR has recognized that taxation constitutes an interference with the right to property under Article 1 of Protocol 1, but has afforded states a wide margin of appreciation in fiscal matters. Tax surcharges, penalties, and confiscation orders must nevertheless be proportionate and prescribed by law.

### Landmark EU Cases

**Apple State Aid Case (2016-2020):** In 2016, the European Commission ordered Ireland to recover up to 13 billion euros in unpaid taxes from Apple, concluding that two Irish tax rulings had granted Apple selective advantages constituting illegal state aid under Article 107 TFEU. In 2020, the General Court annulled the Commission's decision, finding that it had not proven the existence of a selective advantage. However, in 2024, the Court of Justice of the European Union reversed the General Court and reinstated the Commission's decision, upholding the 13 billion euro recovery order. The case represents the largest tax state aid recovery in EU history and has shaped the relationship between EU state aid law and national tax sovereignty.

**Fiat and Starbucks Transfer Pricing Cases:** The Commission investigated the transfer pricing arrangements of Fiat Finance and Trade (Luxembourg) and Starbucks (Netherlands), finding that favorable tax rulings constituted illegal state aid. These cases established that transfer pricing rulings granting selective advantages to multinational enterprises may violate EU state aid rules, even if they comply with domestic tax law.

## Comparative Analysis

### Burden of Proof and Mental Element

The mental element required for criminal tax evasion varies significantly across jurisdictions. The United States imposes the highest threshold, requiring proof of "willfulness" -- the voluntary, intentional violation of a known legal duty -- as interpreted by the Supreme Court in Cheek v. United States. This effectively requires proof that the defendant knew the law and deliberately chose to violate it. Germany's requirement of intent, including conditional intent (dolus eventualis), is broader: a taxpayer who recognizes the possibility that their tax return is incorrect but files it anyway satisfies the mental element. South Korea similarly requires intent, but the practical threshold is modulated by the prosecution gatekeeping role of the National Tax Service.

### Penalty Severity

South Korea imposes the most severe maximum penalties, with life imprisonment available for tax evasion exceeding 1 billion won (approximately $750,000). Germany provides the longest standard imprisonment range at up to 10 years for serious cases. The United States caps imprisonment at 5 years per count, though multiple counts can result in consecutive sentences. Germany's sentencing practice is closely tied to the evaded amount, with case law establishing relatively predictable thresholds for fines, suspended sentences, and custodial sentences.

### Voluntary Disclosure Mechanisms

The three national jurisdictions take markedly different approaches to voluntary disclosure. Germany's Selbstanzeige is the most formalized, offering complete immunity from criminal prosecution if the disclosure is timely, complete, and accompanied by full payment (including surcharges). The United States offers no statutory guarantee of immunity, but the IRS Voluntary Disclosure Practice provides strong practical incentives by generally declining to recommend prosecution where disclosure is made before an investigation commences. South Korea offers limited mitigation through cooperation but does not provide a comparable formal immunity mechanism.

### Enforcement Architecture

A common feature across South Korea, the United States, and Germany is the gatekeeping role of the tax authority in initiating criminal proceedings. In South Korea, the NTS Commissioner must formally accuse before prosecution can begin. In the United States, the DOJ Tax Division must authorize prosecution based on IRS-CI referrals. In Germany, the tax authority (Finanzamt) may refer cases to the public prosecutor or handle less serious cases through its own penalty proceedings (Bussgeld- und Strafsachenverfahren). At the EU level, the EPPO now provides a supranational prosecution capability for cross-border VAT fraud exceeding 10 million euros.

### Interaction Between Administrative and Criminal Proceedings

All jurisdictions face the challenge of coordinating administrative tax penalties with criminal prosecution. The ECHR's jurisprudence on ne bis in idem -- particularly the A and B v. Norway framework permitting dual proceedings where there is a "sufficiently close connection in substance and in time" -- has shaped this balance across European states. In the United States, the IRS may impose civil fraud penalties (75% of the underpayment under IRC section 6663) alongside criminal prosecution, with no constitutional double jeopardy bar because civil and criminal proceedings are considered distinct. South Korea similarly permits parallel administrative penalties and criminal sanctions.
