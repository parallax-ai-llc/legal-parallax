## Summary

Food labeling -- the system of mandatory and voluntary disclosures on food products regarding ingredients, nutritional content, allergens, origin, and health claims -- is a critical intersection of consumer protection, public health, and agricultural trade law that varies significantly across jurisdictions. South Korea regulates food labeling through the Food Labeling and Advertising Act (식품 등의 표시·광고에 관한 법률, Act No. 15483, enacted 2018) and its Enforcement Decree, administered by the Ministry of Food and Drug Safety (식품의약품안전처, MFDS), which requires comprehensive nutritional information panels, allergen declarations for 24 designated allergens, country-of-origin labeling, and front-of-pack nutritional traffic-light indicators on certain products. The United States governs food labeling primarily through the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (21 U.S.C. Section 301 et seq.) and the Fair Packaging and Labeling Act (15 U.S.C. Section 1451 et seq.), administered by the Food and Drug Administration (FDA), with the Nutrition Labeling and Education Act of 1990 mandating the Nutrition Facts panel and the 2016 final rule updating it to reflect current nutrition science, while the USDA Food Safety and Inspection Service (FSIS) regulates labeling of meat, poultry, and egg products. Germany, as an EU member state, applies Regulation (EU) No. 1169/2011 on the provision of food information to consumers, which harmonizes mandatory labeling requirements across the EU including the 14-allergen declaration system, mandatory nutritional declarations, origin labeling for certain products, and the voluntary Nutri-Score front-of-pack labeling system that Germany officially adopted in 2020. The European framework, developed through decades of harmonization, represents the most comprehensive mandatory food labeling regime globally, with the ECHR's Article 10 jurisprudence protecting commercial expression rights that intersect with mandatory labeling requirements.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's food labeling regime is governed by the Food Labeling and Advertising Act (식품 등의 표시·광고에 관한 법률), enacted on March 13, 2018, separating labeling and advertising regulation from the broader Food Sanitation Act (식품위생법). The Ministry of Food and Drug Safety (식품의약품안전처, MFDS) administers the regulatory framework.

**Article 4 (Mandatory Labeling / 의무 표시사항):** All food products sold in South Korea must display: product name, type of food, manufacturer name and address, date of manufacture or expiration date, net contents, ingredients list (in descending order of weight), nutritional information, allergen information, storage conditions, and country of origin.

**Article 5 (Nutritional Labeling / 영양표시):** Mandatory nutritional information must include calories, carbohydrates, sugars, protein, fat, saturated fat, trans fat, cholesterol, and sodium. The daily reference values are based on a 2,000-calorie diet. Since 2022, total sugars must be separately declared.

**Article 8 (Allergen Labeling / 알레르기 유발 물질 표시):** South Korea requires declaration of 24 allergens, among the highest number of any jurisdiction globally: eggs, milk, buckwheat, peanuts, soybeans, wheat, mackerel, crab, shrimp, pork, peach, tomato, sulfites, walnuts, chicken, beef, squid, shellfish (including oyster, abalone, mussel), pine nuts, sesame, mollusks, almonds, cashews, and mustard.

### Country-of-Origin Labeling (원산지 표시)

Under the Agricultural and Fishery Products Quality Control Act (농수산물 품질관리법) and its enforcement decrees, South Korea maintains one of the world's most extensive country-of-origin labeling (COOL) requirements. Restaurants serving more than a specified number of meals must display the origin of rice, beef, pork, chicken, and certain seafood items. Violation of COOL requirements in restaurants can result in fines up to 10 million KRW and business suspension.

### Penalties

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| False or misleading food labeling | Up to 10 years imprisonment or fine up to 100 million KRW |
| Failure to provide mandatory labeling | Fine up to 10 million KRW; product recall |
| False allergen declaration | Product recall; fine up to 50 million KRW; criminal prosecution |
| Violation of advertising restrictions | Fine up to 50 million KRW; corrective advertising order |
| COOL violation (restaurants) | Fine up to 10 million KRW; business suspension |

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States regulates food labeling through multiple overlapping statutes administered by two primary agencies: the FDA (most foods) and the USDA FSIS (meat, poultry, and processed egg products).

**Nutrition Labeling and Education Act of 1990 (NLEA):** Amended the FDCA to require nutritional labeling on most packaged food products. The NLEA authorized the FDA to establish the Nutrition Facts panel, which was updated through a final rule published on May 27, 2016 (81 Fed. Reg. 33742), with compliance required by January 1, 2020 (extended to January 1, 2021, for manufacturers with less than $10 million in annual food sales).

**Updated Nutrition Facts Panel:** The 2016 revision introduced several significant changes: mandatory declaration of added sugars (in grams and as percent daily value), updated serving sizes reflecting actual consumption patterns, updated daily reference values based on current scientific evidence, declaration of Vitamin D and potassium (replacing Vitamins A and C), and a revised format with larger and bolder calorie declarations.

**Food Allergen Labeling and Consumer Protection Act (FALCPA, 2004):** Requires declaration of the eight major food allergens (milk, eggs, fish, crustacean shellfish, tree nuts, peanuts, wheat, and soybeans) in plain language on the label of all packaged foods regulated by the FDA. The FASTER Act of 2021 added sesame as the ninth major allergen, effective January 1, 2023.

**National Bioengineered Food Disclosure Standard (7 C.F.R. Part 66):** The USDA Agricultural Marketing Service's mandatory bioengineered (GMO) food disclosure standard took full effect on January 1, 2022. Regulated entities must disclose bioengineered foods or food ingredients through text, a symbol, an electronic/digital link, or a phone number. The standard has been criticized for permitting disclosure through QR codes rather than requiring on-pack text, and for using the term "bioengineered" rather than the more commonly understood "GMO."

### Landmark Cases

**POM Wonderful LLC v. Coca-Cola Co., 573 U.S. 102 (2014):** The Supreme Court held that the Lanham Act permits competitors to bring suit challenging food labeling that is allegedly misleading, even when the labels comply with FDA regulations. The ruling established that FDA regulatory compliance does not preempt unfair competition claims under federal trademark law.

**National Association of Manufacturers v. SEC (D.C. Cir. 2015):** While not a food labeling case, the D.C. Circuit's First Amendment analysis of compelled commercial disclosure has influenced food labeling litigation, particularly challenges to mandatory GMO disclosure requirements.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany applies the EU's harmonized food labeling framework, supplemented by national provisions.

**Regulation (EU) No. 1169/2011 (LMIV -- Lebensmittelinformationsverordnung):** This regulation, applicable since December 13, 2014, establishes mandatory food information requirements across all EU member states. Germany implements the regulation through the Lebensmittelinformations-Durchführungsverordnung (LMIDV) and the Lebensmittel- und Futtermittelgesetzbuch (LFGB).

**Mandatory Information (LMIV Article 9):**
- Name of the food
- List of ingredients (descending order of weight)
- Allergen information (14 allergens listed in Annex II, emphasized in the ingredients list)
- Quantity of certain ingredients
- Net quantity
- Date of minimum durability or "use by" date
- Storage and use conditions
- Name and address of the food business operator
- Country of origin or place of provenance (where required)
- Nutritional declaration
- Alcohol content (for beverages exceeding 1.2% ABV)

**Nutri-Score:** Germany officially authorized the use of the Nutri-Score front-of-pack nutrition label through the Nutri-Score-Verordnung of November 2020. The Nutri-Score, developed by French researchers and first adopted by France in 2017, classifies products from A (dark green, most favorable) to E (red, least favorable) based on an algorithm considering energy, sugars, saturated fat, sodium, protein, fiber, and fruit/vegetable/nut content per 100g. While voluntary, adoption by major German food manufacturers and retailers has been widespread.

### Allergen Labeling

Under Regulation 1169/2011 Annex II, the 14 mandatory allergens are: cereals containing gluten, crustaceans, eggs, fish, peanuts, soybeans, milk, nuts (almonds, hazelnuts, walnuts, cashews, pecans, Brazil nuts, pistachios, macadamia nuts), celery, mustard, sesame seeds, sulphur dioxide/sulphites (>10 mg/kg), lupin, and molluscs. Allergens must be emphasized in the ingredients list through typographical means (bold, italics, or contrasting background).

### Penalties

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| Missing mandatory labeling information | Administrative fine up to EUR 50,000 (LFGB Section 60) |
| Misleading food labeling | Fine up to EUR 500,000; criminal prosecution possible under LFGB Section 59 |
| Allergen labeling failure | Fine up to EUR 50,000; product recall |
| False health or nutrition claims | Fine up to EUR 50,000 under Regulation (EC) No. 1924/2006 implementation |

### Health Claims Regulation

Germany applies Regulation (EC) No. 1924/2006 on nutrition and health claims made on foods, which prohibits health claims that have not been authorized through the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) scientific assessment process. The register of authorized health claims (EU Register) lists approximately 260 permitted claims. German enforcement authorities have been particularly active in challenging unauthorized health claims on herbal and dietary supplement products.

## ECHR / EU

### Regulation (EU) No. 1169/2011

The EU Food Information to Consumers Regulation (FIC) represents the most comprehensive mandatory food labeling regime globally. Key provisions include:

**Article 9 (Mandatory Particulars):** Establishes 12 categories of mandatory information for all pre-packaged foods, including the nutritional declaration that became mandatory on December 13, 2016.

**Article 26 (Country of Origin / Place of Provenance):** Mandatory origin labeling applies to fresh, chilled, and frozen meat of swine, sheep, goats, and poultry (Implementing Regulation (EU) No. 1337/2013), unprocessed beef (Regulation (EC) No. 1760/2000), olive oil, honey, fresh fruits and vegetables, fish, and wine. The Commission has been evaluating extension of mandatory origin labeling to milk, meat used as an ingredient, single-ingredient foods, and minimally processed foods.

**Article 36 (Voluntary Food Information):** Permits voluntary food information provided it does not mislead, is not ambiguous or confusing, and is based on relevant scientific data. This provision governs voluntary labeling schemes including front-of-pack nutrition labels.

### Front-of-Pack Nutrition Labeling Proposal

The European Commission published a proposal in December 2022 for a harmonized mandatory front-of-pack nutrition labeling (FOPNL) system. As of 2026, negotiations continue among member states, with significant debate between the Nutri-Score model (supported by France, Germany, Belgium, Netherlands, Spain, Luxembourg, and Switzerland) and the NutrInform Battery model (supported by Italy, Czech Republic, and several eastern European states). The proposal aims to replace the current patchwork of national voluntary FOPNL systems with a single EU-wide mandatory system.

### ECHR Jurisprudence -- Commercial Expression

**Markt Intern Verlag GmbH and Klaus Beermann v. Germany (Application No. 10572/83, ECHR, November 20, 1989):** The Court recognized that commercial expression falls within the scope of Article 10 (freedom of expression) of the ECHR, establishing that mandatory food labeling requirements must be assessed against the right to freedom of expression, though states enjoy a wide margin of appreciation in regulating commercial speech to protect consumers and public health.

**Casado Coca v. Spain (Application No. 15450/89, ECHR, February 24, 1994):** The Court confirmed that states may restrict commercial advertising and impose labeling requirements where necessary to protect consumers, subject to proportionality analysis under Article 10(2).

### Regulation (EC) No. 1924/2006 -- Health and Nutrition Claims

This regulation establishes EU-wide rules on health and nutrition claims made on food products. Claims must be scientifically substantiated and authorized by the European Commission following EFSA assessment. The regulation distinguishes between nutrition claims (e.g., "low fat," "high fiber"), health claims (e.g., "calcium contributes to normal bone maintenance"), and reduction of disease risk claims, each subject to different authorization requirements.

### Court of Justice of the European Union

**Case C-609/12, Ehrmann (2014):** The CJEU ruled that health claims on food products must comply with Regulation 1924/2006 even when the claim is well-established and commonly used, confirming the comprehensive scope of the EU health claims authorization system.

**Case C-195/14, Teekanne (2015):** The Court held that food labeling must be assessed based on the overall impression produced on the average consumer, and that accurate ingredient lists do not cure misleading visual impressions created by images on packaging. The case involved herbal tea with images of raspberries and vanilla flowers despite containing no raspberry or vanilla ingredients.

## Comparative Analysis

### Allergen Labeling

The most significant divergence among jurisdictions concerns the scope of mandatory allergen declarations. South Korea leads with 24 designated allergens, the EU requires 14, and the United States mandates only 9. These differences create compliance challenges for multinational food companies and can leave consumers with less-common allergies unprotected when traveling between jurisdictions. South Korea's broader list reflects the country's dietary patterns, including allergens such as buckwheat, mackerel, and peach that are more prevalent in Korean cuisine.

### Front-of-Pack Labeling

The approach to front-of-pack nutrition labeling reveals fundamental differences in regulatory philosophy. The EU and its member states are moving toward mandatory, interpretive labeling (Nutri-Score or equivalent), South Korea has implemented a voluntary traffic-light system, and the United States has no mandatory front-of-pack system. The EU's proposed harmonized FOPNL system, if adopted, would represent the most ambitious mandatory front-of-pack regime globally.

### GMO Disclosure

All four jurisdictions require some form of GMO disclosure, but the threshold and methodology differ markedly. The EU imposes the lowest threshold (0.9% adventitious presence), South Korea applies a 3% threshold, and the US standard permits non-text disclosure methods (QR codes). The EU's strict labeling requirement has been a persistent source of transatlantic trade friction.

### Enforcement Intensity

South Korea imposes the most severe criminal penalties for food labeling fraud (up to 10 years imprisonment), reflecting the country's vigorous approach to food safety enforcement following several high-profile food scandals. The United States relies primarily on FDA warning letters, seizures, and injunctions, with criminal prosecution reserved for egregious cases. Germany's enforcement operates through a combination of state-level administrative action and the EU's Rapid Alert System for Food and Feed (RASFF).
