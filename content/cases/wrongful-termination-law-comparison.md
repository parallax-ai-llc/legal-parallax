## Summary

Wrongful termination -- the unlawful dismissal of an employee in violation of statutory protections, contractual obligations, or public policy -- reveals one of the sharpest divergences in labor law philosophy among developed nations, particularly the fundamental divide between employment-at-will systems and just-cause regimes. South Korea's Labor Standards Act (근로기준법) Articles 23 through 28 establish a robust just-cause dismissal system under which employers may not dismiss workers without "justifiable reason" (정당한 이유), with cases adjudicated by the Labor Relations Commission (노동위원회) and reinstatement as the primary remedy; the 1998 amendment during the Asian Financial Crisis introduced limited provisions for "managerial necessity" layoffs (경영상 이유에 의한 해고) under Article 24, requiring urgent business necessity, exhaustion of alternatives, fair selection criteria, and prior consultation with worker representatives. The United States remains the most significant adherent to the employment-at-will doctrine, under which either party may terminate the employment relationship at any time for any reason not specifically prohibited by law; wrongful termination claims arise only through narrow exceptions including anti-discrimination statutes (Title VII, ADA, ADEA), whistleblower protections, breach of implied contract, and the public policy exception recognized in approximately 43 states. Germany provides among the world's strongest dismissal protections through the Kündigungsschutzgesetz (KSchG) of 1951, which requires social justification (soziale Rechtfertigung) for all dismissals in establishments with more than 10 employees, categorized as person-related, conduct-related, or operationally necessary, with extensive procedural requirements including mandatory Betriebsrat (works council) consultation under Section 102 of the Betriebsverfassungsgesetz. At the European level, Article 30 of the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights guarantees the right to protection against unjustified dismissal, ILO Convention No. 158 on Termination of Employment (1982) establishes the international just-cause standard, and the European Court of Human Rights has addressed dismissal rights through Articles 8, 10, and 11 of the ECHR, including the landmark case of Heinisch v. Germany (2011) protecting whistleblower dismissals.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's dismissal protection system is anchored in Article 23(1) of the Labor Standards Act (근로기준법): "An employer shall not dismiss, lay off, suspend, or transfer a worker, reduce wages, or take other punitive measures against a worker without justifiable reason." This provision creates a just-cause regime that applies to all workplaces with five or more employees (extended from the previous threshold of ten employees by the 2007 amendment).

### Justifiable Reason Standard

Korean courts and the Labor Relations Commission have developed a substantial body of precedent defining "justifiable reason" (정당한 이유). The Supreme Court has held that the following factors must be considered: whether the employee's conduct was sufficiently serious to warrant dismissal, whether progressive discipline was applied, whether the employer followed proper procedures, and whether dismissal was proportionate to the offense. The burden of proof rests entirely on the employer.

### Procedural Requirements

**Notice (Article 26):** Employers must provide at least 30 days' advance written notice of dismissal or pay 30 days' ordinary wages in lieu of notice. This requirement does not apply during probationary periods of three months or less, or in cases of worker fault.

**Written Statement (Article 27):** Since the 2007 amendment, employers must provide written notice specifying the grounds for dismissal and the effective date. Failure to provide written notice renders the dismissal procedurally invalid regardless of whether substantive grounds exist.

### Managerial Necessity Layoffs (Article 24)

The 1998 amendment, enacted during the Asian Financial Crisis under IMF conditionality, introduced Article 24 permitting layoffs for "urgent managerial necessity" (긴박한 경영상의 필요). Four cumulative requirements must be met:

| Requirement | Description |
|-------------|-------------|
| Urgent business necessity | Financial difficulties, technological change, restructuring, or other urgent operational need |
| Effort to avoid dismissal | Employer must exhaust all reasonable alternatives (transfers, reduced hours, voluntary retirement) |
| Fair selection criteria | Rational and fair criteria for selecting affected employees |
| Prior consultation | 50+ days advance notification and good-faith consultation with labor union or employee representatives |

Failure to satisfy any single requirement renders the layoff unlawful. The Supreme Court has interpreted "urgent managerial necessity" broadly to include not only imminent bankruptcy but also rational business restructuring decisions, while requiring genuine exhaustion of alternatives.

### Remedies

Workers who believe they have been wrongfully dismissed may file a complaint with the Regional Labor Relations Commission within three months of dismissal. If the Commission finds the dismissal unjust, it orders reinstatement and payment of back wages. Either party may appeal to the National Labor Relations Commission and subsequently to the administrative courts. As of 2023, approximately 35 percent of unfair dismissal claims filed with Regional Labor Relations Commissions resulted in reinstatement orders.

### Special Protections

Korean law provides enhanced dismissal protections for specific categories: pregnant workers and workers within one year of childbirth (Article 23(2)), workers on occupational injury leave and for 30 days thereafter (Article 23(2)), and union officials engaged in legitimate union activities (Trade Union and Labor Relations Adjustment Act, 노동조합 및 노동관계조정법).

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States adheres to the employment-at-will doctrine, under which either the employer or the employee may terminate the employment relationship at any time, for any reason or no reason at all, absent a specific legal prohibition. This doctrine, established in Horace Wood's 1877 treatise and adopted by courts nationwide, represents the most permissive dismissal regime among developed nations.

### Statutory Exceptions

Despite the at-will baseline, numerous federal and state statutes prohibit termination on specific grounds:

| Statute | Protected Ground |
|---------|-----------------|
| Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 | Race, color, religion, sex (including sexual orientation and gender identity per Bostock v. Clayton County, 590 U.S. 644 (2020)), national origin |
| Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), 1990 | Disability |
| Age Discrimination in Employment Act (ADEA), 1967 | Age (40+) |
| Pregnancy Discrimination Act, 1978 | Pregnancy, childbirth, related medical conditions |
| Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA), 1993 | Retaliation for exercising leave rights |
| National Labor Relations Act (NLRA), 1935 | Union activity, protected concerted activity |
| Sarbanes-Oxley Act, 2002 | Whistleblowing (publicly traded companies) |
| Dodd-Frank Act, 2010 | Whistleblowing (financial sector) |

### Common Law Exceptions

Courts in most states have recognized common law exceptions to at-will employment:

**Public Policy Exception (~43 states):** An employee may not be discharged for reasons that violate a clear mandate of public policy, such as refusing to commit a crime, exercising a statutory right, or reporting illegal conduct. The seminal case is Petermann v. International Brotherhood of Teamsters, 174 Cal. App. 2d 184 (1959).

**Implied Contract Exception (~37 states):** Employer representations in handbooks, policies, or oral statements may create an implied contract limiting the employer's right to terminate. Toussaint v. Blue Cross & Blue Shield of Michigan, 408 Mich. 579 (1980) is the leading case.

**Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing (~11 states):** A minority of states, including California and Massachusetts, recognize an implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing in employment relationships.

### WARN Act

The Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification (WARN) Act of 1988, 29 U.S.C. Section 2101, requires employers with 100 or more employees to provide 60 calendar days' advance written notice of plant closings and mass layoffs affecting 50 or more employees at a single site. Several states have enacted "mini-WARN" acts with lower thresholds.

### Montana -- The Exception

Montana is the sole U.S. state that has abolished the at-will doctrine by statute. The Montana Wrongful Discharge from Employment Act (1987) requires employers to show "good cause" for terminating employees who have completed a probationary period.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's dismissal protection system is one of the world's most comprehensive, centered on the Kündigungsschutzgesetz (KSchG), originally enacted on August 10, 1951, and the general notice period provisions of Section 622 BGB.

### Scope of Protection

The KSchG applies to employees who have been continuously employed for at least six months (Section 1(1) KSchG) in establishments with more than 10 employees (Section 23(1) KSchG, as amended in 2004 -- previously the threshold was 5 employees). Employees outside this scope retain protection under general civil law principles and Section 622 BGB notice periods.

### Social Justification Requirement

Section 1(2) KSchG provides that a dismissal is socially unjustified -- and therefore invalid -- unless it is justified by reasons relating to:

**Person-related reasons (personenbedingte Kündigung):** The employee's personal characteristics or circumstances render them unable to perform the contracted work. Examples include long-term illness, loss of required qualifications (e.g., driver's license for a delivery driver), or imprisonment. The employer must demonstrate a negative prognosis regarding future ability to work.

**Conduct-related reasons (verhaltensbedingte Kündigung):** The employee has breached contractual obligations through their behavior. Generally, a prior warning (Abmahnung) is required before dismissal, except for particularly serious misconduct. The principle of proportionality (Verhältnismäßigkeitsprinzip) requires that dismissal be the ultima ratio.

**Operational necessity (betriebsbedingte Kündigung):** Urgent operational requirements -- such as restructuring, outsourcing, or economic downturn -- necessitate workforce reduction. The employer must demonstrate that the position has been permanently eliminated, no alternative employment exists within the enterprise, and proper social selection (Sozialauswahl) criteria have been applied.

### Social Selection (Sozialauswahl)

Under Section 1(3) KSchG, when selecting employees for operationally necessitated dismissals, the employer must consider four mandatory criteria: length of service (Betriebszugehörigkeit), age (Lebensalter), maintenance obligations (Unterhaltspflichten), and severe disability (Schwerbehinderung). Employees with greater social vulnerability enjoy priority retention.

### Works Council Consultation

Section 102 of the Betriebsverfassungsgesetz (BetrVG) requires the employer to consult the Betriebsrat (works council) before any dismissal. The employer must inform the works council of the identity of the affected employee, the type of dismissal, the reasons for dismissal, and the period of notice. The works council has one week to respond (three days for extraordinary dismissal). A dismissal made without proper works council consultation is automatically void under Section 102(1) BetrVG.

### Notice Periods

Section 622 BGB establishes minimum notice periods based on length of employment:

| Length of Employment | Notice Period |
|---------------------|--------------|
| Up to 2 years | 4 weeks |
| 2-5 years | 1 month |
| 5-8 years | 2 months |
| 8-10 years | 3 months |
| 10-12 years | 4 months |
| 12-15 years | 5 months |
| 15-20 years | 6 months |
| 20+ years | 7 months |

### Extraordinary (Summary) Dismissal

Section 626 BGB permits immediate dismissal without notice for "important cause" (wichtiger Grund) -- circumstances so severe that the employer cannot reasonably be expected to continue the employment relationship until the end of the notice period. Such dismissal must occur within two weeks of the employer learning of the relevant facts.

### Remedies

The primary remedy under the KSchG is reinstatement: if a dismissal is found socially unjustified, the employment relationship continues as if the dismissal never occurred. However, in practice, many cases are resolved through negotiated severance payments (Abfindung). Section 9 KSchG permits the labor court to dissolve the employment relationship and order severance of up to 12 months' salary if continued employment is unreasonable for either party.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Charter of Fundamental Rights

Article 30 of the EU Charter provides: "Every worker has the right to protection against unjustified dismissal, in accordance with Union law and national laws and practices." This provision, which became legally binding with the entry into force of the Lisbon Treaty in 2009, establishes unjustified dismissal protection as a fundamental right within the EU legal order.

### ILO Convention No. 158

ILO Convention No. 158 on Termination of Employment (1982) establishes the international standard for dismissal protection. Article 4 provides that a worker shall not be dismissed unless there is a valid reason connected with the capacity or conduct of the worker or based on the operational requirements of the undertaking. The Convention has been ratified by 36 states. Notably, neither the United States nor South Korea has ratified it, though South Korea's domestic law substantially exceeds Convention standards. Germany ratified the Convention in 1985.

### EU Collective Redundancies Directive 98/59/EC

This Directive establishes minimum requirements for mass dismissals, including prior notification to the competent public authority, information and consultation with worker representatives, and minimum waiting periods before dismissals take effect. The CJEU has interpreted the Directive extensively, including in AGET Iraklis (Case C-201/15, 2016), where the Court held that national rules requiring administrative authorization for collective redundancies may be justified as a proportionate restriction on the freedom of establishment.

### European Court of Human Rights

**Heinisch v. Germany (Application No. 28274/08, Judgment of July 21, 2011):** The Court found that Germany violated Article 10 (freedom of expression) by failing to protect a geriatric nurse dismissed for publicly reporting deficiencies in her employer's elder care practices. The judgment established that whistleblower dismissals must be assessed under the framework of free expression rights.

**Bărbulescu v. Romania (Application No. 61496/08, Grand Chamber Judgment of September 5, 2017):** The Grand Chamber held that Article 8 (private life) applies in the workplace context and that the dismissal of an employee for personal use of employer-provided communications must be assessed against the proportionality principle. States must strike a fair balance between the employer's right to monitor and the employee's right to privacy.

**Redfearn v. United Kingdom (Application No. 47335/06, Judgment of November 6, 2012):** The Court found a violation of Article 11 (freedom of association) where UK law permitted dismissal of an employee for membership in a political party (BNP) without requiring an employment tribunal to examine whether the dismissal was proportionate.

## Comparative Analysis

### Fundamental Divergence: At-Will vs. Just Cause

The most striking difference is between the U.S. at-will system and the just-cause systems of South Korea, Germany, and the EU framework. The U.S. approach presumes termination freedom as the default, with statutory carve-outs for specific protected grounds. South Korea and Germany presume employment continuity, requiring the employer to demonstrate justification. This divergence reflects fundamentally different conceptions of the employment relationship: the U.S. treats it as a freely terminable contract between equal parties, while the European and Korean models recognize the inherent power imbalance and treat employment as a social institution warranting special protection.

### Remedies

Germany and South Korea both provide reinstatement as the primary remedy, reflecting the just-cause model's emphasis on employment continuity. The United States, even in cases of unlawful termination, rarely orders reinstatement, instead relying on monetary damages (back pay, front pay, compensatory and punitive damages). This difference has practical consequences: the availability of reinstatement in Germany and South Korea creates a stronger deterrent against unlawful dismissal.

### Procedural Requirements

Germany imposes the most extensive procedural requirements, including mandatory works council consultation, social selection criteria, and graduated notice periods. South Korea requires written notice with stated reasons. The United States generally requires no notice or stated reasons, except in mass layoff situations under the WARN Act.
