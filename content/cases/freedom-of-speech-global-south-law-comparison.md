## Summary

Freedom of expression remains one of the most contested constitutional rights across the Global South, where postcolonial legacies, authoritarian governance traditions, and rapid digital transformation create legal landscapes fundamentally different from Western liberal frameworks. China's approach is defined by the Communist Party's doctrine of "public opinion guidance" (yulun daoxiang), operationalized through the Great Firewall (fanghuochangcheng), the Cybersecurity Law of 2017, and the more recent Personal Information Protection Law (PIPL) of 2021; Article 35 of the PRC Constitution nominally guarantees freedom of speech, press, assembly, and demonstration, yet these rights are subordinated to the "Four Cardinal Principles" and the practical supremacy of Party directives. India inherited British colonial sedition law through Section 124A of the Indian Penal Code (1860), which the Supreme Court nominally upheld in Kedar Nath Singh v. State of Bihar (AIR 1962 SC 955) but which was effectively suspended by the Court in S.G. Vombatkere v. Union of India (2022) pending government review; India's Article 19(1)(a) of the Constitution guarantees freedom of speech and expression, subject to the "reasonable restrictions" of Article 19(2), a framework that has generated thousands of contested cases. In Africa, South Africa's post-apartheid Constitution of 1996 provides robust protections under Section 16, explicitly excluding hate speech, incitement to violence, and advocacy of hatred based on race, ethnicity, gender, or religion -- a framework tested in cases like Qwelane v. South African Human Rights Commission (2021), while Nigeria's Cybercrimes (Prohibition, Prevention etc.) Act of 2015 and the controversial Twitter ban of June 2021 reveal a more restrictive trajectory. Latin American neo-constitutionalism has produced strong textual protections -- Brazil's Constitution of 1988 (Article 5, IX and XIV), Colombia's Constitution of 1991 (Article 20), and Mexico's reformed Article 6 -- yet confronts challenges from the Brazilian "Fake News" Bill (PL 2630/2020, enacted in modified form as the Brazilian Internet Freedom Law amendments), and from Mexico's systematic violence against journalists (Reporters Without Borders ranks Mexico among the deadliest countries for press). In Southeast Asia, Indonesia's Electronic Information and Transactions Law (UU ITE, Law No. 11 of 2008 as amended by Law No. 19 of 2016) and the Philippines' Cybercrime Prevention Act of 2012 (Republic Act No. 10175) illustrate how cybercrime and "online decency" provisions are weaponized against dissent, notwithstanding constitutional protections in the Indonesian Constitution (Article 28E) and the Philippine Constitution (Article III, Section 4).

## China

### Constitutional Framework and Party Supremacy

Article 35 of the Constitution of the People's Republic of China (1982, as amended through 2018) states: "Citizens of the People's Republic of China enjoy freedom of speech, press, assembly, association, procession, and demonstration." However, this provision must be read alongside the Preamble's assertion that the Chinese people are led by the Communist Party of China (CPC) under the guidance of Marxism-Leninism, Mao Zedong Thought, Deng Xiaoping Theory, the Theory of Three Represents, the Scientific Outlook on Development, and Xi Jinping Thought on Socialism with Chinese Characteristics for a New Era. Article 51 further provides that citizens exercising their freedoms "may not infringe upon the interests of the State, of society, or of the collective."

The Supreme People's Court does not exercise constitutional review in the manner of Western courts. The National People's Congress Standing Committee (NPCSC) holds the power of constitutional interpretation under Article 67, and in practice this power has never been exercised to expand individual expression rights.

### The Great Firewall and Digital Control

The Golden Shield Project (jindun gongcheng), initiated by the Ministry of Public Security in 1998 and operational since 2003, forms the technical backbone of China's internet censorship infrastructure. The Cybersecurity Law (wangluo anquan fa), effective June 1, 2017, formalized obligations for "network operators" (wangluo yunyingzhe) to verify user identities (real-name registration under Article 24), store data domestically (Article 37), and cooperate with state security investigations (Article 28).

The Data Security Law (shuju anquan fa), effective September 1, 2021, introduced a tiered classification of data and imposed strict controls on cross-border data transfers. The Personal Information Protection Law (geren xinxi baohu fa, PIPL), effective November 1, 2021, while ostensibly modeled on the GDPR, includes broad national security exemptions (Article 4 combined with Article 73) that enable state surveillance.

Blocked platforms include Google, Facebook, Twitter/X, YouTube, Wikipedia (Chinese-language edition since April 2019), and thousands of foreign news sites. The Regulations on the Administration of Internet News Information Services (2017) require all entities publishing news online to obtain licenses from the Cyberspace Administration of China (CAC).

### Criminal Provisions

Article 105 of the Criminal Law (xingfa) criminalizes "inciting subversion of state power" with sentences of up to 15 years. Article 293(1)(4), the "picking quarrels and provoking trouble" (xunxin zishi) provision, has become the most commonly used charge against online speech, applied in cases ranging from criticizing COVID-19 policies to sharing politically sensitive historical information. The 2013 Supreme People's Court judicial interpretation established that social media posts viewed 5,000 times or reposted 500 times constitute "serious circumstances" triggering criminal liability for defamation under Article 246.

### Notable Cases

Citizen journalist Zhang Zhan was sentenced to four years in December 2020 for "picking quarrels" after reporting on the early COVID-19 outbreak in Wuhan. Ren Zhiqiang, a CPC member and real estate executive, received an 18-year sentence in September 2020 after publishing an essay criticizing Xi Jinping's pandemic response. These cases illustrate the practical nullification of Article 35 protections.

## India

### Constitutional Architecture

Article 19(1)(a) of the Constitution of India guarantees all citizens the right to freedom of speech and expression. This is not absolute; Article 19(2) permits the State to impose "reasonable restrictions" in the interests of "the sovereignty and integrity of India, the security of the State, friendly relations with foreign States, public order, decency or morality, or in relation to contempt of court, defamation, or incitement to an offence."

The Supreme Court in Romesh Thappar v. State of Madras (AIR 1950 SC 124) established early that "freedom of speech and of the press lay at the foundation of all democratic organisations." The First Amendment to the Constitution (1951) expanded the permissible grounds of restriction, adding "public order" and "incitement to an offence" -- an expansion prompted partly by the desire to restrict communal speech during Partition-era tensions.

### Sedition: Section 124A IPC

Section 124A of the Indian Penal Code (1860) -- drafted by Thomas Babington Macaulay and enacted in 1870 specifically to suppress the Indian independence movement -- criminalized bringing or attempting to bring "hatred or contempt, or excit[ing] or attempt[ing] to excite disaffection towards the Government established by law in India." Conviction carries imprisonment up to life.

In Kedar Nath Singh v. State of Bihar (AIR 1962 SC 955), a five-judge constitutional bench upheld Section 124A but read it down to apply only where speech constituted "incitement to violence or the tendency or the intention to create public disorder." Despite this narrowing construction, Section 124A was applied in over 13,000 cases between 2010 and 2021 (National Crime Records Bureau data), disproportionately against journalists, activists, and political opponents.

In S.G. Vombatkere v. Union of India (2022), the Supreme Court effectively suspended Section 124A, directing the Union Government to reconsider the provision. The Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS), 2023, which replaced the IPC effective July 1, 2024, introduced Section 152 (acts endangering sovereignty, unity, and integrity of India), which critics argue repackages sedition under a different label.

### Digital Speech: Section 66A and the IT Act

The landmark decision in Shreya Singhal v. Union of India (AIR 2015 SC 1523) struck down Section 66A of the Information Technology Act, 2000, which had criminalized sending "offensive" messages through communication devices. Justice Nariman, writing for the Court, distinguished between "discussion," "advocacy," and "incitement," holding that only the last could constitutionally be restricted. Despite this ruling, research by the Internet Freedom Foundation documented continued arrests under the struck-down provision through 2023.

The Information Technology (Intermediary Guidelines and Digital Media Ethics Code) Rules, 2021, imposed compliance requirements on social media platforms including traceability of "first originator" of messages (Rule 4(2)), which WhatsApp challenged as incompatible with end-to-end encryption. Rule 3(1)(b) requires removal of content that threatens "the unity, integrity, defence, security or sovereignty of India."

### Internet Shutdowns

India leads the world in internet shutdowns. The Software Freedom Law Centre documented over 700 shutdowns between 2012 and 2023. The Supreme Court in Anuradha Bhasin v. Union of India (2020) 3 SCC 637 held that internet access is a fundamental right under Articles 19(1)(a) and 19(1)(g), and that shutdown orders must satisfy proportionality review and be published. The prolonged Kashmir shutdown (August 2019 - March 2020) was the longest internet blackout in any democracy.

## Africa

### South Africa: Post-Apartheid Expression Rights

Section 16(1) of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa (1996) guarantees the right to freedom of expression, including "freedom of the press and other media," "freedom to receive or impart information or ideas," "freedom of artistic creativity," and "academic freedom and freedom of scientific research." Section 16(2) explicitly carves out exceptions: propaganda for war, incitement of imminent violence, and "advocacy of hatred that is based on race, ethnicity, gender or religion, and that constitutes incitement to cause harm."

The Constitutional Court's decision in Islamic Unity Convention v. Independent Broadcasting Authority (2002 (4) SA 294 (CC)) established that hate speech regulation must be interpreted narrowly to avoid chilling legitimate expression. In Laugh It Off Promotions CC v. SAB International (2006 (1) SA 144 (CC)), the Court held that trademark protection could not override the right to parody as an exercise of free expression.

The watershed case of Qwelane v. South African Human Rights Commission (2021 (6) SA 579 (CC)) addressed Section 10 of the Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act (PEPUDA), 2000 (the Equality Act). Jon Qwelane, a journalist, had published a homophobic column in 2008. The Constitutional Court found the original Section 10 overbroad and unconstitutional but issued a reading-in that prohibited speech that "could reasonably be construed to demonstrate a clear intention to be hurtful, to incite harm, or to promote or propagate hatred." The Court developed a four-part test incorporating context, the identity of the speaker and audience, the content of the expression, and its likely impact.

### Nigeria: Constitutional Promise and Statutory Restriction

Section 39 of the Nigerian Constitution (1999) guarantees the right to freedom of expression and the press, subject to laws "reasonably justifiable in a democratic society" for the purpose of "preventing the disclosure of information received in confidence, maintaining the authority and independence of courts, or regulating telephony, wireless broadcasting, television or the exhibition of cinematograph films."

The Cybercrimes (Prohibition, Prevention etc.) Act (2015) has become the primary instrument for restricting online speech. Section 24 criminalizes sending messages that are "grossly offensive, pornographic, or of an indecent, obscene, or menacing character," with penalties of up to 3 years imprisonment or a fine of N7 million. Amnesty International documented dozens of cases where Section 24 was used against journalists and critics between 2015 and 2024.

The Federal Government's ban on Twitter (June 4 - January 13, 2021-22) following the platform's deletion of President Buhari's tweet was challenged in Incorporated Trustees of the Socio-Economic Rights and Accountability Project (SERAP) v. Federal Government of Nigeria (ECW/CCJ/JUD/02/22), where the ECOWAS Community Court of Justice held that the ban violated the right to freedom of expression under Article 9 of the African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights.

### African Charter Framework

Article 9 of the African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights (Banjul Charter, 1981) provides: "Every individual shall have the right to receive information" and "every individual shall have the right to express and disseminate his opinions within the law." The African Commission on Human and Peoples' Rights adopted the Declaration of Principles on Freedom of Expression and Access to Information in Africa (2019), which addresses internet shutdowns, surveillance, and whistleblower protection. The African Court on Human and Peoples' Rights in Lohé Issa Konaté v. Burkina Faso (Application No. 004/2013, 2014) held that criminal defamation laws imposing custodial sentences for expression directed at public officials violate the African Charter.

## Latin America

### Brazilian Constitutional Framework

The Brazilian Constitution of 1988 (Constituicao Federal, CF) provides extensive speech protections. Article 5(IV) guarantees "the free expression of thought, anonymity being forbidden"; Article 5(IX) ensures "the free expression of intellectual, artistic, scientific, and communications activity, independent of censorship or license"; Article 5(XIV) guarantees "access to information." Article 220 provides that "the expression of thought, creation, expression, and information, in any form, process, or vehicle, shall not suffer any restriction," subject only to constitutional provisions regarding minors' protection (Article 220, para. 3) and advertising regulation (Article 220, para. 4).

The Supremo Tribunal Federal (STF) in ADPF 130 (2009) struck down the entirety of the Press Law of 1967 (Lei de Imprensa, Law No. 5,250/1967), a military-era statute, holding it incompatible with the 1988 Constitution's commitment to free expression. In ADI 4815 (2015), the STF held that prior authorization from the biographical subject was not required for publication of biographies, finding such a requirement an unconstitutional form of prior censorship.

### The Fake News Bill and Platform Regulation

PL 2630/2020, known as the "Fake News Bill" (Lei das Fake News), was introduced by Senator Alessandro Vieira following concerns about disinformation in the 2018 presidential election. The bill underwent extensive modification and was partially incorporated into broader internet regulation amendments. Key provisions include: requirements for social media platforms to maintain transparency reports on content moderation; prohibitions on inauthentic accounts and mass automated messaging; and obligations on platforms to preserve content that is subject to judicial or administrative proceedings.

The STF played an unusually direct role in content regulation following the January 8, 2023 insurrection at the Praca dos Tres Poderes, ordering the blocking of dozens of social media accounts and establishing an ongoing investigation (Inquerito No. 4781) into digital militias and disinformation networks. Minister Alexandre de Moraes's orders blocking the platform X (formerly Twitter) in Brazil in August-September 2024 following the company's refusal to comply with court orders generated significant international controversy regarding the boundaries of judicial power over global platforms.

### Colombian and Mexican Frameworks

Colombia's Constitution of 1991 (Article 20) guarantees freedom of expression and access to information, with the Constitutional Court (Corte Constitucional) developing an extensive proportionality jurisprudence. In Sentencia T-391/07, the Court established a three-tiered framework: political speech receives the highest protection, commercial speech intermediate, and speech involving private affairs of non-public figures the lowest. Colombia's unique challenge is violence against journalists; the Inter-American Commission on Human Rights (IACHR) Special Rapporteur for Freedom of Expression has repeatedly condemned attacks, with Fundacion para la Libertad de Prensa (FLIP) documenting hundreds of threats annually.

Mexico's constitutional reform of 2007 (Article 6) established the right to information as a fundamental right enforceable through the transparency framework administered by the Instituto Nacional de Transparencia, Acceso a la Informacion y Proteccion de Datos Personales (INAI). Despite strong textual protections, Mexico remains among the world's deadliest countries for journalists; the Committee to Protect Journalists documented over 60 journalist murders between 2019 and 2025, with impunity exceeding 90% according to Article 19's annual reports.

## Southeast Asia

### Indonesian UU ITE and Blasphemy Framework

Article 28E of the Indonesian Constitution (as amended in 2000) provides that "every person shall have the right to freedom to hold beliefs and to express his/her views and thoughts, in accordance with his/her conscience." Article 28F guarantees the right "to communicate and to obtain information to develop his/her personal and social environment, and shall have the right to seek, obtain, possess, store, process, and convey information by using all available channels."

The Electronic Information and Transactions Law (UU ITE, Law No. 11 of 2008, amended by Law No. 19 of 2016) has become the primary instrument for speech prosecution. Article 27(3) criminalizes online defamation with up to 4 years imprisonment and fines up to Rp 750 million. Article 28(2) criminalizes the intentional dissemination of "information aimed at inflicting hatred or dissension on individuals and/or certain groups of community based on ethnic groups, religions, races, and inter-groups (SARA)."

The Southeast Asia Freedom of Expression Network (SAFEnet) documented over 400 UU ITE criminal cases between 2008 and 2023, with a conviction rate exceeding 90%. Amendments in 2024 (Law No. 1 of 2024) introduced limited reforms including reduced maximum sentences and a requirement for mediation before prosecution, but retained the fundamental criminal defamation framework.

Indonesia's blasphemy provision -- Article 156a of the Criminal Code (KUHP), upheld by the Constitutional Court (Mahkamah Konstitusi) in Decision No. 140/PUU-VII/2009 -- criminalizes expressions of hostility, hatred, or contempt toward one of Indonesia's six officially recognized religions. Former Jakarta Governor Basuki Tjahaja Purnama (Ahok) was sentenced to two years in May 2017 for blasphemy against Islam, in a case that highlighted the intersection of religious politics and expression rights.

### Philippine Framework

The Philippine Constitution of 1987 (Article III, Section 4) provides that "no law shall be passed abridging the freedom of speech, of expression, or of the press, or the right of the people peaceably to assemble and petition the government for redress of grievances." The Supreme Court in Chavez v. Gonzales (G.R. No. 168338, 2008) reaffirmed that prior restraint bears a "heavy presumption of unconstitutionality."

The Cybercrime Prevention Act of 2012 (Republic Act No. 10175) included online libel provisions (Section 4(c)(4)) with penalties one degree higher than the Revised Penal Code's libel provisions (prision correccional, 6 months to 6 years). In Disini v. Secretary of Justice (G.R. No. 203335, February 18, 2014), the Supreme Court upheld the constitutionality of online libel but struck down provisions on aiding or abetting cybercrime (Section 5 insofar as it applied to online libel) and the take-down clause without judicial intervention.

The prosecution of journalist Maria Ressa under the Cybercrime Prevention Act -- convicted in June 2020 of cyber libel for a 2012 Rappler article, a verdict upheld by the Court of Appeals in 2022 before being reversed by the Supreme Court in January 2023 -- became an internationally prominent test case for press freedom. Ressa received the Nobel Peace Prize in 2021 for her efforts to safeguard freedom of expression.

## Comparative Analysis

### Structural Patterns

Three fundamental patterns emerge from this Global South comparison. First, every jurisdiction examined possesses formal constitutional guarantees of expression rights, yet the effective protection varies dramatically based on institutional factors -- judicial independence, prosecutorial discretion, and the political economy of media ownership. China's Article 35 and the Philippines' Article III, Section 4 share similar textual breadth, but produce radically different outcomes because of the presence or absence of independent judicial review.

Second, "cybercrime" legislation has become the preferred vehicle for speech restriction across the Global South. China's Cybersecurity Law, India's IT Act, Nigeria's Cybercrimes Act, Indonesia's UU ITE, and the Philippines' RA 10175 all demonstrate a pattern where digital regulation initially framed around fraud, hacking, or child exploitation is expanded to encompass political speech, defamation, and religious expression. This "cybercrime creep" represents a distinctly 21st-century challenge to expression rights.

Third, the Global South reveals a distinctive tension between collective harmony and individual expression absent from Western liberal frameworks. China's emphasis on "social harmony" (shehui hexie), India's "reasonable restrictions" for "public order," Indonesia's Pancasila-based communal values, and South Africa's constitutional exclusion of hate speech all reflect philosophical traditions in which expression rights are understood relationally rather than individualistically. This is not merely a deficit of liberal rights but represents an alternative jurisprudential tradition deserving of analysis on its own terms.

### The Disinformation Challenge

Brazil's Fake News Bill, India's IT Rules 2021, Nigeria's responses to alleged disinformation, and Indonesia's content moderation regulations reveal a shared challenge: combating genuine disinformation while preventing governments from weaponizing "anti-fake-news" measures against political opposition. The Inter-American Court of Human Rights in its Advisory Opinion OC-5/85 established that "the same concept of public order in a democratic society requires the guarantee of the widest possible circulation of news, ideas and opinions," a principle that applies across the Global South but is inconsistently honored.
