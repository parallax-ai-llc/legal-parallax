## Summary

Good Samaritan laws -- statutes that provide legal protection to persons who voluntarily render emergency assistance to those who are injured, ill, or in peril, and in some jurisdictions impose affirmative duties to rescue -- reflect fundamental choices about the relationship between individual liberty, social solidarity, and tort liability that differ markedly across legal traditions. South Korea imposes an affirmative duty to rescue under the Emergency Medical Service Act (응급의료에 관한 법률) and provides rescuer immunity under the same statute, while the Criminal Act (형법) Article 271 criminalizes abandonment of persons in danger, and the Civil Act (민법) provisions on unjust enrichment and negotiorum gestio (사무관리) provide a framework for rescuer reimbursement. The United States follows the traditional common law rule of no duty to rescue, with all fifty states having enacted Good Samaritan statutes providing immunity from civil liability for good-faith emergency assistance, the federal Cardiac Arrest Survival Act (42 U.S.C. Section 238q) protecting AED users, and the federal Good Samaritan Food Donation Act (42 U.S.C. Section 1791) protecting food donors, while Vermont (12 V.S.A. Section 519) and Minnesota (Minn. Stat. Section 604A.01) are among the few states imposing a duty to rescue with criminal penalties. Germany imposes a broad duty to rescue under StGB Section 323c (unterlassene Hilfeleistung), with criminal penalties of up to one year imprisonment for failure to assist, while BGB Section 680 provides rescuer immunity by lowering the standard of care to intentional misconduct and gross negligence for persons acting in negotiorum gestio during emergencies. At the European level, the ECHR has recognized positive obligations on states under Articles 2 and 3 to protect life and prevent inhuman treatment, many EU member states impose duties to rescue through criminal law, and the European Resuscitation Council has advocated for harmonized Good Samaritan protections to improve bystander CPR rates across the EU.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea combines a duty to rescue, rescuer immunity provisions, and a comprehensive emergency medical services system.

### Emergency Medical Service Act (응급의료에 관한 법률)

This Act, originally enacted in 1994 and substantially amended multiple times, provides the primary framework for Good Samaritan protections in Korea.

**Article 5-2 (Rescuer Immunity):** A person who provides emergency medical treatment in good faith to a person in an emergency situation shall not bear civil or criminal liability for damages arising from such treatment, unless the damage results from gross negligence or willful misconduct. This provision was strengthened in 2008 following public concern that fear of liability was deterring bystander rescue.

**Article 6 (Emergency Reporting Obligation):** Any person who discovers that another person is in an emergency medical situation is obligated to report the situation promptly to emergency services (119 emergency call).

### Criminal Act Provisions

Article 271 of the Criminal Act (형법) criminalizes abandonment of persons who require assistance due to age, infirmity, illness, or other circumstances. The abandonment offense requires a pre-existing duty of care (보호의무) or a special relationship. Article 275 imposes enhanced penalties when abandonment results in death or injury.

### Negotiorum Gestio (사무관리)

Civil Act Articles 734-740 establish the framework for management of affairs without mandate (사무관리), which applies when a rescuer acts on behalf of another in an emergency. The rescuer is entitled to reimbursement of expenses and may not be held to a strict standard of care. The Supreme Court has held that emergency rescuers acting under negotiorum gestio are subject only to the gross negligence standard.

### Cultural and Social Context

Despite legal protections, surveys conducted by the Korean Medical Association have indicated that many Korean citizens remain hesitant to provide emergency assistance due to fear of legal liability or lack of confidence in their skills. The government has responded with public awareness campaigns, expanded CPR training programs in schools and workplaces, and increased deployment of public access AEDs. The 2019 amendment to the School Health Act (학교보건법) mandated CPR training in all primary and secondary schools.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States adheres to the common law no-duty-to-rescue rule while providing extensive immunity for voluntary rescuers through statutory Good Samaritan protections.

### No Duty to Rescue

The general rule, stated in Restatement (Second) of Torts Section 314, is that there is no duty to take affirmative action for the aid or protection of another. The classic articulation appears in Buch v. Amory Manufacturing Co., 69 N.H. 257 (1898), and is illustrated by the hypothetical that an expert swimmer may watch a stranger drown without legal liability.

Exceptions to the no-duty rule include:

| Exception | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Special relationship | Common carriers, innkeepers, employers, school-child, parent-child |
| Voluntary undertaking | Once rescue is begun, rescuer must act with reasonable care (Restatement Section 323) |
| Creation of peril | A person who creates a dangerous situation has a duty to rescue |
| Statutory duty | Vermont, Minnesota, Rhode Island, Wisconsin, and Hawaii impose rescue duties |

### State Good Samaritan Statutes

All fifty states and the District of Columbia have enacted Good Samaritan statutes providing immunity from civil liability for persons who voluntarily provide emergency assistance. The scope of protection varies:

**Broad Protection (majority):** Most states protect any person who renders emergency care at the scene of an emergency from liability for ordinary negligence. Protection is lost for gross negligence, willful misconduct, or wanton conduct.

**California Health and Safety Code Section 1799.102:** Provides immunity for persons who render emergency care at the scene of an emergency, other than in a hospital or other medical facility, regardless of whether the person has medical training.

**Limited Protection (minority):** Some states limit protection to specific categories of rescuers (trained medical personnel, certified first responders) or specific types of assistance (CPR, AED use, hemorrhage control).

### Federal Good Samaritan Statutes

**Cardiac Arrest Survival Act (42 U.S.C. Section 238q, 2000):** Provides immunity from civil liability for persons who use an AED in a perceived medical emergency, provided the person acts as an ordinary reasonably prudent person and the AED device is properly maintained.

**Good Samaritan Food Donation Act (42 U.S.C. Section 1791, 1996):** Protects good-faith food donors from civil and criminal liability when donating apparently wholesome food to nonprofit organizations for distribution to the needy. Liability attaches only for gross negligence or intentional misconduct.

**Volunteer Protection Act (42 U.S.C. Section 14501, 1997):** Provides federal immunity for volunteers of nonprofit organizations and governmental entities acting within the scope of their responsibilities, for harm caused by ordinary negligence.

### Duty-to-Rescue States

Vermont's statute (12 V.S.A. Section 519) is the most well-known, providing: "A person who knows that another is exposed to grave physical harm shall, to the extent that the same can be rendered without danger or peril to himself or without interference with important duties owed to others, give reasonable assistance to the exposed person." Violation is punishable by a fine of up to $100. Minnesota imposes a similar duty (Minn. Stat. Section 604A.01) with a petty misdemeanor classification.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany imposes one of the world's most robust duty-to-rescue regimes, backed by criminal sanctions and complemented by comprehensive rescuer protections.

### StGB Section 323c -- Unterlassene Hilfeleistung

Section 323c(1) StGB provides: "Whoever does not render assistance in the case of an accident or common danger or emergency, although this was necessary and could reasonably be expected under the circumstances, in particular without significant personal danger and without violating other important duties, shall be punished with imprisonment for a term not exceeding one year or with a fine" (Wer bei Unglücksfällen oder gemeiner Gefahr oder Not nicht Hilfe leistet, obwohl dies erforderlich und ihm den Umständen nach zuzumuten, insbesondere ohne erhebliche eigene Gefahr und ohne Verletzung anderer wichtiger Pflichten möglich ist, wird mit Freiheitsstrafe bis zu einem Jahr oder mit Geldstrafe bestraft).

Section 323c(2), added in 2017, specifically criminalizes obstructing persons providing or intending to provide first aid, punishable with imprisonment of up to one year or a fine.

### Scope of the Duty

The duty under Section 323c applies to every person who becomes aware of an emergency situation. The duty is limited by reasonable expectations (Zumutbarkeit) -- a person is not required to rescue if doing so would create significant personal danger or violate other important duties. The BGH has held that the duty includes:

- Calling emergency services (112)
- Providing first aid within the person's capabilities
- Securing the accident scene
- Staying with the injured person until professional help arrives

### Rescuer Immunity (BGB Section 680)

BGB Section 680 provides enhanced protection for emergency rescuers acting under negotiorum gestio: "If the management of affairs [without mandate] has the purpose of averting a danger threatening the principal, the manager is only liable for intentional misconduct and gross negligence" (Bezweckt die Geschäftsführung die Abwendung einer dem Geschäftsherrn drohenden dringenden Gefahr, so hat der Geschäftsführer nur Vorsatz und grobe Fahrlässigkeit zu vertreten). This effectively immunizes good-faith rescuers from liability for ordinary negligence.

### Insurance Protection

Persons who are injured while providing emergency assistance are covered by the statutory accident insurance system (gesetzliche Unfallversicherung) under the Social Code Book VII (SGB VII) Section 2(1)(13). This insurance covers medical treatment, rehabilitation, and compensation for the rescuer's injuries, regardless of whether the rescue was successful.

### CPR and AED

Germany has one of the lowest bystander CPR rates in Western Europe (approximately 40% as of 2024, compared to over 70% in Scandinavian countries). This has prompted legislative and educational initiatives, including the "Prüfen - Rufen - Drücken" (Check - Call - Push) campaign and expansion of public access AED networks.

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR has addressed rescue duties through the positive obligations framework.

**Osman v. United Kingdom (Application No. 23452/94, Judgment of October 28, 1998):** The Court established that Article 2 creates a positive obligation on states to take preventive operational measures to protect life where authorities know or ought to know of a real and immediate risk to life.

**Mastromatteo v. Italy (Application No. 37703/97, Grand Chamber Judgment of October 24, 2002):** The Court confirmed that the state's positive obligation under Article 2 extends to taking reasonable measures to protect individuals from the criminal acts of third parties, including institutional duty-to-rescue obligations.

### EU Member State Duties to Rescue

The majority of EU member states impose criminal duties to rescue, reflecting the civil law tradition:

| Country | Statute | Maximum Penalty |
|---------|---------|----------------|
| France | Code pénal Art. 223-6 | 5 years imprisonment, EUR 75,000 fine |
| Germany | StGB Section 323c | 1 year imprisonment or fine |
| Italy | Codice Penale Art. 593 | Up to 1 year imprisonment |
| Spain | Código Penal Art. 195 | Fine of 3-12 months |
| Belgium | Code pénal Art. 422bis | Up to 1 year imprisonment |
| Netherlands | Wetboek van Strafrecht Art. 450 | Up to 3 months imprisonment |
| Poland | Kodeks karny Art. 162 | Up to 3 years imprisonment |

Common law jurisdictions (Ireland, United Kingdom before Brexit) generally do not impose duties to rescue.

### European Resuscitation Council

The European Resuscitation Council (ERC) has published guidelines recommending that all EU member states adopt legislation providing: (1) legal immunity for lay rescuers performing CPR, (2) mandatory training in first aid and CPR in schools, and (3) public access to AEDs. The ERC's 2021 guidelines note that fear of legal consequences remains a significant barrier to bystander CPR in many European countries and that Good Samaritan legislation is associated with higher bystander intervention rates.

## Comparative Analysis

### Duty vs. No Duty

The most fundamental divergence is between civil law jurisdictions (South Korea, Germany, most EU states) that impose affirmative duties to rescue and common law jurisdictions (United States, historically) that reject such duties:

**Civil Law Tradition:** The duty to rescue is grounded in social solidarity principles and the recognition that minimal intervention (calling for help) imposes negligible burdens while potentially saving lives.

**Common Law Tradition:** The no-duty rule reflects the priority of individual autonomy and the concern that imposing affirmative duties creates difficult enforcement questions and potentially exposes rescuers to liability.

### Immunity Scope

All four jurisdictions provide some form of rescuer immunity, but the scope varies. The United States provides the broadest statutory immunity through comprehensive Good Samaritan statutes in all 50 states. Germany provides effective immunity through BGB Section 680's gross negligence standard. South Korea provides similar protection through the Emergency Medical Service Act. The ECHR does not directly address rescuer immunity but encourages states to ensure that legal frameworks do not deter rescue.

### Enforcement Reality

Despite criminal penalties for failure to rescue in civil law jurisdictions, prosecutions are relatively rare. German courts prosecute Section 323c violations primarily in cases of egregious failure to assist (such as failing to call emergency services when witnessing a traffic accident) rather than failure to perform technical first aid. The practical effect of the duty-to-rescue statutes may lie more in their cultural and educational impact than in direct criminal enforcement.
