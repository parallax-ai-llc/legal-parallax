## Summary

Freedom of movement -- encompassing the right to travel within a state's territory, the right to leave and return to one's country, and the right to choose one's place of residence -- is recognized as a fundamental right in virtually all democratic constitutions and international human rights instruments, yet its scope is invariably subject to limitations related to national security, public health, criminal justice, and immigration control, with the COVID-19 pandemic (2020-2023) having tested the boundaries of permissible restrictions on an unprecedented scale. South Korea guarantees freedom of movement under Article 14 of the Constitution (대한민국 헌법 제14조), which provides that "all citizens shall enjoy the freedom of residence and the freedom to move at will," while the Passport Act (여권법) governs the right to leave and enter the country; unique to South Korea is the complete prohibition on travel to North Korea under the Inter-Korean Exchange and Cooperation Act (남북교류협력에 관한 법률), and the Constitutional Court has addressed movement restrictions in the context of military service obligations and passport revocation. The United States protects freedom of movement through the Privileges and Immunities Clause (Article IV), the Commerce Clause, and the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment, with the Supreme Court recognizing a constitutional right to interstate travel in Saenz v. Roe, 526 U.S. 489 (1999), while international travel is protected but subject to passport restrictions under Haig v. Agee, 453 U.S. 280 (1981); the COVID-19 pandemic prompted significant litigation over state-imposed quarantine and travel restrictions. Germany guarantees freedom of movement under Article 11 of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz), which provides the right to move freely throughout the federal territory and to choose one's place of residence, subject to restrictions that may be imposed only by statute and only in cases where adequate means of subsistence are lacking, where the restriction is necessary to avert an imminent danger to the existence or the free democratic basic order, or to combat epidemic dangers, natural disasters, or grave accidents; Germany's participation in the Schengen Area enables passport-free travel across 29 European countries. The European framework protects freedom of movement under ECHR Protocol 4 Article 2, EU Treaty provisions on free movement of persons (Articles 20-21 TFEU), and the Schengen Agreement and Borders Code, with the Court of Justice of the European Union having developed the most extensive supranational freedom of movement jurisprudence through cases from Van Gend en Loos (1963) through recent decisions on the limits of free movement during health emergencies.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Article 14 of the Constitution provides: "All citizens shall enjoy the freedom of residence and the freedom to move at will." This provision protects both the right to choose one's place of residence (거주의 자유) and the right to physical movement (이전의 자유), including the right to travel domestically and internationally.

### Passport and International Travel

The Passport Act (여권법) governs the issuance and revocation of passports. The Minister of Foreign Affairs may refuse to issue or may revoke a passport on national security grounds, when the applicant has unpaid taxes exceeding a certain threshold, or when the applicant is subject to criminal proceedings.

The Inter-Korean Exchange and Cooperation Act (남북교류협력에 관한 법률) prohibits South Korean citizens from traveling to North Korea without prior government approval. Unauthorized travel to North Korea is punishable by imprisonment. The Constitutional Court has upheld this restriction as a legitimate limitation on freedom of movement given the unique security situation on the Korean Peninsula.

### Resident Registration System (주민등록제도)

All South Korean citizens must register their place of residence under the Resident Registration Act (주민등록법). The resident registration number (주민등록번호) serves as a universal identifier for government services, banking, and telecommunications. While the registration system does not restrict movement, it creates a comprehensive tracking framework that has implications for privacy and freedom of movement.

### COVID-19 Restrictions

South Korea's COVID-19 response prioritized testing, tracing, and quarantine over domestic movement restrictions. The government did not impose domestic lockdowns but implemented mandatory quarantine for international arrivals, electronic monitoring of quarantined individuals, and social distancing guidelines. The Infectious Disease Control and Prevention Act (감염병의 예방 및 관리에 관한 법률) provided the legal basis for these measures. The Constitutional Court has not directly ruled on the constitutionality of COVID-19 movement restrictions.

### Military Service Movement Restrictions

Men subject to military service obligations face restrictions on international travel. The Military Service Act (병역법) requires men of military service age to obtain permission before traveling abroad for extended periods and may restrict passport issuance for draft evaders.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The Constitution does not contain an explicit right to travel, but the Supreme Court has recognized a constitutional right to interstate travel rooted in multiple constitutional provisions:

**Saenz v. Roe, 526 U.S. 489 (1999):** The Court identified three components of the right to travel: (1) the right of citizens to enter and leave any state (derived from Article IV Privileges and Immunities Clause); (2) the right to be treated as a welcome visitor rather than an unfriendly alien (derived from the Commerce Clause); and (3) the right of new residents to be treated equally with established residents (derived from the Fourteenth Amendment Citizenship Clause).

### International Travel

**Haig v. Agee, 453 U.S. 280 (1981):** The Supreme Court upheld the Secretary of State's authority to revoke passports when the holder's activities abroad are causing or are likely to cause serious damage to the national security or foreign policy of the United States.

**Kent v. Dulles, 357 U.S. 116 (1958):** The Court held that the right to travel abroad is a liberty interest protected by the Fifth Amendment Due Process Clause, limiting the Secretary of State's discretion to deny passports.

The FAST Act (2015) requires the State Department to deny or revoke passports of individuals with seriously delinquent tax debt exceeding $62,000.

### COVID-19 and Freedom of Movement

The COVID-19 pandemic prompted extensive litigation over movement restrictions:

**State Travel Restrictions:** Multiple states imposed quarantine requirements for arriving travelers. In Bayley's Campground v. Mills (D. Me. 2020), a federal court upheld Maine's 14-day quarantine for out-of-state visitors. Other courts reached mixed results on interstate travel restrictions.

**Stay-at-Home Orders:** State and local stay-at-home orders were generally upheld under the states' police power, though courts in some jurisdictions struck down specific orders as overbroad or procedurally deficient.

### Immigration

The United States exercises broad sovereignty over immigration under the plenary power doctrine. Congress has near-absolute authority to determine immigration admissions, with the Supreme Court historically providing minimal judicial review of immigration decisions. The Immigration and Nationality Act (INA, 8 U.S.C. Section 1101 et seq.) establishes the framework for immigrant and nonimmigrant visa categories, admission, removal, and naturalization.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Article 11 of the Basic Law provides: "(1) All Germans shall have the right of free movement throughout the federal territory. (2) This right may be restricted only by or pursuant to a law, and only in cases in which the absence of adequate means of subsistence would result in a particular burden for the community, or in which such restriction is necessary to avert an imminent danger to the existence or the free democratic basic order of the Federation or of a Land, to combat the danger of an epidemic, to respond to a grave accident or a natural disaster, or to protect young persons from serious neglect."

### Schengen Area Participation

As a Schengen Area member, Germany has eliminated border controls with 28 other European states, enabling passport-free travel across most of the European continent. German citizens may travel, reside, and work freely in all EU/EEA member states under the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union provisions on free movement of persons.

### COVID-19 Bundesnotbremse

Germany's COVID-19 response involved significant movement restrictions under the Infektionsschutzgesetz (Infection Protection Act, IfSG). The Fourth Population Protection Act (Viertes Bevölkerungsschutzgesetz), enacted on April 22, 2021, introduced the "federal emergency brake" (Bundesnotbremse, Section 28b IfSG), which imposed automatic, nationally uniform restrictions when COVID-19 incidence rates exceeded specified thresholds, including nighttime curfews (ausgangsbeschränkungen) between 10 PM and 5 AM.

The Federal Constitutional Court upheld the Bundesnotbremse in its landmark decision of November 30, 2021 (1 BvR 781/21), finding that the contact and movement restrictions, while interfering with fundamental rights including freedom of movement (Article 11 GG), were proportionate and constitutionally justified in light of the state's duty to protect life and physical integrity under Article 2(2) GG.

### Fachkräfteeinwanderungsgesetz (Skilled Workers Immigration Act)

The Fachkräfteeinwanderungsgesetz, enacted in 2019 and substantially reformed in 2023, establishes a framework for labor migration by skilled workers from non-EU countries. The 2023 reform introduced a points-based Chancenkarte (opportunity card) for skilled workers seeking employment, expanded recognition of foreign qualifications, and facilitated family reunification.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Protocol 4, Article 2

"(1) Everyone lawfully within the territory of a State shall, within that territory, have the right to liberty of movement and freedom to choose his residence. (2) Everyone shall be free to leave any country, including his own. (3) No restrictions shall be placed on the exercise of these rights other than such as are in accordance with law and are necessary in a democratic society in the interests of national security or public safety, for the maintenance of ordre public, for the prevention of crime, for the protection of health or morals, or for the protection of the rights and freedoms of others. (4) The rights set forth in paragraph 1 may also be subject, in particular areas, to restrictions imposed in accordance with law and justified by the public interest in a democratic society."

### EU Free Movement of Persons

**Articles 20-21 TFEU and Directive 2004/38/EC:** EU citizens have the right to move and reside freely within the territory of the member states, subject to conditions and limitations. Directive 2004/38/EC establishes a graduated system of residence rights based on duration:

| Duration | Rights |
|----------|--------|
| Up to 3 months | Free residence with valid ID; no conditions |
| 3 months to 5 years | Residence as worker, self-employed, student, or self-sufficient person |
| Over 5 years | Permanent residence right; not subject to conditions |

### Schengen Area

The Schengen Area, established by the 1985 Schengen Agreement and incorporated into EU law by the Treaty of Amsterdam (1997), creates a zone of passport-free travel currently comprising 29 states (25 EU members plus Iceland, Liechtenstein, Norway, and Switzerland). Bulgaria and Romania fully joined in 2024. The Schengen Borders Code (Regulation 2016/399) governs the external border and permits temporary reintroduction of internal border controls in exceptional circumstances.

### COVID-19 and EU Free Movement

The COVID-19 pandemic prompted unprecedented restrictions on free movement within the EU. Member states unilaterally reintroduced internal border controls and imposed travel restrictions. The EU responded with coordination measures:

- Council Recommendation on a coordinated approach to restrictions on free movement (October 2020)
- EU Digital COVID Certificate (Regulation 2021/953): standardized vaccination, testing, and recovery certificates to facilitate safe cross-border movement
- Commission enforcement actions against disproportionate national restrictions

### Key ECHR Jurisprudence

**Stamose v. Bulgaria (Application No. 29713/05, 2012):** The Court held that Bulgaria's prohibition on a citizen leaving the country due to unpaid debts to a private entity violated Article 2 of Protocol 4, finding the restriction disproportionate.

**De Tommaso v. Italy (Application No. 43395/09, Grand Chamber, 2017):** The Grand Chamber held that preventive measures imposing residence obligations and movement restrictions on a person suspected of criminal activity violated Article 2 of Protocol 4 because the legal basis was insufficiently precise.

**Khlyustov v. Russia (Application No. 28975/05, 2013):** Found that a travel ban imposed on a criminal defendant during investigation violated Article 2 of Protocol 4 due to its excessive duration and the failure to periodically review its necessity.

## Comparative Analysis

### Internal Movement

All four jurisdictions guarantee free internal movement, though the scope varies. The EU/Schengen framework provides the broadest zone of free movement, spanning 29 states. Germany's Article 11 GG is notable for explicitly enumerating the permissible grounds for restriction. The United States' right to interstate travel is constitutionally grounded but not textually explicit. South Korea's unique prohibition on travel to North Korea adds a geopolitical dimension not present in the other jurisdictions.

### COVID-19 Lessons

The pandemic revealed different approaches to balancing movement rights against public health. Germany's Bundesnotbremse represented a centralized, legislatively authorized approach that was upheld by the Constitutional Court. The United States experienced a fragmented state-by-state approach with inconsistent judicial review. South Korea avoided domestic movement restrictions by relying on testing, tracing, and quarantine. The EU's temporary border controls exposed the fragility of the Schengen achievement, though the Digital COVID Certificate represented an innovative coordination mechanism.

### Immigration and Free Movement

The EU's supranational free movement framework has no equivalent in the other jurisdictions. EU citizenship creates rights that transcend traditional immigration categories. South Korea, the United States, and non-EU states retain sovereign control over immigration admissions, though all are increasingly adopting skills-based approaches to labor migration.
