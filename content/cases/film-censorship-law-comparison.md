## Summary

Film classification and censorship represent a critical intersection of freedom of expression, cultural policy, and the protection of minors, with jurisdictions adopting fundamentally different approaches reflecting their constitutional traditions and political values. South Korea operates a mandatory government-supervised classification system administered by the Korea Media Rating Board (영상물등급위원회, KMRB), which replaced the earlier censorship board following the Constitutional Court's landmark 1996 ruling striking down prior restraint of motion pictures. The United States has no government film censorship system; instead, the Motion Picture Association (MPA, formerly MPAA) operates a voluntary industry self-regulatory rating system, protected by the First Amendment's prohibition on government-imposed prior restraints as established in Freedman v. Maryland, 380 U.S. 51 (1965). Germany operates the Freiwillige Selbstkontrolle der Filmwirtschaft (FSK, Voluntary Self-Regulation of the Film Industry), a nominally voluntary but practically mandatory classification body, supplemented by the Jugendschutzgesetz (JuSchG, Youth Protection Act) and the Bundeszentrale für Kinder- und Jugendmedienschutz (BzKJ, Federal Agency for the Protection of Children and Youth in the Media). At the EU level, the Audiovisual Media Services Directive (AVMSD, Directive (EU) 2018/1808) establishes minimum standards for the protection of minors in audiovisual content, while the ECHR's Article 10 jurisprudence defines the boundaries of permissible state interference with artistic expression.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's film classification system has evolved from outright censorship to a classification-based regime following landmark constitutional jurisprudence:

- **Promotion of Motion Pictures and Video Products Act (영화 및 비디오물의 진흥에 관한 법률, Act No. 18973):** The primary statute governing film classification. Article 29 mandates that all films intended for public exhibition must receive a rating from the KMRB prior to exhibition. Article 29(2) establishes five rating categories: All Ages (전체관람가), 12+, 15+, 18+ (청소년관람불가, "Youth Viewing Prohibited"), and Restricted Exhibition (제한상영가). Article 50 criminalizes exhibition of unrated films with penalties of up to 2 years imprisonment or KRW 20 million in fines.
- **Restricted Exhibition Rating (제한상영가):** The most controversial category, applied to films deemed unsuitable for general exhibition but not meeting the threshold for outright prohibition. Films receiving this rating may only be exhibited in designated venues (제한상영관), face severe restrictions on advertising and promotion, and are effectively marginalized from mainstream commercial distribution. Critics argue that the Restricted Exhibition category functions as a de facto ban.
- **Korea Media Rating Board (영상물등급위원회, KMRB):** A statutory body established under the Motion Pictures Act, consisting of 9 commissioners appointed by the President on recommendation of the National Assembly and relevant ministries. The KMRB rates films based on criteria including: violence, sexual content, profanity, drug use, and potential to harm youth development. Decisions are subject to administrative appeal and judicial review.
- **Game Industry Promotion Act (게임산업진흥에 관한 법률):** Extends age-based rating requirements to video games, administered by the Game Rating and Administration Committee (게임물관리위원회, GRAC).

### Constitutional Jurisprudence

The Constitutional Court has played a decisive role in shaping Korea's film classification system:

- **Constitutional Court Decision 93 Hun-Ka 13 (4 October 1996):** The landmark ruling striking down the mandatory pre-screening (사전검열) system as unconstitutional prior restraint. The Court held that Article 21(2) of the Constitution ("censorship of speech and the press shall not be permitted") imposed an absolute prohibition on prior censorship by government bodies. The Court distinguished between impermissible prior censorship (사전검열, where government approval is required before expression) and permissible classification (등급분류, where content is categorized but not prohibited). This decision led to the replacement of the Public Performance Ethics Committee (공연윤리위원회) with the KMRB.
- **Constitutional Court Decision 2006 Hun-Ka 20 (2008):** Upheld the constitutionality of the Restricted Exhibition rating, finding that the category did not constitute prior censorship because films were not prohibited outright but merely subject to exhibition restrictions. However, the Court emphasized that the classification must be administered through fair procedures and subject to judicial review.
- **Constitutional Court Decision 2013 Hun-Ma 619 (2016):** Ruled on the KMRB's refusal to classify a documentary film critical of the government, finding that the Board's broad discretion in applying the Restricted Exhibition category must be exercised in a manner consistent with freedom of expression.

### Notable Cases

- **"Moebius" Rating Controversy (2013):** Director Kim Ki-duk's film was initially refused classification by the KMRB due to explicit sexual content. The Seoul Administrative Court overturned the decision, and the film was eventually rated Restricted Exhibition. The case highlighted tensions between artistic expression and the classification system.
- **"The Interview" (2014):** The KMRB's handling of the controversial Seth Rogen/James Franco comedy about a plot to assassinate North Korean leader Kim Jong-un drew attention to the geopolitical dimensions of film classification in Korea.
- **"Burning" (2018) and International Festival Films:** Korean filmmakers have complained that the classification system disadvantages art-house films that receive high ratings, limiting their domestic distribution while they succeed internationally.

## United States

### Constitutional Framework

The United States' approach to film classification is defined by the First Amendment's robust protection of expression:

- **First Amendment (U.S. Const. amend. I):** "Congress shall make no law... abridging the freedom of speech, or of the press." Applied to state governments through the Fourteenth Amendment.
- **Prior Restraint Doctrine:** Government-imposed prior restraints on expression bear "a heavy presumption against constitutional validity" (Bantam Books, Inc. v. Sullivan, 372 U.S. 58, 70 (1963)). In the film context, the Supreme Court in Freedman v. Maryland, 380 U.S. 51 (1965), struck down Maryland's mandatory film licensing scheme, establishing that any government film classification system must include procedural safeguards: (1) the burden of proving the film is unprotected expression rests on the censor; (2) the censor must seek a judicial order within a brief period; and (3) there must be prompt judicial determination.
- **Obscenity Exception:** Under Miller v. California, 413 U.S. 15 (1973), material meeting the three-part obscenity test is unprotected by the First Amendment and may be prohibited: (1) the average person, applying contemporary community standards, would find that the work taken as a whole appeals to the prurient interest; (2) the work depicts sexual conduct specifically defined by applicable state law in a patently offensive way; and (3) the work, taken as a whole, lacks serious literary, artistic, political, or scientific value.
- **Protection of Minors:** While adults have broad rights to view non-obscene content, the government may restrict minors' access to material that is "harmful to minors" even if not obscene for adults (Ginsberg v. New York, 390 U.S. 629 (1968)). However, the Supreme Court in Brown v. Entertainment Merchants Association, 564 U.S. 786 (2011), struck down California's ban on selling violent video games to minors, holding that video games are protected speech and that the government cannot restrict their distribution to minors absent a showing that less restrictive alternatives are inadequate.

### MPA Rating System

The MPA (formerly MPAA) rating system, operated through the Classification and Rating Administration (CARA), is a voluntary industry self-regulatory mechanism:

- **G (General Audiences):** Nothing that would offend parents for viewing by children.
- **PG (Parental Guidance Suggested):** Parents urged to give parental guidance; some material may not be suitable for children.
- **PG-13 (Parents Strongly Cautioned):** Some material may be inappropriate for children under 13.
- **R (Restricted):** Under 17 requires accompanying parent or adult guardian. Contains adult material.
- **NC-17 (No One 17 and Under Admitted):** Patently adult content; no children admitted.

The NC-17 rating carries significant commercial consequences: many theater chains will not exhibit NC-17 films, major retailers will not carry NC-17 home video releases, and most television networks and online platforms refuse NC-17 advertising. This commercial impact has led to criticism that the voluntary system functions as a de facto censorship mechanism, with filmmakers self-censoring to avoid the NC-17 designation.

### Notable Cases

- **Freedman v. Maryland, 380 U.S. 51 (1965):** Struck down Maryland's mandatory film licensing law, establishing that government film classification must include procedural safeguards against administrative censorship.
- **Joseph Burstyn, Inc. v. Wilson, 343 U.S. 495 (1952):** The Supreme Court held that motion pictures are a form of expression protected by the First Amendment, overruling Mutual Film Corp. v. Industrial Commission of Ohio, 236 U.S. 230 (1915), which had held that films were mere entertainment not entitled to constitutional protection.
- **Miramax Films Corp. v. MPAA (1990):** The controversy over the NC-17 rating of Pedro Almodovar's "Tie Me Up! Tie Me Down!" led Miramax to challenge the rating. While the rating stood, the case contributed to the replacement of the X rating with NC-17 in 1990 to distinguish serious films from pornography.
- **This Film Is Not Yet Rated (2006):** Kirby Dick's documentary exposing the secretive MPAA rating process itself received an NC-17 rating, highlighting criticisms of the system's lack of transparency and accountability.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's film classification system combines industry self-regulation with statutory youth protection obligations:

- **Grundgesetz Article 5(1):** Guarantees freedom of expression, press freedom, and freedom of art and science. Article 5(1) sentence 3 states: "Eine Zensur findet nicht statt" ("Censorship does not take place"). The Federal Constitutional Court has interpreted this as prohibiting prior censorship (Vorzensur) by government authorities but permitting post-publication restrictions for the protection of youth and personal honor (Article 5(2)).
- **Jugendschutzgesetz (JuSchG, Youth Protection Act):** Establishes the legal framework for protecting minors from harmful media content. § 11 prohibits the exhibition of films to minors that have not been classified as appropriate for their age group by the supreme Landesbehörde (state authority) or an organization recognized by the state. § 12 restricts the distribution of video recordings to minors in the same manner. § 14 establishes the age classification categories: FSK 0 (no age restriction), FSK 6, FSK 12, FSK 16, FSK 18. § 15 prohibits the distribution to minors of media included in the Index (Liste jugendgefährdender Medien).
- **FSK (Freiwillige Selbstkontrolle der Filmwirtschaft):** Founded in 1949 under the supervision of the Spitzenorganisation der Filmwirtschaft (SPIO, Peak Organization of the Film Industry). Despite its name suggesting voluntariness, FSK classification is practically mandatory because § 11 JuSchG requires age classification for exhibition to minors. FSK decisions are made by committees including both industry representatives and government-appointed assessors (Ständige Vertreter der Obersten Landesjugendbehörden). Classification criteria include: violence and its depiction, sexual content, use of drugs and alcohol, frightening content, and antisocial behavior.
- **Bundeszentrale für Kinder- und Jugendmedienschutz (BzKJ, Federal Agency for the Protection of Children and Youth in the Media):** Successor to the Bundesprüfstelle für jugendgefährdende Medien (BPjM), reconstituted under the 2021 JuSchG reform. The BzKJ maintains the Index (Liste jugendgefährdender Medien), listing media that are harmful to youth. Indexed media may not be advertised, displayed, or made available to minors. Indexing does not prohibit the media for adults but severely restricts its commercial distribution. The BzKJ's indexing decisions are subject to judicial review before the administrative courts.
- **Jugendmedienschutz-Staatsvertrag (JMStV, Interstate Treaty on Youth Media Protection):** Governs online media and broadcasting, administered by the Kommission für Jugendmedienschutz (KJM, Commission for Youth Media Protection). The JMStV requires content providers to ensure that content harmful to minors is not accessible to children, through technical measures (age verification) or time-based access restrictions.

### Indizierung (Indexing) Procedure

The BzKJ's indexing procedure (Indizierung) is a distinctive feature of German media regulation:

- **Section A (publicly indexed):** Media listed in Section A are publicly identified as indexed. They may not be advertised, displayed in public, or made available to minors. Distribution to adults remains legal but must be conducted in a manner preventing minor access.
- **Section B (confidentially indexed):** Media whose indexing itself is confidential, typically involving content that is potentially criminal (e.g., extreme violence, child exploitation material). These media face the same distribution restrictions plus the prohibition on publicly identifying them as indexed.
- **Beschlagnahme (Confiscation):** In extreme cases, criminal courts may order the confiscation of media under StGB § 131 (depictions of violence) or § 184 (pornography). Confiscated media may not be distributed at all, even to adults.

### Notable Cases

- **BVerfG, 1 BvR 1783/05 (13 June 2007):** The Federal Constitutional Court addressed the interaction between artistic freedom (Kunstfreiheit, GG Art. 5(3)) and youth protection. The Court upheld the indexing of a graphic novel, holding that youth protection constitutes a legitimate restriction on artistic freedom but requiring a careful proportionality assessment.
- **"Apocalypse Now" Indexing Controversy (1983-2003):** Francis Ford Coppola's film was indexed by the BPjM in 1983 due to its violence. The indexing was removed in 2003 following a reassessment, illustrating the evolving standards of the indexing system and its responsiveness to changing societal norms.
- **Video Game Indexing Cases:** Germany's indexing system has been particularly active regarding video games, with titles such as "Doom," "Wolfenstein 3D," and "Dead Rising" being indexed or confiscated. The 2021 JuSchG reform introduced a more nuanced approach, requiring consideration of the interactive context and age-appropriate design features.

## ECHR / EU Framework

### Convention Articles

- **Article 10 -- Freedom of Expression:** The primary ECHR provision governing film censorship. Article 10(1) protects the right to receive and impart information and ideas "regardless of frontiers" and "through any medium." Article 10(2) permits restrictions that are "prescribed by law" and "necessary in a democratic society" for specified purposes including "the protection of morals" and "the protection of the reputation or rights of others." The ECtHR has consistently held that artistic expression enjoys a high level of protection under Article 10.
- **Article 10(1) in fine:** States may require the licensing of broadcasting, television, or cinema enterprises. This provision has been interpreted to permit film classification systems, provided they do not function as prior censorship inconsistent with Article 10's overall protections.

### Key Case Law

- **Wingrove v. United Kingdom (ECtHR, 1996, App. No. 17419/90):** The Court found no violation of Article 10 where the British Board of Film Classification (BBFC) refused to certify a short film depicting the ecstatic visions of St. Teresa of Avila in a sexually explicit manner. The Court afforded the UK a wide margin of appreciation in matters of morals and blasphemy, finding the restriction proportionate.
- **Vereinigung Bildender Künstler v. Austria (ECtHR, 2007, App. No. 68354/01):** The Court found a violation of Article 10 where Austrian courts ordered the removal of a painting by Otto Mühl that satirically depicted public figures in sexual positions. The Court held that artistic satire is a form of expression protected by Article 10 and that the injunction was disproportionate.
- **Akdas v. Turkey (ECtHR, 2010, App. No. 41056/04):** The Court found a violation of Article 10 where Turkish courts convicted the publisher of Guillaume Apollinaire's novel "The Exploits of a Young Don Juan." The Court held that the conviction of a publisher for distributing a recognized work of European literature was disproportionate.
- **Mouvement raelien suisse v. Switzerland (ECtHR Grand Chamber, 2012, App. No. 16354/06):** While not a film case, the Grand Chamber addressed the margin of appreciation in content regulation, holding that states have broader latitude regarding the protection of morals but must still demonstrate proportionality.

### AVMSD (Audiovisual Media Services Directive)

Directive (EU) 2018/1808 (amending Directive 2010/13/EU) establishes the EU framework for audiovisual content regulation:

- **Article 6a -- Protection of Minors:** Requires member states to ensure that audiovisual media services contain measures to protect minors from content that may seriously impair their physical, mental, or moral development. For on-demand services, the most harmful content must be subject to the strictest measures (age verification, parental controls).
- **Article 28b -- Video-Sharing Platforms:** Requires platforms to implement appropriate measures to protect minors, including age verification, parental control systems, and content classification.
- **Country of Origin Principle (Article 2):** An audiovisual media service is subject to the jurisdiction of the member state in which the provider is established, creating a framework for cross-border content regulation.
- **Digital Services Act (Regulation (EU) 2022/2065):** Complements the AVMSD by establishing due diligence obligations for online platforms regarding illegal content, including content harmful to minors.
