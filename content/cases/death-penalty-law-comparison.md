## Summary

Capital punishment remains one of the most divisive issues in international law, sitting at the intersection of criminal justice, constitutional rights, and human rights norms. The four jurisdictions examined here represent fundamentally different positions on the spectrum. South Korea retains the death penalty in statute but has not executed anyone since 1997, occupying an ambiguous "de facto abolitionist" status now tested by the 2026 insurrection trial of former President Yoon Suk-yeol. The United States stands as the only Western democracy that actively carries out executions, with 27 states retaining capital punishment and a sharp uptick in executions in 2025 following the reversal of federal moratoriums. Germany abolished the death penalty constitutionally in 1949 through Article 102 of the Basic Law -- notably before public opinion supported abolition -- and has maintained an absolute ban for over 75 years. The European Court of Human Rights (ECHR) and the European Union have elevated abolition to a foundational principle of European legal order, with Protocol No. 6 (1983) prohibiting it in peacetime and Protocol No. 13 (2002) extending the ban to all circumstances including wartime.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's Criminal Act (형법), enacted as Act No. 293 on September 18, 1953, lists capital punishment as the most severe penalty under Article 41. The death penalty is authorized for a wide range of offenses spread across multiple statutes:

**Criminal Act (형법):**
- **Article 87** -- Leading an insurrection or rebellion: death penalty or life imprisonment
- **Article 92** -- Conspiracy with foreign countries endangering national security: death penalty
- **Article 93** -- Providing aid to enemy states: death penalty
- **Article 250** -- Murder: death penalty, life imprisonment, or imprisonment of 5 years or more
- **Article 338** -- Robbery resulting in death: death penalty or life imprisonment

**National Security Act (국가보안법):**
- Forming or leading an anti-government organization with the purpose of overthrowing the state
- Espionage activities benefiting hostile states

**Additional Statutes:**
- Military Criminal Act (군형법): various military offenses in wartime
- Act on the Punishment of Sexual Crimes: certain aggravated sexual offenses
- Narcotics Control Act: large-scale drug trafficking (rarely applied)

### Execution Moratorium

South Korea's last executions occurred on December 30, 1997, when 23 prisoners were put to death by hanging at multiple facilities simultaneously. Following the inauguration of President Kim Dae-jung in February 1998 -- himself a former death row inmate who had been sentenced to death in 1980 under the Chun Doo-hwan military government -- an informal moratorium on executions took effect. This moratorium has been maintained by every subsequent administration, making South Korea a "de facto abolitionist" state under Amnesty International's classification.

### Constitutional Court Ruling (2010)

On February 25, 2010, the Constitutional Court of Korea ruled in a 5-4 decision that the death penalty does not violate the constitutional guarantees of human dignity and worth (Article 10 of the Constitution) or the right to life. The majority held that capital punishment serves legitimate penological purposes of deterrence and retribution for the most heinous crimes. The four dissenting justices argued that the death penalty constitutes an excessive restriction on the right to life and violates human dignity.

This narrow margin -- one vote -- has fueled ongoing debate about whether a future challenge could tip the balance toward unconstitutionality.

### Legislative Efforts

Multiple bills to abolish the death penalty have been introduced in the National Assembly since 1999. In 2004, 175 members of the National Assembly (out of 299) co-sponsored an abolition bill, but it expired without a vote. Subsequent abolition bills were introduced in 2008, 2012, 2016, and 2020, none reaching a floor vote.

### The Yoon Suk-yeol Case (2026)

The death penalty debate in South Korea was dramatically reignited in January 2026 when special prosecutors recommended a death sentence for former President Yoon Suk-yeol for his role in the December 2024 insurrection. This marks the first time in over three decades that a sitting or former head of state has faced capital charges for insurrection under Article 87 of the Criminal Act. International human rights organizations, including Human Rights Watch and Amnesty International, issued a joint statement on January 30, 2026, opposing the death penalty in this case and urging South Korea to move toward formal abolition.

## United States

### Constitutional Framework

The death penalty in the United States operates within a constitutional framework shaped primarily by the Eighth Amendment (prohibiting "cruel and unusual punishments") and the Fourteenth Amendment (guaranteeing "due process" and "equal protection"). The Supreme Court has never held that capital punishment is per se unconstitutional but has imposed significant procedural and substantive limitations.

### Federal Law

Federal capital offenses are codified primarily in 18 U.S.C. Chapter 228 (Federal Death Penalty Act of 1994). Eligible offenses include:

- Murder related to specified federal crimes (terrorism, drug trafficking, carjacking, kidnapping)
- Treason (18 U.S.C. Section 2381)
- Espionage (18 U.S.C. Section 794)
- Large-scale drug trafficking resulting in death (21 U.S.C. Section 848(e))

On December 23, 2024, President Biden commuted the sentences of 37 of the 40 individuals on federal civilian death row to life imprisonment without parole. Three individuals remained: Dylann Roof (Charleston church shooting), Robert Bowers (Pittsburgh synagogue shooting), and Dzhokhar Tsarnaev (Boston Marathon bombing). On January 20, 2025, President Trump signed Executive Order 14164, and Attorney General Pam Bondi subsequently rescinded the Garland-era moratorium on federal executions on February 5, 2025, directing prosecutors to actively seek the death penalty.

### State-Level Landscape (as of 2025)

**27 states retain the death penalty**, though with significant variation in practice:
- Active execution states: Texas, Florida, Oklahoma, Alabama, Missouri, Georgia, Mississippi
- Governor moratoriums: California, Oregon, Pennsylvania, Ohio (unofficial since 2019)

**23 states and Washington D.C. have abolished the death penalty**, including most recently Virginia (2021), which became the first Southern state to do so.

### Execution Methods

States employ various methods, often with alternatives if lethal injection is unavailable:

| Method | States Authorizing |
|--------|--------------------|
| Lethal injection | All retaining states (primary) |
| Electrocution | 8 states (secondary) |
| Lethal gas | 7 states (secondary) |
| Firing squad | Idaho (primary as of 2025), Utah, Mississippi, Oklahoma, South Carolina |
| Hanging | 2 states (secondary) |

Idaho enacted HB 37 on March 12, 2025, making the firing squad its primary execution method. South Carolina conducted the first U.S. firing squad execution in 15 years on March 7, 2025, with the execution of Brad Sigmon.

### 2025 Execution Surge

After years of declining execution numbers, 2025 saw a dramatic increase. Florida led the nation with 15 executions, followed by Alabama and Texas with 5 each. The vast majority occurred in Southern states, with only five taking place elsewhere (two in Arizona, two in Indiana, one in Missouri).

### Landmark Supreme Court Cases

| Case | Year | Holding |
|------|------|---------|
| **Furman v. Georgia**, 408 U.S. 238 | 1972 | Arbitrary and inconsistent imposition of the death penalty violates the 8th and 14th Amendments; imposed de facto nationwide moratorium |
| **Gregg v. Georgia**, 428 U.S. 153 | 1976 | Reinstated the death penalty under guided-discretion statutes with bifurcated trial (guilt and sentencing phases) |
| **Coker v. Georgia**, 433 U.S. 584 | 1977 | Death penalty disproportionate for rape of an adult woman |
| **Atkins v. Virginia**, 536 U.S. 304 | 2002 | Execution of intellectually disabled persons violates the 8th Amendment; "evolving standards of decency" standard |
| **Roper v. Simmons**, 543 U.S. 551 | 2005 | Execution of juvenile offenders (under 18 at time of crime) unconstitutional |
| **Kennedy v. Louisiana**, 554 U.S. 407 | 2008 | Death penalty unconstitutional for crimes where the victim did not die (child rape); limited capital punishment to homicide offenses and crimes against the state |
| **Glossip v. Gross**, 576 U.S. 863 | 2015 | Lethal injection using midazolam does not constitute cruel and unusual punishment; challengers must identify a known and available alternative method |
| **Glossip v. Oklahoma**, 604 U.S. ___ | 2025 | Reversed conviction and death sentence for prosecutorial misconduct; suppression of material psychiatric evidence of key witness |

### Defiance of Kennedy v. Louisiana

In a notable trend, Florida (2023), Tennessee (2024), Idaho (2025), and Alabama (2025) have enacted laws imposing the death penalty for child rape, directly challenging the Supreme Court's 2008 holding in Kennedy v. Louisiana. These laws are expected to face constitutional challenges.

## Germany

### Constitutional Abolition

Article 102 of the Basic Law for the Federal Republic of Germany (Grundgesetz) provides, in its entirety:

> *"Die Todesstrafe ist abgeschafft."*
> ("The death penalty is abolished.")

This provision took effect on May 23, 1949, making Germany one of the earliest nations to constitutionally abolish capital punishment. Article 102 is protected by the "eternity clause" (Ewigkeitsklausel) of Article 79(3), which prohibits constitutional amendments affecting the principles laid down in Articles 1 and 20 -- and by extension, legal scholars widely argue, Article 102 as an expression of human dignity under Article 1(1).

### Historical Context

The path to abolition was paradoxical. The provision was proposed on December 6, 1948, by Hans-Christoph Seebohm, a delegate from the small, far-right German Party (Deutsche Partei), during deliberations of the Parliamentary Council (Parlamentarischer Rat). The motivation was not purely humanitarian: conservative delegates from the German Party and CDU supported abolition partly in hopes of saving former Nazi officials from Allied-imposed death sentences at the Nuremberg and subsequent trials.

Despite its pragmatic origins, Article 102 became a cornerstone of Germany's post-war constitutional identity. A 1949 opinion poll showed 77% of Germans supported the death penalty, and as late as 1960, 71% favored reinstatement. The CDU made unsuccessful attempts to restore capital punishment, but the constitutional prohibition held firm.

### Post-Abolition Legal Adjustments

- **1951**: The Penal Code (Strafgesetzbuch, StGB) was formally amended to conform to Article 102, replacing all death penalty provisions with life imprisonment (lebenslange Freiheitsstrafe)
- **1981**: East Germany (DDR) abolished the death penalty; the last East German execution took place on June 26, 1981 (Werner Teske, convicted of espionage)
- **1990**: Upon reunification, the Basic Law and its Article 102 extended to the entirety of Germany

### Current Penalty Structure

With capital punishment abolished, the most severe penalty under German law is life imprisonment (lebenslange Freiheitsstrafe) under Section 211 StGB for murder (Mord). In practice, inmates serving life sentences become eligible for parole review after 15 years (Section 57a StGB), though courts may impose a finding of "particular severity of guilt" (besondere Schwere der Schuld) that extends the minimum period.

### International Engagement

Germany is one of the most active international advocates for worldwide abolition of the death penalty. It co-sponsors biennial UN General Assembly resolutions calling for a universal moratorium and regularly raises the issue in bilateral diplomacy, including with the United States, China, and Iran.

## ECHR & EU

### European Convention on Human Rights

The original text of the European Convention on Human Rights (1950) did not prohibit the death penalty. Article 2(1) protected the right to life but explicitly permitted deprivation of life "in the execution of a sentence of a court following his conviction of a crime for which this penalty is provided by law."

This exception has been progressively narrowed and ultimately eliminated through two protocols:

**Protocol No. 6 (1983) -- Abolition in Peacetime:**
- Opened for signature: April 28, 1983
- Entered into force: March 1, 1985
- Article 1: "The death penalty shall be abolished. No one shall be condemned to such penalty or executed."
- Article 2 permitted states to retain the death penalty for acts committed "in time of war or of imminent threat of war"
- Ratified by all 46 Council of Europe member states

**Protocol No. 13 (2002) -- Abolition in All Circumstances:**
- Opened for signature: May 3, 2002
- Entered into force: July 1, 2003
- Article 1: "The death penalty shall be abolished. No one shall be condemned to such penalty or executed."
- No wartime exception; no reservations or derogations permitted (Articles 2 and 3)
- Ratified by 44 of 46 Council of Europe member states (Armenia signed but has not ratified; Azerbaijan signed in 2024 but has not ratified as of 2026)

### EU Charter of Fundamental Rights

Article 2(2) of the Charter of Fundamental Rights of the European Union states: "No one shall be condemned to the death penalty, or executed." This provision is legally binding on all EU institutions and on member states when implementing EU law. Abolition of the death penalty is a precondition for EU membership under the Copenhagen criteria.

### EU Guidelines on Death Penalty

The EU adopted its first Guidelines on the Death Penalty in 1998, updated most recently in 2013. These guidelines commit the EU to:
- Working toward universal abolition
- Pressing for moratoria as an intermediate step
- Raising death penalty cases in political dialogues with retentionist states
- Supporting civil society organizations working for abolition
- Funding international advocacy efforts

### Landmark ECHR Cases

**Soering v. United Kingdom (1989), Application No. 14038/88:**
The foundational case on the death penalty and extradition. Jens Soering, a German national, faced extradition from the UK to Virginia to stand trial for capital murder. The Court unanimously held that extradition would violate Article 3 (prohibition of torture and inhuman or degrading treatment) because of the "death row phenomenon" -- the prolonged period of extreme psychological stress awaiting execution under harsh conditions in the United States. This case established that ECHR obligations extend to situations where a state's action (extradition) would expose an individual to Convention violations in another jurisdiction.

**Ocalan v. Turkey (2005), Application No. 46221/99:**
The Grand Chamber ruled that Turkey violated Articles 3, 5, and 6 by sentencing Abdullah Ocalan, leader of the PKK, to death following an unfair trial in 1999. The Court held that the imposition of a death sentence after proceedings that did not satisfy fair trial requirements amounted to inhuman treatment under Article 3, even though the sentence was never carried out. Turkey formally abolished the death penalty in 2004 during the proceedings.

**Al-Saadoon and Mufdhi v. United Kingdom (2010), Application No. 61498/08:**
The Court found that transferring two Iraqi detainees held by British forces to Iraqi authorities, where they faced the death penalty, violated Article 3. Significantly, the Court stated that Article 2 of the Convention had been "amended so as to prohibit the death penalty in all circumstances" through the consistent practice of member states and the adoption of Protocols 6 and 13, confirming that the death penalty prohibition had become part of the baseline Convention right to life.

### Council of Europe Membership Conditions

Since 1994, the Council of Europe has required all new member states to impose an immediate moratorium on executions and commit to ratifying Protocol No. 6 within a specified timeframe as conditions of accession. Russia, which joined in 1996 with such commitments but never ratified Protocol No. 6, was expelled from the Council of Europe in March 2022 following its invasion of Ukraine. Belarus remains the only European state actively carrying out executions and has never been admitted to the Council.

## Comparative Analysis

### Abolition Trajectories

The four jurisdictions illustrate distinct pathways toward or away from abolition:

1. **Constitutional abolition (Germany):** Abolition was embedded in the constitution at the founding moment of the state, preceding and ultimately shaping public opinion. This top-down approach proved durable even against sustained majority opposition.

2. **Treaty-based abolition (ECHR/EU):** A gradual, multilateral process that moved from permitting the death penalty (1950) to banning it in peacetime (1983) to absolute prohibition (2002), reflecting evolving European consensus.

3. **De facto moratorium (South Korea):** Executive restraint without legislative change, creating legal uncertainty. The death penalty remains available in statute, death row inmates remain in limbo, and the moratorium could theoretically be reversed by executive decision.

4. **Active retention with internal fragmentation (United States):** A federalist patchwork where abolition and retention coexist, with a recent trend toward divergence -- some states abolishing while others expand eligible offenses and execution methods.

### Human Dignity as Legal Doctrine

All four systems engage with human dignity as a limiting principle, but reach different conclusions:

- **Germany:** Human dignity (Menschenwurde, Art. 1 GG) is inviolable and absolute, providing the philosophical foundation for Article 102's abolition
- **ECHR:** The prohibition of inhuman treatment (Art. 3) has been progressively interpreted to encompass the death penalty, particularly through the "death row phenomenon"
- **South Korea:** The Constitutional Court acknowledged human dignity but held (5-4) that capital punishment does not inherently violate it when applied to the most serious crimes
- **United States:** The Eighth Amendment's "cruel and unusual" standard evolves with "standards of decency that mark the progress of a maturing society" (Trop v. Dulles, 1958) but has not matured to the point of categorical prohibition

### The Retentionist-Abolitionist Divide

The global trend is strongly toward abolition: more than two-thirds of the world's countries have abolished the death penalty in law or practice. The United States' continued retention places it alongside China, Iran, Saudi Arabia, and Egypt among the world's most active executioners -- a geopolitical alignment that creates diplomatic tension, particularly with European allies. South Korea's ambiguous position represents a third category, where legal retention coexists with practical restraint, leaving the question unresolved.
