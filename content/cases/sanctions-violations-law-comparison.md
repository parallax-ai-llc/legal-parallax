## Summary

Sanctions violations — the breach of economic restrictions imposed by governments or international bodies to achieve foreign policy and security objectives — constitute an increasingly prosecuted category of economic crime with severe consequences for individuals and corporations. As geopolitical tensions intensify, particularly following Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine in 2022 and ongoing North Korean proliferation activities, jurisdictions worldwide have significantly strengthened their sanctions enforcement regimes. South Korea's framework rests on three principal statutes: the Foreign Exchange Transactions Act (외국환거래법), the Foreign Trade Act (대외무역법), and the Act on Prohibition Against the Financing of Terrorism and Proliferation of Weapons of Mass Destruction (공중 등 협박목적 및 대량살상무기확산을 위한 자금조달행위의 금지에 관한 법률). The United States enforces sanctions primarily through the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA, 50 U.S.C. §§ 1701–1706), the Trading with the Enemy Act (TWEA, 50 U.S.C. App. §§ 1–44), and various country-specific statutes administered by the Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC) within the Department of the Treasury. Germany criminalizes sanctions breaches under Sections 17 and 18 of the Foreign Trade and Payments Act (Außenwirtschaftsgesetz, AWG), read in conjunction with the Foreign Trade and Payments Ordinance (Außenwirtschaftsverordnung, AWV), with landmark reforms enacted in January 2026 transposing EU Directive 2024/1226. The European Union adopted Directive (EU) 2024/1226 on 24 April 2024, establishing for the first time minimum rules on criminal offenses and penalties for sanctions violations across all member states, with a transposition deadline of 20 May 2025. The European Court of Human Rights (ECHR) has developed significant jurisprudence on the tension between sanctions implementation and fundamental rights, most notably in the cases of Nada v. Switzerland (2012) and Al-Dulimi and Montana Management Inc. v. Switzerland (2016).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's sanctions regime is anchored by three principal statutes operating in tandem. The Foreign Trade Act (대외무역법) governs export and import controls on strategic items and dual-use goods, particularly those related to weapons of mass destruction and their delivery systems. The Foreign Exchange Transactions Act (외국환거래법) provides the legal basis for implementing financial sanctions, including asset freezes and restrictions on capital movements. The Act on Prohibition Against the Financing of Terrorism and Proliferation of Weapons of Mass Destruction (공중 등 협박목적 및 대량살상무기확산을 위한 자금조달행위의 금지에 관한 법률) addresses the financing dimensions of sanctions enforcement.

**Strategic Items and Export Controls:** Under the Foreign Trade Act, the export, import, transit, brokering, or transshipment of "strategic items" — goods, technologies, and software designated by presidential decree as having potential weapons-of-mass-destruction or conventional-weapons applications — requires prior authorization from the Minister of Trade, Industry and Energy. The Strategic Items Control System is operated through the Korea Strategic Trade Institute (KOSTI), which maintains the national strategic items list aligned with international control regimes including the Wassenaar Arrangement, the Nuclear Suppliers Group (NSG), the Australia Group, and the Missile Technology Control Regime (MTCR).

**Financial Sanctions Implementation:** Asset freezes and financial restrictions mandated by United Nations Security Council (UNSC) resolutions are implemented under the Foreign Exchange Transactions Act. The Bank of Korea and the Financial Services Commission coordinate enforcement. South Korea also maintains its own unilateral sanctions designations, particularly targeting North Korean entities and individuals involved in nuclear and missile proliferation.

### Penalties

| Offense | Legal Basis | Penalty |
|---------|------------|---------|
| Export of strategic items without license (proliferation-related) | Foreign Trade Act Art. 53 | Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine up to KRW 500 million, or 3x transaction value |
| Export of strategic items without license (non-proliferation) | Foreign Trade Act Art. 53-2 | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine up to KRW 300 million |
| Obtaining export license through false statements | Foreign Trade Act Art. 54 | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine up to KRW 300 million |
| Violation of export/import restrictions during national emergency | Foreign Trade Act Art. 52 | Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine up to KRW 300 million |
| Export of national core technologies to foreign entities without approval | Industrial Technology Protection Act | Minimum 3 years imprisonment; fine up to KRW 6.5 billion |
| Violation of financial sanctions (asset freeze breach) | Foreign Exchange Transactions Act | Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine up to KRW 500 million, or 3x transaction value |
| Illegal transfer of strategic materials to North Korea | South-North Exchanges and Cooperation Act Art. 27 | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine up to KRW 10 million |

**Joint Penal Provisions:** Under the Foreign Trade Act, criminal offenses are subject to joint penal provisions (양벌규정), meaning a company may be held jointly liable for the criminal acts of its officers or employees committed in the course of their duties, unless the company can demonstrate that it exercised due diligence in supervision.

**Administrative Penalties:** Beyond criminal sanctions, the Ministry of Trade, Industry and Energy may impose administrative measures including suspension or revocation of export licenses, prohibition on future export activities for specified periods, and public disclosure of violations. The Fair Trade Commission has emphasized the strengthening of economic sanctions enforcement, including up to a 50% surcharge for repeat violations and significantly increased penalty calculation rates.

### Notable Enforcement and Cases

South Korea's sanctions enforcement has historically focused on North Korea-related proliferation. Although there have been few publicly reported civil enforcement cases for sanctions violations, several developments are noteworthy:

- **North Korea Unilateral Sanctions Designations (2024):** On four separate occasions in 2024 (2 April, 24 May, 1 July, and 19 December), South Korea designated individuals and entities that violated UNSC resolutions as sanctioned parties under its domestic framework.
- **Inter-Korean Liaison Office Petroleum Deliveries:** South Korea itself faced scrutiny from a UN panel in 2019 for failing to notify the Security Council about petroleum product deliveries for use at the inter-Korean joint liaison office in Kaesong, which were alleged to constitute a technical violation of UNSC sanctions.
- **Enforcement Decree Amendment (March 2025):** The Enforcement Decree of the Foreign Trade Act was amended on 12 March 2025, strengthening compliance requirements and expanding the scope of controlled items.

### Recent Reforms

The Korean government has undertaken significant modernization of its sanctions and export control regime in recent years. The 2025 amendment to the Foreign Trade Act Enforcement Decree expanded the list of strategic items subject to export controls and enhanced end-use monitoring requirements. Korea has also strengthened its "catch-all" controls, requiring exporters to obtain authorization when they know or have reason to suspect that items may be diverted to WMD programs, even if the items are not on the strategic items list.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States possesses the most extensive and aggressively enforced sanctions regime in the world, leveraging the dominance of the US dollar in international finance and the centrality of US financial institutions to global commerce. The legal architecture rests on several interlocking statutes:

**International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA), 50 U.S.C. §§ 1701–1706:** IEEPA is the primary statutory authority under which the President imposes economic sanctions in response to "unusual and extraordinary threats" to national security, foreign policy, or the economy originating wholly or substantially outside the United States. The President must declare a national emergency under the National Emergencies Act (50 U.S.C. §§ 1601–1651) before exercising IEEPA authorities. Once a national emergency is declared, IEEPA grants the President broad powers to regulate, prohibit, or block transactions involving property in which a foreign country or national has an interest.

**Trading with the Enemy Act (TWEA), 50 U.S.C. App. §§ 1–44:** Originally enacted in 1917, TWEA now applies principally to the comprehensive embargo on Cuba, which predates IEEPA.

**Country-Specific Statutes:** Additional statutory authorities include the Iran Sanctions Act of 1996 (as amended), the Comprehensive Iran Sanctions, Accountability, and Divestment Act of 2010 (CISADA), the Syria Accountability and Lebanese Sovereignty Restoration Act of 2003, and the North Korea Sanctions and Policy Enhancement Act of 2016.

### OFAC Enforcement

The Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC), a division of the Department of the Treasury, administers and enforces US sanctions programs. OFAC maintains the Specially Designated Nationals and Blocked Persons List (SDN List), which identifies individuals and entities whose assets are blocked and with whom US persons are generally prohibited from dealing. As of 2025, the SDN List contains over 12,000 entries across more than 30 sanctions programs.

**Enforcement Process:** When OFAC suspects a violation, it may open an enforcement proceeding and pursue one of several outcomes: a finding of no violation, a cautionary letter, a finding of violation (with or without civil monetary penalty), or a criminal referral to the Department of Justice.

### Penalties

| Category | Civil Penalties | Criminal Penalties |
|----------|----------------|-------------------|
| IEEPA violations | Greater of $377,700 per violation or twice the transaction value (adjusted annually for inflation) | Up to $1 million fine and/or 20 years imprisonment per willful violation |
| TWEA violations | Up to $105,083 per violation | Up to $1 million fine and/or 20 years imprisonment |
| Strict liability | Civil penalties may be imposed without proof of knowledge or intent | Criminal prosecution requires willful violation |

**Statute of Limitations:** The statute of limitations for both criminal and civil violations under IEEPA and TWEA is 10 years, applicable to violations occurring after April 24, 2019.

### Landmark Cases

The United States has pursued some of the most consequential sanctions enforcement actions in history, particularly against financial institutions:

- **BNP Paribas SA (2014):** The largest sanctions-related settlement in history. BNP Paribas pleaded guilty to conspiring to violate IEEPA and TWEA after systematically concealing, removing, and obscuring references to sanctioned parties in 3,897 financial and trade transactions routed through US banks between 2005 and 2012. The bank processed over $8.8 billion in transactions on behalf of Sudanese, Iranian, and Cuban sanctioned entities. Total penalties: $8.9 billion (including a $963 million OFAC settlement, the largest of any kind at that time, plus DOJ criminal fines and penalties to NYDFS and the Federal Reserve).
- **ZTE Corporation (2017):** The Chinese telecommunications company agreed to pay $1.19 billion in combined civil and criminal penalties for violations of US sanctions regarding sales of products to Iran and North Korea.
- **Commerzbank AG (2015):** The German bank paid total penalties of $1.45 billion for sanctions violations involving transactions with Iran, Sudan, and other sanctioned jurisdictions.
- **ING Bank (2012):** The Dutch bank paid $619 million for systematic violations involving the stripping of sanctioned-party information from wire transfers related to Cuba, Iran, Libya, and Myanmar.
- **HSBC Holdings (2012):** HSBC paid $1.9 billion in a deferred prosecution agreement for willfully failing to maintain an effective anti-money laundering program and facilitating transactions on behalf of sanctioned entities.

### Institutional Changes (2025)

The enforcement landscape shifted notably in early 2025. The Department of Justice disbanded Task Force KleptoCapture (established in March 2022 to enforce sanctions against Russian oligarchs), along with the Kleptocracy Team and the Kleptocracy Asset Recovery Section, pursuant to a memorandum from Attorney General Pam Bondi. These institutional changes have raised questions about the future trajectory of US sanctions enforcement priorities.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany criminalizes sanctions violations primarily through Sections 17 and 18 of the Foreign Trade and Payments Act (Außenwirtschaftsgesetz, AWG), read in conjunction with Sections 80 et seq. of the Foreign Trade and Payments Ordinance (Außenwirtschaftsverordnung, AWV). As a member of the European Union, Germany implements EU sanctions regulations (adopted as EU Council Regulations, which are directly applicable in all member states) through its domestic enforcement mechanisms.

**Section 18 AWG — Criminal Offenses:** Section 18 AWG constitutes the central criminal provision. It criminalizes the violation of prohibitions established by EU sanctions regulations, including:

- Export, import, transit, transfer, sale, purchase, delivery, or provision bans
- Bans on the provision of broadcasting, transmission, distribution, or other services
- Bans on the disposal of frozen funds and economic resources
- Arms embargo violations

**Section 17 AWG — Serious Offenses:** Section 17 AWG addresses particularly serious offenses involving the unauthorized export of weapons of war and dual-use items with knowledge that the goods will be used in connection with WMD programs. Penalties under Section 17 range up to 15 years imprisonment.

### Penalties

| Offense Category | Legal Basis | Penalty Range |
|-----------------|------------|---------------|
| Standard sanctions violations (export/import/financial bans) | Section 18(1)-(5) AWG | 3 months to 5 years imprisonment |
| Aggravated violations (concealment, use of shell companies) | Section 18(6a) AWG | 6 months to 10 years imprisonment |
| Export of weapons of war without license | Section 17(1) AWG | 1 to 15 years imprisonment |
| Export of dual-use items for WMD purposes | Section 17(2) AWG | 1 to 15 years imprisonment |
| Administrative offenses (negligent violations) | Section 19 AWG | Fines up to EUR 500,000 |
| Corporate fines (intentional sanctions breaches) | Sections 30, 130 OWiG (as amended) | Up to EUR 40 million |
| Corporate fines (supervisory duty breaches) | Sections 30, 130 OWiG (as amended) | Up to EUR 10 million |

**Enhanced Penalties Under Section 18(6a) AWG:** Following the 2026 AWG amendment, aggravated penalties apply in two scenarios: (1) providing incomplete or incorrect information to a public authority regarding a movement of goods in order to conceal a sanctions violation, and (2) using a third-country company over which the violator exercises direct or indirect control to circumvent EU sanctions. These provisions target the increasingly sophisticated circumvention schemes that emerged following the imposition of comprehensive sanctions on Russia in 2022.

### 2026 Legislative Reform

On 15 January 2026, the German Bundestag adopted legislation transposing EU Directive (EU) 2024/1226, which harmonizes criminal offenses and penalties for violations of EU restrictive measures across all member states. The transposition was delayed from the original May 2025 deadline due to the snap federal election held in February 2025, which interrupted the legislative process.

Key changes introduced by the 2026 reform include:

- **Expanded criminalization:** Sanctions violations can now become criminally relevant more quickly; the threshold for criminal liability has been lowered.
- **Significantly higher corporate fines:** Maximum corporate fines under the Administrative Offenses Act (OWiG) were increased from EUR 500,000 to EUR 10 million for certain violations, with a ceiling of EUR 40 million for intentional sanction breaches.
- **Aggravated penalty categories:** Introduction of Section 18(6a) AWG creating enhanced penalties for concealment and circumvention schemes.
- **Expanded scope of services bans:** Criminalization now extends to violations of bans on providing specified professional services (legal, accounting, consulting) to sanctioned entities.

### Enforcement Activity

Germany has seen a substantial increase in sanctions-related criminal investigations since 2022:

- **Over 1,400 criminal investigation proceedings** into alleged sanctions breaches were pending across the country as of 2025, reflecting the massive scale of enforcement activity triggered by Russia sanctions.
- **Sector focus:** Export-intensive industries — particularly machinery, automotive, electronics, and chemicals — are under heightened scrutiny for sanctions and export control compliance, including suspected circumvention through distributors and after-sales channels in third countries (particularly Central Asia, Turkey, and the UAE).
- **German Economic Ministry digest (April 2025):** The Federal Ministry for Economic Affairs and Climate Action published an official digest on criminal enforcement related to Russia sanctions, underscoring the government's prioritization of enforcement.
- **UN criticism (2024):** In February 2024, a UN independent expert warned that prosecuting an entrepreneur for alleged sanctions violations raised concerns about international law and human rights, highlighting the tensions inherent in aggressive enforcement.

### Institutional Framework

The Federal Office for Economic Affairs and Export Control (Bundesamt für Wirtschaft und Ausfuhrkontrolle, BAFA) is the primary administrative authority responsible for licensing and monitoring compliance with export controls and sanctions. Criminal investigations are conducted by the Customs Investigation Office (Zollkriminalamt, ZKA) and state-level prosecutors' offices (Staatsanwaltschaften), with complex cases often handled by specialized economic crime units.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Legal Framework

The European Union imposes sanctions (referred to as "restrictive measures" in EU terminology) through a two-step process. The Council of the European Union adopts Common Foreign and Security Policy (CFSP) decisions establishing the political framework, followed by Council Regulations that implement the measures with binding legal force across all member states. EU sanctions may implement UN Security Council resolutions or constitute autonomous EU measures.

**Types of EU Restrictive Measures:**
- Asset freezes and economic resource restrictions
- Travel bans (entry and transit restrictions)
- Arms embargoes
- Trade restrictions (import and export bans on specific goods and technologies)
- Restrictions on financial services, investment, and specified professional services
- Sectoral restrictions (e.g., restrictions on energy, transport, or luxury goods sectors)

**Directive (EU) 2024/1226 — Harmonization of Criminal Penalties:** Adopted on 24 April 2024, this Directive represents a watershed moment in EU sanctions enforcement. For the first time, the EU established minimum rules on the definition of criminal offenses and penalties for the violation of EU restrictive measures. The Directive was motivated by the significant disparities among member state penalty provisions, which undermined the effectiveness and deterrent value of EU sanctions.

### Key Provisions of Directive 2024/1226

**Criminal Offenses Defined:** The Directive requires member states to criminalize the following conduct when committed intentionally or with serious negligence:

- Making funds or economic resources available to or for the benefit of designated persons or entities in violation of asset freezes
- Failing to freeze funds or economic resources belonging to designated persons or entities
- Enabling designated persons to enter or transit through the territory of a member state in violation of travel bans
- Engaging in trade, import, export, sale, purchase, transfer, transit, or transport of goods and technologies subject to restrictions
- Providing financial services or conducting financial activities prohibited under sanctions regulations
- Providing services (including legal advisory, accounting, or consulting services) prohibited under sanctions regulations
- Circumventing any of the above prohibitions

**Penalty Requirements:**

| Category | Minimum Maximum Penalty Required |
|----------|--------------------------------|
| Individuals (standard offenses) | At least 1 year maximum imprisonment |
| Individuals (serious offenses, e.g., large-scale or organized) | At least 5 years maximum imprisonment |
| Legal persons (corporate fines) | Up to 5% of total worldwide turnover or EUR 40 million (whichever is higher) |
| Additional measures | Exclusion from public benefits, disqualification from business activities, judicial winding-up |

**Implementation Status:** The transposition deadline was 20 May 2025. On 24 July 2025, the European Commission opened infringement proceedings against 18 member states for failing to fully transpose the Directive by the deadline, demonstrating the challenges of achieving harmonized enforcement across the EU's diverse legal systems.

### ECHR Jurisprudence on Sanctions and Fundamental Rights

The European Court of Human Rights has developed a significant body of case law addressing the tension between the implementation of international sanctions — particularly those imposed by the UN Security Council — and the protection of fundamental rights guaranteed by the European Convention on Human Rights.

**Nada v. Switzerland (Application No. 10593/08, Grand Chamber, 12 September 2012):**

Youssef Nada, an Egyptian-Italian businessman residing in the Italian enclave of Campione d'Italia within Switzerland, was listed on the UN Al-Qaida and Taliban Sanctions Committee's Consolidated List in November 2001. Switzerland implemented the listing by restricting Nada's ability to enter or transit through Swiss territory, effectively confining him to the 1.6 km² enclave.

The Grand Chamber found violations of Article 8 (right to respect for private and family life) and Article 13 (right to an effective remedy) of the Convention. The Court held that Switzerland had not sufficiently taken into account the possibility of applying the sanctions in a manner less restrictive of Nada's rights. The ruling was significant because the Court departed from its previous deferential approach toward UN Security Council measures, influenced by the ECJ's parallel jurisprudence in the Kadi case.

**Al-Dulimi and Montana Management Inc. v. Switzerland (Application No. 5809/08, Grand Chamber, 21 June 2016):**

Khalaf M. Al-Dulimi, identified by the UN Security Council as the former head of finance for the Iraqi secret services under Saddam Hussein, challenged Switzerland's confiscation of his assets and those of his company Montana Management Inc., implemented pursuant to UNSC Resolution 1483 (2003).

By 15 votes to 2, the Grand Chamber found a violation of Article 6(1) of the Convention (right of access to a court). The Court held that Swiss courts failed to provide meaningful judicial review of the applicant's sanctions listings, establishing the principle that provided a UN resolution does not expressly exclude judicial examination, it must be interpreted as permitting appropriate review by domestic courts. This review may be limited to verifying that sanctions have not been applied arbitrarily.

**Kadi v. Council and Commission (ECJ Cases C-402/05 P and C-415/05 P, 3 September 2008):**

While decided by the European Court of Justice rather than the ECHR, the Kadi judgment is foundational to European sanctions law. Yassin Abdullah Kadi, a Saudi resident with assets frozen pursuant to EU regulations implementing UNSC Al-Qaida sanctions, challenged the freezing as a violation of EU fundamental rights. The ECJ annulled the regulations, holding that EU courts retain jurisdiction to review the lawfulness of sanctions measures against EU fundamental rights standards, even when those measures implement UN Security Council resolutions. The Kadi judgment established the "autonomous legal order" doctrine, affirming that international obligations cannot override the constitutional principles of the EU legal order.

### Key Principles Established

The combined effect of these judgments has established several principles governing sanctions enforcement in the European context:

1. **Right to judicial review:** Individuals and entities subject to sanctions designations have a right to meaningful judicial review, including access to a court and the ability to challenge the factual basis for their listing.
2. **Proportionality:** Sanctions must be proportionate to their objectives; states must consider less restrictive alternatives when implementing sanctions that affect fundamental rights.
3. **Due process in delisting:** Effective procedures must exist for individuals to seek removal from sanctions lists, including access to the reasons for listing and an opportunity to respond.
4. **No automatic primacy of UNSC resolutions:** While states have obligations under the UN Charter, the implementation of UNSC resolutions does not automatically override obligations under the European Convention or EU fundamental rights law.

## Comparative Analysis

### Enforcement Philosophy

The four jurisdictions reflect fundamentally different enforcement philosophies. The United States has adopted the most aggressive extraterritorial posture, leveraging the dominance of the US dollar and the centrality of US financial infrastructure to assert jurisdiction over transactions with even a tenuous nexus to the United States. OFAC's enforcement actions against European banks (BNP Paribas, Commerzbank, ING, HSBC) demonstrate the practical reach of this approach — non-US entities face severe penalties for processing transactions through US correspondent banking channels.

South Korea's enforcement has been comparatively restrained, with few publicly reported enforcement actions despite a sophisticated legal framework. This likely reflects both the relatively smaller scale of Korean financial institutions' exposure to sanctions-evading transactions and a prioritization of diplomatic over punitive approaches in the North Korea context.

Germany has historically adopted a more cautious enforcement posture, but the post-2022 Russia sanctions environment has triggered a dramatic increase in investigative activity. The over 1,400 pending criminal investigations represent an unprecedented enforcement surge. The January 2026 AWG reform, transposing EU Directive 2024/1226, further signals a shift toward more aggressive prosecution.

The EU's harmonization effort through Directive 2024/1226 addresses a persistent weakness: the dramatic variation in member state enforcement, which created perverse incentives for sanctions evasion through jurisdictions with weaker penalties or less active prosecutors. The Directive's requirement for minimum prison terms and corporate fines calibrated to worldwide turnover represents a substantial step toward consistent enforcement.

### Extraterritorial Jurisdiction

The question of extraterritorial application is perhaps the most contentious issue in international sanctions law. US secondary sanctions — which penalize non-US persons for engaging in lawful transactions under their own domestic law if those transactions involve sanctioned jurisdictions — have generated significant diplomatic friction, particularly with the EU. The EU's Blocking Statute (Council Regulation (EC) No. 2271/96, as updated) prohibits EU persons from complying with specified extraterritorial US sanctions, creating a direct normative conflict for companies operating in both jurisdictions.

South Korea and Germany generally limit their sanctions enforcement to territorial and nationality-based jurisdiction, though both have expanded their reach through catch-all controls and anti-circumvention provisions.

### Corporate Liability

All four jurisdictions provide for corporate sanctions, but the mechanisms differ significantly. The US imposes strict civil liability (no intent requirement for OFAC penalties), creating powerful compliance incentives. Germany's system of administrative fines under the OWiG has traditionally imposed lower maximum penalties than the US, but the 2026 reform dramatically increased maximum corporate fines to EUR 40 million. The EU Directive's 5% worldwide turnover threshold for corporate penalties matches the scale of the most consequential US enforcement actions. South Korea's joint penal provisions allow criminal liability to attach to companies, but with a due diligence defense.

### Human Rights Safeguards

The ECHR and EU frameworks provide the most developed due process protections for sanctioned persons. The Kadi, Nada, and Al-Dulimi line of cases establish binding requirements for judicial review, proportionality, and procedural fairness. The United States provides limited administrative review through OFAC's licensing and delisting procedures, but judicial challenges to sanctions designations face significant obstacles, particularly under the political question and state secrets doctrines. South Korea and Germany provide judicial review of sanctions-related enforcement actions through their respective administrative court systems, but relatively few cases have tested these mechanisms.
