## Summary

Appellate procedure -- the mechanisms by which parties challenge judicial decisions before higher courts -- is a cornerstone of the rule of law, ensuring the correction of errors, the uniform development of legal principles, and the accountability of lower courts, while balancing these objectives against the finality of judgments, judicial economy, and the efficient administration of justice. South Korea operates a three-tier court system with appeals of right to the High Court (고등법원) from the District Court (지방법원) under Articles 390-410 of the Civil Procedure Act (민사소송법) and Articles 358-372 of the Criminal Procedure Act (형사소송법), and further appeal to the Supreme Court (대법원) on points of law under Article 423 of the Civil Procedure Act, with the Supreme Court functioning primarily as a court of legal review that may examine whether the lower court's judgment contravenes law or the Constitution, and the Constitutional Court (헌법재판소) exercising jurisdiction over constitutional complaints (헌법소원) under Article 68 of the Constitutional Court Act (헌법재판소법). The United States has the most complex appellate system of any jurisdiction, featuring 13 federal courts of appeals and 94 district courts at the federal level, with the Supreme Court exercising discretionary review through the certiorari process (28 U.S.C. Section 1254), the final judgment rule under 28 U.S.C. Section 1291 limiting appellate jurisdiction to final decisions with exceptions for interlocutory appeals under 28 U.S.C. Section 1292 and the collateral order doctrine established in Cohen v. Beneficial Industrial Loan Corp., 337 U.S. 541 (1949), and standards of review ranging from de novo for questions of law to clearly erroneous for findings of fact (FRCP 52(a)(6)) and abuse of discretion for procedural rulings. Germany provides a comprehensive multi-tier appellate system with Berufung (full appeal) to the Landgericht or Oberlandesgericht under Sections 511-541 ZPO permitting review of both facts and law, Revision (legal review) to the Bundesgerichtshof under Sections 542-566 ZPO limited to questions of law, and the Verfassungsbeschwerde (constitutional complaint) before the Bundesverfassungsgericht under Article 93(1)(4a) of the Basic Law and Sections 90-95 of the Federal Constitutional Court Act (BVerfGG), enabling individuals to challenge any act of public authority alleged to violate fundamental rights, with the Federal Constitutional Court processing over 5,000 constitutional complaints annually though granting full review (Annahme zur Entscheidung) in approximately 1 to 2 percent of cases. At the European level, the ECHR does not guarantee a right of appeal in civil cases but recognizes a limited right under Article 2 of Protocol 7 in criminal matters, with the Court in Delcourt v. Belgium (1970) holding that Article 6 applies to appellate proceedings when they exist, while EU law provides for appeals from the General Court to the Court of Justice of the European Union under Article 256(1) TFEU limited to points of law.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's appellate system operates through a three-tier court structure with distinct civil and criminal appellate tracks, supplemented by the Constitutional Court's jurisdiction over constitutional complaints.

### First Appeal (항소) -- Civil

**Articles 390-410 of the Civil Procedure Act** govern appeals from District Courts to High Courts:

- **Article 390 (Right of Appeal):** A party dissatisfied with a first-instance judgment may appeal to the appellate court. The right of appeal is not subject to leave requirements.
- **Article 396 (Time for Filing):** The notice of appeal must be filed within 14 days of service of the judgment.
- **Article 397 (Appellate Court):** Appeals from single-judge divisions of the District Court are heard by the collegiate division of the same District Court; appeals from collegiate divisions are heard by the High Court.
- **Article 407 (Scope of Review):** The appellate court conducts a de novo review of both facts and law within the scope of the appeal. The court may receive new evidence and make new factual findings.

### First Appeal (항소) -- Criminal

**Articles 358-372 of the Criminal Procedure Act:**

- **Article 358 (Right of Appeal):** The defendant, prosecutor, or both may appeal from a first-instance criminal judgment.
- **Article 361 (Time for Filing):** The notice of appeal must be filed within 7 days of the pronouncement of the judgment.
- **Article 361-5 (Grounds for Appeal):** Grounds include: (1) errors of law affecting the judgment; (2) errors of fact; (3) excessive or insufficient sentencing; (4) procedural violations (e.g., lack of jurisdiction, failure to recuse a disqualified judge).

### Supreme Court Appeal (상고)

**Articles 422-438 of the Civil Procedure Act** and **Articles 373-390 of the Criminal Procedure Act** govern appeals to the Supreme Court:

- **Article 423 CPA (Grounds for Appeal):** An appeal to the Supreme Court may be filed on the ground that the judgment of the appellate court contravenes law or the Constitution. Questions of fact are not reviewable unless the lower court's factual findings are affected by a legal error (e.g., violation of the rules of evidence or logic).
- **Article 424 CPA (Absolute Grounds):** An appeal is always permitted when: (a) the court was not lawfully composed; (b) a judge who should have been disqualified participated; (c) the court lacked jurisdiction; (d) reasons for the judgment were not provided; (e) the judgment contradicted a prior Supreme Court decision.

### Small Claims Appellate Restrictions

For small claims (소액사건), the Small Claims Trial Act (소액사건심판법) restricts the grounds for appeal to the Supreme Court. Under Article 3, an appeal from the appellate court is permitted only on the ground that the judgment contravenes the Constitution, law, order, or regulation, or conflicts with Supreme Court precedent.

### Constitutional Complaints (헌법소원)

**Article 68 of the Constitutional Court Act:**

- **Article 68(1):** Any person whose fundamental rights guaranteed by the Constitution have been violated by an exercise or non-exercise of governmental power (excluding judgments of ordinary courts) may file a constitutional complaint with the Constitutional Court after exhausting all available remedies. This provision explicitly excludes ordinary court judgments from the scope of constitutional complaints, unlike the German Verfassungsbeschwerde.
- **Article 68(2):** When a party in ordinary court proceedings contests the constitutionality of a statute and the court rejects the request for constitutional referral, the party may file a constitutional complaint directly with the Constitutional Court.

### Interlocutory Appeals (즉시항고)

**Article 444 CPA:** An immediate appeal (즉시항고) may be filed against orders and rulings specified by statute within 7 days. Interlocutory appeals are limited to specifically enumerated orders, including orders on provisional remedies, discovery disputes, and jurisdictional rulings.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has the most complex appellate system of any jurisdiction, reflecting its federal structure and the constitutional significance of appellate review.

### Federal Appellate Structure

The federal court system comprises:
- **94 District Courts** (trial courts)
- **13 Courts of Appeals** (11 numbered circuits, D.C. Circuit, Federal Circuit)
- **Supreme Court** (discretionary review)

### Final Judgment Rule (28 U.S.C. Section 1291)

The courts of appeals have jurisdiction over "final decisions" of the district courts. This final judgment rule limits appellate review to judgments that end the litigation on the merits and leave nothing for the court to do but execute the judgment. The rule promotes judicial economy by preventing piecemeal appeals.

### Exceptions to the Final Judgment Rule

| Exception | Authority | Scope |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| **Interlocutory injunctions** | 28 U.S.C. Section 1292(a)(1) | Orders granting, denying, modifying, or refusing to dissolve injunctions |
| **Certified questions** | 28 U.S.C. Section 1292(b) | District court certifies controlling question of law; circuit court accepts |
| **Collateral order doctrine** | Cohen v. Beneficial Industrial Loan Corp. (1949) | Orders conclusively determining a disputed question, resolving an important issue separate from the merits, and effectively unreviewable after final judgment |
| **Mandamus** | 28 U.S.C. Section 1651 (All Writs Act) | Extraordinary writ for clear legal error and irreparable harm |
| **Class certification** | FRCP 23(f) | Discretionary review of class certification orders |
| **Arbitrability** | 9 U.S.C. Section 16 | Appeals from orders refusing to compel arbitration |

### Standards of Review

Standards of review determine the degree of deference the appellate court gives to the lower court's decisions:

**De novo review (questions of law):** The appellate court reviews legal conclusions without deference. Applicable to statutory interpretation, constitutional questions, and mixed questions of law and fact where the legal component predominates.

**Clearly erroneous (findings of fact, bench trials):** Under FRCP 52(a)(6), the appellate court will not set aside a finding of fact unless, after reviewing all the evidence, it is "left with the definite and firm conviction that a mistake has been made" (United States v. U.S. Gypsum Co., 333 U.S. 364 (1948)).

**Abuse of discretion:** Applied to procedural and evidentiary rulings, including discovery orders, sanctions, and case management decisions. The appellate court defers unless the trial court's decision was arbitrary, irrational, or contrary to law.

**Substantial evidence (administrative review):** Under the Administrative Procedure Act (5 U.S.C. Section 706), appellate courts review agency fact-finding under the substantial evidence standard, asking whether a reasonable mind could accept the evidence as adequate.

### Supreme Court Certiorari

**28 U.S.C. Section 1254:** Cases may be reviewed by the Supreme Court by writ of certiorari upon the petition of any party. The Court exercises broad discretion, granting certiorari in approximately 70-80 of the 7,000-8,000 petitions filed annually. Under Supreme Court Rule 10, factors favoring certiorari include:
- Circuit splits (conflicting holdings among federal courts of appeals)
- Important federal questions not yet addressed by the Court
- Decisions conflicting with Supreme Court precedent

### Harmless Error

**28 U.S.C. Section 2111:** Courts of appeals must give judgment "without regard to errors or defects which do not affect the substantial rights of the parties." The harmless error doctrine prevents reversal for errors that did not affect the outcome:
- **Civil cases:** Error is harmless if it more likely than not did not affect the verdict.
- **Criminal cases (constitutional error):** Chapman v. California, 386 U.S. 18 (1967): the prosecution must prove beyond a reasonable doubt that the error did not contribute to the verdict.
- **Structural error:** Certain constitutional errors (e.g., denial of counsel, biased judge) are never harmless and require automatic reversal.

### Key Supreme Court Appellate Procedure Decisions

| Case | Year | Holding |
|------|------|---------|
| Cohen v. Beneficial Industrial Loan Corp. | 1949 | Collateral order doctrine established |
| Anderson v. Bessemer City | 1985 | Clearly erroneous standard clarified |
| Pullman-Standard v. Swint | 1982 | De novo review for ultimate legal conclusions |
| Mohawk Industries v. Carpenter | 2009 | Attorney-client privilege orders not collateral orders |
| Microsoft Corp. v. Baker | 2017 | Voluntary dismissal after class certification denial does not create appellate jurisdiction |

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's appellate system provides comprehensive multi-tier review through Berufung (full appeal), Revision (legal review), Beschwerde (complaint), and the Verfassungsbeschwerde (constitutional complaint).

### Berufung (Full Appeal) -- Civil Proceedings

**Sections 511-541 ZPO** govern the first appeal in civil matters:

- **Section 511 (Admissibility):** Berufung is available against first-instance judgments when: (a) the value of the appeal exceeds EUR 600; or (b) the first-instance court grants leave to appeal because the case involves a question of fundamental significance, the development of the law or the assurance of uniform jurisprudence requires a decision by the appellate court.
- **Section 513 (Grounds):** The Berufung may be based on the ground that the decision rests on a violation of law or that the facts to be taken as a basis for the decision justify a different decision.
- **Section 517 (Time):** The Berufung must be filed within one month of service of the complete judgment.
- **Section 520 (Statement of Grounds):** The appellant must file a statement of grounds (Berufungsbegründung) within two months of service of the judgment, identifying the errors alleged and the evidence supporting them.

### 2001 ZPO Reform and Limitations on Berufung

The comprehensive reform of the ZPO in 2001 significantly limited the Berufung:

- **Section 529 (Basis for Appellate Decision):** The appellate court bases its decision on the facts established by the first-instance court, unless concrete grounds for doubt about the completeness or correctness of the relevant factual findings exist.
- **Section 531 (New Attacks and Defenses):** New facts and evidence are admissible in the Berufung only if: (a) they concern an aspect the first-instance court demonstrably overlooked or deemed irrelevant; (b) they were not raised due to a procedural defect in the first instance; or (c) they arose after the first-instance oral hearing.

These restrictions moved the German Berufung closer to a limited review model, though it remains more open than U.S. appellate review on facts.

### Revision (Legal Review) -- Bundesgerichtshof

**Sections 542-566 ZPO** govern appeals to the BGH:

- **Section 543 (Admissibility):** Revision is available only when: (a) the appellate court grants leave (Zulassung der Revision); or (b) the BGH grants leave on complaint (Nichtzulassungsbeschwerde under Section 544 ZPO). Leave is granted when the case involves a question of fundamental significance, the development of the law requires a decision, or there is a divergence from BGH or BVerfG jurisprudence.
- **Section 545 (Grounds):** The Revision may be based only on violations of law. Factual findings of the appellate court are binding on the BGH unless they are affected by a procedural error.
- **Section 546 (Definition of Violation of Law):** A law is violated when a legal norm is not applied or is applied incorrectly.

### Criminal Appeals

**StPO Sections 296-358:** Criminal appeals follow a parallel structure:

- **Berufung (Sections 312-332 StPO):** Full appeal on facts and law from the Amtsgericht to the Landgericht. Provides a complete new trial (neue Hauptverhandlung) with fresh evidence.
- **Revision (Sections 333-358 StPO):** Appeal on law only to the Oberlandesgericht or BGH. The Revision examines whether the judgment rests on a violation of law, including violations of procedural rules (Verfahrensrüge) and substantive law (Sachrüge).

### Verfassungsbeschwerde (Constitutional Complaint)

**Article 93(1)(4a) GG and Sections 90-95 BVerfGG:**

The Verfassungsbeschwerde is a unique feature of German constitutional law, enabling any person to challenge an act of public authority -- including legislation, administrative acts, and judicial decisions -- that allegedly violates fundamental rights under the Basic Law.

**Requirements:**
1. **Exhaustion of remedies (Section 90(2) BVerfGG):** The complainant must have exhausted all available legal remedies.
2. **Specificity (Section 92 BVerfGG):** The complaint must specifically identify the fundamental right allegedly violated and explain how the challenged act violates it.
3. **Personal, present, and direct effect:** The complainant must be personally, presently, and directly affected by the challenged act.
4. **Time limit (Section 93(1) BVerfGG):** The complaint must be filed within one month of service of the last decision on ordinary legal remedies.

**Acceptance for Decision (Section 93a BVerfGG):** The Federal Constitutional Court accepts a Verfassungsbeschwerde for decision only if: (a) the case raises a constitutional question of fundamental significance; or (b) acceptance is indicated for the enforcement of fundamental rights, particularly where the complainant would suffer a particularly grave disadvantage from the refusal to decide.

**Statistics:** The BVerfG receives approximately 5,000-6,000 Verfassungsbeschwerden annually but accepts only 1-2 percent for full decision. However, even the screening process provides a form of constitutional oversight, as the BVerfG's three-judge panels (Kammern) may grant relief without full Senate review in clear cases under Section 93c BVerfGG.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR and the Right to Appeal

The ECHR does not guarantee an absolute right to appeal in civil matters. However, when appellate proceedings exist, Article 6 fair trial guarantees apply.

**Article 2 of Protocol 7 (Criminal Appeals):** "Everyone convicted of a criminal offence by a tribunal shall have the right to have his conviction or sentence reviewed by a higher tribunal." The exercise of this right may be subject to exceptions for minor offences or cases where the person was tried in the first instance by the highest tribunal or was convicted following an appeal against acquittal.

**Delcourt v. Belgium, Application No. 2689/65, Judgment of January 17, 1970:** The Court held that Article 6 applies to proceedings before appellate courts. While the article does not require States to establish courts of appeal, once created, such courts must conform to Article 6 requirements.

**Meftah and Others v. France [GC], Application Nos. 32911/96, 35237/97, and 34595/97, Judgment of July 26, 2002:** The Grand Chamber held that requiring representation by a specialized lawyer (avocat aux Conseils) for cassation appeals does not violate Article 6, provided the restriction serves a legitimate aim and is proportionate.

**Cudak v. Lithuania [GC], Application No. 15869/02, Judgment of March 23, 2010:** The Grand Chamber addressed limitations on access to appellate courts, holding that restrictions on the right of appeal must not impair the very essence of the right and must be proportionate to the legitimate aim pursued.

### Leave to Appeal Under ECHR

**Ekbatani v. Sweden, Application No. 10563/83, Judgment of May 26, 1988:** The Court held that where an appellate court has full jurisdiction to examine questions of fact and law, Article 6 may require an oral hearing and the personal attendance of the defendant, particularly when the court must assess the credibility of witnesses.

**Helmers v. Sweden, Application No. 11826/85, Judgment of October 29, 1991:** The Court found a violation where an appellate court refused leave to appeal without adequately considering the merits of the applicant's arguments.

### EU Appellate Framework

**Article 256(1) TFEU:** An appeal from the General Court to the Court of Justice may be brought on points of law only, within two months of notification of the decision. Grounds include:
- Lack of competence of the General Court
- Breach of procedure adversely affecting the appellant's interests
- Infringement of EU law by the General Court

**CJEU, Commission v. Schneider Electric (C-440/07 P, 2009):** The Court clarified that the scope of review on appeal is limited to determining whether the General Court correctly applied EU law, including whether it distorted the facts or evidence.

### Preliminary Reference Procedure

**Article 267 TFEU:** While not an appeal in the strict sense, the preliminary reference procedure allows national courts to refer questions of EU law interpretation to the CJEU. The procedure operates as a form of appellate-level legal clarification, ensuring uniform interpretation across member states. The CJEU's decision is binding on the referring court and on all national courts applying the same provision.

**CILFIT (C-283/81, 1982):** The CJEU established that national courts of last instance are obligated to make a preliminary reference unless: (a) the question is irrelevant; (b) the provision has already been interpreted by the CJEU (acte eclaire); or (c) the correct application of EU law is so obvious as to leave no room for doubt (acte clair).

## Comparative Analysis

### Scope of Appellate Review

The most significant divergence concerns the scope of appellate review of factual findings. Germany's Berufung permits the most extensive factual review, though limited by the 2001 reform. South Korea's first appeal provides de novo factual review. The United States applies the most restrictive standard, with clearly erroneous review of trial court factual findings in bench trials and virtual deference to jury findings. The differing scopes reflect fundamental choices about the function of appellate courts: error correction (broader review) versus law development (narrower review).

### Access to the Highest Court

All jurisdictions restrict access to their highest courts, but through different mechanisms. The U.S. Supreme Court's certiorari process is entirely discretionary. Germany's BGH requires leave to appeal based on legal significance or divergence. South Korea's Supreme Court accepts appeals limited to legal error. The common trend is toward greater selectivity at the highest level, reflecting the shift from error correction to law-clarifying functions.

### Constitutional Complaints

Germany's Verfassungsbeschwerde is unique in its scope, permitting direct challenge of judicial decisions for fundamental rights violations. South Korea's constitutional complaint explicitly excludes ordinary court judgments (Article 68(1) of the Constitutional Court Act), creating a significant structural difference. The United States integrates constitutional review into the ordinary appellate process through certiorari. The ECHR provides a supranational form of constitutional complaint through individual applications under Article 34.

### Interlocutory Appeals

All jurisdictions restrict interlocutory appeals to promote efficient litigation, but the exceptions vary. The United States has developed the most elaborate framework of exceptions through Section 1292, the collateral order doctrine, and mandamus. Germany permits interlocutory appeals (Beschwerde) for specifically enumerated orders. South Korea follows a similar approach with its immediate appeal (즉시항고) system. The ECHR has not addressed interlocutory appeal rights specifically.
