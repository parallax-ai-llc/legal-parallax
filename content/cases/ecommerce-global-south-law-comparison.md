## Summary

E-commerce regulation across the Global South reflects divergent approaches to governing digital marketplaces, platform economies, and cross-border digital trade, shaped by each region's relationship with multinational technology companies, domestic industrial policy, and consumer protection traditions. China's E-Commerce Law (电子商务法, effective 1 January 2019) is the world's first comprehensive e-commerce statute, governing platform operators, in-platform business operators, and self-built website operators, with landmark provisions on platform liability, consumer protection, and anti-monopoly obligations. India regulates e-commerce primarily through the Foreign Exchange Management Act (FEMA) and Press Note 2 of 2018 (consolidated FDI Policy 2020), which restricts foreign-owned e-commerce entities to the "marketplace model" and prohibits the "inventory model," alongside the Consumer Protection (E-Commerce) Rules 2020 and the proposed Digital India Act. Nigeria's National Information Technology Development Agency (NITDA) Act 2007 and the Nigeria Data Protection Act 2023 provide the regulatory backbone, supplemented by the Federal Competition and Consumer Protection Act (FCCPA) 2018. Brazil's Marco Civil da Internet (Lei 12.965/2014, the "Internet Bill of Rights") establishes principles of net neutrality, freedom of expression, and privacy as the foundational framework for digital commerce, complemented by the Consumer Protection Code (CDC, Lei 8.078/1990) and the E-Commerce Decree (Decreto 7.962/2013). The Philippines' Electronic Commerce Act (Republic Act No. 8792, 2000) was one of Asia's earliest e-commerce laws, providing legal recognition for electronic documents and signatures, subsequently supplemented by the Internet Transactions Act (Republic Act No. 11967, 2024).

## China (中国)

### Legal Framework

China's e-commerce regulatory framework is the world's most comprehensive, reflecting the sector's significance (over RMB 15 trillion in online retail sales in 2023, approximately 30% of total retail):

- **E-Commerce Law (中华人民共和国电子商务法, effective 1 January 2019):** The world's first standalone comprehensive e-commerce statute, containing 89 articles organized into seven chapters:
  - **Platform Operator Obligations (Arts. 27-46):** Platform operators must: verify and register the identity, address, and contact information of in-platform business operators (Art. 27); establish and publish platform rules for service agreements, transaction rules, and intellectual property protection (Art. 32); not unreasonably restrict in-platform operators' transactions or impose unreasonable conditions (Art. 35, the "er xuan yi" provision prohibiting exclusive dealing); display search results clearly, distinguishing paid promotions from natural results (Art. 40).
  - **Platform Liability (Art. 38):** If a platform operator knows or should know that goods or services sold on its platform infringe on consumers' legitimate rights and interests and fails to take necessary measures, the platform bears joint and several liability. If the platform fails to verify the qualifications of in-platform operators or fails to protect consumer safety, and this results in damage, the platform bears "corresponding" (相应的) liability.
  - **Consumer Protection (Arts. 17-25):** Seven-day no-reason return right for online purchases (Art. 25, mirroring the Consumer Protection Law Art. 25); prohibition of fabricating transactions or reviews (Art. 17); requirement to disclose deposit terms and refund procedures (Art. 21).
  - **Cross-Border E-Commerce (Arts. 71-73):** Promotes cross-border e-commerce through pilot free trade zones, customs facilitation, and mutual recognition of electronic documents.
- **Anti-Monopoly Law (反垄断法, 2007, revised 2022) and Platform Economy Antitrust Guidelines (2021):** The State Administration for Market Regulation (SAMR) issued the "Guidelines for Anti-Monopoly in the Platform Economy" in February 2021, targeting exclusive dealing arrangements ("er xuan yi" 二选一), algorithmic price discrimination, data-driven barriers to entry, and "kill the acquainted customer" (大数据杀熟) practices. These guidelines provided the basis for the landmark Alibaba fine.
- **Provisions on the Management of Internet Live Marketing Information Content (2023):** Regulates live-streaming e-commerce (a RMB 4.9 trillion market in 2023), requiring platform registration, age restrictions for live-stream hosts, and product quality verification.

### Notable Enforcement

- **Alibaba Group Antitrust Fine (April 2021):** SAMR imposed a record RMB 18.228 billion (approximately USD 2.8 billion) fine on Alibaba Group for abusing market dominance through "er xuan yi" exclusive dealing practices, requiring merchants to choose exclusively between Alibaba platforms and competitors. The fine represented 4% of Alibaba's 2019 domestic revenue, the largest antitrust penalty in Chinese history.
- **Meituan Antitrust Investigation (October 2021):** SAMR fined Meituan (food delivery platform) RMB 3.442 billion (approximately USD 530 million) for exclusive dealing practices with restaurant partners, applying a 3% revenue penalty.
- **Pinduoduo/Temu Regulatory Scrutiny (2023-2024):** Regulatory attention to ultra-low-price e-commerce models, quality control, and labor conditions in supply chains, reflecting evolving enforcement priorities.
- **Cross-Border E-Commerce Pilot Zones:** China has established 165 Cross-Border E-Commerce Comprehensive Pilot Zones as of 2024, with simplified customs procedures, favorable tax treatment (no customs duty on retail imports below RMB 5,000/transaction and RMB 26,000/year), and streamlined foreign exchange settlement.

## India (भारत)

### Legal Framework

India's e-commerce regulation is distinctive for its explicit use of FDI policy to shape market structure, reflecting concerns about foreign platform dominance over domestic retailers:

- **FDI Policy -- Press Note 2 of 2018 (consolidated in FDI Policy 2020):** The most consequential e-commerce regulation in India, issued by the Department for Promotion of Industry and Internal Trade (DPIIT):
  - **Marketplace Model:** 100% FDI permitted only in the marketplace (platform) model, where the e-commerce entity acts as a technology facilitator connecting buyers and sellers. The entity may not own inventory or influence pricing.
  - **Inventory Model Prohibition:** FDI is prohibited in inventory-based e-commerce. An e-commerce entity with FDI may not exercise ownership or control over inventory (except for B2B marketplace operations).
  - **Entity-Related Party Restrictions:** No entity in which the marketplace entity or its group companies hold equity shall be permitted to sell on the platform. If the marketplace entity has equity in a seller, purchases from that seller cannot exceed 25% of total sales on the platform.
  - **Pricing Restrictions:** The marketplace entity shall not directly or indirectly influence the sale price of goods or services and shall maintain a level playing field.
  - **Cashback/Discount Restrictions:** Cashbacks, discounts, and incentives offered by e-commerce entities may not be platform-funded in a manner that creates an uneven playing field.
- **Consumer Protection (E-Commerce) Rules, 2020:** Issued under the Consumer Protection Act 2019 (Section 101), these rules require: display of seller details, total price, return/refund/exchange policy, grievance redress mechanism, country of origin for imported goods, and prohibition of manipulating search results or reviews. The 2021 proposed amendments (not finalized) sought to introduce flash sale restrictions, algorithmic transparency, compliance officers, and a registration requirement with DPIIT.
- **Information Technology (Intermediary Guidelines and Digital Media Ethics Code) Rules, 2021 (IT Rules 2021):** Require significant social media intermediaries (with over 5 million registered users) to appoint compliance officers (Chief Compliance Officer, Nodal Contact Person, Resident Grievance Officer), implement automated monitoring of certain content types, and enable traceability of the first originator of messages (challenged in multiple High Courts).
- **Open Network for Digital Commerce (ONDC, 2022):** A government-backed initiative to create an open, interoperable e-commerce network using Beckn protocol, allowing buyers and sellers to transact across platforms without being locked into a single marketplace. ONDC aims to democratize digital commerce and reduce platform dominance. By 2024, ONDC had over 535,000 sellers and processed approximately 12 million monthly transactions.

### Notable Cases

- **Confederation of All India Traders (CAIT) v. Flipkart/Amazon (2020-2024):** CAIT has filed multiple complaints with the Competition Commission of India (CCI) alleging that Amazon and Flipkart violate FDI policy through related party arrangements, deep discounting, and preferential seller treatment. The CCI ordered investigations against Amazon and Flipkart in 2020, with proceedings ongoing.
- **Amazon/Future Group Dispute (2020-2022):** Amazon's investment in Future Coupons (holding company of Future Retail) and subsequent arbitration at the Singapore International Arbitration Centre (SIAC) to block Future Group's sale to Reliance, involving complex questions of FDI compliance and platform-retailer relationships. The Supreme Court in Amazon.com NV Investment Holdings LLC v. Future Retail Limited (2021) upheld the enforceability of the SIAC emergency arbitrator's order.
- **CCI v. Google (Android) (2022):** The CCI fined Google INR 1,337.76 crore for anti-competitive practices related to Android OS, including mandatory pre-installation of Google apps. While not exclusively an e-commerce case, the decision impacts the Google Play Store's dominance in app distribution and in-app commerce.

## Africa (Nigeria and South Africa)

### Nigeria

#### Legal Framework

- **NITDA Act 2007:** The National Information Technology Development Agency Act established NITDA as the regulatory authority for information technology in Nigeria, with powers to develop regulations for e-commerce, data protection, and digital services.
- **Federal Competition and Consumer Protection Act (FCCPA) 2018:** Established the Federal Competition and Consumer Protection Commission (FCCPC, replacing the Consumer Protection Council) with expanded mandate covering digital markets. Section 17 grants the FCCPC power to investigate anti-competitive practices including by digital platforms.
- **Nigeria Startup Act 2022:** Provides a regulatory framework for technology startups including e-commerce companies, establishing the Startup Support and Engagement Portal, the Startup Investment Seed Fund, and regulatory sandbox provisions.
- **Significant Economic Presence (SEP) Order (2020, Finance Act 2019 s4):** Introduces a 6% tax on income derived by non-resident companies with "significant economic presence" in Nigeria through digital platforms, including streaming, downloading, data collection, and intermediation. The SEP threshold is set at NGN 25 million in annual turnover from Nigeria.
- **E-Commerce Market:** Jumia Technologies (the first African tech company to list on NYSE, 2019) and Konga are the leading platforms. Nigeria's e-commerce market was valued at approximately USD 8 billion in 2024, with significant growth constrained by logistics infrastructure, payment system limitations, and trust deficits.

### South Africa

#### Legal Framework

- **Electronic Communications and Transactions Act (ECTA, No. 25 of 2002):** South Africa's foundational e-commerce statute, providing: legal recognition of electronic communications, data messages, and electronic signatures (Chapters II-III); consumer protection for electronic transactions including cooling-off period for direct marketing and disclosure requirements (Chapter VII); limitation of liability for service providers acting as intermediaries (Chapter XI -- the "safe harbor" provisions similar to the EU E-Commerce Directive); and establishment of the .za Domain Name Authority.
- **Consumer Protection Act (CPA, No. 68 of 2008):** Applies to all consumer transactions including electronic transactions, providing a 5-day cooling-off period for direct marketing (s16), right to fair value, good quality, and safety (s54-56), and prohibition of unconscionable conduct.
- **Competition Act (No. 89 of 1998, amended 2018):** The Competition Commission has investigated digital platform practices, including the Takealot/Superbalist merger inquiry and investigations into platform pricing algorithms.
- **South African Revenue Service (SARS) -- VAT on Electronic Services (2014):** South Africa was one of the first countries globally to impose VAT on imported electronic services, requiring foreign electronic service providers to register for and charge 15% VAT on supplies to South African consumers. The Electronic Services Regulations (2014, updated 2019) list taxable services including e-books, music, games, and software.

## Latin America (Brazil, Mexico, and Colombia)

### Legal Framework

Latin American e-commerce regulation is characterized by strong consumer protection traditions rooted in civil law and social constitutionalism:

- **Brazil -- Marco Civil da Internet (Lei 12.965/2014):** Often called the "Internet Bill of Rights," this statute establishes foundational principles for internet governance in Brazil:
  - **Net Neutrality (Arts. 9-10):** Prohibits network operators from blocking, throttling, or discriminating against internet traffic, subject to limited exceptions for network management and prioritization of emergency services.
  - **Platform Liability (Art. 19):** Internet application providers are liable for third-party content only after receiving a specific court order to remove such content and failing to comply. This "judicial notice" model provides stronger platform protection than the DMCA's notice-and-takedown system, reflecting Brazilian free expression values.
  - **Privacy (Arts. 7-8):** Establishes internet users' rights including inviolability and secrecy of stored communications (Art. 7(II)), non-disclosure of internet connection and application access records to third parties without court order (Art. 7(III)), and consent requirement for collection, use, and storage of personal data (Art. 7(IX)).
  - **Data Retention (Art. 13-15):** Connection providers must retain connection logs for one year; application providers must retain application access logs for six months. These provisions have been critical for law enforcement and civil litigation.
- **Brazil -- Consumer Protection Code (CDC, Lei 8.078/1990):** Brazil's consumer law applies fully to e-commerce transactions. Article 49 grants consumers a 7-day right of withdrawal (arrependimento) from purchases made outside the commercial establishment (including online), during which the consumer may return the product for a full refund without justification. The CDC's strict (objective) product liability (Arts. 12-14) applies to e-commerce platforms in their capacity as suppliers.
- **Brazil -- E-Commerce Decree (Decreto 7.962/2013):** Supplements the CDC with e-commerce specific requirements: clear and visible identification of the supplier (CNPJ, physical address); summary of contract before purchase; easy and accessible mechanisms for exercising the right of withdrawal; and prominent customer service channels.
- **Mexico -- Federal Consumer Protection Law (LFPC) and NOM-151-SCFI-2002:** The LFPC applies to electronic transactions, with PROFECO (Federal Consumer Protection Agency) as the enforcement body. NOM-151 establishes requirements for conservation of data messages (electronic invoicing, electronic signatures). The 2021 amendment to the LFPC added specific provisions for digital platforms including registration, transparency, and consumer complaint mechanisms.
- **Colombia -- Law 527/1999 (E-Commerce Law):** Provides legal recognition for electronic messages, digital signatures, and e-commerce transactions, based on the UNCITRAL Model Law on Electronic Commerce. Complemented by Decree 1074/2015 (regulatory framework) and the Consumer Statute (Law 1480/2011) that extends consumer protection to electronic transactions.

### Notable Developments

- **Mercado Libre Dominance:** Mercado Libre (Argentina-headquartered but operating across Latin America) dominates e-commerce in Brazil, Mexico, Colombia, and other markets, with a market capitalization exceeding USD 85 billion (2024). Its fintech arm, Mercado Pago, has become a significant player in digital payments.
- **Brazil -- STF WhatsApp Blocking Cases (2015-2016):** The Supreme Federal Tribunal addressed multiple instances of judicial orders blocking WhatsApp nationwide (affecting 100+ million users), ruling that proportionality limits the power of individual judges to impose internet blockades, with implications for e-commerce platform enforcement.
- **Mexico -- PROFECO v. Uber (2023):** PROFECO investigated Uber's pricing practices and cancellation policies, imposing fines for violations of consumer protection provisions in the LFPC related to transparent pricing and complaint handling.

## Southeast Asia (Indonesia and Philippines)

### Legal Framework

- **Philippines -- Electronic Commerce Act (RA 8792, 2000):** One of Asia's earliest e-commerce laws, providing legal recognition for electronic data messages, electronic signatures, and electronic documents (including their admissibility as evidence). The Act also addresses: electronic contracts (formation, validity); obligations of parties to electronic transactions; and hacking and piracy offenses.
- **Philippines -- Internet Transactions Act (RA 11967, 2024):** Enacted to address gaps in RA 8792, this law:
  - Requires all e-commerce platforms and online merchants to register with the Department of Trade and Industry (DTI) and display registration information on their platforms.
  - Establishes an Internet Transactions Bureau under the DTI to monitor compliance and resolve consumer complaints.
  - Requires platforms to implement mechanisms for product returns, refunds, and exchanges within a specified period.
  - Imposes liability on platforms for failure to take reasonable measures against counterfeit products and fraudulent merchants.
  - Penalties include fines of PHP 100,000-1,000,000 for first offenses and PHP 500,000-2,000,000 for subsequent offenses, with potential platform suspension for repeat violations.
- **Philippines -- Department of Trade and Industry Department Administrative Order No. 21-04 (2021):** Established guidelines for e-commerce enterprises including price display, product information, customer service, and data protection requirements.
- **Indonesia -- Government Regulation No. 80/2019 on Trade through Electronic Systems (GR 80/2019):** Indonesia's primary e-commerce regulation, requiring:
  - All electronic merchants (in-country and cross-border) to obtain business licenses through the Online Single Submission (OSS) system.
  - Foreign e-commerce platforms with over a specified number of Indonesian users to establish local entities or appoint local representatives.
  - Platforms to verify seller identities and maintain transaction records.
  - Consumer protection obligations including product information disclosure, complaint mechanisms, and return/refund policies.
- **Indonesia -- Government Regulation No. 36/2023 (amending GR 80/2019):** This regulation, issued in response to concerns about the dominance of social commerce (particularly TikTok Shop), prohibited social media platforms from conducting direct e-commerce transactions. The regulation requires separation between social media and e-commerce functionalities, leading TikTok to acquire a 75% stake in Tokopedia (GoTo's e-commerce unit) in December 2023 for approximately USD 1.5 billion.
- **Indonesia -- Ministerial Regulation No. 31/2023 (Private Scope Electronic System Operators):** Requires all electronic system operators serving Indonesian users (including foreign e-commerce platforms) to register with the Ministry of Communication, establish local registered offices, and store certain data domestically.

### Notable Developments

- **TikTok Shop Ban and Tokopedia Acquisition (Indonesia, 2023):** GR 36/2023 forced TikTok to shut down its in-app e-commerce feature (TikTok Shop) in Indonesia on 4 October 2023. TikTok subsequently acquired 75.01% of Tokopedia from GoTo Group in December 2023, relaunching as "Shop Tokopedia powered by TikTok" in January 2024, complying with the separation requirement.
- **Shopee Dominance:** Shopee (Sea Group, Singapore) dominates e-commerce in both Indonesia and the Philippines, with approximately 40% market share in both countries. Regulatory attention has focused on Shopee's pricing practices, seller subsidies, and data handling.
- **Philippines -- Digital Economy:** E-commerce in the Philippines reached approximately PHP 1.1 trillion (USD 19 billion) in 2024, representing approximately 8% of GDP. The Internet Transactions Act is expected to accelerate formalization of online merchants, many of whom currently operate through social media (Facebook Marketplace, Instagram) without DTI registration.
- **Indonesia -- E-Commerce Market:** Indonesia's e-commerce GMV exceeded USD 62 billion in 2024, the largest in Southeast Asia. The Tokopedia-TikTok Shop combination, Shopee, Bukalapak, and Blibli compete for market share, with regulatory attention focused on seller protection, tax compliance, and the impact of cross-border low-value goods on domestic manufacturers.

## Comparative Analysis

### Platform Liability Models

The five regions represent distinct platform liability paradigms: (1) **Joint liability** (China), where platforms bear joint and several liability for defective products and consumer harm; (2) **Safe harbor with FDI restrictions** (India), where platforms enjoy intermediary safe harbor under IT Act s79 but face structural restrictions through FDI policy; (3) **Judicial notice model** (Brazil), where platforms are liable for third-party content only after failing to comply with a court order; (4) **Notice and takedown** (South Africa), where ECTA provides conditional safe harbor similar to the EU E-Commerce Directive; and (5) **Evolving platform accountability** (Philippines, Indonesia), where recent legislation (RA 11967, GR 80/2019) is shifting toward greater platform responsibility.

### Market Structure and Industrial Policy

China and India have most actively used regulation to shape e-commerce market structure. China's antitrust enforcement against Alibaba and Meituan sought to limit platform monopoly power, while India's FDI restrictions aim to prevent foreign-owned platforms from displacing domestic small retailers. Indonesia's social commerce regulation (GR 36/2023) represents a novel form of market structure intervention targeting platform convergence. Brazil, Mexico, and South Africa have adopted more market-neutral regulatory approaches.

### Digital Taxation

All five regions have implemented or are implementing digital service taxes targeting cross-border e-commerce revenues: China through VAT on imported digital services; India's now-withdrawn Equalization Levy (replaced by reliance on OECD Pillar One/Two framework); Nigeria's 6% Significant Economic Presence tax; South Africa's VAT on electronic services; Brazil's ISS and PIS/COFINS; Mexico's 16% VAT on digital services; Indonesia's 10% VAT; and the Philippines' 12% VAT on digital services (RA 11967). These taxes reflect the Global South's collective effort to capture tax revenue from digital business models designed to minimize physical presence in market jurisdictions.

### Consumer Protection

Consumer protection intensity varies significantly. Brazil provides the strongest consumer protections (7-day unconditional withdrawal, strict product liability, accessible consumer courts -- JECs/Procon). China's 7-day no-reason return is similarly strong. India's rules are detailed but enforcement is inconsistent. The Philippines' RA 11967 represents a significant upgrade. Nigeria and Indonesia have the weakest consumer protection enforcement in e-commerce.
