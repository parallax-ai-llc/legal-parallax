## Summary

Land reform -- the redistribution or restructuring of agricultural land ownership, tenure, and use rights -- has been one of the most consequential and politically contentious areas of law across modern history, shaping rural economies, social structures, and political stability in virtually every jurisdiction. South Korea's land reform program, codified through the Agricultural Land Reform Amendment Act (농지개혁법) of 1949 and subsequent amendments, dismantled the colonial-era landlord system inherited from Japan's occupation, redistributing approximately 330,000 hectares to roughly 900,000 tenant farmers under a "land to the tiller" principle, and current regulation under the Farmland Act (농지법, Act No. 4817 of 1994, most recently amended 2023) restricts agricultural land ownership to actual cultivators and regulates farmland conversion. The United States has pursued land reform primarily through the Homestead Act of 1862, which transferred approximately 270 million acres of federal land to private settlers, and through ongoing programs administered by the U.S. Department of Agriculture, including the Farm Service Agency's loan programs and the landmark Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 which allocated $3.1 billion for debt relief for socially disadvantaged farmers. Germany's land reform history is defined by the radical expropriation of estates exceeding 100 hectares in the Soviet occupation zone under Order No. 209 (1945), the subsequent collectivization under the GDR's Agricultural Production Cooperatives (Landwirtschaftliche Produktionsgenossenschaften), and post-reunification restitution and privatization managed by the Bodenverwertungs- und -verwaltungs GmbH (BVVG) under the Agriculture Adjustment Act (Landwirtschaftsanpassungsgesetz) of 1990. At the European and international level, the Council of Europe has addressed land reform through Protocol No. 1 to the ECHR (Article 1, protection of property), the European Court of Human Rights has developed extensive jurisprudence balancing property rights against the public interest in land redistribution, and the EU's Common Agricultural Policy (CAP) indirectly shapes land use and tenure patterns across all member states through direct payments and rural development programs.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's land reform is among the most successful in global history, fundamentally transforming the agrarian social structure within a single decade. The Agricultural Land Reform Amendment Act (농지개혁법), enacted on June 21, 1949, and implemented beginning in March 1950, established the principle that only actual tillers of the soil could own agricultural land. The reform targeted the highly concentrated landlord system inherited from the Joseon Dynasty and reinforced during the Japanese colonial period (1910-1945), under which approximately 3 percent of landowners controlled roughly 64 percent of all arable land.

Under the 1949 Act, the government purchased all farmland holdings exceeding 3 jeongbo (approximately 3 hectares) at a price equivalent to 150 percent of average annual harvest value, payable in government bonds over five years, and redistributed the land to tenant farmers at the same price, payable in kind over five years. Approximately 330,000 hectares were redistributed to roughly 900,000 tenant households, reducing tenancy rates from approximately 49 percent to less than 7 percent by 1956.

The current regulatory framework is governed by the Farmland Act (농지법, Act No. 4817), enacted on December 22, 1994, and most recently amended in 2023. Key provisions include:

**Article 6 (Owner-Cultivator Principle / 자경원칙):** Agricultural land shall, in principle, be owned by persons who cultivate or intend to cultivate such land. This foundational principle continues the 1949 reform's core objective.

**Article 8 (Farmland Acquisition Qualification Certificate / 농지취득자격증명):** Any person seeking to acquire farmland must obtain a Farmland Acquisition Qualification Certificate from the local government, demonstrating the capacity and intent to cultivate the land.

**Article 10 (Restrictions on Ownership by Non-Cultivators / 비농업인 소유 제한):** Non-cultivators may own farmland only in specifically enumerated circumstances, including inheritance (up to 10,000 square meters), farmland within designated agricultural promotion zones acquired before the Act's enactment, and farmland acquired by the Korean Rural Community Corporation for public purposes.

### Penalties

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| Acquisition of farmland by false qualification certificate | Mandatory disposition order; fine up to 50 million KRW |
| Failure to comply with disposition order | Fine up to 20% of land value per year |
| Illegal conversion of farmland | Restoration order; imprisonment up to 5 years or fine up to 50 million KRW |
| Violation of farmland lease restrictions | Fine up to 10 million KRW |

### Contemporary Challenges

The owner-cultivator principle has faced persistent challenges from urbanization, aging rural populations, and speculative investment. The 2023 amendments strengthened enforcement mechanisms against illegal farmland ownership by non-cultivators, introduced enhanced monitoring through the Farmland Management Information System (농지관리정보시스템), and expanded penalties for fraudulent acquisition. The Korean Supreme Court in Decision 2020Da248952 (2021) confirmed that farmland acquired through fraudulent qualification certificates may be subject to compulsory disposition orders without compensation for improvements.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has addressed land reform through a patchwork of federal land distribution programs, anti-discrimination legislation, and agricultural support mechanisms rather than through a single comprehensive reform statute.

**Homestead Act of 1862 (12 Stat. 392):** Signed by President Abraham Lincoln on May 20, 1862, the Act provided 160 acres of unappropriated public land to any citizen or intended citizen who was the head of a household or over 21 years of age, subject to five years of continuous residence and cultivation. Approximately 1.6 million homestead claims were filed, distributing roughly 270 million acres (10 percent of U.S. land area) before the Act's repeal in 1976 (1986 in Alaska). The Homestead Act explicitly excluded land already occupied by Native Americans, facilitating the dispossession of Indigenous peoples from ancestral territories.

**Southern Homestead Act of 1866:** Extended homesteading rights to freed enslaved persons in five southern states, though its impact was severely limited by discriminatory enforcement and the absence of complementary economic support.

**Indian Reorganization Act of 1934 (25 U.S.C. Section 5101 et seq.):** Reversed the Dawes Act's allotment policy, restoring tribal land ownership and establishing a land acquisition program for Native American nations. The Act halted the transfer of approximately 90 million acres of tribal land to non-Indigenous owners that had occurred under the Dawes Act of 1887.

**Farm Bill (Agricultural Improvement Act):** Reauthorized approximately every five years, the Farm Bill governs federal agricultural policy including commodity programs, conservation programs, and farm credit. The most recent reauthorization (2024) continues programs supporting beginning farmers, socially disadvantaged farmers, and conservation practices.

**Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 (Public Law 117-169):** Section 22007 allocated $3.1 billion for debt relief for farmers who experienced discrimination in USDA lending programs, addressing decades of documented racial discrimination in farm credit allocation.

### State-Level Foreign Ownership Restrictions

| State | Restriction |
|-------|------------|
| Iowa | Prohibits foreign ownership of agricultural land (Iowa Code Section 9I.3) |
| Nebraska | Constitutional prohibition on corporate farming (Article XII, Section 8) |
| North Dakota | Prohibits corporate ownership of farmland (N.D.C.C. Section 10-06.1) |
| Minnesota | Restricts corporate and foreign ownership of agricultural land (Minn. Stat. Section 500.24) |
| Wisconsin | Prohibits foreign ownership of more than 640 acres (Wis. Stat. Section 710.02) |

The Agricultural Foreign Investment Disclosure Act (AFIDA) of 1978 (7 U.S.C. Section 3501 et seq.) requires foreign persons who acquire or dispose of interests in U.S. agricultural land to report such transactions to the Secretary of Agriculture through the Farm Service Agency.

### Landmark Cases

**Wickard v. Filburn, 317 U.S. 111 (1942):** The Supreme Court upheld federal regulation of wheat production for personal consumption under the Commerce Clause, establishing the constitutional basis for comprehensive federal agricultural regulation.

**Pigford v. Glickman (Civil Action No. 97-1978, D.D.C. 1999):** The landmark class action settlement addressed decades of racial discrimination by the USDA against Black farmers in farm loan and benefit programs. The settlement provided approximately $1 billion in relief. A subsequent settlement, Pigford II (2010), provided an additional $1.25 billion.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's land reform history is uniquely shaped by division and reunification, encompassing radical expropriation, collectivization, and complex post-unification restitution processes.

**Soviet Zone Land Reform (1945-1948):** Following World War II, Soviet Military Administration Order No. 209 (September 1945) mandated the expropriation without compensation of all agricultural estates exceeding 100 hectares, all land owned by Nazi war criminals and active Nazi Party members, and all land owned by the state. Approximately 3.3 million hectares were redistributed to roughly 560,000 "new farmers" (Neubauern), landless agricultural workers, refugees, and small farmers.

**GDR Collectivization (1952-1960):** The German Democratic Republic progressively collectivized agriculture through the establishment of Agricultural Production Cooperatives (Landwirtschaftliche Produktionsgenossenschaften, LPG). By 1960, approximately 85 percent of agricultural land had been incorporated into LPGs, often through coercive measures including imprisonment of resisters.

**Agriculture Adjustment Act (Landwirtschaftsanpassungsgesetz, LwAnpG, 1990):** Following reunification, the LwAnpG governed the transformation of LPGs into entities compatible with the market economy. LPG members could choose to continue as cooperatives, convert to corporations, or dissolve and redistribute assets.

**Grundstücksverkehrsgesetz (Land Transaction Act, 1961):** This federal statute requires government approval for the sale of agricultural and forestry land exceeding a certain threshold (varying by state, typically 0.25 to 2 hectares). Approval may be denied if the sale would result in an "unhealthy distribution of land" (ungesunde Verteilung von Grund und Boden), if the purchase price is grossly disproportionate to the land's value, or if the buyer does not intend to use the land for agriculture.

### Post-Reunification Restitution

The property restitution process in eastern Germany was governed by the principle of "restitution before compensation" (Rückgabe vor Entschädigung) established in the Vermögensgesetz (Property Act) of 1990. However, a critical exception applied to the 1945-1949 Soviet zone land reform: the Unification Treaty (Einigungsvertrag) of August 31, 1990, Article 41, explicitly excluded the Soviet-era expropriations from restitution claims, a provision upheld by the Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) in BVerfGE 84, 90 (1991) and confirmed by the European Court of Human Rights in Jahn and Others v. Germany (Application Nos. 46720/99, 72203/01, and 72552/01, Grand Chamber Judgment of June 30, 2005).

The Bodenverwertungs- und -verwaltungs GmbH (BVVG), established in 1992 as a subsidiary of the Treuhandanstalt's successor, manages the privatization of approximately 1.5 million hectares of formerly state-owned agricultural and forestry land in eastern Germany.

### Penalties

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| Land transaction without required approval | Transaction is void (Grundstücksverkehrsgesetz Section 9) |
| Unauthorized conversion of agricultural land | Restoration order; administrative fine (Baugesetzbuch) |
| Violation of tenant farmer protections | Civil liability under the Landpachtverkehrsgesetz |

## ECHR / EU

### ECHR -- Protocol No. 1, Article 1

The European Convention on Human Rights, through Article 1 of Protocol No. 1, establishes the right to peaceful enjoyment of possessions while permitting states to "enforce such laws as it deems necessary to control the use of property in accordance with the general interest." This provision has generated extensive jurisprudence on the permissible scope of land reform.

**Jahn and Others v. Germany (Application Nos. 46720/99, 72203/01, and 72552/01, Grand Chamber, June 30, 2005):** The applicants were heirs of persons who had received land under the 1945 Soviet zone land reform and subsequently lost it through the 1990 Modrow Law (Gesetz über die Rechte der Eigentümer von Grundstücken aus der Bodenreform), which required the return of Bodenreform land to the state if the original recipients or their heirs were no longer farming. The Grand Chamber found no violation of Article 1 of Protocol No. 1, holding by 11 votes to 6 that the deprivation served a legitimate aim in the general interest -- namely, rectifying the consequences of the GDR's approach to land distribution during reunification -- and was proportionate given the unique historical context.

**James and Others v. United Kingdom (Application No. 8793/79, Judgment of February 21, 1986):** The Court upheld the United Kingdom's Leasehold Reform Act 1967, which allowed long-term tenants to compulsorily acquire the freehold of their homes. The Court held that the taking of property "in the public interest" under Protocol No. 1 is not limited to takings for literal public use and that states enjoy a wide margin of appreciation in determining what constitutes "the general interest of the community."

**Vistiņš and Perepjolkins v. Latvia (Application No. 71243/01, Grand Chamber, October 25, 2012):** The Grand Chamber addressed compulsory acquisition of land for the Freeport of Riga, finding a violation of Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 due to inadequate compensation. The Court established that while expropriation for economic development purposes can serve the public interest, the compensation must bear a reasonable relationship to the market value of the property.

### EU Common Agricultural Policy

The EU's Common Agricultural Policy (CAP), established by the Treaty of Rome (1957) and reformed multiple times, indirectly shapes land tenure across all member states through its subsidy and regulatory mechanisms. The current CAP period (2023-2027) allocates approximately EUR 387 billion, with direct payments under Pillar I linked to the area of land farmed rather than production volumes, creating incentives that influence land values, tenure patterns, and consolidation.

**CAP and Land Concentration:** The European Parliament has recognized that CAP direct payments, by being area-based, tend to flow disproportionately to large landholdings, potentially exacerbating land concentration. The 2023-2027 CAP introduced complementary redistributive income support (CRISS) requiring member states to redistribute at least 10 percent of direct payment allocations from larger to smaller farms.

**EU Regulation on Free Movement of Capital:** Article 63 TFEU prohibits restrictions on the movement of capital between member states and between member states and third countries. This provision limits member states' ability to restrict foreign ownership of agricultural land, though the Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU) has recognized that restrictions may be justified on grounds of agricultural policy objectives, including preventing land speculation and maintaining viable farming communities (Case C-370/05, Festersen, 2007).

## Comparative Analysis

### Historical Models

The four jurisdictions illustrate fundamentally different approaches to land reform:

**Redistributive (South Korea):** South Korea's 1949 reform represents the classic "land to the tiller" model, directly transferring ownership from large landlords to tenant cultivators with partial compensation. The reform's success was facilitated by the Korean War's destruction of landlord political power and by U.S. occupation authorities' strategic interest in preventing communist appeal among rural populations.

**Distributive (United States):** The U.S. model distributed previously unclaimed or seized public land to private settlers rather than redistributing existing private holdings. This approach avoided direct confrontation with existing property rights but was premised on the dispossession of Indigenous peoples.

**Expropriative-Restitutive (Germany):** Germany experienced two cycles of radical reform -- Soviet-era expropriation followed by post-reunification privatization and partial restitution -- creating a uniquely complex legal landscape.

**Regulatory (EU/ECHR):** The European framework regulates land tenure through property rights protections, agricultural subsidies, and free movement of capital rules rather than mandating direct redistribution.

### Current Ownership Restrictions

South Korea maintains the strictest ownership restrictions, requiring actual cultivation as a precondition for farmland ownership. Germany requires governmental approval for agricultural land transactions but does not impose a cultivator requirement. The United States has no federal-level ownership restrictions, though several states restrict foreign and corporate ownership. The EU framework generally prohibits discriminatory restrictions on land ownership but permits justified agricultural policy measures.

### Foreign Ownership

All jurisdictions regulate foreign ownership of agricultural land to varying degrees. South Korea requires approval and limits foreign agricultural land acquisition. Germany, as an EU member state, must permit acquisition by EU nationals but may restrict non-EU foreign ownership. The United States requires disclosure under AFIDA but leaves restrictions to individual states. The EU permits member states to maintain transitional restrictions on agricultural land sales to nationals of newer member states, as applied by several eastern European states following accession.
