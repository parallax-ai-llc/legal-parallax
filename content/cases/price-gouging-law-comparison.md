## Summary

Price gouging -- the practice of charging excessively high prices for essential goods and services during emergencies, natural disasters, or supply disruptions -- occupies a contested space between free market principles and consumer protection imperatives, with legal approaches ranging from outright prohibition to tolerance of market-driven pricing. South Korea maintains a comprehensive price control framework through the Price Stabilization Act (물가안정에 관한 법률) and related statutes administered by the Ministry of Economy and Finance (기획재정부) and the Fair Trade Commission (공정거래위원회, KFTC), which authorize price controls on designated essential goods, monitoring and disclosure of pricing by major retailers, and penalties for speculative hoarding (매점매석); during the COVID-19 pandemic, the government imposed direct price controls and distribution limits on face masks through the Emergency Mask Supply and Demand Stabilization Measures (긴급 마스크 수급 안정화 조치) of March 2020, demonstrating the system's capacity for rapid intervention. The United States addresses price gouging primarily through state law, with approximately 37 states and the District of Columbia having enacted price gouging statutes that are triggered by gubernatorial or presidential emergency declarations, typically prohibiting price increases of more than 10-25 percent above pre-emergency levels for essential consumer goods; there is no federal price gouging statute, though the FTC Act's prohibition on unfair practices and proposals for federal legislation (such as the Price Gouging Prevention Act) have been debated repeatedly in Congress. Germany addresses excessive pricing through the Gesetz gegen Wettbewerbsbeschränkungen (GWB, Act Against Restraints of Competition) Section 19(2)(2), which prohibits dominant enterprises from demanding prices that differ from those obtainable in competitive markets, and through the Ordnungswidrigkeiten (administrative offenses) framework for wartime or crisis profiteering, though Germany generally relies more heavily on competition law than specific price gouging statutes. At the European level, the EU has no harmonized price gouging framework, but the COVID-19 pandemic prompted the European Commission to take unprecedented action through export controls on personal protective equipment (Implementing Regulation 2020/402), the Temporary Framework for State Aid, and coordination of national price control measures, while the European Court of Human Rights has recognized that price controls may be justified under Article 1 of Protocol 1 (protection of property) in the public interest.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's price control framework reflects a tradition of active government intervention in pricing that dates to the country's developmental state era under Park Chung-hee.

**Price Stabilization Act (물가안정에 관한 법률):** This Act authorizes the government to:
- Designate essential goods and services subject to price controls
- Set maximum prices for designated items
- Require prior notification of price increases by major producers and distributors
- Investigate and penalize speculative hoarding (매점매석)
- Impose corrective orders on businesses engaged in unfair pricing

**Act on Fair Transactions in Large Retail Business (대규모유통업에서의 거래 공정화에 관한 법률):** Regulates pricing practices by large retailers, preventing abusive price-setting that exploits market dominance.

**Monopoly Regulation and Fair Trade Act (독점규제 및 공정거래에 관한 법률):** The KFTC enforces prohibitions on abuse of market-dominant position, including charging unfairly high prices (부당한 가격 결정).

### COVID-19 Mask Crisis Response (2020)

The most significant recent application of Korea's price control framework occurred during the COVID-19 mask crisis of early 2020:

| Measure | Date | Effect |
|---------|------|--------|
| Mask export ban | February 2020 | Prohibited export of masks to ensure domestic supply |
| Price cap on masks | March 2020 | Government-set maximum price of 1,500 KRW per mask |
| 5-Day Rotation System (마스크 5부제) | March 9, 2020 | Citizens could purchase masks on designated days based on birth year |
| Distribution through pharmacies | March 2020 | Masks distributed through pharmacies with identification verification |
| Production requisition | March 2020 | Government requisitioned 80% of mask production for public distribution |

The mask supply management system was widely praised as effective public health policy, though it required significant government intervention in production, distribution, and pricing. The system was gradually relaxed as production capacity expanded, with the five-day rotation system ending in June 2020.

### Hoarding and Speculation (매점매석)

The Price Stabilization Act specifically targets hoarding behavior. Article 7 prohibits speculative hoarding of goods designated as essential, with penalties including imprisonment of up to 5 years and fines up to 50 million KRW. During the 2020 mask crisis, police investigated multiple cases of mask hoarding, including a businessman who stockpiled 4 million masks for resale at inflated prices.

### Agricultural Price Stabilization

South Korea maintains permanent price stabilization programs for agricultural products through the Agricultural Product Price Stabilization Act (농산물 유통 및 가격안정에 관한 법률), which authorizes the government to release reserve stocks to prevent price spikes and to support minimum prices for farmers.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States addresses price gouging primarily through state law, with no comprehensive federal price gouging statute.

### State Price Gouging Laws

Approximately 37 states and the District of Columbia have enacted price gouging statutes. Common elements include:

**Triggering Event:** Most statutes are activated only during a declared state of emergency (gubernatorial proclamation or presidential disaster declaration). Some states have permanent anti-price-gouging provisions.

**Price Threshold:** States vary in defining what constitutes "unconscionable" or "excessive" pricing:
- Percentage-based: 10% above pre-emergency price (California, New York, Texas, others)
- 15% above pre-emergency price (Florida)
- 25% above pre-emergency price (some states)
- "Unconscionable" or "grossly excessive" (subjective standard in some states)

**Covered Goods:** Typically essential consumer goods and services including food, water, fuel, shelter, medical supplies, building materials, and transportation.

**Notable State Statutes:**

| State | Statute | Threshold | Penalties |
|-------|---------|-----------|----------|
| California | Penal Code §396 | 10% above pre-emergency price | Misdemeanor: up to 1 year jail, $10,000 fine; civil: $2,500+ per violation |
| Florida | Stat. §501.160 | "Unconscionable" (15% presumption) | $1,000 per violation; $25,000 for multiple violations |
| New York | Gen. Bus. Law §396-r | "Unconscionably excessive" price | $25,000 per violation |
| Texas | Bus. & Com. Code §17.46(b)(27) | "Exorbitant or excessive" price | $10,000 per violation; $250,000 for elderly/disabled |

### Federal Proposals

No federal price gouging statute has been enacted, though several have been proposed:
- **Price Gouging Prevention Act (2022, 2023):** Would prohibit unconscionably excessive pricing during a presidentially declared emergency. Passed the House in 2022 but not the Senate.
- **FTC Authority:** The FTC does not have specific price gouging enforcement authority, though deceptive pricing practices may violate Section 5.
- **Defense Production Act:** The President may invoke the DPA to address supply shortages and coordinate production, as done during the COVID-19 pandemic for ventilators and vaccines, but the Act does not directly regulate prices.

### COVID-19 Enforcement

During the COVID-19 pandemic, state attorneys general aggressively enforced price gouging statutes. The National Association of Attorneys General reported that member offices received over 130,000 price gouging complaints between March 2020 and December 2021. Notable enforcement actions included multi-million-dollar settlements with online resellers of hand sanitizer, face masks, and disinfectants.

### Economic Debate

Price gouging laws are the subject of vigorous economic debate. Proponents argue that they prevent exploitation of vulnerable consumers during crises, maintain social solidarity, and prevent distributional injustice. Opponents, drawing on free market economics, argue that price controls create shortages by discouraging supply increases, that high prices signal demand and incentivize production, and that controls lead to hoarding by consumers. This debate remains unresolved both in the academy and in policy.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany does not have a dedicated price gouging statute comparable to American state laws. Instead, excessive pricing is addressed through competition law and general regulatory frameworks.

**GWB Section 19(2)(2) -- Abuse of Dominant Position:** Prohibits a dominant enterprise from "demanding payment or other business terms which differ from those which would very likely arise if effective competition existed." This provision allows the Bundeskartellamt to intervene against excessive pricing by dominant firms but requires proof of market dominance and a competitive benchmark, limiting its applicability to general price gouging by non-dominant sellers.

**Ordnungswidrigkeitengesetz (OWiG):** Administrative offense provisions may apply to crisis profiteering, though this framework is rarely used outside of wartime or extreme crisis situations.

### COVID-19 Response

Germany's response to pandemic-related price inflation relied primarily on:
- **Mask Price Caps:** The Federal Ministry of Health set maximum prices for FFP2 masks distributed through pharmacies (EUR 6 per mask under the Coronavirus-Schutzmasken-Verordnung).
- **Competition Law Enforcement:** The Bundeskartellamt monitored pricing practices for essential goods and warned retailers against exploitative pricing.
- **Consumer Complaints:** The Verbraucherzentralen (consumer centers) documented and publicized instances of extreme pricing for disinfectants, masks, and other essential goods.

### Historical Context: Preisbindung and Ordoliberalism

Germany's approach to price regulation reflects the Ordoliberal tradition, which favors maintaining competitive market structures over direct price intervention. The Ordoliberal school, associated with economists Walter Eucken and Franz Böhm, influenced the postwar German economic order (Soziale Marktwirtschaft) and the design of the GWB. Under this framework, excessive pricing is best addressed by ensuring competitive markets rather than imposing price controls, which are viewed as distortionary. Direct price controls are reserved for exceptional circumstances -- typically natural monopolies (energy, water) and regulated sectors.

### Energy Price Crisis (2022-2023)

The energy price crisis following Russia's invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 tested Germany's approach to price gouging. Natural gas and electricity prices spiked dramatically, leading to:
- **Energy Price Brake (Energiepreisbremse):** The government enacted the Strompreisbremse (electricity price cap) and Gaspreisbremse (gas price cap) in December 2022, subsidizing energy costs for households and businesses above a baseline price.
- **Windfall Profits Levy:** The EU-wide revenue cap for electricity generators (Council Regulation (EU) 2022/1854) and the German implementation through the Strompreisbremsengesetz captured excess revenues from low-cost generators.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Framework

The EU has no harmonized price gouging framework, as pricing regulation is primarily a member state competence. However, several EU-level instruments and actions are relevant:

**Article 102 TFEU (Abuse of Dominant Position):** Analogous to GWB §19, Article 102 prohibits dominant enterprises from "directly or indirectly imposing unfair purchase or selling prices or other unfair trading conditions." The European Commission and national competition authorities can act against exploitative pricing by dominant firms, but the application to general consumer price gouging is limited by the dominance requirement.

**COVID-19 Emergency Measures:**
| Measure | Date | Effect |
|---------|------|--------|
| Implementing Regulation (EU) 2020/402 | March 2020 | Export authorization requirement for PPE |
| EU Joint Procurement Agreement | March 2020 | Coordinated procurement of medical equipment |
| Temporary Framework for State Aid | March 2020 | Permitted member states to provide emergency support |
| Commission Communication on prices and supply | April 2020 | Guidance on price monitoring and enforcement |

**Council Regulation (EU) 2022/1854 -- Energy Emergency Measures:** Adopted in response to the energy crisis, this regulation imposed a revenue cap on electricity generators and a solidarity contribution from fossil fuel companies, effectively addressing windfall profits without traditional price controls.

### ECHR -- Article 1, Protocol 1 (Property)

The ECHR has recognized that price controls interfere with property rights but may be justified in the public interest:

**Stec and Others v. United Kingdom (Grand Chamber, 2006):** While not directly about price gouging, the Court established that the state has a wide margin of appreciation in enacting economic and social legislation, including measures affecting prices and market conditions.

**James and Others v. United Kingdom (1986):** The Court held that the state's margin of appreciation in property regulation is broad, particularly where the legislation pursues social justice objectives.

### European Competition Network (ECN)

During crises, the ECN -- comprising the European Commission and national competition authorities of all EU member states -- coordinates enforcement against exploitative pricing practices. During the COVID-19 pandemic, the ECN issued a joint statement in March 2020 warning that competition authorities would not hesitate to act against firms exploiting the crisis through excessive pricing.

## Comparative Analysis

### Government Intervention Philosophy

The jurisdictions reflect fundamentally different philosophies about government intervention in pricing. South Korea maintains the most interventionist approach, with permanent price stabilization mechanisms and the capacity for rapid, comprehensive price controls during emergencies. The United States takes a targeted approach, restricting price control authority to emergency periods and essential goods. Germany favors structural competition law over direct price intervention, reflecting the Ordoliberal tradition. The EU coordinates but generally defers to member state approaches.

### Emergency vs. Permanent Mechanisms

South Korea maintains permanent price monitoring and stabilization infrastructure that can be rapidly intensified during emergencies. American price gouging statutes are almost entirely emergency-triggered, requiring formal declarations to activate. Germany's GWB applies continuously but requires market dominance, while crisis-specific measures (energy price brakes) are enacted ad hoc. This structural difference affects response time and comprehensiveness.

### Enforcement Effectiveness

South Korea's centralized enforcement through the KFTC and Ministry of Economy and Finance enables rapid, coordinated action across the economy. American enforcement is decentralized across 50+ state attorneys general, creating variation in enforcement intensity and outcomes. Germany's competition authority model is effective for dominant firm behavior but less suited to addressing widespread small-scale price gouging. The EU's coordination role adds value for cross-border issues but cannot substitute for national enforcement.

### The Economic Efficiency Debate

The tension between consumer protection and economic efficiency is most pronounced in the United States, where the price gouging debate reflects deep divisions in economic philosophy. The South Korean and European approaches generally accept price controls as a legitimate policy tool during emergencies, while the American debate continues to contest the fundamental legitimacy of price intervention.
