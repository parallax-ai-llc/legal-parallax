## Summary

Public procurement law governs the procedures by which governments and public entities purchase goods, services, and works from private sector suppliers, establishing rules designed to ensure transparency, fair competition, value for money, and the prevention of corruption in the expenditure of public funds. Government procurement accounts for a significant share of GDP in all major economies -- typically 10-15% -- making procurement regulation one of the most economically significant areas of administrative law. South Korea's public procurement system is governed by the Act on Contracts to Which the State is a Party (국가를 당사자로 하는 계약에 관한 법률, State Contract Act) and the Act on Contracts to Which a Local Government is a Party (지방자치단체를 당사자로 하는 계약에 관한 법률), administered by the Public Procurement Service (조달청, PPS), which operates the Korea ON-line E-Procurement System (국가종합전자조달, KONEPS); South Korea is a party to the WTO Government Procurement Agreement (GPA) and has implemented electronic procurement as a global model of e-government efficiency. The United States regulates federal procurement through the Federal Acquisition Regulation (FAR, 48 C.F.R.), administered by executive agencies under the oversight of the Office of Federal Procurement Policy (OFPP) within the Office of Management and Budget, the Government Accountability Office (GAO) bid protest system, and the Court of Federal Claims (28 U.S.C. Section 1491(b)); the Competition in Contracting Act of 1984 (CICA, 41 U.S.C. Sections 3301-3308) mandates full and open competition as the default procurement method, and the Buy American Act (41 U.S.C. Sections 8301-8305) imposes domestic preference requirements. Germany, as an EU member state, implements the EU procurement directives through the Gesetz gegen Wettbewerbsbeschränkungen (GWB, Act Against Restraints of Competition, Part 4, Sections 97-184) and the Vergabeverordnung (VgV, Public Procurement Regulation), with the Vergabekammern (procurement chambers) at the Bundeskartellamt providing the primary review mechanism; the system is fully harmonized with EU Directive 2014/24/EU (Public Sector Directive) and Directive 2014/25/EU (Utilities Directive). The ECHR/EU framework establishes the most comprehensive supranational procurement regime through the 2014 directives, the Remedies Directives (89/665/EEC and 92/13/EEC as amended), and the principle of transparency derived from the TFEU, with the CJEU playing a central role in developing procurement principles applicable even below the directive thresholds.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea has developed one of the world's most advanced public procurement systems, combining strict legal requirements with pioneering electronic procurement technology.

**State Contract Act (국가를 당사자로 하는 계약에 관한 법률):** This Act, along with its Enforcement Decree and Enforcement Rules, governs contracts entered into by central government agencies. The Act establishes the fundamental principles of competition, transparency, economy, and fairness in public contracting. Competitive bidding is the default procurement method, with limited tendering (수의계약) permitted only in specified circumstances, including emergencies, sole-source situations, contracts below prescribed thresholds, and specialized technical requirements.

**Local Government Contract Act (지방자치단체를 당사자로 하는 계약에 관한 법률):** This parallel Act governs procurement by local governments, applying substantially similar principles and procedures adapted to the local government context.

### Public Procurement Service (조달청, PPS)

The PPS, established in 1949, serves as the centralized procurement agency for the Korean government. It functions as both a purchasing agency (conducting procurement on behalf of other government agencies) and a regulatory body (issuing procurement guidelines and standards). The PPS manages approximately 30% of total government procurement expenditure, with the remainder conducted directly by individual agencies under PPS guidelines.

### KONEPS (Korea ON-line E-Procurement System)

KONEPS, launched in 2002, is widely recognized as one of the world's most successful e-procurement systems. The system integrates the entire procurement cycle electronically:

**Functionality:** Publication of tender notices, submission and receipt of bids, electronic bid evaluation, contract award notification, contract execution, invoice processing, and payment -- all conducted through a single integrated platform.

**Mandatory Use:** Use of KONEPS is mandatory for all central government agencies and encouraged for local governments and public institutions.

**Integration:** KONEPS is integrated with over 160 external systems, including the Business Registration Information System, the National Tax Service, the Social Insurance Information System, and financial institution payment systems, enabling automated eligibility verification and payment processing.

**International Recognition:** KONEPS has been recognized by the UN, the World Bank, and the OECD as a best-practice model for e-procurement, and South Korea has provided technical assistance to over 30 countries in developing their e-procurement systems.

### WTO GPA and International Commitments

South Korea has been a party to the WTO Government Procurement Agreement since 1997 and revised its commitments under the GPA 2012 revision. Korea's GPA coverage includes central government agencies, sub-central government entities, and major public enterprises (including Korea Electric Power Corporation (KEPCO), Korea Gas Corporation (KOGAS), and Korea Railroad Corporation (KORAIL)). The threshold for GPA-covered procurement is approximately SDR 130,000 for goods and services (approximately KRW 210 million) and SDR 5,000,000 for construction works.

### Procurement Integrity and Anti-Corruption

South Korea has implemented robust anti-corruption measures in procurement:

**Integrity Pact (청렴계약제):** Public procurement contracts above prescribed thresholds require bidders to sign an integrity pact committing to refrain from bribery and other corrupt practices, with contractual penalties for violations.

**Bid Rigging Prevention:** The Fair Trade Commission (공정거래위원회, KFTC) actively investigates and sanctions bid rigging in public procurement, imposing substantial fines under the Monopoly Regulation and Fair Trade Act. In KFTC Decision 2019-001, the Commission imposed fines totaling KRW 250 billion on construction companies for bid rigging in public works contracts.

**Electronic Bid Security:** KONEPS employs advanced encryption and digital signature technology to prevent bid manipulation and ensure the integrity of the electronic bidding process.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States operates one of the world's most complex and heavily regulated public procurement systems, with a vast regulatory framework developed over decades of legislative and regulatory evolution.

**Federal Acquisition Regulation (FAR, 48 C.F.R.):** The FAR is the primary regulation governing federal executive agency procurement of supplies and services with appropriated funds. Spanning over 50 parts and thousands of pages, the FAR establishes uniform policies and procedures for all federal acquisitions, supplemented by agency-specific FAR supplements (e.g., DFARS for the Department of Defense, GSAR for the General Services Administration).

**Competition in Contracting Act of 1984 (CICA, 41 U.S.C. Sections 3301-3308):** CICA mandates "full and open competition" as the default method for federal procurement, requiring agencies to obtain competition to the maximum extent practicable. Exceptions to full and open competition (sole-source, limited competition) require written justification and approval by designated agency officials. CICA also established the GAO bid protest system, providing disappointed bidders with an independent review mechanism.

### Procurement Methods

| Method | Applicability | Key Requirements |
|--------|--------------|-----------------|
| Sealed bidding (FAR Part 14) | When price is the determinative factor and specifications are clear | Public opening; award to lowest responsive, responsible bidder |
| Competitive negotiation (FAR Part 15) | When best value determination requires evaluation of technical and price factors | Technical and price evaluation; discussions permitted; best value award |
| Simplified acquisition (FAR Part 13) | Purchases at or below $250,000 | Streamlined procedures; reduced documentation |
| Commercial item acquisition (FAR Part 12) | Commercial products and services | Simplified terms; commercial practices permitted |
| Task/delivery order contracting (FAR Part 16) | Indefinite-quantity contracts | Competition among multiple awardees for individual orders |

### Buy American Act and Domestic Preferences

**Buy American Act (41 U.S.C. Sections 8301-8305):** Requires federal agencies to purchase domestic end products and construction materials unless an exception applies (non-availability, unreasonable cost, public interest). The price preference for domestic products ranges from 20% to 30% depending on the supplier size (small business vs. large business).

**Build America, Buy America Act (BABA, 2021):** Part of the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act, BABA significantly expanded domestic content requirements for federally funded infrastructure projects, requiring that all iron, steel, manufactured products, and construction materials used in covered projects be produced in the United States.

**Trade Agreements Act (19 U.S.C. Sections 2501-2582):** Waives Buy American Act restrictions for products from countries that are parties to trade agreements with the United States, including the WTO GPA, bilateral FTAs, and Caribbean Basin Economic Recovery Act. The interaction between domestic preference and trade agreement obligations creates a complex matrix of applicable rules.

### Bid Protest System

The U.S. bid protest system provides multiple avenues for challenging procurement decisions:

**GAO Bid Protests (31 U.S.C. Section 3554):** The Government Accountability Office provides the most frequently used bid protest forum. GAO must issue a decision within 100 days of filing. Filing a protest triggers an automatic stay of contract performance (CICA stay), preventing the agency from proceeding with the award while the protest is pending (subject to override). In fiscal year 2024, GAO received approximately 2,300 bid protests, sustaining approximately 13% of decided cases.

**Court of Federal Claims (28 U.S.C. Section 1491(b)):** The Court of Federal Claims provides judicial review of procurement decisions, with the authority to issue injunctive relief and declaratory judgment. Appeals proceed to the Federal Circuit.

**Agency-Level Protests:** Agencies may also establish internal protest procedures, providing a less formal first line of review.

### Small Business Set-Asides

The Small Business Act (15 U.S.C. Section 631 et seq.) and FAR Part 19 establish a comprehensive system of small business preferences and set-asides, including: general small business set-asides (contracts reserved for small businesses); 8(a) program set-asides (for socially and economically disadvantaged small businesses); HUBZone set-asides (for businesses in historically underutilized business zones); service-disabled veteran-owned small business (SDVOSB) set-asides; and women-owned small business (WOSB) set-asides. The government-wide goal is for 23% of federal contract dollars to go to small businesses.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's public procurement system is fully integrated with the EU procurement framework, implementing the 2014 directives through a comprehensive national regulatory structure.

**GWB Part 4 (Sections 97-184):** The Act Against Restraints of Competition Part 4 provides the overarching legal framework for public procurement above the EU thresholds. Section 97 establishes the fundamental principles: competition (Wettbewerbsgrundsatz), transparency (Transparenzgebot), equal treatment (Gleichbehandlungsgrundsatz), and proportionality (Verhältnismäßigkeitsgrundsatz). Section 97(4) mandates that procurement procedures be conducted in accordance with the applicable EU directives.

**Vergabeverordnung (VgV, Public Procurement Regulation):** The VgV, enacted in 2016 to transpose Directive 2014/24/EU, provides detailed procedural rules for procurement above the EU thresholds, including rules on procurement methods, qualification criteria, award criteria, and procedural requirements.

**Unterschwellenvergabeordnung (UVgO):** For procurement below the EU thresholds, the UVgO (issued by the federal government in 2017) provides voluntary procedural guidelines. While not directly mandated by EU law, the UVgO reflects the CJEU's jurisprudence requiring transparency and non-discrimination even for below-threshold procurement with cross-border interest.

### Procurement Methods

The German system recognizes the procurement methods established by the EU directives:

**Offenes Verfahren (Open Procedure, Section 15 VgV):** The primary procedure, open to all interested economic operators. Tender notices are published in the Official Journal of the EU (via TED) and in national publication outlets.

**Nicht offenes Verfahren (Restricted Procedure, Section 16 VgV):** A two-stage procedure with pre-qualification, in which only pre-qualified candidates are invited to submit tenders.

**Verhandlungsverfahren (Negotiated Procedure, Section 17 VgV):** Permitted in specified circumstances (complex contracts, innovation procurement), allowing the contracting authority to negotiate with tenderers.

**Wettbewerblicher Dialog (Competitive Dialogue, Section 18 VgV):** For particularly complex contracts where the contracting authority cannot define the technical specifications or legal or financial arrangements with sufficient precision.

**Innovationspartnerschaft (Innovation Partnership, Section 19 VgV):** A new procedure introduced by Directive 2014/24/EU for the development and subsequent purchase of innovative products, services, or works.

### Review Mechanism (Vergabekammern)

Germany's procurement review system is a key feature of its procurement regime:

**Vergabekammern (Procurement Chambers):** Above-threshold procurement decisions are reviewed by the Vergabekammern, located at the Bundeskartellamt (for federal procurement) and at the state level (for Länder procurement). The Vergabekammer procedure is quasi-judicial, with oral hearings, access to procurement files, and binding decisions. The Vergabekammer must issue a decision within five weeks of receipt of the application (Section 167 GWB), providing rapid resolution.

**Oberlandesgericht (Higher Regional Court):** Decisions of the Vergabekammern may be appealed to the competent Oberlandesgericht (Section 171 GWB). The appeal court reviews questions of law and, to a limited extent, questions of fact. The appeal court's decision is final; there is no further appeal to the Bundesgerichtshof in procurement matters.

**Automatic Suspensive Effect:** The filing of a review application before the Vergabekammer triggers an automatic suspension of the contract award (Section 169 GWB), preventing the contracting authority from concluding the contract until the review is decided. This suspensive effect is a critical safeguard, as it prevents the creation of fait accompli situations that would render review ineffective.

### Sustainability and Strategic Procurement

The 2021 amendment to GWB Section 97(3) explicitly recognized the permissibility of strategic procurement objectives, including environmental sustainability, social criteria, and innovation promotion, in procurement decisions, implementing the "most economically advantageous tender" concept from Directive 2014/24/EU Article 67. Contracting authorities may include sustainability criteria in technical specifications, selection criteria, and award criteria, provided they are linked to the subject matter of the contract and comply with the proportionality principle.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Procurement Directives (2014 Reform Package)

The 2014 reform package represents the most significant overhaul of EU procurement law since the original 1970s directives:

**Directive 2014/24/EU (Public Sector Directive):** Governs procurement by central and sub-central government authorities and bodies governed by public law. Key innovations include: the innovation partnership procedure; enhanced provisions for strategic procurement (environmental, social, and innovation criteria); simplified procedures for social and other specific services; electronic procurement requirements; and provisions on subcontracting transparency.

**Directive 2014/25/EU (Utilities Directive):** Governs procurement by entities operating in the water, energy, transport, and postal services sectors, applying more flexible procedures reflecting the commercial nature of utility operations.

**Directive 2014/23/EU (Concessions Directive):** Establishes for the first time a comprehensive EU-wide framework for the award of concession contracts for works and services, replacing the previously fragmented approach.

### Thresholds and Publication

EU procurement directives apply only above specified financial thresholds, which are revised every two years:

| Category | 2024-2025 Threshold |
|----------|-------------------|
| Central government supplies and services | EUR 143,000 |
| Sub-central government supplies and services | EUR 221,000 |
| Works contracts | EUR 5,538,000 |
| Utilities (all categories) | EUR 443,000 |
| Concessions | EUR 5,538,000 |

Above-threshold procurement must be published in the Official Journal of the EU through Tenders Electronic Daily (TED), using the standardized eForms format introduced in 2023. Below-threshold procurement is governed by national rules, but the CJEU has held that procurement with cross-border interest must comply with the fundamental principles of the TFEU (transparency, non-discrimination, equal treatment), even below the directive thresholds (Case C-324/98, Telaustria, 2000).

### Remedies Directives

**Directive 89/665/EEC (Public Sector Remedies)** and **Directive 92/13/EEC (Utilities Remedies)**, as amended by Directive 2007/66/EC, establish minimum standards for national review procedures:

**Standstill Period:** A mandatory standstill period of at least 10 days (15 days if notice is sent by non-electronic means) between the contract award decision and the conclusion of the contract, during which disappointed tenderers may seek review.

**Automatic Suspension:** The filing of a review application triggers automatic suspension of the contract award decision, preventing the conclusion of the contract until the review body has decided on the application for interim measures or on the merits.

**Ineffectiveness:** Contracts concluded in violation of the standstill period, in violation of the automatic suspension, or after an illegal direct award (without publication in the OJ) may be declared ineffective by the review body, requiring the contract to be unwound.

### CJEU Procurement Jurisprudence

**Telaustria (Case C-324/98, 2000):** The Court established that even below the directive thresholds, contracting authorities must comply with fundamental rules of the Treaty, including the principle of non-discrimination on grounds of nationality and the obligation of transparency.

**Pressetext Nachrichtenagentur (Case C-454/06, 2008):** The Court defined when contract modifications require a new procurement procedure, establishing criteria later codified in Directive 2014/24/EU Article 72.

**Fastweb (Case C-100/12, 2013):** The Court held that a tenderer whose offer should have been excluded cannot be denied standing to challenge the award to another tenderer whose offer should also have been excluded, reinforcing the effectiveness of procurement review.

**Klaipedos (Case C-927/19, 2022):** The Court addressed the lawfulness of abnormally low tenders and the obligations of contracting authorities to investigate and potentially reject such tenders.

### WTO Government Procurement Agreement (GPA)

The EU is a party to the WTO GPA, with all member states bound by the EU's commitments. The GPA establishes principles of non-discrimination (national treatment and most-favored-nation treatment), transparency (publication of procurement opportunities), and procedural fairness (time limits, qualification criteria, evaluation) for covered procurement above specified thresholds. The GPA 2012 revision expanded coverage and improved market access, with the EU's annexes covering central and sub-central government entities and specified utilities.

## Comparative Analysis

### Centralization versus Decentralization

The degree of centralization in procurement systems varies significantly. South Korea's PPS model, with centralized purchasing through KONEPS, provides efficiency, standardization, and transparency benefits. The United States operates a decentralized system with each agency conducting its own procurement under the FAR framework, supplemented by centralized purchasing through the General Services Administration (GSA) schedules and government-wide acquisition contracts (GWACs). Germany and the EU operate a mixed model, with EU-level harmonization of procedures but national and sub-national contracting authorities conducting individual procurements.

### Electronic Procurement

South Korea leads globally in e-procurement implementation through KONEPS, which has been operational since 2002 and processes the vast majority of government procurement electronically. The EU mandated electronic submission of tenders for above-threshold procurement from October 2018 (Directive 2014/24/EU Article 22), with the eForms standard introduced in 2023. The United States has been slower to implement comprehensive e-procurement, relying on multiple systems (SAM.gov, FPDS-NG, eBuy) that are less integrated than KONEPS.

### Domestic Preference versus Open Competition

The treatment of domestic preference reveals a significant policy divergence. The United States maintains the most extensive domestic preference regime through the Buy American Act, BABA, and the Berry Amendment (10 U.S.C. Section 4862, requiring domestic sourcing for defense textiles and food), with trade agreement waivers creating a complex matrix of applicable rules. The EU prohibits national preference within the single market and provides access to GPA parties. South Korea complies with its GPA obligations for above-threshold procurement but maintains flexibility for below-threshold procurement. The tension between domestic preference and open competition reflects broader debates about industrial policy, supply chain security, and trade liberalization.

### Remedies and Enforcement

The effectiveness of procurement review mechanisms varies. The EU's Remedies Directives, with mandatory standstill periods, automatic suspension, and the possibility of contract ineffectiveness, provide the strongest procedural protections. The United States' GAO bid protest system, with its CICA automatic stay and 100-day decision timeline, provides effective relief but has been criticized for increasing procurement costs and delays. South Korea's review mechanisms, while available, are less frequently used, reflecting a cultural preference for administrative resolution. Germany's Vergabekammer system, with its five-week decision deadline and automatic suspensive effect, provides perhaps the most efficient judicial review mechanism.
