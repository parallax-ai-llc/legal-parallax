## Summary

Loan sharking -- the practice of lending money at usuriously high interest rates, often accompanied by threats of violence, extortion, or other coercive collection methods -- represents a persistent criminal phenomenon at the intersection of financial regulation, consumer protection, and organized crime enforcement. South Korea addresses loan sharking through the Act on Registration of Credit Business and Protection of Finance Users (대부업 등의 등록 및 금융이용자 보호에 관한 법률), which caps maximum interest rates at 20 percent per annum (reduced from 27.9 percent in 2018) and imposes criminal penalties of up to three years' imprisonment for exceeding the cap, supplemented by Criminal Act provisions on extortion (공갈, Article 350) and intimidation for collection-related violence; the Korean legal framework was significantly strengthened following widespread public outcry over predatory lending practices targeting vulnerable populations including the elderly, students, and low-income workers. The United States addresses loan sharking through a combination of federal racketeering statutes -- principally 18 U.S.C. Section 1962 (RICO) and 18 U.S.C. Section 891-896 (Extortionate Credit Transactions) -- and state usury laws that vary dramatically, with some states imposing strict interest rate caps while others have effectively deregulated consumer lending; the federal Consumer Credit Protection Act, Title II (Extortionate Extensions of Credit), specifically targets organized crime-connected loan sharking. Germany regulates usurious lending through Section 138 of the Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB, Civil Code), which renders usurious contracts void, and Section 291 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB, Wucher), which criminalizes the exploitation of another's distressed situation, inexperience, or weakness of will to extract grossly disproportionate economic benefits, with penalties of up to three years' imprisonment and up to ten years for commercial or gang-based usury. At the European level, the Consumer Credit Directive (2008/48/EC, revised by Directive 2023/2225) establishes disclosure requirements and consumer protections, while the ECHR has addressed usury through Article 4 (prohibition of servitude) in cases where debt bondage results from exploitative lending practices.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's approach to loan sharking combines financial regulation with criminal enforcement, reflecting the country's experience with widespread predatory lending.

### Interest Rate Caps

| Period | Maximum Rate | Statute |
|--------|-------------|---------|
| Pre-2007 | 66% per annum | Various |
| 2007-2010 | 49% | Credit Business Act |
| 2010-2014 | 44% → 39% | Progressive reductions |
| 2014-2016 | 34.9% | Credit Business Act amendment |
| 2016-2018 | 27.9% | Credit Business Act amendment |
| 2018-2021 | 24% | Credit Business Act amendment |
| 2021-present | 20% | Credit Business Act amendment |

### Criminal Provisions

| Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|
| Exceeding maximum interest rate | Up to 3 years or fine up to 30 million KRW |
| Operating unregistered lending business | Up to 5 years or fine up to 50 million KRW |
| Violent or threatening collection practices | Criminal Act Art. 350 (extortion): up to 10 years |
| Harassing collection calls/visits | Act on Fair Debt Collection: up to 5 years |
| Fraudulent lending practices | Up to 5 years |

### Enforcement Framework

The Financial Supervisory Service (금융감독원) oversees registered credit businesses, while the National Police Agency targets unregistered loan sharks. The government has established a joint task force combining financial regulators and law enforcement to combat illegal lending operations. In 2023, the task force dismantled over 300 illegal lending operations, resulting in more than 1,200 arrests.

### Vulnerable Population Protections

South Korean law provides enhanced protections for particularly vulnerable borrowers. The Act on Special Cases Concerning the Repayment of Personal Debts (개인채무자 회생 및 파산에 관한 법률) provides debt restructuring mechanisms for individuals trapped in usurious debt. The government operates the Comprehensive Credit Recovery Committee (신용회복위원회) and the Microfinance Foundation (미소금융재단) providing legitimate lending alternatives for low-income borrowers.

### Digital Loan Sharking

The proliferation of smartphone lending applications has created new forms of loan sharking in South Korea. Operators of unregistered lending apps -- often targeting young adults and students -- charge effective annual rates exceeding 1,000 percent through short-term "paycheck advance" structures. Law enforcement has prioritized these operations, with the Financial Services Commission issuing regulations requiring app store operators to verify lending app registrations.

## United States

### Legal Framework

**Federal Extortionate Credit Transactions (18 U.S.C. §§ 891-896):**

| Section | Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|---------|
| § 892 | Making extortionate extensions of credit | Up to 20 years |
| § 893 | Financing extortionate extensions of credit | Up to 20 years |
| § 894 | Collection of extensions of credit by extortionate means | Up to 20 years |

The statutes define "extortionate" as involving the use or threat of force, violence, or other criminal means to cause harm to the person, reputation, or property of any person. Importantly, an interest rate exceeding 45 percent per annum creates a rebuttable presumption of extortionate intent.

### RICO and Loan Sharking

The Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act (18 U.S.C. § 1962) has been extensively used against organized loan sharking operations. RICO requires a pattern of racketeering activity, and extortionate credit transactions are included in the definition of racketeering activity. RICO convictions carry penalties of up to 20 years per count, plus mandatory forfeiture of proceeds.

### State Usury Laws

State usury laws vary enormously:

| State | General Usury Cap | Exceptions |
|-------|------------------|------------|
| New York | 16% (civil); 25% (criminal) | Licensed lenders exempt from civil cap |
| California | 10% (personal/non-exempt) | No cap for licensed lenders |
| Texas | 10% (default rate) | Extensive exemptions for commercial lenders |
| South Dakota | No cap | National bank preemption base |
| Colorado | 12% (consumer) | Supervised lenders: 21% |

The Supreme Court's decision in Marquette National Bank v. First of Omaha Service Corp., 439 U.S. 299 (1978), established that nationally chartered banks may export the interest rate of their home state to borrowers nationwide, effectively allowing banks headquartered in states with no usury caps (such as South Dakota and Delaware) to charge high rates nationally.

### Payday Lending Controversy

The payday lending industry, which charges effective annual rates often exceeding 300 percent, occupies a contested legal space. The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) has attempted to regulate payday lending through the Payday Rule (2017), which was significantly weakened in 2020. Several states have enacted rate caps specifically targeting payday lenders, while others have permitted the industry to operate with minimal regulation.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

**Section 291 StGB (Wucher -- Usury):**

**(1)** Whoever exploits another person's distressed situation, inexperience, lack of judgment, or significant weakness of will to secure for themselves or a third party, in exchange for the grant or promise of a benefit, pecuniary advantages which are conspicuously disproportionate (auffällig missverhältnis) to the benefit granted, shall be punished with imprisonment for not more than three years or a fine.

**(2)** In particularly serious cases, the penalty is imprisonment from six months to ten years. A particularly serious case typically exists where the offender acts commercially (gewerbsmäßig) or as a member of a gang.

### Definition of "Conspicuously Disproportionate"

German courts have developed a two-pronged test for determining whether lending terms are usurious:

**Objective Element:** The economic performance (interest rate) must be conspicuously disproportionate to the market rate. The Bundesgerichtshof has established that a rate exceeding approximately double the market rate for comparable legitimate credit constitutes a prima facie showing of disproportion (BGH XI ZR 156/03).

**Subjective Element:** The lender must have exploited the borrower's distressed situation (Zwangslage), inexperience (Unerfahrenheit), lack of judgment (Mangel an Urteilsvermögen), or significant weakness of will (erhebliche Willensschwäche).

### Civil Consequences (Section 138 BGB)

Section 138(2) BGB renders usurious contracts void (nichtig) from the outset. The borrower is obligated to repay only the principal amount without interest. Courts may additionally order the lender to return any usurious interest already paid under unjust enrichment principles (Section 812 BGB).

### Penalty Structure

| Offense | Provision | Penalty |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Basic usury | Section 291(1) | Up to 3 years or fine |
| Commercial usury (gewerbsmäßig) | Section 291(2) | 6 months to 10 years |
| Gang-based usury | Section 291(2) | 6 months to 10 years |
| Usury causing existential ruin | Enhanced within (2) | Higher end of range |

### BaFin Enforcement

The Bundesanstalt für Finanzdienstleistungsaufsicht (BaFin) supervises credit institutions and may revoke banking licenses for usurious practices. Additionally, the Gewerberecht (trade law) requires registration of credit intermediaries, and unauthorized lending operations face both administrative and criminal sanctions.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Consumer Credit Framework

**Consumer Credit Directive 2008/48/EC (revised by Directive 2023/2225):** Establishes EU-wide requirements for consumer credit including mandatory disclosure of the annual percentage rate of charge (APR), right of withdrawal, and responsible lending obligations. The revised 2023 Directive strengthened affordability assessment requirements and introduced measures addressing digital lending.

**Mortgage Credit Directive 2014/17/EU:** Requires responsible lending practices in mortgage markets and establishes conduct-of-business obligations for creditors.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Siliadin v. France (Application No. 73316/01, 2005):** While primarily a forced labor case, the Court's analysis of debt bondage -- where victims are trapped in exploitative arrangements through accumulated debt -- established principles applicable to extreme loan sharking. The Court found positive obligations under Article 4 to protect against servitude resulting from exploitative economic relationships.

**Chowdury and Others v. Greece (Application No. 21884/15, 2017):** The Court addressed forced labor and trafficking, finding that economic exploitation involving debt bondage violates Article 4. While not a traditional loan sharking case, the decision reinforced that states must combat economic exploitation that reduces individuals to conditions of servitude.

### EU Anti-Money Laundering Framework

Loan sharking operations frequently involve money laundering, bringing them within the scope of the EU's AML directives. The Anti-Money Laundering Authority (AMLA), established by Regulation (EU) 2024/1620, will oversee compliance by financial institutions, including detection of loan sharking proceeds in the financial system.

## Comparative Analysis

### Interest Rate Regulation

The approaches to interest rate regulation diverge significantly. South Korea imposes a clear numerical cap (20 percent). Germany uses a relative standard (double market rate). The United States has no uniform federal cap, with wide state-by-state variation and significant federal preemption for national banks.

### Criminal vs. Civil Enforcement

All jurisdictions combine criminal and civil approaches, but the emphasis differs. The United States focuses criminal enforcement on organized crime-connected loan sharking through RICO and extortionate credit statutes. Germany primarily addresses usury through civil remedies (void contracts) with criminal prosecution reserved for exploitative cases. South Korea combines regulatory enforcement against unlicensed lenders with criminal penalties for rate violations.

### Digital Lending Challenges

All jurisdictions face new challenges from digital lending platforms, including smartphone apps offering high-interest short-term loans. South Korea has been most aggressive in regulating lending apps. The United States debates CFPB authority over fintech lenders. Germany and the EU address digital lending through the revised Consumer Credit Directive.
