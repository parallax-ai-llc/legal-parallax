## Summary

Social welfare systems -- the comprehensive frameworks through which governments provide income support, social insurance, and basic services to protect individuals from poverty, unemployment, disability, and other life risks -- represent one of the most significant areas of divergence in comparative law, reflecting fundamentally different conceptions of the state's role in ensuring economic security. South Korea has rapidly expanded its social welfare framework since democratization in 1987, with the current system anchored by the National Basic Living Security Act (국민기초생활 보장법, Act No. 5999, 1999, comprehensively amended 2014), which guarantees a minimum standard of living to all citizens meeting means-tested criteria, the National Pension Act (국민연금법), the Employment Insurance Act (고용보험법), the National Health Insurance Act (국민건강보험법), and the Industrial Accident Compensation Insurance Act (산업재해보상보험법), collectively comprising the "Four Social Insurance" (4대 사회보험) system, with the Constitutional Court (헌법재판소) interpreting the Constitution's social state principle (사회국가원리) under Article 34 to create a constitutional obligation for the state to guarantee a minimum standard of human dignity. The United States operates a fragmented welfare system combining federal programs -- Social Security (42 U.S.C. Section 401 et seq.), Medicare, Medicaid (Title XIX of the Social Security Act), the Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP), Temporary Assistance for Needy Families (TANF), and the Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC) -- with the Supreme Court having rejected a constitutional right to welfare in Dandridge v. Williams (1970) while recognizing procedural due process protections for welfare recipients in Goldberg v. Kelly (1970). Germany, the birthplace of the modern welfare state under Otto von Bismarck's social insurance legislation of the 1880s, operates one of the world's most comprehensive systems under the Social Code (Sozialgesetzbuch, SGB), comprising twelve books covering health insurance, long-term care insurance, pension insurance, unemployment insurance, social assistance, and other social benefits, with the Federal Constitutional Court's landmark Hartz IV decision (BVerfGE 125, 175 (2010)) establishing that the right to a subsistence minimum consistent with human dignity is a directly enforceable constitutional right derived from Article 1(1) (human dignity) read with Article 20(1) (social state principle) of the Basic Law. The European framework, anchored in the European Social Charter (1961, revised 1996), the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights (Articles 34-36), and the European Pillar of Social Rights (2017), establishes minimum standards for social protection across EU member states, with the ECHR's evolving jurisprudence under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 protecting welfare benefits as possessions once established.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's social welfare system has undergone extraordinary expansion, growing from minimal social protection during the development era to a comprehensive four-pillar social insurance system supplemented by means-tested assistance.

**Constitutional Foundation -- Article 34 (인간다운 생활을 할 권리):**
1. "All citizens shall have the right to a life worthy of human dignity."
2. "The State shall have the duty to endeavor to promote social security and welfare."
3. "The State shall endeavor to promote the welfare and rights of women."
4. "The State shall have the duty to implement policies for the welfare of senior citizens and the young."
5. "Citizens who are incapable of earning a livelihood due to a physical disability, disease, old age or other reasons shall be protected by the State under the conditions as prescribed by Act."

**National Basic Living Security Act (국민기초생활 보장법, 1999):** This landmark statute, enacted during the Asian Financial Crisis, established the right to a minimum standard of living for all citizens meeting income and asset criteria. Key provisions:

- **Article 5 (Eligibility / 수급권자):** Persons whose household income falls below the minimum cost of living established annually by the Central Livelihood Security Committee (중앙생활보장위원회)
- **Article 7 (Types of Benefits / 급여의 종류):** Benefits include livelihood assistance (생계급여), housing assistance (주거급여), medical assistance (의료급여), education assistance (교육급여), childbirth assistance (해산급여), funeral assistance (장제급여), and self-sufficiency assistance (자활급여)
- **2014 Amendment (Customized Benefits System / 맞춤형 급여체계):** Replaced the all-or-nothing benefit structure with a graduated system applying separate income thresholds for each benefit type, expanding eligibility and reducing the "poverty cliff" effect

### Four Social Insurance System (4대 사회보험)

| Insurance | Law | Coverage | Contribution |
|-----------|-----|----------|-------------|
| National Pension (국민연금) | National Pension Act (1988) | Workers 18-59; self-employed | 9% of income (employer 4.5% + employee 4.5%) |
| National Health Insurance (건강보험) | National Health Insurance Act (1977/2000) | Universal | ~7% of income (split employer/employee) |
| Employment Insurance (고용보험) | Employment Insurance Act (1993) | Most workers | 1.8% (varies; employer pays larger share) |
| Industrial Accident Insurance (산재보험) | Industrial Accident Act (1963) | All workers | Employer only (varies by industry risk) |

### Constitutional Court Jurisprudence

**Constitutional Court Decision 2002Hun-Ma328 (2004):** The Court held that Article 34(1) of the Constitution creates a subjective right to a minimum standard of living, not merely a programmatic aspiration. However, the legislature has broad discretion in determining the specific level and form of benefits, and the Constitutional Court will intervene only if the level set is so low as to fall below the minimum required for human dignity.

**Constitutional Court Decision 2009Hun-Ma701 (2012):** The Court addressed the constitutionality of excluding certain categories of non-citizens from the National Basic Living Security system, holding that while the right to a life worthy of human dignity under Article 34 extends in principle to all persons present in Korea, the legislature may differentiate between citizens and non-citizens in the scope of welfare benefits, provided the differentiation is not arbitrary.

### Recent Developments

South Korea's social welfare spending has grown from approximately 6% of GDP in 2000 to approximately 14.8% in 2023, the fastest growth rate among OECD countries. The Gyeonggi Province Youth Dividend (경기도 청년기본소득), launched in 2019, provided quarterly payments of 250,000 KRW to all 24-year-old residents, representing one of Asia's first universal basic income pilot programs. The program was expanded in scope and is informing national-level discussions on UBI feasibility.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States operates a fragmented, categorical welfare system without a constitutional right to public assistance.

**Social Security Act (42 U.S.C. Section 301 et seq., 1935):** The foundational federal welfare statute, establishing:
- **Old-Age, Survivors, and Disability Insurance (OASDI):** Universal social insurance providing retirement, survivor, and disability benefits funded by payroll taxes (12.4%, split equally between employer and employee). In 2024, approximately 67 million Americans receive Social Security benefits.
- **Medicare (Title XVIII):** Universal health insurance for persons 65 and older and certain disabled persons.
- **Medicaid (Title XIX):** Joint federal-state program providing healthcare coverage to low-income individuals. Following the Affordable Care Act (ACA) and the Supreme Court's decision in NFIB v. Sebelius (2012), Medicaid expansion to adults earning up to 138% of the federal poverty level is optional for states; 40 states and D.C. have adopted expansion as of 2025.
- **Supplemental Security Income (SSI, Title XVI):** Means-tested cash assistance for aged, blind, and disabled individuals.

**Temporary Assistance for Needy Families (TANF, 1996):** Replaced Aid to Families with Dependent Children (AFDC) through the Personal Responsibility and Work Opportunity Reconciliation Act of 1996 (PRWORA). TANF provides block grants to states with significant flexibility in program design. Key features:
- Five-year lifetime limit on federal TANF benefits
- Work requirements: recipients must engage in work activities within 24 months
- States set benefit levels (varying from $170/month for a family of three in Mississippi to $923/month in New Hampshire)

**Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP, 7 U.S.C. Section 2011 et seq.):** The largest U.S. anti-hunger program, providing food purchasing assistance to approximately 42 million Americans (2024). SNAP benefits are determined by household income and size. Work requirements apply to able-bodied adults without dependents (ABAWDs), ages 18-54 (expanded from 50 by the Fiscal Responsibility Act of 2023).

### Constitutional Framework

**Dandridge v. Williams, 397 U.S. 471 (1970):** The Supreme Court held that Maryland's cap on AFDC benefits, regardless of family size, did not violate the Equal Protection Clause, applying rational basis review. The decision effectively foreclosed constitutional welfare rights claims under the Equal Protection Clause.

**Goldberg v. Kelly, 397 U.S. 254 (1970):** The Court held that welfare benefits constitute a "property" interest protected by the Due Process Clause, requiring a pre-termination hearing before benefits can be discontinued. This decision established procedural protections for welfare recipients but did not create a substantive right to benefits.

**King v. Burwell, 576 U.S. 473 (2015):** Upheld the ACA's provision of tax subsidies for health insurance purchased on both federal and state exchanges, preserving the ACA's expansion of health coverage.

### Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC)

The EITC (26 U.S.C. Section 32) is the largest federal anti-poverty program for working families, providing refundable tax credits to low- and moderate-income workers. In 2023, approximately 31 million workers claimed the EITC, with total credits exceeding $64 billion. The EITC has been credited with lifting approximately 5.6 million people out of poverty annually.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany operates one of the world's most comprehensive welfare states, codified in the Sozialgesetzbuch (Social Code, SGB).

**Constitutional Foundation:**
- **Article 1(1) GG (Menschenwürde):** "Human dignity shall be inviolable. To respect and protect it shall be the duty of all state authority."
- **Article 20(1) GG (Sozialstaatsprinzip):** "The Federal Republic of Germany is a democratic and social federal state."

**BVerfGE 125, 175 (2010) -- Hartz IV Decision:** The Federal Constitutional Court's most significant social welfare decision held that the right to a subsistence minimum consistent with human dignity (menschenwürdiges Existenzminimum) is a directly enforceable constitutional right derived from Article 1(1) read with Article 20(1) GG. The Court held:
- The state must guarantee the material conditions necessary for physical existence and a minimum of social, cultural, and political participation
- The legislature must establish benefits through a transparent, comprehensible procedure based on reliable data and sound methodology
- While the legislature has discretion in determining the method of calculation, the Court will review whether the methodology is internally consistent and produces constitutionally adequate results
- The Court found the existing Hartz IV standard rates unconstitutional because their calculation methodology was insufficiently transparent and contained unjustified deductions

### Social Code (Sozialgesetzbuch) Structure

| Book | Subject | Key Provisions |
|------|---------|---------------|
| SGB I | General Part | Fundamental principles; social rights |
| SGB II | Basic Income Support (Bürgergeld) | Means-tested assistance for employable persons |
| SGB III | Employment Promotion | Unemployment insurance; job placement; training |
| SGB IV | Common Provisions | Social insurance administration |
| SGB V | Statutory Health Insurance | Universal health coverage |
| SGB VI | Statutory Pension Insurance | Old-age, disability, survivor pensions |
| SGB VII | Statutory Accident Insurance | Work accident and occupational disease coverage |
| SGB VIII | Child and Youth Welfare | Child protection; youth services |
| SGB IX | Rehabilitation and Participation of Disabled Persons | Disability rights; inclusion |
| SGB X | Administrative Procedure | Procedural rules for social administration |
| SGB XI | Social Long-Term Care Insurance | Long-term care benefits |
| SGB XII | Social Assistance (Sozialhilfe) | Means-tested assistance for non-employable persons |

### Bürgergeld (Citizens' Income, 2023)

Effective January 1, 2023, the Bürgergeld (Citizens' Income) replaced the controversial Hartz IV system (Arbeitslosengeld II). Key changes include:
- **Standard Rates:** EUR 563/month for single adults (2024), with supplements for partners and children
- **Asset Protection:** Increased protected asset threshold during the first year (Karenzzeit) -- EUR 40,000 for the first person
- **Housing:** Actual housing costs covered during the first year without adequacy review
- **Activation Requirements:** Softened sanctions regime; cooperation plan (Kooperationsplan) replaces integration agreement; maximum sanction reduced to 30% of standard rate
- **Training Priority:** Enhanced support for vocational training and qualification

### BVerfGE 152, 68 (2019) -- Sanction Decision

The Federal Constitutional Court ruled on the constitutionality of Hartz IV benefit sanctions:
- Held that a 30% sanction (reduction) of the standard rate is constitutional as it serves the legitimate purpose of promoting self-sufficiency
- Ruled that sanctions exceeding 30% (including total benefit cuts) are unconstitutional because they fall below the subsistence minimum
- The Bürgergeld legislation of 2023 incorporated these constraints

## ECHR / EU

### European Social Charter

The European Social Charter (1961, revised 1996), monitored by the European Committee of Social Rights (ECSR), establishes social welfare rights including:
- **Article 12:** Right to social security
- **Article 13:** Right to social and medical assistance
- **Article 14:** Right to benefit from social welfare services
- **Article 30 (Revised Charter):** Right to protection against poverty and social exclusion

The ECSR issues conclusions on state compliance and can receive collective complaints. In European Federation of National Organisations Working with the Homeless (FEANTSA) v. France (Complaint No. 39/2006), the Committee found France in violation of Articles 31 and 16 for insufficient housing policies contributing to homelessness.

### EU Charter of Fundamental Rights

- **Article 34:** Right to social security benefits and social services
- **Article 35:** Right to healthcare
- **Article 36:** Access to services of general economic interest

### European Pillar of Social Rights (2017)

Proclaimed by the European Parliament, Council, and Commission on November 17, 2017, the Pillar establishes 20 principles organized around three categories: equal opportunities and access to the labor market, fair working conditions, and social protection and inclusion. Principle 14 states: "Everyone lacking sufficient resources has the right to adequate minimum income benefits ensuring a life in dignity at all stages of life."

### ECHR Jurisprudence -- Welfare as Property

**Stec and Others v. United Kingdom (Application Nos. 65731/01 and 65900/01, Grand Chamber, April 12, 2006):** The Grand Chamber held that where a state has legislation in force providing for the payment of a welfare benefit, this generates a proprietary interest under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 for persons satisfying the eligibility requirements. Reductions or elimination of established benefits must therefore satisfy the proportionality requirements of Article 1 of Protocol No. 1.

**Koufaki and ADEDY v. Greece (Application Nos. 57665/12 and 57657/12, May 7, 2013):** The Court addressed the constitutionality of pension reductions imposed during the Greek financial crisis, holding that while states have a wide margin of appreciation in times of economic crisis, reductions must not deprive individuals of their means of subsistence.

### EU Coordination Regulation (EC) No. 883/2004

This regulation coordinates social security systems across EU member states, ensuring that mobile workers do not lose social security rights when moving between member states. Key principles include: single applicable legislation, equal treatment, aggregation of periods, and export of benefits.

## Comparative Analysis

### Constitutional Right to Welfare

Germany provides the strongest constitutional protection, with a directly enforceable right to a subsistence minimum derived from human dignity. South Korea's Article 34 creates a judicially enforceable right, though with broad legislative discretion. The United States has no constitutional welfare right (Dandridge v. Williams). The ECHR protects established welfare benefits as property but does not create affirmative obligations to establish welfare programs.

### Welfare Generosity

Germany's Bürgergeld (EUR 563/month for a single adult, plus housing costs) provides the most generous means-tested assistance. South Korea's National Basic Living Security provides comparable relative generosity (approximately 600,000-700,000 KRW/month including housing). U.S. TANF benefits vary dramatically by state and are generally lower. The EU average falls between the German and U.S. levels.

### Work Requirements

All four systems incorporate activation requirements, but with different emphasis. The United States imposes the strictest work requirements (TANF five-year lifetime limit; SNAP ABAWD requirements). Germany's Bürgergeld softened work requirements from the Hartz IV regime but maintains activation obligations with limited sanctions. South Korea's self-sufficiency program emphasizes vocational training alongside work requirements. The EU's European Employment Strategy encourages "active inclusion" combining employment support with adequate income support.

### Universal Basic Income

None of the four jurisdictions has implemented a national UBI program. South Korea has conducted the most significant pilot (Gyeonggi Province Youth Dividend). Finland conducted a two-year UBI experiment (2017-2018) with promising results for well-being but limited employment effects. The concept remains under active discussion in all jurisdictions.
