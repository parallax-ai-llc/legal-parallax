## Summary

Mandatory minimum sentencing -- statutory provisions requiring courts to impose a minimum term of imprisonment upon conviction for specified offenses, regardless of the individual circumstances of the offense or the offender -- has been one of the most debated aspects of criminal justice policy over the past five decades, praised by proponents as ensuring consistency and deterrence while criticized by opponents for producing disproportionate sentences, eliminating judicial discretion, exacerbating racial and socioeconomic disparities, and contributing to mass incarceration. South Korea employs mandatory minimums (법정형 하한) for numerous serious offenses, with the Criminal Act (형법) and special criminal statutes prescribing minimum sentences for crimes including murder (Article 250: minimum 5 years), sexual assault (Act on Special Cases Concerning the Punishment of Sexual Crimes, 성폭력범죄의 처벌 등에 관한 특례법), and drug trafficking, while providing judicial mitigation mechanisms through Articles 53 (discretionary mitigation) and 55 (statutory mitigation) that permit courts to impose sentences below the mandatory minimum in exceptional circumstances -- a flexibility that distinguishes Korea's approach from the more rigid U.S. system. The United States has the most extensive mandatory minimum regime of any jurisdiction, with over 200 federal mandatory minimum provisions under Title 18 and Title 21 of the U.S. Code, most prominently the Anti-Drug Abuse Acts of 1986 and 1988 that established severe mandatory minimums for drug offenses (including the notorious 100:1 crack-to-powder cocaine disparity, partially remedied by the Fair Sentencing Act of 2010 and the First Step Act of 2018) -- generating sustained criticism from the U.S. Sentencing Commission, the American Bar Association, and numerous federal judges. Germany generally avoids mandatory minimums, relying instead on broad statutory sentencing ranges (Strafrahmen) under the StGB that provide courts with extensive discretion, with the Federal Constitutional Court (BVerfG) having held that mandatory minimum sentences may violate the guilt principle (Schuldprinzip) and the human dignity guarantee of Article 1(1) Grundgesetz where they prevent individualized sentencing; the limited mandatory minimums that exist in German law (such as Section 211 StGB's mandatory life sentence for murder) have been the subject of significant jurisprudential development to ensure proportionality. The European Court of Human Rights has addressed mandatory sentencing primarily through Article 3 (prohibition of inhuman or degrading treatment), holding in Vinter v. United Kingdom (2013) that irreducible life sentences violate Article 3 but leaving significant discretion to states regarding the appropriate level of mandatory sentences for determinate terms.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's sentencing system incorporates mandatory minimums for numerous serious offenses but provides courts with important mitigation mechanisms that preserve a degree of judicial discretion.

### Mandatory Minimums in the Criminal Act

The Criminal Act prescribes minimum sentences for specified offenses:

| Offense | Statutory Range (법정형) |
|---------|----------------------|
| Murder (Art. 250) | 5 years to life imprisonment or death |
| Rape (Art. 297) | 3 years or more imprisonment |
| Robbery (Art. 333) | 3 years or more imprisonment |
| Arson of occupied building (Art. 164) | 3 years or more imprisonment |
| Kidnapping (Art. 287) | 1 year or more imprisonment |

### Special Criminal Statutes

Special statutes have imposed increasingly severe mandatory minimums, particularly for sex offenses:

**Act on Special Cases Concerning the Punishment of Sexual Crimes (성폭력범죄의 처벌 등에 관한 특례법):** Following a series of high-profile sexual assault cases, including the Jo Doo-soon case (2008) and the Na Young case, the National Assembly substantially increased mandatory minimums for sexual offenses. Rape of a child under 13 carries a minimum of 7 years; sexual assault causing injury carries a minimum of 5 years.

**Act on the Protection of Children and Youth Against Sex Offenses (아동·청소년의 성보호에 관한 법률):** Establishes mandatory minimums for offenses against minors, with the 2012 amendment eliminating the possibility of suspended sentences for sexual offenses against children under 13.

### Mitigation Mechanisms

Unlike the U.S. system, South Korea provides courts with statutory authority to impose sentences below mandatory minimums:

**Article 53 (Discretionary Mitigation -- 작량감경):** The court may reduce the sentence when there are extenuating circumstances warranting mitigation, even below the statutory minimum. This provision is widely used.

**Article 55 (Statutory Mitigation -- 법률상 감경):** For specific statutory grounds (e.g., juvenile offender, self-defense excess, voluntary desistance from attempt), the court shall reduce the sentence by specified proportions.

The combined effect of Articles 53 and 55 means that Korean mandatory minimums function more as strong presumptive sentences than as absolute floors, preserving greater judicial discretion than comparable U.S. provisions.

### The Jo Doo-soon Case and Public Response

The 2008 sexual assault of an 8-year-old girl by Jo Doo-soon, who received a 12-year sentence (with a mitigated minimum due to alcohol intoxication under Article 10(2)), generated enormous public outrage and prompted a wave of mandatory minimum increases for sex offenses, chemical castration legislation, and the 2018 amendment excluding voluntary intoxication from sentence mitigation.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has the most extensive mandatory minimum sentencing regime of any jurisdiction examined, with over 200 federal mandatory minimum provisions and widespread state-level mandatory minimums.

### Federal Drug Mandatory Minimums

The Anti-Drug Abuse Act of 1986 established the cornerstone of the federal mandatory minimum system:

| Drug Type and Quantity | Mandatory Minimum | Mandatory Maximum |
|-----------------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| 5 kg+ cocaine or 280 g+ crack | 10 years | Life |
| 500 g-5 kg cocaine or 28-280 g crack | 5 years | 40 years |
| 1 kg+ heroin | 10 years | Life |
| 100-1,000 g heroin | 5 years | 40 years |
| Prior serious drug felony | Double the applicable minimum | N/A |

### Crack-Powder Cocaine Disparity

The most criticized aspect of federal mandatory minimums was the 100:1 sentencing disparity between crack and powder cocaine, established by the Anti-Drug Abuse Act of 1986 and the Omnibus Anti-Drug Abuse Act of 1988. Five grams of crack cocaine triggered the same five-year mandatory minimum as 500 grams of powder cocaine. Given that crack cocaine use was disproportionately associated with Black communities, the disparity produced severe racial disparities in federal sentencing.

**Fair Sentencing Act of 2010:** Reduced the disparity from 100:1 to 18:1, raising the quantity threshold for the five-year minimum from 5 grams to 28 grams of crack.

**First Step Act of 2018:** Made the Fair Sentencing Act retroactive, allowing approximately 2,600 federal prisoners to petition for reduced sentences.

### Safety Valve (18 U.S.C. Section 3553(f))

The safety valve permits federal judges to sentence below the mandatory minimum for certain drug offenses if the defendant meets five criteria, including no prior criminal history points, no use of violence, no leadership role in the offense, full cooperation with the government, and not possessing a firearm. The First Step Act of 2018 expanded safety valve eligibility.

### Judicial and Institutional Criticism

The U.S. Sentencing Commission has repeatedly recommended congressional action to reduce or eliminate mandatory minimums. In a 2011 report, the Commission found that mandatory minimum sentences were often more severe than necessary, that they resulted in unwarranted sentencing disparities (particularly by race), and that they transferred sentencing authority from judges to prosecutors whose charging decisions determine applicable minimums.

### State Reforms

Several states have enacted reforms:
- **California:** Proposition 47 (2014) reduced certain drug and property offenses from felonies to misdemeanors
- **New York:** Rockefeller Drug Law reforms (2009) reduced mandatory minimums for drug offenses
- **Michigan:** Eliminated many mandatory minimums for drug offenses (2002)

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's sentencing system is fundamentally different from the U.S. approach, prioritizing judicial discretion within broad statutory ranges (Strafrahmen) and imposing very few true mandatory minimum sentences.

### Strafrahmen (Sentencing Ranges)

German criminal statutes prescribe sentencing ranges rather than fixed minimums. For example:
- Simple theft (Section 242 StGB): Fine or imprisonment up to 5 years
- Robbery (Section 249 StGB): Imprisonment from 1 to 15 years
- Aggravated assault (Section 226 StGB): Imprisonment from 1 to 10 years

Within these ranges, courts exercise broad discretion under Section 46 StGB, considering the offender's guilt, the effects of the sentence on the offender's future life, and all relevant aggravating and mitigating circumstances.

### Limited Mandatory Sentences

The most significant mandatory sentence in German law is the mandatory life sentence for murder (Mord) under Section 211 StGB. The Federal Constitutional Court addressed this provision in BVerfGE 45, 187 (1977), holding that while the mandatory life sentence for murder is constitutional, the Basic Law requires the possibility of release (creating the mandatory parole review after 15 years under Section 57a StGB).

### Minder Schwerer Fall (Less Serious Case)

German law provides courts with a general mechanism to reduce sentencing ranges for "less serious cases" (minder schwere Fälle) under specific offense provisions (e.g., Section 249(2) StGB for robbery: 1-10 years instead of 1-15 years) and through statutory mitigation under Section 49 StGB, which reduces both the minimum and maximum of the applicable range.

### Constitutional Constraints

The Federal Constitutional Court has held that:
- The guilt principle (Schuldprinzip) requires that punishment be proportionate to individual guilt (BVerfGE 86, 288 (1992))
- Sentences that bear no reasonable relationship to the gravity of the offense and the guilt of the offender violate human dignity under Article 1(1) GG
- Legislation that entirely eliminates judicial discretion in sentencing must be narrowly limited and must still permit proportionate outcomes

### German Resistance to Mandatory Minimums

German legal scholarship and judicial practice are generally hostile to mandatory minimum sentences. The prevailing view holds that mandatory minimums violate the Schuldprinzip by preventing individualized sentencing, lead to unjust outcomes in atypical cases, and transfer sentencing power from the judiciary to the legislature in a manner inconsistent with the separation of powers.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Article 3 -- Grossly Disproportionate Sentences

**Vinter and Others v. United Kingdom [GC], Applications Nos. 66069/09, 130/10, 3896/10, Judgment of July 9, 2013:** The Grand Chamber held that whole-life sentences without the possibility of review violate Article 3. The Court established that Article 3 requires a review mechanism for life sentences, enabling consideration of whether continued detention is justified on penological grounds, and that this review must take place no later than 25 years after the imposition of the sentence, with further periodic reviews thereafter.

**Harkins and Edwards v. United Kingdom, Application No. 9146/07, Judgment of January 17, 2012:** The Court held that a mandatory sentence is not in itself incompatible with Article 3, provided it is not grossly disproportionate to the offense committed. The Court declined to establish a precise threshold of disproportionality.

**Khamtokhu and Aksenchik v. Russia [GC], Applications Nos. 60367/08 and 961/11, Judgment of January 24, 2017:** The Grand Chamber examined differential application of life imprisonment based on age and sex (Russian law exempts women and men over 65 from life imprisonment), finding no violation of Article 14 combined with Article 5.

### EU Framework

The EU has not harmonized sentencing practices regarding mandatory minimums, leaving this area to member state discretion. However, certain EU directives establish minimum penalty thresholds:

**Directive (EU) 2024/1385 on combating violence against women:** Requires member states to provide for maximum penalties of at least one year (basic offenses) to five years (aggravated offenses), effectively establishing minimum maximum sentences rather than mandatory minimums.

**Framework Decision 2004/757/JHA on drug trafficking:** Establishes minimum maximum penalties for drug offenses (at least 1-3 years for basic trafficking; 5-10 years for large-scale trafficking).

### Council of Europe Recommendations

Recommendation CM/Rec(2017)3 on the European Rules on Community Sanctions and Measures encourages member states to avoid mandatory sentences where possible, emphasizing that sentencing should be individualized and that community-based alternatives should be preferred over imprisonment for non-violent offenses.

## Comparative Analysis

### Judicial Discretion

Germany provides the broadest judicial discretion, with wide sentencing ranges and multiple mitigation mechanisms. South Korea occupies a middle position, with mandatory minimums that can be reduced through Articles 53 and 55. The United States provides the least discretion, with the safety valve available only for a narrow category of drug offenses and defendant profiles.

### Relationship to Mass Incarceration

The United States' extensive mandatory minimum regime has been identified as a primary driver of mass incarceration, with the federal prison population increasing from approximately 25,000 in 1980 to over 150,000 by 2024. South Korea's prison population has remained relatively stable. Germany's incarceration rate (approximately 64 per 100,000) is among the lowest in Europe, reflecting its preference for judicial discretion and alternatives to imprisonment.

### Prosecutorial Power

Mandatory minimums shift sentencing power from judges to prosecutors in all systems, but the effect is most pronounced in the United States, where prosecutorial charging decisions (which drug quantity to charge, whether to file a prior felony information) determine the applicable mandatory minimum. Germany's legality principle (Legalitätsprinzip) constrains prosecutorial discretion, while South Korea's system provides moderate prosecutorial influence.

### Reform Trajectories

The United States shows a slow trend toward reform (Fair Sentencing Act, First Step Act), though the pace of change is contested. South Korea has moved in the opposite direction for sex offenses, significantly increasing mandatory minimums in response to public outrage over specific cases. Germany continues to resist the expansion of mandatory minimums, consistent with its constitutional commitment to individualized sentencing.
