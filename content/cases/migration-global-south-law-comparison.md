## Summary

Migration and displacement in the Global South encompass vast internal population movements, cross-border refugee flows, and labor migration systems that collectively affect hundreds of millions of people and operate within markedly different legal frameworks. China's internal migration regime, governed by the hukou (household registration) system established under the 1958 Household Registration Regulations, affects approximately 376 million internal migrants (National Bureau of Statistics, 2023) who face systematic restrictions on access to public services, education, healthcare, and social insurance in their destination cities -- creating what scholars describe as the world's largest system of institutionalized internal displacement without the label. India's inter-state migration, estimated at 60 million inter-state and 450 million total internal migrants (Census 2011 projections and Economic Survey 2017), operates within a constitutional framework guaranteeing freedom of movement (Article 19(1)(d)-(e)) but without comprehensive protective legislation for migrant workers until the Occupational Safety, Health and Working Conditions Code, 2020 (which subsumed the Inter-State Migrant Workmen Act, 1979). Nigeria's internal displacement crisis, with approximately 3.6 million internally displaced persons (IDPs) as of 2024 (UNHCR data), primarily from Boko Haram insurgency in the Northeast and communal conflicts in the Middle Belt, lacks a comprehensive national IDP law, though the National Policy on Internally Displaced Persons (2012) and the ratification of the African Union Kampala Convention (2012) provide a framework. Brazil has emerged as the primary Latin American host for Venezuelan refugees and migrants (approximately 510,000 with regularized status as of 2024), implementing one of the most progressive refugee and migration frameworks in the Global South through the Migration Law (Lei No. 13.445/2017) and humanitarian visa programs. The Philippines has developed the world's most elaborate legal framework for protecting overseas workers through the Migrant Workers and Overseas Filipinos Act (Republic Act No. 10022, 2009, amending RA 8042, 1995) and institutional infrastructure centered on the Department of Migrant Workers (established by RA 11641, 2021). Indonesia's labor migration, involving approximately 9 million overseas workers, is governed by Law No. 18 of 2017 on the Protection of Indonesian Migrant Workers Abroad, which reformed the previously exploitative recruitment system.

## China

### The Hukou System and Internal Migration

China's internal migration regime is anchored in the Household Registration Regulations of the People's Republic of China (中华人民共和国户口登记条例), enacted by the National People's Congress Standing Committee on January 9, 1958. This foundational regulation establishes the hukou system, requiring every citizen to register with local authorities and classifying individuals by type (agricultural/non-agricultural) and locality. As discussed in the caste discrimination document, the hukou system creates structural barriers to internal migrants' integration in destination cities.

The scale of China's internal migration is unmatched globally. The National Bureau of Statistics (NBS) reported 376 million internal migrants (renkou liudong) in 2023, of whom approximately 172 million were inter-provincial migrants. This floating population (liudong renkou) -- roughly equivalent to the entire population of the United States -- faces a regime of differential access to public services based on hukou status.

### Legal Framework for Migrant Rights

The legal framework governing internal migrants has evolved through a patchwork of State Council regulations and local government measures:

**Provisional Regulations on Residence Permits (暂住证暂行条例, 2016):** The State Council's Interim Regulations on Residence Permits (Guowuyuan Ling No. 663, effective January 1, 2016) replaced the former temporary residence permit (zanzhuzheng) system with a residence permit (juzhuzheng) system, theoretically granting permit holders access to basic public services including compulsory education for children, basic public employment services, basic public health services, family planning services, legal aid, and certain municipal services. However, access to the full range of services (public school enrollment, social insurance, housing provident fund) requires meeting local conditions -- often including minimum years of social insurance contributions, stable employment, and stable housing.

**Employment Promotion Law (2007):** Article 31 prohibits employment discrimination based on household registration, requiring that migrant workers receive equal pay for equal work. Article 35 requires local governments to improve public employment services for rural workers entering cities. In practice, enforcement is weak, and studies document persistent wage gaps of 30-40% between hukou holders and migrants performing equivalent work.

**Labor Contract Law (2007):** Applies equally to migrant workers, requiring written labor contracts, overtime pay, and social insurance enrollment. However, the informality of much migrant employment (construction, domestic work, street vending) limits the Law's reach.

### Children of Migrants

One of the most acute humanitarian consequences of the hukou system is the estimated 13.9 million "left-behind children" (liushou ertong) in rural areas (UNICEF China data), separated from one or both parents who have migrated for work, plus approximately 35.8 million children living with migrant parents in cities (liudong ertong) who face barriers to local schooling. The State Council's 2016 Opinions on Strengthening Care and Protection of Left-Behind Children in Rural Areas (Guofa [2016] No. 13) directed local governments to improve services, but structural reform of the hukou-linked education system remains incomplete.

The National New-Type Urbanization Plan (2014-2020) and its successor plan (2021-2035) call for progressive equalization of public services for urban migrants and the settlement (luohu) of migrants in small and medium cities, but megacities continue to restrict hukou transfers through competitive points-based systems (e.g., Beijing's system requires minimum 7 years of residence permit holding, continuous social insurance, and scoring on education, employment, and other criteria).

## India

### Constitutional Framework

The Constitution of India guarantees freedom of internal movement:

- **Article 19(1)(d):** All citizens shall have the right to move freely throughout the territory of India.
- **Article 19(1)(e):** All citizens shall have the right to reside and settle in any part of the territory of India.

These rights are subject to reasonable restrictions under Article 19(5) in the interests of the general public or for the protection of the interests of any Scheduled Tribe. Several northeastern states (Nagaland, Mizoram, Arunachal Pradesh) maintain inner-line permit (ILP) systems restricting entry by non-locals under the Bengal Eastern Frontier Regulation, 1873, as extended.

### Inter-State Migrant Workers

India's inter-state labor migration was historically governed by the Inter-State Migrant Workmen (Regulation of Employment and Conditions of Service) Act, 1979 (ISMW Act), which required contractors to register, provide equal wages, and ensure suitable living conditions for workers recruited across state lines. The Act was widely regarded as ineffective -- the registration requirement was routinely evaded, and enforcement was negligible. The estimated 60 million inter-state migrants (Economic Survey 2016-17, based on Census and railway passenger data analysis) overwhelmingly worked in the informal sector beyond the Act's reach.

The **Occupational Safety, Health and Working Conditions Code, 2020** (one of the four labor codes replacing 29 central labor laws) subsumed the ISMW Act, providing in Chapter XII for inter-state migrant workers. Key provisions include:

- **Section 59:** Definition of inter-state migrant worker (employed through a contractor or directly recruited by an employer from one state to work in another, earning up to a prescribed monthly wage).
- **Section 62:** Duties of employers to provide working conditions and benefits at par with local workers.
- **Section 63:** Journey allowance for travel between home state and work state.

However, the Code's notification and implementation (requiring state-level rules) remains incomplete as of 2026, and the COVID-19 migrant crisis of 2020 -- when millions of stranded workers attempted to walk hundreds of kilometers to their home states after the sudden March 24, 2020 nationwide lockdown -- starkly exposed the absence of effective protection.

### COVID-19 Migrant Crisis and Judicial Response

The COVID-19 lockdown crisis generated significant Supreme Court intervention:

- In **In Re: Problems and Miseries of Migrant Labourers (2020)**, the Supreme Court took suo motu cognizance of the migrant crisis, directing state governments to provide food, shelter, and transport to stranded workers. The Court issued orders requiring registration of migrant workers, operation of free transport, and provision of employment under MGNREGA upon return.
- In **Alakh Alok Srivastava v. Union of India (2020)**, the Court addressed the plight of migrants walking on railway tracks, directing that no migrants be charged fares for travel.

### Citizenship and Cross-Border Migration

India's cross-border migration issues intersect with citizenship law. The **Citizenship Amendment Act, 2019 (CAA)** amended the Citizenship Act, 1955, to provide accelerated citizenship for persecuted religious minorities (Hindus, Sikhs, Buddhists, Jains, Parsis, Christians) from Afghanistan, Bangladesh, and Pakistan who entered India before December 31, 2014. The Act's exclusion of Muslims triggered massive protests and was challenged as violating Article 14 (right to equality). The Supreme Court has not yet rendered a final decision on the CAA's constitutionality, though the rules for implementation were notified in March 2024.

India's treatment of Rohingya refugees from Myanmar illustrates the absence of a comprehensive refugee law. India is not a signatory to the 1951 Refugee Convention or the 1967 Protocol. Rohingya in India (estimated 40,000) operate under ad hoc arrangements, with the government characterizing them as "illegal immigrants" subject to deportation under the Foreigners Act, 1946, despite UNHCR registration. In **Mohammad Salimullah v. Union of India (2021)**, the Supreme Court declined to halt the deportation of Rohingya refugees to Myanmar, finding no violation of the non-refoulement principle under Indian law.

## Africa

### South Africa: Immigration and Xenophobia

South Africa's migration framework operates under the Immigration Act 13 of 2002 (as amended) and the Refugees Act 130 of 1998 (substantially amended by the Refugees Amendment Act 11 of 2017). The constitutional framework provides:

- **Section 21(1):** Everyone has the right to freedom of movement.
- **Section 21(3):** Every citizen has the right to enter, to remain in, and to reside anywhere in the Republic.
- **Section 22:** Every citizen has the right to choose their trade, occupation, or profession freely.

South Africa hosts approximately 250,000 recognized refugees and asylum seekers, primarily from the Democratic Republic of Congo, Somalia, Ethiopia, and Zimbabwe. The Refugees Act provides for non-refoulement (Section 2), the right to work and study (Section 27(f)), and access to basic services. However, the asylum system faces chronic backlogs (over 180,000 pending applications as of 2024), and the 2017 amendments introduced restrictions including a requirement that asylum applications be submitted within 5 days of entry.

The most distinctive feature of South Africa's migration landscape is recurring xenophobic violence against African foreign nationals. Major outbreaks occurred in May 2008 (62 deaths, 100,000 displaced), 2015, 2019, and periodically since. In **Union of Refugee Women v. Director: Private Security Industry Regulatory Authority (2007 (4) SA 395 (CC))**, the Constitutional Court upheld the restriction barring non-citizens from employment in the private security industry, though Justice O'Regan's minority judgment argued that permanent residents should be included.

### Nigeria: Internal Displacement Crisis

Nigeria faces one of the world's largest internal displacement crises. As of 2024, approximately 3.6 million people were internally displaced, primarily due to:

1. **Boko Haram/ISWAP insurgency** in the Northeast (Borno, Adamawa, Yobe states): approximately 2.2 million IDPs
2. **Farmer-herder conflicts** in the Middle Belt (Benue, Plateau, Nasarawa, Kaduna states): approximately 600,000 IDPs
3. **Banditry** in the Northwest (Zamfara, Katsina, Kaduna, Niger states): approximately 800,000 IDPs

Nigeria's legal framework for IDPs includes:

- **National Policy on Internally Displaced Persons (2012):** A non-binding policy framework establishing principles for IDP protection, including freedom of movement, non-discrimination, access to humanitarian assistance, and durable solutions (return, local integration, or resettlement).
- **African Union Convention for the Protection and Assistance of Internally Displaced Persons (Kampala Convention, 2009):** Nigeria ratified the Convention in 2012. Article 3 obligates State Parties to refrain from, prohibit, and prevent arbitrary displacement, and Article 9 requires States to provide IDPs with adequate humanitarian assistance.
- **National Commission for Refugees, Migrants and Internally Displaced Persons (NCFRMI):** Established by Act No. 8 of 2004, the NCFRMI coordinates IDP protection and assistance alongside the National Emergency Management Agency (NEMA).

Despite these frameworks, Nigeria lacks a comprehensive IDP-specific statute. A National IDP Bill has been introduced in the National Assembly multiple times but has not been enacted. The absence of statutory protection leaves IDPs dependent on executive policy and humanitarian assistance without enforceable legal entitlements.

### South Africa: Refugee Jurisprudence

The Constitutional Court and Supreme Court of Appeal have developed important refugee protection jurisprudence:

- **Minister of Home Affairs v. Watchenuka (2004 (4) SA 326 (SCA)):** The Supreme Court of Appeal held that asylum seekers have the right to work and study while their applications are pending, as denial would violate their right to dignity.
- **Ruta v. Minister of Home Affairs (2019 (2) SA 329 (CC)):** The Constitutional Court held that asylum seekers who enter South Africa through a "safe" third country are not barred from applying for asylum, rejecting the "first safe country" doctrine.

## Latin America

### Brazil: Progressive Migration Law

Brazil has developed one of the most progressive migration legal frameworks in the Global South. The **Migration Law (Lei No. 13.445 of May 24, 2017)**, replacing the authoritarian-era Foreigners' Statute (Estatuto do Estrangeiro, Lei No. 6.815/1980), establishes migration as a human right and provides:

- **Article 3:** Migration policy principles including universality, indivisibility, and interdependence of human rights; repudiation of racism, xenophobia, and related forms of discrimination; non-criminalization of migration; social inclusion and access to public services; equal and free access to justice; and the right to family reunification.
- **Article 4:** Rights of migrants including inviolability of the right to life, liberty, equality, security, and property; freedom of movement; civil, social, cultural, and economic rights on an equal basis with nationals; access to public health services (SUS), education, the justice system, social assistance, and other public services.
- **Article 14:** Humanitarian visa for persons from countries experiencing grave or imminent institutional instability, armed conflict, disaster, or serious human rights violations.

### Venezuelan Migration Response

Brazil's response to the Venezuelan crisis demonstrates the Migration Law's practical application. Since 2015, approximately 510,000 Venezuelans have obtained regularized migration status in Brazil through:

- **Humanitarian Residence Permits:** Granted under CONARE (National Committee for Refugees) Resolution No. 126/2017 and subsequent regulations, providing two-year renewable residence permits to Venezuelan nationals.
- **Refugee Recognition:** CONARE has recognized Venezuelans as refugees under the expanded Cartagena Declaration definition (which Brazil incorporated through Lei No. 9.474/1997, Article 1(III): "generalized violation of human rights").
- **Operacao Acolhida (Operation Welcome):** A federal humanitarian operation established in 2018, coordinated by the military with civilian agencies and international organizations (UNHCR, IOM), providing reception, shelter, and voluntary internal relocation (interiorizacao) of Venezuelans from the border state of Roraima to other Brazilian states. By 2024, over 100,000 Venezuelans had been voluntarily relocated through the program.

### Regional Framework: Cartagena Declaration

The 1984 Cartagena Declaration on Refugees, while non-binding, has profoundly shaped Latin American refugee law by expanding the refugee definition beyond the 1951 Convention to include persons who have fled their country because their "lives, safety or freedom have been threatened by generalized violence, foreign aggression, internal conflicts, massive violation of human rights or other circumstances which have seriously disturbed public order." Brazil, Colombia, Mexico, and other Latin American states have incorporated this expanded definition into domestic legislation.

### Colombia: Displacement and Return

Colombia's internal displacement crisis -- the largest in the Western Hemisphere, with over 8.9 million registered victims including approximately 7.7 million displaced persons (Registro Unico de Victimas) -- has generated the most extensive domestic legal framework for IDPs:

- **Sentencia T-025/2004:** The Colombian Constitutional Court declared an "unconstitutional state of affairs" (estado de cosas inconstitucional) regarding the government's failure to protect displaced persons' rights, issuing over 400 follow-up orders through its Auto mechanism directing specific government actions.
- **Law 1448 of 2011 (Victims and Land Restitution Law):** Established a comprehensive framework for victim reparation and land restitution, creating specialized land restitution judges and reversing the burden of proof regarding land abandonment.

## Southeast Asia

### Philippines: Overseas Filipino Workers (OFW) Protection

The Philippines has developed the world's most comprehensive legal and institutional framework for protecting overseas labor migrants. Approximately 10 million Overseas Filipino Workers (OFWs) are deployed in over 200 countries and territories, remitting approximately USD 37 billion annually (Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas, 2023) -- over 8% of GDP.

**Legislative Framework:**

- **Republic Act No. 8042, Migrant Workers and Overseas Filipinos Act of 1995 (as amended by RA 10022, 2009):** The foundational statute provides for: mandatory licensing and regulation of recruitment agencies (Section 10); the "money claim" mechanism for worker complaints (Section 10); presumption of illegal recruitment for violations (Section 6); death penalty (later reduced to life imprisonment) for illegal recruitment in large scale involving economic sabotage (Section 7); mandatory insurance coverage for OFWs (Section 23-A); and the obligation of the state to provide legal assistance to OFWs in distress (Section 24).

- **Republic Act No. 11641, Department of Migrant Workers Act (2021):** Created a cabinet-level department specifically for migrant worker affairs -- the only country in the world with such an institution. The DMW consolidated functions previously dispersed across the Philippine Overseas Employment Administration (POEA), Overseas Workers Welfare Administration (OWWA), Philippine Overseas Labor Offices (POLOs), and other agencies.

- **Republic Act No. 10801, Overseas Workers Welfare Administration Act (2016):** Strengthened OWWA's mandate to provide welfare services including insurance, education, training, and social services to OFWs and their families.

**Institutional Architecture:**

The Philippine government maintains over 30 Philippine Overseas Labor Offices (POLOs) and Migrant Workers Offices (MWOs) worldwide, staffed by labor attaches who mediate disputes, provide legal assistance, and monitor working conditions. The Overseas Workers Welfare Administration (OWWA) manages a membership-funded welfare system providing life insurance, disability benefits, education scholarships for dependents, pre-departure training, and repatriation assistance.

**Landmark Case -- Prieto v. POEA (2015):** The Supreme Court in **Prieto v. NLRC and POEA** upheld the POEA's authority to regulate and discipline recruitment agencies, affirming the state's police power to protect overseas workers even when this restricts the contractual freedom of recruitment firms.

### Indonesia: Migrant Worker Protection

Indonesia's approximately 9 million overseas workers (Tenaga Kerja Indonesia, TKI / Pekerja Migran Indonesia, PMI) are concentrated in Malaysia, Saudi Arabia, Hong Kong, Taiwan, and the UAE, predominantly in domestic work, construction, and plantation agriculture.

**Law No. 18 of 2017 on the Protection of Indonesian Migrant Workers Abroad** replaced the previous Law No. 39 of 2004, which was widely criticized for treating migrant workers as commodities rather than rights-holders. Key reforms include:

- **Article 3:** Protection principles including respect for human rights, non-discrimination, gender equality, anti-trafficking, and informed consent.
- **Article 6:** Rights of migrant workers including the right to information, legal certainty, equal treatment, religious worship, and access to communication.
- **Article 49-55:** Prohibition of charging placement fees to migrant workers (shifting costs to employers); this provision addresses the pervasive debt bondage problem where workers previously incurred debts of USD 2,000-5,000 for placement fees.
- **Articles 39-48:** Regulation of private placement agencies (Pelaksana Penempatan Pekerja Migran Indonesia, P3MI), requiring licensing, financial guarantees, and accountability for worker welfare.

The **Badan Pelindungan Pekerja Migran Indonesia (BP2MI)** (National Agency for the Protection of Indonesian Migrant Workers), reconstituted from the former BNP2TKI by Government Regulation No. 9 of 2020, coordinates migrant worker placement and protection. Indonesia also participates in bilateral labor agreements (Memoranda of Understanding) with destination countries, though these have been criticized for insufficient enforcement mechanisms.

### Moratorium and Kafala

Indonesia has periodically imposed deployment moratoriums to destination countries following severe abuse cases. The 2015 moratorium on domestic worker deployment to 21 Middle Eastern countries (later partially lifted) followed the execution of Indonesian domestic workers in Saudi Arabia and widespread reports of physical and sexual abuse. The kafala (sponsorship) system in Gulf states, which ties workers' immigration status to their employer, remains the primary structural barrier to migrant worker protection, effectively creating conditions of bonded labor that neither Indonesian nor Philippine law can fully address extraterritorially.

## Comparative Analysis

### Internal vs. External Migration Governance

The Global South's migration challenges divide between internal migration governance (dominant in China, India, and Nigeria) and overseas labor migration governance (dominant in the Philippines, Indonesia, and to a lesser extent South Africa):

1. **China's hukou system** represents a unique model where internal migration barriers are deliberately maintained through administrative law, creating a regulated internal labor market that serves economic development objectives while systematically disadvantaging migrants.

2. **India's internal migration** operates in a constitutional framework that guarantees free movement but provides weak protective legislation and essentially no institutional infrastructure for migrant workers' welfare in destination states.

3. **Nigeria's IDP framework** addresses forced displacement rather than voluntary economic migration, with the Kampala Convention providing an international legal framework but domestic implementation remaining insufficient.

4. **The Philippines and Indonesia** have developed the world's most elaborate overseas labor migration governance systems, reflecting their economic dependence on remittances and the political salience of worker protection abroad.

5. **Brazil** demonstrates how a progressive migration law can create an enabling environment for refugee reception and integration, though implementation challenges persist.

### Rights vs. Control Paradigms

A fundamental tension exists between rights-based and control-based approaches:

- **Brazil's Migration Law** most fully embraces the rights paradigm, treating migration as a human right and granting migrants access to public services.
- **The Philippines' framework** balances worker protection with state-managed deployment, maintaining pre-departure requirements and deployment bans as regulatory tools.
- **China's hukou system** represents the control paradigm at its most developed, using administrative status to manage population movement and resource allocation.
- **South Africa's immigration regime** has shifted from a relatively open post-apartheid stance toward increasingly restrictive measures, driven by xenophobic politics and economic pressures.
