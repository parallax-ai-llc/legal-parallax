## Summary

Solicitation of crime -- the act of requesting, commanding, encouraging, or inducing another person to commit a criminal offense -- occupies a distinct position among inchoate offenses, criminalizing the solicitor's conduct regardless of whether the solicited person agrees or acts upon the request. South Korea addresses solicitation through the doctrine of instigation (교사, Article 31 of the Criminal Act/형법), which punishes a person who "induces another to commit a specific crime" with the same penalty as the principal offender, but critically requires that the instigated person actually commence the crime for the instigator's liability to attach; where the instigated person does not act, Korean law addresses the situation through the separate concept of failed instigation (교사의 미수) under Article 31(2)-(3), which applies only to specific serious offenses. The United States treats solicitation as a standalone inchoate offense under the Model Penal Code Section 5.02 and various federal statutes including 18 U.S.C. Section 373 (solicitation to commit a crime of violence), with the offense complete upon the making of the request regardless of the response; the Supreme Court has recognized solicitation's First Amendment implications in cases involving speech that advocates criminal conduct, most notably in Brandenburg v. Ohio, 395 U.S. 444 (1969), which distinguished protected advocacy from unprotected incitement. Germany addresses solicitation through Section 26 StGB (Anstiftung/instigation) and Section 30 StGB (attempted instigation), the latter of which criminalizes the attempt to instigate another to commit a felony even if the instigated person refuses or fails to act, while also criminalizing the declaration of willingness to commit a felony and the acceptance of an offer to commit one. The ECHR has examined solicitation in the context of agent provocateur and entrapment defenses, with the landmark Grand Chamber judgment in Ramanauskas v. Lithuania (Application No. 74420/01, 2008) establishing that police incitement to commit crime violates Article 6(1) of the Convention, and the EU has required criminalization of solicitation for specific offenses including terrorism (Directive 2017/541, Article 15) and trafficking (Directive 2011/36/EU).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's approach to solicitation operates through the doctrine of instigation (교사), which functions as a form of criminal participation rather than a standalone inchoate offense.

**Article 31 -- Instigation (교사범):**
- Paragraph 1: "A person who instigates another to commit a specific crime shall be punished in accordance with the punishment prescribed for the principal offender." This establishes that a successful instigator receives the same penalty as the person who actually commits the crime.
- Paragraph 2: "A person who instigates another to commit a crime, where the person instigated does not commence the execution of the instigated crime, or the instigation does not produce a result, shall be punished for the attempted crime only where specific provisions so provide."
- Paragraph 3: "The provisions of Article 26 [voluntary abandonment] and Article 27 [impossible crime] shall apply mutatis mutandis to the cases of paragraph 2."

### Requirements for Instigation

Korean criminal doctrine identifies four elements for instigation liability:

**Specific Crime (특정한 범죄):** The instigator must induce the commission of a specific, identifiable crime. General encouragement of criminal behavior without reference to a particular offense does not constitute instigation. The Supreme Court held in Decision 95도2551 (1996) that the instigator must have a specific criminal act in mind.

**Creation of Criminal Intent (범의의 야기):** The instigator must create the criminal intent in the mind of a person who did not previously harbor such intent. If the person was already determined to commit the crime, the conduct constitutes aiding (방조) rather than instigation.

**Principal's Commission (정범의 실행):** For full instigation liability under Article 31(1), the instigated person must actually commence the crime. This distinguishes Korean law from jurisdictions where the offense is complete upon making the request.

**Causal Connection (인과관계):** There must be a causal connection between the instigation and the principal's commission of the crime.

### Failed Instigation

Where the instigated person does not act on the solicitation, liability attaches only for offenses where Article 31(2) expressly applies. These include:

| Offense | Provision |
|---------|-----------|
| Insurrection (내란) | Art. 90 |
| Foreign aggression (외환) | Art. 101 |
| Explosion (폭발물) | Art. 120 |
| Murder (살인) | Art. 255 |

### Landmark Cases

**Supreme Court Decision 95도2551 (1996):** The Court held that instigation requires the creation of a specific criminal intent in the mind of the principal offender and cannot be satisfied by vague or general encouragement of unlawful conduct.

**Supreme Court Decision 2008도9436 (2009):** The Court addressed internet-based solicitation, holding that posting a request online for someone to commit a specific crime could constitute instigation where the post was accessed by and motivated the principal offender.

**Supreme Court Decision 2010도1017 (2010):** The Court examined the boundary between instigation and mere suggestion, holding that a defendant who repeatedly urged another to assault a third party had crossed the line from suggestion to instigation.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States treats solicitation as a standalone inchoate offense, complete upon the making of the request regardless of the response.

**Model Penal Code Section 5.02 -- Criminal Solicitation:** "(1) Definition of Solicitation. A person is guilty of solicitation to commit a crime if with the purpose of promoting or facilitating its commission he commands, encourages or requests another person to engage in specific conduct that would constitute such crime or an attempt to commit such crime or which would establish his complicity in its commission or attempted commission."

**18 U.S.C. Section 373 -- Solicitation to Commit a Crime of Violence:** "Whoever, with intent that another person engage in conduct constituting a felony that has as an element the use, attempted use, or threatened use of physical force against property or against the person of another in violation of the laws of the United States, and under circumstances strongly corroborative of that intent, solicits, commands, induces, or otherwise endeavors to persuade such other person to engage in such conduct, shall be imprisoned not more than half the maximum term of imprisonment or (notwithstanding section 3571) fined not more than half the maximum fine prescribed for the punishment of the crime solicited, or both."

### First Amendment Considerations

Solicitation doctrine in the United States intersects with First Amendment free speech protections:

**Brandenburg v. Ohio, 395 U.S. 444 (1969):** The Supreme Court held that the government cannot punish abstract advocacy of crime or lawlessness unless the advocacy is "directed to inciting or producing imminent lawless action and is likely to incite or produce such action." This standard distinguishes protected political speech from unprotected criminal solicitation.

**United States v. Williams, 553 U.S. 285 (2008):** The Court upheld the PROTECT Act's prohibition on pandering or solicitation of child pornography, holding that offers to engage in illegal transactions are categorically excluded from First Amendment protection.

### Key Elements

**Uncommunicated Solicitation:** Under the MPC Section 5.02(2), if the solicitation is not communicated to the intended recipient (e.g., the letter is intercepted), the solicitor is still liable but may be sentenced for an offense one grade lower.

**Renunciation Defense:** MPC Section 5.02(3) provides a defense if the solicitor, after soliciting the crime, "persuaded [the solicited person] not to do so or otherwise prevented the commission of the crime, under circumstances manifesting a complete and voluntary renunciation of his criminal purpose."

**Merger Doctrine:** If the solicited crime is actually committed, the solicitation merges into the substantive offense or into a conspiracy charge. Solicitation is thus typically charged only when the solicited crime was not committed.

### Penalties

| Statute | Maximum Penalty |
|---------|----------------|
| 18 U.S.C. Section 373 (general) | Half the maximum of solicited offense |
| 18 U.S.C. Section 2153 (solicitation of sabotage) | 10 years imprisonment |
| 18 U.S.C. Section 1958 (solicitation of murder-for-hire) | 10 years (no injury); 20 years (injury); death/life (death results) |
| State jurisdictions | Varies: typically one or two grades below target offense |

### Key Cases

**United States v. Rahman, 189 F.3d 88 (2d Cir. 1999):** The Second Circuit upheld the conviction of Sheikh Omar Abdel-Rahman for soliciting attacks on U.S. military installations and soliciting the murder of Egyptian President Hosni Mubarak, distinguishing criminal solicitation from protected religious speech.

**United States v. Alkhabaz, 104 F.3d 1492 (6th Cir. 1997):** The court addressed internet-based solicitation, examining when online discussions about criminal acts cross the line from fantasy to solicitation.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany addresses solicitation through two provisions: Section 26 StGB (successful instigation) and Section 30 StGB (attempted participation), the latter being one of the most expansive solicitation provisions in continental European law.

**Section 26 StGB -- Instigation (Anstiftung):** "Whoever intentionally instigates another to commit an intentional unlawful act shall be punished as an instigator in the same manner as the perpetrator." This provision applies when the instigated person actually commits or attempts the crime.

**Section 30 StGB -- Attempted Participation (Versuchte Beteiligung):** This uniquely German provision addresses several forms of inchoate solicitation:
- Paragraph 1: Attempting to instigate another to commit a felony is punishable from the penalty framework applicable to attempt.
- Paragraph 2: Declaring one's willingness to commit a felony, accepting an offer to commit one, or agreeing with another to commit a felony is punishable from the same framework.

### Requirements for Instigation (Section 26)

**Determination of Another (Bestimmen):** The instigator must cause the principal to form the decision to commit the offense. If the principal was already determined to commit the crime (omnimodo facturus), the conduct is not instigation but may constitute aiding.

**Double Intent (Doppelter Anstiftervorsatz):** The instigator must: (1) intend to cause the principal to decide to commit the crime; and (2) intend that the principal actually commit the specific crime. The instigator's intent must encompass the essential features of the principal's crime.

**Specificity Requirement (Bestimmtheit):** The instigated crime must be sufficiently specific. The BGH has held that instigation to commit "some crime" or "any crime" does not satisfy this requirement.

### Agent Provocateur (Lockspitzel)

German law has developed extensive jurisprudence on agent provocateur situations:

**BGH, Grand Senate Decision of June 18, 1996 (GSSt 1/96 -- Tatprovokation):** The Federal Court of Justice held that evidence obtained through police provocation (Tatprovokation) may be used in trial but requires a sentence reduction as compensation for the violation of the defendant's rights.

**BVerfG Decision of December 18, 2014 (2 BvR 209/14):** The Federal Constitutional Court held that particularly egregious police provocation may violate the fair trial principle (rechtsstaatliches Verfahren) and require a stay of proceedings.

### Withdrawal Under Section 31

**Section 31 StGB -- Withdrawal (Rücktritt):** A person who attempts to instigate another under Section 30 shall not be punished if they voluntarily abandon the plan and avert any existing danger that the offense will be committed. This withdrawal provision is more demanding than the general attempt withdrawal under Section 24 because the instigator must actively prevent the crime.

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights -- Entrapment Jurisprudence

The ECHR has developed the most significant body of case law on solicitation through its entrapment jurisprudence:

**Teixeira de Castro v. Portugal (Application No. 25829/94, Judgment of June 9, 1998):** The Court held that the use of undercover agents must be restricted and safeguarded, and that their involvement must not amount to incitement to commit an offense. The Court found a violation of Article 6(1) where police officers effectively instigated the applicant to commit a drug offense he would not otherwise have committed.

**Ramanauskas v. Lithuania (Application No. 74420/01, Grand Chamber Judgment of February 5, 2008):** The Grand Chamber established the definitive standard for police incitement: "Police incitement occurs where the officers involved -- whether members of the security forces or persons acting on their instructions -- do not confine themselves to investigating criminal activity in an essentially passive manner, but exert such an influence on the subject as to incite the commission of an offence that would otherwise not have been committed, in order to make it possible to establish the offence." Where such incitement is established, Article 6(1) is violated regardless of whether the evidence is admissible under domestic law.

**Bannikova v. Russia (Application No. 18757/06, Judgment of November 4, 2010):** The Court elaborated on the substantive and procedural tests for entrapment, establishing that states must have adequate procedures to distinguish permissible undercover operations from impermissible incitement.

### EU Framework

**Directive (EU) 2017/541 on Combating Terrorism:** Article 15 requires member states to criminalize "inciting" terrorist offenses, defined as distributing or otherwise making available a message to the public with the intent to incite the commission of terrorist offenses.

**Directive 2011/36/EU on Trafficking in Human Beings:** Article 2(2) requires criminalization of "inciting" trafficking offenses alongside aiding and abetting.

**Council Framework Decision 2008/913/JHA on Combating Racism and Xenophobia:** Article 2 requires member states to punish "instigating, aiding or abetting" conduct constituting racial hatred or violence.

## Comparative Analysis

### Offense Structure

The jurisdictions reflect two fundamentally different approaches to solicitation:

**Standalone Inchoate Offense (United States):** Solicitation is complete upon making the request, regardless of the response. This maximizes early intervention but raises concerns about criminalizing speech.

**Accessory-Dependent Offense (South Korea, Germany -- Section 26):** Full solicitation/instigation liability requires that the principal actually commit or attempt the crime. This approach is more restrained but may create gaps in coverage.

**Hybrid (Germany -- Section 30):** Germany bridges the gap through Section 30, which criminalizes attempted instigation as a standalone offense for felonies, providing coverage similar to the American approach but limited to serious offenses.

### Entrapment and Police Solicitation

The four systems show a spectrum of approaches to police-initiated solicitation:

- **ECHR (strictest):** Police incitement violates Article 6(1) and renders proceedings fundamentally unfair
- **Germany:** Police provocation requires sentence reduction; egregious cases may warrant stay of proceedings
- **United States:** Entrapment is an affirmative defense; jurisdictions split between subjective test (defendant's predisposition) and objective test (government conduct)
- **South Korea:** Entrapment defense recognized but applied restrictively

### Speech and Solicitation

Only the United States has a constitutional framework (First Amendment) that explicitly limits the scope of criminal solicitation to protect freedom of expression. Germany's Article 5 GG provides similar but less absolute protection. The ECHR's Article 10 (freedom of expression) provides contextual protection analyzed through the proportionality framework.
