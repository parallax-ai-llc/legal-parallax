## Summary

Embezzlement -- the fraudulent appropriation of property or funds by a person entrusted with their care -- is a distinct category of theft recognized in every major legal system, yet its statutory architecture, relationship to breach of trust offenses, and enforcement patterns vary dramatically across jurisdictions. South Korea criminalizes embezzlement (횡령죄) and breach of trust (배임죄) under Articles 355 and 356 of the Criminal Act, with aggravated penalties under the Specific Economic Crimes Act reaching life imprisonment for amounts exceeding 5 billion won, though the country's judicial system has become internationally notorious for its lenient treatment of chaebol founding families under the so-called "three-five rule." The United States addresses embezzlement through a patchwork of federal statutes, most notably 18 U.S.C. section 641 for government property (up to 10 years imprisonment) and the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002, which imposed sweeping corporate accountability measures including criminal penalties of up to 20 years for willful certification of misleading financial reports. Germany's Untreue (breach of trust) under section 266 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB) is one of the most significant yet controversial provisions in German white-collar criminal law, carrying up to 5 years imprisonment in ordinary cases and up to 10 years in particularly serious cases, though scholars and practitioners have long criticized the provision's exceptional breadth and vagueness. At the supranational level, the European Union reached political agreement in December 2025 on its first comprehensive anti-corruption directive, which for the first time establishes binding EU-wide minimum definitions for embezzlement and breach of trust offenses across all 27 Member States.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Under the Korean Criminal Act (형법), embezzlement and breach of trust are addressed through two principal provisions:

- **Article 355 (Embezzlement and Breach of Trust, 횡령과 배임):** A person who is in custody of another's property and embezzles it, or a person who administers another's business and obtains pecuniary advantage or causes a third person to do so in violation of their duty, thereby causing loss to such person, shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than 5 years or by a fine not exceeding 15 million won.
- **Article 356 (Occupational Embezzlement and Breach of Trust, 업무상의 횡령과 배임):** When a person who commits the crimes specified in Article 355 does so in the capacity of a professional duty holder, the penalty is increased to imprisonment for not more than 10 years or a fine not exceeding 30 million won.

Korean law distinguishes between embezzlement (횡령죄), which involves the misappropriation of property already in the offender's lawful custody, and breach of trust (배임죄), which involves a violation of fiduciary duty causing financial loss to the principal. This distinction is critical in corporate criminal law, where executives may be charged with breach of trust for business decisions that cause loss to the company, even absent personal enrichment.

### Aggravated Penalties

The Specific Economic Crimes Aggravated Punishment Act (특정경제범죄 가중처벌 등에 관한 법률) imposes enhanced penalties based on the amount of illicit gain:

| Illicit Gain | Penalty |
|-------------|---------|
| Under 500 million won | Standard penalties under Criminal Act |
| 500 million to 5 billion won | Imprisonment for at least 3 years |
| Over 5 billion won | Life imprisonment or imprisonment for at least 5 years |

### Landmark Chaebol Cases

South Korea's treatment of chaebol embezzlement cases has attracted sustained international criticism. The pattern is well documented: conviction, suspended sentence or early release, and presidential pardon -- a cycle critics describe as "too big to jail."

**Samsung -- Lee Kun-hee (2008):** Samsung Group chairman Lee Kun-hee was convicted of embezzlement and tax evasion in 2008 and sentenced to 3 years imprisonment, suspended for 5 years, along with a fine of 110 billion won. He received a presidential pardon from President Lee Myung-bak in 2009, justified publicly as necessary to allow Lee Kun-hee to participate in South Korea's bid to host the 2018 Winter Olympics. It was later revealed during Lee Myung-bak's own corruption trial that the pardon had been exchanged for bribes.

**Samsung -- Lee Jae-yong (2017-2022):** Samsung heir Lee Jae-yong was convicted in August 2017 of bribery, embezzlement, and concealment of criminal proceeds for funneling approximately $38 million to Choi Soon-sil, a confidant of former President Park Geun-hye, and sentenced to 5 years imprisonment. The Seoul High Court reduced this to 2.5 years with a suspended sentence in February 2018, leading to his release after one year of detention. In January 2021, he was again sentenced to 2 years and 6 months for bribery, embezzlement, and concealment of criminal proceeds worth approximately 8.6 billion won. He was pardoned by President Yoon Suk Yeol in August 2022. In February 2024, a Seoul court acquitted Lee Jae-yong of separate financial crimes related to a 2015 Samsung merger.

**SK Group -- Chey Tae-won (2013):** SK Group chairman Chey Tae-won was convicted in January 2013 of embezzling over $40 million from SK companies to cover personal trading losses and sentenced to 4 years imprisonment. He was pardoned by President Park Geun-hye in August 2015.

**Hyundai Motor -- Chung Mong-koo (2007):** Hyundai Motor Group chairman Chung Mong-koo was convicted in February 2007 of embezzlement and breach of fiduciary duty for selling securities to his son at below-market prices. He was sentenced to 3 years imprisonment, which was immediately suspended. He received a full pardon from President Lee Myung-bak in 2008.

### The "Three-Five Rule"

Korea's judiciary has been widely criticized for a pattern in chaebol cases known as the "three-five rule" (삼오 공식): a 3-year prison sentence whose execution is suspended for 5 years, after which the sentence is effectively extinguished if no further violations occur. Courts have repeatedly justified lenient treatment by arguing that chaebol leaders are so valuable to the national economy that imprisonment would harm public interest -- a rationale that has drawn sharp criticism from legal scholars and anti-corruption advocates.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States addresses embezzlement through a network of overlapping federal and state statutes, with federal prosecution focused on several key provisions:

- **18 U.S.C. section 641 (Embezzlement of Government Property):** Prohibits embezzling, stealing, purloining, or knowingly converting to one's use any record, voucher, money, or thing of value of the United States or any department or agency thereof. The statute also criminalizes receiving, concealing, or retaining such property with intent to convert it.
- **18 U.S.C. section 666 (Theft or Embezzlement from Programs Receiving Federal Funds):** Applies to embezzlement from organizations receiving more than $10,000 in federal funds in any one-year period, covering state and local governments, universities, hospitals, and other entities.
- **18 U.S.C. section 656 (Theft, Embezzlement, or Misapplication by Bank Officers or Employees):** Specifically targets officers, directors, agents, or employees of federally chartered or insured banks.

### Penalties

| Offense | Maximum Imprisonment | Maximum Fine |
|---------|---------------------|-------------|
| Government property embezzlement, over $1,000 (section 641) | 10 years | $250,000 |
| Government property embezzlement, $1,000 or less (section 641) | 1 year | $100,000 |
| Theft from federally funded programs (section 666) | 10 years | $250,000 |
| Bank embezzlement (section 656) | 30 years | $1,000,000 |

### Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002

Enacted in the wake of the Enron and WorldCom scandals, the Sarbanes-Oxley Act (SOX) fundamentally restructured corporate financial accountability in the United States and introduced severe criminal penalties for corporate fraud and embezzlement:

- **Section 802 (Criminal Penalties for Altering Documents):** Knowingly altering, destroying, mutilating, concealing, or falsifying records, documents, or tangible objects with intent to obstruct a federal investigation is punishable by up to 20 years imprisonment.
- **Section 906 (Corporate Responsibility for Financial Reports):** CEOs and CFOs must personally certify that periodic financial reports fully comply with securities laws and fairly present the company's financial condition. Knowing violations carry up to $1 million in fines and 10 years imprisonment; willful violations carry up to $5 million in fines and 20 years imprisonment.
- **Section 304 (Forfeiture of Certain Bonuses and Profits):** Requires CEOs and CFOs to reimburse the company for any bonus or incentive-based compensation and profits from stock sales received during the 12-month period following the issuance of financial statements that are later restated due to misconduct.

### Landmark Cases

**WorldCom (2002-2005):** CEO Bernard Ebbers orchestrated an $11 billion accounting fraud -- the largest in American history at the time -- through fraudulent accounting entries that inflated earnings and concealed expenses. Ebbers was convicted of securities fraud, conspiracy, and filing false reports with the SEC, and was sentenced to 25 years in federal prison. CFO Scott Sullivan, who cooperated with prosecutors, received a 5-year sentence.

**Tyco International (2002-2005):** CEO Dennis Kozlowski and CFO Mark Swartz were convicted of grand larceny, conspiracy, securities fraud, and falsifying business records for looting approximately $600 million from the company through unauthorized bonuses, loans, and stock sales. Both were sentenced to 8 to 25 years in state prison.

**Rita Crundwell (2012):** The comptroller of Dixon, Illinois embezzled $53.7 million in public funds over a 22-year period from 1990 to 2012 -- the largest municipal fraud in United States history. Crundwell used the funds to finance a lavish quarter horse breeding operation. She was sentenced to 19 years and 7 months in federal prison.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German criminal law addresses embezzlement and breach of trust through two principal provisions in the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB):

- **Section 246 StGB (Unterschlagung -- Misappropriation):** Covers the unlawful appropriation of movable property belonging to another that is already in the offender's possession. The basic offense carries imprisonment of up to 3 years or a fine. Aggravated misappropriation (Veruntreuung), where the property was entrusted to the offender, carries up to 5 years imprisonment or a fine.
- **Section 266 StGB (Untreue -- Breach of Trust):** The central provision for white-collar embezzlement, this section punishes anyone who abuses a power of disposition over the property of another granted by law, official mandate, legal transaction, or fiduciary relationship, or who violates a duty to safeguard the proprietary interests of another, thereby causing damage to those interests. The basic offense carries up to 5 years imprisonment or a fine.

### Elements of Section 266 (Untreue)

Section 266 encompasses two distinct variants:

1. **Abuse of Trust Variant (Missbrauchstatbestand):** The offender misuses a legally granted power to dispose of another's property or to obligate another person, thereby causing financial damage.
2. **Breach of Duty Variant (Treubruchtatbestand):** The offender violates a duty to safeguard the proprietary interests of another person, thereby causing financial damage.

Both variants require proof of a concrete financial loss (Vermogensschaden) to the victim. The Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) ruled in 2010 that section 266 must be interpreted narrowly to satisfy constitutional certainty requirements, emphasizing that a clear breach of duty and concrete financial damage must be established.

### Aggravated Cases

Under section 266, paragraph 2, in conjunction with section 263, paragraph 3 StGB, particularly serious cases of breach of trust are punishable by imprisonment of 3 months to 10 years. A particularly serious case typically applies when the offender:

- Acts on a commercial basis or as a member of a gang
- Causes a large-scale financial loss
- Abuses their position as a public official or acts in a professional capacity
- Causes economic hardship to the victim

### Landmark Cases

**Mannesmann Affair (2004-2006):** Deutsche Bank CEO Josef Ackermann and other supervisory board members of Mannesmann AG were charged with Untreue for approving approximately 57 million euros in bonuses and pension supplements to Mannesmann executives following the hostile takeover by Vodafone in 2000. The Dusseldorf Regional Court acquitted all defendants in 2004, but the Federal Court of Justice (Bundesgerichtshof) reversed the acquittals in 2005, finding that the bonuses constituted a breach of trust because they provided no benefit to the company. The case was ultimately settled in 2006 with the defendants paying fines without admission of guilt, and it remains a landmark ruling on the limits of executive compensation under German criminal law.

**HSH Nordbank (2013-2016):** Several former executives of HSH Nordbank were convicted of Untreue in connection with a complex derivatives transaction (Omega 55) that caused losses exceeding 150 million euros to the bank. The case illustrated the application of section 266 to risky financial transactions undertaken by bank executives.

### Academic Criticism

Section 266 StGB has been described by German legal scholars as an offense of "almost unparalleled vagueness" (nahezu beispiellose Unbestimmtheit). Critics argue that the broad language of the breach of duty variant effectively allows prosecutors and courts to retroactively criminalize business decisions that resulted in losses, even when those decisions were not unreasonable at the time they were made. The Federal Constitutional Court's 2010 ruling attempted to address these concerns by requiring strict interpretation, but the debate over the proper scope of Untreue remains one of the most contested questions in German criminal law.

## European Framework

### Historical Background

Prior to 2025, the European Union lacked a comprehensive criminal law instrument addressing corruption and embezzlement across Member States. Anti-corruption efforts were fragmented across several instruments:

- **Council Framework Decision 2003/568/JHA:** Addressed corruption in the private sector but did not cover embezzlement.
- **Convention on the Protection of the EU's Financial Interests (PIF Convention, 1995):** Addressed fraud against the EU budget, later replaced by Directive 2017/1371 (PIF Directive).
- **PIF Directive (2017/1371):** Harmonized Member State criminal laws to protect the EU's financial interests, supported by the European Anti-Fraud Office (OLAF) and the European Public Prosecutor's Office (EPPO), established in 2021.

### The EU Anti-Corruption Directive (2025-2026)

On 2 December 2025, the European Parliament and Council reached political agreement on the EU's first comprehensive anti-corruption directive after more than two years of negotiation, beginning with the European Commission's proposal in May 2023.

The directive establishes binding EU-wide minimum definitions for nine criminal offenses, including:

- Bribery of public officials (active and passive)
- Bribery in the private sector
- **Embezzlement** by public officials
- **Embezzlement** in the private sector
- Trading in influence
- Abuse of functions
- Obstruction of justice
- Illicit enrichment
- Misappropriation of property

Notably, the directive's embezzlement offense also covers breach of trust cases, punishing both public officials and private sector employees who misappropriate, spend, or appropriate entrusted funds for purposes other than intended.

### Implementation Timeline

| Milestone | Expected Date |
|-----------|--------------|
| Political agreement reached | December 2025 |
| Formal approval by European Parliament and Council | Early 2026 |
| Publication in Official Journal of the European Union | Spring 2026 |
| Entry into force (20 days after publication) | Spring 2026 |
| Beginning of transposition period | Mid-2026 |
| Deadline for Member State implementation | Approximately 2028 |

### Significance

The directive represents a major step toward harmonizing anti-corruption criminal law across the EU. For the first time, Member States will be required to criminalize embezzlement -- both in the public and private sectors -- according to common minimum definitions and to impose minimum maximum penalties. The directive also introduces provisions on aggravating and mitigating circumstances, statute of limitations periods, and cooperation between Member State authorities.
