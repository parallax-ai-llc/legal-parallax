## Summary

Affirmative action -- policies and programs designed to address historical disadvantage and promote equal opportunity for underrepresented groups through preferential treatment, targeted outreach, quota systems, or other proactive measures -- is one of the most debated areas of equality law, raising fundamental questions about the relationship between formal equality and substantive equality, and about the permissibility of using group-based classifications to remedy group-based disadvantage. South Korea implements affirmative action through several statutory frameworks, most notably the Act on the Employment Promotion and Vocational Rehabilitation of Persons with Disabilities (장애인고용촉진 및 직업재활법), which mandates a disability employment quota of 3.1 percent for public sector employers and 3.1 percent for private sector employers with 50 or more employees, and the Equal Employment Opportunity Act (남녀고용평등법), which authorizes temporary preferential measures for gender equality; additionally, the regional quota system (지역인재할당제) reserves a percentage of civil service positions for graduates of universities in non-capital regions, and various programs support North Korean defectors (새터민) in employment and education. The United States has experienced the most contested affirmative action debate, with the Supreme Court permitting race-conscious university admissions under strict scrutiny from Regents of the University of California v. Bakke (1978) through Grutter v. Bollinger (2003), before definitively ending race-conscious admissions in Students for Fair Admissions v. Harvard, 600 U.S. 181 (2023); Executive Order 11246 (1965) established affirmative action requirements for federal contractors, though Executive Order 14173 (2025) revoked it. Germany's Basic Law explicitly permits affirmative action for women under Article 3(2), which provides that "the state shall promote the actual implementation of equal rights for women and men and take steps to eliminate disadvantages that now exist," and implements gender quotas in corporate governance through the Gesetz für die gleichberechtigte Teilhabe von Frauen und Männern an Führungspositionen (FüPoG, 2015); however, German law generally prohibits ethnic or racial quotas. The European framework permits positive action under Article 157(4) TFEU and the equality directives, with the Court of Justice of the European Union having developed a nuanced jurisprudence in cases from Kalanke (1995) through Marschall (1997) and Badeck (2000) requiring that positive measures include a savings clause allowing individual circumstances to be considered.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's affirmative action programs are structured around specific categories of disadvantage rather than race or ethnicity, reflecting the country's relative ethnic homogeneity. Key programs include:

### Disability Employment Quota (장애인 의무고용)

The Act on the Employment Promotion and Vocational Rehabilitation of Persons with Disabilities (장애인고용촉진 및 직업재활법) mandates employment quotas:

| Sector | Quota (2024) | Enforcement |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| Government agencies | 3.6% | Mandatory; reporting requirements |
| Public institutions | 3.6% | Mandatory; reporting requirements |
| Private employers (50+ employees) | 3.1% | Non-compliance levy (부담금) |

Employers who fail to meet the quota must pay a levy (장애인고용부담금) for each unfilled position. The levy was approximately 1.2 million KRW per month per unfilled position as of 2024. The Korea Employment Agency for Persons with Disabilities (한국장애인고용공단) administers the system.

### Gender Equality Measures

The Equal Employment Opportunity Act (남녀고용평등과 일·가정 양립 지원에 관한 법률) authorizes temporary preferential measures (잠정적 우대조치) to promote gender equality in employment. The Act on the Promotion of the Economic Activities of Career-interrupted Women (경력단절여성등의 경제활동 촉진법) provides support programs for women re-entering the workforce.

The National Assembly enacted the Gender Representation Act for Public Committees in 2003, requiring that neither gender constitute less than 40 percent of membership on government advisory committees. Compliance has been uneven.

### Regional Quota System (지역인재할당제)

To address the concentration of economic and educational opportunity in the Seoul metropolitan area, South Korea operates a regional talent quota system for civil service recruitment. A designated percentage of national civil service positions (currently 30 percent for Grade 5 and above) must be filled by graduates of universities located outside the Seoul metropolitan area.

### North Korean Defector Support

The Act on the Protection and Settlement Support of Residents Escaping from North Korea (북한이탈주민의 보호 및 정착지원에 관한 법률) provides comprehensive settlement support for North Korean defectors (새터민), including preferential treatment in university admissions, employment placement assistance, housing subsidies, and vocational training.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment requires that government classifications based on race satisfy strict scrutiny: they must be narrowly tailored to serve a compelling governmental interest.

### Students for Fair Admissions v. Harvard, 600 U.S. 181 (2023)

The Supreme Court's 6-3 decision held that the race-conscious admissions programs of Harvard College and the University of North Carolina violated the Equal Protection Clause. Chief Justice Roberts's majority opinion held that the programs: (1) used race as a negative factor, penalizing applicants based on race; (2) operated as racial balancing; (3) lacked a meaningful connection between the means used and the educational diversity goals pursued; and (4) had no logical end point. The decision effectively ended race-conscious admissions in American higher education, overruling the framework established in Grutter v. Bollinger, 539 U.S. 306 (2003).

### Executive Order 11246 and Its Revocation

President Lyndon B. Johnson's Executive Order 11246 (1965) required federal contractors to take "affirmative action" to ensure non-discrimination and to develop written affirmative action programs. The Office of Federal Contract Compliance Programs (OFCCP) enforced these requirements. President Trump's Executive Order 14173 (January 2025) revoked EO 11246 and directed federal agencies to terminate "illegal DEI and DEIA programs."

### Employment Context

Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 permits voluntary affirmative action plans by employers under the framework established in United Steelworkers v. Weber, 443 U.S. 193 (1979), which held that Title VII does not prohibit voluntary race-conscious affirmative action plans designed to eliminate conspicuous racial imbalance in traditionally segregated job categories. However, the scope of permissible voluntary affirmative action has narrowed over time, and the current legal landscape following SFFA v. Harvard creates uncertainty about the viability of employer DEI programs.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Article 3(2) of the Basic Law provides: "Men and women shall have equal rights. The state shall promote the actual implementation of equal rights for women and men and take steps to eliminate disadvantages that now exist." This provision, amended in 1994 to add the second sentence, provides an explicit constitutional basis for gender-based affirmative action.

### Gender Quotas in Corporate Governance

**FüPoG I (2015):** The Gesetz für die gleichberechtigte Teilhabe von Frauen und Männern an Führungspositionen in der Privatwirtschaft und im öffentlichen Dienst requires publicly listed companies subject to full co-determination (approximately 100 companies) to maintain a minimum 30 percent representation of each gender on supervisory boards (Aufsichtsräte). Non-compliance results in the "empty chair" sanction (leerer Stuhl): supervisory board seats that would otherwise be filled with the underrepresented gender remain vacant.

**FüPoG II (2021):** Extended requirements to executive boards (Vorstände) of listed companies with more than 2,000 employees, requiring at least one woman on boards with more than three members.

### Disability Employment Quota

The Social Code Book IX (Sozialgesetzbuch IX, SGB IX) requires employers with 20 or more employees to fill at least 5 percent of positions with persons with severe disabilities (schwerbehinderte Menschen). Employers who fail to meet the quota pay a compensatory levy (Ausgleichsabgabe) of 140-360 euros per month per unfilled position, depending on the degree of non-compliance. The Integration Offices (Integrationsämter) administer the system.

### Limitations on Racial/Ethnic Affirmative Action

Unlike the United States (historically) and South Korea (regional quotas), Germany does not practice racial or ethnic affirmative action. The AGG prohibits discrimination based on race and ethnic origin, and the general approach is one of formal equality in these categories. Limited programs exist for integration of immigrants and refugees, but these are framed as integration support rather than affirmative action.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Legal Framework

Article 157(4) TFEU provides: "With a view to ensuring full equality in practice between men and women in working life, the principle of equal treatment shall not prevent any Member State from maintaining or adopting measures providing for specific advantages in order to make it easier for the underrepresented sex to pursue a vocational activity or to prevent or compensate for disadvantages in professional careers."

### CJEU Positive Action Jurisprudence

**Kalanke v. Freie Hansestadt Bremen (C-450/93, 1995):** The Court held that a German state law providing automatic priority to equally qualified women in sectors where they were underrepresented violated the Equal Treatment Directive because it guaranteed absolute and unconditional priority without allowing individual circumstances to be considered.

**Marschall v. Land Nordrhein-Westfalen (C-409/95, 1997):** The Court distinguished Kalanke, upholding a priority rule for women that included a savings clause (Öffnungsklausel) allowing male candidates to be preferred if reasons specific to an individual candidate tilted the balance in his favor. This decision established the "savings clause" requirement for positive action measures.

**Badeck and Others (C-158/97, 2000):** The Court upheld a more comprehensive positive action program for women in the Hesse civil service, permitting preferential hiring, training quotas, and interview guarantees, provided individual circumstances could be considered.

### EU Gender Balance Directive (2022)

Directive (EU) 2022/2381 on improving the gender balance among directors of listed companies, adopted November 22, 2022, requires member states to ensure that by June 30, 2026, listed companies achieve either 40 percent representation of the underrepresented sex among non-executive directors or 33 percent among all directors. Member states must transpose the Directive by December 28, 2024.

### ECHR Approach

The European Court of Human Rights has addressed affirmative action primarily through Article 14 (non-discrimination) read with other Convention rights. In Stec and Others v. United Kingdom (Application No. 65731/01, Grand Chamber, 2006), the Court held that differential treatment may be justified to correct factual inequalities, recognizing a margin of appreciation for states in implementing positive measures.

## Comparative Analysis

### Conceptual Frameworks

The jurisdictions reflect fundamentally different approaches to the relationship between equality and preferential treatment. The United States has moved from permitting race-conscious measures under strict scrutiny to effectively prohibiting them in education and potentially in employment. Germany permits and constitutionally encourages gender-based positive measures but prohibits racial/ethnic quotas. South Korea focuses on disability, regional, and gender categories. The EU framework permits positive action with a savings clause requirement.

### Quota Systems

South Korea and Germany both employ mandatory quota systems with non-compliance levies for disability employment. Germany extends this approach to gender representation on corporate boards. The United States does not employ mandatory quotas, with the Supreme Court having prohibited racial quotas in Bakke (1978) and the Court's recent jurisprudence further restricting preferential treatment. The EU Gender Balance Directive introduces the first EU-wide quota requirement for corporate governance.

### Enforcement Mechanisms

South Korea and Germany use the levy/compensatory payment model for disability quotas, creating financial incentives for compliance. Germany's "empty chair" sanction for gender quotas provides a structural enforcement mechanism. The United States historically relied on federal contractor compliance through OFCCP, but the revocation of EO 11246 eliminated this mechanism. The EU framework leaves enforcement to member states.
