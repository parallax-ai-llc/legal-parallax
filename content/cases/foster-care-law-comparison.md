## Summary

Foster care and out-of-home placement systems reflect deeply held societal values about the role of the state in family life, the rights of children, and the balance between family preservation and child protection. The legal frameworks governing foster care vary dramatically across jurisdictions, from the highly regulated and federally influenced US system to the decentralized German Pflegefamilie (foster family) model embedded within the broader Kinder- und Jugendhilfe (child and youth welfare) framework. South Korea's foster care system has undergone a fundamental transformation with the enactment of the Child Welfare Act (아동복지법) reforms and the shift from institutional care to family-based care following international criticism of the country's heavy reliance on group homes and residential institutions. The United States system is governed by a complex interplay of federal funding statutes -- principally the Adoption and Safe Families Act (ASFA, Pub. L. 105-89, 1997), the Fostering Connections to Success and Increasing Adoptions Act (Pub. L. 110-351, 2008), and the Family First Prevention Services Act (FFPSA, Pub. L. 115-123, Title VII, 2018) -- and state-specific child welfare laws. Germany's system operates under the Social Code Book VIII (SGB VIII, Kinder- und Jugendhilfe), reformed significantly by the Kinder- und Jugendstärkungsgesetz (KJSG, 2021). The UN Convention on the Rights of the Child (UNCRC, 1989) and the European Convention on Human Rights provide the overarching human rights framework, with ECHR Article 8 jurisprudence generating a substantial body of case law on the proportionality of state intervention in family life.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's foster care system has undergone dramatic reform driven by international pressure regarding its historically high rates of institutional care and international adoption:

- **Child Welfare Act (아동복지법, Act No. 19777):** The foundational statute governing child protection, amended extensively in 2024 to strengthen family-based care. Article 3 establishes the principle that children have the right to grow up in a family environment. Article 15 empowers the head of a Si/Gun/Gu (municipal government) to take protective measures for abused or neglected children, including emergency removal. Article 16 establishes the decision-making process for out-of-home placement, requiring consideration of: kinship care, foster care, group home care, and institutional care, in that order of preference.
- **Foster Care Support Act (가정위탁보호 활성화에 관한 법률):** Enacted to promote foster care as an alternative to institutional placement. Establishes support structures including: foster parent recruitment, training, and certification requirements; financial support for foster families (approximately KRW 200,000-400,000 per month per child, varying by municipality); respite care services; and post-placement monitoring.
- **Child Abuse Special Act (아동학대범죄의 처벌 등에 관한 특례법, 2014):** Strengthened penalties for child abuse and established emergency removal procedures. Article 12 authorizes emergency protective measures including immediate separation of the child from the abusive environment.
- **Single-Parent Family Support Act (한부모가족지원법):** Provides support to prevent family breakdown that may lead to out-of-home placement.
- **2024 Comprehensive Reform:** Major amendments effective in 2024 introduced: a mandatory 2-year maximum goal for temporary placement with reunification or permanent placement decision required; kinship care preference hierarchy; extended after-care support for youth aging out (up to age 24, increased from 18); and establishment of regional foster care support centers.

### Institutional Context

South Korea's child welfare system has historically been characterized by a heavy reliance on institutional care and international adoption:

- **Institutional Care Legacy:** As of 2020, approximately 50% of children in out-of-home care in South Korea were in residential institutions, compared to less than 15% in most Western countries. The government has committed to reducing this to under 30% by 2027.
- **International Adoption History:** South Korea sent over 200,000 children for international adoption between 1953 and 2023, one of the highest rates globally. The Special Adoption Act (입양특례법, 2011 reform) introduced court approval requirements for all international adoptions and a mandatory 5-month domestic adoption consideration period.
- **Korea National Child Protection Agency (중앙아동보호전문기관):** Operates the national child abuse hotline (112) and coordinates regional child protection agencies.

### Notable Cases

- **Jeong-in Case (2020):** The death of 16-month-old Jeong-in from abuse by her adoptive parents, despite multiple reports to child protective services, catalyzed sweeping reforms. The case revealed systemic failures in the post-placement monitoring system. Both adoptive parents were convicted of murder (Criminal Act Art. 250). The case led to the "Jeong-in Act" amendments to the Child Welfare Act, strengthening mandatory reporting requirements and establishing a court-approved placement review system.
- **Constitutional Court Decision on Best Interests (2021-Hun-Ba-234, 2023):** The Constitutional Court ruled that provisions of the Child Welfare Act permitting institutional placement without judicial review violated the child's constitutional rights under Article 10 (human dignity) and Article 36(1) (family life protection) of the Constitution. The Court required legislative amendment to ensure judicial oversight of all out-of-home placements.

## United States

### Federal Framework

The US foster care system is governed by a layered structure of federal funding statutes that condition federal financial participation on state compliance with specified requirements:

- **Adoption and Safe Families Act (ASFA, Pub. L. 105-89, 1997):** The cornerstone of modern US child welfare law. Key provisions: (1) requires states to file a petition to terminate parental rights (TPR) when a child has been in foster care for 15 of the most recent 22 months, unless an exception applies (relative placement, compelling reason, failure to provide services); (2) requires permanency hearings within 12 months of a child entering foster care; (3) establishes "reasonable efforts" requirements for both family preservation/reunification and timely permanency; (4) defines permanency options hierarchy: reunification, adoption, legal guardianship, another planned permanent living arrangement (APPLA).
- **Fostering Connections to Success and Increasing Adoptions Act (Pub. L. 110-351, 2008):** Expanded federal support by: extending federal foster care payments to age 21 (state option, adopted by approximately 30 states); requiring notification to all adult relatives within 30 days of a child's removal; promoting kinship care through the Kinship Guardianship Assistance Program (KinGAP); and requiring transition planning for youth aging out of foster care.
- **Family First Prevention Services Act (FFPSA, Pub. L. 115-123, Title VII, 2018):** Transformational reform allowing Title IV-E federal funding for prevention services (mental health, substance abuse treatment, in-home parenting) to keep children safely with their families, reducing unnecessary foster care entries. Also restricts federal funding for congregate care (group homes) to placements meeting specified criteria: qualified residential treatment programs (QRTPs) with accreditation, clinical assessment within 30 days, judicial approval within 60 days, and ongoing review. Exceptions for settings such as prenatal/postnatal programs, supervised independent living, and specialized programs for trafficked youth.
- **Indian Child Welfare Act (ICWA, 25 U.S.C. § 1901 et seq., 1978):** Establishes heightened protections for Native American children, including: jurisdictional preference for tribal courts; active efforts (higher than reasonable efforts) standard for removal and TPR; placement preferences prioritizing extended family, tribal members, and Native institutions. The Supreme Court upheld ICWA's constitutionality in Haaland v. Brackeen, 599 U.S. 255 (2023).
- **Multiethnic Placement Act (MEPA, 42 U.S.C. § 1996b, 1994, amended 1996):** Prohibits delaying or denying placement based on race, color, or national origin of the foster parent or child, with an exception for ICWA placements.

### State Implementation

Each state administers its own child welfare system, resulting in significant variation:

- **California Continuum of Care Reform (CCR, 2017):** Restructured the foster care system to prioritize resource families (certified foster homes meeting unified standards) and reduce group home placements.
- **Texas Foster Care System Litigation (M.D. v. Abbott, S.D. Tex.):** Class-action lawsuit resulting in a 2015 finding by Judge Janis Jack that Texas's foster care system violated children's substantive due process rights under the Fourteenth Amendment, with "an organizational culture and target system that undervalues the safety and well-being of children." Remedial orders mandated caseload caps, placement improvements, and monitoring.
- **New York Family Court Act § 1027-1089:** Establishes detailed procedural protections for child protective proceedings, including the right to counsel for parents and children.

### Notable Cases

- **Haaland v. Brackeen, 599 U.S. 255 (2023):** The Supreme Court upheld the constitutionality of the Indian Child Welfare Act against challenges that it violated equal protection, the Tenth Amendment, and the non-delegation doctrine. Justice Barrett's majority opinion held that Congress had authority under the Indian Commerce Clause to enact ICWA and that ICWA's placement preferences did not constitute racial classifications.
- **DeShaney v. Winnebago County, 489 U.S. 189 (1989):** The Supreme Court held that the Due Process Clause does not require the state to protect a child from private violence, even when the state is aware of the danger. The decision established that the state's failure to protect a child in parental custody does not violate constitutional rights, though it does not preclude state tort liability.
- **Santosky v. Kramer, 455 U.S. 745 (1982):** The Supreme Court held that the Due Process Clause requires "clear and convincing evidence" for termination of parental rights, rather than the lower "preponderance of the evidence" standard. This heightened standard reflects the fundamental liberty interest of parents in the care and custody of their children.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's foster care system is embedded within its comprehensive child and youth welfare framework under Social Code Book VIII:

- **SGB VIII (Sozialgesetzbuch Achtes Buch -- Kinder- und Jugendhilfe):** The foundational federal statute governing all child and youth welfare services. Key provisions:
  - **§ 1 (Right to Education and Development):** Every young person has the right to support for development and education to become a responsible and socially competent personality.
  - **§ 27 (Hilfe zur Erziehung / Assistance in Education):** Parents who cannot adequately fulfill their educational responsibilities are entitled to assistance, including out-of-home placement. This framing as an entitlement of the parents (not a coercive state intervention) reflects the German system's emphasis on the supportive rather than punitive character of child welfare.
  - **§ 33 (Vollzeitpflege / Full-Time Foster Care):** Provides for placement of a child with a foster family (Pflegefamilie) as a form of Hilfe zur Erziehung. Placement should correspond to the child's age and developmental needs and provide the child with a family-like environment. Prioritizes placement with suitable relatives (Verwandtenpflege).
  - **§ 34 (Heimerziehung / Residential Care):** Provides for placement in a residential institution (Heim) or other supervised living arrangement. Residential care should prepare the child for return to the family, placement in another family, or independent living.
  - **§ 36 (Hilfeplan / Care Plan):** Requires the Jugendamt (youth office) to develop an individualized care plan in cooperation with the parents, child, and foster family. The plan must be reviewed at regular intervals (typically every 6 months).
  - **§ 41 (Hilfe für junge Volljährige / Assistance for Young Adults):** Provides continued support for young adults who have been in care, originally up to age 21, extended to age 27 by the KJSG 2021 reform with a "coming back" option allowing resumed support.
- **BGB (Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch / Civil Code) §§ 1666, 1666a:** Govern judicial intervention in parental authority (elterliche Sorge). § 1666 authorizes the family court (Familiengericht) to take measures necessary to avert danger to the child's welfare, including partial or complete withdrawal of parental authority. § 1666a establishes the proportionality principle: measures separating the child from the family are only permissible if the danger cannot be averted by other means, including public assistance.
- **BGB § 1688:** Grants foster parents certain rights regarding everyday matters (Angelegenheiten des täglichen Lebens) of the child in their care, unless the parent or guardian objects.
- **Kinder- und Jugendstärkungsgesetz (KJSG, 2021):** Major reform of SGB VIII effective 10 June 2021, introducing: inclusive children and youth welfare services (Inklusive Kinder- und Jugendhilfe) integrating services for children with and without disabilities; strengthened participation rights for children and youth (Ombudsstellen, § 9a); extended assistance for young adults up to age 27 (§ 41(1)); mandatory independent complaints mechanisms; and strengthened cooperation between youth welfare and family courts.

### Institutional Architecture

- **Jugendämter (Youth Offices):** Approximately 600 Jugendämter at the municipal level (Kreise and kreisfreie Städte) are the primary responsible authorities for child welfare, including foster care. The Jugendamt assesses the need for assistance, develops care plans, approves foster families, and monitors placements. The dual structure of the Jugendamt (professional staff + Jugendhilfeausschuss civic committee) ensures both professional and democratic governance.
- **Landesjugendämter (State Youth Offices):** Provide oversight, quality assurance, and technical support to the Jugendämter within each Bundesland.
- **Pflegekinderdienste (Foster Care Services):** Specialized units within the Jugendämter responsible for recruiting, training, certifying, and supporting foster families.

### Notable Cases

- **BVerfG, 1 BvR 2569/16 (19 November 2021):** The Federal Constitutional Court ruled that the separation of a child from foster parents who had raised the child for several years violated the child's fundamental right to family life under Article 6(1) GG, recognizing the foster family as the child's "social family" (soziale Familie). The Court emphasized that the child's bonds with foster parents must be given significant weight alongside the biological parents' rights.
- **BVerfG, 1 BvR 1178/14 (2019):** The Court held that the return of children from foster care to biological parents requires a comprehensive assessment of the child's best interests, including the child's attachment relationships and the risk of harm from disrupting established bonds. The Court criticized mechanical application of the biological parents' priority right (Elternrecht) without individualized assessment.
- **BGH, XII ZB 407/20 (2021):** The Federal Court of Justice clarified that foster parents have standing to appeal family court decisions regarding the child's welfare, strengthening the procedural position of foster families in custody proceedings.

## ECHR / EU Framework

### Convention Articles

The European Convention on Human Rights provides the most developed supranational jurisprudence on foster care and state intervention in family life:

- **Article 8 -- Right to Respect for Private and Family Life:** The central ECHR provision in foster care cases. The ECtHR applies a three-part test: (1) does the measure constitute an interference with family life? (taking a child into care is always an interference); (2) is the interference "in accordance with the law" and pursued a "legitimate aim" (protection of health or morals, or the rights and freedoms of others)? and (3) is the interference "necessary in a democratic society"? The last element requires a proportionality assessment, with the Court emphasizing that taking a child into care should normally be regarded as a temporary measure and that the ultimate aim should be to reunite the natural parent with the child. The state has a positive obligation to facilitate reunification as soon as reasonably feasible.
- **Article 6 -- Right to a Fair Trial:** Parents have the right to effective participation in proceedings concerning the care of their children, including access to information, legal representation, and the opportunity to present evidence.
- **Article 3 -- Prohibition of Inhuman or Degrading Treatment:** Applicable where conditions in foster care or institutional care amount to inhuman or degrading treatment. Also relevant where the state fails to protect a child from abuse within the family.
- **Article 14 -- Prohibition of Discrimination:** Prohibits discrimination in the application of Article 8 rights, relevant to cases involving same-sex foster parents, ethnic minority families, or Roma families.

### Key Case Law

- **Strand Lobben and Others v. Norway (ECtHR Grand Chamber, 2019, App. No. 37283/13):** The Grand Chamber found a violation of Article 8 where Norwegian authorities terminated a mother's parental rights and authorized adoption of her foster child, despite evidence that her circumstances had improved. The Court held that the domestic authorities had not conducted a genuine balancing exercise between the child's interests and the mother's rights, and had not adequately considered less intrusive measures such as contact rights.
- **K and T v. Finland (ECtHR Grand Chamber, 2001, App. No. 25702/94):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where Finnish authorities placed a newborn in emergency care immediately after birth based on the mother's mental health history, without sufficient evidence of imminent danger. Established that emergency removal requires "extraordinarily compelling reasons."
- **Johansen v. Norway (ECtHR, 1996, App. No. 17383/90):** Found a violation of Article 8 where Norway deprived a mother of access and parental rights with a view to adoption. The Court stated that such measures should only be applied in "exceptional circumstances" and can only be justified by an "overriding requirement pertaining to the child's best interests."
- **Y.C. v. United Kingdom (ECtHR, 2012, App. No. 4547/10):** The Court confirmed that Article 8 requires decision-makers considering a care order to conduct a fair balancing exercise, giving weight to reunification as the primary objective, but acknowledged that the child's best interests may override the parent's right to family life.
- **Abdi Ibrahim v. Norway (ECtHR Grand Chamber, 2021, App. No. 15379/16):** Found a violation of Article 8 where a Somali Muslim child was placed with a Christian foster family and subsequently adopted, without adequate consideration of the mother's religious and cultural identity. The Court emphasized that cultural and religious background must be taken into account in placement decisions.

### UN Convention on the Rights of the Child

- **Article 3 (Best Interests):** The best interests of the child shall be a primary consideration in all actions concerning children.
- **Article 9 (Separation from Parents):** States must ensure that a child is not separated from parents against their will except when competent authorities subject to judicial review determine separation is necessary for the best interests of the child.
- **Article 20 (Children Deprived of Family Environment):** Children temporarily or permanently deprived of their family environment are entitled to special protection and assistance, including foster placement, kafalah of Islamic law, adoption, or institutional care. Due regard shall be paid to the desirability of continuity in a child's upbringing and to the child's ethnic, religious, cultural, and linguistic background.
- **Article 25 (Periodic Review):** A child placed for the purposes of care, protection, or treatment has the right to periodic review of the treatment and all circumstances relevant to the placement.

| **State Intervention Threshold** | Emergency removal + court review (post-2024 reform) | Varies by state; "imminent danger" for emergency removal | BGB § 1666: danger to child's welfare | "Relevant and sufficient reasons" (Art. 8 test) |

| **Key Tension** | Institutional legacy vs. family-based reform | Permanency timelines vs. family preservation | Parental rights (Elternrecht) vs. child welfare (Kindeswohl) | Reunification goal vs. child's established bonds |
