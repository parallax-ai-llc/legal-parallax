## Summary

Workers' compensation -- the system of mandatory insurance or statutory benefits providing medical treatment, wage replacement, and disability payments to employees injured or made ill in the course of employment -- represents one of the oldest forms of social insurance and embodies the "grand bargain" in which employees receive guaranteed benefits regardless of fault while employers receive immunity from tort liability. South Korea's Industrial Accident Compensation Insurance Act (산업재해보상보험법), first enacted in 1963 as one of the nation's earliest social insurance programs, establishes a comprehensive public insurance system administered by the Korea Workers' Compensation and Welfare Service (근로복지공단) covering virtually all workers in establishments with one or more employees; the system provides medical benefits, temporary disability benefits at 70 percent of average wages, permanent disability benefits through 14 disability grades, survivor benefits, and vocational rehabilitation, with significant expansions in 2021 to cover mental health conditions including work-related depression, PTSD, and overwork-related cardiovascular disease (과로사). The United States operates workers' compensation through 50 separate state systems with significant variation in coverage, benefits, and administration, supplemented by federal programs for specific worker categories including the Federal Employees' Compensation Act (FECA), Longshore and Harbor Workers' Compensation Act, and the Black Lung Benefits Act; most states follow a no-fault model with exclusive remedy provisions that bar employees from suing employers in tort. Germany pioneered statutory accident insurance through Otto von Bismarck's Unfallversicherungsgesetz of 1884, now codified in Sozialgesetzbuch VII (SGB VII) and administered by the Berufsgenossenschaften (employer liability insurance associations), which combine workers' compensation functions with workplace safety regulation in a unique integrated system; the German system covers all employees, students, and volunteers with no employee contribution requirement. At the European level, workers' compensation remains primarily a national competence, with EU coordination limited to Regulation (EC) No 883/2004 on social security coordination for cross-border workers, while ILO Convention No. 121 on Employment Injury Benefits (1964) establishes minimum international standards.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

The Industrial Accident Compensation Insurance Act (산업재해보상보험법) was enacted on November 5, 1963, and took effect on July 1, 1964, making it one of the first social insurance programs established under the Park Chung-hee government's economic development plans. Originally covering only mining and manufacturing workplaces with 500 or more employees, coverage has been progressively expanded and since 2000 applies to all workplaces with one or more employees.

### Administration

The Korea Workers' Compensation and Welfare Service (근로복지공단, COMWEL), established under the Act, manages all aspects of the insurance system: premium collection, claims adjudication, medical treatment through its network of hospitals and rehabilitation centers, vocational rehabilitation, and return-to-work programs. The system is entirely employer-funded through experience-rated premiums; employees make no contribution.

### Benefits Structure

| Benefit Type | Description | Calculation |
|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| Medical treatment (요양급여) | All necessary medical care for work-related injury/disease | Full cost coverage; no co-payment |
| Temporary disability (휴업급여) | Wage replacement during treatment period | 70% of average daily wages |
| Permanent disability (장해급여) | Compensation for lasting disability | 14 grades: Grade 1 (most severe) = 1,474 days' wages lump sum or annuity; Grade 14 = 55 days' wages |
| Survivor benefits (유족급여) | Benefits to dependents of deceased workers | Annuity: 52-67% of average wages; or lump sum: 1,300 days' wages |
| Funeral expenses (장의비) | Funeral costs | 120 days' average wages |
| Vocational rehabilitation (직업재활급여) | Retraining and return-to-work support | Various programs including job modification subsidies |

### 2021 Mental Health and Overwork Reforms

The 2021 amendment significantly expanded the recognition of work-related mental health conditions:

**Overwork-related Diseases (과로사):** The amendment formalized criteria for recognizing cardiovascular and cerebrovascular diseases (뇌심혈관계 질병) caused by chronic overwork, particularly relevant given South Korea's intensive working culture. Workers who worked excessive hours (more than 60 hours per week on average over 12 weeks) and developed heart attacks, strokes, or related conditions are presumed to have work-related diseases.

**Mental Health:** Work-related depression, anxiety disorders, PTSD, and adjustment disorders are now explicitly covered, with streamlined approval processes. The amendment also recognized workplace bullying (직장 내 괴롭힘) as a causal factor for compensable mental health conditions.

### Statistics

In 2023, COMWEL processed approximately 130,000 industrial accident claims, with an approval rate of approximately 89 percent. Total benefit expenditure exceeded 8.5 trillion KRW. The occupational fatality compensation rate has declined steadily but remains higher than in Germany or most EU member states.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Workers' compensation in the United States is governed primarily by state law, resulting in 50 distinct systems with significant variation. The first state workers' compensation law was enacted by Wisconsin in 1911, and by 1948 all states had established workers' compensation programs.

### State System Variations

| Feature | Majority Approach | Notable Variations |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Insurance mechanism | Private insurance or state fund | Ohio, Washington, Wyoming, North Dakota: exclusive state funds (monopolistic) |
| Wage replacement rate | 66.67% of average weekly wage | Range from 60% to 80% |
| Maximum weekly benefit | Typically 100-150% of state average weekly wage | Significant variation |
| Waiting period | 3-7 days before benefits begin | Some states retroactively pay if disability exceeds specified period |
| Attorney fees | Regulated by workers' comp board | Contingency fees typically 15-25% |
| Exclusive remedy | Yes, with exceptions | Some states allow tort suits for intentional conduct |

### Federal Programs

| Program | Coverage | Administering Agency |
|---------|----------|---------------------|
| Federal Employees' Compensation Act (FECA) | Federal civilian employees | DOL Office of Workers' Compensation Programs |
| Longshore and Harbor Workers' Compensation Act | Maritime workers (non-seamen) | DOL OWCP |
| Black Lung Benefits Act | Coal miners with pneumoconiosis | DOL OWCP |
| Energy Employees Occupational Illness Compensation Program | DOE workers exposed to radiation/toxins | DOL OWCP |
| Defense Base Act | Contractors on overseas military bases | DOL OWCP |

### Contemporary Issues

**Opt-Out Movement:** Texas is the only state that does not require most private employers to carry workers' compensation insurance. Oklahoma enacted an opt-out provision in 2013 allowing qualifying employers to establish alternative benefit plans, but the Oklahoma Supreme Court struck down the law as unconstitutional in 2016 (Vasquez v. Dillard's, Inc.).

**Gig Economy:** The classification of gig workers as independent contractors has excluded millions of workers from workers' compensation coverage. California's AB 5 (2019) and subsequent Proposition 22 (2020) created a contested framework attempting to address this gap.

**Grand Bargain Erosion:** A 2015 investigative series by ProPublica and NPR, "Demolition of Workers' Comp," documented a decades-long trend of benefit reductions across states, raising questions about whether the "grand bargain" of guaranteed benefits in exchange for tort immunity remains equitable.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany established the world's first statutory workers' compensation system through the Unfallversicherungsgesetz (Accident Insurance Act) of 1884, one of three social insurance laws enacted under Chancellor Otto von Bismarck. The system is now codified in Sozialgesetzbuch VII (SGB VII), effective January 1, 1997.

### Berufsgenossenschaften System

The German system is unique in combining workers' compensation with workplace safety functions within a single institutional framework. The Berufsgenossenschaften (BG) are public-law corporations organized by industry sector:

| BG | Industry Sector |
|----|----------------|
| BG BAU | Construction |
| BG ETEM | Energy, textiles, electrical, media |
| BGW | Health services and welfare |
| BG Verkehr | Transport |
| BGHW | Trade and logistics |
| BG RCI | Raw materials and chemicals |
| BG Holz und Metall | Wood and metal |
| VBG | Administration |
| BG Nahrungsmittel | Food industry |

Each BG performs three integrated functions: prevention (establishing safety rules and conducting inspections), rehabilitation (providing comprehensive medical and vocational rehabilitation), and compensation (paying disability and survivor benefits).

### Benefits

| Benefit | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Medical treatment (Heilbehandlung) | Complete medical care including specialized BG-run trauma hospitals (BG-Kliniken) |
| Injury benefit (Verletztengeld) | 80% of regular earnings during treatment/rehabilitation (§ 47 SGB VII) |
| Disability pension (Verletztenrente) | Based on reduction of earning capacity (Minderung der Erwerbsfähigkeit, MdE); full pension at 2/3 of annual earnings for 100% MdE (§ 56 SGB VII) |
| Survivor pension (Hinterbliebenenrente) | Widower/widow: 30% (or 40% if raising children/over 47) of deceased's annual earnings |
| Pflegegeld | Care allowance for those requiring ongoing personal care |

### Exclusive Remedy (Haftungsprivileg)

Section 104 SGB VII provides that employees may not sue their employers in tort for workplace injuries covered by the statutory insurance system, except where the employer caused the injury intentionally (Vorsatz). Section 105 extends this immunity to co-employees. This exclusive remedy (Haftungsprivileg) is the German implementation of the grand bargain.

### Occupational Diseases (Berufskrankheiten)

The Berufskrankheiten-Verordnung (BKV) lists recognized occupational diseases (currently 82 listed diseases as of the 2021 amendment). The 2021 reform (Seventh SGB VII Amendment Act) eliminated the requirement that workers cease the disease-causing activity before receiving occupational disease benefits (Unterlassungszwang), a longstanding barrier to claims recognition. The reform also introduced an "open clause" allowing recognition of diseases not on the formal list if new scientific evidence establishes work-causation.

### Performance Indicators

In 2022, the Berufsgenossenschaften processed approximately 822,000 reportable workplace accidents and recognized approximately 19,000 new occupational disease cases. Total expenditures across all BGs were approximately EUR 16.7 billion. Germany's workplace fatality rate of 0.7 per 100,000 workers is among the lowest in the world, attributed in part to the integrated prevention-compensation model.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Social Security Coordination

Regulation (EC) No 883/2004 on the coordination of social security systems ensures that workers moving between EU member states do not lose workers' compensation coverage. The Regulation establishes rules for determining the applicable legislation (generally the law of the state of employment), aggregation of insurance periods, and export of benefits. It is supplemented by Implementing Regulation (EC) No 987/2009.

### EU Recommendation on Occupational Diseases

Commission Recommendation 2003/670/EC establishes a European schedule of occupational diseases that member states are encouraged to adopt. The Recommendation covers diseases caused by chemical, physical, and biological agents, as well as occupational skin diseases, respiratory diseases, and cancers. While not legally binding, it serves as a reference framework for national occupational disease lists.

### ILO Convention No. 121

ILO Convention No. 121 on Employment Injury Benefits (1964) establishes minimum standards for workers' compensation systems. Key requirements include coverage of all employees including public sector workers, compensation for workplace accidents and occupational diseases, medical treatment without limit, wage replacement of at least 60 percent during temporary incapacity, and periodic payments (rather than lump sums) for permanent disability and death. South Korea ratified Convention 121 in 1992; neither the United States nor Germany has ratified it, though both substantially exceed its minimum standards.

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR has addressed workers' compensation-related issues through the lens of Article 1 of Protocol 1 (protection of property) and Article 6 (fair trial). In Moskal v. Poland (Application No. 10373/05, 2009), the Court held that social security benefits, including disability benefits, constitute "possessions" under Article 1 of Protocol 1, and that withdrawal of benefits must satisfy the proportionality test. The Court has also held that workers' compensation proceedings must comply with Article 6 fair trial guarantees, including reasonable duration and access to an independent tribunal.

## Comparative Analysis

### System Architecture

The three jurisdictions represent three distinct architectural models: South Korea operates a centralized public insurance system administered by a single national agency (COMWEL); the United States operates 50+ fragmented systems with a mix of private insurance, state funds, and self-insurance; Germany operates an industry-based system of quasi-public self-governing associations (Berufsgenossenschaften). The German model is unique in integrating prevention with compensation, creating institutional incentives for accident prevention.

### Benefit Adequacy

Germany provides the most generous wage replacement (80 percent), followed by South Korea (70 percent) and the United States (typically 66.67 percent, subject to state caps). However, the U.S. system's maximum weekly benefit caps often result in effective replacement rates well below 66.67 percent for higher-wage workers.

### Coverage Gaps

The United States has the most significant coverage gaps, with Texas allowing employers to opt out entirely, and the classification of gig economy workers as independent contractors excluding millions from coverage. South Korea's expansion to cover all one-plus-employee establishments and Germany's inclusion of students and volunteers provide near-universal coverage within their respective systems.

### Occupational Disease Recognition

Germany's 2021 elimination of the cessation requirement (Unterlassungszwang) represented a major reform removing a barrier that had prevented many workers from receiving occupational disease benefits while continuing to work. South Korea's 2021 expansion to mental health conditions and overwork-related diseases reflects growing recognition of psychosocial workplace hazards. U.S. states vary significantly in their occupational disease recognition, with many using restrictive lists or requiring proof of unique workplace causation.
