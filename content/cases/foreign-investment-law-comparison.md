## Summary

Foreign investment regulation -- the legal framework governing the entry, operation, and protection of capital from foreign sources -- has undergone a dramatic tightening across all major economies in response to geopolitical competition, technology transfer concerns, and national security anxieties, while simultaneously remaining subject to international investment law obligations under bilateral investment treaties (BITs) and multilateral trade agreements. South Korea regulates foreign investment through the Foreign Investment Promotion Act (외국인투자 촉진법, FIPA) of 1998, which replaced the more restrictive Foreign Capital Inducement Act and established a generally open investment regime with sector-specific restrictions under the negative list system (네거티브 리스트), supervised by the Ministry of Trade, Industry and Energy (MOTIE) and the Korea Trade-Investment Promotion Agency (KOTRA, 대한무역투자진흥공사), while national security screening was strengthened through the 2020 amendment requiring prior notification for acquisitions in critical technology sectors. The United States screens foreign investment through the Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States (CFIUS), dramatically expanded by the Foreign Investment Risk Review Modernization Act (FIRRMA, 2018) to cover non-controlling investments in critical technology, critical infrastructure, and sensitive personal data companies, while also maintaining comprehensive economic sanctions through the Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC) and recently introducing outbound investment screening for China-directed investments in semiconductor, AI, and quantum computing sectors through Executive Order 14105 (2023). Germany reviews foreign investments under the Foreign Trade and Payments Act (Außenwirtschaftsgesetz, AWG) and the Foreign Trade and Payments Ordinance (Außenwirtschaftsverordnung, AWV), with screening thresholds progressively lowered since 2020 to 10% voting rights in sensitive sectors, and screening activity increasing substantially with approximately 306 procedures initiated in 2023. At the EU level, the FDI Screening Regulation (2019/452) established a cooperation mechanism among member states and the European Commission for evaluating foreign investments on security and public order grounds, while the EU maintains the most extensive network of bilateral investment treaties and is developing an autonomous investment screening capability through the proposed amendment to the FDI Screening Regulation (2024).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea transitioned from a highly restrictive to a generally open foreign investment regime through the enactment of FIPA in 1998 during the Asian financial crisis.

### Foreign Investment Promotion Act (외국인투자 촉진법)

FIPA establishes a presumption of openness: foreign investment is permitted in all sectors except those on the negative list. The Act defines foreign investment as the acquisition of 10% or more of the outstanding shares of a Korean company, long-term loans of five years or more, or contributions to a nonprofit corporation.

**Negative List:** The Consolidated Public Notice of Foreign Investment (외국인투자 통합공고) specifies restricted and prohibited sectors. Fully restricted sectors include broadcasting (지상파 방송), nuclear power, and defense. Partially restricted sectors (foreign ownership caps) include telecommunications (49% cap for key operators), airlines (49%), and news media.

### National Security Screening

The 2020 FIPA amendment strengthened national security review for foreign acquisitions in designated "core technologies" (국가핵심기술). Acquisitions of companies possessing core technologies designated under the Industrial Technology Protection Act (산업기술의 유출방지 및 보호에 관한 법률) require prior notification to MOTIE. The ministry may prohibit or impose conditions on transactions that threaten national security.

### Investment Incentives

FIPA provides generous incentives for foreign investment designated as "foreign investment eligible for benefits" (외국인투자 지원):

| Incentive | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| Corporate tax reduction | 5-7 year exemption or reduction for high-technology investments |
| Customs duty exemption | Exemption on capital goods imports for 5 years |
| Land and infrastructure | Reduced lease rates in foreign investment zones (외국인투자지역) |
| Free economic zones | Eight designated free economic zones (경제자유구역) with regulatory simplification |
| Cash grants | Available for investments exceeding specified thresholds in targeted sectors |

### Foreign-Invested Companies

As of 2024, over 18,000 foreign-invested companies operate in Korea, with cumulative FDI stock exceeding $270 billion. Major source countries include the United States, Japan, the Netherlands, Singapore, and Germany. The semiconductor, automotive, and financial services sectors attract the largest investments.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States maintains one of the world's most developed foreign investment screening mechanisms through CFIUS, while also operating extensive sanctions and export control regimes.

### CFIUS and FIRRMA

CFIUS is an inter-agency committee chaired by the Secretary of the Treasury that reviews foreign acquisitions and investments for national security implications. FIRRMA (2018) significantly expanded CFIUS jurisdiction:

**Covered Transactions:** CFIUS may review any transaction that could result in foreign control of a U.S. business. FIRRMA extended jurisdiction to non-controlling investments in "TID U.S. businesses" -- companies involved in critical technologies, critical infrastructure, or sensitive personal data.

**Mandatory Filing:** Transactions involving foreign government-controlled investors and TID U.S. businesses require mandatory declarations. Other covered transactions may be filed voluntarily, but CFIUS may initiate reviews of non-notified transactions.

**Review Timeline:** 45-day initial review period, extendable to a 45-day formal investigation, followed by a 15-day presidential decision period if CFIUS cannot resolve national security concerns.

### Notable CFIUS Actions

| Year | Transaction | Outcome |
|------|-----------|---------|
| 2018 | Broadcom/Qualcomm | Presidential order blocking acquisition (national security) |
| 2020 | ByteDance/TikTok | Presidential order requiring divestiture (pending as of 2026) |
| 2023 | Nippon Steel/U.S. Steel | Presidential order blocking acquisition (national security, critical industry) |

### Outbound Investment Screening

Executive Order 14105, issued August 9, 2023, established a new outbound investment screening regime targeting U.S. investments in Chinese entities involved in semiconductors and microelectronics, quantum information technologies, and artificial intelligence. The Treasury Department's implementing regulations (effective January 2, 2025) require notification of certain transactions and prohibit others entirely.

### CHIPS and Science Act (2022)

The CHIPS Act provides $52.7 billion in subsidies for domestic semiconductor manufacturing and research, with "guardrails" restricting recipients from expanding semiconductor manufacturing capacity in countries of concern (primarily China) for ten years. This represents a new form of investment regulation that conditions domestic subsidies on outbound investment restrictions.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany has progressively tightened foreign investment screening since 2020, driven by concerns about Chinese acquisitions of German technology companies.

### AWG/AWV Screening Framework

The Foreign Trade and Payments Act (AWG) Section 55 empowers the Federal Ministry for Economic Affairs and Climate Action (BMWK) to review and prohibit acquisitions of German companies by non-EU/non-EFTA investors.

**Cross-Sector Review (Section 55 AWV):** Applies to acquisitions of 25% or more of voting rights in any German company. The BMWK may review and prohibit the transaction if it threatens public order or security. Review is triggered by notification or BMWK initiative within five years of the transaction.

**Sector-Specific Review (Section 60 AWV):** Applies to acquisitions of 10% or more of voting rights in companies operating in sensitive sectors: defense, IT security, telecommunications, cloud computing, media, critical infrastructure, AI, robotics, semiconductors, aerospace, quantum technology, and healthcare. The BMWK must clear or prohibit the transaction within a statutory review period.

### Notable Screening Decisions

**KUKA Robotics (2016):** The Chinese Midea Group's acquisition of KUKA AG, a leading German robotics manufacturer, for EUR 4.5 billion proceeded without prohibition but catalyzed political demands for stronger screening powers, directly contributing to the 2017 and 2020 AWG amendments.

**Aixtron (2016):** The BMWK withdrew its initial clearance of the Fujian Grand Chip Investment Fund's acquisition of semiconductor equipment manufacturer Aixtron SE, following new intelligence assessments regarding national security implications. The acquisition was ultimately abandoned.

**Elmos Semiconductor (2022):** The BMWK prohibited the acquisition of Elmos Semiconductor's wafer fabrication division by Swedish-Chinese Silex Microsystems, marking the first formal prohibition under the tightened screening regime.

### EU Coordination

Germany actively participates in the FDI Screening Regulation cooperation mechanism, both submitting and receiving notifications about foreign investments in other member states. Germany's screening decisions are increasingly coordinated with the European Commission's opinions.

## ECHR & EU

### FDI Screening Regulation (2019/452)

Regulation 2019/452, applicable since October 11, 2020, establishes a cooperation mechanism for FDI screening among member states and the Commission:

**Cooperation Mechanism:** Member states must notify the Commission and other member states of foreign direct investments undergoing screening. Other member states may provide comments, and the Commission may issue non-binding opinions. The final screening decision remains with the member state where the investment takes place.

**Screening Factors:** Article 4 lists factors member states may consider: effects on critical infrastructure, critical technologies, supply of critical inputs, access to sensitive information, and freedom/pluralism of media.

**Annual Reports:** The Commission publishes annual reports on FDI screening trends. The 2023 report noted that 24 member states had adopted national screening mechanisms (up from 14 in 2019), and approximately 1,500 FDI transactions were screened across the EU.

### Proposed Amendment (2024)

The European Commission proposed a comprehensive amendment to the FDI Screening Regulation in January 2024, which would make national screening mechanisms mandatory for all member states, extend screening to intra-EU investments by entities ultimately controlled by non-EU investors, establish binding minimum standards for screening procedures, and broaden the scope of screenable transactions.

### ECHR Investment Protection

The ECHR has addressed foreign investment disputes through the property protection guarantee of Protocol 1, Article 1:

**Yukos v. Russia (Application No. 14902/04, Just Satisfaction Judgment of July 31, 2014):** The Grand Chamber awarded EUR 1.866 billion in just satisfaction -- the largest award in ECHR history -- finding that Russia's tax assessments and enforcement against the Yukos oil company constituted a violation of Article 1, Protocol 1. The case demonstrated that the ECHR can function as an investment protection mechanism.

**OAO Neftyanaya Kompaniya Yukos v. Russia (Application No. 14902/04, Merits Judgment of September 20, 2011):** The Court found that while taxation measures pursued legitimate aims, the enforcement methods were disproportionate and violated the right to peaceful enjoyment of possessions.

### EU International Investment Agreements

Since the Lisbon Treaty (2009), the EU has exclusive competence over foreign direct investment under the Common Commercial Policy (Article 207 TFEU). The EU has concluded or is negotiating investment chapters in comprehensive trade agreements including the EU-Canada CETA (provisional application since 2017), the EU-Vietnam Investment Protection Agreement (2020), and the EU-Singapore Investment Protection Agreement (2019).

## Comparative Analysis

### Open vs. Restrictive Orientation

Despite recent tightening, all four jurisdictions maintain fundamentally open foreign investment regimes with targeted restrictions:

**Most Open (South Korea):** FIPA establishes a presumption of openness with a defined negative list. Screening is limited to core technology sectors.

**Open with Extensive Screening (United States):** While foreign investment is generally welcome, CFIUS's broad jurisdictional reach and the introduction of outbound investment screening make the U.S. system the most comprehensive.

**Tightening (Germany/EU):** Germany has rapidly expanded its screening from narrow defense-focused review to broad technology and critical infrastructure screening. The EU is moving toward mandatory screening across all member states.

### Outbound Investment Controls

The United States is the pioneer of outbound investment screening -- restricting domestic companies from investing abroad in sensitive sectors. No other major jurisdiction has implemented equivalent outbound controls, though the EU has discussed the concept in the context of economic security strategies.

### Investment Incentives vs. Security Screening

All four jurisdictions maintain investment promotion programs alongside security screening, creating a policy tension between attracting foreign capital and protecting national interests. The CHIPS Act exemplifies this tension by simultaneously subsidizing domestic investment and restricting outbound investment in the same sector.
