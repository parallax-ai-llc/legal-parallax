## Summary

The principle of proportionality -- requiring that government action restricting fundamental rights be suitable, necessary, and proportionate in the narrow sense to the pursued legitimate aim -- has emerged as the dominant methodology for constitutional adjudication worldwide, though its formulation, rigor, and relationship to other doctrinal frameworks vary profoundly across jurisdictions. South Korea's Constitutional Court (헌법재판소) adopted proportionality analysis as its primary framework for reviewing fundamental rights restrictions under Article 37(2) of the Constitution (헌법), which provides that rights may be restricted by Act "only when necessary for national security, the maintenance of law and order, or for public welfare" and that "even when such restriction is imposed, no essential aspect of the freedom or right shall be violated"; the Court applies a four-step test derived from German doctrine comprising legitimacy of purpose (목적의 정당성), suitability of means (수단의 적절성), necessity/least restrictive means (피해의 최소성), and proportionality in the narrow sense/balance of interests (법익의 균형성). The United States does not employ a unified proportionality framework, instead operating through a tiered system of scrutiny -- strict scrutiny, intermediate scrutiny, and rational basis review -- that assigns different levels of judicial review depending on the classification of the right or the type of government action, a structure that has been both praised for predictability and criticized for rigidity; however, proportionality-like reasoning appears in numerous doctrines including the undue burden test of Planned Parenthood v. Casey (1992), the excessive fines analysis under the Eighth Amendment, and the balancing tests in First and Fourth Amendment jurisprudence. Germany developed the three-step proportionality test (Verhältnismäßigkeitsprüfung) through the Federal Constitutional Court's jurisprudence, beginning with the Pharmacy Case (BVerfGE 7, 377, Apothekenurteil, 1958), establishing the framework that has been exported globally: the measure must pursue a legitimate aim (legitimer Zweck), must be suitable to achieve that aim (Geeignetheit), must be necessary in the sense that no less restrictive equally effective means exists (Erforderlichkeit), and must be proportionate in the narrow sense/reasonable (Angemessenheit/Verhältnismäßigkeit im engeren Sinne). The European Court of Human Rights applies proportionality analysis to assess whether interferences with qualified Convention rights (Articles 8-11 ECHR) are "necessary in a democratic society," employing a margin of appreciation doctrine that grants states varying degrees of deference depending on the right at issue, the nature of the interference, and the existence or absence of European consensus, as elaborated in cases from Handyside v. United Kingdom (1976) through the Grand Chamber's structured proportionality analysis in Animal Defenders International v. United Kingdom (2013).

## South Korea

### Constitutional Framework

**Article 37(2) Constitution:** "The freedoms and rights of citizens may be restricted by Act only when necessary for national security, the maintenance of law and order, or for public welfare. Even when such restriction is imposed, no essential aspect of the freedom or right shall be violated (국민의 자유와 권리는 국가안전보장·질서유지 또는 공공복리를 위하여 필요한 경우에 한하여 법률로써 제한할 수 있으며, 제한하는 경우에도 자유와 권리의 본질적인 내용을 침해할 수 없다)."

This provision establishes both the proportionality requirement ("only when necessary") and the absolute limit on restrictions ("essential aspect cannot be violated"), creating a dual protection framework.

### The Korean Four-Step Test

The Constitutional Court has developed a four-step proportionality analysis closely modeled on German doctrine:

**Step 1 -- Legitimacy of Purpose (목적의 정당성):** The legislative purpose must constitute a legitimate aim justifiable under the Constitution. The Court has interpreted the three enumerated grounds (national security, law and order, public welfare) broadly, rarely finding legislation to fail at this step.

**Step 2 -- Suitability of Means (수단의 적절성):** The legislative means must be appropriate to achieve the stated purpose. The Court applies a relatively lenient standard, requiring only that the means contribute to the purpose and not that it be the most effective means.

**Step 3 -- Least Restrictive Means (피해의 최소성):** This is typically the most demanding step. The Court asks whether the same purpose could be achieved through means that impose a lesser burden on the affected fundamental right. The Korean formulation is notably strict, often more rigorously applied than the German Erforderlichkeit test.

**Step 4 -- Balance of Interests (법익의 균형성):** The benefit achieved by the legislative measure must not be disproportionate to the harm inflicted on the fundamental right. This step involves a holistic weighing of the competing interests.

### Landmark Constitutional Court Decisions

**Constitutional Court Decision 89Hun-Ka95 (1990):** The Court's early application of proportionality struck down a National Security Act provision criminalizing praise or sympathy for anti-state organizations, holding that the provision was overbroad and failed the least restrictive means test.

**Constitutional Court Decision 2002Hun-Ma518 (2004):** The Court struck down the prohibition on door-to-door campaigning during elections, applying the four-step test and finding that a blanket prohibition failed the least restrictive means requirement when less restrictive alternatives (time/place/manner restrictions) could achieve the legislative purpose.

**Constitutional Court Decision 2009Hun-Ma256 (2012) (Internet Real-Name System):** The Court struck down the mandatory internet real-name verification system, holding that while the purpose of reducing online defamation was legitimate, the comprehensive restriction on anonymous expression failed the proportionality test because less restrictive means existed and the chilling effect on free expression outweighed the modest benefits in reducing defamation.

**Constitutional Court Decision 2017Hun-Ba127 (2019) (Adultery Decriminalization):** The Court declared unconstitutional the criminal punishment for adultery, applying proportionality analysis to find that the criminal sanction failed the least restrictive means test and disproportionately infringed upon sexual self-determination and privacy.

### Essential Content Guarantee (본질적 내용 침해 금지)

Article 37(2)'s prohibition on violation of the "essential aspect" of rights functions as an absolute limit beyond which no proportionality balancing can justify restriction. The Constitutional Court has interpreted this as an absolute minimum core that the legislature cannot transgress regardless of the weight of the competing public interest. In practice, however, the Court rarely relies on this provision independently of the proportionality analysis, as measures found to violate the essential content of a right would typically also fail proportionality.

## United States

### The Tiered Scrutiny System

Unlike the proportionality approach, the United States employs a categorical system of judicial review:

**Strict Scrutiny:** Applied when government action infringes a fundamental right (speech, religion, voting, interstate travel) or employs a suspect classification (race, national origin, alienage). The government must demonstrate that the measure serves a compelling governmental interest and is narrowly tailored to achieve that interest. Strict scrutiny is described as "strict in theory and fatal in fact," though the Court has occasionally upheld measures under this standard (Grutter v. Bollinger, 539 U.S. 306 (2003); Korematsu v. United States, 323 U.S. 214 (1944), now discredited).

**Intermediate Scrutiny:** Applied to quasi-suspect classifications (sex, legitimacy) and to content-neutral speech restrictions. The measure must serve an important governmental interest and be substantially related to achieving that interest. Applied in Craig v. Boren, 429 U.S. 190 (1976), and United States v. Virginia, 518 U.S. 515 (1996).

**Rational Basis Review:** The default standard for all other legislation. The measure need only be rationally related to a legitimate governmental interest. This highly deferential standard results in virtually automatic validation, with rare exceptions such as City of Cleburne v. Cleburne Living Center, 473 U.S. 432 (1985), and Romer v. Evans, 517 U.S. 620 (1996), where the Court applied a more searching rational basis review.

### Origins

**United States v. Carolene Products, 304 U.S. 144 (1938), Footnote 4:** Justice Stone's famous footnote suggested that legislation affecting fundamental rights, discrete and insular minorities, or the political process might warrant "more exacting judicial scrutiny," laying the foundation for the tiered system.

### Proportionality-Like Reasoning in U.S. Law

While the United States does not employ an explicit proportionality framework, proportionality-like reasoning appears in several doctrinal contexts:

**Undue Burden Test (Planned Parenthood v. Casey, 505 U.S. 833 (1992)):** The plurality opinion introduced the "undue burden" standard for abortion regulations, asking whether a regulation has the purpose or effect of placing a substantial obstacle in the path of a woman seeking an abortion. This test resembles proportionality analysis in its attention to the severity of the burden relative to the government's interest, though the Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization (2022) decision overruling Roe and Casey returned abortion regulation to rational basis review.

**Excessive Fines (United States v. Bajakajian, 524 U.S. 321 (1998)):** The Court held that a forfeiture is constitutionally excessive under the Eighth Amendment if it is "grossly disproportional to the gravity of a defendant's offense," a direct proportionality analysis.

**Pickering Balancing (Pickering v. Board of Education, 391 U.S. 563 (1968)):** Public employee speech cases require courts to balance the employee's free speech interest against the employer's interest in efficient government operations, a form of proportionality reasoning.

**Second Amendment (New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen, 597 U.S. 1 (2022)):** The Court rejected means-end scrutiny (and by extension proportionality analysis) for Second Amendment cases, instead requiring a historical tradition analysis, illustrating the U.S. resistance to proportionality as a general framework.

### Academic Debate

American constitutional scholars are deeply divided on proportionality. Proponents including Vicki Jackson and Jamal Greene argue that proportionality would provide a more transparent and flexible framework than tiered scrutiny, enabling courts to address the actual severity of rights infringements. Opponents including Bernhard Schlink (from the German perspective) and Jud Mathews argue that proportionality grants excessive judicial discretion and that the American categorical approach provides greater predictability and constrains judicial overreach.

## Germany

### Constitutional Framework

The German proportionality principle (Verhältnismäßigkeitsgrundsatz) is not explicitly stated in the Grundgesetz but is derived by the Federal Constitutional Court from the Rechtsstaatsprinzip (rule of law principle, Article 20(3) GG) and from the essence of fundamental rights themselves.

### The Three-Step Test (Verhältnismäßigkeitsprüfung)

**Step 1 -- Suitability (Geeignetheit):** The governmental measure must be capable of promoting the legitimate aim. The BVerfG applies a deferential standard, requiring only that the measure is not objectively unsuitable (nicht offensichtlich ungeeignet). This step rarely leads to invalidation.

**Step 2 -- Necessity (Erforderlichkeit):** Among equally effective alternative means of achieving the legitimate aim, the legislature must choose the means that least restricts the affected fundamental right. The BVerfG formulates this as asking whether an equally effective but less restrictive means exists (ein gleich wirksames, aber milderes Mittel). This is the most technically demanding step and frequently determines the outcome.

**Step 3 -- Proportionality in the Narrow Sense (Verhältnismäßigkeit im engeren Sinne / Angemessenheit):** Even if suitable and necessary, the measure must not impose a burden on the individual that is excessive relative to the benefit achieved for the public interest. This step involves a direct weighing (Abwägung) of the competing interests, taking into account the severity of the infringement, the weight of the public interest, and the probability of achieving the aim.

### Landmark Cases

**Apothekenurteil (BVerfGE 7, 377, 1958):** The foundational proportionality case. The Court struck down a Bavarian statute restricting the establishment of new pharmacies, holding that the restriction on occupational freedom (Article 12 GG) failed the proportionality test. The Court developed the Stufentheorie (three-step theory) for occupational freedom, distinguishing between regulations of how an occupation is practiced (lowest scrutiny), subjective admission requirements (intermediate), and objective admission requirements (highest scrutiny).

**Lüth (BVerfGE 7, 198, 1958):** While primarily a free expression case establishing the objective order of values (objektive Wertordnung), Lüth employed proportionality reasoning in assessing whether a civil court's injunction against a boycott call disproportionately restricted freedom of expression.

**Cannabis Decision (BVerfGE 90, 145, 1994):** The Court upheld the criminalization of cannabis possession but held that prosecution of minor possession for personal use would violate proportionality, requiring prosecutorial discretion (Einstellung) for small quantities. This decision illustrates proportionality's capacity to produce nuanced outcomes short of full invalidation.

**Kopftuch I (BVerfGE 108, 282, 2003) and Kopftuch II (BVerfGE 138, 296, 2015):** The headscarf decisions illustrate evolving proportionality analysis: Kopftuch I permitted Länder to ban teachers' headscarves through legislation; Kopftuch II held that a blanket ban without evidence of concrete disruption disproportionately restricted religious freedom (Article 4 GG).

### Wesensgehaltsgarantie (Essential Content Guarantee)

**Article 19(2) Grundgesetz:** "In no case may the essential content (Wesensgehalt) of a basic right be infringed." This provision establishes an absolute limit on rights restrictions, functioning as a complement to proportionality. The dominant interpretation views Article 19(2) as establishing an absolute minimum content of each right that cannot be overridden by any proportionality analysis, though in practice the BVerfG rarely relies on it independently.

### Übermaßverbot and Untermaßverbot

German proportionality doctrine encompasses two directions:
- **Übermaßverbot (prohibition of excess):** The state must not restrict rights more than necessary -- the classical proportionality analysis.
- **Untermaßverbot (prohibition of insufficient protection):** Where the state has a duty to protect fundamental rights, it must provide a minimum level of protection -- a proportionality analysis applied to state omissions. Developed in the Second Abortion Decision (BVerfGE 88, 203, 1993).

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Proportionality Framework

The ECHR's qualified rights (Articles 8-11) follow a three-part test:
1. **Prescribed by Law:** The interference must have a basis in domestic law meeting the requirements of accessibility and foreseeability.
2. **Legitimate Aim:** The interference must pursue one of the enumerated aims (e.g., national security, public safety, protection of health or morals, protection of rights of others).
3. **Necessary in a Democratic Society:** The interference must correspond to a "pressing social need" and be proportionate to the legitimate aim pursued.

### Margin of Appreciation

The margin of appreciation doctrine, first articulated in Handyside v. United Kingdom (Application No. 5493/72, 1976), grants member states varying degrees of deference in determining whether an interference is "necessary in a democratic society":

| Factor | Wider Margin | Narrower Margin |
|--------|-------------|-----------------|
| European consensus | No consensus among member states | Strong consensus exists |
| Nature of right | Peripheral aspects; moral/ethical questions | Core aspects of personal autonomy |
| Type of measure | Positive obligations; social/economic policy | Criminal sanctions; prior restraints |
| Area of policy | National security; morals | Political expression; discrimination |

**Handyside v. United Kingdom (1976):** The Court upheld the UK's seizure of a book on the grounds of protecting morals, according a wide margin of appreciation due to the absence of a common European standard on public morality.

**Sunday Times v. United Kingdom (Application No. 6538/74, 1979):** The Court applied stricter scrutiny to restrictions on press freedom regarding matters of public interest, finding that the injunction against publication was not "necessary in a democratic society."

**Animal Defenders International v. United Kingdom (Application No. 48876/08, Grand Chamber 2013):** The Court upheld the UK's blanket ban on political advertising on broadcast media, giving significant weight to the quality of the parliamentary process that produced the restriction, illustrating a more structured proportionality analysis.

### EU Law Proportionality

EU law applies proportionality in multiple contexts:

**Article 52(1) EU Charter:** "Any limitation on the exercise of the rights and freedoms recognised by this Charter must be provided for by law and respect the essence of those rights and freedoms. Subject to the principle of proportionality, limitations may be made only if they are necessary and genuinely meet objectives of general interest recognised by the Union or the need to protect the rights and freedoms of others."

**CJEU Proportionality in Free Movement:** The Court applies proportionality to assess member state restrictions on free movement (goods, services, persons, capital), requiring that restrictions be suitable, necessary, and proportionate. The Cassis de Dijon principle (Case 120/78, Rewe-Zentral, 1979) and its progeny establish a proportionality framework for non-discriminatory restrictions on trade.

**Schrems II (C-311/18, 2020):** The CJEU applied proportionality analysis to invalidate the EU-US Privacy Shield, finding that US surveillance programs constituted disproportionate interference with fundamental rights under Articles 7 and 8 of the EU Charter.

### Comparison of ECtHR and CJEU Proportionality

The ECtHR and CJEU apply proportionality differently:
- The ECtHR grants a margin of appreciation to member states; the CJEU generally does not defer to member states on EU law matters.
- The CJEU's proportionality review of member state measures restricting EU freedoms is typically more stringent than the ECtHR's review of Convention rights restrictions.
- The CJEU explicitly requires that restrictions respect the "essence" of Charter rights (Article 52(1)), mirroring the German Wesensgehaltsgarantie and the Korean essential content protection.

## Comparative Analysis

### Structural Differences

The most fundamental difference is between the continental proportionality model (Germany, South Korea, ECHR, EU) and the American tiered scrutiny system. The proportionality model applies a unified analytical framework to all rights restrictions, adjusting the intensity of review through the application of individual steps. The American model categorizes rights and classifications ex ante and applies different standards of review to each category, producing more predictable but less flexible outcomes.

### Balancing and Its Critics

Proportionality's balancing step (German Angemessenheit, Korean 법익의 균형성, ECHR "fair balance") is the most controversial element across all jurisdictions. Critics argue that balancing grants courts excessive discretion to weigh incommensurable values, while proponents contend that balancing makes explicit the trade-offs that all constitutional adjudication necessarily involves. The United States' general avoidance of explicit balancing (with notable exceptions) reflects a constitutional culture that prefers rules to standards and distrusts judicial discretion.

### Global Migration of Proportionality

German proportionality has been the most successfully exported legal doctrine of the post-war era. Beyond South Korea (which adopted it directly), proportionality analysis has been adopted by the supreme and constitutional courts of Canada (R. v. Oakes, 1986), Israel (United Mizrahi Bank v. Migdal, 1995), South Africa (S v. Makwanyane, 1995), and numerous other jurisdictions. The ECtHR's necessity analysis and the CJEU's proportionality review have further propagated the framework across Europe.

### Intensity of Review

Despite shared doctrinal structure, the intensity of proportionality review varies significantly. The Korean Constitutional Court's strict application of the least restrictive means requirement has led to numerous invalidations of legislation. The German BVerfG applies a more calibrated approach, varying the intensity of review depending on the severity of the interference and the complexity of the factual assessment. The ECtHR's margin of appreciation produces the most deferential review, particularly in areas where member states lack consensus.
