## Summary

Animal cruelty -- the intentional infliction of suffering, harm, or death upon animals -- has undergone a dramatic legal transformation across the globe over the past two decades, evolving from a minor regulatory infraction to a serious criminal offense in most developed jurisdictions. South Korea regulates animal cruelty primarily through the **Animal Protection Act** (동물보호법), which was substantially amended in 2024 with the passage of the landmark **Special Act on the End of Breeding, Slaughtering, and Distribution of Dogs for Food Purposes**, banning the dog meat trade by 2027 and imposing penalties of up to three years imprisonment or 30 million won. The United States addresses animal cruelty through a dual federal-state system: the federal **Preventing Animal Cruelty and Torture (PACT) Act of 2019** (18 U.S.C. section 48) criminalizes acts of animal crushing with penalties of up to seven years imprisonment, while all 50 states maintain their own anti-cruelty statutes, with 46 states providing felony-level penalties for aggravated cruelty. Germany enshrines animal welfare at the constitutional level through **Article 20a of the Basic Law** (Grundgesetz), which since 2002 has expressly mandated state protection of animals, and enforces criminal penalties of up to three years imprisonment under **Section 17 of the Animal Welfare Act** (Tierschutzgesetz), supplemented by the Civil Code's declaration that animals are not things (section 90a BGB). At the European level, **Article 13 of the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union (TFEU)**, established by the Treaty of Lisbon (2007), recognizes animals as sentient beings and requires the EU and Member States to pay full regard to animal welfare in formulating and implementing policy. The European Court of Human Rights has increasingly engaged with animal welfare as a legitimate public interest under the Convention, most notably in a 2024 unanimous judgment linking animal welfare to "public morality" under Article 9 ECHR in the context of ritual slaughter bans.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's animal cruelty laws are anchored by the **Animal Protection Act** (동물보호법, Act No. 14651), first enacted in 1991 and substantially revised multiple times, most recently in 2024. The Act establishes the principle that animals should be protected from abuse, neglect, and unnecessary suffering, and imposes affirmative duties of care on animal owners and keepers.

Key statutory instruments include:

- **Animal Protection Act** (동물보호법): The primary statute prohibiting cruelty to animals, establishing registration requirements for companion animals, regulating animal testing, and providing for the rescue and sheltering of abandoned animals.
- **Special Act on the End of Breeding, Slaughtering, and Distribution of Dogs for Food Purposes** (Act No. 20195, promulgated February 6, 2024): Bans the breeding, slaughtering, distributing, and selling of dogs for consumption, with a three-year phase-out period ending in February 2027.
- **Laboratory Animal Act** (실험동물에 관한 법률): Regulates the use of animals in scientific research and testing, requiring institutional animal ethics committees.
- **Civil Act Amendment (2021)**: Amended to declare that animals are not things, granting them a distinct legal status separate from mere property.

In 2021, South Korea moved to grant legal status to animals to tackle abuse, amending the Civil Act so that animals are no longer classified as objects or property. This was a significant symbolic and legal shift.

### Penalties

| Offense | Maximum Imprisonment | Maximum Fine |
|---------|---------------------|-------------|
| Killing an animal by cruel methods | 3 years | KRW 30 million (~USD 22,500) |
| Causing injury through abuse | 2 years | KRW 20 million (~USD 15,000) |
| Slaughtering a dog for food (from 2027) | 3 years | KRW 30 million (~USD 22,500) |
| Breeding dogs for food (from 2027) | 2 years | KRW 20 million (~USD 15,000) |
| Distributing/selling dog meat products (from 2027) | 2 years | KRW 20 million (~USD 15,000) |
| Abandonment of a companion animal | -- | KRW 3 million (~USD 2,250) |
| Failure to register companion animal | -- | KRW 1 million (~USD 750) |

### Landmark Cases and Developments

**Dog Meat Ban (2024):** On January 9, 2024, the National Assembly unanimously passed the Special Act banning the dog meat trade, making South Korea one of the last East Asian countries to outlaw the practice. The law provides a three-year transition period to allow approximately 1,100 remaining dog farms to close. By early 2025, 40% of the reported 1,537 dog farms had voluntarily closed. In June 2024, the Dog Meat Farmers Association filed a constitutional challenge and injunction against the ban at the Constitutional Court, with proceedings ongoing.

**Sentencing Guidelines Reform (2025):** The Korean Sentencing Commission drafted enhanced sentencing guidelines for animal abuse crimes, envisioning substantially tougher penalties and more consistent sentencing across courts. The guidelines address the previously wide disparities in sentencing outcomes for similar offenses.

**Expanded Animal Testing Oversight (2025-2026):** South Korea expanded ethical oversight requirements for animal testing in national research and development projects, bringing companies governed by the Commercial Act, small and medium-sized enterprises, and nonprofit organizations under regulatory oversight. These entities are now required to establish and operate animal ethics committees.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States addresses animal cruelty through a dual system of federal and state laws. At the federal level, the principal statutes are:

- **Preventing Animal Cruelty and Torture (PACT) Act** (18 U.S.C. section 48, signed November 25, 2019): Criminalizes intentional acts of animal crushing -- defined as conduct in which living non-human mammals, birds, reptiles, or amphibians are purposely crushed, burned, drowned, suffocated, impaled, or otherwise subjected to serious bodily injury. The PACT Act built upon the Animal Crush Video Prohibition Act of 2010, which itself was enacted after the Supreme Court struck down the original crush video statute in United States v. Stevens (2010).
- **Animal Welfare Act (AWA)** (7 U.S.C. sections 2131-2159): Regulates the treatment of animals in research, exhibition, transport, and by dealers. Enforced by the USDA's Animal and Plant Health Inspection Service (APHIS).
- **Animal Fighting Prohibition** (7 U.S.C. section 2156): Makes it a federal felony to sponsor, exhibit, buy, sell, transport, or deliver animals for participation in animal fighting ventures, punishable by up to 5 years imprisonment.
- **Twenty-Eight Hour Law** (49 U.S.C. section 80502): Requires that animals transported across state lines for slaughter be unloaded for rest, water, and feeding every 28 hours.
- **Humane Methods of Slaughter Act** (7 U.S.C. sections 1901-1907): Requires that livestock be rendered insensible to pain before slaughter.

At the state level, all 50 states have anti-cruelty statutes. As of 2025, 46 states include felony provisions for aggravated cruelty involving intentional torture, mutilation, or killing.

### Penalties

| Statute / Jurisdiction | Violation Type | Maximum Imprisonment | Maximum Fine |
|----------------------|---------------|---------------------|-------------|
| PACT Act (federal) | Animal crushing | 7 years | USD 250,000 |
| Animal Fighting (federal) | Sponsoring or participating | 5 years | USD 250,000 |
| California (Penal Code section 597) | Aggravated cruelty | 3 years (state prison) | USD 20,000 |
| New York (Agriculture and Markets Law section 353-a) | Aggravated cruelty (felony) | 2 years | USD 5,000 |
| Texas (Penal Code section 42.092) | Cruelty to non-livestock animals | 2 years (state jail felony) | USD 10,000 |
| Florida (section 828.12) | Aggravated animal cruelty (third-degree felony) | 5 years | USD 10,000 |

The PACT Act contains exemptions for conduct related to customary and normal veterinary, agricultural husbandry, or other animal management practices; slaughter for food; hunting, trapping, or fishing; medical or scientific research; actions necessary to protect the life or property of a person; and euthanasia performed by an animal control officer or veterinarian.

### Landmark Cases

**United States v. Stevens, 559 U.S. 460 (2010):** The Supreme Court struck down 18 U.S.C. section 48 (the original 1999 statute criminalizing depictions of animal cruelty) as an unconstitutional content-based restriction on speech under the First Amendment. Robert Stevens had been sentenced to 37 months for selling videotapes of dogfights. In an 8-1 decision authored by Chief Justice Roberts, the Court held the statute was substantially overbroad because it could apply to hunting videos, veterinary training, and other protected speech. Congress responded by enacting the more narrowly tailored Animal Crush Video Prohibition Act of 2010, which was later expanded into the PACT Act in 2019.

**State v. Nix, 334 Or. 34 (2002):** The Oregon Supreme Court upheld animal cruelty charges against a defendant who starved cattle, ruling that animal cruelty statutes do not violate equal protection or due process, and that the state has a legitimate interest in preventing unnecessary animal suffering regardless of whether the animals are property.

**People v. Garcia, 29 Cal. App. 5th Supp. 1 (2018):** A California appellate court upheld felony animal cruelty convictions, reinforcing the principle that intentional acts of cruelty to animals warrant felony-level prosecution and that the state's interest in animal welfare justifies serious criminal penalties.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany provides among the strongest legal protections for animals in the world, combining constitutional mandate, comprehensive statutory regulation, and criminal penalties.

- **Article 20a of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz):** Amended in 2002 to include the words "and the animals" (und die Tiere), this constitutional provision reads: "Mindful also of its responsibility towards future generations, the state shall protect the natural foundations of life and the animals by legislation and, in accordance with law and justice, by executive and judicial action." Germany was one of the first countries in the world to enshrine animal welfare in its constitution.
- **Section 90a of the Civil Code (BGB):** Declares that "animals are not things" (Tiere sind keine Sachen) and that they are protected by special statutes. Where no specific provision exists, the rules applicable to things apply correspondingly.
- **Animal Welfare Act (Tierschutzgesetz, TierSchG):** The primary statute, first enacted on July 24, 1972, and comprehensively revised multiple times. Section 1 states its purpose: "No one may cause an animal pain, suffering or harm without good reason." The Act regulates the keeping of animals (Section 2), killing of animals (Section 4), operations on animals (Section 5-6), animal experiments (Sections 7-9), and breeding (Section 11b).

### Criminal Penalties Under Section 17

Section 17 of the Tierschutzgesetz establishes criminal liability for animal cruelty:

| Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|
| Killing a vertebrate animal without reasonable cause | Up to 3 years imprisonment or criminal fine |
| Inflicting considerable pain or suffering on a vertebrate out of cruelty (Rohheit) | Up to 3 years imprisonment or criminal fine |
| Inflicting prolonged or repeated considerable pain or suffering on a vertebrate | Up to 3 years imprisonment or criminal fine |
| Administrative violations (Ordnungswidrigkeiten, Section 18) | Fine up to EUR 25,000 |

Beyond imprisonment and fines, courts may impose an **animal-keeping ban** (Tierhalteverbot) for one to five years, or indefinitely if there is a danger of repetition. This supplementary prohibition applies to keeping, trading, or all other professional handling of animals.

A distinctive feature of German law is criminal liability for **cruelty by omission**: individuals who fail to provide care for sick or injured animals, or who hit an animal with a vehicle and fail to render assistance, can be prosecuted for animal cruelty.

### Landmark Cases and Developments

**Constitutional Animal Welfare Amendment (2002):** The Bundestag amended Article 20a of the Basic Law to include animals alongside the environment as objects of state protection. This amendment had significant practical consequences: in subsequent cases, courts could no longer automatically subordinate animal welfare to competing constitutional rights such as freedom of religion (Article 4) or freedom of research (Article 5). The amendment was prompted in part by debates over ritual slaughter (Schachten), where animal welfare interests had previously been held to yield to religious freedom under the Federal Constitutional Court's 2002 ruling in the Schachten case (BVerfGE 104, 337).

**Ritual Slaughter Decision, BVerfGE 104, 337 (2002):** The Federal Constitutional Court ruled that a Muslim butcher could not be denied an exemption from stunning requirements for religious slaughter, as the prohibition violated his fundamental right to occupational freedom (Article 12 GG) read in conjunction with freedom of religion (Article 4 GG). This decision was a catalyst for the subsequent constitutional amendment elevating animal welfare.

**Puppy Mill Prosecutions:** German courts have increasingly prosecuted operators of large-scale commercial breeding facilities (so-called Welpenfabriken) for violations of Section 17 TierSchG, with convictions resulting in imprisonment, substantial fines, and permanent animal-keeping bans. These cases have highlighted enforcement challenges in detecting and investigating conditions at breeding operations.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Legal Framework

The European Union has developed a comprehensive body of animal welfare legislation since the 1970s, grounded in the recognition that animals are sentient beings.

#### Treaty Basis

**Article 13 of the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union (TFEU)**, introduced by the Treaty of Lisbon (2007), provides: "In formulating and implementing the Union's agriculture, fisheries, transport, internal market, research and technological development and space policies, the Union and the Member States shall, since animals are sentient beings, pay full regard to the welfare requirements of animals, while respecting the legislative or administrative provisions and customs of the Member States relating in particular to religious rites, cultural traditions and regional heritage."

#### Principal Legislation

- **Council Directive 98/58/EC** (20 July 1998): Establishes minimum standards for the protection of animals kept for farming purposes, requiring that owners and keepers take all reasonable steps to ensure the welfare of animals under their care.
- **Council Regulation (EC) No 1099/2009** (24 September 2009): Governs the protection of animals at the time of killing, requiring effective stunning before slaughter with specific exemptions for religious rites. Member States are required to establish penalties that are "effective, proportionate and dissuasive."
- **Regulation (EC) No 1/2005** (22 December 2004): Regulates the protection of animals during transport, setting maximum journey times, space allowances, and rest intervals.
- **Directive 2010/63/EU** (22 September 2010): Governs the protection of animals used for scientific purposes, implementing the "3Rs" principle (Replacement, Reduction, Refinement) and requiring prior authorization for animal experiments.
- **Council Directive 1999/74/EC**: Bans conventional battery cages for laying hens (fully implemented since 2012).
- **Council Directive 2008/120/EC**: Sets minimum standards for the protection of pigs.

#### Enforcement and Penalties

EU animal welfare regulations require Member States to establish national enforcement mechanisms and penalties, but do not prescribe specific penalty levels. Penalties vary substantially across Member States:

| Member State | Maximum Imprisonment | Maximum Fine |
|-------------|---------------------|-------------|
| United Kingdom (pre-Brexit, Animal Welfare Act 2006) | 5 years (as amended 2021) | Unlimited |
| France (Code penal, Art. 521-1) | 5 years | EUR 75,000 |
| Netherlands (Wet dieren) | 3 years | EUR 22,500 |
| Spain (Ley 7/2023) | 18-24 months | EUR 200,000 |
| Italy (Codice penale, Art. 544-bis/ter) | 2 years | EUR 30,000 |

### ECHR Jurisprudence on Animal Welfare

The European Convention on Human Rights contains no explicit animal welfare provision, but the European Court of Human Rights has addressed animal protection through the lens of existing Convention rights on multiple occasions.

#### Key Cases

**Cha'are Shalom Ve Tsedek v. France (Grand Chamber, 27 June 2000):** A Jewish liturgical association challenged France's refusal to grant it a separate license for ritual slaughter. The Grand Chamber held there was no violation of Article 9 (freedom of religion), reasoning that the applicant's members could obtain glatt kosher meat through alternative means. The Court recognized that the state retained a margin of appreciation to regulate slaughter in the interest of public health and animal welfare.

**Herrmann v. Germany (Grand Chamber, 26 June 2012):** The applicant objected on ethical grounds to being compelled to tolerate hunting on his land under German hunting law. The Grand Chamber found a violation of Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (protection of property), holding that the obligation to tolerate hunting on private land without the possibility of opting out for ethical objectors was disproportionate. This case indirectly advanced animal welfare by recognizing that ethical convictions regarding animals can ground Convention claims.

**Animal Defenders International v. United Kingdom (Grand Chamber, 22 April 2013):** The Grand Chamber upheld the UK's blanket ban on political advertising by bodies whose objects are "wholly or mainly of a political nature," including animal welfare organizations. While this case did not advance animal protection directly, it highlighted the tension between advocacy for animal welfare and restrictions on expression under Article 10.

**Ritual Slaughter Ban Cases (2024):** On February 13, 2024, the ECtHR delivered a landmark unanimous judgment upholding the Belgian regions of Flanders and Wallonia's bans on slaughter without prior stunning. The Court accepted for the first time that the protection of animal welfare can be linked to "public morality" as a legitimate aim under Article 9 section 2 ECHR. This marked a significant doctrinal development, as the Court effectively endorsed the proposition that societal moral concern for animal suffering constitutes a legitimate ground for restricting religious practices.

**CJEU -- Centraal Israëlitisch Consistorie van België and Others (Case C-336/19, 17 December 2020):** The Court of Justice of the European Union ruled that EU law permits Member States to require prior stunning of animals before ritual slaughter, provided that the restriction pursues the legitimate objective of animal welfare and does not go beyond what is necessary. The Court found that Regulation 1099/2009 does not preclude national legislation prohibiting slaughter without stunning, including for religious rites, as long as it respects the essence of Article 10 of the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights.

## Comparative Analysis

### Constitutional and Legal Status of Animals

The most fundamental distinction among these jurisdictions lies in the constitutional and legal recognition afforded to animals. Germany stands alone in providing explicit constitutional protection for animals (Article 20a GG), creating a constitutional value that can be balanced against other fundamental rights. The EU recognizes animal sentience at the treaty level (Article 13 TFEU) but frames this as a policy consideration rather than a justiciable right. South Korea has amended its Civil Act to declare that animals are not things, a symbolic but legally meaningful shift. The United States provides no federal constitutional protection for animals, and animals remain classified as property under most state laws.

### Penalty Severity

Penalties for animal cruelty vary significantly. The United States imposes the harshest maximum penalties at the federal level (seven years under the PACT Act), while South Korea and Germany both cap imprisonment at three years. However, comparing raw penalty maxima can be misleading: enforcement rates, prosecution priorities, and judicial sentencing practices vary widely. Germany compensates for its relatively lower statutory maxima with robust supplementary measures, particularly animal-keeping bans that can be imposed indefinitely.

### Scope of Protection

Germany's Tierschutzgesetz applies to all animals, including invertebrates, making it among the broadest in scope. The US PACT Act applies only to non-human mammals, birds, reptiles, and amphibians. South Korea's Animal Protection Act primarily protects vertebrates, with enhanced protections for companion animals. EU legislation varies by instrument: Directive 98/58/EC covers farmed animals, Regulation 1099/2009 covers animals at slaughter, and Directive 2010/63/EU covers animals used in research.

### Enforcement Models

South Korea and Germany both employ a predominantly administrative enforcement model, where violations are initially addressed by local authorities through warnings, fines, and confiscation orders, with criminal prosecution reserved for serious cases. The United States relies more heavily on law enforcement and prosecutorial action, with animal control officers, state police, and federal agents (in cases involving interstate commerce) empowered to investigate and charge offenders. The EU establishes minimum standards that Member States must enforce through their own institutional frameworks, creating significant disparities in enforcement intensity across the Union.

### The Ritual Slaughter Question

The most legally complex intersection in animal cruelty law involves the conflict between animal welfare requirements and religious freedom. Germany's Federal Constitutional Court initially prioritized religious freedom in its 2002 Schachten decision, but the subsequent constitutional amendment elevating animal welfare has shifted the balance. The CJEU's 2020 ruling in Centraal Israëlitisch Consistorie confirmed that Member States may require pre-slaughter stunning even for religious slaughter. The ECtHR's 2024 ruling linking animal welfare to public morality under Article 9 section 2 represents the most significant doctrinal development, suggesting that the European consensus on animal protection is now strong enough to justify restrictions on religious practices.

### Link Between Animal Cruelty and Other Violence

All four jurisdictions have increasingly recognized the empirical link between animal cruelty and interpersonal violence. The United States has been at the forefront of this development: the FBI began tracking animal cruelty as a distinct crime category in the National Incident-Based Reporting System (NIBRS) in 2016. Many US states now require veterinarians to report suspected animal abuse, and cross-reporting laws mandate information sharing between animal welfare and child welfare agencies. South Korea's 2025 sentencing guidelines reform was partly motivated by research linking animal abuse to broader patterns of domestic violence.
