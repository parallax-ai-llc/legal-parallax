## Summary

Cyberstalking -- the use of electronic communications or digital platforms to harass, threaten, monitor, or intimidate another person -- has emerged as one of the most significant challenges for criminal law in the digital age. Legal frameworks must address the definition of prohibited conduct in an online context, the distinction between legitimate expression and criminal harassment, the adequacy of traditional stalking laws for digital behavior, jurisdictional challenges posed by borderless communications, and the availability of protective orders for online conduct. South Korea enacted comprehensive cyberstalking legislation through the Stalking Punishment Act (스토킹범죄의 처벌 등에 관한 법률, effective April 20, 2021), supplemented by the Act on the Prevention of Stalking and Protection of Victims (스토킹방지 및 피해자보호 등에 관한 법률, 2021), which explicitly cover digital surveillance, repeated unwanted electronic communications, and unauthorized monitoring through GPS devices or spyware, though enforcement has been criticized following several high-profile cases where victims were killed despite having reported stalking to police. The United States addresses cyberstalking through the federal Interstate Stalking Act (18 U.S.C. Section 2261A), the Violence Against Women Act (VAWA) amendments, and 50 state criminal stalking statutes that have been progressively expanded to cover electronic conduct, while the Supreme Court's decision in Counterman v. Colorado (600 U.S. 66, 2023) established that the First Amendment requires at least a recklessness standard for true threats prosecutions. Germany criminalizes cyberstalking under Section 238 StGB (Nachstellung), significantly reformed in 2017 to lower the threshold from "severe impairment of the victim's life" to conduct "suited to" severely impairing the victim's life, while also addressing digital forms of harassment. The ECHR framework provides protection through Article 3 (prohibition of degrading treatment), Article 8 (right to respect for private life), and the Istanbul Convention (Council of Europe Convention on Preventing and Combating Violence Against Women, 2011), which requires states to criminalize stalking including through electronic means.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's comprehensive anti-stalking legislation, enacted in 2021, was the product of decades of advocacy following repeated high-profile cases where existing laws failed to protect victims from persistent harassment.

### Stalking Punishment Act (스토킹범죄의 처벌 등에 관한 법률, 2021)

Enacted on April 20, 2021, the Act defines and criminalizes stalking comprehensively:

**Article 2 (Definitions):**
- **Stalking conduct (스토킹행위):** Any of the following acts directed at a specific person, against the person's will, causing anxiety or fear:
  1. Approaching, following, or blocking the victim's path.
  2. Monitoring, observing, or surveilling the victim's residence, workplace, school, or other frequently visited locations.
  3. Making telephone calls, sending texts, emails, or other communications.
  4. Delivering or placing objects at the victim's location.
  5. Utilizing the information and communications network (internet, social media, messaging apps) to transmit text, audio, images, or video.
  6. **Installing or using GPS tracking devices, spyware, or monitoring software** on the victim's person, vehicle, or digital devices.

- **Stalking crime (스토킹범죄):** Repeatedly engaging in stalking conduct.

**Article 18 (Penalties):**

| Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|
| Basic stalking crime | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine up to KRW 30 million |
| Stalking with weapon or by multiple perpetrators | Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine up to KRW 50 million |
| Stalking violating protective order | Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine up to KRW 50 million |
| Stalking causing injury | Up to 7 years imprisonment |
| Stalking causing death | Up to 10 years imprisonment |

### Emergency and Protective Measures

The Act establishes a tiered protection system:

1. **Emergency Measures (긴급응급조치, Article 3):** Police officers who receive a stalking report may immediately impose: separation of the perpetrator from the victim (100m minimum distance); prohibition on approaching the victim's residence, workplace, or school; prohibition on contacting the victim via telecommunications.

2. **Provisional Measures (잠정조치, Article 4):** A prosecutor may request, and a court may order, provisional measures for up to two months (renewable once for two months), including: prohibition on approaching within a specified distance; prohibition on all forms of contact; electronic monitoring (GPS ankle bracelet).

3. **Protective Orders (피해자보호명령, Article 9):** The court may issue longer-term protective orders during or after criminal proceedings, lasting up to six months (renewable).

### Enforcement Challenges

Despite the comprehensive legislative framework, enforcement has faced significant criticism:

- **Shin Dang-dong Murder (September 2022):** A Seoul Metro employee was stabbed to death at Sindang Station by a former colleague who had stalked her for years. The victim had filed multiple police reports and obtained protective measures, but the perpetrator violated them without consequence. The case prompted national outrage and led to the 2023 amendments strengthening penalty provisions.
- **Police Response Failures:** A 2023 National Human Rights Commission survey found that 42% of stalking victims reported that police had advised them to "resolve it privately" or declined to take reports.
- **Amendment of 2023:** Following the Shin Dang-dong case, the National Assembly amended the Act to: increase penalties for protective order violations; make stalking a "non-compoundable" offense (preventing withdrawal of complaints); and mandate police acceptance and investigation of all stalking reports.

### Information and Communications Network Act

The **Act on Promotion of Information and Communications Network Utilization and Information Protection** (정보통신망 이용촉진 및 정보보호 등에 관한 법률) provides additional protections:

- **Article 44-7:** Prohibits the distribution of content that "infringes on others' rights" through information and communications networks, including repeated harassing communications.
- **Article 74:** Criminal penalties for transmission of obscene material, defamatory content, or threatening communications via electronic means.

Platform operators have duties to remove reported harmful content and may be subject to administrative sanctions for failure to act.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States addresses cyberstalking through a combination of federal and state statutes, with the First Amendment imposing significant constraints on the scope of criminalization.

### Federal Statutes

**Interstate Stalking Act (18 U.S.C. Section 2261A):** As amended by VAWA reauthorizations, criminalizes:

- Using any interactive computer service or electronic communication service to engage in a **course of conduct** that causes, attempts to cause, or would be reasonably expected to cause **substantial emotional distress** to a person in another state or tribal jurisdiction, or that places the person in **reasonable fear of death or serious bodily injury** (Section 2261A(2)).
- Penalties: up to 5 years imprisonment (basic offense); up to 10 years if serious bodily injury results; up to life imprisonment if death results.

**47 U.S.C. Section 223 (Telecommunications Harassment):** Criminalizes the use of telecommunications devices to annoy, abuse, threaten, or harass any specific person, with penalties of up to 2 years imprisonment.

### State Statutes

All 50 states and the District of Columbia have criminal stalking statutes, and the vast majority have been amended to explicitly address electronic/cyber conduct:

- **California (Cal. Penal Code Section 646.9):** Criminalizes willfully, maliciously, and repeatedly following or willfully and maliciously harassing another person, including through electronic means, combined with a credible threat of violence. Cyberstalking is a wobbler (misdemeanor or felony); felony penalties up to 5 years.
- **Texas (Penal Code Section 42.072):** Criminalizes engaging in a course of conduct directed at a specific person that would cause a reasonable person to fear for safety or feel harassed, including conduct via electronic communications. Third-degree felony (2-10 years).
- **New York (Penal Law Section 120.45-120.60):** Defines stalking in four degrees, with first-degree stalking (involving physical injury) a Class D felony (up to 7 years). Electronic communications are explicitly covered.

### Counterman v. Colorado (600 U.S. 66, 2023)

In this landmark First Amendment case, the Supreme Court addressed the mental state required for "true threats" prosecutions:

- Billy Raymond Counterman sent hundreds of Facebook messages to a local musician over two years, including messages that she interpreted as threatening (e.g., "Was prior arrangement, who asked you out... Staying in prior arrangement, you're not being good for me").
- The Colorado Court of Appeals applied an **objective reasonable person** standard -- whether a reasonable person would view the statements as threats.
- The Supreme Court reversed (7-2), holding that the First Amendment requires the prosecution to prove at minimum that the defendant acted with **recklessness** -- that the defendant "consciously disregarded a substantial risk that his communications would be viewed as threatening violence." The Court rejected both the purely objective standard and the requirement of specific intent to threaten.

This decision has significant implications for cyberstalking prosecutions, requiring prosecutors to prove the defendant was at least aware of the risk that their conduct would be perceived as threatening.

### Protective Orders

All states provide mechanisms for protective orders (also called restraining orders or orders of protection) that can address cyberstalking:

- **Emergency/Temporary Orders:** Issued ex parte, typically lasting 14-21 days, prohibiting contact including electronic contact.
- **Permanent Orders:** Issued after a hearing, typically lasting 1-5 years (renewable), and may include: prohibition on all forms of contact (phone, text, email, social media); prohibition on monitoring through electronic devices; requirement to remove or disable GPS trackers; prohibition on posting about the victim on social media; mandatory distance from the victim's home, workplace, and school.

Violation of a protective order is a criminal offense in all states, typically a misdemeanor for first violation and a felony for subsequent violations.

### Section 230 and Platform Liability

**Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act (47 U.S.C. Section 230)** provides broad immunity to online platforms for third-party content, limiting the ability to hold platforms liable for cyberstalking occurring on their services. However:

- Section 230(e)(1) excludes federal criminal law from immunity, meaning platforms can face criminal liability for knowingly facilitating cyberstalking.
- Various legislative proposals (EARN IT Act, SAFE TECH Act) have sought to narrow Section 230 immunity for platforms that fail to address harassment and stalking.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany criminalized stalking through the introduction of **Section 238 StGB (Nachstellung)** in 2007, subsequently reformed in 2017 and 2021 to address digital stalking and lower evidentiary thresholds.

### Section 238 StGB (Nachstellung)

**Section 238(1) -- Basic Offense:** Whoever unlawfully pursues another person by persistently:

1. Seeking the person's physical proximity,
2. Attempting to establish contact via telecommunications, electronic means, or third parties,
3. Misusing the person's personal data to order goods or services or causing third parties to make contact,
4. Threatening the person or a person close to them with bodily harm, damage to health, restriction of liberty, or death,
5. Engaging in any other comparable act,

and thereby **is suited to severely impairing the victim's life** (geeignet ist, deren Lebensgestaltung schwerwiegend zu beeintraechtigen), is punishable by imprisonment of up to three years or a fine.

**The 2017 Reform (Gesetz zur Verbesserung des Schutzes gegen Nachstellungen):** The critical reform changed the threshold from requiring that the victim's life was **actually** severely impaired (requiring proof of psychological harm, relocation, job loss, etc.) to conduct that was merely **suited to** (geeignet) causing such impairment. This eliminated the requirement that the victim prove concrete life changes and shifted the standard to an objective assessment of the conduct's severity.

**Section 238(2) -- Aggravated Offense:** If the perpetrator places the victim or a person close to the victim in danger of death or serious health injury, the penalty is imprisonment of three months to five years.

**Section 238(3) -- Causing Death:** If the perpetrator causes the death of the victim or a person close to the victim, the penalty is imprisonment of one to ten years.

### Digital Stalking Conduct

Section 238(1) expressly encompasses digital conduct through several provisions:

- **"Telecommunications" (Telekommunikation):** Covers phone calls, SMS, WhatsApp/Telegram messages, email, and all internet-based communication.
- **"Electronic means":** Covers social media contact, creation of fake profiles, doxxing (publishing the victim's personal information), image-based abuse (sharing intimate images), and monitoring via spyware or tracking applications.
- **"Misusing personal data" (Section 238(1)(3)):** Specifically addresses "identity stalking" -- using the victim's data to order goods, sign up for services, or create false social media profiles in the victim's name.
- **"Comparable acts" (Section 238(1)(5)):** The catch-all provision allows prosecution of novel forms of digital harassment not explicitly listed.

### Protection Against Violence Act (Gewaltschutzgesetz, GewSchG, 2002)

The **GewSchG** provides civil protective orders for stalking victims:

- **Section 1 GewSchG:** Courts may issue orders prohibiting the perpetrator from: entering the victim's residence; approaching within a specified distance of the victim, their residence, workplace, or school; making contact via telephone, electronic means, or letter; arranging contact through third parties.
- **Section 4 GewSchG:** Violation of a GewSchG protective order is a criminal offense punishable by up to two years imprisonment or a fine.
- **Expedited Proceedings:** Protective orders may be issued through expedited proceedings (einstweiliges Verfuegungsverfahren) within days, with the possibility of ex parte emergency orders in urgent cases.

### Network Enforcement Act (Netzwerkdurchsetzungsgesetz, NetzDG, 2017)

The **NetzDG** imposes duties on social media platforms with more than 2 million registered users in Germany to:

- Maintain effective complaint management procedures for illegal content, including content constituting Nachstellung.
- Remove "manifestly unlawful" content within 24 hours of notification.
- Remove other unlawful content within seven days after notification.
- Publish transparency reports on content removal.

Violations are punishable by fines of up to EUR 50 million. The NetzDG has been partially superseded by the **EU Digital Services Act (DSA)** for very large online platforms.

## ECHR & EU

### European Convention on Human Rights

**Article 3 (Prohibition of Torture and Degrading Treatment):**

Severe cyberstalking may engage Article 3 obligations. In **Volodina v. Russia (Application No. 41261/17, July 9, 2019)**, the Court found that Russia's failure to effectively investigate and prosecute domestic violence and stalking (including cyber surveillance using GPS tracking) violated Article 3. The Court held that the pattern of harassment and stalking constituted degrading treatment, and that the state's systemic failure to protect the victim breached its positive obligations.

**Article 8 (Right to Respect for Private Life):**

**K.U. v. Finland (Application No. 2872/02, December 2, 2008):** A 12-year-old boy's photograph and personal details were posted on an internet dating site by an unknown person, soliciting sexual contact. The Court found a violation of Article 8, holding that Finland failed to provide adequate legal mechanisms to identify the perpetrator and protect the victim, as the internet service provider refused to disclose the poster's identity and Finnish law provided no compulsory disclosure mechanism.

**Buturugă v. Romania (Application No. 56867/15, February 11, 2020):** In the first ECHR case specifically addressing cyber violence, the Court found that Romania violated Article 8 by failing to adequately investigate a husband's unauthorized access to his wife's email and social media accounts. The Court recognized that digital surveillance constitutes a form of violence requiring effective criminal law protection.

### Istanbul Convention (CETS No. 210, 2011)

**Article 34 (Stalking):** "Parties shall take the necessary legislative or other measures to ensure that the intentional conduct of repeatedly engaging in threatening conduct directed at another person, causing her or him to fear for her or his safety, is criminalised." The Explanatory Report clarifies that stalking includes "following a person, engaging in unwanted communication with a person through any available means of communication, including modern communication tools" and "monitoring a person's activities by misusing their personal data."

**Article 33 (Psychological Violence):** Requires criminalization of conduct that "seriously impairs a person's psychological integrity through coercion or threats."

**Article 53 (Restraining or Protection Orders):** Requires states to ensure that "appropriate restraining or protection orders are available to victims of all forms of violence," including stalking, and that such orders be available with "immediate effect" and be "independent of, or in addition to, other legal proceedings."

As of 2025, the Istanbul Convention has been ratified by 38 states and signed by a further 7. Notably, Turkey withdrew from the Convention in 2021, and several states (Bulgaria, Czech Republic, Hungary, Latvia, Lithuania, Slovakia) have signed but not ratified.

### EU Digital Services Act (Regulation 2022/2065)

The **DSA**, fully applicable since February 17, 2024, imposes obligations on online platforms relevant to cyberstalking:

- **Article 16:** Users must be able to notify platforms of illegal content, including cyberstalking content.
- **Article 20:** Platforms must provide reasons for content moderation decisions and offer internal complaint mechanisms.
- **Article 22:** Trusted flaggers (including anti-violence organizations) receive prioritized notice processing.
- **Article 34-35:** Very large online platforms (VLOPs) must assess and mitigate systemic risks, including risks related to gender-based violence and harassment.

### EU Victims' Rights Directive (2012/29/EU)

The Directive establishes minimum standards for the rights, support, and protection of victims of crime, including cyberstalking victims:

- **Article 22:** Individual assessment to identify specific protection needs, with recognition that victims of stalking and gender-based violence have heightened needs.
- **Article 23-24:** Right to protection measures during criminal proceedings, including avoidance of contact with the offender and the use of communication technology for testimony.

## Comparative Analysis

### Definitional Approaches

The jurisdictions differ in how broadly they define cyberstalking:

- **South Korea** provides the most specific enumeration, explicitly listing GPS tracking, spyware installation, and network-based contact as prohibited acts.
- **Germany** uses a combination of specific categories and a catch-all "comparable acts" provision, providing both legal certainty and flexibility.
- **The United States** varies by state but generally requires a "course of conduct" causing fear or distress, with the constitutional overlay of Counterman v. Colorado requiring proof of at least recklessness.
- **The ECHR/Istanbul Convention** establishes a broad framework of "threatening conduct directed at another person" that encompasses all digital forms.

### Enforcement Gaps

All jurisdictions demonstrate a significant gap between legal protections and effective enforcement:

- South Korea's Shin Dang-dong case illustrates how protective orders are ineffective without proactive police enforcement.
- Germany's 5% conviction rate for reported stalking cases highlights evidentiary challenges.
- The United States' First Amendment constraints (Counterman) create additional prosecutorial hurdles.
- The ECHR's Volodina and Buturugă cases demonstrate that state inaction itself constitutes a Convention violation.

### Platform Accountability

Approaches to platform responsibility diverge significantly:

- Germany's NetzDG and the EU's DSA impose affirmative content removal obligations on platforms.
- The United States' Section 230 provides broad platform immunity, with limited carve-outs for federal criminal law.
- South Korea's Information and Communications Network Act requires platform cooperation but enforcement has been inconsistent.

### Protective Orders

Protective order frameworks show convergence in the types of restrictions available (contact bans, distance requirements, electronic monitoring) but diverge in accessibility and enforcement:

- Germany's GewSchG provides relatively fast and accessible civil protection orders.
- South Korea's tiered system (emergency, provisional, protective orders) provides escalating protections.
- U.S. protective orders are widely available but enforcement varies dramatically by jurisdiction.
