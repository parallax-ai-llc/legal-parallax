## Summary

Sedition — the incitement of resistance to or insurrection against the lawful authority of the state — occupies a uniquely contested space in criminal law, sitting at the intersection of state security and freedom of expression. Unlike treason, which requires overt acts against the state, sedition traditionally encompasses speech, writing, and organizational activities that fall short of direct violence but are deemed to threaten the stability of the political order. Each jurisdiction examined here approaches sedition through a distinct legal lens shaped by its particular historical experience. South Korea criminalizes seditious expression primarily through the National Security Act (국가보안법), a Cold War-era statute enacted in 1948 that prohibits praising, encouraging, or propagandizing for "anti-state organizations," while the Criminal Act (형법) addresses insurrection and its instigation under Articles 87-90. The United States has a complex sedition history stretching from the Alien and Sedition Acts of 1798 through the Smith Act of 1940, with the modern federal framework centered on 18 U.S.C. Section 2384 (seditious conspiracy), which requires proof of a conspiracy to use force against the government — a statute dramatically revived in the January 6, 2021 Capitol attack prosecutions. Germany does not use the term "sedition" (Aufwiegelung) in its modern criminal code but addresses the concept through a constellation of offenses including high treason preparation (Section 83 StGB), incitement to hatred (Volksverhetzung, Section 130 StGB), and preparation of serious violent offences endangering the state (Section 89a StGB), reflecting lessons drawn from the Weimar Republic's collapse and the Nazi era. The European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR) does not define sedition but profoundly constrains how member states may prosecute seditious expression, requiring under Article 10 that any restriction on freedom of expression be prescribed by law, pursue a legitimate aim, and be necessary in a democratic society — standards the European Court of Human Rights has applied to invalidate convictions in numerous cases, particularly those originating in Turkey.

## South Korea

### Statutory Framework

South Korea addresses sedition and sedition-adjacent offenses through two principal legislative instruments: the Criminal Act (형법) and the National Security Act (국가보안법). The two statutes overlap significantly, but the National Security Act imposes broader liability by criminalizing expression and association that need not involve any use or advocacy of force.

**Criminal Act — Insurrection Offenses (Articles 87-90):**

The Criminal Act criminalizes insurrection and its preparatory stages. Article 87 defines insurrection as collective violence aimed at "usurping the national territory or subverting the Constitution." The offense structure distinguishes between ringleaders, active participants, and followers:

| Article | Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Art. 87 | Insurrection — ringleader | Death, life imprisonment with labor, or life imprisonment without labor |
| Art. 87 | Participation in essential tasks (commanding, plotting) | Death, life imprisonment, or minimum 5 years |
| Art. 87 | Persons who merely follow or join in violence | Up to 5 years imprisonment |
| Art. 88 | Conspiracy or instigation of insurrection not followed by the act | Minimum 3 years (conspirators); minimum 5 years (instigators) |
| Art. 89 | Acts auxiliary to insurrection (disrupting transport, seizing weapons) | Minimum 1 year imprisonment |
| Art. 90 | Preparatory conspiracy for insurrection | Minimum 3 years imprisonment |

Article 88 is the provision most analogous to sedition in the common law tradition, as it criminalizes the instigation of insurrection — that is, speech or conduct aimed at provoking collective violence against the constitutional order — even where no insurrection materializes.

**National Security Act — Anti-State Expression (Articles 7-8):**

The National Security Act extends criminal liability far beyond the Criminal Act by targeting expression sympathetic to North Korea or other "anti-state organizations." Article 7 is the most frequently invoked and controversial provision:

| Article | Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Art. 7(1) | Praising, encouraging, propagandizing for, or sympathizing with anti-state organizations or their members, with knowledge that such acts may endanger state security | Up to 7 years imprisonment |
| Art. 7(3) | Forming or joining an organization for the purpose of committing acts under Art. 7(1) | Minimum 1 year imprisonment |
| Art. 7(5) | Manufacturing, importing, reproducing, possessing, transporting, distributing, selling, or acquiring documents, drawings, or other materials that benefit anti-state organizations | Up to 7 years imprisonment |

### Criticism and International Response

The National Security Act has been the subject of persistent condemnation from international human rights bodies. The UN Human Rights Committee, Amnesty International, Human Rights Watch, and ARTICLE 19 have all called for the repeal or fundamental reform of Article 7, arguing that it criminalizes peaceful expression, violates Article 19 of the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR), and has a chilling effect on academic research, journalism, and political debate regarding North Korea.

According to the Supreme Prosecutor's Office, prosecutions under the NSA surged from 46 cases in 2008 to 129 cases in 2013 under the Park Geun-hye administration. Notable prosecutions include:

- **Park Jung-geun (2012):** Indicted under Article 7 for reposting altered North Korean propaganda images on social media. Park described the posts as satirical. He received a ten-month suspended sentence.
- **Lee Seok-ki (2013):** National Assembly member convicted under the NSA for conspiring to establish a pro-North Korean underground revolutionary organization. Sentenced to 12 years, later reduced to 9 years on appeal.

### The Yoon Suk Yeol Insurrection Case (2024-2026)

On December 3, 2024, President Yoon Suk Yeol declared emergency martial law, claiming the presence of "anti-state forces" sympathetic to North Korea within the opposition. The National Assembly voted unanimously (190-0) to lift martial law within hours. On February 19, 2026, the Seoul Central District Court convicted Yoon of insurrection under Article 87 and sentenced him to life imprisonment. Former Prime Minister Han Duck-soo received 23 years, and former Defense Minister Kim Yong-hyun received 30 years. The case represents the most significant application of Korea's insurrection laws since the 1996 convictions of former Presidents Chun Doo-hwan and Roh Tae-woo.

## United States

### Historical Development

The United States has a long and contentious history with sedition legislation, punctuated by periods in which the government used sedition laws to suppress political dissent during times of perceived national crisis.

**Alien and Sedition Acts (1798):** The Sedition Act of 1798, enacted by the Federalist-controlled Congress during the Quasi-War with France, made it a crime to publish "false, scandalous, and malicious writing" against the government or its officials. The Act was used to prosecute newspaper editors critical of President John Adams. It expired in 1801 and was never renewed, though it cast a long shadow over American free speech jurisprudence. Thomas Jefferson pardoned all those convicted under the Act upon taking office.

**Espionage Act of 1917 and Sedition Act of 1918:** The Sedition Act of 1918 amended the Espionage Act to criminalize "disloyal, profane, scurrilous, or abusive language" about the government, Constitution, military, or flag during wartime. More than 2,000 people were prosecuted, including Socialist Party leader Eugene V. Debs, who was sentenced to ten years for an anti-war speech. The Supreme Court upheld the conviction in Debs v. United States, 249 U.S. 211 (1919). The Sedition Act was repealed in 1920.

**Smith Act (1940):** The Alien Registration Act of 1940, known as the Smith Act, made it a crime to knowingly advocate, abet, or teach the desirability of overthrowing the government by force or violence, or to organize or be a member of any group that does so. Penalty: up to 10 years imprisonment and a $10,000 fine. The Act was used extensively during the early Cold War to prosecute leaders of the Communist Party USA. In Dennis v. United States, 341 U.S. 494 (1951), the Supreme Court upheld the convictions, but in Yates v. United States, 354 U.S. 298 (1957), the Court significantly narrowed the Act by distinguishing between abstract advocacy of violent overthrow (protected) and advocacy directed at producing imminent action (prosecutable).

### Modern Federal Statute: 18 U.S.C. Section 2384

The current seditious conspiracy statute provides:

> "If two or more persons in any State or Territory, or in any place subject to the jurisdiction of the United States, conspire to overthrow, put down, or to destroy by force the Government of the United States, or to levy war against them, or to oppose by force the authority thereof, or by force to prevent, hinder, or delay the execution of any law of the United States, or by force to seize, take, or possess any property of the United States contrary to the authority thereof, they shall each be fined under this title or imprisoned not more than twenty years, or both."

**Key elements:**

1. **Agreement:** Two or more persons must enter into a conspiracy.
2. **Intent:** The conspiracy must aim to overthrow or oppose the government by force.
3. **Force:** The use of force must be an object of the conspiracy, though actual violence need not occur.

**Related statutes:**

| Statute | Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|---------|
| 18 U.S.C. § 2383 | Rebellion or insurrection | Up to 10 years; disqualification from office |
| 18 U.S.C. § 2384 | Seditious conspiracy | Up to 20 years |
| 18 U.S.C. § 2385 | Advocating overthrow of the government (Smith Act) | Up to 20 years; 5-year disqualification from federal employment |

### Landmark Cases

**Dennis v. United States, 341 U.S. 494 (1951):** Eleven leaders of the Communist Party USA were convicted under the Smith Act for conspiring to advocate the overthrow of the U.S. government. The Supreme Court upheld the convictions, applying a modified "clear and present danger" test that weighed the gravity of the threatened evil against its improbability.

**Yates v. United States, 354 U.S. 298 (1957):** The Supreme Court reversed Smith Act convictions of fourteen Communist Party members, holding that the Act required advocacy of concrete action toward violent overthrow, not merely abstract doctrinal advocacy. This decision effectively ended mass prosecutions under the Smith Act.

**Brandenburg v. Ohio, 395 U.S. 449 (1969):** Though not a sedition prosecution, this case established the controlling First Amendment test for incitement: speech may only be punished when it is "directed to inciting or producing imminent lawless action and is likely to incite or produce such action." This test significantly constrains sedition prosecutions by requiring imminence and likelihood.

**United States v. Omar Abdel-Rahman (1995):** Sheikh Omar Abdel-Rahman and nine co-defendants were convicted of seditious conspiracy for plotting to bomb New York City landmarks following the 1993 World Trade Center bombing. Rahman was sentenced to life in prison.

**January 6, 2021 — Capitol Attack Prosecutions (2022-2024):**

The January 6 attack on the United States Capitol produced the most significant wave of seditious conspiracy prosecutions in modern American history:

| Defendant | Organization | Sentence | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|----------|-------|
| Stewart Rhodes | Oath Keepers founder | 18 years | First seditious conspiracy conviction related to Jan. 6 |
| Kelly Meggs | Oath Keepers leader | 12 years | Florida chapter leader |
| Enrique Tarrio | Proud Boys chairman | 22 years | Longest sentence of any Jan. 6 defendant |
| Joseph Biggs | Proud Boys | 17 years | Organizer |
| Zachary Rehl | Proud Boys | 15 years | Philadelphia chapter leader |
| Ethan Nordean | Proud Boys | 18 years | Self-described sergeant-at-arms |

On January 20, 2025, President Donald Trump commuted the sentences of the convicted Oath Keepers and Proud Boys members to time served upon taking office, allowing their immediate release while leaving the convictions intact. Broader pardons were issued to most other January 6 defendants.

## Germany

### Constitutional and Statutory Framework

Germany's approach to sedition-related offenses reflects a constitutional order explicitly designed to prevent the subversion of democracy — the concept of wehrhafte Demokratie (militant or defensive democracy) enshrined in the Basic Law (Grundgesetz). Article 18 of the Basic Law permits the forfeiture of fundamental rights by those who abuse freedom of expression, press, assembly, or association to combat the free democratic basic order. Article 21(2) permits the Federal Constitutional Court to ban political parties that seek to undermine or abolish the free democratic basic order.

The Strafgesetzbuch (StGB) addresses sedition-related conduct through multiple provisions:

### High Treason (Hochverrat) — Sections 81-83a

| Section | Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|---------|
| § 81 | High treason against the Federation — undertaking to alter the constitutional order by force or threat of force, or to impair the existence of the Federal Republic | Life imprisonment or minimum 10 years |
| § 82 | High treason against a Land (federal state) — undertaking to alter the constitutional order of a Land by force or threat of force | 1 to 10 years imprisonment |
| § 83 | Preparation of high treason | 1 to 10 years imprisonment |
| § 83a | Active repentance — voluntary abandonment of the enterprise and prevention of danger | Court may mitigate sentence or dispense with punishment |

High treason requires the element of force or threat of force directed at altering the constitutional order. The offense is classified as an Unternehmensdelikt (enterprise offense), meaning that the attempt is punished equally with the completed act.

### Incitement to Hatred (Volksverhetzung) — Section 130

Section 130 is the provision most analogous to traditional sedition law, as it criminalizes speech that incites hatred against segments of the population:

| Subsection | Offense | Penalty |
|------------|---------|---------|
| § 130(1) | Inciting hatred against national, racial, religious, or ethnic groups, or against segments of the population; calls for violence or arbitrary measures; assaults on human dignity by insulting, maliciously maligning, or defaming such groups | 3 months to 5 years imprisonment |
| § 130(2) | Disseminating or producing content that incites hatred, calls for violence, or assaults human dignity of protected groups | Up to 3 years or fine |
| § 130(3) | Publicly approving, denying, or downplaying genocide committed under National Socialism (Holocaust denial) | Up to 5 years or fine |
| § 130(4) | Publicly disturbing public peace by approving, glorifying, or justifying Nazi tyranny in a manner that violates the dignity of victims | Up to 3 years or fine |

Section 130 has been upheld by the Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) as compatible with Article 5 of the Basic Law (freedom of expression), which expressly permits restrictions by "general laws, provisions for the protection of young persons, and the right to personal honour."

### Preparation of Serious Violent Offences Endangering the State — Section 89a

Enacted in 2009 in response to the growing terrorism threat in Europe, Section 89a criminalizes preparatory acts for serious violent offences endangering the state:

| Element | Provision |
|---------|-----------|
| Definition | Preparation of an offence against life (§§ 211-212) or personal freedom (§§ 239a-239b) intended to impair the existence or security of a state or international organization, or to abolish constitutional principles |
| Penalty | 6 months to 10 years imprisonment |
| Covered acts | Receiving instruction in weapons, explosives, or other skills; procuring or storing weapons, substances, or materials; collecting funds for the offence |

### Landmark Cases

**Communist Party Ban (1956):** The Federal Constitutional Court banned the Communist Party of Germany (KPD) under Article 21(2) of the Basic Law for pursuing goals incompatible with the free democratic basic order. The decision remains one of only two party bans in the history of the Federal Republic.

**Reichsbürger Coup Plot (2022-ongoing):** In December 2022, authorities arrested 25 members of the Reichsbürger (Citizens of the Reich) movement, including Heinrich XIII Prince Reuss, on charges of preparing to overthrow the federal government. The group allegedly planned to storm the Bundestag and install a new government. Trials on charges including preparation of high treason (§ 83 StGB) and membership in a terrorist organization (§ 129a StGB) commenced in 2024 and remain ongoing.

## ECHR & EU

### The European Convention on Human Rights — Article 10

The European Convention on Human Rights does not itself define or criminalize sedition. Instead, it provides the framework through which the European Court of Human Rights evaluates whether national sedition-related prosecutions violate the fundamental right to freedom of expression. Article 10 provides:

> **Article 10(1):** "Everyone has the right to freedom of expression. This right shall include freedom to hold opinions and to receive and impart information and ideas without interference by public authority and regardless of frontiers."

> **Article 10(2):** "The exercise of these freedoms, since it carries with it duties and responsibilities, may be subject to such formalities, conditions, restrictions or penalties as are prescribed by law and are necessary in a democratic society, in the interests of national security, territorial integrity or public safety, for the prevention of disorder or crime, for the protection of health or morals, for the protection of the reputation or rights of others, for preventing the disclosure of information received in confidence, or for maintaining the authority and impartiality of the judiciary."

### The Three-Part Test

The Court applies a structured three-part test to any restriction on expression:

1. **Prescribed by law:** The restriction must have a basis in domestic law that is accessible and foreseeable in its application.
2. **Legitimate aim:** The restriction must pursue one of the aims listed in Article 10(2), including national security, territorial integrity, prevention of disorder, or protection of the rights of others.
3. **Necessary in a democratic society:** The restriction must correspond to a "pressing social need" and be proportionate to the legitimate aim pursued. The state bears the burden of demonstrating necessity.

### Article 17 — Prohibition of Abuse of Rights

Article 17 provides that nothing in the Convention may be interpreted as implying a right to engage in any activity or perform any act aimed at the destruction of Convention rights. The Court has invoked Article 17 to deny the protection of Article 10 to speech that fundamentally undermines democratic values, including Holocaust denial and incitement to racial hatred.

### Landmark Cases

**Handyside v. United Kingdom (1976):** The foundational Article 10 case. The Court established that freedom of expression "is applicable not only to 'information' or 'ideas' that are favourably received or regarded as inoffensive or as a matter of indifference, but also to those that offend, shock or disturb the State or any sector of the population." However, the Court upheld the UK's obscenity prosecution, finding a margin of appreciation for the state in protecting morals.

**Incal v. Turkey (1998):** Mehmet Ibrahim Incal, a member of the Izmir branch of a pro-Kurdish party, was convicted by a Turkish national security court for preparing a leaflet critical of government policy toward Kurdish residents. The ECtHR found a violation of Article 10, holding that the leaflet did not incite violence and that prosecution by a specialized security court was disproportionate. The decision established that the participation of a military judge in such proceedings violated the right to a fair trial.

**Zana v. Turkey (1997):** Mehdi Zana, a former mayor and prominent Kurdish political figure, was convicted for expressing support for the PKK in a press interview. The ECtHR found no violation of Article 10, holding that the statements had to be considered in context — they coincided with lethal PKK attacks on civilians and could reasonably be regarded as likely to exacerbate violence in the region.

**Sürek v. Turkey (No. 1) (1999):** The Court upheld Turkey's conviction of a newspaper owner for publishing readers' letters that "amounted to an appeal to bloody revenge" and identified persons who could be targeted for violence. The Court found the speech constituted incitement to violence, justifying the restriction under Article 10(2).

**Yildirim v. Turkey (2012):** The Court found Turkey in violation of Article 10 for blocking access to Google Sites, holding that the blanket restriction was not "prescribed by law" because Turkish law did not authorize wholesale blocking of internet platforms and the measure was not foreseeable in its application.

**Perinçek v. Switzerland (Grand Chamber, 2015):** The Court found that Switzerland violated Article 10 by convicting a Turkish politician for denying the Armenian genocide. The Grand Chamber distinguished this from Holocaust denial, holding that the criminalization of genocide denial must be assessed in its specific historical and social context and that Switzerland had not demonstrated a pressing social need for the conviction.

### EU Framework Decision 2008/913/JHA

The European Union's Framework Decision on combating racism and xenophobia requires all EU member states to criminalize:

- Public incitement to violence or hatred against groups defined by race, colour, religion, descent, or national or ethnic origin
- Public condoning, denying, or grossly trivializing genocide, crimes against humanity, and war crimes when directed against such groups

This decision effectively harmonizes the minimum threshold for Volksverhetzung-type offenses across EU member states, while leaving implementation details to national law.

## Comparative Analysis

### Speech vs. Action: The Fundamental Divide

The most significant divergence among these jurisdictions concerns whether sedition requires an element of force or violence, or whether speech alone suffices for criminal liability:

- **South Korea** occupies the most speech-restrictive position, criminalizing mere praise, sympathy, or possession of materials favorable to anti-state organizations under NSA Article 7, without any requirement of incitement to violence.
- **Germany** occupies an intermediate position, criminalizing incitement to hatred (Section 130) and Holocaust denial without requiring imminent danger, but requiring force or its threat for high treason offenses.
- **The United States** requires either a conspiracy involving the intended use of force (Section 2384) or, under the Brandenburg test, speech directed at producing imminent lawless action that is likely to produce such action — the most speech-protective standard among the jurisdictions surveyed.
- **The ECHR** applies a contextual proportionality analysis, tolerating restrictions on speech that incites violence or hatred but invalidating prosecutions of political expression that merely offends or disturbs, placing it closer to the U.S. position for political speech but closer to Germany for hate speech.

### Historical Pattern: Sedition Laws as Products of Crisis

Each jurisdiction's sedition framework was shaped by specific historical crises:

- **South Korea:** The National Security Act was enacted three months after national founding, during the existential confrontation with North Korea that would erupt into open war in 1950. It reflects a security paradigm in which political dissent can be equated with enemy collaboration.
- **United States:** The major sedition statutes — the 1798 Sedition Act, the 1918 Sedition Act, the 1940 Smith Act — were all enacted in the context of imminent or actual war. The pattern of enactment during wartime and subsequent repeal or judicial narrowing has repeated itself throughout American history.
- **Germany:** The Volksverhetzung provision and the militant democracy framework emerged directly from the failure of the Weimar Republic to defend itself against anti-democratic movements, and from the experience of National Socialism. The 2009 addition of Section 89a responded to the post-9/11 terrorism threat.
- **ECHR:** The Convention itself was drafted in the aftermath of World War II and the totalitarian experience, with its protections designed to prevent the recurrence of state suppression of political expression.

### Prosecutorial Trends

Recent years have seen a resurgence of sedition-related prosecutions across jurisdictions:

- South Korea continues to apply the NSA, with prosecutions fluctuating according to the political orientation of the government in power.
- The United States deployed seditious conspiracy charges against participants in the January 6, 2021 Capitol attack, producing the first successful seditious conspiracy convictions since the 1995 Abdel-Rahman case. The subsequent commutation of those sentences by the incoming president highlights the political dimension of sedition enforcement.
- Germany is conducting the Reichsbürger trials, representing the first major high treason prosecution in decades.
- The ECHR continues to hear cases challenging member states' sedition-related prosecutions, with Turkey remaining the most frequent respondent state in Article 10 cases.
