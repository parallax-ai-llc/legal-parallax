## Summary

Cultivated meat -- also referred to as cell-cultured, lab-grown, or in vitro meat -- has emerged as a transformative food technology that challenges existing regulatory frameworks worldwide. The product is derived from animal cells cultured in bioreactors rather than through traditional animal husbandry and slaughter, raising novel questions regarding food safety authorization, labeling requirements, and the intersection of agricultural policy with biotechnology regulation. The United States established a dual regulatory framework under the FDA and USDA in 2019, and in June 2023 granted the first commercial approvals to Upside Foods and GOOD Meat. The European Union classifies cultivated meat as a "novel food" under Regulation (EC) No 2015/2283, requiring pre-market authorization through the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA), with no application having been approved as of early 2026. Italy became the first country to ban cultivated meat outright through Law No. 172 of 1 December 2023. South Korea has pursued a regulatory pathway through the Ministry of Food and Drug Safety (MFDS) and the Ministry of Agriculture, Food and Rural Affairs (MAFRA), with draft guidelines issued in 2024. Germany, as an EU member state, operates under the EU Novel Food Regulation while its domestic agricultural policy debates reflect significant tensions between traditional farming lobbies and biotechnology innovation interests.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's regulation of cultivated meat is governed by the intersection of several existing statutes, as no dedicated cultivated meat legislation has been enacted as of early 2026. The primary regulatory authorities are the Ministry of Food and Drug Safety (MFDS, 식품의약품안전처) and the Ministry of Agriculture, Food and Rural Affairs (MAFRA, 농림축산식품부).

Key statutory provisions include:

- **Food Sanitation Act (식품위생법, Act No. 14839):** Establishes the general framework for food safety regulation, including pre-market notification and approval requirements for new food products. Article 7 empowers the MFDS to establish food standards and specifications, and Article 18 requires safety assessments for foods produced using novel technologies.
- **Livestock Products Sanitary Control Act (축산물 위생관리법):** Regulates the production, processing, and distribution of meat and meat products. The classification of cultivated meat under this Act remains contested -- the question of whether cell-cultured products constitute "livestock products" (축산물) triggers significant regulatory consequences regarding inspection requirements.
- **Health Functional Food Act (건강기능식품에 관한 법률):** Potentially applicable if cultivated meat products are marketed with health claims.

In 2024, the MFDS issued draft guidelines for the safety evaluation of cell-cultured food products, establishing a regulatory pathway that includes: (1) pre-market safety assessment requirements covering cell line characterization, culture medium composition, and potential allergenicity; (2) manufacturing process standards including Good Manufacturing Practice (GMP) requirements; and (3) labeling and marketing guidelines requiring clear disclosure of the cell-cultured production method.

### Key Principles

South Korea's approach reflects its broader regulatory philosophy of balancing innovation promotion with consumer safety. The government has identified cultivated meat as a strategic technology under the Korean New Deal 2.0 framework and has allocated significant R&D funding. The Korea Institute of Science and Technology (KIST) and Seoul National University have led research efforts, with SpaceF (a Korean cultivated meat startup) receiving government support.

The MFDS has signaled a preference for a science-based, risk-assessment-driven approval process rather than the politically charged debates seen in the EU and US. However, the powerful Korean livestock industry lobby, represented by the Korea Meat Trade Association (한국축산물판매협회), has advocated for strict labeling requirements that differentiate cultivated meat from conventional meat products.

### Notable Cases

- **National Food Safety Forum Debate (2023):** The MFDS convened a public forum on cultivated meat regulation, receiving over 12,000 public comments. Consumer groups were divided, with the Citizens' Coalition for Economic Justice (경제정의실천시민연합) supporting cultivated meat development as an environmental measure, while the Korean Consumers' Union (한국소비자연맹) demanded rigorous safety testing and clear labeling.
- **SpaceF Regulatory Consultation (2024):** SpaceF submitted a pre-market consultation request to the MFDS for its cultivated pork product, marking the first formal regulatory engagement by a Korean cultivated meat company.

## United States

### Federal Framework

The United States operates a unique dual regulatory framework for cultivated meat, established through a formal agreement between the FDA and USDA-FSIS in March 2019:

- **FDA Jurisdiction (Pre-Harvest):** The FDA oversees cell collection, cell banking, cell growth, and cell differentiation under its authority over food safety in the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (21 U.S.C. § 301 et seq.). The FDA conducts pre-market consultations evaluating the safety of cell lines, culture media, scaffolding materials, and the overall production process. The FDA issued a "no questions" letter to Upside Foods in November 2022 and to GOOD Meat in March 2023, confirming that the products were safe for human consumption.
- **USDA-FSIS Jurisdiction (Post-Harvest):** Once cells have been harvested from bioreactors, the USDA-FSIS assumes regulatory authority under the Federal Meat Inspection Act (21 U.S.C. § 601 et seq.) and the Poultry Products Inspection Act (21 U.S.C. § 451 et seq.). USDA-FSIS grants establishment permits, conducts facility inspections, and approves product labels. In June 2023, USDA-FSIS granted the first commercial approvals, allowing Upside Foods and GOOD Meat to sell cultivated chicken products.

### Labeling Disputes

Labeling has been the most contentious regulatory issue in the US. USDA-FSIS requires that cultivated meat labels include the term "cell-cultivated" and not use terms that could mislead consumers about the nature of the product. The National Cattlemen's Beef Association and other livestock industry groups have lobbied for more restrictive labeling, arguing that terms like "meat" and "chicken" should be reserved for conventionally raised animal products.

Several states have enacted laws restricting the labeling of cultivated meat:

- **Florida SB 1084 (2024):** Banned the manufacture, sale, and distribution of cultivated meat within the state, making Florida the first state to prohibit the product entirely. Violations are punishable as second-degree misdemeanors.
- **Alabama HB 23 (2024):** Similarly prohibited the sale of cultivated meat, with penalties including fines of up to $25,000.
- **Missouri RSMo § 265.494 (2018):** The earliest state labeling law, prohibiting the use of the term "meat" for products not derived from harvested livestock or poultry. Challenged in Turtle Island Foods v. Thompson, No. 4:18-cv-04173 (W.D. Mo.), where the court upheld the law's constitutionality under a rational basis review.
- **Texas HB 764 (proposed 2025):** Would require cultivated meat products to carry prominent "LAB-GROWN" labeling.

### Notable Cases

- **Upside Foods and GOOD Meat Approvals (June 2023):** The first commercial approvals of cultivated meat in the US, with initial sales limited to high-end restaurants. Upside Foods' cultivated chicken was served at Bar Crenn in San Francisco; GOOD Meat's product was offered at China Chilcano by Jose Andres in Washington, D.C.
- **Turtle Island Foods v. Thompson (W.D. Mo. 2019):** Challenge to Missouri's meat labeling law by the plant-based food company Tofurky. The court rejected First Amendment arguments, finding that the state had a rational basis for preventing consumer confusion.
- **Florida Cultivated Meat Ban Litigation (2024-2025):** Upside Foods and GOOD Meat filed suit challenging Florida's SB 1084 as a violation of the Dormant Commerce Clause, arguing that the ban impermissibly discriminated against interstate commerce.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

As an EU member state, Germany's regulation of cultivated meat is primarily governed by Regulation (EU) 2015/2283 on Novel Foods. No cultivated meat product has been authorized for sale in the EU as of early 2026. Key legal provisions include:

- **Regulation (EU) 2015/2283 (Novel Food Regulation):** Article 3(2)(a) defines "novel food" as any food that was not used for human consumption to a significant degree within the EU before 15 May 1997. Cultivated meat falls squarely within this definition, requiring pre-market authorization. Applications must be submitted to the European Commission, which requests a scientific risk assessment from EFSA. The authorization process typically takes 18 months but can extend significantly.
- **Regulation (EU) No 1169/2011 (Food Information to Consumers):** Establishes labeling requirements applicable to cultivated meat, including mandatory information on the production method, ingredients, allergens, and nutritional content.
- **Lebensmittel- und Futtermittelgesetzbuch (LFGB, Food and Feed Code):** Germany's national food safety law, which supplements EU regulations with additional enforcement provisions. The Federal Office for Consumer Protection and Food Safety (BVL) serves as the competent national authority for novel food applications, coordinating with EFSA.
- **Grundgesetz Article 2(2) (Right to Physical Integrity):** Germany's constitutional guarantee of physical integrity informs the precautionary approach to food safety regulation, supporting rigorous pre-market safety assessments.

### Political and Industry Dynamics

Germany's position on cultivated meat is shaped by competing interests:

- **Agricultural Lobby (Deutscher Bauernverband, DBV):** The German Farmers' Association has been skeptical of cultivated meat, arguing that it threatens traditional farming livelihoods and questioning its environmental credentials. The DBV has advocated for strict labeling requirements and opposed any public subsidies for cultivated meat research.
- **Green Party (Bündnis 90/Die Grünen):** During the coalition government (2021-2025), the Green Party supported cultivated meat research as part of its food system transformation agenda, though internal divisions existed regarding the role of technological solutions versus systemic agricultural reform.
- **German Federal Institute for Risk Assessment (BfR):** The BfR has published risk assessments on potential food safety issues with cultivated meat, including the use of fetal bovine serum in cell culture media, potential for microbial contamination in bioreactor environments, and the long-term safety of novel cell lines.

### Notable Developments

- **BfR Risk Communication on Cultivated Meat (2023):** The Federal Institute for Risk Assessment published a comprehensive consumer communication report finding that 62% of German consumers were unaware of cultivated meat technology, and that acceptance correlated strongly with education level and environmental awareness.
- **Aleph Farms Pre-Submission Meeting with BVL (2024):** Israeli cultivated meat company Aleph Farms held pre-submission meetings with the BVL in preparation for a novel food application under Regulation 2015/2283, representing one of the first formal regulatory engagements for cultivated meat in Germany.

## ECHR / EU Framework

### Convention Articles

The European Convention on Human Rights provides a foundational framework relevant to cultivated meat regulation through several articles:

- **Article 8 -- Right to Respect for Private and Family Life:** The right to private life encompasses aspects of personal autonomy, including dietary choices. Restrictions on cultivated meat (such as Italy's outright ban) could potentially be challenged under Article 8 if they disproportionately interfere with individuals' right to make autonomous food choices.
- **Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 -- Protection of Property:** Applicable to cultivated meat companies whose property rights (including investment-backed expectations and intellectual property) may be affected by regulatory bans or excessively restrictive authorization procedures.
- **Article 10 -- Freedom of Expression:** Relevant to labeling disputes, as restrictions on how cultivated meat companies may describe their products engage commercial expression rights.

### EU Regulatory Framework

The EU's approach to cultivated meat is defined by the precautionary principle (TFEU Article 191(2)) and the following regulatory instruments:

- **Regulation (EU) 2015/2283 (Novel Food Regulation):** The primary regulatory gateway. Article 10 requires applicants to submit a dossier demonstrating the safety of the novel food, including toxicological data, compositional analysis, and allergenicity assessment. EFSA evaluates the dossier and issues a scientific opinion. The European Commission then decides whether to authorize the product, with member states voting in the Standing Committee on Plants, Animals, Food and Feed (SCoPAFF).
- **Regulation (EU) No 1829/2003 (GM Food and Feed):** Potentially applicable if genetically modified cell lines are used in cultivated meat production, triggering additional authorization requirements under the GMO framework.
- **Regulation (EU) 2024/1781 (Ecodesign for Sustainable Products):** The broader EU sustainability framework may require life-cycle assessments for cultivated meat products, comparing their environmental footprint against conventional meat production.

### Italy's Cultivated Meat Ban

Italy's Law No. 172 of 1 December 2023 (Legge sugli alimenti sintetici) represents the most significant regulatory intervention against cultivated meat globally:

- The law prohibits the production, marketing, import, and export of food and feed derived from vertebrate animal cell cultures within Italian territory.
- Violations are punishable by fines ranging from EUR 10,000 to EUR 60,000, plus confiscation of products and suspension of business activity for up to three years.
- The Italian government justified the ban on grounds of protecting Italian food heritage, the precautionary principle, and the interests of traditional livestock farmers.
- The European Commission is examining whether the ban violates the free movement of goods under TFEU Articles 34-36 and the Novel Food Regulation's harmonized authorization procedure. A preliminary reference to the CJEU is considered likely.
- The Good Food Institute Europe and several cultivated meat companies have challenged the ban, arguing it violates EU law and the proportionality principle.

### Key Case Law

- **Commission v. Italy (pending):** The European Commission has initiated an infringement procedure examining Italy's cultivated meat ban under Article 258 TFEU, focusing on whether the ban contravenes the Novel Food Regulation's preemptive effect on member state food safety measures.
- **CJEU, Case C-528/16, Confédération paysanne v. Premier ministre (2018):** While concerning gene-edited organisms rather than cultivated meat, this landmark ruling established that organisms produced by mutagenesis techniques developed after 2001 fall within the scope of the GMO Directive, signaling the CJEU's broad approach to regulating novel food technologies.
- **CJEU, Case C-422/16, TofuTown.com (2017):** The Court ruled that plant-based products cannot use dairy terms such as "milk," "butter," or "cheese" under Regulation (EU) No 1308/2013. This precedent is directly relevant to the labeling debate for cultivated meat, though the specific Annex VII restrictions apply only to dairy, not meat terminology.
