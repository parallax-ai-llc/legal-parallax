## Summary

The right to food -- recognized as a fundamental human right under international law encompassing the right to adequate, sufficient, nutritious, and culturally appropriate food accessible to all persons at all times, and the fundamental right to be free from hunger -- stands at the intersection of human rights law, constitutional law, agricultural policy, trade law, and social welfare systems, with its legal operationalization varying dramatically across jurisdictions from justiciable constitutional rights to programmatic policy commitments; the right encompasses both a negative obligation (not to interfere with existing access to food) and positive obligations (to facilitate, provide, and ensure access to adequate food), and increasingly intersects with debates about food sovereignty, genetically modified organisms, food waste, and the right to sustainable food systems. South Korea's Constitution does not explicitly enumerate a right to food, but the Constitutional Court (헌법재판소) has recognized elements of food security as implicit in the right to a dignified human existence under Article 34(1) ("All citizens shall be entitled to a life worthy of human dignity") and the state's duty to promote social welfare; the Framework Act on Agriculture, Rural Community, and Food Industry (농업·농촌 및 식품산업 기본법) Article 3 declares the right of citizens to safe and quality food, while the Food Security Basic Act (식량안보기본법, enacted 2024) establishes food security as a national priority. The United States has no constitutional right to food, though the state of Maine amended its constitution in 2021 to become the first U.S. state to enshrine a right to food (Maine Constitution, Art. I, Section 25); federal food assistance programs -- SNAP (7 U.S.C. Section 2011 et seq.), WIC (42 U.S.C. Section 1786), and the National School Lunch Program (42 U.S.C. Section 1751 et seq.) -- constitute the primary statutory framework; courts have consistently rejected attempts to establish a federal constitutional right to food, as in Hannon v. Hartley, No. 19-cv-1127 (W.D. Pa. 2019). Germany recognizes the right to a dignified subsistence minimum (menschenwuerdiges Existenzminimum) as a justiciable constitutional right derived from Article 1(1) GG (human dignity) in conjunction with Article 20(1) GG (social state principle), as established by the BVerfG in its Hartz IV decision (1 BvL 1/09, February 9, 2010), which includes adequate nutrition as a component; Germany's agricultural policy operates within the EU Common Agricultural Policy (CAP) framework. The ECHR has not recognized a freestanding right to food under the Convention, but the European Committee of Social Rights has interpreted the European Social Charter Article 11 (right to protection of health) and Article 13 (right to social and medical assistance) as encompassing obligations related to food adequacy; internationally, ICESCR Article 11 establishes the most comprehensive articulation of the right to food, with General Comment No. 12 (1999) of the UN Committee on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights providing the authoritative interpretation, and the FAO Voluntary Guidelines on the Right to Food (2004) offering implementation guidance.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's approach to the right to food combines constitutional principles, dedicated agricultural and food security legislation, and an extensive food assistance system.

**Constitutional Foundation:** The Constitution of the Republic of Korea does not contain an express right to food. However, Article 34(1) provides: "All citizens shall be entitled to a life worthy of human dignity." Article 34(2) adds: "The State shall have the duty to endeavor to promote social welfare and social security." Article 123 specifically addresses agriculture: "The State shall establish and implement a plan necessary to foster and support the agricultural and fishing communities and to protect and promote the agriculture and fisheries to ensure the balanced development of regions."

The Constitutional Court has not ruled directly on a justiciable right to food, but in Constitutional Court Decision 2002Hun-Ma328 (2004), addressing the adequacy of social assistance benefits, the Court recognized that the state's obligation under Article 34 requires providing a minimum standard of living that includes adequate food, housing, and other basic necessities. The Court applied a minimum-core standard, holding that legislative decisions on benefit levels enjoy a wide margin of discretion but must not fall below a minimum necessary for a dignified existence.

**Framework Act on Agriculture, Rural Community, and Food Industry (농업·농촌 및 식품산업 기본법):** Article 3 declares: "Citizens shall have the right to receive safe and quality food." Article 4 establishes the state's duty to ensure stable food supply and to promote food security. Though framed as a programmatic right, Article 3 provides a normative foundation for food-related policy and legislation.

**Food Security Basic Act (식량안보기본법, 2024):** Enacted in 2024 in response to global food supply disruptions during the COVID-19 pandemic and the Ukraine conflict, this Act establishes food security as a national priority. Key provisions:

- **Article 2 (Definition):** Defines food security as the stable supply of food sufficient in quantity and quality to meet the dietary needs of all citizens at all times.
- **Article 5 (National Food Security Plan):** Requires the government to establish a comprehensive food security plan every five years, addressing domestic production capacity, strategic food reserves, import diversification, and emergency food distribution.
- **Article 8 (Strategic Grain Reserves):** Mandates the maintenance of strategic grain reserves, including rice and wheat, at levels sufficient to meet at least 60 days of national consumption.
- **Article 12 (Food Security Committee):** Establishes a Food Security Committee under the Prime Minister to coordinate inter-ministerial food security policy.

**National Basic Livelihood Security Act (국민기초생활 보장법):** This Act, enacted in 1999 and significantly amended in 2014, provides the primary statutory basis for food assistance to persons living below the minimum cost of living. Article 7 entitles eligible recipients to livelihood benefits, which include a food component calculated based on the minimum cost of living determined by the Central Livelihood Security Committee.

### Food Safety

**Food Safety Basic Act (식품안전기본법, 2008):** Establishes the Food Safety Policy Committee (식품안전정책위원회) and principles for food safety governance. Article 3 declares the right of consumers to safe food.

**Food Sanitation Act (식품위생법):** The primary operational food safety statute, administered by the Ministry of Food and Drug Safety (MFDS). Covers food standards, labeling, inspection, and enforcement.

### Agricultural Policy and Food Sovereignty

South Korea's rice self-sufficiency policy, reflecting the cultural and strategic importance of rice, has been a central element of agricultural policy. The direct payment system for farmers, restructured under the Public Benefits Direct Payment Act (공익직불제법, 2020), links agricultural subsidies to environmental stewardship and food security objectives. South Korea's agricultural sector is also shaped by WTO obligations and free trade agreements, with rice being a particularly sensitive commodity in trade negotiations.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has no constitutionally recognized right to food at the federal level, relying instead on an extensive but discretionary system of food assistance programs.

**Constitutional Status:** U.S. courts have consistently held that the federal Constitution does not guarantee a right to food. In Lindsey v. Normet, 405 U.S. 56 (1972), the Supreme Court declined to recognize a constitutional right to housing, reasoning that has been applied by lower courts to reject food rights claims. In Hannon v. Hartley, No. 19-cv-1127 (W.D. Pa. 2019), the court dismissed a prisoner's claim of a constitutional right to nutritious food as a freestanding substantive due process right, though the Eighth Amendment prohibition on cruel and unusual punishment requires adequate food for incarcerated persons.

**Maine Right to Food Amendment (2021):** Maine became the first U.S. state to constitutionally protect the right to food. Article I, Section 25 of the Maine Constitution provides: "All individuals have a natural, inherent and unalienable right to food, including the right to save and exchange seeds and the right to grow, raise, harvest, produce and consume the food of their own choosing for their own nourishment, sustenance, bodily health and well-being, as long as an individual does not commit trespassing, theft, poaching or other abuses of private property rights, public lands or natural resources in the harvesting, production or acquisition of food."

The amendment was approved by referendum in November 2021 with approximately 60% support. Its scope and justiciability remain untested, as no court has yet interpreted its provisions.

**Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP, 7 U.S.C. Section 2011 et seq.):** SNAP, formerly known as Food Stamps, is the largest federal food assistance program, serving approximately 42 million individuals (as of FY 2023). Key features:

- **Section 2011 (Congressional Declaration of Policy):** Congress declares it to be the policy of the United States to "safeguard the health and well-being of the Nation's population by raising levels of nutrition among low-income households."
- **Section 2014 (Eligibility):** Eligibility is based on household income (generally at or below 130% of the federal poverty level) and resources.
- **Section 2017 (Value of Allotment):** Benefit levels are tied to the Thrifty Food Plan, an estimate of the cost of a nutritionally adequate diet.
- **Section 2029 (Employment and Training):** Able-bodied adults without dependents (ABAWDs) are generally limited to three months of benefits in a 36-month period unless they meet work requirements.

**Special Supplemental Nutrition Program for Women, Infants, and Children (WIC, 42 U.S.C. Section 1786):** WIC provides supplemental food, nutrition education, and healthcare referrals to low-income pregnant and postpartum women, infants, and children up to age five. WIC is a discretionary (non-entitlement) program, meaning that funding is subject to annual appropriations and eligible persons may not receive benefits if funding is exhausted.

**National School Lunch Program (42 U.S.C. Section 1751 et seq.):** Provides free or reduced-price lunches to children in participating schools. The Community Eligibility Provision (CEP) allows high-poverty schools to offer free meals to all students without individual applications.

### Food Security Policy

**Global Food Security Act of 2016 (22 U.S.C. Section 9301 et seq.):** Addresses U.S. global food security policy, authorizing the Feed the Future initiative. Reauthorized in 2018 and 2023.

**Farm Bill:** The omnibus agricultural legislation, reauthorized approximately every five years, contains both agricultural commodity support programs (Title I) and nutrition assistance programs (Title IV, which includes SNAP). The integration of farm subsidies and food assistance in a single legislative vehicle reflects the political alliance between agricultural and anti-hunger constituencies.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany has developed the most robust justiciable right to food among the jurisdictions studied, grounded in the constitutional guarantee of a dignified subsistence minimum.

**Constitutional Foundation -- Menschenwuerdiges Existenzminimum:** The BVerfG has derived a justiciable right to a dignified subsistence minimum from Article 1(1) GG ("Human dignity shall be inviolable. To respect and protect it shall be the duty of all state authority") in conjunction with Article 20(1) GG (social state principle, Sozialstaatsprinzip). This right includes adequate nutrition as a core component.

**BVerfG, Judgment of February 9, 2010 (1 BvL 1/09, "Hartz IV Decision"):** The landmark decision held that the legislature is constitutionally required to ensure that social assistance benefits are sufficient to cover the "physical existence" (physische Existenz) of recipients, including food, clothing, housing, heating, and hygiene, as well as a minimum level of participation in social, cultural, and political life. The Court:

- Declared that the methodology used to calculate Regelleistungen (standard benefits) under SGB II was incompatible with Articles 1(1) and 20(1) GG because it failed to employ a transparent, needs-based calculation method.
- Required the legislature to develop a transparent, evidence-based procedure for determining benefit levels that ensures adequate coverage of subsistence needs including nutrition.
- Held that the right is not merely a right to survival (Ueberleben) but a right to a dignified existence (menschenwuerdiges Dasein), which includes food that meets nutritional standards.

**BVerfG, Judgment of July 18, 2012 (1 BvL 10/10, "Asylum Seekers Benefits"):** Extended the Existenzminimum right to asylum seekers, holding that human dignity does not admit of limitation based on immigration status. The Court declared the benefit levels under the Asylbewerberleistungsgesetz unconstitutional because they were arbitrarily set below the levels for German citizens without a transparent needs-based methodology.

### Social Welfare Legislation

**SGB II (Sozialgesetzbuch Zweites Buch -- Buergergeld):** Provides means-tested benefits to employable persons and their household members. The Regelbedarfe (standard requirements, formerly Regelleistungen) include an amount for food and non-alcoholic beverages, calculated based on the consumption expenditure of low-income reference households as determined by the Einkommens- und Verbrauchsstichprobe (EVS, Income and Consumption Survey). As of 2025, the Regelbedarf for a single adult is EUR 563 per month, of which approximately 36% (approximately EUR 203) is allocated to food and beverages.

**SGB XII (Sozialgesetzbuch Zwoelftes Buch -- Sozialhilfe):** Provides social assistance to persons who are not employable (e.g., due to age or disability), with benefit levels structured similarly to SGB II.

**Regelbedarfsermittlungsgesetz (RBEG -- Standard Requirements Determination Act):** Establishes the methodology for calculating Regelbedarfe, implementing the BVerfG's requirements for transparent, needs-based determination. The RBEG requires that benefit levels be updated annually based on price indices and periodically recalculated based on new EVS data.

### Agricultural Policy

Germany's agricultural policy operates within the EU Common Agricultural Policy (CAP). The CAP provides direct payments to farmers (Pillar I) and rural development support (Pillar II). Germany's implementation of the CAP through the National Strategic Plan (2023-2027) emphasizes environmental sustainability, animal welfare, and the economic viability of small and medium-sized farms.

The Ernaehrungssicherstellungsgesetz (ESG -- Food Supply Security Act, 1965, as amended) provides the legal basis for government intervention in the food supply in times of crisis, including the authority to ration food, requisition food stocks, and direct agricultural production.

### Food Safety

**Lebensmittel- und Futtermittelgesetzbuch (LFGB -- Food and Feed Code):** Implements EU food safety law, including Regulation (EC) 178/2002, at the national level. Administered by the Bundesamt fuer Verbraucherschutz und Lebensmittelsicherheit (BVL) and the Bundesinstitut fuer Risikobewertung (BfR).

## ECHR & EU

### International Law -- ICESCR Article 11

**ICESCR Article 11(1):** "The States Parties to the present Covenant recognize the right of everyone to an adequate standard of living for himself and his family, including adequate food, clothing and housing, and to the continuous improvement of living conditions."

**ICESCR Article 11(2):** "The States Parties to the present Covenant, recognizing the fundamental right of everyone to be free from hunger, shall take, individually and through international co-operation, the measures, including specific programmes, which are needed: (a) To improve methods of production, conservation and distribution of food..."

**General Comment No. 12 (1999) -- The Right to Adequate Food:** The CESCR provided the authoritative interpretation:

- **Paragraph 6:** The right to adequate food is realized when "every man, woman and child, alone or in community with others, has physical and economic access at all times to adequate food or means for its procurement."
- **Paragraph 8:** The core content includes: (a) the availability of food in sufficient quantity and quality to satisfy dietary needs; (b) the accessibility of such food in ways consistent with human dignity.
- **Paragraph 15:** The right encompasses three obligations: to respect (not interfere with existing access), to protect (prevent third parties from interfering with access), and to fulfil (facilitate, and as a last resort, provide food directly).
- **Paragraph 17:** A State party violates the Covenant if a significant number of individuals are deprived of adequate food and the State fails to demonstrate that this is beyond its control.

### European Social Charter

**Article 11 (Right to Protection of Health):** The European Committee of Social Rights has interpreted this provision as requiring states to ensure access to adequate nutrition. In International Federation of Human Rights Leagues (FIDH) v. France (Complaint No. 14/2003), the Committee found that France's failure to ensure adequate health care for irregular migrants included concerns about access to basic necessities including food.

**Article 13 (Right to Social and Medical Assistance):** Requires states to provide adequate assistance to persons without resources. The Committee has interpreted this as including food assistance.

**Article 30 (Right to Protection Against Poverty and Social Exclusion):** Added by the 1996 Revised Charter, this article requires states to take measures to prevent and combat poverty, which the Committee has interpreted as encompassing food security.

### EU Framework

**Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union (TFEU) Article 39:** The original objectives of the Common Agricultural Policy include ensuring "a fair standard of living for the agricultural community" and ensuring "that supplies reach consumers at reasonable prices."

**EU Fund for European Aid to the Most Deprived (FEAD, Regulation (EU) 2014/223):** Supports member state programs providing food and material assistance to the most deprived persons. The FEAD has been integrated into the ESF+ (European Social Fund Plus) under Regulation (EU) 2021/1057 for the 2021-2027 programming period.

**EU Farm to Fork Strategy (2020):** Part of the European Green Deal, the strategy aims to make EU food systems "fair, healthy and environmentally-friendly." While not creating justiciable rights, the strategy frames food access and sustainability as policy priorities.

### FAO Voluntary Guidelines on the Right to Food (2004)

Adopted by the FAO Council in November 2004, the Voluntary Guidelines to Support the Progressive Realization of the Right to Adequate Food in the Context of National Food Security provide practical guidance for states implementing the right to food. Key guidelines address: enabling environments, social safety nets, food quality and consumer protection, natural disasters, and monitoring and accountability.

## Comparative Analysis

### Justiciability Spectrum

The jurisdictions form a clear spectrum of justiciability. Germany provides the most robust justiciable right, with the BVerfG requiring transparent, evidence-based calculation of subsistence benefits including food. South Korea occupies a middle position: the Constitutional Court recognizes a minimum standard implied by Article 34 but grants the legislature wide discretion. The United States has no federal constitutional right, though Maine's 2021 amendment creates an untested state-level right. The ECHR framework provides soft justiciability through the European Social Charter complaints procedure.

### Programmatic vs. Entitlement Approaches

South Korea and Germany combine constitutional principles with statutory entitlement programs (National Basic Livelihood Security Act; SGB II/XII). The United States relies primarily on statutory programs (SNAP, WIC, NSLP), with SNAP functioning as a legal entitlement but WIC as a discretionary program subject to funding limits. This structural difference means that in the United States, the right to food is contingent on legislative appropriation, while in Germany, the constitutional obligation constrains legislative discretion over benefit levels.

### Food Sovereignty

The concept of food sovereignty -- the right of peoples to define their own food and agricultural systems -- has gained limited legal recognition. South Korea's Framework Agriculture Act Article 3 comes closest to a food sovereignty provision, and Korea's rice self-sufficiency policy reflects food sovereignty principles. The United States and EU treat food security as a trade and agricultural policy matter rather than a rights-based framework. Maine's right to food amendment, with its emphasis on the right to "grow, raise, harvest, produce and consume the food of their own choosing," incorporates elements of food sovereignty at the individual level.

### International Obligations

South Korea and Germany, as ICESCR States Parties, have binding international obligations under Article 11. The United States, having signed but not ratified the ICESCR, is not bound by Article 11 but is obligated under the Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties not to act contrary to the treaty's object and purpose. The practical significance of this distinction has been limited, as the CESCR's enforcement mechanisms are relatively weak.
