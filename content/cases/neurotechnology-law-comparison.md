## Summary

Neurotechnology regulation -- the legal governance of devices, techniques, and systems that interface with, monitor, decode, or modulate the nervous system, including brain-computer interfaces (BCIs), neural implants, neurostimulation devices, consumer electroencephalography (EEG) headsets, and artificial intelligence-driven brain data analytics -- represents one of the most rapidly emerging and least developed areas of global regulatory law, raising fundamental questions about mental privacy, cognitive liberty, personal identity, and the boundaries between therapeutic medical devices and human enhancement technologies. South Korea has no specific neurotechnology legislation but regulates neural devices through the Medical Devices Act (의료기기법) and addresses brain data through the Personal Information Protection Act (개인정보 보호법, PIPA); the Brain Research Promotion Act (뇌연구 촉진법), enacted in 1998 and amended in 2018, establishes a national brain research strategy through the Korea Brain Research Institute (한국뇌연구원) but does not address neurorights or consumer neurotechnology. The United States regulates neurotechnology primarily through the FDA's medical device framework under the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (21 U.S.C. Section 360c et seq.), with BCIs classified as Class III devices requiring premarket approval; Neuralink received FDA breakthrough device designation in 2023 and began its first human clinical trial (PRIME study) in January 2024; no federal neurodata privacy law exists, though Colorado's SB 24-058 (2024) became the first state law explicitly protecting neural data as sensitive personal data. Germany regulates neurotechnology through the Medical Devices Regulation (EU) 2017/745 (MDR) and the Medizinprodukterecht-Durchführungsgesetz (MPDG), with neural data protected under the GDPR as health data (Article 9) and potentially as biometric data; the German Ethics Council (Deutscher Ethikrat) published a comprehensive opinion on brain-computer interfaces in 2023 recommending specific neurorights legislation. At the European and international level, the OECD adopted the first international Recommendation on Responsible Innovation in Neurotechnology in December 2019, the Council of Europe launched a strategic action plan on neurotechnology and human rights in 2023, UNESCO's International Bioethics Committee (IBC) published a report on the ethics of neurotechnology in 2021, and Chile became the first country to constitutionally enshrine neurorights through its 2021 amendment to Article 19 No. 1 of the Chilean Constitution, establishing a model that other jurisdictions are studying.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's regulatory approach to neurotechnology remains fragmented across existing medical device, data protection, and research promotion statutes without a dedicated neurotechnology governance framework.

**Brain Research Promotion Act (뇌연구 촉진법):** Enacted in 1998 and substantially amended in 2018, this statute establishes the institutional framework for brain research in South Korea. Key provisions include:

- **Article 3:** The government shall formulate a comprehensive brain research promotion plan every five years.
- **Article 5:** Establishment of the Korea Brain Research Institute (KBRI, 한국뇌연구원) in Daegu as the national brain research hub.
- **Article 6:** Government support for brain science research projects, brain-machine interface development, and brain data infrastructure.

The Act focuses exclusively on research promotion and does not address neurorights, consumer neurotechnology, or brain data governance.

**Medical Devices Act (의료기기법):** Neural devices intended for medical use -- including deep brain stimulation (DBS) systems, cochlear implants, and BCI systems for medical purposes -- are regulated by the Ministry of Food and Drug Safety (식품의약품안전처, MFDS) under this Act. Medical BCIs are classified as high-risk devices (Class III or IV) requiring premarket approval through clinical evaluation data. The 2020 amendment introduced expedited approval pathways for innovative medical devices (혁신의료기기) including AI-based neural devices.

**Personal Information Protection Act (개인정보 보호법, PIPA):** Neural data -- electrical signals, brain imaging data, and derived cognitive or emotional state information -- falls under PIPA as personal information when it can identify or be linked to a specific individual. The 2023 amendment to PIPA, effective September 15, 2023, strengthened requirements for processing sensitive information and introduced the concept of pseudonymized data, but did not create a specific category for neural data.

### Research and Industry

South Korea's brain technology sector is growing rapidly. The Korea Brain Initiative, announced in 2016, allocated KRW 3.4 trillion over 10 years for brain mapping and neurotechnology development. Samsung has invested in neural interface technology through Samsung Research, and several Korean startups are developing consumer EEG devices for meditation, sleep improvement, and cognitive enhancement applications. The Gwangju Institute of Science and Technology (GIST) and KAIST operate leading BCI research programs.

### Ethical Governance

The National Bioethics Committee (국가생명윤리심의위원회), established under the Bioethics and Safety Act (생명윤리 및 안전에 관한 법률), provides ethical oversight for neuroscience research involving human subjects. However, its jurisdiction is limited to research protocols and does not extend to commercial neurotechnology products or neural data governance.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States regulates neurotechnology primarily through its medical device and consumer protection frameworks, with emerging state-level neurodata privacy legislation.

**FDA Medical Device Classification:** Under 21 U.S.C. Section 360c, the FDA classifies medical devices into three classes based on risk level. BCIs are generally classified as Class III (highest risk), requiring premarket approval (PMA) with clinical trial data demonstrating safety and effectiveness. The FDA has established several relevant product codes:

- **QMT:** Implanted brain stimulator for pain relief
- **GWF:** Electroencephalograph (EEG)
- **PHL:** Deep brain stimulation for movement disorders

**Neuralink PRIME Study (2024):** Neuralink Corporation received FDA Breakthrough Device Designation in 2023 for its N1 implant, a 1,024-electrode BCI designed to enable paralyzed individuals to control digital devices through thought. The first human implant was performed in January 2024 as part of the PRIME (Precise Robotically Implanted Brain-Computer Interface) study, conducted under an Investigational Device Exemption (IDE). The subject, Noland Arbaugh, demonstrated the ability to control a computer cursor through neural signals.

**Colorado SB 24-058 -- Colorado Privacy Act Amendment (2024):** Signed by Governor Jared Polis on April 17, 2024, this statute amended the Colorado Privacy Act (CPA) to explicitly include "biological data" and "neural data" in the definition of "sensitive data" subject to enhanced privacy protections. Neural data is defined as "information that is generated by measuring the activity of a consumer's central or peripheral nervous system, and that is not inferred from nonneural information." The law requires opt-in consent for the collection and processing of neural data and grants consumers the right to delete, correct, and opt out of the sale of their neural data.

**Federal Research Funding:** The BRAIN Initiative (Brain Research through Advancing Innovative Neurotechnologies), launched by President Obama in 2013 and funded at approximately $6 billion through 2024 across NIH, NSF, DARPA, and IARPA, supports foundational neurotechnology research. DARPA's Neural Engineering System Design (NESD) program specifically targets development of high-bandwidth BCI systems.

**State Proposals:** Following Colorado, several states have introduced neurodata privacy bills:

| State | Bill | Status |
|-------|------|--------|
| California | AB 1008 (2024) | Introduced; would add neural data to CCPA |
| Minnesota | HF 1370 (2024) | Introduced; comprehensive neurodata protection |
| Vermont | S 203 (2024) | Committee review |

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany regulates neurotechnology through the EU medical device framework and GDPR, supplemented by influential policy recommendations from the German Ethics Council.

**EU Medical Devices Regulation (MDR) 2017/745:** The MDR, which became fully applicable on May 26, 2021, applies directly in Germany and classifies neural implants as Class III devices under Rule 8 (active devices intended to monitor or influence brain function). The Medizinprodukterecht-Durchführungsgesetz (MPDG), enacted in 2020, implements the MDR's national provisions including designating the Bundesinstitut für Arzneimittel und Medizinprodukte (BfArM) as the competent authority.

**GDPR -- Neural Data as Health and Biometric Data:** The General Data Protection Regulation (Regulation (EU) 2016/679) provides significant protections for neural data under two special categories:

- **Article 9(1) -- Health Data:** Neural data revealing health conditions (neurological disorders, mental health status) constitutes "data concerning health" subject to the general prohibition on processing unless an Article 9(2) exception applies.
- **Article 9(1) -- Biometric Data:** Neural activity patterns used for identification purposes constitute biometric data. The GDPR defines biometric data as "personal data resulting from specific technical processing relating to the physical, physiological, or behavioural characteristics of a natural person."

**Bundesdatenschutzgesetz (BDSG) Section 22:** The Federal Data Protection Act supplements the GDPR with additional conditions for processing sensitive data including health data, requiring appropriate and specific measures to safeguard data subjects' interests.

### German Ethics Council Opinion (2023)

The Deutscher Ethikrat published its opinion "Mensch und Maschine -- Herausforderungen durch Neurotechnologie" (Human and Machine -- Challenges of Neurotechnology) on November 14, 2023. Key recommendations included:

- Recognition of "mental privacy" (mentale Privatsphäre) as a fundamental right requiring explicit legal protection
- Classification of neural data as a distinct sensitive data category under data protection law
- Prohibition on the use of neural data for employment, insurance, or credit decisions without specific legal authorization
- Establishment of an independent neurotechnology oversight body
- International cooperation on neurotechnology governance through the Council of Europe and OECD

### Research Landscape

Germany hosts significant neurotechnology research capacity. The Max Planck Institute for Human Cognitive and Brain Sciences in Leipzig conducts foundational neuroscience research. The Charité -- Universitätsmedizin Berlin operates one of Europe's leading BCI clinical programs. Fraunhofer Institutes are developing commercial applications for neural interface technology. The Federal Ministry of Education and Research (BMBF) funds neurotechnology research through the National Decade for the Brain (Nationale Dekade gegen Krebs und für die Hirngesundheit).

## ECHR & EU

### OECD Recommendation on Responsible Innovation in Neurotechnology (2019)

Adopted by the OECD Council on December 11, 2019, this was the first international standard on neurotechnology governance. The Recommendation establishes nine principles:

1. Promoting responsible innovation and prioritizing safety assessment
2. Prioritizing safety assessment
3. Promoting inclusivity
4. Fostering scientific collaboration
5. Enabling capacity of oversight bodies
6. Safeguarding personal brain data and other neural data
7. Promoting cultures of stewardship and trust
8. Anticipating and monitoring potential unintended use
9. Promoting debate on neurotechnology among the public and stakeholders

The Recommendation is non-binding but establishes normative expectations for OECD member states including South Korea, the United States, and Germany.

### Council of Europe Strategic Action Plan (2023)

The Council of Europe's Committee on Bioethics (DH-BIO) launched a strategic action plan on neurotechnology and human rights in 2023, examining whether existing human rights instruments -- particularly the European Convention on Human Rights and the Oviedo Convention on Human Rights and Biomedicine (1997) -- adequately protect against neurotechnology-related threats to mental privacy, cognitive liberty, and personal identity.

The key question under examination is whether Article 8 ECHR (right to respect for private life) extends to protect "mental privacy" -- the right to keep one's thoughts, emotions, and cognitive processes free from unauthorized external access or manipulation. The Committee on Bioethics is developing recommendations for a possible additional protocol or new instrument.

### EU AI Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689)

The EU AI Act, adopted on June 13, 2024, addresses neurotechnology indirectly through its risk-based classification system:

- **Article 5(1)(a):** Prohibits AI systems that deploy "subliminal techniques beyond a person's consciousness" or "purposefully manipulative or deceptive techniques" to distort behavior, which could encompass certain neurotechnology applications.
- **Annex III High-Risk AI Systems:** Includes AI systems used in biometric identification and categorization, which may encompass neural data-based identification systems.
- **Recital 14:** Acknowledges that AI systems interacting with biological systems raise unique concerns requiring specific governance approaches.

### UNESCO International Bioethics Committee

The IBC published its report "The Ethics of Neurotechnology" in December 2021, recommending that states develop specific legal frameworks for neurotechnology governance addressing mental privacy, cognitive liberty (the right to alter one's own mental states), the right to mental integrity (protection against unauthorized manipulation), and psychological continuity (preservation of personal identity).

### Chile -- Constitutional Neurorights

While outside the four core jurisdictions, Chile's pioneering neurorights legislation warrants mention. On October 25, 2021, Chile became the first country to constitutionally protect neurorights through an amendment to Article 19 No. 1 of the Chilean Constitution, establishing that "scientific and technological development shall be at the service of people and shall be carried out with respect for life and physical and psychic integrity." The implementing law (Ley 21.383/2024) prohibits the sale, commercialization, and unauthorized use of brain data and establishes protections for mental privacy.

## Comparative Analysis

### Regulatory Maturity

Neurotechnology regulation is at an early stage across all jurisdictions. No jurisdiction has enacted a comprehensive neurotechnology governance statute addressing the full range of issues from medical devices to consumer products to neurodata privacy to enhancement applications. Colorado's SB 24-058 and Chile's constitutional amendment represent the furthest advances in neurodata privacy, but neither provides a complete regulatory framework.

### Medical vs. Consumer Regulation Gap

All four jurisdictions maintain robust regulatory frameworks for medical neurotechnology through their medical device approval systems (MFDS, FDA, MDR). However, a significant governance gap exists for consumer neurotechnology products -- EEG headsets, neurofeedback devices, brain-training applications -- that are marketed as wellness products rather than medical devices and therefore escape medical device regulation. The data collected by these consumer devices may be equally sensitive to medically collected neural data but receives no special protection in most jurisdictions.

### Data Protection Approaches

Germany and the EU provide the strongest existing protections for neural data through the GDPR's classification of health and biometric data as special categories requiring enhanced safeguards. The United States has no federal neural data protection, creating a significant governance gap that state-level legislation is beginning to address. South Korea's PIPA provides general personal data protection but does not recognize neural data as a distinct sensitive category.

### International Governance Architecture

The absence of a binding international treaty on neurotechnology governance creates a risk of regulatory fragmentation and competition. The OECD Recommendation provides normative guidance but lacks enforcement mechanisms. The Council of Europe's ongoing work may produce binding standards for its 46 member states, while the EU AI Act's provisions affect the world's largest single market.
