## Summary

Rioting -- the collective use of violence, intimidation, or destruction by a group of persons -- occupies a uniquely contested space in criminal law, sitting at the intersection of public order enforcement and the fundamental right to peaceful assembly. Every jurisdiction must strike a balance between deterring collective violence and protecting the democratic right to protest. South Korea's Criminal Act (Articles 115-116) treats rioting as a serious offense carrying up to ten years of imprisonment, reflecting the country's turbulent history of mass demonstrations and authoritarian crackdowns. The United States federalizes riot offenses through the Anti-Riot Act of 1968 (18 U.S.C. Sections 2101-2102), which criminalizes the use of interstate commerce facilities to incite or participate in a riot, while individual states maintain their own riot statutes with widely varying definitions and penalties. Germany's Strafgesetzbuch addresses rioting through the offense of Landfriedensbruch (Sections 125-125a), distinguishing between basic and aggravated forms with penalties ranging from three to ten years of imprisonment. The European Court of Human Rights has developed a substantial body of case law under Articles 10 and 11 of the Convention, establishing that freedom of assembly protects even disruptive protest and that individuals do not lose Convention protection merely because others in a demonstration turn violent.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea addresses rioting primarily through Chapter 8 of the Criminal Act (hyeongbeop), which deals with crimes against public peace (gongan-eul haehaneun joe). The two central provisions are Article 115 (Riot) and Article 116 (Failure to Disperse).

**Article 115 -- Riot (Soyo-joe):** Persons who assemble in large numbers and commit acts of violence, intimidation, or destruction shall be punished by imprisonment with or without labor for not less than one year and not more than ten years, or by a fine not exceeding 15 million won. The statute requires three elements: (1) assembly in large numbers, (2) commission of violence, intimidation, or destructive acts, and (3) a collective character to the conduct. Unlike many Western counterparts, the Korean provision does not specify a minimum number of participants, instead relying on the qualitative standard of "large numbers" (dasu), which courts have interpreted contextually.

**Article 116 -- Failure to Disperse (Bulhaesanje):** Persons who assemble in large numbers for the purpose of committing violence, intimidation, or destruction and fail to disperse after being ordered to do so three or more times by authorized officials shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than three years, a fine not exceeding five million won, detention, or a minor fine. This provision criminalizes continued assembly after lawful dispersal orders even if no violence has yet occurred, provided the assembly's purpose was violent.

**Assembly and Demonstration Act (Jiphoe mit Siwie Gwanhan Beomnyul):** This separate statute governs the procedural framework for public assemblies and demonstrations. Key provisions include a requirement for advance notification (48 hours) for outdoor assemblies, restrictions on assemblies near the National Assembly, courts, and foreign diplomatic missions, a historical prohibition on nighttime outdoor assemblies (sunset to sunrise) -- partially struck down by the Constitutional Court in 2009 (2008Hun-Ka25), and penalties of up to six months imprisonment or 500,000 won fine for violations of assembly restrictions.

**Article 136 -- Obstruction of Official Duties:** Protesters who physically resist police during demonstrations are frequently charged under Article 136, which carries penalties of up to five years imprisonment or a fine of up to ten million won.

|---------|-----------|---------|

| Failure to disperse | Criminal Act Art. 116 | Up to 3 years / up to 5 million won fine |

| Violation of Assembly Act | Assembly and Demonstration Act Art. 20 | Up to 6 months / up to 500,000 won fine |

### Key Principles

**Collective nature of the offense:** Korean courts require that the violence arise from the collective character of the assembly, not merely from individual acts occurring within a crowd. The Supreme Court has held that isolated acts of violence by individual participants do not automatically render the entire assembly a "riot" under Article 115.

**Nighttime assembly restrictions:** The Constitutional Court's 2009 decision in 2008Hun-Ka25 was a watershed moment, ruling that the blanket ban on all outdoor assemblies between sunset and sunrise was an unconstitutional infringement on the freedom of assembly guaranteed by Article 21 of the Constitution. The Court held that nighttime assemblies are not inherently more dangerous than daytime ones and that the government must use less restrictive means.

**Candlelight protest jurisprudence:** The massive candlelight protests of 2008 (against U.S. beef imports) and 2016-2017 (demanding President Park Geun-hye's impeachment) tested the boundaries of Korean riot law. During the 2008 protests, which extended over 100 days with millions participating, courts distinguished between peaceful candlelight vigil participants and those who committed specific acts of violence. The Seoul Central District Court convicted individuals only where specific violent acts could be attributed, such as the case of a participant sentenced to 18 months for assaulting police officers with a steel pipe.

**Police accountability:** In June 2018, the Seoul District Court convicted former Seoul riot police chief Shin Yoon-gyoon and two other officers of neglect of duty in connection with the death of farmer Baek Nam-gi, who sustained fatal injuries after being struck by a water cannon fired at close range and excessive pressure during a 2015 protest.

### Landmark Cases

- **2008 Candlelight Protests:** Mass demonstrations against U.S. beef imports resulted in selective prosecutions targeting individuals who committed identifiable acts of violence, establishing the principle that peaceful participation in a large assembly does not constitute rioting.
- **2016-2017 Candlelight Revolution:** Over 17 million cumulative participants across weekly demonstrations demanding President Park Geun-hye's removal. Remarkably few riot charges were filed, demonstrating that Korean authorities can accommodate massive peaceful protest within the existing legal framework.
- **Constitutional Court Decision 2008Hun-Ka25 (2009):** Struck down the blanket nighttime assembly ban, reinforcing that the freedom of assembly under Article 21 of the Constitution applies around the clock.
- **Baek Nam-gi Water Cannon Case (2018):** Established police accountability for excessive force during protest policing and confirmed that unlawful police conduct during demonstrations can constitute a criminal offense.

## United States

### Legal Framework

U.S. riot law operates at both the federal and state levels. The primary federal statute is the Anti-Riot Act of 1968, codified at 18 U.S.C. Sections 2101-2102, enacted as Title X of the Civil Rights Act of 1968. State riot laws vary enormously in their definitions, thresholds, and penalties.

**18 U.S.C. Section 2101 -- Riots:** This provision criminalizes the use of any facility of interstate or foreign commerce (including mail, telephone, radio, television, and the internet) with the intent to incite, organize, promote, encourage, or participate in a riot, or to commit any act of violence in furtherance of a riot, or to aid or abet such conduct. The statute requires an overt act in furtherance of the prohibited purpose. Penalties include fines and/or imprisonment for up to five years.

**18 U.S.C. Section 2102 -- Definitions:** A "riot" is defined as a public disturbance involving (1) an act or acts of violence by one or more persons part of an assemblage of three or more persons, which act or acts constitute a clear and present danger of, or result in, damage or injury to property or persons; or (2) a threat or threats of the commission of such violence where the threatened acts could be immediately executed. Importantly, "to incite a riot" is defined to include urging or instigating others to riot but expressly excludes "mere oral or written advocacy of ideas or expression of belief, not involving advocacy of any act or acts of violence."

| New York | Penal Law Section 240.05-240.06 | 4+ persons (riot in 2nd degree); 11+ (1st degree) | Up to 4 years (Class E felony, 1st degree) |

### Key Principles

**Interstate commerce nexus:** The federal Anti-Riot Act requires a connection to interstate or foreign commerce, reflecting Congress's reliance on the Commerce Clause for constitutional authority. Purely intrastate riots without any use of interstate communication or travel fall outside federal jurisdiction and are prosecuted under state law.

**First Amendment constraints:** The Anti-Riot Act includes an express carve-out protecting advocacy and expression of belief that does not involve advocacy of violence. Courts have required that any prosecution under the Act demonstrate specific intent to incite or participate in violence, not merely inflammatory speech. The statute's constitutionality was upheld in United States v. Dellinger (1972), arising from prosecutions of the "Chicago Seven" for their conduct during the 1968 Democratic National Convention.

**Clear and present danger standard:** The statutory definition of riot incorporates the "clear and present danger" language, requiring that acts of violence within the assemblage must constitute a genuine and proximate threat to persons or property, not merely a theoretical possibility.

**Selective enforcement concerns:** The Anti-Riot Act was invoked only four times between 1970 and 2018 but saw renewed use during the 2020 protests following the killing of George Floyd. Over 300 people faced federal charges for crimes committed during nationwide demonstrations, with more than 120 defendants pleading guilty or being convicted of federal crimes including rioting, arson, and conspiracy. Those sentenced received an average of approximately 27 months in prison.

### Landmark Cases

- **United States v. Dellinger (7th Cir., 1972):** Upheld the constitutionality of the Anti-Riot Act in prosecutions arising from the 1968 Democratic National Convention protests, establishing that Congress may criminalize the use of interstate commerce to incite riots without violating the First Amendment.
- **Brandenburg v. Ohio, 395 U.S. 444 (1969):** While not a riot case per se, the Supreme Court's "imminent lawless action" test provides the constitutional baseline for all incitement prosecutions, including those under riot statutes. Speech may only be criminalized where it is directed to inciting imminent lawless action and is likely to produce such action.
- **January 6, 2021 Capitol Breach:** While most defendants were charged under statutes other than the Anti-Riot Act (e.g., 18 U.S.C. Section 1512 obstruction, 18 U.S.C. Section 231 civil disorder, and 40 U.S.C. Section 5104 restricted building), the event generated the largest body of federal public disorder prosecutions in U.S. history, with over 1,200 defendants charged. Average sentences were approximately 48 days in jail, roughly 40 percent below federal sentencing guidelines.
- **George Floyd Protest Prosecutions (2020):** Federal authorities charged over 300 people in connection with summer 2020 protests, with at least 10 defendants receiving prison terms of five years or more, primarily for arson and destruction of property during demonstrations.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German riot law is codified in Sections 125 and 125a of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), under the chapter on crimes against public order (Straftaten gegen die oeffentliche Ordnung). The offense is known as Landfriedensbruch, literally "breach of the public peace."

**Section 125 StGB -- Landfriedensbruch (Breach of Public Peace):** This provision criminalizes three alternative forms of conduct committed from within a crowd (Menschenmenge) that collectively engages in acts of violence against persons or property or threats thereof: (1) committing acts of violence or threats as a participant in the crowd (Gewalttaeter), (2) inciting the crowd to commit such acts (Aufwiegler), or (3) participating in the crowd in a manner that supports the violence (Teilnehmer). The basic offense carries a penalty of up to three years of imprisonment or a fine. Critically, mere presence in a violent crowd is insufficient -- the prosecution must demonstrate active participation in or support of the violent acts.

**Section 125a StGB -- Aggravated Landfriedensbruch (Besonders schwerer Fall):** This provision addresses especially serious cases and imposes imprisonment from six months to ten years. Aggravating factors include carrying a weapon or other dangerous instrument with the intent to use it during the riot, placing another person in danger of death or serious bodily injury through violence, committing plunder (Pluenderung), and causing significant damage to another person's property.

**Versammlungsgesetz (Assembly Act):** Germany's federal Assembly Act (and its state-level equivalents following federalism reform in 2006) protects the right of peaceful assembly under Article 8 of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz). The Act distinguishes between peaceful assemblies (which enjoy full constitutional protection) and assemblies that become violent (which may be dissolved). Key provisions include a notification requirement for outdoor assemblies (typically 48 hours), authority for police to dissolve assemblies that pose an immediate danger to public safety, a prohibition on weapons and protective equipment at assemblies (Vermummungsverbot -- the ban on face coverings), and criminal penalties for organizing or participating in prohibited assemblies.

| Offense | Provision | Penalty |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Simple Landfriedensbruch | Section 125 StGB | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine |
| Aggravated Landfriedensbruch | Section 125a StGB | 6 months to 10 years imprisonment |
| Violation of assembly ban | Versammlungsgesetz Section 26 | Up to 1 year imprisonment or fine |
| Wearing face coverings at assembly | Versammlungsgesetz Section 27(2) | Up to 1 year imprisonment or fine |
| Carrying weapons at assembly | Versammlungsgesetz Section 27(1) | Up to 1 year imprisonment or fine |

### Key Principles

**Three forms of participation:** German law distinguishes between the Gewalttaeter (person committing violence), the Aufwiegler (person inciting violence), and the Teilnehmer (person supporting violence through participation). Each form carries the same maximum penalty under Section 125 but requires different evidentiary showings.

**Crowd requirement (Menschenmenge):** The offense requires that the acts occur within or from a crowd. German courts have generally interpreted "crowd" to mean a group sufficiently large that it cannot be immediately surveyed or controlled, though no fixed minimum number has been established. The crowd must collectively engage in violence or threats.

**Vermummungsverbot (masking ban):** German assembly law prohibits participants from wearing face coverings or carrying items designed to prevent identification during demonstrations. Violations are independently criminal and can also serve as evidence of intent in Landfriedensbruch prosecutions. In May 2025, the European Court of Human Rights ruled in a case against Germany that convicting a protester solely for wearing a plastic visor violated freedom of assembly under Article 11 ECHR, signaling limits on the masking ban's application.

**Proportionality under the Basic Law:** Article 8 of the Grundgesetz guarantees the right to assemble peacefully and without weapons. The Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) has repeatedly emphasized that this right must be interpreted broadly and that restrictions must satisfy strict proportionality analysis. In its landmark Brokdorf decision (BVerfGE 69, 315, 1985), the Court held that the right to demonstrate is essential to a functioning democracy and that the state bears a duty to facilitate peaceful assemblies rather than suppress them at the first sign of disorder.

### Landmark Cases

- **Brokdorf Decision (BVerfGE 69, 315, 1985):** The Federal Constitutional Court established that the right to demonstrate is a fundamental pillar of democratic society and imposed a "friendliness toward assemblies" (Versammlungsfreundlichkeit) obligation on the state, requiring authorities to use cooperation and de-escalation before resorting to bans or dispersal.
- **G20 Hamburg Summit Prosecutions (2017-2024):** The violent clashes during the July 2017 G20 summit in Hamburg generated approximately 3,580 criminal investigations. Notable convictions included a Dutch man sentenced to two and a half years imprisonment and a French citizen sentenced to three years for inciting arson, causing bodily harm, and assaulting police. Some proceedings were later dropped, and the extended prosecution timeline drew criticism regarding proportionality and the right to a speedy trial.
- **ECHR Visor Case (2025):** The European Court of Human Rights ruled that Germany violated Article 11 of the Convention by convicting a protester for wearing a plastic visor at a demonstration, finding the measure disproportionate to any legitimate public order aim.

## ECHR & EU Framework

### Legal Framework

The European Convention on Human Rights does not contain a criminal prohibition on rioting. Instead, it establishes a rights-based framework that constrains how member states may define, prosecute, and punish riot-related conduct. The two primary provisions are Article 10 (freedom of expression) and Article 11 (freedom of assembly and association).

**Article 11 -- Freedom of Assembly:** Everyone has the right to freedom of peaceful assembly. This right may be restricted only where prescribed by law and necessary in a democratic society in the interests of national security or public safety, for the prevention of disorder or crime, for the protection of health or morals, or for the protection of the rights and freedoms of others. The Court has interpreted "peaceful assembly" broadly, holding that it encompasses conduct that may annoy, disturb, or offend.

**Article 10 -- Freedom of Expression:** Protects expression in the context of protests and demonstrations, including symbolic speech and protest art. Restrictions must satisfy the same tripartite test of legality, legitimate aim, and necessity in a democratic society.

**Article 3 -- Prohibition of Torture:** Relevant to riot policing, this absolute prohibition bars the use of disproportionate force by law enforcement during crowd control operations. The Court has found violations where police use of water cannons, rubber bullets, or batons against protesters crossed the threshold into inhuman or degrading treatment.

**Article 5 -- Right to Liberty:** Governs the legality of detention during and after public disorder situations, including mass arrests and the practice of "kettling" (police containment of crowds).

**EU Charter of Fundamental Rights:** Article 12 of the EU Charter mirrors Article 11 ECHR, guaranteeing freedom of assembly and association. The Charter binds EU institutions and member states when implementing EU law, providing an additional layer of protection.

### Key Principles

**Individual responsibility in collective action:** The Court has consistently held that an individual does not lose the protection of Article 11 merely because others in the same demonstration engage in violence, provided the individual remains peaceful in intent and behavior. This principle, established in Ezelin v. France (1991) and reinforced in Kudrevicius and Others v. Lithuania (2015), prevents states from applying collective punishment to all participants in a demonstration that turns partially violent.

**Proportionality of penalties:** Even where a conviction for riot-related conduct is justified, the nature and severity of the penalty must be proportionate to the aim pursued. The Court has found violations where penalties were disproportionately harsh relative to the individual's actual conduct, particularly in cases involving peaceful protesters convicted for participation in assemblies that became disorderly.

**State obligation to facilitate assembly:** Member states bear a positive obligation to take reasonable steps to enable peaceful assemblies to take place without participants fearing physical violence from counter-demonstrators. This includes providing adequate policing and protecting demonstrators from hostile third parties.

**Tolerance of disruption:** The Court has established that authorities must show "a certain degree of tolerance" toward disruptive but peaceful protest. In Kudrevicius v. Lithuania, the Grand Chamber held that a roadblock by farmers, while disruptive, fell within the scope of Article 11 protection and that the criminal convictions of the organizers were disproportionate.

**Police use of force standards:** Under Articles 2 and 3, the Court requires that force used to disperse demonstrations or control riots must be strictly proportionate to the threat. In Giuliani and Gaggio v. Italy (2011), concerning the death of a protester during the G8 summit in Genoa, the Grand Chamber assessed the legality of lethal force used by a police officer during violent clashes, ultimately finding no violation of Article 2 given the extreme circumstances but emphasizing the obligation to conduct an effective investigation.

### Landmark Cases

- **Ezelin v. France (1991):** A lawyer who participated in a demonstration was disciplined by French authorities for failing to "disassociate" himself from violent acts committed by others. The Court held unanimously that this violated Article 11, establishing the foundational principle that individuals cannot be penalized merely for being present at a protest that turns violent if they did not personally engage in violence.
- **Kudrevicius and Others v. Lithuania (2015):** The Grand Chamber's first comprehensive judgment on freedom of peaceful assembly addressed farmers who blocked highways in protest. The Court found that while disruption was significant, the criminal convictions were disproportionate, emphasizing the duty of tolerance toward disruptive protest.
- **Austin and Others v. United Kingdom (2012):** The Grand Chamber assessed the practice of "kettling" -- police containment of a crowd for approximately seven hours during May Day 2001 demonstrations in London. The Court found no violation of Article 5, holding that containment was the "least intrusive and most effective" crowd control measure available, though three judges dissented.
- **Giuliani and Gaggio v. Italy (2011):** Arising from the death of Carlo Giuliani during the 2001 G8 Genoa summit, the Grand Chamber found no violation of Article 2 (right to life) regarding the shooting itself given the context of extreme violence, but scrutinized the adequacy of the subsequent investigation.
- **Yaroslav Belousov v. Russia (2016):** The Court found that Russia violated the rights of a protester arrested during the 2012 Bolotnaya Square demonstrations in Moscow, holding that the criminal prosecution and pre-trial detention were disproportionate and constituted an interference with freedom of assembly.
- **ECHR v. Germany -- Visor Case (2025):** Found that convicting a protester solely for wearing a plastic visor at a demonstration violated Article 11, setting limits on Germany's Vermummungsverbot as applied to passive protective equipment.

## Comparative Analysis

### Definition and Scope

The four jurisdictions reveal fundamentally different approaches to defining rioting. South Korea employs a broad, qualitative standard requiring "large numbers" engaged in collective violence, without specifying a minimum group size. The United States sets the lowest numerical threshold at three persons but requires a nexus to interstate commerce for federal jurisdiction. Germany requires a "crowd" (Menschenmenge) engaged in collective violence but leaves the minimum size to judicial interpretation. The ECHR, rather than defining rioting, evaluates whether state responses to collective disorder comply with Convention rights.

### Individual vs. Collective Liability

The tension between individual culpability and collective responsibility represents the most significant divergence across jurisdictions. The ECHR framework most strongly protects individual participants, holding through Ezelin and Kudrevicius that individuals cannot be punished for the violent acts of others in the same assembly. German law requires proof of active participation in or support of violence, though the "Teilnehmer" category creates some ambiguity. South Korean law focuses on collective character but courts have increasingly required attribution of specific acts. U.S. federal law requires an individual overt act but state laws vary, with some permitting prosecution for mere presence at a riot after a dispersal order.

### Penalties

South Korea imposes the most severe basic penalty for rioting (one to ten years of imprisonment), reflecting the historical significance of mass disorder in Korean political history. Germany's aggravated Landfriedensbruch (six months to ten years) approaches Korean severity for serious cases. The U.S. federal maximum of five years is moderate by comparison, though state penalties for aggravated riot can reach similar levels. The ECHR does not impose penalties directly but constrains member state penalties through proportionality review.

### Assembly Rights Protection

All four jurisdictions recognize freedom of assembly as a fundamental right, but the degree of protection varies. Germany's Brokdorf decision imposes the most affirmative state duty to facilitate assemblies. The ECHR's "tolerance of disruption" doctrine provides the broadest protection for disruptive protest. South Korea's legal framework has evolved significantly, with the 2009 Constitutional Court decision on nighttime assemblies marking a shift toward stronger protection. The U.S. First Amendment provides robust protection for expressive conduct but permits content-neutral time, place, and manner restrictions.

### Police Use of Force

The ECHR framework provides the most developed standards for police conduct during public disorder. The absolute prohibition on inhuman treatment under Article 3, combined with the right to life under Article 2, creates enforceable standards that have led to findings of state liability. South Korea's Baek Nam-gi case demonstrates judicial willingness to hold police accountable. German law subjects police to proportionality requirements under the Basic Law. U.S. standards are governed by the Fourth Amendment's "objective reasonableness" test from Graham v. Connor (1989), which affords police significant discretion.
