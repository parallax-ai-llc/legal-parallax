## Summary

Pyramid schemes and Ponzi schemes represent some of the most persistent forms of financial fraud, causing billions of dollars in losses to consumers worldwide. The legal challenge lies in distinguishing legitimate multi-level marketing (MLM) from illegal pyramid structures, defining the threshold at which recruitment-based compensation becomes fraudulent, and imposing effective criminal and civil penalties. South Korea regulates pyramid schemes primarily through the Act on Door-to-Door Sales, etc. (방문판매 등에 관한 법률), which establishes detailed criteria for distinguishing lawful multi-level marketing (다단계판매) from illegal pyramid sales schemes (피라미드판매), and the Criminal Act provisions on fraud (사기죄, Article 347), with the Fair Trade Commission (공정거래위원회) exercising primary enforcement authority. The United States addresses pyramid schemes through Federal Trade Commission Act Section 5 (15 U.S.C. Section 45), wire fraud (18 U.S.C. Section 1343), securities fraud statutes, and state anti-pyramid scheme laws, with the critical legal test established in FTC v. Koscot Interplanetary, Inc. (86 F.T.C. 1106, 1975) distinguishing pyramids from legitimate MLM based on whether participants' income derives primarily from recruitment rather than retail sales. Germany criminalizes pyramid schemes under Section 16(2) of the Act Against Unfair Competition (Gesetz gegen den unlauteren Wettbewerb, UWG) and regulates multi-level marketing through the Commercial Code (HGB) and trade regulation law, while Ponzi schemes are prosecuted as fraud under Section 263 StGB. The EU framework includes the Unfair Commercial Practices Directive (2005/29/EC), which lists pyramid promotional schemes in Annex I as commercial practices that are unfair in all circumstances, and the Anti-Money Laundering Directives that address the proceeds of pyramid fraud.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's regulation of pyramid schemes and multi-level marketing is primarily governed by the **Act on Door-to-Door Sales, etc.** (방문판매 등에 관한 법률, hereinafter "Door-to-Door Sales Act" or DDSA), which was substantially amended in 2012 and 2018 to strengthen protections against pyramid fraud.

### Multi-Level Marketing (다단계판매) Regulation

The DDSA establishes a comprehensive regulatory framework for lawful multi-level marketing:

**Article 2(5) (Definition of Multi-Level Marketing):** Multi-level marketing means a sales method in which a company sells goods or services through a network of distributors organized in multiple levels, where distributors receive compensation based on both their own sales and the sales of distributors they recruit.

**Article 13 (Registration Requirement):** Multi-level marketing companies must register with the Fair Trade Commission, providing detailed business plans, compensation structures, and capital adequacy documentation. Unregistered MLM activity is punishable by up to three years imprisonment.

**Article 15 (Prohibited Acts):** MLM companies are prohibited from:

1. Requiring participants to purchase excessive inventory (강매 금지).
2. Promising unrealistic returns or income.
3. Imposing unreasonable conditions for withdrawal or refund.
4. Making recruitment compensation the primary source of participant income.

**Article 17 (Cooling-Off Period and Refund Rights):** Consumers/participants have a 14-day cooling-off period to withdraw from purchases, and a 3-month window to return unsold inventory for at least 90% refund.

### Pyramid Sales Schemes (피라미드판매)

**Article 24-2 (Prohibition of Pyramid Sales Schemes):** Defines and prohibits pyramid schemes as schemes characterized by:

1. **Recruitment-Based Revenue:** More than 50% of total payments received by the organization derive from participants' entry fees, membership fees, or mandatory product purchases rather than from retail sales to non-participant consumers.
2. **No Genuine Product or Service:** The goods or services sold have minimal intrinsic value relative to their price, serving primarily as a pretext for collecting recruitment fees.
3. **Unsustainable Structure:** The compensation plan requires continuous recruitment to sustain payments to existing participants.

**Penalties (Article 58):**

| Offense | Criminal Penalty | Administrative Penalty |
|---------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Operating a pyramid scheme | Up to 7 years imprisonment or fine up to KRW 200 million | Business suspension; corrective order |
| Promoting/recruiting for a pyramid scheme | Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine up to KRW 150 million | Administrative fine up to KRW 100 million |
| Unregistered MLM operation | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine up to KRW 100 million | Business closure order |
| Fraud (Criminal Act Art. 347) | Up to 10 years imprisonment or fine up to KRW 20 million | N/A |

### Notable Cases

**JU-JU Group Case (주주그룹, 2006):** One of Korea's largest pyramid scheme prosecutions. JU-JU Group operated an investment scheme promising 300% annual returns from foreign exchange trading, collecting approximately KRW 4 trillion (USD 4 billion) from over 100,000 victims. The founders were convicted of fraud under the Criminal Act and violations of the DDSA, receiving sentences of up to 15 years imprisonment.

**Meritz Securities Ponzi Scheme (2011):** A broker at Meritz Securities operated a Ponzi scheme collecting KRW 51 billion from 200 investors over five years. Prosecuted under the Financial Investment Services and Capital Markets Act (자본시장과 금융투자업에 관한 법률) and the Criminal Act.

**Digital Currency Pyramid Schemes (2021-2023):** The rise of cryptocurrency-based pyramid schemes prompted the FTC to issue specific guidance. The collapse of the **Terraform/Luna ecosystem (2022)**, while primarily a cryptocurrency failure, led to criminal fraud charges against founder Do Kwon under the Capital Markets Act, with prosecutors alleging elements of a pyramid structure in the marketing of LUNA tokens.

### Fair Trade Commission Enforcement

The FTC exercises primary administrative enforcement:

- Conducts investigations based on complaints or ex officio.
- Issues corrective orders, business suspension orders, and administrative fines.
- Refers criminal cases to prosecutors.
- Maintains a public registry of authorized MLM companies and a blacklist of fraudulent schemes.

In 2023, the FTC investigated 45 suspected pyramid schemes and took enforcement action against 28, imposing total administrative fines of KRW 12.3 billion.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States regulates pyramid schemes through a combination of federal agency enforcement (FTC, SEC, DOJ), state consumer protection laws, and private litigation.

### The Koscot/Amway Framework

The legal distinction between lawful MLM and illegal pyramid schemes was established by two foundational FTC decisions:

**FTC v. Koscot Interplanetary, Inc. (86 F.T.C. 1106, 1975):** The FTC established that a pyramid scheme exists where:

1. Participants make a payment to the company.
2. In exchange for which they receive the right to sell a product (or service).
3. **The right to receive in return for recruiting other participants** into the program rewards that are unrelated to the sale of the product to ultimate users.

The critical element is whether participant rewards derive primarily from **recruitment** rather than **retail sales to non-participant consumers**.

**In re Amway Corp. (93 F.T.C. 618, 1979):** The FTC found that Amway's MLM structure was not an illegal pyramid because it incorporated three safeguards:

1. **Retail Sales Rule:** Distributors had to sell at least 70% of their inventory to non-distributor customers before ordering more.
2. **10-Customer Rule:** Distributors had to make retail sales to at least 10 different customers per month.
3. **Buy-Back Policy:** Amway repurchased any unsold, marketable inventory from departing distributors at 90% of the original cost.

These "Amway safeguards" became the de facto standard for MLM compliance, though subsequent enforcement actions have demonstrated they can be circumvented.

### FTC Enforcement

**FTC v. Herbalife International of America, Inc. (Case No. 2:16-cv-05217, C.D. Cal., 2016):** The FTC settled its investigation of Herbalife -- one of the world's largest MLM companies -- for $200 million and a comprehensive business restructure. While the FTC did not formally designate Herbalife a pyramid scheme, the consent order required Herbalife to restructure its compensation plan to ensure rewards are based on retail sales rather than recruitment, and to track and verify retail sales to non-participant consumers.

**FTC v. Vemma Nutrition Company (Case No. 2:15-cv-01578, D. Ariz., 2015):** The court found that Vemma was an illegal pyramid scheme where 73% of participants earned less than they paid, new recruit purchases constituted the majority of revenue, and participant income was primarily linked to recruitment volume rather than retail sales. The court ordered disgorgement of $238 million.

**FTC v. AdvoCare International (2019):** AdvoCare agreed to pay $150 million and convert from MLM to single-level direct selling after the FTC alleged it operated as a pyramid scheme where 72% of distributors made no income.

### Federal Criminal Prosecution

Pyramid and Ponzi schemes are prosecuted federally under:

- **Wire Fraud (18 U.S.C. Section 1343):** Up to 20 years imprisonment per count; covers schemes that use electronic communications.
- **Mail Fraud (18 U.S.C. Section 1341):** Up to 20 years imprisonment per count.
- **Securities Fraud (15 U.S.C. Section 78j(b); SEC Rule 10b-5):** Where the scheme involves the offer of securities (investment contracts under the Howey test from SEC v. W.J. Howey Co., 328 U.S. 293, 1946).
- **Money Laundering (18 U.S.C. Sections 1956-1957):** Laundering the proceeds of fraud.

**United States v. Bernard Madoff:** The most notorious Ponzi scheme in history, with estimated losses of $64.8 billion (principal invested: approximately $17.5 billion). Madoff was sentenced to 150 years imprisonment in 2009 for securities fraud, wire fraud, money laundering, and other charges. The Irving Picard trustee has recovered approximately $14.7 billion (84% of allowed claims) through clawback litigation as of 2025.

### State Anti-Pyramid Laws

Most states have specific anti-pyramid scheme statutes:

- **California Business and Professions Code Section 327:** Defines and prohibits "endless chain" schemes; violation is a felony punishable by imprisonment for 16 months, 2 years, or 3 years.
- **New York General Business Law Section 359-fff:** Prohibits "chain distributor" schemes; misdemeanor for first offense, felony for subsequent.
- **Texas Business and Commerce Code Chapter 17 (DTPA):** Authorizes treble damages for victims of deceptive pyramid scheme promotions.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany addresses pyramid schemes through the Act Against Unfair Competition (UWG), criminal fraud provisions (StGB Section 263), and trade regulation law.

### UWG Section 16(2) -- Progressive Customer Solicitation (Progressives Kundenfangsystem)

**Section 16(2) UWG:** "Whoever, in the course of trade, induces consumers to purchase goods, services, or rights by promising them special advantages if they in turn persuade other consumers to conclude similar transactions, who in turn are promised the same advantages for further solicitation, shall be punished by imprisonment of up to two years or a fine."

Key elements:

- The scheme must operate in the course of trade (geschaeftlicher Verkehr).
- It must promise consumers advantages (typically financial) contingent on recruiting further consumers.
- The recruitment chain must be designed to continue progressively (progressiv).
- The advantage must be conditional on others making similar transactions.

**Section 16(2) UWG** is a criminal provision (Straftatbestand), not merely a regulatory prohibition. Prosecution requires referral to the state prosecutor's office. However, the maximum penalty of two years allows only suspended sentences, which has been criticized as insufficient for large-scale pyramid fraud.

### Fraud Under Section 263 StGB

Large-scale pyramid and Ponzi schemes are additionally (or alternatively) prosecuted as fraud:

**Section 263 StGB (Betrug):**
- Basic offense: imprisonment up to five years or fine.
- Aggravated cases (Section 263(3)): imprisonment of six months to ten years -- applicable where the perpetrator acts commercially (gewerbsmaessig) or as a member of a gang, or causes losses of a "large extent" (grosses Ausmass, interpreted as exceeding EUR 50,000).

**Ponzi schemes** (Schneeballsystem in German) are prosecuted under Section 263 because the payment of returns to earlier investors using later investors' funds constitutes a deception (Taeuschung) about the existence and profitability of the underlying investment.

### Notable German Cases

**European Kings Club (1994):** One of Germany's largest pyramid schemes, collecting an estimated DM 1 billion (approximately EUR 500 million) from investors in Germany, Austria, and Switzerland. Founders Damara Bertges and Hans Guenter Spachtholz were convicted of fraud and sentenced to imprisonment. The case led to enhanced cross-border enforcement cooperation within the EU.

**S&K Group (2013):** Frankfurt-based real estate investment fraud collecting approximately EUR 240 million from 11,000 investors. Prosecuted as aggravated fraud (Section 263(3) StGB); founders sentenced to eight and a half years imprisonment. The Federal Court of Justice (BGH) upheld the conviction in **BGH 1 StR 508/16 (2018)**, confirming that Ponzi-structured investment schemes constitute criminal fraud.

**OneCoin (2017-2023):** The cryptocurrency Ponzi scheme OneCoin, founded by Ruja Ignatova (the "Cryptoqueen"), had significant German operations. German prosecutors in Bielefeld convicted multiple OneCoin promoters under Section 263 StGB and Section 16(2) UWG. Total estimated global losses exceeded $4 billion.

### Multi-Level Marketing Regulation

Lawful MLM in Germany must comply with:

- **Trade Registration (Gewerbeanmeldung):** MLM operators must register their trade with the local Gewerbeamt.
- **Commercial Code (HGB):** MLM distributors may be classified as commercial agents (Handelsvertreter, HGB Section 84) or independent contractors, with corresponding legal protections including the right to compensation for goodwill upon termination (Ausgleichsanspruch, HGB Section 89b).
- **UWG Sections 3-7:** General prohibitions on unfair commercial practices, aggressive commercial practices, and misleading advertising.
- **Transparency Requirements:** The Preisangabenverordnung (Price Indication Regulation) requires clear disclosure of all costs to participants.

### BaFin Oversight

Where pyramid or Ponzi schemes involve the acceptance of funds constituting banking business (Einlagengeschaeft) or the offer of securities, the **Federal Financial Supervisory Authority (BaFin)** has jurisdiction:

- **Section 32 KWG (Banking Act):** Conducting banking business without authorization is a criminal offense punishable by up to five years imprisonment.
- BaFin may issue cease-and-desist orders, apply to courts for asset freezes, and refer matters to prosecutors.

## ECHR & EU

### European Convention on Human Rights

The ECHR is primarily engaged in pyramid scheme contexts through the protection of property rights and the right to a fair trial:

**Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (Protection of Property):**
States have a positive obligation to maintain a regulatory and enforcement framework that protects citizens' property from fraudulent schemes. In **Zolotas v. Greece (No. 2) (Application No. 66610/09, June 29, 2013)**, the Court recognized that the state's failure to adequately regulate financial institutions may engage property protection obligations.

**Article 6 (Right to a Fair Trial):**
Victims of pyramid schemes have the right to access courts for civil remedies. Excessive delays in criminal proceedings against pyramid scheme operators, or in civil recovery actions, may violate Article 6. In **Stran Greek Refineries and Stratis Andreadis v. Greece (Application No. 13427/87, December 9, 1994)**, the Court found that state interference with court-ordered compensation constituted a violation of both Article 6 and Article 1 of Protocol No. 1.

### Unfair Commercial Practices Directive (2005/29/EC)

The UCPD is the primary EU instrument addressing pyramid schemes:

**Annex I, No. 14:** Lists as a commercial practice that is "in all circumstances considered unfair":

> "Establishing, operating or promoting a pyramid promotional scheme where a consumer gives consideration for the opportunity to receive compensation that is derived primarily from the introduction of other consumers into the scheme rather than from the sale or consumption of products."

Key elements of the EU definition:

1. **Consumer gives consideration:** The participant must pay money or provide other value.
2. **Opportunity to receive compensation:** The scheme promises financial rewards.
3. **Derived primarily from introduction of others:** The compensation is primarily based on recruitment rather than product sales.
4. **Rather than from sale or consumption of products:** Genuine product sales to end consumers are the distinguishing factor.

The UCPD's "blacklist" approach means that pyramid schemes are per se unfair, without the need for case-by-case assessment of unfairness. This has been transposed into all EU member state legal systems.

### EU Anti-Money Laundering Framework

The proceeds of pyramid schemes constitute criminal property subject to the Anti-Money Laundering Directives:

- **Directive (EU) 2024/1640 (6th AMLD):** Requires member states to criminalize money laundering of proceeds from all serious crimes, including fraud.
- **Regulation (EU) 2024/1624 (AMLR):** Establishes a single EU rulebook for AML/CFT, including customer due diligence requirements that may help detect pyramid scheme proceeds flowing through the financial system.
- **Anti-Money Laundering Authority (AMLA):** The new EU-level authority, operational from 2025, will directly supervise certain financial entities and coordinate national investigations.

### Cross-Border Enforcement

Pyramid schemes frequently operate across borders, creating enforcement challenges:

- **European Investigation Order (Directive 2014/41/EU):** Enables judicial authorities in one member state to request investigative measures in another, facilitating cross-border pyramid scheme investigations.
- **Eurojust:** Coordinates criminal prosecutions involving pyramid schemes operating in multiple member states. Eurojust facilitated the multinational investigation of the OneCoin scheme.
- **European Consumer Protection Cooperation Network (CPC):** Regulation (EU) 2017/2394 enables consumer protection authorities to cooperate in cross-border enforcement actions against unfair commercial practices, including pyramid schemes.

### Directive on Representative Actions (2020/1828/EU)

Effective since June 25, 2023, this directive enables qualified entities to bring representative actions on behalf of groups of consumers against traders engaging in unfair practices, including pyramid schemes. This provides a EU-wide mechanism for collective redress that was previously unavailable in many member states.

## Comparative Analysis

### The MLM/Pyramid Distinction

All four jurisdictions struggle with the fundamental challenge of distinguishing legitimate MLM from illegal pyramids:

- **South Korea** uses a quantitative threshold (50% of revenue from recruitment) combined with qualitative factors.
- **The United States** applies the Koscot/Amway framework, focusing on whether rewards derive primarily from recruitment vs. retail sales.
- **Germany** criminalizes progressive customer solicitation where rewards are contingent on ongoing recruitment.
- **The EU** UCPD definition focuses on compensation "derived primarily from the introduction of other consumers."

The convergence around the "primarily from recruitment" test is notable, though the precise threshold remains ambiguous across all jurisdictions.

### Penalty Severity

Significant divergence exists in penalty severity:

- **The United States** imposes the harshest penalties, with wire fraud carrying up to 20 years per count and securities fraud up to 20 years, as illustrated by Madoff's 150-year sentence.
- **South Korea** imposes up to 7 years under the DDSA and up to 10 years for fraud.
- **Germany's** UWG Section 16(2) maximum of 2 years is widely criticized as insufficient, though the StGB fraud provisions allow up to 10 years for aggravated cases.
- **The EU** leaves penalties to member states but requires "effective, proportionate and dissuasive" sanctions.

### Victim Recovery

Victim recovery mechanisms vary significantly:

- The United States has the most developed receivership and disgorgement framework, as illustrated by the Madoff trustee's recovery of 84% of allowed claims.
- South Korea provides civil remedies and FTC-ordered disgorgement but victim recovery rates are lower.
- Germany relies primarily on insolvency proceedings and civil litigation, with limited government compensation funds.
- The EU's Representative Actions Directive may improve collective recovery but is still in early implementation.
