## Summary

Genetic privacy -- the right of individuals to control the collection, use, storage, and disclosure of their genetic information, encompassing DNA sequences, genomic data, genetic test results, family genetic history, and biologically derived information that can reveal predispositions to disease, ancestry, behavioral traits, and familial relationships -- has become one of the most consequential privacy challenges of the genomic era, as rapid advances in sequencing technology, the proliferation of direct-to-consumer (DTC) genetic testing services, and the expanding use of genetic data in law enforcement, insurance, employment, and research have created risks of genetic discrimination, surveillance, and unauthorized familial identification that existing privacy frameworks were not designed to address. South Korea regulates genetic privacy primarily through the Bioethics and Safety Act (생명윤리 및 안전에 관한 법률), which governs genetic testing, genetic information storage, and biobank operation, requiring informed consent for genetic testing (Article 46), prohibiting genetic discrimination in employment and insurance (Article 46-2), and mandating Institutional Review Board (IRB) approval for research use of genetic samples; the Personal Information Protection Act (개인정보 보호법, PIPA) classifies genetic data as sensitive information (민감정보) under Article 23, requiring explicit consent for processing. The United States enacted the Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act (GINA, 42 U.S.C. Section 2000ff) in 2008, prohibiting genetic discrimination in health insurance and employment, but GINA does not cover life insurance, disability insurance, or long-term care insurance; the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) protects genetic information held by covered entities, and state laws including the California Genetic Information Privacy Act (GIPA, SB 41, 2022) and the Illinois Genetic Information Privacy Act (GIPA, 2024) provide additional protections; forensic genetic genealogy using consumer DNA databases has raised significant Fourth Amendment concerns, highlighted by the Golden State Killer case (2018). Germany protects genetic privacy through the Gendiagnostikgesetz (GenDG, Genetic Diagnosis Act, 2009), which is one of the world's most comprehensive genetic privacy statutes, prohibiting genetic testing by employers (Section 19) and insurers (Section 18), requiring genetic counseling before and after predictive genetic testing (Sections 10-11), restricting prenatal genetic testing to medical purposes (Section 15), and mandating destruction of genetic samples after testing; the GDPR classifies genetic data as a special category of personal data (Article 9) requiring explicit consent or specific legal authorization for processing. At the European level, the GDPR's classification of genetic data as special category data (Article 9(1)), the EU Clinical Trials Regulation 536/2014's provisions on genetic research, the Council of Europe's Convention on Human Rights and Biomedicine (Oviedo Convention, 1997) Article 12 on predictive genetic testing, and the European Court of Human Rights' landmark ruling in S. and Marper v. United Kingdom (2008) establishing that indefinite retention of DNA profiles of unconvicted persons violates Article 8 ECHR, collectively establish the most protective genetic privacy framework globally.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's genetic privacy framework operates through the intersection of bioethics legislation and general data protection law.

**Bioethics and Safety Act (생명윤리 및 안전에 관한 법률):** Enacted in 2005 and substantially amended in 2012 and 2020, this statute provides the primary framework for genetic testing, biobanking, and genetic research.

- **Article 46 (Genetic Testing):** Genetic testing may only be performed by licensed medical institutions and authorized testing agencies. The person to be tested must provide written informed consent after receiving information about the purpose, method, and implications of the test.
- **Article 46-2 (Prohibition of Genetic Discrimination):** No person shall be discriminated against in education, employment, promotion, insurance, or other social activities on the basis of genetic information. This provision was added by the 2012 amendment.
- **Article 47 (Biobank Regulation):** The collection, storage, and provision of human biological materials for research requires approval from the National Bioethics Committee (국가생명윤리심의위원회) and informed consent from donors.
- **Article 49 (Destruction of Genetic Samples):** Genetic samples must be destroyed after the purpose of testing or research has been fulfilled, unless the donor consents to continued storage.

**Personal Information Protection Act (개인정보 보호법, PIPA) Article 23:** Classifies genetic information (유전정보) as "sensitive information" (민감정보), which may only be processed with the data subject's explicit (separate) consent or specific legal authorization. The 2023 amendment strengthened protections for sensitive information including genetic data.

**Act on the Use and Protection of DNA Identification Information (DNA신원확인정보의 이용 및 보호에 관한 법률):** Enacted in 2010, this statute establishes the legal framework for the National DNA Database (국가 DNA 데이터베이스) used by law enforcement. DNA samples may be collected from persons convicted of designated serious offenses, including sex crimes, murder, and robbery. The DNA database is managed by the Supreme Prosecutors' Office.

### DTC Genetic Testing

The Korean DTC genetic testing market has grown rapidly, with companies including Macrogen, Clinomics, and Genoplan offering consumer genomic services. The Ministry of Health and Welfare has expanded the scope of permissible DTC genetic testing through regulatory amendments, allowing non-medical wellness-oriented testing (disease susceptibility excluded) without physician involvement. In 2022, the permitted DTC test categories were expanded to include 70 items covering ancestry, nutrition, skin characteristics, and exercise response.

### Biobank Korea

The National Biobank of Korea (국립중앙인체자원은행), operated by the Korea National Institute of Health (KNIH), collects and stores biological samples from approximately 300,000 participants for genomic research. The Biobank operates under strict ethical oversight, with IRB review and re-consent requirements for new research uses of stored samples.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States provides genetic privacy protections through a combination of federal and state statutes, with significant gaps in coverage.

**Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act (GINA, 42 U.S.C. Section 2000ff, 2008):** GINA prohibits genetic discrimination in two domains:

- **Title I (Health Insurance):** Prohibits health insurers from using genetic information to deny coverage, adjust premiums, or impose pre-existing condition exclusions. Genetic information includes genetic test results, family medical history, and participation in genetic research.
- **Title II (Employment):** Prohibits employers with 15 or more employees from using genetic information in hiring, firing, promotion, or other employment decisions. Employers may not request, require, or purchase genetic information about employees or their family members, with narrow exceptions for wellness programs, inadvertent acquisition, and FMLA compliance.

**GINA Gaps:** GINA does not cover life insurance, disability insurance, long-term care insurance, or educational institutions. An estimated 10 percent of individuals who receive positive genetic test results have reported genetic discrimination in these uncovered areas.

**HIPAA (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act, 1996):** The HIPAA Privacy Rule (45 C.F.R. Parts 160, 164) protects genetic information held by covered entities (healthcare providers, health plans, healthcare clearinghouses) as protected health information (PHI). Use and disclosure require individual authorization with limited exceptions for treatment, payment, and healthcare operations.

**State Laws:** States have enacted extensive supplemental genetic privacy protections:

| State | Law | Key Provision |
|-------|-----|--------------|
| California | SB 41 (2022, GIPA) | Requires DTC companies to obtain consent before sharing genetic data; private right of action |
| Illinois | GIPA (2024) | Comprehensive genetic data protection with $2,500-$15,000 penalties per violation |
| Florida | Genetic Privacy Act (2021) | Prohibits genetic information use in life insurance underwriting |
| Maryland | Genetic Information Privacy Act (2024) | Prohibits genetic discrimination in life, disability, and long-term care insurance |

### Forensic Genetic Genealogy

**Golden State Killer Case (2018):** The arrest of Joseph James DeAngelo in April 2018 through forensic genetic genealogy -- investigators uploaded crime scene DNA to the public genealogy database GEDmatch and identified the suspect through genetic relatives -- transformed law enforcement practice and genetic privacy debates. The technique has subsequently been used to solve hundreds of cold cases.

**Fourth Amendment Concerns:** In Carpenter v. United States, 585 U.S. 296 (2018), the Supreme Court held that access to historical cell-site location information constitutes a search requiring a warrant. Legal scholars have argued that familial DNA searching similarly implicates Fourth Amendment protections, as it reveals information about genetic relatives who did not consent to law enforcement use.

**DOJ Interim Policy (2019):** The Department of Justice issued interim guidance requiring federal investigators to exhaust traditional DNA databases (CODIS) before using forensic genetic genealogy, to obtain prosecutor approval, and to use the technique only for violent crimes and missing person cases. Several states have enacted legislation restricting forensic genetic genealogy, including Maryland (HB 240, 2021) requiring judicial authorization.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's Gendiagnostikgesetz is among the world's most protective genetic privacy statutes.

**Gendiagnostikgesetz (GenDG) -- Genetic Diagnosis Act (2009):** Enacted on April 24, 2009, and effective February 1, 2010, the GenDG establishes comprehensive regulations for genetic testing in medical, employment, and insurance contexts.

- **Section 7 (Physician Requirement):** Genetic examinations may only be performed by physicians (Arztvorbehalt). For predictive genetic testing (prädiktive genetische Untersuchungen), only physicians qualified in human genetics (Fachärzte für Humangenetik) or with equivalent qualification may conduct the test and communicate results. This requirement effectively prohibits DTC genetic testing for disease-related information.
- **Section 8 (Informed Consent):** Genetic testing requires written informed consent (Einwilligung nach Aufklärung) following comprehensive counseling about the nature, significance, and scope of the genetic examination.
- **Section 10 (Genetic Counseling):** Before and after predictive genetic testing, the physician must offer genetic counseling (genetische Beratung). After the test, counseling must be offered regardless of the result. The content and quality of counseling are monitored by the Gendiagnostik-Kommission (Genetic Diagnosis Commission) at the Robert Koch Institut.
- **Section 12 (Sample Destruction):** Genetic samples must be destroyed immediately after the purpose of the genetic examination has been fulfilled, unless the affected person consents in writing to continued storage for other purposes.
- **Section 15 (Prenatal Genetic Testing):** Prenatal genetic testing is restricted to medical purposes -- specifically, detecting health impairments that may affect the health of the embryo or fetus. Prenatal testing for sex determination (except for sex-linked diseases) and for conditions that would only manifest after age 18 is prohibited.
- **Section 18 (Insurance):** Insurers may not require or use genetic test results or genetic information from applicants or policyholders for any insurance product. The only exception is life insurance policies exceeding EUR 300,000 or annuity insurance exceeding EUR 30,000 per year, for which the insured person must disclose already-conducted diagnostic genetic test results (not predictive test results).
- **Section 19 (Employment):** Employers are absolutely prohibited from requiring, requesting, or using genetic test results or genetic information for any employment-related decision. Unlike GINA, this prohibition applies to all employers regardless of size and has no exceptions.
- **Section 20 (Prohibition of Genetic Discrimination):** No person may be disadvantaged because of their genetic characteristics (Benachteiligungsverbot).

### GDPR -- Genetic Data as Special Category

The GDPR (Regulation (EU) 2016/679) classifies genetic data as a "special category of personal data" under Article 9(1), alongside data revealing racial or ethnic origin, political opinions, religious beliefs, trade union membership, biometric data, health data, and data concerning sex life or sexual orientation. Processing of genetic data is prohibited unless one of the Article 9(2) exceptions applies, including explicit consent, vital interests, or substantial public interest.

The GDPR defines genetic data in Article 4(13) as "personal data relating to the inherited or acquired genetic characteristics of a natural person which give unique information about the physiology or the health of that natural person and which result, in particular, from an analysis of a biological sample from the natural person in question."

### Forensic DNA Database

The DNA-Identitätsfeststellungsgesetz (DNA Identification Act, 1998, as amended) authorizes the Bundeskriminalamt (BKA) to maintain the national DNA database (DNA-Analyse-Datei) for criminal investigation. DNA profiles may be stored from persons convicted of or charged with significant offenses. The Federal Constitutional Court (BVerfG) upheld the constitutionality of mandatory DNA profiling of convicted offenders in its decision of December 14, 2000 (2 BvR 1741/99), subject to proportionality requirements.

### DTC Genetic Testing

Germany's physician-directed model (GenDG Section 7) effectively prohibits DTC genetic testing for health-related genetic information. Companies such as 23andMe and AncestryDNA are available in Germany but may only offer ancestry and non-health-related analyses. Health-related genetic testing requires physician involvement, informed consent, and genetic counseling in compliance with the GenDG.

## ECHR & EU

### S. and Marper v. United Kingdom (Grand Chamber, 2008)

In S. and Marper v. United Kingdom (Applications Nos. 30562/04 and 30566/04, Grand Chamber Judgment of December 4, 2008), the European Court of Human Rights established landmark genetic privacy protections. The Court unanimously found that the United Kingdom's blanket and indiscriminate retention of DNA profiles, cellular samples, and fingerprints of persons arrested but not convicted violated Article 8 ECHR. Key findings:

- DNA profiles contain significant information about an individual and their ethnic origin, creating a legitimate Article 8 interest.
- The blanket retention regime was disproportionate to its legitimate aim (prevention of crime) because it drew no distinction between convicted and unconvicted persons.
- The stigmatizing effect of being treated as a suspect despite acquittal reinforced the disproportionality finding.

The judgment forced the United Kingdom to enact the Protection of Freedoms Act 2012, which established time-limited retention periods and mandatory destruction of DNA profiles for unconvicted persons.

### Oviedo Convention (1997)

The Convention on Human Rights and Biomedicine (CETS No. 164) addresses genetic privacy in several provisions:

- **Article 11:** Prohibits discrimination against a person on grounds of their genetic heritage.
- **Article 12:** Restricts predictive genetic testing to health purposes or health-related scientific research, subject to appropriate genetic counseling.
- **Article 13:** Interventions on the human genome may only be undertaken for preventive, diagnostic, or therapeutic purposes, not for modification of germ line characteristics.

### EU Regulation on European Reference Networks

Regulation (EU) 2024/_ on the European Health Data Space (EHDS), proposed in 2022 and under negotiation, would establish rules for the secondary use of health data including genetic data for research, innovation, and public health purposes. The EHDS proposal includes provisions for data altruism, anonymization requirements, and secure processing environments for genetic data.

### Prüm Convention (2005)

The Convention on the stepping up of cross-border cooperation, particularly in combating terrorism, cross-border crime, and illegal immigration (Prüm Convention), signed on May 27, 2005, and implemented through Council Decisions 2008/615/JHA and 2008/616/JHA, establishes a framework for automated exchange of DNA profiles between EU member states for criminal investigation. The Convention includes data protection provisions requiring that exchanged data be used only for the purposes specified.

## Comparative Analysis

### Scope of Protection

Germany's GenDG provides the broadest protections, with an absolute prohibition on employer use of genetic information, comprehensive insurance restrictions, mandatory physician involvement and genetic counseling, and immediate sample destruction requirements. GINA provides strong but incomplete U.S. protections, with significant gaps in life insurance, disability insurance, and long-term care insurance. South Korea's Bioethics Act provides anti-discrimination protections but with less detailed procedural requirements than the GenDG.

### DTC Genetic Testing

The jurisdictions take starkly different approaches to consumer genetic testing. Germany restricts health-related genetic testing to physician-directed settings. South Korea permits non-medical wellness testing without physician involvement but restricts disease-related testing. The United States has the most permissive approach, with DTC companies offering extensive health-related genetic testing under general FDA and FTC oversight.

### Forensic DNA

All four jurisdictions maintain forensic DNA databases, but with dramatically different retention and access rules. The ECHR's S. and Marper ruling establishes the strictest standard, requiring time-limited retention and distinction between convicted and unconvicted persons. Germany and South Korea focus DNA collection on convicted offenders. The United States permits collection from arrestees in many states (upheld in Maryland v. King, 569 U.S. 435 (2013)).

### Genetic Discrimination Insurance Gap

The most significant divergence concerns insurance discrimination. Germany prohibits genetic discrimination across all insurance types with a narrow exception for high-value life insurance. South Korea prohibits discrimination broadly but with less specific implementation. The United States prohibits discrimination only in health insurance, leaving a significant gap that has deterred some individuals from undergoing genetic testing.
