## Summary

Non-disclosure agreements (NDAs) -- contracts that create confidentiality obligations between parties sharing proprietary information -- have become ubiquitous in commercial transactions, employment relationships, and settlement agreements, yet their enforceability and permissible scope vary significantly across jurisdictions. South Korea regulates confidentiality obligations primarily through the Unfair Competition Prevention and Trade Secret Protection Act (부정경쟁방지 및 영업비밀보호에 관한 법률) and general contract law under the Korean Civil Code (민법), with courts enforcing NDAs subject to reasonableness requirements regarding scope, duration, and the balance between business interests and individual rights; the labor law dimension is governed by the Labor Standards Act (근로기준법) and judicial precedent restricting overly broad post-employment confidentiality restraints. The United States enforces NDAs under state contract law with significant variation across jurisdictions, while recent federal and state legislation -- including the Defend Trade Secrets Act of 2016 (18 U.S.C. Sections 1831-1839), state laws restricting NDAs in sexual harassment contexts (beginning with New York and California in 2018-2019), and the federal Speak Out Act of 2022 (Public Law 117-224) -- has introduced both stronger trade secret protection and new limitations on the use of NDAs to silence misconduct allegations. Germany enforces NDAs through freedom of contract principles under the BGB, trade secret protections under the Geschäftsgeheimnisgesetz (GeschGehG, Trade Secret Act of 2019 implementing EU Directive 2016/943), and employment law restrictions under the Gewerbeordnung (GewO) Section 110 and judicial precedent of the Bundesarbeitsgericht (Federal Labor Court). The EU harmonized trade secret protection through Directive (EU) 2016/943, while the European Court of Human Rights has addressed confidentiality obligations in the context of Article 10 (freedom of expression) and Article 8 (right to respect for private life) of the Convention, establishing limits on NDAs that suppress matters of public interest.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's NDA regime operates through the interplay of three legal frameworks: general contract law, trade secret legislation, and labor law.

**Korean Civil Code (민법):** NDAs are enforceable as contracts under the general provisions of the Civil Code, subject to requirements of lawful purpose (Article 103, public order and morals), freedom from unconscionable terms (Article 104), and valid consent. Courts assess the reasonableness of NDA terms including temporal scope, subject matter scope, geographic limitations, and the balance of interests between the parties.

**Unfair Competition Prevention and Trade Secret Protection Act (부정경쟁방지 및 영업비밀보호에 관한 법률):** This Act, originally enacted in 1961 and substantially amended multiple times (most recently in 2024), provides both civil and criminal remedies for trade secret misappropriation. Article 2(2) defines a trade secret as information that is not publicly known, has independent economic value, and is subject to reasonable efforts to maintain secrecy. Articles 10-14 establish injunctive relief, damages (including lost profits, unjust enrichment, and reasonable royalty calculations), and criminal penalties of up to 10 years imprisonment or fines up to 500 million KRW for trade secret theft.

### Employment NDAs

The enforceability of employment NDAs in South Korea is assessed under both contract law and labor law principles. The Supreme Court of Korea (대법원) has established in a line of decisions that post-employment non-disclosure obligations must satisfy several requirements: the information must qualify as a legitimate business secret worthy of protection; the temporal restriction must be reasonable; the scope must be limited to information actually accessed during employment; and adequate compensation must be provided for the restrictive obligation.

### Restrictions on NDAs in Harassment Cases

Following the #MeToo movement's impact in South Korea beginning in 2018, amendments to the Gender Equality Employment and Work-Family Balance Act (남녀고용평등과 일·가정 양립 지원에 관한 법률) strengthened protections for victims of workplace sexual harassment. While South Korea has not enacted a blanket prohibition on NDAs in harassment settlement agreements comparable to some U.S. states, the Act provides that any agreement that restricts a victim's right to report harassment to authorities or to cooperate with investigations is void under public policy principles.

### Digital Economy NDAs

The Korean Fair Trade Commission has scrutinized NDA provisions in technology industry contracts, particularly in the context of franchise agreements (governed by the Fair Transactions in Franchise Business Act, 가맹사업거래의 공정화에 관한 법률) and subcontracting relationships (governed by the Fair Transactions in Subcontracting Act, 하도급거래 공정화에 관한 법률). The KFTC has issued corrective orders against companies imposing excessively broad confidentiality obligations that effectively prevent franchisees or subcontractors from conducting legitimate business activities.

## United States

### Legal Framework

NDA enforcement in the United States is governed primarily by state contract law, with an overlay of federal trade secret protection and emerging federal restrictions on certain categories of NDAs.

**State Contract Law:** Each state applies its own contract law principles to NDA enforcement. Common requirements include: a legitimate protectable interest (trade secrets, confidential business information, customer relationships); reasonable scope (subject matter, duration, geographic limitation); adequate consideration (which varies by state -- some require independent consideration beyond continued at-will employment, while others accept employment itself as sufficient); and clear and specific terms (vague or overbroad NDAs may be narrowed by courts applying "blue pencil" or "reformation" doctrines, or may be struck entirely depending on the jurisdiction).

**Defend Trade Secrets Act of 2016 (DTSA), 18 U.S.C. Sections 1831-1839:** The DTSA created the first federal civil cause of action for trade secret misappropriation, supplementing (but not preempting) state trade secret laws. Critically, Section 7 (18 U.S.C. Section 1833(b)) requires that any contract or agreement with an employee governing trade secrets or other confidential information must include a notice of immunity provisions protecting whistleblowers who disclose trade secrets to government officials or in sealed court filings. Failure to include this notice forfeits the employer's right to exemplary damages and attorney's fees under the DTSA.

### Restrictions on NDAs in Sexual Misconduct Context

Beginning with the #MeToo movement in 2017, numerous states have enacted laws restricting the use of NDAs in sexual harassment and misconduct contexts:

| State | Legislation | Key Provisions |
|-------|------------|----------------|
| New York | 2018 amendments to CPLR Section 5003-b; 2019 amendments | NDAs in sexual harassment settlements must allow disclosure to law enforcement; 21-day consideration period |
| California | SB 820 (2018); SB 331 "Silenced No More Act" (2021) | Prohibits NDAs preventing disclosure of sexual harassment facts; extended to all forms of workplace discrimination |
| New Jersey | NJ Senate Bill 121 (2019) | Unenforceable provisions in employment contracts waiving rights related to discrimination or harassment claims |
| Illinois | Workplace Transparency Act (2020) | Restricts confidentiality provisions in employment agreements; requires employer reporting of adverse judgments |
| Washington | SB 5996 (2022) | Prohibits nondisclosure/nondisparagement clauses in employment agreements regarding illegal conduct |

**Speak Out Act of 2022 (Public Law 117-224):** Signed into law on December 7, 2022, this federal statute renders unenforceable any pre-dispute NDA or non-disparagement clause that restricts the ability of an individual to discuss or disclose sexual assault or sexual harassment. The Act applies to NDAs entered into before a dispute arises, leaving post-dispute settlement NDAs largely unaffected.

### Enforceability Considerations

U.S. courts have developed extensive jurisprudence on NDA enforceability. Key principles include: specificity of protected information (NDAs that purport to cover "all information" without further definition are vulnerable to challenge); temporal reasonableness (courts generally uphold durations of 2-5 years for most commercial NDAs; perpetual NDAs for true trade secrets may be enforceable); return/destruction obligations (provisions requiring return or destruction of confidential materials upon termination are generally enforced); and residual knowledge (the "residual knowledge" or "mental residuals" clause, which permits use of information retained in unaided memory, is a contested provision with varying enforceability across jurisdictions).

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's NDA framework has been significantly reshaped by the Geschäftsgeheimnisgesetz (GeschGehG, Trade Secret Act), which entered into force on April 26, 2019, implementing EU Directive 2016/943 on the protection of undisclosed know-how and business information.

**BGB Freedom of Contract:** NDAs are enforceable as contracts under BGB Section 311(1) (freedom of contract). However, standard form NDA provisions in employment contracts are subject to the BGB's general terms control under Sections 305-310, meaning that unreasonable or surprising terms may be struck down.

**Geschäftsgeheimnisgesetz (GeschGehG):** The Act defines trade secrets (Geschäftsgeheimnisse) in Section 2(1) as information that is not generally known or readily accessible, has economic value because of its secrecy, is subject to reasonable protective measures by the lawful holder, and in respect of which there is a legitimate interest in secrecy. Sections 6-8 establish civil remedies including injunctive relief (Unterlassung), damages, destruction, recall, and publication of court decisions. Section 23 provides criminal penalties of up to three years imprisonment or a fine for intentional trade secret misappropriation.

### Employment NDA Specifics

**During Employment:** Employees are subject to an implied duty of confidentiality (Verschwiegenheitspflicht) during the employment relationship, derived from the duty of loyalty (Treuepflicht) under Section 241(2) BGB. This duty exists even without an express NDA, though written agreements provide certainty regarding scope and remedies.

**Post-Employment:** Post-employment confidentiality obligations are subject to stricter scrutiny. The Bundesarbeitsgericht (Federal Labor Court) has held that post-employment NDAs must be distinguished from non-compete agreements (nachvertragliche Wettbewerbsverbote) governed by Sections 74-75f of the Handelsgesetzbuch (HGB), which require compensation of at least 50 percent of the employee's last remuneration (Karenzentschädigung). Where a post-employment NDA is so broad that it effectively constitutes a non-compete restriction, courts may require compliance with the HGB compensation requirements or invalidate the agreement.

### Whistleblower Protection

The Hinweisgeberschutzgesetz (Whistleblower Protection Act), which entered into force on July 2, 2023, transposing EU Directive 2019/1937, provides that NDAs and other contractual confidentiality obligations cannot prevent employees from reporting legal violations through protected channels. Section 5 of the GeschGehG additionally provides that obtaining, using, or disclosing trade secrets is lawful when done for the purpose of exercising the right to freedom of expression and information, detecting an illegal activity or professional or other misconduct, or protecting a legitimate interest recognized by EU or national law.

### Practical Enforcement

German courts apply a proportionality analysis to NDA enforcement, weighing the employer's or disclosing party's legitimate interest in confidentiality against the other party's professional freedom (Article 12 Grundgesetz) and freedom of expression (Article 5 Grundgesetz). Liquidated damages clauses (Vertragsstrafen) in NDAs are common in Germany but are subject to judicial review for reasonableness under Section 343 BGB (judicial reduction of excessive contractual penalties).

## ECHR & EU

### EU Trade Secrets Directive (2016/943)

Directive (EU) 2016/943 of June 8, 2016, on the protection of undisclosed know-how and business information (trade secrets) against their unlawful acquisition, use, and disclosure established the first harmonized EU framework for trade secret protection, providing the legal foundation for NDA enforcement across the single market.

**Definition (Article 2):** A trade secret is information that is secret (not generally known among or readily accessible to persons within the circles that normally deal with the kind of information in question), has commercial value because of its secrecy, and has been subject to reasonable steps to keep it secret.

**Lawful and Unlawful Acquisition (Articles 3-4):** The Directive distinguishes between lawful acquisition (independent discovery, observation, reverse engineering) and unlawful acquisition (unauthorized access, copying, or breach of a confidentiality agreement). Critically, Article 3(1)(d) provides that trade secret acquisition is lawful when done in the exercise of the right of workers or workers' representatives to information and consultation in accordance with EU and national law.

**Exceptions (Article 5):** Trade secret protection does not extend to cases involving the exercise of the right to freedom of expression and information as set out in the Charter of Fundamental Rights of the EU; revealing misconduct, wrongdoing, or illegal activity for the purpose of protecting the general public interest; disclosure by workers to their representatives in the legitimate performance of their functions; or any other legitimate interest recognized by EU or national law.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

The European Court of Human Rights has addressed NDA-related issues through its Article 10 (freedom of expression) jurisprudence:

**Heinisch v. Germany (Application No. 28274/08, Judgment of July 21, 2011):** The Court held that the dismissal of a geriatric nurse who reported deficiencies in care to the prosecuting authority after internal complaints failed violated her right to freedom of expression under Article 10. The Court established that confidentiality obligations owed to employers must yield to the public interest in disclosure of information about serious wrongdoing, provided the employee acts in good faith and external disclosure is a proportionate response.

**Guja v. Moldova (Application No. 14277/04, Grand Chamber Judgment of February 12, 2008):** The Grand Chamber established six criteria for assessing whether a public employee's disclosure of confidential information is protected under Article 10: whether alternative channels were available; the public interest in the information disclosed; the authenticity of the information; the damage to the authority; good faith of the discloser; and the severity of the sanction imposed.

### EU Whistleblower Directive (2019/1937)

Directive (EU) 2019/1937, adopted on October 23, 2019, establishes minimum standards for the protection of persons reporting breaches of Union law. The Directive requires member states to ensure that confidentiality clauses in employment contracts, settlement agreements, or other agreements cannot prevent workers from reporting breaches through the channels established by the Directive. Article 21(7) specifically provides that any provision in an agreement that restricts the right to make reports or public disclosures shall be deemed void.

### Practical Impact on NDA Drafting

The combined effect of the Trade Secrets Directive, the Whistleblower Directive, and ECHR jurisprudence has created a framework in which NDAs in the EU must include carve-outs for whistleblower disclosures and reports to competent authorities, cannot restrict the exercise of freedom of expression on matters of public interest, must define protected information with reasonable specificity rather than using blanket confidentiality terms, and are subject to proportionality review balancing the disclosing party's commercial interests against the recipient's fundamental rights.

## Comparative Analysis

### Philosophical Approaches

The four legal systems reflect fundamentally different philosophical orientations toward NDAs. The United States historically adopted the most permissive approach, treating NDAs as freely negotiable private contracts with minimal public policy constraints -- though this position has shifted markedly since 2018 in the sexual misconduct context. South Korea and Germany both apply proportionality principles that balance commercial confidentiality interests against individual rights, with Germany's framework particularly influenced by constitutional protections for professional freedom and expression. The EU framework, shaped by the Charter of Fundamental Rights and ECHR jurisprudence, most explicitly subordinates contractual confidentiality to public interest considerations.

### Whistleblower Protections

All four systems now provide some form of whistleblower carve-out for NDAs, but with varying scope and enforcement mechanisms. The EU Whistleblower Directive provides the most comprehensive protection, rendering void any contractual provision that restricts protected reporting. The U.S. DTSA whistleblower notice requirement is narrower, applying only to trade secret disclosures made to government officials or in sealed court filings. Germany's Hinweisgeberschutzgesetz closely mirrors the EU Directive. South Korea's Public Interest Reporters Protection Act (공익신고자 보호법) protects whistleblowers who report violations of specified statutes but does not explicitly void conflicting NDA provisions.

### Sexual Misconduct NDAs

The movement to restrict NDAs in sexual harassment and misconduct contexts originated in the United States following the #MeToo movement and has produced the most legislative activity there, with the Speak Out Act and numerous state laws creating a patchwork of restrictions. Europe has addressed the issue primarily through existing fundamental rights frameworks rather than targeted NDA legislation, relying on ECHR Article 10 protections and the proportionality principle. South Korea has addressed the issue through amendments to gender equality employment legislation but with less comprehensive NDA-specific restrictions than the U.S. approach.

### Enforcement Remedies

The available remedies for NDA breach differ significantly. The United States offers the broadest range through the DTSA, including exemplary damages (up to two times actual damages) for willful and malicious misappropriation and attorney's fees, in addition to state law remedies. Germany provides criminal penalties under the GeschGehG alongside civil remedies. South Korea similarly offers both criminal penalties under the Unfair Competition Prevention Act and civil damages. The EU Directive establishes minimum harmonized remedies but leaves implementation to member states.
