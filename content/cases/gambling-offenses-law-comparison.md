## Summary

Gambling -- the wagering of money or something of value on an event with an uncertain outcome -- occupies a uniquely contested space in criminal law, sitting at the intersection of public morality, economic regulation, consumer protection, and organized crime prevention. South Korea maintains one of the world's most restrictive gambling regimes, criminalizing virtually all forms of gambling under Articles 246 and 247 of the Criminal Act (형법), with extraterritorial reach that extends to Korean nationals gambling abroad. The United States operates a complex federalist model where federal statutes -- the Wire Act (18 U.S.C. section 1084), the Unlawful Internet Gambling Enforcement Act (UIGEA, 31 U.S.C. sections 5361-5367), and the now-defunct Professional and Amateur Sports Protection Act (PASPA) -- coexist with widely divergent state-level regulations, a landscape dramatically reshaped by the Supreme Court's 2018 decision in Murphy v. NCAA. Germany regulates gambling primarily through the Interstate Treaty on Gambling 2021 (Glucksspielstaatsvertrag 2021, GluStV 2021) alongside criminal prohibitions in Sections 284 and 285 of the Criminal Code (Strafgesetzbuch, StGB), requiring operators to hold federal or state licenses. At the supranational level, the European Union does not harmonize gambling law directly, but the Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU) has developed extensive case law -- from Schindler (1994) through Gambelli (2003), Placanica (2007), and Liga Portuguesa v Bwin (2009) -- applying free movement of services principles while recognizing broad member state discretion to restrict gambling on grounds of public policy, consumer protection, and crime prevention.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea maintains one of the most restrictive gambling regimes among developed nations. The legal framework rests primarily on the Criminal Act (형법), specifically:

- **Article 246 (Gambling, 도박):** A person who gambles or bets for the purpose of obtaining property shall be punished by a fine not exceeding KRW 10 million. However, if the gambling involves only temporary amusement and the amount at stake is minor, no punishment applies. Habitual gambling is subject to imprisonment for not more than three years or a fine not exceeding KRW 20 million.
- **Article 247 (Opening of a Place for Gambling, 도박장소 개설):** A person who opens a place for gambling for the purpose of gaining profit shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than five years or by a fine not exceeding KRW 30 million.
- **Article 248 (Lottery Offenses):** Distributing lottery tickets or acting as an intermediary in their distribution without authorization is punishable by imprisonment for up to three years or a fine up to KRW 20 million.
- **Article 249 (Confiscation):** Property used or obtained through gambling may be confiscated.

### Regulatory Structure

The **National Gambling Control Commission (NGCC)**, established under the National Gambling Control Act (사행산업통합감독위원회법), serves as the central regulatory authority. It operates under the Ministry of Culture, Sports and Tourism and coordinates with the National Police Agency to enforce gambling prohibitions and oversee the limited number of authorized gambling operations.

Authorized forms of gambling in South Korea include:

1. **Kangwon Land Casino:** The only casino where Korean citizens may gamble, established under the Special Act on the Assistance to the Development of Abandoned Mine Areas (1995). Located in a former coal-mining region, it was intended as an economic revitalization measure.
2. **Foreigner-Only Casinos:** Sixteen casinos across the country that serve exclusively foreign passport holders.
3. **Korea Racing Authority:** Horse racing, cycling, and motorboat racing under specific governing statutes.
4. **Sports Toto and Sports Proto:** Government-authorized fixed-odds and parimutuel sports betting, limited in scope and stakes.
5. **National Lottery (Lotto 6/45):** State-run lottery system.

### Extraterritorial Application

A distinctive feature of Korean gambling law is its extraterritorial reach. Under Article 3 of the Criminal Act, Korean criminal law applies to Korean nationals who commit offenses abroad. Courts have consistently held that Korean citizens who gamble at casinos in Macau, Las Vegas, or other jurisdictions can be prosecuted upon return to Korea, particularly for habitual gambling. This extraterritorial enforcement has been bolstered by cooperation between the NGCC and immigration authorities.

### Penalties

| Offense | Imprisonment | Fine |
|---------|-------------|------|
| Simple gambling (Art. 246(1)) | -- | Up to KRW 10 million |
| Habitual gambling (Art. 246(2)) | Up to 3 years | Up to KRW 20 million |
| Opening a gambling place (Art. 247) | Up to 5 years | Up to KRW 30 million |
| Lottery offenses (Art. 248) | Up to 3 years | Up to KRW 20 million |

### Landmark Cases and Developments

**Illegal Online Gambling Crackdown (2024-2025):** South Korean police dismantled multiple large-scale illegal online gambling operations. In one major case, authorities busted an operation worth approximately KRW 800 billion (over $580 million), tracking operators who had been running illegal sports betting and casino websites. The crackdown led to dozens of arrests and highlighted the scale of illegal gambling in the country.

**National Gambling Control Guidelines (2024):** In response to an estimated two million Koreans experiencing gambling addiction, the NGCC issued updated guidelines designed to tackle illegal gambling businesses that had been exploiting legal loopholes, including "game rooms" that offered pseudo-gambling activities by skirting the definition of games of chance under the Criminal Act.

**Constitutional Challenge to Extraterritorial Enforcement:** Korean courts have upheld the constitutionality of prosecuting citizens for gambling abroad, reasoning that the harms of gambling -- addiction, family breakdown, and financial ruin -- transcend national borders, and that the state's interest in protecting citizens from these harms justifies extraterritorial application of criminal law.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States employs a complex, multi-layered approach to gambling regulation, with authority divided between federal and state governments. The key federal statutes are:

- **Wire Act (18 U.S.C. section 1084, 1961):** Prohibits anyone "engaged in the business of betting or wagering" from knowingly using wire communication facilities for the transmission of bets or wagers, or information assisting in their placement, in interstate or foreign commerce. The statute targets operators and intermediaries rather than individual bettors.
- **Unlawful Internet Gambling Enforcement Act (UIGEA, 31 U.S.C. sections 5361-5367, 2006):** Prohibits gambling businesses from "knowingly accepting payments in connection with the participation of another person in a bet or wager that involves the use of the Internet and that is unlawful under any federal or state law." The UIGEA primarily targets payment processors and financial institutions rather than individual gamblers, and explicitly exempts state-licensed operations.
- **Professional and Amateur Sports Protection Act (PASPA, 28 U.S.C. sections 3701-3704, 1992-2018):** Prohibited states from authorizing sports betting, with exceptions for Nevada, Delaware, Montana, and Oregon. Struck down as unconstitutional in 2018.
- **Illegal Gambling Business Act (18 U.S.C. section 1955):** Targets gambling operations that violate state law, involve five or more persons, and have been in continuous operation for more than 30 days or gross more than $2,000 in a single day.
- **Travel Act (18 U.S.C. section 1952):** Prohibits interstate travel or use of interstate facilities with the intent to promote, manage, or carry on any unlawful gambling activity.

### Federal Penalties

| Statute | Maximum Imprisonment | Maximum Fine |
|---------|---------------------|-------------|
| Wire Act (section 1084) | 2 years | $250,000 |
| UIGEA violations | 5 years | $250,000 |
| Illegal Gambling Business Act (section 1955) | 5 years | $250,000 |
| Travel Act (section 1952) | 5 years | $250,000 |

Additional consequences may include forfeiture of assets, cancellation of telecommunications services, and prosecution under RICO (18 U.S.C. sections 1961-1968) or money laundering statutes (18 U.S.C. sections 1956-1957) when gambling offenses form part of broader criminal enterprises.

### State-Level Regulation

Following Murphy v. NCAA (2018), gambling regulation in the United States has become predominantly a state-level matter. As of early 2026, sports betting has been legalized in some form in approximately 39 states and the District of Columbia. State approaches range from:

- **Highly permissive:** Nevada, New Jersey, and Pennsylvania permit comprehensive casino gambling, online gambling, and sports betting with well-developed regulatory frameworks.
- **Sports betting only:** Many states have legalized sports betting while maintaining restrictions on casino gambling and online poker.
- **Restrictive:** Utah and Hawaii maintain near-total prohibitions on gambling, with no casinos, no lottery, and no legal sports betting.

State penalties vary enormously: in some jurisdictions, simple gambling is a civil infraction or petty misdemeanor carrying a small fine, while in others it remains a felony offense, particularly for operators of illegal gambling businesses.

### Landmark Cases

**Murphy v. National Collegiate Athletic Association, 584 U.S. 453 (2018):** The Supreme Court, in a 6-3 decision authored by Justice Samuel Alito, struck down PASPA as unconstitutional under the anti-commandeering doctrine of the Tenth Amendment. The Court held that PASPA impermissibly "dictate[d] what a state legislature may and may not do" by prohibiting states from authorizing sports gambling. Justice Alito wrote: "Congress can regulate sports gambling directly, but if it elects not to do so, each state is free to act on its own." The decision triggered a wave of state-level legalization that transformed the American sports betting landscape.

**United States v. Cohen, 260 F.3d 68 (2d Cir. 2001):** Jay Cohen, founder of the Antigua-based World Sports Exchange, was convicted under the Wire Act for accepting sports bets from U.S. customers via the internet. The Second Circuit upheld his conviction and 21-month prison sentence, establishing an important precedent that the Wire Act applies to internet-based gambling operations even when headquartered offshore.

**In re Mastercard International Internet Gambling Litigation, 313 F.3d 257 (5th Cir. 2002):** The Fifth Circuit dismissed civil RICO claims against credit card companies for processing online gambling transactions, finding that the Wire Act applies only to sports betting and not to other forms of online gambling such as casino games. This interpretation was later echoed in a 2011 DOJ Office of Legal Counsel opinion, though the Trump administration's 2018 OLC opinion reversed that view (subsequently challenged in litigation).

**United States v. DiCristina, 886 F. Supp. 2d 164 (E.D.N.Y. 2012):** Judge Jack Weinstein ruled that poker is a game of skill rather than chance and therefore does not constitute "gambling" under the Illegal Gambling Business Act. However, the case was vacated on appeal by the Second Circuit on jurisdictional grounds, leaving the underlying question unresolved.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany regulates gambling through a combination of federal criminal law and interstate treaty regulation:

- **Glucksspielstaatsvertrag 2021 (GluStV 2021):** The Interstate Treaty on Gambling, effective July 1, 2021, is the central regulatory instrument governing all forms of gambling -- both land-based and online -- across Germany's sixteen federal states. It replaced the more restrictive 2012 treaty and introduced a licensing regime for online casinos, online poker, and virtual slot machines for the first time, while maintaining state monopolies on lotteries and sports betting through licensed operators.
- **Section 284 StGB (Organizing Illegal Gambling):** Criminalizes the unauthorized organization, operation, management, or brokering of public gambling without an official license.
- **Section 285 StGB (Participation in Illegal Gambling):** Criminalizes participation as a player in unauthorized public gambling.

### Regulatory Structure

The **Gemeinsame Glucksspielbehorde der Lander (GGL)**, or Joint Gambling Authority of the Lander, was established under the GluStV 2021 as a centralized regulatory body headquartered in Halle (Saale), Saxony-Anhalt. The GGL is responsible for:

- Issuing and revoking federal gambling licenses
- Monitoring compliance with advertising restrictions and player protection requirements
- Maintaining a centralized blocking system (OASIS) for self-excluded players
- Enforcing deposit limits (EUR 1,000 per month for online gambling)
- Coordinating with state authorities on enforcement actions

### Criminal Penalties

| Offense | Provision | Penalty |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Organizing illegal gambling | Section 284(1) StGB | Up to 2 years imprisonment or fine |
| Commercial or gang-related organizing | Section 284(3) StGB | Up to 5 years imprisonment |
| Advertising illegal gambling | Section 284(4) StGB | Up to 1 year imprisonment or fine |
| Participation in illegal gambling | Section 285 StGB | Up to 6 months imprisonment or fine (up to 180 daily units) |

### Administrative Penalties

Under the GluStV 2021, administrative fines of up to EUR 500,000 may be imposed per violation of regulatory requirements. In addition, profits obtained through violations may be confiscated (Gewinnabschopfung). Interdiction letters issued in administrative proceedings typically carry fines ranging from EUR 10,000 to EUR 50,000 per infringement, depending on the relevant state law.

### Confiscation and Asset Recovery

Winnings obtained from illegal gambling may be confiscated as illegally obtained assets under Section 73 StGB. Equipment used in connection with the offense -- including computers, mobile phones, and gambling apparatus -- may also be seized and confiscated. The statute of limitations for offenses under Sections 284 and 285 StGB is five years.

### Landmark Cases and Developments

**Federal Administrative Court on Online Casino Legality (2021-2023):** Multiple German courts addressed the legality of online casino gambling that took place before the GluStV 2021 came into force. Players who had gambled at online casinos operating without a German license successfully sued operators for the return of their losses, with courts ruling that the gambling contracts were void under Section 134 BGB (German Civil Code) because they violated the then-applicable prohibition on online casino gambling. Several landmark rulings by regional courts (Landgerichte) established that players could recover losses even when they had knowingly participated in illegal online gambling.

**Enforcement Against Unlicensed Operators (2022-2025):** The GGL has taken increasingly aggressive action against unlicensed gambling operators, including ordering internet service providers to block access to illegal gambling websites and directing payment service providers to refuse transactions with unlicensed operators. These enforcement actions have been upheld by administrative courts, though they have raised concerns about proportionality and freedom of commerce.

**Constitutional Review of the State Monopoly:** The Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) has ruled on multiple occasions that state gambling monopolies are compatible with the Basic Law (Grundgesetz) only insofar as they genuinely and consistently pursue public interest objectives such as addiction prevention and consumer protection, rather than merely serving as revenue-generation mechanisms. Monopolies that engage in extensive advertising to maximize revenues undermine their own justification and may be unconstitutional.

## ECHR & EU

### Legal Framework

Gambling regulation at the European level is characterized by the absence of harmonized legislation and the central role of the Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU) in defining the boundaries of member state regulatory autonomy:

- **Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union (TFEU), Article 56:** Guarantees the freedom to provide services across EU member states, which the CJEU has held applies to gambling services, including those provided by electronic means.
- **TFEU, Article 49:** Guarantees freedom of establishment, also applicable to gambling operators seeking to establish themselves in another member state.
- **No Harmonizing Directive:** Unlike most service sectors, the EU has not adopted a harmonizing directive for gambling. The Services Directive (2006/123/EC) explicitly excludes gambling activities from its scope. The European Commission's 2012 Communication "Towards a Comprehensive European Framework for Online Gambling" proposed regulatory coordination but did not result in binding legislation.
- **2014 Commission Recommendation:** The European Commission issued a non-binding recommendation on principles for the protection of consumers and players of online gambling services (2014/478/EU), addressing matters such as registration requirements, player identification, responsible gambling, and advertising standards.

### CJEU Landmark Case Law

The CJEU has delivered over 20 judgments on gambling regulation, establishing a body of case law that balances free movement principles against member state discretion:

**Her Majesty's Customs and Excise v Schindler, Case C-275/92 (1994):** The foundational case in EU gambling law. German lottery operators challenged UK customs seizure of lottery advertisements sent to British citizens. The Court held that lotteries constitute economic activities falling within the scope of freedom to provide services under the Treaty, but that member states may restrict gambling services on grounds of consumer protection, crime prevention, and the preservation of public morality. The Court identified gambling's "peculiar nature" given its moral, religious, and cultural dimensions, the risk of crime and fraud, and the potential for damaging individual and social consequences.

**Gambelli and Others, Case C-243/01 (2003):** Italian nationals were prosecuted for organizing betting through a UK-licensed operator (Stanley International Betting). The Court held that Italian criminal penalties restricting cross-border gambling services constituted restrictions on the freedom to provide services and freedom of establishment, and must satisfy the proportionality test. Critically, the Court stated that a member state cannot simultaneously encourage citizens to gamble through state-run operations in order to raise revenue while arguing that gambling must be restricted on public order grounds -- a principle known as the "coherence" requirement.

**Placanica, Palazzese and Sorricchio, Joined Cases C-338/04, C-359/04 and C-360/04 (2007):** Following Gambelli, the Court subjected Italy's gambling licensing system to detailed scrutiny. While confirming that licensing systems are "perfectly acceptable" in principle, the Court held that Italy's specific system was disproportionate because it had excluded an entire category of operators (publicly listed companies) from obtaining licenses, effectively reserving the market for Italian operators. Criminal penalties imposed on operators who had been unlawfully excluded from the licensing process were held to violate EU law.

**Liga Portuguesa de Futebol Profissional and Bwin International v Santa Casa da Misericordia de Lisboa, Case C-42/07 (2009):** The Court upheld Portugal's gambling monopoly system, under which Santa Casa held exclusive rights to organize games of chance, including via the internet. The Court accepted that the fight against fraud and crime in the online gambling sector could justify a state monopoly, noting that internet gambling carried particular risks due to the lack of direct contact between operator and consumer. This decision was seen as a retreat from the more liberalizing approach of Gambelli and Placanica.

**Markus Stoss and Others, Joined Cases C-316/07, C-358/07 to C-360/07, C-409/07 and C-410/07 (2010):** The Court clarified that German state gambling monopolies could be justified under EU law, but only if the monopoly genuinely and consistently pursued objectives of public interest. If a state monopoly engaged in expansive advertising designed to increase gambling revenue rather than to channel existing demand, it would fail the coherence test and could not be justified as a restriction on free movement of services.

### ECHR Considerations

While the European Convention on Human Rights does not directly address gambling, several Convention rights are potentially engaged:

- **Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (Protection of Property):** State restrictions on gambling licenses or the revocation of existing licenses may engage property rights. The European Court of Human Rights has recognized that the state enjoys a wide margin of appreciation in regulating economic activities, including gambling, and that gambling restrictions are generally justifiable under the public interest exception.
- **Article 8 (Right to Private Life):** Arguments that gambling prohibitions infringe on personal autonomy have generally not succeeded, as the Court has recognized the state's legitimate interest in protecting individuals from the harms associated with gambling.
- **Article 10 (Freedom of Expression):** Restrictions on gambling advertising may engage freedom of expression, though such restrictions are typically upheld as necessary in a democratic society for the protection of public health and morals.

### EU Enforcement and Infringement Proceedings

The European Commission has historically pursued infringement proceedings against member states whose gambling restrictions were deemed disproportionate or discriminatory. However, in 2017, the Commission closed all pending infringement proceedings relating to gambling, recognizing that there was no political consensus for EU-level gambling regulation and that member states should be given deference in this area.

## Comparative Analysis

### Philosophical Approaches

The four jurisdictions reflect fundamentally different philosophical orientations toward gambling:

1. **South Korea (Prohibition Model):** Treats gambling primarily as a moral and social evil to be suppressed. The near-total prohibition, combined with extraterritorial enforcement, reflects a paternalistic approach rooted in Confucian values emphasizing social harmony, family stability, and productive economic activity. The limited exceptions (Kangwon Land, sports toto) are tightly controlled state concessions rather than expressions of individual liberty.

2. **United States (Federalist/Regulatory Model):** Exhibits the widest internal variation of any jurisdiction studied. The post-Murphy landscape reflects a pragmatic, revenue-driven turn toward legalization, particularly for sports betting, while maintaining federal backstops against interstate criminal enterprises. The American approach is less concerned with gambling as a moral issue and more focused on ensuring taxation, preventing fraud, and combating organized crime.

3. **Germany (Licensed Regulation Model):** The GluStV 2021 represents a shift from prohibition toward regulated permission, acknowledging the impossibility of suppressing online gambling through criminal law alone. The German model emphasizes channeling gambling demand into licensed, supervised markets with strong player protection measures (deposit limits, self-exclusion databases, advertising restrictions).

4. **EU/ECHR (Subsidiarity Model):** The absence of harmonizing legislation reflects the principle of subsidiarity -- gambling regulation remains a matter for member states, subject only to the proportionality and non-discrimination constraints of the internal market. The CJEU's "coherence" requirement ensures that states cannot exploit gambling monopolies for revenue while claiming to restrict gambling on public interest grounds.

### Enforcement Challenges

All jurisdictions face common enforcement challenges in the digital age:

- **Online Gambling:** Cross-border internet gambling undermines territorial regulatory regimes. South Korea and Germany actively pursue website blocking and payment processing restrictions; the United States relies primarily on the UIGEA's payment-processing provisions; the EU recognizes member state authority to block unlicensed operators.
- **Cryptocurrency and Decentralized Platforms:** The emergence of cryptocurrency-based gambling and decentralized prediction markets poses novel challenges for all regulatory models, as these platforms can operate without traditional financial intermediaries that serve as enforcement chokepoints.
- **Extraterritorial Enforcement:** South Korea's aggressive extraterritorial prosecution of citizens gambling abroad stands in contrast to the more limited reach of other jurisdictions, though it raises questions about enforceability and proportionality.

### Penalty Severity Comparison

South Korea imposes the harshest penalties on individual gamblers, with habitual gambling carrying up to three years imprisonment. Germany's six-month maximum for participants is moderate by comparison, while the United States generally targets operators rather than individual bettors at the federal level. The EU framework imposes no penalties directly but requires that member state penalties be proportionate to the restriction's objectives.
