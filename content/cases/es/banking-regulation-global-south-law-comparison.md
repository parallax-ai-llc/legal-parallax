## Resumen

Banking regulation across the Global South reveals a striking divergence between systems anchored in state-directed financial policy and those embracing fintech-led disruption, with each jurisdiction navigating the tension between financial stability, inclusion, and innovation through distinct institutional and legal mechanisms. China's banking system, the world's largest by total assets (exceeding RMB 400 trillion/USD 56 trillion as of 2025), is dominated by state-owned commercial banks regulated under the Banking Supervision and Administration Law (银行业监督管理法, 2003 as amended 2006) and supervised by the National Financial Regulatory Administration (NFRA, 国家金融监督管理总局), created in 2023 to replace the China Banking and Insurance Regulatory Commission (CBIRC). India's Reserve Bank of India (RBI), operating under the Reserve Bank of India Act, 1934 and the Banking Regulation Act, 1949, has overseen a payments revolution through the Unified Payments Interface (UPI), which processed over 13 billion transactions monthly by 2025. Nigeria's Central Bank of Nigeria (CBN), governed by the CBN Act 2007 and the Banks and Other Financial Institutions Act (BOFIA) 2020, has pursued financial inclusion through digital banking licensing while grappling with the eNaira central bank digital currency (CBDC) experiment. Brazil's Central Bank (Banco Central do Brasil, BCB) has emerged as a global fintech regulatory leader through the PIX instant payment system (launched November 2020), Open Finance framework (Resolution 1/2020), and Digital Real CBDC pilot. The Philippine Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas (BSP), under the New Central Bank Act (Republic Act No. 7653, as amended by RA 11211 in 2019), has licensed six digital banks under BSP Circular 1105 (2020), positioning the Philippines at the forefront of Southeast Asian digital banking.

## China

### Marco juridico

China's banking regulatory architecture was fundamentally reorganized in March 2023 when the State Council established the National Financial Regulatory Administration (国家金融监督管理总局, NFRA) to replace the China Banking and Insurance Regulatory Commission (CBIRC). The NFRA assumed the CBIRC's functions while also taking over consumer protection and investor protection responsibilities from the People's Bank of China (PBOC) and the China Securities Regulatory Commission (CSRC), creating a more consolidated supervisory structure.

The principal statutes governing banking include:

- **Commercial Banking Law** (中华人民共和国商业银行法, 1995, revised 2003, 2015): Establishes the legal framework for commercial bank operations, governance, risk management, and supervision. Article 39 sets loan-to-deposit ratio and asset-liability management requirements. A comprehensive revision draft was released for public comment in 2020 and remains under legislative review.
- **Banking Supervision and Administration Law** (银行业监督管理法, 2003, amended 2006): Grants the banking regulator authority to conduct on-site examinations, impose administrative penalties (Article 46: fines of RMB 200,000-500,000 on institutions; RMB 50,000-500,000 on individuals), and take corrective actions including revoking business licenses.
- **People's Bank of China Law** (中国人民银行法, 1995, revised 2003): Establishes the PBOC's monetary policy, macroprudential supervision, and financial stability mandate. A draft amendment published in 2020 would expand the PBOC's systemic risk authority.

### State-Owned Banks and Mixed Ownership

China's banking sector remains dominated by the "Big Five" state-owned commercial banks: Industrial and Commercial Bank of China (ICBC), China Construction Bank (CCB), Agricultural Bank of China (ABC), Bank of China (BOC), and Bank of Communications (BoCom), collectively holding approximately RMB 150 trillion in assets. These banks operate under a dual governance structure with Communist Party committees (党委) exercising leadership over major decisions alongside boards of directors, formalized by CBIRC guidance on integrating Party leadership into corporate governance (2017 Notice on Strengthening Party Leadership in Banking and Insurance Institutions).

The State Council's Central Huijin Investment Ltd. and the Ministry of Finance hold controlling stakes. The mixed ownership reform agenda has introduced strategic and financial investors, with ICBC, CCB, ABC, and BOC listed on both the Shanghai and Hong Kong stock exchanges, subject to concurrent compliance with CSRC, NFRA, and Hong Kong SFC requirements.

### Shadow Banking Crackdown

China's shadow banking system -- encompassing wealth management products (WMPs), trust company lending, entrusted loans, bankers' acceptances, and peer-to-peer (P2P) lending platforms -- peaked at approximately RMB 100 trillion (USD 15 trillion) in 2017 before a sustained regulatory crackdown. Key measures include:

- **Asset Management New Rules** (资管新规, April 2018): Jointly issued by the PBOC, CBIRC, CSRC, and SAFE, these rules prohibited guaranteed returns on WMPs, required net asset value (NAV) management, limited leverage, restricted maturity mismatch, and mandated transition to compliance by end-2021 (extended to end-2022). The rules fundamentally reshaped the RMB 29 trillion WMP market.
- **P2P Lending Elimination**: Following the Interim Measures for Management of Business Activities of Online Lending Information Intermediary Institutions (2016), regulators systematically wound down the P2P sector from over 5,000 platforms in 2017 to zero by November 2020, with total outstanding loans of approximately RMB 830 billion (USD 120 billion) resolved through liquidation, conversion to licensed micro-lending companies, or consumer finance companies.
- **Trust Sector Reform**: The revised Trust Law framework and the CBIRC's classification reform (2023) required trust companies to restructure into asset management trusts, asset service trusts, and charitable trusts, with restrictions on non-standard credit asset business.

### e-CNY (Digital Yuan)

The PBOC's digital currency, e-CNY (数字人民币), has been in pilot operation since April 2020 across 26 cities and regions including Shenzhen, Suzhou, Shanghai, and Beijing. As of 2025, cumulative e-CNY transactions exceeded RMB 7 trillion (USD 1 trillion), with over 120 million personal wallets opened. The e-CNY operates on a two-tier system: the PBOC issues e-CNY to authorized operators (the Big Five banks plus several others), which distribute to the public. Unlike cryptocurrency, e-CNY is centrally controlled with "managed anonymity" -- small transactions are anonymous to counterparties but traceable by the PBOC for anti-money laundering and counter-terrorism financing purposes. Dedicated legislation for e-CNY has not yet been enacted, with the legal basis currently derived from the PBOC Law's provisions on currency issuance.

## India

### Marco juridico

India's banking regulatory framework is anchored in the Reserve Bank of India Act, 1934 (as amended) and the Banking Regulation Act, 1949 (as amended). The RBI serves simultaneously as the monetary authority, banking regulator, payments system regulator (under the Payment and Settlement Systems Act, 2007), and foreign exchange regulator (under FEMA, 1999). This concentration of functions is supplemented by the Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI) for capital markets, the Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority (IRDAI) for insurance, and the Pension Fund Regulatory and Development Authority (PFRDA) for pensions.

Key regulatory developments include:

- **Section 35A, Banking Regulation Act**: Grants the RBI power to issue directions to banking companies "in the public interest" or "in the interest of depositors" -- an expansive authority upheld by the Supreme Court in Reserve Bank of India v. Peerless General Finance and Investment Co. Ltd. (1987) 1 SCC 424.
- **Section 45-IE (Prompt Corrective Action)**: Introduced by the Banking Regulation (Amendment) Act, 2017 and the Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code (Amendment) Ordinance, 2017, empowering the RBI to direct banking companies to initiate insolvency resolution proceedings against defaulting borrowers. The Reserve Bank of India (Prudential Framework for Resolution of Stressed Assets) Directions, 2019 replaced the earlier controversial circular of February 12, 2018, which was struck down by the Supreme Court in Dharani Sugars and Chemicals Ltd. v. Union of India (2019) 5 SCC 480.
- **Section 22, Banking Regulation Act**: On-tap licensing of universal banks (RBI Guidelines, 2016) and differentiated banking licenses including Small Finance Banks (SFBs, 12 licensed) and Payments Banks (6 operational).

### UPI Revolution

The Unified Payments Interface (UPI), launched by the National Payments Corporation of India (NPCI) in April 2016 under the Payment and Settlement Systems Act, 2007, has transformed India's retail payments landscape. UPI processed 16.6 billion transactions valued at INR 23.4 lakh crore (approximately USD 280 billion) in December 2025 alone, making it the world's largest real-time payments system by transaction volume.

UPI operates under the NPCI's operational guidelines and the RBI's regulatory oversight. Key regulatory measures include:

- **Zero Merchant Discount Rate (MDR)**: Section 10A of the Payment and Settlement Systems Act (inserted by the Finance Act, 2019) prohibited banks and payment system providers from imposing charges on merchants for UPI and RuPay debit card transactions, with the government subsidizing NPCI's costs through annual budgetary allocations (INR 1,500 crore for FY2024-25).
- **Market Cap Regulation**: The NPCI's Circular on UPI Market Share Cap (March 2021) imposed a 30% volume cap on third-party application providers (TPAPs), directly affecting PhonePe (~47% market share) and Google Pay (~34% market share). The deadline has been extended multiple times, with a revised implementation date of December 2026.
- **UPI International**: Cross-border UPI acceptance launched in Singapore (February 2023), UAE, France, Sri Lanka, Mauritius, and Nepal, with bilateral linkages to Singapore's PayNow and planned integration with Brazil's PIX.

### IL&FS Crisis and NBFC Regulation

The collapse of Infrastructure Leasing & Financial Services (IL&FS) in September 2018, with approximately INR 91,000 crore (USD 12.5 billion) in debt, exposed systemic risks in the Non-Banking Financial Company (NBFC) sector and prompted a comprehensive regulatory overhaul. The RBI's Scale-Based Regulation for NBFCs (October 2022) introduced a four-layer structure: Base Layer (NBFC-BL), Middle Layer (NBFC-ML), Upper Layer (NBFC-UL, identified by the RBI based on size, interconnectedness, and complexity), and Top Layer (NBFC-TL, for systemically significant entities). Upper Layer NBFCs face bank-like regulatory requirements including higher CET1 ratios, mandatory listing within three years, and independent compliance functions.

### Account Aggregator Framework

The RBI's Account Aggregator (AA) framework, implemented through the Master Direction on Non-Banking Financial Company - Account Aggregator (Reserve Bank) Directions, 2016 (updated 2021), established India as a pioneer in consent-based financial data sharing. Operating on the Data Empowerment and Protection Architecture (DEPA), Account Aggregators enable customers to share financial data across banks, NBFCs, insurance companies, and mutual funds through encrypted data flows, without storing customer data. As of 2025, over 1.8 billion accounts were linked to the AA ecosystem.

## Africa

### Nigeria

The Central Bank of Nigeria (CBN) exercises comprehensive regulatory authority under the CBN Act 2007 and the Banks and Other Financial Institutions Act (BOFIA) 2020, which replaced the repealed BOFIA 1991. BOFIA 2020 expanded the CBN's supervisory perimeter to include fintech companies and introduced enhanced penalties for regulatory violations (Section 60: fines up to NGN 5 million per day for continuing offenses; imprisonment up to 10 years for directors).

Nigeria's banking sector comprises 24 deposit money banks (DMBs) following the 2005 consolidation exercise that reduced banks from 89 to 25 through a minimum capital requirement increase from NGN 2 billion to NGN 25 billion. The CBN subsequently classified banks into international (NGN 50 billion minimum capital), national (NGN 25 billion), and regional (NGN 10 billion) tiers. A 2024 recapitalization exercise further increased minimum capital requirements to NGN 500 billion for international banks, NGN 200 billion for national banks, and NGN 50 billion for regional banks, with a March 2026 compliance deadline.

Key regulatory innovations include:

- **Digital Banking Licensing**: The CBN's Framework for the Licensing and Regulation of Payment Service Banks (2018) and the Framework for Licensing of Digital Banks (January 2021) created three license categories -- digital bank with national authorization, digital bank with regional authorization, and payment service bank -- enabling fintechs such as Kuda, Moniepoint, and OPay to operate. Regulatory arbitrage between payment service bank licenses and mobile money operator licenses (under the CBN Guidelines on Mobile Money Services, 2015) has created complexity.
- **eNaira**: Launched on October 25, 2021, as Africa's first CBDC, the eNaira operates on a Hyperledger Fabric blockchain under the CBN's direct management. Adoption has been slow, with approximately 13 million wallets but limited transaction volumes relative to mobile money and POS transactions. The CBN issued the eNaira Design Paper and Guidelines on the eNaira (2021) but dedicated CBDC legislation remains pending.
- **Cash Withdrawal Limits**: The controversial CBN Circular on Cash Withdrawal Limits (December 2022) restricted individual cash withdrawals to NGN 100,000/week and corporate withdrawals to NGN 500,000/week, ostensibly to promote a cashless economy. This policy, combined with the chaotic redesign of the NGN 200, 500, and 1,000 banknotes, was partially struck down by the Supreme Court in AG Federation v. AG Zamfara & Ors (2023) for exceeding the CBN's statutory authority.

### Sudafrica

The South African Reserve Bank (SARB) regulates banks under the SARB Act 90 of 1989 and the Banks Act 94 of 1990, supplemented by the Financial Sector Regulation Act 9 of 2017 (FSRA), which introduced the Twin Peaks regulatory model. The Prudential Authority (PA), housed within the SARB, is responsible for safety and soundness supervision, while the Financial Sector Conduct Authority (FSCA) oversees market conduct.

South Africa's banking sector is highly concentrated, with the "Big Five" (Standard Bank, FirstRand, Absa, Nedbank, and Capitec) controlling approximately 90% of banking assets. The Dedicated Banks Bill (2018, not yet enacted) would create a framework for lower-cost retail banking, while mutual banks operate under the Mutual Banks Act 124 of 1993.

TymeBank, launched in 2019 as South Africa's first fully digital bank, obtained a full banking license under the Banks Act and has attracted over 9 million customers, demonstrating the viability of low-cost digital banking models in Africa.

## America Latina

### Brasil

Brazil's Central Bank (BCB), established by Law 4,595/1964 and granted formal autonomy by Complementary Law 179/2021, has emerged as a global fintech regulatory pioneer. Key innovations include:

- **PIX**: Launched on November 16, 2020 under BCB Resolution 1/2020, PIX is Brazil's instant payment system that processed over 42 billion transactions in 2024 alone, surpassing credit card, debit card, and bank transfer volumes combined. PIX is mandatory for all financial institutions with over 500,000 active accounts. PIX features include Pix Saque (cash-out at merchants), Pix Troco (cashback), and Pix Automático (scheduled payments). Security measures include the Special Return Mechanism (MED, BCB Resolution 103/2021) enabling transaction reversal in fraud cases within 80 minutes.
- **Open Finance**: Brazil's Open Finance framework, one of the most comprehensive globally, operates under Joint Resolution 1/2020 (CMN and BCB) with four phases mandating the sharing of product information, customer data, payment initiation, and insurance/investment/pension data. As of 2025, over 800 participating institutions share data covering over 30 million consenting customers through standardized APIs.
- **Regulatory Sandbox**: BCB's sandbox framework (Resolution 29/2020) has run multiple cohorts testing innovations including tokenized assets, fractional real estate investment, and decentralized finance applications.
- **Digital Real (Drex)**: The BCB's wholesale CBDC pilot, branded Drex, utilizes Hyperledger Besu (Ethereum-based) technology for tokenized asset settlement. The pilot involves 16 consortium groups and focuses on use cases including tokenized government bonds, trade finance, and interbank settlement.

### Mexico

The Bank of Mexico (Banxico) and the National Banking and Securities Commission (CNBV) jointly regulate Mexico's banking sector under the Credit Institutions Law (Ley de Instituciones de Crédito) and the Fintech Law (Ley para Regular las Instituciones de Tecnología Financiera, 2018). Mexico's Fintech Law was the first comprehensive fintech regulatory framework in Latin America, creating two license categories: Electronic Payment Funds Institutions (IFPEs) and Crowdfunding Institutions (ITFCs). The law also established a regulatory sandbox and required CNBV authorization for virtual asset operations. As of 2025, the CNBV had issued approximately 40 fintech licenses under the Fintech Law.

Mexico's banking sector is dominated by foreign-owned institutions: Citibanamex (Grupo México acquisition pending), BBVA México (BBVA Spain), Santander México, HSBC México, and Scotiabank México collectively hold over 70% of banking assets. The Mexican Deposit Insurance Institute (IPAB) provides deposit insurance up to 400,000 UDIs (approximately MXN 3.2 million).

## Sudeste Asiatico

### Indonesia

Indonesia's Financial Services Authority (Otoritas Jasa Keuangan, OJK), established by Law 21/2011 and assuming full authority from Bank Indonesia (BI) in 2013, regulates banking, capital markets, and non-bank financial institutions under a consolidated supervisory model. Bank Indonesia retains monetary policy, macroprudential supervision, and payment systems authority under the BI Law (Law 23/1999, as amended by Law 6/2009 and Law 4/2023).

Key regulatory features include:

- **BUKU (Bank Umum Berdasarkan Kegiatan Usaha) Classification**: Now renamed KBMI (Kelompok Bank Berdasarkan Modal Inti) under OJK Regulation 12/POJK.03/2021, banks are classified into four groups based on core capital: KBMI 1 (IDR 1-6 trillion), KBMI 2 (IDR 6-14 trillion), KBMI 3 (IDR 14-70 trillion), and KBMI 4 (above IDR 70 trillion). Digital banking activities are permitted across all KBMI groups under OJK Regulation 12/POJK.03/2021 on Commercial Banks.
- **P2P Lending Regulation**: OJK Regulation 10/POJK.05/2022 governs peer-to-peer lending platforms, requiring minimum capital of IDR 25 billion, maximum lending interest rate caps, and borrower data protection measures. As of 2025, approximately 100 licensed P2P platforms operate in Indonesia, down from 164 at peak.
- **Digital Rupiah**: Bank Indonesia's Project Garuda, announced in 2022, outlines a three-phase CBDC development roadmap. The wholesale digital rupiah pilot launched in 2024, focusing on interbank settlement and government bond transactions.

### Filipinas

The Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas (BSP), empowered by the New Central Bank Act (RA 7653, as amended by RA 11211 in 2019), gained financial and administrative autonomy, increased its capitalization from PHP 50 billion to PHP 200 billion, and strengthened its supervisory and resolution powers. The BSP regulates banks under the General Banking Law (RA 8791) and the National Payment Systems Act (RA 11127, 2018).

The BSP's digital banking framework (Circular 1105, Series of 2020) licensed six digital banks: Tonik, GoTyme (partnership of Tyme Group and Gokongwei Group), Maya Bank (Voyager Innovations/PLDT), UNObank, UnionDigital (UnionBank subsidiary), and OFBank (Overseas Filipino Bank, Land Bank subsidiary). Digital bank requirements include minimum capital of PHP 1 billion, compliance with the BSP's IT Risk Management Framework (Circular 808), and physical or virtual customer assistance mechanisms.

Key regulatory initiatives include:

- **QR Ph**: The BSP's standardized QR code payment system, launched in 2019 using the EMVCo QR standard, enables interoperability across all BSP-supervised financial institutions.
- **Open Finance Framework**: BSP Circular 1122 (2021) established the Open Finance Framework promoting API-based data sharing among financial institutions, with voluntary adoption transitioning toward mandatory participation.
- **Virtual Asset Service Provider (VASP) Registration**: BSP Circular 1108 (2021) requires VASPs to register with the BSP and comply with anti-money laundering, cybersecurity, and consumer protection requirements.
- **Financial Inclusion Strategy**: The BSP's National Strategy for Financial Inclusion (NSFI) 2022-2028 targets banking the 44% of Filipino adults who remain unbanked (as of 2021 Financial Inclusion Survey), leveraging GCash (operated by Mynt/Globe Fintech Innovations) and Maya (operated by Voyager Innovations) as primary digital payment channels.

## Analisis Comparativo

### State Direction vs. Market Innovation

The banking systems of the Global South reveal a spectrum from state-directed (China, where the Big Five state banks execute government policy priorities including infrastructure lending and SME support) to market-driven innovation regulated ex post (Brazil, where PIX and Open Finance were regulator-initiated but driven by competitive dynamics among banks and fintechs). India occupies an intermediate position where the state-owned banks (controlling approximately 60% of banking assets) coexist with private banks and fintechs that drive most innovation, with the RBI actively shaping the ecosystem through UPI, Account Aggregator, and licensing frameworks.

### Financial Inclusion Strategies

Each jurisdiction addresses financial inclusion through distinct mechanisms: India's JAM trinity (Jan Dhan accounts, Aadhaar biometric ID, Mobile connectivity) brought over 500 million previously unbanked adults into the formal system; Nigeria's tiered KYC regime (CBN Circular on Three-Tiered KYC Requirements, 2013) allows accounts with reduced documentation; Brazil's PIX eliminated the cost barrier to digital payments; and the Philippines' GCash ecosystem reaches rural populations through agent networks. China's mobile payment ecosystem (Alipay and WeChat Pay, collectively processing over USD 30 trillion annually) achieved near-universal coverage but raised concentration concerns addressed by the PBOC's antitrust investigation of Ant Group (2020-2021) and the requirement for payment platform interoperability.

### CBDC Experimentation

The Global South leads CBDC development, with China's e-CNY, Nigeria's eNaira, and Brazil's Drex representing the three most advanced CBDC projects in developing countries. Their contrasting designs -- e-CNY as a retail CBDC substitute for cash with managed anonymity, eNaira as a financial inclusion tool with limited adoption, and Drex as a wholesale settlement infrastructure -- reflect different policy priorities and technological choices. India's e-Rupee pilot remains smaller in scope, while Indonesia and the Philippines have pursued cautious exploratory approaches.
