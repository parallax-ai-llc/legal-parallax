## Resumen

Anti-greenwashing regulation -- the body of law designed to prevent and penalize false, misleading, or unsubstantiated environmental claims by businesses in their marketing, advertising, labeling, and sustainability reporting -- has emerged as a critical regulatory priority as the proliferation of ESG commitments and green marketing has outpaced the ability of consumers, investors, and regulators to verify such claims. South Korea addresses greenwashing through the Act on Fair Labeling and Advertising (표시·광고의 공정화에 관한 법률), enforced by the Korea Fair Trade Commission (KFTC, 공정거래위원회), supplemented by the Environmental Technology and Industry Support Act (환경기술 및 환경산업 지원법) which governs environmental product declarations and eco-labeling, and the Framework Act on Carbon Neutrality (탄소중립기본법) which addresses misleading carbon neutrality claims; the Korean government has increased enforcement actions against deceptive green claims, with the KFTC issuing corrective orders and fines against companies making unsubstantiated "eco-friendly" and "carbon-neutral" claims in consumer products and financial services. The United States regulates greenwashing primarily through Section 5 of the Federal Trade Commission Act (15 U.S.C. Section 45), which prohibits unfair or deceptive acts or practices, supplemented by the FTC's Guides for the Use of Environmental Marketing Claims ("Green Guides," 16 C.F.R. Part 260), last revised in 2012 and currently undergoing a comprehensive update initiated in December 2022; the SEC additionally addresses greenwashing in financial markets through anti-fraud provisions of the Securities Act and Securities Exchange Act, having brought notable enforcement actions including the $19 million settlement with Goldman Sachs Asset Management (2022) and $25 million with DWS/Deutsche Bank (2023) for ESG-related misstatements. Germany combats greenwashing through the Gesetz gegen den unlauteren Wettbewerb (UWG, Act Against Unfair Competition) which prohibits misleading commercial practices, supplemented by EU directives including the recently adopted Directive on Empowering Consumers for the Green Transition (Directive 2024/825) and the proposed EU Green Claims Directive (COM/2023/166), with German courts having developed an extensive body of greenwashing case law through actions brought by competitors and consumer organizations under the UWG. The EU has established the most comprehensive anti-greenwashing framework globally, combining the Unfair Commercial Practices Directive (UCPD, 2005/29/EC) as amended by the Empowering Consumers Directive, the proposed Green Claims Directive requiring substantiation and verification of environmental claims, the CSRD/ESRS framework for sustainability reporting, and the SFDR for financial product green claims, creating a multi-layered regulatory architecture addressing greenwashing across consumer markets, corporate reporting, and financial services.

## Corea del Sur

### Marco juridico

South Korea's anti-greenwashing regime operates through a combination of consumer protection, environmental, and financial regulatory laws. The Fair Labeling and Advertising Act (표시·광고의 공정화에 관한 법률) serves as the primary instrument, prohibiting false or exaggerated advertising, deceptive advertising, and unfairly comparative advertising across all sectors.

### KFTC Enforcement

The Korea Fair Trade Commission enforces advertising standards through corrective orders (시정명령), surcharges (과징금) of up to 2 percent of relevant revenue, and criminal referrals for serious violations. The KFTC has increasingly targeted environmental claims:

| Ano | Action | Details |
|------|--------|---------|
| 2022 | Corrective orders against cosmetics companies | Unsubstantiated "natural" and "eco-friendly" ingredient claims |
| 2023 | Investigation of automotive green claims | Misleading fuel efficiency and emissions comparisons in advertising |
| 2023 | Guidance on ESG advertising | Published guidelines requiring substantiation for corporate sustainability claims |
| 2024 | Enforcement against food packaging | "Carbon neutral" claims on consumer products without recognized certification |

### Environmental Labeling

The Environmental Technology and Industry Support Act (환경기술 및 환경산업 지원법) established the Korea Eco-Label system (환경표지) administered by the Korea Environmental Industry and Technology Institute (KEITI, 한국환경산업기술원). The Korea Eco-Label applies to 170+ product categories, and unauthorized use of the eco-label or confusingly similar marks constitutes a criminal offense. Environmental product declarations (환경성적표지) provide verified life-cycle environmental impact data.

### Carbon Neutrality Claims

The Framework Act on Carbon Neutrality (탄소중립기본법) and its implementing regulations address carbon neutrality claims by entities. The Ministry of Environment (환경부) operates a carbon offset certification system, and claims of "carbon neutral" status must be based on verified emissions inventories and recognized offset credits. The Korean government has signaled increased scrutiny of corporate net-zero pledges that lack credible implementation pathways.

### Financial Sector Greenwashing

The FSC and FSS have issued guidelines on ESG-related disclosures by financial institutions, focusing on preventing misleading marketing of ESG funds and green financial products. The FSS conducted a thematic review in 2023 finding that approximately 40 percent of self-labeled ESG funds in Korea did not apply systematic ESG integration methodologies.

## Estados Unidos

### Marco juridico

The United States addresses greenwashing through general consumer protection and advertising law rather than dedicated green claims legislation. The Federal Trade Commission Act Section 5 (15 U.S.C. Section 45) broadly prohibits "unfair or deceptive acts or practices in or affecting commerce," providing the legal basis for FTC enforcement against misleading environmental claims.

### FTC Green Guides (16 C.F.R. Part 260)

The Green Guides, first issued in 1992 and last revised in 2012, provide guidance on environmental marketing claims. While technically non-binding guidance rather than enforceable rules, the FTC can bring enforcement actions under Section 5 for claims inconsistent with the Guides. Key provisions include:

- **General Environmental Benefit Claims:** Should be qualified to avoid implying that a product provides a general environmental benefit; unqualified "green" or "eco-friendly" claims are almost certainly deceptive
- **Carbon Offsets:** Must represent actual emissions reductions; must not have already occurred (additionality)
- **Degradable/Compostable/Recyclable Claims:** Specific standards for each type of claim, including conditions and qualifications
- **"Free of" Claims:** Must be truthful and not misleading about the significance of the absence

### Green Guides Update (2022-Present)

In December 2022, the FTC initiated a regulatory review of the Green Guides, seeking public comment on potential updates to address carbon offset claims, "net zero" and "carbon neutral" marketing, recyclability claims in the context of limited recycling infrastructure, "sustainable" and "ESG" as marketing terms, and claims related to climate change. The updated Guides are expected to be finalized in 2025 or 2026 and may be promulgated as binding rules rather than guidance, significantly strengthening enforcement.

### SEC Financial Greenwashing Enforcement

The SEC has pursued greenwashing in financial markets through its existing anti-fraud authority:

| Case | Ano | Outcome |
|------|------|---------|
| BNY Mellon Investment Adviser | 2022 | $1.5 million penalty for ESG quality review misstatements |
| Goldman Sachs Asset Management | 2022 | $19 million penalty for ESG integration failures in mutual funds and separately managed accounts |
| DWS (Deutsche Bank subsidiary) | 2023 | $25 million SEC penalty ($19M CFTC) for ESG overstatements in marketing materials |
| Wahed Invest | 2024 | Enforcement action for misleading ESG compliance claims |

### Lanham Act (15 U.S.C. Section 1125)

Section 43(a) of the Lanham Act provides a private right of action for competitors injured by false or misleading advertising, including environmental claims. Notable cases include Coca-Cola Co. v. Procter & Gamble (green packaging claims) and various challenges to "biodegradable" plastics advertising. This competitor-driven enforcement mechanism provides a significant supplement to government enforcement.

### State-Level Enforcement

State attorneys general have become increasingly active in greenwashing enforcement. New York Attorney General Letitia James filed suit against JBS S.A. in 2024 for "net zero" and sustainability claims that allegedly lacked credible plans. California's SB 1036 (2024) expanded the state's false advertising law to specifically cover environmental marketing claims.

## Alemania

### Marco juridico

Germany's anti-greenwashing enforcement operates primarily through the Gesetz gegen den unlauteren Wettbewerb (UWG, Act Against Unfair Competition), which prohibits misleading commercial practices (Section 5 UWG) and provides robust private enforcement mechanisms allowing competitors, trade associations, and consumer organizations to seek injunctions and damages.

### UWG Greenwashing Case Law

German courts have developed extensive greenwashing jurisprudence through UWG actions:

**"Klimaneutral" (Climate Neutral) Claims:** The BGH (Bundesgerichtshof, Federal Court of Justice) addressed climate neutrality claims in a series of decisions holding that:
- A claim of "klimaneutral" may be misleading if consumers understand it to mean zero emissions rather than offset-based neutrality (BGH, Judgment of June 27, 2024, I ZR 98/23)
- Companies must clearly communicate whether "climate neutral" is achieved through actual emissions reductions, carbon offsets, or a combination
- The type, quality, and verification standard of offsets must be accessible to consumers

**LG Karlsruhe (2023):** Held that a "climate neutral" claim on consumer product packaging was misleading where the company relied entirely on offset certificates without disclosing this basis; ordered removal of the claim.

**OLG Düsseldorf (2023):** Found that vague "sustainable" claims on financial products required substantiation through specific, verifiable criteria and that general ESG commitments did not suffice.

**LG München I (2023):** Prohibited a food company from advertising products as "CO2-positive" where the claimed carbon removal was based on unverified future offset projects.

### Abmahnverfahren (Warning Procedure)

The German Abmahnverfahren system allows competitors and authorized consumer protection organizations to issue formal cease-and-desist warnings (Abmahnungen) before filing suit, creating a low-cost, rapid enforcement mechanism. Deutsche Umwelthilfe (DUH, German Environmental Aid) has been particularly active, filing dozens of greenwashing complaints against major corporations including TotalEnergies, Shell, and various airlines for misleading carbon neutrality advertising.

### EU Directive Transpositions

Germany is transposing the Empowering Consumers for the Green Transition Directive (2024/825) through amendments to the UWG and Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB, Civil Code), which will:
- Prohibit generic environmental claims ("environmentally friendly," "eco") without recognized certification or substantiation
- Ban sustainability labels not based on third-party certification schemes
- Prohibit claims of environmental neutral/reduced/positive impact based solely on offsetting
- Strengthen consumer remedies for misleading green claims

### BaFin and Financial Greenwashing

BaFin, as the integrated financial supervisor, enforces SFDR and EU Taxonomy requirements for German financial institutions. BaFin conducted sustainability-related inspections of asset managers beginning in 2022 and issued guidance on sustainable investment fund naming conventions, prohibiting the use of "sustainable," "green," or "ESG" in fund names without meeting minimum sustainability criteria.

## CEDH y UE

### Unfair Commercial Practices Directive (UCPD) Amendments

The Empowering Consumers for the Green Transition Directive (Directive 2024/825), adopted on February 28, 2024, amends the UCPD (2005/29/EC) to specifically target greenwashing:

**New Prohibited Practices (added to Annex I "Blacklist"):**
- Displaying a sustainability label not based on a certification scheme or established by public authorities
- Making a generic environmental claim without recognized excellent environmental performance or compliance with environmental criteria
- Making an environmental claim about the entire product when it actually concerns only a certain aspect
- Claiming carbon neutral/reduced/positive environmental impact based on offsetting schemes

**Enhanced Misleading Actions (Article 6):**
- Environmental or social claims that are unsubstantiated
- Sustainability labels not verified by third parties
- Future environmental performance claims without clear, objective, measurable commitments and independent monitoring

Member states must transpose the Directive by March 27, 2026.

### Proposed Green Claims Directive (COM/2023/166)

The proposed Green Claims Directive, presented on March 22, 2023, would establish the most comprehensive framework globally for substantiating and communicating environmental claims:

| Requirement | Details |
|-------------|---------|
| **Substantiation** | Claims must be based on widely recognized scientific evidence, using life-cycle considerations, covering all significant environmental aspects |
| **Verification** | Independent third-party verification before claims can be made to consumers |
| **Communication** | Specific rules on how environmental claims must be presented, including information on whether the product/trader performs better than common practice |
| **Carbon Offset Transparency** | Offsetting information must be provided separately from the claim about the product's environmental performance |
| **Penalties** | Maximum fines of at least 4% of annual turnover; exclusion from public procurement; confiscation of revenues |

The Directive completed trilogue negotiations and political agreement was reached in early 2025, with formal adoption expected in 2025.

### SFDR Anti-Greenwashing (Financial Products)

The Sustainable Finance Disclosure Regulation (SFDR, Regulation 2019/2088) addresses financial greenwashing through mandatory disclosures:
- Pre-contractual and periodic reporting on sustainability characteristics (Article 8 products) or sustainable investment objectives (Article 9 products)
- Principal adverse impact statements at entity level
- Product-level taxonomy alignment disclosures

The European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) published its final report on greenwashing in June 2024, finding widespread misleading sustainability claims across the financial value chain and recommending enhanced enforcement coordination.

### ESMA Fund Naming Guidelines

ESMA's Guidelines on Funds' Names Using ESG or Sustainability-Related Terms (effective November 2024) require:
- Funds using "ESG" in their name: minimum 80% of investments meeting ESG characteristics plus Paris-Aligned Benchmark exclusions
- Funds using "sustainable" or "sustainability" in their name: minimum 80% meeting ESG characteristics, with at least 50% being sustainable investments under SFDR Article 2(17), plus Climate Transition Benchmark exclusions

### ECHR Dimensions

While the ECHR has not directly addressed greenwashing, Article 10 (freedom of expression) case law recognizes that commercial speech receives a lower level of protection than political speech, permitting regulatory restrictions on advertising that mislead consumers. Article 8 (right to private and family life) and the right to environmental information established in Guerra v. Italy and Tatar v. Romania support access to accurate environmental information as a fundamental right.

## Analisis Comparativo

### Enforcement Paradigms

The jurisdictions demonstrate fundamentally different enforcement approaches. Germany relies heavily on private enforcement through UWG competitor and consumer organization actions, creating a decentralized but highly active enforcement ecosystem. The United States combines FTC public enforcement with Lanham Act private competitor suits and state attorney general actions. South Korea primarily relies on centralized KFTC enforcement. The EU is moving toward a dual system of public enforcement (by national consumer authorities) and private enforcement (by qualified entities under the Representative Actions Directive).

### Substantiation Standards

A clear trend toward requiring pre-market substantiation of environmental claims is emerging. The EU's proposed Green Claims Directive would require third-party verification before claims can be made, the most stringent standard globally. The FTC's updated Green Guides may adopt stronger substantiation requirements but are unlikely to mandate pre-verification. Germany's courts have already effectively required substantiation through UWG case law. South Korea's standards remain primarily post-hoc, with claims challenged after publication.

### Carbon Offset Claims

The regulation of carbon neutrality and offset-based claims is the most rapidly evolving area. The EU's Empowering Consumers Directive effectively bans claims of carbon neutrality based solely on offsetting. Germany's BGH has established that offset-based climate neutrality claims must be transparently disclosed. The FTC Green Guides update is expected to address offset quality and additionality requirements. This converging skepticism toward offset-based claims represents a significant shift from the voluntary carbon market's growth trajectory.

### Financial vs. Consumer Greenwashing

A notable distinction exists between consumer-facing greenwashing (regulated through advertising and consumer protection law) and financial greenwashing (regulated through securities and financial services law). The EU uniquely addresses both through an integrated framework (UCPD/Green Claims for consumer markets; SFDR/Taxonomy for financial markets; CSRD for corporate reporting). The United States has separate regimes (FTC for consumer; SEC for financial) that do not always coordinate. South Korea's FSC and KFTC are increasingly coordinating on ESG-related greenwashing.

### Climate Litigation Nexus

Greenwashing claims are increasingly intertwined with climate litigation. NGOs like ClientEarth, Deutsche Umwelthilfe, and Greenpeace have filed complaints against major corporations challenging the credibility of net-zero pledges and green marketing, using both consumer protection and corporate governance legal bases. This represents a new frontier where environmental advocacy, consumer protection, and securities regulation converge.
