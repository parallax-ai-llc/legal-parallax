## Summary

International child abduction -- the wrongful removal or retention of a child across international borders in breach of custody rights -- represents one of the most complex intersections of family law, international law, and sovereign jurisdiction, with the Hague Convention on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction (1980) serving as the primary international instrument but its implementation varying significantly across jurisdictions. South Korea acceded to the Hague Convention effective March 1, 2013, implementing it through the Act on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction (국제아동탈취의 민사적 측면에 관한 법률), designating the Ministry of Justice as the Central Authority; Korea's accession was significant as one of the last major economies to join the Convention, and its implementation has faced challenges including a limited number of return applications, a relatively high refusal rate compared to global averages, and cultural factors including the strong emphasis on maternal custody in Korean family law. The United States was an early adopter of the Convention, ratifying it in 1988 and implementing it through the International Child Abduction Remedies Act (ICARA, 22 U.S.C. Section 9001 et seq., formerly 42 U.S.C. Section 11601 et seq.), with the Supreme Court addressing several critical interpretive questions including the definition of habitual residence in Monasky v. Taglieri, 589 U.S. ___ (2020), the scope of the Article 13(b) grave risk exception in Golan v. Saada, 596 U.S. 666 (2022), and the relationship between the Convention and domestic custody determinations; the Office of Children's Issues within the State Department serves as the Central Authority. Germany implements the Convention through the Internationales Familienrechtsverfahrensgesetz (IntFamRVG, International Family Law Procedure Act), with specialized family courts and a well-established return procedure; German courts have developed significant jurisprudence on the Article 13 exceptions and the interaction between the Convention and EU law, particularly the Brussels II ter Regulation. The European framework includes the Brussels II ter Regulation (EU 2019/1111), which establishes enhanced return procedures that override certain Convention exceptions within the EU, the ECHR's Article 8 jurisprudence requiring states to take effective measures to facilitate return while also considering the child's best interests, and landmark Grand Chamber decisions including Neulinger and Shuruk v. Switzerland (2010) and X v. Latvia (2013) that have generated debate about the proper balance between the Convention's prompt return mechanism and individualized best-interests analysis.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's accession to the Hague Convention was implemented through the Act on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction (국제아동탈취의 민사적 측면에 관한 법률), enacted on December 11, 2012, and effective March 1, 2013.

### Central Authority

The Ministry of Justice (법무부) serves as the Central Authority, responsible for receiving and processing applications for the return of children and for access rights. The Ministry has established a specialized unit for handling Convention cases and cooperates with foreign Central Authorities.

### Jurisdictional Concentration

The Act designates the Seoul Family Court as the exclusive court of first instance for all Convention return applications. This concentration was intended to develop specialized expertise and ensure consistent application of the Convention. Appeals lie to the Seoul High Court.

### Return Proceedings

| Stage | Procedure | Time Frame |
|-------|-----------|-----------|
| Application | Filed with Central Authority or directly with Seoul Family Court | N/A |
| Location of child | Central Authority assistance; cooperation with police | Prompt |
| Mediation | Voluntary mediation offered | Before or during proceedings |
| Hearing | Court hearing; child's views considered | Target: 6 weeks |
| Decision | Return order or refusal | Within 6 weeks of application |
| Appeal | Seoul High Court | Expedited |
| Enforcement | Civil enforcement; police assistance if necessary | Prompt |

### Challenges in Implementation

South Korea's implementation has faced several challenges:

**Low Caseload:** As of 2024, South Korea had processed fewer than 100 incoming return applications since accession, a relatively low number given its population and international mobility.

**Cultural Factors:** Korean family courts' strong tradition of maternal custody preferences may influence the application of Article 13(b) exceptions, particularly where the taking parent is the mother who was the primary caregiver.

**Enforcement Difficulties:** Physical enforcement of return orders has proved challenging, with taking parents sometimes evading enforcement through concealment or non-cooperation.

**Limited Bilateral Relationships:** South Korea does not have acceptance relationships with all Convention states, limiting the geographic scope of the Convention's application.

### Korean Law on Wrongful Removal

Beyond the Convention framework, Korean domestic law addresses parental child abduction through:

- Criminal Act (형법) provisions on kidnapping and abduction (Articles 287-292), though intra-parental abduction may not meet the statutory elements
- Civil Act custody provisions (Articles 837, 909) providing for modification of custody where a parent has wrongfully removed a child
- Passport Act restrictions on passport issuance for children subject to custody disputes

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States implemented the Hague Convention through ICARA, originally codified at 42 U.S.C. Section 11601 et seq., recodified at 22 U.S.C. Section 9001 et seq. by the Sean and David Goldman International Child Abduction Prevention and Return Act of 2014 (Goldman Act).

### Supreme Court Jurisprudence

**Abbott v. Abbott, 560 U.S. 1 (2010):** The Court held that a ne exeat right (a right to consent before the child is removed from the country) constitutes a "right of custody" under the Convention, making the removal wrongful if conducted without the holder's consent.

**Chafin v. Chafin, 568 U.S. 165 (2013):** The Court held that a Hague Convention return case is not rendered moot by the child's return to the foreign country, ensuring that courts retain jurisdiction to review return orders on appeal.

**Lozano v. Montoya Alvarez, 572 U.S. 1 (2014):** The Court held that the one-year period in Article 12 of the Convention is not subject to equitable tolling, meaning that even if the taking parent conceals the child's location, the one-year period runs from the date of wrongful removal.

**Monasky v. Taglieri, 589 U.S. ___ (2020):** The Court held that the determination of a child's habitual residence under the Convention is a mixed question of law and fact, reviewed on appeal under a deferential clear error standard. For an infant, habitual residence depends on the totality of circumstances, not solely on the parents' last shared intent.

**Golan v. Saada, 596 U.S. 666 (2022):** The Court held that courts considering the Article 13(b) grave risk exception are not required to consider ameliorative measures (such as undertakings or protective orders in the requesting state) before denying return. This decision rejected the categorical rule applied by some circuits requiring consideration of ameliorative measures.

### Goldman Act (2014)

The Goldman Act, named after the case of Sean Goldman who was taken to Brazil by his mother, established enhanced mechanisms for addressing countries that demonstrate patterns of noncompliance with the Convention. The Act authorizes the Secretary of State to take actions including demarches, suspension of bilateral programs, and visa restrictions against countries that fail to resolve abduction cases.

### Enforcement

|-----------|-------------|
| Civil return proceedings | ICARA |
| Criminal prosecution | International Parental Kidnapping Crime Act (18 U.S.C. Section 1204): up to 3 years |
| Federal warrant | Unlawful Flight to Avoid Prosecution (18 U.S.C. Section 1073) |
| INTERPOL Red Notice | Coordinated through NCIC/INTERPOL |
| Passport revocation | 22 C.F.R. Section 51.60 |

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany implements the Convention through the Internationales Familienrechtsverfahrensgesetz (IntFamRVG), enacted in 2005. The Act designates 22 family courts with concentrated jurisdiction for Convention cases and establishes expedited procedures.

### Procedural Features

**Expedited Proceedings:** IntFamRVG Section 38 requires courts to process return applications with particular urgency. Courts are expected to schedule hearings within the first six weeks and render decisions promptly thereafter.

**Mandatory Representation:** Unlike many jurisdictions, German law does not require mandatory legal representation for Convention proceedings, though parties are strongly encouraged to seek counsel. Legal aid (Verfahrenskostenhilfe) is available.

**Child's Hearing:** Under FamFG Section 159, the court must hear the child in person if the child has reached the age of 14, and should hear younger children unless it would be contrary to their interests. The child's views are particularly relevant to the Article 13(2) exception (child's objection).

### German Case Law on Article 13

German courts have developed significant jurisprudence on the Convention exceptions:

**BGH XII ZB 101/16 (2017):** The Federal Court of Justice (Bundesgerichtshof) held that the Article 13(b) grave risk exception must be interpreted narrowly and that the court must consider whether adequate protective measures are available in the requesting state before refusing return.

**BVerfG 1 BvR 2604/06 (2007):** The Federal Constitutional Court held that the child's fundamental rights under Article 2(1) (personal development) and Article 6(2) (parental care) of the Grundgesetz must be considered in Convention proceedings, requiring courts to conduct a constitutionally adequate assessment of the child's situation.

### Interaction with Brussels II ter

For intra-EU cases, the Brussels II ter Regulation (EU 2019/1111) supplements and partially overrides the Convention. Key provisions:

**Article 27 (Override Mechanism):** If a court refuses return under Article 13, the court of the state of habitual residence may subsequently order the child's return, and this order is directly enforceable in the state where the child is located. This "last word" mechanism ensures that the courts of the child's habitual residence retain ultimate decision-making authority.

**Article 24 (Time Limits):** Courts must issue return orders within six weeks of the application being lodged, except where exceptional circumstances make this impossible.

### Criminal Provisions

StGB Section 235 (Entziehung Minderjähriger -- child stealing) criminalizes the removal of a child from the custody of parents or other custodians by force, threat, or deception. Penalties range from fines to imprisonment up to five years, with enhanced penalties for cases involving removal to a foreign country (up to ten years).

## ECHR & EU

### Hague Convention Framework

The Convention on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction, concluded on October 25, 1980, at the Fourteenth Session of the Hague Conference on Private International Law, entered into force on December 1, 1983. As of 2026, 103 states are parties.

**Core Mechanism:** The Convention mandates the prompt return of a child wrongfully removed from or retained outside their state of habitual residence, unless an exception applies. The Convention does not address custody merits; its purpose is to restore the status quo ante and ensure that custody disputes are resolved in the appropriate jurisdiction.

**Exceptions to Return:**
| Exception | Article | Description |
|-----------|---------|-------------|
| One year + settled | Art. 12(2) | Application filed more than one year after wrongful removal and child now settled |
| Not exercising custody rights | Art. 13(a) | Left-behind parent was not actually exercising custody |
| Consent or acquiescence | Art. 13(a) | Left-behind parent consented to or subsequently acquiesced in the removal |
| Grave risk | Art. 13(b) | Return would expose child to physical or psychological harm or intolerable situation |
| Child's objection | Art. 13(2) | Child of sufficient age and maturity objects to return |
| Fundamental freedoms | Art. 20 | Return not permitted by fundamental principles of the requested state |

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Neulinger and Shuruk v. Switzerland (Application No. 41615/07, Grand Chamber, July 6, 2010):** The Grand Chamber held that in return proceedings, the child's best interests must be assessed through an "in-depth examination of the entire family situation and a whole series of factors." This decision was controversial because it appeared to introduce a comprehensive best-interests analysis into Convention proceedings, potentially undermining the Convention's summary return mechanism.

**X v. Latvia (Application No. 27853/09, Grand Chamber, November 26, 2013):** The Grand Chamber sought to clarify the relationship between the Convention and Article 8, holding that the child's best interests must be considered but that this assessment takes place within the specific framework of the Convention. Courts must give genuine consideration to the Convention exceptions but need not conduct a full custody evaluation.

**K.J. v. Poland (Application No. 30813/14, March 1, 2016):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where Polish authorities failed to take adequate measures to enforce a Hague Convention return order, emphasizing states' positive obligation to facilitate return.

### Brussels II ter Enhanced Procedures

The Brussels II ter Regulation (EU 2019/1111) establishes enhanced return procedures for intra-EU cases:

| Feature | Convention Alone | Brussels II ter Enhancement |
|---------|-----------------|----------------------------|
| Override mechanism | No | Yes: court of habitual residence has "last word" (Art. 27) |
| Time limits | 6-week target (non-binding) | 6 weeks per instance (Art. 24) |
| Child hearing | Art. 13(2) | Mandatory (Art. 21) |
| Enforcement | National law | Direct enforcement of return orders (Arts. 42-43) |
| Reasons for refusal | Convention exceptions | Limited grounds for refusal (Art. 39) |

## Comparative Analysis

### Convention Implementation Maturity

The three jurisdictions represent different stages of Convention implementation maturity. The United States, with ratification in 1988 and extensive Supreme Court jurisprudence, has the most developed interpretive framework. Germany, ratifying in 1990 and operating within the enhanced EU framework, has a well-functioning system with concentrated jurisdiction and expedited procedures. South Korea, with accession only in 2013, is still building institutional capacity and developing case law.

### Jurisdictional Concentration

All three jurisdictions employ some degree of jurisdictional concentration, but the approaches differ. South Korea concentrates all cases in a single court (Seoul Family Court), maximizing expertise but potentially creating geographic access barriers. Germany designates 22 family courts, balancing expertise with geographic accessibility. The United States allows filing in federal or state court without concentration, resulting in broader access but less specialized adjudication.

### Article 13(b) Grave Risk Exception

The interpretation of the grave risk exception is the most contested issue across all jurisdictions. The U.S. Supreme Court in Golan v. Saada rejected a categorical requirement to consider ameliorative measures, while German courts (BGH XII ZB 101/16) continue to require consideration of protective measures in the requesting state. South Korea's limited case law suggests a potentially broader application of the exception. The ECHR's Neulinger decision initially appeared to expand the exception but was narrowed by X v. Latvia.

### Domestic Violence and the Convention

All jurisdictions grapple with cases where the taking parent (typically the mother) fled the habitual residence state due to domestic violence. The Convention's grave risk exception was not originally designed to address domestic violence, and its application in this context remains contentious. The 2020 Guide to Good Practice on Article 13(1)(b) issued by the Hague Conference provides guidance but does not bind national courts.
