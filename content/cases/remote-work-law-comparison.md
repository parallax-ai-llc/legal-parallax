## Summary

Remote work -- also termed telework, telecommuting, or home office work -- has transformed from a marginal employment arrangement to a central feature of modern labor markets, with the COVID-19 pandemic accelerating adoption dramatically. The legal frameworks governing remote work must address employer obligations, employee rights, occupational health and safety, data protection, work-time regulation, and the increasingly complex cross-border dimensions of digital nomadism and international remote work. South Korea introduced remote work guidelines through the Ministry of Employment and Labor's Telework Guide (원격근무 종합 가이드라인, 2020) and subsequently strengthened protections through amendments to the Labor Standards Act (근로기준법), though no standalone remote work statute exists and the regulatory framework remains largely guidance-based. The United States has no federal remote work legislation, with regulation occurring through existing employment law frameworks including the Fair Labor Standards Act (29 U.S.C. Section 201 et seq.), the Occupational Safety and Health Act (29 U.S.C. Section 651 et seq.), and state-level initiatives such as California's reimbursement requirements under Labor Code Section 2802. Germany enacted the **Mobile Work Act** (Mobile-Arbeit-Gesetz) concept through amendments to existing workplace safety legislation and the Works Constitution Act (Betriebsverfassungsgesetz, BetrVG), while the Federal Ministry of Labour proposed a comprehensive right to mobile work (Recht auf mobile Arbeit) that was partially realized through the 2021 works council modernization. The EU adopted the **Framework Agreement on Telework** (2002) through social partner dialogue, and the **European Parliament Resolution on the Right to Disconnect** (January 21, 2021) called for a binding EU directive, while several member states have enacted domestic right-to-disconnect legislation.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea does not have a dedicated remote work statute. Instead, remote work is regulated through the existing labor law framework, supplemented by government guidelines:

**Labor Standards Act (근로기준법):** The LSA applies to all employees regardless of work location. Key provisions relevant to remote work include:

- **Article 50 (Working Hours):** Standard working hours of 40 hours per week and 8 hours per day apply equally to remote workers. Overtime is permitted up to 12 hours per week with employee agreement and must be compensated at 150% of the ordinary wage (Article 56).
- **Article 54 (Rest Periods):** Employers must provide a minimum 30-minute rest period for every 4 hours of work. For remote workers, this raises practical enforcement questions.
- **Article 58(2) (Deemed Working Hours):** Where work is performed outside the workplace and working hours are difficult to calculate, the contractually agreed hours are deemed to be the hours worked. This provision is frequently applied to remote work arrangements.

**Industrial Safety and Health Act (산업안전보건법, ISHA):** Article 5 imposes a general duty on employers to ensure the safety and health of workers. The Act's applicability to remote work environments remains somewhat ambiguous, but the Ministry of Employment and Labor has confirmed that employer occupational health obligations extend to remote work settings.

### Telework Guidelines (2020)

On June 9, 2020, the Ministry of Employment and Labor published the **Comprehensive Telework Guide** (원격근무 종합 가이드라인), which provides non-binding but influential guidance:

- **Types of Remote Work:** The guidelines define three categories: (1) home-based work (재택근무), (2) satellite office work (거점 오피스 근무), and (3) mobile work (이동 근무).
- **Employment Contracts:** Employers should specify remote work arrangements in writing, including work location, working hours, communication methods, performance evaluation criteria, and equipment provision.
- **Equipment and Expenses:** Employers are recommended (not required) to provide necessary equipment (computers, monitors, internet access) or reimburse expenses incurred by employees for remote work.
- **Privacy and Monitoring:** Electronic monitoring of remote workers is permitted but must be disclosed and proportionate. GPS tracking or continuous webcam monitoring is discouraged.
- **Occupational Health:** Employers should conduct periodic assessments of remote workers' workstation ergonomics and provide guidance on preventing musculoskeletal disorders.

### Flexible Work Arrangements Act Proposals

In 2023 and 2024, several bills were introduced in the National Assembly to establish a statutory right to request flexible work arrangements, including remote work:

- **Representative Kwon Eun-hee's bill (2023):** Proposed amending the LSA to give employees with caregiving responsibilities (children under 12, elderly family members) the right to request remote work, with employers required to provide written reasons for any refusal.
- **Representative Park Hong-keun's bill (2024):** Broader proposal covering all employees, requiring employers to consider remote work requests and establish workplace-level consultation procedures.

Neither bill has been enacted as of early 2026, leaving Korea without a statutory right to request remote work.

### Cross-Border Remote Work

South Korea's income tax framework does not specifically address cross-border remote work. Under the **Income Tax Act** (소득세법), employment income is generally sourced based on where the services are performed. A Korean tax resident working remotely from abroad may be subject to taxation in both countries, with relief available under applicable double taxation agreements (조세조약). The National Tax Service issued guidance in 2023 clarifying that foreign employers with employees working remotely from Korea for extended periods may create a **permanent establishment** (PE) risk under Korean tax law.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States lacks federal remote work legislation. Instead, remote work is governed by the application of existing employment laws to the remote context, supplemented by agency guidance and state-level requirements.

### Federal Framework

**Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA, 29 U.S.C. Section 201 et seq.):** The FLSA's minimum wage, overtime, and recordkeeping requirements apply regardless of work location. The Department of Labor has confirmed that employers must compensate non-exempt remote workers for all hours worked, including unscheduled overtime, and must maintain accurate time records. **Fact Sheet #22** addresses home workers under the FLSA.

**Occupational Safety and Health Act (OSHA, 29 U.S.C. Section 651 et seq.):** OSHA's General Duty Clause (Section 5(a)(1)) technically applies to home offices. However, in a February 2000 directive letter, OSHA clarified that it would not conduct inspections of home offices, would not hold employers liable for home office conditions, and would not require employers to inspect home offices. This position was reaffirmed during the pandemic. The practical result is minimal federal enforcement of workplace safety standards for remote workers.

**Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA, 42 U.S.C. Section 12101 et seq.):** Remote work may constitute a reasonable accommodation under the ADA. The EEOC has guidance confirming that telework is a form of reasonable accommodation that employers must consider, though it is not automatically required. Post-pandemic, EEOC v. ISS Facility Services (2023) and related cases have addressed when employers can revoke previously granted remote work accommodations.

**National Labor Relations Act (NLRA, 29 U.S.C. Section 151 et seq.):** Remote workers retain their Section 7 rights to organize and engage in concerted activity. The NLRB has addressed whether employer monitoring of remote workers' electronic communications may chill protected activity, with evolving guidance under different administrations.

### State-Level Requirements

**California (Labor Code Section 2802):** Requires employers to reimburse employees for all "necessary expenditures" incurred in connection with their duties. For remote workers, this has been interpreted to include a reasonable portion of internet costs, phone expenses, and office supplies. In **Gattuso v. Harte-Hanks Shoppers, Inc.** (42 Cal.4th 554, 2007), the California Supreme Court held that the reimbursement obligation is not waivable.

**Illinois (Expense Reimbursement Act, 820 ILCS 115/9.5):** Similar to California, requires reimbursement of expenses that are authorized by the employer or required in the discharge of the employee's duties.

**New York (Labor Law Section 198-c):** Requires reimbursement of business expenses, interpreted by courts to cover remote work expenses where the employer has directed or benefited from the remote arrangement.

### Tax Implications

Remote work has created significant state tax complications in the United States:

- **"Convenience of the Employer" Rule:** New York, Connecticut, Pennsylvania, and several other states apply rules that tax nonresident employees based on where their employer is located, even if the employee works remotely from another state. In **Zelinsky v. Tax Appeals Tribunal** (1 N.Y.3d 85, 2003), the New York Court of Appeals upheld this doctrine.
- **State Nexus:** An employer with remote employees in a state may establish sufficient "nexus" to subject the employer to that state's corporate income tax, sales tax registration requirements, and unemployment insurance obligations. **South Dakota v. Wayfair** (585 U.S. 162, 2018) broadened economic nexus concepts that apply in this context.

| Tax Issue | Approach | Example |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| Employee income tax | Generally taxed where work performed; exceptions for "convenience" rules | NY taxes remote worker even if working from NJ |
| Employer nexus | Remote employee may create corporate tax nexus | CA FTB guidance (2020) |
| Unemployment insurance | Generally owed where work performed | DOL UIPL 20-24 guidance |
| State payroll withholding | Rules vary; reciprocity agreements between some states | IL-WI reciprocity |

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany has a well-developed framework for remote work regulation, rooted in workplace safety legislation, the Works Constitution Act, and collective bargaining.

### Arbeitsstaettenverordnung (Workplace Ordinance, ArbStaettV)

The ArbStaettV, as amended in 2016, distinguishes between:

- **Telearbeit (Telework, Section 2(7)):** Defined as work performed at a fixed workstation established by the employer in the employee's home. For Telearbeit, the employer must conduct a **risk assessment** under the Arbeitsschutzgesetz (ArbSchG, Section 5), provide ergonomic equipment compliant with Annex 6 of the ArbStaettV, and ensure compliance with working time regulations (Arbeitszeitgesetz, ArbZG).

- **Mobile Arbeit (Mobile Work):** Work performed from various locations using mobile devices, not limited to a fixed home workstation. Mobile work falls outside the strict ArbStaettV requirements but remains subject to the ArbSchG general duty of care and ArbZG working time limits.

This distinction is legally significant: Telearbeit imposes substantially greater obligations on employers, which is one reason many companies prefer to classify remote arrangements as mobile work.

### Works Constitution Act (Betriebsverfassungsgesetz, BetrVG)

The **Works Council Modernization Act** (Betriebsraetemodernisierungsgesetz, June 18, 2021) strengthened works councils' co-determination rights regarding mobile work:

- **Section 87(1)(14) BetrVG:** Introduced a new co-determination right for works councils regarding the "design of mobile work" (Ausgestaltung von mobiler Arbeit), including determination of which positions are eligible, scheduling, equipment, and reachability expectations.
- This provision does not create an individual employee right to remote work, but empowers works councils to negotiate works agreements (Betriebsvereinbarungen) establishing remote work frameworks at the enterprise level.

### The Failed Right to Mobile Work

In October 2020, Federal Labour Minister Hubertus Heil (SPD) proposed a **Mobile Work Act** (Mobile-Arbeit-Gesetz) that would have established a statutory right for employees to work remotely for at least 24 days per year, provided the nature of their work permitted it. The proposal was blocked by the CDU/CSU coalition partner, and only the narrower works council co-determination provision was enacted in 2021.

The 2021 coalition agreement (SPD-Greens-FDP) included a commitment to "create a legal framework for mobile work," but no comprehensive legislation was enacted during the Scholz government's term. As of early 2026, Germany does not have a statutory right to request remote work.

### Working Time (Arbeitszeitgesetz, ArbZG)

The ArbZG applies fully to remote workers:

- **Section 3:** Maximum working time of 8 hours per day, extendable to 10 hours if averaged to 8 hours over 6 months.
- **Section 5:** Minimum rest period of 11 consecutive hours between working days.
- **Section 9:** Prohibition of Sunday and public holiday work (with sector-specific exceptions).

Compliance with the ArbZG is particularly challenging for remote workers, as the **ECJ ruling in CCOO v. Deutsche Bank** (C-55/18, May 14, 2019) established that member states must require employers to implement systems for recording daily working time. Germany has not yet enacted implementing legislation for this ruling, though a draft bill was circulated in 2023.

### Cross-Border Remote Work in Germany

Germany has addressed cross-border remote work challenges through several mechanisms:

- **EU Social Security Coordination (Regulation 883/2004):** Generally, a worker is subject to the social security legislation of the member state where they physically work. For cross-border remote workers, Article 13 provides that a person working in two or more member states is subject to the legislation of the state of residence if they perform a "substantial part" (at least 25%) of their work there.
- **Framework Agreement on Cross-Border Telework (July 1, 2023):** A multilateral agreement under Article 16(1) of Regulation 883/2004, signed by Germany and 17 other EU/EEA states, allows cross-border workers to telework up to 49.99% of their working time in their state of residence without changing the applicable social security legislation.
- **Double Taxation Agreements (Doppelbesteuerungsabkommen, DBA):** Germany's tax treaties typically allocate taxation rights for employment income to the state where the work is physically performed. Special COVID-era mutual agreements with neighboring countries (e.g., the Konsultationsvereinbarung with Luxembourg, 2020) treated pandemic-related home office days as if performed in the usual state of work, but these expired.

## ECHR & EU

### European Convention on Human Rights

The ECHR does not directly address remote work but several Convention rights are engaged:

**Article 8 (Right to Respect for Private Life and Home):** Employer monitoring of remote workers may interfere with Article 8 rights. In **Barbulescu v. Romania** (Application No. 61496/08, Grand Chamber, September 5, 2017), the Grand Chamber held that employer monitoring of electronic communications constitutes an interference with Article 8, requiring that the monitoring be foreseeable, pursue a legitimate aim, and be proportionate. For remote work, this means employers must transparently communicate monitoring practices and limit surveillance to what is strictly necessary.

**Article 11 (Freedom of Assembly and Association):** Remote workers' right to organize and participate in trade union activities must be respected. The right extends to digital forms of collective action and communication.

### EU Framework Agreement on Telework (2002)

On July 16, 2002, the European social partners (ETUC, UNICE/BusinessEurope, UEAPME, CEEP) concluded the **Framework Agreement on Telework**, implemented through national social partner agreements or legislation in member states. Key provisions:

- **Voluntary Nature:** Telework is voluntary for both employer and employee. If not part of the initial job description, the employee has the right to refuse.
- **Employment Conditions:** Teleworkers enjoy the same rights as comparable on-site workers regarding employment conditions, training, and career development.
- **Data Protection:** The employer must inform teleworkers of data protection requirements and restrictions on use of IT equipment.
- **Equipment and Costs:** The employer is generally responsible for providing, installing, and maintaining equipment and covering costs directly caused by telework.
- **Health and Safety:** The employer, workers' representatives, and relevant authorities have access to the telework location (subject to consent and prior notification) to verify occupational health and safety conditions.
- **Privacy:** The employer must respect the teleworker's privacy. Monitoring systems must be proportionate and comply with applicable data protection legislation.

### European Parliament Resolution on the Right to Disconnect (January 21, 2021)

On January 21, 2021, the European Parliament adopted a resolution (2019/2181(INL)) calling on the European Commission to propose a directive on the right to disconnect. Key elements:

- Workers should have the right to refrain from work-related electronic communications outside working hours without adverse consequences.
- The right to disconnect should be a fundamental social right.
- Member states should ensure effective enforcement mechanisms.

The Commission has not yet proposed a binding directive, though several member states have enacted domestic legislation:

| Country | Legislation | Year | Key Provisions |
|---------|------------|------|----------------|
| France | Loi Travail (Code du travail, Art. L2242-17) | 2017 | Companies with 50+ employees must negotiate right to disconnect policies |
| Spain | Ley Orgánica 3/2018 (LOPDGDD), Art. 88 | 2018 | Workers have the right to digital disconnection |
| Belgium | Act of 3 October 2022 | 2022 | Public sector and companies with 20+ employees must define right to disconnect framework |
| Portugal | Law 83/2021 | 2021 | Employers prohibited from contacting workers outside working hours except emergencies |
| Italy | Law 81/2017 (Lavoro agile) | 2017 | Smart work agreements must include rest and disconnect provisions |

### EU Social Security Coordination for Remote Workers

**Regulation 883/2004** and its implementing **Regulation 987/2009** establish the conflict-of-law rules for determining which member state's social security legislation applies:

- **Article 11(3)(a):** A person pursuing an activity as an employed person in a member state shall be subject to the legislation of that member state.
- **Article 13:** A person who normally pursues an activity in two or more member states is subject to the legislation of the member state of residence if they perform a substantial part (at least 25%) of their activity there.
- **Framework Agreement on Cross-Border Telework (July 1, 2023):** Raises the threshold from 25% to 49.99%, allowing cross-border workers to telework up to half their time in their residence state without changing applicable social security legislation.

## Comparative Analysis

### Statutory Rights vs. Soft Law

The jurisdictions reveal a spectrum from hard law to soft law approaches:

- **South Korea** relies primarily on non-binding ministry guidelines, with legislative proposals stalled.
- **The United States** has no federal remote work legislation and relies on applying existing employment laws to remote contexts.
- **Germany** has enacted specific co-determination rights but failed to establish an individual statutory right to remote work.
- **The EU** has a social partner framework agreement but no binding directive on telework or the right to disconnect.

### Employer Obligations

Equipment provision and expense reimbursement represent a key divergence:

- South Korea's guidelines recommend but do not require employer provision.
- U.S. federal law is silent, but states like California mandate full reimbursement of necessary expenses.
- Germany's Telearbeit classification triggers comprehensive equipment and safety obligations, while mobile work imposes fewer requirements.
- The EU Framework Agreement establishes employer responsibility as the general principle.

### Cross-Border Complexity

Cross-border remote work creates the most complex regulatory challenges, particularly regarding tax and social security:

- All jurisdictions face permanent establishment risks when employees work remotely from other countries.
- The EU has the most developed framework through Regulation 883/2004 and the 2023 Framework Agreement, but coordination gaps remain.
- The OECD has issued guidance on the tax treatment of cross-border remote workers, but no binding international framework exists.

### The Right to Disconnect

This emerging right illustrates the regulatory gap between EU aspiration and implementation:

- No jurisdiction among the four studied has enacted a comprehensive right to disconnect.
- France, Portugal, Belgium, and Spain lead globally on this issue.
- The EU Parliament's 2021 resolution has not yet yielded a directive.
