## Summary

Child abuse -- encompassing physical violence, sexual exploitation, emotional maltreatment, and neglect of minors -- is universally condemned but varies significantly in its legal definition, criminalization, and enforcement across jurisdictions. South Korea's framework, anchored in the Criminal Act (형법) and the Act on Special Cases Concerning the Punishment of Crimes of Child Abuse (아동학대범죄의 처벌 등에 관한 특례법), was substantially strengthened following the 2020 death of 16-month-old Jeong-in, which exposed systemic failures in the child protection system and led to sweeping legislative reforms. The United States addresses child abuse through a layered system of federal legislation -- principally the Child Abuse Prevention and Treatment Act (CAPTA) and Title 18 of the U.S. Code -- combined with fifty distinct state criminal codes, each defining abuse, mandating reporting, and prescribing penalties independently. Germany criminalizes child abuse primarily through Section 225 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), supplemented by a landmark 2000 amendment to Section 1631(2) of the Burgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB) that established children's affirmative right to a violence-free upbringing and prohibited all forms of corporal punishment. At the supranational level, the European Convention on Human Rights (Articles 3 and 8), the Council of Europe's Lanzarote Convention (2007), and EU Directive 2011/93/EU collectively impose positive obligations on states to criminalize child abuse, protect victims, and conduct effective investigations, as interpreted through a body of European Court of Human Rights case law including the landmark decisions in *A v. United Kingdom* (1998) and *Z and Others v. United Kingdom* (2001).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's child abuse legal framework operates through the Criminal Act and several specialized statutes enacted and amended over the past decade:

**Criminal Act (형법):** The foundational criminal provisions addressing child abuse are found in:

| Article | Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Art. 273 | Cruelty/maltreatment of a person under one's protection | Imprisonment up to 5 years or fine up to KRW 7 million |
| Art. 271 | Abandonment of a person under one's care | Imprisonment up to 3 years (basic); up to 10 years (resulting in death) |
| Art. 257 | Bodily injury | Imprisonment up to 7 years or fine up to KRW 10 million |
| Art. 298 | Indecent acts by compulsion | Imprisonment of at least 2 years or fine KRW 10-30 million |
| Art. 287 | Kidnapping or abduction of a minor | Imprisonment up to 10 years |

**Act on Special Cases Concerning the Punishment of Crimes of Child Abuse (아동학대범죄의 처벌 등에 관한 특례법, 2014):** Enacted on January 28, 2014 and effective from September 29, 2014, this statute heightened penalties for child abusers, established mandatory reporting obligations, and introduced emergency protective measures. The Act defines child abuse crimes broadly, encompassing acts committed by guardians or persons in positions of authority that cause physical, emotional, or sexual harm to children or constitute neglect.

**Child Welfare Act (아동복지법):** Article 17 of the Child Welfare Act prohibits physical, emotional, and sexual abuse of children, as well as abandonment and neglect. Violations are punishable by imprisonment of up to 5 years or a fine of up to KRW 50 million.

**Act on the Protection of Children and Youth Against Sex Offenses (아동청소년의 성보호에 관한 법률):** This statute criminalizes all forms of sexual abuse of persons under 19, with the age of consent raised from 13 to 16 in 2020. Production of child sexual abuse material is punishable by imprisonment of not less than 5 years.

### Protective Measures

Under the Special Act, the following protective mechanisms are available:

**Emergency Measures:** Police responding to child abuse reports may immediately separate the child from the abuser, issue a temporary restraining order, and refer the case for judicial protective disposition. Emergency measures last up to 72 hours.

**Protective Dispositions:** A family court may order separation of the child from the abuser, restriction of the abuser's parental rights, mandatory counseling or treatment for the abuser, and placement of the child in a protective facility. Dispositions may last up to one year with the possibility of extension.

**Mandatory Reporting:** Teachers, medical professionals, childcare workers, social workers, and other designated professionals are legally obligated to report suspected child abuse. Failure to report is punishable by a fine of up to KRW 10 million.

### Landmark Cases

**The Jeong-in Case (2020):** The death of 16-month-old Jeong-in in October 2020 at a Seoul hospital after she arrived with bruises, fractures, and severe organ damage became South Korea's most consequential child abuse case. The National Forensic Service concluded that the child died of serious internal bleeding caused by external force. Her adoptive mother, Jang Ha-young, was initially charged with murder and sentenced to life imprisonment by the Seoul High Court. The case exposed catastrophic failures in the child protection system -- welfare workers had visited the home multiple times but failed to remove the child. In the wake of public outrage, the National Assembly fast-tracked passage of reforms that had languished for years, revising the enforcement decrees of the Act on Special Cases Concerning Punishment of Child Abuse Crimes and strengthening mandatory reporting obligations.[^1]

**The Won-yeong Case (2013):** The death of an 8-year-old boy at the hands of his stepmother, who had subjected him to prolonged physical abuse, was a catalyst for the enactment of the Act on Special Cases Concerning the Punishment of Crimes of Child Abuse in 2014. The case highlighted the inadequacy of existing criminal provisions in addressing sustained patterns of child abuse within families.[^2]

### Criticisms and Ongoing Reform

South Korea's child abuse framework has faced persistent criticism for treating child abuse as a family matter rather than a criminal offense, a tendency rooted in Confucian cultural norms that emphasize family privacy and parental authority. The 2019-2020 legislative reforms shifted responsibility for child abuse investigations from private child protection agencies to local government authorities, and the April 2020 amendments to the Child Welfare Act and the Child Abuse Punishment Act strengthened the government's role in responding to child abuse reports. However, enforcement gaps remain, including understaffing at child protection agencies, insufficient coordination between police and social services, and a judicial tendency toward lenient sentencing in cases involving biological parents.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States addresses child abuse through a complex, multi-layered system of federal, state, and tribal laws. No single federal statute criminalizes child abuse comprehensively; instead, the federal government sets minimum standards and provides funding, while states retain primary responsibility for defining offenses, mandating reports, and prosecuting cases.

**Child Abuse Prevention and Treatment Act (CAPTA):** Originally enacted in 1974 as Public Law 93-247, CAPTA (42 U.S.C. Section 5106g) defines child abuse and neglect at the federal level as, at minimum: "Any recent act or failure to act on the part of a parent or caretaker which results in death, serious physical or emotional harm, sexual abuse or exploitation" or "an act or failure to act which presents an imminent risk of serious harm." CAPTA does not itself create criminal offenses but conditions federal funding on state compliance with minimum standards for child abuse prevention, reporting, and investigation. The Act was most recently reauthorized in 2010.[^3]

**Federal Criminal Statutes (Title 18, U.S. Code):**

| Provision | Statute | Offense | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|---------|---------|
| Sexual abuse of a minor | 18 U.S.C. Section 2243 | Sexual contact with a person under 16 in federal jurisdiction | Up to 15 years imprisonment |
| Aggravated sexual abuse | 18 U.S.C. Section 2241 | Forcible sexual acts | Up to life imprisonment |
| Sexual exploitation of children | 18 U.S.C. Section 2251 | Producing child sexual abuse material | 15-30 years (first offense); 25-50 years (repeat) |
| Selling/buying children | 18 U.S.C. Section 2251A | Commercial sexual exploitation | 30 years to life |
| Transportation for sexual activity | 18 U.S.C. Section 2423 | Transporting a minor across state lines for sexual activity | 10 years to life |
| Child abuse in Indian Country | 18 U.S.C. Section 1153 | Major crimes on tribal lands | Varies by underlying offense |

**State Criminal Codes:** Each of the 50 states maintains its own criminal statutes addressing child abuse. While definitions and penalties vary significantly, common elements include:

- **Physical abuse:** Intentional infliction of physical injury on a child, typically classified as a felony with penalties ranging from 2 to 20 years' imprisonment depending on severity.
- **Sexual abuse:** Sexual contact with or exploitation of a minor, with penalties ranging from 5 years to life imprisonment.
- **Neglect:** Failure to provide adequate food, shelter, medical care, or supervision, classified as a misdemeanor or felony depending on severity and resulting harm.
- **Emotional abuse:** Patterns of behavior that impair a child's emotional development, though this category is more difficult to prosecute and is not recognized as a standalone offense in all states.
- **Mandatory reporting:** All 50 states and the District of Columbia have mandatory reporting statutes, though the categories of mandated reporters and the penalties for failure to report vary by jurisdiction.

### Landmark Cases

**DeShaney v. Winnebago County Department of Social Services, 489 U.S. 189 (1989):** Joshua DeShaney, born in 1979, was repeatedly beaten by his father, Randy DeShaney, in Neenah, Wisconsin. The Winnebago County Department of Social Services received multiple reports of abuse beginning in 1982 -- including a hospital admission with multiple bruises in January 1983 that led to a temporary protective order -- but ultimately returned Joshua to his father's custody. In March 1984, Randy beat Joshua so severely that the child fell into a coma and suffered permanent brain damage, rendering him profoundly intellectually disabled. The Supreme Court held 6-3 that the Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause does not impose an affirmative obligation on the state to protect individuals from private violence, even when the state is aware of the danger. Chief Justice Rehnquist, writing for the majority, concluded that the state's failure to protect Joshua did not constitute a deprivation of liberty because the harm was inflicted by a private actor. Justice Blackmun's dissent, noting "Poor Joshua!", became one of the most cited dissents in constitutional law.[^4]

**Prince v. Massachusetts, 321 U.S. 158 (1944):** The Supreme Court upheld Massachusetts child labor laws against a First Amendment challenge by a Jehovah's Witness who had her nine-year-old ward sell religious literature on public streets. Justice Rutledge, writing for the 5-4 majority, established the foundational principle that "the family itself is not beyond regulation in the public interest" and that the state has broad authority to regulate the treatment of children, even against claims of parental or religious liberty. The decision declared: "The state's authority over children's activities is broader than over like actions of adults."[^5]

**Maryland v. Craig, 497 U.S. 836 (1990):** The Supreme Court upheld the constitutionality of a Maryland statute allowing child abuse victims to testify via one-way closed-circuit television, finding that the procedure did not violate the defendant's Sixth Amendment right to confront witnesses. The decision recognized the state's compelling interest in protecting child witnesses from the trauma of face-to-face confrontation with their abusers.[^6]

**Pennsylvania v. Ritchie, 480 U.S. 39 (1987):** The Supreme Court held that a defendant charged with child sexual abuse had a due process right to have privileged child protective services records reviewed by the trial court in camera for potentially exculpatory material, balancing the defendant's right to a fair trial against the state's interest in maintaining the confidentiality of child abuse reports.[^7]

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany addresses child abuse through a combination of criminal law provisions in the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), civil law protections in the Burgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB), and administrative child protection mechanisms under the Social Welfare Code (Sozialgesetzbuch, SGB VIII).

**Strafgesetzbuch (StGB) -- Criminal Provisions:**

| Section | Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Section 225 | Abuse of persons in care (Misshandlung von Schutzbefohlenen) -- torment, cruel abuse, or malicious neglect of a person under 18 in one's care | 6 months to 10 years imprisonment |
| Section 225(3) | Aggravated abuse of persons in care -- resulting in serious bodily harm, danger to life, or death | 1 to 15 years imprisonment |
| Section 171 | Violation of duty of care or upbringing (Verletzung der Fursorge- oder Erziehungspflicht) -- grossly neglecting the duty of care, risking physical or psychological harm | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine |
| Section 176 | Sexual abuse of children (Sexueller Missbrauch von Kindern) -- sexual acts with a person under 14 | 1 to 15 years imprisonment (reclassified as a felony in 2021) |
| Section 176a | Aggravated sexual abuse of children | 2 to 15 years imprisonment |
| Section 176b | Sexual abuse of children resulting in death | Not less than 5 years (up to life) |
| Section 184b | Distribution, acquisition, and possession of child sexual abuse material | 1 to 10 years imprisonment (elevated to felony status in 2021) |

**Burgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB) -- Civil Provisions:**

Section 1631(2) of the BGB, amended by the Act to Prohibit Violence in the Upbringing of the Child (Gesetz zur Achtung der Gewalt in der Erziehung) in November 2000, provides: "Children have the right to a non-violent upbringing. Corporal punishment, psychological injuries, and other humiliating measures are prohibited." This provision established Germany as one of the first countries to grant children an affirmative statutory right to a violence-free childhood, prohibiting not only physical punishment but also emotional abuse and humiliation in the context of child-rearing.[^8]

**Sozialgesetzbuch VIII (SGB VIII) -- Administrative Provisions:**

Section 8a of Book VIII of the Social Code (Kinder- und Jugendhilfegesetz) establishes the duty of the Youth Welfare Office (Jugendamt) to assess risks to child welfare and, where necessary, to intervene. The Jugendamt has authority to take a child into care (Inobhutnahme) under Section 42 SGB VIII when an immediate threat to the child's well-being exists.

### 2021 Criminal Law Reforms

In response to a series of high-profile child abuse scandals, the German Bundestag enacted the Act to Combat Sexualized Violence Against Children (Gesetz zur Bekampfung sexualisierter Gewalt gegen Kinder) in June 2021. Key changes included:

- Reclassification of child sexual abuse (Section 176 StGB) from a misdemeanor (Vergehen) to a felony (Verbrechen), raising the minimum sentence from 6 months to 1 year imprisonment.
- Elevation of possession and distribution of child sexual abuse material (Section 184b StGB) to felony status with a mandatory minimum sentence of 1 year imprisonment.
- Introduction of mandatory court-appointed guardians ad litem for child victims in criminal proceedings.
- Expansion of disqualification from employment in child-related fields for convicted offenders.

### Landmark Cases

**The Staufen Case (2017-2018):** A couple in Staufen, near Freiburg in southwestern Germany, were convicted of sexually abusing the woman's nine-year-old son and selling access to the child to pedophiles via the dark web. The mother, Berrin T., and her partner, Christian L., were charged with nearly 60 offenses including forced prostitution, rape, and severe physical abuse. Christian L. was sentenced to 12 years' imprisonment, and Berrin T. to 12.5 years. The case revealed catastrophic failures by the Freiburg Youth Welfare Office and family courts, which had allowed the child to remain in the mother's custody despite Christian L.'s prior convictions for child sexual abuse. The Staufen case directly contributed to the 2021 legislative reforms.[^9]

**The Lugde Campsite Case (2019):** Andreas V. and Mario S. were convicted of sexually abusing more than 40 children, aged 3 to 14, at the "Eichwald" campsite in Lugde, Lower Saxony, over a period spanning from 1998 to 2018. Andreas V. was sentenced to 13 years' imprisonment with subsequent preventive detention. The case exposed systemic failures by police and local child protection authorities in Lippe, North Rhine-Westphalia, who had received multiple reports but failed to act. Evidence -- including hundreds of data storage devices -- was lost by police, leading to disciplinary proceedings. The Lugde case prompted nationwide outrage and served as a further catalyst for the 2021 reforms.[^10]

**The Munster Case (2020):** Adrian V. was convicted as the principal perpetrator in a child sexual abuse network based in Munster, North Rhine-Westphalia, and sentenced to 14 years' imprisonment with subsequent preventive detention. The network involved multiple perpetrators who abused Adrian V.'s 10-year-old nephew in a garden allotment. Twenty-nine suspects were identified across Germany and other countries. The case deepened public and political pressure for the comprehensive reforms enacted in 2021.[^11]

### Impact of the Corporal Punishment Ban

Research following the 2000 amendment to BGB Section 1631(2) has documented measurable shifts in attitudes and behavior: between 1992 and 2011, the proportion of children raised entirely without violence nearly doubled from 26.4% to 52.1%, and parental self-reports of using severe physical punishment declined substantially. The ban's success has been attributed in part to its integration with public education campaigns and amendments to Section 16(1) of SGB VIII requiring family support services to promote non-violent conflict resolution.

## ECHR and EU Framework

### European Convention on Human Rights

The European Convention on Human Rights does not contain specific provisions addressing child abuse, but the European Court of Human Rights (ECtHR) has developed a substantial body of case law establishing state obligations to protect children from abuse under several Convention articles:

**Article 3 (Prohibition of Torture):** Article 3, which provides that "no one shall be subjected to torture or to inhuman or degrading treatment or punishment," has been the primary vehicle for child abuse cases before the ECtHR. The Court has held that states have a positive obligation to take measures providing effective protection for children and other vulnerable persons, including reasonable steps to prevent ill-treatment of which the authorities had or ought to have had knowledge.

**Article 8 (Right to Respect for Private and Family Life):** Article 8 protects the physical and psychological integrity of individuals within their private and family life. The Court has recognized that the obligation to protect children from abuse may require interference with family life, and that states must strike a fair balance between the rights of parents and the welfare of the child.

**Article 13 (Right to an Effective Remedy):** States must provide effective domestic remedies for victims of child abuse, including access to investigation, prosecution, and compensation.

**Article 14 (Prohibition of Discrimination):** States must ensure that child protection laws and their enforcement do not discriminate based on the child's status, including birth status, nationality, or disability.

### Landmark ECtHR Cases

**A v. United Kingdom (1998), Application No. 25599/94:** A nine-year-old boy was repeatedly caned by his stepfather with a garden cane, resulting in significant bruising. The stepfather was prosecuted under English law but acquitted after successfully arguing "reasonable chastisement" as a defense. The ECtHR unanimously held that the United Kingdom had violated Article 3 by failing to provide adequate protection to the child through its criminal law. The Court established that Articles 1 and 3 together impose a positive obligation on states to provide effective criminal law protection for children against treatment reaching the threshold of Article 3, and that the defense of reasonable chastisement was insufficient to meet this obligation.[^12]

**Z and Others v. United Kingdom (2001), Application No. 29392/95:** Four children suffered severe and prolonged abuse and neglect at the hands of their parents over several years. Despite the local authority's awareness of the situation -- social workers had documented appalling conditions including children being locked in rooms, starved, and found scavenging for food in dustbins -- no effective action was taken for over four years. The ECtHR held unanimously that the United Kingdom had violated Article 3, finding that the neglect and abuse clearly reached the threshold of inhuman and degrading treatment and that the state had failed to take reasonable measures to prevent the ill-treatment of which the authorities had knowledge. The Court also found a violation of Article 13, as the children had no effective domestic remedy.[^13]

**Opuz v. Turkey (2009), Application No. 33401/02:** While primarily a domestic violence case, the ECtHR's ruling established important principles applicable to child protection. The Court found violations of Articles 2, 3, and 14, holding that the state's failure to protect the applicant and her mother from domestic violence -- which culminated in the mother's murder -- constituted a breach of positive obligations. The decision established that a pattern of judicial and prosecutorial passivity creates a climate conducive to violence, a principle subsequently applied in child abuse contexts.[^14]

**O'Keeffe v. Ireland (2014), Application No. 35810/09:** The applicant had been sexually abused by her primary school teacher in the 1970s. The Grand Chamber held that Ireland had violated Articles 3, 8, and 13 by failing to have in place a mechanism for the detection and reporting of child sexual abuse in national schools. The Court emphasized that the state's obligation to protect children from abuse extends to the educational context and that states cannot absolve themselves of responsibility by delegating educational functions to private bodies.[^15]

### Council of Europe Instruments

**Lanzarote Convention (2007):** The Council of Europe Convention on the Protection of Children against Sexual Exploitation and Sexual Abuse, opened for signature in Lanzarote on October 25, 2007 and entered into force on July 1, 2010, is the first international instrument to criminalize all forms of sexual violence against children, including abuse committed within the family. The Convention requires signatory states to:

- Criminalize sexual abuse, child prostitution, child pornography, the solicitation of children for sexual purposes (grooming), and corruption of children through exposure to sexual content.
- Establish prevention programs, including screening and awareness measures for persons working with children.
- Provide support services for child victims, including specialized interview procedures and therapeutic assistance.
- Facilitate international cooperation in investigating and prosecuting cross-border offenses.

As of 2026, the Lanzarote Convention has been ratified by 49 states. The Lanzarote Committee monitors implementation through thematic monitoring rounds.

**European Social Charter (Revised, 1996):** Article 17 guarantees the right of children and young persons to social, legal, and economic protection, and has been interpreted by the European Committee of Social Rights to require states to prohibit all forms of corporal punishment of children.

### EU Legislative Framework

**Directive 2011/93/EU:** Adopted on December 13, 2011, this Directive on combating the sexual abuse and sexual exploitation of children and child pornography establishes minimum rules concerning the definition of criminal offenses and sanctions across EU member states. Key requirements include:

- Minimum maximum penalties of at least 5 years for sexual abuse and at least 10 years for aggravated offenses.
- Criminalization of online grooming and solicitation of children for sexual purposes.
- Mandatory disqualification of convicted offenders from professional activities involving direct and regular contact with children.
- Obligations to remove or block access to child sexual abuse material hosted within the member state's territory.
- Provision of assistance and support to child victims before, during, and for an appropriate period after criminal proceedings.

All EU member states were required to transpose the Directive into national law by December 18, 2013. A revision process was initiated in 2024 to update the Directive in light of technological developments and evolving patterns of online child sexual exploitation.[^16]

**Directive 2012/29/EU (Victims' Rights Directive):** This Directive establishes minimum standards on the rights, support, and protection of victims of crime, with specific provisions for child victims including individual assessments of protection needs, the right to be heard, and limitations on the number of interviews.

## Comparative Analysis

### Definitional Differences

The four jurisdictions reflect distinct approaches to defining and categorizing child abuse:

- **South Korea** has moved from a narrow Criminal Act definition focused on physical maltreatment (Art. 273) toward a broader statutory framework under the 2014 Special Act that encompasses physical, emotional, and sexual abuse as well as neglect. The expansion was driven by public outcry over specific cases rather than incremental policy development.
- **The United States** lacks a uniform national definition, with CAPTA providing only minimum definitional standards that states must meet to receive federal funding. This results in significant cross-state variation in what conduct constitutes criminal child abuse, particularly regarding emotional abuse and neglect.
- **Germany** distinguishes between criminal sanctions for active abuse (Section 225 StGB) and a civil law prohibition on all forms of violence in upbringing (Section 1631(2) BGB), creating a comprehensive framework that addresses both severe abuse and lower-level corporal punishment.
- **The ECHR/EU framework** does not impose specific definitions but requires states to maintain effective criminal law frameworks capable of deterring and punishing treatment that reaches the threshold of Article 3 (inhuman or degrading treatment), leaving member states discretion in defining specific offenses.

### Cultural Factors

Cultural attitudes toward parental authority, corporal punishment, and family privacy significantly influence both the content and enforcement of child abuse laws:

- **South Korea's** Confucian tradition emphasizing filial piety and parental authority historically treated child discipline as a private family matter. The shift toward treating child abuse as a criminal and public welfare issue has been driven by high-profile cases and public pressure rather than gradual cultural evolution.
- **The United States** maintains a tension between parental rights -- recognized as fundamental liberty interests under the Fourteenth Amendment since *Meyer v. Nebraska* (1923) and *Pierce v. Society of Sisters* (1925) -- and state authority to protect children, as articulated in *Prince v. Massachusetts* (1944). Corporal punishment remains legal in the home in all 50 states and in schools in 17 states.
- **Germany's** comprehensive ban on corporal punishment in 2000, and the documented cultural shift that followed, demonstrates that legal reform can drive attitudinal change. The integration of the legal ban with public education campaigns and family support services has been cited as a model for other jurisdictions.
- **The ECHR/EU framework** has progressively established that cultural or religious traditions cannot justify treatment that reaches the threshold of Article 3, as demonstrated in *A v. United Kingdom*, where the Court rejected the defense of "reasonable chastisement."

### Enforcement Mechanisms

The jurisdictions differ markedly in enforcement architecture:

- **South Korea** has shifted primary investigative responsibility from private child protection agencies to local government authorities, though enforcement remains hampered by understaffing and cultural reluctance to intervene in families.
- **The United States** relies on a decentralized system of Child Protective Services (CPS) agencies operating at the county or state level, with law enforcement involvement varying by jurisdiction and severity.
- **Germany's** Jugendamt system provides a dedicated institutional framework for child protection, with legal authority to take children into care, though the Staufen, Lugde, and Munster cases exposed significant gaps in inter-agency coordination.
- **The ECHR/EU framework** focuses on procedural obligations -- requiring effective investigation, identification and punishment of perpetrators, and access to remedies -- rather than prescribing specific institutional structures.
