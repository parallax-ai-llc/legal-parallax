## Summary

Climate change litigation has transformed from a niche legal strategy into a global phenomenon reshaping environmental governance, with a cumulative 3,099 climate-related cases filed in 55 national jurisdictions and 24 international or regional courts, tribunals, or quasi-judicial bodies as of June 2025. South Korea's Constitutional Court delivered a landmark ruling in August 2024 in Do-Hyun Kim et al. v. South Korea, unanimously finding that the Framework Act on Carbon Neutrality and Green Growth (탄소중립기본법, 2021) violated constitutional rights by failing to set adequate intermediate greenhouse gas reduction targets beyond 2030, marking the first successful constitutional climate case in Asia. Germany's Federal Constitutional Court issued the groundbreaking Neubauer et al. v. Germany decision (BVerfG, 1 BvR 2656/18, 24 March 2021), holding that the Federal Climate Change Act (Bundes-Klimaschutzgesetz, KSG) violated the fundamental rights of younger generations by concentrating emission reduction burdens after 2030, prompting an immediate legislative amendment setting a legally binding net-zero target of 2045. The United States has seen the largest volume of climate litigation globally, with cases proceeding under constitutional, statutory, and common-law tort theories, including the landmark Held v. State of Montana (2023), the first US trial on constitutional climate rights, and a wave of municipal tort actions against fossil fuel companies. The ECHR has established that state inaction on climate change violates the European Convention on Human Rights through its historic ruling in Verein KlimaSeniorinnen Schweiz v. Switzerland (2024), the first international court decision finding a human rights violation based on inadequate climate policy.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's climate litigation landscape is built on constitutional provisions and the Framework Act on Carbon Neutrality and Green Growth for Coping with Climate Crisis (탄소중립·녹색성장 기본법, 2021):

**Constitutional Provisions:**

- **Article 10 -- Human Dignity and Pursuit of Happiness (인간의 존엄과 행복추구권):** The Constitutional Court held this provision encompasses the right to a healthy and safe environment as a precondition for human dignity.
- **Article 35 -- Right to a Healthy Environment (환경권):** "All citizens shall have the right to a healthy and pleasant environment. The State and all citizens shall endeavor to protect the environment." This provision has been interpreted as imposing an affirmative duty on the state to take measures addressing the climate crisis.
- **Article 37(2) -- Limitation of Rights:** The state may restrict fundamental rights by law when necessary for national security, maintenance of order, or public welfare, but the essential content of the right must not be violated. The Court applied this provision to assess whether the government's climate targets adequately protected future generations' rights.

**Framework Act on Carbon Neutrality and Green Growth (2021):**

- **Article 1 -- Purpose:** To prevent serious impacts of the climate crisis by strengthening measures to reduce greenhouse gases and adapt to climate change.
- **Article 8 -- National GHG Reduction Targets:** Mandates a 40% reduction in greenhouse gas emissions by 2030 compared to 2018 levels, and carbon neutrality by 2050.
- **Article 25 -- Sectoral Emission Reduction Plans:** Requires the government to establish and implement sector-specific emission reduction plans, including for energy, industry, transportation, buildings, agriculture, and waste.
- **Article 38 -- Climate Change Impact Assessment:** Requires environmental impact assessments for major projects to evaluate and address climate change impacts.

**Environmental Dispute Mediation Act (환경분쟁 조정법, 1991):**

Provides an administrative mediation mechanism for environmental disputes, including those related to climate impacts, through the Environmental Dispute Mediation Committee.

### Key Principles

South Korea's climate litigation framework reflects the influence of German and European jurisprudence, particularly the intergenerational equity principle articulated in Neubauer. The Constitutional Court's 2024 ruling explicitly referenced the German BVerfG decision and the concept that one generation must not consume a disproportionate share of the remaining carbon budget while shifting reduction burdens to the future. The Court found that the government's failure to set quantitative intermediate targets between 2031 and 2049 effectively granted the current generation excessive latitude while imposing potentially catastrophic obligations on future generations.

South Korea's Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) under the Paris Agreement pledges a 40% reduction in GHG emissions by 2030 relative to 2018 levels. However, as of 2024, South Korea's emissions trajectory was not on track to meet this target, with the Climate Action Tracker rating the country's overall climate action as "highly insufficient."

### Notable Cases

- **Do-Hyun Kim et al. v. South Korea (Constitutional Court, 29 August 2024):** Four consolidated cases filed by 255 plaintiffs, many of whom were minors. The Court unanimously held that Article 8 of the Carbon Neutrality Act was unconstitutional in failing to provide adequate intermediate targets for the period 2031-2049. The Court ordered the National Assembly to amend the provision by 28 February 2026. This ruling was the first successful constitutional climate case in Asia and established that the state's duty to protect citizens' fundamental rights includes addressing the climate crisis.
- **Youth Climate Petition (2020):** Filed by 19 young people aged 11-25, this was the first climate petition to the Constitutional Court, alleging that the government's insufficient climate action violated their constitutional rights.
- **Korean Climate Litigation Coalition Cases (2020-2024):** A series of cases coordinated by civil society organizations including Green Korea United and the Korean Federation for Environmental Movement, challenging various aspects of the government's climate policy.

## United States

### Federal Framework

The United States has the largest body of climate litigation globally, with cases proceeding under diverse legal theories:

**Constitutional Claims:**

- **Due Process Clause (5th and 14th Amendments):** Plaintiffs have argued that governmental promotion of fossil fuels and failure to address climate change deprives them of life, liberty, and property without due process. The most prominent case, Juliana v. United States (No. 18-36082, 9th Cir.), alleged a substantive due process right to a "climate system capable of sustaining human life." The Ninth Circuit dismissed for lack of standing in 2020, but the case was remanded for further proceedings in 2023.
- **Public Trust Doctrine:** Several cases invoke the public trust doctrine, arguing that the atmosphere is a public trust resource that governments have a fiduciary duty to protect. This theory succeeded at the state level in Held v. State of Montana (2023).

**Federal Statutory Framework:**

- **Clean Air Act (42 U.S.C. §§ 7401-7671q):** The Supreme Court held in Massachusetts v. EPA, 549 U.S. 497 (2007), that CO2 is an "air pollutant" regulable under the Clean Air Act. However, in West Virginia v. EPA, 597 U.S. 697 (2022), the Court invoked the "major questions doctrine" to limit EPA's authority to adopt generation-shifting regulations under Section 111(d).
- **National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA, 42 U.S.C. §§ 4321-4370m):** Requires federal agencies to prepare environmental impact statements assessing the climate impacts of major federal actions, providing a basis for challenging fossil fuel projects.
- **Inflation Reduction Act (IRA, 2022):** Invested approximately $369 billion in climate and clean energy provisions. While primarily a spending and incentive statute rather than a regulatory mandate, the IRA has been cited in litigation defending continued federal climate action.

**Common-Law Tort Claims:**

A wave of municipal and state tort actions against major fossil fuel companies has emerged since 2017, alleging public nuisance, failure to warn, product liability, consumer fraud, and/or negligence:

- Municipal plaintiffs include the City of New York, City and County of Honolulu, State of Minnesota, State of Connecticut, and numerous California municipalities.
- In 2023-2024, the Supreme Court repeatedly declined to remove these cases to federal court, allowing state-court proceedings to advance under state tort law.

### State Variations

- **Montana Constitution, Article II, § 3 and Article IX, § 1:** Montana's constitution explicitly guarantees a "right to a clean and healthful environment" and requires the state to maintain and improve a clean and healthful environment. These provisions formed the basis for Held v. State of Montana.
- **Hawaii Constitution, Article XI, § 9:** "Each person has the right to a clean and healthful environment, as defined by laws relating to environmental quality." Hawaii has seen climate litigation under this provision.
- **California Global Warming Solutions Act (AB 32, 2006):** Established a cap-and-trade program and mandated GHG emission reductions to 1990 levels by 2020, later extended to 40% below 1990 levels by 2030 (SB 32, 2016).
- **New York Climate Leadership and Community Protection Act (CLCPA, 2019):** Mandates economy-wide emission reductions of 40% by 2030 and 85% by 2050, with net-zero by 2050.

### Notable Cases

- **Held v. State of Montana (2023):** In the first US trial on constitutional climate rights, Judge Kathy Seeley ruled that Montana's blanket prohibition on considering climate impacts in environmental reviews (Mont. Code Ann. § 75-1-201(6)(a)(ii)) violated the Montana Constitution's environmental rights provisions. The decision was appealed to the Montana Supreme Court.
- **Juliana v. United States (2015-present):** Twenty-one youth plaintiffs alleged that federal government's fossil fuel policies violated their constitutional rights. The Ninth Circuit dismissed for lack of standing (2020) but the case was remanded for amendment in 2023 and remains pending.
- **Massachusetts v. EPA, 549 U.S. 497 (2007):** The Supreme Court held that the EPA has authority and obligation to regulate greenhouse gas emissions under the Clean Air Act if it finds they endanger public health and welfare.
- **West Virginia v. EPA, 597 U.S. 697 (2022):** The Supreme Court limited EPA's authority under the Clean Air Act by applying the major questions doctrine, holding that generation-shifting regulations required clear congressional authorization.
- **City of Honolulu v. Sunoco LP (2020-present):** Hawaii municipal climate tort action advancing in state court, with the Supreme Court declining certiorari on the jurisdictional question in 2023.
- **Multnomah County v. Exxon Mobil (2023):** Oregon's most populous county sued 17 fossil fuel companies for $51.5 billion in connection with the deadly 2021 Pacific Northwest heat dome, alleging negligence, fraud, and public nuisance.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's climate litigation framework is grounded in constitutional law, federal climate legislation, and EU climate policy:

**Basic Law (Grundgesetz) Provisions:**

- **Article 2(1) -- General Freedom of Action (Allgemeine Handlungsfreiheit):** Protects individuals' freedom to live their lives as they choose, which the BVerfG held is prospectively threatened by inadequate climate action that would necessitate severe future restrictions on freedom.
- **Article 2(2) -- Right to Life and Physical Integrity:** Protects against threats to life and health, including those arising from climate change.
- **Article 14 -- Right to Property:** Protects against climate-related property damage and the economic impacts of abrupt future climate regulation.
- **Article 20a -- Protection of Natural Foundations of Life:** Establishes a state duty to protect the natural foundations of life "mindful also of its responsibility towards future generations." The BVerfG interpreted this provision as requiring a proportionate distribution of emission reduction burdens across time.

**Federal Climate Change Act (Bundes-Klimaschutzgesetz, KSG, 2019, amended 2021):**

- **§ 1 -- Purpose:** To protect against the effects of global climate change and to implement the Paris Agreement, including the commitment to limit global warming to well below 2°C and pursue efforts toward 1.5°C.
- **§ 3 -- National Climate Targets (as amended 2021):** GHG emission reductions of at least 65% by 2030 (compared to 1990 levels), 88% by 2040, and net-zero by 2045.
- **§ 4 -- Annual Emission Budgets:** Establishes legally binding annual emission budgets for sectors including energy, industry, buildings, transport, agriculture, and waste. The 2024 amendment shifted from annual sectoral budgets to multi-year cross-sectoral budgets.
- **§ 8 -- Climate Expert Council (Expertenrat für Klimafragen):** An independent five-member scientific body that reviews the government's emissions data and assesses the effectiveness of climate protection measures.

**Federal Immission Control Act (Bundes-Immissionsschutzgesetz, BImSchG):**

The primary federal statute regulating air pollution and industrial emissions. Amended to incorporate EU emissions trading requirements and to regulate greenhouse gas emissions from industrial installations.

**EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS):**

Germany participates in the EU ETS (Directive 2003/87/EC, as amended by Directive (EU) 2023/959), covering approximately 40% of EU greenhouse gas emissions from power generation, industry, and aviation. From 2027, the ETS 2 will extend to buildings and road transport.

### Key Principles

The Neubauer decision introduced the concept of "intertemporal freedom protection" (intertemporale Freiheitssicherung) into German constitutional law. The BVerfG held that Article 20a GG, when read together with the Paris Agreement's temperature targets, establishes a constitutionally derived "remaining carbon budget" that must be distributed equitably across generations. If the current generation consumes a disproportionate share of this budget, future generations will face either catastrophic climate impacts or draconian restrictions on their freedom -- both of which violate the Basic Law.

This doctrinal innovation has influenced climate litigation worldwide, including the South Korean Constitutional Court's 2024 decision, and has been cited in cases in Brazil, Colombia, and France.

### Notable Cases

- **Neubauer et al. v. Germany (BVerfG, 1 BvR 2656/18, 24 March 2021):** Constitutional complaints filed by Fridays for Future activists and environmental organizations. The First Senate held that §§ 3(1) and 4(1)(3) KSG were incompatible with fundamental rights insofar as they lacked sufficient provisions for emission reductions from 2031 onwards. The Court found that "one generation must not be allowed to consume large portions of the CO2 budget while bearing a relatively minor share of the reduction effort, if this would involve leaving subsequent generations with a radical reduction burden and expose their lives to comprehensive losses of freedom." The legislature amended the KSG within five months.
- **Lliuya v. RWE AG (OLG Hamm, 2017-present):** A Peruvian farmer sued the German energy company RWE AG for its proportional contribution to climate change, seeking compensation for costs of protecting his property and community from glacial flooding caused by global warming. The Hamm Higher Regional Court allowed the case to proceed to the evidentiary phase in 2017 -- the first time a court anywhere in the world permitted a private climate liability claim against a specific emitter to advance to evidence gathering. The case remains pending.
- **Deutsche Umwelthilfe (DUH) v. Federal Government (2024):** The environmental organization sued the federal government for failing to comply with the KSG's sectoral emission budgets in the buildings and transport sectors. The Berlin Administrative Court ruled in DUH's favor in November 2024, ordering the government to implement emergency climate measures, though the government appealed.
- **Greenpeace Germany v. Volkswagen (2021-present):** Greenpeace filed suit against Volkswagen in the Braunschweig Regional Court, seeking a court order requiring VW to cease manufacturing and selling combustion engine vehicles by 2030, relying on the BVerfG's Neubauer reasoning and tort law principles.

## ECHR / EU Framework

### Convention Articles

- **Article 2 -- Right to Life:** The ECtHR has held that Article 2 imposes positive obligations on states to take reasonable measures to protect life against environmental hazards, including those arising from climate change. In KlimaSeniorinnen, the Grand Chamber confirmed that this obligation extends to climate-related threats.
- **Article 8 -- Right to Respect for Private and Family Life:** Encompasses the right to live in an environment that does not pose undue risks to health and well-being. The Court applies a proportionality test requiring that the state's failure to act constitute a disproportionate interference with private life.
- **Article 34 -- Individual Applications:** The standing requirements for climate cases were significantly clarified in KlimaSeniorinnen, where the Court held that associations can bring claims on behalf of members particularly affected by climate change, but found that individual applicants who were not members of a particularly vulnerable group lacked victim status.

### Key Case Law

- **Verein KlimaSeniorinnen Schweiz v. Switzerland (Grand Chamber, 9 April 2024, App. No. 53600/20):** Over 2,500 elderly Swiss women alleged that Switzerland's inadequate climate policy violated their rights under Articles 2 and 8. The Grand Chamber held, by 16 votes to 1, that Switzerland violated Article 8 by failing to: (a) quantify national greenhouse gas emissions through a carbon budget or equivalent; (b) adopt a credible pathway to carbon neutrality; (c) demonstrate compliance through timely action; and (d) update climate targets with due diligence. This was the first ruling by an international court finding a state in violation of human rights obligations due to inadequate climate action.
- **Urgenda Foundation v. State of the Netherlands (Supreme Court, 20 December 2019, ECLI:NL:HR:2019:2007):** While a domestic Dutch case, the Supreme Court relied extensively on ECHR Articles 2 and 8 to uphold the order requiring the Dutch government to reduce emissions by at least 25% by 2020 compared to 1990 levels. This was the first case worldwide in which a court ordered a government to reduce emissions based on human rights obligations.
- **Duarte Agostinho et al. v. Portugal and 32 Other States (Grand Chamber, 9 April 2024, App. No. 39371/20):** Six Portuguese youth alleged that 33 European states' inadequate climate policies violated their rights under Articles 2, 8, and 14. The Grand Chamber declared the case inadmissible for failure to exhaust domestic remedies, establishing that climate litigants must first pursue claims in national courts before resorting to the ECtHR.
- **Carême v. France (Grand Chamber, 9 April 2024, App. No. 7189/21):** A former mayor of Grande-Synthe challenged France's climate policy. The Grand Chamber found that the applicant lacked victim status because he had moved away from the municipality, clarifying the personal nexus required for standing in climate cases.

**EU Legislative Framework:**

- **European Climate Law (Regulation (EU) 2021/1119):** Establishes a legally binding target of climate neutrality by 2050 and a net GHG reduction target of at least 55% by 2030 compared to 1990 levels.
- **EU ETS Directive (2003/87/EC, as amended):** The cornerstone of EU climate policy, creating a cap-and-trade system for industrial emissions.
- **Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD, Directive (EU) 2024/1760):** Requires large companies to identify, prevent, and mitigate adverse environmental and human rights impacts in their value chains, including climate-related impacts. Came into force in July 2024 with member state transposition required by July 2026.

### Member State Obligations

Under the evolving ECHR and EU framework, states must:

- Establish quantified greenhouse gas reduction targets consistent with the Paris Agreement temperature goals.
- Adopt and implement credible policies and measures to achieve those targets.
- Monitor and regularly update targets based on the best available science.
- Ensure public participation in climate policy-making.
- Provide access to courts for individuals and associations to challenge inadequate climate action.

The International Court of Justice issued an advisory opinion on 25 May 2025 declaring that states have binding obligations under international law, including human rights law, to prevent climate harm, regulate emissions, and support vulnerable nations -- further reinforcing the legal framework for climate litigation globally.

## Legal Theories and Strategies

### Constitutional and Human Rights Claims

Constitutional climate litigation has proven most successful in jurisdictions with explicit environmental rights provisions or strong constitutional frameworks for interpreting implied environmental rights:

- **Explicit Environmental Rights:** South Korea (Article 35), Montana (Article II, § 3 and Article IX, § 1), and several Latin American constitutions provide textual environmental rights that courts can directly enforce. These provisions lower the barrier for standing and provide a clearer legal basis for ordering governmental action.
- **Implied Rights through Interpretation:** Germany (Article 20a GG read with Articles 2 and 14) and the ECHR (Articles 2 and 8) derive climate obligations from provisions not explicitly addressing the environment. This approach requires more judicial creativity but has proven effective in the Neubauer and KlimaSeniorinnen decisions.
- **Intergenerational Equity:** The German BVerfG's concept of "intertemporal freedom protection" and South Korea's similar reasoning represent a doctrinal innovation allowing courts to assess the constitutionality of current policy based on its prospective impact on future generations' rights.

### Tort Liability Against Corporations

Corporate climate tort litigation represents a distinct and growing strategy:

- **Attribution Science:** The scientific basis for climate tort claims has strengthened significantly through carbon attribution research, particularly the Climate Accountability Institute's "Carbon Majors" dataset establishing that approximately 70% of cumulative global emissions are traceable to just 100 fossil fuel producers.
- **Causation Challenges:** The primary legal obstacle remains establishing specific causation between individual emitters and localized climate harms. The Lliuya v. RWE case in Germany is pioneering the use of proportional liability (RWE's 0.47% contribution to global emissions) to overcome the "drop in the ocean" causation defense.
- **Failure-to-Warn and Fraud Theories:** US municipal tort cases increasingly rely on evidence that fossil fuel companies knew about climate risks as early as the 1970s (paralleling tobacco litigation) and actively funded disinformation campaigns. Internal corporate documents from Exxon, Shell, and others form the evidentiary basis for these claims.

### Greenwashing and Disclosure Litigation

An emerging category of climate litigation targets misleading corporate climate claims:

- **EU Green Claims Directive (proposed):** Would establish harmonized requirements for environmental marketing claims, creating a statutory basis for challenging corporate greenwashing.
- **US SEC Climate Disclosure Rules (2024):** The SEC adopted rules requiring publicly traded companies to disclose climate-related risks and greenhouse gas emissions, providing a basis for securities fraud claims based on misleading climate disclosures. The rules were immediately challenged by both industry (arguing overreach) and environmental groups (arguing inadequacy).
- **ClientEarth v. Shell (2023):** The UK-based environmental law organization filed a shareholder derivative action against Shell's board of directors, alleging breach of fiduciary duty for failing to adopt a climate transition strategy consistent with the Paris Agreement. Though the UK High Court dismissed the claim, it generated significant precedent discussion.

### Rights of Nature

A novel legal strategy emerging primarily in the Global South:

- **Ecuador:** The 2008 Constitution (Articles 71-74) recognizes the rights of nature (Pachamama), providing a constitutional basis for climate claims brought on behalf of ecosystems.
- **Colombia:** The Supreme Court in Future Generations v. Ministry of the Environment (2018) recognized the Amazon as a legal subject with rights to "protection, conservation, maintenance and restoration."
- **New Zealand:** The Te Awa Tupua (Whanganui River Claims Settlement) Act 2017 granted legal personhood to the Whanganui River, establishing a model subsequently applied to natural features in other jurisdictions.

## Global Perspectives

- **Brazil:** In 2024, Brazil's Superior Tribunal de Justiça affirmed corporate liability for climate-related environmental harm under the National Environmental Policy Act (Law No. 6,938/1981). Brazilian courts have begun using Article 225 of the Federal Constitution (right to an ecologically balanced environment) as a basis for climate claims.
- **India:** The Supreme Court's March 2024 decision in MK Ranjitsinh & Others v. Union of India established a constitutional right to be free from the adverse effects of climate change, derived from Articles 14 (right to equality) and 21 (right to life) of the Indian Constitution.
- **Colombia:** The Supreme Court in Future Generations v. Ministry of the Environment (2018) ordered the government to halt deforestation in the Amazon, recognizing the Amazon as a legal subject with rights, in a case brought by 25 youth plaintiffs.
- **Japan:** Climate litigation remains nascent, with the first significant case filed in 2024 challenging the government's coal plant approvals under the Environmental Impact Assessment Act.
- **Australia:** In Sharma v. Minister for the Environment (2021), the Federal Court initially found that the Minister owed a duty of care to Australian children, but this was overturned on appeal. Bushfire and flood-related climate attribution litigation is emerging.
- **Nigeria:** In Centre for Oil Pollution Watch v. NNPC (2019), the Supreme Court affirmed that communities affected by oil pollution can seek remediation. Climate-specific litigation is nascent but growing in the context of gas flaring and flooding.

## Remedies and Enforcement

### Types of Remedies Ordered

Climate litigation has produced a range of judicial remedies:

| Remedy Type | Examples | Jurisdiction |
|------------|----------|-------------|
| **Declaratory relief** | Court declares government policy unconstitutional or in violation of human rights | Germany (Neubauer); South Korea (Kim); Switzerland (KlimaSeniorinnen) |
| **Mandatory orders** | Court orders specific emission reduction targets | Netherlands (Urgenda: 25% by 2020) |
| **Legislative amendment orders** | Court orders legislature to revise statutes by a deadline | Germany (KSG amendment by end of 2022); South Korea (amendment by Feb 2026) |
| **Injunctive relief** | Court prohibits specific activities or projects | Montana (Held: prohibition on ignoring climate impacts in MEPA reviews) |
| **Compensatory damages** | Court awards monetary compensation for climate-related harm | Emerging (Lliuya v. RWE: proportional damages sought) |
| **Restoration orders** | Court orders environmental restoration | Brazil (various Amazon deforestation cases) |

### Compliance and Implementation

The effectiveness of climate litigation depends critically on post-judgment compliance:

- **Germany:** Demonstrated the most rapid compliance, with the Bundestag amending the KSG within five months of the Neubauer ruling, strengthening the 2030 target to 65% (from 55%) and establishing a 2045 net-zero target.
- **Netherlands:** Compliance with the Urgenda ruling was contested. The government initially resisted but ultimately achieved the 25% reduction target by the end of 2020, partly due to pandemic-related emission reductions.
- **South Korea:** The Constitutional Court set a February 2026 deadline for legislative amendment. As of early 2026, the National Assembly is in the process of revising the Carbon Neutrality Act, with debate centering on the specificity of intermediate targets for 2031-2049.
- **United States:** Compliance is complicated by the separation of powers and political dynamics. The Montana district court's ruling in Held was appealed, and federal climate policy continues to shift with changes in presidential administration.
