## Summary

Coercion -- the use of threats, force, or other forms of compulsion to override another person's free will and compel them to act, refrain from acting, or tolerate a particular situation -- constitutes a fundamental criminal offense recognized across virtually all legal systems, reflecting the universal legal value placed on individual autonomy and freedom of decision. South Korea criminalizes coercion under Article 324 of the Criminal Act (형법 제324조, 강요죄), which prohibits compelling a person to perform an act without legal obligation or obstructing the exercise of a legal right through violence or intimidation, with penalties of up to five years' imprisonment or a fine of up to thirty million KRW. The United States addresses coercion through a fragmented framework of federal statutes -- including 18 U.S.C. Section 1951 (the Hobbs Act, covering extortion through coercion), 18 U.S.C. Section 1589 (forced labor), and 18 U.S.C. Section 241 (conspiracy against rights) -- supplemented by state criminal coercion statutes that vary from misdemeanors to serious felonies depending on the means employed and harm threatened. Germany treats coercion as a central offense against personal freedom under Section 240 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB, Nötigung), which criminalizes the use of force (Gewalt) or threat of a perceptible harm (Drohung mit einem empfindlichen Übel) to compel another person to do, tolerate, or omit an act, with the additional requirement that the coercion be "reprehensible" (verwerflich) in light of the relationship between the means used and the end sought. At the European level, the ECHR has developed robust jurisprudence on coercion primarily through Article 3 (prohibition of torture and inhuman treatment), Article 4 (prohibition of forced labor), and Article 8 (right to private life), while the EU has addressed specific forms of coercion through the Anti-Trafficking Directive 2011/36/EU and the Victims' Rights Directive 2012/29/EU.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Coercion in South Korean criminal law is governed by Article 324 of the Criminal Act (형법 제324조), situated within Chapter 31 on crimes against freedom (자유에 관한 죄). The provision states: "A person who, by violence or intimidation, compels another to perform an act which the person has no obligation to perform or obstructs the exercise of a right shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than five years or by a fine not exceeding thirty million won."

### Penalty Structure

| Offense | Provision | Penalty |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Basic coercion | Art. 324(1) | Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine up to 30 million KRW |
| Coercion against lineal ascendant | Art. 324(2) | Up to 10 years imprisonment or fine up to 50 million KRW |
| Habitual coercion | Punishment of Violences Act | Enhanced penalties |
| Coercion with dangerous weapon | Punishment of Violences Act Art. 3 | Up to 10 years imprisonment |

### Relationship to Related Offenses

Korean criminal law distinguishes coercion (강요) from several adjacent offenses. Threats (협박, Article 283) involve intimidation without the additional element of compelling specific action. Robbery (강도, Article 333) involves coercion for the purpose of taking property. Extortion (공갈, Article 350) involves obtaining property through intimidation. Sexual coercion is addressed separately under the Act on Special Cases Concerning the Punishment of Sexual Crimes (성폭력범죄의 처벌 등에 관한 특례법).

### Judicial Interpretation

The Supreme Court of Korea has developed a broad interpretation of "violence" (폭력) in the context of coercion. In Supreme Court Decision 2006Do734, the court held that "violence" need not be directed at the victim's physical person but includes any exercise of physical force that can restrict the victim's freedom of will or action. This includes blocking a person's path, seizing their belongings, or creating physical conditions that prevent free movement. The court has also held that "intimidation" (협박) in the coercion context is broader than the standalone threat offense, encompassing any communication that could induce fear sufficient to override the victim's free will.

### Workplace Coercion

South Korea has increasingly addressed coercion in workplace contexts. The amended Labor Standards Act (근로기준법) and the Serious Accidents Punishment Act (중대재해 처벌 등에 관한 법률) establish employer liability for coercive labor practices. The Anti-Corruption and Civil Rights Commission (국민권익위원회) has developed guidelines addressing coercive conduct by persons in positions of authority, particularly following high-profile cases involving chaebol families who used corporate authority to coerce employees and business partners.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States addresses coercion through multiple federal statutes and a patchwork of state laws, with no single unified coercion offense at the federal level.

**Federal Statutes:**

| Statute | Scope | Penalty |
|---------|-------|---------|
| 18 U.S.C. § 1951 (Hobbs Act) | Extortion through coercion affecting interstate commerce | Up to 20 years imprisonment |
| 18 U.S.C. § 1589 | Forced labor through coercion | Up to 20 years; life if death results |
| 18 U.S.C. § 241 | Conspiracy to deprive rights through coercion | Up to 10 years; life if death results |
| 18 U.S.C. § 242 | Deprivation of rights under color of law | Up to 1 year; up to life if death results |
| 18 U.S.C. § 1591 | Sex trafficking through force, fraud, or coercion | 15 years to life |

### State Coercion Statutes

State approaches to coercion vary significantly:

**New York Penal Law Sections 135.60-135.65:** New York distinguishes between coercion in the second degree (Class A misdemeanor, up to one year imprisonment) and coercion in the first degree (Class D felony, up to seven years). Second-degree coercion involves compelling action through threats to commit a crime, accuse someone of a crime, expose secrets, or cause strikes or boycotts. First-degree coercion involves compelling action through threats of physical injury.

**California Penal Code Section 518-527:** California addresses coercion primarily through its extortion statute, which criminalizes obtaining property or compelling official action through force or threat. The penalty is imprisonment for two, three, or four years.

**Model Penal Code Section 212.5:** The ALI's Model Penal Code defines criminal coercion as threatening to commit any criminal offense, accuse anyone of a criminal offense, expose any secret tending to subject any person to hatred, contempt, or ridicule, or take or withhold action as an official. The MPC treats criminal coercion as a misdemeanor unless the threat is to commit a felony, in which case it becomes a felony of the third degree.

### Duress as Defense

Coercion also functions as a criminal defense (duress) in U.S. law. Under the common law standard, a defendant may assert duress when they committed a criminal act under an unlawful threat of imminent death or serious bodily harm, they had a well-grounded fear that the threat would be carried out, and there was no reasonable opportunity to escape. The Supreme Court's decision in Dixon v. United States, 548 U.S. 1 (2006), held that the defendant bears the burden of proving duress by a preponderance of evidence in federal prosecutions.

### First Amendment Considerations

The distinction between criminal coercion and protected persuasion has generated significant First Amendment litigation. In NAACP v. Claiborne Hardware Co., 458 U.S. 886 (1982), the Supreme Court held that politically motivated boycotts involving speech and peaceful picketing are constitutionally protected, even when accompanied by strong language that might be perceived as coercive. However, the Court distinguished between protected advocacy and "true threats" or actual intimidation.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's coercion offense, Section 240 of the Strafgesetzbuch (Nötigung), is one of the most extensively analyzed provisions in German criminal law. The provision reads:

**(1)** Whoever unlawfully, by force (Gewalt) or by threat of a perceptible harm (Drohung mit einem empfindlichen Übel), compels another person to do, tolerate, or omit an act shall be punished with imprisonment for not more than three years or a fine.

**(2)** The act is unlawful if the use of force or the threat of harm for the intended purpose is deemed reprehensible (verwerflich).

### The Reprehensibility Clause (Verwerflichkeitsklausel)

Section 240(2) introduces a unique element in German law: the court must assess not merely whether the defendant used force or threats to compel conduct, but whether the relationship between the means employed and the end sought is "reprehensible." This means-end evaluation (Zweck-Mittel-Relation) serves as a corrective mechanism ensuring that socially acceptable forms of pressure -- such as threatening to exercise a legal right, engaging in industrial action, or using competitive business tactics -- are not criminalized.

The Bundesgerichtshof (Federal Court of Justice) has developed extensive case law on the reprehensibility test. In BGH 1 StR 447/98, the court established that the evaluation must consider the totality of circumstances, including the nature and severity of the means used, the legitimacy of the purpose sought, and the proportionality between means and ends.

### Sit-In Demonstrations and the Violence Concept

The definition of "force" (Gewalt) in Section 240 has been one of the most contested issues in German criminal law. The Bundesverfassungsgericht (Federal Constitutional Court) addressed this in the landmark second sit-in decision (BVerfGE 92, 1, 1995), holding that the broad interpretation of "force" as including purely psychological compulsion violated the constitutional principle of legal certainty (Bestimmtheitsgrundsatz) under Article 103(2) of the Grundgesetz. The court ruled that passive sit-in blockades do not constitute "force" within the meaning of Section 240 merely because they create psychological pressure on blocked individuals to stop.

### Penalty Structure

| Offense | Provision | Penalty |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Basic coercion | Section 240(1) | Fine or up to 3 years imprisonment |
| Particularly serious case | Section 240(4) | 6 months to 5 years imprisonment |
| Coercion in office | Section 240 in conjunction with Section 343 (Aussageerpressung) | 1 to 10 years imprisonment |
| Sexual coercion | Section 177 StGB (separate offense) | 6 months to 5 years; aggravated: 1 to 15 years |

### Nötigung im Straßenverkehr (Traffic Coercion)

A distinctive application of Section 240 in German law involves coercive driving behavior. Tailgating (dichtes Auffahren) with the intent to compel the driver ahead to change lanes, flashing headlights aggressively, or deliberately blocking another vehicle have been prosecuted as coercion. The BGH has confirmed that such conduct constitutes "force" when it creates a situation of physical compulsion or danger that restricts the other driver's freedom of action (BGH 4 StR 551/10).

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR has addressed coercion primarily through three Convention articles:

**Article 3 -- Prohibition of Torture and Inhuman Treatment:** The Court has held that coercion by state authorities may amount to inhuman or degrading treatment. In Gäfgen v. Germany [GC] (Application No. 22978/05, 2010), the Grand Chamber found that police threats of physical violence used to coerce a kidnapping suspect into revealing the victim's location violated Article 3, even though the threats were never carried out and were motivated by the urgent need to save the child's life. The Court held that the absolute prohibition of inhuman treatment admits of no exception, regardless of the circumstances or the victim's conduct.

**Article 4 -- Prohibition of Forced Labour:** In Siliadin v. France (Application No. 73316/01, 2005), the Court found that France had violated Article 4 by failing to provide effective criminal law protection against forced labor and servitude. The applicant, a Togolese national, had been coerced into domestic servitude through the confiscation of her passport and exploitation of her vulnerable immigration status. The Court established that states have positive obligations to criminalize and effectively prosecute coercive labor practices.

**Article 8 -- Right to Private Life:** In Vavřička v. Czech Republic [GC] (Application No. 47621/13, 2021), the Grand Chamber considered whether mandatory vaccination schemes constituted impermissible coercion, finding that the Czech system was proportionate and within the state's margin of appreciation, as it did not involve forcible administration of vaccines but rather imposed consequences (exclusion from preschool) for non-compliance.

### EU Anti-Trafficking Directive 2011/36/EU

The Directive provides the most comprehensive EU-level definition of coercion in the trafficking context. Article 2 includes "coercion" as one of the means by which trafficking can be accomplished, alongside threat or use of force, abduction, fraud, deception, and abuse of authority. The Directive requires member states to ensure maximum penalties of at least five years' imprisonment for basic trafficking offenses and at least ten years for aggravated offenses.

### EU Victims' Rights Directive 2012/29/EU

The Directive recognizes coercion victims' particular vulnerability and establishes minimum standards for the protection, support, and rights of victims of crime throughout the EU. Article 22 requires individual assessment of victims to identify specific protection needs, with particular attention to victims of crimes involving coercion such as trafficking and domestic violence.

### Istanbul Convention

The Council of Europe's Istanbul Convention (2011) addresses coercion in multiple contexts. Article 36 requires criminalization of sexual acts performed through coercion, Article 37 addresses forced marriage, and Article 38 deals with female genital mutilation performed through coercion. The Convention's definition of coercion is broad, encompassing physical force, psychological pressure, intimidation, and exploitation of vulnerability.

## Comparative Analysis

### Definitional Approaches

The four jurisdictions exhibit distinct approaches to defining coercion:

**Act-Focused (South Korea):** Article 324 focuses on the compelled act -- performing an obligation-free act or being obstructed from exercising a right -- rather than enumerating specific coercive means. This approach is broad but relies on judicial interpretation to determine the boundaries of "violence" and "intimidation."

**Purpose-Focused (United States):** U.S. federal law often defines coercion in relation to its purpose -- obtaining property (Hobbs Act), compelling labor (Section 1589), or depriving rights (Section 241). This purpose-specific approach creates comprehensive coverage but results in fragmented statutory frameworks.

**Means-End Evaluated (Germany):** Germany's unique reprehensibility clause requires courts to evaluate the relationship between coercive means and sought ends, acknowledging that some forms of social pressure are legitimate. This nuanced approach provides a built-in proportionality safeguard but introduces complexity and potential inconsistency.

**Rights-Based (ECHR):** The ECHR framework evaluates coercion through the lens of fundamental rights protections, focusing on whether state action or inaction regarding coercion violates Convention rights. This approach provides flexible, evolving standards but may offer less legal certainty than statutory definitions.

### Treatment of Passive Resistance

A revealing divergence concerns how jurisdictions treat passive resistance -- such as sit-in demonstrations or blockades:

Germany's Federal Constitutional Court has held that purely passive blockades do not constitute "force" under Section 240. In contrast, many U.S. jurisdictions treat sit-in blockades as forms of trespass or unlawful obstruction rather than analyzing them through a coercion framework. South Korea has addressed protest blockades primarily through the Assembly and Demonstration Act (집회 및 시위에 관한 법률) rather than through the coercion offense.

### Coercion in Labor Relations

All jurisdictions recognize coercion in labor contexts but through different mechanisms. South Korea relies on labor-specific legislation and the general coercion offense. The United States has developed sophisticated anti-trafficking provisions targeting forced labor. Germany applies the general Section 240 alongside specific labor protection laws. The EU Anti-Trafficking Directive provides a comprehensive supranational framework for labor coercion.
