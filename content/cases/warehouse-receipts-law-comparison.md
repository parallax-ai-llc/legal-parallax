## Summary

Warehouse receipts are documents issued by a warehouse operator acknowledging the receipt of goods for storage, serving the dual function of evidence of the bailment relationship and, when negotiable, as a document of title enabling the transfer of constructive possession of stored goods without physical movement. Warehouse receipts play a critical role in commodity financing, agricultural trade, and collateral management, as they allow stored goods to be pledged, traded, and financed through documentary transactions. South Korea regulates warehouse receipts through the Korean Commercial Code (상법) Articles 155-170 (창고업, warehouse business) and Articles 156-163 (창고증권, warehouse receipts), which establish the warehouse operator's duties, the issuance and negotiability of receipts, and the rights of receipt holders; the Korean system provides for both negotiable and non-negotiable receipts, with negotiable warehouse receipts functioning as documents of title transferable by endorsement and delivery. The United States governs warehouse receipts primarily through UCC Article 7 (Documents of Title), as revised in 2003, which establishes a comprehensive framework for both paper and electronic warehouse receipts, supplemented by the United States Warehouse Act (USWA, 7 U.S.C. §§ 241-273) for federally licensed agricultural warehouses; the American system provides the most developed framework for electronic warehouse receipts, with USDA-authorized providers operating electronic warehouse receipt systems for agricultural commodities since the early 2000s. Germany regulates warehouse receipts through the Handelsgesetzbuch (HGB) Sections 467-475h (Lagergeschäft, warehouse transactions), with the warehouse receipt (Lagerschein) functioning as a Traditionspapier (document of title conferring constructive possession) when issued as an order document (Orderlagerschein); the German system distinguishes between the Lagerschein as a mere evidentiary document and as a negotiable document of title, with the Traditionswirkung (proprietary transfer effect) attaching only to the latter. The EU/ECHR framework has not harmonized warehouse receipt law directly, but relevant instruments include the UNCITRAL Model Law on Electronic Transferable Records (MLETR, 2017) for electronic warehouse receipts, the Common Agricultural Policy (CAP) regulations affecting agricultural storage, EBRD guidance on warehouse receipt financing in transition economies, and the EU's work on capital markets integration that indirectly touches on commodity-backed financing instruments.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's warehouse receipt regime is codified in the Korean Commercial Code (상법), Chapter 9 on Warehouse Business (창고업, Articles 155-170). The warehouse operator (창고업자) is defined as a person who stores goods deposited by others for a fee (Article 155).

**Warehouse Receipt Issuance (Article 156):** The warehouse operator must, upon the depositor's request after receiving goods, issue a warehouse receipt (창고증권). The receipt must contain: a description of the goods (type, quality, quantity, packaging), the depositor's name and address, the place of storage, the storage period (if agreed), insurance details (if the goods are insured), the date of issuance, and the warehouse operator's signature or seal.

**Types of Receipt:** The Commercial Code permits the issuance of both negotiable (지시식, order form) and non-negotiable (기명식, named form) warehouse receipts. A negotiable warehouse receipt made out to the order of a named person is transferable by endorsement and delivery (Article 160). The transfer of a negotiable warehouse receipt has the same effect as the delivery of the goods with respect to the acquisition of rights over the goods (Article 161), establishing the receipt as a document of title (유가증권).

### Document of Title Function

**Article 161 (Proprietary Effect of Transfer):** The transfer of a negotiable warehouse receipt to a person entitled to receive the goods under the document has, for the acquisition of rights in the goods, the same effect as the delivery of the goods themselves. This provision is the foundation of warehouse receipt financing in Korea: a bank that takes a negotiable warehouse receipt as collateral acquires constructive possession of the stored goods.

**Article 162 (Delivery to Receipt Holder):** The warehouse operator must deliver the goods to the legitimate holder of the warehouse receipt. Delivery to a person who does not present the receipt is at the warehouse operator's risk.

### Warehouse Operator's Obligations and Liability

**Duty of Care (Article 157):** The warehouse operator must exercise the care of a good manager (선관주의의무,善管注意義務) in storing and preserving the deposited goods. This standard requires the diligence that a prudent businessperson would exercise in managing their own affairs.

**Liability for Damage (Article 158):** The warehouse operator is liable for loss of or damage to the goods unless the operator proves that the loss or damage was not caused by the operator's failure to exercise the requisite care. The burden of proof is on the warehouse operator.

**Warehouse Operator's Lien (Article 167):** The warehouse operator has a statutory lien (유치권) on the stored goods for unpaid storage charges, transportation costs, and other expenses incurred in connection with the storage. This lien has priority over general creditors of the depositor.

### Warehouse Receipts in Korean Commodity Financing

In practice, negotiable warehouse receipts are used in Korea primarily in the context of:

| Use | Description |
|-----|-------------|
| Agricultural commodity financing | Farmers and agricultural cooperatives deposit grain and other commodities; banks accept warehouse receipts as collateral |
| Metals trading | Warehouse receipts for metals stored in bonded or free warehouses used in commodity trading |
| Import financing | Imported goods stored in bonded warehouses; banks take warehouse receipts as security pending customs clearance |
| Collateral management | Third-party collateral management agreements supplementing warehouse receipt-based financing |

### Key Korean Case Law

- **Supreme Court Decision 98Da26475 (2000):** Held that a warehouse operator who delivers goods without surrender of the negotiable warehouse receipt is liable to the legitimate receipt holder for the value of the goods.
- **Supreme Court Decision 2005Da35765 (2007):** Addressed the priority of a warehouse receipt holder over the depositor's general creditors in insolvency, affirming the proprietary nature of the receipt holder's right.
- **Supreme Court Decision 2011Da53845 (2013):** Clarified that the warehouse operator's lien for unpaid charges has priority over the claims of a bank holding the warehouse receipt as collateral, because the lien secures the preservation costs that benefit all parties.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States provides the most comprehensive legal framework for warehouse receipts among the studied jurisdictions, through UCC Article 7 (Documents of Title) as revised in 2003 and the United States Warehouse Act (USWA).

**UCC Article 7 (Documents of Title):** Article 7, as revised in 2003 to accommodate electronic documents, applies to both warehouse receipts and bills of lading. The revision was a landmark in recognizing electronic documents of title with the same legal effect as their paper counterparts.

**United States Warehouse Act (USWA, 7 U.S.C. §§ 241-273):** The USWA provides for federal licensing of warehouse operators storing agricultural commodities (grain, cotton, tobacco, peanuts, etc.). Federally licensed warehouses must comply with USDA storage standards, maintain bonds or other financial assurance, and issue receipts conforming to USDA regulations. The USWA was amended in 2000 to authorize electronic warehouse receipts.

### Negotiable and Non-Negotiable Receipts

**Negotiable Receipt (§ 7-104(a)):** A warehouse receipt is negotiable if by its terms the goods are to be delivered to bearer or to the order of a named person. A negotiable receipt can be duly negotiated to a holder who takes it in good faith, without notice of any defense or claim, for value, and by endorsement and delivery (§ 7-501).

**Due Negotiation (§ 7-501):** A negotiable warehouse receipt is "duly negotiated" when negotiated to a holder who purchases it in good faith, without notice of adverse claims, for value, in the regular course of business or financing. The holder to whom a negotiable receipt has been duly negotiated acquires title to the document, title to the goods, the right to the goods delivered to the bailee, and the direct obligation of the issuer to hold or deliver the goods according to the document's terms (§ 7-502).

**Non-Negotiable Receipt (§ 7-104(b)):** A receipt that does not meet the requirements of negotiability. A non-negotiable receipt can be transferred by assignment, but the assignee acquires only the rights of the assignor, subject to all defenses and claims.

### Warehouse Operator's Obligations

**Duty of Care (§ 7-204):** A warehouse operator is liable for damages for loss of or injury to goods caused by failure to exercise the care that a reasonably careful person would exercise in regard to similar goods under similar circumstances. The warehouse operator may limit liability by a term in the receipt setting a specific amount per article or per unit of weight, provided the depositor has the option of increasing the limit upon payment of a higher storage charge.

**Warehouseman's Lien (§ 7-209):** A warehouse operator has a lien against the bailor on goods covered by a warehouse receipt for charges for storage, transportation, insurance, labor, and other charges present or future in relation to the goods. Against a purchaser for value of a negotiable receipt, the lien is limited to charges stated in the receipt and charges for storage of the goods since the date of the receipt.

### Electronic Warehouse Receipts

The 2003 revision of UCC Article 7 was specifically designed to accommodate electronic warehouse receipts:

**Electronic Document (§ 7-102(a)(5)):** An electronic document of title is defined as a document of title evidenced by a record consisting of information stored in an electronic medium.

**Control (§ 7-106):** A person has "control" of an electronic document of title if a system employed for evidencing the transfer of interests in the document reliably establishes that person as the person to which the document was issued or transferred. Control of an electronic document is the functional equivalent of possession of a paper document.

**USDA Electronic Warehouse Receipt Providers:** Under the USWA, as amended, the USDA authorizes electronic warehouse receipt providers to operate systems for the issuance, transfer, and cancellation of electronic warehouse receipts for agricultural commodities. Currently authorized providers include:

| Provider | Commodities | System |
|----------|------------|--------|
| CME Group (formerly Clearing Corporation of India) | Grains, oilseeds, cotton | EWR system |
| ICC (International Compliance Corporation) | Cotton | Electronic cotton receipt system |

### UCC Article 7 and Article 9 Interaction

Warehouse receipts interact with UCC Article 9 (Secured Transactions) in important ways. A security interest in goods represented by a negotiable warehouse receipt may be perfected by perfecting a security interest in the receipt itself (§ 9-312(c)). Filing a financing statement is not required if the secured party has possession or control of the negotiable receipt. This enables banks to take warehouse receipts as collateral without filing, provided they maintain possession or control.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany regulates the warehouse business (Lagergeschäft) in HGB Sections 467-475h, as substantially reformed by the Transportrechtsreformgesetz (Transport Law Reform Act) of 1998, which modernized the warehouse provisions alongside the broader reform of the law of carriage.

**Warehouse Contract (Lagervertrag, HGB § 467):** The warehouse contract is a commercial contract by which the warehouse operator (Lagerhalter) undertakes to store and preserve the deposited goods. The warehouse operator has the duties of a Kaufmann (merchant), including the duty to exercise the care of an orderly merchant (Sorgfalt eines ordentlichen Kaufmanns, HGB § 475).

**Lagerschein (Warehouse Receipt, HGB § 475c):** Upon request of the depositor, the warehouse operator must issue a warehouse receipt (Lagerschein). The Lagerschein must contain: a description of the goods (nature, quantity, marks), the name and address of the depositor, the name and business address of the warehouse operator, the place of storage, the date of issuance, and the signature of the warehouse operator.

### Types of Lagerschein

**Orderlagerschein (Order Warehouse Receipt):** When the Lagerschein is issued as an order document (Orderpapier), it is negotiable by endorsement and delivery (HGB § 363, applicable to all commercial order documents). The Orderlagerschein has the Traditionswirkung (proprietary transfer effect): transfer of the endorsed Lagerschein to the transferee has, with respect to the acquisition of rights over the stored goods, the same effect as the delivery of the goods themselves (HGB § 475d).

**Namenslagerschein (Named/Straight Warehouse Receipt):** A Lagerschein issued to a named depositor without the order clause is not negotiable in the same sense. Transfer is possible only by assignment (Abtretung) of the depositor's contractual claim to delivery, which does not carry the proprietary (sachenrechtliche) effect of negotiation.

**Rektapapier:** A Lagerschein may also be issued as a Rektapapier (straight document), which can only be transferred by assignment and does not carry the Traditionswirkung.

### Traditionspapier Function

The Orderlagerschein is a Traditionspapier, a concept with deep roots in German commercial law:

**HGB § 475d (Verfügung durch Übergabe des Lagerscheins):** The delivery of the Lagerschein to a person entitled to receive the goods under the document has, for the acquisition of rights in the goods (Sachenrecht), the same effect as the delivery of the goods. This means that the holder of an endorsed Orderlagerschein acquires constructive possession (mittelbarer Besitz) and can acquire ownership through the Lagerschein transfer mechanisms.

**Practical Implications:**

| Function | Effect |
|----------|--------|
| Sale of stored goods | Transfer of Orderlagerschein transfers ownership without physical movement |
| Pledge of stored goods | Endorsement and delivery of Orderlagerschein to pledgee constitutes pledge (Pfandrecht) |
| Security transfer | Transfer of Orderlagerschein to secured creditor effects Sicherungsübereignung |

### Warehouse Operator's Liability

**Standard of Care (HGB § 475):** The warehouse operator must exercise the care of an orderly merchant (Sorgfalt eines ordentlichen Kaufmanns) in storing and preserving the goods. This is an objective standard based on what a careful and diligent merchant would do.

**Liability for Loss or Damage (HGB § 475a):** The warehouse operator is liable for loss of or damage to the goods during the storage period, unless the operator proves that the loss or damage could not have been prevented by the exercise of due care. The burden of proof lies on the warehouse operator.

**Limitation of Liability (HGB § 475a(2)):** The warehouse operator's liability is limited to the value of the goods at the time and place of storage, not exceeding 8.33 SDR per kilogram of gross weight of the goods lost or damaged. The limitation can be increased by agreement or declaration of value.

### Warehouse Operator's Lien

**HGB § 475b (Pfandrecht des Lagerhalters):** The warehouse operator has a statutory lien (gesetzliches Pfandrecht) on the stored goods for all claims arising from the warehouse contract, including storage charges, costs of preservation, and expenses. This lien is a possessory lien that the warehouse operator may enforce by public auction (öffentliche Versteigerung) under HGB § 368.

### German Case Law

- **BGH, Urteil vom 24.06.1985 -- II ZR 255/84:** Addressed the Traditionswirkung of the Orderlagerschein, confirming that delivery of the endorsed receipt to a buyer transfers constructive possession and ownership of the stored goods.
- **OLG Düsseldorf, Urteil vom 12.03.2009 -- I-18 U 160/08:** Held that the warehouse operator's liability standard requires active preservation measures appropriate to the nature of the stored goods, not merely passive custody.
- **BGH, Urteil vom 16.07.2003 -- VIII ZR 274/02:** Clarified the interaction between warehouse receipts and security interests, holding that a bank holding an Orderlagerschein as security has a superior right to delivery against the depositor's general creditors.

## ECHR & EU

### UNCITRAL Model Law on Electronic Transferable Records (MLETR, 2017)

The MLETR is the primary international instrument relevant to electronic warehouse receipts. While not specifically designed for warehouse receipts, its technology-neutral framework applies to all transferable documents and instruments, including warehouse receipts:

**Functional Equivalence (Article 7):** An electronic transferable record meets the requirements of a transferable document or instrument if a reliable method is used to identify the record, render it subject to control, and retain its integrity.

**Control (Article 11):** The requirement of possession of a transferable document is met with respect to an electronic transferable record if a reliable method is used to establish exclusive control and to identify the person in control.

**Change of Medium (Article 17):** An electronic transferable record may be replaced by a paper-based transferable document, and vice versa, provided the method used ensures the replacement record is singular (only one version exists).

### EBRD Model Law on Warehouse Receipts

The European Bank for Reconstruction and Development (EBRD) has developed a Model Law on Secured Transactions and a separate guidance framework on warehouse receipt financing, designed for transition economies seeking to develop commodity financing markets. Key elements include:

- **Licensed Warehouses:** The model requires warehouse operators to be licensed and supervised by a regulatory authority.
- **Negotiable Receipts:** The model provides for negotiable electronic warehouse receipts with document of title status.
- **Collateral Management:** The model addresses third-party collateral management as a complement to warehouse receipt-based financing.
- **Indemnity Fund:** The model recommends establishing an indemnity fund to compensate receipt holders for losses caused by warehouse operator default.

Several EBRD partner countries (including Serbia, Ukraine, Kazakhstan, and Georgia) have enacted warehouse receipt legislation based on EBRD guidance, significantly expanding commodity financing in their markets.

### EU Common Agricultural Policy (CAP)

While the CAP does not directly regulate warehouse receipts, it affects the agricultural storage and commodity financing environment within the EU:

- **Public Intervention Storage:** Under Regulation (EU) No. 1308/2013 on the common organization of agricultural markets, the EU provides for public intervention buying and storage of specified agricultural commodities (cereals, butter, skimmed milk powder). Goods entering public intervention storage are covered by EU-issued storage documents rather than private warehouse receipts.
- **Private Storage Aid (PSA):** The EU provides aid for private storage of certain products, requiring warehouse operators to comply with EU storage standards and documentation requirements.

### Union Customs Code (Regulation 952/2013)

The Union Customs Code (UCC) establishes the framework for customs warehousing within the EU:

**Customs Warehousing (Articles 240-242):** Goods may be stored in customs warehouses (Zolllager) without payment of import duties or application of commercial policy measures. Warehouse receipts issued for customs-warehoused goods have particular significance for import financing and entrepot trade.

**Authorized Economic Operator (AEO):** Customs warehouse operators holding AEO status benefit from simplified procedures and reduced customs formalities, affecting the reliability and marketability of warehouse receipts issued by such operators.

### Cross-Border Recognition

The cross-border recognition of warehouse receipts within the EU faces challenges similar to those affecting other documents of title:

| Challenge | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Applicable law | The property law effect of a warehouse receipt is governed by the lex rei sitae (law of the country where the goods are located), not harmonized at EU level |
| Electronic recognition | Member states at different stages of recognizing electronic warehouse receipts; MLETR adoption uneven |
| Negotiability | Whether a warehouse receipt issued in one member state is recognized as negotiable in another depends on the receiving state's document of title law |
| Insolvency treatment | The warehouse receipt holder's priority in the depositor's insolvency varies by member state law |

## Comparative Analysis

### Document of Title Status

All four legal systems recognize the negotiable warehouse receipt as a document of title enabling transfer of constructive possession, but the theoretical foundations differ. Germany treats the Orderlagerschein as a Traditionspapier with sachenrechtliche Wirkung (proprietary effect rooted in property law). South Korea similarly treats the negotiable warehouse receipt as a 유가증권 with proprietary transfer effect. The United States treats the duly negotiated warehouse receipt as conferring title to the goods and the document through the due negotiation doctrine of UCC § 7-502. The EU has no harmonized document of title framework.

### Electronic Warehouse Receipts

The United States leads in electronic warehouse receipt adoption, with the 2003 UCC Article 7 revision and the USWA electronic provider system creating a mature legal and operational infrastructure. Germany and South Korea have not enacted specific electronic warehouse receipt legislation, though general e-commerce frameworks may apply. The MLETR provides a framework for electronic transferable records that could enable electronic warehouse receipts, but adoption remains limited.

### Warehouse Operator Liability

The liability standards are broadly comparable across all three national systems: the warehouse operator must exercise reasonable care, with the burden of proof on the operator to demonstrate compliance. The key difference is in limitation of liability: German law limits liability to 8.33 SDR per kilogram unless a higher value is declared. American law permits contractual limitation but requires that the depositor have the option of a higher limit. South Korea applies the duty of care of a good manager without a specific statutory liability cap for warehouse operators.

### Financing Function

The economic function of warehouse receipts as collateral instruments is well developed in all studied jurisdictions but operates through different legal mechanisms. In the United States, a security interest in goods represented by a negotiable warehouse receipt is perfected by taking possession or control of the receipt itself (UCC § 9-312(c)), creating an elegant integration between document of title law and secured transactions law. In Germany, the transfer of the Orderlagerschein to a secured creditor effects a Sicherungsübereignung through the Traditionswirkung. In South Korea, the 2010 Act on Security over Movable Property operates alongside the warehouse receipt's document of title function. The absence of a unified EU framework creates friction in cross-border commodity financing.
