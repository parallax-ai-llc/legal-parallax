## Summary

Education funding systems -- the legal and fiscal frameworks through which governments finance primary, secondary, and higher education -- reflect fundamental choices about equality, federalism, and the state's obligation to provide education, with dramatic variation across jurisdictions in both the quantum of funding and the constitutional requirements governing its distribution. South Korea has transformed from one of the most family-dependent education financing systems in the OECD to a more balanced model through the Local Education Finance Grant Act (지방교육재정교부금법) and the Elementary and Secondary Education Act (초·중등교육법), allocating a constitutionally mandated proportion of internal tax revenue to education; however, the country's extraordinary private spending on supplementary education (사교육, hagwon) -- estimated at approximately KRW 26 trillion (USD 20 billion) annually -- creates a parallel funding system that exacerbates educational inequality. The United States relies primarily on local property taxes for K-12 funding, a system that generates enormous per-pupil spending disparities and has been the subject of sustained constitutional litigation since San Antonio Independent School District v. Rodriguez (1973), where the Supreme Court held that education is not a fundamental right under the federal Constitution and that wealth-based disparities in school funding do not violate the Equal Protection Clause; subsequent state court litigation under state constitutional education clauses has been far more successful, with landmark decisions in Serrano v. Priest (California, 1971), Rose v. Council for Better Education (Kentucky, 1989), and Abbott v. Burke (New Jersey, 1990 and subsequent decisions) establishing state-level adequacy and equity requirements. Germany distributes education funding responsibility through its federal structure, with the Länder bearing primary responsibility under the Kulturhoheit (cultural sovereignty) principle, supplemented by federal funding through the Digitalpakt Schule (2019, EUR 5.5 billion for digital infrastructure) and the constitutional amendment to Article 104c GG (2017) permitting federal investment in educationally disadvantaged municipalities; the Grundgesetz's Gleichwertigkeitsprinzip (principle of equivalent living conditions, Article 72(2) GG) provides a constitutional basis for equalization. The European framework establishes education as a fundamental right through ECHR Protocol 1, Article 2 (right to education), EU Charter Article 14, and the European Pillar of Social Rights, while the European Court of Human Rights has addressed funding-related access issues in cases such as Leyla Sahin v. Turkey (2005) and the Committee of Ministers' supervision of education spending in member states.

## South Korea

### Constitutional Framework

The Korean Constitution provides the most detailed education provisions among the four jurisdictions:

**Article 31:**
1. "All citizens shall have the right to receive an equal education corresponding to their ability (모든 국민은 능력에 따라 균등하게 교육을 받을 권리를 가진다)."
2. "All citizens who have children to protect shall be responsible for their elementary education at least and other education as provided by Act."
3. "Compulsory education shall be free of charge (의무교육은 무상으로 한다)."
4. "The autonomy, professionalism, and political neutrality of education and the autonomy of institutions of higher learning shall be guaranteed."
5. "The State shall promote lifelong education."
6. "Fundamental matters pertaining to the educational system, including in-school education and lifelong education, administration of education, finance of education, and the status of teachers shall be determined by Act."

### Public Funding Architecture

The Korean education funding system operates through several channels:

**Local Education Finance Grant Act (지방교육재정교부금법):** Enacted in 1971 and substantially reformed in 2001, this statute requires the central government to allocate 20.79% of internal tax revenue (내국세) to education grants distributed to 17 metropolitan and provincial education offices. The grant consists of:

| Component | Purpose | Distribution Basis |
|-----------|---------|-------------------|
| General Grant (보통교부금) | 97% of total; general educational expenditure | Need-based formula considering student numbers, school characteristics, regional costs |
| Special Grant (특별교부금) | 3% of total; special needs and policy projects | Ministerial discretion; emergency needs; national policy implementation |

**Local Education Tax (지방교육세):** A surtax on various local taxes (acquisition tax, registration tax, property tax, etc.) that generates additional education revenue at the local level.

**Elementary and Secondary Education Act (초·중등교육법):** Establishes the framework for school operations, requiring that public education be provided without tuition charges for compulsory education (grades 1-9) and, since 2021, extending free education to upper secondary schools (고등학교 무상교육).

### Free High School Education

The expansion of free education to high school represents a major recent development. Previously, upper secondary education required tuition, with government subsidies covering most costs for disadvantaged families. The phased implementation began in the third year of high school in 2019 and extended to all high school students by 2021 under the Elementary and Secondary Education Act amendments, at an estimated annual cost of KRW 2 trillion (approximately USD 1.5 billion).

### The Private Education (Hagwon) Problem

South Korea's most distinctive education funding challenge is the enormous private supplementary education market:

**Scale:** The Korean Statistical Information Service (KOSIS) reported that in 2023, 78.5% of elementary and secondary students participated in private supplementary education, with average monthly spending per student of KRW 430,000 (approximately USD 330). Total annual private education expenditure exceeded KRW 26 trillion.

**Constitutional Court Decision 2000Hun-Ka4 (2000):** The Constitutional Court struck down a provision of the School Education Normalization Act (학원의 설립·운영 및 과외교습에 관한 법률) that prohibited late-night hagwon operation, holding that it violated parents' right to educate their children (Article 31) and the freedom of occupation (Article 15). This decision effectively limited the government's ability to regulate the private education market through direct prohibition.

**Education Equalization Policy:** The government has attempted to reduce hagwon dependency through the High School Equalization Policy (고교평준화정책), which assigns students to high schools by lottery within districts to reduce competitive pressure, and through the expansion of after-school programs (방과후학교) in public schools.

## United States

### Constitutional Framework

The United States presents the most complex and contested education funding landscape, characterized by the absence of a federal constitutional right to education and extraordinary interstate variation.

### San Antonio v. Rodriguez and Federal Constitutional Law

**San Antonio Independent School District v. Rodriguez, 411 U.S. 1 (1973):** The Supreme Court held 5-4 that the Texas school finance system, which relied heavily on local property taxes and produced substantial per-pupil spending disparities between wealthy and poor districts, did not violate the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. The Court's reasoning rested on two holdings:

1. Education is not a fundamental right under the federal Constitution, and therefore wealth-based classifications in education funding are subject only to rational basis review.
2. The system bore a rational relationship to the legitimate state purpose of local control over education.

Justice Marshall's dissent argued for a sliding-scale approach to equal protection that would recognize education's special significance without declaring it a fundamental right, a position that influenced subsequent state court litigation.

### State Constitutional Litigation

The failure of the federal approach redirected school finance litigation to state courts, producing three waves of decisions:

**First Wave -- Equity (1970s-1980s):**
- **Serrano v. Priest, 5 Cal.3d 584 (1971):** The California Supreme Court held that the state's school finance system violated the California Constitution's equal protection clause, finding that education is a fundamental interest under the state constitution and that wealth-based disparities are subject to strict scrutiny.
- **Robinson v. Cahill, 62 N.J. 473 (1973):** The New Jersey Supreme Court held that the state's school funding system violated the "thorough and efficient" education clause of the New Jersey Constitution.

**Second Wave -- Adequacy (1989-2000s):**
- **Rose v. Council for Better Education, 790 S.W.2d 186 (Ky. 1989):** The Kentucky Supreme Court declared the entire state education system unconstitutional under the state's education clause, leading to the landmark Kentucky Education Reform Act (KERA) of 1990 and establishing the adequacy framework defining the capacities that an adequate education must provide.
- **Abbott v. Burke, 119 N.J. 287 (1990) and subsequent decisions (Abbott II through XX):** The New Jersey Supreme Court issued a series of landmark decisions requiring the state to fund education in the poorest urban districts at parity with the wealthiest suburban districts, eventually requiring preschool, supplemental programs, and facility improvements.

**Third Wave -- Standards-Based (2000s-present):**
- **Campaign for Fiscal Equity v. State, 100 N.Y.2d 893 (2003):** The New York Court of Appeals held that the state must ensure every school has sufficient resources to provide a "meaningful high school education."
- **Gannon v. State, 298 Kan. 1107 (2014) and subsequent decisions:** The Kansas Supreme Court found the state's school finance system inadequate under the Kansas Constitution, leading to a protracted confrontation with the legislature over funding levels.

### Current Funding Landscape

As of the 2022-23 school year, the average per-pupil expenditure in the United States was approximately USD 16,080, but this average conceals enormous variation:

| State | Per-Pupil Expenditure (2022-23) | Primary Funding Source |
|-------|-------------------------------|----------------------|
| New York | ~$30,000 | State formula + local property tax |
| Vermont | ~$24,000 | State education fund (statewide property tax) |
| Utah | ~$9,500 | State formula + local property tax |
| Idaho | ~$9,000 | State formula + local property tax |

The Elementary and Secondary Education Act (ESEA), originally enacted in 1965 and reauthorized as the Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA) in 2015, provides approximately 10% of total K-12 funding through Title I grants targeting high-poverty schools.

## Germany

### Constitutional Framework

German education funding is shaped by the federal structure and the Kulturhoheit (cultural sovereignty) of the Länder:

**Article 7(1) Grundgesetz:** "The entire school system shall be under the supervision of the state." This provision establishes comprehensive state responsibility for education but does not specify funding mechanisms.

**Article 104c Grundgesetz (2017 amendment):** "The Federation may grant the Länder financial assistance for significant investments by the Länder and municipalities in the municipal educational infrastructure in financially weak municipalities." This amendment, adopted as part of the Investitionsoffensive Bildung, represented a significant expansion of federal competence in education funding, overcoming the traditional Kooperationsverbot (cooperation prohibition) that had limited federal involvement in education.

**Article 72(2) Grundgesetz:** The Gleichwertigkeitsprinzip (principle of equivalent living conditions) provides a constitutional basis for equalization of resources across Länder, including in education.

### Länder Responsibility and Funding

The primary responsibility for education funding lies with the 16 Länder, which bear approximately 70% of education costs:

| Funding Level | Approximate Share | Responsibilities |
|--------------|------------------|-----------------|
| Länder | ~70% | Teacher salaries (largest component); curricula; school supervision; examinations |
| Municipalities (Kommunen) | ~20% | School buildings; maintenance; non-teaching staff; school equipment |
| Federal Government (Bund) | ~10% | Research funding; BAföG (student financial aid); Digitalpakt; Art. 104c investments |

### Digitalpakt Schule (2019-2024)

The DigitalPakt Schule represented the largest federal investment in school infrastructure:

**First DigitalPakt (2019):** EUR 5.5 billion in federal funds (supplemented by EUR 500 million from Länder) for digital infrastructure including broadband connectivity, WiFi, interactive displays, and digital devices. Implementation required a constitutional amendment (Article 104c GG) to permit federal funding for municipal education infrastructure.

**COVID-19 Supplements:** Three additional packages totaling EUR 1.5 billion were added during the pandemic for student devices, administrator support, and teacher training.

**DigitalPakt 2.0 (2024 negotiations):** Extended negotiations between the federal government and Länder over a successor program revealed persistent tensions between federal funding authority and Länder educational sovereignty.

### Länderfinanzausgleich and Education Equalization

The horizontal fiscal equalization system (Länderfinanzausgleich), reformed in 2017 and implemented from 2020, indirectly affects education funding by redistributing tax revenue among Länder to achieve approximately equivalent fiscal capacity. The system brings each Land's fiscal capacity to at least 99.75% of the national average, reducing but not eliminating disparities in per-pupil education spending.

### Spending Disparities

Despite equalization, significant per-pupil spending disparities persist across Länder:

| Land | Per-Pupil Expenditure (2022, primary) | GDP per Capita |
|------|--------------------------------------|---------------|
| Bayern | ~EUR 10,500 | High |
| Hamburg | ~EUR 12,000 | Highest |
| Sachsen-Anhalt | ~EUR 9,800 | Low |
| Nordrhein-Westfalen | ~EUR 8,500 | Medium |

The Bildungsfinanzbericht (Education Finance Report) published by the Statistisches Bundesamt provides annual comparative data on education spending across Länder and in international comparison.

### Private School Funding

Article 7(4) Grundgesetz guarantees the right to establish private schools (Privatschulfreiheit) while requiring that they not fall below the standard of public schools. Most Länder provide substantial public subsidies to approved private schools (Ersatzschulen), typically covering 60-80% of operating costs, provided that they meet quality standards and do not select students based on parents' financial circumstances (Sonderungsverbot).

## ECHR & EU

### Right to Education

**ECHR Protocol 1, Article 2:** "No person shall be denied the right to education." This provision establishes a negative obligation on states not to deny access to existing educational institutions but has been interpreted to also impose limited positive obligations regarding the quality and accessibility of education.

**Belgian Linguistics Case (Application No. 1474/62, ECtHR 1968):** The foundational case on the right to education established that Article 2 of Protocol 1 does not require states to establish or subsidize any particular type of education but guarantees a right of access to existing educational institutions. The Court held that while no obligation exists to fund education in any particular language, the manner in which existing institutions are funded must not be discriminatory.

**Leyla Sahin v. Turkey (Application No. 44774/98, ECtHR Grand Chamber 2005):** While primarily concerning headscarf bans in universities, the Court addressed the scope of the right to education, holding that access to higher education is within the scope of Article 2 of Protocol 1 and that restrictions must be proportionate and not impair the essence of the right.

### EU Framework

**Article 165 TFEU:** "The Union shall contribute to the development of quality education by encouraging cooperation between Member States and, if necessary, by supporting and supplementing their action." Education remains primarily a member state competence, with the EU limited to supporting and coordinating roles.

**EU Charter of Fundamental Rights, Article 14:**
1. "Everyone has the right to education and to have access to vocational and continuing training."
2. "This right includes the possibility to receive free compulsory education."

**European Pillar of Social Rights, Principle 1:** "Everyone has the right to quality and inclusive education, training and life-long learning in order to maintain and acquire skills that enable them to participate fully in society and manage successfully transitions in the labour market."

### OECD Comparisons and EU Policy Influence

The OECD's Education at a Glance publications provide the most comprehensive international comparison of education spending. Key metrics (2023 data):

### EU Structural Funds and Education

The European Social Fund Plus (ESF+) and the European Regional Development Fund (ERDF) provide significant funding for education-related investments in member states, particularly in less developed regions. The Recovery and Resilience Facility (2021-2026) has directed substantial resources to education reform, with education and skills development representing a significant component of national recovery plans.

## Comparative Analysis

### Centralization vs. Decentralization

The four jurisdictions represent different points on the centralization spectrum. South Korea operates the most centralized funding system, with the central government's Local Education Finance Grant constituting the dominant funding source. Germany's system delegates primary responsibility to the Länder but provides equalization through horizontal fiscal transfers. The United States has the most decentralized system, with local property taxes generating the largest share of K-12 funding and producing the greatest disparities. The EU framework is purely supportive, respecting member state sovereignty over education.

### Equity and Adequacy

The constitutional treatment of education funding equality reflects fundamentally different approaches. The U.S. Supreme Court's refusal in Rodriguez to recognize education as a fundamental right pushed equity litigation to state courts, producing a patchwork of standards. Germany's Gleichwertigkeitsprinzip provides a constitutional basis for equalization but has not generated education-specific litigation comparable to the American experience. South Korea's constitutional guarantee of equal education (Article 31(1)) coexists with extreme private education spending inequality. The ECHR's minimal positive obligations standard provides limited leverage for addressing funding disparities.

### Private Spending and Shadow Education

South Korea's enormous private supplementary education market (사교육) represents a unique challenge among developed democracies, creating what scholars describe as a "shadow education system" that effectively supplements public education but deepens socioeconomic stratification. The United States' significant private spending is concentrated in higher education tuition and private K-12 schools. Germany's comparatively low private spending reflects the strength of the public education system and the cultural norm of free education through the university level.

### Federal Structures and Education Funding

Both Germany and the United States face structural challenges arising from federalism, but their responses differ dramatically. Germany's robust Länderfinanzausgleich and the 2017 Article 104c amendment demonstrate a willingness to use constitutional reform to address education funding gaps. The United States has relied primarily on litigation rather than constitutional reform, producing a more adversarial and unpredictable approach to education funding equalization.
