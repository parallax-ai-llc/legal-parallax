## Summary

Robbery — the taking of property from a person through force or threat of force — occupies a distinct position in criminal law as a hybrid offense combining elements of theft and assault. South Korea's Criminal Act (형법) treats robbery under Articles 333 through 346, imposing penalties starting at 3 years and escalating to death for robbery resulting in murder. The United States addresses robbery at both the federal level through the Hobbs Act (18 U.S.C. Section 1951) and across 50 state jurisdictions, with degree-based classification systems producing sentences from 2 years to life imprisonment. Germany's Strafgesetzbuch structures robbery (Raub) through Sections 249-252, with a minimum of 1 year for basic robbery rising to life imprisonment when the victim dies. The European Court of Human Rights, while lacking a criminal code of its own, imposes positive obligations on member states to protect individuals from violent crime and to guarantee fair trial rights to those accused of robbery under Articles 1, 3, and 6 of the Convention.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

The Korean Criminal Act (형법) addresses robbery (강도죄) in Chapter XXXVII, Articles 333 through 346. The statutory framework creates a graduated system of offenses based on circumstances and outcomes:

| Article | Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Art. 333 | Basic Robbery (강도) | 3 years or more imprisonment |
| Art. 334 | Special Robbery (특수강도) — use of deadly weapons or joint commission by 2+ persons | 5 years or more imprisonment |
| Art. 335 | Quasi-Robbery (준강도) — use of force after theft to retain property or escape | Same as Art. 333 |
| Art. 336 | Robbery by Intimidation (인질강도) — taking hostages | 3 years or more imprisonment |
| Art. 337 | Robbery Causing Bodily Injury (강도상해) | Life imprisonment or 7 years or more |
| Art. 338 | Robbery Causing Murder (강도살인) | Death or life imprisonment |
| Art. 339 | Robbery and Rape (강도강간) | Life imprisonment or 10 years or more |
| Art. 341 | Habitual Robbery (상습강도) | Life imprisonment or 10 years or more |
| Art. 343 | Preparation and Conspiracy for Robbery | Up to 7 years imprisonment |

### Key Principles

**Suppression of resistance (반항 억압):** Korean courts require that the force or threat used in robbery must be sufficient to suppress the victim's resistance. In the Supreme Court decision 2004Do2389, the Court clarified that the degree of force must be assessed objectively, considering the totality of circumstances including the physical disparity between perpetrator and victim, time of day, and location.

**Quasi-robbery doctrine (준강도):** Article 335 extends robbery liability to a thief who uses violence or threats after completing the theft in order to retain stolen property, resist arrest, or destroy evidence. The Supreme Court in 2003Do3906 held that the force used must reach the same threshold as that required for basic robbery — mere pushing to escape is insufficient.

**Temporal connection:** Korean law requires a close temporal and spatial connection between the theft and the use of force. The Supreme Court (2008Do8601) established that force exercised hours after a theft at a separate location does not constitute quasi-robbery.

### Sentencing Trends

The Korean Sentencing Commission (양형위원회) has issued sentencing guidelines for robbery offenses. Courts have demonstrated increasing severity, particularly in cases involving elderly or vulnerable victims. The Act on Punishment of Specific Violent Crimes (특정범죄 가중처벌 등에 관한 법률) further enhances penalties when robbery involves organized crime or repeated offenses.

## United States

### Federal Law

The Hobbs Act, codified at 18 U.S.C. Section 1951, criminalizes robbery that "in any way or degree obstructs, delays, or affects commerce or the movement of any article or commodity in commerce." The statute defines robbery as the unlawful taking or obtaining of personal property from the person or in the presence of another, against his will, by means of actual or threatened force, violence, or fear of injury.

**Elements:**
1. Taking or obtaining of personal property from another
2. Against the victim's will
3. By means of actual or threatened force, violence, or fear
4. Affecting interstate or foreign commerce

**Penalties:** Conviction under the Hobbs Act carries imprisonment of up to 20 years per count. When a firearm is used or carried during a robbery, 18 U.S.C. Section 924(c) imposes consecutive mandatory minimum sentences: 5 years for possession, 7 years for brandishing, and 10 years for discharge of the firearm. A second or subsequent Section 924(c) conviction carries a mandatory 25 years to life.

### State Law

State robbery statutes vary widely but generally follow a degree-based classification:

**California (Penal Code Section 211):**
- First-degree robbery (victim is a transit operator, in an inhabited dwelling, or at an ATM): 3, 6, or 9 years imprisonment
- Second-degree robbery (all other cases): 2, 3, or 5 years imprisonment
- Robbery committed in concert inside an inhabited dwelling: 3, 6, or 9 years

**New York (Penal Law Article 160):**
- Robbery in the first degree (Class B felony) — armed, causes serious injury, or displays a firearm: up to 25 years, mandatory minimum of 5 years
- Robbery in the second degree (Class C felony) — aided by another, causes injury, or displays a weapon: up to 15 years
- Robbery in the third degree (Class D felony) — basic forcible taking: up to 7 years

**Texas (Penal Code Section 29.02-29.03):**
- Robbery (second-degree felony): 2 to 20 years
- Aggravated robbery (first-degree felony) — serious bodily injury, use of deadly weapon, or elderly/disabled victim: 5 to 99 years or life

### Landmark Cases

**Taylor v. United States, 579 U.S. 301 (2016):** The Supreme Court held that the Government need not prove a specific economic effect on interstate commerce in Hobbs Act robbery cases involving drugs, since drug dealing by its nature affects interstate commerce.

**United States v. Davis, 588 U.S. ___ (2019):** The Supreme Court struck down the residual clause of 18 U.S.C. Section 924(c)(3)(B) as unconstitutionally vague, affecting how Hobbs Act robbery qualifies as a "crime of violence" for purposes of mandatory firearm sentence enhancements.

**Stokeling v. United States, 586 U.S. 73 (2019):** The Court ruled that robbery offenses that require overcoming victim resistance through force — even minimal force — qualify as "violent felonies" under the Armed Career Criminal Act.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

The German Criminal Code (Strafgesetzbuch, StGB) addresses robbery in Sections 249 through 256, establishing a tiered structure of increasing severity:

**Section 249 — Basic Robbery (Raub):**
Whoever takes another person's movable property from the person or in the presence of another by using force against a person or by threatening the person with imminent danger to life or limb, with the intent to unlawfully appropriate the property, shall be punished with imprisonment of not less than 1 year. As a Verbrechen (felony), robbery carries a minimum sentence that reflects its classification as one of the most serious property offenses in German law.

**Section 250 — Aggravated Robbery (Schwerer Raub):**
Aggravated robbery applies when specific qualifying circumstances are present. The section distinguishes two tiers:

| Tier | Circumstances | Minimum Penalty |
|------|--------------|-----------------|
| Section 250(1) | Carrying a weapon or dangerous instrument; carrying a tool for overcoming resistance; placing victim in danger of severe health damage; acting as member of an organized gang | 3 years imprisonment |
| Section 250(2) | Using a weapon or dangerous instrument during the robbery; using force resulting in severe physical abuse; using a firearm | 5 years imprisonment |

The concept of a "dangerous instrument" (gefaehrliches Werkzeug) has been broadly interpreted by German courts to include screwdrivers, carpet knives, pepper spray, and even large dogs when deployed to intimidate victims.

**Section 251 — Robbery Resulting in Death (Raub mit Todesfolge):**
If the perpetrator causes the death of another person at least through recklessness (Leichtfertigkeit) during the robbery, the penalty is life imprisonment or imprisonment of not less than 10 years. This is a success-qualified offense (erfolgsqualifiziertes Delikt) that requires a specific causal connection between the robbery violence and the death — not merely a temporal coincidence.

**Section 252 — Robbery by Theft (Raeuberischer Diebstahl):**
Similar to Korea's quasi-robbery doctrine, this provision applies to a thief caught in the act who uses force or threats to retain the stolen property. The penalty mirrors that of basic robbery under Section 249.

### Key Principles

**Dual nature of robbery:** German doctrine treats robbery as a compound offense (zusammengesetztes Delikt) combining elements of theft (Diebstahl, Section 242) and coercion (Noetigung, Section 240). This doctrinal classification affects issues of attempt, participation, and sentencing.

**Finality requirement (Finalitaet):** The force or threat must serve as the means of taking — that is, the perpetrator must use coercion to enable the taking of property. Force applied after the taking is complete does not constitute robbery under Section 249 but may fall under Section 252.

**Less serious cases (minder schwerer Fall):** Section 249(2) allows courts to impose a reduced sentence of 1 to 10 years in less serious cases, providing judicial discretion where the strict minimum might produce disproportionate results.

### Notable Jurisprudence

The Bundesgerichtshof (Federal Court of Justice) in BGH 4 StR 558/11 clarified that the use of a gas pistol during robbery qualifies under Section 250(2) No. 1 only when the weapon is used as a means of force, not merely carried. In BGH 3 StR 324/17, the court held that the dangerous instrument must be employed in a manner that creates an objective danger to the victim's physical integrity.

## European Framework

### ECHR Provisions Relevant to Robbery

The European Convention on Human Rights does not directly criminalize robbery but establishes a framework of rights that shapes how member states investigate, prosecute, and punish robbery offenses:

**Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 — Protection of Property:**
Every natural or legal person is entitled to the peaceful enjoyment of his possessions. No one shall be deprived of his possessions except in the public interest and subject to conditions provided for by law. This provision generates positive obligations on states to establish legal frameworks that protect individuals against unlawful deprivation of property by private actors, including through criminal robbery statutes.

**Article 3 — Prohibition of Torture and Inhuman Treatment:**
This provision applies in two directions in robbery cases. First, states bear a positive obligation to protect individuals from criminal violence — including armed robbery — when authorities are aware of a real and immediate risk. Second, states must ensure that the treatment of robbery suspects during investigation and detention does not amount to torture or inhuman or degrading treatment.

**Article 6 — Right to a Fair Trial:**
All persons charged with robbery are entitled to a fair and public hearing within a reasonable time by an independent and impartial tribunal. This includes the right to be presumed innocent, the right to legal assistance, the right to examine witnesses, and the right to free interpretation if the accused cannot understand the language of the proceedings.

### Landmark Cases

**Delta v. France (1990):** The applicant was convicted of robbery based solely on statements from two witnesses who were never examined at trial. The Court found a violation of Article 6(1) and 6(3)(d), holding that the defense must have an adequate opportunity to challenge and question witnesses whose testimony forms the basis of a conviction.

**Gafgen v. Germany (2010):** Following a kidnapping and murder, police threatened the suspect with torture to force disclosure of the victim's location. The Court held that the threat of torture during interrogation violated Article 3, though the subsequent conviction based on independent evidence did not violate Article 6, provided the tainted evidence was not relied upon.

**Brecknell v. United Kingdom (2007):** The Court established that when new evidence or information emerges regarding violent crimes previously investigated, Article 2 requires states to reopen the investigation. This procedural obligation extends to violent robbery cases resulting in death.

**M.C. v. Bulgaria (2003):** Though primarily a sexual violence case, the Court established the broad principle that states have positive obligations under Articles 3 and 8 to enact and effectively apply criminal law provisions that deter violent offenses, including robbery, and to conduct effective investigations when such offenses occur.

### Proportionality of Sentencing

The ECHR has addressed whether robbery sentences may themselves constitute inhuman or degrading treatment under Article 3. In Vinter v. United Kingdom (2013), the Grand Chamber held that life sentences without any prospect of review are incompatible with Article 3. This principle constrains member states that impose life sentences for aggravated robbery, requiring that some mechanism for sentence review must exist.
