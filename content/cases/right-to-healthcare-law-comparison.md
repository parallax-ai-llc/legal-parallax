## Summary

The right to healthcare -- encompassing access to medical services, medication, preventive care, and public health measures without financial hardship -- is recognized as a fundamental social right in numerous constitutions and international instruments, yet the mechanism of delivery, the extent of coverage, and the justiciability of health rights vary enormously across legal systems. South Korea operates the National Health Insurance (국민건강보험) system, a mandatory single-payer social health insurance program that covers approximately 97 percent of the population, with the remaining three percent covered by Medical Aid (의료급여), achieving universal health coverage at a relatively low cost; the National Health Insurance Act (국민건강보험법) and the Medical Care Assistance Act (의료급여법) form the statutory basis, while the Constitutional Court (헌법재판소) has derived health rights from the right to life (Article 10) and the right to human dignity, holding that the state has a duty to maintain minimum levels of healthcare provision. The United States is the only major developed country without a universal healthcare system, instead operating a patchwork of employer-sponsored insurance, government programs (Medicare for individuals 65 and older, Medicaid for low-income individuals, the Children's Health Insurance Program), and individual market coverage through the Affordable Care Act (ACA, 2010) marketplace exchanges; the Supreme Court upheld the ACA's individual mandate as a tax in National Federation of Independent Business v. Sebelius, 567 U.S. 519 (2012), but struck down the Medicaid expansion mandate, making expansion optional for states. Germany operates one of the oldest social health insurance systems in the world, established by Chancellor Otto von Bismarck's Health Insurance Act (Krankenversicherungsgesetz) in 1883, with the current framework governed by the Social Code Book V (Sozialgesetzbuch V, SGB V) providing coverage through approximately 96 statutory health insurance funds (gesetzliche Krankenversicherung, GKV) for roughly 90 percent of the population and private health insurance (private Krankenversicherung, PKV) for the remainder; Germany's system is characterized by the principle of solidarity (Solidaritätsprinzip), whereby contributions are income-based and coverage is independent of health status. The European framework recognizes the right to health under Article 35 of the EU Charter, Article 11 of the European Social Charter (revised), and through ECHR jurisprudence interpreting Articles 2 (right to life) and 3 (prohibition of torture and inhuman treatment) to encompass healthcare obligations, while EU law facilitates cross-border healthcare access through Directive 2011/24/EU and the coordination of social security systems under Regulation (EC) 883/2004.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

The Constitution does not explicitly guarantee a right to healthcare but provides several relevant provisions. Article 10 guarantees human dignity and the right to pursue happiness. Article 34 provides that "(1) All citizens shall be entitled to a life worthy of human dignity" and "(2) The State shall have the duty to endeavor to promote social security and welfare." Article 36(3) provides that "the health of all citizens shall be protected by the State."

### National Health Insurance (국민건강보험)

South Korea achieved universal health insurance coverage remarkably rapidly, expanding from initial coverage of employees in large firms (1977) to universal coverage in just 12 years (1989). The system is administered by the National Health Insurance Service (국민건강보험공단, NHIS), a single insurer.

**Coverage:** The NHI covers approximately 97 percent of the population. The remaining three percent (approximately 1.5 million persons) are covered by Medical Aid (의료급여), a public assistance program funded by general tax revenue.

**Financing:** Contributions are calculated as a percentage of income (currently 7.09 percent of monthly salary for employees, split equally between employer and employee). Self-employed persons pay income- and asset-based contributions.

**Benefits Package:** The Health Insurance Review and Assessment Service (건강보험심사평가원, HIRA) determines covered services and reimbursement rates. Coverage extends to ambulatory care, hospital care, prescription drugs, preventive services, and some dental and traditional Korean medicine (한의학) services.

### Moon Jae-in Care (문재인 케어)

The Moon Jae-in administration's National Health Insurance Coverage Strengthening Plan, launched in 2017, aimed to reduce out-of-pocket costs by expanding NHI coverage of non-covered services (비급여) and introducing catastrophic illness coverage. Key measures included coverage expansion for MRI, ultrasound, and other diagnostic procedures; reduction of copayments for hospitalization; and expanded coverage for dental and traditional medicine.

### Constitutional Court on Healthcare

The Constitutional Court has addressed healthcare rights in several decisions:

**National Health Insurance Constitutionality (2001헌마370, 2003):** The Court upheld the mandatory nature of the NHI system, holding that compulsory participation does not violate the right to property or freedom of contract, as the system implements the state's constitutional duty to promote social security.

**Medical Aid Standards (2009헌마519):** The Court held that the state's determination of Medical Aid benefit levels must maintain a minimum standard consistent with human dignity, though the specific level is within legislative discretion.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States does not recognize a constitutional right to healthcare. The Supreme Court has not found healthcare to be a fundamental right under the Due Process or Equal Protection Clauses. However, multiple federal statutes establish healthcare entitlements and obligations.

### Major Federal Programs

| Program | Year | Coverage |
|---------|------|----------|
| Medicare | 1965 | Individuals 65+; certain disabilities; ESRD |
| Medicaid | 1965 | Low-income individuals (income thresholds vary by state) |
| CHIP | 1997 | Children in families above Medicaid thresholds |
| ACA Marketplace | 2010/2014 | Individual market with premium subsidies |
| Veterans Health Administration | 1930 | Military veterans |
| EMTALA | 1986 | Emergency screening and stabilization regardless of ability to pay |

### Affordable Care Act (ACA, 2010)

The Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act, signed March 23, 2010, represented the most significant healthcare reform since Medicare and Medicaid. Key provisions include:

- Individual mandate to maintain health insurance (tax penalty eliminated in 2017)
- Health insurance marketplace exchanges for individual coverage
- Premium tax credits and cost-sharing reductions for eligible individuals
- Essential health benefits requirements for individual and small-group plans
- Prohibition on pre-existing condition exclusions and lifetime benefit caps
- Medicaid expansion to 138 percent of the federal poverty level (optional after NFIB v. Sebelius)

**NFIB v. Sebelius, 567 U.S. 519 (2012):** The Supreme Court upheld the individual mandate as a valid exercise of the taxing power but struck down the Medicaid expansion as unconstitutionally coercive, holding that Congress could not threaten to withhold existing Medicaid funding from states that declined to expand.

**King v. Burwell, 576 U.S. 473 (2015):** Upheld the availability of premium tax credits in states using the federal marketplace exchange.

### Emergency Care

The Emergency Medical Treatment and Labor Act (EMTALA, 42 U.S.C. Section 1395dd), enacted in 1986, requires hospitals participating in Medicare to provide emergency medical screening and stabilizing treatment to all individuals regardless of insurance status or ability to pay. EMTALA creates a right to emergency care but does not guarantee ongoing treatment or coverage.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Article 20(1) of the Basic Law establishes Germany as a "democratic and social federal state" (demokratischer und sozialer Bundesstaat). The Sozialstaatsprinzip (social state principle) derived from this provision has been interpreted by the Federal Constitutional Court as requiring the state to ensure a minimum standard of living and access to essential services, including healthcare.

### Statutory Health Insurance (Gesetzliche Krankenversicherung, GKV)

Germany's GKV system, the world's oldest social health insurance system, covers approximately 90 percent of the population. Key features:

**Mandatory Coverage:** Since 2009, all residents must have health insurance. Employees earning below the annual income threshold (Versicherungspflichtgrenze, EUR 69,300 in 2024) are mandatorily enrolled in GKV. Those earning above may opt for private health insurance (PKV).

**Insurance Funds (Krankenkassen):** As of 2024, approximately 96 Krankenkassen compete for members. All must offer a standardized benefits package determined by the Federal Joint Committee (Gemeinsamer Bundesausschuss, G-BA). Members may freely choose and switch among funds.

**Contributions:** Total contribution rate is 14.6 percent of gross salary, split equally between employer and employee (7.3 percent each). Funds may levy supplementary contributions (Zusatzbeitrag), averaging approximately 1.7 percent in 2024.

**Benefits:** The GKV benefits package includes ambulatory and hospital care, prescription drugs, rehabilitation, dental care, mental health services, preventive care, and maternity care. The principle of in-kind benefits (Sachleistungsprinzip) means that insured persons receive services directly without upfront payment.

### Private Health Insurance (Private Krankenversicherung, PKV)

Approximately 10 percent of the population is insured through PKV, including civil servants (who receive state aid, Beihilfe, covering a portion of costs), self-employed persons, and high-income employees who opt out of GKV. PKV premiums are risk-based rather than income-based, and coverage terms are individually contracted.

### Federal Constitutional Court on Healthcare

The Federal Constitutional Court has addressed healthcare through the Sozialstaatsprinzip:

**Existenzminimum (BVerfGE 125, 175, 2010):** While primarily addressing welfare benefits, the Hartz IV decision established that the state must guarantee a minimum subsistence level (Existenzminimum) including healthcare, and that this guarantee is judicially enforceable.

**Nikolaus Decision (BVerfGE 115, 25, 2005):** The Court held that the GKV may not deny coverage for a life-threatening or typically fatal condition where the insured person claims a not entirely remote prospect of treatment success, even if the treatment is not yet included in the standard benefits catalog.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Jurisprudence

The ECHR does not contain an explicit right to healthcare, but the Court has derived healthcare obligations from Articles 2 and 3:

**Lopes de Sousa Fernandes v. Portugal (Application No. 56080/13, Grand Chamber, 2017):** The Grand Chamber clarified the framework for medical negligence cases under Article 2, distinguishing between individual cases of medical negligence (requiring systemic failures or denial of treatment to engage Article 2) and cases involving systemic or structural dysfunction in hospital services.

**Paposhvili v. Belgium (Application No. 41738/10, Grand Chamber, 2016):** The Grand Chamber lowered the threshold for Article 3 protection against removal of seriously ill persons, holding that removal may violate Article 3 where the person would face a real risk of being exposed to a serious, rapid, and irreversible decline in health resulting in intense suffering or a significant reduction in life expectancy, due to the absence of appropriate treatment in the receiving country.

**N. v. United Kingdom (Application No. 26565/05, Grand Chamber, 2008):** Set the earlier higher threshold (refined in Paposhvili) for healthcare-related expulsion cases under Article 3.

### EU Cross-Border Healthcare

**Directive 2011/24/EU (Cross-Border Healthcare):** Establishes the right of EU citizens to receive healthcare in another member state and be reimbursed by their home state, subject to conditions. The Directive codified CJEU case law from Kohll (C-158/96, 1998) and Decker (C-120/95, 1998).

**Regulation (EC) 883/2004 (Social Security Coordination):** Coordinates healthcare coverage for persons moving between member states, including the European Health Insurance Card (EHIC) system providing access to state-provided healthcare during temporary stays.

### EU Pharmaceutical Legislation

The EU harmonizes pharmaceutical regulation through Directive 2001/83/EC (medicinal products for human use) and Regulation (EC) 726/2004 (European Medicines Agency). The 2023 proposed EU pharmaceutical reform aims to improve access to medicines, address antimicrobial resistance, and streamline market authorization.

## Comparative Analysis

### Universal Coverage Models

South Korea and Germany achieve universal coverage through different mechanisms: South Korea uses a single-payer model with one insurer, while Germany uses a multi-payer model with competing insurance funds. Both systems achieve near-universal coverage with income-based contributions. The United States remains the outlier, with approximately 8 percent of the population uninsured and coverage dependent on employment, age, income, and state of residence.

### Financing and Affordability

Germany's system minimizes out-of-pocket costs through the Sachleistungsprinzip and modest copayments. South Korea's NHI system has historically had higher copayment rates, though the Moon Jae-in Care reforms reduced these. The United States has the highest out-of-pocket costs among the four systems, with deductibles, copayments, and coinsurance creating significant financial barriers to care.

### Justiciability of Health Rights

The justiciability of healthcare rights varies significantly. Germany's Federal Constitutional Court has enforced healthcare access through the Existenzminimum and Nikolaus decisions. South Korea's Constitutional Court has recognized minimum healthcare standards. The ECHR framework provides limited but expanding protection through Articles 2 and 3. The United States provides no constitutional right to healthcare, with access dependent on statutory entitlements and market mechanisms.

### Emergency Care

All four jurisdictions guarantee some form of emergency care access. The United States' EMTALA provides the narrowest guarantee (emergency screening and stabilization only). South Korea, Germany, and EU member states generally provide more comprehensive emergency care obligations.
