## Summary

Agricultural subsidies -- government payments and support programs designed to supplement farm income, manage commodity supply, influence production decisions, and ensure food security -- represent one of the most economically significant and politically contentious areas of government intervention, with global agricultural support totaling approximately $851 billion annually according to the OECD. South Korea maintains among the highest levels of agricultural support in the developed world, with a Producer Support Estimate (PSE) of approximately 48% of gross farm receipts (compared to the OECD average of approximately 18%), delivered primarily through rice market price support under the Grain Management Act (양곡관리법) and the Act on the Rice Income Compensation (쌀 소득 등의 보전에 관한 법률), direct payments to farmers through the Public Direct Payment Act (공익직접지불제도 운영에 관한 법률, 2020), and border protection measures including high tariffs on rice (513% until the 2015 tariffication) and other sensitive agricultural products. The United States provides agricultural support through the Farm Bill -- omnibus legislation reauthorized approximately every five years, most recently in the Agriculture Improvement Act of 2018 (Pub. L. 115-334) -- encompassing commodity programs (Price Loss Coverage, Agriculture Risk Coverage), crop insurance subsidies (Federal Crop Insurance Act), conservation payments (Conservation Reserve Program), and nutrition assistance (Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program, which constitutes approximately 80% of Farm Bill spending); total U.S. agricultural support is estimated at approximately $48 billion annually. Germany operates within the European Union's Common Agricultural Policy (CAP), the EU's largest single expenditure program at approximately 55 billion euros annually (approximately one-third of the EU budget), structured since the 2023 CAP reform into two pillars: Pillar I direct payments (including the Basic Income Support for Sustainability and eco-schemes) and Pillar II rural development programs; Germany's national CAP Strategic Plan allocates approximately 30 billion euros over the 2023-2027 programming period. At the international level, the WTO Agreement on Agriculture (1995) classifies agricultural subsidies into "boxes" based on their trade-distorting effects (amber box for trade-distorting subsidies subject to reduction commitments, blue box for production-limiting subsidies, and green box for non-trade-distorting subsidies exempt from reduction), and the Doha Development Round's agricultural negotiations -- stalled since 2008 -- remain the central unresolved issue in multilateral trade liberalization; the ECHR has addressed agricultural subsidies through Protocol 1, Article 1 (protection of property), finding in cases such as Brosset-Triboulet v. France (2010) and J.A. Pye (Oxford) Ltd v. United Kingdom (2007) that states enjoy a wide margin of appreciation in regulating agricultural property in the public interest.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's agricultural sector employs approximately 5% of the population but generates only about 2% of GDP, reflecting the rapid industrialization that transformed the country from an agrarian to a manufacturing and service economy. Agricultural support policy remains politically significant due to the outsized electoral influence of rural constituencies.

**Grain Management Act (양곡관리법):** This statute, originally enacted during the food-scarce postwar period, governs rice procurement, storage, and price stabilization. The government historically purchased rice from farmers at above-market prices and sold it to consumers at below-market prices, absorbing the difference as a subsidy. The deficit rice purchase program has been progressively reformed but remains a cornerstone of agricultural support.

**Rice Income Compensation Act (쌀 소득 등의 보전에 관한 법률):** This statute establishes income support payments for rice farmers, providing variable payments when market prices fall below a reference price. The program compensates farmers for the gap between the target price and the market price, effectively guaranteeing a minimum income level for rice production.

**Public Direct Payment Act (공익직접지불제도 운영에 관한 법률, 2020):** This landmark reform consolidated multiple fragmented subsidy programs into a unified public direct payment system with two components:
- **Basic direct payment (기본직불금):** Fixed per-hectare payments to all eligible farmers, with higher rates for smaller farms (up to 2.05 million KRW per hectare for farms under 0.5 hectares, declining for larger farms)
- **Conditional direct payment (선택직불금):** Additional payments for farmers who voluntarily adopt environmentally friendly practices, contribute to rural community development, or produce specific public goods

### Rice Tariffication (2015)

South Korea maintained a quantitative import restriction on rice under a special waiver from GATT/WTO obligations from 1995 to 2014. Upon expiration of the waiver, Korea tariffied its rice import restriction in 2015, replacing the quota with a 513% tariff -- one of the highest agricultural tariffs in the world. The tariff effectively maintained the previous level of border protection, and Korean rice prices remain approximately three to four times world market levels.

### WTO Disputes

South Korea has been both a complainant and respondent in WTO agricultural disputes. In Korea -- Measures Affecting Imports of Fresh, Chilled and Frozen Beef (DS161/DS169, 2001), the WTO Appellate Body found that Korea's dual retail system for domestic and imported beef was inconsistent with Article III:4 of GATT 1994.

## United States

### Legal Framework

**Farm Bill Structure:** The Farm Bill is omnibus legislation covering twelve titles:

| Title | Subject | Approximate % of Spending |
|-------|---------|--------------------------|
| Title I | Commodities | ~9% |
| Title II | Conservation | ~7% |
| Title IV | Nutrition (SNAP) | ~80% |
| Title V | Credit | <1% |
| Title XI | Crop Insurance | ~9% |
| Other titles | Trade, research, rural development, etc. | ~5% |

**Commodity Programs:**
- **Price Loss Coverage (PLC):** Counter-cyclical payments when market prices fall below statutory reference prices for covered commodities (corn, wheat, soybeans, rice, peanuts, etc.)
- **Agriculture Risk Coverage (ARC):** Revenue-based payments when county or individual revenue falls below a benchmark, providing shallow loss coverage above crop insurance deductibles

**Federal Crop Insurance:** The Federal Crop Insurance Corporation (FCIC), managed by the USDA Risk Management Agency, administers a subsidized crop insurance program that covers over 300 million acres annually. The federal government subsidizes approximately 62% of crop insurance premiums, costing taxpayers approximately $10-12 billion annually.

**Conservation Programs:**
- **Conservation Reserve Program (CRP):** Pays farmers annual rental payments to retire environmentally sensitive cropland for 10-15 years
- **Conservation Stewardship Program (CSP):** Payments for adopting and maintaining conservation practices on working lands
- **Environmental Quality Incentives Program (EQIP):** Cost-sharing for conservation practices

### WTO Compliance

U.S. agricultural support has been challenged at the WTO. In United States -- Subsidies on Upland Cotton (DS267, Brazil v. United States), the WTO Appellate Body found in 2005 that U.S. cotton subsidies caused serious prejudice to Brazilian cotton interests. The dispute was settled in 2014 with the U.S. agreeing to reform its cotton programs and pay Brazil $300 million.

### 2018 Farm Bill and Pending Reauthorization

The 2018 Agriculture Improvement Act authorized programs through fiscal year 2023, with extensions maintaining programs pending full reauthorization. Key 2018 changes included: legalization of hemp production, expanded conservation funding, and strengthened work requirements for SNAP recipients. The delayed reauthorization process has become a recurring feature of Farm Bill politics, with competing priorities between commodity, conservation, and nutrition interests.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany is the EU's largest agricultural producer and the largest recipient of CAP payments, with approximately 256,000 agricultural holdings managing 16.7 million hectares of utilized agricultural area.

**EU Common Agricultural Policy (CAP):** The CAP, established by the Treaty of Rome (1957) and reformed multiple times (most recently for the 2023-2027 period under Regulation (EU) 2021/2115), is structured in two pillars:

**Pillar I -- Direct Payments:**
- **Basic Income Support for Sustainability (BISS):** Decoupled per-hectare payments to eligible farmers
- **Complementary Redistributive Income Support (CRIS):** Additional payments for the first 40 hectares, benefiting smaller farms
- **Eco-schemes:** Annual payments for voluntary environmental practices (precision farming, biodiversity strips, agroforestry, organic farming, etc.)
- **Young farmer supplement:** Additional payments for farmers under 40

**Pillar II -- Rural Development:**
- Agri-environment-climate measures
- Organic farming conversion and maintenance payments
- Investment support for farm modernization
- Leader local development programs
- Areas facing natural constraints (ANC) payments

**Germany's CAP Strategic Plan (2023-2027):** Germany allocates approximately 30 billion euros over the programming period. Key features include:
- 25% of Pillar I direct payments dedicated to eco-schemes
- Enhanced conditionality requirements (GAEC standards including crop rotation, minimum soil cover, and landscape features)
- Redistribution of payments toward smaller farms through CRIS

### CAP Reform History

| Reform | Year | Key Changes |
|--------|------|-------------|
| MacSharry Reform | 1992 | Shift from price support to direct payments |
| Agenda 2000 | 1999 | Further price cuts; rural development pillar established |
| Fischler Reform | 2003 | Decoupling of payments from production; cross-compliance |
| Health Check | 2008 | Modulation (transfer from Pillar I to Pillar II); single payment scheme |
| Ciolos Reform | 2013 | Greening component (30% of direct payments for environmental practices) |
| 2023 Reform | 2023 | National Strategic Plans; eco-schemes; enhanced conditionality |

### German Agricultural Policy Debates

Germany's agricultural policy debates center on several issues:
- **Farm structural change:** The number of farms has declined from approximately 1.6 million in 1949 to 256,000 in 2023, with increasing consolidation raising concerns about rural community viability
- **Environmental performance:** The agricultural sector accounts for approximately 8% of Germany's greenhouse gas emissions, and nitrate pollution from intensive livestock production remains a persistent environmental challenge
- **Organic farming:** The German government has set a target of 30% organic farming by 2030 (currently approximately 14%), requiring significant expansion of organic conversion support

## ECHR / EU

### WTO Agreement on Agriculture (1995)

The WTO Agreement on Agriculture classifies domestic support into three categories:

| Box | Description | Examples | Disciplines |
|-----|-------------|---------|-------------|
| **Amber (AMS)** | Trade-distorting subsidies linked to production or prices | Price support, input subsidies, deficiency payments | Subject to de minimis threshold (5% for developed; 10% for developing) and aggregate reduction commitments |
| **Blue** | Production-limiting subsidies | Payments contingent on production limitations | Exempt from reduction if criteria met |
| **Green** | Non-trade-distorting subsidies | Environmental payments, disaster relief, research, decoupled income support | Exempt from reduction commitments |

**Doha Round Agriculture Negotiations:** The Doha Development Round, launched in 2001, sought to substantially reduce agricultural trade distortions. Key contested issues include:
- Reduction of amber box subsidies by developed countries
- Special safeguard mechanisms for developing countries
- Cotton subsidies (the "Cotton Four" African nations' demand)
- Special products for developing countries (food security exemptions)

Negotiations have been effectively stalled since the 2008 ministerial collapse, though incremental progress has been achieved at subsequent ministerials (Bali 2013: agreement on public stockholding for food security; Nairobi 2015: elimination of export subsidies).

### EU External Agricultural Trade

The EU's CAP has been a central issue in EU trade negotiations:
- **EU-Mercosur Agreement (concluded 2019, ratification pending):** Provides market access for South American agricultural products in exchange for EU industrial goods access, with beef, poultry, sugar, and ethanol quotas
- **EU-Japan Economic Partnership Agreement (2019):** Eliminated tariffs on approximately 85% of EU agricultural exports to Japan
- **EU-New Zealand Free Trade Agreement (2024):** Includes agricultural market access provisions with tariff-rate quotas for dairy, beef, and sheep meat

### ECHR and Agricultural Property

**J.A. Pye (Oxford) Ltd v. United Kingdom (Grand Chamber, Application No. 44302/02, 2007):** The Grand Chamber held that the acquisition of title to agricultural land through adverse possession under English law did not violate Article 1 of Protocol 1, finding that the limitation period served the legitimate aim of land management certainty and fell within the state's margin of appreciation.

**Brosset-Triboulet v. France (Grand Chamber, Application No. 34078/02, 2010):** The Court addressed restrictions on agricultural property in coastal zones, holding that environmental protection measures restricting agricultural use of coastal land were proportionate and within the state's margin of appreciation.

**Chassagnou v. France (Grand Chamber, Application No. 25088/94, 1999):** The Court found violations of Article 11 (freedom of association) and Protocol 1, Article 1 in French legislation requiring landowners to surrender their hunting rights to a municipal hunting association, even if the landowners objected to hunting on ethical grounds. This case established limits on the state's ability to compel landowner participation in rural management schemes.

## Comparative Analysis

### Support Levels and Mechanisms

South Korea provides the highest level of agricultural support among the jurisdictions, primarily through market price support (high tariffs and government purchasing). The EU's support is predominantly through decoupled direct payments (green box under WTO terms). The United States combines commodity-linked programs (amber box) with insurance subsidies and conservation payments. The trend across all jurisdictions is toward less trade-distorting forms of support, driven by WTO disciplines and policy reform.

### Environmental Conditionality

The EU leads in linking agricultural payments to environmental performance, with mandatory conditionality (GAEC standards) and voluntary eco-schemes consuming 25% of Pillar I budgets. South Korea's 2020 Public Direct Payment reform introduced environmental conditionality for the first time. The U.S. maintains conservation compliance requirements (farmers must follow approved conservation plans to receive commodity program benefits) and operates large standalone conservation programs (CRP, CSP, EQIP).

### Farm Structure

The vast differences in average farm size -- 1.6 hectares in South Korea, 63 hectares in Germany, 180 hectares in the United States -- profoundly shape subsidy design. South Korea's and the EU's redistributive mechanisms favor smaller farms. U.S. programs historically benefit larger operations, though payment limitations (currently $125,000 per person for commodity programs) attempt to constrain concentration.

### Trade Implications

Agricultural subsidies remain the most contentious issue in international trade. The WTO's box system has constrained the most trade-distorting forms of support, but significant domestic support continues under green box provisions. The stalled Doha Round reflects the political difficulty of further agricultural trade liberalization, particularly for developing countries seeking to protect domestic food production and for developed countries' farm lobbies.
