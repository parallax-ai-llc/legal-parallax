## Summary

Gun control -- the regulation of the manufacture, sale, possession, and use of firearms and ammunition -- represents one of the most starkly divergent areas of law across major legal systems, reflecting fundamentally different constitutional traditions, cultural attitudes, and policy choices regarding the relationship between individual liberty, state authority, and public safety. South Korea maintains one of the world's strictest firearms regimes under the Act on the Safety Management of Guns, Swords, Explosives, Etc. (총포·도검·화약류 등의 안전관리에 관한 법률), which effectively prohibits civilian ownership of firearms except for extremely limited licensed purposes such as hunting and sport shooting, requires storage at police stations when not in authorized use, and imposes severe criminal penalties for illegal possession including up to ten years' imprisonment; South Korea's firearms homicide rate is among the lowest in the world, with fewer than five such deaths annually in a population of approximately 52 million. The United States stands as a global outlier, with the Second Amendment to the Constitution protecting "the right of the people to keep and bear Arms," interpreted by the Supreme Court in District of Columbia v. Heller (2008) as an individual right and further expanded in New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen (2022) to require that firearms regulations be consistent with the historical tradition of firearms regulation, rendering the means-end scrutiny framework previously applied by most federal courts unconstitutional; the U.S. has approximately 400 million civilian-owned firearms and one of the highest rates of gun-related deaths among developed nations. Germany permits civilian firearms ownership under the Weapons Act (Waffengesetz, WaffG) subject to a licensing system requiring demonstrated need, reliability, personal aptitude, knowledge, and adequate storage, with the Federal Administrative Court and state licensing authorities exercising significant discretion; Germany tightened its firearms laws following mass shootings in Erfurt (2002) and Winnenden (2009). The European Union harmonizes minimum firearms standards through the European Firearms Directive (Directive 91/477/EEC, as amended by Directive 2017/853), while the European Court of Human Rights has not recognized an individual right to bear arms under the Convention, and the Arms Trade Treaty (2014) establishes international standards for the cross-border transfer of conventional arms.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

The Act on the Safety Management of Guns, Swords, Explosives, Etc. (총포·도검·화약류 등의 안전관리에 관한 법률), commonly referred to as the Firearms Act, establishes a near-total prohibition on civilian firearms ownership. Article 11 requires a license for the possession of any firearm, and Article 12 enumerates the extremely limited purposes for which a license may be granted: hunting, sport shooting, academic research, and other purposes approved by the Commissioner General of the National Police Agency.

### Licensing and Storage

The licensing process involves a thorough background investigation by the National Police Agency (경찰청), including criminal history checks, mental health evaluations, and interviews with neighbors and associates. Licenses are issued for specific firearms and must be renewed periodically. Most critically, Article 15 requires that licensed firearms be stored at the jurisdictional police station when not in active authorized use, eliminating virtually all household firearm possession.

### Penalties

| Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|
| Unauthorized possession of firearms | Up to 10 years imprisonment |
| Unauthorized manufacture of firearms | Up to 15 years imprisonment |
| Possession of firearms for criminal purpose | Up to life imprisonment |
| Unauthorized possession of swords/blades over 15cm | Up to 3 years imprisonment |

### Military Context

All male South Korean citizens undergo mandatory military service of 18-21 months, during which they receive firearms training. Upon discharge, reservists may be called for periodic training. This creates a situation where a substantial portion of the male population has firearms training but no legal access to firearms in civilian life. The military context also means that most firearms-related incidents in South Korea involve military weapons or illegally obtained firearms rather than legally held civilian weapons.

### Notable Incidents

Despite the strict regime, South Korea has experienced rare but impactful firearms incidents. The most significant was the 2005 shooting at a military checkpoint where a conscript killed eight fellow soldiers with a service weapon, leading to enhanced mental health screening protocols for military personnel.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The Second Amendment provides: "A well regulated Militia, being necessary to the security of a free State, the right of the people to keep and bear Arms, shall not be infringed."

**District of Columbia v. Heller, 554 U.S. 570 (2008):** The Supreme Court held 5-4 that the Second Amendment protects an individual right to possess firearms unconnected with service in a militia, and struck down the District of Columbia's handgun ban and trigger-lock requirement.

**McDonald v. City of Chicago, 561 U.S. 742 (2010):** The Court incorporated the Second Amendment against state and local governments through the Fourteenth Amendment.

**New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen, 597 U.S. 1 (2022):** The Court held 6-3 that the Second and Fourteenth Amendments protect an individual's right to carry a handgun for self-defense outside the home and that firearms regulations must be "consistent with this Nation's historical tradition of firearm regulation." The decision replaced the means-end scrutiny framework with a text, history, and tradition test.

**United States v. Rahimi, 602 U.S. ___ (2024):** The Court upheld a federal law (18 U.S.C. Section 922(g)(8)) prohibiting firearm possession by individuals subject to domestic violence restraining orders, applying the Bruen historical tradition framework while adopting a more flexible approach to historical analogy.

### Federal Statutory Framework

| Statute | Year | Key Provisions |
|---------|------|----------------|
| National Firearms Act (NFA) | 1934 | Regulates machine guns, short-barreled rifles/shotguns, silencers through registration and tax |
| Gun Control Act (GCA) | 1968 | Federal firearms licensing; prohibited person categories; interstate commerce restrictions |
| Brady Handgun Violence Prevention Act | 1993 | Established NICS background check system for licensed dealer sales |
| Federal Assault Weapons Ban | 1994-2004 | Prohibited manufacture/sale of certain semi-automatic firearms; expired 2004 |
| Bipartisan Safer Communities Act | 2022 | Enhanced juvenile record reviews; funding for state crisis intervention programs; closing "boyfriend loophole" |

### State Variation

State firearms laws vary enormously. As of 2026, 29 states have enacted "permitless carry" or "constitutional carry" laws allowing concealed firearm carry without a permit. Conversely, states such as California, New York, New Jersey, and Massachusetts maintain comprehensive licensing, waiting period, and assault weapon ban requirements. This variation creates significant interstate enforcement challenges.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

The Waffengesetz (Weapons Act, WaffG), most recently comprehensively revised in 2002 and amended in 2009, 2017, and 2020, establishes a licensing-based system for firearms ownership. There is no constitutional right to bear arms in Germany. The Basic Law does not address individual firearms possession.

### Licensing Requirements

Section 4 WaffG requires any person seeking to acquire, possess, or carry a firearm to obtain a license (Waffenbesitzkarte for possession; Waffenschein for carrying). Requirements include:

- **Age:** Minimum 18 years for firearms generally; 21 for certain categories
- **Reliability (Zuverlässigkeit, Section 5):** Applicants must demonstrate reliability through a clean criminal record and absence of membership in prohibited organizations
- **Personal aptitude (persönliche Eignung, Section 6):** Applicants must pass mental and physical fitness evaluations
- **Expertise (Sachkunde, Section 7):** Demonstrated knowledge of firearms safety and relevant law
- **Need (Bedürfnis, Section 8):** Applicants must demonstrate a recognized need, such as hunting (Section 13), sport shooting (Section 14), or professional necessity (Section 18)
- **Secure storage (Section 36):** Firearms must be stored in approved safes meeting DIN/EN standards

### Post-Shooting Reforms

**Erfurt Massacre (April 26, 2002):** A 19-year-old former student killed 16 people at the Gutenberg-Gymnasium in Erfurt, Thuringia, using legally owned firearms. The shooting prompted the 2002 comprehensive revision of the WaffG, raising the minimum age for large-caliber sport shooting weapons to 21 and enhancing storage requirements.

**Winnenden Shooting (March 11, 2009):** A 17-year-old killed 15 people at the Albertville-Realschule in Winnenden, Baden-Württemberg, using his father's legally owned pistol, which had been stored improperly. The 2009 amendments introduced unannounced inspections of firearms storage, a national firearms register, and prohibited certain large-caliber firearms for sport shooting.

**2020 Amendments:** Following the Halle synagogue attack (2019) and the Hanau attack (2020), the WaffG was further amended to strengthen background checks, including queries to domestic intelligence agencies (Verfassungsschutz) before license issuance.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Firearms Directive

The European Firearms Directive (Directive 91/477/EEC), originally enacted in 1991 and substantially amended by Directive 2008/51/EC and Directive (EU) 2017/853, establishes minimum harmonized standards for the acquisition, possession, and transfer of firearms within the EU. The Directive classifies firearms into four categories:

| Category | Description | Regulatory Approach |
|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| A (Prohibited) | Military weapons, automatic firearms, firearms disguised as other objects | Prohibited with limited exceptions for museums, dealers |
| B (Subject to authorization) | Semi-automatic firearms, repeating firearms, single-shot handguns | Require authorization from national authorities |
| C (Subject to declaration) | Repeating long firearms other than Category B, single-shot long firearms | Must be declared to national authorities |
| D (Other) | Single-shot long firearms with smooth bore | Subject to national regulations |

Directive 2017/853 was enacted following the November 2015 Paris attacks and strengthened controls on Category A firearms, enhanced tracing and marking requirements, and established an EU-wide framework for the deactivation of firearms.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

The European Court of Human Rights has not recognized an individual right to bear arms under the Convention. However, the Court has addressed firearms-related issues through the lens of Article 2 (right to life) and Article 8 (private life):

**Kotilainen and Others v. Finland (Application No. 62439/12, 2020):** The Court examined Finland's firearms licensing regime following the Jokela school shooting (2007) and Kauhajoki school shooting (2008), holding that Article 2 imposes positive obligations on states to establish and enforce adequate firearms licensing procedures to prevent foreseeable threats to life.

### Arms Trade Treaty (ATT)

The Arms Trade Treaty (ATT), adopted by the UN General Assembly on April 2, 2013, and entered into force on December 24, 2014, establishes international standards for the cross-border trade in conventional arms. The Treaty requires states parties to assess the risk that exported arms could be used to commit serious international humanitarian law violations, and to deny export authorization where an overriding risk exists. As of 2026, 113 states are party to the ATT. The United States signed but has not ratified the Treaty.

## Comparative Analysis

### Constitutional Framework

The most fundamental divergence is constitutional. Only the United States recognizes a constitutional right to bear arms, which the Supreme Court has interpreted as an individual right subject only to regulations consistent with the historical tradition of firearms regulation. South Korea, Germany, and the ECHR framework treat firearms as dangerous instrumentalities subject to comprehensive state regulation as a matter of public safety, with no individual right to arms ownership.

### Licensing Stringency Spectrum

South Korea maintains the most restrictive regime, effectively prohibiting civilian firearms ownership with narrow exceptions. Germany permits civilian ownership but requires demonstrated need, comprehensive vetting, and secure storage. The EU Directive establishes harmonized minimum standards across member states. The United States varies from permissive (permitless carry states) to moderately restrictive (California, New York) but generally does not require demonstrated need for firearm ownership.

### Policy Outcomes

The correlation between firearms regulation stringency and firearms violence rates is striking. South Korea (near-total prohibition) has fewer than five firearms homicides annually. Germany (strict licensing) has approximately 50. The United States (constitutional right, widespread ownership) has approximately 20,000 annually. While causation is debated and confounding variables are numerous, the correlation between regulatory stringency and firearms mortality is consistent across comparative analyses.
