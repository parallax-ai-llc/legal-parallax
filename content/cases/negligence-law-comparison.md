## Summary

Negligence -- the failure to exercise the standard of care that a reasonably prudent person would exercise in similar circumstances, resulting in harm to another -- constitutes the foundational principle of modern tort law across virtually all legal systems, yet its doctrinal contours, evidentiary standards, and procedural mechanisms vary significantly among jurisdictions. South Korea regulates negligence primarily through the Civil Act (민법), particularly Articles 750 through 766, which establish a general fault-based liability framework rooted in the concept of 불법행위 (tort/unlawful act), supplemented by specialized statutes such as the Product Liability Act (제조물 책임법) of 2002 and the Automobile Damage Compensation Guarantee Act (자동차손해배상 보장법). The United States applies negligence doctrine through state common law, with the landmark formulation emerging from Palsgraf v. Long Island Railroad Co. (1928) and the duty-breach-causation-damages framework refined through centuries of judicial development, while also incorporating statutory modifications such as tort reform caps and comparative fault regimes. Germany addresses negligence through the Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB), principally Section 823 (unlawful interference with protected rights) and Section 276 (defining Fahrlässigkeit as failure to exercise the care required in ordinary dealings), with a sophisticated tripartite structure distinguishing between contractual, delictual, and quasi-contractual fault. At the European level, the European Court of Human Rights has recognized positive obligations on states under Articles 2 and 8 of the ECHR to protect individuals from negligent state action, while the EU has harmonized negligence standards in specific sectors through directives including the Product Liability Directive 85/374/EEC (revised by Directive 2024/2853) and the AI Liability Directive proposal, reflecting an ongoing effort to modernize fault-based liability for emerging technologies.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's negligence law is anchored in the Civil Act (민법), enacted in 1958 and substantially modeled on German and Japanese civil law traditions. Article 750 establishes the general principle: "A person who causes damage to another person by an unlawful act, intentionally or negligently, shall be liable to compensate for resulting damages" (고의 또는 과실로 인한 위법행위로 타인에게 손해를 가한 자는 그 손해를 배상할 책임이 있다). This broad formulation encompasses both intentional torts and negligence within a single provision, unlike the more differentiated approach found in German law.

### Standard of Care

The Korean standard of care is measured against the objective benchmark of a "good manager's duty of care" (선량한 관리자의 주의의무), derived from Civil Act Article 681. Courts evaluate whether the defendant exercised the level of care that a reasonable and prudent person in the same profession or position would have exercised under the same circumstances. The Supreme Court of Korea has consistently held that this standard must be assessed contextually, accounting for the nature of the activity, the foreseeability of harm, and the social utility of the conduct.

### Causation and Proof

Korean courts apply the adequate causation theory (상당인과관계설), which requires the plaintiff to demonstrate that the defendant's negligent conduct was both a factual cause and a legally adequate cause of the harm. The Supreme Court has relaxed the causation burden in environmental pollution and medical malpractice cases. In the landmark Supreme Court Decision 2011Da22092 (2012), the Court held that in medical negligence cases, where the patient proves the occurrence of an adverse outcome during or following treatment that would not ordinarily occur absent negligence, a presumption of causal connection arises, shifting the burden to the medical provider to demonstrate an alternative cause.

### Comparative Fault

Article 763 of the Civil Act incorporates Article 396 (contributory negligence provision from contract law) into tort claims by reference, enabling courts to reduce damages proportionally based on the victim's share of fault (과실상계). Korean courts apply a pure proportional reduction system, and the Supreme Court has established that courts may raise comparative fault sua sponte even if not pleaded by the defendant (Supreme Court Decision 2007Da18883).

### Special Negligence Regimes

The Product Liability Act (제조물 책임법), enacted in 2002 and amended in 2017, imposes strict liability on manufacturers for defective products but preserves a negligence-based framework for distributors and sellers. The Automobile Damage Compensation Guarantee Act (자동차손해배상 보장법) establishes a near-strict liability regime for vehicle operators, requiring operators to prove the absence of negligence to escape liability.

### Medical Malpractice

Korea's medical malpractice system relies on the general negligence framework under Article 750, supplemented by the Medical Disputes Mediation and Arbitration Act (의료분쟁 조정 및 중재에 관한 법률), enacted in 2011. The Korea Medical Dispute Mediation and Arbitration Agency (한국의료분쟁조정중재원) provides an alternative dispute resolution mechanism, and mediation is deemed accepted if neither party objects within 14 days of a mediation decision.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Negligence law in the United States is primarily governed by state common law, resulting in substantial jurisdictional variation within the overarching duty-breach-causation-damages framework. The Restatement (Third) of Torts: Liability for Physical and Emotional Harm (2010), published by the American Law Institute, provides the most influential scholarly synthesis of American negligence doctrine.

**Elements of a Negligence Claim:** A plaintiff must establish four elements: (1) the defendant owed a duty of care to the plaintiff; (2) the defendant breached that duty; (3) the breach was the actual and proximate cause of the plaintiff's injury; and (4) the plaintiff suffered legally cognizable damages.

### Duty of Care

The duty analysis in American law was profoundly shaped by Judge Benjamin Cardozo's opinion in Palsgraf v. Long Island Railroad Co., 248 N.Y. 339 (1928), which held that a defendant owes a duty of care only to foreseeable plaintiffs. Judge Learned Hand's formulation in United States v. Carroll Towing Co., 159 F.2d 169 (2d Cir. 1947) -- the "Hand formula" (B < PL, where liability exists when the burden of precaution is less than the probability of loss multiplied by the magnitude of loss) -- remains the dominant analytical framework for determining breach.

### Causation

American courts apply a two-pronged causation analysis. Actual cause (cause-in-fact) is typically determined by the "but-for" test: but for the defendant's negligence, would the harm have occurred? In cases involving multiple sufficient causes, many jurisdictions apply the "substantial factor" test from Anderson v. Minneapolis, St. Paul & Sault Ste. Marie Railway Co., 146 Minn. 430 (1920). Proximate cause (legal cause) limits liability to harms within the scope of the risk created by the defendant's negligence, as articulated in the Restatement (Third).

### Comparative and Contributory Fault

American jurisdictions follow one of four approaches to plaintiff fault:

| System | States | Rule |
|--------|--------|------|
| Pure contributory negligence | Alabama, Maryland, North Carolina, Virginia, District of Columbia | Any plaintiff fault bars recovery entirely |
| Pure comparative fault | California, New York, Florida, and approximately 10 others | Plaintiff's recovery reduced by percentage of fault regardless of amount |
| Modified comparative fault (50% bar) | Colorado, Georgia, Idaho, and approximately 10 others | Plaintiff barred if 50% or more at fault |
| Modified comparative fault (51% bar) | Texas, Pennsylvania, Ohio, and approximately 20 others | Plaintiff barred if 51% or more at fault |

### Tort Reform

Beginning in the 1980s, many states enacted tort reform legislation imposing caps on non-economic damages. California's Medical Injury Compensation Reform Act (MICRA, 1975) capped non-economic damages in medical malpractice at $250,000 -- a cap that remained unchanged until voters approved Proposition 35 in 2022, raising the cap to $350,000 for non-death cases and $500,000 for wrongful death cases, with annual inflation adjustments. Texas Civil Practice and Remedies Code Chapter 74 caps non-economic damages in medical malpractice at $250,000 per physician and $500,000 total for healthcare institutions.

### Res Ipsa Loquitur

The doctrine of res ipsa loquitur ("the thing speaks for itself"), established in Byrne v. Boadle (1863) and refined in Escola v. Coca Cola Bottling Co. of Fresno, 24 Cal.2d 453 (1944), permits an inference of negligence when (1) the accident is of a kind that ordinarily does not occur without negligence, (2) the instrumentality was within the exclusive control of the defendant, and (3) the plaintiff did not contribute to the injury.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German negligence law is codified primarily in the Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB), which has been in force since January 1, 1900. The system distinguishes sharply between contractual liability (Vertragshaftung) and delictual liability (Deliktshaftung), each governed by distinct provisions.

**Section 823(1) BGB -- Delictual Liability:** "A person who, intentionally or negligently, unlawfully injures the life, body, health, freedom, property or another right of another person is liable to make compensation to the other party for the damage arising from this" (Wer vorsätzlich oder fahrlässig das Leben, den Körper, die Gesundheit, die Freiheit, das Eigentum oder ein sonstiges Recht eines anderen widerrechtlich verletzt, ist dem anderen zum Ersatz des daraus entstehenden Schadens verpflichtet).

**Section 276 BGB -- Fahrlässigkeit:** Negligence is defined as the failure to exercise the care required in ordinary dealings (die im Verkehr erforderliche Sorgfalt). This is an objective standard measured against a reasonable person in the defendant's position, though professional standards apply to persons acting in a professional capacity.

### Protected Rights under Section 823(1)

Unlike the broad Anglo-American duty of care concept, Section 823(1) protects only enumerated rights: life, body, health, freedom, property, and "other rights" (sonstige Rechte). Pure economic loss is not compensable under Section 823(1) but may be recoverable under Section 823(2) (violation of a protective statute) or Section 826 (intentional damage contra bonos mores).

### Verkehrssicherungspflichten (Traffic Safety Duties)

German courts have developed an extensive body of judge-made duties known as Verkehrssicherungspflichten -- obligations to secure sources of danger that one creates or controls. These duties function analogously to the common law duty of care and have been applied to property owners, organizers of events, operators of businesses, and governmental entities responsible for infrastructure.

### Causation

German law applies a dual causation test: (1) conditio sine qua non (equivalence theory) -- the act must be a necessary condition of the harm; and (2) Adäquanztheorie (adequate causation theory) -- the act must be generally suitable to bring about the type of harm that occurred. Additionally, the Schutzzweck der Norm (protective purpose of the norm) doctrine limits liability to harms within the scope of the risk the violated norm was designed to prevent.

### Comparative Fault

Section 254 BGB (Mitverschulden) provides for proportional reduction of damages based on the claimant's contributory negligence. Courts exercise broad discretion in apportioning fault, considering the degree of causative contribution and the degree of culpability of each party. Unlike some American jurisdictions, German law does not employ a threshold system that bars recovery entirely.

### Strict Liability Statutes

German law supplements fault-based liability with numerous strict liability statutes (Gefährdungshaftung), including the Road Traffic Act (Straßenverkehrsgesetz, StVG) Section 7, the Environmental Liability Act (Umwelthaftungsgesetz, UmweltHG) of 1990, the Product Liability Act (Produkthaftungsgesetz, ProdHaftG) implementing EU Directive 85/374/EEC, and the Gene Technology Act (Gentechnikgesetz, GenTG).

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR has developed a significant body of jurisprudence addressing state negligence through the positive obligations doctrine, holding that states must take reasonable measures to protect individuals' Convention rights.

**Osman v. United Kingdom (Application No. 23452/94, Judgment of October 28, 1998):** The Grand Chamber established the "Osman test" for state liability under Article 2 (right to life): authorities must take preventive operational measures to protect an individual whose life is at risk from criminal acts of another individual, provided the authorities knew or ought to have known of the existence of a real and immediate risk to the life of an identified individual and failed to take measures within the scope of their powers which, judged reasonably, might have been expected to avoid that risk.

**Öneryıldız v. Turkey (Application No. 48939/99, Grand Chamber Judgment of November 30, 2004):** The Court found a violation of Article 2 arising from a methane explosion at a municipal waste-collection site that killed 39 people. Turkey was held liable for negligent failure to enforce regulations and warn inhabitants of the known risk, establishing that state negligence in regulatory enforcement can give rise to Article 2 liability.

**Z and Others v. United Kingdom (Application No. 29392/95, Judgment of May 10, 2001):** The Court found a violation of Article 3 where local authority social services negligently failed to protect children from prolonged abuse and neglect despite being aware of the situation, establishing that negligent failure to act can constitute inhuman or degrading treatment.

### EU Product Liability Framework

Directive 85/374/EEC on liability for defective products (1985) established a strict liability regime for product manufacturers across the EU, removing the need for victims to prove negligence. This directive was replaced by Directive 2024/2853 on liability for defective products, adopted on October 23, 2024, which modernizes the framework to cover digital products, AI systems, and software, extends liability to online platforms in certain circumstances, and introduces a rebuttable presumption of defectiveness when the product fails to comply with mandatory safety requirements. Member states must transpose the new directive by December 9, 2026.

### AI Liability Directive Proposal

The European Commission proposed a Directive on adapting non-contractual civil liability rules to artificial intelligence (AI Liability Directive, COM/2022/496) in September 2022. The proposal introduces a rebuttable presumption of causation linking negligent non-compliance with AI-related duties of care to the AI system's output, easing the burden of proof for claimants harmed by AI systems. The proposal introduces disclosure obligations requiring defendants to reveal evidence about high-risk AI systems.

### Rome II Regulation

Regulation (EC) No 864/2007 on the law applicable to non-contractual obligations (Rome II) establishes choice-of-law rules for cross-border negligence claims within the EU. The general rule under Article 4 applies the law of the country where the damage occurs (lex loci damni), regardless of the country in which the negligent act took place.

## Comparative Analysis

### Structural Approaches to Negligence

The four jurisdictions reveal two fundamental structural approaches to negligence liability:

**Unified Tort Clause (South Korea):** Article 750 of the Korean Civil Act contains a single, broadly worded provision covering all tortious conduct, including both intentional and negligent acts. This approach provides flexibility but may create uncertainty regarding the precise boundaries of liability.

**Differentiated Protected Interests (Germany):** Section 823 BGB limits delictual liability to interference with enumerated rights, creating a more restricted but more predictable liability framework. Pure economic loss claims require resort to separate provisions (Sections 823(2), 826 BGB).

**Common Law Incrementalism (United States):** American negligence law has developed through centuries of judicial precedent, resulting in a highly detailed but jurisdictionally fragmented framework. The duty-breach-causation-damages structure provides analytical clarity but permits substantial variation in application.

### Causation Doctrines

All four systems require both factual and legal causation, but employ different terminology and analytical frameworks. South Korea and Germany both apply the adequate causation theory (Adäquanztheorie), reflecting their shared civil law heritage. The United States employs the but-for/proximate cause analysis, which serves a functionally similar purpose. Germany adds the additional limiting principle of Schutzzweck der Norm, which has no direct equivalent in American or Korean law.

### Treatment of Pure Economic Loss

The most significant doctrinal divergence concerns the recoverability of pure economic loss. Germany categorically excludes pure economic loss from Section 823(1) protection. The United States generally follows the economic loss rule, barring negligence claims for pure economic loss absent physical injury or property damage, though exceptions exist for negligent misrepresentation. South Korea's broad Article 750 does not categorically exclude pure economic loss, and Korean courts have been more willing to allow such claims.

### Burden of Proof Innovations

Each jurisdiction has developed mechanisms to ease the plaintiff's burden in cases where direct evidence of negligence is unavailable: Korea's presumption of causation in medical malpractice (Supreme Court Decision 2011Da22092), America's res ipsa loquitur doctrine, Germany's Anscheinsbeweis (prima facie evidence) doctrine, and the EU's rebuttable presumption of defectiveness in the revised Product Liability Directive.
