## Summary

Debt collection regulation -- the legal framework governing the practices, methods, and limitations on creditors and third-party collectors seeking to recover outstanding debts from consumers -- addresses one of the most conflict-prone areas of consumer financial services, balancing the legitimate rights of creditors to recover amounts owed against the need to protect debtors from harassment, deception, and abusive practices. South Korea regulates debt collection primarily through the Act on Fair Debt Collection Practices (채권의 공정한 추심에 관한 법률), enacted in 2009, which prohibits collection practices involving violence, intimidation, harassment of third parties, deceptive representations, and unfair conduct, administered by the Financial Services Commission (금융위원회) and the Financial Supervisory Service (금융감독원); the 2020 and 2024 amendments significantly strengthened protections by expanding coverage to include non-financial debts, imposing stricter time-of-day restrictions on collection contacts, and enhancing penalties. The United States regulates debt collection primarily through the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA, 15 U.S.C. §§1692-1692p), enacted in 1977, which restricts the practices of third-party debt collectors and provides consumers with a private right of action and statutory damages, supplemented by the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau's (CFPB) Regulation F (2021) modernizing the rules for electronic communications and social media contacts; the FDCPA is one of the most litigated consumer protection statutes, generating thousands of federal lawsuits annually. Germany regulates debt collection through the Rechtsdienstleistungsgesetz (RDG, Legal Services Act) requiring registration of debt collection agencies (Inkassounternehmen), the BGB and ZPO provisions governing enforcement of debts, and specific consumer protection provisions in the UWG (Unfair Competition Act) prohibiting aggressive and misleading collection practices; the 2021 reform of the Inkasso framework through the Gesetz zur Verbesserung des Verbraucherschutzes im Inkassorecht significantly reduced permissible collection fees and increased transparency requirements. At the European level, the EU Consumer Credit Directive (2008/48/EC, recast as Directive (EU) 2023/2225) addresses debt collection in the context of consumer credit, the Unfair Commercial Practices Directive 2005/29/EC prohibits aggressive collection practices as unfair commercial practices, and the European Court of Human Rights has addressed debtor protections under Article 8 (private life) and Article 1 of Protocol 1 (property), while the European Commission's proposal for a cross-border debt recovery framework aims to harmonize enforcement across the single market.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

The Act on Fair Debt Collection Practices (채권의 공정한 추심에 관한 법률, commonly 채권추심법) was enacted on February 6, 2009, in response to widespread abusive collection practices by loan sharks and unauthorized collectors that had become a significant social problem.

### Prohibited Practices

The Act prohibits the following collection practices:

**Violence and Intimidation (Article 9):**
- Use or threat of violence against the debtor or their family
- Damage to or seizure of the debtor's property without legal authority
- Display of force intended to intimidate

**Harassment (Article 10):**
- Repeated phone calls or visits at unreasonable hours
- Contact with the debtor's employer, family, or acquaintances about the debt (with limited exceptions)
- Public disclosure of the debtor's indebtedness

**Deception (Article 11):**
- Misrepresenting the legal status or amount of the debt
- Falsely claiming to be a government official or attorney
- Threatening legal action not actually intended or authorized

**Unfair Practices (Article 12):**
- Demanding payment of debts that have been discharged through bankruptcy or exceeded the statute of limitations
- Collecting unauthorized fees or charges
- Harassing guarantors or family members not legally liable for the debt

### 2020 and 2024 Amendments

**2020 Amendment:** Expanded the Act's coverage from professional debt collectors (채권추심업자) to include original creditors (금융회사 등), addressing the significant gap where financial institutions' own collection departments were not subject to the same restrictions as third-party collectors.

**2024 Amendment:** Extended the permitted contact hours to 9:00 PM (from the previous 8:00 PM cutoff), while simultaneously strengthening penalties for violations and expanding the definition of harassment to include persistent digital communications.

### Enforcement

The Financial Supervisory Service (금융감독원, FSS) oversees compliance by registered debt collection companies. The FSS may impose:
- Corrective orders requiring cessation of violative practices
- Suspension of business operations (up to 6 months)
- Registration revocation
- Administrative fines (과태료)

Criminal penalties for serious violations include imprisonment up to 5 years and fines up to 50 million KRW.

### Debt Restructuring: Individual Rehabilitation and Personal Bankruptcy

The Debtor Rehabilitation and Bankruptcy Act (채무자 회생 및 파산에 관한 법률) provides two primary paths for overwhelmed debtors:
- **Individual Rehabilitation (개인회생):** Court-supervised repayment plan over 3-5 years with discharge of remaining debt. Available to individuals with regular income and unsecured debts below 1 billion KRW.
- **Personal Bankruptcy and Discharge (개인파산 및 면책):** Full discharge of qualifying debts after liquidation of non-exempt assets. Available to individuals unable to pay debts as they come due.

## United States

### Legal Framework

**Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA, 15 U.S.C. §§1692-1692p):** Enacted in 1977 and effective March 20, 1978, the FDCPA is the primary federal statute governing debt collection practices. It applies only to third-party debt collectors, not to original creditors collecting their own debts.

### Key FDCPA Provisions

**Section 1692c -- Communication Practices:**
- Collectors may not contact consumers at unusual or inconvenient times (before 8 AM or after 9 PM)
- Collectors must cease communication upon written request
- Collectors may not contact consumers known to be represented by an attorney
- Collectors may not contact consumers at their workplace if the employer disapproves

**Section 1692d -- Harassment or Abuse:**
- Prohibition on threats of violence
- Prohibition on obscene or profane language
- Prohibition on publishing lists of debtors ("deadbeat lists")
- Prohibition on causing a telephone to ring repeatedly with intent to annoy

**Section 1692e -- False or Misleading Representations:**
- 16 specific categories of prohibited misrepresentations
- Includes false claims of attorney status, false threat of legal action, misrepresentation of amount owed

**Section 1692f -- Unfair Practices:**
- Prohibition on collecting unauthorized amounts
- Prohibition on depositing post-dated checks prematurely
- Prohibition on using deceptive means to collect (e.g., disguised letters)

**Section 1692g -- Validation of Debts:**
- Within 5 days of initial communication, collector must send written notice containing amount of debt, name of creditor, and statement that consumer may dispute the debt within 30 days
- If consumer disputes within 30 days, collector must cease collection until verification is provided

### CFPB Regulation F (2021)

The CFPB's Regulation F, effective November 30, 2021, modernized FDCPA implementation:
- **Call Frequency:** Presumptive limit of 7 telephone calls per 7-day period per debt
- **Electronic Communications:** Collectors may use email, text messages, and social media private messaging, subject to opt-out rights
- **Validation Requirements:** Enhanced requirements for itemization of debt in validation notices
- **Time-Barred Debt:** Collectors must disclose that a debt is time-barred and that payment may restart the limitations period

### Litigation Volume

The FDCPA is one of the most litigated consumer statutes in the United States, with approximately 10,000-12,000 federal lawsuits filed annually. Key Supreme Court decisions include:

| Case | Year | Holding |
|------|------|---------|
| Heintz v. Jenkins | 1995 | FDCPA applies to attorneys who regularly collect debts |
| Jerman v. Carlisle, McNellie, Rini, Kramer & Ulrich LPA | 2010 | Bona fide error defense does not apply to legal errors |
| Sheriff v. Gillie | 2018 | State-appointed special counsel for government debts are not "debt collectors" |
| Henson v. Santander Consumer USA | 2017 | Companies that purchase debts and collect for their own account are not "debt collectors" under FDCPA |

### State Laws

Many states have enacted additional debt collection regulations, some applying to original creditors:
- **California Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (Rosenthal Act):** Extends FDCPA-like protections to original creditors
- **New York:** Department of Financial Services regulations impose additional requirements on debt collectors
- **Texas:** Texas Debt Collection Act provides separate state enforcement mechanism

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany regulates debt collection through a combination of registration requirements, fee limitations, and general consumer protection law.

**Rechtsdienstleistungsgesetz (RDG, Legal Services Act):** Requires debt collection agencies (Inkassounternehmen) to register with the competent Landgericht (regional court). Only registered entities may engage in out-of-court debt collection as a commercial activity. Attorneys are exempt from registration requirements.

**BGB and ZPO Enforcement Framework:** Creditors seeking to enforce debts must generally obtain a court judgment (Vollstreckungstitel) before pursuing enforcement measures. The Mahnverfahren (order for payment procedure) under ZPO §§688-703d provides a streamlined process for uncontested claims, enabling creditors to obtain an enforceable title without a full trial.

### 2021 Inkasso Reform

The Gesetz zur Verbesserung des Verbraucherschutzes im Inkassorecht (Act to Improve Consumer Protection in Debt Collection Law), effective October 1, 2021, represented a significant reform:

**Fee Reductions:**
- Simple standard collection: reduced from 1.3 fee factor to 0.5 fee factor under the RVG (Rechtsanwaltsvergütungsgesetz) scale
- Second collection letter: capped at 0.3 fee factor
- Collection activities for small debts: significantly reduced fees

**Transparency Requirements:**
- First collection letter must clearly state the legal basis of the claim, the original creditor, the principal amount, interest calculation, and all fees
- The consumer must be informed of the right to dispute the claim and the consequences of non-payment

**Information Duties:**
- Collectors must inform consumers about the possibility of installment agreements
- Consumers must be informed about debt counseling services (Schuldnerberatung)

### Gerichtsvollzieher (Court Bailiff) System

Enforcement of monetary judgments in Germany is primarily carried out by Gerichtsvollzieher (court bailiffs), who are state officials authorized to seize property, garnish wages, and conduct asset searches. The Pfändungsfreigrenzen (wage garnishment exemption limits), adjusted annually, protect a debtor's minimum subsistence income from garnishment (approximately EUR 1,409/month as of 2024 for a single person without dependents).

### Restschuldbefreiung (Discharge of Residual Debt)

The Insolvenzordnung (InsO, Insolvency Code) provides for discharge of residual debts (Restschuldbefreiung) after a Wohlverhaltensperiode (good conduct period) of three years (reduced from six years by the 2020 reform implementing EU Directive 2019/1023). This reform significantly improved the situation for over-indebted consumers.

### Consumer Insolvency Statistics

Germany processes approximately 60,000-70,000 consumer insolvency proceedings annually. The Schuldnerberatung (debt counseling) system, funded by Länder and municipalities, provides free counseling to over-indebted consumers and assists with out-of-court debt resolution attempts required before filing for consumer insolvency.

## ECHR & EU

### Unfair Commercial Practices Directive 2005/29/EC

The UCPD addresses aggressive debt collection practices under its framework of "aggressive commercial practices" (Articles 8-9):
- Harassment, coercion, or undue influence that impairs the consumer's freedom of choice
- Annex I, Point 25: "Making persistent and unwanted solicitations by telephone" (included in the "black list" of always-unfair practices)
- Annex I, Point 28: "Creating the impression that the consumer cannot leave the premises until a contract is formed or a payment is made"

### Consumer Credit Directive (EU) 2023/2225

The recast Consumer Credit Directive, adopted in October 2023 (replacing Directive 2008/48/EC), addresses debt collection in the consumer credit context:
- **Article 36:** Requirements for communication with consumers in arrears, including treating consumers with forbearance and considering their financial situation
- **Article 37:** Obligation for creditors to adopt reasonable forbearance measures before enforcement
- **Article 38:** Information requirements for debt collection

### Restructuring Directive (EU) 2019/1023

The Directive on preventive restructuring, insolvency, and discharge of debt requires member states to provide for full discharge of debts for entrepreneurs within a maximum of three years. While primarily aimed at business insolvency, several member states have extended the shorter discharge period to consumer insolvency as well (as Germany did in 2020).

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Zehentner v. Austria (Application No. 20082/02, 2009):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where a debtor's apartment was sold at a forced auction without adequate safeguards for the debtor, who was mentally incapacitated. The case established that forced debt recovery measures must respect the debtor's private and family life.

**Rousk v. Sweden (Application No. 27183/04, 2013):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where the sale of a debtor's home for tax arrears was disproportionate given the relatively small amount owed and the availability of less intrusive means of recovery.

### European Account Preservation Order

Regulation (EU) No 655/2014 establishes a European Account Preservation Order procedure, enabling a creditor in one member state to obtain a preservation order to prevent the transfer or withdrawal of funds held by the debtor in a bank account in another member state. The regulation includes safeguards for debtors, including exemption of amounts necessary for subsistence.

## Comparative Analysis

### Scope of Coverage

A critical distinction is whether debt collection regulation covers only third-party collectors or also original creditors. The FDCPA covers only third-party collectors, leaving original creditors' collection practices to state law and general consumer protection statutes. South Korea's 2020 amendment extended coverage to original creditors, addressing a significant regulatory gap. Germany's framework applies primarily to registered Inkassounternehmen and attorneys. The EU's UCPD framework applies to all commercial practices regardless of whether the collector is a first-party or third-party actor.

### Fee Regulation

Germany's 2021 Inkasso reform is notable for directly regulating the fees that collectors may charge, reducing them substantially. Neither the United States nor South Korea impose comparable detailed fee schedules, though both prohibit the collection of unauthorized amounts. The fee issue is significant because collection fees often constitute a large portion of the total amount demanded from debtors.

### Discharge and Fresh Start

All jurisdictions provide mechanisms for over-indebted consumers to obtain a fresh start, but the speed and accessibility vary. Germany's three-year discharge period (since 2020) is among the shortest in Europe. The United States provides the fastest discharge through Chapter 7 bankruptcy (typically 3-4 months for asset-less cases), but the means test and stigma create barriers. South Korea's individual rehabilitation provides a 3-5 year supervised repayment plan. The EU Restructuring Directive has pushed member states toward shorter discharge periods.

### Private Enforcement vs. Administrative Enforcement

The United States relies heavily on private enforcement through FDCPA lawsuits, with thousands of cases filed annually. South Korea relies primarily on administrative enforcement by the FSS. Germany relies on a combination of regulatory oversight and consumer organization enforcement. The EU framework increasingly emphasizes both administrative enforcement (through national authorities) and collective redress (through the Representative Actions Directive).
