## Summary

Perjury -- the act of knowingly making false statements under oath -- strikes at the foundation of every legal system that depends on truthful testimony for the administration of justice. Despite universal condemnation, the four jurisdictions examined here diverge sharply in definition, severity, and enforcement. South Korea, which faces an unusually high rate of perjury prosecutions, imposes up to 5 years imprisonment for standard perjury and up to 10 years for malicious perjury in criminal cases, while offering sentence reductions for voluntary confessions. Germany treats perjury as a Verbrechen (felony) carrying a minimum of one year imprisonment -- one of the harshest baseline penalties among Western democracies -- while separately criminalizing unsworn false testimony. The United States addresses perjury through multiple federal statutes (18 U.S.C. sections 1621 and 1623) with a maximum of 5 years imprisonment, though the Supreme Court has narrowed the offense by excluding literally true but misleading answers. The ECHR does not directly criminalize perjury but addresses its consequences through the Article 6 fair trial guarantee, holding that convictions obtained through false witness testimony may violate the Convention.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Perjury in South Korea is governed by Articles 152 and 153 of the Korean Criminal Act (형법). South Korea is notable for having one of the highest perjury prosecution rates in the world. Statistics from the late 2000s show that the number of people tried for perjury climbed from 1,210 in 2006 to 1,983 in 2009 -- a rate approximately 420 times higher than Japan's when adjusted for population.

### Article 152: Perjury (위증)

**Paragraph 1 -- Standard Perjury:** A witness who, having been sworn in accordance with law, gives a false statement shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than 5 years or by a fine not exceeding 10 million won.

**Paragraph 2 -- Malicious Perjury (모해위증):** In criminal or disciplinary proceedings, a witness who commits perjury for the purpose of causing injury to the accused or suspect shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than 10 years. This elevated penalty reflects the legislature's recognition that perjury aimed at securing a wrongful conviction poses an extreme threat to justice.

### Article 153: Voluntary Confession (자수 감면)

When a person who has committed perjury makes a voluntary confession or self-denunciation before the judgment in the case where the false statement was given becomes final, the court shall mitigate or remit the punishment. This provision serves as a powerful incentive for witnesses to recant false testimony before irreversible harm is done.

### Key Principles

**Defendant Exemption:** Under established Korean Supreme Court precedent, defendants cannot be charged with perjury for false testimony regarding their own criminal case. Even when a defendant testifies as a witness in a co-defendant's case, their status as a defendant takes precedence, shielding them from perjury liability for statements connected to their own alleged crimes.

**Oath Requirement:** Perjury can only be committed by a witness who has taken a formal oath as prescribed by law. Statements made without a proper oath, even if false, do not constitute perjury under Article 152, though they may give rise to other charges.

**Subornation of Perjury:** Inducing another person to commit perjury is separately punishable. In a high-profile 2024 case, opposition leader Lee Jae-myung was tried on charges of subornation of perjury (위증교사) for allegedly persuading a witness to minimize Lee's prior criminal record. The Seoul Central District Court acquitted Lee in November 2024, though the witness, Kim Jin-seong, was found guilty of perjury and fined 5 million won.

### Landmark Cases

**Namsan 300 Million Won Case:** Former Shinhan Bank Chairman Shin Sang-hoon and former president Lee Baek-soon were acquitted of perjury charges. The appellate court, relying on Supreme Court precedent, held that even when defendants are procedurally eligible to testify as witnesses, their status as defendants takes precedence, and they cannot be charged with perjury for false testimony related to their own crimes.

**Lee Jae-myung Subornation Case (2024):** This politically charged case tested the boundaries of perjury subornation law. Prosecutors sought a 3-year prison term for the opposition leader, alleging he instructed a witness to lie about Lee's past criminal conviction. The acquittal highlighted the high evidentiary burden required to prove subornation.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Federal perjury law is codified primarily in two statutes:

**18 U.S.C. s 1621 -- Perjury Generally:** The traditional, broadly applicable statute covers perjury committed before any competent tribunal, officer, or person, whether within or outside the United States. Anyone who willfully states or subscribes as true any material matter which they do not believe to be true is guilty of perjury and shall be fined or imprisoned not more than 5 years, or both. For proceedings before the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court (FISA), the maximum increases to 10 years.

**18 U.S.C. s 1623 -- False Declarations Before Grand Jury or Court:** This statute applies specifically to federal court proceedings and grand jury testimony. It carries the same 5-year maximum but includes a recantation defense not available under section 1621: a person may avoid conviction if they retract the false statement during the same proceeding before it substantially affected the proceeding and before it became manifest that the falsity had been or would be exposed.

### Elements of the Offense

To secure a perjury conviction, prosecutors must prove four elements:

1. **Oath:** The defendant made a statement under a lawfully administered oath or equivalent affirmation.
2. **Falsity:** The statement was objectively false.
3. **Knowledge:** The defendant knew the statement was false at the time it was made (willfulness).
4. **Materiality:** The false statement was material to the proceeding -- that is, it had a natural tendency to influence or was capable of influencing the outcome.

The "two-witness rule" historically required corroboration of perjury by either two witnesses or one witness plus independent corroborating evidence. Under section 1623, this requirement was relaxed, allowing proof by inconsistent declarations alone.

### State Perjury Laws

Each of the 50 states has its own perjury statutes. Penalties vary considerably:

- **California (Penal Code s 118):** Perjury is a felony punishable by 2, 3, or 4 years in state prison.
- **New York (Penal Law s 210.15):** First-degree perjury is a Class D felony carrying up to 7 years imprisonment.
- **Texas (Penal Code s 37.02):** Perjury is a Class A misdemeanor (up to 1 year) unless committed during a capital felony trial, where it becomes a third-degree felony (2-10 years).

### Landmark Cases

**Bronston v. United States, 409 U.S. 352 (1973):** The Supreme Court unanimously held that a literally true but misleading answer to a question under oath does not constitute perjury. Samuel Bronston, a movie producer in bankruptcy proceedings, was asked whether he had ever had any Swiss bank accounts. He responded that his company had an account there for about six months -- a truthful statement that concealed his personal Swiss account held for five years. The Court reasoned that the perjury statute targets willful falsehood, not misleading implication, and that the burden falls on the examining attorney to ask precise follow-up questions. This case remains the leading authority on the "literal truth" defense.

**United States v. Dunnigan, 507 U.S. 87 (1993):** The Supreme Court upheld sentence enhancement for perjury committed during trial testimony. Sharon Dunnigan testified in her own defense at a cocaine conspiracy trial, denying the inculpatory statements of other witnesses. The district court found she committed perjury and enhanced her sentence under section 3C1.1 of the Sentencing Guidelines. The Court held that this practice does not unconstitutionally burden the right to testify in one's own defense, provided the trial court makes specific findings that the defendant gave false testimony on a material matter with willful intent.

**Clinton Impeachment (1998-1999):** President Bill Clinton was impeached by the House of Representatives on charges including perjury before a federal grand jury regarding his testimony about his relationship with Monica Lewinsky. The Senate acquitted Clinton, with the perjury article failing 45-55 -- 22 votes short of the two-thirds majority required for removal. The case demonstrated the political dimensions of perjury enforcement at the highest levels of government.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany maintains one of the strictest perjury regimes in the Western world, distinguishing between three distinct offenses in the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB):

**Section 153 -- False Unsworn Testimony (Falsche uneidliche Aussage):** Whoever, as a witness or expert, gives false unsworn testimony before a court or other agency competent to examine witnesses and experts under oath shall be punished with imprisonment from 3 months to 5 years.

**Section 154 -- Perjury (Meineid):** Whoever falsely takes an oath before a court or another agency competent to administer oaths shall be punished with imprisonment for not less than 1 year. In less serious cases, the penalty ranges from 6 months to 5 years. The minimum one-year sentence classifies perjury as a Verbrechen (felony) under German law, reflecting the gravity with which the legal system treats violations of the oath.

**Section 156 -- False Affirmations in Lieu of Oath (Falsche Versicherung an Eides statt):** Whoever makes a false affirmation in lieu of an oath before an authority competent to receive such affirmations shall be punished with imprisonment of up to 3 years or a fine.

### The Role of the Oath in German Proceedings

A distinctive feature of German procedure is that witnesses are not routinely sworn in. Under the Code of Criminal Procedure (StPO), oaths are administered only when the court deems it necessary for establishing the truth. This procedural choice has significant criminal law implications: a false statement given without an oath is punished under section 153 (maximum 5 years), while the same statement given under oath triggers section 154 (minimum 1 year). In practice, German courts increasingly dispense with oaths, meaning that most false witness testimony is prosecuted under section 153 rather than the more severe section 154.

### Mitigation and Rectification

**Section 157 -- Mitigation in Special Circumstances:** The court may mitigate the penalty for offenses under sections 153 and 154 if the offender gave false testimony in order to avert a danger of criminal punishment from a relative or from themselves. This provision recognizes the human difficulty of testifying truthfully when the truth would incriminate oneself or a family member.

**Section 158 -- Rectification (Berichtigung):** The court may mitigate the penalty or dispense with punishment entirely if the offender rectifies the false statement in a timely manner. Rectification is deemed "too late" if: (a) the false statement has already influenced the decision in the proceedings; (b) detriment to another person has already arisen; or (c) a criminal investigation against the offender has already been initiated.

### Key Principles

**Objective Theory of Falsity:** German courts apply the "objective theory" (objektive Theorie) to determine whether testimony is false. A statement is false if its content does not correspond to objective reality, regardless of the witness's subjective beliefs. This contrasts with the "subjective theory" used in some jurisdictions, where a statement is false only if it contradicts what the witness believes to be true.

**No Defendant Testimony:** In Germany, defendants do not testify as witnesses. They have the right to make unsworn statements but cannot be placed under oath. Consequently, defendants are structurally exempt from perjury charges for statements made in their own proceedings, though they may face charges for false unsworn declarations under section 156 in other contexts.

**Expert Witnesses:** Sections 153 and 154 expressly apply to expert witnesses (Sachverstandige) who give false opinions before a court or competent agency. This extension reflects the significant influence that expert testimony exerts on judicial outcomes.

## ECHR & EU

### The ECHR Framework

The European Convention on Human Rights does not contain a standalone perjury provision. Instead, perjury and false testimony are addressed indirectly through several Convention rights, most prominently Article 6 (right to a fair trial).

### Article 6: Fair Trial Implications

**Article 6(1) -- Fair Hearing:** The overarching right to a fair hearing encompasses the principle that convictions must rest on reliable evidence. When a conviction is based substantially on testimony later shown to be perjured, the ECHR may find a violation of Article 6(1) on the ground that the proceedings, taken as a whole, were unfair.

**Article 6(3)(d) -- Examination of Witnesses:** Every person charged with a criminal offense has the right to examine or have examined witnesses against them and to obtain the attendance and examination of witnesses on their behalf under the same conditions as witnesses against them. This provision ensures that defendants can challenge the credibility of testimony -- a critical safeguard against perjury.

### Landmark ECHR Cases

**Vidal v. Belgium (1992):** The European Court found a violation of Article 6 when the Brussels Court of Appeal refused to hear any defense witnesses and convicted the applicant, a prison warden accused of aiding an escape, to four years imprisonment. The Court held that while Article 6(3)(d) does not require every requested witness to be heard, the refusal to hear any defense witnesses, without reasons, restricted the rights of the defense to an extent incompatible with a fair trial. The case underscores the importance of adversarial testing of testimony as a safeguard against unreliable or false evidence.

**Doorson v. The Netherlands (1996):** The Court accepted the use of anonymous witness testimony but held that convictions must not be based "solely or to a decisive extent" on such testimony. This principle, later codified as the "sole or decisive rule," limits the risk that unchallenged -- and potentially false -- testimony can form the basis of a conviction.

**Al-Khawaja and Tahery v. United Kingdom (2011, Grand Chamber):** The Grand Chamber refined the sole or decisive rule, holding that even when a conviction is based decisively on the statement of an absent witness, there is no automatic violation of Article 6 if sufficient counterbalancing factors exist to ensure overall fairness. The case recognized that while the inability to cross-examine a witness increases the risk of undetected perjury, procedural safeguards can compensate.

### EU Framework

The European Union does not harmonize perjury law across Member States. Criminal law remains primarily a national competence, and perjury definitions, penalties, and procedures vary substantially across the 27 Member States. However, several EU instruments touch on related issues:

**Directive 2012/29/EU (Victims' Rights Directive):** Establishes minimum standards for the rights, support, and protection of victims of crime. While not directly addressing perjury, it ensures that victims -- who may be harmed by false testimony -- have procedural rights to participate in criminal proceedings and challenge evidence.

**European Arrest Warrant (Framework Decision 2002/584/JHA):** Facilitates cross-border surrender of persons accused of serious crimes, including perjury when it meets the dual criminality requirement or falls within the list of 32 offenses for which dual criminality is not required. While perjury is not specifically enumerated in the list, it may qualify under related categories.

**Regulation (EU) 2017/1939 -- European Public Prosecutor's Office (EPPO):** The EPPO has competence over crimes affecting the EU's financial interests. Perjury committed in the context of fraud against the EU budget could potentially fall within the EPPO's investigative scope, though perjury itself is not a core EPPO offense.

### Council of Europe Instruments

**Recommendation No. R (97) 13** on intimidation of witnesses and the rights of the defense addresses the problem of witness tampering and false testimony, recommending that Member States adopt measures to protect witnesses while preserving fair trial rights. The Recommendation acknowledges that the threat of perjury prosecution alone is often insufficient to ensure truthful testimony and calls for complementary protective measures.

## Comparative Analysis

### Severity of Penalties

Germany imposes the harshest baseline penalty, with a minimum of 1 year imprisonment for sworn perjury -- a sentence that automatically classifies the offense as a felony. South Korea's malicious perjury provision (up to 10 years) represents the highest maximum among the jurisdictions for aggravated forms. The United States occupies a middle ground at 5 years maximum, though sentence enhancements for obstruction of justice (as in Dunnigan) can significantly increase actual sentences. The ECHR framework imposes no penalties directly but can require states to reopen cases tainted by perjured testimony.

### The Oath Distinction

Germany uniquely distinguishes between sworn and unsworn false testimony, with dramatically different penalties for each. South Korea and the United States generally require an oath as a precondition for perjury charges. This distinction has practical significance: in Germany, where oaths are increasingly rare in practice, most false testimony is prosecuted under the less severe section 153 rather than the felony-level section 154.

### Self-Correction Incentives

All three national jurisdictions provide incentives for witnesses who voluntarily correct false testimony. South Korea's Article 153 mandates mitigation or remission for voluntary confessions made before the judgment becomes final. The U.S. section 1623(d) provides a complete recantation defense if the correction occurs within the same proceeding. Germany's section 158 allows mitigation or dispensation but imposes a stricter timeliness requirement. These provisions reflect a pragmatic recognition that encouraging recantation serves justice better than rigid punishment.

### Materiality

The United States is distinctive in requiring that a false statement be "material" -- capable of influencing the proceeding -- to constitute perjury. Neither South Korea nor Germany imposes an explicit materiality requirement; any false sworn statement, regardless of its significance to the outcome, can support a perjury charge. This difference reflects contrasting philosophies: the U.S. approach treats perjury primarily as a crime against the integrity of specific proceedings, while the Korean and German approaches treat it as a crime against the oath itself.

### Enforcement Disparity

South Korea stands out for the sheer volume of perjury prosecutions. With approximately 1,500 to 2,000 perjury indictments annually in a country of 52 million people, South Korea's per capita perjury prosecution rate is estimated at 420 times that of Japan. Commentators attribute this to cultural factors and a legal system in which witness testimony plays a disproportionate role in case outcomes. By contrast, perjury prosecutions in the United States and Germany are relatively rare, often pursued only in high-profile cases or when the false testimony causes demonstrable harm.

### Defendant Protections

All four systems recognize, in varying forms, that defendants occupy a special position with respect to perjury. In Germany, defendants simply cannot be sworn witnesses and thus cannot commit perjury. In South Korea, Supreme Court precedent holds that a defendant's status shields them from perjury liability even when they testify as a witness in a co-defendant's case. In the United States, the Fifth Amendment protects against compelled self-incrimination, but a defendant who voluntarily takes the stand and lies is subject to both perjury prosecution and sentence enhancement. The ECHR reinforces these protections through the presumption of innocence (Article 6(2)) and the privilege against self-incrimination.
