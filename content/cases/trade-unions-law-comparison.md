## Summary

Trade union law -- the legal framework governing the formation, operation, rights, and obligations of workers' organizations and their collective bargaining activities -- reflects fundamental choices about the balance of power between labor and capital in each legal system. South Korea guarantees the right to organize, bargain collectively, and act collectively through Article 33 of the Constitution (헌법 제33조), implemented through the Trade Union and Labor Relations Adjustment Act (노동조합 및 노동관계조정법, TULRAA), which was substantially amended in 2021 to permit multiple unions at the enterprise level (복수노조), ending a decades-long single-union system, and again in 2023 to ratify ILO Conventions Nos. 87 and 98 after 30 years of international pressure; South Korea's union density rate stands at approximately 14.2 percent as of 2023, with the Korean Confederation of Trade Unions (전국민주노동조합총연합, KCTU) and the Federation of Korean Trade Unions (한국노동조합총연합, FKTU) serving as the two major national confederations. The United States protects the right to organize under the National Labor Relations Act (NLRA) of 1935 (29 U.S.C. Section 151 et seq.), administered by the National Labor Relations Board (NLRB), but union density has declined from approximately 35 percent in the mid-1950s to 10 percent in 2023 (6 percent in the private sector), with the Taft-Hartley Act of 1947 permitting state "right-to-work" laws that prohibit union security agreements. Germany's trade union rights are protected by Article 9(3) of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz), which guarantees Koalitionsfreiheit (freedom of association), with collective bargaining governed by the Tarifvertragsgesetz (TVG) of 1949 and workplace-level representation provided through works councils (Betriebsräte) under the Betriebsverfassungsgesetz (BetrVG) of 1972, operating within a dual-channel system that separates sectoral collective bargaining from enterprise-level co-determination; German union density is approximately 16.3 percent, though collective agreement coverage reaches approximately 49 percent due to the extension mechanism. At the European level, Article 11 of the ECHR protects the right to form and join trade unions, the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights guarantees collective bargaining rights under Article 28, and the CJEU has addressed the relationship between collective action and economic freedoms in landmark cases including Viking (C-438/05) and Laval (C-341/05).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Trade union rights in South Korea derive from Article 33(1) of the Constitution: "Workers shall have the right to independent association, collective bargaining, and collective action." These rights are given statutory form through the Trade Union and Labor Relations Adjustment Act (노동조합 및 노동관계조정법, TULRAA), originally enacted in 1997 and extensively amended.

### Historical Context

South Korea's labor movement has been shaped by the country's authoritarian past and democratic transition. Under the Park Chung-hee and Chun Doo-hwan military governments (1961-1987), independent unions were severely repressed. The Great Workers' Struggle (노동자 대투쟁) of July-September 1987, which saw over 3,000 labor disputes, was a watershed moment coinciding with democratization. The KCTU was founded in 1995 but was not legally recognized until 1999.

### Key Reforms

**Multiple Union System (복수노조, 2011):** Prior to July 2011, only one union could be established per enterprise under the single-union system. The amendment permitted multiple unions, with a "bargaining representative" system (교섭대표노동조합) modeled partly on the U.S. exclusive representation concept. The majority union or a joint bargaining committee represents all workers in negotiations.

**ILO Convention Ratification (2022-2023):** After three decades of international pressure, South Korea ratified ILO Convention No. 87 (Freedom of Association) and Convention No. 98 (Right to Organise and Collective Bargaining) on April 20, 2022, effective April 20, 2023. Legislative amendments to bring domestic law into compliance included:
- Permitting dismissed workers and unemployed persons to join or maintain union membership
- Allowing foreign workers with irregular immigration status to join unions
- Reducing the scope of "essential services" where strikes may be restricted

### Restrictions on Public Sector Workers

Article 33(2) of the Constitution restricts collective action rights for government officials: "Only those public officials who are designated by Act shall have the right to association, collective bargaining and collective action." Teachers gained the right to unionize in 1999, but civil servants' union rights remain limited under the Act on the Establishment and Operation of Public Officials' Trade Unions (공무원의 노동조합 설립 및 운영 등에 관한 법률, 2005). Military personnel, police, and firefighters are excluded from union rights.

### Unfair Labor Practices

Articles 81-86 of TULRAA define employer unfair labor practices including dismissal or discrimination against union members, refusal to bargain in good faith, interference with union administration, and "yellow dog" contracts. The National Labor Relations Commission adjudicates unfair labor practice complaints.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The National Labor Relations Act (NLRA or Wagner Act), enacted on July 5, 1935, established the right of private-sector employees to organize, form unions, and bargain collectively. The Act created the National Labor Relations Board (NLRB) as an independent federal agency to administer representation elections and adjudicate unfair labor practice charges.

### Key Amendments

**Taft-Hartley Act (1947):** Significantly amended the NLRA by adding unfair labor practices for unions (secondary boycotts, jurisdictional strikes), permitting states to enact "right-to-work" laws prohibiting union security agreements (Section 14(b)), and requiring union officers to sign non-communist affidavits (later repealed). The Act also created the Federal Mediation and Conciliation Service.

**Landrum-Griffin Act (1959):** Established a "Bill of Rights" for union members, requiring democratic union governance, financial transparency, and reporting to the Department of Labor.

### Exclusive Representation

The U.S. system is characterized by exclusive representation: once a union is certified as the bargaining representative (through NLRB election or voluntary recognition), it represents all employees in the bargaining unit, including non-members. This differs from the European model where multiple unions may coexist and bargain separately.

### Right-to-Work Laws

Section 14(b) of the Taft-Hartley Act permits states to prohibit union security agreements (clauses requiring employees to pay union dues as a condition of employment). As of 2025, approximately 27 states have enacted right-to-work laws. The Supreme Court's decision in Janus v. AFSCME, 585 U.S. 878 (2018), extended the right-to-work principle to the public sector nationwide, holding that mandatory agency fees violate the First Amendment.

### Recent Developments

A resurgence of union organizing activity has occurred since 2021, including successful campaigns at Amazon (JFK8 warehouse in Staten Island, April 2022), Starbucks (over 400 stores filing for elections since 2021), and Apple (Towson, Maryland, June 2022). The NLRB under the Biden administration issued the Cemex decision in 2023, establishing that employers who commit unfair labor practices during an organizing campaign may be ordered to bargain with the union without an election.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's labor relations system is characterized by its dual-channel structure, separating sectoral collective bargaining from enterprise-level co-determination:

**Sectoral Level:** Trade unions negotiate binding collective agreements (Tarifverträge) with employer associations under the Tarifvertragsgesetz (TVG) of 1949. These agreements cover wages, working hours, and general employment conditions for entire industries and regions (Flächentarifverträge).

**Enterprise Level:** Works councils (Betriebsräte), elected by all employees regardless of union membership, exercise co-determination rights under the Betriebsverfassungsgesetz (BetrVG) of 1972. Works councils are legally distinct from unions and may not call strikes. In companies with more than 2,000 employees, board-level co-determination under the Mitbestimmungsgesetz (MitbestG) of 1976 requires that half of supervisory board (Aufsichtsrat) seats be allocated to employee representatives.

### Constitutional Protection

Article 9(3) of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz) provides: "The right to form associations to safeguard and improve working and economic conditions shall be guaranteed to every individual and to every occupation or profession. Agreements which restrict or seek to impair this right shall be null and void; measures directed to this end shall be unlawful."

The Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) has interpreted this provision expansively in several landmark decisions:

- **BVerfGE 84, 212 (1991):** Confirmed that Koalitionsfreiheit protects both the positive right to join a union and the negative right not to join.
- **BVerfGE 146, 71 (2017):** Upheld the Tarifeinheitsgesetz (2015) establishing the "one collective agreement per establishment" principle, rejecting claims that it violated small unions' constitutional rights.

### Tarifeinheitsgesetz (2015)

The Tarifeinheitsgesetz (Collective Agreement Unity Act), enacted on July 10, 2015, addresses the problem of competing collective agreements in a single establishment by establishing that where multiple unions have members in the same enterprise and conclude separate collective agreements, the agreement of the union with the most members in the establishment prevails for overlapping matters. The law was a response to the growing influence of occupational unions like GDL (train drivers) and Vereinigung Cockpit (pilots), whose strikes caused significant public disruption. The Bundesverfassungsgericht upheld the law in 2017 with the caveat that minority unions' core collective bargaining rights must be preserved.

### Allgemeinverbindlicherklärung (Extension Mechanism)

Under Section 5 TVG, the Federal Ministry of Labor and Social Affairs may declare a collective agreement generally binding (allgemeinverbindlich) for an entire industry, extending its terms to non-unionized employers and employees. This mechanism, combined with the Arbeitnehmer-Entsendegesetz (Posted Workers Act), explains why collective agreement coverage (~49 percent) significantly exceeds union density (~16.3 percent).

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Article 11

Article 11 of the European Convention on Human Rights protects the right to freedom of peaceful assembly and freedom of association, including "the right to form and to join trade unions for the protection of his interests."

**Demir and Baykara v. Turkey (Application No. 34503/97, Grand Chamber Judgment of November 12, 2008):** The Grand Chamber held that Article 11 encompasses the right of trade unions to engage in collective bargaining. This landmark judgment reversed earlier case law that had treated collective bargaining as outside Article 11's scope.

**Enerji Yapı-Yol Sen v. Turkey (Application No. 68959/01, Judgment of April 21, 2009):** The Court found that a blanket ban on public servants participating in a one-day national strike violated Article 11, establishing that the right to strike is protected under the Convention.

**National Union of Rail, Maritime and Transport Workers (RMT) v. United Kingdom (Application No. 31045/10, Judgment of April 8, 2014):** The Court found no violation of Article 11 in the UK's statutory ban on secondary strikes, holding that the restriction fell within the state's margin of appreciation.

### EU Charter and Treaty Provisions

Article 28 of the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights provides: "Workers and employers, or their respective organisations, have, in accordance with Union law and national laws and practices, the right to negotiate and conclude collective agreements at the appropriate levels and, in cases of conflicts of interest, to take collective action to defend their interests, including strike action."

### Viking and Laval Cases

**International Transport Workers' Federation v. Viking Line (Case C-438/05, CJEU, December 11, 2007):** The Court held that the right to take collective action, including the right to strike, is a fundamental right under EU law but must be balanced against the freedom of establishment (Article 49 TFEU). Collective action restricting free movement must be proportionate to the objective pursued.

**Laval un Partneri v. Svenska Byggnadsarbetareförbundet (Case C-341/05, CJEU, December 18, 2007):** The Court held that Swedish unions' blockade of a Latvian construction company to force it to sign a Swedish collective agreement violated the free provision of services (Article 56 TFEU), as Swedish law did not set minimum terms that could be required of posted workers.

These decisions generated significant controversy, with critics arguing they subordinated fundamental labor rights to economic freedoms. The Posted Workers Directive was subsequently revised in 2018 (Directive 2018/957) to strengthen the "equal pay for equal work in the same place" principle.

### EU Directive on Adequate Minimum Wages (2022/2041)

Article 4 of the Directive requires member states where collective bargaining coverage falls below 80 percent to establish an action plan with measures to promote collective bargaining. This provision reflects EU-level recognition of the declining coverage of collective agreements as a policy problem.

## Comparative Analysis

### Bargaining Structure

The most fundamental structural difference is between enterprise-level bargaining (United States, South Korea) and sectoral bargaining (Germany). Enterprise-level bargaining requires organizing each workplace individually, contributing to lower coverage rates. Sectoral bargaining establishes industry-wide standards that cover even non-unionized workplaces through extension mechanisms. The EU Minimum Wage Directive's 80 percent coverage target implicitly favors the sectoral model.

### Union Security

The jurisdictions span the spectrum from closed-shop models to right-to-work systems. Germany's system does not require union membership but achieves broad coverage through sectoral agreements and extension. South Korea moved from a single-union enterprise system to multiple unions with a bargaining representative model. The United States permits but does not require union security, with 27 states prohibiting it entirely through right-to-work laws, and Janus extending this to the public sector.

### Public Sector

South Korea maintains the most restrictive approach to public sector unionization, with military, police, and firefighters excluded and civil servants' rights limited. The United States varies by state, with most states permitting public sector bargaining but some prohibiting strikes. Germany extends full collective bargaining and co-determination rights to the public sector, though civil servants (Beamte) have a separate regime under the Beamtenstatusgesetz.
