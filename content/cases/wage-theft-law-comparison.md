## Summary

Wage theft -- the illegal practice of employers failing to pay workers their legally owed wages, including minimum wage violations, overtime non-payment, off-the-clock work, tip theft, misclassification, and final paycheck withholding -- constitutes the largest category of theft by dollar value in many jurisdictions, yet frequently receives weaker enforcement and penalties than comparably valued property crimes. South Korea addresses wage theft (임금 체불) through Articles 36 and 43 of the Labor Standards Act (근로기준법), which require payment of all wages on a fixed, regular date and impose criminal penalties of up to three years' imprisonment or 30 million KRW in fines for non-payment; despite these ostensibly strong provisions, wage theft remains endemic, with the Ministry of Employment and Labor reporting approximately 1.58 trillion KRW ($1.2 billion) in unpaid wages affecting approximately 299,000 workers in 2023, driven primarily by chronic non-payment by small and medium enterprises and the construction industry's multi-layered subcontracting system. The United States addresses wage theft through the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) at the federal level, providing for back wages plus an equal amount in liquidated damages, with willful violations carrying criminal penalties of up to $10,000 and six months' imprisonment; the DOL's Wage and Hour Division recovered $274 million in back wages for approximately 163,000 workers in fiscal year 2023, though the Economic Policy Institute estimates that workers lose over $50 billion annually to wage theft, making it the nation's largest form of theft by a wide margin. Germany addresses wage non-payment through civil law remedies under the Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB), the general criminal fraud and embezzlement provisions of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), and the Schwarzarbeitsbekämpfungsgesetz (Act to Combat Undeclared Work), with the Mindestlohngesetz (MiLoG) providing specific enforcement mechanisms for minimum wage violations through the Finanzkontrolle Schwarzarbeit (FKS, customs authority); the Arbeitnehmer-Entsendegesetz (Posted Workers Act) specifically targets wage theft in the context of cross-border labor mobility. At the European level, the EU Posted Workers Directive (Directive 96/71/EC, revised by Directive 2018/957) addresses wage theft in cross-border employment contexts, the European Labour Authority (ELA) established in 2019 facilitates enforcement cooperation, and the European Pillar of Social Rights Principle 6 guarantees fair wages.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's approach to wage theft is distinctive in its reliance on criminal sanctions as the primary enforcement mechanism, reflecting the historical severity of wage non-payment in the Korean economy.

### Labor Standards Act Provisions

**Article 36 -- Payment of Wages on Termination:** All wages, compensation, and other money owed must be paid within 14 days of the date on which the cause for payment arose (termination, death, or other qualifying event). Extension is possible by agreement only under extraordinary circumstances.

**Article 43 -- Principles of Wage Payment:** Wages must be paid (1) in legal tender (통화), (2) directly to the worker, (3) in full, and (4) at least once per month on a fixed date. Deductions are permitted only when authorized by law or collective agreement.

**Article 109 -- Criminal Penalty:** Violation of wage payment obligations is punishable by imprisonment for up to three years or a fine of up to 30 million KRW.

### Wage Claim Guarantee Fund (임금채권보장기금)

Established under the Wage Claim Guarantee Act (임금채권보장법) of 1998, this fund guarantees payment of unpaid wages when an employer becomes insolvent. The fund is financed by employer contributions (0.06 percent of total payroll) and covers up to three months of unpaid wages, three months of unpaid severance, and three months of unpaid leave allowance. In 2023, the fund paid approximately 635 billion KRW to approximately 68,000 workers.

### Construction Industry Issues

The construction sector accounts for a disproportionate share of wage theft in South Korea due to its multi-layered subcontracting system (다단계 하도급). Workers employed by third- or fourth-tier subcontractors often cannot recover wages when their direct employer becomes insolvent. The 2020 amendment to the Construction Industry Framework Act (건설산업기본법) strengthened the liability of principal contractors for subcontractor wage obligations, establishing a "joint payment guarantee" (공동지급보증) system.

### Criminal Enforcement

Despite the availability of criminal sanctions, actual imprisonment for wage theft remains relatively rare. In 2023, approximately 23,000 criminal complaints for wage non-payment were filed with prosecutors, but fewer than 3 percent resulted in imprisonment. Most cases were resolved through payment of arrears, suspended prosecution (기소유예), or fines. Critics argue that the low actual imprisonment rate undermines deterrence.

### Delayed Payment Interest

Article 37 of the Labor Standards Act provides that when an employer fails to pay wages within 14 days of separation, delayed payment interest accrues at an annual rate of 20 percent, creating a strong financial incentive for timely payment. This rate is significantly higher than general civil law interest rates.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Wage theft in the United States is addressed through federal law (FLSA), state wage payment laws, and in some jurisdictions, criminal law.

### FLSA Remedies

| Remedy | Amount |
|--------|--------|
| Back wages | Full amount of wages owed |
| Liquidated damages | Equal to back wages (effectively doubling recovery) unless employer proves good faith |
| Attorney's fees and costs | Reasonable attorney's fees and costs |
| Criminal penalty (willful) | Up to $10,000 fine and/or 6 months imprisonment |

### Scale of Wage Theft

The Economic Policy Institute (EPI) estimated in a 2017 study that employers steal approximately $50 billion annually from workers through minimum wage violations alone, exceeding the combined losses from all robberies, burglaries, larcenies, and motor vehicle thefts reported to the FBI ($14.3 billion in the same period). When overtime violations, off-the-clock work, meal break violations, and tip theft are included, total losses are estimated to be significantly higher.

### DOL Enforcement

The Wage and Hour Division recovered $274 million in back wages for approximately 163,000 workers in fiscal year 2023. The most common violations involved overtime (approximately 42 percent), minimum wage (approximately 18 percent), and child labor (approximately 12 percent). Key enforcement actions included food processing, agriculture, healthcare, and construction sectors.

### State Laws

Many states provide stronger protections than the FLSA:

| State | Key Provision |
|-------|--------------|
| California | Treble damages for willful wage theft (Lab. Code § 1194.2); criminal penalties up to felony level |
| New York | WTPA (2010): treble damages; 6 years statute of limitations |
| Colorado | CWCA (2019): criminal wage theft at felony level |
| Minnesota | Criminal wage theft statute with felony penalties for amounts over $1,000 |
| Washington | Criminal wage theft; mandatory liquidated damages |

### Wage Theft Prevention Acts

Since 2010, numerous states and localities have enacted Wage Theft Prevention Acts requiring employers to provide written wage notices at hiring, specifying rate of pay, pay frequency, and employer identity. These disclosure requirements address the information asymmetry that enables wage theft.

### Criminal Prosecution Trend

A growing number of jurisdictions have classified wage theft as a criminal offense equivalent to other forms of theft. Colorado's 2019 CWCA makes wage theft a felony when the amount exceeds $2,000, with penalties matching those for equivalent property theft. District attorneys in several major cities (Manhattan, Los Angeles, San Francisco, Philadelphia) have established dedicated wage theft prosecution units.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany addresses wage non-payment through a combination of civil law, criminal law, and specialized enforcement mechanisms, with particular emphasis on combating undeclared work (Schwarzarbeit) and enforcing minimum wage compliance.

### Civil Law Remedies

**BGB § 611a -- Employment Contract:** The employment contract obligates the employer to pay agreed wages for services rendered. Non-payment constitutes breach of contract, entitling the employee to sue for performance (Erfüllungsklage) in the Arbeitsgericht.

**Default Interest (BGB § 288):** When wages are overdue, default interest accrues at 5 percentage points above the base rate (Basiszinssatz). For commercial transactions, the rate is 9 percentage points above the base rate.

**Retention Right (BGB § 273):** Workers may exercise a right of retention (Zurückbehaltungsrecht), refusing to work until overdue wages are paid, without this constituting a breach of the employment contract.

### Criminal Provisions

While Germany does not have a specific wage theft criminal statute, general criminal provisions apply:

| StGB Section | Offense | Application to Wage Theft |
|-------------|---------|--------------------------|
| § 263 (Betrug/Fraud) | Obtaining advantage through deception | Employer who deceptively fails to pay wages |
| § 266 (Untreue/Breach of trust) | Misuse of authority to dispose of another's assets | Manager who diverts wage funds |
| § 266a (Vorenthalten und Veruntreuen von Arbeitsentgelt) | Withholding and misappropriating employee remuneration | Specifically criminalizes failure to pay social insurance contributions; up to 5 years imprisonment |

Section 266a StGB is particularly significant: it specifically criminalizes the failure to pay employee social insurance contributions (Arbeitnehmeranteile zur Sozialversicherung) to the relevant social insurance carriers. Prosecutors treat this offense seriously because it simultaneously defrauds workers and the social insurance system.

### Minimum Wage Enforcement

The Finanzkontrolle Schwarzarbeit (FKS), a specialized division of the customs authority with approximately 7,600 staff, enforces minimum wage compliance under the MiLoG. The FKS conducts unannounced workplace inspections, reviews wage records, and may impose administrative fines of up to EUR 500,000 for minimum wage violations. In 2023, the FKS completed approximately 48,000 employer inspections and initiated approximately 5,100 criminal proceedings and 13,400 administrative fine proceedings.

### Insolvenzgeld (Insolvency Wage Guarantee)

Sections 165-172 SGB III provide that when an employer becomes insolvent, the Bundesagentur für Arbeit pays affected workers their net wages for the three months preceding the insolvency event (Insolvenzgeld). This system is funded by employer contributions (currently approximately 0.06 percent of payroll). In 2023, approximately 49,000 workers received Insolvenzgeld totaling approximately EUR 510 million.

### Posted Workers Act (Arbeitnehmer-Entsendegesetz)

The AEntG addresses wage theft in cross-border contexts by requiring that foreign employers posting workers to Germany comply with German minimum employment conditions, including minimum wage and sector-specific collectively agreed minimum wages. The principal contractor bears joint and several liability (Haftungsdurchgriff) for the wage obligations of subcontractors in the chain.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Posted Workers Directive (Directive 2018/957)

The 2018 revision of the Posted Workers Directive strengthened equal treatment protections, requiring posted workers to receive the same remuneration (not merely the minimum rate of pay) as local workers for the same work at the same place. This principle of "equal pay for equal work in the same place" directly addresses the wage undercutting that frequently accompanied cross-border posting.

### European Labour Authority (ELA)

Established by Regulation (EU) 2019/1149 and operational since 2019, the ELA facilitates cooperation between national labor authorities on cross-border enforcement, coordinates joint inspections (conducting 58 joint inspections in 2023), mediates disputes between national authorities, and supports the fight against undeclared work and wage fraud.

### Insolvency Protection Directive (2008/94/EC)

Directive 2008/94/EC requires member states to establish guarantee institutions that ensure payment of employees' outstanding claims in the event of employer insolvency. Member states may set temporal limitations (covering at least three months of claims) and financial ceilings. This Directive underpins the German Insolvenzgeld and South Korean Wage Claim Guarantee Fund models.

### European Pillar of Social Rights

Principle 6 provides: "Workers have the right to fair wages that provide for a decent standard of living." Principle 6 further states that "adequate minimum wages shall be ensured, in a way that provides for the satisfaction of the needs of the worker and his/her family."

### ECHR

The ECHR has addressed unpaid wages through Article 1 of Protocol 1 (protection of property) and Article 6 (right to a fair trial). In Sudzhashvili v. Georgia (Application No. 12876/07, 2019), the Court found a violation of Article 1 Protocol 1 where Georgian authorities failed to enforce a final court judgment ordering payment of salary arrears, holding that the state's failure to ensure execution of an enforceable judgment amounted to unjustified interference with the applicant's possessions.

## Comparative Analysis

### Criminal vs. Civil Enforcement

South Korea relies most heavily on criminal sanctions as a deterrent against wage theft, though actual imprisonment remains rare. Germany criminalizes specific aspects (particularly social insurance contribution non-payment under § 266a StGB) while relying primarily on civil enforcement for wage claims. The United States provides only misdemeanor-level federal criminal penalties but has seen a growing trend toward state-level felony classification.

### Scale and Impact

All three jurisdictions face significant wage theft challenges. The absolute scale is largest in the United States ($50 billion+ annually), while South Korea's problem is most concentrated (1.58 trillion KRW affecting 299,000 workers in a smaller economy). Germany's problem is less precisely quantified but is concentrated in sectors with high rates of undeclared work and posted worker exploitation.

### Wage Guarantee Funds

South Korea and Germany both maintain robust wage guarantee funds for insolvency situations, mandated by and exceeding the requirements of ILO Convention No. 173 (Protection of Workers' Claims in the event of Employer's Insolvency) and EU Directive 2008/94/EC respectively. The United States lacks a comparable federal mechanism.

### Subcontracting Liability

All three jurisdictions have recognized that multi-layered subcontracting enables wage theft by distancing the principal beneficiary from the wage obligation. South Korea's 2020 construction industry reform, Germany's AEntG joint and several liability, and California's Labor Code Section 2810.3 (joint employer liability for client employers using labor contractors) address this through ascending liability chains.
