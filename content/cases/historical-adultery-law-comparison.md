## Summary

Adultery -- sexual intercourse between a married person and someone other than their spouse -- has been condemned, regulated, and punished across virtually all known legal systems throughout human history, though the specific definitions, scope of liability, and severity of punishment have varied enormously depending on each civilization's conceptions of marriage, gender, property, honor, and religious morality. Ancient Near Eastern law codes, including the Code of Hammurabi (c. 1754 BCE) and the Middle Assyrian Laws (c. 1076 BCE), treated adultery primarily as a property offense against the husband, punishing the wife and her paramour with death while granting the husband considerable power to pardon or punish at his discretion. Roman law underwent a dramatic transformation from the Republic, when adultery was a private matter addressed within the family, to the Empire, when Augustus's lex Julia de adulteriis coercendis (18 BCE) made it a public crime prosecuted by the state, prescribing relegation (exile) and confiscation of property. East Asian legal traditions, rooted in Confucian family ethics, developed elaborate systems governing marital fidelity: the Tang Code (唐律疏議, 653 CE) punished adultery with penal servitude and provided the "Seven Grounds for Divorce" (七出, qichu), while Korean Joseon law incorporated these principles with distinctive local adaptations through the Daemyeongnyul and Gyeongguk Daejeon. Islamic law classified adultery (zina) as a hadd offense with the Quranic penalty of one hundred lashes for unmarried offenders and, through Hadith authority, stoning (rajm) for married offenders, but surrounded these penalties with extraordinarily stringent evidentiary requirements including four male eyewitnesses to the act of penetration. Germanic and medieval European law treated adultery through a complex mix of secular penalty and ecclesiastical jurisdiction, with the Church claiming authority over marital offenses while secular courts retained jurisdiction over accompanying violence. English common law, uniquely among European systems, never treated adultery as a crime at common law, relegating it to the ecclesiastical courts, though adultery served as a partial defense to homicide (provocation) and as the primary ground for divorce from the time of the Matrimonial Causes Act 1857.

## Ancient Near East

### Mesopotamian Adultery Law

Mesopotamian legal codes consistently treated adultery as an offense committed by the wife against her husband's exclusive sexual rights, while the husband's extramarital relations with unmarried women were generally unregulated.

**Code of Hammurabi (c. 1754 BCE):**

- **Law 129:** "If the wife of a man is caught lying with another man, they shall bind them and cast them into the water. If the master of the wife wishes to spare his wife, then the king shall spare his servant (the paramour)." This foundational provision established several principles: adultery was punishable by death (drowning); both parties were equally liable; but the husband had the power to pardon his wife, and if he did so, the paramour was also pardoned (the king acting on behalf of the state paralleling the husband's private mercy).
- **Law 130:** "If a man has violated the wife of another who has not yet known a man (a virgin bride still in her father's house), and they catch him lying in her bosom, that man shall be put to death; that woman shall go free." This addressed rape of a betrothed woman, distinguishing it from consensual adultery.
- **Law 131:** "If a man's wife has been accused by her husband but has not been caught lying with another man, she shall swear by the god and return to her house." This provision addressed accusation without evidence, requiring the accused wife to undergo a divine oath to establish her innocence.
- **Law 132:** "If a finger has been pointed at the wife of a man because of another man, but she has not been caught lying with another man, she shall throw herself into the river for her husband." When a wife was accused by public rumor (rather than by her husband), she underwent the river ordeal -- a trial by water in which the river deity rendered judgment.
- **Law 133:** "If a man has been taken captive and there is no food in his house, and his wife enters another man's house, that woman bears no blame." This remarkable provision excused a wife's adultery when her husband was a prisoner of war and she had no means of support -- an early recognition of necessity as a defense.

**Middle Assyrian Laws (c. 1076 BCE):** These laws were significantly harsher than the Hammurabi Code regarding adultery:

- **Tablet A, Law 13:** If a wife goes to a man's house and he has intercourse with her knowing she is married, both the man and the woman shall be put to death.
- **Tablet A, Law 15:** If a man catches another man with his wife, and the cuckolded husband kills both, he bears no guilt. If he brings them before the king or judges, and proves the case, the king may punish both as the husband sees fit -- if the husband puts his wife to death, he must also put the man to death; if he cuts off his wife's nose, he must castrate the man and have his face mutilated. This law gave the husband the power to determine the punishment, but required proportionality between the penalties imposed on the two offenders.
- **Tablet A, Law 16:** Consent of the woman was irrelevant -- if a man had intercourse with another man's wife, even with her consent, he was liable as if it were against her will.

### Egyptian Adultery Law

Egyptian sources suggest that adultery was condemned but the legal response was less systematically codified than in Mesopotamia:

- **The Westcar Papyrus (c. 1600 BCE):** Contains a tale in which a wife's adultery is discovered through magic, and the adulterous wife is burned and her lover eaten by a crocodile -- reflecting the narrative expectation of severe punishment.
- **Papyrus D'Orbiney (Tale of Two Brothers, c. 1185 BCE):** Features a wife who falsely accuses her brother-in-law of attempted seduction; when the truth is revealed, she is killed and thrown to the dogs.
- **Diodorus Siculus (1st century BCE):** Reported that under Egyptian law, the adulterous wife received one thousand blows with a rod, and the adulterous man had his genitals cut off.

## Roman Law

### From Private Affair to Public Crime

Roman adultery law underwent a revolutionary transformation under Augustus, shifting from family-based self-help to state prosecution.

**Pre-Augustan Period:**

Under the Republic, adultery was treated as a private matter within the jurisdiction of the paterfamilias and the family council (consilium domesticum). The paterfamilias of the adulterous wife could punish her, including by death, if she was caught in the act. There was no public prosecution for adultery. Cato the Elder (Aulus Gellius, Noctes Atticae 10.23) articulated the double standard: "If you were to catch your wife in adultery, you could kill her with impunity without a trial; but if she were to catch you, she would not dare to lay a finger on you, nor would it be right."

The ius occidendi (right to kill) was exercised within the family, typically by the wife's father rather than the husband, reflecting the Roman patriarchal structure in which the daughter remained under her father's potestas.

**Lex Julia de Adulteriis Coercendis (18 BCE):**

Augustus's law fundamentally transformed adultery from a private wrong into a public crime (crimen publicum), part of his broader moral legislation aimed at reversing perceived Roman decadence.

Key provisions (reconstructed from Digest 48.5 and Codex 9.9):

- **Definition:** Adulterium was sexual intercourse with a married woman (matrona). Stuprum was sexual intercourse with an unmarried woman of respectable status (virgo or vidua honesta). The law covered both, but the term adulterium applied specifically to the married woman's offense.
- **Mandatory Prosecution:** The husband who knew of his wife's adultery was required to divorce her and bring charges within sixty days. If he failed to do so, he himself became liable for the crime of lenocinium (pimping/procuring). After the husband's sixty-day window, any member of the public could prosecute within a further four months.
- **Ius Occidendi Restricted:** The law narrowly restricted the right to kill: the wife's father could kill both the daughter and the paramour if caught in the act in his own home or the husband's home, and if the father killed both (not just one). The husband could kill only a paramour of low social status (a slave, freedman, gladiator, actor, or convicted criminal) caught in the act in the husband's home; he could never lawfully kill his wife.
- **Penalties:** The adulterous wife was subject to relegation (a milder form of exile to a specific location) and forfeiture of one-third of her property and one-half of her dowry. The paramour was subject to relegation and forfeiture of one-half of his property. Both were prohibited from remarrying a person of respectable status.
- **Evidence:** The law prohibited the torture of slaves to obtain evidence against their masters in adultery cases (an exception to the general imperial practice), though this prohibition was later relaxed.

**Post-Augustan Developments:**

- **Constantine (C. 9.9.29, 326 CE):** Dramatically increased penalties, prescribing death by the sword for adultery. Constantine also restricted the right to accuse: only close relatives (the husband, father, brother, paternal uncle, or cousin) could bring charges, eliminating the Republican provision for public prosecution.
- **Justinian (Novel 134.10, 556 CE):** Provided that an adulterous wife could be sent to a convent; if the husband did not reclaim her within two years, she would take the veil permanently. This Christianized penalty reflected the growing influence of Christian sexual morality on Roman law.

## East Asian Law

### Confucian Family Ethics and Adultery

East Asian adultery law was embedded in the broader Confucian framework of family relationships, filial piety, and social harmony.

**Tang Code (唐律疏議, 653 CE):**

- **Article 410 (和姦, Consensual Illicit Intercourse):** Consensual sexual intercourse between unmarried persons was punished by one and a half years of penal servitude. If the woman was married, the penalty increased to two years of penal servitude for both parties. The husband's consent was not a defense for the wife's paramour.
- **Article 411 (強姦, Rape):** Rape was punished by two and a half years of penal servitude; rape of a married woman by death.
- **Relational Aggravation:** Consistent with the Confucian emphasis on relational hierarchy, adultery with a relative's wife was punished more severely. Sexual intercourse with one's father's or grandfather's concubine was punished by strangulation -- classified as one of the Ten Abominations (內亂, incest/internal disorder).

**The Seven Grounds for Divorce (七出, Qichu):**

The Tang Code formalized the Confucian seven grounds on which a husband could divorce his wife:
1. Failure to serve parents-in-law (不事舅姑)
2. Failure to bear a son (無子)
3. Licentiousness/adultery (淫佚)
4. Jealousy (嫉妒) -- particularly jealousy that prevented the husband from taking concubines
5. Serious illness (惡疾)
6. Excessive talking/gossiping (多言)
7. Theft (竊盗)

However, the code also provided "Three Conditions for Non-Divorce" (三不去, san buqu): the wife could not be divorced if she had mourned her parents-in-law for three years, if the family had become wealthy after the marriage, or if she had no family to return to.

### Korean Legal Tradition

**Joseon Dynasty (1392-1897):**

Joseon Korea adapted Chinese adultery law within its own social framework:

- **Daemyeongnyul Application:** Korean courts applied the Ming Code provisions on adultery, prescribing eighty blows with the heavy stick and penal servitude for consensual adultery.
- **Chilgeojiag (七去之惡, Seven Grounds for Divorce):** The Korean adaptation of the Chinese qichu, applied within the Joseon social system where yangban (aristocratic) families placed enormous emphasis on female chastity as a marker of family honor.
- **Yeollyeo (烈女, Virtuous Women) Ideology:** Joseon society elevated female chastity to an extreme degree, with the state awarding posthumous honors (旌門, jeongmun) to women who died rather than submit to sexual violation. This ideology, while not a legal provision per se, profoundly shaped the social context in which adultery law operated. The state erected memorial gates (旌閭門) for exemplary wives and widows.
- **Remarriage Prohibition:** The Gyeongguk Daejeon prohibited the sons and grandsons of women who remarried from entering the civil service examinations (科擧, gwageo). This provision, while not directly about adultery, reflected the intense Joseon concern with female sexual exclusivity and its intergenerational social consequences.
- **Catching in the Act (現場, hyeonjang):** Korean law recognized the husband's right to kill an adulterous wife and her paramour if caught in flagrante delicto. The Annals of the Joseon Dynasty (朝鮮王朝實錄) record multiple cases where this right was invoked.

## Islamic Law

### Zina as a Hadd Offense

Islamic law treats adultery (and more broadly, all extramarital sexual intercourse, termed zina) as one of the hudud offenses for which the Quran prescribes a fixed penalty.

**Quranic Provisions:**

- **Quran 24:2:** "The woman and the man guilty of zina -- flog each of them with a hundred stripes. Let not compassion move you in their case, in a matter prescribed by Allah." This verse prescribes one hundred lashes for zina.
- **Quran 24:4:** "Those who accuse chaste women and then do not produce four witnesses -- flog them with eighty stripes and do not accept their testimony ever after." This verse established the four-witness requirement and created the offense of qadhf (false accusation of zina), itself a hadd offense.
- **Quran 24:6-9:** The procedure of li'an (mutual oath-taking/imprecation) for a husband who accuses his wife of adultery without four witnesses: the husband swears four times that he is truthful and invokes God's curse on himself if lying; the wife then swears four times that he is lying and invokes God's wrath on herself if he is truthful. This procedure results in dissolution of the marriage without hadd punishment.

**The Rajm (Stoning) Controversy:**

The Quran prescribes only flogging (100 lashes) for zina. The penalty of stoning (rajm) for married offenders (muhsan/muhsana) derives from the Hadith tradition and is not found in the Quranic text. The majority of Sunni jurists accept the rajm penalty based on:

- The hadith of Ma'iz ibn Malik, who confessed to adultery and was stoned on the Prophet's order (Sahih Muslim 1691).
- The hadith of the Ghamidiyyah woman, who also confessed and was stoned after giving birth and weaning her child (Sahih Muslim 1695).
- The so-called "verse of stoning" (ayat al-rajm), which early authorities claimed was part of the Quran but was abrogated in recitation while its ruling remained in force (naskh al-tilawa duna al-hukm).

The Kharijite and some Mu'tazilite scholars rejected rajm, arguing that the Quran's silence was dispositive. Modern reformist scholars have revisited this debate.

**Evidentiary Requirements:**

The stringency of the evidentiary requirements for proving zina has led many scholars to argue that the hadd penalty was functionally almost impossible to impose through judicial testimony:

1. **Four Male Eyewitnesses:** Four adult Muslim men of good character must testify to having witnessed the act of penetration (specifically described as "like the kohl stick entering the kohl bottle" -- kal-mirwad fi al-mukuhla). Each witness must describe the same act of intercourse; discrepancies in testimony invalidate the case.
2. **No Circumstantial Evidence:** Pregnancy alone was not sufficient evidence of zina according to the majority of Sunni schools (though the Maliki school held that unexplained pregnancy in an unmarried woman created a presumption of zina). The Hanafi and Shafi'i schools required direct testimony or confession.
3. **Confession:** Confession was the more common basis for hadd punishment. The jurists required the confession to be repeated four times in four separate sessions, and the confessing party could retract at any time, even during the execution of the punishment.
4. **Shubuhat (Doubts):** Any doubt or ambiguity negated the hadd penalty. If the accused believed the sexual act was lawful (e.g., a mistaken belief that a marriage contract existed), the hadd was not imposed.

**Gender Symmetry:** Unlike most pre-modern legal systems, Islamic law formally applied the same penalties for zina to both men and women. The evidentiary requirements were equally stringent regardless of the offender's gender. However, practical enforcement often reflected patriarchal social norms.

## Medieval European Law

### Ecclesiastical and Secular Jurisdiction

Medieval European adultery law was characterized by the overlapping and sometimes competing jurisdictions of Church and secular courts.

**Canon Law:**

The medieval Church claimed exclusive jurisdiction over marriage and, consequently, over marital offenses including adultery. Key developments:

- **Decretum of Gratian (c. 1140):** Causa 32 collected patristic and conciliar authorities on adultery. Gratian articulated the principle that adultery was equally sinful for both sexes, drawing on Augustine and Jerome. However, the practical penalties imposed by Church courts were markedly lenient: public penance, fasting, pilgrimage, and almsgiving.
- **Decretals of Gregory IX (Liber Extra, 1234):** Title X.5.16 (De adulteriis et stupro) systematized the canonical treatment. Adultery was grounds for separation from bed and board (separatio a mensa et thoro) but not for dissolution of the marriage bond (vinculum matrimonii), which was indissoluble.
- **The Guilty Party Rule:** Canon law prohibited the adulterous spouse from remarrying after separation. The "innocent" spouse was also prohibited from remarrying during the other's lifetime, though this was a consequence of the indissolubility doctrine rather than a penalty for adultery.

**Secular Law:**

Secular courts retained jurisdiction over the violent aspects of adultery, particularly the husband's response:

- **Fuero Juzgo (Visigothic Code, 654 CE):** Title III, v permitted the husband who caught an adulterous wife in the act to kill both wife and paramour without penalty, provided he killed both and not just one.
- **Beaumanoir, Coutumes de Beauvaisis (c. 1283):** Recorded that in the Beauvaisis region, the penalties for adultery included public shaming rituals: the guilty parties were paraded naked through the streets, sometimes tied together.
- **The Constitutio Criminalis Carolina (1532):** Article 120 addressed adultery, prescribing corporal punishment and, for aggravated cases, death. However, enforcement was uneven, and by the 16th century, most jurisdictions had shifted toward fines and public penance.

### Punitive Spectacles

Medieval and early modern adultery punishments frequently involved public shaming rituals designed to reinforce communal sexual norms:

- **The Charivari (Rough Music):** Community-organized rituals of noise-making, mockery, and harassment directed at adulterous couples or cuckolded husbands, documented across Western Europe from the 14th century.
- **The Cucking Stool and Scold's Bridle:** While primarily associated with scolding wives rather than adultery per se, these instruments of public punishment reflected the broader gendered regulation of women's behavior.
- **Ducking:** In some jurisdictions, adulterous women were punished by public ducking in water.

## Common Law Origins

### Adultery in English Law

English common law was distinctive in never treating adultery as a secular crime, instead relegating it to ecclesiastical jurisdiction.

**Ecclesiastical Court Jurisdiction:**

The Church courts (consistory courts, archdeaconry courts) exercised jurisdiction over adultery through the medieval and early modern periods. Penalties were ecclesiastical: public penance (standing in a white sheet in church), excommunication, and fines (commutation of penance for payment). The Puritan revolution briefly changed this -- the Adultery Act 1650 under Cromwell's Commonwealth made adultery a capital offense, but this statute was largely unenforced and was repealed at the Restoration (1660).

**Adultery and Homicide -- The Provocation Doctrine:**

The most significant common law intersection with adultery was through the criminal defense of provocation. A husband who killed his wife's paramour (or his wife) upon discovering them in the act of adultery could plead provocation, reducing murder to voluntary manslaughter. Sir Michael Foster (Crown Cases, 1762) wrote: "The law condescends to human frailty...jealousy is the rage of a man." The provocation had to be sudden -- a premeditated killing after discovery was still murder.

**Criminal Conversation (Crim. Con.):**

English law developed the tort of criminal conversation -- a civil action by the husband against his wife's paramour for damages. This action, available from at least the 17th century, treated the wife's sexual fidelity as a property right of the husband. Damages could be substantial: in Howard v. Bingham (1697), Lord Bingham was ordered to pay £1,000 for criminal conversation with Lady Howard. The action was abolished by the Matrimonial Causes Act 1857.

**Divorce:**

Before 1857, divorce was obtainable only by private Act of Parliament, and adultery (specifically, the wife's adultery) was the necessary ground. The Matrimonial Causes Act 1857 transferred divorce jurisdiction from Parliament to the new Court for Divorce and Matrimonial Causes. The Act maintained an asymmetry: a husband could obtain divorce for his wife's adultery alone, but a wife had to prove adultery aggravated by cruelty, desertion, bigamy, incest, or other offenses. This asymmetry was not fully eliminated until the Matrimonial Causes Act 1923.

## Historical Significance and Modern Legacy

The history of adultery law reveals how legal systems encode cultural assumptions about gender, sexuality, marriage, and honor:

**The Gender Asymmetry:** Nearly all pre-modern legal systems defined adultery asymmetrically by gender, punishing the wife's infidelity far more severely than the husband's. Mesopotamian, Roman, Chinese, and European laws all reflected this double standard. Islamic law was formally gender-neutral, though enforcement patterns were not. The gradual equalization of adultery law represents one of the most significant developments in the history of gender and law.

**From Property to Morality to Privacy:** The conceptual basis for adultery law has shifted over time: from a property offense against the husband (Mesopotamia, early Rome), to a crime against public morality (Augustan Rome, Confucian China, Islamic law), to a religious sin (medieval canon law), to a matter of private morality beyond the law's reach (modern liberal democracies). The 2003 U.S. Supreme Court decision in Lawrence v. Texas (539 U.S. 558), while addressing sodomy rather than adultery, articulated the modern liberal principle that consensual sexual conduct between adults is a matter of constitutional privacy.

**Evidentiary Restrictions as Mercy:** Islamic law's extraordinarily stringent evidentiary requirements for zina have been interpreted by many scholars as a deliberate mechanism to prevent the imposition of the hadd penalty in practice, reflecting a theological preference for concealment (sitr) of sexual transgressions over public punishment.
