## Summary

Medical device regulation (의료기기 규제) -- the legal framework governing the design, manufacture, marketing authorization, post-market surveillance, and liability associated with instruments, apparatus, appliances, software, implants, and other articles intended for medical purposes -- has undergone dramatic transformation across all major jurisdictions in response to high-profile device failures, the emergence of artificial intelligence and machine learning-based devices, and the increasing complexity of modern medical technology. South Korea regulates medical devices primarily through the Medical Devices Act (의료기기법, Act No. 6909, 2003, extensively amended) as administered by the Ministry of Food and Drug Safety (식품의약품안전처, MFDS), which operates a four-class risk-based classification system, requires pre-market approval or notification depending on device class, and has established a dedicated regulatory pathway for AI-based medical devices through the 2020 guidance on Software as a Medical Device (SaMD). The United States regulates medical devices through the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (FDCA) as amended by the Medical Device Amendments of 1976, the Safe Medical Devices Act of 1990, and the 21st Century Cures Act (2016), administered by the FDA's Center for Devices and Radiological Health (CDRH), which operates a three-class risk-based system with premarket notification (510(k)), premarket approval (PMA), and De Novo pathways; the FDA has also developed the Predetermined Change Control Plan framework for AI/ML-enabled devices. Germany and the EU transitioned to a fundamentally new regulatory regime with the full application of the EU Medical Devices Regulation (MDR, Regulation (EU) 2017/745) on May 26, 2021, replacing the prior Medical Devices Directives (93/42/EEC and 90/385/EEC), establishing stricter clinical evidence requirements, enhanced post-market surveillance obligations, the Eudamed database for transparency, and a reformed notified body system; the transition has been marked by significant implementation challenges, prompting the EU to adopt transitional extensions through Regulation (EU) 2023/607. The ECtHR has addressed medical device safety through its Article 2 and Article 8 jurisprudence, particularly in cases arising from the PIP breast implant scandal.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea has developed a sophisticated medical device regulatory system that has positioned the country as a leading market for advanced medical technologies, including AI-based diagnostic devices.

**Medical Devices Act (의료기기법):** The primary statute governing medical device regulation in Korea. Originally enacted in 2003 and extensively amended, the Act establishes the legal framework for device classification, pre-market review, manufacturing standards, post-market surveillance, and import/export regulation. The Act is supplemented by numerous enforcement decrees and MFDS notifications that provide detailed technical requirements.

**Classification System:** Korea employs a four-class risk-based classification:

| Class | Risk Level | Examples | Pre-Market Requirement |
|-------|-----------|----------|----------------------|
| Class I | Lowest risk | Surgical instruments, bandages | Notification (신고) |
| Class II | Low-moderate risk | Powered wheelchairs, hearing aids | Technical review (기술문서심사) |
| Class III | Moderate-high risk | Infusion pumps, orthopedic implants | MFDS approval (허가) |
| Class IV | Highest risk | Cardiac pacemakers, coronary stents | MFDS approval with clinical data |

### MFDS Approval Process

The MFDS pre-market review process includes technical documentation review (design, manufacturing, performance data), biocompatibility evaluation (per ISO 10993 series), electrical safety assessment (per IEC 60601 series for electrical medical devices), software validation (per IEC 62304 for software lifecycle), and clinical evaluation (clinical trial data for Class III-IV devices; literature review may suffice for lower classes).

**Clinical Trials:** Clinical trials for medical devices require MFDS approval under the Medical Devices Act Article 10, Institutional Review Board (IRB) approval, and compliance with the Korean Good Clinical Practice for medical devices (K-GCP-MD). Korea participates in the IMDRF MDSAP (Medical Device Single Audit Program), and MFDS accepts data from multinational clinical trials conducted under recognized GCP standards.

### AI/ML Medical Devices

South Korea has emerged as a global leader in AI-based medical device regulation. The MFDS published the Guideline on Review and Approval of Artificial Intelligence and Big Data-Based Medical Devices in 2020, establishing a structured pathway for AI/ML-enabled devices.

**Key Features:**
- AI-based SaMD is classified based on the significance of information provided (informing clinical management, driving clinical management, treating/diagnosing) and the seriousness of the healthcare situation.
- Continuous learning algorithms require pre-defined change protocols and periodic re-evaluation.
- MFDS has approved over 200 AI-based medical devices as of 2025, including AI-powered diagnostic imaging analysis tools (chest X-ray, retinal imaging, pathology).

### Post-Market Surveillance

The MFDS operates a comprehensive post-market surveillance system including mandatory adverse event reporting (within 15 days for serious events; within 30 days for non-serious events), periodic safety update reporting for Class III-IV devices, post-market clinical follow-up requirements, and field safety corrective actions (recalls and safety alerts).

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States operates one of the world's most established medical device regulatory systems, with the FDA serving as the primary regulatory authority.

**Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (FDCA):** The FDCA, as amended by the Medical Device Amendments of 1976 (Public Law 94-295), the Safe Medical Devices Act of 1990, the FDA Modernization Act of 1997, and the 21st Century Cures Act of 2016, provides the statutory basis for FDA regulation of medical devices.

**Classification and Pre-Market Pathways:**

| Pathway | Applicable Classes | Standard | Review Time | Key Requirement |
|---------|-------------------|----------|-------------|-----------------|
| 510(k) Premarket Notification | Class I (exempt with limitations), Class II | Substantial equivalence to predicate device | ~100 days (average) | Demonstrate substantial equivalence |
| De Novo | Novel Class I or II devices without predicates | Safety and effectiveness for intended use | ~150 days | For low-to-moderate risk devices with no predicate |
| PMA (Premarket Approval) | Class III | Reasonable assurance of safety and effectiveness | ~180 days (statutory) | Clinical data typically required |
| HDE (Humanitarian Device Exemption) | Devices for conditions affecting <8,000 patients/year | Probable benefit outweighs risk | ~75 days | IRB approval for use required |

### 510(k) Pathway

The 510(k) premarket notification pathway is the most commonly used route to market, covering approximately 80% of new device submissions. The manufacturer must demonstrate that the device is "substantially equivalent" to a legally marketed predicate device in terms of intended use and technological characteristics. The 510(k) process has been criticized for permitting marketing of devices based on comparison to predicates that may themselves never have been subject to rigorous clinical testing, creating so-called "predicate creep."

### PMA Pathway

The PMA pathway requires the most rigorous demonstration of safety and effectiveness, typically including clinical investigation data from well-controlled studies. PMA is required for Class III devices, which support or sustain human life, are of substantial importance in preventing impairment of human health, or present a potential unreasonable risk of illness or injury. Approximately 50-80 original PMA applications are approved annually.

### AI/ML-Enabled Devices

The FDA has developed an evolving framework for AI/ML-based medical devices:

**AI/ML Action Plan (2021):** Announced five key action areas including a tailored regulatory framework for AI/ML-based SaMD, development of good machine learning practice (GMLP) principles, a patient-centered approach incorporating transparency, regulatory science methods, and real-world performance monitoring.

**Predetermined Change Control Plan (PCCP):** Finalized guidance in 2023 allows manufacturers to include a PCCP in their marketing submission describing planned modifications to the AI/ML device and the methodology for implementing those changes, potentially avoiding the need for new premarket submissions for each algorithm update.

**Authorized AI/ML Devices:** The FDA has authorized over 950 AI/ML-enabled medical devices as of 2025, predominantly in radiology (75%), cardiology (10%), and ophthalmology (5%). The majority have been cleared through the 510(k) pathway.

### Post-Market Requirements

**Medical Device Reporting (MDR, 21 C.F.R. Part 803):** Manufacturers, importers, and device user facilities are required to report to FDA certain device-related adverse events, including deaths, serious injuries, and malfunctions that could cause or contribute to death or serious injury.

**Recalls:** FDA classifies recalls into three classes based on the seriousness of the health hazard. Class I recalls involve a reasonable probability that use of the product will cause serious adverse health consequences or death. The FDA maintains a publicly searchable recall database.

**Unique Device Identification (UDI):** The UDI system (21 C.F.R. Part 801, Subpart B) requires most medical devices to bear a unique device identifier on their labels and packages, with information submitted to the FDA's Global UDI Database (GUDID). Full implementation was phased in through 2025.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's medical device regulation has been fundamentally transformed by the transition to the EU MDR.

**EU Medical Devices Regulation (MDR, Regulation (EU) 2017/745):** Fully applicable since May 26, 2021, the MDR replaced the prior Medical Devices Directives (MDD 93/42/EEC and AIMDD 90/385/EEC). The MDR introduced substantially more stringent requirements for clinical evidence, post-market surveillance, economic operator obligations, and notified body oversight. Germany transitioned from its national Medizinproduktegesetz (MPG) to the Medizinprodukterecht-Durchfuehrungsgesetz (MPDG, Medical Devices Implementation Act) to implement the MDR's national provisions.

### Classification and Conformity Assessment

The MDR establishes a four-class risk-based classification system with 22 classification rules in Annex VIII:

| Class | Conformity Assessment | Examples |
|-------|----------------------|----------|
| Class I | Self-declaration (Annex II/III) | Non-sterile, non-measuring instruments |
| Class I (sterile, measuring, reusable surgical) | Notified body involvement for specific aspects | Sterile bandages, measuring devices |
| Class IIa | Notified body audit (Annex IX or XI) | Hearing aids, surgical sutures |
| Class IIb | Notified body assessment (Annex IX or X+XI) | Ventilators, orthopedic implants |
| Class III | Full notified body assessment (Annex IX) | Cardiac valves, hip implants, breast implants |

### Notified Bodies

The MDR significantly strengthened the requirements for notified bodies -- the independent organizations designated to assess device conformity. Notified bodies must be re-designated under the MDR by national competent authorities following joint assessment with the European Commission and other member states. In Germany, the Zentralstelle der Laender fuer Gesundheitsschutz bei Arzneimitteln und Medizinprodukten (ZLG) coordinates the designation of notified bodies. As of 2025, the number of notified bodies designated under the MDR remains significantly below the number previously operating under the MDD, creating capacity constraints.

### Clinical Evidence Requirements

The MDR substantially strengthened clinical evidence requirements under Articles 61-82 and Annex XIV-XV:

- **Clinical Evaluation:** All devices must undergo clinical evaluation based on clinical data (clinical investigations, literature review, clinical experience).
- **Clinical Investigations:** Class III and implantable devices generally require clinical investigation data unless the manufacturer can justify reliance on existing clinical data for equivalent devices (with strict equivalence criteria under MDR Article 61(5)).
- **Post-Market Clinical Follow-Up (PMCF):** Required for all devices as part of the post-market surveillance system. PMCF plans must be included in the clinical evaluation report and actively updated.

### Transition Challenges

The MDR transition has been marked by significant implementation difficulties, including insufficient notified body capacity, the re-certification backlog for legacy devices, and concerns about market withdrawal of established devices. In response, the EU adopted Regulation (EU) 2023/607, extending the transition periods for certain MDD-certified devices, and additional transitional measures through 2027-2028 for specific device categories.

### BfArM Role

The Bundesinstitut fuer Arzneimittel und Medizinprodukte (BfArM) serves as the German competent authority for medical devices, operating the vigilance system, maintaining the German medical device database (DMIDS), conducting market surveillance, and evaluating clinical investigation applications.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Medical Devices Regulation (MDR 2017/745)

The MDR represents the most comprehensive supranational medical device regulatory framework:

**Eudamed (European Database on Medical Devices):** Article 33 of the MDR establishes Eudamed as a centralized EU-level database for medical device registration, UDI/device identification, notified body and certificate information, clinical investigations, vigilance data, and market surveillance. Eudamed has been deployed in modules, with full functionality targeted for completion by 2026.

**Post-Market Surveillance (PMS) System:** Articles 83-86 require manufacturers to establish PMS systems proportionate to the risk class of the device, including proactive and systematic collection and analysis of data on device performance throughout its lifetime, periodic safety update reports (PSURs) at least annually for Class IIa, IIb, and III devices, and trend reporting to competent authorities.

**Vigilance (Articles 87-92):** Manufacturers must report serious incidents (any malfunction or deterioration in characteristics or performance that has led to or might lead to death or serious deterioration in health) to the competent authority within 15 days (or 2 days for imminent serious public health threats, 10 days for death or unanticipated serious deterioration).

### EU AI Act and Medical Devices

The EU Artificial Intelligence Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689) intersects significantly with medical device regulation. AI systems that are safety components of medical devices, or that are themselves medical devices, are classified as "high-risk AI systems" under Annex I, Section A of the AI Act. This means they must comply with both the MDR requirements and the AI Act's requirements for risk management, data governance, technical documentation, transparency, human oversight, and accuracy/robustness.

### PIP Breast Implant Scandal

The Poly Implant Prothese (PIP) scandal, in which a French manufacturer used industrial-grade silicone rather than medical-grade silicone in breast implants sold to hundreds of thousands of patients worldwide, exposed fundamental weaknesses in the EU's pre-MDR regulatory framework.

**CJEU, Schmitt v. TUeV Rheinland (C-219/15, Judgment of February 16, 2017):** The Court held that the purpose of notified body involvement in the conformity assessment procedure is to protect end users of medical devices, and that a notified body may be liable for breach of its obligations under the MDD. However, liability for failure to conduct unannounced inspections or device testing depends on whether the notified body breached specific obligations arising from the directive.

### ECtHR Jurisprudence

**Dupin v. France (Application No. 2282/17, Judgment of January 18, 2024):** In a case arising from the PIP breast implant scandal, the ECtHR examined whether France's regulatory framework adequately protected the applicant's Article 8 rights. The Court analyzed the adequacy of pre-market controls, the effectiveness of post-market surveillance, and the availability of domestic remedies for persons harmed by defective medical devices.

### In Vitro Diagnostic Medical Devices Regulation (IVDR, Regulation (EU) 2017/746)

The IVDR, fully applicable since May 26, 2022, modernized the regulatory framework for in vitro diagnostic devices (IVDs) including laboratory diagnostics, companion diagnostics, and self-testing devices. The IVDR introduced a new four-class risk classification (A, B, C, D) and substantially increased notified body involvement for higher-risk IVDs, particularly companion diagnostics and high-risk infectious disease tests.

## Comparative Analysis

### Regulatory Architecture

All four jurisdictions employ risk-based classification systems, though they differ in granularity and specific classification rules. South Korea and the EU use four-class systems, while the US uses three classes. The EU's MDR imposes the most detailed classification rules (22 rules in Annex VIII), while the US system relies more heavily on FDA discretion in classification determinations. A key structural difference is the EU's reliance on third-party notified bodies for conformity assessment, compared to the direct government review model of the US FDA and Korean MFDS.

### Pre-Market Evidence Requirements

The MDR has significantly elevated clinical evidence requirements in the EU, particularly for Class III and implantable devices, potentially exceeding PMA-level requirements for some device types. The US 510(k) pathway remains the most permissive major market pathway, requiring substantial equivalence rather than independent demonstration of safety and effectiveness. Korea's system falls between the EU and US, with MFDS direct review providing more centralized oversight than the notified body system but with somewhat less stringent clinical data requirements than the MDR for many device types.

### AI/ML Device Regulation

All jurisdictions are grappling with the regulatory challenges of AI/ML-enabled medical devices, particularly continuously learning algorithms. The US FDA's PCCP framework and Korea's MFDS AI guidance are the most developed regulatory pathways specifically designed for AI/ML devices. The EU's approach layers the AI Act's requirements on top of MDR compliance, creating a potentially more complex regulatory landscape for AI medical devices. The fundamental challenge across all jurisdictions is balancing the need for pre-market assurance with the iterative nature of AI/ML algorithm development.

### Post-Market Surveillance

The MDR establishes the most prescriptive post-market surveillance requirements, with detailed PSUR obligations, PMCF requirements, and the Eudamed database for centralized data collection. The US MDR (Medical Device Reporting) system relies primarily on adverse event reporting, supplemented by post-market studies and the FDA's Sentinel Initiative for active surveillance. Korea's system requires periodic safety updates for higher-risk devices and maintains a national adverse event reporting database.

### Liability for Device Failures

The EU Product Liability Directive (and its proposed revision) provides strict liability for defective medical devices. In the US, medical device liability is governed by state tort law, with federal preemption under Riegel v. Medtronic, Inc., 552 U.S. 312 (2008), shielding PMA-approved devices from most state tort claims. Korea's Product Liability Act applies to medical devices alongside the Medical Devices Act's safety requirements.
