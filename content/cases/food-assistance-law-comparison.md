## Summary

Food assistance programs -- government-administered systems providing nutritional support to individuals and families unable to secure adequate food through market participation alone -- represent one of the most widespread forms of social welfare intervention across developed economies, though the legal architectures underlying these programs differ dramatically in scope, eligibility criteria, delivery mechanisms, and constitutional grounding. South Korea operates a multi-layered food assistance framework anchored by the National Basic Livelihood Security Act (국민기초생활보장법), enacted in 1999 and effective October 2000, which replaced the earlier Livelihood Protection Act of 1961 and guarantees a minimum standard of living including food support through livelihood benefits (생계급여) calculated at 30 percent of the median income threshold; supplementary programs include the School Meals Act (학교급식법) mandating free school lunches, the Food Donation Activation Act (식품기부활성화에 관한 법률) encouraging corporate food donations with tax incentives and liability protections, and the Emergency Welfare Support Act (긴급복지지원법) providing crisis food assistance within 24 hours of application. The United States operates the world's largest food assistance system through the Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP), authorized under the Food and Nutrition Act of 2008 (7 U.S.C. Sections 2011-2036a) and serving approximately 42 million Americans as of 2024, alongside the Special Supplemental Nutrition Program for Women, Infants, and Children (WIC), the National School Lunch Program (42 U.S.C. Section 1751 et seq.), and The Emergency Food Assistance Program (TEFAP); SNAP benefits are delivered through Electronic Benefit Transfer (EBT) cards and eligibility is generally limited to households with gross income at or below 130 percent of the federal poverty level. Germany provides food assistance primarily through the Sozialgesetzbuch (SGB) II and XII, which guarantee subsistence-level support including food as part of the Regelbedarf (standard needs rate), currently set at EUR 563 per month for single adults in 2024, while the Tafel (food bank) network -- comprising over 970 charitable food distribution centers -- supplements governmental provisions; the 2023 Bürgergeld reform replaced the prior Hartz IV system and increased benefit levels. At the EU level, the Fund for European Aid to the Most Deprived (FEAD), established by Regulation (EU) No 223/2014 and succeeded by the European Social Fund Plus (ESF+) under Regulation (EU) 2021/1057, allocates approximately EUR 4 billion per programming period to food and material assistance across member states, while the European Court of Human Rights has addressed food security tangentially through Article 3 ECHR (prohibition of inhuman or degrading treatment) jurisprudence establishing that state failure to provide minimum subsistence to vulnerable persons in state custody or care may constitute degrading treatment.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's food assistance system operates within a comprehensive social safety net anchored by the constitutional guarantee of a dignified minimum standard of living. Article 34 of the Constitution of the Republic of Korea (대한민국 헌법 제34조) provides that all citizens have the right to a life worthy of human dignity and that the state shall endeavor to promote social welfare. This constitutional mandate is implemented through several interconnected statutes.

**National Basic Livelihood Security Act (국민기초생활보장법):** Enacted on September 7, 1999, and effective October 1, 2000, this statute replaced the Livelihood Protection Act of 1961 (생활보호법). The Act establishes a legally enforceable right to minimum subsistence support, including food, for individuals and households whose income falls below defined thresholds. Livelihood benefits (생계급여) are provided to households with recognized income below 30 percent of the median income level, as determined annually by the Central Livelihood Security Committee (중앙생활보장위원회). In 2024, the livelihood benefit threshold for a single-person household was set at approximately KRW 713,102 per month.

**Customized Benefit System (맞춤형 급여체계):** Following the 2014 amendment to the National Basic Livelihood Security Act, the system was restructured into four separate benefit categories -- livelihood (생계), medical (의료), housing (주거), and education (교육) -- each with distinct eligibility thresholds. This reform eliminated the previous all-or-nothing approach that denied all benefits to households exceeding a single income threshold, allowing households to receive food-related livelihood benefits even if they exceeded thresholds for other benefit categories.

**School Meals Act (학교급식법):** Originally enacted in 1981 and substantially amended in 2006 and 2021, this statute mandates the provision of school meals in all elementary, middle, and high schools. As of 2021, all elementary and middle school students nationwide receive free school lunches, with high school coverage varying by province. The Act establishes nutritional standards, food safety requirements, and financing mechanisms shared between central and local governments.

**Food Donation Activation Act (식품기부활성화에 관한 법률):** Enacted in 2006, this statute encourages food donations by providing tax deductions for corporate donors, establishing liability protections for good-faith donors similar to the American Good Samaritan model, and creating the Korea Food Bank network infrastructure. The Act also mandates the establishment of food donation centers (푸드뱅크) in each metropolitan and provincial area.

**Emergency Welfare Support Act (긴급복지지원법):** Enacted in 2005, this statute provides immediate crisis assistance -- including food, shelter, and medical support -- to individuals and families facing sudden hardship due to the death or disappearance of the primary breadwinner, serious illness, domestic violence, fire, or other emergencies. Emergency food assistance must be provided within 24 hours of an eligible application, with support lasting up to one month (extendable to six months).

### Penalties and Enforcement

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| Fraudulent receipt of livelihood benefits | Imprisonment up to 1 year or fine up to KRW 5 million (Art. 49) |
| Obstruction of welfare officials' duties | Fine up to KRW 3 million |
| Failure to report income changes | Benefit recoupment plus penalty surcharge of 40% |
| School meals safety violations | Imprisonment up to 5 years or fine up to KRW 50 million |

### Recent Developments

The COVID-19 pandemic prompted significant expansion of emergency food assistance. The government issued emergency relief funds (긴급재난지원금) in 2020 and 2021, with portions designated for food purchases. The 2023 amendment to the National Basic Livelihood Security Act raised the livelihood benefit threshold from 30 percent to 32 percent of median income, expanding eligibility to approximately 200,000 additional households. In 2024, the Ministry of Health and Welfare announced plans to further expand coverage and simplify application procedures through digital platforms.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States operates the world's most extensive government food assistance system, comprising over fifteen distinct programs administered primarily by the U.S. Department of Agriculture (USDA) Food and Nutrition Service (FNS).

**Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP):** Authorized by the Food and Nutrition Act of 2008 (7 U.S.C. Sections 2011-2036a), SNAP is the largest domestic food assistance program, serving approximately 42.1 million individuals in 22.3 million households as of fiscal year 2024. The program's origins trace to the Food Stamp Act of 1964, signed by President Lyndon B. Johnson, which itself was inspired by a pilot food stamp program initiated under President John F. Kennedy in 1961. Benefits are delivered through Electronic Benefit Transfer (EBT) cards redeemable at authorized retail food stores. General eligibility requires gross monthly income at or below 130 percent of the federal poverty level (FPL) and net monthly income at or below 100 percent FPL. Able-bodied adults without dependents (ABAWDs) aged 18-49 are subject to time-limited benefits of three months in a 36-month period unless they meet work requirements of at least 80 hours per month.

**Special Supplemental Nutrition Program for Women, Infants, and Children (WIC):** Authorized by Section 17 of the Child Nutrition Act of 1966 (42 U.S.C. Section 1786), WIC provides supplemental foods, nutrition education, and health care referrals to pregnant women, breastfeeding women, postpartum women, and children up to age five whose income is at or below 185 percent of FPL. WIC served approximately 6.3 million participants per month in fiscal year 2024.

**National School Lunch Program (NSLP):** Established by the Richard B. Russell National School Lunch Act (42 U.S.C. Section 1751 et seq.), originally signed into law in 1946. The program provides free or reduced-price lunches to approximately 30 million children daily. The Community Eligibility Provision (CEP), authorized by the Healthy, Hunger-Free Kids Act of 2010, allows schools in high-poverty areas to provide free meals to all students without individual income verification.

**The Emergency Food Assistance Program (TEFAP):** Authorized by the Emergency Food Assistance Act of 1983 (7 U.S.C. Sections 7501-7518), TEFAP provides USDA-purchased commodities to state agencies for distribution through emergency food organizations including food banks and food pantries.

**Bill Emerson Good Samaritan Food Donation Act (42 U.S.C. Section 1791):** Enacted in 1996, this statute provides federal liability protection to persons and organizations that donate food in good faith, encouraging food recovery and redistribution by protecting donors from civil and criminal liability arising from donated food unless the harm results from gross negligence or intentional misconduct.

### Penalties and Enforcement

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| SNAP trafficking (selling benefits for cash) | Up to 20 years imprisonment and $250,000 fine (7 U.S.C. Section 2024) |
| SNAP fraud by individual recipients | Disqualification: 1 year (first offense), 2 years (second), permanent (third) |
| Retailer SNAP fraud | Permanent disqualification from program; civil money penalties |
| Fraudulent WIC claims | Up to $25,000 fine per violation |

### Constitutional Dimensions

The U.S. Constitution does not contain an explicit right to food. In Dandridge v. Williams, 397 U.S. 471 (1970), the Supreme Court held that welfare benefits are subject to rational basis review rather than strict scrutiny. However, in Goldberg v. Kelly, 397 U.S. 254 (1970), the Court held that welfare recipients are entitled to due process protections before benefits can be terminated, establishing that welfare benefits constitute a property interest under the Fourteenth Amendment. King v. Burwell, 576 U.S. 473 (2015), while concerning health insurance subsidies, reinforced judicial deference to agency interpretations of complex social welfare statutes.

### Recent Developments

The Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2023, made the Summer EBT program permanent, providing approximately $40 per month per child during summer months when school meals are unavailable. The 2024 Thrifty Food Plan reassessment resulted in a 12.5 percent increase in SNAP maximum allotments, the largest permanent increase in the program's history outside of annual inflation adjustments. Several states have enacted legislation expanding categorical eligibility for SNAP through Broad-Based Categorical Eligibility (BBCE), raising the gross income limit to 200 percent of FPL.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's food assistance framework derives from the constitutional guarantee of a minimum subsistence level (Existenzminimum) established through the interaction of Article 1(1) of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz, GG) -- guaranteeing human dignity -- and Article 20(1) GG -- establishing Germany as a social state (Sozialstaat). The Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht, BVerfG) has repeatedly affirmed that these provisions require the state to guarantee a minimum standard of living sufficient for physical existence and participation in social, cultural, and political life.

**Bürgergeld (Citizens' Income) -- SGB II:** The Bürgergeld-Gesetz, which entered into force on January 1, 2023, replaced the prior Hartz IV system (Arbeitslosengeld II) established by the Fourth Act for Modern Services on the Labour Market (2003). The Regelbedarf (standard needs rate) for a single adult was set at EUR 502 per month in January 2023, increased to EUR 563 per month in January 2024. This amount is calculated to cover food, clothing, personal hygiene, household goods, and participation in social and cultural life. The food component constitutes approximately 35-37 percent of the total standard needs rate, or approximately EUR 200 per month for a single adult.

**Social Assistance -- SGB XII:** Individuals who are not capable of working (elderly, disabled) receive social assistance (Sozialhilfe) under SGB XII, with benefit levels equivalent to Bürgergeld rates. The Hilfe zum Lebensunterhalt (subsistence assistance) under Sections 27-40 SGB XII covers identical needs categories including food.

**Bildungs- und Teilhabepaket (Education and Participation Package):** Under Section 28 SGB II and Section 34 SGB XII, children in benefit-receiving households are entitled to additional allowances for school meals, school supplies, and participation in social and cultural activities. The school meals component covers the cost of communal school lunches minus a co-payment of EUR 1 per meal.

### Constitutional Jurisprudence

**BVerfG, Judgment of February 9, 2010 (1 BvL 1/09, 1 BvL 3/09, 1 BvL 4/09) -- Hartz IV Decision:** The Federal Constitutional Court held that the Hartz IV benefit levels were unconstitutional because the legislature had not employed a transparent, needs-based methodology for calculating the standard needs rate. The Court established that the right to a minimum subsistence level (Existenzminimum) is a fundamental right derived from Article 1(1) GG in conjunction with Article 20(1) GG, requiring the legislature to ensure a minimum standard sufficient not only for physical survival but also for meaningful social participation.

**BVerfG, Judgment of November 5, 2019 (1 BvL 7/16) -- Sanctions Decision:** The Court ruled that benefit sanctions exceeding 30 percent of the standard needs rate were unconstitutional, as they threatened the minimum subsistence level. This decision directly affected food security by limiting the extent to which food-related benefits could be reduced as punishment for non-compliance with employment requirements.

### Tafel (Food Bank) Network

The Deutsche Tafel e.V. network, founded in 1993 and inspired by the American food bank model, comprises over 970 Tafeln (food distribution centers) operated by approximately 60,000 volunteers. The Tafeln collect surplus food from retailers, producers, and restaurants and distribute it to approximately 2 million people at nominal cost (typically EUR 1-2 per visit). The Tafeln operate under general charitable law (Gemeinnützigkeitsrecht) and are recognized as tax-exempt organizations under Section 52 of the Abgabenordnung (AO). There is no specific federal statute governing food bank operations.

### Recent Developments

The Bürgergeld reform of 2023 increased the Karenzzeit (grace period) to twelve months, during which new benefit recipients are not required to downsize housing, and asset limits are relaxed to EUR 40,000 for the first household member. The 2024 benefit increase of 12 percent (from EUR 502 to EUR 563) was driven by the Regelbedarfsstufen-Fortschreibungsverordnung 2024, which applies a mixed index of 70 percent price development and 30 percent net wage development. In 2025, the government proposed tightening sanctions for Bürgergeld recipients who refuse reasonable job offers, sparking significant political debate about the balance between subsistence guarantees and work incentives.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Fund for European Aid to the Most Deprived (FEAD)

Regulation (EU) No 223/2014, adopted on March 11, 2014, established the Fund for European Aid to the Most Deprived (FEAD) for the 2014-2020 programming period, with a total budget of approximately EUR 3.8 billion. The FEAD succeeded the EU Programme for Distribution of Food to the Most Deprived Persons (MDP), which had operated since 1987 using agricultural surplus from the Common Agricultural Policy (CAP). FEAD provided funding for food assistance and basic material assistance to the most deprived persons, as well as social inclusion activities. Member states had discretion to distribute food assistance through partner organizations (typically food banks and social services) and to determine eligibility criteria within the framework of the Regulation.

### European Social Fund Plus (ESF+)

For the 2021-2027 programming period, FEAD was integrated into the European Social Fund Plus (ESF+) under Regulation (EU) 2021/1057, adopted on June 24, 2021. Article 4(1)(m) of the ESF+ Regulation specifically addresses "addressing material deprivation through food and/or basic material assistance to the most deprived persons, including children, and providing accompanying measures supporting their social inclusion." Member states with food deprivation levels above the EU average are required to allocate a minimum of 3 percent of their ESF+ resources to addressing material deprivation. The total ESF+ budget for 2021-2027 exceeds EUR 99 billion, of which approximately EUR 4 billion is earmarked for material deprivation programs.

### EU School Scheme

Regulation (EU) 2016/791 established the EU School Fruit, Vegetables, and Milk Scheme, which provides EU co-financing for member state programs distributing fresh fruits, vegetables, and milk products to schoolchildren. The scheme has an annual budget of approximately EUR 250 million (EUR 150 million for fruits and vegetables, EUR 100 million for milk) and is intended to promote healthy eating habits while providing nutritional support.

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR does not contain an explicit right to food. However, the Court has addressed food security indirectly through several Convention provisions:

**Article 3 (Prohibition of Inhuman or Degrading Treatment):** In M.S.S. v. Belgium and Greece (Application No. 30696/09, Grand Chamber Judgment of January 21, 2011), the Court found that Greece violated Article 3 by failing to provide an asylum seeker with adequate living conditions, including access to food. The applicant had lived for months in extreme poverty without any means of providing for essential needs including food. The Court held that the Greek authorities' failure to provide for the applicant's basic needs constituted degrading treatment.

**Budina v. Russia (Application No. 45603/05, Decision of June 18, 2009):** The Court accepted in principle that state failure to provide sufficient welfare to ensure a minimum standard of living could raise an issue under Article 3, though the application was ultimately declared inadmissible on the facts.

**Article 1 Protocol 1 (Protection of Property):** In Stec and Others v. United Kingdom (Applications Nos. 65731/01 and 65900/01, Grand Chamber Judgment of April 12, 2006), the Court confirmed that contributory and non-contributory welfare benefits fall within the scope of Article 1 Protocol 1, meaning that arbitrary withdrawal of food assistance benefits could constitute a violation of the right to property.

### EU Charter of Fundamental Rights

Article 34(3) of the Charter of Fundamental Rights of the European Union provides: "In order to combat social exclusion and poverty, the Union recognises and respects the right to social and housing assistance so as to ensure a decent existence for all those who lack sufficient resources." While this provision does not create a directly enforceable right to food assistance, it establishes a guiding principle for EU legislation and policy.

## Comparative Analysis

### Constitutional Foundations

The four jurisdictions exhibit fundamentally different constitutional relationships with food assistance:

**Enforceable Constitutional Right (Germany):** Germany's Basic Law, as interpreted by the Federal Constitutional Court, establishes a judicially enforceable right to a minimum subsistence level that includes adequate nutrition. The legislature must calculate benefit levels using a transparent, evidence-based methodology and cannot reduce benefits below the Existenzminimum.

**Constitutional Directive (South Korea):** Article 34 of the Korean Constitution establishes a right to a dignified standard of living and imposes a duty on the state to promote social welfare, but the Constitutional Court has granted the legislature significant discretion in determining the specific content and level of benefits.

**Statutory Entitlement (United States):** The U.S. Constitution contains no right to food or welfare, but statutory programs such as SNAP create legally enforceable entitlements with due process protections, as established in Goldberg v. Kelly.

**Emerging Supranational Norm (EU/ECHR):** EU law establishes programmatic obligations for food assistance funding, while the ECHR recognizes that extreme deprivation including lack of food can trigger positive obligations under Article 3, though no freestanding right to food assistance exists.

### Delivery Mechanisms

South Korea and Germany primarily use cash transfers that subsume food assistance within broader subsistence benefits, giving recipients maximum purchasing discretion. The United States restricts SNAP benefits to food purchases through the EBT system, reflecting a paternalistic approach that limits recipient autonomy but ensures benefits are spent on nutrition. The EU distributes food through a hybrid of member state cash benefit systems and direct food distribution programs.

### Work Requirements

The most significant policy divergence concerns the relationship between food assistance and work obligations. The United States imposes strict work requirements on able-bodied adults without dependents, with non-compliance resulting in complete benefit termination after three months. Germany's Bürgergeld system imposes employment-related obligations but, following the 2019 Constitutional Court sanctions decision, may not reduce benefits by more than 30 percent for non-compliance. South Korea requires livelihood benefit recipients to participate in self-sufficiency programs but provides exceptions for the elderly, disabled, and caregivers.

### Coverage Gaps

Despite extensive legal frameworks, significant coverage gaps persist across all jurisdictions. In the United States, undocumented immigrants are ineligible for SNAP, creating food insecurity in mixed-status households. In South Korea, the strict asset test excludes many working poor households that own modest property. In Germany, the Tafel network's reliance on surplus food donations means supply is unstable and geographically uneven. At the EU level, the FEAD/ESF+ framework depends on member state implementation capacity, resulting in significant variation in service delivery quality.
