## Summary

Foreign exchange regulation -- the legal framework governing cross-border capital movements, currency conversion, international payments, and the management of exchange rate policy -- represents a critical intersection of monetary sovereignty, trade policy, and financial stability. South Korea's foreign exchange regime is governed by the Foreign Exchange Transactions Act (외국환거래법) of 1998, which replaced the more restrictive Foreign Exchange Management Act (외국환관리법) of 1961, reflecting the country's dramatic shift from tight capital controls toward progressive liberalization following the 1997 Asian Financial Crisis, while retaining significant reporting requirements, approval thresholds, and macroprudential measures administered by the Ministry of Economy and Finance (기획재정부) and the Bank of Korea (한국은행). The United States maintains a generally liberal foreign exchange regime under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA, 50 U.S.C. §§ 1701-1707), the Trading with the Enemy Act (TWEA, 50 U.S.C. §§ 4301-4341), and the Treasury Department's Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC) sanctions programs, with the Federal Reserve managing dollar liquidity through swap lines and the Treasury overseeing exchange rate policy. Germany, as a eurozone member, has delegated exchange rate policy to the European Central Bank under Article 219 TFEU, with residual capital movement regulation governed by the Außenwirtschaftsgesetz (AWG, Foreign Trade and Payments Act) of 2013 and the Außenwirtschaftsverordnung (AWV), while the EU's free movement of capital under Articles 63-66 TFEU -- one of the four fundamental freedoms -- prohibits restrictions on capital movements between member states and between member states and third countries, subject to exceptions for public policy, tax supervision, and prudential oversight, with the ECHR protecting property rights and freedom to conduct business in the context of foreign exchange regulation.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's foreign exchange regulatory framework underwent a fundamental transformation during and after the 1997 Asian Financial Crisis, shifting from the restrictive Foreign Exchange Management Act (외국환관리법) regime to the more liberal Foreign Exchange Transactions Act (외국환거래법), effective April 1, 1999.

**Foreign Exchange Transactions Act (외국환거래법):** The Act establishes a notification-based system replacing the prior approval-based system. Key provisions include:

| Article | Content |
|---------|---------|
| Article 3 | Definitions: foreign exchange, foreign exchange transactions, residents, non-residents |
| Article 8 | Foreign exchange business authorization (banks, securities firms) |
| Article 15 | Capital transactions: notification or approval requirements |
| Article 16 | Notification requirements for residents' overseas direct investment |
| Article 18 | Emergency measures: government authority to restrict capital movements during crises |
| Article 19 | Reporting obligations for foreign exchange transactions |

**Capital Transaction Classifications:**

| Category | Requirement | Examples |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| Free transactions | No notification/approval | Current account transactions below thresholds; portfolio investment |
| Notification transactions | Post-transaction reporting to designated bank | Overseas direct investment below $50 million |
| Approval transactions | Prior MOF/BOK approval | Large-scale capital transactions; capital transactions with sanctioned countries |

### Macroprudential Measures

South Korea has maintained several macroprudential measures to manage capital flow volatility:

**Foreign Exchange Soundness Measures (2010):**
- Cap on banks' foreign exchange derivative positions: 50 percent of equity capital for domestic banks; 250 percent for foreign bank branches (subsequently tightened)
- Foreign exchange liquidity coverage ratio requirements
- Levy on non-deposit foreign currency liabilities of banks (macroprudential stability levy, introduced 2011)

**Foreign Exchange Stabilization Fund:** The MOF operates a Foreign Exchange Stabilization Fund for market intervention, financed through Foreign Exchange Stabilization Bonds. South Korea's foreign exchange reserves stood at approximately $420 billion as of early 2026.

### Recent Liberalization

The Korean government has pursued phased liberalization:
- 2022: Relaxation of overseas remittance documentation requirements
- 2023: Extended foreign exchange trading hours to 2:00 AM KST (from 3:30 PM) to increase market liquidity
- 2024: Introduction of non-resident Korean won (KRW) offshore settlement mechanisms
- 2025: MSCI inclusion campaign supported by FX market reforms including the establishment of an onshore KRW-USD clearing mechanism

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States maintains a generally open capital account, with foreign exchange regulation primarily focused on sanctions enforcement, anti-money laundering, and macroeconomic policy.

**Exchange Rate Policy:** Under the Omnibus Trade and Competitiveness Act of 1988 (22 U.S.C. § 5305), the Treasury Secretary is responsible for monitoring and reporting on foreign exchange policies of major trading partners. The Treasury's semi-annual "Report to Congress on Macroeconomic and Foreign Exchange Policies of Major Trading Partners" uses three criteria to identify potential currency manipulators:
1. Bilateral trade surplus with the United States exceeding $15 billion
2. Current account surplus exceeding 3 percent of GDP
3. Net foreign exchange purchases exceeding 2 percent of GDP over 12 months

**IEEPA (50 U.S.C. §§ 1701-1707):** The President may declare a national emergency and impose comprehensive foreign exchange restrictions on targeted countries, entities, and individuals. IEEPA serves as the legal basis for OFAC sanctions programs including:

| Sanctions Program | Target | FX Impact |
|-------------------|--------|-----------|
| Iran | Comprehensive | Near-total prohibition on USD transactions |
| Russia/Belarus | Sectoral and comprehensive | Restrictions on FX transactions with designated banks; sovereign debt prohibitions |
| North Korea | Comprehensive | Complete FX prohibition |
| Cuba | Comprehensive | Near-total prohibition |
| Venezuela | Sectoral | Restrictions on financial sector transactions |

**Bank Secrecy Act Cross-Border Provisions:**
- CTR: Cash transactions exceeding $10,000 (31 C.F.R. § 1010.310)
- FBAR: Foreign Bank Account Report for U.S. persons with foreign accounts exceeding $10,000 aggregate (31 C.F.R. § 1010.350)
- FATCA: Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act (26 U.S.C. §§ 1471-1474) requiring foreign financial institutions to report accounts held by U.S. persons

### Federal Reserve Swap Lines

The Federal Reserve operates a network of bilateral central bank liquidity swap lines providing dollar liquidity to foreign central banks during periods of market stress. Standing swap lines exist with the Bank of Canada, Bank of England, Bank of Japan, ECB, and Swiss National Bank. Temporary lines were activated extensively during the 2008 financial crisis and the 2020 COVID-19 pandemic.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

As a eurozone member state, Germany has delegated monetary policy and exchange rate competence to the ECB. Residual foreign exchange regulation is governed by the Außenwirtschaftsgesetz (AWG, Foreign Trade and Payments Act) of 2013 and the Außenwirtschaftsverordnung (AWV, Foreign Trade and Payments Ordinance).

**AWG Key Provisions:**

| Section | Content |
|---------|---------|
| § 1 | Principle of free foreign trade and payments |
| § 4 | Restrictions for protection of essential security interests |
| § 5 | Restrictions to prevent disturbance of peaceful coexistence of nations |
| § 6 | Restrictions to prevent adverse effects on the economy |
| § 11 | Reporting obligations for cross-border payments |
| § 13 | Investment screening for acquisitions by non-EU investors |

**Cross-Border Payment Reporting (AWV § 67):** Residents must report cross-border payments exceeding €12,500 to the Deutsche Bundesbank for balance of payments statistics. This is a reporting obligation, not an approval requirement.

**Investment Screening (AWG §§ 4-5; AWV §§ 55-62):** Germany has significantly expanded foreign investment screening since 2017:
- Acquisition of 10+ percent of voting rights in critical infrastructure companies triggers mandatory notification to the Federal Ministry for Economic Affairs and Climate Action
- Screening thresholds lowered to 10 percent for defense, critical infrastructure, and media companies (2020 amendment)
- Sectors covered: defense, telecommunications, IT security, energy, water, food, health, finance, transport, media

### Euro and ECB

Germany's adoption of the euro in 1999 eliminated foreign exchange risk within the eurozone and transferred exchange rate policy to the ECB:

**Article 219 TFEU:** The Council may conclude formal exchange rate agreements with non-EU currencies (on ECB or Commission recommendation) or adopt general orientations for exchange rate policy vis-à-vis non-EU currencies. No formal exchange rate agreements currently exist.

**ERM II:** EU member states preparing for euro adoption may participate in the Exchange Rate Mechanism II, maintaining their currency within a fluctuation band of ±15 percent against the euro. As of 2026, Bulgaria and Denmark participate in ERM II (Denmark with a narrower ±2.25 percent band under a special arrangement).

## ECHR / EU

### Free Movement of Capital (TFEU Articles 63-66)

The EU's free movement of capital is the most liberal capital movement regime in the world, prohibiting restrictions not only between member states but also between member states and third countries:

**Article 63 TFEU:** "Within the framework of the provisions set out in this Chapter, all restrictions on the movement of capital between Member States and between Member States and third countries shall be prohibited."

**Article 65 TFEU (Permitted Restrictions):**
- Tax law distinctions based on residence or place of investment (Art. 65(1)(a))
- Measures to prevent infringements of national law (Art. 65(1)(b))
- Measures justified by public policy or public security (Art. 65(1)(b))
- Declaration requirements for capital movements for administrative or statistical purposes (Art. 65(1)(b))

**Article 66 TFEU (Safeguard Clause):** In exceptional circumstances where capital movements to or from third countries cause or threaten serious difficulties for EMU functioning, the Council may adopt safeguard measures for up to 6 months.

### CJEU Jurisprudence on Capital Movements

**Case C-163/94, Sanz de Lera (1995):** The CJEU established that the free movement of capital provisions have direct effect, meaning individuals can rely on them directly before national courts. Prior authorization requirements for capital exports constitute prohibited restrictions unless justified under Article 65 TFEU.

**Case C-483/99, Commission v. France (Golden Shares) (2002):** The Court held that French golden share provisions in privatized companies constituted restrictions on the free movement of capital, finding that the discretionary nature of the government's power to approve foreign acquisitions was disproportionate.

**Case C-212/09, Commission v. Portugal (Golden Shares II) (2011):** The Court struck down Portuguese golden share provisions as disproportionate restrictions on capital movements, establishing that even non-discriminatory restrictions require justification.

### EU Cross-Border Cash Controls

Regulation (EU) 2018/1672 on controls on cash entering or leaving the Union, applicable since June 3, 2021:
- Accompanied cash: declaration obligation for cash of €10,000 or more when crossing EU external borders (Article 3)
- Unaccompanied cash: disclosure obligation for cash sent by post, freight, or courier (Article 4)
- Temporary seizure: competent authorities may temporarily retain undeclared/undisclosed cash (Article 7)

### ECHR Considerations

**Beyeler v. Italy (Application No. 33202/96, Grand Chamber, January 5, 2000):** While concerning cultural property pre-emption, the Court established important principles about state interference with property rights in the context of economic regulation. Foreign exchange controls that restrict the ability of individuals to transfer or convert property constitute interference with Article 1 of Protocol No. 1.

**Centro Europa 7 S.r.l. and Di Stefano v. Italy (Application No. 38433/09, June 7, 2012):** The Court recognized that regulatory decisions with economic impact must comply with Convention requirements, including legal certainty and proportionality, relevant to discretionary foreign exchange authorization systems.

## Comparative Analysis

### Capital Account Openness

The four jurisdictions span the spectrum of capital account openness:

**Fully open (United States, Germany/EU):** No general restrictions on capital movements, with targeted restrictions for sanctions, national security, and AML purposes.

**Substantially open with macroprudential tools (South Korea):** Generally liberal regime with notification requirements, residual approval requirements for large transactions, and active macroprudential measures to manage capital flow volatility.

### Exchange Rate Management

Approaches to exchange rate management differ fundamentally:
- **United States:** Free floating; Treasury determines policy, Fed implements; no routine intervention
- **Germany/eurozone:** Euro determined by ECB monetary policy; no national exchange rate management
- **South Korea:** Managed floating; BOK intervenes in FX markets to smooth volatility; extensive foreign exchange reserves

### Investment Screening Convergence

All jurisdictions have significantly strengthened foreign investment screening mechanisms in recent years, driven by concerns about foreign acquisitions of critical infrastructure and technology:
- **South Korea:** Foreign Investment Promotion Act; sector-specific restrictions
- **United States:** CFIUS (Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States) under FIRRMA (2018)
- **Germany/EU:** AWG investment screening; EU FDI Screening Regulation (EU) 2019/452
- Convergence reflects growing geopolitical competition and national security concerns

### De-Dollarization and Currency Competition

The global foreign exchange landscape is being reshaped by geopolitical developments:

**Dollar Dominance:** The U.S. dollar remains the dominant global reserve currency (approximately 58 percent of identified official reserves as of 2025), but its share has gradually declined from approximately 72 percent in 2000. The use of dollar-based sanctions has prompted sanctioned states and their trading partners to develop alternative payment mechanisms.

**Alternative Systems:**
- **China's CIPS (Cross-Border Interbank Payment System):** Processes approximately $8 billion daily in RMB cross-border transactions
- **Russia's SPFS (System for Transfer of Financial Messages):** Domestic alternative to SWIFT, developed after 2014 sanctions
- **BRICS+ Currency Discussions:** Ongoing exploration of alternative settlement currencies among BRICS nations

**Korean Won Internationalization:** South Korea's efforts to internationalize the KRW include:
- Extension of FX market trading hours (2023)
- Development of offshore KRW settlement mechanisms (2024)
- Exploration of KRW inclusion in the IMF SDR basket (long-term goal)

### Cryptocurrency and Foreign Exchange

The rise of cryptocurrency has created new challenges for foreign exchange regulation:

| Jurisdiction | Approach |
|-------------|----------|
| South Korea | VASP registration under Special Financial Transaction Reports Act; FX regulations do not directly cover crypto |
| United States | FinCEN treats cryptocurrency exchanges as money transmitters; OFAC sanctions apply to crypto transactions |
| Germany/EU | MiCA framework; crypto-to-fiat subject to FX reporting; AMLR covers crypto service providers |

All jurisdictions are grappling with the reality that cryptocurrency enables cross-border value transfer outside traditional FX regulatory channels, potentially undermining capital flow management and sanctions enforcement.

### Sanctions and Financial Warfare

Foreign exchange controls have become a primary instrument of geopolitical competition:

- **United States:** OFAC administers comprehensive and sectoral sanctions programs; secondary sanctions threaten non-U.S. persons transacting with sanctioned entities; the weaponization of SWIFT access (Russia 2022) demonstrated the dollar system's coercive capacity
- **EU:** EU sanctions regulations under CFSP (TFEU Article 215) have expanded dramatically since 2022; asset freezing measures now cover over 2,000 Russian individuals and entities
- **South Korea:** FETA Article 15 authorizes transaction restrictions for UN and multilateral sanctions implementation; Korea's sanctions enforcement has intensified regarding North Korea-related transactions under UNSCR 1718 and successor resolutions
- **Germany:** Bundesbank and BaFin enforce EU sanctions; AWG § 4 authorizes restrictions on payments and capital transfers for foreign policy and security reasons

### Capital Flow Management Measures

The IMF's Institutional View on Capital Flows (2012, updated 2022) provides a framework for evaluating capital flow management measures (CFMs):

- **Pre-emptive CFMs:** South Korea's macroprudential stability levies on non-core FX liabilities (2011) were endorsed as appropriate macroprudential tools
- **Inflow CFMs:** Korea's limits on FX derivatives positions for banks (2010) successfully reduced speculative capital inflows
- **Outflow CFMs:** Generally disfavored but accepted as a last resort during crises; Malaysia (1998) and Iceland (2008) are prominent examples
- The IMF framework represents a significant departure from the Washington Consensus's presumption against capital controls, reflecting lessons from the Asian Financial Crisis and the Global Financial Crisis

### Central Bank Swap Line Networks

Central bank swap lines have become a critical component of the global foreign exchange infrastructure:
- **Federal Reserve:** Standing facilities with 5 major central banks; temporary facilities extended during crises
- **BOK:** Bilateral swap agreements with the Fed ($60 billion), Bank of China (CNY 400 billion), Bank of Japan (JPY 3 trillion), and others
- **ECB:** Swap lines with major non-eurozone central banks
- **People's Bank of China:** Over 40 bilateral swap agreements globally, primarily in RMB
- These swap lines function as a global financial safety net, providing dollar liquidity during crises and reducing the need for countries to hold excessive foreign exchange reserves

These arrangements provide crucial liquidity backstops during periods of FX market stress and have become instruments of financial diplomacy.

### Real-Time Payment Systems and FX

The development of real-time gross settlement systems and instant payment platforms is transforming cross-border FX transactions:

- **South Korea:** BOK-Wire+ system processes high-value FX settlements in real time; Korea Financial Telecommunications and Clearings Institute (KFTC) operates retail payment clearing
- **United States:** FedNow (2023) enables instant domestic payments; Clearing House Interbank Payments System (CHIPS) processes approximately $1.8 trillion daily in cross-border dollar payments
- **EU:** TARGET2 processes euro-denominated cross-border payments; TARGET Instant Payment Settlement (TIPS) enables instant cross-border euro payments
- Project mBridge (BIS Innovation Hub, BOK participant) is exploring multi-CBDC platforms for wholesale cross-border FX settlement, potentially reducing reliance on correspondent banking networks
