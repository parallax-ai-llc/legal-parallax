## Summary

Trade secret law protects confidential business information that derives economic value from its secrecy, providing an alternative or supplement to patent protection that requires no registration, has no fixed term, and extends to any information -- from manufacturing processes and customer lists to algorithms and business strategies -- as long as reasonable measures are taken to maintain its secrecy, though this protection ceases immediately if the secret is publicly disclosed or independently discovered. South Korea protects trade secrets through the Unfair Competition Prevention and Trade Secret Protection Act (부정경쟁방지 및 영업비밀보호에 관한 법률), which defines trade secrets as information with independent economic value that is not publicly known and is subject to reasonable secrecy efforts, providing both civil remedies (injunctive relief, damages including treble damages for intentional misappropriation since 2019) and criminal penalties of up to 15 years imprisonment for foreign economic espionage and up to 5 years for domestic trade secret theft; the Act was substantially strengthened in 2019 and 2024 with enhanced penalties and expanded protections for technology-related trade secrets. The United States protects trade secrets through the Defend Trade Secrets Act of 2016 (DTSA, 18 U.S.C. Sections 1831-1839), which created the first federal civil cause of action for trade secret misappropriation, supplemented by the Economic Espionage Act of 1996 (criminal), and state trade secret laws in all 50 states predominantly based on the Uniform Trade Secrets Act (UTSA, 1979/1985); the DTSA introduced novel remedies including ex parte seizure orders and whistleblower immunity provisions. Germany enacted the Geschäftsgeheimnisgesetz (GeschGehG, Trade Secrets Act) on April 26, 2019, transposing EU Directive 2016/943, replacing the prior fragmentary protection under the UWG (Sections 17-19 old version), and establishing a comprehensive framework with civil remedies including injunction, damages, recall, and destruction, plus criminal penalties for intentional misappropriation; the GeschGehG notably requires "reasonable measures" to maintain secrecy, a new requirement in German law that prompted significant compliance activity by German businesses. The EU harmonized trade secret protection through Directive (EU) 2016/943, which established minimum standards across member states while preserving important exceptions for whistleblowing, worker mobility, and freedom of expression.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's trade secret protection has been progressively strengthened, reflecting the country's transition from a technology-importing to a technology-exporting economy.

**Unfair Competition Prevention and Trade Secret Protection Act (부정경쟁방지 및 영업비밀보호에 관한 법률):** Article 2(2) defines a trade secret as information, including production methods, sales methods, or other useful technical or business information used in business activities, that is not publicly known, has independent economic value, and has been the subject of considerable effort to maintain its secrecy.

### Elements of Protection

| Element | Standard | Judicial Interpretation |
|---------|----------|----------------------|
| Not publicly known (비공지성) | Information not generally known to or readily ascertainable by persons who normally deal with such information | Supreme Court: not limited to the general public; assessed within relevant industry |
| Economic value (경제적 유용성) | Information that provides actual or potential economic advantage | Includes negative know-how (knowledge of what does not work) |
| Reasonable secrecy efforts (비밀관리성) | Holder must have taken considerable (상당한) efforts to maintain secrecy | Physical access controls, NDAs, password protection, classification systems |

### Enhanced Penalties (2019 and 2024 Amendments)

The 2019 amendment significantly strengthened trade secret enforcement:

**Treble Damages:** Article 14-2 introduced enhanced damages of up to three times actual damages for intentional trade secret misappropriation, aligning Korea with the U.S. DTSA framework.

**Increased Criminal Penalties:** Maximum imprisonment for foreign economic espionage was increased to 15 years, and fines were increased to 150 million KRW for domestic theft and up to 15 billion KRW for foreign espionage. Proceeds of trade secret crimes are subject to confiscation and additional criminal fines equivalent to the proceeds obtained.

**Presumption of Damages:** Where trade secret misappropriation is proven, the court may presume damages based on the infringer's profits unless the defendant proves otherwise.

The 2024 amendment further expanded protections for "national core technologies" (국가핵심기술) defined under the Act on Prevention of Divulgence and Protection of Industrial Technology, creating a direct link between trade secret enforcement and national technology security policy.

### Enforcement Statistics

Trade secret cases in Korean courts have increased substantially, from approximately 200 cases filed annually in the early 2010s to over 500 in 2024. The Prosecutor's Office has established specialized intellectual property divisions in major cities to handle trade secret criminal cases, and the KIPO operates a Trade Secret Protection Center providing advisory services to businesses.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States provides the most developed and litigated trade secret framework in the world, operating through parallel federal and state systems.

**Defend Trade Secrets Act of 2016 (DTSA, 18 U.S.C. Sections 1831-1839):** The DTSA created the first federal civil cause of action for trade secret misappropriation, supplementing (but not preempting) state trade secret laws. The Act defines "trade secret" broadly as all forms and types of financial, business, scientific, technical, economic, or engineering information, provided the owner has taken reasonable measures to keep it secret and it derives independent economic value from not being generally known to or readily ascertainable by another person who can obtain economic value from its disclosure or use.

### DTSA Distinctive Features

**Ex Parte Seizure (18 U.S.C. Section 1836(b)(2)):** The DTSA provides for ex parte seizure orders in "extraordinary circumstances" to prevent the propagation or dissemination of trade secrets. The applicant must demonstrate by specific facts that an ordinary temporary restraining order would be inadequate, the harm from denial of the seizure outweighs the harm to the legitimate interests of the person against whom seizure would be ordered, and the person against whom seizure would be ordered has actual possession of the trade secret.

**Whistleblower Immunity (18 U.S.C. Section 1833(b)):** Any individual who discloses a trade secret in confidence to a government official or attorney solely for the purpose of reporting or investigating a suspected violation of law is immune from liability under the DTSA and any state trade secret law. Employers must provide notice of this immunity in any contract governing trade secrets.

### State Trade Secret Laws

All 50 states have enacted trade secret statutes, with 48 states and D.C. adopting some version of the Uniform Trade Secrets Act (UTSA). New York adopted its own trade secret statute in 2024 after decades as the only major holdout. Notable state variations include the inevitable disclosure doctrine (recognized in some states, allowing injunctions where former employees would inevitably use trade secrets in new employment, even without actual misappropriation), and varying standards for preliminary injunctive relief.

### Landmark Cases

**Waymo LLC v. Uber Technologies, Inc. (N.D. Cal. 2017-2018):** One of the most high-profile trade secret cases in U.S. history, involving allegations that a former Google/Waymo engineer took 14,000 proprietary files relating to autonomous vehicle technology before joining Uber. The case settled in February 2018, with Uber providing Waymo approximately $245 million in equity and agreeing not to use Waymo's confidential information.

**Epic Games, Inc. v. Apple Inc. (N.D. Cal. 2021):** The trade secret aspects of this landmark antitrust case addressed the protection of Apple's App Store commission structure and revenue data as trade secrets.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's trade secret protection was fundamentally transformed by the enactment of the Geschäftsgeheimnisgesetz (GeschGehG) on April 26, 2019, transposing EU Directive 2016/943.

**Prior Framework:** Before the GeschGehG, German trade secret protection was fragmented across the UWG (Sections 17-19 old version), the BGB (duty of loyalty), and the HGB (employee duties). The prior UWG provisions focused on criminal sanctions for trade secret theft but provided limited civil remedies and did not require the trade secret holder to have taken "reasonable measures" to maintain secrecy.

**GeschGehG -- Key Provisions:**

**Definition (Section 2(1)):** A trade secret is information that (a) is not generally known among or readily accessible to persons within the circles that normally deal with the kind of information in question, (b) has economic value because of its secrecy, (c) has been subject to reasonable protective measures by the lawful holder, and (d) there is a legitimate interest in its secrecy. The addition of requirements (c) and (d) represented a significant change from prior German law.

### Reasonable Measures Requirement

The GeschGehG's requirement of "reasonable measures" (angemessene Geheimhaltungsmaßnahmen) to maintain secrecy prompted substantial compliance activity by German businesses:

| Measure Category | Examples |
|-----------------|---------|
| Organizational | Classification systems; access-on-need-only policies; exit procedures |
| Contractual | NDAs with employees, contractors, and business partners; specific identification of protected information |
| Technical | Password protection; encryption; access logging; secure storage |
| Physical | Restricted areas; visitor protocols; document security |

### Lawful Acquisition and Use

Section 3 GeschGehG defines lawful means of acquiring trade secrets, including independent discovery or creation, observation, study, disassembly, or testing of a product or object that has been made available to the public or lawfully obtained (reverse engineering), and exercise of workers' or workers' representatives' right to information and consultation.

### Criminal Provisions

Section 23 GeschGehG provides criminal penalties of up to 3 years imprisonment or a fine for unlawful acquisition, use, or disclosure of trade secrets. Aggravated offenses -- including trade secret theft for commercial purposes, acting on behalf of a foreign power, or causing substantial economic harm -- carry enhanced penalties.

### Impact on Employment Relationships

The GeschGehG has required German employers to reassess their approach to employee confidentiality. The Act's reasonable measures requirement means that implied duties of loyalty alone may be insufficient to establish trade secret protection -- employers must implement concrete organizational, contractual, and technical measures. Post-employment confidentiality remains subject to the HGB's non-compete provisions (Sections 74-75f), which require compensation (Karenzentschädigung) of at least 50 percent of the employee's last remuneration for non-compete obligations.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Trade Secrets Directive (2016/943)

Directive (EU) 2016/943 of June 8, 2016, represents the first comprehensive EU harmonization of trade secret law, establishing minimum standards for the definition, acquisition, use, and disclosure of trade secrets across member states.

**Harmonized Definition (Article 2):** A trade secret is information that is (a) secret (not generally known among or readily accessible to persons within the circles that normally deal with the kind of information in question), (b) has commercial value because it is secret, and (c) has been subject to reasonable steps under the circumstances by the person lawfully in control of the information to keep it secret.

### Lawful and Unlawful Conduct

**Article 3 (Lawful Acquisition):** Trade secret acquisition is lawful through independent discovery or creation, reverse engineering of a lawfully obtained product, exercise of worker information rights, and any other practice in conformity with honest commercial practices.

**Article 4 (Unlawful Acquisition):** Unlawful acquisition includes unauthorized access, appropriation, or copying. Unlawful use or disclosure includes use or disclosure by a person who acquired the secret unlawfully, who is in breach of a confidentiality agreement, or who is in breach of a contractual or other duty not to disclose.

### Exceptions (Article 5)

The Directive establishes important limitations on trade secret protection:

**Freedom of Expression (Article 5(a)):** Trade secret protection cannot override the exercise of the right to freedom of expression and information.

**Whistleblowing (Article 5(b)):** Disclosure for the purpose of revealing misconduct, wrongdoing, or illegal activity is lawful, provided the disclosure serves the general public interest.

**Worker Mobility (Recital 13):** The Directive explicitly states that it should not be understood as restricting employees' use of experience and skills honestly acquired in the normal course of their employment, protecting the principle of worker mobility.

### CJEU Trade Secret Jurisprudence

The CJEU has begun developing trade secret jurisprudence under the Directive. In Roquette Frères SA (Case C-694/20, 2022), the Court addressed the relationship between trade secret protection and access to court files, holding that national courts must balance trade secret protection against the right to effective judicial protection when deciding whether to disclose trade secrets included in court files.

### Relationship with Whistleblower Directive

Directive (EU) 2019/1937 (Whistleblower Protection Directive), applicable from December 17, 2021, provides that reporting persons who disclose trade secrets in the course of protected whistleblowing are exempt from trade secret liability under Directive 2016/943. This creates a clear hierarchy: where the conditions for protected whistleblowing are met, trade secret protection yields to the public interest in disclosure.

### ECHR and Trade Secrets

The ECHR has addressed trade secret protection primarily through Article 10 (freedom of expression) and Article 6 (fair trial). In Heinisch v. Germany (2011), the Court established that duties of confidentiality owed to employers must yield to the public interest in disclosure of information about serious wrongdoing, providing that the employee acts in good faith and external disclosure is proportionate.

## Comparative Analysis

### Definition Convergence

The definition of trade secrets has substantially converged across jurisdictions, with all four systems requiring three core elements: secrecy (not generally known or readily ascertainable), economic value derived from secrecy, and reasonable measures to maintain secrecy. The EU Directive and German GeschGehG explicitly codify the "reasonable measures" requirement, while the U.S. DTSA and Korean Act use similar language. The practical effect of this convergence is that businesses operating across jurisdictions can implement largely unified trade secret protection programs.

### Criminal versus Civil Emphasis

The jurisdictions differ in their emphasis on criminal versus civil enforcement. South Korea and the United States maintain the most severe criminal penalties, with maximum sentences of 15 years for foreign economic espionage. Germany's GeschGehG provides relatively modest criminal penalties (up to 3 years), reflecting a shift toward greater emphasis on civil remedies following the Directive's transposition. The EU Directive does not harmonize criminal penalties, leaving this to member states.

### Reverse Engineering

All four systems permit reverse engineering of lawfully obtained products as a matter of principle, but the practical scope of this exception varies. The EU Directive and GeschGehG explicitly recognize reverse engineering as lawful acquisition. U.S. law treats reverse engineering as a defense to misappropriation. Korean law permits reverse engineering but restricts it where the product was obtained through breach of confidence.

### Employee Mobility

The tension between trade secret protection and employee mobility is addressed differently across jurisdictions. Germany imposes the most burdensome requirements on employers seeking to restrict former employees, requiring compensation of at least 50 percent of salary for post-employment non-compete restrictions. The EU Directive explicitly protects worker mobility. U.S. law varies dramatically by state, with California prohibiting non-compete agreements entirely while other states enforce them subject to reasonableness review. South Korea applies a general reasonableness standard to post-employment restrictions.
