## Summary

The right to silence -- the privilege of an accused person to refuse to answer questions or provide information that might contribute to their own criminal prosecution -- is one of the most deeply rooted procedural protections in criminal law, tracing its origins to resistance against the inquisitorial practices of the Star Chamber and ecclesiastical courts in England and recognized today as a fundamental human right across all major legal systems. South Korea enshrines the right to silence in Article 12(2) of the Constitution (헌법 제12조 제2항), guaranteeing that no person shall be compelled to testify against themselves in criminal cases, implemented through Article 283-2 of the Criminal Procedure Act (형사소송법 제283조의2) which mandates judicial notification of the right and Article 244-3 requiring police notification during interrogation; the right was significantly strengthened by the Constitutional Court's 2004 decision on interrogation procedures and the 2007 Criminal Procedure Act reforms. The United States protects the right through the Fifth Amendment privilege against self-incrimination, most famously operationalized through the Miranda warnings established by Miranda v. Arizona, 384 U.S. 436 (1966), which require police to inform custodial suspects of their right to remain silent, that anything they say may be used against them, and their right to counsel; the privilege extends to all "compelled" testimonial communications and cannot be used to draw adverse inferences against the accused. Germany guarantees the right to silence (Schweigerecht) through the nemo tenetur se ipsum accusare principle, codified in Sections 136(1) and 243(5) of the Strafprozessordnung (StPO), which require that the accused be informed before any interrogation that they are free to make a statement or remain silent, with the Bundesgerichtshof having established that silence may not be used as evidence of guilt. The ECHR protects the right to silence as an implicit component of the fair trial guarantee under Article 6, with the Grand Chamber's decisions in Saunders v. United Kingdom (1996) and Ibrahim and Others v. United Kingdom (2016) establishing that the right is not absolute but that compelling self-incriminatory testimony fundamentally undermines the fairness of proceedings, while EU Directive 2016/343 on the presumption of innocence explicitly codifies the right to silence across all member states.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

**Constitution Article 12(2):** "No citizen shall be tortured or be compelled to testify against himself or herself in criminal cases."

### Criminal Procedure Act Provisions

| Provision | Content |
|-----------|---------|
| Art. 200(2) | Suspect must be informed of right to silence during voluntary attendance |
| Art. 244-3 | Police must inform suspect of right to silence before interrogation |
| Art. 283-2 | Court must inform defendant of right to silence at trial |
| Art. 289 | Defendant may refuse to answer individual questions during trial |
| Art. 309 | Confessions obtained through torture, violence, threat, or undue delay are inadmissible |
| Art. 310-2 | Confession alone is insufficient for conviction (corroboration requirement) |

### 2007 Reform

The 2007 amendments to the Criminal Procedure Act significantly strengthened the right to silence:

- **Video recording of interrogations (Art. 244-2):** All interrogations of suspects must be video-recorded, with the recording available to the defense. This reform was driven by documented cases of coerced confessions and ensures that the exercise of the right to silence is documented.
- **Enhanced notification requirements:** Officers must inform suspects not only of the right to silence but also of the right to counsel and the right to have counsel present during interrogation.
- **Exclusionary provisions:** Article 309 was strengthened to exclude not only confessions obtained through physical coercion but also those obtained through "undue prolongation of detention" (부당한 장기구금) or other improper methods.

### Judicial Interpretation

**Supreme Court Decision 2013Do11233:** The court held that a suspect's exercise of the right to silence cannot be used as evidence of guilt, either directly or as a basis for adverse inference. The prosecution cannot argue that the defendant's silence supports an inference of consciousness of guilt.

**Constitutional Court Decision 2003헌마282 (2004):** While primarily addressing the right to counsel, this decision also reinforced the right to silence by establishing that meaningful exercise of the right requires the presence of defense counsel during interrogation.

### Confession Culture and Reform

South Korea's criminal justice system has historically been characterized by a heavy reliance on confessions (자백), reflecting the inquisitorial heritage of the legal system. The National Human Rights Commission (국가인권위원회) has documented cases of coerced confessions and has advocated for stronger protections for the right to silence. The 2007 reforms and subsequent Constitutional Court decisions represent a significant shift toward an adversarial model that respects the defendant's right to remain silent.

## United States

### Legal Framework

**Fifth Amendment:** "No person... shall be compelled in any criminal case to be a witness against himself."

### Miranda v. Arizona (1966)

The Supreme Court held that the prosecution may not use statements stemming from custodial interrogation of a defendant unless it demonstrates the use of procedural safeguards effective to secure the privilege against self-incrimination. The now-famous Miranda warnings require officers to inform suspects that:

1. They have the right to remain silent.
2. Anything they say can and will be used against them in court.
3. They have the right to an attorney.
4. If they cannot afford an attorney, one will be provided for them.

### Key Supreme Court Decisions

| Case | Year | Holding |
|------|------|---------|
| Griffin v. California | 1965 | Prosecutor cannot comment on defendant's failure to testify |
| Miranda v. Arizona | 1966 | Custodial interrogation requires warnings |
| Harris v. New York | 1971 | Un-Mirandized statements admissible for impeachment |
| Doyle v. Ohio | 1976 | Post-Miranda silence cannot be used for impeachment |
| Salinas v. Texas | 2013 | Pre-Miranda, pre-custody silence may be used against defendant |
| Vega v. Tekoh | 2022 | Miranda violation alone does not support § 1983 civil suit |

### Salinas v. Texas (2013)

In a significant limitation, the Supreme Court held in Salinas v. Texas, 570 U.S. 178 (2013), that a suspect who is not in custody and has not been given Miranda warnings cannot rely on the Fifth Amendment privilege merely by remaining silent. To invoke the privilege in a non-custodial setting, the suspect must expressly assert it. The prosecution could use Salinas's silence during a voluntary police interview as evidence of guilt.

### Vega v. Tekoh (2022)

The Supreme Court held in Vega v. Tekoh, 597 U.S. 134 (2022), that a violation of the Miranda rules does not provide a basis for a claim under 42 U.S.C. Section 1983. The Court reasoned that Miranda established a prophylactic rule that is not itself a constitutional right but rather a safeguard designed to protect the Fifth Amendment privilege. This decision significantly limited the remedies available for Miranda violations.

### Document Production and the Act of Production Doctrine

The Fifth Amendment protects against compelled "testimonial" communications but does not protect against the production of pre-existing documents whose creation was not compelled. However, the "act of production" doctrine, established in Fisher v. United States (1976) and United States v. Hubbell (2000), recognizes that the act of producing documents can itself be testimonial -- by acknowledging the existence, authenticity, and possession of the documents.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's right to silence is derived from the nemo tenetur se ipsum accusare principle (no one is bound to accuse themselves) and is codified in the Strafprozessordnung.

**Section 136(1) StPO:** "At the beginning of the first examination, the accused shall be informed of the offense he or she is charged with and the applicable penal provisions. He or she shall be advised that the law grants the right to comment on the charges or not to make a statement on the matter."

**Section 243(5) StPO:** At the opening of the trial, the presiding judge must inform the defendant of the right to make a statement or to remain silent regarding the charges.

### Key Principles

| Principle | Content |
|-----------|---------|
| Nemo tenetur | No person is obligated to contribute to their own prosecution |
| Schweigerecht | Right to remain completely silent; no obligation to answer any questions |
| No adverse inference | Silence cannot be used as evidence of guilt (BGH 5 StR 15/99) |
| Free from coercion | Section 136a StPO: confession obtained through maltreatment, fatigue, physical interference, drugs, torture, deception, or hypnosis is inadmissible |
| Notification requirement | Must be informed before every interrogation; failure to inform renders statements inadmissible |

### BGH Jurisprudence

**BGH 5 StR 15/99 (1999):** The Bundesgerichtshof established that a defendant's exercise of the right to silence may not be used to draw adverse inferences of guilt. The court emphasized that the right to silence is not merely a procedural formality but a fundamental protection that would be undermined if silence could be held against the accused.

**BGH 5 StR 73/06 (2007):** The court addressed the scope of the right to silence in the context of pre-trial police questioning. The court held that where a suspect has been questioned by undercover police officers without being informed of the right to silence, any statements obtained are inadmissible, even if the suspect was not formally in custody at the time.

### Section 136a StPO: Prohibited Methods of Interrogation

Section 136a provides comprehensive protection against coerced statements:

| Prohibited Method | Effect |
|------------------|--------|
| Maltreatment (Misshandlung) | Statement absolutely inadmissible |
| Fatigue (Ermüdung) | Statement absolutely inadmissible |
| Physical interference | Statement absolutely inadmissible |
| Administration of substances (drugs) | Statement absolutely inadmissible |
| Torture (Folter) | Statement absolutely inadmissible |
| Deception (Täuschung) | Statement absolutely inadmissible |
| Hypnosis | Statement absolutely inadmissible |
| Threat with an impermissible measure | Statement absolutely inadmissible |
| Promise of a benefit not provided by law | Statement absolutely inadmissible |

The exclusion of statements obtained through prohibited methods is absolute (absolutes Beweisverwertungsverbot) -- unlike the general balancing approach for other procedural violations.

### Partial Silence

German law recognizes the right to selective or partial silence. The defendant may choose to answer some questions and remain silent on others, and no adverse inference may be drawn from the selective exercise of the right. However, the BGH has noted that a defendant's inconsistent statements -- answering some questions and remaining silent on related ones -- may be considered in the overall assessment of credibility.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Article 6

The right to silence is not explicitly mentioned in Article 6 but is recognized as an implicit component of the fair trial guarantee.

**Saunders v. United Kingdom (Application No. 19187/91, 1996):** The Court held that the use in criminal proceedings of statements compelled from the applicant during a DTI investigation violated Article 6. The Court established that the right not to incriminate oneself is "primarily concerned with respecting the will of an accused person to remain silent" and is closely linked to the presumption of innocence.

**Murray v. United Kingdom (Application No. 18731/91, 1996):** The Court held that drawing adverse inferences from a defendant's silence does not automatically violate Article 6, but that the weight of such inferences must be limited and the conviction must not be based solely or mainly on the defendant's silence. This decision permits a more qualified approach to adverse inferences than the absolute prohibition in the United States and Germany.

**Bykov v. Russia [GC] (Application No. 4378/02, 2009):** The Court addressed the use of covert recording of conversations with the suspect, holding that the right against self-incrimination is primarily engaged by compulsion and that evidence obtained through deception, without formal interrogation, does not automatically violate Article 6.

### EU Directive 2016/343 on the Presumption of Innocence

**Article 7 (Right to Remain Silent):** "Member States shall ensure that suspects and accused persons have the right to remain silent in relation to the criminal offence that they are suspected or accused of having committed."

**Article 7(4):** "Member States may allow their judicial authorities to take into account the cooperative behaviour of suspects and accused persons when sentencing." This provision permits sentence reductions for cooperation but does not authorize adverse inferences from silence.

**Article 7(5):** "The exercise by suspects and accused persons of the right to remain silent shall not be used against them and shall not be considered as evidence that they committed the criminal offence concerned."

### EU Directive 2012/13/EU on the Right to Information

Article 3 requires that suspects be informed of the right to remain silent promptly and in a language they understand. The Directive requires that this information be provided in writing (Letter of Rights) when the suspect is detained.

## Comparative Analysis

### Adverse Inferences

The most significant divergence concerns whether adverse inferences may be drawn from silence. The United States (Griffin) and Germany absolutely prohibit the use of silence as evidence of guilt. South Korea follows the same approach. The ECHR (Murray) permits limited adverse inferences under carefully circumscribed conditions. The EU Directive 2016/343 prohibits using silence as evidence of guilt.

### Pre-Custody Silence

A critical gap exists regarding pre-custody silence. The United States (Salinas) permits the use of pre-custody, pre-Miranda silence as evidence unless the suspect expressly invokes the privilege. Germany and South Korea extend the protection to all pre-trial silence. The ECHR assesses pre-custody silence under the overall fairness standard.

### Compelled vs. Voluntary Statements

All jurisdictions agree that physically coerced statements are absolutely inadmissible. However, the boundaries of "compulsion" differ. Germany's Section 136a provides the most comprehensive prohibition, extending to fatigue, deception, and promises of unlawful benefits. The U.S. voluntariness standard (Dickerson v. United States, 2000) applies a totality-of-circumstances test. The ECHR focuses on whether compulsion destroyed the will of the accused.
