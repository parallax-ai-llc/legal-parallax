## Summary

Antitrust law -- the body of regulation designed to promote market competition and prevent monopolistic abuses -- is a cornerstone of modern economic governance, yet its scope, enforcement mechanisms, and penalties differ markedly across jurisdictions. South Korea regulates anticompetitive conduct primarily through the Monopoly Regulation and Fair Trade Act (MRFTA), enforced by the Korea Fair Trade Commission (KFTC), with administrative fines of up to 10 percent of relevant turnover and criminal penalties including imprisonment of up to three years for certain violations such as cartel participation. The United States relies on three foundational statutes -- the Sherman Act (1890), the Clayton Act (1914), and the Federal Trade Commission Act (1914) -- uniquely combining criminal prosecution (up to 10 years imprisonment and $100 million in corporate fines under the Sherman Act) with private treble-damages litigation. Germany enforces competition law through the Gesetz gegen Wettbewerbsbeschränkungen (GWB -- Act against Restraints of Competition), administered by the Bundeskartellamt (Federal Cartel Office), imposing administrative fines of up to 10 percent of worldwide group turnover plus a further 10 percent disgorgement of illicit gains, while reserving criminal liability for bid-rigging offenses under Section 298 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB). At the supranational level, the European Union enforces Articles 101 and 102 of the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union (TFEU) through the European Commission under Council Regulation (EC) No 1/2003, with fines reaching up to 10 percent of worldwide annual turnover, while the European Court of Human Rights (ECHR) has shaped antitrust enforcement through landmark rulings confirming that competition proceedings engage Article 6 fair-trial protections.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's competition regime is governed by the Monopoly Regulation and Fair Trade Act (MRFTA, also known as the Fair Trade Act), first enacted in 1980 and comprehensively amended multiple times, most recently through major amendments taking effect in the second half of 2024. The MRFTA establishes four principal categories of prohibition: (1) unfair collaborative acts (cartels and anticompetitive agreements), (2) abuse of market-dominant position, (3) unfair trade practices, and (4) mergers and acquisitions that substantially lessen competition.

The Korea Fair Trade Commission (KFTC), a ministerial-level central administrative organization under the authority of the Prime Minister, formulates and administers competition policies, investigates potential violations, and adjudicates antitrust cases. The KFTC holds exclusive authority to refer antitrust cases for criminal prosecution -- private individuals and the public prosecutor cannot independently initiate criminal antitrust proceedings without a KFTC referral.

### Prohibited Conduct

**Cartels (Unfair Collaborative Acts):** Article 40 of the MRFTA prohibits agreements between competitors to fix prices, limit output, divide markets, restrict facilities, limit types or specifications of products, jointly manage or establish a company, or interfere with each other's business activities.

**Abuse of Market Dominance:** Article 5 prohibits market-dominant enterprises from engaging in conduct such as unreasonably setting, maintaining, or changing the price of goods or services; unreasonably controlling the sale of goods or provision of services; unreasonably interfering with the business activities of other enterprises; or unreasonably impeding new market entry.

**Unfair Trade Practices:** Articles 45-46 address a broad range of practices including refusal to deal, discriminatory treatment, exclusionary conduct, and tying arrangements.

### Penalties

Administrative surcharges (과징금) constitute the KFTC's primary enforcement tool:

- **Cartel violations:** Up to 10 percent of the relevant turnover related to the infringing conduct. For cartels where turnover cannot be determined, the KFTC may impose a fixed surcharge of up to 2 billion KRW.
- **Abuse of dominance:** Up to 3 percent of the relevant turnover (up to 6 percent for repeat violations).
- **Unfair trade practices:** Up to 2 percent of relevant turnover.

Criminal penalties apply when the KFTC refers cases for prosecution:

- Cartel participation: imprisonment of up to 3 years or a fine of up to 200 million KRW.
- Abuse of dominance or unfair trade practices: imprisonment of up to 2 years or a fine of up to 150 million KRW.

### Recent Developments

The KFTC has been actively expanding its enforcement reach into digital markets. In September 2024, the Commission revealed its strategy for tackling monopolies on online platforms, with a significant overhaul of the MRFTA targeting dominant platforms and self-preferencing behavior. The proposed "Fairness Act" would grant the KFTC power to fine firms up to 10 percent of their global sales and impose broad restrictions and obligations on online platforms.

### Landmark Cases

**Qualcomm (2009, 2016-2023):** The KFTC imposed a fine of 273.1 billion KRW (approximately $218 million) in 2009 for Qualcomm's abuse of its dominant position in CDMA modem chips. In a second, larger action in 2016, the KFTC imposed its largest-ever fine of over 1 trillion KRW (approximately $873 million) against Qualcomm for unfair licensing practices related to standard-essential patents. The Seoul High Court upheld the fine, and in April 2023, the Supreme Court affirmed the decision.

**Google Android (2021):** The KFTC fined Google 207.4 billion KRW (approximately $177 million) for abusing its market dominance by restricting smartphone manufacturers -- including Samsung Electronics and LG Electronics -- from installing customized or alternative operating systems through anti-fragmentation agreements (AFAs).

**LNG Bid-Rigging (2019):** The KFTC fined 13 Korean firms for openly anticompetitive bid-rigging behavior in the liquefied natural gas (LNG) procurement sector, demonstrating continued vigilance against traditional cartel conduct.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States possesses the world's oldest and most developed antitrust regime, built on three foundational federal statutes:

**Sherman Antitrust Act (1890):** The cornerstone of American antitrust law. Section 1 (15 U.S.C. § 1) prohibits "[e]very contract, combination in the form of trust or otherwise, or conspiracy, in restraint of trade or commerce." Section 2 (15 U.S.C. § 2) makes it unlawful to "monopolize, or attempt to monopolize, or combine or conspire with any other person or persons, to monopolize any part of the trade or commerce among the several States." Violations of the Sherman Act are criminal felonies.

**Clayton Act (1914):** Addresses specific anticompetitive practices not clearly covered by the Sherman Act. Section 3 prohibits exclusive dealing and tying arrangements. Section 7 prohibits mergers and acquisitions "where in any line of commerce or in any activity affecting commerce in any section of the country, the effect of such acquisition may be substantially to lessen competition, or to tend to create a monopoly." Unlike the Sherman Act, Clayton Act violations carry only civil penalties.

**Federal Trade Commission Act (1914):** Section 5 broadly prohibits "unfair methods of competition" and "unfair or deceptive acts or practices," giving the FTC authority to address conduct that may not rise to the level of a Sherman or Clayton Act violation.

### Enforcement Structure

The United States uniquely employs a dual-agency enforcement model:

- **Department of Justice (DOJ) Antitrust Division:** Holds exclusive authority for criminal prosecution of antitrust violations. Also brings civil enforcement actions, particularly in monopolization and merger cases.
- **Federal Trade Commission (FTC):** Exercises administrative enforcement under Section 5 of the FTC Act. Shares civil enforcement authority with the DOJ on mergers under the Hart-Scott-Rodino Act.

Private enforcement is a distinctive feature of American antitrust law. Section 4 of the Clayton Act permits "any person who shall be injured in his business or property by reason of anything forbidden in the antitrust laws" to sue for treble damages (three times the actual damages sustained) plus reasonable attorney's fees.

### Criminal Penalties

The Antitrust Criminal Penalty Enhancement and Reform Act of 2004 substantially increased Sherman Act penalties:

- **Corporations:** Maximum fine of $100 million per offense, or alternatively, twice the gross pecuniary gain derived from the crime or twice the gross pecuniary loss caused to victims -- whichever is greatest.
- **Individuals:** Maximum fine of $1 million per offense and/or imprisonment of up to 10 years (increased from 3 years by the 2004 Act).

Criminal prosecution is generally limited to per se violations -- price fixing, bid rigging, market allocation, and wage-fixing agreements among competitors -- where the anticompetitive nature of the conduct is clear and intentional.

### DOJ Amnesty Program

Since 1993, the DOJ has operated a Corporate Leniency (Amnesty) Program under which the first corporation to report its participation in a cartel and cooperate fully with the investigation receives a complete pass from criminal prosecution. The program has been instrumental in detecting and prosecuting international cartels.

### Landmark Cases

**Standard Oil Co. of New Jersey v. United States (1911):** The Supreme Court ordered the dissolution of John D. Rockefeller's Standard Oil trust into 34 independent companies, establishing the "rule of reason" standard for evaluating restraints of trade under Section 1 of the Sherman Act.

**United States v. AT&T (1982):** The consent decree resulted in the breakup of the American Telephone & Telegraph Company into seven regional Bell operating companies ("Baby Bells"), ending AT&T's monopoly over local telephone service while allowing it to retain its long-distance business.

**United States v. Microsoft Corp. (2001):** Judge Thomas Penfield Jackson found that Microsoft had violated Sections 1 and 2 of the Sherman Act by monopolizing the market for Intel-compatible PC operating systems and tying its Internet Explorer browser to the Windows operating system. Although the trial court initially ordered a breakup, the D.C. Circuit reversed the structural remedy, and the case was ultimately resolved through a consent decree imposing behavioral restrictions.

**United States v. Google LLC (2024-present):** In August 2024, Judge Amit P. Mehta of the U.S. District Court for the District of Columbia ruled that Google had maintained an illegal monopoly in general search services and general search text advertising through exclusive distribution agreements -- particularly payments of billions of dollars annually to Apple and other device manufacturers to secure default search engine status. Remedies proceedings are ongoing.

**United States v. Apple Inc. (2024-present):** The DOJ and 16 states filed suit alleging that Apple illegally monopolized the smartphone market in violation of Section 2 of the Sherman Act by employing exclusionary practices that locked consumers into its ecosystem, inhibited cross-platform interoperability, and suppressed competition from rival technologies.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's competition law regime is governed primarily by the Gesetz gegen Wettbewerbsbeschränkungen (GWB -- Act against Restraints of Competition), which came into force on January 1, 1958. The GWB has undergone eleven major amendments, with the 10th amendment (2021) significantly expanding provisions for digital markets and the 11th amendment (2023) introducing new sector inquiry powers.

The GWB closely aligns with EU competition law and covers four principal areas:

1. **Anticompetitive agreements** (GWB §§ 1-3): Mirrors Article 101 TFEU, prohibiting agreements between undertakings, decisions by associations of undertakings, and concerted practices that have the object or effect of preventing, restricting, or distorting competition.
2. **Abuse of market dominance** (GWB §§ 18-21): Mirrors Article 102 TFEU, prohibiting dominant undertakings from engaging in exploitative or exclusionary conduct. Uniquely, German law also prohibits abusive practices by undertakings with "relative" or "superior" market power -- a concept not found in EU law.
3. **Merger control** (GWB §§ 35-43): Requires prior notification and clearance for transactions exceeding specified turnover thresholds.
4. **Sector inquiries and new competition tools** (GWB §§ 32e-32g): The 11th amendment empowered the Bundeskartellamt to order remedies following a sector inquiry where it identifies significant and continuing market dysfunction, without first having to demonstrate a specific violation of law.

### Enforcement Authority

The Bundeskartellamt (Federal Cartel Office), headquartered in Bonn, is the primary competition enforcement authority. It operates independently within the federal government's administrative structure and cooperates with the European Commission and national competition authorities of other EU member states through the European Competition Network (ECN). State-level competition authorities (Landeskartellbehörden) enforce competition law in cases of purely regional significance.

### Penalties

German antitrust enforcement relies predominantly on administrative fines (Bußgelder):

- **Undertakings:** Fines of up to 10 percent of worldwide group turnover in the preceding financial year. Additionally, the Bundeskartellamt may order disgorgement of economic benefits derived from the infringement, up to a further 10 percent of turnover -- meaning total financial exposure can reach 20 percent of annual turnover.
- **Individuals:** Fines of up to €1 million for natural persons involved in antitrust infringements.
- **Procedural violations:** Fines of up to €100,000 for individuals and up to 1 percent of group worldwide turnover for undertakings for obstruction of investigations, failure to provide information, or interference with dawn raids.

**Criminal liability** is limited in scope. Bid rigging in public procurement constitutes a criminal offense under Section 298 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), carrying imprisonment of up to five years or a fine. Other antitrust infringements -- including price fixing cartels, abuse of dominance, and vertical restraints -- are treated as administrative (Ordnungswidrigkeiten) rather than criminal offenses.

### Private Enforcement

Section 33a of the GWB provides a right of action for any person harmed by an antitrust infringement to claim damages. The EU Damages Directive (2014/104/EU), transposed into German law, has further strengthened private enforcement by establishing a rebuttable presumption that cartel infringements cause harm, facilitating access to evidence, and harmonizing limitation periods.

### Landmark Cases

**Facebook/Meta Data Collection (2019-2020):** In February 2019, the Bundeskartellamt found that Facebook (now Meta) had abused its dominant position in the German social networking market (with over 90 percent market share) by conditioning use of its platform on extensive cross-service and cross-device data collection without meaningful user consent. In June 2020, the Bundesgerichtshof (Federal Court of Justice) upheld the Bundeskartellamt's assessment in full, establishing a precedent linking data protection violations to antitrust abuse of dominance.

**Steel Cartel (2018):** The Bundeskartellamt imposed fines totaling approximately €205 million on several German steel manufacturers for operating a long-running price-fixing and market-sharing cartel in the steel sector.

**Sausage Casings Cartel (2014):** Fines of approximately €116 million were imposed on manufacturers of artificial sausage casings for coordinating prices and allocating customers across European markets.

### Digital Markets Regulation

Germany was a pioneer in expanding competition law to address digital market power. Section 19a of the GWB (introduced by the 10th amendment in 2021) gives the Bundeskartellamt authority to designate companies of "paramount significance for competition across markets" and impose specific behavioral obligations -- a precursor to the EU Digital Markets Act (DMA). The Bundeskartellamt has designated Alphabet/Google, Meta/Facebook, Amazon, Apple, and Microsoft under this provision.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Legal Framework

The European Union's competition law regime is anchored in the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union (TFEU):

**Article 101 TFEU** prohibits agreements between undertakings, decisions by associations of undertakings, and concerted practices that may affect trade between Member States and which have as their object or effect the prevention, restriction, or distortion of competition within the internal market. Article 101(2) renders such agreements automatically void. Article 101(3) provides an exemption for agreements that contribute to improving production, promoting technical or economic progress, or benefiting consumers, provided the restrictions are indispensable and do not eliminate competition for a substantial part of the products in question.

**Article 102 TFEU** prohibits any abuse by one or more undertakings of a dominant position within the internal market insofar as it may affect trade between Member States. Examples of prohibited conduct include imposing unfair purchase or selling prices, limiting production, applying dissimilar conditions to equivalent transactions, and tying.

### Enforcement under Regulation 1/2003

Council Regulation (EC) No 1/2003 modernized the enforcement of Articles 101 and 102, replacing the prior notification and authorization system with a directly applicable exception system. Key features include:

- **Decentralized enforcement:** National competition authorities (NCAs) and national courts may directly apply Articles 101 and 102, not just the European Commission.
- **Commission investigative powers:** The Commission may conduct on-site inspections (dawn raids), request information from undertakings, take statements, and seal business premises.
- **Commitment decisions:** Under Article 9, the Commission may accept binding commitments from undertakings to address competition concerns without making a formal finding of infringement.

### EU Penalties

**Substantive fines** (Article 23(2) of Regulation 1/2003): The European Commission may impose fines of up to 10 percent of the total worldwide annual turnover of the undertaking's corporate group in the preceding business year. The 2006 Guidelines on Fines establish a methodology based on the value of sales affected by the infringement, the gravity and duration of the infringement, and aggravating or mitigating circumstances.

**Periodic penalty payments** (Article 24): The Commission may impose fines of up to 5 percent of average daily worldwide turnover for each day an undertaking fails to comply with a Commission decision.

**Procedural fines** (Article 23(1)): Fines of up to 1 percent of worldwide aggregate group turnover for supplying incorrect, incomplete, or misleading information; failing to comply with information requests; or obstructing inspections.

### EU Leniency and Cartel Settlement

The European Commission operates a leniency program under which the first undertaking to reveal its participation in a cartel and provide sufficient evidence receives full immunity from fines. Subsequent applicants may receive fine reductions of up to 50 percent. The ECN+ Directive (Directive 2019/1) harmonized leniency programs across all EU Member States.

A cartel settlement procedure allows undertakings to acknowledge their participation in a cartel in exchange for a 10 percent reduction in fines.

### Landmark EU Cases

**Google Shopping (2017, upheld 2024):** The European Commission fined Google €2.42 billion for abusing its dominant position in general internet search by systematically favoring its own comparison shopping service (Google Shopping) over those of competitors. In September 2024, the Court of Justice of the European Union upheld the fine in full.

**Google Android (2018):** The Commission imposed a then-record fine of €4.34 billion on Google for imposing illegal restrictions on Android mobile device manufacturers and mobile network operators, including requiring pre-installation of Google Search and Chrome as conditions for licensing the Google Play app store.

**Google AdSense (2019, modified 2024):** The Commission fined Google €1.49 billion for abusing its dominant position in online search advertising intermediation. In September 2024, the General Court largely upheld the finding but reduced the fine.

**Apple Music Streaming (2024):** In March 2024, the European Commission imposed its first antitrust fine against Apple -- €1.84 billion -- for abusing its dominant position in the market for the distribution of music streaming apps through its App Store, by imposing restrictions (anti-steering provisions) that prevented app developers from informing iOS users about alternative subscription options.

**Intel (2009, partially annulled 2022):** The Commission originally fined Intel €1.06 billion for abusing its dominant position by granting exclusivity rebates to computer manufacturers. The General Court annulled the decision in 2022, finding that the Commission had failed to properly apply the "as efficient competitor" test, and ordered a reassessment.

**Trucks Cartel (2016-2017):** The Commission imposed fines totaling approximately €3.8 billion on major European truck manufacturers -- including Daimler, DAF, Iveco, MAN, and Volvo/Renault -- for operating a cartel involving collusion on pricing and gross price increases for medium and heavy trucks over a 14-year period.

### ECHR and Fundamental Rights

The European Court of Human Rights has played a significant role in shaping the procedural standards applicable to competition law enforcement through its interpretation of the European Convention on Human Rights:

**A. Menarini Diagnostics S.R.L. v. Italy (2011):** In this landmark judgment, the ECHR confirmed that antitrust proceedings are "criminal" in nature for the purposes of Article 6 of the Convention (right to a fair trial), applying the Engel criteria. The Italian competition authority (AGCM) had fined Menarini €6 million for price-fixing and market-sharing in the diabetes diagnosis test market. The Court held that while the infringement was formally classified as administrative under Italian law, the nature of the offense and the severity of the potential penalty rendered it criminal for Convention purposes. However, the Court found no violation of Article 6 because the Italian administrative courts had exercised sufficiently full judicial review -- examining both the facts and the proportionality of the penalty -- to satisfy the requirement of access to a "tribunal with full jurisdiction."

**B. Société Stenuit v. France (1992):** The European Commission of Human Rights (the precursor body) found that competition law fines imposed by the French Competition Council were of a "criminal" nature under the Engel criteria, recognizing that even administrative competition penalties engage Article 6 protections.

**C. Jussila v. Finland (2006):** The Grand Chamber established that while Article 6 criminal-limb guarantees apply to competition proceedings, the full panoply of procedural safeguards may be modulated for regulatory offenses that do not carry the "hard core" stigma of traditional criminal law -- permitting administrative adjudication followed by full judicial review rather than requiring a criminal trial.

**Key ECHR Principles in Antitrust:**

- **Right to a fair and public hearing** by an independent and impartial tribunal (Article 6(1)).
- **Presumption of innocence** (Article 6(2)) -- particularly relevant in leniency proceedings and burden-of-proof questions.
- **Right against self-incrimination** -- tension with the extensive information-gathering powers of competition authorities.
- **Ne bis in idem (double jeopardy)** -- Article 4 of Protocol No. 7 prohibits being tried or punished twice for the same offense; this principle has been raised in cases where an undertaking faces parallel proceedings by a national competition authority and the European Commission.

### Digital Markets Act (2022)

Regulation (EU) 2022/1925 -- the Digital Markets Act (DMA) -- entered into force on November 1, 2022, and became fully applicable on March 6, 2024. The DMA complements traditional antitrust enforcement by imposing ex ante obligations on designated "gatekeepers" -- large online platforms that serve as important gateways for business users to reach end users. Non-compliance may result in fines of up to 10 percent of worldwide annual turnover, rising to 20 percent for repeat infringements, and in extreme cases, structural remedies including divestiture.

## Comparative Analysis

### Philosophical Approaches

The four jurisdictions reflect distinct philosophical approaches to competition policy:

- **United States:** Rooted in the consumer welfare standard, American antitrust law historically intervenes only when conduct demonstrably harms consumer welfare -- typically through higher prices or reduced output. Criminal enforcement reflects a distinctly punitive orientation toward hardcore cartel conduct.
- **South Korea:** Combines consumer welfare concerns with a strong developmental-state tradition, using competition policy as an instrument to manage the influence of large conglomerates (chaebol) and promote fair market participation.
- **Germany/EU:** Embraces a broader ordoliberal tradition that views competition as an institutional value deserving protection in its own right -- the competitive process itself is protected, not merely consumer welfare outcomes. The EU's approach increasingly incorporates fairness and market-contestability objectives, particularly in digital markets.
- **ECHR:** Functions as a constitutional overlay, ensuring that antitrust enforcement -- regardless of its administrative classification -- respects fundamental rights including fair trial, proportionality, and legal certainty.

### Criminal vs. Administrative Enforcement

A fundamental divergence exists in whether antitrust violations are treated as criminal or administrative offenses:

| Jurisdiction | Criminal Enforcement | Administrative Enforcement |
|-------------|---------------------|---------------------------|
| **United States** | Yes -- Sherman Act felonies (price fixing, bid rigging, market allocation) with imprisonment up to 10 years | FTC administrative proceedings; DOJ civil suits |
| **South Korea** | Limited -- criminal referral by KFTC for cartel participation (up to 3 years) | Primary enforcement mechanism; KFTC surcharges |
| **Germany** | Only bid rigging (StGB § 298, up to 5 years) | Primary mechanism; Bundeskartellamt administrative fines |
| **EU** | No EU-level criminal enforcement | European Commission fines under Regulation 1/2003 |

The United States stands alone in treating antitrust violations as serious criminal offenses warranting substantial imprisonment. This approach has been credited with creating a powerful deterrent effect but has also been criticized for potentially over-deterring legitimate business conduct.

### Convergence and Divergence in Digital Markets

All four jurisdictions have moved toward strengthened regulation of digital platform markets, though through different mechanisms:

- **South Korea:** Proposed Fairness Act targeting online platforms with enhanced KFTC enforcement powers.
- **United States:** Major monopolization cases against Google and Apple under existing Sherman Act framework.
- **Germany:** Pioneer Section 19a GWB provisions for undertakings of paramount cross-market significance.
- **EU:** Digital Markets Act establishing ex ante obligations on designated gatekeepers.

### Private Enforcement

The availability and effectiveness of private antitrust enforcement varies considerably:

- **United States:** The most developed private enforcement regime, with treble damages, class actions, and broad discovery rules making private litigation a major component of antitrust enforcement.
- **South Korea:** Private damages actions are permitted but relatively infrequent, with claimants facing significant evidentiary burdens.
- **Germany:** Private enforcement has been strengthened by the transposition of the EU Damages Directive, including a presumption that cartel infringements cause harm.
- **EU:** The Damages Directive (2014/104/EU) establishes minimum harmonized standards for private damages actions across all Member States, but practical enforcement still varies significantly by jurisdiction.
