## Summary

Extradition -- the formal process by which one sovereign state surrenders a person accused or convicted of a crime to another state for prosecution or punishment -- represents a critical intersection of international cooperation, national sovereignty, and individual rights protection in the global criminal justice system. South Korea's extradition framework is governed by the Act on Extradition (범죄인 인도법), originally enacted in 1988 and substantially amended in 2012, which establishes dual criminality and specialty requirements and operates through a hybrid judicial-executive process in which the Seoul High Court conducts an extradition hearing and the Minister of Justice makes the final surrender decision; Korea currently maintains bilateral extradition treaties with over 30 countries, including the United States (1998), Australia (1990), and China (2000). The United States operates one of the world's most extensive extradition networks through 18 U.S.C. Sections 3181-3196, supplemented by over 100 bilateral extradition treaties and several multilateral conventions, with the process requiring judicial certification under 18 U.S.C. Section 3184 that the evidence is sufficient to sustain the charge followed by executive discretion by the Secretary of State -- a system tested by high-profile cases such as the Julian Assange extradition proceedings (2019-2024). Germany's extradition law is governed by the Act on International Cooperation in Criminal Matters (Gesetz über die internationale Rechtshilfe in Strafsachen, IRG), with a critical constitutional limitation in Article 16(2) of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz) that prohibits the extradition of German nationals except to EU member states or international courts, subject to rule-of-law conditions established by the Federal Constitutional Court. At the European level, the European Arrest Warrant (EAW), established by Council Framework Decision 2002/584/JHA and operational since January 1, 2004, has fundamentally transformed extradition within the EU by replacing traditional extradition with a streamlined judicial surrender mechanism based on mutual recognition, while the European Court of Human Rights has developed critical case law constraining extradition where it would expose the person to treatment violating Article 3 (prohibition of torture), notably in Soering v. United Kingdom (1989).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's extradition system is governed by the Act on Extradition (범죄인 인도법), originally enacted on August 5, 1988, and significantly amended on March 21, 2012. The Act establishes the substantive and procedural requirements for both incoming and outgoing extradition requests and operates in conjunction with bilateral extradition treaties concluded with individual countries.

### Treaty Network

South Korea has concluded bilateral extradition treaties with over 30 countries, including:

| Treaty Partner | Year of Entry into Force |
|----------------|--------------------------|
| United States | 1999 |
| Australia | 1991 |
| Canada | 1995 |
| China | 2002 |
| Japan | 2002 |
| France | 2007 |
| Spain | 2004 |
| Thailand | 1998 |

### Substantive Requirements

The Act on Extradition establishes several mandatory conditions:

**Dual Criminality (이중범죄성):** The offense must be punishable by imprisonment of one year or more under the laws of both Korea and the requesting state (Article 6).

**Specialty Principle (특정성의 원칙):** The extradited person may only be tried for the offense for which extradition was granted (Article 15).

**Political Offense Exception (정치범 불인도 원칙):** Extradition shall be refused for purely political offenses, though the exception does not extend to acts of terrorism or genocide (Article 8).

**Non Bis In Idem:** Extradition is refused where the person has already been tried and acquitted or convicted for the same offense in Korea (Article 9).

### Procedure

The extradition process follows a hybrid judicial-executive model. Upon receipt of a formal extradition request through diplomatic channels, the Minister of Justice refers the case to the Seoul High Court (서울고등법원), which conducts an extradition hearing (인도심사) to determine whether the legal requirements for extradition are met. If the Seoul High Court issues a favorable ruling, the Minister of Justice retains discretion to approve or refuse the surrender. The person sought has the right to legal representation and may appeal the Seoul High Court's ruling to the Supreme Court.

### Notable Cases

**Yoo Byung-eun (2014):** Following the sinking of the MV Sewol ferry, international cooperation was sought to locate assets and associates of the Chonghaejin Marine Company owner, involving extradition discussions with multiple jurisdictions, though the suspect died before formal proceedings concluded.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States operates the world's most extensive bilateral extradition network, with treaties in force with over 100 countries. The domestic statutory framework is found in 18 U.S.C. Sections 3181-3196, supplemented by individual treaty provisions.

### Procedure

**Judicial Phase (18 U.S.C. Section 3184):** An authorized judge or magistrate judge conducts an extradition hearing to determine whether the extradition request complies with the applicable treaty, whether the person before the court is the person sought, and whether there is probable cause to believe the person committed the offense charged. This certification proceeding is not a trial; the rules of evidence do not strictly apply, and the fugitive has limited rights to present evidence.

**Executive Phase:** If the judge certifies the case as extraditable, the Secretary of State exercises final discretion on whether to surrender the person. The Secretary may consider humanitarian concerns, the likelihood of torture, and diplomatic factors. The Secretary's decision is not subject to judicial review except under narrow circumstances.

### Constitutional Limitations

The Supreme Court has established several constitutional constraints on extradition:

**Factor v. Laubenheimer, 290 U.S. 276 (1933):** Established that extradition treaties are to be liberally construed to effectuate their purpose of surrender of fugitives.

**Munaf v. Geren, 553 U.S. 674 (2008):** The Court held that federal courts have habeas jurisdiction over U.S. citizens held abroad by U.S. forces but that transfer to a foreign sovereign for criminal prosecution does not require the same protections as extradition.

### High-Profile Cases

**Julian Assange (2019-2024):** The United States sought Assange's extradition from the United Kingdom on 17 counts under the Espionage Act and one count of computer intrusion. After years of litigation in British courts, including challenges based on Article 3 ECHR (risk of solitary confinement and suicide), the case concluded in June 2024 with a plea agreement in the U.S. District Court for the Northern Mariana Islands.

**Meng Wanzhou (2018-2021):** The arrest of Huawei CFO Meng Wanzhou in Canada at the request of the United States on fraud charges related to Iran sanctions generated significant diplomatic tension between the U.S., Canada, and China, illustrating the geopolitical dimensions of extradition.

### Rule of Non-Inquiry

Under the "rule of non-inquiry," U.S. courts traditionally decline to examine the fairness of the requesting state's criminal justice system or the conditions of confinement the fugitive may face, leaving such considerations to the executive branch. This doctrine has been criticized by commentators as inadequately protecting human rights.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's extradition law is governed by the Act on International Cooperation in Criminal Matters (Gesetz über die internationale Rechtshilfe in Strafsachen -- IRG), which addresses both traditional extradition to non-EU states and the European Arrest Warrant process with EU member states. The Act operates under significant constitutional constraints imposed by Article 16(2) of the Basic Law.

### Constitutional Prohibition on Extradition of Nationals

Article 16(2) of the Basic Law provides: "No German may be extradited to a foreign country. A different regulation may be made by law for extraditions to a member state of the European Union or to an international court, provided that the principles of the rule of law are observed." This provision was amended in 2000 specifically to permit implementation of the European Arrest Warrant while maintaining rule-of-law safeguards.

### Federal Constitutional Court Jurisprudence

**BVerfGE 113, 273 (2005) -- European Arrest Warrant Case:** The Federal Constitutional Court struck down Germany's first implementing law for the European Arrest Warrant (Europäisches Haftbefehlsgesetz) as unconstitutional. The Court held that the law failed to provide adequate protection for German nationals, particularly regarding the right not to be extradited under Article 16(2) GG. The Court required that the implementing law include mandatory grounds for refusal when the offense was committed wholly or partly on German territory and when the surrendered German national could not serve any sentence imposed in Germany. A revised implementing law was enacted in 2006.

**BVerfG, 2 BvR 2735/14 (2015) -- Solange III / Identity Review:** The Court established that it would review EAW surrender orders to ensure that they do not violate the constitutional identity (Verfassungsidentität) of the Basic Law, including the guarantee of human dignity under Article 1(1). This decision established an ultimate constitutional limit on mutual recognition within the EU.

### Substantive Requirements under IRG

For non-EU extradition, the IRG requires:
- Dual criminality (Section 3 IRG)
- Minimum penalty threshold of one year imprisonment (Section 3 IRG)
- Specialty protection (Section 11 IRG)
- Prohibition of extradition for political offenses (Section 6 IRG)
- Prohibition where the death penalty may be imposed (Section 8 IRG)
- Prohibition where extradition would violate fundamental rights (Section 73 IRG)

### Procedure

Extradition proceedings involve two stages: a judicial phase before the Oberlandesgericht (Higher Regional Court), which assesses the legal admissibility of extradition, and an executive phase in which the federal government (Bundesregierung) or the relevant state government (Landesregierung) decides whether to approve the surrender. The judicial admissibility decision is binding on the executive; if the court finds extradition inadmissible, the government cannot proceed.

## ECHR & EU

### European Arrest Warrant

The European Arrest Warrant (EAW), established by Council Framework Decision 2002/584/JHA of June 13, 2002, and operational since January 1, 2004, fundamentally transformed extradition within the EU:

**Mutual Recognition Principle:** The EAW is based on the principle that judicial decisions should be recognized and executed directly between member states, without the traditional diplomatic intermediation of the executive branch.

**Abolition of Key Barriers:** The EAW abolished the political offense exception, eliminated the nationality exception (though member states may require a guarantee that the surrendered national can serve any sentence in the home state), and removed dual criminality for 32 listed offense categories when punishable by at least three years' imprisonment in the issuing state.

**Time Limits:** The executing judicial authority must decide on surrender within 60 days of arrest, extendable to 90 days in exceptional circumstances. Where the person consents, the decision must be made within 10 days.

### ECHR Jurisprudence on Extradition

**Soering v. United Kingdom, Application No. 14038/88, Judgment of July 7, 1989:** The landmark case establishing that extradition to face a real risk of treatment contrary to Article 3 ECHR (prohibition of torture and inhuman or degrading treatment) engages the responsibility of the extraditing state. The Court held that extraditing Jens Soering to Virginia to face capital murder charges would violate Article 3 due to the "death row phenomenon" -- the prolonged period of uncertainty and harsh conditions on death row.

**Trabelsi v. Belgium, Application No. 140/10, Judgment of September 4, 2014:** The Court found that Belgium's extradition of the applicant to the United States to face terrorism charges, despite an interim measure by the Court prohibiting the extradition, violated Article 3 (risk of irreducible life sentence) and Article 34 (failure to comply with interim measures).

**Aranyosi and Căldăraru, CJEU Joined Cases C-404/15 and C-659/15 PPU, Judgment of April 5, 2016:** The Court of Justice of the European Union held that EAW execution must be postponed if there is a real risk that the person would be subject to inhuman or degrading detention conditions in the issuing member state, requiring a two-step test: assessment of systemic deficiencies, followed by assessment of the individual risk.

### EU Directive 2013/48/EU

This directive guarantees the right of access to a lawyer in EAW proceedings, ensuring that the requested person has access to a lawyer in both the executing and issuing member states from the time of arrest.

## Comparative Analysis

### Treaty Requirements

The jurisdictions diverge on whether a treaty is required for extradition. The United States and South Korea generally require a treaty basis, though the U.S. has occasionally used alternative legal authorities for fugitive transfers. Germany does not require a treaty but prefers one. The EAW replaces treaties between EU member states with a unified framework.

### Nationality Exceptions

Germany's constitutional prohibition on extraditing nationals (with limited EU exceptions) represents the most protective approach. South Korea permits but does not require refusal to extradite nationals. The United States has no general prohibition on extraditing its own nationals and routinely surrenders U.S. citizens pursuant to treaty obligations.

### Human Rights Protections

The ECHR framework provides the most robust human rights protection in extradition, with the absolute prohibition on extradition to face torture or inhuman treatment under Article 3 as interpreted in Soering. Germany's Section 73 IRG incorporates fundamental rights as a mandatory bar. The United States' "rule of non-inquiry" provides the weakest judicial protection of human rights in extradition proceedings.

### Efficiency

The EAW system is dramatically more efficient than traditional extradition, with average surrender times of approximately 16 days (with consent) to 48 days (without consent), compared to traditional extradition processes that frequently take months or years.
