## Summary

Sovereign debt law -- the legal framework governing the issuance, management, restructuring, and default of government debt obligations -- sits at the intersection of domestic public finance law, international law, and private contract law, raising unique challenges because sovereign borrowers cannot be subjected to standard insolvency proceedings and because the doctrine of sovereign immunity complicates creditor enforcement. South Korea's government debt is governed by the National Finance Act (국가재정법) of 2006 and the Act on the Management of Treasury Bonds (국고채권에 관한 법률), with the Ministry of Economy and Finance (기획재정부) managing issuance and the National Assembly exercising approval authority over the government's borrowing ceiling, while Korea's sovereign debt management was fundamentally shaped by the 1997 Asian Financial Crisis that necessitated a $57 billion IMF bailout and transformed the country's fiscal governance framework. The United States, the world's largest sovereign debtor with national debt exceeding $36 trillion as of 2025, manages its debt under the authority of Article I, Section 8 of the Constitution, the Second Liberty Bond Act of 1917 (31 U.S.C. § 3101) establishing the statutory debt ceiling, and Treasury Department regulations, with the debt ceiling mechanism creating recurring political crises that have twice led to credit rating downgrades. Germany's federal debt is governed by the Grundgesetz Article 115 debt brake (Schuldenbremse), introduced by constitutional amendment in 2009, which limits structural federal deficits to 0.35 percent of GDP, supplemented by the Bundeshaushaltsordnung (Federal Budget Code) and the Stabilitätsrat (Stability Council), while at the EU level, the Stability and Growth Pact (SGP), reformed in April 2024, the Fiscal Compact (TSCG), and the prohibition on monetary financing under Article 123 TFEU establish the supranational fiscal framework, with the ECHR addressing sovereign debt restructuring through the lens of property rights under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's sovereign debt framework was fundamentally restructured following the 1997 Asian Financial Crisis, which saw the country's sovereign creditworthiness collapse from investment grade to junk status within weeks.

**National Finance Act (국가재정법, 2006):** The comprehensive fiscal management statute, replacing the earlier Budget and Accounts Act, establishes:

| Provision | Content |
|-----------|---------|
| Article 7 | National fiscal management plan (5-year rolling) |
| Article 16 | Principles of fiscal soundness |
| Article 21 | National Assembly approval of budget |
| Article 91 | Government bond issuance authority |
| Article 92 | National Assembly approval of borrowing ceiling |
| Article 95 | National debt management plan |

**Treasury Bond Management:** Korean Treasury Bonds (국고채권, KTBs) are issued by the Ministry of Economy and Finance through competitive auction, with maturities of 2, 3, 5, 10, 20, 30, and 50 years. As of 2025, outstanding KTBs totaled approximately 1,100 trillion KRW (approximately $850 billion).

### 1997 Asian Financial Crisis

The crisis fundamentally reshaped Korea's fiscal governance:
- **November 21, 1997:** Korea requested IMF assistance
- **December 3, 1997:** IMF approved $57 billion rescue package (the largest to that date)
- **Sovereign rating:** Downgraded from A1 to Ba1 (Moody's) in less than one month
- **Conditionality:** IMF program required fiscal austerity, financial sector restructuring, capital account liberalization, and corporate governance reform
- **Recovery:** Korea repaid the IMF loan in full by August 2001, three years ahead of schedule

### Fiscal Rules

Korea does not have a constitutional debt brake but operates under executive-branch fiscal rules:
- **National fiscal management plan:** Medium-term fiscal framework submitted to the National Assembly
- **Pay-as-you-go (PAYGO) principle:** New mandatory spending must be offset by revenue measures or spending cuts (Article 87 National Finance Act)
- **Fiscal Responsibility:** The government has informally targeted maintaining the national debt-to-GDP ratio below 60 percent, though this is a policy target rather than a legal requirement

### Credit Rating

Korea's sovereign credit ratings have recovered fully from the 1997 crisis:
- Moody's: Aa2 (stable)
- S&P: AA (stable)
- Fitch: AA- (stable)

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States is unique among major democracies in employing a statutory debt ceiling that requires periodic Congressional action to raise or suspend.

**Constitutional Foundation:** Article I, Section 8, Clause 2 of the Constitution grants Congress the power "to borrow Money on the credit of the United States." The Fourteenth Amendment, Section 4, provides that "the validity of the public debt of the United States, authorized by law . . . shall not be questioned."

**Second Liberty Bond Act of 1917 (31 U.S.C. § 3101):** Originally enacted to give the Treasury flexibility to finance World War I without seeking Congressional approval for each bond issuance, the Act established an aggregate limit on federal debt. The debt ceiling has been raised or suspended over 100 times since 1939.

**Debt Ceiling Crises:**

| Year | Crisis | Outcome |
|------|--------|---------|
| 2011 | Debt ceiling standoff | Budget Control Act of 2011; S&P downgraded U.S. from AAA to AA+ |
| 2013 | Government shutdown (16 days) | Continuing Resolution and Debt Ceiling Suspension Act |
| 2023 | Debt ceiling standoff | Fiscal Responsibility Act of 2023; Fitch downgraded U.S. from AAA to AA+ |
| 2025 | Debt ceiling reinstated | "Extraordinary measures" employed by Treasury Secretary |

**Treasury Securities:**

| Security Type | Maturity | Purpose |
|--------------|----------|---------|
| Treasury Bills (T-Bills) | 4, 8, 13, 17, 26, 52 weeks | Short-term financing |
| Treasury Notes | 2, 3, 5, 7, 10 years | Medium-term financing |
| Treasury Bonds | 20, 30 years | Long-term financing |
| TIPS | 5, 10, 30 years | Inflation-protected |
| Savings Bonds (I, EE) | Various | Retail investor access |

### Fourteenth Amendment Debate

During debt ceiling standoffs, legal scholars have debated whether the President may unilaterally authorize borrowing beyond the statutory ceiling under the Fourteenth Amendment's public debt clause. The Biden administration considered but ultimately rejected this approach during the 2023 standoff, citing legal uncertainty and potential market disruption.

### Foreign Holdings

Approximately $8.1 trillion of U.S. Treasury securities are held by foreign entities as of 2025, with Japan ($1.1 trillion) and China ($770 billion) as the largest foreign holders. The dollar's status as the world's primary reserve currency creates unique demand dynamics for U.S. sovereign debt.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's sovereign debt governance is characterized by its constitutionally enshrined debt brake (Schuldenbremse) and the EU's supranational fiscal rules.

**Article 115 Grundgesetz (Debt Brake):** Introduced by constitutional amendment in 2009 (effective for the federal government from 2016), the Schuldenbremse limits the federal government's structural deficit to 0.35 percent of GDP:

| Component | Provision |
|-----------|-----------|
| Article 115(2) sentence 2 | Structural deficit limit: 0.35% of GDP |
| Article 115(2) sentence 3 | Cyclical adjustment: automatic stabilizers permitted beyond the limit |
| Article 115(2) sentence 6 | Escape clause: natural disasters or extraordinary emergency situations beyond state control |
| Article 109(3) | States (Länder) must achieve balanced budgets (no structural deficit permitted from 2020) |

**Escape Clause Activation:**

| Year | Basis | Suspension Period |
|------|-------|------------------|
| 2020-2022 | COVID-19 pandemic | Bundestag resolution under Art. 115(2)(6) |
| 2022 | Energy crisis (Russia-Ukraine conflict) | Retroactive escape clause (BVerfG later struck down supplementary budget) |

**BVerfG Decision of November 15, 2023 (2 BvF 1/22):** The Federal Constitutional Court struck down the Second Supplementary Budget Act 2021, which had reallocated €60 billion in unused pandemic emergency borrowing authorization to the Climate and Transformation Fund. The Court held that emergency borrowing authorization is crisis-specific and cannot be transferred to other purposes or carried over to subsequent fiscal years. This landmark decision significantly constrained the federal government's fiscal flexibility and triggered a budgetary crisis.

### Finanzagentur

The Bundesrepublik Deutschland -- Finanzagentur GmbH, a wholly federal-government-owned company, manages Germany's sovereign debt issuance and outstanding obligations. German federal securities (Bundeswertpapiere) include:
- Bundesanleihen (10-year and 30-year bonds)
- Bundesobligationen (5-year bonds)
- Bundesschatzanweisungen (2-year notes)
- Green Federal Securities (since 2020)

Germany issued its first Green Federal Security (Grüne Bundesanleihe) in September 2020, using a "twin bond" concept linking each green bond to a conventional bond of the same maturity.

## ECHR / EU

### EU Fiscal Framework

The EU's sovereign debt governance framework has evolved significantly since the Maastricht Treaty:

**Stability and Growth Pact (SGP) -- Reformed April 2024:**

| Element | Original SGP (1997) | Reformed SGP (2024) |
|---------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Deficit reference value | 3% of GDP | 3% of GDP (unchanged) |
| Debt reference value | 60% of GDP | 60% of GDP (unchanged) |
| Adjustment path | 1/20th rule for debt reduction | Country-specific 4-7 year fiscal adjustment paths |
| Enforcement | Excessive Deficit Procedure (EDP) | Streamlined EDP; net expenditure growth benchmark |
| Flexibility | Limited | Greater national ownership; investment considerations |

**Treaty on Stability, Coordination and Governance (TSCG, "Fiscal Compact," 2012):**
- Structural deficit limit of 0.5 percent of GDP (or 1.0 percent for states with debt significantly below 60 percent)
- Automatic correction mechanism
- Balanced budget rule to be enshrined in national law (preferably constitutional)
- Ratified by 25 EU member states

**Article 123 TFEU -- Prohibition on Monetary Financing:** Prohibits the ECB and national central banks from extending overdraft facilities or credit to EU institutions, national governments, or public bodies. This provision was tested by the ECB's asset purchase programs (SMP, OMT, PSPP, PEPP), with the CJEU upholding these programs as monetary policy measures rather than prohibited monetary financing.

### EU Common Debt

The COVID-19 pandemic prompted the first issuance of joint EU sovereign debt:
- **NextGenerationEU (NGEU):** €806.9 billion recovery instrument financed through EU bond issuance (2021-2026)
- **SURE (Support to mitigate Unemployment Risks in an Emergency):** €98.4 billion in social bonds
- The European Commission has become one of the largest supranational bond issuers globally
- Debate over whether common debt should become permanent remains politically contentious

### ECHR Jurisprudence on Sovereign Debt

**Mamatas and Others v. Greece (Application Nos. 63066/14, 64297/14, and 66106/14, ECtHR, July 21, 2016):** The landmark case concerning Greece's 2012 sovereign debt restructuring. Greek bondholders challenged the retroactive introduction of Collective Action Clauses (CACs) that enabled a 53.5 percent nominal haircut (approximately 75 percent NPV loss) on privately held Greek government bonds. The Court held:
- Government bonds constitute "possessions" under Article 1, Protocol No. 1
- The retroactive introduction of CACs constituted an interference with property rights
- The interference was justified by the legitimate aim of maintaining economic stability and preventing sovereign default
- The measure was proportionate given the severity of the crisis and the public interest in debt sustainability
- No violation of Article 1, Protocol No. 1

**Burdov v. Russia (No. 2) (Application No. 33509/04, ECtHR, January 15, 2009):** The Court held that systematic failure to execute final court judgments ordering government payments constitutes a violation of Article 6(1) and Article 1 of Protocol No. 1, relevant to domestic sovereign debt obligations.

**Stran Greek Refineries and Stratis Andreadis v. Greece (Application No. 13427/87, ECtHR, December 9, 1994):** The Court found a violation of Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 where legislation retroactively annulled an arbitration award for damages against the Greek state, establishing that legislative interference with enforceable claims against the state requires compelling justification.

### Sovereign Debt Restructuring

No formal sovereign insolvency mechanism exists in international law. Restructuring occurs through:

| Mechanism | Forum | Application |
|-----------|-------|-------------|
| Collective Action Clauses (CACs) | Bond contract terms | Greece (2012); standard in eurozone bonds since 2013 |
| Paris Club | Bilateral official creditors | Developing country debt |
| London Club | Commercial bank creditors | Syndicated loan restructuring |
| HIPC/MDRI | IMF/World Bank | Heavily Indebted Poor Countries |
| G20 Common Framework | Bilateral official creditors | Post-COVID sovereign debt (Chad, Zambia, Ethiopia, Ghana) |

## Comparative Analysis

### Fiscal Rules and Constitutional Constraints

The jurisdictions demonstrate different approaches to constraining government borrowing:

**Constitutional limit (Germany):** The Schuldenbremse provides the strongest constraint, requiring constitutional amendment to modify and enforced by the Federal Constitutional Court. However, the escape clause and the November 2023 BVerfG decision demonstrate both the mechanism's flexibility and its rigidity.

**Statutory limit (United States):** The debt ceiling creates periodic political crises without actually constraining spending (which is authorized separately by Congress), leading many economists and legal scholars to advocate for its abolition.

**Legislative approval (South Korea):** Annual National Assembly approval of the borrowing ceiling provides democratic accountability without the rigidity of constitutional constraints or the brinksmanship of a statutory ceiling.

**Supranational framework (EU):** The SGP and Fiscal Compact create peer-based fiscal discipline, though enforcement has been inconsistent, with major economies repeatedly exceeding reference values without facing sanctions.

### Sovereign Immunity and Enforcement

Creditor enforcement against sovereigns remains fundamentally limited by sovereign immunity:
- **United States FSIA:** Provides exceptions for commercial activity and waiver, but enforcement against sovereign assets remains difficult
- **NML Capital v. Argentina (U.S. Supreme Court, 2014):** Established that discovery of sovereign assets for enforcement is not precluded by the FSIA, but actual attachment remains limited to property used for commercial activity
- **EU:** Intra-EU sovereign immunity is modified by EU law obligations, but enforcement against member state assets for bondholder claims remains practically limited

### Collective Action Clauses (CACs)

CACs -- contractual provisions in sovereign bonds that allow a supermajority of bondholders to agree to debt restructuring terms binding on all holders -- have become standard in sovereign debt issuance:

| Jurisdiction | CAC Status |
|-------------|-----------|
| Eurozone | Mandatory single-limb CACs since January 1, 2013 (ESM Treaty requirement) |
| United States | Standard in New York law-governed bonds (market convention) |
| South Korea | Market convention for international issuances |
| International | ICMA Model CACs (2014) with aggregation features widely adopted |

The eurozone's single-limb aggregated CACs allow restructuring across multiple bond series through a single vote (75 percent supermajority across all affected series, with a minimum 2/3 in each series), significantly reducing holdout creditor problems.

### Vulture Fund Litigation

Holdout creditor litigation has significantly shaped sovereign debt law:
- **NML Capital v. Argentina:** Decade-long litigation over Argentine bonds; pari passu clause interpreted as requiring ratable payment; Argentina ultimately settled in 2016
- **Donegal International v. Zambia (UK High Court, 2007):** UK court awarded $15.5 million to a vulture fund on original debt purchased for $3 million, prompting the UK Debt Relief (Developing Countries) Act 2010
- **Bundesgerichtshof (BGH), XI ZR 103/14 (2015):** German Federal Court of Justice addressed the enforceability of Greek law bonds restructured under Greek CAC legislation, finding that the retroactive application of CACs was permissible under applicable EU law

### Climate-Related Sovereign Debt

An emerging trend in sovereign debt management:
- **Green sovereign bonds:** Germany, France, South Korea, and the UK have issued green sovereign bonds with proceeds earmarked for environmental purposes
- **Blue bonds:** The Seychelles issued the world's first sovereign blue bond in 2018 for marine conservation
- **Disaster-resilient debt clauses:** Caribbean states have pioneered debt instruments with natural disaster suspension clauses
- **Debt-for-nature swaps:** Ecuador (2023) completed the world's largest debt-for-nature swap ($1.6 billion), converting commercial debt into conservation funding

### Sovereign Credit Ratings

The role of credit rating agencies in sovereign debt markets raises regulatory and accountability questions:

- **South Korea:** Sovereign ratings upgraded steadily from near-junk (1997) to AA (S&P, Fitch) and Aa2 (Moody's), reflecting post-crisis fiscal discipline; the FSC regulates credit rating agencies operating in Korea under the Financial Investment Services and Capital Markets Act (자본시장과 금융투자업에 관한 법률)
- **United States:** S&P's 2011 downgrade from AAA to AA+ and Fitch's 2023 downgrade from AAA to AA+ highlighted debt ceiling dysfunction; rating agencies are registered as NRSROs under the Exchange Act § 15E
- **EU:** Regulation (EC) No 1060/2009 (CRA Regulation) requires registration and supervision of credit rating agencies by ESMA; sovereign rating methodology disclosure requirements; over-reliance on external ratings being addressed through CRD IV/V provisions requiring banks to develop internal credit assessment capabilities
- The "Big Three" agencies (S&P, Moody's, Fitch) continue to dominate sovereign ratings, raising concentration risk and concerns about objectivity, particularly following their failure to predict the European sovereign debt crisis
