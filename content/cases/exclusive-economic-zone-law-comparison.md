## Summary

The exclusive economic zone (EEZ) -- the maritime area extending up to 200 nautical miles from a coastal state's baselines in which the state exercises sovereign rights for the purpose of exploring, exploiting, conserving, and managing natural resources, and jurisdiction over artificial islands, marine scientific research, and environmental protection -- is one of the most significant innovations of modern international law, transforming approximately 36% of the world's ocean surface from high seas to zones of national jurisdiction. The legal framework is established by Part V of the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS, 1982), Articles 55-75, which entered into force on November 16, 1994. The EEZ represents a compromise between coastal state sovereignty claims and the freedom of the high seas, creating a sui generis zone that is neither territorial sea nor high seas. South Korea proclaimed its EEZ through the Act on the Exclusive Economic Zone (배타적 경제수역법, 1996) and the Act on the Exercise of Sovereign Rights over the Exclusive Economic Zone (배타적 경제수역에서의 외국인어업 등에 대한 주권적 권리의 행사에 관한 법률, 1996), with its EEZ boundaries subject to ongoing delimitation disputes with China in the Yellow Sea and East China Sea and with Japan in the East Sea/Sea of Japan, complicated by the presence of disputed features including Ieodo/Suyangjiao (이어도/苏岩礁) and Dokdo/Takeshima (독도/竹島). The United States established its EEZ through Presidential Proclamation No. 5030 (March 10, 1983) by President Reagan, claiming the world's largest EEZ (approximately 11.35 million square kilometers including overseas territories), governed domestically by the Magnuson-Stevens Fishery Conservation and Management Act (16 U.S.C. Sections 1801-1891d) for fisheries, the Deep Water Port Act, and various environmental statutes, while not ratifying UNCLOS itself. Germany exercises EEZ rights in the North Sea and Baltic Sea under the Seeaufgabengesetz (Maritime Tasks Act) and UNCLOS, with relatively limited EEZ area but significant offshore wind energy development. The ECHR and EU framework intersects through the Common Fisheries Policy (CFP), EU environmental directives applicable to EEZ waters, the freedom of navigation controversies (particularly regarding military activities), and the CJEU's jurisprudence on the application of EU law in member states' EEZ areas.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea proclaimed its EEZ through the Exclusive Economic Zone Act (배타적 경제수역법, Act No. 5151, August 8, 1996) and the complementary Act on the Exercise of Sovereign Rights over the Exclusive Economic Zone (배타적 경제수역에서의 외국인어업 등에 대한 주권적 권리의 행사에 관한 법률, Act No. 5152, 1996). These statutes establish sovereign rights over living and non-living resources within 200 nautical miles from the baselines, jurisdiction over artificial islands and installations, marine scientific research, and environmental protection.

The Ministry of Oceans and Fisheries (해양수산부) administers EEZ-related policies, including fisheries management, marine environmental protection, and the oversight of foreign fishing activities. The Korea Coast Guard (해양경찰청) enforces EEZ regulations and patrols against illegal, unreported, and unregulated (IUU) fishing.

### Delimitation Disputes

Korea's EEZ boundaries remain incompletely delimited due to overlapping claims with both China and Japan. In the Yellow Sea (서해/황해) and East China Sea (동중국해), Korea and China have overlapping EEZ claims because the distance between their facing coastlines is less than 400 nautical miles, requiring delimitation by agreement. Korea favors the median/equidistance line, while China advocates for equitable principles that would take into account its longer coastline and larger population, a position Korea rejects as inconsistent with established international jurisprudence.

In the East Sea/Sea of Japan (동해/일본해), the delimitation between Korea and Japan is complicated by the Dokdo/Takeshima sovereignty dispute. Korea's 1996 EEZ Act deliberately excluded the specification of EEZ boundaries with Japan, pending resolution of the sovereignty question. Korea and Japan concluded the Korea-Japan Fisheries Agreement (한일어업협정, November 28, 1998, entered into force January 22, 1999), which established a provisional measures zone in the East Sea for fisheries management without prejudicing the maritime boundary delimitation.

### Ieodo/Suyangjiao Dispute

Ieodo (이어도, Chinese: Suyangjiao/苏岩礁, also known as Socotra Rock) is a submerged rock in the East China Sea approximately 149 kilometers southwest of Marado, Korea's southernmost island, and 287 kilometers from the nearest Chinese coast. Korea constructed the Ieodo Ocean Research Station (이어도 해양과학기지) atop the feature in 2003. Because Ieodo is submerged, it does not generate maritime zones under UNCLOS Article 121(3) and Article 60, but its location within the overlapping EEZ claims of Korea and China makes it a focal point of the delimitation dispute. China has repeatedly protested Korea's construction on the feature.

### Illegal Fishing Enforcement

Chinese fishing vessels operating illegally in Korean EEZ waters have been a persistent enforcement challenge. Korea has strengthened enforcement measures, including amendments to the EEZ Act authorizing higher fines and expanded enforcement authority for the Korea Coast Guard. Violent incidents between Korean enforcement vessels and Chinese fishing boats have occurred repeatedly, including the fatal stabbing of a Korean Coast Guard officer in December 2011.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States proclaimed its EEZ on March 10, 1983, through Presidential Proclamation No. 5030, issued by President Ronald Reagan. The proclamation established a 200-nautical-mile EEZ "in which the United States will exercise sovereign rights... for the purpose of exploring, exploiting, conserving, and managing natural resources." Notably, the proclamation was issued simultaneously with the decision not to sign UNCLOS, reflecting the Reagan administration's objections to the Convention's deep seabed mining provisions in Part XI.

The U.S. EEZ, encompassing approximately 11.35 million square kilometers (including the EEZs of overseas territories such as Guam, American Samoa, the Northern Mariana Islands, Puerto Rico, the U.S. Virgin Islands, and the Pacific Remote Islands), is the largest in the world. The U.S. claims that its EEZ rights under customary international law are coextensive with those recognized under UNCLOS Part V.

### Fisheries Management

The Magnuson-Stevens Fishery Conservation and Management Act (MSA, 16 U.S.C. Sections 1801-1891d), originally enacted as the Fishery Conservation and Management Act of 1976, is the primary domestic statute governing fisheries within the U.S. EEZ. The MSA establishes eight Regional Fishery Management Councils that develop fishery management plans for their respective regions, subject to review and approval by the National Marine Fisheries Service (NMFS/NOAA Fisheries).

The MSA's 1996 Sustainable Fisheries Act amendments introduced the requirement to prevent overfishing and rebuild overfished stocks, and the 2006 Magnuson-Stevens Reauthorization Act imposed annual catch limits and accountability measures. The U.S. has achieved significant success in fisheries management, with NOAA reporting that the number of overfished stocks declined from 92 in 2000 to 25 in 2023.

### Freedom of Navigation and Military Activities

The United States maintains that military activities, including surveillance, intelligence gathering, and naval exercises, are permissible in foreign states' EEZs as high seas freedoms preserved under UNCLOS Article 58. This position has been a source of significant tension, particularly with China, which asserts that military activities in its EEZ require prior consent. The U.S. conducts Freedom of Navigation (FON) operations in the South China Sea to challenge what it considers excessive maritime claims, including China's assertions in the Nine-Dash Line area.

The incident involving the USNS Impeccable and Chinese vessels in March 2009, approximately 75 nautical miles south of Hainan Island, illustrated the fundamental disagreement over military activities in the EEZ. The EP-3 incident of April 1, 2001, involving a collision between a U.S. Navy reconnaissance aircraft and a Chinese fighter jet approximately 70 miles southeast of Hainan, raised related questions about military aerial surveillance near the EEZ.

### Artificial Islands and Installations

The U.S. has extensive experience with artificial islands and installations in its EEZ, primarily oil and gas platforms in the Gulf of Mexico. Under UNCLOS Article 60 (which the U.S. considers reflective of customary law), coastal states have exclusive jurisdiction over artificial islands and installations in the EEZ, including the right to establish safety zones of up to 500 meters around them. Artificial islands do not generate their own maritime zones.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany exercises EEZ rights in the North Sea (approximately 28,600 km2) and Baltic Sea (approximately 4,500 km2), proclaimed under UNCLOS upon ratification on November 14, 1994. The Seeaufgabengesetz (Maritime Tasks Act) and related ordinances provide the domestic legal framework, while the Federal Maritime and Hydrographic Agency (Bundesamt fur Seeschifffahrt und Hydrographie, BSH) serves as the principal regulatory authority for the German EEZ.

Germany's EEZ boundaries are fully delimited through bilateral agreements with Denmark, the Netherlands, the United Kingdom, Norway, Poland, and Sweden. The limited geographic extent of the German EEZ contrasts with its intense utilization, particularly for offshore wind energy, shipping, and fisheries.

### Common Fisheries Policy

As an EU member state, Germany's fisheries management in the EEZ is governed by the Common Fisheries Policy (CFP), established by Regulation (EU) No 1380/2013. The CFP establishes Total Allowable Catches (TACs) set by the Council of the European Union, allocated among member states based on the principle of relative stability. Germany's fishing quotas are implemented through the Seefischereigesetz (Sea Fisheries Act) and managed by the Bundesanstalt fur Landwirtschaft und Ernahrung (Federal Agency for Agriculture and Food, BLE).

### Offshore Wind Energy

Germany's EEZ has become one of the world's most intensive areas for offshore wind energy development. The Offshore Wind Energy Act (Windenergie-auf-See-Gesetz, WindSeeG), as amended, provides the regulatory framework. Germany's installed offshore wind capacity exceeded 8 GW by 2023, with a target of 30 GW by 2030 and 70 GW by 2045 under the 2023 amendment to the WindSeeG. The BSH's Maritimer Raumordnungsplan (Maritime Spatial Plan), adopted in 2021, allocates zones within the EEZ for offshore wind, shipping, pipeline corridors, and nature conservation.

### Environmental Protection in the EEZ

Germany has designated Natura 2000 protected areas within its EEZ under the EU Habitats Directive (92/43/EEC) and Birds Directive (2009/147/EC), including several areas in the North Sea (e.g., Dogger Bank, Sylt Outer Reef) and Baltic Sea (e.g., Fehmarnbelt, Pomeranian Bay). The designation of marine protected areas has created tensions with fisheries interests and shipping activities.

### Penalties

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| Unauthorized fishing in German EEZ | Fines under Seefischereigesetz; EU regulation enforcement |
| CFP quota violations | Administrative fines; license suspension/revocation |
| Unauthorized construction in EEZ | Administrative orders under Seeaufgabengesetz |
| Marine pollution in EEZ | Penalties under Wasserhaushaltsgesetz and EU Directive 2005/35/EC |
| Violation of marine protected area regulations | Fines under Federal Nature Conservation Act (BNatSchG) |

## ECHR & EU

### Common Fisheries Policy

The Common Fisheries Policy (CFP), governed by Regulation (EU) No 1380/2013, is the EU's primary instrument for managing fisheries within member states' EEZs. The CFP establishes the principle of equal access to EU waters (with limited exceptions for the 0-12 nautical mile zone), maximum sustainable yield (MSY) targets, the landing obligation (discard ban), and structural funding through the European Maritime, Fisheries and Aquaculture Fund (EMFAF).

The CFP's reform in 2013 introduced the legally binding commitment to achieve fishing at MSY for all managed stocks by 2020 (extended for certain data-limited stocks), the landing obligation phased in between 2015 and 2019, regionalization of management through Advisory Councils, and multiannual management plans. The CJEU has addressed CFP issues in numerous cases, including Spain v. Council (Case C-36/98, 2001) concerning quota allocation and Associazione Italia Nostra Onlus (Case C-444/15, 2016) on environmental assessment of fishing activities.

### Brexit and EEZ Fisheries

The United Kingdom's withdrawal from the EU (effective January 31, 2020) had profound implications for EEZ fisheries management. The EU-UK Trade and Cooperation Agreement (TCA, December 24, 2020) established a transitional period for fisheries access, with a gradual transfer of 25% of EU fishing rights in UK waters over a 5.5-year adjustment period ending June 30, 2026. The agreement preserves reciprocal access arrangements while establishing annual consultations on fishing opportunities. The UK's departure from the CFP particularly affects Germany, whose distant-water fishing fleet has historically relied on access to UK EEZ waters.

### Application of EU Law in the EEZ

The CJEU has clarified the application of EU law in member states' EEZ areas in several significant judgments. In Criminal proceedings against Akerberg Fransson (Case C-617/10, 2013), the Court held that EU fundamental rights apply when member states implement EU law, including in their EEZs. In Salemink v. Raad van bestuur van het Uitvoeringsinstituut werknemersverzekeringen (Case C-347/10, 2012), the Court held that EU rules on freedom of movement for workers apply to activities on artificial installations in the EEZ.

### ECHR and Maritime Zones

The European Court of Human Rights' jurisprudence on the application of the Convention in maritime zones remains developing. In Medvedyev v. France (Application No. 3394/03, Grand Chamber Judgment of March 29, 2010), the Court addressed the exercise of jurisdiction on the high seas in the context of drug interdiction, establishing principles relevant to the exercise of jurisdiction in the EEZ. Article 1 of the ECHR requires states to secure Convention rights to everyone "within their jurisdiction," which extends to the exercise of authority in maritime zones.

## Comparative Analysis

### EEZ Extent and Strategic Significance

The vast disparities in EEZ size shape each jurisdiction's approach. The United States' enormous EEZ, amplified by overseas territories, provides unparalleled access to marine resources and strategic maritime space. South Korea's contested EEZ, compressed by proximity to China and Japan, makes every square kilometer of maritime space a diplomatic issue. Germany's compact EEZ focuses attention on intensive utilization rather than territorial expansion. The EU's collective EEZ, managed partly through the CFP, represents a unique experiment in shared sovereignty over marine resources.

### Military Activities and Freedom of Navigation

The most contentious EEZ issue in contemporary international law concerns military activities. The United States insists that military operations are protected freedoms under UNCLOS Article 58, while China (and to varying degrees other states including Korea) contend that certain military activities require prior consent. This disagreement, centered on the South China Sea and East China Sea, has implications for all four jurisdictions' security interests.

### Fisheries Management Models

The jurisdictions represent three distinct fisheries management models: the U.S. council-based system under the Magnuson-Stevens Act, which has been relatively successful in stock recovery; the EU's centralized CFP, which has struggled with overfishing and the politically difficult allocation of quotas; and South Korea's bilateral agreement-based system with China and Japan, which faces chronic enforcement challenges against IUU fishing. Germany operates within the CFP framework, illustrating the constraints and benefits of supranational fisheries management.

### Artificial Islands and Maritime Construction

UNCLOS Article 60 permits coastal states to construct artificial islands in the EEZ but specifies that such installations do not generate maritime zones. This principle is tested by Korea's Ieodo Ocean Research Station and, more dramatically, by China's island-building in the South China Sea (rejected as a basis for maritime claims by the South China Sea Arbitration, PCA Case No. 2013-19, Award of July 12, 2016).
