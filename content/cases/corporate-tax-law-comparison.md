## Summary

Corporate tax -- a direct tax levied on the profits of incorporated business entities -- plays a central role in fiscal policy, international competition for investment, and ongoing debates about base erosion and profit shifting by multinational enterprises. South Korea imposes corporate income tax under the Corporate Tax Act (법인세법), with progressive rates ranging from 9 percent on the first 200 million KRW of taxable income to 24 percent on income exceeding 300 billion KRW, plus a 10 percent local income tax surcharge, reflecting a 2023 reform that reduced each bracket by one percentage point. The United States fundamentally restructured its corporate tax through the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017, replacing a graduated rate structure with a top rate of 35 percent with a flat 21 percent rate, while simultaneously introducing the Global Intangible Low-Taxed Income (GILTI) regime and the Base Erosion and Anti-Abuse Tax (BEAT) to address international profit shifting. Germany levies corporate income tax (Körperschaftsteuer) at a flat rate of 15 percent under the Körperschaftsteuergesetz (KStG), but the effective tax burden on corporate profits reaches approximately 30 percent when combined with the solidarity surcharge (5.5 percent of corporate tax) and municipal trade tax (Gewerbesteuer) averaging 14-17 percent depending on the municipality. At the supranational level, the OECD/G20 Inclusive Framework's Two-Pillar Solution, implemented within the EU through Council Directive (EU) 2022/2523 on Pillar Two (global minimum tax of 15 percent) and the ongoing negotiations on Pillar One (reallocation of taxing rights), represents the most significant transformation of international corporate taxation since the establishment of the arm's length principle in the mid-twentieth century.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's corporate income tax is governed by the Corporate Tax Act (법인세법, Act No. 19930), administered by the National Tax Service (국세청). The Act applies to both domestic corporations (내국법인) taxed on worldwide income and foreign corporations (외국법인) taxed only on Korean-source income. The Framework Act on National Taxes (국세기본법) provides general procedural rules.

### Rate Structure (2023 Reform)

| Taxable Income (KRW) | Rate (Post-2023 Reform) | Previous Rate |
|----------------------|------------------------|---------------|
| Up to 200 million | 9% | 10% |
| 200 million - 20 billion | 19% | 20% |
| 20 billion - 300 billion | 21% | 22% |
| Over 300 billion | 24% | 25% |

The 2023 tax reform reduced each bracket by one percentage point, effective for fiscal years beginning on or after January 1, 2023. A local income tax surcharge of 10 percent of national corporate tax liability applies under the Local Tax Act (지방세법).

### Group Taxation and Consolidated Filing

South Korea permits consolidated tax filing (연결납세) for corporate groups where the parent company holds 100 percent of the subsidiary's shares. The election for consolidated filing is irrevocable for a minimum of five years. Intra-group transactions must be conducted at arm's length under the International Tax Coordination Act (국제조세조정에 관한 법률).

### Investment Incentives

The Restriction of Special Taxation Act (조세특례제한법) provides various investment incentives including tax credits for R&D expenditure (up to 25 percent for SMEs and 2-6 percent for large enterprises), investment tax credits for facilities in designated areas, and special tax treatment for foreign-invested enterprises under the Foreign Investment Promotion Act (외국인투자 촉진법).

## United States

### Legal Framework

U.S. corporate income tax is imposed under the Internal Revenue Code, Subtitle A, Chapter 1, Subchapter C. The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 (Pub. L. 115-97) represented the most significant reform of corporate taxation since the Tax Reform Act of 1986, fundamentally altering the rate structure and international provisions.

### TCJA Corporate Provisions

**Flat 21 Percent Rate:** The TCJA replaced the prior graduated rate structure (15%-35%) with a single flat rate of 21 percent, effective January 1, 2018. Unlike the individual provisions, the corporate rate reduction is permanent and does not expire.

**International Provisions:**

| Provision | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Territorial System Transition | Shifted from worldwide to a modified territorial system with a one-time transition tax on accumulated offshore earnings |
| GILTI (Section 951A) | Imposes current U.S. tax on global intangible low-taxed income of controlled foreign corporations |
| FDII (Section 250) | Provides a deduction for foreign-derived intangible income earned by domestic corporations |
| BEAT (Section 59A) | Minimum tax targeting base-eroding payments to related foreign persons |
| Section 245A DRD | 100% dividends received deduction for foreign-source dividends from 10%-owned foreign corporations |

### Corporate Alternative Minimum Tax (CAMT)

The Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 introduced a new 15 percent Corporate Alternative Minimum Tax (CAMT) under Section 55, effective for tax years beginning after December 31, 2022. The CAMT applies to corporations with average annual adjusted financial statement income exceeding $1 billion over a three-year period (reduced to $100 million for certain foreign-parented groups).

### Landmark Cases

**Gregory v. Helvering, 293 U.S. 465 (1935):** The Supreme Court established the "business purpose" doctrine, holding that a transaction must have economic substance beyond mere tax avoidance to be respected for tax purposes.

**Wayfair v. South Dakota, 585 U.S. 162 (2018):** While primarily concerning sales tax, the Court's decision overturning the physical presence requirement for state tax nexus has had significant implications for corporate state tax liability.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's corporate tax system comprises two primary taxes: the Körperschaftsteuer (corporate income tax) governed by the Körperschaftsteuergesetz (KStG) and the Gewerbesteuer (trade tax) governed by the Gewerbesteuergesetz (GewStG). The combined burden creates an effective corporate tax rate of approximately 30 percent, varying by municipality.

### Rate Components

| Tax Component | Rate | Basis |
|--------------|------|-------|
| Körperschaftsteuer | 15% | Taxable income under KStG |
| Solidarity surcharge | 5.5% of KSt (= 0.825% of income) | Applied to corporate tax |
| Gewerbesteuer | Varies (~14-17%) | Trade income × base rate (3.5%) × municipal multiplier (Hebesatz) |
| **Combined effective rate** | **~29.8-32.8%** | Depending on municipality |

The Gewerbesteuer Hebesatz varies significantly: Munich applies 490 percent, Hamburg 470 percent, Berlin 410 percent, while some smaller municipalities charge as low as 200 percent.

### Organschaft (Fiscal Unity)

Germany's group taxation system, the Organschaft, requires a profit and loss transfer agreement (Gewinnabführungsvertrag) between parent and subsidiary executed for a minimum period of five years. Under an Organschaft, the subsidiary's income is attributed to the parent company for both Körperschaftsteuer and Gewerbesteuer purposes. This creates true tax consolidation but imposes significant corporate law obligations, as the parent must absorb the subsidiary's losses.

### Loss Utilization

Corporate losses may be carried back one year up to EUR 10 million (temporarily increased from EUR 1 million during COVID-19 measures) and carried forward indefinitely, subject to the minimum taxation rule (Mindestbesteuerung): losses up to EUR 1 million may be fully offset, but only 60 percent of income exceeding EUR 1 million may be offset by loss carryforwards. Additional restrictions apply under Section 8c KStG when there is a change of ownership exceeding 50 percent.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Corporate Tax Harmonization

While the EU has not achieved full harmonization of corporate tax rates, significant coordination exists through several instruments:

**Parent-Subsidiary Directive (2011/96/EU):** Eliminates withholding taxes on dividend distributions between associated companies of different member states and prevents double taxation of parent companies on subsidiaries' profits.

**Interest and Royalties Directive (2003/49/EC):** Eliminates withholding taxes on interest and royalty payments between associated companies of different member states.

**Anti-Tax Avoidance Directives (ATAD I and II):** Directive 2016/1164 (ATAD I) and Directive 2017/952 (ATAD II) require member states to implement interest limitation rules, exit taxation, a general anti-abuse rule, CFC rules, and rules addressing hybrid mismatches.

### Pillar Two -- Global Minimum Tax

Council Directive (EU) 2022/2523, adopted on December 14, 2022, implements the OECD/G20 Inclusive Framework's Pillar Two rules. The directive establishes:

- An Income Inclusion Rule (IIR) requiring parent entities to pay a top-up tax where their subsidiaries are taxed below the 15 percent minimum in any jurisdiction.
- An Undertaxed Profits Rule (UTPR) providing a backstop mechanism where the IIR does not apply.
- A Qualified Domestic Minimum Top-up Tax (QDMTT) option allowing jurisdictions to collect the top-up tax domestically.

### BEFIT Proposal

The European Commission proposed the Business in Europe: Framework for Income Taxation (BEFIT) regulation in September 2023, which would establish common rules for determining the corporate tax base of EU groups and allocate the aggregate tax base among member states using a formulary apportionment mechanism. This proposal, if adopted, would represent a fundamental shift from separate entity accounting to consolidated group taxation at the EU level.

### State Aid Enforcement

The European Commission has actively used state aid rules (Articles 107-108 TFEU) to challenge preferential corporate tax arrangements:

- **Starbucks/Netherlands:** Commission found that a transfer pricing ruling provided selective advantage.
- **Amazon/Luxembourg:** Commission ordered recovery of approximately EUR 250 million.
- **Apple/Ireland:** CJEU upheld the Commission's order to recover EUR 13 billion in September 2024.

## Comparative Analysis

### Rate Competition and Convergence

The global minimum tax represents a historic constraint on the "race to the bottom" in corporate tax rates that has characterized the past four decades. Ireland's 12.5 percent rate, long a cornerstone of its economic strategy, was adjusted to 15 percent for in-scope companies following the OECD agreement. However, rate competition continues for companies below the EUR 750 million revenue threshold, and jurisdictions increasingly compete through tax incentives, credits, and preferential regimes rather than headline rates.

### Territorial vs. Worldwide Systems

The TCJA marked the United States' transition from a worldwide to a modified territorial system, aligning it more closely with South Korea and Germany. However, the GILTI regime means that the U.S. system is only partially territorial, as it imposes current U.S. tax on certain foreign earnings of controlled foreign corporations. Germany and South Korea both exempt most foreign subsidiary dividends from domestic taxation, subject to anti-avoidance provisions.

### Group Taxation Approaches

The three jurisdictions offer different models of group taxation. Germany's Organschaft requires a binding profit transfer agreement with significant corporate law implications. The U.S. consolidated return system requires only 80 percent ownership and has no corporate law prerequisites. South Korea's consolidated filing requires 100 percent ownership, the most restrictive threshold.

### Digital Economy Challenges

All jurisdictions struggle with taxing the digital economy, where value creation and profit booking are geographically disconnected. The OECD Pillar One proposal would reallocate some taxing rights to market jurisdictions, but multilateral implementation has been delayed. South Korea introduced a digital services-related provision in 2019, requiring foreign digital service providers to register for VAT purposes.
