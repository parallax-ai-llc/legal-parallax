## Summary

Alternative dispute resolution (ADR) -- encompassing mediation, arbitration, conciliation, and other non-litigation mechanisms for resolving disputes -- has grown from a marginal practice to a central pillar of civil justice systems worldwide, driven by concerns over court congestion, litigation costs, and the desire for more flexible dispute resolution processes. South Korea has established a comprehensive ADR framework through the Arbitration Act (중재법, Act No. 1767, originally enacted 1966, comprehensively revised 1999 and 2016 to align with the UNCITRAL Model Law), the Judicial Conciliation of Civil Disputes Act (민사조정법, 1990), and sector-specific mediation statutes including the Framework Act on the Resolution of Disputes (분쟁의 조정 등에 관한 기본법), with the Korean Commercial Arbitration Board (대한상사중재원, KCAB) serving as the principal arbitral institution and court-annexed mediation (조정) playing an increasingly prominent role, resolving approximately 60 percent of cases referred to mediation in 2023. The United States has the most developed ADR ecosystem globally, governed by the Federal Arbitration Act (FAA, 9 U.S.C. Section 1 et seq., 1925), the Uniform Mediation Act (adopted by 12 states), and a robust framework of court-mandated ADR programs, though the extensive use of mandatory pre-dispute arbitration clauses in consumer and employment contracts has generated significant controversy culminating in the Ending Forced Arbitration of Sexual Assault and Sexual Harassment Act of 2021 and ongoing legislative efforts. Germany regulates arbitration through Sections 1025-1066 of the Code of Civil Procedure (Zivilprozessordnung, ZPO), based on the UNCITRAL Model Law as adopted in 1998, and promotes mediation through the Mediation Act (Mediationsgesetz) of 2012, while maintaining a distinctive tradition of mandatory conciliation hearings (obligatorische Streitschlichtung) in several states for certain civil disputes. At the European level, the EU has promoted ADR through Directive 2008/52/EC on mediation in cross-border disputes, Directive 2013/11/EU on consumer ADR, the Online Dispute Resolution Regulation (EU) No. 524/2013, and the New York Convention on the Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards (1958) to which all EU member states are party, while the ECHR's Article 6 right to a fair trial sets the outer boundary on compulsory ADR by requiring that access to courts not be effectively foreclosed.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's ADR system operates through three principal mechanisms: institutional arbitration, court-annexed mediation (조정), and administrative dispute resolution.

**Arbitration Act (중재법):** Originally enacted in 1966, comprehensively revised in 1999 to incorporate the UNCITRAL Model Law on International Commercial Arbitration, and further amended in 2016 to adopt the 2006 revisions to the Model Law. Key provisions include:

- **Article 3 (Scope):** The Act applies to arbitration with its seat in Korea, regardless of whether the dispute is domestic or international.
- **Article 8 (Arbitration Agreement):** An arbitration agreement must be in writing, which includes electronic communications under the 2016 amendment.
- **Article 35 (Recognition and Enforcement):** Arbitral awards have the same effect as a final court judgment and are enforced through the courts.
- **Article 36 (Grounds for Refusal):** Mirrors Article V of the New York Convention, permitting refusal of enforcement on limited grounds.

**Korean Commercial Arbitration Board (대한상사중재원, KCAB):** Established in 1966, KCAB is Korea's principal arbitral institution, administering both domestic and international arbitration under the KCAB Domestic Arbitration Rules and KCAB International Arbitration Rules. KCAB also operates the Seoul International Dispute Resolution Center (SIDRC). In 2023, KCAB administered approximately 450 arbitration cases, with international cases comprising approximately 25 percent.

**Court-Annexed Mediation (조정):** The Judicial Conciliation of Civil Disputes Act (민사조정법, 1990) authorizes judges to refer civil cases to mediation at any stage of proceedings. Mediation is conducted by a mediation committee (조정위원회) comprising a judge and two lay mediators. If the parties reach agreement, the mediation settlement has the same effect as a court judgment. In 2023, approximately 60 percent of cases referred to court-annexed mediation resulted in settlement.

### Family Court Mediation

The Family Litigation Act (가사소송법) mandates mediation in family disputes prior to trial. Family court judges refer virtually all divorce, custody, and inheritance disputes to mediation. The mandatory mediation requirement has contributed to a mediation settlement rate exceeding 50 percent in family cases.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has the most expansive ADR ecosystem globally, operating at federal, state, and private institutional levels.

**Federal Arbitration Act (FAA, 9 U.S.C. Section 1 et seq., 1925):** The FAA establishes a strong federal policy favoring arbitration agreements. Section 2 provides that arbitration agreements "shall be valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, save upon such grounds as exist at law or in equity for the revocation of any contract." The Supreme Court has interpreted the FAA expansively over several decades, applying it to consumer, employment, and statutory claims.

**Key Supreme Court Arbitration Decisions:**

| Case | Year | Holding |
|------|------|---------|
| Southland Corp. v. Keating | 1984 | FAA preempts conflicting state law |
| Mitsubishi Motors v. Soler Chrysler-Plymouth | 1985 | Statutory antitrust claims arbitrable |
| AT&T Mobility v. Concepcion | 2011 | FAA preempts state law prohibiting class action waivers in arbitration |
| Epic Systems Corp. v. Lewis | 2018 | Collective action waivers in employment arbitration enforceable |
| Morgan v. Sundance | 2022 | No prejudice requirement for waiver of right to arbitrate |
| Coinbase v. Bielski | 2023 | Discovery stays automatically during interlocutory appeal of arbitrability |

**Ending Forced Arbitration of Sexual Assault and Sexual Harassment Act (EFAA, 2022):** Signed into law on March 3, 2022, the EFAA amends the FAA to permit individuals alleging sexual assault or sexual harassment to void pre-dispute arbitration agreements and class action waivers with respect to those claims. The Act applies to disputes arising after its enactment date.

**Alternative Dispute Resolution Act of 1998 (ADRA, 28 U.S.C. Section 651-658):** Requires each federal district court to authorize the use of ADR processes in all civil actions. Courts must provide litigants with at least one ADR process, and most districts offer mediation, early neutral evaluation, and mini-trials.

### Mediation

Mediation in the United States is governed primarily by state law and court rules rather than federal statute. The Uniform Mediation Act (UMA), promulgated by the Uniform Law Commission in 2001, has been adopted in 12 states and the District of Columbia. The UMA establishes a mediation privilege protecting communications made during mediation from discovery and admissibility in subsequent proceedings.

### Controversies

The widespread use of mandatory pre-dispute arbitration clauses in consumer contracts, employment agreements, and terms of service has generated extensive controversy. The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) issued a rule in 2017 prohibiting mandatory arbitration in consumer financial contracts, but Congress overturned the rule under the Congressional Review Act. The Forced Arbitration Injustice Repeal (FAIR) Act, which would prohibit pre-dispute arbitration agreements in consumer, employment, antitrust, and civil rights disputes, has been introduced in multiple Congressional sessions but has not been enacted.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's ADR framework combines UNCITRAL Model Law-based arbitration, EU-directive-compliant mediation, and a distinctive tradition of mandatory conciliation.

**Arbitration (ZPO Sections 1025-1066):** Germany adopted the UNCITRAL Model Law on International Commercial Arbitration in 1998, incorporating it into the Tenth Book of the Zivilprozessordnung. The Deutsche Institution für Schiedsgerichtsbarkeit (DIS) is Germany's leading arbitral institution, administering arbitrations under the DIS Arbitration Rules (most recently revised 2018). The DIS administered approximately 150 cases in 2023.

**Mediation Act (Mediationsgesetz, 2012):** Enacted on July 21, 2012, implementing EU Directive 2008/52/EC, the Mediationsgesetz defines mediation as a "confidential and structured process in which the parties voluntarily and independently seek to reach a mutually acceptable resolution with the assistance of one or more mediators." Key provisions:

- **Section 1 (Definition):** Mediators are independent and neutral; they have no decision-making authority.
- **Section 2 (Procedure):** The mediator ensures that the parties understand the principles and procedure of mediation.
- **Section 4 (Confidentiality):** The mediator and persons involved in the mediation procedure are bound by confidentiality.
- **Section 5 (Training Requirements):** Persons may designate themselves as "certified mediators" (zertifizierter Mediator) only after completing specified training requirements (120 hours, per the Zertifizierter-Mediator-Ausbildungsverordnung of 2016).

**Obligatorische Streitschlichtung (Mandatory Conciliation):** Under the Introductory Act to the Code of Civil Procedure (EGZPO Section 15a), several German states have enacted laws requiring mandatory conciliation proceedings before a recognized conciliation body (Gütestelle) as a prerequisite to filing certain civil claims. This requirement typically applies to disputes between neighbors, defamation claims, and small-value disputes. States implementing mandatory conciliation include Baden-Württemberg, Bavaria, Brandenburg, Hesse, North Rhine-Westphalia, Saarland, Saxony-Anhalt, and Schleswig-Holstein.

### Consumer ADR

Germany implemented EU Directive 2013/11/EU through the Consumer Dispute Resolution Act (Verbraucherstreitbeilegungsgesetz, VSBG) of 2016, establishing a system of recognized consumer dispute resolution bodies (Verbraucherschlichtungsstellen). The Universalschlichtungsstelle des Bundes (Federal Universal Conciliation Body) handles consumer disputes not covered by sector-specific bodies. Participation in consumer ADR is voluntary for businesses unless sector-specific regulation requires it.

### Penalties and Enforcement

| Issue | Legal Consequence |
|-------|-------------------|
| Failure to participate in mandatory conciliation | Claim dismissed as inadmissible |
| Breach of arbitration agreement | Court must refer parties to arbitration (ZPO Section 1032) |
| Violation of mediator confidentiality | Civil liability; potential criminal liability for data protection violations |
| Non-compliance with mediated settlement agreement | Enforcement as contractual agreement; notarized agreements directly enforceable |

## ECHR / EU

### EU Mediation Directive 2008/52/EC

Adopted on May 21, 2008, the Directive promotes the use of mediation in cross-border disputes within the EU. Key provisions:

- **Article 3 (Definition):** Mediation is a structured process where two or more parties voluntarily attempt to reach an agreement with the assistance of a mediator.
- **Article 5 (Court Referral):** Courts may invite parties to use mediation; member states may make mediation mandatory for cross-border disputes provided it does not prevent access to the judicial system.
- **Article 6 (Enforceability):** Member states shall ensure that mediated settlement agreements can be made enforceable through court approval or notarial authentication.
- **Article 7 (Confidentiality):** Mediators shall not be compelled to give evidence in judicial proceedings regarding information arising from mediation.
- **Article 8 (Limitation Periods):** Mediation must not prevent parties from subsequently accessing judicial proceedings; limitation periods shall be tolled during mediation.

### Consumer ADR Directive 2013/11/EU

This Directive requires all EU member states to establish quality-assured ADR entities covering all sectors of economic activity. ADR entities must be impartial, transparent, effective, and fair. Traders must inform consumers about available ADR procedures. The Online Dispute Resolution (ODR) Regulation (EU) No. 524/2013 established an EU-wide online platform for resolving cross-border consumer disputes electronically, though this platform was discontinued in 2025 due to low usage rates.

### ECHR Article 6 -- Right to a Fair Trial

The ECHR's right to a fair trial (Article 6) sets limits on compulsory ADR:

**Deweer v. Belgium (Application No. 6903/75, February 27, 1980):** The Court established that the right of access to a court under Article 6 is not absolute but may be subject to limitations, provided the limitations do not impair the very essence of the right and pursue a legitimate aim with proportionate means.

**Brumarescu v. Romania (Application No. 28342/95, October 28, 1999):** The Court confirmed that the right of access to a court is a fundamental element of the right to a fair trial.

**Compulsory ADR Standard:** The ECHR permits mandatory ADR as a precondition to court proceedings provided that: (1) the ADR process is not a substitute for but a prerequisite to court access; (2) the process does not unreasonably delay court access; (3) the limitation period is tolled during ADR; and (4) the process does not impose disproportionate costs or procedural burdens.

### New York Convention

The Convention on the Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards (New York, 1958) is the cornerstone of international commercial arbitration. With 172 state parties as of 2025, it ensures that arbitral awards made in one contracting state are recognized and enforced in others, subject to limited grounds for refusal (Article V). All EU member states, the United States, and South Korea are parties.

## Comparative Analysis

### Arbitration Culture

The United States has the most pro-arbitration legal framework, with the FAA's strong preemptive effect and decades of Supreme Court decisions expanding the scope of arbitrable disputes. South Korea's UNCITRAL-based framework provides a modern, internationally compatible regime with growing caseload. Germany's UNCITRAL adoption is technically sophisticated but cultural preference for the highly efficient court system limits domestic arbitration uptake. The EU framework facilitates cross-border arbitration through the New York Convention while setting minimum consumer protection standards.

### Mandatory vs. Voluntary ADR

The jurisdictions differ significantly on mandatory ADR. South Korea mandates mediation in family cases and encourages it broadly. The United States mandates court-annexed ADR in federal courts while permitting contractually mandated private arbitration (with emerging exceptions). Germany requires pre-litigation conciliation in certain categories of disputes in several states. The EU permits but does not require mandatory ADR, subject to ECHR Article 6 access-to-court requirements.

### Consumer Protection

The most significant divergence concerns consumer arbitration. The United States permits and enforces mandatory pre-dispute consumer arbitration clauses, including class action waivers (AT&T Mobility v. Concepcion). The EU framework, by contrast, treats mandatory pre-dispute consumer arbitration clauses as potentially unfair under Directive 93/13/EEC (Unfair Terms Directive), and the CJEU in Mostaza Claro v. Centro Móvil (Case C-168/05, 2006) held that national courts must raise the unfairness of arbitration clauses ex officio. South Korea permits consumer arbitration but the Korea Consumer Agency mediation system provides an accessible alternative.
