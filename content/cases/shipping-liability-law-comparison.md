## Summary

Shipping liability -- the legal framework governing the responsibility of carriers, shipowners, and operators for loss of or damage to cargo, personal injury and death of passengers, and environmental damage arising from maritime transport -- is governed by a complex web of international conventions, national statutes, and contractual terms that reflect centuries of maritime commercial practice. The international regime is anchored by the Hague Rules (1924), the Hague-Visby Rules (1968), the Hamburg Rules (1978), and the Rotterdam Rules (2009, not yet in force), supplemented by liability limitation conventions including the Convention on Limitation of Liability for Maritime Claims (LLMC, 1976/1996) and environmental liability conventions including the International Convention on Civil Liability for Oil Pollution Damage (CLC, 1969/1992). South Korea implements maritime liability through Part 5 of the Commercial Act (상법 제5편 해상편), which underwent comprehensive revision in 2007 incorporating Hague-Visby Rules principles, supplemented by the Act on Limitation of Liability for Maritime Claims (선주책임제한법) and the Oil Pollution Damage Compensation Guarantee Act (유류오염손해배상보장법). The United States applies the Carriage of Goods by Sea Act (COGSA, 46 U.S.C. Section 30701 note), which incorporates the Hague Rules with modifications, alongside general maritime law developed over two centuries of admiralty jurisprudence, the Limitation of Liability Act (46 U.S.C. Sections 30501-30512), and the Oil Pollution Act of 1990. Germany implements the Hague-Visby Rules through the Handelsgesetzbuch (HGB) Sections 498-525, comprehensively reformed in 2013, alongside the LLMC Convention through the Seerechtliche Verteilungsordnung (Maritime Distribution Ordinance) and EU environmental liability provisions. At the European level, EU Regulation 392/2009 on the liability of carriers of passengers by sea incorporates the Athens Convention (2002 Protocol), and the EU has established comprehensive environmental liability frameworks for maritime pollution.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's shipping liability regime is primarily codified in Part 5 of the Commercial Act (상법 제5편 해상편), which was comprehensively revised in 2007 to modernize Korean maritime law and incorporate principles from the Hague-Visby Rules and other international conventions. The revision replaced the former system that was largely based on the Hague Rules.

### Cargo Liability

Articles 795-814 of the Commercial Act establish the carrier's liability for loss of or damage to cargo. The carrier is liable for loss or damage arising between the time the goods are received by the carrier and the time of delivery, unless the carrier proves that the master, crew, pilot, or servants exercised due diligence. Article 797 establishes the liability limit at 666.67 SDR per package or shipping unit, or 2 SDR per kilogram of gross weight, whichever is higher -- consistent with the Hague-Visby Rules.

**Excepted Perils:** Article 796 enumerates excepted perils (carrier defenses) including: act of God; act of war; act of public enemies; seizure under legal process; quarantine restrictions; act or omission of the shipper; strikes or lockouts; defect in packaging; inherent vice of the goods; and fire (unless caused by the carrier's actual fault).

### Limitation of Liability

The Act on Limitation of Liability for Maritime Claims (선주책임제한법) implements the Convention on Limitation of Liability for Maritime Claims (LLMC 1996 Protocol). The Act permits shipowners to limit their liability by constituting a limitation fund. The limitation amounts are periodically adjusted in accordance with the IMO's tacit acceptance amendment procedure.

### Oil Pollution

The Oil Pollution Damage Compensation Guarantee Act (유류오염손해배상보장법) implements the International Convention on Civil Liability for Oil Pollution Damage (CLC 1992 Protocol) and the International Convention on the Establishment of an International Fund for Compensation for Oil Pollution Damage (Fund Convention 1992 Protocol). South Korea is also a party to the Supplementary Fund Protocol (2003), providing a third tier of compensation.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States applies a distinctive maritime liability regime reflecting its long admiralty law tradition. The Carriage of Goods by Sea Act (COGSA, 46 U.S.C. Section 30701 note), enacted in 1936, incorporates the Hague Rules (1924) with modifications. COGSA applies mandatorily to the carriage of goods by sea in foreign trade to or from the United States.

**COGSA Section 4(5) -- Package Limitation:** COGSA limits the carrier's liability to $500 per package or customary freight unit, unless the shipper declares a higher value. This limit, unchanged since 1936, is significantly lower than the Hague-Visby Rules limit (666.67 SDR, approximately $900). Courts have extensively litigated the definition of "package" in containerized shipping, with the leading case being Mitsui & Co. v. American Export Lines, 636 F.2d 807 (2d Cir. 1981).

**Limitation of Liability Act (1851):** The Limitation of Liability Act (46 U.S.C. Sections 30501-30512), one of the oldest US maritime statutes, permits a vessel owner to limit liability to the value of the vessel and its pending freight after the voyage. The Act applies broadly to all maritime claims. The US has not ratified the LLMC Convention.

### Personal Injury and Death

**Jones Act (46 U.S.C. Section 30104):** Provides a cause of action for seamen injured in the course of employment, based on negligence. The Jones Act incorporates the Federal Employers' Liability Act (FELA) standards.

**Death on the High Seas Act (DOHSA, 46 U.S.C. Section 30301):** Provides a wrongful death cause of action for deaths occurring beyond three nautical miles from US shores. Damages are limited to pecuniary loss.

**Oil Pollution Act of 1990 (OPA):** Enacted following the Exxon Valdez spill, OPA imposes strict, joint, and several liability on responsible parties for oil pollution removal costs and damages. Unlike the CLC Convention, OPA does not allow limitation of liability for gross negligence or willful misconduct, and the liability limits are substantially higher than international standards.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's shipping liability regime was comprehensively reformed by the Gesetz zur Reform des Seehandelsrechts (Maritime Trade Law Reform Act) of 2013, which restructured the maritime commerce provisions of the Handelsgesetzbuch (HGB). Sections 498-525 HGB now govern the carrier's liability for cargo, replacing the former provisions that had not been substantially updated since the 19th century.

### Cargo Liability (HGB §§ 498-525)

The 2013 reform aligned German cargo liability law with the Hague-Visby Rules. Section 504 HGB establishes the carrier's liability for loss of or damage to goods from receipt to delivery. Section 502 requires the carrier to exercise due diligence to make the vessel seaworthy before and at the beginning of the voyage. Section 504 provides the carrier with excepted perils defenses consistent with the Hague-Visby Rules.

**Liability Limits:** Section 504a HGB limits the carrier's liability to 666.67 SDR per package or shipping unit, or 2 SDR per kilogram of gross weight, whichever is higher. The carrier loses the right to limit liability where the loss or damage resulted from an act or omission done with intent to cause such loss or damage, or recklessly and with knowledge that such loss or damage would probably result (Section 507 HGB).

### Limitation of Liability

Germany is a party to the LLMC 1976 Convention and the 1996 Protocol. The Seerechtliche Verteilungsordnung (SVertO, Maritime Distribution Ordinance) implements the LLMC framework in German law. The Hamburg maritime courts have significant experience in limitation proceedings.

### Passenger Liability

EU Regulation 392/2009 on the liability of carriers of passengers by sea implements the Athens Convention 2002 Protocol for all EU member states, including Germany. The Regulation establishes compulsory insurance, strict liability for shipping incidents up to 250,000 SDR per passenger, and fault-based liability up to 400,000 SDR per passenger.

## ECHR & EU

### International Convention Framework

The international shipping liability regime consists of multiple interconnected conventions:

**Hague-Visby Rules:** The International Convention for the Unification of Certain Rules of Law Relating to Bills of Lading (Brussels, 1924), as amended by the Visby Protocol (1968) and the SDR Protocol (1979), remains the dominant cargo liability regime. Approximately 90% of world trade by volume is carried under bills of lading governed by the Hague or Hague-Visby Rules.

**Hamburg Rules (1978):** The United Nations Convention on the Carriage of Goods by Sea (Hamburg, 1978) adopted a fault-based liability system more favorable to cargo interests, with higher liability limits. The Rules have been ratified by approximately 35 states but not by the major maritime nations.

**Rotterdam Rules (2009):** The United Nations Convention on Contracts for the International Carriage of Goods Wholly or Partly by Sea (Rotterdam, 2009) represents the most comprehensive attempt to modernize maritime cargo liability law, extending coverage to door-to-door transport. The Convention has been signed by 25 states (including the US) but has not yet entered into force (requiring 20 ratifications; only 5 states have ratified as of 2026).

### EU Regulation 392/2009 -- Passenger Liability

Regulation (EC) No 392/2009 of the European Parliament and of the Council of April 23, 2009, on the liability of carriers of passengers by sea in the event of accidents, incorporates the Athens Convention (2002 Protocol) into EU law. The Regulation applies to all carriers operating passenger vessels in EU waters, regardless of flag state.

**Key Provisions:** Strict liability for shipping incidents (collision, sinking, fire) up to 250,000 SDR per passenger; fault-based liability for non-shipping incidents up to 400,000 SDR; compulsory insurance; direct action against insurers; and advance payments for death or personal injury.

### EU Directive 2009/20/EC -- Insurance

Directive 2009/20/EC on the insurance of shipowners for maritime claims requires shipowners of vessels of 300 gross tonnage and above calling at EU ports to maintain insurance or financial security for maritime claims subject to limitation under the LLMC Convention. This directive ensures minimum insurance coverage for all vessels operating in EU waters.

### CLC and Fund Conventions

The International Convention on Civil Liability for Oil Pollution Damage (CLC, 1992 Protocol) and the International Convention on the Establishment of an International Fund for Compensation for Oil Pollution Damage (Fund Convention, 1992 Protocol), together with the Supplementary Fund Protocol (2003), provide a three-tier compensation system for oil pollution damage from tankers. The International Oil Pollution Compensation Funds (IOPC Funds), headquartered in London, administer the fund system.

## Comparative Analysis

### Cargo Liability Limits

The most significant divergence in cargo liability is the US COGSA $500 per package limit versus the Hague-Visby 666.67 SDR limit applied in South Korea, Germany, and most EU states. The US limit, unchanged since 1936, is substantially lower and has been the subject of persistent criticism. The Rotterdam Rules would raise the limit further to 875 SDR per package or 3 SDR per kilogram, but their entry into force remains uncertain.

### Liability Limitation Systems

The United States applies the 1851 Limitation of Liability Act, which limits liability to the vessel's post-casualty value -- a system that can produce very low limits for damaged or sunken vessels. South Korea and Germany apply the LLMC 1996 Protocol, which provides substantially higher limits calculated based on vessel tonnage rather than post-casualty value.

### Oil Pollution Liability

The US OPA regime is significantly more stringent than the international CLC/Fund system. OPA imposes unlimited liability for gross negligence, does not allow channeling of liability exclusively to the shipowner, and applies to persistent and non-persistent oil. The international system provides for channeled liability to the shipowner with mandatory insurance and supplementary fund compensation.

### Passenger Protection

EU Regulation 392/2009 provides the most comprehensive passenger protection regime, incorporating the Athens Convention with mandatory EU-wide application. The US protects passengers through general maritime law and DOHSA, without a specific convention-based regime. South Korea applies Commercial Act provisions alongside Athens Convention principles.
