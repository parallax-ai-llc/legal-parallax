## Summary

Tobacco and alcohol regulation -- the legal framework governing the production, sale, advertising, taxation, and consumption of tobacco products and alcoholic beverages -- represents one of the most significant intersections of public health policy, individual liberty, commercial freedom, and fiscal policy across all legal systems. South Korea regulates tobacco through the National Health Promotion Act (국민건강증진법), the Tobacco Business Act (담배사업법), and comprehensive advertising restrictions, imposing graphic health warning requirements covering at least 50 percent of cigarette packaging surfaces, banning all tobacco advertising on television and radio, and maintaining a state tobacco monopoly through Korea Tobacco & Ginseng Corporation (KT&G); alcohol regulation is comparatively permissive, governed primarily by the Liquor Tax Act (주세법) and the National Health Promotion Act, with a legal drinking age of 19 and relatively limited advertising restrictions compared to tobacco, though Korea's high per capita alcohol consumption (among the highest in the OECD) has prompted increasing regulatory attention. The United States regulates tobacco primarily through the Family Smoking Prevention and Tobacco Control Act of 2009, which granted the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) authority over tobacco products for the first time, supplemented by the Federal Cigarette Labeling and Advertising Act and state-level regulations; alcohol regulation operates under the Twenty-First Amendment's delegation of regulatory authority to states following the end of Prohibition, creating a complex three-tier distribution system and wide variation in state laws governing sale, consumption, and advertising. Germany regulates tobacco through the Tabakerzeugnisgesetz (TabakerzG, Tobacco Products Act) implementing the EU Tobacco Products Directive 2014/40/EU, with comprehensive advertising restrictions under the Tabakwerbeverbot that was significantly expanded in 2022 to cover outdoor advertising and cinema advertising, and regulated alcohol through the Jugendschutzgesetz (JuSchG, Youth Protection Act) setting minimum purchase ages at 16 for beer and wine and 18 for spirits, with comparatively limited advertising restrictions reflecting Germany's significant beer and wine culture. At the European level, the EU Tobacco Products Directive 2014/40/EU establishes harmonized standards for packaging, health warnings, ingredient regulation, and e-cigarette controls, the WHO Framework Convention on Tobacco Control (FCTC) provides the global treaty framework ratified by all four jurisdictions, and the European Court of Human Rights has addressed tobacco and alcohol regulation under multiple Convention articles, upholding member states' broad margin of appreciation in public health regulation.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework -- Tobacco

**National Health Promotion Act (국민건강증진법):** The primary tobacco control statute, addressing:
- Article 9-2: Smoking prohibited in designated non-smoking areas (공공장소 금연)
- Article 9-4: Tobacco advertising restrictions
- Health warning label requirements

**Tobacco Business Act (담배사업법):** Regulates tobacco production, distribution, and the state's historical monopoly role through KT&G (한국담배인삼공사, privatized in 2002 but government retains significant influence).

### Tobacco Advertising and Packaging

| Restriction | Status |
|------------|--------|
| TV/radio advertising | Banned |
| Newspaper/magazine advertising | Prohibited (limited exception for tobacco trade publications) |
| Outdoor advertising (billboards) | Banned near schools; restricted elsewhere |
| Point-of-sale advertising | Limited to inside retail establishments |
| Internet advertising | Banned on websites accessible to minors |
| Health warnings | Graphic pictorial warnings: 50% of front and back of package |
| Plain packaging | Not implemented (under discussion) |

### Smoking Bans

South Korea has progressively expanded smoke-free zones:
- All indoor public spaces including restaurants, bars, and cafés (since 2015)
- Within 10 meters of building entrances (select municipalities)
- Public parks, bus stops, and pedestrian zones (expanding)
- Apartment complex common areas (since 2016, with resident vote)

### Tobacco Taxation

Tobacco taxes in South Korea are among the highest in Asia. The 2015 cigarette tax increase raised the price of a standard pack from approximately 2,500 KRW to 4,500 KRW, representing a near-doubling of the retail price. The tax components include:
- Tobacco consumption tax (담배소비세): 1,007 KRW
- Local education tax (지방교육세): 443 KRW
- National Health Promotion charge (건강증진부담금): 841 KRW
- Individual consumption tax (개별소비세): 594 KRW
- VAT: ~410 KRW
- Total tax burden: approximately 73% of retail price

### Legal Framework -- Alcohol

South Korea has one of the highest per capita alcohol consumption rates in the OECD, with soju (소주) being the world's best-selling spirit brand by volume. Alcohol regulation is comparatively permissive:

**Liquor Tax Act (주세법):** Governs production licensing and excise taxation of alcoholic beverages. Tax rates vary by product type (soju: 72% ad valorem; beer: 830.3 KRW/liter).

**National Health Promotion Act:** Prohibits selling alcohol to persons under 19. Advertising restrictions are limited compared to tobacco:
- TV advertising permitted but restricted to certain hours (after 10 PM for spirits)
- Mandatory warning labels required but text-only and small
- Self-regulation through the Korea Advertising Review Board

**Drunk Driving:** The legal blood alcohol limit for driving is 0.03% (among the strictest in the world), reduced from 0.05% in June 2019 following the "Yoon Chang-ho Law" (윤창호법), named after a college student killed by a drunk driver.

## United States

### Legal Framework -- Tobacco

**Family Smoking Prevention and Tobacco Control Act (2009):** Granted the FDA authority over tobacco products for the first time. Key provisions:
- FDA Center for Tobacco Products established
- Premarket Tobacco Product Application (PMTA) required for new products
- Authority to set product standards (nicotine levels, ingredient restrictions)
- Prohibition on characterizing flavors in cigarettes (except menthol, though FDA proposed a menthol ban in 2022)
- Restrictions on marketing to youth

**Federal Cigarette Labeling and Advertising Act (1965, amended):**
- Mandates health warnings on cigarette packages
- Current warnings are text-only and relatively small (U.S. is the last OECD country without graphic warnings, though the FDA finalized graphic warning rules in 2020; litigation has delayed implementation)
- Banned TV and radio cigarette advertising (1971)

**State and Local Regulation:**
| Measure | Status |
|---------|--------|
| Indoor smoking bans | 28 states + D.C. have comprehensive smoke-free laws covering workplaces, restaurants, and bars |
| Tobacco purchase age | 21 nationwide since December 2019 (Tobacco 21) |
| Flavored tobacco bans | California, Massachusetts, and several cities have banned flavored tobacco sales |
| E-cigarette regulation | State-level variation; federal PMTA required since 2020 |

### Tobacco Taxation

Federal excise tax: $1.01 per pack (unchanged since 2009). State taxes range from $0.17/pack (Missouri) to $4.35/pack (Connecticut and New York). Combined federal/state/local taxes average approximately 44% of the retail price -- significantly lower than South Korea, the EU, and most OECD countries.

### Legal Framework -- Alcohol

**Twenty-First Amendment (1933):** Repealed Prohibition and granted states primary authority over alcohol regulation. This constitutional delegation creates the most decentralized alcohol regulatory system among the four jurisdictions.

**National Minimum Drinking Age Act (1984):** Conditions federal highway funding on states maintaining a minimum purchase age of 21. All states comply, making 21 the de facto national drinking age -- the highest among the four jurisdictions.

**Three-Tier System:** Most states require a three-tier distribution system separating producers, wholesalers/distributors, and retailers. This post-Prohibition framework prevents vertical integration and is intended to reduce market power concentration but has been criticized as reducing competition and increasing consumer prices.

**Alcohol Advertising:** No federal ban on alcohol advertising. The industry relies primarily on self-regulation through voluntary codes (Distilled Spirits Council, Beer Institute). The FTC monitors alcohol advertising practices but has not imposed comprehensive mandatory restrictions. Several studies have questioned the effectiveness of self-regulation.

## Germany

### Legal Framework -- Tobacco

**Tabakerzeugnisgesetz (TabakerzG, 2016):** Transposed the EU Tobacco Products Directive 2014/40/EU into German law. Key provisions:
- Graphic health warnings covering 65% of both front and back of packages
- Standardized warning format with color photographs and text
- Ban on characterizing flavors (implementation completed by May 2020)
- Track-and-trace and security features to combat illicit trade
- E-cigarette regulation (labeling, packaging, nicotine limits of 20 mg/mL)

**Tabakwerbeverbot (Tobacco Advertising Ban):** Germany was historically the last EU member state to permit tobacco billboard advertising. The comprehensive expansion took effect in phases:
- January 1, 2022: Ban on outdoor advertising (billboards) for tobacco products
- January 1, 2023: Ban on outdoor advertising for heated tobacco products
- January 1, 2024: Ban on outdoor advertising for e-cigarettes
- Cinema advertising banned for all tobacco and related products (previously only before films rated for under-18 audiences)

**Nichtraucherschutzgesetze (Non-Smoker Protection Laws):** Each Land has its own non-smoker protection law. All Länder prohibit smoking in public buildings, hospitals, schools, and public transport. Restaurant and bar regulations vary:
- Bavaria: Complete smoking ban (strictest, since 2010 referendum)
- Berlin: Ban with exceptions for designated smoking rooms
- North Rhine-Westphalia: Ban with exceptions for "smoking pubs" under 75 m²

### Legal Framework -- Alcohol

**Jugendschutzgesetz (JuSchG, Youth Protection Act):**
- Section 9: Beer, wine, and sparkling wine may be sold to persons aged 16 and over; spirits (and spirit-mixed drinks) may be sold only to persons aged 18 and over
- Accompanied by a parent: children aged 14-15 may consume beer and wine

**German Beer Purity Law (Reinheitsgebot):** While primarily a product quality standard dating to 1516, the Reinheitsgebot (codified in the Provisional German Beer Act) reflects Germany's deep cultural relationship with beer and influences the regulatory approach, which tends to regulate product quality rather than restrict access.

**Alcohol Advertising:** Germany has minimal statutory restrictions on alcohol advertising. The UWG (Unfair Competition Act) and JuSchG prohibit advertising alcohol to minors, but general advertising to adults is largely unrestricted. The German Advertising Council (Deutscher Werberat) operates a self-regulatory code for alcohol advertising.

**Alcohol Taxation:**
- Beer: EUR 0.787/hectoliter/degree Plato (approximately EUR 0.10 per standard 0.5L beer)
- Wine: EUR 0 (wine is exempt from excise tax in Germany)
- Spirits: EUR 13.03/hectoliter of pure alcohol

Germany's alcohol taxation is among the lowest in the EU, reflecting the cultural and economic importance of the beer and wine industries.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Tobacco Products Directive 2014/40/EU (TPD)

The TPD establishes harmonized rules for tobacco and related products across all EU member states:

**Health Warnings (Articles 8-12):**
- Combined text and pictorial warnings covering 65% of front and back of cigarette packages
- General warning "Smoking kills" on 50% of package side
- Information message directing smokers to cessation services
- Picture library updated regularly by the European Commission

**Ingredients (Articles 3-7):**
- Ban on characterizing flavors (menthol banned since May 20, 2020)
- Maximum nicotine yield: 1 mg, tar yield: 10 mg, CO yield: 10 mg
- Ingredient reporting requirements

**E-Cigarettes (Article 20):**
- Maximum nicotine concentration: 20 mg/mL
- Maximum tank/cartridge volume: 2 mL; maximum refill container: 10 mL
- Health warnings required
- Advertising restrictions (cross-border advertising prohibited)
- Member states may ban or further restrict e-cigarettes

### WHO Framework Convention on Tobacco Control (FCTC)

The FCTC, adopted on May 21, 2003, and entered into force on February 27, 2005, is the first public health treaty negotiated under the WHO. All four jurisdictions are parties. Key obligations:

| Article | Obligation |
|---------|------------|
| Art. 6 | Price and tax measures to reduce demand |
| Art. 8 | Protection from exposure to tobacco smoke |
| Art. 11 | Packaging and labeling (warnings covering ≥50% of principal display areas) |
| Art. 13 | Comprehensive ban on tobacco advertising, promotion, and sponsorship |
| Art. 14 | Demand reduction through cessation programs |

### EU Alcohol Policy

The EU has not enacted a comprehensive alcohol directive comparable to the TPD. Relevant instruments include:

**Audiovisual Media Services Directive (AVMSD, Directive 2010/13/EU, revised 2018):**
- Article 22: Audiovisual commercial communications for alcoholic beverages shall not be aimed at minors, encourage immoderate consumption, or link alcohol to physical performance or social success
- Applies to TV broadcasting and video-sharing platforms

**EU Cancer Plan (2021):** The European Commission's Beating Cancer Plan includes proposals to review alcohol labeling rules, including mandatory ingredient lists and nutrition declarations on alcoholic beverages, and proposals to lower the threshold for tax-free allowances for cross-border purchases.

**Excise Duty Directive 2020/1151:** Harmonizes minimum excise duty rates for alcohol and alcoholic beverages across EU member states, including minimum rates for beer, wine, intermediate products, and ethyl alcohol.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**British American Tobacco v. United Kingdom (Application No. 39067/16, 2017):** The ECHR declared inadmissible a challenge to the UK's Standardised Packaging of Tobacco Products Regulations 2015 (plain packaging), finding that the interference with the applicant's property rights under Article 1 of Protocol 1 was justified as a proportionate measure in the public interest of health protection.

**Mouvement Raëlien Suisse v. Switzerland (Grand Chamber, 2012):** While about poster advertising generally, the Court's reasoning on the margin of appreciation for restrictions on commercial advertising in the public interest is applicable to tobacco and alcohol advertising restrictions.

## Comparative Analysis

### Tobacco Regulation Convergence

The four jurisdictions show strong convergence in tobacco regulation, driven by the FCTC's binding obligations. All have banned TV/radio tobacco advertising, required health warnings, and imposed indoor smoking bans (with varying comprehensiveness). The EU/Germany has the largest health warnings (65% of package). South Korea has the highest combined tax burden. The United States is notable for still lacking graphic health warnings and for not having a federal indoor smoking ban.

### Alcohol Regulation Divergence

In contrast to tobacco convergence, alcohol regulation shows significant divergence reflecting different cultural attitudes. Germany's low drinking age (16 for beer/wine) and minimal taxation contrast sharply with the United States' 21 drinking age and South Korea's strict 0.03% BAC limit for driving. The EU has been reluctant to regulate alcohol as aggressively as tobacco, reflecting the economic and cultural importance of wine and beer production in member states.

### E-Cigarettes and Novel Products

E-cigarette regulation represents the frontier of tobacco policy. The EU TPD provides a comprehensive framework with nicotine limits and advertising restrictions. South Korea regulates e-cigarettes as tobacco products subject to taxation and advertising restrictions. The United States' FDA PMTA process has resulted in authorization of some products while denying millions of others. Germany implemented the TPD requirements and expanded its advertising ban to cover e-cigarettes in 2024. The long-term regulatory trajectory for novel nicotine products remains uncertain.

### Advertising Freedom vs. Public Health

The tension between commercial speech protections and public health objectives is most acute in the United States, where First Amendment protections for commercial speech constrain regulatory options (though the Supreme Court in Lorillard Tobacco Co. v. Reilly, 2001, permitted significant restrictions). European jurisdictions and South Korea provide less protection for commercial speech, enabling more comprehensive advertising bans. Germany's belated expansion of its tobacco advertising ban in 2022 illustrated how even within the EU, cultural and industry resistance can delay implementation.
