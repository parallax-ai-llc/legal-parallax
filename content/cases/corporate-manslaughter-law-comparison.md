## Summary

Corporate manslaughter -- the criminal liability of corporations and their officers for deaths caused by gross organizational negligence -- represents one of the most challenging intersections of criminal law, corporate governance, and workplace safety regulation. Jurisdictions worldwide have adopted strikingly different approaches to this problem. South Korea enacted its Serious Accidents Punishment Act (SAPA) in 2021, imposing direct criminal liability on business owners and responsible management personnel, with penalties including imprisonment of one year or more for fatal accidents. The United States lacks a unified federal corporate manslaughter statute, instead relying on a patchwork of OSHA criminal provisions (limited to six months imprisonment for willful violations causing death), state-level involuntary manslaughter prosecutions, and regulatory enforcement -- as demonstrated by PG&E's guilty plea to 84 counts of involuntary manslaughter for the 2018 Camp Fire. Germany adheres to the principle of "societas delinquere non potest" (a corporation cannot commit a crime), prosecuting individual managers under Section 222 StGB (negligent homicide) while imposing only administrative fines on corporate entities under the Act on Regulatory Offences (OWiG); a proposed Corporate Sanctions Act (Verbandssanktionengesetz) that would have introduced true corporate criminal liability collapsed in 2021. The European Court of Human Rights, through its Article 2 jurisprudence -- most notably Oneryildiz v. Turkey (2004) -- imposes positive obligations on states to establish legislative and administrative frameworks that effectively deter threats to life from hazardous industrial activities, while the EU Framework Directive 89/391/EEC sets baseline occupational safety standards that member states must transpose into national law with adequate enforcement mechanisms.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's approach to corporate manslaughter underwent a fundamental transformation with the enactment of the Serious Accidents Punishment Act (SAPA, 중대재해 처벌 등에 관한 법률), promulgated on January 26, 2021 and enforced beginning January 27, 2022.

Prior to SAPA, workplace deaths were addressed primarily through:

| Statute | Provision | Penalty |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Occupational Safety and Health Act (OSHA-K) | Employer safety violations causing death | Up to 7 years imprisonment or fine up to KRW 100 million |
| Criminal Act, Article 268 | Death by occupational or gross negligence (업무상과실치사) | Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine up to KRW 20 million |

SAPA significantly escalated penalties and broadened the scope of liability:

| Category | Individual Penalty | Corporate Penalty |
|----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Serious industrial accident causing death | Imprisonment of 1 year or more; fine up to KRW 1 billion (~USD 770,000) | Fine up to KRW 5 billion (~USD 3.8 million) |
| Serious industrial accident causing injury/illness | Up to 7 years imprisonment; fine up to KRW 100 million | Fine up to KRW 1 billion |
| Serious public accident causing death | Imprisonment of 1 year or more; fine up to KRW 1 billion | Fine up to KRW 5 billion |
| Serious public accident causing injury/illness | Up to 7 years imprisonment; fine up to KRW 100 million | Fine up to KRW 1 billion |

### Scope and Application

SAPA applies to two broad categories of serious accidents:

1. **Serious industrial accidents (중대산업재해):** Accidents at workplaces resulting in death, serious injury requiring six months or more of treatment, or occupational disease affecting three or more workers within one year.

2. **Serious public accidents (중대시민재해):** Disasters caused by defects in the design, manufacture, installation, or management of products, product ingredients, public facilities, or public transportation.

The Act initially applied only to businesses with 50 or more employees. From January 27, 2024, its scope expanded to cover businesses and workplaces with 5 or more regular workers and construction projects of any size.

### Key Obligations

SAPA imposes affirmative duties on "business owners or responsible management personnel" (사업주 또는 경영책임자), requiring them to:

- Establish and implement a safety and health management system
- Allocate dedicated safety and health personnel and budget
- Develop procedures for identifying and improving hazards and risks
- Establish and implement a safety and health management system for public safety obligations

### Landmark Cases

**Aricell Battery Factory Fire (June 2024):** This case produced the first arrest and detention under SAPA. On June 24, 2024, a fire at the Aricell lithium battery manufacturing plant in Hwaseong, Gyeonggi Province, killed 23 workers. The CEO and his son were arrested for failing to fulfill their safety management obligations under SAPA. The case highlighted the law's reach to the highest levels of corporate management and represented a watershed moment in its enforcement.

**SPC Samlip Conveyor Belt Death (2025):** Authorities conducted a search and seizure operation at the headquarters and bread factory of confectionery giant SPC Samlip after a worker became trapped and died on a moving conveyor belt. The investigation examined potential violations of both SAPA and the Occupational Safety and Health Act. SPC Samlip had previously faced public outcry over recurring worker safety failures at its facilities.

**Conviction Statistics (2022-2024):** Between SAPA's enforcement in January 2022 and late 2024, 15 cases resulted in finalized convictions. Fourteen company chiefs received suspended prison sentences of one to three years, while only one received an actual prison term of one year. The 15 companies were assessed penalties ranging from KRW 20 million to KRW 100 million (~USD 14,000-70,000), amounts that critics argue are far below the statutory maximums and insufficient to serve as effective deterrents.

**Itaewon Crowd Crush (October 2022):** While primarily prosecuted under existing negligent homicide provisions (Criminal Act Article 268) rather than SAPA, the disaster that killed 159 people during Halloween festivities in Seoul's Itaewon district intensified public discourse about institutional accountability for mass casualties. The former chief of Yongsan Police Station was sentenced to three years imprisonment for professional negligence resulting in death.

### Punitive Damages

SAPA also introduced a civil punitive damages provision: victims and their families may claim up to five times the actual damages suffered from the responsible business owner or corporation, marking a significant departure from Korean civil law's traditionally compensatory damages framework.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States lacks a dedicated federal corporate manslaughter statute. Corporate criminal liability for workplace deaths is instead addressed through a fragmented system of federal and state laws:

**Federal Level:**

| Statute | Provision | Penalty |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| OSH Act Section 17(e), 29 U.S.C. Section 666(e) | Willful violation causing employee death | Up to 6 months imprisonment; fine up to $250,000 (individual) or $500,000 (corporation) |
| 18 U.S.C. Section 1112 | Involuntary manslaughter (federal jurisdiction) | Up to 8 years imprisonment |
| 18 U.S.C. Section 1115 | Misconduct or neglect of ship officers (seaman's manslaughter) | Up to 10 years imprisonment |

The criminal penalty under OSHA for a willful violation causing death -- a maximum of six months imprisonment, classified as a misdemeanor -- has been widely criticized as inadequate. Since the Occupational Safety and Health Act was enacted in 1970, only approximately 100 workplace fatalities have been criminally prosecuted under its provisions despite tens of thousands of workplace deaths during that period.

**State Level:**

States possess broader tools for prosecuting corporate actors for workplace deaths, typically through general involuntary manslaughter or negligent homicide statutes. Penalties vary dramatically:

| State | Charge | Maximum Penalty |
|-------|--------|-----------------|
| California | Involuntary manslaughter (Penal Code Section 192(b)) | 2-4 years imprisonment |
| New York | Manslaughter in the second degree (Penal Law Section 125.15) | 5-15 years imprisonment |
| Washington | Workplace safety violation causing death (RCW 49.17.190) | 6 months imprisonment; $100,000 fine |
| Illinois | Involuntary manslaughter (720 ILCS 5/9-3) | 2-5 years imprisonment |

### Doctrine of Corporate Criminal Liability

Under U.S. law, a corporation may be held vicariously liable for criminal acts committed by its employees if the employee acted: (1) within the scope of employment, and (2) with the intent, at least in part, to benefit the corporation. This doctrine, established in New York Central & Hudson River Railroad Co. v. United States (1909), permits corporate prosecution for manslaughter in appropriate cases.

### Landmark Cases

**People v. Film Recovery Systems, Inc. (Illinois, 1985):** In a groundbreaking case, three executives of Film Recovery Systems were convicted of murder (later reduced to involuntary manslaughter on appeal) after a worker died from cyanide poisoning at the company's silver recovery plant. The executives had knowingly exposed workers -- many of them non-English-speaking immigrants -- to lethal cyanide fumes without adequate safety equipment or warnings. This was one of the earliest successful prosecutions of individual corporate officers for a workplace death under homicide statutes.

**BP Exploration and Production Inc. (2012-2013):** Following the Deepwater Horizon explosion on April 20, 2010, which killed 11 workers and caused the largest marine oil spill in history, BP pleaded guilty to 11 counts of felony manslaughter, one count of felony obstruction of Congress, and violations of the Clean Water Act and Migratory Bird Treaty Act. BP was sentenced to pay $4 billion in criminal fines and penalties -- the largest criminal resolution in U.S. history at that time. However, individual manslaughter charges against two BP well site leaders, Robert Kaluza and Donald Vidrine, were ultimately dropped by federal prosecutors, with Vidrine pleading to a single misdemeanor Clean Water Act violation.

**PG&E and the Camp Fire (2020):** Pacific Gas and Electric Company pleaded guilty to 84 felony counts of involuntary manslaughter stemming from the November 2018 Camp Fire, which was ignited by a failing C-hook on a nearly century-old transmission tower. The fire killed 84 people and destroyed the town of Paradise, California, burning 153,336 acres. PG&E had previously been convicted of six federal felonies related to a 2010 gas pipeline explosion in San Bruno that killed eight people. The Camp Fire prosecution demonstrated that state-level involuntary manslaughter statutes can serve as far more potent tools than federal OSHA provisions for holding corporations accountable for mass workplace and public safety failures.

**Imperial Food Products Fire (1991):** The owner of Imperial Food Products in Hamlet, North Carolina, Emmett Roe, pleaded guilty to 25 counts of involuntary manslaughter after 25 workers died in a fire at his chicken processing plant. The plant's fire doors had been locked or blocked to prevent employee theft. Roe was sentenced to 19 years and 11 months in prison -- one of the longest sentences ever imposed for a U.S. workplace safety crime.

### Structural Limitations

The U.S. system faces several persistent structural limitations in addressing corporate manslaughter:

- **OSHA criminal penalties are misdemeanor-level**, limiting deterrent effect and prosecutorial interest
- **Prosecutorial discretion** favors civil enforcement and deferred prosecution agreements over criminal charges
- **Proving individual mens rea** for corporate officers remains challenging under general homicide statutes
- **No federal corporate manslaughter statute** exists, despite repeated legislative proposals

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany adheres to the foundational principle of "societas delinquere non potest" -- a corporation cannot commit a crime. German criminal law does not recognize corporate criminal liability. Instead, the system addresses corporate misconduct causing death through two parallel tracks:

**Individual Criminal Liability:**

| Provision | Description | Penalty |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| Section 222 StGB | Negligent killing (Fahrlassige Totung) | Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine |
| Section 229 StGB | Negligent bodily harm (Fahrlassige Korperverletzung) | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine |
| Section 13 StGB | Commission by omission (Garantenstellung) | Liability for failure to act where a legal duty exists |

Managers, directors, and safety officers who fail to fulfill their supervisory duties may be personally prosecuted under Section 222 StGB if their negligence causes a worker's death. The legal basis for individual responsibility rests on the concept of Garantenstellung (guarantor position): persons who hold a position of responsibility for the safety of others have a legal duty to prevent harm.

**Corporate Administrative Sanctions:**

| Provision | Description | Penalty |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| Section 30 OWiG | Administrative fine against legal entities for offences by responsible persons | Up to EUR 10 million |
| Section 130 OWiG | Violation of supervisory duties | Fine; amount varies by underlying offence |

Under Section 30 of the Act on Regulatory Offences (Ordnungswidrigkeitengesetz, OWiG), a corporate entity may be fined if a person acting in a managerial capacity commits a criminal offence or regulatory violation that breaches duties incumbent upon the entity or through which the entity was or was intended to be enriched.

### The Failed Verbandssanktionengesetz (Corporate Sanctions Act)

In 2020, the German Federal Ministry of Justice and Consumer Protection introduced a draft bill for the Gesetz zur Starkung der Integritat in der Wirtschaft (Act to Strengthen Integrity in the Economy), which would have created the Verbandssanktionengesetz (VerSanG) -- Germany's first corporate criminal liability regime. Key proposed provisions included:

- **Corporate criminal liability** for criminal acts committed by management or due to inadequate supervision
- **Sanctions for large companies** (turnover above EUR 100 million): fines of up to 10% of global annual group turnover
- **Internal investigation incentives**: mitigated sanctions for companies that conduct internal investigations and cooperate with law enforcement
- **Public register** of corporate sanctions
- **Association-specific sanctions** including disgorgement of profits

The bill collapsed in the summer of 2021 during coalition negotiations, and the incoming coalition government did not revive it. Germany thus remains one of the few major industrialized nations without a comprehensive corporate criminal liability framework.

### Workplace Safety Enforcement

Germany's occupational safety regime operates primarily through the Arbeitsschutzgesetz (Occupational Safety Act), the Gewerbeordnung (Industrial Code), and sector-specific regulations enforced by the Gewerbeaufsichtsamter (trade supervisory offices) and Berufsgenossenschaften (employers' liability insurance associations). Enforcement is predominantly administrative, with criminal prosecution reserved for egregious cases of individual negligence.

### Notable Cases

**Eschede Train Disaster (1998):** On June 3, 1998, an ICE high-speed train derailed near Eschede, killing 101 people in Germany's worst rail disaster. The accident was caused by a broken wheel tire. Three engineers from Deutsche Bahn were charged with negligent homicide (Section 222 StGB) and negligent bodily harm, but were acquitted in 2003 after the court found insufficient evidence of individual negligence. Deutsche Bahn as a corporation could not be criminally charged. The case underscored the limitations of Germany's individual-liability-only approach to industrial disasters.

**ThyssenKrupp Steel Factory Fire (2010):** An explosion and fire at a ThyssenKrupp steel mill in Turin, Italy (a German-headquartered company's Italian subsidiary), killed seven workers. While prosecuted under Italian law (which does recognize corporate criminal liability), the case prompted renewed debate within Germany about the adequacy of its administrative-fine-only approach to corporate misconduct causing death.

**Loveparade Crowd Crush (2010):** Twenty-one people died in a crowd crush at the Loveparade music festival in Duisburg. Criminal proceedings against event organizers and city officials for negligent homicide under Section 222 StGB were initiated but ultimately terminated in 2020 due to statute of limitations expiration and insufficient evidence of individual culpability -- again highlighting the structural challenges of prosecuting organizational failures through individual criminal liability alone.

## ECHR & EU

### European Convention on Human Rights -- Article 2

The European Court of Human Rights (ECtHR) does not directly legislate corporate manslaughter offences. Instead, through its Article 2 (right to life) jurisprudence, the Court imposes positive obligations on Contracting States to establish legal frameworks that effectively protect life in the context of hazardous activities, including industrial and corporate operations.

The Court has articulated three categories of positive obligation under Article 2 relevant to corporate manslaughter:

1. **Substantive obligation to prevent:** States must put in place a legislative and administrative framework designed to provide effective deterrence against threats to the right to life, including regulations geared to the special features of hazardous activities.

2. **Procedural obligation to investigate:** Where lives have been lost in circumstances engaging state responsibility, Article 2 requires an effective official investigation capable of leading to a determination of whether the force used was justified and to the identification and punishment of those responsible.

3. **Obligation to inform and warn:** States must ensure that adequate information is provided to the public about risks to life arising from hazardous industrial activities.

### Landmark ECHR Cases

**Oneryildiz v. Turkey (Grand Chamber, 2004):** This seminal case arose from a methane explosion at a municipal rubbish tip in Istanbul that killed 39 people living in an adjacent unauthorized settlement. The Grand Chamber held that Turkey had violated Article 2 in both its substantive and procedural dimensions. Substantively, the authorities had known of the risk of a methane explosion (a 1993 expert report had warned of the danger) but had failed to take practical measures to protect residents. Procedurally, the criminal proceedings against the responsible officials had resulted in inadequate penalties (a minimum fine, subsequently amnestied). The Court established that where dangerous industrial activities are at stake, Article 2 requires states to establish a framework of laws, administrative measures, and enforcement mechanisms that effectively protect the right to life. This was the first successful Article 2 claim in the context of industrial hazards.

**Budayeva and Others v. Russia (2008):** The Court found violations of Article 2 after mudslides killed eight people in the town of Tyrnauz. The Russian authorities had been aware of the risk but had failed to maintain protective infrastructure or implement an evacuation plan. The case extended the Oneryildiz principles to natural hazards exacerbated by state inaction.

**Kolyadenko and Others v. Russia (2012):** The Court held that Russia violated Article 2 when emergency water releases from a reservoir flooded a residential area, finding that the authorities had failed to maintain the drainage infrastructure and to establish an adequate early warning system.

### EU Framework Directive 89/391/EEC

The EU Framework Directive on Occupational Safety and Health (89/391/EEC), adopted June 12, 1989, establishes fundamental obligations for employers across all EU member states:

- **General duty of prevention:** Employers must ensure the safety and health of workers in every aspect related to work
- **Risk assessment:** Employers must evaluate risks to safety and health and implement protective measures
- **Information and training:** Workers must be informed of occupational risks and trained in safety procedures
- **Worker consultation:** Workers and their representatives must be consulted on safety matters

The Directive does not prescribe specific criminal penalties. Instead, it requires member states to ensure "adequate penalties" for violations, leaving the choice between criminal, administrative, and civil enforcement to national discretion. This has produced significant variation across the EU:

| Member State | Corporate Criminal Liability for Workplace Death | Maximum Penalty |
|-------------|--------------------------------------------------|-----------------|
| United Kingdom (pre-Brexit) | Corporate Manslaughter and Corporate Homicide Act 2007 | Unlimited fine; remedial orders; publicity orders |
| France | Code Penal, Articles 121-2, 221-6 | EUR 225,000 fine (corporate); 5 years imprisonment (individual) |
| Italy | Legislative Decree 231/2001 | Corporate fines; disqualification measures; confiscation |
| Netherlands | Dutch Criminal Code, Article 51 | Fines; publication of judgment; disqualification |
| Spain | Ley Organica 1/2015 (amendment to Criminal Code) | Fines; dissolution; suspension of activities |

### EU Directive 2017/1371 (PIF Directive)

While not specifically targeting corporate manslaughter, Directive (EU) 2017/1371 on the fight against fraud to the Union's financial interests by means of criminal law is notable as the EU's most explicit requirement for corporate criminal liability. Articles 6 and 9 oblige member states to establish liability of legal persons for offences committed for their benefit by persons in leading positions, with sanctions that are "effective, proportionate and dissuasive." The PIF Directive demonstrates the EU's capacity to mandate corporate criminal liability frameworks, a model that workplace safety advocates have urged be extended to occupational deaths.

### EU Strategic Framework on Health and Safety at Work 2021-2027

The European Commission's Strategic Framework on Health and Safety at Work 2021-2027 emphasizes a "Vision Zero" approach to work-related deaths, calling for improved enforcement of existing directives, updated occupational exposure limits, and enhanced preparedness for future health crises. While the framework does not introduce new criminal liability mechanisms, it signals the EU's continued prioritization of workplace safety as a policy objective.

## Comparative Analysis

### Philosophical Approaches

The four jurisdictions reflect fundamentally different philosophical approaches to corporate accountability for death:

**South Korea (Regulatory Interventionism):** SAPA represents the most aggressive recent legislative response to corporate manslaughter, directly imposing criminal liability on business owners and senior management with mandatory minimum prison sentences. The law reflects a societal demand -- intensified by disasters such as the Sewol ferry sinking (2014) and the Itaewon crowd crush (2022) -- for personal accountability at the highest corporate levels.

**United States (Fragmented Pragmatism):** The U.S. approach is characterized by the tension between weak federal enforcement (OSHA misdemeanor penalties) and potentially powerful but inconsistently applied state prosecutorial tools. The PG&E and BP cases demonstrate that, when political will exists, existing legal frameworks can produce significant corporate accountability -- but such prosecutions remain exceptional rather than systematic.

**Germany (Individual Liability Orthodoxy):** Germany's rejection of corporate criminal liability in favor of individual prosecution and administrative fines reflects a deeply rooted legal philosophy that criminal culpability requires personal moral blameworthiness. The failure of the VerSanG reform demonstrates the enduring strength of this tradition, even as major industrial disasters expose its practical limitations.

**ECHR/EU (Supranational Standard-Setting):** The ECHR and EU operate at a meta-level, establishing minimum standards and positive obligations that member states must fulfill through national law. The ECHR's Article 2 jurisprudence has been particularly influential in requiring states to maintain effective regulatory and investigative frameworks, even if the choice of specific legal mechanisms remains within the state's margin of appreciation.

### Enforcement Gaps

Across all jurisdictions, a common enforcement gap persists: the disparity between statutory maximum penalties and actual sentences imposed. South Korea's early SAPA convictions have resulted overwhelmingly in suspended sentences. The United States' OSHA criminal provisions are rarely invoked. Germany's administrative fines under OWiG are capped far below levels that would deter large corporations. The ECHR can only adjudicate individual complaints after domestic remedies have been exhausted, limiting its systemic impact.

### Emerging Trends

Several convergent trends are visible across jurisdictions:

1. **Expansion of individual officer liability:** All systems are moving toward holding senior management personally accountable, not merely the corporation as an abstract entity
2. **Punitive damages and civil remedies:** South Korea's five-times punitive damages provision and the U.S. system's reliance on civil litigation supplement criminal enforcement
3. **Compliance program incentives:** The failed German VerSanG and existing U.S. sentencing guidelines both reflect a trend toward rewarding corporate compliance programs with reduced sanctions
4. **Public pressure as enforcement catalyst:** In every jurisdiction examined, major disasters and public outrage have been the primary drivers of legislative reform and prosecutorial action
