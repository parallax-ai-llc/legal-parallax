## Summary

Restorative justice -- an approach to criminal justice that focuses on repairing the harm caused by crime through cooperative processes involving victims, offenders, and community members, rather than on punishing the offender through adversarial proceedings and incarceration -- has grown from a marginal alternative justice movement in the 1970s to an increasingly mainstream component of criminal justice systems worldwide, supported by substantial empirical evidence demonstrating higher victim satisfaction, reduced recidivism, and greater offender accountability compared to conventional criminal proceedings. South Korea formalized restorative justice through the Criminal Mediation Act (형사조정법, 2010) and the Restorative Justice in Criminal Matters Pilot Program (형사사건 회복적 사법 시범사업), launched by the Ministry of Justice in 2019 and expanded nationally in 2022, operating alongside the juvenile restorative justice framework under the Juvenile Act (소년법) which permits family courts to order restorative conferences and victim-offender mediation for juvenile offenders. The United States has developed restorative justice through a decentralized patchwork of state and local programs, with Vermont's community justice centers (established 1994), Colorado's Restorative Justice Council (statutory since 2007), and the federal SUPPORT for Patients and Communities Act (2018) representing key institutional developments, though no comprehensive federal restorative justice statute exists; empirical studies by the U.S. Department of Justice's Office of Justice Programs have found that restorative justice programs reduce recidivism by approximately 27% compared to traditional criminal justice processing. Germany has integrated restorative justice most deeply into its criminal code through the Täter-Opfer-Ausgleich (offender-victim mediation, TOA) provisions of Sections 46a and 155a-b of the Strafprozessordnung (StPO) and Section 10(1)(7) of the Jugendgerichtsgesetz (JGG -- Youth Courts Act), with approximately 30,000 TOA cases processed annually through a nationwide network of over 400 mediation services. The European framework is shaped by EU Directive 2012/29/EU (Victims' Rights Directive), which guarantees victims' access to restorative justice services under Article 12, the Council of Europe Recommendation CM/Rec(2018)8 on restorative justice in criminal matters, and the United Nations Basic Principles on the Use of Restorative Justice Programmes in Criminal Matters (ECOSOC Resolution 2002/12).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea has developed restorative justice through two primary mechanisms: criminal mediation administered by prosecutors, and a newer restorative justice program administered by the Ministry of Justice.

### Criminal Mediation Act (형사조정법, 2010)

The Criminal Mediation Act, effective September 2010, established a formal framework for victim-offender mediation (형사조정) within the prosecution system. Key features include:

**Eligibility:** Cases involving property crimes, minor assault, defamation, and other offenses where the relationship between victim and offender makes resolution through mediation appropriate. The Act excludes serious violent crimes and cases where power imbalances make mediation inappropriate.

**Process:** The prosecutor refers eligible cases to a Criminal Mediation Committee (형사조정위원회), composed of community members with expertise in law, counseling, or social work. The committee facilitates dialogue between victim and offender, aiming to reach an agreement on restitution, apology, and other reparative measures.

**Effect:** A successful mediation agreement may result in the prosecutor's decision not to indict (기소유예) or to apply for a suspended sentence. The agreement itself is not legally binding in the same manner as a court judgment but is treated as a significant factor in prosecutorial and judicial decision-making.

### Restorative Justice Pilot Program (2019)

The Ministry of Justice launched the Restorative Justice in Criminal Matters Pilot Program (형사사건 회복적 사법 시범사업) in 2019, initially in six pilot sites and expanded nationally in 2022. The program differs from criminal mediation in several respects:

- Employs trained restorative justice facilitators rather than prosecution-appointed mediators
- Includes community members and support persons in the process
- Uses conferencing and circle models in addition to direct mediation
- Addresses the broader impact of crime on community relationships

### Juvenile Restorative Justice

The Juvenile Act (소년법) provides the most established restorative justice framework in South Korea. Family courts may order:
- Victim-offender mediation (피해자-가해자 조정)
- Restorative conferences (회복적 대화모임)
- Community service with a restorative component
- Participation in restorative programs operated by the Korean Institute of Criminology

### Statistics

Criminal mediation cases have grown from approximately 3,500 in 2011 to approximately 10,000 annually by 2024. Agreement rates average approximately 65-70%, with victim satisfaction surveys indicating higher satisfaction than conventional prosecution outcomes.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has no comprehensive federal restorative justice statute, resulting in a highly decentralized landscape of state and local programs with varying degrees of statutory authorization and institutional support.

### State Frameworks

**Vermont (1994):** Vermont established the first statewide restorative justice system through its Community Justice Centers, which operate reparative boards as an alternative to incarceration for non-violent offenses. The program has processed tens of thousands of cases since inception, with recidivism rates approximately 30% lower than conventional prosecution.

**Colorado (2007):** The Restorative Justice Council Act (C.R.S. Section 19-2.5-1102) established a Restorative Justice Coordinating Council within the Division of Criminal Justice, tasked with developing standards, providing training, and funding restorative justice programs statewide. Colorado law permits restorative justice at the pre-filing, post-filing, and post-conviction stages for both juvenile and adult offenses.

**Minnesota:** Minnesota has one of the longest histories of restorative justice practice in the United States, dating to the first documented victim-offender mediation program in Elkhart, Indiana (1974) and its adaptation in Minnesota communities in the early 1980s. Minnesota's community mediation centers process thousands of cases annually.

### Federal Developments

While no comprehensive federal restorative justice statute exists, several federal initiatives have supported restorative justice:
- The Office of Justice Programs (OJP) has funded restorative justice research and demonstration projects
- The SUPPORT for Patients and Communities Act (2018) included provisions promoting restorative justice approaches in juvenile drug offense cases
- The Department of Education has promoted restorative practices in schools as an alternative to exclusionary discipline

### Empirical Evidence

A meta-analysis by Sherman and Strang (2007), commissioned by the Smith Institute, found that restorative justice conferencing:
- Reduced recidivism by an average of 27%
- Produced higher victim satisfaction than conventional criminal justice
- Increased the likelihood of offender compliance with reparation agreements
- Reduced victims' desire for violent revenge against offenders

### Challenges

Restorative justice in the United States faces several challenges:
- Inconsistent funding and institutional support
- Variation in training standards for facilitators
- Racial and socioeconomic disparities in access to restorative justice programs
- Tension between restorative justice principles and mandatory sentencing requirements

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany has integrated restorative justice most deeply into its formal criminal justice system of the four jurisdictions examined, primarily through the Täter-Opfer-Ausgleich (TOA -- offender-victim mediation) framework.

### Täter-Opfer-Ausgleich (TOA)

**Section 46a StGB -- Offender-Victim Mediation:** This provision, introduced in 1994, allows courts to mitigate the sentence or, for offenses punishable by up to one year's imprisonment, to dispense with punishment entirely where the offender has:
- Made serious efforts to reach mediation with the victim (Täter-Opfer-Ausgleich), with the result that the offense was made good entirely or to a large extent; or
- Made personal reparation requiring significant personal sacrifice or personal effort

**Section 155a StPO:** Requires the prosecution and the court to examine at every stage of the proceedings whether a mediation between the accused and the victim can be achieved, effectively creating a statutory duty to consider restorative justice.

**Section 155b StPO:** Authorizes referral of cases to mediation services and establishes the framework for cooperation between criminal justice authorities and TOA services.

### Juvenile Justice

Section 10(1)(7) JGG (Youth Courts Act) permits youth courts to order juvenile offenders to participate in victim-offender mediation as an educational measure (Erziehungsmaßregel). Section 45(2) JGG allows the prosecutor to refrain from prosecution where the juvenile has participated in mediation. TOA is particularly prevalent in juvenile cases, accounting for approximately 60% of all TOA referrals.

### Institutional Infrastructure

Germany operates approximately 400 TOA services (Ausgleichsstellen), staffed by trained social workers and mediators. The services are funded by the Länder (federal states) and operate independently from the criminal justice authorities, though they cooperate closely with prosecutors and courts. The Servicebüro für Täter-Opfer-Ausgleich, operated by the DBH (Fachverband für Soziale Arbeit, Strafrecht und Kriminalpolitik), provides national coordination, quality standards, and training.

### Statistics and Outcomes

| Metric | Data |
|--------|------|
| Annual TOA cases | Approximately 30,000 |
| Referral source: prosecution | Approximately 70% |
| Referral source: court | Approximately 20% |
| Referral source: police/other | Approximately 10% |
| Agreement rate | Approximately 75-80% |
| Victim participation rate | Approximately 60-65% |

Research by Kerner, Marks, and Schreckling found that TOA participants had significantly lower recidivism rates than comparison groups, with the effect strongest for juvenile offenders and for cases involving direct personal contact between victim and offender.

### Limitations

Despite strong statutory support, TOA in Germany faces challenges:
- Referral rates have plateaued since approximately 2010
- Wide regional variation in TOA availability and use
- Underutilization for serious offenses, despite statutory applicability
- Limited integration with community-based restorative practices beyond direct mediation

## ECHR & EU

### EU Directive 2012/29/EU -- Victims' Rights Directive

Article 12 of the Victims' Rights Directive establishes the framework for restorative justice in EU member states:

**Article 12(1):** Member states shall take measures to safeguard the victim from secondary and repeat victimisation, from intimidation and from retaliation, to be applied when providing any restorative justice services. These measures shall ensure that the victim receives full and unbiased information about the restorative justice process and its potential outcomes, and any information necessary to make a decision about participation.

**Article 12(2):** Restorative justice services shall only be used where they are in the interest of the victim and the offender acknowledges the basic facts of the case. Participation must be voluntary and may be withdrawn at any time.

### Council of Europe Recommendation CM/Rec(2018)8

Adopted on October 3, 2018, this Recommendation provides the most comprehensive European framework for restorative justice in criminal matters:

**Scope:** Restorative justice should be a generally available service and should not be limited to minor offenses or first-time offenders. It should be available at all stages of the criminal justice process.

**Principles:** Participation must be voluntary; processes must be fair, impartial, and confidential; there must be adequate safeguards to protect the rights of both victim and offender; outcomes must be reasonable and proportionate.

**Training:** Facilitators must receive initial and ongoing training in conflict resolution, the specific needs of victims and offenders, and relevant legal and procedural requirements.

**Research:** Member states should support research on restorative justice processes and outcomes and should use evidence-based approaches to develop and improve restorative justice services.

### United Nations Framework

The UN Basic Principles on the Use of Restorative Justice Programmes in Criminal Matters (ECOSOC Resolution 2002/12) established international guidelines, defining restorative justice as "any process in which the victim and the offender, and, where appropriate, any other individuals or community members affected by a crime, participate together actively in the resolution of matters arising from the crime, generally with the help of a facilitator."

### ECHR Considerations

The ECHR has not directly addressed restorative justice, but several Convention provisions are relevant:
- Article 6 (fair trial): Restorative justice processes must not undermine the right to a fair trial
- Article 13 (effective remedy): Restorative justice must not deprive victims of access to effective remedies
- The Court's victim-protection jurisprudence under Articles 2, 3, and 8 supports restorative approaches that empower victims

## Comparative Analysis

### Integration into Criminal Justice

Germany has achieved the deepest integration, with statutory provisions requiring consideration of TOA at every stage of proceedings and sentence mitigation incentives. South Korea's system is formalized through the Criminal Mediation Act but remains prosecutor-driven. The United States has the least integration, with programs operating largely outside the formal criminal justice system.

### Scope of Application

Germany applies TOA in principle to all offenses, though in practice it is most common for property crimes and minor violence. The United States varies widely, with some programs handling violent offenses (including homicide cases through dialogue programs). South Korea restricts criminal mediation primarily to non-violent offenses.

### Institutional Support

Germany provides the strongest institutional infrastructure with 400+ dedicated TOA services. South Korea's system operates through prosecution offices with growing independent mediator networks. The United States lacks consistent institutional support, with programs dependent on local funding and initiative.

### Outcomes

All jurisdictions report positive outcomes from restorative justice programs, including higher victim satisfaction, higher compliance with agreements, and reduced recidivism. The most robust evidence comes from the United States (Sherman-Strang meta-analysis) and Germany (Kerner-Marks-Schreckling studies).
