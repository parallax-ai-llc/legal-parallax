## Summary

Statutes of repose -- absolute time bars that extinguish a cause of action after a fixed period measured from a specific event (such as the delivery of a product, completion of construction, or performance of a professional service), irrespective of when the injury occurs or is discovered -- represent a distinct and more rigid form of temporal limitation on litigation, functioning alongside but fundamentally differently from statutes of limitation, which run from the accrual or discovery of the cause of action. South Korea addresses absolute time bars through the Civil Code (민법), which distinguishes between the general statute of limitations (소멸시효) under Articles 162-184 and specific absolute cutoff periods embedded in particular statutory schemes; Korean law recognizes a 10-year general limitations period for civil claims (Article 162(1)) and a shorter 3-year period for tort claims (Article 766(1)), with an absolute 10-year bar from the date of the tortious act for tort claims (Article 766(2)) that functions as a repose-like provision; the Korean Supreme Court (대법원) has grappled with the tension between these absolute bars and the discovery rule, particularly in cases of latent injury and concealed wrongdoing. The United States has developed the most extensive and explicit statute of repose jurisprudence, with repose periods enacted across numerous substantive areas including construction defects (typically 6-12 years from completion), product liability (varying by state, typically 10-15 years from delivery), medical malpractice (typically 3-10 years from the act), and securities claims (3 years from the offering under 15 U.S.C. § 77m, as interpreted in CTS Corp. v. Dynamics Corp. of America); the Supreme Court in CTS Corp. v. Dynamics Corp. of America, 481 U.S. 69 (1987), and more recently in Gabelli v. SEC, 568 U.S. 442 (2013), and Cal. Pub. Employees' Ret. Sys. v. ANZ Securities, 582 U.S. 497 (2017), has affirmed the absolute character of repose periods, holding that they are not subject to tolling. Germany employs a sophisticated distinction between limitation (Verjährung, BGB §§ 194-218) and preclusion or forfeiture (Ausschlussfrist or Verwirkung), with the regular limitation period of three years under BGB § 195 subject to the discovery rule (BGB § 199), but with an absolute maximum limitation period (Höchstfrist) of 10 years from accrual (BGB § 199(3) No. 1) or 30 years from the injurious act (BGB § 199(3) No. 2) for claims involving injury to life, body, health, or liberty; additional repose-like periods apply in specific contexts such as construction law (BGB § 634a), product liability (Produkthaftungsgesetz § 13), and professional liability. The ECHR has examined absolute time bars under Article 6(1) (right of access to court) and Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (property protection), requiring that repose periods pursue a legitimate aim and be proportionate, while EU law establishes specific repose-type periods in the Product Liability Directive (85/374/EEC, Article 11, providing a 10-year absolute bar from the date the product was put into circulation) and the Environmental Liability Directive (2004/35/CE).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Korean law does not employ the distinct concept of "statute of repose" found in American law. Instead, the Korean system achieves functionally similar results through absolute limitation periods embedded within the general limitations framework of the Civil Code and specific statutory regimes.

### General Limitation Periods

**Article 162(1) (General Civil Claims):** Civil claims are extinguished by prescription after 10 years. This period runs from the time the claim becomes exercisable (권리를 행사할 수 있는 때), which the Supreme Court has generally interpreted as the date of accrual rather than discovery.

**Article 162(2) (Short-Period Claims):** Certain commercial and professional claims are subject to shorter limitation periods of 3 years or 5 years.

### Tort Limitation: Dual Period Structure

**Article 766 (Tort Claims):** This provision establishes a dual limitation structure that most closely approximates the limitation/repose distinction:

**Article 766(1) (Discovery Period):** Tort claims are extinguished by prescription after 3 years from the date the victim became aware of the damage and the identity of the tortfeasor. This is a subjective, discovery-based period analogous to a statute of limitations.

**Article 766(2) (Absolute Period):** Tort claims are extinguished by prescription after 10 years from the date of the tortious act, regardless of when the victim learned of the damage or the tortfeasor's identity. This is an objective, event-based period that functions as a repose provision.

### Supreme Court Jurisprudence on Absolute Periods

The tension between the absolute 10-year period under Article 766(2) and the need to protect victims of latent injuries has generated significant Supreme Court litigation.

**Decision 2009Da72599 (2013):** In a case involving latent asbestos-related disease, the Supreme Court addressed whether the 10-year absolute period under Article 766(2) could be extended or tolled when the injury manifested long after the tortious exposure. The Court held that the absolute period begins to run from the date of the last exposure to the harmful substance, but acknowledged the potential for injustice when diseases have long latency periods.

**Decision 2012Da202819 (2018):** The Supreme Court held that invoking the absolute limitation period under Article 766(2) may, in exceptional circumstances, constitute an abuse of rights (권리남용) under Article 2 of the Civil Code, particularly where the tortfeasor actively concealed the wrongdoing or where applying the absolute bar would be contrary to the principles of good faith (신의성실의 원칙). This judicially created exception provides a narrow safety valve against the harshest consequences of absolute time bars.

### Construction Defects

Korean construction defect claims are governed by Article 668 of the Civil Code (warranty for defects in work contracts) and the Housing Act (주택법). Article 671 provides that the warranty period for structural defects in buildings is 10 years from delivery. The Framework Act on the Construction Industry (건설산업기본법) establishes additional warranty periods depending on the type of defect and the building component.

### Product Liability

The Product Liability Act (제조물책임법, enacted 2002) provides that claims for damage caused by defective products are subject to the general tort limitation periods under Article 766 of the Civil Code. Additionally, Article 7(2) of the Product Liability Act provides an absolute 10-year bar from the date the manufacturer supplied the product, unless the damage was caused by a substance with a long latency period, in which case the period begins when the damage occurs.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has developed the most explicit and extensive body of statute of repose law, with the concept firmly established as distinct from statutes of limitation. The conceptual distinction was crystallized in First United Methodist Church v. U.S. Gypsum Co., 882 F.2d 862 (4th Cir. 1989), which explained: "A statute of limitations bars the right to prosecute an accrued cause of action; a statute of repose prevents what might otherwise be a cause of action from ever arising."

### Constitutional Foundation

**CTS Corp. v. Dynamics Corp. of America, 481 U.S. 69 (1987):** While primarily a Commerce Clause case, the decision addressed the absolute nature of repose periods.

**Cal. Public Employees' Retirement System v. ANZ Securities, Inc., 582 U.S. 497 (2017):** The Supreme Court held that the 3-year repose period under Securities Act § 13 (15 U.S.C. § 77m) is not subject to equitable tolling, including American Pipe class action tolling. Justice Kennedy, writing for the majority, emphasized that statutes of repose "effect a legislative judgment that a defendant should be free from liability after the legislatively determined period of time," and that this legislative policy would be undermined by equitable tolling.

**Gabelli v. SEC, 568 U.S. 442 (2013):** The Court held that the discovery rule does not apply to the 5-year limitation period for SEC enforcement actions, treating the period as repose-like in character.

### Construction Defect Repose

Nearly all states have enacted statutes of repose for construction defects, typically providing an absolute cutoff of 6-12 years from substantial completion or occupancy:

| State | Repose Period | Measured From |
|-------|--------------|---------------|
| California | 10 years (patent defects); 4 years (latent) | Substantial completion (Cal. Civ. Proc. Code § 337.15) |
| New York | 10 years | Completion of improvement (N.Y. CPLR § 214-d) |
| Texas | 10 years | Substantial completion (Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code § 16.009) |
| Florida | 10 years (15 for latent defects) | Actual possession, issuance of CO, or abandonment (Fla. Stat. § 95.11(3)(c)) |
| Illinois | 10 years | Act or omission (735 ILCS 5/13-214(a)) |
| Georgia | 8 years | Substantial completion (O.C.G.A. § 9-3-51) |

### Product Liability Repose

Many states have enacted product liability statutes of repose, typically providing 10-15 years from the date of sale or delivery:

**Connecticut:** 10-year repose from date of sale (Conn. Gen. Stat. § 52-577a).

**Oregon:** 10-year repose from date product was first purchased for use or consumption (Or. Rev. Stat. § 30.905).

**Indiana:** 10-year repose from date of delivery (Ind. Code § 34-20-3-1); extended to 12 years for certain claims.

**North Carolina:** 12-year repose from date of initial purchase for use or consumption (N.C. Gen. Stat. § 1-46.1).

### Medical Malpractice Repose

State medical malpractice statutes of repose typically provide absolute cutoffs of 3-10 years from the act of malpractice:

**Tennessee:** 3-year repose from date of the negligent act (Tenn. Code § 29-26-116), with a 1-year discovery period.

**Ohio:** 4-year repose from the act or omission (Ohio Rev. Code § 2305.113).

**North Carolina:** 4-year repose from the last act giving rise to the claim (N.C. Gen. Stat. § 1-15(c)).

### Constitutional Challenges

Statutes of repose have survived most constitutional challenges, though some states have struck down specific repose provisions under state constitutions. In Hanover Shoe, Inc. v. United Shoe Machinery Corp., 392 U.S. 481 (1968), the Supreme Court acknowledged the legislative prerogative to set time limits on causes of action. State courts in Florida, New Hampshire, and Wyoming have struck down certain construction defect repose statutes as violating open-courts provisions or due process guarantees, though such holdings remain a minority position.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German law employs a systematic approach to temporal limitations on claims, distinguishing between Verjährung (limitation/prescription), Ausschlussfrist (preclusive or forfeiture period), and Verwirkung (equitable forfeiture based on delay and reliance). The 2002 reform of limitation law (Schuldrechtsmodernisierungsgesetz) fundamentally restructured these temporal bars.

### Regular Limitation Period (Regelverjährung)

**BGB § 195:** The regular limitation period is 3 years.

**BGB § 199(1):** The regular period begins at the end of the year in which the claim arose and the creditor obtained knowledge (or should have obtained knowledge absent gross negligence) of the circumstances giving rise to the claim and the identity of the debtor. This is a subjective, discovery-based commencement rule.

### Maximum Limitation Periods (Höchstfristen)

**BGB § 199(2):** Claims based on intentional acts are subject to an absolute maximum period of 30 years from the commission of the act, the breach of duty, or the injurious event.

**BGB § 199(3):** Other claims (not based on intentional acts) are subject to maximum periods of:

- **10 years from accrual** (BGB § 199(3) No. 1), regardless of knowledge; or
- **30 years from the act, breach, or event** (BGB § 199(3) No. 2), regardless of knowledge;

whichever expires first. The 30-year maximum applies to claims for injury to life, body, health, or liberty.

**BGB § 199(4):** Claims other than those covered by paragraphs (2) and (3) are subject to an absolute maximum of 10 years from accrual.

These Höchstfristen function as repose periods: they run from objective events, are not dependent on the claimant's knowledge, and cannot be extended by discovery. However, unlike American statutes of repose, they are subject to limited suspension (Hemmung) in certain circumstances under BGB §§ 203-213.

### Construction Defect Limitation

**BGB § 634a (Limitation of Claims for Defects in Work Contracts):**

**§ 634a(1) No. 2:** Claims for defects in buildings and building works are subject to a 5-year limitation period from acceptance (Abnahme) of the work.

**§ 634a(1) No. 3:** Other defect claims are subject to the regular 2-year limitation period from acceptance.

**§ 634a(3):** If the contractor has fraudulently concealed a defect, the regular 3-year discovery-based limitation period applies instead, subject to the absolute maximum periods under § 199.

The German construction defect regime is supplemented by the VOB/B (Vergabe- und Vertragsordnung für Bauleistungen), which is incorporated by reference into most construction contracts and provides a 4-year warranty period for construction defects.

### Product Liability

**Produkthaftungsgesetz (ProdHaftG) § 13:** The rights of the injured party under the Product Liability Act are extinguished 10 years from the date on which the producer put the product that caused the damage into circulation, unless the injured party has commenced legal proceedings before that date. This provision implements Article 11 of the EU Product Liability Directive (85/374/EEC) and functions as an absolute repose period.

**ProdHaftG § 12:** Claims under the Product Liability Act are subject to a 3-year limitation period from the date the claimant became aware (or should have become aware) of the damage, the defect, and the identity of the producer. This discovery-based period operates within the 10-year absolute bar of § 13.

### Ausschlussfrist vs. Verjährung

The distinction between Ausschlussfrist (preclusive period) and Verjährung (limitation) is doctrinally significant in German law:

**Verjährung** is a defense (Einrede) that must be raised by the debtor; the court does not apply it ex officio. Expired claims remain as natural obligations (Naturalobligationen) -- payments made on prescribed claims cannot be reclaimed. Verjährung may be suspended (gehemmt) or restarted (neu begonnen).

**Ausschlussfrist** extinguishes the right itself (not merely the ability to enforce it), operates automatically (the court must apply it ex officio), and is generally not subject to suspension or tolling. Examples include the 2-week period for filing an Einspruch against a Versäumnisurteil (ZPO § 339) and the 3-week period for unfair dismissal claims under the Kündigungsschutzgesetz (KSchG § 4).

### Bundesverfassungsgericht (Federal Constitutional Court)

The BVerfG has examined absolute limitation periods under the proportionality principle derived from the Grundgesetz (Basic Law). In BVerfG Decision 1 BvR 1421/02 (2006), the Court held that limitation periods must strike a proportionate balance between legal certainty (Rechtssicherheit) and the right of access to justice (Justizgewährungsanspruch). Absolute periods that extinguish claims before the injured party could reasonably have discovered them may raise constitutional concerns, though the Court has generally upheld the Höchstfristen as proportionate.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Proportionality Analysis

The European Court of Human Rights has examined absolute time bars under both Article 6(1) (right of access to court) and Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (protection of property, to the extent that a cause of action constitutes a possession).

**Stubbings v. United Kingdom (Application No. 22083/93, 1996):** The Court examined a case where victims of childhood sexual abuse were barred from bringing civil claims by an absolute 6-year limitation period running from the age of majority. The Court held that limitation periods serve the legitimate aims of legal certainty and the protection of defendants, and found that the UK's absolute bar was not disproportionate. However, this decision has been widely criticized and its reasoning has been distinguished in subsequent case law.

**Howald Moor and Others v. Switzerland (Application No. 52067/10, 2014):** In a landmark decision involving asbestos-related disease, the Court held that the application of an absolute limitation period (10 years from exposure) that expired before the claimant could possibly have known of the disease violated Article 6(1). The Court emphasized that where it is scientifically established that a disease could not be diagnosed before the limitation period expired, the application of the absolute bar undermined the very essence of the right of access to court.

**Stagno v. Belgium (Application No. 1062/07, 2009):** The Court found a violation of Article 6(1) where a limitation period expired while the applicants were minors and lacked legal capacity to bring proceedings, holding that the state had failed to ensure effective access to court.

### EU Product Liability Directive (85/374/EEC)

**Article 11:** "Member States shall provide in their legislation that the rights conferred upon the injured person pursuant to this Directive shall be extinguished upon the expiry of a period of 10 years from the date on which the producer put into circulation the actual product which caused the damage, unless the injured person has in the meantime instituted proceedings against the producer."

This provision establishes a harmonized 10-year repose period across EU member states. The CJEU in O'Byrne v. Sanofi Pasteur MSD Ltd (C-127/04, 2006) clarified that the 10-year period begins when the specific product that caused the damage left the production process and entered the marketing process.

**Article 10:** A 3-year limitation period from the date on which the plaintiff became aware (or should reasonably have become aware) of the damage, defect, and identity of the producer operates within the 10-year repose.

### EU Product Liability Directive Revision (2024)

The revised Product Liability Directive (2024/2853) maintains the 10-year repose period but introduces a significant exception: the period is extended to 25 years for products where scientific or technical evidence shows that the damage typically manifests after the standard 10-year period (recital 64 and Article 14(2)). This addresses the Howald Moor problem of latent-onset diseases while preserving the general repose principle.

### Environmental Liability Directive (2004/35/CE)

**Article 17:** The Environmental Liability Directive does not apply to damage caused by emissions, events, or incidents that took place more than 30 years ago. This functions as a repose period for environmental claims, reflecting the particular difficulty of establishing causation and responsibility for historical environmental contamination.

## Comparative Analysis

### Conceptual Clarity vs. Functional Equivalence

The United States provides the clearest conceptual distinction between statutes of limitation and statutes of repose, treating them as fundamentally different legal instruments. Germany achieves functional equivalence through its Höchstfristen (maximum periods) within the Verjährung framework, supplemented by Ausschlussfristen in specific contexts. South Korea embeds absolute bars within its general limitation structure (particularly Article 766(2)). The ECHR and EU apply functional analysis, focusing on the effect of absolute time bars rather than their doctrinal classification.

### Tolling and Equitable Relief

The most significant practical difference concerns the availability of tolling and equitable relief. American statutes of repose are, following Cal. Public Employees' Retirement System v. ANZ Securities, generally impervious to equitable tolling. German Höchstfristen are subject to limited Hemmung (suspension) under specific statutory provisions. Korean law permits the abuse of rights defense to mitigate absolute bars in exceptional cases. The ECHR has required that absolute bars not deprive claimants of access to court when it was impossible to bring claims within the period.

### Construction Defects: Duration and Starting Point

Construction defect repose periods vary substantially: U.S. state statutes typically provide 6-12 years from completion; German law provides 5 years from acceptance; Korean law provides 10 years for structural defects. The starting point also differs: U.S. statutes use substantial completion or occupancy; Germany uses acceptance (Abnahme); Korea uses delivery. These differences significantly affect the duration of potential liability for builders and contractors.

### Product Liability: EU Harmonization vs. U.S. Fragmentation

Product liability repose is the most internationally harmonized area, with the EU Product Liability Directive establishing a uniform 10-year period. The U.S., by contrast, has no federal product liability repose statute, leaving the issue to states with periods ranging from 6 to 15 years. The 2024 revision of the EU Directive, extending the period to 25 years for latent-onset injuries, represents a sophisticated response to the ECHR's Howald Moor jurisprudence.

### Latent Injury: The Central Challenge

The most difficult policy challenge for all repose regimes is the problem of latent injury -- harm that manifests only years or decades after the causative event. Asbestos-related disease (with latency periods of 20-50 years), contaminated blood products, and environmental exposure cases have tested the limits of repose periods worldwide. The responses range from strict application (U.S. repose periods generally cannot be tolled) to judicial mitigation (Korean abuse of rights doctrine; ECHR Howald Moor), to legislative exception (EU revised Product Liability Directive's 25-year extension for latent injuries).
