## Summary

School voucher systems -- publicly funded mechanisms that allow families to direct education funding toward private or alternative schools of their choice -- represent one of the most contentious intersections of education policy, constitutional law, and religious freedom across modern legal systems. South Korea maintains a highly centralized education system without a general school voucher program, though the government provides targeted educational subsidies through the Education Voucher Program (교육 바우처) for disadvantaged students and the Free Semester System (자유학기제), while private schools (사립학교) -- which constitute approximately 40 percent of high schools -- operate under heavy government regulation through the Act on Establishment and Operation of Private Schools (사립학교법) and receive substantial public subsidies in exchange for adhering to government-mandated curricula, tuition limits, and admissions policies. The United States has developed the most extensive school voucher landscape, with the Supreme Court's landmark decision in Zelman v. Simmons-Harris (2002) upholding the constitutionality of publicly funded vouchers used at religious schools under the Establishment Clause, followed by Espinoza v. Montana Department of Revenue (2020) and Carson v. Makin (2022) holding that states cannot exclude religious schools from generally available public benefit programs under the Free Exercise Clause; as of 2026, over 30 states operate some form of school choice program including vouchers, tax credit scholarships, and education savings accounts. Germany operates a distinctive constitutional framework under Article 7(4) of the Grundgesetz, which guarantees the right to establish private schools (Privatschulfreiheit) while requiring them to meet public school standards and prohibiting the segregation of pupils according to the financial means of parents (Sonderungsverbot); most Länder provide substantial public funding to approved private schools (Ersatzschulen) typically covering 60-90 percent of operating costs, creating a de facto voucher-like system without formal voucher mechanisms. At the European level, the ECHR Protocol 1, Article 2 protects both the right to education and parents' rights to ensure education conforming to their convictions, the European Court of Human Rights has upheld states' wide margin of appreciation in organizing educational systems, and the EU has limited competence in education under Article 165 TFEU, leaving school funding mechanisms to member state discretion.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's education system is highly centralized with strong government control over both public and private schools. The constitutional foundation is Article 31 of the Constitution (헌법), which guarantees the right to education and establishes free compulsory elementary education.

**Private Schools Act (사립학교법):** Private schools in South Korea receive significant public subsidies but are subject to extensive government regulation. The Act requires private schools to follow the national curriculum, comply with government-set tuition limits, and adhere to admissions procedures determined by local education offices. In exchange, the government provides subsidies covering approximately 60-70 percent of operating costs for approved private high schools.

### Targeted Education Subsidies

While South Korea does not operate a general voucher system, it provides targeted educational assistance:

| Program | Target | Benefit |
|---------|--------|---------|
| Education Voucher (교육 바우처) | Low-income students | Subsidies for tutoring, educational materials, extracurricular activities |
| Free High School Education | All students (since 2021) | Tuition waived at all public and private high schools |
| School Meal Program (무상급식) | All elementary and middle school students | Free school lunches |
| Nuri Curriculum Subsidies | All children ages 3-5 | Subsidies for kindergarten/preschool regardless of public/private |

### 2025 High School Equalization Policy Reform

The high school equalization policy (고교평준화정책), implemented since 1974, assigns students to high schools by lottery within their district, eliminating selective admissions. This policy effectively functioned as an anti-voucher mechanism by removing parental school choice at the high school level. The policy has been modified over time to allow specialized high schools (특수목적고등학교) and autonomous private high schools (자율형 사립고등학교), though the Moon Jae-in administration moved to phase out many autonomous private high schools in 2019, reclassifying them as regular high schools subject to the equalization lottery.

### Hagwon (학원) as De Facto Choice

South Korea's massive private tutoring industry -- the hagwon (학원) system -- functions as a de facto school choice mechanism outside the formal education system. Spending on private tutoring was estimated at approximately 26 trillion KRW ($20 billion) in 2023, with over 75 percent of students participating. While not a voucher system, the hagwon phenomenon reflects the intense demand for educational choice that the formal system does not fully accommodate.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has developed the most extensive and contested school voucher framework in the world, shaped by a series of Supreme Court decisions interpreting the Religion Clauses of the First Amendment.

### Key Supreme Court Decisions

**Zelman v. Simmons-Harris, 536 U.S. 639 (2002):** The Court upheld Cleveland's school voucher program, which permitted parents to use publicly funded vouchers at private schools including religious schools. The 5-4 decision held that the program did not violate the Establishment Clause because it was neutral with respect to religion, provided aid directly to parents rather than schools, and offered a genuine choice among religious and secular options.

**Espinoza v. Montana Department of Revenue, 591 U.S. ___ (2020):** The Court held that Montana's Blaine Amendment (state constitutional provision barring aid to religious schools) violated the Free Exercise Clause by excluding religious schools from a tax credit scholarship program available to other private schools. The decision established that states cannot discriminate against religious schools in distributing public benefits.

**Carson v. Makin, 596 U.S. 767 (2022):** The Court struck down Maine's exclusion of "sectarian" schools from its town tuition program, holding that the Free Exercise Clause prohibits excluding schools that provide religious instruction, not just schools with a religious identity. This decision eliminated the "status vs. use" distinction that had allowed some states to fund religious schools while barring the use of funds for religious instruction.

### Program Types

| Type | Mechanism | Examples |
|------|-----------|---------|
| Voucher | Government payment to private school chosen by parent | Milwaukee (1990); Cleveland (1996); Indiana |
| Tax Credit Scholarship | Tax credit for donations to scholarship-granting organizations | Florida (Step Up); Arizona; Georgia |
| Education Savings Account (ESA) | Government deposits funds in parent-controlled account for education expenses | Arizona (universal, 2022); Florida; West Virginia |
| Charter Schools | Publicly funded, independently operated schools | All 50 states except Montana permit charters |

### Universal ESA Movement

The most significant recent development is the expansion of universal education savings accounts. Arizona's Empowerment Scholarship Account program, expanded to all students in 2022, provides approximately $7,000 per student in a parent-controlled account usable for private school tuition, tutoring, curriculum materials, and educational therapy. By 2026, over 15 states have enacted or expanded ESA programs, many with universal eligibility regardless of family income.

### Opposition and Criticism

Critics argue that voucher programs divert funding from public schools, lack adequate accountability for academic outcomes, facilitate discrimination (particularly against LGBTQ+ students and students with disabilities), and that public funding of religious education violates the separation of church and state. Empirical evidence on academic outcomes is mixed, with studies in Louisiana and Indiana showing negative initial effects on voucher students' test scores, while Milwaukee and Washington D.C. programs showed modest positive effects, particularly for minority students.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's approach to public funding of private schools is rooted in a unique constitutional framework that creates a voucher-like system without formal voucher mechanisms.

**Article 7(4) Grundgesetz -- Privatschulfreiheit:**

"The right to establish private schools shall be guaranteed. Private schools that serve as alternatives to state schools (Ersatzschulen) shall require the approval of the State and shall be subject to the laws of the Länder. Such approval shall be given where private schools are not inferior to the state schools in terms of their educational aims, their facilities, or the professional training of their teaching staff, and where segregation of pupils according to the financial means of parents is not furthered thereby (Sonderungsverbot). Approval shall be withheld where the economic and legal position of the teaching staff is not sufficiently assured."

### Categories of Private Schools

| Category | Description | Public Funding |
|----------|-------------|---------------|
| Ersatzschulen (alternative schools) | Replace public schools; must meet equivalent standards | 60-90% of per-pupil operating costs, varying by Land |
| Ergänzungsschulen (supplementary schools) | Offer programs not available in public system | Generally no public funding |
| International schools | Serve international community | Limited or no public funding |

### The Sonderungsverbot (Segregation Prohibition)

The Sonderungsverbot of Article 7(4) sentence 3 GG is the most distinctive feature of German private school funding law. It prohibits private schools from selecting students based on their parents' financial means, requiring that tuition be kept sufficiently low to ensure accessibility. The Federal Constitutional Court addressed this in BVerfGE 75, 40 (1987), holding that the state has a positive obligation to fund private schools sufficiently to enable compliance with the Sonderungsverbot.

In practice, private Ersatzschulen charge modest tuition fees (typically EUR 100-500 per month) and receive substantial public subsidies. The subsidies vary by Land:

| Land | Approximate Subsidy (% of public school per-pupil cost) |
|------|-------------------------------------------------------|
| Hamburg | ~90% |
| North Rhine-Westphalia | ~87% |
| Bavaria | ~65-75% |
| Berlin | ~93% |
| Saxony | ~80% |

### Waldorf and Montessori Schools

Germany has a particularly rich landscape of alternative pedagogical schools, including approximately 250 Waldorf (Rudolf Steiner) schools and numerous Montessori schools. These schools operate as Ersatzschulen with state approval and public funding. The Waldorf school movement originated in Germany (Stuttgart, 1919) and the country remains the global center of Waldorf education.

### Religious Schools

Approximately 2,000 Catholic and Protestant schools operate in Germany as Ersatzschulen, receiving public subsidies on the same terms as secular private schools. The relationship between church and state in education is governed by concordats (with the Catholic Church) and state church agreements (with Protestant churches), as well as Article 7(3) GG, which makes religious instruction a regular subject in public schools.

### Federal Constitutional Court: Private School Funding Obligation

The BVerfG has held that the state's duty to fund private schools arises from the intersection of the Privatschulfreiheit and the Sonderungsverbot: if the state requires private schools to keep tuition low (to avoid socioeconomic segregation) but the schools cannot operate without adequate resources (to meet the quality standards required for approval), then the state must provide sufficient funding to bridge the gap. This creates a constitutional right to public funding for approved private schools -- a far stronger foundation than exists in any other jurisdiction.

## ECHR & EU

### Protocol 1, Article 2 -- Right to Education

"No person shall be denied the right to education. In the exercise of any functions which it assumes in relation to education and to teaching, the State shall respect the right of parents to ensure such education and teaching in conformity with their own religious and philosophical convictions."

### Key ECHR Jurisprudence

**Kjeldsen, Busk Madsen and Pedersen v. Denmark (1976):** The Court held that the second sentence of Protocol 1, Article 2 does not require the state to fund private schools that accord with parental convictions but does require the state to respect parental convictions within public education.

**Folgerø and Others v. Norway (Grand Chamber, 2007):** The Court found a violation where Norway's religious instruction curriculum did not provide adequate exemption mechanisms for non-Christian families, emphasizing that the right to education includes protection against indoctrination.

**Osmanoğlu and Kocabaş v. Switzerland (2017):** The Court upheld mandatory mixed swimming classes for Muslim students, holding that the state's interest in integration justified the interference with parental convictions. The decision illustrated the tension between parental choice and state integration objectives.

### EU Competence

Article 165(1) TFEU provides: "The Union shall contribute to the development of quality education by encouraging cooperation between Member States and, if necessary, by supporting and supplementing their action, while fully respecting the responsibility of the Member States for the content of teaching and the organisation of education systems and their cultural and linguistic diversity."

This provision expressly limits EU competence in education, leaving school funding mechanisms -- including vouchers and private school subsidies -- entirely to member state discretion. The EU's role is limited to supporting cooperation, mobility (Erasmus+), and quality assurance.

### European Models

European countries exhibit wide variation in school choice mechanisms:
- **Netherlands:** The most choice-oriented European system. Article 23 of the Dutch Constitution guarantees freedom of education and requires equal public funding for all schools, including religious schools, that meet quality standards. Approximately 70% of students attend publicly funded private schools.
- **Sweden:** Introduced a universal voucher system in 1992, allowing for-profit and non-profit schools to compete for students with public funding following each student. The system has been controversial, with concerns about quality and segregation leading to regulatory tightening.
- **France:** Maintains a dual public-private system where private schools under contract (écoles sous contrat) receive public funding for teacher salaries in exchange for following the national curriculum.

## Comparative Analysis

### Constitutional Frameworks

The constitutional treatment of private school funding differs fundamentally across jurisdictions. Germany provides the strongest constitutional foundation, with Article 7(4) GG creating both a right to establish private schools and a state obligation to fund them sufficiently to prevent socioeconomic segregation. The United States has moved from permitting religious school funding (Zelman) to requiring it when secular private schools are funded (Carson), creating a strong but negative obligation. South Korea's Constitution guarantees education rights but does not mandate school choice mechanisms. The ECHR establishes a right to education but leaves funding mechanisms to state discretion.

### Choice vs. Equity

The fundamental tension in school voucher policy is between parental choice and educational equity. The United States' expansion of universal ESAs maximizes choice but raises concerns about public school disinvestment and accountability. Germany's Sonderungsverbot uniquely attempts to reconcile choice with equity by constitutionally prohibiting socioeconomic segregation in private schools. South Korea's equalization policy sacrifices choice for equity, though the hagwon system reveals the limits of suppressing demand for choice.

### Religious Schools

The treatment of religious schools has been the most constitutionally contentious issue. The United States' trajectory from permitting to requiring equal treatment of religious schools represents a dramatic shift. Germany funds religious schools routinely as part of its Ersatzschulen framework, without the constitutional controversy that characterizes American debates. South Korea provides subsidies to religious private schools subject to general regulatory requirements.

### Accountability

A critical distinction concerns accountability mechanisms for publicly funded private schools. Germany requires Ersatzschulen to meet equivalent standards to public schools as a condition of approval and funding. The United States generally imposes fewer academic accountability requirements on voucher and ESA schools than on public schools, creating a "regulation gap." South Korea's heavy regulation of private schools ensures curricular alignment but limits educational diversity.
