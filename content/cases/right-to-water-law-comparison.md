## Summary

The right to water -- recognized as an essential human right under international law through UNGA Resolution 64/292 (2010), ICESCR General Comment No. 15 (2002), and the subsequent UNGA Resolution 68/157 (2013), encompassing the right to sufficient, safe, acceptable, physically accessible, and affordable water for personal and domestic uses -- represents one of the most consequential intersections of human rights law, environmental law, public utility regulation, and development policy, as the legal recognition of water as a human right rather than merely an economic commodity has profound implications for water privatization, tariff setting, infrastructure investment, service disconnection, and the obligations of states to ensure universal access even in contexts of fiscal constraint, climate change, and growing demand. South Korea does not explicitly enshrine a right to water in its Constitution, but the Waterworks Act (수도법) Article 1 declares the purpose of ensuring clean and safe water supply for all citizens, and the Framework Act on Water Management (물관리기본법, 2018) Article 8 establishes the basic right of all citizens to clean water (물에 대한 국민의 기본적 권리); the National Basic Livelihood Security Act (국민기초생활 보장법) provides assistance for water utility costs for low-income households, and the Supreme Court has addressed disconnection protections in the context of essential utility services. The United States has no federal constitutional right to water, though the Safe Drinking Water Act (42 U.S.C. Section 300f et seq.) establishes national primary drinking water standards; the crisis in Flint, Michigan, catalyzed nationwide attention to water access and quality disparities, resulting in the America's Water Infrastructure Act of 2018 (AWIA) and the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law of 2021 allocating $55 billion for water infrastructure; litigation in cases such as Concerned Pastors for Social Action v. Khouri (E.D. Mich. 2016) and the Flint Water Cases have advanced but not established a constitutional right to water. Germany derives water access obligations from the Existenzminimum principle (Article 1(1) in conjunction with Article 20(1) GG), with the BVerfG recognizing clean water as part of the dignified subsistence minimum; the Wasserhaushaltsgesetz (WHG, Federal Water Resources Act) and state-level water supply ordinances (Wasserversorgungssatzungen) establish the framework for public water supply, and the principle of Anschluss- und Benutzungszwang (mandatory connection and use) ensures universal access to municipal water systems. At the international and European level, UNGA Resolution 64/292 (July 28, 2010) recognized the right to safe and clean drinking water and sanitation as a human right essential for the full enjoyment of life and all human rights; the EU Drinking Water Directive (Directive (EU) 2020/2184, recast) explicitly references the human right to water and requires member states to improve access for vulnerable and marginalized groups; and the ECHR has addressed water access under Articles 2, 3, and 8, with the Court finding violations where state actions or failures to act deprived individuals of access to safe water.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's approach to the right to water combines environmental law, public utility regulation, and social welfare protections, with a 2018 statute establishing the most explicit statutory right to water among the jurisdictions studied.

**Constitutional Foundation:** The Constitution of the Republic of Korea does not enumerate a specific right to water. However, Article 34(1) (right to a dignified human existence), Article 35(1) (right to a healthy and pleasant environment -- "All citizens shall have the right to a healthy and pleasant environment. The State and all citizens shall endeavor to protect the environment"), and Article 34(2) (state duty to promote social welfare) provide the constitutional basis for water access obligations. The Environmental Policy Basic Act (환경정책기본법) Article 6 further elaborates the citizen's right to a clean environment, which encompasses access to clean water.

**Framework Act on Water Management (물관리기본법, 2018):** This Act, enacted in 2018 to unify and rationalize Korea's fragmented water governance system, contains the most explicit statutory recognition of a right to water in South Korean law.

- **Article 8 (Basic Right to Water, 물에 대한 국민의 기본적 권리):** "All citizens shall have the basic right to clean and safe water. The State and local governments shall take necessary measures to ensure this right." This provision, while declaratory in character, establishes a normative foundation for water policy and may influence judicial interpretation of related obligations.
- **Article 11 (Water Welfare, 물복지):** "The State and local governments shall endeavor to provide water welfare services so that all citizens may enjoy basic water services regardless of their area of residence or economic status."
- **Article 12 (Water Management Principles):** Establishes principles including public interest priority, sustainable use, integrated water management, and equitable allocation.

**Waterworks Act (수도법):** The primary statute governing public water supply systems. Key provisions:

- **Article 1 (Purpose):** "This Act aims to establish matters concerning the installation and management of waterworks to supply clean and safe water to all citizens, thereby contributing to improving public health."
- **Article 7 (Duty to Supply):** Local governments (지방자치단체) have the duty to install and manage waterworks to supply water to residents within their jurisdiction.
- **Article 33 (Water Quality Standards):** Drinking water quality standards are set by Presidential Decree, implementing the Ministry of Environment's detailed standards covering microbiological, chemical, physical, and radiological parameters.
- **Article 34 (Water Quality Testing):** Water suppliers must conduct regular water quality testing and report results to the relevant authority and the public.

### Affordability and Social Protection

**National Basic Livelihood Security Act (국민기초생활 보장법):** Provides housing benefits (주거급여) to eligible low-income households, which include an allowance for water and sewage utility costs. The Act ensures that the poorest households are not deprived of water access due to inability to pay.

**Local Government Fee Reduction Programs:** Many municipal water providers offer reduced tariff rates or fee exemptions for elderly residents, persons with disabilities, recipients of basic livelihood security benefits, and multi-child families. These programs are established by local government ordinance (조례) and vary among municipalities.

### Disconnection Protections

South Korean law does not provide comprehensive statutory protections against water service disconnection for non-payment. However, the courts have recognized that water, as an essential service, cannot be disconnected without due process. Municipal waterworks ordinances (수도사업조례) typically require advance notice before disconnection and provide for payment plans. The Korean National Human Rights Commission (국가인권위원회) has recommended strengthening disconnection protections, particularly for vulnerable populations.

### Water Privatization

South Korea's water supply is overwhelmingly public, operated by local governments and the Korea Water Resources Corporation (한국수자원공사, K-water). Proposals for water privatization have faced strong public opposition. The Waterworks Act Article 23 permits outsourcing of water supply operations to private entities (민간위탁) but retains public ownership of infrastructure and regulatory oversight.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has no recognized constitutional right to water at either the federal or state level, though the Flint water crisis and related litigation have intensified advocacy for such recognition.

**Safe Drinking Water Act (SDWA, 42 U.S.C. Section 300f et seq.):** The primary federal statute governing drinking water quality, enacted in 1974 and amended in 1986 and 1996. Key provisions:

- **Section 300g-1 (National Primary Drinking Water Regulations, NPDWR):** The EPA establishes maximum contaminant levels (MCLs) and treatment technique requirements for public water systems. As of 2025, the EPA has established NPDWRs for over 90 contaminants.
- **Section 300g-3 (Enforcement):** The EPA may take enforcement action against public water systems that violate NPDWRs, including issuing compliance orders and pursuing civil penalties.
- **Section 300j-19a (Assistance for Small and Disadvantaged Communities):** Provides grants for water infrastructure in small and disadvantaged communities that cannot otherwise afford compliance with NPDWRs.

**America's Water Infrastructure Act of 2018 (AWIA, P.L. 115-270):** Enacted in response to the Flint crisis, AWIA strengthened requirements for community water systems regarding risk assessments and emergency response plans. Section 2013 required community water systems serving more than 3,300 persons to conduct risk and resilience assessments.

**Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (Bipartisan Infrastructure Law, P.L. 117-58, 2021):** Allocated approximately $55 billion for water infrastructure, including $15 billion for lead service line replacement, $10 billion for PFAS remediation, and $26 billion for the Drinking Water State Revolving Fund (DWSRF) and Clean Water State Revolving Fund (CWSRF). This represents the largest federal investment in water infrastructure in American history.

### The Flint Water Crisis

The Flint, Michigan, water crisis (2014-2019), in which the city's water source was switched to the Flint River without proper corrosion control treatment, resulting in lead contamination affecting over 100,000 residents, became a defining moment for water rights discourse in the United States.

**Concerned Pastors for Social Action v. Khouri, No. 16-10277 (E.D. Mich. 2016):** Plaintiffs alleged that Flint's contaminated water violated their due process and equal protection rights under the Fourteenth Amendment. While the court permitted some claims to proceed, it did not establish a freestanding constitutional right to clean water. The Sixth Circuit's subsequent rulings in related cases recognized qualified immunity for some government officials while allowing some claims to proceed.

**Flint Water Cases (In re Flint Water Cases, No. 5:16-cv-10444, E.D. Mich.):** A consolidated class action resulting in a $626 million settlement (approved 2021), with 80% allocated to children affected by lead exposure. The settlement, while the largest in Michigan's history, was based on state tort claims rather than constitutional rights.

### Affordability and Disconnection

**Low Income Household Water Assistance Program (LIHWAP):** Established under the Consolidated Appropriations Act of 2021, LIHWAP provides financial assistance to low-income households for water and wastewater utility bills. The program received $1.1 billion in federal funding and was administered through the same state agencies that manage LIHEAP (energy assistance).

**State Disconnection Protections:** Protections against water service disconnection vary dramatically by state and municipality. Some states (e.g., New Jersey) prohibit disconnection during winter months or for vulnerable populations. Others have minimal protections. The COVID-19 pandemic prompted temporary disconnection moratoria in many jurisdictions.

**UNGA Resolution 64/292:** The United States abstained from the 2010 vote recognizing the human right to water, expressing concerns about the resolution's implications for existing international obligations and the appropriate means of achieving universal access.

### Water Privatization

The United States has a mix of public and private water utilities, with approximately 12% of the population served by private systems (National Association of Water Companies). Private water utility rates are regulated by state public utility commissions. Privatization has been controversial, with critics citing rate increases and service quality concerns, and supporters citing infrastructure investment needs.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany derives robust water access obligations from the constitutional Existenzminimum principle and maintains a predominantly public water supply system with strong universal access protections.

**Constitutional Foundation:** The BVerfG's Existenzminimum jurisprudence (derived from Articles 1(1) and 20(1) GG) encompasses access to clean water as a core component of the dignified subsistence minimum. The Hartz IV decision (1 BvL 1/09, 2010) established that social assistance benefits must cover the physical existence of recipients, including clean water and sanitation. Benefits under SGB II and SGB XII include an allowance for water and sanitation costs as part of the housing allowance (Kosten der Unterkunft und Heizung) under SGB II Section 22.

**Wasserhaushaltsgesetz (WHG -- Federal Water Resources Act, 2009):** The WHG, enacted as a federal statute under the reformed constitutional competence for water management (Article 75 GG, now Article 72(3) GG), establishes the framework for water resource management. Section 6 establishes principles including sustainable management, protection of water as a natural resource, and the priority of public water supply.

**Anschluss- und Benutzungszwang (Mandatory Connection and Use):** Under German municipal law (Kommunalrecht), municipalities may impose an obligation on property owners to connect to the public water supply and sewage systems and to use these systems. This principle, established in state municipal codes (e.g., Gemeindeordnung Baden-Wuerttemberg Section 11), ensures universal participation in public water supply while also securing the economic viability of the system. The mandatory connection obligation has been upheld by the BVerwG (Federal Administrative Court) as compatible with property rights under Article 14 GG.

### Water Quality

**Trinkwasserverordnung (TrinkwV -- Drinking Water Ordinance, 2001, amended 2023):** Implements the EU Drinking Water Directive. The TrinkwV establishes quality standards for drinking water including:

- **Microbiological parameters:** E. coli, enterococci (zero tolerance).
- **Chemical parameters:** Lead (current limit: 10 ug/L, to be reduced to 5 ug/L by 2036 per revised Drinking Water Directive), nitrate (50 mg/L), pesticides (0.1 ug/L individual; 0.5 ug/L total).
- **PFAS (Per- and Polyfluoroalkyl Substances):** The 2023 amendment to TrinkwV, implementing the revised EU Drinking Water Directive, introduced limits for PFAS compounds (100 ng/L for the sum of 20 specified PFAS).
- **Section 37 (Monitoring):** Water suppliers must conduct regular monitoring at specified frequencies based on the volume of water supplied.

### Water Supply Structure

Germany's water supply is predominantly public (oeffentliche Wasserversorgung), operated by municipal utilities (Stadtwerke), inter-municipal associations (Zweckverbaende), and public-law corporations. Unlike some EU member states, Germany has not pursued large-scale water privatization. The debate over water privatization in Berlin, where the partial privatization of Berliner Wasserbetriebe in 1999 was followed by re-municipalization in 2013 after significant public pressure and a successful referendum, is illustrative of German skepticism toward water privatization.

**Berlin Re-Municipalization:** In 2013, the State of Berlin repurchased the shares of private investors (RWE and Veolia) in Berliner Wasserbetriebe for approximately EUR 1.3 billion, following a 2011 referendum in which 98.2% of participants voted in favor of disclosing the privatization contracts (the Berliner Wassertisch initiative). The re-municipalization was driven by concerns about tariff increases and lack of transparency under private management.

### Affordability Mechanisms

**SGB II Section 22 (Kosten der Unterkunft und Heizung):** For recipients of Buergergeld (formerly Arbeitslosengeld II), the cost of reasonable housing, including water and wastewater charges, is covered by the Jobcenter. The actual costs are reimbursed up to locally determined limits (Angemessenheitsgrenzen).

**Social Water Tariffs:** Some German water utilities offer social tariffs (Sozialtarife) or fee reductions for low-income households, though this practice is less common than in some other EU member states.

### Disconnection Protections

German law provides robust protections against water service disconnection. The Grundversorgung (basic supply) principle, applicable to essential utilities, requires service providers to continue supply unless non-payment is substantial and the consumer has been given adequate warning and opportunity to pay. The BGH has held that disconnection of essential utility services (including water) requires proportionality, and disconnection that endangers health or life is impermissible.

## ECHR & EU

### UNGA Resolution 64/292 (July 28, 2010)

The UN General Assembly adopted Resolution 64/292 with 122 votes in favor, 0 against, and 41 abstentions (including the United States, United Kingdom, Canada, and Australia). The resolution "Recognizes the right to safe and clean drinking water and sanitation as a human right that is essential for the full enjoyment of life and all human rights." While not legally binding, the resolution carries significant political and normative weight and has influenced subsequent legal developments.

**UNGA Resolution 68/157 (2013):** Affirmed that "the human right to safe drinking water and sanitation entitles everyone, without discrimination, to have access to sufficient, safe, acceptable, physically accessible and affordable water for personal and domestic uses and to have physical and affordable access to sanitation, in all spheres of life, that is safe, hygienic, secure, socially and culturally acceptable and that provides privacy and ensures dignity."

### ICESCR General Comment No. 15 (2002)

The CESCR interpreted Articles 11 and 12 of the ICESCR as implicitly guaranteeing the right to water:

- **Paragraph 2:** "The human right to water entitles everyone to sufficient, safe, acceptable, physically accessible and affordable water for personal and domestic uses."
- **Paragraph 10:** "The right to water contains both freedoms and entitlements. The freedoms include the right to maintain access to existing water supplies necessary for the right to water, and the right to be free from interference, such as the right to be free from arbitrary disconnections."
- **Paragraph 12:** The normative content includes: (a) availability (sufficient and continuous supply -- 50-100 liters per person per day per WHO guidelines); (b) quality (safe, free from substances constituting a threat to health); (c) accessibility (physical, economic, non-discriminatory, information accessible).

### EU Drinking Water Directive (Directive (EU) 2020/2184)

The recast Drinking Water Directive, adopted on December 16, 2020, is the first EU legislation to explicitly reference the human right to water.

- **Recital 2:** "The European Parliament called for the Commission and the Member States to recognise the right to water and sanitation as a human right."
- **Article 16 (Access to Water Intended for Human Consumption):** Member states shall take necessary measures to improve or maintain access to water for all, in particular for vulnerable and marginalized groups. Member states shall identify and take measures to improve access for persons without access or with limited access, including vulnerable and marginalized groups.
- **Article 16(2):** Member states shall ensure that the price of water services is affordable and does not prevent persons from having access to water.
- **Right2Water European Citizens' Initiative:** The Directive was adopted in response to the first successful European Citizens' Initiative (ECI), "Right2Water" (2013), which collected over 1.8 million signatures calling for EU legislation implementing the human right to water and sanitation.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

The ECHR does not contain a specific right to water, but the Court has addressed water access under several Convention provisions:

**Article 3 (Prohibition of Inhuman Treatment):** In Hristovi v. Bulgaria (Application No. 42697/05, 2012), the Court found a violation of Article 3 where authorities cut off the water supply to Roma settlements, holding that deliberate deprivation of water access constitutes inhuman treatment.

**Article 8 (Right to Respect for Private and Family Life):** In Dubetska and Others v. Ukraine (Application No. 30499/03, 2011), the Court found a violation of Article 8 where coal mining operations contaminated the applicants' water supply and the state failed to take adequate measures to protect the affected community. The Court held that severe environmental pollution may affect individuals' well-being and prevent them from enjoying their homes.

**Article 2 (Right to Life):** In Oneryildiz v. Turkey (Application No. 48939/99, 2004), the Grand Chamber recognized positive obligations on states to protect life from environmental hazards, a principle applicable to water contamination causing or risking death.

**Hudorovič and Others v. Slovenia (Application Nos. 24816/14 and 25140/14, 2020):** The Court examined complaints by Roma communities about lack of access to clean water, finding no violation under Articles 3 and 8 because the applicants had not demonstrated that the state's measures were inadequate. However, the Court acknowledged that "access to safe drinking water" is a significant factor in assessing compliance with Article 8, advancing the normative recognition of water access under the Convention.

### Water Privatization in the EU

The EU framework does not mandate either public or private provision of water services. TFEU Article 345 provides that "The Treaties shall in no way prejudice the rules in Member States governing the system of property ownership," preserving member state discretion. The Concessions Directive (Directive 2014/23/EU) initially included water concessions within its scope but was amended following significant opposition -- particularly from the Right2Water campaign -- to exclude water services (Article 12).

## Comparative Analysis

### Constitutional Status

Germany provides the strongest constitutional protection, with the Existenzminimum principle creating a justiciable obligation to ensure water access as part of the dignified subsistence minimum. South Korea's 2018 Water Management Act Article 8 provides the most explicit statutory right to water, though its justiciability remains untested. The United States has no constitutional right to water, and the ECHR provides indirect protection through Articles 2, 3, and 8 rather than a freestanding right.

### Public vs. Private Provision

South Korea and Germany maintain predominantly public water supply systems, with privatization either prohibited or subject to strong public opposition. The United States has a mixed system with approximately 12% private provision. The EU framework is neutral on ownership but the political dynamics -- reflected in the Right2Water ECI and the exclusion of water from the Concessions Directive -- strongly favor public provision.

### Affordability Approaches

All jurisdictions address affordability, but through different mechanisms. Germany uses social assistance (SGB II/XII) to cover water costs for eligible recipients. South Korea combines social assistance with local government fee reduction programs. The United States has established LIHWAP but the program's funding is temporary and insufficient to address systemic affordability challenges. The EU Drinking Water Directive's Article 16(2) requirement that water be "affordable" represents a new harmonized obligation.

### Disconnection Protections

Germany provides the strongest disconnection protections through the Grundversorgung principle. South Korea provides moderate protections through essential service doctrine and municipal ordinances. The United States has the weakest and most fragmented protections, varying by state and municipality. ECHR case law (Hristovi) establishes that deliberate water deprivation constitutes inhuman treatment, providing a minimum standard.

### International Engagement

South Korea and Germany voted in favor of UNGA Resolution 64/292 and have ratified the ICESCR. The United States abstained from the UNGA vote and has not ratified the ICESCR. This divergence in international engagement reflects fundamentally different perspectives on whether water access constitutes a human right or an economic service best delivered through market mechanisms.
