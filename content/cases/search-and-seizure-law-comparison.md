## Summary

Search and seizure -- the power of the state to examine persons, property, and premises for evidence of criminal activity and to confiscate such evidence -- is subject to rigorous constitutional and statutory constraints in every modern legal system, reflecting the fundamental tension between effective law enforcement and the protection of individual privacy and property rights. South Korea guarantees protection against unreasonable searches and seizures under Article 12(3) of the Constitution (헌법 제12조 제3항), requiring judicial warrants issued upon presentation of probable cause, with the Criminal Procedure Act (형사소송법) Articles 106-138 establishing detailed procedures for search, seizure, and inspection; the Constitutional Court has significantly developed the law in the digital age, including its 2012 landmark decision on the scope of digital evidence searches. The United States provides protection through the Fourth Amendment, which prohibits unreasonable searches and seizures and requires warrants supported by probable cause, with the Supreme Court developing extensive jurisprudence from Katz v. United States (1967) through Carpenter v. United States (2018), which established that accessing historical cell-site location information constitutes a Fourth Amendment search requiring a warrant. Germany protects against unreasonable searches under Article 13 of the Grundgesetz (Unverletzlichkeit der Wohnung, inviolability of the home) and the comprehensive warrant requirements of the Strafprozessordnung (StPO), Sections 94-111n, with the Bundesverfassungsgericht having developed the landmark right to informational self-determination (Recht auf informationelle Selbstbestimmung) in the Census Decision (Volkszählungsurteil, BVerfGE 65, 1, 1983) and the right to the confidentiality and integrity of information technology systems in the Online-Durchsuchung decision (BVerfGE 120, 274, 2008). The ECHR protects against arbitrary searches under Article 8 (right to respect for private life and home), with extensive jurisprudence requiring that searches be "in accordance with the law," pursue a legitimate aim, and be "necessary in a democratic society," while EU Directive 2014/41/EU on the European Investigation Order facilitates cross-border evidence gathering.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

**Constitution Article 12(3):** "Warrants issued by a judge through due procedures upon the request of a prosecutor shall be presented in case of arrest, detention, seizure or search."

**Constitution Article 16:** "All citizens shall be free from intrusion into their place of residence. In case of search or seizure in a residence, a warrant issued by a judge upon request of a prosecutor shall be presented."

### Criminal Procedure Act Provisions

| Provision | Content |
|-----------|---------|
| Art. 106 | Seizure of items related to a crime |
| Art. 107 | Seizure of postal items (requires separate warrant) |
| Art. 109 | Search of premises related to a crime |
| Art. 113 | Search and seizure warrants must be issued by a judge |
| Art. 114 | Warrant must specify the suspected offense, items to be seized, and premises to be searched |
| Art. 115 | Execution of warrants during daytime only (exceptions for designated premises) |
| Art. 216 | Warrantless search incident to arrest |
| Art. 217 | Emergency seizure with subsequent court approval |

### Constitutional Court Digital Evidence Decisions

**Constitutional Court Decision 2009헌마471 (2012):** The court addressed the scope of digital evidence searches, holding that the seizure of entire hard drives or comprehensive data copies exceeds the constitutional warrant requirement. The court established that digital searches must be limited to data relevant to the suspected offense, and that law enforcement must use targeted keyword searches or other methods to minimize the acquisition of irrelevant personal information.

**Constitutional Court Decision 2018헌마927 (2019):** The court addressed the constitutionality of warrantless access to telecommunications metadata, establishing that such access requires judicial authorization given the comprehensive personal information that can be inferred from communications records.

### Warrant Exceptions

| Exception | Legal Basis | Requirements |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| Search incident to arrest | Art. 216 | Contemporaneous with lawful arrest |
| Emergency seizure | Art. 217 | Imminent destruction of evidence; subsequent court approval within 48 hours |
| Consent search | Art. 218 | Voluntary consent of occupant |
| Plain view doctrine | Judicial interpretation | Evidence in plain sight during lawful activity |

### Digital Evidence Reform

The Criminal Procedure Act was amended in 2011 (Article 106(3)) to specifically address digital evidence, requiring that electronic information be seized through "methods narrowly tailored to relevant information" (관련성 있는 정보에 한정하여). This statutory reform codified the Constitutional Court's proportionality principles and established Korea as one of the first jurisdictions to enact specific statutory protections for digital search and seizure.

## United States

### Legal Framework

**Fourth Amendment:** "The right of the people to be secure in their persons, houses, papers, and effects, against unreasonable searches and seizures, shall not be violated, and no Warrants shall issue, but upon probable cause, supported by Oath or affirmation, and particularly describing the place to be searched, and the persons or things to be seized."

### Key Supreme Court Decisions

| Case | Year | Holding |
|------|------|---------|
| Weeks v. United States | 1914 | Exclusionary rule applies to federal proceedings |
| Mapp v. Ohio | 1961 | Exclusionary rule incorporated against states |
| Katz v. United States | 1967 | Fourth Amendment protects "reasonable expectations of privacy" |
| Terry v. Ohio | 1968 | Stop-and-frisk permitted on reasonable suspicion |
| United States v. Leon | 1984 | Good faith exception to exclusionary rule |
| Kyllo v. United States | 2001 | Thermal imaging of home requires warrant |
| Riley v. California | 2014 | Warrant required to search cell phone incident to arrest |
| Carpenter v. United States | 2018 | Warrant required for historical cell-site location information |

### Carpenter v. United States (2018)

In Carpenter v. United States, 585 U.S. 296 (2018), the Supreme Court held that the government's acquisition of 127 days of historical cell-site location information (CSLI) from a wireless carrier constituted a Fourth Amendment search requiring a warrant. Chief Justice Roberts, writing for a 5-4 majority, reasoned that CSLI provides "an intimate window into a person's life, revealing not only his particular movements, but through them his familial, political, professional, religious, and sexual associations." The decision significantly narrowed the third-party doctrine established in Smith v. Maryland (1979) and United States v. Miller (1976).

### Warrant Exceptions

| Exception | Leading Case | Scope |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| Search incident to arrest | Chimel v. California (1969) | Person and area within immediate control |
| Exigent circumstances | Kentucky v. King (2011) | Imminent destruction of evidence; hot pursuit |
| Automobile exception | Carroll v. United States (1925) | Vehicles based on probable cause (no warrant) |
| Consent | Schneckloth v. Bustamonte (1973) | Voluntary consent |
| Plain view | Horton v. California (1990) | Evidence in plain sight during lawful activity |
| Special needs | Board of Education v. Earls (2002) | Drug testing; administrative searches |
| Border search | United States v. Ramsey (1977) | Routine searches at international borders |

### Exclusionary Rule

The exclusionary rule -- which prohibits the use of illegally obtained evidence in criminal proceedings -- is the primary enforcement mechanism for the Fourth Amendment. The rule has been subject to significant limitations, including the good faith exception (Leon), the independent source doctrine (Murray v. United States), the inevitable discovery doctrine (Nix v. Williams), and the attenuation doctrine (Utah v. Strieff).

## Germany

### Legal Framework

**Article 13 GG (Inviolability of the Home):**

"(1) The home is inviolable. (2) Searches may be authorized only by a judge or, when time is of the essence, by other authorities designated by law, and may be carried out only in the manner therein prescribed."

### StPO Search and Seizure Provisions

| Provision | Content |
|-----------|---------|
| § 94 | Seizure of objects as evidence |
| § 98 | Judicial authorization for seizure; Gefahr im Verzug exception |
| § 100a | Telecommunications surveillance (requires judicial order) |
| § 100b | Online searches of IT systems (Quellen-TKÜ) |
| § 100c | Acoustic surveillance of homes (Großer Lauschangriff) |
| § 102 | Search of suspect's premises |
| § 103 | Search of third-party premises (heightened standard) |
| § 105 | Judicial warrant requirement for searches |
| § 110 | Examination of papers and electronic data |

### Landmark Constitutional Decisions

**Volkszählungsurteil (Census Decision, BVerfGE 65, 1, 1983):** The Federal Constitutional Court derived the right to informational self-determination (Recht auf informationelle Selbstbestimmung) from the general personality right (Article 2(1) in conjunction with Article 1(1) GG). This right provides comprehensive protection over personal data and has profoundly influenced German search and seizure law, requiring that any state acquisition of personal information be proportionate and subject to strict procedural safeguards.

**Online-Durchsuchung (BVerfGE 120, 274, 2008):** The court created a new fundamental right -- the right to the confidentiality and integrity of information technology systems (Grundrecht auf Gewährleistung der Vertraulichkeit und Integrität informationstechnischer Systeme). This right protects against state infiltration of personal computers, smartphones, and other digital devices, requiring judicial authorization based on concrete evidence of a serious threat to a particularly important legal interest.

**Großer Lauschangriff (BVerfGE 109, 279, 2004):** The court held that acoustic surveillance of private residences under Section 100c StPO must comply with strict proportionality requirements, and that surveillance must cease when conversations touching the core area of private life (Kernbereich privater Lebensgestaltung) are detected.

### Beweisverwertungsverbot (Evidence Exclusion)

Unlike the U.S. automatic exclusionary rule, Germany applies a balancing approach to illegally obtained evidence. The Bundesgerichtshof weighs the severity of the procedural violation, the importance of the evidence, the seriousness of the offense, and the availability of alternative evidence. Evidence obtained through violations of the core area of private life is subject to an absolute prohibition on use (absolutes Beweisverwertungsverbot).

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Article 8

The Court applies a three-part test to search and seizure under Article 8:

1. **Interference:** Does the search constitute an interference with private life, home, or correspondence?
2. **In accordance with the law:** Is the interference based on accessible and foreseeable domestic law?
3. **Necessary in a democratic society:** Is the interference proportionate to the legitimate aim pursued?

### Key ECHR Decisions

**Funke v. France (Application No. 10828/84, 1993):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where French customs officers conducted a search of the applicant's home without a judicial warrant, based solely on administrative authorization. The Court emphasized that searches of private premises require effective judicial oversight.

**Iliya Stefanov v. Bulgaria (Application No. 65755/01, 2008):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 in a search of a lawyer's office that was not subject to adequate judicial supervision and procedural safeguards protecting legal professional privilege.

**Big Brother Watch v. United Kingdom [GC] (Application No. 58170/13, 2021):** The Grand Chamber addressed bulk interception of communications by intelligence services, finding that the ECHR permits bulk surveillance programs in principle but requires adequate safeguards including independent authorization, limits on the categories of selectors used, and independent oversight of the selection process.

### EU European Investigation Order (Directive 2014/41/EU)

The EIO Directive facilitates cross-border evidence gathering within the EU, allowing judicial authorities in one member state to request that investigative measures be carried out in another member state. The Directive includes specific safeguards for search and seizure requests, requiring that the requested measures be proportionate and that they would be available in a similar domestic case.

### EU Data Protection and Law Enforcement

The Law Enforcement Directive (Directive 2016/680/EU) regulates the processing of personal data by competent authorities for the prevention, investigation, detection, or prosecution of criminal offenses. The Directive establishes principles of lawfulness, purpose limitation, data minimization, and accuracy that constrain law enforcement search and seizure of digital data.

## Comparative Analysis

### Warrant Requirements

All four jurisdictions require judicial warrants for searches, but the exceptions differ. The United States recognizes the broadest range of warrant exceptions (automobile, border, special needs). Germany maintains stricter warrant requirements with a narrower Gefahr im Verzug (danger in delay) exception. South Korea requires subsequent judicial approval for emergency searches. The ECHR assesses each case under proportionality principles.

### Digital Privacy

The jurisdictions have all grappled with adapting search and seizure law to the digital age, but through different mechanisms. South Korea enacted specific statutory protections for digital evidence. The United States developed constitutional doctrine through Carpenter. Germany created an entirely new fundamental right (computer system integrity). The ECHR assesses digital searches under the existing Article 8 framework.

### Exclusionary Rules

The strength of the exclusionary rule varies dramatically. The United States maintains the strongest exclusionary rule, though significantly limited by exceptions. Germany applies a balancing approach. South Korea is developing its exclusionary jurisprudence. The ECHR does not require exclusion of illegally obtained evidence but assesses its impact on overall trial fairness.
