## Summary

Carbon pricing mechanisms -- encompassing carbon taxes, emissions trading systems (cap-and-trade), and carbon border adjustment mechanisms -- represent the primary market-based instruments for reducing greenhouse gas emissions by internalizing the external costs of carbon pollution, with fundamental design choices regarding price predictability, emission cap certainty, revenue recycling, competitiveness protection, and distributional equity shaping their effectiveness and political feasibility across jurisdictions. South Korea operates the Korea Emissions Trading Scheme (K-ETS, 한국 배출권거래제) under the Act on the Allocation and Trading of Greenhouse Gas Emission Permits (온실가스 배출권의 할당 및 거래에 관한 법률, 2012), covering approximately 73% of national greenhouse gas emissions across 684 entities in the power, industry, buildings, transport, waste, and public sectors, with the Ministry of Environment (환경부) serving as the competent authority since 2016; Korea also imposes an energy tax and transport-energy-environment tax (교통·에너지·환경세) that implicitly prices carbon. The United States lacks a federal carbon pricing mechanism, with legislative proposals including the Energy Innovation and Carbon Dividend Act repeatedly failing to advance, while subnational action through the Regional Greenhouse Gas Initiative (RGGI, covering northeastern states since 2009) and California's cap-and-trade program (linked with Quebec under the Western Climate Initiative since 2014) provides partial coverage; the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA, 2022) adopted a predominantly incentive-based approach through tax credits rather than carbon pricing. Germany participates in the EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS) for power and heavy industry and operates a national Fuel Emissions Trading Act (Brennstoffemissionshandelsgesetz, BEHG, 2021) covering transport and buildings not included in the EU ETS, with carbon prices rising from EUR 25/tonne CO2 in 2021 to EUR 45/tonne in 2024 on a fixed-price trajectory transitioning to auctioning. The EU framework centers on the EU ETS (Directive 2003/87/EC, reformed under the Fit for 55 package), the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM, Regulation (EU) 2023/956), the new ETS 2 for buildings and road transport (from 2027), and the Social Climate Fund to address distributional impacts.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea, as a major industrialized economy and significant emitter, has developed a comprehensive emissions trading system as the centerpiece of its climate policy.

**Framework Act on Carbon Neutrality and Green Growth (탄소중립·녹색성장 기본법, 2022):** This overarching legislation establishes Korea's 2050 carbon neutrality target and 2030 nationally determined contribution (NDC) of 40% reduction from 2018 levels. The Act provides the legal basis for climate policy instruments including the K-ETS.

**Korea Emissions Trading Scheme (K-ETS):** Launched in January 2015 under the Act on the Allocation and Trading of Greenhouse Gas Emission Permits (2012), K-ETS is Asia's first nationwide mandatory emissions trading system:

**Coverage:** K-ETS covers approximately 684 entities responsible for roughly 73% of national greenhouse gas emissions. Covered sectors include power generation, steel, cement, petrochemicals, refining, aviation, waste, and buildings. The threshold for inclusion is annual emissions of 125,000 tonnes CO2-equivalent at the entity level or 25,000 tonnes at the facility level.

**Allocation Phases:** K-ETS operates in multi-year planning periods: Phase 1 (2015-2017) with near-complete free allocation using a grandfathering approach; Phase 2 (2018-2020) introducing benchmarking-based allocation and limited auctioning (3%); Phase 3 (2021-2025) increasing auction share to approximately 10% and tightening the cap in line with the enhanced NDC.

**Market Mechanisms:** Allowances (KAU, Korean Allowance Units) are tradable on the Korea Exchange (KRX). Banking and borrowing between compliance years are permitted within limits. Offset credits (KCU, Korean Credit Units) from approved domestic and international offset projects may be used for up to 5% of compliance obligations (increased to 10% in Phase 3 for international credits).

### Carbon Price Dynamics

The K-ETS carbon price has remained relatively low compared to European benchmarks, trading in the range of KRW 8,000-20,000 per tonne CO2 (approximately $6-16 USD). Several factors contribute:

**Market Liquidity:** Low liquidity resulting from corporate reluctance to trade and information asymmetry. The Korean government has intervened through the Market Maker system and Allowance Reserve to stabilize prices.

**Free Allocation Surplus:** Generous free allocation, particularly in Phase 1, created an oversupply of allowances that depressed prices. The government has responded by tightening allocation benchmarks and increasing the auction share.

**Energy Tax (교통·에너지·환경세):** Korea levies a Transport-Energy-Environment Tax on petroleum products under the Transport, Energy, and Environment Tax Act, functioning as an implicit carbon price. Combined with individual consumption taxes on fossil fuels, the effective carbon rate on energy use in Korea is among the highest in the OECD, though the rate is not explicitly linked to carbon content.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States remains the largest major economy without a federal carbon pricing mechanism, with climate policy relying on a fragmented combination of regulation, tax incentives, and subnational carbon markets.

**Federal Inaction:** Multiple federal carbon pricing proposals have been introduced but failed to advance through Congress. The American Clean Energy and Security Act (Waxman-Markey, H.R. 2454, 2009) passed the House but died in the Senate. Subsequent proposals including the Energy Innovation and Carbon Dividend Act (H.R. 2307, introduced in multiple Congresses) and the American Opportunity Carbon Fee Act have similarly failed. Political polarization, fossil fuel industry opposition, and concerns about economic competitiveness and energy prices have prevented federal action.

**Clean Air Act Authority:** The Supreme Court's decision in Massachusetts v. EPA, 549 U.S. 497 (2007), confirmed EPA's authority to regulate greenhouse gases as air pollutants under the Clean Air Act. However, West Virginia v. EPA, 597 U.S. 697 (2022), significantly limited EPA's ability to adopt "generation shifting" approaches under Section 111(d), invoking the major questions doctrine to restrict transformative regulatory actions without clear congressional authorization.

### Inflation Reduction Act (2022)

The Inflation Reduction Act (IRA, Pub. L. 117-169) adopted an incentive-based approach rather than carbon pricing, providing approximately $369 billion in energy security and climate spending through:

**Clean Energy Tax Credits:** Extended and expanded production tax credits (PTC, 26 U.S.C. Section 45) and investment tax credits (ITC, 26 U.S.C. Section 48) for renewable energy, with new technology-neutral clean electricity credits (Sections 45Y, 48E) beginning in 2025.

**Methane Emissions Charge:** The IRA's Methane Emissions Reduction Program (Section 60113) imposes a charge on methane emissions from petroleum and natural gas facilities reporting more than 25,000 metric tonnes CO2-equivalent, at $900/tonne in 2024 rising to $1,500/tonne in 2026 -- the first explicit federal GHG emissions fee.

### Subnational Carbon Pricing

**Regional Greenhouse Gas Initiative (RGGI):** Launched in 2009, RGGI is a cooperative cap-and-trade program covering CO2 emissions from the power sector across participating northeastern and mid-Atlantic states (currently Connecticut, Delaware, Maine, Maryland, Massachusetts, New Hampshire, New Jersey, New York, Pennsylvania (litigation pending), Rhode Island, Vermont, and Virginia (withdrew 2024)). RGGI operates through quarterly auctions of CO2 allowances, with prices ranging from $13-16/tonne. Revenue is invested in energy efficiency, renewable energy, and consumer benefit programs.

**California Cap-and-Trade Program:** Established under AB 32 (Global Warming Solutions Act of 2006) and administered by the California Air Resources Board (CARB), California's program covers approximately 80% of statewide GHG emissions from major industrial sources, electricity generators, and fuel distributors. The program is linked with Quebec's system under the Western Climate Initiative (WCI). Recent allowance prices have ranged from $30-40/tonne, with a price ceiling and floor mechanism. AB 1279 (2022) codified the 2045 carbon neutrality target, and SB 905 (2022) established a framework for carbon capture and sequestration.

### Border Adjustment Proposals

The PROVE IT Act (S. 1863, introduced 2023) proposed requiring DOE to study the emissions intensity of domestic and foreign production of key commodities, establishing the data infrastructure for a potential CBAM. The Foreign Pollution Fee Act and Clean Competition Act have proposed border carbon adjustments but have not advanced beyond committee consideration.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany operates under a dual carbon pricing system: the EU ETS for power generation and heavy industry, and the national BEHG for transport and buildings.

**Treibhausgas-Emissionshandelsgesetz (TEHG):** The Greenhouse Gas Emissions Trading Act transposes the EU ETS Directive into German law, with the Deutsche Emissionshandelsstelle (DEHSt, German Emissions Trading Authority) at the Umweltbundesamt (Federal Environment Agency) serving as the competent authority for administration and enforcement.

**Brennstoffemissionshandelsgesetz (BEHG, 2021):** The national Fuel Emissions Trading Act established a carbon pricing mechanism for fossil fuels used in the transport and buildings sectors not covered by the EU ETS:

**Fixed Price Phase:** From 2021-2025, certificates are sold at fixed prices: EUR 25 (2021), EUR 30 (2022), EUR 35 (2023), EUR 45 (2024), EUR 55 (2025). This provides price certainty for businesses and consumers but does not guarantee specific emission reductions.

**Transition Phase (2026):** Certificates will be auctioned within a price corridor of EUR 55-65, introducing limited market dynamics while maintaining price boundaries.

**Market Phase (from 2027):** The BEHG will be integrated into the EU ETS 2 (see below), with full market-based pricing through auctioning. The German national system will transition to the EU-wide framework.

**Constitutional Challenge:** The Bundesverfassungsgericht (Federal Constitutional Court) upheld the constitutionality of the BEHG in its landmark climate decision of March 24, 2021 (BVerfGE 157, 30 -- Klimaschutz), while simultaneously holding that the Federal Climate Protection Act (Bundes-Klimaschutzgesetz, KSG) was partially unconstitutional insofar as it lacked sufficient reduction pathways beyond 2030, thereby violating the fundamental rights of younger generations under Article 20a (state objective of environmental protection) in conjunction with Article 2(1) GG (general freedom of action). This decision accelerated the tightening of German climate targets and carbon pricing trajectories.

### Revenue Recycling

Revenue from the BEHG is directed to the Climate and Transformation Fund (Klima- und Transformationsfonds, KTF), which finances:

**Building Efficiency:** Federal support for energy-efficient building renovation (Bundesförderung für effiziente Gebäude, BEG).

**Transport Decarbonization:** Support for electric vehicle purchase (Umweltbonus, discontinued 2023), charging infrastructure, and rail investment.

**Electricity Price Relief:** Reduction of the EEG surcharge (Erneuerbare-Energien-Gesetz Umlage), which was abolished entirely from July 1, 2022, partially funded by BEHG revenue.

**Industry Transformation:** Carbon Contracts for Difference (Klimaschutzverträge) supporting industrial decarbonization investments by guaranteeing a carbon price for the duration of the contract, covering the cost gap between carbon-intensive and clean production.

### Competitiveness Concerns

The dual exposure to EU ETS costs (for installations) and BEHG costs (for fuels) has raised concerns about carbon leakage and industrial competitiveness, particularly for energy-intensive trade-exposed (EITE) sectors. The German government has relied on EU ETS free allocation, CBAM (transitional phase), and national support measures including electricity price compensation and carbon contracts for difference.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS)

The EU ETS, established by Directive 2003/87/EC and reformed multiple times, is the world's largest carbon market:

**Coverage and Cap:** The EU ETS covers approximately 10,000 installations in power generation, manufacturing industry, and intra-EU aviation across all 27 EU member states plus Iceland, Liechtenstein, and Norway. The cap decreases annually by a linear reduction factor of 4.3% (from 2024, increased from 2.2%) to achieve a 62% reduction from 2005 levels by 2030.

**Market Stabilisation Reserve (MSR):** Established in 2019 and strengthened under the Fit for 55 reforms, the MSR automatically adjusts the supply of allowances by absorbing surplus allowances when the total number in circulation exceeds 833 million and releasing them when below 400 million, improving price stability and ensuring the cap remains binding.

**Free Allocation and Benchmarking:** Allowances in the industrial sector are allocated free based on product-specific benchmarks representing the 10% most efficient installations. The carbon leakage list determines which sectors receive 100% free allocation versus those that receive declining free allocation. Under the Fit for 55 reforms, free allocation for CBAM sectors will phase out between 2026 and 2034.

**Price Development:** EU ETS allowance (EUA) prices have risen dramatically from below EUR 5/tonne in 2017 to peaks exceeding EUR 100/tonne in 2023, before stabilizing in the EUR 65-80 range. This price signal has driven significant fuel switching from coal to gas and renewables in the power sector.

### Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM)

Regulation (EU) 2023/956 establishing the CBAM is the EU's primary instrument for addressing carbon leakage and ensuring the environmental integrity of EU climate policy:

**Transitional Phase (October 2023-December 2025):** Importers of CBAM goods (iron and steel, cement, aluminum, fertilizers, electricity, and hydrogen) must report the embedded emissions of their imports quarterly, without financial obligations. The European Commission has established the CBAM Transitional Registry for reporting purposes.

**Definitive Phase (from January 2026):** Importers must purchase CBAM certificates corresponding to the embedded emissions in their imports, priced at the weekly average auction price of EU ETS allowances. The number of certificates required will be reduced by the amount of free allocation still provided to EU producers (declining to zero by 2034) and by any carbon price effectively paid in the country of origin.

**WTO Compatibility:** The CBAM has been designed to comply with WTO rules, particularly GATT Article III (national treatment) and Article XX (general exceptions for measures necessary to protect human health or the environment). The mechanism applies equally to domestic and foreign producers (through the parallel phase-out of free allocation), does not discriminate based on the country of origin, and is linked to actual carbon content rather than national origin. Nonetheless, WTO challenges are anticipated from major exporting countries.

### ETS 2 (Buildings and Road Transport)

The Fit for 55 package established a new, separate emissions trading system (ETS 2) for fuel combustion in buildings, road transport, and additional industrial sectors not covered by the existing EU ETS, with emissions trading starting in 2027:

**Price Containment:** Given the sensitivity of fuel prices for households, the ETS 2 includes a price containment mechanism: if the price exceeds EUR 45/tonne, additional allowances are released from the MSR. The start date may be delayed to 2028 if energy prices are exceptionally high.

**Social Climate Fund:** Regulation (EU) 2023/955 establishes the Social Climate Fund with EUR 65 billion (2026-2032) to address the distributional impacts of the ETS 2 on vulnerable households, micro-enterprises, and transport users, funded by ETS 2 auction revenue.

### ECHR and Climate Obligations

**KlimaSeniorinnen v. Switzerland (Application No. 53600/20, Grand Chamber, 2024):** The landmark Grand Chamber judgment held that Switzerland violated Article 8 ECHR (right to respect for private and family life) by failing to adopt adequate measures to combat climate change. The Court established that the Convention requires states to adopt and implement regulations and measures capable of mitigating the effects of climate change, providing a human rights basis for climate action that reinforces the legal foundations for carbon pricing mechanisms.

## Comparative Analysis

### Carbon Tax versus Cap-and-Trade

The fundamental design choice between carbon taxes (providing price certainty) and cap-and-trade systems (providing emission quantity certainty) manifests differently across jurisdictions. Germany's BEHG hybrid approach -- fixed prices transitioning to trading -- reflects an attempt to combine both advantages. The EU ETS, as a mature cap-and-trade system with the MSR, has achieved meaningful emission reductions but experienced periods of price volatility and oversupply. The U.S. subnational programs demonstrate that both approaches can coexist, with RGGI using a narrow cap-and-trade design and California implementing a broad cap-and-trade with price floors and ceilings.

### Revenue Recycling

Revenue use is critical for political sustainability and distributional equity. Germany's direction of BEHG revenue to the KTF supports green investment while offsetting regressive impacts through electricity price reductions. The EU's Social Climate Fund addresses equity concerns from ETS 2. RGGI states invest proceeds in energy efficiency and clean energy. California directs a portion of revenue to disadvantaged communities through the Greenhouse Gas Reduction Fund. The absence of federal carbon pricing in the U.S. eliminates revenue recycling as a policy tool at the national level.

### Border Carbon Adjustments

The EU's CBAM represents the most advanced border carbon adjustment mechanism globally, designed to prevent carbon leakage and maintain competitiveness as the EU tightens its carbon cap. The U.S. lacks a comparable federal mechanism, though proposals continue to be introduced. South Korea and other major trading partners are closely monitoring the CBAM's implementation and WTO implications. The interaction between CBAM and bilateral carbon pricing recognition agreements remains a key area of development.

### Effectiveness and Ambition

The EU ETS has demonstrated that emissions trading can drive significant emission reductions when the cap is sufficiently tight and price signals are robust. Korea's K-ETS has achieved modest reductions but faces challenges from low carbon prices and generous free allocation. The absence of U.S. federal carbon pricing means the world's second-largest emitter relies on regulation and incentives rather than market-based pricing, potentially undermining the efficiency of global emission reduction efforts. Germany's constitutional court decision establishing intergenerational climate obligations provides a unique legal foundation for ambitious carbon pricing trajectories.
