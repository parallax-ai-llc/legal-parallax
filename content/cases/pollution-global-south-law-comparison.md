## Summary

Pollution regulation across the Global South encompasses radically different legal approaches to industrial contamination, air quality, water pollution, and environmental disasters, shaped by the tension between rapid industrialization and environmental protection. China declared a "war on pollution" (污染防治攻坚战) in 2014, deploying the revised Environmental Protection Law (2014), the Air Pollution Prevention and Control Law (大气污染防治法, 2015 revision), the Water Pollution Prevention and Control Law (水污染防治法, 2017 revision), and the Soil Pollution Prevention and Control Law (土壤污染防治法, 2018) to address decades of environmental degradation that cost an estimated 6.5% of GDP annually. India's National Green Tribunal (NGT), established under the National Green Tribunal Act 2010, created Asia's first specialized environmental court with original jurisdiction over environmental disputes and penalties up to INR 100 crore (approximately USD 12 million). In Africa, the Ogoniland environmental disaster in Nigeria's Niger Delta -- documented in the landmark 2011 UNEP Environmental Assessment of Ogoniland -- revealed systemic failures in oil spill regulation under the Oil Pipelines Act 1956 and the Petroleum Act 1969. Brazil's twin mining dam disasters at Mariana (November 2015, Samarco/BHP Billiton/Vale) and Brumadinho (January 2019, Vale) killed 19 and 270 people respectively, triggering the National Dam Safety Policy (Lei 12.334/2010, amended by Lei 14.066/2020) and the largest environmental reparation in Latin American history. Indonesia confronts transboundary haze pollution from peatland fires under the ASEAN Agreement on Transboundary Haze Pollution (2002) and domestic Law No. 32/2009 on Environmental Protection and Management.

## China (中国)

### Legal Framework

China's pollution governance framework underwent a fundamental transformation starting in 2014, driven by public outrage over severe air pollution episodes and the Communist Party's recognition that environmental degradation threatened social stability and economic growth:

- **Environmental Protection Law (环境保护法, 2014 Revision):** Often called the "toughest EPL in history," the revision introduced: consecutive daily penalties for ongoing violations with no maximum cap (Art. 59), replacing the previous one-time fine system; seizure of polluting facilities and administrative detention of responsible persons for up to 15 days (Arts. 25, 63); mandatory disclosure of environmental information by listed polluters (Art. 55); public interest litigation standing for NGOs registered for five or more years with pollution-related mandates (Art. 58); and accountability for local officials who interfere with enforcement (Art. 68).
- **Air Pollution Prevention and Control Law (大气污染防治法, 2015 Revision):** Effective 1 January 2016, the revision raised maximum fines for violations from RMB 500,000 to "no upper limit" based on daily penalties. It established regional joint prevention and control mechanisms (Art. 86) and authorized local governments to restrict vehicle use during heavy pollution episodes.
- **Water Pollution Prevention and Control Law (水污染防治法, 2017 Revision):** Strengthened standards for industrial wastewater discharge, introduced the "River Chief" (河长制) and "Lake Chief" (湖长制) systems requiring local government leaders to take personal responsibility for water quality in their jurisdictions.
- **Soil Pollution Prevention and Control Law (土壤污染防治法, 2018):** China's first dedicated soil pollution statute, effective 1 January 2019, addressing contamination of over 16% of arable land (revealed in the 2014 National Soil Pollution Survey). Establishes a risk-based management system, soil pollution investigation requirements, mandatory remediation for contaminated sites, and a soil pollution prevention fund.
- **Criminal Law Amendment (XI) (2020):** Article 338 (environmental pollution causing major accidents) was strengthened, with penalties of 3-7 years for serious environmental pollution. The 2016 Supreme People's Court Interpretation on Environmental Crimes lowered conviction thresholds and clarified that exceeding pollutant discharge standards by 100% constitutes "serious environmental pollution."

### Key Principles

China's "war on pollution" is operationalized through the Three-Year Action Plans for Blue Sky, Clear Water, and Clean Land (蓝天、碧水、净土保卫战), launched in 2018 under the State Council's "Opinions on Strengthening Ecological Environmental Protection and Resolutely Fighting the Battle against Pollution." Performance targets are embedded in cadre evaluation metrics, creating political incentives for provincial and municipal leaders to meet environmental goals.

The Central Environmental Inspection (中央生态环境保护督察) system, launched in 2015 as a pilot and expanded nationwide, deploys inspection teams answering directly to the CPC Central Committee and State Council. Two full rounds (2016-2018, 2019-2022) and targeted follow-ups have held over 18,000 officials accountable, including provincial and ministerial-level cadres.

### Notable Enforcement

- **Tengger Desert Illegal Pollution Dumping (2018):** Central Environmental Inspectors discovered massive illegal dumping of industrial waste in the Tengger Desert on the Gansu-Inner Mongolia border. Fifteen government officials were disciplined, eight individuals criminally prosecuted, and responsible companies fined over RMB 100 million.
- **Changzhou "Toxic School" Case (2016):** The Jiangsu Higher People's Court ruled on a public interest litigation case where over 500 students at Changzhou Foreign Language School developed health problems from exposure to contaminated soil from adjacent former chemical plants. The court ordered ecological restoration and compensation.
- **Beijing Air Quality Improvement:** Beijing's PM2.5 annual average declined from 89.5 μg/m³ in 2013 to 32 μg/m³ in 2023, achieving a 64% reduction through coal-to-gas switching, industrial relocation, vehicle emission standards (China VI), and regional coordination across the Beijing-Tianjin-Hebei region.

## India (भारत)

### Legal Framework

India's pollution law framework combines colonial-era nuisance principles, post-independence legislation, and an extraordinarily active judiciary that has effectively legislated environmental standards through expansive constitutional interpretation:

- **Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1974:** India's first pollution-specific statute, establishing the Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) and State Pollution Control Boards (SPCBs) with powers to set effluent standards, grant consent to establish and operate polluting industries, and take enforcement action including closure orders.
- **Air (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1981:** Extended the CPCB/SPCB system to air quality, establishing ambient air quality standards and emission standards for industries and vehicles.
- **Environment (Protection) Act, 1986 (EPA):** The umbrella legislation enacted following Bhopal, granting the Central Government broad rule-making power. Key subordinate legislation includes the Hazardous Waste Management Rules 2016, the Environmental Impact Assessment Notification 2006, Coastal Regulation Zone Notification 2019, and National Ambient Air Quality Standards 2009.
- **National Green Tribunal Act, 2010:** Established the NGT as a specialized judicial body with original jurisdiction over "substantial questions relating to the environment" and civil disputes involving environmental damage. The NGT can award compensation up to INR 100 crore (approximately USD 12 million), impose penalties, and direct restoration measures. Its orders are binding and enforceable as court decrees. The NGT has five benches (Principal Bench in Delhi, circuit benches in Bhopal, Pune, Kolkata, and Chennai).
- **Public Liability Insurance Act, 1991:** Enacted in response to Bhopal, requiring industries handling hazardous substances to take insurance policies providing immediate relief to persons affected by accidents.

### Key Principles

The Supreme Court's decision in M.C. Mehta v. Union of India (Oleum Gas Leak case, AIR 1987 SC 1086) established the principle of "absolute liability" -- enterprises engaged in inherently dangerous or hazardous activities are absolutely liable for harm caused, regardless of due diligence, with no exceptions for acts of God or third-party acts. This goes beyond the English strict liability rule in Rylands v. Fletcher by eliminating all defenses.

The "polluter pays" principle was incorporated into Indian law through Indian Council for Enviro-Legal Action v. Union of India (1996) 3 SCC 212, where the Supreme Court held that polluting industries are absolutely liable for the cost of environmental restoration, and this liability extends to the cost of reversing ecological damage.

### Notable Cases

- **Bhopal Gas Tragedy (Union Carbide Corporation v. Union of India, 1989):** The world's worst industrial disaster (December 3-4, 1984) killed an estimated 3,787 people (official figure, with unofficial estimates of 15,000-20,000) and affected over 500,000. The Supreme Court approved a settlement of USD 470 million in 1989, widely criticized as inadequate. Criminal proceedings resulted in the conviction of seven former Union Carbide India Limited officers in 2010 under Section 304A IPC (causing death by negligence), with sentences of two years imprisonment -- the maximum under that section.
- **M.C. Mehta v. Union of India (Ganga Pollution, 1987-2017):** A series of orders spanning three decades directing the closure of polluting tanneries in Kanpur, establishment of effluent treatment plants, and cleanup of the Ganga, culminating in the Namami Gange Programme (2014) with INR 20,000 crore (USD 2.4 billion) allocation.
- **M.C. Mehta v. Union of India (Vehicular Pollution, 1999):** The Supreme Court mandated the conversion of Delhi's entire public transport fleet (buses, three-wheelers, taxis) to compressed natural gas (CNG) by a fixed deadline, transforming Delhi's air quality and establishing the judiciary's power to mandate technological transitions.
- **Commission for Air Quality Management in NCR and Adjoining Areas Act, 2021:** Established a statutory commission replacing the EPCA (Environment Pollution (Prevention and Control) Authority) for the Delhi-NCR region, with powers to impose fines up to INR 1 crore and imprisonment up to 5 years for air quality violations.

## Africa (Nigeria and South Africa)

### Nigeria

#### Legal Framework

Nigeria's pollution regulation is defined by the catastrophic failure to regulate oil industry contamination in the Niger Delta, combined with a broader regulatory framework that excludes the country's primary pollution source:

- **NESREA Act 2007:** Establishes the National Environmental Standards and Regulations Enforcement Agency with powers to enforce environmental standards, conduct inspections, and impose penalties. However, Section 7(g) explicitly excludes "the oil and gas sector and all its ancillary activities" from NESREA's jurisdiction -- a carve-out that renders the agency irrelevant to Nigeria's most severe pollution crisis.
- **National Oil Spill Detection and Response Agency (NOSDRA) Act 2006:** Establishes NOSDRA to detect, respond to, and coordinate cleanup of oil spills. NOSDRA's Joint Investigation Visit (JIV) process determines spill cause and volume, but has been criticized for industry co-option, as oil companies participate alongside government officials and affected communities.
- **Oil Pipelines Act 1956 (Cap O7 LFN 2004):** Provides for compensation for damage caused by oil pipeline operations. Section 11(5) limits liability to damage arising from the pipeline itself, and the negligence-based liability standard (rather than strict liability) creates significant evidentiary barriers for affected communities.
- **Petroleum Industry Act 2021 (PIA):** The PIA's environmental provisions (Chapter 3) require environmental management plans, remediation, and decommissioning, but enforcement mechanisms remain weak and the 3% of OPEX allocated to host community development trusts has been criticized as insufficient.

#### UNEP Ogoniland Assessment (2011)

The United Nations Environment Programme's Environmental Assessment of Ogoniland is the most comprehensive independent assessment of oil pollution in the Niger Delta. Key findings include:

- Contamination of drinking water in Ogoniland communities by hydrocarbons at levels 900 times above WHO guidelines.
- Soil contamination by petroleum hydrocarbons to a depth of 5 meters in many locations.
- Mangrove forest destruction affecting livelihoods of fishing communities.
- UNEP recommended a USD 1 billion, 25-30 year cleanup program through the establishment of an Ogoniland Environmental Restoration Authority. The Hydrocarbon Pollution Remediation Project (HYPREP), established in 2016 with a governing council including UNEP, the Nigerian government, and community representatives, began remediation work in 2019 but has been criticized for slow progress, with fewer than 30 of 57 emergency measures completed by 2024.

### South Africa

#### Legal Framework

South Africa's pollution framework is comparatively well-developed, anchored in the constitutional right to an environment not harmful to health or well-being (Section 24):

- **National Environmental Management: Air Quality Act (NEM:AQA, No. 39 of 2004):** Replaced the Atmospheric Pollution Prevention Act 1965, establishing national ambient air quality standards, listed activities requiring atmospheric emission licenses, priority areas (including the Highveld, Vaal Triangle, and Waterberg-Bojanala), and the National Air Quality Officer.
- **National Environmental Management: Waste Act (NEM:WA, No. 59 of 2008):** Establishes a waste management hierarchy (avoid, reduce, reuse, recycle, recover, treat, dispose), licensing of waste management activities, contaminated land assessment and remediation, and the Waste Management Bureau.
- **National Water Act (No. 36 of 1998, NWA):** Implements the constitutional right to water (s27) through water use licensing, pollution prevention, and the reserve system ensuring ecological and basic human water needs are met before other allocations.
- **NEMA Section 28 (Duty of Care):** Imposes a general duty of care on every person who causes, has caused, or may cause significant pollution or degradation to take reasonable measures to prevent or minimize such pollution. Section 28(2) empowers the Director-General to direct responsible persons to take specific remediation measures.

#### Notable Cases

- **Groundwork Trust v. Minister of Environmental Affairs (2017):** Environmental organizations challenged the government's failure to enforce air quality standards in the Highveld Priority Area, where coal-fired power stations, petrochemical plants, and mining operations create some of Africa's worst air pollution. The court ordered the Minister to develop and implement adequate plans to bring air quality into compliance.
- **Federation for a Sustainable Environment v. Minister of Water Affairs (2012):** The North Gauteng High Court found that acid mine drainage from abandoned gold mines on the Witwatersrand posed an imminent threat to the environment and ordered the government to take remedial action.
- **Settler v. Médecins Sans Frontières (2021):** The lead contamination case in Kabwe, Zambia (brought before South African courts under the Companies Act provisions allowing jurisdiction over parent companies) highlighted transboundary pollution liability, with implications for South African corporate liability for pollution caused by subsidiaries in other African countries.

## Latin America (Brazil, Mexico, and Colombia)

### Legal Framework

Latin American pollution governance is characterized by comprehensive legislation, strict (objective) liability, and high-profile environmental disasters that have catalyzed legal reforms:

- **Brazil -- National Environmental Policy (PNMA, Lei 6.938/1981):** Brazil's foundational environmental statute establishing the National Environmental System (SISNAMA), the National Environmental Council (CONAMA), and objective (strict) liability for environmental damage (Art. 14 §1). The polluter is liable regardless of fault, and liability is joint and several.
- **Brazil -- Environmental Crimes Act (Lei 9.605/1998):** Criminalizes pollution causing harm to human health, mortality of animals, or significant destruction of flora, with penalties of 1-5 years imprisonment. Critically, the statute extends criminal liability to legal persons (corporations), making Brazil one of the first countries to allow corporate criminal prosecution for environmental offenses.
- **Brazil -- National Dam Safety Policy (Lei 12.334/2010, amended by Lei 14.066/2020):** Following the Mariana (2015) and Brumadinho (2019) disasters, the 2020 amendment banned upstream tailings dam construction, mandated emergency action plans, established dam safety inspection requirements, and created the National Dam Safety Information System.
- **Mexico -- General Law on Ecological Equilibrium and Environmental Protection (LGEEPA, 1988):** Mexico's framework environmental law establishing EIA requirements, environmental standards (NOMs), licensing, and the Federal Environmental Protection Attorney (PROFEPA) as an enforcement agency.
- **Colombia -- Law 99/1993:** Restructured the environmental management system, creating the Ministry of Environment, the National Environmental Licensing Authority (ANLA), and Regional Autonomous Corporations (CARs) as the primary pollution enforcement bodies.

### Mariana and Brumadinho Disasters

The collapse of the Fundão tailings dam in Mariana, Minas Gerais, on 5 November 2015 released 43.7 million cubic meters of iron ore tailings, killing 19 people and devastating 680 km of the Rio Doce river system. The dam was operated by Samarco, a joint venture between Vale S.A. and BHP Billiton. The Renova Foundation was established in 2016 under a Conduct Adjustment Agreement (TAC) between Samarco, Vale, BHP, and federal and state governments, with BRL 20 billion (USD 3.8 billion) committed for reparation and restoration.

On 25 January 2019, the Córrego do Feijão tailings dam in Brumadinho collapsed, killing 270 people -- the deadliest mining disaster in Brazilian history. Vale S.A. agreed to a BRL 37.68 billion (approximately USD 7 billion) settlement with the state of Minas Gerais in February 2021, the largest environmental reparation agreement in Latin American history. Criminal charges were filed against 16 Vale and TÜV SÜD employees for qualified homicide (homicídio qualificado). In the UK, proceedings in Municipio de Mariana v. BHP Group (2022) EWCA Civ 951 allowed 200,000+ claimants to pursue BHP in English courts.

### Notable Cases

- **Chevron/Texaco v. Ecuador (Lago Agrio):** While involving Ecuador rather than the five focal jurisdictions, this case profoundly influenced Latin American pollution jurisprudence. Indigenous communities in the Ecuadorian Amazon obtained a USD 9.5 billion judgment against Chevron for oil contamination (Corte Provincial de Justicia de Sucumbíos, 2011), though enforcement has been contested internationally.
- **Mexico -- Grupo México/Buenavista del Cobre Spill (2014):** The Sonora mine spill released 40,000 cubic meters of copper sulfate acid solution into the Sonora and Bacanuchi rivers. PROFEPA imposed a MXN 23.5 million fine, widely criticized as inadequate. A trust fund of MXN 2 billion was established for affected communities.
- **Colombia -- Drummond Coal v. Comunidad de Boqueron (2015):** The Constitutional Court ordered Drummond to implement measures to prevent coal dust pollution affecting communities near open-pit mines in Cesar Department, applying the precautionary principle under Law 99/1993.

## Southeast Asia (Indonesia and Philippines)

### Legal Framework

- **Indonesia -- Environmental Protection and Management Law (Law No. 32/2009, PPLH):** The cornerstone environmental statute establishing strict liability for environmental damage (Art. 88), EIA (AMDAL) requirements, environmental permits, and criminal sanctions of up to 15 years imprisonment and IDR 15 billion in fines. The Omnibus Law on Job Creation (2020/2023) controversially modified AMDAL requirements, replacing environmental permits with "environmental approvals" integrated into business licensing.
- **Indonesia -- Government Regulation No. 22/2021 on Environmental Protection:** Implementing regulation under the Omnibus Law, consolidating environmental approval processes and establishing risk-based environmental assessment categories (low, medium-low, medium-high, high risk).
- **Philippines -- Philippine Clean Air Act (RA 8749, 1999):** Establishes the framework for air quality management, including ambient and emission standards, mobile source standards (requiring the phase-out of leaded gasoline), banning incineration (Section 20), and establishing the Pollution Adjudication Board (PAB).
- **Philippines -- Philippine Clean Water Act (RA 9275, 2004):** Regulates water quality through the National Water Quality Status Report, water quality management areas, wastewater charge system, and effluent standards.
- **Philippines -- Toxic Substances and Hazardous and Nuclear Wastes Control Act (RA 6969, 1990):** Regulates import, manufacture, processing, sale, distribution, use, and disposal of chemical substances and hazardous wastes.

### Transboundary Haze

The 2015 Indonesian haze crisis was the worst in a series of transboundary pollution events caused by peatland and forest fires, primarily for oil palm and pulpwood plantation development in Sumatra and Kalimantan. The crisis affected Singapore, Malaysia, the Philippines, and Thailand, with the World Bank estimating economic losses of USD 16.1 billion for Indonesia alone and health impacts including an estimated 100,300 premature deaths across the region (Koplitz et al., Environmental Research Letters, 2016).

The ASEAN Agreement on Transboundary Haze Pollution (2002, entered into force 2003) is the primary multilateral framework. Indonesia ratified in 2014 after years of delay. The Agreement establishes a monitoring and notification system, an ASEAN Coordinating Centre for Transboundary Haze Pollution Control, and obligations to prevent and mitigate haze, but contains no binding enforcement mechanism, relying on the "ASEAN Way" of non-interference and consensus.

### Notable Cases

- **PT Bumi Mekar Hijau v. KLHK (Indonesia, Supreme Court, 2016):** The Supreme Court upheld IDR 78.5 billion in civil damages against a pulp plantation company for deliberately burning peatlands in South Sumatra, the highest environmental damage award in Indonesian history at the time.
- **PT Kallista Alam v. KLHK (Indonesia, Supreme Court, 2015):** The Supreme Court upheld IDR 114.3 billion in damages and IDR 251.7 billion in restoration costs against a palm oil company for illegal peatland burning in the Tripa peat swamp ecosystem in Aceh.
- **Manila Bay Rehabilitation (MMDA v. Concerned Residents of Manila Bay, G.R. Nos. 171947-48, 2008):** The Philippine Supreme Court ordered a multi-agency cleanup of Manila Bay, invoking the government's duty under the Clean Water Act and the constitutional right to a balanced ecology. The continuing mandamus has required ongoing compliance reports for over 15 years.
- **Metropolitan Manila Development Authority v. Jancom Environmental Corporation (2002):** The Supreme Court addressed waste management contract disputes in Metro Manila, reinforcing the prohibition on incineration under the Clean Air Act.

## Comparative Analysis

### Liability Frameworks

The Global South reveals a spectrum of pollution liability standards. Brazil and India anchor the strict/absolute liability end -- Brazil's objective liability under Lei 6.938/1981 requires no proof of fault, and India's absolute liability (MC Mehta 1987) eliminates even the defenses available under strict liability. China's 2014 EPL introduced reversal of burden of proof (the polluter must prove they did not cause the harm), a significant shift from the plaintiff-bears-the-burden norm. Indonesia's Law 32/2009 provides for strict liability (Art. 88) but enforcement is inconsistent. Nigeria stands at the opposite end, with the Oil Pipelines Act's negligence standard creating nearly insurmountable evidentiary barriers for communities affected by oil spills.

### Enforcement Mechanisms

China's central environmental inspection system represents the most powerful top-down enforcement mechanism, leveraging CPC authority to overcome local protectionism. India's NGT provides specialized judicial expertise but is constrained by limited enforcement capacity and government delays in implementing orders. Brazil's Ministério Público Federal and Estadual have independent prosecution authority for environmental crimes, creating accountability that other jurisdictions lack. South Africa's constitutional framework provides justiciable environmental rights with meaningful court enforcement. Nigeria and Indonesia face the most severe enforcement gaps, driven by regulatory capture (particularly in extractive industries), corruption, and institutional capacity constraints.

### Environmental Justice

All five regions display patterns of environmental racism/classism, with pollution disproportionately affecting marginalized communities: tribal/adivasi communities near mines and industrial zones in India; Niger Delta communities in Nigeria; Black and "coloured" communities in South Africa's coal belt (legacy of apartheid-era industrial siting); ribeirinhos (riverside communities) and quilombolas affected by mining in Brazil; and indigenous and poor communities near palm oil plantations and coal mines in Indonesia.
