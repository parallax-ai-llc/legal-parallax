## Summary

Gig economy regulation -- the legal framework governing the classification, rights, and protections of workers who provide services through digital platforms such as ride-hailing, food delivery, and freelance marketplaces -- represents one of the most rapidly evolving areas of labor law, as traditional binary employment classifications struggle to accommodate new forms of work organization. South Korea has addressed platform labor (플랫폼 노동) through incremental regulatory measures, including the 2020 amendment to the Industrial Accident Compensation Insurance Act (산업재해보상보험법) extending occupational accident coverage to delivery riders and other "special-type workers" (특수형태근로종사자), the 2021 Severe Accidents Punishment Act's application to platform companies with operational control, and ongoing legislative proposals for a comprehensive Platform Workers Protection Act (플랫폼 종사자 보호법); the deaths of multiple overworked delivery riders during the COVID-19 pandemic intensified calls for reform. The United States has seen the most contentious legal battles over gig worker classification, centered on California's Assembly Bill 5 (AB 5, 2019), which codified the ABC test from Dynamex Operations West v. Superior Court, 4 Cal. 5th 903 (2018), and was subsequently partially rolled back by Proposition 22 (2020), which exempted app-based ride-hailing and delivery drivers; the Department of Labor issued a final rule in January 2024 modifying the independent contractor test under the FLSA to adopt a totality-of-the-circumstances economic reality test. Germany has grappled with the classification of platform workers through its existing Arbeitnehmer (employee) versus Selbständiger (self-employed) framework, with the Bundesarbeitsgericht applying traditional subordination tests, while the Platform Workers Directive at the EU level, adopted as Directive (EU) 2024/2831 on December 14, 2024, establishes the first comprehensive European framework including a legal presumption of employment for platform workers, algorithmic management transparency requirements, and data protection safeguards, with a transposition deadline of December 2, 2026.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea has adopted an incremental approach to platform worker protection, extending specific protections to designated categories rather than enacting comprehensive platform labor legislation.

### Special-Type Workers (특수형태근로종사자)

Korean labor law has developed a distinct legal category -- "special-type workers" (특수형태근로종사자) -- for individuals who are economically dependent on a single client but do not meet the traditional subordination test for employee status. This category currently encompasses insurance solicitors, delivery drivers, learning material tutors, golf caddies, quick-service couriers, loan officers, credit card recruiters, home appliance installers, and freight vehicle owners.

### 2020 Reforms

Following a series of highly publicized deaths of overworked delivery riders during the COVID-19 pandemic -- including the death of a Coupang Eats rider in 2020 that became a symbol of platform worker exploitation -- the government enacted several protective measures:

**Industrial Accident Insurance Extension:** The Industrial Accident Compensation Insurance Act was amended to mandate occupational accident insurance coverage for platform-based delivery riders and designated special-type workers. Platform companies must enroll workers and pay insurance premiums.

**Employment Insurance Extension:** The Employment Insurance Act was similarly amended to extend unemployment insurance coverage to designated special-type workers, providing access to unemployment benefits and job training programs.

### Proposed Platform Workers Protection Act (플랫폼 종사자 보호법)

Multiple versions of comprehensive platform worker legislation have been introduced in the National Assembly:

| Bill Element | Key Provisions |
|-------------|---------------|
| Scope | All workers providing services through digital platforms |
| Written contract requirement | Platforms must provide written service agreements specifying compensation, working conditions |
| Algorithmic transparency | Platforms must disclose algorithmic criteria affecting work allocation, evaluation, and deactivation |
| Termination protection | Prohibition of arbitrary deactivation without notice and reason |
| Occupational safety | Platform company safety obligations for all platform workers |
| Collective representation | Right to form representative organizations and negotiate service conditions |

The bill has faced opposition from platform companies arguing it would increase costs and from some worker groups preferring the flexibility of independent contractor status. As of early 2026, the legislation remained under committee deliberation.

### Platform Workers' Organizing

Platform delivery riders have organized through the Riders Union (라이더유니온) affiliated with the KCTU, which has negotiated voluntary agreements with some platforms regarding working conditions, despite the workers' non-employee legal status limiting their formal collective bargaining rights.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The classification of gig workers has been the most legally contested employment law issue in the United States in the 2020s, with the outcome varying dramatically by jurisdiction and by which legal standard is applied.

### California -- AB 5 and Proposition 22

**Dynamex Operations West v. Superior Court (2018):** The California Supreme Court adopted the ABC test for determining employee status under the state wage orders: a worker is an employee unless the hiring entity proves (A) the worker is free from control and direction, (B) the worker performs work outside the usual course of the hiring entity's business, and (C) the worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade or business.

**AB 5 (2019):** Codified the ABC test as the default classification standard. The "B" prong proved particularly challenging for platform companies, as it is difficult to argue that a ride-hailing driver performs work "outside the usual course" of a ride-hailing company's business.

**Proposition 22 (2020):** A ballot initiative, funded by $200 million from Uber, Lyft, DoorDash, and Instacart, exempted app-based transportation and delivery network companies from AB 5. Prop 22 created a hybrid "independent contractor with benefits" model, guaranteeing earnings of at least 120 percent of the local minimum wage for engaged time, healthcare subsidies for drivers averaging 15+ hours per week, and occupational accident insurance. A California Superior Court declared Prop 22 unconstitutional in August 2021, but the California Court of Appeal reversed in March 2023, upholding the measure's validity.

### DOL Final Rule (January 2024)

The Department of Labor issued a final rule effective March 11, 2024, rescinding the Trump-era 2021 independent contractor rule and adopting a totality-of-the-circumstances "economic reality" test with six factors: opportunity for profit or loss depending on managerial skill, investments by the worker and potential employer, degree of permanence of the work relationship, nature and degree of control, extent to which the work is integral to the employer's business, and the worker's skill and initiative. The rule does not create a presumption of employment but adopts a worker-protective analytical framework.

### State Variations

| Approach | States | Key Feature |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| ABC test | California (via AB 5), Massachusetts, New Jersey | Broad presumption of employment |
| Economic reality test | Most federal circuits; DOL | Multi-factor, totality analysis |
| Entrepreneurial opportunity test | Some circuits (pre-2024) | Focus on economic independence |
| Third category legislation | Washington (2022 -- for TNC drivers), Connecticut (proposed) | Intermediate status with selected protections |

### Antitrust and Collective Bargaining

Independent contractors are generally prohibited from engaging in collective bargaining under federal antitrust law (Sherman Act), as collective price-setting by independent businesses constitutes per se illegal price-fixing. Some state and local governments have attempted to carve out exceptions: Seattle enacted an ordinance in 2015 allowing ride-hailing drivers to bargain collectively, though it faced legal challenges.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany addresses platform worker classification through its traditional binary framework of Arbeitnehmer (employee, § 611a BGB) versus Selbständiger (self-employed), with an intermediate category of arbeitnehmerähnliche Personen (employee-like persons) receiving some statutory protections.

### Classification Tests

**Arbeitnehmer (Employee, § 611a BGB):** A person who, on the basis of a private-law contract, is obligated to provide services for another in personal dependence. Personal dependence is characterized by the right to give instructions regarding content, execution, time, duration, and place of work. The BAG applies a comprehensive assessment (Gesamtbetrachtung) considering all circumstances.

**Arbeitnehmerähnliche Person:** An intermediate category for economically dependent self-employed persons who derive more than half their income from a single client and are comparable in social protection needs to employees. These persons receive certain statutory protections including annual leave (BUrlG), occupational safety provisions, and access to labor courts, but are not entitled to full employment protections.

### Platform-Specific Issues

The BAG has addressed platform worker classification in several decisions. In its December 1, 2020 decision (9 AZR 102/20), the BAG held that crowd workers who were free to accept or refuse individual tasks and had no minimum work obligation were not employees, as they lacked the personal dependence characteristic of an employment relationship. However, the Court noted that the assessment could differ where the platform's app creates de facto obligations through rating systems, algorithmic assignment, or economic dependency.

### Algorithmic Management and Works Councils

The Betriebsverfassungsgesetz (BetrVG) provides works councils with co-determination rights regarding the introduction and application of technical devices designed to monitor employee performance (§ 87(1)(6) BetrVG). This provision has been applied to algorithmic management systems in traditional employment settings but has limited applicability to platform work where workers are classified as self-employed.

### Crowdsourcing Agreements

The German Crowdsourcing Association (Deutscher Crowdsourcing Verband) developed a voluntary Code of Conduct for Paid Crowdsourcing in 2017, establishing minimum standards for task descriptions, payment, and communication. IG Metall launched the FairCrowdWork platform to rate and compare platform working conditions.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Platform Workers Directive (EU) 2024/2831

Adopted on December 14, 2024, after years of negotiation, the Platform Workers Directive is the first comprehensive EU legislative framework for platform work.

**Legal Presumption of Employment (Article 5):** The Directive establishes a rebuttable legal presumption of employment for platform workers. Member states must establish a legal presumption of employment when facts indicating control and direction are found, based on national law and collective agreements. The presumption applies in administrative and legal proceedings. The platform bears the burden of rebutting the presumption by proving the worker is genuinely self-employed.

**Algorithmic Management (Articles 7-11):** The Directive requires platforms to inform workers about automated monitoring and decision-making systems, ensure human review of significant automated decisions (deactivation, suspension, material restriction of account), provide transparency on how algorithms affect working conditions, earnings, and task allocation, and prohibit the processing of certain categories of personal data (emotional state, private conversations, biometric data for identification purposes).

**Data Protection (Article 12):** Platforms must comply with GDPR requirements and provide workers with access to data concerning their work, including ratings, task history, and performance evaluations.

**Transposition Deadline:** December 2, 2026.

### European Commission Guidelines on Collective Agreements (2022)

The Commission adopted Guidelines on December 29, 2022, clarifying that EU competition law does not prevent collective bargaining by solo self-employed persons who are in a comparable situation to workers. This addresses the antitrust barrier that had prevented platform workers classified as self-employed from negotiating collectively.

### CJEU

**Uber BV v. Aslam (UK Supreme Court, 2021):** While technically a UK case decided post-Brexit, the UK Supreme Court's unanimous decision that Uber drivers are "workers" entitled to minimum wage, paid holidays, and other protections has been influential across Europe.

**B v. Yodel Delivery Network (Case C-692/19, 2020):** The CJEU provided guidance on the distinction between workers and self-employed persons under Directive 2003/88/EC (Working Time), holding that a delivery courier who could use substitutes, decline tasks, and work for competitors was not necessarily a worker, while emphasizing that national courts must assess the reality of the relationship beyond contractual terms.

### European Social Charter

The European Committee of Social Rights has addressed platform work through its interpretation of Article 1 (right to work) and Article 4 (right to fair remuneration), finding that states must ensure that platform workers have access to fair working conditions regardless of their classification status.

## Comparative Analysis

### Classification Approaches

The jurisdictions represent three approaches: binary classification (Germany, United States -- traditional), third category (South Korea's special-type workers, Washington State's TNC driver status), and presumption-based (EU Directive, California's ABC test). The EU Directive's rebuttable presumption of employment represents the most significant regulatory innovation, shifting the burden of proof from workers to platforms.

### Social Insurance

South Korea has been most proactive in extending social insurance to platform workers regardless of classification, covering delivery riders under industrial accident and employment insurance since 2020. Germany and the United States generally condition social insurance on employee status, leaving self-employed platform workers to arrange their own coverage. The EU Directive's employment presumption would extend social insurance coverage to a much larger share of platform workers.

### Algorithmic Transparency

The EU Directive establishes the most comprehensive algorithmic transparency requirements. South Korea's proposed legislation includes similar provisions but has not been enacted. The United States has no federal algorithmic transparency requirements for platform work. Germany's existing works council rights provide a framework for algorithmic oversight but are inapplicable to non-employees.

### Collective Action

The EU Commission's 2022 Guidelines resolving the antitrust barrier for solo self-employed collective bargaining represent the most significant development, potentially enabling millions of platform workers to negotiate collectively regardless of classification. This contrasts sharply with the U.S. approach, where antitrust law continues to restrict independent contractor collective action.
