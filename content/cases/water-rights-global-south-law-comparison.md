## Summary

Water law in the Global South operates at the nexus of human rights, environmental sustainability, economic development, and sovereignty, reflecting distinctive challenges of scarcity, unequal access, colonial-era infrastructure, and rapid urbanization that distinguish these legal systems from the riparian and prior appropriation doctrines of the Global North. China's water governance framework, anchored in the Water Law (水法, 2002 revision) and the Water Pollution Prevention and Control Law (水污染防治法, 2017 revision), declares all water resources state-owned (Article 3, Water Law: "Water resources belong to the state") and administers them through the Ministry of Water Resources and seven major river basin commissions, including the Yellow River Conservancy Commission and the Yangtze River Water Resources Commission; the South-North Water Transfer Project (南水北调工程), the largest water diversion infrastructure in human history, transfers approximately 10 billion cubic meters annually from the water-abundant Yangtze River basin to the water-scarce North China Plain through three routes (Eastern, Central, and planned Western), at a total investment exceeding $80 billion -- raising fundamental questions about inter-basin equity, environmental externalities, and the limits of engineering solutions to water scarcity. India's water law is characterized by a constitutional tension between the Centre's power over inter-state rivers (Entry 56, Union List) and state sovereignty over water (Entry 17, State List), producing a fragmented landscape where the Inter-State River Water Disputes Act 1956, the colonial easements and riparian rights under the Indian Easements Act 1882, and the emerging "public trust doctrine" articulated by the Supreme Court in M.C. Mehta v. Kamal Nath (1997) 1 SCC 388 coexist uneasily; India's groundwater crisis -- with the country extracting more groundwater than any other nation, approximately 250 billion cubic meters annually -- has prompted efforts to move from an English common law-based riparian/landowner model to a regulated permit system under the Model Groundwater (Sustainable Management) Bill 2017, though only a few states have adopted it. South Africa's National Water Act 36 of 1998 (NWA) is widely regarded as the most transformative post-apartheid water legislation, abolishing the colonial riparian rights system, declaring all water a national resource owned by the people with the National Government acting as trustee (section 3), establishing a hierarchical water allocation system that prioritizes the "Reserve" (section 16: a non-negotiable allocation for basic human needs and ecological sustainability before any other uses), and creating a compulsory licensing system to redistribute water from historical over-allocations to previously disadvantaged communities. Brazil's National Water Resources Policy (Lei No. 9.433/1997, the "Water Law") established water as a public good (domínio público), created the National Water Resources Management System including River Basin Committees (Comitês de Bacia Hidrográfica) with civil society participation, and introduced water use charges (cobrança pelo uso da água) -- a pioneering user-pays mechanism; the National Water and Sanitation Agency (ANA, established by Lei No. 9.984/2000) regulates federal water bodies, while the 2020 Legal Framework for Basic Sanitation (Lei No. 14.026/2020) mandated private sector participation and universal coverage targets. The Philippines' Water Code (Presidential Decree No. 1067, 1976) declares all waters belonging to the state (Regalian doctrine), requires water permits for appropriation, and establishes priority uses (domestic, municipal, irrigation, power, fisheries, industry), administered by the National Water Resources Board (NWRB); Indonesia's water governance was fundamentally reshaped when the Constitutional Court in Decision No. 85/PUU-XI/2013 struck down the Water Resources Law No. 7 of 2004 as unconstitutional for excessively enabling water privatization, reverting to the 1974 Water Resources Law and prompting enactment of the new Water Resources Law No. 17 of 2019, which reinforces state control (hak penguasaan negara atas air) while providing for community-based water management (pengelolaan sumber daya air berbasis masyarakat).

## China

### State Ownership and the Water Law

The Water Law of the People's Republic of China (中华人民共和国水法), originally enacted in 1988 and comprehensively revised in 2002, establishes the legal framework:

**Article 2:** Water resources include surface water and groundwater.

**Article 3:** Water resources belong to the state (国家所有). The State Council exercises the right of ownership over water resources on behalf of the state. Rural collective economic organizations may use and manage ponds and reservoirs built by collective investment within their territories.

**Article 4:** The state implements a system of water resources development, utilization, conservation, and protection that is adapted to economic and social development. Priority shall be given to domestic water use (城乡居民生活用水), taking proper account of agricultural, industrial, ecological, and navigational needs.

**Article 12:** The state applies a river basin management and administrative regional management system. The Ministry of Water Resources is responsible for unified management and supervision at the national level. Seven river basin management institutions (流域管理机构) -- including the Yellow River Conservancy Commission, the Yangtze River Water Resources Commission, the Huai River Commission, and others -- exercise management functions delegated by the Ministry.

**Water Abstraction Permits (取水许可):** The Regulations on Water Abstraction Permits and the Collection of Water Resource Fees (取水许可和水资源费征收管理条例, State Council Decree No. 460, 2006) require any entity or individual abstracting water directly from rivers, lakes, or groundwater to obtain a water abstraction permit and pay water resource fees. Exemptions include: small-quantity domestic use, livestock drinking, and emergency abstraction.

**"Three Red Lines" (三条红线):** In 2011, the State Council established the "Strictest Water Resources Management System" (最严格水资源管理制度) with three red lines:
1. **Total quantity control (用水总量控制):** National water use capped at 700 billion cubic meters by 2030
2. **Water use efficiency (用水效率控制):** Industrial water use per unit of value-added must decrease; irrigation water effective utilization coefficient must increase
3. **Pollution limits (水功能区限制纳污):** Pollutant discharge into water bodies must stay within the capacity of designated water functional zones

### The South-North Water Transfer Project (南水北调工程)

The South-North Water Transfer Project, conceived by Mao Zedong in 1952 and formally approved by the State Council in 2002, is the largest water infrastructure project in human history:

**Eastern Route (东线):** Utilizes the ancient Grand Canal infrastructure, pumping water from the Yangtze River near Yangzhou northward through Jiangsu, Shandong, and Hebei to Tianjin. Operational since 2013. Capacity: approximately 14.8 billion cubic meters annually. Cost: approximately RMB 65 billion.

**Central Route (中线):** Channels water from the Danjiangkou Reservoir on the Han River (a Yangtze tributary) in Hubei Province northward through a purpose-built canal across Henan and Hebei to Beijing and Tianjin. Operational since 2014. Capacity: approximately 13 billion cubic meters annually. Required the displacement of approximately 345,000 residents from the reservoir area. Cost: approximately RMB 220 billion.

**Western Route (西线):** Planned to transfer water from the upper reaches of the Yangtze (Tongtian River, Yalong River, Dadu River) across the Bayankala Mountains to the upper Yellow River. Remains in the planning and research phase due to: extreme engineering challenges (Tibetan Plateau terrain), massive costs (estimated RMB 300+ billion), environmental concerns, and the sensitivity of diverting water from the Tibetan Plateau.

**Legal and Policy Issues:**
- **Inter-basin equity:** The project transfers water from the relatively water-abundant south (though Hubei and Henan source provinces also face scarcity challenges) to the politically powerful but water-scarce north (Beijing, Tianjin, Hebei). The source provinces receive compensation payments, but the adequacy of these payments and the environmental impacts on source regions remain contested.
- **Resettlement:** The Central Route required the largest resettlement in Chinese water project history after the Three Gorges Dam. The Danjiangkou Reservoir Resettlement Regulations (丹江口水库移民条例) govern compensation, relocation, and livelihood restoration.
- **Water pricing:** Water transferred through the project is significantly more expensive than local water sources, raising issues of willingness to pay and the equitable distribution of costs among beneficiary cities and users.

### Groundwater Crisis

China faces severe groundwater depletion, particularly in the North China Plain, where decades of over-extraction have created a massive groundwater "cone of depression." The Water Law (Article 36) mandates planned extraction and replenishment, and the 2021 Groundwater Management Regulations (地下水管理条例, State Council Decree No. 748) established comprehensive regulation including: groundwater level control indicators, extraction permit requirements, prohibition of new wells in over-exploited areas, and well-sealing requirements. By 2025, the Ministry of Water Resources reported that groundwater levels in the North China Plain had stabilized or risen in some areas, attributed to extraction controls and the South-North Water Transfer supplement.

## India

### Constitutional Framework and Inter-State Disputes

Water governance in India is shaped by the constitutional distribution of powers:

**Entry 17 (State List):** "Water, that is to say, water supplies, irrigation and canals, drainage and embankments, water storage and water power subject to the provisions of Entry 56 of List I."

**Entry 56 (Union List):** "Regulation and development of inter-State rivers and river valleys to the extent to which such regulation and development under the control of the Union is declared by Parliament by law to be expedient in the public interest."

This creates a framework where states claim sovereignty over water within their territories, while the Centre asserts authority over inter-state rivers -- producing some of India's most intractable political conflicts.

**Inter-State River Water Disputes Act, 1956:** Provides for the constitution of ad hoc tribunals to adjudicate inter-state water disputes when negotiations fail. Major tribunals include:

- **Cauvery Water Disputes Tribunal (1990-2007, modified by Supreme Court 2018):** Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, Kerala, and Puducherry disputed allocation of the Cauvery River. The Tribunal's 2007 award allocated 419 TMC to Tamil Nadu, 270 TMC to Karnataka, 30 TMC to Kerala, and 7 TMC to Puducherry. The Supreme Court in **State of Karnataka v. State of Tamil Nadu (2018) 4 SCC 1** modified the allocation, increasing Karnataka's share to 284.75 TMC -- a politically explosive decision that triggered protests in Karnataka.
- **Krishna Water Disputes Tribunal II (2004-present):** Maharashtra, Karnataka, and Andhra Pradesh (now also Telangana) continue to dispute Krishna River waters.
- **Ravi and Beas Waters Tribunal (1986-present):** Punjab and Haryana dispute, complicated by Punjab's 2004 unilateral legislative abrogation of water-sharing agreements.

**The Inter-State River Water Disputes (Amendment) Bill, 2019:** Passed by Lok Sabha, proposes replacing ad hoc tribunals with a single permanent tribunal with multiple benches, to reduce delays (some disputes have lasted decades). The bill also establishes a Disputes Resolution Committee as a pre-litigation mechanism.

### Groundwater: From Riparian to Regulatory

Under the Indian Easements Act 1882 (Section 7, Illustration (g)), a landowner has the right to extract groundwater beneath their land without limitation, subject to the principle that they must not cause malicious damage to neighbors. This common-law riparian approach has been disastrous given India's groundwater dependence:

- India extracts approximately 250 billion cubic meters of groundwater annually -- more than the US and China combined.
- The Central Ground Water Authority (CGWA, established 1997) regulates groundwater extraction in over-exploited and critical areas through a "No Objection Certificate" requirement.
- The **Model Groundwater (Sustainable Management) Bill, 2017** (developed by the Ministry of Water Resources) proposes shifting from a landowner-rights model to a public trust model: groundwater as a common pool resource, extraction subject to permits, and a Groundwater Protection Authority in each state. As of 2026, only a handful of states have adopted versions of the model bill.

### The Right to Water

The Supreme Court has progressively recognized a right to clean water as part of the Right to Life under Article 21:

- **Subhash Kumar v. State of Bihar (1991) 1 SCC 598:** "Right to life includes the right to enjoy pollution-free water."
- **M.C. Mehta v. Kamal Nath (1997) 1 SCC 388:** Introduced the "public trust doctrine" into Indian environmental law, holding that the state holds natural resources (including water) in trust for the public and cannot transfer them for private commercial exploitation.
- **Narmada Bachao Andolan v. Union of India (2000) 10 SCC 664:** While upholding the Sardar Sarovar Dam, the Court emphasized the obligation to ensure water supply to affected communities.

The Jal Jeevan Mission (JJM), launched in 2019, aims to provide functional household tap connections to all rural households by 2024 (extended). As of 2025, over 75% of rural households have been connected, though water quality and supply regularity remain challenges.

## Africa

### Nigeria: Water Resources Act and Access Crisis

The **Water Resources Act, 1993** (Cap W2, LFN 2004) vests the right to use and control all surface water and groundwater in the Federal Government (section 1). No person may divert, abstract, or use water without a license from the Federal Ministry of Water Resources (section 3).

In practice, the Act is poorly enforced, and water access remains a crisis:
- Approximately 40% of Nigerians lack access to basic water services (UNICEF/WHO JMP, 2023).
- The distinction between federal waters (inter-state rivers, boundary waters) and state/local waters is unclear in implementation.
- The National Water Resources Bill (reintroduced repeatedly, most recently 2022) has been controversial, with critics (particularly in the Niger Delta and Middle Belt) alleging it would centralize water control and undermine community water rights.

**Section 20 of the 1999 Constitution** (Fundamental Objectives and Directive Principles) directs the state to "protect and improve the environment and safeguard the water, air and land, forest and wildlife of Nigeria" -- but as a Chapter II directive principle, it is not justiciable (section 6(6)(c)).

The lack of a constitutional right to water, combined with infrastructure deficiencies and regulatory gaps, leaves Nigeria heavily dependent on: boreholes (often privately drilled), water vendors, and community water supply systems. UNICEF and the World Bank have supported the National Urban Water Sector Reform Programme, but progress has been uneven.

### South Africa: The National Water Act and the Reserve

South Africa's **National Water Act 36 of 1998** (NWA), enacted to replace the racially discriminatory water allocation system inherited from the apartheid-era Water Act 54 of 1956, is one of the most progressive water laws globally:

**Section 3:** "The National Government, acting through the Minister, must ensure that water is protected, used, developed, conserved, managed, and controlled in a sustainable and equitable manner, for the benefit of all persons."

**Section 3(1):** The national government is the public trustee of the nation's water resources.

**The Reserve (Section 16-18):** The Reserve is the foundational concept of the NWA. It consists of two components:
1. **Basic Human Needs Reserve:** The quantity and quality of water required to meet basic human needs as prescribed under section 27(1)(b) of the Constitution ("everyone has the right to have access to sufficient food and water"). Set at 25 liters per person per day under the Regulations Relating to Compulsory National Standards (2001).
2. **Ecological Reserve:** The quantity and quality of water required to protect aquatic ecosystems to secure ecologically sustainable development and use of the resource.

The Reserve has the highest priority in water allocation -- it must be met before any other water uses are authorized.

**Compulsory Licensing (Section 43-48):** Where existing water use allocations are inequitable (reflecting apartheid-era concentrations), the Minister may require compulsory licensing in a catchment area. All users must apply for new licenses, and the responsible authority may reallocate water to redress past inequities. This mechanism has been implemented in only a few catchments due to political and administrative complexity.

**Free Basic Water Policy (2001):** The government provides 6,000 liters per household per month (equivalent to 25 liters per person per day for a household of eight) free of charge. This policy implements the constitutional right to water and the NWA's basic human needs Reserve. As of 2025, approximately 88% of households have access to basic water supply.

**Water Services Act 108 of 1997:** Provides the regulatory framework for water services (supply and sanitation), establishing the role of water services authorities (municipalities) and water services providers.

**Mazibuko v. City of Johannesburg [2009] ZACC 28:** The Constitutional Court held that the City's Free Basic Water Policy of 6 kiloliters per month per household was reasonable and consistent with section 27 of the Constitution, declining to set a higher minimum. The Court emphasized progressive realization and the executive's latitude in determining the quantity of free water, while the applicants (Phiri residents in Soweto) had argued for 50 liters per person per day.

## Latin America

### Brazil: The Water Law and Participatory Governance

Brazil's **National Water Resources Policy** (Política Nacional de Recursos Hídricos, Lei No. 9.433/1997), known as the "Water Law" (Lei das Águas), established a modern water governance framework:

**Foundational Principles (Article 1):**
1. Water is a public good (bem de domínio público)
2. Water is a limited natural resource with economic value
3. In conditions of scarcity, priority use is for human consumption and animal watering
4. Water resources management must promote multiple uses
5. The river basin is the territorial unit for water resources management
6. Management must be decentralized with public participation

**Water Use Grants (Outorga de Direito de Uso, Articles 11-18):** The state grants the right to use water resources through an administrative instrument (outorga). The grant does not transfer water ownership (which remains public) but authorizes use for a specified period (maximum 35 years, renewable). The responsible authority (ANA for federal rivers; state agencies for state rivers) may suspend grants in emergencies, including drought.

**Water Charges (Cobrança pelo Uso da Água, Articles 19-22):** Users who abstract, consume, or discharge effluents into water bodies are subject to charges. Revenue is earmarked for water resource management investments in the respective basin. The Paraíba do Sul basin (2003) and the São Francisco basin (2010) were among the first to implement water charges. The mechanism has been praised as a pioneering application of the polluter-pays and user-pays principles in the Global South.

**River Basin Committees (Comitês de Bacia Hidrográfica, Articles 37-40):** "Parliaments of water" -- deliberative bodies composed of representatives from: government (federal, state, municipal), water users, and civil society organizations. Functions include: approving the basin water resources plan, arbitrating conflicts, establishing mechanisms and values for water charges, and proposing measures. As of 2025, over 240 river basin committees operate at federal and state levels.

### The São Francisco River Transfer (Transposição do Rio São Francisco)

Brazil's equivalent of the South-North Water Transfer, the São Francisco River Integration Project (Projeto de Integração do Rio São Francisco, PISF) diverts water from the São Francisco River in Pernambuco to the semi-arid Northeast (Nordeste) through two channels (Northern Axis and Eastern Axis), covering approximately 700 kilometers.

- Construction began in 2007; the Eastern Axis was inaugurated in 2017 and the Northern Axis in stages through 2024.
- Total investment: approximately R$ 12 billion (approximately $2.4 billion).
- Designed to serve 12 million people in 390 municipalities across four states (Pernambuco, Paraíba, Rio Grande do Norte, Ceará).
- Criticized by environmental groups and communities along the São Francisco (particularly indigenous and ribeirinho communities) who argue the project diverts water from a river already under stress, with the São Francisco's flow having decreased significantly due to deforestation and upstream water use.

### Sanitation Reform (Lei No. 14.026/2020)

The New Legal Framework for Basic Sanitation updated Lei No. 11.445/2007, establishing:
- Universal coverage targets: 99% water supply and 90% sewage collection by December 31, 2033
- Regional blocks for water and sanitation services to achieve economies of scale
- Mandatory competitive bidding for concession contracts (ending direct contracting to state water companies)
- ANA as the national regulatory reference entity
The reform was controversial for its emphasis on private sector participation; critics argued it prioritized profitability over access for low-income communities.

### Colombia: Constitutional Right to Water

The Colombian Constitutional Court has been among the most progressive globally in recognizing water rights. In **Sentencia T-740/2011**, the Court recognized the fundamental right to drinking water (derecho fundamental al agua potable) as connected to the rights to life (Article 11), health (Article 49), and human dignity (Article 1). In **Sentencia T-622/2016**, the Court declared the Atrato River a subject of rights (sujeto de derechos), ordering government protection and appointing legal guardians for the river -- an expression of the "rights of nature" doctrine.

## Southeast Asia

### Indonesia: Constitutional Court and Water Privatization

Indonesia's water governance was dramatically shaped by the Constitutional Court's **Decision No. 85/PUU-XI/2013**, which struck down the Water Resources Law No. 7 of 2004:

**Background:** Law 7/2004 replaced the 1974 Water Law (UU No. 11/1974) and was perceived as facilitating water privatization by: granting commercial water use rights (hak guna air), enabling water concessions to private companies, and reducing the state's direct role in water provision. Civil society organizations (including KRUHA, the People's Coalition for the Right to Water) challenged the law as violating the constitutional principle that water is controlled by the state for the people's welfare (Article 33(3), Constitution: "The land, the waters, and the natural resources within shall be controlled by the state and used for the greatest benefit of the people").

**Constitutional Court Ruling:** The Court held that Law 7/2004 violated Article 33(3) because it:
1. Did not adequately prioritize the state's obligation to fulfill citizens' right to water
2. Enabled excessive commercialization and privatization
3. Failed to ensure that the state retained control over water resources
4. Did not adequately protect the priority of community and small-scale use over commercial exploitation

The Court reverted water governance to the 1974 law pending new legislation.

**Water Resources Law No. 17 of 2019:** Enacted to replace the struck-down law, this statute:
- **Article 6:** Water resources are controlled by the state and used for the greatest benefit and welfare of the people (restating Article 33(3)).
- **Article 8:** The right to water (hak atas air) is guaranteed to every person for daily basic needs (kebutuhan pokok sehari-hari).
- **Article 46-53:** Water use permits (izin penggunaan air) are required for commercial and large-scale use. The permit hierarchy prioritizes: daily basic needs, smallholder agriculture, and environmental flows, before commercial and industrial uses.
- **Article 62-69:** Community-based water management (pengelolaan sumber daya air berbasis masyarakat) is recognized and supported. Customary (adat) water management systems are protected insofar as they do not conflict with national interests.

**Jakarta Water Concessions:** The privatization of Jakarta's water supply in 1997 (two 25-year concessions to Suez/Ondeo and Thames Water/Aetra) became a flashpoint. The Central Jakarta District Court in 2015 ruled the privatization unlawful (later overturned on appeal). The concessions expired in 2022-2023, and the Jakarta provincial government elected not to renew, bringing water services back under public operation through PAM Jaya.

### Philippines: Water Code and the Manila Water Crisis

**Presidential Decree No. 1067 (Water Code of the Philippines, 1976):** Declares all waters belonging to the state (Article 3, implementing the Regalian doctrine) and establishes a permit system for water appropriation:

**Article 13-14:** Water may be appropriated for: domestic and municipal use, irrigation, power generation, fisheries, livestock, industrial, and other purposes. In times of shortage, the order of preference is: domestic, municipal, irrigation, power, fisheries, livestock, industrial, and other uses.

**Article 15-26:** Water permits issued by the NWRB authorize the appropriation of specified quantities for specified purposes. Permits are revocable for non-use, wastage, or public interest. Existing lawful water use is protected through "vested rights" recognized under the Code.

**The Manila Water Concession:** In 1997, the Metropolitan Waterworks and Sewerage System (MWSS) was privatized through two concessions: Manila Water Company (east zone) and Maynilad Water Services (west zone). The concessions were initially hailed as models but became controversial:
- Maynilad (originally Lopez Group/Ondeo) nearly failed financially in 2003; it was taken over by the MWSS administrator and eventually re-bid to the Consunji/DMCI group.
- Manila Water Company achieved significant coverage expansion (from 26% to over 94% in the east zone) but faced criticism for rising tariffs.
- In 2019, a concession dispute led President Duterte to threaten cancellation; the concessions were eventually renegotiated and new 25-year agreements signed in 2022 (Maynilad) and 2024 (Manila Water) under revised terms.
- The Supreme Court in **Metropolitan Waterworks v. Act Theater (G.R. No. 174052, 2009)** addressed the interplay between MWSS franchise rights and the NWRB's regulatory authority.

**Clean Water Act (Republic Act No. 9275, 2004):** Establishes the framework for water quality management, including: water quality standards, wastewater discharge permits, designation of water quality management areas, and the "polluter-pays" principle. The DENR (Department of Environment and Natural Resources) administers the Act through the Environmental Management Bureau.

**Groundwater:** The National Integrated Water Resources Management Plan (2020) identifies groundwater depletion in Metro Manila and Central Luzon as critical. The NWRB requires well permits for groundwater extraction, but enforcement is weak -- an estimated 50% of wells in Metro Manila are unregistered.

## Comparative Analysis

### State Ownership vs. Public Trust

All Global South jurisdictions examined assert some form of state or public control over water, but the legal mechanisms differ:
- **China:** Direct state ownership (水资源属于国家所有)
- **South Africa:** Public trust doctrine (state as trustee for the people)
- **Brazil:** Public domain (domínio público) with participatory governance
- **Indonesia:** State control (hak penguasaan negara) interpreted as precluding privatization
- **India:** Evolving from riparian/landowner rights toward public trust
- **Philippines:** Regalian doctrine (all waters belong to the state)

The practical implications vary significantly: China's state ownership enables massive infrastructure projects (South-North Water Transfer) through centralized decision-making, while Brazil's public domain/participatory model empowers river basin committees to influence allocation decisions.

### The Right to Water

South Africa is the only jurisdiction with an explicit constitutional right to water (section 27(1)(b)), implemented through the Free Basic Water Policy and the NWA's Reserve. India has judicially derived a right to water from the right to life (Article 21). Colombia's Constitutional Court has recognized a fundamental right to drinking water through progressive interpretation. Indonesia's Constitutional Court established that water provision is a state obligation. Brazil and China rely on policy commitments rather than constitutional rights. The Philippines has no explicit right to water.

The South African model of the "Reserve" -- guaranteeing basic human and ecological needs before any other allocation -- represents the most innovative legal mechanism, though its implementation through compulsory licensing has been slow.

### Privatization Debates

Indonesia's Constitutional Court decision striking down water privatization and Brazil's contested sanitation reform represent opposite poles: Indonesia moved toward re-municipalization (Jakarta water), while Brazil moved toward mandatory private concessions. South Africa's Free Basic Water Policy operates within a framework that permits private participation but guarantees a minimum free allocation. The Philippines' Manila water concession represents a middle ground -- private operation under public regulatory oversight -- with mixed results.

### Mega-Infrastructure and Inter-Basin Transfer

China's South-North Water Transfer and Brazil's São Francisco Transposition are the two largest inter-basin water transfers in the Global South. Both raise similar issues: displacement of communities, environmental impacts on source basins, equity between donor and recipient regions, and the question of whether supply-side engineering can substitute for demand-side management. India's proposed National River Linking Project would dwarf both projects but faces even greater political, environmental, and legal obstacles.
