## Summary

Deposit insurance -- the guarantee system that protects bank depositors against the loss of their deposits in the event of bank failure -- is a foundational element of modern financial safety nets, designed to maintain public confidence in the banking system and prevent destabilizing bank runs. South Korea's deposit insurance system is administered by the Korea Deposit Insurance Corporation (예금보험공사, KDIC) under the Depositor Protection Act (예금자보호법) of 1995, which provides coverage of up to 50 million KRW (approximately $38,000) per depositor per financial institution, a limit that has remained unchanged since 2001 despite periodic calls for increase. The United States operates the world's oldest permanent deposit insurance system through the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC), established by the Banking Act of 1933 (Glass-Steagall Act) and governed by the Federal Deposit Insurance Act (12 U.S.C. §§ 1811-1835a), currently providing coverage of $250,000 per depositor per insured institution per ownership category, a level set by the Dodd-Frank Act of 2010. Germany participates in the EU's harmonized deposit guarantee framework under the Deposit Guarantee Schemes Directive 2014/49/EU, implemented through the Einlagensicherungsgesetz (EinSiG) of 2015, with statutory coverage of €100,000 per depositor per institution, supplemented by the private Einlagensicherungsfonds of the Bundesverband deutscher Banken (BdB) providing voluntary additional protection historically exceeding several million euros per depositor. At the EU level, the Deposit Guarantee Schemes Directive 2014/49/EU establishes minimum harmonization at €100,000 coverage with a target payout period of 7 working days, while the long-debated European Deposit Insurance Scheme (EDIS) -- the proposed third pillar of the Banking Union -- remains under negotiation as of 2026, with the European Commission's revised proposal of April 2023 introducing a hybrid model combining national and European elements.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea established its deposit insurance system through the Depositor Protection Act (예금자보호법), enacted on December 29, 1995, and operational from January 1, 1996. The Korea Deposit Insurance Corporation (예금보험공사, KDIC) was established as the administering body.

**Coverage Scope:** KDIC provides uniquely broad coverage compared to other national deposit insurance systems, protecting deposits across five financial sectors:

| Sector | Covered Products | Coverage Limit |
|--------|-----------------|----------------|
| Banks (은행) | Demand deposits, time deposits, CDs, trust accounts | 50 million KRW per depositor |
| Securities firms (증권회사) | Customer deposits, CMA accounts | 50 million KRW per depositor |
| Insurance companies (보험회사) | Insurance policy reserves, guarantee reserves | 50 million KRW per policyholder |
| Mutual savings banks (상호저축은행) | Deposits | 50 million KRW per depositor |
| Credit unions (신용협동조합) | Deposits (via separate credit union deposit insurance) | 50 million KRW per depositor |

**Coverage Limit:** The 50 million KRW limit was established in 2001, reduced from full coverage (blanket guarantee) that had been in effect during the 1997-2001 Asian Financial Crisis period. Despite significant inflation since 2001, the coverage limit has not been adjusted, leading to debates about its adequacy. As of 2025, the 50 million KRW limit covers approximately 93 percent of deposit accounts by number but only about 45 percent by value.

### KDIC Functions

KDIC performs several roles beyond deposit payouts:

| Function | Legal Basis |
|----------|-------------|
| Deposit insurance fund management | Depositor Protection Act Articles 24-30 |
| Failed institution resolution | Depositor Protection Act Articles 36-38 |
| Financial institution examination | Depositor Protection Act Article 21 |
| Recovery of public funds | Special Act on Public Fund Recovery |
| Moral hazard prevention | Depositor Protection Act Articles 21-2 |

### Resolution Framework

KDIC serves as both deposit insurer and resolution authority. Resolution methods include:
- **Deposit payout (예금대지급):** Direct payment to depositors up to the coverage limit
- **Purchase and assumption (P&A, 자산부채이전):** Transfer of deposits and assets to an acquiring institution
- **Bridge institution (정리금융기관):** Temporary institution to maintain critical functions
- **Open bank assistance (자금지원):** Financial support to keep a failing bank operating

### Asian Financial Crisis Legacy

During the 1997-1998 crisis, KDIC disbursed approximately 156 trillion KRW in public funds to stabilize the financial system, covering 787 financial institutions. The crisis fundamentally shaped Korea's deposit insurance framework, leading to:
- Transition from blanket guarantee to limited coverage (2001)
- Risk-based premium system (2003)
- Enhanced early intervention authority (Prompt Corrective Action framework)
- KDIC's expanded role as resolution authority

## United States

### Legal Framework

The FDIC, established by the Banking Act of 1933 (Glass-Steagall Act) and signed into law by President Franklin D. Roosevelt on June 16, 1933, was created in direct response to the bank failures of the Great Depression, during which approximately 9,000 banks failed between 1930 and 1933.

**Federal Deposit Insurance Act (12 U.S.C. §§ 1811-1835a):** The comprehensive statutory framework governing the FDIC's operations, powers, and obligations.

**Coverage Structure:** The United States uniquely applies coverage limits per ownership category, significantly expanding effective protection:

| Ownership Category | Coverage Limit | Example |
|-------------------|---------------|---------|
| Single accounts | $250,000 per depositor | Individual checking/savings |
| Joint accounts | $250,000 per co-owner | Joint checking/savings |
| Revocable trust accounts | $250,000 per beneficiary (up to 5) | POD/ITF accounts |
| Retirement accounts (IRA, etc.) | $250,000 per depositor | IRA CDs |
| Corporation/partnership accounts | $250,000 per entity | Business operating accounts |
| Government accounts | $250,000 per official custodian | Municipal deposits |

This ownership category structure means that a married couple can have up to $1 million in FDIC-insured deposits at a single institution ($250,000 each in single accounts, $500,000 in a joint account).

**Coverage Limit History:**

| Year | Coverage Limit | Legislative Basis |
|------|---------------|-------------------|
| 1934 | $2,500 | Banking Act of 1933 |
| 1935 | $5,000 | Banking Act of 1935 |
| 1950 | $10,000 | Federal Deposit Insurance Act of 1950 |
| 1966 | $15,000 | Financial Institutions Supervisory Act |
| 1969 | $20,000 | P.L. 91-151 |
| 1974 | $40,000 | P.L. 93-495 |
| 1980 | $100,000 | DIDMCA |
| 2008 | $250,000 (temporary) | Emergency Economic Stabilization Act |
| 2010 | $250,000 (permanent) | Dodd-Frank Act Section 335 |

### Deposit Insurance Fund

The Deposit Insurance Fund (DIF) is funded through risk-based assessments on insured institutions (12 U.S.C. § 1817). The Dodd-Frank Act established a minimum Designated Reserve Ratio (DRR) of 1.35 percent and a long-term target of 2 percent. Following the 2023 bank failures (Silicon Valley Bank, Signature Bank, First Republic Bank), the DIF balance declined to $116.1 billion, prompting the FDIC to impose a special assessment of approximately $16.3 billion on institutions with over $5 billion in uninsured deposits.

### 2023 Banking Crisis

The failures of Silicon Valley Bank (March 10, 2023), Signature Bank (March 12, 2023), and First Republic Bank (May 1, 2023) tested the deposit insurance framework:
- SVB had approximately $175 billion in deposits, of which approximately 94 percent were uninsured
- The FDIC, Federal Reserve, and Treasury invoked the "systemic risk exception" under 12 U.S.C. § 1823(c)(4)(G) to protect all depositors, including uninsured depositors
- This action prompted congressional debate about raising the FDIC coverage limit, with proposals ranging from $2 million (for business accounts) to unlimited coverage

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's deposit insurance system comprises two layers: a mandatory statutory scheme and voluntary supplementary private schemes.

**Statutory Scheme -- Einlagensicherungsgesetz (EinSiG, 2015):** Transposing the EU DGS Directive 2014/49/EU, the EinSiG establishes:
- Coverage of €100,000 per depositor per CRR credit institution
- Temporary high balance protection of €500,000 for up to 6 months for certain life events (property sale, divorce, retirement, insurance payout, injury compensation) (§ 8 EinSiG)
- Payout within 7 working days of a compensation event (§ 14 EinSiG)
- Risk-based contributions from member institutions (§ 18 EinSiG)
- Target fund level of 0.8 percent of covered deposits by July 3, 2024 (§ 17 EinSiG)

**Statutory Deposit Guarantee Schemes:**

| Scheme | Sector | Legal Form |
|--------|--------|------------|
| Entschädigungseinrichtung deutscher Banken (EdB) | Private commercial banks | Statutory scheme under EinSiG |
| Sparkassenstützungsfonds | Savings banks (Sparkassen) | Institutional protection scheme |
| Sicherungseinrichtung des BVR | Cooperative banks | Institutional protection scheme |

**Voluntary Supplementary Protection -- Einlagensicherungsfonds:** The Bundesverband deutscher Banken (BdB, Association of German Banks) operates a voluntary deposit protection fund that historically provided coverage of up to 20 percent of a bank's liable capital per depositor -- often amounting to several million euros. Following the 2008 financial crisis and the Greensill Bank failure in 2021, the BdB reduced voluntary coverage to a maximum of €5 million per depositor (effective 2023), with further reductions to €3 million (2025) and €1 million (2025).

### Institutional Protection Schemes

Germany's public savings banks (Sparkassen) and cooperative banks (Genossenschaftsbanken) operate institutional protection schemes (IPS) recognized under Article 113(7) CRR. These schemes prevent member institution failure rather than compensating depositors after failure, by providing mutual financial support. No depositor of a German Sparkasse or cooperative bank has ever lost deposits.

### Greensill Bank AG Failure (2021)

The insolvency of Greensill Bank AG (Bremen) in March 2021 represented the largest German deposit insurance payout since 2008. The EdB paid approximately €2.7 billion to approximately 16,000 retail depositors within the statutory €100,000 limit. The case exposed vulnerabilities in the deposit-gathering business model of non-traditional banks and prompted reforms to the voluntary protection fund.

## ECHR / EU

### DGS Directive 2014/49/EU

The current EU deposit guarantee framework is established by Directive 2014/49/EU of the European Parliament and of the Council of April 16, 2014, replacing the original Directive 94/19/EC:

**Key Provisions:**

| Article | Provision |
|---------|-----------|
| Article 6(1) | Coverage level: €100,000 per depositor per credit institution |
| Article 6(2) | Temporary high balances: member states must protect above €100,000 for at least 3 months |
| Article 8(1) | Payout deadline: 7 working days from determination of unavailability |
| Article 10(2) | Target fund level: 0.8% of covered deposits (by July 3, 2024) |
| Article 11(4) | Extraordinary contributions: up to 0.5% of covered deposits per calendar year |
| Article 13 | Risk-based contributions |
| Article 14(2) | DGS must accept alternative funding arrangements (borrowing, financial backstop) |

### European Deposit Insurance Scheme (EDIS)

The proposed third pillar of the Banking Union, EDIS has been under discussion since the European Commission's original proposal of November 24, 2015:

**Commission Communication of April 18, 2023 (Revised Approach):** The Commission proposed a hybrid model for EDIS:
- National DGS remain the first line of defense
- A European layer provides supplementary funding (liquidity support and, in later phases, loss coverage)
- Conditions for activation include the exhaustion of national DGS resources
- The European fund would be built from contributions by all participating credit institutions

**Political Obstacles:** EDIS has faced persistent opposition, particularly from Germany and the Netherlands, due to concerns about:
- Moral hazard: risk-sharing before legacy asset clean-up is complete
- Fiscal transfers: indirect subsidization of banking systems perceived as less sound
- Sovereignty: national deposit guarantee schemes reflect national fiscal choices

### CJEU Jurisprudence

**Case C-671/13, Indėlių ir investicijų draudimas VĮ v. Nemaniūnas (2015):** The CJEU held that the DGS Directive requires member states to ensure that deposit guarantee schemes have the capacity to make payouts within the prescribed deadlines, and that individual depositors may rely directly on the Directive's provisions against the state in cases of failure to transpose correctly.

**Case C-222/02, Paul and Others v. Germany (2004):** The CJEU addressed whether individual depositors could claim damages against a member state for failure of banking supervision that resulted in the loss of deposits beyond the guarantee limit. The Court held that the then-applicable Directive 94/19/EC did not confer directly enforceable rights on depositors against supervisory authorities, though this does not preclude national law providing such rights.

### ECHR Considerations

**Article 1, Protocol No. 1 (Protection of Property):** Bank deposits constitute "possessions" protected under the Convention. The failure of a deposit guarantee scheme to make timely or complete payouts could engage state responsibility under the ECHR.

**Ališić and Others v. Bosnia and Herzegovina, Croatia, Serbia, Slovenia and the former Yugoslav Republic of Macedonia (Application No. 60642/08, Grand Chamber, July 16, 2014):** The Court found violations of Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 regarding the failure of successor states of Yugoslavia to repay "old" foreign-currency savings deposits. The judgment established that states have positive obligations to establish and maintain effective deposit protection systems, and that prolonged inability to access deposits constitutes a continuing violation.

## Comparative Analysis

### Coverage Adequacy

Coverage limits vary substantially both in absolute terms and relative to economic indicators:

| Jurisdiction | Coverage Limit | Per Capita GDP (2025 est.) | Coverage/GDP Ratio |
|-------------|---------------|---------------------------|-------------------|
| South Korea | 50M KRW (~$38,000) | ~$35,000 | 1.1x |
| United States | $250,000 | ~$85,000 | 2.9x |
| Germany/EU | €100,000 (~$108,000) | ~$52,000 (Germany) | 2.1x |

South Korea's coverage ratio is notably lower than peer jurisdictions, covering only about 45 percent of total deposits by value, compared to approximately 60 percent in the United States and 55 percent in the EU.

### Funding Models

All jurisdictions have converged on ex ante, risk-based funding, but target levels differ:
- **South Korea:** Target of 1.0 percent of insured deposits
- **United States:** Target of 2.0 percent (raised from 1.35 percent post-2023 crisis)
- **EU:** Target of 0.8 percent of covered deposits

### Resolution Integration

The degree of integration between deposit insurance and bank resolution varies:
- **South Korea:** KDIC serves as both deposit insurer and primary resolution authority -- the most integrated model
- **United States:** FDIC serves as both deposit insurer and resolution authority for insured banks (Orderly Liquidation Authority under Dodd-Frank for SIFIs)
- **EU:** Separation between national DGS (deposit insurance) and the SRB (resolution), with coordination requirements under the BRRD

### Uninsured Deposit Risk

The 2023 U.S. banking crisis highlighted the systemic risk posed by high concentrations of uninsured deposits, prompting a global reassessment of coverage adequacy and the role of deposit insurance in maintaining financial stability.

### Moral Hazard and Market Discipline

Deposit insurance creates an inherent tension between depositor protection and moral hazard:

- **Too-big-to-fail problem:** The 2023 U.S. bank failures demonstrated that authorities will extend protection beyond formal coverage limits when systemic stability is threatened. The FDIC's invocation of the systemic risk exception for SVB and Signature Bank effectively provided unlimited coverage, undermining the disciplining effect of coverage caps
- **Risk-based premiums:** All jurisdictions have adopted risk-based premium structures to mitigate moral hazard, requiring riskier banks to pay higher insurance premiums. South Korea's KDIC uses a five-grade risk classification system; the FDIC uses a scorecard methodology for large banks; the EU DGS Directive requires risk-based contributions
- **Coinsurance debate:** Some scholars advocate for coinsurance (requiring depositors to bear a percentage of losses above a threshold) to preserve market discipline. The EU considered and rejected coinsurance during the DGS Directive negotiations in 2014

### Cross-Border Deposit Insurance

Depositors with accounts at branches of foreign banks face unique challenges:

- **EU:** Home country principle -- the DGS of the bank's home member state covers deposits at branches in other member states, supplemented by "topping-up" arrangements where host country coverage exceeds home country coverage
- **South Korea:** Foreign bank branches operating in Korea participate in the KDIC scheme for Korean-denominated deposits
- **United States:** Foreign bank branches are generally not FDIC-insured unless they accept retail deposits through separately incorporated U.S. subsidiaries
- The Icesave dispute (2008-2013), in which Iceland's deposit guarantee fund proved insufficient to cover deposits at branches of Landsbanki in the UK and Netherlands, demonstrated the fragility of cross-border deposit insurance arrangements

### Cryptocurrency and Deposit Insurance Gaps

The collapse of cryptocurrency exchanges and platforms (FTX, Celsius, Voyager Digital) in 2022-2023 exposed significant gaps in deposit protection:

- No jurisdiction extends traditional deposit insurance to cryptocurrency holdings or stablecoin balances
- The FDIC issued cease-and-desist orders against companies falsely claiming FDIC coverage for crypto-related products
- The EU's Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA, 2023) requires stablecoin issuers to maintain reserves but does not create a deposit insurance mechanism
- South Korea's Virtual Asset User Protection Act (가상자산 이용자 보호 등에 관한 법률, 2024) requires exchanges to segregate customer assets and maintain insurance reserves

### Payout Speed and Operational Efficiency

The speed of depositor reimbursement following a bank failure varies significantly:

- **EU:** DGS Directive requires payout within 7 working days (reduced from 20 days under the 2009 revision)
- **United States:** FDIC typically provides access to insured deposits within 1-2 business days through assumption transactions or direct payment
- **South Korea:** KDIC targets payout within 2 months; actual payout speed has varied depending on the complexity of the failure
- Rapid payout is critical for preventing bank runs at other institutions and maintaining public confidence in the banking system
