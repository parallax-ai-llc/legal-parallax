## Summary

The insanity defense -- the legal principle that persons who lack the mental capacity to understand the nature or wrongfulness of their conduct at the time of the offense should not be held criminally responsible -- represents one of the oldest and most contested doctrines in criminal law, reflecting fundamental assumptions about moral agency, culpability, and the purposes of punishment. South Korea addresses mental incapacity through Articles 10 and 11 of the Criminal Act (형법), distinguishing between persons of unsound mind (심신상실자) who are not punishable and persons of diminished mental capacity (심신미약자) who receive mandatory sentence reductions, with the 2018 amendment to Article 10(3) excluding voluntary intoxication-induced mental incapacity from sentence reduction -- a reform catalyzed by the so-called "soju defense" controversy following public outrage at alcohol-related violent crime receiving reduced sentences. The United States employs multiple and conflicting standards across federal and state jurisdictions: the federal Insanity Defense Reform Act of 1984 (18 U.S.C. Section 17), enacted after John Hinckley Jr.'s acquittal for the attempted assassination of President Reagan, narrowed the defense to require proof by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant was unable to appreciate the nature and quality or wrongfulness of the act, while four states (Kansas, Montana, Idaho, and Utah) have effectively abolished the insanity defense, and the Supreme Court in Kahler v. Kansas, 589 U.S. 271 (2020) held that the Fourteenth Amendment does not require states to adopt any particular formulation of the insanity defense. Germany codifies mental incapacity in Section 20 StGB (Schuldunfähigkeit -- incapacity for guilt) and diminished responsibility in Section 21 StGB (verminderte Schuldfähigkeit), requiring a biological-psychological analysis identifying a qualifying mental condition (mental disorder, profound consciousness disturbance, intellectual disability, or other serious mental abnormality) and establishing that this condition eliminated or substantially reduced the person's capacity to appreciate the unlawfulness of the act or to act in accordance with such appreciation -- with the important additional mechanism of Maßregeln der Besserung und Sicherung (measures of reform and prevention) under Sections 61-72 StGB, including commitment to a psychiatric hospital. The European Court of Human Rights has addressed the intersection of mental illness and criminal responsibility primarily through Article 5(1)(e) (detention of persons of unsound mind) as interpreted in Winterwerp v. the Netherlands (1979) and through Article 3 (prohibition of inhuman treatment of mentally ill prisoners), establishing procedural and substantive requirements for the detention and treatment of mentally disordered offenders.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's approach to the insanity defense is codified in Articles 10 and 11 of the Criminal Act (형법):

**Article 10(1) -- Mental Incompetence (심신상실):** "The act of a person who, because of mental disorder, is unable to make discriminations or to control his/her will shall not be punished." This provision establishes a complete defense resulting in acquittal for persons who, due to mental disorder, lack either the cognitive capacity to distinguish right from wrong or the volitional capacity to control their conduct.

**Article 10(2) -- Diminished Capacity (심신미약):** "The punishment of a person whose capacities described in the preceding paragraph are weak shall be mitigated." This provision mandates a reduced sentence for persons with diminished but not entirely absent mental capacity at the time of the offense.

**Article 10(3) -- Voluntary Intoxication Exception (2018 amendment):** "The provisions of paragraphs (1) and (2) shall not apply to a person who has anticipated or could have anticipated the danger of committing a crime due to voluntary intoxication." This amendment, effective December 18, 2018, was enacted in direct response to widespread public criticism of the "soju defense" (주취감경), where defendants charged with violent crimes, including sexual assault and murder, received reduced sentences based on alcohol-induced diminished capacity.

### The "Soju Defense" Controversy

The 2018 amendment was precipitated by a series of high-profile cases in which defendants convicted of violent offenses received significantly reduced sentences under Article 10(2) based on alcohol intoxication at the time of the crime. Public outrage intensified following cases such as the 2018 sexual assault at Hongik University, where the defendant's sentence was reduced due to alcohol-induced diminished capacity. A Blue House petition calling for reform gathered over 200,000 signatures, prompting swift legislative action.

### Treatment and Custody (치료감호)

The Treatment and Custody Act (치료감호법) provides for the commitment of persons acquitted by reason of mental incompetence or convicted with diminished capacity to the National Forensic Hospital (국립법무병원) in Gongju. Treatment and custody orders have a maximum duration of fifteen years, during which periodic review determines whether continued commitment is necessary.

### Forensic Evaluation

Forensic psychiatric evaluation (정신감정) is typically conducted by court-appointed psychiatrists from university hospitals or the National Forensic Hospital. The evaluation assesses the defendant's mental state at the time of the offense, distinguishing between diagnoses that qualify under Article 10 (such as schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, severe intellectual disability) and conditions that do not (such as personality disorders, which Korean courts have generally held insufficient for Article 10 relief).

## United States

### Legal Framework

The U.S. insanity defense landscape is among the most fragmented in the world, with federal law, state law, and military law each employing different standards, burdens, and procedures.

### Historical Evolution of Standards

| Standard | Origin | Test |
|----------|--------|------|
| M'Naghten Rule (1843) | English House of Lords | Defendant did not know the nature and quality of the act or did not know it was wrong |
| Irresistible Impulse Test (1887) | Parsons v. State (Alabama) | Defendant was unable to control conduct despite knowing it was wrong |
| Durham Rule (1954) | Durham v. United States (D.C. Cir.) | Unlawful act was the product of mental disease or defect |
| Model Penal Code Test (1962) | American Law Institute | Defendant lacked substantial capacity to appreciate criminality or conform conduct to law |
| Federal Standard (1984) | Insanity Defense Reform Act | Defendant unable to appreciate nature and quality or wrongfulness of act |

### Insanity Defense Reform Act of 1984 (18 U.S.C. Section 17)

Enacted in direct response to the acquittal of John Hinckley Jr. for the 1981 attempted assassination of President Ronald Reagan, the Act:
- Eliminated the volitional (irresistible impulse) prong from the federal standard
- Shifted the burden of proof to the defendant (clear and convincing evidence)
- Prohibited expert testimony on the ultimate issue of whether the defendant was insane
- Established mandatory commitment following acquittal (18 U.S.C. Section 4243)

### State Variations

As of 2025, the landscape across states includes:
- **M'Naghten only:** Approximately 25 states follow the cognitive-only M'Naghten test
- **M'Naghten + volitional:** Several states include both cognitive and volitional elements
- **MPC test:** A declining number of states continue to use the Model Penal Code formulation
- **Abolished:** Kansas, Montana, Idaho, and Utah have effectively abolished the insanity defense, though they permit evidence of mental illness to negate mens rea
- **Guilty but mentally ill (GBMI):** Approximately 20 states offer a GBMI verdict as an alternative to NGRI, resulting in a conviction with mental health treatment during incarceration

### Kahler v. Kansas, 589 U.S. 271 (2020)

In a 6-3 decision, the Supreme Court held that the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment does not require states to adopt an insanity test that turns on a defendant's ability to recognize the wrongfulness of their conduct. Kansas's approach -- permitting evidence of mental illness to negate mens rea but not recognizing a separate insanity defense -- was held constitutional. Justice Kagan's majority opinion reasoned that states have broad discretion in defining the contours of the insanity defense, and that history does not support a single formulation as constitutionally required.

### Post-Acquittal Commitment

Under the federal system (18 U.S.C. Section 4243), a person found not guilty by reason of insanity is automatically committed to a federal facility for evaluation, followed by a hearing on whether release would create a substantial risk of danger. In Jones v. United States, 463 U.S. 354 (1983), the Supreme Court upheld automatic commitment and held that the commitment may exceed the maximum sentence for the underlying offense.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's approach to criminal responsibility and mental illness is widely considered among the most systematic and conceptually rigorous in comparative law, drawing on a long tradition of cooperation between criminal law and forensic psychiatry.

### Section 20 StGB -- Incapacity for Guilt (Schuldunfähigkeit)

Section 20 StGB provides: "A person is not criminally responsible if, at the time of the act, they are incapable of appreciating the unlawfulness of the act or of acting in accordance with such appreciation due to a pathological mental disorder, a profound disturbance of consciousness, intellectual disability, or any other serious mental abnormality."

The provision requires a two-step analysis:
1. **Biological-medical element:** Identification of a qualifying mental condition from the four statutory categories
2. **Psychological-normative element:** Establishment that the condition eliminated the capacity to either appreciate unlawfulness (cognitive capacity) or act accordingly (volitional capacity)

### Section 21 StGB -- Diminished Responsibility (Verminderte Schuldfähigkeit)

Section 21 StGB provides that if the capacity described in Section 20 is "substantially diminished" at the time of the act, the sentence may (not must) be reduced. Unlike South Korea's mandatory reduction under Article 10(2), Germany provides judicial discretion, and courts frequently decline to reduce sentences where the diminished capacity results from voluntary intoxication or where the offense is particularly serious.

### Measures of Reform and Prevention (Maßregeln der Besserung und Sicherung)

Germany's dual-track system allows courts to impose both punishment and preventive measures. For persons acquitted under Section 20 or convicted with diminished responsibility under Section 21:

**Section 63 StGB -- Commitment to Psychiatric Hospital:** The court may order commitment to a psychiatric hospital if the person committed an unlawful act in a state of incapacity for guilt or diminished responsibility and the overall assessment of the person and the act indicates that further unlawful acts of considerable significance are to be expected.

**Section 64 StGB -- Commitment to Addiction Treatment Facility:** For offenders whose acts are connected to addiction, the court may order commitment to an addiction treatment facility, with a maximum duration of two years.

### Actio Libera in Causa and Section 323a StGB

German law addresses the problem of self-induced incapacity through two mechanisms:
- **Actio libera in causa doctrine:** Where a person intentionally or negligently brings about their own incapacity in order to commit the offense, they may be held responsible for the planned offense
- **Section 323a StGB (Vollrausch -- full intoxication):** Where a person commits an unlawful act in a state of self-induced full intoxication that rendered them incapable under Section 20, they may be convicted of the separate offense of full intoxication, punishable by up to five years' imprisonment

### Federal Constitutional Court

The Federal Constitutional Court has held that the guilt principle (Schuldprinzip) is constitutionally mandated under Article 1(1) (human dignity) and Article 20(3) (rule of law) of the Basic Law, meaning that punishment without guilt is constitutionally impermissible. This effectively prevents Germany from abolishing the insanity defense, unlike the approach permitted in some U.S. states after Kahler v. Kansas.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Article 5(1)(e) -- Detention of Persons of Unsound Mind

Article 5(1)(e) permits the "lawful detention of persons of unsound mind," but the ECHR has imposed strict conditions:

**Winterwerp v. the Netherlands, Application No. 6301/73, Judgment of October 24, 1979:** The Court established three minimum conditions for the lawful detention of persons of unsound mind: (1) the individual must be reliably shown by objective medical expertise to be of unsound mind, (2) the mental disorder must be of a kind or degree warranting compulsory confinement, and (3) the validity of continued confinement depends on the persistence of such a disorder. The Winterwerp criteria remain the foundational standard across the Council of Europe.

**Varbanov v. Bulgaria, Application No. 31365/96, Judgment of October 5, 2000:** The Court held that Article 5(1)(e) requires a medical assessment before detention, not merely after, finding that the applicant's detention in a psychiatric institution without prior medical assessment violated Article 5.

**Ilnseher v. Germany [GC], Applications Nos. 10211/12 and 27505/14, Judgment of February 4, 2021:** The Grand Chamber upheld Germany's system of subsequent preventive detention (nachträgliche Sicherungsverwahrung) for dangerous offenders with mental disorders, finding that the measure pursued a legitimate aim of protecting the public and was accompanied by sufficient safeguards.

### ECHR Article 3 -- Treatment of Mentally Ill Prisoners

**Keenan v. United Kingdom, Application No. 27229/95, Judgment of April 3, 2001:** The Court found a violation of Article 3 where a mentally ill prisoner committed suicide after inadequate psychiatric care and punitive measures, establishing that states have a positive obligation to protect the life and health of mentally ill persons in custody.

**Rooman v. Belgium [GC], Application No. 18052/11, Judgment of January 31, 2019:** The Grand Chamber held that the failure to provide adequate psychiatric treatment to a person detained under a security measure violated Article 3 and Article 5, finding that detention without appropriate treatment undermines both the therapeutic purpose and the legitimacy of the measure.

### EU Framework

The EU has not harmonized substantive criminal responsibility standards, leaving the insanity defense to national law. However, EU Directive 2012/29/EU on victims' rights and Regulation (EU) 2016/796 on Europol include provisions addressing the handling of mentally disordered offenders in cross-border contexts.

## Comparative Analysis

### Scope of the Defense

Germany and South Korea both recognize cognitive and volitional incapacity (inability to appreciate wrongfulness and inability to control conduct). The U.S. federal standard, post-1984, recognizes only cognitive incapacity, eliminating the volitional prong. This narrowing was a direct political response to public outrage over the Hinckley acquittal.

### Diminished Capacity

South Korea mandates sentence reduction for diminished capacity, Germany permits discretionary reduction, and the United States varies by state with many states not recognizing diminished capacity as a distinct doctrine.

### Voluntary Intoxication

South Korea's 2018 amendment categorically excludes voluntary intoxication from the defense. Germany addresses the issue through the actio libera in causa doctrine and the independent offense of full intoxication (Section 323a StGB). The United States generally does not permit voluntary intoxication as a basis for the insanity defense but may allow it to negate specific intent.

### Post-Acquittal Treatment

All jurisdictions provide for involuntary commitment following acquittal by reason of insanity. Germany's system is the most developed, with a dual-track approach combining measures of reform and prevention. The key difference is that U.S. commitment may be indefinite (Jones v. United States), while South Korea imposes a fifteen-year maximum.
