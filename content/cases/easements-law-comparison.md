## Summary

Easements -- non-possessory rights to use another person's land for a specified purpose -- are among the oldest and most fundamental institutions of property law, enabling land access, utility infrastructure, conservation, and the productive interrelation of neighboring properties while creating complex legal relationships between dominant and servient estate owners that every legal system must regulate. South Korea codifies easements as part of its system of real rights (물권) under the Korean Civil Code (민법), recognizing the servitude (지역권, Articles 291-302), which grants the owner of a dominant estate the right to use a servient estate for specified purposes including passage, water supply, and view protection, with creation through agreement and registration in the real estate register, and also recognizing analogous rights through the right of superficies (지상권, Articles 279-290) and customary law rights of passage (관습상 통행권); the Supreme Court of Korea has developed significant jurisprudence on the intersection of easements with the mandatory right of passage for landlocked parcels under Article 219 of the Civil Code. The United States recognizes easements through common law, state statutes, and the Restatement (Third) of Property: Servitudes (2000), distinguishing among easements appurtenant (benefiting a dominant estate), easements in gross (benefiting a person or entity), and negative easements (preventing certain uses), with creation through express grant or reservation, implication, necessity, prescription, and estoppel; conservation easements, authorized under the Uniform Conservation Easement Act (1981) and federal tax incentives under Internal Revenue Code Section 170(h), have become a major land conservation tool. Germany codifies servitudes (Dienstbarkeiten) in the BGB under three forms: Grunddienstbarkeit (predial servitude, Sections 1018-1029), Nießbrauch (usufruct, Sections 1030-1089), and beschränkte persönliche Dienstbarkeit (restricted personal servitude, Sections 1090-1093), all requiring registration in the Grundbuch for creation and binding effect on successors. The EU has not harmonized easement law, though cross-border easement issues arise in the context of trans-European infrastructure networks and the free movement of capital, while the ECHR has addressed easement-related issues under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (property protection) and Article 8 (right to respect for home).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's easement law operates within the Civil Code's system of real rights (물권법정주의, the principle that real rights exist only as recognized by law).

**Servitude (지역권, Articles 291-302):** Article 291 defines a servitude as the right to use another person's land for the convenience of one's own land, in accordance with a specified purpose. The servitude is appurtenant -- it benefits a dominant estate (요역지) and burdens a servient estate (승역지). The servitude runs with the land and binds successors in title.

**Article 292 (Scope of Servitude):** The scope of the servitude is determined by the agreement between the parties and the needs of the dominant estate, as assessed at the time of creation. The servitude holder must exercise the right in the manner least burdensome to the servient estate.

**Article 294 (Acquisitive Prescription):** A servitude may be acquired by prescription through 20 years of continuous, peaceful, and public exercise of the easement right, with the intent to exercise a servitude. The Supreme Court has held that prescriptive easements are subject to the same requirements as acquisitive prescription under Article 245.

### Mandatory Right of Passage (Article 219)

Article 219 of the Civil Code provides a critically important statutory right of access for landlocked parcels. Where a parcel has no access to a public road, the owner may pass through surrounding land to reach the road. This right arises by operation of law and does not require registration. The owner of the surrounding land is entitled to compensation for damages caused by the passage. The Supreme Court has developed extensive jurisprudence on Article 219, addressing issues including the determination of the least burdensome route, the scope of permitted use (pedestrian versus vehicular access), the obligation to pay compensation, and the relationship between Article 219 rights and formally registered servitudes.

### Classification of Easement Types

Korean law recognizes several functional categories of servitudes:

| Type | Purpose | Common Examples |
|------|---------|----------------|
| Right of way (통행지역권) | Access across servient estate | Road access; path access to landlocked parcels |
| Water servitude (수도지역권/인수지역권) | Water supply or drainage | Pipeline easements; drainage channels |
| View servitude (조망지역권) | Protection of views or light | Height restrictions; setback requirements |
| Utility servitude (공작물설치지역권) | Installation of infrastructure | Electrical lines; telecommunications cables |

### Registration System

Servitudes must be registered in the real property register (부동산등기부) to have effect against third parties. The registration must identify the dominant and servient estates, describe the purpose and scope of the servitude, and specify any conditions or limitations. Unregistered servitudes may be enforceable between the original parties as contractual obligations but do not bind successors of the servient estate.

## United States

### Legal Framework

American easement law combines common law principles with statutory provisions and is comprehensively treated in the Restatement (Third) of Property: Servitudes (2000).

### Types of Easements

**Easements Appurtenant:** Benefit a dominant estate and burden a servient estate. The benefit and burden run with the respective parcels, binding and benefiting successors in title. Creation requires intent that the easement be appurtenant and a relationship between the easement and the use of the dominant estate.

**Easements in Gross:** Benefit a person or entity rather than a parcel of land. Historically, commercial easements in gross (such as utility easements) were transferable while personal easements in gross (such as fishing rights) were not. The Restatement (Third) Section 4.6 provides that all easements in gross are transferable unless the original agreement restricts transfer.

**Negative Easements:** Restrict the servient owner from performing specified acts on their own land. At common law, negative easements were limited to four categories: light and air, support, stream flow, and artificial stream flow. Modern law has expanded negative easements, most significantly through conservation easements.

### Methods of Creation

| Method | Requirements | Key Cases/Authority |
|--------|-------------|-------------------|
| Express Grant | Written instrument satisfying Statute of Frauds; clear intent | Restatement (Third) Section 2.1 |
| Express Reservation | Grantor reserves easement in deed conveying servient estate | Restatement (Third) Section 2.6 |
| Implication (Prior Use) | Common ownership; prior apparent use; reasonable necessity | Van Sandt v. Royster, 148 Kan. 495 (1938) |
| Necessity | Common ownership; strict necessity (landlocked parcel) | Restatement (Third) Section 2.15 |
| Prescription | Open, notorious, hostile, continuous use for statutory period | Warsaw v. Chicago Metallic Ceilings, Inc., 35 Cal.3d 564 (1984) |
| Estoppel | Representation of permission; reasonable reliance; change of position | Restatement (Third) Section 2.10 |

### Conservation Easements

Conservation easements have become one of the most important tools in American land conservation. The Uniform Conservation Easement Act (UCEA, 1981), adopted in approximately 28 states, authorizes the creation of easements restricting development for conservation, preservation, or scenic purposes. Federal tax incentives under IRC Section 170(h) allow donors of qualifying conservation easements to claim charitable deductions for the value of the development rights donated. By 2024, conservation easements protected approximately 40 million acres of U.S. land. However, conservation easement tax deductions have been the subject of extensive IRS enforcement and litigation concerning syndicated conservation easement transactions used primarily as tax shelters.

### Utility Easements

Utility easements, typically held as easements in gross by utility companies, are among the most common easements in American property law. These easements may be created by grant, reservation, condemnation (eminent domain), or prescription. The scope of utility easements -- including whether they may be used for new types of infrastructure (e.g., fiber optic cables in a telephone easement) -- has generated significant litigation.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German easement law is characterized by the numerus clausus of real rights (Typenzwang) and the requirement of Grundbuch registration for creation and third-party effect.

**Grunddienstbarkeit (Predial Servitude, Sections 1018-1029 BGB):** Section 1018 provides that land may be burdened in favor of the owner of another parcel in such a way that the owner of the other parcel may use the burdened land in certain ways (affirmative servitude), certain acts may not be performed on the burdened land (restrictive servitude), or the exercise of a right that the owner of the burdened land has against the owner of the other parcel is excluded (negative servitude).

The Grunddienstbarkeit must serve the use of the dominant estate (Section 1019 BGB), ensuring that servitudes cannot be used for purely personal benefits unrelated to the dominant parcel. The servient owner's obligation is primarily one of tolerance (Duldungspflicht) -- they must tolerate the dominant owner's exercise of the servitude -- rather than an obligation to perform affirmative acts.

**Beschränkte persönliche Dienstbarkeit (Restricted Personal Servitude, Sections 1090-1093 BGB):** This form of servitude benefits a specific person rather than a parcel, analogous to the American easement in gross. Unlike the Grunddienstbarkeit, it is generally non-transferable and non-inheritable (Section 1092 BGB), with an exception for servitudes granted to legal persons or serving business operations.

### Notwegerecht (Right of Necessity, Section 917 BGB)

Section 917 BGB provides a statutory right of access for landlocked parcels, analogous to South Korea's Article 219 and the American easement by necessity. The owner of a parcel that lacks necessary access to a public road may demand from a neighbor the toleration of use of their land for purposes of establishing the necessary access (Notweg). The neighbor is entitled to compensation in the form of an annuity (Geldrente). The right arises by operation of law and does not require Grundbuch registration.

### Grundbuch Registration

The creation of a Grunddienstbarkeit or beschränkte persönliche Dienstbarkeit requires: agreement between the parties (dingliche Einigung, Section 873 BGB); registration in the Grundbuch of the servient estate (Eintragung); and, for servitudes created by legal transaction, a legally valid underlying obligation (Grundgeschäft). The Grundbuch registration creates a conclusive presumption of the servitude's existence (Section 891 BGB) and ensures that the servitude binds successors in title of the servient estate.

### Altrechtliche Dienstbarkeiten

Historical servitudes (altrechtliche Dienstbarkeiten) created before the BGB entered into force in 1900 may continue to exist under Article 187 EGBGB (Einführungsgesetz zum Bürgerlichen Gesetzbuche), even without Grundbuch registration. These historical servitudes are relatively common in parts of Germany with long-established rural property patterns and create ongoing interpretive challenges.

### Leitungsrechte (Utility Rights)

German law provides specific mechanisms for utility infrastructure easements. The Grundbuchbereinigungsgesetz (Land Register Adjustment Act) of 1993, enacted following German reunification, addressed the regularization of utility infrastructure in the former East Germany that had been installed without formal easement creation. Sections 1-10 of the Act created statutory easements (gesetzliche Leitungsrechte) for utility lines that existed on the reunification date, securing the continued operation of essential infrastructure.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR and Easement Rights

The ECHR has addressed easement-related issues under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 and Article 8:

**Sovtransavto Holding v. Ukraine (Application No. 48553/99, 2002):** While primarily a corporate property case, the Court's principles regarding the state's positive obligation to protect property rights extend to easement rights registered in official records.

**Öneryıldız v. Turkey (Application No. 48939/99, Grand Chamber, 2004):** The Court's broad interpretation of "possessions" under Protocol 1 encompasses not only ownership but also lesser property interests including rights of use, access, and passage.

### EU Infrastructure and Easements

While the EU has not harmonized easement law, EU policies affect easement creation and management in several contexts:

**Trans-European Networks (TEN-T and TEN-E):** EU regulations on trans-European transport and energy networks (Regulation (EU) 1315/2013 and Regulation (EU) 2022/869) require member states to facilitate the creation of rights of way for cross-border infrastructure, including pipelines, electrical transmission lines, and transport corridors. These requirements may necessitate the creation of easements through national legal mechanisms, including compulsory acquisition where voluntary agreement is not possible.

**Renewable Energy and Grid Infrastructure:** Directive (EU) 2018/2001 on the promotion of renewable energy and its implementing measures require member states to streamline permitting for renewable energy installations, including the creation of easements for grid connections, access roads, and maintenance rights. The European Green Deal's infrastructure requirements are expected to generate substantial easement-related legal activity across member states.

### Cross-Border Easements

Cross-border easements -- servitudes burdening land in one EU member state for the benefit of land in another -- present unique legal challenges. The applicable law for creation, scope, and termination of such easements is determined by the lex rei sitae principle (the law of the state where the servient estate is located). Brussels I Regulation (Recast) Article 24(1) provides that courts of the member state where the immovable property is situated have exclusive jurisdiction for proceedings relating to rights in rem, including easements.

### Environmental Easements in EU Law

EU environmental law creates functional equivalents of environmental easements through habitat protection obligations. The Habitats Directive 92/43/EEC requires member states to designate Special Areas of Conservation (SACs) and ensure that land uses within and around these areas do not adversely affect the conservation objectives. These obligations may be implemented through national easement or servitude mechanisms, effectively creating binding use restrictions on private land for the benefit of biodiversity conservation.

## Comparative Analysis

### Structural Approaches

The four systems reveal fundamentally different structural approaches to easement law. Germany follows the strictest numerus clausus principle, limiting servitudes to the three forms defined in the BGB (Grunddienstbarkeit, Nießbrauch, beschränkte persönliche Dienstbarkeit) and requiring Grundbuch registration for creation. South Korea similarly limits easement types to those recognized by the Civil Code but permits prescriptive creation without registration. The United States recognizes the broadest range of easement types and creation methods, including easements by estoppel, prescriptive easements, and conservation easements, with the Restatement (Third) taking a particularly flexible approach. The EU defers to member state property law while creating functional easement-like obligations through environmental and infrastructure directives.

### Access Rights for Landlocked Parcels

All three national systems provide mandatory access rights for landlocked parcels, reflecting the universal recognition that denial of access to public infrastructure would render property effectively useless. South Korea's Article 219, Germany's Section 917 BGB (Notwegerecht), and the American easement by necessity all provide access rights that arise by operation of law, though they differ in scope (whether vehicular access is included), the obligation to pay compensation, and the method for determining the access route.

### Conservation and Environmental Easements

The use of easements for conservation purposes reveals significant divergences. The United States has developed conservation easements into a major land conservation tool, with federal tax incentives driving adoption. Germany achieves similar conservation outcomes through planning law restrictions (Bebauungspläne) and nature conservation designations rather than private easement mechanisms. South Korea relies primarily on development restriction zones and environmental protection designations. The EU creates functional conservation restrictions through the Habitats and Birds Directives.

### Registration and Publicity

The role of registration differs fundamentally. Germany's Grundbuch system requires registration for the creation of all servitudes, providing maximum certainty. South Korea requires registration for servitudes but not for statutory rights of passage. The United States does not require recording for easement creation but uses recording systems to establish priority among competing interests. These differences reflect broader divergences in property registration philosophy -- the German title registration system versus the American recording system versus the Korean hybrid approach.
