## Summary

Divorce -- the legal dissolution of a marriage -- is regulated through fundamentally different legal frameworks across jurisdictions, reflecting divergent cultural, religious, and policy attitudes toward marriage, family, and individual autonomy. South Korea governs divorce through Articles 834-843 of the Civil Act (민법), recognizing two distinct pathways: consensual divorce (협의이혼) requiring a mandatory counseling and reflection period followed by family court confirmation, and judicial divorce (재판이혼) available on enumerated grounds under Article 840 including infidelity, malicious desertion, severe mistreatment, and any other serious reason making continuation of the marriage difficult; the divorce rate in South Korea rose dramatically from 1.1 per 1,000 population in 1990 to a peak of 3.4 in 2003 before declining to approximately 2.1 in 2024, with the 2007 revision to the Civil Act introducing mandatory counseling and a reflection period to address concerns about overly hasty consensual divorces. The United States has moved almost entirely to no-fault divorce since California became the first state to adopt it in 1970 under the Family Law Act, with all fifty states now offering some form of no-fault dissolution, though recent legislative efforts in several states have sought to restrict no-fault divorce or impose waiting periods; federal law does not govern divorce directly, but the Full Faith and Credit Clause requires states to recognize divorces granted by other states, and federal tax and benefits laws are significantly affected by marital status. Germany regulates divorce under Sections 1564-1587 of the Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB), employing a breakdown principle (Zerrüttungsprinzip) under which a marriage can be dissolved only if it has irretrievably broken down, with irrebuttable presumptions of breakdown after one year of separation with mutual consent or three years of separation without consent, and a hardship clause (Härteklausel) permitting divorce without the separation period in exceptional circumstances. The European framework includes the Rome III Regulation (EU No 1259/2010) on applicable law in cross-border divorces through enhanced cooperation among participating member states, the Brussels II ter Regulation (EU 2019/1111) on jurisdiction and recognition of divorce judgments, and ECHR jurisprudence recognizing that while Article 12 protects the right to marry, it does not guarantee a right to divorce, though Article 8 may be engaged where divorce-related proceedings affect family life.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Divorce in South Korea is governed by Chapter 3, Section 2 of the Civil Act (민법 제3편 제2장), distinguishing between consensual divorce and judicial divorce.

### Consensual Divorce (협의이혼)

**Article 834:** A husband and wife may divorce by mutual agreement. Consensual divorce requires both parties to appear before the family court and confirm their agreement.

**2007 Reform -- Mandatory Counseling and Reflection Period:** The Civil Act was amended in 2007 (effective June 22, 2008) to require:

| Requirement | With Minor Children | Without Minor Children |
|-------------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Counseling | Mandatory pre-divorce counseling (이혼안내) | Mandatory pre-divorce counseling |
| Reflection period | 3 months (숙려기간) | 1 month |
| Agreement on children | Custody, visitation, child support agreement required | N/A |
| Court confirmation | Family court judge confirms voluntariness and agreements | Family court judge confirms voluntariness |

The reflection period may be waived by the court in cases of domestic violence or other compelling circumstances.

### Judicial Divorce (재판이혼)

**Article 840 Grounds for Judicial Divorce:**
1. An act of unchastity (부정한 행위) by the other spouse
2. Malicious desertion (악의의 유기) by the other spouse
3. Extreme ill-treatment (심히 부당한 대우) by the other spouse or the other spouse's lineal ascendants
4. Extreme ill-treatment of one's own lineal ascendants by the other spouse
5. Uncertain whether the other spouse is alive or dead for three or more years
6. Any other serious reason making it difficult to continue the marriage (기타 혼인을 계속하기 어려운 중대한 사유)

Article 840(6) functions as a general breakdown clause, widely applied by courts to encompass emotional estrangement, irreconcilable differences, and de facto separation.

### Fault and Compensation

The "at-fault" spouse (유책배우자) faces significant legal consequences in Korean divorce law. The Supreme Court has historically held that the at-fault spouse may not seek judicial divorce against the will of the innocent spouse (대법원 전원합의체 2015므2352 판결). However, the Court has gradually softened this position, recognizing that in long-term separations, maintaining the formal marriage serves no purpose. The at-fault spouse may be required to pay consolation money (위자료) and property division may be adjusted to account for fault.

### Property Division

Article 839-2 governs property division (재산분할), requiring courts to consider each party's contributions to the acquisition, maintenance, and increase of property during the marriage. The Supreme Court has established that homemaking and child-rearing contributions are recognized as property formation contributions, with a general presumption of equal contribution in long-term marriages, though the exact ratio is determined case by case.

### Divorce Statistics

South Korea's divorce rate peaked at 3.4 per 1,000 population in 2003, the highest in East Asia at the time. Following the introduction of mandatory counseling and reflection periods in 2008, the rate declined to approximately 2.1 in 2024. The crude divorce rate remains significantly below the U.S. rate but above Germany's.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Divorce in the United States is governed by state law, with no federal divorce statute. The constitutional framework requires states to grant full faith and credit to divorce decrees issued by other states (Williams v. North Carolina, 317 U.S. 287 (1942)), though states may impose residency requirements ranging from six weeks (Nevada) to one year (several states).

### No-Fault Revolution

California became the first state to adopt pure no-fault divorce in 1970 under the Family Law Act (Cal. Fam. Code Section 2310), allowing dissolution based solely on "irreconcilable differences." By 1985, all states had adopted some form of no-fault divorce. Key milestones:

| Year | Development |
|------|-------------|
| 1970 | California adopts pure no-fault divorce |
| 1970 | Uniform Marriage and Divorce Act recommends no-fault grounds |
| 1980 | Most states have adopted no-fault options |
| 1985 | South Dakota becomes last state to adopt no-fault grounds |
| 2010 | New York adopts no-fault divorce (last state to do so) |

### Fault Grounds

Although no-fault divorce is universally available, many states retain fault-based grounds including adultery, cruelty, desertion, imprisonment, and habitual intoxication. Fault may be relevant to property division, alimony, and in some jurisdictions, custody determinations.

### Waiting Periods

State waiting periods vary substantially:

| State | Waiting Period |
|-------|---------------|
| Nevada, Alaska, others | None |
| California | 6 months from filing |
| New York | None (no-fault); 1 year separation (separation ground) |
| North Carolina | 1 year separation |
| Maryland | Varies by ground |

### Covenant Marriage

Three states -- Louisiana (1997), Arizona (1998), and Arkansas (2001) -- offer "covenant marriage," an optional form of marriage with more restrictive divorce grounds requiring fault, counseling, and extended waiting periods. Couples must receive premarital counseling and agree to seek marital counseling before filing for divorce. However, covenant marriage has been adopted by only a small fraction of couples in these states.

### Recent Movements to Restrict No-Fault Divorce

Since 2023, several state legislatures have introduced bills to restrict or eliminate no-fault divorce, particularly for couples with children. These proposals have drawn significant public attention but have not been enacted as of early 2026. Proponents argue that no-fault divorce encourages family dissolution; opponents contend that restricting no-fault divorce would trap spouses in harmful marriages.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German divorce law is governed by Sections 1564-1568 of the Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB). Germany employs a pure breakdown (Zerrüttungsprinzip) rather than a fault-based system.

### Irretrievable Breakdown (Section 1565 BGB)

**Section 1565(1):** A marriage may be dissolved by divorce if it has broken down (gescheitert). A marriage has broken down if the conjugal community of the spouses no longer exists and it cannot be expected that the spouses will restore it.

**Section 1565(2):** If the spouses have lived apart for less than one year, the marriage may be dissolved only if the continuation of the marriage would constitute an unreasonable hardship (unzumutbare Härte) for the applicant due to reasons attributable to the other spouse.

### Presumptions of Breakdown

| Circumstance | Legal Effect | BGB Section |
|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| 1 year separation + both consent | Irrebuttable presumption of breakdown | Section 1566(1) |
| 3 years separation | Irrebuttable presumption of breakdown (consent not required) | Section 1566(2) |
| Under 1 year | Divorce only for unreasonable hardship (Härtefall) | Section 1565(2) |

### Hardship Clause (Härteklausel, Section 1568)

Even where breakdown is established, divorce may be refused if exceptional circumstances in the interest of minor children of the marriage make the continuation of the marriage necessary, or if dissolution would constitute an unusually severe hardship for the respondent. In practice, this clause is invoked rarely and sustained even more rarely.

### Consequences of Divorce

**Spousal Maintenance (Unterhalt, Sections 1569-1586b BGB):** Post-divorce maintenance is governed by the principle of self-sufficiency (Eigenverantwortung). Since the 2008 Unterhaltsrechtsänderungsgesetz reform, post-divorce maintenance is limited in duration and scope. Child-related maintenance (Betreuungsunterhalt) generally extends until the youngest child reaches age three, after which the custodial parent is expected to pursue employment.

**Pension Equalization (Versorgungsausgleich, Sections 1-54 VersAusglG):** Germany's Versorgungsausgleich system divides pension entitlements accrued during the marriage equally between the spouses. This system, reformed by the Versorgungsausgleichsgesetz of 2009, divides each pension right individually (Totalrevision) rather than calculating a single balance.

**Property Division:** Germany's default matrimonial property regime is the Zugewinngemeinschaft (community of accrued gains), under which each spouse retains separate ownership of their property during marriage, but upon divorce, the spouse with the lesser gain receives a claim for half the difference in gains (Zugewinnausgleich, BGB Section 1378).

### Divorce Procedure

Divorce proceedings are conducted before the family court (Familiengericht). Legal representation is mandatory for the applicant; the respondent may but need not be represented. The court must hear both spouses in person before granting the divorce. The Verbund principle requires that certain ancillary matters (particularly pension equalization) be decided together with the divorce itself.

## ECHR & EU

### Rome III Regulation (EU No 1259/2010)

The Rome III Regulation, effective June 21, 2012, establishes uniform conflict-of-law rules for divorce and legal separation in participating member states under enhanced cooperation. As of 2026, 17 member states participate.

**Choice of Law:** Spouses may agree to designate the applicable law from among: the law of the state of their habitual residence at the time the agreement is concluded, the law of the last habitual residence of the spouses if one still resides there, the law of the state of nationality of either spouse, or the law of the forum.

**Absent Choice:** Where no valid choice has been made, the applicable law is determined by a cascade: first, the law of the state of habitual residence at the time the court is seised; second, the law of the last joint habitual residence if that ended less than a year ago and one spouse still resides there; third, the law of common nationality; fourth, the law of the forum.

### Brussels II ter Regulation (EU 2019/1111)

This Regulation governs jurisdiction, recognition, and enforcement of judgments in matrimonial matters and matters of parental responsibility. It entered into application on August 1, 2022, replacing the Brussels II bis Regulation. Key provisions include:

- Seven grounds of jurisdiction for divorce based on habitual residence and nationality (Article 3)
- Automatic recognition of divorce judgments across member states without special procedures (Article 30)
- Residual jurisdiction rules for cases with connections outside the EU (Article 6)

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Johnston v. Ireland (Application No. 9697/82, December 18, 1986):** The Court held that Article 12 ECHR does not encompass a right to divorce, finding that Ireland's constitutional prohibition of divorce did not violate the Convention. However, the failure to provide any legal framework for family life after separation violated Article 8.

**Airey v. Ireland (Application No. 6289/73, October 9, 1979):** The Court found a violation of Article 6 where the applicant could not obtain access to judicial separation proceedings due to the absence of legal aid, establishing that states must provide effective access to family proceedings.

**Babiarz v. Poland (Application No. 1955/10, January 10, 2017):** The Court found no violation of Article 8 or Article 12 in Poland's refusal to grant a divorce to the "solely guilty" spouse, holding that states enjoy a wide margin of appreciation in regulating divorce.

### EU Fundamental Rights

Article 9 of the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights provides that the right to marry and found a family shall be guaranteed in accordance with national laws. Unlike Article 12 ECHR, Article 9 does not reference "men and women," potentially encompassing same-sex marriage. Neither Article 12 ECHR nor Article 9 of the Charter includes an explicit right to divorce.

## Comparative Analysis

### Fault vs. No-Fault

The jurisdictions represent different points on the spectrum from pure fault-based to pure no-fault divorce. South Korea retains significant fault elements through Article 840's enumerated grounds and the restriction on at-fault spouse divorce petitions. The United States has moved almost entirely to no-fault, though fault grounds survive in many states. Germany employs a pure breakdown system with no consideration of fault. The ECHR framework leaves this choice to member states within a wide margin of appreciation.

### Mandatory Reflection and Counseling

South Korea's mandatory counseling and reflection period (2007) stands out as an innovative approach to balancing divorce accessibility with family preservation. Germany achieves a similar cooling-off effect through the separation year requirement. The United States generally does not require counseling, except in covenant marriage states. The EU does not harmonize counseling or reflection requirements.

### Property and Financial Consequences

Germany's system is the most elaborate, with three distinct mechanisms: property division (Zugewinnausgleich), spousal maintenance (Unterhalt), and pension equalization (Versorgungsausgleich). South Korea provides property division and consolation money (위자료) but lacks a mandatory pension equalization system. The United States varies enormously by state, with community property states (9 states) dividing marital property equally and equitable distribution states dividing property "equitably."

### Procedural Requirements

Germany imposes the most stringent procedural requirements, including mandatory legal representation and the Verbund principle requiring joint resolution of divorce and pension equalization. South Korea requires personal appearance and court confirmation. The United States permits uncontested divorces by mail or online in many states, and some states have adopted simplified dissolution procedures for short marriages without children or significant assets.
