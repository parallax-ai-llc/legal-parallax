## Summary

Student rights -- the legal protections afforded to individuals enrolled in educational institutions regarding discipline, expression, privacy, and due process -- occupy a unique position in law, balancing the educational institution's need for order and its in loco parentis authority against the individual rights of students as persons entitled to constitutional and statutory protections. South Korea enacted the Student Human Rights Ordinances (학생인권조례) at the metropolitan and provincial level beginning with Gyeonggi Province in 2010 and Seoul in 2012, establishing comprehensive protections including the right to freedom from corporal punishment (체벌), freedom of expression and assembly, protection of personal appearance (hair, clothing), and non-discrimination, though these ordinances have been controversial and several have faced legal challenges -- notably, the Seoul Student Human Rights Ordinance was invalidated by the Supreme Court in 2024 on procedural grounds, while the broader debate on student rights intersects with the teacher rights crisis prompted by multiple teacher suicides in 2023 leading to the enactment of the Act on Protection of Teachers' Educational Activities. The United States has developed student rights primarily through constitutional litigation, with the Supreme Court's landmark decisions in Tinker v. Des Moines Independent Community School District (1969) establishing that students do not "shed their constitutional rights at the schoolhouse gate," Goss v. Lopez (1975) guaranteeing due process in school suspensions, and the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act (FERPA, 1974) protecting student educational records, though subsequent decisions in Bethel School District v. Fraser (1986), Hazelwood School District v. Kuhlmeier (1988), and Morse v. Frederick (2007) have significantly narrowed student speech protections. Germany protects student rights through the Grundgesetz (Basic Law) and state education acts (Schulgesetze der Länder), with the Federal Constitutional Court recognizing students as holders of fundamental rights including personal development (Article 2(1) GG), human dignity (Article 1(1) GG), and religious freedom (Article 4 GG), while the sixteen Länder maintain detailed disciplinary procedures in their respective school laws. At the European level, the European Convention on Human Rights and Protocol 1 Article 2 (right to education), the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child (Articles 12, 28, 29), and the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights (Article 14) establish a framework of educational rights that the European Court of Human Rights has interpreted to include procedural protections against expulsion and the right to education consistent with parental convictions.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Student rights in South Korea are primarily regulated through two levels: national legislation (Elementary and Secondary Education Act, 초·중등교육법) and metropolitan/provincial student human rights ordinances (학생인권조례).

**Elementary and Secondary Education Act (초·중등교육법):** Article 18-4, added in 2007 and amended in 2011, provides that schools shall protect students' human rights and prohibits corporal punishment that involves direct physical force against students. The Act delegates implementation details to presidential decrees and local education office regulations.

### Student Human Rights Ordinances (학생인권조례)

Student human rights ordinances have been enacted by several metropolitan and provincial education offices:

| Jurisdiction | Enactment Year | Key Features |
|-------------|---------------|-------------|
| Gyeonggi Province | 2010 | First ordinance; comprehensive rights catalogue |
| Gwangju Metropolitan City | 2011 | Protection from discrimination; freedom of expression |
| Seoul Metropolitan City | 2012 | Hair/appearance freedom; assembly rights; anti-discrimination |
| North Chungcheong Province | 2012 | Similar to Seoul model |
| Jeonbuk Province | 2013 | Comprehensive student rights protections |

**Rights Typically Protected:**
- Freedom from corporal punishment (체벌 금지)
- Freedom of personal appearance (hair length, style, accessories)
- Freedom of expression and assembly
- Right to participate in school governance
- Right to privacy (personal belongings, communications)
- Non-discrimination (gender, disability, family background, sexual orientation in some ordinances)
- Right to rest and leisure

### 2024 Supreme Court Decision on Seoul Ordinance

In 2024, the Supreme Court of Korea invalidated the Seoul Student Human Rights Ordinance, finding that the Seoul Metropolitan Office of Education exceeded its delegated legislative authority. The Court held that certain provisions -- particularly the prohibition on discrimination based on sexual orientation and gender identity -- exceeded the scope of education-related regulation authorized by the Elementary and Secondary Education Act. This decision did not reach the substantive merits of student rights protections but rather addressed the procedural question of ordinance-making authority.

### Teacher Rights Crisis (2023)

The student rights framework in South Korea cannot be understood without reference to the 2023 teacher rights crisis. A series of teacher suicides in 2023, most notably the death of a young elementary school teacher in Seocho-gu, Seoul, in July 2023, catalyzed a national debate about the balance between student (and parent) rights and teacher authority. Teachers protested that student rights ordinances, combined with aggressive parental complaints (악성 민원), had made it impossible to maintain classroom discipline. In response, the National Assembly enacted the Act on Protection of Teachers' Educational Activities (교원의 지위 향상 및 교육활동 보호를 위한 특별법 개정), strengthening protections for teachers against frivolous complaints and physical or verbal abuse by students and parents.

### Corporal Punishment

South Korea prohibited direct physical corporal punishment in schools through the 2011 amendment to the Enforcement Decree of the Elementary and Secondary Education Act. Prior to this, corporal punishment was widely practiced and culturally accepted. The prohibition distinguishes between direct physical punishment (직접체벌, prohibited) and indirect disciplinary measures such as standing in the hall or cleaning duties (간접체벌, permitted under reasonable conditions). Enforcement remains uneven, and surveys indicate that corporal punishment persists in some schools despite the prohibition.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Student rights in the United States derive primarily from constitutional provisions as interpreted by the Supreme Court, supplemented by federal statutes.

### Landmark Supreme Court Decisions

**Tinker v. Des Moines Independent Community School District, 393 U.S. 503 (1969):** Students wore black armbands to protest the Vietnam War and were suspended. The Court held that students retain First Amendment rights in school, protected unless the expression "materially and substantially interfere[s] with the requirements of appropriate discipline in the operation of the school." This case established the foundational principle that students are "persons" under the Constitution with rights that schools must respect.

**Goss v. Lopez, 419 U.S. 565 (1975):** Students were suspended without a hearing. The Court held that students facing suspension of 10 days or less have a property interest in education and a liberty interest in reputation, requiring at minimum oral or written notice of charges and an opportunity to present their side. Longer suspensions may require more elaborate procedures.

**New Jersey v. T.L.O., 469 U.S. 325 (1985):** The Court held that school officials may search students under a "reasonable suspicion" standard rather than the probable cause required of law enforcement. The search must be justified at its inception and reasonably related in scope to the circumstances.

**Bethel School District v. Fraser, 478 U.S. 675 (1986):** The Court upheld discipline for sexually suggestive speech at a school assembly, holding that schools may prohibit vulgar and lewd speech inconsistent with the "fundamental values of public school education."

**Hazelwood School District v. Kuhlmeier, 484 U.S. 260 (1988):** The Court held that school-sponsored speech (such as a school newspaper) may be regulated by educators if the regulation is "reasonably related to legitimate pedagogical concerns."

**Morse v. Frederick, 551 U.S. 393 (2007):** The Court upheld discipline for a student who displayed a banner reading "BONG HiTS 4 JESUS" at a school-supervised event, holding that schools may restrict student expression that can reasonably be regarded as promoting illegal drug use.

**Mahanoy Area School District v. B.L., 594 U.S. ___ (2021):** The Court held that a student's vulgar off-campus social media post could not be punished by the school, though it declined to establish a bright-line rule and held that schools retain some authority to regulate off-campus speech that materially disrupts school operations.

### Federal Statutes

| Statute | Year | Protection |
|---------|------|------------|
| FERPA | 1974 | Student educational records privacy; parental access rights |
| Title IX | 1972 | Prohibition of sex discrimination in federally funded education |
| IDEA | 1975 (reauthorized 2004) | Rights of students with disabilities to free appropriate public education |
| Section 504 of Rehabilitation Act | 1973 | Prohibits disability discrimination in federally funded programs |
| Equal Access Act | 1984 | Student religious and political groups equal access to school facilities |

### Corporal Punishment

The Supreme Court held in Ingraham v. Wright, 430 U.S. 651 (1977), that corporal punishment in public schools does not violate the Eighth Amendment's prohibition on cruel and unusual punishment and that no prior hearing is constitutionally required. As of 2026, approximately 17 states still permit corporal punishment in public schools, primarily in the South, though the practice has declined significantly.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Student rights in Germany are governed by the Grundgesetz (Basic Law) and the Schulgesetze (school laws) of the sixteen Länder. Article 7(1) GG places the entire school system under state supervision (Schulaufsicht), but this authority is limited by students' fundamental rights.

### Constitutional Protections

**Article 2(1) GG (Free Development of Personality):** Students have the right to develop their personality freely, limited only by the rights of others, the constitutional order, and moral law. This right has been invoked in cases concerning dress codes, hair regulations, and disciplinary measures.

**Article 1(1) GG (Human Dignity):** Protects students against degrading treatment, including corporal punishment and humiliating disciplinary measures.

**Article 5(1) GG (Freedom of Expression):** Students enjoy freedom of expression, including the right to publish school newspapers and express political views. This right may be limited by the school's educational mission and the need for orderly school operation.

**Article 4 GG (Religious Freedom):** The leading case is the Kopftuchverbot controversy. The Federal Constitutional Court in its 2003 Ludin decision (BVerfGE 108, 282) addressed teacher headscarves and held that a legislative basis was needed for prohibition. For students, the 2015 BVerfG headscarf decision (1 BvR 471/10) held that a blanket prohibition on teacher headscarves violated religious freedom, with implications for student religious expression as well.

### Corporal Punishment

Corporal punishment is prohibited in all German schools. The prohibition derives from multiple sources:
- Section 1631(2) BGB (amended 2000): "Children have a right to be raised without violence. Corporal punishment, psychological injuries, and other degrading measures are impermissible."
- All sixteen Länder school laws explicitly prohibit corporal punishment.
- The prohibition is backed by criminal law: teachers who use corporal punishment may be prosecuted for Körperverletzung (bodily harm) under Section 223 StGB.

### Disciplinary Procedures

Each Land's school law establishes detailed disciplinary procedures (Ordnungsmaßnahmen):

| Measure | Typical Procedure |
|---------|-----------------|
| Written reprimand (Verweis) | Notice to student and parents |
| Exclusion from class (Unterrichtsausschluss) | Up to 4 weeks; hearing required |
| Transfer to another class | Hearing required; parental notification |
| Transfer to another school (Umschulung) | Formal hearing; proportionality review; appeal right |
| Expulsion (Entlassung) | Only for most serious cases; full hearing; administrative court appeal |

The principle of proportionality (Verhältnismäßigkeit) governs all disciplinary measures. The administrative courts (Verwaltungsgerichte) exercise full judicial review over school disciplinary decisions, including review of the factual basis and the proportionality of the sanction.

### Student Participation (Schülervertretung)

All Länder school laws provide for student councils (Schülervertretungen) with elected representatives at the school, district, and state levels. Student representatives have the right to participate in school conferences (Schulkonferenzen) and to be heard on matters affecting students' interests, including school rules, disciplinary policies, and curriculum planning.

## ECHR & EU

### Protocol 1, Article 2 -- Right to Education

"No person shall be denied the right to education. In the exercise of any functions which it assumes in relation to education and to teaching, the State shall respect the right of parents to ensure such education and teaching in conformity with their own religious and philosophical convictions."

The ECHR has interpreted this provision broadly:

**Belgian Linguistics Case (1968):** The Court held that Article 2 guarantees a right of access to existing educational institutions, the right to an effective education, and official recognition of educational studies.

**Campbell and Cosans v. United Kingdom (1982):** The Court held that the threat of corporal punishment in Scottish schools violated the second sentence of Protocol 1, Article 2, as it was contrary to the parents' philosophical convictions against corporal punishment. This case was instrumental in the eventual abolition of corporal punishment in UK schools.

### Article 3 -- Prohibition of Degrading Treatment

**Costello-Roberts v. United Kingdom (1993):** The Court considered whether corporal punishment in a private school violated Article 3 but found that three strikes with a rubber-soled shoe did not reach the minimum level of severity. However, the case established that Article 3 applies to school discipline in both public and private schools.

**Tyrer v. United Kingdom (1978):** Although not a school case, this judgment holding that judicial corporal punishment on the Isle of Man violated Article 3 established principles applicable to school punishment.

### UN Convention on the Rights of the Child

The UNCRC (ratified by all four jurisdictions except the United States, which signed but did not ratify) provides:

**Article 12:** The right of the child to express views freely in all matters affecting the child and to have those views given due weight.

**Article 28:** The right to education, with states required to ensure that school discipline is administered in a manner consistent with the child's human dignity.

**Article 29:** Education shall be directed to the development of the child's personality, talents, and mental and physical abilities to their fullest potential.

### EU Charter of Fundamental Rights

**Article 14:** Right to education, including access to vocational and continuing training.

**Article 24:** Children's rights, including the right to express their views freely and the principle that the child's best interests shall be a primary consideration.

### GDPR and Student Data

The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) applies to the processing of student data by educational institutions. Article 8 establishes that for information society services offered directly to a child, consent must be given by the holder of parental responsibility where the child is below 16 years (member states may lower this to 13). Schools must comply with GDPR principles in handling student records, attendance data, disciplinary records, and educational assessments.

## Comparative Analysis

### Rights Source: Constitutional vs. Ordinance-Based

A fundamental structural difference concerns the source of student rights. In the United States and Germany, student rights derive from constitutional provisions interpreted by courts. In South Korea, the most detailed protections emerge from local ordinances -- a more politically vulnerable source, as demonstrated by the Supreme Court's invalidation of the Seoul ordinance. The ECHR provides a supranational floor through Protocol 1, Article 2, but leaves significant discretion to states.

### Corporal Punishment

The jurisdictions show a clear trend toward prohibition, though at different stages. Germany and South Korea have enacted comprehensive prohibitions. The ECHR has established that severe corporal punishment violates Article 3 and that the threat of corporal punishment may violate Protocol 1, Article 2. The United States remains an outlier, with the Supreme Court having declined to constitutionalize a prohibition and approximately 17 states still permitting the practice.

### Student Speech

American jurisprudence on student speech is the most developed, with a cascade of Supreme Court decisions establishing different standards for different contexts. The trajectory has been toward increasing school authority: from Tinker's robust protection to Morse's drug-promotion exception. German law applies general constitutional speech protections to the school context with proportionality analysis. South Korea's ordinance-based protections are broad in principle but limited in practice by cultural deference to educational authority.

### The Teacher-Student Rights Balance

South Korea's 2023 teacher rights crisis highlights a tension present in all jurisdictions: expanding student rights can constrain teacher authority, potentially undermining educational effectiveness. Germany addresses this through detailed statutory frameworks balancing rights and obligations. The United States relies on constitutional boundaries that limit both state power (protecting students) and student behavior (permitting reasonable regulation). The EU framework emphasizes the child's best interests as an organizing principle.
