## Summary

The right to strike -- the collective withholding of labor by workers to exert economic pressure on employers during labor disputes -- occupies a contested position in comparative labor law, ranging from constitutionally guaranteed fundamental right to judicially constrained economic tort. South Korea enshrines the right to collective action (단체행동권) in Article 33 of the Constitution (헌법 제33조), regulated through the Trade Union and Labor Relations Adjustment Act (노동조합 및 노동관계조정법, TULRAA), which requires a mandatory mediation period of 10 to 15 days before strikes, a strike ballot with majority approval of union membership, and maintains an "essential public services" (필수공익사업) designation for industries including railways, hospitals, water supply, electricity, and telecommunications where minimum service requirements apply and compulsory arbitration may be imposed; South Korea's strike activity remains among the most intense in the OECD, with approximately 1.2 million days lost to industrial action in 2023. The United States protects the right to strike under Section 7 of the National Labor Relations Act (NLRA) of 1935 but significantly constrains it through judicially developed doctrines and statutory amendments, including the employer's right to permanently replace economic strikers established in NLRB v. Mackay Radio & Telegraph Co., 304 U.S. 333 (1938), the Taft-Hartley Act's prohibition of secondary boycotts and jurisdictional strikes, and a near-total prohibition on public-sector strikes at both federal and state levels. Germany recognizes the right to strike as protected under Article 9(3) of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz) through judicial interpretation by the Bundesarbeitsgericht (Federal Labor Court) rather than by explicit statutory provision, subjecting strikes to the judicially developed principles of Verhältnismäßigkeit (proportionality), Tarifbezug (strikes must pursue collective agreement objectives), and Arbeitskampfparität (balance of power between labor and management); Germany's ultima ratio principle requires that strikes may only occur after all negotiation and mediation options have been exhausted. At the European level, the right to strike is recognized under Article 6(4) of the European Social Charter, Article 28 of the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights, and the ECHR through Article 11 as interpreted in Enerji Yapı-Yol Sen v. Turkey (2009), while the CJEU's Viking and Laval decisions controversially balanced strike action against EU economic freedoms.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

The right to collective action is constitutionally guaranteed under Article 33(1) of the Constitution: "Workers shall have the right to independent association, collective bargaining, and collective action." The procedural and substantive requirements for lawful strikes are set out in TULRAA, particularly Articles 37 through 46.

### Requirements for Lawful Strikes

**Mandatory Mediation (Article 45):** Before a union may commence a strike, the dispute must be referred to the Labor Relations Commission for mediation. The mediation period is 10 days for general industries and 15 days for essential public services. Only after mediation fails or the mediation period expires may a strike proceed.

**Strike Ballot (Article 41):** A strike requires approval by a direct, secret, anonymous ballot of a majority of the union's membership (not merely a majority of those voting). This high threshold has been a subject of significant debate, as it makes legal strikes more difficult to initiate than in many other jurisdictions.

**Prohibition of Violence and Destruction (Article 38):** Strike activity must be peaceful. Acts of violence, destruction of property, or occupation of employer facilities are prohibited and may result in criminal prosecution.

### Essential Public Services (필수공익사업)

Article 71 designates certain industries as essential public services where special restrictions apply:

| Designated Services | Special Requirements |
|--------------------|---------------------|
| Railways and urban transit | Minimum service maintenance required |
| Water, electricity, gas supply | Emergency staffing levels must be maintained |
| Hospitals | Critical patient care must continue |
| Telecommunications | Core infrastructure must be maintained |
| Bank of Korea | Financial system stability |
| Air traffic control | Safety services maintained |

The Essential Services Commission (필수유지업무위원회) determines minimum service levels. If parties cannot agree on minimum service staffing, the Labor Relations Commission makes a binding determination. Failure to maintain minimum service may result in compulsory arbitration.

### Replacement Workers

Article 43(1) prohibits employers from using replacement workers during a strike -- a protection significantly stronger than in the United States. Article 43(2) further prohibits the employer from contracting out struck work to outside entities. These prohibitions were reaffirmed and strengthened in the 2006 amendment.

### Criminal Liability

Article 314 of the Criminal Act (형법) criminalizes "obstruction of business" (업무방해), which has been controversially applied to strike activity. Courts have used this provision to impose criminal penalties on union leaders for strikes involving workplace occupations, even where no violence occurred. This practice has drawn sustained criticism from the ILO Committee on Freedom of Association, which has repeatedly called on South Korea to cease applying criminal obstruction charges to peaceful industrial action.

### Notable Strikes

The 2022-2023 truckers' strike (화물연대 파업), led by the Cargo Truckers Solidarity Union, resulted in the government invoking its first-ever "start-work" order (업무개시명령) under the Trucking Transport Business Act, directing strikers to return to work under threat of license revocation. This executive intervention was criticized by labor organizations as undermining the constitutional right to strike.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Section 7 of the NLRA guarantees employees "the right to self-organization, to form, join, or assist labor organizations, to bargain collectively through representatives of their own choosing, and to engage in other concerted activities for the purpose of collective bargaining or other mutual aid or protection." The right to strike is derived from this provision but is subject to extensive limitations.

### Categories of Strikes

| Strike Type | Legal Status | Replacement Workers |
|------------|-------------|-------------------|
| Economic strikes (wages, hours, conditions) | Protected | Permanent replacement permitted (Mackay Radio) |
| Unfair labor practice strikes | Protected | Only temporary replacement; strikers must be reinstated |
| Sympathy/secondary strikes | Prohibited (§ 8(b)(4)) | N/A |
| Jurisdictional strikes | Prohibited (§ 8(b)(4)(D)) | N/A |
| Wildcat strikes (unauthorized) | Unprotected | Discharge permitted |
| Sit-down strikes (workplace occupation) | Unprotected | Discharge permitted |

### Permanent Replacement Doctrine

The Supreme Court's decision in NLRB v. Mackay Radio & Telegraph Co., 304 U.S. 333 (1938), established that employers may permanently replace economic strikers -- that is, hire new workers to fill strikers' positions on a permanent basis. While permanently replaced strikers are not "discharged" and retain certain preferential rehire rights, the practical effect is to allow employers to eliminate strikers' jobs. This doctrine significantly weakens the economic leverage of strikes and has no parallel in other developed democracies. The Protecting the Right to Organize (PRO) Act, which would have prohibited permanent replacement, passed the House in 2021 and 2022 but was not enacted.

### Public Sector

Federal employees are prohibited from striking under 5 U.S.C. Section 7311 and Executive Order 10988. President Ronald Reagan's firing of 11,345 striking air traffic controllers (PATCO strike) on August 5, 1981, remains the most significant enforcement action. Most states prohibit public employee strikes, with penalties ranging from fines to automatic termination. A handful of states (including Alaska, California, Hawaii, Illinois, Montana, Ohio, Oregon, Pennsylvania, and Vermont) permit some categories of public employees to strike, typically excluding law enforcement, firefighters, and essential service workers.

### Cooling-Off Periods

The Railway Labor Act (RLA) of 1926, governing railroads and airlines, imposes extensive pre-strike procedures including mediation by the National Mediation Board and a 30-day cooling-off period. The President may appoint a Presidential Emergency Board to investigate and recommend settlement terms. Under the Taft-Hartley Act Section 206, the President may obtain an 80-day injunction against strikes that "imperil the national health or safety."

## Germany

### Legal Framework

The right to strike in Germany is not codified in any statute. It is derived from the Federal Labor Court's (Bundesarbeitsgericht, BAG) interpretation of Article 9(3) of the Basic Law, which protects Koalitionsfreiheit (freedom of association) including the right to take Arbeitskampfmaßnahmen (industrial action measures). This entirely judge-made framework establishes detailed requirements through case law.

### Judicially Developed Principles

**Tarifbezug (Collective Agreement Nexus):** Strikes may only be called to achieve objectives that can be regulated in a collective agreement (Tarifvertrag). Political strikes -- strikes aimed at influencing government policy rather than achieving contractual terms -- are unlawful. This principle was established in the BAG's 1955 Schleswig-Holstein Metal Workers decision.

**Friedenspflicht (Peace Obligation):** During the term of a valid collective agreement, both parties are bound by a peace obligation (Friedenspflicht) prohibiting strikes and lockouts on matters covered by the agreement. Strikes may only occur after the agreement expires or regarding matters not covered by the agreement.

**Ultima Ratio (Last Resort):** Strikes are permissible only after all negotiation and mediation possibilities have been exhausted. The BAG has interpreted this to mean that the union must have genuinely attempted to reach agreement through negotiation before resorting to industrial action.

**Verhältnismäßigkeit (Proportionality):** Strike action must be proportionate to the objective pursued. Excessive or disproportionate strikes -- for example, indefinite general strikes over minor contractual demands -- may be found unlawful. The proportionality assessment considers the nature of the dispute, the duration and scope of the strike, and its effects on third parties.

**Arbeitskampfparität (Balance of Industrial Action):** The legal framework must maintain a rough balance of power between unions' right to strike and employers' right to lock out. This principle guides the BAG's assessment of the legality of both offensive and defensive measures.

### Strike Ballots

Unlike in South Korea, Germany has no statutory requirement for a strike ballot. However, individual union constitutions (Satzungen) typically require a ballot with a supermajority threshold (usually 75 percent) before calling a strike. These internal requirements are binding on the union but are not subject to judicial review regarding the technical conduct of the ballot.

### Lockouts

The BAG recognizes employers' right to lock out workers as a defensive measure (Abwehraussperrung). However, lockouts must be proportionate and are generally restricted to defensive responses to ongoing strikes. Purely offensive lockouts are considered disproportionate and unlawful.

### Beamte (Civil Servants)

Civil servants (Beamte) are categorically prohibited from striking under the traditional principles of the Berufsbeamtentum (professional civil service), confirmed by the Bundesverfassungsgericht in BVerfGE 148, 296 (2018). The Court held that the strike prohibition for Beamte is a constitutional principle (hergebrachter Grundsatz des Berufsbeamtentums) under Article 33(5) GG, even considering ECHR Article 11 jurisprudence. Public employees who are not Beamte (Tarifbeschäftigte) have the full right to strike.

### Notable Recent Strikes

The 2023-2024 wave of strikes in Germany included unprecedented action by the Gewerkschaft Deutscher Lokomotivführer (GDL) against Deutsche Bahn, involving multiple rounds of strikes lasting up to five consecutive days -- testing the boundaries of proportionality. ver.di's 2023 public transport strike, coordinated with the climate movement's Fridays for Future protest, raised questions about the permissibility of "mixed" strikes combining trade-union and political objectives.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Article 11

**Enerji Yapı-Yol Sen v. Turkey (Application No. 68959/01, Judgment of April 21, 2009):** The Court found that Turkey's blanket prohibition on public servants' participation in a one-day national strike violated Article 11. The judgment established that the right to strike is an essential element of freedom of association under Article 11, though it may be subject to restrictions that are "necessary in a democratic society."

**Demir and Baykara v. Turkey (2008):** While primarily addressing collective bargaining rights, the Grand Chamber's expansive interpretation of Article 11 incorporated international labor standards, including ILO Conventions and the European Social Charter, into the assessment of Article 11 obligations.

**RMT v. United Kingdom (2014):** The Court held that the UK's statutory ban on secondary (solidarity) strikes did not violate Article 11, finding it within the state's margin of appreciation. The judgment confirmed that while the right to strike is protected, states retain significant discretion in regulating its exercise.

### European Social Charter

Article 6(4) of the European Social Charter explicitly recognizes "the right of workers and employers to collective action in cases of conflicts of interest, including the right to strike, subject to obligations that might arise out of collective agreements previously entered into." The European Committee of Social Rights has issued numerous decisions finding states in violation for excessive restrictions on strike action, including blanket bans on strikes in essential services without adequate alternative protections.

### EU Charter and CJEU

Article 28 of the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights recognizes the right to take collective action, including strike action. However, the CJEU's Viking (C-438/05) and Laval (C-341/05) decisions in 2007 held that the right to strike, while a fundamental right, must be balanced against EU economic freedoms (freedom of establishment, free provision of services) and is subject to a proportionality test. These decisions provoked intense academic and political debate, with critics arguing they subordinated social rights to market freedoms.

The European Parliament adopted a resolution on January 15, 2014, calling for a Social Progress Protocol to be attached to the Treaties, clarifying that economic freedoms cannot take precedence over fundamental social rights, including the right to strike. However, no such Protocol has been adopted.

### ILO Standards

The ILO Committee on Freedom of Association has developed an extensive body of principles on the right to strike, derived from ILO Conventions Nos. 87 and 98. Key principles include: the right to strike is a fundamental right of workers and their organizations; it may be restricted only for essential services in the strict sense (services whose interruption would endanger the life, safety, or health of the whole or part of the population); minimum service requirements are permissible as an alternative to complete strike bans; and the determination of essential services should involve worker representatives.

## Comparative Analysis

### Constitutional Recognition

South Korea provides the strongest constitutional protection with an explicit right to collective action. Germany derives the right from the broader freedom of association through judicial interpretation. The United States provides only statutory protection, with no constitutional guarantee of the right to strike. The ECHR recognizes the right under Article 11 but affords states a wide margin of appreciation.

### Permanent Replacement

The sharpest divergence is the U.S. Mackay Radio doctrine permitting permanent replacement of economic strikers -- a practice without parallel in any other developed democracy. South Korea explicitly prohibits replacement workers. Germany's proportionality framework effectively precludes permanent replacement. This difference fundamentally shapes the economic leverage available to striking workers.

### Essential Services

All jurisdictions restrict strikes in essential services, but definitions and mechanisms differ. South Korea uses a statutory enumeration with minimum service requirements. The United States largely prohibits public-sector strikes. Germany distinguishes between Beamte (no strike right) and public employees (full right). The ILO's narrow definition of essential services (limited to life-safety services) is more restrictive than any of the three national approaches.

### Political Strikes

All three domestic jurisdictions prohibit purely political strikes, though the boundaries are contested. Germany's ver.di/Fridays for Future coordination tested the limits of "mixed" actions. The European Social Charter provides more expansive protection for political demonstrations by workers than national systems.
