## Summary

The right to privacy -- encompassing protections against governmental surveillance, data collection, and intrusions into personal life, as well as informational self-determination and data protection -- has evolved from a largely judge-made concept to a comprehensive regulatory framework across major legal systems, though the scope and mechanisms of protection vary dramatically. South Korea protects privacy through Article 17 of the Constitution (대한민국 헌법 제17조), which guarantees the right to privacy, and has enacted one of the world's most comprehensive data protection regimes through the Personal Information Protection Act (개인정보 보호법, PIPA), enacted in 2011 and substantially amended in 2020 and 2023, with the Personal Information Protection Commission (개인정보보호위원회, PIPC) serving as an independent supervisory authority with enforcement powers including administrative fines of up to three percent of relevant revenue. The United States lacks a comprehensive federal privacy law but protects privacy through a patchwork of constitutional provisions (the Fourth Amendment's prohibition on unreasonable searches, the penumbral right to privacy recognized in Griswold v. Connecticut (1965)), sector-specific federal statutes (HIPAA, FERPA, COPPA, the FTC Act), and increasingly robust state privacy legislation led by the California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) as amended by the California Privacy Rights Act (CPRA). Germany has been a global pioneer in data protection, with the state of Hesse enacting the world's first data protection law in 1970, and the Federal Constitutional Court establishing the constitutional right to informational self-determination (Recht auf informationelle Selbstbestimmung) in the Census decision (BVerfGE 65, 1 (1983)), now complemented by the EU General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR, Regulation 2016/679) as implemented through the Federal Data Protection Act (Bundesdatenschutzgesetz, BDSG). The European framework provides the most comprehensive privacy protection through Article 8 ECHR (right to respect for private life), Article 7 and 8 of the EU Charter (private life and data protection respectively), and the GDPR, which has become a global standard-setter since its application began on May 25, 2018.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Article 17 of the Constitution provides: "The privacy of no citizen shall be infringed." Article 18 adds: "The privacy of correspondence of no citizen shall be infringed." The Constitutional Court has interpreted these provisions to encompass both a negative right against governmental intrusion and a positive dimension requiring the state to protect privacy against private actors.

### Personal Information Protection Act (개인정보 보호법, PIPA)

PIPA, enacted in March 2011, is one of the most comprehensive data protection statutes globally. Key provisions include:

**Scope:** PIPA applies to all personal information processors (개인정보처리자), encompassing both public and private sector entities. Personal information is defined broadly as information pertaining to an identified or identifiable living individual.

**Consent Framework:** PIPA requires informed, specific consent for the collection and use of personal information, with separate consent required for the provision of personal information to third parties, cross-border transfers, and the processing of sensitive information.

**2020 Amendment:** The "Data Three Acts" amendment package of 2020 introduced the concept of pseudonymized data (가명정보), allowing pseudonymized data to be processed for statistical, scientific research, and public interest archiving purposes without individual consent. The amendment also consolidated supervisory authority in the PIPC.

**2023 Amendment:** Effective September 2023, PIPA was substantially revised to strengthen enforcement, increase the maximum administrative fine to three percent of relevant revenue, expand automated decision-making rights, and enhance cross-border data transfer mechanisms including adequacy assessments.

### Government Surveillance

The Protection of Communications Secrets Act (통신비밀보호법), enacted in 1993, regulates wiretapping and access to communications data. The Act requires judicial authorization for wiretapping but permits access to communications metadata (subscriber information, connection records) through administrative requests. The Constitutional Court has struck down provisions permitting overly broad seizure of communications data, including the 2018 decision invalidating the blanket requirement for telecommunications providers to retain all packet data.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States protects privacy through multiple constitutional and statutory sources without a single, comprehensive framework:

**Fourth Amendment:** Protects against "unreasonable searches and seizures" by the government. The Supreme Court has expanded this protection to encompass digital privacy, notably in Carpenter v. United States, 585 U.S. 296 (2018), which held that the government generally needs a warrant to access historical cell-site location information.

**Constitutional Right to Privacy:** In Griswold v. Connecticut, 381 U.S. 479 (1965), the Supreme Court recognized a constitutional right to privacy derived from the "penumbras" of the Bill of Rights. This line of jurisprudence was significantly narrowed by Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization, 597 U.S. 215 (2022), which overruled Roe v. Wade and held that the Constitution does not confer a right to abortion.

### State Privacy Laws

California has led state-level privacy regulation with the California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA, 2018) as amended by the California Privacy Rights Act (CPRA, 2020, effective January 2023). The CPRA establishes rights to know, delete, correct, and opt out of the sale or sharing of personal information, and created the California Privacy Protection Agency as a dedicated enforcement authority. As of 2026, comprehensive privacy laws have been enacted in at least 15 states including Virginia, Colorado, Connecticut, Utah, Texas, Oregon, Montana, Iowa, Indiana, Tennessee, Florida, Delaware, New Hampshire, New Jersey, and Maryland.

### Government Surveillance

The Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act (FISA, 1978) establishes a framework for intelligence surveillance, including the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court (FISC). Section 702 of FISA, enacted in 2008 and reauthorized in 2024, permits the collection of foreign intelligence information from non-U.S. persons located abroad through compelled assistance from electronic communication service providers. The Snowden disclosures of 2013 revealed the scope of NSA surveillance programs, leading to the USA FREEDOM Act of 2015, which ended bulk telephone metadata collection under Section 215.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

The Federal Constitutional Court derived the right to informational self-determination (Recht auf informationelle Selbstbestimmung) from Article 2(1) in conjunction with Article 1(1) of the Basic Law in the Census decision (Volkszählungsurteil, BVerfGE 65, 1 (1983)). This right empowers individuals to determine the disclosure and use of their personal data and requires any limitation to be based on a statute that serves a compelling public interest and satisfies the proportionality principle.

### GDPR and BDSG

The GDPR (Regulation 2016/679), directly applicable in Germany since May 25, 2018, provides the primary framework for data protection. The Federal Data Protection Act (Bundesdatenschutzgesetz, BDSG), re-enacted in 2017 to complement the GDPR, exercises the regulation's opening clauses in areas such as employment data processing, consent requirements for minors, and the appointment of data protection officers.

Germany's data protection supervisory structure comprises the Federal Commissioner for Data Protection and Freedom of Information (Bundesbeauftragter für den Datenschutz und die Informationsfreiheit, BfDI) and 16 state-level data protection authorities (Landesdatenschutzbeauftragte). Each authority exercises independent supervisory powers within its competence.

### Surveillance Jurisprudence

The Federal Constitutional Court has issued landmark rulings constraining government surveillance:

**Online Searches Decision (BVerfGE 120, 274 (2008)):** The Court recognized a new fundamental right to the confidentiality and integrity of information technology systems (Grundrecht auf Gewährleistung der Vertraulichkeit und Integrität informationstechnischer Systeme), requiring strict proportionality for government access to computers and digital devices.

**BND Surveillance Decision (1 BvR 2835/17, 2020):** The Court held that the surveillance of foreign-to-foreign telecommunications by the Federal Intelligence Service (Bundesnachrichtendienst, BND) must comply with fundamental rights under the Basic Law, extending constitutional protections to non-German persons abroad.

## ECHR & EU

### Article 8 ECHR and Data Protection

Article 8 ECHR protects the right to respect for private and family life, home, and correspondence. The ECtHR has interpreted this provision to encompass data protection, holding in numerous cases that the collection, storage, and use of personal data by public authorities constitutes an interference with Article 8 rights that must be justified under Article 8(2).

### Key ECHR Jurisprudence

**Klass and Others v. Germany (Application No. 5029/71, 1978):** The Court's first surveillance case held that secret surveillance measures are permissible under Article 8 only when they are strictly necessary for safeguarding democratic institutions, with adequate and effective safeguards against abuse.

**S. and Marper v. United Kingdom (Application No. 30562/04, Grand Chamber, 2008):** The Grand Chamber held that the blanket and indiscriminate retention of DNA profiles and fingerprints of persons who had been acquitted or had charges dropped violated Article 8, establishing that retention of biometric data must be proportionate and differentiated.

**Big Brother Watch and Others v. United Kingdom (Application No. 58170/13, Grand Chamber, 2021):** The Grand Chamber found that the UK's bulk interception regime violated Articles 8 and 10, establishing six minimum safeguards required for bulk interception programs.

### Schrems Decisions (CJEU)

**Schrems I (C-362/14, 2015):** The CJEU invalidated the EU-U.S. Safe Harbor framework, holding that the Commission had not adequately assessed whether the United States provided an adequate level of protection for personal data.

**Schrems II (C-311/18, 2020):** The CJEU invalidated the EU-U.S. Privacy Shield and imposed enhanced requirements for Standard Contractual Clauses, holding that U.S. surveillance laws, particularly Section 702 FISA and Executive Order 12333, did not provide EU citizens with adequate protection.

Following Schrems II, the EU-U.S. Data Privacy Framework was adopted by the European Commission's adequacy decision in July 2023, based on Executive Order 14086 and the establishment of the Data Protection Review Court.

## Comparative Analysis

### Comprehensive vs. Sectoral Approaches

The most fundamental structural difference lies between the comprehensive approach (South Korea, EU/Germany) and the sectoral approach (United States). Comprehensive regimes apply a single framework to all personal data processing, creating uniform protections but potentially imposing greater compliance burdens. The U.S. sectoral approach provides targeted protections for specific categories (health, finance, children) but leaves significant gaps in coverage.

### Enforcement Models

Enforcement mechanisms reflect different regulatory philosophies. The EU/Germany model relies on independent supervisory authorities with binding decision-making power and the ability to impose substantial administrative fines. South Korea has converged on this model through the strengthened PIPC. The United States relies primarily on the FTC's unfair or deceptive practices authority, supplemented by state attorneys general enforcement and private litigation, creating a more fragmented enforcement landscape.

### Government Surveillance Constraints

All four systems recognize that government surveillance must be subject to legal constraints, but the stringency varies. Germany's Constitutional Court has imposed the most demanding requirements through the Census decision, the Online Searches decision, and the BND surveillance ruling. The ECHR requires six minimum safeguards for bulk interception. The United States relies on FISA procedures and Fourth Amendment protections, but the scope of permissible foreign intelligence collection remains broader than in European systems. South Korea's surveillance framework provides judicial oversight for wiretapping but has been criticized for inadequate protections for metadata access.
