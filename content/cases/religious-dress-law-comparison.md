## Summary

Religious dress regulation -- the body of law governing the wearing of religiously mandated or inspired clothing, headcoverings, and symbols in public spaces, workplaces, and educational institutions -- has become one of the most contested areas of comparative human rights law, particularly in the context of Islamic headscarves, Sikh turbans, Jewish kippot, and Christian crosses. South Korea, operating under Article 20 of the Constitution (대한민국 헌법) guaranteeing religious freedom (종교의 자유) and Article 11 prohibiting discrimination, imposes no general restrictions on religious dress in public spaces, educational institutions, or government employment, with the Constitutional Court (헌법재판소) having addressed the issue only indirectly in cases concerning school uniform regulations and military dress codes, and the National Human Rights Commission (국가인권위원회) issuing recommendations against religious dress discrimination in employment contexts. The United States protects religious dress primarily through the Free Exercise Clause of the First Amendment, Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 (42 U.S.C. Section 2000e et seq.) requiring employers to provide reasonable accommodation for religious practices including dress, and the Religious Land Use and Institutionalized Persons Act (RLUIPA), with the Supreme Court's landmark decision in EEOC v. Abercrombie & Fitch Stores (2015) strengthening protections by holding that an employer may not refuse to hire an applicant to avoid accommodating religious dress. Germany has been a central battleground for religious dress litigation, with the Federal Constitutional Court's two headscarf decisions -- BVerfGE 108, 282 (2003, Ludin) and BVerfGE 138, 296 (2015) -- establishing that blanket bans on teacher headscarves violate Article 4 GG and require demonstration of a concrete danger to school peace or state neutrality. The European Court of Human Rights has addressed religious dress in numerous landmark cases including Sahin v. Turkey (2005), Dahlab v. Switzerland (2001), S.A.S. v. France (2014), and Osmanoğlu and Kocabaş v. Switzerland (2017), generally granting states a wide margin of appreciation while requiring proportionality analysis, leading to divergent outcomes that permit both French-style total bans and more accommodating approaches within the Convention framework.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea imposes no general restrictions on religious dress. The constitutional framework protects religious expression through multiple provisions:

**Article 20(1) (종교의 자유):** "All citizens shall enjoy freedom of religion." This encompasses the right to manifest religious belief through dress and appearance.

**Article 20(2) (정교분리):** The separation of church and state prevents the government from favoring or disfavoring particular religious expressions, including dress.

**Article 11(1) (평등원칙):** Prohibits discrimination on the basis of religion in all areas of public life.

**National Human Rights Commission Act (국가인권위원회법), Article 2(3):** Defines discriminatory acts to include unfavorable treatment based on religion, which the Commission has interpreted to encompass discrimination based on religious dress.

### National Human Rights Commission Recommendations

The National Human Rights Commission of Korea (국가인권위원회) has issued several recommendations addressing religious dress in employment:

**Recommendation 09-Jin-0008820 (2010):** The Commission recommended that a private employer rescind a workplace dress code that prohibited the wearing of hijab by a Muslim employee, finding that the policy constituted religious discrimination under the National Human Rights Commission Act.

**Recommendation 15-Jin-0756800 (2016):** The Commission addressed a case where a school prohibited a Muslim student from wearing hijab with the school uniform, recommending accommodation and finding that the school's uniform policy violated the student's religious freedom.

### Military Context

The Military Service Act (병역법) and military regulations prescribe standard military uniforms and grooming standards. The Korean military does not currently provide religious exemptions to uniform and grooming requirements, though religious practice is accommodated in other ways (chaplains, worship services). This has not generated significant litigation, partly because South Korea's Muslim population remains relatively small (approximately 200,000 persons).

### Employment Discrimination

The Equal Employment Opportunity and Work-Family Balance Assistance Act (남녀고용평등과 일·가정 양립 지원에 관한 법률) prohibits employment discrimination based on religion. Employers must demonstrate a bona fide occupational qualification to justify restrictions on religious dress. Court decisions on this issue remain limited due to the relatively low incidence of religious dress disputes in Korean workplaces.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States provides robust legal protections for religious dress through constitutional, statutory, and regulatory provisions.

**First Amendment Free Exercise Clause:** Protects religious dress from government restriction, subject to the neutral, generally applicable law standard from Employment Division v. Smith, 494 U.S. 872 (1990), supplemented by heightened protections under the Religious Freedom Restoration Act (RFRA, 42 U.S.C. Section 2000bb) for federal government actions.

**Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 (42 U.S.C. Section 2000e):** Prohibits employment discrimination based on religion and requires employers to provide reasonable accommodation for employees' religious practices, including religious dress, unless accommodation would cause undue hardship on the employer's business. The Supreme Court's decision in Groff v. DeJoy, 600 U.S. 447 (2023), raised the undue hardship standard from the de minimis threshold of Trans World Airlines v. Hardison (1977) to requiring the employer to demonstrate that accommodation would result in "substantial increased costs in relation to the conduct of its particular business."

**EEOC v. Abercrombie & Fitch Stores, Inc., 575 U.S. 768 (2015):** The Supreme Court held 8-1 that an employer violates Title VII when a religious practice is a motivating factor in an adverse employment decision, even if the employer lacks actual knowledge of the applicant's need for accommodation. Samantha Elauf, a Muslim applicant, was denied employment because her headscarf conflicted with the company's "Look Policy." The decision eliminated the requirement that the applicant explicitly request accommodation before the employer's obligation is triggered.

### Military Religious Dress

The Department of Defense has progressively liberalized religious dress accommodations. The Religious Liberty in the Armed Forces Act and subsequent DoD Instruction 1300.17 (updated 2023) establish that service members may request accommodation for religious apparel, including turbans, hijabs, beards, and unshorn hair. Accommodations are granted unless they create an identifiable military necessity concern. The U.S. Army has granted numerous religious dress accommodations for Sikh soldiers to wear turbans and maintain unshorn beards since 2017.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany has been a central arena for religious dress litigation, particularly regarding Islamic headscarves worn by public school teachers.

**Article 4 GG (Glaubensfreiheit):** "(1) Freedom of faith, of conscience, and freedom of professing a religious or philosophical creed shall be inviolable. (2) The undisturbed practice of religion shall be guaranteed." The Federal Constitutional Court has interpreted this provision as protecting the right to wear religiously motivated clothing.

### First Headscarf Decision -- BVerfGE 108, 282 (2003, Ludin)

Fereshta Ludin, a German-Afghan teacher, was denied appointment to the Baden-Württemberg civil service because she wore an Islamic headscarf. The Federal Constitutional Court held (5-3) that the denial violated her rights under Article 4 GG because no statutory basis existed for the restriction. The Court ruled that restricting religious dress of public school teachers requires a specific legislative foundation (Gesetzesvorbehalt) and cannot be imposed by administrative order alone. However, the Court left open the possibility that state legislatures could enact such restrictions.

### State Headscarf Laws

Following the Ludin decision, eight German states enacted legislation restricting religious symbols for teachers:

| State | Legislation | Scope |
|-------|------------|-------|
| Baden-Württemberg | School Act Section 38(2) (2004) | Banned "political, religious, ideological" demonstrations; exempted Christian-Western tradition |
| Bavaria | Education and Teaching Personnel Act Art. 59(2) (2004) | Similar exemption for Christian-Occidental values |
| North Rhine-Westphalia | School Act Section 57(4) (2006) | Banned religious expression contrary to constitutional values |
| Hesse | Civil Service Act Section 45 (2004) | Banned religious symbols undermining state neutrality |
| Lower Saxony | School Act Section 51(3) (2004) | Restricted religious dress of teachers |
| Saarland | School Governance Act (2004) | Restricted religious symbols for teachers |
| Berlin | Neutrality Act (Neutralitätsgesetz, 2005) | Banned all religious symbols for public employees in certain sectors |
| Bremen | School Act amendment (2005) | Restricted religious symbols for teachers |

### Second Headscarf Decision -- BVerfGE 138, 296 (2015)

The Federal Constitutional Court revisited the issue and struck down the North Rhine-Westphalia headscarf law, holding that:
- A blanket prohibition of headscarves for public school teachers, based merely on the abstract potential for conflict, violates Article 4 GG.
- Only a concrete, demonstrable danger to school peace or state neutrality (konkrete Gefahr) can justify restricting a teacher's religious dress.
- Laws that privilege Christian-Western dress traditions while restricting Islamic dress violate Article 3(3) GG (prohibition of religious discrimination) and Article 33(3) GG (equal access to public office).

This landmark decision effectively invalidated the Christian-privilege exemptions in several state laws and established that restrictions must be religion-neutral and based on demonstrated concrete danger.

### Federal Labor Court -- Muslim Headscarf in Private Employment

**BAG, Decision of August 27, 2020 (8 AZR 62/19):** Following the CJEU's decisions in Achbita and WABE (see below), the Federal Labor Court held that a private employer's dress code prohibiting conspicuous religious symbols can be justified as a genuine occupational requirement if the employer pursues a policy of political, philosophical, and religious neutrality and the policy is applied consistently.

## ECHR / EU

### ECHR Jurisprudence

The European Court of Human Rights has addressed religious dress in numerous cases, generally granting states a wide margin of appreciation.

**Dahlab v. Switzerland (Application No. 42393/98, Admissibility Decision, February 15, 2001):** The Court declared inadmissible the complaint of a Swiss primary school teacher prohibited from wearing an Islamic headscarf while teaching. The Court found that Switzerland's restriction pursued the legitimate aims of protecting the rights of others and public order, noting the potential "proselytising effect" of a headscarf worn by a teacher of young children.

**Sahin v. Turkey (Application No. 44774/98, Grand Chamber, November 10, 2005):** The Grand Chamber upheld Turkey's ban on Islamic headscarves at universities, finding no violation of Article 9. The Court emphasized the principle of secularism (laiklik) in the Turkish constitutional context and granted Turkey a wide margin of appreciation, holding that the ban was "necessary in a democratic society" for the protection of the rights and freedoms of others and the maintenance of public order.

**S.A.S. v. France (Application No. 43835/11, Grand Chamber, July 1, 2014):** The Grand Chamber upheld France's Law No. 2010-1192 prohibiting the concealment of the face in public places, finding no violation of Article 9. The Court accepted France's novel justification based on the concept of "living together" (vivre ensemble), holding that the state could consider full-face covering as undermining the minimum requirements of life in society.

**Belcacemi and Oussar v. Belgium (Application No. 37798/13, July 11, 2017):** The Court upheld Belgium's 2011 ban on full-face covering in public places, following the same reasoning as S.A.S. v. France.

**Osmanoğlu and Kocabaş v. Switzerland (Application No. 29086/12, January 10, 2017):** The Court upheld Switzerland's requirement that Muslim girls attend mixed swimming lessons, finding that the obligation served the purposes of integrating foreign nationals and socializing children according to local customs. The availability of a burkini as a compromise was noted approvingly.

**Hamidović v. Bosnia and Herzegovina (Application No. 57792/15, December 5, 2017):** The Court found a violation of Article 9 where a Muslim witness was sanctioned for refusing to remove his skullcap in court. The Court held that maintaining courtroom authority did not necessitate requiring the removal of discreet religious headcovering.

### CJEU -- Workplace Religious Dress

**Case C-157/15, Achbita v. G4S (CJEU, Grand Chamber, March 14, 2017):** The CJEU held that a private employer's internal rule prohibiting the visible wearing of any political, philosophical, or religious sign does not constitute direct discrimination under Directive 2000/78/EC. However, such a rule may constitute indirect discrimination if it places persons of a particular religion at a particular disadvantage, which must be objectively justified by a legitimate aim (pursuit of neutrality) and pursued through proportionate means.

**Joined Cases C-804/18 and C-341/19, IX v. WABE and MH Müller Handel (CJEU, Grand Chamber, July 15, 2021):** The CJEU refined the Achbita framework, holding that: (1) an employer's prohibition on conspicuous large-scale signs may constitute indirect discrimination that must be justified; (2) the employer must demonstrate a genuine need established by reference to customer expectations or adverse consequences; (3) a prohibition limited to conspicuous, large-scale signs of political, philosophical, or religious beliefs may be justified; but (4) national constitutional provisions protecting religious freedom may be considered in the proportionality analysis.

## Comparative Analysis

### Restrictive vs. Accommodative Approaches

The jurisdictions fall on a spectrum from highly accommodative (South Korea, United States) to context-dependent (Germany, ECHR). South Korea and the United States impose virtually no restrictions on religious dress in any context and affirmatively require accommodation in employment. Germany's position has evolved from permitting blanket bans to requiring concrete danger for restrictions on public employees, while permitting proportionate neutrality policies in private employment. The ECHR's margin of appreciation permits both France's broad public-space bans and more accommodating national approaches.

### Full-Face Covering

The full-face covering (niqab, burqa) is the most divisive issue. France and Belgium have enacted comprehensive public-space bans upheld by the ECHR. South Korea, the United States, and Germany have no such bans. The ECHR's acceptance of "living together" as a legitimate aim for face-covering bans has been criticized by some scholars and the Court's own dissenting judges as insufficiently grounded in established Convention jurisprudence.

### Public School Teachers

The treatment of teacher religious dress illustrates the sharpest divergence. The United States protects teacher religious dress under Title VII. Germany requires demonstrated concrete danger before restriction. The ECHR has upheld both permissive and restrictive approaches (Dahlab vs. post-2015 German standards). South Korea imposes no restrictions.

### Private Employment

All jurisdictions have converged on a framework that permits employer neutrality policies restricting religious dress in private employment, provided they are applied consistently, non-discriminatorily, and proportionately. However, the stringency of justification required varies: the U.S. Abercrombie standard is the most protective of employees, while the CJEU's Achbita/WABE framework gives employers more latitude.
