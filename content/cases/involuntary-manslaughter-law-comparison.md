## Summary

Involuntary manslaughter -- the unlawful killing of a human being without malice aforethought, typically resulting from criminal negligence, recklessness, or the commission of an unlawful act not amounting to a felony -- represents a critical category within homicide law that addresses killings where the perpetrator lacked the intent to cause death but bears sufficient culpability to warrant criminal punishment. South Korea addresses involuntary manslaughter primarily through Article 267 of the Criminal Act (형법), which provides for punishment of a person who "causes the death of another through negligence" (과실치사) with imprisonment of up to two years or a fine, and through Article 268, which enhances the penalty to up to five years for deaths caused by occupational negligence (업무상 과실치사) or gross negligence; Korean law does not employ the specific term "involuntary manslaughter" but achieves equivalent coverage through the negligent homicide and occupational negligent homicide categories, with the Supreme Court developing a substantial body of jurisprudence on the distinction between negligent killing and intentional killing through omission (부작위에 의한 살인). The United States recognizes involuntary manslaughter as a distinct grade of criminal homicide at both state and federal levels, with the Model Penal Code classifying negligent homicide (Section 210.4) and manslaughter through recklessness (Section 210.3) as separate offenses, while common law jurisdictions typically define involuntary manslaughter through two theories: criminally negligent conduct and the misdemeanor-manslaughter (unlawful act) doctrine; federal involuntary manslaughter under 18 U.S.C. Section 1112 carries penalties of up to eight years imprisonment. Germany addresses the equivalent concept through Section 222 StGB (fahrlässige Tötung, negligent killing), which provides for imprisonment of up to five years or a fine, without distinguishing between grades of unintentional killing; German courts draw a critical line between conscious negligence (bewusste Fahrlässigkeit) and conditional intent (bedingter Vorsatz/dolus eventualis), the latter of which elevates the offense to intentional homicide under Section 212 StGB. The ECHR has addressed involuntary manslaughter primarily through Article 2 (right to life), establishing that states bear positive obligations to investigate deaths resulting from negligence by state agents and to maintain adequate criminal law frameworks for negligent killings, as demonstrated in the Grand Chamber's judgment in Calvelli and Ciglio v. Italy (Application No. 32967/96, 2002), while EU law has increasingly mandated criminal penalties for negligent conduct causing death in specific sectors including environmental protection and workplace safety.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea does not use the term "involuntary manslaughter" (과실치사 translates directly as "negligent causing of death"), but its criminal law framework covers the same conceptual space through specific negligent homicide provisions.

**Article 267 -- Negligent Homicide (과실치사):** "A person who causes the death of another through negligence shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than 2 years or a fine not exceeding 7 million KRW." This provision covers ordinary negligence resulting in death.

**Article 268 -- Occupational/Gross Negligent Homicide (업무상 과실·중과실 치사상):** "A person who causes the death or bodily injury of another through occupational negligence (업무상 과실) or gross negligence (중과실) shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than 5 years or a fine not exceeding 20 million KRW."

**Article 250 -- Murder (살인):** For comparison, intentional homicide carries the death penalty, life imprisonment, or imprisonment of five or more years. The gap between negligent homicide (maximum 2-5 years) and intentional homicide (minimum 5 years) is significant.

### Distinction Between Negligent Homicide and Murder by Omission

Korean courts have developed jurisprudence on when failure to act transforms negligent homicide into intentional killing through omission (부작위에 의한 살인):

**Supreme Court Decision 2015도6809 (2015) -- Sewol Ferry Case (Captain):** The Gwangju High Court convicted Captain Lee Jun-seok of murder through abandonment rather than negligent homicide, holding that his deliberate failure to order evacuation while knowing passengers would drown constituted intentional killing through omission. The Supreme Court upheld this analysis, establishing that when an actor with a duty of care consciously decides not to act despite certain knowledge of fatal consequences, the offense transitions from negligence to intent.

**Supreme Court Decision 2002도995 (2002):** The Court addressed a case where a doctor failed to provide emergency treatment to a patient despite knowing the patient would die without treatment. The Court held that the doctor's deliberate failure to act, combined with awareness of the near-certainty of death, constituted murder through omission rather than negligent homicide.

### Traffic-Related Deaths

The Act on Special Cases Concerning the Settlement of Traffic Accidents (교통사고처리 특례법) provides special rules for vehicular negligence resulting in death:

| Circumstance | Legal Treatment |
|-------------|-----------------|
| Ordinary traffic negligence causing death | Criminal complaint possible; civil settlement may bar prosecution |
| DUI causing death | Art. 268 applies; enhanced penalties under Specific Crimes Aggravated Punishment Act |
| Hit-and-run causing death | Enhanced penalties; civil settlement does not bar prosecution |
| Violation of traffic signal causing death | Prosecution regardless of civil settlement |

### Statistics and Enforcement

According to the Korean National Police Agency, approximately 2,700-3,000 traffic fatalities occur annually in South Korea. Criminal prosecutions for vehicular negligent homicide have increased significantly since the 2018 amendment to the Specific Crimes Aggravated Punishment Act, which imposed mandatory minimum sentences for DUI causing death.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States recognizes involuntary manslaughter as a distinct grade of criminal homicide under both common law and statutory frameworks.

**Common Law Definition:** Involuntary manslaughter is an unintentional killing resulting from either: (1) criminal negligence (gross negligence), or (2) the commission of an unlawful act not amounting to a felony (misdemeanor-manslaughter rule).

**Model Penal Code Approach:** The MPC divides unintentional killings into two grades:
- **Section 210.3 -- Manslaughter:** A homicide committed recklessly (conscious disregard of a substantial and unjustifiable risk). A second-degree felony (up to 10 years).
- **Section 210.4 -- Negligent Homicide:** A homicide committed negligently (failure to be aware of a substantial and unjustifiable risk involving a gross deviation from reasonable care). A third-degree felony (up to 5 years).

**Federal Involuntary Manslaughter -- 18 U.S.C. Section 1112:** "(a) Manslaughter is the unlawful killing of a human being without malice. It is of two kinds: Voluntary -- Upon a sudden quarrel or heat of passion. Involuntary -- In the commission of an unlawful act not amounting to a felony, or in the commission in an unlawful manner, or without due caution and circumspection, of a lawful act which might produce death. (b) ... Whoever is guilty of involuntary manslaughter, shall be fined under this title or imprisoned not more than 8 years, or both."

### Misdemeanor-Manslaughter Rule

The misdemeanor-manslaughter (or unlawful act) doctrine holds that a death occurring during the commission of a misdemeanor or other non-felony unlawful act constitutes involuntary manslaughter. This doctrine operates as a lesser analog to the felony murder rule.

**Limitations:** Courts have imposed several limits:
- The unlawful act must be malum in se (inherently wrong) rather than malum prohibitum (wrong only because prohibited)
- There must be a causal connection between the unlawful act and the death
- Some jurisdictions require that the unlawful act be dangerous to human life

### Key Cases

**Commonwealth v. Welansky, 55 N.E.2d 902 (Mass. 1944):** The Cocoanut Grove nightclub fire case established the standard for criminal negligence in involuntary manslaughter: "wanton or reckless conduct" demonstrated by the operator's failure to maintain adequate fire safety measures, resulting in the deaths of 492 patrons. This case remains the leading precedent on criminally negligent involuntary manslaughter.

**People v. Watson, 30 Cal. 3d 290 (1981):** The California Supreme Court held that drunk driving resulting in death could support a second-degree murder charge (implied malice) rather than mere involuntary manslaughter, based on the defendant's awareness of the risk created by driving while intoxicated.

**United States v. Browner, 889 F.2d 549 (5th Cir. 1989):** The Fifth Circuit addressed the standard for federal involuntary manslaughter, holding that "without due caution and circumspection" requires proof of gross negligence rather than ordinary negligence.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

**Section 222 StGB -- Negligent Killing (Fahrlässige Tötung):** "Whoever causes the death of a person through negligence shall be punished with imprisonment for a term not exceeding five years or a fine" (Wer durch Fahrlässigkeit den Tod eines Menschen verursacht, wird mit Freiheitsstrafe bis zu fünf Jahren oder mit Geldstrafe bestraft).

Unlike the American and common law systems, German law does not distinguish between "involuntary manslaughter" and "negligent homicide." All unintentional killings resulting from negligence fall under Section 222.

### The Critical Boundary: Negligence vs. Conditional Intent

The most litigated question in German homicide law is the distinction between conscious negligence (bewusste Fahrlässigkeit) and conditional intent (bedingter Vorsatz/dolus eventualis):

**Conscious Negligence:** The actor recognizes the risk of death but trusts that it will not materialize. This falls under Section 222 (negligent killing, max 5 years).

**Conditional Intent (Dolus Eventualis):** The actor recognizes the risk of death and accepts it as a possible consequence of their conduct. This constitutes intentional homicide under Section 212 (Totschlag, 5-15 years) or potentially murder under Section 211 (Mord, life imprisonment).

**BGH, Judgment of March 1, 2018 (4 StR 399/17 -- Berliner Raser):** In the Berlin street racing case, the BGH upheld the murder conviction of a driver who killed a pedestrian during an illegal street race, finding conditional intent based on the driver's acceptance of the lethal risk. This landmark decision significantly expanded the scope of intentional homicide for reckless driving cases that would traditionally have been classified as negligent killing.

**BGH, Judgment of June 18, 2020 (4 StR 482/19):** The Court further refined the dolus eventualis analysis for traffic cases, establishing that the determination must consider the overall circumstances including speed, traffic conditions, the driver's perception of risk, and any self-endangering behavior.

### Causation and Objective Attribution

German law requires proof of causation through the theory of objective attribution (objektive Zurechnung):

**Conditio sine qua non:** But-for causation must be established.

**Objective attribution:** The harmful result must fall within the scope of the risk created by the negligent conduct. If the death would have occurred regardless of the negligent act, or resulted from an unforeseeable intervening cause, the death is not objectively attributable to the defendant.

**BGH, Judgment of November 20, 2008 (4 StR 328/08):** The Court held that a doctor who negligently failed to diagnose a terminal condition was not liable for negligent killing where the patient would have died regardless, because the death was not within the scope of the risk created by the misdiagnosis.

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR has established significant obligations regarding involuntary manslaughter through Article 2 (right to life):

**Substantive Obligation:** States must establish a criminal law framework that adequately deters and punishes negligent killings, particularly where state agents are responsible.

**Procedural Obligation:** States must conduct effective investigations into deaths resulting from negligence and ensure adequate accountability mechanisms.

**Calvelli and Ciglio v. Italy (Application No. 32967/96, Grand Chamber Judgment of January 17, 2002):** The Court held that the state's positive obligation under Article 2 requires an effective independent judicial system to establish the cause of death and hold those responsible accountable, including in cases of medical negligence. The Court noted that while the obligation does not necessarily require criminal proceedings in every case, the judicial system must at least provide an effective remedy.

**Silih v. Slovenia (Application No. 71463/01, Grand Chamber Judgment of April 9, 2009):** The Court established the "detachable obligation" doctrine, holding that the procedural obligation to investigate deaths (including negligent deaths) can be assessed independently of the substantive aspects of Article 2 and may extend to deaths occurring before a state's ratification of the Convention.

**Lopes de Sousa Fernandes v. Portugal (Application No. 56080/13, Grand Chamber Judgment of December 19, 2017):** The Court refined its medical negligence jurisprudence, distinguishing between cases involving systemic regulatory failures (which engage the substantive limb of Article 2) and individual medical negligence cases (which primarily engage the procedural limb).

### EU Framework

**Directive (EU) 2024/1226 on Environmental Crime:** Requires member states to criminalize environmental offenses committed with at least serious negligence when they cause death, establishing minimum penalty thresholds.

**Framework Directive 89/391/EEC on Workplace Safety:** Establishes employer obligations that, when violated negligently resulting in death, form the basis for criminal prosecution in most member states.

**Directive 2003/88/EC on Working Time:** Sets maximum working hours and rest periods; negligent violations contributing to fatal workplace accidents may support criminal liability.

## Comparative Analysis

### Grading of Unintentional Killings

The jurisdictions show distinct approaches to grading:

- **United States:** Most granular -- distinguishes between reckless manslaughter, negligent homicide, vehicular homicide, and various subcategories
- **South Korea:** Two-tier system -- ordinary negligent homicide (Art. 267) and occupational/gross negligent homicide (Art. 268)
- **Germany:** Single category -- all negligent killings under Section 222 with sentencing discretion
- **ECHR/EU:** No grading requirement; focus on adequacy of overall framework

### The Recklessness-Intent Boundary

All jurisdictions struggle with the boundary between extreme recklessness/negligence and intent:

- **United States:** Implied malice murder (e.g., Watson) for extreme recklessness
- **South Korea:** Murder through omission (부작위에 의한 살인) for deliberate failure to act
- **Germany:** Dolus eventualis (bedingter Vorsatz) for acceptance of fatal risk
- **ECHR:** No specific guidance; defers to national classification
