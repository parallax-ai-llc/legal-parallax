## Summary

Burglary — the unlawful entry into a structure with intent to commit a crime — has evolved dramatically from its common law origins as a nighttime offense against dwellings into a broad category of property crime prosecuted worldwide. South Korea's Criminal Act (형법) addresses burglary through a combination of residential trespass provisions (Articles 319-322) and compound larceny offenses (Articles 330-332), imposing penalties of up to 10 years for nighttime dwelling theft and enhanced sentences for habitual offenders. The United States treats burglary as a felony across all 50 states, with the Model Penal Code defining it as unlawful entry into a building or occupied structure with intent to commit a crime, and state penalties ranging from 1 year to life imprisonment depending on degree and aggravating factors. Germany's Strafgesetzbuch criminalizes residential burglary (Wohnungseinbruchdiebstahl) under Section 244, with a 2017 reform raising the minimum sentence for burglary of permanently occupied private dwellings to 1 year imprisonment. The European Court of Human Rights, while lacking direct criminal jurisdiction, imposes positive obligations on member states under Articles 8 and Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 to protect the right to home and property, and the EU Victims' Rights Directive 2012/29/EU establishes minimum standards for the treatment of burglary victims across member states.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korean criminal law addresses burglary through two intersecting sets of provisions in the Criminal Act (형법): residential trespass offenses (Chapter XXXVI, Articles 319-322) and compound larceny offenses involving dwelling intrusion (Chapter XXXVIII, Articles 330-332). Unlike common law systems that define burglary as a single unified offense, Korean law constructs the equivalent through the combination of trespass and theft elements.

**Residential Trespass Offenses (주거침입죄):**

| Article | Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Art. 319(1) | Intrusion upon Habitation (주거침입) — trespass into a dwelling, guarded building, ship, aircraft, or occupied room | Imprisonment up to 3 years or fine up to 5 million won |
| Art. 319(2) | Refusal to Leave (퇴거불응) — failure to comply with a request to leave | Same as Art. 319(1) |
| Art. 320 | Special Intrusion (특수주거침입) — trespass committed by group, by display of power, or while carrying dangerous objects | Imprisonment up to 5 years |
| Art. 321 | Unlawful Search (주거·신체 수색) — unauthorized search of a person's body, dwelling, or managed building | Imprisonment up to 3 years |
| Art. 322 | Attempt (미수) | Punishable under general attempt provisions |

**Compound Larceny Offenses (절도와 강도의 죄):**

| Article | Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Art. 329 | Basic Theft (절도) | Imprisonment up to 6 years or fine up to 10 million won |
| Art. 330 | Nighttime Dwelling Theft (야간주거침입절도) — theft by intrusion into a dwelling, guarded building, ship, or occupied room at night | Imprisonment up to 10 years |
| Art. 331 | Special Larceny (특수절도) — theft committed with deadly weapons, by two or more persons jointly, or by breaking barriers at night | Imprisonment 1-10 years |
| Art. 332 | Habitual Crimes (상습범) — habitual commission of offenses under Art. 329-331 | Enhanced penalties under the Act on Punishment of Specific Crimes |

### Key Principles

**Protected legal interest (보호법익):** The Korean Supreme Court has held that the protected interest in residential trespass is the de facto domestic peace (사실상의 주거의 평온) of all persons controlling or inhabiting the residence. This extends beyond property rights to encompass personal tranquility and security within the home.

**Nighttime element (야간):** Article 330 specifically targets theft committed through dwelling intrusion at night, reflecting the traditional view — shared with common law — that nighttime offenses against habitations carry heightened danger. Korean courts define "night" as the period from sunset to sunrise, and the nighttime element elevates what would otherwise be basic theft into a significantly more serious compound offense.

**Concurrent liability:** When a perpetrator commits theft after trespassing into a dwelling, Korean courts apply Article 330 (or 331) as a single compound offense rather than separately charging trespass and theft. However, if the intrusion is accompanied by additional violence or threats, robbery provisions (Articles 333 et seq.) may apply instead.

**Habitual offender treatment:** Article 332, combined with the Act on the Aggravated Punishment of Specific Crimes (특정범죄 가중처벌 등에 관한 법률), provides for significantly enhanced penalties for repeat burglary offenders. Courts have broad discretion in determining whether a pattern of behavior qualifies as "habitual."

### Notable Cases

The Korean Supreme Court in Decision 2009Do14376 addressed the scope of "dwelling" under Article 319, holding that the concept extends to enclosed yards (위요지) and appurtenant areas that are functionally part of a residence. In Decision 2021Do15507, the Court addressed whether entry through an unlocked door constitutes trespass, affirming that the absence of physical barriers does not negate the offense when entry is unauthorized and against the occupant's presumed will.

## United States

### Common Law Origins and Modern Evolution

At common law, burglary was narrowly defined by Lord Coke in 1641 as the "breaking and entering of the dwelling of another in the nighttime with the intent to commit a felony therein." This definition required all five elements: (1) breaking, (2) entering, (3) a dwelling, (4) nighttime commission, and (5) felonious intent. Modern American burglary law has dramatically expanded beyond each of these limitations.

### Model Penal Code (Section 221.1)

The American Law Institute's Model Penal Code defines burglary as entering a building or occupied structure, or separately secured portion thereof, with purpose to commit a crime therein, unless the premises are open to the public or the actor is licensed or privileged to enter. The MPC classifies burglary as follows:

- **Second-degree felony** if perpetrated in the dwelling of another at night
- **Third-degree felony** in all other cases (the default classification)
- The MPC affirmative defense applies if the building was abandoned

### State Law Variations

State legislatures have adopted diverse approaches to grading burglary offenses:

**California (Penal Code Sections 459-464):**
- First-degree burglary (residential): 2, 4, or 6 years in state prison; classified as a strike under the Three Strikes law
- Second-degree burglary (commercial): 16 months, 2, or 3 years; may be charged as a misdemeanor under Proposition 47 if the value of property is under $950
- Burglary with explosives: 3, 5, or 7 years

**New York (Penal Law Sections 140.20-140.30):**
- Burglary in the third degree (Class D felony): unlawful entry with intent to commit crime; up to 7 years
- Burglary in the second degree (Class C felony): dwelling entry, armed, or causes injury; up to 15 years
- Burglary in the first degree (Class B felony): armed with deadly weapon or causes physical injury in a dwelling; up to 25 years, mandatory minimum 3.5 years

**Texas (Penal Code Sections 30.02-30.05):**
- Burglary of a building (state jail felony): 180 days to 2 years
- Burglary of a habitation (second-degree felony): 2 to 20 years
- Burglary of a habitation with intent to commit felony other than theft (first-degree felony): 5 to 99 years or life

### Federal Treatment

While there is no general federal burglary statute, burglary is addressed in several federal contexts:
- **18 U.S.C. Section 924(e) — Armed Career Criminal Act (ACCA):** A conviction for "generic burglary" triggers a 15-year mandatory minimum for felons in possession of firearms
- **18 U.S.C. Section 2113 — Bank Burglary:** Entry into a bank or similar institution with intent to commit a felony carries up to 20 years imprisonment

### Landmark Cases

**Taylor v. United States, 495 U.S. 575 (1990):** The Supreme Court established the "generic" definition of burglary for federal sentencing purposes: unlawful or unprivileged entry into, or remaining in, a building or other structure, with intent to commit a crime. The Court adopted a categorical approach, comparing elements of the prior conviction with this generic definition rather than examining the underlying facts. This decision remains foundational for federal sentencing involving prior burglary convictions.

**United States v. Taylor, 596 U.S. 845 (2022):** The Supreme Court held that attempted Hobbs Act robbery does not qualify as a "crime of violence" under 18 U.S.C. Section 924(c)(3)(A), narrowing the scope of mandatory consecutive firearms sentences in federal robbery and burglary-related prosecutions.

**Descamps v. United States, 570 U.S. 254 (2013):** The Court clarified the application of the Taylor categorical approach, holding that sentencing courts may not apply the modified categorical approach to statutes that contain a single, indivisible set of elements broader than the generic offense.

**Mathis v. United States, 579 U.S. 500 (2016):** The Court further refined the categorical approach, distinguishing between statutory "elements" (which define separate crimes) and "means" (which describe alternative ways of committing a single crime), significantly affecting how prior burglary convictions are evaluated for sentence enhancement purposes.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German criminal law does not recognize "burglary" as a standalone offense equivalent to the common law concept. Instead, the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB) addresses burglary conduct primarily through an aggravated form of theft known as Wohnungseinbruchdiebstahl (residential burglary theft) under Section 244, which combines the elements of theft (Section 242) with unlawful entry into a dwelling.

**Section 242 — Basic Theft (Diebstahl):**
Whoever takes movable property belonging to another away from another with the intention of unlawfully appropriating it for himself or a third party shall be sentenced to imprisonment for not more than 5 years or to a fine.

**Section 243 — Theft in Especially Serious Cases (Besonders schwerer Fall des Diebstahls):**
An especially serious case of theft applies when the perpetrator breaks into, enters, or hides in a building, a service room, or a business premises, or uses a false key or other tool not intended for proper opening. Penalty: 3 months to 10 years imprisonment. Notably, this provision does not apply to theft of items of minor value (Geringwertigkeitsklausel).

**Section 244 — Theft with Weapons; Gang Theft; Residential Burglary Theft:**

| Subsection | Offense | Penalty |
|------------|---------|---------|
| Section 244(1) No. 1 | Theft while carrying a weapon or dangerous tool | 6 months to 10 years |
| Section 244(1) No. 2 | Theft as a member of an organized gang (Bande) that engages in continued theft | 6 months to 10 years |
| Section 244(1) No. 3 | Residential burglary theft (Wohnungseinbruchdiebstahl) — theft by breaking into, entering, or hiding in a dwelling | 6 months to 10 years |
| Section 244(3) | Less serious cases of the above | 3 months to 5 years |
| Section 244(4) | Private dwelling burglary — burglary of a permanently used private dwelling | 1 year to 10 years |

### The 2017 Reform

The 55th Act to Amend the Criminal Code, effective July 22, 2017 (BGBl. I S. 2442), significantly strengthened the penalties for residential burglary. The reform added Section 244(4), creating a separate qualified offense for burglary targeting permanently used private dwellings (dauerhaft genutzte Privatwohnung). This raised the minimum sentence from 6 months to 1 year, elevating the offense from a Vergehen (misdemeanor) to a Verbrechen (felony) in German legal classification. The reform also amended the Code of Criminal Procedure (StPO) to allow telecommunications surveillance (Section 100a) and source telecommunications surveillance for residential burglary investigations.

The legislative motivation cited the severe psychological impact on victims, noting that residential burglaries intrude particularly intensively into the private and intimate domestic sphere and can cause prolonged anxiety, sleep disturbances, and lasting psychological harm.

### Key Principles

**Dwelling requirement (Wohnung):** Section 244(1) No. 3 requires that the burglarized premises qualify as a "dwelling" (Wohnung) — a space that serves as the center of someone's private domestic life. Hotel rooms, vacation apartments, and mobile homes qualify; offices, warehouses, and purely commercial spaces do not. Section 244(4) further narrows this to "permanently used private dwellings."

**Means of entry:** The perpetrator must break in (einbrechen), climb in (einsteigen), enter with a false key (falscher Schluessel), or use another tool not intended for proper opening (anderes nicht zur ordnungsmaessigen Oeffnung bestimmtes Werkzeug), or hide within the dwelling (sich verbergen). The BGH has held that a key becomes "false" when it is no longer authorized for use by the entitled person — for example, a key retained by a former tenant.

**Prosecution as public offense (Offizialdelikt):** Residential burglary under Section 244 is prosecuted ex officio. Unlike basic theft (Section 242), which in certain circumstances requires a formal complaint (Strafantrag) from the victim, residential burglary is always pursued by the prosecution upon discovery.

### Notable Jurisprudence

The Bundesgerichtshof (BGH) has developed extensive case law interpreting Section 244. In BGH 2 StR 78/17, the court addressed the scope of "entering" (Einsteigen), holding that it requires entry through an opening not designated for regular access — climbing through a window qualifies, but walking through an open front door does not. In BGH 4 StR 300/18, the court clarified that a screwdriver used to force open a door qualifies as a tool "not intended for proper opening" under Section 244(1) No. 3.

## ECHR & EU

### European Convention on Human Rights

The European Convention on Human Rights does not directly define or criminalize burglary. However, several Convention provisions create a framework of obligations and protections that profoundly shape how member states legislate and adjudicate burglary offenses:

**Article 8 — Right to Respect for Private and Family Life and Home:**
"Everyone has the right to respect for his private and family life, his home and his correspondence." This provision is the most directly relevant to burglary, as it protects the inviolability of the home. The European Court of Human Rights has interpreted Article 8 as imposing positive obligations on states to protect individuals against interference with their homes by private actors, not only by state agents. States must maintain effective criminal law provisions to deter offenses against the home and must conduct effective investigations when such offenses occur.

**Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 — Protection of Property:**
"Every natural or legal person is entitled to the peaceful enjoyment of his possessions." This provision complements Article 8 by protecting the property rights implicated in burglary offenses. States must ensure adequate legal frameworks for the protection of property, including criminal sanctions for theft.

**Article 3 — Prohibition of Torture and Inhuman Treatment:**
Applies to the investigation and prosecution of burglary, requiring that suspects not be subjected to torture or inhuman treatment during interrogation or detention. The principle established in Gafgen v. Germany (2010) — that threats of torture during interrogation violate Article 3 regardless of the severity of the offense — applies equally to burglary investigations.

**Article 6 — Right to a Fair Trial:**
Guarantees fair trial protections for those accused of burglary, including the presumption of innocence, the right to legal counsel, and the right to examine witnesses.

### Positive Obligations Doctrine

The landmark case Osman v. United Kingdom (1998) established the general principle that states bear positive obligations to protect individuals from criminal acts by third parties when authorities know or ought to know of a real and immediate risk. While decided primarily under Article 2 (right to life), the reasoning extends to Article 8 and the protection of the home. States that systematically fail to investigate or prosecute burglaries could face liability under this framework.

### EU Victims' Rights Directive (2012/29/EU)

Directive 2012/29/EU of the European Parliament and of the Council of 25 October 2012 establishes minimum standards on the rights, support, and protection of victims of crime across all EU member states. Key provisions relevant to burglary victims include:

- **Right to information** (Article 4): Victims must be informed about available support services, complaint procedures, and the progress of their case
- **Right to support** (Article 8): Member states must ensure access to confidential victim support services, including emotional and psychological support
- **Right to participate in proceedings** (Articles 10-17): Victims have the right to be heard, to provide evidence, and to receive information about the offender's release
- **Individual assessment** (Article 22): Each victim must receive a timely individual assessment to identify specific protection needs, considering the nature and circumstances of the crime
- **Protection of victims with specific needs** (Article 23): Victims identified as having specific protection needs — which may include burglary victims suffering severe psychological trauma — are entitled to special measures during criminal proceedings

The Directive replaced the earlier Council Framework Decision 2001/220/JHA and was required to be transposed into national law by 16 November 2015. The European Commission has initiated infringement proceedings against member states that failed to fully implement its provisions.

### Relevant ECHR Case Law

**Osman v. United Kingdom (1998):** Established positive obligations on states to protect individuals from foreseeable criminal violence, creating a framework applicable to systematic failures in burglary prevention and investigation.

**Vinter v. United Kingdom (2013):** The Grand Chamber held that life imprisonment without any prospect of review violates Article 3, constraining member states that impose life sentences for aggravated burglary or related offenses.

**Blecic v. Croatia (2006):** The Grand Chamber addressed the termination of a tenancy following prolonged absence, with implications for the scope of "home" under Article 8. The Court clarified that Article 8 protection attaches to an established home regardless of the legality of the occupation, influencing how member states define the protected premises in burglary statutes.

**Surugiu v. Romania (2004):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where Romanian authorities failed to effectively investigate and prevent repeated acts of harassment and intrusion into the applicant's home by private individuals. This case directly supports the proposition that states bear positive obligations to protect the domestic sphere against criminal intrusion.

## Comparative Analysis

### Structural Differences

The four jurisdictions reveal fundamentally different approaches to defining and structuring burglary offenses. The United States and common law tradition treat burglary as a standalone inchoate-like offense — the crime is complete upon entry with criminal intent, regardless of whether the intended crime is actually committed. Germany, by contrast, defines residential burglary as an aggravated form of theft, requiring that the theft actually be carried out or at least attempted. South Korea takes a hybrid approach, maintaining separate trespass and theft provisions but recognizing compound offenses (Articles 330-331) that merge both elements.

### Protected Interests

All jurisdictions recognize that burglary offenses protect interests beyond mere property. South Korea explicitly identifies domestic peace (주거의 평온) as the protected legal interest. Germany's 2017 reform was motivated by recognition of the psychological harm inflicted by residential intrusion. The ECHR's Article 8 protects the broader concept of "respect for the home," encompassing privacy, security, and personal autonomy. The United States has moved toward a property-security model, with the Supreme Court in Taylor defining generic burglary as entry into a "building or structure" rather than limiting it to dwellings.

### Penalty Severity

Germany imposes the highest minimum sentences for residential burglary among the jurisdictions studied, with 1 year for private dwelling burglary under Section 244(4). South Korea's Article 330 provides for up to 10 years for nighttime dwelling theft but sets no mandatory minimum. U.S. penalties vary enormously by state — from potential misdemeanor treatment for commercial burglary in some states to decades of imprisonment for armed residential burglary in others. The ECHR constrains maximum penalties through Article 3 proportionality requirements.

### Victim Protection

The EU Victims' Rights Directive provides the most comprehensive framework for burglary victim protection, mandating support services, information rights, and individual needs assessment. South Korea's Crime Victim Protection Act provides compensation and support. The United States operates through the federal Victims of Crime Act (VOCA) and state-level compensation programs. Germany integrates victim rights directly into criminal procedure through the StPO and the Opferentschaedigungsgesetz.
