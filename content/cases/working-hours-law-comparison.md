## Summary

Working hours and overtime regulation -- the legal framework governing the maximum duration of daily and weekly work, mandatory rest periods, and premium compensation for hours exceeding statutory limits -- constitutes a foundational pillar of labor law that directly affects worker health, productivity, and economic competitiveness. South Korea reformed its Labor Standards Act (근로기준법) through a landmark 2018 amendment that reduced the maximum weekly working hours from 68 to 52 (40 regular hours plus 12 overtime hours), phased in by employer size, following decades of having one of the longest average working hours among OECD nations; a 2023 proposal by the Yoon administration to introduce a flexible overtime banking system of up to 69 hours per week was withdrawn after significant public backlash. The United States regulates working hours primarily through the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) of 1938 (29 U.S.C. Section 201 et seq.), which establishes a 40-hour standard workweek beyond which non-exempt employees must receive overtime pay at 1.5 times the regular rate, but notably imposes no absolute cap on maximum working hours and exempts large categories of workers, including salaried employees meeting the executive, administrative, or professional exemption thresholds updated in 2024. Germany regulates working hours through the Arbeitszeitgesetz (ArbZG) of 1994, which establishes a maximum 8-hour workday (extendable to 10 hours if averaged to 8 hours over 24 weeks), mandatory rest periods of 11 consecutive hours between shifts, and a general prohibition on Sunday and holiday work, with enforcement by the Gewerbeaufsichtsämter (trade supervisory offices). At the European level, the EU Working Time Directive 2003/88/EC establishes minimum standards including a 48-hour maximum weekly working time averaged over a reference period, minimum daily rest of 11 consecutive hours, minimum weekly rest of 24 consecutive hours, and a minimum four-week annual paid leave entitlement, while the CJEU has delivered landmark judgments including CCOO v. Deutsche Bank (C-55/18, 2019) requiring member states to mandate employer recording of daily working time.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Working hours in South Korea are governed by Chapter IV of the Labor Standards Act (근로기준법), originally enacted in 1953 and substantially amended through numerous reforms. South Korea's working hours regulation has undergone the most dramatic transformation among OECD nations over the past two decades, transitioning from one of the world's longest working-hour cultures to a regime with strict statutory limits.

### 2018 Working Hours Reform

The most consequential reform came through the amendment of July 1, 2018, which redefined the statutory workweek:

**Previous System:** The Labor Standards Act technically limited regular working hours to 40 per week (Article 50) but allowed 12 hours of overtime (Article 53). However, the Ministry of Employment and Labor had long interpreted "one week" as comprising only weekdays (Monday through Friday), treating Saturday and Sunday work as outside the overtime cap. This interpretation effectively permitted 68 hours per week (40 regular + 16 weekend + 12 overtime).

**Reformed System:** The 2018 amendment explicitly defined "one week" (1주) as seven consecutive days including weekends, capping total working hours at 52 per week (40 regular + 12 overtime). This represented an effective reduction of 16 hours per week from the prior regime.

### Phased Implementation

| Employer Size | Implementation Date |
|--------------|-------------------|
| 300+ employees | July 1, 2018 |
| 50-299 employees | January 1, 2020 |
| 5-49 employees | July 1, 2021 |

### Overtime and Premium Pay

Under Article 56 of the Labor Standards Act, overtime work, night work (10 PM to 6 AM), and holiday work are compensated at a premium of 50 percent above the regular hourly rate. When overtime coincides with night work, the premiums are additive, resulting in compensation at 200 percent of the regular rate. Holiday work premiums were established through the 2018 amendment.

### Special Provisions

**Flexible Working Time (탄력적 근로시간제):** Articles 51 and 51-2 permit flexible scheduling over two-week or three-month periods (extended to six months by 2021 amendment), allowing daily hours to exceed 8 provided the weekly average over the reference period does not exceed 40 hours.

**Selective Working Time (선택적 근로시간제):** Article 52 permits employees to choose their own working hours within agreed parameters, commonly used in research, design, and creative industries.

### 2023 Overtime Reform Proposal and Withdrawal

In March 2023, the Yoon Suk-yeol administration proposed the Working Hours Flexibility Bill, which would have allowed employees to accumulate overtime hours in a "time bank" over extended periods, potentially permitting up to 69 hours in a single week if compensated with reduced hours in subsequent weeks. The proposal drew massive public opposition, particularly from younger workers, and was formally withdrawn in April 2023. The episode underscored the political sensitivity of working hours regulation in South Korea.

### Working Culture Context

Despite legislative reforms, South Korea's average annual working hours remain among the highest in the OECD at approximately 1,872 hours in 2023, compared to the OECD average of approximately 1,716 hours. The concept of "야근 문화" (overtime culture) remains deeply embedded in Korean workplace norms, and enforcement gaps persist, particularly in small and medium enterprises.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) of 1938 establishes the primary federal framework for working hours regulation. Unlike most other developed nations, the FLSA does not impose an absolute maximum on working hours. Instead, it uses a financial incentive mechanism: employers must pay non-exempt employees a premium of 1.5 times the regular rate ("time-and-a-half") for all hours worked beyond 40 in a workweek.

### Exemptions

The FLSA's overtime provisions do not apply to employees classified as "exempt" under Section 13(a)(1). The exemptions require satisfaction of both a salary threshold and a duties test:

**2024 Rule Update (Partially Vacated):** In April 2024, the DOL issued a final rule raising the salary threshold for the executive, administrative, and professional (EAP) exemption from $684/week ($35,568/year) to $844/week ($43,888/year) effective July 1, 2024, with a further increase to $1,128/week ($58,656/year) scheduled for January 1, 2025. However, a federal district court in Texas vacated the rule nationwide in November 2024 (State of Texas v. Department of Labor), reverting the threshold to $684/week.

**Key Exempt Categories:**

| Exemption | Requirements |
|-----------|-------------|
| Executive | Salary threshold + manages enterprise or department + supervises 2+ employees |
| Administrative | Salary threshold + office/non-manual work + exercises independent judgment on significant matters |
| Professional | Salary threshold + work requiring advanced knowledge in a field of science or learning |
| Computer employees | $684/week salary or $27.63/hour + systems analysis, programming, or similar duties |
| Outside sales | No salary test + customarily engaged away from employer's place of business |

### State Variations

Several states provide stronger working hours protections than the FLSA:

- **California:** Daily overtime (1.5x after 8 hours; 2x after 12 hours); mandatory meal and rest breaks
- **Alaska, Nevada, Colorado:** Daily overtime provisions
- **Oregon:** Predictive scheduling requirements for retail, food service, and hospitality employers (2017)

### No Federal Requirements for Rest Periods or Paid Leave

The United States is notably the only OECD nation that does not guarantee any minimum paid annual leave at the federal level. The FLSA does not require meal breaks, rest periods, or paid vacation. These benefits are left entirely to employer discretion and private negotiation, supplemented by state laws in some jurisdictions.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's working hours are governed by the Arbeitszeitgesetz (ArbZG), enacted on June 6, 1994, replacing the earlier Arbeitszeitordnung of 1938. The ArbZG implements EU Working Time Directive requirements while incorporating distinctively German features, particularly the Sunday work prohibition.

### Core Provisions

**Maximum Daily Hours (Section 3 ArbZG):** The standard maximum workday is 8 hours. This may be extended to 10 hours provided that the average over a 24-week (or 6-month) reference period does not exceed 8 hours per working day. Based on a 6-day working week (Monday through Saturday), this establishes a theoretical maximum of 48 regular hours per week, with temporary peaks of up to 60 hours.

**Daily Rest Period (Section 5 ArbZG):** Workers must receive at least 11 consecutive hours of rest between the end of one working day and the beginning of the next. Exceptions apply in hospitals, care facilities, hospitality, transport, broadcasting, and agriculture, where the rest period may be reduced to 10 hours if compensated within the reference period.

**Break Periods (Section 4 ArbZG):** Workers must receive a break of at least 30 minutes for work exceeding 6 hours, and at least 45 minutes for work exceeding 9 hours. Breaks may be split into segments of at least 15 minutes each.

### Sunday and Holiday Work Prohibition

Section 9 ArbZG establishes a general prohibition on work on Sundays and public holidays (Sonn- und Feiertagsruhe). This provision has constitutional dimensions, as Article 140 of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz) incorporates Article 139 of the Weimar Constitution, which protects Sundays and holidays as "days of rest from work and of spiritual elevation."

Exceptions are enumerated in Section 10 ArbZG for essential services including emergency services, hospitals, hospitality, media, transport, agriculture, and continuous production processes. Workers required to work on Sundays must receive a compensatory rest day within two weeks.

### Overtime Regulation

The ArbZG does not prescribe a specific overtime premium. Overtime compensation is left to collective agreements (Tarifverträge), works council agreements (Betriebsvereinbarungen), or individual employment contracts. In practice, most collective agreements in major sectors provide overtime premiums of 25 to 50 percent.

### Working Time Recording

Following the CJEU's ruling in CCOO v. Deutsche Bank (C-55/18, 2019) and the Bundesarbeitsgericht's decision of September 13, 2022 (1 ABR 22/21), employers in Germany are required to record daily working hours. The BAG held that the existing obligation under Section 3(2)(1) of the Arbeitsschutzgesetz (Occupational Safety Act) already requires systematic recording of working hours. Draft legislation to formalize the recording requirement (Arbeitszeiterfassungsgesetz) has been under consideration but was not finalized as of early 2026.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Working Time Directive 2003/88/EC

The Working Time Directive, adopted on November 4, 2003, consolidating earlier Directive 93/104/EC, establishes minimum health and safety requirements for the organization of working time across EU member states.

**Core Requirements:**

| Provision | Minimum Standard |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Maximum weekly working time (Art. 6) | 48 hours averaged over reference period (up to 4 months; extendable to 6 or 12 months by collective agreement) |
| Daily rest (Art. 3) | 11 consecutive hours per 24-hour period |
| Weekly rest (Art. 5) | 24 consecutive hours per 7-day period (plus 11-hour daily rest) |
| Breaks (Art. 4) | Where working day exceeds 6 hours; details left to member states |
| Annual leave (Art. 7) | Minimum 4 weeks paid annual leave; may not be replaced by payment except on termination |
| Night work (Art. 8) | Maximum 8 hours per 24-hour period on average; immediate health assessment |

**Opt-Out (Article 22(1)):** The Directive permits member states to allow individual workers to opt out of the 48-hour maximum, provided the worker consents freely and in writing, no detriment results from refusal, and the employer maintains records. The United Kingdom was the primary proponent of this provision; following Brexit, the opt-out remains available in the remaining member states, with approximately 15 states utilizing it as of 2025.

### CJEU Landmark Cases

**CCOO v. Deutsche Bank (Case C-55/18, Judgment of May 14, 2019):** The Grand Chamber held that the Working Time Directive and the Charter of Fundamental Rights require member states to oblige employers to set up a system for measuring daily working time. Without such a system, it is impossible to determine objectively whether maximum working time limits and rest periods are being observed.

**Jaeger v. Nordwestmecklenburg (Case C-151/02, Judgment of September 9, 2003):** The Court held that on-call time spent at the workplace constitutes working time in its entirety under the Directive, even during periods of inactivity. This decision had major implications for healthcare sectors across the EU.

**Federación de Servicios de CCOO v. Tyco (Case C-266/14, Judgment of September 10, 2015):** The Court held that travel time between home and the first/last customer of the day for mobile workers without a fixed workplace constitutes working time under the Directive.

### European Social Charter

Article 2 of the European Social Charter (Revised) guarantees the right to just conditions of work, including reasonable daily and weekly working hours, paid public holidays, a minimum of four weeks' annual holiday with pay, and the elimination of risks in dangerous or unhealthy occupations by reducing working hours.

### European Court of Human Rights

While the ECHR does not directly regulate working hours, the Court has addressed related issues under Article 4 (prohibition of forced labor). In Chowdury and Others v. Greece (Application No. 21884/15, 2017), the Court found a violation of Article 4(2) regarding migrant strawberry pickers forced to work under threat of violence for excessive hours with minimal pay, holding that Greece had failed in its positive obligation to prevent human trafficking and forced labor.

## Comparative Analysis

### Maximum Hours

The jurisdictions reflect a spectrum from strict to permissive: South Korea imposes the firmest absolute weekly cap (52 hours), Germany permits temporary peaks up to 60 hours but requires averaging to 48, the EU Directive requires 48-hour averaging with an opt-out possibility, and the United States imposes no maximum whatsoever. This divergence reflects fundamentally different philosophical approaches: the European model treats maximum hours as a health and safety imperative, while the U.S. model treats overtime pay as a sufficient deterrent to excessive hours.

### Overtime Compensation

South Korea and the United States both mandate a 50 percent overtime premium, providing clear and uniform rules. Germany leaves overtime premiums to collective bargaining, while the EU Directive is silent on premium rates entirely. The practical result is that German workers' overtime compensation varies significantly by sector and employer.

### Rest and Leave

Germany and the EU Directive guarantee the most comprehensive rest protections (11-hour daily rest, Sunday prohibition in Germany, 4-week minimum annual leave). South Korea mandates one paid rest day per week and 15 days of annual leave. The United States mandates neither rest periods nor annual leave at the federal level, representing a significant outlier among developed nations.

### Enforcement and Recording

The CJEU's CCOO v. Deutsche Bank ruling has catalyzed a shift toward mandatory working time recording across the EU. South Korea implemented recording requirements as part of its 2018 reform. The United States requires limited recordkeeping under the FLSA but does not mandate comprehensive daily time recording.
