## Summary

The right of publicity -- the legal right of an individual to control and profit from the commercial use of their name, image, likeness, voice, or other identifying attributes -- occupies a unique position between intellectual property, privacy, and personal autonomy, with jurisdictions diverging fundamentally on whether this right is primarily a property right (alienable, descendible, and commercially exploitable) or a personality right (inalienable, tied to human dignity, and extinguishing at death). South Korea has developed the right of publicity (퍼블리시티권) primarily through judicial decisions rather than legislation, with courts recognizing commercial exploitation rights in a person's name and likeness while debating whether this constitutes a standalone property right or an aspect of the personality right (인격권) protected under the Civil Act (민법); the Supreme Court has acknowledged the economic value of celebrity identities but has not definitively settled the doctrinal classification, and recent legislative proposals have sought to codify the right. The United States is the jurisdiction where the right of publicity originated and is most extensively developed, recognized by statute in approximately 35 states and by common law in additional states, with significant variation in scope -- Indiana's Right of Publicity Act (IC 32-36) being among the broadest, providing protection for 100 years post-mortem, while New York's Civil Rights Law Sections 50-51 was historically the most restrictive, providing only a narrow statutory privacy right until the 2021 amendment adding post-mortem protection; the Supreme Court's sole right-of-publicity decision, Zacchini v. Scripps-Howard Broadcasting Co. (1977), recognized the right but left most doctrinal development to state courts. Germany addresses the right of publicity through the Allgemeines Persönlichkeitsrecht (general personality right) derived from Articles 1(1) and 2(1) of the Grundgesetz and the specific provisions of the Kunsturhebergesetz (KUG -- Art Copyright Act) Section 22, which requires consent for the publication of a person's image, with the Federal Court of Justice (Bundesgerichtshof) recognizing that the personality right includes both commercial and non-commercial aspects through the Marlene Dietrich decision (BGH, December 1, 1999). The European Court of Human Rights addresses publicity rights primarily through Article 8 (right to respect for private life), with the Von Hannover v. Germany series of cases establishing the framework for balancing personality rights against press freedom under Article 10.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea has no dedicated statute governing the right of publicity. The right has developed through judicial decisions interpreting existing provisions of the Civil Act (민법) and the broader constitutional right to personality.

**Civil Act (민법) Article 751:** Provides for compensation for non-pecuniary damage (위자료) resulting from wrongful acts. Courts have used this provision to award damages for unauthorized commercial use of a person's identity.

**Constitutional Basis:** The Constitutional Court has recognized a general personality right (인격권) derived from the constitutional right to dignity (Article 10) and the right to pursue happiness. Lower courts have extended this to encompass commercial aspects of personal identity.

### Judicial Development

**Seoul Central District Court, 2006Gahap80450 (2007):** One of the earliest explicit recognitions of the right of publicity in Korean law. The court acknowledged that individuals have a protectable interest in the commercial value of their name and likeness, particularly when that value has been developed through their own efforts and talent.

**Supreme Court Decision 2012Da31628 (2015):** While not using the term "right of publicity" directly, the Supreme Court recognized that the unauthorized commercial use of a person's portrait or name constitutes a violation of personality rights and gives rise to both compensatory and consolation damages. The Court stopped short of declaring the right of publicity an independent property right, instead grounding protection in personality rights.

### Legislative Proposals

Multiple proposals to codify the right of publicity have been introduced in the National Assembly:

| Year | Proposal | Key Feature |
|------|----------|-------------|
| 2009 | Right of Publicity Act | Standalone property right; 50-year post-mortem protection |
| 2013 | Revised proposal | Personality right approach; 30-year post-mortem |
| 2020 | Digital likeness proposal | Address AI-generated deepfakes of celebrities |
| 2023 | Comprehensive proposal | Hybrid approach; digital likeness protections |

None have been enacted, leaving the right in a state of judicial development.

### Entertainment Industry Context

South Korea's K-pop and entertainment industries have made the right of publicity a particularly pressing commercial issue. Entertainment agencies (e.g., HYBE, SM Entertainment, JYP Entertainment) routinely include publicity rights provisions in artist contracts. Disputes over the scope of these contractual rights -- particularly whether agencies retain publicity rights after contract termination -- have generated significant litigation.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has the most developed right of publicity doctrine, originating from the seminal article by William Prosser on privacy torts (1960) and the Second Circuit's decision in Haelan Laboratories v. Topps Chewing Gum (1953), which first recognized a distinct "right of publicity" separate from the right of privacy.

**Zacchini v. Scripps-Howard Broadcasting Co., 433 U.S. 562 (1977):** The Supreme Court's only right-of-publicity case. Hugo Zacchini, a "human cannonball" performer, sued a television station that broadcast his entire 15-second act without consent. The Court held that the First Amendment does not bar state right-of-publicity claims for the unauthorized broadcast of a performer's entire act, analogizing the right to patent and copyright protections.

### State Law Variation

| State | Statute | Post-Mortem Duration | Key Feature |
|-------|---------|---------------------|-------------|
| Indiana | IC 32-36 | 100 years | Broadest protection; covers any attribute identifying a person |
| California | Civil Code Section 3344; Section 3344.1 | 70 years | Separate statutory and common law rights; transferable |
| New York | Civil Rights Law Sections 50-51 (amended 2021) | 40 years (added 2021) | Historically narrow; 2021 amendment expanded significantly |
| Tennessee | Personal Rights Protection Act (ELVIS Act, 2024) | Indefinite (while commercially exploited) | First state to specifically address AI-generated likeness |
| Texas | Common law only | Duration uncertain | Property right recognized; no statute |

### First Amendment Defenses

The tension between the right of publicity and the First Amendment has produced several significant doctrinal approaches:

**Transformative Use Test (California):** In Comedy III Productions v. Gary Saderup, 25 Cal. 4th 387 (2001), the California Supreme Court adopted a test asking whether the defendant's work adds "significant creative elements" that transform the celebrity's likeness into something more than a mere representation.

**Rogers v. Grimaldi Test:** Originally a Lanham Act test from the Second Circuit (875 F.2d 994 (1989)), applied in some jurisdictions to right-of-publicity claims involving expressive works.

**Newsworthiness Defense:** Use of a person's identity in news reporting, commentary, and editorial content is generally protected.

### AI and Digital Likeness

The emergence of AI-generated deepfakes and synthetic media has accelerated legislative activity:
- **Tennessee ELVIS Act (2024):** The Ensuring Likeness Voice and Image Security Act specifically addresses AI-generated likenesses, making it unlawful to publish a deepfake that misappropriates a person's voice or likeness without consent
- **Federal NO FAKES Act (proposed 2023):** The Nurture Originals, Foster Art, and Keep Entertainment Safe Act would create a federal right against unauthorized digital replicas
- **SAG-AFTRA negotiations (2023):** The actors' union strike was significantly motivated by AI likeness concerns, resulting in contract provisions requiring performer consent for digital replicas

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany addresses publicity rights through the Allgemeines Persönlichkeitsrecht (general personality right) rather than a separate property right, reflecting the civil law tradition's emphasis on human dignity over commercial exploitation.

**Kunsturhebergesetz (KUG) Section 22:** "Images may be disseminated or publicly displayed only with the consent of the person depicted." This provision, dating from 1907, provides the statutory basis for image protection. Consent is presumed when the person received payment for being depicted.

**Section 23 KUG (Exceptions):** Images may be disseminated without consent when they concern:
- Portraits from the domain of contemporary history (Bildnisse aus dem Bereich der Zeitgeschichte)
- Images where the person appears merely as an accessory to a landscape or event
- Images of assemblies, processions, or similar events
- Images serving a higher interest of art

### Marlene Dietrich Decision (BGH, December 1, 1999, I ZR 49/97)

The Federal Court of Justice's landmark decision established that the general personality right contains both an ideal (non-commercial) and a material (commercial) component. When the commercial aspects of a person's identity are exploited without authorization, the injured party (or their heirs) may claim damages measured by a license analogy (Lizenzanalogie) -- the fee that would have been paid for authorized use.

**Post-Mortem Protection:** The Court held that the commercial aspects of the personality right survive death and are exercisable by heirs for a period corresponding to the KUG Section 22 protection period (10 years post-mortem). This was a groundbreaking extension, as the personality right had traditionally been considered to extinguish at death.

### Nena Decision (BGH, 2006)

The Federal Court of Justice held that the unauthorized use of a singer's (Nena's) image for advertising purposes violated both KUG Section 22 and the personality right, and that damages could be calculated using the license analogy method even for non-celebrities whose commercial value was limited.

### Constitutional Dimension

The Federal Constitutional Court has repeatedly emphasized the primacy of human dignity (Menschenwürde, Article 1(1) GG) in personality right cases. The personality right is not merely a commercial instrument but a protection of individual autonomy and self-determination. Commercial exploitation is protected not because of economic value per se, but because control over one's own image is an aspect of personal autonomy.

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR addresses personality and publicity rights primarily through Article 8 (right to respect for private life), balanced against Article 10 (freedom of expression).

**Von Hannover v. Germany (No. 1) (Application No. 59320/00, Judgment of June 24, 2004):** Princess Caroline of Monaco challenged the publication of photographs showing her in public places. The Court found Germany violated Article 8 by failing to protect her private life, holding that the decisive factor is the "contribution that the published photos and articles make to a debate of general interest."

**Von Hannover v. Germany (No. 2) (Application Nos. 40660/08, 60641/08, Grand Chamber Judgment of February 7, 2012):** The Grand Chamber established detailed balancing criteria between Articles 8 and 10: contribution to a debate of general interest, how well known the person is, subject of the report, prior conduct of the person, content/form/consequences of the publication, and circumstances of image capture.

**Reklos and Davourlis v. Greece (Application No. 1234/05, Judgment of January 15, 2009):** The Court held that photographing a newborn baby in a clinic without parental consent violated Article 8, even though the photos were not published. The right to control one's image is an essential component of personal development and privacy.

### EU Framework

There is no harmonized EU right-of-publicity regime. The matter is left to national law, creating significant disparities:

- The GDPR (Regulation 2016/679) provides some protection through personal data provisions (a person's image constitutes personal data), requiring consent for processing
- The EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689) imposes transparency obligations for AI-generated deepfakes but does not create a specific publicity right
- The European Commission has not proposed harmonization of personality rights

### International Considerations

The absence of international harmonization creates significant conflicts-of-law issues, particularly in digital contexts where commercial use of a person's identity may originate in one jurisdiction and be accessible globally. The Hague Conference on Private International Law has not addressed personality rights specifically.

## Comparative Analysis

### Property vs. Personality Right

The fundamental doctrinal divergence is whether the right of publicity is a property right or a personality right. The U.S. treats it predominantly as a property right -- freely transferable, commercially exploitable, and surviving death for extended periods. Germany treats it as a personality right with commercial aspects -- inalienable at its core, with only limited post-mortem protection. South Korea's courts remain undecided, with competing judicial approaches. This classification has profound practical consequences for assignability, inheritance, and duration.

### Post-Mortem Duration

The variation in post-mortem protection is striking. Indiana provides 100 years. California provides 70 years. Tennessee provides indefinite protection while commercial value persists. Germany provides only 10 years. South Korea has no settled law. The ECHR provides no guidance on duration. This disparity creates significant commercial uncertainty for global entertainment properties.

### AI and Synthetic Media

The emergence of AI-generated deepfakes represents the most significant contemporary challenge to publicity rights doctrine. Tennessee's ELVIS Act is the first statute specifically addressing AI likenesses. The U.S. NO FAKES Act would create federal protection. Germany's existing personality right framework likely extends to synthetic media but has not been explicitly tested. The ECHR's Article 8 jurisprudence should apply but no case has directly addressed AI-generated likenesses. South Korea's entertainment industry faces particularly acute AI likeness challenges given the commercial significance of K-pop idol images.

### Deceased Celebrities and Estate Management

The treatment of deceased celebrities' publicity rights illustrates the doctrinal divergence:

**United States:** Several high-profile estate disputes have shaped the law. The estate of Marilyn Monroe engaged in multi-jurisdictional litigation over post-mortem publicity rights, with Indiana, California, and New York applying different standards. The Michael Jackson estate generates hundreds of millions in annual licensing revenue, relying on California's 70-year post-mortem protection. The Prince estate litigated unauthorized use following his 2017 death.

**Germany:** The Marlene Dietrich and Nena decisions established the framework, but the 10-year post-mortem limitation significantly constrains estate rights compared to U.S. jurisdictions. After expiration, the commercial identity enters a form of public domain.

**South Korea:** The growing commercial value of K-pop group identities -- some groups generate billions of dollars in merchandise and endorsement revenue -- has intensified interest in post-mortem and post-contract publicity rights. The dissolution or contract expiration of major groups raises questions about whether individual members or agencies retain publicity rights in group-associated imagery.

### Lookalikes and Soundalikes

The use of lookalikes (persons resembling celebrities) and soundalikes (voice imitators) in advertising raises distinct questions:

**United States:** The Ninth Circuit held in Midler v. Ford Motor Co. (849 F.2d 460, 1988) that the deliberate imitation of a celebrity singer's voice for advertising purposes violates the right of publicity. In Waits v. Frito-Lay (978 F.2d 1093, 1992), the court extended protection against voice imitation with damages.

**Germany:** The use of lookalikes is assessed under the personality right framework, with the determining factor being whether consumers would believe the actual celebrity endorsed the product.

**South Korea:** The growing prevalence of AI-generated K-pop content, including virtual idols modeled on real performers, has created new challenges that existing law does not explicitly address.
