## Summary

Counter-terrorism law across the Global South reflects diverse security threats, colonial legacies, and varying commitments to civil liberties. China's counter-terrorism framework, anchored in the Counter-Terrorism Law of the People's Republic of China (中华人民共和国反恐怖主义法, 2015) and the Criminal Law Articles 120-120F, has been deployed extensively in the Xinjiang Uyghur Autonomous Region through a system of "vocational education and training centers" (职业技能教育培训中心) that the UN Office of the High Commissioner for Human Rights (OHCHR) Assessment of August 2022 found may constitute "crimes against humanity." India deploys an extensive counter-terrorism legal arsenal including the Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act 1967 (UAPA, amended 2004, 2008, 2012, 2019), the Armed Forces (Special Powers) Act 1958 (AFSPA), and the National Investigation Agency Act 2008, operating in a context where terrorism ranges from cross-border threats in Jammu and Kashmir to Maoist/Naxalite insurgency in central India. Nigeria's response to Boko Haram and the Islamic State West Africa Province (ISWAP) operates through the Terrorism (Prevention) Act 2011 (amended 2013), the Armed Forces Act, and the controversial National Security Agencies Act (Cap N74 LFN 2004), while South Africa's Protection of Constitutional Democracy against Terrorist and Related Activities Act 33 of 2004 (POCDATARA) was drafted against the backdrop of the country's own liberation movement history. Brazil's Anti-Terrorism Law (Law 13.260/2016), enacted ahead of the 2016 Rio Olympics, was carefully drafted to exclude social movements from the terrorism definition after intense civil society opposition. Colombia's decades-long armed conflict produced a unique counter-terrorism-to-peace trajectory, transitioning from the Estatuto de Seguridad (1978) through the Democratic Security Policy (2002-2010) to the 2016 peace agreement with FARC-EP. Indonesia's counter-terrorism law (Law No. 5 of 2018 amending Law No. 15 of 2003, originally Government Regulation in Lieu of Law No. 1 of 2002) responds to the post-Bali bombing threat environment, while the Philippines' Anti-Terrorism Act of 2020 (Republic Act 11479) replaced the Human Security Act of 2007 amid concerns about its potential use against dissent in Mindanao and beyond.

## China

### Counter-Terrorism Law 2015

The Counter-Terrorism Law (反恐怖主义法), effective January 1, 2016, provides a comprehensive framework. Article 3 defines terrorism as "propositions and actions that create social panic, endanger public security, infringe upon personal and property rights, or coerce state organs and international organizations, through violence, destruction, intimidation and other means, to achieve political, ideological and other purposes." Article 25 authorizes emergency response measures, while Articles 30-39 address intelligence gathering, including obligations on telecommunications operators and internet service providers to provide technical interfaces for encryption decryption (Article 18) and content monitoring.

### Criminal Law Provisions

The Ninth Amendment to the Criminal Law (2015) added Articles 120A-120F, significantly expanding terrorism-related offenses. Article 120A criminalizes assisting terrorist activities; Article 120B criminalizes preparing to commit terrorist activities (mere preparation as a completed offense); Article 120C criminalizes promoting terrorism or extremism; Article 120D criminalizes inciting terrorism with "extremist" materials; Article 120E criminalizes forcing others to wear "extremist" clothing or symbols; and Article 120F criminalizes illegally possessing materials promoting terrorism or extremism.

### Xinjiang Counter-Terrorism and De-Extremification

The Xinjiang Uyghur Autonomous Region Regulation on De-Extremification (新疆维吾尔自治区去极端化条例, 2017, revised 2018) established the legal basis for what Chinese authorities term "vocational education and training centers" (VETCs) and what the international community, including the OHCHR Assessment of August 31, 2022, documented as mass arbitrary detention. The Regulation identifies 15 types of "extremist" behavior including wearing burqas, having "abnormal" beards, refusing to watch state television, and abandoning alcohol and tobacco. The OHCHR Assessment concluded that "serious human rights violations have been committed in XUAR in the context of the Government's application of counter-terrorism and counter-extremism strategies" and that these "may constitute international crimes, in particular crimes against humanity."

The Counter-Terrorism Law Article 30 authorizes "de-extremification through education and transformation," while the Xinjiang Regulation Article 17 mandates "education and transformation" for those exhibiting extremist tendencies. Estimates of the number of Uyghurs, Kazakhs, and other Turkic minorities detained in VETCs range from several hundred thousand to over one million (Australian Strategic Policy Institute, Xinjiang Data Project). China characterized international criticism as interference in internal affairs and issued a White Paper titled "The Fight Against Terrorism and Extremism and Human Rights Protection in Xinjiang" (2019).

### Hong Kong National Security Law

The Law of the People's Republic of China on Safeguarding National Security in the Hong Kong Special Administrative Region (港区国安法, June 30, 2020), while framed as national security rather than counter-terrorism legislation, contains terrorism provisions. Article 24 criminalizes "terrorist activities" including serious violence against persons, arson, and sabotage of transport facilities, with penalties up to life imprisonment. The Safeguarding National Security Ordinance (Article 23 legislation, enacted March 2024) further expanded the security framework.

## India

### UAPA Framework

The Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act 1967 (UAPA), extensively amended in 2004, 2008, 2012, and 2019, is India's primary counter-terrorism statute. Section 15 defines "terrorist act" broadly as any act committed with intent to threaten or likely to threaten the unity, integrity, security, economic security, or sovereignty of India or to strike terror in the people. Section 43D(5) imposes a near-impossible bail standard, requiring the court to deny bail if, on perusal of the case diary or report, there are "reasonable grounds for believing that the accusation against such person is prima facie true." The Supreme Court in NIA v. Zahoor Ahmad Shah Watali (2019) 5 SCC 1 interpreted this provision restrictively, holding that courts must accept the prosecution's case at face value without conducting a detailed analysis of evidence.

The 2019 Amendment (Act 28 of 2019) introduced the power to designate individuals as terrorists (Section 35, as amended), not merely organizations.

The UAPA has been criticized for extensive misuse:

- Over 10,000 individuals arrested under UAPA between 2018 and 2022 (NCRB data)
- Conviction rate below 3%, suggesting widespread overcharging
- High-profile cases include the Bhima Koregaon case (2018), where 16 activists, academics, and lawyers were arrested under UAPA for alleged Maoist links
- Father Stan Swamy, an 84-year-old Jesuit priest and tribal rights activist arrested under UAPA in the Bhima Koregaon case, died in judicial custody in July 2021 after being denied bail despite suffering from Parkinson's disease
- Delhi riots cases (2020), where student activists including Umar Khalid, Sharjeel Imam, and Natasha Narwal were charged under UAPA; Narwal, Devangana Kalita, and Asif Iqbal Tanha were granted bail by the Delhi High Court in a judgment criticizing the state's conflation of protest with terrorism

### Armed Forces (Special Powers) Act

AFSPA 1958 (applicable to northeast India) and AFSPA 1990 (applicable to Jammu and Kashmir) grant extraordinary powers to armed forces in areas declared "disturbed" by the central or state government. Section 4(a) authorizes use of force "even to the causing of death" against any person acting in contravention of any law or order. Section 4(c) authorizes arrest without warrant. Section 6 provides immunity from prosecution without central government sanction. The Supreme Court in Extra Judicial Execution Victim Families Association v. Union of India (2016) 14 SCC 536 (the "Manipur encounter killings case") held that AFSPA does not provide blanket immunity and that every death must be investigated. The Jeevan Reddy Committee (2005) recommended AFSPA's repeal, as did the Second Administrative Reforms Commission, but the Act remains in force.

### Counter-Terrorism Institutional Architecture

The National Investigation Agency (NIA), established by the NIA Act 2008 following the 26/11 Mumbai attacks, has jurisdiction over scheduled offenses including terrorism, with nationwide investigative authority without requiring state consent. The National Security Guard (NSG, "Black Cats"), Multi-Agency Centre (MAC), and National Counter Terrorism Centre (NCTC, proposed but not established due to state opposition) complete the institutional architecture. The National Intelligence Grid (NATGRID), operationalized in 2022, integrates databases across 11 agencies for counter-terrorism intelligence.

### Maoist/Naxalite Insurgency

India's counter-terrorism framework also addresses the Maoist/Naxalite insurgency in the "Red Corridor" -- an area spanning parts of Chhattisgarh, Jharkhand, Odisha, Bihar, West Bengal, Andhra Pradesh, Telangana, and Maharashtra. The CPI(Maoist), designated as a terrorist organization under UAPA (2009), has been the target of Operation Green Hunt (2009-present) and subsequent military-style operations involving Central Armed Police Forces (CRPF, BSF, ITBP) and state police.

The Salwa Judum (literally "peace march") -- a state-sponsored counter-insurgency militia in Chhattisgarh -- was declared unconstitutional by the Supreme Court in Nandini Sundar v. State of Chhattisgarh (2011) 7 SCC 547, which held that arming tribal youth against Maoists violated Articles 14 and 21 of the Constitution. Despite this ruling, community-based counter-insurgency operations continue in various forms.

### Kashmir and Northeast India

The revocation of Article 370 of the Constitution (August 5, 2019) through the Constitution (Application to Jammu and Kashmir) Order 2019 and the Jammu and Kashmir Reorganization Act 2019 transformed the region from a state with special constitutional status to two Union Territories. This was accompanied by a communications blackout, mass preventive detention under the Jammu and Kashmir Public Safety Act 1978, and internet shutdowns -- the longest in any democracy (Anuradha Bhasin v. Union of India (2020) 3 SCC 637, where the SC held that internet shutdowns must satisfy the proportionality standard but declined to restore services). The Armed Forces (Jammu and Kashmir) Special Powers Act 1990 continues to apply, though "disturbed area" notification has been partially withdrawn from some districts.

## Africa

### Nigeria: Boko Haram Response

Nigeria's Terrorism (Prevention) Act 2011 (as amended 2013) was enacted in response to the Boko Haram insurgency (which began in 2009 with the extrajudicial killing of founder Mohammed Yusuf in police custody). Section 1 defines terrorism; Section 2 prescribes death penalty for acts of terrorism causing death; and Section 28 permits pre-charge detention of up to 90 days with judicial authorization. The Office of the National Security Adviser (ONSA) coordinates counter-terrorism strategy, including the Soft Approach (deradicalization) program known as Operation Safe Corridor, established in 2016 at Mallam Sidi Camp, Gombe State.

The military response -- including Operations Zaman Lafiya Dole, Hadin Kai, and Tura Takaibango -- has been accompanied by documented human rights violations. Amnesty International, Human Rights Watch, and the International Criminal Court Preliminary Examination (2010-2023) documented extrajudicial executions, mass arbitrary detention at Giwa Barracks (Maiduguri), torture, enforced disappearances, and civilian deaths in airstrikes. The ICC Prosecutor's 2020 report identified a reasonable basis to believe that both Boko Haram/ISWAP and the Nigerian Security Forces committed crimes within ICC jurisdiction, but the ICC closed the preliminary examination in December 2020, referring it to Nigeria for domestic investigation under the principle of complementarity, while reserving the right to reconsider.

Nigeria's northeast, particularly Borno, Adamawa, and Yobe States, has been subject to a State of Emergency (first declared 2013) and military-administered camps for internally displaced persons (IDPs), with an estimated 2.2 million IDPs as of 2024 (UNHCR data). The Civilian Joint Task Force (CJTF), a government-allied vigilante group, has been listed by the UN Secretary-General for the recruitment and use of child soldiers.

### South Africa: Post-Liberation Counter-Terrorism

South Africa's POCDATARA (Act 33 of 2004) was drafted with acute awareness of the ANC's own historical designation as a "terrorist" organization under apartheid-era legislation (the Terrorism Act 83 of 1967, repealed). Section 1(4) explicitly excludes from the definition of terrorist activity "advocacy, protest, dissent or industrial action" that does not involve the commission of offenses. The Act implements UNSC Resolution 1373 (2001) obligations and the AU Convention on the Prevention and Combating of Terrorism (1999).

South Africa's Financial Intelligence Centre Act 38 of 2001 (FICA) established the Financial Intelligence Centre (FIC) for anti-money laundering and counter-terrorist financing. South Africa was "greylisted" by the Financial Action Task Force (FATF) in February 2023 for strategic deficiencies in its anti-money laundering/counter-terrorist financing framework, with specific concerns about terrorist financing linked to the Islamist insurgency in Mozambique's Cabo Delgado province, which has cross-border dimensions.

## Latin America

### Brazil: Anti-Terrorism Law

Brazil's Anti-Terrorism Law (Law 13.260/2016), enacted in March 2016 ahead of the Rio Olympics, was the product of intense debate between security concerns and civil liberties protection. Article 2 defines terrorism as acts committed "for reasons of xenophobia, discrimination or prejudice of race, color, ethnicity and religion" that cause social terror through violence against persons or damage to property, transport, communications, or essential services. Critically, Article 2 paragraph 2 explicitly excludes "individual or collective conduct of persons in social movements, unions, religious movements, or movements with political or ideological purposes that aim to contest, criticize, protest against or question, through permitted channels, the political, economic or social order."

The maximum penalty is 12-30 years' imprisonment for terrorism causing death (Article 2 paragraph 1, II), with the aggravating circumstances of Article 7 (international connection, use of explosives, targeting essential services). The law created the terrorism financing offense (Article 6, 15-30 years). To date, very few prosecutions have occurred under the law. The STF has not yet ruled on its constitutionality, though the exclusion of social movements was specifically designed to address concerns raised by the MST (Movimento dos Trabalhadores Rurais Sem Terra), CUT (Central Unica dos Trabalhadores), and indigenous movements.

### Colombia: From Conflict to Peace

Colombia's counter-terrorism trajectory is unique: a multi-decade armed conflict involving the FARC-EP (Fuerzas Armadas Revolucionarias de Colombia - Ejercito del Pueblo), the ELN (Ejercito de Liberacion Nacional), and paramilitary groups (AUC, Autodefensas Unidas de Colombia) evolved through several legal phases. President Alvaro Uribe's Democratic Security Policy (Politica de Seguridad Democratica, 2002-2010) involved the designation of conflict zones (zonas de rehabilitacion y consolidacion), mass arrests, informant networks (red de cooperantes), and the controversial "false positives" (falsos positivos) scandal -- extrajudicial killings by the military of civilians dressed as guerrillas to inflate body counts. The JEP's Case 03 has documented at least 6,402 victims of false positives between 2002 and 2008.

The 2016 Final Agreement for the Termination of the Conflict and the Construction of a Stable and Lasting Peace, endorsed by Congressional vote (Acto Legislativo 01 de 2016) after the initial plebiscite narrowly failed (50.2% against), created the Comprehensive System of Truth, Justice, Reparation and Non-Repetition (SIVJRNR) comprising the Truth Commission (Comision de la Verdad, which completed its mandate in June 2022 and published its Final Report), the Special Jurisdiction for Peace (JEP), and the Unit for the Search for Persons Deemed as Missing (UBPD). Former FARC combatants who acknowledged responsibility receive "restorative sanctions" of 5-8 years of effective restriction of liberty (restriccion efectiva de libertad) involving community reparation projects, rather than prison terms.

The Constitutional Court's review of the peace agreement's implementing legislation in Sentencia C-674/2017 and Sentencia C-007/2018 upheld the transitional justice framework as consistent with the Constitution, finding that peace as a constitutional value (Article 22) justifies modified criminal sanctions. The ongoing ELN peace negotiations (resumed 2022) and the "Total Peace" (Paz Total) policy of President Gustavo Petro seek to extend this framework to remaining armed groups.

## Southeast Asia

### Indonesia: Post-Bali Bombing Framework

Indonesia's counter-terrorism legal framework was catalyzed by the October 12, 2002 Bali bombings (202 killed), which prompted Government Regulation in Lieu of Law (Perppu) No. 1 of 2002, subsequently enacted as Law No. 15 of 2003. The law was comprehensively amended by Law No. 5 of 2018 following the 2018 Surabaya church bombings. Key provisions include: Article 6 (death penalty, life, or minimum 4 years for terrorist acts causing death); Article 10A (criminal liability for planning terrorism); Article 12A (involvement in military-style training abroad); Article 12B (criminalizing citizens who join foreign terrorist organizations); and Article 25 (pre-charge detention of 21 days by investigators, extendable by 120 days with prosecutorial authorization).

Key institutional components of Indonesia's counter-terrorism architecture include:

- The National Counter-Terrorism Agency (Badan Nasional Penanggulangan Terorisme, BNPT), established by Presidential Regulation No. 46 of 2010, coordinating national strategy
- Densus 88 (Detachment 88), the Special Detachment for Counter-Terrorism of the Indonesian National Police, conducting operational responses
- The National Intelligence Agency (Badan Intelijen Negara, BIN), providing intelligence support

Densus 88 conducts operational responses. Densus 88, trained with Australian and U.S. assistance, has been effective in dismantling Jemaah Islamiyah (JI) and ISIS-affiliated cells but has faced allegations of extrajudicial killings and torture documented by Komnas HAM and human rights organizations.

The deradicalization program, administered by BNPT, includes prison-based programs, community engagement, and economic empowerment. Indonesia has also pursued "soft approach" strategies, including the engagement of former militants like Ali Imron (Bali bombing perpetrator) as deradicalization facilitators. The approach has been studied internationally as a model of counter-terrorism that combines security operations with community-based prevention.

### Philippines: Anti-Terrorism Act 2020

Republic Act 11479 (Anti-Terrorism Act of 2020, ATA), signed July 3, 2020, replaced the Human Security Act of 2007 (RA 9372). The ATA broadened the definition of terrorism (Section 4), extended pre-charge detention from 3 to 14 days (extendable by 10 days) under Section 29, empowered the Anti-Terrorism Council (ATC) to designate terrorist individuals and organizations (Sections 25-26), and authorized surveillance and wiretapping (Section 16). Section 4's exclusion clause states the Act does not apply to "advocacy, protest, dissent, stoppage of work, industrial or mass action and other similar exercises of civil and political rights."

The Supreme Court in Calleja v. Executive Secretary (G.R. No. 252578, December 7, 2021) upheld the constitutionality of most ATA provisions but struck down Section 4's qualifier "which are not intended to cause death or serious physical harm" (as applied to the exclusion clause) as void for vagueness, and struck down Section 25's designation power of the ATC insofar as it authorized proscription without judicial review. The ATA's application in Mindanao, where martial law was declared from 2017 to 2019 (Proclamation No. 216, upheld in Lagman v. Medialdea, G.R. No. 231658, 2017) following the Marawi siege by Maute Group/ISIS-affiliated fighters, remains a primary concern.

The Philippines' counter-terrorism experience in Mindanao encompasses the Moro conflict (dating to the Spanish colonial period), with the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao (BARMM) established by Republic Act 11054 (Bangsamoro Organic Law, 2018) as a political resolution to the Moro Islamic Liberation Front (MILF) insurgency under the 2014 Comprehensive Agreement on the Bangsamoro. However, splinter groups including the Bangsamoro Islamic Freedom Fighters (BIFF) and Abu Sayyaf Group (ASG) continue to pose security threats.

## Comparative Analysis

### Definition of Terrorism

The Global South illustrates the political nature of terrorism definitions. Brazil's explicit exclusion of social movements reflects the lived experience of criminalization of dissent in Latin America. South Africa's exclusion of advocacy and protest reflects the ANC's history as a designated "terrorist" organization. By contrast, China's broad definition encompassing "coercion of state organs" and India's sweeping UAPA provisions have been used against peaceful dissent -- environmental activists, journalists, academics, and human rights lawyers.

### Exceptional Detention Powers

Pre-charge detention periods vary dramatically: China's liuzhi system permits 6 months; India's UAPA allows 180 days; Nigeria's TPA allows 90 days; Indonesia allows 141 days; the Philippines 24 days; and Brazil 30 days. These periods vastly exceed the 48-hour limit considered a baseline under international human rights law (ICCPR Article 9(3)). The absence of effective habeas corpus during these extended detention periods raises systematic due process concerns.

### Counter-Terrorism and Minority Communities

Across the Global South, counter-terrorism disproportionately impacts ethnic and religious minorities: Uyghurs in China, Kashmiris and Muslims more broadly in India, northern Nigerian Muslims, Moro Muslims in the Philippines, and indigenous and Afro-descendant communities in Colombia. This pattern suggests that counter-terrorism law functions not merely as a security instrument but as a tool for managing ethno-religious diversity and minority political claims.

### Transitional Justice Alternative

Colombia's transition from counter-terrorism to transitional justice through the JEP represents a unique contribution to global counter-terrorism discourse, demonstrating that accountability for political violence can take forms other than conventional criminal prosecution. The "restorative sanctions" model -- restricting liberty through community reparation projects rather than imprisonment -- challenges the punitive paradigm that dominates counter-terrorism in China, India, Nigeria, and Southeast Asia.
