## Summary

Renewable energy mandates -- legal requirements that a specified share of electricity generation or total energy consumption derive from renewable sources such as solar, wind, hydroelectric, biomass, and geothermal -- have become central instruments in global climate policy, with their design reflecting each jurisdiction's energy mix, industrial structure, and political economy. South Korea enacted the Act on the Promotion of the Development, Use, and Diffusion of New and Renewable Energy (신에너지 및 재생에너지 개발·이용·보급 촉진법) and operates a Renewable Portfolio Standard (RPS, 신재생에너지 공급의무화제도) requiring large-scale electricity generators to supply a progressively increasing percentage of their output from renewables, reaching 25% by 2034; Korea's 2050 Carbon Neutrality and Green Growth Framework Act (탄소중립·녹색성장 기본법, 2021) established a legally binding net-zero target for 2050 and an interim target of 40% greenhouse gas reduction by 2030 relative to 2018 levels. The United States lacks a federal renewable energy mandate but operates through a patchwork of state-level Renewable Portfolio Standards (RPS), with 30 states plus the District of Columbia maintaining mandatory RPS programs as of 2026 -- California's SB 100 (2018) requires 100% clean energy by 2045, the most ambitious state target -- supplemented by federal tax incentives including the Production Tax Credit (PTC) and Investment Tax Credit (ITC) significantly expanded by the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 (IRA, Pub. L. 117-169). Germany's Renewable Energy Sources Act (Erneuerbare-Energien-Gesetz, EEG), first enacted in 2000 and comprehensively reformed in 2023 (EEG 2023), established the global model for feed-in tariff systems and now targets 80% renewable electricity by 2030 and climate neutrality by 2045 under the Federal Climate Protection Act (Bundes-Klimaschutzgesetz, KSG); the EEG has driven Germany's renewable share from 6% in 2000 to over 50% of gross electricity consumption by 2025. At the European level, the Renewable Energy Directive (RED III, Directive (EU) 2023/2413) sets a binding EU-wide target of at least 42.5% renewable energy in gross final energy consumption by 2030, with an aspirational target of 45%, building on the earlier RED II (2018) target of 32%, and integrated into the broader European Green Deal and Fit for 55 legislative package; the European Court of Human Rights has engaged with climate and energy policy through landmark judgments including KlimaSeniorinnen v. Switzerland (2024), which established for the first time that states have positive obligations under Article 8 of the Convention to protect individuals from the adverse effects of climate change.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's renewable energy policy operates through several interconnected statutes and regulatory mechanisms.

**Act on the Promotion of the Development, Use, and Diffusion of New and Renewable Energy (신에너지 및 재생에너지 개발·이용·보급 촉진법):** This foundational statute, originally enacted in 2004, defines new and renewable energy sources, establishes the institutional framework for promotion and diffusion, and authorizes the Renewable Portfolio Standard. The Act covers eleven categories of renewable energy including solar photovoltaic, solar thermal, wind, hydroelectric, marine, geothermal, biomass, waste energy, and hydrogen fuel cells, as well as "new energy" sources including fuel cells, hydrogen energy, and coal liquefaction/gasification.

**Renewable Portfolio Standard (RPS, 신재생에너지 공급의무화제도):** Introduced in 2012 to replace the previous feed-in tariff system, the RPS requires electricity generators with installed capacity exceeding 500 MW to supply a mandated percentage of their output from renewable sources. The obligation is fulfilled through self-generation or the purchase of Renewable Energy Certificates (RECs, 신재생에너지 공급인증서) on the Korea Power Exchange. The mandated percentage has been progressively increased:

| Year | RPS Obligation (%) |
|------|-------------------|
| 2012 | 2.0% |
| 2016 | 3.5% |
| 2020 | 7.0% |
| 2023 | 13.0% |
| 2026 | 17.5% |
| 2030 | 21.5% |
| 2034 | 25.0% |

**2050 Carbon Neutrality and Green Growth Framework Act (탄소중립·녹색성장 기본법, 2021):** Enacted on September 24, 2021, this landmark legislation established South Korea's net-zero target for 2050 as a legally binding obligation and set an interim target of reducing greenhouse gas emissions by at least 40% below 2018 levels by 2030. The Act established the 2050 Carbon Neutrality and Green Growth Commission (탄소중립녹색성장위원회) as the principal advisory and coordination body.

### Korea Emissions Trading Scheme (K-ETS)

Launched on January 1, 2015, the K-ETS was the first national emissions trading scheme in East Asia. It covers approximately 684 entities responsible for about 74% of national greenhouse gas emissions. The scheme operates in allocation periods, with the third period (2021-2025) introducing increased auctioning of allowances and expanded sectoral coverage.

### Energy Transition Challenges

South Korea's renewable energy transition faces distinctive structural challenges. The country's high population density limits available land for solar and wind installations. Its geographic isolation as an "energy island" (no interconnections with neighboring grids due to North Korea) prevents electricity imports from renewable-rich regions. Nuclear power remains a contentious issue: President Moon Jae-in's nuclear phase-out policy (2017) was reversed by President Yoon Suk-yeol (2022), who restored nuclear energy as a pillar of the energy mix alongside renewables.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States lacks a comprehensive federal renewable energy mandate, resulting in a fragmented regulatory landscape driven primarily by state-level action and federal tax incentives.

**State Renewable Portfolio Standards:** As of 2026, thirty states plus the District of Columbia maintain mandatory RPS programs, and an additional seven states have voluntary renewable energy goals.

| State | Target | Year | Key Features |
|-------|--------|------|-------------|
| California | 100% clean energy | 2045 | SB 100 (2018); includes non-renewable zero-carbon sources |
| New York | 70% renewable electricity | 2030 | Climate Leadership and Community Protection Act (2019) |
| Hawaii | 100% renewable electricity | 2045 | First state to set 100% renewable target (2015) |
| Virginia | 100% clean energy | 2045/2050 | Virginia Clean Economy Act (2020) |
| Colorado | 100% clean energy | 2040 | SB 19-236 (2019) for Xcel Energy territory |
| New Jersey | 50% renewable electricity | 2030 | Clean Energy Act (2018) |
| Massachusetts | 40% renewable electricity | 2030 | Increasing at 2% annually |

**Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 (IRA, Pub. L. 117-169):** The IRA represents the largest federal investment in clean energy in U.S. history, allocating approximately $369 billion in energy and climate spending over ten years. Key provisions include:
- Extension of the Production Tax Credit (PTC) and Investment Tax Credit (ITC) for renewable energy through at least 2032
- New Clean Electricity Production Credit (Section 45Y) and Clean Electricity Investment Credit (Section 48E) replacing technology-specific credits with technology-neutral credits for zero-emission generation
- Domestic content bonuses, energy community bonuses, and low-income community adders
- $27 billion Greenhouse Gas Reduction Fund for green banks and community financing

**West Virginia v. EPA (597 U.S. 697, 2022):** The Supreme Court invoked the "major questions doctrine" to limit the EPA's authority to require generation-shifting from fossil fuels to renewables under the Clean Air Act. The decision constrained federal regulatory approaches to emissions reduction, reinforcing the reliance on tax incentives and state mandates rather than federal command-and-control regulation.

### Federal Energy Regulatory Framework

The Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC) regulates interstate electricity transmission and wholesale electricity markets. FERC Order 2222 (2020) required regional transmission organizations and independent system operators to allow distributed energy resources, including rooftop solar and battery storage, to participate in wholesale electricity markets. FERC Order 2023 (2023) reformed interconnection procedures to address the massive backlog of renewable energy projects waiting to connect to the grid -- over 2,000 GW of generation capacity in interconnection queues as of 2023, predominantly solar and wind.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's Energiewende (energy transition) is anchored in the Renewable Energy Sources Act (EEG) and the Federal Climate Protection Act.

**Erneuerbare-Energien-Gesetz (EEG):** First enacted on April 1, 2000, the EEG established the global model for feed-in tariff systems. The original law guaranteed renewable energy generators fixed prices for twenty years, with grid operators required to purchase all renewable electricity at above-market rates. The law has been comprehensively reformed multiple times:

| Version | Key Changes |
|---------|-------------|
| EEG 2000 | Established feed-in tariffs; priority dispatch for renewables |
| EEG 2004 | Differentiated tariff rates by technology; introduced digression mechanisms |
| EEG 2009 | Increased tariff rates; expanded biomass provisions |
| EEG 2012 | Market premium option; mandatory direct marketing for large installations |
| EEG 2014 | Introduced competitive auctions for large-scale projects; expansion corridors |
| EEG 2017 | Auctions made mandatory for most technologies; transition from feed-in tariffs to market premiums |
| EEG 2021 | Raised 2030 target to 65% renewable electricity; technology-specific auction volumes |
| EEG 2023 | Raised 2030 target to 80% renewable electricity; declared renewable energy expansion as being in the "overriding public interest"; streamlined permitting |

**Federal Climate Protection Act (Bundes-Klimaschutzgesetz, KSG):** Enacted in 2019 and amended in 2021 following the Federal Constitutional Court's landmark Neubauer decision (BVerfG, 1 BvR 2656/18, March 24, 2021), the KSG establishes annual sectoral emission budgets and legally binding greenhouse gas reduction targets: 65% reduction by 2030 (relative to 1990), 88% by 2040, and climate neutrality by 2045.

### Neubauer Decision (BVerfG, 2021)

The Federal Constitutional Court's Neubauer decision is one of the most significant climate law rulings globally. The Court held that the original KSG's 2030 reduction targets were constitutionally insufficient because they effectively shifted the burden of emissions reduction to future generations, violating the complainants' fundamental rights under Article 20a GG (state obligation to protect natural foundations of life) in conjunction with Article 2(1) GG (right to free development of personality). The Court found that insufficient near-term action would necessitate drastic restrictions on freedoms in later years, constituting a constitutionally impermissible "advance interference" (eingriffsähnliche Vorwirkung) with future liberties.

### Permitting Reform

The EEG 2023 declaration that renewable energy expansion is in the "overriding public interest and serves public security" (im überragenden öffentlichen Interesse und dient der öffentlichen Sicherheit) was designed to accelerate environmental permitting by establishing a legal presumption in favor of renewables in the balancing of interests under environmental impact assessment and species protection law. Implementation is governed by the Wind Energy Area Requirements Act (Windenergieflächenbedarfsgesetz, WindBG), which requires states to designate 2% of their territory for wind energy by 2032.

## ECHR / EU

### EU Renewable Energy Directive (RED III)

Directive (EU) 2023/2413 (RED III), adopted in October 2023 as part of the Fit for 55 package, amends the original Renewable Energy Directive (2009/28/EC) and its successor RED II (2018/2001).

**Key Targets and Provisions:**
- Binding EU-wide target of at least 42.5% renewable energy in gross final energy consumption by 2030, with an aspirational target of 45%
- Sectoral sub-targets: transport (29% renewable energy or 14.5% GHG reduction), industry (1.6% annual increase in renewable use), buildings (heating/cooling: 0.8% annual increase nationally)
- Accelerated permitting: "renewable acceleration areas" where permitting must be completed within 12 months for renewable energy projects; outside acceleration areas, maximum 24 months
- Strengthened sustainability criteria for bioenergy
- Framework for guarantees of origin for renewable hydrogen

**REPowerEU Plan (2022):** Adopted in response to the Russian invasion of Ukraine and the resulting energy supply crisis, REPowerEU accelerated the renewable energy transition by proposing an increase in the 2030 target from 32% to 45% (which RED III set as the aspirational target), fast-tracked permitting for solar and wind, and a Solar Rooftop Initiative mandating solar installations on new public and commercial buildings.

### European Climate Law

Regulation (EU) 2021/1119 (European Climate Law) established the EU's binding commitment to climate neutrality by 2050 and a binding target of at least 55% net greenhouse gas emission reduction by 2030 relative to 1990 levels. The law establishes a European Scientific Advisory Board on Climate Change and requires a trajectory for achieving the targets.

### KlimaSeniorinnen v. Switzerland (ECtHR Grand Chamber, 2024)

In Verein KlimaSeniorinnen Schweiz and Others v. Switzerland (Application No. 53600/20, Grand Chamber Judgment of April 9, 2024), the European Court of Human Rights established for the first time that states have positive obligations under Article 8 (right to respect for private and family life) to protect individuals from the adverse effects of climate change. The Court found that Switzerland had violated Article 8 by failing to adopt sufficient measures to mitigate climate change, including by failing to quantify national greenhouse gas emission limits through a carbon budget or other means and by failing to meet past emission reduction targets. The judgment has significant implications for renewable energy mandates, as inadequate promotion of renewables may now constitute a Convention violation.

### EU Emissions Trading System

The EU ETS, established by Directive 2003/87/EC and reformed by Directive (EU) 2023/959, is the world's largest carbon market. The ETS Phase IV (2021-2030) introduces a linear reduction factor of 4.3% annually, reducing the overall cap to achieve 62% emissions reduction in ETS sectors by 2030 relative to 2005. The carbon price, which exceeded 100 euros per tonne in 2023, creates economic incentives favoring renewable energy over fossil fuel generation.

## Comparative Analysis

### Mandate Design

The jurisdictions employ fundamentally different mandate designs. South Korea and U.S. states use Renewable Portfolio Standards, which obligate electricity suppliers to source a percentage from renewables, using tradeable certificates as compliance instruments. Germany's EEG pioneered the feed-in tariff model, providing guaranteed prices to generators -- a supply-side instrument that reduces investment risk but may produce above-market costs. The EU's RED III operates at the meta-level, setting binding national targets that member states implement through their chosen domestic mechanisms.

### Ambition Level

Germany leads in ambition with its 80% renewable electricity target for 2030. The EU's 42.5% target for total energy consumption (not just electricity) is structurally different but comparably ambitious. South Korea's targets are more modest, reflecting its structural constraints. The United States has no unified national ambition, but individual states like California and New York match or exceed Germany's targets.

### Constitutional Dimensions

Germany's Neubauer decision and the ECHR's KlimaSeniorinnen judgment have created constitutional and human rights mandates for climate action that indirectly require renewable energy expansion. South Korea's Carbon Neutrality Act similarly creates binding legal obligations. The United States lacks comparable constitutional climate jurisprudence, and the major questions doctrine in West Virginia v. EPA constrains federal regulatory authority, placing greater reliance on legislative tax incentives and state mandates.

### Grid Integration Challenges

All jurisdictions face the common challenge of integrating variable renewable energy into electricity grids designed for dispatchable fossil fuel and nuclear generation. Germany's curtailment of wind power in northern regions due to insufficient north-south transmission capacity, the U.S. interconnection queue backlog, and South Korea's energy island status all illustrate different facets of this technical challenge.
