## Summary

Disinformation regulation -- the legal frameworks addressing the deliberate creation and dissemination of false or misleading information intended to deceive, cause harm, or manipulate public opinion, particularly through digital platforms -- has emerged as one of the most urgent and contentious areas of contemporary media law, pitting the imperative of protecting democratic processes and public health against the fundamental right to freedom of expression. South Korea has grappled with disinformation through the Act on the Aggravated Punishment for False Information (허위정보 가중처벌에 관한 법률) proposals that have repeatedly failed, while existing tools include the Public Official Election Act (공직선거법) Article 250 criminalizing the dissemination of false information about election candidates, the Information and Communications Network Act (정보통신망법) provisions on harmful online content, and the Korea Communications Standards Commission's (방송통신심의위원회) content review authority; the Constitutional Court struck down Article 47(1) of the Telecommunications Basic Act (전기통신기본법) criminalizing "false communications" in 2010 (2008헌바157) as unconstitutionally vague. The United States has the most permissive approach, with the Supreme Court's decision in United States v. Alvarez (2012) holding that the First Amendment protects false statements of fact absent specific harm, and with no federal disinformation statute; regulation operates primarily through platform self-governance, FEC election advertising rules, and the limited authority of the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA), though state-level laws addressing election-related deepfakes have proliferated since 2023. Germany has developed a multi-layered approach through the Netzwerkdurchsetzungsgesetz (NetzDG, 2017), the Medienstaatsvertrag (MStV, 2020) provisions on media intermediary transparency, and participation in the EU's comprehensive framework. At the EU level, the Digital Services Act (Regulation 2022/2065) establishes platform obligations for addressing illegal content and systemic risks including disinformation, the European Democracy Action Plan (2020) provides the strategic framework, the Code of Practice on Disinformation (2022, strengthened) creates a co-regulatory mechanism, and the European Media Freedom Act (2024) addresses media independence as a safeguard against disinformation, while the ECHR has held that false information may in some circumstances be protected under Article 10 but that states may restrict it when necessary to protect democratic processes or rights of others.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's approach to disinformation has been shaped by the Constitutional Court's 2010 decision striking down the most broadly worded false communications law.

**Constitutional Court Decision 2008헌바157 (2010):** The Court struck down Article 47(1) of the Telecommunications Basic Act, which imposed up to five years' imprisonment for distributing "false information through telecommunications with the purpose of harming the public interest." The Court found the provision unconstitutionally vague, particularly the term "public interest" (공익), which could be interpreted to suppress legitimate political expression. The decision effectively foreclosed broad criminalization of online disinformation.

**Public Official Election Act (공직선거법) Article 250:** Criminalizes the publication or dissemination of false information about election candidates with the intent to influence the election outcome. Penalty: up to 7 years imprisonment or fine up to 5 million KRW. This provision has been actively enforced, with dozens of prosecutions per election cycle. The Constitutional Court upheld this provision as a proportionate restriction on expression in the specific context of electoral integrity (2015헌바75, 2019).

### Korea Communications Standards Commission (KCSC)

The KCSC (방송통신심의위원회) reviews online content and may request internet service providers to block or remove content deemed to violate statutory standards. The Commission's authority extends to "false information" that may harm the public interest, though the scope of this authority following the 2010 Constitutional Court decision remains contested. The KCSC reviewed over 200,000 content items in 2023, though the proportion specifically addressing disinformation is not separately reported.

### COVID-19 Pandemic Response

During the COVID-19 pandemic, South Korea utilized the Infectious Disease Control and Prevention Act (감염병의 예방 및 관리에 관한 법률) to address health-related disinformation. Article 34-2 provides authority to correct false information about infectious diseases. The Korean authorities issued correction orders for over 100 items of COVID-19-related disinformation in 2020-2021.

## United States

### Legal Framework

**United States v. Alvarez, 567 U.S. 709 (2012):** The Supreme Court struck down the Stolen Valor Act, which criminalized false claims of military decoration. The plurality opinion (Kennedy, J.) held that "the remedy for speech that is false is speech that is true" and that content-based restrictions on false speech must satisfy strict scrutiny. Justice Breyer's concurrence advocated intermediate scrutiny. The decision confirmed that false statements of fact enjoy significant First Amendment protection.

**No Federal Disinformation Statute:** The United States has no general federal law criminalizing the creation or dissemination of disinformation. Regulatory approaches include:

- **FEC Advertising Transparency:** Federal election advertising on broadcast media must include disclaimers identifying the sponsor. The FEC has not extended comparable requirements to online political advertising.
- **Section 230 CDA:** Provides broad immunity to online platforms for third-party content, including disinformation, while also immunizing platforms' good-faith content moderation decisions.
- **CISA:** The Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency operated a "Mis-, Dis-, and Malinformation" (MDM) team that tracked and flagged disinformation threats. Following political controversy and litigation (Murthy v. Missouri, 603 U.S. ___ (2024)), the program was significantly curtailed. The Supreme Court in Murthy held that the plaintiffs lacked standing to challenge government communications with platforms about content moderation, but the ruling left open questions about permissible government involvement in addressing disinformation.

### State-Level Deepfake Laws

States have moved to address election-related deepfakes:

| State | Law | Provision |
|-------|-----|-----------|
| Texas | SB 751 (2019) | Criminalizes deepfake videos intended to influence elections within 30 days of election |
| California | AB 730 (2019) | Prohibits distribution of deceptive audio/video of candidates within 60 days of election |
| Minnesota | SF 1394 (2023) | Requires disclosure labels on AI-generated political advertisements |
| Washington | SB 5152 (2024) | Prohibits synthetic media in political advertising without disclosure |

### Platform Self-Governance

In the absence of legal mandates, social media platforms have developed voluntary disinformation policies:
- Meta's removal of COVID-19 misinformation during the pandemic (subsequently relaxed)
- Twitter/X's Community Notes fact-checking system
- YouTube's policies on election and health misinformation
- Platform participation in the Election Integrity Partnership and similar coordination efforts

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany has developed a multi-layered approach to disinformation that combines criminal law, platform regulation, and media law.

**NetzDG (2017):** While primarily targeting hate speech, the NetzDG also required platforms to remove "manifestly unlawful" content, which includes certain categories of disinformation where they constitute criminal offenses (e.g., defamation under Section 186 StGB). The law has been largely superseded by the DSA.

**Medienstaatsvertrag (MStV, 2020):** The Interstate Media Treaty addresses disinformation indirectly through:
- **Media intermediary transparency (Section 93):** Search engines and social networks must disclose the criteria governing content selection and ranking
- **Non-discrimination (Section 94):** Media intermediaries may not systematically disadvantage journalistic content
- **Discoverability (Section 84):** Platforms must ensure easy findability of public interest content

**Strafgesetzbuch Provisions:** Specific forms of disinformation may be prosecuted under:
- Section 186 StGB (Üble Nachrede): false factual claims damaging reputation
- Section 187 StGB (Verleumdung): knowingly false defamatory claims
- Section 130 StGB (Volksverhetzung): incitement including through false claims about groups
- Section 109d StGB: intentional dissemination of false security-policy claims to endanger external security

### Institutional Responses

The Bundesamt für Verfassungsschutz (Federal Office for the Protection of the Constitution, BfV) monitors foreign influence operations and disinformation campaigns targeting Germany. The BfV has publicly attributed disinformation campaigns to Russian state actors, particularly following Russia's invasion of Ukraine in 2022.

The Bundeszentrale für politische Bildung (Federal Agency for Civic Education) operates media literacy programs aimed at building societal resilience against disinformation.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Digital Services Act -- Disinformation Provisions

The DSA (Regulation 2022/2065) addresses disinformation primarily through its systemic risk framework:

**Article 34 (Risk Assessment):** Very large online platforms (VLOPs) and very large online search engines (VLOSEs) must assess systemic risks including "actual or foreseeable negative effects on civic discourse and electoral processes, and public security."

**Article 35 (Risk Mitigation):** VLOPs must implement reasonable, proportionate, and effective mitigation measures, which may include adapting content moderation processes, algorithmic recommender systems, and advertising systems.

**Article 36 (Crisis Response):** The Commission may require VLOPs to take urgent action during extraordinary circumstances affecting public security or public health (e.g., pandemics, armed conflicts).

### Strengthened Code of Practice on Disinformation (2022)

The 2022 Strengthened Code of Practice, signed by 44 signatories including major platforms and advertisers, creates a co-regulatory framework under the DSA. Key commitments include:

- Demonetizing disinformation (cutting advertising revenue)
- Transparency of political advertising (ad libraries)
- Empowering users with tools to identify disinformation
- Supporting fact-checking and research access to platform data
- Regular reporting through a transparency center

### European Democracy Action Plan (EDAP, 2020)

The EDAP provides the strategic framework for EU action on disinformation, with three pillars:
1. Protecting election integrity (including regulation of political advertising)
2. Strengthening media freedom and pluralism
3. Countering disinformation

### EU AI Act -- Deepfake Provisions

Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (AI Act), Article 50(4), requires that providers and deployers of AI systems that generate deepfakes (synthetic audio, image, video, or text content) must disclose that the content has been artificially generated or manipulated. This transparency obligation applies unless the content is part of an obviously artistic, creative, or satirical work, or the content is for law enforcement purposes.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Salov v. Ukraine (Application No. 65518/01, Judgment of September 6, 2005):** The Court found that Ukraine's criminal conviction of an individual for distributing false information about a presidential candidate during an election violated Article 10. The Court held that while states may restrict the dissemination of false information to protect electoral integrity, criminal sanctions must be proportionate and the definition of "false information" must be sufficiently clear.

**Magyar Kétfarkú Kutya Párt v. Hungary (Application No. 201/17, Grand Chamber Judgment of January 20, 2020):** The Grand Chamber found that Hungary's sanctioning of a political party for operating a mobile application that allowed citizens to photograph and share spoiled ballots violated Article 10. The judgment emphasized the importance of digital tools for political participation and the need for narrow restrictions during elections.

### Health Disinformation

The COVID-19 pandemic created a specific category of health disinformation that received distinct regulatory treatment:

**South Korea:** The Infectious Disease Control and Prevention Act (감염병의 예방 및 관리에 관한 법률) Article 34-2 authorizes the Korea Disease Control and Prevention Agency (KDCA) to issue correction orders for false information about infectious diseases. During the pandemic, over 100 correction orders were issued, and the police investigated several individuals for spreading false health claims.

**United States:** The FDA and FTC issued warning letters to entities making false COVID-19 treatment claims, using existing authority under the FTC Act and the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act. Social media platforms implemented COVID-19 misinformation policies under voluntary agreements with the Surgeon General's office, though these policies were controversial and largely rolled back by 2023.

**Germany:** The Federal Ministry of Health (BMG) operated a dedicated COVID-19 fact-checking initiative. The Robert Koch Institute published regular rebuttals of health disinformation. Criminal prosecutions for health-related Volksverhetzung increased during the pandemic, particularly targeting anti-vaccination activists who incited hatred against health workers.

**EU:** The European Commission activated the Rapid Alert System for disinformation and coordinated with platforms through the Code of Practice. EDMO (European Digital Media Observatory) provided fact-checking support across member states.

### Foreign Interference and Information Operations

State-sponsored disinformation campaigns from foreign actors have become a major regulatory focus:

| Actor | Target | Key Operations |
|-------|--------|---------------|
| Russia | All four jurisdictions | Election interference; COVID-19 disinformation; Ukraine war narratives |
| China | South Korea, EU | Strategic influence operations; COVID-19 origin narratives |
| North Korea | South Korea | Ongoing propaganda; social media manipulation |
| Iran | United States | Election-related influence operations |

Responses vary: the EU established the EUvsDisinfo project under the European External Action Service. The United States created the Global Engagement Center (State Department). South Korea's NIS monitors North Korean influence operations. Germany's BfV tracks foreign influence campaigns.

## Comparative Analysis

### Regulatory Philosophy

The United States represents the most permissive approach, treating disinformation primarily as a speech issue to be addressed through counter-speech and market mechanisms rather than regulation. Germany and the EU have developed the most regulatory frameworks, combining criminal law, platform obligations, and co-regulatory mechanisms. South Korea occupies a middle position, constrained by the Constitutional Court's 2010 decision but maintaining sector-specific regulations, particularly for elections.

### Platform Responsibility

The most significant divergence concerns platform liability. The EU's DSA imposes comprehensive obligations on platforms to assess and mitigate systemic risks from disinformation. Germany's NetzDG pioneered mandatory content moderation for illegal content. The United States' Section 230 regime provides near-complete platform immunity, with the Murthy decision leaving the government's role in addressing platform disinformation legally uncertain. South Korea's KCSC exercises content review authority but with limited platform-specific mandates.

### Deepfake and AI-Generated Content

The emergence of AI-generated disinformation has prompted convergent responses across jurisdictions. The EU AI Act mandates disclosure of synthetic content. U.S. states have enacted election-specific deepfake laws. South Korea has proposed but not enacted deepfake legislation. Germany applies existing frameworks while implementing the AI Act. The challenge of regulating synthetic content that is indistinguishable from authentic media represents the cutting edge of disinformation law.

### Election Integrity

All jurisdictions recognize election disinformation as a particularly acute concern warranting stronger regulation than general disinformation. South Korea's Public Official Election Act and the EU's EDAP specifically target election-related false information. U.S. state-level deepfake laws focus primarily on election contexts. Germany applies general frameworks with enhanced attention during election periods.
