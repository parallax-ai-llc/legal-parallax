## Summary

Artificial intelligence regulation has emerged as one of the most consequential and rapidly evolving areas of law worldwide, with jurisdictions adopting fundamentally different approaches reflecting distinct philosophical, economic, and political priorities. The European Union enacted the world's first comprehensive AI law through Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (the AI Act), establishing a risk-based classification system with administrative fines of up to EUR 35 million or 7% of global annual turnover for prohibited practices. South Korea followed closely with its AI Basic Act (인공지능 기본법), passed on 26 December 2024 and effective 29 January 2026, adopting a lighter-touch risk-based framework with administrative fines capped at KRW 30 million (approximately USD 22,000). The United States has no federal AI statute as of early 2026, instead relying on an emerging patchwork of state laws -- including California's AI Transparency Act (SB 942), Colorado's AI Act (SB 24-205), and Texas's Responsible AI Governance Act (HB 1709) -- alongside executive orders and sector-specific agency guidance. Germany, as an EU member state, implements the EU AI Act directly while supplementing it with national provisions, and its robust data protection infrastructure under the BDSG positions it as a leading enforcer within the European framework.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's AI Basic Act (인공지능 기본법, Framework Act on the Development of Artificial Intelligence) was passed by the National Assembly on 26 December 2024 and promulgated on 21 January 2025. It takes effect on 29 January 2026 following a one-year transition period. The Act represents South Korea's effort to establish itself as the first country in Asia with comprehensive AI legislation.

Key statutory provisions include:

- **Article 2 -- Definitions:** Defines AI broadly as technology that learns, reasons, judges, and acts autonomously using electronic means, including machine learning, deep learning, and generative AI systems.
- **Article 4 -- Basic Principles:** Establishes principles of human dignity, safety, transparency, fairness, accountability, and innovation promotion.
- **Article 10 -- AI Committee:** Creates an interministerial AI Committee under the Prime Minister to coordinate national AI policy.
- **Article 27 -- High-Impact AI:** Designates AI systems that significantly affect the life, physical safety, or fundamental rights of individuals as "high-impact AI" requiring enhanced obligations including impact assessments, human oversight, and transparency measures.
- **Article 28 -- Generative AI:** Imposes specific obligations on providers of generative AI, including disclosure that content is AI-generated and measures to prevent harmful outputs.
- **Article 44 -- Administrative Fines:** Provides for administrative fines of up to KRW 30 million for violations of notification obligations, failure to designate a domestic agent, or non-compliance with corrective orders.

### Key Principles

The AI Basic Act reflects South Korea's "light-touch" regulatory philosophy, prioritizing innovation promotion alongside risk management. Unlike the EU AI Act, South Korea's law does not establish a list of prohibited AI practices. The Act requires AI business operators to implement self-regulatory measures and encourages industry-led standards development. The Ministry of Science and ICT (MSIT) serves as the primary regulatory authority, with the power to conduct investigations, issue corrective orders, and impose administrative fines.

Notably, the Act applies extraterritorially to foreign entities whose AI activities affect individuals in South Korea, requiring the designation of a domestic agent. The enforcement approach emphasizes a grace period of at least one year before imposing fines, reflecting the government's stated priority of industry preparedness over punitive enforcement.

### Notable Cases

As the AI Basic Act only takes effect in January 2026, formal enforcement actions under the statute are not yet recorded. However, South Korea's existing regulatory framework has produced significant AI-related enforcement:

- **Lee Luda Chatbot Controversy (2021):** The PIPC fined Scatter Lab KRW 103 million for training the chatbot "Lee Luda" on personal KakaoTalk conversations without consent, applying the Personal Information Protection Act (PIPA) to AI training data collection.
- **Deepfake Pornography Enforcement (2024):** South Korea amended the Sexual Violence Prevention Act to specifically criminalize AI-generated deepfake pornography, with penalties of up to five years imprisonment or KRW 50 million in fines, following widespread public outrage over the "Nth Room" cases.
- **AI Hiring Algorithm Investigations (2023-2024):** The National Human Rights Commission investigated complaints that AI-powered hiring tools used by major Korean corporations produced discriminatory outcomes based on gender and educational background.

## United States

### Federal Framework

As of early 2026, the United States has no comprehensive federal AI statute. The regulatory landscape is shaped by:

- **Executive Order 14110 (30 October 2023):** President Biden's "Safe, Secure, and Trustworthy Development and Use of Artificial Intelligence" order directed federal agencies to develop AI safety standards, established reporting requirements for developers of powerful AI models, and tasked NIST with developing AI risk management guidelines. However, much of this order was rescinded by President Trump's Executive Order 14179 (20 January 2025), which emphasized removing barriers to AI innovation.
- **NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF 1.0, January 2023):** A voluntary framework providing guidance for managing AI risks across the AI lifecycle, organized around the functions of Govern, Map, Measure, and Manage.
- **FTC Act Section 5 (15 U.S.C. § 45):** The Federal Trade Commission has used its authority over unfair or deceptive trade practices to pursue AI-related enforcement, including actions against companies making false claims about AI capabilities or using AI in discriminatory ways.
- **Equal Credit Opportunity Act (15 U.S.C. § 1691) and Fair Housing Act (42 U.S.C. § 3601):** Federal anti-discrimination statutes applied to AI-powered lending and housing decisions.

### State Variations

State-level AI legislation has proliferated rapidly:

- **Colorado AI Act (SB 24-205, effective 1 February 2026):** The first comprehensive state AI law, requiring developers and deployers of "high-risk AI systems" to use reasonable care to avoid algorithmic discrimination. Imposes obligations for impact assessments, risk management policies, and consumer notification. Violations enforceable by the state attorney general with penalties of up to $20,000 per violation under the Colorado Consumer Protection Act.
- **California AI Transparency Act (SB 942, effective 1 January 2026):** Requires providers of generative AI systems with over one million monthly users to provide AI detection tools, disclose training data sources, and implement content provenance standards. California also enacted SB 896, requiring state agencies to inventory their AI uses.
- **Texas Responsible AI Governance Act (HB 1709, effective 2026):** Establishes transparency and accountability requirements for high-risk AI systems used in consequential decisions affecting Texans.
- **Illinois AI Video Interview Act (820 ILCS 42, 2020):** One of the earliest state AI laws, requiring employers to notify applicants and obtain consent before using AI to analyze video interviews.
- **New York City Local Law 144 (2023):** Requires bias audits for automated employment decision tools used by employers in New York City.

### Notable Cases

- **EEOC v. iTutorGroup (2023):** The Equal Employment Opportunity Commission settled its first AI-related age discrimination case, alleging the company's recruitment software automatically rejected applicants over 55 and women over 60. Settlement: $365,000 and injunctive relief.
- **FTC v. Rite Aid (2023):** The FTC prohibited Rite Aid from using facial recognition technology for five years after finding the company's AI surveillance system disproportionately generated false matches for women and people of color.
- **FTC v. DoNotPay (2024):** The FTC fined the "robot lawyer" company $193,000 for deceptively claiming its AI could substitute for a human lawyer, establishing that marketing AI capabilities beyond actual performance constitutes an unfair or deceptive practice.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

As an EU member state, Germany is directly subject to Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (the AI Act). Germany's implementation is shaped by its existing robust regulatory infrastructure and its constitutional framework under the Basic Law (Grundgesetz).

Key legal provisions applicable in Germany include:

- **EU AI Act Article 5 -- Prohibited AI Practices:** Bans social scoring systems, real-time remote biometric identification in public spaces (with limited law enforcement exceptions), emotion recognition in workplaces and educational institutions, and AI systems that exploit vulnerabilities of specific groups. Violations carry fines of up to EUR 35 million or 7% of global annual turnover.
- **EU AI Act Article 6 and Annex III -- High-Risk AI Systems:** Classifies AI systems used in critical infrastructure, education, employment, essential services, law enforcement, migration, and administration of justice as high-risk, requiring conformity assessments, risk management systems, data governance, technical documentation, transparency, human oversight, and accuracy/robustness standards.
- **EU AI Act Article 50 -- Transparency Obligations:** Requires that individuals be informed when they interact with an AI system, that AI-generated content be marked as such, and that deepfakes be labeled.
- **EU AI Act Article 99 -- Penalties:** Establishes three tiers of administrative fines: up to EUR 35 million or 7% of turnover for prohibited practices; up to EUR 15 million or 3% of turnover for other non-compliance; up to EUR 7.5 million or 1% of turnover for providing incorrect information.
- **German Basic Law (Grundgesetz) Article 1 -- Human Dignity:** Germany's constitutional guarantee of human dignity (Menschenwürde) provides an additional constitutional basis for restricting AI applications that undermine individual autonomy or dignity.
- **BDSG and GDPR:** Germany's Federal Data Protection Act and the GDPR provide the data protection framework that intersects significantly with AI regulation, particularly regarding automated decision-making under GDPR Article 22.

### Key Principles

Germany designated the Federal Network Agency (Bundesnetzagentur, BNetzA) as its primary national AI supervisory authority, leveraging the agency's existing expertise in telecommunications and digital infrastructure regulation. The BfDI (Federal Commissioner for Data Protection and Freedom of Information) retains jurisdiction over AI systems that process personal data.

Germany's approach is characterized by strong institutional capacity for enforcement, a constitutional framework that emphasizes human dignity and informational self-determination (informationelle Selbstbestimmung, as recognized by the Federal Constitutional Court in the 1983 Census Decision, BVerfGE 65, 1), and a robust industrial base in automotive, manufacturing, and healthcare AI that necessitates practical regulatory guidance.

The German government has also invested heavily in AI research and development through its National AI Strategy (Nationale KI-Strategie), updated in 2020 with a commitment of EUR 5 billion, balancing regulatory oversight with industrial competitiveness objectives.

### Notable Cases

- **Clearview AI Decision (Hamburg DPA, 2023):** The Hamburg Commissioner for Data Protection fined Clearview AI EUR 5.1 million for illegally processing biometric data of German citizens by scraping facial images from the internet to build its facial recognition database, applying GDPR Article 9 (processing of special categories of data) in an AI context.
- **SCHUFA Scoring Decisions (BGH, 2024):** Following the CJEU's ruling in C-634/21 (SCHUFA Holding, December 2023) that credit scoring constitutes automated individual decision-making under GDPR Article 22, German courts have applied this precedent to require greater transparency in AI-powered credit scoring algorithms.
- **ChatGPT Data Protection Investigation (2023-2024):** The BfDI and several state DPAs opened investigations into OpenAI's ChatGPT regarding compliance with GDPR requirements on data accuracy, legal basis for processing, and data subject rights in the context of large language model training.

## ECHR / EU Framework

### Convention Articles

The European Convention on Human Rights provides foundational human rights principles applicable to AI regulation:

- **Article 8 -- Right to Respect for Private and Family Life:** The most frequently invoked Convention article in AI-related cases, providing a basis for challenging AI-powered surveillance, automated profiling, and data processing that intrudes on private life. The ECtHR applies a proportionality test requiring that interference be "in accordance with the law," pursue a "legitimate aim," and be "necessary in a democratic society."
- **Article 6 -- Right to a Fair Trial:** Relevant to AI systems used in judicial decision-making, requiring transparency and the ability to challenge automated decisions that affect legal rights.
- **Article 14 -- Prohibition of Discrimination:** Applicable to AI systems that produce discriminatory outcomes, particularly in conjunction with other substantive Convention rights.
- **Article 10 -- Freedom of Expression:** Relevant to AI content moderation, content generation, and the regulation of deepfakes and AI-generated misinformation.

### Key Case Law

- **Szabó and Vissy v. Hungary (2016, App. No. 37138/14):** The ECtHR held that mass surveillance systems must have adequate safeguards, establishing principles applicable to AI-powered surveillance. The Court emphasized the need for independent oversight of automated surveillance technologies.
- **Bărbulescu v. Romania (Grand Chamber, 2017, App. No. 61496/08):** Established that employers' monitoring of employees' electronic communications (including through automated systems) must comply with Article 8, requiring proportionality and prior notice.
- **Big Brother Watch v. United Kingdom (Grand Chamber, 2021, App. No. 58170/13):** Addressed bulk interception of communications, finding that the UK's surveillance regime violated Articles 8 and 10. The Court established that automated processing of intercepted data requires specific safeguards.
- **Glukhin v. Russia (2023, App. No. 11519/20):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where Russian authorities used facial recognition technology to identify and prosecute a protester, marking the first ECtHR ruling directly addressing law enforcement use of AI facial recognition.

The EU's AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689) represents the legislative culmination of these human rights principles, translating Convention-level protections into binding regulatory requirements with enforcement mechanisms. The Act explicitly references fundamental rights as enshrined in the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights, which mirrors and extends ECHR protections.

### Member State Obligations

Under the AI Act, EU member states must:

- Designate national competent authorities and market surveillance authorities by 2 August 2025 (Article 70).
- Establish at least one AI regulatory sandbox to facilitate innovation under regulatory oversight (Article 57).
- Ensure effective enforcement through penalties that are "effective, proportionate and dissuasive" (Article 99).
- Report annually to the European Commission on enforcement activities and the state of AI in their territory.

The Council of Europe also adopted the Framework Convention on Artificial Intelligence and Human Rights, Democracy and the Rule of Law (CETS No. 225) on 17 May 2024, the first binding international treaty on AI. Open to non-European states, it has been signed by the United States, Israel, and several other non-member states, establishing baseline principles for AI governance grounded in human rights.

## Enforcement and Compliance Mechanisms

### Enforcement Architecture Compared

The enforcement architecture varies significantly across jurisdictions:

**South Korea** relies on the Ministry of Science and ICT as the primary regulator, supplemented by existing agencies such as the Personal Information Protection Commission (PIPC) for data-related AI issues and the Korea Communications Commission (KCC) for communications-related AI. The enforcement model prioritizes voluntary compliance and industry self-regulation, with administrative fines serving as a backstop rather than a primary deterrent. The relatively modest fine ceiling of KRW 30 million has drawn criticism from consumer groups who argue it is insufficient to deter major technology companies.

**United States** enforcement is distributed across multiple federal agencies with overlapping jurisdictions:
- The **Federal Trade Commission (FTC)** has been the most active federal enforcer, using Section 5 authority to pursue deceptive AI practices, algorithmic discrimination, and AI-related privacy violations. The FTC has issued policy statements clarifying that existing consumer protection law applies to AI and has pursued over 20 AI-related enforcement actions since 2021.
- The **Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC)** has focused on AI bias in hiring, issuing technical assistance documents on the Americans with Disabilities Act and AI (2022) and Title VII implications of automated hiring tools.
- The **Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB)** has issued guidance on algorithmic credit underwriting, emphasizing that the Equal Credit Opportunity Act's adverse action notice requirements apply to AI-based decisions.
- The **Department of Commerce / NIST** provides standards and frameworks but lacks direct enforcement authority.
- **State attorneys general** exercise enforcement authority under state AI laws and consumer protection statutes, with the Colorado Attorney General expected to be among the first state-level AI enforcers.

**Germany / EU** has the most comprehensive enforcement structure:
- The **European AI Office**, established within the European Commission in February 2024, oversees implementation of the AI Act, particularly regarding general-purpose AI models and systemic risk assessments.
- **National market surveillance authorities** are responsible for enforcing high-risk AI requirements within their territories.
- **Notified bodies** conduct conformity assessments for high-risk AI systems before they can be placed on the EU market.
- The tiered fine structure (7% / 3% / 1% of global turnover) provides substantial deterrent effect, modeled on the GDPR's successful enforcement framework.

## Global Perspectives

Beyond the four primary jurisdictions, several other countries have adopted notable approaches to AI regulation:

- **Japan:** Adopted a non-binding "Social Principles of Human-Centric AI" (2019) and published AI governance guidelines emphasizing agile governance and industry self-regulation, reflecting a deliberately soft-law approach.
- **China:** Enacted the Interim Measures for the Management of Generative AI Services (effective 15 August 2023), the Algorithm Recommendation Regulation (2022), and the Deep Synthesis Provisions (2023), creating one of the most prescriptive regulatory frameworks globally, with requirements that AI outputs reflect "core socialist values."
- **Brazil:** The AI Bill (PL 2338/2023) advanced through the Brazilian Senate, proposing a risk-based framework inspired by the EU AI Act, with provisions for algorithmic impact assessments and rights of affected persons.
- **India:** As of 2026, India has not enacted comprehensive AI legislation but has issued advisories through the Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology requiring platforms to label AI-generated content and obtain government approval before deploying "unreliable" AI models.
- **Canada:** The Artificial Intelligence and Data Act (AIDA), Part 3 of Bill C-27, proposed a federal regulatory framework for high-impact AI systems but died on the order paper when Parliament was dissolved in January 2025.
- **Australia:** Released a voluntary AI Ethics Framework (2024) and initiated consultations on mandatory guardrails for high-risk AI, with legislation expected in 2026-2027.
- **Nigeria:** The National Information Technology Development Agency (NITDA) published a National AI Strategy (2024) and draft AI regulatory framework emphasizing capacity building and inclusive AI development.

## Key Regulatory Challenges

### Defining AI Systems

All jurisdictions face the fundamental challenge of defining "artificial intelligence" for regulatory purposes. The EU AI Act adopts a broad, technology-neutral definition (Article 3(1)): "a machine-based system that is designed to operate with varying levels of autonomy and that may exhibit adaptiveness after deployment and that, for explicit or implicit objectives, infers, from the input it receives, how to generate outputs such as predictions, content, recommendations, or decisions that can influence physical or virtual environments." South Korea's AI Basic Act similarly employs a broad functional definition. By contrast, US state laws vary considerably: Colorado's AI Act focuses on "high-risk artificial intelligence systems" that make "consequential decisions," while California's AI Transparency Act centers on "generative artificial intelligence" specifically.

### Extraterritorial Application and Jurisdictional Conflicts

The global nature of AI systems creates significant jurisdictional challenges. The EU AI Act applies to any AI system placed on the EU market regardless of where the provider is established, potentially creating compliance obligations for AI systems developed in the US, South Korea, or elsewhere. Similarly, South Korea's AI Basic Act applies to foreign entities whose AI activities affect South Korean citizens. The risk of conflicting requirements across jurisdictions has prompted calls for international harmonization, with the OECD AI Principles and the G7 Hiroshima AI Process serving as potential frameworks for convergence.

### Open-Source AI and Innovation

The treatment of open-source AI models presents a particularly challenging regulatory question. The EU AI Act provides partial exemptions for open-source GPAI models (Article 53(2)), excluding them from some documentation requirements unless they present systemic risks. South Korea's AI Basic Act does not specifically address open-source models. US state laws generally focus on deployers rather than developers, potentially reducing the burden on open-source creators. The tension between enabling innovation through open-source development and managing the risks of widely distributed AI capabilities remains unresolved in all jurisdictions.

## Liability and Redress Mechanisms

### AI-Specific Liability Frameworks

The question of liability for harm caused by AI systems remains one of the most challenging areas of AI law:

- **EU AI Liability Directive (proposed, COM/2022/496):** Would establish a rebuttable presumption of causation where a plaintiff demonstrates that an AI system violated the AI Act and that the violation is likely to have caused the harm. This would shift the burden of proof from the plaintiff to the AI developer or deployer, addressing the "black box" problem where plaintiffs cannot access or understand the AI system's internal workings.
- **EU Product Liability Directive (revised, Directive (EU) 2024/2853):** The revised directive extends strict product liability to software, including AI systems, treating them as "products" for liability purposes. This means that defective AI systems that cause personal injury or property damage trigger strict liability without the need to prove fault.
- **South Korea:** No AI-specific liability regime. General tort law under Civil Act Articles 750 (general tort liability) and 758 (liability for defective structures/installations) applies. The AI Basic Act does not establish a separate liability framework, relying instead on existing legal principles supplemented by mandatory transparency and risk management requirements.
- **United States:** No federal AI liability statute. Liability is governed by existing frameworks: product liability (Restatement (Third) of Torts: Products Liability), negligence, and, in some jurisdictions, strict liability for abnormally dangerous activities. The question of whether AI systems are "products" or "services" for liability purposes remains unsettled, with significant implications for the applicable standard of care.
- **Germany:** Beyond the EU frameworks, German courts apply general delictual liability (BGB § 823) and product liability law (Produkthaftungsgesetz, ProdHaftG) to AI-related harms. The question of whether the operator or the developer of an AI system bears primary liability remains the subject of active academic and judicial debate.

## Sector-Specific AI Regulation

Beyond comprehensive AI frameworks, all jurisdictions are developing sector-specific AI rules that intersect with general AI legislation:

### Healthcare AI

- **EU:** AI systems used in medical diagnostics are classified as high-risk under Annex III of the AI Act and must also comply with the Medical Device Regulation (MDR, Regulation (EU) 2017/745). This creates dual compliance obligations.
- **South Korea:** The Ministry of Food and Drug Safety (MFDS) regulates AI-based medical devices under the Medical Devices Act (의료기기법), with a dedicated approval pathway for software as a medical device (SaMD). South Korea was among the first countries to approve AI diagnostic tools for clinical use, including Lunit INSIGHT for chest X-ray analysis.
- **United States:** The FDA has cleared or approved over 950 AI/ML-enabled medical devices as of 2025, primarily through the 510(k) pathway. The FDA's 2021 Action Plan for AI/ML-based Software as a Medical Device proposes a "predetermined change control plan" allowing manufacturers to modify AI algorithms without new submissions.
- **Germany:** The Federal Institute for Drugs and Medical Devices (BfArM) implements both the EU MDR and the AI Act for medical AI, with the German Digital Healthcare Act (DVG, 2019) enabling reimbursement of AI-based digital health applications (DiGA) through the statutory health insurance system.

### Financial Services AI

- **EU:** AI systems used for credit scoring and creditworthiness assessment are classified as high-risk (Annex III, Area 5(b)). The CJEU's SCHUFA ruling (C-634/21, 2023) established that automated credit scoring constitutes "automated individual decision-making" under GDPR Article 22.
- **United States:** Federal financial regulators (OCC, FDIC, Federal Reserve, CFPB) issued joint guidance on AI risk management in financial services (2023), emphasizing model risk management (SR 11-7) and fair lending compliance.
- **South Korea:** The Financial Services Commission (FSC) published AI guidelines for the financial sector (2021, updated 2024), requiring explainability and fairness testing for AI used in lending, insurance, and investment decisions.

### Autonomous Vehicles

- **Germany:** Enacted the Autonomous Driving Act (Gesetz zum autonomen Fahren, 2021), becoming the first country to establish a comprehensive legal framework for Level 4 autonomous vehicles, including liability provisions holding the technical supervisor responsible.
- **United States:** NHTSA has issued guidance documents but no comprehensive federal autonomous vehicle legislation has been enacted. State laws vary significantly, with California, Arizona, and Texas having the most permissive testing frameworks.
- **South Korea:** The Autonomous Vehicles Act (자율주행자동차법, 2024) established a regulatory framework for Level 4 autonomous vehicles, including insurance requirements and liability allocation between operators and manufacturers.
