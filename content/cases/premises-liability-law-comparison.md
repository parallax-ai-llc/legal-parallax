## Summary

Premises liability -- the body of law holding property owners and occupiers responsible for injuries sustained by persons on their property due to unsafe conditions -- operates at the intersection of tort law and property law and varies significantly across jurisdictions in its treatment of the duty of care owed to different categories of visitors, the standard of maintenance required, and the availability of defenses. South Korea addresses premises liability primarily through the Civil Act (민법) Article 758, which imposes liability on possessors of structures (공작물) for damage caused by defects in the installation or maintenance of the structure, with a statutory presumption of fault that effectively creates strict liability for structural defects, supplemented by the general tort provision of Article 750 and the Occupational Safety and Health Act (산업안전보건법) for workplace premises. The United States has traditionally applied a tripartite classification system distinguishing between invitees, licensees, and trespassers, with varying duties of care owed to each category, though a growing number of jurisdictions have abandoned this classification in favor of a general reasonable care standard following Rowland v. Christian, 69 Cal.2d 108 (1968), while maintaining special protections for child trespassers under the attractive nuisance doctrine. Germany addresses premises liability through a combination of Verkehrssicherungspflichten (traffic safety duties) developed under BGB Section 823(1), the structural liability provision of BGB Section 836 (liability for building collapse), and the Occupiers' Liability Act principles embedded in general negligence law, with no formal visitor classification system but a flexible duty calibrated to the nature of the premises and the reasonable expectations of entrants. At the European level, the ECHR has recognized state liability under Article 2 for failure to maintain public premises in a condition that protects the right to life, while EU law addresses premises-related liability through the General Product Safety Directive, the Workplace Safety Framework Directive 89/391/EEC, and the Services Directive 2006/123/EC, which collectively establish minimum safety standards for commercial and public premises across member states.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's premises liability regime centers on Civil Act Article 758, a provision specifically addressing liability for structures and improvements.

### Civil Act Article 758 -- Structural Liability

Article 758 provides: "The possessor of a structure shall be liable for damage caused to another by defects in the installation or maintenance of the structure" (공작물의 설치 또는 보존의 하자로 인하여 타인에게 손해를 가한 때에는 공작물점유자가 손해를 배상할 책임이 있다). If the possessor proves due care, liability shifts to the owner. The term "structure" (공작물) is interpreted broadly to include buildings, roads, bridges, escalators, elevators, swimming pools, and other constructed or improved facilities.

The Supreme Court of Korea has held that the "defect" (하자) standard under Article 758 is assessed objectively: whether the structure lacks the safety characteristics that would ordinarily be expected given its type, purpose, and the manner in which it is typically used (Supreme Court Decision 2005Da65678, 2008).

### Presumed Fault

Article 758 creates a presumption of fault against the possessor, effectively imposing a form of strict liability. The possessor bears the burden of proving that they exercised due care in the installation and maintenance of the structure. If the possessor establishes this defense, liability shifts to the owner, who bears absolute liability without a due care defense. This two-tier structure ensures that someone is always liable for structural defects.

### Workplace Premises

The Occupational Safety and Health Act (산업안전보건법), comprehensively amended in 2019, imposes extensive premises safety obligations on employers and business owners. The Serious Accidents Punishment Act (중대재해 처벌 등에 관한 법률) of 2022 further strengthened employer liability for workplace premises defects, imposing criminal penalties and punitive damages (up to five times actual damages) when failure to comply with safety duties results in death or serious injury.

### Key Cases

**Seongsu Bridge Collapse (성수대교 붕괴, 1994):** The collapse of the Seongsu Bridge over the Han River killed 32 people. The Seoul Metropolitan Government was held liable under Article 758 for maintenance defects. The disaster led to comprehensive reforms in infrastructure inspection and maintenance requirements.

**Sampoong Department Store Collapse (삼풍백화점 붕괴, 1995):** The collapse of the Sampoong Department Store killed 502 people, making it the deadliest building collapse in modern Korean history. The building's owner was convicted criminally, and extensive civil liability claims were resolved under Article 758. The disaster prompted the enactment of the Special Act on Safety Control of Structures (시설물의 안전관리에 관한 특별법).

## United States

### Legal Framework

American premises liability law is primarily governed by state common law, with significant jurisdictional variation in the treatment of visitor categories and duty standards.

### Traditional Tripartite Classification

The traditional common law distinguished three categories of entrants, each owed a different duty:

| Category | Definition | Duty Owed |
|----------|-----------|-----------|
| **Invitee** | Person entering for possessor's business purpose or as member of public | Duty to inspect, discover, and make safe (or warn of) unreasonably dangerous conditions |
| **Licensee** | Social guest or person entering with permission but not for possessor's benefit | Duty to warn of known hidden dangers |
| **Trespasser** | Person entering without permission | No duty except to refrain from willful/wanton harm |

### Modern Trend: General Duty of Care

**Rowland v. Christian, 69 Cal.2d 108 (1968):** The California Supreme Court abolished the tripartite classification, holding that a possessor owes a general duty of reasonable care to all entrants regardless of their status. The Court reasoned that the visitor classification system was arbitrary, overly complex, and did not reflect modern social realities. As of 2026, approximately one-third of states have adopted the Rowland approach, while the remaining states retain the traditional classification or a modified version.

### Attractive Nuisance Doctrine

The Restatement (Second) of Torts Section 339 establishes special duties to child trespassers. A possessor is liable for harm to trespassing children caused by artificial conditions on the land if: (1) the possessor knows children are likely to trespass, (2) the condition poses an unreasonable risk of death or serious harm, (3) children cannot appreciate the risk due to youth, (4) the utility of maintaining the condition is slight compared to the risk, and (5) the possessor fails to exercise reasonable care.

### Landlord Liability

Traditional common law provided landlords broad immunity from premises liability once possession was transferred to tenants (the "bare walls" doctrine). This immunity has been substantially eroded. In Sargent v. Ross, 113 N.H. 388 (1973), the New Hampshire Supreme Court abolished landlord immunity, applying a general negligence standard. Most jurisdictions now impose duties on landlords for common areas, hidden defects known at the time of lease, conditions violating housing codes, and areas where the landlord retains control.

### Slip and Fall Litigation

Slip and fall cases constitute the largest single category of premises liability litigation in the United States. The "mode of operation" approach, adopted in Jasko v. F.W. Woolworth Co., 177 Colo. 418 (1972), eliminates the requirement to prove notice in self-service establishments, reasoning that spills are foreseeable incidents of the business operation.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German premises liability operates through general negligence principles, particularly the Verkehrssicherungspflichten doctrine, rather than a specific premises liability statute.

### Verkehrssicherungspflichten (Traffic Safety Duties)

The duty to secure sources of danger (Verkehrssicherungspflicht) is the primary basis for premises liability in German law. Any person who creates, maintains, or controls a source of danger -- including premises open to the public -- is obligated to take reasonable measures to protect those who may foreseeably encounter the danger. The duty is derived from BGB Section 823(1) and calibrated to the specific circumstances.

The Bundesgerichtshof has developed detailed principles for various premises types:

| Premises Type | Duty |
|--------------|------|
| Commercial premises (stores, restaurants) | High duty: regular inspection, prompt hazard removal, adequate lighting |
| Public paths and roads | Municipality duty: surface maintenance, winter service (Streupflicht) |
| Sports and leisure facilities | Duty appropriate to activity risk; participants assume inherent risks |
| Construction sites | Comprehensive securing: fencing, warning signs, supervision |
| Private residences | Limited duty to social visitors; no duty regarding obvious hazards |

### BGB Section 836 -- Building Collapse

Section 836 BGB imposes liability on the possessor of a building or other structure on land for damage caused by the collapse of the building or the detachment of parts of it, unless the possessor proves that they exercised due care in maintenance or that the damage would have occurred despite due care. This provision creates a presumption of fault similar to Korean Article 758.

### Winter Service (Streupflicht)

German law has developed particularly detailed obligations for snow and ice removal (Streupflicht). Property owners and municipalities must clear sidewalks and pathways during winter months. The BGH has established that the duty typically extends from 7:00 AM to 8:00 PM on weekdays and from 8:00 or 9:00 AM on weekends and holidays. The duty applies to the sidewalk adjacent to the property and, in commercial areas, may extend to parking areas and access paths.

### Assumption of Risk (Handeln auf eigene Gefahr)

German law recognizes that participants in inherently dangerous activities (sports, recreational activities) assume certain risks. The BGH applies a proportionality analysis: the Verkehrssicherungspflicht is reduced for activities where participants knowingly accept inherent dangers, but the possessor remains liable for dangers beyond the inherent risks of the activity.

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR has addressed premises-related liability through the positive obligations doctrine under Article 2 (right to life).

**Öneryıldız v. Turkey (Application No. 48939/99, Grand Chamber Judgment of November 30, 2004):** Turkey was held liable under Article 2 for the deaths of 39 people in a methane explosion at a waste dump, establishing that states have a positive obligation to ensure that premises under their regulatory authority do not pose foreseeable risks to life.

**Kolyadenko and Others v. Russia (Application No. 17423/05, Judgment of February 28, 2012):** The Court found Russia violated Article 2 when flooding from an improperly maintained reservoir caused deaths, holding that the state's failure to maintain infrastructure constituted a breach of its positive obligation to protect life.

**Cevrioğlu v. Turkey (Application No. 69546/12, Judgment of October 4, 2016):** A diving accident at a hotel pool led to Article 2 findings against Turkey for inadequate safety regulations and inspection of commercial leisure facilities.

### EU Workplace Safety Directive

Directive 89/391/EEC (Framework Directive on Safety and Health at Work) requires employers across the EU to ensure the safety and health of workers in every aspect related to work, including premises safety. The Directive mandates risk assessment, prevention planning, and workplace safety measures. Specific daughter directives address minimum safety requirements for workplaces (Directive 89/654/EEC), including floor conditions, emergency exits, lighting, ventilation, and temperature.

### Services Directive

Directive 2006/123/EC (Services Directive) requires member states to ensure that service providers comply with national safety requirements for premises where services are provided to consumers. Article 26 addresses the quality of services, including requirements for professional liability insurance and safety standards for commercial premises.

## Comparative Analysis

### Visitor Classification vs. Universal Duty

The most fundamental structural difference is whether jurisdictions categorize entrants and assign different duties accordingly:

**Classification Systems (Traditional U.S.):** The tripartite invitee/licensee/trespasser distinction creates categorical duties, providing predictability but potentially arbitrary results. The distinction between a social guest (licensee) and a business visitor (invitee) may result in different recovery outcomes for identical injuries from identical hazards.

**Universal Duty (Modern U.S., Germany, South Korea):** The trend toward a single standard of reasonable care, calibrated to circumstances, provides flexibility but may reduce predictability. South Korea's Article 758 effectively imposes strict liability for structural defects regardless of the visitor's status.

### Structural Defect Liability

South Korea (Article 758) and Germany (Section 836) both create presumptions of fault for structural defects, but differ in their fallback provisions. Korean law shifts liability to the owner if the possessor establishes due care; German law provides no such secondary liability. The American approach generally requires the plaintiff to prove negligence in the maintenance of the structure.

### Winter Maintenance

German law has developed the most detailed framework for winter premises maintenance (Streupflicht), with specific time windows, methods, and area requirements. American jurisdictions vary between the "natural accumulation rule" (no liability for natural accumulations of snow and ice) and affirmative clearing duties. South Korea imposes municipal winter maintenance obligations but with less detailed judicial development.
