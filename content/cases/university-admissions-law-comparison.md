## Summary

University admissions law -- the legal framework governing how students are selected for admission to higher education institutions, including the permissibility of affirmative action, quota systems, standardized testing, and legacy preferences -- has produced some of the most consequential constitutional jurisprudence of the modern era, directly implicating equality, meritocracy, and social mobility. South Korea operates the College Scholastic Ability Test (대학수학능력시험, CSAT or Suneung) as the centerpiece of its university admissions system, supplemented by school records (학생부) and institution-specific selection criteria, with the government exercising direct control over admissions policies through the Higher Education Act (고등교육법) and annual admissions guidelines; the country has grappled with fairness controversies including the Cho Kuk scandal (2019), which exposed alleged manipulation of extracurricular activities and academic credentials for elite university admissions, leading to significant reforms restricting the use of subjective criteria. The United States experienced a seismic shift with the Supreme Court's decision in Students for Fair Admissions v. Harvard (2023), which held that race-conscious admissions programs at Harvard College and the University of North Carolina violated the Equal Protection Clause, effectively ending decades of affirmative action in university admissions that had been permitted under Regents of the University of California v. Bakke (1978) and Grutter v. Bollinger (2003); universities are now developing race-neutral alternatives while navigating the decision's implications for diversity. Germany's university admissions are governed by the Grundgesetz Article 12(1) (freedom of occupation and right to choose a place of training) as interpreted by the Federal Constitutional Court's landmark Numerus Clausus decisions (BVerfGE 33, 303, 1972; BVerfGE 147, 253, 2017), which established that capacity-limited (NC) programs must use constitutionally adequate selection criteria and that exclusive reliance on the Abitur grade violates Article 12(1); the Staatsvertrag über die Hochschulzulassung (2019) reformed the centralized admissions system following the 2017 BVerfG decision. At the European level, the ECHR Protocol 1, Article 2 (right to education) applies to university admissions, the EU's anti-discrimination directives provide a framework for challenging discriminatory admissions, and the CJEU has addressed cross-border admissions restrictions in landmark cases including Commission v. Austria (C-147/03, 2005) and Bressol v. Gouvernement de la Communauté française (C-73/08, 2010) concerning free movement of students within the EU.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

University admissions in South Korea are among the most high-stakes and heavily regulated processes in the world, reflecting the society's intense emphasis on educational achievement and the critical role of university prestige in career outcomes.

**Higher Education Act (고등교육법):** Article 34 authorizes universities to select students through methods prescribed by presidential decree. The Ministry of Education issues annual admissions guidelines (대학입학전형 기본사항) that specify the ratio of different admissions tracks and permissible selection criteria.

### Admissions Tracks

| Track | Korean Name | Description | Share (approx.) |
|-------|-------------|-------------|----------------|
| CSAT-based (정시) | 정시모집 | Selection primarily based on CSAT scores | ~40% |
| Early admission (수시) | 수시모집 | School records, recommendations, extracurriculars | ~60% |
| - School record comprehensive | 학생부종합전형 | Holistic review of school records and activities | ~25% |
| - School record quantitative | 학생부교과전형 | GPA-based selection | ~25% |
| - Other early tracks | 논술/실기 등 | Essay exams, practical skill tests | ~10% |

### The CSAT (수능)

The College Scholastic Ability Test (대학수학능력시험, commonly called Suneung) is administered once annually, typically in November, to approximately 500,000 students. The exam covers Korean Language, Mathematics, English, Korean History, Exploration Subjects (social studies/science), and a Second Foreign Language. The CSAT is notable for the extraordinary social mobilization surrounding it: police provide escorts for late students, airplane flights are rerouted during the English listening section, and the stock market opens later on test day.

### The Cho Kuk Scandal (2019)

The Cho Kuk (조국) scandal represented the most significant crisis in Korean university admissions fairness. Cho Kuk, nominated as Minister of Justice by President Moon Jae-in, was investigated for allegedly manipulating his daughter's academic credentials to secure admission to Korea University medical school. Allegations included fabricated internship certificates, improper co-authorship on academic papers, and falsified extracurricular activities. The scandal triggered massive protests and fundamental reforms to the early admissions process.

**Reform Consequences:**
- The Ministry of Education restricted the use of extracurricular activities and self-introduction letters in the school record comprehensive track.
- Universities were prohibited from considering certain external activities (awards, certifications, volunteer work) in admissions.
- The CSAT-based (정시) track was expanded from approximately 30% to 40% of admissions at Seoul-area universities.
- The Korea University professor who allegedly provided the fraudulent internship certificate was prosecuted.

### Equity Measures

South Korea employs several mechanisms to address regional and socioeconomic disparities:
- Regional quotas: Universities in Seoul are required to admit a minimum percentage of students from non-Seoul regions.
- Opportunity balanced selection (기회균형전형): Reserved slots for students from low-income families, multicultural families, and North Korean defectors.
- Rural area special admissions (농어촌전형): Preferences for students from rural areas.

## United States

### Legal Framework

University admissions law in the United States has been dominated by the constitutional debate over race-conscious admissions (affirmative action), resolved by the Supreme Court in 2023.

### Key Supreme Court Decisions

**Regents of the University of California v. Bakke, 438 U.S. 265 (1978):** Justice Powell's controlling opinion held that racial quotas violate the Equal Protection Clause but that race may be considered as one factor in a holistic admissions process. Powell invoked academic freedom and the educational benefits of diversity as compelling interests.

**Grutter v. Bollinger, 543 U.S. 306 (2003):** The Court upheld the University of Michigan Law School's holistic race-conscious admissions program, holding that the educational benefits of student body diversity constitute a compelling state interest. Justice O'Connor's majority opinion expressed the expectation that race-conscious admissions would no longer be necessary 25 years hence.

**Gratz v. Bollinger, 539 U.S. 244 (2003):** The Court struck down the University of Michigan's undergraduate admissions point system, which automatically awarded 20 points for minority race, as not narrowly tailored.

**Students for Fair Admissions v. Harvard, 600 U.S. 181 (2023):** The Court held that the admissions programs at Harvard and UNC violate the Equal Protection Clause. Chief Justice Roberts' majority opinion held that the programs lacked sufficiently focused and measurable objectives, used race as a negative for some applicants, involved racial stereotyping, and lacked a meaningful endpoint. The decision effectively ended race-conscious admissions at American universities.

### Post-SFFA Landscape

Following the 2023 decision, universities have adopted various race-neutral strategies:
- Increased emphasis on socioeconomic factors and first-generation status
- Expansion of geographic diversity initiatives targeting underserved areas
- Test-optional or test-free admissions policies
- "Personal experience" essays allowing applicants to discuss how race has affected their lives (permitted under SFFA)
- Partnerships with community organizations in underrepresented communities

### Legacy and Donor Preferences

Legacy admissions (preferential treatment for children of alumni) and donor preferences have come under increased scrutiny post-SFFA. A 2023 Department of Education investigation into Harvard's legacy admissions practices highlighted the tension between ending race-conscious admissions while maintaining preferences that disproportionately benefit affluent white applicants. Several states (Colorado, Virginia, Maryland) have enacted or considered legislation restricting legacy preferences at public universities. Johns Hopkins and Amherst College voluntarily eliminated legacy preferences, reporting no significant decline in alumni engagement.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German university admissions are governed by the constitutional right to freely choose a place of education under Article 12(1) Grundgesetz, as interpreted by the Federal Constitutional Court's Numerus Clausus jurisprudence.

### Numerus Clausus Decisions

**BVerfGE 33, 303 (1972) -- Numerus Clausus I:** The foundational decision held that Article 12(1) GG guarantees a derivative right of access (derivatives Teilhaberecht) to existing university places. Where demand exceeds capacity, admission restrictions must be justified, proportionate, and based on rational selection criteria. The Court required that capacity be fully exhausted and that selection criteria be constitutionally adequate.

**BVerfGE 147, 253 (2017) -- Numerus Clausus II (Medical School Admissions):** The Court held that the admissions system for medical school was partially unconstitutional. Key holdings:
- Exclusive reliance on the Abitur grade for a quota of places (Abiturbestenquote) was constitutional, but only if Abitur grades are sufficiently comparable across Länder.
- The waiting time quota (Wartezeitquote), which admitted students based on semesters waited, was unconstitutional as disproportionate because excessive waiting periods undermined the right to education.
- University-specific selection procedures (Auswahlverfahren der Hochschulen) must use multiple criteria, not just the Abitur grade.

### Staatsvertrag 2019 (State Treaty on University Admissions)

Following the 2017 BVerfG decision, the Länder negotiated a new Staatsvertrag (state treaty) on university admissions, effective December 2019. Key reforms:

| Quota | Share | Selection Criteria |
|-------|-------|-------------------|
| Abiturbestenquote (best Abitur grades) | 30% | Abitur grade with Land-specific adjustments |
| Additional aptitude quota (Eignungsquote) | 10% | University-defined criteria (may not include Abitur grade) |
| University selection (Auswahlverfahren) | 60% | Must use at least two criteria; Abitur grade + at least one aptitude criterion |
| Hardship quota (Härtefallquote) | Up to 2% | Social hardship cases |
| Pre-selected quota (Vorabquote) | Varies | Foreign students, second-degree students, military |

### Stiftung für Hochschulzulassung

The Stiftung für Hochschulzulassung (Foundation for University Admissions, formerly ZVS) coordinates centralized admissions for capacity-restricted programs (currently medicine, dentistry, veterinary medicine, and pharmacy) through the Dialogorientiertes Serviceverfahren (DoSV) platform.

### Test for Medical Studies (TMS)

The Test für Medizinische Studiengänge (TMS), a subject-specific aptitude test for medical school, has gained importance following the 2017 BVerfG decision's requirement for multiple selection criteria. Approximately 90% of German medical faculties now consider TMS scores as part of their selection procedures.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Protocol 1, Article 2

The right to education applies to university admissions, though states retain a wide margin of appreciation:

**Leyla Şahin v. Turkey (Grand Chamber, 2005):** While primarily about the headscarf ban, the Court confirmed that access to higher education falls within the scope of Protocol 1, Article 2, but that states have discretion in regulating admissions.

**Mürsel Eren v. Turkey (2006):** The Court found a violation of Protocol 1, Article 2 where a student was denied university admission based on an arbitrary and unjustified annulment of his exam results.

### Free Movement and University Admissions

The CJEU has addressed restrictions on cross-border university admissions as potential violations of EU free movement and non-discrimination principles:

**Commission v. Austria (C-147/03, 2005):** The Court held that Austria's requirement that foreign students hold a university entrance qualification from their home country constituted indirect discrimination based on nationality, violating Article 18 TFEU (non-discrimination) and Article 165 TFEU.

**Bressol v. Gouvernement de la Communauté française (C-73/08, 2010):** The Court addressed Belgium's cap on non-resident students in medical and paramedical programs. The Court held that a quota limiting non-resident students could be justified if necessary to protect public health (ensuring sufficient domestically trained healthcare professionals) but must be proportionate and evidence-based.

### Bologna Process

The Bologna Process has significant implications for university admissions through its harmonization of degree structures (bachelor/master) and recognition of qualifications. The Lisbon Recognition Convention (1997) requires that qualifications from one signatory state be recognized in another unless "substantially different," facilitating cross-border mobility and admission.

### EU Anti-Discrimination Framework

Directive 2000/43/EC (Racial Equality Directive) and Directive 2000/78/EC (Equal Treatment Directive) apply to access to education, prohibiting discrimination based on racial or ethnic origin, religion, disability, age, and sexual orientation. These directives provide a framework for challenging discriminatory admissions practices in EU member states but do not directly address affirmative action.

## Comparative Analysis

### Meritocracy Conceptions

Each jurisdiction reflects a different conception of meritocracy in university admissions. South Korea's CSAT-centric system represents the most purely examination-based model, reflecting a belief that standardized testing provides objective and fair assessment. The United States' holistic admissions model -- now constrained by SFFA's prohibition on race-conscious criteria -- reflects a broader conception of merit encompassing experiences, character, and potential contributions to the educational community. Germany's multi-criteria approach, mandated by the 2017 BVerfG decision, requires a combination of academic achievement and aptitude assessments. The EU framework emphasizes non-discrimination and free movement rather than prescribing any particular merit conception.

### Government Control

South Korea exercises the most direct government control over university admissions, with the Ministry of Education setting track ratios and permissible criteria. Germany's government role is mediated through the Länder and the Staatsvertrag but includes constitutional requirements from the BVerfG. The United States provides the greatest institutional autonomy, with universities designing their own admissions processes subject to constitutional constraints. The EU respects member state educational sovereignty.

### Equity vs. Uniformity

South Korea addresses equity through targeted quotas (rural students, low-income families) within a standardized framework. The United States has moved from race-conscious admissions to socioeconomic and geographic alternatives. Germany uses hardship quotas and is piloting socioeconomic bonus schemes at some universities. The fundamental tension between standardized selection (promoting uniformity and perceived fairness) and holistic selection (accommodating diverse backgrounds and abilities) remains unresolved in all jurisdictions.
