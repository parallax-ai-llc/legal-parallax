## Summary

Rent control and rent regulation represent one of the most politically contentious areas of housing law worldwide. These laws aim to protect tenants from excessive rent increases, ensure housing affordability, and prevent displacement, while critics argue they distort housing markets and reduce supply. South Korea employs a relatively recent tenant protection framework anchored in the Housing Lease Protection Act (주택임대차보호법), significantly strengthened by the "Tenant 3 Act" reforms of 2020 that introduced a 5% rent increase cap during lease renewals and a contract renewal request right (계약갱신요구권), though these measures have generated fierce debate about their effects on jeonse (lump-sum deposit) and monthly rental markets. The United States has no federal rent control legislation, with regulation occurring exclusively at the state and municipal level; New York City's rent stabilization system -- governing approximately one million units under the Rent Stabilization Law of 1969 and the Housing Stability and Tenant Protection Act of 2019 -- represents the most extensive regime, while over 30 states have enacted preemption laws prohibiting local rent control. Germany's approach is codified in the Civil Code (Buergerliches Gesetzbuch, BGB) Sections 556d-556g, establishing the Mietpreisbremse (rent brake) that limits new-tenancy rents to 10% above the local reference rent (Mietspiegel), supplemented by Berlin's failed experiment with the Mietendeckel (rent cap) that was struck down by the Federal Constitutional Court in 2021. The ECHR framework protects tenants' rights under Article 8 (respect for home) and Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (protection of property), requiring states to balance landlord property rights against tenant housing needs, as established in cases such as Hutten-Czapska v. Poland (2006) and Bittó v. Slovakia (2014).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's residential rental market is uniquely shaped by the jeonse (전세) system -- a deposit-based lease arrangement where tenants pay a large lump-sum deposit (typically 50-80% of the property value) instead of monthly rent, with the deposit returned at lease termination. This system, combined with monthly rental (월세) arrangements, is regulated primarily through the **Housing Lease Protection Act** (주택임대차보호법, HLPA), first enacted in 1981.

The HLPA establishes several foundational tenant protections:

- **Minimum Lease Duration:** Article 4 provides that residential leases are deemed to have a minimum duration of two years, regardless of any shorter period agreed by the parties. If a lease is concluded for less than two years, the tenant may assert the two-year minimum.
- **Deposit Security:** Articles 3-3-2 establish a priority claim system for tenant deposits, granting tenants with confirmed residence dates (확정일자) priority over subsequent secured creditors in the event of the landlord's insolvency or property sale.
- **Implied Renewal (묵시적 갱신):** Article 6 provides that if neither party gives notice of termination between six months and two months before the lease expiration, the lease is deemed renewed on the same terms.

### The Tenant 3 Act (임대차 3법, 2020)

On July 31, 2020, the National Assembly enacted the "Tenant 3 Act" -- a package of three legislative amendments that represented the most significant strengthening of tenant protections in decades:

1. **Contract Renewal Request Right (계약갱신요구권, Article 6-3):** Tenants gained the right to request one renewal of their lease, which landlords can refuse only on specified grounds (e.g., landlord's own occupancy, building reconstruction, tenant default). This effectively extends the minimum tenancy from two to four years.

2. **Rent Increase Cap (Article 7):** During a contract renewal, the landlord may not increase the rent or deposit by more than 5% of the previous amount. This applies to both monthly rent increases and jeonse deposit increases.

3. **Jeonse/Monthly Rent Reporting System:** The Real Estate Transaction Reporting Act was amended to require reporting of all residential lease contracts to local authorities, creating transparency in the rental market.

### Constitutional Challenge

The Constitutional Court of Korea upheld the Tenant 3 Act in a 2021 decision, finding that the 5% cap and renewal request right constituted a proportionate restriction on landlords' property rights under Article 23 of the Constitution, given the compelling public interest in housing stability. The Court noted that landlords retain the right to set initial rents freely and may refuse renewal on enumerated grounds.

### Market Impact and Controversy

The Tenant 3 Act generated intense debate about unintended market consequences:

- **Jeonse Cliff (전세절벽):** Critics argued that the 5% cap incentivized landlords to dramatically increase rents at the initial lease stage, leading to a surge in jeonse deposits of 20-40% in Seoul's popular districts during 2020-2021.
- **Shift to Monthly Rent:** The proportion of jeonse leases declined from 45.7% in 2019 to 36.1% in 2023, as landlords shifted to monthly rental arrangements to maintain flexibility.
- **Supply Reduction:** The Korea Real Estate Board reported that the number of rental units listed declined by approximately 18% in Seoul in the year following enactment.

### Recent Developments

In 2024, the Yoon Suk-yeol administration proposed amendments to relax the Tenant 3 Act, including raising the renewal refusal grounds and introducing a "market rent adjustment" mechanism for areas where the 5% cap had created significant divergence from market rates. As of early 2026, these amendments remain under National Assembly deliberation, with progressive opposition parties blocking modifications.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has no federal rent control legislation. The regulation of residential rents falls exclusively within state and local jurisdiction, creating a highly fragmented landscape. The most significant rent control systems operate in New York City, California, Oregon, and a handful of other jurisdictions.

### New York City: The Most Extensive System

New York City operates the oldest and most comprehensive rent regulation system in the United States, covering approximately one million apartments -- roughly half of all rental units in the city.

**Rent Control (NYC Administrative Code, Title 26, Chapter 3):** Applies to apartments in buildings constructed before February 1, 1947, where the tenant (or lawful successor) has been in continuous occupancy since July 1, 1971. Approximately 16,400 apartments remain under rent control as of 2025. The Maximum Base Rent (MBR) system sets permissible rents, adjusted biennially.

**Rent Stabilization (Rent Stabilization Law of 1969; RSC, 9 NYCRR Section 2520 et seq.):** Applies to apartments in buildings with six or more units constructed before January 1, 1974. The **Rent Guidelines Board (RGB)** annually sets the maximum percentage increase permitted for one-year and two-year lease renewals. Recent RGB determinations:

| Year | 1-Year Lease Increase | 2-Year Lease Increase |
|------|----------------------|----------------------|
| 2022 | 3.25% | 5.0% |
| 2023 | 3.0% | 2.75% |
| 2024 | 2.75% | 5.25% |
| 2025 | 2.5% | 4.75% |

**Housing Stability and Tenant Protection Act (HSTPA, 2019):** This landmark legislation dramatically strengthened tenant protections by:

- Eliminating **vacancy decontrol** (previously, apartments became deregulated when the legal rent exceeded a threshold upon vacancy).
- Repealing the **vacancy bonus** (landlords could no longer automatically increase rent by 20% upon a new tenant taking occupancy).
- Capping **Individual Apartment Improvements (IAIs)** at $15,000 over 15 years, with rent increases limited to 1/168th of the cost (previously 1/40th for buildings with 35+ units).
- Strengthening **succession rights** for family members.
- Eliminating **preferential rent** reversion upon lease renewal.

### State Preemption Laws

Over 30 states have enacted laws preempting local governments from adopting rent control ordinances. These statutes effectively prohibit rent regulation throughout the state:

- **Texas Property Code Section 214.902:** Prohibits any municipality from adopting rent control ordinances.
- **Florida Statutes Section 166.043:** Bans rent control except during a housing emergency declared by the governor.
- **Georgia Code Section 44-7-19:** Preempts all local rent control ordinances.

### Oregon: Statewide Rent Stabilization (2019)

Oregon became the first state to enact a statewide rent stabilization measure when it passed **SB 608** (2019), codified at ORS 90.323. The law caps annual rent increases at 7% plus the Consumer Price Index (CPI), applies to all residential tenancies in buildings older than 15 years, and requires landlords to provide 90 days' notice for any rent increase exceeding 5%. Buildings less than 15 years old are exempt to avoid discouraging new construction.

### California: The Tenant Protection Act (2019)

**AB 1482 (Tenant Protection Act of 2019)**, codified at California Civil Code Sections 1946.2 and 1947.12, established statewide rent caps at 5% plus local CPI (maximum 10%) for buildings older than 15 years. The Act also introduced just-cause eviction protections for tenants who have occupied a unit for more than 12 months. The law sunsets on January 1, 2030.

### Constitutional Framework

Rent control has survived federal constitutional challenges. In **Yee v. City of Escondido** (503 U.S. 519, 1992), the Supreme Court rejected a Takings Clause challenge to a mobile home rent control ordinance, holding that rent control does not constitute a physical taking. In **Pennell v. City of San Jose** (485 U.S. 1, 1988), the Court upheld a rent control ordinance that considered tenant hardship in setting rents. However, **Cedar Point Nursery v. Hassid** (594 U.S. 139, 2021) signaled greater scrutiny of regulations that grant third parties access to private property.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's rent regulation system is among the most comprehensive in Europe, reflecting the country's strong renter culture -- approximately 50.4% of German households rent their homes, the second-highest rate in the EU after Switzerland. The legal framework is codified primarily in the **Buergerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB)** and supplemented by state-level regulations.

### Existing Tenancy Protections (BGB Sections 535-580a)

German tenancy law provides extensive baseline protections:

- **Indefinite Tenancies:** Section 542 BGB establishes that residential leases are presumed to be of indefinite duration. Fixed-term leases are permitted only if the landlord has a legitimate reason (Section 575 BGB), such as planned personal occupancy or substantial renovation.
- **Termination Protection (Kuendigungsschutz):** Section 573 BGB provides that landlords may only terminate an indefinite lease for "legitimate interest" (berechtigtes Interesse), including personal need (Eigenbedarf), material breach by the tenant, or economic necessity. Notice periods range from 3 months (tenancies under 5 years) to 9 months (tenancies over 8 years).
- **Rent Increase Limits (Existing Tenancies):** Sections 558-558e BGB cap rent increases for existing tenancies to the **local reference rent** (ortsübliche Vergleichsmiete) as established by the Mietspiegel (rent index). Increases may not exceed 20% over three years (reduced to 15% in designated "tight housing market" areas under Section 558(3)).

### The Mietpreisbremse (Rent Brake, 2015)

Enacted through the **Mietrechtsnovellierungsgesetz** (Rent Law Amendment Act) of April 2015, the Mietpreisbremse (BGB Sections 556d-556g) addresses rent levels in new tenancies:

- **Section 556d:** In areas designated by state governments as "tight housing markets" (angespannte Wohnungsmaerkte), the rent at the beginning of a new tenancy may not exceed 10% above the local reference rent.
- **Section 556e:** Exceptions exist for new construction (first occupancy after October 1, 2014), comprehensive modernization, and cases where the previous tenant's rent already exceeded the cap.
- **Section 556f:** Furnished apartments and temporary subleases are partially exempt.
- **Section 556g:** Tenants may demand disclosure of the prior rent from the landlord and reclaim any excess rent paid.

The Mietpreisbremse was strengthened in 2019 and 2020:

- Landlords must now **proactively disclose** the previous tenant's rent before the lease is signed.
- Tenants may reclaim excess rent from the **beginning of the tenancy** (previously only from the date of formal complaint).
- The designation period for tight housing markets was extended to 2025, and further extended to 2029 by the Bundestag in 2024.

### The Berlin Mietendeckel (2020-2021)

On February 23, 2020, the **Gesetz zur Mietenbegrenzung im Wohnungswesen in Berlin** (Berlin Rent Cap Act, commonly known as the Mietendeckel) entered into force. This was the most ambitious rent control measure in modern German history:

- **Rent Freeze:** All rents for approximately 1.5 million apartments in Berlin were frozen at their June 18, 2019 levels for five years.
- **Rent Caps (Mietobergrenzen):** Maximum permissible rents were set by table based on the building's construction year, equipment, and location, ranging from EUR 3.92 to EUR 9.80 per square meter.
- **Rent Reduction Requirement:** From November 2020, landlords were required to reduce rents that exceeded the caps by more than 20%.
- **Penalties:** Violations were punishable by fines of up to EUR 500,000.

On **April 15, 2021**, the Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) struck down the Mietendeckel in decision **2 BvF 1/20, 2 BvL 4/20, 2 BvL 5/20**, holding that the Berlin state legislature lacked competence to enact the law. The Court ruled that the federal Mietpreisbremse constituted a "conclusive" (abschliessend) federal regulation of rent levels for new tenancies, and that under Article 72(1) of the Basic Law, state legislatures were precluded from enacting parallel regulations in the same field. The decision did not address the substantive merits of rent caps.

| Feature | Mietpreisbremse (Federal) | Mietendeckel (Berlin, struck down) |
|---------|--------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| Scope | New tenancies in designated areas | All existing and new tenancies |
| Cap | 10% above reference rent | Absolute caps by building category |
| Rent freeze | No | Yes (5-year freeze) |
| Rent reduction | No | Yes (rents exceeding cap by >20%) |
| Constitutional status | Upheld (BVerfG, August 20, 2019) | Struck down (BVerfG, April 15, 2021) |

### Constitutional Court Jurisprudence

The BVerfG has addressed rent regulation in several key decisions:

- **BVerfG, August 20, 2019 (1 BvL 1/18):** Upheld the Mietpreisbremse as a proportionate restriction on landlord property rights under Article 14 of the Basic Law, finding that it served a legitimate public interest in affordable housing.
- **BVerfGE 37, 132 (1974):** Held that rent regulation may restrict property rights (Article 14 GG) provided it serves the social obligation of property (Sozialpflichtigkeit des Eigentums, Article 14(2) GG).

## ECHR & EU

### European Convention on Human Rights

The ECHR framework addresses rent regulation primarily through two provisions:

**Article 8 (Right to Respect for Private and Family Life and Home):** Protects the tenant's right to respect for their home. Eviction or forced displacement may constitute an interference with Article 8 rights, requiring justification as necessary in a democratic society.

**Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (Protection of Property):** Protects the landlord's property rights, including the right to enjoy economic returns from rental property. Rent control constitutes an interference with this right, requiring justification as proportionate to a legitimate aim.

### Key ECHR Cases

**Hutten-Czapska v. Poland (Application No. 35014/97, Grand Chamber, June 19, 2006):**
This landmark case involved Poland's system of statutory rent ceilings that kept rents far below market levels and maintenance costs. The Grand Chamber found a violation of Article 1 of Protocol No. 1, holding that the Polish system imposed a "disproportionate and excessive burden" on landlords by preventing them from receiving rents sufficient to cover maintenance costs. The Court emphasized that while rent control serves a legitimate social aim, states must strike a "fair balance" between the general interest of the community and the protection of property rights. The Court issued a "pilot judgment" (its first in a property case), requiring Poland to adopt systemic measures to reform its rent control regime.

**Bittó and Others v. Slovakia (Application No. 30255/09, January 28, 2014):**
Slovak rent control legislation kept rents for certain tenants at levels representing approximately 4% of market rents. The Court found a violation of Article 1 of Protocol No. 1, holding that the extreme disproportion between controlled and market rents, combined with the duration of the restrictions (over 20 years), imposed an excessive individual burden on landlords.

**Lindheim and Others v. Norway (Application No. 13221/08, June 12, 2012):**
Norway's Ground Lease Act allowed tenants to extend ground leases indefinitely at the original rent. The Court found a violation of Article 1 of Protocol No. 1, concluding that the legislation failed to strike a fair balance between the parties' interests.

**Mellacher v. Austria (Application No. 10522/83, December 19, 1989):**
The Court upheld Austria's Rent Act, which reduced rents by up to 80%, finding that it fell within the state's wide margin of appreciation in housing policy. This remains a leading authority for the proposition that even drastic rent reductions may be compatible with the Convention.

### Core ECHR Principles on Rent Control

| Principle | Source Case | Rule |
|-----------|-------------|------|
| Rent control must achieve fair balance | Hutten-Czapska v. Poland | States must balance tenant protection against landlord property rights |
| Rents must cover maintenance costs | Hutten-Czapska v. Poland | Rents set below maintenance costs impose disproportionate burden |
| Duration matters | Bittó v. Slovakia | Prolonged extreme controls violate proportionality |
| Wide margin of appreciation | Mellacher v. Austria | States have broad discretion in housing policy |
| Systemic reform may be required | Hutten-Czapska v. Poland (pilot judgment) | Structural violations require legislative reform |

### EU Framework

The European Union has no direct competence over rent regulation, which falls within member-state housing policy. However, several EU-level instruments are relevant:

- **European Pillar of Social Rights (Principle 19, Housing and Assistance for the Homeless):** Declares that access to social housing or housing assistance of good quality shall be provided for those in need, and that vulnerable people have the right to appropriate assistance and protection against forced eviction.
- **European Parliament Resolution on Decent Housing for All (January 21, 2021):** Called on member states to ensure adequate housing affordability and recommended that member states "develop and implement effective rent regulation and tenant protection policies."
- **EU Urban Agenda Housing Partnership (2018):** Published recommendations encouraging member states to adopt transparent rent regulation mechanisms and housing supply strategies.

## Comparative Analysis

### Regulatory Philosophy

The four jurisdictions reveal fundamentally different approaches to the tension between market freedom and tenant protection:

- **South Korea** adopted a targeted intervention model with the Tenant 3 Act, capping renewal increases at 5% while leaving initial rents unregulated -- an approach that generated significant market distortions as landlords front-loaded increases.
- **The United States** exhibits maximum regulatory fragmentation, with some jurisdictions (NYC) maintaining extensive controls dating to the 1940s, while others constitutionally prohibit any rent regulation, reflecting deep ideological divisions about the proper role of government in housing markets.
- **Germany** pursues a layered approach combining strong baseline tenant protections (indefinite tenancies, termination restrictions) with the Mietpreisbremse for new tenancies, all within a constitutional framework that recognizes the "social obligation" of property.
- **The ECHR** provides a proportionality framework requiring states to balance competing rights, without prescribing specific regulatory models, while establishing a floor below which rent controls become disproportionate burdens on landlord property rights.

### Effectiveness and Market Distortions

All jurisdictions confront the fundamental economic tension of rent regulation: short-term tenant protection versus long-term supply effects. South Korea's experience with the Tenant 3 Act illustrates how rent caps can paradoxically increase initial rents. New York City's experience demonstrates that long-term rent stabilization, while protecting existing tenants, may reduce rental housing supply and quality. Germany's Mietpreisbremse has been criticized for weak enforcement, with studies finding that 36% of new Berlin tenancies exceeded the legal cap in 2022.

### Constitutional Dimensions

Property rights protections provide the primary constitutional constraint on rent regulation across all jurisdictions. South Korea and Germany have both seen their highest courts validate rent regulation as proportionate restrictions on property rights, while the German experience with the Mietendeckel illustrates the federalism constraint -- substance may be acceptable but competence allocation may not.
