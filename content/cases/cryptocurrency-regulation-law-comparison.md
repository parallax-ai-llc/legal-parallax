## Summary

The regulation of cryptocurrency and digital assets has rapidly evolved from a legal afterthought to a central challenge of financial governance, with jurisdictions adopting strikingly divergent approaches. South Korea enacted the Act on the Protection of Virtual Asset Users (VAUPA) in 2023, effective July 2024, imposing rigorous consumer protection obligations on virtual asset service providers (VASPs) including asset segregation, insurance requirements, and criminal penalties for unfair trading practices. The United States has pursued a fragmented regulatory path, with oversight split among the SEC, CFTC, FinCEN, and state regulators, though the GENIUS Act (2025) established the first federal stablecoin framework and signaled a pro-innovation policy shift. Germany, operating within the EU framework, was among the first to integrate crypto-assets into banking regulation through amendments to the Kreditwesengesetz (KWG) and has emerged as a leading jurisdiction for Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA) licensing through BaFin. The European Union's MiCA Regulation (Regulation (EU) 2023/1114), fully applicable since December 30, 2024, represents arguably the most comprehensive crypto-asset regulatory framework in the world, covering issuance, trading, and custody of crypto-assets across all 27 member states.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea has developed one of the most detailed cryptocurrency regulatory frameworks in Asia, driven by the country's exceptionally high rates of crypto adoption (estimated 13-15% of the population holds crypto-assets).

**Act on the Protection of Virtual Asset Users (VAUPA, 2023):**

Passed by the National Assembly on June 30, 2023, and effective July 19, 2024, the VAUPA represents South Korea's first comprehensive crypto-specific legislation. Key provisions:

| Provision | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Article 2 (Definitions)** | Defines "virtual assets" broadly, excluding CBDCs, securities tokens (regulated under Capital Markets Act), and NFTs with limited exchange function |
| **Article 5 (Asset Segregation)** | VASPs must segregate customer deposits at banks and customer virtual assets in cold wallets; commingling prohibited |
| **Article 6 (Insurance/Reserves)** | VASPs must maintain insurance or reserves to cover losses from hacking, system failures, or operational errors |
| **Article 7 (Deposit Interest)** | VASPs must pay interest on customer fiat deposits held at banks |
| **Article 10 (Unfair Trading Prohibition)** | Prohibits market manipulation, insider trading, and fraudulent schemes involving virtual assets |
| **Article 17 (Reporting)** | VASPs must report suspicious transactions to the Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU) |
| **Articles 19-24 (Penalties)** | Criminal penalties for unfair trading: up to life imprisonment for profits exceeding 5 billion won; up to 1 year imprisonment or 30 million won fine for disclosure violations |

**Specific Act on Reporting and Using Specified Financial Transaction Information (SFRTA):**

Prior to the VAUPA, the SFRTA (amended in 2021) required VASPs to register with the Korea Financial Intelligence Unit (KoFIU), implement real-name bank accounts for customer transactions, and obtain Information Security Management System (ISMS) certification.

**Digital Asset Basic Act (DABA, 2025):**

Passed in June 2025, the DABA complements the VAUPA by establishing licensing, reserve, and operational obligations for stablecoin issuers and other digital asset businesses. It creates a taxonomy of digital assets and defines the regulatory perimeter more precisely.

### Key Principles

**Real-Name Account System:** South Korea requires all crypto exchange transactions to be linked to real-name verified bank accounts. This system, implemented through cooperation between exchanges and banks, has been credited with reducing money laundering but criticized for creating a concentration of market power among the few exchanges able to secure banking partnerships -- notably Upbit (partnered with K-Bank) and Bithumb.

**Travel Rule Compliance:** South Korea was among the first jurisdictions to implement the FATF Travel Rule for virtual asset transfers, requiring VASPs to collect and transmit originator and beneficiary information for transfers above 1 million won (approximately $750).

### Notable Cases

**Terraform Labs / Do Kwon (2022-2024):** The collapse of the TerraUSD (UST) stablecoin and LUNA token in May 2022 resulted in estimated losses of $40 billion globally. South Korean prosecutors charged co-founder Do Kwon with fraud and violations of the Capital Markets Act. Kwon was arrested in Montenegro in March 2023 and extradited to the United States, where he faced SEC charges. The case was a primary catalyst for the VAUPA's enactment.

**First VAUPA Enforcement (2024):** In late 2024, the FSC referred the first prosecution under the VAUPA's unfair trading provisions, targeting a crypto "pump-and-dump" scheme. This marked the practical activation of the VAUPA's enforcement apparatus.

**Bithumb Tax Dispute (2021-2022):** Bithumb Korea challenged a 80.3 billion won ($69 million) withholding tax assessment by the National Tax Service on foreign users' crypto gains, raising questions about the taxation and jurisdictional reach of crypto exchanges.

## United States

### Federal Framework

The United States regulatory approach to cryptocurrency is characterized by jurisdictional fragmentation, with multiple federal agencies asserting overlapping authority:

**Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC):**

- Applies the Howey test (*SEC v. W.J. Howey Co.*, 328 U.S. 293 (1946)) to determine whether crypto-assets constitute "investment contracts" subject to securities regulation
- Under Chair Gary Gensler (2021-2025), pursued aggressive enforcement, classifying most tokens as securities
- Major enforcement actions: *SEC v. Ripple Labs* (2023) -- partial victory establishing that secondary market sales of XRP were not securities offerings; *SEC v. Coinbase* (2023); *SEC v. Binance* (2023)
- Policy shift under the Trump administration (2025): formation of the Crypto Task Force, signaling a more permissive regulatory approach

**Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC):**

- Classified Bitcoin and Ether as commodities
- Regulates crypto derivatives markets under the Commodity Exchange Act (7 U.S.C. Chapter 1)
- Enforcement actions focused on fraud and manipulation in spot and derivatives markets

**Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN):**

- Applies Bank Secrecy Act (31 U.S.C. Section 5311 et seq.) to crypto exchanges as money services businesses (MSBs)
- Requires AML/KYC programs, suspicious activity reporting, and Currency Transaction Reports
- Proposed rule on "unhosted wallets" generated significant controversy (2020-2021)

**GENIUS Act (Guiding and Establishing National Innovation for U.S. Stablecoins Act, 2025):**

Signed into law in July 2025, the GENIUS Act established the first comprehensive federal framework for payment stablecoins:

- Defines payment stablecoins as digital assets pegged to a fiat currency and redeemable on demand
- Requires issuers to maintain 1:1 reserves in high-quality liquid assets
- Creates federal licensing pathway through the OCC alongside state regulatory options
- Establishes reserve composition, audit, and disclosure requirements
- Prohibits algorithmic stablecoins from qualifying as "payment stablecoins"

### State Variations

| State | Approach |
|-------|----------|
| **New York** | BitLicense (2015) -- comprehensive licensing regime under NYDFS; most stringent state-level framework |
| **Wyoming** | Special Purpose Depository Institution (SPDI) charter; crypto-friendly legislation |
| **Texas** | Explicitly permits state-chartered banks to custody crypto-assets |
| **California** | Digital Financial Assets Law (AB-39, 2023) -- licensing requirement for crypto businesses |
| **Montana** | Enacted protections for cryptocurrency mining operations |

### Notable Cases

**SEC v. Ripple Labs, Inc., No. 20-cv-10832 (S.D.N.Y. 2023):** Judge Analisa Torres ruled that XRP sales on exchanges to retail investors were not securities transactions, while institutional sales constituted unregistered securities offerings. The decision created a nuanced framework but was limited to the Southern District of New York.

**Commodity Futures Trading Commission v. Ooki DAO (2022):** The CFTC sued a decentralized autonomous organization for operating an unregistered trading platform, raising fundamental questions about regulatory jurisdiction over decentralized protocols.

**United States v. Roman Storm (2023-present):** The prosecution of Tornado Cash developer Roman Storm under money laundering and sanctions evasion charges raised critical questions about developer liability for decentralized protocols.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany has positioned itself as one of Europe's most crypto-forward jurisdictions, integrating digital assets into its existing financial regulatory architecture before layering on EU-wide MiCA requirements.

**Kreditwesengesetz (KWG -- German Banking Act):**

Germany was the first EU member state to define crypto-custody (Kryptoverwahrgeschaft) as a regulated financial service. The Fourth EU Anti-Money Laundering Directive Implementation Act (2020) amended the KWG to include:

- **Section 1(1a) No. 6 KWG:** Crypto-custody business -- the safekeeping, management, and securing of crypto-assets or private cryptographic keys used to hold, store, or transfer crypto-assets for others
- **Section 32(1) KWG:** Requires BaFin authorization for conducting crypto-custody business

**Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA) Implementation:**

| Timeline | Event |
|----------|-------|
| **June 2023** | MiCA Regulation (EU) 2023/1114 published in Official Journal |
| **June 2024** | Titles III and IV (stablecoin provisions) become applicable |
| **December 30, 2024** | Full applicability of MiCA, including Title V (CASP authorization) |
| **December 31, 2025** | End of transitional period for existing German crypto license holders |

Germany enacted the Kryptomarkteaufsichtsgesetz (KMAG -- Crypto Markets Supervision Act) to supplement MiCA at the national level:

- Designates BaFin as the national competent authority for MiCA
- Establishes administrative penalties and enforcement powers
- Empowers BaFin's "name-and-warn" strategy to publicly flag firms suspected of violations
- Integrates MiCA terms into the Geldwaschegesetz (GwG -- AML Act), listing CASPs as "obliged entities" under GwG Section 2

**BaFin Licensing:**

As of mid-2025, Germany has emerged as the leading EU jurisdiction for MiCA CASP authorizations, with over 25 firms licensed. Existing holders of German crypto-custody licenses under the KWG may benefit from simplified MiCA authorization procedures during the transitional period.

### Key Principles

**Technology-Neutral Regulation:** German regulators have adopted a principle of "same business, same risks, same rules," applying existing financial regulation to crypto-assets rather than creating entirely separate frameworks. This approach predates MiCA and influenced the EU-wide design.

**Electronic Securities Act (eWpG, 2021):** Germany enacted the Gesetz uber elektronische Wertpapiere (Electronic Securities Act), enabling the issuance of electronic securities (including crypto securities) on distributed ledger technology without the requirement of a physical certificate (Globalurkunde). This was a significant departure from Germany's traditional paper-based securities framework.

### Notable Cases

**BaFin v. Various Unlicensed CASPs (2023-2025):** BaFin has issued multiple cease-and-desist orders against firms operating crypto businesses in Germany without authorization, utilizing both pre-MiCA (KWG) and post-MiCA enforcement powers.

**Wirecard Aftermath and Crypto Oversight Reform:** The Wirecard scandal (2020) led to significant reforms of BaFin's supervisory powers, including enhanced authority over fintech and crypto businesses. These reforms improved BaFin's capacity to oversee the rapidly growing German crypto market.

## ECHR / EU Framework

### Convention Articles

While the ECHR does not directly regulate financial markets, several Convention rights are relevant to cryptocurrency regulation:

- **Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (Protection of property):** Crypto-assets may constitute "possessions" protected against disproportionate state interference, including confiscation or regulatory actions that effectively destroy asset value.
- **Article 8 (Right to respect for private life):** Relevant to data protection and privacy concerns in the context of KYC/AML requirements and blockchain surveillance.
- **Article 10 (Freedom of expression):** Potentially relevant to decentralized applications and censorship-resistant communication protocols.

### Key Case Law

**MiCA Regulation (EU) 2023/1114 -- Markets in Crypto-Assets:**

MiCA is the primary EU legislative instrument for crypto regulation. Its structure:

| Title | Subject | Key Requirements |
|-------|---------|-----------------|
| **Title II** | Crypto-assets other than ARTs/EMTs | White paper requirements; disclosure obligations |
| **Title III** | Asset-referenced tokens (ARTs) | Authorization by competent authority; reserve requirements; governance standards |
| **Title IV** | E-money tokens (EMTs) | Must be issued by credit institution or EMI; 1:1 fiat reserve; redemption rights |
| **Title V** | Crypto-asset service providers (CASPs) | Authorization; prudential requirements; conduct of business rules; custody safeguards |
| **Title VI** | Market abuse | Insider dealing; market manipulation; unlawful disclosure -- modeled on MAR |

**Key MiCA Provisions:**

- **Article 59:** CASPs must obtain authorization from their home member state competent authority, with passporting rights across the EU
- **Article 67:** CASPs must act honestly, fairly, and professionally in the best interests of clients
- **Article 70:** Custody requirements: CASPs must segregate client assets and ensure prompt return
- **Article 86:** Market manipulation prohibition: manipulative trading, wash trading, spoofing
- **Article 92:** Penalties: member states must provide for administrative penalties of at least 5 million euros for natural persons and 15 million euros or 12.5% of annual turnover for legal persons

**EU Anti-Money Laundering Package:**

The 2024 EU AML Package (including Regulation (EU) 2024/1624 -- AMLR) incorporates the FATF Travel Rule for crypto transfers, requiring CASPs to collect and transmit originator and beneficiary data for all transfers (no de minimis threshold, unlike the FATF's recommended $1,000/EUR 1,000 threshold).

### Member State Obligations

All 27 EU member states must apply MiCA directly (as a regulation, not requiring transposition). However, member states retain discretion over:

- Designation of the national competent authority
- Transitional period duration (up to 18 months from December 30, 2024)
- Administrative penalty levels (above MiCA minimums)
- Criminal sanctions (MiCA prescribes administrative penalties; member states may add criminal penalties)

Globally, Japan operates under the Payment Services Act and the Financial Instruments and Exchange Act, with the Japan Financial Services Agency (JFSA) requiring exchange registration since 2017. Brazil enacted its Crypto Framework Law (Lei 14,478/2022), designating the Central Bank of Brazil as the primary regulator. India imposed a 30% tax on crypto gains and 1% TDS on transactions (2022 Finance Act), while regulatory classification remains uncertain. Australia has proposed a licensing framework through the Treasury's token mapping exercise and consultation on digital asset regulation (2023-2025). Singapore's Payment Services Act (2019, amended 2022) provides a licensing regime administered by the Monetary Authority of Singapore.

| **DeFi coverage** | Limited | Expanding (enforcement-driven) | Limited | Under review (MiCA Art. 142 report) |
| **CBDC position** | Pilot program (digital won) | Opposed (anti-CBDC policy) | ECB digital euro project | ECB digital euro regulation proposed |

### Emerging Regulatory Challenges

Several cross-cutting issues challenge regulators across all four jurisdictions:

**Decentralized Finance (DeFi):** The regulation of truly decentralized protocols -- where no single entity controls the platform -- poses fundamental jurisdictional and definitional challenges. MiCA's Article 142 requires the European Commission to produce a report by December 2025 on DeFi regulation. The U.S. has pursued enforcement-based DeFi regulation (e.g., CFTC v. Ooki DAO; prosecution of Tornado Cash developers), while South Korea and Germany have largely deferred the issue.

**Non-Fungible Tokens (NFTs):** NFT classification varies: South Korea's VAUPA generally excludes NFTs with limited exchange function; the U.S. treats them case-by-case (some may be securities under Howey); MiCA generally excludes "unique" NFTs but captures fractional or fungible NFTs. Japan's Financial Services Agency has indicated that certain NFTs may fall under the Payment Services Act.

**Stablecoin Systemic Risk:** The Terraform/LUNA collapse demonstrated that stablecoins can pose systemic risks. All four jurisdictions have responded: the GENIUS Act (U.S.), MiCA Titles III-IV (EU/Germany), and South Korea's DABA all impose reserve, disclosure, and licensing requirements on stablecoin issuers.

**Cross-Border Enforcement:** Cryptocurrency's borderless nature challenges national regulatory frameworks. The FATF Travel Rule addresses cross-border AML, but enforcement coordination remains limited. Mutual legal assistance treaties (MLATs) are increasingly used for crypto-related investigations, as demonstrated by the international cooperation in the Do Kwon case involving South Korea, Montenegro, and the United States.

**Environmental Regulation:** Bitcoin mining's energy consumption has attracted regulatory attention. China's 2021 mining ban pushed operations to the U.S. (particularly Texas and New York), where state-level environmental regulations now apply. The EU considered and rejected a de facto proof-of-work ban during MiCA negotiations. South Korea has not enacted mining-specific regulations.
