## Summary

Theft -- the unauthorized taking of another's property -- represents one of the oldest and most universally prohibited offenses in human legal history, yet the specific definitions, penalties, and procedural mechanisms for addressing it have varied enormously across civilizations and eras. Ancient Near Eastern law codes imposed dramatically severe penalties for certain forms of theft, with the Code of Hammurabi (c. 1754 BCE) prescribing death for stealing from temples or palaces and multiple restitution (up to thirtyfold) for other thefts, while also developing sophisticated rules about receipt of stolen goods, theft by bailees, and the evidentiary procedures for recovering stolen property. Roman law created the delict of furtum, a conceptually rich category that encompassed not only the taking of movable property but also the unauthorized use of borrowed or deposited goods, with the Twelve Tables (c. 450 BCE) distinguishing between manifest theft (furtum manifestum, caught in the act) and non-manifest theft (furtum nec manifestum), prescribing fourfold damages for the former and twofold for the latter. East Asian legal traditions, particularly the Tang Code (唐律疏議, 653 CE) and its Korean and Japanese derivatives, created elaborate systems distinguishing theft (窃盗, qiedao) from robbery (強盜, qiangdao) with penalties escalating by the value of goods stolen and the offender's relationship to the victim, reflecting Confucian relational ethics. Islamic law classified theft as a hadd offense (sariqah) subject to the Quranic penalty of amputation of the hand (Quran 5:38), but surrounded this penalty with such stringent evidentiary and definitional requirements -- including a minimum threshold value (nisab), taking from a secure enclosure (hirz), and strict witness requirements -- that its actual application was limited. Germanic and medieval European law addressed theft through composition payments and ordeals, with the gradual emergence of royal criminal jurisdiction transforming theft from a private wrong into a public crime punished by hanging. English common law developed the technical doctrine of larceny -- requiring a trespassory taking and carrying away (asportation) of the personal property of another with intent to permanently deprive -- whose rigid formalism spawned centuries of judicial and legislative innovation including the crimes of embezzlement, larceny by trick, obtaining by false pretenses, and ultimately modern statutory theft.

## Ancient Near East

### Mesopotamian Theft Law

Mesopotamian legal codes reveal a sophisticated body of theft law that addressed not only the act of stealing but also problems of evidence, third-party purchasers, and institutional theft.

**Code of Hammurabi (c. 1754 BCE):**

The most comprehensive Mesopotamian treatment of theft is found in Laws 6-25 of the Code of Hammurabi. The system was characterized by dramatic severity and sharp distinctions based on the victim's status:

- **Law 6:** "If a man has stolen property of a god (temple) or of the palace, that man shall be put to death; also the one who received the stolen goods from his hand shall be put to death." Temple and palace theft was capitally punished, reflecting the sacred and royal character of institutional property.
- **Law 8:** "If a man has stolen an ox, or a sheep, or a donkey, or a pig, or a boat -- if it belongs to a god or to the palace, he shall pay thirtyfold; if it belongs to a mushkenum (commoner/dependent), he shall pay tenfold. If the thief cannot pay, he shall be put to death." This graduated restitution system distinguished institutional from private property and imposed capital punishment on those unable to pay.
- **Law 9-13:** These provisions established a remarkable procedure for recovering stolen property and adjudicating disputes over ownership. If a man who has lost property finds it in the possession of another, the possessor must produce the seller and witnesses to the sale; the original owner must produce witnesses who know the lost property. A panel of judges evaluates the testimony. If the seller cannot produce witnesses, "he is a thief, he shall be put to death." If the original owner cannot produce witnesses, "he is a liar, he has made a false charge, he shall be put to death." This system required documented chains of ownership and severely punished both theft and false accusations of theft.
- **Law 21:** "If a man has broken through the wall (of a house to rob), they shall put him to death before the breach and bury him there." Burglary (breaking into a house) was punished by execution at the scene of the crime, the body serving as a deterrent.
- **Law 22-24:** These laws established a primitive form of victim compensation by the community. If a robber is not caught, the victim declares his losses before a god (under oath), and the city or district governor must compensate the victim. If the robbery results in a death, the community pays one mina of silver to the heirs.

**Laws of Eshnunna (c. 1930 BCE):** Section 12 prescribed a fine of sixty shekels of silver for theft committed at night within a house; Section 13 prescribed death for theft committed during the daytime involving entry into a house -- a curious inversion of later European law, which treated nocturnal theft more severely.

**Hittite Laws (c. 1650-1500 BCE):** The Hittite approach was notably more moderate than the Babylonian. Laws 57-70 addressed theft with compositional penalties: theft of a horse required payment of fifteen horses; theft of a draft ox, fifteen oxen; theft of a sheep, fifteen sheep. The multiple varied but was typically far less than Hammurabi's thirtyfold. Law 95 provided that if a slave stole from a house, the slave would make restitution; if unable to pay, his nose and ears would be cut off.

### Egyptian Theft Law

**The Tale of the Eloquent Peasant (c. 1850 BCE):** This literary text describes a peasant whose goods are stolen by a dishonest land steward, and his eloquent appeals to higher officials for justice. The text reveals that Egyptian law provided for restitution of stolen goods and punishment of the thief.

**Tomb Robbery Papyri (c. 1125 BCE):** The Papyrus Abbott and Papyrus Amherst record the trials of tomb robbers during the reign of Ramesses IX. The accused were examined under torture (beating with a double rod), and those convicted faced severe punishment including impalement. The records reveal sophisticated investigative techniques including site inspection, cross-examination of witnesses, and reconstruction of the crime.

## Roman Law

### The Delict of Furtum

Roman theft law was classified primarily as a delict (private wrong) rather than a crime (public offense), though the praetorian edict and later imperial legislation introduced criminal penalties for certain forms of theft.

**The Twelve Tables (c. 450 BCE):**
- **Table VIII.12:** A thief caught in the act at night (fur nocturnus) could be killed on the spot with impunity. A thief caught by day could be killed only if he resisted with a weapon; otherwise, the victim was required to call neighbors (endoplorare) and have the thief delivered to the magistrate.
- **Table VIII.14:** Manifest theft (furtum manifestum -- caught in the act or found with the goods) was punished by a fourfold penalty; non-manifest theft (furtum nec manifestum) by twofold.
- **Table VIII.16:** A special procedure, the lance et licio (searching with platter and loincloth), allowed an owner to search another's house for stolen goods while wearing only a loincloth and carrying a platter -- a ritual whose original significance is debated.

**Classical Jurisprudence:** The jurists developed furtum into one of the most conceptually elaborate areas of Roman private law:

- **Definition:** Gaius (Institutes 3.195) defined furtum broadly as "the fraudulent handling of a thing (contrectatio rei fraudulosa), whether of the thing itself, or of its use, or of its possession, committed with the intention of gain, whether of the thing itself or of its use or possession." This definition was remarkably wide -- it covered not only taking another's property but also the unauthorized use of property entrusted (furtum usus) and even a borrower using an item beyond the agreed scope.
- **Paul (D. 47.2.1.3):** "Theft is not committed by the handling alone, nor by the taking alone, but by both together." The requirement of both objective handling and subjective fraudulent intent made Roman furtum conceptually more sophisticated than many later theft definitions.
- **Classification by Sabinus and Ulpian:** The classical jurists refined the categories: furtum manifestum (caught in the act), furtum nec manifestum (not caught), furtum conceptum (goods found in one's house during a formal search), and furtum oblatum (goods planted in one's house by the actual thief). Each category carried different penalty multiples.

**Penalties:** The remedy for furtum was primarily civil: the actio furti for the penalty (double or quadruple the value) and the condictio furtiva for recovery of the thing itself. However, by the late Republic and Empire, criminal prosecution (cognitio extra ordinem) was available for theft. Imperial penalties included exile, forced labor in mines (damnatio in metallum), and, for slaves, death.

**Robbery (Rapina):** In 76 BCE, the praetor Lucullus introduced the actio vi bonorum raptorum for robbery (theft with violence), imposing a fourfold penalty if brought within a year, simple value thereafter. This distinguished robbery from simple theft, recognizing the additional wrong of violence.

## East Asian Law

### Chinese Legal Tradition

**Tang Code (唐律疏議, 653 CE):**

The Tang Code devoted extensive attention to theft, creating a system of remarkable precision:

- **Article 281 (窃盗, Theft):** Defined theft as secretly taking another's property (潛形隱面而取). Penalties were calibrated to the value of goods stolen:
  - 1 chi (foot) of silk: 50 blows with the light stick (笞)
  - 1 bolt of silk (匹): 60 blows with the heavy stick (杖)
  - 5 bolts: 1 year of penal servitude (徒)
  - 50 bolts: life exile (流) of 2,000 li
  - 120 bolts or more: strangulation (絞)

- **Article 281 Commentary:** The Shuyi (疏議, subcommentary) clarified that theft required secret taking (暗取) as opposed to robbery, which involved force or intimidation. The thief must have intended to appropriate the property for personal benefit.

- **Article 282 (強盜, Robbery):** Robbery (taking by force or threat) was punished far more severely: even for property of minimal value, the penalty was two years of penal servitude. If the robber injured the victim, the penalty was death by strangulation; if the robber killed the victim, death by decapitation.

- **Article 287 (監臨主守自盜, Theft by Officials):** Officials who stole from the properties they administered received enhanced punishment, one degree above ordinary theft. This reflected the Confucian emphasis on official integrity: a granary keeper stealing grain was punished more severely than an outsider stealing the same grain.

- **Relational Adjustments:** Theft within the family was treated differently according to Confucian relational hierarchy. Stealing from a senior relative (尊長) was punished more severely; stealing from a junior relative received reduced punishment. Theft between spouses was treated with significant mitigation.

### Korean Legal Tradition

**Joseon Dynasty (1392-1897):**

Korean law followed the Ming Code (大明律) system with local adaptations. The Gyeongguk Daejeon (經國大典, 1485) and the Sokdaejeon (續大典, 1746) addressed theft:

- **Repeat Offender Provisions:** Korean law was particularly concerned with habitual thieves. Under the sambok beopnyul (三覆法律, three-strikes provision), a thief convicted three times faced significantly enhanced punishment, including potential exile or death.
- **Livestock Theft:** Given the agrarian economy, theft of draft animals (cattle, horses) was treated with particular severity. The Sokdaejeon provided that stealing a draft ox could result in military exile (充軍).
- **Nobi (Slave) Theft:** Theft of or by nobi (奴婢, slaves/bondservants) had special rules. Theft of a nobi was treated similarly to abduction given the nobi's status as quasi-property. A nobi who stole from his master received enhanced punishment.

## Islamic Law

### Sariqah as a Hadd Offense

Islamic law classifies theft (sariqah) as one of the hudud (fixed penalties) offenses for which the Quran prescribes a specific punishment.

**Quranic Foundation:**
- **Quran 5:38:** "As for the thief, male or female, cut off their hands as recompense for what they have earned, as a deterrent punishment from Allah." This verse establishes amputation as the prescribed penalty.
- **Quran 5:39:** "But whoever repents after his wrongdoing and reforms, indeed, Allah will turn to him in forgiveness." This verse establishes the possibility of repentance mitigating the penalty.

**Conditions for Hadd Application:**

The Sunni schools of jurisprudence surrounded the hadd penalty with stringent requirements that significantly limited its application:

1. **Nisab (Minimum Value):** The stolen property must meet a minimum threshold. The Hanafi school (Abu Hanifa) set this at ten dirhams; the Maliki, Shafi'i, and Hanbali schools at one-quarter dinar (three dirhams of silver). Property below this value was subject to ta'zir (discretionary punishment) rather than hadd.

2. **Hirz (Secure Enclosure):** The property must have been taken from a hirz -- a locked room, guarded storehouse, or other enclosure appropriate to the type of property. Property left unguarded, such as produce on trees or unpenned livestock, was not subject to hadd if stolen (though the thief was still liable for restitution and ta'zir). Ibn Rushd (Averroes, Bidayat al-Mujtahid, 12th century) devoted extensive discussion to what constituted hirz for different types of property.

3. **Trespassory Taking:** The thief must have removed the property from the hirz by his own hand. If an accomplice inside passed goods out to someone outside, jurists debated which person was liable for hadd.

4. **Specific Intent:** The thief must have known the property belonged to another and intended to steal it. Taking by mistake or under a claim of right (shubha) negated the hadd penalty. The hadith "avoid the hudud in cases of doubt" (idra'u al-hudud bi-l-shubuhat) was widely applied to reduce hadd punishments.

5. **Excluded Categories:** The Hanafi school excluded from hadd theft: property that perishes quickly (food, fruit); property to which the thief has partial ownership or access (communal property, property of relatives); property of little value; and theft by minors or the insane. These exclusions dramatically narrowed the scope of the hadd penalty.

**Implementation:** Historical evidence suggests that the hadd penalty of amputation was imposed relatively rarely compared to ta'zir alternatives. Ottoman court records from the 16th-18th centuries show that judges frequently found procedural or evidentiary grounds to avoid imposing amputation, instead ordering imprisonment, fines, or corporal punishment.

### Highway Robbery (Hiraba)

Distinct from ordinary theft, hiraba (highway robbery/brigandage) was addressed in Quran 5:33: "The penalty for those who wage war against Allah and His Messenger and strive upon earth to cause corruption is none but that they be killed or crucified or that their hands and feet be cut off from opposite sides or that they be exiled from the land." The jurists developed an elaborate scale of penalties for hiraba depending on whether the robber took property, killed, or both.

## Medieval European Law

### Germanic Theft Law

**Lex Salica (c. 507-511 CE):**

The Salic Law provided detailed tariffs for theft, with penalties varying by the type of property stolen:

- **Title II (Theft of Pigs):** Stealing a suckling pig from the first or middle enclosure: 120 denarii (3 solidi). Stealing a pig from the third (innermost) enclosure: 600 denarii (15 solidi). Stealing a sow with piglets: 700 denarii (17.5 solidi).
- **Title III (Theft of Cattle):** Stealing a bull leading a herd: 1800 denarii (45 solidi). Stealing a cow with calf: 1400 denarii (35 solidi).
- **Title IV (Theft of Sheep):** Stealing three or more sheep: 1400 denarii (35 solidi).
- **Title XI (Theft of Slaves/Serfs):** Stealing a slave or servant: 1200 denarii (30 solidi) plus return of the stolen person.
- **Title XIV (Theft from a Mill):** 600 denarii (15 solidi), reflecting the communal importance of mills.

The Salic Law also addressed attempted theft (paying reduced composition), theft by slaves (corporal punishment), and theft discovered through tracking (following tracks to the thief's house).

**Anglo-Saxon England:** English law before the Conquest developed increasingly severe penalties for theft:
- The Laws of Ine (c. 694 CE) allowed a thief caught in the act to be killed or ransomed for his wergild. Ine distinguished between theft of less than and more than the value of a cow.
- Æthelstan's laws (c. 924-939 CE) prohibited the grant of sanctuary to thieves and established that no thief should be spared if caught in the act and the stolen goods were worth more than eight pence, with the significant qualifier "provided he be over twelve years old" -- one of the earliest provisions addressing the criminal responsibility of children.

### The Transition to Capital Punishment

By the High Middle Ages, theft had become a capital offense across much of Europe:

**The Constitutio Criminalis Carolina (1532):** Articles 157-175 addressed theft with a graduated system:
- **Article 157:** First-time theft of small value: whipping and banishment.
- **Article 159:** Second offense: whipping, loss of ears, and banishment.
- **Article 160:** Third offense: death.
- **Article 162:** Grand theft (over five Gulden in value): death even on first offense.
- **Article 164:** Nocturnal burglary or armed burglary: death.

## Common Law Origins

### The Doctrine of Larceny

English common law developed larceny into a highly technical offense whose rigid requirements would profoundly shape the development of theft law worldwide.

**Bracton (c. 1235):** Bracton defined larceny (latrocinium) as the taking and carrying away of another's movable property with felonious intent. He distinguished between grand larceny (goods worth twelve pence or more, a capital felony) and petit larceny (under twelve pence, punished by loss of an ear, whipping, or imprisonment).

**Elements of Common Law Larceny:**

1. **Trespassory Taking (caption and asportation):** The property must be taken from the possession of another (trespass to possession) and carried away, even slightly. This requirement created the critical distinction between larceny and other forms of dishonest appropriation.

2. **Personal Property:** Only tangible movable property (goods and chattels) was subject to larceny. Real property, choses in action, and fixtures were excluded, creating loopholes that persisted for centuries.

3. **Intent to Permanently Deprive:** The thief must have intended to deprive the owner permanently. A temporary taking with intent to return was not larceny -- a rule that would create the separate offense of unauthorized borrowing.

4. **Breaking Bulk (the Carrier's Case, 1473):** In the foundational case of The Carrier's Case (Y.B. 13 Edw. IV, f. 9, pl. 5), a carrier hired to transport bales of goods to Southampton instead broke open the bales and took the contents. The court held this was larceny because breaking the bales terminated the carrier's lawful possession, converting his holding into a trespassory taking. This case, extensively analyzed by legal historians (see Jerome Hall, *Theft, Law and Society*), represents a critical step in adapting theft law to commercial society.

5. **Larceny by Finding:** A person who found lost goods committed larceny only if he took them with knowledge of the owner's identity or with clues sufficient to ascertain ownership. This rule was established in cases such as *R v. Thurborn* (1849).

**Statutory Extensions:**

The rigidity of common law larceny led to statutory gap-filling:

- **Embezzlement (39 Geo. 3, c. 85, 1799):** Created because servants and clerks who received property from third parties on their master's behalf had lawful possession and thus could not commit larceny by converting it.
- **Larceny by Trick (R v. Pear, 1779):** Where a person obtained possession by fraud with intent to steal from the outset, the consent to transfer was vitiated, and larceny could be charged.
- **Obtaining by False Pretenses (30 Geo. 2, c. 24, 1757):** Created to address situations where a person obtained title (not just possession) through fraud.
- **Receiving Stolen Goods (3 & 4 Will. & Mary, c. 9, 1691):** Created a separate offense for those who received property knowing it to be stolen.

These piecemeal statutory extensions created an extraordinarily complex web of overlapping offenses that reformers would spend centuries rationalizing, culminating in modern comprehensive theft statutes such as the English Theft Act 1968.

## Historical Significance and Modern Legacy

The history of theft law illuminates several enduring themes in legal development:

**Restitution vs. Punishment:** Ancient legal systems (Mesopotamian, Hittite, Roman, Germanic) primarily treated theft as requiring restitution to the victim, often at a multiple of the stolen value. The shift toward punishment as the primary response -- incarceration, corporal punishment, execution -- represented a fundamental change in the purpose of theft law from compensating victims to deterring and punishing offenders. Modern restorative justice movements represent, in some respects, a return to ancient priorities.

**The Severity Paradox:** Historical theft law exhibits a paradox: as civilizations became wealthier and property more secure, penalties for theft often became more severe rather than less. Eighteenth-century England, at the height of the "Bloody Code," imposed death for stealing goods worth more than forty shillings from a dwelling house, five shillings from a shop, or any amount from a ship on a navigable river -- a severity far exceeding that of the Code of Hammurabi for comparable offenses.

**Property and Social Order:** Every legal tradition's theft law reflects its understanding of property, social hierarchy, and legitimate distribution. The Hammurabi Code's distinction between temple/palace and private theft reflected the central role of institutions; the Tang Code's relational adjustments reflected Confucian social ethics; Islamic law's stringent requirements for hadd reflected a theology that preferred mercy to severity; and the common law's obsessive focus on trespassory taking reflected an individualist conception of property rights.
