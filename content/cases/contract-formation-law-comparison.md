## Summary

Contract formation -- the process by which legally binding agreements arise between parties -- constitutes one of the foundational pillars of private law across all legal systems, yet the requirements and mechanisms vary substantially between civil law and common law traditions. South Korea's contract law is governed primarily by the Korean Civil Code (민법), enacted in 1958 and modeled largely on the Japanese and German civil codes, which establishes contract formation through the meeting of offer (청약) and acceptance (승낙) under Articles 527 through 534, without requiring consideration as a formation element; the Code adopts a principle of contractual freedom (계약자유의 원칙) subject to public policy limitations, and the Electronic Commerce Basic Act (전자상거래 등에서의 소비자보호에 관한 법률) addresses formation issues in digital transactions. The United States follows the common law tradition requiring offer, acceptance, and consideration as essential elements of contract formation, with the Restatement (Second) of Contracts and the Uniform Commercial Code (UCC) Article 2 providing the primary frameworks; unique doctrines such as the mailbox rule, the firm offer under UCC Section 2-205, and promissory estoppel under Restatement Section 90 supplement the basic formation framework. Germany's contract law, codified in the Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB) Sections 145 through 157, served as a model for many civil law jurisdictions including Korea and Japan, establishing formation through the consensus of declarations of intent (Willenserklärungen) without a consideration requirement; the BGB's abstract system of juristic acts and its sophisticated treatment of offer, acceptance, and conditions provides a highly systematic approach to formation. At the European level, the Principles of European Contract Law (PECL), the Draft Common Frame of Reference (DCFR), and the United Nations Convention on Contracts for the International Sale of Goods (CISG) provide harmonized frameworks, while the European Court of Justice has developed jurisdiction rules for contractual disputes under the Brussels I Regulation (recast) and the Rome I Regulation governs choice of law in contractual obligations.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's contract formation rules are codified in the Korean Civil Code (민법), which was enacted on February 22, 1958, and took effect on January 1, 1960. The Civil Code was heavily influenced by the Japanese Civil Code (民法典) and, through it, by the German Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch. Contract law provisions are found primarily in Book III (Obligations, 채권편), with general formation rules in Part I (General Provisions) and specific contract types in Part II (Contracts).

### Formation Requirements

Under Korean civil law, a contract is formed through the concurrence of two corresponding declarations of intent: an offer (청약, Article 527) and an acceptance (승낙, Article 528). Unlike common law systems, no consideration is required. The principle of freedom of contract (계약자유의 원칙) is recognized as a fundamental principle, encompassing freedom to contract or not, freedom to choose the contracting party, freedom to determine content, and freedom of form.

**Offer (청약):** An offer under Article 527 is a declaration of intent directed at a specific person proposing the conclusion of a contract with definite terms. An offer specifying a period for acceptance is irrevocable during that period (Article 527(1)). An offer without a specified acceptance period made to a person at a distance remains binding for a reasonable period (Article 528(1)).

**Acceptance (승낙):** Acceptance must correspond to the terms of the offer. A purported acceptance that modifies the terms of the offer constitutes a rejection and counter-offer (Article 534). Acceptance becomes effective upon dispatch (발신주의, Article 531), adopting the dispatch theory rather than the receipt theory used in German law.

### Special Formation Rules

| Rule | Civil Code Provision | Effect |
|------|---------------------|--------|
| Late acceptance | Article 528(2) | Offeror may treat late acceptance as new offer |
| Death/incapacity of offeror | Article 525 | Offer not extinguished by death or incapacity unless contrary intent expressed |
| Cross-offers | Article 533 | Contract formed when both offers coincide in content |
| Public offer (현상광고) | Articles 675-676 | Unilateral promise binding upon completion of specified act |

### Electronic Commerce

The Act on Consumer Protection in Electronic Commerce (전자상거래 등에서의 소비자보호에 관한 법률) and the Framework Act on Electronic Documents and Electronic Transactions (전자문서 및 전자거래 기본법) address formation in digital contexts. Electronic documents are accorded the same legal effect as paper documents (Article 4 of the Framework Act). The display of goods on an e-commerce platform generally constitutes an invitation to treat (청약의 유인) rather than an offer, with the consumer's order constituting the offer and the merchant's confirmation constituting acceptance.

### Standard Form Contracts

The Regulation of Standardized Contracts Act (약관의 규제에 관한 법률), enacted in 1986, governs the incorporation and validity of standard form contract terms. Under this Act, terms that are unreasonably disadvantageous to the adhering party are void (Article 6). The Fair Trade Commission (공정거래위원회) has authority to review and order amendment of unfair standard terms across industries.

## United States

### Legal Framework

American contract formation law derives from two primary sources: the common law (as synthesized in the Restatement (Second) of Contracts, published by the American Law Institute in 1981) and the Uniform Commercial Code (UCC), particularly Article 2 governing the sale of goods (adopted in some form by all 50 states except Louisiana, which has a civil law heritage for private obligations).

### Essential Elements

**Offer:** A manifestation of willingness to enter into a bargain, made so as to justify another person in understanding that assent to that bargain is invited (Restatement Section 24). Advertisements are generally invitations to treat rather than offers (Lefkowitz v. Great Minneapolis Surplus Store, 1957, establishing a narrow exception for clear, definite, explicit advertisements leaving nothing open for negotiation).

**Acceptance:** The common law "mirror image rule" requires that acceptance match the offer's terms exactly; any variation constitutes a counter-offer (Restatement Section 59). UCC Section 2-207 substantially modifies this rule for sales of goods, providing that a definite expression of acceptance operates as acceptance even with additional or different terms, with such terms treated as proposals for addition to the contract between merchants.

**Consideration:** A bargained-for exchange of value -- the element that most distinguishes common law formation from civil law systems. The doctrine requires that each party's promise or performance be the inducement for the other's (Restatement Section 71). Nominal consideration is generally sufficient, but past consideration and moral obligation are generally insufficient (with exceptions under Restatement Section 86 for promises recognizing past material benefits).

### Promissory Estoppel

Restatement Section 90 provides that "[a] promise which the promisor should reasonably expect to induce action or forbearance on the part of the promisee or a third person and which does induce such action or forbearance is binding if injustice can be avoided only by enforcement of the promise." This doctrine serves as a substitute for consideration where reliance has occurred and has been applied in diverse contexts including employment promises (Grouse v. Group Health Plan, Inc., 1981), charitable pledges, and pre-contractual negotiations.

### Statute of Frauds

Certain categories of contracts must be evidenced by a writing signed by the party to be charged:
- Contracts for the sale of goods priced at $500 or more (UCC Section 2-201)
- Contracts for the sale of real property
- Contracts not capable of performance within one year
- Contracts in consideration of marriage
- Suretyship agreements

The UCC's Statute of Frauds has been revised to $5,000 in the 2003 amendments, though most states have not adopted this revision.

### Battle of the Forms

UCC Section 2-207 addresses the common commercial situation where parties exchange forms with conflicting terms. Under the "last shot" rule of common law, the last form sent before performance would govern. Section 2-207 replaces this with a more nuanced approach: between merchants, additional terms in the acceptance become part of the contract unless they materially alter it, the offer expressly limits acceptance to its terms, or the offeror objects within a reasonable time.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German contract formation law is codified in the Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB), which entered into force on January 1, 1900. The formation provisions appear in Book 1 (General Part), Sections 104-185 (legal transactions and declarations of intent), and Book 2 (Law of Obligations), Sections 241-853. The Schuldrechtsmodernisierung (Modernization of the Law of Obligations) of 2002 was the most significant reform since the BGB's enactment, integrating EU consumer protection directives and modernizing remedial provisions.

### Declarations of Intent (Willenserklärungen)

The BGB constructs contract formation through its general theory of juristic acts (Rechtsgeschäfte) and declarations of intent (Willenserklärungen). A contract requires two corresponding declarations of intent: an offer (Antrag or Angebot, Section 145) and an acceptance (Annahme, Sections 147-149).

**Offer (Section 145 BGB):** "A person who offers to another the conclusion of a contract is bound by the offer, unless he has excluded being so bound." The offer is binding upon receipt by the offeree and is irrevocable by default -- a significant difference from common law, where offers are generally revocable until acceptance. The offeror may reserve the right to revoke (Widerrufsvorbehalt) in the offer itself.

**Acceptance (Sections 147-149 BGB):** Acceptance must be declared within the period specified in the offer, or if no period is specified, within a period in which the offeror may expect to receive the answer under normal circumstances. An offer made to a person present (unter Anwesenden) can only be accepted immediately (Section 147(1)). Acceptance of an offer made to a person at a distance (unter Abwesenden) must be received within the reasonable expected period (Section 147(2)).

### Receipt Theory (Zugangstheorie)

Under Section 130(1) BGB, a declaration of intent directed at another person that is made in the absence of that person becomes effective at the time it reaches the other person (Zugang). This receipt theory contrasts with the dispatch theory used in Korean law and the common law mailbox rule. The distinction is practically significant: under the receipt theory, a revocation can be effective if it reaches the offeree before or simultaneously with the acceptance.

### Abstraction Principle (Abstraktionsprinzip)

A distinctive feature of German law is the separation of the obligatory transaction (Verpflichtungsgeschäft -- the contract creating obligations) from the dispositive transaction (Verfügungsgeschäft -- the transfer of rights). This means that a contract of sale creates obligations to transfer ownership and pay the price, but the actual transfer of ownership occurs through a separate abstract act. The validity of the dispositive act is independent of the validity of the underlying obligatory contract.

### Standard Business Terms (AGB)

Sections 305-310 BGB (formerly the AGB-Gesetz, integrated into the BGB in 2002) provide comprehensive regulation of standard business terms (Allgemeine Geschäftsbedingungen). These provisions require that standard terms be clearly incorporated into the contract (Section 305(2)), prohibit surprising clauses (Section 305c), establish that ambiguous terms are construed against the user (Section 305c(2)), and declare void terms that unreasonably disadvantage the other party (Sections 307-309). Sections 308 and 309 contain specific lists of prohibited clauses (with and without the possibility of judicial evaluation).

### Form Requirements

The BGB generally follows a principle of freedom of form (Formfreiheit). However, specific form requirements apply to important transactions:

| Transaction | Form Required | BGB Provision |
|-------------|--------------|---------------|
| Real estate sale | Notarial authentication (notarielle Beurkundung) | Section 311b(1) |
| Gift promise | Notarial authentication | Section 518(1) |
| Surety by non-merchant | Written form | Section 766 |
| Consumer credit agreement | Written form | Section 492 |
| Assignment of future wages | Written form | Section 400 |

## ECHR & EU

### United Nations Convention on Contracts for the International Sale of Goods (CISG)

The CISG, adopted in Vienna on April 11, 1980, provides a unified framework for international sales contracts. All four jurisdictions examined in this analysis are CISG contracting states (South Korea acceded in 2005). The CISG applies automatically to contracts for the sale of goods between parties in different contracting states (Article 1(1)(a)).

**Formation under CISG Part II:**
- **Offer (Article 14):** A proposal addressed to specific persons is an offer if sufficiently definite (indicating goods, quantity, and price or means of determining them) and indicates the offeror's intention to be bound.
- **Acceptance (Article 18):** A statement or conduct indicating assent. Silence alone does not constitute acceptance. Acceptance with modifications constitutes a counter-offer unless the modifications are non-material (Article 19(2)).
- **No consideration requirement:** The CISG does not require consideration for contract formation.

### Principles of European Contract Law (PECL)

The PECL, developed by the Commission on European Contract Law (Lando Commission), provides principles for contract formation that synthesize common law and civil law approaches. Article 2:101 provides that a contract is concluded if the parties intend to be legally bound and reach a sufficient agreement. The PECL does not require consideration (Article 2:101(2)) and adopts the receipt theory for the effectiveness of communications.

### Draft Common Frame of Reference (DCFR)

The DCFR, published in 2009, represents the most comprehensive attempt to harmonize European private law. Book II addresses formation, adopting formation through offer and acceptance without consideration, the receipt theory for communications, and detailed rules on standard terms.

### Rome I Regulation (EC) No 593/2008

The Rome I Regulation governs choice of law in contractual obligations in EU member states. In the absence of a choice of law by the parties (Article 3), the Regulation applies the law of the country of habitual residence of the characteristic performer (Article 4). For consumer contracts, Article 6 provides that the law of the consumer's habitual residence applies if the professional directed activities to that country.

### Brussels I Regulation (Recast) No 1215/2012

Article 7(1) of the Brussels I Regulation provides that a person domiciled in a member state may be sued in matters relating to a contract in the courts of the place of performance of the obligation in question. The CJEU has interpreted "place of performance" in numerous cases, including Car Trim GmbH v. KeySafety Systems Srl (C-381/08, 2010).

### Consumer Rights Directive 2011/83/EU

The Consumer Rights Directive harmonizes rules on pre-contractual information and withdrawal rights for distance and off-premises contracts. It establishes a 14-day withdrawal period for distance contracts (Article 9), during which the consumer may withdraw without giving any reason.

## Comparative Analysis

### The Consideration Divide

The most fundamental divergence in contract formation across these jurisdictions is the consideration requirement. The United States stands alone among the four systems in requiring consideration as an essential element of contract formation. South Korea, Germany, and all European harmonization instruments dispense with consideration, relying instead on the parties' intent to be bound. The consideration doctrine has been extensively criticized by scholars as a formalistic barrier that creates artificial distinctions and necessitates workarounds (such as promissory estoppel and the doctrine of past consideration), yet it remains deeply embedded in American contract law.

### Offer Irrevocability

The jurisdictions diverge significantly on whether and when an offer may be revoked. Germany and South Korea both treat offers as irrevocable by default once received, providing security to the offeree. The common law treats offers as freely revocable until acceptance (with exceptions for option contracts and the UCC firm offer). The CISG adopts an intermediate position, making offers irrevocable when the offeree has reasonably relied on their irrevocability.

### Dispatch vs. Receipt

The moment at which acceptance becomes effective determines when the contract is formed and the risk of communication failures is allocated. South Korea follows the dispatch theory (acceptance effective upon sending), while Germany follows the receipt theory (acceptance effective upon receipt). American common law applies the mailbox rule (dispatch theory for postal communications) but the receipt theory for instantaneous communications. The CISG and PECL adopt the receipt theory.

### Standard Terms

All jurisdictions regulate standard form contracts, but the intensity and methodology differ. Germany's BGB Sections 305-310 provide the most detailed statutory framework, with specific lists of prohibited clauses. South Korea's Regulation of Standardized Contracts Act provides administrative enforcement through the Fair Trade Commission. American law addresses standard terms primarily through the unconscionability doctrine (UCC Section 2-302) and contractual interpretation principles, without a comparable statutory framework for prohibited clauses.
