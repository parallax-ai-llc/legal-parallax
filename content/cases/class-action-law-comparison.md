## Summary

Class action lawsuits -- collective litigation mechanisms that permit one or more representative plaintiffs to bring suit on behalf of a larger group sharing common legal claims -- serve as a critical tool for access to justice, regulatory enforcement, and corporate accountability, yet their availability, procedural requirements, and practical significance vary enormously across legal systems. South Korea enacted the Securities-Related Class Action Act (증권관련 집단소송법) in 2005, introducing opt-out class actions in the securities context, and has since debated expansion to consumer, environmental, and data protection claims through the pending General Class Action Act (일반 집단소송법), while also maintaining group litigation through the Consumer Fundamental Act (소비자기본법) group action provisions. The United States operates the world's most developed class action system under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23, refined through landmark Supreme Court decisions including Wal-Mart Stores, Inc. v. Dukes (2011) and Epic Systems Corp. v. Lewis (2018), and remains the only major jurisdiction with opt-out class actions of general application, a robust contingency fee structure, and the possibility of treble damages in antitrust class actions under the Clayton Act. Germany has historically rejected class actions in favor of model case proceedings, introducing the Capital Markets Model Case Act (Kapitalanleger-Musterverfahrensgesetz, KapMuG) in 2005 and the Model Declaratory Action (Musterfeststellungsklage) in 2018, while transposing EU Directive 2020/1828 on representative actions through amendments effective in 2023. At the EU level, Directive 2020/1828 on representative actions for the protection of the collective interests of consumers requires all member states to provide representative action mechanisms by June 25, 2023, while the ECHR has recognized the importance of collective redress under Article 6 (right to a fair trial) and Article 13 (right to an effective remedy).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's collective litigation system reflects a cautious, incremental approach to class actions, beginning with a narrow securities-specific statute and gradually expanding through consumer protection mechanisms.

### Securities-Related Class Action Act (증권관련 집단소송법)

Enacted in January 2004 and effective January 1, 2005, this act introduced the first true opt-out class action mechanism in Korean law. The Act permits class actions for securities fraud, including false or misleading statements in securities filings, insider trading, and market manipulation under the Financial Investment Services and Capital Markets Act (자본시장과 금융투자업에 관한 법률).

**Standing Requirements:** A class must consist of 50 or more members, and the named plaintiffs must hold at least 0.01% of the outstanding shares of a listed company. The court must grant permission (허가) to proceed as a class action after evaluating commonality, typicality, adequacy, and superiority.

**Key Case -- Samsung Securities Accounting Fraud (2009):** The first significant securities class action in Korea involved allegations that Samsung Electronics Co. subsidiaries engaged in accounting fraud. The case highlighted procedural challenges, including the high threshold for class certification and the lengthy judicial process, ultimately settling after years of litigation.

### Consumer Group Actions

The Consumer Fundamental Act (소비자기본법) empowers designated consumer organizations and the Korea Consumer Agency (한국소비자원) to bring collective actions on behalf of affected consumers. These are not true class actions but rather organizational representative actions. The Korean Fair Trade Commission (공정거래위원회) also plays a quasi-judicial role in collective consumer disputes.

### Proposed General Class Action Act (일반 집단소송법)

Successive administrations have proposed expanding class action availability beyond securities to encompass consumer protection, environmental harm, data privacy breaches, and corporate misconduct. The General Class Action Act was introduced in the National Assembly in 2020 and revised versions were proposed in 2022 and 2024, but the legislation has faced opposition from business groups concerned about litigation costs and abuse. The proposed act would establish an opt-out mechanism applicable to civil claims generally, lower the class size threshold, and permit broader attorney fee arrangements.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States operates the world's most extensive and consequential class action system, rooted in Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23, the Class Action Fairness Act of 2005 (CAFA, 28 U.S.C. Section 1332(d)), and analogous state procedural rules.

### FRCP Rule 23 Requirements

Rule 23(a) establishes four prerequisites for class certification: (1) numerosity -- the class is so numerous that joinder of all members is impracticable; (2) commonality -- there are questions of law or fact common to the class; (3) typicality -- the claims of the representative parties are typical of the class; and (4) adequacy -- the representative parties will fairly and adequately protect the interests of the class.

Rule 23(b) requires that one of three additional conditions be met: (b)(1) individual adjudication would risk inconsistent obligations for the defendant or impair the interests of non-parties; (b)(2) the defendant has acted on grounds generally applicable to the class, making injunctive or declaratory relief appropriate; or (b)(3) common questions predominate over individual issues and a class action is superior to other methods of adjudication.

### Landmark Decisions

**Wal-Mart Stores, Inc. v. Dukes, 564 U.S. 338 (2011):** The Supreme Court decertified a class of 1.5 million female employees alleging sex discrimination, holding that commonality under Rule 23(a)(2) requires that the class members' claims "depend upon a common contention" that is "of such a nature that it is capable of classwide resolution." This heightened the commonality standard significantly.

**Epic Systems Corp. v. Lewis, 584 U.S. 497 (2018):** The Court held that arbitration agreements requiring individual proceedings are enforceable under the Federal Arbitration Act, even when they effectively prevent class action participation. This decision substantially reduced the availability of class actions in the employment context.

**TransUnion LLC v. Ramirez, 594 U.S. 413 (2021):** The Court held that only class members who suffered concrete harm have Article III standing, reducing the certified class from 8,185 to 1,853 members and reinforcing the requirement for individualized injury analysis.

### Class Action Fairness Act of 2005

CAFA expanded federal jurisdiction over class actions by granting original jurisdiction to federal courts over class actions where (1) any class member is diverse from any defendant (minimal diversity), (2) the proposed class has at least 100 members, and (3) the aggregate amount in controversy exceeds $5 million. CAFA also regulates coupon settlements and imposes enhanced scrutiny on settlements where class counsel receives disproportionately large fees relative to class member recovery.

### Fee Structure and Incentives

The contingency fee system is the primary economic engine of American class actions. Attorneys typically receive 25-40% of the class recovery, creating strong incentives for litigation. The Private Securities Litigation Reform Act (PSLRA, 1995) added procedural safeguards against frivolous securities class actions, including lead plaintiff provisions, heightened pleading standards, and a discovery stay pending motions to dismiss.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany has historically been skeptical of American-style class actions, viewing them as inconsistent with the principle of individual legal autonomy (Dispositionsmaxime) and the loser-pays cost regime. Instead, Germany has developed model case proceedings and representative actions.

### Capital Markets Model Case Act (KapMuG)

The Kapitalanleger-Musterverfahrensgesetz (KapMuG), enacted in 2005 and amended in 2012, provides a model case procedure for capital markets disputes. When at least ten plaintiffs file individual claims raising the same factual or legal issues, a model claimant is selected to litigate common questions. The model case decision binds all pending individual cases. The KapMuG was initially enacted as temporary legislation and has been extended multiple times, most recently through 2027.

**Deutsche Telekom Model Case (2005-2024):** The most prominent KapMuG proceeding involved approximately 17,000 investors alleging that Deutsche Telekom AG's IPO prospectus was misleading. After nearly two decades of litigation, the Bundesgerichtshof (Federal Court of Justice) rendered final decisions establishing key principles of prospectus liability.

### Model Declaratory Action (Musterfeststellungsklage)

Effective November 1, 2018, the Musterfeststellungsklage (Section 606 et seq. of the Zivilprozessordnung, ZPO) permits qualified consumer organizations (qualifizierte Einrichtungen) to seek a declaratory judgment on common factual and legal questions affecting at least 50 consumers who register in a public claims register.

**Volkswagen Dieselgate Settlement (2020):** The first major Musterfeststellungsklage was brought by the Verbraucherzentrale Bundesverband (vzbv, Federation of German Consumer Organizations) against Volkswagen AG concerning the diesel emissions scandal. The case resulted in a settlement of approximately EUR 830 million, covering approximately 260,000 registered consumers who received individual payments averaging EUR 3,000.

### EU Representative Actions Implementation

Germany transposed Directive 2020/1828 through amendments to the Unterlassungsklagengesetz (UKlaG, Injunctions Act) and the introduction of new provisions in the ZPO, effective in 2023. Qualified entities may now seek both injunctive relief and redress measures on behalf of consumers. The German implementation adopted an opt-in model for redress actions and an opt-out model for injunctive relief.

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR has addressed collective redress through the lens of access to justice under Article 6 and the right to an effective remedy under Article 13.

**Lithgow and Others v. United Kingdom (Application No. 9006/80, Judgment of July 8, 1986):** The Court recognized that grouping claims with common issues for joint adjudication does not violate the right to a fair hearing, provided individual rights are adequately protected within the collective proceeding.

**Gorraiz Lizarraga and Others v. Spain (Application No. 62543/00, Judgment of April 27, 2004):** The Court held that associations formed to defend the interests of their members have standing to bring claims under the Convention, reinforcing the legitimacy of representative litigation.

### EU Directive 2020/1828 on Representative Actions

Directive 2020/1828, adopted on November 25, 2020, with a transposition deadline of June 25, 2023 (applicable from June 25, 2023), represents the most significant EU-level collective redress instrument. The Directive requires all member states to establish representative action procedures for injunctive and redress measures protecting the collective interests of consumers.

**Key Provisions:**

**Qualified Entities:** Only designated qualified entities -- consumer organizations, public bodies, or independent bodies meeting transparency, financial capacity, and experience criteria -- may bring representative actions. This gatekeeper model contrasts sharply with the American approach where any individual with standing may serve as class representative.

**Cross-Border Actions:** The Directive enables qualified entities designated in one member state to bring representative actions in other member states, facilitating cross-border collective redress within the EU.

**Safeguards Against Abuse:** The Directive prohibits punitive damages, restricts litigation funding arrangements to avoid conflicts of interest, and requires courts to assess the suitability of collective proceedings.

**Member State Flexibility:** Member states may choose between opt-in and opt-out models for redress measures. Most member states have adopted opt-in mechanisms, though the Netherlands and Portugal have opted for opt-out systems.

### EU Antitrust Damages Directive

Directive 2014/104/EU on antitrust damages actions, adopted November 26, 2014, establishes rules facilitating damages claims by victims of antitrust infringements. While not specifically a class action instrument, it creates an environment conducive to collective antitrust litigation by establishing a rebuttable presumption that cartel infringements cause harm, harmonizing limitation periods, and addressing the interaction between public enforcement and private damages actions.

## Comparative Analysis

### Opt-In vs. Opt-Out

The most fundamental structural difference in collective litigation design is whether absent class members are included by default (opt-out) or must affirmatively join the proceedings (opt-in). The United States applies opt-out for Rule 23(b)(3) damages classes, South Korea applies opt-out for securities class actions, while Germany and most EU member states prefer opt-in mechanisms. Opt-out systems maximize class coverage and deterrent effect but raise due process concerns about binding absent parties. Opt-in systems respect individual autonomy but typically achieve lower participation rates, reducing both compensation coverage and deterrent effect.

### Litigation Financing

The American contingency fee system remains unique among major legal systems and is widely regarded as the essential economic foundation for class action litigation. Germany's loser-pays system creates significant financial barriers to collective litigation, partially mitigated by the availability of litigation funding from third-party investors (Prozessfinanzierung). The EU Directive permits litigation funding but requires safeguards against funder control of proceedings.

### Subject Matter Scope

The United States permits class actions across virtually all areas of civil law. South Korea confines true class actions to securities but provides weaker group action mechanisms for consumer disputes. Germany and the EU have expanded collective redress incrementally, moving from securities-specific model cases to broader consumer representative actions.

### Deterrent Function

American class actions serve a significant private enforcement and deterrence function, particularly in antitrust, securities, and consumer protection. The availability of treble damages in antitrust and punitive damages in tort amplifies this effect. European systems generally prioritize compensation over deterrence, reflected in the prohibition on punitive damages in Directive 2020/1828.
