## Summary

Abortion -- the deliberate termination of a pregnancy -- remains one of the most legally and ethically contested areas of law worldwide. The four jurisdictions examined here illustrate profoundly different constitutional, legislative, and judicial approaches. South Korea moved from strict criminalization under Articles 269-270 of the Criminal Act to effective decriminalization following the Constitutional Court's landmark 2019 decision (2017Hun-Ba127), which declared the criminal abortion provisions unconstitutional; however, the National Assembly's failure to enact replacement legislation by the December 31, 2020 deadline has left the country in a regulatory vacuum, with abortion legal but largely unregulated and uncovered by national health insurance. The United States underwent a seismic reversal in 2022, when the Supreme Court's decision in Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization (597 U.S. 215) overturned Roe v. Wade (410 U.S. 113, 1973) and Planned Parenthood v. Casey (505 U.S. 833, 1992), returning abortion regulation entirely to individual states; as of 2025, 13 states maintain near-total bans while others have enshrined abortion rights in their state constitutions. Germany retains abortion as a criminal offense under Section 218 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), but renders it non-punishable within the first 12 weeks of pregnancy provided mandatory counseling and a three-day waiting period are observed; a 2024 expert commission recommended full decriminalization, but a Bundestag vote on removing Section 218 was blocked in early 2025. At the European level, the ECHR has consistently declined to recognize an autonomous right to abortion under the Convention, instead granting states a wide margin of appreciation, while requiring that where abortion is lawful, states must provide accessible and effective procedures; the European Parliament adopted a non-binding resolution in April 2024 calling for inclusion of the right to abortion in the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights, though this requires unanimous member-state consent that remains politically unachievable.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's abortion law underwent a fundamental transformation between 2019 and 2021. The prior criminal framework was rooted in the Korean Criminal Act (형법):

- **Article 269 (Self-Induced Abortion):** A pregnant woman who procured her own abortion through drugs or other means faced imprisonment of up to one year or a fine of up to 2 million KRW (approximately USD 1,750).
- **Article 270 (Abortion by Physician):** A physician, midwife, pharmacist, or similar medical professional who performed an abortion with the woman's consent faced imprisonment of up to two years. Additionally, the medical professional's license could be suspended for up to seven years.

The **Mother and Child Health Act** (모자보건법, 1973) provided narrow exceptions permitting abortion in cases of: (1) hereditary or communicable disease of either parent; (2) rape or incest; (3) serious threat to the mother's health; and (4) pregnancy that would be legally or ethically unjustifiable. In practice, however, these exceptions were interpreted narrowly, and illegal abortions were widespread -- an estimated 169,000 annually according to government data (2011).

### Constitutional Court Decision (2019)

On **April 11, 2019**, the Constitutional Court of Korea delivered its landmark ruling in case **2017Hun-Ba127**. A practicing obstetrician, indicted for performing 69 illegal abortions between 2013 and 2015, challenged the constitutionality of Articles 269(1) and 270(1) of the Criminal Act.

The Court ruled **7-to-2** that the criminal abortion provisions were unconstitutional. The majority held that the blanket criminalization violated a pregnant woman's right to self-determination (자기결정권) as protected under the Korean Constitution, including the rights to privacy, bodily autonomy, and reproductive choice. The Court emphasized that the state must balance the protection of fetal life against the fundamental rights of the pregnant woman, and that an outright criminal ban failed to achieve proportionality.

The Court ordered the National Assembly to enact replacement legislation by **December 31, 2020**. The Constitutional Court specified that lawmakers could adopt various regulatory approaches -- gestational limits, counseling requirements, waiting periods -- so long as they struck a proportionate balance.

### Current Status (Post-Decriminalization)

The National Assembly failed to enact any replacement legislation by the deadline. Consequently, Articles 269 and 270 of the Criminal Act became **null and void on January 1, 2021**, rendering abortion effectively legal at all stages of pregnancy. South Korea currently exists in a regulatory vacuum:

- **No gestational limits** are in force.
- **No mandatory counseling** or waiting period requirements exist.
- **National Health Insurance** does not cover abortion procedures, making them an out-of-pocket expense averaging 500,000-1,500,000 KRW (USD 380-1,150).
- **Mifepristone and misoprostol** (pharmaceutical abortion) remain unapproved by the Ministry of Food and Drug Safety, though regulatory review began in 2023.

Human Rights Watch reported in June 2024 that the government had made "little effort" to establish a regulatory framework, leaving women and girls uncertain about where and how to access affordable and safe abortions. As of early 2025, no pharmaceutical abortion options are fully authorized or regulated.

|--------|-------------------|----------------------|---------------------|

### Historical Context

South Korea's abortion history is intertwined with its population control policies. During the 1960s-1980s, the government actively promoted family planning, and enforcement of anti-abortion laws was lax because reducing birth rates aligned with economic development goals. As fertility rates declined precipitously (from 6.0 in 1960 to 1.17 in 2024 -- among the lowest globally), the government reversed course, tightening enforcement of abortion restrictions to encourage childbirth. This policy-driven oscillation fueled criticism that women's reproductive autonomy was treated as subordinate to demographic targets.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The legal landscape of abortion in the United States was fundamentally restructured on **June 24, 2022**, when the Supreme Court issued its decision in **Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization** (597 U.S. 215). Writing for the 6-3 majority, Justice Samuel Alito held that the Constitution does not confer a right to abortion, overruling both **Roe v. Wade** (410 U.S. 113, 1973) and **Planned Parenthood of Southeastern Pennsylvania v. Casey** (505 U.S. 833, 1992). The decision returned abortion regulation entirely to the political process at the state level.

### Pre-Dobbs Framework

For nearly 50 years, the U.S. constitutional framework rested on two foundational decisions:

1. **Roe v. Wade (1973):** Established that the right to privacy under the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment encompassed a woman's decision to terminate a pregnancy. The Court adopted a trimester framework: states could not regulate abortion in the first trimester, could regulate for maternal health in the second, and could prohibit abortion after viability (with life/health exceptions).

2. **Planned Parenthood v. Casey (1992):** Replaced the trimester framework with the "undue burden" standard. States could regulate abortion before viability so long as the regulation did not impose a "substantial obstacle" in the path of a woman seeking an abortion. The viability line (approximately 24 weeks) remained the key constitutional marker.

### Post-Dobbs State Landscape (2025)

As of 2025, the United States presents a patchwork of dramatically divergent state laws:

**States with Total or Near-Total Bans (13 states):**
Alabama, Arkansas, Idaho, Indiana, Kentucky, Louisiana, Mississippi, Missouri, North Dakota, Oklahoma, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, and West Virginia have enacted bans that prohibit abortion at all stages or from conception, with narrow exceptions typically limited to life-threatening medical emergencies. Penalties for providers range from felony charges carrying 1-10 years imprisonment (most states) to up to 99 years or life imprisonment (Alabama, under the Human Life Protection Act, Ala. Code Section 26-23H-4).

**States with Early Gestational Limits (6 states):**
Florida (6 weeks), Georgia (6 weeks, under litigation), Iowa (6 weeks), Nebraska (12 weeks), North Carolina (12 weeks), and South Carolina (6 weeks) restrict abortion to early pregnancy stages, often before many women know they are pregnant.

**States Protecting Abortion Access:**
Conversely, numerous states have enacted affirmative protections. Following the 2024 elections, voters in seven states approved ballot measures enshrining abortion rights in their state constitutions, including Arizona, Colorado, Maryland, Missouri, Montana, Nevada, and New York. States like California, Illinois, New York, and Washington have enacted statutory protections and "shield laws" to protect patients traveling from restrictive states and providers offering telehealth abortion services.

|----------|---------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Total ban | Alabama, Texas, Tennessee | Conception | Felony; up to 99 years (AL) |
| 6-week ban | Florida, Georgia, South Carolina | ~6 weeks LMP | Felony; varies |

| Viability standard | Arizona, Missouri (post-2024) | ~24 weeks | None within legal limits |
| No gestational limit | Colorado, New Jersey, Oregon | None | None |

### Key Federal Dimensions

While Dobbs eliminated the federal constitutional right, several federal statutes and policies remain significant:

- **Hyde Amendment (annually since 1976):** Prohibits the use of federal Medicaid funds for abortion except in cases of rape, incest, or life endangerment. Seventeen states use their own funds to cover Medicaid abortions.
- **Emergency Medical Treatment and Labor Act (EMTALA, 42 U.S.C. Section 1395dd):** The Biden administration argued EMTALA preempts state bans when abortion is necessary emergency stabilizing care. In Moyle v. United States (2024), the Supreme Court dismissed the case on procedural grounds without resolving the EMTALA question.
- **FDA v. Alliance for Hippocratic Medicine (2024):** The Supreme Court unanimously rejected a challenge to FDA approval of mifepristone, preserving access to medication abortion on standing grounds.

### Impact

Research published in 2025 documented significant consequences of state bans: an estimated 155,000 women traveled to other states for abortion care in 2024; 40% of OB/GYN practitioners in ban states reported new constraints on miscarriage and emergency pregnancy care; and maternal health outcomes have deteriorated in states with the most restrictive laws.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany maintains one of the most distinctive regulatory approaches to abortion in the world: abortion is formally a criminal offense, but is rendered non-punishable under specified conditions. This paradoxical framework -- described as "illegal but not punishable" (rechtswidrig aber straffrei) -- emerges from the interplay of the German Criminal Code (Strafgesetzbuch, StGB) and the Pregnancy Conflict Act (Schwangerschaftskonfliktgesetz, SchKG).

**Section 218 StGB (Abortion):** Paragraph 1 provides that whoever terminates a pregnancy is punishable by imprisonment of up to three years or a fine. In particularly serious cases, the penalty increases to imprisonment of up to five years. The pregnant woman herself faces up to one year imprisonment or a fine.

**Section 218a StGB (Non-Punishability of Abortion):** An abortion is not punishable if all of the following conditions are met:

1. The pregnant woman requests the termination.
2. She demonstrates by certificate that she received counseling at a state-approved counseling center at least **three days** before the procedure (Section 219 StGB).
3. The abortion is performed by a licensed physician.
4. Not more than **twelve weeks** have elapsed since conception.

**Section 218a(2) -- Medical Indication:** Abortion is lawful (not merely non-punishable) at any point during pregnancy if a physician determines that the termination is necessary to avert a danger to the life or serious impairment to the physical or mental health of the pregnant woman, and the danger cannot be averted by other means reasonably acceptable to her.

**Section 218a(3) -- Criminological Indication:** Abortion is lawful within 12 weeks of conception if the pregnancy resulted from a criminal act (rape, sexual assault) under Sections 176-179 StGB. Mandatory counseling is not required for this indication.

| Condition | Gestational Limit | Counseling Required | Legal Status |
|-----------|------------------|--------------------|--------------|
| Counseling model (Section 218a(1)) | 12 weeks post-conception | Yes (3-day waiting period) | Not punishable (rechtswidrig aber straffrei) |
| Medical indication (Section 218a(2)) | No limit | No (but counseling offered) | Lawful (rechtmaessig) |
| Criminological indication (Section 218a(3)) | 12 weeks post-conception | No (but counseling offered) | Lawful (rechtmaessig) |

### Mandatory Counseling System

The counseling requirement under Section 219 StGB is a cornerstone of the German model. Counseling must:

- Take place at a **state-recognized pregnancy conflict counseling center** (Schwangerschaftskonfliktberatungsstelle), which may not be affiliated with the facility performing the abortion.
- Be conducted **in person** and must serve "the protection of unborn life" while respecting the woman's autonomy.
- Inform the woman of available public and private assistance for continuing the pregnancy, including financial support, childcare, and adoption options.
- Result in the issuance of a **counseling certificate** (Beratungsschein) confirming attendance, which must be presented to the physician performing the abortion.

The counseling center is prohibited from disclosing the woman's identity to other authorities. The counseling itself is free of charge.

### Constitutional Court Jurisprudence

Germany's abortion framework was shaped by two landmark decisions of the Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht, BVerfG):

1. **BVerfGE 39, 1 (February 25, 1975):** Following German reunification's precursor debate, the Court struck down the Bundestag's 1974 liberalization of abortion, holding that Article 2(2) of the Basic Law (right to life) protects unborn life from the moment of implantation. The state has a constitutional duty (Schutzpflicht) to protect fetal life, and the criminal law is in principle a necessary instrument for fulfilling that duty. However, the Court acknowledged that exceptions to criminalization were constitutionally permissible.

2. **BVerfGE 88, 203 (May 28, 1993):** Following German reunification, the Court reviewed the compromise legislation that sought to reconcile West Germany's counseling model with East Germany's more permissive regime. The Court upheld the counseling model but insisted that abortions performed under it remain formally "unlawful" (rechtswidrig). The critical distinction: an abortion under the counseling model is *not punishable* but it is *not lawful*, meaning it does not qualify for health insurance coverage (except in cases of financial hardship).

### Section 219a Repeal (2022)

On June 24, 2022, the Bundestag repealed **Section 219a StGB**, which had criminalized the "advertising" of abortion services. Enacted in 1933 during the Nazi era, Section 219a had been controversially used to prosecute physicians who provided factual information about abortion procedures on their websites. The most prominent case involved Dr. Kristina Haenel, a general practitioner convicted in 2017 for listing abortion as a service on her practice website.

### 2024-2025 Reform Efforts

In April 2024, a government-appointed **expert commission** (Kommission zur reproduktiven Selbstbestimmung und Fortpflanzungsmedizin) published its final report recommending the legalization of abortion in the early stages of pregnancy and its removal from the criminal code. The commission, chaired by former Federal Constitutional Court judges, concluded that full decriminalization during the first trimester would be compatible with the Basic Law.

In November 2024, **328 Bundestag members** supported a cross-party draft bill to remove Section 218, legalize abortion in the first 12 weeks, eliminate the mandatory waiting period, and regulate lawful abortions through the Pregnancy Conflict Act. However, in February 2025, the Legal Affairs Committee (Rechtsausschuss) declined to bring the bill to a vote, with CDU and FDP leadership blocking the measure. The reform effort thus stalled for the current legislative period.

## ECHR & EU

### European Convention on Human Rights

The European Court of Human Rights (ECtHR) has addressed abortion through a series of landmark cases but has consistently refrained from recognizing an autonomous right to abortion under the Convention. The Court's approach rests on the doctrine of the **margin of appreciation**, granting member states broad discretion in balancing the rights of the pregnant woman against interests in protecting potential life.

### Key ECHR Cases

**Vo v. France (Application No. 53924/00, Grand Chamber, July 8, 2004):**
The Grand Chamber held that the question of when the right to life begins falls within the margin of appreciation of individual states. The Court declined to determine whether the unborn child is a "person" for purposes of Article 2 (right to life), noting that "if the unborn do have a 'right' to 'life', it is implicitly limited by the mother's rights and interests." This decision established that the Convention does not mandate either the protection or the non-protection of fetal life.

**Tysiac v. Poland (Application No. 5410/03, March 20, 2007):**
Alicja Tysiac, diagnosed with severe myopia, was denied an abortion despite warnings that her pregnancy could cause further eye damage (which ultimately occurred, leaving her nearly blind). The Court held that Poland violated **Article 8** (right to private life) by failing to provide an effective procedural mechanism through which Tysiac could challenge the doctors' refusal. The Court did not establish a right to abortion, but mandated that states permitting abortion must establish fair, timely, and accessible procedures for determining eligibility.

**A, B and C v. Ireland (Application Nos. 25579/05, 30024/05 and 27505/05, Grand Chamber, December 16, 2010):**
Three women challenged Ireland's near-total ban on abortion. The Court dismissed the claims of applicants A and B, finding that although a "substantial majority" of Council of Europe member states permitted broader access to abortion, this consensus did not "decisively narrow" Ireland's wide margin of appreciation. However, the Court found a violation of **Article 8** with respect to applicant C, who had a right to lawful abortion (due to a risk to her life from cancer) but was unable to access an effective procedure to vindicate that right. The judgment led to Ireland's enactment of the **Protection of Life During Pregnancy Act 2013**.

**P. and S. v. Poland (Application No. 57375/08, October 30, 2012):**
A 14-year-old rape victim was denied timely access to a lawful abortion due to obstruction by hospital staff and officials. The Court found violations of Articles 3 (prohibition of inhuman or degrading treatment) and 8. This case underscored the obligation of states to ensure that lawful abortions are accessible in practice, not merely in theory.

**R.R. v. Poland (Application No. 27617/04, May 26, 2011):**
A woman was denied timely prenatal genetic testing, effectively depriving her of the ability to obtain a lawful abortion based on fetal abnormality within the gestational limit. The Court found violations of Articles 3 and 8, emphasizing that procrastination by medical professionals can amount to inhuman treatment.

### Core ECHR Principles

| Principle | Source Case | Rule |
|-----------|-------------|------|
| No Convention right to abortion | A, B and C v. Ireland | Article 8 does not confer a right to abortion |
| Wide margin of appreciation | A, B and C v. Ireland; Vo v. France | States may determine the balance between fetal life and women's rights |
| Procedural obligation | Tysiac v. Poland; A, B and C v. Ireland | Where abortion is lawful, states must provide accessible, effective procedures |
| Article 3 implications | P. and S. v. Poland; R.R. v. Poland | Denial of lawful abortion can constitute inhuman or degrading treatment |
| Fetal status unresolved | Vo v. France | The Convention does not define when life begins |

### European Union Framework

The EU has no direct competence over abortion law, which falls within the domain of member-state health and criminal law. However, several EU-level instruments are relevant:

**EU Charter of Fundamental Rights (Article 3):** Guarantees the right to physical and mental integrity. The European Parliament has called for its interpretation to encompass reproductive autonomy.

**European Parliament Resolution (April 11, 2024):** Adopted with **336 votes in favor, 163 against, and 39 abstentions**, the resolution called for the amendment of Article 3 of the EU Charter to state that "everyone has the right to bodily autonomy, to free, informed, full and universal access to sexual and reproductive health and rights, and to all related healthcare services without discrimination, including access to safe and legal abortion." The resolution was inspired in part by France's constitutionalization of abortion rights in March 2024 (Article 34 of the French Constitution). However, amending the Charter requires **unanimous consent** of all 27 member states -- a threshold that remains unattainable given the positions of Poland, Malta, and other states.

**Abortion Law Across EU Member States:** Approaches vary dramatically:

|-------|-----------|------------------|
| Available on request | France, Sweden, Denmark, Belgium, Netherlands, Spain, Portugal, Austria | 12-24 weeks |
| Counseling model (non-punishable) | Germany, Italy, Luxembourg | 12-14 weeks |
| Highly restrictive | Poland (near-total ban since 2021 Constitutional Tribunal ruling) | Life/health threat only |
| Total ban | Malta (partial reform 2023: life-threat exception only) | Life threat only |

## Comparative Analysis

### Constitutional Foundations

The four jurisdictions reveal fundamentally different constitutional starting points:

- **South Korea** grounded its decriminalization in the pregnant woman's right to self-determination under the Korean Constitution, effectively prioritizing individual autonomy.
- **The United States** under Dobbs held that the Constitution is *silent* on abortion, rejecting both a right to abortion and a fetal right to life, and deferring entirely to democratic processes.
- **Germany's** Federal Constitutional Court uniquely held that the state has an affirmative *duty* to protect unborn life derived from Article 2(2) of the Basic Law, but that this duty can be fulfilled through means other than criminal punishment -- leading to the counseling model.
- **The ECHR** maintains deliberate ambiguity, declining to resolve the status of the fetus under Article 2 and granting states broad discretion, while insisting on procedural safeguards where abortion is permitted.

### Criminalization vs. Decriminalization Trends

A clear global trend toward decriminalization is evident, though not uniform:

- South Korea moved from full criminalization to no regulation.
- Multiple U.S. states moved from regulated access to total prohibition after Dobbs, while others constitutionalized abortion rights -- a rare example of bifurcation within a single country.
- Germany's expert commission recommended decriminalization, but legislative reform stalled; the "illegal but not punishable" framework persists.
- At the European level, the EP resolution signals political aspiration toward recognizing abortion as a right, but the legal framework has not changed.

### The Role of Counseling

Germany's mandatory counseling model represents a distinctive "third way" -- neither prohibiting abortion nor treating it as an unrestricted right. South Korea's Constitutional Court envisioned a similar approach but the legislature failed to implement it. Several U.S. states maintained counseling requirements pre-Dobbs, though critics argued they were designed to discourage rather than inform.

### Access vs. Legal Right

All four jurisdictions demonstrate that formal legality does not guarantee practical access:

- In South Korea, absence of insurance coverage and unapproved medication abortion create significant barriers.
- In the United States, geographic access has become the primary barrier, with women in ban states traveling hundreds of miles.
- In Germany, declining numbers of providers (particularly in rural and eastern regions) create practical access difficulties.
- ECHR case law (Tysiac, P. and S.) demonstrates that even where abortion is legal, procedural obstacles can constitute human rights violations.
