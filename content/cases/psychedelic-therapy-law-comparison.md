## Summary

Psychedelic-assisted therapy regulation (사이키델릭 보조 치료 규제) -- the legal framework governing the therapeutic use of psychedelic substances including psilocybin, MDMA (3,4-methylenedioxymethamphetamine), LSD (lysergic acid diethylamide), DMT (dimethyltryptamine), and ketamine in clinical and supervised settings -- represents one of the most rapidly evolving areas of drug policy and healthcare law across all major jurisdictions, driven by a growing body of clinical evidence demonstrating the therapeutic potential of these substances for treatment-resistant depression, post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), substance use disorders, and end-of-life psychological distress. South Korea maintains strict prohibition of all classic psychedelic substances under the Act on the Control of Narcotics (마약류 관리에 관한 법률), with psilocybin, LSD, MDMA, and DMT classified as Schedule I narcotics or psychotropic substances; however, ketamine is available as a licensed anesthetic, and the Korean Ministry of Food and Drug Safety (MFDS) has approved clinical trials investigating esketamine (Spravato) for treatment-resistant depression, reflecting a cautious opening toward psychedelic-adjacent therapeutics. The United States has witnessed the most significant regulatory developments: the FDA designated psilocybin as a "Breakthrough Therapy" for treatment-resistant depression (2018) and MDMA-assisted therapy for PTSD (2017), Oregon became the first state to legalize psilocybin-assisted therapy through Measure 109 (2020, services operational from 2023), Colorado passed Proposition 122 (2022) creating a framework for regulated access to psilocybin, DMT, ibogaine, and mescaline, the federal Right to Try Act (21 U.S.C. Section 360bbb-0a, 2018) has been invoked for psychedelic access, and the DEA's Schedule I classification under the Controlled Substances Act (21 U.S.C. Section 812) remains the primary federal barrier despite growing bipartisan support for rescheduling. Germany prohibits classic psychedelics under the Betaeubungsmittelgesetz (BtMG, Narcotics Act), with psilocybin, LSD, and MDMA listed in Anlage I (non-prescribable narcotics); however, Germany has approved esketamine nasal spray (Spravato) for treatment-resistant depression through the EU centralized marketing authorization procedure, and the Bundesinstitut fuer Arzneimittel und Medizinprodukte (BfArM) has authorized clinical trials with psilocybin and MDMA under BtMG Section 3 research exemptions. At the EU level, the European Medicines Agency (EMA) has engaged with psychedelic research through its scientific advice and clinical trial oversight functions, the EU Clinical Trials Regulation (536/2014) provides the framework for psychedelic clinical trials across member states, and the ECtHR has addressed the boundaries of state drug policy through its margin of appreciation doctrine while the UN Single Convention on Narcotic Drugs (1961) and the Convention on Psychotropic Substances (1971) establish the international scheduling framework that constrains national reform.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea maintains one of the strictest regulatory frameworks for psychedelic substances in the developed world, reflecting the country's broadly prohibitionist approach to controlled substances.

**Act on the Control of Narcotics (마약류 관리에 관한 법률):** This Act provides the comprehensive framework for the classification, control, and criminalization of narcotics, psychotropic substances, and hemp. Psychedelic substances are regulated as follows:

| Substance | Classification | Legal Status |
|-----------|---------------|-------------|
| Psilocybin/Psilocin | Psychotropic substance (향정신성의약품) | Prohibited; no medical use recognized |
| LSD | Psychotropic substance (향정신성의약품) | Prohibited; no medical use recognized |
| MDMA | Psychotropic substance (향정신성의약품) | Prohibited; no medical use recognized |
| DMT | Psychotropic substance (향정신성의약품) | Prohibited; no medical use recognized |
| Ketamine | Psychotropic substance (향정신성의약품) | Medical use authorized; anesthetic |
| Mescaline | Psychotropic substance (향정신성의약품) | Prohibited; no medical use recognized |

**Penalties:** Illegal manufacture, import, export, or distribution of psychotropic substances carries penalties of up to 10 years imprisonment. Simple possession for personal use carries penalties of up to 3 years imprisonment or fines up to 30 million won. South Korea's drug enforcement approach emphasizes deterrence, with mandatory drug testing in certain professional contexts and strong social stigma attached to drug use.

### Esketamine and Therapeutic Developments

Despite the strict prohibition of classic psychedelics, South Korea has moved toward acceptance of psychedelic-adjacent therapeutics:

**Esketamine (Spravato):** The MFDS approved esketamine nasal spray (manufactured by Janssen/Johnson & Johnson) for treatment-resistant depression in adults in 2020, following the FDA's 2019 approval. Administration is restricted to REMS-equivalent certified healthcare facilities, where patients must be monitored for at least two hours post-administration due to risks of dissociation, sedation, and blood pressure changes.

**Research Landscape:** Korean academic institutions, including Seoul National University Hospital and Samsung Medical Center, have participated in international clinical trials for esketamine and have expressed interest in future psilocybin research. However, the regulatory barriers under the Narcotics Control Act make purely domestic psychedelic clinical trials challenging to initiate.

### Cultural and Policy Context

South Korea's strict drug policy reflects deep cultural conservatism regarding substance use, reinforced by the country's proximity to major methamphetamine trafficking routes and the historical experience with opium during the colonial period. The public discourse on drug policy reform in Korea focuses primarily on cannabis rather than psychedelics, and there is currently minimal political advocacy for psychedelic therapy legalization. Academic and medical interest in psychedelic research is growing, but translation into policy reform remains distant.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has the most dynamic and complex psychedelic therapy regulatory landscape, characterized by tension between federal prohibition and state-level legalization.

**Controlled Substances Act (CSA, 21 U.S.C. Section 812):** The CSA classifies drugs into five schedules based on their accepted medical use, potential for abuse, and safety profile. Psilocybin, MDMA, LSD, DMT, mescaline, and other classic psychedelics are classified as Schedule I substances, defined as having "a high potential for abuse," "no currently accepted medical use in treatment in the United States," and "a lack of accepted safety for use under medical supervision." This classification creates significant regulatory barriers for clinical research, requiring DEA Schedule I research licenses in addition to FDA IND authorization.

### FDA Breakthrough Therapy Designations

The FDA has granted Breakthrough Therapy designations -- which expedite the development and review process -- for two psychedelic therapies:

| Substance | Indication | Year Designated | Sponsor |
|-----------|-----------|-----------------|---------|
| Psilocybin | Treatment-resistant depression | 2018 | Compass Pathways (COMP 360) |
| Psilocybin | Major depressive disorder | 2019 | Usona Institute |
| MDMA | Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) | 2017 | Multidisciplinary Association for Psychedelic Studies (MAPS) |

**MDMA-Assisted Therapy for PTSD:** MAPS completed Phase 3 clinical trials demonstrating significant efficacy of MDMA-assisted therapy for PTSD. However, the FDA issued a Complete Response Letter in August 2024 for the MDMA-assisted therapy NDA, citing concerns about clinical trial conduct and the need for additional data. MAPS/Lykos Therapeutics has been working to address the FDA's concerns with supplementary data.

**Psilocybin Clinical Trials:** Multiple Phase 2 and Phase 3 clinical trials are underway for psilocybin-assisted therapy for treatment-resistant depression, major depressive disorder, alcohol use disorder, and end-of-life distress. The FDA has been actively engaged in developing regulatory frameworks for psychedelic-assisted therapies, including novel guidance on the role of the therapeutic setting and therapist training.

### Oregon Measure 109

Oregon Measure 109, approved by voters in November 2020, created the Oregon Psilocybin Services (OPS) program, making Oregon the first jurisdiction in the United States to establish a regulated framework for psilocybin-assisted therapy outside of clinical trials.

**Key Features:**
- **Licensing System:** Established four license types: manufacturers (cultivators), testing laboratories, service centers (where sessions occur), and facilitators (who guide sessions).
- **Service Model:** Clients undergo a preparation session, an administration session at a licensed service center (lasting 4-6 hours with a licensed facilitator present), and an integration session.
- **No Prescription Required:** Psilocybin services are available to adults 21 and older without a medical diagnosis or prescription; however, a screening process identifies contraindications.
- **Training Requirements:** Facilitators must complete an approved training program of at least 120 hours and pass an examination.
- **Operational Launch:** The first licensed service centers began operating in June 2023. As of 2025, over 30 service centers and 200 licensed facilitators are operational across Oregon.

### Colorado Proposition 122 (Natural Medicine Health Act)

Colorado voters approved Proposition 122 in November 2022, which decriminalized the personal use, possession, growing, and sharing of psilocybin, psilocin, DMT, ibogaine, and mescaline (excluding peyote) for adults 21 and older, and directed the Colorado Department of Regulatory Agencies (DORA) to establish a regulated access framework for "natural medicine" healing centers by 2025. The regulated access program began operations in 2025, following Oregon's service center model with modifications.

### Municipal Decriminalization

Numerous US cities have decriminalized psilocybin and/or other psychedelic substances through local ordinances or ballot initiatives, beginning with Denver, Colorado (2019). Other cities include Oakland and Santa Cruz (California), Ann Arbor (Michigan), Washington D.C., Seattle (Washington), and Cambridge and Somerville (Massachusetts). These local measures typically make enforcement of psychedelic possession laws the "lowest law enforcement priority" but do not legalize manufacture, distribution, or therapeutic use.

### Right to Try Act

The Right to Try Act (21 U.S.C. Section 360bbb-0a, 2018) permits eligible patients with life-threatening diseases or conditions to access investigational drugs that have completed Phase I clinical trials, without requiring FDA emergency use authorization. Several advocates and patients have invoked the Right to Try Act to access psilocybin for end-of-life distress and treatment-resistant conditions, though the practical application remains limited due to manufacturer willingness to supply, DEA scheduling barriers, and the Act's narrow eligibility criteria.

### Federal Legislative Proposals

Multiple federal bills have been introduced to facilitate psychedelic research or therapy access, including the Breakthrough Therapies Act (proposed to remove scheduling barriers for FDA-designated breakthrough therapies), the Right to Try Clarification Act (proposed to clarify that the Right to Try Act applies to Schedule I substances), and bipartisan proposals to fund federal psychedelic research through the National Institutes of Health (NIH) and the Department of Veterans Affairs (VA) for veteran PTSD treatment.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany maintains a prohibitionist framework for classic psychedelics while engaging with the emerging therapeutic evidence through clinical trial authorizations and esketamine approval.

**Betaeubungsmittelgesetz (BtMG, Narcotics Act):** The BtMG classifies controlled substances into three annexes:

| Annex | Description | Psychedelic Substances |
|-------|-------------|----------------------|
| Anlage I | Non-marketable, non-prescribable narcotics | Psilocybin, psilocin, LSD, MDMA, DMT, mescaline |
| Anlage II | Marketable but non-prescribable narcotics | Certain precursor substances |
| Anlage III | Marketable, prescribable narcotics | Ketamine, certain opioids |

**BtMG Section 3 -- Research Exemption:** BfArM may grant exemptions from the prohibitions in BtMG for scientific or public-interest purposes. This provision has been used to authorize clinical trials with psilocybin and MDMA in Germany, typically in the context of multinational clinical trials.

### Clinical Trial Activity

Germany has participated in several significant psychedelic clinical trials:

**Psilocybin Research:** The Charite - Universitaetsmedizin Berlin and the Central Institute of Mental Health (Zentralinstitut fuer Seelische Gesundheit, ZI) in Mannheim have been sites for the COMPASS Pathways Phase 2b and Phase 3 psilocybin trials for treatment-resistant depression. BfArM has authorized these trials under BtMG Section 3, and the ethics committees at the respective institutions have provided ethical approval.

**MDMA Research:** German sites have participated in MAPS-sponsored Phase 2 clinical trials for MDMA-assisted therapy for PTSD, with BfArM authorization.

**Esketamine:** Esketamine nasal spray (Spravato) received EU centralized marketing authorization via the EMA in December 2019 for treatment-resistant depression in adults (in combination with an SSRI or SNRI), and subsequently for the rapid reduction of depressive symptoms in patients with major depressive disorder who constitute a psychiatric emergency. In Germany, esketamine is reimbursed through the GKV system following the Gemeinsamer Bundesausschuss (G-BA) benefit assessment (AMNOG evaluation), which acknowledged a minor additional benefit for a subpopulation of treatment-resistant depression patients.

### Off-Label Ketamine Use

Ketamine, listed in Anlage III BtMG (prescribable), is used off-label by some German psychiatrists for treatment-resistant depression and acute suicidality. The legal basis for off-label use in Germany is established through BSG (Bundessozialgericht, Federal Social Court) jurisprudence requiring a serious condition with no therapeutic alternative and sufficient evidence of efficacy. Several German clinics offer ketamine infusion therapy, though this remains an individual Heilversuch (individual therapeutic attempt) rather than standard care.

### Policy Discourse

Germany's drug policy debate has been dominated by the cannabis legalization process (Cannabisgesetz, CanG, enacted 2024), and psychedelic therapy reform has received comparatively less political attention. However, the German Association for Psychiatry, Psychotherapy and Psychosomatics (DGPPN) and individual academic researchers have called for evidence-based reconsideration of psychedelic scheduling as clinical trial results accumulate. The Gruene (Green Party) and elements of the FDP have expressed openness to exploring regulatory pathways for psychedelic-assisted therapy if Phase 3 trial evidence supports efficacy and safety.

## ECHR & EU

### UN International Drug Control Conventions

The international scheduling framework constrains national reform options:

**Single Convention on Narcotic Drugs (1961, as amended by 1972 Protocol):** Psilocybin and mescaline-containing plants are not explicitly scheduled under the 1961 Convention, creating legal ambiguity regarding natural psychedelics.

**Convention on Psychotropic Substances (1971):** This Convention schedules synthetic psychedelics:
- **Schedule I:** DMT, LSD, MDMA (added 1986), psilocybin, psilocin, mescaline, cathinone.
- Schedule I substances are subject to the most restrictive controls, with use limited to scientific and very limited medical purposes by duly authorized persons.

The 1971 Convention permits the medical and scientific use of Schedule I substances under appropriate controls, providing legal space for clinical trials and, arguably, for regulated therapeutic access programs. The International Narcotics Control Board (INCB) has acknowledged the growing therapeutic research but has cautioned that any non-research access must comply with Convention requirements.

### EU Regulatory Framework

The EU does not have a harmonized policy on psychedelic substances, as drug scheduling and criminal law remain primarily national competences. However, several EU-level mechanisms are relevant:

**European Medicines Agency (EMA):** The EMA provides scientific advice to sponsors conducting psychedelic clinical trials and will evaluate any future marketing authorization applications for psychedelic therapies submitted through the centralized procedure. The EMA has engaged with the therapeutic psychedelic field through scientific meetings and published considerations on the regulatory challenges of psychedelic-assisted therapy, including the role of the therapeutic setting as an integral part of the treatment.

**EU Clinical Trials Regulation (536/2014):** Psychedelic clinical trials in EU member states are conducted under this Regulation, submitted through the EU Clinical Trials Information System (CTIS). The multinational trial facilitation provisions of the CTR are particularly relevant for psychedelic research, which typically requires large, multi-site trials.

**European Monitoring Centre for Drugs and Drug Addiction (EMCDDA/EUDA):** Renamed the European Union Drugs Agency (EUDA) in 2024, this agency monitors drug trends and provides evidence-based analysis. EUDA has published analyses of the therapeutic psychedelic landscape and the regulatory challenges of novel psychoactive substance-based therapies.

### ECtHR and Drug Policy

The ECtHR has generally afforded states a wide margin of appreciation in drug policy matters:

**Pagnon v. France (Application No. 61087/16, Decision of January 11, 2022):** The Court declared inadmissible a complaint challenging France's prohibition of ayahuasca (containing DMT) under Article 9 (freedom of religion) and Article 8 (right to respect for private life), holding that the interference with the applicant's rights was justified under the public health and public safety exceptions. The Court acknowledged that the prohibition pursued legitimate aims but did not require strict necessity analysis.

**Van der Velden v. the Netherlands (Application No. 29514/05, Decision of December 7, 2006):** The Court rejected a challenge to the Netherlands' classification of dried psilocybin mushrooms, noting the state's margin of appreciation in public health matters.

### Comparative International Developments

Beyond the four primary jurisdictions, several countries have made significant advances:

- **Australia:** The Therapeutic Goods Administration (TGA) rescheduled psilocybin and MDMA from Schedule 9 (prohibited) to Schedule 8 (controlled drug) for authorized psychiatrist prescribing for treatment-resistant depression and PTSD, effective July 1, 2023 -- making Australia the first country to authorize prescribing of these substances.
- **Canada:** Health Canada has granted individual Section 56 exemptions to patients for psilocybin-assisted therapy for end-of-life distress, and the Special Access Programme (SAP) was amended in 2022 to allow practitioner requests for restricted drugs including psilocybin and MDMA.
- **Jamaica and the Netherlands:** Psilocybin truffles (the Netherlands) and psilocybin mushrooms (Jamaica, unregulated) are legally available, creating de facto psychedelic therapy tourism industries.

## Comparative Analysis

### Scheduling and Access Barriers

The fundamental challenge across all jurisdictions is the tension between the international scheduling framework (particularly the 1971 Convention's Schedule I classification of psilocybin, MDMA, LSD, and DMT) and the accumulating clinical evidence of therapeutic efficacy. All four primary jurisdictions maintain Schedule I or equivalent classification for classic psychedelics, but the practical barriers to research and therapeutic access vary significantly. The US has developed the most extensive research infrastructure despite Schedule I classification, facilitated by the FDA's Breakthrough Therapy designations and the state-level legalization in Oregon and Colorado. Germany provides research access through BtMG Section 3 exemptions. South Korea has the most restrictive practical landscape.

### State/Subnational Reform

The US experience demonstrates the power of subnational reform in drug policy. Oregon's Measure 109 and Colorado's Proposition 122 have created operating models for regulated psychedelic therapy that exist in tension with federal CSA scheduling. This federal-state tension mirrors the earlier cannabis legalization experience. In Germany and South Korea, drug scheduling is exclusively a national-level matter, precluding subnational experimentation.

### Therapeutic Model Regulation

A distinctive regulatory challenge of psychedelic-assisted therapy is that the therapeutic setting, the therapist-patient relationship, and the preparation/integration sessions are integral to the treatment -- not merely a vehicle for drug delivery. Oregon's Measure 109 addressed this by creating a novel licensing framework for facilitators and service centers. Traditional pharmaceutical regulatory pathways (FDA, EMA, MFDS) are designed to evaluate drug products rather than drug-plus-psychotherapy combination treatments, creating regulatory gaps that agencies are working to address through novel guidance documents and clinical trial designs.

### Equity and Access

A cross-cutting concern is equitable access to psychedelic therapy. Oregon's out-of-pocket cost model (sessions typically cost $1,000-3,000 with no insurance coverage) raises access concerns. The potential FDA/EMA approval of psilocybin or MDMA through conventional pathways would facilitate insurance coverage but would also impose the constraints of conventional pharmaceutical distribution. South Korea's strict prohibition eliminates any access pathway outside of clinical trials. Germany's GKV system would likely cover approved psychedelic therapies following G-BA benefit assessment, potentially providing the most equitable access framework.

### Risk and Safety Regulation

All jurisdictions acknowledge the need for appropriate safeguards around psychedelic therapy, including screening for contraindications (particularly psychotic spectrum disorders and cardiovascular conditions), training and competency requirements for therapists/facilitators, physical safety during sessions (monitoring and supervised environments), and integration support and adverse event reporting. Oregon's regulatory framework addresses these through licensing requirements and service center standards. Clinical trial protocols include rigorous safety monitoring. The challenge of translating clinical trial safety protocols into scalable regulated access programs remains a central regulatory question across all jurisdictions.
