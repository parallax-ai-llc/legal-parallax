## Summary

Submarine communications cables carry approximately 99% of intercontinental data traffic, making their legal regulation a matter of critical national security, economic infrastructure, and international cooperation. The regulatory framework for undersea cables spans international maritime law, telecommunications regulation, environmental protection, national security review, and criminal law provisions addressing sabotage. South Korea regulates submarine cables through its Telecommunications Business Act (전기통신사업법) and the Public Waters Management Act (공유수면 관리 및 매립에 관한 법률), with the Korea Communications Commission and the Ministry of Oceans and Fisheries exercising overlapping jurisdiction. The United States requires cable landing licenses under the Cable Landing License Act of 1921 (47 U.S.C. § 34-39) administered by the FCC, with Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States (CFIUS) review for national security concerns and the Department of State's "Team Telecom" (now the Committee for the Assessment of Foreign Participation in the United States Telecommunications Services Sector) evaluating foreign ownership. Germany regulates cable landing and operation through the TKG 2021, the Federal Mining Act (Bundesberggesetz, BBergG) for seabed activities, and the Federal Maritime and Hydrographic Agency (BSH) for environmental permitting. The international legal framework rests on the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS, 1982) Articles 79, 112-115, and the International Convention for the Protection of Submarine Cables (Paris, 1884), supplemented by the International Cable Protection Committee's (ICPC) recommended practices.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's submarine cable regulatory framework reflects its geographic position as a peninsula nation critically dependent on undersea cables for international connectivity:

- **Telecommunications Business Act (전기통신사업법):** Article 6 requires telecommunications business operators, including submarine cable system operators, to register with the Korea Communications Commission. Article 36 grants the KCC authority to regulate international telecommunications facilities, including approval of submarine cable routes and landing points. The KCC coordinates with Japanese and Chinese telecommunications authorities for bilateral cable systems crossing shared maritime boundaries.

- **Public Waters Management Act (공유수면 관리 및 매립에 관한 법률):** Article 8 requires permits for installation of structures on or under the seabed within South Korea's territorial waters, including cable landing facilities. The Ministry of Oceans and Fisheries administers these permits, conducting environmental and navigational safety assessments.

- **Marine Environment Management Act (해양환경관리법):** Article 84 requires environmental impact assessments for submarine cable installation within Korean waters. The assessment covers impacts on marine ecosystems, sediment disturbance, electromagnetic fields from power cables, and potential interference with fishing activities. The Korean Marine Environment Management Corporation (KOEM) conducts environmental monitoring during and after installation.

- **Maritime Safety Act (해사안전법):** Article 10 empowers the Korea Maritime Safety Tribunal to designate cable protection zones where anchoring, bottom trawling, and other potentially damaging activities are prohibited. Violations carry fines of up to KRW 50 million and potential criminal liability under Article 92.

- **Criminal Act (형법) Article 141:** Criminalizes the destruction or damage of "public utility installations" (공용물파괴), which Korean courts have interpreted to include submarine telecommunications cables. Penalties range from 1 to 10 years imprisonment, reflecting the critical infrastructure status of submarine cables.

- **National Security Review:** The National Intelligence Service (NIS) conducts security assessments of submarine cable projects involving foreign ownership or routing through sensitive maritime areas. The 2023 amendments to the Telecommunications Business Act formalized the NIS review process, requiring security clearance for cable landing station operators and route approval for cables passing through Korea's exclusive economic zone (EEZ).

### Key Principles

South Korea's submarine cable interests are shaped by its unique geographic and geopolitical position. As a de facto island nation (due to the Korean Demilitarized Zone blocking land-based connections to the Eurasian continent), South Korea depends entirely on submarine cables for international telecommunications connectivity. Approximately 15 submarine cable systems connect South Korea to Japan, China, the United States (via Pacific routes), and Southeast Asia.

This dependency has elevated submarine cable protection to a national security priority. The government's "Digital Infrastructure Protection Plan" (2023) classified submarine cables as "Grade 1 Critical National Infrastructure" (국가핵심기반시설), triggering enhanced physical security requirements, redundancy planning, and cyber-physical protection measures under the Critical Infrastructure Protection Act (국가기반시설보호법).

A distinctive feature of South Korea's approach is the close coordination between cable operators and the fishing industry. The Korea Submarine Cable Protection Association (한국해저케이블보호협회), a private-public body, mediates disputes between cable operators and fishermen, administers compensation funds for fishing gear lost to cable avoidance requirements, and coordinates cable route planning to minimize impacts on traditional fishing grounds.

### Notable Cases

- **Korea-Japan Submarine Cable Repair Dispute (2019):** A cable break in the Korea-Japan East Sea cable system required emergency repair operations in disputed maritime areas near the Dokdo/Takeshima territorial dispute. The incident required diplomatic coordination between Korean and Japanese authorities and highlighted the challenges of cable repair jurisdiction in contested waters.

- **KCC Administrative Action on Foreign Cable Landing (2022):** The KCC imposed conditions on a Chinese telecommunications company's application to land a new submarine cable in Busan, requiring that the cable landing station be operated by a Korean-majority joint venture, that traffic data not be accessible to foreign government entities, and that redundant routing be provided to ensure Korean network resilience.

- **Fishing Vessel Cable Damage Prosecution (2023):** A criminal prosecution under Criminal Act Article 141 against a fishing vessel operator whose bottom trawling activities severed a submarine cable connecting Jeju Island to the Korean mainland, disrupting telecommunications for approximately 48 hours. The Jeju District Court sentenced the operator to 2 years imprisonment (suspended) and ordered KRW 3.2 billion in damages for repair costs and service interruption losses.

## United States

### Federal Framework

The United States regulates submarine cables through a multi-agency framework reflecting the cables' intersection with telecommunications, national security, maritime, and environmental jurisdictions:

- **Cable Landing License Act of 1921 (47 U.S.C. §§ 34-39):** The foundational statute requiring that any submarine cable connecting the United States to a foreign country must obtain a license from the FCC (authority delegated from the President by Executive Order 10530). Section 34 grants the President discretionary authority to issue, withhold, or revoke cable landing licenses, with the power delegated to the FCC subject to interagency consultation.

- **Executive Order 13913 (April 2020, "Committee for the Assessment of Foreign Participation in the United States Telecommunications Services Sector"):** Formalized the previously informal "Team Telecom" interagency review process for cable landing license applications. The Committee, chaired by the Department of Justice and including the Departments of Defense and Homeland Security, reviews applications involving foreign ownership or control for national security and law enforcement concerns. The Committee has 120 days for initial review, extendable to 180 days with a possible additional 90-day secondary review.

- **Submarine Cable Act of 1888 (47 U.S.C. § 21):** Criminalizes the willful or negligent breaking or injuring of a submarine cable, with penalties of up to $5,000 and two years imprisonment. This statute implements the 1884 International Convention for the Protection of Submarine Cables. The penalties, unchanged since 1888, are widely regarded as inadequate for the current value of submarine cable infrastructure.

- **18 U.S.C. § 1362 (Communication Lines, Stations, or Systems):** Criminalizes the willful or malicious destruction of "any communication line, station, or system" used for military or civilian purposes, with penalties of up to 10 years imprisonment. This broader criminal statute has been applied to submarine cable sabotage as a supplement to the antiquated Submarine Cable Act.

- **Foreign Investment Risk Review Modernization Act (FIRRMA, 2018):** Expanded CFIUS jurisdiction to cover investments in critical telecommunications infrastructure, including submarine cable systems. CFIUS review can result in mitigation conditions or transaction prohibition.

- **Outer Continental Shelf Lands Act (OCSLA, 43 U.S.C. § 1333):** The Department of the Interior's Bureau of Ocean Energy Management (BOEM) issues permits for activities on the outer continental shelf, including cable installation. These permits require environmental review and coordination with other seabed users (oil/gas, offshore wind, fishing).

- **National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA, 42 U.S.C. § 4321ff):** Cable landing license applications require environmental review. For major cable projects, this may require a full Environmental Impact Statement (EIS) assessing impacts on marine habitats, endangered species (under the ESA), marine mammals (under the MMPA), and designated critical habitat.

### Team Telecom / National Security Review

The Team Telecom review process has become the most consequential aspect of U.S. submarine cable regulation. Key patterns include:

- **China-connected cables:** Since 2020, the Team Telecom Committee has effectively blocked or imposed prohibitive conditions on multiple cable projects involving Chinese ownership or Chinese landing points. The Pacific Light Cable Network (PLCN), backed by Google and Meta, was denied authorization for its Hong Kong segment in 2021, and the application was eventually restructured to exclude the Hong Kong landing.

- **Mitigation Agreements:** For approved cables with foreign ownership, Team Telecom typically requires "Network Security Agreements" or "Mitigation Agreements" imposing conditions on data handling, lawful intercept capabilities, physical security of landing stations, and limitations on foreign access to network management systems.

- **Review Timelines:** Despite the EO 13913 timeline requirements, reviews regularly extend beyond statutory deadlines. The average processing time for cable landing licenses with foreign ownership components exceeds 18 months.

### Notable Cases

- **Pacific Light Cable Network (PLCN) Denial (2020-2021):** The Team Telecom Committee recommended denial of the U.S.-Hong Kong segment of the PLCN submarine cable, a joint venture involving Google, Meta, and a Hong Kong-based entity (Pacific Light Data Communication Co. Ltd.). The FCC followed the recommendation, marking the first outright denial of a submarine cable landing license on national security grounds. The cable was subsequently restructured as the Apricot cable system, connecting the U.S. to Taiwan, Japan, and the Philippines instead.

- **SubCom / Huawei Marine Networks (HMN Tech) Competition Concerns (2019-2024):** The U.S. government expressed national security concerns about Huawei Marine Networks (subsequently renamed HMN Technologies) building submarine cable systems serving U.S. allies and partners. The "Clean Network" initiative encouraged allies to exclude HMN Tech from critical cable projects, influencing cable procurement decisions in the Pacific Islands and Southeast Asia.

- **United States v. Zheng (S.D.N.Y., 2024):** Federal prosecution under 18 U.S.C. § 1362 of an individual who allegedly surveilled submarine cable landing stations on the U.S. Pacific coast on behalf of a foreign intelligence service. The case, while primarily an espionage prosecution, highlighted the physical security vulnerabilities of cable landing infrastructure.

- **BOEM/NOAA Offshore Wind-Submarine Cable Conflict (2023-2025):** The rapid expansion of offshore wind energy development off the U.S. East Coast has created significant conflicts with existing submarine cable routes. BOEM's environmental reviews for offshore wind projects now include detailed cable crossing and proximity analyses, and NOAA has proposed new guidelines for minimum separation distances between offshore wind installations and submarine cables.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's submarine cable regulation reflects its position as a major North Sea and Baltic Sea coastal state with extensive cable infrastructure:

- **Telecommunications Act 2021 (TKG):** The BNetzA regulates the telecommunications aspects of submarine cable operations in Germany, including operator licensing, interconnection requirements, and service obligations. Cable operators providing international telecommunications capacity must hold a TKG notification or license.

- **Federal Mining Act (Bundesberggesetz, BBergG):** The BBergG governs activities on and under the German continental shelf, including submarine cable installation. Section 132 requires permits for installations on the seabed of the German EEZ, administered by the State Mining Offices (Bergämter) in coordination with the BSH.

- **Federal Maritime and Hydrographic Agency (BSH) Permitting:** The BSH issues permits for submarine cable installation in the German EEZ under the Marine Facilities Ordinance (Seeanlagenverordnung, SeeAnlV). Environmental impact assessments are mandatory under the Environmental Impact Assessment Act (UVPG), and the BSH evaluates impacts on marine habitats protected under the Habitats Directive (92/43/EEC) and the Marine Strategy Framework Directive (2008/56/EC).

- **Offshore Wind Energy Act (WindSeeG):** As Germany has one of the world's largest offshore wind programs, the WindSeeG coordinates submarine cable routes (both export cables from wind farms and telecommunications cables) through maritime spatial planning. The BSH's Maritime Spatial Plans (Raumordnungspläne) for the North Sea and Baltic Sea designate priority areas and corridors for submarine cables.

- **Criminal Code (Strafgesetzbuch, StGB):** Section 317 criminalizes the disruption of telecommunications installations, including submarine cables, with penalties of up to five years imprisonment. Section 305a addresses the destruction of "important" technical facilities, carrying penalties of up to five years. Section 88 (anti-sabotage provisions) applies where cable damage constitutes sabotage of defense-critical infrastructure.

- **Foreign Trade and Payments Act (Außenwirtschaftsgesetz, AWG) § 55ff:** The Federal Ministry of Economy and Climate Action (BMWK) screens foreign investments in submarine cable operators as "critical infrastructure" under the expanded investment screening regime, with review authority for acquisitions of 10% or more of voting rights in cable operators by non-EU/non-EFTA investors.

### Key Principles

Germany's submarine cable regulatory environment has been dramatically reshaped by the Nord Stream pipeline incidents of September 2022 and the subsequent Baltic Sea cable damage incidents of October 2023 and November 2024. These events transformed submarine cable protection from a routine regulatory matter into a critical national security priority:

1. **Critical Infrastructure Classification:** Submarine cables have been classified as critical infrastructure under the IT Security Act 2.0 (IT-Sicherheitsgesetz 2.0, 2021), requiring cable operators to implement cybersecurity measures, report security incidents, and undergo BSI (Federal Office for Information Security) audits.

2. **Enhanced Physical Protection:** Following the Nord Stream and Baltic cable incidents, the Bundeswehr (German armed forces) has increased maritime patrols in areas with dense submarine cable infrastructure, and NATO has established a Critical Undersea Infrastructure Coordination Cell at MARCOM (Maritime Command) to coordinate allied protection efforts.

3. **Cable Route Planning and Conflict Management:** The BSH's maritime spatial planning process systematically addresses conflicts between submarine cables and other sea uses (offshore wind, fishing, aggregate extraction, shipping). Germany's approach is considered among the most comprehensive in Europe.

4. **EU Coordination:** Germany has been a leading advocate within the EU for enhanced submarine cable protection legislation, supporting the European Commission's 2024 Recommendation on the Security and Resilience of Submarine Cable Infrastructures (C(2024) 1099).

### Notable Cases

- **Baltic Sea Cable Damage Investigation (2023-2024):** In October 2023, the Balticconnector gas pipeline and a submarine telecommunications cable (C-Lion1, connecting Finland to Germany) were simultaneously damaged in the Baltic Sea. The investigation, coordinated between Finnish, Estonian, German, and Chinese authorities, concluded that a Chinese-flagged vessel (Newnew Polar Bear) dragged its anchor across the seabed, severing both the pipeline and the cable. The incident demonstrated the vulnerability of submarine infrastructure and prompted calls for enhanced international protection mechanisms. Germany's Federal Prosecutor (Generalbundesanwalt) opened an investigation under StGB § 317 and § 305a.

- **Nord Stream Sabotage Investigation (2022-2025):** While primarily involving gas pipelines, the Nord Stream investigations established legal precedents relevant to submarine cable protection, including the scope of German criminal jurisdiction for damage to infrastructure on the continental shelf, the application of StGB sabotage provisions to undersea installations, and international cooperation mechanisms for investigating attacks on submarine infrastructure.

- **BSH v. Offshore Wind Developer (Cable Crossing Dispute, 2024):** The BSH mediated a dispute between a submarine telecommunications cable operator and an offshore wind developer whose export cable route crossed the existing telecommunications cable. The BSH established crossing design standards (minimum vertical separation of 1 meter, protective crossing structures) and cost-sharing principles for crossing installations.

- **AWG Investment Screening of Submarine Cable Acquisition (2023):** The BMWK subjected a proposed acquisition of a minority stake in a North Sea submarine cable operator by a non-EU investor to investment screening under AWG § 55, ultimately clearing the transaction subject to conditions requiring maintenance of German operational control and notification of any subsequent changes in beneficial ownership.

## ECHR / EU Framework

### Convention Articles

- **Article 10 -- Freedom of Expression and Information:** Submarine cables are the physical infrastructure enabling the exercise of Article 10 rights in the digital age. States' positive obligations under Article 10 arguably extend to protecting the physical infrastructure necessary for information exchange, including submarine cables.

- **Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 -- Protection of Property:** Submarine cable systems represent investments of hundreds of millions to billions of euros. Cable licenses, route rights, and the physical infrastructure itself constitute "possessions" protected by Article 1 Protocol No. 1. State regulatory actions affecting cable operations (route modifications, landing denials, forced infrastructure sharing) must satisfy proportionality requirements.

- **Article 8 -- Right to Respect for Private Life:** Submarine cables carry vast quantities of private communications data. State interception of submarine cable traffic (as addressed in Big Brother Watch v. UK, Grand Chamber, 2021) engages Article 8, requiring that any interception regime be in accordance with the law and necessary in a democratic society.

### EU Legislative Framework

- **UNCLOS (United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea, 1982):** While not EU legislation, UNCLOS provides the foundational international law framework that EU and member state regulations implement:
  - **Article 79:** Coastal states' sovereign rights over the continental shelf do not prevent the laying of submarine cables, subject to coastal state consent for cable route delineation and reasonable conditions. States must not impede the laying or maintenance of cables.
  - **Article 112:** Freedom of the high seas includes the freedom to lay submarine cables.
  - **Article 113:** Requires states to make it a punishable offense for their nationals to break or injure a submarine cable willfully or through culpable negligence.
  - **Article 114:** Requires states to ensure that cable owners who, while laying or repairing their own cable, cause a break or injury to another cable, are liable for the cost of repairs.
  - **Article 115:** Requires states to ensure that vessel owners who sacrifice an anchor or fishing gear to avoid injuring a cable are compensated by the cable owner.

- **EU Critical Entities Resilience Directive (CER Directive, Directive (EU) 2022/2557):** Requires member states to identify critical entities in the digital infrastructure sector, including submarine cable operators, and to ensure these entities implement resilience measures including risk assessments, business continuity plans, and security measures. Member states must transpose by 17 October 2024.

- **NIS 2 Directive (Directive (EU) 2022/2555):** Classifies submarine cable operators as "essential entities" in the digital infrastructure sector, requiring cybersecurity risk management measures, incident reporting (within 24 hours of becoming aware of a significant incident), and supply chain security.

- **European Commission Recommendation on Submarine Cable Security and Resilience (C(2024) 1099, February 2024):** A non-binding recommendation calling on member states to: (a) conduct risk assessments of their submarine cable infrastructure; (b) ensure cable repair capacity and emergency repair procedures; (c) improve information sharing on cable threats; (d) enhance maritime surveillance of cable routes; (e) coordinate cable protection through existing EU maritime security structures.

- **Maritime Spatial Planning Directive (Directive 2014/89/EU):** Requires member states to establish maritime spatial plans that, inter alia, coordinate the routing of submarine cables with other sea uses including shipping, fishing, offshore energy, and marine protected areas.

### Key Case Law

- **Big Brother Watch v. UK (Grand Chamber, 2021, App. No. 58170/13):** The ECtHR addressed bulk interception of communications on submarine cables by GCHQ (the UK's signals intelligence agency). The Court found violations of Articles 8 and 10, holding that while bulk interception could be justified in principle, the UK's regime lacked adequate safeguards including independent authorization, category-based selectors, and independent oversight of the selection process. The decision has significant implications for lawful interception of submarine cable traffic.

- **ECtHR, Centrum för rättvisa v. Sweden (Grand Chamber, 2021, App. No. 35252/08):** Companion case to Big Brother Watch, addressing Swedish signals intelligence (FRA) bulk interception of fiber-optic cable traffic. The Court found violations of Articles 8 and 10 due to inadequate safeguards in the bulk interception regime, reinforcing protections for submarine cable communications.

- **ICJ, Maritime Delimitation Cases:** Various ICJ maritime boundary decisions have indirectly addressed submarine cable routing, as boundary delimitations affect the jurisdictional framework for cable installation and protection on continental shelves and in EEZs.
