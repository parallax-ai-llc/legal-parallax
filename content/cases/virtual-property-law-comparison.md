## Summary

Virtual property -- digital assets existing within online environments including in-game items, virtual real estate, avatars, digital currencies, non-fungible tokens (NFTs), and metaverse holdings that may possess economic value independent of the platforms on which they exist -- poses fundamental challenges to traditional property law frameworks that were designed for tangible goods and real property, raising unresolved questions about whether users hold proprietary rights or mere contractual licenses, whether theft of virtual items constitutes criminal property offenses, and how inheritance, taxation, and consumer protection principles apply to assets that exist only as database entries controlled by private platform operators. South Korea has been a global pioneer in recognizing virtual property rights, with the Supreme Court of Korea (대법원) ruling in a landmark 2010 decision (2008도5200) that online game items constitute property subject to criminal theft provisions under Articles 329 and 346 of the Criminal Act (형법); the Game Industry Promotion Act (게임산업진흥에 관한 법률) regulates game item trading, and the Virtual Asset User Protection Act (가상자산 이용자 보호 등에 관한 법률), effective July 19, 2024, establishes a comprehensive regulatory framework for cryptocurrency and digital tokens. The United States lacks a unified federal framework for virtual property, with legal treatment depending on characterization as intellectual property, contractual rights, or securities; courts have generally upheld end-user license agreements (EULAs) limiting user rights under ProCD v. Zeidenberg, 86 F.3d 1447 (7th Cir. 1996), though the IRS treats virtual currency as property for tax purposes (IRS Notice 2014-21) and the SEC has asserted jurisdiction over certain digital assets as securities under SEC v. Howey Co., 328 U.S. 293 (1946). Germany addresses virtual property through its established civil law framework, with the Bundesgerichtshof (BGH) applying contract law principles to digital content and virtual accounts, while the Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB) provisions on inheritance have been held to apply to digital accounts (BGH, Judgment of July 12, 2018, III ZR 183/17 -- Facebook Inheritance case); cryptocurrency is regulated under the Kreditwesengesetz (KWG) as a financial instrument following BaFin classification. At the EU level, the Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA, Regulation (EU) 2023/1114), which became fully applicable on December 30, 2024, establishes the world's first comprehensive regulatory framework for crypto-assets, while the Digital Content Directive 2019/770 and Consumer Rights Directive 2011/83/EU address digital content and services purchased by consumers, and the ECHR's protection of property under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 has been interpreted by scholars as potentially extending to virtual assets with established economic value.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's legal system has been among the most proactive globally in recognizing and regulating virtual property, driven by the country's status as one of the world's largest online gaming markets.

**Criminal Act (형법) -- Virtual Item Theft:** The Supreme Court of Korea established the criminal property status of virtual items in its landmark judgment of February 11, 2010 (Case No. 2008도5200). The Court held that online game items, despite being intangible digital data, constitute "property" (재물) within the meaning of Articles 329 (theft) and 346 (definition of property for purposes of theft and fraud) of the Criminal Act. The Court reasoned that game items possess independent economic value, are subject to exclusive control by the account holder, and are transferable -- characteristics sufficient to bring them within the scope of criminal property protection.

**Game Industry Promotion Act (게임산업진흥에 관한 법률):** This statute, administered by the Ministry of Culture, Sports and Tourism, regulates the gaming industry including virtual item transactions. Key provisions:

- **Article 32(1)(7):** Prohibits speculative game item trading that may be linked to gambling.
- **Article 32(1)(4):** Prohibits the exchange of game results (items, currency) for cash or goods through means not approved by the game operator, with exceptions for certain types of items in registered games.
- **Article 44:** Violations carry penalties of up to 5 years imprisonment or fines up to KRW 50 million.

**Virtual Asset User Protection Act (가상자산 이용자 보호 등에 관한 법률):** Enacted on July 18, 2023, and effective July 19, 2024, this comprehensive statute establishes the first dedicated legal framework for virtual asset regulation in South Korea. Key provisions include:

- **Article 2:** Defines "virtual assets" (가상자산) as electronically traded or transferred economic representations of value, excluding central bank digital currencies, securities, and electronic money.
- **Article 6:** Requires virtual asset service providers to segregate customer assets from proprietary assets.
- **Article 10:** Prohibits unfair trading practices including market manipulation and insider trading in virtual assets.
- **Article 17:** Establishes the Financial Services Commission (금융위원회) as the supervisory authority.
- **Article 27:** Violations of unfair trading prohibitions carry penalties of imprisonment of 1 year or more or fines of 3-5 times the unfair profit.

**Taxation:** Starting January 1, 2025 (after multiple deferrals from the originally planned 2022 implementation), gains from virtual asset transactions exceeding KRW 2.5 million annually are subject to a 20 percent capital gains tax (plus local surtax) under the Income Tax Act (소득세법) amendments.

### Digital Inheritance

Under the Civil Act (민법), the general principles of inheritance (상속) apply to all property of the deceased without explicit limitation to tangible property. Article 1005 provides that the heir succeeds to all rights and obligations of the deceased. Korean courts have not yet issued a definitive ruling on the inheritance of virtual game items or social media accounts, but legal scholars generally agree that virtual assets with economic value are inheritable under existing law.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States' approach to virtual property is characterized by fragmentation, with no unified federal framework and significant reliance on private contractual ordering through EULAs.

**EULA Supremacy:** U.S. courts have generally held that users of virtual environments hold contractual license rights rather than property rights, with the terms of the EULA governing the relationship. In Bragg v. Linden Research, Inc., 487 F. Supp. 2d 593 (E.D. Pa. 2007), the court found certain unconscionable provisions in Second Life's terms of service unenforceable but did not establish a property right in virtual land independent of the contract. In MDY Industries, LLC v. Blizzard Entertainment, Inc., 629 F.3d 928 (9th Cir. 2010), the Ninth Circuit held that the EULA granted a license rather than ownership of World of Warcraft software copies.

**Cryptocurrency Regulation:** The regulatory landscape for crypto-assets is defined by the SEC-CFTC jurisdictional divide:

- **SEC v. W.J. Howey Co., 328 U.S. 293 (1946):** The Howey test determines whether a digital asset constitutes an "investment contract" (and therefore a security): an investment of money in a common enterprise with an expectation of profits derived from the efforts of others.
- **SEC v. Ripple Labs, Inc. (S.D.N.Y. 2023):** Judge Analisa Torres held that programmatic sales of XRP tokens on exchanges did not constitute securities transactions, while institutional sales did, creating a contextual framework for digital asset classification.
- **SEC v. Coinbase, Inc. (2nd Cir. 2024):** Ongoing litigation addressing whether crypto exchange listings constitute securities offerings.

**IRS Tax Treatment:** IRS Notice 2014-21 established that virtual currency is treated as property for federal tax purposes. Each transaction (sale, exchange, or use to purchase goods/services) is a taxable event potentially generating capital gain or loss. The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act of 2021 expanded reporting requirements for digital asset brokers (effective 2026).

**Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act (RUFADAA, 2015):** Adopted by the Uniform Law Commission and enacted in 49 states as of 2025, RUFADAA provides a legal framework for fiduciary access to digital assets. The Act establishes a three-tier priority system: (1) the user's directions in an online tool provided by the service, (2) the user's estate planning documents, (3) the service's terms of service. RUFADAA covers virtual property including email, social media accounts, and digital assets.

### Criminal Treatment

U.S. federal law does not specifically criminalize theft of virtual items. The Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (18 U.S.C. Section 1030) may apply to unauthorized access to computer systems to steal virtual items, but addresses the means of access rather than the property nature of the items. State courts have reached varying results: in People v. Ler (2012), a California court allowed prosecution for theft of virtual poker chips.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany addresses virtual property through its sophisticated civil law framework, with particularly influential jurisprudence on digital inheritance.

**BGH Facebook Inheritance Case (Judgment of July 12, 2018, III ZR 183/17):** The Bundesgerichtshof held that the heirs of a deceased minor were entitled to full access to her Facebook account including private messages. The Court reasoned that the contractual relationship between the user and the platform passes to the heirs under Section 1922(1) BGB (universal succession), which provides that upon death all property rights and obligations of the deceased devolve upon the heir. The Court rejected arguments that data protection or telecommunications secrecy precluded access, finding that the heir steps into the deceased's contractual position.

**Contractual Framework under BGB:** Virtual items and digital content are generally treated as contractual rights under the BGB rather than as property rights (Eigentum) under Section 903 BGB, which requires a tangible thing (Sache). Section 90 BGB defines Sachen as corporeal objects, excluding data and virtual assets from the traditional property law framework. However, Section 453(1) BGB on sale of rights extends the law of sales to transactions involving rights and "other objects" (sonstige Gegenstände), which may encompass virtual items.

**Umsetzungsgesetz der Richtlinie über digitale Inhalte (2021):** Germany transposed the EU Digital Content Directive 2019/770 through amendments to the BGB (Sections 327 et seq.), establishing that contracts for digital content and digital services -- including in-game items and virtual goods -- are subject to conformity requirements, remedies for defective performance, and restrictions on unilateral modification by the trader.

**Crypto-Asset Regulation under KWG:** The Bundesanstalt für Finanzdienstleistungsaufsicht (BaFin) classified crypto-assets as financial instruments (Finanzinstrumente) under a 2020 amendment to the Kreditwesengesetz (KWG), specifically under Section 1(11) sentence 1 No. 10 KWG (Kryptowerte). Entities providing crypto-asset services require a BaFin license. This regulatory approach predated MiCA and influenced the EU's framework.

**Taxation:** Under Section 23(1) No. 2 EStG, gains from the sale of crypto-assets held for less than one year are subject to income tax if they exceed EUR 600. Crypto-assets held for more than one year are tax-exempt on sale, creating a significant holding incentive. The tax treatment applies to all "private disposals" of virtual assets.

### Criminal Law

The criminal law treatment of virtual item theft in Germany remains doctrinally debated. Section 242 StGB (theft) requires the taking of a "movable thing" (fremde bewegliche Sache), which under the prevailing interpretation requires a corporeal object. Data theft is addressed separately under Section 202a StGB (data espionage) and Section 303a StGB (data alteration), which protect data integrity rather than economic value. Legal scholars have advocated for reform to extend theft or fraud provisions to virtual property.

## ECHR & EU

### Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA) -- Regulation (EU) 2023/1114

MiCA, adopted on May 31, 2023, and fully applicable from December 30, 2024, establishes the world's first comprehensive regulatory framework for crypto-assets. Key provisions:

- **Article 3(1)(5):** Defines "crypto-asset" as "a digital representation of a value or of a right that is able to be transferred and stored electronically, using distributed ledger technology or similar technology."
- **Title II (Articles 4-15):** Requirements for crypto-assets other than asset-referenced tokens or e-money tokens, including white paper requirements.
- **Title III (Articles 16-47):** Asset-referenced tokens (stablecoins) regime, including reserve requirements and redemption rights.
- **Title V (Articles 59-83):** Authorization and supervision of crypto-asset service providers (CASPs), including requirements for segregation of client assets, governance, and consumer protection.
- **Article 4(3):** NFTs that are "unique and not fungible with other crypto-assets" are excluded from the scope of MiCA, though the Commission may adopt delegated acts to specify criteria.

### Digital Content Directive 2019/770

This Directive, which member states were required to transpose by July 1, 2021, establishes rights for consumers who purchase or receive digital content or digital services. The Directive applies to in-game items, virtual goods, and digital content subscriptions:

- **Article 7:** Conformity requirements: digital content must be of the quality and possess the features that are normal and that the consumer may reasonably expect.
- **Article 14:** The trader may not modify digital content in a way that goes beyond what is necessary to maintain conformity.
- **Article 16:** Termination rights: upon termination, the trader must refrain from using consumer-generated content except personal data.

### DAC8 Directive -- Crypto-Asset Tax Reporting

Directive (EU) 2024/2967 (DAC8), adopted on October 17, 2023, amends the Directive on Administrative Cooperation to extend mandatory automatic exchange of information to crypto-asset transactions. CASPs will be required to report transaction information to tax authorities, effective January 1, 2026.

### ECHR -- Article 1 Protocol 1

Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 to the ECHR protects the right to peaceful enjoyment of possessions. The Court has given an autonomous meaning to "possessions" that extends beyond physical property to include economic interests and legitimate expectations. In Anheuser-Busch Inc. v. Portugal (Application No. 73049/01, Grand Chamber Judgment of January 11, 2007), the Court held that intellectual property rights, including pending applications, constitute "possessions." Legal scholars argue by analogy that virtual assets with established economic value and recognized legal status (such as crypto-assets or virtual items recognized as property under national law) may constitute "possessions" protected under Protocol 1.

### European Court of Justice -- Usedsoft and Digital Exhaustion

In UsedSoft GmbH v. Oracle International Corp. (Case C-128/11, CJEU Judgment of July 3, 2012), the CJEU held that the principle of exhaustion applies to software downloaded online, meaning that a purchaser may resell a lawfully acquired software license. However, in Tom Kabinet (Case C-263/18, 2019), the Court held that exhaustion does not apply to e-books, limiting the UsedSoft principle. The extent to which digital exhaustion applies to virtual items remains unresolved.

## Comparative Analysis

### Property vs. License Paradigm

South Korea stands alone among the four jurisdictions in clearly recognizing virtual items as criminal property. Germany and the United States generally treat virtual items as contractual licenses, though Germany's inheritance jurisprudence recognizes certain proprietary aspects. The EU framework focuses on consumer protection and market regulation rather than property classification.

### Crypto-Asset Governance

The EU's MiCA regulation represents the most comprehensive approach, establishing uniform rules across 27 member states. South Korea's Virtual Asset User Protection Act provides robust domestic regulation. The United States' fragmented SEC/CFTC approach creates significant regulatory uncertainty. Germany's early BaFin classification preceded and influenced both the EU and Korean frameworks.

### Digital Inheritance

Germany leads through the BGH Facebook inheritance case, establishing that digital accounts are universally inheritable. The United States has achieved broad adoption through RUFADAA but with varying state implementation. South Korea and the EU lack specific digital inheritance frameworks.

### Taxation Convergence

All four jurisdictions tax crypto-asset gains, though the mechanisms differ. Germany's one-year holding exemption is unique. The EU's DAC8 directive will harmonize reporting requirements. South Korea's implementation was repeatedly deferred, reflecting political sensitivity around crypto taxation.
