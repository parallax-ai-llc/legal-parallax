## Summary

Quarantine powers -- the legal authority of governments to restrict the movement and liberty of individuals to prevent the spread of communicable diseases -- represent one of the most significant intersections of public health necessity and individual rights protection in any legal system. The COVID-19 pandemic that began in 2019 dramatically tested and reshaped quarantine legal frameworks worldwide, exposing gaps, triggering emergency legislation, and generating landmark judicial decisions on the constitutionality of movement restrictions. South Korea's response was governed by the Infectious Disease Control and Prevention Act (감염병의 예방 및 관리에 관한 법률), originally enacted in 2009 and amended extensively during 2020-2022, which empowered the Korea Disease Control and Prevention Agency (질병관리청, KDCA) to impose mandatory quarantine, contact tracing through mobile phone data, and electronic wristband monitoring of quarantine compliance, generating international debate about the balance between effective disease control and privacy rights. The United States relies on a dual federal-state framework: federal quarantine authority derives from the Commerce Clause and is implemented through 42 U.S.C. Sections 264-272 and regulations at 42 C.F.R. Parts 70-71, administered by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), while states exercise their police powers under the Tenth Amendment, with most states having enacted updated communicable disease laws following the Model State Emergency Health Powers Act (MSEHPA) proposed by Georgetown and Johns Hopkins scholars in 2001. Germany's quarantine powers are codified in the Infektionsschutzgesetz (Protection Against Infection Act, IfSG) of 2000, substantially amended by the successive COVID-19 Protection Acts (Bevölkerungsschutzgesetze) of 2020-2022, with the Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) delivering major rulings on the proportionality of lockdown measures in its "Federal Emergency Brake" decision of November 2021. At the European level, the ECHR framework requires that quarantine measures satisfy the strict requirements of Article 5(1)(e) (lawful detention to prevent spread of infectious diseases) and the ECtHR has developed important jurisprudence on proportionality, while the EU adopted Regulation 2021/953 establishing the EU Digital COVID Certificate as a framework for cross-border movement during the pandemic.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's quarantine authority rests primarily on the Infectious Disease Control and Prevention Act (감염병의 예방 및 관리에 관한 법률), initially enacted as a comprehensive reform in 2009 replacing the earlier Communicable Disease Prevention Act. The Act underwent rapid and extensive amendment during the COVID-19 pandemic, with major revisions in February 2020, August 2020, March 2021, and December 2022.

The Korea Disease Control and Prevention Agency (질병관리청, KDCA), elevated to an independent agency under the Government Organization Act in September 2020, serves as the primary implementing authority. Previously a subordinate organization under the Ministry of Health and Welfare (보건복지부), the KDCA's elevation reflected lessons learned during the MERS outbreak of 2015 about the need for independent disease control authority.

### Quarantine Measures and Powers

Article 41 of the Act empowers the KDCA Director to order mandatory hospitalization or quarantine of persons confirmed or suspected of having designated infectious diseases. Article 42 authorizes epidemiological investigation officers to conduct contact tracing and to require cooperation from telecommunications providers, credit card companies, and CCTV operators to reconstruct the movements of infected individuals.

**Electronic Monitoring:** During the COVID-19 pandemic, South Korea implemented mandatory self-quarantine monitoring through mobile applications. Individuals subject to quarantine orders were required to install the "자가격리 안전보호" (Self-Quarantine Safety Protection) app, which used GPS tracking to verify compliance. In 2020, the government introduced electronic wristbands for quarantine violators, drawing comparisons to criminal monitoring technology.

### MERS Response and Lessons

The 2015 Middle East Respiratory Syndrome (MERS) outbreak, which resulted in 186 confirmed cases and 38 deaths in South Korea, exposed critical weaknesses in the quarantine infrastructure and prompted the legislative reforms that proved instrumental during COVID-19. The MERS response was hampered by delayed disclosure of affected hospital names, inadequate contact tracing, and insufficient legal authority for movement restrictions. Post-MERS amendments in 2015 and 2016 expanded the government's powers to collect personal data for epidemiological investigation and required healthcare facilities to implement infection control measures.

### Privacy Concerns

South Korea's aggressive contact tracing and public disclosure of infected individuals' movement histories generated significant domestic and international privacy concerns. The government published detailed movement logs (동선공개) including timestamps and locations visited by confirmed cases, often enabling de facto identification of individuals. Following criticism from the National Human Rights Commission of Korea (국가인권위원회) and privacy advocates, the government revised its disclosure guidelines in 2020 to limit the specificity of published information and the time window of disclosed movements to 24 hours before symptom onset.

### Penalties

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| Violation of quarantine order (Art. 79-3) | Up to 1 year imprisonment or 10 million KRW fine |
| Refusal of epidemiological investigation (Art. 79-2) | Up to 2 years imprisonment or 20 million KRW fine |
| False reporting during epidemiological investigation | Up to 2 years imprisonment or 20 million KRW fine |
| Healthcare facility failure to report infectious disease | Up to 5 million KRW fine |

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States quarantine framework operates through a dual federal-state system rooted in constitutional federalism. Federal quarantine authority derives from the Commerce Clause (Article I, Section 8) and is codified in the Public Health Service Act, 42 U.S.C. Sections 264-272. The implementing regulations at 42 C.F.R. Parts 70 (interstate quarantine) and 71 (foreign quarantine) authorize the CDC Director to take measures to prevent the introduction, transmission, or spread of communicable diseases from foreign countries into the United States or between states.

**Executive Orders Specifying Quarantinable Diseases:** The President specifies which communicable diseases are subject to federal quarantine authority through executive order. Executive Order 13295 (April 4, 2003), as amended by Executive Orders 13375 (April 1, 2005) and 13674 (July 31, 2014), lists diseases including cholera, diphtheria, infectious tuberculosis, plague, smallpox, yellow fever, viral hemorrhagic fevers, SARS, and influenza caused by novel or re-emergent strains with pandemic potential.

**State Police Powers:** The Tenth Amendment reserves to the states the primary responsibility for public health, including quarantine within state borders. Most states have enacted communicable disease control statutes, many influenced by the Model State Emergency Health Powers Act (MSEHPA), drafted by Professor Lawrence Gostin at Georgetown University's O'Neill Institute in cooperation with Johns Hopkins Center for Health Security and published in December 2001.

### COVID-19 Legal Controversies

The COVID-19 pandemic generated extensive litigation over quarantine and movement restriction powers at both federal and state levels.

**CDC Eviction Moratorium -- Alabama Association of Realtors v. HHS (2021):** The Supreme Court, in an unsigned opinion on August 26, 2021, vacated the CDC's nationwide eviction moratorium, holding that the agency's interpretation of 42 U.S.C. Section 264 to authorize a nationwide eviction ban exceeded its statutory authority. The Court observed that the statute authorizes measures like "fumigation" and "pest extermination" and does not grant "a breathtaking amount of authority."

**Biden v. Missouri (2022):** The Supreme Court upheld CMS's authority to require COVID-19 vaccination for healthcare workers at Medicare and Medicaid-certified facilities, finding it a valid exercise of the Secretary's authority under the Social Security Act to impose conditions of participation.

**State-Level Challenges:** Numerous state court decisions addressed the legality of gubernatorial emergency orders imposing lockdowns, curfews, and gathering restrictions. In Wisconsin Legislature v. Palm (2020), the Wisconsin Supreme Court struck down the state's "Safer at Home" order, holding that the state Health Secretary exceeded her statutory authority by issuing the order without following rulemaking procedures.

### Penalties

| Violation | Federal Penalty |
|-----------|----------------|
| Violation of federal quarantine order (42 U.S.C. § 271) | Up to $250,000 fine and 1 year imprisonment |
| Violation of communicable disease regulations (42 C.F.R.) | Civil monetary penalties up to $100,000 per violation per day |
| False health declaration at port of entry | Federal criminal prosecution |

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's quarantine authority is codified in the Infektionsschutzgesetz (Protection Against Infection Act, IfSG), enacted on July 20, 2000, and substantially amended during the COVID-19 pandemic through a series of laws: the First through Fourth Bevölkerungsschutzgesetz (Population Protection Acts) of 2020-2021 and the COVID-19-Schutzgesetz of 2022.

**Sections 28-31 IfSG:** These provisions form the core of Germany's quarantine powers. Section 28 authorizes the competent authority (zuständige Behörde) to take "necessary protective measures" (notwendige Schutzmaßnahmen) to prevent the spread of communicable diseases, including restricting or prohibiting events, closing institutions, and ordering quarantine (Absonderung). Section 29 permits observation of individuals suspected of infection. Section 30 provides for mandatory segregation (Absonderung) of individuals with confirmed communicable diseases in suitable hospitals or other facilities. Section 31 permits occupational bans for individuals who may transmit infectious diseases.

**Section 28a IfSG (Inserted November 2020):** The Third Bevölkerungsschutzgesetz added Section 28a, which codified specific types of protective measures that could be ordered during an epidemic of national significance (epidemische Lage von nationaler Tragweite), including contact restrictions, closure of businesses and educational institutions, mandatory mask-wearing, curfews, and travel restrictions. This provision was added to provide a more specific legal basis for COVID-19 restrictions after legal challenges argued that the general clause in Section 28 was insufficiently determinate.

**Section 28b IfSG -- "Federal Emergency Brake" (Bundesnotbremse):** The Fourth Bevölkerungsschutzgesetz (April 22, 2021) inserted Section 28b, which established automatic nationwide restrictions triggered by incidence rates exceeding specified thresholds in any county (Landkreis). Measures included nighttime curfews (10 PM to 5 AM) in counties with seven-day incidence rates exceeding 100 per 100,000.

### Bundesverfassungsgericht Decision -- "Federal Emergency Brake" (November 30, 2021)

The Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht, BVerfG) issued a landmark ruling on November 30, 2021 (1 BvR 781/21 and others), upholding the constitutionality of the "Federal Emergency Brake" measures, including the nighttime curfew. The Court held that the curfew interfered with fundamental rights under Article 2(2) GG (freedom of the person), Article 6 GG (protection of marriage and family), and Article 8 GG (freedom of assembly) but was justified as a proportionate response to protect life and health under Article 2(2) sentence 1 GG. The Court emphasized the legislature's broad margin of appreciation in assessing uncertain scientific evidence during a pandemic.

### Corona-Warn-App

Germany's digital contact tracing approach contrasted sharply with South Korea's. The Corona-Warn-App, developed by SAP and Deutsche Telekom and launched on June 16, 2020, used a decentralized, Bluetooth-based protocol (Exposure Notification framework developed by Apple and Google) that did not collect GPS location data or personal identifiers. This privacy-preserving design reflected both the GDPR framework and Germany's heightened sensitivity to surveillance, rooted in historical experiences with the Stasi and Nazi-era surveillance. The app was downloaded over 48 million times but was discontinued on June 1, 2023.

### Penalties

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| Violation of quarantine order (IfSG § 73(1a)) | Administrative fine up to 25,000 EUR |
| Intentional violation causing disease spread (IfSG § 74) | Up to 5 years imprisonment |
| Negligent violation causing disease spread (IfSG § 74) | Up to 1 year imprisonment or fine |
| Failure to report infectious disease (IfSG § 73) | Administrative fine up to 25,000 EUR |

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Article 5(1)(e) -- Detention of Infectious Persons

Article 5(1)(e) of the European Convention on Human Rights explicitly permits "the lawful detention of persons for the prevention of the spreading of infectious diseases." This provision predates the modern era of pandemic response but has been the subject of important ECtHR jurisprudence.

**Enhorn v. Sweden (Application No. 56529/00, Judgment of January 25, 2005):** The landmark case involved an HIV-positive man involuntarily detained under Swedish communicable disease legislation for nearly seven years. The Court found a violation of Article 5(1), establishing that detention under Article 5(1)(e) requires that: (1) the spreading of the infectious disease is dangerous to public health or safety; (2) detention of the infected person is the last resort to prevent spreading; and (3) less severe measures have been considered and found insufficient. The detention must also be subject to periodic review.

**Vavřička and Others v. Czech Republic (Application No. 47621/13, Grand Chamber Judgment of April 8, 2021):** While concerning mandatory childhood vaccination rather than quarantine, the Grand Chamber's decision affirmed states' wide margin of appreciation in public health matters, holding that compulsory vaccination policies could be "necessary in a democratic society" for the protection of health under Article 8(2).

### COVID-19 Related Applications

The ECtHR received numerous applications challenging COVID-19 quarantine and lockdown measures. In Communauté genevoise d'action syndicale (CGAS) v. Switzerland (Application No. 21881/20, Grand Chamber Judgment of November 15, 2022), the Grand Chamber held, by eleven votes to six, that Article 11 (freedom of assembly and association) was applicable to the Swiss ban on public gatherings during the pandemic but found no violation, accepting the government's justification on public health grounds.

### EU Digital COVID Certificate

Regulation (EU) 2021/953 of June 14, 2021, established the EU Digital COVID Certificate (EUDCC) framework, providing a standardized, interoperable system for verifying vaccination, recovery, and testing status for cross-border travel within the EU. The regulation entered into force on July 1, 2021, and expired on June 30, 2023. During its operation, over 2.3 billion certificates were issued across the EU/EEA. The WHO subsequently adopted the EU DCC technical framework as the basis for the Global Digital Health Certification Network.

### EU Health Security Framework

Decision 1082/2013/EU on serious cross-border threats to health, replaced by Regulation (EU) 2022/2371 on November 23, 2022, establishes the EU framework for coordinated response to cross-border health threats, including provisions for coordinated quarantine measures and information sharing between member states. The European Centre for Disease Prevention and Control (ECDC), established by Regulation (EC) No 851/2004, plays a central role in risk assessment and coordination.

## Comparative Analysis

### Centralization vs. Federalism

The jurisdictions exhibit strikingly different degrees of centralization in quarantine authority. South Korea operates a highly centralized system with KDCA as the primary authority. Germany federalizes quarantine powers to the Länder but introduced the "Federal Emergency Brake" as a centralized override mechanism during the pandemic. The United States maintains the most decentralized approach, with primary quarantine authority residing in states and federal authority limited to interstate and international contexts.

### Privacy and Surveillance

The most significant divergence concerns the use of surveillance technology for quarantine enforcement. South Korea's extensive use of GPS tracking, credit card records, CCTV footage, and electronic wristbands represents one extreme. Germany's privacy-preserving approach, using voluntary Bluetooth-based contact tracing with no personal data collection, represents the other. The United States fell between these poles, with most contact tracing conducted manually and limited deployment of digital tools.

### Judicial Review

All jurisdictions provide some form of judicial review of quarantine orders, but the mechanisms differ substantially. Germany requires immediate judicial review under Article 104 GG for any deprivation of liberty. The United States provides habeas corpus relief and due process protections. South Korea permits administrative court challenges. The ECHR requires that detention under Article 5(1)(e) be proportionate and subject to periodic review.

### Post-Pandemic Reforms

All jurisdictions have engaged in post-pandemic legislative review, with debates centering on whether emergency powers exercised during COVID-19 should be normalized into permanent law or allowed to expire. South Korea has maintained its expanded surveillance capabilities. Germany allowed most COVID-specific provisions to expire. The United States saw several states enact laws limiting gubernatorial emergency powers.
