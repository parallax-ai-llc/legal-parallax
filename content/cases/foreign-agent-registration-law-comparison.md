## Summary

Foreign agent registration laws require individuals and entities acting on behalf of foreign governments, political parties, or principals to disclose their activities, thereby enabling democratic oversight of foreign influence on domestic political processes. South Korea regulates foreign agency primarily through the Political Funds Act (정치자금법) and the National Security Act (국가보안법), the latter of which criminalizes activities conducted on behalf of anti-state organizations (primarily North Korea) under Articles 3 through 10; while South Korea lacks a dedicated foreign agent registration statute comparable to the U.S. model, the 2023 amendments to the Act on the Promotion of Information and Communications Network Utilization and Information Protection introduced transparency requirements for foreign-funded online political content. The United States operates the most comprehensive foreign agent registration regime through the Foreign Agents Registration Act of 1938 (FARA, 22 U.S.C. Sections 611-621), which requires agents of foreign principals to register with the Department of Justice, file detailed activity reports, and label informational materials; FARA enforcement experienced a dramatic resurgence following the 2016 election interference investigations, with the number of active registrants more than doubling between 2016 and 2023. Germany adopted the Foreign Agent Transparency Act (Gesetz zur Transparenz der Interessenvertretung im Auftrag ausländischer Regierungen) in 2024, establishing a register for representatives of foreign state interests maintained by the Federal Ministry of Justice; previously, Germany relied on the scattered provisions of the Lobbying Register Act (Lobbyregistergesetz, 2022), the Political Parties Act (Parteiengesetz), and the German Criminal Code Section 99 StGB (agent activity for a foreign power). At the European level, the European Commission proposed a Defence of Democracy Package in December 2023, including a directive on transparency of interest representation on behalf of third countries, which would require registration across all EU member states; the European Court of Human Rights has addressed foreign agent laws in landmark cases including Ecodefence v. Russia (2022), where the Grand Chamber found that Russia's 2012 Foreign Agents Law violated Article 11 (freedom of association) of the Convention due to its disproportionate restrictions, stigmatizing labeling requirements, and chilling effect on civil society.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea does not maintain a comprehensive foreign agent registration system analogous to the U.S. FARA. Instead, regulation of foreign influence operates through multiple overlapping statutes, with the National Security Act (국가보안법) serving as the most prominent -- and most controversial -- instrument.

**National Security Act (국가보안법, 1948):** Originally enacted during the Korean War period, the NSA criminalizes activities conducted on behalf of or in concert with "anti-state organizations" (반국가단체), which in practice refers primarily to North Korea and its affiliated entities. Key provisions include:

| Article | Offense | Maximum Penalty |
|---------|---------|----------------|
| Article 3 | Forming or joining an anti-state organization | Death, life imprisonment, or minimum 5 years |
| Article 4 | Collaborating with anti-state organization on objectives | Death, life imprisonment, or minimum 5 years |
| Article 5 | Infiltration and escape | Death, life imprisonment, or minimum 5 years |
| Article 6 | Providing classified information to anti-state organization | Death or minimum 7 years imprisonment |
| Article 7 | Praising, encouraging, or propagating activities of anti-state organization | Up to 7 years imprisonment |
| Article 8 | Meeting or communicating with members of anti-state organization | Up to 10 years imprisonment |
| Article 10 | Failure to report knowledge of NSA offenses | Up to 5 years imprisonment |

The Constitutional Court of Korea has upheld the NSA's constitutionality in multiple decisions, most notably in Decision 89Hun-Ka113 (1990) and Decision 2011Hun-Ba69 (2015), while the UN Human Rights Committee has repeatedly recommended its repeal or substantial amendment, most recently in its 2023 concluding observations (CCPR/C/KOR/CO/5).

**Political Funds Act (정치자금법):** Article 31 prohibits political parties, candidates, and political fund associations from receiving contributions from foreign nationals or foreign entities. Violations carry penalties of up to five years imprisonment or fines up to 10 million KRW. This provision prevents direct foreign funding of domestic politics but does not require registration of individuals engaged in advocacy or lobbying on behalf of foreign interests.

**2023 Online Content Transparency Amendments:** In response to growing concerns about foreign information operations, the 2023 amendments to the Act on the Promotion of Information and Communications Network Utilization and Information Protection (정보통신망 이용촉진 및 정보보호 등에 관한 법률) require online platforms to identify and label content funded by foreign governments or foreign political entities, particularly during election periods. The Korea Communications Commission (방송통신위원회) oversees enforcement.

### Debate on Dedicated Legislation

Following the exposure of foreign influence operations targeting the 2022 presidential election, multiple bills proposing a comprehensive Foreign Agent Registration Act (외국대리인등록법) were introduced in the National Assembly. Proponents cite the need for transparency regarding lobbying by foreign governments and state-owned enterprises, particularly in the context of Korea's geopolitical position between the United States and China. Opponents raise concerns that such legislation could suppress legitimate academic and cultural exchange, disproportionately affect ethnic Korean diaspora communities, and replicate the authoritarian potential of Russia's foreign agents law.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The Foreign Agents Registration Act of 1938 (FARA, 22 U.S.C. Sections 611-621) is the world's oldest and most comprehensive foreign agent registration statute.

**Historical Origin:** FARA was enacted in response to the extensive propaganda activities of Nazi Germany and Fascist Italy in the United States during the 1930s. The original statute focused on agents engaged in political propaganda; the 1966 amendments shifted the focus to agents engaged in political activities on behalf of foreign principals, broadening the scope while narrowing certain exemptions.

**Key Definitions:**

| Term | FARA Definition |
|------|----------------|
| **Foreign principal** | A government of a foreign country; a foreign political party; a person outside the United States (unless a U.S. citizen); or a partnership, association, corporation, or other organization organized under the laws of or having its principal place of business in a foreign country |
| **Agent of a foreign principal** | Any person who acts at the order, request, or under the direction or control of a foreign principal; or whose activities are directly or indirectly supervised, directed, controlled, financed, or subsidized in whole or in major part by a foreign principal |
| **Political activities** | Any activity that the agent believes will, or that the agent intends to, prevail upon, influence, or persuade any agency or official of the U.S. Government or the public on matters of policy |

**Registration and Reporting Requirements:** Agents must file a registration statement within ten days of becoming obligated to register, supplemental statements every six months, and short-form registrations for additional agents within the same agency. Registered agents must label all "informational materials" disseminated in the United States with a conspicuous disclosure that the material is distributed by or on behalf of a foreign principal.

### Enforcement Revival Post-2016

FARA enforcement was historically minimal, with the Department of Justice preferring informal compliance letters over criminal prosecution. Between 1966 and 2015, only seven criminal FARA prosecutions were brought. The 2016 election interference investigations dramatically changed this landscape:

| Year | Active Registrants | New Registrations | Criminal Prosecutions |
|------|-------------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| 2016 | ~400 | ~50 | 0 |
| 2017 | ~600 | ~150 | 2 (Paul Manafort, Rick Gates) |
| 2018 | ~650 | ~100 | 1 (Maria Butina -- related NSD charge) |
| 2019 | ~500 | ~80 | 1 |
| 2020 | ~530 | ~70 | 2 |
| 2023 | ~900 | ~120 | 3 |

**United States v. Manafort (E.D. Va. 2018):** Paul Manafort was convicted of, among other charges, failing to register under FARA for his lobbying activities on behalf of the Ukrainian government and pro-Russian political parties. Manafort was sentenced to 7.5 years imprisonment (later commuted).

**United States v. Barrack (S.D.N.Y. 2022):** Thomas Barrack, former inaugural committee chairman, was acquitted of charges that he acted as an unregistered agent of the United Arab Emirates. The acquittal highlighted the evidentiary challenges of proving agency relationships and foreign direction.

### Relationship with Lobbying Disclosure Act

The Lobbying Disclosure Act of 1995 (LDA, 2 U.S.C. Sections 1601-1614) provides a broader but less stringent registration framework for all lobbyists, including those representing foreign commercial interests. The LDA exemption from FARA (22 U.S.C. Section 613(h)) allows agents of foreign principals whose activities are limited to lobbying to register under the LDA rather than FARA, provided they are not agents of foreign governments or foreign political parties. This exemption has been criticized for creating a loophole that permits foreign-funded advocacy to avoid FARA's more rigorous disclosure and labeling requirements.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's regulation of foreign agent activity has undergone significant transformation, culminating in the 2024 Foreign Agent Transparency Act.

**Section 99 StGB -- Agent Activity for a Foreign Power (Geheimdienstliche Agententätigkeit):** This criminal provision, part of the German Criminal Code's chapter on offenses against the state, criminalizes engaging in intelligence activity for a foreign power or one of its intermediaries directed against the Federal Republic of Germany. The offense carries a penalty of imprisonment up to five years or a fine. Section 99 focuses on covert intelligence gathering rather than open lobbying or advocacy.

**Lobbying Register Act (Lobbyregistergesetz, 2022):** Effective January 1, 2022, this act established a mandatory public register of interest representatives seeking to influence the Bundestag and the Federal Government. While not specifically targeting foreign agents, the register captures organizations acting on behalf of foreign interests if they engage in lobbying activities directed at federal institutions. Registrants must disclose their clients, fields of interest, and financial resources.

**Foreign Agent Transparency Act (Gesetz zur Transparenz der Interessenvertretung im Auftrag ausländischer Regierungen, 2024):** Adopted following the European Commission's Defence of Democracy Package proposal, this act establishes a dedicated register for individuals and entities conducting interest representation on behalf of foreign governments. Key features include:

- **Scope:** Covers any natural or legal person who, on behalf of a government of a non-EU third country, undertakes activities aimed at influencing decision-making processes of the Federal Government, the Bundestag, or the Bundesrat
- **Registration:** Mandatory registration with the Federal Ministry of Justice within 14 days of commencing representational activities
- **Disclosure:** Registrants must disclose the identity of the foreign government principal, the nature and scope of activities, and financial arrangements
- **Exemptions:** Diplomatic and consular activities governed by the Vienna Conventions; legal representation in judicial or administrative proceedings; journalistic activities; purely academic research
- **Penalties:** Administrative fines up to 500,000 euros for failure to register or provide false information; criminal liability under Section 99 StGB for covert intelligence activities

### Political Party Foreign Funding

The Political Parties Act (Parteiengesetz, PartG) Section 25(2) prohibits German political parties from accepting donations from foreign sources exceeding 1,000 euros, with exceptions for donations from EU citizens, German nationals living abroad, and foreign enterprises with significant German ownership stakes. The Bundestag administration publishes all donations exceeding 50,000 euros promptly and donations exceeding 10,000 euros annually. Violations can result in penalties of up to three times the illegally accepted donation amount.

### Notable Cases

**BfV Intelligence Reports:** The Federal Office for the Protection of the Constitution (Bundesamt für Verfassungsschutz, BfV) regularly reports on foreign influence activities in Germany. The 2023 annual report identified significant influence operations by Russia, China, Turkey, and Iran, including the use of proxy organizations, media outlets, and cultural associations. These findings supported the legislative push for the 2024 Transparency Act.

## ECHR / EU

### Ecodefence v. Russia (Grand Chamber, 2022)

The landmark Grand Chamber judgment in Ecodefence and Others v. Russia (Application No. 9988/13, Judgment of June 14, 2022) addressed Russia's 2012 Law on Non-Commercial Organizations Performing the Functions of a Foreign Agent (commonly known as the "Foreign Agents Law").

**Background:** The Russian law required non-commercial organizations that received foreign funding and engaged in broadly defined "political activity" to register as "foreign agents" -- a term carrying strong pejorative connotations from the Soviet era. Organizations designated as foreign agents were subject to enhanced reporting requirements, mandatory labeling of all published materials, and regular audits. Non-compliance carried significant financial penalties and potential forced dissolution.

**Court's Findings:**
- **Article 11 Violation (Freedom of Association):** The Court found unanimously that the Russian law violated Article 11, both alone and in conjunction with Article 10 (freedom of expression). The "foreign agent" designation constituted an interference with the right to freedom of association.
- **Lack of Legitimate Aim:** While the Court acknowledged that transparency of NGO funding could serve a legitimate aim, the specific design of the Russian law went far beyond what was necessary, pursuing stigmatization rather than genuine transparency.
- **Disproportionality:** The Court identified multiple disproportionate elements: the pejorative "foreign agent" label, the breadth of the definition of "political activity" (which encompassed virtually all civic engagement), the burdensome reporting obligations, and the severity of sanctions including forced dissolution.
- **Chilling Effect:** The Court found that the law created a significant chilling effect on civil society, deterring organizations from receiving legitimate foreign funding for human rights, environmental, and social welfare activities.

**Significance:** The judgment established that while states have a legitimate interest in ensuring transparency of foreign funding for politically active organizations, foreign agent registration regimes must be proportionate, non-stigmatizing, and narrowly tailored to genuine national security or democratic transparency concerns.

### EU Defence of Democracy Package (2023)

In December 2023, the European Commission proposed the Defence of Democracy Package, including a proposed Directive on transparency of interest representation on behalf of third countries.

**Key Elements of the Proposed Directive:**
- **Scope:** Entities engaged in interest representation activities on behalf of third-country (non-EU) governments, including lobbying, public communications campaigns, and grassroots organizing
- **Registration:** Mandatory registration in a national register in the member state where the entity is established or, for non-EU entities, where they primarily conduct activities
- **Harmonized Standards:** The directive would establish common minimum transparency standards across all member states while allowing national registers to impose additional requirements
- **Safeguards:** Explicitly prohibits the use of stigmatizing terminology (no "foreign agent" label); registration cannot be used as a basis for restricting activities; judicial review must be available for registration decisions
- **Penalties:** Member states must establish effective, proportionate, and dissuasive penalties for non-compliance

**Legislative Progress:** The proposed directive has been subject to extensive debate in the European Parliament and Council, with civil society organizations expressing concerns that even a non-stigmatizing registration regime could be used to target human rights organizations, and member states differing on the appropriate scope and exemptions. As of early 2026, trilogue negotiations between the Parliament, Council, and Commission continue.

### Council of Europe Recommendations

The Council of Europe's Group of States against Corruption (GRECO) has addressed foreign influence on political processes in its Fifth Evaluation Round, recommending that member states establish clear rules on foreign donations to political parties and require transparency for lobbying activities conducted on behalf of foreign interests.

## Comparative Analysis

### Scope and Targeting

The jurisdictions differ fundamentally in their approach to defining covered "foreign agency." The U.S. FARA is the broadest, covering agents of any foreign principal regardless of whether the principal is a government, political party, or private entity. Germany's 2024 act focuses specifically on representation of foreign governments, excluding purely private foreign interests. South Korea's National Security Act is the narrowest in scope but most severe in punishment, targeting activities linked to North Korea rather than foreign influence generally. The proposed EU directive occupies a middle ground, covering representation of third-country governments while explicitly incorporating proportionality safeguards informed by the Ecodefence judgment.

### Labeling and Stigmatization

The tension between transparency and stigmatization is central to the comparative analysis. The U.S. FARA requires labeling of informational materials but uses the relatively neutral term "registered agent." Russia's foreign agents law -- found to violate the ECHR -- used the deliberately pejorative term "foreign agent" (иностранный агент). Germany's 2024 act and the proposed EU directive explicitly avoid stigmatizing terminology. South Korea's National Security Act uses the highly charged language of "anti-state" activities, which human rights organizations argue creates a chilling effect comparable to that identified by the ECHR in Ecodefence.

### Enforcement Intensity

FARA enforcement illustrates the cyclical nature of foreign agent regulation: decades of minimal enforcement followed by sudden intensification in response to a perceived crisis. Germany's 2024 act is too recent to assess enforcement patterns. South Korea's National Security Act has been enforced continuously since 1948, though the frequency of prosecutions has declined significantly since democratization -- from hundreds of prosecutions annually in the 1980s to approximately 30-50 per year in the 2020s.

### Civil Society Impact

All jurisdictions face the challenge of regulating foreign influence without suppressing legitimate civil society activity. International human rights organizations, development NGOs, and academic institutions routinely receive funding from foreign governments and foundations. The ECHR's Ecodefence judgment established the principle that registration regimes must distinguish between genuine national security concerns and the legitimate activities of civil society, setting a constitutional benchmark that informs the design of the EU directive and Germany's 2024 act.
