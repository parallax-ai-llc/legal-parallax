## Summary

Property ownership in the Global South challenges the Western liberal assumption that property is primarily about individual fee simple title, revealing instead a rich tapestry of collective, communal, religious, and time-limited property forms that reflect distinctive political economies and philosophical traditions. China's property system is structured around the fundamental distinction between ownership (所有权) and use rights (用益物权): all urban land is state-owned, with the right to use residential land granted for 70 years (商品住宅建设用地使用权期限为七十年, Property Rights Law 2007, now Civil Code Article 359) and commercial land for 40-50 years; the automatic renewal provision for residential land use rights upon expiration (Civil Code Article 359: "automatically renewed") was a major innovation, though the renewal fee remains unresolved legislatively, creating significant uncertainty for China's 300 million urban homeowners. India's property law framework encompasses the Transfer of Property Act 1882, the Registration Act 1908, and state-specific land revenue codes, but is profoundly complicated by the phenomenon of benami transactions -- transactions in which property is held in the name of one person but paid for by another -- which the Prohibition of Benami Property Transactions Act, 1988 (as amended in 2016) criminalizes and subjects to confiscation, though enforcement has been hampered by the estimated 40-60% of Indian real estate transactions conducted in cash/black money. Nigeria's Land Use Act 1978 vests all land in state governors, converting what were previously freehold and customary ownership into statutory and customary "rights of occupancy" -- neither of which constitutes ownership in the full proprietary sense -- creating a system where the governor's consent is required for most land transactions and the distinction between urban and rural land has significant practical consequences for security of tenure. Brazil's property law is distinctive for its constitutional doctrine of the "social function of property" (função social da propriedade), which is not merely an external limitation on property rights but an internal element of the property right itself: property that does not fulfill its social function loses constitutional protection, enabling mechanisms such as adverse possession of urban land after five years of occupancy (usucapião urbana, Constitution Article 183; Civil Code Article 1240), progressive taxation on unbuilt urban land (IPTU progressivo, City Statute Article 7), and expropriation of unproductive rural land. Indonesia's property system reflects the UUPA's (1960) attempt to unify the colonial dual system under adat-based principles, establishing hak milik (right of ownership) as the strongest land right (Article 20: "hereditary, strongest, and most complete right"), but restricting it to Indonesian citizens and certain legal entities -- foreign nationals may hold only hak pakai (right to use, 25-30 years, renewable) or hak sewa (leasehold), while the expanding practice of nominee arrangements (using Indonesian citizens as nominal owners) operates in a legal gray zone that the Investment Law No. 25/2007 and subsequent regulations attempt to address.

## China

### The 70-Year Question

China's urban property system creates a distinctive form of ownership: citizens own their apartments (as real property, 不动产) but hold only a time-limited right to use the land beneath them.

**Civil Code Article 359:**
> "The term of the right to use land for construction of residential buildings shall be automatically renewed upon expiration. The payment or reduction of renewal fees shall be in accordance with the provisions of law or administrative regulations. The term of the right to use land for construction of non-residential buildings shall be renewed in accordance with the provisions of law. [...]"

The "automatic renewal" provision, first introduced in the Property Rights Law of 2007 (物权法) and carried into the 2021 Civil Code, was a major reassurance to homeowners, but it left the crucial question of renewal fees unresolved. The word used is "自动续期" (automatic renewal), but the subsequent clause -- "the payment or reduction of renewal fees shall be in accordance with the provisions of law" -- implies fees may be charged while creating uncertainty about amounts.

The issue became practically urgent when Wenzhou (温州) properties with only 20-year land use rights (a local experiment from the 1990s) began expiring in 2016. The State Council and Ministry of Natural Resources intervened, directing that expired residential land use rights be renewed without additional fees pending comprehensive legislation -- a stopgap that has become the de facto national policy but lacks statutory foundation.

**Practical Dimensions:**
- Approximately 300 million urban households hold apartments on 70-year land use rights, most originating from the housing commercialization reform of the late 1990s.
- The earliest 70-year rights will begin expiring around 2070, but the issue affects current property values and mortgage lending.
- The NPC Standing Committee has not enacted comprehensive renewal legislation as of 2026, though various drafts have circulated.

### Unified Real Estate Registration

The **Interim Regulations on Unified Real Estate Registration** (不动产登记暂行条例, State Council Decree No. 656, 2014, effective March 1, 2015) established a unified national registration system for all immovable property rights, consolidating what had previously been separate systems for land, buildings, forests, grasslands, maritime areas, and mineral rights.

The system, administered by the Ministry of Natural Resources (自然资源部), assigns a unique real estate certificate (不动产权证书) with a unified identification code. Full national coverage was announced in 2023. The system serves multiple purposes: confirming property rights, facilitating transactions, supporting anti-corruption investigations (by revealing officials' property holdings), and providing data for potential future property taxation.

### Housing Market Regulation

China's property market is subject to extensive administrative controls:
- **Purchase restrictions (限购):** Many cities limit the number of properties a household may own, with different rules for local vs. non-local hukou holders.
- **Price controls (限价):** Local governments may cap the selling prices of new developments.
- **Loan-to-value limits (限贷):** Differentiated down payment requirements for first and subsequent properties.
- **"Houses are for living in, not for speculation" (房住不炒):** This phrase, first used by President Xi Jinping in December 2016, became the foundational policy principle, though its implementation has fluctuated with economic conditions.

### Rural Property: Homestead Rights

The rural homestead right (宅基地使用权) permits rural collective members to use collectively-owned land for housing. Civil Code Article 362 provides that the homestead right holder has the right to occupy and use the collectively-owned land and to build residential housing and ancillary facilities. Unlike urban land use rights, homestead rights have no fixed term and are not freely transferable -- sales to non-collective members are prohibited, creating a significant constraint on rural property markets and a barrier to rural-urban wealth transfer.

Pilot reforms launched in 2015 (expanding in 2020) have experimented with separating homestead qualification rights, use rights, and transfer rights (宅基地"三权分置"), permitting limited transfer of use rights to outsiders for rural tourism and other purposes while maintaining collective ownership and member qualification rights.

## India

### Benami Transactions

The **Prohibition of Benami Property Transactions Act, 1988** (as substantially amended by the Benami Transactions (Prohibition) Amendment Act, 2016) targets a practice deeply embedded in Indian property markets:

**Section 2(8) (Definition):** A benami transaction is a transaction where property is transferred to or held by a person, and the consideration has been provided by another person. The benamidar (nominal owner) holds the property for the benefit of the beneficial owner.

**Section 3 (Prohibition):** No person shall enter into any benami transaction. Violation is punishable by imprisonment of 1-7 years and a fine up to 25% of the property's fair market value.

**Section 5 (Confiscation):** Benami property is liable for confiscation by the central government without payment of compensation.

**Enforcement:** The Initiating Officer under the Act (typically from the Income Tax Department) may provisionally attach benami property. The Adjudicating Authority (a quasi-judicial body) determines whether the property is benami after hearing the parties. Appeals lie to the Appellate Tribunal and thence to the High Court.

The Supreme Court in **Union of India v. Ganpati Dealcom Pvt. Ltd. (2023)** upheld the constitutionality of the 2016 amendments, rejecting challenges based on retroactivity and fundamental rights violations. However, the Court in a separate matter noted concerns about the potential for misuse of confiscation powers without adequate procedural safeguards.

**Scale of the Problem:** By some estimates, benami holdings represent a significant portion of Indian real estate. The practice is driven by: tax evasion (understating property values, avoiding capital gains), circumventing land ceiling laws, hiding corruption proceeds, and concentration of property through family members. The 2016 amendments were part of the Modi government's broader "black money" campaign that included demonetization and the Real Estate (Regulation and Development) Act 2016 (RERA).

### Registration and Title

India follows a system of **deed registration** (not title registration): the Registration Act 1908 requires registration of documents transferring immovable property above a threshold value. Critically, registration does not guarantee title -- it merely records the transaction. The burden of verifying title falls on the purchaser, requiring a chain-of-title search through often poorly maintained and handwritten registries. The Transfer of Property Act 1882 governs substantive property transfers.

Efforts to move toward **conclusive/guaranteed title** have been ongoing:
- The Digital India Land Records Modernization Programme (DILRMP) has digitized land records in most states, but digitization of incorrect or disputed records does not resolve underlying title issues.
- Rajasthan enacted the Rajasthan Urban Land (Certification of Titles) Act, 2016, as a pilot for title certification.
- The central government's draft Model Title Guarantee of Land Act has not been adopted by states.

### Adverse Possession

The Limitation Act 1963 provides for adverse possession: 12 years for private land (Article 65) and 30 years for government land (Article 112). The Supreme Court in **Hemaji Waghaji Jat v. Bhikhabhai Khengarbhai Harijan (2009) 16 SCC 517** criticized the doctrine as rewarding a "dishonest person who had illegally taken possession" but acknowledged it as settled law. A 12th Law Commission Report (2008) recommended reversing the burden to require the adverse possessor to prove entitlement, but this has not been enacted.

## Africa

### Nigeria: Rights of Occupancy Under the Land Use Act

The Land Use Act transformed all previous forms of land tenure -- freehold, leasehold, customary ownership -- into rights of occupancy:

**Statutory Right of Occupancy (Urban Land):** Granted by the Governor for urban land. The holder acquires a right akin to leasehold but: (a) the right is alienable only with the Governor's consent (section 22), (b) the Governor may revoke it for public interest or breach of conditions (section 28), and (c) compensation upon revocation covers only improvements, not the land itself.

**Customary Right of Occupancy (Rural Land):** Granted by the Local Government for non-urban land. Similar restrictions apply, but these rights more closely approximate traditional customary tenure.

**Governor's Consent Requirement:** Section 22 provides that any holder of a right of occupancy who wishes to alienate (sell, transfer, mortgage) must obtain the Governor's consent. This requirement, intended to prevent land speculation, has become a major impediment to property markets: consent is often delayed (months to years), requires payment of consent fees (often a percentage of the transaction value), and creates opportunities for corruption. The Supreme Court in **Savannah Bank of Nigeria Ltd v. Ajilo [1989] 1 NWLR (Pt. 97) 305** held that failure to obtain consent renders a transaction void, not merely voidable -- creating significant commercial uncertainty.

**Certificate of Occupancy (C of O):** The formal document evidencing a right of occupancy. In practice, the vast majority of Nigerian landholders -- particularly in rural areas -- have never obtained a C of O, relying instead on customary title, purchase receipts, or family allocation documents. This creates a massive tenure security gap.

### South Africa: Deeds Registry and Communal Tenure

South Africa maintains one of the most reliable land registration systems in the Global South:

**Deeds Registry Act 47 of 1937:** South Africa uses a negative registration system -- registration does not guarantee title absolutely, but a registered deed is prima facie evidence of the rights it reflects. The Registrar of Deeds examines documents for compliance before registration. In practice, the system functions with high reliability, and title fraud is relatively rare compared to other jurisdictions.

**Communal Land Tenure:** Approximately 17 million South Africans live on communal land (approximately 13% of the country's land area), administered by traditional authorities under customary law. Security of tenure on communal land is protected by:
- **Interim Protection of Informal Land Rights Act 31 of 1996 (IPILRA):** Protects informal rights to land (including customary rights) from deprivation without the consent of the holder. Has been renewed annually.
- **Communal Land Rights Act 11 of 2004 (CLRA):** Attempted to formalize communal tenure but was declared unconstitutional in **Tongoane v. National Minister for Agriculture and Land Affairs [2010] ZACC 10** for failure to follow the correct legislative procedure (it should have been tagged as section 76 legislation requiring provincial participation).
- The Communal Land Tenure Bill remains under development as of 2026.

**The Ingonyama Trust (KwaZulu-Natal):** The Ingonyama Trust Act 3KZ of 1994 vests approximately 2.8 million hectares of KwaZulu-Natal communal land in the Ingonyama Trust, administered by the Zulu monarch. The practice of converting customary tenure to leasehold in favor of the Trust (rather than community members) has been challenged -- the High Court in **Council for the Advancement of the South African Constitution v. Ingonyama Trust [2021] ZAKZPHC 13** found the Trust's leasehold policy unlawful. The matter has been appealed.

## Latin America

### Brazil: Social Function as Property's DNA

The "social function" doctrine in Brazilian property law is not merely a regulatory limitation imposed from outside; it is conceived as an inherent element of the property right:

**Constitutional Framework:**
- Article 5(XXII): The right of property is guaranteed.
- Article 5(XXIII): Property shall fulfill its social function.
- Article 170(III): The economic order is founded on the social function of property.
- Article 182(§4): The municipality may require adequate use of underutilized urban land, imposing successively: (1) compulsory subdivision or building, (2) progressive property tax (IPTU progressivo no tempo), and (3) expropriation with payment in municipal debt bonds.
- Article 183: Adverse possession (usucapião) of urban land up to 250 m² occupied for 5 years for residential purposes (usucapião urbana especial/constitucional).
- Article 191: Adverse possession of rural land up to 50 hectares occupied for 5 years and made productive (usucapião rural/pro labore).

**City Statute (Estatuto da Cidade, Lei No. 10.257/2001):** Implements the constitutional urban policy provisions, establishing:
- **Master Plans (Planos Diretores):** Mandatory for cities over 20,000 inhabitants; define the social function of urban property.
- **IPTU Progressivo no Tempo (Article 7):** Progressive property tax rates (increasing annually for 5 years, up to 15%) on underutilized urban land that fails to comply with the master plan.
- **Adverse Possession (Usucapião Especial Urbano, Article 9):** 5-year occupancy of urban land up to 250 m² used for habitation, where the possessor has no other property.
- **Collective Adverse Possession (Usucapião Coletivo, Article 10):** Available to low-income populations occupying urban areas over 250 m² for more than 5 years, where individual lots cannot be identified -- enabling regularization of entire favela communities.
- **Concession of Real Use Right (CUEM/CDRU):** For public land occupied for habitation, the government may grant real use rights rather than ownership.

**Favela Regularization (REURB -- Lei No. 13.465/2017):** Established a comprehensive framework for regularizing informal urban settlements (favelas/ocupações), distinguishing between social-interest regularization (REURB-S, for low-income populations, with subsidized fees and simplified procedures) and specific-interest regularization (REURB-E, for other situations).

**Quilombo Lands:** Article 68 of the Transitional Constitutional Provisions (ADCT) guarantees definitive property to the remnants of quilombo communities (communities descended from escaped enslaved persons). The STF in **ADI 3.239 (2018)** upheld the constitutionality of the Quilombo Decree (Decreto 4.887/2003), confirming that the right extends to lands traditionally occupied and is based on self-identification.

### Mexico: Fideicomiso in the Restricted Zone

The Mexican Constitution prohibits foreign ownership of land within 100 kilometers of international borders or 50 kilometers of coastline (the "restricted zone"). To accommodate foreign investment (particularly in tourist areas), the fideicomiso (trust) mechanism allows a Mexican bank to hold title as trustee while the foreign beneficiary has full use rights for 50-year renewable periods. The Foreign Investment Law (Ley de Inversión Extranjera, 1993) regulates the system.

### Colombia: Collective Territories

The Colombian Constitution (1991) recognizes collective territories of indigenous communities (resguardos, Article 63: inalienable, imprescriptible, and non-attachable) and Afro-Colombian communities (Law 70 of 1993). These collective titles cover approximately 34% of Colombia's territory, creating one of the largest collective property systems in the world.

## Southeast Asia

### Indonesia: Hak Milik and the Nationality Principle

The UUPA establishes a hierarchy of land rights:

**Hak Milik (Right of Ownership, Article 20):** "The hereditary, strongest, and most complete right that a person may hold over land." Only Indonesian citizens (and certain designated legal entities) may hold hak milik. Foreigners are absolutely prohibited. Government Regulation No. 38/1963 limits the legal entities eligible for hak milik to state-owned enterprises, cooperatives, religious bodies, and social bodies.

**Hak Guna Bangunan (Right to Build, Article 35):** The right to construct and own buildings on land belonging to another. Available to Indonesian citizens and Indonesian-incorporated companies. Term: maximum 30 years, extendable by 20 years. Commonly used for commercial development.

**Hak Guna Usaha (Right to Cultivate, Article 28):** The right to cultivate state land for agriculture, fishery, or animal husbandry. Available to Indonesian citizens and Indonesian-incorporated companies. Term: maximum 35 years, extendable by 25 years. Used for plantation concessions (palm oil, rubber, timber).

**Hak Pakai (Right to Use, Article 41):** The right to use and/or collect products from land. Available to Indonesian citizens, Indonesian companies, **foreign nationals domiciled in Indonesia**, and foreign companies with a representative office. Term: maximum 25 years (30 years for residential), renewable. This is the only right available to foreign individuals.

**The Nominee Problem:** Despite the prohibition on foreign hak milik, the practice of nominee arrangements (pinjam nama) -- where an Indonesian citizen holds title on behalf of a foreign beneficial owner -- is widespread, particularly in Bali and other tourist areas. The Supreme Court in Decision No. 12/PDT/2014 and subsequent cases has generally held nominee agreements void as violating the UUPA's nationality principle. The Investment Law No. 25/2007 (Article 33) prohibits nominee arrangements in the investment context. Despite this, enforcement is sporadic and the practice continues.

**Agrarian Reform Object (TORA):** Under the government's agrarian reform program, hak milik may be granted to individuals who previously held no formal land rights, including through redistribution of ex-HGU land and state land. The target of 9 million hectares under Perpres 86/2018 has focused predominantly on certification/titling rather than genuine redistribution.

### Philippines: Regalian Doctrine and Torrens Title

**Regalian Doctrine (Jura Regalia):** Article XII, Section 2 of the Constitution provides: "All lands of the public domain, waters, minerals, coal, petroleum, and other mineral oils, all forces of potential energy, fisheries, forests or timber, wildlife, flora and fauna, and other natural resources are owned by the State." Private land exists only where the state has alienated it -- creating the fundamental distinction between alienable and disposable (A&D) public land and inalienable public domain land.

**Foreign Ownership Prohibition:** Article XII, Section 7: "Save in cases of hereditary succession, no private lands shall be transferred or conveyed except to individuals, corporations, or associations qualified to acquire or hold lands of the public domain in the Philippines." Only Filipino citizens and corporations at least 60% Filipino-owned may acquire private land. Foreign nationals may only lease land (maximum 75 years under RA 7652, the Investors' Lease Act). Condominium units may be owned by foreigners up to 40% of the project's total units (Condominium Act, RA 4726).

**Torrens System (PD 1529, Property Registration Decree):** The Philippines uses the Torrens system of land registration, under which a certificate of title is conclusive evidence of ownership. Once land is registered under the Torrens system, it is generally indefeasible -- immune to adverse claims and prescription. The Land Registration Authority administers the system.

**Ancestral Domain (Indigenous Peoples' Rights Act, RA 8371/1997):** Recognizes the rights of Indigenous Cultural Communities/Indigenous Peoples (ICCs/IPs) to their ancestral domains and ancestral lands, issuing Certificates of Ancestral Domain Title (CADT) and Certificates of Ancestral Land Title (CALT). The Supreme Court in **Cruz v. Secretary of DENR (G.R. No. 135385, 2000)** narrowly upheld IPRA's constitutionality, though concerns about the relationship between ancestral domain claims and the Regalian doctrine persist. As of 2025, the NCIP has issued over 250 CADTs covering approximately 5.7 million hectares.

## Comparative Analysis

### Ownership vs. Use Rights

The Global South reveals that "ownership" is not a binary concept. China separates ownership (state/collective) from use rights (individual), creating a functional market in use rights. Nigeria's Land Use Act similarly separates underlying state ownership from rights of occupancy. Indonesia's hierarchy of rights (hak milik, hak guna bangunan, hak pakai) creates a spectrum of property interests. This contrasts with Brazil and South Africa, where full fee simple ownership remains the primary form, though subject to social function requirements and constitutional constraints.

### Foreign Ownership Restrictions

Restrictions on foreign land ownership are nearly universal in the Global South: China prohibits it entirely; Indonesia limits foreigners to hak pakai; the Philippines prohibits it constitutionally; Mexico's restricted zone requires trust arrangements; Nigeria requires gubernatorial consent; India restricts agricultural land purchases. Only South Africa permits unrestricted foreign land ownership. These restrictions reflect concerns about neocolonial resource extraction, national sovereignty, and the political sensitivity of land in post-colonial states.

### Formalization of Informal Tenure

A critical challenge across the Global South is the gap between formal registration systems and the reality of informal, customary, or undocumented tenure. Brazil's REURB and collective usucapião address favela regularization. Nigeria's massive C of O gap leaves most landholders without formal documentation. Indonesia's agrarian reform titling program seeks to formalize customary tenure. The Philippines' IPRA creates a pathway for indigenous ancestral domain recognition. South Africa's IPILRA provides interim protection. In each case, the tension between formalization (which provides security and market access) and the risk of dispossession through formalization (where the process may not reflect complex customary arrangements) remains unresolved.

### Social Function: A Global South Innovation

The social function doctrine, originating in Leon Duguit's sociological jurisprudence but given its most robust legal expression in Latin America, represents a genuinely distinctive Global South contribution to property theory. Brazil's four-part test (Article 186), Indonesia's UUPA Article 6, and the Philippines' constitutional provisions all condition property rights on social obligations. This stands in contrast to the Anglo-American tradition where property limitations are generally conceptualized as external "takings" or "regulations" rather than as inherent to the property right itself.
