## Summary

Public housing -- government-funded or government-operated residential accommodation provided to low-income households at below-market rents, encompassing social housing, subsidized rental programs, and affordable housing mandates -- is a fundamental component of social welfare systems that reflects deeply divergent approaches to housing as a right, a commodity, or a combination of both. South Korea has developed one of the most extensive public housing programs in Asia under the Housing Act (주택법) and the Special Act on Public Housing (공공주택 특별법, 2015), administered primarily through the Korea Land and Housing Corporation (한국토지주택공사, LH) and the Seoul Housing and Communities Corporation (SH공사), with public rental housing (공공임대주택) comprising approximately 8.7 percent of total housing stock as of 2024, and the government pursuing an ambitious target of increasing this to 10 percent under the Yoon administration's housing supply plan, while the Housing Lease Protection Act (주택임대차보호법) provides strong tenant protections including deposit protection and renewal rights. The United States has the most fragmented public housing system among developed nations, comprising approximately 970,000 public housing units managed by approximately 3,300 public housing authorities (PHAs) under the United States Housing Act of 1937 (42 U.S.C. Section 1437 et seq.), supplemented by the Housing Choice Voucher Program (Section 8, approximately 2.3 million vouchers), the Low-Income Housing Tax Credit (LIHTC, IRC Section 42) which has financed approximately 3.6 million affordable housing units since 1987, and various HUD programs, with federal public housing stock declining steadily through demolitions and RAD (Rental Assistance Demonstration) conversions while approximately 10 million extremely low-income renter households spend more than 50 percent of income on housing. Germany operates one of Europe's most significant social housing systems, though the sector has contracted dramatically from approximately 4 million social housing units in the 1980s to approximately 1.07 million in 2023, governed by the Social Housing Promotion Act (Wohnraumförderungsgesetz, WoFG, 2001) and supplemented by the Mietpreisbremse (rent brake) under BGB Section 556d (2015), with the Federal Constitutional Court addressing the constitutional status of housing in the Berlin rent cap decision (BVerfGE 157, 223 (2021)), and Chancellor Scholz's government pledging to build 400,000 new apartments annually including 100,000 social housing units. The European framework addresses housing through the European Social Charter Article 31 (right to housing), the Revised European Social Charter, ECHR Article 8 (home) jurisprudence protecting against eviction, EU structural funds supporting social housing investment, and the European Pillar of Social Rights Principle 19 affirming the right to housing and assistance for the homeless, while the ECHR Grand Chamber in Yordanova v. Bulgaria (2012) established significant procedural protections against forced evictions under Article 8.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea has built an extensive public housing system from virtually nothing since the 1980s, responding to rapid urbanization and the housing affordability crisis created by concentrated population growth in the Seoul metropolitan area.

**Special Act on Public Housing (공공주택 특별법, 2015):** The comprehensive statute governing public housing development, management, and allocation:

- **Article 2 (Definitions / 정의):** Public housing (공공주택) includes public construction rental housing (공공건설임대주택), public purchase rental housing (공공매입임대주택), and national rental housing (국민임대주택)
- **Article 4 (Supply Plans / 공급계획):** The Minister of Land, Infrastructure and Transport shall establish a 10-year public housing supply plan
- **Article 48 (Eligibility / 입주자 자격):** Eligibility is determined by household income relative to the median income, household size, and housing need, with priority given to persons with disabilities, elderly persons, and families with young children

### Types of Public Housing

| Public Supported Private Rental | 공공지원민간임대 | Middle income | 8 years | Controlled annual increases |

### Korea Land and Housing Corporation (한국토지주택공사, LH)

LH is the world's largest public housing developer, managing approximately 1.2 million public rental housing units. LH also develops new towns, manages land banks, and implements urban renewal projects. The corporation operates under the Korea Land and Housing Corporation Act (한국토지주택공사법).

### Housing Lease Protection Act (주택임대차보호법)

This statute, originally enacted in 1981 and significantly amended in 2020, provides strong tenant protections applicable to both public and private rental housing:

- **Deposit Priority (임차보증금 우선변제):** Tenants' lease deposits receive priority in bankruptcy and foreclosure proceedings, ahead of most other creditors
- **Contract Renewal Right (계약갱신청구권):** Added in 2020 amendment, tenants have the right to one contract renewal of up to two years, for a total minimum lease period of four years
- **Rent Increase Cap (전월세상한제):** During the renewal period, rent increases are capped at 5% of the previous rent

### 2020 Housing Reform ("Tenant Three Laws" / 임대차 3법)

The Moon Jae-in administration enacted three housing reform laws in July 2020:
1. **Contract renewal right** (계약갱신청구권) -- guaranteed one-time renewal
2. **Rent increase cap** (전월세상한제) -- 5% cap during renewal
3. **Lease reporting system** (전월세신고제) -- mandatory reporting of lease terms

These reforms provoked significant controversy, with critics arguing they reduced rental supply and increased jeonse (lump-sum deposit) amounts.

### Constitutional Court

**Constitutional Court Decision 2017Hun-Ba441 (2019):** The Court upheld the constitutionality of public housing allocation criteria that prioritized certain demographic groups, holding that preferential allocation to vulnerable populations serves the state's constitutional obligation to promote public welfare under Articles 34 and 35(3) and does not violate the right to equality under Article 11.

## United States

### Legal Framework

**United States Housing Act of 1937 (42 U.S.C. Section 1437 et seq.):** The foundational federal public housing statute, establishing the framework for public housing authorities to develop and manage subsidized rental housing. The Act has been amended numerous times, most significantly by the Housing and Community Development Act of 1974 (creating Section 8) and the Quality Housing and Work Responsibility Act of 1998 (QHWRA).

**Housing Choice Voucher Program (Section 8):** The largest federal rental assistance program, providing approximately 2.3 million vouchers that subsidize tenants' rent in privately owned housing. Tenants pay 30 percent of adjusted income toward rent; the PHA pays the difference up to a payment standard. Demand far exceeds supply, with waiting lists of years in most jurisdictions.

**Low-Income Housing Tax Credit (LIHTC, IRC Section 42):** Created by the Tax Reform Act of 1986, LIHTC is the primary mechanism for financing affordable housing development in the United States. State housing finance agencies allocate tax credits to developers of qualifying rental projects. Since 1987, LIHTC has financed approximately 3.6 million affordable housing units, representing virtually all new affordable rental construction.

**HOPE VI and Choice Neighborhoods:** HOPE VI (Housing Opportunities for People Everywhere), enacted in 1992, provided grants for demolition and redevelopment of severely distressed public housing. The program resulted in the demolition of approximately 100,000 public housing units and their replacement with mixed-income developments. The Choice Neighborhoods Initiative (2010) succeeded HOPE VI with a broader community development focus.

### Fair Housing Act (42 U.S.C. Section 3601 et seq.)

The Fair Housing Act of 1968 prohibits discrimination in housing on the basis of race, color, religion, sex, national origin, familial status, and disability. In Texas Department of Housing and Community Affairs v. Inclusive Communities Project, 576 U.S. 519 (2015), the Supreme Court held that disparate impact claims are cognizable under the Fair Housing Act, enabling challenges to policies that disproportionately affect protected classes even without proof of discriminatory intent.

### Constitutional Framework

The United States has no constitutional right to housing. In Lindsey v. Normet, 405 U.S. 56 (1972), the Supreme Court stated: "We do not denigrate the importance of decent, safe, and sanitary housing. But the Constitution does not provide judicial remedies for every social and economic ill."

However, procedural due process protections apply to public housing tenants:
- **Escalera v. New York City Housing Authority, 425 F.2d 853 (2d Cir. 1970):** Public housing tenants have a due process right to a hearing before eviction
- **HUD v. Rucker, 535 U.S. 125 (2002):** Public housing authorities may evict tenants for drug activity by household members or guests, even without the tenant's knowledge

### Affordability Crisis

The United States faces an acute affordable housing shortage:
- Approximately 10 million extremely low-income renter households (income below 30% of area median income)
- Only 3.6 million affordable and available rental units for these households
- Approximately 70% of extremely low-income renters spend more than 50% of income on housing
- Federal public housing stock has declined from approximately 1.4 million units in the 1990s to approximately 970,000 in 2024

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany has a distinctive housing law tradition combining strong tenant protections, social housing promotion, and rent regulation.

**Wohnraumförderungsgesetz (Social Housing Promotion Act, WoFG, 2001):** Replaced the earlier social housing construction laws, establishing the framework for federal-state cooperation in social housing:

- **Section 1 (Purpose):** Social housing promotion serves the objective of providing housing for households unable to secure adequate housing on the market
- **Section 2 (Target Groups):** Households with income below state-defined thresholds; priority for families with children, elderly persons, disabled persons, and other vulnerable groups
- **Section 8 (Instruments):** Social housing is promoted through construction subsidies (Baudarlehen), rent subsidies, and building land provision

### Social Housing (Sozialer Wohnungsbau)

German social housing operates on a time-limited binding model (Sozialbindung): developers receive public subsidies in exchange for renting units to eligible households at below-market rents for a specified period (typically 15-30 years). Once the binding period expires, the units revert to market-rate housing. This model has contributed to the dramatic contraction of social housing stock:

| Year | Social Housing Units | % of Total Housing |
|------|---------------------|-------------------|
| 1987 (West Germany) | ~3.9 million | ~17% |
| 2002 | ~2.6 million | ~7% |
| 2010 | ~1.6 million | ~4% |
| 2020 | ~1.13 million | ~2.8% |
| 2023 | ~1.07 million | ~2.7% |

### Mietpreisbremse (Rent Brake, BGB Section 556d)

Enacted on June 1, 2015, and strengthened in 2019 and 2020, the Mietpreisbremse limits rent increases in "tight housing markets" (angespannte Wohnungsmärkte) designated by state governments:

- New rental agreements may not exceed 110% of the local reference rent (Vergleichsmiete) as determined by the Mietspiegel (rent index)
- Exceptions for new construction (first occupancy), comprehensive modernization, and existing rents already above the cap
- Tenants may reclaim excess rent paid above the cap
- The provision is valid until December 31, 2025 (extended from the original 2020 expiration)

### Berlin Rent Cap (Mietendeckel) and Federal Constitutional Court

The Berlin state parliament enacted the Mietendeckelgesetz (Rent Cap Act) in February 2020, imposing a five-year rent freeze and rent reduction requirements on approximately 1.5 million apartments.

**BVerfGE 157, 223 (2021) -- Berlin Rent Cap Decision:** The Federal Constitutional Court struck down the Berlin rent cap on April 15, 2021, holding that rent regulation for private tenancies is a matter of competing federal legislation under Article 74(1)(1) GG (civil law) and that the federal legislature had conclusively exercised its competence through the BGB tenancy provisions and the Mietpreisbremse. The decision was based on competence grounds (Kompetenzwidrigkeit) rather than substantive proportionality, leaving open whether a federal-level rent cap would be constitutional.

### Tenant Protections

German tenancy law provides among the world's strongest tenant protections:

- **Indefinite Tenancies (BGB Section 542):** Residential leases are indefinite by default; fixed-term leases are permitted only in limited circumstances
- **Eviction Limitations (BGB Section 573):** Landlords may terminate only for "legitimate interest" (berechtigtes Interesse), including personal use (Eigenbedarf), significant breach by tenant, or economic exploitation (Verwertungskündigung)
- **Notice Periods (BGB Section 573c):** Landlord notice periods increase with tenancy duration: 3 months (up to 5 years), 6 months (5-8 years), 9 months (over 8 years)
- **Social Hardship (Sozialklausel, BGB Section 574):** Tenants may oppose even legitimate termination if it would cause undue hardship, considering age, health, pregnancy, and housing market conditions

## ECHR / EU

### ECHR Article 8 -- Right to Respect for Home

The ECHR's "home" jurisprudence provides significant protections against forced eviction:

**Yordanova and Others v. Bulgaria (Application No. 25446/06, April 24, 2012):** The Court found that Bulgaria's eviction of Roma residents from an informal settlement violated Article 8. The Court established that:
- Any person at risk of losing their home should be able to have the proportionality of the measure determined by an independent tribunal
- The eviction of a vulnerable individual from their home without considering the proportionality or providing alternative accommodation may violate Article 8
- Particular attention must be paid to the situation of vulnerable groups, including Roma

**Connors v. United Kingdom (Application No. 66746/01, May 27, 2004):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where a local authority evicted a Gypsy family from a local authority caravan site without providing adequate procedural safeguards, including the opportunity to challenge the proportionality of the eviction before a court.

**FJM v. United Kingdom (Application No. 76202/16, November 6, 2018):** The Court addressed the UK's "bedroom tax" (removal of the spare room subsidy for social housing tenants with spare bedrooms), finding no violation of Article 14 read with Article 1 of Protocol No. 1, as the policy pursued the legitimate aim of incentivizing efficient use of social housing stock and fell within the state's margin of appreciation.

### European Social Charter Article 31

The Revised European Social Charter (1996) Article 31 (Right to Housing) requires states parties to:
1. Promote access to housing of an adequate standard
2. Prevent and reduce homelessness with a view to its gradual elimination
3. Make the price of housing accessible to those without adequate resources

The European Committee of Social Rights has issued multiple findings of non-conformity under Article 31, including against France for insufficient social housing construction and against Italy for inadequate Roma housing conditions.

### European Pillar of Social Rights (2017)

**Principle 19 (Housing and Assistance for the Homeless):** "(a) Access to social housing or housing assistance of good quality shall be provided for those in need. (b) Vulnerable people have the right to appropriate assistance and protection against forced eviction. (c) Adequate shelter and services shall be provided to the homeless in order to promote their social inclusion."

### EU Structural Funds

The European Regional Development Fund (ERDF) and the Recovery and Resilience Facility (RRF) provide significant funding for social and affordable housing across member states. The 2021-2027 ERDF programming period explicitly includes energy-efficient social housing renovation as an eligible investment priority.

## Comparative Analysis

### Right to Housing

Germany provides the strongest constitutional protection for housing through the social state principle and robust BGB tenancy protections, though no explicit constitutional right to housing exists. South Korea's Article 35(3) creates a state obligation regarding the housing environment. The ECHR provides procedural protections against eviction under Article 8. The United States has no constitutional housing right and provides the weakest legal protections.

### Public Housing Models

South Korea and the United States operate the most direct public housing programs, with government-owned and managed rental units. Germany's social housing model relies on time-limited subsidies to private developers, which has contributed to the contraction of the social housing stock as binding periods expire. The EU framework supports diverse national approaches without mandating a specific model.

### Tenant Protections

Germany provides the strongest tenant protections globally, with indefinite leases, limited eviction grounds, extended notice periods, and the social hardship clause. South Korea's 2020 reforms significantly strengthened tenant protections, though they remain less comprehensive than Germany's. The United States provides the weakest tenant protections, with significant state variation and no federal rent regulation.

### Affordability Challenge

All four jurisdictions face significant housing affordability challenges. South Korea's housing prices in Seoul have increased dramatically despite extensive government intervention. The United States faces a severe shortage of affordable housing with approximately 7 million unit shortfall. Germany's social housing stock has been declining for decades despite new construction targets. Across the EU, rising housing costs have been identified as one of the principal drivers of poverty and social exclusion.
