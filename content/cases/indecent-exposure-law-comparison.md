## Summary

Indecent exposure -- the deliberate display of one's body, typically the genitals, in a public place or in view of others in a manner that causes alarm, offense, or distress -- occupies a contested space between public order regulation and sexual offense law, with jurisdictions differing significantly in whether the offense is classified as a minor nuisance, a serious sexual crime, or a precursor offense warranting enhanced monitoring. South Korea addresses indecent exposure through Article 245 of the Criminal Act (형법), which criminalizes "public obscenity" (공연음란) with imprisonment of up to one year or a fine of up to 5 million KRW, and through the Act on Special Cases Concerning the Punishment of Sexual Crimes (성폭력범죄의 처벌 등에 관한 특례법), which under Article 12 provides enhanced penalties for obscene acts using telecommunications (통신매체를 이용한 음란행위) including sending obscene images or engaging in sexual exposure via video calls, punishable by up to two years imprisonment; Korean law has grappled extensively with digital forms of exposure, particularly in the context of the "molka" (몰래카메라, hidden camera) crisis that prompted sweeping reforms. The United States treats indecent exposure under state and local law, with penalties ranging from misdemeanors to felonies, and approximately 13 states require sex offender registration for indecent exposure convictions; the Model Penal Code Section 213.5 classifies "indecent exposure" as a misdemeanor, requiring the purpose of arousing or gratifying sexual desire. Germany addresses the offense through Section 183 StGB (Exhibitionistische Handlungen, exhibitionist acts), which notably applies only to male perpetrators and requires that the act cause annoyance to another person, punishable by imprisonment of up to one year or a fine; the gender-specific nature of Section 183 has been a subject of ongoing academic and legislative debate, with critics arguing it violates the equality principle of Article 3 GG. The ECHR has addressed indecent exposure tangentially through Article 8 (private life) and Article 10 (freedom of expression) jurisprudence, while the EU has not harmonized indecent exposure law but has addressed related online offenses through Directive 2011/93/EU on combating sexual abuse of children.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

**Article 245 -- Public Obscenity (공연음란):** "A person who commits an obscene act in public shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than one year or a fine not exceeding 5 million KRW." The term "public" (공연히) has been interpreted by the Supreme Court to mean in a place or manner where the act could be observed by an unspecified or multiple persons.

**Sexual Crimes Special Act, Article 12 -- Obscene Acts Using Telecommunications (통신매체를 이용한 음란행위):** "A person who makes the other party feel sexual shame by reaching the other party through telephone, mail, computer, or other communication media with sexual desire or interest, using obscene language, sounds, writings, pictures, images, or objects, shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than 2 years or a fine not exceeding 10 million KRW."

**Sexual Crimes Special Act, Article 13 -- Obscene Acts in Public Places (공중 밀집 장소에서의 추행):** Addresses groping and other sexual touching in crowded public places such as public transportation, punishable by up to 3 years imprisonment or 30 million KRW fine.

### The "Molka" Crisis and Digital Exposure

South Korea has faced a widespread crisis of digital sexual exposure and voyeurism:

**"Molka" (몰래카메라):** Hidden cameras placed in bathrooms, hotel rooms, changing rooms, and other private spaces to record individuals without consent. While distinct from traditional indecent exposure, the crisis has shaped Korean law's approach to all forms of sexual display.

**Reform Timeline:**
- 2012: The Act on Special Cases Concerning Sexual Crimes strengthened to address digital crimes
- 2018: Over 70,000 people participated in Hyehwa Station protest demanding stronger enforcement against digital sex crimes
- 2020: "Nth Room" scandal -- Cho Ju-bin convicted for operating Telegram chatrooms distributing sexual exploitation material, sentenced to 40 years
- 2021: Further amendments strengthened penalties for non-consensual distribution of sexual images

### Enforcement Statistics

According to the Korean National Police Agency, approximately 6,000-8,000 cases of public obscenity (공연음란) and telecommunications obscenity are prosecuted annually.

### Sex Offender Management

South Korea operates an electronic monitoring system for sex offenders:
- The Act on the Electronic Monitoring of Specific Criminals (특정 범죄자에 대한 보호관찰 및 전자장치 부착 등에 관한 법률) allows electronic ankle monitoring
- The Sex Offender Registry (성범죄자 알림e) provides public notification for certain offenders
- Repeat indecent exposure offenders may be subject to enhanced monitoring

## United States

### Legal Framework

**Model Penal Code Section 213.5 -- Indecent Exposure:** "A person commits a misdemeanor if, for the purpose of arousing or gratifying sexual desire of himself or of any person other than his spouse, he exposes his genitals under circumstances in which he knows his conduct is likely to cause affront or alarm."

### Key Elements

**Purpose of Sexual Gratification:** The MPC requires that the exposure be for the "purpose of arousing or gratifying sexual desire." This distinguishes criminal indecent exposure from non-sexual nudity (e.g., nudist activities, breastfeeding, streaking).

**Knowledge of Offensive Context:** The defendant must know the conduct is "likely to cause affront or alarm," requiring awareness of the social context.

### Sex Offender Registration

Approximately 13 states require sex offender registration for indecent exposure:

The requirement is controversial. Advocates argue that indecent exposure is often a precursor to more serious sexual offenses. Critics argue that registration imposes disproportionate lifelong consequences for what may be relatively minor conduct.

### Constitutional Issues

**First Amendment and Nudity:** Public nudity has been treated as conduct rather than expression in most contexts. In Barnes v. Glen Theatre, Inc., 501 U.S. 560 (1991), the Supreme Court held that state prohibition of public nudity (in the context of nude dancing) does not violate the First Amendment, as the state's interest in protecting public morality justifies the minimal restriction on expression.

**Breastfeeding Exemptions:** All 50 states have enacted laws protecting the right to breastfeed in public, exempting breastfeeding from indecent exposure statutes.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

**Section 183 StGB -- Exhibitionist Acts (Exhibitionistische Handlungen):**
- Paragraph 1: "A man who annoys another person by an exhibitionist act shall be punished with imprisonment for a term not exceeding one year or a fine."
- Paragraph 2: "The act shall only be prosecuted upon complaint unless the prosecuting authority deems prosecution to be required in the public interest on account of the special importance of the case."
- Paragraph 3: "The court may order placement in a psychiatric hospital pursuant to Section 63 if the overall assessment of the perpetrator and his acts indicates that, due to his propensity to commit exhibitionist acts, he poses a risk of committing unlawful acts of a serious nature."
- Paragraph 4: "Prosecution and conviction shall be suspended, and measures already ordered shall be stayed, if the person voluntarily undergoes curative treatment with the aim of eliminating his propensity; prosecution shall be resumed and measures carried out if treatment is discontinued or conditions for probation are not met."

### Gender-Specific Application

Section 183 is one of the few gender-specific criminal provisions in German law, applying only to "a man" (ein Mann). Female exhibitionism is addressed under the more general Section 183a StGB (Erregung öffentlichen Ärgernisses, causing public annoyance), which carries a penalty of up to one year imprisonment or a fine and applies to any person.

**Section 183a StGB -- Causing Public Annoyance (Erregung öffentlichen Ärgernisses):** "Whoever publicly performs sexual acts and thereby intentionally or knowingly causes an annoyance shall be punished with imprisonment for a term not exceeding one year or a fine."

### Constitutional Debate

The gender-specific nature of Section 183 has been criticized:

**Article 3(2) GG:** "Men and women shall have equal rights." Critics argue that treating male and female exhibitionism differently violates this constitutional equality guarantee.

**Defenders' Position:** Proponents argue that the gendered provision reflects empirical reality -- the vast majority of exhibitionism cases involve male perpetrators -- and that the provision's therapeutic orientation (Section 183(4)) justifies the specific treatment.

**Reform Proposals:** The German Women Lawyers' Association (Deutscher Juristinnenbund) and academic commentators have proposed making Section 183 gender-neutral, either by extending it to all persons or by consolidating it with Section 183a.

### Therapeutic Orientation

A distinctive feature of German law is Section 183(4)'s emphasis on treatment:
- Prosecution may be suspended if the offender voluntarily undergoes therapeutic treatment
- This reflects the classification of exhibitionism as a recognized paraphilic disorder in psychiatric classification systems
- Treatment programs focus on impulse control and behavioral modification

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

**Article 8 -- Private Life:** The Court's jurisprudence on public nudity and indecent exposure balances the right to private life against the state's interest in maintaining public morals and protecting the rights of others.

**Gough v. United Kingdom (Application No. 49327/11, Decision of October 28, 2014):** The "Naked Rambler" case -- Stephen Gough was repeatedly arrested and imprisoned for walking nude through public spaces in Scotland. The Court declared the application inadmissible, holding that the repeated arrests and prosecutions did not violate Article 8, as the interference was proportionate to the legitimate aim of preventing disorder and protecting the rights of others.

**Key Principles:**
- States enjoy a wide margin of appreciation in regulating public nudity
- The right to express one's personality through nudity is not absolute
- Repeated prosecution for persistent exhibitionist conduct is not disproportionate

### EU Framework

The EU has not harmonized indecent exposure law specifically, but related provisions include:

**Directive 2011/93/EU on Combating Sexual Abuse of Children:** Addresses online solicitation of children for sexual purposes, including exposure via digital means (Article 6 -- "solicitation of children for sexual purposes").

**Digital Services Act (Regulation (EU) 2022/2065):** Requires online platforms to address illegal content, which may include non-consensual distribution of intimate images and online sexual exposure.

## Comparative Analysis

### Classification: Nuisance vs. Sexual Offense

The jurisdictions diverge on whether indecent exposure is primarily a public order offense or a sexual offense:

- **South Korea:** Hybrid -- public obscenity provision (Art. 245) treats it as public order; sexual crimes special act treats digital exposure as sexual offense
- **United States:** Increasingly classified as sexual offense (sex offender registration in some states)
- **Germany:** Treated as a disorder-specific offense with therapeutic orientation; closer to nuisance classification
- **ECHR:** Wide margin of appreciation; treated as public order matter

### Digital Dimension

The expansion of indecent exposure to digital contexts represents a major area of legal development:
- **South Korea:** Most developed framework -- explicit criminalization of telecommunications obscenity
- **United States:** Emerging "cyber-flashing" statutes in some states
- **Germany:** Primarily addressed through general provisions
- **EU:** Addressed through child protection and online platform regulation

### Gender and Equality

The German approach highlights fundamental questions about gender-specific criminal provisions:
- Is gendered treatment justified by empirical patterns?
- Does equal protection require identical treatment of male and female exhibitionism?
- How should criminal law respond to paraphilic disorders?
