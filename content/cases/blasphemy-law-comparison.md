## Summary

Blasphemy and religious offense laws vary dramatically across jurisdictions, reflecting deep tensions between freedom of expression and the protection of religious sensibilities. South Korea lacks a formal blasphemy statute but addresses religious offense through general defamation and insult provisions, while the United States has effectively nullified its colonial-era blasphemy laws through robust First Amendment protections. Germany retains Section 166 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), which criminalizes the vilification of religious beliefs and organizations when it disturbs public peace, with penalties of up to three years' imprisonment. The European Court of Human Rights, most notably in E.S. v. Austria (2018), has applied a wide margin of appreciation, permitting member states to maintain blasphemy laws where deemed necessary to protect religious peace -- a position that has drawn significant criticism from free expression advocates.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea does not have a dedicated blasphemy or religious offense statute. The Constitution of the Republic of Korea guarantees both freedom of religion (Article 20) and freedom of expression (Article 21), and the legal system does not single out religious speech for special criminal treatment. However, several existing provisions may capture conduct that in other jurisdictions would fall under blasphemy laws:

**Criminal Act (hyeongbeop):**

- **Article 307 (Defamation):** Publicly alleging facts that damage a person's reputation -- including religious leaders or figures -- is punishable by up to 2 years' imprisonment (true facts) or 5 years (false facts). Religious organizations and clergy have used defamation provisions to silence critics.
- **Article 311 (Insult):** Publicly insulting another person without alleging specific facts carries up to 1 year imprisonment or a fine up to 2 million won. This provision has occasionally been invoked in the context of religious disputes.
- **Article 158 (Interference with Religious Services):** A person who obstructs or disturbs a religious service by violence or threat shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than 3 years or by a fine not exceeding 5 million won.

**National Security Act:**

While not directly a blasphemy law, the National Security Act has historically been used to restrict expression deemed threatening to social order, and in rare instances has intersected with religious speech by minority or dissident religious groups.

### Key Principles

South Korean courts have generally upheld broad protections for religious criticism. The Constitutional Court has emphasized that freedom of religion includes the freedom to criticize religion, and that the state must remain neutral among competing religious claims. However, the breadth of defamation law -- particularly the criminalization of true-fact defamation under Article 307(1) -- creates an indirect mechanism by which religious offense can be penalized when it targets identifiable individuals or organizations.

In practice, religious disputes in South Korea more commonly arise in the context of conflicts between established religions (Buddhism, Christianity) and newer religious movements (such as Shincheonji or the Unification Church), where defamation claims serve as proxies for blasphemy-like complaints.

### Notable Cases

**Shincheonji Church Defamation Cases (2020-2023):** Following the COVID-19 outbreak linked to the Shincheonji Church of Jesus, numerous defamation claims were filed both by and against the church. Critics of the church faced defamation charges for public statements about the organization, while church members filed complaints alleging religious persecution. These cases illustrated how South Korea's defamation framework functions as a de facto blasphemy mechanism.

**Buddhist Temple Land Dispute Speech Cases:** In several instances, public criticism of Buddhist temple administrations led to criminal defamation prosecutions, where the religious dimension of the speech was incidental to the legal framework but central to the underlying dispute.

**Pastor Jun-gwang's Anti-Buddhist Statements (2008-2010):** A Christian pastor's public statements denigrating Buddhism and Buddhist temples generated significant public controversy and inter-religious tension. While no criminal charges were ultimately sustained, the incident prompted calls from some legislators for a dedicated religious harmony law -- proposals that were ultimately not pursued.

## United States

### Federal Framework

The United States has no federal blasphemy law, and the First Amendment to the Constitution -- providing that "Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof; or abridging the freedom of speech" -- has been interpreted to preclude criminal punishment for blasphemous expression.

The Supreme Court has consistently held that the government may not restrict speech on the basis of its religious content or viewpoint:

- **Joseph Burstyn, Inc. v. Wilson, 343 U.S. 495 (1952):** The Supreme Court struck down a New York statute that permitted the banning of "sacrilegious" films, holding that the term was unconstitutionally vague and that censorship based on religious offense violated the First Amendment. Justice Clark wrote that "it is not the business of government in our nation to suppress real or imagined attacks upon a particular religious doctrine."
- **Epperson v. Arkansas, 393 U.S. 97 (1968):** The Court invalidated an Arkansas statute prohibiting the teaching of evolution, reinforcing the principle that the state may not tailor its laws to conform to any particular religious orthodoxy.

### State Variations

Several U.S. states retain blasphemy statutes in their criminal codes, though these are universally regarded as unenforceable after Burstyn:

**Pennsylvania Example:** In 2010, a federal district court in *Kalman v. Cortes* held that Pennsylvania's blasphemy statute violated both the Establishment Clause and the Free Exercise Clause. The court found that the statute impermissibly advanced religion by criminalizing speech offensive to religious sensibilities.

### Notable Cases

**The People v. Ruggles, 8 Johns. 290 (N.Y. 1811):** One of the earliest American blasphemy prosecutions, in which the defendant was convicted for stating that "Jesus Christ was a bastard, and his mother must be a whore." Chief Justice James Kent upheld the conviction, reasoning that Christianity was part of the common law. This case is now universally regarded as superseded by modern First Amendment jurisprudence.

**Commonwealth v. Kneeland, 37 Mass. 206 (1838):** The Massachusetts Supreme Court upheld a blasphemy conviction of Abner Kneeland, a Universalist minister turned freethinker, for publishing a letter denying belief in God. This was one of the last successful blasphemy prosecutions in the United States and is widely cited as an example of how blasphemy laws were historically used to suppress religious dissent.

**Burstyn v. Wilson (1952):** The landmark case effectively ended blasphemy prosecutions in the United States, establishing that religious censorship has no place in constitutional governance.

**Hustler Magazine v. Falwell, 485 U.S. 46 (1988):** While primarily a defamation case, the Supreme Court's unanimous ruling that a parody of Reverend Jerry Falwell was protected speech under the First Amendment reinforced that emotional distress caused by speech about public figures -- including religious leaders -- does not justify legal liability.

**Masterpiece Cakeshop v. Colorado Civil Rights Commission, 584 U.S. 617 (2018):** While not a blasphemy case per se, this decision addressed the intersection of religious belief and public accommodation law. The Court ruled narrowly in favor of a baker who refused to create a wedding cake for a same-sex couple, finding that the Colorado commission had shown hostility toward the baker's religious views. The case illustrated how religious offense claims can arise in modern contexts far removed from traditional blasphemy frameworks.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany retains a blasphemy provision in its criminal code, Section 166 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), titled "Beschimpfung von Bekenntnissen, Religionsgesellschaften und Weltanschauungsvereinigungen" (Defamation of Religions, Religious Societies, and Ideological Associations).

**Section 166 StGB:**

- **Paragraph 1:** Whosoever publicly or through dissemination of content (Inhalte) defames the substance of the religious or ideological convictions of others in a manner suitable to disturb the public peace (den offentlichen Frieden zu storen) shall be punished with imprisonment for not more than three years or a fine.
- **Paragraph 2:** Whosoever publicly or through dissemination of content defames a church or other religious society or ideological association within Germany, its institutions, or customs in a manner suitable to disturb the public peace shall incur the same penalty.

**Key Elements of the Offense:**

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Beschimpfung (defamation/vilification)** | Must go beyond mere criticism; requires a particularly crass form of disparagement that denies any value to the targeted beliefs |
| **Offentlich (publicly)** | The statement must be made to an indefinite number of persons |
| **Geeignet, den offentlichen Frieden zu storen** | The act must be capable of disturbing public peace; actual disturbance is not required |
| **Inhalt (content)** | Covers written materials, images, audio, data storage, and digital content (amended 2020) |

### Key Principles

The public peace requirement (Friedensschutzklausel) is the most significant limitation on the scope of Section 166. German courts have interpreted this to mean that the provision does not protect religious feelings per se, but rather the peaceful coexistence of religious and ideological communities in society. Legitimate theological debate, scholarly criticism, and even provocative artistic expression are generally not captured by Section 166, provided they engage with religious content substantively rather than merely denigrating it.

The Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) has held that Section 166 must be interpreted narrowly in light of Article 5 of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz), which protects freedom of expression, freedom of the press, and freedom of art and scholarship. The provision protects only against the most extreme forms of vilification that threaten social cohesion.

In practice, Section 166 is rarely applied. Statistical data indicate approximately 15 adjudicated cases per year on average, with most resulting in fines rather than imprisonment.

### Notable Cases

**Manfred van H. Case (Bochum, 2006):** A man was convicted under Section 166 for distributing rolls of toilet paper printed with the word "Quran." The court found that the act constituted vilification of Islamic religious beliefs in a manner capable of disturbing public peace, and imposed a fine.

**"Pope Satire" Cases:** Several satirical publications targeting Pope Benedict XVI and the Catholic Church were investigated under Section 166 but resulted in dismissals, as courts found the satire constituted legitimate criticism protected under Article 5 GG rather than vilification.

**Debate on Abolition:** In 2020 and again in 2023, parliamentary motions were introduced to repeal Section 166, with proponents arguing that the provision is anachronistic and incompatible with a secular democracy. These motions have not yet secured a majority, though they reflect growing domestic debate. Notably, Ireland repealed its blasphemy law in 2018, and Denmark did so in 2017, adding momentum to the abolitionist argument within Europe.

### Related Provisions

Beyond Section 166, several other StGB provisions address religious-adjacent offenses:

| Provision | Description | Maximum Penalty |
|-----------|-------------|----------------|
| **Section 167 (Disturbance of Religious Practice)** | Intentionally and grossly disturbing a religious service or act of worship | Up to 3 years imprisonment |
| **Section 167a (Disturbance of Funeral)** | Intentionally and grossly disturbing a funeral ceremony | Up to 3 years imprisonment |
| **Section 168 (Desecration of Graves)** | Destroying or damaging a place of burial or public memorial | Up to 3 years imprisonment |
| **Section 130(1) (Incitement to Hatred)** | Inciting hatred against segments of the population, including religious groups | 3 months to 5 years imprisonment |

The interplay between Sections 166 and 130 is particularly significant: where anti-religious expression escalates from vilification of beliefs to incitement of hatred against believers, the more severe penalties under Section 130 apply. German prosecutors have increasingly preferred Section 130 charges over Section 166 in cases involving anti-Muslim or anti-Semitic expression, as the incitement framework avoids the philosophical difficulties of defining blasphemy while addressing the more tangible harm of hatred directed at persons.

## ECHR / EU Framework

### Convention Articles

The European Convention on Human Rights does not contain a specific blasphemy prohibition. The relevant provisions are:

- **Article 9 (Freedom of thought, conscience and religion):** Protects the right to manifest one's religion, subject to limitations prescribed by law and necessary in a democratic society for public safety, public order, health, morals, or the protection of the rights and freedoms of others.
- **Article 10 (Freedom of expression):** Protects the right to hold opinions and to receive and impart information and ideas, subject to restrictions prescribed by law and necessary in a democratic society, including for the protection of the rights of others.

The tension between Articles 9 and 10 lies at the heart of the ECHR's blasphemy jurisprudence: to what extent may states restrict expression (Article 10) in order to protect religious sensibilities (arguably linked to Article 9)?

### Key Case Law

**Otto-Preminger-Institut v. Austria, App. No. 13470/87 (1994):** The Court upheld Austria's seizure of a satirical film depicting God, Jesus, and the Virgin Mary in provocative scenarios. The Court held that states have an obligation to ensure the peaceful enjoyment of rights under Article 9, and that expressions gratuitously offensive to religious feelings could be restricted. This case established the "margin of appreciation" approach to blasphemy regulation.

**Wingrove v. United Kingdom, App. No. 17419/90 (1996):** The Court upheld the UK's refusal to grant a classification certificate to a video portraying the ecstatic visions of St. Teresa of Avila in an explicitly sexual manner. The Court reaffirmed the wide margin of appreciation in matters liable to offend intimate personal convictions within the sphere of morals or religion.

**I.A. v. Turkey, App. No. 42571/98 (2005):** The Court upheld Turkey's conviction of a publisher for a novel containing passages offensive to Islam. However, the Court noted that "those who choose to exercise the freedom to manifest their religion cannot reasonably expect to be exempt from all criticism."

**E.S. v. Austria, App. No. 38450/12 (2018):** In the most recent and controversial decision, the Court upheld Austria's conviction of a woman who, during a seminar, stated that the Prophet Muhammad "had a thing for little girls" -- a reference to his marriage to Aisha. The Court held that the domestic courts had carefully balanced the applicant's right to freedom of expression with the rights of others to have their religious feelings protected, and that the conviction was a proportionate interference with Article 10. The decision was widely criticized by free expression organizations, scholars, and commentators who argued the Court had effectively legitimized blasphemy laws through the doctrine of margin of appreciation.

### Member State Obligations

The ECHR does not require member states to have blasphemy laws, nor does it prohibit them. The Venice Commission (European Commission for Democracy through Law) has recommended that blasphemy laws be abolished, noting in its 2008 report that "it is neither necessary nor desirable to create an offence of religious insult" and that existing protections against incitement to hatred are sufficient. Despite this, several Council of Europe member states retain blasphemy provisions, including Germany (Section 166 StGB), Austria (Section 188 StGB), Finland (Chapter 17, Section 10 of the Criminal Code), Greece (Article 199 of the Criminal Code), and Italy (Article 724 of the Codice Penale, though significantly limited by Constitutional Court rulings).

Countries like Pakistan (Sections 295-298 of the Pakistan Penal Code, carrying a maximum sentence of death), Indonesia (Article 156a of the Criminal Code), and Saudi Arabia maintain far more severe blasphemy regimes. India's Section 295A of the Indian Penal Code (now Section 299 of the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita 2023) criminalizes deliberate acts intended to outrage religious feelings, with penalties of up to 3 years imprisonment. Japan has no blasphemy law. Brazil and Australia likewise lack blasphemy statutes, though Australia's state of Tasmania retains an unenforced provision.

### Philosophical and Policy Perspectives

The divergence in blasphemy law across these jurisdictions reflects fundamentally different conceptions of the relationship between the state, religion, and individual expression:

**Harm-Based vs. Dignity-Based Models:** The United States exemplifies a harm-based model, where speech restrictions are justified only by tangible harms (e.g., imminent violence) rather than offense to feelings. Germany and the ECHR framework blend harm-based and dignity-based considerations, requiring that the speech be capable of disturbing public peace -- a bridge between mere offense and tangible harm. South Korea's defamation-based approach protects individual reputation rather than religious sensibilities per se.

**Secularism and State Neutrality:** France's laicite model treats blasphemy prosecution as incompatible with state neutrality, having repealed its blasphemy law in 1791 during the Revolution. Germany's "cooperative secularism" model, by contrast, maintains partnerships between the state and major religious communities (through church tax and religious instruction in public schools), providing cultural context for the retention of Section 166.

**International Human Rights Standards:** The UN Human Rights Committee (General Comment No. 34, CCPR/C/GC/34, 2011) has stated unequivocally that "prohibitions of displays of lack of respect for a religion or other belief system, including blasphemy laws, are incompatible with the Covenant" (ICCPR), except where such expression constitutes advocacy of religious hatred that constitutes incitement to discrimination, hostility, or violence (Article 20(2) ICCPR). This creates a tension with the ECHR's margin of appreciation approach, which permits blasphemy laws that the ICCPR framework would prohibit.

**The "Defamation of Religions" Debate at the UN:** From 1999 to 2011, the Organisation of Islamic Cooperation (OIC) sponsored annual resolutions at the UN General Assembly and Human Rights Council on "combating defamation of religions." These resolutions were consistently opposed by Western democracies and ultimately replaced in 2011 by Resolution 16/18, which shifted focus from protecting religions to protecting individuals from discrimination, hostility, and violence based on religion.
