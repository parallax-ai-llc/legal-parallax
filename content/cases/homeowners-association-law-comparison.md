## Summary

Homeowners association (HOA) law governs the collective management of residential communities where individual unit or lot owners share ownership of or responsibility for common areas, facilities, and infrastructure, addressing governance structures, financial obligations, rule-making authority, dispute resolution, and the balance between community standards and individual property rights that arises when private entities exercise quasi-governmental regulatory power over their members' homes. South Korea regulates common-interest communities through the Multi-Family Housing Management Act (공동주택관리법, enacted 2016 as a standalone law separated from the Housing Act), which governs the management of apartment complexes (아파트) housing approximately 60 percent of the Korean population, establishing mandatory residents' representative councils (입주자대표회의), professional management requirements, long-term repair reserve funds (장기수선충당금), and transparent financial management obligations; the Act reflects the distinctive Korean housing landscape dominated by large apartment complexes of hundreds to thousands of units, creating governance challenges different from the smaller-scale communities typical in other jurisdictions. The United States regulates homeowners associations through the Uniform Common Interest Ownership Act (UCIOA, 1982/2008), the Uniform Condominium Act (1977/1980), and state-specific statutes, with approximately 75 million Americans living in over 365,000 community associations governed by recorded declarations of covenants, conditions, and restrictions (CC&Rs); the legal framework has been shaped by landmark cases including Nahrstedt v. Lakeside Village Condominium Association, 33 Cal.4th 1 (1994), which established a presumption of validity for recorded CC&R restrictions. Germany regulates condominium ownership through the Wohnungseigentumsgesetz (WEG, Condominium Ownership Act), fundamentally reformed on December 1, 2020, which establishes the Wohnungseigentümergemeinschaft (community of apartment owners) as a legal entity with capacity to sue and be sued, governed by the owners' assembly (Eigentümerversammlung) and managed by a professional administrator (Verwalter), with the reform significantly strengthening the community's powers regarding renovation, modernization, and energy-efficient building upgrades. The EU has not harmonized condominium or HOA law, though EU energy efficiency directives (particularly the Energy Performance of Buildings Directive 2010/31/EU as recast by Directive 2024/1275) increasingly affect multi-unit residential management, while the ECHR has addressed common-interest community issues under Article 8 (home) and Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (property).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's apartment management law reflects the country's distinctive housing landscape, where approximately 60 percent of the population lives in apartment complexes (아파트단지), many containing hundreds to thousands of units in high-rise buildings.

**Multi-Family Housing Management Act (공동주택관리법):** Enacted in 2016 as a standalone law separated from the Housing Act (주택법) to address the growing complexity of apartment management, the Act governs complexes of 300 or more units (or 150+ units with elevators or central heating). Key provisions include mandatory establishment of residents' representative councils, requirements for professional management companies or self-management, detailed financial management and transparency obligations, long-term repair planning and reserve fund requirements, and dispute resolution mechanisms.

### Governance Structure

**Residents' Representative Council (입주자대표회의):** The council consists of elected representatives (동별 대표자) from each building or section of the complex. The council exercises significant powers including selecting and supervising the management company, approving the annual management budget and fee structure, overseeing the long-term repair reserve fund, establishing community rules (관리규약), and representing residents in dealings with the management company and external parties.

**Management Company (관리사무소/위탁관리업체):** Complexes of 300+ units must be managed either by a licensed professional management company (위탁관리) or through self-management (자치관리) with qualified personnel. The management company is responsible for daily operations including maintenance, cleaning, security, parking management, and financial administration.

### Financial Management

The Act imposes detailed financial transparency requirements:

| Obligation | Requirement |
|-----------|------------|
| Monthly management fees (관리비) | Itemized billing with breakdown of costs by category |
| Long-term repair reserve (장기수선충당금) | Mandatory reserve based on 30-year repair plan; minimum contribution levels |
| Financial audit | Annual audit by external auditor for complexes above threshold size |
| Disclosure | Monthly financial reports posted in common areas; online disclosure through e-Apartment (공동주택관리정보시스템) |
| Procurement | Competitive bidding required for contracts above threshold amounts |

### Common Disputes

Apartment management disputes in South Korea are pervasive and often intense. The most common disputes involve noise between floors (층간소음), which has become a major social issue, prompting the enactment of the Floor Noise Prevention Act and the establishment of the Floor Noise Mediation Committee under the Korea Environment Corporation. Other common disputes concern management fee transparency, the selection and performance of management companies, unauthorized modifications to common areas, parking allocation, and the use of long-term repair reserves.

### Government Oversight

The Ministry of Land, Infrastructure and Transport (국토교통부) and local governments exercise supervisory authority over apartment management. Local governments may investigate management irregularities, order corrective action, and impose administrative penalties on management companies that violate the Act. The centralized e-Apartment system (공동주택관리정보시스템) provides public access to management data for all registered complexes.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has the world's largest community association sector, with over 365,000 associations governing approximately 75 million residents as of 2024.

**Types of Common-Interest Communities:**

| Type | Characteristic | Governance |
|------|---------------|-----------|
| Condominium | Individual ownership of units; shared ownership of common elements | Condominium association; unit owners' association |
| Planned community/HOA | Individual ownership of lots; common areas owned by association | Homeowners association (nonprofit corporation) |
| Cooperative | Corporation owns building; residents hold shares and proprietary leases | Cooperative board of directors |

**Uniform Common Interest Ownership Act (UCIOA):** Promulgated in 1982 and revised in 2008, the UCIOA provides a comprehensive model statute covering creation, governance, management, and termination of common-interest communities. Approximately 10 states have adopted the UCIOA, while others have adopted portions or developed independent statutory frameworks.

### CC&Rs and Rule-Making Authority

The declaration of covenants, conditions, and restrictions (CC&Rs) serves as the community's foundational document, analogous to a constitution. CC&Rs are recorded against the property and run with the land, binding all current and future owners. In Nahrstedt v. Lakeside Village Condominium Association, 33 Cal.4th 1 (1994), the California Supreme Court established that recorded CC&R restrictions are presumptively valid and enforceable unless they are wholly arbitrary, violate a fundamental public policy, or impose a burden that far outweighs any benefit to the community.

### Assessment and Lien Authority

HOAs derive revenue through regular assessments (monthly or annual dues) and special assessments for extraordinary expenses. In most states, unpaid assessments create a lien on the unit or lot that is superior to all other liens except property tax liens and first mortgage liens. Some states (approximately 20) authorize non-judicial foreclosure of assessment liens, a practice that has generated significant controversy and legislative reform efforts.

### Federal and State Regulation

**Fair Housing Act:** HOAs are subject to the Fair Housing Act (42 U.S.C. Sections 3601-3619), which prohibits discriminatory rules, enforcement, and governance practices. HUD and the Department of Justice have brought enforcement actions against associations for discriminatory covenant enforcement, unequal amenity access, and disability accommodation failures.

**State Regulatory Trends:** Recent state legislative trends include mandatory reserve studies and disclosure of reserve fund adequacy (California, Florida, Washington); restrictions on non-judicial foreclosure of assessment liens; requirements for open meetings and transparent governance; prohibitions on certain restrictive covenants (e.g., solar panel restrictions, flagpole restrictions, artificial turf restrictions); and mandatory alternative dispute resolution before litigation.

### Florida Surfside Tragedy and Regulatory Response

The collapse of Champlain Towers South in Surfside, Florida, on June 24, 2021, killing 98 people, exposed critical failures in condominium structural maintenance and reserve funding. The tragedy prompted Florida to enact SB 4-D (2022) and subsequent legislation requiring mandatory structural inspections for condominium buildings 30 years or older (25 years in coastal areas), mandatory reserve funding for structural components (eliminating the previous ability of owners to vote to waive reserves), and milestone inspections with public reporting requirements.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's condominium law underwent its most significant reform since original enactment through the Wohnungseigentumsmodernisierungsgesetz (WEMoG), effective December 1, 2020.

**Wohnungseigentumsgesetz (WEG):** The WEG, originally enacted in 1951 to facilitate post-war housing reconstruction, establishes the legal framework for dividing a building into individually owned units (Sondereigentum) and shared common property (Gemeinschaftseigentum). The 2020 reform fundamentally restructured the WEG, particularly regarding the legal status of the community, management authority, and renovation/modernization powers.

### 2020 Reform (WEMoG)

Key changes introduced by the WEMoG:

**Legal Personality:** The WEG community (Gemeinschaftseigentum) was reconceived as a fully legally capable entity (rechtsfähige Gemeinschaft, Section 9a WEG). The community can acquire rights and assume obligations, hold assets, and be a party to legal proceedings. This resolved longstanding uncertainty about the community's legal status.

**Enhanced Management Authority:** The administrator (Verwalter) received expanded powers to manage the common property and represent the community externally (Section 9b WEG). The administrator may undertake measures of ordinary administration without specific owner authorization.

**Renovation and Modernization:** The reform significantly liberalized the decision-making rules for renovation and modernization measures. Simple majority decisions (einfache Mehrheitsbeschlüsse) now suffice for most renovation and modernization measures, including energy-efficient building upgrades, installation of charging stations for electric vehicles, barrier-free modifications, and fiber-optic broadband connections. Previously, many such measures required qualified majority or even unanimous consent.

### Governance Structure

**Eigentümerversammlung (Owners' Assembly):** The owners' assembly is the supreme decision-making body of the WEG community. Each owner has voting rights proportional to their ownership share (Miteigentumsanteil). The assembly decides on the annual economic plan (Wirtschaftsplan), special assessments, appointment and dismissal of the administrator, maintenance and repair measures, community rules (Hausordnung), and legal proceedings.

**Verwalter (Administrator):** The administrator manages the common property and represents the community. The 2020 reform introduced a right for each owner to demand appointment of a certified administrator (zertifizierter Verwalter) meeting professional qualification requirements. The administrator prepares the annual economic plan, collects assessments, manages the community's accounts, and coordinates maintenance and repair work.

### Financial Management

The WEG community finances its operations through Wohngeld (common charges) paid by each owner according to their ownership share. The community maintains an Instandhaltungsrücklage (maintenance reserve) for future repair and replacement costs. The 2020 reform strengthened the framework for reserve funding and clarified that the community's assets (including the reserve fund) belong to the community as a legal entity rather than to the individual owners.

### Dispute Resolution

WEG disputes are heard by the Amtsgericht (local court) where the property is located (Section 43 WEG). Common disputes include challenges to owners' assembly resolutions (Beschlussanfechtungsklage), claims for payment of common charges, disputes over maintenance responsibilities, and conflicts between the community and the administrator.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Energy and Building Directives

While the EU has not harmonized HOA or condominium law, EU energy policy increasingly affects multi-unit residential management:

**Energy Performance of Buildings Directive (EPBD):** Directive 2010/31/EU, as recast by Directive (EU) 2024/1275, requires member states to establish minimum energy performance requirements for buildings, including existing buildings undergoing major renovation. For multi-unit residential buildings, compliance often requires collective decision-making by condominium or HOA governance structures, creating tensions between EU energy policy objectives and national property law.

**Renewable Energy Directive (EU) 2018/2001:** The Directive promotes the installation of renewable energy systems in buildings, including multi-unit residential buildings. This has implications for HOA and condominium governance, as the installation of solar panels or other renewable energy systems on common property typically requires collective approval.

**Electric Vehicle Infrastructure:** Directive 2014/94/EU on alternative fuel infrastructure and Regulation (EU) 2023/1804 require the deployment of electric vehicle charging infrastructure, including in residential buildings. Germany's WEG reform specifically addressed this by allowing individual owners to demand the installation of charging stations in common parking areas.

### ECHR and Community Association Rights

The ECHR has addressed community association issues under several Convention provisions:

**Article 8 (Home):** The Court has recognized that HOA or condominium rules that restrict how residents use their homes may engage Article 8 rights. In Kroon v. the Netherlands (Application No. 18535/91, 1994), while not directly addressing HOAs, the Court established principles regarding the state's positive obligation to protect individuals' enjoyment of their home that extend to the community association context.

**Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (Property):** HOA assessments, fines, and liens constitute interferences with property that must be prescribed by law and pursue a legitimate aim. The Court has afforded states a wide margin of appreciation in regulating common-interest communities but requires that restrictions be proportionate.

**Article 6 (Fair Trial):** Community association members are entitled to access to a fair and public hearing by an independent tribunal for the determination of their civil rights. Internal HOA dispute resolution mechanisms must not substitute for judicial access.

### Comparative EU Member State Approaches

EU member states take varying approaches to condominium/HOA regulation:

| Country | Key Features |
|---------|-------------|
| France | Loi du 10 juillet 1965 (copropriété); mandatory syndic (administrator); strict accounting rules |
| Spain | Ley de Propiedad Horizontal (1960/2013); comunidad de propietarios; qualified majority requirements |
| Italy | Codice Civile Articles 1117-1139 (condominio); reformed 2012; mandatory administrator for 8+ units |
| Netherlands | Vereniging van Eigenaars (VvE); mandatory reserve fund since 2018 |

## Comparative Analysis

### Community Scale and Governance

The scale of common-interest communities differs dramatically across jurisdictions, driving different governance challenges. South Korea's apartment complexes frequently contain thousands of units in dozens of high-rise buildings, creating governance structures resembling small municipalities. U.S. communities range from small townhouse developments to large planned communities with thousands of homes. German WEG communities are typically smaller, often comprising a single building with dozens of units. These scale differences affect governance complexity, financial management challenges, and the potential for disputes.

### Professional Management

The requirement for professional management varies across jurisdictions. South Korea mandates professional management for complexes of 300+ units, reflecting the large scale and complexity of Korean apartment management. Germany's 2020 reform introduced a right to demand a certified administrator, moving toward greater professionalization. U.S. law generally does not mandate professional management, though most larger communities voluntarily engage management companies.

### Financial Transparency

South Korea provides the most comprehensive financial transparency framework, with mandatory itemized billing, external audits, and centralized public disclosure through the e-Apartment system. Germany's WEG reform improved financial management standards but relies more on internal governance mechanisms. U.S. states increasingly require financial disclosure and reserve studies, particularly following the Surfside tragedy, but the level of mandatory transparency varies significantly.

### Renovation and Modernization

The ability to make collective decisions about building renovation and modernization has become increasingly important in the context of climate policy. Germany's 2020 WEG reform most directly addressed this challenge by lowering decision-making thresholds for energy-efficient upgrades. South Korea's long-term repair planning system facilitates systematic maintenance but does not specifically address energy modernization. U.S. HOA law varies significantly in the ease of approving major capital improvements.
