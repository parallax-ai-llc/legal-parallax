## Summary

The right to a speedy trial -- the guarantee that criminal proceedings will be conducted and concluded within a reasonable time -- serves multiple purposes: protecting the accused from prolonged uncertainty and the anxiety of pending charges, preventing the deterioration of evidence and the fading of witness memory, reducing the duration of pretrial detention, and maintaining public confidence in the administration of justice. South Korea enshrines the speedy trial right in Article 27(3) of the Constitution (대한민국 헌법), which provides that "all citizens shall have the right to a speedy trial," implemented through specific time limits in the Criminal Procedure Act (형사소송법) including maximum detention periods during trial and appellate proceedings, and enforced through the Constitutional Court's landmark Decision 93HeonBa12 (1997) establishing that unreasonable delay violates the constitutional right and may warrant dismissal. The United States protects the right through the Sixth Amendment to the Constitution ("the accused shall enjoy the right to a speedy and public trial") and the Speedy Trial Act of 1974 (18 U.S.C. Sections 3161-3174), which imposes specific time limits of 30 days from arrest to indictment and 70 days from indictment to trial in federal cases, with the Supreme Court's four-factor balancing test from Barker v. Wingo, 407 U.S. 514 (1972) governing constitutional speedy trial claims. Germany guarantees the right through the Beschleunigungsgrundsatz (acceleration principle) derived from Article 6(1) ECHR as incorporated through Article 20(3) of the Basic Law, with the Federal Constitutional Court requiring courts to give priority to cases involving pretrial detention and providing for compensation through sentence reduction or dismissal for unreasonable delay. The European Court of Human Rights has produced its most voluminous body of case law on the reasonable time requirement of Article 6(1), with hundreds of judgments finding violations across virtually every Council of Europe member state, applying criteria established in Neumeister v. Austria (1968) and refined in subsequent cases examining the complexity of the case, the conduct of the applicant, the conduct of the authorities, and what is at stake for the applicant.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

The right to a speedy trial is constitutionally guaranteed under Article 27(3) of the Constitution, which provides: "All citizens shall have the right to a speedy trial. The accused shall have the right to a public trial without delay in the absence of justifiable reasons to the contrary." This constitutional guarantee is implemented through specific procedural time limits in the Criminal Procedure Act and enforced through Constitutional Court review.

### Statutory Time Limits

The Criminal Procedure Act imposes specific maximum detention periods that function as de facto trial speed requirements:

| Proceeding Stage | Maximum Detention Period |
|-----------------|------------------------|
| First instance trial | 6 months (renewable) |
| Appeal (High Court) | 4 months (renewable) |
| Supreme Court review | 4 months (renewable) |

While these periods are renewable, courts must provide specific justifications for extension, and the Supreme Court has instructed lower courts to exercise restraint in granting renewals.

### Constitutional Court Jurisprudence

**Decision 93HeonBa12 (1997):** The Constitutional Court established that unreasonable delay in criminal proceedings violates the right to a speedy trial under Article 27(3) and may constitute grounds for dismissal of the prosecution. The Court identified factors relevant to the assessment: the length of delay, the reason for the delay, the defendant's assertion of the right, and the prejudice to the defendant.

**Decision 2008HeonMa798 (2010):** The Constitutional Court held that a delay of over five years between indictment and first-instance judgment, attributable primarily to court scheduling difficulties, violated Article 27(3). The Court ordered the lower court to expedite proceedings and noted that systemic court delays cannot justify prolonged infringement of the speedy trial right.

### Special Procedures for Detained Defendants

Under Article 92 of the Criminal Procedure Act, courts must give scheduling priority to cases involving detained defendants. The prosecution must commence trial proceedings within a reasonable time following indictment, and failure to do so may result in release of the defendant from pretrial detention.

### Criminal Procedure Reforms

The 2007 reform of the Criminal Procedure Act introduced several measures to accelerate proceedings, including mandatory pretrial conferences (공판준비절차) for complex cases, expanded use of written evidence submissions, and the concentration principle (집중심리주의) requiring continuous trial sessions rather than scattered hearing dates.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The U.S. speedy trial right operates on two levels: the constitutional right under the Sixth Amendment and the statutory right under the Speedy Trial Act.

### Sixth Amendment -- Constitutional Right

**Barker v. Wingo, 407 U.S. 514 (1972):** The Supreme Court established a four-factor balancing test for evaluating constitutional speedy trial claims:

1. **Length of the delay:** A threshold factor; the defendant must demonstrate that the delay is "presumptively prejudicial" (generally one year or more)
2. **Reason for the delay:** Government negligence weighs against the prosecution; deliberate delay weighs heavily against; legitimate reasons (complex case, missing witness) weigh in favor
3. **Defendant's assertion of the right:** Failure to assert the right may weigh against the claim, though it is not dispositive
4. **Prejudice to the defendant:** Including oppressive pretrial incarceration, anxiety and concern, and impairment of the defense

**Strunk v. United States, 412 U.S. 434 (1973):** The Court held that the only possible remedy for a violation of the constitutional speedy trial right is dismissal of the indictment, either with or without prejudice. This "drastic" remedy reflects the irreversible nature of the harm caused by unreasonable delay.

**Doggett v. United States, 505 U.S. 647 (1992):** The Court found a Sixth Amendment violation where the government negligently delayed prosecution for eight and a half years, holding that extreme delay creates a presumption of prejudice even without a showing of specific harm to the defense.

### Speedy Trial Act of 1974 (18 U.S.C. Sections 3161-3174)

The Speedy Trial Act imposes specific time limits for federal criminal proceedings:

| Stage | Time Limit |
|-------|-----------|
| Arrest to indictment | 30 days |
| Indictment to trial | 70 days |
| Total maximum (arrest to trial) | 100 days (approximate, with exclusions) |

### Excludable Time (18 U.S.C. Section 3161(h))

The Act contains detailed provisions for excludable time, including:
- Delays resulting from pretrial motions
- Delays resulting from mental competency examinations
- Delays resulting from interlocutory appeals
- Continuances granted in the interests of justice
- Delays resulting from the unavailability of essential witnesses
- Delays resulting from the joinder of co-defendants

### Sanctions

Under 18 U.S.C. Section 3162, failure to commence trial within the statutory time limits results in dismissal of the indictment or information. The court has discretion to dismiss with or without prejudice, considering the seriousness of the offense, the facts and circumstances leading to the dismissal, and the impact of reprosecution on the administration of justice.

### State Variation

State speedy trial provisions vary significantly. Some states impose specific time limits (e.g., California's Penal Code Section 1382 requires trial within 60 days for felonies), while others rely on constitutional standards similar to Barker v. Wingo.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany guarantees the right to trial within a reasonable time through the Beschleunigungsgrundsatz (acceleration principle), derived from the Rechtsstaatsprinzip (rule of law principle) under Article 20(3) of the Basic Law and the reasonable time guarantee of Article 6(1) ECHR, which has been incorporated into German domestic law through the ECHR's status as a federal statute and the Federal Constitutional Court's directive that the ECHR be used as an interpretive guide for fundamental rights.

### Acceleration Requirement in Detention Cases (Beschleunigungsgebot in Haftsachen)

For cases involving pretrial detention, the Federal Constitutional Court has imposed a particularly stringent acceleration requirement. The court must give priority to the case and schedule hearings at the earliest possible date. Failure to comply may result in the release of the detained person:

**BVerfG, 2 BvR 2299/09 (2010):** The Federal Constitutional Court ordered the release of a pretrial detainee where the trial court had failed to schedule hearings with sufficient frequency, holding that the right to liberty under Article 2(2) GG requires courts to proceed with particular urgency in detention cases.

**BVerfG, 2 BvR 1235/11 (2012):** The Court established that a delay of more than three months between the end of investigation and the commencement of trial in a detention case constitutes a prima facie violation of the acceleration principle, requiring specific justification from the court.

### Remedy: Sentence Reduction and Compensation

Prior to the enactment of the Act on Protection Against Unreasonable Length of Court Proceedings (Gesetz über den Rechtsschutz bei überlangen Gerichtsverfahren und strafrechtlichen Ermittlungsverfahren), enacted on November 24, 2011, the primary remedy for unreasonable delay was sentence reduction (Strafzumessungslösung), established by the BGH in BGHSt 35, 137 (1988). Following the ECHR's judgment in Sürmeli v. Germany (2006), which found a violation of Article 13 for the lack of an effective domestic remedy for excessive length of proceedings, Germany enacted the 2011 Act providing for monetary compensation of EUR 1,200 per year of unreasonable delay.

### Concentration Principle (Konzentrationsmaxime)

Section 229 StPO embodies the concentration principle, requiring that trial hearings be conducted without interruptions exceeding three weeks (extended to one month by the 2004 reform). If the interruption exceeds the permitted period, the entire trial must begin anew. This provision serves as a structural safeguard against unnecessarily prolonged proceedings.

### Statistics

German courts generally resolve criminal cases considerably faster than courts in many other jurisdictions. According to the Federal Statistical Office, the average duration of first-instance criminal proceedings in 2023 was approximately 5.5 months for cases before the Amtsgericht (local court) and 11.2 months for cases before the Landgericht (regional court).

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Article 6(1) -- "Reasonable Time"

Article 6(1) provides that "everyone is entitled to a fair and public hearing within a reasonable time by an independent and impartial tribunal." This provision has generated the largest volume of ECHR case law of any Convention right, with hundreds of judgments finding violations across virtually every member state.

### Assessment Criteria

**Neumeister v. Austria, Application No. 1936/63, Judgment of June 27, 1968:** The Court established the initial framework for assessing reasonable time, examining the complexity of the case and the conduct of the applicant and the authorities.

**König v. Germany, Application No. 6232/73, Judgment of June 28, 1978:** The Court added a fourth criterion: what is at stake for the applicant, with particular weight given where the applicant's liberty, livelihood, or family situation is affected by the delay.

**Frydlender v. France [GC], Application No. 30979/96, Judgment of June 27, 2000:** The Grand Chamber reiterated that states have a general obligation to organize their judicial systems so as to comply with the requirements of Article 6(1), and that a chronic backlog of cases does not constitute a justification for delay.

### Running of Time

The reasonable time period runs from the moment a person is "charged" (which the Court interprets autonomously, including arrest, formal charge, or the moment proceedings substantially affect the individual's situation) until the final determination of the charge (including all appeals).

### Pilot Judgments

Given the systemic nature of excessive length violations, the Court has issued pilot judgments requiring structural reforms:

**Rumpf v. Germany, Application No. 46344/06, Judgment of September 2, 2010:** The Court found that Germany lacked an effective domestic remedy for excessive length of proceedings and ordered Germany to introduce such a remedy within one year. This judgment prompted the enactment of the 2011 Act on Protection Against Unreasonable Length of Court Proceedings.

**Rutkowski and Others v. Poland, Application No. 72287/10, Judgment of July 7, 2015:** A pilot judgment addressing systemic excessive delay in Polish courts, requiring Poland to take general measures to resolve its backlog problem.

### EU Charter of Fundamental Rights

Article 47(2) of the EU Charter guarantees the right to a fair and public hearing within a reasonable time, mirroring Article 6(1) ECHR. The Court of Justice of the European Union has applied this provision in competition law proceedings, finding that excessive delay in Commission proceedings may warrant compensation (Gascogne Sack v. Commission, Case C-58/12 P (2013)).

## Comparative Analysis

### Fixed Time Limits vs. Flexible Standards

The United States provides the most specific time limits through the Speedy Trial Act (30 + 70 days), creating predictability but also generating extensive litigation over excludable time. South Korea imposes fixed maximum detention periods that indirectly regulate trial speed. Germany and the ECHR apply flexible proportionality standards that account for case complexity but may create less certainty.

### Remedies

The United States provides the most severe remedy: mandatory dismissal (with or without prejudice) for both constitutional and statutory violations. Germany has shifted from sentence reduction to compensation and, in extreme cases, dismissal. The ECHR primarily provides declaratory relief and monetary compensation under Article 41, though its pilot judgments have prompted systemic reforms.

### Systemic vs. Individual Focus

The ECHR has been most effective at addressing systemic delays through pilot judgments and structural reform requirements. The U.S. Speedy Trial Act focuses on individual cases with specific time limits. Germany's acceleration principle operates on a case-by-case basis but with strong institutional expectations.

### Pretrial Detention

All four systems recognize that detention creates a heightened obligation to proceed promptly. Germany and the ECHR (Article 5(3)) provide the strongest protections, with Germany requiring release when the acceleration requirement is not met. The U.S. Speedy Trial Act gives priority to detained defendants but does not provide for automatic release upon delay.
