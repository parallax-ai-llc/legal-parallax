## Summary

Sex offender registries -- systems requiring persons convicted of sexual offenses to provide personal information to law enforcement authorities, with varying degrees of public disclosure -- represent one of the most contentious areas of post-conviction regulation, balancing the state's interest in protecting communities (particularly children) from sexual violence against the offender's interests in privacy, rehabilitation, and freedom from perpetual punishment. South Korea established its sex offender registration and notification system through the Act on Special Cases Concerning the Punishment of Sexual Crimes (성폭력범죄의 처벌 등에 관한 특례법) and the Act on the Protection of Children and Youth Against Sex Offenses (아동·청소년의 성보호에 관한 법률), creating a publicly accessible online registry operated by the Ministry of Gender Equality and Family (여성가족부) that displays the offender's name, photograph, age, physical description, offense details, and residential address for periods ranging from 10 to 20 years depending on the severity of the offense, with lifetime registration for repeat offenders -- a system substantially strengthened following the Jo Doo-soon case (2008) and the Na Young case that generated intense public demand for stronger measures. The United States operates the most extensive sex offender registry system in the world through Megan's Law (1996), the Jacob Wetterling Act (1994), the Adam Walsh Child Protection and Safety Act of 2006 (codifying SORNA -- the Sex Offender Registration and Notification Act, 34 U.S.C. Sections 20911-20932), and state-level registries in all 50 states, with the Supreme Court upholding public notification in Connecticut Department of Public Safety v. Doe, 538 U.S. 1 (2003) and registration requirements in Smith v. Doe, 538 U.S. 84 (2003) as non-punitive regulatory measures not subject to the Ex Post Facto Clause -- though state courts have increasingly challenged this characterization, notably the Pennsylvania Supreme Court in Commonwealth v. Muniz (2017). Germany does not maintain a public sex offender registry, relying instead on the Führungszeugnis (certificate of good conduct) system under the Bundeszentralregistergesetz (BZRG), the extended certificate (erweitertes Führungszeugnis) for individuals working with children under Section 30a BZRG, and post-release supervision (Führungsaufsicht) under Sections 68-68g StGB as its mechanisms for managing convicted sex offenders in the community. The ECHR has addressed sex offender registration under Article 8 (private life), holding in cases such as Gardel v. France (2009) and Bouchacourt v. France (2009) that registration requirements are permissible interferences with private life provided they pursue a legitimate aim and are proportionate.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's sex offender registry system has been progressively strengthened since its establishment, driven by high-profile sexual violence cases that generated intense public demand for accountability and transparency.

### Registration and Notification System

The registration system operates under two primary statutes:

**Act on Special Cases Concerning the Punishment of Sexual Crimes (성폭력범죄의 처벌 등에 관한 특례법):** Establishes registration requirements for adult sex offenders, including mandatory reporting of personal information to police and the disclosure system.

**Act on the Protection of Children and Youth Against Sex Offenses (아동·청소년의 성보호에 관한 법률):** Establishes enhanced registration and notification for offenses against minors.

### Registration Requirements

| Offense Category | Registration Period | Public Disclosure |
|-----------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Basic sexual offense | 10 years | Yes, online registry |
| Aggravated sexual offense | 15 years | Yes, online registry |
| Sexual offense against minor | 20 years | Yes, online registry + neighborhood notification |
| Repeat sex offenders | Lifetime | Yes, all disclosure mechanisms |

### Online Registry

The Ministry of Gender Equality and Family (여성가족부) operates the Sex Offender Information Disclosure System (성범죄자 알림e), an online portal accessible to the public that displays:
- Full name and photograph
- Age and physical description
- Summary of offense
- Current residential address
- Employment information
- Vehicle registration (for certain offenses)

Access to the registry is available to all adults who verify their identity through the national identification system.

### Neighborhood Notification (고지제도)

For offenses against children, the system includes direct neighborhood notification: residents within the offender's community receive mail or electronic notification containing the offender's information upon the offender's release or residential relocation.

### Electronic Monitoring

Since 2008, convicted sex offenders may be subject to electronic monitoring (GPS ankle bracelet) under the Electronic Monitoring Act (전자장치 부착 등에 관한 법률). As of 2025, approximately 2,800 sex offenders are subject to electronic monitoring nationwide. The monitoring period ranges from 1 to 30 years and may be imposed in addition to the criminal sentence and registration requirements.

### Chemical Castration

South Korea became the first country in Asia to authorize chemical castration (성충동 약물치료) through the Act on Pharmacological Treatment of Sex Offenders (성폭력범죄자의 성충동 약물치료에 관한 법률, 2010), effective July 2011. The measure may be ordered by a court for sex offenders assessed as having a high risk of reoffending, with a maximum duration of 15 years. The Constitutional Court upheld the constitutionality of chemical castration in Decision 2012HeonBa81 (2015), finding it proportionate given the state's interest in preventing sexual violence.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States maintains the most comprehensive and expansive sex offender registry system in the world, operating at federal, state, and local levels.

### Federal Legislative History

**Jacob Wetterling Crimes Against Children and Sexually Violent Offender Registration Act (1994):** The first federal sex offender registration law, requiring states to establish registration programs for sex offenders. Named after 11-year-old Jacob Wetterling, abducted in Minnesota in 1989.

**Megan's Law (1996):** Named after 7-year-old Megan Kanka, murdered by a neighbor with prior sex convictions in New Jersey in 1994. The law amended the Wetterling Act to require states to establish community notification systems.

**Adam Walsh Child Protection and Safety Act (2006):** The most comprehensive federal legislation, establishing SORNA (Sex Offender Registration and Notification Act) with a three-tier classification system:

| Tier | Offenses | Registration Duration | Verification |
|------|----------|----------------------|-------------|
| Tier I | Misdemeanor sex offenses; voyeurism | 15 years | Annual |
| Tier II | Sexual offenses against minors; distribution of child pornography | 25 years | Semi-annual |
| Tier III | Aggravated sexual abuse; sexual abuse of children under 13 | Lifetime | Quarterly |

### Constitutional Status

**Smith v. Doe, 538 U.S. 84 (2003):** The Supreme Court held (6-3) that Alaska's sex offender registration and public notification law is non-punitive and therefore not subject to the Ex Post Facto Clause. Justice Kennedy's majority opinion applied the "intent-effects" test, finding that the legislature intended the law to be civil and regulatory rather than punitive, and that the regulatory scheme is not so punitive in purpose or effect as to negate the state's intention.

**Connecticut Department of Public Safety v. Doe, 538 U.S. 1 (2003):** The Court held that convicted sex offenders are not entitled to a hearing to determine their current dangerousness before being included on a publicly disseminated registry, because the registry is based on conviction status rather than individualized risk assessment.

### State Court Challenges

State courts have increasingly challenged the non-punitive characterization:

**Commonwealth v. Muniz, 164 A.3d 1189 (Pa. 2017):** The Pennsylvania Supreme Court held that SORNA's registration requirements constitute punishment under the Pennsylvania Constitution, making retroactive application a violation of the ex post facto prohibition.

**Does v. Snyder, 834 F.3d 696 (6th Cir. 2016):** The Sixth Circuit found that Michigan's sex offender registry amendments (adding restrictions and extending durations) constituted punishment, invalidating retroactive application.

### Residency Restrictions

Many jurisdictions impose residency restrictions on registered sex offenders, prohibiting them from living within specified distances (typically 500 to 2,500 feet) of schools, parks, daycare centers, and other locations where children congregate. These restrictions have generated significant criticism for creating "exclusion zones" that effectively render sex offenders homeless or push them into concentrations in limited areas.

### National Sex Offender Public Website (NSOPW)

The NSOPW, operated by the U.S. Department of Justice, provides a single online portal for searching sex offender registries across all 50 states, the District of Columbia, U.S. territories, and participating tribal nations.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany does not maintain a public sex offender registry, reflecting a fundamentally different approach that prioritizes rehabilitation and privacy over public notification. The German framework relies on several alternative mechanisms for managing sex offender risk.

### Extended Certificate of Good Conduct (Erweitertes Führungszeugnis) -- Section 30a BZRG

Introduced in 2010 in response to institutional abuse scandals, the extended Führungszeugnis includes convictions for sexual offenses, offenses against personal liberty, and offenses against life and physical integrity regardless of the sentence imposed (whereas ordinary Führungszeugnisse exclude minor convictions). This certificate is required for:
- Persons seeking employment involving supervision of, or contact with, minors
- Volunteers working with children and youth
- Persons working in schools, childcare, and youth welfare institutions

The extended certificate provides targeted disclosure to institutions responsible for child protection without general public notification.

### Post-Release Supervision (Führungsaufsicht) -- StGB Sections 68-68g

Führungsaufsicht is a supervisory measure that may be imposed by the court upon the release of sex offenders from prison:

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Duration | 2-5 years; unlimited following life sentence release |
| Supervision authority | Probation officer + Führungsaufsichtsstelle (supervision agency) |
| Conditions | Reporting requirements; residence restrictions; therapy participation; contact prohibitions |
| Electronic monitoring | Available since 2011 for high-risk cases |
| Violation consequence | Up to 3 years imprisonment (Section 145a StGB) |

### Preventive Detention (Sicherungsverwahrung)

For particularly dangerous sex offenders, Sections 66-66c StGB permit preventive detention following completion of the prison sentence, providing the most intensive risk management available in the German system. The reformed provisions (post-2013) require therapeutic and rehabilitative programs during detention.

### No Public Notification

German law does not permit public notification of sex offenders' identities or residences. The BZRG's removal provisions (Sections 45-47) ensure that convictions are removed from the register after specified periods, and Section 51 BZRG prohibits using removed convictions against the person. This approach reflects Germany's constitutional emphasis on human dignity (Article 1(1) GG) and the rehabilitation principle, which the Federal Constitutional Court has recognized as a component of the right to free development of personality (Article 2(1) GG).

### Debate on Registry Introduction

Periodic calls for a U.S.-style sex offender registry have been made in Germany, particularly following high-profile cases, but have been consistently rejected by legal scholars and policymakers on grounds that:
- Public registries have not been shown to reduce recidivism
- Registries may increase recidivism by undermining reintegration
- Public notification violates the rehabilitation principle and human dignity
- The extended Führungszeugnis provides targeted protection where needed

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Article 8 -- Sex Offender Registration

The ECHR has addressed sex offender registration requirements under the right to private life:

**Gardel v. France, Application No. 16428/05, Judgment of December 17, 2009:** The Court upheld France's sex offender registry (FIJAISV -- Fichier judiciaire automatisé des auteurs d'infractions sexuelles ou violentes) as a proportionate interference with Article 8. The Court considered the legitimate aim of preventing sexual offenses, the limited nature of the data stored, the restricted access to the registry, and the possibility of removal after 30 years (or 20 years for minors).

**Bouchacourt v. France, Application No. 5335/06, Judgment of December 17, 2009:** Companion case to Gardel, similarly finding no violation where the registration system was accompanied by adequate safeguards.

**S. and Marper v. United Kingdom [GC], Applications Nos. 30562/04, 30566/04, Judgment of December 4, 2008:** While primarily addressing DNA retention, the Grand Chamber established important principles regarding the indefinite retention of personal data of persons not convicted, finding a violation of Article 8. The principles regarding data minimization and proportionate retention periods are directly relevant to sex offender registries.

### EU Directive 2011/93/EU

Directive 2011/93/EU on combating the sexual abuse and sexual exploitation of children requires member states to ensure that persons convicted of child sexual offenses may be temporarily or permanently prevented from exercising professional activities involving direct and regular contact with children. The Directive does not require public registries but mandates effective mechanisms for ensuring that convicted offenders do not gain unsupervised access to children through employment.

### Council of Europe Convention on the Protection of Children (Lanzarote Convention)

The Council of Europe Convention on the Protection of Children against Sexual Exploitation and Sexual Abuse (Lanzarote Convention, CETS No. 201, 2007) requires states parties to:
- Maintain records of persons convicted of sexual offenses against children (Article 37)
- Ensure that employers can verify whether candidates have been convicted of sexual offenses against children
- Cooperate internationally in sharing conviction information

## Comparative Analysis

### Public vs. Private Registries

The most fundamental divergence is between public registries (South Korea, United States) and non-public systems (Germany, most of Europe). The United States and South Korea prioritize community awareness and self-protective behavior, while Germany prioritizes offender rehabilitation and privacy. The ECHR permits both approaches but requires proportionality.

### Duration and Scope

U.S. registration requirements are the most extensive, with lifetime registration for Tier III offenders and broad public disclosure. South Korea's 10-20 year periods are substantial but shorter. Germany has no registration period, relying on time-limited supervision and the automatic removal of convictions from the register.

### Risk Assessment

The U.S. SORNA system classifies offenders by offense type rather than individualized risk assessment, which critics argue leads to over-registration of low-risk offenders and under-management of high-risk offenders. Germany's approach, using Führungsaufsicht and Sicherungsverwahrung, is based on individualized judicial assessment of dangerousness.

### Effectiveness Evidence

Research on the effectiveness of sex offender registries is mixed. A 2008 meta-analysis by Zgoba, Witt, Dalessandro, and Veysey found no significant effect of New Jersey's Megan's Law on sex offense rates. Research by Prescott and Rockoff (2011) found that registration may reduce crime by higher-risk offenders but that community notification may increase recidivism by disrupting offender stability. Germany's non-registry approach has not produced higher sex offense rates than registry jurisdictions.
