## Summary

Environmental impact assessment (EIA) -- the systematic process of evaluating the likely environmental effects of a proposed project or plan before a decision is made on whether to proceed -- has become a near-universal requirement in environmental governance, with over 190 countries implementing some form of EIA legislation since the United States pioneered the concept with the National Environmental Policy Act of 1969. South Korea's EIA system is governed by the Environmental Impact Assessment Act (환경영향평가법), which establishes a three-tiered assessment framework comprising strategic environmental assessment (전략환경영향평가) for plans and policies, environmental impact assessment (환경영향평가) for major development projects, and small-scale environmental impact assessment (소규모 환경영향평가) for minor projects, with the Korean Environment Institute (한국환경연구원) providing technical support and the Ministry of Environment exercising review authority over assessment reports. The United States conducts environmental review under the National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA, 42 U.S.C. Sections 4321-4370m), which requires federal agencies to prepare Environmental Impact Statements (EIS) for "major Federal actions significantly affecting the quality of the human environment," with the process shaped by decades of judicial interpretation and the 2020 CEQ regulatory revisions (partially revised again in 2024) that significantly altered the scope and procedural requirements. Germany implements environmental impact assessment through the Gesetz über die Umweltverträglichkeitsprüfung (UVPG, EIA Act) transposing the EU EIA Directive (2011/92/EU as amended by Directive 2014/52/EU), with the EU framework also requiring Strategic Environmental Assessment under Directive 2001/42/EC for plans and programmes, and the Aarhus Convention guaranteeing public participation in environmental decision-making and access to justice.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's EIA system is governed by the Environmental Impact Assessment Act (환경영향평가법, Act No. 19953), which was substantially revised in 2012 to create the current three-tiered structure. The Act is administered by the Ministry of Environment (환경부) with technical support from the Korean Environment Institute (한국환경연구원, KEI) and the National Institute of Environmental Research (국립환경과학원).

### Three-Tiered Assessment System

| Tier | Korean Term | Application | Review Period |
|------|-------------|-------------|---------------|
| Strategic Environmental Assessment | 전략환경영향평가 | Government plans and policies (development plans, land use plans) | 30 days |
| Environmental Impact Assessment | 환경영향평가 | 17 categories of major development projects | 45 days |
| Small-scale Environmental Impact Assessment | 소규모 환경영향평가 | Projects in environmentally sensitive areas below EIA thresholds | 20 days |

### EIA Process

The EIA process for major projects includes:
1. **Scoping (범위설정):** Determination of assessment scope through consultation with the Ministry of Environment and affected communities
2. **Draft Assessment Report:** Preparation by the project proponent (often through licensed environmental consulting firms)
3. **Public Review and Hearing (주민의견 수렴):** Mandatory public notice and hearing with a minimum 40-day public review period; residents within the project area and surrounding 1km radius are notified
4. **Ministry Review (환경부 협의):** The Ministry of Environment reviews the report and issues an agreement (협의 의견) with binding conditions
5. **Post-assessment Monitoring (사후환경영향조사):** The proponent must conduct monitoring and report results to the Ministry

### Consultation Agreement System

The Ministry of Environment's consultation agreement (협의 의견) is legally binding on the project proponent. If the proponent fails to comply with conditions, the Ministry may request the approval authority to revoke or suspend the project approval. This gives the Ministry significant leverage over development decisions made by other government agencies.

### Challenges

Critics have argued that Korea's EIA system is often treated as a procedural formality rather than a substantive constraint on development, with assessment reports frequently prepared after the decision to proceed has effectively been made. The 2012 reforms attempted to address this through strategic environmental assessment at the planning stage, but implementation remains uneven.

## United States

### Legal Framework

NEPA, signed into law on January 1, 1970, is often called the "Magna Carta" of environmental law. Section 102(2)(C) requires federal agencies to prepare an Environmental Impact Statement for "major Federal actions significantly affecting the quality of the human environment." The Council on Environmental Quality (CEQ) issues implementing regulations (40 C.F.R. Parts 1500-1508).

### Three-Level Review

| Level | Trigger | Output |
|-------|---------|--------|
| Categorical Exclusion (CatEx) | Actions that normally do not have significant environmental effects | Brief documentation |
| Environmental Assessment (EA) | Actions that may or may not have significant effects | Finding of No Significant Impact (FONSI) or decision to prepare EIS |
| Environmental Impact Statement (EIS) | Major federal actions significantly affecting the environment | Full EIS with alternatives analysis and public comment |

### Alternatives Analysis

The alternatives analysis is considered the "heart" of the EIS (40 C.F.R. 1502.14). Agencies must:
- Rigorously explore and objectively evaluate all reasonable alternatives
- Include a "no action" alternative
- Identify the agency's preferred alternative
- Analyze the environmental impacts of each alternative in comparative form

### 2020 and 2024 CEQ Regulatory Revisions

The Trump administration's 2020 CEQ rule (85 Fed. Reg. 43304) significantly revised NEPA regulations for the first time since 1978, including: eliminating the requirement to consider cumulative impacts, imposing page limits (150/300 pages) and time limits (1/2 years), narrowing the definition of "effects," and creating categorical exclusions for certain projects. The Biden administration partially reversed these changes in 2024 (89 Fed. Reg. 35442), but the Fiscal Responsibility Act of 2023 codified some streamlining provisions.

### Landmark Cases

**Robertson v. Methow Valley Citizens Council, 490 U.S. 332 (1989):** The Supreme Court held that NEPA is a procedural statute that does not require agencies to adopt environmentally preferable alternatives -- it only requires that environmental consequences be considered in the decision-making process.

**Calvert Cliffs' Coordinating Committee v. AEC, 449 F.2d 1109 (D.C. Cir. 1971):** The seminal case establishing that NEPA imposes substantive obligations on agencies to consider environmental impacts, not merely to prepare documents.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's EIA system is governed by the Gesetz über die Umweltverträglichkeitsprüfung (UVPG), which transposes the EU EIA Directive and SEA Directive. The UVPG was substantially revised in 2017 to implement Directive 2014/52/EU. EIA is integrated into the existing approval procedures (integrierte Verfahrensgestaltung) rather than operating as a separate process.

### EIA Procedure

**Screening (Vorprüfung):** Projects listed in Anlage 1 UVPG above specified thresholds require mandatory EIA. Projects below thresholds undergo a case-by-case screening (Einzelfallprüfung) to determine whether EIA is required.

**Scoping (Unterrichtung über den Untersuchungsrahmen):** The competent authority determines the scope and depth of the assessment in consultation with the project proponent and other authorities (Section 15 UVPG).

**Environmental Impact Study (Umweltverträglichkeitsprüfung):** The proponent prepares the EIA report (UVP-Bericht), which the authority evaluates as part of the overall approval decision. The EIA is not a separate decision but an integral part of the Genehmigungsverfahren (approval procedure).

**Public Participation:** The UVPG and the Verwaltungsverfahrensgesetz (VwVfG, Administrative Procedure Act) require public display of documents and opportunity for written and oral comment. Environmental organizations with recognized standing (anerkannte Umweltvereinigungen) have enhanced participation and litigation rights under the Umwelt-Rechtsbehelfsgesetz (UmwRG).

### Strategic Environmental Assessment (SUP)

Part 3 of the UVPG implements the SEA Directive, requiring strategic assessment of plans and programmes likely to have significant environmental effects. This includes spatial development plans, transport infrastructure plans, waste management plans, and water management plans.

### Environmental Organizations' Standing

The Umwelt-Rechtsbehelfsgesetz (UmwRG) grants recognized environmental organizations standing to challenge administrative decisions under the EIA Directive and other environmental EU law, implementing the Aarhus Convention's access to justice provisions. This has enabled organizations like BUND and Deutsche Umwelthilfe to bring significant environmental litigation.

## ECHR & EU

### EIA Directive (2011/92/EU, amended by 2014/52/EU)

The EU EIA Directive establishes the fundamental framework:

**Annex I Projects:** Mandatory EIA for large-scale projects including refineries, power stations above 300 MW thermal, motorways, airports, chemical installations, and waste disposal facilities.

**Annex II Projects:** Member states must determine whether EIA is required through screening, considering criteria in Annex III (characteristics of the project, location sensitivity, characteristics of potential impact).

**2014 Amendment Key Changes:**
- Enhanced screening procedure with reasoned conclusion
- Mandatory assessment of climate change impacts and vulnerability
- Strengthened public participation with reasonable time frames
- Monitoring requirements for significant adverse environmental effects
- Conflicts of interest provisions for competent authorities

### SEA Directive (2001/42/EC)

The SEA Directive requires environmental assessment of plans and programmes prepared by public authorities that are likely to have significant environmental effects. This includes plans required by legislative, regulatory, or administrative provisions and plans that set the framework for future development consent of projects listed in the EIA Directive.

### Aarhus Convention

The UNECE Convention on Access to Information, Public Participation in Decision-Making and Access to Justice in Environmental Matters (Aarhus Convention, 1998) provides three interconnected rights:
1. **Access to environmental information** (Articles 4-5)
2. **Public participation in environmental decision-making** (Articles 6-8)
3. **Access to justice in environmental matters** (Article 9)

The EU ratified the Convention in 2005, and its provisions are reflected in the EIA and SEA Directives.

### CJEU Enforcement

**Kraaijeveld (C-72/95, 1996):** The Court held that member states may not set screening thresholds so high that entire classes of projects are exempted from EIA, regardless of their potential environmental impact.

**Wells (C-201/02, 2004):** Established that failure to carry out a required EIA can result in the suspension or revocation of the project's development consent.

**Commission v. Ireland (C-215/06, 2008):** Ireland found in breach for systematic failure to subject wind farm and peat extraction projects to EIA screening.

### ECHR and EIA

**Hatton and Others v. United Kingdom (Application No. 36022/97, Grand Chamber, 2003):** While concerning noise from Heathrow Airport night flights, the Grand Chamber held that Article 8 requires states to consider environmental impacts in decision-making, implicitly supporting the value of EIA processes.

**Taskin and Others v. Turkey (Application No. 46117/99, 2004):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where Turkish authorities authorized a gold mine to operate despite an EIA process that identified significant risks, establishing that the existence of an EIA process creates legitimate expectations.

## Comparative Analysis

### Substantive vs. Procedural Requirements

The fundamental philosophical divide is between NEPA's procedural approach (requiring agencies to consider but not necessarily avoid environmental harm) and the EU's more substantive framework (where EIA outcomes can preclude project approval). South Korea's system falls between these poles, with the Ministry of Environment's binding consultation agreement providing substantive leverage within a procedural framework. Germany's integration of EIA into the approval procedure gives it direct influence on substantive decisions.

### Scope of Assessment

NEPA's scope is limited to "major federal actions," excluding purely private or state actions without a federal nexus. The EU EIA Directive covers public and private projects above specified thresholds regardless of government involvement. South Korea's system covers designated categories of projects meeting size thresholds. Germany's UVPG scope mirrors the EU Directive's project-based approach.

### Strategic Assessment

South Korea and the EU both require strategic-level assessment for plans and policies, addressing a recognized limitation of project-level EIA -- by the time individual projects are assessed, strategic decisions about development patterns have already been made. NEPA can be applied to "programmatic" federal actions but has no separate SEA requirement.

### Public Participation

All jurisdictions require public participation, but the mechanisms differ. The EU's Aarhus Convention framework provides the most robust legal basis for public access to information, participation, and justice. Germany's UmwRG provides organizational standing for environmental groups. NEPA requires public comment on draft EIS documents. South Korea requires public hearings with a 40-day review period.
