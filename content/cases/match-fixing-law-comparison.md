## Summary

Match-fixing and sports corruption represent a growing threat to the integrity of competitive sport, with the global illegal sports betting market estimated at over USD 1.7 trillion annually. Jurisdictions have adopted fundamentally different approaches to criminalization and enforcement, ranging from specific standalone offenses to application of general fraud and corruption statutes. The Council of Europe's Convention on the Manipulation of Sports Competitions (Macolin Convention, CETS No. 215, 2014) represents the first international treaty specifically targeting match-fixing, establishing minimum standards for criminalization, prevention, and cooperation. South Korea enacted specific match-fixing provisions through the National Sports Promotion Act (국민체육진흥법) and has prosecuted some of the most high-profile match-fixing scandals in Asian football and baseball. The United States lacks a dedicated federal match-fixing statute but applies the federal wire fraud statute (18 U.S.C. § 1343), sports bribery statute (18 U.S.C. § 224), and RICO (18 U.S.C. § 1961 et seq.) to sports corruption cases. Germany criminalized sports betting fraud through § 265c and § 265d of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), introduced in 2017, making it one of the few countries with specific criminal provisions targeting the manipulation of professional sports competitions.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea has been at the forefront of match-fixing enforcement in Asia, driven by a series of devastating scandals that shook Korean professional sports:

- **National Sports Promotion Act (국민체육진흥법) Article 47:** Criminalizes match-fixing in sports competitions, imposing penalties of up to 7 years imprisonment or fines of up to KRW 70 million for any person who manipulates or attempts to manipulate the result of a sports competition through bribery, threats, or other improper means. The article covers athletes, coaches, referees, team officials, and any third parties who instigate or facilitate match-fixing.
- **National Sports Promotion Act Article 48:** Establishes additional penalties including lifetime bans from sports activities, forfeiture of prizes and awards, and disgorgement of illegal profits. Article 48-2 creates a duty for sports organizations to report suspected match-fixing to the authorities.
- **Criminal Act (형법) Article 347 (Fraud):** Applied alongside the National Sports Promotion Act where match-fixing involves fraudulent obtaining of betting proceeds or other financial benefits through deception. Penalties range up to 10 years imprisonment or fines of up to KRW 20 million.
- **Act on Special Cases Concerning the Punishment of Specific Economic Crimes (특정경제범죄 가중처벌 등에 관한 법률):** Provides enhanced penalties where the financial benefit from match-fixing exceeds KRW 500 million, with maximum penalties of up to life imprisonment.
- **National Gambling Control Commission Act (사행산업통합감독위원회법):** Establishes the regulatory framework for legal sports betting through Sports Toto (operated by Korea Sports Promotion Foundation), with provisions for detecting irregular betting patterns that may indicate match-fixing.

### Institutional Framework

South Korea's anti-match-fixing infrastructure includes:

- **Korea Sports Ethics Center (한국스포츠윤리센터, established 2020):** Created following the broader sports abuse scandal, this independent body investigates integrity violations including match-fixing, doping, and abuse in Korean sports. It operates a confidential reporting hotline and has the authority to refer cases to prosecutors.
- **Sports Toto Integrity Monitoring System:** Korea Sports Promotion Foundation operates a real-time betting monitoring system that detects irregular betting patterns and shares alerts with law enforcement and sports governing bodies.
- **Prosecution Service Special Investigation Division:** The Seoul Central District Prosecutors' Office has a specialized unit for sports-related criminal investigations.

### Notable Cases

- **2011 K-League Match-Fixing Scandal:** The largest match-fixing scandal in Korean football history, involving over 50 players across multiple K-League clubs who were found to have fixed matches in exchange for payments from illegal gambling operators. Prosecutors secured 46 indictments. Key defendant Sung Hyung-chul received 10 years imprisonment. The scandal led to the relegation of clubs and lifetime bans for dozens of players. The Korean Football Association (KFA) and FIFA cooperated in the investigation.
- **2012 Korean Baseball (KBO) Match-Fixing Scandal:** Multiple players in the Korea Baseball Organization were prosecuted for fixing games in connection with illegal gambling syndicates. The Suwon District Court convicted several players, with sentences ranging from 2 to 5 years imprisonment. The KBO subsequently implemented enhanced integrity programs including mandatory education and monitoring.
- **2016 Volleyball Match-Fixing Prosecution:** Professional volleyball players were convicted under Article 47 of the National Sports Promotion Act for manipulating set scores in exchange for payments. The Seoul Central District Court imposed prison sentences of 1.5 to 3 years.
- **2023 e-Sports Match-Fixing Investigation:** Prosecutors investigated allegations of match-fixing in professional League of Legends competitions, marking the expansion of match-fixing enforcement into e-sports. Charges were brought under both the National Sports Promotion Act and the Criminal Act fraud provisions.

## United States

### Federal Framework

The United States relies on general federal criminal statutes rather than sport-specific match-fixing legislation:

- **18 U.S.C. § 224 (Sports Bribery Act, 1964):** The only federal statute specifically targeting sports corruption. Criminalizes bribery to influence the outcome of "any sporting contest" with a maximum penalty of 5 years imprisonment and $250,000 fine. The statute applies to "any person" who carries, delivers, or receives bribes to influence a sporting contest involving one or more teams traveling in interstate or foreign commerce. However, § 224 has been rarely prosecuted due to its narrow scope and the availability of more powerful general statutes.
- **18 U.S.C. § 1343 (Wire Fraud):** The most frequently used federal statute in match-fixing prosecutions. Criminalizes any scheme to defraud using interstate wire communications (including internet betting), with penalties of up to 20 years imprisonment. The "scheme to defraud" element captures match-fixing where the manipulation is used to obtain money through betting.
- **18 U.S.C. § 1341 (Mail Fraud):** Parallel to wire fraud, applicable where physical mail is used in furtherance of the scheme.
- **18 U.S.C. § 1961-1968 (RICO, Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act):** Applied to organized match-fixing operations involving a "pattern of racketeering activity" (at least two predicate offenses within 10 years). RICO provides enhanced penalties of up to 20 years imprisonment per count, asset forfeiture, and civil treble damages.
- **18 U.S.C. § 1084 (Wire Act, 1961):** Prohibits the use of wire communications for transmitting bets or wagering information in interstate or foreign commerce. Applied in match-fixing cases where illegal sports betting operations cross state lines.
- **18 U.S.C. § 1952 (Travel Act):** Criminalizes interstate or foreign travel in aid of racketeering enterprises, applicable where match-fixing conspirators travel across state lines.

### Post-Murphy v. NCAA Landscape

The Supreme Court's decision in Murphy v. National Collegiate Athletic Association, 584 U.S. 453 (2018) struck down the Professional and Amateur Sports Protection Act (PASPA, 28 U.S.C. § 3701-3704) as violating the Tenth Amendment's anti-commandeering principle. This opened the door for states to legalize sports betting, with over 38 states and the District of Columbia authorizing some form of legal sports wagering by 2025. The expansion of legal sports betting has both facilitated integrity monitoring (through regulated betting operators' suspicious activity reporting) and increased the surface area for potential corruption.

State-level integrity measures include:

- **New Jersey Sports Wagering Law (N.J.S.A. 5:12A):** Requires licensed operators to report suspicious betting activity to the Division of Gaming Enforcement.
- **Nevada Gaming Control Board Regulation 22:** Long-standing requirement for Nevada sportsbooks to report unusual betting patterns.
- **Multi-State Integrity Compact:** Several states have entered cooperative agreements for sharing suspicious betting information.

### Notable Cases

- **United States v. Donaghy (E.D.N.Y. 2007):** NBA referee Tim Donaghy pleaded guilty to wire fraud conspiracy (18 U.S.C. § 1343) and transmitting wagering information (18 U.S.C. § 1084) for providing inside information to gamblers and making calls to influence point spreads. Sentenced to 15 months imprisonment. The case exposed vulnerabilities in professional sports officiating oversight.
- **United States v. Battista (S.D.N.Y. 2013):** Large-scale soccer match-fixing prosecution involving an international gambling syndicate that fixed matches in leagues across the United States, Central America, and Africa. Defendant Wilson Raj Perumal and associates were convicted under wire fraud and conspiracy charges.
- **United States v. Gagliano (M.D. Tenn. 2016):** Former University of San Diego basketball players convicted of wire fraud for conspiring to fix games and profit through sports betting. Sentences ranged from 6 to 18 months imprisonment.
- **United States v. Erpenbeck (N.D. Ohio 2023):** Federal prosecution of a match-fixing ring targeting semi-professional soccer matches, using wire fraud and RICO charges. The case demonstrated the application of federal tools to lower-tier sporting competitions.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's 2017 reform of the Strafgesetzbuch introduced specific criminal provisions targeting match-fixing, among the most targeted legislation globally:

- **StGB § 265c (Sportwettbetrug / Sports Betting Fraud):** Criminalizes the giving or receiving of a benefit in exchange for manipulating a sports competition that is the subject of public sports betting, where the manipulation is intended to gain an unlawful advantage from the betting. The offense carries a maximum penalty of 3 years imprisonment or a fine. Aggravated cases (e.g., commercial scale, organized group involvement) carry up to 5 years. The statute covers athletes, coaches, referees, and other persons influencing the competition.
- **StGB § 265d (Manipulation berufssportlicher Wettbewerbe / Manipulation of Professional Sports Competitions):** Criminalizes the manipulation of professional sports competitions even without a connection to sports betting. This provision recognizes that match-fixing harms the integrity of sport independently of any betting connection. The same penalty range applies (3 years, aggravated 5 years). "Professional sports competition" is defined as a competition organized by or on behalf of a national or international sports organization in which a majority of participants earn a substantial portion of their income from the sport.
- **StGB § 263 (Betrug / Fraud):** The general fraud provision remains applicable in match-fixing cases, particularly where the perpetrator obtains a financial advantage through deception. Penalties: up to 5 years (basic), up to 10 years (aggravated/commercial scale under § 263(3)(1)).
- **StGB § 299 (Bestechung im geschäftlichen Verkehr / Commercial Bribery):** Applied to match-fixing involving corruption within sports organizations treated as commercial enterprises.
- **Glücksspielstaatsvertrag 2021 (Interstate Treaty on Gambling):** The treaty, in force since 1 July 2021, established a unified regulatory framework for online sports betting across all 16 Bundesländer. The Gemeinsame Glücksspielbehörde der Länder (Joint Gambling Authority, GGL) in Halle (Saale) licenses and supervises sports betting operators, including requirements for suspicious activity reporting and cooperation with law enforcement.

### Enforcement Architecture

Germany's match-fixing enforcement combines criminal prosecution with regulatory and sports governance measures:

- **State Prosecution Offices (Staatsanwaltschaften):** Criminal prosecution is the responsibility of state prosecutors, with specialized units in several Bundesländer. The Staatsanwaltschaft Bochum has developed particular expertise in sports corruption cases.
- **Bundeskriminalamt (BKA, Federal Criminal Police Office):** Coordinates intelligence gathering and international cooperation on transnational match-fixing networks, working with INTERPOL and Europol.
- **DFB (German Football Association) and DFL (German Football League):** Operate integrity programs including the "Early Warning System" (Frühwarnsystem) monitoring betting patterns in Bundesliga and lower-division matches. The DFB's Sports Court (Sportgericht) imposes disciplinary sanctions (bans, point deductions, relegation) alongside criminal proceedings.
- **Gemeinsame Glücksspielbehörde der Länder (GGL):** The joint gambling authority monitors licensed operators' suspicious transaction reports and shares data with prosecution authorities.

### Notable Cases

- **Bochum Match-Fixing Trial (Ante Sapina, 2011):** The largest match-fixing prosecution in German history, arising from the "Operation Veto" investigation. Croatian national Ante Sapina and accomplices were convicted of fraud (§ 263 StGB) for fixing over 30 football matches across European leagues. Sapina received a sentence of 5 years and 6 months imprisonment. The Landgericht Bochum proceedings revealed connections to organized crime networks across Asia and Eastern Europe.
- **Robert Hoyzer Case (2005):** German football referee Robert Hoyzer was convicted of fraud for fixing multiple matches in the DFB-Pokal (German Cup) and Regionalliga in exchange for payments from a Croatian gambling syndicate. Sentenced to 2 years and 5 months imprisonment by the Landgericht Berlin. The case catalyzed the introduction of §§ 265c-d StGB.
- **Schiedsrichter-Skandal (Referee Scandal) Follow-up Prosecutions (2006-2009):** Multiple additional convictions arising from the broader investigation into referee corruption in German football, leading to the DFB's implementation of mandatory financial disclosure requirements for referees.
- **Cottbus Match-Fixing Investigation (2024):** Investigation into alleged match-fixing in lower-division German football involving energy drink-sponsored teams, testing the applicability of § 265d to semi-professional competitions.

## ECHR / EU Framework

### Convention Articles

- **Article 6 -- Right to a Fair Trial:** Match-fixing prosecutions engage the right to a fair trial, including the presumption of innocence, the right to adequate time to prepare a defense, and the right to examine witnesses. The use of betting data analytics and intercepted communications as evidence in match-fixing trials raises Article 6 issues regarding the admissibility of evidence and the defendant's ability to challenge algorithmic assessments.
- **Article 8 -- Right to Respect for Private and Family Life:** Surveillance measures used in match-fixing investigations (phone tapping, financial monitoring, digital surveillance) must comply with Article 8's requirement that interference be "in accordance with the law," pursue a "legitimate aim," and be "necessary in a democratic society."
- **Article 7 -- No Punishment Without Law (Nullum crimen sine lege):** The principle of legal certainty requires that match-fixing offenses be defined with sufficient precision. The application of general fraud statutes to match-fixing (rather than specific legislation) has been challenged as potentially violating Article 7's foreseeability requirement.

### Macolin Convention

The Council of Europe Convention on the Manipulation of Sports Competitions (CETS No. 215, opened for signature in Magglingen/Macolin on 18 September 2014) is the first international treaty specifically addressing match-fixing:

- **Article 15 -- Criminal Offenses:** Requires parties to establish criminal offenses for the manipulation of sports competitions involving coercion, fraud, or corruption. States must ensure these offenses carry "effective, proportionate and dissuasive" sanctions including imprisonment.
- **Article 11 -- Measures Regarding Illegal Sports Betting:** Requires parties to identify and combat illegal sports betting operators and to facilitate cooperation between betting regulators and law enforcement.
- **Article 12 -- Exchange of Information:** Establishes requirements for information sharing between sports organizations, betting regulators, and law enforcement both domestically and internationally.
- **Article 13 -- National Platform:** Requires each party to establish a national platform serving as an information hub for sports integrity, bringing together law enforcement, sports organizations, and betting regulators.
- **Article 19-21 -- International Cooperation:** Provides for mutual assistance in criminal matters, sharing of information, and cooperation through the Convention's Follow-Up Committee.

As of 2026, the Macolin Convention has been ratified by 9 states (including Italy, Portugal, Norway, Ukraine, and Moldova) and signed by an additional 26 states (including Germany, France, and the United Kingdom). The EU's accession has been complicated by a jurisdictional dispute with member states regarding exclusive EU competence over betting regulation aspects of the Convention.

### EU Approach

The EU has addressed match-fixing through soft law instruments and policy coordination rather than binding legislation:

- **Council Conclusions on Combating Match-Fixing (2014):** Invited member states to strengthen cooperation between law enforcement, sports organizations, and betting regulators.
- **EU Expert Group on Match-Fixing:** Produced recommendations on prevention, detection, and sanctioning of match-fixing.
- **Directive (EU) 2019/1937 (Whistleblower Protection Directive):** Provides protection for persons reporting breaches of EU law, potentially applicable to whistleblowers reporting match-fixing in contexts where EU competition law or anti-money laundering directives are engaged.
- **Directive (EU) 2018/1673 (Anti-Money Laundering Criminal Law Directive):** Match-fixing proceeds constitute the proceeds of crime, triggering money laundering offenses across EU member states.
