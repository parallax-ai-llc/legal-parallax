## Summary

Parental leave law -- the legal framework guaranteeing employees the right to take time off work following the birth, adoption, or fostering of a child, with varying degrees of job protection and wage replacement -- reveals stark differences in how legal systems balance work, family, and gender equality objectives. South Korea provides one of the most generous parental leave systems in the OECD on paper, with 90 days of paid maternity leave (출산전후휴가) under the Labor Standards Act (근로기준법) and up to one year of parental leave (육아휴직) per parent per child under the Act on Equal Employment (남녀고용평등법), compensated at 80 percent of ordinary wages (capped at 1.5 million KRW monthly) through the Employment Insurance Fund; despite these provisions, take-up rates remain heavily gendered, with men accounting for only approximately 28.9 percent of parental leave users in 2023, leading the government to introduce the "3+3 Parental Leave System" (3+3 부모육아휴직제) in 2022 providing enhanced benefits when both parents take leave during the child's first year. The United States stands alone among developed nations in providing no federally mandated paid parental leave; the Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA) of 1993 (29 U.S.C. Section 2601) guarantees only 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave to eligible employees at employers with 50 or more workers, while paid leave exists only through a patchwork of 13 state programs and voluntary employer policies, with the most comprehensive being California's Paid Family Leave (2002), New York's Paid Family Leave (2018), and Washington State's Paid Family and Medical Leave (2020). Germany provides among the world's most comprehensive systems through the Mutterschutzgesetz (MuSchG, Maternity Protection Act), which guarantees 14 weeks of fully paid maternity leave, and the Bundeselterngeld- und Elternzeitgesetz (BEEG, Federal Parental Allowance and Parental Leave Act), which provides up to three years of job-protected parental leave with Elterngeld (parental allowance) at 65-67 percent of net income for 12-14 months; the ElterngeldPlus reform of 2015 introduced flexible part-time parental leave options, and a 2024 reform reduced the income ceiling for eligibility. At the European level, the EU Work-Life Balance Directive 2019/1158 established minimum standards including four months of parental leave per parent (with two months non-transferable and compensated at a level set by member states), 10 working days of paternity leave, and five days of carers' leave per year.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's parental leave system combines protections under the Labor Standards Act (근로기준법), the Act on Equal Employment and Support for Work-Family Reconciliation (남녀고용평등법), and the Employment Insurance Act (고용보험법).

### Maternity Leave (출산전후휴가)

Article 74 of the Labor Standards Act provides 90 days of maternity leave (120 days for multiple births), with at least 45 days after delivery. Compensation is split: for large employers, the employer pays for the first 60 days and the Employment Insurance Fund covers the remaining 30 days. For employers with fewer than a priority-support-eligible threshold, the entire 90 days are covered by Employment Insurance.

### Parental Leave (육아휴직)

Article 19 of the Equal Employment Act provides that any employee with an infant under the age of 8 (or in the second year of elementary school) is entitled to parental leave of up to one year. Both parents are independently entitled, allowing up to two years of combined leave per child.

### Compensation

| Category | Rate | Cap |
|----------|------|-----|
| First 3 months of parental leave | 80% of ordinary wages | 1,500,000 KRW/month |
| Remaining months | 80% of ordinary wages | 1,500,000 KRW/month |
| 3+3 system (both parents during child's first year) | 100% of ordinary wages for first 3 months each | 3,000,000 KRW/month (2024 increase) |
| Return-to-work bonus | 25% of monthly parental leave pay | Paid as lump sum upon return |

### 3+3 Parental Leave System (3+3 부모육아휴직제)

Introduced on January 1, 2022, this system provides enhanced incentives for both parents to take parental leave during the child's first year of life. When both parents take leave, each receives 100 percent of ordinary wages (up to a higher cap) for the first three months. The policy aims to increase paternal leave uptake and promote gender equality in caregiving.

### Paternity Leave (배우자 출산휴가)

Article 18-2 of the Equal Employment Act provides 10 days of paid paternity leave (increased from 3 days in 2012 and from 5 days in 2019). The first 5 days are employer-paid; the remaining 5 days are covered by Employment Insurance.

### Usage Statistics

Despite generous entitlements, significant gender disparities persist:

### Workplace Retaliation

Despite legal protections, surveys indicate that many employees, particularly men, face informal workplace pressure not to take parental leave. The Ministry of Employment and Labor reported over 500 complaints of retaliation or disadvantageous treatment related to parental leave in 2023, with corrective actions ordered in approximately 40 percent of cases.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA) of 1993 is the primary federal law addressing parental leave. The United States remains the only OECD member state without a national paid parental leave program.

### FMLA Provisions

**Eligibility:** Employees must have worked for the employer for at least 12 months, accumulated at least 1,250 hours of service during the previous 12 months, and work at a location where the employer has 50 or more employees within 75 miles. These requirements exclude approximately 44 percent of the U.S. workforce from FMLA coverage.

**Leave Entitlement:** Up to 12 workweeks of unpaid, job-protected leave in a 12-month period for the birth, adoption, or foster placement of a child. Leave may be taken intermittently with employer agreement.

**Job Protection:** Employees must be restored to the same or an equivalent position upon return, with equivalent pay, benefits, and terms and conditions of employment.

### Federal Proposals

The Build Back Better Act (2021) included a national paid family and medical leave program of four weeks, but it was removed during negotiations. The FAMILY Act (proposed repeatedly since 2013) would establish a national 12-week paid leave program funded through employee payroll contributions, but has not been enacted. Federal employees received 12 weeks of paid parental leave through the Federal Employee Paid Leave Act of 2019.

### Employer Voluntarism

In the absence of federal mandate, many large employers voluntarily provide paid parental leave. A 2023 Bureau of Labor Statistics survey found that 27 percent of private-sector workers had access to paid family leave, with significant disparities by income: 43 percent of the highest-wage quartile versus 12 percent of the lowest-wage quartile had access.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's parental leave system is among the most comprehensive in the world, combining strong maternity protection (Mutterschutz), generous parental allowance (Elterngeld), and extended job-protected parental leave (Elternzeit).

### Mutterschutzgesetz (MuSchG)

The Maternity Protection Act provides: 6 weeks of pre-birth leave (Schutzfrist vor der Entbindung) -- voluntary, may be waived by the employee; 8 weeks of post-birth leave (Schutzfrist nach der Entbindung) -- mandatory, cannot be waived (12 weeks for premature or multiple births); full pay during leave through the Mutterschaftsgeld system (health insurance pays up to EUR 13/day; employer pays the difference to full salary).

### Elternzeit (Parental Leave)

Under the BEEG, each parent is entitled to up to three years of job-protected parental leave per child, which can be taken until the child's eighth birthday. Up to 24 months of the three-year entitlement may be taken between the child's third and eighth birthday (with employer agreement required for the portion after age 3). Both parents may take leave simultaneously.

### Elterngeld (Parental Allowance)

| Variant | Rate | Duration | Introduced |
|---------|------|----------|-----------|
| Basiselterngeld | 65-67% of net income (min EUR 300, max EUR 1,800/month) | Up to 12 months (14 with Partnermonate) | 2007 |
| ElterngeldPlus | 65-67% of net income, halved (min EUR 150, max EUR 900/month) | Up to 24 months (28 with Partnermonate) | 2015 |
| Partnerschaftsbonus | 65-67% for 2-4 additional months each | When both parents work part-time (24-32 hrs/week) simultaneously | 2015 |

### Partnermonate (Partner Months)

The 2007 Elterngeld reform introduced two additional "partner months" (Partnermonate), extending the total Elterngeld period from 12 to 14 months if the other parent (typically the father) takes at least two months of parental leave. This "use it or lose it" mechanism was modeled on the Swedish and Icelandic systems and has significantly increased paternal leave uptake in Germany. Male take-up of Elterngeld increased from 3.5 percent in 2006 to approximately 26 percent in 2023, though the average duration of fathers' leave remains significantly shorter (3.7 months) than mothers' (14.6 months).

### 2024 Reform

Effective April 1, 2024, the income ceiling for Elterngeld eligibility was reduced from EUR 300,000 to EUR 200,000 (for couples filing jointly; EUR 150,000 for single parents). This reform was a cost-saving measure within the 2024 federal budget negotiations. Additionally, simultaneous parental leave by both parents was restricted to the first month after birth only.

### Dismissal Protection

Section 18 BEEG provides absolute dismissal protection (Kündigungsverbot) from the date the employee requests parental leave (but no earlier than eight weeks before the leave begins) until the end of the leave period. Exceptions require prior authorization by the relevant state authority (Landesbehörde).

## ECHR & EU

### EU Work-Life Balance Directive 2019/1158

Adopted on June 20, 2019, with a transposition deadline of August 2, 2022, this Directive establishes minimum EU-wide standards for parental leave, paternity leave, and carers' leave.

**Parental Leave (Article 5):** Each parent is entitled to four months of parental leave per child, to be taken before the child reaches a specified age (up to 8 years, set by member state). Two months are non-transferable between parents and must be compensated at a level set by the member state. The non-transferable element (the "use it or lose it" principle) aims to incentivize fathers' uptake.

**Paternity Leave (Article 4):** Fathers (or equivalent second parents) are entitled to 10 working days of leave around the time of birth, compensated at least at sick pay level.

**Carers' Leave (Article 6):** Each worker is entitled to five working days of carers' leave per year to provide care to a relative or household member with a serious medical condition.

**Flexible Working (Article 9):** Parents of children up to age 8 and carers have the right to request flexible working arrangements, including remote work, flexible schedules, and reduced hours.

### CJEU Jurisprudence

**Roca Álvarez v. Sesa Start (Case C-104/09, 2010):** The Court held that Spanish law granting breastfeeding leave exclusively to employed mothers constituted sex discrimination, as it perpetuated a traditional division of roles between men and women.

**Maïstrellis v. Ypourgos Dikaiosynis (Case C-222/14, 2015):** The Court held that denying parental leave to a male civil servant because his wife was not employed constituted direct discrimination on grounds of sex.

**Lyreco Belgium v. Rogiers (Case C-588/12, 2014):** The Court held that dismissal protection during parental leave applies from the date the employee informs the employer of the intention to take leave, not only from the leave's commencement.

### ECHR

The European Court of Human Rights has addressed parental leave discrimination under Article 14 in conjunction with Article 8 (private and family life). In Konstantin Markin v. Russia (Application No. 30078/06, Grand Chamber Judgment of March 22, 2012), the Court found that Russia's denial of parental leave to military servicemen while granting it to servicewomen violated Article 14 taken in conjunction with Article 8. The Grand Chamber held that the different treatment could not be justified by reference to traditional gender roles or the special nature of military service.

### ILO Standards

ILO Convention No. 183 on Maternity Protection (2000) establishes minimum standards including 14 weeks of maternity leave, cash benefits of at least two-thirds of previous earnings during leave, and the right to return to the same or equivalent position. ILO Recommendation No. 191 (2000) recommends extending leave to 18 weeks.

## Comparative Analysis

### Paid vs. Unpaid Leave

The starkest divergence is between the United States, which provides only unpaid leave at the federal level, and all other jurisdictions, which provide wage replacement during parental leave. This gap affects lower-income workers disproportionately, as they are least able to afford unpaid leave. The emerging state-by-state approach in the United States mirrors the bottom-up development of social insurance in other countries but creates a fragmented system with uneven coverage.

### Gender Equality Mechanisms

All three domestic jurisdictions have adopted mechanisms to increase paternal leave uptake: South Korea's 3+3 system, Germany's Partnermonate, and the EU Directive's two non-transferable months. However, the effectiveness varies significantly. Germany's 26 percent male take-up (though for short durations) and South Korea's 28.9 percent represent meaningful progress from very low baselines. The United States has no comparable incentive structure at the federal level.

### Duration vs. Generosity Trade-off

Germany offers the longest leave (up to three years) but only compensates 12-14 months. South Korea offers one year with 80 percent replacement. The EU Directive requires four months with two compensated at an adequate level. Research suggests that moderate-length, well-compensated leave (around 6-12 months) optimizes outcomes for both child development and maternal labor force participation, while very long leaves may negatively affect women's career progression.
