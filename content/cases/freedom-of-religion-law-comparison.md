## Summary

Freedom of religion -- encompassing the right to hold, practice, change, or renounce religious beliefs without state interference -- is recognized as a fundamental human right in virtually all modern constitutional democracies, yet the precise contours of state-religion relations, permissible limitations on religious practice, and the treatment of religious minorities diverge significantly across legal systems. South Korea guarantees freedom of religion under Article 20 of the Constitution (대한민국 헌법 제20조), which affirms both individual religious freedom and the separation of religion and state, while the Constitutional Court (헌법재판소) has adjudicated critical disputes including the right of conscientious objectors to refuse military service based on religious beliefs -- a right finally recognized by the Court in 2018 (2011헌바379) after decades of imprisoning objectors, predominantly Jehovah's Witnesses. The United States protects religious freedom through two complementary First Amendment clauses -- the Free Exercise Clause and the Establishment Clause -- with the Supreme Court having developed a complex jurisprudence from Sherbert v. Verner (1963) through Employment Division v. Smith (1990) and the subsequent enactment of the Religious Freedom Restoration Act (RFRA, 42 U.S.C. Section 2000bb) to restore heightened scrutiny for laws burdening religious exercise. Germany establishes a cooperative model of church-state relations under Articles 4 and 140 of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz) in conjunction with the Weimar Constitution Articles 136-141, granting recognized religious communities the status of public-law corporations (Körperschaften des öffentlichen Rechts) with the right to levy church taxes (Kirchensteuer), while the Federal Constitutional Court has balanced religious freedom against competing rights in cases involving headscarves in public institutions and crucifixes in classrooms. The European Convention on Human Rights protects freedom of thought, conscience, and religion under Article 9, and the European Court of Human Rights has developed extensive jurisprudence applying the margin of appreciation doctrine to permit states considerable latitude in structuring church-state relations while imposing minimum standards of pluralism, tolerance, and broadmindedness.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Article 20 of the Constitution of the Republic of Korea (대한민국 헌법 제20조) provides: "(1) All citizens shall enjoy the freedom of religion. (2) No state religion shall be recognized, and religion and state shall be separated." This establishes both an individual right to religious freedom and an institutional principle of church-state separation.

South Korea is characterized by significant religious pluralism, with Buddhism, Protestantism, Catholicism, and various indigenous traditions coexisting alongside a substantial non-religious population. The 2015 census indicated approximately 44 percent of the population identified as religious, with Protestantism (19.7%), Buddhism (15.5%), and Catholicism (7.9%) as the largest communities.

### Conscientious Objection (양심적 병역거부)

The most significant religious freedom litigation in South Korea concerned conscientious objection to military service. Under the Military Service Act (병역법), all male citizens are subject to mandatory military service. For decades, conscientious objectors -- predominantly Jehovah's Witnesses -- were systematically imprisoned, with South Korea holding more imprisoned conscientious objectors than the rest of the world combined at various points.

The Constitutional Court initially upheld the constitutionality of the Military Service Act's lack of alternative service provisions in 2004 (2002헌가1) and 2011. However, in its landmark decision of June 28, 2018 (2011헌바379), the Court reversed course, ruling that the absence of an alternative service option violated the Constitution by excessively restricting freedom of conscience (양심의 자유, Article 19). The Court found that providing alternative civilian service would adequately fulfill the state's defense needs while accommodating sincere religious and conscientious beliefs.

Following this decision, the Alternative Service Act (대체역의 편입 및 복무 등에 관한 법률) was enacted in December 2019 and took effect on January 1, 2020. Alternative service requires 36 months of service at correctional facilities, compared to 18-21 months for regular military service.

### Religious Discrimination

The National Human Rights Commission of Korea (국가인권위원회) has investigated and issued recommendations regarding religious discrimination, including cases involving workplace discrimination based on religious affiliation and mandatory religious activities at religious-affiliated universities. The proposed Anti-Discrimination Act, which has failed to pass the National Assembly on multiple occasions, would explicitly prohibit discrimination based on religion.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The First Amendment provides: "Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof." These two clauses -- the Establishment Clause and the Free Exercise Clause -- create a dual framework that simultaneously prevents government promotion of religion and protects individual religious exercise.

**Free Exercise Clause:** In Sherbert v. Verner, 374 U.S. 398 (1963), the Supreme Court established that laws substantially burdening religious exercise must satisfy strict scrutiny. However, in Employment Division v. Smith, 494 U.S. 872 (1990), the Court dramatically narrowed this protection, holding that neutral, generally applicable laws that incidentally burden religious practice need only satisfy rational basis review. Congress responded by enacting the Religious Freedom Restoration Act (RFRA) in 1993, which requires the federal government to satisfy strict scrutiny when substantially burdening religious exercise.

**Establishment Clause:** The Court replaced the Lemon v. Kurtzman (1971) three-part test with a historical practices and understandings approach in Kennedy v. Bremerton School District, 597 U.S. 507 (2022), holding that the Establishment Clause must be interpreted by reference to historical practices and understandings rather than the endorsement or coercion tests previously applied.

### Religious Freedom Restoration Act (RFRA)

RFRA, 42 U.S.C. Section 2000bb, provides that the "[g]overnment shall not substantially burden a person's exercise of religion even if the burden results from a rule of general applicability" unless it "demonstrates that application of the burden to the person (1) is in furtherance of a compelling governmental interest; and (2) is the least restrictive means of furthering that compelling governmental interest." Following City of Boerne v. Flores, 521 U.S. 507 (1997), which held RFRA unconstitutional as applied to state and local governments, 21 states enacted their own state-level RFRAs.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Article 4 of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz) provides: "(1) Freedom of faith and of conscience, and freedom to profess a religious or philosophical creed, shall be inviolable. (2) The undisturbed practice of religion shall be guaranteed. (3) No person shall be compelled against his conscience to render military service involving the use of arms." Article 140 GG incorporates Articles 136-139 and 141 of the Weimar Constitution, establishing the institutional framework for church-state relations.

Germany employs a cooperative model of church-state separation. While there is no state church, major religious communities -- including the Catholic Church, the Evangelical Church in Germany (EKD), and Jewish communities -- hold the status of public-law corporations (Körperschaften des öffentlichen Rechts). This status grants them the right to levy taxes (Kirchensteuer) collected by the state, provide religious instruction in public schools, and exercise certain public functions.

### Church Tax (Kirchensteuer)

The Kirchensteuer is a tax surcharge of typically 8-9 percent of income tax, collected by the state tax authorities on behalf of recognized religious communities. Church membership and tax obligations are automatic for members of participating denominations. An individual may exit (Kirchenaustritt) by making a formal declaration to a civil authority, which terminates the tax obligation. The Federal Constitutional Court has upheld the constitutionality of the Kirchensteuer system as consistent with the cooperative model established by the Weimar Constitution articles.

### Headscarf Debates

The headscarf question has been one of the most contested religious freedom issues in Germany. In the first Kopftuch decision (BVerfGE 108, 282 (2003)), the Federal Constitutional Court held that a state law was needed to prohibit teachers from wearing headscarves, as the Basic Law alone did not mandate or prohibit such a restriction. Several Länder subsequently enacted prohibitions. In the second Kopftuch decision (1 BvR 471/10, 2015), the Court reversed course, holding that a blanket ban on religious headscarves for teachers violated Article 4 GG and that a prohibition could only be justified by a concrete danger to school peace or state neutrality.

### Blasphemy Provision

Section 166 StGB (Beschimpfung von Bekenntnissen, Religionsgesellschaften und Weltanschauungsvereinigungen) criminalizes publicly defaming the content of the religious or ideological beliefs of others in a manner that is capable of disturbing the public peace. Penalties include a fine or imprisonment up to three years. This provision is rarely prosecuted but remains on the books.

## ECHR & EU

### Article 9 ECHR

Article 9 provides: "(1) Everyone has the right to freedom of thought, conscience and religion; this right includes freedom to change his religion or belief and freedom, either alone or in community with others and in public or private, to manifest his religion or belief, in worship, teaching, practice and observance. (2) Freedom to manifest one's religion or beliefs shall be subject only to such limitations as are prescribed by law and are necessary in a democratic society in the interests of public safety, for the protection of public order, health or morals, or for the protection of the rights and freedoms of others."

### Key ECHR Jurisprudence

**Kokkinakis v. Greece (Application No. 14307/88, 1993):** The Court's first major Article 9 case held that Greece's conviction of a Jehovah's Witness for proselytism violated Article 9, while acknowledging that states may restrict "improper proselytism" (offering material or social advantages or applying improper pressure).

**Sahin v. Turkey (Application No. 44774/98, Grand Chamber, 2005):** The Grand Chamber upheld Turkey's ban on wearing Islamic headscarves in universities, applying a wide margin of appreciation and deferring to Turkey's principle of secularism (laiklik).

**Lautsi v. Italy (Application No. 30814/06, Grand Chamber, 2011):** The Grand Chamber reversed the Chamber's finding and held that the display of crucifixes in Italian public school classrooms did not violate Article 2 of Protocol No. 1 (right to education) read in conjunction with Article 9, applying a wide margin of appreciation.

**S.A.S. v. France (Application No. 43835/11, Grand Chamber, 2014):** The Grand Chamber upheld France's ban on full-face coverings in public spaces, accepting the French government's argument that the ban pursued the legitimate aim of protecting the conditions of "living together" (vivre ensemble).

**Bayatyan v. Armenia (Application No. 23459/03, Grand Chamber, 2011):** The Grand Chamber overturned prior case law and held that Article 9 protects the right of conscientious objectors to refuse military service, finding Armenia's imprisonment of a Jehovah's Witness for refusal to serve violated the Convention.

### EU Framework

The EU Charter of Fundamental Rights, Article 10, mirrors Article 9 ECHR. EU Directive 2000/78/EC establishes a general framework for equal treatment in employment and occupation, prohibiting discrimination on grounds including religion. The Court of Justice of the European Union has addressed workplace religious expression in G4S Secure Solutions (C-157/15, 2017) and WABE (C-804/18, 2021), holding that employer policies prohibiting visible political, philosophical, or religious signs may be justified if applied neutrally and based on a genuine need.

## Comparative Analysis

### Church-State Models

The four jurisdictions represent three distinct models of church-state relations: South Korea and France-influenced ECHR states follow a strict separationist model; the United States follows a separationist model with significant accommodationist elements under RFRA; Germany follows a cooperative model with institutional partnership between state and recognized religious communities. The ECHR framework accommodates all three models through the margin of appreciation doctrine.

### Conscientious Objection

The trajectory of conscientious objection rights illustrates convergence across jurisdictions. Germany recognized conscientious objection constitutionally from the outset in 1949. The ECHR established the right in 2011. South Korea recognized it in 2018 after decades of imprisonment. The United States has a statutory rather than constitutional framework, with the Selective Service Act providing for conscientious objector classification.

### Religious Symbols in Public Spaces

This remains the most divisive issue across jurisdictions. France and Turkey (within the ECHR system) take the most restrictive approach, banning religious symbols in public institutions. Germany has oscillated between restriction and accommodation. The United States generally permits individual religious expression in public spaces while prohibiting government endorsement of religion. South Korea has seen relatively little litigation on this issue.

### Balancing Religious Freedom with Anti-Discrimination

The tension between religious freedom claims and LGBTQ+ anti-discrimination protections represents a frontier issue in all jurisdictions. The United States has seen the most litigation, from Masterpiece Cakeshop to 303 Creative, with the Court increasingly favoring religious and speech-based exemptions. The EU framework addresses this tension through Directive 2000/78/EC but leaves significant discretion to member states.

### Blasphemy and Defamation of Religion

The treatment of blasphemy and defamation of religion illustrates divergent approaches. The United States provides no protection against blasphemy, with the Supreme Court having effectively invalidated blasphemy laws. Germany retains Section 166 StGB (Beschimpfung von Bekenntnissen) criminalizing defamation of religious beliefs where it is likely to disturb public peace. South Korea has no specific blasphemy statute. The ECHR has upheld blasphemy convictions in some cases (Otto-Preminger-Institut v. Austria, 1994) while protecting artistic and critical expression in others (Vereinigung Bildender Künstler v. Austria, 2007), applying a margin of appreciation that gives states latitude in protecting religious sensibilities.

### Religious Education in Public Schools

Religious education in public schools represents a significant structural difference. Germany constitutionally mandates religious instruction as part of the regular curriculum under Article 7(3) GG, with parents retaining the right to opt children out. The United States prohibits government-directed religious instruction under the Establishment Clause, though the Court's Kennedy decision (2022) has expanded space for individual religious expression by public school employees. South Korea's public schools are secular, though private religious schools may include religious instruction. The ECHR permits religious education but requires that it be delivered in an "objective, critical and pluralistic" manner (Folgerø v. Norway, 2007).

### New Religious Movements and Cults

The legal treatment of new religious movements varies significantly. South Korea has experienced high-profile controversies involving new religious movements, including the Shincheonji Church's role in early COVID-19 transmission and ongoing concerns about coercive practices in certain groups. The United States generally protects new religious movements under the Free Exercise Clause. Germany actively monitors certain groups through the Verfassungsschutz (domestic intelligence service) and has investigated Scientology's compatibility with democratic principles. The ECHR framework protects new religious movements under Article 9 but permits restrictions on harmful practices.

### Tax Exemptions and State Funding

The relationship between religious organizations and tax policy reflects different church-state models. The United States provides tax-exempt status to religious organizations under IRC Section 501(c)(3) without requiring disclosure of financial information. Germany's Kirchensteuer system provides direct state-facilitated funding through the church tax. South Korea does not provide direct state funding to religious organizations but grants tax exemptions for religious activities. The EU framework leaves tax treatment to member states while requiring non-discrimination.

### Religious Dietary and Dress Practices

Legal accommodation of religious dietary practices (halal, kosher) and dress codes raises issues at the intersection of religious freedom and public regulation. The ECHR has addressed ritual slaughter in Cha'are Shalom Ve Tsedek v. France (2000). Germany permits ritual slaughter under limited conditions. South Korea has limited jurisprudence on this issue. The United States protects religious dietary practices under RFRA and the Free Exercise Clause.
