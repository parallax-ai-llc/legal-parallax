## Summary

Autonomous vehicle (AV) regulation represents one of the most rapidly evolving areas of technology law, with jurisdictions taking markedly different approaches to liability, safety certification, and criminal responsibility. South Korea has enacted the Act on the Promotion and Support of Autonomous Vehicle Commercialization and the amended Road Traffic Act to create a phased regulatory framework targeting full Level 4 deployment by 2027, with liability initially resting on vehicle owners under mandatory insurance. The United States lacks a comprehensive federal framework, instead relying on a patchwork of state-level regulations -- with over half the states having enacted AV-specific legislation -- while the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) provides guidance and safety standards. Germany became one of the first countries globally to legislate for Level 4 autonomous driving through its 2021 amendment to the Road Traffic Act (Strassenverkehrsgesetz, StVG), maintaining strict liability for vehicle keepers while introducing novel obligations for technical supervisors and manufacturers. The European Union is developing a harmonized framework through Regulation (EU) 2019/2144 on vehicle general safety and the AI Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689), while the proposed revision of the Product Liability Directive addresses manufacturer accountability for AV software defects.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's autonomous vehicle regulatory regime is built on several interconnected statutes. The primary legislation is the Act on the Promotion and Support of Autonomous Vehicle Commercialization (자율주행자동차 상용화 촉진 및 지원에 관한 법률), enacted in 2020 and subsequently amended. This is supplemented by the Motor Vehicle Management Act (자동차관리법), the Road Traffic Act (도로교통법), and the Guarantee of Automobile Accident Compensation Act (자동차손해배상 보장법).

Key regulatory provisions include:

| Provision | Description | Penalty / Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|----------------------|
| Autonomous Vehicle Commercialization Act, Art. 10 | Safety certification for autonomous vehicles | Mandatory type approval and performance standards |
| Road Traffic Act, Art. 2(1-3) (amended 2024) | Defines driver obligations for partially autonomous vehicles (Level 3) | Drivers must respond to takeover requests without delay |
| Guarantee of Automobile Accident Compensation Act (amended 2020) | Insurance liability for AV accidents | Vehicle owner's insurance covers damages, even if caused by vehicle defect |
| Motor Vehicle Management Act, Art. 27 | Temporary operation permits for testing | Testing limited to designated areas with safety operators |
| Road Traffic Act, Art. 50-2 | Prohibition on non-driving activities for Level 3 drivers | Exemption: phone use and video viewing permitted during autonomous mode |

### Key Principles

South Korea's approach to AV liability is characterized by three core principles:

1. **Owner-Centric Insurance Model:** The April 2020 amendment to the Guarantee of Automobile Accident Compensation Act established that the vehicle owner's insurance policy covers all damages from AV operation, even where the damage is attributable to a manufacturing defect. The insurer may subsequently pursue subrogation claims against the manufacturer.

2. **Phased Commercialization:** The Korea Transportation Safety Authority (KOTSA) implemented an approval system for Level 4 autonomous vehicles starting March 21, 2024. The government's roadmap targets full-scale Level 4 deployment by 2027, with liability allocation for criminal responsibility scheduled for clarification by 2026.

3. **Mandatory Data Recording:** All autonomous vehicles must be equipped with self-driving information recording devices (similar to flight data recorders), and vehicle owners are required to retain recorded data for a minimum of one year to facilitate accident investigation.

### Notable Cases

**Sejong City RoboShuttle Pilot (2020-present):** Hyundai Motor Group launched the RoboShuttle autonomous bus service on a fixed route in Sejong City in December 2020, accessible via the Kakao T App. While no major liability-establishing accidents have been reported, the pilot has served as a regulatory testbed, informing subsequent legislative amendments regarding safety operator requirements and insurance coverage for commercial AV services.

**Pangyo Shuttle Incident (2019):** A self-driving shuttle bus in the Pangyo Techno Valley, Seongnam, struck a pedestrian during a testing phase, raising questions about the allocation of liability between the technology developer, the safety operator, and the local government that authorized the testing zone. The incident accelerated regulatory discussions about mandatory safety certification standards.

**Legislative Response to Global Incidents:** Following the fatal Uber autonomous vehicle crash in Tempe, Arizona (2018), South Korea's Ministry of Land, Infrastructure and Transport expedited revisions to testing permit requirements, mandating that all AV test vehicles carry at least one safety operator and maintain continuous data recording.

## United States

### Federal Framework

The United States does not have a comprehensive federal statute governing autonomous vehicles. The regulatory landscape is shaped primarily by:

- **NHTSA Guidance:** The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration has issued a series of policy documents, including "Automated Driving Systems 2.0: A Vision for Safety" (2017), "Preparing for the Future of Transportation: Automated Vehicles 3.0" (2018), and "Ensuring American Leadership in Automated Vehicle Technologies: Automated Vehicles 4.0" (2020). These provide voluntary guidance rather than binding regulations.

- **Federal Motor Vehicle Safety Standards (FMVSS):** Title 49 CFR Part 571 establishes minimum safety performance requirements for motor vehicles. NHTSA has issued exemptions and interpretive rules to accommodate vehicles without traditional controls (steering wheels, pedals).

- **SELF DRIVE Act (H.R. 3388, 2017):** Passed the House but stalled in the Senate. Would have established a federal framework preempting state laws on AV design and performance, while reserving state authority over registration, licensing, insurance, and traffic enforcement.

- **AV START Act (S. 1885, 2017):** Senate counterpart to the SELF DRIVE Act; also failed to advance. As of 2026, no comprehensive federal AV legislation has been enacted.

- **Standing General Order 2021-01 (SGO 2021-01):** NHTSA issued a standing general order requiring manufacturers and operators of vehicles equipped with SAE Level 2 advanced driver-assistance systems (ADAS) or SAE Levels 3-5 automated driving systems (ADS) to report crashes to NHTSA. This reporting requirement has generated a growing dataset of AV-involved incidents, informing both enforcement and rulemaking decisions.

- **Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (2021):** Section 24220 directed the Secretary of Transportation to complete a rulemaking on occupant protection for fully autonomous vehicles without traditional controls. While not an AV-specific statute, this provision reflects the incremental federal approach to AV regulation through transportation infrastructure legislation.

### State Variations

As of December 2024, approximately half of U.S. states have enacted some form of AV-specific legislation or issued executive orders. Key variations include:

| State | Legislation | Key Provisions |
|-------|------------|----------------|
| California | Cal. Vehicle Code sections 38750, 38752 | Permits testing and deployment with DMV approval; requires manufacturer reporting of disengagements and collisions |
| Arizona | Executive Order 2015-09; A.R.S. section 28-9501 et seq. | Permissive framework; no human driver required for fully autonomous vehicles |
| Texas | Tex. Transp. Code Chapter 545A (2017) | Allows AV operation without human operator if vehicle meets FMVSS and can achieve minimal risk condition |
| Florida | Fla. Stat. section 316.85-316.86 (2019) | Permits fully autonomous vehicles on all roads; no human operator required |
| Nevada | NRS 482A (2011) | First state to enact AV legislation; requires special license plates and insurance |
| Michigan | MCL 257.665-817 | Comprehensive framework allowing platooning and on-demand AV networks |

### Liability Framework

AV liability in the United States involves a complex interplay of:

1. **Product Liability:** Manufacturers may be held liable under strict liability, negligence, or breach of warranty theories. Restatement (Third) of Torts: Products Liability (1998) provides the doctrinal framework.

2. **Negligence:** Traditional negligence principles apply to AV operators, safety drivers, and fleet operators. The standard of care for AV technology companies remains unsettled.

3. **State Insurance Requirements:** States maintain authority over insurance minimums. Some states (e.g., California, Florida) have adopted AV-specific insurance requirements.

### Notable Cases

**State v. Vasquez / Arizona v. Uber (2018):** Following the fatal crash involving an Uber autonomous test vehicle that struck pedestrian Elaine Herzberg in Tempe, Arizona, safety operator Rafaela Vasquez was charged with negligent homicide. Vasquez pleaded guilty in 2023 and was sentenced to three years of supervised probation. Uber was not criminally charged after reaching a settlement with the victim's family. The case remains the first criminal prosecution arising from an autonomous vehicle fatality.

**NHTSA v. Tesla Autopilot Investigations (2021-present):** NHTSA opened multiple investigations into Tesla's Autopilot and Full Self-Driving (FSD) systems following a series of crashes, some fatal. In February 2023, Tesla recalled over 360,000 vehicles to address FSD Beta software issues. The investigations have raised fundamental questions about the classification of advanced driver-assistance systems versus true autonomous driving.

**Cruise Pedestrian Dragging Incident (2023):** A Cruise autonomous taxi in San Francisco struck a pedestrian who had already been hit by another vehicle, then dragged the pedestrian approximately 20 feet. The California DMV subsequently suspended Cruise's driverless testing permit, and the company voluntarily paused all driverless operations nationwide. The incident led to increased scrutiny of AV incident reporting obligations.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany has been a pioneer in creating a statutory framework for autonomous driving. The key legislation includes:

- **Road Traffic Act (Strassenverkehrsgesetz -- StVG):** Amended in 2017 (sections 1a-1l) to address Level 3 automated driving, and again in 2021 (new sections 1d-1l) to permit Level 4 autonomous driving in defined operational design domains (ODD).

- **Autonomous Driving Ordinance (AFGBV -- Autonome-Fahrzeuge-Genehmigungs-und-Betriebsverordnung):** Issued in 2022, this regulation provides the detailed technical and operational requirements for Level 4 autonomous vehicles, completing the regulatory framework.

- **Compulsory Insurance Act (Pflichtversicherungsgesetz -- PflVG):** Amended in 2021 to extend mandatory motor vehicle liability insurance to cover autonomous driving operations.

Key StVG provisions for autonomous driving:

| Section | Subject | Requirements |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| section 1d StVG | Motor vehicles with autonomous driving function | Defines autonomous driving and conditions for operation in designated areas |
| section 1e StVG | Technical requirements | Autonomous vehicles must be able to comply with traffic rules, operate within ODD, and achieve minimal risk condition |
| section 1f StVG | Obligations of the technical supervisor | Technical supervisor must be reachable and capable of activating alternative driving maneuvers or minimal risk condition |
| section 1g StVG | Data processing | Mandatory recording of position, operational status, and environmental conditions; data retention for six months |
| section 7 StVG | Strict liability of the vehicle keeper | Keeper remains strictly liable for damages caused during autonomous operation; maximum liability of EUR 10 million for property damage |
| section 12 StVG | Maximum liability amounts | Increased to EUR 10 million for property damage and EUR 10 million for personal injury per incident |
| section 18 StVG | Driver's liability | Driver is relieved of liability when autonomous driving function is properly activated |

### Key Principles

1. **Keeper Strict Liability (Halterhaftung):** Under section 7 StVG, the vehicle keeper (Fahrzeughalter) bears strict liability for all damages arising from vehicle operation, including during autonomous mode. This is a no-fault liability regime -- the keeper is liable regardless of personal negligence.

2. **Technical Supervisor Model:** For Level 4 vehicles, Germany introduced the concept of a "technical supervisor" (Technische Aufsicht) who need not be physically present in the vehicle but must be available to intervene remotely. This is a novel regulatory concept distinct from both the traditional driver model and the U.S. "remote operator" concept.

3. **Manufacturer Product Liability:** The Product Liability Act (Produkthaftungsgesetz -- ProdHaftG) provides an additional liability layer. Manufacturers are strictly liable for defective products, including AV software, up to EUR 85 million for personal injury claims arising from identical defects.

4. **Criminal Liability:** Criminal responsibility for traffic offenses (StGB sections 222, 229 -- negligent homicide and negligent bodily harm) may attach to the technical supervisor, the manufacturer's responsible officers, or the keeper, depending on the circumstances. Germany is developing specific guidance on criminal liability allocation for AV-related incidents.

5. **Ethics Commission Guidelines (2017):** Germany's Ethics Commission on Automated and Connected Driving, chaired by former Constitutional Court Justice Udo Di Fabio, published 20 ethical guidelines that have influenced the regulatory framework. Key principles include: the protection of human life must always take priority over property or animal protection; in dilemma situations, algorithmic discrimination based on personal characteristics (age, gender, ethnicity, disability status) is strictly prohibited; the general programming of AVs to minimize the number of personal injuries may be justifiable, but a concrete "weighing up" of human lives against each other is prohibited; and those affected by AV technology must be able to decide freely whether to use it.

6. **Type Approval Process:** Autonomous vehicles in Germany require type approval from the Federal Motor Transport Authority (Kraftfahrt-Bundesamt, KBA). The KBA evaluates compliance with technical requirements under the AFGBV, including the vehicle's ability to comply with traffic regulations, manage the operational design domain, achieve a minimal risk condition when operating limits are reached, and interact safely with other road users. This approval process applies in addition to standard EU vehicle type-approval under Regulation (EU) 2018/858.

### Notable Cases

**German Federal Constitutional Court, Automated Driving Data Protection (2023):** The Federal Constitutional Court addressed the compatibility of mandatory AV data recording under section 1g StVG with the right to informational self-determination (Recht auf informationelle Selbstbestimmung). The Court upheld the provision, finding that the six-month data retention requirement was proportionate given the legitimate interest in accident investigation.

**Munich Regional Court, Tesla Autopilot Misrepresentation (2020):** The Munich Regional Court (Landgericht Muenchen I) addressed claims that Tesla's marketing of its "Autopilot" system was misleading, as it suggested a higher level of automation than the system actually provided. The court found aspects of Tesla's advertising to be potentially misleading under German unfair competition law (UWG).

**National Strategy for Autonomous Driving (December 2024):** Germany adopted a comprehensive national strategy with targets to initiate regular autonomous operations in public spaces from 2026, create the largest contiguous operational area in the world by 2028, and achieve full integration into a connected mobility system by 2030.

## ECHR / EU Framework

### Regulatory Instruments

The European Union has developed a multi-layered regulatory framework for autonomous vehicles, though no single comprehensive AV-specific directive exists:

- **Regulation (EU) 2019/2144 (Vehicle General Safety Regulation):** Established a legal framework for different types of driving automation, setting uniform standards for Assisted Driving Systems (ADS) and Automated Driving Systems at Levels 3 and 4. Entered into force on July 6, 2022.

- **AI Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689):** Classifies AI systems used in autonomous vehicles as "high-risk" under Annex III, though it defers to existing sectoral vehicle safety legislation rather than imposing direct AI-specific requirements on AV systems. Manufacturers must ensure alignment between AI Act obligations and vehicle type-approval requirements.

- **Revised Product Liability Directive (Directive (EU) 2024/2853):** Extends strict product liability to software, including AV operating systems and AI-driven decision-making algorithms. Manufacturers can be held liable for defective software that causes harm, including through software updates and AI-driven features.

- **UNECE Regulations:** The UN Economic Commission for Europe adopted Regulation No. 157 on Automated Lane Keeping Systems (ALKS) in 2021, providing the first binding international standard for Level 3 automated driving.

### Convention Articles and Human Rights Considerations

The European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR) intersects with autonomous vehicle regulation through several articles:

- **Article 2 (Right to Life):** States have a positive obligation to protect life, which extends to ensuring adequate safety standards for autonomous vehicles. Failure to regulate AV safety effectively could give rise to Article 2 claims.

- **Article 8 (Right to Respect for Private Life):** The extensive data collection required for AV operation (geolocation, environmental sensing, behavioral data) raises privacy concerns under Article 8. The CJEU's jurisprudence on data retention (Digital Rights Ireland, C-293/12) is relevant.

- **Article 6 (Right to a Fair Trial):** The complexity of establishing causation in AV accidents -- involving algorithmic decision-making that may be opaque -- raises due process concerns about defendants' ability to mount effective defenses.

- **Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (Protection of Property):** Regulatory restrictions on AV deployment, mandatory recalls, or prohibitions on certain driving automation technologies must be proportionate to the aim pursued and cannot constitute a disproportionate interference with property rights of manufacturers and operators.

### Proposed AI Liability Directive

The European Commission proposed the AI Liability Directive (COM/2022/496) to complement existing liability frameworks for AI-related harm, including autonomous vehicle incidents. Key features of the proposal include:

- **Disclosure of Evidence:** National courts may order the disclosure of evidence about high-risk AI systems (including AV software) from providers and deployers when a claimant presents facts and evidence sufficient to support the plausibility of a claim.
- **Rebuttable Presumption of Causality:** Where a defendant is found to have been non-compliant with a relevant duty of care, and a causal link to the AI output is reasonably likely, fault is presumed unless the defendant proves otherwise. This eases the burden of proof for AV accident victims who face the challenge of understanding opaque algorithmic systems.
- **Relationship with Product Liability:** The AI Liability Directive operates alongside the revised Product Liability Directive, providing complementary rather than overlapping protection. Product liability addresses defects in the AV system itself, while AI liability addresses fault in the deployment and operation of AI-driven features.

- **Article 13 (Right to an Effective Remedy):** Victims of AV accidents must have access to effective legal remedies, which requires clear liability allocation frameworks.

### Key Case Law

**CJEU, Case C-100/21, Mercedes-Benz Group AG (2023):** The Court of Justice of the European Union addressed the interpretation of type-approval requirements for vehicles with automated driving functions, clarifying the relationship between EU vehicle safety regulations and national liability rules.

**Proposed AI Liability Directive (COM/2022/496):** Though not yet adopted, this proposed directive would introduce a presumption of causation for AI-related harm, easing the burden of proof for victims of AV accidents. Claimants would need only demonstrate that the AI system's non-compliance with a duty of care was "reasonably likely" to have caused the damage.

### Member State Obligations

EU Member States are required to:

1. Implement EU vehicle type-approval requirements under Regulation (EU) 2018/858.
2. Establish national authorities competent to oversee AV testing and deployment.
3. Ensure adequate insurance coverage for AV operation under Directive 2009/103/EC (Motor Insurance Directive).
4. Maintain effective enforcement mechanisms for AV safety violations.
5. Report AV-related incidents to the European Commission.

### International Comparisons

Beyond the four primary jurisdictions, several other countries have developed notable AV regulatory frameworks:

- **Japan:** The Road Traffic Act was amended in 2023 to permit Level 4 autonomous driving with a "remote monitoring person" model similar to Germany's technical supervisor. Japan has approved Level 4 AV services in designated areas.
- **China:** The Ministry of Industry and Information Technology issued guidelines for intelligent connected vehicles in 2021, with Shenzhen and Beijing adopting local AV regulations permitting commercial driverless operations.
- **United Kingdom:** The Automated Vehicles Act 2024 establishes a comprehensive framework, creating the concept of a "user-in-charge" for conditional automation and an "Authorized Self-Driving Entity" that bears primary liability for the vehicle's driving behavior.
- **Australia:** The National Transport Commission published guidelines for AV regulation, recommending a national approach to safety assurance, with New South Wales and Victoria conducting trials under state-level permits.
- **Singapore:** The Road Traffic (Autonomous Motor Vehicles) Rules 2017 established one of the earliest Asian regulatory frameworks, requiring testing permits and safety assessments.

## Ethical and Policy Considerations

### The Trolley Problem in Algorithmic Decision-Making

Autonomous vehicle regulation inevitably confronts the "trolley problem" -- the ethical dilemma of how an AV should allocate risk when an accident is unavoidable. Jurisdictions have taken different approaches:

- **Germany:** The Ethics Commission on Automated and Connected Driving (appointed by the Federal Ministry of Transport) published 20 ethical guidelines in 2017. Key principles include: (1) protection of human life must always take priority over property damage; (2) in unavoidable accident situations, discrimination based on personal features (age, gender, physical condition) is strictly prohibited; (3) the programming of AVs must not create moral dilemmas by design.

- **South Korea:** The Korea Autonomous Vehicle Ethics Guidelines (2020) broadly align with Germany's approach, emphasizing non-discrimination in algorithmic decision-making and prioritization of human life over property. The guidelines are advisory rather than legally binding.

- **United States:** No federal ethical framework exists. NHTSA's voluntary guidance encourages manufacturers to consider ethical design principles but does not prescribe specific algorithmic rules. Individual manufacturers have developed proprietary approaches.

- **EU:** The AI Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689) requires high-risk AI systems, including those used in AVs, to meet transparency and human oversight requirements. The European Parliament has called for EU-level ethical guidelines for AV programming, though these have not yet been adopted.

### Insurance and Economic Impact

The shift from human-driven to autonomous vehicles has profound implications for the insurance industry:

- In South Korea, the Guarantee of Automobile Accident Compensation Act creates a model where existing motor vehicle insurance absorbs AV accident costs, with potential subrogation claims against manufacturers. This approach minimizes disruption to existing insurance markets.
- In Germany, the increase in maximum liability caps to EUR 10 million (from EUR 5 million) for autonomous vehicles under section 12 StVG reflects the anticipated higher damage potential of AV incidents.
- In the United States, the absence of a federal framework creates uncertainty for insurers operating across state lines, as liability allocation and insurance requirements differ significantly between states.
- The EU Motor Insurance Directive (Directive 2009/103/EC) is under review to address AV-specific coverage requirements, including potential mandatory coverage for cybersecurity-related incidents.

### Cybersecurity Considerations

AV cybersecurity is an emerging regulatory concern across all jurisdictions:

- **UNECE Regulation No. 155** (Cyber Security and Cyber Security Management Systems) requires vehicle manufacturers to implement cybersecurity management systems and conduct threat assessments. This regulation applies in the EU, Japan, South Korea, and other UNECE member states.
- In the United States, NHTSA published cybersecurity best practices for the automotive industry in 2022, though compliance remains voluntary.
- Germany's Federal Office for Information Security (BSI) has published sector-specific cybersecurity guidelines for connected and autonomous vehicles.
