## Summary

Term limits -- constitutional or statutory restrictions on the number of terms or years an individual may serve in a particular elected office -- represent a fundamental mechanism for preventing the concentration of power and ensuring democratic rotation, though their design and enforcement vary dramatically across jurisdictions. South Korea's Constitution (대한민국 헌법) imposes a strict single-term limit for the President under Article 70, which provides for a five-year non-renewable term (5년 단임제), a provision adopted in the 1987 democratic constitution specifically to prevent the authoritarian abuses of extended presidential rule that characterized the Park Chung-hee and Chun Doo-hwan eras; National Assembly members face no term limits. The United States imposes presidential term limits through the Twenty-Second Amendment (ratified 1951), restricting presidents to two four-year terms or a maximum of ten years if succeeding to the presidency mid-term, while members of Congress face no federal term limits following the Supreme Court's decision in U.S. Term Limits, Inc. v. Thornton (514 U.S. 779, 1995), which held that states cannot impose qualifications for federal office beyond those specified in the Constitution; however, fifteen states impose term limits on state legislators. Germany's Basic Law (Grundgesetz) imposes no term limits on the Federal Chancellor, who may serve unlimited consecutive terms as long as the Bundestag continues to provide a majority -- as demonstrated by Helmut Kohl's sixteen years in office (1982-1998) and Angela Merkel's sixteen years (2005-2021); however, the Federal President is limited to two consecutive five-year terms under Article 54(2) GG. At the European level, the European Commission President and European Council President serve renewable five-year and two-and-a-half-year terms respectively under the Treaty on European Union, with no formal term limits beyond political constraints, while the European Court of Human Rights has addressed term limit removal as a democratic backsliding indicator, most notably in its consideration of constitutional amendments designed to circumvent existing term limits.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's presidential term limit is among the strictest in the world: a single five-year term with absolutely no possibility of re-election.

**Article 70 of the Constitution (헌법 제70조):** "The term of office of the President shall be five years, and the President shall not be reelected" (대통령의 임기는 5년으로 하며, 중임할 수 없다). This provision is reinforced by Article 128(2), which states that any constitutional amendment proposal to extend the presidential term or to allow re-election shall not be effective for the President in office at the time of the proposal.

**Historical Context:** The single-term presidency was the product of Korea's traumatic experience with extended authoritarian rule. The trajectory of constitutional term limit provisions illustrates this evolution:

| Republic | Constitution | Presidential Term |
|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| First Republic (1948-1960) | Original 1948 Constitution | 4 years, one re-election; amended in 1954 to remove limits for first president (Syngman Rhee) |
| Second Republic (1960-1961) | 1960 Constitution | Parliamentary system (President was ceremonial) |
| Third Republic (1963-1972) | 1963 Constitution | 4 years, one re-election; amended in 1969 to allow third term (Park Chung-hee) |
| Fourth Republic (1972-1980) | 1972 Yushin Constitution | 6 years, unlimited re-election by rubber-stamp National Conference for Unification |
| Fifth Republic (1980-1988) | 1980 Constitution | 7 years, single term (Chun Doo-hwan) |
| Sixth Republic (1988-present) | 1987 Constitution | 5 years, single term |

The 1987 provision was a deliberate compromise during the democratic transition: pro-democracy activists demanded direct presidential elections, while the ruling party sought protections against perpetual opposition presidency. The single-term limit satisfied both concerns.

### Debates on Reform

The single-term presidency has generated persistent calls for reform:

**Four-Year Two-Term Proposal (4년 연임제):** Multiple constitutional amendment proposals have sought to replace the single five-year term with a four-year term renewable once, arguing that the single-term system creates "lame duck" dynamics from the midpoint of the presidency, discourages long-term policy planning, and prevents voters from rewarding good governance with re-election. President Moon Jae-in included this change in his 2018 constitutional amendment package, which failed in the National Assembly.

**National Assembly Term Limits:** No constitutional or statutory provision limits the number of terms a National Assembly member may serve. Several senior legislators have served six or more consecutive terms. Proposals to introduce legislative term limits have been introduced periodically but have never advanced beyond committee review, as sitting legislators have an obvious interest in maintaining their eligibility.

### Constitutional Court Jurisprudence

The Constitutional Court has addressed term limit issues in several decisions:

**Decision 96Hun-Ma74 (1996):** The Court affirmed that the prohibition on presidential re-election in Article 70 is a core constitutional provision reflecting the fundamental democratic values of the 1987 constitution.

**Decision 2004Hun-Na1 (2004, Presidential Impeachment):** In the impeachment proceedings against President Roh Moo-hyun, the Court's analysis implicitly relied on the single-term structure as a factor in evaluating the severity of the alleged constitutional violations, noting that the single-term presidency concentrates power and responsibility in ways that demand heightened compliance with constitutional norms.

## United States

### Legal Framework

**Twenty-Second Amendment (1951):** "No person shall be elected to the office of the President more than twice, and no person who has held the office of President, or acted as President, for more than two years of a term to which some other person was elected President shall be elected to the office of the President more than once." The amendment was ratified on February 27, 1951, following Franklin D. Roosevelt's unprecedented four election victories (1932, 1936, 1940, 1944).

**Historical Background:** Prior to the Twenty-Second Amendment, presidential term limits were governed solely by the precedent established by George Washington, who declined to seek a third term in 1796. This two-term tradition held for 150 years until Roosevelt broke it in 1940. The Republican-controlled 80th Congress proposed the amendment in 1947, and it was ratified by the required three-fourths of state legislatures in 1951.

**Congressional Term Limits:**

**U.S. Term Limits, Inc. v. Thornton (514 U.S. 779, 1995):** The Supreme Court held 5-4 that neither states nor Congress may alter or add to the qualifications for congressional service fixed by the Constitution (age, citizenship, and residency requirements in Article I, Sections 2 and 3). Arkansas's Amendment 73, which had imposed term limits on the state's congressional delegation, was struck down. Justice Stevens, writing for the majority, held that allowing states to impose additional qualifications would fundamentally undermine the Framers' vision of a uniform national legislature.

**State Legislative Term Limits:** Despite the Thornton ruling's applicability to federal legislators, states retain the power to impose term limits on their own state legislators. As of 2026, fifteen states impose term limits on state legislators:

| State | Year Adopted | House Limit | Senate Limit |
|-------|-------------|-------------|--------------|

| Colorado | 1990 | 8 years (consecutive) | 8 years (consecutive) |
| Michigan | 1992 | 6 years (lifetime) | 8 years (lifetime) |
| Florida | 1992 | 8 years (consecutive) | 8 years (consecutive) |
| Ohio | 1992 | 8 years (consecutive) | 8 years (consecutive) |
| Montana | 1992 | 8 years (consecutive) | 8 years (consecutive) |
| Arizona | 1992 | 8 years (consecutive) | 8 years (consecutive) |
| Missouri | 1992 | 8 years (lifetime) | 8 years (lifetime) |
| Oklahoma | 1990 | 12 years total in legislature | 12 years total in legislature |
| Nebraska | 2000 | N/A (unicameral) | 8 years (consecutive) |

### Scholarly Debate

The academic literature on term limits is divided. Proponents argue that term limits prevent political careerism, increase diversity in elected bodies, and reduce the incumbency advantage that distorts democratic competition. Critics contend that term limits transfer power from elected officials to unelected staff and lobbyists who possess institutional memory, reduce legislative expertise, and may increase partisan polarization by eliminating moderate long-serving members who build cross-party relationships.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's approach to term limits is distinctive: no limits on the most powerful executive position (Chancellor) but limits on the largely ceremonial head of state (President).

**Federal Chancellor (Bundeskanzler):** The Basic Law contains no provision limiting the Chancellor's tenure. The Chancellor is elected by the Bundestag under Article 63 GG and serves until the Bundestag elects a successor, whether through a regular election, a constructive vote of no-confidence under Article 67 GG, or a failed vote of confidence under Article 68 GG. This structure has produced two sixteen-year chancellorships:

| Chancellor | Period | Years | Reason for Departure |
|-----------|--------|-------|---------------------|
| Konrad Adenauer | 1949-1963 | 14 | Resigned under party pressure |
| Helmut Kohl | 1982-1998 | 16 | Lost federal election |
| Angela Merkel | 2005-2021 | 16 | Did not stand for re-election |

**Federal President (Bundespräsident):** Article 54(2) GG provides: "The Federal President shall be elected for a term of five years. Re-election for a consecutive term shall be permitted only once" (Der Bundespräsident wird auf fünf Jahre gewählt. Anschließende Wiederwahl ist nur einmal zulässig). This limits the President to a maximum of ten consecutive years. The provision does not prohibit a non-consecutive return to office, though this has never occurred. Presidents Theodor Heuss (1949-1959) and Richard von Weizsäcker (1984-1994) each served the maximum two terms.

**Länder Level:** German Länder generally do not impose term limits on their Minister-Presidents. Several Minister-Presidents have served extended tenures, including Franz Josef Strauß in Bavaria (1978-1988) and Kurt Biedenkopf in Saxony (1990-2002).

### Constructive Vote of No-Confidence

The absence of Chancellor term limits is balanced by the constructive vote of no-confidence mechanism under Article 67 GG, which allows the Bundestag to replace the Chancellor only by simultaneously electing a successor by an absolute majority. This mechanism has been used successfully only once -- in 1982, when Helmut Kohl replaced Helmut Schmidt -- and has been attempted unsuccessfully once -- in 1972, when Rainer Barzel failed to replace Willy Brandt by two votes. The constructive vote ensures governmental stability while maintaining democratic accountability as a substitute for formal term limits.

### Debate on Chancellor Term Limits

Following Angela Merkel's sixteen-year tenure, proposals for Chancellor term limits gained renewed attention. The CDU/CSU briefly debated incorporating a voluntary two-term commitment into its party statutes. However, constitutional scholars including Hans-Peter Schneider and Christoph Möllers have argued that formal Chancellor term limits would require a constitutional amendment and could conflict with the Bundestag's sovereign right to elect and maintain its preferred government under the principles of parliamentary democracy protected by the eternity clause (Article 79(3) GG).

## ECHR / EU

### EU Institutional Terms

The Treaty on European Union establishes term structures for key EU positions:

| Position | Term | Renewability | Legal Basis |
|----------|------|-------------|-------------|
| European Commission President | 5 years | Renewable (no formal limit) | TEU Art. 17(3) |
| European Council President | 2.5 years | Once renewable | TEU Art. 15(5) |
| High Representative for Foreign Affairs | 5 years | Renewable (no formal limit) | TEU Art. 18 |
| Members of European Parliament | 5 years | No term limit | TEU Art. 14(3) |
| Judges of the CJEU | 6 years | Renewable (no formal limit) | TFEU Art. 253 |

In practice, political constraints have limited renewal. No European Commission President has served more than two terms; José Manuel Barroso served two terms (2004-2014), the maximum to date.

### ECHR and Term Limit Removal as Democratic Backsliding

The European Court of Human Rights has engaged with term limits primarily through the lens of democratic backsliding -- the manipulation of constitutional provisions to extend incumbents' tenures.

**Venice Commission Guidelines:** The Venice Commission's Report on Term Limits (CDL-AD(2018)010) established that while term limits are not a requirement of democratic governance, their removal or circumvention during an incumbent's tenure raises serious rule of law concerns. The Commission identified several problematic patterns:
- Constitutional amendments specifically designed to allow sitting presidents to remain in power
- "Reset" provisions that restart term counts following constitutional changes
- Transitional provisions exempting current incumbents from new term limits

**Relevant Case Law:**

**Sejdic and Finci v. Bosnia and Herzegovina (2009):** While primarily addressing ethnic restrictions on candidacy rather than term limits, the Grand Chamber's analysis of the right to stand for election under Protocol 1, Article 3 established that restrictions on electoral eligibility must be proportionate and must not impair the essence of the right.

**Mugemangango v. Belgium (2020):** The Grand Chamber held that procedural fairness in electoral processes is required under Protocol 1, Article 3, establishing principles applicable to term limit disputes regarding the transparency and predictability of eligibility rules.

### Council of Europe and African Union Comparisons

The Council of Europe's monitoring mechanisms have noted the contrast between European and non-European approaches to term limits. While European democracies generally maintain stable constitutional term limit provisions (where they exist), several countries globally have amended constitutions to remove or extend term limits in recent years, including Russia (2020 constitutional amendments resetting Vladimir Putin's term count), China (2018 removal of presidential term limits), and several African and Central Asian states.

## Comparative Analysis

### Presidential vs. Parliamentary Systems

The most significant comparative insight concerns the relationship between system of government and term limit design. Presidential systems (South Korea, United States) tend to impose formal constitutional term limits on the executive, reflecting the concentration of power in a single elected individual. Parliamentary systems (Germany) rely on parliamentary accountability rather than term limits to prevent power concentration, on the theory that a chancellor who loses legislative confidence is automatically removed. The EU's hybrid structure combines elements of both approaches.

### Strictness Spectrum

South Korea represents the strictest end of the spectrum (single non-renewable term), the United States occupies a moderate position (two terms), Germany's chancellor faces no formal limits, and the EU's institutional leaders face political rather than legal constraints. The strictness generally correlates with each jurisdiction's historical experience with authoritarian concentration of power.

### Democratic Renewal vs. Governance Continuity

The fundamental policy trade-off runs through all jurisdictions. South Korea's single-term presidency maximizes democratic renewal but produces well-documented lame duck effects and policy discontinuity. Germany's unlimited chancellorship maximizes governance continuity but risks democratic fatigue, as the sixteen-year tenures of Kohl and Merkel demonstrated. The U.S. two-term limit attempts a middle path. Empirical evidence on which approach produces better governance outcomes remains contested.

### Anti-Circumvention Mechanisms

South Korea's Article 128(2) -- preventing an incumbent president from benefiting from a term limit amendment -- represents the most explicit anti-circumvention provision among the four jurisdictions. The Venice Commission's guidelines provide soft-law standards against circumvention but lack enforcement mechanisms. The ECHR's engagement with term limit manipulation remains indirect, operating through the general principles of free elections and rule of law rather than specific term limit jurisprudence.
