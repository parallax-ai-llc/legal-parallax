## Summary

Res judicata -- the principle that a final judgment on the merits by a court of competent jurisdiction is conclusive between the parties and constitutes a bar to subsequent litigation of the same cause of action -- is a foundational doctrine in every developed legal system, serving the dual purposes of protecting litigants from the burden of re-litigation and promoting judicial economy and the stability of legal relations, though its precise scope, operation, and exceptions vary significantly across jurisdictions. South Korea recognizes the binding force of judgments (기판력) under Article 216 of the Civil Procedure Act (민사소송법), which provides that a final and conclusive judgment has binding effect between the parties with respect to the matters determined in the operative part of the judgment (주문), with the Supreme Court in Decision 96Da11747 (1996) confirming that res judicata attaches only to the dispositive portion of the judgment and not to the reasoning, and Article 218 extending the binding effect to successors in interest and certain third parties, while the Constitutional Court in Decision 2012HeonBa298 (2014) addressed the tension between res judicata and constitutional rights. The United States applies two distinct but related doctrines: claim preclusion (res judicata in the narrow sense), which bars re-litigation of any claims that were or could have been raised in the prior action between the same parties, as articulated in Cromwell v. County of Sac, 94 U.S. 351 (1876), and issue preclusion (collateral estoppel), which prevents re-litigation of issues actually litigated and necessarily decided in the prior action, with the Supreme Court in Taylor v. Sturgell, 553 U.S. 880 (2008) rejecting virtual representation as a basis for binding non-parties and in Parklane Hosiery Co. v. Shore, 439 U.S. 322 (1979) approving offensive non-mutual collateral estoppel subject to the trial court's discretion. Germany applies the doctrine of Rechtskraft (legal force) under Sections 322-327 ZPO, with Section 322(1) providing that judgments are binding only to the extent of the operative portion (Tenor) of the decision on the claim or counterclaim asserted, reflecting a narrower scope of preclusion than the U.S. system, and the BGH in BGHZ 157, 47 (2004) confirming that the reasoning (Entscheidungsgründe) does not acquire binding force, while the ne bis in idem principle under Article 103(3) of the Basic Law provides absolute protection against double jeopardy in criminal proceedings. At the European level, the ECHR has recognized finality of judgments as an element of the rule of law under Article 6, with the Court in Brumarescu v. Romania [GC] (1999) holding that the quashing of a final judgment through extraordinary review violates legal certainty, while EU law addresses recognition and enforcement of foreign judgments through Regulation (EU) No. 1215/2012 (Brussels I Recast), which establishes automatic recognition without exequatur and defines limited grounds for refusal.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's res judicata doctrine operates through the concept of 기판력 (binding force of judgments), codified in Articles 216-218 of the Civil Procedure Act.

### Article 216 -- Binding Force of Final Judgments

**Article 216(1):** A final and conclusive judgment has binding force (기판력) between the parties. This binding force attaches when the judgment can no longer be challenged through ordinary appeal (항소, 상고).

**Article 216(2):** The binding force operates as of the time of the final oral argument (변론종결시) in the court that rendered the judgment. Facts arising after this date are not precluded and may form the basis of a new action.

### Scope of Binding Force

**Supreme Court Decision 96Da11747 (1996):** The Court held that res judicata attaches only to the dispositive portion of the judgment (주문) -- the court's operative ruling on the claims presented -- and not to the reasons given in support of that ruling. This principle has been consistently reaffirmed, most recently in Decision 2018Da287362 (2019).

This narrow scope means that:
- A finding of negligence in the reasoning of a tort judgment does not have binding force in subsequent proceedings.
- Factual determinations and legal interpretations in the reasoning may be re-litigated in a separate action involving different claims.
- The preclusive effect is limited to the specific claim adjudicated, as defined by the cause of action (소송물) presented.

### Cause of Action Theory (소송물론)

Korean procedural law has debated whether to adopt a narrow or broad definition of the cause of action:

- **Old Theory (구소송물론):** Defines the cause of action by the specific substantive right asserted. A claim based on tort and a claim based on contract arising from the same facts constitute separate causes of action.
- **New Theory (신소송물론):** Defines the cause of action by the factual transaction, similar to the U.S. transactional test. Claims arising from the same set of facts constitute a single cause of action.

The Supreme Court has not definitively adopted either theory but has generally followed the old theory, resulting in narrower preclusive effects than the U.S. system.

### Extension to Third Parties (Article 218)

**Article 218** extends the binding force of judgments to certain non-parties:

1. **Successors in interest (승계인):** Persons who acquire an interest in the subject matter after the litigation is commenced.
2. **Possessors on behalf of a party:** Persons who hold property on behalf of a party to the judgment.
3. **Statutory extensions:** Specific statutes may extend binding force to additional third parties (e.g., corporate litigation affecting shareholders).

### Retrial (재심)

**Articles 451-461** provide for retrial (재심) of final judgments on limited grounds:

| Ground | Article | Description |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| Judicial misconduct | Art. 451(1)(1) | Judge committed criminal offense in connection with the judgment |
| Forged evidence | Art. 451(1)(6) | Judgment based on forged or altered documents |
| False testimony | Art. 451(1)(7) | Judgment based on false testimony or expert opinion |
| Conflicting judgments | Art. 451(1)(8) | Judgment contradicts a prior final judgment between the same parties |
| Constitutional Court decision | Art. 451(1)(9) | Judgment based on statute later declared unconstitutional |
| New evidence | Art. 451(1)(10) | Discovery of dispositive evidence that could not have been produced in the prior action |

### Criminal Double Jeopardy

**Article 13(1) of the Constitution:** "No citizen shall be placed in double jeopardy." The Criminal Procedure Act reinforces this through Article 326(1), which requires dismissal of prosecution when a final judgment has been rendered on the same offense. The Supreme Court has interpreted "same offense" broadly, applying the basic facts test (기본적 사실관계의 동일성).

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has the most extensively developed and broadly applied preclusion doctrines of any jurisdiction, operating through two complementary doctrines: claim preclusion and issue preclusion.

### Claim Preclusion (Res Judicata)

Claim preclusion bars a party from re-litigating any claim that was or could have been raised in a prior action. The Restatement (Second) of Judgments (1982) articulates three requirements:

1. **Final judgment on the merits:** The prior action resulted in a valid, final judgment on the merits. Dismissals with prejudice and default judgments qualify; dismissals without prejudice generally do not.
2. **Same parties or their privies:** The subsequent action involves the same parties or persons in privity with them.
3. **Same claim or cause of action:** The subsequent action involves the same claim, defined under the transactional test as all claims arising from the same transaction or occurrence.

**Cromwell v. County of Sac, 94 U.S. 351 (1876):** The foundational case distinguishing between bar (claim preclusion) and merger (issue preclusion), establishing that a judgment bars not only the specific claim litigated but all claims that could have been raised in the prior action.

**Migra v. Warren City School District Board of Education, 465 U.S. 75 (1984):** Under 28 U.S.C. Section 1738 (Full Faith and Credit statute), federal courts must give state court judgments the same preclusive effect they would have under the law of the rendering state, including claims that could have been but were not raised, even if the unraised claims are federal.

### Issue Preclusion (Collateral Estoppel)

Issue preclusion prevents re-litigation of specific issues that were:
1. Actually litigated in the prior proceeding
2. Actually decided by the court or jury
3. Necessary to the judgment (not merely dictum or alternative holdings)
4. Decided by a court of competent jurisdiction in a final judgment

**Parklane Hosiery Co. v. Shore, 439 U.S. 322 (1979):** The Supreme Court approved offensive non-mutual collateral estoppel -- allowing a plaintiff who was not a party to the prior action to invoke issue preclusion against a defendant who litigated and lost the issue. The Court left the decision to the trial court's discretion, identifying factors including whether the plaintiff could easily have joined the prior action and whether it would be unfair to the defendant.

### Non-Party Preclusion

**Taylor v. Sturgell, 553 U.S. 880 (2008):** The Supreme Court rejected the doctrine of "virtual representation" as a basis for binding non-parties and identified six recognized categories of non-party preclusion:

1. Agreement to be bound by the prior litigation
2. Pre-existing substantive legal relationship (e.g., preceding and succeeding property owners)
3. Adequate representation in the prior action (e.g., class actions, trusts)
4. Assumption of control over the prior litigation
5. Litigation through a proxy
6. Special statutory schemes (e.g., bankruptcy, in rem proceedings)

### Federal-State Preclusion

The intersection of federal and state court systems creates additional complexity:

| Scenario | Rule |
|----------|------|
| State judgment in federal court | Full Faith and Credit (28 U.S.C. Section 1738) |
| Federal judgment in state court | Federal common law of preclusion; states generally apply it |
| Federal judgment in federal court | Federal common law of preclusion |
| Federal Section 1983 claims | Allen v. McCurry (1980): state criminal proceedings may preclude |

### Criminal Double Jeopardy

The Fifth Amendment provides: "nor shall any person be subject for the same offence to be twice put in jeopardy of life or limb." Key principles include:

- **Blockburger v. United States, 284 U.S. 299 (1932):** The same-elements test: two offenses are different if each requires proof of an element that the other does not.
- **Dual sovereignty doctrine (Gamble v. United States, 587 U.S. ___ (2019)):** Successive prosecutions by different sovereigns (federal and state) do not violate the Double Jeopardy Clause.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's res judicata doctrine operates through the concept of Rechtskraft (legal force), codified in Sections 322-327 ZPO for civil proceedings and Article 103(3) GG for criminal proceedings.

### Materielle Rechtskraft (Substantive Legal Force)

**Section 322(1) ZPO:** Judgments are binding (rechtskräftig) only to the extent of the decision on the claim or counterclaim that was asserted (Streitgegenstand). This provision establishes two critical limitations:

1. **Only the Tenor is binding:** The operative portion (Tenor) of the judgment -- the court's ruling on whether to grant or deny relief -- acquires binding force. The reasoning (Entscheidungsgründe) does not.
2. **Limited to the Streitgegenstand:** Binding force attaches only to the specific claim (Streitgegenstand) decided, defined by the combination of the relief sought and the factual basis (Lebenssachverhalt).

**BGH, BGHZ 157, 47 (2004):** The Federal Court of Justice reaffirmed that the binding force of a civil judgment does not extend to preliminary legal questions (Vorfragen) resolved in the reasoning, even if these questions were extensively litigated. A finding of negligence, a determination of property ownership, or an interpretation of a contract provision in the reasoning does not preclude re-litigation of these issues in subsequent proceedings involving different claims.

### No General Issue Preclusion

Unlike the U.S. system, Germany does not have a general doctrine of issue preclusion (Präjudizialität). Factual and legal determinations in the reasoning of a judgment may be freely re-litigated in subsequent proceedings involving different claims between the same parties. This represents the most significant structural difference from the U.S. approach.

Limited exceptions exist:
- **Section 322(2) ZPO (Set-Off):** When a defendant raises a set-off defense, the court's determination of the existence and amount of the counter-claim acquires binding force up to the amount of the set-off.
- **Section 256(2) ZPO (Intermediate Declaratory Judgment):** A party may request a declaratory judgment on a preliminary legal relationship during the proceedings, which then acquires binding force.

### Streitgegenstand (Cause of Action)

German procedural law defines the Streitgegenstand through a two-element test:

1. **Antrag (Relief Sought):** The specific remedy requested by the plaintiff.
2. **Lebenssachverhalt (Factual Basis):** The factual complex underlying the claim.

The prevailing view defines the Streitgegenstand broadly by the Lebenssachverhalt, similar to the U.S. transactional test. However, the absence of issue preclusion means that even when the Streitgegenstand is defined broadly, only the decision on the specific relief sought is precluded.

### Formelle Rechtskraft (Formal Legal Force)

**Section 705 ZPO:** A judgment acquires formal legal force (formelle Rechtskraft) when it can no longer be challenged through ordinary appeals (Berufung, Revision). Formal legal force is a prerequisite for substantive legal force and enforcement.

### Extension to Third Parties

Binding force generally applies only between the parties (inter partes effect). Extensions to third parties are limited and statutory:

- **Section 325 ZPO:** Successors in interest to the subject matter of the litigation are bound.
- **Section 326 ZPO:** Holders of property on behalf of a party are bound.
- **Section 327 ZPO:** In family law proceedings, certain judgments have erga omnes effect (e.g., status judgments in parentage proceedings).

### Restitutionsklage (Action for Restitution)

**Section 580 ZPO** provides for reopening final judgments on limited grounds:

1. Forged documents (Section 580(1))
2. False testimony or perjury (Section 580(2)-(3))
3. Criminal misconduct by a judge or representative (Section 580(4)-(5))
4. Conflict with a prior binding judgment (Section 580(7)(a))
5. Discovery of a prior decisive judgment (Section 580(7)(b))
6. ECHR judgment finding a violation that affected the domestic decision (Section 580(8), added 2006)

### Criminal Ne Bis In Idem

**Article 103(3) GG:** "No one may be punished for the same act more than once under general criminal law." This provision is absolute and does not admit exceptions comparable to the U.S. dual sovereignty doctrine. Section 264 StPO defines the scope of the "act" (Tat) broadly as the entire historical event, encompassing all possible legal qualifications.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR and Finality of Judgments

The ECHR has developed significant jurisprudence on the finality of judgments as an element of the rule of law and the right to a fair trial under Article 6.

**Brumarescu v. Romania [GC], Application No. 28342/95, Judgment of October 28, 1999:** The Grand Chamber held that the quashing of a final judgment by the Procurator General through an extraordinary appeal violated the principle of legal certainty inherent in Article 6(1). The Court stated: "One of the fundamental aspects of the rule of law is the principle of legal certainty, which requires, inter alia, that where the courts have finally determined an issue, their ruling should not be called into question."

**Ryabykh v. Russia, Application No. 52854/99, Judgment of July 24, 2003:** The Court found a violation where a supervisory review procedure allowed a final judgment to be quashed and the case remitted for new examination. The power of a higher court to set aside a binding judicial decision must be exercised only to correct fundamental defects.

**Salov v. Ukraine, Application No. 65518/01, Judgment of September 6, 2005:** The Court reiterated that the principle of legal certainty requires that parties have a right to rely on final judgments, and that extraordinary review mechanisms must be confined within narrow limits.

### Criminal Ne Bis In Idem Under ECHR and EU Law

**Protocol 7, Article 4 ECHR:** "No one shall be liable to be tried or punished again in criminal proceedings under the jurisdiction of the same State for an offence for which he has already been finally acquitted or convicted."

**Sergey Zolotukhin v. Russia [GC], Application No. 14939/03, Judgment of February 10, 2009:** The Grand Chamber harmonized the definition of "same offence" under Article 4 of Protocol 7, adopting the approach that the guarantee applies to prosecution or trial for a second "offence" that arises from identical facts or facts that are substantially the same, abandoning the prior inconsistent jurisprudence that focused on legal classification.

**Article 50 of the Charter of Fundamental Rights of the EU:** "No one shall be liable to be tried or punished again in criminal proceedings for an offence for which he or she has already been finally acquitted or convicted within the Union in accordance with the law." This provision extends the ne bis in idem principle to a transnational level within the EU.

**CJEU, Mantello (C-261/09, 2010):** The CJEU held that the concept of "same acts" under the Convention Implementing the Schengen Agreement (CISA, Article 54) and the EU Charter must be interpreted uniformly, focusing on the identity of the material acts irrespective of their legal classification.

### Brussels I Recast and Recognition of Judgments

**Regulation (EU) No. 1215/2012** establishes the framework for recognition and enforcement of judgments in civil and commercial matters within the EU:

- **Article 36:** A judgment given in a member state shall be recognized in the other member states without any special procedure being required (automatic recognition).
- **Article 45 (Grounds for Refusal):** Recognition may be refused if: (a) manifestly contrary to public policy; (b) the defendant was not served in sufficient time to arrange a defense; (c) irreconcilable with a judgment between the same parties in the addressed state; (d) irreconcilable with an earlier judgment from another member state or third state involving the same cause of action.

## Comparative Analysis

### Scope of Preclusion

The most significant divergence concerns the scope of what is precluded. The United States applies the broadest preclusion through both claim preclusion (all claims arising from the same transaction) and issue preclusion (specific issues actually litigated and decided). Germany and South Korea apply narrow preclusion limited to the operative portion of the judgment, with no general issue preclusion. This means that identical factual questions may be re-litigated in subsequent proceedings in Germany and South Korea if the claims are different.

### Definition of the "Claim"

While all jurisdictions apply some version of the transactional test for defining the scope of preclusion, the U.S. approach is the most aggressive: claims that could have been raised but were not are also precluded. Germany and South Korea preclude only the specific claim adjudicated, though the broad definition of Streitgegenstand in Germany limits the practical difference.

### Treatment of Reasoning

The universal principle among the civil law jurisdictions (Germany, South Korea) is that the reasoning of a judgment does not acquire binding force. The U.S. system, through issue preclusion, effectively gives binding force to factual and legal determinations in the reasoning when they were actually litigated and necessarily decided. This represents a fundamental structural difference.

### Non-Party Preclusion

All jurisdictions limit the binding effect of judgments on non-parties, but through different mechanisms. The U.S. approach (Taylor v. Sturgell's six categories) is the most developed. Germany and South Korea rely on statutory extensions to successors and related parties. The EU's Brussels I Recast focuses on automatic recognition between member states rather than extending the binding effect to new parties.
