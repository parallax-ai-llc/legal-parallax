## Summary

Euthanasia and assisted suicide occupy one of the most contested intersections of criminal law, medical ethics, and human rights. The legal landscape varies dramatically across jurisdictions: South Korea criminalizes active euthanasia and assisted suicide under Articles 252-253 of its Criminal Act while permitting withdrawal of life-sustaining treatment since 2018. The United States has no federal right to assisted suicide following *Washington v. Glucksberg* (1997), but fourteen jurisdictions now authorize medical aid in dying through state legislation. Germany, after the Federal Constitutional Court struck down Section 217 StGB in 2020 as unconstitutional, exists in a legal vacuum where assisted suicide is effectively unpunished but unregulated. The European Court of Human Rights, most recently in *Daniel Karsai v. Hungary* (2024), has consistently held that the Convention does not guarantee a right to assisted death, leaving member states a wide margin of appreciation.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea maintains a strict prohibition on active euthanasia and assisted suicide under the Korean Criminal Act (형법), while permitting the withdrawal of life-sustaining treatment through specialized legislation.

**Article 252 - Murder upon Request or with Consent (촉탁, 승낙에 의한 살인):** A person who kills another at that person's request or with their consent shall be punished by imprisonment for not less than one year and not more than ten years. Paragraph 2 extends the same penalty to anyone who instigates or aids another person to commit suicide.

**Article 253 - Murder upon Request through Fraudulent Means (위계 등에 의한 촉탁살인 등):** Where the acts described in Article 252 are committed by fraudulent means or the exercise of authority, the penalties for ordinary murder under Articles 250-251 apply, meaning sentences of five years to life imprisonment or death.

### The Well-Dying Act (2016/2018)

The Act on Hospice and Palliative Care and Decisions on Life-Sustaining Treatment for Patients at the End of Life (호스피스 완화의료 및 임종과정에 있는 환자의 연명의료결정에 관한 법률) was enacted on February 3, 2016 and took full effect on February 4, 2018, following a pilot program at 13 hospitals from October 2017.

Key provisions:

- **Scope:** Applies only to patients in the dying process with no possibility of recovery, as determined by the attending physician and one confirming physician.
- **Eligible treatments for withdrawal:** Cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR), mechanical ventilation, hemodialysis, chemotherapy, and other treatments designated by presidential decree.
- **Patient consent mechanisms:** (1) Advance Medical Directive (사전연명의료의향서) created by any adult aged 19 or older; (2) Life-Sustaining Treatment Plan (연명의료계획서) created with the attending physician; or (3) Unanimous agreement of all family members when the patient's wishes cannot be ascertained.
- **Hospice care:** The Act simultaneously expanded access to hospice and palliative care services.

Between February 2018 and April 2019, more than 45,000 patients exercised their right to forgo life-sustaining treatment under the Act.

### Landmark Cases

**Boramae Hospital Case (1997-2004):** In 1997, two physicians at Boramae Hospital in Seoul discharged a 58-year-old brain-damaged patient on a mechanical ventilator at the request of his wife, contrary to medical policy. The patient died shortly after discharge. In 1998, both physicians were convicted of aiding and abetting homicide, receiving suspended prison sentences. The Supreme Court upheld the convictions in June 2004 (Supreme Court 2004Do611). This case had a chilling effect on end-of-life medical decisions in South Korea, as physicians became reluctant to withdraw any life-sustaining treatment for fear of criminal prosecution.

**Severance Hospital "Grandmother Kim" Case (2008-2009):** A 77-year-old woman identified as "Grandmother Kim" (김 할머니) fell into a persistent vegetative state after a lung biopsy at Severance Hospital. Her family sought removal of the ventilator. On May 21, 2009, the Supreme Court of Korea (Supreme Court 2009Da17417) ruled that withdrawal of life-sustaining treatment could be permitted when a terminally ill patient would have wished for such withdrawal, based on inference of the patient's presumed intent. The Court grounded its decision in the constitutional right to dignity and the right to pursue happiness (Articles 10 and 37 of the Constitution). This decision directly catalyzed the legislative process that produced the 2018 Well-Dying Act.

### Proposed Legislation

In 2022, Rep. Ahn Kyu-baek of the Democratic Party of Korea introduced the "Act on Assisted Dying with Dignity" (존엄한 죽음에 관한 법률안), which would have permitted physician-assisted death for terminally ill patients. The bill was scrapped upon the expiration of the 21st National Assembly's term without reaching a vote. Public opinion polls indicate growing support: a 2024 Statista survey found a majority of Korean adults expressed a need for legal assisted suicide, though the issue remains deeply divisive.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has no federal statute governing euthanasia or assisted suicide. Active euthanasia is illegal in all 50 states and can be prosecuted as murder or manslaughter. Assisted suicide is governed state by state, creating a patchwork of laws.

**Constitutional baseline:** In two companion cases decided on June 26, 1997, the U.S. Supreme Court unanimously held that there is no constitutional right to physician-assisted suicide:

- **Washington v. Glucksberg, 521 U.S. 702 (1997):** The Court held that Washington State's ban on assisted suicide did not violate the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. Chief Justice Rehnquist, writing for the majority, applied a rational basis test and found the state had legitimate interests in preserving life, preventing suicide, protecting the integrity of the medical profession, and protecting vulnerable groups.
- **Vacco v. Quill, 521 U.S. 793 (1997):** The Court held that New York's prohibition on assisted suicide did not violate the Equal Protection Clause. The Court rejected the argument that there is no meaningful distinction between withdrawing life support and prescribing lethal medication, holding that the distinction between "letting die" and "making die" is rational and well-established.

Crucially, both decisions left states free to legalize assisted suicide through their own legislative processes.

### Common Safeguards in Aid-in-Dying States

Most state laws follow the Oregon model and require:

- The patient must be a mentally competent adult (18+).
- Diagnosis of a terminal illness with a prognosis of six months or less to live.
- Two oral requests separated by a waiting period (typically 15 days, though some states have shortened this).
- One written request signed by two witnesses.
- Confirmation by a consulting physician.
- The patient must self-administer the prescribed medication; no physician may administer it directly (distinguishing this from euthanasia).
- Physicians may conscientiously object and are not compelled to participate.

### Criminal Penalties in Prohibiting States

Approximately 40 states expressly criminalize assisted suicide. Penalties vary:

- **New York (prior to 2026 legalization):** Manslaughter in the second degree, Class C felony, up to 15 years.
- **Texas:** Punishable as a state jail felony (180 days to 2 years) or third-degree felony (2-10 years).
- **Florida:** Manslaughter, second-degree felony, up to 15 years.

### Landmark U.S. Cases

**Cruzan v. Director, Missouri Department of Health, 497 U.S. 261 (1990):** The Supreme Court recognized for the first time that competent adults have a constitutionally protected liberty interest in refusing unwanted medical treatment, including life-sustaining treatment. However, the Court upheld Missouri's requirement for clear and convincing evidence of the patient's wishes.

**Gonzales v. Oregon, 546 U.S. 243 (2006):** The Supreme Court held that the U.S. Attorney General could not use the Controlled Substances Act to prohibit physicians from prescribing drugs for assisted suicide under Oregon law, preserving state authority over medical practice.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German law distinguishes sharply between three categories of end-of-life conduct, each with different legal consequences:

**Section 216 StGB - Killing on Request (Totung auf Verlangen):** "If someone has been induced to kill by the express and earnest request of the person killed, a prison sentence of six months to five years shall be imposed." This provision criminalizes active euthanasia regardless of consent and remains in full force. Attempted killing on request is also punishable.

**Section 217 StGB - Commercial Promotion of Suicide (Geschaftsmassige Forderung der Selbsttotung):** Enacted in December 2015, this provision criminalized anyone who, on a commercial or organized basis, provided, procured, or arranged the opportunity for another person to commit suicide, with a penalty of up to three years imprisonment or a fine. The provision was struck down by the Federal Constitutional Court in 2020 (see below).

**Assisted Suicide (Beihilfe zum Suizid):** Because suicide is not a criminal offense under German law, participation in another person's suicide (providing means, advice, or support) is not punishable under the general principles of accessory liability. This was the legal position before Section 217 was enacted in 2015, and it is again the legal position after Section 217 was struck down in 2020.

### The BVerfG Landmark Decision (February 26, 2020)

In its judgment of February 26, 2020 (BVerfG, 2 BvR 2347/15), the Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) unanimously declared Section 217 StGB unconstitutional and void. The Court held:

- The general right of personality (allgemeines Personlichkeitsrecht) under Article 2(1) in conjunction with Article 1(1) of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz) encompasses a **right to a self-determined death** (Recht auf selbstbestimmtes Sterben).
- This right includes the freedom to take one's own life and to seek the voluntary assistance of third parties.
- The right is not limited to terminally ill patients or elderly persons but applies to every individual regardless of age, illness, or life stage.
- Section 217 StGB, by effectively eliminating access to assisted suicide services in practice, rendered the right to self-determined death "empty" and thus violated the Basic Law.
- The legislature may regulate assisted suicide to protect autonomous decision-making but may not effectively prohibit it.

### Legislative Stalemate (2020-2026)

Following the BVerfG ruling, the Bundestag has been unable to pass replacement legislation. On July 6, 2023, two competing draft bills were presented:

1. **The Helling-Plahr/Castellucci Bill:** Proposed a liberal framework permitting assisted suicide after two counseling sessions and a waiting period.
2. **The Castellucci/Kuenast Bill:** Proposed stricter regulation with mandatory psychiatric or psychotherapeutic assessment to exclude autonomy-limiting mental illness.

Neither bill achieved a majority. As of March 2026, assisted suicide in Germany remains in an unregulated legal gray area: it is not criminal, but there is no statutory framework governing access, safeguards, or professional obligations. Medical professional organizations, particularly the Bundesarztekammer (Federal Chamber of Physicians), have revised their professional guidelines to permit physicians to provide assisted suicide, though no physician can be compelled to do so.

### Key Distinctions in German Law

| Category | German Term | Legal Status | Penalty |
|----------|------------|-------------|---------|
| Active euthanasia (physician administers lethal drug) | Totung auf Verlangen | Criminal (Section 216 StGB) | 6 months - 5 years |

| Withdrawal of treatment at patient's request | Behandlungsabbruch | Legal | None |

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Convention Framework

The European Convention on Human Rights does not explicitly address euthanasia or assisted suicide. The Court has developed its jurisprudence through the interplay of several Convention articles:

- **Article 2 (Right to Life):** Imposes positive obligations on states to protect life. The Court has consistently held that Article 2 cannot be interpreted as conferring a "right to die" or a right to self-determination regarding death.
- **Article 3 (Prohibition of Torture):** Relevant where the absence of end-of-life options arguably subjects a person to inhuman or degrading treatment. The Court has not found a violation of Article 3 in assisted-suicide cases.
- **Article 8 (Right to Respect for Private Life):** The primary vehicle for claims. The Court recognizes that end-of-life decisions fall within the scope of private life, meaning that bans on assisted suicide constitute an interference with Article 8. However, such interference can be justified under Article 8(2) as necessary for the protection of health, morals, or the rights of others.
- **Article 14 (Non-Discrimination):** Invoked in cases where applicants argue that blanket bans disproportionately affect disabled persons who cannot end their lives without assistance.

### Landmark ECHR Cases

**Pretty v. United Kingdom (Application No. 2346/02, 2002):** Diane Pretty suffered from motor neurone disease and sought assurance that her husband would not be prosecuted for assisting her suicide under Section 2(1) of the UK Suicide Act 1961. The Court held:

- Article 2 does not confer a right to die: "the right to life...cannot, without a distortion of language, be interpreted as conferring the diametrically opposite right, namely a right to die."
- Article 8 was engaged, as the prohibition affected her ability to make choices about her own body and life. However, the interference was justified under Article 8(2) as necessary to protect the rights of vulnerable persons.
- No violation of Articles 3, 8, 9, or 14.

**Haas v. Switzerland (Application No. 31322/07, 2011):** Ernst Haas, who suffered from bipolar disorder, sought access to a lethal dose of sodium pentobarbital without a medical prescription. The Court held that Article 8 encompasses "an individual's right to decide by what means and at what point his or her life will end" but found no violation. Switzerland's requirement of a medical prescription served the legitimate aim of protecting people from hasty decisions and abuse.

**Koch v. Germany (Application No. 497/09, 2012):** Ulrich Koch's wife, who was almost completely paralyzed, wished to obtain a lethal dose of medication. The German authorities refused. The Court found a violation of Article 8 on procedural grounds: Germany had failed to examine the merits of the wife's claim, thereby violating Mr. Koch's right to have the substance of his complaint determined.

**Gross v. Switzerland (Application No. 67810/10, 2013-2014):** Alda Gross, an elderly woman who was not terminally ill, sought access to lethal medication. A Chamber judgment found a violation of Article 8, holding that Swiss law lacked sufficient clarity. However, the Grand Chamber declared the application inadmissible in September 2014 after discovering that Ms. Gross had already died by assisted suicide before the Chamber judgment, and that her lawyer had concealed this fact.

**Lambert and Others v. France (Application No. 46043/14, 2015):** Concerned withdrawal of artificial nutrition and hydration from Vincent Lambert, who was in a vegetative state. The Grand Chamber, by 12 votes to 5, held no violation of Article 2. The Court established that where there is no consensus among member states on end-of-life issues, states enjoy a wide margin of appreciation. The decision affirmed the importance of establishing the patient's wishes and the adequacy of the domestic decision-making process.

**Daniel Karsai v. Hungary (Application No. 32171/21, 2024):** Daniel Karsai, suffering from amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS), challenged Hungary's blanket ban on assisted suicide, including its extraterritorial provisions that criminalized assisting a Hungarian citizen to travel abroad for assisted death. On June 13, 2024, the Court held:

- No violation of Article 8: Hungary's ban fell within its margin of appreciation.
- The Court reaffirmed that the Convention does not guarantee a right to physician-assisted death.
- However, the Court noted that Hungary's obligation to protect lives of vulnerable persons who might be pressured to die justified the restriction.
- Importantly, the Court also held that states which *do* permit assisted suicide do not thereby violate their positive obligations under Article 2.

### EU Member State Landscape (2026)

There is no EU-wide legislation on euthanasia or assisted suicide. The European Parliament has issued briefings on the topic but has no legislative competence in this area, which remains under national sovereignty. The current landscape among EU member states:

| Status | Countries |
|--------|-----------|
| **Euthanasia legal** | Netherlands (2002), Belgium (2002), Luxembourg (2009), Spain (2021) |
| **Euthanasia legislated but not yet in force** | Portugal (2023 law; implementing regulation pending as of 2026) |
| **Assisted suicide legal** | Germany (since 2020 BVerfG ruling, unregulated), Austria (since 2022), Italy (following Constitutional Court ruling in *Cappato/DJ Fabo* case, 2019) |
| **Both prohibited** | All other EU member states, including France, Poland, Ireland, Hungary |

Outside the EU, Switzerland remains the most prominent jurisdiction permitting assisted suicide through organizations such as Dignitas and Exit, under Article 115 of the Swiss Penal Code (which only criminalizes assistance motivated by "selfish motives").

## Comparative Analysis

### The Central Tension: Autonomy vs. Protection

All four jurisdictions grapple with the same fundamental tension between individual autonomy (the right to determine one's own death) and state protection of life (preventing abuse, protecting vulnerable persons). However, they resolve this tension differently:

- **South Korea** prioritizes collective values, family decision-making, and medical authority. Even its 2018 liberalization is limited to withdrawal of treatment in terminal cases, not active assistance in dying. The Confucian emphasis on filial piety and the sanctity of life continues to shape the legal framework.
- **The United States** delegates the question to individual states under its federalist structure, producing the widest internal variation of any jurisdiction studied. The Supreme Court's refusal to constitutionalize either a right to assisted suicide or a prohibition on it ensures continuing state-level experimentation.
- **Germany** has gone furthest in constitutionalizing an individual right to self-determined death, yet paradoxically lacks implementing legislation. The BVerfG's 2020 decision represents the most expansive judicial recognition of a right to die in any major jurisdiction, untethered to terminal illness.
- **The ECHR** framework defers to national governments through the margin of appreciation doctrine, effectively permitting the full spectrum from blanket prohibition to regulated legalization. The Court acts as a floor (no state may fail to protect life) but sets no ceiling on permissiveness.

| Conduct | South Korea | United States | Germany | ECHR Standard |
|---------|------------|---------------|---------|---------------|
| Administering lethal injection at patient's request | 1-10 years (Art. 252) | Murder/manslaughter (varies by state) | 6 months - 5 years (Section 216) | Within state discretion |

| Withdrawing life support at patient's request | Legal (2018 Act) | Legal nationwide | Legal | Permitted (Lambert) |
| Assisting travel abroad for euthanasia | Not specifically addressed | Not specifically addressed | Not criminal | Bans permissible (Karsai) |

### Trends and Trajectory

1. **Expanding legalization:** The number of jurisdictions permitting some form of assisted dying continues to grow. In the U.S. alone, the number of states with aid-in-dying laws has roughly doubled since 2018.
2. **From passive to active:** Most jurisdictions began by permitting withdrawal of treatment (passive euthanasia) before considering active assistance. South Korea is currently at this transitional stage.
3. **Procedural safeguards as compromise:** Where assisted dying is legalized, detailed procedural safeguards (waiting periods, multiple physician confirmations, mental competency assessments) serve as the political and legal compromise between autonomy and protection.
4. **Judicial leadership:** In both Germany (BVerfG 2020) and the U.S. (state court decisions in Montana), courts have been ahead of legislatures in recognizing end-of-life rights, creating pressure for legislative action.
