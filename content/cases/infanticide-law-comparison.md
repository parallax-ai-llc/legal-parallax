## Summary

Infanticide -- the killing of an infant, particularly by its mother -- has been treated with distinctive legal approaches across jurisdictions, reflecting deep tensions between the severity of homicide and the recognition that postpartum mental disturbance may reduce a mother's culpability. South Korea addresses infanticide through Article 251 of the Criminal Act (형법), which provides a specific reduced penalty for a lineal ascendant who kills a child "to prevent disgrace" (치욕을 은폐하기 위하여) during or immediately after childbirth -- a provision reflecting the historical Confucian concern with family honor and shame, punishable by imprisonment of up to ten years; this provision has faced growing criticism from feminist legal scholars and human rights organizations as reflecting patriarchal values and failing to address the medical realities of postpartum mental illness, and the National Human Rights Commission of Korea (국가인권위원회) has recommended its reform. The United States does not recognize infanticide as a separate offense at the federal level, treating the killing of an infant as murder or manslaughter under general homicide statutes; however, postpartum psychosis and postpartum depression may serve as the basis for an insanity defense or diminished capacity defense in some jurisdictions, as illustrated by the high-profile case of Andrea Yates (2001), who drowned her five children and was ultimately found not guilty by reason of insanity on retrial. Germany abolished its separate infanticide provision (former Section 217 StGB -- Kindestötung) in 1998, previously providing reduced penalties for a mother who killed her "illegitimate" (unehelich) child during or immediately after birth; the abolition reflected both the constitutional prohibition on discrimination against children born outside marriage (Article 6(5) GG) and the recognition that the provision's rationale -- honor and shame regarding illegitimate births -- was incompatible with modern values, with postpartum mental conditions now addressed through general diminished capacity provisions (Section 21 StGB). The ECHR has addressed infanticide through the positive obligations doctrine under Article 2, and the European comparative landscape reveals a divide between jurisdictions that maintain specific infanticide statutes (England and Wales under the Infanticide Act 1938, as amended 2009) and those that have abolished or never enacted such provisions.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

**Article 251 -- Infanticide (영아살해):** "A lineal ascendant who kills a child during or immediately after childbirth to prevent disgrace shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than ten years." This provision creates a privileged form of homicide -- the penalty is significantly lower than for murder under Article 250 (death, life imprisonment, or 5+ years).

### Elements of the Offense

**Perpetrator -- Lineal Ascendant (직계존속):** The provision applies only to lineal ascendants of the victim, which includes the mother, grandparents, or other direct ancestors. In practice, it almost exclusively applies to the birth mother.

**Timing -- During or Immediately After Childbirth (분만 중 또는 분만 직후):** The act must occur during the birth process or immediately afterwards. The Supreme Court has interpreted "immediately after" restrictively:

**Supreme Court Decision 72도1084 (1972):** The Court held that "immediately after childbirth" requires a close temporal connection to the birth, during the period when the mother may be in a state of physical and emotional disturbance associated with delivery. A killing occurring hours or days after birth does not qualify.

**Motive -- Prevention of Disgrace (치욕의 은폐):** The provision requires that the killing be motivated by a desire to conceal the shame or disgrace associated with the birth. This element reflects historical Korean social attitudes toward births outside marriage, births to very young mothers, or births resulting from illicit relationships.

### Criticism and Reform Proposals

The infanticide provision has drawn sustained criticism:

**Gender Discrimination:** Critics argue that the provision reflects patriarchal values, reducing the mother's culpability based on her desire to preserve family honor rather than recognizing the medical realities of postpartum mental illness. The provision essentially treats the mother's shame as a mitigating factor rather than her mental state.

**National Human Rights Commission Recommendation (2009):** The NHRC (국가인권위원회) recommended reform of Article 251, arguing that the provision's emphasis on "disgrace" reflects outdated social attitudes and fails to account for the medical understanding of postpartum conditions.

**Feminist Legal Scholarship:** Korean feminist scholars such as Professor Cho Kuk (조국) have argued that Article 251 should be replaced with a provision that recognizes postpartum mental disturbance as the mitigating factor, consistent with international approaches.

**Constitutional Challenge:** Scholars have raised questions about whether Article 251's differentiation based on "disgrace" rather than mental condition violates the constitutional equality principle (Article 11 of the Korean Constitution) and the right to dignity (Article 10).

### Practical Application

In practice, prosecutions under Article 251 have declined significantly as social stigma around unmarried motherhood has decreased and as access to contraception and reproductive healthcare has expanded. When infanticide cases do arise, they increasingly involve postpartum mental illness, which prosecutors may address through Article 10 (insanity/diminished capacity) rather than Article 251.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States does not recognize infanticide as a separate offense. The killing of an infant by its mother is prosecuted under general homicide statutes as murder (first or second degree) or manslaughter.

### Postpartum Mental Illness as Defense

Postpartum mental conditions may serve as the basis for defenses under general criminal law:

**Insanity Defense:** Defendants may raise postpartum psychosis as the basis for an insanity defense. The applicable standard varies by jurisdiction:
- **M'Naghten Test:** Defendant did not understand the nature of the act or did not know it was wrong (majority of states)
- **Model Penal Code/ALI Test:** Defendant lacked substantial capacity to appreciate the criminality of the act or to conform conduct to the law (minority of states)
- **Durham/Product Test:** The criminal act was the "product" of mental disease or defect (largely abandoned)

**Diminished Capacity:** In jurisdictions that recognize it, postpartum depression or psychosis may reduce murder to manslaughter by negating the specific intent element.

### The Andrea Yates Case (2001)

The case of Andrea Yates became the defining American case on infanticide and postpartum mental illness:

**Facts:** On June 20, 2001, Andrea Yates drowned her five children (ages 6 months to 7 years) in a bathtub at the family home in Clear Lake City, Texas. Yates had a documented history of severe postpartum depression and postpartum psychosis, including prior suicide attempts and hospitalizations.

**First Trial (2002):** Yates was convicted of capital murder. The prosecution secured testimony from Dr. Park Dietz that Yates had been influenced by a Law & Order episode depicting a similar scenario. This testimony was later revealed to be false -- no such episode existed.

**Appeal and Retrial (2005-2006):** The conviction was overturned due to the false testimony. On retrial in July 2006, Yates was found not guilty by reason of insanity and committed to a state mental health facility.

**Legal Impact:** The Yates case generated nationwide debate about the adequacy of the insanity defense for postpartum psychosis and prompted legislative proposals in several states.

### State Legislative Responses

| State | Provision | Effect |
|-------|-----------|--------|
| Texas | Andrea Yates Act proposal (not enacted) | Would have allowed juries to find defendant "guilty but mentally ill" |
| Illinois | Perinatal Mental Health Disorders Act (2008) | Established screening and treatment programs; no specific criminal provision |
| New Jersey | Postpartum Depression Education Act | Focuses on prevention and education rather than criminal law |

### Safe Haven Laws

All 50 states have enacted "safe haven" or "baby surrender" laws allowing parents to legally surrender newborns at designated locations (hospitals, fire stations, police stations) without criminal prosecution, provided the infant is unharmed. These laws aim to prevent infanticide by providing an alternative to abandonment.

## Germany

### Legal Framework -- Historical and Current

**Former Section 217 StGB -- Infanticide (Kindestötung):** "A mother who kills her illegitimate child during or immediately after birth shall be punished with imprisonment for not less than three years." This provision was in force from 1871 until its abolition in 1998.

### Rationale and Abolition

The former Section 217 was premised on three rationales:
1. **Social stigma:** Unmarried motherhood carried severe social consequences in German society
2. **Reduced culpability:** The physical and emotional stress of childbirth was presumed to diminish the mother's capacity for rational judgment
3. **Illegitimacy distinction:** The provision applied only to killings of "illegitimate" (unehelich) children

**Abolition -- Sixth Criminal Law Reform Act (6. Strafrechtsreformgesetz, 1998):** The provision was abolished effective April 1, 1998, for several reasons:

**Constitutional Concerns:** Article 6(5) of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz) provides that "children born outside of marriage shall be provided by legislation with the same opportunities for physical and mental development and for their position in society as are enjoyed by those born within marriage." The distinction between legitimate and illegitimate children in Section 217 was increasingly viewed as incompatible with this constitutional mandate.

**Changed Social Conditions:** The social stigma attached to births outside marriage had dramatically decreased. The rationale of "preventing disgrace" was no longer socially or legally tenable.

**Medical Understanding:** Postpartum mental conditions were better addressed through the general diminished capacity provision (Section 21 StGB) rather than through a categorical reduction based on the child's legitimacy status.

### Current Legal Treatment

Since 1998, the killing of an infant by its mother is treated under general homicide provisions:

**Section 212 StGB -- Manslaughter (Totschlag):** 5-15 years imprisonment. Applies where the mother intentionally kills the infant.

**Section 213 StGB -- Less Serious Case of Manslaughter (Minder schwerer Fall des Totschlags):** 1-10 years. May apply where the mother acted under exceptional emotional distress.

**Section 222 StGB -- Negligent Killing (Fahrlässige Tötung):** Up to 5 years. Applies where the death results from negligence.

**Section 21 StGB -- Diminished Capacity (Verminderte Schuldfähigkeit):** Provides for discretionary sentence reduction where the offender's capacity to appreciate the wrongfulness of the act or to act accordingly was "substantially diminished" at the time of the offense. This provision is now used to account for postpartum psychosis or severe postpartum depression.

### Babyklappe and Anonymous Birth

Germany has developed alternative mechanisms to prevent infanticide:

**Babyklappe (Baby Hatch):** First established in Hamburg in 2000, baby hatches allow mothers to anonymously surrender newborns in a safe, warm, monitored container. Approximately 100 baby hatches operate across Germany.

**Confidential Birth (Vertrauliche Geburt):** The Act on the Expansion of Assistance for Pregnant Women and the Regulation of Confidential Birth (Gesetz zum Ausbau der Hilfen für Schwangere und zur Regelung der vertraulichen Geburt, 2014) allows women to give birth under medical supervision while keeping their identity confidential. The child's right to know their origins is preserved through sealed records accessible at age 16.

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

**Article 2 -- Right to Life:** The ECHR requires states to maintain effective criminal law frameworks to protect life, including the lives of newborns. The positive obligations doctrine requires both substantive protections (adequate laws) and procedural protections (effective investigation of deaths).

**Kontrová v. Slovakia (Application No. 7510/04, Judgment of May 31, 2007):** While addressing domestic violence rather than infanticide directly, the Court established that state authorities' failure to protect children known to be at risk of violence from family members violates Article 2. This principle extends to situations where authorities are aware of risk factors for infanticide.

**Article 3 -- Prohibition of Torture and Inhuman Treatment:** Relevant in cases where infants survive attempted infanticide and suffer lasting injuries.

### European Comparative Landscape

The treatment of infanticide varies significantly across European jurisdictions:

| Finland | Yes -- Chapter 21, Section 4 Penal Code | Mother who kills newborn under influence of disturbance caused by childbirth |

### EU Framework

The EU has not specifically addressed infanticide but has adopted instruments relevant to child protection:

**Directive 2011/93/EU on Combating the Sexual Abuse and Sexual Exploitation of Children:** While focused on sexual offenses, this directive establishes the principle that EU member states must maintain effective criminal law protections for children.

**EU Charter of Fundamental Rights, Article 24:** "Children shall have the right to such protection and care as is necessary for their well-being."

## Comparative Analysis

### Theoretical Foundations

Three distinct rationales underlie reduced penalties for infanticide:

**Honor/Shame Model (Historical South Korea, Historical Germany):** The original rationale in both jurisdictions focused on the social disgrace associated with illegitimate or unwanted births. This model has been widely criticized as reflecting patriarchal values and has been abandoned in Germany.

**Postpartum Mental Disturbance Model (England/Wales, Finland):** These jurisdictions specifically recognize the effects of childbirth on the mother's mental state as the basis for reduced culpability. This model aligns with medical evidence regarding postpartum psychosis.

**General Diminished Capacity Model (United States, Modern Germany):** These jurisdictions address postpartum conditions through general mental health defenses rather than specific infanticide provisions. This approach avoids the need for a separate offense but may require the defense to meet high evidentiary thresholds.

### Gender and Equality

The treatment of infanticide raises fundamental gender equality questions:
- Specific infanticide provisions apply almost exclusively to women and may be viewed as either protective (recognizing unique vulnerability) or paternalistic (reducing women's agency)
- The shift from "disgrace" to "mental disturbance" as the mitigating factor represents a move from patriarchal values to medical understanding
- Safe haven laws provide a gender-neutral alternative focused on preventing harm rather than reducing punishment

### Global Trend

The global trend moves toward abolishing specific infanticide provisions in favor of addressing postpartum mental conditions through general diminished capacity doctrines and preventing infanticide through safe haven laws and maternal mental health support.
