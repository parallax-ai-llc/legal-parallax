## 요약

Insolvency law across the Global South has undergone transformative reform in the 21st century, driven by the recognition that effective bankruptcy regimes are essential for credit markets, entrepreneurship, and economic resilience. China's Enterprise Bankruptcy Law (企业破产法, 2006), supplemented by specialized bankruptcy courts established since 2019, addresses the politically sensitive challenge of SOE insolvency while grappling with "zombie enterprise" elimination. India's Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code, 2016 (IBC) represents the most ambitious single reform, creating an entirely new institutional architecture -- including the National Company Law Tribunal (NCLT), Insolvency Professionals, and Information Utilities -- that reduced average resolution time from over four years to approximately 450 days and has resolved claims worth over INR 3.16 lakh crore through resolution plans by 2025. South Africa's business rescue procedure under Chapter 6 of the Companies Act 71 of 2008, modeled partly on the US Chapter 11 and the UK administration procedure, has emerged as an influential model across sub-Saharan Africa but faces criticism for low success rates (approximately 12-15% of business rescue proceedings result in a successful plan). Brazil's judicial recovery (recuperação judicial) under Law 11,101/2005, as substantially amended by Law 14,112/2020, handles large-scale corporate restructurings including the landmark Oi S.A. telecommunications restructuring (BRL 65 billion in debt, the largest in Latin American history). The Philippines' Financial Rehabilitation and Insolvency Act of 2010 (FRIA, Republic Act No. 10142) introduced modern rehabilitation and liquidation procedures, while Indonesia's Bankruptcy Law (Law 37/2004) operates through the Commercial Court with notable enforcement challenges.

## 중국

### 법적 체계

The Enterprise Bankruptcy Law of the People's Republic of China (中华人民共和国企业破产法), adopted on August 27, 2006 and effective June 1, 2007, replaced the earlier Enterprise Bankruptcy Law (Trial Implementation) of 1986 (applicable only to SOEs) and the civil procedure provisions governing collective enterprises and private companies. The 2006 law unified bankruptcy procedures for all enterprise types and introduced three proceedings:

- **Liquidation (破产清算, Chapter 10)**: Court-supervised winding up and asset distribution. The priority waterfall under Article 113 provides: bankruptcy expenses and common benefit debts (Article 43) are paid first; then employee wages, medical expenses, and social insurance premiums owed before bankruptcy acceptance; then tax claims; and finally ordinary unsecured claims.
- **Reorganization (重整, Chapter 8)**: Debtor-in-possession or administrator-managed restructuring. Article 79 permits a debtor or administrator to propose a reorganization plan within six months (extendable by three months). Article 85 requires vote by classified creditor groups, with secured creditors, employee claims, tax claims, and ordinary unsecured creditors voting separately. Article 87 permits "cram-down" approval by the court if at least one creditor class approves and the plan is fair and equitable.
- **Conciliation/Settlement (和解, Chapter 9)**: A simpler consensual arrangement between the debtor and unsecured creditors, requiring approval by a majority of unsecured creditors holding more than two-thirds of the total unsecured claims.

The Supreme People's Court (SPC) has issued multiple judicial interpretations, including: Interpretation (I) on Application of the Enterprise Bankruptcy Law (2011); Interpretation (II) on Determination of Debtor's Property (2013); and Interpretation (III) on the Liquidation Obligations of Creditors (2019). These interpretations address practical issues including standards for accepting bankruptcy petitions (the debtor must be unable to pay matured debts and have insufficient assets or clearly lack solvency), administrator duties, and creditor claim verification procedures.

### Bankruptcy Courts

China established specialized bankruptcy courts in Shenzhen (January 2019), Beijing (January 2019), and Shanghai (February 2019), with over 15 additional cities subsequently establishing dedicated bankruptcy divisions or courts. These courts address jurisdictional complexities previously caused by local government interference in bankruptcy proceedings -- particularly reluctance by intermediate people's courts to accept bankruptcy petitions against local SOEs and large employers due to employment and social stability concerns.

The Shenzhen Bankruptcy Court has been particularly innovative, including:

- **Personal Bankruptcy Pilot**: The Shenzhen Special Economic Zone Personal Bankruptcy Regulation (深圳经济特区个人破产条例), effective March 1, 2021, is China's first personal bankruptcy law, allowing individuals domiciled in Shenzhen with debts exceeding RMB 300,000 to apply for liquidation (three-year discharge period), reorganization, or settlement. The regulation requires debtors to undergo a "honest but unfortunate" assessment and restricts access for debtors with fraudulent or speculative debts.
- **Simplified Procedures**: Streamlined procedures for small enterprise bankruptcies with liabilities under RMB 10 million, reducing timelines from an average of 18 months to 3-6 months.

### Zombie Enterprise Elimination

The elimination of "zombie enterprises" (僵尸企业, jiangshi qiye) -- SOEs that are insolvent but kept alive through government subsidies and bank forbearance -- has been a central policy objective since the State Council's 2016 guidance on disposing of zombie enterprises and resolving excess capacity. The policy was implemented primarily through:

- **Supply-side structural reform** in steel and coal sectors, with approximately 1.4 billion tonnes of excess steel capacity and 800 million tonnes of excess coal capacity eliminated during 2016-2020.
- **Administrative bankruptcy filing push**: SASAC directed central SOEs to resolve zombie enterprise subsidiaries, with approximately 1,900 zombie enterprises disposed of by central SOEs during 2016-2020.
- **Bankruptcy-reorganization for viable enterprises**: Major reorganizations including Dongbei Special Steel Group (RMB 70 billion in debt, reorganized in 2017), Bohai Steel Group (RMB 192 billion in debt, China's largest private enterprise bankruptcy in 2018), and HNA Group (RMB 1.2 trillion in total liabilities, reorganization approved 2021-2022).

The HNA Group bankruptcy (2021-2022) was the largest and most complex corporate restructuring in Chinese history. The Hainan No. 1 Intermediate People's Court consolidated over 320 affiliated entities into a single coordinated proceeding and approved a reorganization plan that wrote down approximately 95% of ordinary unsecured creditor claims while transferring aviation and airport assets to newly established entities.

## 인도

### 법적 체계

The Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code, 2016 (IBC), enacted on May 28, 2016 and progressively brought into force through 2016-2017, created an entirely new institutional architecture for corporate and individual insolvency:

- **National Company Law Tribunal (NCLT)**: Adjudicating authority for corporate insolvency, with 15 benches across India. The NCLT admits applications for Corporate Insolvency Resolution Process (CIRP) under Section 7 (by financial creditors), Section 9 (by operational creditors), or Section 10 (by the corporate debtor itself).
- **Insolvency and Bankruptcy Board of India (IBBI)**: Regulatory body overseeing insolvency professionals, insolvency professional agencies, and information utilities. The IBBI issues regulations, conducts inspections, and disciplines errant professionals.
- **Committee of Creditors (CoC)**: Comprising financial creditors with voting share proportional to their admitted claims, the CoC drives the resolution process by evaluating and approving resolution plans. Section 30(4) requires the CoC to approve a plan by a vote of not less than 66% of voting share.
- **Resolution Professional (RP)**: Licensed insolvency professional who manages the corporate debtor during CIRP as a going concern.

### CIRP Process

The CIRP timeline has been a critical feature and point of contention:

1. **Admission**: The NCLT must admit or reject an application within 14 days of filing (Section 7(4)/9(5)/10(4)).
2. **Moratorium**: Upon admission, Section 14 imposes a moratorium prohibiting institution or continuation of suits, transferring assets, enforcement of security interests, and recovery of property occupied by the corporate debtor.
3. **Public Announcement and Claims**: The interim resolution professional makes a public announcement inviting claims within 90 days.
4. **Resolution Plan**: The CoC evaluates resolution plans from prospective resolution applicants (Section 29A disqualifies promoters of NPA companies, willful defaulters, and connected persons). The plan must provide for payment to operational creditors of not less than the amount receivable in liquidation (Section 30(2)(b)).
5. **Time Limit**: Section 12 provides for completion within 180 days, extendable by 90 days. The Supreme Court in Committee of Creditors of Essar Steel India Limited v. Satish Kumar Gupta (2019) 15 SCC 1 held that the outer limit of 330 days (including extensions and litigation time) is mandatory but directory, and delays attributable to judicial proceedings should be excluded.

### 주요 판례

The IBC has generated a substantial body of Supreme Court jurisprudence:

- **Swiss Ribbons Pvt. Ltd. v. Union of India, (2019) 4 SCC 17**: Upheld the constitutional validity of the IBC against challenges on grounds of violation of Articles 14, 19(1)(g), and 300A. The Court held that the differentiation between financial and operational creditors was based on intelligible differentia bearing a rational nexus with the legislative object.
- **Committee of Creditors of Essar Steel India Limited v. Satish Kumar Gupta, (2019) 15 SCC 1**: Established that the CoC's commercial decision on distribution of value under a resolution plan is paramount and can only be interfered with on limited grounds; that the NCLT/NCLAT cannot substitute their commercial wisdom for the CoC's; and that Section 30(2)(b) requires only that operational creditors receive not less than the liquidation value, not an equitable share of resolution value.
- **Jaypee Kensington Boulevard Apartments Welfare Association v. NBCC (India) Ltd., (2021) 2 SCC 1 (Jaypee Infratech)**: The Supreme Court approved the resolution plan for Jaypee Infratech (the first large real estate insolvency), holding that homebuyers (treated as financial creditors under the 2018 amendment) could vote in the CoC and that the NCLT could modify the plan to protect their interests within the statutory framework.
- **Vidarbha Industries Power Ltd. v. Axis Bank Ltd., (2022) 8 SCC 352**: The Supreme Court clarified that the NCLT has discretion under Section 7(5)(a) to reject an admission application even where the corporate debtor has defaulted, if the circumstances warrant (e.g., debtor's financial position is viable, disputes exist about the debt).

### Outcomes and Statistics

By December 2025, the IBC had yielded the following outcomes (per IBBI data):

- Total CIRPs admitted: approximately 7,700
- CIRPs closed through resolution plans: approximately 1,000 (with aggregate resolution value exceeding INR 3.16 lakh crore)
- CIRPs closed through liquidation: approximately 2,100
- Average resolution timeline: approximately 450 days (against the statutory target of 180-330 days)
- Recovery rate for financial creditors through resolution plans: approximately 32% of admitted claims (compared to approximately 5-8% under previous regimes)
- Haircut percentage: approximately 68% on average for financial creditor claims

## 아프리카

### 남아프리카공화국

South Africa's business rescue procedure (Chapter 6, Companies Act 71 of 2008, effective May 1, 2011) was designed to provide a debtor-friendly restructuring mechanism as an alternative to judicial management (the prior regime under the Companies Act 61 of 1973, widely considered a failure):

- **Section 128(1)(b)**: Defines business rescue as proceedings to facilitate the rehabilitation of a company that is financially distressed by providing for temporary supervision of the company and management of its affairs; a temporary moratorium on rights of claimants; and the development and implementation of a plan to rescue the company or achieve a better return for creditors than immediate liquidation.
- **Section 129**: Voluntary commencement by board resolution when the board reasonably believes the company is financially distressed (unable to pay debts as they become due within the next six months or will likely become insolvent within the next six months).
- **Section 131**: Court-ordered commencement on application by any affected person, including a creditor, shareholder, employee, or trade union.
- **Section 135**: The business rescue practitioner is appointed and takes full management control of the company, displacing the board (unlike US Chapter 11's debtor-in-possession model).
- **Section 150**: The business rescue plan must be published within 25 business days after appointment (extendable) and approved by a majority of creditors' voting interests (more than 75% in value for each class if class voting is used).
- **Section 134**: During business rescue, post-commencement financing (PCF) receives super-priority over all unsecured claims and subordinated only to employee claims for services rendered during rescue.

Notable business rescue proceedings include:

- **South African Airways (SAA, 2019-2021)**: SAA entered business rescue in December 2019, with the business rescue practitioner Les Matuson initially and then a team appointed by the government. The proceeding was controversial due to the ZAR 10.5 billion government bailout required to fund the rescue plan, which was adopted in 2021, resulting in a significantly downsized airline.
- **Comair (2020-2022)**: Comair, operator of British Airways' South African franchise and kulula.com, entered business rescue in May 2020 due to COVID-19. After initial restructuring attempts, the company was placed in final liquidation in June 2022 when the rescue plan became unviable.
- **Edcon (2020)**: The Edgars/Jet retail group entered business rescue in April 2020, resulting in closure of approximately 1,000 stores and loss of over 20,000 jobs.

The Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA) and Labour Court play significant roles in business rescue proceedings, as Section 136(1)(a) provides that employees retain their employment during business rescue and the business rescue practitioner cannot unilaterally retrench employees without following the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995, Section 189/189A procedures.

### 나이지리아

Nigeria lacks a dedicated insolvency statute. Insolvency procedures are governed by CAMA 2020 (arrangements and compromises under Sections 434-441; receivership under Sections 387-412; and winding up under Sections 564-636) and the Bankruptcy Act 1979 (Cap B2, LFN 2004) for individual insolvency. The absence of a modern restructuring mechanism comparable to business rescue, Chapter 11, or judicial recovery is widely recognized as a significant gap in Nigeria's commercial law framework. The Asset Management Corporation of Nigeria (AMCON) Act 2010 created a "bad bank" to absorb non-performing loans from the banking sector (approximately NGN 3.6 trillion in eligible bank assets), but this was a financial sector-specific intervention rather than a general insolvency mechanism.

CAMA 2020 Section 434-441 permits companies and their creditors to enter into schemes of arrangement or compromise approved by the Federal High Court, requiring a majority in number representing three-fourths in value of each class of creditors. However, this mechanism lacks the automatic stay, DIP financing framework, and cramdown provisions that characterize modern restructuring regimes.

## 라틴 아메리카

### 브라질

Brazil's insolvency regime, governed by Law 11,101 of February 9, 2005 (Lei de Recuperação Judicial, Extrajudicial e Falência), as substantially reformed by Law 14,112 of December 24, 2020, provides three proceedings:

- **Judicial Recovery (Recuperação Judicial)**: Available to debtors that have been in business for more than two years. The debtor files a petition with the judicial recovery court and has 60 days to present a recovery plan. An automatic stay of 180 days (the "stay period," prorrogable by the court under the 2020 reform) protects the debtor from enforcement actions. Creditors are organized into four classes: (1) labor claims up to 150 minimum wages per creditor; (2) secured claims up to the value of the collateral; (3) unsecured claims; and (4) SME creditors (added by the 2020 reform). The plan requires approval by majority vote in each class (majority in number and more than half in value, except for class 1 which requires simple majority). Article 58 permits court cram-down if no creditor class voted against the plan and at least one-third in each dissenting class approved.
- **Extrajudicial Recovery (Recuperação Extrajudicial)**: A pre-negotiated plan agreed with creditors representing more than 3/5 of total claims in each class, submitted to the court for homologation. The 2020 reform allowed the court to impose the extrajudicial plan on dissenting creditors (cram-down), previously unavailable.
- **Bankruptcy/Liquidation (Falência)**: Court-ordered liquidation with priority distribution: labor claims (up to 150 minimum wages per creditor), secured claims, tax claims, general privilege claims, and unsecured claims.

The 2020 reform (Law 14,112) introduced several critical changes:

- **DIP Financing**: Article 69-A through 69-F created a framework for debtor-in-possession financing with super-priority over all pre-petition claims (except labor and workplace accident claims).
- **Cross-Border Insolvency**: Articles 167-A through 167-X adopted provisions based on the UNCITRAL Model Law on Cross-Border Insolvency (though not a full adoption of the Model Law).
- **Producer Insolvency**: Special provisions for agricultural producers (Articles 70-A through 70-E), addressing the significant role of agribusiness in the Brazilian economy.
- **Successor Liability**: Clarified that the acquirer of assets in judicial recovery proceedings is not liable for the debtor's obligations (Article 60, paragraph), removing a major obstacle to asset sales in Brazilian restructurings.

### 주요 판례

- **Oi S.A. (2016-2022)**: Brazil's largest judicial recovery with BRL 65 billion in debt, involving the fourth-largest telecommunications company. The proceeding lasted over five years, with the recovery plan approved in December 2017 and substantially amended in 2022, converting debt into equity and attracting new investment. The Oi case exposed deficiencies in Brazil's cross-border insolvency framework, as the company had concurrent proceedings in the Netherlands (where its holding company Oi Brasil Holdings was incorporated) and disputes over recognition of foreign proceedings.
- **Odebrecht (2019-2023)**: The construction and engineering conglomerate, also embroiled in the Lava Jato corruption scandal, filed judicial recovery with approximately BRL 98 billion in debt. The recovery plan involved asset sales across multiple jurisdictions and debt restructuring.

### 멕시코

Mexico's Commercial Insolvency Law (Ley de Concursos Mercantiles, 2000, as amended) establishes a two-phase proceeding: conciliation (conciliación, up to 365 days) followed by bankruptcy (quiebra) if conciliation fails. The Federal Institute of Commercial Insolvency Specialists (IFECOM) maintains a registry of insolvency specialists. Mexico has adopted the UNCITRAL Model Law on Cross-Border Insolvency. However, Mexico's insolvency system is underutilized, with fewer than 100 commercial insolvency proceedings filed annually, as most restructurings occur informally or through the banking system's loan workout mechanisms.

## 동남아시아

### 인도네시아

Indonesia's Bankruptcy Law (Undang-Undang Kepailitan dan Penundaan Kewajiban Pembayaran Utang, Law 37/2004) provides two proceedings:

- **Bankruptcy (Kepailitan)**: A debtor with two or more creditors who has failed to pay at least one matured debt may be declared bankrupt by the Commercial Court. The standard is notably debtor-unfriendly: the court must declare bankruptcy if the debtor has two or more creditors and at least one debt is due and payable. There is no requirement of balance-sheet or cash-flow insolvency tests. The curator (kurator) is appointed by the court to administer the estate.
- **Suspension of Debt Payment Obligations (PKPU, Penundaan Kewajiban Pembayaran Utang)**: The debtor-friendly alternative allows a debtor that cannot or anticipates being unable to pay its debts to apply for a temporary suspension (45 days, automatically granted) followed by a permanent suspension (up to 270 days total) during which a composition plan may be negotiated. The plan requires approval by more than half the number of creditors representing two-thirds of the total admitted claims.

The Commercial Court, established in 1998 during the Asian Financial Crisis at the requirement of the IMF structural adjustment program, sits in five locations (Jakarta, Surabaya, Semarang, Medan, and Makassar) with appeals to the Supreme Court (Mahkamah Agung) bypassing the High Court.

Significant enforcement challenges include:

- **PT Merpati Nusantara Airlines (2014)**: The state-owned airline's PKPU proceedings exposed the challenge of applying bankruptcy law to SOEs, where political considerations influence creditor willingness to file and court willingness to grant bankruptcy.
- **International Arbitration and Bankruptcy**: The Supreme Court's decision in PT Telekomunikasi Indonesia (Persero) Tbk v. PT Aria West International (2009) established that foreign arbitral awards can be used as the basis for a bankruptcy petition, though enforcement remains inconsistent.

### 필리핀

The Financial Rehabilitation and Insolvency Act of 2010 (FRIA, RA 10142), effective July 18, 2010, replaced the prior insolvency law (Act No. 1956 of 1909, the Insolvency Law dating from the American colonial period) and the SEC's jurisdiction over corporate rehabilitation (under Presidential Decree No. 902-A):

- **Court-Supervised Rehabilitation (Title II)**: An insolvent debtor or creditors holding at least 25% of the debtor's total liabilities may file a petition for rehabilitation. The court issues a Commencement Order within five working days, appointing a rehabilitation receiver and imposing a Stay or Suspension Order effective for the duration of the proceedings. The rehabilitation plan must be approved by creditors holding at least two-thirds of the total liabilities and by a majority of each class.
- **Pre-Negotiated Rehabilitation (Title III)**: A debtor that has obtained the written approval of creditors representing at least two-thirds of total liabilities (including secured creditors holding more than 50% of secured claims) may file an approved rehabilitation plan directly for court confirmation.
- **Out-of-Court or Informal Restructuring Agreements (Title IV)**: Requires approval of creditors representing at least 67% of secured obligations and at least 75% of unsecured obligations.
- **Liquidation (Title V)**: Court-ordered or voluntary liquidation with priority distribution following statutory order: administrative costs, taxes, labor claims, secured claims, and unsecured claims.

The Supreme Court issued the Financial Rehabilitation Rules of Procedure (A.M. No. 12-12-11-SC, effective August 31, 2013) to govern rehabilitation proceedings in designated Special Commercial Courts.

## 비교 분석

### 채권자-채무자 균형

The Global South insolvency regimes exhibit different orientations on the creditor-debtor spectrum. Indonesia's bankruptcy standard (mere existence of two creditors and one unpaid matured debt) is among the most creditor-friendly in the world, potentially allowing bankruptcy petitions based on minor trade debts. Brazil's judicial recovery, by contrast, is debtor-friendly, with the debtor retaining management control (debtor-in-possession) and significant latitude in proposing recovery plans. India's IBC created a creditor-in-control model through the CoC, reflecting a deliberate policy response to decades of debtor-friendly regimes that allowed promoters to maintain control while creditors suffered losses. China's regime is formally neutral but practically subject to significant government influence, particularly in SOE restructurings where local government interests in employment preservation and social stability often override creditor interests.

### 제도적 역량

The effectiveness of insolvency systems depends critically on institutional capacity. India's creation of a specialized insolvency profession (approximately 4,200 registered insolvency professionals as of 2025), dedicated tribunals (NCLT), and a regulatory body (IBBI) represents the most comprehensive institutional investment. China's bankruptcy courts are building capacity but face case backlogs and limited specialization outside major cities. South Africa's business rescue practitioner profession (approximately 600 licensed practitioners) faces quality concerns. Brazil's bankruptcy judges in major commercial centers (São Paulo, Rio de Janeiro) have developed considerable expertise, but smaller jurisdictions lack specialization. Nigeria's absence of a dedicated insolvency tribunal or practitioner framework is a critical gap.

### 국경 간 차원

Cross-border insolvency remains a significant challenge across the Global South. South Africa is the only jurisdiction in this comparison to have adopted the UNCITRAL Model Law on Cross-Border Insolvency (Cross-Border Insolvency Act 42 of 2000), though its application has been limited. Mexico adopted the Model Law. Brazil's 2020 reform introduced Model Law-inspired provisions but not a complete adoption. China, India, Indonesia, and the Philippines have not adopted the Model Law, creating coordination challenges for multinational insolvencies. The Oi S.A. case (Brazil/Netherlands), HNA Group case (China/multiple jurisdictions), and Jet Airways case (India/Netherlands) illustrate the practical difficulties.
