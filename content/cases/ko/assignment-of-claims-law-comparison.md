## 요약

Assignment of claims (also called cession or transfer of receivables) is the legal mechanism by which a creditor transfers to a third party the right to receive performance from a debtor, enabling the monetization, financing, and trading of obligations without the debtor's consent in most legal systems. The law of assignment is of fundamental importance to receivables financing, factoring, securitization, and the broader functioning of credit markets. South Korea regulates the assignment of claims through the Korean Civil Code (민법) Articles 449-459 (채권양도, assignment of claims), which establish the general framework for assignability, anti-assignment restrictions, the requirement of notification to the debtor (통지) or debtor's consent (승낙) for effectiveness against third parties, and the debtor's defenses; the Supreme Court of Korea has developed extensive jurisprudence on bulk assignment, future receivables, and the interaction between assignment and the Act on Security over Movable Property, Claims, etc. (2010). The United States governs assignment of contract rights primarily through UCC Article 9 (Secured Transactions) for assignments that serve a financing function, supplemented by common law principles for non-financing assignments, the Restatement (Second) of Contracts §§ 317-341 for general assignment rules, and federal regulations for specific receivable types; the American system's distinctive feature is the application of Article 9's filing and priority rules to outright sales of accounts and chattel paper, not merely security assignments. Germany codifies the assignment of claims in the Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB) §§ 398-413 (Abtretung), providing a comprehensive framework under which assignment is effective by mere agreement between assignor and assignee (§ 398), without any requirement of notification to or consent of the debtor, and without registration; the German system recognizes the assignability of future claims, bulk assignment (Globalzession), and the validity of anti-assignment clauses (§ 399), while the BGH has developed extensive case law on the conflict between Globalzession and extended retention of title (verlängerter Eigentumsvorbehalt). The EU/ECHR framework addresses assignment of claims primarily through Rome I Regulation Article 14 (applicable law for voluntary assignment), the UNCITRAL Convention on the Assignment of Receivables in International Trade (2001), the EU's proposed Regulation on the third-party effects of assignments of claims, Directive 2002/47/EC on financial collateral arrangements, and the DCFR Book III Chapter 5 on assignment of rights.

## 한국

### 법적 체계

South Korea's assignment of claims regime is codified in the Korean Civil Code (민법), Chapter III, Section 1 on Assignment of Claims (채권양도, Articles 449-459). The framework balances the assignor's right to dispose of claims with the debtor's interest in knowing to whom performance is owed and third parties' interest in transparency.

**Assignability (Article 449(1)):** Claims are generally assignable (양도성). The assignor may transfer a claim to a third party (the assignee) by agreement between the assignor and assignee. No consent or participation of the debtor is required for the assignment to be effective between the parties.

**Anti-Assignment Clauses (Article 449(2)):** The parties to the original obligation may agree to restrict or prohibit assignment. Such an anti-assignment clause (양도금지특약) is effective and renders any assignment in violation of it void, unless the assignee did not know and could not have known of the restriction (good faith without negligence). The Korean Supreme Court has interpreted this provision strictly:

- **Supreme Court Decision 99Da67752 (2000):** Held that an anti-assignment clause is effective against a knowing assignee, rendering the assignment void. The burden of proving the assignee's knowledge is on the debtor.
- **Supreme Court Decision 2009Da47685 (2010):** Clarified that where an anti-assignment clause exists, the debtor may assert the clause against the assignee unless the assignee proves good faith and absence of negligence.

### Notification and Third-Party Effectiveness

**Article 450 (Notification and Consent):** The assignment is not effective against the debtor or third parties unless the assignor notifies the debtor (통지) or the debtor consents (승낙). This notification/consent requirement serves as the Korean substitute for a registration system:

**Certified Date Requirement (Article 450(2)):** To be effective against third parties other than the debtor, the notification or consent must bear a certified date (확정일자, hwakjeong ilcha). In practice, this is typically achieved through:

| Method | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| Content-certified mail (내용증명우편) | A notification sent via the Korean postal service with certified content and date |
| Notarized document | A notification or consent authenticated by a notary with a certified date |
| Court service | Service of a notification through court process |

**Priority Among Multiple Assignees (Article 450(2)):** Where the same claim is assigned to multiple assignees, priority is determined by the order of certified-date notifications reaching the debtor. The Supreme Court has addressed complex priority disputes:

- **Supreme Court Decision 94Da44521 (1996):** Held that where two notifications with certified dates are delivered to the debtor simultaneously, neither assignee has priority, and the debtor may discharge the obligation by paying either assignee.
- **Supreme Court Decision 2005Da41443 (2007):** Clarified that the priority-determining event is the time of receipt of the certified-date notification by the debtor, not the date of the notification itself.

### Future Claims and Bulk Assignment

The Korean Supreme Court has recognized the assignability of future claims, subject to sufficient identifiability:

- **Supreme Court Decision 97Da3828 (1997):** Held that future claims are assignable if the claim is sufficiently identifiable by reference to the debtor, the legal basis of the claim, and the approximate amount or range of amounts.
- **Supreme Court Decision 2010Da85162 (2012):** Upheld the validity of a bulk assignment of present and future receivables arising from specified contracts, where the receivables were identifiable by reference to the underlying contractual relationships.

### Interaction with the 2010 Act

The Act on Security over Movable Property, Claims, etc. (2010) introduced a registration-based security interest in claims (채권담보권). This creates a dual system:

- **Outright Assignment:** Governed by Civil Code Articles 449-459; effectiveness against third parties requires certified-date notification.
- **Security Assignment:** May be registered under the 2010 Act; registration provides priority over subsequent assignees and security takers.

The coexistence of these two regimes creates priority conflicts that the Supreme Court continues to address.

### Debtor's Position

**Defenses (Article 451):** The debtor may assert against the assignee all defenses (항변) that the debtor had against the assignor at the time of receiving notification of the assignment or giving consent. This includes set-off rights, counterclaims, and defenses arising from the underlying contract.

**Payment to Assignor (Article 452):** The debtor who has not received notification of assignment and does not know of it may validly discharge the obligation by paying the assignor. After notification, the debtor must pay the assignee.

## 미국

### 법적 체계

The United States applies a bifurcated regime: UCC Article 9 governs assignments that serve a financing function (including outright sales of accounts, chattel paper, payment intangibles, and promissory notes), while common law and the Restatement (Second) of Contracts govern non-financing assignments.

**UCC Article 9 Scope (§ 9-109(a)(3)):** Article 9 applies to sales of accounts, chattel paper, payment intangibles, and promissory notes, in addition to security interests in these assets. This means that an outright sale of accounts receivable (as in factoring) is subject to the same filing, priority, and enforcement rules as a loan secured by accounts receivable.

**Rationale:** The UCC's inclusion of outright sales within Article 9 reflects the practical difficulty of distinguishing between a sale of receivables and a loan secured by receivables, and the policy of providing a single priority framework for all interests in receivables.

### Creation and Perfection

**Attachment (§ 9-203):** An assignment (whether security assignment or sale) is effective between the parties when: value has been given, the assignor has rights in the receivable, and the assignor has authenticated a security agreement or the assignee has taken possession or control.

**Perfection by Filing (§ 9-310):** An assignment is perfected against third parties by the filing of a financing statement (UCC-1) with the designated state filing office. Filing is the primary perfection method for accounts and payment intangibles.

**Automatic Perfection (§ 9-309):** Certain assignments are automatically perfected without filing:
- An assignment of accounts or payment intangibles that does not by itself or in conjunction with other assignments transfer a significant part of the assignor's outstanding accounts or payment intangibles (§ 9-309(2)).
- A sale of a payment intangible (§ 9-309(3)).
- A sale of a promissory note (§ 9-309(4)).

### 양도금지 조항

**§ 9-406(d) (Override of Anti-Assignment Clauses):** A term in an agreement between an account debtor and an assignor that prohibits, restricts, or requires the consent of the account debtor to the assignment of an account or chattel paper is ineffective. This is one of the most significant features of American assignment law, reflecting the policy of promoting receivables financing by ensuring that contractual restrictions cannot impede the assignment of payment obligations.

**§ 9-408 (Override for Other General Intangibles):** Similarly, anti-assignment clauses are rendered ineffective for security interests in promissory notes, health-care-insurance receivables, and general intangibles (including contract rights), though the override for general intangibles has limitations designed to protect the obligor's legitimate interests.

### Priority Rules

**General Priority (§ 9-322(a)):** Among competing interests in the same receivable, priority is determined by the first-to-file-or-perfect rule. The first assignee to file a financing statement or otherwise perfect has priority, regardless of the order of the assignments.

**Future Advance Priority (§ 9-323):** A secured party with a perfected interest has priority over a subsequent assignee for future advances made after the subsequent assignment, provided the future advance is made without knowledge of the subsequent assignment or pursuant to a commitment entered into without knowledge.

### Account Debtor's Position

**§ 9-404 (Defenses Against Assignee):** The account debtor may assert against the assignee all defenses that the account debtor could assert against the assignor, as well as any claim in recoupment arising from the transaction that gave rise to the account. However, the account debtor may not assert claims of other persons against the assignee.

**§ 9-406(a) (Notification and Payment):** After notification of the assignment, the account debtor must pay the assignee. Before notification, the account debtor may discharge the obligation by paying the assignor.

### Federal Regulation

Federal law restricts assignment of certain claims:

| Type | Restriction |
|------|------------|
| Government contracts | Anti-Assignment Act (41 U.S.C. § 6305); Assignment of Claims Act (31 U.S.C. § 3727) limits assignment of claims against the government |
| Wages | State wage assignment statutes limit the assignment of future wages |
| Personal injury claims | Most states prohibit assignment of personal injury tort claims |
| Social Security benefits | 42 U.S.C. § 407 prohibits assignment of Social Security benefits |

## 독일

### 법적 체계

Germany codifies the assignment of claims (Abtretung) in BGB §§ 398-413, providing a framework that is notably simple in its formal requirements but complex in its practical application, particularly in the context of financing transactions.

**BGB § 398 (Abtretung):** A claim (Forderung) may be transferred by the creditor (Zedent, assignor) to a third party (Zessionar, assignee) by agreement between the assignor and assignee. Upon agreement, the assignee steps into the position of the former creditor. The assignment takes effect immediately upon agreement; no delivery, registration, notification, or other formality is required.

**BGB § 399 (Non-Assignable Claims):** A claim may not be assigned if performance to anyone other than the original creditor would change its content (e.g., claims for personal services), or if the assignment is excluded by agreement between the creditor and debtor (vertragliches Abtretungsverbot, anti-assignment clause). Anti-assignment clauses are generally effective in German law, creating a significant restriction on receivables financing.

**HGB § 354a (Exception for Commercial Claims):** To mitigate the effect of anti-assignment clauses on commercial financing, HGB § 354a provides that an anti-assignment clause in a contract between merchants (Kaufleute) does not prevent the effective assignment of a monetary claim (Geldforderung). However, the debtor may still discharge the obligation by paying the original creditor (assignor). This compromise preserves the debtor's interest in payment simplicity while allowing the assignment to be effective for financing purposes.

### Future Claims and Globalzession

**Vorausabtretung (Advance Assignment):** German law recognizes the assignment of future claims (künftige Forderungen) provided the claim is sufficiently identifiable at the time of the assignment agreement. The assignment becomes effective automatically upon the future claim's coming into existence:

- **BGH, Urteil vom 27.05.1953 -- IV ZR 214/52:** Recognized the validity of the advance assignment of future claims, holding that the assignment agreement may precede the existence of the claim.

**Globalzession (Bulk/Global Assignment):** The Globalzession is the assignment of all present and future claims arising from the assignor's business to a financing bank. This is a standard financing technique in German banking practice. The Globalzession creates significant priority conflicts:

**Conflict with Extended Retention of Title (Verlängerter Eigentumsvorbehalt):** The verlängerter Eigentumsvorbehalt involves the advance assignment of resale proceeds to the retention of title seller. When the buyer has both a Globalzession with its bank and extended retention of title obligations to its suppliers, the same receivable is subject to competing advance assignments:

- **BGH, Urteil vom 09.11.1978 -- VII ZR 54/77:** The BGH developed the principle that a Globalzession that includes receivables arising under extended retention of title is, to that extent, void due to Sittenwidrigkeit (immorality, § 138 BGB), because it deprives the retention of title supplier of the substitute security (Surrogat) that the extended retention provides.
- **BGH, Urteil vom 08.12.1998 -- IX ZR 302/97:** Modified the earlier approach, holding that a Globalzession is only partially void to the extent that it conflicts with specific extended retention of title claims, not void in its entirety.
- **BGH, Urteil vom 19.09.2006 -- IX ZR 249/04:** Further refined the rules, establishing that the conflict is resolved on a claim-by-claim basis: for each receivable, the party whose advance assignment was first in time has priority, unless the Globalzession explicitly excludes claims arising under extended retention of title.

### Debtor's Position

**BGB § 404 (Defenses):** The debtor (Schuldner) may assert against the assignee all defenses that existed against the assignor at the time of assignment. This includes set-off rights (Aufrechnungsrecht), defenses from the underlying contract (Einwendungen), and rights of retention (Zurückbehaltungsrecht).

**BGB § 406 (Set-Off After Assignment):** The debtor may set off against the assignee a claim that the debtor had against the assignor before the debtor learned of the assignment, provided the set-off claim was due before the debtor learned of the assignment or became due no later than the assigned claim.

**BGB § 407 (Payment to Assignor):** The debtor who makes payment to the assignor or performs another legal act in relation to the assignor after the assignment is protected if the debtor did not know of the assignment at the time of performance. This provision makes notification practically important even though it is not legally required for the assignment's effectiveness.

**BGB § 409 (Notification Effect):** If the assignor notifies the debtor of the assignment, the assignor cannot assert against the debtor that the assignment did not take place or is invalid. The notification creates an estoppel (Rechtsscheinwirkung) in favor of the debtor.

### Assignment in Insolvency

In the assignor's insolvency, an assignment completed before insolvency is effective against the insolvency estate. However, the insolvency administrator may challenge assignments made within the suspect period under the Insolvenzordnung (InsO) avoidance provisions:

- **InsO § 130 (Congruent Coverage):** An assignment made within three months before the insolvency petition, at a time when the assignor was unable to pay and the assignee knew of the inability, may be avoided.
- **InsO § 131 (Incongruent Coverage):** An assignment made within one month before the petition that provides the assignee with a security or satisfaction not owed in that manner or at that time is avoidable.

## ECHR & EU

### Rome I Regulation (Regulation (EC) No. 593/2008), Article 14

Article 14 of the Rome I Regulation governs the applicable law for voluntary assignments of claims:

**Article 14(1):** The relationship between assignor and assignee in a voluntary assignment of a claim against another person (the debtor) is governed by the law that applies to the contract between the assignor and assignee. This covers the mutual obligations of the assignor and assignee, the conditions for the assignment's effectiveness between them, and whether the assignment is a sale or security assignment.

**Article 14(2):** The law governing the assigned claim determines its assignability, the relationship between the assignee and the debtor, the conditions under which the assignment can be invoked against the debtor, and whether the debtor's obligations have been discharged.

**Article 14 Gap (Third-Party Effects):** Critically, Article 14 does not specify which law governs the effectiveness of the assignment against third parties (such as competing assignees, the assignor's insolvency administrator, or other creditors). This gap has been recognized as a major deficiency in EU private international law, leading to the EU's proposed Regulation on third-party effects.

### EU Proposed Regulation on Third-Party Effects of Assignments of Claims

The European Commission proposed a Regulation in 2018 (COM(2018) 96 final) to fill the Article 14 gap by specifying the law applicable to the third-party effects of assignments of claims:

**Proposed Rule:** The third-party effects of an assignment of claims would generally be governed by the law of the assignor's habitual residence. This rule is designed to provide certainty and predictability, as a single law (that of the assignor) would govern the priority of competing interests in the same receivable.

**Exceptions:** Special rules would apply for assignments of claims arising from securitization (law governing the assigned claim), assignments of cash credited to an account (law of the account-holding institution), and assignments governed by the Financial Collateral Directive.

**Status:** The proposed Regulation has been subject to prolonged legislative debate. As of 2026, it remains under consideration by the Council and Parliament, with ongoing disagreements on the appropriate connecting factor.

### UNCITRAL Convention on the Assignment of Receivables in International Trade (2001)

The UNCITRAL Convention, adopted in 2001, provides a comprehensive framework for international receivables financing. Key features:

**Scope (Article 1):** Applies to assignments of international receivables and international assignments of receivables.

**Anti-Assignment Override (Article 9):** An assignment of a receivable is effective notwithstanding any agreement between the assignor and the debtor limiting the assignor's right to assign. This override is similar to UCC § 9-406(d) and represents a significant departure from the German and Korean rules that generally give effect to anti-assignment clauses.

**Future Receivables (Article 8):** An assignment of future receivables is effective at the time of the original assignment agreement, without a new act of transfer being required when the receivable comes into existence.

**Priority (Article 22):** The Convention defers to the law of the assignor's location (place of business) for the priority of competing interests in the same receivable. An optional Annex provides registration-based priority rules.

**Status:** The Convention has not yet entered into force, lacking sufficient ratifications (only Liberia has ratified as of 2026). However, its provisions have influenced national law reforms and the EU's proposed Regulation.

### Directive 2002/47/EC on Financial Collateral Arrangements

The Financial Collateral Directive simplifies the creation and enforcement of security interests in financial assets, including through assignment:

- **Title Transfer Financial Collateral Arrangement (Article 6):** Member states must recognize the full transfer of title to financial collateral (including claims) as a valid security mechanism, notwithstanding recharacterization laws that might otherwise treat the transfer as a pledge.
- **Formalities (Article 3):** The creation, validity, perfection, enforceability, or admissibility in evidence of a financial collateral arrangement may not be made dependent on any formal act (such as registration, filing, or notification).

### DCFR Book III, Chapter 5: Assignment of Rights

The DCFR provides a modernized framework for the assignment of rights that draws on comparative analysis of EU member state laws:

- **III.-5:104 (Assignability):** All rights to performance are assignable unless the assignment would alter the obligation to the debtor's detriment, the right is for performance of a personal nature, or the assignment is prohibited by law.
- **III.-5:108 (Anti-Assignment Clauses):** A contractual prohibition on assignment does not affect the validity of the assignment. The assignor who assigns in breach of such a clause is liable to the debtor for breach of contract, but the assignment is effective. This represents a significant departure from the German § 399 BGB approach.
- **III.-5:113 (Priority of Competing Assignments):** Priority among competing assignees is determined by the order of notification to the debtor, a rule similar to the South Korean approach.

## 비교 분석

### 형식적 요건

The jurisdictions exhibit striking differences in the formal requirements for an effective assignment. Germany requires only the agreement of assignor and assignee -- no notification, registration, or other formality (§ 398 BGB). South Korea requires the agreement plus certified-date notification to the debtor for third-party effectiveness (Art. 450). The United States requires the agreement plus filing of a financing statement for Article 9 assignments. The contrast between the German "secret" assignment (effective without any publicity) and the American filing requirement (providing public notice through the UCC filing system) reflects fundamentally different policy choices regarding transparency versus simplicity.

### 양도금지 조항

The treatment of anti-assignment clauses represents a major policy divergence. Germany generally enforces anti-assignment clauses (§ 399 BGB), with the commercial exception of HGB § 354a. South Korea similarly enforces them unless the assignee is in good faith (Art. 449(2)). The United States renders anti-assignment clauses ineffective for accounts and payment intangibles (§ 9-406(d)). The UNCITRAL Convention and DCFR override anti-assignment clauses entirely. The trend in international harmonization is clearly toward overriding anti-assignment clauses to facilitate receivables financing, but Germany and South Korea have resisted full adoption of this approach.

### 우선순위 메커니즘

The priority mechanisms differ fundamentally. The United States uses a filing-based system (first-to-file-or-perfect). South Korea uses a notification-based system (first certified-date notification to reach the debtor). Germany has no general priority mechanism, relying on the first-in-time principle for competing assignments, which is difficult to prove and creates practical uncertainty. The EU's proposed Regulation would adopt the assignor's habitual residence law as the governing law for priority, without mandating a specific priority rule. The UNCITRAL Convention's optional Annex proposes a registration-based system.

### 장래 채권 및 포괄양도

All four systems permit the assignment of future receivables and bulk assignment, but with different conditions and effects. American law (UCC § 9-204) provides the most permissive framework, with the assignment attaching automatically as the debtor acquires rights in the collateral. German law (Vorausabtretung) recognizes advance assignment but creates complex priority conflicts with competing advance assignments (particularly the Globalzession vs. verlängerter Eigentumsvorbehalt conflict). South Korean law requires sufficient identifiability of future claims. The international instruments universally endorse the assignability of future receivables.

### 채무자 보호

All systems protect the debtor against prejudice from the assignment. The debtor retains pre-existing defenses in all jurisdictions. The key difference is the notification requirement: Germany does not require notification but protects the debtor who pays the assignor in ignorance of the assignment (§ 407 BGB). South Korea requires notification for effectiveness against the debtor. The United States does not require notification for perfection but protects the debtor who pays the assignor before receiving notification (§ 9-406(a)). The practical convergence is greater than the doctrinal divergence: in all systems, the debtor may safely pay the original creditor until notified of the assignment.
