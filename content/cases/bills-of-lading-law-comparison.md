## Summary

Bills of lading are foundational documents in international trade, serving the triple function of receipt for goods shipped, evidence of the contract of carriage, and document of title enabling transfer of constructive possession of goods in transit. The legal regime governing bills of lading varies significantly across jurisdictions, though international conventions have achieved substantial harmonization of carrier liability rules. South Korea regulates bills of lading through the Korean Commercial Code (상법) Articles 852-862 (선하증권, bills of lading) and Articles 787-851 (해상운송, carriage of goods by sea), incorporating the Hague-Visby Rules framework into domestic law through the 2007 Commercial Code amendments; the Korean Supreme Court has developed extensive case law on the negotiability of bills of lading, carrier liability limitations, and the relationship between charterparty terms and bill of lading terms. The United States governs bills of lading through the Carriage of Goods by Sea Act (COGSA) of 1936 (46 U.S.C. § 30701 note), which adopts the Hague Rules (1924) rather than the Hague-Visby amendments, supplemented by the Pomerene Bills of Lading Act (49 U.S.C. §§ 80101-80116) for document of title functions, and the Federal Bills of Lading Act; American courts have developed the doctrine of "clause paramount" and extensive jurisprudence on the package limitation, deviation, and fair opportunity requirements. Germany applies the Hamburg commercial shipping provisions of the Handelsgesetzbuch (HGB) Sections 513-525 (Konnossement) as reformed by the Seehandelsrechtsreform of 2013, which modernized German maritime law and brought it closer to the Rotterdam Rules framework, while also being bound by the Hague-Visby Rules as ratified; the German approach distinguishes between the Konnossement (bill of lading) as a Traditionspapier (document of title conveying constructive possession) and as evidence of the contract of carriage. The EU/ECHR framework addresses bills of lading primarily through the Rotterdam Rules (United Nations Convention on Contracts for the International Carriage of Goods Wholly or Partly by Sea, 2008), which the EU has encouraged member states to ratify, and through the UNCITRAL Model Law on Electronic Transferable Records (MLETR, 2017) for electronic bills of lading; additionally, the EU Regulation 1215/2012 (Brussels I bis) provides jurisdictional rules for maritime disputes, and several EU member states have adopted or are considering adopting the Rotterdam Rules.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's bills of lading regime is codified in the Korean Commercial Code (상법), Part V on Maritime Commerce (해상), as substantially amended in 2007 to modernize Korean maritime law and align it with the Hague-Visby Rules. Korea ratified the Hague-Visby Rules (1968 Protocol), making the Convention directly applicable alongside domestic provisions.

**Carriage of Goods by Sea (Articles 787-851):** The Commercial Code imposes on the carrier a duty of seaworthiness (Article 794, 감항능력주의의무) requiring the carrier to exercise due diligence before and at the beginning of the voyage to make the ship seaworthy, properly man, equip, and supply the ship, and make the holds and other parts of the ship fit and safe for reception, carriage, and preservation of goods. This obligation mirrors Article III(1) of the Hague-Visby Rules.

**Carrier Liability (Article 795):** The carrier is liable for loss of or damage to goods occurring between the time of receipt and the time of delivery, unless the carrier proves that the carrier, the carrier's servants, and agents exercised due diligence to avoid the loss or damage. The 2007 amendments removed the nautical fault defense (previously available under Article 795(2)), aligning Korean law with the Hamburg Rules approach rather than maintaining the Hague-Visby nautical fault exception.

### Bills of Lading Provisions

**Issuance (Article 852):** The carrier must, on demand of the shipper after receiving the goods, issue a bill of lading. The bill of lading must contain: a general description of the goods, leading marks, number of packages or pieces, weight or quantity, apparent order and condition, name of the shipper, name of the carrier, name of the consignee or indication that the bill is to order, port of loading and discharge, freight, place and date of issuance, and number of originals.

**Negotiability and Document of Title (Article 853):** The bill of lading is a document of title (유가증권). A negotiable bill of lading made out "to order" (지시식) is transferable by endorsement and delivery. The transfer of the bill of lading has the same effect as the delivery of the goods with respect to the acquisition of rights over the goods (Article 861). This provision establishes the bill of lading as a Wertpapier equivalent, enabling goods in transit to be traded by transfer of the document.

**Evidentiary Effect (Article 855):** The bill of lading constitutes prima facie evidence of the receipt by the carrier of the goods as described therein. As against a third party acting in good faith (선의의 제3자), the bill of lading is conclusive evidence (Article 855(2)), preventing the carrier from denying the accuracy of the statements in the bill against a bona fide holder.

### Package Limitation and Liability Cap

Under Articles 797-798, the carrier's liability is limited to 666.67 SDR per package or shipping unit, or 2 SDR per kilogram of gross weight of the goods lost or damaged, whichever is higher. This follows the Hague-Visby limitation formula. The limitation is broken if the loss resulted from an act or omission of the carrier done with intent to cause damage or recklessly and with knowledge that damage would probably result (Article 797(2)).

### Key Korean Case Law

The Korean Supreme Court has addressed bills of lading in numerous decisions:

- **Supreme Court Decision 2001Da75318 (2003):** Held that the identity of the carrier must be determined by reference to the bill of lading; where the bill of lading identifies a party as carrier, that party bears carrier liability regardless of charterparty arrangements.
- **Supreme Court Decision 2014Da229152 (2016):** Addressed the conclusive evidence rule, holding that a carrier cannot challenge the description of goods in a bill of lading against a consignee bank that advanced funds in reliance on the bill.
- **Supreme Court Decision 2009Da93383 (2012):** Clarified that the one-year time bar applies from the date of actual delivery or, where goods are not delivered, from the date when they should have been delivered.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States applies a layered regulatory framework to bills of lading, with the Carriage of Goods by Sea Act (COGSA) governing carrier liability for international ocean shipments, the Pomerene Bills of Lading Act governing the document of title function, and the Harter Act (46 U.S.C. §§ 30701-30707) applying to domestic coastwise and inland water carriage.

**COGSA (46 U.S.C. § 30701 note):** Enacted in 1936, COGSA incorporates the Hague Rules (International Convention for the Unification of Certain Rules of Law Relating to Bills of Lading, 1924). Notably, the United States has not ratified the 1968 Visby Protocol or the 1979 SDR Protocol, meaning that the original Hague Rules per-package limitation of $500 applies rather than the Hague-Visby SDR-based limitation.

**Scope of Application (COGSA § 1):** COGSA applies mandatorily to contracts for carriage of goods by sea between U.S. ports and ports of foreign countries, covering the "tackle to tackle" period from loading to discharge. Parties may contractually extend COGSA's application to periods before loading and after discharge through a "clause paramount."

### Carrier Liability under COGSA

**Obligations (COGSA § 3):** The carrier must exercise due diligence before and at the beginning of the voyage to make the ship seaworthy, properly load, handle, stow, carry, keep, care for, and discharge the goods carried.

**Defenses (COGSA § 4(2)):** The carrier is not liable for loss or damage arising from 17 enumerated causes, including:

| Defense | COGSA § 4(2) Provision |
|---------|----------------------|
| Nautical fault | Act, neglect, or default of master, mariner, pilot in navigation or management of ship |
| Fire | Unless caused by actual fault or privity of carrier |
| Perils of the sea | Dangers and accidents of the sea |
| Act of God | Including natural disasters |
| Act of war | War, civil unrest |
| Act of public enemies | Piracy |
| Quarantine restrictions | Government-imposed restrictions |
| Shipper fault | Act or omission of shipper or owner of goods |
| Strikes | Labor disputes |
| Saving life or property | Deviation to save life or property |

**Package Limitation (COGSA § 4(5)):** The carrier's liability is limited to $500 per package or customary freight unit, unless the shipper declares a higher value and inserts it in the bill of lading. The Supreme Court in Kirby v. Norfolk Southern Railway Co., 543 U.S. 14 (2004), held that COGSA limitations may extend inland through intermodal bills of lading where the bill of lading contains a Himalaya clause.

### Fair Opportunity Doctrine

American courts have developed the "fair opportunity" doctrine, requiring that the shipper be given a fair opportunity to declare a higher value for the goods before the $500 limitation applies. In Nemeth v. General Steamship Corp., 694 F.2d 609 (9th Cir. 1982), the court held that the carrier bears the burden of proving that the shipper was given a fair opportunity to declare excess value.

### Pomerene Bills of Lading Act

The Pomerene Act (49 U.S.C. §§ 80101-80116) governs the document of title aspects of bills of lading. A negotiable bill of lading is one that states that goods are consigned to the order of a named person (§ 80103). A negotiable bill may be negotiated by endorsement and delivery, transferring to the holder all rights of the transferor (§ 80104). A carrier delivering goods without surrender of an outstanding negotiable bill is liable to any person to whom the bill has been duly negotiated (§ 80110).

### Electronic Bills of Lading in the United States

The United States has not enacted specific federal legislation on electronic bills of lading. However, the Uniform Electronic Transactions Act (UETA), adopted by 49 states, and the federal E-SIGN Act (15 U.S.C. §§ 7001-7031) provide general frameworks for electronic records and signatures. Industry platforms such as BOLERO, essDOCS, and TradeLens (discontinued 2022) have facilitated electronic bill of lading usage through contractual rulebooks and registry systems.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany fundamentally reformed its maritime commercial law through the Seehandelsrechtsreform (Maritime Commercial Law Reform Act) of 2013, replacing the antiquated Fourth Book of the HGB with modernized provisions in HGB Sections 476-619. The reform drew on the Rotterdam Rules framework, even though Germany has not yet ratified the Rotterdam Rules, creating a domestic regime more modern than the Hague-Visby Rules that Germany has ratified.

**Carrier Liability (HGB § 498):** The carrier is liable for loss of or damage to goods occurring during the carrier's period of responsibility (from receipt to delivery), as well as for delay in delivery. The carrier is exonerated if the loss, damage, or delay was caused by circumstances that could not be avoided by the exercise of due diligence and whose consequences could not be prevented (§ 498(2)). The reform eliminated the nautical fault defense that had previously been available under German law.

**Period of Responsibility (HGB § 498(1)):** The carrier's period of responsibility covers the entire time from receipt of goods to delivery, encompassing the port-to-port period. This is broader than the tackle-to-tackle period under the Hague-Visby Rules.

### Konnossement (Bills of Lading, HGB §§ 513-525)

**Issuance (HGB § 513):** The carrier must, upon request of the shipper, issue a bill of lading (Konnossement) after receiving the goods. The bill of lading must contain: a description of the goods suitable for identification, leading marks, number of packages, weight, apparent order and condition, name and address of the carrier, name of the ship, name of the shipper, the consignee (if named by the shipper), port of loading and discharge, freight terms, place and date of issuance, number of originals, and applicable general terms.

**Traditionspapier Function (HGB § 519):** The Konnossement is a Traditionspapier (document of title conferring constructive possession). The transfer of the Konnossement to a person entitled to receive the goods under the document has, for the acquisition of rights over the goods, the same effect as the delivery of the goods themselves (§ 519(2)). This sachenrechtliche Wirkung (proprietary effect) is a distinctive feature of German law, rooting the document of title function in property law rather than mere contractual rights.

**Types of Konnossement:**

| Type | Description | HGB Provision |
|------|-------------|---------------|
| Orderkonnossement | Order bill, negotiable by endorsement | § 513(1) Nr. 5; default form |
| Namenskonnossement | Straight (named consignee) bill | § 516 |
| Inhaberkonnossement | Bearer bill | Rare in practice |

**Evidentiary Effect (HGB § 517):** The Konnossement establishes a presumption (Vermutung) that the carrier received the goods as described. As against a third-party holder acting in good faith (gutgläubiger Dritter), the carrier is estopped from proving that the goods were not as described (§ 517(2)).

### Liability Limitations

Under HGB § 504, the carrier's liability is limited to 666.67 SDR per package or shipping unit, or 2 SDR per kilogram of gross weight, whichever is higher, consistent with the Hague-Visby formula. The limitation is broken in cases of intentional misconduct (Vorsatz) or reckless conduct with knowledge that damage would probably result (§ 507).

### German Case Law

- **BGH, Urteil vom 18.12.2008 -- I ZR 195/05:** The Bundesgerichtshof held that a carrier who issues a clean bill of lading despite visible damage to the goods is estopped from relying on the bill's statements against a bona fide endorsee.
- **BGH, Urteil vom 15.11.2001 -- I ZR 284/99:** Addressed the Traditionspapier function, confirming that transfer of the bill of lading transfers constructive possession and that the holder has a right to delivery that is independent of the underlying sales contract.
- **OLG Hamburg, Urteil vom 07.04.2016 -- 6 U 138/14:** Applied the 2013 reformed HGB provisions to a container shipping dispute, confirming the elimination of the nautical fault defense.

## ECHR & EU

### Rotterdam Rules (2008)

The United Nations Convention on Contracts for the International Carriage of Goods Wholly or Partly by Sea (Rotterdam Rules), adopted in 2008, represents the most ambitious attempt to modernize and unify the international law of carriage of goods by sea. The EU has encouraged member states to sign and ratify the Convention, and several EU member states (including the Netherlands, Spain, Denmark, and France) have signed, though ratification remains limited.

**Scope (Articles 1-7):** The Rotterdam Rules apply to contracts of carriage from one state to another where the port of loading or port of discharge is in a contracting state. Uniquely, the Convention covers door-to-door carriage, not merely port-to-port, addressing the needs of modern containerized multimodal transport.

**Carrier Liability (Article 17):** The carrier is liable for loss, damage, or delay if the claimant proves that the loss occurred during the carrier's period of responsibility. The carrier is exonerated if it proves the cause of the loss was not attributable to its fault or the fault of persons for whom it is responsible. Article 17(3) lists specific excepted perils (fire, act of God, war, etc.) that shift the burden of proof back to the claimant.

**Limitation of Liability (Article 59):** The carrier's liability is limited to 875 SDR per package or shipping unit, or 3 SDR per kilogram of gross weight, whichever is higher. These limits are significantly higher than the Hague-Visby amounts.

**Time Bar (Article 62):** The Rotterdam Rules extend the time bar from one year (Hague-Visby) to two years from the date of delivery or the date the goods should have been delivered.

### Electronic Transport Records

**Chapter 8 (Articles 35-40):** The Rotterdam Rules contain provisions on the use and effect of electronic transport records. Article 8(a) permits the issuance of a negotiable electronic transport record with the consent of the carrier and shipper, having the same legal effect as a negotiable transport document. The transfer of the electronic record effects a transfer of rights equivalent to endorsement and delivery of a paper bill.

### UNCITRAL Model Law on Electronic Transferable Records (MLETR, 2017)

The MLETR provides a technology-neutral legal framework enabling the use of electronic transferable records, including electronic bills of lading, that are functionally equivalent to paper-based transferable documents. Key principles include:

- **Functional Equivalence (Article 7):** An electronic transferable record satisfies the requirements of a transferable document if a reliable method is used to identify the record as the electronic transferable record, render it capable of being subject to control, and retain the integrity of the record.
- **Control (Article 11):** The requirement of possession of a transferable document is met with respect to an electronic transferable record if a reliable method is used to establish exclusive control of the record and to identify the person in control.
- **Singularity (Article 10):** An electronic transferable record must be singular -- only one person may exercise control at any given time.

The EU has actively promoted the adoption of MLETR. France adopted MLETR-based legislation in 2024. Germany and the Netherlands have ongoing legislative initiatives to incorporate MLETR principles.

### EU Jurisdictional Framework

Regulation (EU) No. 1215/2012 (Brussels I bis) provides jurisdictional rules applicable to maritime disputes involving bills of lading within the EU. Under Article 7(1)(b), matters relating to a contract for the provision of services (including carriage) may be brought in the courts of the place of performance of the obligation in question. The CJEU in Coreck Maritime GmbH v. Handelsveem BV (Case C-387/98, 2000) held that a jurisdiction clause in a bill of lading is enforceable against a third-party holder of the bill only if the holder succeeded to the shipper's rights and obligations under the applicable national law.

## Comparative Analysis

### Convention Regime Fragmentation

The international law of bills of lading suffers from significant fragmentation. Three major conventions coexist: the Hague Rules (1924) as applied by the United States, the Hague-Visby Rules (1968) as applied by South Korea, Germany, and most EU member states, and the Hamburg Rules (1978) adopted by a number of developing states. The Rotterdam Rules (2008) were intended to replace all three prior conventions but have not yet achieved sufficient ratifications to enter into force. This fragmentation creates uncertainty in international trade, as the applicable liability regime depends on the port of loading, port of discharge, and the contracting state status of the relevant countries.

### Package Limitation Disparities

The per-package limitation reveals substantial differences: the United States applies the lowest ceiling ($500 per package under COGSA, approximately 370 SDR), South Korea and Germany apply the Hague-Visby standard (666.67 SDR per package or 2 SDR per kg), and the Rotterdam Rules propose the highest limits (875 SDR per package or 3 SDR per kg). These disparities incentivize forum shopping and affect cargo insurance calculations.

### Nautical Fault Defense

A major divergence concerns the nautical fault defense, which exempts the carrier from liability for negligence of the master and crew in the navigation or management of the ship. The United States retains this defense under COGSA § 4(2)(a). South Korea eliminated it in the 2007 amendments. Germany eliminated it in the 2013 reform. The Rotterdam Rules do not include a nautical fault defense. The trend across all studied jurisdictions is clearly toward abolition.

### Document of Title Function

All four systems recognize the bill of lading as a document of title, but the theoretical foundation differs. Germany treats the Konnossement as a Traditionspapier with sachenrechtliche Wirkung (proprietary effect under property law). South Korea similarly treats the bill as a 유가증권 (negotiable instrument). American law treats the negotiable bill under the Pomerene Act as creating a right to delivery that can be transferred by negotiation, grounded in commercial law rather than property law theory. The Rotterdam Rules adopt a functional approach, specifying the effects of transfer without prescribing the theoretical basis.

### Electronic Transition

The transition from paper to electronic bills of lading remains at different stages. The Rotterdam Rules and MLETR provide the most comprehensive frameworks, but adoption is limited. South Korea and the United States lack specific electronic bill of lading legislation, relying on general e-commerce frameworks and contractual platforms. Germany's 2013 reform did not address electronic bills. The EU's promotion of MLETR and individual member state initiatives (France's 2024 legislation) represent the most active regulatory developments.
