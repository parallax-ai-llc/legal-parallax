## Summary

Prostitution -- the exchange of sexual services for money or other consideration -- occupies one of the most contested intersections of criminal law, human rights, public health, and moral philosophy. National legal frameworks span a wide spectrum: full criminalization of all parties (South Korea), predominantly state-level criminalization with limited exceptions (United States), comprehensive legalization with regulatory oversight (Germany), and a patchwork of divergent approaches across the Council of Europe and European Union member states, with the European Court of Human Rights granting states a wide margin of appreciation. South Korea criminalizes both the buying and selling of sex under the Act on the Punishment of Intermediating in the Sex Trade (성매매알선 등 행위의 처벌에 관한 법률, 2004), with penalties of up to three years imprisonment. The United States treats prostitution primarily as a state-level offense, illegal in 49 states (with limited licensed brothel exceptions in certain Nevada counties), while federal law targets interstate and online facilitation through the Mann Act (18 U.S.C. Chapter 117) and FOSTA-SESTA (Pub. L. 115-164, 2018). Germany legalized prostitution through the Prostitution Act (Prostitutionsgesetz, ProstG) of 2002 and subsequently enacted the Prostitutes Protection Act (Prostituiertenschutzgesetz, ProstSchG) of 2017, establishing a registration and licensing regime. At the European level, the ECHR's landmark 2024 judgment in M.A. and Others v. France (Application Nos. 63664/19 et al.) upheld France's Nordic-model criminalization of sex purchase as compatible with Article 8 of the Convention, while EU Directive 2011/36/EU (as amended by Directive (EU) 2024/1712) addresses trafficking for sexual exploitation without mandating a uniform approach to consensual prostitution.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea operates under a full criminalization model, prohibiting the buying, selling, and facilitation of prostitution. The legal foundation rests on two interconnected statutes enacted simultaneously on 22 March 2004 and effective from 23 September 2004:

1. **Act on the Punishment of Intermediating in the Sex Trade and Related Acts** (성매매알선 등 행위의 처벌에 관한 법률, commonly the "Sex Trade Punishment Act"): Establishes criminal penalties for all parties involved in prostitution transactions.
2. **Act on the Prevention of the Sex Trade and Protection of Victims** (성매매방지 및 피해자보호 등에 관한 법률, the "Sex Trade Prevention Act"): Provides a protective framework for victims of coerced or trafficked prostitution, including shelter, counseling, and legal aid.

**Penalties for Selling Sex:** Under Article 21(1) of the Sex Trade Punishment Act, a person who engages in selling sex faces imprisonment for up to one year or a fine of up to 3 million KRW (approximately USD 2,300).

**Penalties for Buying Sex:** Under the same article, a person who purchases sexual services faces identical penalties -- imprisonment for up to one year or a fine of up to 3 million KRW.

**Penalties for Intermediaries and Operators:** Persons who facilitate, arrange, or profit from prostitution face significantly harsher penalties. Operating a brothel or arranging prostitution may result in imprisonment of up to three years or fines of up to 30 million KRW (approximately USD 23,000). Where force, fraud, or coercion is involved, penalties increase to up to ten years imprisonment.

| Offense | Statute | Maximum Penalty |
|---------|---------|-----------------|
| Selling sex | Art. 21(1) Sex Trade Punishment Act | 1 year imprisonment / 3 million KRW fine |
| Buying sex | Art. 21(1) Sex Trade Punishment Act | 1 year imprisonment / 3 million KRW fine |
| Arranging / facilitating | Art. 18 Sex Trade Punishment Act | 3 years imprisonment / 30 million KRW fine |
| Coerced prostitution facilitation | Art. 18(2) Sex Trade Punishment Act | 10 years imprisonment |
| Advertising prostitution | Art. 20 Sex Trade Punishment Act | 3 years imprisonment / 30 million KRW fine |

### Victim Protection

The 2004 reforms introduced a critical distinction between voluntary participants and victims of coerced prostitution. Under the Prevention Act, persons who were forced, deceived, or coerced into selling sex are classified as victims and are exempt from criminal punishment. The government established a network of shelters (성매매피해자지원시설) and counseling centers, and victims are entitled to legal representation, medical assistance, and vocational training.

### Constitutional Court Ruling (2016)

The constitutionality of South Korea's prostitution ban was directly challenged in the landmark case **2012Hun-Ba196**, decided on 31 March 2016. The case originated when a woman surnamed Kim, indicted for prostitution in December 2012, petitioned the Seoul Northern District Court to refer the question of the law's constitutionality to the Constitutional Court. Kim argued that consensual prostitution between adults was an exercise of sexual self-determination protected under the Constitution.

The Constitutional Court upheld the ban in a **6-to-3 decision**, ruling that the prohibition on voluntary prostitution serves the legitimate purpose of maintaining "sound sexual ethics" and that the public interest in protecting social morality outweighs the individual's right to sexual self-determination. The three dissenting justices argued that punishing consensual adult prostitution constituted an excessive restriction on bodily autonomy and the right to pursue happiness.

### Historical Context

South Korea's relationship with prostitution is deeply entangled with the presence of U.S. military bases. During the 1960s-1980s, government-sanctioned "camp towns" (기지촌) operated near military installations, and women were subjected to mandatory health examinations. In January 2017, a Seoul court ruled that the government's forced detention and compulsory treatment of sex workers in the 1960s and 1970s was illegal, ordering compensation for surviving victims. The 2004 legislation represented a decisive break from this history of state tolerance and complicity.

### Enforcement Challenges

Despite strict legal prohibitions, prostitution remains widespread in South Korea. Research estimates the industry generates between 1-4% of GDP. Red-light districts, while diminished after the 2004 crackdown, have been partially displaced to online platforms, room salons (룸살롱), and massage establishments (안마시술소). The 2004 police crackdown on major red-light districts demonstrated measurable crime displacement effects, with prostitution migrating to less visible venues rather than being eliminated.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States addresses prostitution through a layered system of state criminal codes and federal statutes. Prostitution is not a federal crime per se; rather, it is regulated primarily at the state level, with federal law targeting interstate activity, trafficking, and online facilitation.

**State-Level Criminalization:** Prostitution is illegal in 49 states and the District of Columbia. In the vast majority of jurisdictions, both selling and buying sex constitute misdemeanor offenses, typically classified as public-order crimes. However, the trend toward asymmetric enforcement has accelerated:

- **Texas** enacted HB 1540 (effective September 2021), making the purchase of sex a state jail felony punishable by up to two years imprisonment for a first offense -- the first state to classify buying sex as a felony.
- **Maine** decriminalized the selling of sex in 2023 while maintaining criminal penalties for buyers, adopting a partial Nordic-model approach.
- **California** imposes three, four, or six years in prison for pimping or pandering under Penal Code Sections 266h-266i.
- **New York** promoted prostitution in the first degree is a Class D felony carrying up to seven years imprisonment under Penal Law Section 230.32.

**Nevada Exception:** Nevada is the sole state permitting legalized prostitution, but only under stringent conditions codified in NRS 201.354 and NRS 244.345:
- Prostitution is only legal in **licensed brothels** in counties with a population of fewer than 700,000.
- Prostitution remains illegal in Clark County (Las Vegas) and Washoe County (Reno).
- Currently, **10 of Nevada's 16 counties** permit licensed brothels, though only 6 have actively operating establishments.
- NRS 201.380 prohibits brothels within 400 yards of schools, churches, or principal business streets.
- Workers must undergo regular health examinations and condom use is mandatory.

### Federal Statutes

**The Mann Act (White-Slave Traffic Act, 1910):** Codified at 18 U.S.C. Sections 2421-2424, the Mann Act prohibits knowingly transporting any individual in interstate or foreign commerce with the intent that the person engage in prostitution or any sexual activity constituting a criminal offense. Violations carry penalties of up to 10 years imprisonment. The act's historical significance extends beyond prostitution -- it was notoriously used to target interracial relationships and political dissidents, including the prosecution of boxing champion Jack Johnson in 1913.

**FOSTA-SESTA (2018):** The Allow States and Victims to Fight Online Sex Trafficking Act (FOSTA) and Stop Enabling Sex Traffickers Act (SESTA), signed into law on 11 April 2018 as Pub. L. 115-164, amended Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act to create exceptions for prostitution-related content. Key provisions include:

- 18 U.S.C. Section 2421A: Anyone who owns, manages, or operates an interactive computer service with intent to promote or facilitate prostitution faces up to **10 years imprisonment**.
- Aggravated violations involving five or more persons: up to **25 years imprisonment**.
- Conduct contributing to sex trafficking in reckless disregard: up to **25 years imprisonment** and fines.

FOSTA-SESTA's impact on Section 230 immunity has been controversial. Critics argue the law pushed sex workers into more dangerous street-based work by shuttering online advertising platforms like Backpage.com (seized by federal authorities in April 2018). The constitutional challenge in **Woodhull Freedom Foundation v. United States** (D.C. Circuit, 2020) upheld the statute against First Amendment challenges, though concerns about chilling effects on legitimate speech persist.

### Landmark Cases

- **United States v. Bitty**, 208 U.S. 393 (1908): The Supreme Court upheld federal authority to exclude non-citizens engaged in prostitution, establishing the principle that immigration law may be used as a tool against prostitution.
- **Cleveland v. United States**, 329 U.S. 14 (1946): The Supreme Court applied the Mann Act to polygamous relationships, expanding its scope beyond commercial sex.
- **Woodhull Freedom Foundation v. United States**, No. 18-5298 (D.C. Cir. 2020): Upheld FOSTA-SESTA against First Amendment facial challenge.

### Enforcement Trends

Modern enforcement in the United States has increasingly shifted toward demand-side operations ("john stings") and online investigation, reflecting a policy emphasis on deterring buyers. Simultaneously, numerous jurisdictions have established Human Trafficking Intervention Courts and diversion programs that treat arrested sex workers as potential trafficking victims rather than criminals, reflecting the broader trend toward recognizing the intersection between prostitution and trafficking.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany operates one of the world's most comprehensive legalization regimes for prostitution, implemented through two successive legislative frameworks:

**Prostitution Act (Prostitutionsgesetz, ProstG) of 2002:** Enacted on 20 December 2001 and effective from 1 January 2002, this one-page law -- sponsored by the Green Party within the governing Social Democrat-Green coalition -- fundamentally transformed the legal status of prostitution in Germany. The ProstG:

- Removed the classification of prostitution as "immoral" (sittenwidrig), which had historically prevented sex workers from enforcing contracts or accessing social benefits.
- Established prostitution as a legitimate occupation, enabling sex workers to enter into enforceable employment contracts.
- Granted sex workers access to health insurance, pension contributions, and unemployment benefits under the social security system.
- Removed the general prohibition on "furthering" (Fordern) prostitution, which had previously criminalized most forms of organized sex work.

**Prostitutes Protection Act (Prostituiertenschutzgesetz, ProstSchG) of 2017:** Enacted on 21 October 2016 and effective from 1 July 2017, the ProstSchG was introduced after evaluations of the 2002 law revealed that few sex workers had accessed social benefits or formal employment contracts, and that the deregulated environment may have facilitated trafficking and exploitation. The ProstSchG introduced comprehensive regulatory requirements:

| Requirement | Detail | Statutory Basis |
|-------------|--------|-----------------|
| **Registration** | All sex workers must register with competent local authorities and receive a registration certificate (Anmeldebescheinigung) | Section 3 ProstSchG |
| **Health consultation** | Mandatory health counseling session before registration and annually thereafter (every 6 months for those under 21) | Section 10 ProstSchG |
| **Business permits** | Prostitution businesses require a permit (Erlaubnis); operators must demonstrate personal reliability | Section 12 ProstSchG |
| **Operator reliability** | Persons convicted of specified offenses (sexual offenses, assault, trafficking, fraud, money laundering) within the past 5 years are barred from operating | Section 14 ProstSchG |
| **Condom mandate** | Mandatory condom use; advertising sexual intercourse without a condom is prohibited | Section 32(3) ProstSchG |
| **Safety standards** | Operators must ensure health and safety protections for sex workers, including emergency call systems and adequate sanitary facilities | Sections 18-24 ProstSchG |

### Criminal Provisions

While prostitution itself is legal, German criminal law continues to prohibit exploitative conduct:

- **Human trafficking for sexual exploitation** (Section 232 StGB): Imprisonment of 6 months to 10 years.
- **Forced prostitution** (Section 232a StGB): Imprisonment of 1 to 10 years.
- **Exploitation of prostitutes** (Section 180a StGB): Imprisonment of up to 3 years for exploiting a personal or economic dependency.
- **Pimping** (Section 181a StGB): Imprisonment of up to 5 years for commercially exploiting a prostitute or exercising supervision to restrict personal or economic freedom.
- **Prostitution involving minors** (Section 182 StGB): Strictly prohibited; engaging in sexual acts with a person under 18 in exchange for payment is a criminal offense.

### Implementation Challenges

The implementation of the ProstSchG has been uneven. Several federal states -- including Baden-Wurttemberg, Berlin, Hamburg, Rhineland-Palatinate, and Saxony -- were unable to establish the necessary administrative infrastructure by the July 2017 effective date. A constitutional complaint was filed against the ProstSchG in June 2017 by sex worker advocacy groups who argued that mandatory registration requirements violated privacy rights and exposed sex workers to stigma and potential data breaches.

By government estimates, there are approximately 400,000 sex workers in Germany, though only a fraction have registered under the ProstSchG. Critics from both sides of the debate point to this gap: abolitionists argue legalization has expanded the market and attracted trafficking, while sex worker advocates contend that registration requirements deter compliance and drive workers underground.

### Historical Context

Before 2002, German law treated prostitution as inherently immoral (sittenwidrig) under Section 138 of the Civil Code (BGB), rendering sex work contracts unenforceable and excluding sex workers from social benefits. The reform was part of a broader Social Democrat-Green agenda of social liberalization. Germany's approach has been influential internationally, serving as the primary reference model for legalization advocates, while simultaneously being criticized by Nordic-model proponents who point to Germany's estimated 3,000-plus brothels and the scale of its sex industry as evidence that legalization normalizes exploitation.

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR does not mandate a specific regulatory model for prostitution. Instead, the Court has addressed prostitution through the lenses of Article 4 (prohibition of slavery and forced labour), Article 8 (right to respect for private life), and the margin of appreciation doctrine.

#### M.A. and Others v. France (2024)

The most significant ECHR pronouncement on prostitution regulation is **M.A. and Others v. France** (Application Nos. 63664/19 et al.), decided on 25 July 2024. The case challenged France's Law No. 2016-444 of 13 April 2016, which criminalized the purchase of sexual services (punishable by a fine of EUR 1,500, rising to EUR 3,750 for repeat offenses) while decriminalizing the selling of sex -- the so-called "Nordic model" or "end demand" approach.

**The Applicants:** 261 individuals of various nationalities, both male and female, who engaged in sex work in France. They argued that criminalization of their clients under the 2016 law violated their rights under Articles 2 (right to life), 3 (prohibition of inhuman treatment), and 8 (right to respect for private life) of the Convention, contending that the law pushed them into more dangerous working conditions.

**The Court's Analysis:** The Grand Chamber examined the complaint exclusively under Article 8. Key holdings include:

- **Lack of consensus:** The Court observed that there is no European consensus on how to regulate prostitution, with member states adopting widely divergent approaches ranging from full criminalization to legalization (Paragraphs 149-153).
- **Wide margin of appreciation:** Given the absence of consensus and the "very sensitive moral and ethical questions" raised by prostitution, the Court granted France a wide margin of appreciation in determining its legislative approach (Paragraph 153).
- **Proportionality:** The Court found that France struck a fair balance between the competing interests -- the protection of human dignity and public order on one hand, and the applicants' right to private life on the other.
- **Unanimous decision:** The Court unanimously found no violation of Article 8.

The ruling was met with sharp criticism from human rights organizations. Amnesty International stated it "fails to recognise that criminalising sex work increases discrimination and jeopardises the safety of sex workers." The International Planned Parenthood Federation called it a "missed opportunity" to guarantee rights of sex workers.

#### Rantsev v. Cyprus and Russia (2010)

**Rantsev v. Cyprus and Russia** (Application No. 25965/04), decided on 7 January 2010, is the foundational ECHR case on human trafficking and forced prostitution. The case concerned the death of Oxana Rantseva, a 20-year-old Russian woman who was trafficked to Cyprus under an "artiste" visa scheme and subjected to sexual exploitation in a cabaret in Limassol.

**Key Holdings:**
- The Court unanimously ruled that **human trafficking falls within the scope of Article 4** (prohibition of slavery, servitude, and forced labour), even though the article does not explicitly mention trafficking.
- **Cyprus** violated Article 4 by failing to maintain an adequate legal and administrative framework to combat trafficking -- specifically, the "artiste" visa regime failed to provide practical and effective protection against trafficking and exploitation.
- **Russia** violated Article 4 by failing to effectively investigate the trafficking of Ms. Rantseva from Russian territory.
- The Court established that states have **positive obligations** under Article 4 to: (1) adopt criminal law provisions to penalize trafficking; (2) take operational measures to protect identified or potential victims; and (3) conduct effective investigations into trafficking situations.

#### S.M. v. Croatia (2020)

In **S.M. v. Croatia** (Application No. 60561/14), the Grand Chamber reinforced the classification of forced prostitution under Article 4, holding that Croatia had an obligation to investigate allegations of forced prostitution as a potential form of trafficking, even where the complainant had not been transported across borders.

### EU Legislative Framework

The European Union does not impose a uniform prostitution model on member states. However, several EU instruments address related matters:

**Directive 2011/36/EU on Preventing and Combating Trafficking in Human Beings:** Adopted on 5 April 2011, this directive establishes minimum standards for the definition of trafficking offenses, penalties, and victim protections across all EU member states. Key provisions include:

- **Definition:** Trafficking is defined as the recruitment, transportation, transfer, harboring, or receipt of persons through coercion, fraud, or abuse of power for the purpose of exploitation, including "the exploitation of the prostitution of others or other forms of sexual exploitation" (Article 2).
- **Penalties:** Member states must ensure maximum penalties of at least 5 years imprisonment for basic trafficking offenses, and at least 10 years for aggravated offenses (Article 4).
- **Irrelevance of consent:** The consent of a victim is irrelevant where any of the specified means has been used (Article 2(4)).
- **Non-prosecution of victims:** Member states must ensure that competent authorities are entitled not to prosecute or impose penalties on trafficking victims for criminal activities they were compelled to commit (Article 8).

**Directive (EU) 2024/1712 (Amending Directive):** This 2024 amendment expanded the scope of Directive 2011/36/EU to:
- Include additional forms of exploitation within its ambit.
- Require EU member states to ensure that persons who knowingly use services provided by victims of trafficking face sanctions.
- Strengthen tools for investigation, prosecution, and victim support.

**European Parliament Resolution of 26 February 2014:** The European Parliament adopted a non-binding resolution recommending that member states consider the Nordic model (criminalizing the buying, not the selling, of sex) as the most effective approach to combating trafficking and protecting human dignity. The resolution passed 343 to 139 with 105 abstentions, reflecting deep divisions among member states.

### Divergent National Approaches within the EU/CoE

| Model | Countries | Key Features |
|-------|-----------|--------------|
| **Full criminalization** | Romania, Croatia, Lithuania | Both buying and selling prohibited |
| **Nordic model (demand criminalization)** | Sweden (1999), Norway (2009), Iceland (2009), France (2016), Ireland (2017), Israel (2020) | Buying criminalized; selling decriminalized |
| **Legalization / regulation** | Germany, Netherlands, Austria, Switzerland, Greece | Prostitution legal with varying regulatory frameworks |
| **Decriminalization** | Belgium (2022), New Zealand (2003 -- non-EU reference) | Full decriminalization of consensual adult sex work |
| **Abolitionist (de facto tolerance)** | Italy, Spain, Portugal, United Kingdom | Prostitution itself not criminal; surrounding activities (soliciting, brothel-keeping) criminalized |

Belgium's 2022 decriminalization law made it the first European country to fully decriminalize sex work, granting sex workers labor rights, social protections, and access to business registration.

## Comparative Analysis

### Regulatory Models and Their Rationales

The four jurisdictions examined represent three fundamentally different philosophical approaches to prostitution:

**1. Criminalization (South Korea, most U.S. states):** Rooted in the view that prostitution is inherently harmful and morally objectionable, this approach seeks to deter all participation through criminal sanctions. South Korea's Constitutional Court explicitly grounded the ban in "sound sexual ethics" (건전한 성풍속). The practical challenge is enforcement: despite decades of criminalization, prostitution persists in both jurisdictions, raising questions about whether prohibition drives the industry underground, increasing risks for participants.

**2. Legalization with regulation (Germany):** Based on the harm-reduction principle that if prostitution cannot be eliminated, it should be regulated to minimize exploitation and health risks. Germany's two-stage legislative approach -- first legalizing (2002), then regulating (2017) -- reflects the difficulty of implementing effective oversight. The gap between the estimated 400,000 sex workers and the much smaller number who have registered under the ProstSchG illustrates the challenge of achieving compliance within a legalization framework.

**3. Margin of appreciation (ECHR/EU):** The ECHR's approach effectively recognizes that no single model commands consensus, allowing states to choose their own path within the bounds of Convention rights. The M.A. v. France ruling confirmed that both the Nordic model and other approaches can coexist within the Convention system, provided they pursue legitimate aims proportionately.

### Effectiveness and Outcomes

No jurisdiction has produced conclusive evidence that its model is definitively superior in reducing trafficking, protecting sex workers, or achieving its stated policy goals:

- **South Korea:** The 2004 crackdown reduced visible prostitution in red-light districts but research documented significant crime displacement to online platforms and less visible establishments.
- **United States:** The patchwork of state approaches creates inconsistency. Nevada's regulated brothels report low rates of violence and STIs, but the scale is tiny. FOSTA-SESTA has been criticized for increasing dangers to sex workers without demonstrably reducing trafficking.
- **Germany:** Legalization has not eliminated trafficking or exploitation. Critics note that Germany's status as a major destination country for trafficking persisted after 2002. However, registered sex workers gain access to healthcare, labor protections, and legal recourse unavailable under criminalization.
- **ECHR/EU:** The lack of harmonization means that regulatory arbitrage occurs across borders, with sex workers and traffickers migrating toward jurisdictions with more permissive or less enforced regimes.

### Human Rights Tensions

Every model involves trade-offs between competing rights:
- **Bodily autonomy and sexual self-determination** (favoring decriminalization or legalization) versus **human dignity and protection from exploitation** (favoring criminalization or the Nordic model).
- **Privacy rights** of sex workers (challenged by Germany's registration requirements) versus **public health and safety** interests (served by mandatory health consultations and condom mandates).
- **Freedom of expression** (implicated by FOSTA-SESTA's impact on internet platforms) versus **prevention of trafficking** (the law's stated purpose).

The ECHR's wide margin of appreciation in M.A. v. France effectively acknowledges that these tensions cannot be resolved through a single universal framework, deferring to democratic processes within individual states.
