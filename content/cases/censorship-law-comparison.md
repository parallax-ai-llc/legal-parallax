## Summary

Censorship -- the suppression, prohibition, or restriction of expression by governmental or institutional authority -- occupies a central position in constitutional law as the most direct form of interference with freedom of speech and the press, with all democratic legal systems prohibiting prior censorship in principle while differing significantly on the permissible scope of post-publication restrictions, content regulation of broadcast and digital media, and the classification of materials for age-appropriate access. South Korea constitutionally prohibits censorship under Article 21(2) of the Constitution (대한민국 헌법 제21조 제2항), which provides that "licensing or censorship of speech and the press, and licensing of assembly and association shall not be recognized," yet the Constitutional Court (헌법재판소) has navigated complex boundaries between impermissible censorship and permissible content regulation, notably distinguishing administrative prior review (검열) from post-publication restrictions in cases involving film classification, internet content regulation by the Korea Communications Standards Commission (방송통신심의위원회, KCSC), and the Korea Media Rating Board (영상물등급위원회); the National Security Act (국가보안법) continues to serve as a basis for restricting expression deemed sympathetic to North Korea. The United States maintains the strongest anti-censorship position globally through the First Amendment, with the Supreme Court having established a near-absolute prohibition on prior restraint from Near v. Minnesota (1931) through New York Times v. United States (1971), while recognizing narrow categories of regulable speech including obscenity, child pornography, and true threats; the tension between anti-censorship principles and content moderation by private platforms has become the defining issue of digital-era free expression law, addressed in Moody v. NetChoice (2024). Germany prohibits censorship under Article 5(1) of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz), which provides that "there shall be no censorship" (Eine Zensur findet nicht statt), while simultaneously maintaining content restrictions for the protection of youth (Jugendschutz) through the Youth Protection Act (Jugendschutzgesetz, JuSchG) and the Interstate Treaty on the Protection of Minors in the Media (Jugendmedienschutz-Staatsvertrag, JMStV), as well as restrictions on extremist content under Sections 86, 86a, and 130 of the Criminal Code (StGB); the NetzDG and its successor framework under the EU Digital Services Act impose content moderation obligations on social media platforms. The European framework addresses censorship through ECHR Article 10, which protects freedom of expression while permitting restrictions prescribed by law and necessary in a democratic society, with the European Court of Human Rights having developed extensive jurisprudence on prior restraint, media regulation, and internet censorship, establishing a presumption against prior restraint while recognizing that states may impose proportionate content restrictions.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Article 21(2) of the Constitution provides an absolute prohibition on censorship: "Licensing or censorship of speech and the press, and licensing of assembly and association shall not be recognized." The Constitutional Court has interpreted this provision to prohibit administrative prior review (행정검열) -- defined as a system in which the government reviews expression before its publication and determines whether it may be disseminated.

### Constitutional Court on Censorship

The Constitutional Court has developed a detailed doctrine distinguishing prohibited censorship from permissible regulation:

**Film Rating System (2001헌가27, 2001):** The Court upheld the mandatory film rating system operated by the Korea Media Rating Board (영상물등급위원회), holding that a classification system requiring age ratings does not constitute censorship so long as it does not prevent the film from being shown entirely but merely restricts the audience based on age appropriateness.

**Music Pre-Screening (93헌가13, 1996):** The Court struck down the requirement that music recordings receive prior review from a government-designated body before distribution, holding this constituted unconstitutional censorship.

**Internet Content Review (2012헌마652, 2019):** The Court examined the KCSC's authority to order the blocking or deletion of internet content. While not declaring the entire system unconstitutional, the Court emphasized that the KCSC's deliberation process must provide adequate procedural safeguards and cannot function as a prior restraint mechanism.

### Korea Communications Standards Commission (KCSC)

The KCSC (방송통신심의위원회) is the primary body responsible for internet content regulation. Under the Telecommunications Business Act and the Information and Communications Network Act, the KCSC may order internet service providers to block access to or delete content deemed:

- Illegal under Korean law (including National Security Act violations, obscenity, defamation)
- Harmful to youth
- Contrary to public morals

The KCSC has been criticized by international press freedom organizations for excessive content removal, particularly regarding political content critical of the government. The UN Special Rapporteur on Freedom of Opinion and Expression has recommended that South Korea reform the KCSC to ensure independence and limit discretionary blocking authority.

### National Security Act

The National Security Act (국가보안법) continues to authorize restrictions on expression deemed to praise, encourage, or propagandize for North Korea or anti-state organizations. While the Constitutional Court has upheld the Act's constitutionality, it has narrowed its application to expression posing an "active and aggressive" threat to national security.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The First Amendment's prohibition on laws "abridging the freedom of speech, or of the press" has been interpreted by the Supreme Court to create a near-absolute prohibition on prior restraint and content-based censorship.

### Prior Restraint Doctrine

**Near v. Minnesota, 283 U.S. 697 (1931):** Struck down a Minnesota statute allowing courts to enjoin publication of "malicious, scandalous, and defamatory" newspapers, establishing that prior restraint carries a "heavy presumption" of unconstitutionality.

**New York Times Co. v. United States, 403 U.S. 713 (1971):** The Pentagon Papers case rejected the government's attempt to enjoin publication of classified documents, reinforcing the near-absolute prohibition on prior restraint.

**Alexander v. United States, 509 U.S. 544 (1993):** Distinguished between prior restraints and subsequent punishments, holding that the forfeiture of an adult bookstore's inventory following an obscenity conviction was not a prior restraint.

### Content Moderation and Section 230

The most significant contemporary censorship debate in the United States concerns content moderation by private platforms:

**Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act (47 U.S.C. Section 230):** Provides that "no provider or user of an interactive computer service shall be treated as the publisher or speaker of any information provided by another information content provider," immunizing platforms from liability for third-party content and for good-faith content moderation decisions.

**Moody v. NetChoice, LLC (2024):** The Supreme Court addressed state laws (Florida and Texas) restricting social media platforms' ability to moderate content. The Court held that the First Amendment protects platforms' editorial discretion in curating and moderating content, while remanding for further analysis of the laws' application to different platform functions.

### Obscenity and Content Restrictions

The Supreme Court in Miller v. California, 413 U.S. 15 (1973), established the three-part test for obscenity: material is obscene if (1) the average person, applying contemporary community standards, would find that the work, taken as a whole, appeals to the prurient interest; (2) the work depicts or describes sexual conduct in a patently offensive way; and (3) the work, taken as a whole, lacks serious literary, artistic, political, or scientific value.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Article 5(1) of the Basic Law states: "Eine Zensur findet nicht statt" (There shall be no censorship). The Federal Constitutional Court has interpreted this prohibition as applying specifically to prior censorship (Vorzensur) -- the systematic review of expression by governmental authorities before publication. Post-publication restrictions, including criminal prosecution for prohibited content and administrative measures to protect minors, are not considered "censorship" under Article 5(1).

### Youth Protection (Jugendschutz)

Germany maintains one of the most comprehensive youth protection frameworks for media:

**Federal Review Board for Media Harmful to Minors (Bundeszentrale für Kinder- und Jugendmedienschutz, BzKJ):** Formerly the Bundesprüfstelle für jugendgefährdende Medien (BPjM), this body maintains an index (Liste) of media deemed harmful to young persons. Indexed media may not be advertised, displayed, or made accessible to minors. Indexing does not prohibit the media from being sold to adults.

**Voluntary Self-Regulation of the Film Industry (FSK):** The Freiwillige Selbstkontrolle der Filmwirtschaft classifies films into age categories (0, 6, 12, 16, 18). While nominally voluntary, FSK classification is effectively mandatory for theatrical release and broadcast.

**Interstate Treaty on the Protection of Minors in the Media (JMStV):** Governs content regulation in broadcasting and online media, establishing content categories and requiring providers to implement age verification systems for content that may be harmful to minors.

### Prohibited Content

German law prohibits specific categories of expression that would be protected in the United States:

| Provision | Content Prohibited | Penalty |
|-----------|-------------------|---------|
| Section 86 StGB | Distribution of propaganda of unconstitutional organizations | Up to 3 years imprisonment |
| Section 86a StGB | Display of symbols of unconstitutional organizations (swastikas, SS runes) | Up to 3 years imprisonment |
| Section 130 StGB | Volksverhetzung (incitement to hatred); Holocaust denial | 3 months to 5 years imprisonment |
| Section 131 StGB | Depictions of violence glorifying or trivializing cruel acts | Up to 1 year imprisonment |
| Section 184 StGB | Distribution of pornographic material to minors | Up to 1 year imprisonment |

### NetzDG and Digital Services Act

The Netzwerkdurchsetzungsgesetz (NetzDG, 2017) required social media platforms to remove "manifestly unlawful" content within 24 hours and other unlawful content within seven days. The NetzDG has been partially superseded by the EU Digital Services Act (DSA, Regulation 2022/2065), which establishes a comprehensive EU-wide framework for content moderation obligations, including transparency requirements, complaint mechanisms, and graduated obligations based on platform size.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Article 10 and Prior Restraint

The European Court of Human Rights has established a strong but not absolute presumption against prior restraint:

**Observer and Guardian v. United Kingdom (Application No. 13585/88, 1991):** The Court found that temporary injunctions preventing The Guardian and The Observer from publishing excerpts from the Spycatcher memoir did not violate Article 10 while the information remained confidential, but that continuation of the injunctions after the book was published worldwide did violate Article 10.

**Editions Plon v. France (Application No. 58148/00, 2004):** The Court found that the continued prohibition on distribution of a book about the medical history of President Mitterrand, after information was widely available, violated Article 10.

**Yildirim v. Turkey (Application No. 3111/10, 2012):** The Court found that Turkey's wholesale blocking of Google Sites, which made the applicant's academic website inaccessible, violated Article 10, establishing that blanket internet blocking measures are disproportionate.

**OOO Flavus and Others v. Russia (Application No. 12468/15, 2020):** The Court found that Russia's blocking of entire websites based on individual pages containing prohibited content violated Article 10, as the measure was disproportionate and affected lawful content.

### EU Digital Services Act (DSA)

The DSA (Regulation 2022/2065), fully applicable since February 2024, establishes a tiered system of content moderation obligations:

| Category | Obligations |
|----------|-------------|
| All intermediary services | Transparency reporting; single point of contact; legal representative in EU |
| Hosting services | Notice-and-action mechanism; statement of reasons for content removal |
| Online platforms | Complaint-and-redress mechanism; out-of-court dispute settlement; trusted flaggers |
| Very large online platforms (VLOPs) | Systemic risk assessment; crisis response mechanism; independent audit; recommender system transparency |

VLOPs (platforms with more than 45 million monthly active users in the EU) face the most stringent obligations, including mandatory risk assessments for systemic risks such as illegal content, negative effects on fundamental rights, and manipulation of public discourse.

### Audiovisual Media Services Directive (AVMSD)

Directive 2018/1808 (revised AVMSD) harmonizes broadcast and on-demand audiovisual media regulation across the EU, including content standards, advertising rules, and promotion of European works. The Directive extends to video-sharing platforms, requiring them to implement measures to protect minors from harmful content and to protect all users from incitement to violence or hatred.

## Comparative Analysis

### Prior Restraint vs. Post-Publication Restriction

All four jurisdictions prohibit prior censorship in principle but differ on the boundary between prior restraint and permissible regulation. The United States maintains the strictest prohibition, with prior restraint presumptively unconstitutional. Germany's censorship ban applies specifically to pre-publication review, permitting extensive post-publication regulation including indexing and criminal prosecution. South Korea's Constitutional Court has developed a nuanced doctrine distinguishing administrative prior review from content classification. The ECHR framework applies a proportionality analysis, permitting temporary prior restraints in exceptional circumstances.

### Content Regulation Approaches

The most significant divergence is between the U.S. categorical approach (very narrow categories of unprotected speech; no general hate speech prohibition) and the European content regulation model (comprehensive restrictions on hate speech, extremist content, and youth-harmful material). Germany's prohibitions on Nazi symbols and Holocaust denial exemplify restrictions that would be unconstitutional in the United States. South Korea's National Security Act restrictions on pro-North Korean expression add a unique geopolitical dimension.

### Digital Platform Regulation

The regulation of digital platforms represents the newest and most rapidly evolving area. The EU's Digital Services Act provides the most comprehensive framework, imposing graduated obligations on platforms. Germany's NetzDG pioneered platform content removal obligations. South Korea's KCSC exercises direct administrative authority over content. The United States, through Section 230, has taken the most permissive approach toward platform content moderation, though this is under increasing pressure from both sides of the political spectrum.

### Classification vs. Censorship

All four jurisdictions distinguish between content classification (age ratings, labeling) and censorship (prohibition). Germany's indexing system, South Korea's media rating boards, and the AVMSD's classification requirements are designed to restrict access by minors without prohibiting content entirely. The line between classification and censorship remains contested, particularly when classification decisions effectively prevent distribution.
