## Summary

Arson -- the deliberate setting of fire to property -- is treated as one of the most dangerous property crimes across all major legal systems, owing to its potential for mass casualties and widespread destruction. South Korea addresses arson through Articles 164-176 of its Criminal Act, imposing penalties up to life imprisonment for fires set in occupied buildings. Germany employs a highly structured five-tier system under Sections 306-306f of the Strafgesetzbuch, distinguishing simple arson from aggravated forms carrying penalties of up to life imprisonment when death results. The United States prosecutes arson at both federal and state levels, with 18 U.S.C. sections 81 and 844(i) governing federal jurisdiction and individual states maintaining their own degree-based classifications. The ECHR, while not criminalizing arson directly, imposes positive obligations on member states under Articles 2 and 8 of the Convention to protect life and property from fire-related risks and to investigate fire deaths effectively.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Arson is governed by Chapter XIII of the Korean Criminal Act ("Crimes of Arson and Fire Caused by Negligence"), encompassing Articles 164 through 176. The severity of punishment is primarily determined by the type and occupancy status of the property set ablaze.

| Article | Offence | Penalty |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Art. 164 | Setting fire to an inhabited building, train, ship, aircraft, or mine in which persons are present | Life imprisonment or not less than 3 years |
| Art. 165 | Setting fire to a public-use structure, train, ship, aircraft, or mine used for official business or the public interest | Life imprisonment or not less than 3 years |
| Art. 166(1) | Setting fire to a non-inhabited building or structure belonging to another | Not less than 1 year, not more than 10 years |
| Art. 166(2) | Setting fire to one's own non-inhabited building, creating public danger | Not more than 7 years or fine not exceeding 10 million won |
| Art. 167(1) | Setting fire to general property of another (not a building), creating public danger | Not less than 1 year, not more than 10 years |
| Art. 167(2) | Setting fire to one's own general property, creating public danger | Not more than 3 years or fine not exceeding 7 million won |
| Art. 170 | Fire caused by negligence | Not more than 1.5 years or fine not exceeding 7.5 million won |
| Art. 174 | Attempt to commit arson | Punishable |
| Art. 175 | Preparation to commit arson under Art. 164 or 165 | Not more than 5 years |

### Key Principles

**Spread of Fire Doctrine (Art. 168):** When a fire set to less-protected property (Arts. 166-167) spreads to an occupied building (Art. 164) or public structure (Art. 165), the offender is punished under the more severe provision. This "spreading fire" doctrine operates as a strict liability escalation, regardless of the offender's intent regarding the higher-category property.

**Public Danger Requirement:** For fires set to non-buildings (Art. 167) or to one's own property (Arts. 166(2), 167(2)), criminal liability attaches only if the fire creates a "public danger" (gonggong-ui wihom). Courts interpret this as a concrete danger to the life, body, or property of an unspecified or large number of persons.

**Negligent Arson:** Article 170 punishes fire caused by gross negligence -- an important provision in a country where dense urban housing and commercial districts increase the risk of fire spreading. Article 171 further provides that negligence causing a fire that results in death carries enhanced penalties.

### Landmark Cases

**Daegu Subway Fire (2003):** On February 18, 2003, Kim Dae-han set fire to a Daegu Metro subway train at Jungangno station using a container of flammable liquid. The fire spread to a second train that stopped at the same station, killing 192 people and injuring 151. Kim was initially sentenced to death under Article 164 in combination with homicide provisions. On appeal in January 2004, the sentence was reduced to life imprisonment after psychological evaluation revealed diminished mental capacity. The train operators were also convicted of criminal negligence, receiving 4-5 year sentences.[^1]

**Namdaemun (Sungnyemun) Gate Fire (2008):** On February 10, 2008, Chae Jong-gi, a 69-year-old man aggrieved over a land dispute, climbed the western wall of the Namdaemun gate -- designated National Treasure No. 1 -- and set fire to the second floor using paint thinner. The 550-year-old wooden structure was almost completely destroyed. Chae, who had previously been charged with setting fire to Changgyeonggung palace in 2006, was convicted and sentenced to 10 years imprisonment. The case prompted major reforms in cultural property protection and fire safety legislation.[^2]

**Seoul Subway Arson Attempt (2025):** A 67-year-old man poured gasoline inside a moving Line 5 subway train between Yeouinaru and Mapo stations, allegedly attempting to kill all 160 passengers and himself. He was convicted and sentenced to 12 years imprisonment, with the court emphasizing the extreme danger posed to a large number of people in an enclosed space.[^3]

## United States

### Legal Framework

Arson in the United States is prosecuted at both federal and state levels, creating a complex patchwork of overlapping statutes.

#### Federal Law

Two primary federal statutes address arson:

**18 U.S.C. section 81 -- Arson within Special Maritime and Territorial Jurisdiction:**
- Applies to federal property, military installations, and U.S. territorial jurisdiction
- Standard penalty: up to 25 years imprisonment
- If a dwelling is burned or life is endangered: imprisonment for any term of years or life

**18 U.S.C. section 844(i) -- Arson Affecting Interstate Commerce:**
- Applies to property "used in interstate or foreign commerce or in any activity affecting interstate or foreign commerce"
- Base offence (no injury): 5 to 20 years mandatory
- Personal injury or substantial risk of injury: 7 to 40 years mandatory
- Death resulting: any term of years, life imprisonment, or death penalty
- Fines up to $250,000

#### State Law

Most states classify arson by degree, though specific definitions and penalties vary widely:

| Degree | Typical Elements | Typical Penalty Range |
|--------|-----------------|----------------------|
| First Degree | Burning an occupied dwelling; intent to harm persons | 10-25 years to life; Class B felony |
| Second Degree | Burning an occupied building (non-dwelling) or unoccupied dwelling | 5-25 years; Class C felony |
| Third Degree | Burning an unoccupied structure or vehicle | 2-10 years; Class D felony |
| Fourth Degree | Burning personal property; reckless burning | 1-5 years; Class E felony |

**New York (Penal Law sections 150.00-150.20):** Classifies arson into five degrees. First-degree arson (section 150.20) -- using an incendiary device to burn an occupied building -- is a Class A-I felony carrying 15-25 years to life.

**California (Penal Code section 451):** Distinguishes arson of inhabited structures (3-8 years state prison), structures/forest land (2-6 years), and other property (16 months to 3 years). Aggravated arson (section 451.5) with prior arson convictions or multiple structures carries 10 years to life.

**Texas (Penal Code section 28.02):** Arson is a second-degree felony (2-20 years), elevated to first degree (5-99 years or life) when bodily injury or death results or the target is a habitation or place of worship.

### Landmark Cases

**Jones v. United States, 529 U.S. 848 (2000):** The Supreme Court unanimously held that an owner-occupied private residence not used for any commercial purpose does not qualify as property "used in" interstate commerce under 18 U.S.C. section 844(i). The defendant had thrown a Molotov cocktail into his cousin's home. Justice Ginsburg, writing for the Court, emphasized that Congress required the property itself to have been used in commerce -- not merely that its destruction might affect commerce. The decision significantly narrowed the reach of federal arson jurisdiction.[^4]

**Russell v. United States, 471 U.S. 858 (1985):** The Supreme Court held that a two-unit apartment building in which the defendant himself rented a unit qualified as property "used in" an activity affecting interstate commerce, because it was rental property and the rental of real estate is commercial activity. This case established the lower boundary of the commerce nexus required under section 844(i).[^5]

**United States v. Huynh, 60 F.3d 1386 (9th Cir. 1995):** The Ninth Circuit affirmed that section 844(i) applied to the arson of a private residence that was being used as a base for a drug trafficking operation, satisfying the interstate commerce requirement through the drug trade's inherently interstate nature.[^6]

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German arson law is codified in Chapter Twenty-Eight of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), Sections 306 through 306f. The structure reflects a carefully graduated system moving from property offences to offences endangering life.

#### Section 306 -- Arson (Brandstiftung)

Whoever sets fire to or by setting fire destroys, in whole or in part, property belonging to another in the following categories shall face imprisonment from 1 to 10 years:

1. Buildings or huts
2. Plants or technical facilities, particularly machines
3. Warehouses or stored goods
4. Motor vehicles, rail vehicles, aircraft, or watercraft
5. Forests, heaths, or moors
6. Agricultural, nutritional, or forestry facilities or products

In less serious cases: 6 months to 5 years.

#### Section 306a -- Aggravated Arson (Schwere Brandstiftung)

Imprisonment of not less than 1 year (up to 15 years) for whoever:
- Sets fire to a building, ship, hut, or premises serving as a human dwelling
- Sets fire to a church or building used for religious worship
- Sets fire to a building in which persons are customarily present at the time of the offence

Also applies when fire is set to objects under Section 306(1) and thereby endangers the health of another person. In less serious cases: 6 months to 5 years.

#### Section 306b -- Especially Aggravated Arson (Besonders schwere Brandstiftung)

Imprisonment of not less than 2 years for arson under Sections 306 or 306a that causes:
- Serious health damage to another person, or
- Health damage to a large number of people

Imprisonment of not less than 5 years when the offender:
- Places another person in danger of death
- Acts with intent to facilitate or conceal another offence
- Prevents or hinders fire extinguishing efforts

#### Section 306c -- Arson Causing Death (Brandstiftung mit Todesfolge)

Life imprisonment or imprisonment of not less than 10 years when arson under Sections 306-306b causes the death of another person through at least negligence.

#### Section 306d -- Arson by Negligence (Fahrlässige Brandstiftung)

Imprisonment up to 5 years or fine for negligent commission of acts described in Section 306(1). Imprisonment up to 3 years or fine for negligent commission of acts described in Section 306a.

#### Section 306e -- Active Repentance (Tätige Reue)

Courts may mitigate the sentence or dispense with punishment entirely if the offender voluntarily extinguishes the fire before significant damage occurs -- a provision unique to German arson law that incentivizes fire suppression.

#### Section 306f -- Causing Danger of Fire (Herbeiführen einer Brandgefahr)

Imprisonment from 1 to 10 years for making a space vulnerable to fire through improper storage of flammable materials, defective manufacturing or installation of fire systems, or removing or disabling fire protection systems, thereby endangering other persons. This provision criminalizes the creation of fire risk even without an actual fire.

### Key Principles

**Property Requirement (Section 306):** Unlike many common law systems, German law requires for basic arson (Section 306) that the property belong to "another." Setting fire to one's own property is only punishable under Section 306a if it serves as a dwelling or endangers others, or under Section 306f if it creates a general fire danger.

**Concrete vs. Abstract Danger:** German courts distinguish between offences requiring concrete danger to persons (Section 306a(2)) and those where the danger is abstractly presumed by law (Section 306a(1) -- occupied dwellings are presumed dangerous regardless of whether anyone was actually present at the time).

**Active Repentance:** Section 306e provides a remarkable incentive mechanism: voluntary extinguishment of the fire before substantial damage occurs can result in complete exemption from punishment, reflecting the legislative priority on preventing harm over punishing intent.

### Landmark Cases

**Solingen Arson Attack (1993):** On May 29, 1993, four young men -- Markus Gartmann, Felix Kohnen, Christian Reher, and Christian Buchholz -- threw Molotov cocktails through the window of a home inhabited by a Turkish immigrant family in Solingen, North Rhine-Westphalia. The ensuing fire killed five family members (aged 4, 9, 12, 18, and 27) and injured 14 others. The perpetrators, all linked to far-right skinhead networks, were convicted in October 1995 of five counts of murder and arson. Gartmann received 15 years; the three juvenile defendants received the maximum juvenile sentence of 10 years each. The Federal Court of Justice (BGH) confirmed the convictions on appeal in 1997. The case became a defining moment in German discourse on far-right violence and prompted major reforms in immigration and hate crime policy.[^7]

**Rostock-Lichtenhagen Riots (1992):** Over several days in August 1992, a mob attacked the Central Reception Centre for Asylum Seekers and a residential building primarily housing Vietnamese workers, hurling Molotov cocktails and setting fires while local police struggled to contain the situation. The arson attacks were prosecuted under Sections 306 and 306a, though convictions remained controversially few relative to the scale of violence, prompting criticism of the investigative response.[^8]

**BGH, Judgment of 22 April 2020 (5 StR 564/19):** The Federal Court of Justice clarified the scope of Section 306a(1), holding that a building "serves as a human dwelling" even when only intermittently occupied, provided it remains designated and equipped for habitation. The ruling expanded the reach of aggravated arson to include holiday homes and temporarily vacant apartments.[^9]

## ECHR & EU Framework

### European Convention on Human Rights

The ECHR does not contain a specific provision criminalizing arson. Instead, the Court addresses arson through the lens of positive obligations derived from substantive Convention rights.

#### Article 2 -- Right to Life

The European Court of Human Rights has developed extensive case law holding that states bear positive obligations to protect the right to life from foreseeable threats, including fire-related dangers. This obligation has two dimensions:

1. **Substantive:** States must establish legislative and administrative frameworks providing effective deterrence against threats to life, including building safety codes, fire prevention regulations, and adequate emergency response systems.
2. **Procedural:** When deaths result from fires -- whether accidental or deliberate -- states must conduct effective, independent investigations capable of leading to identification and punishment of those responsible.

#### Article 8 -- Right to Respect for Private and Family Life

Arson attacks on homes engage Article 8 obligations. States must take reasonable measures to protect individuals' homes from criminal destruction, including maintaining adequate criminal penalties and effective law enforcement.

#### Protocol 1, Article 1 -- Protection of Property

The destruction of property through arson can engage state obligations under the right to peaceful enjoyment of possessions, particularly where state negligence or inadequate legal frameworks contribute to the loss.

### EU Framework

#### Council Framework Decision 2002/584/JHA (European Arrest Warrant)

Arson is included in the list of 32 offence categories under Article 2(2) of the Framework Decision establishing the European Arrest Warrant (EAW). For arson offences punishable by a custodial sentence of at least 3 years in the issuing member state, surrender must be granted without verification of double criminality. This means an arson suspect can be transferred between EU member states without the executing state needing to verify that the specific conduct constitutes arson under its own law.[^10]

#### EU Directive 2017/541 on Combating Terrorism

Where arson is committed as a terrorist act -- such as deliberate fire-setting intended to seriously intimidate a population or compel government action -- it falls within the scope of the EU's counter-terrorism directive, which mandates minimum penalties across member states.

#### Council of Europe Convention on the Prevention of Terrorism (CETS No. 196)

This convention requires signatory states to criminalize provocation, recruitment, and training for terrorist offences, which includes arson when committed with terrorist intent.

### Landmark Cases

**Oneryildiz v. Turkey, App. No. 48939/99 (Grand Chamber, 2004):** A methane explosion at a waste-collection site in Istanbul caused a landslide that killed 39 people, including nine members of the applicant's family. The Grand Chamber held that Article 2 imposes a positive obligation on states to take appropriate steps to safeguard life, particularly in connection with dangerous industrial activities. Despite this not being an arson case per se, the decision established foundational principles regarding state responsibility for fire and explosion risks that apply equally to arson prevention frameworks. The Court found violations of both the substantive and procedural limbs of Article 2.[^11]

**Budayeva and Others v. Russia, App. Nos. 15339/02 et al. (2008):** Though arising from a mudslide rather than arson, this case reinforced the principle that states must establish legislative and administrative frameworks designed to deter threats to the right to life from known and identifiable hazards. The Court held that Russia violated Article 2 by failing to implement land-planning and emergency relief policies despite documented evidence of recurring danger. The decision strengthened the doctrinal basis for holding states accountable when inadequate fire prevention or building safety regimes contribute to arson-related deaths.[^12]

**Bljakaj and Others v. Croatia, App. No. 74448/12 (2014):** The Court examined state responsibility for failure to protect individuals from known criminal threats. While the case involved a shooting rather than arson, the positive obligation framework it reinforced -- that authorities who know or ought to know of a real and immediate risk to life from criminal acts must take reasonable preventive measures -- applies directly to arson prevention scenarios.

**McCann and Others v. United Kingdom, App. No. 18984/91 (1995):** While primarily a use-of-force case, this Grand Chamber judgment established the foundational principle that Article 2 must be interpreted to impose practical and effective safeguards for the right to life, laying the groundwork for later cases that extended positive obligations to dangerous activities including fire hazards.

## Comparative Analysis

### Structural Approaches

The four jurisdictions reveal fundamentally different approaches to structuring arson offences:

- **South Korea** employs a property-type classification: the severity of punishment depends primarily on what was burned (occupied building, public structure, other structure, general property) and whether the property belonged to the offender or another.
- **Germany** uses a danger-based graduated system: the base offence targets property destruction, while aggravated forms focus on whether human life or health was endangered, with a unique "active repentance" mitigating provision.
- **The United States** combines a jurisdictional trigger (interstate commerce nexus for federal cases) with state-level degree classifications that generally emphasize occupancy and intent.
- **The ECHR/EU framework** operates as a meta-system, imposing floor standards on domestic arson regimes through positive obligations to protect life and property without directly criminalizing conduct.

### Penalty Comparison

| Scenario | South Korea | United States (Federal) | Germany | ECHR/EU |
|----------|------------|------------------------|---------|---------|
| Basic property arson | 1-10 years | 5-20 years | 1-10 years | Domestic law applies |
| Arson of occupied dwelling | 3 years to life | 7-40 years or life | Not less than 1 year (up to 15) | Domestic law applies |
| Arson causing death | Life imprisonment | Life or death penalty | 10 years to life | Art. 2 procedural obligation triggered |
| Negligent fire | Up to 1.5 years | Varies by state | Up to 5 years | Domestic law applies |
| Arson of own property | Up to 3-7 years (if public danger) | Generally not federal | Not punishable unless dwelling/danger | Domestic law applies |

### Notable Distinctions

**Intent Standards:** German law uniquely applies different intent standards across its arson tiers. Simple arson (Section 306) requires intent regarding property destruction. Aggravated arson (Section 306a(1)) is an abstract endangerment offence -- no intent to endanger persons is required. South Korea and the United States generally require willful and malicious intent for the base offence.

**Own-Property Burning:** All three domestic systems treat burning one's own property differently from burning another's. South Korea and Germany both require proof of "public danger" for self-property arson to be criminal. Many U.S. states similarly require that the fire endanger others or be set for insurance fraud purposes.

**Federal-State Complexity:** The United States is unique in its dual-sovereignty prosecution model. An arsonist may face both federal charges under 18 U.S.C. section 844(i) and state charges for the same fire, with no double jeopardy bar between sovereigns (Gamble v. United States, 587 U.S. 678 (2019)).

**Active Repentance:** Germany's Section 306e -- allowing sentence mitigation or elimination for voluntary fire extinguishment -- has no equivalent in South Korean or U.S. law. This reflects the German Criminal Code's broader emphasis on Taeterstraefrecht (offender-focused punishment) and its preference for incentivizing harm prevention.
