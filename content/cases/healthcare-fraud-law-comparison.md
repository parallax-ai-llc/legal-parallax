## Summary

Healthcare fraud -- the deliberate misrepresentation or deception in connection with the delivery of or payment for healthcare services, benefits, or products -- represents one of the most financially damaging categories of white-collar crime worldwide. The schemes range from billing for services never rendered and upcoding procedures to kickback arrangements and pharmaceutical rebate manipulation. South Korea addresses healthcare fraud through its National Health Insurance Act, Medical Service Act, and the Special Act on Prevention of Insurance Fraud, with penalties of up to 10 years imprisonment and fines of up to KRW 50 million. The United States maintains the most aggressive enforcement regime globally, anchored by 18 U.S.C. section 1347 (carrying up to 10 years, or life imprisonment if death results), the False Claims Act (allowing treble damages), the Anti-Kickback Statute, and the Stark Law, with the Department of Justice recovering a record $5.7 billion in healthcare-related False Claims Act settlements in fiscal year 2025 alone. Germany prosecutes healthcare billing fraud (Abrechnungsbetrug) under the general fraud provision of section 263 StGB (up to 5 years, or 10 years in aggravated cases) and, since 2016, targets healthcare-specific corruption through sections 299a and 299b StGB, enacted to close a gap identified by the Bundesgerichtshof in 2012. At the European level, the ECHR engages healthcare fraud indirectly through Articles 2, 3, and 8 of the Convention when fraudulent practices endanger patient welfare, while the EU combats fraud affecting its financial interests through the PIF Directive (2017/1371), the European Anti-Fraud Office (OLAF), and the European Public Prosecutor's Office (EPPO), with the European Healthcare Fraud and Corruption Network estimating annual losses of 56 billion euros across EU member states.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's healthcare fraud enforcement operates through multiple overlapping statutes within the context of its single-payer National Health Insurance (NHI) system, which covers approximately 97% of the population:

- **National Health Insurance Act (NHIA, 국민건강보험법):** Governs the NHI system administered by the National Health Insurance Service (NHIS). Providers who fraudulently claim reimbursement from the NHIS are subject to administrative sanctions including refund orders, penalty surcharges (up to five times the fraudulent amount), and suspension or cancellation of insurance provider status.
- **Medical Service Act (의료법):** Articles 27 and 33 regulate the licensing and conduct of healthcare providers. Violations of Article 27(3) and (4) -- including unauthorized practice and fraudulent billing -- carry imprisonment of up to 3 years or fines up to KRW 10 million. Violations of Article 27(2) and Article 33(1), (3), and (5) carry fines up to KRW 3 million.
- **Special Act on Prevention of Insurance Fraud (보험사기방지 특별법, 2016):** Enacted in March 2016, this law specifically targets insurance fraud, including healthcare-related schemes. It imposes imprisonment of up to 10 years or fines up to KRW 50 million for direct fraud. Those who broker, entice, or advertise insurance fraud face equivalent penalties. The Act also permits fines equivalent to the amount of illegally gained funds.
- **Specific Economic Crimes Aggravated Punishment Act (특정경제범죄 가중처벌 등에 관한 법률):** For large-scale healthcare fraud, this Act imposes enhanced penalties: 3 years to life imprisonment for fraud exceeding KRW 500 million, and 5 years to life for fraud exceeding KRW 5 billion.

### Enforcement Mechanisms

The Health Insurance Review and Assessment Service (HIRA) conducts claims reviews and audits of providers' billing practices. When fraud is detected, HIRA refers cases to the NHIS for administrative action and, where criminal conduct is suspected, to the Prosecutor's Office. The government may impose sanctions including monetary penalties, suspension of practice, and criminal prosecution.

The Financial Supervisory Service (FSS) additionally oversees insurance fraud detection and works with criminal justice agencies including the police and Prosecutor's Office, though coordination among these agencies has been criticized as fragmented.

### Landmark Cases

**Changwon Mass Insurance Fraud Case (2012):** The Financial Supervisory Service uncovered a large-scale insurance fraud ring in Changwon involving as many as 1,361 individuals, mostly residents of South Gyeongsang Province, who posed as fake patients or fabricated medical conditions to file fraudulent insurance claims. The case highlighted systemic vulnerabilities in the NHI claims review process.

**NHIS Executive Embezzlement Case (2024-2025):** A senior executive of the National Health Insurance Corporation was sentenced to 15 years in prison for embezzling approximately KRW 4.6 billion (roughly $3.4 million USD) from the organization and squandering the proceeds on cryptocurrency futures investments. The severity of the sentence reflected the breach of public trust inherent in the defendant's position.

**Medical Data Leak Prosecution (2023-2024):** Twenty-four individuals were indicted on charges of illegally collecting and leaking the medical records of approximately 44 million South Korean citizens to a U.S.-based company. The leaked data included resident registration numbers, disease names, and prescription records, representing both a massive privacy violation and potential fraud infrastructure.

### Penalties Summary

| Offense | Statute | Maximum Penalty |
|---------|---------|-----------------|
| Insurance fraud | Special Act on Prevention of Insurance Fraud | 10 years imprisonment or KRW 50 million fine |
| Fraudulent NHI billing | National Health Insurance Act | Refund + surcharge up to 5x; provider status revocation |
| Unauthorized practice / billing fraud | Medical Service Act Art. 27(3)(4) | 3 years imprisonment or KRW 10 million fine |
| Large-scale fraud (over KRW 500 million) | Specific Economic Crimes Act | 3 years to life imprisonment |
| Insurance fraud data violation | Special Act Art. 13 | 3 years imprisonment or KRW 30 million fine |

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States maintains the most extensive and aggressive healthcare fraud enforcement regime in the world, reflecting both the scale of its healthcare expenditure (over $4.5 trillion annually) and the multiplicity of public and private payers. The principal federal statutes include:

- **18 U.S.C. section 1347 (Health Care Fraud Statute):** Enacted as part of the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) of 1996, this statute makes it a federal crime to knowingly and willfully execute, or attempt to execute, a scheme to defraud any healthcare benefit program or to obtain money or property from a healthcare benefit program through false pretenses. Crucially, a person need not have actual knowledge of the statute or specific intent to violate it.
- **False Claims Act (FCA, 31 U.S.C. sections 3729-3733):** Originally enacted during the Civil War (the "Lincoln Law"), the FCA imposes civil liability on any person who knowingly presents a false claim to the government for payment. It authorizes treble damages plus civil penalties of $11,000 or more per false claim. Its qui tam (whistleblower) provisions allow private citizens to file suit on behalf of the government and receive 15-30% of any recovery.
- **Anti-Kickback Statute (AKS, 42 U.S.C. section 1320a-7b(b)):** Prohibits the knowing and willful payment or receipt of remuneration to induce or reward patient referrals or the generation of business involving any item or service payable by a federal healthcare program. Violation is a felony carrying up to 10 years imprisonment and fines up to $100,000 per violation, plus civil monetary penalties of up to $50,000 per violation and treble the remuneration involved.
- **Stark Law (Physician Self-Referral Law, 42 U.S.C. section 1395nn):** Prohibits physicians from referring Medicare or Medicaid patients for designated health services to entities with which the physician or an immediate family member has a financial relationship (unless a specific exception applies). Violations trigger civil penalties of up to $15,000 per improper service, up to $100,000 per circumvention scheme, treble damages, and exclusion from Medicare.
- **Civil Monetary Penalties Law (CMPL, 42 U.S.C. section 1320a-7a):** Authorizes HHS to impose administrative penalties for various forms of healthcare fraud, including false claims and kickback violations.

### Elements of the Offense (18 U.S.C. section 1347)

To convict under the federal healthcare fraud statute, the prosecution must prove:

1. **Scheme to Defraud:** The defendant devised or participated in a scheme or artifice to defraud a healthcare benefit program.
2. **Materiality:** The false representations or omissions were material to the healthcare benefit program's decision to pay.
3. **Knowing and Willful Conduct:** The defendant acted knowingly and willfully, though actual knowledge of the statute is not required.
4. **Connection to Healthcare:** The scheme was executed in connection with the delivery of or payment for healthcare benefits, items, or services.

### Criminal Penalties

| Offense | Statute | Maximum Penalty |
|---------|---------|-----------------|
| Healthcare fraud (general) | 18 U.S.C. section 1347 | 10 years imprisonment and/or $250,000 fine |
| Healthcare fraud (serious bodily injury) | 18 U.S.C. section 1347 | 20 years imprisonment |
| Healthcare fraud (death results) | 18 U.S.C. section 1347 | Life imprisonment |
| Anti-Kickback violation | 42 U.S.C. section 1320a-7b | 10 years imprisonment and/or $100,000 fine |
| False statement relating to healthcare | 18 U.S.C. section 1035 | 5 years imprisonment |
| Obstruction of healthcare fraud investigation | 18 U.S.C. section 1518 | 5 years imprisonment |

### Landmark Cases

**Columbia/HCA Healthcare Corporation (2000-2003):** The largest healthcare fraud settlement in U.S. history at the time. Columbia/HCA (now HCA Healthcare) agreed to plead guilty to criminal conduct and pay more than $840 million in criminal fines, civil penalties, and damages for systematically overbilling Medicare and Medicaid, paying kickbacks to physicians for patient referrals, and billing for unnecessary laboratory tests. The total combined settlements exceeded $1.7 billion.

**Multi-Billion Dollar Healthcare Fraud Scheme (2024):** In one of the largest cases by alleged loss amount ever charged by the DOJ, 11 defendants were indicted in the Eastern District of New York for a multi-billion-dollar healthcare fraud scheme involving fraudulent billing to Medicare and Medicaid programs.

**National Healthcare Fraud Takedown (2024):** The DOJ announced charges against 324 defendants in connection with over $14.6 billion in alleged healthcare fraud, demonstrating the scale of coordinated federal enforcement efforts.

**FY 2025 Record Recoveries:** The DOJ's False Claims Act settlements and judgments exceeded $6.8 billion in fiscal year 2025 -- the highest annual total in the statute's history -- with over $5.7 billion related to healthcare matters. A record 1,297 whistleblower qui tam actions were filed, alongside 401 new government-initiated investigations.

**Skilling v. United States (2010) and Ciminelli v. United States (2023):** While not healthcare-specific, these Supreme Court decisions narrowed the scope of federal fraud statutes relevant to healthcare fraud prosecutions by limiting honest services fraud to bribery and kickback schemes (Skilling) and rejecting the "right to control" theory of fraud (Ciminelli).

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's approach to healthcare fraud operates through general criminal law provisions supplemented by sector-specific anti-corruption statutes enacted in 2016 to address identified gaps:

- **Section 263 StGB (Betrug / Fraud):** The foundational criminal provision for healthcare billing fraud (Abrechnungsbetrug). It covers any deception-induced, self-damaging action by the victim resulting in immediate financial loss. Basic fraud carries up to 5 years imprisonment; aggravated cases (section 263(3)) carry 6 months to 10 years; gang-related fraud (section 263(5)) carries a minimum of 1 year imprisonment.
- **Sections 299a and 299b StGB (Corruption in the Healthcare Sector):** Enacted on 4 June 2016, these provisions criminalize passive bribery (accepting benefits, section 299a) and active bribery (offering benefits, section 299b) in the healthcare sector. They were introduced to close a legal gap identified by the BGH in its landmark 2012 decision (Az.: GSSt 2/11), which held that private practitioners could not be prosecuted for corruption because they were neither public officials nor agents of a business. Both offenses carry up to 3 years imprisonment or a fine.
- **SGB V section 197a (Social Code Book V):** Establishes dedicated anti-fraud units (Stellen zur Bekampfung von Fehlverhalten im Gesundheitswesen) within all statutory health insurance funds, their associations, and the GKV-Spitzenverband. These units are responsible for investigating suspected fraud and abuse in the statutory health insurance system.
- **SGB XI section 47a:** Establishes parallel anti-fraud units for the long-term care insurance system.
- **Professional Fee Schedules (GOA, GOZ):** The fee schedules for physicians (Gebuhrenordnung fur Arzte) and dentists (Gebuhrenordnung fur Zahnarzte) define permissible billing codes and amounts; systematic misuse constitutes Abrechnungsbetrug.

### Elements of the Offense (Section 263 StGB in Healthcare Context)

Healthcare billing fraud under section 263 requires:

1. **Deception (Tauschung):** Submission of false or inflated billing to a health insurer or patient, including billing for services not rendered, upcoding (using higher-value billing codes than warranted), or unbundling services to inflate reimbursement.
2. **Error (Irrtum):** The insurer or patient must form a mistaken belief about the legitimacy of the claim based on the deception.
3. **Property Disposition (Vermogensverfugung):** Payment by the insurer or patient based on the fraudulent claim.
4. **Financial Loss (Vermogensschaden):** Measurable financial damage to the insurer or patient corresponding to the improper payment.

### Elements of Healthcare Corruption (Sections 299a/299b StGB)

The healthcare corruption offenses cover conduct in which benefits are granted to a healthcare professional in exchange for unfairly favoring a provider when:

- Prescribing medicines, therapies, or medical products
- Purchasing medicines, therapies, aids, or medical products
- Assigning patients or study material

The scope extends to both "academic HCPs" (physicians, dentists, veterinarians, pharmacists, psychotherapists) and "paramedical professions" (nurses, occupational therapists, speech therapists, physiotherapists).

### Landmark Cases

**BGH Decision on Healthcare Corruption Gap (2012, Az.: GSSt 2/11):** In March 2012, the Bundesgerichtshof (Federal Court of Justice) issued a landmark ruling finding that the German Criminal Code did not allow doctors operating private practices to be punished for corruption, nor could the companies bribing them be held liable. The court acknowledged that such corruption was morally reprehensible and called on the legislature to close the gap -- which it did four years later with sections 299a and 299b StGB.

**Burgdorf Billing Fraud Case:** A prosecution in Burgdorf revealed a scheme in which patients were sent between medical practices without medical justification, and doctors submitted bills for treatments that were never provided. The case illustrated the classic Abrechnungsbetrug pattern of phantom billing within the statutory health insurance system.

**Wirecard (2020-present):** While primarily a financial fraud case, the Wirecard scandal -- in which the Munich-based payment processor admitted that 1.9 billion euros on its balance sheet did not exist -- had implications for healthcare payment processing and demonstrated systemic weaknesses in German fraud detection. The ongoing trial of CEO Markus Braun in Munich is one of the most complex white-collar criminal proceedings in German history.

### Estimated Impact

Healthcare billing fraud and corruption in Germany are estimated to cause annual damage of approximately 14 billion euros to the healthcare system, according to Transparency International analyses.

### Penalties Summary

| Offense | Provision | Penalty |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Basic billing fraud | Section 263(1) StGB | Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine |
| Aggravated fraud (commercial/gang) | Section 263(3) StGB | 6 months to 10 years imprisonment |
| Organized fraud | Section 263(5) StGB | 1 to 10 years imprisonment |
| Healthcare corruption (passive) | Section 299a StGB | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine |
| Healthcare corruption (active) | Section 299b StGB | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine |

## ECHR & EU

### European Convention on Human Rights Framework

The ECHR does not contain a specific provision addressing healthcare fraud. However, the European Court of Human Rights has developed jurisprudence through which healthcare fraud may engage Convention rights when fraudulent practices compromise patient care:

- **Article 2 (Right to Life):** Engaged when a patient's life is knowingly put in danger by denial of access to life-saving emergency treatment, or when systemic dysfunction in hospital services -- potentially resulting from fraudulent resource diversion -- deprives patients of necessary care. The Court has held that a substantive violation of Article 2 may arise where the regulatory framework fails to ensure proper protection of patients' lives.
- **Article 3 (Prohibition of Torture and Inhuman Treatment):** Applicable where inadequate healthcare -- potentially linked to fraudulent diversion of resources -- results in suffering meeting the minimum threshold of severity. In a 2024 case, the Court found a violation of Article 3 where a patient with a condition requiring regular physiotherapy received only ten sessions over two years.
- **Article 8 (Right to Private and Family Life):** Relevant where healthcare fraud involves misuse of patient data or where fraudulent practices result in inappropriate medical treatment affecting physical or psychological integrity.

### EU Legislative Framework

The European Union addresses healthcare fraud through several interconnected instruments:

- **PIF Directive (Directive 2017/1371):** Adopted on 5 July 2017, this directive harmonizes the definitions, sanctions, jurisdiction rules, and limitation periods for criminal offenses affecting the EU's financial interests. Covered offenses include fraud (including cross-border VAT fraud involving total damage of at least 10 million euros), corruption, money laundering, and misappropriation. Member states must ensure maximum penalties of at least 4 years imprisonment for fraud exceeding 100,000 euros affecting EU financial interests.
- **TFEU Article 325:** Requires the EU and member states to counter fraud and any other illegal activities affecting the financial interests of the Union through deterrent measures.
- **EU Whistleblower Directive (2019/1937):** Provides protection for persons reporting breaches of EU law, including healthcare fraud affecting EU financial interests. Member states were required to transpose the directive by December 2021.
- **Directive 2011/24/EU (Cross-Border Healthcare):** Governs patients' rights in cross-border healthcare and contains provisions relevant to preventing fraud in cross-border healthcare billing.

### EU Enforcement Institutions

- **European Anti-Fraud Office (OLAF):** Investigates fraud against the EU budget, corruption, and serious misconduct within EU institutions. OLAF has operational independence within the European Commission and investigates fraudulent use of EU funds, including those allocated to healthcare programs.
- **European Public Prosecutor's Office (EPPO):** Established pursuant to the PIF Directive, the EPPO conducts investigations and prosecutions of crimes affecting the EU's financial interests, including healthcare fraud involving EU-funded programs. Proper transposition of the PIF Directive by member states is essential to enabling effective EPPO operations.
- **European Healthcare Fraud and Corruption Network (EHFCN):** A network of European organizations active in healthcare and counter-fraud, supporting members in prevention, detection, investigation, and prosecution of healthcare fraud. The EHFCN held its 16th International Conference in Madrid in November 2025, with over 115 participants from 16 countries and 45 organizations.

### Scale of Healthcare Fraud in Europe

The EHFCN and related studies estimate that approximately 56 billion euros are lost to healthcare fraud annually across Europe -- nearly 80 million euros per day, representing more than 5% of all EU national health budgets combined. This figure underscores the magnitude of the challenge and the impetus behind coordinated enforcement efforts.

### Key ECHR Cases with Healthcare Implications

**Lopes de Sousa Fernandes v. Portugal (2017, Grand Chamber):** The Court established that Article 2 may be engaged in healthcare contexts where (a) the regulatory framework fails to protect patients' lives, or (b) the authorities knew or ought to have known of systemic risk to patients from dysfunction in hospital services. While primarily a medical negligence case, the principles apply to situations where healthcare fraud causes systemic resource deprivation.

**Paposhvili v. Belgium (2016, Grand Chamber):** The Court refined the Article 3 threshold in medical cases, holding that removal of a seriously ill person could engage Article 3 where substantial grounds existed for believing it would result in a real risk of serious, rapid, and irreversible decline in health resulting in intense suffering or significant reduction in life expectancy.

## Comparative Analysis

### Structural Differences

The four jurisdictions reflect fundamentally different approaches to healthcare fraud enforcement:

1. **South Korea** relies on administrative enforcement through the NHIS and HIRA as the primary mechanism, with criminal prosecution as a secondary tool. The single-payer NHI system provides centralized billing data that facilitates detection, but fragmented coordination among enforcement agencies has been identified as a weakness.

2. **The United States** has developed the most comprehensive and punitive enforcement regime, combining criminal prosecution, civil liability (through the False Claims Act's treble damages), administrative exclusion, and powerful whistleblower incentives. The qui tam provisions have proved extraordinarily effective, generating a record 1,297 whistleblower-initiated actions in FY 2025.

3. **Germany** initially relied on general fraud provisions (section 263 StGB) for healthcare fraud, but the BGH's 2012 identification of a corruption gap prompted targeted legislative action through sections 299a and 299b StGB in 2016. The establishment of dedicated anti-fraud units under SGB V section 197a reflects a growing institutional commitment to enforcement.

4. **The ECHR/EU framework** operates at a supranational level, setting minimum standards and facilitating cross-border cooperation rather than directly prosecuting individual cases. The PIF Directive's requirement of minimum 4-year maximum sentences for serious fraud affecting EU financial interests represents an important harmonization effort.

### Key Distinctions

- **Whistleblower Incentives:** The United States is unique in providing substantial financial rewards (15-30% of recovery) to whistleblowers through the FCA's qui tam provisions. No other jurisdiction studied offers comparable incentive structures.
- **Penalty Severity:** U.S. penalties are the most severe, with potential life imprisonment where healthcare fraud results in patient death. South Korea's Specific Economic Crimes Act also allows life sentences for large-scale fraud. Germany's penalties are comparatively moderate.
- **Civil vs. Criminal:** The U.S. and South Korea maintain parallel civil and criminal enforcement tracks. Germany primarily relies on criminal prosecution supplemented by insurer civil claims. The EU framework emphasizes member state criminal law harmonization.
- **Healthcare Corruption:** Germany's 2016 enactment of sections 299a/299b StGB filled a significant gap, while the U.S. Anti-Kickback Statute and Stark Law have addressed healthcare corruption since 1972 and 1989, respectively.
