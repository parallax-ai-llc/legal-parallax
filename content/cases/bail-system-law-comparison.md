## Summary

Bail and pretrial detention represent one of the most consequential intersections of individual liberty and state power in any criminal justice system. The decision to confine an accused person before trial — or to release them subject to conditions — directly implicates the presumption of innocence, the right to liberty, and the practical capacity of a defendant to prepare an adequate defense. Despite universal recognition that pretrial liberty should be the norm rather than the exception, the mechanisms by which jurisdictions implement this principle vary dramatically. South Korea's Criminal Procedure Act (hyeongsasosongbeop) provides for both mandatory bail (pillyojeok bosek, Article 95) and discretionary bail (imuijeok bosek, Article 96), but historically grants bail at lower rates than Western democracies, with detention periods extending up to six months during investigation. The United States operates a uniquely complex system shaped by the Eighth Amendment's prohibition on excessive bail, the Bail Reform Act of 1984, and a patchwork of state-level cash bail systems that have drawn increasing criticism for disproportionately impacting indigent defendants. Germany eschews a traditional bail system in favor of pretrial detention (Untersuchungshaft) governed by Sections 112-130 of the Code of Criminal Procedure (Strafprozessordnung, StPO), emphasizing proportionality and alternatives to custody under Section 116. The European Convention on Human Rights enshrines the right to liberty and trial within a reasonable time under Article 5, with the European Court of Human Rights developing an extensive body of jurisprudence requiring that pretrial detention remain the exception, be supported by relevant and sufficient reasons, and be subject to periodic judicial review.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's pretrial detention and bail system is governed by the Criminal Procedure Act (hyeongsasosongbeop, Act No. 341 of 1954, as amended), specifically Articles 69 through 107. The Korean Constitution, Article 12, guarantees personal liberty and requires that arrest, detention, or search be conducted only upon warrant issued by a judge, except in cases of flagrant offenses.

**Pretrial Detention (gusoek):** A suspect may be detained during investigation upon issuance of an arrest warrant (chaepojangjangsang) under Article 200-2 or a detention warrant (gusokjangjangsang) under Article 73. The prosecutor must apply to a judge for the warrant, and the judge must conduct a warrant hearing (yeongjangsiljilsimsa) to determine whether detention is justified.

**Detention Periods:**

| Stage | Duration | Extension | Legal Basis |
|-------|----------|-----------|-------------|
| Police investigation | Up to 10 days | Not extendable | Art. 202 |
| Prosecutorial investigation | Up to 10 days | One extension of 10 days (total 20 days) | Art. 203, 205 |
| Post-indictment detention | Up to 2 months | Renewable up to 4 times (total 6 months for district court) | Art. 92 |
| Appellate stage | Additional 2 months per instance | Renewable | Art. 92(2) |

### Bail Provisions

**Article 95 — Mandatory Bail (pillyojeok bosek):** When a bail application has been filed, the court shall grant bail except in the following circumstances: (1) the accused is charged with an offense punishable by death, life imprisonment, or imprisonment exceeding 10 years; (2) the accused is a repeat offender or habitual criminal; (3) there are reasonable grounds to suspect the accused has destroyed or may destroy evidence; (4) there are reasonable grounds to suspect the accused has fled or may flee; (5) the accused has no known address.

**Article 96 — Discretionary Bail (imuijeok bosek):** Even where the mandatory exceptions of Article 95 apply, the court retains discretion to grant bail if it considers release appropriate. This provision serves as a safety valve ensuring that detention remains proportional to the circumstances of each case.

**Article 97 — Prosecutorial Opinion:** The presiding judge must solicit the opinion of the public prosecutor before ruling on a bail application, though the court is not bound by the prosecutor's recommendation.

**Bail Conditions (Article 98-100):** The court may impose conditions including: deposit of bail money (bosek boeunggeum) in an amount determined by the court considering the weight of evidence, the accused's criminal history, character, circumstances, and financial ability; restriction to a specified residence; prohibition on contacting victims or witnesses; surrender of passport; and a pledge not to destroy evidence.

### Pre-Indictment Bail Reform

In 2019, South Korea introduced a significant reform allowing pre-indictment bail (gisogeon bosek), enabling suspects detained during the investigation phase — before formal charges are filed — to apply for release on bail. This addressed longstanding criticism that suspects could be held for up to 20 days during investigation without any mechanism to challenge their detention through bail.

### Landmark Cases

**Park Geun-hye Detention (2017-2021):** Former President Park Geun-hye was arrested in March 2017 on charges of bribery, abuse of authority, and coercion related to the influence-peddling scandal involving her confidante Choi Soon-sil. Her pretrial detention was extended multiple times, sparking debate about the maximum permissible duration of pretrial custody for complex cases. She was ultimately convicted and sentenced to over 20 years, later receiving a presidential pardon in December 2021.

**Lee Jae-yong (Samsung Heir) — Bail and Detention (2017-2021):** Samsung Vice Chairman Lee Jae-yong's case illustrated the operation of South Korea's bail system in high-profile corporate crime. An initial arrest warrant request was rejected by the Seoul Central District Court in January 2017, but a second application succeeded in February 2017. After conviction and a five-year sentence in August 2017, the Seoul High Court reduced the sentence to 2.5 years suspended in February 2018, resulting in Lee's release. He was later resentenced to 2.5 years of actual imprisonment in January 2021, then released on parole in August 2021.

**Constitutional Court Decision 2015HunMa1177 (2018):** The Constitutional Court addressed the constitutionality of extended pretrial detention periods, affirming that while prolonged detention implicates the right to personal liberty under Article 12 of the Constitution, the statutory framework provides sufficient procedural safeguards when courts exercise their review functions diligently.

### Practical Considerations

South Korea's bail grant rate has historically been lower than that of many Western democracies, though the rate has increased significantly in recent years following judicial reforms. A 2019 study by the Judicial Policy Research Institute found that courts were increasingly receptive to bail applications, particularly following the introduction of pre-indictment bail. Conditions such as electronic monitoring, travel restrictions, and regular reporting have become more common as alternatives to cash-only bail.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States bail system operates at both federal and state levels, creating a uniquely complex and fragmented landscape. The constitutional foundation rests on the Eighth Amendment ("Excessive bail shall not be required") and the due process guarantees of the Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments.

**Federal System — The Bail Reform Act of 1984 (18 U.S.C. Sections 3141-3156):** This Act replaced the Bail Reform Act of 1966 and fundamentally reshaped federal pretrial release by introducing preventive detention — the authority to deny bail entirely based on dangerousness. Under Section 3142, there is a general presumption that defendants "shall" be released pending trial unless the government demonstrates at a detention hearing that no condition or combination of conditions will reasonably assure the appearance of the person and the safety of the community.

**Section 3142(e) — Rebuttable Presumption of Detention:** For certain serious offenses, the Act creates a rebuttable presumption favoring detention. These include offenses carrying a maximum sentence of life imprisonment or death, serious drug offenses (with maximum sentence of 10 years or more), offenses involving firearms use, and cases involving minor victims of sexual offenses.

**Detention Hearing Requirements (Section 3142(f)):** The government may request a detention hearing on grounds of flight risk or dangerousness. The defendant has the right to counsel at the hearing, to testify, to present witnesses, and to cross-examine government witnesses. The judicial officer must consider: the nature and circumstances of the offense, the weight of evidence, the defendant's history and characteristics, and the nature and seriousness of the danger posed by release.

### State Bail Systems

State systems vary enormously, but generally fall into several models:

| Model | Description | States |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| Cash bail dominant | Bail schedules set by offense; defendants must post cash or use commercial bail bondsmen | Most states (traditional model) |
| Reform / risk assessment | Algorithmic risk assessment tools guide release decisions; reduced reliance on cash bail | New Jersey, Kentucky, Virginia |
| Bail abolition (partial) | Eliminated cash bail for most offenses | Illinois (Pretrial Fairness Act, 2023), New York (2019 reforms, partially rolled back) |
| Hybrid | Cash bail retained but with expanded pretrial services and alternatives | California, Colorado |

**The Commercial Bail Bond Industry:** The United States and the Philippines are the only countries in the world that permit a commercial bail bond industry. Bail bondsmen typically charge a non-refundable premium of 10-15% of the bail amount and post the full bail on the defendant's behalf. This system has been criticized for creating a two-tier justice system where wealthy defendants secure immediate release while indigent defendants remain incarcerated.

### Constitutional Jurisprudence

**Stack v. Boyle, 342 U.S. 1 (1951):** The Supreme Court held that bail set higher than an amount reasonably calculated to ensure the defendant's appearance at trial is "excessive" under the Eighth Amendment. The Court stated: "The right to release before trial is conditioned upon the accused's giving adequate assurance that he will stand trial and submit to sentence if found guilty." In this case, bail of $50,000 for each defendant charged under the Smith Act was found excessive given the defendants' financial circumstances and lack of flight risk evidence.

**United States v. Salerno, 481 U.S. 739 (1987):** The most significant bail case in modern American jurisprudence. The Supreme Court upheld the Bail Reform Act of 1984's preventive detention provisions against Eighth Amendment and due process challenges. Chief Justice Rehnquist, writing for the majority, held that the Eighth Amendment does not guarantee an absolute right to bail — it merely prohibits excessive bail when bail is available. The Court found that Congress could deny bail entirely based on dangerousness, as "preventing danger to the community is a legitimate regulatory goal." Salerno effectively established that pretrial detention based on predicted future dangerousness is constitutionally permissible.

**County of Riverside v. McLaughlin, 500 U.S. 44 (1991):** The Court held that a jurisdiction that provides judicial determinations of probable cause within 48 hours of a warrantless arrest will, as a general matter, comply with the Fourth Amendment's promptness requirement.

**Schilb v. Kuebel, 404 U.S. 357 (1971):** The Court upheld an Illinois bail reform statute that allowed defendants to post 10% of bail directly with the court (less a service charge) rather than using a commercial bondsman, recognizing the state's legitimate interest in reforming its bail system.

### Speedy Trial Act

The federal Speedy Trial Act (18 U.S.C. Sections 3161-3174) requires that trial commence within 70 days of indictment or initial appearance, whichever is later. Various exclusions apply for pretrial motions, continuances, and other proceedings. This effectively places an indirect limit on pretrial detention duration, though excludable time can extend the period considerably.

### Bail Reform Movement

The American bail reform movement gained significant momentum in the 2010s and 2020s, driven by research demonstrating that cash bail disproportionately impacts Black and Latino defendants and those in poverty. Key developments include:

- **New Jersey (2017):** Implemented the Criminal Justice Reform Act, virtually eliminating cash bail in favor of risk-based decision-making using the Public Safety Assessment (PSA) tool.
- **Illinois Pretrial Fairness Act (2023):** Made Illinois the first state to abolish cash bail entirely (upheld by the Illinois Supreme Court in Rowe v. Raoul, 2023).
- **New York (2019-2020):** Enacted sweeping bail reform eliminating cash bail for most misdemeanors and nonviolent felonies, later partially rolled back amid political pressure following increases in certain crime categories.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany does not operate a traditional bail system comparable to the Anglo-American model. Instead, pretrial detention (Untersuchungshaft, commonly abbreviated U-Haft) is governed by Sections 112 through 130 of the Code of Criminal Procedure (Strafprozessordnung, StPO). The German Basic Law (Grundgesetz) provides the constitutional foundation through Article 2(2) (right to liberty of person), Article 104 (legal guarantees in cases of deprivation of liberty), and Article 19(4) (right to judicial review).

**Section 112(1) StPO — Prerequisites for Detention:** Pretrial detention may be ordered only if: (1) there is strong suspicion (dringender Tatverdacht) that the accused committed the offense — meaning a high probability based on available evidence; and (2) a ground for arrest (Haftgrund) exists; and (3) detention is not disproportionate to the significance of the case and the likely penalty.

**Grounds for Arrest (Haftgruende):**

| Ground | German Term | StPO Section | Description |
|--------|------------|--------------|-------------|
| Flight risk | Fluchtgefahr | Section 112(2) No. 2 | Circumstances suggest the accused will evade proceedings; assessed based on ties to community, family, employment, prior conduct, severity of expected sentence |
| Risk of evidence tampering | Verdunkelungsgefahr | Section 112(2) No. 3 | Behavior of the accused gives rise to strong suspicion that the accused will destroy, alter, or suppress evidence, or improperly influence co-accused, witnesses, or experts |
| Gravity of offense | Schwere der Tat | Section 112(3) | For offenses listed in Section 112(3) — including murder, manslaughter, genocide, crimes against humanity, terrorism, and certain sexual offenses — detention may be ordered solely based on the gravity of the offense even without specific flight or tampering risk |
| Risk of reoffending | Wiederholungsgefahr | Section 112a | For specific catalog offenses (sexual offenses, stalking, bodily harm, theft, fraud, drug offenses), detention may be ordered to prevent the accused from committing further serious offenses |

### Proportionality Principle

The proportionality requirement (Verhaeltnismaessigkeitsgrundsatz) is explicitly codified in Section 112(1) sentence 2: pretrial detention "may not be ordered if it is disproportionate to the significance of the case and the penalty or measure of reform and prevention likely to be imposed." This means that if the expected sentence would not involve imprisonment (e.g., a fine is the likely outcome), pretrial detention is generally impermissible.

### Alternatives to Detention (Section 116 StPO)

Before ordering detention, courts must consider less restrictive alternatives (mildere Mittel). Section 116 StPO provides that the execution of an arrest warrant shall be suspended if less severe measures suffice to achieve the purpose of detention. These alternatives include:

- **Reporting requirements (Meldeauflagen):** The accused must report to police or court at specified intervals.
- **Residence restrictions (Aufenthaltsbeschraenkungen):** The accused must remain at a designated address.
- **Prohibition on contacting witnesses or co-accused.**
- **Security deposit / bail (Sicherheitsleistung / Kaution):** Under Section 116(1) No. 4 and Section 116a, the court may require the deposit of cash, securities, a pledge, or a guarantee from suitable persons. The judge determines the amount and form at his or her discretion (Section 116a(2)).
- **Surrender of passport and identity documents.**
- **Electronic monitoring (elektronische Aufenthaltsueberwachung):** Increasingly used as a modern alternative.

### Duration and Judicial Review

**Six-Month Rule (Sections 121-122 StPO):** Pretrial detention exceeding six months requires review and authorization by the Higher Regional Court (Oberlandesgericht, OLG). The OLG must determine whether the "particular difficulty or unusual scope of the investigation or another important reason" justifies continued detention. This review serves as a critical safeguard against indefinite pretrial custody.

**Extension beyond 12 months** is extremely rare and subject to heightened judicial scrutiny. The Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) has repeatedly emphasized that the longer detention continues, the more stringent the justification must be.

**Detention Review (Section 117 StPO):** The accused may apply for judicial review of detention at any time. The court must rule on the application promptly. Additionally, if the accused has been detained for three months without being informed of the results of the investigation, the accused may demand an oral hearing on the continued necessity of detention.

### Landmark Cases

**BVerfG, 2 BvR 2299/09 (2009):** The Federal Constitutional Court held that prolonged pretrial detention of over one year violated the accused's fundamental right to liberty where the court had failed to conduct proceedings with sufficient expedition. The Court emphasized the state's duty of expedition (Beschleunigungsgrundsatz) in cases involving pretrial detention.

**BVerfG, 2 BvR 1190/20 (2020):** The Federal Constitutional Court ruled on the proportionality of pretrial detention during the COVID-19 pandemic, noting that delays in proceedings attributable to pandemic-related court closures could not automatically justify extended detention and that courts must make additional efforts to avoid disproportionate custody.

**BGH StB 16/19 (2019):** The Federal Court of Justice addressed the standard of "strong suspicion" required under Section 112(1), clarifying that the evidentiary threshold must be reassessed throughout the detention period and cannot be sustained solely on the basis of the initial arrest warrant evidence.

### 2019 Reform — Mandatory Defense Counsel

Since a legislative amendment effective in 2019, a public defense lawyer (Pflichtverteidiger) must be appointed by the court for any defendant in pretrial detention (Section 140(1) No. 4 StPO). This reform brought German law into closer alignment with EU Directive 2013/48/EU on the right of access to a lawyer and strengthened the procedural rights of detained suspects.

## ECHR & EU

### European Convention on Human Rights — Article 5

Article 5 of the European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR) provides the foundational framework for the right to liberty and security across the 46 member states of the Council of Europe. Its provisions on pretrial detention are contained primarily in paragraphs 1(c), 3, 4, and 5.

**Article 5(1)(c) — Lawful Detention on Reasonable Suspicion:** Permits deprivation of liberty only "in accordance with a procedure prescribed by law" for the purpose of bringing a person before a competent legal authority on reasonable suspicion of having committed an offense, or when it is reasonably considered necessary to prevent the commission of an offense or flight after having done so.

**Article 5(3) — Right to Trial Within Reasonable Time or Release:** "Everyone arrested or detained in accordance with the provisions of paragraph 1(c) of this Article shall be brought promptly before a judge or other officer authorised by law to exercise judicial power and shall be entitled to trial within a reasonable time or to release pending trial. Release may be conditioned by guarantees to appear for trial."

**Article 5(4) — Habeas Corpus:** "Everyone who is deprived of his liberty by arrest or detention shall be entitled to take proceedings by which the lawfulness of his detention shall be decided speedily by a court and his release ordered if the detention is not lawful."

**Article 5(5) — Right to Compensation:** "Everyone who has been the victim of arrest or detention in contravention of the provisions of this Article shall have an enforceable right to compensation."

### ECHR Jurisprudence — Grounds for Continued Detention

The European Court of Human Rights has established that continued pretrial detention is justified only where there are "relevant and sufficient" reasons falling within four recognized categories:

| Ground | Description | Leading Case |
|--------|-------------|--------------|
| Risk of absconding | Danger that the accused will fail to appear for trial | Stogmuller v. Austria (1969) |
| Interference with justice | Risk of pressure on witnesses or evidence tampering | Wemhoff v. Germany (1968) |
| Prevention of crime | Risk that the accused will commit further offenses | Matznetter v. Austria (1969) |
| Preservation of public order | Risk of public disturbance (applied restrictively) | Letellier v. France (1991) |

The Court has consistently held that the severity of the charges and the prospect of a heavy sentence cannot alone justify prolonged detention. As detention continues, the justification must become increasingly compelling.

### Landmark ECHR Cases

**Wemhoff v. Germany, Application No. 2122/64 (1968):** The first case in which the Court examined the reasonableness of pretrial detention duration under Article 5(3). Wemhoff was detained for over three years during investigation and trial for fraud and breach of trust. The Court found no violation, holding that the complexity of the case and the risk of flight justified the detention period, but established the analytical framework for all subsequent cases — that courts must examine whether the grounds for detention remained "relevant and sufficient" throughout the period of custody.

**Neumeister v. Austria, Application No. 1936/63 (1968):** The Court found its first violation of Article 5(3) in a pretrial detention case. Neumeister was detained for over two years on tax evasion charges. The Court held that Austrian courts had failed to adequately justify the continued detention, particularly given the availability of bail as an alternative. The Court established the principle that when assessing flight risk, authorities must consider whether bail or other guarantees could adequately mitigate the risk, rather than defaulting to continued custody.

**Letellier v. France, Application No. 12369/86 (1991):** The Court held that the danger of absconding cannot be gauged solely on the basis of the severity of the sentence faced and that courts must examine all relevant factors, including the accused's character, morals, home, occupation, assets, family ties, and connections to the country. The Court also established that the "preservation of public order" ground must be applied restrictively and can justify detention only in exceptional circumstances and for a limited period.

**Buzadji v. Moldova, Application No. 23755/07 (2016):** A Grand Chamber judgment that significantly strengthened protections for pretrial detainees. The Court held that from the very first judicial decision authorizing detention, the domestic court must provide relevant and sufficient reasons for detention and must consider alternative measures. The Court rejected the practice of ordering detention based on "stereotyped, abstract or inconsistent" reasoning, requiring instead that judges demonstrate how detention grounds apply concretely to the specific circumstances of each case.

**Idalov v. Russia, Application No. 5826/03 (2012):** The Grand Chamber reiterated that the persistence of reasonable suspicion is a sine qua non for the lawfulness of continued detention, but after a certain lapse of time it no longer suffices alone, and the Court must then establish whether the other grounds cited by the judicial authorities continue to justify the deprivation of liberty.

### EU Framework

**Directive 2016/343 on the Presumption of Innocence:** Adopted on 9 March 2016, this directive strengthens the presumption of innocence across EU member states. While it does not directly regulate pretrial detention, it provides that pre-trial decisions should not refer to the suspect as guilty. Preliminary decisions of a procedural nature (including detention orders) are exempted from the general prohibition on public references to guilt, but the competent authority must verify that sufficient elements of incriminating evidence exist before taking such decisions.

**EU Recommendation on Pretrial Detention (2022):** The European Commission issued a recommendation on procedural rights of suspects and accused persons subject to pretrial detention and on material detention conditions. It calls on member states to: ensure pretrial detention is used as a measure of last resort; provide for judicial review at reasonable intervals; guarantee access to a lawyer from the outset of detention; and ensure adequate material conditions of detention.

**Framework Decision 2009/829/JHA — European Supervision Order (ESO):** This Framework Decision allows for mutual recognition of supervision measures as an alternative to pretrial detention across EU member states. An accused person awaiting trial in one member state may be transferred to their home member state under supervision conditions, reducing the need for pretrial detention of non-residents.

**EU Directive 2013/48 — Right of Access to a Lawyer:** Guarantees the right of access to a lawyer in criminal proceedings and European arrest warrant proceedings, including from the earliest stages of detention, strengthening procedural safeguards for pretrial detainees.

## Comparative Analysis

### Philosophical Foundations

The four jurisdictions reflect distinct philosophical approaches to the tension between pretrial liberty and the state's interest in securing criminal proceedings:

**South Korea** reflects a historically prosecution-oriented system that has gradually shifted toward greater protection of suspects' rights through legislative reforms. The introduction of warrant hearings (yeongjangsiljilsimsa) in 1997 and pre-indictment bail in 2019 represent significant steps, but the system still grants prosecutors substantial authority during the investigation phase.

**The United States** occupies a unique position globally through its reliance on financial conditions of release. The cash bail system, combined with the commercial bail bond industry, creates a de facto wealth-based detention system that no other major democracy replicates. The Salerno decision's endorsement of preventive detention based on predicted dangerousness also represents a distinctive approach that has been criticized by international human rights bodies.

**Germany** exemplifies the continental European approach, treating pretrial detention as ultima ratio (last resort) and requiring courts to actively consider alternatives before ordering custody. The absence of a commercial bail industry and the emphasis on proportionality reflect a system designed to minimize pretrial incarceration.

**The ECHR framework** provides a supranational layer of protection that has driven reform across European jurisdictions, establishing minimum standards that individual states must meet regardless of their domestic legal traditions. The Court's insistence on concrete, case-specific reasoning has been particularly influential in combating "rubber stamp" detention orders.

### Key Divergences

**Financial Conditions:** The United States is the only jurisdiction among the four that relies heavily on cash bail as a primary release mechanism. South Korea uses bail money but as one of several conditions rather than as the default. Germany permits security deposits (Kaution) only as an alternative to detention under Section 116a StPO, and the ECHR requires that financial conditions be proportionate to the accused's means (Neumeister v. Austria).

**Duration Limits:** Germany imposes the most structured time limits (six months, extendable to twelve months with OLG approval). South Korea sets graduated limits by procedural stage. The United States lacks a fixed maximum for pretrial detention, relying instead on the Speedy Trial Act's indirect constraints. The ECHR applies a flexible "reasonable time" standard that has resulted in findings of violations for detention periods ranging from several months to several years depending on the circumstances.

**Proportionality:** Germany and the ECHR framework apply proportionality most explicitly, requiring that detention be proportionate to the likely sentence and that less restrictive alternatives be considered. The United States applies proportionality primarily through the Eighth Amendment's excessive bail prohibition but permits outright denial of bail for serious offenses. South Korea's proportionality analysis operates primarily through the discretionary bail provision of Article 96.

**Prosecution vs. Defense Balance:** South Korea requires the court to solicit the prosecutor's opinion before ruling on bail (Article 97), giving prosecutors formal influence over bail decisions. In the United States, the government bears the burden of proving that no conditions of release will suffice at federal detention hearings. Germany places the burden on the state to demonstrate that detention prerequisites are met and alternatives are insufficient. The ECHR requires that the prosecution demonstrate relevant and sufficient reasons for continued detention.

### Common Trends

Despite these divergences, several common trends are discernible across all four jurisdictions:

1. **Movement toward alternatives to custody:** All jurisdictions have expanded the range of non-custodial supervision measures, including electronic monitoring, reporting requirements, and travel restrictions.
2. **Strengthening defense rights:** Recent reforms in all jurisdictions have enhanced access to counsel during the pretrial phase, with Germany's 2019 mandatory defense counsel reform and the ECHR's consistent emphasis on the right to legal representation being notable examples.
3. **Judicial accountability:** Courts in all systems face increasing pressure to provide individualized, case-specific reasoning for detention decisions rather than relying on formulaic or categorical approaches.
4. **Scrutiny of disparate impact:** The bail reform movement in the United States and reform efforts in South Korea reflect growing awareness that pretrial detention systems can disproportionately affect marginalized populations.
