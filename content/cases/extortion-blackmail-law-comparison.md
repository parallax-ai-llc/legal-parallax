## Summary

Extortion and blackmail — the obtaining of property or advantages through coercion, threats, or intimidation — represent a category of criminal conduct that straddles the boundary between property offenses and crimes against personal autonomy. South Korea's Criminal Act (형법) addresses blackmail (공갈죄) under Articles 350 through 352, imposing imprisonment of up to 10 years for basic extortion and up to 15 years for special extortion involving groups or dangerous objects. The United States criminalizes extortion at the federal level primarily through the Hobbs Act (18 U.S.C. Section 1951), carrying up to 20 years imprisonment, and through 18 U.S.C. Section 873 for blackmail specifically, carrying up to 1 year. Germany's Strafgesetzbuch addresses Erpressung (extortion) under Section 253 with up to 5 years imprisonment for basic offenses, escalating through Section 255 (robbery by extortion) to a minimum of 1 year. The European Court of Human Rights, while lacking direct criminal jurisdiction, imposes positive obligations on member states to protect individuals from coercive crimes and to ensure effective investigation and fair trial rights under Articles 3, 4, 6, and 8 of the Convention.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

The Korean Criminal Act (형법) addresses blackmail and extortion (공갈죄) in Chapter XXXIX (Fraud and Blackmail), Articles 350 through 352. The statutory framework distinguishes between basic and aggravated forms:

| Article | Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Art. 350(1) | Basic Blackmail/Extortion (공갈) — obtaining property or property benefits by intimidating another person | Up to 10 years imprisonment or fine up to 20 million won |
| Art. 350(2) | Blackmail for Third Party — causing delivery to a third person by the same means | Same as Art. 350(1) |
| Art. 350-2 | Special Extortion (특수공갈) — extortion committed by displaying the force of a group or carrying a dangerous object | 1 to 15 years imprisonment |
| Art. 351 | Extortion by a Person in a Special Position (상습공갈) — habitual extortion | Enhanced penalties |
| Art. 352 | Attempted Extortion | Punishable under attempt provisions |

### Key Principles

**Intimidation (협박):** Korean courts define the intimidation element of extortion broadly. Unlike robbery (강도죄), which requires force sufficient to suppress the victim's resistance entirely, extortion requires only that the threat or intimidation be sufficient to restrict the victim's free will. The Supreme Court has held that the threat need not be explicit — implied threats and veiled language can satisfy this element when the surrounding circumstances make the coercive intent clear.

**Property or property benefits (재물 또는 재산상의 이익):** Article 350 extends beyond tangible property to include any economic advantage or benefit. This broad interpretation allows prosecution where the perpetrator obtains services, debt forgiveness, contractual advantages, or other intangible economic benefits through intimidation.

**Distinction from robbery:** The critical distinction between extortion (공갈) and robbery (강도) in Korean law lies in the degree of coercion. Robbery requires suppression of resistance through force or threat, while extortion requires only restriction of free will through intimidation. This doctrinal boundary is significant because robbery carries a minimum of 3 years imprisonment (Art. 333) while basic extortion has no mandatory minimum.

**Relationship to fraud:** Extortion and fraud are codified in the same chapter of the Criminal Act (Chapter XXXIX). The Korean Sentencing Commission treats extortion as a weightier offense than fraud. Joint extortion (organized group extortion) receives sentencing treatment equivalent to organizational fraud.

### Sentencing Trends

The Korean Sentencing Commission (양형위원회) has issued guidelines placing extortion in a severity tier above fraud. Courts have shown increasing willingness to apply Article 350-2 (special extortion) in cases involving organized crime groups (조직폭력배), reflecting the legislature's 2016 amendments that introduced this aggravated offense. Digital extortion — including ransomware and sextortion — has been prosecuted under the general extortion provisions supplemented by the Act on Promotion of Information and Communications Network Utilization and Information Protection.

## United States

### Federal Law

Two principal federal statutes address extortion and blackmail:

**The Hobbs Act (18 U.S.C. Section 1951):**
The Hobbs Act defines extortion as "the obtaining of property from another, with his consent, induced by wrongful use of actual or threatened force, violence, or fear, or under color of official right." Conviction carries imprisonment of up to 20 years, a fine, or both. The statute also criminalizes attempts and conspiracies to commit extortion affecting interstate or foreign commerce.

Elements of Hobbs Act extortion:
1. The defendant obtained or attempted to obtain property from another
2. With the victim's consent, induced by wrongful use of actual or threatened force, violence, or fear, or under color of official right
3. The conduct affected, delayed, or obstructed interstate or foreign commerce

**18 U.S.C. Section 873 (Blackmail):**
This narrower statute provides: "Whoever, under a threat of informing, or as a consideration for not informing, against any violation of any law of the United States, demands or receives any money or other valuable thing, shall be fined under this title or imprisoned not more than one year, or both." Section 873 carries the lightest sentence of all offenses in Chapter 41 (Extortion and Threats) of Title 18.

**Additional federal provisions:**
- 18 U.S.C. Section 871-880 (Chapter 41) covers various threat-based offenses
- 18 U.S.C. Section 1030 addresses computer-based extortion (ransomware)
- 18 U.S.C. Section 875 criminalizes interstate communications containing extortionate threats

### State Law

State extortion and blackmail statutes vary considerably:

**California (Penal Code Section 518-527):**
- Extortion defined as obtaining property or official action through force, fear, or threat
- Conviction carries 2, 3, or 4 years imprisonment (felony)
- Attempted extortion is a wobbler offense (misdemeanor or felony)
- Threat to expose a secret or to accuse of a crime constitutes extortion

**New York (Penal Law Article 155):**
- Grand larceny by extortion (Class C or D felony depending on value): up to 15 years
- Coercion in the first degree (Class D felony): up to 7 years
- Coercion in the second degree (Class A misdemeanor): up to 1 year

**Texas (Penal Code Section 31.02 combined with Section 1.07):**
- Theft by coercion is prosecuted as theft, with penalties scaling by value of property obtained
- Third-degree felony (value $30,000-$150,000): 2 to 10 years
- Second-degree felony (value $150,000-$300,000): 2 to 20 years

### Landmark Cases

**Evans v. United States, 504 U.S. 255 (1992):** The Supreme Court held that an affirmative act of inducement by a public official is not a required element of extortion "under color of official right" under the Hobbs Act. A public official who passively accepts a bribe can be convicted of extortion. This decision significantly expanded the reach of the Hobbs Act as a public corruption tool.

**Sekhar v. United States, 570 U.S. 729 (2013):** The Court unanimously held that attempting to compel a person to recommend an investment did not constitute extortion under the Hobbs Act because the recommendation of the General Counsel was not "obtainable property" that could be transferred from one person to another. Justice Scalia, writing for the majority, drew on the common-law tradition to constrain the meaning of "obtaining property."

**Scheidler v. National Organization for Women, Inc., 537 U.S. 393 (2003):** The Court held that the Hobbs Act requires that the defendant actually obtain or attempt to obtain property, and that merely interfering with or depriving another of property rights does not suffice. This narrowed the application of the Hobbs Act in protest-related cases.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

The German Criminal Code (Strafgesetzbuch, StGB) addresses extortion in Chapter Twenty (Robbery and Blackmail), Sections 253 through 256:

**Section 253 — Extortion (Erpressung):**
Whoever unlawfully, by force or by threat of serious harm (Drohung mit einem empfindlichen Ubel), coerces a person to do, acquiesce in, or refrain from an act, and thereby causes damage to the assets of that person or another for the purpose of wrongful personal enrichment or enrichment of a third party, incurs a penalty of imprisonment for a term not exceeding 5 years or a fine.

Elements:
1. Use of force or threat of serious harm (Gewalt oder Drohung mit einem empfindlichen Ubel)
2. Coercion of the victim to act, tolerate, or omit an action (Notigung zu einer Handlung, Duldung oder Unterlassung)
3. Resulting damage to the victim's or another's assets (Vermogensnachteil)
4. Intent to achieve wrongful enrichment (Bereicherungsabsicht)
5. Unlawfulness of the means-end relationship (Verwerflichkeit)

**Section 253(4) — Aggravated Cases:**
In particularly serious cases (besonders schwere Falle), the penalty is imprisonment from 1 to 15 years. A particularly serious case typically exists when the offender acts professionally (gewerbsmassig) or as a member of a gang (Bande).

**Section 255 — Robbery by Extortion (Rauberische Erpressung):**
Whoever commits extortion by force against a person or by threatening present danger to life or limb shall be punished as a robber under Section 249 StGB. This carries a minimum sentence of 1 year imprisonment, rising to a minimum of 3 or 5 years when aggravating circumstances under Section 250 apply.

### Key Principles

**Means-end unlawfulness (Verwerflichkeitspruefung):** German law requires not only that the threat itself be wrongful but that the connection between the threat and the demanded action be reprehensible. Threatening to file a legitimate criminal complaint to recover a genuine debt may not constitute extortion because the means-end relationship is not verwerflich (reprehensible), even though the threat is coercive. The Bundesgerichtshof has developed nuanced case law on this distinction.

**Distinction between Erpressung and Rauberische Erpressung:** The critical dividing line lies in the nature of the coercion. Simple extortion (Section 253) requires force or threat of serious harm. Robbery by extortion (Section 255) requires force against a person or threat of present danger to life or limb — a higher threshold that triggers robbery-level penalties.

**Sicherungserpressung doctrine:** The BGH has held that when a perpetrator uses force after obtaining property through fraud merely to retain the fraudulently acquired benefit, the offense constitutes fraud (Section 263) combined with coercion (Section 240), rather than extortion or robbery by extortion. This classification as Sicherungserpressung (securing extortion) carries lower penalties than robbery by extortion.

**Vermogensschaden (property damage):** German extortion law requires actual damage to the victim's assets as an element of the completed offense. Unlike some jurisdictions where the coercive act alone completes the crime, German law treats extortion as a result-offense requiring proof of financial harm.

### Notable Jurisprudence

The BGH has addressed the boundary between legitimate pressure in commercial disputes and criminal extortion. In cases where creditors threaten to expose debtors' conduct unless payment is made, the court applies the Verwerflichkeit test: if the exposure of the conduct bears a reasonable relationship to the claimed debt, the threat may be lawful. However, if the threat is disproportionate to the demand or unrelated to the underlying dispute, it constitutes criminal extortion.

German courts have also addressed digital extortion extensively, applying Section 253 to ransomware attacks and online sextortion schemes. The Bundesamt fuer Sicherheit in der Informationstechnik (BSI) has reported significant increases in cyber-extortion cases, leading to enhanced investigative cooperation under Europol's European Cybercrime Centre (EC3).

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Framework

The European Convention on Human Rights does not directly criminalize extortion or blackmail but establishes a rights framework that imposes obligations on member states in both protecting victims and ensuring fair treatment of the accused:

**Article 3 — Prohibition of Torture and Inhuman Treatment:**
States bear positive obligations to protect individuals from coercive criminal conduct that reaches the threshold of inhuman or degrading treatment. Where extortion involves sustained psychological pressure, threats of violence, or exploitation of vulnerability, the state's failure to prevent or investigate such conduct may engage Article 3 liability.

**Article 4 — Prohibition of Slavery and Forced Labour:**
The landmark case of Rantsev v. Cyprus and Russia (2010) established that human trafficking — which frequently involves extortion and coercion — falls within the scope of Article 4. The Court held that states of origin, transit, and destination all bear positive obligations to prevent trafficking, protect victims, and investigate allegations. This framework extends to extortion-based labor exploitation and debt bondage.

**Article 8 — Right to Respect for Private Life:**
Blackmail that involves threats to expose private information engages Article 8 protections. States must establish adequate legal frameworks to protect individuals against such interference with their private life, including effective criminal sanctions for blackmail. Where the treatment falls short of Article 3 severity, it may still constitute a violation of Article 8.

**Article 6 — Right to a Fair Trial:**
Persons accused of extortion are entitled to the full panoply of fair trial rights, including the presumption of innocence, the right to legal representation, the right to examine witnesses, and the right to trial within a reasonable time.

**Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 — Protection of Property:**
States must protect individuals from unlawful deprivation of their possessions by private actors, generating positive obligations to maintain effective criminal law frameworks against extortion.

### EU Legislative Framework

The European Union has adopted several instruments relevant to extortion and blackmail:

**Directive 2013/40/EU (Attacks Against Information Systems):**
This directive addresses cyber-dependent crime including ransomware and computer-based extortion. It requires member states to criminalize illegal access to information systems, illegal system interference, and illegal data interference, with maximum penalties of at least 2-5 years depending on the offense.

**EU Strategy to Tackle Organised Crime 2021-2025:**
This strategy identifies extortion as a core activity of organized crime groups operating within the EU and calls for enhanced cross-border investigative cooperation, asset recovery, and victim support. Europol's Internet Organised Crime Threat Assessment (IOCTA) has consistently flagged ransomware and cyber-extortion as top-tier threats.

**Directive 2012/29/EU (Victims' Rights Directive):**
This directive establishes minimum standards for the rights, support, and protection of victims of crime, including extortion victims. It requires member states to ensure victims receive adequate information, access to support services, and protection from secondary victimization.

### Landmark ECHR Cases

**Rantsev v. Cyprus and Russia (2010):** The Court found that both Cyprus and Russia had violated Article 4 in connection with the trafficking and death of a young Russian woman subjected to coercion and exploitation in Cyprus. The Court established that trafficking — which inherently involves extortionate control over victims — triggers positive obligations on all states in the trafficking chain to prevent, investigate, and prosecute.

**Gafgen v. Germany (2010):** While primarily addressing police interrogation methods, this Grand Chamber decision confirmed that evidence obtained through threats or coercion by state agents violates Article 3. The case is relevant to extortion law insofar as it reinforces the absolute prohibition on state-sponsored coercion, while affirming that independent evidence may sustain a conviction even when tainted evidence is excluded.

**Vinter v. United Kingdom (2013):** The Grand Chamber held that whole-life sentences without any prospect of review violate Article 3. This principle constrains member states that impose life sentences for aggravated extortion or robbery by extortion, requiring that some mechanism for sentence review must exist.

## Comparative Analysis

### Definitional Differences

The four jurisdictions reveal significant variation in how extortion and blackmail are conceptualized. South Korea treats extortion (공갈) as a species of property crime closely related to fraud, requiring intimidation that restricts free will. The United States distinguishes between extortion (obtaining property through threats) and blackmail (threatening to inform on someone), with distinct federal statutes for each. Germany requires a specific means-end relationship and applies a distinctive reprehensibility test (Verwerflichkeit) that may exempt some threatening behavior from criminal liability. The ECHR framework, lacking a criminal code, addresses extortion through the lens of human rights obligations — protection from coercion, privacy, property, and fair trial rights.

### Penalty Structures

Germany imposes the most lenient baseline penalties, with basic extortion capped at 5 years. South Korea occupies a middle position with up to 10 years for basic extortion. The United States imposes the most severe federal penalties, with Hobbs Act extortion carrying up to 20 years. However, Germany's robbery by extortion (Section 255) can produce sentences comparable to the US when aggravating circumstances under Section 250 apply (minimum 5 years for use of weapons).

### Public Official Extortion

The United States stands apart in its aggressive use of extortion statutes against public corruption. The Hobbs Act's "under color of official right" prong, as interpreted in Evans v. United States, allows prosecution of officials who passively accept bribes without any affirmative demand. South Korea and Germany address public official corruption through separate bribery statutes rather than through their extortion provisions.

### Digital Extortion

All jurisdictions have adapted their extortion frameworks to address cyber threats, though through different mechanisms. The US relies on 18 U.S.C. Section 1030 (Computer Fraud and Abuse Act) alongside traditional extortion statutes. Germany applies Section 253 StGB directly to digital conduct. South Korea supplements its Criminal Act provisions with the Information and Communications Network Act. The EU has harmonized minimum standards through Directive 2013/40/EU, while Europol's EC3 coordinates cross-border investigations into ransomware and cyber-extortion networks.
