## Summary

Bilateral tax treaties -- agreements between two sovereign states designed to prevent double taxation and fiscal evasion with respect to taxes on income and capital -- form the backbone of the international tax system, allocating taxing rights between source and residence states, reducing withholding tax rates on cross-border payments, and establishing mechanisms for resolving disputes between tax authorities, with the OECD Model Tax Convention and the UN Model Double Taxation Convention providing the predominant templates. South Korea maintains approximately 93 tax treaties (조세조약), negotiated and administered by the Ministry of Economy and Finance under the Framework Act on International Tax Coordination (국제조세조정에 관한 법률), generally following the OECD Model while incorporating provisions reflecting its position as both a capital-importing and capital-exporting nation, with notable treaties including the Korea-U.S. Tax Convention (1976, protocol 2006), the Korea-Germany Tax Treaty (2000), and treaties with major trading partners that typically reduce withholding tax rates to 10-15 percent on dividends, interest, and royalties. The United States maintains approximately 66 income tax treaties, negotiated by the Treasury Department under the authority of Article II of the Constitution, following the U.S. Model Income Tax Convention (last updated 2016) which departs from the OECD Model in significant respects including the "savings clause" preserving U.S. taxing jurisdiction over its citizens and residents regardless of treaty provisions, the "limitation on benefits" (LOB) article targeting treaty shopping, and generally lower withholding rates including zero percent on inter-company dividends meeting ownership thresholds. Germany has one of the world's most extensive tax treaty networks, with over 96 income tax treaties administered under the Abgabenordnung (AO) and interpreted through the lens of the OECD Model and its Commentary, with German courts according significant weight to the Commentary as an interpretive aid, and Germany's treaties typically following the OECD Model closely while reflecting its position as a major capital-exporting economy. At the multilateral level, the OECD/G20 Inclusive Framework on Base Erosion and Profit Shifting (BEPS) has fundamentally reshaped the treaty landscape through the Multilateral Convention to Implement Tax Treaty Related Measures (MLI, 2017), the Two-Pillar Solution (Pillar One on reallocation of taxing rights and Pillar Two establishing a 15 percent global minimum tax), and ongoing reforms to the mutual agreement procedure and mandatory binding arbitration.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's tax treaty practice is governed by the Framework Act on International Tax Coordination (국제조세조정에 관한 법률, ITCA), which provides the domestic legal basis for treaty implementation, transfer pricing rules, and mutual agreement procedures. Treaties are negotiated by the Ministry of Economy and Finance (기획재정부) and ratified with the consent of the National Assembly under Article 60 of the Constitution. Under Korean law, ratified treaties have the same effect as domestic legislation, though in cases of conflict the treaty prevails under the principle of lex specialis.

### Treaty Network and Policy

South Korea maintains approximately 93 tax treaties, covering all major trading partners. Key treaties include:

| Treaty Partner | Year (Latest Protocol) | Dividend WHT | Interest WHT | Royalty WHT |
|---------------|----------------------|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| United States | 1976 (Protocol 2006) | 10/15% | 12% | 10/15% |
| Japan | 1998 (Protocol 2013) | 5/15% | 10% | 10% |
| China | 1994 (Protocol 2006) | 5/10% | 10% | 10% |
| Germany | 2000 | 5/15% | 10% | 2/10% |
| United Kingdom | 1996 | 5/15% | 10% | 2/10% |
| Singapore | 2019 | 5/10% | 10% | 5% |

South Korea generally follows the OECD Model but departs in several important respects. Korean treaties frequently include a services permanent establishment provision (similar to the UN Model Article 5(3)(b)), deeming a PE to exist where services are furnished for more than 183 days within a 12-month period, reflecting Korea's interest in preserving source-state taxing rights on service income.

### Permanent Establishment

Korean treaty practice defines PE broadly. In addition to the standard fixed-place-of-business PE, Korean treaties typically include:

- **Construction PE:** 6-month or 12-month threshold (depending on treaty)
- **Services PE:** 183-day threshold within any 12-month period
- **Dependent agent PE:** Agent habitually exercising authority to conclude contracts

The Supreme Court (대법원) in Decision 2013Du7711 (2015) addressed the scope of the dependent agent PE, holding that an agent's activities in Korea constituted a PE where the agent regularly negotiated material terms of contracts even though formal execution occurred abroad.

### Transfer Pricing

The ITCA Articles 4-14 implement arm's length transfer pricing requirements consistent with the OECD Transfer Pricing Guidelines. Korea requires contemporaneous documentation (이전가격 자료) and has adopted Country-by-Country Reporting (CbCR) for multinational groups with consolidated revenue exceeding 100 billion KRW. The advance pricing agreement (APA) program is administered by the National Tax Service, with bilateral APAs available under treaty MAP articles.

### BEPS Implementation

South Korea ratified the MLI in 2020, modifying approximately 75 of its existing treaties. Korea adopted the Principal Purpose Test (PPT) as its anti-treaty-shopping measure under Article 7 of the MLI. Korea is also implementing Pillar Two through the Global Minimum Tax Act (글로벌최저한세법), effective for fiscal years beginning on or after January 1, 2024, applying to multinational groups with consolidated revenue exceeding EUR 750 million.

## United States

### Legal Framework

U.S. tax treaties are negotiated by the Treasury Department's Office of International Tax Counsel and ratified with the advice and consent of the Senate under Article II, Section 2 of the Constitution (two-thirds supermajority). The U.S. Model Income Tax Convention (last updated November 2016) serves as the starting negotiating position. Under the Internal Revenue Code Section 7852(d), treaties and statutes are given equal weight, with the later-in-time rule resolving conflicts, creating the possibility of treaty override by subsequent legislation.

### Distinctive Features of U.S. Treaty Practice

**Savings Clause (Article 1(4) of U.S. Model):** The United States reserves the right to tax its citizens and residents as if the treaty had not come into effect, subject to limited exceptions. This clause, unique to U.S. treaties, ensures that U.S. citizens abroad cannot use treaty provisions to reduce their U.S. tax liability, consistent with the U.S. practice of citizenship-based taxation.

**Limitation on Benefits (Article 22 of U.S. Model):** U.S. treaties contain comprehensive LOB provisions designed to prevent treaty shopping, requiring residents of a treaty partner to satisfy one of several objective tests (publicly traded, ownership/base erosion, active trade or business, derivative benefits, or discretionary determination by the competent authority) before claiming treaty benefits.

**Zero Withholding on Inter-Company Dividends:** Many U.S. treaties provide for zero percent withholding tax on dividends paid to a parent company holding 80 percent or more of the voting stock (compared to the OECD Model's 5 percent rate at a 25 percent threshold).

### Portfolio Interest Exemption

Under IRC Section 871(h) and 881(c), the United States domestically exempts portfolio interest from withholding tax regardless of treaty provisions, effectively providing zero-rate treatment for most cross-border debt instruments held by foreign investors (subject to a 10 percent related-party exclusion).

### Treaty Override and Constitutional Issues

The later-in-time doctrine permits Congress to override treaty obligations through subsequent legislation. Notable overrides include Section 884 (branch profits tax, enacted 1986) and FATCA (2010). In Cook v. United States, 288 U.S. 102 (1933), the Supreme Court established that treaty overrides must be clear and unambiguous, but subsequent practice has seen Congress override treaties with varying degrees of explicitness.

### U.S. and the MLI

The United States is notably absent from the Multilateral Instrument. The Treasury Department has taken the position that the U.S. treaty network already contains robust anti-abuse provisions (particularly the LOB) and that bilateral renegotiation is preferable to multilateral modification. This decision means that U.S. treaties are not automatically updated to reflect BEPS recommendations and must be renegotiated individually.

### Landmark Cases

**Boulez v. Commissioner, 83 T.C. 584 (1984):** The Tax Court examined whether payments to conductor Pierre Boulez constituted royalties or personal service income under the U.S.-Germany tax treaty, illustrating the importance of treaty characterization for determining withholding rates.

**National Westminster Bank, PLC v. United States, 512 F.3d 1347 (Fed. Cir. 2008):** The Federal Circuit addressed the interaction between treaty benefits and domestic provisions regarding branch profits tax, clarifying the relationship between the non-discrimination article and domestic anti-abuse rules.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's extensive tax treaty network is negotiated by the Federal Ministry of Finance (Bundesministerium der Finanzen) and ratified through federal legislation with Bundestag and Bundesrat approval under Article 59(2) of the Grundgesetz. Ratified treaties become domestic law through the transformation statute (Vertragsgesetz) and take precedence over ordinary domestic legislation under Section 2 of the Abgabenordnung (AO). German courts, particularly the Bundesfinanzhof (BFH), accord significant interpretive weight to the OECD Commentary, treating it as a key source for treaty interpretation.

### Treaty Network

Germany maintains over 96 income tax treaties, one of the most extensive networks globally. German treaty policy closely follows the OECD Model:

| Treaty Partner | Year (Latest Protocol) | Dividend WHT | Interest WHT | Royalty WHT |
|---------------|----------------------|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| United States | 1989 (Protocol 2006) | 0/5/15% | 0% | 0% |
| France | 1959 (Protocol 2015) | 0/5/15% | 0% | 0% |
| United Kingdom | 2010 (Protocol 2014) | 5/10% | 0% | 0% |
| China | 2014 | 5/10% | 10% | 10% |
| Japan | 2015 | 5/15% | 0% | 0% |
| South Korea | 2000 | 5/15% | 10% | 2/10% |

Germany's domestic law exempts interest and royalties paid to non-residents from withholding tax in most circumstances (no general withholding tax on interest; limited withholding on royalties under Section 50a EStG, typically reduced to zero under treaties). This means German treaty withholding rates on interest and royalties are often confirmatory rather than reductive.

### Permanent Establishment Approach

Germany adheres closely to the OECD PE definition in Article 5. The BFH has developed significant case law interpreting PE concepts:

**BFH Decision I R 75/09 (2013):** The court held that a foreign company's use of server infrastructure in Germany could constitute a PE if the company had effective control over the servers, applying the OECD Commentary's guidance on electronic commerce.

**BFH Decision I R 54/14 (2016):** Addressed the independent agent exception, holding that an agent with broad authority to negotiate and execute contracts in Germany constituted a dependent agent PE even though the agent also represented other principals.

### Treaty Abuse Prevention

Germany has implemented anti-treaty-shopping measures through:

- **Section 50d(3) EStG:** Denies treaty benefits to foreign entities that lack economic substance, requiring that the entity have adequate economic reasons for interposition, genuine economic activity, and not primarily serve to obtain the treaty benefit. This provision was reformed in 2012 following CJEU criticism in Deister Holding and Juhler Holding (C-504/16 and C-613/16, 2017).
- **MLI Implementation:** Germany ratified the MLI in 2021, adopting the PPT for most treaties and mandatory binding arbitration for certain treaty partners.

### EU Tax Dispute Resolution

Council Directive (EU) 2017/1852 on tax dispute resolution mechanisms in the European Union, implemented by Germany through the EU-Doppelbesteuerungsabkommen-Streitbeilegungsgesetz (EU-DBA-SBG), provides mandatory binding arbitration for unresolved MAP cases between EU member states, ensuring that double taxation is eliminated within defined timeframes (maximum 3 years for MAP, plus 6 months for arbitration).

### BEPS and Pillar Two Implementation

Germany implemented the Pillar Two global minimum tax through the Mindeststeuergesetz (MinStG, 2023), effective for fiscal years beginning on or after December 31, 2023, following the EU Minimum Tax Directive (2022/2523). The MinStG implements the Income Inclusion Rule (IIR), the Undertaxed Payments Rule (UTPR), and a Qualified Domestic Minimum Top-up Tax (QDMTT).

## ECHR & EU

### OECD Model Tax Convention

The OECD Model Tax Convention on Income and on Capital (2017, with subsequent updates) provides the predominant template for bilateral treaty negotiation. Key articles:

| Article | Subject | Allocation Rule |
|---------|---------|----------------|
| Art. 5 | Permanent Establishment | Defines PE threshold for source-state taxation of business profits |
| Art. 7 | Business Profits | Source state may tax only profits attributable to a PE |
| Art. 10 | Dividends | Source state limited to 5% (25%+ holding) or 15% WHT |
| Art. 11 | Interest | Source state limited to 10% WHT |
| Art. 12 | Royalties | Exclusive residence state taxation (0% source WHT) |
| Art. 13 | Capital Gains | Generally residence state; source state for immovable property and PE assets |
| Art. 23A/B | Elimination of Double Taxation | Exemption method (23A) or credit method (23B) |
| Art. 25 | Mutual Agreement Procedure | Competent authorities must resolve treaty disputes |
| Art. 26 | Exchange of Information | Automatic, spontaneous, and on-request exchange |

### BEPS Actions and Treaty Impact

The OECD/G20 BEPS Project (15 Actions, finalized 2015) has profoundly affected treaty practice:

- **Action 2 (Hybrid Mismatches):** Model treaty provisions neutralizing hybrid arrangements that exploit treaty residence rules
- **Action 6 (Treaty Abuse):** Minimum standard requiring either LOB, PPT, or both in all treaties
- **Action 7 (PE Avoidance):** Revised PE definition to address commissionnaire arrangements and artificial fragmentation of activities
- **Action 13 (Transfer Pricing Documentation):** Three-tiered documentation including CbCR
- **Action 14 (Dispute Resolution):** Minimum standard for effective MAP, with optional mandatory binding arbitration
- **Action 15 (MLI):** The Multilateral Convention to Implement Tax Treaty Related Measures, with 101 signatories as of 2024

### Multilateral Instrument (MLI)

The MLI (opened for signature June 7, 2017; in force July 1, 2018) allows jurisdictions to modify existing bilateral treaties simultaneously without bilateral renegotiation. As of 2024, 101 jurisdictions have signed the MLI. The MLI operates through "covered tax agreements" matched bilaterally -- both parties must list the same treaty and choose compatible options for modifications to take effect.

Key MLI provisions adopted most broadly:
- **Article 6 (Purpose of Covered Tax Agreement):** Preamble text on BEPS prevention
- **Article 7 (Prevention of Treaty Abuse):** PPT as minimum standard (most common adoption)
- **Article 16 (MAP):** Enhanced MAP provisions per BEPS Action 14

### Two-Pillar Solution

**Pillar One (Amount A):** Proposes reallocation of a portion of residual profits (25 percent of profit above 10 percent of revenue) of the largest and most profitable multinational enterprises (revenue over EUR 20 billion, profitability above 10 percent) to market jurisdictions. Implementation requires a new Multilateral Convention, which remains under negotiation as of early 2026.

**Pillar Two (Global Anti-Base Erosion Rules):** Establishes a 15 percent global minimum effective tax rate for multinational enterprises with consolidated revenue exceeding EUR 750 million. Implemented through the GloBE Rules (Model Rules, December 2021) and domestically through EU Directive 2022/2523 and national legislation. The rules operate through:
- Income Inclusion Rule (IIR): Top-up tax collected by the parent jurisdiction
- Undertaxed Payments Rule (UTPR): Backstop mechanism allocating top-up tax among jurisdictions
- Qualified Domestic Minimum Top-up Tax (QDMTT): Domestic top-up tax with priority over IIR/UTPR

### ECHR and Tax Treaty Disputes

**Stec v. United Kingdom (Application No. 65731/01, 2006):** While not directly a tax treaty case, the Grand Chamber's discussion of fiscal policy margin of appreciation has been applied to treaty benefit disputes.

**National & Provincial Building Society v. United Kingdom (Application No. 21319/93, 1997):** The Court upheld retroactive tax legislation enacted to close an unintended tax loophole, establishing that states have wide latitude in designing tax systems, relevant to treaty override situations.

## Comparative Analysis

### Treaty Philosophy

South Korea's treaty policy reflects its dual status as both a capital-importing and capital-exporting economy, maintaining moderate source-state withholding rates (typically 10-15 percent) that preserve some source taxation while encouraging foreign investment. The United States aggressively reduces source taxation (zero percent on inter-company dividends, portfolio interest) while protecting its taxing rights over citizens through the savings clause. Germany, as a net capital exporter, generally favors residence-state taxation consistent with the OECD Model's approach, though the domestic exemption of interest and royalties from withholding reduces the practical importance of treaty rate reductions.

### Anti-Treaty Shopping

The United States has been the global leader in anti-treaty-shopping measures, with comprehensive LOB provisions in treaties since the 1980s. Germany initially relied on domestic anti-avoidance provisions (Section 50d(3) EStG) but has joined the MLI and adopted the PPT. South Korea has adopted the PPT through the MLI and is developing domestic beneficial ownership requirements. The BEPS Action 6 minimum standard has converged practice globally, though the U.S. preference for objective LOB tests and the OECD/MLI preference for the subjective PPT represent distinct philosophical approaches.

### Dispute Resolution

The EU Directive 2017/1852 provides Germany with the most robust dispute resolution framework among the three jurisdictions, guaranteeing mandatory binding arbitration for intra-EU disputes. The United States includes mandatory arbitration in several recent treaties (e.g., U.S.-Germany Protocol 2006, U.S.-Canada Protocol 2007) but not universally. South Korea participates in MAP but has not adopted mandatory arbitration, relying on bilateral negotiations to resolve disputes.

### MLI Engagement

Germany and South Korea have both ratified the MLI, modifying the majority of their existing treaty networks. The U.S. refusal to sign the MLI is a significant outlier, reflecting concerns about the subjective nature of the PPT, the adequacy of existing U.S. LOB provisions, and constitutional considerations regarding treaty modification without Senate advice and consent for each covered agreement.

### Pillar Two Implementation Speed

All three jurisdictions have moved to implement Pillar Two, but at different speeds. South Korea enacted the Global Minimum Tax Act effective January 1, 2024. Germany implemented through the Mindeststeuergesetz with a similar effective date. The United States has not enacted Pillar Two implementing legislation, though the existing GILTI regime imposes a minimum tax on foreign income that partially overlaps with Pillar Two objectives. The question of whether GILTI qualifies as a "qualified IIR" under the GloBE rules remains contested.
