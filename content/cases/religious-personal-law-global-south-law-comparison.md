## Summary

The relationship between religious law and state law varies dramatically across the Global South, ranging from rigorous state secularism that suppresses religious law to full incorporation of religious legal systems into state judicial architecture. China's socialist legal system enforces strict state control over religion through the Religious Affairs Regulations (2017, revised 2018) and the principle of "sinicization of religion" (zongjiao zhongguohua), treating religious practice as a matter of state administration rather than personal law. India presents the world's most complex religious personal law system, with separate Hindu, Muslim, Christian, and Parsi codes governing marriage, divorce, succession, and adoption -- a system under sustained pressure from the constitutional directive for a Uniform Civil Code (Article 44) and the Supreme Court's landmark Shah Bano (1985) and Shayara Bano (2017) decisions. Nigeria operates the most institutionalized dual religious law system in the Global South, with twelve northern states having adopted expanded Sharia criminal codes between 1999 and 2003, operating alongside Sharia Courts of Appeal recognized in the 1999 Constitution. South Africa's Constitution protects religious freedom (Section 15) while the Recognition of Customary Marriages Act (1998) and the Muslim Marriages Bill (debated since 2003, not yet enacted) illustrate the tensions between religious plurality and constitutional equality. Brazil's 1988 Constitution establishes a secular state (Article 19) while guaranteeing religious freedom (Article 5(VI)), with the growing political influence of evangelical Christianity creating new tensions around LGBTQ+ rights and reproductive law. Indonesia's Pancasila state ideology mandates belief in "the One and Only God" (Ketuhanan Yang Maha Esa) and operates a system of Religious Courts (Pengadilan Agama) for Muslim personal law under Law No. 7 of 1989, while the Philippines maintains the Code of Muslim Personal Laws (Presidential Decree No. 1083, 1977) and Sharia courts, alongside the comprehensive Bangsamoro Organic Law (Republic Act No. 11054, 2018) establishing autonomous Muslim governance in Mindanao.

## China

### State Control over Religion

China's approach to religious law is fundamentally different from all other Global South jurisdictions: rather than accommodating religious legal norms within the state system, the party-state asserts comprehensive control over religious practice itself. The constitutional framework is paradoxical -- Article 36 of the Constitution declares that citizens "enjoy freedom of religious belief," yet the same article provides that "no one may make use of religion to engage in activities that disrupt public order, impair the health of citizens or interfere with the educational system of the state" and that "religious bodies and religious affairs are not subject to any foreign domination."

The **Religious Affairs Regulations** (宗教事务条例), first issued by the State Council in 2004 (Decree No. 426) and substantially revised in 2017 (Decree No. 686, effective February 1, 2018), constitute the primary regulatory framework. Key provisions include:

- **Article 5:** The registration requirement -- all religious organizations (zongjiao tuanti) must register with the civil affairs department; religious venues (zongjiao huodong changsuo) require approval from the religious affairs department.
- **Article 41:** Internet religious information services require permits from provincial-level religious affairs departments and internet information offices.
- **Article 44:** Prohibition on establishing religious schools outside the state system; religious education of minors is restricted.
- **Articles 68-74:** Penalties including fines and facility closure for unauthorized religious activities.

### Sinicization of Religion

Since 2015, the Communist Party has promoted the "sinicization of religion" (宗教中国化) policy, requiring all recognized religions (Buddhism, Taoism, Islam, Catholicism, Protestantism) to adapt their practices, interpretations, and governance to "Chinese characteristics" and "socialist core values." This policy has been implemented through:

- The Five-Year Working Plan for the Sinicization of Christianity (2018-2022), requiring churches to incorporate Chinese cultural elements and socialist values into theology and practice.
- The "de-Arabization" and "de-Saudization" of mosques in Ningxia and other Muslim-majority areas, including removal of Arabic-script signage, domes, and minarets.
- The Xinjiang De-extremification Regulation (2017), which defines as "extremist" a range of Islamic practices including wearing face veils, growing "abnormal" beards, refusing to watch state television, and "interfering with" family planning.

### Religious Marriage and Personal Status

Unlike India, Nigeria, or Indonesia, China does not maintain religious personal law systems. The Civil Code (2020), Book V (Marriage and Family), applies uniformly to all citizens regardless of religious belief. The Marriage Registration Regulations (2003, revised 2021) require civil registration as the sole legal basis for marriage -- religious ceremonies have no legal effect. However, the Regional Ethnic Autonomy Law allows certain accommodations: several ethnic autonomous areas permit marriages at ages below the national minimum (e.g., Xinjiang and Tibet have historically permitted marriage at 18 for males and 16 for females), and some provisions accommodate minority marriage customs such as polyandry among certain Tibetan communities (though this exists in a legal gray area).

## India

### The Personal Law System

India operates the world's most elaborate system of religion-based personal law, governing marriage, divorce, maintenance, adoption, guardianship, and succession through separate legislative codes for each major religious community:

**Hindu Personal Law** (applicable to Hindus, Buddhists, Jains, and Sikhs):
- Hindu Marriage Act, 1955 (regulating marriage, divorce, restitution of conjugal rights)
- Hindu Succession Act, 1956 (as amended 2005 to give daughters equal coparcenary rights)
- Hindu Minority and Guardianship Act, 1956
- Hindu Adoptions and Maintenance Act, 1956

**Muslim Personal Law:**
- Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act, 1937 (applying Islamic personal law to Muslims in matters of succession, marriage, dissolution, and maintenance)
- Dissolution of Muslim Marriages Act, 1939 (granting Muslim women the right to seek divorce)
- Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Marriage) Act, 2019 (criminalizing triple talaq)

**Christian Personal Law:**
- Indian Christian Marriage Act, 1872
- Indian Divorce Act, 1869 (as amended)

**Parsi Personal Law:**
- Parsi Marriage and Divorce Act, 1936

**Secular/General:**
- Special Marriage Act, 1954 (permitting civil marriage regardless of religion)
- Indian Succession Act, 1925 (applicable to Christians and Parsis; Muslims and Hindus governed by separate succession laws)

### The Shah Bano Case and Its Aftermath

The most consequential religious personal law case in Indian history is **Mohd. Ahmed Khan v. Shah Bano Begum (1985 AIR SC 945)**. Shah Bano, a 62-year-old Muslim woman, was divorced by her husband through triple talaq after 40 years of marriage and sought maintenance under Section 125 of the Code of Criminal Procedure (CrPC), which provides maintenance for wives, children, and parents regardless of religion.

The Supreme Court, in a judgment by Chief Justice Y.V. Chandrachud, held that Section 125 CrPC applies to all women regardless of religion and that Shah Bano was entitled to maintenance. The Court went further, endorsing the enactment of a Uniform Civil Code under Article 44: "It is a matter of regret that Article 44 of our Constitution has remained a dead letter... A common civil code will help the cause of national integration."

The ruling provoked massive opposition from Muslim community leaders who viewed it as interference in Islamic personal law. The Rajiv Gandhi government, responding to political pressure, enacted the **Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Divorce) Act, 1986**, which effectively reversed Shah Bano by requiring divorced Muslim women to seek maintenance from relatives or the Wakf Board rather than from the former husband after the iddat period. In **Danial Latifi v. Union of India (2001 (7) SCC 740)**, the Supreme Court reinterpreted the 1986 Act to require that reasonable and fair maintenance be provided during the iddat period in a manner that essentially covers the woman's future needs, partially restoring Shah Bano's principle.

### Triple Talaq: Shayara Bano v. Union of India (2017)

In **Shayara Bano v. Union of India (2017 (9) SCC 1)**, a five-judge Constitution Bench struck down the practice of instant triple talaq (talaq-e-biddat) by a 3:2 majority. The majority held that instant triple talaq was not an integral part of Islamic religious practice entitled to protection under Article 25 (freedom of religion) and that it was "manifestly arbitrary" under Article 14. Following the decision, Parliament enacted the **Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Marriage) Act, 2019**, criminalizing the pronouncement of triple talaq with imprisonment of up to three years.

### The Uniform Civil Code Debate

Article 44 of the Constitution (Directive Principles of State Policy) directs: "The State shall endeavour to secure for the citizens a uniform civil code throughout the territory of India." While non-justiciable, this directive has been invoked repeatedly by the Supreme Court:

- **Sarla Mudgal v. Union of India (1995 (3) SCC 635):** The Court urged the government to enact a UCC, holding that conversion to Islam solely to contract a second marriage without dissolving the first constituted bigamy.
- **Jose Paulo Coutinho v. Maria Luiza Valentina Pereira (2019 (16) SCC 479):** The Court praised Goa's common civil code (inherited from the Portuguese Civil Code) as a model for a national UCC.

In February 2024, the state of Uttarakhand became the first to enact a Uniform Civil Code (Uttarakhand Uniform Civil Code Act, 2024), replacing personal law-based marriage, divorce, and inheritance rules with a uniform secular code for all residents, while exempting Scheduled Tribes. The Uttarakhand UCC serves as a potential pilot for national legislation, though its constitutionality may face challenges given the concurrent jurisdiction over personal law matters.

## Africa

### Nigeria: Sharia Implementation

Nigeria's 1999 Constitution establishes a secular federation but provides for Sharia Courts of Appeal in states that desire them (Section 275). Between 1999 and 2003, twelve northern states -- Zamfara (first, October 1999), Kano, Sokoto, Kebbi, Niger, Katsina, Kaduna (partially), Jigawa, Yobe, Borno, Bauchi, and Gombe -- enacted expanded Sharia criminal codes, applying Islamic criminal law (hudud) including penalties such as amputation for theft and stoning for adultery.

The implementation generated international attention through several high-profile cases:

- **Safiya Hussaini Tungar-Tudu (2002):** Convicted of zina (adultery) and sentenced to death by stoning by a Sharia court in Sokoto State. The sentence was overturned on appeal by the Sokoto Sharia Court of Appeal on procedural grounds (the pregnancy that served as evidence occurred before the Sharia code took effect).

- **Amina Lawal (2002-2003):** Convicted of zina and sentenced to stoning by a Sharia court in Katsina State. The Katsina Sharia Court of Appeal overturned the conviction in September 2003, finding procedural defects including the failure to allow a sleeping pregnancy (raqqada) defense recognized in Maliki fiqh.

The constitutional validity of the expanded Sharia codes has never been definitively resolved by the Supreme Court of Nigeria. The core question -- whether state adoption of Sharia criminal law violates Section 10 ("The Government of the Federation or of a State shall not adopt any religion as State Religion") -- remains untested because affected individuals have obtained relief at the state appellate level, preventing the issue from reaching the Supreme Court.

### Sharia Personal Law in Nigeria

Pre-dating the 1999 Sharia expansion, Islamic personal law (family law) has applied in northern Nigeria since the colonial era, administered through Area Courts and the Sharia Courts of Appeal. The Constitution (Section 262) establishes the Sharia Court of Appeal of the FCT and (Section 275) Sharia Courts of Appeal of the states, with jurisdiction over "questions of Islamic personal law regarding a marriage concluded in accordance with that law; family relationship of a person under Islamic law; and any question regarding a wakf, gift, will or succession under Islamic law."

The Grand Khadi of each state's Sharia Court of Appeal must be learned in Islamic personal law and qualified as a legal practitioner (Section 276). Appeals from the Sharia Court of Appeal lie to the Court of Appeal (Section 277) and ultimately the Supreme Court, though in practice the Supreme Court has heard very few Sharia personal law cases.

### South Africa: Religious Pluralism without Religious Courts

South Africa's Constitution (Section 15) protects freedom of religion, belief, and opinion, while Section 15(3)(a) permits legislation recognizing "marriages concluded under any tradition, or a system of religious, personal or family law." The Recognition of Customary Marriages Act 120 of 1998 gave legal recognition to marriages concluded under African customary law, including polygynous marriages, subject to constitutional rights.

However, Muslim marriages remain unrecognized by statute, despite decades of advocacy. The South African Law Reform Commission's Project 59 produced the Muslim Marriages Bill, first introduced in 2003, which would recognize Muslim marriages (including polygynous marriages subject to conditions), provide for Muslim matrimonial property regimes, and establish a Judicial Commission to address Islamic law matters. The Bill has stalled repeatedly due to disagreements among Muslim communities, feminist organizations, and conservative religious groups.

In the absence of legislation, courts have progressively extended protection to Muslim marriages:

- **Amod v. Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accidents Fund (1999 (4) SA 1319 (SCA)):** The Supreme Court of Appeal recognized a surviving spouse of an Islamic marriage as a "dependant" for purposes of wrongful death claims.
- **Daniels v. Campbell NO (2004 (5) SA 331 (CC)):** The Constitutional Court held that surviving spouses of monogamous Muslim marriages qualify as "survivors" under the Intestate Succession Act and "spouses" under the Maintenance of Surviving Spouses Act.
- **Hassam v. Jacobs NO (2009 (5) SA 572 (CC)):** The Court extended Daniels to surviving spouses of polygynous Muslim marriages.

## Latin America

### Brazil: Laicidade and Evangelical Influence

Brazil's Constitution establishes a secular state (Estado laico) through Article 19(I), which prohibits the Union, states, and municipalities from establishing religious cults, subsidizing them, or maintaining relationships of dependence or alliance with them. Article 5(VI) guarantees freedom of conscience and belief, ensuring "the free exercise of religious worship and guaranteeing, in the form of the law, the protection of places of worship and their liturgies." Article 5(VIII) provides that no person shall be deprived of rights by reason of religious belief unless invoked to exempt from legal obligation and no alternative is offered.

Brazil has never operated a system of religious personal law; the Civil Code of 2002 (Lei No. 10.406) applies uniformly to all citizens in matters of marriage, divorce, succession, and family law. Religious marriage (casamento religioso) may have civil effect if registered (Civil Code, Article 1.515-1.516), but the underlying legal rules are secular.

However, the dramatic growth of evangelical Christianity (approximately 31% of the population as of 2023, up from 15% in 2000) has created new tensions around legislation affecting gender and sexuality:

- The **Estatuto da Familia (Family Statute, PL 6583/2013):** Proposed legislation defining the family unit as "the union between a man and a woman," directly challenging the STF's 2011 decision in ADPF 132 and ADI 4277 recognizing same-sex unions.
- **Abortion restrictions:** Evangelical legislative caucuses have consistently blocked abortion law reform, with PL 1904/2024 (the "Anti-Abortion PL") seeking to equate late-term abortions performed after 22 weeks (including for victims of rape) with homicide.
- **Religious education in public schools:** The STF in ADI 4439 (2017) ruled 6-5 that confessional (denominational) religious education in public schools is constitutional, reversing the expectation of strictly non-confessional instruction.

### Colombia: From Catholic Concordat to Secular Pluralism

Colombia's relationship with religious law has undergone dramatic transformation. The 1887 Concordat with the Holy See granted the Catholic Church jurisdiction over marriage, education, and civil registration, effectively creating a Catholic personal law system. The 1991 Constitution established a secular state (Article 19: freedom of religion and worship) and the Constitutional Court progressively dismantled Catholic legal privileges:

- **Sentencia C-027/1993:** The Court struck down several provisions of the Concordat, including Catholic jurisdiction over marriage dissolution.
- **Sentencia C-577/2011 and SU-214/2016:** The Court recognized same-sex couples' right to form a family unit and ultimately to marry, overriding Catholic institutional objections.

## Southeast Asia

### Indonesia: Pancasila and Religious Courts

Indonesia's state ideology, Pancasila (Five Principles), establishes "Belief in the One and Only God" (Ketuhanan Yang Maha Esa) as its first principle, creating a unique "religious state" that is neither secular nor theocratic. The Constitution (Article 29) provides: "(1) The State is based upon the belief in the One and Only God. (2) The State guarantees all persons the freedom of worship, each according to his/her own religion or belief."

This framework has been interpreted to require all citizens to profess one of six officially recognized religions: Islam, Protestantism, Catholicism, Hinduism, Buddhism, and Confucianism (restored as a recognized religion in 2006 under Presidential Decree No. 6 of 2000). Citizens must declare a religion on their identity cards (KTP), and the Constitutional Court in Decision No. 97/PUU-XIV/2016 struck down the obligation to choose one of the six recognized religions for the KTP, allowing adherents of local faiths (aliran kepercayaan) to leave the religion field blank. However, the practical consequences of this decision remain limited.

### Religious Courts System

Indonesia operates a parallel Religious Courts (Pengadilan Agama) system for Muslim personal law, established by Law No. 7 of 1989 on Religious Courts (as amended by Law No. 3 of 2006 and Law No. 50 of 2009). The Religious Courts have jurisdiction over:

- Marriage (perkawinan) -- including divorce, reconciliation, and polygamy approval
- Inheritance (waris) -- Islamic succession law (faraid)
- Waqf (endowment) administration
- Zakat (charitable donation) disputes
- Sharia economic disputes (added by the 2006 amendment)

The Religious Courts operate at three levels: Religious Courts of First Instance (Pengadilan Agama), Religious High Courts (Pengadilan Tinggi Agama), and the Religious Chamber of the Supreme Court (Kamar Agama Mahkamah Agung). As of 2024, there were 368 Religious Courts of First Instance and 30 Religious High Courts.

The **Compilation of Islamic Law (Kompilasi Hukum Islam, KHI)**, promulgated through Presidential Instruction No. 1 of 1991, serves as the primary reference for Religious Courts in personal status matters. The KHI covers three books: Marriage (Book I), Inheritance (Book II), and Waqf (Book III). It represents a compromise between classical Islamic jurisprudence (predominantly Shafi'i school) and Indonesian legal norms, including provisions that deviate from classical fiqh (e.g., the requirement of court-approved divorce rather than unilateral talaq, and restrictions on polygamy requiring judicial permission).

### Marriage Law 1974

Indonesia's Marriage Law (Law No. 1 of 1974) applies to all citizens but integrates religious requirements. Article 2(1) provides: "Marriage is legal if it has been performed according to the laws of the respective religions and beliefs of the parties concerned." Article 2(2) requires registration. The combination of these provisions effectively delegates the substantive validity of marriage to religious law while requiring state registration for legal effect.

This framework creates significant challenges for interfaith marriages, which no recognized religion explicitly sanctions. The Supreme Court Circular Letter No. 2 of 2023 attempted to address this by permitting Civil Registry Offices to register interfaith marriages based on a court determination, but this was criticized by Islamic organizations including the Majelis Ulama Indonesia (MUI). The Constitutional Court in Decision No. 68/PUU-XII/2014 rejected a challenge to Article 2(1), maintaining the religious validity requirement.

### Blasphemy Law

Indonesia's blasphemy law (Article 156a of the Criminal Code, derived from Presidential Decree No. 1/PNPS/1965 on the Prevention of Misuse and/or Defamation of Religion, upheld by the Constitutional Court in Decision No. 140/PUU-VII/2009) has been used against religious minorities and individuals accused of insulting recognized religions. The most prominent prosecution was that of **Basuki Tjahaja Purnama ("Ahok")**, the Chinese-Christian Governor of Jakarta, convicted in May 2017 of blasphemy against Islam and sentenced to two years' imprisonment for remarks about Quran verse Al-Ma'idah 51 during a campaign speech.

### Philippines: Muslim Personal Law and Bangsamoro Autonomy

The Philippines maintains a dual system for Muslim Filipinos through:

**Code of Muslim Personal Laws (Presidential Decree No. 1083, 1977):** Governs marriage, divorce, inheritance, and family relations among Muslims. Key provisions include:
- Recognition of Muslim marriages solemnized according to Islamic rites (Article 14)
- Allowance of polygamy (up to four wives) subject to judicial permission and demonstration of ability to provide equally (Articles 27-28)
- Recognition of talaq (divorce by husband), khul' (divorce at wife's request), and other Islamic divorce forms (Articles 45-54)
- Islamic inheritance law (faraid) applying to the estate of Muslim decedents (Articles 89-141)

**Sharia Courts:** The Code establishes Sharia District Courts and Sharia Circuit Courts with jurisdiction over personal status and commercial disputes among Muslims. Sharia District Courts are presided over by Sharia judges (jurisconsults) who must be natural-born Filipino Muslims and members of the Philippine bar. As of 2024, five Sharia District Courts and 51 Sharia Circuit Courts operate in Mindanao and Metro Manila.

**Bangsamoro Organic Law (Republic Act No. 11054, 2018):** Established the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao (BARMM) following the 2014 Comprehensive Agreement on the Bangsamoro between the Philippine government and the Moro Islamic Liberation Front (MILF). The BARMM has authority to enact legislation in a range of areas including Sharia law and Islamic studies, customary law of indigenous peoples, and aspects of land management. The Bangsamoro Parliament (established 2019) has legislative authority over personal, family, and property law applicable to Bangsamoro people within the autonomous region.

## Comparative Analysis

### The Secularism Spectrum

Global South states occupy a wide spectrum of state-religion relations:

1. **State Atheism/Control (China):** The party-state asserts authority over religion itself, prohibiting religious legal norms from operating within the state system. No religious personal law exists; the secular state legal code applies uniformly.

2. **Religious Pluralist Secularism (India):** The state is secular (non-establishment) but actively accommodates and administers multiple religious personal law systems through state courts. The state does not endorse any religion but gives legal force to religious norms in personal matters.

3. **Constitutional Secularism with Religious Tensions (Brazil, South Africa):** Formally secular states with uniform civil law, but religious interests increasingly shape legislation (Brazil's evangelical caucus) or remain unaccommodated (South Africa's Muslim marriage recognition gap).

4. **Religious State without Theocracy (Indonesia):** The state mandates religious belief, operates religious courts, and integrates religious law into civil law (marriage), but maintains a pluralistic approach across six recognized religions.

5. **Dual-Track Systems (Nigeria, Philippines):** State courts and religious/customary courts operate in parallel, with varying degrees of integration and conflict. Nigeria's Sharia criminal law expansion pushes the boundary of secular federalism; the Philippines' BARMM creates autonomous Muslim governance.

### Gender Equality as a Cross-Cutting Challenge

Across all five regions, religious personal law systems disproportionately affect women:

- **India's** triple talaq practice (abolished 2017/2019) and the Shah Bano controversy illustrate how religious personal law can deprive women of maintenance rights.
- **Nigeria's** Sharia criminal codes have been applied primarily against women in zina cases (where pregnancy serves as evidence of extramarital sex but men's conduct is harder to prove).
- **Indonesia's** polygamy provisions, though requiring judicial approval, remain a contested issue among women's rights organizations.
- **The Philippines'** Code of Muslim Personal Laws permits polygamy without equivalent protections for first wives' property rights.
- **South Africa's** failure to enact the Muslim Marriages Bill leaves women in Islamic marriages without full legal protection.

### Blasphemy and Religious Dissent

The treatment of blasphemy and religious dissent reveals stark differences in how Global South states mediate between religious sensibilities and freedom of expression. Indonesia's blasphemy law (Article 156a), Pakistan's notorious Section 295-C, and Nigeria's Sharia codes all criminalize religious insult. Brazil's Constitution protects religious freedom without blasphemy restrictions. India's Section 295A of the Indian Penal Code (now Section 299 of the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita, 2023) criminalizes "deliberate and malicious acts intended to outrage religious feelings," though its application has been debated.
