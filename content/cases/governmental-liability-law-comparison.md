## Summary

Governmental liability -- the legal responsibility of the state and its organs for harm caused to individuals through wrongful acts, omissions, or maladministration -- represents a fundamental dimension of the rule of law, reflecting the principle that the state is not above the law and that individuals harmed by governmental action are entitled to effective remedies. The evolution from absolute sovereign immunity to modern state liability regimes constitutes one of the most significant developments in public law across all major legal systems. South Korea establishes governmental liability through the State Compensation Act (국가배상법), which provides compensation for damages caused by the intentional or negligent acts of public officials in the exercise of their duties (Article 2) and for defects in the construction or management of public facilities (Article 5); claims are adjudicated by the courts with a mandatory pre-litigation deliberation by the State Compensation Council (국가배상심의회) or District State Compensation Council, and the Constitutional Court (헌법재판소) has played a significant role in expanding governmental liability through fundamental rights adjudication. The United States waived sovereign immunity through the Federal Tort Claims Act (FTCA, 28 U.S.C. Sections 1346(b), 2671-2680), which permits tort claims against the federal government for the negligent or wrongful acts of government employees acting within the scope of their employment, subject to significant exceptions including the discretionary function exception (Section 2680(a)), the intentional tort exception, and the foreign country exception; Section 1983 (42 U.S.C. Section 1983) provides a separate cause of action against state and local officials for deprivation of constitutional rights, with qualified immunity serving as the primary defense. Germany provides one of the most comprehensive governmental liability regimes through Amtshaftung (official liability) under Section 839 of the Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB) read in conjunction with Article 34 of the Grundgesetz (Basic Law), which transfers liability from the individual official to the state; additionally, the Enteignungsgleicher Eingriff (expropriation-equivalent interference) and the Aufopferungsanspruch (sacrifice claim) provide compensation for lawful but disproportionately burdensome state action, with the Bundesgerichtshof (BGH) having developed a rich body of case law. The ECHR/EU framework provides multiple layers of state liability: Article 41 ECHR provides just satisfaction for Convention violations, Article 13 guarantees effective domestic remedies, and EU law establishes the Francovich principle of member state liability for breaches of EU law (Francovich v. Italy, Joined Cases C-6/90 and C-9/90, 1991), as further developed in Brasserie du Pecheur/Factortame III (Joined Cases C-46/93 and C-48/93, 1996).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's governmental liability regime reflects the constitutional commitment to the rule of law and the protection of individual rights against state power, with roots in both the civil law tradition and Korean constitutional jurisprudence.

**Constitutional Basis:** Article 29(1) of the Constitution of the Republic of Korea (대한민국 헌법) provides: "In case a person has sustained damages by an unlawful act committed by a public official in the course of official duties, he may claim just compensation from the State or public organization under the conditions as prescribed by Act." This constitutional guarantee forms the foundation of the State Compensation Act.

**State Compensation Act (국가배상법):** Enacted in 1951 and substantially revised in 1967 and 2009, the Act establishes two primary bases of governmental liability:

**Article 2 (Liability for Unlawful Acts of Officials):** The state or a local government shall compensate for damages caused to any person by the intentional or negligent unlawful act of a public official in the exercise of their official duties. "Public official" is interpreted broadly to include all persons exercising public authority, including civil servants, military personnel, police officers, prosecutors, judges, and persons entrusted with public functions.

**Article 5 (Liability for Facility Defects):** The state or local government shall compensate for damages caused by defects in the construction or management of roads, rivers, and other public facilities. This strict liability provision does not require proof of fault, only a defect in the facility and a causal connection to the damages.

### Fault and Unlawfulness

The Article 2 liability requires both fault (intent or negligence) and unlawfulness (위법성). The Supreme Court (대법원) has developed a dual standard for unlawfulness:

**Conduct Standard (행위불법론):** The traditional approach examines whether the official's specific conduct violated applicable laws, regulations, or established procedures.

**Result Standard (결과불법론):** The more expansive approach, increasingly applied by the courts, examines whether the result of the official's action violates the plaintiff's protected rights, even if the specific procedural requirements were technically followed. In Supreme Court Decision 2008Da38288 (2010), the Court applied the result standard to find governmental liability for environmental damage caused by inadequate regulatory enforcement, even though the officials had technically complied with existing procedures.

### Military and Police Liability

The State Compensation Act's application to military and police contexts has been a significant area of development:

**Article 2(1) Proviso (Military Personnel):** The Act originally contained a proviso restricting compensation claims by military personnel and police officers for injuries sustained in the line of duty, directing them instead to the Military Pension Act (군인연금법) or similar benefit schemes. The Constitutional Court has narrowed this restriction significantly, holding in Constitutional Court Decision 2008Hun-Ba96 (2011) that the restriction violates the Constitution when applied to deny compensation for injuries caused by the gross negligence or intentional misconduct of superior officers.

### Pre-Litigation Deliberation

**State Compensation Council (국가배상심의회):** Before filing a court action, claimants must submit their claims to the State Compensation Council (at the national level) or District State Compensation Council (at the district level) for deliberation. The Council, composed of government officials and private members, reviews the claim and attempts to reach a settlement. If the claimant accepts the Council's decision, the settlement is binding. If the claimant rejects the decision, they may proceed to file a court action. The deliberation process typically takes 2-3 months and serves both as a filter mechanism and as a means of encouraging settlement.

### Compensation and Damages

The courts award compensatory damages (손해배상) covering: (1) property damages (재산적 손해), including medical expenses, lost income, and property repair costs; (2) non-pecuniary damages (위자료, consolation money), which the courts award liberally in governmental liability cases, particularly those involving violations of personal liberty, dignity, or constitutional rights; and (3) future damages, calculated based on actuarial tables and the Hoffman method or Leibniz method of present value calculation.

### Constitutional Court Influence

The Constitutional Court (헌법재판소) has significantly expanded the scope of governmental liability through constitutional complaints (헌법소원):

**Legislative Omission (입법부작위):** The Constitutional Court has recognized governmental liability arising from unconstitutional legislative omission, where the legislature fails to enact laws necessary to protect fundamental rights. In Constitutional Court Decision 2006Hun-Ma788 (2008), the Court held that the legislature's failure to enact adequate compensation legislation for victims of state violence during the democratization period constituted a constitutional violation.

**Inadequate Protection (과소보호금지):** The Court has applied the prohibition of insufficient protection (과소보호금지원칙) to require the state to provide adequate remedial mechanisms for rights violations, influencing the interpretation and expansion of the State Compensation Act.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States' governmental liability regime reflects the historical development from absolute sovereign immunity to a system of limited waivers, creating a complex patchwork of federal and state liability rules.

**Sovereign Immunity Doctrine:** The doctrine that "the sovereign can do no wrong" was inherited from English common law and established as a principle of federal law in Cohens v. Virginia, 19 U.S. (6 Wheat.) 264 (1821). The Eleventh Amendment extends sovereign immunity to the states, prohibiting suits against states in federal court by their own citizens or citizens of other states (Hans v. Louisiana, 134 U.S. 1 (1890)).

**Federal Tort Claims Act (FTCA, 28 U.S.C. Sections 1346(b), 2671-2680):** Enacted in 1946, the FTCA waives federal sovereign immunity for tort claims arising from the negligent or wrongful acts or omissions of government employees acting within the scope of their employment, under circumstances where the United States, if a private person, would be liable under the law of the place where the act occurred.

### FTCA Exceptions

The FTCA contains significant exceptions that preserve sovereign immunity:

**Discretionary Function Exception (Section 2680(a)):** The most important exception, excluding claims "based upon the exercise or performance or the failure to exercise or perform a discretionary function or duty on the part of a federal agency or employee, whether or not the discretion involved be abused." The Supreme Court established a two-part test in Berkovitz v. United States, 486 U.S. 531 (1988): (1) the challenged conduct must involve an element of judgment or choice (not mandated by statute or regulation); and (2) the judgment must be of the kind the exception was designed to shield -- i.e., based on policy considerations. In United States v. Gaubert, 499 U.S. 315 (1991), the Court expanded the exception, holding that even routine operational decisions may be discretionary if they involve policy judgments.

**Intentional Tort Exception (Section 2680(h)):** The FTCA excludes claims for assault, battery, false imprisonment, false arrest, malicious prosecution, abuse of process, libel, slander, misrepresentation, deceit, and interference with contract. However, a 1974 amendment created an exception to this exception for law enforcement officers, permitting claims for assault, battery, false imprisonment, false arrest, abuse of process, and malicious prosecution committed by federal investigative or law enforcement officers.

**Foreign Country Exception (Section 2680(k)):** Claims arising in a foreign country are excluded, though the Supreme Court in Sosa v. Alvarez-Machain, 542 U.S. 692 (2004), noted that the Alien Tort Statute (28 U.S.C. Section 1350) may provide a limited cause of action for certain violations of international law.

### Section 1983 and Qualified Immunity

**42 U.S.C. Section 1983:** Enacted as part of the Civil Rights Act of 1871 (Ku Klux Klan Act), Section 1983 provides a cause of action against any person who, under color of state law, deprives another of rights secured by the Constitution and laws of the United States. Section 1983 is the primary vehicle for constitutional tort claims against state and local officials.

**Qualified Immunity:** The Supreme Court has developed the doctrine of qualified immunity as the primary defense for government officials sued under Section 1983. Under this doctrine, officials are not liable for civil damages unless they violated "clearly established" statutory or constitutional rights of which a reasonable person would have known (Harlow v. Fitzgerald, 457 U.S. 800 (1982)). The "clearly established" requirement has been interpreted stringently, with the Court requiring a high degree of factual specificity in existing precedent (Ashcroft v. al-Kidd, 563 U.S. 731 (2011)). Qualified immunity has been widely criticized by scholars, advocates, and some jurists as shielding excessive force and other serious misconduct, though legislative proposals to modify or abolish the doctrine (such as the George Floyd Justice in Policing Act) have not been enacted.

**Bivens Actions:** In Bivens v. Six Unknown Named Agents, 403 U.S. 388 (1971), the Supreme Court recognized an implied cause of action directly under the Fourth Amendment against federal officials for constitutional violations. However, the Court has dramatically curtailed the availability of Bivens remedies in recent decades, declining to extend Bivens to new contexts in Ziglar v. Abbasi, 582 U.S. 120 (2017), and Egbert v. Boule, 596 U.S. 482 (2022), effectively limiting Bivens to its original three factual contexts (Fourth Amendment unreasonable search, Fifth Amendment due process, Eighth Amendment cruel and unusual punishment).

### Municipal Liability (Monell)

Under Monell v. Department of Social Services, 436 U.S. 658 (1978), municipalities may be held liable under Section 1983 only when the violation results from an official policy, custom, or practice, or from the decision of a final policymaker. Respondeat superior liability (vicarious liability for employee actions) does not apply. The Monell requirement significantly limits municipal liability, as plaintiffs must demonstrate that the constitutional violation was caused by a municipal policy rather than the individual action of a municipal employee.

### Administrative Claims Process

The FTCA requires claimants to file an administrative claim with the relevant federal agency before bringing suit (28 U.S.C. Section 2675). The agency has six months to respond; if the claim is denied or the agency fails to respond within six months, the claimant may file suit in federal district court. The claim must specify the amount of damages sought, and the claimant is generally limited to the amount specified in the administrative claim.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany provides arguably the most comprehensive and doctrinally developed governmental liability system among the jurisdictions compared, with multiple overlapping bases of liability addressing different categories of state action.

**Amtshaftung (Official Liability -- BGB Section 839, GG Article 34):** The primary basis of governmental liability. Section 839 BGB provides that an official who breaches an official duty (Amtspflicht) intentionally or negligently toward a third party is liable for the resulting damages. Article 34 of the Grundgesetz transfers this liability from the individual official to the state or the public body that employs the official (Haftungsüberleitung). The state then has a right of recourse (Rückgriffsanspruch) against the official in cases of intent or gross negligence.

### Elements of Amtshaftung

The Amtshaftung claim requires proof of five elements:

**1. Public Official (Beamter im haftungsrechtlichen Sinne):** The concept of "official" in Section 839 BGB is interpreted broadly by the BGH to include all persons exercising public authority, regardless of their formal employment status. This includes civil servants, judges, soldiers, police officers, employees of public bodies, notaries (in their capacity as bearers of public authority), and private persons entrusted with public functions (Beliehene).

**2. Exercise of Public Authority (Ausübung eines öffentlichen Amtes):** The act must be performed in the exercise of a public office, i.e., in the exercise of sovereign (hoheitliche) rather than purely commercial (fiskalische) functions.

**3. Breach of Official Duty (Amtspflichtverletzung):** The official must have breached a duty owed to the claimant. Official duties are derived from constitutional law, statutes, administrative regulations, and general principles of administrative law (particularly the principles of proportionality, equal treatment, and legality). The BGH has recognized a broad range of official duties, including the duty to act lawfully, the duty to exercise discretion properly, the duty to protect fundamental rights, and the duty to provide information accurately.

**4. Third-Party Protection (Drittbezogenheit):** The breached duty must have been owed to the claimant specifically, not merely to the general public. This requirement, known as the Schutznormtheorie (protective norm theory), limits liability by requiring that the violated legal norm was intended to protect the individual claimant or the class of persons to which the claimant belongs.

**5. Fault (Verschulden):** Intent (Vorsatz) or negligence (Fahrlässigkeit), assessed by the standard of a diligent official in the same position.

### Enteignungsgleicher Eingriff (Expropriation-Equivalent Interference)

The BGH has developed a no-fault liability for unlawful state interference with property that has the effect of an expropriation. Under this doctrine, rooted in Article 14 GG (protection of property) and developed through judicial creation (Richterrecht), the state is liable for compensation when it unlawfully interferes with property rights in a manner equivalent to expropriation, even in the absence of fault. The compensation claim is based on the principle that the individual should not be required to bear a disproportionate burden for the benefit of the public.

In BGH III ZR 245/98 (2001), the Court applied the enteignungsgleicher Eingriff to award compensation for property damage caused by unauthorized road construction that diverted water flow, even though the responsible officials acted without fault.

### Aufopferungsanspruch (Sacrifice Claim)

The Aufopferungsanspruch, derived from Sections 74-75 of the Einleitung zum Allgemeinen Landrecht für die Preußischen Staaten (Introduction to the General Land Law for the Prussian States, 1794), provides compensation for lawful state action that imposes a special sacrifice (Sonderopfer) on the individual exceeding what is expected of the general public. This doctrine has been applied by the BGH to compensate individuals for injuries resulting from lawful but harmful state activities, such as compulsory vaccination injuries (BGH III ZR 120/17, 2019) and injuries resulting from military training exercises.

### Legislative Failure and Judicial Liability

**Legislative Liability:** The BGH has recognized in principle that legislative failure (gesetzgeberisches Unterlassen) can give rise to Amtshaftung, though the threshold is high. In BGH III ZR 48/01 (2003), the Court held that legislators breach their official duties only when they enact or fail to repeal legislation that is "obviously and clearly" unconstitutional.

**Judicial Liability:** Under Section 839(2) BGB, judicial liability for breaches of official duty in the rendering of judgments is limited to cases where the breach constitutes a criminal offense. This limitation, known as the Spruchrichterprivileg (judge's privilege), protects judicial independence but has been criticized as inconsistent with the general principle of governmental liability.

### Procedural Aspects

Amtshaftung claims are brought before the ordinary civil courts (Landgericht or Oberlandesgericht as first instance, depending on the amount in dispute), not the administrative courts. This jurisdictional allocation, mandated by Article 34(3) GG, reflects the civil law character of the claim. The limitation period is three years from knowledge of the damage and the identity of the liable party (Section 195, 199 BGB). The burden of proof lies with the claimant for all elements of the claim.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR -- Article 41 (Just Satisfaction)

Article 41 of the European Convention on Human Rights provides: "If the Court finds that there has been a violation of the Convention or the Protocols thereto, and if the internal law of the High Contracting Party concerned allows only partial reparation to be made, the Court shall, if necessary, afford just satisfaction to the injured party."

**Scope of Just Satisfaction:** The ECtHR may award: (1) pecuniary damages (material loss directly caused by the violation); (2) non-pecuniary damages (moral suffering, distress, frustration, inconvenience); and (3) costs and expenses (legal fees and other costs incurred in the domestic and Convention proceedings). The Court exercises broad discretion in determining awards, applying an equitable basis rather than strict causation principles.

**Key Jurisprudence:**

**Papamichalopoulos v. Greece (Application No. 14556/89, 1995):** The Court awarded restitution of property or, alternatively, compensation at current market value, establishing the principle that just satisfaction should aim at restitutio in integrum where possible.

**Varnava v. Turkey (Application No. 16064/90, 2009):** The Grand Chamber awarded significant non-pecuniary damages for the continued failure to account for missing persons, illustrating the Court's willingness to use just satisfaction as a response to systemic violations.

### Article 13 (Effective Remedy)

Article 13 requires that "Everyone whose rights and freedoms as set forth in this Convention are violated shall have an effective remedy before a national authority." This provision creates an obligation on states to provide domestic mechanisms for challenging governmental action and obtaining redress, including governmental liability regimes that provide compensation for Convention violations.

**Kudla v. Poland (Application No. 30210/96, 2000):** The Grand Chamber held that Article 13 requires an effective remedy for unreasonable length of proceedings (Article 6(1)), establishing that states must provide compensatory mechanisms for systemic governmental failures.

### EU Law -- Francovich Principle (State Liability for EU Law Breaches)

The CJEU's development of the principle of member state liability for breaches of EU law represents one of the most significant contributions to governmental liability doctrine:

**Francovich v. Italy (Joined Cases C-6/90 and C-9/90, 1991):** The Court established that member states are liable to compensate individuals for damages caused by the state's failure to transpose EU directives. The Court held that state liability is "inherent in the system of the Treaty" and is necessary to ensure the full effectiveness of EU law.

**Brasserie du Pecheur/Factortame III (Joined Cases C-46/93 and C-48/93, 1996):** The Court extended and refined the Francovich principle, establishing three conditions for member state liability: (1) the rule of EU law breached must be intended to confer rights on individuals; (2) the breach must be "sufficiently serious"; and (3) there must be a direct causal link between the breach and the damage. The "sufficiently serious" standard requires a "manifest and grave disregard" of the limits on the state's discretion, assessed by considering factors including the clarity of the rule breached, the extent of discretion, whether the infringement was intentional, and whether an error of law was excusable.

**Kobler v. Austria (Case C-224/01, 2003):** The Court extended the Francovich principle to judicial breaches of EU law, holding that member state liability arises from decisions of courts of last instance that manifestly infringe applicable EU law. This decision is notable for overcoming the traditional resistance to judicial liability found in many national systems (including Germany's Spruchrichterprivileg).

### EU Member State Implementation

Member states implement the Francovich principle through their national governmental liability regimes, adapting existing frameworks to accommodate EU law requirements. In Germany, the BGH has integrated Francovich liability into the Amtshaftung framework, treating breaches of EU law as breaches of official duty. In the United Kingdom (pre-Brexit), the courts developed a dedicated body of Francovich jurisprudence. The CJEU's requirements of equivalence (EU law claims must be treated no less favorably than similar domestic claims) and effectiveness (national procedures must not make it practically impossible or excessively difficult to obtain reparation) provide minimum standards for national implementation.

## Comparative Analysis

### The Spectrum of Sovereign Immunity Waiver

The jurisdictions occupy different positions on the spectrum from sovereign immunity to full governmental liability:

**Germany** represents the most complete waiver of sovereign immunity, with Amtshaftung providing comprehensive liability for all exercises of public authority, supplemented by no-fault doctrines (enteignungsgleicher Eingriff, Aufopferungsanspruch) that ensure compensation even when fault cannot be established. The absence of a discretionary function exception comparable to the U.S. doctrine means that virtually all governmental action is subject to liability review.

**South Korea** provides broad governmental liability through the State Compensation Act, with constitutional backing that limits the legislature's ability to restrict liability. The dual standard of unlawfulness (conduct and result) and the liberal approach to non-pecuniary damages provide substantial protection for individuals.

**The United States** retains the most significant limitations on governmental liability. The FTCA's discretionary function exception, the intentional tort exception, qualified immunity for Section 1983 claims, the near-elimination of Bivens remedies for federal officials, and the Monell limitation on municipal liability create substantial barriers to governmental accountability. The contrast with the German and Korean systems is particularly stark in areas of regulatory failure and law enforcement misconduct.

### Fault versus No-Fault Liability

All jurisdictions primarily employ fault-based liability (requiring intent or negligence) as the default standard for governmental liability. However, Germany's development of no-fault doctrines (enteignungsgleicher Eingriff, Aufopferungsanspruch) and South Korea's strict facility liability (Article 5) provide important supplements. The ECHR and EU frameworks do not require fault in the traditional sense but apply their own standards (sufficiently serious breach for Francovich liability; Convention violation for Article 41 just satisfaction).

### Discretionary Function and Judicial Deference

The treatment of discretionary governmental decisions represents a critical divergence. The United States' broad discretionary function exception effectively immunizes a wide range of policy-based decisions from tort liability. Germany recognizes Ermessensspielraum (margin of discretion) but subjects discretionary decisions to comprehensive judicial review under the proportionality principle, with liability arising from improper exercise of discretion. South Korea's courts similarly review discretionary decisions for conformity with constitutional principles and the purpose of the enabling legislation. The ECHR's margin of appreciation doctrine provides states with some deference but does not create an exception from liability.

### Personal Official Liability and Accountability

The relationship between state liability and personal official accountability varies significantly. Germany's Haftungsüberleitung (transfer of liability to the state) ensures that injured parties can recover from the state while preserving the state's recourse against culpable officials. South Korea similarly channels claims against the state with a right of recourse. The United States' qualified immunity doctrine, by contrast, shields individual officials from personal liability, and the near-elimination of Bivens remedies means that federal officials face minimal personal accountability for constitutional violations. This divergence has significant implications for deterrence and governmental accountability.
