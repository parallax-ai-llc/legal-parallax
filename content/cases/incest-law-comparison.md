## Summary

Incest -- sexual relations between persons who are closely related by blood or, in some jurisdictions, by marriage or adoption -- remains one of the most universally prohibited sexual offenses, though the scope of prohibited relationships, the rationale for criminalization, and the severity of penalties vary significantly across legal systems. South Korea criminalizes sexual acts between lineal relatives under Article 9 of the Act on Special Cases Concerning the Punishment of Sexual Crimes (성폭력범죄의 처벌 등에 관한 특례법), which addresses sexual assault by relatives, and more broadly prohibits marriage between close relatives under Article 809 of the Civil Act (민법), which bars marriage between blood relatives within the eighth degree of kinship (8촌 이내의 혈족) and between certain affinal relatives; while Korean criminal law does not have a standalone incest offense, sexual relations between close family members are prosecuted under the aggravated sexual assault provisions with enhanced penalties of up to life imprisonment. The United States criminalizes incest in all 50 states, with definitions and penalties varying widely -- some states limit the prohibition to consanguineous relations (blood relatives) while others extend it to step-relatives and adoptive relations, and penalties range from misdemeanors to felonies carrying up to life imprisonment; the Supreme Court has never directly addressed the constitutionality of incest statutes, though Lawrence v. Texas, 539 U.S. 558 (2003) expressly excluded incest from its holding. Germany criminalizes consensual sexual intercourse between lineal relatives (parents, grandparents, children, grandchildren) and between siblings under Section 173 StGB (Beischlaf zwischen Verwandten), with penalties of up to three years for lineal relatives and up to two years for siblings, and the Federal Constitutional Court upheld the constitutionality of this provision in a landmark 2008 decision (2 BvR 392/07), finding that the criminalization of consensual adult incest serves legitimate purposes including protection of the family, prevention of genetic harm, and reinforcement of societal taboos. The ECHR addressed incest directly in Stübing v. Germany (Application No. 43547/08, 2012), in which the Court upheld Germany's incest prohibition within the state's margin of appreciation, holding that criminalizing consensual sexual relations between adult siblings does not violate Article 8, while recognizing the diversity of approaches across Council of Europe member states.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea does not have a standalone incest offense in the Criminal Act (형법). Instead, incestuous conduct is addressed through a combination of aggravated sexual assault provisions and civil marriage prohibitions.

**Act on Special Cases Concerning the Punishment of Sexual Crimes (성폭력범죄의 처벌 등에 관한 특례법):**

**Article 5 -- Sexual Assault by Relatives (친족관계에 의한 강간 등):** "A person who commits rape under Article 297 of the Criminal Act against a person related by blood or marriage within the fourth degree of kinship, or a person in a relationship of cohabitation, shall be punished by imprisonment for not less than 7 years." This provision imposes enhanced penalties for sexual assault by relatives, reflecting the heightened breach of trust and the vulnerability of victims within family relationships.

**Article 7 -- Sexual Assault Against Minors by Relatives:** Provides additional enhancements for sexual offenses against minors committed by relatives, with penalties up to life imprisonment.

### Civil Act Marriage Prohibitions

**Article 809 of the Civil Act (민법):**
- Paragraph 1: "Marriage may not be entered into between blood relatives within the eighth degree of kinship" (8촌 이내의 혈족 사이에서는 혼인하지 못한다).
- Paragraph 2: "Marriage may not be entered into between lineal affinal relatives or between collateral affinal relatives within the sixth degree of kinship who are or have been in such relationship" (6촌 이내의 혈족의 배우자, 배우자의 6촌 이내의 혈족, 배우자의 4촌 이내의 혈족의 배우자인 인척이거나 이러한 인척이었던 자 사이에서는 혼인하지 못한다).

### Historical Context

**Clan Exogamy and the Same-Surname, Same-Origin Rule:** Until 2005, Article 809(1) of the Civil Act prohibited marriage between persons sharing the same surname and ancestral seat (동성동본 금혼, dongseonggonbon geumhon), a rule derived from Confucian patrilineal kinship principles. This prohibition was effectively broader than Western incest laws, preventing marriage between distantly related persons who shared a patrilineal ancestor, even those separated by many generations.

**Constitutional Court Decision 97헌가12 (1997):** The Court ruled the same-surname, same-origin marriage ban unconstitutional, finding it violated the right to marry and the right to equality. The provision was formally abolished by amendment in 2005.

### Enforcement

Korean courts typically prosecute incestuous conduct under the aggravated sexual assault provisions rather than seeking criminal penalties for the incestuous relationship per se. This approach reflects a focus on the coercive or abusive nature of most reported incestuous relationships rather than the relationship status itself.

**Supreme Court Decision 2017도21549 (2018):** The Court addressed a case of repeated sexual assault of a minor by a parent, upholding a sentence of 15 years imprisonment under the enhanced penalty provisions for sexual assault by relatives.

## United States

### Legal Framework

All 50 states and the District of Columbia criminalize incest, but definitions and penalties vary dramatically:

| Step-relatives included | Many states | Various state codes |
| Adoptive relatives included | Majority | Various state codes |

### Constitutional Analysis

**Lawrence v. Texas, 539 U.S. 558 (2003):** Justice Kennedy's majority opinion striking down sodomy laws explicitly carved out incest: "The present case does not involve minors. It does not involve persons who might be injured or coerced or who are situated in relationships where consent might not easily be refused." Justice Scalia's dissent argued the majority's reasoning logically undermined incest laws.

**State Constitutional Challenges:** Several lower courts have rejected constitutional challenges to incest statutes post-Lawrence:

**State v. Lowe, 861 N.W.2d 380 (Ohio App. 2014):** The Ohio Court of Appeals rejected a due process challenge to incest statute, holding that Lawrence's reasoning did not extend to incestuous relationships given the "inherent power imbalance" in familial relationships.

### Genetic Sexual Attraction

The concept of "genetic sexual attraction" (GSA) -- sexual attraction between close biological relatives who meet for the first time as adults, typically following adoption separation -- has entered legal discussions. While GSA has not been recognized as a legal defense, it has been raised as a mitigating factor in sentencing.

### First-Cousin Marriage

The legality of first-cousin marriage varies by state:
- **Legal:** Approximately 19 states (including California, New York, Colorado)
- **Illegal:** Approximately 24 states
- **Legal with conditions:** Several states (e.g., only if over age 50 or unable to procreate)

## Germany

### Legal Framework

**Section 173 StGB -- Sexual Intercourse Between Relatives (Beischlaf zwischen Verwandten):**
- Paragraph 1: "Whoever performs sexual intercourse with a consanguineous descendant shall be punished with imprisonment for a term not exceeding three years or a fine."
- Paragraph 2: "Whoever performs sexual intercourse with a consanguineous relative in the ascending line shall be punished with imprisonment for a term not exceeding two years or a fine; this also applies where the kinship relationship has been terminated. Descendants and siblings who were not yet eighteen years old at the time of the act shall not be punished."
- Paragraph 3: "Whoever performs sexual intercourse with a consanguineous sibling shall be punished with imprisonment for a term not exceeding two years or a fine."

### Key Features

**Consanguinity Requirement:** Section 173 applies only to consanguineous (blood-related) relatives. Step-relatives, adoptive relatives, and affinal relatives are not covered. This limitation reflects the provision's focus on genetic and biological family relationships.

**Age Exemption:** Descendants and siblings under 18 are exempted from prosecution, reflecting the recognition that younger family members may be victims rather than willing participants.

**Limited to "Beischlaf" (Sexual Intercourse):** The provision covers only sexual intercourse (vaginal penetration) in its traditional definition. Other sexual acts between relatives are not covered by Section 173, though they may be punishable under other provisions if coercive.

### The Patrick Stübing Case

The case that brought Germany's incest law to international attention:

**Facts:** Patrick Stübing was adopted as a young child and met his biological sister Susan Karolewski for the first time in 2000, when he was 23 and she was 16. They entered into a sexual relationship and had four children together (two of whom had disabilities). Stübing was convicted multiple times under Section 173 and served several prison sentences.

**BVerfG, Decision of February 26, 2008 (2 BvR 392/07):** The Federal Constitutional Court upheld Section 173(2) by a 7-1 vote, finding that the criminalization of consensual adult incest serves multiple legitimate purposes:
1. **Protection of the family:** The prohibition protects the family as a social institution and prevents the confusion of familial roles
2. **Prevention of genetic harm:** The increased risk of genetic disorders in offspring of closely related parents justifies state intervention
3. **Societal taboo reinforcement:** The incest prohibition reflects and reinforces a deep-seated societal taboo that serves important social functions
4. **Protection against abuse:** Even in ostensibly consensual relationships, the power dynamics within families create a risk of exploitation

**Dissent (Justice Hassemer):** The lone dissenter argued that criminalizing consensual sexual conduct between adults who are both capable of consent violates the right to sexual self-determination, as the state's interest does not justify criminal sanctions where no third party is directly harmed.

### Academic Debate

The German Council of Ethics (Deutscher Ethikrat) issued a recommendation in September 2014 calling for the decriminalization of consensual sexual intercourse between adult siblings, arguing that criminal law is not the appropriate means of addressing the issue. This recommendation generated significant public debate but has not led to legislative reform.

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

**Stübing v. Germany (Application No. 43547/08, Judgment of April 12, 2012):** Following the BVerfG's rejection of his constitutional complaint, Patrick Stübing brought his case to the ECtHR, alleging a violation of Article 8 (right to respect for private life).

The Court held:
- The prohibition of incest constitutes an interference with the right to private life under Article 8
- However, the interference was "in accordance with the law" (Section 173 StGB) and pursued legitimate aims (protection of morals, protection of the rights of others)
- States enjoy a wide margin of appreciation in this area, given the lack of European consensus -- while some member states have decriminalized consensual adult incest (France, Belgium, Netherlands, Spain, Turkey), many others maintain criminal prohibitions
- The German legislature was entitled to consider the protection of family structures, the prevention of genetic harm, and the protection against exploitation within familial power dynamics
- **Conclusion:** No violation of Article 8. The criminalization of consensual adult incest falls within Germany's margin of appreciation.

### European Landscape

The diversity of approaches across Council of Europe member states is significant:

| Approach | Countries |
|----------|-----------|
| **Criminalized (all consensual adult incest)** | Germany, UK, Austria, Switzerland, Denmark, Norway |
| **Decriminalized (consensual adult incest)** | France, Belgium, Netherlands, Spain, Portugal, Turkey |
| **Partially criminalized** | Sweden (only lineal relatives), Russia (no specific offense) |

### EU Framework

The EU has not harmonized incest law. However, Directive 2011/93/EU on combating sexual abuse and exploitation of children requires enhanced penalties where the sexual offense is committed by a family member or person abusing a recognized position of trust, authority, or influence over the child (Article 9(d)).

## Comparative Analysis

### Rationale for Criminalization

Three principal justifications recur across jurisdictions:

**Prevention of Abuse/Exploitation:** All jurisdictions recognize the inherent power imbalances in family relationships. This rationale is strongest for parent-child incest and weakest for sibling relationships between adults of similar age.

**Genetic Harm Prevention:** The increased risk of autosomal recessive genetic disorders in offspring of closely related parents provides a eugenic rationale. Critics argue this logic, taken to its conclusion, would justify prohibiting reproduction by any carriers of genetic disorders.

**Social Taboo/Family Integrity:** The incest taboo is one of the most universal cultural norms. The criminalization serves to reinforce this taboo and protect family structures from sexual confusion.

### Scope Variations

The jurisdictions show significant variation in scope:
- **Broadest:** South Korea (8th degree kinship marriage ban) and some U.S. states (including step and adoptive relations)
- **Intermediate:** Germany (consanguineous lineal relatives and siblings only)
- **Variable:** ECHR member states range from full criminalization to full decriminalization

### Consent and Power Dynamics

The role of consent differs significantly:
- **South Korea:** Focus on abuse -- prosecuted under aggravated sexual assault provisions
- **United States:** Consent generally irrelevant; some states distinguish between adult and minor victims
- **Germany:** Section 173 specifically addresses consensual incest; coercive incest is prosecuted under sexual assault provisions
- **ECHR:** Consent does not preclude criminalization within state margin of appreciation
