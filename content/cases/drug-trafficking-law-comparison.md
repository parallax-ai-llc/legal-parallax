## Summary

Drug trafficking — the manufacture, transport, distribution, and sale of controlled substances — constitutes one of the most heavily penalized criminal offenses worldwide, yet the philosophical foundations, enforcement strategies, and sentencing outcomes differ dramatically across jurisdictions. South Korea maintains one of the world's strictest zero-tolerance regimes under the Narcotics Control Act (mayangnyugwanribeop, 마약류관리법), where even overseas drug use by Korean nationals is criminalized and trafficking carries penalties up to life imprisonment. The United States employs a tiered system of mandatory minimum sentences under the Controlled Substances Act (CSA) of 1970, where drug type and quantity mechanically determine prison terms ranging from 5 years to life. Germany's Narcotics Act (Betaeubungsmittelgesetz, BtMG) balances enforcement with prosecutorial discretion, permitting cases involving small quantities for personal use to be dropped entirely, and made history in April 2024 by legalizing recreational cannabis under the Cannabis Act (Cannabisgesetz, CanG). The European Union's Drugs Strategy 2021-2025 frames drug policy as a public health issue alongside a security concern, embedding harm reduction as a core pillar equal to supply reduction. These contrasts illuminate a fundamental divide: East Asian zero-tolerance models that treat any drug involvement as a grave moral failing versus European harm-reduction approaches that increasingly distinguish between trafficking and personal consumption.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's drug control regime rests on the Narcotics Control Act (마약류관리법), which classifies controlled substances into three categories: narcotics (mayak), psychotropic substances (hyangjeongsinseong uiyakpum), and hemp (daema). The Act imposes severe penalties across the entire chain of drug activity, from cultivation and manufacture through distribution and simple possession.

**Trafficking Penalties:** Any person who imports, exports, manufactures, sells, or facilitates the sale of narcotics, or who possesses narcotics for such purposes, faces a minimum of 5 years and a maximum of life imprisonment. For profit-driven trafficking of narcotics such as methamphetamine, heroin, or cocaine, courts routinely impose sentences exceeding 10 years.

| Offense | Provision | Penalty |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Import/export of narcotics | Art. 58 | 5 years to life imprisonment |
| Manufacture of narcotics | Art. 58 | 5 years to life imprisonment |
| Sale/distribution of narcotics | Art. 58 | 5 years to life imprisonment |
| Possession for trafficking purposes | Art. 58 | 5 years to life imprisonment |
| Simple possession/use of narcotics | Art. 59 | Up to 5 years imprisonment |
| Overseas drug use by Korean nationals | Art. 59 | Up to 5 years imprisonment |
| Possession of psychotropic substances | Art. 60 | Up to 5 years imprisonment |
| Cannabis possession | Art. 61 | Up to 5 years imprisonment |

### Key Principles

**Extraterritorial jurisdiction:** South Korea is one of the few countries that criminalizes drug use by its citizens abroad. Under Article 3 of the Criminal Act combined with the Narcotics Control Act, a Korean national who consumes cannabis in a jurisdiction where it is legal — such as Canada or certain U.S. states — remains subject to prosecution upon return to South Korea. Authorities have conducted mandatory drug testing at airports on individuals returning from countries with permissive drug laws.

**Zero-tolerance culture:** South Korean society treats drug offenses with exceptional severity. Unlike assault or fraud, drug crimes carry intense social stigma, and offenders face near-certain career destruction. Korean entertainment figures caught using drugs have been permanently blacklisted from the industry, with their existing works removed from streaming platforms.

**Increasing enforcement intensity:** South Korea recorded 27,611 drug offenders in 2023 — the highest number in the nation's history — before a partial decline to 23,022 in 2024. In response, the Supreme Prosecutors' Office established a dedicated Narcotics Crime Special Investigation Unit, and the National Police Agency launched targeted operations against international trafficking networks. In November 2024, authorities arrested eight individuals, including Canadian nationals, who smuggled liquid cocaine from Colombia and manufactured 61 kg of solid cocaine domestically.

**Crackdown on foreign nationals:** In March 2025, the National Police Agency launched a nationwide crackdown on organized criminal activity involving foreign nationals, running through June 2025, with drug trafficking identified as a primary target alongside vehicle theft and other organized crime.

### Sentencing Patterns

Korean courts apply aggravating factors liberally in drug trafficking cases. Profit motive, gang involvement, large quantities, and use of international networks all push sentences toward the upper range. Suspended sentences are extremely rare for trafficking offenses, though first-time simple possession cases involving small amounts of cannabis may occasionally receive probation.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Federal drug trafficking law in the United States is governed primarily by the Controlled Substances Act (CSA) of 1970, codified at 21 U.S.C. Sections 801-971. The CSA classifies drugs into five Schedules based on medical utility and abuse potential, with Schedule I (heroin, LSD, cannabis, ecstasy) carrying the most severe restrictions and Schedule V (certain cough preparations) the least.

**Mandatory Minimum Sentences:** The Anti-Drug Abuse Act of 1986 established mandatory minimum prison terms triggered by specific drug quantities. These minimums operate as a floor — judges cannot impose shorter sentences except through limited safety-valve provisions or government motions for substantial assistance.

| Drug | Quantity (5-Year Minimum) | Quantity (10-Year Minimum) | Maximum |
|------|--------------------------|---------------------------|---------|
| Heroin | 100 g | 1 kg | 40 years to life |
| Cocaine powder | 500 g | 5 kg | 40 years to life |
| Crack cocaine | 28 g | 280 g | 40 years to life |
| Methamphetamine | 5 g (pure) / 50 g (mixture) | 50 g (pure) / 500 g (mixture) | 40 years to life |
| Marijuana | 100 kg | 1,000 kg | 40 years to life |
| Fentanyl | 40 g | 400 g | 40 years to life |

**Enhanced penalties for prior offenders:** If a defendant has a prior felony drug conviction, the 5-year mandatory minimum doubles to 10 years, and the 10-year minimum increases to 20 years. A defendant with two or more prior felony drug convictions faces a mandatory minimum of 25 years to life.

### Key Principles

**Federal sentencing guidelines:** Beyond mandatory minimums, the U.S. Sentencing Guidelines provide a detailed matrix combining offense level (determined by drug quantity, role in the offense, and enhancements) with criminal history category. While advisory since United States v. Booker (2005), the Guidelines remain highly influential on actual sentences imposed.

**Conspiracy liability:** Under 21 U.S.C. Section 846, conspiracy to commit drug trafficking carries the same penalties as the underlying offense. This provision enables prosecutors to charge individuals with limited physical involvement in trafficking — such as lookouts, drivers, or landlords who knowingly allowed drug activity — with sentences equivalent to those of principal traffickers.

**State-federal tension on cannabis:** As of 2025, 24 states and the District of Columbia have legalized recreational cannabis, creating a stark contradiction with federal law, which continues to classify cannabis as Schedule I. The Department of Justice's enforcement posture has fluctuated between administrations, with the Cole Memorandum (2013) adopting a hands-off approach later rescinded by Attorney General Sessions in 2018.

**Racial disparities:** Extensive research has documented significant racial disparities in federal drug trafficking sentencing. The crack-powder cocaine disparity — initially set at 100:1 before being reduced to 18:1 by the Fair Sentencing Act of 2010 — disproportionately affected Black defendants, as crack cocaine was more prevalent in Black communities while powder cocaine was more common among white users.

### Landmark Cases

**United States v. Booker, 543 U.S. 220 (2005):** The Supreme Court held that the mandatory application of the Federal Sentencing Guidelines violated the Sixth Amendment right to jury trial, rendering the Guidelines advisory rather than binding. This decision gave judges greater discretion in drug trafficking sentencing.

**Alleyne v. United States, 570 U.S. 99 (2013):** The Court ruled that any fact increasing the mandatory minimum sentence must be submitted to a jury and proven beyond a reasonable doubt, applying Sixth Amendment protections to mandatory minimums in drug cases.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's drug trafficking law is primarily governed by the Narcotics Act (Betaeubungsmittelgesetz, BtMG), which has been in force since 1981. The BtMG classifies controlled substances across three annexes: Annex I (non-marketable narcotics such as heroin and MDMA), Annex II (marketable but non-prescribable narcotics used in manufacturing), and Annex III (prescribable narcotics such as morphine and codeine).

**Basic Offenses (Section 29 BtMG):** Unlawful cultivation, manufacture, trade, import, export, sale, supply, or possession of narcotics carries a penalty of up to 5 years imprisonment or a fine. This broad provision covers the full range of drug activity, with the relatively moderate maximum reflecting Germany's emphasis on proportional sentencing.

**Aggravated Offenses (Section 29a BtMG):** Trafficking in "non-small quantities" (nicht geringe Menge) of narcotics carries a minimum sentence of 1 year imprisonment. Supplying narcotics to persons under 18 also triggers enhanced penalties under this section.

**Serious Trafficking (Section 30 BtMG):** The most severe penalties apply to gang-based trafficking, trafficking in non-small quantities as a member of a gang, carrying weapons during drug offenses, or causing the death of another person through drug supply. These offenses carry a minimum of 5 years imprisonment, with a maximum of 15 years.

| Offense | Provision | Penalty |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Simple trafficking/possession | Section 29(1) BtMG | Up to 5 years or fine |
| Trafficking in non-small quantities | Section 29a BtMG | 1-15 years imprisonment |
| Supplying drugs to minors | Section 29a BtMG | 1-15 years imprisonment |
| Gang-based trafficking | Section 30 BtMG | 5-15 years imprisonment |
| Armed trafficking | Section 30 BtMG | 5-15 years imprisonment |
| Causing death by supply | Section 30 BtMG | Not less than 3 years |
| Negligent trafficking offenses | Section 29(4) BtMG | Up to 1 year or fine |

### Key Principles

**Prosecutorial discretion (Opportunitaetsprinzip):** Section 31a BtMG allows prosecutors to drop cases involving small quantities of drugs intended for personal use, without court approval. This provision is applied unevenly across the 16 federal states (Bundeslaender), with Berlin and Hamburg generally applying more lenient thresholds than Bavaria or Saxony. For cannabis, the threshold considered "small quantity" ranged historically from 6 grams in some southern states to 15 grams in Berlin.

**Cannabis legalization (Cannabisgesetz, CanG):** On April 1, 2024, Germany became the first major EU member state to legalize recreational cannabis. Adults aged 18 and over may possess up to 25 grams in public and 50 grams at home, and cultivate up to three plants for personal use. From July 1, 2024, non-profit cannabis social clubs with up to 500 members were permitted to operate. This landmark reform removed cannabis from the BtMG framework for personal-use quantities, though commercial sale remains prohibited and trafficking in cannabis above the legal thresholds continues to be prosecuted under the BtMG.

**Therapy over punishment (Therapie statt Strafe):** Section 35 BtMG allows courts to defer execution of prison sentences of up to 2 years for drug-dependent offenders who enter treatment programs. If therapy is completed successfully, the sentence may be suspended. This provision reflects Germany's broader approach of treating addiction as a health condition rather than purely a criminal matter.

**"Non-small quantity" thresholds:** German courts have established specific quantity thresholds that trigger aggravated penalties. For example, the Federal Court of Justice (Bundesgerichtshof) has set the non-small quantity threshold at 5 grams for heroin (based on active ingredient), 5 grams for cocaine, and 5 grams for amphetamine.

### Sentencing Patterns

German courts routinely impose suspended sentences (Bewaehrung) for first-time drug offenders convicted of basic trafficking under Section 29 BtMG, particularly when quantities are moderate and the defendant shows willingness to undergo treatment. For aggravated and gang-related trafficking, however, sentences of 3-8 years are typical, with the 15-year statutory maximum reserved for the most serious cases.

## European Framework

### EU Drugs Strategy 2021-2025

The European Union adopted the EU Drugs Strategy 2021-2025 on December 18, 2020, establishing the political framework and priorities for EU drug policy. The Strategy takes an evidence-based, integrated, balanced, and multidisciplinary approach, built on three pillars: drug demand reduction, drug supply reduction, and addressing drug-related harm.

**Harm reduction as a core pillar:** For the first time, the EU Strategy included a dedicated chapter on drug-related harm, elevating harm reduction from a subordinate component to a co-equal policy objective alongside supply and demand reduction. Harm reduction encompasses needle exchange programs, drug consumption rooms, opioid substitution therapy, naloxone distribution, drug checking services, and outreach to marginalized populations.

**Supply reduction and trafficking enforcement:** The Strategy simultaneously emphasizes dismantling drug trafficking networks, combating drug-related organized crime, and strengthening EU-level cooperation through Europol, Eurojust, and the European Monitoring Centre for Drugs and Drug Addiction (EMCDDA, now the EU Drugs Agency, EUDA).

### EU Minimum Standards on Drug Trafficking

Council Framework Decision 2004/757/JHA established minimum rules on the definition of drug trafficking offenses and penalties across EU member states. The Decision requires that the following acts be punishable when committed without authorization: production, manufacture, extraction, preparation, offering, distribution, sale, delivery, brokerage, dispatch, transit, transport, import, and export of drugs. Minimum maximum penalties were set at 1-3 years for basic offenses and 5-10 years for aggravated offenses involving organized crime or large quantities.

### National Variations Within the EU

Despite the common framework, enormous variation persists among EU member states:

| Country | Approach | Cannabis Policy | Trafficking Penalties |
|---------|----------|----------------|----------------------|
| Portugal | Decriminalization (2001) | Decriminalized personal use | 4-12 years (trafficking) |
| Netherlands | Tolerance (gedoogbeleid) | Coffee shops permitted | Up to 12 years |
| Germany | Legalization (cannabis, 2024) | Legal for personal use since April 2024 | Up to 15 years (aggravated) |
| France | Strict enforcement | Fine for use since 2020 | Up to 30 years (organized trafficking) |
| Sweden | Zero tolerance | All use criminalized | Up to 18 years |
| Czech Republic | Liberal thresholds | Small amounts decriminalized | Up to 18 years (aggravated) |
| Spain | Private use tolerance | Private cultivation/use permitted | 3-9 years (basic) |

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR has addressed drug trafficking primarily through Article 3 (prohibition of torture) and Article 6 (right to fair trial) of the European Convention on Human Rights. In cases such as Jalloh v. Germany (2006), the Court held that forcing an emetic on a suspected drug dealer to recover swallowed drugs constituted inhuman and degrading treatment under Article 3, establishing limits on enforcement methods even in trafficking cases.

## Comparative Analysis

### Philosophical Foundations

| Dimension | South Korea | United States | Germany | EU Framework |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|---------|--------------|
| Core philosophy | Zero tolerance; moral absolutism | Punitive deterrence with quantity-based triggers | Proportionality; health-oriented | Balanced; harm reduction as co-equal pillar |
| Cannabis status | Fully illegal; no exceptions | Federal Schedule I; state-level legalization varies | Legal for personal use (April 2024) | Member state discretion |
| Extraterritorial reach | Criminalizes overseas drug use | Limited to international trafficking | No extraterritorial personal use prosecution | Not applicable |
| Harm reduction | Virtually absent | Limited; varies by state | Extensive; needle exchanges, drug consumption rooms | Strongly promoted |
| Prosecutorial discretion | Very limited for drug crimes | Significant (plea bargaining) | Broad (Section 31a drop provisions) | Varies by member state |
| Maximum penalty (trafficking) | Life imprisonment | Life imprisonment | 15 years imprisonment | Varies; 10-30 years |
| Treatment alternatives | Limited; secondary to punishment | Drug courts in some jurisdictions | Therapy-over-punishment (Section 35 BtMG) | Encouraged by Strategy |
| Mandatory minimums | 5 years (trafficking) | 5-10 years (quantity-dependent) | 1-5 years (severity-dependent) | Minimum maximums set by Framework Decision |

### Sentencing Severity Spectrum

The four jurisdictions occupy distinct positions on the sentencing severity spectrum for a hypothetical case of trafficking 100 grams of methamphetamine:

- **South Korea:** 10+ years imprisonment likely; no suspended sentence; permanent social stigma and career destruction.
- **United States:** 10-year federal mandatory minimum (5 grams pure triggers the threshold); potential life sentence with prior convictions.
- **Germany:** 5-15 years under Section 30 BtMG if gang-related; 1-15 years under Section 29a for non-small quantity trafficking; possibility of reduced sentence with cooperation or therapy.
- **EU average:** Wide variation, but most Western European states would impose 5-10 years for this quantity.

### Zero Tolerance vs. Harm Reduction

The starkest philosophical divide separates South Korea's zero-tolerance model from the European harm-reduction approach. South Korea views drug use as an existential threat to social order, deploying extraterritorial jurisdiction, mandatory testing, and severe social consequences alongside criminal penalties. Europe — particularly the Netherlands, Portugal, and Germany — increasingly treats drug consumption as a public health issue, reserving the harshest criminal penalties for large-scale trafficking while offering treatment pathways for users and small-scale offenders.

The United States occupies an uncomfortable middle position, with federal mandatory minimums rivaling South Korean severity for trafficking quantities while state-level cannabis legalization mirrors European liberalization. This federal-state contradiction remains one of the most distinctive features of American drug policy.
