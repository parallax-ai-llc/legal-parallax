## Summary

Criminal record expungement -- the legal process of sealing, destroying, or otherwise removing criminal records from public access -- addresses the fundamental tension between the state's interest in maintaining accurate records of criminal conduct and the individual's interest in rehabilitation, privacy, and freedom from the collateral consequences of a criminal conviction that may persist long after a sentence has been served. South Korea provides for the rehabilitation of criminal records through the Criminal Records Management Act (범죄경력자료 관리법) and the Act on the Lapse of Criminal Sentences (형의 실효 등에 관한 법률), under which criminal records are automatically sealed after specified waiting periods following completion of the sentence -- ten years for imprisonment, five years for fines -- and the individual is treated as having no criminal record for most civil purposes, though records remain accessible to law enforcement and prosecutors. The United States has no federal expungement statute for most federal convictions, creating a patchwork of state-level systems that vary enormously in scope and accessibility: as of 2025, all fifty states and the District of Columbia provide some form of expungement or record sealing, with major reforms including Utah's Clean Slate Act (2019), Pennsylvania's Clean Slate Act (2018), and Michigan's Clean Slate Act (2020), which pioneered automatic expungement based on eligibility criteria without requiring a petition. Germany's approach is governed by the Federal Central Criminal Register Act (Bundeszentralregistergesetz, BZRG), under which convictions are removed from the criminal register after specified periods ranging from five to twenty years depending on the severity of the sentence, with an absolute prohibition under Section 51 BZRG on using removed convictions against the person in legal proceedings or employment decisions. The European framework is shaped by the EU's General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), particularly the right to erasure under Article 17, Directive (EU) 2016/680 on data processing in criminal matters, and the European Court of Human Rights' landmark judgment in M.M. v. United Kingdom (2012), which established that systematic retention and disclosure of criminal record data engages Article 8 of the Convention.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's criminal record management system operates under two primary statutes: the Criminal Records Management Act (범죄경력자료 관리법) and the Act on the Lapse of Criminal Sentences (형의 실효 등에 관한 법률). Together, these laws establish a system of automatic rehabilitation in which criminal records lose their legal effect after specified periods, enabling individuals to reintegrate into society without the perpetual stigma of a criminal conviction.

### Automatic Lapse System

Under the Act on the Lapse of Criminal Sentences, a criminal sentence automatically lapses (실효) upon the expiration of the following waiting periods after completion of the sentence:

| Sentence Type | Waiting Period |
|---------------|---------------|
| Imprisonment of more than 3 years | 10 years |
| Imprisonment of 3 years or less | 5 years |
| Fine (벌금) | 5 years from payment |
| Suspended sentence (집행유예) | 2 years from completion of suspension period |
| Probation | Period of probation plus 2 years |

### Effect of Lapse

Once a sentence has lapsed, the individual is treated as having no criminal record for most purposes. Employers are prohibited from requiring disclosure of lapsed records in hiring decisions, and the individual may lawfully deny having a criminal record on employment applications. However, records remain accessible to:

- Law enforcement agencies for investigation purposes
- Prosecutors for charging decisions and sentencing recommendations
- Courts for sentencing in subsequent criminal proceedings
- Certain licensing authorities for specific professions (e.g., teaching, childcare)

### Recent Reforms

The 2021 amendments to the Criminal Records Management Act strengthened privacy protections for individuals with lapsed records by imposing stricter penalties on unauthorized disclosure of criminal record information and expanding the categories of records eligible for lapse. The amendments also introduced enhanced protections for juvenile records, requiring automatic sealing upon reaching adulthood for minor offenses.

### Constitutional Court Decisions

In Decision 2017HeonMa630 (2019), the Constitutional Court addressed the constitutionality of maintaining unsealable criminal records for sex offenses. The Court upheld the provision, finding that the public interest in protecting vulnerable populations justified the permanent retention of records for serious sex crimes, while noting that the legislature should regularly review whether the exclusion categories remain proportionate.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States lacks a comprehensive federal expungement statute, resulting in a fragmented landscape of state-level systems. At the federal level, no general mechanism exists for expunging federal convictions, with limited exceptions for certain drug offenses under 18 U.S.C. Section 3607 (first-time drug possession) and under specific statutes such as the Cannabis Administration and Opportunity Act proposals (not enacted as of 2025).

### Clean Slate Movement

The most significant development in U.S. expungement law has been the "Clean Slate" movement, which advocates for automatic record clearing for eligible individuals:

**Pennsylvania Clean Slate Act (2018):** The first comprehensive automatic expungement law, effective June 28, 2019, providing for automatic sealing of non-conviction records, summary offense convictions after 10 years with no subsequent convictions, and qualifying misdemeanor convictions.

**Utah Clean Slate Act (2019):** Effective February 2022, providing automatic expungement of eligible misdemeanor and felony convictions after waiting periods of five to seven years.

**Michigan Clean Slate Act (2020):** Effective April 11, 2023, expanding automatic expungement to include certain felony convictions after ten years and misdemeanor convictions after seven years without subsequent offenses.

As of 2025, twelve states have enacted automatic expungement or record-clearing laws, with several additional states considering similar legislation.

### State Variation

| Feature | Most Restrictive States | Most Expansive States |
|---------|------------------------|----------------------|
| Eligible offenses | Limited to misdemeanors | Includes many felonies |
| Waiting period | 10-15 years | 1-5 years |
| Number of convictions | Single conviction only | Multiple convictions |
| Process | Petition-based with hearing | Automatic |
| Fee | $100-$500+ | Free or nominal |

### Ban the Box Movement

Complementing expungement reforms, the "Ban the Box" movement has led 37 states and over 150 cities and counties to adopt fair-chance hiring policies that delay criminal history inquiries until later in the hiring process. The federal government adopted Ban the Box for federal employment through the Fair Chance Act, effective December 20, 2021.

### Collateral Consequences

Research by the National Institute of Justice indicates that individuals with criminal records face over 44,000 collateral consequences across federal and state law, including barriers to employment, housing, education, professional licensing, voting rights, and public benefits. Expungement addresses only a portion of these consequences, as many statutory disqualifications are triggered by the existence of a conviction regardless of whether the record has been sealed.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's criminal record system is governed by the Federal Central Criminal Register Act (Bundeszentralregistergesetz -- BZRG), which establishes the Bundeszentralregister (Federal Central Criminal Register) maintained by the Federal Office of Justice (Bundesamt für Justiz) in Bonn. The system is designed around the principle that criminal convictions should not permanently stigmatize individuals and should be removed from public records after sufficient time has elapsed.

### Removal Periods

Under Sections 45-47 BZRG, convictions are automatically removed from the register after the following periods:

| Sentence | Removal Period |
|----------|---------------|
| Fine of up to 90 daily rates (first offense) | 5 years |
| Fine of more than 90 daily rates or imprisonment up to 3 months | 10 years |
| Imprisonment of more than 3 months up to 1 year | 10 years |
| Imprisonment of more than 1 year up to 3 years | 15 years |
| Imprisonment of more than 3 years | 20 years |
| Life imprisonment | Never removed |
| Convictions of sex offenders placed on sex offender register | Never removed while on register |

### Section 51 BZRG -- Prohibition on Use

Section 51 BZRG contains one of the most powerful rehabilitation provisions in any legal system: once a conviction has been removed from the register, "the fact of the conviction and the underlying facts may not be used against the person concerned in legal transactions." This provision creates an affirmative right not to be confronted with past criminal conduct, extending to:

- Employment decisions and job applications
- Housing applications
- Credit decisions
- Insurance applications
- Legal proceedings (with limited exceptions for criminal sentencing)

Violation of Section 51 may give rise to civil liability and, in certain circumstances, criminal liability for privacy violations under Section 203 StGB.

### Certificate of Good Conduct (Führungszeugnis)

The Führungszeugnis (certificate of good conduct) is the primary document through which criminal record information is disclosed to third parties. Section 32 BZRG provides that minor convictions (fines of up to 90 daily rates or imprisonment of up to 3 months for first offenses) are not included in the Führungszeugnis even before their removal from the register, creating an immediate rehabilitation effect for minor offenses.

### Extended Certificate (Erweitertes Führungszeugnis)

For individuals working with children and vulnerable persons, Section 30a BZRG provides for an extended certificate of good conduct that includes convictions for sexual offenses, offenses against personal liberty, and offenses against life and physical integrity regardless of the sentence imposed. This extended certificate was introduced in 2010 in response to institutional child abuse scandals.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Article 8 and Criminal Records

The European Court of Human Rights has established that the retention and disclosure of criminal record data engages the right to respect for private life under Article 8 of the Convention:

**M.M. v. United Kingdom, Application No. 24029/07, Judgment of November 13, 2012:** The Court found a violation of Article 8 in the United Kingdom's system of criminal record disclosure for employment purposes. The applicant had received a caution for child abduction (of her own child during a custody dispute), which was subsequently disclosed on an enhanced criminal record check required for her social care employment. The Court held that the disclosure scheme lacked adequate safeguards, was not sufficiently circumscribed, and was not "in accordance with the law" as required by Article 8(2).

**Brunet v. France, Application No. 21010/10, Judgment of October 18, 2014:** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where information about criminal proceedings that had been discontinued was retained in a police database for twenty years. The Court emphasized the importance of adequate procedures for challenging the accuracy and necessity of retained data.

### General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)

Article 17 of the GDPR (the "right to erasure" or "right to be forgotten") provides individuals with the right to request deletion of personal data, including criminal record information, when it is no longer necessary for the purposes for which it was collected. However, Article 17(3)(b) exempts data processing necessary for compliance with a legal obligation, limiting the direct applicability of the right to erasure to criminal records maintained by state authorities.

### Directive (EU) 2016/680

Directive 2016/680 on the protection of natural persons with regard to the processing of personal data by competent authorities for the purposes of prevention, investigation, detection, or prosecution of criminal offenses specifically governs criminal justice data processing. Article 5 requires that personal data be kept for no longer than necessary for the purposes of processing, and Article 16 provides data subjects with the right to rectification or erasure.

### European Criminal Records Information System (ECRIS)

ECRIS, operational since April 2012 under Council Framework Decision 2009/315/JHA, enables the electronic exchange of criminal record information between EU member states. The system allows a member state to request the complete criminal record of an EU national from that person's member state of nationality. ECRIS raises complex questions regarding the interaction between different national expungement standards: a conviction that has been expunged or removed in one member state may still be communicated through ECRIS if the requesting state's law does not recognize the expungement.

## Comparative Analysis

### Automatic vs. Petition-Based Systems

Germany and South Korea employ fully automatic systems in which records are removed or lapsed upon expiration of the waiting period without any action by the individual. The majority of U.S. states still require individuals to file petitions, appear in court, and often pay substantial fees -- creating a system that disproportionately burdens those least able to navigate legal processes. The U.S. Clean Slate movement represents a shift toward automatic systems, but adoption remains limited.

### Strength of Rehabilitation Effect

Germany's Section 51 BZRG provides the strongest rehabilitation effect, affirmatively prohibiting the use of removed convictions against the individual in virtually all contexts. South Korea's system is somewhat weaker, as records remain accessible to law enforcement and certain licensing authorities. U.S. expungement effects vary dramatically, with some states merely sealing records while others destroy them entirely.

### Waiting Periods

Waiting periods reflect different assumptions about the relevance of criminal history over time. South Korea's periods (5-10 years) are moderate. Germany's periods (5-20 years) are graduated based on sentence severity. U.S. periods vary enormously, from one year to fifteen years depending on the state and offense category.

### International Implications

Criminal record expungement raises particularly complex issues in the international context. Even where records are expunged domestically, they may be accessible through international channels such as ECRIS, Interpol, or bilateral information-sharing agreements, potentially undermining the rehabilitation purposes of domestic expungement laws.
