## Summary

Child custody -- the legal determination of which parent or guardian has the right and responsibility to make decisions for and physically care for a child following parental separation or divorce -- has undergone a global transformation from patriarchal presumptions favoring fathers, through maternal preference doctrines, to the now-dominant best interests of the child standard, though the practical application of this standard varies substantially across legal systems. South Korea governs custody through Articles 837 and 909 of the Civil Act (민법), requiring parents to agree on custody arrangements in consensual divorce and authorizing family courts to determine custody in judicial divorce based on the child's welfare (자녀의 복리); historically characterized by strong maternal custody awards, Korean courts have increasingly moved toward joint custody arrangements and enhanced paternal visitation rights, with the 2007 Civil Act amendments requiring comprehensive parenting plans covering custody, visitation, and child support as a prerequisite for consensual divorce. The United States applies the best interests of the child standard across all fifty states, with a marked trend since the 1970s away from maternal preference and toward joint custody presumptions -- as of 2025, over thirty states have enacted some form of presumptive joint custody -- while the Uniform Child Custody Jurisdiction and Enforcement Act (UCCJEA) governs interstate jurisdictional conflicts and the Parental Kidnapping Prevention Act (28 U.S.C. Section 1738A) requires interstate recognition of custody orders. Germany regulates custody through Sections 1626-1698b of the Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB), maintaining joint parental custody (gemeinsames Sorgerecht) as the default for married parents following divorce and providing since 2013 a pathway for unmarried fathers to obtain joint custody without the mother's consent, following the ECHR's judgment in Zaunegger v. Germany (2009) and the Bundesverfassungsgericht's subsequent decision declaring the prior maternal-consent requirement unconstitutional. The European framework includes the Hague Convention on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction (1980), the Brussels II ter Regulation (EU 2019/1111) on parental responsibility, the ECHR's extensive Article 8 jurisprudence on custody and contact rights, and the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child's requirement that the best interests of the child be a primary consideration in all actions concerning children.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Child custody in South Korea is governed primarily by Articles 837 (custody determination upon divorce), 909 (parental authority), and 912 (exercise of parental authority) of the Civil Act (민법).

**Article 837:** Upon divorce, the parents shall determine by agreement who shall have custody of any children. If no agreement is reached, or if agreement is impossible, the family court shall determine custody at the request of either parent. The court may modify custody arrangements at any time if circumstances require.

**Article 909(4):** In the case of divorce, the parents shall determine the person who exercises parental authority by agreement. If no agreement is reached, the family court shall designate the person.

### 2007 Parenting Plan Requirement

The 2007 Civil Act amendment (effective June 22, 2008) requires that parties seeking consensual divorce submit a comprehensive agreement on:

| Element | Required Content |
|---------|-----------------|
| Custodian designation | Which parent will have primary custody |
| Visitation (면접교섭권) | Specific schedule and conditions for non-custodial parent contact |
| Child support (양육비) | Amount, payment schedule, and duration |
| Parenting plan | Detailed plan for the child's upbringing, education, and welfare |

The family court judge reviews these agreements before confirming the divorce and may require modifications if the arrangements are deemed inadequate for the child's welfare.

### Custody Trends

Historically, Korean mothers have received sole custody in approximately 80 percent of divorce cases. However, recent trends show increasing acceptance of joint custody (공동양육권):

- In 2019, the Supreme Court emphasized that joint custody should be actively considered when both parents are suitable caregivers.
- The Ministry of Gender Equality and Family has promoted shared parenting models.
- Family courts have increasingly ordered detailed visitation schedules rather than vague "reasonable visitation" provisions.

### Visitation Rights (면접교섭권)

Article 837-2 guarantees the non-custodial parent's right to visitation (면접교섭권). The court may restrict or deny visitation only if it would harm the child's welfare. In practice, enforcement of visitation orders has been a persistent challenge. The 2021 amendment to the Family Litigation Act (가사소송법) strengthened enforcement mechanisms, including fines for obstruction of visitation.

### Grandparent Visitation

Unlike many Western jurisdictions, Korean law does not explicitly provide for grandparent visitation rights. However, the Civil Act's broad welfare provisions have been interpreted by some courts to permit grandparent visitation in exceptional circumstances, particularly following the death of the custodial parent.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Child custody in the United States is governed by state family law, with the best interests of the child serving as the universal standard. The Uniform Child Custody Jurisdiction and Enforcement Act (UCCJEA), adopted in some form by all fifty states, governs jurisdictional questions. Federal law addresses interstate enforcement through the Parental Kidnapping Prevention Act (PKPA, 28 U.S.C. Section 1738A).

### Best Interests Factors

While specific factors vary by state, common considerations include:

| Factor | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| Child's wishes | Preferences of sufficiently mature children (typically 12+) |
| Parental fitness | Mental and physical health of each parent |
| Stability | Continuity of education, community, and relationships |
| Parental cooperation | Willingness to facilitate relationship with other parent |
| Domestic violence | History of abuse (often a statutory factor) |
| Sibling relationships | Preference to keep siblings together |
| Parental involvement | Pre-separation caregiving history |

### Joint Custody Movement

The trend toward joint custody has accelerated dramatically:

- **1970s-1980s:** Joint custody statutes first enacted (California, 1979).
- **2000s:** Approximately 20 states established preferences or presumptions for joint custody.
- **2020-2025:** Over 30 states now have some form of joint custody presumption or preference, with Kentucky's 2018 equal-time presumption (KRS 403.270) and Arkansas's 2021 equal custody presumption among the most far-reaching.

### Relocation

Parental relocation -- where the custodial parent seeks to move with the child to a distant location -- is one of the most litigated custody issues. The leading framework is the ALI Principles of the Law of Family Dissolution (2002), which distinguishes between relocations within a "reasonable" distance and those requiring modification of custody. Most states require notice to the non-relocating parent and court approval for significant relocations, with the burden of proof varying by jurisdiction.

### Constitutional Dimensions

The Supreme Court has recognized parents' fundamental right to direct the care and upbringing of their children under the Fourteenth Amendment Due Process Clause (Troxel v. Granville, 530 U.S. 57 (2000)). This right limits state interference in parental decisions but does not dictate how custody should be allocated between two fit parents.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German child custody law is governed by Sections 1626-1698b BGB and procedural provisions in the Act on Proceedings in Family Matters (FamFG).

### Joint Parental Custody (gemeinsames Sorgerecht)

**Married Parents:** Joint custody continues automatically after divorce unless one parent applies for sole custody. The court may grant sole custody to one parent only if it serves the child's best interests (Section 1671 BGB). In practice, joint custody is maintained in approximately 95 percent of divorces.

**Unmarried Parents:** Before 2013, the mother of a child born outside marriage had sole custody, and the father could obtain joint custody only with the mother's consent. This was reformed following:

- **Zaunegger v. Germany (ECtHR, Application No. 22028/04, December 3, 2009):** The Court found a violation of Article 14 (discrimination) read with Article 8 (private and family life), holding that the requirement of the mother's consent for joint custody discriminated against unmarried fathers.
- **BVerfG Decision (1 BvR 420/09, July 21, 2010):** The Federal Constitutional Court declared the maternal-consent requirement unconstitutional.
- **Legislative Reform (2013):** Section 1626a BGB was amended to allow fathers to apply for joint custody without the mother's consent, with the family court deciding based on the child's best interests.

### Hearing of the Child (Section 159 FamFG)

German law requires that the child be heard in person by the court in custody proceedings if the child is at least 14 years old. Children under 14 should also be heard unless this would be contrary to their interests. The child's expressed preferences carry increasing weight with age but are not determinative.

### Residence Determination (Aufenthaltsbestimmungsrecht)

Joint custody does not necessarily mean equal physical custody. The Aufenthaltsbestimmungsrecht (right to determine the child's habitual residence) is a component of parental custody that may be exercised jointly or allocated to one parent. The Wechselmodell (alternating residence model) has gained increasing judicial recognition, with the BGH ruling in 2017 (BGH XII ZB 601/15) that family courts may order alternating residence even over one parent's objection if it serves the child's best interests.

### Child's Right to Contact (Umgangsrecht)

Section 1684 BGB establishes that the child has a right to contact with each parent, and each parent has both a right and a duty to contact with the child. Courts may restrict or exclude contact only if it would endanger the child's welfare. Section 1685 BGB provides grandparents and siblings with a right to contact if it serves the child's best interests.

## ECHR & EU

### UN Convention on the Rights of the Child

The UNCRC, ratified by all jurisdictions except the United States, establishes:

- **Article 3:** The best interests of the child shall be a primary consideration in all actions concerning children.
- **Article 9:** A child shall not be separated from their parents against their will, except when such separation is necessary for the best interests of the child.
- **Article 12:** The child has the right to express views freely in all matters affecting them, with views given due weight according to age and maturity.
- **Article 18:** Both parents have common responsibilities for the upbringing and development of the child.

### ECHR Article 8 Jurisprudence

The ECHR has developed extensive custody-related jurisprudence under Article 8:

**Neulinger and Shuruk v. Switzerland (Application No. 41615/07, Grand Chamber, July 6, 2010):** The Court held that in custody and return proceedings, the best interests of the child must be the paramount consideration, and that the domestic authorities must conduct an in-depth examination of the entire family situation.

**Mennesson v. France (Application No. 65192/11, June 26, 2014):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where France refused to recognize parent-child relationships established abroad through surrogacy, emphasizing the child's right to identity and family life.

**Strand Lobben v. Norway (Application No. 37283/13, Grand Chamber, September 10, 2019):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 in the adoption of a child without the biological mother's consent, emphasizing that the best interests of the child include preserving family ties unless particularly unfit.

### Brussels II ter Regulation (EU 2019/1111)

The Brussels II ter Regulation governs cross-border parental responsibility matters, including:

- Jurisdiction based on the child's habitual residence (Article 7)
- Automatic recognition of custody judgments across member states (Article 30)
- Enhanced enforcement procedures for access rights (Articles 42-43)
- Return proceedings in international child abduction cases (Articles 22-29)

### Hague Convention on International Child Abduction (1980)

The Hague Convention on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction, to which South Korea acceded in 2013 (effective March 1, 2013), the United States ratified in 1988, and Germany ratified in 1990, provides for the prompt return of children wrongfully removed to or retained in a contracting state. The Convention does not determine custody merits but ensures that custody disputes are resolved in the child's state of habitual residence. Exceptions to return include grave risk of harm (Article 13(b)) and the child's own objections (if of sufficient age and maturity).

## Comparative Analysis

### Best Interests Standard

All four jurisdictions employ some version of the best interests standard, but its application differs. The United States has the most developed list-based approach, with state statutes enumerating specific factors. Germany emphasizes procedural fairness and the child's hearing. South Korea focuses on broad welfare considerations with significant judicial discretion. The ECHR applies the best interests standard as a framework for reviewing national decisions rather than as a directly applicable rule.

### Joint vs. Sole Custody Trends

Germany maintains the strongest joint custody framework, with automatic continuation of joint custody after divorce and no presumption of sole custody. The United States has rapidly moved toward joint custody presumptions through state legislation. South Korea, despite lacking a formal joint custody presumption, is seeing increasing judicial awards of joint custody. The ECHR jurisprudence has pushed toward equal parental rights, particularly for unmarried fathers, through decisions like Zaunegger.

### Enforcement Challenges

Enforcement of custody and visitation orders remains a universal challenge. South Korea's strengthened enforcement mechanisms (2021) address a persistent problem of visitation obstruction. U.S. states employ contempt proceedings and the UCCJEA's enforcement provisions. Germany uses Ordnungsgeld (coercive fines) under FamFG Section 89. Cross-border enforcement is facilitated by the Brussels II ter Regulation in the EU and the Hague Convention globally.

### The Child's Voice

Germany provides the strongest procedural guarantee for hearing the child (mandatory for children 14+). The UNCRC's Article 12 right is implemented differently across jurisdictions: U.S. states vary in the age at which children's preferences are formally considered, South Korea has no fixed age threshold, and the ECHR requires that children be given the opportunity to be heard in proceedings affecting them.
