## Summary

Legal standing -- the requirement that a party demonstrate a sufficient connection to and harm from the law or action challenged to justify their participation in litigation -- is a foundational doctrine of procedural law that determines who may invoke judicial power and under what circumstances, with profound implications for access to justice, separation of powers, and the enforcement of public law. South Korea's standing doctrine operates through the Administrative Litigation Act (행정소송법, Act No. 951, 1951, comprehensively amended 1984) Article 12 requiring a "legal interest" (법률상 이익) for standing to challenge administrative actions, supplemented by the Constitutional Court Act (헌법재판소법) Article 68 establishing standing requirements for constitutional complaints, with the Korean Supreme Court (대법원) and Constitutional Court (헌법재판소) progressively expanding standing to include interests protected by the purpose of the relevant statute rather than only direct legal rights, most recently in landmark decisions addressing environmental standing and consumer standing. The United States applies the most restrictive standing doctrine among Western democracies, rooted in Article III of the Constitution's "case or controversy" requirement and elaborated through the three-part test from Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife (1992) requiring injury in fact, causation, and redressability, with the Supreme Court further tightening standing requirements in TransUnion LLC v. Ramirez (2021) by requiring plaintiffs to demonstrate concrete harm analogous to traditional causes of action. Germany applies a more permissive standing framework under the principle of subjective rights protection (Individualrechtsschutz), requiring a violation of subjective public rights (subjektive öffentliche Rechte) under VwGO Section 42(2) for administrative litigation, while the Federal Constitutional Court applies a broader standing test for constitutional complaints under Article 93(1)(4a) GG requiring only that the complainant be "self, presently, and directly" affected (selbst, gegenwärtig und unmittelbar betroffen). The ECHR requires applicants to demonstrate "victim" status under Article 34, which the Court has interpreted to include both direct and indirect victims, while EU law provides standing for individuals to challenge EU acts under Article 263(4) TFEU, with the Court of Justice's restrictive interpretation of "direct and individual concern" in the Plaumann test (1963) remaining controversial despite partial liberalization through the Lisbon Treaty's addition of "regulatory acts" standing.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's standing doctrine operates through two primary channels: administrative litigation and constitutional complaint.

**Administrative Litigation Act (행정소송법) Article 12 (원고적격):** "An action for revocation may be brought by a person who has a legal interest in seeking the revocation of a disposition, etc." (처분등의 취소를 구할 법률상 이익이 있는 자). The interpretation of "legal interest" (법률상 이익) has been the central question in Korean standing jurisprudence.

**Constitutional Court Act (헌법재판소법) Article 68(1):** Any person whose fundamental rights guaranteed by the Constitution have been violated by an exercise or non-exercise of governmental power may file a constitutional complaint, provided all available legal remedies have been exhausted.

### Evolution of "Legal Interest"

The Korean Supreme Court has progressively expanded the concept of "legal interest" beyond direct legal rights:

**Supreme Court Decision 2006Du330 (2006) -- Environmental Standing:** The Court held that residents living near a proposed development project had standing to challenge the environmental impact assessment, recognizing that the "legal interest" requirement encompasses interests protected by the regulatory purpose of the relevant statute, including environmental quality interests protected by the Environmental Impact Assessment Act (환경영향평가법).

**Supreme Court Decision 2015Du46598 (2018) -- Consumer Standing:** The Court recognized standing for a consumer organization to challenge a government approval of a pharmaceutical product, holding that the organization had legal interest in ensuring the safety of consumer products where the relevant statute was designed to protect consumer interests.

**Supreme Court Decision 2012Du28704 (2014) -- Competitor Standing:** The Court held that a business competitor has standing to challenge an administrative licensing decision where the relevant statute protects competitive interests, applying the "protective norm" (보호규범) theory borrowed from German administrative law.

### Constitutional Court Standing

The Constitutional Court applies a four-part test for standing in constitutional complaints:

1. **Self-relatedness (자기관련성):** The complainant's own fundamental rights must be affected
2. **Present effect (현재관련성):** The rights violation must be current, not merely potential
3. **Direct effect (직접관련성):** The violation must result directly from the governmental act without need for an intervening implementing act
4. **Legal interest in the complaint (권리보호이익):** The complainant must have a continuing interest in the resolution of the complaint

**Constitutional Court Decision 2011Hun-Ma814 (2014) -- Climate Change Standing:** In a notable expansion, the Constitutional Court accepted a constitutional complaint by environmental organizations and individuals challenging government climate change policy, recognizing standing based on the right to a healthy environment under Article 35 of the Constitution.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States applies the most restrictive standing doctrine among the four jurisdictions, rooted in constitutional, prudential, and statutory requirements.

**Article III Standing (Constitutional Minimum):** Under Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife, 504 U.S. 555 (1992), a plaintiff must demonstrate three elements:

1. **Injury in Fact:** A concrete, particularized, and actual or imminent invasion of a legally protected interest
2. **Causation (Traceability):** The injury must be fairly traceable to the challenged conduct of the defendant
3. **Redressability:** It must be likely, not merely speculative, that the injury will be redressed by a favorable decision

**TransUnion LLC v. Ramirez, 594 U.S. 413 (2021):** The Supreme Court (5-4) held that to establish Article III standing, plaintiffs must demonstrate concrete harm -- meaning harm that bears a close relationship to a harm traditionally recognized by American courts. The majority held that approximately 6,332 of the 8,185 class members lacked standing because their inaccurate credit files were never disseminated to third parties, and thus they suffered no concrete harm despite a statutory violation of the Fair Credit Reporting Act. This decision significantly restricted standing for statutory violations where Congress has created a private right of action.

### Prudential Standing Limitations

Beyond Article III requirements, federal courts apply additional prudential standing limitations:
- **Zone of Interests:** The plaintiff's injury must arguably fall within the "zone of interests" protected by the statute invoked (Association of Data Processing Service Organizations v. Camp, 1970)
- **Third-Party Standing (Jus Tertii):** Parties generally cannot assert the rights of third parties absent a close relationship and obstacle to the third party's own assertion (Kowalski v. Tesmer, 2004)
- **Generalized Grievances:** Courts disfavor claims based on generalized grievances shared by all citizens

### Congressional Standing

Members of Congress have very limited standing to challenge executive action. In Raines v. Byrd, 521 U.S. 811 (1997), the Supreme Court held that individual legislators lacked standing to challenge the Line Item Veto Act, as their alleged injury was not sufficiently concrete and particularized.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany applies a standing doctrine based on the protection of subjective public rights (subjektive öffentliche Rechte), less restrictive than the U.S. approach but more structured than open-standing systems.

**VwGO Section 42(2) (Klagebefugnis):** "Unless otherwise provided by statute, the action is admissible only if the plaintiff alleges that his rights have been violated by the administrative act or its refusal or omission." This provision enshrines the Individualrechtsschutz (individual rights protection) principle: administrative courts protect individual rights, not objective legality.

**Protective Norm Theory (Schutznormtheorie):** Standing depends on whether the violated legal norm is intended to protect the interests of the specific plaintiff (or the class of persons to which the plaintiff belongs). A plaintiff may challenge an administrative act only if it violates a norm that is designed to protect the plaintiff's individual interests, not merely the public interest. This theory, developed by the Federal Administrative Court (Bundesverwaltungsgericht), is the core analytical tool for German standing analysis.

**Article 19(4) GG (Rechtsschutzgarantie):** "Should any person's rights be violated by public authority, he may have recourse to the courts." This constitutional guarantee of legal protection requires that standing rules not effectively foreclose judicial review of governmental action affecting individual rights.

### Constitutional Complaint Standing

For constitutional complaints to the Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht, BVerfG), the complainant must be "selbst, gegenwärtig und unmittelbar betroffen" (self, presently, and directly affected):

- **Self-affected (selbst betroffen):** The complainant's own fundamental rights must be affected
- **Presently affected (gegenwärtig betroffen):** The rights violation must be current
- **Directly affected (unmittelbar betroffen):** The act must directly affect the complainant without need for an implementing measure

**BVerfGE 53, 30 (1979) -- Mülheim-Kärlich Nuclear Power Plant:** The Federal Constitutional Court recognized standing for neighboring residents to challenge a nuclear power plant construction permit, holding that the right to life and physical integrity (Article 2(2) GG) creates standing to challenge governmental actions that create risks to personal safety.

### Environmental Standing -- Verbandsklage

The Umwelt-Rechtsbehelfsgesetz (Environmental Remedies Act, 2006), transposing EU Directive 2003/35/EC and implementing the Aarhus Convention, introduced a collective environmental action right (Verbandsklage) permitting recognized environmental organizations to challenge administrative decisions that violate environmental law, even without demonstrating a violation of their own subjective rights. This represents a significant departure from the Individualrechtsschutz principle.

**BVerwGE 147, 312 (2013):** The Federal Administrative Court expanded the scope of the Verbandsklage, holding that environmental organizations may challenge not only the procedural adequacy of environmental impact assessments but also substantive compliance with environmental protection norms.

## ECHR / EU

### ECHR Article 34 -- Victim Status

Article 34 ECHR provides: "The Court may receive applications from any person, non-governmental organisation or group of individuals claiming to be the victim of a violation by one of the High Contracting Parties."

**Direct Victims:** Persons directly affected by the alleged violation.

**Indirect Victims:** The Court recognizes indirect victim status for close family members of direct victims, particularly in cases involving the right to life (Article 2) and prohibition of torture (Article 3). In Centre for Legal Resources on Behalf of Valentin Câmpeanu v. Romania (Application No. 47848/08, Grand Chamber, July 17, 2014), the Court exceptionally recognized standing for an NGO to bring a claim on behalf of a deceased person with no known family members.

**Potential Victims:** In Dudgeon v. United Kingdom (Application No. 7525/76, October 22, 1981), the Court recognized that a person may claim to be a victim even if they have not been directly subjected to the challenged measure, provided they are at real risk of being directly affected.

**No Actio Popularis:** The Court consistently rejects actio popularis claims. In Burden v. United Kingdom (Application No. 13378/05, Grand Chamber, April 29, 2008), the Court confirmed that applicants must demonstrate that they are personally affected by the alleged violation.

### EU Article 263(4) TFEU -- Standing to Challenge EU Acts

**Plaumann Test (Case 25/62, Plaumann v. Commission, 1963):** The CJEU established the highly restrictive test that an individual has standing to challenge an EU act only if it affects them "by reason of certain attributes which are peculiar to them or by reason of circumstances in which they are differentiated from all other persons." This test has been repeatedly criticized for being excessively restrictive.

**Lisbon Treaty Amendment:** Article 263(4) TFEU, as amended by the Lisbon Treaty (2009), added standing to challenge "regulatory acts which are of direct concern to [the applicant] and do not entail implementing measures," eliminating the individual concern requirement for this category of acts. In Inuit Tapiriit Kanatami v. Parliament and Council (Case C-583/11 P, 2013), the CJEU defined "regulatory act" as a non-legislative act of general application, limiting the scope of this liberalization.

### Aarhus Convention

The UNECE Convention on Access to Information, Public Participation in Decision-Making and Access to Justice in Environmental Matters (Aarhus, 1998) requires parties to ensure broad standing for environmental claims. The CJEU in Protect Natur- (Case C-664/15, 2017) held that national standing rules in environmental matters must be interpreted in conformity with the Aarhus Convention's access-to-justice requirements.

## Comparative Analysis

### Restrictiveness Spectrum

The United States applies the most restrictive standing doctrine, requiring concrete injury, causation, and redressability as constitutional minimums. Germany's Schutznormtheorie is moderately restrictive, requiring violation of a protective norm. South Korea's "legal interest" test has been progressively liberalized. The ECHR's "victim" status requirement is relatively flexible, recognizing indirect and potential victims.

### Environmental Standing

All four jurisdictions have expanded environmental standing, but through different mechanisms. The United States recognizes organizational standing through the Sierra Club v. Morton/Hunt framework. Germany introduced the Verbandsklage, departing from its traditional individual-rights model. South Korea has expanded "legal interest" to include environmental quality. The EU's Aarhus Convention implementation requires broad environmental standing across member states.

### Separation of Powers

The standing doctrine reflects different conceptions of the judicial role. The U.S. approach, rooted in Article III, is explicitly linked to separation of powers -- standing limits judicial power to "cases and controversies" to prevent courts from functioning as general supervisors of government. Germany's approach reflects the Rechtsschutzgarantie (legal protection guarantee), viewing standing as a mechanism to ensure judicial protection of individual rights. South Korea's approach is evolving from a rights-based model toward a broader interest-based model. The ECHR's approach focuses on ensuring effective individual access to the Convention's protective mechanisms.
