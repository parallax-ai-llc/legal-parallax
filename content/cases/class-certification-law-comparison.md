## Summary

Class certification -- the judicial process by which a court determines whether a lawsuit may proceed as a collective action on behalf of a group of similarly situated individuals -- is among the most powerful and controversial mechanisms in civil procedure, enabling mass enforcement of rights while raising fundamental questions about due process, litigation economics, and access to justice. South Korea introduced a securities class action system through the Securities-Related Class Action Act (증권관련 집단소송법, Act No. 7074, 2004, effective January 1, 2005) and subsequently enacted the broader Consumer Class Action Act (소비자단체소송법) as part of the Framework Act on Consumer Policy (소비자기본법) in 2006, with the class action mechanism limited to specific statutory categories and requiring court permission to proceed, while the Korean Supreme Court (대법원) has developed certification standards addressing commonality, typicality, and adequacy of representation. The United States operates the most expansive class action system globally, governed by Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23 and the Class Action Fairness Act of 2005 (CAFA, 28 U.S.C. Section 1332(d)), with the Supreme Court significantly tightening certification requirements through decisions including Wal-Mart v. Dukes (2011), Comcast v. Behrend (2013), and TransUnion v. Ramirez (2021). Germany does not have a traditional American-style class action but has developed collective litigation mechanisms including the Model Declaratory Action (Musterfeststellungsklage) introduced in 2018 under ZPO Sections 606-614 and the Capital Markets Model Case Act (Kapitalanleger-Musterverfahrensgesetz, KapMuG), while the EU's Representative Actions Directive (EU) 2020/1828 requires all member states to establish representative action mechanisms for consumer protection by June 25, 2023. The ECHR's right to a fair trial under Article 6 provides the overarching framework, with the Court addressing due process in collective proceedings, while the EU has moved from skepticism about American-style class actions toward embracing European-style collective redress mechanisms that balance effective enforcement with safeguards against abusive litigation.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea has adopted a limited, sector-specific approach to class actions rather than a general class action procedure.

**Securities-Related Class Action Act (증권관련 집단소송법, 2004):** This Act, the first true class action statute in Korean law, permits class actions exclusively for securities-related claims including false disclosure in securities registration statements, insider trading, market manipulation, and auditor negligence. Key provisions:

- **Article 3 (Standing / 원고적격):** A class action may be brought by a member of the class who holds securities issued by the defendant, provided the class comprises 50 or more members.
- **Article 12 (Permission / 소송허가):** The court must grant permission to proceed as a class action, determining that: (1) the class members share common interests of fact and law; (2) the class action is superior to individual litigation for the fair and efficient resolution of the dispute; (3) the representative plaintiff will fairly and adequately represent the interests of the class; and (4) the representative plaintiff's attorney is qualified to conduct the class action.
- **Article 34 (Opt-In / 가입):** The Korean securities class action follows an opt-in model: class members must affirmatively join the action within a court-specified period to be bound by the judgment.
- **Article 35 (Judgment Effect / 판결의 효력):** The judgment binds only those class members who opted in. Non-participating class members retain the right to bring individual actions.

**Consumer Class Action (소비자단체소송):** Under the Framework Act on Consumer Policy (소비자기본법) Articles 70-76, qualified consumer organizations may bring injunctive actions on behalf of consumers to stop unlawful business practices. However, this mechanism is limited to injunctive relief and does not permit class-wide damages awards. Only organizations registered with the Fair Trade Commission and meeting specified criteria may bring such actions.

### Landmark Cases

**Seoul Central District Court Decision 2009Gahap116019 (2012) -- Samsung Securities Case:** One of the first significant securities class actions in Korea, involving allegations of false disclosure in Samsung C&T Corporation's financial statements. The court granted class action permission, establishing important precedent on the adequacy of representation and commonality requirements.

**Supreme Court Decision 2015Da248342 (2018):** The Supreme Court clarified that the "superiority" requirement for class action permission requires comparison with alternative dispute resolution mechanisms and individual litigation, and that class actions are appropriate where individual claims are small relative to litigation costs, making individual actions economically impractical.

### Statistics

Since the Securities Class Action Act's enactment in 2005, approximately 30 securities class actions have been filed, with a relatively low permission grant rate (approximately 40 percent). The limited scope of class action eligibility and the opt-in requirement have been criticized as insufficiently protective of investor interests.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has the most expansive and well-developed class action system globally, governed primarily by Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23.

**Rule 23(a) -- Prerequisites:** All class actions must satisfy four prerequisites:
1. **Numerosity:** The class is so numerous that joinder of all members is impracticable
2. **Commonality:** There are questions of law or fact common to the class
3. **Typicality:** The claims of the representative parties are typical of the class
4. **Adequacy:** The representative parties will fairly and adequately protect the interests of the class

**Rule 23(b) -- Types of Class Actions:**
- **Rule 23(b)(1):** Prosecuting separate actions would create a risk of inconsistent adjudications or would impair the interests of non-party class members
- **Rule 23(b)(2):** The defendant has acted on grounds generally applicable to the class, making injunctive or declaratory relief appropriate for the class as a whole
- **Rule 23(b)(3):** Common questions of law or fact predominate over individual questions, and a class action is superior to other methods of adjudication (the damages class action)

### Key Supreme Court Decisions

**Wal-Mart Stores, Inc. v. Dukes, 564 U.S. 338 (2011):** The Supreme Court decertified the largest employment discrimination class in history (1.5 million female employees), holding that commonality under Rule 23(a)(2) requires proof that the class members' claims "depend upon a common contention" that is "capable of classwide resolution" -- meaning "determination of its truth or falsity will resolve an issue that is central to the validity of each one of the claims in one stroke."

**Comcast Corp. v. Behrend, 569 U.S. 27 (2013):** The Court held that at the class certification stage, plaintiffs must demonstrate that damages are capable of measurement on a classwide basis consistent with the theory of liability. The decision heightened the certification requirement for damages class actions.

**Epic Systems Corp. v. Lewis, 584 U.S. 497 (2018):** The Court held that employment arbitration agreements waiving class and collective actions are enforceable under the Federal Arbitration Act, effectively eliminating the class action mechanism for many employment disputes.

**TransUnion LLC v. Ramirez, 594 U.S. 413 (2021):** While primarily a standing decision, this case significantly impacts class actions by requiring that every class member demonstrate concrete injury -- not merely a statutory violation -- to establish Article III standing.

### Class Action Fairness Act (CAFA, 2005)

CAFA (28 U.S.C. Section 1332(d)) expanded federal jurisdiction over class actions by establishing minimal diversity jurisdiction for class actions where: (1) the amount in controversy exceeds $5 million; (2) any member of the plaintiff class is a citizen of a different state from any defendant; and (3) the class has at least 100 members. CAFA was designed to prevent "forum shopping" in plaintiff-friendly state courts.

### Settlement Dynamics

Class action settlements in the United States generate enormous economic consequences. According to the Stanford Securities Class Action Clearinghouse, securities class action settlements totaled approximately $5 billion in 2023. Attorneys' fees in class action settlements typically range from 25 to 33 percent of the recovery, with courts reviewing and approving fees under Rule 23(e).

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany has historically resisted American-style class actions, viewing them as incompatible with the civil law tradition and raising concerns about litigation abuse. However, mounting pressure from mass harm events and EU harmonization has led to the introduction of collective litigation mechanisms.

**Kapitalanleger-Musterverfahrensgesetz (KapMuG, 2005):** The Capital Markets Model Case Act, enacted in response to the Deutsche Telekom mass litigation involving approximately 17,000 individual claims, establishes a model case procedure for securities disputes. Under KapMuG:

- Individual plaintiffs file separate actions in the Landgericht
- If 10 or more claims raise the same questions of fact or law, the court refers the common questions to the Oberlandesgericht (Higher Regional Court) for determination in a model case (Musterverfahren)
- The model case determination is binding on all individual pending proceedings involving the same questions
- Individual damages are assessed in the original proceedings after the model case decision

**Musterfeststellungsklage (Model Declaratory Action, ZPO Sections 606-614, 2018):** Introduced on November 1, 2018, primarily in response to the Volkswagen "Dieselgate" scandal, this mechanism allows qualified consumer associations to bring declaratory actions establishing facts and legal questions relevant to at least 50 consumers:

- **Section 606 (Admissibility):** Only qualified entities listed in the Federal Office of Justice's register may bring a Musterfeststellungsklage
- **Section 607 (Registration):** Affected consumers must register their claims in a publicly accessible register (Klageregister) maintained by the Federal Office of Justice within two months of the filing
- **Section 608 (Opt-In):** At least 50 consumers must register for the action to proceed
- **Section 611 (Binding Effect):** The declaratory judgment binds all registered claimants, who must then pursue individual follow-on actions for damages
- **Section 612 (Settlement):** Settlements must be approved by the court and accepted by a qualified majority of registered claimants

### Volkswagen Dieselgate Litigation

The first and most significant Musterfeststellungsklage was filed by the Federation of German Consumer Organisations (Verbraucherzentrale Bundesverband, vzbv) against Volkswagen AG on November 1, 2018. Approximately 470,000 vehicle owners registered their claims. The case settled on February 28, 2020, with Volkswagen agreeing to pay a total of EUR 830 million, providing individual payments ranging from EUR 1,350 to EUR 6,257 depending on the vehicle model and age.

### EU Representative Actions Directive Implementation

Germany transposed Directive (EU) 2020/1828 through the Verbraucherrechtedurchsetzungsgesetz (Consumer Rights Enforcement Act), effective October 13, 2023. This Act introduced the Abhilfeklage (redress action), allowing qualified entities to seek not only declaratory relief but also direct damages, payment, and other redress on behalf of consumers. The Abhilfeklage represents a significant expansion beyond the Musterfeststellungsklage by enabling direct monetary recovery.

## ECHR / EU

### EU Representative Actions Directive (EU) 2020/1828

Adopted on November 25, 2020, the Directive requires all member states to establish representative action mechanisms for consumer protection:

**Article 3 (Qualified Entities):** Member states must designate qualified entities (consumer organizations, public bodies, or ad hoc entities) that may bring representative actions. Entities must demonstrate independence from third-party funding sources, have a legitimate interest in protecting consumer interests, and be nonprofit in character.

**Article 7 (Domestic Representative Actions):** Qualified entities may bring representative actions seeking injunctive measures (Article 8) or redress measures (Article 9) including compensation, repair, replacement, price reduction, contract termination, or reimbursement.

**Article 9 (Redress Measures):** Member states must ensure that qualified entities can obtain redress orders requiring the trader to provide remedies to affected consumers. Member states may choose between opt-in and opt-out mechanisms for the redress order.

**Article 10 (Funding):** The Directive requires transparency regarding funding sources and prohibits third-party funders from influencing procedural decisions. The claimant-pays principle and prohibition of punitive damages are maintained.

**Transposition Deadline:** June 25, 2023 (application from June 25, 2023).

### ECHR Due Process in Collective Proceedings

**Lithgow and Others v. United Kingdom (Application No. 9006/80, July 8, 1986):** While not a class action case, the Court's analysis of grouped proceedings established that Article 6 due process protections apply to all forms of collective adjudication.

**Eskelinen v. Finland (Application No. 63235/00, Grand Chamber, April 19, 2007):** The Grand Chamber established that the right of access to court under Article 6(1) cannot be restricted in a manner that impairs its very essence, a principle applicable to class certification decisions that effectively determine whether individuals can access justice for small-value claims.

### European Commission's Approach

The EU's approach to collective redress has evolved from strong skepticism about American-style class actions to cautious embrace of European-style mechanisms. The 2013 Commission Recommendation on collective redress established principles including the "loser pays" principle, prohibition of punitive damages, prohibition of contingency fees, and preference for opt-in mechanisms. The 2020 Directive partially retreated from these positions by permitting opt-out mechanisms and allowing third-party funding with safeguards.

## Comparative Analysis

### Opt-In vs. Opt-Out

The fundamental structural difference between the systems is the default participation mechanism. The United States uses opt-out (all class members are included unless they affirmatively exclude themselves), maximizing class size and leverage. South Korea (securities), Germany, and the EU Directive default to opt-in (members must affirmatively join). Opt-out systems capture a larger proportion of affected individuals but raise due process concerns about binding absent class members; opt-in systems respect individual autonomy but typically result in much lower participation rates.

### Scope of Available Relief

The United States permits class-wide damages in certified class actions. Germany's Musterfeststellungsklage provides only declaratory relief (though the new Abhilfeklage allows redress). South Korea's consumer class action is limited to injunctive relief (securities class actions permit damages). The EU Directive requires member states to provide redress mechanisms, representing a significant advance.

### Economic Incentives

The United States' contingency fee system (typically 25-33 percent of recovery) creates powerful economic incentives for attorneys to pursue class actions. None of the other jurisdictions permit contingency fees, fundamentally altering the economic dynamics of collective litigation. Third-party litigation funding is emerging as an alternative financing mechanism in Germany and under the EU Directive.

### Certification Rigor

The United States has the most rigorous certification process, with the Supreme Court requiring merits inquiry at the certification stage (Wal-Mart, Comcast). South Korea requires court permission with criteria similar to Rule 23. Germany's model procedures do not require formal class certification. The EU Directive requires qualified entity status but not judicial certification of the class.
