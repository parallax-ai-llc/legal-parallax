## Summary

Organ donation (장기 기증/이식) -- the legal framework governing the procurement, allocation, and transplantation of human organs from living and deceased donors -- is a critical area of healthcare law that balances the acute shortage of transplantable organs against ethical principles of bodily autonomy, informed consent, equitable allocation, and the prohibition of commercial trafficking. South Korea regulates organ transplantation through the Internal Organs, Etc. Transplant Act (장기등 이식에 관한 법률, enacted 2000), which establishes the Korean Network for Organ Sharing (KONOS, 국립장기조직혈액관리원) as the national organ procurement and allocation organization, implements an opt-in consent system with family consent provisions, and criminalizes organ trafficking; Korea's donation rates have historically been among the lowest in the OECD, though legislative reforms and public awareness campaigns have gradually increased deceased donation from approximately 8.7 per million population in 2015 to approximately 12.3 in 2024. The United States enacted the National Organ Transplant Act (NOTA, 42 U.S.C. Sections 273-274e, 1984) establishing the Organ Procurement and Transplantation Network (OPTN) administered by the United Network for Organ Sharing (UNOS), implementing an opt-in consent system with the Uniform Anatomical Gift Act (UAGA) providing the state-level framework for donation decisions; the 2024 OPTN modernization transferred oversight from UNOS to HHS with new contractors. Germany operates under the Transplantationsgesetz (TPG, Transplantation Act, 1997, substantially reformed in 2019), which implements a "decision solution" (Entscheidungslösung) requiring health insurers to regularly prompt citizens to register their organ donation decision, after a failed 2020 legislative attempt to introduce presumed consent (Widerspruchslösung); Germany's deceased donation rate of approximately 10.3 per million population remains among the lowest in the EU. At the European level, the EU Directive 2010/53/EU on standards of quality and safety for human organs intended for transplantation establishes harmonized quality standards, the Council of Europe's Convention against Trafficking in Human Organs (2015) criminalizes organ trafficking, and the European Court of Human Rights has addressed organ donation-related issues through its Articles 2, 3, and 8 jurisprudence.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

**Internal Organs, Etc. Transplant Act (장기등 이식에 관한 법률):** Enacted in February 2000, this Act provides the comprehensive legal framework for organ procurement, allocation, and transplantation. Key provisions:

- **Consent Requirements:** Organ procurement from deceased donors requires either the decedent's prior registered consent (사전 동의) or, in the absence of registered consent, family consent. The hierarchy of family consent follows: spouse, direct lineal descendants, direct lineal ascendants, siblings.
- **Brain Death Determination:** Brain death (뇌사) must be certified by a Brain Death Determination Committee (뇌사판정위원회) consisting of at least two physicians, following specific medical criteria. Brain death determination triggers the organ procurement process.
- **Living Donation Restrictions:** Living donation is permitted only among family members (within the fourth degree of kinship) or persons who have maintained a close personal relationship for a specified period. Anonymous altruistic donation is not permitted under current law.

### KONOS (국립장기조직혈액관리원)

The Korean Network for Organ Sharing, established under the Ministry of Health and Welfare (보건복지부), serves as the national organ procurement and allocation organization. KONOS maintains the national waiting list, coordinates organ allocation according to medical urgency and compatibility criteria, manages the organ donor registry, and oversees transplant center quality.

### Donation Rate Challenges

South Korea's deceased donation rate has historically lagged behind other OECD nations, attributed to cultural factors including Confucian beliefs about bodily integrity (신체발부 수지부모), insufficient infrastructure for brain death identification, and limited public awareness. Legislative reforms in 2010 and 2017 expanded brain death identification protocols and incentivized hospital participation. The government launched the "Life Sharing" (생명나눔) campaign, and donation rates have gradually increased from 8.7 per million in 2015 to approximately 12.3 per million in 2024.

### Organ Trafficking Prohibition

The Act criminalizes organ trafficking with penalties of up to 10 years imprisonment. Article 11 prohibits giving or receiving compensation for organs, with exceptions for reimbursement of documented medical expenses and lost income for living donors. Korean law enforcement has investigated cases involving Korean nationals traveling abroad for transplant tourism, and the government cooperates with international efforts against organ trafficking.

## United States

### Legal Framework

**National Organ Transplant Act (NOTA, 42 U.S.C. Sections 273-274e, 1984):** NOTA established the national framework for organ procurement and transplantation, creating the Organ Procurement and Transplantation Network (OPTN) and prohibiting the sale of human organs.

**Organ Procurement and Transplantation Network (OPTN):** The OPTN maintains the national organ waiting list, develops allocation policies, and collects transplantation data. Until 2024, UNOS (United Network for Organ Sharing) served as the sole OPTN contractor since 1986. The OPTN Modernization Act of 2024 restructured the system, transferring oversight to HHS and enabling multiple contractors for different OPTN functions (technology, allocation policy, data management).

**Uniform Anatomical Gift Act (UAGA):** The UAGA, revised in 2006 and adopted by all 50 states and the District of Columbia, provides the state-level framework for organ donation decisions. Key principles:
- First-person consent (donor's own documented decision) is legally binding and cannot be overridden by family objection.
- Donation can be registered through driver's licenses, donor registries, or advance directives.
- In the absence of first-person consent, next of kin may authorize donation.

### Allocation System

OPTN allocation policies prioritize medical urgency, compatibility, time on the waiting list, and geographic proximity. The 2023 policy reforms replaced the previous concentric-circle geographic allocation system with a more distance-based model designed to reduce geographic disparities and organ waste.

### Organ Shortage and Policy Responses

As of 2025, over 100,000 patients are on the US organ waiting list, with approximately 17 people dying daily while waiting. Policy responses include:

| Initiative | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Paired kidney exchange | Enables incompatible donor-recipient pairs to swap donors |
| HOPE Act (2013) | Permits organ donation from HIV-positive donors to HIV-positive recipients |
| Organ donor tax credit | Several states offer tax credits for living donor expenses |
| OPO accountability reforms | CMS rules (2020) establishing performance benchmarks for organ procurement organizations |
| Xenotransplantation research | FDA-regulated clinical trials of pig-to-human organ transplants |

### Prohibition on Organ Sales

NOTA Section 274e criminalizes the transfer of human organs for "valuable consideration," with penalties of up to 5 years imprisonment and $50,000 fine. The statute exempts reasonable payments for removal, transportation, implantation, and storage. Academic debate over legalizing organ markets continues, with proposals including regulated markets, tax incentives, and guaranteed medical coverage for living donors.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

**Transplantationsgesetz (TPG, Transplantation Act, 1997):** Enacted on November 5, 1997, and substantially reformed through the Gesetz zur Stärkung der Entscheidungsbereitschaft bei der Organspende (Act to Strengthen Decision-Readiness in Organ Donation, 2020), the TPG governs all aspects of organ transplantation in Germany.

### The "Decision Solution" (Entscheidungslösung)

Germany's consent model underwent significant legislative debate in 2019-2020:

**Failed Presumed Consent Proposal (Widerspruchslösung):** In January 2020, the Bundestag voted on a proposal by Health Minister Jens Spahn to introduce presumed consent (opt-out), under which all adults would be considered potential organ donors unless they had registered an objection. The proposal was defeated 379-292, with opponents citing bodily autonomy concerns and the constitutional right to physical integrity (Article 2(2) Grundgesetz).

**Adopted Decision Solution:** Instead, the Bundestag adopted the decision solution (Entscheidungslösung), under which health insurance companies must regularly (every two years) contact their members, provide educational materials about organ donation, and prompt them to register a decision -- whether positive, negative, or deferred -- in the national organ donation registry (Organspenderegister). The registry became operational in March 2024.

### Organ Procurement and Allocation

**Deutsche Stiftung Organtransplantation (DSO):** The DSO serves as Germany's national coordination center for organ procurement, responsible for identifying potential donors, managing the procurement process, and coordinating with transplant centers.

**Eurotransplant:** Germany participates in Eurotransplant, the multi-national organ allocation organization serving eight countries (Germany, Austria, Belgium, Croatia, Hungary, Luxembourg, Netherlands, Slovenia). Eurotransplant manages the shared waiting list and allocates organs based on medical urgency, compatibility, and waiting time.

### Living Donation

TPG Section 8 permits living organ donation under strict conditions:
- The donor must be of legal age and capable of consent.
- Donation is limited to close relatives (first or second degree), spouses, registered partners, and persons who demonstrably have a close personal relationship with the recipient.
- An independent Living Donation Commission (Lebendspendekommission) must evaluate each case and confirm that the donation is voluntary and not coerced.
- Anonymous altruistic donation is not permitted.

### Organ Trafficking and Transplant Scandals

TPG Section 18 criminalizes organ trafficking with imprisonment of up to 5 years. The 2012 Göttingen organ transplant scandal, in which a transplant surgeon was accused of manipulating patient data to move patients up the waiting list, led to significant reforms in transplant oversight, including enhanced auditing and the establishment of interdisciplinary oversight committees at transplant centers.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Directive 2010/53/EU

Directive 2010/53/EU on standards of quality and safety of human organs intended for transplantation establishes minimum quality and safety standards across EU member states:

- **Quality Framework:** Member states must establish a quality and safety framework for all stages from donation to transplantation.
- **Organ Characterization:** Minimum data sets must be collected for every organ offered for transplantation.
- **Traceability:** Systems must be in place to trace organs from donor to recipient and vice versa.
- **Adverse Events Reporting:** Serious adverse events and reactions must be reported and investigated.
- **Competent Authorities:** Each member state must designate competent authorities to implement and oversee compliance.

The Directive intentionally does not harmonize consent systems, leaving the choice between opt-in and opt-out to member states.

### Council of Europe Convention against Trafficking in Human Organs (2015)

Opened for signature on March 25, 2015, this Convention requires states parties to criminalize the removal of organs without valid consent, organ trafficking, and preparation or preservation of unlawfully removed organs. It is the first international treaty specifically targeting organ trafficking as a distinct criminal offense.

### European Court of Human Rights

**Petrova v. Latvia (Application No. 4605/05, Judgment of June 24, 2014):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where organs were removed from the applicant's deceased son without her knowledge or consent under Latvia's presumed consent system. The Court held that while presumed consent systems are not inherently incompatible with the Convention, they must include adequate safeguards, including accessible information about the system and effective mechanisms for individuals to register objections.

**Elberte v. Latvia (Application No. 61243/08, Judgment of January 13, 2015):** The Court found violations of Articles 3 and 8 where tissue was removed from the applicant's deceased husband without her informed consent. The Court emphasized that the removal of organs or tissue from a deceased person engages the rights of close relatives and requires a legal framework with clear and accessible procedures.

### Organ Allocation Ethics

The allocation of scarce organs raises fundamental rights questions addressed by the ECHR:
- **Non-discrimination (Article 14):** Allocation criteria must not discriminate on prohibited grounds. Eurotransplant's allocation algorithms are periodically reviewed for compliance with non-discrimination principles.
- **Right to life (Article 2):** States' positive obligations under Article 2 include ensuring effective and non-discriminatory organ allocation systems.

## Comparative Analysis

### Consent Models

The choice between opt-in and opt-out consent is the most consequential policy decision in organ donation law. Countries with presumed consent (opt-out) systems -- including Spain, France, and Austria -- generally achieve higher donation rates. Germany's failed attempt to introduce opt-out in 2020 illustrates the political difficulty of this reform. South Korea and the United States maintain opt-in systems, while actively promoting registration. The EU leaves this choice to member states, resulting in a mix across the Union.

### Living Donation Restrictions

Germany and South Korea restrict living donation to persons with close relationships to the recipient, reflecting concerns about coercion and exploitation. The United States permits broader living donation, including non-directed altruistic donation (to any suitable recipient), which has enabled paired exchange programs. The ethical debate centers on whether restrictive eligibility criteria protect potential donors from exploitation or deny patients access to life-saving organs.

### Organ Trafficking

All jurisdictions criminalize organ trafficking, but enforcement varies. The Council of Europe Convention provides the most comprehensive international framework. South Korea and the US have investigated transplant tourism cases. Germany's transplant scandal exposed vulnerabilities in allocation integrity rather than trafficking per se.

### Allocation Equity

The tension between medical utility (maximizing organ survival) and equity (ensuring fair access regardless of geography, socioeconomic status, or demographics) pervades all allocation systems. The US's 2023 geographic allocation reform sought to reduce geographic disparities. Eurotransplant's multi-national system pools organs across borders but creates cross-country allocation tensions. South Korea's single-national system faces urban-rural disparities in organ availability.
