## Summary

Harassment -- a broad category encompassing repeated, unwanted conduct that causes distress, alarm, or fear in the victim -- spans criminal law, employment law, and civil rights law, and has evolved dramatically in recent decades as jurisdictions have expanded protections to cover new forms of technology-facilitated harassment, workplace bullying, and discriminatory conduct. South Korea has developed an extensive anti-harassment framework addressing multiple contexts: the Act on Punishment of Stalking Crime (스토킹범죄의 처벌 등에 관한 법률, 2021) addresses persistent harassment, while the Workplace Harassment Prevention Act (직장 내 괴롭힘 금지, an amendment to the Labor Standards Act effective 2019) prohibits workplace harassment (직장 내 괴롭힘) and defines it as conduct by an employer or employee that causes physical or mental suffering to another employee or degrades the working environment by exploiting a superior position; the 2019 workplace harassment law was prompted by the public outcry following the "nut rage incident" (2014) and multiple workplace bullying suicides, and Korean criminal law further addresses harassment through the general provisions on assault (폭행), intimidation (협박), and insult (모욕). The United States addresses harassment through a patchwork of federal, state, and local provisions: federal employment law under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 prohibits workplace harassment based on protected characteristics (race, color, religion, sex, national origin), with the Supreme Court establishing the framework for hostile work environment claims in Meritor Savings Bank v. Vinson, 477 U.S. 57 (1986) and clarifying employer liability in Burlington Industries v. Ellerth, 524 U.S. 742 (1998) and Faragher v. City of Boca Raton, 524 U.S. 775 (1998); criminal harassment is addressed through state statutes typically requiring a course of conduct directed at a specific person that serves no legitimate purpose and causes substantial emotional distress or reasonable fear. Germany addresses harassment through the Allgemeines Gleichbehandlungsgesetz (AGG, General Equal Treatment Act of 2006), which implements EU anti-discrimination directives and defines harassment (Belästigung) as unwanted conduct related to a protected ground that violates the dignity of the affected person, while criminal harassment is addressed through Section 238 StGB (Nachstellung/stalking), Section 185 StGB (Beleidigung/insult), and Section 240 StGB (Nötigung/coercion). The ECHR addresses harassment through Article 3 (prohibition of inhuman treatment), Article 8 (private life), and Article 14 (non-discrimination), while EU law provides a comprehensive anti-harassment framework through the Employment Equality Directive (2000/78/EC), the Race Equality Directive (2000/43/EC), and Directive (EU) 2024/1385 on combating violence against women, which for the first time explicitly criminalizes cyber harassment across all member states.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea has developed a multi-layered anti-harassment framework:

### Criminal Provisions

**Insult (모욕, Article 311 Criminal Act):** "A person who publicly insults another shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than 1 year or a fine not exceeding 2 million KRW." This provision is unusual in comparative law, as many jurisdictions have decriminalized simple insult.

**Intimidation (협박, Article 283 Criminal Act):** "A person who intimidates another shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than 3 years, a fine not exceeding 5 million KRW, detention, or a minor fine."

**Stalking Crime Act (스토킹범죄의 처벌 등에 관한 법률, 2021):** Criminalizes repeated unwanted pursuit or contact (see dedicated stalking analysis).

### Workplace Harassment (직장 내 괴롭힘)

**Labor Standards Act, Article 76-2 (effective July 16, 2019):**

"An employer or employee shall not cause physical or mental suffering to, or degrade the working environment of, another employee beyond the scope appropriate for work by taking advantage of their superior status or relationship in the workplace."

**Definition Elements:**
1. Conduct by an employer or employee (동료 또는 상급자)
2. Using a superior position or relationship within the workplace (직장에서의 지위 또는 관계 등의 우위를 이용)
3. Beyond the scope appropriate for work (업무상 적정범위를 넘어)
4. Causing physical or mental suffering or degrading the work environment (신체적·정신적 고통을 주거나 근무환경을 악화시키는 행위)

**Employer Obligations:**
- Investigate upon receiving a workplace harassment complaint
- Take protective measures for the victim during investigation
- Take disciplinary or other measures against confirmed harassers
- Prohibition on retaliation against complainants

**Penalties for Non-Compliance:** Employers who fail to investigate or who retaliate may face administrative penalties of up to 10 million KRW.

### "Gapjil" Culture (갑질)

The Korean concept of "gapjil" -- the abuse of power in hierarchical relationships, particularly by those in positions of economic or organizational authority -- has shaped the development of harassment law:

**Korean Air "Nut Rage" Incident (2014):** Cho Hyun-ah, vice president of Korean Air, assaulted a flight attendant for improper nut service, forcing the plane to return to the gate. The incident became a symbol of "gapjil" culture and galvanized public support for workplace harassment legislation.

**Yang Jin-ho Case (2018):** The chairman of a web hosting company was arrested for systematically abusing employees, including physical assault and forced degradation. The case further demonstrated the need for workplace harassment legislation.

### Online Harassment

South Korea has addressed online harassment through:
- Information and Communications Network Act provisions against defamation and insult via electronic means
- The "right to be forgotten" recognized by the Seoul Central District Court in 2015
- Korea Communications Standards Commission (KCSC) content review and removal authority
- The tragic suicides of K-pop stars Sulli (2019) and Goo Hara (2019), partly attributed to online harassment, prompted calls for stronger cyber harassment laws

## United States

### Legal Framework

### Employment Harassment (Title VII)

**Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964:** Section 703(a) prohibits discrimination in employment on the basis of race, color, religion, sex, or national origin. The Supreme Court has recognized two forms of harassment under Title VII:

**Quid Pro Quo Harassment:** Where submission to or rejection of unwelcome sexual conduct is used as the basis for employment decisions.

**Hostile Work Environment:** Where unwelcome conduct based on a protected characteristic is "sufficiently severe or pervasive to alter the conditions of the victim's employment and create an abusive working environment" (Meritor Savings Bank v. Vinson, 477 U.S. 57 (1986)).

**Employer Liability Framework:**

**Burlington Industries v. Ellerth, 524 U.S. 742 (1998) and Faragher v. City of Boca Raton, 524 U.S. 775 (1998):** The Court established a two-pronged test for employer liability:
- When harassment results in a tangible employment action: employer is strictly liable
- When no tangible action: employer liable unless it proves (1) it exercised reasonable care to prevent and correct harassment, and (2) the employee unreasonably failed to take advantage of preventive or corrective opportunities

### Criminal Harassment

State criminal harassment statutes typically require:
1. A course of conduct (repeated acts)
2. Directed at a specific person
3. That serves no legitimate purpose
4. And causes or would reasonably cause substantial emotional distress or fear

**State Variations:**

| State | Statute | Classification |
|-------|---------|---------------|
| New York (PL 240.25) | Second degree: course of conduct causing alarm | Violation (non-criminal) |
| New York (PL 240.26) | First degree: following or engaging in course of conduct causing fear | Class B misdemeanor |
| California (PC 646.9) | Stalking/harassment statute | Misdemeanor or felony |
| Illinois (720 ILCS 5/26.5-2) | Cyber harassment | Class A misdemeanor |

### Recent Developments

**#MeToo Movement (2017-):** Generated significant legislative activity:
- Several states enacted laws limiting non-disclosure agreements in harassment cases
- The Ending Forced Arbitration of Sexual Assault and Sexual Harassment Act (2022) amended the Federal Arbitration Act to allow victims to void pre-dispute arbitration agreements

## Germany

### Legal Framework

### General Equal Treatment Act (AGG, 2006)

**Section 3(3) -- Harassment (Belästigung):** "Harassment shall be deemed to be discrimination where unwanted conduct related to any of the grounds referred to in Section 1 takes place with the purpose or effect of violating the dignity of the person concerned and of creating an intimidating, hostile, degrading, humiliating or offensive environment."

**Section 3(4) -- Sexual Harassment (Sexuelle Belästigung):** "Sexual harassment shall be deemed to be discrimination in relation to Section 2(1)(1) to (4) where unwanted conduct of a sexual nature, including unwanted sexual acts and requests for sexual favours, sexually suggestive physical contact, comments of a sexual nature, as well as unwanted showing or public display of pornographic images, takes place with the purpose or effect described in subsection (3)."

**Section 12 -- Employer's Duties:** Employers must take measures to protect employees from harassment, including preventive measures, training, and complaint mechanisms. Failure to act may result in liability for damages under Section 15 AGG.

### Criminal Provisions

**Section 185 StGB -- Insult (Beleidigung):** "Insult shall be punished with imprisonment for a term not exceeding two years or a fine." This provision covers verbal abuse, obscene gestures, and other conduct targeting individual dignity.

**Section 238 StGB -- Stalking (Nachstellung):** Addresses persistent harassment (see dedicated stalking analysis).

**Section 240 StGB -- Coercion (Nötigung):** "Whoever unlawfully uses force or threatens a person with a perceptible harm to compel them to do, tolerate or refrain from doing something shall be punished with imprisonment for a term not exceeding three years or a fine."

### Workplace Bullying (Mobbing)

German labor courts have developed extensive jurisprudence on workplace bullying (Mobbing):

**BAG, Judgment of January 28, 2010 (8 AZR 546/09):** The Federal Labor Court defined Mobbing as "systematic hostile behavior by one or more persons directed against another person, carried out over a longer period, with the aim or effect of excluding the person from the workplace."

**BGB Section 611a:** The employer's duty of care (Fürsorgepflicht) requires employers to protect employees from harassment by colleagues and supervisors.

### NetzDG and Online Harassment

The Netzwerkdurchsetzungsgesetz (NetzDG, Network Enforcement Act, 2017) requires social media platforms with over 2 million German users to:
- Establish complaint mechanisms for illegal content (including harassment)
- Remove "manifestly unlawful" content within 24 hours
- Remove other unlawful content within 7 days
- Publish transparency reports

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

**Article 3 -- Prohibition of Inhuman Treatment:** The Court has held that severe workplace harassment by state agents may engage Article 3.

**Đorđević v. Croatia (Application No. 41526/10, Judgment of July 24, 2012):** The Court found violations of Articles 3 and 8 where authorities failed to protect a mentally and physically disabled person from persistent harassment by neighbors, establishing that states have positive obligations to protect against private harassment.

**Article 8 -- Private Life:** Harassment that affects a person's psychological integrity engages Article 8.

**Söderman v. Sweden (Application No. 5786/08, Grand Chamber Judgment of November 12, 2013):** The Grand Chamber held that states have positive obligations under Article 8 to protect individuals from intrusions by private parties, including through effective criminal law remedies for harassment.

### EU Anti-Harassment Framework

**Employment Equality Directive (2000/78/EC):** Article 2(3): "Harassment shall be deemed to be a form of discrimination" when unwanted conduct related to religion/belief, disability, age, or sexual orientation occurs with the purpose or effect of violating dignity.

**Race Equality Directive (2000/43/EC):** Article 2(3): Same harassment definition applied to race and ethnic origin discrimination.

**Gender Equality Directive (2006/54/EC):** Articles 2(1)(c)-(d): Defines both harassment and sexual harassment in the employment context.

**Directive (EU) 2024/1385 on Combating Violence Against Women:** For the first time, this directive requires EU member states to criminalize cyber harassment, defined as repeatedly engaging in threatening or intimidating behavior directed at a person through information and communications technology (ICT) tools. Member states must transpose by June 2027.

### ILO Convention No. 190 (2019)

The ILO Convention on Violence and Harassment in the World of Work, adopted June 21, 2019, provides the first international definition of workplace harassment:
- "Violence and harassment" includes a range of unacceptable behaviours and practices that aim at, result in, or are likely to result in physical, psychological, sexual, or economic harm
- The Convention covers workers in all sectors, including informal economy
- Ratified by 43 countries as of 2025

## Comparative Analysis

### Definitional Approaches

The jurisdictions show three distinct approaches to defining harassment:

**Conduct-Specific (South Korea):** Korean law enumerates specific types of harassment (workplace bullying, stalking, insult) and addresses each through separate statutory frameworks.

**Discrimination-Linked (United States, EU):** Workplace harassment law is primarily framed as a form of discrimination based on protected characteristics.

**Dignity-Based (Germany, EU):** The AGG and EU directives define harassment as conduct that "violates the dignity" of the person, providing a broader conceptual foundation.

### Employer Liability

| Jurisdiction | Standard | Key Feature |
|-------------|----------|-------------|
| South Korea | Duty to investigate and act | Administrative penalties for non-compliance |
| United States | Vicarious liability (Ellerth/Faragher) | Affirmative defense available |
| Germany | Duty of care (Fürsorgepflicht) | AGG Section 12 duties |
| EU | Framework requirements | Member states implement |

### Online Harassment

The rapid growth of online harassment has driven legislative innovation:
- **South Korea:** KCSC content removal; platform cooperation
- **United States:** Limited by Section 230 CDA immunity
- **Germany:** NetzDG imposes platform duties
- **EU:** Directive 2024/1385 criminalizes cyber harassment; DSA creates platform obligations
