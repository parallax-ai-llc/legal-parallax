## Summary

Non-consensual intimate images (NCII) -- commonly referred to as "revenge porn" -- encompasses the creation, distribution, possession, or threat of distribution of sexually explicit or intimate images without the depicted person's consent. Over the past decade, this form of image-based sexual abuse has been recognized as a distinct criminal offense across an expanding number of jurisdictions, driven by the proliferation of digital communications, smartphone cameras, and AI-generated deepfake technology. South Korea addresses NCII primarily through the Act on Special Cases Concerning the Punishment of Sexual Crimes (성폭력범죄의 처벌 등에 관한 특례법), particularly Article 14, which criminalizes non-consensual filming, distribution, and possession with penalties of up to seven years' imprisonment, and through expanded 2024 deepfake-specific legislation that further criminalizes production, distribution, possession, and even viewing of deepfake intimate content. The United States, which historically lacked a federal criminal statute on NCII, enacted the TAKE IT DOWN Act in May 2025 -- the first federal law criminalizing the non-consensual publication of intimate images, carrying penalties of up to two years' imprisonment for adult-depicting offenses and three years for minor-depicting offenses -- while all 50 states now maintain individual criminal statutes following South Carolina's enactment in May 2025. Germany criminalizes NCII under Section 201a of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), which prohibits the unauthorized creation and distribution of recordings that violate intimate privacy, with penalties of up to two years' imprisonment and fines up to 60,000 euros, supplemented by provisions on coercion (Section 240 StGB) and extortion (Section 253 StGB) where images are used as leverage. At the European level, the European Court of Human Rights has recognized positive obligations on states to criminalize and effectively prosecute NCII under Article 8 of the European Convention on Human Rights, most notably in Buturuga v. Romania (2020) and M.S.D. v. Romania (2024), while EU Directive 2024/1385 on combating violence against women and domestic violence explicitly requires all member states to criminalize the non-consensual sharing of intimate or manipulated material by June 2027.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea has developed one of the most comprehensive legal frameworks addressing non-consensual intimate images, driven by a series of high-profile digital sex crime scandals. The primary criminal statute is the Act on Special Cases Concerning the Punishment of Sexual Crimes (성폭력범죄의 처벌 등에 관한 특례법), with Article 14 serving as the central provision targeting camera-based sexual offenses and non-consensual image distribution. The law was first enacted in 2010 and has undergone multiple expansions -- most significantly in 2020 following the Nth Room scandal and in 2024 in response to a deepfake sexual abuse crisis.

The Act on Promotion of Information and Communications Network Utilization and Information Protection provides additional regulatory tools, requiring platforms to remove illegal sexual content and establishing obligations for service providers operating within South Korean jurisdiction.

### Penalties

| Offense Category | Penalty |
|-----------------|---------|
| Non-consensual filming of another's body (Art. 14(1)) | Up to 7 years imprisonment or fine up to 50 million KRW (~$42,500) |
| Distribution, sale, or public exhibition of non-consensual images (Art. 14(2)) | Up to 7 years imprisonment or fine up to 50 million KRW |
| Profit-driven distribution via information and communications network | Imprisonment of at least 3 years (mandatory minimum) |
| Possession or viewing of non-consensual images (Art. 14(3)) | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine up to 30 million KRW (~$25,500) |
| Habitual offenses (Art. 14(4)) | Punishment aggravated by up to one-half of the prescribed penalty |
| Deepfake intimate image creation/distribution (2024 law) | Up to 7 years imprisonment (increased from 5 years) |
| Purchasing, selling, or viewing deepfake sexual content | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine up to 30 million KRW |

### The Nth Room Case (2018--2020)

The Nth Room case stands as one of the most notorious digital sex crime cases in South Korean history. From late 2018 to March 2020, perpetrators operating under pseudonyms -- most prominently Cho Ju-bin (known as "Baksa" or "the Doctor") -- created a network of encrypted chat rooms on the Telegram messaging platform. Victims, numbering at least 1,154 confirmed individuals (60.7 percent of whom were in their 20s or younger), were coerced through blackmail and deception into producing sexually explicit photographs and videos. The material was then distributed to paying subscribers within these "Nth Rooms."

Cho Ju-bin was sentenced to 40 years' imprisonment in 2020. The case triggered widespread public outrage and directly precipitated the passage of sweeping legislative reforms in the summer of 2020, which went into effect in December 2021. These reforms increased penalties for online sex offenders, criminalized the possession and purchase of illicit sexual content, and imposed new obligations on platform operators.

### 2024 Deepfake Crisis and Legislative Response

In 2024, South Korea experienced a second wave of digital sex crime involving AI-generated deepfake pornography, predominantly targeting female students and public figures. The crisis led to rapid legislative action: the maximum imprisonment for creating and distributing deepfake intimate images was increased from five to seven years, and for the first time, mere viewing or possession of deepfake sexual content was criminalized with penalties of up to three years' imprisonment or a fine of up to 3 million won. Covered platforms were required to remove flagged content within 48 hours of receiving a valid notice from the victim.

### Enforcement Challenges

Despite robust statutory provisions, enforcement remains a persistent challenge. A 2021 Human Rights Watch report found that 79 percent of those convicted of capturing intimate images without consent received only suspended sentences, fines, or a combination of the two. Furthermore, the laws apply only to operators located within South Korea, limiting effectiveness against crimes committed on foreign-based platforms such as Telegram.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has addressed NCII through a patchwork of state criminal statutes and, as of 2025, federal legislation. Historically, there was no federal criminal law specifically targeting the non-consensual distribution of intimate images, though prosecutors occasionally relied on general statutes relating to cyberstalking (18 U.S.C. Section 2261A), extortion, or child exploitation.

**TAKE IT DOWN Act (Signed May 19, 2025):** This landmark federal law -- the first to directly criminalize NCII at the federal level -- prohibits any person from knowingly publishing, without consent, intimate visual depictions of non-consenting adults or minors, including AI-generated deepfakes intended to cause harm. The Act also mandates that covered platforms (public websites and mobile applications) establish notice-and-takedown procedures, with a 48-hour window to investigate and remove flagged material after receiving a valid report. Failure to comply is treated as an unfair or deceptive practice under Section 18(a)(1)(B) of the Federal Trade Commission Act, subjecting platforms to FTC enforcement.

**SHIELD Act (Reintroduced February 2025, Pending):** The Stopping Harmful Image Exploitation and Limiting Distribution Act, championed by Senator Amy Klobuchar and Senator John Cornyn, would establish broader federal criminal liability for distributing or threatening to distribute private intimate images, with enhanced penalties for offenses involving minors. The bill passed the Senate in 2024 but failed in the House; it was reintroduced in the 119th Congress.

**DEFIANCE Act (Passed Senate January 2026):** The Disrupt Explicit Forged Images and Non-Consensual Edits Act, introduced by Representative Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez, creates a federal civil cause of action enabling victims of deepfake intimate imagery to seek damages from those who created or distributed the images.

### Penalties

| Offense Level | Federal Penalty (TAKE IT DOWN Act) |
|---------------|-------------------------------------|
| Publication of non-consensual intimate images (adults) | Fine and/or up to 2 years imprisonment |
| Publication of non-consensual intimate images (minors) | Fine and/or up to 3 years imprisonment |
| Threats to publish authentic intimate images (adults) | Fine and/or up to 2 years imprisonment |
| Threats to publish authentic intimate images (minors) | Fine and/or up to 3 years imprisonment |
| Threats involving digital forgeries (adults) | Fine and/or up to 18 months imprisonment |
| Threats involving digital forgeries (minors) | Fine and/or up to 30 months imprisonment |
| Platform non-compliance with takedown obligations | FTC enforcement as unfair/deceptive practice |

### State Laws

As of May 2025, all 50 states, the District of Columbia, and Guam have enacted criminal statutes addressing non-consensual distribution of intimate images. South Carolina was the last state to criminalize NCII, enacting its law in May 2025. State laws vary significantly in scope, severity, and approach:

- **California (2013):** Penal Code Section 647(j)(4) criminalizes intentional distribution of intimate images with intent to cause emotional distress; misdemeanor offense.
- **New Jersey (2004):** The first state to enact revenge porn legislation, criminalizing invasion of privacy through non-consensual disclosure of intimate images as a third-degree felony with up to five years' imprisonment.
- **Texas (2015, revised 2019):** Initially struck down as unconstitutional on First Amendment grounds in Ex parte Jones (2018); legislature revised the statute in 2019 to include an intent-to-harm element.
- **Illinois (2015):** Non-Consensual Dissemination of Private Sexual Images Act, 720 ILCS 5/11-23.5; felony offense.

### Key Legal Issues

**First Amendment Considerations:** U.S. revenge porn laws must navigate the constitutional guarantee of free expression. Several statutes have faced constitutional challenges, with courts generally upholding laws that include a specific intent requirement. The Texas Court of Criminal Appeals struck down the state's original revenge porn statute in Ex parte Jones (2018), finding it overbroad, prompting legislative revision.

**Section 230 Limitations:** Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act historically provided broad immunity to online platforms for user-generated content. The TAKE IT DOWN Act represents a significant carve-out, imposing affirmative takedown obligations on platforms regardless of Section 230 protections.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany addresses non-consensual intimate images primarily through Section 201a of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), titled "Violation of Intimate Privacy by Taking Photographs or Using Other Technical Means." Introduced in 2004 and reformed in 2015 and 2021, this provision criminalizes both the unauthorized creation and the unauthorized distribution of recordings that invade a person's intimate sphere of life. The protection extends to images taken in private settings -- such as bedrooms and bathrooms -- as well as to any intimate image distributed without the depicted person's consent, even if the original recording was made with consent.

### Penalties

| Offense Category | Penalty |
|-----------------|---------|
| Unauthorized creation of intimate recordings (Section 201a(1) StGB) | Up to 2 years imprisonment or fine |
| Unauthorized distribution of intimate recordings (Section 201a(1) StGB) | Up to 2 years imprisonment or fine (up to 60,000 EUR) |
| Distribution with coercion or threats (Section 240 StGB) | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine; in serious cases up to 5 years |
| Extortion using intimate images (Section 253 StGB) | Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine; in serious cases up to 15 years |
| Violation of the right to one's own image (KunstUrhG Section 33) | Up to 1 year imprisonment or fine |

### Scope and Application

Section 201a StGB protects the "most personal sphere of life" (hoechstpersoenlicher Lebensbereich). Critically, the law covers several scenarios beyond stereotypical revenge porn:

- **Consensually taken images distributed without consent:** Even if a person voluntarily provided intimate photographs or videos to a partner, redistribution without consent constitutes a criminal offense.
- **Upskirting:** Following a 2020 legislative amendment, non-consensual photographing under clothing (upskirting) was explicitly criminalized under Section 184k StGB with penalties of up to two years' imprisonment.
- **Deepfakes:** Section 201a StGB applies to manipulated images, including AI-generated intimate depictions, as the provision covers any recording that invades intimate privacy regardless of its method of creation.

### Civil Remedies

Beyond criminal prosecution, victims in Germany may pursue civil remedies under the Buergerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB). Section 823 BGB provides a general tortious liability claim for violation of personal rights, while Section 1004 BGB (applied by analogy) enables injunctive relief to prevent further distribution. The right to one's own image (Recht am eigenen Bild), established under Sections 22-23 of the Kunsturhebergesetz (KunstUrhG), provides additional civil protection, requiring consent for any publication of a person's likeness.

### Criticisms and Reform Proposals

German legal scholars and advocacy organizations have criticized the relatively low maximum penalty of two years under Section 201a StGB as insufficient to deter image-based sexual abuse, particularly in comparison to the penalties available in South Korea or under emerging EU standards. The organization Aktiv gegen digitale Gewalt (Active Against Digital Violence) has called for dedicated revenge porn legislation with enhanced penalties and streamlined victim support mechanisms.

## ECHR & EU

### European Convention on Human Rights -- Article 8

The European Court of Human Rights has addressed non-consensual intimate images under Article 8 of the Convention, which guarantees the right to respect for private and family life. The Court has progressively recognized that states bear positive obligations not only to refrain from interference with private life but also to take active measures to protect individuals from image-based sexual abuse by private actors.

**K.U. v. Finland (2008):** While not a revenge porn case per se, this foundational decision (Application no. 2872/02) established the principle that Article 8 requires states to ensure effective criminal law mechanisms to protect individuals against violations of personal integrity through digital means. The Court held that Finland had failed to provide adequate protection when a child's identity was used in a sexually suggestive online advertisement.

**Buturuga v. Romania (2020):** In this landmark case (Application no. 56867/15), the Court examined whether Romanian authorities had fulfilled their positive obligations under Article 8 to protect a victim from online harassment involving the dissemination of intimate photographs by a former partner. The Court affirmed that the protection of Article 8 extended to image-based sexual abuse in the digital domain and that states must ensure effective investigation and prosecution of such conduct.

**M.S.D. v. Romania (2024):** On December 3, 2024, the Court condemned Romania for failing to punish a man who published intimate photos of his former partner online without her consent. The Court found a violation of Article 8, holding that in cases of revenge pornography -- defined as the online public dissemination without consent of intimate photographs by a former intimate partner -- the protection of Article 8 requires a criminal-law response. This ruling strengthened the obligation on Council of Europe member states to maintain effective criminal frameworks addressing NCII.

### EU Directive 2024/1385 -- Violence Against Women and Domestic Violence

Adopted on May 14, 2024, Directive (EU) 2024/1385 is the first binding EU-wide legislation to address image-based sexual abuse as a criminal offense. The Directive marks a watershed moment in EU criminal law, establishing minimum standards across all member states.

**Key Provisions on NCII:**

- **Article 5 -- Non-Consensual Sharing of Intimate or Manipulated Material:** Criminalizes the non-consensual production and dissemination of intimate images, including AI-generated or manipulated material depicting a person appearing to engage in sexual acts. Distribution must be "likely to cause serious harm" to the victim.
- **Cyber Flashing:** The unsolicited sending of intimate images to others without their consent is criminalized as a distinct offense.
- **Platform Obligations:** Member states must ensure accessible and secure ICT-based reporting mechanisms for victims. Removal orders and rapid evidence preservation mechanisms must be established.
- **Victim Support:** The Directive mandates that member states provide specialized support services, including counseling, legal aid, and assistance with content removal.

**Transposition Deadline:** Member states must incorporate the Directive's requirements into national law by June 14, 2027.

### Complementary EU Framework

The Directive operates alongside other EU instruments that address aspects of image-based sexual abuse:

- **Digital Services Act (DSA, 2022):** Imposes obligations on online platforms to remove illegal content, including NCII, and establishes transparency requirements and trusted flagger mechanisms.
- **AI Act (2024):** Addresses risks posed by artificial intelligence systems, including the generation of deepfake content, with transparency and labeling requirements.
- **General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR, 2016):** Provides data protection grounds for victims to demand removal of intimate images, with the right to erasure under Article 17 applicable to non-consensual intimate content hosted by data controllers within EU jurisdiction.

## Comparative Analysis

### Severity of Penalties

South Korea imposes the most severe penalties among the jurisdictions surveyed, with imprisonment of up to seven years for non-consensual filming or distribution and mandatory minimum sentences of at least three years for profit-driven distribution via networks. Germany's maximum of two years under Section 201a StGB is the lowest, though supplementary charges under coercion or extortion provisions can substantially increase sentences. The United States federal penalties under the TAKE IT DOWN Act are moderate at two to three years, though state-level penalties vary widely. The EU Directive does not prescribe specific penalty ranges, leaving this to member state transposition.

### Breadth of Criminalization

South Korea is the only jurisdiction among those surveyed to explicitly criminalize the mere possession and viewing of non-consensual intimate images, a provision expanded in 2024 to cover deepfake content. The United States federal approach focuses on publication and distribution, without criminalizing possession. Germany similarly targets creation and distribution rather than possession per se. The EU Directive does not mandate criminalization of possession.

### Deepfake Coverage

All four jurisdictions now address deepfake intimate images in some form. South Korea enacted specific deepfake legislation in 2024 with penalties of up to seven years. The US TAKE IT DOWN Act explicitly covers "digital forgeries." Germany applies the existing Section 201a StGB framework to manipulated images. EU Directive 2024/1385 Article 5 specifically references "manipulated material," ensuring coverage of AI-generated content.

### Platform Accountability

Both the United States (TAKE IT DOWN Act) and South Korea impose specific 48-hour takedown obligations on platforms. The EU Digital Services Act provides a broader framework for platform liability and content moderation, while Germany, as an EU member state, is bound by both the DSA and will implement the Directive's additional requirements. The ECHR framework addresses platform obligations indirectly through state positive obligations to ensure effective remedies.

### Constitutional and Rights Tensions

The United States faces unique tensions between NCII criminalization and the First Amendment's guarantee of free expression, as demonstrated by the judicial invalidation of the original Texas revenge porn statute. Other jurisdictions do not face comparable constitutional hurdles: South Korea's Constitutional Court has upheld digital sex crime provisions, Germany balances privacy rights against expression through its established proportionality framework, and the ECHR Court has consistently prioritized Article 8 privacy protections over Article 10 expression rights in NCII contexts.
