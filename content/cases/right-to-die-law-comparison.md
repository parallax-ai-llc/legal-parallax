## Summary

The right to die -- encompassing the legal doctrines of voluntary euthanasia, physician-assisted suicide, and the withdrawal of life-sustaining treatment -- represents one of the most contested intersections of constitutional law, medical ethics, and human dignity across global legal systems. South Korea enacted the Act on Hospice and Palliative Care and Decisions on Life-Sustaining Treatment for Patients at the End of Life (호스피스·완화의료 및 임종과정에 있는 환자의 연명의료결정에 관한 법률, commonly known as the Well-Dying Act, 연명의료결정법) in February 2016, effective February 2018, which permits the withdrawal or withholding of life-sustaining treatment but does not legalize active euthanasia or physician-assisted suicide, following the landmark Supreme Court decision in the Grandmother Kim case (대법원 2009다17417, 2009). The United States prohibits physician-assisted suicide at the federal level but permits it in eleven states and the District of Columbia under state Death with Dignity statutes, beginning with Oregon's Death with Dignity Act (1994); the Supreme Court in Washington v. Glucksberg, 521 U.S. 702 (1997) held that there is no fundamental constitutional right to assisted suicide, while simultaneously recognizing in Cruzan v. Director, Missouri Department of Health, 497 U.S. 261 (1990) a constitutionally protected liberty interest in refusing unwanted medical treatment. Germany's Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) issued a transformative decision on February 26, 2020 (BVerfG, 2 BvR 2347/15), striking down Section 217 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), which had criminalized organized assisted suicide services, finding that the right to a self-determined death is a fundamental aspect of the general right of personality under Articles 1 and 2 of the Grundgesetz, and the Bundestag has since debated but failed to pass new regulatory frameworks. The European Court of Human Rights has addressed right-to-die questions in several landmark cases, including Pretty v. United Kingdom (2002), Haas v. Switzerland (2011), and Mortier v. Belgium (2022), recognizing that Article 8 encompasses the right to decide the manner and timing of one's death while permitting states a wide margin of appreciation in regulating or prohibiting assistance in dying.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's approach to end-of-life decisions is governed by the Act on Hospice and Palliative Care and Decisions on Life-Sustaining Treatment for Patients at the End of Life (호스피스·완화의료 및 임종과정에 있는 환자의 연명의료결정에 관한 법률), enacted on February 3, 2016, and effective in two phases: the hospice care provisions from August 4, 2017, and the life-sustaining treatment decision provisions from February 4, 2018. Active euthanasia and physician-assisted suicide remain criminal offenses under Articles 252 (촉탁·승낙에 의한 살인) and 253 (위계 등에 의한 촉탁살인 등) of the Criminal Act (형법).

### The Grandmother Kim Case (2009)

The catalyst for legislative reform was the Supreme Court's decision in the case commonly known as "Grandmother Kim" (대법원 2009.5.21. 선고 2009다17417 전원합의체 판결). Kim, a 76-year-old woman, was placed on a ventilator in November 2008 following a lung biopsy complication at Severance Hospital (세브란스병원). Her family requested removal of the ventilator, arguing that she had previously expressed a wish not to be kept alive by artificial means. The Supreme Court held that withdrawal of life-sustaining treatment is permissible when the patient is in an irreversible dying process and the patient's presumed will can be established. The ventilator was removed in June 2009; Kim survived until January 2010.

### Well-Dying Act Provisions

| Aspect | Provision |
|--------|-----------|

| Advance directive registry | National Agency for Management of Life-Sustaining Treatment (국립연명의료관리기관) |
| Institutional review | Institutional Ethics Committee review required |

### 2024 Amendment

In January 2024, the Well-Dying Act was amended to expand the types of life-sustaining treatment that may be withheld or withdrawn to include blood transfusion, vasopressor administration, and ECMO. The amendment also strengthened provisions for hospice palliative care and improved the advance directive registration system. As of 2025, over 2.5 million Koreans had registered advance directives.

### Ongoing Debate

Despite the Well-Dying Act, public discourse on expanding end-of-life options continues. A 2023 Gallup Korea survey found that approximately 76 percent of respondents supported allowing physician-assisted death for terminally ill patients. The National Bioethics Committee (국가생명윤리심의위원회) has conducted multiple deliberations on whether to expand the law beyond withdrawal of treatment. Several bills proposing the legalization of physician-assisted dying have been introduced in the National Assembly but have not advanced past committee stage.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States addresses the right to die through a combination of constitutional jurisprudence, federal statute, and state law. The Supreme Court has established two key constitutional boundaries: a liberty interest in refusing unwanted medical treatment (Cruzan, 1990) and the absence of a fundamental right to physician-assisted suicide (Glucksberg, 1997).

**Cruzan v. Director, Missouri Department of Health, 497 U.S. 261 (1990):** The Court assumed for purposes of its decision that a competent person has a constitutionally protected liberty interest under the Fourteenth Amendment Due Process Clause in refusing unwanted medical treatment, including life-sustaining hydration and nutrition. However, the Court upheld Missouri's requirement of clear and convincing evidence of the patient's wishes before treatment could be withdrawn for an incompetent patient.

**Washington v. Glucksberg, 521 U.S. 702 (1997):** The Court held unanimously that the Washington state statute prohibiting assisted suicide did not violate the Due Process Clause. Chief Justice Rehnquist's majority opinion applied the rational basis test, finding that the state's interests in preserving life, preventing suicide, protecting the integrity of the medical profession, and protecting vulnerable groups provided rational bases for the prohibition.

### Patient Self-Determination Act (1990)

The Patient Self-Determination Act (42 U.S.C. Section 1395cc(f)), enacted as part of the Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act of 1990, requires healthcare facilities receiving Medicare or Medicaid funding to inform patients of their right to execute advance directives, including living wills and durable powers of attorney for healthcare decisions. This federal legislation does not create a right to assisted suicide but establishes a framework for documenting treatment preferences.

### Gonzales v. Oregon (2006)

In Gonzales v. Oregon, 546 U.S. 243 (2006), the Supreme Court rejected the Bush administration's attempt to use the Controlled Substances Act to prohibit physicians from prescribing lethal medications under Oregon's Death with Dignity Act. The Court held that the Attorney General lacked authority to declare physician-assisted suicide illegitimate under the CSA, preserving the ability of states to enact death with dignity legislation.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's legal framework on the right to die distinguishes between three categories: active euthanasia (aktive Sterbehilfe), which remains prohibited under StGB Section 216 (Tötung auf Verlangen -- killing on request); assisted suicide (Beihilfe zum Suizid), which is not itself criminal because suicide is not a criminal offense; and passive euthanasia (passive Sterbehilfe), which encompasses the withdrawal of treatment in accordance with the patient's will.

### StGB Section 216 -- Killing on Request

Section 216 StGB provides that if a person is induced to kill by the express and earnest request of the person killed, a prison sentence of six months to five years shall be imposed. This provision criminalizes active euthanasia regardless of the patient's consent, motive, or medical condition. Attempts are also punishable.

### The Section 217 Saga

**Enactment (2015):** The Bundestag enacted Section 217 StGB on December 3, 2015, criminalizing organized assisted suicide services (geschäftsmäßige Förderung der Selbsttötung). The provision targeted organizations like Sterbehilfe Deutschland and Dignitas that provided systematic assistance with suicide. The penalty was imprisonment of up to three years or a fine. Family members and close associates were exempted from prosecution.

**Constitutional Court Decision (February 26, 2020, BVerfG 2 BvR 2347/15):** In a landmark judgment, the Second Senate of the Federal Constitutional Court struck down Section 217 as unconstitutional, holding that:

- The general right of personality (allgemeines Persönlichkeitsrecht) under Articles 2(1) and 1(1) of the Grundgesetz encompasses a right to a self-determined death (Recht auf selbstbestimmtes Sterben).
- This right includes the freedom to end one's own life and to seek assistance from third parties willing to provide it.
- The right applies to all individuals, not only those who are terminally ill or suffering, and exists at any stage of life.
- Section 217 de facto rendered the fundamental right meaningless by eliminating the practical possibility of receiving professional assistance.

**Failed Legislative Attempts (2022-2025):** Following the Court's decision, the Bundestag debated three competing bills on June 24, 2023. All three failed to achieve a majority: a proposal requiring counseling and a waiting period, a more restrictive proposal requiring psychiatric evaluation, and a liberal proposal with minimal restrictions. As of early 2026, Germany lacks a regulatory framework for assisted suicide beyond the constitutional right recognized by the Court.

### Advance Directives (Patientenverfügung)

BGB Section 1901a, introduced by the Third Act Amending Guardianship Law (Drittes Gesetz zur Änderung des Betreuungsrechts) of September 1, 2009, provides a statutory framework for advance directives. A written advance directive that specifies consent to or refusal of particular medical treatments is directly binding on attending physicians and guardians if the measures described correspond to the patient's current life and treatment situation.

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR has developed a nuanced body of jurisprudence on the right to die, primarily under Article 2 (right to life) and Article 8 (right to respect for private life).

**Pretty v. United Kingdom (Application No. 2346/02, April 29, 2002):** Diane Pretty, suffering from motor neurone disease, sought an undertaking from the Director of Public Prosecutions that her husband would not be prosecuted if he assisted her suicide. The Court held that Article 2 could not be interpreted as conferring a right to die, but recognized that Article 8 encompasses the right to avoid an undignified and distressing end to life. However, the UK's blanket prohibition on assisted suicide was found to be proportionate, given the state's legitimate aim of protecting vulnerable persons.

**Haas v. Switzerland (Application No. 31322/07, January 20, 2011):** The applicant, suffering from bipolar disorder, challenged Switzerland's requirement of a medical prescription for obtaining a lethal substance. The Court confirmed that Article 8 encompasses the right to decide the manner and timing of one's death, but held that states enjoy a considerable margin of appreciation in this area. The prescription requirement was proportionate.

**Koch v. Germany (Application No. 497/09, December 19, 2012):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 on procedural grounds, holding that German courts had failed to examine the merits of a husband's claim that his wife (who suffered from near-total paralysis) had been denied access to a lethal dose of medication. The judgment required Germany to address the substantive question but did not dictate a particular outcome.

**Lambert and Others v. France (Application No. 46043/14, Grand Chamber, June 5, 2015):** The Grand Chamber held that France's withdrawal of life-sustaining treatment from Vincent Lambert, who was in a chronic vegetative state, did not violate Article 2. The Court accorded France a wide margin of appreciation, finding that the Leonetti Act (2005) provided an adequate legal framework.

**Mortier v. Belgium (Application No. 78017/17, October 4, 2022):** The Court examined Belgium's euthanasia law for the first time, finding no violation of Article 2 in the euthanasia of the applicant's mother. The judgment validated the Belgian model while emphasizing the need for proper procedural safeguards, including effective a posteriori review mechanisms.

### Council of Europe

The Parliamentary Assembly of the Council of Europe adopted Recommendation 1418 (1999) on the Protection of the Human Rights and Dignity of the Terminally Ill and the Dying, calling on member states to ensure that no person is subjected to medical treatment against their will and that advance directives are respected. Resolution 1859 (2012) affirmed that euthanasia must always be prohibited but called for the development of palliative care.

### Oviedo Convention

The Council of Europe Convention on Human Rights and Biomedicine (Oviedo Convention, 1997), Article 9, provides that the previously expressed wishes of a patient regarding medical intervention shall be taken into account when the patient is not able to express their wishes at the time of the intervention. Article 5 establishes the general rule of free and informed consent to medical intervention.

## Comparative Analysis

### Spectrum of Permissiveness

The four jurisdictions occupy distinct positions on the spectrum of end-of-life legal permissiveness. Germany, following the 2020 BVerfG decision, has the broadest constitutional protection for the right to die, recognizing it as a fundamental right not limited to the terminally ill. The United States permits physician-assisted suicide in a minority of jurisdictions but restricts eligibility to terminal patients. South Korea permits only the withdrawal of life-sustaining treatment, not active assistance in dying. The ECHR framework defers to national legislatures through the margin of appreciation doctrine, permitting both permissive and restrictive approaches.

### Constitutional Grounding

The constitutional bases differ significantly. Germany grounds the right to die in the general right of personality (allgemeines Persönlichkeitsrecht), a broad concept derived from Articles 1 and 2 of the Grundgesetz. The United States locates the right to refuse treatment in the Fourteenth Amendment's liberty interest but has declined to find a fundamental right to assisted suicide. South Korea's Well-Dying Act was enacted legislatively rather than through constitutional adjudication, though the Supreme Court's Grandmother Kim decision invoked the constitutional right to dignity. The ECHR grounds its analysis in Article 8's right to respect for private life.

### The Role of Safeguards

All permissive jurisdictions employ safeguards, though their stringency varies. Oregon's Death with Dignity Act requires two oral requests separated by 15 days, a written request witnessed by two persons, and certification of a terminal illness with a six-month prognosis by two physicians. South Korea's Well-Dying Act requires either an advance directive or family consensus plus institutional ethics committee review. The ECHR, in Mortier v. Belgium, emphasized the need for effective a posteriori review mechanisms. Germany's regulatory gap following the failure of the 2023 legislative proposals leaves the practical exercise of the constitutional right uncertain.

### Active vs. Passive Distinction

All four jurisdictions maintain the distinction between active euthanasia and passive euthanasia (withdrawal of treatment), though the moral and legal significance attached to this distinction varies. South Korea and the United States permit passive euthanasia while prohibiting active euthanasia. Germany prohibits active euthanasia under Section 216 StGB but has constitutionally protected assisted suicide. The ECHR has upheld both approaches, finding that neither a blanket prohibition (Pretty v. UK) nor a permissive regime (Mortier v. Belgium) necessarily violates the Convention.
