## Summary

Warranty law -- the body of rules governing sellers' and manufacturers' obligations regarding the quality, fitness, and conformity of goods and services -- reflects fundamentally different approaches to consumer protection and risk allocation across legal traditions, ranging from statutory mandatory guarantees that cannot be contractually excluded to freely negotiable commercial warranties supplemented by implied terms. South Korea regulates warranties through the Korean Civil Code (민법) provisions on seller's warranty liability (담보책임, Articles 580-584), the Framework Act on Consumers (소비자기본법), and the Product Liability Act (제조물 책임법), with the Korea Fair Trade Commission and Korea Consumer Agency enforcing warranty-related consumer protections and maintaining detailed Consumer Dispute Resolution Standards (소비자분쟁해결기준) that specify repair, replacement, and refund timelines for hundreds of product categories. The United States addresses warranties through the Uniform Commercial Code (UCC) Article 2 provisions on express warranties (Section 2-313), implied warranty of merchantability (Section 2-314), and implied warranty of fitness for a particular purpose (Section 2-315), supplemented by the federal Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act (15 U.S.C. Sections 2301-2312), which regulates written warranties on consumer products without mandating that sellers provide them but restricting disclaimers of implied warranties when written warranties are given. Germany provides comprehensive warranty protection through the BGB's provisions on the seller's liability for material and legal defects (Sachmängelhaftung and Rechtsmängelhaftung, Sections 434-442 BGB), mandating a minimum two-year statutory warranty period (Gewährleistungsfrist) for consumer sales that cannot be shortened by contract, with remedies structured in a hierarchical system prioritizing repair or replacement (Nacherfüllung) before price reduction (Minderung) or rescission (Rücktritt). The EU has harmonized consumer warranty law through the Consumer Sales Directive (EU) 2019/771, which replaced Directive 1999/44/EC and establishes a minimum two-year legal guarantee with a one-year reversal of the burden of proof, while the Digital Content Directive (EU) 2019/770 extends conformity requirements to digital products.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's warranty regime combines Civil Code provisions on seller's liability with consumer protection legislation and detailed administrative standards.

**Civil Code (민법) -- Seller's Warranty Liability (담보책임):** Articles 580-584 establish the seller's liability for defects in sold goods. Article 580 provides that where a sold item has a hidden defect that the buyer could not have discovered through ordinary inspection, the buyer may rescind the contract or claim damages within six months of discovering the defect. Article 581 extends this to cases where the sold item lacks qualities that the seller guaranteed or represented. These provisions apply to all sales contracts but are supplemented by consumer-specific legislation.

### Consumer Dispute Resolution Standards

A distinctive feature of South Korea's warranty system is the Consumer Dispute Resolution Standards (소비자분쟁해결기준), established by the Korea Fair Trade Commission under the Framework Act on Consumers. These Standards provide detailed, product-specific warranty guidelines covering hundreds of product categories:

| Product Category | Warranty Period | Repair Guarantee |
|-----------------|----------------|------------------|
| Automobiles | 3 years or 60,000 km (general); 5 years or 100,000 km (powertrain) | Free repair during warranty period; lemon law provisions after 3 major repairs |
| Mobile phones | 1 year from purchase | Free repair; replacement if same defect recurs 3+ times |
| Home appliances (major) | 1-2 years depending on product type | Free repair; replacement after 4th repair for same defect |
| Clothing and footwear | 6 months to 1 year depending on material | Exchange or refund within 30 days; repair for defects within warranty |
| Software/digital content | 1 year from purchase or download | Patch/update; refund if not correctable |

### Product Liability Act (제조물 책임법)

The Product Liability Act, enacted in 2000 and most recently amended in 2017, imposes strict liability on manufacturers for damages caused by defective products. Article 3 establishes liability for manufacturing defects, design defects, and failure-to-warn defects. The 2017 amendment introduced a rebuttable presumption of defect where the damage occurred during the normal use of the product and the damage is of a kind ordinarily caused by a product defect, significantly easing the burden of proof for consumers.

### Korea Consumer Agency Enforcement

The Korea Consumer Agency (한국소비자원) plays an active role in warranty enforcement through its complaint investigation and mediation process. The Agency investigates consumer complaints, conducts product testing, and mediates disputes between consumers and businesses. Where a product fails to meet the Consumer Dispute Resolution Standards, the Agency can recommend corrective action and, if necessary, refer the matter to the KFTC for administrative enforcement.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States employs a multi-layered warranty system combining state-adopted UCC provisions, federal legislation, and common law principles.

**UCC Article 2 -- Sales:** The Uniform Commercial Code, adopted in all states (with variations), establishes three types of warranties:

**Express Warranties (Section 2-313):** Created by any affirmation of fact or promise made by the seller to the buyer relating to the goods that becomes part of the basis of the bargain, any description of the goods that becomes part of the basis of the bargain, or any sample or model that becomes part of the basis of the bargain. No specific words such as "warranty" or "guarantee" are required, and sellers need not intend to create a warranty.

**Implied Warranty of Merchantability (Section 2-314):** Arises automatically in every sale by a merchant with respect to goods of the kind sold. Merchantability requires that goods pass without objection in the trade, are fit for the ordinary purposes for which such goods are used, are adequately contained, packaged, and labeled, and conform to the affirmations of fact on the container or label.

**Implied Warranty of Fitness for a Particular Purpose (Section 2-315):** Arises when the seller knows or has reason to know any particular purpose for which the buyer requires the goods and the buyer relies on the seller's skill or judgment to select suitable goods.

### Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act

The federal Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act (15 U.S.C. Sections 2301-2312), enacted in 1975, does not require sellers to provide written warranties but imposes significant obligations on sellers who choose to do so:

| Requirement | Provision |
|-------------|-----------|
| Disclosure | Written warranties must clearly and conspicuously disclose terms in simple language (FTC Rule 16 CFR 701) |
| Full vs. Limited designation | Must be designated "Full" or "Limited" warranty (Section 2303) |
| Full warranty requirements | Must remedy defects within reasonable time without charge; no unreasonable conditions; implied warranties cannot be limited in duration |
| Implied warranty protection | When a written warranty is given, implied warranties cannot be disclaimed (Section 2308) |
| Federal cause of action | Consumers may bring suit in federal court (minimum $50,000 amount in controversy) or state court (Section 2310) |

### Lemon Laws

All 50 states have enacted automobile "lemon laws" providing enhanced warranty protections for consumers who purchase new vehicles with substantial defects that cannot be repaired after a reasonable number of attempts. While specific provisions vary, most lemon laws require manufacturers to provide a replacement vehicle or full refund after 3-4 unsuccessful repair attempts or 30 cumulative days out of service during the warranty period.

### Warranty Disclaimers

UCC Section 2-316 permits disclaimers of implied warranties, subject to specific requirements: disclaimers of the implied warranty of merchantability must mention "merchantability" and, if in writing, must be conspicuous; disclaimers of the implied warranty of fitness must be in writing and conspicuous; and language such as "as is" or "with all faults" can disclaim all implied warranties if the buyer has had the opportunity to examine the goods.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's warranty law, comprehensively reformed during the 2002 Schuldrechtsmodernisierung, provides among the strongest statutory warranty protections in the world, particularly for consumer transactions.

**Conformity Standard (Section 434 BGB):** A good is free of material defects if, at the time risk passes, it has the agreed quality (subjective requirements), is suitable for both the ordinary use and the specific use contemplated by the contract, has been delivered with the agreed accessories and instructions, and corresponds to what the buyer may reasonably expect based on public statements by the seller or manufacturer (objective requirements). The 2022 reform implementing Directive (EU) 2019/771 added assembly requirements and requirements regarding continuous supply of updates for goods with digital elements.

### Remedy Hierarchy (Nacherfüllung Priority)

German warranty law imposes a strict hierarchical remedy system:

**First Tier -- Nacherfüllung (Supplementary Performance, Section 439 BGB):** The buyer must first give the seller the opportunity to cure the defect through repair (Nachbesserung) or replacement (Nachlieferung), at the buyer's choice. The seller bears all costs of Nacherfüllung, including transport, labor, and materials (Section 439(2)). The seller may refuse the buyer's chosen form of Nacherfüllung only if it would involve disproportionate costs compared to the alternative form.

**Second Tier -- Price Reduction or Rescission (Sections 441, 323 BGB):** Only after Nacherfüllung has failed (typically after two unsuccessful attempts), been refused by the seller, or is unreasonable for the buyer, may the buyer proceed to price reduction (Minderung) or rescission (Rücktritt). Rescission is excluded for trivial defects.

**Parallel Claim -- Damages (Sections 280-281 BGB):** The buyer may claim damages alongside or instead of the hierarchical remedies, provided the seller is at fault (Verschulden).

### Consumer Sales Provisions (Sections 474-479 BGB)

Sections 474-479 BGB provide enhanced protection for consumer sales (Verbrauchsgüterkauf):

| Protection | Provision |
|-----------|-----------|
| Non-derogability | Warranty rights cannot be excluded or limited before notification of defect (Section 476) |
| Burden of proof reversal | Defects appearing within 1 year of delivery are presumed to have existed at delivery (Section 477) |
| Minimum warranty period | 2 years for new goods; may be reduced to 1 year for used goods by agreement (Section 475e) |
| Seller's recourse | Seller may recover warranty costs from upstream supplier (Section 478) |
| Guarantee declarations | Commercial guarantees (Garantieerklärungen) are binding and supplement statutory rights (Section 479) |

### Digital Products Warranty

Following transposition of EU Directive 2019/770, Sections 327-327u BGB (effective January 1, 2022) establish warranty rules for digital content and digital services. These provisions require conformity with both subjective (contractual) and objective (reasonable consumer expectation) standards, impose update obligations for the duration the consumer can reasonably expect to receive updates, and provide remedies analogous to the goods warranty hierarchy.

## ECHR & EU

### Consumer Sales Directive (EU) 2019/771

Directive (EU) 2019/771, adopted on May 20, 2019, and applicable from January 1, 2022, establishes the modern EU framework for consumer goods warranties, replacing Directive 1999/44/EC.

**Conformity Requirements (Articles 5-8):** The Directive establishes both subjective conformity requirements (conformity with the contract, including agreed quality, quantity, description, type, functionality, compatibility, and interoperability) and objective conformity requirements (fitness for ordinary purposes, conformity with reasonable consumer expectations based on the nature of the goods and public statements, delivery with accessories and instructions). Where the goods include digital elements, the seller must ensure that the consumer is informed of and supplied with updates necessary to keep the goods in conformity.

**Legal Guarantee (Article 10):** Member states must ensure a minimum two-year legal guarantee period from delivery. Member states may provide for a longer guarantee period.

**Burden of Proof (Article 11):** Any lack of conformity that becomes apparent within one year of delivery is presumed to have existed at delivery, unless this presumption is incompatible with the nature of the goods or the lack of conformity. Member states may extend this period to two years.

**Remedies (Articles 13-16):** The Directive establishes a hierarchical remedy system: the consumer is entitled first to have the goods brought into conformity through repair or replacement (at the consumer's choice, unless the chosen remedy is impossible or disproportionate); where repair or replacement fails, is not possible, or is not completed within a reasonable time, the consumer may obtain a proportionate price reduction or terminate the contract.

### Digital Content Directive (EU) 2019/770

Directive (EU) 2019/770 establishes conformity and remedy rules for digital content and digital services supplied to consumers, including streaming services, cloud storage, downloadable software, social media platforms, and digital files. The Directive applies to contracts where the consumer pays a price or provides personal data as counter-performance, extending warranty-like protections beyond traditional sales transactions.

### Product Liability Directive

The original Product Liability Directive 85/374/EEC established strict liability for defective products across the EU. The new Product Liability Directive (EU) 2024/2853, adopted on October 23, 2024, modernizes this framework by covering AI systems and software as "products," extending the scope to online platforms that present themselves as manufacturers, establishing a rebuttable presumption of defectiveness where the claimant faces excessive difficulty in proving the defect, and providing for collective redress mechanisms.

### CJEU Warranty Jurisprudence

**Weber and Putz (Joined Cases C-65/09 and C-87/09, 2011):** The Court held that the seller's obligation to replace defective goods includes bearing the costs of removing the defective goods and installing replacement goods, even where the original contract did not include installation, provided that such costs are not disproportionate.

**Faber v. Autobedrijf Hazet Ochten BV (Case C-497/13, 2015):** The Court clarified the scope of the burden of proof reversal under the then-applicable Directive 1999/44/EC, holding that the consumer need not prove the cause of the lack of conformity or attribute it to the seller.

## Comparative Analysis

### Philosophical Approaches

The jurisdictions reflect fundamentally different philosophies regarding the allocation of quality risk between sellers and buyers. Germany and the EU adopt the strongest pro-consumer position, treating the statutory warranty as a non-derogable consumer right that cannot be excluded or limited by contract in consumer transactions. South Korea takes a similar approach but with greater flexibility in warranty duration depending on product category through the Consumer Dispute Resolution Standards system. The United States occupies a middle position: while the UCC creates implied warranties, it permits their disclaimer through conspicuous language, subject to the restriction that disclaimers are prohibited when written warranties are given under the Magnuson-Moss Act.

### Remedy Structure

The remedy hierarchy represents one of the most significant practical differences. Germany and the EU impose a strict hierarchy requiring the consumer to permit the seller a cure opportunity (Nacherfüllung/repair or replacement) before proceeding to price reduction or rescission. This approach prioritizes contract preservation and gives the seller a "second chance" to perform. The United States permits the buyer broader remedial choice, including rejection of non-conforming goods (UCC Section 2-601, the "perfect tender rule") or revocation of acceptance (Section 2-608). South Korea does not impose a strict hierarchy, allowing consumers to choose among repair, replacement, refund, or damages based on the circumstances and the Consumer Dispute Resolution Standards.

### Duration and Proof

The statutory warranty duration and burden of proof rules create significant practical differences in consumer protection levels. The EU mandates a minimum two-year guarantee with a one-year burden of proof reversal. Germany goes further, with a one-year reversal period (previously six months, extended in 2022). South Korea's system varies by product category, with some products receiving longer warranty periods than the EU minimum. The United States has no mandatory warranty period, instead providing a four-year statute of limitations from delivery under UCC Section 2-725 within which warranty claims must be brought.

### Digital Economy Adaptation

The EU has led the adaptation of warranty law to digital products through Directive 2019/770 and the goods-with-digital-elements provisions of Directive 2019/771, creating a comprehensive framework that Germany has transposed. South Korea has addressed digital product warranties through the E-Commerce Consumer Protection Act but without the same level of specificity. The United States lacks a federal digital content warranty framework, creating regulatory uncertainty for consumers of digital products and services.
