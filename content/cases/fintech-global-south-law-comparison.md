## Summary

Fintech regulation across the Global South encompasses radically different approaches to governing digital payments, lending platforms, cryptocurrency, central bank digital currencies (CBDCs), and financial inclusion technologies, reflecting each region's financial system maturity, regulatory capacity, and development priorities. China's fintech regulatory crackdown, epitomized by the suspension of Ant Group's USD 37 billion IPO in November 2020 and subsequent restructuring, represents the most dramatic assertion of state authority over fintech globally, deploying the Financial Stability and Development Committee (金融稳定发展委员会), the People's Bank of China (PBOC), and sector-specific regulators to bring platform finance under the same prudential framework as traditional banking. India's Unified Payments Interface (UPI), developed by the National Payments Corporation of India (NPCI) under the Payment and Settlement Systems Act 2007 and NPCI's operating guidelines, has become the world's largest real-time payment system with over 13 billion monthly transactions by December 2024, while the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) has implemented a Digital Lending Guidelines framework (2022) addressing predatory app-based lending. Nigeria launched Africa's first CBDC, the eNaira, on 25 October 2021 under the Central Bank of Nigeria's (CBN) regulatory authority, while simultaneously imposing restrictions on cryptocurrency trading (CBN Circular of 5 February 2021, partially relaxed December 2023). Brazil's PIX instant payment system (launched November 2020) processes over 4 billion monthly transactions, and the Central Bank of Brazil (BCB) has established a regulatory sandbox (Resolução BCB 29/2020) and open finance framework. The Philippines' GCash (operated by Mynt/Globe Fintech Innovations) and Maya (formerly PayMaya) dominate mobile money, regulated by the Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas (BSP) under the National Payment Systems Act (Republic Act No. 11127, 2018).

## China (中国)

### Legal Framework

China's fintech regulatory framework underwent a dramatic transformation from permissive encouragement (2013-2020) to comprehensive oversight (2020-present), driven by concerns about systemic financial risk, consumer harm, and the concentration of financial power in platform companies:

- **Ant Group Regulatory Intervention (November 2020-present):** The suspension of Ant Group's planned USD 37 billion dual listing on the Shanghai and Hong Kong Stock Exchanges on 3 November 2020 -- two days before the scheduled IPO -- marked the beginning of China's fintech crackdown. The PBOC, CBIRC (now NFRA), CSRC, and State Administration of Foreign Exchange (SAFE) summoned Ant Group's controllers on 2 November 2020 and demanded restructuring. In April 2021, Ant Group was required to restructure as a financial holding company under PBOC supervision, bringing its credit, insurance, asset management, and payment businesses under the same prudential framework as banks. Ant Group submitted its financial holding company application in June 2022 and received approval in principle in July 2023, with a RMB 7.123 billion fine imposed for violations across all business lines.
- **Interim Measures for the Administration of Internet Loans of Commercial Banks (商业银行互联网贷款管理暂行办法, 2020):** Requires banks engaged in internet lending to independently conduct core risk management functions (credit assessment, loan approval) rather than outsourcing to technology platforms. Prohibits cross-regional internet lending by local banks.
- **Administrative Measures for the Internet-Based Marketing of Deposits by Commercial Banks (2021):** Prohibited third-party internet platforms from offering deposit products on behalf of banks, effectively ending Ant Group's and other fintech platforms' practice of distributing high-yield bank deposits.
- **Anti-Monopoly Enforcement in Platform Finance:** The SAMR's "Guidelines for Anti-Monopoly in the Platform Economy" (2021) apply to fintech platforms, targeting practices such as leveraging payment data for exclusionary lending, tying financial products to ecosystem services, and algorithmic discrimination in credit pricing.
- **Digital Yuan (数字人民币, e-CNY):** The PBOC's CBDC, in pilot since April 2020 across expanding cities (26 pilot regions by 2024). By December 2024, over 260 million individual e-CNY wallets and 100 million corporate wallets had been created, with cumulative transaction volume exceeding RMB 1.8 trillion (approximately USD 250 billion). The e-CNY is designed as M0 (cash replacement) with controlled anonymity ("anonymity for small values, traceability for large values," 小额匿名、大额可溯) and programmable payment capabilities. No standalone e-CNY legislation has been enacted; regulation operates through PBOC circulars and pilot program rules.
- **Cryptocurrency Prohibition:** The PBOC and ten ministries jointly issued the "Notice on Further Preventing and Dealing with the Risks of Virtual Currency Trading and Speculation" (24 September 2021), declaring all cryptocurrency-related business activities illegal, including exchanges, token issuance, derivatives trading, and mining. This built on earlier prohibitions in 2013 (ICOs) and 2017 (exchange closures).

### Key Principles

China's fintech regulatory philosophy evolved from "observe first, regulate later" (先观察、后规范) during the boom period to "same business, same rules" (同样业务、同样规则) in the crackdown phase. The principle that all financial activities must be licensed and supervised (金融活动必须持牌经营、纳入监管) became the foundational regulatory principle following the 2020 Ant Group intervention. The 2023 institutional reform creating the NFRA (replacing the CBIRC and absorbing some PBOC functions) consolidated financial supervision under a more powerful regulator with direct State Council authority.

### Notable Enforcement

- **Ant Group Fine (July 2023):** The PBOC, NFRA, CSRC, and SAFE jointly imposed a total fine of RMB 7.123 billion (approximately USD 985 million) on Ant Group and its subsidiaries for violations spanning payment services, micro-lending, insurance brokerage, fund distribution, and consumer protection. The fine concluded the three-year regulatory rectification process.
- **Didi Chuxing (financial services):** Didi's financial services arm was required to wind down non-compliant lending and insurance distribution operations as part of the broader regulatory action.
- **P2P Lending Platform Closure:** China's P2P lending sector peaked at over 5,000 platforms in 2015 before a systematic cleanup reduced the number to zero by November 2020. Total outstanding P2P loans declined from RMB 1.3 trillion (2017) to zero. The cleanup resulted in criminal prosecutions of operators, including the founder of Ezubao (e租宝) sentenced to life imprisonment for fraud involving RMB 59.8 billion from 900,000 investors.

## India (भारत)

### Legal Framework

India's fintech ecosystem has been built on a public digital infrastructure model ("India Stack") combining biometric identity (Aadhaar), bank accounts (Jan Dhan Yojana), and digital payments (UPI):

- **Payment and Settlement Systems Act, 2007 (PSS Act):** Grants the RBI authority to regulate all payment systems in India. Section 4 requires authorization from the RBI for operating a payment system. Section 18 empowers the RBI to issue directions to payment system operators.
- **Unified Payments Interface (UPI):** Developed by NPCI (a Section 8 company under the Companies Act promoted by the RBI and Indian Banks' Association), UPI enables instant, 24x7 interbank transfers through mobile devices using Virtual Payment Addresses (VPAs). UPI processed 13.14 billion transactions worth INR 20.64 lakh crore (approximately USD 248 billion) in December 2024. UPI's architecture is open (any bank or fintech can participate as a Third-Party Application Provider, or TPAP), interoperable (transactions work across any UPI-enabled app), and zero-cost for consumers (the RBI mandated zero merchant discount rate for UPI transactions up to INR 2,000 under Circular DPSS.CO.PD.No.629/02.01.014/2019-20).
- **RBI Digital Lending Guidelines (2 September 2022):** Addressing the predatory lending crisis (hundreds of unregulated lending apps, many Chinese-operated, charged effective interest rates of 100-500% APR and engaged in harassment, data theft, and extortion), the RBI issued comprehensive guidelines:
  - All digital loans must be disbursed and repaid directly between the borrower's bank account and the Regulated Entity's (RE's) bank account -- no pass-through of funds via lending apps.
  - Digital Lending Apps (DLAs) acting as agents of REs must prominently disclose their RE relationship and cannot charge fees directly to borrowers.
  - A key fact statement (KFS) disclosing APR, recovery mechanisms, and grievance redressal must be provided before loan execution.
  - First Loss Default Guarantee (FLDG) by fintech platforms to REs is permitted up to 5% of the loan portfolio (RBI Circular June 2023).
- **RBI Regulatory Sandbox (2019):** Four cohorts have been conducted: (1) retail payments, (2) cross-border payments, (3) MSME lending, and (4) prevention of financial fraud. Entities admitted undergo a testing phase with regulatory relaxations, followed by evaluation for full licensing or exit.
- **Digital Rupee (e₹):** The RBI launched pilot programs for both wholesale (e₹-W, November 2022) and retail (e₹-R, December 2022) CBDCs. The retail pilot involves select banks issuing digital rupee tokens to customers via dedicated wallets. Adoption has been limited, with approximately 4.4 million retail e₹ transactions by December 2024.
- **Cryptocurrency Taxation (Finance Act 2022):** Section 115BBH imposes a flat 30% tax on income from transfer of "virtual digital assets" (VDAs), with no deduction for expenses other than cost of acquisition. Section 194S imposes 1% TDS on VDA transfers above INR 10,000. These provisions effectively discourage domestic crypto trading without an outright ban. The RBI has consistently advocated for a complete ban on private cryptocurrencies.

### Key Principles

India's fintech governance is built on the principle of "same activity, same regulation" combined with a "regulatory stack" approach that leverages public digital infrastructure (Aadhaar, UPI, Account Aggregator) to enable private innovation within a regulated framework. The Account Aggregator (AA) framework (RBI Master Direction on NBFC-AA, 2016, effective September 2021) enables consent-based financial data sharing between regulated entities, creating the foundation for open banking in India without using the term.

### Notable Cases

- **RBI Moratorium on Paytm Payments Bank (January 2024):** The RBI ordered Paytm Payments Bank Limited (PPBL) to stop accepting new deposits, credit transactions, and top-ups after 29 February 2024, citing persistent non-compliance, including KYC deficiencies and supervisory concerns. The action sent PPBL's parent company One97 Communications' stock down by over 40%, and resulted in the migration of Paytm's payment services to other bank partners.
- **Google Pay/PhonePe UPI Market Share Concentration:** Google Pay and PhonePe (Walmart subsidiary) together control approximately 85% of UPI transaction volume, raising concerns about market concentration in a public digital infrastructure. The NPCI issued a 30% market share cap for third-party UPI apps (effective January 2025), though enforcement has been delayed.
- **Chinese Lending App Crackdowns (2020-2024):** Indian law enforcement (Enforcement Directorate, state police cyber cells) arrested operators and froze assets of over 100 predatory digital lending apps with Chinese ownership or control, including seizures exceeding INR 4,600 crore under PMLA.

## Africa (Nigeria and South Africa)

### Nigeria

#### Legal Framework

- **eNaira -- Africa's First CBDC (launched 25 October 2021):** The eNaira is a CBDC issued by the Central Bank of Nigeria, operating on a two-tier distribution model (CBN issues eNaira to licensed financial institutions, which distribute to end users through eNaira wallets). Key design features include:
  - **Speed Wallets:** Tiered KYC (Tier 0: phone number only, daily transaction limit NGN 20,000; Tier 1: BVN-linked, daily limit NGN 200,000; Tier 2/3: full KYC, higher limits).
  - **Non-interest bearing:** The eNaira does not earn interest, to avoid bank disintermediation.
  - **Adoption:** Limited -- approximately 13 million wallets created but fewer than 1.5 million active users (0.5% of the population) by 2024. Low merchant acceptance and mobile money competition from OPay, PalmPay, and Kuda have constrained adoption.
- **CBN Cryptocurrency Circular (5 February 2021):** The CBN directed all deposit money banks, non-bank financial institutions, and other financial institutions to close accounts of persons and entities involved in cryptocurrency trading. This de-banking approach drew criticism from Nigeria's tech community. In December 2023, the CBN issued a new circular partially relaxing the position, requiring VASPs to obtain CBN approval and implementing transaction monitoring rather than blanket prohibition.
- **SEC Nigeria -- Digital Assets Regulation (2022):** The Securities and Exchange Commission classified digital assets as securities and issued the Rules on Issuance, Offering Platforms, and Custody of Digital Assets, requiring exchanges to register as "Digital Asset Offering Platforms" (DAOPs) and implement KYC/AML measures.
- **PSSD Framework 2020 (CBN Payment System Vision 2025):** The CBN's vision document targeting financial inclusion through mobile money licensing, agent banking, and tiered KYC. The Shared Agent Network Expansion Facility (SANEF) has deployed over 1.6 million agent touchpoints.

### South Africa

#### Legal Framework

- **Financial Sector Regulation Act (FSRA, No. 9 of 2017):** Established the "Twin Peaks" regulatory model -- the Prudential Authority (PA, within the SARB) for prudential regulation and the Financial Sector Conduct Authority (FSCA) for market conduct regulation. Fintech entities fall under one or both regulators depending on their activities.
- **National Payment System Act (No. 78 of 1998):** Regulates the South African payment system, administered by the SARB. The SARB has adopted a "risk-proportionate" approach to fintech payments regulation, with the Payment Systems Act Amendment Bill (proposed) seeking to broaden oversight to include new payment service providers.
- **FSCA Declaration of Crypto Assets as Financial Products (October 2022):** Under the Financial Advisory and Intermediary Services Act (FAIS Act 37/2002), the FSCA declared crypto assets as financial products, requiring all crypto asset service providers (CASPs) to be licensed as Financial Service Providers (FSPs). Over 130 CASP applications were received by the April 2023 deadline, with 59 approved by December 2024.
- **Project Khokha (SARB):** A wholesale CBDC experiment conducted in two phases: Phase 1 (2018) tested DLT-based interbank payments; Phase 2 (2022) explored tokenized bond issuance and settlement. South Africa has not committed to a retail CBDC.
- **Intergovernmental Fintech Working Group (IFWG):** Co-chaired by the SARB, FSCA, National Treasury, FIC, and National Credit Regulator, the IFWG coordinates fintech policy. Its Innovation Hub provides a regulatory testing environment (not a formal sandbox).

## Latin America (Brazil, Mexico, and Colombia)

### Legal Framework

Latin America has produced some of the world's most innovative fintech regulatory frameworks, driven by large unbanked populations and regulator willingness to experiment:

- **Brazil -- PIX Instant Payment System (launched 16 November 2020):** Developed and operated by the Central Bank of Brazil (BCB), PIX enables instant, 24x7, free-of-charge person-to-person transfers using keys (CPF/CNPJ, phone number, email, or random key). PIX surpassed 4 billion monthly transactions by 2024, used by over 150 million individuals and 14 million businesses -- making it the world's second-most-used real-time payment system after UPI. PIX is mandatory for all banks and payment institutions with over 500,000 customer accounts. The BCB has extended PIX functionality to include PIX Parcelado (installment payments), PIX Saque/Troco (cash withdrawal at merchants), and PIX Automático (recurring payments).
- **Brazil -- Regulatory Sandbox (Resolução BCB No. 29/2020):** The BCB's sandbox allows qualifying entities to test innovative financial products under temporary authorization with modified prudential requirements. Three cohorts have been conducted, covering credit operations, payment services, and insurance.
- **Brazil -- Open Finance (Resolução Conjunta No. 1/2020):** Brazil's mandatory open banking/open finance framework, phased in from February 2021 to December 2022, requires participating institutions to share customer data (with consent), product information, and transaction initiation capabilities through standardized APIs. By 2024, over 1,300 institutions participated with 46 million active consents.
- **Brazil -- Legal Framework for Virtual Assets (Lei 14.478/2022, effective June 2023):** Establishes the BCB as the primary regulator of virtual asset service providers (VASPs), requiring licensing, segregation of customer assets, capital requirements, and AML/CFT compliance. The BCB is developing implementing regulations through a phased consultation process.
- **Brazil -- DREX (Digital Real):** The BCB's wholesale CBDC project (formerly "Digital Real"), using DLT for tokenized financial asset settlement. A pilot with 16 consortia (banks, fintechs, cooperatives) was launched in 2023, testing tokenized bank deposits, government bonds, and smart contracts. DREX is designed for institutional use rather than retail payments (PIX serves the retail function).
- **Brazil -- Sociedades de Crédito Direto (SCD) and Sociedades de Empréstimo entre Pessoas (SEP) -- Resolução 4.656/2018:** Created two new types of regulated fintech lenders: SCDs (direct credit companies, lending from own capital) and SEPs (peer-to-peer lending platforms connecting borrowers and investors). By 2024, approximately 130 SCDs and 40 SEPs were licensed by the BCB.
- **Mexico -- Fintech Law (Ley para Regular las Instituciones de Tecnología Financiera, 2018):** Mexico was the first Latin American country to enact comprehensive fintech legislation, establishing two categories of Financial Technology Institutions (ITFs): Electronic Payment Fund Institutions (IFPEs, similar to e-money issuers) and Collective Financing Institutions (IFCs, covering crowdfunding/P2P lending). The law also regulates "virtual assets" (cryptocurrencies), requiring ITFs and financial entities to obtain Banxico authorization for operations involving virtual assets. The CNBV (National Banking and Securities Commission) supervises ITFs. By 2024, approximately 60 ITFs were licensed.
- **Mexico -- CoDi/DiMo:** The Banxico-operated mobile payment platform (originally CoDi -- Cobro Digital, relaunched as DiMo -- Dinero Móvil in 2023) enables QR code and NFC-based payments linked to bank accounts. Adoption has been limited compared to PIX or UPI, with fewer than 20 million enrolled users.
- **Colombia -- SFC Regulatory Sandbox (Decree 1234/2020):** The Superintendencia Financiera de Colombia operates a regulatory sandbox allowing fintech companies to test innovative products for up to two years with relaxed requirements. Focus areas include digital lending, mobile payments, and insurance technology.

### Notable Cases

- **Brazil -- BCB v. PicPay/Mercado Pago Remuneration Controversy (2022-2023):** The BCB intervened to prevent PIX-linked savings accounts offered by fintechs from creating regulatory arbitrage with traditional deposit-taking, limiting how fintechs could remunerate customer balances held in payment accounts.
- **Mexico -- CNBV Enforcement Against Unlicensed Fintech Operations (2023):** The CNBV issued multiple warnings and enforcement actions against entities operating as IFPEs or IFCs without licenses, including ordering the closure of platforms operating in the regulatory gray zone.

## Southeast Asia (Indonesia and Philippines)

### Legal Framework

- **Philippines -- National Payment Systems Act (NPSA, RA 11127, 2018):** Grants the Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas (BSP) oversight authority over all payment systems in the Philippines, including fintech payment service providers. The BSP registers Operators of Payment Systems (OPS) and Payment System Management Bodies (PSMBs).
- **Philippines -- BSP Circular No. 1153/2022 (Regulatory Sandbox Framework):** Establishes a regulatory sandbox for testing innovative financial products and services, with a testing period of up to 24 months, allowing qualified fintech companies to operate with modified regulatory requirements.
- **Philippines -- GCash and Maya:** GCash (operated by Mynt/Globe Fintech Innovations Inc., a subsidiary of Globe Telecom with investments from Ant Financial and Ayala Corporation) had over 93 million registered users by 2024, processing over USD 74 billion in gross transaction value. Maya (formerly PayMaya, rebranded under the Voyager Innovations/PLDT group) had approximately 54 million registered users. Together, they dominate Philippine mobile money, particularly for remittances (PHP 2.5 trillion in overseas Filipino remittances in 2023), bill payments, and e-commerce.
- **Philippines -- BSP Circular No. 1108/2020 (Virtual Asset Service Providers):** Requires all VASPs (crypto exchanges, custodians) to register with the BSP as Remittance and Transfer Companies, implement KYC/AML measures, and obtain a Certificate of Authority. The BSP was one of the earliest central banks globally to license crypto exchanges (since 2017, Circular No. 944).
- **Philippines -- Digital Banks (BSP Circular No. 1133/2021):** Framework for digital banks -- banks that offer financial products and services processed end-to-end through a digital platform. Six digital bank licenses have been issued: Tonik, GoTyme, Maya Bank, UNObank, UnionDigital, and Overseas Filipino Bank. Digital banks must maintain minimum capital of PHP 1 billion.
- **Indonesia -- OJK P2P Lending Regulation (POJK 10/2022, replacing POJK 77/2016):** Regulates information technology-based money lending services (peer-to-peer lending or P2P lending). Key provisions include: maximum lending rate caps (total economic cost must not exceed 0.4% per day of principal outstanding); mandatory registration and licensing with OJK; prohibition on using violence or threats in debt collection; and minimum registered capital of IDR 2.5 billion (for registration) and IDR 25 billion (for licensing). By 2024, 102 licensed P2P lending platforms operated in Indonesia, down from over 150 in 2019 following OJK enforcement.
- **Indonesia -- QRIS (Quick Response Code Indonesian Standard):** Launched by Bank Indonesia in 2019, QRIS standardizes QR code payments across all payment service providers, enabling interoperability between GoPay, OVO, DANA, ShopeePay, and bank-issued wallets. Over 30 million merchants accepted QRIS by 2024, with transaction volume exceeding IDR 230 trillion.
- **Indonesia -- Bappebti Crypto Regulation (Regulation No. 5/2019, replaced by Regulation No. 8/2021):** The Commodity Futures Trading Regulatory Agency (Bappebti) regulates crypto assets as tradeable commodity assets (not currencies or securities). Registered crypto exchanges must maintain minimum capital of IDR 50 billion and segregate customer assets. Regulatory jurisdiction is transferring from Bappebti to OJK effective January 2025 under the Financial Sector Development and Strengthening Law (UU P2SK, Law 4/2023).

### Notable Developments

- **Philippines -- E-Money Adoption:** The Philippines' e-money transaction volume grew from PHP 1.3 trillion in 2020 to PHP 4.7 trillion in 2023, driven by COVID-19 pandemic digital adoption and the BSP's Digital Payments Transformation Roadmap targeting 50% of payment transactions to be digital by 2023 (achieved at approximately 52.8%).
- **Indonesia -- P2P Lending Crisis and Reform:** The P2P lending sector experienced significant consumer harm from 2018-2020, with reports of predatory lending, illegal data harvesting, and violent debt collection by unlicensed operators. OJK blocked over 7,000 unlicensed lending apps and filed criminal complaints. The reformed POJK 10/2022 imposed rate caps, stricter licensing, and consumer protection requirements.
- **Indonesia -- UU P2SK (Financial Sector Development and Strengthening Law, Law No. 4/2023):** This omnibus financial law strengthened OJK's authority over fintech, transferred crypto regulation from Bappebti to OJK (effective January 2025), established the Sovereign Wealth Fund (Lembaga Pengelola Investasi), and enhanced deposit insurance (through LPS). The law provides OJK with authority to regulate "financial sector technology innovation" comprehensively.

## Comparative Analysis

### Public Digital Infrastructure vs. Platform-Led Models

The Global South reveals two distinct fintech development pathways: (1) **Public digital infrastructure** (India's UPI/India Stack, Brazil's PIX/Open Finance, Indonesia's QRIS), where central banks and government entities build open, interoperable payment rails that private fintechs access competitively; and (2) **Platform-led ecosystems** (China's Alipay/WeChat Pay before the crackdown, Philippines' GCash, Nigeria's OPay), where private super-apps create integrated financial services ecosystems. China's regulatory crackdown represents a forcible transition from model (2) to model (1), with the e-CNY designed as public infrastructure to reduce dependence on Ant Group and Tencent.

India's UPI and Brazil's PIX have demonstrated that public digital infrastructure can achieve massive scale (combined 17+ billion monthly transactions) while maintaining regulatory control and interoperability. This model is increasingly influential in the Global South, with NPCI offering UPI licensing to other countries (Singapore, UAE, France, Sri Lanka).

### CBDC Approaches

The five regions represent the full spectrum of CBDC development: (1) **Advanced pilot** (China -- e-CNY at scale with hundreds of billions in transactions); (2) **Launched but low adoption** (Nigeria -- eNaira with minimal uptake); (3) **Wholesale pilot** (Brazil -- DREX for tokenized assets); (4) **Early-stage retail pilot** (India -- Digital Rupee with limited transactions); and (5) **Research/study phase** (Philippines, Indonesia, South Africa). The eNaira's adoption challenges (low merchant acceptance, competition from mobile money, trust deficits) serve as a cautionary tale for other countries considering retail CBDCs.

### Cryptocurrency Regulation Spectrum

The spectrum ranges from: **Comprehensive ban** (China) → **Tax-based deterrence** (India -- 30% tax) → **Conditional licensing** (Philippines, South Africa, Brazil) → **Evolving/ambiguous** (Nigeria -- from ban to partial reopening; Indonesia -- commodity classification). The lack of a unified international framework has enabled regulatory arbitrage, with crypto businesses relocating to more permissive jurisdictions.

### Digital Lending Regulation

All five regions have confronted predatory digital lending, with India, Indonesia, and Nigeria experiencing the most severe consumer harm from unregulated lending apps. Regulatory responses have followed a common pattern: initial permissive period → consumer harm crisis → regulatory crackdown (India's Digital Lending Guidelines 2022, Indonesia's POJK 10/2022, Nigeria's CBN enforcement). China's P2P lending sector cleanup (5,000+ platforms to zero) represents the most extreme regulatory intervention, while Brazil's SCD/SEP framework demonstrates a more market-friendly approach.
