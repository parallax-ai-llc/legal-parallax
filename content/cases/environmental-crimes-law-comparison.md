## Summary

Environmental crime -- the violation of laws designed to protect the natural environment, public health, and ecological systems -- has emerged as one of the fastest-growing categories of transnational criminal activity, with INTERPOL and the UN Environment Programme estimating its annual value at $91 billion to $259 billion globally. Despite universal recognition of the threat, the legal frameworks for criminalizing and punishing environmental offenses vary dramatically across jurisdictions. South Korea employs a multi-statute regime anchored by the Act on the Control and Aggravated Punishment of Environmental Offenses (APEO), imposing strict liability and penalties of up to 10 years imprisonment for organized waste dumping. The United States enforces environmental crimes through a patchwork of powerful federal statutes -- the Clean Air Act, Clean Water Act, and Resource Conservation and Recovery Act -- backed by the Environmental Protection Agency's Criminal Investigation Division, with penalties reaching 15 years imprisonment for knowing endangerment offenses. Germany criminalizes environmental offenses under Chapter 29 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), sections 324 through 330d, with penalties of up to 10 years for the most serious pollution offenses endangering public water supplies, though prosecution has historically targeted individuals rather than corporations. At the supranational level, the European Union's landmark Environmental Crime Directive 2024/1203, which entered into force on 20 May 2024 and must be transposed by Member States by 21 May 2026, doubled the catalogue of criminal offenses from nine to twenty, introduced a near-ecocide qualified offense carrying a minimum-maximum penalty of at least 8 years imprisonment, and established corporate liability with fines of up to 5% of worldwide annual turnover or EUR 40 million. The European Court of Human Rights has further reinforced environmental protection through its interpretation of Articles 2 (right to life) and 8 (right to private and family life) of the ECHR, culminating in the 2024 ruling in Verein KlimaSeniorinnen Schweiz v. Switzerland, the Court's first climate change judgment.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea regulates environmental crimes through a layered statutory system. The **Framework Act on Environmental Policy** (환경정책기본법) establishes the foundational principle that any person who causes environmental pollution or damage bears strict liability for compensation, regardless of negligence. This strict liability standard is among the most stringent in Asia.

Criminal enforcement is carried out under several specialized statutes:

- **Act on the Control and Aggravated Punishment of Environmental Offenses (APEO)** (환경범죄 등의 단속 및 가중처벌에 관한 법률): Provides enhanced penalties for serious environmental crimes, including illegal discharge of pollutants causing harm to human health or the environment.
- **Clean Air Conservation Act** (대기환경보전법): Criminalizes unauthorized emissions of air pollutants, with penalties including imprisonment and fines for violations of emission standards.
- **Water Environment Conservation Act** (물환경보전법): Prohibits illegal discharge of wastewater and pollutants into water bodies.
- **Wastes Control Act** (폐기물관리법): Regulates the treatment, storage, and disposal of waste, with severe criminal penalties for illegal dumping.
- **Soil Environment Conservation Act** (토양환경보전법): Addresses soil contamination and imposes obligations for remediation.

### Penalties

| Offense | Maximum Imprisonment | Maximum Fine |
|---------|---------------------|-------------|
| Illegal discharge causing death or serious injury (APEO) | Life imprisonment | Unlimited (proportional) |
| Organized illegal waste dumping (Wastes Control Act, Art. 63) | 2-10 years | 2x to 10x the value of proceeds |
| Violation of emission standards (Clean Air Conservation Act) | 7 years | KRW 100 million |
| Illegal wastewater discharge (Water Environment Conservation Act) | 7 years | KRW 100 million |
| Falsification of environmental monitoring data | 5 years | KRW 50 million |

Under the dual punishment provision (양벌규정), both the individual offender and the employing corporation can be prosecuted simultaneously. Administrative fines under the APEO were increased to cover clean-up costs plus 5% of sales revenue.

### Landmark Cases

**Humidifier Disinfectant Scandal (2011-present):** South Korea's worst consumer product disaster involved toxic chemicals in humidifier disinfectants sold by multiple manufacturers, including Reckitt Benckiser (brand name "Oxy Ssak Ssak"), SK Chemical, and Aekyung Industrial. The chemicals caused fatal lung diseases, with 1,262 confirmed deaths and the Social Disasters Commission estimating approximately 20,366 total deaths including unreported cases. In January 2017, Shin Hyun-woo, former CEO of Oxy from 1991 to 2005, was sentenced to 7 years imprisonment for homicide through professional negligence and false advertising. Additional executives were indicted in 2019, though in 2024 the Supreme Court ordered retrials for the former heads of SK Chemical and Aekyung Industrial, overturning their 4-year prison sentences on the ground that it was unclear whether victims were harmed solely by their products or by a combination of disinfectants.

**Gumi Hydrofluoric Acid Leak (2012):** A leak of 8 tons of hydrofluoric acid at a chemical plant in Gumi, North Gyeongsang Province, killed 5 workers and sickened thousands of nearby residents. The incident led to significant revisions of the Chemical Substances Control Act and the introduction of the Chemical Substances Registration and Evaluation Act (K-REACH) in 2015.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States enforces environmental crimes through a comprehensive network of federal statutes, primarily administered by the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) and prosecuted by the Department of Justice's Environment and Natural Resources Division (ENRD). The principal federal environmental criminal statutes are:

- **Clean Air Act (CAA), 42 U.S.C. sections 7401-7671q:** Criminalizes knowing violations of emission standards, releases of hazardous air pollutants, and failures to comply with permit requirements. Section 113(c) establishes criminal penalties.
- **Clean Water Act (CWA), 33 U.S.C. sections 1251-1387:** Prohibits the unauthorized discharge of pollutants into navigable waters of the United States. Section 309(c) provides criminal penalties for negligent and knowing violations.
- **Resource Conservation and Recovery Act (RCRA), 42 U.S.C. sections 6901-6992k:** Establishes a "cradle-to-grave" regulatory system for hazardous waste generation, transportation, treatment, storage, and disposal. Section 3008(d) and (e) provide criminal penalties.
- **Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act (CERCLA/Superfund), 42 U.S.C. sections 9601-9675:** Imposes strict, joint and several liability for cleanup of hazardous waste sites and criminalizes failure to report releases.
- **Toxic Substances Control Act (TSCA), 15 U.S.C. sections 2601-2697:** Regulates the manufacture, processing, distribution, use, and disposal of chemical substances.

### Penalties

| Statute | Violation Type | Maximum Imprisonment | Maximum Fine per Day |
|---------|---------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Clean Water Act | Negligent violation | 1 year | $25,000 |
| Clean Water Act | Knowing violation | 3 years (6 for repeat) | $50,000 |
| Clean Water Act | Knowing endangerment | 15 years (30 for repeat) | $250,000 ($1M for repeat) |
| Clean Air Act | Knowing violation | 5 years | $250,000 |
| Clean Air Act | Knowing endangerment | 15 years | $1,000,000 |
| RCRA | Knowing violation | 5 years (10 for repeat) | $50,000 |
| RCRA | Knowing endangerment | 15 years | $1,000,000 |
| CERCLA | Failure to report release | 3 years (5 for repeat) | $250,000 |

Civil penalties are adjusted annually for inflation. As of 2025, maximum civil penalties under the Clean Air Act reached $119,164 per day per violation, and Clean Water Act civil penalties reached $65,618 per day per violation.

### Landmark Cases

**BP Deepwater Horizon Oil Spill (2010):** BP Exploration and Production Inc. pleaded guilty to 14 criminal counts -- including 11 counts of felony manslaughter, 1 count of felony obstruction of Congress, and violations of the Clean Water Act and Migratory Bird Treaty Act -- arising from the explosion of the Deepwater Horizon drilling rig on April 20, 2010, which killed 11 workers and caused the largest marine oil spill in history. BP was sentenced to pay $4 billion in criminal fines and penalties, the largest criminal resolution in United States history. The total resolution, including civil and state claims, exceeded $20 billion.

**Volkswagen Emissions Fraud (2015-2017):** Volkswagen AG pleaded guilty to three felony counts -- conspiracy to defraud the United States, wire fraud, and violation of the Clean Air Act -- for installing defeat device software in approximately 590,000 diesel vehicles sold in the United States designed to cheat on federal emissions tests. VW was sentenced to pay $2.8 billion in criminal penalties. Individual prosecutions resulted in a 40-month prison sentence for engineer James Liang and an 84-month (7-year) sentence for Oliver Schmidt, former general manager of VW's U.S. Environment and Engineering Office.

**W.R. Grace Asbestos Contamination (1999-2009):** W.R. Grace & Co. and several executives were indicted in 2005 on federal charges of knowingly releasing asbestos from a vermiculite mine in Libby, Montana, causing hundreds of deaths and illnesses among residents. Although the company was acquitted of criminal charges in 2009, the case led to a $250 million Superfund cleanup and became a landmark in the development of environmental justice enforcement.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German environmental criminal law (Umweltstrafrecht) is codified primarily in **Chapter 29 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB)**, titled "Offences Against the Environment" (Straftaten gegen die Umwelt), encompassing sections 324 through 330d. This chapter was introduced in 1980 through the 18th Criminal Law Amendment Act and has been expanded several times since.

Key provisions include:

- **Section 324 StGB -- Water Pollution** (Gewasserverunreinigung): Criminalizes the unauthorized contamination of a body of water or the adverse alteration of its qualities. Punishable by up to 5 years imprisonment.
- **Section 324a StGB -- Soil Pollution** (Bodenverunreinigung): Prohibits the introduction of substances into the soil in violation of administrative duties, causing potential harm. Up to 5 years imprisonment.
- **Section 325 StGB -- Air Pollution** (Luftverunreinigung): Criminalizes the release of substances causing air pollution outside a facility in violation of administrative duties. Up to 5 years imprisonment.
- **Section 326 StGB -- Unauthorized Waste Disposal** (Unerlaubter Umgang mit Abfallen): Prohibits the collection, transport, treatment, or disposal of waste outside authorized facilities. Up to 5 years imprisonment.
- **Section 327 StGB -- Unauthorized Operation of Facilities** (Unerlaubtes Betreiben von Anlagen): Criminalizes operating a nuclear facility, waste disposal site, or similar installation without the required permit. Up to 5 years imprisonment.
- **Section 328 StGB -- Unauthorized Handling of Radioactive Materials** (Unerlaubter Umgang mit radioaktiven Stoffen): Up to 5 years imprisonment.
- **Section 329 StGB -- Endangerment of Protected Areas** (Gefahrdung schutzbedurfter Gebiete): Protects nature reserves, water protection areas, and other specially designated zones. Up to 5 years imprisonment.
- **Section 330 StGB -- Especially Serious Environmental Offenses** (Besonders schwerer Fall einer Umweltstraftat): Covers cases involving risk to another person's life, serious danger to the health of a large number of persons, or danger to the public water supply. Punishable by imprisonment of 1 to 10 years; in especially severe cases endangering the public water supply, up to 15 years.
- **Section 330a StGB -- Serious Endangerment through Release of Poisons** (Schwere Gefahrdung durch Freisetzen von Giften): Criminalizes the release of poisons causing danger of death or serious injury to another person or danger to the health of a large number of people. Up to 10 years imprisonment; at least 1 year if the act causes death.

Supplementary criminal provisions are found in the Federal Water Act (Wasserhaushaltsgesetz), Circular Economy Act (Kreislaufwirtschaftsgesetz), Chemicals Act (Chemikaliengesetz), Federal Nature Conservation Act (Bundesnaturschutzgesetz), and Federal Immission Control Act (Bundes-Immissionsschutzgesetz).

### Corporate Liability

A distinctive feature of German environmental criminal law is its historical limitation to individual criminal liability. German law does not recognize corporate criminal liability (Unternehmensstrafrecht) in the traditional sense. However, since 2017, section 73b StGB permits the confiscation of proceeds of crime from corporations if the criminal act was committed by an employee. Additionally, the Ordnungswidrigkeitengesetz (Administrative Offenses Act), section 30, allows for administrative fines of up to EUR 10 million to be imposed on legal entities for regulatory violations by their officers.

Germany is currently required to transpose EU Directive 2024/1203 by 21 May 2026, which will necessitate the introduction of corporate criminal liability for environmental offenses. A draft bill by the Federal Ministry of Justice was published in December 2025, proposing amendments to expand the StGB's environmental chapter and introduce a new qualified offense akin to ecocide.

### Landmark Cases

**Sandoz Rhine Chemical Spill (1986):** On November 1, 1986, a fire at the Sandoz chemical warehouse in Schweizerhalle near Basel, Switzerland, released approximately 30 tons of pesticides, mercury, and other toxic chemicals into the Rhine River, killing all aquatic life over a 400-kilometer stretch extending through Germany, France, and the Netherlands. While the fire occurred in Switzerland, the transboundary contamination prompted Germany to significantly strengthen its environmental criminal provisions and led to the establishment of the International Commission for the Protection of the Rhine's Rhine Action Programme.

**Diesel Emissions Prosecutions (2015-present):** Following the Volkswagen emissions scandal, German prosecutors in Braunschweig pursued criminal investigations against VW executives. In 2023, charges were brought against former VW CEO Martin Winterkorn for fraud and market manipulation related to the installation of defeat devices in diesel engines. The case highlighted limitations in German law's ability to prosecute environmental offenses committed through corporate structures.

### Enforcement Statistics

In 2016, German police registered approximately 12,000 environmental offenses. Of these, 38% involved illegal waste handling, 33% involved animal protection violations, and 13% concerned water pollution. Conviction rates for environmental crimes remain comparatively low, reflecting the complexity of proving causation in pollution cases.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Environmental Crime Directive 2024/1203

The European Union adopted **Directive 2024/1203 on the protection of the environment through criminal law** on 11 April 2024, replacing the 2008 Environmental Crime Directive (Directive 2008/99/EC). The new Directive entered into force on 20 May 2024, with a transposition deadline of 21 May 2026 for all EU Member States.

#### Expanded Catalogue of Offenses

The Directive doubles the list of criminal offenses from 9 to 20, including:

1. Illegal discharge of pollutants into air, water, or soil
2. Illegal collection, transport, treatment, or disposal of waste
3. Illegal shipment of waste
4. Illegal operation of installations handling hazardous substances
5. Illegal manufacture, use, storage, or disposal of radioactive materials
6. Illegal killing, destruction, or taking of specimens of protected wild fauna or flora
7. Illegal trading in wildlife specimens
8. Illegal trading in timber and timber products (new)
9. Illegal recycling of polluting ship components (new)
10. Serious breaches of chemicals legislation (REACH, biocides, etc.) (new)
11. Illegal use of mercury and fluorinated greenhouse gases (new)
12. Illegal introduction or spread of invasive alien species (new)
13. Illegal depletion or failure to manage water resources (new)
14. Ship-source pollution in violation of MARPOL (new)
15. Illegal deforestation or forest degradation (new)

#### Penalties for Natural Persons

The Directive establishes a graduated penalty framework with minimum-maximum imprisonment terms:

| Offense Category | Minimum-Maximum Imprisonment |
|-----------------|----------------------------|
| Standard environmental offenses | At least 5 years maximum |
| Offenses causing or likely to cause death or serious injury | At least 10 years maximum |
| Qualified offenses ("ecocide") -- widespread, long-lasting, and irreversible environmental damage | At least 8 years maximum |

#### Corporate Liability and Fines

For **legal persons** (corporations and other entities), the Directive provides two alternative fining methods:

| Method | Penalty Level |
|--------|--------------|
| Turnover-based fines (serious offenses) | Up to 5% of total worldwide annual turnover |
| Fixed amount fines (serious offenses) | Up to EUR 40 million |
| Turnover-based fines (other offenses) | Up to 3% of total worldwide annual turnover |
| Fixed amount fines (other offenses) | Up to EUR 24 million |

Additional non-criminal sanctions include obligations to restore the environment, payment of compensation, exclusion from public procurement, withdrawal of permits, and bans on certain business activities.

#### Qualified Offenses (Near-Ecocide)

Article 3(3) of the Directive introduces a qualified offense for conduct that causes "destruction of, or widespread and substantial damage which is either irreversible or long-lasting to, an ecosystem of considerable size or environmental value, or to a habitat within a protected site, or widespread and substantial damage which is either irreversible or long-lasting to the quality of air, the quality of soil, or the quality of water." This provision, while stopping short of creating a standalone crime of "ecocide," represents the most significant step toward criminalizing large-scale environmental destruction at the supranational level.

### ECHR Environmental Jurisprudence

Although the European Convention on Human Rights contains no explicit right to a healthy environment, the European Court of Human Rights (ECtHR) has developed a substantial body of environmental case law through its interpretation of existing Convention rights:

- **Article 2 (Right to Life):** Imposes positive obligations on states to protect life from environmental hazards, including industrial pollution and natural disasters.
- **Article 8 (Right to Respect for Private and Family Life):** Protects individuals from severe environmental pollution that affects their home, private life, and physical well-being, even without proving a serious health risk.

#### Landmark ECHR Cases

**Lopez Ostra v. Spain (1994):** The ECtHR held that severe environmental pollution from a waste-treatment plant near the applicant's home violated Article 8 ECHR, establishing the foundational principle that environmental degradation can constitute an interference with private and family life. The Court awarded damages and held that the state had failed to strike a fair balance between economic interests and the applicant's right to respect for her home.

**Oneryildiz v. Turkey (2004):** The Grand Chamber ruled that Turkey violated Article 2 (right to life) after a methane explosion at a municipal rubbish tip killed 39 people in a nearby slum. The Court held that states have a positive obligation to take preventive operational measures to protect individuals whose lives are at risk from dangerous activities, including the obligation to establish adequate regulatory frameworks.

**Urgenda Foundation v. State of the Netherlands (2019):** Although decided by the Dutch Supreme Court rather than the ECtHR, the Urgenda judgment was the first court ruling in Europe holding a government liable for the consequences of climate change. The Court ordered the Netherlands to reduce greenhouse gas emissions by at least 25% by 2020 compared to 1990 levels, relying significantly on Articles 2 and 8 ECHR. The decision has been cited in climate litigation worldwide.

**Verein KlimaSeniorinnen Schweiz and Others v. Switzerland (2024):** On 9 April 2024, the ECtHR Grand Chamber delivered its first climate change judgment, finding that Switzerland's failure to adopt adequate measures to combat climate change violated Article 8 ECHR. The Court held that Article 8 encompasses the right of individuals to effective protection by the state against the serious adverse effects of climate change on their lives, health, well-being, and quality of life. This landmark ruling established that states have a duty to adopt and implement regulations and measures capable of mitigating the existing and potentially irreversible future effects of climate change.

**Duarte Agostinho and Others v. Portugal and 32 Others (2024):** Decided on the same day as KlimaSeniorinnen, the Court declared this youth-led climate case inadmissible for failure to exhaust domestic remedies, while affirming the principles established in the Swiss case.

## Comparative Analysis

### Definitional Approaches

The four jurisdictions take markedly different approaches to defining environmental crimes. South Korea relies on a **fragmented multi-statute model** with separate acts for air, water, waste, and soil pollution, each containing its own criminal provisions. The United States follows a similar **statute-specific approach** but with broader federal jurisdictional reach through the commerce clause. Germany employs a **consolidated criminal code model**, housing most environmental offenses within a single chapter of the StGB, supplemented by provisions in specialized environmental statutes. The EU Directive 2024/1203 establishes **minimum harmonization standards** that Member States must meet or exceed in their domestic legislation.

### Mens Rea Requirements

A critical distinction lies in the mental state required for criminal liability. The United States uniquely grades offenses by culpability, with separate penalties for negligent violations (misdemeanors) and knowing violations (felonies), plus enhanced "knowing endangerment" provisions. Germany generally requires at least negligence (Fahrlassigkeit) for environmental offenses, with intentional (vorsatzlich) conduct attracting heavier penalties. South Korea's strict liability standard under the Framework Act on Environmental Policy is the most prosecution-friendly, eliminating the need to prove fault for compensation claims, though criminal penalties still generally require proof of intent or negligence. The EU Directive requires at least serious negligence for most offenses and intent for qualified offenses.

### Corporate Accountability

The treatment of corporate criminal liability represents perhaps the most significant divergence among these systems. The United States has long recognized full corporate criminal liability, as demonstrated by the multi-billion dollar penalties imposed on BP and Volkswagen. South Korea's dual punishment system (양벌규정) allows simultaneous prosecution of companies and individuals. Germany's traditional rejection of corporate criminal liability stands as an outlier, though this is changing under pressure from EU harmonization requirements. The EU Directive 2024/1203 explicitly requires Member States to ensure that legal persons can be held liable and faces fines of up to 5% of worldwide turnover.

### Enforcement Priorities and Challenges

All four jurisdictions share common enforcement challenges: proving causation between pollution and harm, prosecuting diffuse or cumulative environmental damage, and balancing economic development against environmental protection. South Korea's enforcement has been galvanized by catastrophic incidents like the humidifier disinfectant scandal. The United States maintains the most well-resourced enforcement apparatus through the EPA's Criminal Investigation Division, which employs approximately 140 special agents with full law enforcement authority. Germany's enforcement is complicated by federalism, as environmental regulation is shared between federal and Land (state) governments. The EU framework faces the inherent challenge of ensuring consistent transposition and enforcement across 27 Member States with widely varying legal traditions and institutional capacities.

### Transnational Dimensions

Environmental crimes are increasingly transnational in character. Illegal waste shipments, wildlife trafficking, illegal logging, and ship-source pollution routinely cross borders. The EU Directive 2024/1203 addresses this through provisions on jurisdictional cooperation and mutual legal assistance. INTERPOL's Environmental Security Programme coordinates global enforcement, and in 2025, Germany, INTERPOL, and WWF announced a joint initiative to combat environmental crime. The United States enforces transboundary environmental crimes through the Lacey Act (illegal wildlife and timber trade) and bilateral agreements with neighboring countries.
