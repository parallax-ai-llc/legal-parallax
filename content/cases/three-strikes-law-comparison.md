## Summary

Three strikes laws -- sentencing statutes that mandate significantly enhanced penalties, typically life imprisonment, for offenders convicted of a third serious or violent felony -- emerged primarily as an American criminal justice phenomenon in the 1990s, embodying a "tough on crime" philosophy that prioritized incapacitation of repeat offenders over rehabilitation, proportionality, or individualized sentencing. South Korea does not employ a formal "three strikes" framework but addresses repeat offending through the aggravated sentencing provisions of Articles 35 (누범 -- repeat offender) and 36 of the Criminal Act (형법), which provide that a person who commits a crime within three years of completing a sentence of imprisonment shall have the maximum sentence increased by up to double the prescribed maximum, effectively functioning as a recidivist enhancement rather than a mandatory life sentence; specialized recidivist provisions in the Act on Special Cases Concerning the Punishment of Specific Crimes (특정범죄 가중처벌 등에 관한 법률) impose further enhanced penalties for repeat offenses in areas including robbery, theft, and sexual assault. The United States is the primary jurisdiction associated with three strikes laws, with California's Proposition 184 (1994) and Washington State's Initiative 593 (1993) serving as the archetypal models; at their peak, 28 states and the federal government had adopted some form of habitual offender or three strikes statute, though significant reforms have followed -- most notably California's Proposition 36 (2012), which restricted the third strike to serious or violent felonies and led to the resentencing of approximately 3,000 prisoners. Germany rejects the three strikes approach entirely, relying instead on Sicherungsverwahrung (preventive detention) under Sections 66-66c StGB as its mechanism for addressing dangerous repeat offenders, a measure that detains offenders after completion of their sentence in separate facilities with therapeutic programs, subject to strict proportionality review by the courts and the Federal Constitutional Court, and substantially reformed following the ECHR's landmark ruling in M. v. Germany (2009) finding that retroactive extension of preventive detention violated Article 5 and Article 7 of the Convention. The ECHR has not directly addressed three strikes laws but has established through cases like Vinter v. United Kingdom (2013) and Harkins and Edwards v. United Kingdom (2012) that grossly disproportionate sentences may violate Article 3, while mandatory life sentences must include a review mechanism.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea does not have a three strikes law in the American sense but addresses repeat offending through general recidivist enhancement provisions and specialized statutes targeting habitual offenders.

### Article 35 -- Repeat Offenders (누범)

Article 35 of the Criminal Act provides: "A person who commits a crime punishable by imprisonment within three years after the completion of a sentence of imprisonment shall be punished within double the maximum of the punishment prescribed for the offense." This provision:

- Applies to any offense punishable by imprisonment (not limited to serious or violent felonies)
- Requires that the new offense occur within three years of completing the prior sentence
- Doubles only the maximum sentence, not the minimum
- Does not mandate a specific minimum sentence

### Act on Special Cases Concerning the Punishment of Specific Crimes (특정범죄 가중처벌 등에 관한 법률)

This Act provides enhanced penalties for repeat offenses in specific categories:

| Offense Category | Enhancement |
|-----------------|-------------|
| Repeat robbery (3+ prior convictions) | 7 years to life imprisonment |
| Repeat theft (3+ prior convictions, total value exceeding specified threshold) | Enhanced sentencing range |
| Repeat sexual assault | Enhanced minimums under Sexual Crimes Act |
| Repeat drug trafficking | Enhanced under Narcotics Control Act |

### Judicial Mitigation

Critically, Korean courts retain the authority to mitigate sentences below enhanced ranges through Articles 53 and 55 of the Criminal Act. The Supreme Court has consistently held that recidivist enhancements must be assessed in light of the individual circumstances of the case, including the nature of the current offense, the time elapsed since prior convictions, and the offender's rehabilitation efforts.

### Constitutional Court Review

In Decision 2003HeonGa12 (2005), the Constitutional Court upheld the constitutionality of Article 35 but emphasized that the provision must be applied consistently with the principle of proportionality (비례의 원칙) under Article 37(2) of the Constitution, and that courts should exercise mitigation powers where the enhanced sentence would be disproportionate.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The three strikes movement originated in the early 1990s and rapidly spread across the United States, driven by public concern about violent crime and high-profile cases involving repeat offenders.

### Washington State -- Initiative 593 (1993)

Washington became the first state to enact a three strikes law on November 2, 1993. The law mandated life imprisonment without the possibility of parole for individuals convicted of a third "most serious offense," defined as specified violent and sex offenses.

### California -- Proposition 184 (1994)

California's three strikes law, the most far-reaching in the nation, was enacted both by the legislature (Assembly Bill 971) and by voter initiative (Proposition 184) in 1994, following the kidnapping and murder of 12-year-old Polly Klaas by Richard Allen Davis, a repeat violent offender on parole.

**Original Provisions:**
- Second strike: Double the normal sentence for any felony with one prior serious/violent felony
- Third strike: 25 years to life for any felony with two prior serious/violent felonies
- The third strike could be triggered by any felony, not just a serious or violent one

### Constitutional Challenges

**Ewing v. California, 538 U.S. 11 (2003):** The Supreme Court upheld California's three strikes law against an Eighth Amendment challenge. Gary Ewing received 25 years to life for stealing three golf clubs (valued at approximately $1,200) as his third strike. Justice O'Connor's plurality opinion held that the sentence was not grossly disproportionate, applying a "narrow proportionality principle" that grants states broad discretion in sentencing recidivists.

**Lockyer v. Andrade, 538 U.S. 63 (2003):** The Court upheld a 50-years-to-life sentence (two consecutive terms) for shoplifting $150 worth of videotapes as a third strike, finding no clearly established federal law prohibiting the sentence.

### California Proposition 36 (2012) -- Three Strikes Reform Act

Proposition 36, approved by 69% of voters on November 6, 2012, reformed California's three strikes law by:
- Requiring that the third strike be a serious or violent felony (not any felony)
- Providing for resentencing of prisoners serving third-strike sentences for non-violent offenses
- Creating exceptions for certain offenses (e.g., drug offenses involving minors, firearm possession)

As of 2024, approximately 3,200 prisoners had been resentenced under Proposition 36, with a recidivism rate of less than 5% among released individuals -- significantly lower than the general recidivism rate.

### California Proposition 47 (2014) and Proposition 57 (2016)

Further reforms reclassified certain non-violent offenses as misdemeanors (Proposition 47) and expanded parole eligibility (Proposition 57), continuing the trend away from California's most aggressive three-strikes-era sentencing policies.

### Federal Three Strikes (18 U.S.C. Section 3559(c))

The federal three strikes provision, enacted as part of the Violent Crime Control and Law Enforcement Act of 1994, mandates life imprisonment for offenders convicted of a serious violent felony who have two or more prior convictions for serious violent felonies or serious drug offenses.

### Impact and Criticism

At its peak, approximately 45,000 inmates were serving second- or third-strike sentences in California alone. Critics documented severe racial disparities: Black defendants were sent to prison under three strikes at 12 times the rate of white defendants. The cost of incarcerating aging three-strikes prisoners was estimated at $19.2 billion over the first 20 years.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's approach to dangerous repeat offenders is fundamentally different from the American three strikes model, relying on Sicherungsverwahrung (preventive detention) rather than enhanced criminal sentences.

### Sicherungsverwahrung (Preventive Detention) -- Sections 66-66c StGB

Preventive detention is a measure of reform and prevention (Maßregel der Besserung und Sicherung) that may be ordered in addition to a prison sentence. Unlike a three strikes enhancement, Sicherungsverwahrung is imposed after the prison sentence has been fully served and is theoretically unlimited in duration, subject to periodic judicial review.

**Requirements under Section 66(1) StGB:**
- Conviction for an intentional offense carrying at least two years' imprisonment
- Two prior convictions for intentional offenses, each resulting in at least one year imprisonment
- Prior imprisonment for at least two years
- Overall assessment indicates the offender poses a significant danger to the public due to a propensity for serious offenses

### M. v. Germany (ECHR, 2009) and Its Aftermath

**M. v. Germany, Application No. 19359/04, Judgment of December 17, 2009:** The ECHR found that the retroactive extension of preventive detention beyond the previous ten-year maximum violated Article 5(1) (right to liberty) and Article 7(1) (no punishment without law). The Court classified Sicherungsverwahrung as a "penalty" for Convention purposes because, in practice, conditions of detention were indistinguishable from ordinary imprisonment.

This landmark judgment precipitated comprehensive reform:

**Federal Constitutional Court, BVerfG 2 BvR 2365/09 (2011):** The BVerfG declared the existing Sicherungsverwahrung provisions unconstitutional and imposed a transitional regime requiring fundamental reform by May 2013. The Court required that preventive detention be qualitatively different from imprisonment (Abstandsgebot -- distance requirement).

**Reform Act of 2013:** The reformed provisions require:
- Separate facilities or separate wings with distinct conditions
- Intensive therapeutic and rehabilitative programs
- Individual treatment plans
- Annual judicial review with the burden on the state to justify continued detention
- Release if the danger can be adequately addressed through supervision and conditions

### Statistics

As of 2024, approximately 550 individuals are held in preventive detention across Germany, a fraction of the number serving three-strikes sentences in the United States. The number has declined since the 2011 reforms, reflecting stricter application of proportionality principles and expanded alternatives.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Jurisprudence

While the ECHR has not directly reviewed a three strikes law, its jurisprudence on proportionality in sentencing, life imprisonment, and preventive detention is directly relevant:

**Vinter and Others v. United Kingdom [GC], Applications Nos. 66069/09 et al., Judgment of July 9, 2013:** Life sentences without any prospect of release violate Article 3. Review must occur no later than 25 years after sentence imposition. This principle applies to any sentencing scheme, including three-strikes-type provisions, that results in irreducible life imprisonment.

**Harkins and Edwards v. United Kingdom, Application No. 9146/07, Judgment of January 17, 2012:** A mandatory sentence is not per se incompatible with Article 3 unless it is grossly disproportionate. The threshold for finding gross disproportionality is high.

**M. v. Germany, Application No. 19359/04, Judgment of December 17, 2009:** Retroactive extension of detention measures violates Article 7. Preventive detention must be materially different from imprisonment.

**Bergmann v. Germany, Application No. 23279/14, Judgment of January 7, 2016:** The Court found no violation of Article 5 or Article 7 in the reformed German Sicherungsverwahrung system, holding that the post-2013 reforms adequately addressed the concerns identified in M. v. Germany.

### EU Framework

The EU has not adopted legislation specifically addressing recidivist sentencing or three strikes laws. Council Framework Decision 2008/675/JHA on taking account of convictions in the member states in the course of new criminal proceedings requires member states to take into account prior convictions handed down in other member states "to the extent previous national convictions are taken into account," but does not prescribe specific sentencing enhancements.

## Comparative Analysis

### Philosophical Approach

The jurisdictions reflect fundamentally different philosophies regarding repeat offenders. The U.S. three strikes model prioritizes incapacitation and deterrence, treating the criminal record as the primary determinant of sentence severity. Germany's Sicherungsverwahrung model focuses on dangerousness assessed through individualized evaluation, treating the offender's current risk rather than their record as the key factor. South Korea occupies a middle position, enhancing sentences based on recidivism but maintaining judicial discretion.

### Proportionality

Germany and the ECHR impose the strictest proportionality requirements, precluding sentences grossly disproportionate to the current offense. The U.S. Supreme Court's "narrow proportionality principle" (Ewing v. California) provides minimal protection, permitting 25-to-life sentences for non-violent theft offenses as a third strike.

### Flexibility

South Korea's system provides the most flexibility through Articles 53 and 55 mitigation. Germany's system allows judicial discretion in ordering Sicherungsverwahrung and requires annual review with the possibility of release. California's original three strikes law provided the least flexibility, though Proposition 36 restored significant judicial discretion.

### Effectiveness

Evidence on the effectiveness of three strikes laws in reducing crime is mixed. A 2004 study by Kovandzic, Sloan, and Vieraitis found no significant effect on crime rates. Research on California's Proposition 36 resentencing found that released three-strikes prisoners had recidivism rates below 5%, suggesting that many individuals were incarcerated well beyond the point of public safety necessity.
