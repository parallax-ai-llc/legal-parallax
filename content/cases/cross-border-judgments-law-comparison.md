## Summary

Cross-border judgment recognition and enforcement -- the legal process by which a judgment rendered by the court of one state is given effect in the territory of another state -- is a foundational element of the international civil justice system, facilitating cross-border commerce, protecting creditor rights, and ensuring that litigants do not need to re-litigate disputes in multiple jurisdictions. South Korea governs foreign judgment recognition through Articles 217 and 217-2 of the Civil Procedure Act (민사소송법) and Article 26 and 27 of the Civil Execution Act (민사집행법), applying a system that requires reciprocity, proper service, public policy compatibility, and the absence of exclusive Korean jurisdiction, with the Supreme Court (대법원) developing significant jurisprudence on the public policy exception, particularly in the context of punitive damages awards from US courts. The United States applies foreign judgment recognition and enforcement through state law rather than federal law, with most states having adopted either the Uniform Foreign-Country Money Judgments Recognition Act (2005) or the earlier Uniform Foreign Money-Judgments Recognition Act (1962), while the recognition of US judgments abroad is complicated by distinctive features of the American litigation system including punitive damages, class actions, and jury trials. Germany applies the provisions of Section 328 of the Zivilprozessordnung (Code of Civil Procedure, ZPO) for recognition of judgments from non-EU states, requiring reciprocity, proper jurisdiction, due process, and public policy compatibility, while within the EU framework, the Brussels I Recast Regulation (EU) No 1215/2012 has abolished exequatur for judgments from other EU member states, creating a near-seamless enforcement regime. At the European level, the Brussels I Recast Regulation represents the world's most advanced cross-border judgment enforcement system, the Hague Convention on Choice of Court Agreements (2005) and the Hague Judgments Convention (2019) seek to extend similar principles globally, and the ECtHR has addressed the right to enforcement of judgments as an element of the right of access to a court under Article 6 ECHR.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's framework for recognition and enforcement of foreign judgments is set out in Article 217 of the Civil Procedure Act (민사소송법) and Articles 26-27 of the Civil Execution Act (민사집행법). Korea is not party to any bilateral judgment enforcement treaty but has signed the Hague Judgments Convention of 2019.

### Requirements for Recognition (Article 217)

Article 217 of the Civil Procedure Act establishes four cumulative requirements for recognition of foreign judgments:

1. **Jurisdiction:** The foreign court must have had jurisdiction over the case under Korean law or international conventions and treaties (Art. 217(1)(i)).
2. **Service:** The losing defendant must have received proper service of the complaint and summons in sufficient time to defend (Art. 217(1)(ii)). Service by publication alone is generally insufficient.
3. **Public Policy:** The recognition must not be contrary to the good morals or social order (선량한 풍속이나 그 밖의 사회질서) of the Republic of Korea (Art. 217(1)(iii)).
4. **Reciprocity:** There must be a guarantee of reciprocity, meaning that Korean judgments would be recognized under equivalent conditions in the rendering state (Art. 217(1)(iv)).

### Punitive Damages

The treatment of foreign punitive damages awards has been a significant issue. The Supreme Court historically rejected enforcement of US punitive damages awards as contrary to Korean public policy. However, following the enactment of several Korean statutes that themselves provide for punitive or treble damages -- including the Fair Trade Act (공정거래법), the Product Liability Act (제조물책임법) as amended in 2017, and the Labor Standards Act (근로기준법) -- the Korean legal system has become more receptive. Article 217-2 of the Civil Procedure Act, added in 2014, specifically addresses punitive damages, providing that damages exceeding the compensatory amount may be recognized to the extent that such damages are equivalent to damages recoverable under Korean law.

### Notable Cases

The Supreme Court's decision in 대법원 2015다207747 (June 29, 2017) clarified the reciprocity requirement, holding that reciprocity should be assessed by reference to the general conditions for recognition in the foreign jurisdiction, not by requiring identical procedural mechanisms. The Court adopted a flexible approach, looking at whether the foreign jurisdiction's requirements are not substantially more burdensome than Korea's.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has no federal statute governing the recognition and enforcement of foreign judgments. Instead, recognition is governed by state law. The National Conference of Commissioners on Uniform State Laws promulgated the Uniform Foreign Money-Judgments Recognition Act (UFMJRA) in 1962 and the revised Uniform Foreign-Country Money Judgments Recognition Act (UFCMJRA) in 2005. As of 2025, most states have adopted one of these uniform acts or have developed common law rules governing recognition.

### Recognition Requirements (UFCMJRA)

The UFCMJRA provides for recognition of final, conclusive, and enforceable foreign-country money judgments, subject to mandatory and discretionary grounds for non-recognition.

**Mandatory Grounds for Non-Recognition:** The judgment was rendered under a system that does not provide impartial tribunals or procedures compatible with due process; the foreign court lacked personal jurisdiction over the defendant; the foreign court lacked subject matter jurisdiction.

**Discretionary Grounds for Non-Recognition:** The defendant did not receive adequate notice; the judgment was obtained by fraud; the underlying cause of action is repugnant to the public policy of the recognizing state; the judgment conflicts with another final and conclusive judgment; the proceeding was contrary to an agreement between the parties; the foreign court was an inconvenient forum; the judgment was rendered in circumstances raising substantial doubt about the integrity of the rendering court; and the specific proceeding was not compatible with due process.

### Reciprocity

Unlike South Korea and Germany, the UFCMJRA does not require reciprocity as a condition for recognition. A few states that have not adopted the uniform act do apply reciprocity requirements (notably, the District of Columbia).

### Hilton v. Guyot (1895)

The Supreme Court's decision in Hilton v. Guyot, 159 U.S. 113 (1895), established the common law framework for foreign judgment recognition, including a reciprocity requirement. However, the UFMJRA and UFCMJRA have largely superseded Hilton in states that have adopted them, eliminating the reciprocity requirement.

### US Judgments Abroad

US judgments frequently face challenges to recognition abroad due to distinctive features of the US litigation system: punitive damages are often reduced or rejected; treble damages may be characterized as penal; class action judgments raise procedural concerns; and jury-determined damages may be viewed as excessive. The Supreme Court's decision in Daimler AG v. Bauman, 571 U.S. 117 (2014), narrowing general personal jurisdiction, has alleviated some concerns about the reach of US courts.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany operates under a dual framework: Section 328 of the Zivilprozessordnung (ZPO) governs recognition of judgments from non-EU states, while the Brussels I Recast Regulation (EU) No 1215/2012 governs recognition and enforcement of judgments from EU member states (replacing the earlier Brussels Convention and Brussels I Regulation).

### ZPO Section 328 Requirements (Non-EU Judgments)

Section 328(1) ZPO establishes five requirements for recognition of non-EU foreign judgments:

1. **Jurisdiction:** The foreign court must have had jurisdiction under German conflict-of-laws principles (§ 328(1)(1)).
2. **Service:** The defendant must have been properly served and given an opportunity to defend (§ 328(1)(2)).
3. **Consistency:** The judgment must not conflict with a prior German judgment or an earlier-recognized foreign judgment (§ 328(1)(3)).
4. **Public Policy:** Recognition must not be manifestly incompatible with essential principles of German law (§ 328(1)(4), ordre public).
5. **Reciprocity:** Reciprocity must be guaranteed (§ 328(1)(5)).

### Punitive Damages

German courts have consistently held that US punitive damages awards are contrary to German public policy (ordre public) to the extent that they exceed compensatory damages. The Bundesgerichtshof (BGH) established this principle in its decision of June 4, 1992 (IX ZR 149/91), holding that the punitive component of a US damages award cannot be recognized under ZPO Section 328(1)(4). However, the BGH has recognized the compensatory portion of such awards. More recent decisions have nuanced this position, recognizing that some component of damages that serves a deterrent function may be compatible with German law, particularly as German law itself has incorporated punitive elements in certain areas.

### Brussels I Recast (EU Judgments)

Within the EU, Regulation (EU) No 1215/2012 (Brussels I Recast) has fundamentally transformed judgment recognition and enforcement. Effective January 10, 2015, the Regulation abolished the exequatur procedure for judgments from other EU member states. A judgment rendered in one member state that is enforceable in that state shall be enforceable in other member states without any declaration of enforceability being required (Article 39). The grounds for refusal of recognition are limited and may not include a review of the merits (Articles 45-46).

### Key Principles

| Framework | Application |
|-----------|-------------|
| ZPO § 328 (non-EU) | Five requirements; reciprocity required; punitive damages restricted |
| Brussels I Recast (EU) | Automatic enforcement; no exequatur; limited refusal grounds |
| Lugano Convention | Switzerland, Norway, Iceland -- similar to Brussels framework |
| Bilateral treaties | Germany has bilateral enforcement treaties with some non-EU states |

## ECHR & EU

### Brussels I Recast Regulation

Regulation (EU) No 1215/2012 of the European Parliament and of the Council of December 12, 2012, on jurisdiction and the recognition and enforcement of judgments in civil and commercial matters represents the most advanced cross-border judgment enforcement system in the world.

**Abolition of Exequatur (Article 39):** The most significant innovation of the Recast was the abolition of the intermediate exequatur procedure. Under the previous Brussels I Regulation (44/2001), a party seeking enforcement had to obtain a declaration of enforceability in the member state of enforcement. The Recast eliminated this requirement: a judgment enforceable in the member state of origin is directly enforceable in all other member states.

**Grounds for Refusal (Article 45):** Recognition may be refused only if: it would be manifestly contrary to public policy; the judgment was given in default and the defendant was not served in time to arrange a defense; the judgment is irreconcilable with a judgment given between the same parties in the member state addressed; or the judgment conflicts with the jurisdiction rules relating to insurance, consumer, or employee matters.

### Hague Convention on Choice of Court Agreements (2005)

The Hague Convention of June 30, 2005, provides for the recognition and enforcement of judgments resulting from exclusive choice of court agreements. The Convention entered into force on October 1, 2015. The EU, Mexico, Singapore, and Montenegro are parties. The United States signed in 2009 but has not ratified. South Korea and other states are considering ratification.

### Hague Judgments Convention (2019)

The Convention of July 2, 2019, on the Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Judgments in Civil or Commercial Matters represents the most ambitious attempt to create a global framework for judgment circulation. The Convention provides for recognition and enforcement of judgments based on specified jurisdictional filters (including defendant's habitual residence, contractual obligation performance place, and tort occurrence place). The EU ratified in September 2022, effective September 2023. Ukraine has also ratified. The United States and other states are evaluating ratification.

### ECtHR Jurisprudence

The ECtHR has addressed cross-border judgment enforcement under Article 6(1) ECHR.

**Hornsby v. Greece (Application No. 18357/91, Judgment of March 19, 1997):** The Court held that the right of access to a court under Article 6(1) includes the right to enforcement of a judgment. A state's failure to enforce a final domestic judgment constitutes a violation of Article 6.

**Pellegrini v. Italy (Application No. 30882/96, Judgment of July 20, 2001):** The Court held that Italian courts were required to verify that the proceedings before the ecclesiastical tribunal (whose annulment decision was being recognized) had afforded the applicant a fair hearing within the meaning of Article 6. This established that Article 6 requires a minimum procedural review when recognizing foreign or international judgments.

**Avotins v. Latvia (Application No. 17502/07, Grand Chamber Judgment of May 23, 2016):** The Grand Chamber addressed the relationship between the Brussels I Regulation and Article 6 ECHR. The Court applied the Bosphorus presumption (Bosphorus v. Ireland, 2005), holding that EU law (including the Brussels I Regulation) is presumed to provide equivalent protection to the ECHR, and that this presumption can be rebutted only where the protection of Convention rights was manifestly deficient.

## Comparative Analysis

### Reciprocity

The most significant structural difference among the jurisdictions concerns reciprocity. South Korea and Germany require it; most US states do not. Reciprocity can create circular deadlocks where neither state recognizes the other's judgments. The Hague Judgments Convention seeks to break this deadlock by providing a treaty-based framework.

### Punitive Damages

All three jurisdictions have developed nuanced approaches to punitive damages. South Korea has moved from rejection to partial recognition (Art. 217-2). Germany rejects the punitive component but recognizes compensatory portions. US courts generally recognize foreign judgments that lack punitive damages without difficulty.

### EU Integration

The Brussels I Recast Regulation has effectively eliminated borders for civil judgment enforcement within the EU, creating a single judicial area. This level of integration has no equivalent in East Asia or across the Pacific, though the Hague Conventions aspire to create a more unified global framework.

### Due Process Standards

All jurisdictions require that the foreign proceeding have met minimum due process standards, but the benchmark varies. US courts assess compatibility with "due process" as understood in American constitutional law. German courts apply German procedural principles. South Korean courts assess compatibility with Korean public policy. The ECtHR requires Article 6 compliance as a minimum standard.
