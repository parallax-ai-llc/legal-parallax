## Summary

Contribution and indemnity -- the legal mechanisms by which liability is allocated among multiple parties responsible for the same harm, either through proportionate sharing (contribution) or complete shifting of loss from one party to another (indemnity) -- are foundational doctrines in tort, contract, and insurance law that determine the ultimate incidence of liability when more than one party bears responsibility for a loss; contribution allows a tortfeasor who has paid more than their proportionate share to recover the excess from co-tortfeasors, while indemnity permits a party who is only derivatively or secondarily liable to shift the entire loss to the party primarily responsible. South Korea addresses contribution among joint tortfeasors under Civil Act (민법) Article 760 (joint tort liability, 공동불법행위) and allows contribution claims under the principle of unjust enrichment (Article 741) and the rules governing joint obligations (Articles 413-427), with the Supreme Court of Korea (대법원) developing a proportionate-fault contribution regime; contractual indemnity is governed by the general freedom of contract principles, subject to limitations under the Regulation of Standardized Contracts Act (약관의 규제에 관한 법률). The United States has moved from the historical common-law rule of no contribution among joint tortfeasors (Merryweather v. Nixan, 1799) to widespread statutory and judicial recognition of contribution, with the Uniform Contribution Among Tortfeasors Act (UCATA, 1939, revised 1955) adopted in modified form by many states, and the Restatement (Third) of Torts: Apportionment of Liability (2000) providing a comprehensive modern framework; contractual indemnity is broadly enforceable subject to anti-indemnity statutes in construction and other contexts. Germany provides a comprehensive contribution framework through the BGB (Buergerliches Gesetzbuch) Section 426 on the internal relationship among joint debtors (Gesamtschuldnerausgleich), supplemented by Section 254 (contributory fault) applied analogously to allocate shares among co-tortfeasors under Section 840, with the BGH (Bundesgerichtshof) developing detailed principles for proportionate liability allocation. The EU and ECHR framework addresses contribution and indemnity primarily through the Rome II Regulation (Regulation (EC) No 864/2007) Article 20 on multiple liability, the Brussels I Recast Regulation for jurisdiction over contribution claims, and the Product Liability Directive provisions on recourse among supply chain participants.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's approach to contribution and indemnity is grounded in the Civil Act's provisions on joint tort liability and joint obligations, with judicial development filling gaps in the statutory framework.

**Civil Act Article 760 -- Joint Tort Liability (공동불법행위):** Article 760(1) provides: "Where several persons have jointly committed a tortious act, they shall be jointly and severally liable for the resulting damage." Article 760(2) extends joint liability to instigators and accessories. Article 760(3) establishes a presumption of joint liability where one of several concurrent tortfeasors cannot be identified as the actual cause.

**Contribution Under Article 760:** Although Article 760 does not expressly provide for contribution among joint tortfeasors, the Supreme Court has firmly established the right of contribution based on the internal relationship among joint debtors. The contribution share of each tortfeasor is determined by their comparative degree of fault (과실비율), which the court assesses considering the nature of each party's conduct, the degree of negligence, and the causal contribution to the harm.

**Supreme Court Decision 97Da52432 (April 10, 1998):** Established that contribution among joint tortfeasors under Article 760 is determined by comparative fault, not equal shares. The court held that the internal allocation of liability should reflect the relative culpability and causal contribution of each tortfeasor, and that a tortfeasor who has paid more than their proportionate share may seek contribution from co-tortfeasors.

**Civil Act Articles 413-427 -- Joint Obligations (연대채무):** These provisions govern the internal relationship among joint and several debtors (연대채무자). Article 425 provides that where one joint debtor satisfies more than their share, they have a right of recourse against the other joint debtors for the excess. Article 427 addresses the internal allocation of shares.

**Contractual Indemnity:** South Korean law recognizes contractual indemnity (면책약정) under the general freedom of contract principle. However, indemnity clauses in standardized contracts (약관) are subject to review under the Regulation of Standardized Contracts Act (약관의 규제에 관한 법률). Article 6 provides that any clause that is unfairly disadvantageous to the customer in violation of the principle of good faith is void. Article 7 further limits clauses that unreasonably exclude or restrict the business operator's liability.

### Product Liability Contribution

Under the Product Liability Act (제조물 책임법) Article 5, where multiple parties in the product supply chain (manufacturer, component supplier, importer) are liable for a defective product, they bear joint and several liability to the injured party. The internal allocation among supply chain participants is determined by their respective contributions to the defect and the contractual arrangements among them.

### Construction Indemnity

The Construction Industry Framework Act (건설산업기본법) and the Occupational Safety and Health Act (산업안전보건법) impose specific liability obligations on principal contractors and subcontractors. Indemnity and contribution disputes in the construction context frequently involve issues of the principal contractor's duty to supervise safety and the subcontractor's direct liability for workplace injuries.

**Supreme Court Decision 2008Da57852 (September 24, 2009):** Held that where both a principal contractor and subcontractor are liable for a construction worker's injury, contribution is allocated based on their respective degrees of fault in failing to maintain safe working conditions, with the principal contractor's supervisory obligation and the subcontractor's direct operational control both being relevant factors.

## United States

### Legal Framework

U.S. contribution and indemnity law has undergone dramatic transformation from the common-law rule barring contribution to a modern regime of comparative allocation, though significant interstate variation persists.

**Historical Background -- Merryweather v. Nixan (1799):** English common law, followed initially by American courts, denied contribution among joint tortfeasors on the theory that courts should not aid wrongdoers in distributing the consequences of their wrongful acts. This harsh rule was gradually eroded by statute and judicial decision throughout the twentieth century.

**Uniform Contribution Among Tortfeasors Act (UCATA):** Originally promulgated in 1939 and revised in 1955, UCATA has been adopted in modified form by many states. The 1955 version provides for contribution on a pro rata basis (equal shares) rather than proportionate fault, though many adopting states have modified the allocation method.

**Restatement (Third) of Torts: Apportionment of Liability (2000):** The Restatement provides the most comprehensive modern framework:

- **Section 23:** Contribution is available to a tortfeasor who has paid more than their comparative responsibility share.
- **Section 22:** Contribution is based on comparative responsibility (proportionate fault), not pro rata shares.
- **Section 17:** In a system of joint and several liability with contribution, each tortfeasor is ultimately responsible for their comparative share, though each is initially liable for the full judgment.
- **Section 16:** Distinguishes jurisdictions that retain joint and several liability from those that have adopted several-only liability.

**Contractual Indemnity:** Contractual indemnity provisions are broadly enforceable, subject to:

- **Anti-Indemnity Statutes:** Most states have enacted anti-indemnity statutes in the construction context, prohibiting clauses that require a party to indemnify another for the indemnitee's own negligence. The scope varies: "broad form" anti-indemnity statutes void clauses indemnifying against the indemnitee's sole negligence; "intermediate form" statutes void clauses indemnifying against the indemnitee's partial negligence.
- **Insurance Requirements:** Indemnity obligations are frequently supported by insurance procurement requirements, with hold-harmless agreements and additional insured endorsements creating a practical indemnification mechanism.

**Implied Indemnity:** Courts recognize implied indemnity where the relationship between the parties is such that one is only derivatively or vicariously liable while the other bears primary responsibility. The distinction between "active" and "passive" negligence historically determined implied indemnity rights, though many jurisdictions have replaced this framework with comparative contribution.

### Key Cases

**McDermott, Inc. v. AmClyde, 511 U.S. 202 (1994):** The Supreme Court held that in admiralty cases with settling and non-settling tortfeasors, a proportionate-share approach to settlement credits best serves the interests of fairness, and non-settling defendants are liable only for their proportionate share of the judgment.

**Knabe v. National Supply Division, 592 F.2d 841 (5th Cir. 1979):** Applied comparative contribution principles in product liability, holding that contribution shares should be based on relative fault rather than equal division.

**Weyerhaeuser Co. v. Commercial Union Insurance Co., 142 Wash. 2d 654 (2000):** Illustrative of the modern approach to contractual indemnity interpretation, requiring clear and unequivocal language to shift liability for the indemnitee's own negligence.

### Federal Statutory Contribution

Several federal statutes create specific contribution regimes:

- **CERCLA Section 113(f)(1) (42 U.S.C. Section 9613(f)(1)):** Provides contribution rights among parties liable for environmental contamination cleanup costs, with allocation based on equitable factors.
- **Securities Exchange Act Section 11(f) (15 U.S.C. Section 77k(f)):** Provides for contribution among persons liable under Section 11 for material misstatements in securities registration statements.
- **Private Securities Litigation Reform Act (1995):** Replaced joint and several liability with proportionate liability for certain securities fraud claims, fundamentally altering the contribution calculus.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's contribution and indemnity framework is systematic and comprehensive, anchored in the BGB's provisions on joint and several liability (Gesamtschuld) and internal allocation (Gesamtschuldnerausgleich).

**BGB Section 840 -- Liability of Multiple Tortfeasors (Haftung mehrerer):** "If several persons are jointly liable for damage arising from a tort, they shall be liable as joint debtors (Gesamtschuldner)." This provision establishes joint and several liability for co-tortfeasors, making the internal contribution provisions of Section 426 applicable.

**BGB Section 426 -- Internal Relationship Among Joint Debtors (Gesamtschuldnerausgleich):** This is the cornerstone of German contribution law.

- **Section 426(1):** "The joint debtors are obligated in equal shares in their relationship to each other, unless otherwise determined." The "unless otherwise determined" clause is the gateway to proportionate fault allocation: courts determine each tortfeasor's internal share based on their relative degree of fault and causation. Despite the statutory default of equal shares, proportionate allocation based on fault is the norm in tort cases.
- **Section 426(2):** "To the extent that a joint debtor satisfies the creditor, the creditor's claim passes to the satisfying debtor against the other joint debtors." This provides for statutory subrogation (cessio legis) in addition to the independent contribution claim under paragraph 1.

**The Dual Basis of Recourse:** German law provides a paying joint debtor with two distinct claims: (1) an independent contribution claim under Section 426(1), and (2) a subrogated claim under Section 426(2) that carries all accessory rights (security interests, guarantees) of the original creditor. The BGH has held that these claims exist independently and may be exercised in parallel.

**BGB Section 254 -- Contributory Fault (Mitverschulden):** Applied analogously to allocate shares among co-tortfeasors in the Section 426 internal relationship. The degree of fault (Verschuldensgrad), the extent of causal contribution (Verursachungsbeitrag), and the sphere of risk assumed by each party (Risikosphare) are the principal allocation criteria.

### BGH Case Law on Allocation

**BGH, Judgment of July 3, 2012 (VI ZR 108/11):** Established that in medical malpractice cases involving multiple treating physicians, internal contribution under Section 426 is allocated based on each physician's degree of deviation from the standard of care and their respective causal contributions to the harm.

**BGH, Judgment of December 18, 2007 (VI ZR 235/06):** Held that in traffic accident cases where both drivers bear fault, the internal allocation under Section 426 considers not only the degree of negligence but also the operational risk (Betriebsgefahr) of each vehicle under StVG Section 17.

**BGH, Judgment of January 11, 2005 (VI ZR 34/04):** Clarified that the Section 426(1) contribution claim arises at the time the joint obligation is established (not at the time of payment), is subject to a three-year limitation period under Section 195, and begins to run when the contributing debtor becomes aware of the facts giving rise to the claim.

### Contractual Indemnity (Freistellungsvereinbarung)

German law recognizes contractual indemnity agreements (Freistellungs- or Haftungsfreistellungsvereinbarungen) under the freedom of contract principle (Vertragsfreiheit). However:

- **AGB-Kontrolle (Standard Terms Review):** Indemnity clauses in standard terms (Allgemeine Geschaeftsbedingungen) are subject to the fairness review under BGB Sections 305-310. Section 307 provides that provisions in standard terms that unreasonably disadvantage the other party are void. Indemnity clauses that shift liability for the indemnitee's own intentional misconduct or gross negligence are typically void under Section 309 No. 7.
- **Construction Indemnity:** The VOB/B (Vergabe- und Vertragsordnung fuer Bauleistungen, Part B), which governs construction contracts in Germany, contains provisions on liability allocation between principal contractors and subcontractors, with indemnity arrangements subject to AGB review.

### Product Liability Recourse

Under ProdHaftG Section 5 (implementing the Product Liability Directive), where multiple supply chain participants are liable for a defective product, they bear joint and several liability to the injured party. Internal recourse among manufacturers, component suppliers, and importers is governed by Section 426 BGB, with allocation based on each party's contribution to the defect and their contractual arrangements.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Private International Law

**Rome II Regulation Article 20 -- Multiple Liability:** "If a creditor has a claim against several debtors who are liable for the same claim, and one of those debtors has already satisfied the claim in whole or in part, the question of that debtor's right to demand compensation from the other debtors shall be governed by the law applicable to that debtor's non-contractual obligation towards the creditor." This provision determines the applicable law for contribution claims in cross-border torts, linking the contribution right to the law governing the tortfeasor's liability to the victim.

**Brussels I Recast Regulation Article 8(2) -- Third-Party Proceedings:** "A person domiciled in a Member State may also be sued: as a third party in an action on a warranty or guarantee or in any other third-party proceedings, in the court seised of the original proceedings, unless these were instituted solely with the object of removing him from the jurisdiction of the court which would be otherwise competent." This provision facilitates cross-border contribution claims by allowing a defendant to implead a co-tortfeasor from another member state in the same court.

**CJEU: Tibor-Trans Kft. v. DAF Trucks NV (Case C-451/18, 2019):** The CJEU held that jurisdiction over damages claims in cartel cases may be established at the place where the damage occurred, a ruling with implications for contribution claims among cartel participants.

### Product Liability Directive

**Revised Product Liability Directive (EU) 2024/2853 Article 15 -- Right of Recourse:** Preserves the right of recourse among supply chain participants: where a person liable under the Directive has compensated the injured party, that person may exercise contribution or recourse rights against other liable parties in the supply chain in accordance with applicable national law. The Directive does not harmonize the internal allocation method, leaving this to member states.

### ECHR Considerations

**Article 6 -- Right to a Fair Trial:** The right to a fair trial encompasses the right to effective access to court for contribution and indemnity claims. Procedural barriers that prevent a liable party from seeking contribution from co-responsible parties may raise Article 6 concerns.

**Regent Company v. Ukraine (Application No. 773/03, 2008):** The Court found a violation of Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 where the state failed to enforce a court judgment, a principle applicable to contribution judgments that remain unenforced.

**Osman v. United Kingdom (Application No. 23452/94, 1998):** Established that blanket immunities from liability may violate Article 6 if they deprive a party of the right to have their civil claim determined, a principle relevant to statutory limitations on contribution rights.

## Comparative Analysis

### Allocation Methods

The central divergence concerns the method for allocating contribution shares. Germany applies a flexible standard under BGB Section 426(1) that considers fault, causation, and risk spheres, with a statutory default of equal shares. The United States has shifted from pro rata allocation (UCATA) to comparative fault allocation (Restatement Third), though both methods persist across jurisdictions. South Korea applies comparative fault allocation through judicial development, without a detailed statutory framework. The EU leaves allocation methods to national law.

### Dual Basis of Recourse in Germany

Germany's distinctive dual-basis system under BGB Section 426 -- providing both an independent contribution claim (paragraph 1) and a subrogated claim carrying accessory rights (paragraph 2) -- gives the paying tortfeasor substantially greater protection than the single contribution claim available in the United States and South Korea. The subrogated claim is particularly valuable because it preserves security interests and other collateral.

### Contractual Indemnity Limitations

All jurisdictions impose limitations on contractual indemnity: South Korea through the Standardized Contracts Act, the United States through anti-indemnity statutes (particularly in construction), and Germany through AGB review under BGB Sections 305-310. The U.S. approach is sector-specific (primarily construction), while the German approach applies the general fairness standard across all standard-form contracts.

### Federal Complexity in the United States

The United States faces unique complexity from the interaction of state contribution regimes, federal statutory contribution provisions (CERCLA, securities laws), and the ongoing tort reform movement that has replaced joint and several liability with proportionate liability in many states. The abolition of joint and several liability in several-only states effectively eliminates the need for contribution, as each defendant pays only their proportionate share.

### Procedural Mechanisms

All jurisdictions provide procedural mechanisms for impleading co-tortfeasors: South Korea's third-party notice (소송고지), the U.S. third-party complaint (FRCP Rule 14), Germany's Streitverkuendung (ZPO Section 72), and the EU's Brussels I Recast Article 8(2). Germany's Streitverkuendung is distinctive because it creates a binding effect (Interventionswirkung) on the third party, preventing them from relitigating issues decided in the original proceedings.
