## Summary

Breach of contract -- the failure of a party to perform its obligations under a binding agreement -- triggers a complex remedial framework that varies significantly across legal traditions in its conceptual foundations, available remedies, and procedural mechanisms. South Korea's breach of contract law, governed by the Korean Civil Code (민법) Articles 390 through 399 on non-performance of obligations (채무불이행), distinguishes among three categories of breach: default in performance (이행지체), impossibility of performance (이행불능), and incomplete or defective performance (불완전이행), with specific performance (이행청구) available as a primary remedy and damages calculated under Article 393's foreseeability principle modeled on the German adequate causation theory. The United States follows the common law expectation interest framework articulated in Hadley v. Baxendale (1854) and codified in the Restatement (Second) of Contracts Sections 344-356, supplemented by UCC Article 2 remedies for the sale of goods, under which monetary damages are the preferred remedy and specific performance is granted only where the legal remedy is inadequate -- typically limited to unique goods, real property, and long-term output contracts. Germany provides the most systematically codified framework through the BGB Sections 280-288 (general breach provisions reformed in the 2002 Schuldrechtsmodernisierung), which unifies various forms of breach under the concept of Pflichtverletzung (duty violation) and makes specific performance (Erfüllungsanspruch) the primary remedy, with damages serving a supplementary function. At the European level, the CISG Articles 45-52 and 61-70 establish a unified remedial framework for international sales contracts emphasizing the fundamental breach doctrine (Article 25), the Principles of European Contract Law and Draft Common Frame of Reference provide harmonized remedial models, and the European Court of Justice has addressed enforcement of contractual remedies across borders through the Brussels I Regulation framework.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's law on breach of contract is governed by the Korean Civil Code (민법), Book III on Obligations (채권편). The Code distinguishes three categories of non-performance, each with distinct requirements and consequences.

**Default in Performance (이행지체, Article 387):** Occurs when a debtor fails to perform an obligation that is due and performable. For obligations with a fixed due date, the debtor is in default upon expiration of the due date. For obligations without a fixed date, default occurs upon the creditor's demand for performance (최고).

**Impossibility of Performance (이행불능, Article 390):** Occurs when performance becomes impossible due to a cause attributable to the debtor. Impossibility may be physical, legal, or economic (though economic impossibility is narrowly construed). Where performance is impossible, the creditor may claim damages without requesting performance.

**Incomplete Performance (불완전이행):** Though not explicitly defined in the Civil Code, this category was developed by the Supreme Court of Korea (대법원) to address situations where performance is tendered but does not conform to contractual requirements. The Supreme Court recognized this category in its Decision 96Da14703 (1997), holding that defective performance constitutes a breach giving rise to damages claims.

### Remedies

**Specific Performance (이행청구, Article 389):** Under Korean law, the creditor has a primary right to demand actual performance. The court may order the debtor to perform the obligation, and if the debtor fails to comply, the creditor may seek enforcement through the Civil Execution Act (민사집행법), including indirect compulsion (간접강제) through imposition of periodic penalty payments.

**Damages (손해배상, Articles 390-393):** Where breach is attributable to the debtor's fault (귀책사유), the creditor may claim damages. Article 393 establishes the scope of compensable damages:
- Paragraph 1: Ordinary damages (통상손해) -- damages that would ordinarily arise from the breach.
- Paragraph 2: Special damages (특별손해) -- damages arising from special circumstances, recoverable only if the debtor knew or should have known of those circumstances.

**Termination (해제, Articles 543-548):** The creditor may terminate the contract if the debtor fails to perform after receiving a reasonable period notice (최고, Article 544). For impossibility of performance, termination is available immediately without notice (Article 546). Termination operates retroactively (Article 548), with mutual restitution obligations.

### Liquidated Damages and Penalty Clauses

Korean law recognizes liquidated damages clauses (손해배상의 예정, Article 398). The court has discretion to reduce an unreasonably excessive liquidated damages amount (Article 398(2)). The Supreme Court has exercised this power in numerous cases, typically reducing clauses exceeding 20-30 percent of the contract value. Penalty clauses (위약벌) are distinguished from liquidated damages and are enforceable as punishment for breach, in addition to actual damages.

### Notable Case: Supreme Court Decision 2014Da235189 (2017)

The Supreme Court clarified the relationship between specific performance and damages, holding that where partial performance has been rendered, the creditor may choose between claiming specific performance of the remaining obligation and terminating the contract for fundamental breach, but may not simultaneously claim both unless the partial performance is of no value to the creditor.

## United States

### Legal Framework

American breach of contract law is governed by the common law (synthesized in the Restatement (Second) of Contracts) for most contracts and the Uniform Commercial Code Article 2 for sales of goods.

**Material vs. Minor Breach:** The Restatement Section 241 establishes factors for determining whether a breach is material: the extent of harm, the likelihood of cure, adequacy of compensation, hardship to the breaching party, and the breaching party's good faith. A material breach excuses the non-breaching party's remaining performance obligations and permits termination. A minor breach gives rise to a damages claim but does not excuse counter-performance.

**Anticipatory Repudiation:** Under Restatement Section 253 and UCC Section 2-610, a party who unequivocally repudiates before performance is due commits an anticipatory breach, allowing the non-breaching party to immediately claim damages, suspend performance, or await performance for a commercially reasonable time.

### Remedies

**Expectation Damages:** The primary remedy is monetary damages calculated to put the non-breaching party in the position they would have occupied had the contract been performed (Restatement Section 347). This includes:
- Direct damages (loss in value of the promised performance)
- Incidental damages (costs incurred in dealing with the breach)
- Consequential damages (foreseeable losses resulting from the breach)
- Less costs avoided and losses mitigated

**Hadley v. Baxendale Foreseeability Rule:** The foundational damages limitation, from Hadley v. Baxendale (1854) 9 Ex. 341, limits recoverable damages to those arising naturally from the breach (general damages) or those in the reasonable contemplation of the parties at the time of contracting (consequential damages). This rule is codified in UCC Section 2-715(2) and Restatement Section 351.

**Specific Performance:** Specific performance is an equitable remedy available only when the legal remedy (damages) is inadequate. Traditional categories include real property contracts (each parcel is unique), unique goods (UCC Section 2-716), and output/requirements contracts where market substitution is impractical. The modern trend, reflected in UCC Section 2-716's expanded language, is toward broader availability.

**Mitigation Duty:** The non-breaching party must take reasonable steps to mitigate damages (Restatement Section 350). Failure to mitigate reduces recoverable damages by the amount that could have been avoided through reasonable effort. In employment contracts, the leading case is Parker v. Twentieth Century-Fox Film Corp. (1970), holding that the employee need not accept a substantially different or inferior position to mitigate.

### UCC Article 2 Remedial Framework

| Party | Key Remedies |
|-------|-------------|
| Buyer | Cover (§2-712); market price damages (§2-713); specific performance (§2-716); incidental/consequential damages (§2-715) |
| Seller | Resale (§2-706); market price damages (§2-708); action for price (§2-709); incidental damages (§2-710) |

### Punitive Damages

Punitive damages are generally not available for breach of contract (Restatement Section 355). The exception arises when the breach also constitutes an independent tort, such as insurance bad faith (first recognized in Gruenberg v. Aetna Insurance Co., 1973). Several states recognize a tort of bad faith breach in the insurance context, permitting substantial punitive awards.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's breach of contract law was comprehensively reformed by the Schuldrechtsmodernisierung (Modernization of the Law of Obligations), effective January 1, 2002. The reform replaced the prior fragmented approach with a unified concept of Pflichtverletzung (duty violation) under Section 280 BGB.

**Section 280(1) BGB -- General Damages Claim:** "If the obligor breaches a duty arising from the obligation, the obligee may demand compensation for the damage caused thereby. This does not apply if the obligor is not responsible for the breach of duty." This provision establishes the unified basis for all damages claims arising from breach.

**Types of Duties Violated:**
- Performance duties (Leistungspflichten) -- primary contractual obligations
- Protective duties (Schutzpflichten) -- duties of care arising from the obligatory relationship under Section 241(2)
- Ancillary duties (Nebenpflichten) -- duties to cooperate, inform, and protect

### Remedial Hierarchy

German law establishes a clear remedial hierarchy:

1. **Specific Performance (Erfüllungsanspruch):** The creditor's primary right is to demand actual performance. Unlike in common law, specific performance is the default remedy, not an exceptional equitable remedy. The claim is excluded only where performance is impossible (Section 275(1)), unreasonably burdensome (Section 275(2)), or involves a personal service the debtor cannot be compelled to render (Section 275(3)).

2. **Nachfrist and Supplementary Damages:** Before claiming damages in lieu of performance (Schadensersatz statt der Leistung), the creditor must generally set a reasonable additional period (Nachfrist) for performance under Section 281(1). If the debtor fails to perform within the Nachfrist, the creditor may claim damages in lieu of the entire performance or, for partial non-performance, damages in lieu of the deficient portion.

3. **Termination (Rücktritt, Sections 323-326):** Termination requires either expiration of a Nachfrist (Section 323(1)) or one of the exceptions where Nachfrist is unnecessary (Section 323(2)) -- such as definitive refusal to perform, breach of a fix-date obligation, or special circumstances justifying immediate termination.

### Damages Calculation

German damages law follows the principle of Naturalrestitution (restoration to the state that would have existed had the breach not occurred) under Section 249 BGB:

| Damages Type | Legal Basis | Scope |
|-------------|-------------|-------|
| Damages alongside performance (Schadensersatz neben der Leistung) | §280(1) | Compensates for damage caused by delayed or defective performance while performance continues |
| Damages in lieu of performance (Schadensersatz statt der Leistung) | §§280(1), (3), 281-283 | Replaces the performance obligation; requires Nachfrist |
| Delay damages (Verzugsschaden) | §§280(1), (2), 286 | Compensates for harm caused specifically by late performance |

The Adäquanztheorie (adequate causation theory) limits recoverable damages to those that, from the perspective of an optimal observer at the time of the harmful event, are not entirely improbable consequences of the breach.

### Clausula Rebus Sic Stantibus

Section 313 BGB codifies the doctrine of disturbance of the basis of the transaction (Störung der Geschäftsgrundlage), permitting contract adaptation or termination where circumstances forming the basis of the contract have fundamentally changed. This provision addresses situations between impossibility (Section 275) and ordinary breach, providing a safety valve for unforeseen disruptions -- applied extensively during the COVID-19 pandemic to commercial lease disputes.

## ECHR & EU

### CISG Remedial Framework

The CISG provides a comprehensive and autonomous remedial framework for international sales contracts.

**Fundamental Breach (Article 25):** "A breach of contract committed by one of the parties is fundamental if it results in such detriment to the other party as substantially to deprive him of what he is entitled to expect under the contract, unless the party in breach did not foresee and a reasonable person of the same kind in the same circumstances would not have foreseen such a result." This concept is pivotal because only fundamental breach permits contract avoidance (Articles 49(1)(a), 64(1)(a)).

**Buyer's Remedies (Articles 45-52):** Include requiring performance (Article 46), fixing an additional period for performance (Nachfrist, Article 47), claiming damages (Articles 74-77), avoiding the contract for fundamental breach (Article 49), and reducing the price for non-conforming goods (Article 50).

**Seller's Remedies (Articles 61-70):** Include requiring performance (Article 62), fixing an additional period (Article 63), claiming damages, and avoiding the contract (Article 64).

**Damages (Articles 74-77):** Article 74 establishes the foreseeability rule: damages may not exceed the loss which the party in breach foresaw or ought to have foreseen at the time of the conclusion of the contract. Articles 75 and 76 provide specific calculation methods for cover transactions and market price differentials.

**Article 28 -- Specific Performance Limitation:** A court is not bound to enter a judgment for specific performance unless the court would do so under its own law in respect of similar contracts not governed by the CISG. This provision accommodates the common law reluctance to grant specific performance.

### PECL and DCFR

The PECL Chapter 9 and DCFR Book III provide harmonized remedial frameworks that synthesize civil law and common law approaches:
- Right to performance is established as a primary remedy (PECL Article 9:102; DCFR III.-3:302)
- Damages for non-performance follow a foreseeability standard (PECL Article 9:503)
- Termination requires fundamental non-performance or expiration of additional time period (PECL Article 9:301)

### Brussels I Regulation -- Enforcement

The Brussels I Regulation (Recast) No 1215/2012 governs the recognition and enforcement of contractual judgments across EU member states. Article 36 provides that a judgment given in a member state shall be recognized in the other member states without any special procedure being required. Article 39 provides for enforcement without a declaration of enforceability, streamlining cross-border enforcement of contractual remedies.

## Comparative Analysis

### Specific Performance vs. Damages Primacy

The most fundamental divergence concerns the hierarchy of remedies. Civil law systems (South Korea, Germany) and the CISG treat specific performance as the primary remedy, reflecting the civil law principle that a promise creates a right to performance, not merely a right to the monetary equivalent. Common law (United States) treats damages as the primary remedy, with specific performance available only in exceptional circumstances, reflecting the economic efficiency perspective that breach should be permitted where the breaching party can compensate the non-breaching party and still benefit. This distinction has practical consequences: in civil law systems, a buyer can compel delivery of contracted goods; in common law systems, the buyer is typically limited to purchasing substitute goods on the market and recovering the price difference as damages.

### The Nachfrist Mechanism

Germany, the CISG, and (to a lesser extent) South Korea employ the Nachfrist (additional time) mechanism, which requires the aggrieved party to give the breaching party one final opportunity to perform before exercising termination or damages-in-lieu-of-performance rights. American law does not employ this mechanism; material breach alone suffices for termination. The Nachfrist promotes contract preservation by giving the breaching party a "last chance," but may delay the aggrieved party's ability to seek alternative performance.

### Foreseeability Standards

All four jurisdictions limit consequential damages through some form of foreseeability test, but the application differs. The Hadley v. Baxendale rule (United States, CISG) focuses on foreseeability at the time of contracting. The Adäquanztheorie (Germany, South Korea) assesses foreseeability from the perspective of an optimal observer at the time of breach. These different temporal reference points can produce different results in cases where circumstances change between formation and breach.

### Fault Requirement

South Korea and Germany generally require fault (culpability) for damages claims, though the burden of proving absence of fault falls on the debtor. American common law imposes strict liability for breach -- the breaching party is liable regardless of fault or good faith efforts to perform. The CISG adopts a strict liability approach with an exemption for impediments beyond the party's control (Article 79).
