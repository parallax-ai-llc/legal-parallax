## Summary

Intellectual property protection across the Global South presents a complex landscape where TRIPS Agreement compliance intersects with development priorities, public health imperatives, traditional knowledge protection, and industrial policy objectives. China has undergone the most dramatic IP transformation, establishing specialized IP courts in Beijing, Shanghai, and Guangzhou (2014) and the Supreme People's Court IP Tribunal (最高人民法院知识产权法庭, 2019), while simultaneously facing persistent criticism over trade secret theft and forced technology transfer. India's IP regime is defined by its assertive use of TRIPS flexibilities, particularly compulsory licensing for pharmaceuticals -- the landmark Natco Pharma Ltd. v. Bayer Corporation (2012) compulsory license for the cancer drug sorafenib (marketed as Nexavar) remains the most cited example globally -- and the rigorous patentability standards under Section 3(d) of the Patents Act, 1970 (as amended 2005), which was upheld by the Supreme Court in Novartis AG v. Union of India (2013) 6 SCC 1. South Africa's IP regime, currently undergoing transformation through the Copyright Amendment Bill and Performers' Protection Amendment Bill (passed by Parliament in 2019, referred back by the President in 2020 for constitutional review), is deeply entangled with access to medicine debates and the legacy of the Pharmaceutical Manufacturers' Association of South Africa v. President of the Republic of South Africa (Pretoria High Court, 2001) case that effectively forced pharmaceutical companies to permit generic antiretroviral production. Brazil's National Institute of Industrial Property (INPI) administers a patent system notorious for backlog (formerly exceeding 10 years for examination, reduced to approximately 2-3 years through the INPI Action Plan since 2019), while the Philippines' Intellectual Property Office (IPOPHL) balances IP protection with access concerns through the Cheaper Medicines Act (Republic Act No. 9502, 2008) and Universally Accessible Cheaper and Quality Medicines Act (RA 9502).

## China

### Legal Framework

China's IP legal framework has undergone four waves of revision, most recently culminating in comprehensive amendments to all three core IP statutes in 2020-2021:

- **Patent Law** (专利法, fourth amendment effective June 1, 2021): Key changes include Article 42(3) introducing patent term extension for new drug inventions to compensate for regulatory review time (up to 5 years, total effective patent term not exceeding 14 years from approval); Article 76 establishing a patent linkage system for pharmaceutical patents; Article 71 increasing statutory damages to a maximum of RMB 5 million (up from RMB 1 million) and introducing punitive damages of one to five times actual damages for intentional infringement (Article 71(1)); and Article 20 introducing a "domestic filing first" requirement for inventions made in China, with security review before foreign filing.
- **Trademark Law** (商标法, 2019 amendment effective November 1, 2019): Article 4 added a requirement that trademark applications be made in good faith and for the purpose of actual use, targeting bad-faith trademark squatting that had plagued the system. Article 68 strengthened penalties for trademark agencies that knowingly assist bad-faith filings.
- **Copyright Law** (著作权法, third amendment effective June 1, 2021): Introduced statutory damages up to RMB 5 million (Article 54), added punitive damages for intentional infringement, and expanded the definition of protected works to include "other intellectual creations" (Article 3(9)), clarifying protection for emerging creative forms.
- **Anti-Unfair Competition Law** (反不正当竞争法, 2019 amendment): Strengthened trade secret protection by: lowering the burden of proof for plaintiffs (shifting to defendants the burden of proving that they did not misappropriate once the plaintiff establishes a prima facie case, Article 32); broadening the definition of trade secrets; and increasing maximum statutory damages to RMB 5 million with punitive damages up to five times actual damages.

### Specialized IP Courts and the SPC IP Tribunal

China's IP judicial infrastructure is the most developed in the Global South:

- **IP Courts**: The Beijing, Shanghai, and Guangzhou IP Courts, established in November 2014 under an NPC Standing Committee decision, exercise original jurisdiction over patent, new plant variety, integrated circuit layout design, and technical know-how cases and appellate jurisdiction over trademark, copyright, and unfair competition cases from basic people's courts. The Hainan Free Trade Port IP Court was added in 2020.
- **Supreme People's Court IP Tribunal** (最高人民法院知识产权法庭, established January 1, 2019): A specialized tribunal within the SPC that exercises exclusive appellate jurisdiction over all patent and technical IP cases nationwide, creating a unified appellate standard. In its first five years (2019-2023), the Tribunal handled over 15,000 cases, establishing precedent on standard-essential patent (SEP) licensing, FRAND rates, anti-suit injunctions, and trade secret protection.
- **Internet Courts**: The Hangzhou Internet Court (2017), Beijing Internet Court (2018), and Guangzhou Internet Court (2018) handle online IP disputes through fully digital proceedings, including blockchain-based evidence preservation.

### Trade Secret and Technology Transfer Issues

Despite dramatic legislative and institutional progress, China continues to face international criticism regarding IP protection, particularly in trade secret misappropriation and technology transfer. The US Trade Representative's Special 301 Report has maintained China on its Priority Watch List. Key developments include:

- **Phase One Trade Agreement** (January 15, 2020): Chapter 1 on IP included commitments to protect trade secrets (including from government entities and SOEs), strengthen pharmaceutical IP protection (patent linkage and patent term extension), address online piracy and counterfeiting, and ensure enforcement through administrative, civil, and criminal channels.
- **SPC Judicial Interpretations on Trade Secrets**: The Interpretation on Application of Law in Civil Cases of Unfair Competition (2022 revision) clarified standards for identifying trade secrets, proving misappropriation, and calculating damages. The SPC's judgment in Valeo Switches and Detection Systems v. Lu Fulai & Vanch Auto (2019), awarding RMB 16.3 million in damages, signaled increasing judicial willingness to impose significant trade secret remedies.

### Patent Activity

China leads the world in patent applications. CNIPA received over 1.58 million invention patent applications in 2024, with domestic applications accounting for approximately 92%. Concerns about patent quality and the role of government subsidies in driving application volumes led to the State Council's 2021 decision to gradually eliminate per-patent subsidies and shift toward quality-oriented incentives. The number of valid invention patents in China exceeded 5 million as of end-2024, with the patents-per-10,000-population ratio reaching 26.1 for high-value invention patents.

## India

### Legal Framework

India's IP regime is anchored in a set of statutes that incorporate TRIPS flexibilities to a degree unmatched in other major economies:

- **Patents Act, 1970** (as amended by the Patents (Amendment) Act, 2005): India's product patent regime for pharmaceuticals (introduced January 1, 2005 per TRIPS transition obligations) is qualified by Section 3(d), which prohibits patents on "the mere discovery of a new form of a known substance which does not result in the enhancement of the known efficacy of that substance." This provision, unique globally, was designed to prevent "evergreening" of pharmaceutical patents.
- **Section 3(d) Interpretation -- Novartis AG v. Union of India, (2013) 6 SCC 1**: The Supreme Court upheld the rejection of Novartis's patent application for the beta-crystalline form of imatinib mesylate (Glivec/Gleevec), holding that "efficacy" in Section 3(d) means "therapeutic efficacy" for pharmaceutical substances, and that Novartis failed to demonstrate enhanced therapeutic efficacy of the beta-crystalline form over the known free base form. This landmark decision confirmed India's right to set a higher inventive step standard than TRIPS minimally requires and has been cited by patent offices and courts globally.
- **Sections 82-94 (Compulsory Licensing)**: India's compulsory licensing framework permits grant on grounds including: the patented invention is not available to the public at a reasonably affordable price (Section 84(1)(b)); the patented invention is not worked in the territory of India (Section 84(1)(c)); and the reasonable requirements of the public are not satisfied (Section 84(1)(a)). The Controller General may also issue compulsory licenses for export to countries with insufficient manufacturing capacity (Section 92A, implementing the Doha Declaration paragraph 6 mechanism).

### Natco v. Bayer Compulsory License

The Controller of Patents' order granting Natco Pharma Ltd. a compulsory license for sorafenib tosylate (Nexavar, Bayer Corporation's cancer drug patent IN/215758) on March 9, 2012 remains the most significant compulsory license grant in the TRIPS era:

- **Findings**: Bayer's pricing of approximately INR 2.8 lakh (USD 5,500) per month was not "reasonably affordable" in the Indian context; Bayer was supplying the drug to only 2% of eligible patients; and the patent was not being "worked" in India (Bayer imported the finished product rather than manufacturing domestically).
- **Terms**: Natco was permitted to manufacture and sell sorafenib at INR 8,800 per month (approximately USD 175), paying Bayer a 6% royalty on net sales. The IPAB (now abolished) upheld the Controller's order in Bayer Corporation v. Union of India (2014) with minor modifications to the royalty rate (increased to 7%).
- **Global Impact**: The Natco license demonstrated that TRIPS Article 31 flexibilities could be practically implemented by developing countries, inspiring compulsory licensing discussions across the Global South. However, India has not granted a second compulsory license since 2012, suggesting that the threat of compulsory licensing may be more significant than its actual use.

### Traditional Knowledge Digital Library (TKDL)

India's TKDL, established in 2001, is the world's most comprehensive traditional knowledge database, containing over 4.5 lakh formulations from Indian traditional medicine systems (Ayurveda, Yoga, Unani, Siddha, Sowa-Rigpa) translated into patent classification formats in five languages (English, French, German, Spanish, Japanese). The TKDL is accessed by patent offices in the US (USPTO), EU (EPO), UK (UKIPO), Australia (IP Australia), Japan (JPO), and Canada (CIPO) through bilateral agreements, enabling examiners to identify prior art from traditional knowledge and reject biopiracy patent applications. Successful challenges include the revocation of turmeric wound-healing patents (US Patent 5,401,504, revoked 1997) and neem-based fungicide patents (EPO, revoked 2005).

### Copyright and Digital Issues

The Copyright Act, 1957 (as amended 2012) provides for: authors' special rights including moral rights (Section 57); statutory licensing for broadcasting (Section 31D, the controversial "statutory license" for radio broadcasters to play sound recordings at government-determined rates); and fair dealing exceptions (Section 52). The Delhi High Court's landmark decision in The Chancellor, Masters & Scholars of the University of Oxford v. Rameshwari Photocopy Services (2016, upheld 2017) permitted photocopying of portions of copyrighted textbooks for educational purposes under the Section 52(1)(i) fair dealing exception, drawing international attention as an expansive educational use ruling.

## Africa

### South Africa

South Africa's IP regime operates under statutes that largely predate the democratic transition: the Patents Act 57 of 1978, Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, Copyright Act 98 of 1978, and Designs Act 195 of 1993. The Companies and Intellectual Property Commission (CIPC) administers patents, trademarks, and designs. South Africa does not conduct substantive patent examination -- patent applications that meet formal requirements are granted without examination of novelty, inventive step, or industrial applicability, a practice that has drawn criticism for allowing low-quality patents to enter the system.

**Access to Medicine and IP**:

South Africa's role in global access to medicine IP debates is foundational:

- **Pharmaceutical Manufacturers' Association v. President of the Republic of South Africa (2001)**: Thirty-nine pharmaceutical companies challenged the Medicines and Related Substances Control Amendment Act 90 of 1997, which authorized the Health Minister to prescribe conditions for supply of affordable medicines including parallel importation and compulsory licensing. The companies withdrew the case in April 2001 under enormous public pressure, establishing the political viability of TRIPS flexibility use in developing countries.
- **Section 15C, Medicines Act**: Authorizes the government to issue compulsory licenses for medicine production during public health emergencies, though this power has not been formally invoked.
- **Patent Reform**: The Department of Trade, Industry and Competition's 2018 IP Policy Phase I recommended introducing substantive patent examination, excluding certain pharmaceutical forms from patentability (similar to India's Section 3(d)), and strengthening compulsory licensing provisions. Draft Patent Amendment Bills incorporating these recommendations have been under consultation but not yet tabled in Parliament.
- **Copyright Amendment Bill (2019)**: Passed by the National Assembly and National Council of Provinces in 2019, the Bill would have introduced: fair use (replacing fair dealing), mimicking US-style flexible exceptions; compulsory licensing for education, research, and persons with disabilities; and artist royalty and resale right provisions. President Ramaphosa referred the Bill back to Parliament in June 2020 on procedural constitutionality grounds (tag of the Bill as a Section 75 ordinary bill rather than Section 76 bill affecting provincial competences). The revised Bill remains under parliamentary consideration.

### Nigeria

Nigeria's IP framework consists of the Patents and Designs Act 1970 (Cap P2, LFN 2004), Trade Marks Act 1965 (Cap T13, LFN 2004), and Copyright Act 2022 (replacing the Copyright Act 1988). The patent system, like South Africa's, operates without substantive examination. The Trademarks, Patents and Designs Registry is housed within the Ministry of Industry, Trade and Investment.

The Copyright Act 2022 (signed by President Buhari on March 17, 2023) modernized Nigeria's copyright framework:

- **Section 8**: Expanded the categories of protected works to include computer programs, databases, and digital works.
- **Section 23-27**: Introduced technological protection measures (TPMs) and rights management information provisions, consistent with the WIPO Internet Treaties.
- **Section 32**: Established the Nigerian Copyright Commission (NCC) as the regulatory authority with enhanced enforcement powers, including the authority to conduct raids and impose administrative penalties.
- **Section 41-45**: Fair dealing and educational exceptions, including provisions for persons with visual impairment consistent with the Marrakesh Treaty (to which Nigeria is a party).

Nigeria faces significant challenges with IP enforcement, particularly counterfeiting of pharmaceuticals (estimated by WHO at 70% of medicines in some markets being counterfeit or substandard), piracy of musical works and Nollywood films, and trademark infringement in consumer goods. The National Agency for Food and Drug Administration and Control (NAFDAC) exercises concurrent enforcement jurisdiction for pharmaceutical counterfeiting.

## Latin America

### Brazil

Brazil's IP system is administered by the National Institute of Industrial Property (Instituto Nacional da Propriedade Industrial, INPI), established by Law 5,648/1970 and operating under the Industrial Property Law 9,279/1996 and the Copyright Law 9,610/1998.

**Patent Examination Backlog and Resolution**:

INPI's patent examination backlog was one of the most severe globally, with average examination pendency exceeding 10 years and a backlog of over 200,000 unexamined applications by 2019. The "Pipeline" patent provision (Articles 229-C of the Industrial Property Law) had allowed revalidation of foreign patents for inventions not previously patented in Brazil, particularly for pharmaceuticals -- a provision controversial under TRIPS Article 27 and declared unconstitutional by the Supreme Federal Tribunal (STF) in ADI 5.529 (2021), which also struck down the automatic patent term extension under Article 40 (sole paragraph) that extended patents beyond 20 years when examination exceeded certain timeframes.

INPI's Action Plan for Combating the Backlog (Plano de Combate ao Backlog, 2019-present) reduced the examination backlog from over 200,000 to approximately 40,000 applications through hiring additional examiners, implementing expedited examination procedures, and utilizing Patent Prosecution Highway (PPH) agreements with the USPTO, EPO, JPO, KIPO, and CNIPA.

**Compulsory Licensing**:

Brazil has used the threat of compulsory licensing as a negotiating tool with pharmaceutical companies:

- **2001**: President Cardoso issued Decree 3,201/1999 (as amended by Decree 4,830/2003) establishing procedures for compulsory licensing for public health purposes. Brazil notified the WTO of its intent to issue a compulsory license for efavirenz (Merck's HIV/AIDS drug) in 2001, ultimately negotiating significant price reductions.
- **2007**: President Lula issued Decree 6,108/2007 granting a compulsory license for efavirenz on public interest/public health grounds (Article 71 of the Industrial Property Law). This was Brazil's only actual compulsory license grant. The license enabled state laboratory Farmanguinhos/FIOCRUZ and authorized generics manufacturers to produce efavirenz, reducing annual per-patient costs from approximately USD 580 to USD 170.
- **2021**: During the COVID-19 pandemic, Law 14,200/2021 established simplified procedures for compulsory licensing of patents necessary for addressing public health emergencies.

### Mexico

Mexico's Industrial Property Law (Ley de la Propiedad Industrial, 2020, replacing the 1991 law) and the Federal Copyright Law (Ley Federal del Derecho de Autor, 1996) provide the statutory framework. The Mexican Institute of Industrial Property (IMPI) conducts substantive patent examination. Mexico's USMCA/T-MEC obligations (Chapter 20) require TRIPS-plus protections including ten years of regulatory data protection for biologics (Article 20.F.14), patent term adjustment for administrative delays (Article 20.C.9), and enhanced trade secret protection. Mexico's Federal Law for the Protection of Industrial Property (2020 revision) incorporated USMCA obligations while maintaining compulsory licensing provisions under Articles 153-163.

## Southeast Asia

### Indonesia

Indonesia's IP framework comprises the Patent Law 13/2016 (replacing Law 14/2001), the Trademark and Geographical Indication Law 20/2016 (replacing Law 15/2001), and the Copyright Law 28/2014 (replacing Law 19/2002). The Directorate General of Intellectual Property (DJKI) within the Ministry of Law and Human Rights administers the system.

Key features include:

- **Compulsory Licensing**: Articles 81-107 of Patent Law 13/2016 authorize compulsory licensing for defense and security, health needs, and agriculture. Government Regulation 46/2017 provides implementing procedures. Indonesia issued Government Regulation 48/2022 on Compulsory Licensing for the COVID-19 vaccine, authorizing domestic production of vaccines patented by foreign entities.
- **Traditional Knowledge and Genetic Resources**: Indonesia's extraordinary biodiversity (estimated 17% of world's species) drives its advocacy in WIPO negotiations on the proposed International Legal Instrument on IP and Genetic Resources. Law 5/1994 ratifies the Convention on Biological Diversity. The case of Indonesian batik patterns being trademarked abroad (notably by Japanese companies) catalyzed the registration of batik as a UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage (2009) and domestic protective measures.
- **Technology Transfer Requirements**: Government Regulation 20/2005 requires technology transfer by patent holders to Indonesian entities, including training Indonesian nationals -- a provision that has drawn criticism from foreign investors.

### Philippines

The Philippines' IP system is governed by the Intellectual Property Code (Republic Act No. 8293, 1997, as amended by RA 10372 in 2012) and administered by the Intellectual Property Office of the Philippines (IPOPHL). The IPOPHL conducts substantive patent examination and operates a comprehensive IP registration system.

Key features include:

- **Cheaper Medicines Act (RA 9502, 2008)**: Amended the IP Code to strengthen compulsory licensing provisions, introduce parallel importation of patented drugs, early working/Bolar exemption for generic drug registration, and safeguards for government use of patented inventions. Section 22 provides for special compulsory licensing for drugs during emergencies.
- **IP Code Section 72**: Patentability exclusions include methods for treatment of the human or animal body by surgery or therapy and diagnostic methods, though products used in such methods remain patentable.
- **IP Code Section 93-100**: Compulsory licensing on grounds including national emergency, public interest, anti-competitive practices, and non-working. The IPOPHL has not issued a compulsory license but the threat has influenced pharmaceutical pricing negotiations.
- **Geographical Indications**: The IP Code and subsequent amendments protect Philippine GIs, with the Philippines joining the Madrid Protocol in 2012 and strengthening GI protection through the IPOPHL's GI registry.

The Supreme Court's decision in Zuneca Pharmaceutical v. Natrapharm, Inc. (2014, G.R. No. 197802) clarified the interplay between the IP Code and the regulatory authority of the Bureau of Food and Drugs (now FDA Philippines), holding that generic drug approvals do not constitute patent infringement per se, consistent with the Bolar exception.

## Comparative Analysis

### TRIPS Flexibilities and Development Policy

The Global South's approach to TRIPS flexibilities defines a spectrum from maximalist use (India, with Section 3(d), compulsory licensing, and the TKDL) to minimalist assertion (China and Mexico, with TRIPS-plus commitments in bilateral agreements). Brazil occupies a middle position, having issued one compulsory license while maintaining the strategic threat of others. South Africa's non-examination patent system paradoxically creates both vulnerability (low-quality patents) and opportunity (no substantive examination barrier to generic entry since patents may be challenged in litigation). The Philippines and Indonesia utilize TRIPS flexibilities primarily through legislative frameworks that have not yet been extensively tested through actual compulsory license grants.

### IP Courts and Enforcement Capacity

China's investment in specialized IP courts and the SPC IP Tribunal has no parallel in the Global South. India abolished its Intellectual Property Appellate Board (IPAB) in 2021 (Tribunal Reforms Ordinance, 2021, upheld in Madras Bar Association v. Union of India), transferring jurisdiction to High Courts, which face existing case backlogs. Brazil's INPI has improved examination capacity but the court system (Federal Regional Tribunals) lacks IP specialization. Nigeria and South Africa have no specialized IP courts. Indonesia's Commercial Court handles IP cases alongside bankruptcy and competition matters. The Philippines' IPOPHL operates a mediation and alternative dispute resolution program but the court system lacks IP specialization.

### Traditional Knowledge and Genetic Resources

The Global South collectively advocates for stronger international legal frameworks protecting traditional knowledge and genetic resources, with the WIPO Intergovernmental Committee on IP and Genetic Resources, Traditional Knowledge and Folklore (IGC) serving as the primary negotiating forum. India's TKDL model (defensive protection through prior art databases) has been the most operationally successful, while Brazil's Biodiversity Law 13,123/2015 (replacing the controversial Provisional Measure 2,186/2001) established benefit-sharing requirements for access to genetic resources and associated traditional knowledge. Indonesia's advocacy for mandatory disclosure of origin in patent applications reflects its biodiversity richness and traditional knowledge concerns.

### Digital IP and Emerging Technologies

AI-generated works and inventions present novel challenges across all jurisdictions. China's Beijing Internet Court held that AI-generated images may qualify for copyright protection if sufficient human creative input is involved (Li Junlin v. Liu Yuanchun, 2023, often cited as China's first AI copyright case). India's Copyright Office has not issued definitive guidance, though the Delhi High Court's approach in Ankit Sahni v. Chaitanya Educational Services (2023) suggests a requirement of human authorship. The lack of specialized AI-IP frameworks across the Global South creates uncertainty for an increasingly significant category of innovation.
