## Summary

Defamation -- the act of making false or damaging statements that injure another person's reputation -- occupies a uniquely contested space at the intersection of personal dignity and freedom of expression. The four jurisdictions examined here illustrate strikingly different philosophies. South Korea criminalizes defamation even when the statement is true, making it one of the most restrictive regimes in any democracy; its Criminal Act (Articles 307-312) and the Information and Communications Network Act (Article 70) impose imprisonment of up to seven years for online defamation using false facts. The United States, by contrast, treats defamation as an almost exclusively civil matter, with constitutional protections under the First Amendment erecting formidable barriers for plaintiffs -- most notably the "actual malice" standard established in New York Times Co. v. Sullivan (1964), which requires public officials to prove the defendant knew a statement was false or acted with reckless disregard for its truth. Germany occupies a middle ground, maintaining criminal defamation provisions (Sections 185-188 of the Strafgesetzbuch) that distinguish between insult (Beleidigung), defamation (uble Nachrede), and intentional defamation (Verleumdung), while also providing robust civil remedies grounded in constitutional protections of personal honor. The European Court of Human Rights (ECHR), interpreting Article 10 of the European Convention on Human Rights, has developed an extensive body of case law that consistently holds criminal defamation sanctions to be disproportionate restrictions on free expression, particularly when directed at political speech, while recognizing that the protection of reputation under Article 8 may justify some restrictions.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea maintains one of the most expansive defamation regimes among democratic nations, criminalizing defamatory speech even when the underlying statements are true. The primary statutory provisions are found in the Criminal Act (hyeongbeop) and the Act on Promotion of Information and Communications Network Utilization and Information Protection (commonly known as the ICNA or ICN Act).

**Criminal Act (Articles 307-312):**

- **Article 307(1) -- Defamation by True Facts:** A person who defames another by publicly alleging facts shall be punished by imprisonment (with or without prison labor) for not more than 2 years or by a fine not exceeding 5 million won.
- **Article 307(2) -- Defamation by False Facts:** A person who defames another by publicly alleging false facts shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than 5 years, suspension of qualifications for not more than 10 years, or by a fine not exceeding 10 million won.
- **Article 308 -- Defamation of the Dead:** Defaming a deceased person with false facts is punishable by imprisonment for not more than 2 years or a fine not exceeding 5 million won.
- **Article 309 -- Defamation by Publication:** Defamation committed through newspapers, magazines, radio, or other publications carries imprisonment for not more than 3 years (true facts) or 7 years (false facts).
- **Article 310 -- Justification:** If the facts alleged under Article 307(1) are true and the act was solely for the public interest, the act shall not be punishable.
- **Article 312 -- Complaint Requirement:** Prosecution for defamation under Articles 307 and 309 requires a complaint from the victim.

**Information and Communications Network Act (Article 70):**

- **Article 70(1):** A person who defames another by disclosing facts through an information and communications network with the purpose of disparaging another's reputation shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than 3 years or by a fine not exceeding 20 million won.
- **Article 70(2):** Where false facts are disclosed online, the penalty increases to imprisonment for not more than 7 years, suspension of qualifications for not more than 10 years, or a fine not exceeding 50 million won (increased to 70 million won under a December 2025 amendment).

### Elements of the Offense

To establish criminal defamation under Korean law, the prosecution must prove:

1. **Public Allegation (gongnyeonseong):** The statement must have been made "publicly," meaning it was communicated to an unspecified or large number of people. Korean courts have interpreted this broadly to include online posts, comments, and even semi-private group communications.
2. **Statement of Fact (sasil jeoksi):** The defendant must have alleged specific facts -- as opposed to pure opinions or value judgments -- capable of harming the victim's social reputation.
3. **Reputational Harm (myeong-ye hweson):** The facts alleged must be of a nature to lower the victim's reputation in the eyes of the general public.
4. **Intent (goui):** The defendant must have acted with at least general intent to make the public allegation, though specific intent to harm is not required under the Criminal Act. Under the ICNA, the heightened requirement of "purpose to disparage" applies.

### The Truth Defense Under Article 310

Unlike most democracies, South Korea does not treat truth as an absolute defense to defamation. Under Article 310 of the Criminal Act, two cumulative conditions must be met: the statement must be true, and the act must have been solely for the public interest. The Supreme Court of Korea has softened this somewhat, holding that: (a) the defendant need not prove the statement was perfectly true in every detail -- substantial truth suffices; (b) if the defendant reasonably believed the statement to be true based on adequate grounds, this may satisfy the truth requirement; and (c) the "solely for the public interest" criterion does not exclude cases where the defendant also had some personal motivation, so long as the primary purpose was public interest.

### Landmark Cases

**Constitutional Court Decision on Article 307(1) (2021):** The Constitutional Court of Korea upheld the constitutionality of Article 307(1), which criminalizes defamation by true facts, ruling 5-4 that the provision does not violate the constitutional guarantee of freedom of expression. Four dissenting justices argued the provision was an excessive restriction on speech. The decision drew significant criticism from international press freedom organizations, including Article 19 and the Committee to Protect Journalists.

**Park Dae-sung "Minerva" Case (2009):** Park Dae-sung, a blogger known by the pseudonym "Minerva," gained prominence for his online economic commentaries criticizing government policy. He was arrested in 2009 under the Telecommunications Basic Act for allegedly "spreading false information" about government economic policy. Although acquitted, the case illustrated how defamation-adjacent provisions could be deployed against online critics of the government.

**K-Pop Industry Defamation Cases:** South Korea's entertainment industry has become a significant driver of criminal defamation complaints. Celebrities and entertainment companies routinely file criminal defamation charges against online commenters, leveraging the low threshold for prosecution. Between 2017 and 2023, thousands of criminal defamation cases were filed in connection with online commentary about K-pop stars, raising concerns about the chilling effect on public discourse.

### Penalties Summary

| Provision | True Facts | False Facts |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| Criminal Act Art. 307 | Up to 2 years / 5 million won | Up to 5 years / 10 million won |
| Criminal Act Art. 309 (publication) | Up to 3 years | Up to 7 years |
| ICNA Art. 70 (online) | Up to 3 years / 20 million won | Up to 7 years / 70 million won |

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States treats defamation primarily as a state-law tort rather than a criminal offense, and the First Amendment to the Constitution imposes substantial constraints on defamation liability. While the common law historically recognized both libel (written defamation) and slander (spoken defamation), the Supreme Court has, since the 1960s, constitutionalized significant portions of defamation law to protect freedom of speech and press.

Although approximately half of U.S. states retain criminal defamation statutes, prosecutions are exceedingly rare, and several state supreme courts have struck down their criminal defamation laws as unconstitutional. The practical landscape of defamation in the United States is overwhelmingly civil.

### Elements of the Offense (Civil)

Under the common law framework (with constitutional modifications), a defamation plaintiff must prove:

1. **A False Statement of Fact:** The defendant made a statement purporting to be factual (not opinion, satire, or rhetorical hyperbole) that is provably false. Truth is an absolute defense.
2. **Publication:** The statement was communicated to at least one third party.
3. **Fault:** The degree of fault required depends on the plaintiff's status:
   - **Public officials and public figures:** Must prove "actual malice" -- that the defendant made the statement with knowledge of its falsity or with reckless disregard for whether it was true or false (New York Times Co. v. Sullivan, 1964; Curtis Publishing Co. v. Butts, 1967).
   - **Private figures:** Must prove at least negligence, though states may set a higher standard. Private figures suing media defendants over matters of public concern must prove actual malice to recover presumed or punitive damages (Gertz v. Robert Welch, Inc., 1974).
4. **Damages:** The plaintiff suffered reputational harm or other cognizable injury.

### Constitutional Protections

The First Amendment provides the most robust speech protections in the world against defamation liability:

- **Actual Malice Standard:** Established in Sullivan, this doctrine requires public officials and public figures to meet a heightened burden that effectively shields the press and public from liability for good-faith errors in reporting on public affairs.
- **Opinion Privilege:** Pure statements of opinion that do not imply undisclosed defamatory facts are constitutionally protected (Milkovich v. Lorain Journal Co., 1990).
- **Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act (47 U.S.C. section 230):** Provides broad immunity to online platforms and internet service providers for content posted by third-party users, making it nearly impossible to hold platforms liable for defamatory user-generated content.
- **Anti-SLAPP Statutes:** Many states have enacted statutes allowing defendants to quickly dismiss "Strategic Lawsuits Against Public Participation" -- defamation suits designed to silence critics through the burden of litigation.

### Landmark Cases

**New York Times Co. v. Sullivan, 376 U.S. 254 (1964):** The foundational case of modern American defamation law. An Alabama police commissioner sued the New York Times over a full-page advertisement containing minor factual inaccuracies about civil rights protests in Montgomery. The Supreme Court held that the First Amendment requires public officials to prove "actual malice" to recover damages for defamation, reasoning that "debate on public issues should be uninhibited, robust, and wide-open, and that it may well include vehement, caustic, and sometimes unpleasantly sharp attacks on government and public officials."

**Gertz v. Robert Welch, Inc., 418 U.S. 323 (1974):** The Court held that private individuals need not meet the actual malice standard for compensatory damages but must still show at least negligence. States cannot impose strict liability for defamation. The decision established a framework distinguishing between public figures (who have access to media to rebut falsehoods and have assumed the risk of public scrutiny) and private figures (who lack such access and have not voluntarily exposed themselves to public attention).

**Hustler Magazine, Inc. v. Falwell, 485 U.S. 46 (1988):** The Court held that public figures and public officials cannot recover damages for intentional infliction of emotional distress based on a publication unless it contains a false statement of fact made with actual malice. The case arose from a crude parody advertisement depicting televangelist Jerry Falwell, and extended Sullivan protections to emotional distress claims.

**Snyder v. Phelps, 562 U.S. 443 (2011):** The Court held that speech on matters of public concern, even if deeply offensive, is protected by the First Amendment when conducted in a public place in compliance with applicable laws.

### Remedies and Damages

| Category | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Compensatory (actual) damages** | Quantifiable losses: lost income, business harm, medical expenses |
| **Presumed damages** | Available in libel per se cases (accusations of crime, loathsome disease, professional incompetence, or sexual misconduct) |
| **Punitive damages** | Require proof of actual malice (knowledge of falsity or reckless disregard) |
| **Injunctive relief** | Courts are reluctant to grant prior restraints; post-judgment injunctions are rare |
| **Retraction** | Many states have retraction statutes that limit damages if a timely retraction is published |

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany maintains a dual system of criminal and civil defamation law, reflecting a constitutional commitment to the protection of personal honor (personliche Ehre) that coexists with the guarantee of freedom of expression under Article 5 of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz). German law distinguishes among three categories of reputational offenses in the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB):

- **Section 185 -- Insult (Beleidigung):** Punishes the expression of contempt or disrespect toward another person. Unlike defamation proper, insult does not require a factual assertion -- it covers value judgments, gestures, and expressions that communicate a lack of respect. Penalty: imprisonment for not more than 1 year or a fine; up to 2 years if committed publicly, in a meeting, by disseminating content, or by means of an assault.
- **Section 186 -- Defamation (Uble Nachrede):** Punishes the assertion or dissemination of facts about another person that may defame them or negatively affect public opinion, unless the facts can be proven to be true. Penalty: imprisonment for not more than 1 year or a fine; up to 2 years if committed publicly or through dissemination of content.
- **Section 187 -- Intentional Defamation / Slander (Verleumdung):** Punishes the deliberate assertion or dissemination of facts known to be untrue that may defame another or undermine their standing. Penalty: imprisonment for not more than 5 years or a fine.
- **Section 188 -- Defamation of Persons in Political Life:** Provides enhanced penalties for insult, defamation, or slander directed against persons involved in political life, with imprisonment of up to 3 years for insult and up to 5 years for defamation and slander, reflecting the special vulnerability of politicians to targeted attacks that could undermine democratic governance.
- **Section 189 -- Disparagement of the Memory of the Dead:** Punishes defamation of a deceased person with imprisonment of up to 2 years or a fine.
- **Section 192 -- Insult Despite Proof of Truth:** Even if a defamatory factual assertion is proven true, the speaker may still be punished for insult if the insult arises from the form of the assertion or the circumstances under which it was made.

### Civil Law Remedies

German civil law provides additional remedies through:

- **Section 823(1) of the Burgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB):** Establishes liability for unlawful, intentional, or negligent violations of "other rights," which courts have interpreted to include the general right of personality (allgemeines Personlichkeitsrecht).
- **Section 823(2) BGB in conjunction with Sections 185-187 StGB:** Creates civil liability for violations of the criminal defamation statutes.
- **Section 1004 BGB (by analogy):** Provides injunctive relief, including cease-and-desist orders and removal of defamatory content.

German courts balance freedom of expression (Article 5 GG) against the right to personal honor and human dignity (Articles 1 and 2 GG) through a case-by-case proportionality analysis, distinguishing between factual assertions (Tatsachenbehauptungen) -- which may be true or false -- and value judgments (Werturteile) -- which are more broadly protected.

### Online Defamation and the NetzDG

Germany's Network Enforcement Act (Netzwerkdurchsetzungsgesetz, NetzDG), enacted in 2017, required social media platforms with more than 2 million registered users in Germany to remove "clearly illegal" content -- including content violating Sections 185-187 StGB -- within 24 hours and other illegal content within 7 days of receiving complaints. Platforms faced fines of up to 50 million euros for systematic non-compliance. The NetzDG was repealed and superseded by the EU Digital Services Act (DSA), which became fully applicable in February 2024 and establishes harmonized content moderation obligations across all EU Member States.

### Landmark Cases

**Luth Decision (BVerfGE 7, 198, 1958):** The Federal Constitutional Court established the doctrine of "indirect horizontal effect" (mittelbare Drittwirkung), holding that fundamental rights -- including freedom of expression -- radiate into private law and must be considered when interpreting civil law provisions, including those governing defamation.

**Caroline von Hannover v. Germany (ECHR, 2004 and 2012):** The European Court of Human Rights ruled that German courts had failed to adequately protect Princess Caroline of Monaco's right to private life under Article 8 ECHR, finding that the publication of paparazzi photographs did not contribute to a debate of general interest. The decision prompted Germany to revise its approach to balancing privacy and press freedom.

**Tucholsky Quote Case (BVerfG, 1995):** The Federal Constitutional Court held that the statement "Soldiers are murderers" (attributed to Kurt Tucholsky) was a constitutionally protected expression of opinion, reversing a conviction for insult. The decision affirmed that provocative and exaggerated statements on matters of public concern enjoy strong constitutional protection.

| 188 | Against politicians | Up to 3 years (insult) | Up to 5 years (defamation) |

## ECHR & EU

### European Convention on Human Rights -- Article 10

The European Court of Human Rights (ECtHR) has developed the most extensive supranational jurisprudence on defamation through its interpretation of Article 10 of the European Convention on Human Rights, which guarantees the right to freedom of expression. Article 10(2) permits restrictions on this right that are "prescribed by law," pursue a "legitimate aim" (including the protection of the reputation or rights of others), and are "necessary in a democratic society."

### Three-Part Test for Restrictions

When a state interferes with freedom of expression through defamation proceedings, the Court applies a three-part test:

1. **Prescribed by Law:** The interference must have a legal basis in domestic law that is sufficiently accessible and foreseeable.
2. **Legitimate Aim:** The interference must pursue one of the aims enumerated in Article 10(2), typically the protection of reputation or the rights of others (Article 8).
3. **Necessary in a Democratic Society:** The interference must correspond to a "pressing social need," be proportionate to the legitimate aim pursued, and be supported by "relevant and sufficient" reasons. This is the most frequently contested element.

### Key Principles from Case Law

**Distinction Between Fact and Opinion:** The Court draws a fundamental distinction between statements of fact, which are susceptible to proof, and value judgments (opinions), which are not. Requiring a person to prove the truth of a value judgment is itself a violation of Article 10 (Lingens v. Austria, 1986).

**Enhanced Protection for Political Speech:** Politicians and public officials must tolerate a wider degree of criticism than private individuals. The limits of acceptable criticism are broader with regard to the government and public officials acting in their official capacity than with regard to private individuals (Castells v. Spain, 1992; Oberschlick v. Austria, 1991).

**Chilling Effect Doctrine:** Disproportionate sanctions, even if ultimately overturned, may violate Article 10 because they produce a chilling effect on free expression, deterring journalists and citizens from speaking on matters of public concern (Cumpana and Mazare v. Romania, 2004; Nikula v. Finland, 2002).

**Criminal Sanctions Disfavored:** The Court has repeatedly held that criminal sanctions for defamation, particularly imprisonment, are disproportionate to the aim of protecting reputation and should be reserved for the most serious cases, such as hate speech or incitement to violence (Mahmudov and Agazade v. Azerbaijan, 2008).

**Balancing Articles 8 and 10:** In cases involving the reputations of private individuals, the Court must balance the right to freedom of expression (Article 10) against the right to respect for private life (Article 8), which protects personal reputation. Neither right takes automatic precedence (Von Hannover v. Germany (No. 2), 2012; Axel Springer AG v. Germany, 2012).

### Landmark Cases

**Lingens v. Austria (1986):** Peter Lingens, an Austrian journalist, was convicted of criminal defamation for publishing articles accusing Chancellor Bruno Kreisky of protecting former Nazis and characterizing his conduct as "immoral" and the "basest opportunism." The ECtHR held unanimously that the conviction violated Article 10, establishing the principle that value judgments cannot be subjected to a truth requirement, and that politicians must accept wider boundaries of acceptable criticism.

**Castells v. Spain (1992):** Miguel Castells, a Basque senator, was convicted of insulting the Spanish government for publishing an article alleging government complicity in attacks on Basque activists. The Court held that the conviction violated Article 10, stating that "the dominant position which the Government occupies makes it necessary for it to display restraint in resorting to criminal proceedings" and that an elected representative's freedom of expression warrants "very close scrutiny."

**Axel Springer AG v. Germany (2012):** The Grand Chamber established a detailed balancing framework for Article 8/10 conflicts, setting out criteria including: the contribution of the publication to a debate of general interest, the degree of notoriety of the person affected, the prior conduct of the person concerned, the method of obtaining information and its veracity, the content, form, and consequences of the publication, and the severity of the sanction imposed.

**Delfi AS v. Estonia (2015):** The Grand Chamber held that holding an internet news portal liable for defamatory third-party comments did not violate Article 10, given the extreme nature of the comments, the commercial benefit derived from user engagement, and the portal's failure to implement adequate content moderation. This decision stands in contrast to the broad platform immunity provided by Section 230 in the United States.

### EU Digital Services Act (2024)

The Digital Services Act (Regulation (EU) 2022/2065), which became fully applicable on February 17, 2024, replaced national platform liability frameworks such as Germany's NetzDG. Key provisions relevant to defamation include:

- **Notice-and-Action Mechanisms (Article 16):** Platforms must establish mechanisms for individuals to notify them of illegal content, including defamatory material under national laws. Platforms must process notices in a timely, non-arbitrary, and objective manner.
- **Statement of Reasons (Article 17):** Platforms must inform users of any content moderation decision and provide a clear and specific statement of reasons, including the legal basis relied upon.
- **Internal Complaint Mechanism (Article 20):** Users affected by content moderation decisions must have access to an internal complaint-handling system.
- **Out-of-Court Dispute Resolution (Article 21):** Users may refer disputes to certified out-of-court dispute settlement bodies.
- **Very Large Online Platforms (VLOPs):** Platforms with more than 45 million monthly active users in the EU face additional obligations, including systemic risk assessments and independent audits.

The DSA does not harmonize substantive defamation law -- each Member State retains its own criminal and civil defamation provisions -- but establishes a uniform procedural framework for content moderation across the EU.

## Comparative Analysis

### Criminal vs. Civil Approaches

The four jurisdictions represent a spectrum from predominantly criminal (South Korea) to predominantly civil (United States) approaches to defamation, with Germany and the ECHR framework occupying positions in between.

**South Korea** stands as an outlier among democracies in its heavy reliance on criminal defamation prosecution. The criminalization of true statements -- with prison sentences of up to three years even for accurate speech made through publications -- is virtually unique among OECD nations. International press freedom organizations, including Article 19, Reporters Without Borders, and the UN Human Rights Committee, have repeatedly called on South Korea to decriminalize defamation by true facts, arguing that it creates a severe chilling effect on journalism and public discourse.

**The United States** occupies the opposite extreme. Criminal defamation is nearly extinct in practice, and the constitutional framework makes successful civil defamation claims difficult to pursue, particularly for public figures. Critics argue that the actual malice standard has become too protective, enabling the spread of disinformation with impunity. In 2021, Justice Clarence Thomas called for the Court to reconsider New York Times v. Sullivan, and Justice Neil Gorsuch expressed similar skepticism, though no case has yet provided a vehicle for such reconsideration.

**Germany** maintains criminal defamation provisions that are actively enforced, particularly the insult provisions under Section 185, which account for tens of thousands of prosecutions annually. However, the penalties are generally modest (fines and short prison terms), and the German constitutional framework ensures robust judicial balancing between expression and honor.

**The ECHR** has consistently pushed European states toward decriminalizing defamation, holding that criminal sanctions -- particularly imprisonment -- are disproportionate in nearly all defamation cases. The Court's jurisprudence has driven legal reform across Council of Europe Member States, though many retain criminal defamation provisions on their books.

### Truth as a Defense

| Jurisdiction | Truth Defense |
|-------------|--------------|
| South Korea | Partial -- truth alone is insufficient; the defendant must also prove the statement was made solely for the public interest (Art. 310) |
| United States | Absolute -- truth is a complete defense in all circumstances |
| Germany | Available for Sections 186-187 (defamation), but not necessarily for Section 185 (insult), where even true statements may be punishable if made in a degrading manner (Section 192) |
| ECHR | The Court strongly disfavors punishing true statements and treats truth as a near-absolute defense, though it recognizes that the manner of disclosure may be relevant |

### Protection of Public Discourse

The Sullivan actual malice standard in the United States and the Lingens/Castells principles under the ECHR both provide enhanced protection for speech about public officials and matters of public concern, though they achieve this through different mechanisms. The U.S. approach imposes a heightened burden of proof on the plaintiff, while the ECHR approach scrutinizes the proportionality of the state's response. South Korea and Germany provide some recognition of public interest as a defense or mitigating factor, but neither offers the level of structural protection for political speech found in U.S. or ECHR law.

### Online Defamation

The digital age has prompted all four jurisdictions to address online defamation, but their approaches diverge sharply. South Korea's ICNA imposes harsher penalties for online defamation than for offline defamation -- a framework that has been criticized for discouraging legitimate online speech. The United States provides near-absolute platform immunity through Section 230, which has enabled the growth of user-generated content platforms but has also been criticized for shielding platforms from accountability for harmful content. Germany's NetzDG pioneered mandatory platform content moderation with significant fines for non-compliance, an approach now superseded by the EU-wide Digital Services Act. The ECHR's Delfi decision established that platforms can in some circumstances be held liable for user-generated content, marking a significant departure from the U.S. Section 230 approach.
