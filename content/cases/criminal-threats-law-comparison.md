## Summary

Criminal threats -- the communication of an intent to inflict harm on another person, whether through verbal, written, or electronic means -- occupy a complex position at the intersection of criminal law and free expression protections across legal systems worldwide. South Korea addresses criminal threats primarily through Article 283 of the Criminal Act (형법 제283조, 협박죄), which criminalizes threats directed at individuals or their relatives with penalties of up to three years' imprisonment or a fine of up to five million KRW, with aggravated provisions under Article 284 for habitual offenders who face up to seven years' imprisonment. The United States regulates criminal threats through a patchwork of federal statutes -- including 18 U.S.C. Section 875 (interstate threats), 18 U.S.C. Section 871 (threats against the President), and the landmark Supreme Court decision in Counterman v. Colorado (2023) establishing that the First Amendment requires proof of recklessness as a minimum mens rea standard -- alongside fifty separate state criminal threat statutes that vary considerably in their definitions and penalty structures. Germany addresses threats through Section 241 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB, Bedrohung), which was substantially reformed in 2021 to expand the scope of criminalized threats beyond the previous limitation to threats of serious criminal offenses (Verbrechen), now encompassing threats of offenses against sexual self-determination, bodily integrity, and personal freedom. At the European level, the European Court of Human Rights has developed extensive jurisprudence balancing the criminalization of threats against Article 10 free expression protections, while the EU has addressed specific categories of threats through Directive 2017/541 on combating terrorism and the Framework Decision 2008/913/JHA on combating racism and xenophobia.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's criminal threat provisions are codified in Chapter 30 of the Criminal Act (형법 제30장, 협박의 죄), which establishes a graduated system of offenses. Article 283, Paragraph 1 (협박죄) provides the basic offense: "A person who threatens another shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than three years, by a fine not exceeding five million won, by detention or by a minor fine." The statute does not require that the threat involve a specific crime or that the threatened harm be imminent; rather, the standard is whether the communication was sufficient to cause fear (공포심) in the recipient.

### Penalty Structure

| Offense | Provision | Penalty |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Basic threat | Art. 283(1) | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine up to 5 million KRW |
| Threat against lineal ascendant | Art. 283(2) | Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine up to 10 million KRW |
| Habitual threatening | Art. 284 | Up to 7 years imprisonment |
| Special intimidation (threat with dangerous weapon) | Art. 284 in conjunction with Punishment of Violences Act | Up to 7 years imprisonment |

### Victim-Consent Clause

The basic threat offense under Article 283(1) is classified as a crime requiring victim consent for prosecution (반의사불벌죄) under Article 283(3). This means the prosecutor cannot pursue the case if the victim expressly objects to prosecution. However, the aggravated offenses under Article 284 (habitual threats) are prosecutable ex officio without victim consent, recognizing the heightened danger posed by repeat offenders.

### Judicial Interpretation

The Supreme Court of Korea (대법원) has established several key principles in interpreting threat offenses. In Supreme Court Decision 2007Do606 (2007), the court held that a threat need not be explicitly verbal but can be communicated through conduct, gestures, or circumstances that convey an implicit promise of harm. The court has also clarified that the threat must be assessed from the perspective of a reasonable person in the victim's position, taking into account the relationship between the parties, the context in which the communication was made, and the specificity of the threatened harm.

### Interaction with Other Statutes

Criminal threats in South Korea often overlap with other offenses. The Act on Punishment of Violence (폭력행위 등 처벌에 관한 법률) provides enhanced penalties when threats are accompanied by the use of dangerous weapons or are committed by groups. The Information and Communications Network Act (정보통신망 이용촉진 및 정보보호 등에 관한 법률) addresses threats transmitted through electronic communications, with penalties of up to one year imprisonment or a fine of up to ten million KRW under Article 44-7 for distributing information inducing fear or anxiety.

### Landmark Cases

The Seoul Central District Court case involving threats against public officials in 2019 highlighted the tension between political expression and criminal threats. The defendant had posted online statements threatening harm to government officials during heated political debates. The court drew a distinction between heated political rhetoric and genuine criminal threats, acquitting the defendant on the grounds that the statements were understood in context as expressions of political frustration rather than genuine communications of intent to harm.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States addresses criminal threats through a dual system of federal and state laws, constrained by the First Amendment's protection of free speech. The "true threats" doctrine, first articulated by the Supreme Court in Watts v. United States (1969) and refined in Virginia v. Black (2003), exempts genuine threats from constitutional protection while safeguarding political hyperbole and protected expression.

**Federal Statutes:**

| Statute | Scope | Penalty |
|---------|-------|---------|
| 18 U.S.C. § 871 | Threats against the President, Vice President, and successors | Up to 5 years imprisonment |
| 18 U.S.C. § 875(c) | Interstate threats transmitted by communication | Up to 5 years imprisonment |
| 18 U.S.C. § 876 | Mailing threatening communications | Up to 5 years imprisonment |
| 18 U.S.C. § 879 | Threats against former Presidents and certain officials | Up to 5 years imprisonment |
| 18 U.S.C. § 2332a | Threats involving weapons of mass destruction | Up to 10 years imprisonment |

### Counterman v. Colorado (2023)

The Supreme Court's decision in Counterman v. Colorado, 600 U.S. 66 (2023), resolved a circuit split on the mens rea requirement for criminal threat prosecutions. Billy Raymond Counterman had sent hundreds of Facebook messages to a local musician over a two-year period, including statements like "Was prior a prior to this for prior to this or prior to this." The Colorado courts convicted him under a statute requiring only that a reasonable person would regard the statements as threatening, without considering whether Counterman himself understood the threatening nature of his communications.

The Supreme Court, in a 7-2 decision authored by Justice Kagan, held that the First Amendment requires proof that the defendant had some subjective understanding of the threatening nature of their statements, establishing recklessness as the minimum mens rea standard. The Court reasoned that a purely objective standard would chill too much protected speech, as speakers might self-censor ambiguous statements for fear of prosecution based on how others might perceive them.

### State Variations

State criminal threat statutes vary significantly:

**California Penal Code Section 422:** Requires a specific intent to have the threat taken as a threat, that the threat was "so unequivocal, unconditional, immediate, and specific as to convey a gravity of purpose and an immediate prospect of execution," and that the victim was placed in sustained fear for their safety or their family's safety. Penalties include up to one year (misdemeanor) or up to three years (felony).

**New York Penal Law Section 120.15 (Menacing):** Criminalizes intentionally placing another person in fear of death, imminent serious physical injury, or physical injury by displaying a weapon. New York does not have a specific "criminal threats" statute comparable to California's.

**Texas Penal Code Section 22.07 (Terroristic Threat):** Broadly defines criminal threats to include threats of violence to persons or property committed with intent to cause a reaction by official or volunteer agencies, place any person in fear of imminent serious bodily injury, or prevent or interrupt the occupation or use of a building or public place.

### Threat Assessment and Law Enforcement

The United States has developed sophisticated threat assessment frameworks, particularly following the September 11 attacks. The FBI's Behavioral Analysis Unit and the Secret Service's National Threat Assessment Center (NTAC) have published research distinguishing between "howlers" (individuals who make threats but do not pose genuine danger) and "hunters" (individuals who pose genuine danger but may not have made explicit threats). This research has influenced both prosecutorial charging decisions and the development of workplace violence prevention programs.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany addresses criminal threats through Section 241 of the Strafgesetzbuch (Bedrohung), which underwent a significant expansion through the Act to Combat Right-Wing Extremism and Hate Crime (Gesetz zur Bekämpfung des Rechtsextremismus und der Hasskriminalität) of March 30, 2021.

**Pre-2021 Version:** Section 241 previously criminalized only threats to commit a Verbrechen (serious criminal offense carrying a minimum sentence of one year imprisonment). This limitation meant that threats of minor assaults, sexual harassment, or property damage were not covered unless they constituted threats of serious criminal offenses.

**Post-2021 Version:** The reformed Section 241 significantly broadened the scope of criminal threats:

| Paragraph | Conduct | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| Section 241(1) | Threatening another with the commission of a crime (Verbrechen) against them or a person close to them | Fine or up to 1 year imprisonment |
| Section 241(2) | Threatening another with a crime against sexual self-determination, bodily integrity, or personal freedom | Fine or up to 2 years imprisonment |
| Section 241(3) | Threat made publicly, in a gathering, or through dissemination of content | Fine or up to 3 years imprisonment |
| Section 241(4) | If the act is committed in a manner suitable to disturb the public peace | 3 months to 5 years imprisonment |

### Anti-Hate Speech Context

The 2021 reform was driven primarily by the escalating problem of online threats against politicians, journalists, and public figures in Germany. The murder of Walter Lübcke, the district president of Kassel, on June 1, 2019, by a right-wing extremist who had been radicalized online, prompted a comprehensive legislative response. The government recognized that threats often serve as precursors to violence and that the narrow scope of the previous Section 241 left dangerous gaps.

### Relationship to Other Offenses

Criminal threats in German law interact with several related provisions. Section 240 StGB (Nötigung, coercion) criminalizes the use of force or threats to compel another to perform, tolerate, or omit an act. Section 126 StGB (Störung des öffentlichen Friedens durch Androhung von Straftaten) addresses disturbance of public peace through threats of specified criminal offenses. Section 241a StGB addresses threats related to whistleblowing activities in the corporate context.

### Constitutional Considerations

The Bundesverfassungsgericht (Federal Constitutional Court) has addressed the boundary between protected expression and criminal threats in several decisions. In its landmark Wunsiedel decision (BVerfGE 124, 300, 2009), the court held that the general framework for restricting expression under Article 5(2) of the Grundgesetz requires that restrictions serve a legitimate purpose, are suitable and necessary, and maintain proportionality. Applied to criminal threats, this means that ambiguous or hyperbolic statements in the context of political discourse receive heightened protection, while specific and credible threats directed at identified individuals fall outside constitutional protection.

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR has developed substantial jurisprudence on criminal threats, primarily through the lens of Article 10 (freedom of expression) and Article 8 (right to respect for private life).

**Perinçek v. Switzerland [GC] (Application No. 27510/08, 2015):** While primarily concerning genocide denial, the Grand Chamber's analysis established a comprehensive framework for evaluating when speech creates sufficient danger to justify criminal sanction. The Court identified six factors: whether the statements were made against a tense political or social background, whether they could be regarded as a direct or indirect call for violence, whether the person making the statements held a position of influence, and whether the statements were disseminated widely.

**Beizaras and Levickas v. Lithuania (Application No. 41288/15, 2020):** The Court found a violation of Articles 14 and 8 where Lithuanian authorities failed to investigate threatening and inciting comments posted online against the applicants based on their sexual orientation. This established that states have positive obligations to investigate and prosecute threats, particularly those motivated by discrimination.

**Škorjanec v. Croatia (Application No. 25536/14, 2017):** The Court held that states must treat hate-motivated threats and violence with particular seriousness and investigate the discriminatory motivation as an integral part of any criminal investigation.

### EU Framework

**Framework Decision 2008/913/JHA:** This decision requires member states to criminalize publicly inciting violence or hatred against groups defined by race, color, religion, descent, or national or ethnic origin, and to ensure penalties of at least one to three years imprisonment for the most serious forms.

**Directive 2017/541 on Combating Terrorism:** Article 5 requires member states to criminalize public provocation to commit a terrorist offense, including threats to commit such offenses, with penalties reflecting the seriousness of the conduct.

**Digital Services Act (Regulation (EU) 2022/2065):** While not a criminal law instrument, the DSA requires online platforms to establish mechanisms for reporting illegal content, including criminal threats, and to act expeditiously upon receiving notice.

### Council of Europe Convention on Cybercrime (Budapest Convention, 2001)

The Budapest Convention, while primarily addressing computer-related offenses, provides a framework for international cooperation in investigating and prosecuting cyberthreats transmitted across borders. Article 29 provides for expedited preservation of stored computer data, and Article 35 establishes a 24/7 network of contact points for urgent assistance in cybercrime investigations, including those involving threats.

## Comparative Analysis

### Definitional Approaches

The four jurisdictions reflect fundamentally different approaches to defining criminal threats:

**Severity-Based (Germany, pre-2021):** Germany's original Section 241 limited criminal threats to threats of Verbrechen -- the most serious category of criminal offenses. This approach provided high legal certainty but created gaps for threats of less serious but nonetheless harmful conduct.

**Fear-Based (South Korea):** The Korean approach focuses on whether the communication was sufficient to induce fear in the recipient, without requiring that the threatened harm constitute a specific criminal offense. This approach provides broad coverage but may raise concerns about vagueness and inconsistent application.

**Subjective Intent (United States post-Counterman):** Following Counterman v. Colorado, the U.S. approach requires proof that the speaker was at minimum reckless as to whether their statements would be perceived as threatening. This approach privileges free expression concerns but may make prosecution more difficult.

**Proportionality-Based (ECHR):** The ECHR framework does not prescribe specific definitions but requires that any criminal threat provision satisfy the proportionality requirements of Article 10(2), balancing the legitimate aim of protecting individuals from threats against the right to free expression.

### Prosecution Models

A significant divergence exists in whether victim consent is required for prosecution. South Korea's basic threat offense remains a victim-consent crime (반의사불벌죄), meaning prosecution cannot proceed against the victim's expressed wishes. All other jurisdictions permit ex officio prosecution. Germany shifted from a complaint offense (Antragsdelikt) to ex officio prosecution as part of the 2021 reform, recognizing that victims of threats may be intimidated into declining prosecution.

### Online Threats

All four jurisdictions have recognized the need to adapt criminal threat laws to the digital environment. South Korea addresses online threats through the Information and Communications Network Act. The United States applies existing federal threat statutes to electronic communications. Germany's 2021 reform specifically addressed online threats through the new Section 241(3), providing enhanced penalties for threats disseminated through digital content. The EU's Digital Services Act creates complementary platform obligations for content moderation.
