## Summary

Martial law -- the imposition of direct military authority over civilian governance, typically involving the suspension of ordinary civil liberties, the replacement of civilian courts with military tribunals, and the exercise of executive power unconstrained by normal legal limits -- represents the most extreme form of emergency governance and has been a defining feature of political crises across jurisdictions, with constitutional frameworks varying dramatically in how they authorize, constrain, and terminate military rule. South Korea has a particularly fraught history with martial law, having experienced multiple declarations under the authoritarian regimes of Park Chung-hee and Chun Doo-hwan, most notoriously during the Gwangju Uprising of May 1980; the Constitution of the Republic of Korea (대한민국 헌법) addresses martial law in Articles 77 and 77, distinguishing between extraordinary martial law (비상계엄) and precautionary martial law (경비계엄), with the Constitutional Court's (헌법재판소) authority to review and the National Assembly's power to demand its lifting serving as constitutional checks -- checks dramatically tested in December 2024 when President Yoon Suk-yeol briefly declared martial law before it was rescinded by the National Assembly within hours, leading to his impeachment. The United States has no express constitutional provision for martial law, though Article I Section 9 permits suspension of habeas corpus "when in Cases of Rebellion or Invasion the public Safety may require it," with the Supreme Court's landmark decision in Ex parte Milligan (1866) establishing that martial law cannot be imposed where civilian courts remain open and functioning, and the Insurrection Act (10 U.S.C. Sections 251-255) providing the statutory framework for domestic military deployment. Germany addresses emergency governance through the comprehensive Notstandsgesetze (Emergency Acts) of 1968, which amended the Grundgesetz to create a graduated system of internal emergency (innerer Notstand), defense emergency (Verteidigungsfall), and tension (Spannungsfall), deliberately avoiding the concept of martial law and maintaining civilian control even during the most severe emergencies through the Joint Committee (Gemeinsamer Ausschuss) mechanism. The European Court of Human Rights has developed substantial jurisprudence on states of emergency and martial law under Article 15 (derogation in time of emergency), consistently requiring that derogating measures be "strictly required by the exigencies of the situation" and subject to judicial oversight.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Martial law in South Korea is governed by Articles 77 of the Constitution and the Martial Law Act (계엄법). The Constitution distinguishes between two forms:

**Extraordinary Martial Law (비상계엄):** The President may declare extraordinary martial law when there is a military necessity or when it is necessary to maintain public order by mobilization of the military in time of war, armed conflict, or similar national emergency. Under extraordinary martial law, special measures may be taken with respect to the freedom of speech, press, assembly, and association; warrants for arrest, detention, search, and seizure may be suspended; and the authority of civilian courts and the government may be transferred to military commanders.

**Precautionary Martial Law (경비계엄):** A less severe form that deploys military forces to maintain public order without suspending civil liberties or transferring judicial authority to military tribunals.

### Gwangju Uprising (1980)

The declaration of nationwide extraordinary martial law on May 17, 1980, by General Chun Doo-hwan led directly to the Gwangju Democratization Movement (광주민주화운동) of May 18-27, 1980. Military special forces deployed to suppress pro-democracy demonstrations in Gwangju killed an officially acknowledged 163 civilians (with estimates ranging to over 600). The Gwangju massacre became the defining event of South Korea's democratization movement.

In 1996, former presidents Chun Doo-hwan and Roh Tae-woo were convicted under the Special Act on the May 18th Democratization Movement (5·18민주화운동 등에 관한 특별법) for their roles in the coup and massacre. Chun was sentenced to death (commuted to life imprisonment) and Roh to 22.5 years imprisonment.

### December 2024 Martial Law Declaration

On December 3, 2024, President Yoon Suk-yeol declared extraordinary martial law, citing threats from "anti-state forces." The National Assembly convened an emergency session and voted unanimously (190-0) to demand the lifting of martial law within approximately six hours, exercising the constitutional check provided by Article 77(5). President Yoon rescinded the declaration. The incident led to impeachment proceedings, with the National Assembly voting to impeach on December 14, 2024, and the Constitutional Court unanimously confirming the impeachment on April 4, 2025.

### Penalties Related to Martial Law Abuse

The Special Act on the May 18th Democratization Movement established that the statute of limitations does not apply to crimes committed in connection with the unconstitutional seizure of power, including martial law abuses. Prosecution of martial law-related crimes may proceed regardless of the passage of time.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The U.S. Constitution contains no express martial law clause. The relevant constitutional and statutory provisions include:

**Suspension of Habeas Corpus (Art. I, Sec. 9, Cl. 2):** "The Privilege of the Writ of Habeas Corpus shall not be suspended, unless when in Cases of Rebellion or Invasion the public Safety may require it." Whether this power belongs to Congress or the President has been debated since the Civil War.

**Insurrection Act (10 U.S.C. Sections 251-255):** The primary statutory authority for domestic deployment of federal military forces, the Act permits the President to use the military to suppress insurrection (Section 252), to enforce federal law when state authorities are unable or unwilling (Section 253), and to protect civil rights when state authorities fail to do so (Section 253). The Act has been invoked over 30 times, most recently considered during the 2020 civil unrest.

### Ex parte Milligan, 71 U.S. (4 Wall.) 2 (1866)

The Supreme Court's most important martial law decision arose from the military tribunal conviction of Lambdin Milligan, an Indiana civilian tried and sentenced to death by a military commission during the Civil War for conspiring against the United States. The Court unanimously reversed the conviction, holding:

- Martial law cannot be applied where civilian courts are open and functioning
- The Constitution operates "equally in war and in peace" and protects "all classes of men, at all times, and under all circumstances"
- Military tribunals have no jurisdiction over civilians in areas where civil courts are operational

Justice Davis, writing for the majority, declared: "Martial law can never exist where the courts are open, and in the proper and unobstructed exercise of their jurisdiction."

### Historical Applications

**Civil War (1861-1865):** President Lincoln suspended habeas corpus along the Washington-Philadelphia corridor in 1861, later extended nationwide. Chief Justice Taney, sitting as circuit judge, ruled the suspension unconstitutional in Ex parte Merryman (1861), but Lincoln ignored the ruling.

**Hawaii (1941-1944):** Following the attack on Pearl Harbor, Hawaii's territorial governor declared martial law and transferred governmental authority to the military commander. Martial law lasted nearly three years, during which military tribunals replaced civilian courts for all criminal matters. The Supreme Court ruled the martial law regime unconstitutional in Duncan v. Kahanamoku, 327 U.S. 304 (1946).

**Little Rock (1957):** President Eisenhower invoked the Insurrection Act to federalize the Arkansas National Guard and deploy the 101st Airborne Division to enforce school desegregation at Little Rock Central High School, representing a civil rights application of military deployment authority.

### Posse Comitatus Act

The Posse Comitatus Act (18 U.S.C. Section 1385), enacted in 1878, prohibits the use of the Army (and by extension other military branches) for civilian law enforcement purposes except when expressly authorized by the Constitution or Act of Congress. The Insurrection Act is the primary exception.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's approach to emergency governance was fundamentally shaped by the Weimar Republic's experience, where Article 48 of the Weimar Constitution allowed presidential emergency powers that were instrumentalized by the Nazi regime to establish dictatorship. The Grundgesetz drafters initially omitted emergency provisions entirely, preferring a potential governance gap to the risk of authorizing emergency powers.

The Notstandsgesetze (Emergency Acts) were enacted on June 28, 1968, amending the Grundgesetz to add Articles 80a, 87a, 91, and 115a-115l, following intense parliamentary and public debate. The amendments established a graduated system that deliberately avoids the concept of martial law:

**Verteidigungsfall (Defense Emergency, Art. 115a-115l GG):** Declared when the federal territory is under armed attack or imminent threat. Requires a two-thirds majority of the Bundestag with a majority of its members, plus Bundesrat consent. The Gemeinsamer Ausschuss (Joint Committee) -- a 48-member body of Bundestag and Bundesrat members -- may assume legislative functions if the Bundestag cannot convene.

**Spannungsfall (State of Tension, Art. 80a GG):** A pre-defense emergency state that allows certain preparatory measures. Declared by a two-thirds Bundestag majority.

**Innerer Notstand (Internal Emergency, Art. 91 GG):** Permits federal intervention in states (Länder) to avert danger to the democratic order. The Federal Government may deploy the Federal Border Police and may direct state police forces. Military deployment is permitted only for protection of civilian objects and organized insurgency (Art. 87a(4) GG) and only as a last resort.

### Constitutional Safeguards

| Safeguard | Provision |
|-----------|-----------|
| Civilian court continuity | Bundesverfassungsgericht and all courts remain operational throughout any emergency |
| Grundgesetz inviolability | Article 79(3) -- the "eternity clause" -- protects human dignity (Art. 1), federal structure (Art. 20), and democratic principles from amendment even during emergencies |
| Legislative control | Bundestag or Gemeinsamer Ausschuss retains legislative authority; no transfer to executive |
| Automatic termination | Verteidigungsfall terminates automatically when conditions cease; Bundestag may declare termination at any time |
| Military subordination | Armed forces remain under civilian ministerial control; no military commander exercises governmental authority |

### Historical Non-Invocation

Germany has never invoked any provision of the Notstandsgesetze since their enactment in 1968. Even during significant security challenges -- including the RAF terrorism of the 1970s (Deutscher Herbst), reunification in 1990, the COVID-19 pandemic, and the security crisis following Russia's invasion of Ukraine in 2022 -- the federal government relied on ordinary legislative and executive powers rather than emergency provisions.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Article 15 -- Derogation

Article 15 of the European Convention on Human Rights permits states to derogate from Convention obligations "in time of war or other public emergency threatening the life of the nation," subject to strict conditions:

- Measures must be "strictly required by the exigencies of the situation"
- Measures must not be inconsistent with other obligations under international law
- No derogation is permitted from Articles 2 (right to life, except lawful acts of war), 3 (prohibition of torture), 4(1) (prohibition of slavery), and 7 (no punishment without law)
- The state must inform the Secretary General of the Council of Europe of measures taken and reasons

### Key ECHR Jurisprudence

**Lawless v. Ireland (No. 3) (Application No. 332/57, Judgment of July 1, 1961):** The first case examining Article 15, the Court accepted Ireland's derogation during IRA-related security operations but established that the Court has competence to review whether a genuine emergency exists and whether measures are proportionate.

**Ireland v. United Kingdom (Application No. 5310/71, Judgment of January 18, 1978):** The Court reviewed the UK's derogation during the Northern Ireland troubles, finding that internment without trial fell within the margin of appreciation but that interrogation techniques constituted inhuman treatment under Article 3 (a non-derogable right).

**A. and Others v. United Kingdom (Application No. 3455/05, Grand Chamber Judgment of February 19, 2009):** The Grand Chamber found that the indefinite detention without trial of foreign terrorism suspects under the Anti-terrorism, Crime and Security Act 2001 was disproportionate and discriminatory, violating Articles 5 and 14 despite the UK's Article 15 derogation.

**Aksoy v. Turkey (Application No. 21987/93, Judgment of December 18, 1996):** The Court found that Turkey's 14-day detention without judicial control during martial law in southeastern Turkey exceeded what was "strictly required," violating Article 5 even taking into account the derogation.

### Venice Commission Guidelines

The European Commission for Democracy through Law (Venice Commission) has issued extensive guidance on emergency powers:

**CDL-AD(2020)014 -- Emergency Powers (2020):** Issued in response to COVID-19 emergency measures, the report reaffirms that:
- States of emergency must be limited in time and scope
- Parliamentary oversight must be maintained
- Judicial review must remain available
- Human rights restrictions must be proportionate and non-discriminatory
- Media freedom and access to information must be preserved

### EU Framework

The EU has no equivalent of Article 15 ECHR, but Article 347 TFEU permits member states to take measures they consider necessary for internal or external security in the event of serious internal disturbances, war, or the threat of war. The provision has never been formally invoked. Article 4(2) TEU recognizes member states' exclusive competence regarding national security.

### COVID-19 and Emergency Powers (Comparative Note)

The COVID-19 pandemic (2020-2023) tested emergency powers frameworks across all jurisdictions without triggering martial law:

**South Korea:** Responded through the Infectious Disease Control and Prevention Act (감염병의 예방 및 관리에 관한 법률) rather than martial law or extraordinary emergency measures. The government implemented extensive contact tracing, quarantine, and social distancing measures under existing public health legislation. The Constitutional Court reviewed several pandemic restrictions and upheld most as proportionate.

**United States:** No declaration of martial law occurred, though the National Emergency declared under the National Emergencies Act (50 U.S.C. Sections 1601-1651) activated emergency statutory authorities. States exercised police power through governor executive orders. The Supreme Court addressed pandemic-related restrictions on religious exercise in Roman Catholic Diocese of Brooklyn v. Cuomo (2020).

**Germany:** The Bundestag authorized the Infektionsschutzgesetz (Infection Protection Act) amendments through ordinary legislative process, explicitly avoiding invocation of the Notstandsgesetze. The Federal Constitutional Court reviewed pandemic restrictions and struck down several as disproportionate (BVerfG, 1 BvR 781/21, November 19, 2021 -- "Bundesnotbremse II").

**ECHR:** Multiple states notified derogations under Article 15 during the pandemic (Armenia, Georgia, Latvia, Moldova, Romania, San Marino, Albania, Estonia, North Macedonia, Serbia). The Venice Commission issued CDL-AD(2020)014 emphasizing the need for parliamentary oversight and judicial review of pandemic emergency measures.

### Curfew Powers

Curfews -- legally mandated restrictions on movement during specified hours -- represent a common element of martial law that may also be imposed under lesser emergency powers:

| Jurisdiction | Curfew Authority | Recent Use |
|-------------|-----------------|-----------|
| South Korea | Martial law (Art. 77); Infectious Disease Act | December 2024 (martial law, hours only); COVID-19 gathering restrictions |
| United States | Insurrection Act; state/local emergency powers | 2020 (civil unrest in multiple cities) |
| Germany | Infektionsschutzgesetz; Polizeigesetze | COVID-19 nighttime curfews (2021, later struck down) |
| ECHR | Permitted under Art. 15 derogation; proportionality required | Multiple states during COVID-19 |

## Comparative Analysis

### Constitutional Design

The four jurisdictions represent fundamentally different approaches to emergency constitutional design. Germany has the most elaborately structured system, with multiple graduated levels and extensive safeguards designed to prevent the historical abuse of emergency powers. South Korea provides express constitutional authority for martial law but subjects it to legislative and judicial checks. The United States relies on a combination of constitutional silences, statutory provisions, and judicial precedent rather than express emergency powers. The ECHR creates a supranational review mechanism applicable to all Council of Europe states.

### Military vs. Civilian Control

The central question in martial law is whether military authority displaces civilian governance. Germany's system categorically maintains civilian control -- military forces may be deployed but never exercise governmental authority. The U.S. Supreme Court in Milligan established that martial law cannot displace functioning civilian courts. South Korea's extraordinary martial law permits transfer of governmental authority to military commanders, a provision exercised repeatedly in the country's authoritarian period. The ECHR requires that any emergency measures remain subject to judicial oversight.

### Democratic Safeguards

South Korea's December 2024 experience demonstrated both the vulnerability and resilience of democratic safeguards. The National Assembly's ability to convene and demand the lifting of martial law within hours -- followed by impeachment proceedings -- validated the constitutional check mechanism. The contrast with the 1980 experience, when the National Assembly was dissolved and martial law was maintained for months, illustrates the maturation of South Korea's democratic institutions.
