## Summary

Sanctions law -- the legal framework governing the imposition of economic, trade, financial, and diplomatic restrictions on foreign states, entities, and individuals to achieve foreign policy and national security objectives -- has become one of the most dynamic and consequential areas of international economic regulation, with the number of sanctioned entities growing exponentially since the end of the Cold War and sanctions emerging as a primary tool of statecraft short of military force. South Korea implements UN Security Council sanctions through the Foreign Trade Act (대외무역법) and supplementary presidential decrees, and maintains unilateral sanctions primarily targeting North Korea (조선민주주의인민공화국) under the Inter-Korean Exchange and Cooperation Act (남북교류협력에 관한 법률) and the Act on Prohibition of Financing for Proliferation of Weapons of Mass Destruction (대량살상무기 확산을 위한 자금조달행위의 금지에 관한 법률); South Korea's sanctions framework is uniquely shaped by its relationship with North Korea, with violations carrying penalties of up to 7 years imprisonment under the Foreign Trade Act. The United States operates the world's most extensive and extraterritorially reaching sanctions regime, administered primarily by the Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC) within the Department of the Treasury under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA, 50 U.S.C. Section 1701 et seq.) and the Trading with the Enemy Act (TWEA, 50 U.S.C. Section 4301 et seq.); as of 2025, the United States maintains over 30 distinct sanctions programs targeting countries including Russia, Iran, North Korea, Cuba, Syria, and Venezuela, as well as thematic programs addressing terrorism, narcotics, cyber threats, and human rights abuses, with criminal penalties of up to 20 years imprisonment and $1 million fines per violation. Germany implements sanctions through EU regulations that are directly applicable under EU law and through the Außenwirtschaftsgesetz (AWG, Foreign Trade Act) and the Außenwirtschaftsverordnung (AWV, Foreign Trade Ordinance); the Bundesamt für Wirtschaft und Ausfuhrkontrolle (BAFA) administers export controls while the Deutsche Bundesbank handles financial sanctions. At the EU level, sanctions are imposed through Council Decisions under Article 29 TEU and Regulations under Article 215 TFEU, with the comprehensive sanctions against Russia following the 2022 invasion of Ukraine representing the most extensive EU sanctions package in history, comprising over 14 successive packages affecting trade, finance, transportation, energy, and individuals; the European Court of Justice has developed significant jurisprudence on the fundamental rights limits of EU sanctions in cases including Kadi (2008, 2013), Bank Mellat (2016), and Rosneft (2017).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's sanctions framework is uniquely shaped by the Korean Peninsula's division and the ongoing legal state of armistice with North Korea.

**Foreign Trade Act (대외무역법):** This statute provides the general framework for implementing trade sanctions, including UN Security Council sanctions. Article 53 authorizes the Minister of Trade, Industry and Energy to restrict or prohibit trade with specific countries or entities when necessary for national security, the fulfillment of international obligations, or the maintenance of international peace. Violations of trade restrictions carry penalties of up to 7 years imprisonment or fines of up to twice the value of the restricted goods (Article 69).

**Inter-Korean Exchange and Cooperation Act (남북교류협력에 관한 법률):** This statute governs all economic, cultural, and humanitarian exchanges between South and North Korea. Under Article 9, any person wishing to engage in trade or cooperation with North Korea must obtain approval from the Unification Minister (통일부장관). Following North Korea's nuclear tests and missile launches, the government has progressively restricted inter-Korean economic activity, culminating in the comprehensive suspension of the Kaesong Industrial Complex (개성공단) in February 2016.

**Act on Prohibition of Financing for Proliferation of WMDs (대량살상무기 확산을 위한 자금조달행위의 금지에 관한 법률):** Enacted in 2017 to strengthen compliance with UNSC Resolution 1540 (2004) and successor resolutions, this Act criminalizes the financing of WMD proliferation with penalties of up to 10 years imprisonment or fines of up to KRW 100 million.

**Financial Services Commission Sanctions Enforcement:** The FSC administers financial sanctions, maintaining a list of designated individuals and entities consistent with UNSC designations. Korean financial institutions must screen all transactions against the sanctions list and report suspicious transactions to the Korea Financial Intelligence Unit (KoFIU).

### North Korea Sanctions

South Korea's sanctions against North Korea operate in coordination with UNSC sanctions (Resolutions 1718, 1874, 2087, 2094, 2270, 2321, 2356, 2371, 2375, and 2397) and bilateral measures. The May 24 Measures (5.24 조치), imposed following North Korea's sinking of the ROKS Cheonan in 2010, prohibit inter-Korean trade (except Kaesong operations, which were later suspended), investment in North Korea, humanitarian aid (except for children and pregnant women), and visits by South Korean nationals to North Korea.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States operates the world's most extensive sanctions regime, with primary legal authority vested in the President through emergency powers statutes.

**International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA, 50 U.S.C. Section 1701 et seq.):** IEEPA authorizes the President, upon declaring a national emergency, to regulate or prohibit economic transactions with foreign countries, entities, or individuals. IEEPA is the legal basis for most U.S. sanctions programs. In Dames & Moore v. Regan, 453 U.S. 654 (1981), the Supreme Court upheld the President's authority to nullify attachments on Iranian government assets and to suspend claims against Iran pursuant to the Algiers Accords.

**Trading with the Enemy Act (TWEA, 50 U.S.C. Section 4301 et seq.):** Originally enacted in 1917, TWEA provides authority for sanctions during declared wars and national emergencies. The Cuba embargo is the only current TWEA-based program.

**Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC):** OFAC administers and enforces U.S. economic sanctions programs. OFAC maintains the Specially Designated Nationals and Blocked Persons List (SDN List), containing approximately 12,000+ entries as of 2025. U.S. persons are generally prohibited from engaging in transactions with SDN-listed parties, and all SDN property within U.S. jurisdiction must be blocked.

**Penalties:**
| Type | Maximum Penalty |
|------|----------------|
| Criminal (IEEPA) | Up to 20 years imprisonment and $1,000,000 per violation |
| Civil (IEEPA) | Up to $368,136 per violation (2025 inflation adjustment) |
| Criminal (TWEA) | Up to 10 years imprisonment and $1,000,000 per violation |

**Secondary Sanctions:** U.S. secondary (extraterritorial) sanctions impose penalties on non-U.S. persons who engage in significant transactions with sanctioned countries or entities, even when no U.S. nexus exists. Major secondary sanctions programs include: CAATSA (Countering America's Adversaries Through Sanctions Act, 2017) targeting Russia, Iran, and North Korea; CISADA (Comprehensive Iran Sanctions, Accountability, and Divestment Act, 2010); and various executive orders targeting Russian energy, technology, and financial sectors following the 2022 invasion of Ukraine.

### Russia Sanctions (Post-2022)

Following Russia's invasion of Ukraine in February 2022, the United States imposed unprecedented sanctions including: blocking of the Central Bank of Russia's assets; full blocking sanctions on major Russian banks including Sberbank, VTB, and Gazprombank; export controls on advanced technology under the Export Control Reform Act (ECRA); price cap on Russian oil (EO 14071); and designation of thousands of Russian individuals and entities. Total Russian assets frozen by the G7 exceed $300 billion.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany implements sanctions primarily through directly applicable EU regulations, supplemented by national legislation for enforcement and additional measures.

**Außenwirtschaftsgesetz (AWG) -- Foreign Trade Act:** The AWG provides the legal framework for German foreign trade regulation, including sanctions implementation. Section 4 authorizes the Federal Government to restrict foreign trade transactions for national security, foreign policy, or international obligation reasons.

**Section 18 AWG -- Criminal Penalties:** Violations of sanctions-related trade restrictions carry penalties of imprisonment from 3 months to 15 years for the most serious offenses (Section 18(1) AWG: export of weapons or dual-use goods to embargoed destinations). Standard sanctions violations carry penalties of up to 10 years (Section 18(2) AWG). Administrative offenses are punishable by fines of up to EUR 500,000 (Section 19 AWG).

**Außenwirtschaftsverordnung (AWV) -- Foreign Trade Ordinance:** The AWV implements detailed requirements including reporting obligations, licensing requirements, and specific prohibitions corresponding to EU and national sanctions.

**Bundesamt für Wirtschaft und Ausfuhrkontrolle (BAFA):** BAFA administers export controls and trade-related sanctions, processing export license applications and conducting compliance monitoring. The Deutsche Bundesbank administers financial sanctions, including asset freezing and transaction screening requirements.

### Russia Sanctions Implementation

Germany faced unique challenges in implementing Russia sanctions given its significant economic ties with Russia, particularly in the energy sector. The suspension of the Nord Stream 2 pipeline certification in February 2022 was one of the most significant German sanctions actions. German companies faced complex compliance challenges in unwinding longstanding business relationships with Russian counterparts while complying with the successive EU sanctions packages.

The Zentralstelle für Sanktionsdurchsetzung (Central Office for Sanctions Enforcement), established in 2022 within the General Customs Directorate (Generalzolldirektion), was created specifically to improve sanctions enforcement, including asset identification and freezing.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Sanctions Framework

**Legal Basis:** EU restrictive measures are adopted through a two-step process: (1) a Council Decision under Article 29 TEU establishing the political framework, and (2) a Council Regulation under Article 215 TFEU giving binding legal effect to the measures. The European Commission implements and monitors compliance.

**Russia Sanctions Packages (2022-2025):** The EU has adopted over 14 sanctions packages against Russia, constituting the most comprehensive sanctions regime in EU history:

| Package | Date | Key Measures |
|---------|------|-------------|
| 1st | February 23, 2022 | Targeted designations; diplomatic measures |
| 4th | March 15, 2022 | Ban on luxury goods exports; steel imports ban |
| 6th | June 3, 2022 | Crude oil import ban (with transition); SWIFT disconnection of Sberbank |
| 8th | October 6, 2022 | Oil price cap mechanism; G7 alignment |
| 12th | December 18, 2023 | Diamond import ban; anti-circumvention measures |
| 14th | June 24, 2024 | LNG restrictions; enhanced circumvention prevention |

### CJEU Sanctions Jurisprudence

**Kadi v. Council (Joined Cases C-402/05 P and C-415/05 P, Grand Chamber Judgment of September 3, 2008):** The Grand Chamber annulled EU regulations implementing UNSC sanctions against individuals suspected of terrorist links, holding that EU courts have jurisdiction to review the lawfulness of EU measures implementing UNSC resolutions, and that fundamental rights (right to be heard, right to effective judicial review, right to property) constitute the "very foundations of the Community legal order" that cannot be overridden by international obligations. This landmark judgment established that even UNSC-mandated sanctions must comply with EU fundamental rights standards.

**Kadi II (Joined Cases C-584/10 P, C-593/10 P, and C-595/10 P, Grand Chamber Judgment of July 18, 2013):** The Grand Chamber reaffirmed and strengthened the Kadi I principles, requiring that the EU authority disclose the reasons for designation to the listed person, provide an opportunity to be heard, and ensure that the reasons are supported by sufficient evidence subject to judicial review.

**Bank Mellat v. Council (Case C-176/13 P, Judgment of November 18, 2016):** The Court annulled sanctions against an Iranian bank, finding insufficient evidence to justify the designation and requiring the Council to demonstrate specific and concrete reasons for listing.

**Rosneft v. Council (Case C-72/15, Grand Chamber Judgment of March 28, 2017):** The Grand Chamber upheld EU sectoral sanctions against Russia's oil sector, holding that the Council has broad discretion in foreign policy matters and that judicial review is limited to verifying compliance with procedural requirements, the accuracy of facts, and the absence of manifest error of assessment.

### European Court of Human Rights

**Al-Dulimi and Montana Management Inc. v. Switzerland (Application No. 5809/08, Grand Chamber Judgment of June 21, 2016):** The Grand Chamber addressed the relationship between UNSC sanctions and ECHR rights, holding that states implementing UNSC sanctions must provide a minimum level of judicial scrutiny to ensure that sanctions application is not arbitrary. The judgment balanced the obligation to implement UNSC resolutions with the ECHR's requirement of access to court (Article 6) and protection of property (Article 1 Protocol 1).

**Nada v. Switzerland (Application No. 10593/08, Grand Chamber Judgment of September 12, 2012):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where Switzerland imposed travel restrictions implementing UNSC sanctions without providing adequate procedural safeguards, holding that the limited humanitarian exception available was insufficient to protect the applicant's private and family life.

## Comparative Analysis

### Extraterritorial Reach

The most significant divergence concerns extraterritorial application. U.S. secondary sanctions uniquely attempt to bind non-U.S. persons to U.S. sanctions policy, creating significant tensions with the EU (which enacted the Blocking Statute in response), South Korea, and other trading partners. The EU's sanctions framework is limited to persons and entities within EU jurisdiction. South Korea's sanctions lack significant extraterritorial reach.

### Due Process Protections

The CJEU's Kadi doctrine provides the strongest judicial review protections for sanctioned persons, requiring disclosure of evidence, opportunity to be heard, and substantive judicial review. U.S. OFAC administrative proceedings provide more limited procedural protections, though federal court review is available. South Korea's administrative review process provides basic procedural safeguards.

### Enforcement Effectiveness

The United States leads in sanctions enforcement, with OFAC processing approximately 200-300 enforcement actions annually and imposing significant financial penalties -- the largest individual settlement being BNP Paribas's $8.97 billion penalty in 2014 for sanctions violations involving Sudan, Cuba, and Iran. EU enforcement varies substantially by member state, prompting the establishment of the EU position of Sanctions Envoy (2022) and national enforcement bodies like Germany's Zentralstelle. South Korea's enforcement is primarily focused on North Korea sanctions compliance, with the Korea Financial Intelligence Unit (KoFIU) monitoring financial transactions for sanctions evasion.

### Humanitarian Exemptions

All four jurisdictions provide mechanisms for humanitarian exemptions to sanctions, but implementation varies. The United States issues general licenses (e.g., General License 4 for humanitarian transactions with Afghanistan) and specific licenses through OFAC. The EU includes humanitarian exemptions in its sanctions regulations, though complexity in application has drawn criticism from humanitarian organizations. South Korea's Inter-Korean Exchange Act permits limited humanitarian aid deliveries even during comprehensive sanctions, particularly for children and pregnant women. The tension between sanctions enforcement and humanitarian access remains one of the most challenging aspects of sanctions policy across all jurisdictions.

### Sanctions Evasion and Circumvention

The proliferation of sanctions has driven sophisticated evasion techniques including ship-to-ship transfers of sanctioned commodities, use of shell companies and front organizations, cryptocurrency transactions to bypass financial system controls, and exploitation of jurisdictions with weak enforcement. All four jurisdictions have strengthened anti-circumvention measures: the United States through enhanced OFAC enforcement and the creation of the KleptoCapture Task Force (2022), the EU through progressive tightening of circumvention provisions in successive Russia sanctions packages, and Germany through the establishment of the Zentralstelle für Sanktionsdurchsetzung with dedicated investigative capabilities.

### Impact Assessment and Effectiveness

The effectiveness of sanctions in achieving their stated policy objectives remains contested. Academic research suggests that comprehensive sanctions succeed in changing target state behavior in approximately 30-40 percent of cases. The Russia sanctions have imposed significant economic costs (estimated 5-10 percent GDP reduction) but have not achieved the stated objective of ending the war in Ukraine. North Korea sanctions have constrained but not eliminated the country's nuclear weapons program. These mixed outcomes have prompted growing interest in "smart sanctions" or "targeted sanctions" that focus on individuals and specific sectors rather than comprehensive economic isolation, an approach reflected in the evolution of both U.S. and EU sanctions practice.

### Legal Challenges and Rule of Law

Sanctions increasingly face legal challenges on rule of law grounds. The CJEU's Kadi doctrine established that even UNSC-mandated sanctions must respect EU fundamental rights. The ECHR's Nada and Al-Dulimi judgments impose minimum procedural requirements. In the United States, the Supreme Court has not directly addressed the constitutionality of OFAC's administrative designation process, though due process challenges have been raised in lower courts. These legal challenges reflect the fundamental tension between the executive's foreign policy prerogatives and the individual's right to due process and judicial review.
