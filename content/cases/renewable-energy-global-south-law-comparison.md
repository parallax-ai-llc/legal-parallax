## Summary

Renewable energy law in the Global South encompasses a diverse array of legislative frameworks governing the transition from fossil fuels to clean energy sources, reflecting each region's unique resource endowments, institutional capacities, and development priorities. China has achieved global solar manufacturing dominance, producing over 80% of the world's solar panels, underpinned by the Renewable Energy Law (可再生能源法, 2005, amended 2009), the 14th Five-Year Plan for Renewable Energy Development (2022), and a feed-in tariff system that has been progressively replaced by competitive auction mechanisms. India's National Solar Mission (Jawaharlal Nehru National Solar Mission, launched 2010) has driven installed solar capacity from 10 MW in 2010 to over 73 GW by December 2024, supported by the Electricity Act 2003, the National Tariff Policy 2016, and production-linked incentive schemes for domestic solar manufacturing. South Africa's Renewable Energy Independent Power Producer Procurement Programme (REIPPPP), launched in 2011, represents a globally recognized competitive bidding model that has attracted over ZAR 210 billion (approximately USD 12 billion) in private investment across seven bid windows. Brazil combines the world's second-largest hydroelectric capacity with a pioneering ethanol mandate under the ProÁlcool program (1975) and rapid wind energy growth under the PROINFA (Programa de Incentivo às Fontes Alternativas de Energia Elétrica, Lei 10.438/2002) and auction system. The Philippines' Renewable Energy Act (Republic Act No. 9513, 2008) established one of Asia's first comprehensive RE legal frameworks with fiscal incentives, a feed-in tariff, and a Renewable Portfolio Standard.

## China (中国)

### Legal Framework

China's renewable energy legal framework has propelled the country to install more renewable energy capacity than the rest of the world combined, through a characteristic combination of top-down planning, industrial policy, and market mechanisms:

- **Renewable Energy Law (中华人民共和国可再生能源法, 2005, amended 2009):** The foundational statute establishing: mandatory grid connection for renewable energy (Art. 14, requiring grid companies to purchase all RE generation within their coverage area); a national renewable energy development plan aligned with five-year plans (Art. 7); a feed-in tariff system set by the State Council pricing authority (Art. 19); and a renewable energy development fund financed through electricity surcharges (Art. 24). The 2009 amendment strengthened grid connection obligations and introduced penalty provisions for grid companies refusing RE connections.
- **14th Five-Year Plan for Renewable Energy Development (十四五可再生能源发展规划, 2022):** Targets renewable energy generating 33% of total electricity consumption by 2025 (up from 28.8% in 2020), with total RE installed capacity reaching 860 GW by 2025. Sets provincial RE consumption quotas and mandates large-scale wind-solar base construction in the Gobi Desert, the Kubuqi Desert, and other arid regions.
- **Notice on Matters Related to the Construction of Wind and Solar Power Generation in 2021 (风电光伏发电建设有关事项的通知):** Marked the transition from feed-in tariffs to grid parity, requiring all new onshore wind and centralized solar projects to achieve parity with coal-fired generation. Offshore wind FIT was eliminated for new projects from 2022.
- **"Desert, Gobi, and Wasteland" Large-Scale Wind-Solar Bases (沙戈荒大型风电光伏基地):** The first phase (97 GW) was approved in 2022-2023, with a second phase (455 GW) announced. These bases combine wind and solar generation with ultra-high-voltage (UHV) DC transmission lines to deliver power from western resource-rich regions to eastern demand centers.
- **Green Electricity Certificate (绿色电力证书, GEC) Trading:** Expanded under the 2023 Notice on Doing a Good Job on Green Electricity Certificates, making GECs the sole proof of renewable electricity consumption and enabling corporate RE procurement.

### Key Principles

China's RE governance embodies the "whole-nation system" (举国体制) approach, combining state-directed industrial policy with market price signals. The "new type power system" (新型电力系统) framework, proposed by President Xi in March 2021, envisions a power system centered on renewable energy with coal transitioning to a peaking and balancing role. The "dual carbon" goals provide the overarching policy mandate, with RE expansion as the primary tool for decarbonizing the power sector.

Provincial RE consumption quotas (可再生能源消纳责任权重), managed by the National Energy Administration (NEA), assign minimum RE consumption shares to each province, with consequences for provincial energy administrators who fail to meet targets. This addresses the historical problem of RE curtailment, which peaked at 17% for wind in 2016 before declining to approximately 3% by 2023.

### Notable Developments

- **Solar Manufacturing Dominance:** China controls over 80% of global solar PV manufacturing capacity across the entire supply chain (polysilicon, ingots, wafers, cells, modules). Module prices declined from over USD 4/W in 2008 to approximately USD 0.10/W by late 2024, driven by Chinese manufacturing scale and competition.
- **World's Largest Renewable Energy Project:** The Kubuqi Desert Phase I wind-solar-hydrogen project in Inner Mongolia (16 GW, approved 2023) exemplifies the desert base strategy.
- **EV-Solar Integration:** Government policies promoting vehicle-to-grid (V2G) integration and distributed solar on EV charging infrastructure, supported by the 2023 "Opinions on Further Strengthening New Energy Vehicle and Charging Infrastructure Construction."

## India (भारत)

### Legal Framework

India's renewable energy framework operates through a complex multi-level governance structure involving central and state legislation, regulatory commissions, and executive policy:

- **Electricity Act, 2003:** The foundational energy sector statute, which: delicensed electricity generation (Section 7), enabling any person to establish a generating station without a license (except hydroelectric stations exceeding a state-determined threshold); established State Electricity Regulatory Commissions (SERCs) with power to determine tariffs (Section 62) and promote renewable energy through preferential tariffs (Section 86(1)(e)); and mandated Renewable Purchase Obligations (RPOs) requiring distribution licensees to purchase a minimum percentage of electricity from RE sources (Section 86(1)(e)).
- **Jawaharlal Nehru National Solar Mission (JNNSM, 2010):** Launched as part of the NAPCC, targeting 20 GW solar by 2022, subsequently revised to 100 GW by 2022 and incorporated into the broader 500 GW non-fossil capacity target by 2030. The mission operates through the Solar Energy Corporation of India (SECI) as the primary implementing agency for central-level solar and wind auctions.
- **National Tariff Policy, 2016 (and 2023 draft amendment):** Mandates that SERCs fix minimum RPO percentages, with a trajectory to 43% RE share in total electricity consumption by FY 2029-30 (including 2.82% for hydroelectric purchase obligations). The Policy prohibits retroactive tariff reductions and provides for "must-run" status for RE generators.
- **Production-Linked Incentive (PLI) Scheme for Solar PV Manufacturing:** Launched in 2022 with an outlay of INR 19,500 crore (USD 2.4 billion) to promote domestic manufacturing of high-efficiency solar modules and reduce dependence on Chinese imports. By 2024, approximately 39 GW of domestic solar cell and module manufacturing capacity was awarded under the scheme.
- **Green Energy Open Access Rules, 2022:** Simplified the procedure for consumers with a connected load of 100 kW or above to procure RE through open access, reducing the minimum threshold from 1 MW and capping cross-subsidy surcharges.
- **Offshore Wind Energy Policy (2015) and Offshore Wind Lease Rules (2023):** Target 30 GW of offshore wind by 2030, with the first auction for 4 GW off the coast of Tamil Nadu and Gujarat conducted by SECI in 2023-2024.

### Key Principles

The principle of "energy justice" underpins India's RE transition, balancing decarbonization with energy access for approximately 240 million people who still lack reliable electricity supply. The Electricity (Amendment) Bill 2022 (lapsed) proposed mandatory RE procurement and distribution sub-licensing to improve last-mile delivery.

RPOs are the primary regulatory mechanism for RE demand creation. Non-compliance by DISCOMs has been a persistent problem, addressed through RPO compliance monitoring by the Central Electricity Regulatory Commission (CERC) and state ERCs. The 2023 amendments strengthened RPO enforcement with penalties for non-compliance.

### Notable Cases

- **SECI Tariff Records:** India's solar auction tariffs have reached record lows -- INR 1.99/kWh (approximately USD 0.024/kWh) in the SECI Rajasthan auction (December 2020) and INR 2.00/kWh in subsequent auctions. Wind tariffs similarly declined to INR 2.43/kWh. However, tariff floor concerns have emerged with the 2023 adoption of a "ceiling tariff" system.
- **State-Level Tariff Disputes:** In Gujarat Urja Vikas Nigam Ltd. v. Solar Semiconductor Power Co. (India) Pvt. Ltd. (2023), the Supreme Court addressed the enforceability of solar PPAs where states sought to renegotiate tariffs after commissioning, affirming the sanctity of contracts and RPO obligations.
- **Adani Green Energy -- World's Largest Solar Park:** The Khavda Renewable Energy Park in Gujarat's Kutch district (30 GW planned capacity, 12,000 acres) exemplifies India's ultra-mega solar park model. Adani Green Energy's 20 GW target by 2025 makes it the world's largest solar developer.
- **PM-KUSUM Scheme (2019):** Targeting solarization of 3.5 million agricultural pumps and 10 GW of solar capacity on agricultural land, addressing the rural energy-agriculture nexus.

## Africa (Nigeria and South Africa)

### South Africa

#### Legal Framework

South Africa's energy transition is dominated by the crisis of Eskom, the state-owned utility that provides over 90% of the country's electricity and has been subject to severe load-shedding (rolling blackouts) since 2007:

- **Integrated Resource Plan (IRP) 2019:** South Africa's electricity capacity expansion plan, allocating 17,742 MW of new renewable energy by 2030 (8,288 MW wind, 8,288 MW solar PV, 1,166 MW CSP) alongside 1,500 MW of new coal and 2,500 MW of gas. The IRP was criticized by environmental groups for including new coal capacity and by industry for failing to account for declining RE costs.
- **REIPPPP (launched 2011):** A competitive bidding program managed by the Department of Mineral Resources and Energy (DMRE) and the Independent Power Producer Office (IPPO). Over seven bid windows (BW1-BW7), the REIPPPP has allocated over 12,000 MW of RE capacity, with tariffs declining from ZAR 2.76/kWh (BW1 onshore wind) to ZAR 0.49/kWh (BW5), a reduction of over 82%. Total investment exceeds ZAR 210 billion. The program has been recognized by the World Bank, IRENA, and the G20 as a best-practice procurement model.
- **Electricity Regulation Amendment Act (2024):** Amended the Electricity Regulation Act to raise the licensing exemption threshold for embedded generation from 1 MW to 100 MW, allowing commercial and industrial consumers to install large-scale solar without NERSA (National Energy Regulator of South Africa) licensing, and to establish a competitive electricity market operator.
- **Just Energy Transition Partnership (JETP, 2021):** A USD 8.5 billion financing package from France, Germany, UK, US, and EU to support South Africa's transition from coal, announced at COP26. The JETP Investment Plan covers RE deployment, green hydrogen, EV manufacturing, and coal community transition.

#### Key Challenges

Eskom's financial and operational crisis (total debt of approximately ZAR 400 billion; 335 days of load-shedding in 2023) has been the primary driver of private RE adoption, as businesses and households install rooftop solar to mitigate blackouts. The unbundling of Eskom into three entities (generation, transmission, distribution) under the Electricity Regulation Amendment Act is intended to enable a competitive electricity market.

### Nigeria

#### Legal Framework

Nigeria's RE legal framework is nascent, reflecting a country where over 90 million people lack grid electricity access:

- **Electric Power Sector Reform Act 2005 (EPSRA):** Unbundled the National Electric Power Authority (NEPA) into generation, transmission, and distribution companies, creating the Nigerian Electricity Regulatory Commission (NERC) as the sector regulator.
- **Rural Electrification Agency (REA):** Established under the EPSRA to promote rural electrification, increasingly through off-grid solar and mini-grid solutions.
- **NERC Mini-Grid Regulation 2016 (amended 2023):** Regulates the development of isolated and interconnected mini-grids, providing permit categories, tariff frameworks, and technical standards. Nigeria has approximately 250 operational mini-grids by 2024, with the Federal Government targeting 10,000 by 2030.
- **Nigerian Energy Transition Plan (ETP, 2022):** A USD 410 billion pathway to carbon neutrality by 2060, targeting 250 GW of RE capacity and universal energy access by 2030. The plan relies heavily on international climate finance.

## Latin America (Brazil, Mexico, and Colombia)

### Legal Framework

Latin America is the world's most renewable electricity region (over 60% of generation from renewable sources, dominated by hydroelectric), with each major country pursuing distinct RE legal strategies:

- **Brazil -- PROINFA (Lei 10.438/2002):** Brazil's first RE incentive program, contracting 3,300 MW of wind, small hydro, and biomass. Superseded by the auction system.
- **Brazil -- Regulated Electricity Auctions:** Managed by ANEEL (Agência Nacional de Energia Elétrica), auctions allocate long-term PPAs (15-30 years) for new generation capacity. Wind energy prices declined from BRL 305/MWh in the 2005 PROINFA auction to BRL 95/MWh in 2023 auctions. Solar declined from BRL 215/MWh (2014) to BRL 67/MWh (2023).
- **Brazil -- Ethanol Mandate (ProÁlcool, 1975; RenovaBio, Lei 13.576/2017):** Brazil pioneered biofuel mandates with the ProÁlcool program requiring ethanol blending in gasoline (currently 27.5% anhydrous ethanol blend, E27). RenovaBio establishes decarbonization credits (CBIOs) for biofuel producers, creating a market-based incentive for low-carbon fuels. Brazil has approximately 360 ethanol mills processing sugarcane and corn.
- **Brazil -- Net Metering (RN 482/2012, revised by Lei 14.300/2022):** Established net metering for distributed generation up to 5 MW. The 2022 revision introduced a transition period (2023-2028) for wire charges, addressing utility concerns about cross-subsidization. By 2024, Brazil had over 2.5 million distributed generation connections totaling approximately 28 GW.
- **Mexico -- Energy Transition Law (Ley de Transición Energética, LTE, 2015):** Established Clean Energy Certificates (CELs) and a 35% clean energy target by 2024. The LTE was undermined by the López Obrador administration (2018-2024), which prioritized CFE (the state utility) and suspended clean energy auctions, leading to constitutional challenges.
- **Mexico -- 2021 Electricity Industry Law Reform:** AMLO's reform prioritized CFE dispatch over private RE generators, violating existing PPAs and the LTE. The Supreme Court of Justice of the Nation (SCJN) struck down several provisions as unconstitutional in 2022, but the conflict between the executive and judiciary over energy policy persisted until the 2024 constitutional energy reform.
- **Colombia -- Law 1715/2014:** Established the legal framework for non-conventional renewable energy sources (FNCER), providing tax incentives (50% income tax deduction for RE investments), VAT exemptions, accelerated depreciation, and customs duty exemptions. The law has driven a boom in solar and wind project development, particularly in La Guajira (wind) and the Caribbean coast (solar).

### Notable Developments

- **Brazil -- Largest Wind Capacity in Latin America:** Brazil's installed wind capacity exceeded 30 GW by 2024, concentrated in the Northeast region (Rio Grande do Norte, Bahia, Piauí, Ceará), making it the sixth-largest wind market globally.
- **Chile-Colombia Green Hydrogen Corridor:** While Chile is outside the scope of this analysis, the emerging green hydrogen framework in Colombia (Law 2099/2021 on Energy Transition, providing hydrogen as a fuel of public interest) reflects broader Latin American clean energy ambitions.
- **Mexico -- SCJN Constitutional Challenge (2022):** The Supreme Court declared unconstitutional multiple provisions of the 2021 electricity reform that prioritized CFE over private generators, affirming the constitutional basis for energy market competition and RE deployment.

## Southeast Asia (Indonesia and Philippines)

### Legal Framework

- **Philippines -- Renewable Energy Act (RA 9513, 2008):** One of Asia's first comprehensive RE laws, establishing: a feed-in tariff (FIT) system managed by the National Renewable Energy Board (NREB), with rates set by the Energy Regulatory Commission (ERC); fiscal incentives including income tax holiday (7 years), duty-free importation of RE equipment, zero-rated VAT, and net metering for distributed generation up to 100 kW; Renewable Portfolio Standards (RPS) requiring distribution utilities and retail electricity suppliers to source a minimum percentage of power from RE; and the Green Energy Auction Program (GEAP) introduced in 2022 to complement the FIT system with competitive procurement.
- **Philippines -- Green Energy Option Program (GEOP, DOE DC2018-01-0001):** Allows end-users with at least 100 kW demand to source electricity directly from RE generators through retail competition and the Wholesale Electricity Spot Market (WESM).
- **Philippines -- Executive Order No. 30 (2017):** Increased the biofuel blend mandates to 20% bioethanol in gasoline (E20) and 5% biodiesel (B5), supporting the coconut and sugarcane industries.
- **Indonesia -- Presidential Regulation No. 112/2022 on Acceleration of Renewable Energy Development:** Replaced the controversial MEMR Regulation 4/2020 that had capped RE purchase prices at 85% of local generation cost (BPP), effectively making most RE projects uneconomic. PR 112/2022 introduced differentiated pricing: RE purchases at 100% of BPP in areas where BPP exceeds the national average, and at 85% of BPP plus a "green premium" negotiated between PLN and the developer in other areas. Business-to-business (B2B) mechanisms allow direct corporate RE procurement.
- **Indonesia -- PLN RUPTL (Electricity Supply Business Plan) 2021-2030:** The state utility PLN's 10-year plan allocates 20.9 GW of new RE capacity (40.6% of total new capacity), including 4.7 GW solar, 2.4 GW wind, 5.8 GW hydro, and 3.3 GW geothermal. However, the RUPTL also includes 13.8 GW of new coal capacity, reflecting Indonesia's coal lock-in.
- **Indonesia -- Asian Development Bank Energy Transition Mechanism (ETM):** A pioneering mechanism for early retirement of coal plants, with the Cirebon-1 coal plant in West Java selected as the pilot (managed transition from 2024, early retirement by 2035 instead of original 2047 end date).
- **Indonesia -- Geothermal Energy:** Indonesia possesses the world's largest geothermal reserves (~29 GW potential) and is the second-largest geothermal power producer after the US (~2.4 GW installed). Law No. 21/2014 on Geothermal Energy removed geothermal from the mining classification, reducing permitting barriers.

### Key Principles

The Philippines' RE Act is notable for its "RE Registrant" system, which creates a regulatory pathway for market participants to develop, own, and operate RE facilities. The Act's fiscal incentive package is among the most comprehensive in ASEAN, though the FIT's limited capacity allocations (feed-in tariff allowance, or FIT-All fund surcharge on consumers) have constrained deployment.

Indonesia's energy governance is dominated by PLN (Perusahaan Listrik Negara), the state-owned monopoly utility that controls the entire electricity value chain under the Electricity Law (Law 30/2009). PLN's role as sole buyer creates structural barriers to RE deployment, as PLN's coal-heavy generation fleet and long-term coal PPAs create economic disincentives for RE procurement.

### Notable Developments

- **Philippines -- Green Energy Auction Program Round 1 (2022):** The DOE auctioned 2,000 MW of RE capacity (solar, wind, biomass, run-of-river hydro), with solar winning the majority at record-low FIT-equivalent prices of PHP 3.3718/kWh (approximately USD 0.06/kWh). Round 2 (2023) auctioned an additional 3,500 MW.
- **Indonesia -- First Utility-Scale Floating Solar:** The Cirata Floating Solar project (145 MW) in West Java, developed by Masdar (UAE) and PLN NP, became Southeast Asia's largest floating solar plant upon commissioning in 2023.
- **Philippines -- RE Share:** RE reached approximately 22% of the Philippines' electricity generation mix by 2024, below the 35% target for 2030, driven by geothermal (10.7%), hydro (8.2%), solar (2.4%), and wind (1.2%).

## Comparative Analysis

### Procurement Models

The five regions have deployed distinct RE procurement models: (1) **State-planned capacity allocation** (China), where the NEA assigns provincial quotas and coordinates massive infrastructure projects; (2) **Competitive auctions** (Brazil, South Africa, India), where market competition has driven dramatic tariff reductions; (3) **Feed-in tariffs transitioning to auctions** (Philippines, China), reflecting the maturation of RE markets; (4) **Single-buyer utility model** (Indonesia, Nigeria), where state utility monopolies control procurement; and (5) **Mixed/contested models** (Mexico), where political reversals have disrupted market signals.

### Manufacturing and Supply Chain

China's dominance of RE manufacturing supply chains has profound implications for the other four regions. India has responded with domestic content requirements, the PLI scheme, and tariffs on Chinese solar cells/modules (40% Basic Customs Duty on modules, 25% on cells from April 2024). Brazil imposes local content requirements in BNDES-financed wind projects. South Africa's REIPPPP includes economic development requirements (minimum 40% local content for wind towers). These industrial policy responses reflect the tension between rapid RE deployment (which favors cheap Chinese imports) and domestic economic development.

### Grid Integration

Grid integration challenges are universal but vary in character: China faces interprovincial transmission constraints and provincial protectionism; India faces DISCOM financial distress and transmission bottlenecks; South Africa faces Eskom's grid instability; Brazil faces long-distance transmission from northeastern wind resources to southeastern demand; Indonesia faces archipelagic grid fragmentation; and the Philippines faces island-by-island grid isolation requiring expensive energy storage or interconnection.

### Just Transition

The "just transition" concept -- ensuring that the shift from fossil fuels does not disproportionately harm workers and communities dependent on fossil fuel industries -- is most developed in South Africa (JETP), India (coal mine closure and alternative livelihood programs), and Colombia (Law 2099/2021 provisions for fossil fuel-dependent regions). China's approach is managed through state planning and reallocation of coal workers. In Nigeria and Indonesia, the just transition concept is nascent, complicated by oil and gas revenues that fund large portions of the national budget.
