## Summary

Military desertion -- the unauthorized abandonment of military service with the intent not to return, or the unauthorized absence from one's unit or post for a prolonged period -- has been treated as one of the gravest military offenses throughout history, reflecting the fundamental importance of military discipline, unit cohesion, and national defense. South Korea, which maintains compulsory military service for all male citizens under the Military Service Act (병역법), addresses desertion through the Military Criminal Act (군형법), Article 30, which provides for imprisonment of one to ten years for desertion during peacetime and the death penalty or life imprisonment for wartime desertion, with the Korean framework shaped by the existential security threat posed by North Korea and the continuing technical state of war on the Korean Peninsula since 1950. The United States criminalizes desertion under Article 85 of the Uniform Code of Military Justice (UCMJ, 10 U.S.C. Section 885), which provides for penalties up to death in time of war and dishonorable discharge with forfeiture of pay and up to five years' confinement in peacetime, though the death penalty for desertion has not been imposed since the execution of Private Eddie Slovik in 1945; the case of Sergeant Bowe Bergdahl (2017) remains the most prominent modern desertion prosecution. Germany addresses military absence offenses through the Wehrstrafgesetz (Military Criminal Code), Sections 15-16 (Eigenmächtige Abwesenheit and Fahnenflucht), with maximum penalties of five years' imprisonment for desertion during peacetime, though Germany suspended conscription in 2011 and maintains an all-volunteer Bundeswehr. At the European level, the ECHR has addressed military service obligations and conscientious objection under Article 4 (prohibition of forced labor) and Article 9 (freedom of thought, conscience, and religion), with the landmark Bayatyan v. Armenia decision (2011) establishing that the right of conscientious objection is protected under the Convention, while the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights recognizes the right to conscientious objection under Article 10(2).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's military desertion provisions must be understood in the context of the country's compulsory military service system and the ongoing military confrontation with North Korea.

**Military Criminal Act (군형법), Article 30 (탈영):**

### Penalty Structure

| Offense | Condition | Penalty |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Desertion (peacetime) | Unauthorized absence with intent not to return | 1-10 years imprisonment |
| Desertion (wartime/armed conflict) | Same during war or national emergency | Death, life imprisonment, or 5+ years |
| AWOL (무단이탈) | Unauthorized absence without intent to desert | Up to 3 years or fine |
| Failure to report for duty (입영거부) | Failure to report for initial military service | Up to 3 years (Military Service Act Art. 88) |

### Conscription System

The Military Service Act (병역법) requires all male citizens aged 18-35 to complete military service. Service duration varies by branch:

| Branch | Service Duration |
|--------|-----------------|
| Army/Marine Corps | 18 months |
| Navy | 20 months |
| Air Force | 21 months |
| Alternative service (conscientious objectors) | 36 months |

### Conscientious Objection: From Criminalization to Recognition

South Korea historically imprisoned more conscientious objectors than any other country in the world. Between 1953 and 2018, approximately 19,000 Jehovah's Witnesses and other conscientious objectors were imprisoned for refusing military service, with sentences typically ranging from 18 months to three years.

**Constitutional Court Decision 2011헌바379 (2018):** The Constitutional Court ruled that the Military Service Act's failure to provide an alternative to military service for conscientious objectors was unconstitutional, holding that the right of conscience under Article 19 of the Constitution requires the state to provide a non-military alternative. This decision reversed the court's own 2004 ruling (2002헌가1) which had upheld the constitutionality of universal conscription without exceptions.

**Supreme Court Decision 2016도10912 (2018):** The Supreme Court overturned decades of precedent, ruling that sincere conscientious objection constitutes "justifiable cause" for refusal of military service under the Military Service Act. This decision immediately affected approximately 900 pending cases of conscientious objectors.

**Alternative Service Implementation (2020):** The government implemented a 36-month alternative civilian service program, with objectors assigned to work in correctional facilities. The extended duration (double that of army service) has been criticized by human rights organizations as punitive, though the Constitutional Court upheld the 36-month term in 2023.

### North Korean Defection Cases

Military desertion takes on unique dimensions in the Korean context due to defections across the Demilitarized Zone (DMZ). North Korean soldiers who defect to South Korea are not prosecuted for desertion from the North Korean military but are instead processed through the Hanawon settlement support facility. Conversely, the extremely rare cases of South Korean soldiers defecting northward are treated as treason in addition to desertion.

## United States

### Legal Framework

**UCMJ Article 85 (10 U.S.C. § 885):** "(a) Any member of the armed forces who-- (1) without authority goes or remains absent from his unit, organization, or place of duty with intent to remain away therefrom permanently; (2) quits his unit, organization, or place of duty with intent to avoid hazardous duty or to shirk important service; or (3) without being regularly separated from one of the armed forces enlists or accepts an appointment in the same or another one of the armed forces without fully disclosing the fact that he has not been regularly separated... is guilty of desertion."

### Penalty Structure

| Scenario | Maximum Penalty |
|----------|----------------|
| Desertion with intent to remain away permanently (peacetime) | Dishonorable discharge, forfeiture of all pay, 5 years confinement |
| Desertion with intent to avoid hazardous duty (peacetime) | Dishonorable discharge, forfeiture, 5 years |
| Desertion in time of war | Death or such other punishment as a court-martial may direct |
| AWOL for 30+ days (Art. 86) | Dishonorable discharge, forfeiture, 1 year confinement |

### Distinction from AWOL

The critical legal distinction between desertion (Article 85) and unauthorized absence (AWOL, Article 86) is the intent element. Desertion requires proof of the specific intent to remain away permanently or to avoid hazardous duty. AWOL requires only unauthorized absence without such specific intent. In practice, an absence of 30 or more days creates a presumption of intent to desert, which the accused may rebut.

### Case of Bowe Bergdahl (2017)

Sergeant Bowe Bergdahl walked away from his post in Afghanistan's Paktika Province on June 30, 2009, and was captured by Taliban forces, spending five years in captivity before being released in a controversial prisoner exchange in May 2014. Bergdahl was charged with desertion (Article 85) and misbehavior before the enemy (Article 99). In October 2017, he pleaded guilty and was sentenced to a dishonorable discharge, reduction in rank to Private E-1, and forfeiture of pay, but no imprisonment -- a sentence that generated significant political controversy.

### Eddie Slovik: Last Deserter Executed

Private Eddie Slovik was the only American soldier executed for desertion since the Civil War. Slovik deserted from his unit in France in October 1944 and was subsequently court-martialed, convicted, and executed by firing squad on January 31, 1945. The case remains historically significant as an illustration of the extreme penalty theoretically available for desertion, though its non-use in the subsequent 80+ years suggests the death penalty for desertion is effectively defunct.

### Vietnam-Era Desertion

During the Vietnam War, desertion rates reached historic highs. In 1971, the Army reported 33,094 deserters -- a rate of 73.5 per 1,000 troops. President Jimmy Carter issued Proclamation 4483 on January 21, 1977, granting a pardon to all persons convicted or accused of violating the Military Selective Service Act between August 4, 1964, and March 28, 1973, covering draft evasion but not desertion.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's military criminal law is codified in the Wehrstrafgesetz (Military Criminal Code), with desertion-related offenses in Sections 15 and 16.

**Section 15 (Eigenmächtige Abwesenheit -- Unauthorized Absence):** A soldier who remains absent from their troop unit or duty station without authorization or does not return to it after the end of authorized absence shall be punished with imprisonment for not more than three years.

**Section 16 (Fahnenflucht -- Desertion):** A soldier who absconds from their troop unit or duty station with the intent to permanently evade their obligations under military service law or who does not return from an absence with such intent shall be punished with imprisonment for not more than five years.

### Penalty Structure

| Offense | Provision | Penalty |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Unauthorized absence | Section 15(1) | Up to 3 years |
| Unauthorized absence in special situations | Section 15(2) | 3 months to 5 years |
| Desertion (basic) | Section 16(1) | Up to 5 years |
| Desertion causing damage to combat readiness | Section 16(2) | 6 months to 5 years |
| Attempted desertion | Section 16(3) | Punishable |
| Self-mutilation to avoid service | Section 17 | Up to 5 years |

### Suspension of Conscription (2011)

Germany suspended mandatory military service (Wehrpflicht) effective July 1, 2011, though Article 12a of the Grundgesetz retains the constitutional basis for conscription should it be reactivated. Since the suspension, desertion concerns have diminished significantly, as all members of the Bundeswehr are volunteers. However, the Wehrstrafgesetz provisions remain in force and applicable to volunteer soldiers.

### Conscientious Objection (Article 4(3) GG)

Article 4(3) of the Grundgesetz provides: "No person shall be compelled against their conscience to render military service involving the use of arms." This right was recognized from the founding of the Federal Republic in 1949, reflecting Germany's post-World War II commitment to individual conscience. When conscription was active, conscientious objectors performed Zivildienst (civilian service) of equal duration.

### Historical Context: Wehrmacht Deserters

The rehabilitation of Wehrmacht deserters from World War II was a protracted legal and political process. It was not until 2002 that the Bundestag enacted the Gesetz zur Aufhebung nationalsozialistischer Unrechtsurteile (Act on the Annulment of National Socialist Unjust Sentences), which annulled all Wehrmacht desertion convictions from the Nazi era. An estimated 22,000 German soldiers were executed for desertion during World War II.

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

**Bayatyan v. Armenia [GC] (Application No. 23459/03, 2011):** The Grand Chamber's landmark decision established that the right of conscientious objection to military service is protected under Article 9 (freedom of thought, conscience, and religion). The Court overturned its previous position that Article 4(3)(b) -- which excludes military service from the definition of "forced labour" -- implicitly excluded conscientious objection from Convention protection. The Court held that the prosecution and imprisonment of a Jehovah's Witness who refused military service in the absence of an alternative civilian service option violated Article 9.

**Adyan and Others v. Armenia (Application No. 75604/11, 2017):** The Court found that even where an alternative service exists, it must be of a genuinely civilian nature. Armenia's alternative service, which was supervised by the military and included elements of military discipline, did not satisfy the requirements established in Bayatyan.

**Savda v. Turkey (Application No. 42730/05, 2012):** The Court found violations of Articles 3 and 9 in the repeated prosecution and imprisonment of a conscientious objector who was subjected to a cycle of conviction, imprisonment, release, and re-conscription. The Court held that such a cycle constituted degrading treatment.

### EU Charter of Fundamental Rights

Article 10(2): "The right to conscientious objection is recognized, in accordance with the national laws governing the exercise of this right." This provision ensures that EU member states must provide for conscientious objection, though the specific modalities are left to national law.

### NATO Standardization

NATO member states have varying approaches to military desertion, but NATO Standardization Agreements (STANAGs) establish common principles for military discipline. The NATO Status of Forces Agreement (SOFA) addresses jurisdictional questions when service members desert while stationed in another member state's territory.

## Comparative Analysis

### Conscription vs. Volunteer Forces

The most fundamental divergence is between conscription-based systems (South Korea) and all-volunteer forces (United States, Germany post-2011). Desertion carries different moral and legal weight in these contexts: in conscription systems, the soldier never chose to serve, making the criminalization of departure more contested; in volunteer systems, the soldier undertook a contractual obligation, making abandonment more clearly a breach of duty.

### Wartime Penalty Severity

Both South Korea and the United States retain the theoretical possibility of executing deserters in wartime, though neither has carried out such a sentence in decades. Germany does not provide for the death penalty under any circumstances (Article 102 GG). The ECHR's Protocol No. 6 and Protocol No. 13 prohibit the death penalty in all circumstances among ratifying states.

### Conscientious Objection

South Korea's 2018 recognition of conscientious objection brought it into alignment with European norms, though the 36-month alternative service (double military service) remains more punitive than the alternatives provided by most European states. The ECHR's Bayatyan decision established the minimum standard that an alternative must exist.
