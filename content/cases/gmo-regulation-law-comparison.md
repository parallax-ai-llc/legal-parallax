## Summary

The regulation of genetically modified organisms (GMOs) represents one of the most polarized areas of science-based policymaking, with jurisdictions adopting fundamentally different approaches based on divergent assessments of risk, precaution, consumer choice, and agricultural innovation. South Korea regulates GMOs through the Living Modified Organisms Act (유전자변형생물체의 국가간 이동 등에 관한 법률, LMO Act, 2001) implementing the Cartagena Protocol on Biosafety, and the Food Sanitation Act (식품위생법) governing GMO food safety assessment and mandatory labeling; South Korea is the world's largest importer of GMO crops for animal feed (importing approximately 10 million tonnes of GM corn and soybeans annually) while prohibiting the commercial cultivation of GM crops domestically, creating a regulatory bifurcation between import permissiveness and cultivation restriction. The United States regulates GMOs through the Coordinated Framework for the Regulation of Biotechnology (1986), distributing oversight across three agencies -- the USDA Animal and Plant Health Inspection Service (APHIS) for plant pest risk, the EPA for pesticide-related traits, and the FDA for food safety -- based on the principle that GMO products should be regulated by their characteristics rather than the process by which they were created; the USDA's SECURE rule (2020, 7 CFR Part 340) modernized the regulatory framework to focus on plants with characteristics that pose a plausible pathway to increased plant pest risk, and the National Bioengineered Food Disclosure Standard (7 U.S.C. Section 1639 et seq., 2018) established the first federal mandatory disclosure requirement for bioengineered foods. Germany, operating within the EU framework, applies one of the world's strictest GMO regulatory regimes under Regulation (EC) No 1829/2003 (GM food and feed authorization), Directive 2001/18/EC (deliberate release), and Regulation (EC) No 1830/2003 (traceability and labeling); EU authorization requires a positive scientific opinion from the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) followed by qualified majority approval in the Council -- a political threshold that has resulted in very few cultivation authorizations; Germany exercised its national opt-out under Directive (EU) 2015/412 to prohibit the cultivation of all authorized GM crops on its territory, and the 2018 CJEU ruling in Confédération paysanne (C-528/16) classified organisms produced by new genomic techniques (gene editing) as GMOs subject to full regulatory requirements, triggering ongoing legislative reform efforts. At the international level, the Cartagena Protocol on Biosafety (2000) to the Convention on Biological Diversity establishes the advance informed agreement procedure for transboundary movement of living modified organisms, while the Codex Alimentarius Commission provides international food safety standards for GM foods; the ECHR has not directly addressed GMO regulation but the precautionary principle recognized in Tatar v. Romania (2009) and the right to environmental information under Article 10 are applicable to GMO governance controversies.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

**Living Modified Organisms Act (유전자변형생물체의 국가간 이동 등에 관한 법률, 2001):** Implementing the Cartagena Protocol on Biosafety, this Act regulates the import, production, and use of living modified organisms. The Act establishes:
- Risk assessment requirements for all LMOs intended for import, production, or release
- An advance informed agreement procedure for transboundary movement
- Post-release monitoring and emergency response provisions
- Public participation in LMO approval decisions

Five government agencies share regulatory responsibility: the Ministry of Trade, Industry and Energy (overall coordination), the Ministry of Agriculture, Food and Rural Affairs (agricultural LMOs), the Ministry of Food and Drug Safety (food safety), the Ministry of Environment (environmental release), and the Ministry of Health and Welfare (pharmaceuticals).

**Food Sanitation Act (식품위생법) -- GMO Food Labeling:** South Korea mandates labeling of foods containing GMO ingredients above a threshold of 3% per ingredient. The labeling requirement applies to 27 categories of processed foods. Foods labeled as "non-GMO" (비유전자변형식품) must contain no detectable GM content. The Ministry of Food and Drug Safety (식품의약품안전처) conducts GMO food safety assessments.

### Import vs. Cultivation Paradox

South Korea's GMO policy presents a striking paradox: the country is one of the world's largest importers of GM crops while prohibiting their domestic cultivation. Approximately 10 million tonnes of GM corn and soybeans are imported annually, primarily for animal feed and industrial use. No GM crop has been authorized for commercial cultivation in South Korea, despite extensive field trial research on GM rice, GM pepper, and other crops at the Rural Development Administration (농촌진흥청).

The prohibition on domestic cultivation reflects:
- Strong consumer opposition (over 70% of Korean consumers express concern about GM foods)
- The cultural significance of rice as a staple food and national symbol
- Small farm structure making coexistence management challenging
- Political sensitivity of agricultural policy

### Gene Editing Policy

South Korea has been developing its regulatory approach to new breeding techniques (NBTs), including CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing. The LMO Act as currently drafted applies to organisms produced through genetic modification techniques, potentially encompassing gene-edited organisms even where no foreign DNA is inserted. The government has been consulting on a risk-proportionate framework that would create a lighter regulatory pathway for gene-edited organisms that do not contain transgenes, but no legislative amendment has been enacted as of 2026.

## United States

### Legal Framework

**Coordinated Framework for the Regulation of Biotechnology (1986):** The foundational U.S. policy document establishing that GMOs would be regulated under existing statutory authorities based on the characteristics of the product rather than the process by which it was created:

| Agency | Statute | Jurisdiction |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| USDA-APHIS | Plant Protection Act | Plant pest risk from GE plants |
| EPA | Federal Insecticide, Fungicide, and Rodenticide Act; Toxic Substances Control Act | Pesticidal traits (e.g., Bt crops); novel microorganisms |
| FDA | Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act | Food safety (voluntary consultation process) |

**SECURE Rule (2020, 7 CFR Part 340):** The USDA's Sustainable, Ecological, Consistent, Uniform, Responsible, Efficient (SECURE) rule modernized the regulatory framework:
- Focuses on whether a GE plant poses a "plausible pathway to increased plant pest risk"
- Exempts plants that could have been developed through conventional breeding
- Exempts most gene-edited plants that do not contain DNA from plant pest organisms
- Creates an expedited regulatory status review process

**National Bioengineered Food Disclosure Standard (7 U.S.C. Section 1639 et seq.):** Enacted in 2016 and implemented January 1, 2022, this standard requires food manufacturers to disclose the presence of bioengineered (BE) ingredients through text, symbol, electronic/digital link, or text message. The 5% threshold is higher than the EU's 0.9%, and the disclosure options (including QR codes) have been criticized as less transparent than traditional label text.

### Market Adoption

The United States has the world's highest GM crop adoption rates:

| Crop | GM Adoption Rate (2024) |
|------|------------------------|
| Soybeans | 95% |
| Corn | 93% |
| Cotton | 96% |
| Sugar beets | >99% |
| Canola | 95% |
| Alfalfa | ~30% |
| Non-browning apples | Limited commercial |

### Key Legal Issues

**Monsanto Co. v. Geertson Seed Farms (561 U.S. 139, 2010):** The Supreme Court addressed the deregulation of Roundup Ready alfalfa, holding that a nationwide injunction against planting was overbroad but that APHIS must complete an environmental impact statement before deregulating the crop.

**Grocery Manufacturers Association v. Sorrell (No. 5:14-CV-117, D. Vt.):** Industry challenged Vermont's mandatory GMO labeling law; the case was mooted by the passage of the federal National Bioengineered Food Disclosure Standard in 2016, which preempted state labeling laws.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany applies one of the world's strictest GMO regulatory regimes, combining EU-level authorization requirements with national-level cultivation restrictions.

**Genetic Engineering Act (Gentechnikgesetz, GenTG):** Germany's primary national statute implementing EU GMO directives. Key provisions include:
- Licensing requirements for contained use and deliberate release of GMOs
- Strict liability for economic damage caused by GMO contamination (Section 36a GenTG)
- Coexistence rules requiring minimum separation distances between GM and non-GM fields
- Public register of GM field trial locations (Standortregister)

**National Cultivation Opt-Out:** Under Directive (EU) 2015/412, which amended Directive 2001/18/EC to allow member states to restrict or prohibit GMO cultivation on their territory, Germany has opted out of cultivation of all authorized GM crops. This opt-out was formally exercised in 2015 and applies to MON 810 maize (the only GM crop authorized for EU cultivation at the time) and any future cultivation authorizations.

**Strict Liability (Section 36a GenTG):** If GM material causes "essential impairment" (wesentliche Beeinträchtigung) to conventional or organic crops through cross-pollination or commingling, the GMO cultivator is strictly liable for economic damage, regardless of fault. This provision has been described as one of the most stringent liability regimes for GMO contamination globally and has been cited as a practical barrier to any future cultivation even if the national opt-out were lifted.

### Confédération paysanne and Gene Editing

The CJEU's judgment in Confédération paysanne et al. (C-528/16, July 25, 2018) held that organisms obtained by directed mutagenesis techniques (including CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing) constitute "genetically modified organisms" within the meaning of Directive 2001/18/EC and are therefore subject to the full GMO authorization, labeling, and traceability requirements. The ruling rejected arguments that gene editing should be treated as conventional mutagenesis (which is exempt from the Directive). The decision has had profound implications for European agricultural biotechnology, effectively subjecting gene-edited crops to the same lengthy and costly authorization process as transgenic GMOs.

### Public Opinion

German public opposition to GMOs is among the strongest in Europe. Eurobarometer surveys consistently show that over 70% of German consumers are concerned about GM food. The anti-GMO movement in Germany draws on broader environmental consciousness, the organic farming tradition, and distrust of agribusiness corporations.

## ECHR / EU

### EU Authorization Process

The EU GMO authorization process under Regulation (EC) No 1829/2003 involves:
1. Application to the relevant member state competent authority
2. Scientific risk assessment by EFSA
3. Draft authorization decision by the European Commission
4. Vote by the Standing Committee on Plants, Animals, Food and Feed (qualified majority)
5. If no qualified majority: referral to the Appeal Committee
6. If still no qualified majority: the Commission may adopt the authorization

In practice, member states have been unable to form qualified majorities either for or against most GMO authorizations, with the Commission ultimately granting authorization based on positive EFSA scientific opinions despite political deadlock. This procedural dynamic has generated persistent criticism from both pro- and anti-GMO camps.

### Proposed New Genomic Techniques (NGT) Regulation (2023)

In response to the Confédération paysanne ruling, the European Commission proposed in July 2023 a regulation creating a differentiated regulatory framework for plants produced by new genomic techniques (NGTs):

**Category 1 NGT plants:** Plants that could also occur naturally or through conventional breeding (no more than 20 genetic modifications). These would be exempt from GMO authorization, risk assessment, and labeling requirements -- essentially treated like conventionally bred plants.

**Category 2 NGT plants:** Plants with more complex modifications. These would require a modified authorization process with risk assessment, labeling, and traceability, but potentially lighter than the current full GMO procedure.

The proposal has been intensely debated, with agricultural biotechnology proponents supporting it as essential for EU competitiveness and climate adaptation, and environmental organizations, organic farming associations, and several member states opposing the exemption of Category 1 plants from labeling as a violation of consumer choice. As of early 2026, legislative negotiations between the European Parliament and Council continue.

### EU Labeling and Traceability

Regulation (EC) No 1830/2003 establishes mandatory traceability and labeling requirements:
- All food and feed consisting of, containing, or produced from GMOs must be labeled
- Adventitious or technically unavoidable presence threshold: 0.9% per ingredient (for authorized GMOs)
- Zero tolerance for unauthorized GMOs
- Traceability documentation required at all stages of the production and distribution chain

### International Framework

**Cartagena Protocol on Biosafety (2000):** The Protocol, to which the EU, South Korea, and most countries are parties (the United States has signed but not ratified), establishes the advance informed agreement procedure for transboundary movement of LMOs intended for intentional introduction into the environment and requires risk assessment before import decisions.

**Codex Alimentarius:** The Codex food safety standards for GM foods (adopted 2003) provide science-based guidelines for risk assessment, including the principles of substantial equivalence and case-by-case assessment.

## Comparative Analysis

### Process vs. Product Regulation

The fundamental regulatory divide is between the U.S. product-based approach (regulate based on characteristics of the final product) and the EU/Korean process-based approach (regulate based on the method of production). This philosophical difference explains why the same gene-edited crop might be unregulated in the United States but require full GMO authorization in the EU. The distinction has significant trade implications and has been a persistent issue in transatlantic trade negotiations.

### Labeling Approaches

The EU's mandatory labeling with a 0.9% threshold, South Korea's 3% threshold, and the U.S.'s 5% threshold with multiple disclosure options (including QR codes) reflect different weights given to consumer information rights versus industry compliance costs. The EU approach is the most transparent but also the most costly to implement. The U.S. approach has been criticized as "label laundering" that obscures rather than informs.

### Gene Editing: The New Frontier

The regulation of gene-edited organisms is the most dynamic area of GMO law. The U.S. SECURE rule largely exempts gene-edited plants without foreign DNA. The EU's Confédération paysanne ruling applies the full GMO framework. South Korea is developing a risk-proportionate approach. The proposed EU NGT regulation, if adopted, would partially converge with the U.S. approach for simple gene edits while maintaining stricter requirements for complex modifications.

### Trade Tensions

GMO regulation is a persistent trade friction point. The WTO ruled in EC -- Biotech Products (DS291/292/293, 2006) that the EU's de facto moratorium on GMO authorizations (1998-2004) constituted undue delay inconsistent with the SPS Agreement. The ruling did not resolve the underlying substantive dispute about the appropriate level of precaution.
