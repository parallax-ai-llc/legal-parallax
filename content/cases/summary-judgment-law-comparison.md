## Summary

Summary judgment -- the procedural mechanism by which a court may dispose of a claim or defense without a full trial upon determining that no genuine dispute of material fact exists and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law -- represents a critical efficiency tool in civil litigation, though its scope, availability, and theoretical justification vary substantially across legal systems. South Korea provides for summary disposition mechanisms under the Code of Civil Procedure (민사소송법), including simplified judgment procedures (간이판결) and a system of uncontested claim procedures (독촉절차) under Articles 462-473 that allow courts to issue payment orders without trial when claims are for specific monetary amounts; the Korean Supreme Court (대법원) has developed jurisprudence on the standards for disposing of cases where the defendant fails to genuinely contest material facts, though Korea's civil law tradition places greater emphasis on judicial investigation than the common law summary judgment model. The United States has developed perhaps the most extensive summary judgment doctrine, codified in Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 56, with the landmark "Celotex trilogy" of Supreme Court decisions in 1986 -- Celotex Corp. v. Catrett (477 U.S. 317), Anderson v. Liberty Lobby, Inc. (477 U.S. 242), and Matsushita Electric Industrial Co. v. Zenith Radio Corp. (475 U.S. 574) -- establishing the modern framework for evaluating summary judgment motions, requiring the nonmoving party to go beyond the pleadings and demonstrate specific facts showing a genuine dispute for trial. Germany employs distinct mechanisms including Versäumnisurteil (default judgment upon failure to appear or respond, ZPO §§ 330-347), early disposition through preliminary legal opinions under ZPO § 139, and the Mahnverfahren (order for payment procedure, ZPO §§ 688-703d), though the German system lacks a direct equivalent of common law summary judgment due to its inquisitorial tradition and the court's obligation to establish facts ex officio. The ECHR has examined summary disposition mechanisms under Article 6(1) (right to a fair trial), requiring that any truncated procedure respect the principles of equality of arms, adversarial proceedings, and proportionality, while EU Regulation 1896/2006 establishes a European Order for Payment Procedure facilitating cross-border summary collection of uncontested monetary claims.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's Code of Civil Procedure (민사소송법) does not contain a procedural device directly analogous to the common law summary judgment motion. Instead, the Korean system provides several mechanisms that serve overlapping functions in disposing of claims without full trial proceedings.

**Payment Order Procedure (독촉절차, Articles 462-473):** This procedure allows a creditor holding a claim for a specific sum of money or a specific quantity of fungible goods to apply to the court for a payment order (지급명령) without a full hearing. The court reviews the application on paper and, if the claim appears well-founded, issues the order. The debtor may file an opposition (이의신청) within two weeks under Article 470, which automatically converts the matter into ordinary litigation. If no opposition is filed, the payment order becomes enforceable. This mechanism closely parallels the German Mahnverfahren and the European Order for Payment Procedure.

**Simplified Judgment (간이판결):** Under the small claims procedure (소액사건심판법) for claims not exceeding a specified monetary threshold (currently 30 million KRW), courts may employ simplified evidentiary procedures, accept written submissions in lieu of oral testimony, and render judgment without full trial proceedings. The Small Claims Act (소액사건심판법) of 1973 grants district courts broad discretion to streamline procedures.

### Judicial Fact-Finding and Disposition

Korean civil procedure, rooted in the civil law tradition, assigns the court an active role in fact-finding. Under Article 136 of the Code of Civil Procedure, the presiding judge has the duty to clarify factual and legal issues (석명의무), and under Article 292, the court may examine evidence ex officio. This inquisitorial element means that cases are less likely to be disposed of on the basis of one party's failure to produce evidence, as the court itself bears responsibility for establishing the factual record.

However, the Korean Supreme Court has recognized that where a party fails to contest specific factual allegations or to produce evidence within its control, the court may draw adverse inferences. Supreme Court Decision 2003Da49542 (2005) held that a party's failure to respond to specific factual allegations in the opposing party's briefs may be treated as an implied admission under Article 150 of the Code of Civil Procedure, enabling the court to render judgment without further factual investigation on those points.

### Preparatory Proceedings (변론준비절차)

Articles 258-286 of the Code of Civil Procedure establish preparatory proceedings (변론준비절차) in which the court and parties identify and narrow disputed issues before the main hearing. Through this process, claims or defenses that lack factual or legal foundation may be eliminated early, serving a function analogous to partial summary judgment. The court may issue a preparatory order (변론준비명령) requiring parties to submit all evidence and legal arguments by a specified date, and issues not raised during preparatory proceedings may be excluded from the main hearing under Article 285.

### Constitutional Court Considerations

The Constitutional Court of Korea (헌법재판소) has addressed the procedural rights of litigants under Article 27(1) of the Constitution, which guarantees the right to trial. In Decision 2012Hun-Ba298 (2014), the Court confirmed that simplified procedures for disposing of uncontested claims do not violate the constitutional right to trial, provided adequate notice is given and the respondent has a meaningful opportunity to convert the matter into ordinary proceedings.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Summary judgment in the United States is governed by Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 56 in federal courts and analogous state rules. Rule 56(a) provides: "The court shall grant summary judgment if the movant shows that there is no genuine dispute as to any material fact and the movant is entitled to judgment as a matter of law."

### The Celotex Trilogy (1986)

Three Supreme Court decisions decided during the October 1985 Term fundamentally reshaped summary judgment practice:

**Celotex Corp. v. Catrett, 477 U.S. 317 (1986):** The Court held that the moving party need not negate the nonmoving party's claim but need only point to the absence of evidence supporting the nonmoving party's case. Justice Rehnquist, writing for the majority, stated that Rule 56 "mandates the entry of summary judgment, after adequate time for discovery and upon motion, against a party who fails to make a showing sufficient to establish the existence of an element essential to that party's case, and on which that party will bear the burden of proof at trial." This decision lowered the initial burden on summary judgment movants and transformed summary judgment from a disfavored procedural shortcut into an integral part of federal civil practice.

**Anderson v. Liberty Lobby, Inc., 477 U.S. 242 (1986):** The Court held that the substantive evidentiary standard of proof applicable to the underlying claim (e.g., preponderance of the evidence, clear and convincing evidence) must be applied in evaluating whether a genuine dispute of material fact exists at the summary judgment stage. A dispute is "genuine" only if "the evidence is such that a reasonable jury could return a verdict for the nonmoving party." This decision integrated the trial standard into the summary judgment analysis.

**Matsushita Electric Industrial Co. v. Zenith Radio Corp., 475 U.S. 574 (1986):** In an antitrust context, the Court held that the nonmoving party must present evidence "that tends to exclude the possibility" that the alleged conspirators acted independently. The decision established that where the nonmoving party's version of events is economically implausible, more specific evidence is required to create a genuine dispute. This heightened scrutiny for implausible claims has been applied across substantive areas.

### Post-Trilogy Developments

**Scott v. Harris, 550 U.S. 372 (2007):** The Court held that when video evidence so clearly contradicts the nonmoving party's version of events that no reasonable jury could believe the nonmoving party, summary judgment is appropriate. This decision expanded the court's role in evaluating the sufficiency of evidence at the summary judgment stage.

**Tolan v. Cotton, 572 U.S. 650 (2014):** The Court, in a per curiam opinion, reversed a grant of summary judgment where the Fifth Circuit failed to draw reasonable inferences in favor of the nonmoving party, reaffirming that courts must view evidence in the light most favorable to the nonmovant.

### Partial Summary Judgment

Rule 56(a) permits partial summary judgment, allowing courts to resolve specific claims, defenses, or issues while leaving remaining disputes for trial. Rule 56(g) further allows the court to establish facts as not genuinely in dispute for purposes of trial, even when full summary judgment is denied.

### State Variations

While most states have adopted rules substantially similar to Federal Rule 56, some notable variations exist. Virginia historically disfavored summary judgment and maintained more restrictive standards. New York's CPLR 3212 requires the moving party to make a prima facie showing of entitlement to judgment, after which the burden shifts to the opponent. California Code of Civil Procedure Section 437c imposes specific notice and timing requirements and provides for summary adjudication of individual issues.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

The German Zivilprozessordnung (ZPO) does not include a mechanism directly equivalent to common law summary judgment. The German procedural tradition, which combines party presentation (Beibringungsgrundsatz) with active judicial case management, employs different tools to achieve early disposition of claims.

### Versäumnisurteil (Default Judgment on Failure to Participate)

**ZPO §§ 330-347** govern the Versäumnisurteil, which may be entered when a party fails to appear at the oral hearing or fails to conduct the oral proceedings. Unlike common law summary judgment, the Versäumnisurteil is triggered by a party's procedural default rather than by the court's assessment of the merits.

**ZPO § 331(1):** If the defendant fails to appear at the oral hearing, the factual allegations in the plaintiff's claim are deemed admitted, and judgment may be entered on the basis of those allegations, provided the claim is well-founded in law. The court does not weigh evidence but simply applies the law to the facts as alleged.

**ZPO § 331(2):** If the plaintiff fails to appear, the defendant may request that the plaintiff's claim be dismissed.

**Einspruch (Opposition, ZPO § 338):** The defaulting party may file an Einspruch within two weeks of service of the Versäumnisurteil, which reopens the proceedings. The Einspruch is not an appeal but returns the case to the same court for a hearing on the merits, effectively nullifying the Versäumnisurteil (ZPO § 342).

### Mahnverfahren (Order for Payment Procedure)

**ZPO §§ 688-703d** establish the Mahnverfahren, a streamlined procedure for collecting undisputed monetary claims. The creditor applies for a Mahnbescheid (payment order), which is issued without substantive review of the claim's merits. In Germany, the Mahnverfahren is largely automated through centralized Mahngerichte (payment order courts), processing millions of applications annually.

The debtor may file a Widerspruch (objection) within two weeks (ZPO § 694), which converts the matter into ordinary proceedings. If no objection is filed, the creditor may apply for a Vollstreckungsbescheid (enforcement order, ZPO § 699), which has the effect of a default judgment. The debtor may then file an Einspruch against the Vollstreckungsbescheid within two weeks (ZPO § 700).

### Early Disposition Through Judicial Case Management

**ZPO § 139 (Materielle Prozessleitung):** The court has a duty to discuss the factual and legal aspects of the dispute with the parties, to ask questions, and to indicate its preliminary legal assessment. This early disclosure of the court's tentative view often leads to settlement or withdrawal of untenable claims, serving a function analogous to summary judgment in promoting early disposition.

**ZPO § 296:** The court may reject belated factual allegations and evidence offers if their admission would delay the proceedings, provided the delay is attributable to the party's gross negligence. This preclusion power, while not identical to summary judgment, prevents parties from prolonging proceedings with untimely factual assertions.

### Urkundenprozess (Documentary Proceedings)

**ZPO §§ 592-605a** provide for the Urkundenprozess, a special procedure in which claims based on documentary evidence may be adjudicated solely on the basis of documents, without oral witness testimony. The court renders a preliminary judgment (Vorbehaltsurteil) that is provisionally enforceable, with the defendant retaining the right to a full hearing in subsequent proceedings (Nachverfahren). This mechanism allows creditors with strong documentary evidence to obtain rapid enforcement, analogous to summary judgment based on documentary proof.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Article 6(1) and Summary Procedures

The European Court of Human Rights has consistently held that Article 6(1) of the Convention, which guarantees the right to a fair and public hearing within a reasonable time by an independent and impartial tribunal, applies to summary disposition mechanisms.

**Dombo Beheer B.V. v. Netherlands (Application No. 14448/88, 1993):** The Court established the principle of equality of arms as a component of fair trial rights, requiring that each party be afforded a reasonable opportunity to present their case under conditions that do not place them at a substantial disadvantage vis-à-vis the opposing party. Summary procedures must respect this principle.

**Ankerl v. Switzerland (Application No. 17748/91, 1996):** The Court held that the right to adversarial proceedings means that each party must have the opportunity to have knowledge of and comment on the observations or evidence adduced by the other party. This principle constrains the use of ex parte or paper-only summary procedures.

**Markovic and Others v. Italy (Application No. 1398/03, Grand Chamber, 2006):** The Court addressed jurisdictional bars that prevent substantive adjudication of claims, noting that while states have discretion in designing procedural rules, such rules must not impair the very essence of the right of access to court.

### Proportionality of Summary Disposition

The ECHR has accepted that summary procedures serve the legitimate aim of efficient administration of justice and protection of defendants against unmeritorious claims. However, the Court requires that such procedures must be proportionate, meaning they must not deny the losing party an adequate opportunity to present their case. Key factors in the proportionality assessment include: whether the party had adequate notice of the proceedings; whether the party had an opportunity to respond; whether the procedure permitted consideration of the substantive merits; and whether effective remedies (including appeal or opposition) were available.

### EU Regulation 1896/2006 (European Order for Payment Procedure)

This regulation establishes a uniform cross-border procedure for the recovery of uncontested pecuniary claims within the EU (excluding Denmark). Key features include:

**Application (Article 7):** The claimant submits a standard form application to the competent court, describing the claim and the evidence supporting it.

**Issuance (Article 12):** The court reviews the application and, if the requirements are met, issues the European Payment Order without substantive examination of the merits.

**Opposition (Article 16):** The defendant may file a statement of opposition within 30 days, which terminates the European Payment Order procedure and, unless the claimant has requested otherwise, transfers the matter to ordinary civil proceedings.

**Enforceability (Article 18):** If no opposition is filed, the European Payment Order becomes enforceable throughout the EU without the need for a declaration of enforceability (exequatur).

### EU Regulation 805/2004 (European Enforcement Order)

This regulation allows judgments, court settlements, and authentic instruments on uncontested claims to be certified as European Enforcement Orders and enforced directly in other member states without exequatur. It complements the European Payment Order by providing a simplified enforcement mechanism for summary judgments on uncontested claims.

### Brussels I Recast (Regulation 1215/2012)

The Brussels I Recast governs jurisdiction and the recognition and enforcement of judgments in civil and commercial matters. Summary judgments rendered in one member state are generally enforceable in other member states under Article 36, subject to the limited grounds for refusal in Article 45 (including public policy and adequate service of the defendant in default proceedings).

## Comparative Analysis

### Functional Equivalence vs. Structural Divergence

Despite significant structural differences, the summary disposition mechanisms across all four systems serve functionally similar purposes: enabling courts to resolve claims efficiently where the substantive merits are clear, protecting defendants against the burden of defending unmeritorious claims, and preserving trial resources for genuinely contested matters. However, the mechanisms reflect fundamentally different procedural philosophies.

### Party Autonomy vs. Judicial Direction

The most significant structural difference lies in the allocation of responsibility for fact-finding. The U.S. summary judgment model places the burden squarely on the parties, with the court serving as a passive arbiter of whether the parties' evidentiary submissions reveal a genuine dispute. The German and Korean systems assign the court an active role in establishing the factual record, meaning that early disposition turns more on the court's assessment of the evidence than on one party's failure to produce contrary evidence.

### Merits Assessment vs. Procedural Default

Common law summary judgment involves a substantive assessment of the evidence, asking whether a reasonable factfinder could find for the nonmoving party. The German Versäumnisurteil, by contrast, is triggered by procedural default and involves no assessment of the evidentiary record. The Korean payment order procedure and the European Payment Order similarly avoid substantive merits assessment, relying instead on the respondent's failure to oppose as an indicator that the claim is uncontested.

### Frequency and Significance

Summary judgment plays a far more prominent role in U.S. civil litigation than analogous mechanisms in civil law systems. Empirical studies suggest that approximately 20% of federal civil cases are resolved by summary judgment, and the rate is substantially higher in certain case categories (e.g., employment discrimination, prisoner civil rights). In Germany and South Korea, early disposition is more commonly achieved through settlement facilitated by judicial case management rather than through formal summary mechanisms.

### Safeguards for Absent or Defaulting Parties

All four systems provide mechanisms for parties affected by summary dispositions to challenge or set aside such judgments. The U.S. allows appeal with de novo review of the legal standard. Germany provides the Einspruch mechanism, which fully reopens proceedings. Korea allows opposition to payment orders, converting the matter to ordinary litigation. The ECHR requires that any summary procedure must afford an adequate remedy, and the EU payment order procedures uniformly provide for opposition within specified time limits.
