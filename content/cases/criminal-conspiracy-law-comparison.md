## Summary

Criminal conspiracy -- an agreement between two or more persons to commit a criminal offense -- represents one of the most expansive and controversial doctrines in criminal law, allowing punishment for the mere act of agreeing to engage in unlawful conduct, often well before any substantive offense is committed. South Korea addresses conspiracy (공모) primarily through Article 30 of the Criminal Act (형법), which establishes co-principal liability for persons who jointly carry out a crime, and through specific conspiracy-to-commit provisions (음모죄) scattered throughout the Criminal Act for serious offenses such as insurrection (Article 90), foreign aggression (Article 104), and murder; the Supreme Court of Korea (대법원) has developed the influential doctrine of "functional control over the act" (기능적 행위지배) in its 2004 Decision 2004도3212 to determine the scope of co-conspiracy liability. The United States has the most expansive conspiracy doctrine in the world, treating conspiracy as both an inchoate offense and a theory of vicarious liability, with the general federal conspiracy statute 18 U.S.C. Section 371 criminalizing both conspiracy to commit any federal offense and conspiracy to defraud the United States, while the Pinkerton doctrine (Pinkerton v. United States, 328 U.S. 640 (1946)) extends each conspirator's liability to all reasonably foreseeable substantive crimes committed by co-conspirators in furtherance of the agreement. Germany does not recognize a general offense of criminal conspiracy, instead addressing group criminal liability through specific provisions for criminal associations (kriminelle Vereinigung, Section 129 StGB), terrorist organizations (Section 129a StGB), and conspiracy provisions limited to the most serious offenses such as high treason (Section 83 StGB), with the Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) having imposed strict limits on the scope of organizational liability to protect freedom of association. The EU has addressed conspiracy primarily through organized crime instruments, including Framework Decision 2008/841/JHA on the fight against organized crime, which requires member states to criminalize either conspiracy or participation in a criminal organization, and the European Court of Human Rights has scrutinized conspiracy prosecutions under Article 6 (fair trial) and Article 7 (legality principle) to ensure that the breadth of conspiracy liability does not undermine fundamental rights.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's approach to conspiracy differs fundamentally from the Anglo-American model. Rather than recognizing a general conspiracy offense, Korean criminal law addresses collective criminal liability primarily through the doctrine of co-principals (공동정범) and through specific conspiracy provisions for the most serious offenses.

**Article 30 -- Co-principals (공동정범):** "When two or more persons jointly carry out a crime, each shall be punished as a principal." This provision establishes that persons who participate in the joint execution of a crime are each fully liable as principals, not merely as accessories.

**Specific Conspiracy (음모) Provisions:** The Criminal Act criminalizes conspiracy to commit only the most serious offenses:

| Provision | Offense | Penalty for Conspiracy |
|-----------|---------|----------------------|
| Article 90(2) | Insurrection (내란) | 1-10 years imprisonment |
| Article 101(2) | Foreign aggression (외환) | 2+ years imprisonment |
| Article 104조의2 | Conspiracy to commit murder | Varies |
| National Security Act Art. 6 | Conspiracy against state security | Varies by category |

### Doctrine of Functional Control (기능적 행위지배)

The Supreme Court of Korea has developed the doctrine of "functional control over the act" (기능적 행위지배론) to determine when a person who did not personally execute all elements of an offense may be held liable as a co-principal under Article 30.

**Supreme Court Decision 2004도3212 (2004):** The Court held that co-principal liability requires: (1) a joint intention (공동의 의사) to commit the crime; (2) functional division of roles based on a common plan; and (3) each participant's contribution of an essential role in the execution of the crime.

**Supreme Court Decision 97도1740 (1997):** The Court established that a person who merely planned or directed a crime without participating in its physical execution could be held liable as a co-principal if they exercised "functional control" over the criminal act through their essential contribution to the planning and coordination.

### Distinction from Instigation

Korean law carefully distinguishes between conspiracy/co-principal liability (공동정범) and instigation (교사, Article 31). An instigator is one who induces another to commit a crime, while a co-principal jointly carries it out. The penalty for instigation equals that of the principal, but the doctrinal distinction is significant because co-principals share liability for the entire criminal act, while an instigator's liability is limited to the offense they induced.

### Organized Crime

South Korea enacted the Act on Aggravated Punishment of Specific Crimes (특정범죄 가중처벌 등에 관한 법률) and the Act on the Punishment of Violent Acts (폭력행위 등 처벌에 관한 법률, since repealed and replaced) to address organized criminal activity. These statutes impose enhanced penalties for crimes committed as part of organized groups without establishing a general conspiracy offense.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has the broadest and most fully developed conspiracy doctrine of any major legal system. Conspiracy functions both as a standalone inchoate offense and as a powerful prosecutorial tool for establishing vicarious liability among co-conspirators.

**18 U.S.C. Section 371 -- General Conspiracy Statute:** "If two or more persons conspire either to commit any offense against the United States, or to defraud the United States, or any agency thereof in any manner or for any purpose, and one or more of such persons do any act to effect the object of the conspiracy, each shall be fined under this title or imprisoned not more than five years, or both." This statute contains two distinct prongs: the "offense clause" (conspiracy to commit a federal crime) and the "defraud clause" (conspiracy to defraud the United States).

**Drug Conspiracy -- 21 U.S.C. Section 846:** Conspiracy to commit drug offenses carries the same penalties as the substantive offense and notably does not require proof of an overt act.

**RICO Conspiracy -- 18 U.S.C. Section 1962(d):** The Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act criminalizes conspiracy to conduct an enterprise through a pattern of racketeering activity. A RICO conspiracy conviction requires proof that the defendant agreed that a co-conspirator would commit at least two predicate racketeering acts (Salinas v. United States, 522 U.S. 52 (1997)).

### Pinkerton Doctrine

The most distinctive feature of American conspiracy law is the Pinkerton doctrine, derived from Pinkerton v. United States, 328 U.S. 640 (1946). Under this doctrine, each member of a conspiracy is vicariously liable for any substantive offense committed by a co-conspirator if that offense: (1) was committed in furtherance of the conspiracy, and (2) was reasonably foreseeable as a natural consequence of the conspiratorial agreement.

The Pinkerton doctrine has been criticized by scholars including Professor George Fletcher of Columbia Law School as creating guilt by association, but it remains firmly established in federal jurisprudence and most state courts. The Model Penal Code rejected the Pinkerton doctrine in Section 2.06, and several states (including New York, Ohio, and Georgia) have declined to adopt it.

### Key Elements and Doctrines

**Agreement:** The essential element of conspiracy is the agreement, which need not be formal or explicit. Circumstantial evidence of a tacit understanding suffices (Iannelli v. United States, 420 U.S. 770 (1975)).

**Plurality Requirement:** The common law "bilateral" approach requires at least two genuinely agreeing parties. The MPC "unilateral" approach allows conviction even if all other alleged co-conspirators are acquitted or were government agents. Federal courts generally follow the bilateral approach for Section 371.

**Wharton's Rule:** Where a substantive offense requires the participation of two persons (e.g., dueling, bigamy), conspiracy cannot be charged unless more persons participate than the minimum necessary for the offense (Iannelli v. United States, 420 U.S. 770 (1975)).

**Withdrawal:** A conspirator may withdraw by taking affirmative steps to communicate their withdrawal to co-conspirators (Smith v. United States, 568 U.S. 106 (2013)). Withdrawal starts the statute of limitations running but does not eliminate liability for the conspiracy itself -- only for subsequent Pinkerton liability.

| Statute | Maximum Penalty |
|---------|----------------|
| 18 U.S.C. Section 371 (general) | 5 years imprisonment and/or $250,000 fine |
| 21 U.S.C. Section 846 (drug conspiracy) | Same as substantive offense (up to life) |
| 18 U.S.C. Section 1962(d) (RICO) | 20 years imprisonment (life if predicate carries life) |
| 18 U.S.C. Section 2384 (seditious conspiracy) | 20 years imprisonment |

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany does not recognize a general offense of criminal conspiracy. This reflects the fundamental principle of individual criminal responsibility (Schuldprinzip) enshrined in German constitutional law, which requires that each person be punished only for their own culpable conduct.

Instead, German law addresses group criminal activity through two mechanisms: (1) specific conspiracy provisions (Verabredung) for the most serious offenses, and (2) criminal organization offenses.

**Section 129 StGB -- Criminal Organizations (Kriminelle Vereinigungen):** "Whoever founds an organization or participates as a member in an organization whose purposes or activities are directed at the commission of offenses that are punishable by a maximum term of imprisonment of at least two years shall be punished with imprisonment for a term not exceeding five years or a fine." The provision was significantly reformed in 2017 to align with EU Framework Decision 2008/841/JHA.

**Section 129a StGB -- Terrorist Organizations (Terroristische Vereinigungen):** Establishes enhanced penalties for founding, membership in, supporting, or recruiting for terrorist organizations. Maximum penalty: 10 years for founding or membership; 5 years for support or recruitment.

**Section 129b StGB -- Foreign Criminal and Terrorist Organizations:** Extends Sections 129 and 129a to organizations based abroad, with prosecution requiring authorization from the Federal Ministry of Justice.

### Specific Conspiracy Provisions (Verabredung)

German law criminalizes conspiracy (Verabredung, literally "arrangement" or "agreement") to commit only the most serious offenses:

| Provision | Offense | Penalty for Conspiracy |
|-----------|---------|----------------------|
| Section 30(2) StGB | General provision for felonies | Punishment from the framework for attempt |
| Section 83(1) StGB | High treason (Hochverrat) | 1-10 years imprisonment |
| Section 234a(3) StGB | Abduction with intent to bring about political persecution | As per Section 30(2) |

**Section 30 StGB -- Attempted Participation (Versuchte Beteiligung):** Paragraph 2 provides that conspiracy (Verabredung) to commit a felony is punishable from the penalty framework applicable to attempt. This is the closest German law comes to a general conspiracy provision, but it applies only to felonies (offenses carrying a minimum penalty of one year imprisonment).

### Constitutional Limitations

The Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) has imposed important limits on criminal organization liability:

**BVerfG Decision of July 25, 1961 (2 BvR 585/59):** The Court held that the criminalization of organizational membership must be compatible with Article 9 of the Basic Law (freedom of association). Mere passive membership in an organization is insufficient for criminal liability; the prosecution must prove active participation or concrete support.

**BVerfG Decision of November 12, 2003 (1 BvR 1316/99):** The Court emphasized that the scope of Section 129 must be interpreted narrowly to prevent conflict with freedom of association and freedom of expression (Article 5 GG).

### Reform of Section 129 (2017)

In 2017, Germany reformed Section 129 StGB to implement EU Framework Decision 2008/841/JHA. Key changes included:
- Replacing the concept of "Vereinigung" (association) with a broader definition encompassing loosely structured groups
- Lowering the organizational threshold from a formally structured association to any "organized combination" of more than two persons existing for a longer period
- Aligning terminology with EU definitions of criminal organizations

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR has addressed conspiracy and organizational liability primarily through three Convention articles:

**Article 6 -- Right to a Fair Trial:** The Court has scrutinized conspiracy prosecutions for compliance with fair trial guarantees, particularly regarding the admission of co-conspirator statements and the rights of defendants tried jointly.

**Doorson v. Netherlands (Application No. 20524/92, Judgment of March 26, 1996):** The Court established principles for the use of anonymous witness testimony in organized crime prosecutions, holding that while the use of such testimony is not automatically incompatible with Article 6, adequate procedural safeguards must be in place.

**Article 7 -- No Punishment Without Law:** The Court has emphasized that conspiracy and organizational offenses must satisfy the legality principle, requiring clear and foreseeable definitions.

**Liivik v. Estonia (Application No. 12157/05, Judgment of June 25, 2009):** The Court examined whether a conviction based on broadly defined conspiracy provisions satisfied Article 7 requirements, finding that vague or overly broad conspiracy definitions may violate the legality principle.

**Article 11 -- Freedom of Assembly and Association:** The Court has held that criminalizing membership in organizations engages Article 11 rights and must satisfy the necessity and proportionality requirements of Article 11(2).

### EU Framework on Organized Crime

**Framework Decision 2008/841/JHA on the Fight Against Organized Crime:** This instrument requires member states to criminalize participation in a criminal organization. Significantly, Article 2 offers member states a choice between two models:
- **Conspiracy model:** Criminalizing the agreement to commit serious offenses (reflecting the Anglo-American tradition)
- **Organizational participation model:** Criminalizing active participation in a criminal organization's criminal activities (reflecting the continental European tradition)

This dual-model approach reflects the fundamental divide between common law and civil law traditions regarding group criminality.

**Directive (EU) 2024/1226 on Environmental Crime:** Requires member states to ensure that participation in a criminal organization for the purpose of committing environmental offenses is criminalized.

**Europol and Eurojust Cooperation:** The EU has established institutional mechanisms for cross-border cooperation in organized crime cases, including Joint Investigation Teams (JITs) under Framework Decision 2002/465/JHA and the European Arrest Warrant (Framework Decision 2002/584/JHA).

### EU Definition of Criminal Organization

Council Framework Decision 2008/841/JHA defines a "criminal organisation" as "a structured association, established over a period of time, of more than two persons acting in concert with a view to committing offences which are punishable by deprivation of liberty or a detention order of a maximum of at least four years or a more serious penalty, to obtain, directly or indirectly, a financial or other material benefit."

## Comparative Analysis

### The Fundamental Divide

The most striking feature of comparative conspiracy law is the divide between common law and civil law approaches:

**Anglo-American Approach (United States):** Conspiracy is a general, standalone offense. The mere agreement to commit any crime, combined with an overt act, triggers criminal liability. The Pinkerton doctrine extends each conspirator's liability to all foreseeable crimes of co-conspirators. This creates an exceptionally broad net of criminal liability.

**Continental European Approach (South Korea, Germany):** Neither jurisdiction recognizes a general conspiracy offense. Group criminal liability is addressed through co-principal doctrines (requiring actual participation in execution) and organizational offenses (requiring membership in structured criminal groups). Conspiracy provisions exist only for the most serious offenses.

### Scope of Liability

The jurisdictions show dramatic variation in the scope of derivative liability:

- **United States (broadest):** Under Pinkerton, a conspirator can be convicted of substantive offenses they did not commit, plan, or even know about, as long as the offenses were foreseeable.
- **South Korea (moderate):** Co-principal liability requires functional control and essential contribution, limiting derivative liability.
- **Germany (narrowest):** The individual guilt principle (Schuldprinzip) prevents conviction for offenses committed by others without personal culpable participation.

### Policy Justifications

Proponents of broad conspiracy liability argue that group criminal activity is inherently more dangerous than individual crime and that conspiracy doctrine is essential for early intervention against organized crime. Critics argue that conspiracy law creates guilt by association, undermines the presumption of innocence, and may chill legitimate political activity and freedom of association.

### Cross-Border Challenges

The EU's dual-model approach in Framework Decision 2008/841/JHA represents a pragmatic accommodation of the common law-civil law divide, but creates challenges for cross-border cooperation when jurisdictions operate under fundamentally different theories of group liability. The European Arrest Warrant Framework Decision partially addresses this by requiring mutual recognition of judicial decisions regardless of the underlying legal theory.
