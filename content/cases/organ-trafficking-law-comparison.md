## Summary

Organ trafficking law (장기매매 관련법) -- the body of criminal, regulatory, and ethical law governing the illicit trade in human organs, the exploitation of vulnerable persons for organ removal, the criminalization of transplant tourism, and the protection of living donors -- represents one of the most urgent intersections of international criminal law, bioethics, and human rights. South Korea addresses organ trafficking primarily through the Act on Organ Transplantation (장기등 이식에 관한 법률, Act No. 5858, 1999, substantially amended), which criminalizes the buying and selling of organs under Article 18, establishes the Korean Network for Organ Sharing (KONOS, 국립장기조직혈액관리원) as the central allocation authority, and provides a comprehensive regulatory framework for both deceased and living donor transplantation; the Act was significantly strengthened following several high-profile organ trafficking cases involving Korean nationals traveling to China. The United States criminalizes organ trafficking through the National Organ Transplant Act of 1984 (NOTA, 42 U.S.C. Section 274e), which prohibits the acquisition, receipt, or transfer of human organs for "valuable consideration," with penalties of up to five years imprisonment and/or $50,000 in fines; the Trafficking Victims Protection Act (TVPA, 22 U.S.C. Section 7101 et seq.) addresses organ trafficking as a form of forced labor or trafficking in persons, and the United Network for Organ Sharing (UNOS) operates the national organ allocation system under federal contract. Germany criminalizes organ trafficking through the Transplantationsgesetz (TPG, Transplantation Act, 1997, substantially revised 2012) Sections 17-20, which prohibit organ trading (Organhandel) with penalties of up to five years imprisonment, and through the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB) Sections 232-233a addressing trafficking in human beings for purposes of organ removal; Germany's transplant system was shaken by the 2012 organ allocation scandal at several university hospitals, leading to comprehensive legislative reform. At the international level, the Council of Europe Convention against Trafficking in Human Organs (Santiago de Compostela Convention, CETS No. 216, 2015) represents the first binding international instrument specifically dedicated to combating organ trafficking, while the ECtHR has addressed related issues through its Article 3 and Article 4 jurisprudence on human trafficking, and the EU Anti-Trafficking Directive 2011/36/EU explicitly includes organ removal as a purpose of trafficking.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea has developed a comprehensive organ transplantation and anti-trafficking framework in response to both domestic and cross-border organ trafficking concerns.

**Act on Organ Transplantation (장기등 이식에 관한 법률):** Originally enacted in 1999 and substantially amended in 2010 and 2017, this Act provides the primary legal framework for organ donation, allocation, transplantation, and the criminalization of organ trafficking.

**Article 18 -- Prohibition of Organ Trading:** Article 18 of the Act prohibits any person from giving, receiving, or promising to give or receive money, property, or other benefits in exchange for an organ or the donation of an organ. Violations carry penalties of up to 10 years imprisonment or fines up to 100 million won (approximately USD 75,000). The prohibition extends to brokers, intermediaries, and any person who facilitates organ trading.

**Article 11 -- Living Donor Requirements:** Living organ donation is permitted only when the donor is a family member or a person with a special relationship to the recipient (as prescribed by Presidential Decree), is at least 16 years of age (19 for non-related donation), has full decision-making capacity, has given voluntary informed consent after receiving adequate information, and the donation does not pose an unreasonable risk to the donor's health.

### KONOS (Korean Network for Organ Sharing)

KONOS (국립장기조직혈액관리원), operated under the Ministry of Health and Welfare, serves as Korea's national organ allocation organization. KONOS manages the organ waiting list, coordinates organ procurement and allocation based on medical criteria (blood type, tissue matching, medical urgency, waiting time), monitors compliance with transplant regulations, and investigates suspected organ trafficking cases.

### Transplant Tourism Countermeasures

Following reports that Korean nationals were traveling to China for organ transplants -- raising concerns about the source of organs in China's transplant system -- South Korea strengthened its legal framework:

- **Reporting Obligations:** Medical professionals who become aware that a patient has received an organ transplant abroad must report this to KONOS (Article 16-2).
- **Post-Transplant Follow-Up:** Patients who receive transplants abroad are required to register with KONOS for follow-up care and monitoring.
- **Public Awareness:** The government has conducted campaigns discouraging transplant tourism, particularly travel to jurisdictions with documented organ trafficking or forced organ harvesting concerns.

### Brain Death Determination

The Act establishes the criteria and procedures for brain death determination, which is a prerequisite for deceased organ donation. Brain death must be certified by two or more physicians (including a specialist in neurology or neurosurgery), following the medically accepted protocols prescribed by the Act and implementing regulations. The Brain Death Determination Committee (뇌사판정위원회) at each transplant hospital must confirm the determination.

### Deceased Donation Rates

Despite legislative efforts, South Korea has historically had relatively low deceased donation rates compared to Western nations. The deceased organ donation rate was approximately 8.6 per million population (pmp) in 2024, compared to 36.1 pmp in Spain and 17.9 pmp in the United States. The government has implemented measures to increase donation rates, including expanded donor criteria, public education campaigns, and streamlined consent procedures, while maintaining the opt-in consent system.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has a multi-layered legal framework addressing organ trafficking, organ procurement, and transplant regulation.

**National Organ Transplant Act (NOTA, 42 U.S.C. Section 274e, 1984):** NOTA is the foundational federal statute prohibiting organ trafficking. Section 274e(a) provides: "It shall be unlawful for any person to knowingly acquire, receive, or otherwise transfer any human organ for valuable consideration for use in human transplantation if the transfer affects interstate commerce." Violations constitute a federal felony punishable by up to five years imprisonment and/or fines up to $50,000.

**"Valuable Consideration" Exception:** NOTA specifically excludes from "valuable consideration" the reasonable payments associated with removal, transportation, implantation, processing, preservation, quality control, and storage of a human organ, as well as expenses of travel, housing, and lost wages incurred by the organ donor.

**Trafficking Victims Protection Act (TVPA, 22 U.S.C. Section 7101 et seq.):** The TVPA addresses organ trafficking as a form of human trafficking, criminalizing the recruitment, harboring, transportation, or procurement of a person for the purpose of organ removal by means of force, fraud, or coercion. Penalties under the TVPA are substantially higher than under NOTA, with up to 20 years imprisonment for trafficking offenses and up to life imprisonment if death results.

### UNOS and the OPTN

The Organ Procurement and Transplantation Network (OPTN), established by NOTA, is operated under contract with the Health Resources and Services Administration (HRSA) by the United Network for Organ Sharing (UNOS). OPTN policies govern organ allocation, including:

| Principle | Implementation |
|-----------|---------------|
| Medical utility | Allocation based on medical criteria to maximize transplant benefit |
| Equity | Geographic and temporal fairness in allocation |
| Urgency | Priority for patients with the most urgent medical need |
| Efficiency | Minimization of organ wastage through broader sharing |

The 2021 OPTN policy reforms moved toward national organ sharing for certain organs (particularly kidneys), reducing the historical reliance on local allocation priority that created geographic disparities in waiting times and access.

### Living Donor Protection

US living donor protections are addressed through a combination of federal and state law:

- **NOTA:** Protects living donors from financial exploitation by prohibiting organ sales.
- **OPTN Policies:** Require transplant programs to use independent living donor advocates (IDAs), separate from the transplant team, to evaluate and counsel potential living donors.
- **National Living Donor Assistance Center (NLDAC):** Provides financial assistance to living donors for travel and subsistence expenses under the Organ Donation and Recovery Improvement Act.
- **Living Donor Protection Act (proposed/enacted in various forms):** Legislation to protect living donors from insurance discrimination, including prohibiting denial of life, disability, and long-term care insurance based on living donor status.

### Enforcement and Prosecutions

Federal prosecutions for organ trafficking under NOTA and the TVPA remain relatively rare but have occurred. In United States v. Rosenbaum (D.N.J. 2012), Levy Izhak Rosenbaum was convicted under NOTA for brokering black-market kidney transplants, representing the first federal conviction for organ trafficking in the United States. Rosenbaum was sentenced to 30 months imprisonment and fined $5,000.

### Organ Trafficking by Transnational Criminal Organizations

The State Department's annual Trafficking in Persons (TIP) Report documents organ trafficking globally, and NOTA's prohibition extends to US persons who facilitate organ trafficking abroad. However, the legal framework for addressing transplant tourism by US citizens remains underdeveloped compared to some other jurisdictions.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's organ trafficking and transplantation law has been substantially reformed following the organ allocation scandal of 2012.

**Transplantationsgesetz (TPG, Transplantation Act, 1997, revised 2012):** The TPG provides the comprehensive legal framework for organ donation, allocation, transplantation, and the criminalization of organ trafficking.

**TPG Section 17 -- Prohibition of Organ Trading (Verbot des Organhandels):** Section 17(1) prohibits trading in organs intended for therapeutic purposes. "Trading" is defined as the buying, selling, or offering to buy or sell organs for financial or material gain. Section 17(2) prohibits removing, transplanting, or making available for transplantation an organ that has been the object of prohibited trading.

**TPG Section 18 -- Penalties:** Violations of the organ trading prohibition are punishable by up to five years imprisonment or a fine. The penalty applies to buyers, sellers, brokers, and medical professionals who knowingly participate in organ trading.

**StGB Sections 232-233a -- Trafficking in Human Beings:** The Strafgesetzbuch criminalizes trafficking in human beings for the purpose of organ removal as part of the broader anti-trafficking provisions. Section 232(1)(1)(c) specifically identifies organ removal as a trafficking purpose. Penalties range from six months to ten years imprisonment, with aggravated penalties for trafficking involving minors, violence, or organized criminal groups.

### The 2012 Organ Allocation Scandal

The discovery in 2012 that transplant physicians at several German university hospitals (including Goettingen, Leipzig, and Munich-Rechts der Isar) had manipulated patient data to secure higher positions on the Eurotransplant waiting list led to the most significant crisis in German transplant history. The scandal eroded public trust in the transplant system and catalyzed comprehensive reforms:

- **Legislative Response:** The Gesetz zur Aenderung des Transplantationsgesetzes (Act Amending the Transplantation Act, 2013) strengthened penalties for waiting list manipulation, enhanced oversight mechanisms, and established the Transplantation Oversight Commission (Ueberwachungskommission) with broader investigatory powers.
- **Criminal Prosecutions:** Several transplant physicians were prosecuted. In the Goettingen case (LG Goettingen, Az. 6 Ks 4/13, 2015), the defendant was acquitted on attempted homicide charges but the case highlighted deficiencies in the legal framework for addressing manipulation of allocation criteria.
- **Structural Reforms:** The Bundesaerztekammer (German Medical Association), Deutsche Krankenhausgesellschaft (German Hospital Association), and GKV-Spitzenverband (central association of statutory health insurance) -- the three entities jointly responsible for the transplant system under TPG Section 11 -- implemented enhanced monitoring, multi-physician oversight of transplant decisions, and mandatory reporting requirements.

### Living Donor Protection

TPG Section 8 establishes rigorous requirements for living organ donation:

- Living donation is permitted only when no suitable deceased donor organ is available.
- The donor must be of legal age and fully capable of consenting.
- The donor must be a close relative of the recipient (first or second degree), a spouse, registered partner, or a person who has a demonstrably close personal relationship with the recipient.
- The Lebendspende-Kommission (Living Donor Commission), an independent commission established at each transplant center, must evaluate the voluntariness of the donation and the absence of organ trading.
- The donor must be informed about the procedure, risks, and consequences by a physician who is not involved in the transplantation.

### Consent System

Germany operates an "extended consent" model (erweiterte Zustimmungsloesung), established by the 2012 TPG reform. Under this system, organ donation requires either the explicit consent of the deceased (via organ donor card or advance directive) or, in the absence of a documented decision, the consent of the next of kin based on the presumed wishes of the deceased. A 2020 legislative proposal to introduce an opt-out (Widerspruchsloesung) system was defeated in the Bundestag. Instead, the Gesetz zur Staerkung der Entscheidungsbereitschaft bei der Organspende (Act to Strengthen Decision-Making Readiness for Organ Donation, 2020) established an online organ donation registry and mandatory information campaigns.

### Eurotransplant

Germany participates in Eurotransplant, the international organ sharing organization that allocates organs across eight member countries (Austria, Belgium, Croatia, Germany, Hungary, Luxembourg, the Netherlands, and Slovenia). Eurotransplant operates the central waiting list and allocation algorithms based on medical criteria, with separate allocation rules for each organ type.

## ECHR & EU

### Council of Europe Convention against Trafficking in Human Organs (Santiago de Compostela Convention, CETS No. 216)

Opened for signature on March 25, 2015, and entered into force on March 1, 2018, this Convention is the first binding international instrument specifically aimed at combating organ trafficking. As of 2025, it has been ratified by 11 Council of Europe member states.

**Key Provisions:**

| Article | Provision | Requirement |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| Art. 4 | Illicit removal of human organs | Criminalize removal without free, informed, specific consent or for financial gain |
| Art. 5 | Use of illicitly removed organs | Criminalize implantation or other use of illicitly removed organs |
| Art. 6 | Implantation outside domestic transplant system | Criminalize implantation outside authorized transplant system |
| Art. 7 | Illicit solicitation, recruitment, offering | Criminalize solicitation and recruitment of organ donors/recipients for financial gain |
| Art. 8 | Preparation, preservation, storage, transport | Criminalize handling of illicitly removed organs |
| Art. 9 | Aiding or abetting and attempt | Criminalize aiding, abetting, and attempting the above offenses |
| Art. 10 | Corporate liability | Establish liability of legal persons for trafficking offenses |

**Article 22 -- Protection of Victims:** Parties must ensure that victims of organ trafficking have access to information on relevant judicial and administrative proceedings, assistance for physical, psychological, and social recovery, and compensation from the perpetrators.

### EU Anti-Trafficking Directive (2011/36/EU)

The Directive explicitly includes "the removal of organs" as a purpose of trafficking in human beings (Article 2(3)). Member states must criminalize trafficking offenses with maximum penalties of at least five years imprisonment (Article 4(1)) and at least ten years for aggravated circumstances (Article 4(2)), including trafficking committed by public officials or within the framework of a criminal organization.

### Declaration of Istanbul on Organ Trafficking and Transplant Tourism (2008)

While not legally binding, the Declaration of Istanbul, developed by the Transplantation Society (TTS) and the International Society of Nephrology (ISN), has been highly influential in shaping national laws and policies. The Declaration defines organ trafficking, transplant commercialism, and transplant tourism, and calls on states to develop legal frameworks to prohibit these practices while promoting ethical organ donation.

### ECtHR Jurisprudence on Human Trafficking

**Rantsev v. Cyprus and Russia (Application No. 25965/04, Judgment of January 7, 2010):** The landmark ECtHR Grand Chamber decision established that human trafficking falls within the scope of Article 4 ECHR (prohibition of slavery and forced labor), and that states have positive obligations to establish a legislative and administrative framework to prevent trafficking, protect victims, and investigate trafficking allegations. While the case involved sex trafficking, the principles apply equally to organ trafficking.

**Chowdury and Others v. Greece (Application No. 21884/15, Judgment of March 30, 2017):** The Court found a violation of Article 4(2) where the state failed to effectively investigate and prosecute trafficking and forced labor, reinforcing the positive obligations framework applicable to all forms of trafficking including organ trafficking.

### WHO Guiding Principles on Human Cell, Tissue and Organ Transplantation

The WHO Guiding Principles (originally adopted in 1991, revised in 2010) provide the foundational ethical framework for transplant policy. Key principles include: cells, tissues, and organs should be donated freely without monetary payment; physicians should not participate in transplant procedures if they have reason to believe the organs are the subject of commercial transactions; and allocation should be guided by clinical criteria and ethical norms, not financial considerations.

## Comparative Analysis

### Scope of Criminalization

All four jurisdictions criminalize the buying and selling of organs, but the scope and penalties differ. South Korea imposes the harshest maximum penalty (10 years) for direct organ trading under the Organ Transplantation Act. The US TVPA provides for significantly higher penalties (up to life imprisonment) for trafficking in persons for organ removal, though these apply to coerced trafficking rather than consensual commercial transactions. Germany's TPG penalties (up to 5 years) align with the NOTA maximum, while the StGB trafficking provisions reach 10 years for serious cases. The Santiago de Compostela Convention requires effective, proportionate, and dissuasive sanctions but does not specify minimum penalties.

### Transplant Tourism

The regulation of transplant tourism -- where patients travel to other jurisdictions to receive organ transplants, potentially from trafficked or exploited donors -- varies significantly. South Korea has been the most proactive, establishing reporting requirements for patients who receive transplants abroad. Most other jurisdictions do not specifically criminalize the act of traveling abroad to receive a transplant, though participating in organ trafficking abroad may fall within general trafficking or organ trading prohibitions.

### Living Donor Protections

Germany's TPG Section 8 provides the most restrictive framework for living donation, limiting eligible donors to close relatives and persons with demonstrably close personal relationships, and requiring independent commission review. South Korea similarly restricts living donation to family members and related persons. The US permits altruistic living donation from unrelated persons (including "Good Samaritan" or non-directed donation), reflecting a different balance between donor autonomy and exploitation prevention.

### Consent for Deceased Donation

The consent model for deceased organ donation directly impacts organ availability and, indirectly, the demand that drives organ trafficking. Opt-out (presumed consent) systems in countries like Spain, France, and Belgium are associated with higher deceased donation rates. South Korea, Germany, and the majority of US states maintain opt-in systems, though Germany debated and rejected opt-out in 2020. The relationship between consent systems and organ trafficking is complex: higher deceased donation rates reduce waiting lists, potentially reducing the incentive for transplant tourism.

### International Cooperation

The Santiago de Compostela Convention provides the most comprehensive international framework, but its limited ratification constrains its effectiveness. The Palermo Protocol (UN Protocol to Prevent, Suppress and Punish Trafficking in Persons) addresses organ trafficking within the broader anti-trafficking framework but lacks the specificity of the Santiago de Compostela Convention. Practical challenges in international cooperation include jurisdictional complexity when trafficking spans multiple countries, difficulty in detecting and investigating covert organ trafficking networks, and political sensitivities surrounding state-sanctioned organ procurement practices.
