## Summary

Sexual assault and rape laws represent one of the most actively evolving areas of criminal law worldwide, with jurisdictions increasingly grappling with the fundamental question of whether the crime should be defined by the perpetrator's use of force or by the absence of the victim's consent. South Korea's Criminal Act still defines rape (ganganjoë) under Article 297 as sexual intercourse accomplished "by means of violence or intimidation," requiring courts to find that the victim was rendered unable to resist — a force-based model that has drawn sustained criticism from human rights organizations and domestic reform advocates. The United States presents a fragmented landscape across 50 states and federal law, with some jurisdictions retaining force-based definitions while others have adopted consent-based or even affirmative-consent standards, particularly following the 2022 federal reform that criminalized knowingly engaging in a sexual act without consent. Germany underwent a landmark transformation in 2016 with its "Nein heißt Nein" (No Means No) reform of Section 177 StGB, shifting from a coercion-based model to one that criminalizes sexual acts performed against the "recognizable will" of another person. At the supranational level, the European Court of Human Rights established in M.C. v. Bulgaria (2003) that member states bear a positive obligation under Articles 3 and 8 of the Convention to criminalize and effectively prosecute all non-consensual sexual acts, not merely those involving physical force — a principle subsequently codified in Article 36 of the Istanbul Convention. The global trend unmistakably moves toward consent-based frameworks, yet significant gaps remain between law on the books and enforcement in practice.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

The Korean Criminal Act (hyeongbeop) addresses sexual violence offenses primarily through Articles 297 through 303, supplemented by the Act on Special Cases Concerning the Punishment of Sexual Crimes (seonpokbeop) and the Sexual Violence Prevention and Victims Protection Act.

**Article 297 — Rape (ganganjoë):** A person who, by means of violence or intimidation, has sexual intercourse with another person shall be punished by imprisonment with labor for a limited term of not less than 3 years. The Supreme Court of Korea has consistently interpreted this provision to require that the violence or intimidation must be of a degree sufficient to render the victim unable to resist — a standard that places an exceptionally high burden on the prosecution.

**Article 298 — Indecent Act by Compulsion:** A person who, by means of violence or intimidation, commits an indecent act against another person shall be punished by imprisonment with labor for not more than 10 years or a fine not exceeding 15 million won.

**Article 299 — Quasi-Rape and Quasi-Indecent Act:** A person who has sexual intercourse or commits an indecent act against another person by taking advantage of the person's state of unconsciousness or inability to resist shall be punished under the same penalties as Articles 297 and 298 respectively. This provision captures incapacitation but does not extend to situations where a conscious person simply does not consent.

| Offense | Provision | Penalty |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Rape | Art. 297 | 3 years to life imprisonment |
| Indecent act by compulsion | Art. 298 | Up to 10 years / 15 million won fine |
| Quasi-rape (incapacitated victim) | Art. 299 | Same as Art. 297 |
| Rape of minor under 13 | Art. 305 | 5 years to life imprisonment |
| Rape resulting in injury | Art. 301 | Life or 5+ years imprisonment |
| Rape resulting in death | Art. 301-2 | Life or 7+ years imprisonment |

### The Consent Reform Debate

South Korea's force-based rape definition has been a focal point of domestic and international criticism. The UN Committee on the Elimination of Discrimination against Women (CEDAW) has repeatedly recommended that South Korea amend its Criminal Act to place the lack of freely given consent at the center of the definition of rape.

**2018 Framework Act:** The Framework Act on Prevention of Violence against Women was enacted on 24 December 2018, mandating government action plans and providing victims with a right to protection, though it did not amend the substantive definition of rape.

**2020 Reforms:** The age of consent was raised from 13 to 16 years through National Assembly reforms enacted on 19 May 2020, addressing longstanding concerns that the previous threshold was among the lowest in developed nations.

**2021-2023 Legislative Efforts:** Multiple proposals were introduced in the National Assembly to adopt a consent-based definition, including by Rep. Jeong Hye-kyung of the Justice Party. The Ministry of Gender Equality and Family pursued a legislative project to revise the rape definition. However, the conservative administration of President Yoon Suk Yeol announced the shutdown of the Ministry of Gender Equality and Family in October 2022, and in February 2023, the government formally cancelled plans to update the definition of rape.

**2025 Presidential Campaign:** Gyeonggi Province Governor Kim Dong-yeon pledged during the presidential campaign to push for redefining rape around the absence of consent, signaling that the issue remains politically active despite prior setbacks.

### Key Judicial Interpretations

Korean courts have applied a "totality of circumstances" test to determine whether the degree of violence or intimidation was sufficient, considering factors such as the time and place of the incident, the relationship between the parties, the age differential, and the specific conduct of the accused. Critics argue that this test effectively puts the victim's behavior on trial, requiring demonstration of physical resistance to prove that force was employed.

## United States

### Legal Framework

U.S. sexual assault law operates across federal, state, and military jurisdictions with substantial variation. The historical common-law definition of rape — "carnal knowledge of a woman forcibly and against her will" — has been progressively reformed since the 1970s, though the pace and direction of reform differ dramatically by jurisdiction.

**Federal Law:** Prior to 2022, federal law lacked a generic statute addressing non-consensual rape outside of specific contexts (federal property, Indian country, military). The VAWA Reauthorization Act of 2022 introduced 18 U.S.C. Section 2244A, criminalizing knowingly engaging in a sexual act with another person without that person's consent, including through coercion. This marked the first general federal sexual assault statute based on absence of consent.

**Military Law (10 U.S.C. Section 920 — Article 120 UCMJ):** The Uniform Code of Military Justice provides a comprehensive definition: consent is a freely given agreement to the conduct at issue by a competent person. An expression of lack of consent through words or conduct means there is no consent; lack of verbal or physical resistance does not constitute consent; and submission resulting from the use of force, threat of force, or placing another person in fear does not constitute consent.

### State Variation: Force vs. Consent Models

States fall along a spectrum between force-based and consent-based definitions:

| Model | Representative States | Key Feature |
|-------|----------------------|-------------|
| Force-based | North Carolina, Mississippi | Prosecution must prove force or threat of force |
| Resistance-diminished | Most states (post-reform) | Force required but resistance by victim is not |
| Consent-based | New York, Wisconsin, Washington | Lack of consent is the central element |
| Affirmative consent (campus) | California, New York, Illinois, Connecticut, Colorado | "Yes means yes" standard for university proceedings |

**Michigan (1974):** Michigan's Criminal Sexual Conduct Act was among the first comprehensive reforms. It eliminated the terms "rape" and "consent," instead defining degrees of criminal sexual conduct based on the presence of force, coercion, or specified circumstances (age, authority relationship, incapacitation).

**New York:** New York Penal Law Section 130.05 defines "lack of consent" as arising from forcible compulsion, incapacity to consent, or any circumstances where the victim does not expressly or impliedly acquiesce. First-degree rape (Section 130.35) requires forcible compulsion, but other degrees capture non-consensual contact.

**California:** While California's criminal rape statute (Penal Code Section 261, replaced by Section 261.6 reforms) historically required force, the state has been a leader in affirmative consent on campuses since SB 967 (2014), requiring "affirmative, conscious, and voluntary agreement to engage in sexual activity."

### Model Penal Code Reform

The American Law Institute (ALI) undertook a revision of the Model Penal Code's sexual assault provisions beginning in 2012. Led by NYU Professor Stephen Schulhofer, the revision sought to shift the MPC definition away from the emphasis on physical force and toward freely expressed consent. In 2016, ALI membership voted overwhelmingly against adopting an "affirmative consent" standard, though the broader reform effort continued. In 2019, the American Bar Association also rejected a resolution urging legislators and courts to adopt affirmative consent standards.

### Landmark Cases

**People v. Barnes, 42 Cal.3d 284 (1986):** The California Supreme Court held that resistance by the victim is not required to prove rape, overturning decades of precedent that had demanded "utmost resistance."

**State of New Jersey v. M.T.S., 129 N.J. 422 (1992):** The New Jersey Supreme Court interpreted the state statute as requiring affirmative and freely-given permission for sexual penetration, finding that any act of sexual penetration without affirmative permission constitutes sexual assault. This decision remains one of the most expansive consent-based judicial interpretations in U.S. law.

## Germany

### Legal Framework Before 2016

Prior to 2016, Section 177 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB) required that sexual assault or rape be accomplished through one of three coercive means: (1) use of force, (2) threat to life or limb, or (3) exploitation of a situation in which the victim is unprotected and at the mercy of the offender. This framework left significant gaps — situations where a victim said "no" but was not subjected to force or explicit threats were not covered, and so-called "shock" or "freeze" responses by victims could mean no crime was recognized.

### The 2016 "Nein heißt Nein" Reform

On 7 July 2016, the German Bundestag passed a comprehensive reform of Section 177 StGB without a single dissenting vote, enacting the "Nein heißt Nein" (No Means No) principle into law. The reform was catalyzed by two events: the Cologne New Year's Eve attacks of 2015-2016, in which approximately 500 women filed complaints of sexual assault by groups of men; and the broader European movement toward consent-based rape definitions following M.C. v. Bulgaria and the Istanbul Convention.

**New Section 177 StGB — Key Provisions:**

The reformed law criminalizes performing sexual acts against the "recognizable will" (erkennbarer Wille) of another person. The recognizable will can be expressed:

- Explicitly through verbal refusal ("no," "stop," or equivalent expressions)
- Implicitly through conduct such as crying, pushing away, freezing, or defensive gestures

| Paragraph | Offense | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| Section 177(1) | Sexual assault against recognizable will | 6 months to 5 years imprisonment |
| Section 177(4) | Severe cases (intercourse, penetration) | 1 to 15 years imprisonment |
| Section 177(5) | Rape through force or threat | 2 to 15 years imprisonment |
| Section 177(6) | Aggravated sexual assault (weapon, group, danger to life) | 5 to 15 years imprisonment |
| Section 177(7) | Sexual assault causing death | 10 years to life imprisonment |
| Section 177(8) | Less serious cases of (5)-(7) | 1 to 10 years imprisonment |

**New Section 184i StGB — Sexual Harassment:** Introduced alongside the reform, this provision criminalizes physical sexual touching that is harassing, punishable by up to 2 years imprisonment or a fine.

**New Section 184j StGB — Offenses Committed from Groups:** Directly responding to the Cologne attacks, this provision criminalizes participation in a group that encourages another person in the group to commit a sexual offense, punishable by up to 2 years imprisonment or a fine.

### "No Means No" vs. "Only Yes Means Yes"

Germany's reform adopted a "no means no" model rather than an affirmative consent ("only yes means yes") model. The distinction is significant: under the German approach, sexual conduct is criminal only when the victim's opposing will is recognizable. If the victim neither expressly nor implicitly signals refusal — including cases of "freezing" without visible external signs — the conduct may not be captured. Advocates for affirmative consent models argue that only a "yes means yes" standard adequately addresses the reality that many victims are unable to express refusal due to fear, shock, or power dynamics.

### Implementation and Assessment

Legal scholars have noted that while the reform represented a historic shift, practical challenges remain. Prosecutors must still prove that the accused recognized or should have recognized the victim's opposing will. The "recognizability" standard introduces a quasi-objective element: the question is not merely whether the victim internally opposed the act, but whether that opposition was externally discernible.

## European Framework

### M.C. v. Bulgaria (Application No. 39272/98, 2003)

The landmark case of M.C. v. Bulgaria before the European Court of Human Rights (ECHR) established the foundational principle that states have a positive obligation to criminalize and effectively investigate all non-consensual sexual acts.

**Facts:** The applicant, M.C., was 14 years old when she alleged that two men raped her on 31 July and 1 August 1995. The Bulgarian authorities investigated but discontinued the prosecution, concluding that there was insufficient evidence of force or resistance. The applicant argued that Bulgaria's requirement of proof of physical resistance violated her rights under the European Convention on Human Rights.

**Judgment:** The ECHR held unanimously that Bulgaria had violated its positive obligations under Article 3 (prohibition of torture and inhuman or degrading treatment) and Article 8 (right to respect for private and family life). The Court stated:

The Court found that member states must penalize and effectively prosecute any non-consensual sexual act, including where the victim has not physically resisted. The Court drew on international criminal law, stating that "any sexual penetration without the victim's consent constitutes rape and that consent must be given voluntarily, as a result of the person's free will, assessed in the context of the surrounding circumstances."

**Significance:** The judgment established that a legal framework requiring proof of physical force or resistance to prosecute rape is incompatible with the Convention. States must ensure that their laws and investigative practices do not leave certain types of rape — particularly acquaintance rape and situations where the victim freezes — effectively unpunished.

### Istanbul Convention (CETS No. 210)

The Council of Europe Convention on Preventing and Combating Violence against Women and Domestic Violence (Istanbul Convention), opened for signature in 2011 and entered into force in 2014, was directly influenced by M.C. v. Bulgaria.

**Article 36 — Sexual Violence, Including Rape:** Requires parties to criminalize:

1. Engaging in non-consensual vaginal, anal, or oral penetration of a sexual nature of the body of another person with any bodily part or object
2. Engaging in other non-consensual acts of a sexual nature with a person
3. Causing another person to engage in non-consensual acts of a sexual nature with a third person

Article 36(2) specifies that "consent must be given voluntarily as the result of the person's free will of the person assessed in the context of the surrounding circumstances."

**Ratification Status:** As of 2025, 38 countries and the European Union have ratified the Convention, including Germany, France, Spain, Italy, Sweden, Denmark, and Finland. Turkey withdrew from the Convention in 2021. Despite ratification, Amnesty International research found that only approximately 16 out of 31 European countries analyzed had definitions of rape based on the absence of consent as required by the Convention.

### GREVIO Monitoring

The Group of Experts on Action against Violence against Women and Domestic Violence (GREVIO) monitors implementation of the Istanbul Convention. GREVIO reports have consistently found gaps between the Convention's consent-based requirements and domestic legislation in many ratifying states, recommending legislative amendments to align national rape definitions with Article 36.

## Comparative Analysis

### Consent-Based vs. Force-Based Models

The fundamental fault line in global sexual assault law divides jurisdictions into those that define the crime through the lens of force (what the perpetrator did) and those that define it through the lens of consent (what the victim agreed to). Within consent-based approaches, a further division exists between "no means no" models (where expressed refusal is required) and "only yes means yes" or affirmative consent models (where the absence of affirmative agreement suffices).

| Dimension | South Korea | United States (Majority) | Germany (Post-2016) | ECHR/Istanbul Convention |
|-----------|-------------|-------------------------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| **Model** | Force-based | Varies (force to consent) | "No means no" (consent-based) | Consent-based (free will) |
| **Core element** | Violence or intimidation | Varies by state | Against recognizable will | Non-consensual penetration/act |
| **Physical resistance required** | Effectively yes (judicial interpretation) | Generally no (post-reform) | No | No |
| **Freeze response captured** | Partially (quasi-rape only) | Varies | Partially (if recognizable) | Yes (contextual assessment) |
| **Marital rape** | Criminalized (2013 Supreme Court ruling) | Criminalized (all states by 1993) | Criminalized (1997) | Required to be criminalized |
| **Age of consent** | 16 (raised from 13 in 2020) | 16-18 (varies by state) | 14 | Set by member states |
| **Gender-neutral** | Yes (2013 revision) | Yes (most states) | Yes | Yes |
| **Penalty range (basic rape)** | 3 years to life | Varies (typically 5-25 years) | 1 to 15 years | Set by member states |

### Persistent Challenges Across Jurisdictions

Regardless of the statutory model, all jurisdictions face common enforcement challenges: low reporting rates (estimated at 10-20% globally), high attrition rates through the criminal justice process, evidentiary difficulties in proving subjective mental states, the influence of rape myths on juror decision-making, and secondary victimization of complainants during investigation and trial.
