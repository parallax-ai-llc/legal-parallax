## Summary

Platform liability (플랫폼 책임) -- the legal responsibility of internet intermediaries for content posted, transmitted, or hosted by their users -- represents one of the most consequential and evolving areas of internet law, fundamentally shaping the structure of online communication, commerce, and public discourse. South Korea's approach operates through the Information and Communications Network Act (정보통신망 이용촉진 및 정보보호 등에 관한 법률, commonly known as the ICN Act), which imposes notice-and-takedown obligations on internet service providers and was significantly amended in 2024 to expand platform transparency requirements and algorithmic accountability; the Telecommunications Business Act (전기통신사업법) and the Korea Communications Standards Commission (방송통신심의위원회) provide additional regulatory mechanisms for content moderation and platform governance. The United States operates under the globally influential Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act (47 U.S.C. Section 230, 1996), which provides broad immunity to interactive computer services for third-party content and for good-faith content moderation decisions -- a framework upheld by the Supreme Court in Moody v. NetChoice (2024) but facing increasing bipartisan calls for reform. Germany enacted the Netzwerkdurchsetzungsgesetz (NetzDG, Network Enforcement Act) in 2017, the first major national law imposing systematic content removal obligations on social media platforms, requiring platforms to remove "manifestly unlawful" content within 24 hours and other illegal content within 7 days, with fines up to 50 million EUR for systemic non-compliance; the NetzDG has been substantially supplemented by the EU Digital Services Act. At the EU level, the Digital Services Act (Regulation (EU) 2022/2065), fully applicable since February 17, 2024, replaces the E-Commerce Directive's intermediary liability framework with a comprehensive system of tiered due diligence obligations, notice-and-action mechanisms, transparency requirements, and systemic risk assessments for very large online platforms, while the European Court of Human Rights has developed significant jurisprudence balancing platform liability against freedom of expression in cases including Delfi AS v. Estonia (2015) and Magyar Tartalomszolgáltatók Egyesülete v. Hungary (2016).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's platform liability framework reflects the country's high internet penetration and the government's active approach to content regulation.

**Information and Communications Network Act (정보통신망 이용촉진 및 정보보호 등에 관한 법률):** The ICN Act, first enacted in 2001 and amended numerous times, is the primary statute governing platform liability. Article 44-2 establishes a notice-and-takedown system for information that infringes individual rights. Upon receiving a complaint, the platform operator must take temporary measures (blocking or deletion) within 30 days, notify the content uploader, and allow for counter-notification. If the uploader objects, the content may be restored after the 30-day period unless the complainant obtains a court order.

**Article 44-7 (Distribution of Prohibited Information):** This provision prohibits the distribution of certain categories of information through telecommunications networks, including content that violates criminal law, infringes intellectual property rights, or constitutes defamation. Platform operators who knowingly fail to remove prohibited content may face administrative sanctions and, in egregious cases, criminal liability.

### Korea Communications Standards Commission (방송통신심의위원회, KCSC)

The KCSC exercises broad content review authority, with the power to order internet service providers and platform operators to block or delete content that violates Korean law or KCSC standards. The Commission reviews content on referral from government agencies, public complaints, or its own monitoring. In 2024, the KCSC issued over 180,000 content correction orders, covering defamation, obscenity, gambling, and false information categories.

### 2024 Platform Transparency Amendments

The 2024 amendments to the Telecommunications Business Act introduced new obligations for large platform operators (대규모 플랫폼 사업자) including:
- Publication of semi-annual transparency reports detailing content moderation actions, government requests, and algorithmic recommendation systems.
- Establishment of internal compliance teams and user appeal mechanisms.
- Cooperation with the Korea Communications Commission (한국방송통신위원회) on systemic risk assessments.

### Defamation and Platform Liability

South Korea's criminal defamation regime significantly affects platform liability. Under Article 70 of the ICN Act, the distribution of false information through telecommunications networks with intent to defame carries penalties of up to 7 years imprisonment. This provision has been criticized for its chilling effect on online speech, but the Constitutional Court upheld its constitutionality in 2016.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act has shaped the American internet ecosystem and influenced global debates on platform liability since its enactment in 1996.

**Section 230(c)(1) -- Publisher Immunity:** "No provider or user of an interactive computer service shall be treated as the publisher or speaker of any information provided by another information content provider." This provision shields platforms from liability for hosting or transmitting third-party content, even if they are aware of the content and choose not to remove it.

**Section 230(c)(2) -- Good Samaritan Protection:** Platforms are protected from liability for good-faith content moderation actions, including "any action voluntarily taken in good faith to restrict access to or availability of material that the provider or user considers to be obscene, lewd, lascivious, filthy, excessively violent, harassing, or otherwise objectionable."

**Exceptions to Section 230:** Federal criminal law, intellectual property claims (though the Ninth Circuit has extended Section 230 to some IP contexts), and the FOSTA-SESTA amendments (2018) creating exceptions for sex trafficking facilitation.

### Supreme Court Jurisprudence

**Moody v. NetChoice, LLC (2024):** The Supreme Court addressed state laws (Florida and Texas) restricting platforms' content moderation abilities, holding that platforms' editorial decisions are protected by the First Amendment but remanding for further analysis of the laws' scope. The decision reaffirmed that platforms' content curation is expressive activity.

**Gonzalez v. Google LLC (2023):** The Court declined to reach the question of whether algorithmic content recommendation transforms a platform from a neutral host to an active publisher, vacating the Ninth Circuit's decision on narrow grounds without resolving the scope of Section 230.

**Twitter, Inc. v. Taamneh (2023):** The Court held that platforms do not "aid and abet" terrorism merely by hosting user-generated content, even content posted by terrorist organizations, absent more specific assistance.

### Reform Proposals

Section 230 reform has attracted bipartisan attention, though from opposing perspectives. Republican proposals generally seek to condition Section 230 immunity on political neutrality in content moderation. Democratic proposals generally seek to impose liability for algorithmic amplification of harmful content. Key proposals include the EARN IT Act (conditioning immunity on child safety compliance), the SAFE TECH Act (narrowing immunity for paid content), and the Platform Accountability and Transparency Act (mandating researcher data access).

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany pioneered statutory platform content removal obligations through the NetzDG, influencing similar legislation globally.

**Netzwerkdurchsetzungsgesetz (NetzDG, Network Enforcement Act, 2017):** Enacted on September 1, 2017, and effective October 1, 2017, the NetzDG applies to social network platforms with more than 2 million registered users in Germany. Key obligations:

**Content Removal Timeframes:**
- Manifestly unlawful content (offensichtlich rechtswidrig): removal within 24 hours of notification.
- Other unlawful content: removal within 7 days of notification, with possibility of extension for complex cases through referral to a regulated self-regulatory body.

**Complaint Management:** Platforms must establish accessible and transparent complaint procedures, designate a domestic representative for service of process, and maintain a permanent German-speaking compliance contact.

**Reporting:** Biannual transparency reports detailing the number and categories of complaints received, response times, content removal actions, and staffing.

**Penalties:** Systemic non-compliance (not individual content decisions) is subject to fines of up to 50 million EUR. The Federal Office of Justice (Bundesamt für Justiz, BfJ) serves as the enforcement authority.

### 2021 NetzDG Amendments

The NetzDG was amended in 2021 to introduce:
- **User Appeal Rights:** Users whose content is removed must be informed of the reasons and given the opportunity to appeal through the platform's internal complaint mechanism.
- **Reporting Obligations to BKA:** Platforms must report certain categories of content (terrorism, child sexual abuse, threat of violence) to the Federal Criminal Police Office (Bundeskriminalamt, BKA), including user identification data.
- **Counter-Notification:** Content uploaders must be notified of complaints and allowed to respond before content is removed (with exceptions for clearly illegal content).

### Transition to the Digital Services Act

With the DSA's full applicability from February 17, 2024, Germany enacted the Digitale-Dienste-Gesetz (DDG, Digital Services Act Implementation Law) in May 2024. The DDG designates the Bundesnetzagentur as the national DSA Coordinator, supplemented by sectoral authorities. The NetzDG remains applicable for provisions not superseded by the DSA, though its scope has been significantly reduced.

### Overblocking Concerns

The NetzDG has faced criticism for incentivizing overblocking -- platforms removing lawful content to avoid regulatory risk. Studies by the Hans-Bredow-Institut and the Alexander von Humboldt Institute for Internet and Society have documented instances where platforms removed legally protected speech, particularly political satire and journalistic content, under NetzDG pressure.

## ECHR & EU

### Digital Services Act (Regulation (EU) 2022/2065)

The DSA, fully applicable since February 17, 2024, establishes the most comprehensive platform liability and governance framework globally:

**Liability Framework (Articles 4-8):** The DSA updates the E-Commerce Directive's intermediary liability regime. Mere conduit, caching, and hosting providers retain conditional liability exemptions. Hosting providers (including platforms) lose their exemption when they obtain actual knowledge of illegal content and fail to act expeditiously to remove or disable access. Crucially, Article 7 provides that voluntary own-initiative investigations do not result in loss of the liability exemption -- addressing the NetzDG overblocking concern.

**Tiered Due Diligence Obligations:**
- **All intermediary services:** Single point of contact, legal representative in EU, transparency reports, cooperation with authorities.
- **Hosting services:** Notice-and-action mechanisms with specific requirements for notification content and reasoned decisions.
- **Online platforms:** Internal complaint handling, out-of-court dispute settlement, trusted flagger systems, protection against misuse, advertising transparency, online marketplace trader verification.
- **Very Large Online Platforms (VLOPs) and VLOSEs:** Systemic risk assessments, mitigation measures, independent audits, data access for researchers, compliance officers, crisis response protocols.

**Enforcement:** The European Commission has direct enforcement authority over designated VLOPs and VLOSEs, with fines up to 6 percent of global annual turnover for non-compliance. The Commission has opened proceedings against X (formerly Twitter), TikTok, AliExpress, and Meta.

### E-Commerce Directive 2000/31/EC (Foundational Framework)

The E-Commerce Directive established the original intermediary liability exemptions (Articles 12-15) that remain influential globally. Article 15 prohibited general monitoring obligations -- a principle preserved by DSA Article 8. The Directive's liability regime was limited by the absence of harmonized notice-and-action procedures, which the DSA now provides.

### European Court of Human Rights

**Delfi AS v. Estonia (Application No. 64569/09, Grand Chamber Judgment of June 16, 2015):** The Grand Chamber held that Estonian courts' imposition of liability on a commercial news portal for defamatory user comments did not violate Article 10. The Court found that the portal ran the comments section for economic benefit, exercised a degree of control through automatic filters, and the comments at issue were clearly defamatory. The judgment is limited to professional, commercially operated news portals with comment sections.

**Magyar Tartalomszolgáltatók Egyesülete (MTE) v. Hungary (Application No. 22947/13, Judgment of February 2, 2016):** The Court found an Article 10 violation where a Hungarian court held a non-profit internet content provider and a self-regulatory body liable for offensive (but not defamatory) user comments. The Court distinguished Delfi, emphasizing that the comments were merely offensive rather than constituting hate speech or incitement, and that the defendants were not commercial entities profiting from user engagement.

**Sanchez v. France (Application No. 45581/15, Grand Chamber Judgment of May 15, 2023):** The Grand Chamber found no Article 10 violation where a French politician was convicted for failing to promptly delete anti-Roma comments on his public Facebook campaign page. The Court held that public figures managing social media for political purposes bear heightened responsibility for user comments on their accounts.

## Comparative Analysis

### Immunity vs. Obligation Models

The most fundamental divide is between the US immunity model and the EU obligation model. Section 230 starts from the premise that platforms should not be liable for user content, with narrow exceptions. The EU model (DSA, NetzDG) starts from the premise that platforms bear responsibility for managing illegal content, with conditional exemptions from liability. South Korea occupies a middle position, imposing notice-and-takedown obligations while not requiring proactive monitoring.

### Content Removal Incentives

The NetzDG's short timeframes (24 hours, 7 days) with severe penalties created incentives for overblocking. The DSA's "without undue delay" standard with Article 7's good Samaritan protection aims to address this by removing the disincentive for voluntary investigation. Section 230(c)(2) also protects voluntary moderation but imposes no affirmative obligation.

### Transparency and Accountability

The DSA imposes the most comprehensive transparency requirements, including systemic risk assessments and researcher data access for VLOPs. Germany's NetzDG pioneered transparency reporting. South Korea's 2024 amendments adopt similar requirements. The US Section 230 framework imposes no transparency obligations, though platform voluntarily publish transparency reports.

### Cross-Border Enforcement

The EU's establishment of the DSA Coordinator network and the Commission's direct enforcement authority over VLOPs represents the most developed cross-border enforcement mechanism. The NetzDG's domestic representative requirement influenced the DSA's legal representative provisions. US enforcement relies on general FTC authority and litigation. South Korea's enforcement is primarily domestic.
