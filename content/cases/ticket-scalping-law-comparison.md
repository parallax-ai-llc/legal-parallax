## Summary

Ticket scalping -- the resale of event tickets at prices above their face value, typically without authorization from the original issuer -- has evolved from a street-level petty offense into a sophisticated global industry involving automated purchasing software ("bots"), professional resale platforms, and complex legal questions about property rights, consumer protection, and market regulation. South Korea criminalized ticket scalping through amendments to the Public Performance Act (공연법) and the Ticket Resale Prevention Act (암표 방지법), with enhanced penalties enacted following widespread public anger over bot-driven scalping of K-pop concert tickets, particularly BTS and BLACKPINK concerts where resale prices exceeded ten times face value; penalties include up to one year imprisonment or fines of up to ten million KRW for unauthorized resale. The United States has no federal anti-scalping law, with regulation left entirely to the states -- approximately half of which have anti-scalping statutes ranging from outright prohibition (Connecticut) to regulated resale with disclosure requirements (New York), while others have repealed scalping laws in favor of free market approaches; the BOTS Act of 2016 (15 U.S.C. Section 45c) specifically prohibits the use of automated software to circumvent ticket purchasing limits. Germany has no specific anti-scalping statute but addresses the issue through general fraud provisions (Section 263 StGB, Betrug) when tickets are obtained through deceptive means, the Gesetz gegen den unlauteren Wettbewerb (UWG, Act Against Unfair Competition) when resale involves deceptive business practices, and terms-of-service enforcement by event organizers. At the European level, the EU Consumer Rights Directive (2011/83/EU) establishes transparency requirements for online ticket sales, the Digital Services Act (2022/2065) provides tools for combating bot-driven purchasing, and several member states have enacted specific anti-scalping legislation following widespread concern about access to major cultural and sporting events.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's anti-scalping legislation has been driven primarily by the K-pop industry and the cultural importance of live entertainment access.

### Public Performance Act (공연법)

The Act was amended to specifically address ticket scalping. Article 36-2 prohibits the unauthorized resale of performance tickets at prices exceeding face value.

### Penalty Structure

| Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|
| Unauthorized resale above face value | Up to 1 year imprisonment or fine up to 10 million KRW |
| Use of automated purchasing software (bots) | Up to 1 year imprisonment or fine up to 10 million KRW |
| Organized scalping operation | Enhanced penalties under general criminal law provisions |
| Platform facilitating unauthorized resale | Administrative sanctions; fines |

### K-pop Scalping Crisis

The scalping problem in South Korea reached crisis proportions with the global explosion of K-pop fandom. Reports documented BTS concert tickets with face values of 130,000-180,000 KRW being resold for 1,500,000-3,000,000 KRW on secondary markets. BLACKPINK concert tickets similarly saw resale markups of 500-1,000 percent. The Korea Consumer Agency (한국소비자원) received thousands of complaints annually about scalped tickets, fraudulent ticket sales, and bot-driven purchasing that prevented genuine fans from accessing tickets at face value.

### Identity Verification Systems

In response to scalping, South Korean ticketing platforms have implemented identity verification systems requiring purchasers to link tickets to their real-name identification (실명인증). Platforms such as Interpark (인터파크), YES24, and Melon Ticket require verification at both purchase and entry, making ticket transfer difficult and reducing the secondary market. However, scalpers have adapted by offering "accompanying entry" services and other workarounds.

### Legislative History

The initial anti-scalping provisions were enacted in 2016, strengthened in 2020, and further enhanced in 2023. The 2023 amendments specifically addressed bot purchasing and imposed obligations on ticketing platforms to implement technical measures preventing automated bulk purchasing.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States lacks a comprehensive federal anti-scalping statute. The BOTS Act of 2016 addresses one specific aspect -- automated purchasing -- while leaving broader scalping regulation to the states.

**Better Online Tickets Sales (BOTS) Act of 2016 (15 U.S.C. § 45c):** Prohibits the use of automated software to circumvent security measures on ticket purchasing websites, purchase tickets in excess of posted limits, or hold tickets pending entry of payment information. The FTC enforces the Act through civil penalties.

### State Approaches

| Approach | States | Key Features |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| **Prohibition** | Connecticut, parts of other states | Resale above face value prohibited |
| **Regulated resale** | New York, Illinois, California | Permitted with disclosure requirements; venue proximity restrictions repealed |
| **Minimal regulation** | Texas, Ohio, Missouri | Repealed anti-scalping laws; free market approach |
| **Licensed resale** | Some localities | Resellers must obtain licenses |

**New York Arts and Cultural Affairs Law Section 25.01-25.35:** New York's framework requires ticket resellers to disclose the face value of tickets, that they are a reseller (not the original venue), and the total price including fees. The law was substantially amended in 2010 and 2016 to address online resale.

### FTC Enforcement

The FTC has brought enforcement actions under the BOTS Act and Section 5 of the FTC Act (unfair or deceptive practices). In 2021, the FTC secured a $31 million judgment against ticket resellers who used bots to purchase and resell tickets for events including concerts and Broadway shows.

### Live Event Industry Self-Regulation

Major ticketing companies have implemented their own anti-scalping measures. Ticketmaster's "Verified Fan" program uses algorithms to identify genuine fans and provide them priority access. The Verified Ticket initiative by the National Association of Ticket Brokers establishes industry standards for legitimate resale.

### Antitrust Considerations

The ticket resale market has attracted antitrust scrutiny. The Department of Justice investigated Live Nation/Ticketmaster's dominance of the primary ticket market and potential anticompetitive effects on secondary market competition. In 2024, the DOJ filed an antitrust lawsuit against Live Nation Entertainment, alleging monopolization of the live entertainment industry.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany does not have a specific anti-scalping statute. Ticket resale is addressed through general legal frameworks.

**Section 263 StGB (Betrug -- Fraud):** When tickets are obtained through deception (such as using false identities to circumvent purchase limits), the acquisition itself may constitute fraud. Penalties range up to five years' imprisonment for basic fraud.

**Gesetz gegen den unlauteren Wettbewerb (UWG -- Act Against Unfair Competition):** Commercial ticket scalping may constitute unfair commercial practice under the UWG, particularly when resellers misrepresent their status or the availability of tickets.

### Terms of Service Enforcement

German courts have generally upheld event organizers' contractual restrictions on ticket resale. The Bundesgerichtshof (BGH) ruled in BGH I ZR 73/12 (2017) that organizers may include transferability restrictions in ticket terms and conditions, and that tickets resold in violation of these terms may be invalidated at the venue. This approach effectively permits private anti-scalping enforcement through contract law.

### Major Event Experiences

Germany's experience hosting major events has shaped the scalping debate:

**2006 FIFA World Cup:** Scalping of World Cup tickets became a major public concern, with tickets for the final resold at up to twenty times face value. FIFA's anti-scalping measures, including personalized tickets, were partially effective but did not eliminate the secondary market.

**Concert Industry:** Major concert promoters in Germany, including CTS Eventim (Europe's largest ticketing company), have implemented personalized ticketing and anti-bot measures. CTS Eventim's headquarters are in Bremen, and the company has been at the forefront of European anti-scalping technology.

### Consumer Protection

The Verbraucherzentrale (Consumer Protection Agency) has issued guidance to consumers about the risks of purchasing tickets on the secondary market, including the risk of invalidation at entry, fraudulent sales, and inflated prices. The agency has advocated for specific anti-scalping legislation, though no such legislation has been enacted at the federal level.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Consumer Rights Directive (2011/83/EU)

The Directive establishes fundamental transparency requirements applicable to ticket sales:

- Article 6: Information requirements for distance contracts, including clear disclosure of total price, payment and delivery arrangements, and the right of withdrawal.
- Article 16(l): Exemption from the right of withdrawal for contracts relating to leisure activities where a specific date or period of performance is provided, which typically applies to event tickets.

### Digital Services Act (Regulation 2022/2065)

The DSA provides tools relevant to combating online ticket scalping:

- Article 14: Requires online intermediaries to include conditions in their terms of service addressing the misuse of their services, which platforms can use to prohibit bot-driven ticket purchasing.
- Article 15: Requires transparency reports from online platforms on content moderation activities, including actions taken against fraudulent ticket listings.

### EU Consumer Protection Cooperation (CPC) Network

The CPC Network has coordinated enforcement actions against deceptive ticket resale practices across the EU. In 2018, the CPC Network secured commitments from major secondary ticket platforms (Viagogo, StubHub) to comply with EU consumer protection rules, including transparent pricing and clear disclosure of reseller status.

### Member State Approaches

| Country | Approach |
|---------|----------|
| France | Loi Macron (2015): resale above face value prohibited for sports events; cultural events regulated |
| United Kingdom (pre-Brexit) | Consumer Rights Act 2015: transparency requirements; Digital Economy Act 2017: bot prohibition |
| Italy | Legislation prohibiting resale above face value for sporting events |
| Belgium | Specific anti-scalping legislation for major events |
| Ireland | Sale of Tickets (Cultural, Entertainment, Recreational and Sporting Events) Act 2021 |

### UEFA and FIFA Regulations

Major sports governing bodies enforce their own anti-scalping rules for events within the EU. UEFA's ticketing conditions prohibit unauthorized resale of tickets for Champions League and European Championship matches, with tickets subject to cancellation. FIFA applies similar rules for World Cup events. These private enforcement mechanisms complement state regulation.

## Comparative Analysis

### Regulatory Philosophy

The jurisdictions reflect distinct regulatory philosophies. South Korea treats ticket scalping as a consumer protection issue requiring criminal prohibition. The United States generally favors market-based approaches, with the BOTS Act targeting only the specific tool (automated software) rather than the practice itself. Germany relies on private enforcement through contract law and general consumer protection frameworks. The EU focuses on transparency and information requirements rather than price controls.

### Bot Regulation

All jurisdictions have recognized that automated purchasing software creates an unfair playing field. The United States addressed this specifically through the BOTS Act. South Korea's 2023 amendments include bot prohibitions. The EU's Digital Services Act provides platform-level tools. Germany relies on general fraud provisions.

### Effectiveness

The effectiveness of anti-scalping measures remains contested. South Korea's identity verification systems have reduced but not eliminated scalping. U.S. state-level restrictions have had limited impact given interstate commerce in tickets. Germany's contract-law approach is effective where enforced at venues but does not address the secondary market broadly. EU-level harmonization remains elusive.
