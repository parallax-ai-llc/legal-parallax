## Summary

Telemedicine -- the delivery of healthcare services through telecommunications technology -- has undergone a dramatic legal transformation worldwide, accelerated by the COVID-19 pandemic. Regulatory frameworks must balance the benefits of improved access and efficiency against concerns about patient safety, quality of care, data protection, and professional licensing. South Korea historically maintained strict restrictions on telemedicine under the Medical Service Act (의료법), limiting remote consultations to specific circumstances such as patients on islands or in remote areas, but the pandemic prompted temporary emergency measures and a 2024 reform initiative by the Yoon administration to permanently allow non-face-to-face medical treatment (비대면 진료), which has generated fierce opposition from the Korean Medical Association. The United States regulates telemedicine through a complex patchwork of federal and state laws; the pandemic-era waivers under the CARES Act (Pub. L. 116-136) and CMS flexibility declarations dramatically expanded telehealth access for Medicare beneficiaries, while the Ryan Haight Online Pharmacy Consumer Protection Act (21 U.S.C. Section 831) governs controlled substance prescribing via telehealth, with the DEA extending temporary flexibilities through 2025. Germany lifted its longstanding ban on remote-only treatment (Fernbehandlungsverbot) at the 121st German Medical Assembly (Aerztetag) in 2018 by amending Section 7(4) of the Model Professional Code (Muster-Berufsordnung), and the Digital Healthcare Act (Digitale-Versorgung-Gesetz, DVG, 2019) established a framework for digital health applications (DiGA) reimbursable by statutory health insurance. The EU framework is shaped by the Cross-Border Healthcare Directive (2011/24/EU), the eHealth Network, and the emerging European Health Data Space (EHDS) Regulation proposed in 2022, while the ECHR protects patient autonomy and access to healthcare under Articles 2, 3, and 8.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's telemedicine regulation has been among the most restrictive in the developed world. The **Medical Service Act** (의료법) establishes the fundamental framework:

- **Article 33 (Establishment of Medical Institutions):** Requires that medical services be provided at registered medical institutions, establishing a presumption of in-person care.
- **Article 34 (Telemedicine):** Originally permitted telemedicine only between healthcare providers (doctor-to-doctor consultations) and for patients in medically underserved areas (islands, remote mountainous regions). Direct doctor-to-patient telemedicine was not authorized.

The **Pharmaceutical Affairs Act** (약사법) further restricted telemedicine by requiring in-person prescribing for most medications and prohibiting the remote prescribing of narcotics and psychotropic substances under the Narcotics Control Act (마약류 관리에 관한 법률).

### COVID-19 Emergency Measures

On February 24, 2020, the Ministry of Health and Welfare issued an emergency administrative order temporarily permitting telephone-based medical consultations for all patients. This measure was extended repeatedly throughout the pandemic period and remained in effect through 2023. During this period:

- Over **40 million** telephone consultations were conducted (2020-2023).
- Prescriptions could be transmitted electronically to pharmacies designated by the patient.
- Patient satisfaction surveys showed 72% approval for telephone consultations (Korean Institute for Health and Social Affairs, 2022).

### 2024 Telemedicine Reform Initiative

On February 23, 2024, the Ministry of Health and Welfare announced a comprehensive telemedicine reform plan:

1. **Non-Face-to-Face Medical Treatment (비대면 진료):** Permanent authorization of video and telephone consultations for initial and follow-up visits across all medical specialties.
2. **Prescription Expansion:** Electronic transmission of prescriptions to any pharmacy, with continued restrictions on narcotics and specific controlled substances.
3. **Platform Regulation:** Telemedicine platforms must register with the Ministry and comply with security, informed consent, and record-keeping requirements.
4. **Quality Safeguards:** Physicians must inform patients of limitations of remote diagnosis and recommend in-person visits where clinically appropriate.

The reform met immediate opposition from the **Korean Medical Association (KMA)**, which argued that telemedicine would compromise patient safety, enable "convenience shopping" for prescriptions, and disproportionately benefit large hospital systems and technology platforms at the expense of small clinics. The KMA's concerns were amplified by the broader 2024 medical crisis, in which thousands of trainee doctors resigned over government plans to increase medical school enrollment. As of early 2026, the reform legislation remains under parliamentary review.

### Data Protection

Telemedicine data is governed by the **Personal Information Protection Act (PIPA, 개인정보 보호법)**, which requires:

- Explicit consent for collection of health data (classified as sensitive information under Article 23).
- Encryption of health data in transit and at rest.
- Mandatory data breach notification within 72 hours.
- Prohibition on transfer of health data to third countries without adequate protection levels.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Telemedicine regulation in the United States involves a complex interplay of federal and state laws. There is no single federal telemedicine statute; instead, the regulatory framework emerges from Medicare/Medicaid rules, the Ryan Haight Act, HIPAA, state medical practice acts, and various emergency declarations.

### Federal Framework

**Medicare Telehealth (Social Security Act Section 1834(m); 42 CFR Section 410.78):** Prior to the pandemic, Medicare covered telehealth services only in limited circumstances -- the patient had to be located at an "originating site" in a rural Health Professional Shortage Area (HPSA), and only specific services were covered. The **CARES Act (2020)** and associated CMS waivers dramatically expanded coverage:

- Removed geographic restrictions on originating sites.
- Allowed the patient's home as an originating site.
- Expanded the list of covered telehealth services from approximately 100 to over 250 HCPCS codes.
- Authorized audio-only (telephone) consultations for Medicare reimbursement.
- Allowed Federally Qualified Health Centers (FQHCs) and Rural Health Clinics (RHCs) to serve as distant-site providers.

The **Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2023** extended many of these flexibilities through December 31, 2024, and subsequent legislation extended key provisions through 2025. The **CONNECT for Health Act**, introduced repeatedly since 2017, seeks to make geographic flexibility permanent but has not been enacted as of early 2026.

**Ryan Haight Online Pharmacy Consumer Protection Act (2008, 21 U.S.C. Section 831):** Requires a valid prescription for controlled substances, which in turn requires at least one in-person medical evaluation. During the pandemic, the DEA exercised enforcement discretion to waive the in-person requirement. In March 2023, the DEA proposed rules to create a permanent "Special Registration" for telemedicine prescribing of controlled substances, but these rules have not been finalized. Temporary flexibilities have been extended through 2025.

**HIPAA (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act, 45 CFR Parts 160, 164):** During the pandemic, the Office for Civil Rights (OCR) exercised enforcement discretion regarding HIPAA requirements for telehealth platforms, allowing the use of non-HIPAA-compliant platforms (FaceTime, Zoom, Skype). This enforcement discretion was gradually withdrawn after the public health emergency ended on May 11, 2023, and all telehealth platforms must now comply with HIPAA Security and Privacy Rules.

### State Regulation

State regulation of telemedicine varies significantly:

- **Licensure:** Physicians generally must hold a license in the state where the patient is located. The **Interstate Medical Licensure Compact (IMLC)**, joined by 40 states and the District of Columbia as of 2025, provides an expedited pathway for physicians to obtain licenses in multiple states.
- **Prescribing:** States vary on whether an initial in-person visit is required before prescribing via telehealth. The majority of states now permit prescribing based on a telehealth encounter alone for non-controlled substances.
- **Parity Laws:** Approximately 40 states have enacted "telehealth parity" laws requiring private insurers to cover telehealth services on the same terms as in-person services, though definitions of "parity" vary.
- **Informed Consent:** Most states require providers to obtain informed consent specific to telehealth, including disclosure of risks, limitations, and data security measures.

|--------------------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| In-person visit before prescribing (non-controlled) | ~10 | ~40 |
| Telehealth parity for private insurance | ~40 | ~10 |
| Specific telehealth informed consent | ~35 | ~15 |

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's telemedicine regulation has evolved from one of the strictest in Europe to a relatively permissive framework, driven by legislative reform and professional code amendments.

### The Fernbehandlungsverbot and Its Lifting (2018)

Historically, German medical professional law prohibited remote-only treatment (Fernbehandlungsverbot). Section 7(4) of the **Model Professional Code for Physicians** (Muster-Berufsordnung fuer die in Deutschland taetigen Aerztinnen und Aerzte, MBO-Ae) stated that physicians could treat patients only on the basis of direct personal contact.

At the **121st German Medical Assembly (Aerztetag)** in Erfurt on May 10, 2018, the profession voted to amend Section 7(4) MBO-Ae to permit:

> "Counseling or treatment exclusively via communication media... if this is medically justifiable and the required medical care is maintained, particularly through the manner in which findings are gathered, counseling is provided, treatment is carried out, and documentation is maintained."

This amendment opened the door to initial consultations conducted entirely via video or telephone, subject to clinical appropriateness. However, because each of Germany's 17 **State Medical Chambers (Landesaerztekammern)** must independently adopt amendments to the MBO-Ae, implementation varied. By 2020, all state chambers had adopted the amendment, though some imposed additional conditions.

### Digital Healthcare Act (Digitale-Versorgung-Gesetz, DVG, 2019)

The **DVG**, enacted on December 9, 2019, created a comprehensive framework for digital health in Germany:

- **Digital Health Applications (DiGA):** Section 33a SGB V established a "fast-track" pathway for digital health applications (Digitale Gesundheitsanwendungen) to be listed in the **DiGA directory** maintained by the Federal Institute for Drugs and Medical Devices (BfArM). Listed DiGA are reimbursable by all statutory health insurance funds (gesetzliche Krankenversicherung, GKV), which cover approximately 90% of the German population.
- **Electronic Health Record (ePA):** Section 341 SGB V mandated that statutory health insurers offer electronic health records to all insured persons by January 1, 2021.
- **Telemedicine Infrastructure:** The DVG promoted the establishment of secure telemedicine infrastructure through the Telematikinfrastruktur (TI), a secure digital network connecting all healthcare providers.

### E-Prescriptions (E-Rezept)

Since January 1, 2024, electronic prescriptions (E-Rezepte) became mandatory for all prescription medications covered by statutory health insurance. The e-prescription system, managed by **gematik GmbH**, enables:

- Physicians to issue prescriptions digitally via the Telematikinfrastruktur.
- Patients to retrieve prescriptions via the ePA app or their electronic health card (elektronische Gesundheitskarte, eGK).
- Direct transmission to pharmacies of the patient's choice.

This system facilitates telemedicine by eliminating the need for paper prescriptions. However, prescribing of narcotics under the Betaeubungsmittelgesetz (BtMG) still requires special documentation procedures.

### Reimbursement

The **Einheitlicher Bewertungsmassstab (EBM)** -- the uniform fee schedule for statutory health insurance -- includes specific billing codes for video consultations:

- **EBM 01450:** Technology surcharge for video consultations (EUR 4.33 per patient per quarter).
- **EBM 01451:** Technical component for video sprechstunde (EUR 10.03 per session).
- Video consultations are capped at 30% of a physician's total consultation volume per quarter.

### Data Protection

Telemedicine data processing in Germany is governed by the **GDPR** (applied via the Bundesdatenschutzgesetz, BDSG) and health-specific provisions in **SGB V** and **SGB X**:

- Health data is classified as a "special category" under Article 9 GDPR, requiring explicit consent or a legal basis for processing.
- The Telematikinfrastruktur must comply with BSI (Federal Office for Information Security) certification requirements.
- Video consultation platforms must be certified by the **Kassenärztliche Bundesvereinigung (KBV)** and meet end-to-end encryption standards.

## ECHR & EU

### European Convention on Human Rights

The ECHR does not directly address telemedicine but establishes rights relevant to remote healthcare delivery:

**Article 2 (Right to Life):** Imposes positive obligations on states to establish regulatory frameworks that protect patients' lives, including in telemedicine contexts. A state's failure to regulate telemedicine adequately, resulting in preventable death, could engage Article 2 obligations as established in **Lopes de Sousa Fernandes v. Portugal** (Application No. 56080/13, Grand Chamber, December 19, 2017).

**Article 3 (Prohibition of Inhuman or Degrading Treatment):** Denial of access to necessary medical treatment, including through unreasonable restrictions on telemedicine, may engage Article 3 where the patient's suffering reaches a sufficient threshold. In **D.v. United Kingdom** (Application No. 30240/96, May 2, 1997), the Court recognized that denial of medical care can constitute inhuman treatment.

**Article 8 (Right to Respect for Private Life):** Encompasses both the right to access healthcare and the right to protection of medical data. The collection and processing of health data through telemedicine platforms must comply with Article 8 requirements, as established in **S. and Marper v. United Kingdom** (Application Nos. 30562/04, 30566/04, Grand Chamber, December 4, 2008).

### EU Legislative Framework

**Cross-Border Healthcare Directive (2011/24/EU):** Establishes the framework for patients receiving healthcare in another EU member state, including via telemedicine:

- Article 3(a) defines "healthcare" broadly to include telemedicine services.
- Article 4(1) requires that cross-border healthcare be provided in accordance with the legislation of the member state of treatment.
- Article 7 provides for reimbursement of cross-border healthcare costs by the patient's home member state.
- Article 14 established the **eHealth Network**, a voluntary network of national eHealth authorities to promote interoperability and cross-border telemedicine.

The **eHealth Network** has adopted guidelines on cross-border telemedicine, including the **2022 Recommendation on a European Electronic Health Record Exchange Format**, and has facilitated the **MyHealth@EU** infrastructure for cross-border exchange of patient summaries and e-prescriptions.

**European Health Data Space (EHDS, proposed May 2022):** The proposed EHDS Regulation seeks to create a single market for health data, including:

- **Primary use:** Individuals' right to access and share their health data electronically across member states.
- **Secondary use:** Framework for using anonymized/pseudonymized health data for research, innovation, and policy-making.
- The EHDS would directly facilitate cross-border telemedicine by ensuring interoperability of electronic health records.

**eIDAS Regulation (910/2014/EU):** Provides the legal framework for electronic identification and trust services, enabling secure patient identification in cross-border telemedicine through electronic identification schemes.

## Comparative Analysis

### Regulatory Evolution

All four jurisdictions demonstrate a common trajectory from restrictive to permissive telemedicine regulation, though at different speeds:

- **South Korea** was the most restrictive, prohibiting direct doctor-to-patient telemedicine until the 2020 emergency measures, and remains embroiled in political conflict over permanent reform.
- **The United States** had a moderately restrictive pre-pandemic framework that was dramatically liberalized through emergency waivers, but faces uncertainty about the permanence of these flexibilities.
- **Germany** made a decisive shift in 2018 through professional self-regulation (the Aerztetag amendment), followed by legislative infrastructure (DVG, e-prescriptions), creating the most systematic reform path.
- **The EU** focuses on cross-border interoperability and data frameworks rather than direct regulation of telemedicine practice, which remains a member-state competence.

### Prescribing and Controlled Substances

All jurisdictions impose heightened restrictions on prescribing controlled substances via telemedicine, reflecting universal concerns about diversion and abuse. The U.S. Ryan Haight Act represents the most detailed framework, while South Korea maintains the strictest approach (prohibiting remote narcotic prescriptions entirely).

### Data Protection Convergence

A notable convergence exists on health data protection requirements. South Korea's PIPA, the U.S. HIPAA framework, the EU's GDPR, and Germany's layered GDPR/SGB V approach all classify health data as requiring enhanced protection, mandate encryption for data in transit, and require informed consent for telemedicine data processing.

### Professional Resistance

South Korea's experience highlights a common tension: physician organizations in all jurisdictions have expressed concerns about telemedicine's impact on patient safety and professional autonomy, though the intensity of opposition varies. The KMA's resistance is the most forceful, while Germany's medical profession adopted the reform through its own self-governing institutions.
