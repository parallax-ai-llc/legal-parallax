## Summary

Lobbying regulation -- the legal framework governing efforts by individuals and organizations to influence government decision-making through direct communication with public officials, provision of information, campaign contributions, and other means -- sits at the tension point between the democratic right to petition government and the risk that well-resourced interests may exercise disproportionate influence over public policy. South Korea enacted the Improper Solicitation and Graft Act (부정청탁 및 금품등 수수의 금지에 관한 법률), commonly known as the Kim Young-ran Act (김영란법), in 2015 (effective September 28, 2016), which prohibits improper solicitation (부정청탁) of public officials regardless of whether compensation is provided and strictly limits the value of meals, gifts, and congratulatory/condolence money that may be provided to public officials, journalists, and educators, but does not establish a comprehensive lobbying registration or disclosure regime. The United States has the most developed lobbying regulatory framework globally, centered on the Lobbying Disclosure Act of 1995 (LDA, 2 U.S.C. §§ 1601-1614), as strengthened by the Honest Leadership and Open Government Act of 2007 (HLOGA), requiring registration and quarterly disclosure by lobbyists who make lobbying contacts with covered officials and meet income/expenditure thresholds, supplemented by the Foreign Agents Registration Act (FARA, 22 U.S.C. §§ 611-621) governing agents of foreign principals. Germany lacks a comprehensive lobbying registration law at the federal level, relying instead on the Lobbyregistergesetz (Lobby Register Act) enacted in 2021 (effective January 1, 2022), which requires interest representatives to register in a public register maintained by the Bundestag but does not mandate disclosure of specific lobbying contacts or expenditures per issue, while the EU operates a Joint Transparency Register established by the Interinstitutional Agreement of 2021 between the European Parliament, the Council, and the Commission, which is mandatory for interactions with Commission officials and European Parliament accreditation but voluntary for Council interactions, and the ECHR's jurisprudence under Article 10 (freedom of expression), Article 11 (freedom of association), and Article 17 (prohibition of abuse of rights) provides the fundamental rights framework within which lobbying regulation must operate.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea does not have a comprehensive lobbying registration law. The regulatory approach focuses on prohibiting corrupt interactions rather than creating a transparency framework for legitimate advocacy.

**Improper Solicitation and Graft Act (부정청탁 및 금품등 수수의 금지에 관한 법률, 2015):**

The Kim Young-ran Act, named after the former Supreme Court Justice who proposed it, was enacted to address pervasive corruption in Korean government and society:

**Scope:** Applies to public officials (공직자), journalists (언론인), and educators at private schools and universities (사립학교·대학 교직원).

**Prohibited Improper Solicitation (Article 5):**
The Act enumerates 15 categories of improper solicitation, including:
- Interference with recruitment, promotion, or transfer of public officials
- Interference with licensing, permitting, or authorization decisions
- Interference with procurement, contracting, or subsidy decisions
- Interference with school admissions, grades, or academic evaluations
- Interference with auditing, inspection, or investigation

Improper solicitation is prohibited regardless of whether compensation is provided. Even requesting a lawful outcome through improper channels is prohibited.

**Gift and Meal Limits (Article 8):**

| Category | Maximum Value |
|----------|--------------|
| Meals (식사) | 30,000 KRW (~$23) |
| Gifts (선물) | 50,000 KRW (~$38); agricultural/fishery products: 100,000 KRW |
| Congratulatory/condolence money (경조금) | 100,000 KRW (~$76) |

**Penalties:**

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| Improper solicitation (third party) | Fine up to 10 million KRW |
| Improper solicitation (public official requesting) | Up to 2 years imprisonment or fine up to 20 million KRW |
| Receiving prohibited gifts/meals | Criminal: up to 3 years imprisonment or fine up to 30 million KRW |
| Providing prohibited gifts/meals | Same penalties as receipt |

### Impact

The Kim Young-ran Act fundamentally transformed Korean business and government culture:
- Restaurant industry reported initial revenue decline of 10-15 percent as business entertainment decreased
- Wedding and funeral congratulatory money practices significantly changed
- Corporate entertainment budgets reduced dramatically
- Public trust in government institutions increased in surveys following implementation

### Lobbying Reform Proposals

Multiple proposals for comprehensive lobbying regulation have been introduced in the National Assembly since 2013:
- **2013 Bill:** Proposed mandatory registration for professional lobbyists; not enacted
- **2017 Bill:** Proposed lobbying disclosure requirements; not enacted
- **2020 Bill:** Proposed comprehensive lobbying transparency act; stalled in committee
- Opposition cites First Amendment-equivalent concerns about freedom of petition (헌법 제26조 청원권)

### Revolving Door

The Public Service Ethics Act (공직자윤리법) imposes post-employment restrictions:
- **Article 17:** Former public officials may not be employed by closely related private entities for 3 years after leaving office
- **Article 18-4:** Former public officials may not represent private interests before their former agencies for 2 years
- Enforcement: Government Ethics Commission (정부공직자윤리위원회)

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has the most comprehensive lobbying regulation framework globally.

**Lobbying Disclosure Act of 1995 (LDA, 2 U.S.C. §§ 1601-1614):**

**Registration Threshold:** Lobbyists must register with the Clerk of the House and Secretary of the Senate if they:
- Make more than one lobbying contact on behalf of a client, AND
- Are employed or retained for lobbying activities involving 20+ percent of their time for that client, AND
- Receive or expect to receive more than $3,000 in income from that client in a quarterly period

**Quarterly Disclosure (LD-2 Reports):**

| Disclosure Element | Detail |
|-------------------|--------|
| Client identity | Name, address, description of business |
| Issues lobbied | Specific bills, executive actions, or policy areas |
| Houses of Congress/agencies contacted | Senate, House, specific executive agencies |
| Lobbyists involved | Name and covered official status |
| Income/expenditure | Good-faith estimate in prescribed ranges |
| Foreign entity interest | Whether foreign entity has interest in client |

**Semi-Annual Political Contributions Report (LD-203):** Lobbyists must disclose political contributions, honorary contributions, payments for events honoring covered officials, and certify compliance with gift rules.

### HLOGA Amendments (2007)

The Honest Leadership and Open Government Act of 2007 strengthened the LDA:
- Increased filing frequency from semi-annual to quarterly
- Enhanced criminal penalties (up to $200,000 fine and/or up to 5 years imprisonment)
- Mandatory electronic filing
- Requirement for lobbyists to certify compliance with Congressional gift rules
- Expanded definition of "lobbyist bundler" (bundling campaign contributions)

### Foreign Agents Registration Act (FARA, 22 U.S.C. §§ 611-621)

FARA, enacted in 1938 (originally to counter Nazi propaganda), requires agents of foreign principals to:
- Register with the DOJ National Security Division
- File supplemental statements every six months
- Label informational materials as being distributed by an agent of a foreign principal
- Disclose all activities, receipts, and disbursements

**Recent FARA Enforcement:** FARA enforcement has significantly increased since 2016:
- Paul Manafort: convicted of FARA violations related to Ukrainian lobbying (2018)
- Multiple lobbyists and consultants charged for undisclosed work for foreign governments
- DOJ established FARA Unit within the Counterintelligence and Export Control Section

### Industry Scale

The U.S. lobbying industry is the world's largest:
- Approximately 12,000 registered lobbyists (2024)
- Total reported lobbying expenditures: approximately $4.2 billion annually (2024)
- Top spending sectors: pharmaceuticals, electronics, insurance, oil and gas, business associations
- K Street (Washington, D.C.): symbolic center of the lobbying industry

### Revolving Door

Post-employment restrictions (18 U.S.C. § 207):

| Former Position | Cooling-Off Period | Restriction |
|----------------|-------------------|-------------|
| Senators | 2 years | No lobbying Congress |
| House members | 1 year | No lobbying Congress |
| Senior executive officials | 2 years | No lobbying former agency; 1 year for Congress |
| Very senior officials | 2 years | No lobbying former agency |
| Cabinet members | 1 year | No lobbying former department |

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany adopted its first comprehensive lobbying transparency law in 2021, following decades of debate.

**Lobbyregistergesetz (Lobby Register Act, 2021, effective January 1, 2022):**

| Provision | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| § 1 | Lobby register maintained by Bundestag |
| § 2 | Registration mandatory for interest representatives contacting Bundestag members or federal government for purpose of influencing political decisions |
| § 3 | Registration information: organization name, address, legal form, interests, number of persons engaged, annual expenditure, public funding, memberships |
| § 4 | Code of conduct (Verhaltenskodex): voluntary initially; adherence declared upon registration |
| § 5 | Publication: register is publicly accessible online |
| § 6 | Exemptions: natural persons acting in personal capacity; political parties; religious organizations; municipalities |
| § 7 | Legislative footprint (Gesetzgebungsfußabdruck): since 2024, federal ministers and parliamentary state secretaries must disclose lobby contacts in draft legislation explanatory memoranda |

**Key Limitations:**
- No requirement to disclose specific lobbying contacts or meetings
- No per-issue expenditure disclosure
- No mandatory code of conduct (voluntary declaration)
- Administrative fines only (maximum €50,000), no criminal penalties
- Exemptions for employer associations, trade unions, churches, and municipalities

### Legislative Footprint (2024 Amendment)

The most significant reform was the January 2024 introduction of the legislative footprint (Gesetzgebungsfußabdruck):
- Federal ministers and parliamentary state secretaries must disclose which interest representatives they met during the development of draft legislation
- Disclosures are included in the explanatory memorandum (Gesetzesbegründung) of draft bills
- Applies to federal government draft legislation submitted to the Bundestag

### Revolving Door

Post-employment restrictions for federal ministers and parliamentary state secretaries (Bundesministergesetz § 6a):
- 12-month waiting period for employment with entities that could create conflicts of interest
- 18-month waiting period for federal chancellors and ministers
- Advisory body (Karenzzeit-Gremium) reviews proposed post-government employment
- No statutory restrictions for Bundestag members (party rules may apply)

### Pre-Lobbyregistergesetz Practice

Before 2022, lobbying transparency was minimal:
- Verbändeliste (association list): Bundestag maintained a voluntary list of associations wishing to present their views (since 1972); non-binding
- Bundestag hearings: expert hearings (Anhörungen) provided some transparency for interest group participation
- Informal norms: German political culture emphasized consensus-building through established associations (Verbände)

## ECHR / EU

### EU Transparency Register

The Interinstitutional Agreement on a Mandatory Transparency Register (2021) established a joint register covering the European Parliament, Council, and Commission:

**Registration Requirements:**

| Element | Requirement |
|---------|-------------|
| Mandatory for | Commission meetings with registered entities; EP accreditation |
| Voluntary for | Council interactions |
| Information | Organization, interests, activities, membership, financial data |
| Annual turnover/budget | Disclosed in ranges |
| Number of persons | Full-time equivalent lobbyists |
| Clients | Names of clients for consultancies |
| EU funding | Amount of EU funding received |
| Code of conduct | Mandatory adherence |

**Categories of Registrants:**
1. Professional consultancies/law firms/self-employed consultants
2. In-house lobbyists and trade/business/professional associations
3. Non-governmental organizations
4. Think tanks, research institutions, academic institutions
5. Organizations representing churches and religious communities
6. Organizations representing local, regional and municipal authorities

As of 2025, approximately 12,500 entities are registered in the EU Transparency Register.

### European Parliament Rules

The EP Rules of Procedure provide additional transparency requirements:
- **Rule 11:** Members' financial interests declarations
- **Rule 176:** EP accreditation requires registration in the Transparency Register
- **Intergroups:** Informal groupings of MEPs around particular interests must disclose support
- **Cooling-off:** Former MEPs face restrictions on immediate lobbying of EP

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Vides Aizsardzibas Klubs v. Latvia (Application No. 57829/00, May 27, 2004):** The Court addressed the relationship between lobbying activities and freedom of expression, finding that NGO advocacy aimed at influencing government decisions falls within the protection of Article 10.

**Comingersoll S.A. v. Portugal (Application No. 35382/97, Grand Chamber, April 6, 2000):** While concerning non-pecuniary damage to a legal entity, the Court recognized that legal persons, including lobbying organizations, enjoy Convention rights including freedom of expression.

**Article 10 and Lobbying:** The ECHR's protection of freedom of expression extends to advocacy and lobbying activities, meaning that unduly restrictive lobbying regulation could potentially violate Article 10. However, states enjoy a wide margin of appreciation in regulating the modalities of political influence, particularly where restrictions aim to prevent corruption or ensure transparency.

### Council of Europe and GRECO

The Group of States Against Corruption (GRECO) regularly evaluates member states' lobbying transparency:
- Fourth Evaluation Round addresses corruption prevention in respect of parliamentarians, judges, and prosecutors
- Fifth Evaluation Round (2017-) addresses prevention of corruption in top executive functions
- GRECO has recommended comprehensive lobbying regulation for multiple member states

## Comparative Analysis

### Regulatory Models

Three distinct regulatory models emerge:

**Comprehensive transparency (United States):** Mandatory registration, quarterly disclosure of contacts, issues, and expenditures; criminal penalties. The most intensive regulatory approach, producing the most detailed public information about lobbying activities.

**Registration-based (Germany, EU):** Mandatory registration with basic organizational and financial information; limited contact or expenditure disclosure. Creates a public directory of interest representatives without detailed activity tracking.

**Anti-corruption (South Korea):** Focus on prohibiting corrupt exchanges (gifts, improper solicitation) rather than creating a lobbying transparency system. Addresses the outputs of improper lobbying rather than the process of legitimate lobbying.

### Effectiveness

| Criterion | United States | Germany | South Korea | EU |
|-----------|---------------|---------|-------------|-----|
| Transparency of who lobbies | High | Moderate | Low | Moderate |
| Transparency of what is lobbied | High | Low | N/A | Low |
| Transparency of spending | High | Low (ranges only) | N/A | Low (ranges only) |
| Prevention of corruption | Moderate (enforcement gaps) | Moderate | High (Kim Young-ran Act) | Moderate |
| Cultural impact | Limited (lobbying normalized) | Moderate | High (transformed business culture) | Moderate |

### Revolving Door

All jurisdictions regulate the revolving door between government and the private sector, but with significant variation:
- **United States:** Most developed statutory framework with position-specific cooling-off periods
- **Germany:** Recent introduction of mandatory cooling-off for ministers and state secretaries
- **South Korea:** Broad 3-year employment restriction for former public officials
- **EU:** 2-year cooling-off for Commissioners; 1 year for senior officials
