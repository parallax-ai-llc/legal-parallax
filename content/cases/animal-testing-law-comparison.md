## Summary

Animal testing and experimentation law varies dramatically across jurisdictions, reflecting divergent cultural, scientific, and ethical approaches to the use of animals in research, product development, and education. South Korea regulates animal experimentation through a dual statutory framework -- the Laboratory Animal Act (실험동물에 관한 법률, LAA, 2008) and the Animal Protection Act (동물보호법, APA, 1991, comprehensively amended 2023) -- requiring institutional ethics committees and imposing criminal penalties of up to two years imprisonment for violations, while notably banning cosmetic testing on animals since 2017-2018. The United States relies primarily on the Animal Welfare Act (7 U.S.C. §§ 2131-2159, AWA, 1966) enforced by the USDA, which mandates Institutional Animal Care and Use Committees (IACUCs) for all research facilities but controversially excludes purpose-bred birds, rats, and mice -- estimated to constitute over 95% of laboratory animals -- from its protections. Germany enforces some of the strictest animal testing regulations globally through its Animal Welfare Act (Tierschutzgesetz, TierSchG, 1972, last amended 2022), Sections 7-9, which constitutionally enshrine animal protection under Basic Law Article 20a and require prior governmental authorization for all vertebrate experiments. The EU's Directive 2010/63/EU on the protection of animals used for scientific purposes establishes the "Three Rs" principle (Replacement, Reduction, Refinement) as binding law and imposes an ultimate goal of full replacement of animal testing, while the ECHR has addressed the tension between scientific freedom and animal welfare through its developing case law.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's animal testing regime is governed by two principal statutes operating in parallel:

**Laboratory Animal Act (실험동물에 관한 법률, LAA, 2008):**

- **Article 2 -- Definitions:** Defines "laboratory animals" as animals used or intended for use in animal experiments, and "animal experiments" as procedures performed on animals for scientific purposes including research, education, and testing.
- **Article 7 -- Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (IACUC):** Requires all animal testing facilities to establish an IACUC (동물실험윤리위원회) comprising at least five members, including a veterinarian and an external member representing animal welfare. The committee must review and approve all experimental protocols before testing begins.
- **Article 9 -- Principles of Animal Experimentation:** Mandates adherence to the Three Rs principles (Replacement, Reduction, Refinement) and requires that researchers use the minimum number of animals necessary and minimize pain and distress.
- **Article 23 -- Supervision and Inspection:** Grants the Ministry of Food and Drug Safety (MFDS) authority to conduct on-site inspections of animal testing facilities and order corrective measures.
- **Article 25 -- Penalties:** Imposes fines of up to KRW 5 million for violations of IACUC requirements and operating without proper registration.

**Animal Protection Act (동물보호법, APA, 1991, comprehensively amended 2023):**

- **Article 8 -- Prohibition of Animal Cruelty:** Prohibits killing animals by cruel methods, inflicting unnecessary injury, and abandoning animals. The 2023 amendment significantly strengthened penalties.
- **Article 24 -- Principles of Animal Experimentation:** Requires experimenters to consider whether alternative methods exist, use the minimum number of animals, and apply pain relief measures.
- **Article 97-98 -- Penalties:** Criminal penalties of up to three years imprisonment or KRW 30 million fine for animal cruelty (strengthened from two years under the 2023 amendment); administrative fines for procedural violations.

**Cosmetics Act (화장품법, amended 2016):**

- **Article 15-2:** Enacted in 2016 and effective 2017, banning animal testing for finished cosmetic products. Extended to cosmetic ingredients in 2018, with exceptions for ingredients where no validated alternative methods exist. In 2025, the Ministry of Food and Drug Safety further closed loopholes by restricting "abnormal toxicity tests" that had been used to circumvent the cosmetics testing ban.

### Key Principles

South Korea's approach reflects a tension between its significant biomedical research sector (ranked among the top 10 globally in research output) and growing public demand for animal welfare protection. The 2023 amendments to the APA, which elevated animal cruelty to a serious criminal offense, followed several high-profile animal abuse cases that generated intense public outcry. Enforcement historically has been uneven, with the Animal and Plant Quarantine Agency responsible for oversight but limited in inspection capacity. The trend in animal use in Korea differs markedly from other developed countries: the number of laboratory animals used has continued to increase annually, reaching approximately 4.9 million in 2022, while countries such as the UK and Germany have seen declining numbers.

### Notable Cases

- **Seoul National University Cloning Scandal (2005-2006):** Researcher Hwang Woo-suk was found to have fabricated stem cell research data and to have violated ethical protocols by obtaining human eggs from subordinate researchers. While the case primarily involved research fraud, it prompted major reforms to South Korea's research ethics oversight, including strengthening IACUC requirements.
- **Beagle Rescue Cases (2018-2023):** Multiple incidents involving the release of beagles from laboratory testing facilities generated public activism and contributed to legislative momentum for stronger animal testing regulations.
- **Korea Institute of Toxicology Investigations (2022):** Public interest groups filed complaints alleging that government-funded toxicology laboratories conducted unnecessary duplicate animal tests, prompting MFDS investigations and policy reforms to promote alternative methods.

## United States

### Federal Framework

The United States regulates animal testing through a multi-layered system of federal statutes, regulations, and institutional policies:

**Animal Welfare Act (7 U.S.C. §§ 2131-2159, AWA, 1966, amended 1970, 1976, 1985, 2002, 2008):**

The AWA is the primary federal statute governing the treatment of animals in research. Key provisions include:

- **7 U.S.C. § 2131 -- Congressional Findings and Policy:** Establishes that regulation of animal use in research is necessary to ensure humane care and treatment.
- **7 U.S.C. § 2143 -- Standards and Certification:** Requires the Secretary of Agriculture to promulgate standards for animal care, including minimum requirements for housing, feeding, watering, sanitation, ventilation, and veterinary care.
- **9 C.F.R. § 2.31 -- IACUC Requirements:** Mandates that each research facility establish an Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee of at least three members (including a veterinarian and a community member unaffiliated with the facility) to review and approve all protocols involving animal use, conduct semiannual facility inspections, and investigate concerns about animal treatment.
- **7 U.S.C. § 2146 -- Enforcement:** USDA Animal and Plant Health Inspection Service (APHIS) conducts inspections and may assess civil penalties of up to $16,036 per violation (adjusted for inflation), per animal, per day. Criminal penalties include fines of up to $10,000 and imprisonment of up to one year.

**Critical Exclusion:** The AWA definition of "animal" (9 C.F.R. § 1.1), as amended by the 2002 Farm Bill, explicitly excludes "birds, rats of the genus Rattus, and mice of the genus Mus, bred for use in research." This exclusion is one of the most criticized aspects of US animal testing law, as these species constitute an estimated 95% or more of all laboratory animals.

**Public Health Service (PHS) Policy on Humane Care and Use of Laboratory Animals (1986, revised 2015):**

Applies to all vertebrate animals used in PHS-funded research (including NIH grants). Unlike the AWA, PHS Policy covers rats, mice, and birds. Requires compliance with the Guide for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals (NRC, 8th ed., 2011) and IACUC oversight. Enforced by the NIH Office of Laboratory Animal Welfare (OLAW); violations may result in suspension or termination of federal funding.

**FDA Modernization Act 2.0 (2022):**

Amended the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act to remove the requirement that drugs be tested on animals before human clinical trials, allowing sponsors to use alternative testing methods including cell-based assays, organ-on-a-chip technology, and computer modeling. This does not ban animal testing but eliminates the federal mandate.

### State Variations

- **California Cruelty-Free Cosmetics Act (SB 1249, 2018):** Prohibits the sale of cosmetics that have been tested on animals, effective 1 January 2020. Applies to finished products and ingredients tested on animals after specified dates.
- **Nevada (SB 197, 2019), Illinois (SB 241, 2019), Virginia (HB 2074, 2021):** Similar cosmetics animal testing bans prohibiting the manufacture or sale of cosmetics tested on animals within their territories.
- **New Jersey (S3401, 2024):** Banned the use of dogs and cats in toxicology testing for consumer products, one of the most restrictive state-level provisions.

### Notable Cases

- **Silver Spring Monkeys Case (1981):** Alex Pacheco of PETA documented abuse at the Institute for Behavioral Research in Silver Spring, Maryland, leading to the first police raid on an animal research laboratory in the US, the first criminal conviction of a researcher for animal cruelty, and ultimately the first US Supreme Court case on standing in animal rights (International Primate Protection League v. Administrators of Tulane Educational Fund, 500 U.S. 72 (1991)).
- **Harvard Primate Center Closures (2015):** Harvard Medical School closed its New England Primate Research Center following multiple USDA citations for animal care violations, federal investigations into the deaths of four primates, and the levying of $24,036 in fines.
- **Envigo Beagle Facility (2022):** The USDA and DOJ obtained a consent decree shutting down an Envigo facility in Cumberland, Virginia, that bred beagles for research, after inspectors documented over 70 AWA violations including inadequate veterinary care, insufficient food, and unsanitary conditions. Approximately 4,000 beagles were rehomed.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany provides some of the strongest legal protections for laboratory animals in the world, grounded in constitutional recognition of animal welfare and implemented through comprehensive federal legislation.

**Basic Law (Grundgesetz) Article 20a:**

Amended in 2002 to include the protection of animals as a state objective: "Mindful also of its responsibility towards future generations, the state shall protect the natural foundations of life and animals by legislation and, in accordance with law and justice, by executive and judicial action." This constitutional provision elevates animal welfare to a value that must be weighed against competing constitutional rights, including the freedom of research (Article 5(3) GG).

**Animal Welfare Act (Tierschutzgesetz, TierSchG, 1972, last comprehensive amendment 2022):**

- **§ 1 -- Purpose:** "The purpose of this Act is to protect the life and well-being of animals out of the responsibility of human beings for animals as fellow creatures. No one may cause an animal pain, suffering or harm without good reason."
- **§ 7 -- Definition of Animal Experimentation:** Defines animal experiments as procedures or treatments carried out on animals for experimental purposes that may involve pain, suffering, or harm. Includes interventions on the genetic material of animals if they may cause pain, suffering, or harm to the animals, their offspring, or carrier animals.
- **§ 7a -- Permissibility of Animal Experiments:** Animal experiments are permissible only for specifically enumerated purposes, including: (1) basic scientific research; (2) translational or applied research for the prevention, diagnosis, or treatment of disease; (3) detection of environmental hazards; (4) testing the safety and efficacy of pharmaceuticals, foods, and other substances; and (5) research for the protection of the environment. Animal experiments are expressly prohibited for the development or testing of weapons, ammunition, and related equipment (§ 7a(3)).
- **§ 8 -- Authorization Requirements:** All experiments on vertebrates and cephalopods require prior authorization from the competent state authority (typically the Landesveterinäramt). Applications must demonstrate that the experiment is indispensable (i.e., no alternative methods exist), the expected scientific benefit justifies the animal harm, and the number of animals and degree of suffering are minimized. Certain experiments required by law (e.g., drug safety testing mandated by statute) require only notification rather than authorization (§ 8a).
- **§ 15 -- Animal Welfare Commissions:** Each state authority must consult an animal welfare commission (Tierschutzkommission) when evaluating applications for animal experiments. The commission must include scientists, veterinarians, and representatives of animal welfare organizations.
- **§ 17 -- Criminal Penalties:** Killing a vertebrate without reasonable cause carries imprisonment of up to three years or a fine. Causing a vertebrate prolonged or repeated significant pain or suffering carries imprisonment of up to three years or a fine.
- **§ 18 -- Administrative Offenses (Ordnungswidrigkeiten):** Violations of procedural requirements (e.g., conducting experiments without authorization) are punishable by administrative fines of up to EUR 25,000.

**Animal Welfare Laboratory Animal Regulation (Tierschutz-Versuchstierverordnung, TierSchVersV, 2013):**

Implements EU Directive 2010/63/EU into German law. Establishes detailed requirements for the care, housing, and use of laboratory animals, including minimum cage sizes, environmental enrichment, and severity classifications for experimental procedures.

### Key Principles

Germany's animal testing regime is characterized by the constitutional balancing of competing values: the freedom of scientific research under Article 5(3) GG must be weighed against the state's duty to protect animals under Article 20a GG. The Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) addressed this tension in its 2004 decision on animal testing (BVerfG, 1 BvR 1778/01, 2004), holding that the 2002 constitutional amendment elevated animal welfare to a constitutionally protected value capable of restricting the previously nearly absolute freedom of research.

The number of laboratory animals used in Germany has been declining: from approximately 2.8 million in 2016 to approximately 1.7 million in 2022, reflecting both regulatory pressure and the increasing availability of alternative methods. Germany is a leading funder of alternative methods research through the German Centre for the Protection of Laboratory Animals (Bf3R) at the Federal Institute for Risk Assessment (BfR).

### Notable Cases

- **Bremen Animal Testing Ban (1993):** The state of Bremen enacted a constitutional provision explicitly banning animal testing, which was challenged as incompatible with the federal freedom of research. The Federal Constitutional Court ruled that while states could not impose an outright ban conflicting with federal law, they could apply stricter standards within the federal framework.
- **Max Planck Institute Primate Research Controversy (2014-2018):** Animal welfare organizations documented conditions at the Max Planck Institute for Biological Cybernetics in Tübingen, leading to public outcry, a temporary suspension of primate experiments, and the institute's eventual decision to phase out primate research.
- **LPT Laboratory Hamburg Investigation (2019):** Undercover footage from an animal welfare organization documented severe animal welfare violations at the Laboratory of Pharmacology and Toxicology (LPT) near Hamburg, leading to criminal investigations, revocation of the facility's operating license, and its permanent closure.

## ECHR / EU Framework

### Convention Articles

The ECHR framework addresses animal testing through the intersection of several Convention rights:

- **Article 10 -- Freedom of Expression:** Relevant to animal welfare activists' rights to document and report on conditions in animal testing facilities. In Steel and Morris v. the United Kingdom (2005, App. No. 68416/01), the Court recognized the legitimate public interest in animal welfare discourse.
- **Article 8 -- Right to Private Life:** Has been invoked in challenges to the environmental and health impacts of facilities conducting animal testing on nearby residents.
- **Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 -- Protection of Property:** Relevant to regulatory takings arguments when animal testing bans affect the economic interests of pharmaceutical or cosmetics companies.

### Key Case Law

- **Tierbefreier e.V. v. Germany (2014, App. No. 45192/09, admissibility decision):** An animal rights organization challenged Germany's refusal to grant an injunction against a primate research facility. The Court found the application inadmissible, holding that the national courts had properly balanced the competing interests of scientific research and animal welfare, and that the Convention did not impose an obligation to ban animal testing.
- **BUAV v. United Kingdom (Commission, 1997, App. No. 35889/97):** The British Union for the Abolition of Vivisection challenged the UK's licensing of animal experiments. The Commission declared the application inadmissible, finding that the regulatory framework provided adequate procedural safeguards.

**EU Directive 2010/63/EU on the Protection of Animals Used for Scientific Purposes:**

- **Article 1(1) -- Objective:** Establishes "the full replacement of procedures on live animals for scientific and educational purposes as soon as it is scientifically possible."
- **Article 4 -- Principle of Replacement, Reduction, and Refinement (Three Rs):** Member states must ensure that an animal procedure is not carried out where a scientifically satisfactory non-animal method is available.
- **Article 15 -- Severity Classification:** All procedures must be classified as non-recovery, mild, moderate, or severe. Projects classified as severe must meet additional justification requirements.
- **Article 36 -- Project Authorization:** Competent authorities must conduct a project evaluation before authorizing any animal testing, including a harm-benefit analysis weighing the scientific merit against the expected animal suffering.
- **Article 38 -- Retrospective Assessment:** Member states must ensure that certain projects undergo retrospective assessment to evaluate whether the objectives were achieved and the actual harm to animals.

**Regulation (EC) No 1223/2009 on Cosmetic Products:**

- **Article 18:** Prohibits the placing on the market of cosmetic products where the final formulation or ingredients have been the subject of animal testing. The ban was implemented in phases: 2004 (testing ban for finished products), 2009 (testing ban for ingredients), and March 2013 (marketing ban for all cosmetic products tested on animals, regardless of the availability of alternative methods).

### Member State Obligations

EU member states must:

- Transpose Directive 2010/63/EU into national law and establish competent authorities for project authorization (Article 36).
- Ensure that all animal testing facilities are registered and subject to regular inspection (Article 34).
- Promote the development, validation, and implementation of alternative methods (Article 47).
- Publish annual statistics on the number and types of animals used in scientific procedures (Article 54).
- Ensure the availability of effective, proportionate, and dissuasive penalties for violations (Article 60).

## Global Perspectives

- **United Kingdom:** Post-Brexit, the UK retained the Animals (Scientific Procedures) Act 1986 (ASPA), which requires a personal license, project license, and establishment license for all regulated procedures. The UK Home Office reports approximately 2.76 million procedures in 2023, with declining trends.
- **Japan:** The Act on Welfare and Management of Animals (1973, amended 2019) provides a general framework, but Japan's animal testing oversight relies heavily on institutional self-regulation through the Science Council of Japan's guidelines. Japan has no mandatory cosmetics testing ban.
- **India:** The Prevention of Cruelty to Animals Act, 1960, and the Committee for the Purpose of Control and Supervision of Experiments on Animals (CPCSEA) regulate animal testing. India banned cosmetic testing on animals in 2014 and banned the import of animal-tested cosmetics in 2014, making it one of the earliest countries to adopt a comprehensive cosmetics testing ban.
- **Brazil:** Law No. 11,794/2008 (the Arouca Law) established the National Council for the Control of Animal Experimentation (CONCEA) and requires ethics committee approval for all animal experiments. Brazil banned cosmetic animal testing nationally in 2023 under state and federal legislation.
- **Australia:** The Australian Code for the Care and Use of Animals for Scientific Purposes (8th ed., 2013) operates under state and territory legislation. Each institution must have an Animal Ethics Committee (AEC). Australia has not enacted a national cosmetics testing ban, though industry self-regulation largely complies with EU standards.
- **Nigeria:** The National Health Research Ethics Committee (NHREC) guidelines address animal use in research, but enforcement infrastructure remains limited. Nigeria's animal welfare legislation is largely focused on livestock and does not have specific laboratory animal protections comparable to developed nations.

## The Three Rs Framework in Practice

### Replacement

The principle of Replacement -- using non-animal methods wherever scientifically possible -- is implemented differently across jurisdictions:

- **EU/Germany:** The strongest legal mandate. Directive 2010/63/EU Article 4 makes replacement a binding obligation: an animal procedure must not be carried out where a scientifically satisfactory non-animal method is available. Germany's Bf3R (Centre for the Protection of Laboratory Animals) actively validates and promotes alternative methods, including organ-on-a-chip technology, in silico modeling, and 3D tissue models.
- **South Korea:** The LAA requires consideration of alternatives, and MFDS has established the Korean Center for the Validation of Alternative Methods (KoCVAM) to evaluate and accept non-animal testing methods. However, mandatory acceptance of validated alternatives has been slower than in the EU.
- **United States:** The FDA Modernization Act 2.0 (2022) removed the legal requirement for animal testing of new drugs, permitting "alternative methods" including cell-based assays, organ chips, and computational models. However, this does not prohibit animal testing; it merely removes the mandate. The Interagency Coordinating Committee on the Validation of Alternative Methods (ICCVAM) evaluates alternative methods but its recommendations are non-binding.

### Reduction

The principle of Reduction -- minimizing the number of animals used -- is supported across all jurisdictions through statistical power analysis requirements and experimental design review by ethics committees. Germany requires explicit justification of animal numbers in authorization applications. The US IACUC system requires investigators to demonstrate consideration of reducing animal numbers, though enforcement varies by institution.

### Refinement

The principle of Refinement -- minimizing pain and distress -- is addressed through:

- **Severity classification systems:** The EU mandates classification of all procedures as non-recovery, mild, moderate, or severe (Directive 2010/63/EU, Article 15). Germany implements this through the TierSchVersV. The US AWA requires that researchers consider alternatives to procedures that cause more than momentary pain or distress (9 C.F.R. § 2.31(d)(1)(ii)).
- **Humane endpoints:** All jurisdictions require the establishment of humane endpoints to prevent unnecessary suffering, though the specificity of requirements varies.
- **Environmental enrichment:** EU and German regulations mandate species-appropriate environmental enrichment (Annex III of Directive 2010/63/EU). US requirements are less detailed, specified primarily in the Guide for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals rather than in statute.

## Emerging Issues and Future Directions

### Artificial Intelligence and Animal Testing

The intersection of AI and animal testing is reshaping the regulatory landscape:

- **In Silico Toxicology:** AI-powered computational toxicology models can predict the toxicity of chemical compounds without animal testing. The EU's REACH Regulation has begun accepting certain in silico predictions as supporting evidence, and the OECD has published guidelines on the use of quantitative structure-activity relationship (QSAR) models.
- **Organ-on-a-Chip Technology:** Microfluidic devices that mimic human organ systems are increasingly accepted as alternatives to animal models. The US FDA has signaled openness to organ-on-a-chip data in drug approval applications following the FDA Modernization Act 2.0.
- **AI-Powered Drug Discovery:** Machine learning models can identify drug candidates and predict their properties, potentially reducing the number of compounds that require animal testing in the preclinical phase. Companies like Recursion Pharmaceuticals and Insilico Medicine have advanced AI-discovered drug candidates to clinical trials with reduced animal testing.

### Primate Research Restrictions

The use of non-human primates in research has become an especially contentious issue:

- **EU:** Directive 2010/63/EU prohibits the use of great apes (chimpanzees, bonobos, gorillas, orangutans) in research, with an emergency exception requiring authorization from the European Commission (Article 55(3)). The use of other non-human primates is permitted only for research into life-threatening or debilitating conditions or for species preservation.
- **United States:** The National Institutes of Health retired all federally owned chimpanzees from research in 2015 and transferred them to sanctuaries. However, research on other non-human primates (macaques, marmosets) continues, with approximately 70,000 non-human primates used annually.
- **Germany:** Primate research has declined significantly, with the Max Planck Society phasing out or reducing primate research at several institutes following public pressure and institutional review.
- **South Korea:** The National Primate Research Center (NPRC) at the Korea Research Institute of Bioscience and Biotechnology (KRIBB) maintains active primate research programs, particularly in infectious disease and neuroscience.

### Sentience Recognition and Legal Personhood

An emerging trend in animal law with implications for testing regulation:

- The **UK Animal Welfare (Sentience) Act 2022** established an Animal Sentience Committee to assess the impact of government policy on the welfare of animals as sentient beings.
- **Germany** recognized animal sentience constitutionally through Article 20a GG (2002), establishing animals as beings deserving of protection beyond mere property.
- The question of whether sentient animals should have legal standing to challenge their use in experiments through human representatives remains a frontier issue, with the Nonhuman Rights Project pursuing such claims in US courts.
