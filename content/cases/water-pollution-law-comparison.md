## Summary

Water pollution law -- the regulatory framework governing the discharge of contaminants into surface waters, groundwater, and marine environments -- represents one of the earliest and most developed domains of environmental regulation, driven by industrialization, urbanization, and repeated ecological disasters that revealed the catastrophic consequences of uncontrolled water contamination. South Korea regulates water pollution primarily through the Water Environment Conservation Act (물환경보전법, formerly the Water Quality and Aquatic Ecosystem Conservation Act), which establishes discharge permit systems, effluent standards, total maximum daily loads (TMDLs) for major river basins, and a polluter-pays principle enforced through effluent charges (배출부과금), supplemented by the Framework Act on Water Management (물관리 기본법) enacted in 2018 consolidating water governance under the Ministry of Environment. The United States addresses water pollution through the Clean Water Act (CWA, 33 U.S.C. Sections 1251-1387), enacted in 1972 as a comprehensive amendment to the Federal Water Pollution Control Act of 1948, establishing the National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System (NPDES) permit program, technology-based effluent limitations, water quality standards, and a goal of "zero discharge" of pollutants, with jurisdiction significantly shaped by the Supreme Court's decisions in Rapanos v. United States (2006) and Sackett v. EPA (2023) regarding the scope of "waters of the United States." Germany regulates water pollution under the Wasserhaushaltsgesetz (WHG, Federal Water Resources Act) implementing the EU Water Framework Directive (2000/60/EC), with discharge permits (Erlaubnis or Bewilligung) required for all uses of water bodies, stringent groundwater protection under the Grundwasserverordnung (GrwV), and the principle of best available technology (Stand der Technik) for industrial discharges. The EU Water Framework Directive establishes a comprehensive framework requiring all member states to achieve "good ecological and chemical status" for surface waters and "good chemical and quantitative status" for groundwater, with subsequent directives addressing priority substances, groundwater protection, and marine strategy.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's water pollution regulatory framework centers on the Water Environment Conservation Act (물환경보전법, Act No. 19953), comprehensively revised and renamed from the Water Quality and Aquatic Ecosystem Conservation Act in 2017. The Act is supplemented by the Framework Act on Water Management (물관리 기본법, 2018), which transferred primary water management authority from the Ministry of Land, Infrastructure and Transport to the Ministry of Environment (환경부), unifying water quality and water quantity management.

### Discharge Permit System

Facilities that discharge wastewater must obtain a discharge permit (배출시설 설치허가) from the regional environmental office. The permit system establishes:

- **Effluent Standards (배출허용기준):** Concentration-based limits for pollutants including BOD, COD, suspended solids, heavy metals, and nutrient parameters (total nitrogen, total phosphorus).
- **Total Maximum Daily Load (TMDL) System (총량규제제도):** Basin-wide pollution load limits for the four major river systems (Han, Nakdong, Geum, Yeongsan). Local governments must ensure total pollution loads remain within allocated limits.

### Effluent Charges

The polluter-pays principle is implemented through the effluent charge system (배출부과금). Two types of charges apply:

| Charge Type | Basis | Application |
|-------------|-------|-------------|
| Basic charge (기본배출부과금) | Volume and concentration of permitted discharge | Applied to all permitted discharges exceeding minimum thresholds |
| Excess charge (초과배출부과금) | Amount exceeding permitted limits | Applied when effluent standards are violated; calculated per kilogram of excess pollutant |

### Four Major Rivers Restoration Project

The Four Major Rivers Restoration Project (4대강 살리기 사업), completed in 2012, involved the construction of 16 weirs and extensive dredging at a cost of approximately 22 trillion KRW. The project was highly controversial, criticized for disrupting aquatic ecosystems and causing algal blooms. Several weirs have been opened or demolished since 2017 as part of reassessment efforts.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The Clean Water Act (CWA), enacted in 1972 (Federal Water Pollution Control Act Amendments of 1972, Pub. L. 92-500), established the modern framework for regulating water pollution. Section 101(a) declares the national objective of restoring and maintaining the "chemical, physical, and biological integrity of the Nation's waters" with an interim goal of "fishable and swimmable" waters.

### NPDES Permit Program (Section 402)

The NPDES permit program prohibits the "discharge of any pollutant" from a "point source" to "navigable waters" without a permit. Key features:

- **Technology-Based Effluent Limitations:** Industrial dischargers must meet Best Available Technology economically achievable (BAT) for toxic and non-conventional pollutants and Best Conventional Pollutant Control Technology (BCT) for conventional pollutants.
- **Water Quality-Based Limits:** Where technology-based limits are insufficient to meet water quality standards, more stringent limits apply.
- **Publicly Owned Treatment Works (POTWs):** Municipal wastewater treatment plants must meet secondary treatment standards; industrial users discharging to POTWs are subject to pretreatment standards.

### Sackett v. EPA, 598 U.S. 651 (2023)

The Supreme Court significantly narrowed the CWA's jurisdictional scope, holding that "waters of the United States" includes only those wetlands with a "continuous surface connection" to traditional navigable waters, such that there is "no clear demarcation between waters and wetlands." This decision rejected the "significant nexus" test previously used by the EPA and substantially reduced federal regulatory authority over isolated wetlands and intermittent waterways.

### Nonpoint Source Pollution

The CWA's permit system covers only "point source" discharges. Nonpoint source pollution (agricultural runoff, stormwater) is addressed primarily through Section 319 grant programs and, indirectly, through TMDLs under Section 303(d). The lack of direct federal regulatory authority over agricultural nonpoint sources remains one of the CWA's most significant limitations.

### Landmark Cases

**Rapanos v. United States, 547 U.S. 715 (2006):** A fractured 4-1-4 decision addressing the scope of CWA jurisdiction over wetlands adjacent to non-navigable tributaries. Justice Kennedy's concurrence established the "significant nexus" test, which served as the operative standard until superseded by Sackett.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's water pollution regulation is governed by the Wasserhaushaltsgesetz (WHG), which implements the EU Water Framework Directive and establishes the federal framework for water management. The Länder (federal states) implement the WHG through state water laws and are responsible for administration and enforcement through state water authorities (Wasserbehörden).

### Discharge Authorization

Any use of a water body (Gewässerbenutzung) requires authorization under WHG Sections 8-13. Two types of authorization exist:

| Type | Duration | Revocability |
|------|----------|-------------|
| Erlaubnis (permit) | Limited duration | Revocable |
| Bewilligung (license) | Extended duration | Limited revocability; stronger legal position |

Discharge of wastewater (Abwassereinleitung) requires authorization under Section 57 WHG, which mandates compliance with the best available technology standard (Stand der Technik) as specified in the Abwasserverordnung (AbwV) for each industrial sector.

### Wastewater Levy (Abwasserabgabe)

Under the Abwasserabgabengesetz (AbwAG), a federal wastewater levy is imposed on direct discharges to water bodies. The levy is calculated based on the "damage units" (Schadeinheiten) of pollutants discharged, with rates per damage unit established by federal law. Revenue is earmarked for water quality improvement measures.

### Groundwater Protection

Groundwater receives particularly strong protection under German law. Section 48 WHG establishes the principle that groundwater must not be contaminated or adversely altered. The Grundwasserverordnung (GrwV) transposes the EU Groundwater Directive and establishes quality standards and trend reversal obligations. Water protection zones (Wasserschutzgebiete) established under Section 51 WHG impose graduated restrictions on land use and activities.

### Criminal Liability

Section 324 StGB (Strafgesetzbuch) criminalizes unauthorized pollution of a water body (Gewässerverunreinigung). The offense carries a penalty of imprisonment up to five years or a fine. Section 324a StGB criminalizes soil contamination that could lead to water pollution.

## ECHR & EU

### Water Framework Directive (2000/60/EC)

The WFD, adopted on October 23, 2000, established a comprehensive framework for EU water policy:

**Objectives:** Member states must achieve "good ecological status" and "good chemical status" for all surface water bodies and "good chemical and quantitative status" for groundwater bodies by 2015, with extensions possible to 2021 or 2027 under strictly limited conditions.

**River Basin Management:** Water management is organized by river basin districts rather than administrative boundaries. Member states must prepare River Basin Management Plans (RBMPs) on six-year cycles (2009, 2015, 2021, 2027).

**Combined Approach:** The WFD requires a combined approach to pollution control, using both emission limit values (based on best available technology) and environmental quality standards (based on receiving water quality).

**Cost Recovery:** Article 9 requires member states to implement water pricing policies that provide adequate incentives for efficient water use and contribute to cost recovery for water services, including environmental and resource costs, in accordance with the polluter-pays principle.

### Priority Substances Directive (2013/39/EU)

Directive 2013/39/EU establishes Environmental Quality Standards (EQS) for 45 priority substances in surface waters, including heavy metals, pesticides, and industrial chemicals. Twelve substances are designated as "priority hazardous substances" subject to phaseout.

### Urban Waste Water Treatment Directive (91/271/EEC)

The UWWTD requires collection and treatment of urban waste water:

| Agglomeration Size | Minimum Treatment Level |
|-------------------|------------------------|
| > 10,000 p.e. (sensitive areas) | Tertiary treatment (nutrient removal) |
| > 15,000 p.e. (normal areas) | Secondary (biological) treatment |
| 2,000 - 15,000 p.e. | Secondary treatment |
| < 2,000 p.e. | Appropriate treatment |

A revised UWWTD was adopted in 2024, extending treatment requirements, introducing energy neutrality targets for treatment plants, and establishing extended producer responsibility for pharmaceutical and cosmetic micropollutants.

### ECHR Environmental Rights

**Budayeva and Others v. Russia (Application No. 15339/02, 2008):** The Court found violations of Articles 2 (right to life) and 13 relating to the state's failure to protect residents from a mudslide that contaminated water supplies, establishing positive obligations for environmental disaster prevention.

**Tatar v. Romania (Application No. 67021/01, 2009):** Concerning the Baia Mare cyanide spill (2000), the Court found a violation of Article 8 (private life) due to the state's failure to regulate mining activities that led to severe water contamination.

## Comparative Analysis

### Regulatory Approach

All jurisdictions employ permit-based systems for point source discharges, but with different emphases. The United States relies on technology-based effluent limitations as the primary tool, supplemented by water quality standards. Germany emphasizes the "best available technology" standard aligned with the EU's approach. South Korea uses a combination of concentration-based standards and basin-wide total load management. The EU's WFD takes the most holistic approach, focusing on achieving defined ecological outcomes rather than merely controlling individual discharges.

### Jurisdictional Scope

The scope of protected waters has been a major legal battleground in the United States, with Sackett v. EPA significantly narrowing federal jurisdiction. Germany and the EU protect all water bodies (including groundwater) without the jurisdictional limitations inherent in the CWA's focus on "navigable waters." South Korea's framework covers all public waters and groundwater.

### Nonpoint Source Regulation

The most significant gap across all jurisdictions is the regulation of agricultural nonpoint source pollution. The United States has the weakest tools, relying primarily on voluntary programs. The EU's Nitrates Directive (91/676/EEC) provides more direct regulation of agricultural nitrogen sources. South Korea's TMDL system addresses aggregate pollution loads but does not directly regulate individual agricultural operations. Germany implements the EU Nitrates Directive through the Düngeverordnung (Fertilizer Ordinance).

### Enforcement and Penalties

Criminal enforcement varies significantly. Germany imposes criminal penalties through the Strafgesetzbuch with imprisonment up to five years. The United States imposes substantial civil penalties ($50,000/day) and criminal penalties for knowing violations. South Korea combines administrative penalties with criminal liability for severe violations. EU enforcement relies on member state implementation, with the Commission pursuing infringement proceedings for non-compliance.
