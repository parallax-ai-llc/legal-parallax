## Summary

Obscenity law -- the regulation and criminalization of material deemed to offend prevailing standards of sexual morality or decency -- represents one of the most complex intersections between criminal law and the protection of freedom of expression, with jurisdictions struggling to define the elusive boundary between protected expression and punishable obscenity. South Korea regulates obscenity through Article 243 of the Criminal Act (형법), which criminalizes the distribution of obscene materials (음란물 반포) with imprisonment of up to one year or a fine, and through the Act on the Promotion of Information and Communications Network Utilization and Information Protection (정보통신망 이용촉진 및 정보보호 등에 관한 법률), which imposes penalties of up to one year for distributing obscene materials online; the Constitutional Court of Korea upheld the constitutionality of obscenity regulations in Decision 2006헌바109 (2009) but struck down certain vague provisions of the former Information and Communications Act in Decision 2008헌바157 (2012), requiring greater definitional precision in online obscenity regulation. The United States has developed the most extensive obscenity jurisprudence of any jurisdiction, with the Supreme Court's three-part test in Miller v. California, 413 U.S. 15 (1973) defining obscenity as material that: (1) appeals to the prurient interest according to contemporary community standards, (2) depicts sexual conduct in a patently offensive way as defined by applicable state law, and (3) lacks serious literary, artistic, political, or scientific value, taken as a whole; the Miller test excludes obscenity from First Amendment protection while affording broad protection to all other sexual expression. Germany addresses obscenity through Sections 184 through 184l of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), which comprehensively regulate the dissemination of pornographic writings (Verbreitung pornographischer Inhalte), with a graduated system that distinguishes between simple pornography (legal for adults), violence pornography, animal pornography, and child sexual abuse material (all strictly prohibited), reflecting a regulatory rather than prohibitionist approach to sexual content. The ECHR addresses obscenity through Article 10 (freedom of expression), with the Court's landmark judgment in Handyside v. United Kingdom (Application No. 5493/72, 1976) establishing that freedom of expression extends to information and ideas that "offend, shock or disturb" while granting states a wide margin of appreciation in determining what constitutes obscenity within their societies, and the EU has focused primarily on combating child sexual abuse material through Directive 2011/93/EU rather than harmonizing general obscenity law.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

**Criminal Act (형법):**

**Article 243 -- Distribution of Obscene Materials (음란물 반포):** "A person who distributes, sells, rents, or publicly displays an obscene document, drawing, film, or other object shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than one year or a fine not exceeding 5 million KRW."

**Article 244 -- Production of Obscene Materials (음란물 제조):** "A person who produces, possesses, imports, or exports an obscene document, drawing, film, or other object for the purpose of distribution, sale, rental, or public display shall be punished in accordance with the preceding Article."

### Definition of Obscenity

The Supreme Court has defined "obscenity" (음란, eumnan) through a series of decisions:

**Supreme Court Decision 2006도3558 (2008):** The Court defined obscene material as "content that sexually arouses or stimulates the viewer, violates normal sexual morality and good customs, and lacks any literary, artistic, scientific, or political value when viewed as a whole." This formulation bears similarities to the American Miller test.

**Supreme Court Decision 2008도5203 (2009):** The Court applied the obscenity standard to online content, holding that the same criteria apply regardless of the medium of distribution.

### Online Regulation

South Korea maintains one of the most extensive online content regulation systems among democratic nations:

**Korea Communications Standards Commission (방송통신심의위원회, KCSC):** This government body reviews online content and may order ISPs and platforms to block or remove content deemed obscene, harmful to minors, or otherwise unlawful.

**Information and Communications Network Act (정보통신망법):** Article 44-7 prohibits distribution of obscene content through information and communications networks. Penalties: up to 1 year imprisonment or 10 million KRW fine.

**Constitutional Court Decision 2008헌바157 (2012):** The Court struck down a provision of the former Telecommunications Basic Act that criminalized the use of telecommunications for "indecent" purposes, finding the term "indecent" (불온) unconstitutionally vague and overbroad.

### Child Exploitation Material

The Act on the Protection of Children and Youth Against Sexual Abuse (아동·청소년의 성보호에 관한 법률) imposes severe penalties:

| Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|
| Production of child exploitation material | 5+ years imprisonment (up to life) |
| Distribution | 3+ years imprisonment |
| Possession | Up to 1 year or fine up to 20 million KRW |

## United States

### Legal Framework

**The Miller Test -- Miller v. California, 413 U.S. 15 (1973):** Chief Justice Burger established the three-part test:

1. **Prurient Interest:** "Whether the average person, applying contemporary community standards, would find that the work, taken as a whole, appeals to the prurient interest"
2. **Patent Offensiveness:** "Whether the work depicts or describes, in a patently offensive way, sexual conduct specifically defined by the applicable state law"
3. **Lacking Serious Value:** "Whether the work, taken as a whole, lacks serious literary, artistic, political, or scientific value"

### Historical Development

**Roth v. United States, 354 U.S. 476 (1957):** The Court first held that obscenity is not protected by the First Amendment, defining it as material "utterly without redeeming social importance."

**Memoirs v. Massachusetts, 383 U.S. 413 (1966):** Modified Roth to require that material be "utterly without redeeming social value" -- a near-impossible standard for prosecutors to meet.

**Miller v. California, 413 U.S. 15 (1973):** Replaced the "utterly without" standard with "lacks serious value," making obscenity prosecution more feasible. Introduced the "contemporary community standards" concept, allowing local variation.

**Ashcroft v. Free Speech Coalition, 535 U.S. 234 (2002):** The Court struck down provisions of the Child Pornography Prevention Act (CPPA) that criminalized "virtual" child pornography (computer-generated images), holding that the government's interest in protecting real children does not justify criminalizing constitutionally protected speech.

### Federal Obscenity Statutes

| Statute | Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|---------|
| 18 U.S.C. Section 1460 | Possession with intent to sell obscene material on federal property | Up to 5 years |
| 18 U.S.C. Section 1461 | Mailing obscene matter | Up to 5 years (first); 10 years (repeat) |
| 18 U.S.C. Section 1465 | Production/transportation of obscene material for sale/distribution | Up to 5 years |
| 18 U.S.C. Section 1466A | Obscene visual representations of sexual abuse of children | Up to 10 years (first); 20 years (repeat) |
| 18 U.S.C. Section 2252 | Child sexual exploitation material | Up to 20 years (first); 40 years (repeat) |

### Internet and Obscenity

**Reno v. ACLU, 521 U.S. 844 (1997):** The Court struck down the anti-indecency provisions of the Communications Decency Act (CDA) as unconstitutional, holding that the internet is entitled to full First Amendment protection comparable to print media.

**Section 230 of the CDA:** While the indecency provisions were struck down, Section 230 survived and provides broad immunity to internet platforms for user-generated content, significantly limiting governmental ability to regulate online obscenity.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany takes a graduated regulatory approach, distinguishing between categories of sexual content:

**Section 184 StGB -- Dissemination of Pornographic Content (Verbreitung pornographischer Inhalte):**
- Paragraph 1: Prohibits making pornographic content accessible to persons under 18 years of age. Penalty: up to 1 year or fine.
- Note: Pornographic content that is not classified as "hard-core" (violence, animal, or child abuse pornography) is legal for adults.

**Section 184a StGB -- Dissemination of Violence Pornography (Gewaltpornographie):** Prohibits the production, dissemination, and possession of pornographic content depicting violence against humans. Penalty: up to 3 years or fine.

**Section 184b StGB -- Dissemination of Child Sexual Abuse Material (Verbreitung kinderpornographischer Inhalte):**
- Production: 1-10 years imprisonment
- Distribution: 1-10 years imprisonment (amended 2021 to increase from 3 months minimum to 1 year minimum, making it a felony)
- Possession: 1-5 years imprisonment

**Section 184c StGB -- Youth Pornography (Jugendpornographie):** Addresses sexual content involving persons aged 14-17. Penalties lower than for child abuse material but still significant.

### Definition of Pornography

German courts define pornographic content through the "dominance of sexuality" test:

**BGH, Judgment of December 21, 2004 (1 StR 469/04):** The Court defined pornographic content as material in which "human sexuality is depicted in a grossly obtrusive manner, with the sexual element pushed into the foreground in a way that goes beyond the prevailing moral standards, reducing humans to mere objects of sexual stimulation."

### Youth Protection Framework

Germany operates a comprehensive youth media protection system:

**Kommission für Jugendmedienschutz (KJM):** The Commission for Youth Media Protection oversees compliance with youth protection standards in broadcast and online media.

**Bundesprüfstelle für jugendgefährdende Medien (BPjM):** The Federal Review Board for Media Harmful to Minors may place media on an "index" restricting advertising and distribution, though not prohibiting adult access.

### 2021 Reform

The Gesetz zur Bekämpfung sexualisierter Gewalt gegen Kinder (Act to Combat Sexualized Violence Against Children, 2021) significantly increased penalties:
- Possession of child sexual abuse material elevated from misdemeanor to felony (minimum 1 year)
- Distribution penalties increased
- New provisions for grooming and live-streaming of abuse

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

**Handyside v. United Kingdom (Application No. 5493/72, Judgment of December 7, 1976):** The foundational case on obscenity and freedom of expression. The Court held that Article 10 protects ideas that "offend, shock or disturb the State or any sector of the population" but recognized that states have a margin of appreciation in determining what constitutes obscenity: "it is not possible to find in the domestic law of the various Contracting States a uniform European conception of morals."

**Müller and Others v. Switzerland (Application No. 10737/84, Judgment of May 24, 1988):** The Court upheld the confiscation of explicit paintings depicting sexual acts, finding no violation of Article 10. The Court emphasized the state's margin of appreciation while noting that artistic expression enjoys strong protection.

**Perrin v. United Kingdom (Application No. 5446/03, Decision of October 18, 2005):** The Court declared inadmissible a complaint against a conviction for publishing obscene material on a freely accessible website, holding that the requirement to verify the age of website visitors was a proportionate restriction on Article 10 rights.

### EU Framework

**Directive 2011/93/EU on Combating Sexual Abuse and Exploitation of Children:** The most comprehensive EU instrument addressing sexual content:
- Article 5: Requires criminalization of offenses concerning child sexual abuse material
- Article 25: Requires member states to ensure prompt removal of web pages containing child sexual abuse material and to facilitate blocking of access

**Digital Services Act (Regulation (EU) 2022/2065):** Establishes obligations for online platforms regarding illegal content:
- Notice-and-action mechanisms for flagging illegal content
- Transparency reporting obligations
- Enhanced obligations for very large online platforms

**No General Obscenity Harmonization:** The EU has deliberately not harmonized general obscenity law, recognizing the diversity of moral standards across member states and the sensitivity of the issue to national cultural contexts.

## Comparative Analysis

### Definition Challenges

All jurisdictions struggle with defining obscenity:
- **South Korea:** "Violates normal sexual morality" -- community standards approach
- **United States:** Three-part Miller test -- most structured but still subjective
- **Germany:** "Dominance of sexuality" test -- focuses on the work's sexual emphasis
- **ECHR:** Wide margin of appreciation -- effectively defers to national definitions

### Regulatory Models

Three distinct models emerge:

**Prohibitionist Model (South Korea):** Distribution of pornographic material is broadly criminalized, with extensive government content filtering. Adult access to pornography is restricted.

**Harm-Based Model (Germany):** Adult pornography is legal but regulated to prevent access by minors. "Hard-core" categories (violence, animal, child abuse) are strictly prohibited.

**Expression-Protective Model (United States):** Only material meeting the stringent Miller test is unprotected. All other sexual content, including hardcore pornography, receives full First Amendment protection.

### Digital Regulation

The internet has transformed obscenity regulation:
- **South Korea:** Most aggressive filtering and content blocking among democracies
- **Germany:** Youth protection framework adapted to online context
- **United States:** Minimal government regulation due to First Amendment and Section 230
- **EU:** Platform responsibility approach through Digital Services Act
