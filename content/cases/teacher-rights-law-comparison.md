## Summary

Teacher employment rights -- encompassing tenure protections, academic freedom within the classroom, dismissal procedures, and collective bargaining entitlements -- represent a critical intersection of labor law, education law, and constitutional law that varies dramatically across legal systems. South Korea regulates teacher employment primarily through the Act on the Establishment, Management, etc. of Private Schools (사립학교법), the Educational Officials Act (교육공무원법), and the State Public Officials Act (국가공무원법), establishing a dual-track system in which public school teachers enjoy civil servant status (교육공무원) with strong protections against dismissal, while private school teachers receive analogous but contractual protections; the Teachers' Status Improvement Special Act (교원지위법) further guarantees teachers' right to professional dignity and due process in disciplinary matters. The United States provides teacher employment protections through a patchwork of state tenure statutes, collective bargaining agreements, and constitutional due process requirements as established in Cleveland Board of Education v. Loudermill (1985) and Perry v. Sindermann (1972); the landscape has shifted dramatically since 2010 with tenure reform movements in states such as Wisconsin (Act 10, 2011), which curtailed collective bargaining rights, and the California decision in Vergara v. State of California (2014), which briefly struck down teacher tenure statutes before reversal on appeal. Germany provides the most robust protections through the Beamte (civil servant) system, under which most public school teachers are appointed as Beamte auf Lebenszeit (civil servants for life) under Article 33(5) of the Grundgesetz, enjoying virtually irrevocable employment and constitutional protections for their traditional principles of service (hergebrachte Grundsätze des Berufsbeamtentums); however, Berlin notably shifted to employing teachers as Angestellte (employees) rather than Beamte from 2004 to 2023, creating a significant natural experiment in teacher employment models. At the European level, the ECHR protects teachers' employment rights through Article 8 (private life), Article 10 (expression), and Article 11 (association), while EU Directive 1999/70/EC on fixed-term work and the Framework Agreement on fixed-term contracts impose requirements on member states to prevent abusive use of successive fixed-term contracts for teachers; the CJEU's ruling in Mascolo v. Ministero dell'Istruzione (C-22/13, 2014) established that member states cannot maintain systems of indefinite temporary teacher appointments that circumvent these protections.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Teacher employment in South Korea operates under a comprehensive statutory regime differentiating between public and private school teachers:

**Educational Officials Act (교육공무원법):** Enacted in 1953 and extensively amended, this statute governs the appointment, promotion, disciplinary proceedings, and dismissal of public school teachers. Public school teachers hold the status of educational civil servants (교육공무원), a subcategory of state public officials (국가공무원) subject to the State Public Officials Act (국가공무원법).

**Private Schools Act (사립학교법):** Private school teachers are formally appointed by the school foundation (학교법인), but Articles 53-58 of the Private Schools Act extend many of the protections applicable to public school teachers, including restrictions on dismissal and requirements for disciplinary due process.

**Teachers' Status Improvement Special Act (교원의 지위 향상 및 교육활동 보호를 위한 특별법):** Enacted in 1991 and substantially amended in 2016 and 2019, this statute establishes the Teacher Grievance Review Committee (교원소청심사위원회) to adjudicate disputes over teacher disciplinary actions and guarantees teachers' right to professional dignity and protection from abuse.

### Appointment and Tenure

The Korean teacher employment system involves multiple stages:

| Stage | Description | Duration |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| Teacher Certification (교원자격증) | Obtained through education degree or certification program | Prerequisite |
| Open Competitive Examination (임용시험) | Written and practical examinations for public school positions | Annual cycle |
| Probationary Period (수습기간) | Initial service period | 1 year |
| Level 2 Teacher (2급 정교사) | Initial appointment status | Minimum 3 years before Level 1 |
| Level 1 Teacher (1급 정교사) | Full qualification after experience and training | Career-long appointment |

Teachers appointed through the public examination system enjoy tenure comparable to other civil servants, with mandatory retirement at age 62 (국가공무원법 Article 74). Dismissal requires formal disciplinary proceedings before the Teacher Disciplinary Committee (교원징계위원회), with grounds limited to violation of duties, dereliction, or conduct unbecoming a public official.

### Disciplinary Procedures and Due Process

Disciplinary sanctions against teachers range from reprimand (견책) to removal from office (해임) and dismissal (파면). The Teacher Grievance Review Committee (교원소청심사위원회) provides an administrative appeals mechanism, and teachers may subsequently pursue judicial review in the Administrative Court (행정법원). The Constitutional Court of Korea has held that teachers' right to hold office constitutes a fundamental right under Article 25 of the Constitution, requiring that any infringement satisfy the proportionality principle under Article 37(2).

In Seoul Metropolitan Office of Education v. Kim (Seoul Administrative Court, 2018), the court overturned the dismissal of a teacher for social media posts criticizing education policy, holding that the disciplinary authority failed to adequately consider the teacher's freedom of expression under Article 21 of the Constitution.

### Strike Prohibition and Collective Action

Public school teachers, as civil servants, are prohibited from striking under Article 66 of the State Public Officials Act (국가공무원법). The Korean Teachers and Education Workers Union (전국교직원노동조합, KTU), established in 1989 and legally recognized in 1999, has repeatedly challenged this prohibition. In 2013, the Park Geun-hye administration deregistered the KTU for admitting dismissed teachers as members, a decision upheld by the Supreme Court in 2015 but later effectively reversed through the amendment of the Trade Union and Labor Relations Adjustment Act (노동조합법) in 2020.

### Teacher Protection from Student/Parent Abuse

A major development in Korean teacher rights law occurred in 2023 following the suicide of a young elementary school teacher in Seoul, which catalyzed the Teachers' Rights Protection Movement (교권보호운동). The National Assembly passed the Act on Protection of Educational Activities (교육활동 보호법) amendments in September 2023, strengthening protections against abusive parents and students, limiting malicious complaints, and providing teachers with clearer authority to maintain classroom discipline without fear of legal retaliation.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Teacher employment in the United States is governed primarily by state law, with federal constitutional protections providing minimum due process standards.

### Constitutional Due Process

The Supreme Court has established that teachers with tenure or a legitimate expectation of continued employment possess a property interest protected by the Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause:

**Cleveland Board of Education v. Loudermill, 470 U.S. 532 (1985):** The Court held that a public employee with a property interest in continued employment is entitled to pre-termination notice and an opportunity to respond before being dismissed, as well as a more formal post-termination hearing.

**Perry v. Sindermann, 408 U.S. 593 (1972):** The Court held that even without formal tenure, a teacher may have a property interest in continued employment based on an implied promise or de facto tenure system, entitling them to due process before non-renewal.

**Board of Regents v. Roth, 408 U.S. 564 (1972):** In contrast, the Court held that a non-tenured teacher with no expectation of continued employment has no property interest requiring due process protections.

### State Tenure Systems

Teacher tenure varies dramatically across the 50 states:

| Model | States (Examples) | Key Features |
|-------|-------------------|-------------|
| Strong Tenure | New Jersey, New York, Massachusetts | Multi-year probation; for-cause dismissal only; elaborate hearing procedures |
| Modified Tenure | California (post-Vergara), Florida, Colorado | Shorter probation; performance-based evaluation integrated; streamlined dismissal |
| Weak/No Tenure | North Carolina, Georgia, Florida (post-2011) | Employment at-will or annual contracts; limited procedural protections |
| Collective Bargaining States | Illinois, Michigan (pre-2012), Connecticut | Tenure supplemented by CBA grievance procedures |

### Tenure Reform Movements

**Vergara v. State of California (2014):** A Los Angeles Superior Court judge ruled that California's teacher tenure statutes -- including the two-year probationary period, the "last in, first out" layoff provision, and the dismissal procedures -- violated the California Constitution's equal protection clause by disproportionately concentrating ineffective teachers in low-income schools. The California Court of Appeal reversed in 2016, holding that the plaintiffs failed to establish a causal link between the statutes and harm to students.

**Wisconsin Act 10 (2011):** Governor Scott Walker's signature legislation effectively eliminated collective bargaining rights for most public employees, including teachers, restricting negotiations to base wages only and requiring annual recertification of unions. The Wisconsin Supreme Court upheld Act 10 in Madison Teachers, Inc. v. Walker (2014).

**Janus v. AFSCME, 585 U.S. 878 (2018):** The Supreme Court held that requiring non-member public employees to pay agency fees to unions violates the First Amendment, fundamentally altering the financial structure of teacher unions nationwide.

### Teacher Speech Rights

**Pickering v. Board of Education, 391 U.S. 563 (1968):** The Court established the balancing test for public employee speech, weighing the teacher's interest in commenting on matters of public concern against the employer's interest in efficient operation of public services.

**Garcetti v. Ceballos, 547 U.S. 410 (2006):** Speech made pursuant to official duties is not protected. The interaction between Garcetti and teacher classroom speech remains unsettled, with circuits split on whether curricular speech is "pursuant to official duties."

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's system of teacher employment is deeply embedded in the tradition of the professional civil service (Berufsbeamtentum), with most public school teachers enjoying the status of civil servants for life.

### Constitutional Foundation

**Article 33(5) Grundgesetz:** "The law governing the public service shall be regulated and developed with due regard to the traditional principles of the professional civil service (hergebrachte Grundsätze des Berufsbeamtentums)." The Federal Constitutional Court has interpreted this provision to guarantee core elements of civil service status including: the principle of lifelong appointment (Lebenszeitprinzip), the duty of loyalty (Treuepflicht), the maintenance principle (Alimentationsprinzip) requiring adequate compensation, and the prohibition of strikes (Streikverbot).

**Article 7(1) Grundgesetz:** "The entire school system shall be under the supervision of the state (Das gesamte Schulwesen steht unter der Aufsicht des Staates)." This provision establishes the state's comprehensive authority over education, within which teacher employment is situated.

### Beamte Status for Teachers

In most German Länder, public school teachers are appointed as Beamte auf Lebenszeit after completing:

| Phase | Duration | Status |
|-------|----------|--------|
| University Studies | 4-5 years (Bachelor + Master of Education) | Student |
| Referendariat (Preparatory Service) | 12-24 months depending on Land | Beamter auf Widerruf (revocable civil servant) |
| Probezeit (Probationary Period) | Typically 2-3 years | Beamter auf Probe (probationary civil servant) |
| Lifetime Appointment | Until retirement (currently age 67 in most Länder) | Beamter auf Lebenszeit |

Once appointed auf Lebenszeit, a teacher can only be dismissed through formal disciplinary proceedings (Disziplinarverfahren) for serious misconduct such as criminal conviction, persistent dereliction of duty, or conduct incompatible with civil service obligations. The disciplinary courts (Disziplinargerichte) exercise jurisdiction over dismissal proceedings, ensuring judicial oversight.

### The Berlin Experiment (2004-2023)

Berlin became the only German Land to stop appointing teachers as Beamte in 2004, instead employing them as Angestellte (employees) under collective agreements (Tarifvertrag der Länder, TV-L). This created several notable differences: Berlin teachers could legally strike (as demonstrated in significant work actions in 2013 and 2019), received net lower compensation due to social security contributions not required of Beamte, and experienced higher turnover. Facing a severe teacher shortage, Berlin reversed course in 2023, reintroducing Beamte status for teachers, providing empirical evidence of the practical consequences of different employment models.

### Treuepflicht and Political Activity

The Beamte duty of loyalty (Treuepflicht) requires teachers to actively support the free democratic basic order (freiheitliche demokratische Grundordnung). This has generated significant jurisprudence:

**Radikalenerlass (1972):** The decree barring political extremists from civil service positions (including teaching) was applied to exclude members of both far-left and far-right organizations. The Federal Constitutional Court upheld the requirement of loyalty to the democratic order in BVerfGE 39, 334 (1975), though the practice was gradually abandoned in most Länder by the 1990s.

**BVerfG, 2 BvR 1368/16 (2020):** The Federal Constitutional Court upheld the dismissal of a teacher who had been active in the far-right NPD party, confirming that the Treuepflicht requires active engagement with democratic values incompatible with extremist political activity, even outside of school.

### Teaching Freedom and Curricular Constraints

While Article 5(3) GG guarantees freedom of teaching (Lehrfreiheit), school teachers' freedom is more constrained than that of university professors. The Kultusministerkonferenz (Standing Conference of Education Ministers) and individual Länder establish binding curricula (Lehrpläne), and teachers must follow these frameworks. However, teachers retain pedagogical freedom (pädagogische Freiheit) in choosing methods of instruction and may deviate from prescribed materials where pedagogically justified.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Framework Directive on Fixed-Term Work

**Directive 1999/70/EC** incorporates the Framework Agreement on Fixed-Term Work concluded by ETUC, UNICE, and CEEP. Clause 5 requires member states to adopt measures to prevent abuse arising from successive fixed-term employment contracts, including: objective reasons justifying renewal, maximum total duration, and maximum number of renewals.

**Mascolo v. Ministero dell'Istruzione (Joined Cases C-22/13, C-61/13, C-63/13, C-418/13, CJEU 2014):** The CJEU held that Italy's system of employing teachers on successive fixed-term contracts for years without any prospect of permanent appointment, while covering permanent staffing needs, violated Directive 1999/70/EC. The Court ruled that the objective of covering temporary vacancies cannot justify indefinite renewal of fixed-term contracts when the needs are in fact permanent.

**Commission v. Luxembourg (C-274/15, 2017):** The CJEU found that Luxembourg failed to transpose adequate anti-abuse measures for fixed-term teacher contracts.

### ECHR Teacher Rights Jurisprudence

**Vogt v. Germany (Application No. 17851/91, ECtHR Grand Chamber 1995):** The Court held that Germany's dismissal of a schoolteacher for her membership in the German Communist Party (DKP) violated Article 10 (expression) and Article 11 (association) of the ECHR. Despite acknowledging Germany's legitimate interest in ensuring loyalty of civil servants, the Court found the dismissal disproportionate given that the teacher had never engaged in anti-constitutional activities within the classroom.

**Fernandez Martinez v. Spain (Application No. 56030/07, ECtHR Grand Chamber 2014):** The Court held that the non-renewal of a Catholic religion teacher's contract after he publicly advocated for optional priestly celibacy did not violate Article 8 ECHR, given the margin of appreciation accorded to states in religious education matters and the autonomy of religious organizations.

**Saniewski v. Poland (Application No. 40319/98, 2001):** The Court addressed teacher disciplinary proceedings, affirming that Article 6 ECHR fair trial guarantees apply to teacher dismissal proceedings where civil rights are at stake.

### European Social Charter

The European Social Charter (Revised), Article 1 (right to work), Article 2 (just conditions of work), and Article 6 (right to bargain collectively) apply to teachers. The European Committee of Social Rights has found violations where teacher employment conditions fail to meet these standards, including in Complaint No. 73/2011 (concerning precarious teacher employment in Greece).

### EU Teachers' Mobility

Directive 2005/36/EC on the recognition of professional qualifications, as amended by Directive 2013/55/EU, facilitates teacher mobility across EU member states by establishing frameworks for mutual recognition of teaching qualifications, though implementation varies significantly and the regulated profession of teacher remains subject to individual member state compensation requirements.

## Comparative Analysis

### Employment Security Spectrum

The four jurisdictions occupy distinct positions on the employment security spectrum. Germany's Beamte system provides the most absolute protection, with lifetime appointment and near-impossibility of dismissal. South Korea's civil servant status for public school teachers offers comparable protections within a Confucian tradition that venerates the teaching profession. The United States presents the widest variation, from strong tenure states where dismissal requires lengthy and expensive proceedings to at-will employment states where teachers serve at the pleasure of the school district. The EU framework establishes a floor against abuse of fixed-term contracts but does not mandate tenure.

### Academic Freedom in the Classroom

A fundamental tension exists in all jurisdictions between teacher autonomy and state curricular authority. Germany resolves this through the distinction between Lehrfreiheit (protected by Article 5(3) GG) and the state's comprehensive school supervision authority (Article 7(1) GG), granting teachers methodological but not substantive freedom. The United States employs the Pickering-Connick balancing test for teacher speech but has not clearly resolved the scope of curricular freedom, particularly after Garcetti. South Korea maintains strong centralized curriculum control through the National Curriculum (국가교육과정), with limited teacher discretion.

### Collective Action Rights

The right to strike presents the starkest contrast: German Beamte teachers are constitutionally prohibited from striking (a position challenged at the ECtHR in Jansen v. Germany but upheld by the Federal Constitutional Court in BVerfGE 148, 296 (2018)); American teachers' strike rights vary by state, with notable recent actions in West Virginia (2018), Oklahoma (2018), and Arizona (2018) demonstrating the power of collective action even in restrictive legal environments; Korean public school teachers face a statutory strike prohibition but have engaged in work-to-rule actions; and the ECHR generally protects the right to strike under Article 11 but permits proportionate restrictions for civil servants.

### Dismissal for Political Activity

The Vogt v. Germany decision highlighted the tension between teacher loyalty requirements and fundamental rights. Germany maintains the most extensive loyalty requirements through the Treuepflicht, though enforcement has moderated since the Radikalenerlass era. South Korea prohibits teachers' political activity under Article 65 of the State Public Officials Act, a restriction upheld by the Constitutional Court but criticized by international observers. The United States provides the broadest political speech protections for teachers, though the Pickering balance permits some restrictions on disruptive speech.
