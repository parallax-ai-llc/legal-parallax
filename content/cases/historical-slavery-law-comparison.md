## Summary

Slavery -- the legal institution by which a human being is treated as property, subject to the ownership and control of another person -- existed in virtually every pre-modern civilization and was regulated by elaborate legal codes that defined the slave's status, the master's rights, the conditions for enslavement and manumission, and the legal consequences of interactions between free persons and slaves. Ancient Near Eastern slavery, documented in the Code of Hammurabi and thousands of cuneiform contracts, recognized slaves as both property and persons with limited legal protections, including the right to engage in commerce, marry, and accumulate property (peculium) under certain conditions. Roman slavery (servitus) produced the most elaborately theorized slave law in the ancient world, with the jurists grappling with the fundamental contradiction of classifying human beings as res (things) while acknowledging their humanity through doctrines such as favor libertatis (the legal presumption in favor of freedom) and the increasingly elaborate pathways to manumission that made Roman slavery distinctively permeable. East Asian slavery, particularly the Korean nobi (奴婢) system that persisted from the Three Kingdoms period through the late Joseon Dynasty, created a hereditary servile class that was legally distinct from both chattel slavery and European serfdom, with the nobi attached to households and government offices, subject to inheritance and sale, but retaining certain customary rights including family formation and, in some periods, separate residence and economic activity. Islamic law (Sharia) regulated slavery within a framework that simultaneously legitimized the institution as part of the divinely ordained social order and imposed distinctive obligations on masters, including requirements for humane treatment, religious instruction, and the strong encouragement of manumission as a meritorious act (hasana), while prohibiting the enslavement of free Muslims and establishing that the children of a slave mother by her master (umm al-walad) were born free. Medieval European law encompassed both the remnants of ancient slavery and the distinct institution of serfdom (servage, Leibeigenschaft), where the serf was bound to the land rather than personally owned, could not be sold separately from the land, and retained limited but legally recognized rights within the manorial system. English and colonial common law developed the Atlantic slave codes of the 17th-18th centuries, which represented a radical departure from earlier slavery systems by creating a racially defined, hereditary chattel slavery that denied slaves virtually all legal personality -- codified in statutes such as the Virginia Slave Code of 1705 and Barbados Slave Code of 1661, and gradually dismantled through the Somerset decision (1772), the Slave Trade Act (1807), the Slavery Abolition Act (1833), and the Thirteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution (1865).

## Ancient Near East

Slavery in the Ancient Near East was a widespread institution regulated by detailed legal codes, but it differed from later Atlantic chattel slavery in significant respects, including the recognition of slaves' limited legal personality and relatively accessible paths to freedom.

### Mesopotamian Slavery

The Code of Hammurabi contains extensive provisions on slavery (Sections 15-20, 170-176, 226-227, 278-282), revealing a system in which slaves were simultaneously property and persons with limited protections.

**Sources of enslavement**: The primary sources were warfare (prisoners of war), debt (a debtor or family member could be enslaved for a maximum of three years under Hammurabi Section 117 -- a remarkable limitation), birth to a slave mother, and sale of children by families in distress. Section 117's three-year limit on debt bondage is historically significant as one of the earliest legal protections against permanent enslavement for debt.

**Slave as property**: Slaves could be bought, sold, pledged, and inherited. Sections 278-282 establish a one-month warranty against certain diseases (the bennu disease) in slave sales. A slave who escaped was to be returned to the owner (Section 15-16), and anyone harboring a fugitive slave faced the death penalty (Section 16). Branding or tattooing of slaves was practiced, though altering a slave's mark was criminally punished (Section 226-227: the barber who shaved the slave-mark without the owner's consent lost his hand).

**Slave as person**: Despite their property status, Mesopotamian slaves retained limited legal personality. Sections 175-176 address marriage between free women and slaves, recognizing the union and specifying inheritance rules for the children. Slaves could accumulate property (peculium), engage in commerce, and in some cases purchase their own freedom. The children of a free man and slave woman could be acknowledged and receive inheritance rights (Sections 170-171). Slaves could testify in court under certain circumstances.

**Manumission**: Freedom could be obtained through purchase (the slave buying freedom with accumulated peculium), the master's voluntary grant, adoption (the master adopting the slave as a child), or dedication to temple service. The process required formal documentation, and freed slaves might retain certain obligations to their former masters.

Muhammad Dandamaev's *Slavery in Babylonia: From Nabopolassar to Alexander the Great (626-331 BC)* (1984) provides the most comprehensive study of neo-Babylonian slavery.

### Egyptian Slavery

Egyptian slavery, long debated by scholars, differed from Mesopotamian patterns. The terminology is ambiguous; the word hem (ḥm) could refer to servants, dependents, or slaves proper. Large-scale state labor (including the monumental building projects) was primarily performed by corvée labor from free peasants, not slaves, as documented by Mark Lehner's archaeological work at the Giza workers' village. Private slavery existed but was less economically central than in Mesopotamia. Slaves in Egypt could own property, marry, and testify in court, and manumission was attested in documentary sources from the New Kingdom onward.

## Roman Law

Roman slavery produced the most elaborate legal framework for human bondage in the ancient world, one that profoundly influenced subsequent slave systems and generated fundamental jurisprudential debates about the nature of legal personality.

### The Slave's Legal Status

Roman law classified slaves as res (things) within the broader category of res mancipi (Gaius, Inst. 2.14a). The slave had no legal personality: could not own property (all acquisitions vested in the master), could not marry (only contubernium, an informal union without legal consequences), could not sue or be sued, and could not make a valid will. Yet the jurists recognized the tension: Ulpian acknowledged that "as far as the civil law is concerned, slaves are regarded as nothing; but not so by natural law, because, as far as natural law is concerned, all men are equal" (Digest 50.17.32).

### The Peculium

In practice, the peculium system significantly modified the slave's propertyless status. Masters routinely permitted slaves to hold a peculium -- a fund of property that the slave managed and used in business, though legally belonging to the master. The praetor's edict created the actio de peculio, allowing third parties to sue the master on the slave's contracts up to the value of the peculium, and the actio tributoria for slaves engaged in trade with the master's knowledge. The most capable slaves (servi ordinarii) managed vast commercial enterprises, held sub-slaves (vicarii), and accumulated substantial wealth, creating a system that Keith Bradley calls "quasi-freedom" within legal bondage.

### Sources of Enslavement

The principal source was warfare: vast numbers of captives were enslaved during the Roman conquests (Caesar reportedly enslaved one million Gauls). Other sources included birth to a slave mother (partus sequitur ventrem -- the child follows the condition of the mother, established by the ius gentium), sale of free persons into slavery across the frontier (prohibited for Roman citizens), penal slavery (condemnation to the mines or gladiatorial schools), and foundlings (expositi).

### Manumission and the Freedman's Status

Roman manumission was distinctively permeable and elaborate:

**Formal manumission** created full freedom and (under the Republic) Roman citizenship: manumissio vindicta (before a magistrate, involving a staged lawsuit), manumissio censu (enrollment on the census rolls), and manumissio testamento (by the master's will, with the slave either directly freed or directed to be freed by the heir).

**Informal manumission** (inter amicos, per epistulam, per mensam) was recognized by the praetor though not creating full citizenship until Justinian's reforms.

Augustus's Lex Fufia Caninia (2 BCE) restricted the number of slaves who could be manumitted by will (proportional to the total number of slaves, maximum 100), and the Lex Aelia Sentia (4 CE) imposed minimum age requirements and prohibited manumission in fraud of creditors. These laws reflected concerns about the social consequences of large-scale manumission, including the influx of freedmen into the citizen body.

Freedmen (liberti) occupied a distinctive intermediate status: they became Roman citizens but could not hold public office, owed continued obligations (obsequium and operae) to their former masters as patrons, and their property reverted to the patron if they died without children. Despite these restrictions, freedmen became enormously successful in Roman commerce and administration. The tombstone of the freedman baker Eurysaces and the literary figure Trimalchio in Petronius's Satyricon illustrate the social prominence some freedmen achieved.

Keith Bradley's *Slaves and Masters in the Roman Empire* (1987) and *Slavery and Society at Rome* (1994) are essential modern treatments.

### Humanitarian Reforms

Imperial legislation progressively restricted the master's absolute power. Claudius granted freedom to sick slaves abandoned by their masters (Suetonius, Claudius 25). Hadrian prohibited killing slaves without judicial authorization and abolished the slave prisons (ergastula). Antoninus Pius extended the rules on killing slaves (Gaius, Inst. 1.53) and established that excessively cruel masters could be compelled to sell their slaves. The Constitutio Antoniniana (212 CE) did not extend citizenship to slaves, but the Christian emperors further expanded protections, and Justinian's legislation strongly favored manumission.

## East Asian Law

The East Asian slavery systems, particularly Korea's nobi institution, represent a distinctive form of hereditary servile status that differed from both Mediterranean chattel slavery and European serfdom.

### Chinese Slavery

Chinese slavery evolved through successive dynasties. The Qin and Han Dynasties distinguished between government slaves (guannu, 官奴) and private slaves (sinu, 私奴). Debt bondage, self-sale during famine, and criminal condemnation were common sources. The Tang Code (Article 193) distinguished between "good people" (liangmin, 良民) and "base people" (jianmin, 賤民), with slaves occupying the lowest category. Slaves could not marry free persons, and their children inherited their status. However, the overall trajectory in China was toward diminishing reliance on slavery: the Song Dynasty significantly restricted the slave trade, and the Ming and Qing Dynasties further reduced the slave population, though various forms of bonded servitude persisted.

### Korean Nobi System

The Korean nobi (奴婢, literally "male slave/female slave") system was one of the most elaborate and long-lasting hereditary servile institutions in world history, persisting from the Three Kingdoms period (c. 57 BCE - 668 CE) through the Joseon Dynasty's abolition in 1894.

**Categories**: The Joseon Gyeongguk Daejeon (1485) distinguished between government nobi (gwannobi, 官奴婢) -- attached to government offices and performing labor or paying tribute cloth -- and private nobi (sanobi, 私奴婢) -- owned by individual yangban (aristocratic) households. Within these categories, "residing nobi" (solje nobi) lived with and served the master directly, while "outside nobi" (oegeo nobi) lived separately and paid annual tribute.

**Status determination**: The principle of jongmobop (從母法, "following the mother's status") generally prevailed from the mid-Joseon period: a child's status followed the mother's. Earlier periods had applied jongbubeop (following the father) or various mixed rules. This meant that a free father and nobi mother produced nobi children, incentivizing the system's expansion. At its peak, the nobi population may have constituted 30-40% of the total population.

**Rights and restrictions**: Unlike Roman slaves, nobi retained some legal personality. They could own property (though with restrictions), form families, and were listed in household registers. Private nobi could be bought, sold, and inherited -- nobi division was a significant component of inheritance documents (bunjaegi). Government nobi performed defined labor obligations but lived in their own households.

**Abolition**: The nobi system was gradually dismantled: the government freed government nobi in stages (1801 for most government nobi registers' destruction; official abolition of the system in 1894 as part of the Gabo Reforms). James B. Palais's *Confucian Statecraft and Korean Institutions* (1996) and Kim Bok-rae's work on the nobi system provide essential analysis.

## Islamic Law

Islamic law simultaneously legitimized slavery as an existing institution while imposing distinctive humanitarian obligations and powerfully encouraging manumission.

### The Legal Framework

The Quran addresses slavery in multiple passages (4:3, 4:25, 4:36, 24:32-33, 90:13), consistently urging kind treatment and manumission. The Prophet Muhammad himself freed slaves and declared that "whoever frees a Muslim slave, God will free every limb of his from the Fire" (Bukhari and Muslim). Islamic jurisprudence developed from these sources a framework that recognized slavery as legally permissible but morally problematic -- an institution to be regulated, humanized, and ultimately reduced.

**Sources of enslavement**: Classical Islamic law restricted legitimate enslavement to two sources: (1) capture of non-Muslims in legitimate jihad, and (2) birth to two slave parents. Crucially, free Muslims could not be enslaved, and the widespread pre-Islamic practice of debt bondage was prohibited. These restrictions theoretically limited the supply of slaves to external acquisition.

**Master's obligations**: The master was required to provide adequate food, clothing, shelter, and medical care (obligations enforceable by the qadi/judge). Excessive cruelty was grounds for compulsory sale (some jurists) or compulsory manumission (others). The master was prohibited from overworking the slave or assigning degrading tasks. The hadith literature contains extensive instructions: "Your slaves are your brothers whom God has placed under your authority. Whoever has a brother under his authority should feed him from what he eats and clothe him from what he wears" (Bukhari).

### Manumission

Islamic law developed elaborate pathways to freedom:

**Kitaba (contractual manumission)**: The Quran (24:33) enjoins masters to grant freedom contracts to slaves who request them: "And those who seek a writing [of emancipation], write it for them if you know there is good in them." Under kitaba, the slave paid an agreed price in installments; during the contract period (mukatab status), the slave had enhanced legal capacity to earn money.

**Umm al-walad**: A female slave who bore her master's child became umm al-walad ("mother of the child") and could not thereafter be sold. Upon the master's death, she became automatically free. The child was born free with full inheritance rights from the father.

**Kafara (expiation)**: The Quran prescribes freeing a slave as expiation for certain sins, including breaking an oath (5:89), accidental homicide (4:92), and zihar (a pre-Islamic form of repudiation, 58:3).

**Tadbir**: The master could make manumission conditional on his death (like Roman testamentary manumission), declaring the slave a mudabbar.

Despite these extensive mechanisms for manumission, slavery persisted across the Islamic world for centuries, with the trans-Saharan and Indian Ocean slave trades continuing into the 19th and 20th centuries. Bernard Lewis's *Race and Slavery in the Middle East* (1990) and Jonathan Brown's *Slavery and Islam* (2019) provide modern critical assessments.

## Medieval European Law

Medieval Europe transitioned from ancient slavery to the distinctive institution of serfdom, creating a complex hierarchy of unfreedom tied to land rather than personal ownership.

### The Transition from Slavery to Serfdom

Late Roman slavery gradually merged with the status of coloni (tied agricultural tenants, bound to the land by Diocletian's and Constantine's legislation) to produce the medieval serf. By the Carolingian period (8th-9th centuries), the distinction between slave (servus) and serf had blurred, with both groups increasingly attached to the land as part of the manorial system. Marc Bloch's classic analysis in *Feudal Society* (1940) traces this transition.

### Serfdom

The serf (villein in England, serf in France, Leibeigener in Germany) differed from a chattel slave in critical respects: the serf was bound to the land, not personally owned; could not be sold separately from the land; retained recognized (if limited) legal rights under the custom of the manor; could hold property, though subject to the lord's claims; and could marry, though sometimes requiring the lord's permission (merchet).

The Sachsenspiegel (c. 1220-1235) details the serf's obligations in Saxon law: labor services (Frondienst), dues in kind and money, the obligation to use the lord's mill and oven (banal rights), the restriction on leaving the manor, and the payment of chevage (head tax) and heriot (death duty). However, the same text recognizes the serf's customary rights to hold land, to inheritance of tenant holdings, and to the protection of the manorial court.

### Emancipation

Serfdom declined at different rates across Europe. In England, the Black Death (1348-1349) and the Peasants' Revolt (1381) accelerated the transition from labor services to money rents, and villeinage effectively disappeared by the 16th century. In France, serfdom persisted in some regions until the Revolution of 1789 (abolished by the August 4 decree). In Eastern Europe, the "second serfdom" of the 16th-18th centuries actually intensified servile obligations, and Russian serfdom persisted until Alexander II's emancipation in 1861.

## Common Law Origins

The English common law and colonial legal systems developed the Atlantic slave codes that created the distinctive institution of racially defined chattel slavery.

### English Common Law and Slavery

Medieval English common law recognized villeinage (serfdom) but had no established body of law governing chattel slavery. When the Atlantic slave trade created the need for slave law in the colonies, English law initially lacked a framework, leading to ad hoc colonial legislation.

The pivotal case of **Somerset v. Stewart** (1772, 98 ER 499), decided by Lord Mansfield, held that chattel slavery had no basis in English common law and that a slave brought to England could not be forcibly removed. Mansfield's famous dictum -- "The state of slavery is of such a nature, that it is incapable of being introduced on any reasons, moral or political, but only by positive law" -- established that slavery required positive legislative authorization and could not exist by custom or common law alone. The decision's scope was debated (it did not abolish slavery in the colonies), but it became a landmark in the history of human freedom.

### Colonial Slave Codes

Colonial slave legislation created a system of racial chattel slavery without historical precedent in its severity:

**Barbados Slave Code (1661)**: One of the earliest comprehensive slave codes, it classified enslaved Africans as real estate (freehold property), denied them any legal personality, and imposed severe punishments for resistance. It became the model for slave codes throughout the British Caribbean and the American South.

**Virginia Slave Code (1705)**: Consolidated earlier Virginia legislation, establishing that all non-Christian servants imported into the colony were slaves; that the children of enslaved women were themselves enslaved (partus sequitur ventrem, borrowed from Roman law but applied exclusively on racial lines); that slaves were real property; and that masters who killed slaves during "correction" were not guilty of a felony.

**South Carolina Slave Code (1740)**: Perhaps the most severe, enacted after the Stono Rebellion (1739). It prohibited slaves from learning to read, assembling in groups, earning money, or traveling without a written pass. The presumption was that all Black persons were enslaved unless they could prove otherwise.

### Abolition

The abolition of slavery in the British Empire and the United States proceeded through stages: the Slave Trade Act 1807 (abolishing the transatlantic slave trade), the Slavery Abolition Act 1833 (freeing slaves throughout the British Empire, with a transition period and compensation to owners), and the Thirteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution (1865). The compensated emancipation model of 1833, under which the British government paid 20 million pounds to slave owners, contrasts sharply with the uncompensated emancipation that followed the American Civil War.

## Historical Significance and Modern Legacy

The comparative study of historical slavery systems reveals both universal patterns and critical differences. Every system grappled with the fundamental contradiction of treating human beings as property while acknowledging their humanity. Yet the Atlantic chattel slavery system was distinctive in its racial definition, its severity, its hereditary character through the maternal line, and its near-total denial of the slave's legal personality. The Roman system, by contrast, was distinguished by its permeability (manumission creating citizens) and its lack of racial basis. The Islamic system's emphasis on humane treatment and encouraged manumission coexisted with the large-scale continuation of the slave trade. The Korean nobi system's hereditary character and potential scale (30-40% of the population) make it one of the most extensive slavery systems in proportional terms. Understanding these systems comparatively remains essential for addressing slavery's enduring legacies in modern law and society, including reparations debates, structural racism, and the ongoing struggle against human trafficking and forced labor.
