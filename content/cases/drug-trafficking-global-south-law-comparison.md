## Summary

Drug trafficking law across the Global South encompasses some of the most punitive criminal provisions in the world, alongside some of the most innovative reform movements. China maintains an uncompromising zero-tolerance approach rooted in the historical trauma of the Opium Wars, with the Criminal Law Articles 347-357 prescribing the death penalty for trafficking 50 grams or more of heroin/methamphetamine or 1,000 grams or more of opium, and executing more people for drug offenses than any other country. India's Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances Act 1985 (NDPS Act), amended significantly in 2001 and 2014, imposes mandatory minimum sentences scaled to quantity thresholds -- commercial quantity trafficking carries 10-20 years' rigorous imprisonment and fine of Rs. 1-2 lakh, with the death penalty available for repeat offenders under Section 31A. Nigeria's National Drug Law Enforcement Agency Act (NDLEA Act, Cap N30 LFN 2004) prescribes life imprisonment for trafficking, while South Africa's Drugs and Drug Trafficking Act 140 of 1992 operates within a constitutional framework that has seen the Constitutional Court partially decriminalize cannabis in Minister of Justice v. Prince 2018 (6) SA 393 (CC). Brazil's drug law (Law 11.343/2006, Lei de Drogas) eliminated imprisonment for personal use (Article 28) while maintaining severe trafficking penalties (5-15 years), yet its application has been marked by profound racial disparities -- the STF in RE 635.659 (2024) issued a landmark ruling establishing quantity thresholds to distinguish use from trafficking. Mexico's General Health Law and Federal Penal Code Articles 193-199 operate against the backdrop of cartel violence and a failed militarized drug war. Indonesia applies the death penalty for drug trafficking under Law No. 35 of 2009 on Narcotics and has executed foreign nationals, provoking diplomatic crises. The Philippines' Comprehensive Dangerous Drugs Act (Republic Act 9165 of 2002) served as the legal foundation for President Duterte's drug war that killed thousands.

## China

### Legal Framework

China's drug control framework rests on the Criminal Law Articles 347-357 and the Anti-Drug Law of the People's Republic of China (中华人民共和国禁毒法, 2007). Article 347 prescribes the death penalty, life imprisonment, or fixed-term imprisonment of not less than 15 years plus confiscation of property for smuggling, trafficking, transporting, or manufacturing narcotic drugs in quantities exceeding thresholds: 1,000 grams of opium, 50 grams of heroin or methamphetamine, or equivalent quantities of other substances. The Regulations on Drug Rehabilitation (戒毒条例, 2011) establish a system of compulsory community-based and institutional drug rehabilitation.

### Anti-Drug Campaigns

China's approach to drugs is inseparable from the historical memory of the Opium Wars (1839-1842, 1856-1860), which remain central to the Chinese Communist Party's narrative of national humiliation and rejuvenation. The annual June 26 International Day Against Drug Abuse is marked by mass rallies, public sentencing events (公开宣判大会, gongkai xuanpan dahui), and, historically, executions timed to the occasion. The National Narcotics Control Commission (国家禁毒委员会, NNCC), chaired by a State Councilor, coordinates national strategy. The China National Drug Control Plan (2021-2025) targets synthetic drugs, particularly methamphetamine produced in Guangdong and Sichuan provinces, and fentanyl precursor chemicals exported internationally.

### Death Penalty for Drug Offenses

The Dui Hua Foundation estimates that drug offenses account for a significant proportion of China's annual executions. The SPC's 2015 Minutes of the National Conference on Trial of Drug Crime Cases (Wuhan Conference Minutes, 武汉会议纪要) provided guidance on death penalty application, emphasizing that capital punishment should generally be reserved for major drug traffickers, ring leaders, and armed traffickers, while mules and addicts who traffic to support their habits may receive lighter sentences. However, enforcement varies significantly by province, with Yunnan and Guangdong -- major transit and production hubs -- executing at higher rates.

### International Dimensions

Following intense U.S. diplomatic pressure, China scheduled all fentanyl-related substances as a class in May 2019, rather than scheduling individual analogs. The U.S.-China Bilateral Agreement on Counternarcotics Cooperation was reaffirmed in the November 2023 Biden-Xi summit. China remains a primary source of precursor chemicals used in Mexican fentanyl production, and the Mekong River region bordering Yunnan Province is a major heroin and methamphetamine corridor from Myanmar's Shan State.

## India

### NDPS Act Framework

The Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances Act 1985 (NDPS Act) is India's primary drug legislation, replacing the colonial-era Opium Act 1878 and Dangerous Drugs Act 1930. The 2001 Amendment introduced the critical three-tier quantity classification: small quantity, intermediate quantity, and commercial quantity, with corresponding graduated penalties.

Key quantity thresholds (per NDPS Act Schedule):

- Cannabis (ganja): small quantity 1 kg, commercial quantity 20 kg
- Heroin: small quantity 5 grams, commercial quantity 250 grams
- Cocaine: small quantity 2 grams, commercial quantity 100 grams
- Methamphetamine: small quantity 2 grams, commercial quantity 50 grams
- Opium: small quantity 25 grams, commercial quantity 2.5 kg

Section 20 addresses cannabis offenses; Section 21 addresses manufactured drugs; Section 22 addresses psychotropic substances.

Section 31A, introduced by the 1989 Amendment, prescribes the death penalty for repeat offenders convicted of specified offenses involving commercial quantities -- making India one of the few countries retaining capital punishment for drug offenses. However, no execution for drug offenses has been carried out.

Section 37 reverses the presumption of bail for commercial quantity offenses, requiring the accused to demonstrate that:

- He is not guilty of the alleged offense, and
- He is not likely to commit any offense while on bail

This reverse burden has resulted in prolonged pretrial detention for thousands of accused, many of whom are eventually acquitted. The NDPS Act's conviction rate of approximately 20-25% (varying by state) suggests significant overcharging.

### Judicial Developments

The Supreme Court in Union of India v. Ranjit Singh Saluja (2020) addressed the strict liability interpretation of NDPS Act provisions. In Hira Singh v. Union of India (2020) 4 SCC 602, the Court held that non-compliance with Section 50 (right to be searched before a Magistrate or gazetted officer) vitiates the prosecution. In Tofan Singh v. State of Tamil Nadu (2021) 4 SCC 1, a Constitution Bench held that statements recorded under Section 67 of the NDPS Act by officers empowered under Section 42 are inadmissible as confessions under Section 25 of the Indian Evidence Act (now Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam 2023).

### Regional Variations

India's northeast -- particularly Manipur, Mizoram, and Nagaland, bordering Myanmar's opium-producing regions -- faces severe heroin trafficking through the so-called "Golden Triangle route." The Manipur War on Drugs campaign, launched by Chief Minister N. Biren Singh in 2017, involved destruction of poppy fields and mass arrests but faced criticism for alleged ethnic targeting. Cannabis (bhang) enjoys cultural and religious exemptions in certain states, with the NDPS Act Section 2(iii)(b) excluding bhang (cannabis leaves) from the definition of cannabis, delegating its regulation to state governments.

## Africa

### Nigeria

The National Drug Law Enforcement Agency (NDLEA), established by Decree No. 48 of 1989 (now NDLEA Act, Cap N30 LFN 2004), is Nigeria's primary drug enforcement body. Section 11 prescribes life imprisonment for trafficking in cocaine, heroin, or LSD, while Section 19 addresses cannabis trafficking with 15 years-life imprisonment. The NDLEA Act Section 3 grants the agency broad powers of arrest, search, and seizure without warrant. Nigeria serves as a major transit hub for South American cocaine destined for European markets, with Nigerian trafficking networks operating across West Africa, Southern Africa, and Southeast Asia.

The Indian Hemp Act (Cap I7 LFN 2004) specifically addresses cannabis, reflecting its historical prevalence. Cannabis cultivation is widespread in states including Ondo, Edo, Oyo, and Osun, with estimates suggesting Nigeria is Africa's largest cannabis producer. The NDLEA reported seizure of over 3.5 million kg of drugs in 2022 under the tenure of Brigadier General Mohamed Buba Marwa (Rtd.).

### South Africa

The Drugs and Drug Trafficking Act 140 of 1992, read with the Prevention and Treatment of Drug Dependency Act 20 of 1992 and the Medicines and Related Substances Act 101 of 1965, forms South Africa's drug control framework. The Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 prescribes minimum sentences of 15 years (first offense) to 25 years (third offense) for drug trafficking under Schedule 2.

The Constitutional Court's decision in Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development v. Prince 2018 (6) SA 393 (CC) declared the prohibition of private use and cultivation of cannabis by adults unconstitutional, finding that it unjustifiably limited the right to privacy under Section 14 of the Constitution. The Cannabis for Private Purposes Bill, introduced in 2020, remains under consideration as of 2026, creating legal uncertainty. The methamphetamine ("tik") crisis in the Western Cape and the nyaope epidemic in Gauteng represent the most pressing drug challenges. The Central Drug Authority, established under the Prevention and Treatment of Drug Dependency Act, coordinates the National Drug Master Plan.

## Latin America

### Brazil

Brazil's Lei de Drogas (Law 11.343/2006) represented a paradigm shift by eliminating imprisonment for personal drug use (Article 28), substituting community service, educational measures, or warning. However, the law failed to specify quantity thresholds distinguishing personal use from trafficking (Article 33, 5-15 years' imprisonment), leaving the determination to judicial discretion. This produced massive racial and socioeconomic disparities: studies by the Nucleo de Estudos da Violencia (NEV-USP) demonstrate that Black defendants possessing identical quantities are significantly more likely to be charged with trafficking than white defendants.

The STF's landmark decision in Recurso Extraordinario 635.659 (decided 2024, after over a decade of deliberation) established that possession of up to 40 grams of cannabis or six female plants constitutes personal use presumptively, though other evidence (scales, packaging, cash) may rebut the presumption. The decision does not legalize cannabis but removes the criminal sanction for users possessing below the threshold.

Brazilian drug policy is inseparable from favela policing. Unidades de Policia Pacificadora (UPPs, Pacifying Police Units), established in Rio de Janeiro from 2008, represented a community policing model that initially reduced violence but subsequently deteriorated. The Guarantees of Law and Order (GLO) operations involving military deployment in favelas, authorized under Complementary Law 97/1999 and Article 142 of the Constitution, have produced mass casualties documented by organizations including Redes da Mare and Human Rights Watch.

### Mexico

Mexico's drug legal framework includes the General Health Law (Ley General de Salud) Articles 234-256, the Federal Penal Code Articles 193-199, and the Federal Law against Organized Crime (Ley Federal contra la Delincuencia Organizada, 1996). The 2009 Narcomenudeo Reform (Decree of August 20, 2009) established personal use thresholds: 5 grams of cannabis, 2 grams of opium, 0.5 grams of cocaine, 40 milligrams of methamphetamine, and 0.015 milligrams of LSD.

The Supreme Court of Justice (SCJN) issued five consecutive amparo rulings (2015-2019) declaring the absolute prohibition of recreational cannabis use unconstitutional, triggering a declaration of unconstitutionality (declaratoria general de inconstitucionalidad) under Article 107 of the Constitution. However, implementing legislation has stalled repeatedly in Congress. The Federal Law for the Regulation of Cannabis was approved by the Senate in November 2020 but never passed the Chamber of Deputies.

Mexico's militarized drug war, initiated by President Felipe Calderon in December 2006, has produced over 350,000 homicides and more than 100,000 disappearances through 2025. The Inter-American Court of Human Rights in Alvarado Espinoza et al. v. Mexico (2018) found Mexico responsible for forced disappearances by military personnel engaged in drug enforcement operations.

## Southeast Asia

### Indonesia

Indonesia's Narcotics Law (Law No. 35 of 2009 concerning Narcotics) classifies substances into three categories and prescribes severe penalties. Article 114 prescribes 5-20 years' imprisonment (or life or death for quantities exceeding 5 grams for Category I substances including heroin, cocaine, and cannabis extracts) for trafficking. Article 111 addresses plant-based narcotics (cannabis), with Article 111(2) prescribing 5-20 years for possession of more than 1 kilogram.

Indonesia has executed numerous drug traffickers, including foreign nationals, provoking intense diplomatic controversy. The 2015 executions of Myuran Sukumaran and Andrew Chan (Australian members of the "Bali Nine") proceeded despite appeals from the Australian government, the UN High Commissioner for Human Rights, and Pope Francis. President Joko Widodo defended the executions, stating Indonesia was in a "drug emergency." The Constitutional Court (Mahkamah Konstitusi) in Decision No. 2-3/PUU-V/2007 upheld the constitutionality of the death penalty for drug offenses, finding it consistent with Article 28J of the 1945 Constitution's limitation clause.

The National Narcotics Agency (Badan Narkotika Nasional, BNN), restructured by Presidential Regulation No. 23 of 2010, coordinates national drug policy. The 2009 law (Articles 54-59) provides for rehabilitation of users and addicts, and a 2014 Supreme Court Circular Letter (SEMA No. 4/2010, updated) directs judges to order rehabilitation rather than imprisonment for users possessing below specified thresholds. However, implementation remains inconsistent, and incarceration of users continues to overwhelm prison capacity.

### Philippines

Republic Act 9165 (Comprehensive Dangerous Drugs Act of 2002) is the Philippines' primary drug legislation. Section 5 prescribes life imprisonment to death (now reclusion perpetua under RA 9346) for sale, trading, delivery, or distribution of dangerous drugs regardless of quantity. Section 11 prescribes varying penalties for possession scaled to quantity -- for shabu (methamphetamine hydrochloride), 10 grams or more carries life imprisonment. Section 15 provides for rehabilitation of first-time drug users, while Section 36 mandates drug testing in schools, workplaces, and for candidates for public office.

The Duterte drug war (2016-2022) remains the Philippines' most controversial law enforcement operation. Executive Order No. 15 (2017) reconstituted the Philippine Drug Enforcement Agency (PDEA) as the sole agency authorized to conduct anti-drug operations, though the Philippine National Police (PNP) continued operations in practice. The "Oplan Tokhang" (Cebuano: knock and plead) campaign involved police visiting homes of suspected drug users, but devolved into systematic extrajudicial killings documented in the CHR (Commission on Human Rights) Report of 2017, Human Rights Watch's "License to Kill" report (2017), and the Amnesty International "If You Are Poor, You Are Killed" report (2017).

The ICC investigation (ICC-01/21, authorized September 15, 2021) examines crimes against humanity (murder) committed in the context of the drug war between November 1, 2011 and March 16, 2019. The Marcos administration initially cooperated with an ICC request to resume investigation in January 2023 but subsequently withdrew cooperation. Domestically, the Department of Justice launched a domestic review under Secretary Jesus Crispin Remulla, and former Senator Leila de Lima -- who was detained from 2017 to 2023 on drug charges widely regarded as politically motivated -- was acquitted of all charges.

## Comparative Analysis

### Punitive Convergence and Divergence

The Global South exhibits a paradox: countries that have been most devastated by the war on drugs maintain some of the most punitive drug laws. China and Indonesia apply the death penalty; India retains it for repeat offenders; the Philippines' drug war killed thousands extrajudicially before any formal death penalty application. Yet Latin America, the region most affected by drug-related violence, has moved toward decriminalization -- Brazil eliminated imprisonment for personal use in 2006, and Mexico's Supreme Court declared cannabis prohibition unconstitutional. South Africa's Constitutional Court decriminalized private cannabis use. This divergence reflects different constitutional cultures: Latin American and South African neo-constitutionalism, with its emphasis on proportionality and dignity, contrasts with Asian legal systems' emphasis on social order and state authority.

### Racial and Class Disparities

Drug enforcement across the Global South disproportionately targets marginalized populations. In Brazil, Black and pardo individuals constitute approximately 67% of those imprisoned for drug trafficking despite comparable rates of drug use across racial groups. In India, Dalit and Adivasi communities are overrepresented in NDPS Act prosecutions. In the Philippines, the drug war's victims were overwhelmingly poor urban residents. In Nigeria, cannabis enforcement targets small-scale farmers and users while large-scale trafficking networks operate with relative impunity. China's enforcement has historically impacted ethnic minorities in Yunnan and Xinjiang disproportionately.

### Harm Reduction vs. Prohibition

Global South countries are increasingly diverging between prohibition and harm reduction models. Brazil's substitution of imprisonment with educational measures for users (Lei de Drogas Art. 28), South Africa's cannabis decriminalization, and Mexico's decriminalization of personal possession represent moves toward harm reduction. In contrast, China's compulsory rehabilitation system, Indonesia's execution of drug traffickers, and the Philippines' extrajudicial killing campaign represent extreme prohibitionist approaches. India occupies a middle ground, with the NDPS Act's rehabilitation provisions (Section 39) coexisting with severe mandatory minimums.

### Transnational Dimensions

Drug trafficking in the Global South is fundamentally transnational. Chinese precursor chemicals fuel Mexican fentanyl production; Colombian cocaine transits through Nigerian networks to European markets; Myanmar's methamphetamine floods Southeast Asian markets through Laotian and Thai corridors; and Afghan heroin reaches India through Pakistan and Iran. The international legal framework -- particularly the 1988 UN Convention against Illicit Traffic in Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances -- provides the baseline, but implementation varies dramatically. BRICS cooperation on counternarcotics, ASEAN's ASOD (ASEAN Senior Officials on Drug Matters) mechanism, and regional bodies like ECOWAS's Drug Action Plan represent Global South-led multilateral responses.

### Prison Overcrowding and Drug Offenses

Drug offenses are the primary driver of prison overcrowding across the Global South. In Brazil, approximately 28% of incarcerated persons are held for drug trafficking (Departamento Penitenciario Nacional, DEPEN, Infopen data), with the prison population exceeding 832,000 in a system designed for approximately 440,000. In Indonesia, drug offenders constitute over 40% of the prison population, with facilities operating at 200% or more of capacity. The Philippines' notoriously overcrowded Bureau of Jail Management and Penology facilities hold large numbers of drug suspects in pretrial detention. In India, NDPS Act accused constitute a growing share of undertrial prisoners, with Section 37's restrictive bail provisions contributing to prolonged pretrial detention. China's compulsory drug rehabilitation centers (强制隔离戒毒所, qiangzhi geli jiedu suo) held an estimated 200,000-300,000 persons annually, a form of administrative detention separate from the criminal justice system.

### Cannabis Reform Movements

A notable Global South trend is the emerging movement toward cannabis reform, challenging the global prohibition regime that originated in the 1961 Single Convention. South Africa's Prince decision (2018), Mexico's SCJN unconstitutionality declaration (2021), Brazil's STF threshold ruling (2024), and Thailand's brief experiment with cannabis decriminalization (2022-2024) represent diverse approaches to cannabis reform. In India, advocates point to cannabis's historical and cultural significance in Hinduism (bhang/charas) and Ayurvedic medicine, while the 2019 Great Legalisation Movement India Trust petition before the Delhi High Court challenged NDPS Act cannabis prohibitions on grounds of personal liberty under Article 21 of the Constitution. In Africa, Lesotho became the first African country to grant a license for medical cannabis cultivation in 2017, followed by South Africa, Zimbabwe, and Malawi developing medical cannabis frameworks.
