## Summary

Bestiality -- sexual contact between a human being and an animal -- has undergone significant legal evolution across jurisdictions, with the primary legislative rationale shifting from historical morality-based prohibitions rooted in religious and cultural norms to modern animal welfare-based frameworks emphasizing the prevention of cruelty and suffering to animals. South Korea does not have a specific criminal prohibition on bestiality in the Criminal Act (형법), but such conduct may be prosecuted under the Animal Protection Act (동물보호법), which was significantly strengthened in 2017, 2020, and 2024, with Article 8 prohibiting animal cruelty including acts causing unnecessary suffering, and which was amended in 2024 to explicitly include sexual abuse of animals as a form of animal cruelty punishable by imprisonment of up to three years or a fine of up to 30 million KRW. The United States has seen a rapid legislative trend toward criminalization, with the number of states explicitly prohibiting bestiality growing from approximately 32 in 2000 to 48 as of 2025; the 2005 "Enumclaw horse sex case" in Washington state, in which a man died from injuries sustained during sexual contact with a horse, prompted Washington to enact its anti-bestiality statute (RCW 16.52.205) in 2006 and catalyzed legislative action across multiple states. Germany recriminalized bestiality in 2012 through an amendment to the Tierschutzgesetz (Animal Protection Act), Section 3, after having decriminalized it in 1969 when the morality-based prohibition in former Section 175b StGB was removed as part of the broader sexual law reform; the 2012 recriminalization was grounded in animal welfare rather than morality, reflecting the 2002 amendment to Article 20a of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz) that elevated animal welfare to constitutional status. The ECHR has not directly addressed bestiality but relevant principles emerge from Article 8 (private life) jurisprudence and the growing European consensus on animal welfare, as reflected in Article 13 of the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union, which recognizes animals as "sentient beings" and requires member states to pay full regard to animal welfare.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's approach to bestiality has evolved from effective non-regulation to animal welfare-based prohibition:

**Animal Protection Act (동물보호법, Act No. 4379 of 1991, as amended):**

The Act underwent major amendments in 2017, 2020, and 2024:

**Article 8 -- Prohibition of Animal Cruelty (동물학대의 금지):** Prohibits acts of cruelty against animals, including acts causing unnecessary pain or suffering. The 2024 amendment explicitly added sexual abuse of animals to the enumerated list of prohibited cruel acts.

**2024 Amendment Key Provisions:**
- Sexual abuse of animals (동물 성학대) explicitly defined as a form of animal cruelty
- Penalty: Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine up to 30 million KRW
- Animal seizure and forfeiture authorized
- Prohibition on owning animals following conviction

### Prior to 2024 Amendment

Before the explicit prohibition, Korean authorities faced challenges in prosecuting bestiality:

**Gap in Coverage:** Without an explicit prohibition, prosecutors had to argue that sexual contact with an animal constituted "unnecessary cruelty" under the general animal cruelty provision -- an argument that required proving physical harm or suffering to the animal.

**Public Pressure for Reform:** Several publicized incidents of animal sexual abuse, including cases involving social media distribution of abuse material, generated public pressure for explicit criminalization. A 2023 National Assembly petition received over 50,000 signatures calling for criminalization.

### Animal Welfare Context

South Korea has undergone a dramatic shift in animal welfare attitudes:
- The 2017 amendment to the Animal Protection Act significantly increased penalties for animal cruelty
- President Moon Jae-in adopted a former stray dog ("Tory") upon taking office in 2017, symbolizing changing attitudes
- The dog meat trade, while declining, remains a contested issue
- Registration of companion animals became mandatory in 2014

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has seen rapid legislative expansion of bestiality prohibitions:

**Federal Level:** There is no general federal bestiality statute, but the Animal Crush Video Prohibition Act (18 U.S.C. Section 48, amended 2010) prohibits the creation, sale, or distribution of "animal crush videos" depicting the intentional torture or killing of animals, which has been applied to some cases involving sexual abuse of animals.

### State-Level Criminalization

| Period | States with Explicit Prohibition | Key Developments |
|--------|----------------------------------|-------------------|
| Pre-2000 | ~32 states | Most derived from colonial-era sodomy provisions |
| 2005-2010 | ~37 states | Catalyzed by Enumclaw case |
| 2010-2020 | ~45 states | Continued legislative expansion |
| 2020-2025 | ~48 states | Near-universal coverage |

### The Enumclaw Case (2005)

On July 2, 2005, Kenneth Pinyan ("Mr. Hands") died from internal injuries caused by receptive anal intercourse with a stallion at a farm near Enumclaw, Washington. The case drew international attention and exposed the absence of an anti-bestiality statute in Washington:

**Legal Significance:** Because Washington had no anti-bestiality law, prosecutors could not file criminal charges related to the sexual act itself. Charges were considered under animal cruelty statutes but were not pursued because the animal was not physically harmed.

**Legislative Response:** Washington enacted RCW 16.52.205 in 2006, making bestiality a Class C felony. The law was named the "animal cruelty" provision and was framed in animal welfare terms.

### Constitutional Considerations

While no court has found a constitutional right to engage in bestiality:

**Lawrence v. Texas, 539 U.S. 558 (2003):** The Supreme Court expressly limited its holding to consensual relations between adults. Animals cannot consent, removing the privacy analysis from consideration.

**First Amendment Issues:** The Supreme Court in United States v. Stevens, 559 U.S. 460 (2010), struck down the original federal animal crush video statute as overbroad. Congress responded by enacting a narrower statute in 2010.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's approach illustrates the transition from morality-based to welfare-based regulation:

### Historical Development

**Former Section 175b StGB (Unzucht mit Tieren -- Unnatural Acts with Animals):** From 1871 until 1969, the Reichsstrafgesetzbuch and its successor prohibited "unnatural acts" (widernatürliche Unzucht) with animals as part of a broader morality offense that also covered homosexual acts. The penalty was imprisonment.

**1969 Sexual Law Reform:** As part of the comprehensive reform of sexual offenses that also decriminalized homosexual acts between consenting adults (former Section 175), Section 175b was repealed. The reform reflected the position that morality-based criminal prohibitions without an identifiable victim were unjustified intrusions into private autonomy.

**2012 Recriminalization:** The Tierschutzgesetz (Animal Protection Act) was amended effective July 13, 2013, to prohibit sexual acts with animals:

**Tierschutzgesetz Section 3, No. 13:** Prohibits the use of animals for one's own or third parties' sexual acts or the training of animals for sexual acts. The provision is framed exclusively in animal welfare terms.

### Constitutional Context

**Article 20a GG (2002 Amendment):** "Mindful also of its responsibility towards future generations, the state shall protect the natural foundations of life and animals by legislation and, in accordance with law and justice, by executive and judicial action, all within the framework of the constitutional order." This amendment elevated animal welfare to constitutional status, providing the constitutional basis for the 2012 recriminalization.

### Penalties

| Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|
| Sexual acts with animals (Tierschutzgesetz) | Administrative fine (Ordnungswidrigkeit) up to €25,000 |
| Animal cruelty through sexual acts (Section 17 TierSchG) | Criminal penalty: up to 3 years imprisonment or fine |
| Distribution of animal pornography | Subject to general pornography distribution provisions |

### Zoophile Organizations

Prior to recriminalization, Germany was home to organized zoophile advocacy groups, including ZETA (Zoophiles Engagement für Toleranz und Aufklärung). These organizations lobbied against recriminalization, arguing that animal welfare was better protected through existing animal cruelty laws. The legislative debate explicitly considered and rejected these arguments.

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR has not directly addressed bestiality. However, several principles from existing jurisprudence are relevant:

**Article 8 -- Private Life:** While Article 8 protects private sexual conduct between consenting adults, animals cannot consent. The Court's analysis in cases such as Dudgeon v. United Kingdom (1981) and Lustig-Prean and Beckett v. United Kingdom (1999) concerning the decriminalization of homosexual conduct does not extend to acts involving animals.

**Margin of Appreciation:** Given the lack of European consensus on the precise scope of bestiality prohibitions (most but not all member states criminalize it), states enjoy a margin of appreciation in determining the appropriate legal framework.

### EU Framework

**TFEU Article 13:** "In formulating and implementing the Union's agriculture, fisheries, transport, internal market, research and technological development and space policies, the Union and the Member States shall, since animals are sentient beings, pay full regard to the welfare requirements of animals." This provision, while not directly creating criminal obligations, establishes the principle that informs member state approaches.

**European Convention for the Protection of Pet Animals (ETS No. 125, 1987):** While not specifically addressing bestiality, Article 3 requires that "nobody shall cause a pet animal unnecessary pain, suffering or distress."

## Comparative Analysis

### Rationale Shift

The four jurisdictions illustrate a global shift in the rationale for criminalizing bestiality:

**Historical Rationale (Morality/Religion):** Traditional prohibitions were rooted in religious concepts of "crimes against nature" and societal moral norms. This rationale is no longer the primary basis for any of the studied jurisdictions.

**Modern Rationale (Animal Welfare):** Contemporary legislation is grounded in animal welfare principles, recognizing animals as sentient beings incapable of consent. This rationale has proven more durable and less susceptible to constitutional challenge than morality-based prohibitions.

**Hybrid Rationale (United States):** Some U.S. states combine animal welfare and sexual offense frameworks, treating bestiality as both animal cruelty and a sex crime (requiring sex offender registration).

### Consent and Sentience

The animal consent framework provides the most robust legal basis for criminalization:
- Animals cannot provide informed consent to sexual acts
- Growing recognition of animal sentience (TFEU Art. 13) supports enhanced protections
- The consent framework avoids the constitutional vulnerabilities of morality-based prohibitions

### Enforcement Challenges

All jurisdictions face significant enforcement challenges:
- Most acts occur in private settings without witnesses
- Animal victims cannot report abuse
- Forensic evidence is difficult to obtain and preserve
- Online distribution of abuse material creates cross-border enforcement issues
