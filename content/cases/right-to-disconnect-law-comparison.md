## Summary

The right to disconnect -- the legal entitlement of employees to refrain from engaging in work-related electronic communications such as emails, messages, and calls outside of their working hours without facing adverse consequences -- has emerged as one of the most significant labor law developments of the 2020s, responding to the blurring of work-life boundaries accelerated by digital technology and remote work practices catalyzed by the COVID-19 pandemic. South Korea does not have a specific right to disconnect statute but addresses the issue indirectly through the Labor Standards Act (근로기준법), which limits standard working hours to 40 hours per week and 52 hours including overtime (Article 50 and Article 53, as amended in 2018), and through the 2017 "PC-Off" system (PC 끄기 제도) implemented by the Ministry of Employment and Labor for government agencies and encouraged in the private sector; the National Human Rights Commission of Korea (국가인권위원회) recommended in 2023 that the National Assembly enact legislation guaranteeing workers the right to disconnect outside working hours. The United States has no federal right to disconnect, and the Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938 (29 U.S.C. Section 201 et seq.) does not address after-hours electronic communications, though the FLSA's overtime provisions require compensation for non-exempt employees who perform work outside regular hours including responding to emails; only limited local legislation exists, such as New York City's proposed Right to Disconnect bill (Int. 0726-2018, never enacted) and California's AB 2751 (2024), signed into law requiring employers with 25 or more employees to establish a right to disconnect policy. Germany has no comprehensive federal right to disconnect statute, but the Arbeitszeitgesetz (Working Time Act, ArbZG) mandates minimum rest periods of 11 consecutive hours between shifts (Section 5 ArbZG), and the Federal Ministry of Labour and Social Affairs (BMAS) published a landmark ruling that employers have a duty of care (Fürsorgepflicht) not to contact employees during rest periods except in genuine emergencies; sector-level collective agreements at companies including Volkswagen, BMW, and Daimler have pioneered after-hours email restrictions. At the European level, the European Parliament adopted a resolution on January 21, 2021, calling on the European Commission to propose a directive on the right to disconnect (2019/2181(INL)), and several EU member states -- including France (Article L2242-17 of the Code du travail, effective January 1, 2017), Belgium (effective February 1, 2022), Italy (Law No. 81/2017), Spain (Article 88 of the Ley Orgánica 3/2018), Portugal (Labor Code Article 199-A, 2021), and Australia (Fair Work Amendment Act 2024) -- have enacted statutory right to disconnect provisions, establishing an emerging international legal norm.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's approach to the right to disconnect operates through a combination of working hours regulation, administrative guidance, and evolving policy recommendations.

**Labor Standards Act (근로기준법) -- Working Hours Provisions:**

- **Article 50:** Standard working hours are limited to 40 hours per week and 8 hours per day.
- **Article 53 (as amended July 1, 2018):** Maximum working hours including overtime are limited to 52 hours per week (40 standard + 12 overtime). This 2018 amendment reduced the maximum from the prior effective limit of 68 hours per week by clarifying that overtime is calculated on a weekly rather than daily basis.
- **Article 54:** Employers must provide rest periods of at least 30 minutes for every 4 hours of work and at least 1 hour for every 8 hours.
- **Article 56:** Overtime work must be compensated at 150 percent of the normal hourly wage; work on holidays at 150-200 percent.

**PC-Off System (PC 끄기 제도):** Introduced by the Ministry of Employment and Labor in 2017, this system automatically shuts down government agency employees' computers at the designated end-of-work time, typically 6:00 PM. Employees who wish to continue working must obtain supervisor approval. Several major private corporations, including SK Telecom, Shinhan Bank, and Samsung, have adopted voluntary PC-Off or similar systems.

**National Human Rights Commission Recommendation (2023):** On June 15, 2023, the National Human Rights Commission of Korea (국가인권위원회) issued a formal recommendation (의견표명) to the National Assembly and the Ministry of Employment and Labor calling for legislation guaranteeing workers the right to disconnect from work-related digital communications outside working hours. The recommendation cited surveys indicating that 73 percent of Korean workers reported receiving work-related messages outside working hours, and 58 percent felt compelled to respond immediately.

**Proposed Legislation:** Multiple bills have been introduced in the National Assembly addressing the right to disconnect. The most comprehensive, proposed by Democratic Party lawmaker Ryu Ho-jeong (류호정) in 2021, would amend the Labor Standards Act to prohibit employers from requiring employees to perform work-related communications outside working hours except in emergencies, require employers to establish clear communication policies, and impose administrative fines for violations. As of early 2026, none of these bills have been enacted.

### Cultural Context

South Korea's notorious overwork culture (과로사회) makes the right to disconnect particularly relevant. The country's average annual working hours of approximately 1,872 hours (2023 OECD data) remain among the highest in the OECD. The concept of nunchi (눈치) -- social awareness and sensitivity to others' expectations -- creates cultural pressure to respond to supervisors' messages at all hours. The 2018 working hours reform was partly catalyzed by high-profile deaths from overwork (과로사, karoshi-equivalent), including several cases involving young public sector employees.

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| Requiring overtime exceeding 52-hour weekly limit | Imprisonment up to 2 years or fine up to KRW 20 million (Art. 110 LSA) |
| Failure to pay overtime compensation | Imprisonment up to 3 years or fine up to KRW 30 million (Art. 109 LSA) |
| PC-Off system violation (government agencies) | Administrative disciplinary action |

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has not enacted federal right to disconnect legislation, and the FLSA framework focuses on compensation rather than communication restrictions.

**Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA), 29 U.S.C. Section 201 et seq.:** The FLSA does not limit working hours for adult employees but requires that non-exempt employees be paid overtime at 1.5 times their regular rate for hours exceeding 40 per week. The Act's application to after-hours digital communications has generated significant litigation:

**De Minimis Doctrine:** Courts have traditionally applied the de minimis doctrine to excuse employers from compensating employees for trivial amounts of after-hours work. However, in Troester v. Starbucks Corp., 5 Cal. 5th 829 (2018), the California Supreme Court rejected the federal de minimis standard under California state law, holding that even small amounts of regularly occurring off-the-clock work must be compensated.

**"Suffered or Permitted to Work" Standard:** Under 29 C.F.R. Section 785.11, work that an employer "suffers or permits" constitutes compensable working time, even if not specifically requested. An employer who knows or has reason to know that an employee is checking and responding to work emails after hours may be liable for unpaid overtime.

**Allen v. City of Chicago, 351 F.3d 306 (7th Cir. 2003):** The Seventh Circuit addressed the compensability of after-hours work, establishing factors for determining whether an employer had constructive knowledge of off-the-clock work through electronic communications.

**California AB 2751 (2024):** Signed by Governor Gavin Newsom on September 28, 2024, this statute requires employers with 25 or more employees to establish a "right to disconnect policy" defining working hours and non-working hours, and guaranteeing employees the right to ignore non-emergency communications during non-working hours. The law does not prohibit employers from sending communications but protects employees from retaliation for not responding. Violations are enforced through the California Labor Commissioner, with penalties starting at $100 per occurrence for employers.

**New York City Int. 0726-2018:** Introduced by Council Member Rafael Espinal in 2018, this bill would have prohibited private employers from requiring employees to access work-related electronic communications outside of work hours. The bill was never enacted but generated significant national debate.

### Exempt vs. Non-Exempt Distinction

The FLSA's exemption framework creates a significant gap in disconnect protections. Executive, administrative, professional, and highly compensated employees (those earning above $43,888 annually under 2024 DOL regulations) are exempt from overtime requirements, meaning they receive no additional compensation for after-hours work regardless of volume. The 2024 DOL rule raising the salary threshold (subsequently blocked by federal courts) sought to narrow this gap.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's approach combines robust statutory working time protections with innovative collective bargaining solutions, though a comprehensive right to disconnect statute has not been enacted.

**Arbeitszeitgesetz (ArbZG) -- Working Time Act (1994):**

- **Section 3:** Working time may not exceed 8 hours per working day, extendable to 10 hours if the average over 6 calendar months or 24 weeks does not exceed 8 hours per working day.
- **Section 5:** After the end of the daily working time, employees must have an uninterrupted rest period of at least 11 hours. Work-related communications during this rest period may constitute a violation.
- **Section 22(1):** Violations of the rest period requirements constitute administrative offenses (Ordnungswidrigkeiten) punishable by fines up to EUR 15,000. Intentional violations endangering employee health are criminal offenses under Section 23 punishable by imprisonment up to 1 year.

**BAG (Federal Labour Court) Jurisprudence:** The Bundesarbeitsgericht has not issued a direct ruling on the right to disconnect, but established relevant principles. In BAG, Judgment of May 23, 2001 (5 AZR 406/99), the Court held that employers' duty of care (Fürsorgepflicht) encompasses protection of employees' health and rest periods, which may be interpreted to restrict after-hours digital communications.

**BMAS Advisory Opinion:** The Federal Ministry of Labour and Social Affairs (Bundesministerium für Arbeit und Soziales) issued guidance in 2016 establishing that the employer's Fürsorgepflicht prohibits routine contact during the 11-hour rest period under Section 5 ArbZG, and that repeated violations may give rise to employee claims for damages. The guidance noted that exceptions are permissible only in genuine emergencies that could not reasonably be addressed during working hours.

### Collective Agreement Innovations

Germany's system of Tarifverträge (collective agreements) and Betriebsvereinbarungen (works council agreements) has produced several pioneering after-hours communication policies:

| Company | Year | Measure |
|---------|------|---------|
| Volkswagen | 2011 | BlackBerry server disconnection for hourly employees from 6:15 PM to 7:00 AM |
| BMW | 2014 | "Erreichbarkeitsrichtlinie" (accessibility guideline): employees may choose availability windows |
| Daimler (Mercedes-Benz) | 2014 | "Mail on Holiday" auto-delete function: emails sent to vacationing employees are automatically deleted with sender notification |
| Deutsche Telekom | 2015 | Management instruction prohibiting supervisors from contacting employees outside working hours except in emergencies |
| Henkel | 2016 | Digital detox policy restricting after-hours email expectations |

### Works Council Co-Determination

Under Section 87(1) No. 2 and No. 6 of the Betriebsverfassungsgesetz (Works Constitution Act, BetrVG), works councils (Betriebsräte) have co-determination rights regarding the beginning and end of daily working hours and the introduction of technical devices capable of monitoring employee performance. These co-determination rights provide a legal basis for works councils to negotiate comprehensive digital disconnect policies, including restrictions on after-hours email systems and messaging platforms.

### Mobile Work Act (Proposed)

The proposed Gesetz zur mobilen Arbeit (Mobile Work Act), championed by former Labor Minister Hubertus Heil (SPD), would have established a legal right to mobile work and included provisions on accessibility during and outside working hours. The draft legislation was not enacted during the 2017-2021 legislative period and was not reintroduced in the subsequent Bundestag session.

## ECHR & EU

### European Parliament Resolution (2021)

On January 21, 2021, the European Parliament adopted a resolution with recommendations to the Commission on the right to disconnect (2019/2181(INL)) by 472 votes to 126, with 83 abstentions. The resolution called on the European Commission to propose a directive establishing:

- A right for workers to not engage in work-related tasks or communications outside working hours
- Protection against retaliation for exercising the right to disconnect
- Requirements for employers to provide written information on disconnect arrangements
- Monitoring and enforcement mechanisms

The Commission acknowledged the resolution but has not yet proposed a formal directive as of early 2026, instead focusing on the broader Platform Workers Directive and revisions to the Working Time Directive.

### EU Working Time Directive 2003/88/EC

Article 3 of the Working Time Directive mandates that every worker is entitled to a minimum daily rest period of 11 consecutive hours per 24-hour period. Article 6 limits average weekly working time to 48 hours including overtime. While the Directive does not explicitly address digital communications, the CJEU's interpretation has laid groundwork for disconnect rights:

**CCOO v. Deutsche Bank SAE (Case C-55/18, CJEU Judgment of May 14, 2019):** The Court of Justice held that member states must require employers to set up a system enabling the duration of daily working time to be measured. This ruling implies that after-hours digital work must be tracked and counted as working time, creating an indirect incentive for employers to limit after-hours communications.

### Member State Legislation

**France -- Article L2242-17 Code du travail (2017):** France became the first country to enact a statutory right to disconnect, effective January 1, 2017, through the El Khomri Law (Loi Travail). Companies with 50 or more employees must negotiate arrangements for the full exercise of employees' right to disconnect and establish procedures for regulating digital tools to ensure rest periods, holidays, and personal life are respected. No specific penalties are prescribed; enforcement operates through collective bargaining obligations.

**Belgium (2022):** Effective February 1, 2022, Belgium amended its labor law to grant employees of companies with 20 or more employees the right not to be reached outside working hours, except in exceptional circumstances. Employers must define the framework through collective agreements or internal regulations.

**Italy -- Law No. 81/2017 (Legge sul Lavoro Agile):** Italy's smart working law requires employment agreements for remote workers to include provisions on the employee's right to disconnect from technological devices.

**Spain -- Article 88, Ley Orgánica 3/2018:** Spain's data protection law includes a provision guaranteeing workers' right to digital disconnection in the context of respecting rest time, leave, and holidays.

**Portugal -- Labor Code Article 199-A (2021):** Portugal enacted the strongest disconnect provisions, prohibiting employers from contacting workers outside working hours except in cases of force majeure, with violations subject to fines.

**Australia -- Fair Work Amendment (Right to Disconnect) Act 2024:** While outside the European framework, Australia's legislation (effective August 26, 2024) is notable as the largest common-law jurisdiction to enact a right to disconnect, protecting employees from adverse action for refusing to monitor, read, or respond to employer communications outside working hours unless the refusal is unreasonable.

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR has not directly addressed the right to disconnect but has established relevant principles through its Article 8 (right to respect for private life) jurisprudence:

**Bărbulescu v. Romania (Application No. 61496/08, Grand Chamber Judgment of September 5, 2017):** The Grand Chamber held that employer monitoring of an employee's personal internet communications at work violated Article 8, establishing that employees have a reasonable expectation of privacy in workplace communications and that monitoring must be proportionate. This principle extends by analogy to the boundary between work and private digital life.

**López Ribalda and Others v. Spain (Applications Nos. 1874/13 and 8567/13, Grand Chamber Judgment of October 17, 2019):** While upholding covert video surveillance in limited circumstances, the Court emphasized the need for proportionality and prior notification in employer monitoring, reinforcing the principle that employees' private lives must be protected from excessive employer intrusion.

## Comparative Analysis

### Statutory vs. Collective Bargaining Approaches

France, Belgium, Spain, Italy, and Portugal have adopted statutory approaches, mandating the right to disconnect through legislation. Germany has relied primarily on collective bargaining and works council agreements, producing innovative but non-universal solutions. South Korea and the United States have not enacted statutory rights, relying instead on working hours limits and overtime compensation requirements.

### Enforcement Mechanisms

The most effective disconnect protections combine statutory rights with institutional enforcement. France's approach, while pioneering, has been criticized for lacking specific penalties. Portugal's model, with direct fines for employer violations, provides stronger enforcement. Germany's works council system offers robust enterprise-level enforcement through co-determination rights but does not extend to workplaces without works councils (typically smaller enterprises).

### Cultural and Economic Dimensions

Cultural factors significantly influence the effectiveness of disconnect legislation. South Korea's hierarchical workplace culture and Japan's similar overwork problems suggest that statutory rights alone may be insufficient without cultural change. The United States' emphasis on individual productivity and the exempt employee category create structural barriers to disconnect rights. Germany's strong tradition of Feierabend (end of workday) culture provides a more receptive environment for disconnect policies.

### Remote Work Acceleration

The COVID-19 pandemic dramatically accelerated remote work adoption, intensifying the need for disconnect protections. Workers reporting difficulty disconnecting from work increased from approximately 27 percent pre-pandemic to over 45 percent during the pandemic across OECD countries. This shift transformed the right to disconnect from a niche labor law concern to a mainstream policy priority.
