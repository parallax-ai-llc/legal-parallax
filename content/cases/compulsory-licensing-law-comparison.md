## Summary

Compulsory licensing of patents allows governments to authorize the use of a patented invention without the consent of the patent holder, typically upon payment of adequate remuneration, serving as a critical policy tool for balancing intellectual property rights against public health, competition, and national security interests -- a tension that reached unprecedented global salience during the COVID-19 pandemic and has reignited debates about pharmaceutical access, technology transfer, and the appropriate scope of TRIPS flexibilities. South Korea's Patent Act (특허법) provides for compulsory licensing under Articles 107-110, authorizing the Commissioner of the Korean Intellectual Property Office (KIPO, 특허청장) to grant non-exclusive licenses where a patented invention has not been worked commercially in Korea for three or more consecutive years, where a compulsory license is necessary for the public interest (공익), or where an unfair competition practice has been determined by a court or the Korea Fair Trade Commission (공정거래위원회); Korea issued its first compulsory license threat during the COVID-19 pandemic regarding remdesivir, ultimately resolved through voluntary licensing, and the Infectious Disease Control and Prevention Act (감염병의 예방 및 관리에 관한 법률) provides additional government use authorization during public health emergencies. The United States provides for compulsory licensing through 28 U.S.C. Section 1498 (government use of patents with reasonable compensation), antitrust remedies requiring compulsory licensing of patents found to sustain monopolistic practices, and 35 U.S.C. Section 203 (march-in rights under the Bayh-Dole Act for federally funded inventions), though these provisions have been invoked sparingly, with no march-in right ever exercised despite repeated petitions regarding pharmaceutical prices; the U.S. activated provisions of the Defense Production Act during COVID-19 but did not issue formal compulsory licenses. Germany's Patent Act (Patentgesetz, PatG) provides for compulsory licensing under Section 24 PatG where the patent has not been worked sufficiently, where a compulsory license is necessary in the public interest (öffentliches Interesse), or where a dependent patent cannot be worked without infringing a prior patent, with the Bundespatentgericht (Federal Patent Court) having jurisdiction; Germany's Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) issued a landmark order in May 2020 in the Remdesivir case (1 BvR 776/20) regarding emergency compulsory licensing during COVID-19, and the Infektionsschutzgesetz (IfSG, Infection Protection Act) Section 5(2) provides for government use authorization during epidemic emergencies. The EU/ECHR framework encompasses the TRIPS Agreement Articles 31 and 31bis (compulsory licensing conditions and export provisions), the Doha Declaration on the TRIPS Agreement and Public Health (2001), the COVID-19 TRIPS Waiver Decision (2022), Regulation (EC) No 816/2006 on compulsory licensing for pharmaceutical export to countries with public health problems, and the proposed EU compulsory licensing regulation for crisis management, while the ECHR protects patent rights as property under Protocol 1, Article 1, subject to the public interest limitation.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's compulsory licensing provisions reflect a sophisticated balance between strong patent protection for its technology-intensive economy and the need for access mechanisms, particularly in pharmaceuticals and public health.

**Patent Act (특허법) Compulsory Licensing (Articles 107-110):**

**Article 107 (Compulsory License for Non-Working):** Where a patented invention has not been worked commercially in Korea for a continuous period of three years or more without justifiable reason, any person who wishes to work the invention may request the Commissioner of KIPO to grant a non-exclusive compulsory license. The applicant must demonstrate prior unsuccessful efforts to obtain a voluntary license on reasonable commercial terms.

**Article 107(1) (Public Interest Compulsory License):** Where a compulsory license is necessary for the public interest (공익을 위하여 비상업적으로 실시할 필요가 있는 경우), including national defense, public health, and the correction of anti-competitive practices, the government or an authorized person may obtain a compulsory license. Prior negotiation requirements are waived in cases of national emergency, extreme urgency, or public non-commercial use.

**Article 107(3) (Dependent Patent):** Where a later patent with an important technical advance of considerable economic significance cannot be worked without infringing an earlier patent, the holder of the later patent may seek a compulsory license on the earlier patent. The earlier patent holder is entitled to a cross-license on the later patent on reasonable terms.

**Article 110 (Remuneration):** The compulsory licensee must pay adequate remuneration (적절한 보상금) to the patent holder. The amount is determined by KIPO or, in case of dispute, by the courts. Factors include the economic value of the license, the scope of the license, and the remuneration that would have been payable under voluntary licensing.

### COVID-19 Response

South Korea's response to the COVID-19 pandemic illustrates the leverage function of compulsory licensing even when not formally exercised:

**Remdesivir (2020):** When Gilead Sciences initially limited the supply of remdesivir, the Korean government publicly discussed invoking Article 107 compulsory licensing provisions. This pressure, combined with similar signals from other governments, contributed to Gilead's decision to grant voluntary licenses to generic manufacturers and to increase supply to Korea. The episode demonstrated the "shadow of the law" effect of compulsory licensing provisions.

**Infectious Disease Control and Prevention Act (감염병의 예방 및 관리에 관한 법률):** Article 40-4 (introduced by amendment during the COVID-19 pandemic) authorizes the government to mandate the production and supply of medicines, medical devices, and other necessary goods during an infectious disease crisis, providing a supplementary authority beyond the Patent Act's compulsory licensing provisions.

**Vaccine Production:** Korea leveraged its biomanufacturing capacity to secure technology transfer agreements with Moderna, Novavax, and AstraZeneca for COVID-19 vaccine production, partly facilitated by the implicit threat of compulsory licensing and the government's investment in pandemic response infrastructure through the Korea Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (질병관리청).

### Pharmaceutical Patent System

Korea's National Health Insurance (NHI) system operates a drug pricing mechanism that interacts with patent status. The Health Insurance Review and Assessment Service (건강보험심사평가원, HIRA) and the National Health Insurance Service (국민건강보험공단, NHIS) negotiate drug prices, with patented drugs typically receiving higher reimbursement rates. Upon patent expiration, the reimbursement rate is reduced, creating economic incentives for generic entry. The Korean government has used the threat of compulsory licensing as leverage in drug pricing negotiations, though formal compulsory licenses have not been issued.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States provides several compulsory licensing mechanisms, though they have been invoked rarely and with significant political constraints.

**28 U.S.C. Section 1498 (Government Use):** The federal government may use or authorize the use of any patented invention without the patent holder's consent, subject to the payment of "reasonable and entire compensation." This provision, derived from the government's eminent domain power, has been the most practically significant compulsory licensing mechanism:

**Scope:** Section 1498 applies to use "by or for the United States," including use by government contractors and subcontractors acting on behalf of the government. The patent holder's sole remedy is a damages action against the United States in the Court of Federal Claims.

**Historical Usage:** The U.S. government has invoked Section 1498 in various contexts, including military procurement (night vision technology, body armor), anthrax response (ciprofloxacin threat in 2001), and nuclear energy. During the 2001 anthrax scare, the government's threat to invoke Section 1498 for ciprofloxacin led Bayer to negotiate a substantial price reduction, demonstrating the provision's leverage function.

### Bayh-Dole March-In Rights (35 U.S.C. Section 203)

The Bayh-Dole Act (Patent and Trademark Law Amendments Act of 1980, Pub. L. 96-517) allows universities and small businesses to retain patent rights in federally funded inventions, but reserves government "march-in rights" under Section 203, permitting the funding agency to grant compulsory licenses to third parties if:

1. The patent holder has not taken effective steps to achieve practical application of the invention
2. Action is necessary to alleviate health or safety needs not reasonably satisfied by the contractor
3. Action is necessary to meet requirements for public use specified by federal regulations
4. The patent holder has failed to comply with the domestic manufacturing requirement

**March-In Right Petitions:** Despite numerous petitions -- primarily from generic pharmaceutical advocates regarding high drug prices -- no federal agency has ever exercised march-in rights. The National Institutes of Health (NIH) has declined petitions regarding Norvir (ritonavir), Xalatan (latanoprost), Fabrazyme (agalsidase beta), and other drugs, consistently holding that high drug prices alone do not constitute a failure of "practical application" under the statute. In 2023, the Biden administration's proposed framework for exercising march-in rights included drug pricing as a relevant consideration, but no formal exercise occurred before the end of the administration.

### Antitrust Remedies

Courts may order compulsory licensing of patents as an antitrust remedy when patent rights have been used to maintain or extend monopoly power in violation of the Sherman Act (15 U.S.C. Sections 1-7) or the Clayton Act (15 U.S.C. Sections 12-27):

**Hartford-Empire Co. v. United States, 323 U.S. 386 (1945):** The Supreme Court upheld compulsory licensing as a remedy for antitrust violations involving the abuse of a patent portfolio to monopolize the glass container industry.

**FTC Consent Decrees:** The Federal Trade Commission has secured compulsory licensing provisions in consent decrees resolving anticompetitive conduct in pharmaceutical and technology markets, including cases involving "pay-for-delay" settlements (reverse payment agreements between brand-name and generic drug manufacturers).

### COVID-19 and the Defense Production Act

During the COVID-19 pandemic, the U.S. government relied primarily on the Defense Production Act (DPA, 50 U.S.C. Section 4501 et seq.) rather than formal compulsory licensing. The DPA was invoked to prioritize vaccine production, allocate scarce materials, and expand manufacturing capacity. Operation Warp Speed contracts included provisions for government rights in resulting intellectual property, and the government's advance purchase agreements provided de facto market guarantees that incentivized rapid vaccine development without formal compulsory licensing.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's compulsory licensing framework combines Patent Act provisions with emergency public health legislation, grounded in the constitutional protection of property rights (Article 14 GG) and its limitations.

**Patentgesetz (PatG) Section 24 (Compulsory License):**

**Section 24(1) (Non-Working and Public Interest):** A non-exclusive license to use a patented invention shall be granted by the Bundespatentgericht (Federal Patent Court) if the applicant has unsuccessfully sought a voluntary license on reasonable commercial terms within a reasonable period, and the grant of the compulsory license is necessary in the public interest (öffentliches Interesse).

**Section 24(2) (Dependent Patent):** Where a patent holder cannot work a later patent without infringing an earlier patent, and the later patent involves an important technical advance of considerable economic significance compared to the earlier patent, the later patent holder may obtain a compulsory license on the earlier patent.

**Section 24(5) (Semiconductor Technology):** Compulsory licenses for semiconductor technology may only be granted to remedy judicially or administratively determined anti-competitive practices.

**Section 24(6) (Remuneration):** The patent holder is entitled to adequate remuneration (angemessene Vergütung), taking into account the circumstances of the individual case and the economic value of the license. Remuneration is determined by the Federal Patent Court or, on appeal, the Bundesgerichtshof.

### Constitutional Framework

**Article 14 Grundgesetz (GG):** Patent rights are protected as property (Eigentum) under Article 14(1) GG, but Article 14(2) provides that property entails obligations and its use should serve the public interest (Sozialbindung des Eigentums). Compulsory licensing represents a determination of the content and limits of property rights (Inhalts- und Schrankenbestimmung) under Article 14(1)(2) GG, subject to proportionality requirements.

**BVerfG, Remdesivir Order (1 BvR 776/20, May 11, 2020):** During the early COVID-19 pandemic, a constitutional complaint was filed seeking an emergency compulsory license for remdesivir under Section 24 PatG. The Federal Constitutional Court declined to grant emergency relief, but its order provided significant guidance: the Court recognized that the public interest in access to essential medicines during a pandemic could justify compulsory licensing, while noting that the existing statutory framework (including Section 24 PatG and IfSG Section 5(2)) provided adequate mechanisms and that the applicant had not demonstrated the unavailability of remdesivir through existing supply channels. The order confirmed that compulsory licensing during public health emergencies is constitutionally permissible under Article 14(2) GG.

### Infection Protection Act (IfSG)

**Section 5(2) IfSG:** During an epidemic emergency of national significance (epidemische Lage von nationaler Tragweite), the Federal Ministry of Health (Bundesministerium für Gesundheit) is authorized to take measures to ensure the supply of medicines, medical devices, diagnostics, and personal protective equipment, including authorizing the use of patented inventions for public health purposes with adequate compensation. This provision was activated during the COVID-19 pandemic and provides a streamlined alternative to the Patent Act's compulsory licensing procedure.

### German Case Law

**BGH, Raltegravir (X ZB 2/17, 2019):** The Bundesgerichtshof addressed the standard for granting compulsory licenses in the pharmaceutical context, holding that a compulsory license under Section 24 PatG may be granted where the applicant demonstrates that the public interest requires access to the patented invention, the applicant has made genuine but unsuccessful efforts to obtain a voluntary license, and the terms of the compulsory license (including remuneration) are proportionate.

**BPatG, Isentress/Raltegravir (3 LiQ 1/16, 2017):** The Federal Patent Court granted a provisional compulsory license (einstweilige Verfügung) for a generic version of the HIV medication raltegravir, holding that the public interest in access to the medication and the applicant's prior unsuccessful licensing negotiations justified the grant. This decision was among the first compulsory licenses actually issued in modern German patent law.

## ECHR & EU

### TRIPS Agreement Framework

The TRIPS Agreement (Agreement on Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights, 1994) provides the international framework for compulsory licensing:

**Article 31 (Other Use Without Authorization of the Right Holder):** Sets conditions for compulsory licensing, including:

| Condition | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| Case-by-case determination | Each application considered on individual merits (Art. 31(a)) |
| Prior negotiation | Efforts to obtain voluntary license on reasonable terms; waived in national emergency, extreme urgency, or public non-commercial use (Art. 31(b)) |
| Scope and duration | Limited to the purpose for which authorized (Art. 31(c)) |
| Non-exclusive | Compulsory license is non-exclusive (Art. 31(d)) |
| Non-assignable | Cannot be assigned except with the enterprise/goodwill (Art. 31(e)) |
| Predominantly domestic supply | Authorized predominantly for domestic market supply (Art. 31(f), modified by Art. 31bis) |
| Remuneration | Adequate remuneration taking into account economic value (Art. 31(h)) |
| Judicial review | Subject to judicial or independent review (Art. 31(i)-(j)) |
| Termination | Must be terminated when circumstances cease to exist (Art. 31(g)) |

**Article 31bis (Export Compulsory Licensing):** Added by the 2017 TRIPS Amendment (originally the 2003 Waiver Decision), Article 31bis permits WTO members to grant compulsory licenses for the production and export of pharmaceutical products to eligible importing members with insufficient manufacturing capacity, addressing the limitation of Article 31(f).

### Doha Declaration on TRIPS and Public Health (2001)

The Doha Declaration, adopted at the WTO Ministerial Conference on November 14, 2001, affirmed that:

- The TRIPS Agreement "does not and should not prevent members from taking measures to protect public health"
- The Agreement "should be interpreted and implemented in a manner supportive of WTO members' right to protect public health and, in particular, to promote access to medicines for all"
- Each WTO member has "the right to grant compulsory licenses and the freedom to determine the grounds upon which such licenses are granted"
- Each member has "the right to determine what constitutes a national emergency or other circumstances of extreme urgency, it being understood that public health crises, including those relating to HIV/AIDS, tuberculosis, malaria and other epidemics, can represent a national emergency"

### COVID-19 TRIPS Waiver

**Ministerial Decision on the TRIPS Agreement (MC12, June 17, 2022):** After extensive negotiations initiated by the India-South Africa TRIPS Waiver Proposal (October 2020), the WTO 12th Ministerial Conference adopted a decision waiving certain obligations under Article 31 for the production and supply of COVID-19 vaccines:

**Scope:** The waiver applied to patents and undisclosed information (trade secrets were excluded from the final decision) necessary for the production and supply of COVID-19 vaccines for a period of five years.

**Key Provisions:** Eligible developing country members were not required to comply with Article 31(f) (predominantly domestic supply), enabling export of vaccines produced under compulsory license; were not required to demonstrate prior unsuccessful efforts to obtain a voluntary license (Article 31(b)); and could apply compulsory licenses to all patents, not just specific patents, needed for the production and supply of COVID-19 vaccines.

**Limitations:** The decision was criticized by public health advocates for its narrow scope (vaccines only, excluding diagnostics and therapeutics), late timing (adopted over 18 months after vaccines became available), and restrictive eligibility (developed countries and certain developing countries opted out). The decision to extend the waiver to diagnostics and therapeutics was not adopted.

### EU Compulsory Licensing Regulation

**Regulation (EC) No 816/2006:** Implements the TRIPS Article 31bis system at the EU level, establishing procedures for granting compulsory licenses for the manufacture of pharmaceutical products for export to eligible importing countries with public health problems. The regulation applies where the importing country has notified its need to the WTO TRIPS Council.

**Proposed EU Compulsory Licensing Regulation for Crisis Management:** In April 2023, the European Commission proposed a regulation establishing a Union compulsory licensing mechanism that could be activated during crisis situations (including pandemics and other emergencies) to ensure the availability of crisis-relevant products protected by patents or supplementary protection certificates. The proposal would centralize the compulsory licensing decision at the EU level (through a Commission implementing act), rather than relying on 27 separate national procedures, and would cover patents and SPCs related to products needed during crises. The proposal has been subject to extensive negotiation in the Council and Parliament.

### ECHR Property Protection

**Protocol 1, Article 1 ECHR (Protection of Property):** Patent rights constitute "possessions" within the meaning of Protocol 1, Article 1. Compulsory licensing constitutes a "control of use" of property, permissible under the second paragraph of Article 1 if it is provided for by law, pursues a legitimate aim in the general interest, and is proportionate (fair balance between the public interest and the individual's property rights, including the requirement of adequate compensation).

**Smith Kline and French Laboratories Ltd v. Netherlands (Application No. 12633/87, Commission Decision, 1990):** The European Commission of Human Rights held that a compulsory license granted under Dutch patent law did not violate Protocol 1, Article 1, finding that the compulsory licensing system pursued a legitimate aim (ensuring the availability of medicines) and that the requirement of adequate remuneration ensured a fair balance between the public interest and the patent holder's rights.

## Comparative Analysis

### Frequency and Political Willingness

The most significant practical difference is in the willingness to use compulsory licensing mechanisms. South Korea and Germany have demonstrated a willingness to threaten and, in Germany's case (Raltegravir), grant compulsory licenses for pharmaceutical access. The United States, despite having multiple statutory mechanisms, has been extremely reluctant to invoke them, with march-in rights never exercised and Section 1498 rarely used for pharmaceutical patents. The EU has pursued a systemic approach through the proposed centralized compulsory licensing regulation, which would lower procedural barriers to use during crises.

### Public Interest Standard

The definition of "public interest" varies significantly. Germany requires a demonstrated public interest assessed by the Federal Patent Court, with the Raltegravir decision providing guidance on pharmaceutical access. South Korea's public interest standard is defined broadly to include public health, national defense, and competition concerns. The U.S. lacks a unified public interest standard, with different mechanisms applying different criteria (reasonable compensation under Section 1498; practical application under Bayh-Dole). The TRIPS framework leaves the definition of national emergency and public interest to individual WTO members.

### Pharmaceutical Access

Compulsory licensing's most consequential application is in pharmaceutical access. The Doha Declaration and Article 31bis represent international recognition that patent protection must accommodate public health needs. However, the COVID-19 TRIPS Waiver's limited scope and delayed adoption demonstrated the difficulty of achieving multilateral action on compulsory licensing, even during a global health emergency. National frameworks remain the primary mechanism for pharmaceutical compulsory licensing, with effectiveness depending on domestic political will and institutional capacity.

### COVID-19 Legacy

The COVID-19 pandemic catalyzed significant developments across all jurisdictions: Germany's BVerfG Remdesivir order clarified constitutional parameters; Korea's implicit compulsory licensing threat facilitated voluntary licensing; the U.S. relied on procurement-based approaches rather than compulsory licensing; and the EU proposed centralized crisis management mechanisms. These developments suggest a trend toward stronger, more accessible compulsory licensing frameworks for future health emergencies, though political and industry opposition remains significant.
