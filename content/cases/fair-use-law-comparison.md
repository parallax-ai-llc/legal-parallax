## Summary

Fair use and fair dealing doctrines represent the primary mechanisms by which copyright systems balance the exclusive rights of authors against the public interest in access to knowledge, education, criticism, news reporting, and creative reuse, with the fundamental distinction being whether limitations are structured as open-ended balancing tests (fair use) or closed lists of permitted purposes (fair dealing and enumerated exceptions) -- a distinction with profound implications for adaptability to new technologies, legal certainty, and the scope of permissible uses. South Korea's Copyright Act (저작권법) provides a hybrid approach combining enumerated exceptions (Articles 23-38) for specific purposes including education, reporting, private use, and library reproduction, with a general fair use provision (Article 35-5, introduced in 2011) modeled on the U.S. four-factor test, making Korea one of the few civil law jurisdictions to adopt an open-ended fair use clause. The United States provides the most expansive fair use doctrine under 17 U.S.C. Section 107, a flexible four-factor balancing test that has been developed through extensive case law including the transformative use doctrine established in Campbell v. Acuff-Rose Music, Inc., 510 U.S. 569 (1994), recently recalibrated by Andy Warhol Foundation for the Visual Arts v. Goldsmith, 598 U.S. 508 (2023), and applied to new technologies including search engines (Perfect 10, Inc. v. Amazon.com, Inc., 508 F.3d 1146 (9th Cir. 2007)), text mining (Authors Guild v. Google, Inc., 804 F.3d 202 (2d Cir. 2015)), and AI training data (ongoing litigation). Germany relies on an exhaustive system of statutory exceptions (Schranken des Urheberrechts, Sections 44a-63a UrhG) including specific provisions for quotation (Section 51), parody and caricature (Section 51a), education and research (Sections 60a-60h), and text and data mining (Section 44b), without a general fair use clause, reflecting the continental European tradition of legal certainty and narrow construction of copyright limitations. The EU framework establishes an exhaustive list of permitted exceptions in Article 5 of the Information Society Directive 2001/29/EC, with only the temporary reproduction exception being mandatory, while the Digital Single Market Directive 2019/790 introduced new mandatory exceptions for text and data mining (Articles 3-4), digital and cross-border teaching (Article 5), and cultural heritage preservation (Article 6), and the CJEU has developed principles of autonomous interpretation and fundamental rights balancing.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's copyright limitation system represents a distinctive hybrid that combines the civil law tradition of enumerated exceptions with the common law fair use approach.

**Enumerated Exceptions (Articles 23-38):** The Copyright Act provides specific exceptions including:

| Article | Exception |
|---------|-----------|
| Article 23 | Reproduction for judicial proceedings |
| Article 24 | Use of political speeches and court decisions |
| Article 24-2 | Free use of public works (open government data) |
| Article 25 | Use for school education purposes |
| Article 26 | Use for news reporting |
| Article 27 | Reproduction in examinations |
| Article 28 | Quotation from published works for criticism, commentary, education, or research |
| Article 29 | Non-commercial performance and broadcasting |
| Article 30 | Reproduction for personal use |
| Article 31 | Library reproduction and digital transmission |
| Article 35-2 | Temporary reproduction (transient copying) |

### General Fair Use Clause (Article 35-5)

Introduced by the 2011 amendment to the Copyright Act, Article 35-5 provides a general fair use clause modeled closely on U.S. Section 107:

The provision permits the use of copyrighted works in cases not covered by the enumerated exceptions, where such use does not conflict with the normal exploitation of the work and does not unreasonably prejudice the legitimate interests of the right holder. Four factors guide the analysis:

1. The purpose and character of the use, including whether the use is commercial or for nonprofit educational purposes
2. The nature of the copyrighted work
3. The amount and substantiality of the portion used in relation to the copyrighted work as a whole
4. The effect of the use upon the current or potential market for or value of the copyrighted work

### Korean Case Law on Fair Use

The Korean courts have applied Article 35-5 in a growing body of decisions:

**Seoul Central District Court, 2014GaHap567810:** The court applied the fair use factors to assess the use of thumbnail images by an internet search engine, finding fair use based on the transformative purpose of facilitating information retrieval and the minimal market impact on the original works.

**Supreme Court of Korea (대법원), 2018Do13945 (2021):** The Supreme Court addressed the scope of the quotation exception under Article 28, holding that quotation for criticism or commentary requires that the quoted work be subordinate to the new work and that the quotation be justified by its purpose. The Court noted that Article 35-5 provides additional flexibility where the enumerated exceptions may not apply.

**Korean Copyright Commission Guidelines:** The Commission has issued guidelines on the application of Article 35-5, emphasizing that the general fair use clause serves as a safety valve for socially beneficial uses not anticipated by the enumerated exceptions, and should be interpreted in light of Korea's international treaty obligations under the Berne Convention three-step test.

### Parody and Satire

Korea does not have a specific statutory exception for parody. Parody uses are analyzed under Article 28 (quotation for criticism) and Article 35-5 (general fair use). Korean courts have generally been more restrictive regarding parody than U.S. courts, requiring a clear critical or commentary purpose and proportionality between the borrowing and the parodic message.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The U.S. fair use doctrine is the most flexible and extensively litigated copyright limitation globally.

**17 U.S.C. Section 107:** Fair use of a copyrighted work for purposes such as criticism, comment, news reporting, teaching, scholarship, or research is not an infringement. The statutory preamble's enumeration of purposes is illustrative, not exhaustive. The four factors are:

1. **Purpose and character of the use** -- including whether the use is of a commercial nature or is for nonprofit educational purposes
2. **Nature of the copyrighted work** -- creative works receive stronger protection than factual ones
3. **Amount and substantiality of the portion used** -- both quantitative extent and qualitative significance
4. **Effect on the market** -- impact on the market for or value of the copyrighted work

### Transformative Use Doctrine

**Campbell v. Acuff-Rose Music, Inc., 510 U.S. 569 (1994):** The Supreme Court held that 2 Live Crew's parody of Roy Orbison's "Oh, Pretty Woman" could qualify as fair use, establishing that the central question under the first factor is whether the new work is "transformative" -- whether it adds something new with a different purpose or character, altering the original with new expression, meaning, or message.

**Andy Warhol Foundation for the Visual Arts v. Goldsmith, 598 U.S. 508 (2023):** The Supreme Court significantly recalibrated the transformative use doctrine. Justice Sotomayor's majority opinion held that the licensing of Warhol's Orange Prince silkscreen for a magazine cover was not fair use because the use had the same commercial licensing purpose as the original photograph. The Court emphasized that the first factor requires examining whether the new use has a "distinct purpose or character" from the original, and that the degree of transformation must be weighed against the commercial nature of the use. The decision refocused fair use analysis on the purpose of the specific use at issue rather than the artistic transformation of the work itself.

### Educational Fair Use

**Cambridge University Press v. Patton (Becker), 769 F.3d 1232 (11th Cir. 2014):** The Eleventh Circuit held that the district court erred in applying a bright-line 10% threshold for fair use of academic excerpts, requiring instead a case-by-case analysis of all four factors. The decision established that nonprofit educational use does not create a presumption of fair use and that the availability of permissions licensing weighs against fair use.

**Section 110:** Separate from fair use, Section 110 provides specific exemptions for performances and displays in face-to-face teaching activities (Section 110(1)) and distance education under the TEACH Act (Section 110(2), Technology, Education, and Copyright Harmonization Act of 2002).

### Fair Use and Technology

**Authors Guild v. Google, Inc., 804 F.3d 202 (2d Cir. 2015):** The Second Circuit held that Google's digitization of millions of books for its Google Books search service was fair use, finding that the provision of search functionality and snippet views was "highly transformative" and that the absence of significant market substitution weighed in Google's favor. The decision endorsed the concept of "non-expressive" or "intermediate" copying as transformative when it enables information retrieval rather than expressive substitution.

**Perfect 10, Inc. v. Amazon.com, Inc., 508 F.3d 1146 (9th Cir. 2007):** The Ninth Circuit held that Google's display of thumbnail images in search results was fair use, finding a transformative purpose in the use of images as pointers to information rather than as aesthetic works.

**AI Training Data Litigation:** Multiple pending cases address whether the use of copyrighted works to train artificial intelligence models constitutes fair use, including Thomson Reuters Enterprise Centre GmbH v. Ross Intelligence Inc. (D. Del.), Andersen v. Stability AI Ltd. (N.D. Cal.), and The New York Times Company v. Microsoft Corporation and OpenAI (S.D.N.Y.). These cases present novel questions about whether AI training constitutes a transformative, non-expressive use analogous to Google Books or a market-substituting use that undermines the incentive structure of copyright.

### Parody versus Satire

Following Campbell, U.S. law distinguishes between parody (which comments on the original work and borrows from it for that purpose) and satire (which uses the original work as a vehicle to comment on broader social themes). Parody has a stronger claim to fair use because it necessarily targets the original work, while satire can typically achieve its purposes without borrowing from the copyrighted work. However, this distinction is not absolute, and satire may still qualify as fair use under the totality of the four-factor analysis.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's approach to copyright limitations reflects the continental European tradition of legal certainty and strictly defined exceptions.

**Numerus Clausus of Exceptions (Sections 44a-63a UrhG):** German copyright law provides an exhaustive (closed) list of statutory limitations. Any use not falling within these enumerated exceptions constitutes infringement, regardless of its social utility. There is no general fair use or fair dealing clause.

### Quotation Right (Section 51 UrhG)

Section 51 permits the reproduction, distribution, and communication to the public of a published work for the purpose of quotation, provided that the extent of the quotation is justified by the particular purpose. The quotation must serve an independent purpose within the new work (Belegfunktion or Zitatzweck) -- it must support, illustrate, or critically engage with an argument:

**Bundesgerichtshof (BGH), Metall auf Metall (I ZR 112/06, 2008):** The BGH initially held that even minimal sampling of phonograms constituted infringement, though this was subsequently modified after the CJEU's Pelham ruling. The BGH's narrow approach to quotation illustrates the restrictive German tradition.

**BGH, Reformistischer Aufbruch (I ZR 69/08, 2010):** The Court held that the complete reproduction of a text may be permissible as a quotation under Section 51 if the quotation is justified by the purpose of the new work and the reproduced text serves as a reference point for independent critical engagement.

### Parody and Caricature (Section 51a UrhG)

Introduced by the 2021 amendment transposing the DSM Directive, Section 51a explicitly permits the use of a work for the purpose of caricature, parody, and pastiche, provided that the extent of use is justified by the specific purpose. This provision filled a significant gap in German copyright law, where parody had previously been addressed through an uneasy combination of the quotation right (Section 51), the general free use provision (former Section 24, now repealed), and constitutional free speech considerations.

**Former Section 24 UrhG (Free Use -- Freie Benutzung):** Until its repeal in 2021, Section 24 permitted the creation of independent works that used existing works as inspiration, provided the new work maintained sufficient creative distance (Verblassen -- the "fading" of the original in the new work). The CJEU's Pelham decision (Case C-476/17, 2019) effectively invalidated this provision as incompatible with EU law, leading to its replacement by Section 51a.

### Educational Exceptions (Sections 60a-60h UrhG)

The 2018 Urheberrechts-Wissensgesellschafts-Gesetz (UrhWissG, Copyright Knowledge Society Act) introduced comprehensive educational exceptions:

**Section 60a (Teaching and Learning):** Permits the reproduction and making available of up to 15% of a published work for the purpose of teaching at educational institutions, for non-commercial purposes, limited to participants and instructors. Complete works of limited scope (articles, illustrations, out-of-print works) may be used in their entirety.

**Section 60c (Scientific Research):** Permits the reproduction of up to 15% of a work for personal scientific research and up to 75% for personal scientific research if the reproduction is necessary for the research purpose.

**Section 60d (Text and Data Mining for Research):** Permits the creation of corpora of lawfully accessible materials for non-commercial scientific research, including the storage of the corpus for verification and quality assurance. This exception cannot be overridden by contract.

**Section 60e-60f (Libraries and Archives):** Permits digitization, lending, and preservation activities by libraries, archives, and museums.

### Text and Data Mining (Section 44b UrhG)

The general TDM exception, transposing DSM Directive Article 4, permits any person to make reproductions of lawfully accessible works for the purpose of text and data mining. Unlike Section 60d (research TDM), the general exception is subject to an opt-out: rights holders may reserve the right to prohibit TDM through machine-readable means. The interaction between Section 44b and AI training has become a major legal battleground, with rights holder organizations deploying opt-out mechanisms and AI developers arguing that training constitutes permissible TDM.

## ECHR & EU

### Information Society Directive 2001/29/EC

Article 5 provides the exhaustive list of exceptions and limitations to copyright in EU law:

**Article 5(1) (Mandatory):** The only mandatory exception -- temporary acts of reproduction that are transient or incidental, form an integral and essential part of a technological process, and have no independent economic significance.

**Article 5(2) (Optional Reproduction Exceptions):** Including private copying with fair compensation (Art. 5(2)(b)), reprographic reproduction (Art. 5(2)(a)), and archival preservation (Art. 5(2)(c)).

**Article 5(3) (Optional Use Exceptions):** Including illustration for teaching or scientific research (Art. 5(3)(a)), use for criticism or review (Art. 5(3)(d)), use for parody, caricature, or pastiche (Art. 5(3)(k)), and incidental inclusion (Art. 5(3)(i)).

**Article 5(5) (Three-Step Test):** All exceptions must be applied only in special cases that do not conflict with the normal exploitation of the work and do not unreasonably prejudice the legitimate interests of the right holder, reflecting the Berne Convention three-step test (Article 9(2)).

### Digital Single Market Directive 2019/790

The DSM Directive introduced new mandatory exceptions:

**Article 3 (TDM for Research):** Mandatory exception for reproductions and extractions made by research organizations and cultural heritage institutions for scientific research. This exception cannot be overridden by contract.

**Article 4 (General TDM):** Mandatory exception for reproductions and extractions of lawfully accessible works for text and data mining, subject to rights holder opt-out through "appropriate means" including machine-readable restrictions.

**Article 5 (Digital and Cross-Border Teaching):** Mandatory exception for digital use of works for the purpose of illustration for teaching, to the extent justified by the non-commercial purpose, in educational establishments recognized by the member state, subject to fair compensation. Member states may limit this exception where adequate licenses are easily available.

### CJEU Jurisprudence on Fair Use Analogues

**Deckmyn v. Vandersteen (Case C-201/13, 2014):** The CJEU held that "parody" under Article 5(3)(k) is an autonomous concept of EU law, defined by two essential characteristics: (1) evoking an existing work while being noticeably different from it, and (2) constituting an expression of humor or mockery. The Court held that it is not necessary for the parody to display an original character of its own, nor must the parody reasonably be attributed to a person other than the author of the parodied work. However, the application of the parody exception must strike a "fair balance" between the interests of rights holders and the freedom of expression of the user.

**Funke Medien NRW GmbH v. Bundesrepublik Deutschland (Case C-469/17, 2019):** The CJEU confirmed that the exceptions listed in Article 5 of the Information Society Directive are exhaustive and that member states cannot introduce additional exceptions beyond those listed. However, the Court recognized that fundamental rights -- including freedom of information (Article 11 of the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights) -- must be taken into account in interpreting the scope of existing exceptions.

**Pelham GmbH v. Ralf Hütter (Case C-476/17, 2019):** In the landmark Metall auf Metall case, the CJEU held that sampling of a phonogram, even a very short extract, requires authorization unless the sample is modified to such a degree as to be unrecognizable. The Court confirmed that the free use concept under former German Section 24 UrhG was incompatible with the exhaustive harmonization of exceptions under EU law, but recognized that the parody exception could apply to sampling that constitutes an expression of humor or mockery.

**Spiegel Online GmbH v. Volker Beck (Case C-516/17, 2019):** The CJEU held that the quotation exception may apply to hyperlinks to documents that have been made publicly available with the author's consent, and that the right to freedom of information does not justify exceptions beyond those exhaustively listed in the Directive.

### ECHR and Freedom of Expression

**Ashby Donald and Others v. France (Application No. 36769/08, 2013):** The ECtHR held that copyright enforcement may constitute an interference with freedom of expression under Article 10 ECHR, subject to the proportionality test. While the Court found the interference justified in the specific case (unauthorized publication of fashion show photographs), the decision established the principle that copyright limitations must be assessed against Article 10 requirements.

## Comparative Analysis

### Open versus Closed Systems

The most fundamental divergence is between the open-ended U.S. fair use doctrine and the closed-list European approach. The U.S. system provides maximum flexibility, allowing courts to adapt copyright limitations to new technologies and social practices without legislative intervention. The EU/German system provides greater legal certainty and predictability but requires legislative amendment to accommodate new uses. South Korea's hybrid approach represents an innovative middle ground, maintaining enumerated exceptions while providing a general fair use safety valve.

### Transformative Use After Warhol

The U.S. Supreme Court's Warhol decision significantly narrowed the transformative use doctrine, requiring courts to focus on whether the specific use at issue has a different purpose or character from the original, rather than whether the work itself transforms the original's expression. This shift may have the practical effect of narrowing fair use for commercial uses that do not clearly serve a different function, potentially bringing U.S. fair use analysis closer to the European focus on specific permitted purposes.

### Educational Use

Germany's UrhWissG provisions provide the most detailed and certain educational exceptions, with specific percentage thresholds (15%) and clear institutional scope. The U.S. relies on the more flexible but less predictable fair use analysis for educational uses. The EU's DSM Directive Article 5 establishes a new mandatory minimum but allows member states to limit the exception where licensing is available, creating potential variation across member states.

### AI and Text Mining

The treatment of AI training data represents the most consequential contemporary divergence. The EU/Germany framework provides explicit TDM exceptions with rights holder opt-out mechanisms, creating a structured framework for AI training on copyrighted works. The U.S. relies on fair use, with the outcome of pending litigation likely to determine whether AI training is treated as transformative non-expressive use or market-substituting infringement. South Korea's general fair use clause provides flexibility to address AI issues but lacks the specificity of the EU approach.
