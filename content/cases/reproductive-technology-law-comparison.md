## Summary

Assisted reproductive technology (ART) -- encompassing in vitro fertilization (IVF), intracytoplasmic sperm injection (ICSI), gamete and embryo donation, gestational surrogacy, and preimplantation genetic testing -- has become one of the most rapidly evolving and jurisdictionally divergent areas of healthcare regulation. South Korea regulates ART primarily through the Bioethics and Safety Act (생명윤리 및 안전에 관한 법률) and the Mother and Child Health Act (모자보건법), which together permit IVF for married couples and restrict surrogacy, embryo research, and sex selection, while the declining birth rate has prompted the government to expand national health insurance coverage for fertility treatments since 2006 and to continuously increase subsidies through the Act on Support for Infertility Treatment. The United States takes a largely laissez-faire approach at the federal level, with minimal direct regulation of ART beyond the Fertility Clinic Success Rate and Certification Act of 1992 (requiring CDC reporting) and FDA tissue regulations (21 C.F.R. Parts 1270-1271), leaving substantive regulation to states that have produced a patchwork of inconsistent laws on issues such as surrogacy, parentage, and embryo disposition. Germany maintains one of the most restrictive ART regimes in Europe through the Embryonenschutzgesetz (Embryo Protection Act, ESchG) of 1990, which prohibits egg donation, surrogacy, and most forms of preimplantation genetic diagnosis, combined with professional guidelines from the Bundesärztekammer (German Medical Association) and Sozialgesetzbuch Book V provisions on insurance coverage. At the European level, the EU Tissues and Cells Directives (2004/23/EC and implementing directives) establish quality and safety standards for gamete and embryo handling, the ECtHR has developed significant jurisprudence on reproductive rights under Articles 8 and 14 of the Convention, and cross-border reproductive care has become a major regulatory challenge as patients travel between jurisdictions to access prohibited or unavailable treatments.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's regulation of assisted reproductive technology operates through several interconnected statutes. The Bioethics and Safety Act (생명윤리 및 안전에 관한 법률) provides the overarching framework for embryo research and genetic testing. The Mother and Child Health Act (모자보건법) governs reproductive health services. The Act on Support for Infertility Treatment (난임 치료 지원에 관한 법률) establishes the framework for government subsidies.

### Eligibility and Access

ART services in South Korea have traditionally been limited to legally married couples, reflecting the legal and social centrality of marriage in Korean family law. The Civil Act (민법) defines parentage through marital presumption, creating legal complexities for children born through ART outside marriage. However, the Constitutional Court (헌법재판소) has increasingly recognized reproductive autonomy, and policy discussions have expanded to address access for unmarried individuals.

### Government Subsidies

Facing one of the world's lowest total fertility rates (0.72 in 2023, the lowest among OECD nations), South Korea has aggressively expanded ART subsidies. The government began providing fertility treatment subsidies in 2006 and has continuously expanded coverage. As of 2024, the National Health Insurance Service (국민건강보험공단) covers a significant portion of IVF costs for up to a specified number of cycles, with additional support for low-income households. In 2022, the government allocated approximately 300 billion KRW for fertility treatment support.

### Surrogacy and Egg Donation

South Korea does not have an explicit statutory prohibition on surrogacy, but the practice is effectively banned through a combination of the Bioethics and Safety Act provisions (which restrict embryo creation and transfer), Medical Service Act regulations, and the Korean Medical Association's ethical guidelines. Commercial surrogacy arrangements are unenforceable in Korean courts. Egg donation is prohibited under the Bioethics and Safety Act, which restricts the provision of eggs (난자) to research purposes only under strict National Bioethics Committee oversight, following the Hwang Woo-suk scandal in which coerced egg donations from laboratory researchers were exposed.

### Penalties

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| Unauthorized embryo creation or transfer | Up to 5 years imprisonment or 50 million KRW fine |
| Sex selection through ART | Up to 3 years imprisonment or 30 million KRW fine |
| Commercial gamete trading | Up to 3 years imprisonment or 30 million KRW fine |
| Operating unlicensed fertility clinic | Up to 5 years imprisonment |

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has the most permissive and least centrally regulated ART market among developed nations. Federal regulation is minimal, limited primarily to reporting requirements and tissue safety standards.

**Fertility Clinic Success Rate and Certification Act (1992):** This Act requires all ART programs to report pregnancy success rates to the CDC and mandates that programs be certified by approved accreditation bodies. The CDC publishes the annual ART Success Rates report, providing consumer information but not directly regulating clinical practice.

**FDA Tissue Regulations (21 C.F.R. Parts 1270-1271):** The FDA regulates reproductive tissue (sperm, oocytes, embryos) as "human cells, tissues, and cellular and tissue-based products" (HCT/Ps), requiring donor screening and testing for communicable diseases, registration of tissue establishments, and compliance with current good tissue practice (cGTP) requirements.

### State Law Patchwork

State laws governing ART vary enormously, creating a complex and often contradictory regulatory landscape.

**Surrogacy:** States range from commercial surrogacy-friendly (California, under Family Code Sections 7960-7962, as revised in 2013; Nevada; Maine) to states prohibiting surrogacy agreements entirely (Michigan, MCL 722.855, criminalizing surrogacy contracts with up to $50,000 fine and 5 years imprisonment) to states with no legislation on point. The Uniform Parentage Act (UPA) of 2017, adopted by approximately 12 states, provides a model framework validating gestational surrogacy agreements under specified conditions.

**Parentage:** The determination of legal parentage for ART-conceived children varies substantially. Some states apply intent-based parentage (recognizing the intended parents as legal parents), others rely on genetic connection, and still others maintain traditional marital presumption rules that may not account for ART scenarios. The UPA 2017 provides for parentage based on the parties' agreement prior to conception.

**Insurance Mandates:** As of 2025, approximately 20 states have enacted laws requiring insurance coverage for fertility diagnosis or treatment, though the scope varies widely. Illinois (Insurance Code Section 356m) and Massachusetts (Chapter 175, Section 47H) require comprehensive IVF coverage, while other states mandate only fertility "diagnosis" or exclude IVF.

### Embryo Disposition Disputes

The question of what happens to cryopreserved embryos upon divorce or disagreement between the progenitors has generated significant case law. Davis v. Davis, 842 S.W.2d 588 (Tenn. 1992), was the first state supreme court case to address the issue, establishing a balancing test between the parties' competing interests. In the absence of a prior agreement, courts generally apply one of three approaches: contractual (enforcing dispositional agreements), contemporaneous mutual consent (requiring current agreement), or balancing of interests.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's ART regulation is among the most restrictive in Europe, centered on the Embryonenschutzgesetz (Embryo Protection Act, ESchG) of December 13, 1990. The Act's strict provisions reflect Germany's historical sensitivity to eugenic practices and the constitutional weight accorded to human dignity under Article 1(1) of the Grundgesetz.

**Key ESchG Prohibitions:**
- Section 1(1) No. 1-2: Egg donation is prohibited; it is a criminal offense to transfer an unfertilized egg cell to another woman or to fertilize an egg cell for any purpose other than bringing about a pregnancy in the woman from whom the egg cell originated.
- Section 1(1) No. 3: No more than three embryos may be transferred to a woman in any one cycle.
- Section 1(1) No. 7: Surrogacy is prohibited; it is a criminal offense to undertake to transfer an embryo to a woman willing to permanently give up the child after birth (ersatzmutterschaft).
- Section 3: Sex selection is prohibited except to prevent sex-linked genetic diseases.

### Preimplantation Genetic Diagnosis (PGD)

The legal status of PGD in Germany was clarified by the Bundesgerichtshof (Federal Court of Justice) in a 2010 decision (5 StR 386/09) and subsequently by the Präimplantationsdiagnostikgesetz (PGD Act, PräimpG) of November 21, 2011. PGD is permitted only in cases where the genetic disposition of one or both parents creates a high risk of serious hereditary disease in the child, or where genetic abnormalities are likely to result in stillbirth or miscarriage. Each case requires approval by an interdisciplinary ethics commission.

### Insurance Coverage

Section 27a SGB V provides for partial insurance coverage of ART for married couples where the woman is between 25 and 40 years of age and the man is between 25 and 50. Coverage extends to 50% of costs for up to three treatment cycles of IVF or ICSI. The Bundesverfassungsgericht confirmed in a 2007 decision (1 BvL 5/03) that restricting coverage to married couples was constitutional. Some Bundesländer provide supplementary funding.

### Penalties

| Violation under ESchG | Penalty |
|----------------------|---------|
| Egg donation (Section 1(1) No. 1-2) | Up to 3 years imprisonment |
| Transferring more than 3 embryos (Section 1(1) No. 3) | Up to 3 years imprisonment |
| Surrogacy (Section 1(1) No. 7) | Up to 3 years imprisonment |
| Unauthorized sex selection (Section 3) | Up to 1 year imprisonment |
| Unauthorized PGD (PräimpG) | Up to 1 year imprisonment or fine |

## ECHR & EU

### EU Tissues and Cells Directives

Directive 2004/23/EC of the European Parliament and of the Council of March 31, 2004, establishes standards of quality and safety for the donation, procurement, testing, processing, preservation, storage, and distribution of human tissues and cells, including reproductive cells. Implementing Directives 2006/17/EC and 2006/86/EC provide technical requirements for donor testing, traceability, and coding. The directives were revised by Regulation (EU) 2024/1143 on substances of human origin (SoHO Regulation), adopted on April 17, 2024, which replaces and updates the tissues and cells framework.

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECtHR has developed extensive jurisprudence on ART under Article 8 (right to respect for private and family life) and Article 14 (prohibition of discrimination).

**Evans v. United Kingdom (Application No. 6339/05, Grand Chamber Judgment of April 10, 2007):** The Grand Chamber held that the United Kingdom's requirement of ongoing consent from both gamete providers for the continued storage and use of embryos did not violate Article 8, granting a wide margin of appreciation to the state on the sensitive ethical question of the status of embryos. The applicant, whose former partner withdrew his consent to the use of their frozen embryos, was not permitted to use them for IVF.

**S.H. and Others v. Austria (Application No. 57813/00, Grand Chamber Judgment of November 3, 2011):** The Grand Chamber upheld Austria's prohibition on egg donation and sperm donation for IVF (as opposed to in vivo fertilization), finding no violation of Articles 8 or 14. The Court granted Austria a wide margin of appreciation but noted that the area was subject to rapid scientific and social developments, and that Austria would need to keep the prohibition under review.

**Mennesson v. France (Application No. 65192/11, Judgment of June 26, 2014):** The Court found a violation of the children's right to private life under Article 8 where France refused to recognize the parent-child relationship between children born through surrogacy in the United States and their biological father. The Court held that the children's right to identity required France to provide a mechanism for recognition of the legal parent-child relationship.

**Advisory Opinion on Surrogacy (Request No. P16-2018-001, April 10, 2019):** In its first advisory opinion under Protocol No. 16, the Grand Chamber advised that Article 8 requires states to provide a mechanism for recognition of the parent-child relationship between a child born through surrogacy abroad and the intended mother (who is not the genetic mother), but that this need not necessarily be through transcription of a foreign birth certificate -- adoption could suffice.

### Cross-Border Reproductive Care

Cross-border reproductive care (CBRC), sometimes termed "reproductive tourism," has become a significant phenomenon within the EU as patients travel to access treatments prohibited or unavailable in their home states. Common patterns include German and French patients traveling to Spain or the Czech Republic for egg donation, and couples from countries prohibiting surrogacy traveling to jurisdictions where it is permitted. The European Society of Human Reproduction and Embryology (ESHRE) has documented these patterns and advocated for regulatory harmonization.

## Comparative Analysis

### Regulatory Philosophy

The four jurisdictions reflect fundamentally different regulatory philosophies. The United States applies a market-based approach with minimal government intervention, prioritizing patient autonomy and innovation. Germany applies a precautionary, dignity-based approach that restricts ART to protect embryos and prevent exploitation. South Korea occupies a middle ground, restricting ART on ethical grounds while simultaneously promoting fertility treatment access for demographic reasons. The EU framework focuses primarily on safety and quality standards rather than ethical regulation, leaving substantive policy choices to member states.

### Surrogacy

Surrogacy represents the area of greatest divergence. The United States permits commercial surrogacy in several states and has a well-developed legal infrastructure supporting it. Germany and South Korea prohibit surrogacy, though neither effectively prevents citizens from pursuing surrogacy abroad. The ECHR has required states to recognize parent-child relationships established through foreign surrogacy arrangements, creating a tension between domestic prohibition and international recognition obligations.

### Access and Equity

Insurance coverage for ART varies dramatically. South Korea provides the most generous public subsidies, driven by pronatalist demographic policy. Germany provides partial coverage for married couples within age limits. The United States has no federal mandate, with coverage dependent on state law and employer insurance plan.

### Embryo Status

The legal status of the embryo is the foundational question underlying ART regulation. Germany accords the embryo the strongest protections, prohibiting creation of surplus embryos and egg donation. The United States treats embryos as a category between persons and property, with disposition governed by contract or judicial balancing. South Korea prohibits embryo creation for research but permits limited research on surplus embryos. The ECHR has declined to resolve the embryo's status, granting states a wide margin of appreciation.
