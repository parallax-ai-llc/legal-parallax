## Summary

Income tax -- a direct tax levied on the earnings of individuals and, in some systems, unincorporated entities -- constitutes the single largest source of government revenue in most advanced economies and has been subject to continuous legislative evolution since its modern inception in the early nineteenth century. South Korea's income tax framework is governed by the Income Tax Act (소득세법), first enacted in 1949 and comprehensively revised in 1974 and again in 1994, which imposes progressive rates ranging from 6 percent on annual income up to 14 million KRW to 45 percent on income exceeding 1 billion KRW, with an additional local income tax surcharge of 10 percent of the national tax liability under the Local Tax Act (지방세법). The United States imposes federal income tax under the Internal Revenue Code (26 U.S.C.), originating from the Revenue Act of 1913 following ratification of the Sixteenth Amendment, with current marginal rates ranging from 10 percent to 37 percent under the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 (TCJA), supplemented by state income taxes in 43 states and the District of Columbia. Germany levies income tax (Einkommensteuer) under the Einkommensteuergesetz (EStG), with progressive rates ranging from 14 percent to 45 percent plus a 5.5 percent solidarity surcharge (Solidaritätszuschlag) that was largely abolished for most taxpayers in 2021, and church tax (Kirchensteuer) of 8-9 percent of income tax liability for members of recognized religious communities. At the European Union level, while direct taxation remains primarily a member state competence under Article 114 TFEU, the EU has increasingly coordinated income tax policy through directives addressing cross-border taxation, the Anti-Tax Avoidance Directives (ATAD I and II), and the landmark agreement on a global minimum tax rate of 15 percent implemented through Council Directive (EU) 2022/2523 on Pillar Two of the OECD/G20 Inclusive Framework on Base Erosion and Profit Shifting.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's income tax system is governed primarily by the Income Tax Act (소득세법, Act No. 14389), which has been amended numerous times since its original enactment in 1949. The Act classifies income into eight categories: global income (종합소득) encompassing interest, dividends, business, employment, pension, and other income; retirement income (퇴직소득); and capital gains income (양도소득). The Framework Act on National Taxes (국세기본법) provides general procedural and administrative rules applicable to all national taxes including income tax.

### Progressive Rate Structure

| Taxable Income (KRW) | Marginal Rate |
|----------------------|---------------|
| Up to 14 million | 6% |
| 14 million - 50 million | 15% |
| 50 million - 88 million | 24% |
| 88 million - 150 million | 35% |
| 150 million - 300 million | 38% |
| 300 million - 500 million | 40% |
| 500 million - 1 billion | 42% |
| Over 1 billion | 45% |

In addition to the national income tax, a local income tax (지방소득세) of 10 percent of the national tax liability is levied under the Local Tax Act (지방세법), bringing the effective top marginal rate to approximately 49.5 percent.

### Employment Income and Year-End Settlement

For salaried employees, income tax is withheld at source through the simplified tax table system. Employers conduct year-end tax settlement (연말정산) in January or February of the following year, adjusting withholdings based on actual deductions claimed. Common deductions include medical expenses, education expenses, insurance premiums, housing loan interest (주택담보대출 이자), credit card usage deductions (신용카드 등 소득공제), and contributions to individual retirement pension accounts.

### Recent Reforms

The 2023 tax reform increased the lowest bracket threshold from 12 million KRW to 14 million KRW, providing modest relief for lower-income earners. The government also expanded childcare-related tax credits, increasing the child tax credit (자녀세액공제) from 150,000 KRW to 200,000 KRW per child for the second child and from 300,000 KRW to 400,000 KRW for the third and subsequent children.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States federal income tax is authorized by the Sixteenth Amendment to the Constitution, ratified on February 3, 1913, which states: "The Congress shall have power to lay and collect taxes on incomes, from whatever source derived, without apportionment among the several States." The Internal Revenue Code of 1986 (26 U.S.C.), as continuously amended, provides the statutory basis, administered by the Internal Revenue Service (IRS) within the Department of the Treasury.

### Federal Rate Structure (Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017)

| Filing Status: Single | Marginal Rate |
|----------------------|---------------|
| Up to $11,600 | 10% |
| $11,601 - $47,150 | 12% |
| $47,151 - $100,525 | 22% |
| $100,526 - $191,950 | 24% |
| $191,951 - $243,725 | 32% |
| $243,726 - $609,350 | 35% |
| Over $609,350 | 37% |

The TCJA provisions for individual taxpayers are currently set to expire after December 31, 2025, though Congressional debate on extension or modification continues as of early 2026. If not extended, rates would revert to pre-TCJA levels, with the top rate returning to 39.6 percent.

### Citizenship-Based Taxation

The United States is unique among major economies in taxing based on citizenship rather than solely on residence. U.S. citizens and permanent residents (green card holders) are subject to federal income tax on worldwide income regardless of where they reside. The Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (26 U.S.C. Section 911) allows qualifying expatriates to exclude up to $126,500 (2024 amount, indexed for inflation) of foreign earned income. The Foreign Tax Credit (26 U.S.C. Sections 901-909) provides relief from double taxation by allowing credits for income taxes paid to foreign governments.

### State Income Taxes

Forty-three states and the District of Columbia impose state income taxes. Nine states -- Alaska, Florida, Nevada, New Hampshire (interest and dividends only, phased out by 2027), South Dakota, Tennessee (phased out in 2021), Texas, Washington, and Wyoming -- impose no broad-based state income tax. California imposes the highest top marginal rate at 13.3 percent, while several states use flat rate systems.

### Landmark Cases

**Pollock v. Farmers' Loan & Trust Co., 157 U.S. 429 (1895):** The Supreme Court struck down the federal income tax imposed by the Wilson-Gorman Tariff Act of 1894, holding that a tax on income derived from property constituted a direct tax requiring apportionment among the states. This decision necessitated the Sixteenth Amendment.

**Commissioner v. Glenshaw Glass Co., 348 U.S. 426 (1955):** The Supreme Court defined gross income broadly as "undeniable accessions to wealth, clearly realized, and over which the taxpayers have complete dominion," establishing the foundational test for income inclusion.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany levies income tax under the Einkommensteuergesetz (EStG), which applies to individuals (natürliche Personen) who are either resident (unbeschränkt steuerpflichtig) or have German-source income (beschränkt steuerpflichtig). The Act distinguishes seven categories of income (Einkunftsarten): income from agriculture and forestry (Land- und Forstwirtschaft), income from trade or business (Gewerbebetrieb), income from independent personal services (selbständige Arbeit), income from employment (nichtselbständige Arbeit), income from capital assets (Kapitalvermögen), income from rents and royalties (Vermietung und Verpachtung), and other income (sonstige Einkünfte).

### Rate Structure

Germany employs a unique progressive rate formula rather than discrete brackets. The rate begins at 14 percent (Eingangssteuersatz) for taxable income above the basic personal allowance (Grundfreibetrag) of EUR 11,604 (2024) and increases linearly through two zones to reach the top rate of 42 percent at approximately EUR 66,761. A surcharge rate of 45 percent (Reichensteuersatz or "rich tax") applies to taxable income exceeding EUR 277,826. The Solidaritätszuschlag (solidarity surcharge) of 5.5 percent of the income tax liability was effectively abolished for approximately 90 percent of taxpayers from January 1, 2021, but continues to apply to higher earners.

### Splitting System (Ehegattensplitting)

Germany allows married couples and registered life partners to elect joint taxation under the Splitting procedure (Ehegattensplitting), codified in Section 32a(5) EStG. Under this system, the combined taxable income of both spouses is halved, the income tax is calculated on that halved amount, and then doubled. Given Germany's progressive rate structure, this provides a significant tax advantage when one spouse earns substantially more than the other. The system has been subject to ongoing political debate, with critics arguing it disincentivizes labor force participation by the lower-earning spouse.

### Wage Tax (Lohnsteuer)

Employed individuals have income tax withheld at source through the Lohnsteuer system. Employees are assigned to one of six tax classes (Steuerklassen I-VI) based on marital status and other factors. The employer withholds Lohnsteuer based on published tables and remits it to the tax office (Finanzamt). The Lohnsteuer is not a separate tax but rather a prepayment of the annual income tax liability.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Framework for Direct Taxation

While direct taxation remains primarily within member state sovereignty under the principle of subsidiarity, the EU exerts significant influence through several mechanisms:

**Free Movement Constraints:** The CJEU has developed extensive case law limiting member state income tax provisions that impede the four fundamental freedoms. In Schumacker (C-279/93, 1995), the Court held that Germany violated free movement of workers by denying a Belgian resident working in Germany the personal allowances available to German residents, where substantially all of the taxpayer's income was earned in Germany.

**Anti-Tax Avoidance Directive (ATAD I) -- Directive 2016/1164:** Adopted on July 12, 2016, ATAD I requires member states to implement five anti-avoidance measures: interest limitation rules, exit taxation, a general anti-abuse rule (GAAR), controlled foreign company (CFC) rules, and rules addressing hybrid mismatches.

**ATAD II -- Directive 2017/952:** Extended the hybrid mismatch rules to cover arrangements involving third countries.

### Pillar Two -- Global Minimum Tax

Council Directive (EU) 2022/2523, adopted on December 14, 2022, implements the OECD/G20 Inclusive Framework's Pillar Two rules within the EU. The directive establishes an effective minimum tax rate of 15 percent for multinational enterprise groups and large-scale domestic groups with combined annual revenue of at least EUR 750 million. Member states were required to transpose the Income Inclusion Rule (IIR) by December 31, 2023, and the Undertaxed Profits Rule (UTPR) by December 31, 2024.

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR has generally afforded states a wide margin of appreciation in tax matters under Protocol 1, Article 1 (protection of property). However, in **N.K.M. v. Hungary (Application No. 66529/11, 2013)**, the Court found that a 98 percent tax rate on severance payments constituted a disproportionate interference with the applicant's property rights, establishing that confiscatory tax rates may violate the Convention.

### EU State Aid and Tax Rulings

The European Commission has pursued several high-profile state aid investigations into preferential tax rulings granted by member states:

- **Apple/Ireland (Case SA.38373):** In 2016, the Commission found that Ireland granted illegal state aid to Apple through tax rulings, ordering recovery of EUR 13 billion. The CJEU upheld the Commission's decision in September 2024, reversing the General Court's earlier annulment.
- **Fiat/Luxembourg (Case T-755/15):** The General Court upheld the Commission's finding that Luxembourg granted selective tax advantages to Fiat Finance and Trade through a transfer pricing ruling.

## Comparative Analysis

### Taxation Basis: Residence vs. Citizenship

The most fundamental divergence among these jurisdictions concerns the basis for asserting taxing jurisdiction. The United States stands alone among major economies in taxing based on citizenship, requiring all U.S. citizens to file returns and pay tax on worldwide income regardless of where they live. South Korea and Germany follow the international norm of residence-based taxation, asserting unlimited tax liability only over individuals who are domiciled in or habitually resident within their territory. The EU constrains member state tax residence rules through free movement principles, preventing discriminatory treatment of non-resident EU nationals.

### Rate Progressivity

All three national jurisdictions employ progressive rate structures, but with different architectures. South Korea uses eight discrete brackets reaching 45 percent (plus 10 percent local surcharge). The United States uses seven brackets reaching 37 percent at the federal level, with potential combined rates exceeding 50 percent when state taxes are added. Germany employs a mathematically continuous formula with a top rate of 45 percent, providing smoother progressivity but greater computational complexity.

### Capital Gains Treatment

The jurisdictions diverge sharply on capital gains taxation. The United States offers preferential rates for long-term capital gains (held over one year) of 0, 15, or 20 percent, reflecting a policy choice to encourage investment. Germany subjects most capital gains from financial assets to a flat withholding tax (Abgeltungsteuer) of 25 percent plus solidarity surcharge. South Korea taxes capital gains from real estate and securities at varying rates, with particularly high rates on short-term and speculative real estate gains to address housing affordability concerns.

### Deduction and Credit Architecture

South Korea uniquely offers income deductions for credit card usage, incentivizing transparent transactions and reducing the shadow economy. The United States provides a binary choice between standard and itemized deductions, with the TCJA significantly increasing the standard deduction and limiting state and local tax (SALT) deductions to $10,000. Germany allows a broader range of business-related expense deductions but has a smaller basic personal allowance.
