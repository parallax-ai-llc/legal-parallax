## Summary

Healthcare systems -- the organizational structures through which societies finance, deliver, and regulate medical care -- represent one of the most significant areas of legal and policy divergence globally, with models ranging from single-payer national health services to market-based insurance systems, each reflecting fundamental choices about the role of government, the nature of healthcare as a right or commodity, and the allocation of finite medical resources. South Korea operates a compulsory single-insurer national health insurance (NHI) system under the National Health Insurance Act (국민건강보험법, Act No. 5854, 1999), administered by the National Health Insurance Service (국민건강보험공단, NHIS), covering the entire population of approximately 52 million through mandatory enrollment and income-based contributions, with the system supplemented by the Medical Aid Program (의료급여) for low-income individuals and the Long-Term Care Insurance Act (노인장기요양보험법, 2007) for elderly care, achieving universal coverage with one of the lowest per-capita healthcare expenditures among OECD nations while providing rapid access to care with minimal wait times. The United States is the only high-income country without universal healthcare coverage, operating a hybrid system combining employer-sponsored private insurance (covering approximately 155 million persons), government programs including Medicare (67 million beneficiaries), Medicaid and CHIP (approximately 92 million), the Affordable Care Act marketplace (approximately 21 million), and the Veterans Health Administration, with the Supreme Court having upheld the ACA's individual mandate as a tax in NFIB v. Sebelius (2012) while holding that Medicaid expansion cannot be coerced, and approximately 25 million persons remaining uninsured as of 2024. Germany operates the world's oldest universal healthcare system, established by Bismarck's Krankenversicherungsgesetz of 1883, now governed by Social Code Book V (SGB V), combining approximately 96 statutory health insurance funds (gesetzliche Krankenkassen) covering about 90 percent of the population with private health insurance (private Krankenversicherung) covering the remaining 10 percent, regulated by the Federal Joint Committee (Gemeinsamer Bundesausschuss, G-BA) and funded through income-based contributions split between employers and employees. The European framework encompasses diverse national models unified by the principle of universal coverage, with the ECHR's evolving jurisprudence addressing healthcare access under Articles 2 (right to life), 3 (prohibition of inhuman treatment), and 8 (private life), the EU's Directive 2011/24/EU on cross-border healthcare establishing patients' rights to seek treatment in other member states, and the European Social Charter Article 11 establishing the right to protection of health.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea achieved universal health coverage in just 12 years (1977-1989), one of the fastest expansions in healthcare history.

**National Health Insurance Act (국민건강보험법, 1999):** Consolidated previously fragmented insurance societies into a single national insurer:

- **Article 5 (Mandatory Coverage / 가입자):** All residents of Korea must be enrolled in the NHI system as either employed insured (직장가입자) or community insured (지역가입자)
- **Article 41 (Benefits / 요양급여):** The NHIS covers diagnosis, treatment, rehabilitation, hospitalization, nursing, prevention, and prescription drugs as determined by the Health Insurance Review and Assessment Service (건강보험심사평가원, HIRA)
- **Article 44 (Cost-Sharing / 본인부담):** Patients pay copayments of approximately 20-60% depending on the type of care and facility, with reduced cost-sharing for chronic diseases and catastrophic illness protection limiting annual out-of-pocket expenses

**National Health Insurance Service (국민건강보험공단, NHIS):** The NHIS is the sole insurer, collecting contributions, managing enrollment, and administering benefit payments. The system is funded by income-based contributions: employed persons pay approximately 7.09% of salary (split equally between employer and employee, 2024), while community-insured persons pay based on income, property, and other factors.

**Health Insurance Review and Assessment Service (건강보험심사평가원, HIRA):** An independent agency that reviews all healthcare claims for appropriateness and cost, sets fee schedules, and evaluates healthcare quality. HIRA's fee schedule authority effectively controls healthcare prices nationwide.

### Moon Jae-in Health Insurance Expansion (2017-2022)

President Moon Jae-in's administration implemented a major coverage expansion program (문재인 케어) aimed at reducing out-of-pocket costs:
- Extended NHI coverage to previously non-covered services including ultrasounds, MRIs, and dental care
- Reduced cost-sharing for catastrophic illness (upper limit on out-of-pocket payments reduced)
- Expanded coverage for traditional Korean medicine (한의학)
- By 2022, NHI coverage rate reached approximately 65% of total healthcare expenditure (up from 62% in 2017)

### Constitutional Court

**Constitutional Court Decision 2003Hun-Ba71 (2005):** The Court held that the mandatory nature of national health insurance, including compulsory contribution requirements, does not violate the right to property (Article 23) or freedom of contract, as the system serves the essential public interest of universal healthcare access under the state's health protection obligation (Article 36(3)).

### Challenges

Despite universal coverage and rapid access, South Korea faces challenges including: relatively high out-of-pocket costs compared to other universal systems, physician concentration in urban areas, rapidly aging population (projected to reach 40% elderly by 2050), and the lowest hospital bed occupancy rates among OECD countries alongside the highest number of hospital beds per capita.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States healthcare system combines multiple coverage pathways without achieving universal coverage.

**Medicare (Title XVIII, Social Security Act):** Universal health insurance for persons 65 and older and certain disabled persons, comprising:
- **Part A (Hospital Insurance):** Funded by payroll tax (2.9%, split employer/employee)
- **Part B (Medical Insurance):** Funded by premiums and general revenue
- **Part C (Medicare Advantage):** Private plan alternative
- **Part D (Prescription Drug):** Added by Medicare Modernization Act (2003)

**Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 (IRA):** Authorized Medicare to negotiate prices for certain high-cost prescription drugs for the first time, beginning with 10 drugs in 2026. The IRA also capped insulin costs at $35/month for Medicare beneficiaries and capped annual out-of-pocket drug spending at $2,000 (effective 2025).

**Medicaid (Title XIX, Social Security Act):** Joint federal-state program for low-income individuals. Following the ACA and NFIB v. Sebelius, 567 U.S. 519 (2012), Medicaid expansion to adults earning up to 138% of the federal poverty level is optional. As of 2025, 40 states and D.C. have expanded Medicaid.

**Affordable Care Act (ACA, 2010):** The most significant healthcare reform since Medicare/Medicaid:
- Individual health insurance marketplaces with income-based subsidies
- Essential health benefits requirements for all marketplace and small-group plans
- Prohibition on coverage denial or premium increase based on pre-existing conditions
- Individual mandate (penalty reduced to $0 by Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017)
- Employer mandate for firms with 50+ full-time employees

### Key Supreme Court Decisions

| Case | Year | Holding |
|------|------|---------|
| NFIB v. Sebelius | 2012 | ACA individual mandate upheld as tax; Medicaid expansion cannot be coerced |
| King v. Burwell | 2015 | ACA subsidies available on both federal and state exchanges |
| California v. Texas | 2021 | ACA challengers lacked standing after mandate penalty reduced to $0 |

### Uninsured Population

Despite the ACA's expansion of coverage, approximately 25 million persons remain uninsured (2024), concentrated among adults in non-expansion states, undocumented immigrants (excluded from ACA coverage), and low-income workers without employer-sponsored insurance. The uninsured rate varies dramatically by state, from approximately 3% (Massachusetts) to over 16% (Texas).

### Emergency Medical Treatment and Labor Act (EMTALA, 1986)

42 U.S.C. Section 1395dd requires hospitals participating in Medicare to provide emergency medical screening and stabilizing treatment regardless of insurance status or ability to pay. EMTALA represents the closest U.S. approximation to a universal right to healthcare, though it is limited to emergency conditions and does not cover ongoing or preventive care.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany operates the world's oldest universal healthcare system, with continuous evolution since 1883.

**SGB V (Statutory Health Insurance):** The comprehensive statute governing the gesetzliche Krankenversicherung (GKV), covering approximately 90% of the population:

- **Section 1 (Solidarity Principle / Solidaritätsprinzip):** The statutory health insurance system is based on the principles of solidarity and self-governance. Contributions are based on ability to pay (income-related), while benefits are based on medical need.
- **Section 5 (Mandatory Insurance / Versicherungspflicht):** Employed persons earning below the Versicherungspflichtgrenze (mandatory insurance threshold, EUR 69,300/year in 2024) are mandatorily enrolled in GKV. Persons earning above this threshold may opt for private health insurance (PKV).
- **Section 12 (Economic Efficiency / Wirtschaftlichkeitsgebot):** Healthcare services must be adequate, appropriate, and efficient. Services exceeding what is necessary are not covered.

**Federal Joint Committee (Gemeinsamer Bundesausschuss, G-BA):** The supreme decision-making body for coverage and quality in the GKV system, composed of representatives of statutory health insurance funds, hospital associations, and physician associations. The G-BA determines which services are covered, establishes quality standards, and issues directives on healthcare delivery.

### Statutory vs. Private Insurance

| Feature | Statutory (GKV) | Private (PKV) |
|---------|-----------------|---------------|
| Coverage | ~74 million persons (~90%) | ~8.7 million persons (~10%) |
| Contributions | Income-based (~14.6% + supplementary, split employer/employee) | Risk-based premiums (age, health status) |
| Benefits | Standardized by G-BA | Individualized by contract |
| Physician Access | Standard appointment availability | Often preferential access |
| Return to GKV | Difficult for persons over 55 | As above |

### Recent Reforms

**Hospital Reform Act (Krankenhausreformgesetz, 2024):** Germany's most significant hospital reform in decades, transitioning hospital financing from a diagnosis-related group (DRG) system to a hybrid model incorporating base payments for maintaining hospital capacity (Vorhaltepauschalen). The reform aims to address hospital overcapacity (approximately 1,900 hospitals nationally) while ensuring access in rural areas.

**Digital Health Applications (Digitale Gesundheitsanwendungen, DiGA):** Germany became the first country to establish a regulatory pathway for prescription digital health applications under the Digital Healthcare Act (Digitale-Versorgung-Gesetz, 2019), with the Federal Institute for Drugs and Medical Devices (BfArM) certifying DiGA for reimbursement through the GKV system.

### Federal Constitutional Court

**BVerfGE 115, 25 (2005) -- Nikolaus Decision:** The Court held that Article 2(2) GG (right to physical integrity) in conjunction with the social state principle (Article 20(1) GG) requires statutory health insurance to cover treatments for life-threatening illnesses even when no conventionally approved treatment exists, if there is a non-negligible chance of therapeutic benefit. This decision established a constitutional floor for healthcare coverage in life-threatening situations.

## ECHR / EU

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Positive Obligations Under Articles 2 and 3:**

**Cyprus v. Turkey (Application No. 25781/94, Grand Chamber, May 10, 2001):** The Grand Chamber held that Article 2 (right to life) imposes positive obligations on states to take appropriate steps to safeguard the lives of those within their jurisdiction, including the provision of medical care.

**N. v. United Kingdom (Application No. 26565/05, Grand Chamber, May 27, 2008):** The Grand Chamber held that the deportation of an HIV-positive individual to Uganda, where medical treatment was less available, did not violate Article 3 unless the applicant was "critically ill" and removal would result in serious, rapid, and irreversible decline. This established a high threshold for Article 3 claims based on healthcare access.

**Paposhvili v. Belgium (Application No. 41738/10, Grand Chamber, December 13, 2016):** The Grand Chamber significantly lowered the N. v. UK threshold, holding that Article 3 may be engaged where substantial grounds exist for believing that removal would expose a seriously ill person to "a serious, rapid and irreversible decline in his or her state of health resulting in intense suffering or to a significant reduction in life expectancy." States must assess the availability and accessibility of treatment in the receiving country.

### EU Cross-Border Healthcare Directive 2011/24/EU

This Directive codifies and clarifies patients' rights to seek healthcare in other EU member states:

- **Article 7:** Patients are entitled to reimbursement for cross-border healthcare up to the amount that would have been covered in their home member state
- **Article 8:** Prior authorization may be required for hospital care and other specified treatments
- **Article 4:** The member state of treatment must ensure healthcare is provided in accordance with its quality and safety standards
- **Article 6:** National contact points must provide patients with information about healthcare providers, quality standards, and complaint procedures

### European Health Union

Following the COVID-19 pandemic, the EU has strengthened its health governance through:
- **Regulation (EU) 2022/2371:** Serious cross-border health threats (replacing Decision 1082/2013)
- **Regulation (EU) 2022/123:** Extended European Medicines Agency mandate
- **European Health Data Space (EHDS) Regulation (proposed 2022):** Would establish a framework for cross-border sharing of electronic health data

### Social Charter

**Article 11 (Right to Protection of Health):** Requires states parties to take appropriate measures to remove the causes of ill-health, provide advisory and educational facilities for health promotion, and prevent epidemic, endemic, and other diseases.

## Comparative Analysis

### System Architecture

The four jurisdictions represent three distinct healthcare models: South Korea's single-insurer system (related to the Beveridge model but with pluralistic delivery), the United States' fragmented multi-payer system, and Germany's social insurance model (the original Bismarck model). The EU encompasses all models, with the UK's NHS (Beveridge), Germany (Bismarck), and various hybrid systems coexisting under the umbrella of universal coverage.

### Cost and Efficiency

The United States spends approximately $12,500 per capita on healthcare (2023 PPP) -- roughly double the average of other high-income countries -- yet achieves worse population health outcomes on many metrics. South Korea achieves comparable or better outcomes at approximately $3,900 per capita. Germany spends approximately $7,400 per capita with generally excellent outcomes. This spending divergence reflects differences in administrative costs, drug pricing, physician compensation, and the fragmentation of the U.S. system.

### Access and Equity

South Korea and Germany achieve universal coverage with minimal uninsured populations. The EU framework aims for universal coverage through diverse national systems. The United States is the only high-income country with a significant uninsured population (~25 million), creating disparities in access strongly correlated with income, race, and geography.

### Constitutional Protection

Germany provides the strongest constitutional protection for healthcare access through the Nikolaus decision. South Korea's Article 36(3) creates a state obligation to protect health. The ECHR provides minimum standards through positive obligations under Articles 2 and 3. The United States has no constitutional right to healthcare.
