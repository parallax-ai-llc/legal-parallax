## Summary

Price-fixing -- agreements among competitors to set prices, rig bids, or allocate markets -- is universally condemned as the most harmful form of anticompetitive conduct, yet the enforcement tools, penalty structures, and procedural mechanisms for combating cartels differ significantly across jurisdictions. South Korea prohibits price-fixing under Article 40 of the Monopoly Regulation and Fair Trade Act (MRFTA), enforced by the Korea Fair Trade Commission (KFTC) with administrative surcharges of up to 10% of relevant turnover and criminal penalties of up to 3 years imprisonment for cartel participants, while operating a leniency program that grants full immunity to the first informant. The United States treats price-fixing as a per se criminal violation of Section 1 of the Sherman Act (15 U.S.C. § 1), prosecuted by the DOJ Antitrust Division with penalties of up to 10 years imprisonment for individuals and $100 million (or twice the gross gain/loss) for corporations, alongside private treble-damages actions under Section 4 of the Clayton Act. Germany criminalizes bid rigging under StGB § 298 (up to 5 years imprisonment) and imposes administrative fines of up to 10% of worldwide group turnover through the Bundeskartellamt under the GWB, while also allowing disgorgement of cartel profits. At the EU level, the European Commission enforces Article 101 TFEU through Regulation 1/2003, imposing fines of up to 10% of worldwide annual turnover, while the ECN+ Directive has harmonized leniency programs and enforcement powers across all Member States, and the ECHR has confirmed that cartel proceedings engage Article 6 fair-trial protections.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

**MRFTA Article 40 (Prohibition of Unfair Collaborative Acts):** The MRFTA prohibits "unfair collaborative acts" (부당한 공동행위) among competitors, defined as agreements to:

1. Fix, maintain, or change prices.
2. Determine conditions for the transaction of goods or services.
3. Restrict the production, delivery, or supply of goods or services.
4. Restrict the territory of activity or trading partners.
5. Restrict the installation or expansion of facilities necessary for production.
6. Restrict the types or specifications of goods or services.
7. Jointly manage or establish a company.
8. Interfere with each other's business activities.

**Presumption of Agreement (Art. 40(5)):** Where conduct by two or more entrepreneurs appears substantially identical and cannot be explained by independent business judgment, the MRFTA presumes the existence of an agreement. This "conscious parallelism plus" presumption shifts the burden to the defendants to demonstrate independent decision-making.

### KFTC Enforcement

The Korea Fair Trade Commission (KFTC) serves as the primary enforcement body for cartel cases:

**Investigation Powers:** The KFTC may conduct dawn raids (현장조사), compel the production of documents and data, summon and question witnesses, and seize evidence. KFTC investigators are authorized to enter business premises during operating hours with prior notice (or without notice in urgent cases).

**Administrative Surcharges (과징금):**
- **Base calculation:** Up to 10% of the relevant turnover attributable to the infringement. The KFTC calculates surcharges based on the severity of the violation, the duration, the degree of market impact, and mitigating or aggravating factors.
- **Minimum surcharges:** The KFTC guidelines establish minimum surcharge rates for hardcore restrictions: 7% for price fixing; 5% for output restriction; 5% for market division.
- **Where turnover is indeterminable:** Fixed surcharges of up to 2 billion KRW.

**Corrective Orders:** The KFTC may order cessation of the anticompetitive conduct, publication of the violation, and other corrective measures.

### Criminal Referral

The KFTC holds exclusive authority to refer cartel cases for criminal prosecution. Private individuals and the public prosecutor cannot independently initiate criminal antitrust proceedings:

- **Referral threshold:** The KFTC refers cases involving particularly egregious conduct, significant market impact, or repeat offenders.
- **Criminal penalties:** Imprisonment of up to 3 years or fines of up to 200 million KRW for individuals who participated in cartel agreements.
- **Dual-track enforcement:** The KFTC may impose administrative surcharges and simultaneously refer the case for criminal prosecution.

### Leniency Program

The KFTC operates a leniency program modeled on international best practices:

**First informant:** Full immunity from administrative surcharges and exemption from criminal referral.

**Second informant:** Reduction of up to 50% of surcharges and potential exemption from criminal referral.

**Third and subsequent informants:** Reduction of up to 30% of surcharges.

**Conditions for leniency:**
1. Provide evidence sufficient to prove the cartel violation.
2. Cooperate fully and continuously with the KFTC investigation.
3. Cease participation in the cartel immediately upon filing the leniency application.
4. Not have coerced other participants into joining the cartel.

### Landmark Cases

**Flour Cartel (2012):** The KFTC imposed surcharges totaling 88.2 billion KRW on four major flour manufacturers (CJ CheilJedang, Daehan Flour Mills, Dongah Flour Mills, and Young Nam Flour Mills) for colluding to fix flour prices from 2006 to 2012.

**Military Fuel Supply Cartel (2013):** The KFTC fined six oil companies 434.8 billion KRW for rigging bids in military fuel procurement contracts over a 10-year period, one of the largest cartel fines in Korean history.

**Display Panel Cartel (2011):** The KFTC imposed surcharges on six LCD panel manufacturers -- including Samsung SDI, LG Display, AU Optronics, Chunghwa Picture Tubes, Chi Mei, and HannStar -- for fixing prices of TFT-LCD panels used in televisions, monitors, and notebooks. Total fines exceeded 194 billion KRW.

**Insurance Premium Cartel (2021):** The KFTC fined 12 non-life insurance companies for colluding to set automobile insurance premium rates, imposing total surcharges of 86.4 billion KRW.

## United States

### Per Se Criminal Prohibition

The United States uniquely treats price-fixing as a per se illegal criminal offense, reflecting the view that cartel conduct is inherently and irremediably harmful:

**Sherman Act Section 1 (15 U.S.C. § 1):** "Every contract, combination in the form of trust or otherwise, or conspiracy, in restraint of trade or commerce among the several States, or with foreign nations, is declared to be illegal."

**Per se rule:** Under the Supreme Court's per se doctrine, horizontal price-fixing agreements are conclusively presumed unreasonable. No inquiry into market power, competitive effects, or business justification is permitted. The per se rule applies to:
- Price fixing (United States v. Socony-Vacuum Oil Co., 310 U.S. 150 (1940))
- Bid rigging
- Market allocation (United States v. Topco Associates, Inc., 405 U.S. 596 (1972))
- Output restriction

**Criminal penalties (as amended by the Antitrust Criminal Penalty Enhancement and Reform Act of 2004):**
- Corporations: Up to $100 million or twice the gross gain/loss from the conspiracy.
- Individuals: Up to $1 million or twice the gross gain/loss, plus imprisonment of up to 10 years.
- In practice, corporate fines regularly exceed the statutory maximum through the alternative fine provision (2x gross gain/loss).

### DOJ Antitrust Division Criminal Enforcement

The DOJ Antitrust Division is the exclusive federal criminal enforcer of the antitrust laws:

**Investigation tools:**
- Grand jury subpoenas
- FBI search warrants
- Court-authorized wiretaps (relatively rare but used in major investigations)
- International cooperation through mutual legal assistance treaties (MLATs)

**Sentencing Guidelines:** The U.S. Sentencing Guidelines (USSG § 2R1.1) provide a framework for cartel sentencing based on the volume of commerce affected:
- Base offense level: 12 (for antitrust offenses)
- Specific offense characteristic: add 1 level for each multiple of the volume of commerce tables
- Bid rigging: add 1 additional level
- The volume of commerce affected is used as a proxy for the seriousness of the offense.

**Average sentences:** In recent years, the average prison sentence for cartel offenses has been approximately 24 months, though sentences exceeding 5 years have been imposed in major cases.

### DOJ Amnesty (Leniency) Program

The DOJ's Corporate Leniency Policy (1993) and Individual Leniency Policy (1994) are considered the most successful cartel detection tools globally:

**Corporate amnesty requirements:**
1. At the time of the application, the Division has not received information about the activity from any other source.
2. The corporation promptly took effective action to terminate its participation in the activity upon discovery.
3. The corporation reports the criminal activity with candor and completeness and provides full cooperation.
4. The confession is a corporate act, not isolated confessions by individuals.
5. Where possible, the corporation makes restitution to injured parties.
6. The corporation did not coerce another party to participate in the illegal activity and was not the leader or originator.

**Amnesty Plus:** A company already under investigation for one cartel that reports its involvement in a separate cartel may receive amnesty for the second cartel and a reduction in the fine for the first.

### Private Enforcement

**Clayton Act Section 4 (15 U.S.C. § 15):** "Any person who shall be injured in his business or property by reason of anything forbidden in the antitrust laws may sue therefor in any district court of the United States... and shall recover threefold the damages by him sustained, and the cost of suit, including a reasonable attorney's fee."

Key features of private cartel enforcement:
- **Treble damages:** Plaintiffs recover three times their actual damages.
- **Class actions:** Indirect purchasers may bring class actions under state antitrust laws (Illinois Brick indirect purchaser doctrine limits federal direct purchaser standing).
- **Discovery:** Broad U.S. discovery rules enable extensive document production and depositions.
- **Joint and several liability:** Each cartel participant is jointly and severally liable for the entire treble damages amount.

### Landmark Cases

**United States v. Socony-Vacuum Oil Co., 310 U.S. 150 (1940):** The Supreme Court established the per se rule for horizontal price-fixing, holding that "a combination formed for the purpose and with the effect of raising, depressing, fixing, pegging, or stabilizing the price of a commodity in interstate or foreign commerce is illegal per se."

**United States v. AU Optronics Corp., 785 F.3d 459 (9th Cir. 2015):** The Ninth Circuit upheld criminal convictions and $500 million fine against AU Optronics for participating in the global LCD panel price-fixing conspiracy.

**Air Cargo Cartel (2006-2015):** The DOJ prosecuted 22 airlines for fixing air cargo fuel and security surcharges, resulting in over $1.8 billion in criminal fines. British Airways paid $300 million; Korean Air paid $300 million; Air France-KLM paid $350 million.

**Auto Parts Cartel (2011-2020):** The largest cartel investigation in DOJ history targeted price-fixing and bid-rigging in the automotive parts industry. Over 60 companies and 100 individuals were charged; total fines exceeded $2.9 billion; 48 individuals were sentenced to prison.

**Broiler Chicken Antitrust Litigation (2016-ongoing):** One of the largest food industry price-fixing cases, alleging that major poultry producers conspired to fix broiler chicken prices. Settlements have exceeded $300 million in civil class action proceedings.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany addresses price-fixing through both administrative (GWB) and criminal (StGB) provisions:

**GWB § 1 (Prohibition of Anticompetitive Agreements):** "Agreements between undertakings, decisions by associations of undertakings and concerted practices which have as their object or effect the prevention, restriction or distortion of competition shall be prohibited." This provision mirrors Article 101(1) TFEU and is interpreted consistently with EU law.

**GWB § 2 (Exemptions):** Agreements that contribute to improving the production or distribution of goods or to promoting technical or economic progress may be exempt if they allow consumers a fair share of the resulting benefit -- mirroring Article 101(3) TFEU. However, this exemption is virtually never applied to hardcore restrictions such as price-fixing.

**StGB § 298 (Anticompetitive Agreements in Procurement Procedures):** "(1) Whoever, in connection with a call for tenders for goods or services, makes an offer that is based on an unlawful agreement aimed at causing the organizer of the call to accept a particular offer shall be punished with imprisonment for not more than five years or a fine." This is the only criminal provision specifically targeting cartel-type conduct.

### Bundeskartellamt Enforcement

**Investigation powers:** The Bundeskartellamt (Federal Cartel Office) possesses extensive investigation tools:
- Dawn raids (Durchsuchungen) authorized by court order
- Requests for information under GWB § 59
- Seizure of documents and digital evidence
- Interviews and oral hearings

**Fines:** The Bundeskartellamt may impose fines of up to 10% of worldwide group turnover in the preceding business year. Unlike many jurisdictions, Germany also permits disgorgement (Abschöpfung) of the economic benefit obtained through the cartel, which may be imposed in addition to the fine.

**Parental liability:** Under the 2017 amendments to the GWB (9th amendment), the Bundeskartellamt may impose fines directly on the parent company of a cartel participant, based on the economic unit doctrine (wirtschaftliche Einheit). This resolved a longstanding enforcement gap where corporate restructuring allowed cartel participants to evade fines.

### Leniency Program

The Bundeskartellamt operates a leniency program (Bonusregelung) closely aligned with the ECN Model Leniency Programme:

**Full immunity (first applicant):**
- Provides evidence that enables the Bundeskartellamt to obtain a search warrant; or
- Provides evidence sufficient to establish the infringement, where the authority does not yet have sufficient evidence.
- Cooperates fully and continuously.
- Has not coerced others into participating.

**Reduced fines:**
- Second applicant: up to 50% reduction.
- Third and subsequent applicants: reduction at the authority's discretion.

**Marker system:** An applicant may reserve its place in the leniency queue by filing a marker, which holds the applicant's position while it gathers additional evidence.

**Interaction with criminal law:** A critical limitation of the German system is that leniency from Bundeskartellamt fines does not protect against criminal prosecution under StGB § 298 for bid rigging. The Bundeskartellamt cannot grant immunity from criminal prosecution, as this lies within the competence of state prosecutors.

### Private Enforcement (GWB §§ 33-33h)

Germany has significantly strengthened private cartel enforcement in recent years:

**GWB § 33a (Liability for Damages):** "Whoever intentionally or negligently violates a provision of this Part, Article 101 or 102 of the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union, or a ruling by the cartel authority shall be liable to compensate the damage caused thereby to the person affected."

**GWB § 33a(2) (Presumption of Harm):** "Where an infringement of competition law involves a cartel, it shall be presumed that the cartel caused harm." This rebuttable presumption, transposing the EU Damages Directive, significantly eases the evidentiary burden on plaintiffs.

**GWB § 33c (Passing-on Defense):** Allows defendants to argue that the overcharge was passed on by the direct purchaser to indirect purchasers, reducing the direct purchaser's claim. However, the defendant bears the burden of proving pass-on.

**GWB § 33g (Disclosure of Evidence):** Courts may order parties to proceedings or third parties to disclose evidence relevant to the claim, subject to proportionality.

### Landmark Cases

**Cement Cartel (2003):** The Bundeskartellamt imposed fines totaling EUR 660 million on six cement companies for operating a nationwide market-sharing and price-fixing cartel -- at the time the largest cartel fine in German history.

**Sausage Casing Cartel (2014):** The Bundeskartellamt fined 21 companies totaling EUR 338 million for price-fixing and market allocation in the sausage casing industry.

**Steel Cartel (2018):** The Bundeskartellamt imposed fines of EUR 646 million on rail steel producers for a long-running price-fixing and customer-allocation cartel affecting Deutsche Bahn and other rail operators.

**Sugar Cartel (2014):** The BGH upheld Bundeskartellamt fines totaling EUR 280 million against three major sugar producers (Südzucker, Nordzucker, and Pfeifer & Langen) for price-fixing.

## ECHR/EU Framework

### Article 101 TFEU

**Article 101(1):** "The following shall be prohibited as incompatible with the internal market: all agreements between undertakings, decisions by associations of undertakings and concerted practices which may affect trade between Member States and which have as their object or effect the prevention, restriction or distortion of competition within the internal market, and in particular those which:
(a) directly or indirectly fix purchase or selling prices or any other trading conditions;
(b) limit or control production, markets, technical development, or investment;
(c) share markets or sources of supply;
(d) apply dissimilar conditions to equivalent transactions with other trading parties, thereby placing them at a competitive disadvantage;
(e) make the conclusion of contracts subject to acceptance by the other parties of supplementary obligations which, by their nature or according to commercial usage, have no connection with the subject of such contracts."

**Restriction by Object:** The Court of Justice has consistently held that price-fixing, bid rigging, market sharing, and output limitation constitute restrictions by object, which are presumed harmful without the need for market analysis. In Expedia Inc. v. Autorité de la concurrence, Case C-226/11 (2012), the Court confirmed that agreements restricting competition by object are always capable of being caught by Article 101(1), regardless of the parties' market shares.

### European Commission Enforcement

**Regulation 1/2003:** The European Commission enforces Article 101 through:

**Investigation:** The Commission may conduct unannounced inspections (dawn raids) at business premises (Art. 20), request information (Art. 18), and interview individuals (Art. 19). Obstruction of an inspection may result in periodic penalty payments of up to 5% of average daily turnover.

**Fines (Art. 23):** The Commission may impose fines of up to 10% of the total worldwide annual turnover of the undertaking's group in the preceding business year.

**2006 Fining Guidelines:** The Commission calculates fines based on:
- **Value of sales:** Percentage of the value of sales of goods/services to which the infringement relates.
- **Gravity:** 0-30% of the value of sales (hardcore restrictions such as price-fixing typically attract rates of 15-25%).
- **Duration:** Multiplied by the number of years of participation.
- **Additional amount ("entry fee"):** 15-25% of the value of sales added for hardcore restrictions.
- **Adjustments:** Aggravating factors (ringleader, repeat offender, obstruction) and mitigating factors (limited participation, cooperation, compliance program).

### European Commission Leniency Notice (2006)

**Full immunity (first applicant):**
- Provides evidence enabling the Commission to carry out a targeted inspection; or
- Provides evidence sufficient to find an infringement of Article 101.
- Cooperates fully, continuously, and expeditiously.
- Ended participation in the infringement no later than the time of its application.
- Did not take steps to coerce other undertakings to participate.

**Reduction of fines:**
- First undertaking providing significant added value: 30-50% reduction.
- Second undertaking: 20-30% reduction.
- Subsequent undertakings: up to 20% reduction.

**Cartel settlement (2008 Settlement Notice):** Undertakings may acknowledge participation in a cartel and receive a 10% reduction in their fine, streamlining the proceedings.

### ECN+ Directive (Directive (EU) 2019/1)

The ECN+ Directive harmonizes national competition authorities' enforcement powers across the EU:

- **Minimum investigative powers:** Dawn raids, information requests, interviews.
- **Minimum fining powers:** National authorities must be able to impose fines of at least 10% of worldwide annual turnover.
- **Harmonized leniency:** National leniency programs must be compatible with the ECN Model Leniency Programme.
- **Mutual assistance:** National authorities must assist each other in conducting dawn raids and enforcing fines across borders.

### Damages Directive (2014/104/EU)

The Damages Directive harmonizes private enforcement of competition law across the EU:

- **Right to full compensation (Art. 3):** Any natural or legal person who has suffered harm caused by a competition law infringement can claim and obtain full compensation.
- **Presumption of harm (Art. 17(2)):** It is presumed that cartel infringements cause harm; the defendant may rebut this presumption.
- **Effect of national decisions (Art. 9):** A final decision by a national competition authority constitutes proof of the infringement in subsequent damages actions.
- **Passing-on (Arts. 12-16):** Both direct and indirect purchasers may claim compensation; defendants may raise a passing-on defense.
- **Limitation period (Art. 10):** At least 5 years from when the infringement ceased and the claimant knew or should have known of the infringement, the harm, and the identity of the infringer.
- **Disclosure (Art. 5):** Courts may order disclosure of evidence held by the opposing party or third parties, subject to proportionality. Leniency statements and settlement submissions are absolutely protected from disclosure.

### ECHR and Cartel Proceedings

**Menarini Diagnostics S.R.L. v. Italy, App. No. 43509/08 (2011):** The landmark ECHR judgment confirming that competition law fines constitute "criminal charges" for the purposes of Article 6 ECHR. The Court applied the three Engel criteria: (1) the domestic classification of the offense; (2) the nature of the offense; and (3) the severity of the potential penalty. While the offense was classified as administrative under Italian law, its punitive nature and the severity of the potential fine (EUR 6 million) rendered it criminal for Convention purposes.

**Jussila v. Finland, App. No. 73053/01 (2006):** The Grand Chamber established that while Article 6 criminal guarantees apply to regulatory proceedings, the full panoply of safeguards may be "modulated" for offenses that do not belong to the "hard core" of criminal law -- permitting administrative adjudication with subsequent full judicial review.

**A. Menarini/Jussila framework:** Together, these decisions establish that cartel proceedings must respect:
- The right to a fair and public hearing by an independent tribunal (Art. 6(1)).
- The presumption of innocence (Art. 6(2)).
- The right against self-incrimination (tension with competition authorities' information-gathering powers).
- Full judicial review of both the factual and legal basis of the decision, including the proportionality of the fine.

## Comparative Analysis

### Criminal vs. Administrative Enforcement

The fundamental divergence in cartel enforcement concerns whether price-fixing is treated as a crime:

| Jurisdiction | Criminal Prosecution Available | Imprisonment for Price-Fixing |
|-------------|-------------------------------|------------------------------|
| **United States** | Yes -- per se felony (Sherman Act § 1) | Up to 10 years |
| **South Korea** | Yes -- upon KFTC criminal referral | Up to 3 years |
| **Germany** | Only for bid rigging (StGB § 298) | Up to 5 years (bid rigging only) |
| **EU** | No EU-level criminal enforcement | N/A (left to Member States) |

The United States is the most aggressive criminal enforcer, treating all horizontal price-fixing as a per se felony. South Korea permits criminal prosecution but only through the KFTC's exclusive referral mechanism. Germany criminalizes only bid rigging, not general price-fixing. The EU has no criminal enforcement authority, though the ECN+ Directive has not prohibited Member States from criminalizing competition law violations.

### Leniency Program Design

All four jurisdictions operate leniency programs, reflecting the global consensus that insider cooperation is essential for cartel detection:

- **Convergence:** Full immunity for the first applicant; reduced penalties for subsequent applicants; requirement of full cooperation and cessation of participation.
- **Divergence:** The U.S. amnesty program uniquely provides protection from criminal prosecution (including imprisonment). Germany's leniency program cannot protect against criminal prosecution under StGB § 298. The KFTC's program can exempt from criminal referral.

### Private Enforcement Effectiveness

| Jurisdiction | Treble/Multiple Damages | Class Actions Available | Presumption of Harm |
|-------------|------------------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| **United States** | Yes -- 3x actual damages | Yes (direct purchasers federally; indirect under state law) | No statutory presumption |
| **South Korea** | No | Limited class action mechanism | No statutory presumption |
| **Germany** | No | Model declaratory proceedings (Musterfeststellungsklage) | Yes (GWB § 33a(2)) |
| **EU** | No | Varies by Member State | Yes (Damages Directive Art. 17(2)) |

The U.S. treble damages provision, combined with class action availability and broad discovery, creates the most powerful private enforcement regime. The EU Damages Directive has significantly strengthened European private enforcement, particularly through the presumption of harm and harmonized disclosure rules, but European private enforcement still lags behind the U.S. in volume and monetary recovery.

### Deterrence Comparison

Effective cartel deterrence depends on the combination of:
1. **Probability of detection:** Enhanced by leniency programs in all jurisdictions.
2. **Severity of sanctions:** The U.S. combines criminal imprisonment with treble private damages, creating the highest total potential cost. EU fines are larger in absolute terms (10% worldwide turnover), but the absence of imprisonment and multiple damages reduces the total deterrent effect.
3. **Speed of enforcement:** Average investigation-to-decision timelines vary: approximately 2-3 years (KFTC), 3-5 years (DOJ criminal), 3-7 years (European Commission), 2-4 years (Bundeskartellamt).
