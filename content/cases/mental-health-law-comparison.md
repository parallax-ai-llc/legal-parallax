## Summary

Mental health legislation (정신건강법) -- the body of law governing the treatment, care, rights, and involuntary detention of persons with mental disorders -- represents one of the most ethically complex and constitutionally sensitive areas of health law across all jurisdictions. South Korea regulates mental health primarily through the Act on the Improvement of Mental Health and the Support for Welfare Services for Mental Patients (정신건강증진 및 정신질환자 복지서비스 지원에 관한 법률), enacted in 2016 as a comprehensive reform of the prior Mental Health Act (정신보건법, 1995), with the Constitutional Court of Korea (헌법재판소) having declared portions of the prior involuntary commitment framework unconstitutional in its landmark 2016 decision (2014Hun-Ga9); the reformed law significantly restricts involuntary hospitalization, requiring two independent psychiatric evaluations and periodic judicial review. The United States addresses mental health through a patchwork of federal and state legislation, with the foundational constitutional framework established in O'Connor v. Donaldson, 422 U.S. 563 (1975), which held that a state cannot constitutionally confine a non-dangerous individual who is capable of surviving safely in freedom, supplemented by the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA, 42 U.S.C. Section 12101 et seq.), the Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act (MHPAEA, 29 U.S.C. Section 1185a), and individual state civil commitment statutes requiring proof of dangerousness or grave disability. Germany regulates mental health through a dual framework: the federal Betreuungsrecht (guardianship law) in the Buergerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB, Sections 1896-1908i) governing legal capacity and surrogate decision-making, and the state-level Psychisch-Kranken-Gesetze (PsychKG, psychologically ill persons acts) governing involuntary commitment and compulsory treatment, with the Bundesverfassungsgericht (Federal Constitutional Court) having established stringent requirements for involuntary treatment in its landmark 2011 decisions (2 BvR 882/09 and 2 BvR 633/11). The European Court of Human Rights (ECtHR) has developed extensive Article 5(1)(e) jurisprudence on the detention of persons of unsound mind, beginning with Winterwerp v. the Netherlands (Application No. 6301/73, 1979), while the EU has addressed mental health through the European Disability Strategy 2021-2030 and the obligations flowing from the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD), ratified by the EU in 2010.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea underwent a fundamental transformation of its mental health legislation in the mid-2010s, driven by constitutional litigation and human rights advocacy.

**Mental Health Welfare Act (정신건강증진 및 정신질환자 복지서비스 지원에 관한 법률, 2016):** This Act replaced the former Mental Health Act (정신보건법) following the Constitutional Court's determination that the prior law's involuntary hospitalization provisions were unconstitutional. The reformed Act, effective May 30, 2017, restructured the entire admission framework and expanded community-based mental health services.

**Constitutional Court Decision (2016, 2014Hun-Ga9):** The Constitutional Court held that the prior law's provision allowing indefinite involuntary hospitalization based solely on the consent of a family member and the opinion of a single psychiatrist violated the constitutional rights to personal liberty (Article 12) and the right to bodily integrity (Article 10). This decision catalyzed the comprehensive legislative reform.

### Involuntary Hospitalization

The reformed framework establishes three categories of psychiatric admission:

| Type | Requirements | Duration |
|------|-------------|----------|
| Voluntary Admission (자의입원) | Patient consent; patient may request discharge at any time | No limit; patient controls |
| Consent-Based Admission (동의입원) | Patient and guardian consent | Renewable every 2 months |
| Involuntary Admission (행정입원/보호입원) | Two independent psychiatric evaluations confirming mental illness and danger; government-appointed psychiatrists | Initial 2 weeks; renewable up to 3 months with review |

**Protective Hospitalization (보호입원, Article 43):** Requires a request by a legal guardian, confirmation by two psychiatrists from different medical institutions (one designated by the local government), and documented evidence that the patient poses a risk to self or others, or is incapable of self-care due to the mental illness. The patient must be reviewed by the Mental Health Review Board (정신건강심사위원회) within 3 days.

**Administrative Hospitalization (행정입원, Article 44):** Permits involuntary admission initiated by local government officials when a person with a suspected mental illness is found to pose an imminent risk in a public setting. Requires psychiatric evaluation within 24 hours and judicial review within 3 days.

### Mental Health Review Boards (정신건강심사위원회)

Established at the municipal and provincial levels, these boards conduct periodic reviews of all involuntary admissions, evaluate discharge requests, and investigate complaints. Board composition includes psychiatrists, legal professionals, persons with lived experience of mental illness, and community representatives.

### Community Mental Health Services

The Act significantly expanded community-based services, establishing community mental health centers (정신건강복지센터) in every municipality. These centers provide case management, crisis intervention, rehabilitation programs, and supported employment. The shift from institutional to community care reflects Korea's alignment with international deinstitutionalization trends, though implementation challenges persist, with Korea's psychiatric bed rate per capita remaining among the highest in OECD nations.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States addresses mental health through an interplay of constitutional due process protections, federal civil rights legislation, and state-level civil commitment statutes.

**Constitutional Framework:** The Supreme Court has established key constitutional boundaries for civil commitment. In O'Connor v. Donaldson, 422 U.S. 563 (1975), the Court held that a finding of mental illness alone cannot justify a state's indefinite involuntary commitment of a non-dangerous individual who is capable of surviving safely in freedom. Addington v. Texas, 441 U.S. 418 (1979), established that the standard of proof for civil commitment must be at least "clear and convincing evidence," higher than the civil preponderance standard. In Olmstead v. L.C., 527 U.S. 581 (1999), the Court held that unjustified institutional isolation of persons with mental disabilities constitutes discrimination under Title II of the ADA.

**Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA, 42 U.S.C. Section 12101 et seq.):** The ADA prohibits discrimination on the basis of disability in employment, public services, and public accommodations. Mental health conditions are covered disabilities. The Olmstead decision established the "integration mandate," requiring states to provide community-based services to persons with mental disabilities when appropriate.

### Civil Commitment

Civil commitment is governed primarily by state law, though federal constitutional requirements set a floor. Common elements across states:

- **Substantive Criteria:** Most states require proof that the individual has a mental illness and is (a) dangerous to self, (b) dangerous to others, or (c) gravely disabled (unable to provide for basic needs). Some states require an overt act or recent threat as evidence of dangerousness.
- **Procedural Protections:** Right to counsel (often appointed), right to a hearing before a judicial officer, right to present evidence and cross-examine witnesses, clear and convincing evidence standard (per Addington), and right to periodic review.
- **Least Restrictive Alternative:** Most states require consideration of less restrictive alternatives to inpatient commitment.

### Outpatient Civil Commitment (Assisted Outpatient Treatment)

Following the enactment of Kendra's Law in New York (N.Y. Mental Hygiene Law Section 9.60, 1999), 47 states and the District of Columbia now authorize some form of outpatient civil commitment or assisted outpatient treatment (AOT). These laws permit courts to order individuals with serious mental illness to comply with treatment plans in the community, typically when they have a history of treatment non-compliance and hospitalizations. The criteria are often less stringent than for inpatient commitment, focusing on deterioration and need for treatment rather than imminent dangerousness.

### Mental Health Parity

The Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act of 2008 (MHPAEA, 29 U.S.C. Section 1185a) requires that financial requirements (such as copays and deductibles) and treatment limitations applicable to mental health or substance use disorder benefits be no more restrictive than the predominant requirements or limitations applied to substantially all medical and surgical benefits. The Consolidated Appropriations Act of 2021 strengthened enforcement by requiring group health plans to perform and document comparative analyses of their nonquantitative treatment limitations.

### Forensic Mental Health

The intersection of mental health and criminal law raises distinct issues, including competency to stand trial (Dusky v. United States, 362 U.S. 402, 1960), the insanity defense (with standards varying by state from M'Naghten to the Model Penal Code's substantial capacity test), and the involuntary commitment of persons found not guilty by reason of insanity (Jones v. United States, 463 U.S. 354, 1983).

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's mental health legal framework operates through a distinctive dual structure of federal and state legislation.

**Psychisch-Kranken-Gesetze (PsychKG):** Each of Germany's 16 Laender (states) has enacted its own PsychKG governing the involuntary commitment and treatment of persons with mental illness. While the specific provisions vary by state, all PsychKG statutes require that the person have a mental illness, that there be a substantial danger to self or others that cannot be averted by less restrictive means, and that a court authorize the detention, typically within 24 hours of the initial emergency admission.

**Betreuungsrecht (Guardianship Law, BGB Sections 1896-1908i):** The federal Buergerliches Gesetzbuch governs the appointment of legal guardians (Betreuer) for adults who cannot manage their affairs due to mental illness or disability. The 2023 reform of guardianship law (Betreuungsrechtsreform) strengthened the principle of supported decision-making, requiring that a Betreuer be appointed only when the person's wishes cannot be implemented through less intrusive means such as a Vorsorgevollmacht (lasting power of attorney).

### Involuntary Treatment -- Constitutional Standards

The Bundesverfassungsgericht (Federal Constitutional Court) fundamentally reshaped involuntary psychiatric treatment law in two landmark 2011 decisions:

**BVerfG, 2 BvR 882/09 (June 23, 2011):** The Court held that the Rheinland-Pfalz PsychKG provisions authorizing involuntary treatment were unconstitutional because they failed to provide sufficient procedural safeguards, including advance notice to the patient, involvement of the patient in decision-making, and independent judicial authorization.

**BVerfG, 2 BvR 633/11 (October 12, 2011):** Addressing involuntary treatment under the Betreuungsrecht, the Court established that compulsory medical treatment of a person under guardianship is only permissible when the person lacks capacity to consent to the specific treatment, the treatment is necessary to avert a serious health danger, no less restrictive alternative is available, the potential benefit clearly outweighs the risks, a serious attempt to achieve voluntary consent has been made and documented, and prior judicial authorization has been obtained.

### PsychKG Procedures

| Step | Requirement | Timeframe |
|------|-------------|-----------|
| Emergency Admission | Police or health authority determination of acute danger | Immediate |
| Medical Certification | Psychiatric evaluation confirming mental illness and danger | Within hours of admission |
| Judicial Authorization | Court hearing with patient present (Richtervorbehalt) | Within 24 hours (most Laender) |
| Ongoing Review | Regular judicial review of continued detention | Every 6 weeks to 3 months (varies by Land) |
| Involuntary Treatment | Separate court order required; strict BVerfG criteria | Case-by-case judicial authorization |

### Patientenverfuegung (Advance Directive)

BGB Section 1901a recognizes the legally binding Patientenverfuegung (patient advance directive), which allows persons to specify their treatment preferences in advance of losing decision-making capacity. In the mental health context, psychiatric advance directives (psychiatrische Patientenverfuegungen) allow individuals to document their treatment preferences, preferred facilities, and crisis plans. The 2023 guardianship reform strengthened the binding nature of advance directives.

### Community Mental Health

Germany's statutory health insurance system (gesetzliche Krankenversicherung, GKV) covers psychiatric and psychotherapeutic treatment, including outpatient psychotherapy (covered for an initial 12-60 sessions depending on modality). The Gemeindepsychiatrische Verbunde (community psychiatric networks) coordinate outpatient, day-clinic, and social support services. Germany has been progressively reducing psychiatric bed capacity, though regional disparities in community mental health infrastructure persist.

## ECHR & EU

### Article 5(1)(e) ECHR -- Detention of Persons of Unsound Mind

The ECHR permits the lawful detention of "persons of unsound mind" under Article 5(1)(e), subject to the procedural safeguards established in the Court's jurisprudence.

**Winterwerp v. the Netherlands (Application No. 6301/73, Judgment of October 24, 1979):** The foundational ECtHR decision established three minimum conditions for the lawful detention of persons with mental disorders: (1) the individual must be reliably shown to have a true mental disorder based on objective medical expertise; (2) the mental disorder must be of a kind or degree warranting compulsory confinement; and (3) the validity of continued confinement depends upon the persistence of the disorder. These "Winterwerp criteria" remain the governing standard.

**Stanev v. Bulgaria (Application No. 36760/06, Grand Chamber Judgment of January 17, 2012):** The Grand Chamber held that the placement of a person with a mental disability in a social care home without his consent constituted a deprivation of liberty under Article 5. The Court also found a violation of Article 5(4) because the applicant had no direct access to a court to challenge his placement, and a violation of Article 3 due to the degrading conditions at the institution.

**Rooman v. Belgium (Application No. 18052/11, Grand Chamber Judgment of January 31, 2019):** The Grand Chamber held that detention under Article 5(1)(e) must maintain a therapeutic purpose, and that the authorities have a positive obligation to ensure that the detained person receives treatment adapted to their condition in a facility appropriate to their needs.

### Article 3 ECHR -- Prohibition of Inhuman Treatment

**Herczegfalvy v. Austria (Application No. 10533/83, Judgment of September 24, 1992):** The Court established that a measure which is a therapeutic necessity cannot be regarded as inhuman or degrading treatment, but the medical necessity must be convincingly shown. The burden is on the state to demonstrate that the treatment was medically necessary and proportionate.

**M.S. v. Croatia (No. 2) (Application No. 75450/12, Judgment of March 19, 2015):** The Court found a violation of Article 3 where a patient was subjected to involuntary electroconvulsive therapy (ECT) without proper informed consent procedures and without adequate medical justification documented in the records.

### UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD)

The EU ratified the CRPD in 2010, and it has been ratified individually by all EU member states plus South Korea (2008), the United States (signed but not ratified), and Germany (2009). The CRPD Committee's General Comment No. 1 (2014) on Article 12 (Equal Recognition Before the Law) has generated significant controversy by interpreting Article 12 as prohibiting all substitute decision-making regimes and calling for their replacement with supported decision-making frameworks. The Committee on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities has also taken the position (General Comment No. 1, para. 40) that involuntary psychiatric detention based on disability constitutes arbitrary deprivation of liberty.

### EU Disability Strategy 2021-2030

The European Commission's Strategy for the Rights of Persons with Disabilities 2021-2030 commits to supporting member states in transitioning from institutional to community-based care, promoting supported decision-making frameworks, and ensuring access to mental health services as part of overall healthcare.

## Comparative Analysis

### Involuntary Commitment Standards

All four jurisdictions require a finding of mental disorder and some form of dangerousness or necessity for involuntary commitment, but the specific standards and procedures diverge. South Korea's reformed framework requires two independent psychiatric evaluations and rapid review board scrutiny. The US constitutional floor of clear and convincing evidence (Addington) coexists with varying state definitions of dangerousness and grave disability. Germany's PsychKG systems uniformly require prompt judicial authorization (Richtervorbehalt), reflecting the strong constitutional emphasis on judicial oversight of deprivations of liberty. The ECtHR's Winterwerp criteria provide a supranational minimum standard, though they have been criticized as insufficiently protective by CRPD advocates.

### Involuntary Treatment

The most significant divergence lies in the regulation of compulsory psychiatric treatment. Germany's Federal Constitutional Court decisions of 2011 established the most rigorous procedural requirements in any jurisdiction, requiring case-by-case judicial authorization for each involuntary treatment decision. The US permits involuntary treatment of committed patients with varying procedural requirements depending on the state, with some states requiring court orders and others permitting treatment decisions by treating physicians with administrative review. South Korea's reformed law requires separate authorization for involuntary treatment beyond hospitalization.

### Community Treatment Orders

The expansion of outpatient civil commitment reflects a global trend toward extending coercive mental health interventions into the community. The US has been the most aggressive adopter, with 47 states authorizing some form of assisted outpatient treatment. Germany has been more cautious, with the PsychKG systems generally limiting compulsory treatment to inpatient settings. South Korea's community mental health infrastructure is still developing. The CRPD Committee has been critical of community treatment orders as a form of disability-based coercion.

### Capacity and Supported Decision-Making

The global trend, driven by the CRPD, is moving from substitute decision-making (guardianship) toward supported decision-making. Germany's 2023 guardianship reform reflects this shift, as does South Korea's evolving framework. However, no jurisdiction has fully implemented the CRPD Committee's radical interpretation of Article 12, which would eliminate all substitute decision-making. The tension between the CRPD Committee's abolitionist position and the practical realities of severe mental illness remains unresolved in all jurisdictions.

### Insurance Parity

South Korea's National Health Insurance provides universal coverage for mental health treatment, though with limitations on outpatient psychotherapy sessions. The US MHPAEA mandates parity in insurance coverage between mental health and medical/surgical benefits, but enforcement has been uneven and significant disparities persist in practice. Germany's statutory health insurance system covers psychiatric and psychotherapeutic treatment comprehensively, with regulated access to outpatient psychotherapy. EU-level harmonization of mental health insurance coverage remains limited.
