## Summary

Hate crime legislation — laws that enhance penalties or create distinct offenses for criminal conduct motivated by bias against a person's race, religion, ethnicity, sexual orientation, gender identity, disability, or other protected characteristic — reflects fundamentally different legal philosophies across jurisdictions. The United States employs a layered federal-state model anchored by the Matthew Shepard and James Byrd Jr. Hate Crimes Prevention Act of 2009 (18 U.S.C. § 249), which criminalizes bias-motivated violence at the federal level while deferring to approximately 47 state-level hate crime statutes for broader coverage. Germany addresses hate-motivated conduct through its distinctive Volksverhetzung (incitement to hatred) provision under Section 130 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), supplemented by the Netzwerkdurchsetzungsgesetz (NetzDG) for online hate speech enforcement. The European framework operates through Council Framework Decision 2008/913/JHA on combating racism and xenophobia, the European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR) Article 14 non-discrimination guarantee, and ongoing efforts to extend the list of "Eurocrimes" under Article 83(1) TFEU to encompass hate speech and hate crime. South Korea stands out among OECD nations for its absence of comprehensive anti-discrimination or hate crime legislation, though the National Human Rights Commission Act (국가인권위원회법, 2001) and repeated legislative proposals — including a renewed anti-discrimination bill introduced in the 22nd National Assembly in January 2026 — signal evolving attention to the issue.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea remains one of the few OECD member states without comprehensive anti-discrimination legislation or a dedicated hate crime statute. The legal framework addressing bias-motivated conduct is fragmented across several instruments of limited scope.

**National Human Rights Commission Act (국가인권위원회법, 2001):** The National Human Rights Commission of Korea (NHRCK), established on November 25, 2001, serves as the principal institutional mechanism for addressing discrimination complaints. The Act prohibits discriminatory acts on 19 enumerated grounds, including race, skin color, ethnicity, national origin, religion, disability, gender, and sexual orientation. However, the NHRCK's enforcement powers are limited to investigation, recommendation, and mediation — it cannot impose binding penalties or injunctions, and compliance with its recommendations is voluntary. Since its establishment, over 51,000 complaints have been filed with the Commission.

**Constitutional Guarantee:** Article 11 of the Constitution of the Republic of Korea (대한민국 헌법) provides: "All citizens shall be equal before the law, and there shall be no discrimination in political, economic, social, or cultural life on account of sex, religion, or social status." This guarantee, while foundational, has not been interpreted by the Constitutional Court to mandate specific hate crime legislation.

**Criminal Code Provisions:** The Korean Criminal Act (형법) does not include bias motivation as an aggravating sentencing factor. Hate-motivated violence is prosecuted under general assault (Art. 257), battery (Art. 260), or homicide (Art. 250) provisions without enhancement for discriminatory intent. Defamation provisions (Art. 307–312) are sometimes invoked against hate speech but were not designed for that purpose and require the statements to concern specific, identifiable individuals.

### Legislative Attempts and Failures

Comprehensive anti-discrimination bills have been introduced in the National Assembly on at least seven occasions since 2007, and all have failed to pass:

- **2007:** The first comprehensive anti-discrimination bill was introduced under the Roh Moo-hyun administration but was withdrawn due to political opposition, particularly from conservative religious groups objecting to the inclusion of sexual orientation as a protected characteristic.
- **2010–2013:** Multiple bills were introduced in the 18th and 19th National Assemblies, each dying in committee.
- **2020–2024:** The Justice Party's Jang Hye-yeong and the Democratic Party introduced separate bills in the 21st National Assembly. Despite endorsement by the NHRCK and international human rights organizations, neither bill advanced to a floor vote.
- **January 2026:** A renewed anti-discrimination bill was introduced in the 22nd National Assembly, reigniting the equality debate. The new draft seeks to ban unjust discrimination in labor, services, and education on grounds including gender, disability, and sexual orientation. It expands coverage to platform and gig workers and would empower the NHRCK to file lawsuits directly on behalf of victims, creating a legal basis for class-action discrimination suits.

### Notable Incidents

**Daegu Mosque Protests (2023):** When plans were announced to build a mosque in Daegu, residents engaged in sustained protests that included placing pig heads near the construction site and hosting pork barbecue events. The NHRCK declared such conduct a form of hate speech directed at Islamic culture, though it lacked enforcement power to impose penalties.

**Itaewon COVID-19 Discrimination (2020):** Following a COVID-19 outbreak linked to nightclubs in the Itaewon district of Seoul, media outlets published reports associating the outbreak with LGBTQ+ venues, leading to widespread outing and discrimination against LGBTQ+ individuals. The NHRCK issued statements condemning the discriminatory reporting, but no legal consequences followed.

**Anti-Chinese Sentiment (2025):** Rising anti-China rallies and public expressions of hatred directed at Chinese nationals and ethnic Koreans of Chinese descent prompted President Lee Jae-myung and senior ministers to call for stronger penalties against hate speech based on race or nationality, instructing police to step up enforcement.

### International Criticism

The UN Committee on the Elimination of Racial Discrimination (CERD) has repeatedly recommended that South Korea adopt comprehensive anti-discrimination legislation. In its 2018 review, CERD noted with concern the absence of hate crime provisions and the persistence of racial discrimination, particularly against migrant workers, mixed-race children, and North Korean defectors. A 2019 NHRCK survey of 338 migrants found that 68.4% agreed racial discrimination exists in Korea, with Korean language skills (62.3%) and nationality (59.7%) identified as the main grounds for discrimination.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States addresses hate crimes through a layered federal-state system. Federal hate crime law has evolved through three principal statutes spanning over four decades.

**Civil Rights Act of 1968 (18 U.S.C. § 245):** The original federal hate crime statute criminalized interference, by force or threat of force, with a person's federally protected activities (such as voting, attending school, or using public accommodations) on account of race, color, religion, or national origin. This statute required proof that the victim was engaged in a federally protected activity, significantly limiting its scope.

**Hate Crime Statistics Act (1990, 34 U.S.C. § 41305):** Required the Attorney General to collect data on crimes motivated by prejudice based on race, religion, sexual orientation, or ethnicity. This Act did not create substantive criminal offenses but established the FBI's hate crime data collection program under the Uniform Crime Reporting (UCR) system.

**Matthew Shepard and James Byrd Jr. Hate Crimes Prevention Act (2009, 18 U.S.C. § 249):** Signed into law by President Barack Obama on October 28, 2009, as a rider to the National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 2010, this landmark statute dramatically expanded federal hate crime jurisdiction. It eliminated the requirement that the victim be engaged in a federally protected activity and extended protected characteristics to include sexual orientation, gender, gender identity, and disability — in addition to race, color, religion, and national origin.

### Statutory Elements and Penalties (18 U.S.C. § 249)

The Act creates two tiers of federal hate crime offenses:

**Section 249(a)(1) — Offenses involving actual or perceived race, color, religion, or national origin:**
Whoever willfully causes bodily injury to any person because of the actual or perceived race, color, religion, or national origin of any person shall be punished as follows:

| Circumstance | Penalty |
|-------------|---------|
| Bodily injury (or attempt with dangerous weapon) | Up to 10 years imprisonment |
| Death results, or offense includes kidnapping, attempted kidnapping, aggravated sexual abuse, attempted murder | Life imprisonment |
| Attempt to cause bodily injury with dangerous weapon | Up to 10 years imprisonment |

**Section 249(a)(2) — Offenses involving actual or perceived religion, national origin, gender, sexual orientation, gender identity, or disability:**
Requires an additional jurisdictional nexus (interstate commerce connection). Same penalty structure as above.

**Supplementary Federal Statutes:**

### State-Level Legislation

Approximately 47 states and the District of Columbia have enacted some form of hate crime legislation, though the scope of protection varies significantly. States employ different models:

- **Penalty enhancement statutes** (most common): Increase the maximum sentence for an underlying crime when bias motivation is proven. Example: Wisconsin's penalty enhancement statute (upheld in *Wisconsin v. Mitchell*, 1993).
- **Standalone hate crime offenses:** Create separate criminal charges for bias-motivated conduct.
- **Institutional vandalism statutes:** Specifically address damage to houses of worship, cemeteries, and community centers.
- **Mandatory data collection and reporting:** Require law enforcement agencies to track and report hate crimes.

Notable gaps: Wyoming, South Carolina, and Arkansas historically have lacked comprehensive hate crime statutes, though legislative efforts continue.

### Landmark Cases

**Wisconsin v. Mitchell, 508 U.S. 476 (1993):** The Supreme Court unanimously held that penalty enhancement for bias-motivated crimes does not violate the First Amendment. Todd Mitchell, a young Black man, had incited a group to attack a white teenager after watching the film *Mississippi Burning*, asking: "Do you all feel hyped up to move on some white people?" His sentence was enhanced under Wisconsin's hate crime statute. Writing for a unanimous court, Chief Justice Rehnquist reasoned that the statute targeted conduct (violence), not protected expression, and served the same purpose as federal antidiscrimination law. This decision established the constitutional foundation for all modern hate crime penalty enhancement statutes.

**R.A.V. v. City of St. Paul, 505 U.S. 377 (1992):** The Supreme Court struck down a municipal hate speech ordinance as unconstitutionally overbroad and content-based under the First Amendment. The case involved a teenager who burned a cross on a Black family's lawn. The Court held that even within unprotected categories of speech (fighting words), the government may not discriminate based on the content or viewpoint of the speech. This decision constrains legislative approaches to hate speech criminalization in the United States.

**Virginia v. Black, 538 U.S. 343 (2003):** The Court held that states may ban cross-burning carried out with intent to intimidate, as such conduct constitutes a "true threat," but struck down a provision that treated cross-burning as prima facie evidence of intimidation. This decision refined the boundary between protected expression and prosecutable hate-related conduct.

**United States v. Cannon (2011):** One of the first successful prosecutions under the Matthew Shepard Act. Defendants were convicted for assaulting a man in Kentucky because of his sexual orientation, resulting in federal prison sentences.

### Hate Crime Statistics

The FBI's Hate Crime Statistics program, operated under the UCR system, reports annually on bias-motivated criminal incidents. The reporting system now encompasses six categories of bias motivation and 34 specific types of bias. Consistent underreporting remains a significant methodological concern; the Bureau of Justice Statistics estimates that the actual number of hate crimes substantially exceeds reported figures, as many victims do not report to law enforcement and many law enforcement agencies do not participate fully in the reporting program.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's approach to hate crime legislation is deeply shaped by its historical experience with National Socialism and the constitutional commitment to human dignity enshrined in Article 1 of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz). The German framework combines standalone hate speech offenses, penalty enhancement provisions, and platform-specific online enforcement mechanisms.

**Section 130 StGB — Volksverhetzung (Incitement to Hatred):**

Section 130 of the Strafgesetzbuch occupies a unique position in German criminal law. Originally enacted in 1871 and substantially redrafted in 1960 in response to a wave of antisemitic incidents in the young Federal Republic, it has been expanded multiple times since.

The provision criminalizes:
1. **Incitement to hatred (§ 130(1)):** Whoever, in a manner suited to disturb the public peace, incites hatred against a national, racial, religious, or ethnic group, or against a segment of the population defined by nationality, calls for violent or arbitrary measures against them, or assaults their human dignity by insulting, maliciously disparaging, or defaming them.
2. **Dissemination of hate material (§ 130(2)):** Distribution, public display, or making available of written material that incites hatred or assaults human dignity as defined in subsection (1).
3. **Holocaust denial and trivialization (§ 130(3)):** Whoever publicly or in an assembly approves of, denies, or downplays an act of genocide committed under the rule of National Socialism in a manner suited to disturb the public peace.
4. **Glorification of National Socialism (§ 130(4)):** Whoever publicly or in an assembly disturbs the public peace by glorifying or justifying the National Socialist rule of tyranny in a manner that violates the dignity of victims.
5. **Condoning or denying recognized genocides (§ 130(5)):** Added in 2022 to implement EU Framework Decision 2008/913/JHA, extending Holocaust denial provisions to cover denial or gross trivialization of other recognized genocides and crimes against humanity directed at groups identified by race, national origin, ethnicity, or religion.

**Section 46(2) StGB — Bias as Aggravating Sentencing Factor:**

Germany's general sentencing provision (§ 46) requires courts to consider the motives and aims of the offender as part of sentencing. Since 2015, this has been interpreted and applied to explicitly include racist, xenophobic, antisemitic, or other bias-related motivations as aggravating factors across all criminal offenses, not merely those charged under § 130.

### Online Enforcement: NetzDG and the Digital Services Act

The Netzwerkdurchsetzungsgesetz (Network Enforcement Act, NetzDG), enacted on October 1, 2017, imposed obligations on social media platforms with more than 2 million registered users in Germany to remove "manifestly unlawful" content — including Volksverhetzung — within 24 hours of receiving a complaint, or within 7 days for content requiring more detailed assessment. Platforms failing to establish compliant reporting and removal systems face fines of up to EUR 50 million.

The NetzDG has been partially superseded by the EU Digital Services Act (Regulation 2022/2065), which took full effect in February 2024 and establishes a harmonized EU-wide framework for platform content moderation, including hate speech obligations.

### Landmark Cases

**Germar Rudolf (2007):** Rudolf, a German chemist formerly associated with the Max Planck Institute, was convicted of Holocaust denial under § 130(3) and sentenced to two and a half years' imprisonment for publishing pseudo-scientific works denying the existence of gas chambers at Auschwitz.

**Ernst Zündel (2007):** Zündel, a German-Canadian Holocaust denier, was convicted by the Mannheim District Court (Landgericht Mannheim) in February 2007 for incitement under § 130. He had published Holocaust denial material from the United States and Canada that was accessible on the internet in Germany. He was sentenced to five years' imprisonment — the maximum under § 130(3).

**Ursula Haverbeck (2004–2024):** Haverbeck, described as the "Nazi grandma" by German media, received multiple prison sentences across numerous trials for repeatedly asserting that no homicidal gas chambers existed at Auschwitz. Her cases tested the limits of § 130 enforcement against persistent offenders, with cumulative sentences totaling several years.

**Federal Constitutional Court — BVerfG, 1 BvR 673/18 (2018):** The Bundesverfassungsgericht dismissed a constitutional complaint against a conviction for Holocaust denial, affirming that § 130(3) does not violate Article 5 (freedom of expression) of the Basic Law. The Court held that Holocaust denial is not protected expression because it constitutes a factual assertion that has been conclusively disproven and whose propagation inherently assaults the dignity of victims.

### Enforcement Statistics

In 2024, German authorities documented 5,097 cases of Volksverhetzung, comprising approximately 10.84% of all politically motivated crimes (politisch motivierte Kriminalität, PMK). The Federal Criminal Police Office (Bundeskriminalamt, BKA) classifies most Volksverhetzung offenses under right-wing extremism. Enforcement has intensified since the 2010s, driven by rising online hate speech, the migration crisis of 2015–2016, and increasing antisemitic incidents following the October 7, 2023 Hamas attack on Israel.

## ECHR & EU

### European Convention on Human Rights

The European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR) does not contain a standalone hate crime provision, but several articles provide the legal foundation for the European Court of Human Rights (ECtHR) to adjudicate bias-motivated conduct.

**Article 14 — Prohibition of Discrimination:** Prohibits discrimination in the enjoyment of Convention rights on grounds including sex, race, colour, language, religion, political or other opinion, national or social origin, association with a national minority, property, birth, or other status. Article 14 is not freestanding — it must be invoked in conjunction with another Convention right.

**Protocol No. 12, Article 1 — General Prohibition of Discrimination:** Provides a freestanding right to non-discrimination, extending the prohibition beyond the enjoyment of Convention rights to any right set forth by law. Not all Council of Europe member states have ratified Protocol No. 12.

**Article 10 — Freedom of Expression:** Protects the right to freedom of expression, subject to restrictions that are "prescribed by law" and "necessary in a democratic society" for specified legitimate aims, including the protection of the reputation or rights of others.

**Article 17 — Prohibition of Abuse of Rights:** Prevents the use of Convention rights to engage in activities aimed at the destruction of other Convention rights. The ECtHR has relied on Article 17 to exclude hate speech — particularly Holocaust denial and incitement to racial hatred — from the protection of Article 10.

### Landmark ECtHR Cases

**Jersild v. Denmark, App. No. 15890/89 (1994):** Danish journalist Jens Olaf Jersild was convicted of aiding and abetting the dissemination of racist statements after broadcasting an interview with members of a racist youth group ("the Greenjackets") who made derogatory remarks about immigrants. The Grand Chamber held (12–7) that Denmark violated Article 10, finding the conviction disproportionate because Jersild's purpose was journalistic reporting in the public interest, not the promotion of racist views. This landmark judgment established that journalistic context is a critical factor in assessing hate speech restrictions.

**Féret v. Belgium, App. No. 15615/07 (2009):** The Court upheld the conviction of Daniel Féret, a Belgian politician, for distributing racist leaflets during an electoral campaign. The Court found no violation of Article 10, holding that hate speech directed at denigrating and dehumanizing groups is not entitled to the protection of freedom of expression, even in the electoral context. The judgment confirmed that incitement to hatred need not include an explicit call to violence to be sanctionable.

**Garaudy v. France, App. No. 65831/01 (2003):** The Court declared inadmissible an application by Roger Garaudy, convicted of Holocaust denial under French law. Applying Article 17, the Court held that "denying crimes against humanity constitutes one of the most serious forms of racial defamation of Jews and of incitement to hatred of them," and that such speech is excluded from Article 10 protection.

**Lehideux and Isorni v. France, App. No. 24662/94 (1998):** The Grand Chamber established the principle that denial or revision of "clearly established historical facts — such as the Holocaust" is removed from Article 10 protection by Article 17. The Court distinguished this case, finding a violation of Article 10 because the applicants' publication, while presenting a favorable view of Marshal Pétain, did not deny or revise Holocaust facts.

**Identoba and Others v. Georgia, App. No. 73235/12 (2015):** The Court held that Georgia violated Articles 3 (prohibition of torture and inhuman treatment), 8 (right to respect for private life), and 14 (non-discrimination) by failing to protect LGBTQ+ demonstrators from violent attacks during the International Day Against Homophobia. This case established the positive obligation of states to investigate and prosecute bias-motivated violence.

### EU Framework Decision 2008/913/JHA

Council Framework Decision 2008/913/JHA on combating certain forms and expressions of racism and xenophobia by means of criminal law, adopted unanimously on November 28, 2008, after seven years of negotiations, constitutes the primary EU-level instrument on hate crime and hate speech.

**Scope of Criminalization (Article 1):**

Member States must ensure the following conduct is punishable:
- Publicly inciting violence or hatred directed against a group of persons or a member of such a group defined by reference to race, colour, religion, descent, or national or ethnic origin
- Publicly condoning, denying, or grossly trivializing crimes of genocide, crimes against humanity, and war crimes as defined in the Statute of the International Criminal Court, when directed against such groups, in a manner likely to incite violence or hatred
- Publicly condoning, denying, or grossly trivializing crimes defined in Article 6 of the Charter of the International Military Tribunal (Nuremberg), when directed against such groups, in a manner likely to incite violence or hatred

**Penalty Requirements (Article 3):**

Member States must ensure offenses are punishable by "effective, proportionate and dissuasive criminal penalties," with a maximum penalty of at least 1 to 3 years of imprisonment.

**Hate Crime Aggravating Factor (Article 4):**

Member States must ensure that racist and xenophobic motivation is considered an aggravating circumstance in sentencing for any criminal offense, or alternatively that such motivation may be taken into account by the courts in determining the applicable penalties.

### Proposed Extension to Eurocrimes

The European Commission, as announced by President Ursula von der Leyen in her 2020 State of the Union address, initiated a process to extend the list of EU crimes under Article 83(1) TFEU to include hate speech and hate crime. This would allow the EU to establish minimum rules concerning the definition of criminal offenses and sanctions across a broader range of protected grounds than the current Framework Decision, including gender, sexual orientation, age, and disability.

**Legislative progress:**
- **December 2021:** Commission Communication proposing the Council Decision to extend the list of EU crimes.
- **November 2023:** The European Parliament's LIBE Committee adopted its report endorsing the extension.
- **January 18, 2024:** The European Parliament adopted a resolution in plenary supporting the extension, calling on the Council to act and deploring the lack of progress. The Parliament noted that Article 83(1) TFEU requires Council unanimity, which has proven a significant obstacle.
- **2025–2026:** The file remains pending before the Council, with Hungary and Poland historically expressing reservations about the scope of protected grounds, particularly sexual orientation and gender identity.

### Digital Services Act (Regulation 2022/2065)

The EU Digital Services Act, which took full effect on February 17, 2024, establishes a comprehensive framework for content moderation by online platforms, including obligations related to hate speech. Very Large Online Platforms (VLOPs) and Very Large Online Search Engines (VLOSEs) must conduct systemic risk assessments, including risks related to the dissemination of illegal content such as hate speech, and adopt proportionate mitigation measures.

## Comparative Analysis

### Philosophical Foundations

The four jurisdictions reflect fundamentally different balances between individual liberty, collective dignity, and state authority:

**United States — Speech Liberty Model:** The First Amendment creates a constitutional presumption against content-based regulation of speech. Hate crime law in the U.S. can constitutionally enhance penalties for bias-motivated *conduct* (Wisconsin v. Mitchell) but cannot criminalize hate *speech* absent a true threat, incitement to imminent lawless action (Brandenburg v. Ohio, 1969), or other narrow exception. This produces a system that punishes hate-motivated violence severely but leaves hate speech largely unregulated.

**Germany — Dignity-First Model:** The Basic Law's placement of human dignity (Art. 1) as the supreme constitutional value, and the historical imperative of confronting National Socialism, produces a framework in which hate speech is a serious criminal offense. The German model prioritizes collective dignity and historical responsibility over individual expressive freedom, criminalizing not only incitement to violence but also insults to group dignity and denial of historical atrocities.

**ECHR/EU — Proportionality Model:** The European framework employs a proportionality analysis, balancing freedom of expression (Art. 10) against the rights of others and the needs of a democratic society. Article 17's prohibition on abuse of rights creates a categorical exclusion for the most extreme forms of hate speech, while lesser forms are assessed under the Art. 10(2) proportionality framework. The EU harmonization effort through the Framework Decision establishes minimum standards while preserving member state discretion.

**South Korea — Institutional-Aspirational Model:** The absence of dedicated hate crime legislation means that bias-motivated conduct is addressed through general criminal law without enhancement, supplemented by a non-binding institutional mechanism (NHRCK). The repeated introduction and failure of anti-discrimination bills reflects a society in which formal legal commitment to anti-discrimination norms has not yet overcome political resistance, particularly from conservative religious constituencies.

### Key Structural Differences

**Scope of Protected Characteristics:** The U.S. federal law and the German framework are relatively narrow in their enumerated grounds (though Germany's general sentencing provision is flexible). The EU Framework Decision is limited to race, colour, religion, descent, and national or ethnic origin. The proposed EU extension and South Korea's pending bill both seek to incorporate sexual orientation, gender identity, disability, and age.

**Speech vs. Conduct:** The sharpest distinction lies between the U.S. approach, which constitutionally limits criminal liability to bias-motivated *conduct* causing bodily injury, and the German and EU approaches, which criminalize bias-motivated *speech* — including speech that does not call for violence but assaults group dignity or denies historical atrocities.

**Enforcement Mechanisms:** Germany's NetzDG and the EU's Digital Services Act represent the most developed platform-level enforcement regimes. The United States relies primarily on traditional criminal prosecution, with Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act shielding platforms from liability for user-generated content. South Korea's KCSC exercises content regulation authority but without a specific hate speech mandate.

**Evidentiary Standards:** Proving bias motivation presents evidentiary challenges across all jurisdictions. The U.S. requires proof that the crime was committed "because of" the victim's protected characteristic. Germany's § 130 requires that the speech be "suited to disturb the public peace." The EU Framework Decision requires member states to treat bias motivation as aggravating, but leaves specific evidentiary standards to national law.
