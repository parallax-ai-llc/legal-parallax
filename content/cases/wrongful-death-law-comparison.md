## Summary

Wrongful death -- a civil cause of action brought by survivors or estates when a person dies due to the negligence, recklessness, or intentional act of another -- occupies a critical intersection between tort law, human rights law, and criminal accountability across world legal systems. South Korea grounds its wrongful death framework in Articles 750 through 752 and Article 763 of the Civil Act, supplemented since 2022 by the Serious Accident Punishment Act (SAPA) which introduced criminal liability for business owners and executives whose safety failures cause worker or public deaths. The United States, where wrongful death statutes trace their origins to England's Lord Campbell's Act of 1846, operates a patchwork of 50 state-level statutes with widely divergent damages caps, statutes of limitations, and eligible claimants, alongside federal maritime death statutes. Germany's wrongful death regime under Sections 823, 844, and 845 of the Burgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB) historically limited survivors' recovery to pecuniary losses, but a landmark 2017 reform introduced Hinterbliebenengeld (bereavement damages) under Section 844(3), finally permitting compensation for non-pecuniary suffering. The European Convention on Human Rights, through Article 2 (right to life) as interpreted by the European Court of Human Rights, imposes positive obligations on states to protect life, investigate suspicious deaths, and provide effective domestic remedies -- a supranational framework that has profoundly shaped wrongful death accountability across 46 member states.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korean wrongful death law is primarily governed by the Civil Act (Minbeop), with the foundational tort provision established in Article 750 and specific wrongful death remedies provided in related articles:

| Provision | Description | Application |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| Article 750 | General tort liability: any person who willfully or negligently causes damage to another by an unlawful act shall compensate for damages | Foundation for all wrongful death civil claims |
| Article 751 | Compensation for non-pecuniary damages (wisaryo/consolation money) to the victim | Applied to the decedent's own claim, inheritable by heirs |
| Article 752 | Independent consolation money claim for bereaved family (spouse, lineal ascendants/descendants, siblings) | Direct claim by surviving family, not inherited from decedent |
| Article 763 | Application of contributory negligence rules (Art. 396) to tort claims | Court reduces damages proportionally based on victim's fault |
| Article 766 | Statute of limitations: 3 years from knowledge; 10 years absolute | Governs time limits for filing wrongful death claims |

### Key Principles

**Dual claim structure:** South Korean law recognizes two distinct categories of wrongful death claims. First, the decedent's own damages claim (including lost future earnings and consolation money under Article 751) passes to the heirs as an inherited right. Second, the bereaved family members have an independent, personal right to consolation money under Article 752, regardless of their status as heirs. This dual structure provides broader coverage than many other civil law jurisdictions.

**Consolation money (wisaryo):** Courts award wisaryo for emotional suffering to the bereaved. While there are no statutory formulas, Korean courts have developed ranges based on the closeness of the relationship, the circumstances of the death, and the degree of fault. Typical consolation money awards for a spouse or child range from approximately KRW 80 million to KRW 150 million (roughly USD 60,000-115,000), though amounts vary significantly.

**Compensatory principle:** Korean tort law is strictly compensatory, and punitive damages are generally not available. Damages include lost future earnings (calculated using actuarial methods based on the decedent's age, income, and life expectancy), funeral expenses, medical expenses incurred before death, and consolation money.

### Serious Accident Punishment Act (SAPA, 2022)

Enacted on January 26, 2021, and effective from January 27, 2022, the Serious Accident Punishment Act (Jungdae Jaehae Cheobeobeop) was a legislative response to industrial disasters and public tragedies, most notably the 2014 Sewol ferry disaster that killed 304 people:

| SAPA Provision | Description | Penalty |
|----------------|-------------|---------|
| Criminal liability for business owners/CEOs | Failure to fulfill safety and health obligations resulting in death | 1 year or more imprisonment and/or fine up to KRW 1 billion (approx. USD 770,000) |
| Criminal liability for serious injury | Failure to fulfill safety obligations resulting in serious injury | Up to 7 years imprisonment and/or fine up to KRW 100 million |
| Punitive damages | Civil damages for SAPA violations | Up to 5 times actual compensatory damages |
| Corporate penalties | Fines against the business entity itself | Up to KRW 5 billion for death; up to KRW 1 billion for serious injury |

SAPA was modeled on the United Kingdom's Corporate Manslaughter and Corporate Homicide Act 2007 and represents a significant expansion of personal criminal liability for corporate leadership. As of 2025, at least 15 company chiefs have been convicted under the Act since its enforcement.

### Key Cases

**Sewol Ferry Disaster (2014):** The sinking of the MV Sewol on April 16, 2014, killing 304 passengers (mostly high school students), was the defining tragedy that catalyzed South Korea's wrongful death legal reforms. In civil proceedings, the government and Chonghaejin Marine Company were held liable for damages to bereaved families. The disaster exposed critical gaps in corporate accountability for safety failures and directly led to the enactment of SAPA. Bereaved families received government compensation and civil settlement awards, though many families pursued additional litigation seeking greater accountability and damages.

**Samsung Semiconductor Worker Death Cases (2007-ongoing):** Beginning in 2007, a series of leukemia and other cancer deaths among Samsung Electronics semiconductor factory workers prompted prolonged litigation. The families of deceased workers brought wrongful death claims arguing that exposure to toxic chemicals caused their illnesses. In 2018, Samsung formally apologized and agreed to compensate affected workers and families through a mediation agreement, with individual compensation amounts reaching up to KRW 150 million per case. The cases highlighted the challenges of proving causation in occupational disease wrongful death claims.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States does not have a single federal wrongful death statute of general application. Instead, wrongful death law is governed by individual state statutes, all tracing their origins to Lord Campbell's Act (Fatal Accidents Act 1846) of England:

**Historical development:**
- **1846:** Lord Campbell's Act (UK) creates the first statutory right for survivors to claim damages for wrongful death
- **1847:** New York becomes the first U.S. state to enact a wrongful death statute
- **1920:** Congress passes the Death on the High Seas Act (DOHSA) and the Jones Act for maritime wrongful death
- **1970:** Supreme Court recognizes a general maritime wrongful death action in Moragne v. States Marine Lines, Inc.

### Federal Statutes

| Statute | Scope | Damages Available |
|---------|-------|-------------------|
| Death on the High Seas Act (46 U.S.C. Section 30302) | Deaths occurring on the high seas beyond 3 nautical miles from U.S. shore | Pecuniary loss only; no non-economic damages |
| Jones Act (46 U.S.C. Section 30104) | Seamen injured or killed in the course of employment | Pecuniary damages; pain and suffering of decedent before death |
| Federal Tort Claims Act (28 U.S.C. Section 2674) | Wrongful death caused by federal government employees | Governed by state law where the act or omission occurred |
| General maritime law (Moragne) | Deaths on navigable waters | Pecuniary loss; loss of society (in some circuits) |

### Elements of a Wrongful Death Claim

Across all U.S. jurisdictions, a wrongful death plaintiff must prove four elements:

1. **Duty of care:** The defendant owed a legal duty to the decedent
2. **Breach:** The defendant breached that duty through negligence, recklessness, or intentional conduct
3. **Causation:** The breach was the direct and proximate cause of the death
4. **Damages:** Surviving family members suffered measurable harm as a result

The burden of proof is preponderance of the evidence (more likely than not), significantly lower than the beyond-a-reasonable-doubt standard in criminal proceedings.

### Damages

**Economic damages:** Lost future earnings and benefits the decedent would have provided; medical and funeral expenses; loss of inheritance; value of household services.

**Non-economic damages:** Loss of companionship, consortium, comfort, care, guidance, and moral support. Available in most but not all states.

**Punitive damages:** Available in some states (e.g., California, Alabama, Mississippi) if the defendant's conduct was especially egregious, willful, or malicious. Prohibited in wrongful death actions in other states (e.g., New York, Virginia).

**Damages caps:** Some states impose caps on non-economic damages in wrongful death cases. For instance, several states cap non-economic damages in medical malpractice wrongful death cases, though such caps have been struck down as unconstitutional in some jurisdictions.

### Landmark Cases

**Moragne v. States Marine Lines, Inc., 398 U.S. 375 (1970):** The Supreme Court unanimously overruled The Harrisburg (1886) and recognized a general maritime cause of action for wrongful death, holding that the common-law rule denying recovery for wrongful death was "an unjustifiable anomaly." Justice Harlan's opinion established that the maritime wrongful death action would be available independent of any statute, fundamentally reshaping federal wrongful death law.

**O.J. Simpson Civil Trial (1997):** After being acquitted of criminal murder charges, O.J. Simpson was found liable in a civil wrongful death action brought by the families of Nicole Brown Simpson and Ronald Goldman. The jury awarded USD 33.5 million in compensatory and punitive damages. The case vividly demonstrated the difference between the criminal standard of proof (beyond reasonable doubt) and the civil standard (preponderance of the evidence) in wrongful death proceedings.

**Liebeck v. McDonald's Restaurants, P.T.S., Inc. (1994):** While primarily known as a products liability case, the underlying facts involved severe burns that ultimately contributed to Stella Liebeck's death in 2004. The case highlighted the intersection of product liability and wrongful death claims and became a landmark in American tort reform debates regarding damages caps and frivolous lawsuits.

**BMW of North America, Inc. v. Gore, 517 U.S. 559 (1996):** The Supreme Court established constitutional limits on punitive damages under the Due Process Clause, holding that grossly excessive punitive damages violate the Fourteenth Amendment. While not a wrongful death case per se, the decision directly impacts punitive damages awards in wrongful death cases nationwide.

### Statute of Limitations

Statutes of limitations for wrongful death vary significantly by state, typically ranging from 1 to 6 years:

| State (Example) | Limitation Period | Notes |
|-----------------|-------------------|-------|
| California | 2 years from date of death | Cal. Code Civ. Proc. Section 335.1 |
| New York | 2 years from date of death | N.Y. E.P.T.L. Section 5-4.1 |
| Texas | 2 years from date of death | Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code Section 16.003 |
| Florida | 2 years from date of death | Fla. Stat. Section 95.11(4)(d) |
| Kentucky | 1 year from appointment of personal representative | Ky. Rev. Stat. Section 413.180 |
| Maine | 6 years from date of death | Me. Rev. Stat. tit. 18-C, Section 2-807 |

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German wrongful death law is codified in the Burgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB), with the primary provisions contained in Sections 823, 844, and 845:

| BGB Section | Description | Application |
|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| Section 823(1) | General tort liability: liability for willful or negligent injury to life, body, health, freedom, property, or other rights | Foundation for all wrongful death claims |
| Section 823(2) | Liability for violation of a protective statute | Extended basis for claims where death results from statutory violations |
| Section 844(1) | Funeral expenses: the tortfeasor must reimburse the costs of a funeral to the person bearing that obligation | Pecuniary claim by heirs responsible for funeral |
| Section 844(2) | Loss of maintenance: the tortfeasor must compensate third parties to whom the deceased owed or would have owed a legal duty of maintenance | Annuity payments to dependents (spouse, children, parents) |
| Section 844(3) | Bereavement damages (Hinterbliebenengeld): compensation for non-pecuniary suffering of close relatives | Introduced in 2017; presumption for spouse/partner, parents, and children |
| Section 845 | Loss of services: compensation for loss of services the deceased had been obligated to provide | Covers household and care services |

### Key Principles

**Full compensation principle (Totalreparation):** German damages law is governed by the principle of full compensation under Sections 249 et seq. BGB, meaning the injured party is entitled to compensation for all losses actually incurred. Simultaneously, the prohibition on enrichment (Bereicherungsverbot) prevents any overcompensation. This dual principle strictly excludes punitive damages in German law.

**Bereavement damages reform (2017):** The introduction of Section 844(3) BGB, effective July 22, 2017, was a watershed moment in German wrongful death law. Previously, Germany was one of the few European legal systems that provided no compensation for the non-pecuniary suffering of bereaved relatives in wrongful death cases. The reform grants an appropriate monetary compensation (angemessene Entschadigung in Geld) to close relatives for their emotional suffering.

**Presumed proximity:** Section 844(3) establishes a rebuttable presumption that a "special personal proximity" (besonderes personliches Naheverhaltnis) exists between the deceased and their spouse or life partner, parents, and children. Other relatives (siblings, stepchildren, foster children, close companions, fiances) may prove this proximity on the facts.

**Damages quantification:** German courts have awarded bereavement damages in a range typically between EUR 10,000 and EUR 15,000 per close relative, though amounts are determined on a case-by-case basis. These amounts are significantly lower than comparable awards in common law jurisdictions.

### Criminal Intersection

Wrongful death cases in Germany frequently involve parallel criminal proceedings under Section 222 StGB (negligent killing / Fahrlassige Totung), which carries a penalty of up to 5 years imprisonment or a fine. A criminal conviction can support but is not required for a civil wrongful death claim. The civil and criminal proceedings are independent.

### Key Cases

**Bundesgerichtshof (BGH), VI ZR 114/17 (2019):** One of the earliest appellate decisions applying the new Section 844(3) bereavement damages provision. The Federal Court of Justice confirmed that the new right applies to deaths occurring after July 22, 2017, and clarified the standard for "appropriate" compensation, holding that courts must consider all circumstances of the individual case, including the nature of the relationship, the manner of death, and the impact on the survivor.

**Germanwings Flight 9525 Litigation (2015-ongoing):** After co-pilot Andreas Lubitz deliberately crashed a Germanwings aircraft into the French Alps on March 24, 2015, killing all 150 people on board, bereaved families filed wrongful death claims in German courts against Germanwings and parent company Lufthansa. The litigation raised complex questions regarding employer liability for psychiatric fitness screening, applicable law (German vs. French), and the adequacy of German bereavement damages. Lufthansa offered initial compensation of EUR 25,000 per victim plus EUR 10,000 per close relative, though many families pursued further claims arguing these amounts were inadequate.

**Loveparade Disaster Litigation (2010):** Following the crowd crush at the Love Parade music festival in Duisburg on July 24, 2010, which killed 21 people and injured over 500, bereaved families pursued wrongful death claims against the event organizer and the City of Duisburg. The criminal trial, which began in 2017, was discontinued in 2020 without a verdict due to the statute of limitations, leaving civil claims as the primary avenue of accountability. The case highlighted limitations in German law regarding corporate criminal liability for mass-casualty events.

## ECHR & EU

### European Convention on Human Rights -- Article 2

Article 2 of the European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR) protects the right to life and forms the supranational foundation for wrongful death accountability across 46 Council of Europe member states:

> "1. Everyone's right to life shall be protected by law. No one shall be deprived of his life intentionally save in the execution of a sentence of a court following his conviction of a crime for which this penalty is provided by law."

The European Court of Human Rights (ECtHR) has described Article 2 as "one of the most fundamental provisions in the Convention," establishing three principal obligations on states:

### State Obligations Under Article 2

| Obligation | Description | Leading Case |
|------------|-------------|--------------|
| **Negative obligation** | Duty to refrain from unlawful or disproportionate use of lethal force | McCann and Others v. United Kingdom (1995) |
| **Positive obligation to protect** | Duty to take preventive operational measures to protect individuals whose lives are at known and immediate risk | Osman v. United Kingdom (1998) |
| **Procedural obligation** | Duty to conduct an effective, independent, and prompt official investigation into deaths | McCann (1995); Calvelli and Ciglio v. Italy (2002) |

### The Osman Test

In Osman v. United Kingdom (1998), the ECtHR established the test for state liability where authorities fail to protect an individual from a known risk to life. The state violates Article 2 where:

1. The authorities knew or ought to have known of the existence of a real and immediate risk to the life of an identified individual
2. The authorities failed to take measures within the scope of their powers which, judged reasonably, might have been expected to avoid that risk

This test has been applied in cases involving failures by police, medical authorities, prison officials, and regulatory bodies to prevent foreseeable deaths.

### Landmark ECHR Cases

**McCann and Others v. United Kingdom (1995):** Three members of the IRA were shot and killed by SAS soldiers in Gibraltar. The Grand Chamber held (10 votes to 9) that the United Kingdom violated Article 2, not because the soldiers acted unlawfully in pulling the trigger, but because the overall control and organization of the operation did not satisfy the requirement that the use of force be "no more than absolutely necessary." The Court established that Article 2(2) exceptions (defense against unlawful violence, lawful arrest, quelling insurrection) must be interpreted strictly and that the force used must be strictly proportionate to the legitimate aim.

**Osman v. United Kingdom (1998):** A schoolteacher who had developed an obsession with a pupil killed the pupil's father and seriously injured the pupil despite prior police knowledge of threats. The Court established the positive obligation test (above) and held that states are not required to prevent every potential threat to life, but must take reasonable measures when they know of a real and immediate risk.

**Calvelli and Ciglio v. Italy (2002):** An infant died due to alleged medical negligence at birth. The Court held that the positive obligation under Article 2 requires states to have in place: (a) regulations compelling hospitals to adopt appropriate measures for the protection of patients' lives; and (b) an effective independent judicial system allowing the cause of death to be determined and those responsible to be held accountable. The case extended Article 2 obligations to the medical negligence context.

**Gongadze v. Ukraine (2005):** Following the murder of journalist Georgiy Gongadze, the Court found that Ukraine violated Article 2 both substantively (by failing to protect the journalist's life despite knowledge of threats) and procedurally (by conducting an inadequate and deliberately obstructed investigation). The case underscored the investigative obligation in cases involving state agent complicity.

**Oneryildiz v. Turkey (2004):** A methane explosion at a municipal rubbish tip killed 39 residents of a neighboring shantytown. The Grand Chamber held that the positive obligation to protect life extends to dangerous activities, whether public or private, and requires states to implement a legislative and administrative framework designed to provide effective deterrence against threats to the right to life.

### EU Product Liability Framework

The European Union has established a separate but complementary framework for wrongful death arising from defective products:

**Directive 85/374/EEC (Product Liability Directive, 1985):** Established strict (no-fault) liability for producers whose defective products cause death or personal injury. Damage includes death and personal injury. Member States may set a limit for total producer liability (not less than EUR 70 million).

**Directive (EU) 2024/2853 (New Product Liability Directive):** Adopted in 2024 and set to replace Directive 85/374/EEC from December 9, 2026, the new directive modernizes product liability rules for the digital age, extending strict liability to software, AI systems, and digital products. It strengthens protections for injured parties by:
- Easing the burden of proof in complex cases (e.g., involving AI or pharmaceutical products)
- Extending liability to online platforms acting as economic operators
- Covering psychological harm as compensable damage

### Just Satisfaction (Article 41 ECHR)

When the ECtHR finds a violation of Article 2, it may award "just satisfaction" to the applicant under Article 41. Awards in wrongful death cases typically include:

- **Pecuniary damages:** Loss of financial support, funeral and legal expenses
- **Non-pecuniary damages:** Emotional suffering of surviving relatives, typically ranging from EUR 20,000 to EUR 100,000 depending on the severity and circumstances
- **Costs and expenses:** Legal fees and costs incurred in the proceedings

## Comparative Analysis

### Philosophical Foundations

The four jurisdictions reflect fundamentally different approaches to wrongful death liability:

**South Korea** blends continental civil law tradition with uniquely Korean concepts, particularly the emphasis on consolation money (wisaryo) as a distinct head of damages for emotional suffering. The 2022 SAPA represents a modern trend toward criminalizing corporate leadership failures, reflecting a societal demand for personal accountability after mass-casualty disasters.

**The United States** is characterized by its common law tradition of jury trials, broad discovery, contingency fee arrangements, and the availability of punitive damages -- features that combine to make the U.S. wrongful death litigation system among the most plaintiff-friendly in the world, though outcomes vary dramatically across 50 state systems.

**Germany** exemplifies the continental European preference for systematic codification and the principle of full (but strictly compensatory) reparation. The 2017 introduction of bereavement damages marked a significant, if modest, departure from the historical position that non-pecuniary loss of survivors was not compensable. Damages awards remain substantially lower than in common law jurisdictions.

**The ECHR/EU framework** operates at a supranational level, establishing minimum standards of life protection and accountability that member states must meet, while leaving the specifics of wrongful death civil remedies to national law. The ECtHR's jurisprudence has been particularly influential in expanding state obligations to protect life proactively and to investigate deaths effectively.

### Key Divergences

1. **Punitive damages:** The most significant transatlantic divide. Available (and sometimes substantial) in many U.S. states; categorically rejected in Germany and at the ECHR level; historically rejected in South Korea until the narrow SAPA exception.

2. **Who can sue:** U.S. states vary widely (some restrict to spouse and children; others include parents, siblings, and domestic partners). South Korea provides both inherited claims (to heirs) and direct family claims (Art. 752). Germany limits claims to dependents and close relatives. The ECHR allows applications by any surviving relative with standing.

3. **Quantum of damages:** U.S. wrongful death awards regularly exceed USD 1 million and can reach hundreds of millions in egregious cases. German bereavement damages typically range from EUR 10,000 to EUR 15,000. Korean consolation money falls between these extremes. ECtHR non-pecuniary awards rarely exceed EUR 100,000.

4. **Corporate criminal liability:** South Korea (SAPA) and the UK (Corporate Manslaughter Act, which influenced SAPA) have moved most aggressively toward holding corporate officers personally criminally liable for wrongful death resulting from safety failures. Germany lacks a general corporate criminal liability regime. The ECHR requires effective criminal sanctions but leaves implementation to states.

5. **Burden of proof:** Civil wrongful death claims universally require a lower burden than criminal proceedings. The U.S. standard (preponderance of the evidence) is arguably the lowest, while German courts apply a higher degree of judicial conviction (richterliche Uberzeugung) closer to but still below the criminal standard.
