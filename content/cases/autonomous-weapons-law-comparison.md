## Summary

Autonomous weapons -- weapons systems that can select and engage targets without human intervention, encompassing a spectrum from semi-autonomous systems requiring human authorization for each engagement to fully autonomous lethal systems capable of independent target identification, selection, and engagement -- represent one of the most consequential and contested areas of international law, raising fundamental questions about the application of international humanitarian law (IHL) principles of distinction, proportionality, and precaution to machines that operate without direct human control over the use of lethal force. South Korea occupies a unique position as both a major autonomous weapons developer and a nation facing an active military threat on the Korean Peninsula; the Samsung SGR-A1 sentry robot deployed along the DMZ since 2010 represents one of the world's first armed autonomous systems, though it operates in a supervised mode requiring human authorization for engagement; the Defense Acquisition Program Administration (방위사업청, DAPA) oversees weapons development within the framework of the Defense Acquisition Act (방위사업법), and South Korea has participated in the Convention on Certain Conventional Weapons (CCW) Group of Governmental Experts (GGE) discussions on lethal autonomous weapons systems (LAWS) while opposing a preemptive ban. The United States has the world's most developed policy framework for autonomous weapons through Department of Defense Directive 3000.09 (2012, updated January 2023), which establishes review and authorization procedures for autonomous and semi-autonomous weapons systems while preserving the option of developing fully autonomous lethal systems; the U.S. has invested heavily in autonomous systems including the X-47B unmanned combat air vehicle, the Sea Hunter autonomous surface vessel, and the Collaborative Combat Aircraft (CCA) program, while opposing binding international restrictions on LAWS development. Germany, as a major arms-producing nation and leading advocate for IHL compliance, has supported international discussions on LAWS regulation within the CCW framework and endorsed the principle that human control must be maintained over life-and-death decisions; domestically, arms exports are regulated through the Kriegswaffenkontrollgesetz (KrWaffKontrG, War Weapons Control Act) and the Außenwirtschaftsgesetz (AWG), and the Bundeswehr's procurement of armed drones (Eurodrohne, Heron TP) has generated significant political debate about the threshold between remotely piloted and autonomous weapons. At the international and European level, the CCW GGE has conducted negotiations on LAWS since 2014 without reaching consensus on a binding treaty, the International Committee of the Red Cross (ICRC) issued recommendations in 2021 calling for prohibitions on unpredictable autonomous weapons and those designed to target humans, the European Parliament adopted resolutions in 2014, 2018, and 2021 calling for a ban on fully autonomous weapons, and a group of states led by Austria, Costa Rica, and others have advocated for a new international treaty through the process that produced the Political Declaration on Responsible Military Use of Artificial Intelligence and Autonomy (2023) endorsed by over 50 states.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's autonomous weapons governance operates within the broader defense procurement and IHL compliance framework, without specific autonomous weapons legislation.

**Defense Acquisition Act (방위사업법):** This statute governs the acquisition, development, and procurement of military systems by the Defense Acquisition Program Administration (DAPA, 방위사업청). All weapons systems, including autonomous and semi-autonomous systems, must undergo a requirements validation process, a systems engineering review, and testing and evaluation before deployment. The Act does not contain specific provisions addressing autonomy in weapons systems.

**Military Service Act (병역법) and Defense Security Command:** The operational deployment of weapons systems, including autonomous systems, is governed by military regulations and rules of engagement (교전규칙) established by the Joint Chiefs of Staff (합동참모본부). Current rules of engagement require human authorization for the use of lethal force in all circumstances, including in the operation of autonomous sentry systems.

**Samsung SGR-A1 Sentry Robot:** Developed by Samsung Techwin (now Hanwha Aerospace) and deployed along the DMZ beginning in 2010, the SGR-A1 is an armed sentry system equipped with surveillance cameras, heat and motion sensors, a 5.56mm machine gun, and a 40mm grenade launcher. The system is capable of autonomous target detection and tracking, but all engagement decisions require human authorization through a command post link. The SGR-A1 represents one of the earliest deployments of an armed robotic sentry system and has been cited extensively in international LAWS debates.

### CCW Position

South Korea participates actively in the CCW GGE on LAWS. Korea's position, articulated in GGE sessions, emphasizes: (1) the importance of maintaining appropriate levels of human judgment in the use of force; (2) opposition to a preemptive legally binding instrument that could constrain legitimate defense technology development; (3) support for voluntary guidelines and best practices; (4) the sufficiency of existing IHL to regulate autonomous weapons.

### Defense Industry

South Korea's defense industry, including Hanwha Aerospace, Korea Aerospace Industries (KAI), and LIG Nex1, is investing heavily in autonomous systems including unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs), autonomous underwater vehicles (AUVs), and AI-enhanced command and control systems. The government's Defense Reform 2.0 (국방개혁 2.0) initiative emphasizes the integration of AI and autonomous technologies into military operations to compensate for declining conscription-age population due to South Korea's low birth rate.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has the most developed domestic policy framework for autonomous weapons, centered on DoD Directive 3000.09.

**DoD Directive 3000.09 -- Autonomy in Weapon Systems (2012, updated January 25, 2023):** This Directive establishes the policy that autonomous and semi-autonomous weapons systems shall be designed to allow commanders and operators to exercise "appropriate levels of human judgment" over the use of force. Key provisions:

- **Section 4.a:** Autonomous weapons systems must comply with the law of war, applicable treaties, Rules of Engagement, and established weapon system safety protocols.
- **Section 4.b:** Persons authorizing the use of autonomous weapons systems must do so consistent with the commander's responsibility for the employment of force.
- **Section 4.c:** Semi-autonomous weapons systems that, once activated, are intended to only engage individual targets that have been selected by a human operator must be designed to complete engagements in a timeframe consistent with commander and operator intentions.

The Directive does not prohibit the development of fully autonomous lethal weapons but establishes a review and approval process. The Under Secretary of Defense for Policy and the Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff must approve the development and fielding of autonomous weapons systems that are designed to select and engage targets without further human input after activation.

**Law of War Manual (2015, updated 2023):** The DoD Law of War Manual addresses autonomous weapons in Section 6.5, requiring that weapons reviews under Article 36 of Additional Protocol I (to which the U.S. is not a party but follows as customary law) assess whether a weapon system can be used in compliance with IHL principles.

**National Defense Authorization Acts:** Congress has addressed autonomous weapons in several NDAAs, including requirements for reporting on autonomous weapons development (NDAA FY2023) and funding restrictions (various appropriations riders proposed but not enacted).

### Congressional Debate

The Congressional debate on autonomous weapons has intensified, with several legislative proposals introduced. The Autonomous Weapons Systems Accountability Act (proposed in multiple sessions) would require congressional notification before deployment of fully autonomous weapons. The Block Nuclear Launch by Autonomous Artificial Intelligence Act would prohibit autonomous AI from controlling nuclear launch decisions.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany addresses autonomous weapons through its constitutional framework, arms control legislation, and active engagement in international negotiations.

**Grundgesetz Article 25:** "The general rules of international law shall be an integral part of federal law." This provision incorporates customary international humanitarian law, including the principles of distinction, proportionality, and precaution, directly into German constitutional law, creating a constitutional basis for ensuring IHL compliance in weapons development and deployment.

**Grundgesetz Article 26(2):** "Weapons designed for warfare may be manufactured, transported, or marketed only with the permission of the Federal Government." This constitutional provision requires government authorization for all weapons production, providing a legal basis for regulating autonomous weapons development.

**Kriegswaffenkontrollgesetz (KrWaffKontrG) -- War Weapons Control Act:** This statute implements Article 26(2) GG, requiring licenses for the manufacture, acquisition, transfer, and transport of war weapons listed in the Kriegswaffenliste (War Weapons List). Autonomous weapons systems would require classification and licensing under this framework.

**Parlamentsvorbehalt (Parliamentary Reservation):** Under BVerfG jurisprudence (BVerfGE 90, 286 -- Out-of-area deployment, 1994; BVerfGE 121, 135 -- AWACS Turkey, 2008), the deployment of armed German military forces requires prior Bundestag approval. This parliamentary reservation would apply to the deployment of autonomous weapons systems by the Bundeswehr, ensuring democratic accountability.

### Armed Drone Debate

The Bundestag's debate over arming German military drones provides context for autonomous weapons governance. The decision to lease Israeli Heron TP drones capable of carrying weapons was debated extensively from 2016 to 2022. The SPD-Grüne-FDP coalition agreement (2021) stated that the government would arm Bundeswehr drones but only for "the protection of soldiers in external deployments." The Eurodrohne (European MALE RPAS), developed jointly by Germany, France, Italy, and Spain, is a remotely piloted system with no autonomous engagement capability.

### International Advocacy

Germany, together with France, has advocated for the development of a "Political Declaration on LAWS" within the CCW framework, calling for meaningful human control (bedeutsame menschliche Kontrolle) over the use of force. Germany's position rejects a comprehensive ban as impractical but supports legally non-binding norms and voluntary codes of conduct. The German Institute for International and Security Affairs (Stiftung Wissenschaft und Politik, SWP) has published influential policy analyses on autonomous weapons governance.

## ECHR & EU

### CCW Group of Governmental Experts (GGE)

The CCW GGE on LAWS has met since 2014, with formal mandate from the Fifth Review Conference of the CCW (2016). Despite extensive discussions, the GGE has not reached consensus on a binding instrument. Key positions:

- **Ban advocates (Austria, Costa Rica, Colombia, others):** Support a preemptive prohibition on fully autonomous weapons through a new legally binding protocol to the CCW or a standalone treaty.
- **Regulation advocates (Germany, France, UK):** Support development of norms, guidelines, and a political declaration emphasizing human control without a binding ban.
- **Opposition to restrictions (United States, Russia, India, South Korea):** Argue that existing IHL is sufficient, that autonomous weapons could reduce civilian casualties, and that premature regulation could inhibit beneficial technology development.

### ICRC Position (2021)

The International Committee of the Red Cross issued its position on autonomous weapons systems in May 2021, recommending:

- Prohibition on autonomous weapons systems that are designed or used to apply force against persons (anti-personnel autonomous weapons).
- Prohibition on autonomous weapons systems whose effects cannot be sufficiently understood, predicted, and explained.
- Requirement for human control sufficient to ensure compliance with IHL for all other autonomous weapons, including constraints on target types, geographic scope, duration of operation, and scale of use.

### European Parliament Resolutions

The European Parliament has adopted multiple resolutions calling for international action on autonomous weapons:

- **Resolution of September 12, 2018 (2018/2752(RSP)):** Called for a legally binding international instrument prohibiting LAWS that lack meaningful human control over critical functions of selecting and attacking targets.
- **Resolution of January 17, 2024:** Reiterated the call for a ban and urged EU member states to pursue an international agreement.

### Political Declaration on Responsible Military Use of AI and Autonomy (2023)

Launched by the United States and the Netherlands at the Responsible AI in the Military Domain Summit (REAIM) in February 2023, the Political Declaration has been endorsed by over 50 states. The Declaration commits endorsing states to implementing practices including human oversight of AI in weapons systems, rigorous testing and evaluation, training requirements, and mechanisms to prevent unintended escalation.

### Applicable IHL Principles

The debate centers on whether autonomous weapons can comply with fundamental IHL principles:

**Distinction (Additional Protocol I, Article 48):** Parties to a conflict must distinguish between civilians and combatants and direct attacks only against military objectives. Whether an AI system can reliably make distinction determinations in complex environments remains a central question.

**Proportionality (Additional Protocol I, Article 51(5)(b)):** Attacks expected to cause incidental civilian harm excessive in relation to the concrete and direct military advantage anticipated are prohibited. Proportionality assessments require contextual human judgment that autonomous systems may not be capable of replicating.

**Precaution (Additional Protocol I, Article 57):** Parties must take all feasible precautions to minimize civilian harm. The precautionary obligation may be enhanced or undermined by autonomous systems depending on their capability and reliability.

**Martens Clause (Preamble to Hague Convention IV, 1907):** In cases not covered by specific treaty law, civilians and combatants remain under the protection of the principles of humanity and the dictates of public conscience. The Martens Clause is invoked by ban advocates as a basis for prohibiting weapons that remove human moral agency from lethal decision-making.

## Comparative Analysis

### Human Control Spectrum

The jurisdictions represent different positions on the required level of human control. The United States requires "appropriate levels" of human judgment, a flexible standard that could accommodate varying degrees of autonomy. Germany advocates for "meaningful human control" (bedeutsame menschliche Kontrolle), a stricter standard implying substantive human involvement in engagement decisions. South Korea maintains operational human control requirements for deployed systems while opposing legal mandates. The ICRC recommends control sufficient to ensure IHL compliance.

### Development vs. Governance

A fundamental tension exists between military technology development and governance. The United States and South Korea prioritize maintaining technological advantage and resist binding restrictions. Germany and the EU Parliament prioritize IHL compliance and human dignity concerns. The lack of consensus has prevented any binding international instrument despite over a decade of negotiations.

### Constitutional and Legal Constraints

Germany's constitutional framework (Articles 25 and 26 GG, Parlamentsvorbehalt) provides the strongest domestic legal constraints on autonomous weapons deployment. South Korea's defense procurement framework provides institutional review but no autonomous-weapons-specific legal constraints. The United States' DoD Directive is policy rather than law, changeable by executive decision.

### Arms Export Implications

All three national jurisdictions are major arms exporters, and autonomous weapons export raises additional concerns. Germany's restrictive arms export policy (Politische Grundsätze der Bundesregierung für den Export von Kriegswaffen und sonstigen Rüstungsgütern) and the EU Common Position 2008/944/CFSP would apply to autonomous weapons exports. The United States' Arms Export Control Act and ITAR framework would regulate autonomous weapons transfers.
