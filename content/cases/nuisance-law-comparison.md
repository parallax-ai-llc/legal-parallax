## Summary

Nuisance law -- the body of legal principles governing unreasonable interference with the use and enjoyment of land or with rights common to the general public -- occupies a critical intersection between tort law, property law, and environmental regulation, functioning as both a private remedy for landowners and a public regulatory tool against pollution, noise, and other community harms. South Korea addresses nuisance through the Civil Act (민법) provisions on immissions (생활방해), particularly the neighbor relations provisions of Articles 217 through 236, supplemented by the Environmental Dispute Adjustment Act (환경분쟁 조정법) of 1991 and the Noise and Vibration Control Act (소음·진동관리법), with courts applying a threshold of "tolerable limit" (수인한도) beyond which interference becomes actionable -- a doctrine derived from German Nachbarrecht principles. The United States addresses nuisance through both private nuisance (unreasonable interference with a particular landowner's use and enjoyment of property) and public nuisance (unreasonable interference with rights common to the general public), with the doctrine recently deployed in novel litigation against opioid manufacturers, fossil fuel companies, and firearm manufacturers under public nuisance theories. Germany regulates immissions through BGB Section 906 (Zuführung unwägbarer Stoffe), which establishes a detailed framework distinguishing between material and immaterial impairments, with a tolerance threshold linked to statutory pollution limits, supplemented by the Federal Immission Control Act (Bundes-Immissionsschutzgesetz, BImSchG). At the EU level, the Environmental Liability Directive 2004/35/CE addresses environmental damage through the polluter-pays principle, while the ECHR has developed extensive jurisprudence under Article 8 (right to respect for private life and home) holding states liable for failure to protect individuals against environmental nuisance, most notably in López Ostra v. Spain (1994) and Fadeyeva v. Russia (2005).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's nuisance law (생활방해법) is rooted in the Civil Act (민법) neighbor relations provisions and supplemented by extensive environmental regulatory legislation.

### Civil Act Provisions

Article 217 of the Civil Act provides the foundation: "An owner of land shall not cause gas, steam, smoke, heat, dust, noise, vibration, or similar substances arising from the use of the land to interfere with the use of neighboring land exceeding the tolerable limit" (토지소유자는 매연, 열기체, 액체, 음향, 진동 기타 이에 유사한 것으로 이웃 토지의 사용을 방해하거나 이웃 거주자의 생활에 고통을 주지 아니하도록 적당한 조처를 할 의무가 있다). The "tolerable limit" (수인한도) is assessed by considering the nature and severity of the interference, the character of the locality, land use, the priority of use, and whether the defendant took reasonable preventive measures.

### Tolerable Limit Doctrine (수인한도론)

The Supreme Court of Korea has developed a comprehensive multi-factor test for the tolerable limit, as articulated in Supreme Court Decision 2004Da37904 (2005):

| Factor | Consideration |
|--------|--------------|
| Nature and degree of harm | Physical harm, property damage, mental distress |
| Character of the locality | Residential, commercial, industrial zoning |
| Land use conformity | Whether activity complies with zoning and environmental regulations |
| Priority of establishment | Whether plaintiff or defendant established use first |
| Preventive measures | Whether defendant took feasible measures to reduce interference |
| Public interest | Social utility of the defendant's activity |
| Regulatory compliance | Whether statutory environmental standards are met |

### Environmental Dispute Resolution

The Environmental Dispute Adjustment Act (환경분쟁 조정법), enacted in 1991, established the Central Environmental Dispute Mediation Committee (중앙환경분쟁조정위원회) and regional committees to provide alternative dispute resolution for environmental nuisance claims. The committees handle mediation, conciliation, and arbitration of disputes involving noise, vibration, air pollution, water pollution, and soil contamination. Decisions by the committee in arbitration proceedings have binding effect equivalent to court judgments.

### Key Cases

**Gimpo Airport Noise Case (Supreme Court Decision 2010Da54293, 2015):** The Supreme Court held that aircraft noise exceeding 75 WECPNL (Weighted Equivalent Continuous Perceived Noise Level) constituted nuisance exceeding the tolerable limit, entitling residents near Gimpo International Airport to compensation. The decision established quantitative thresholds for aircraft noise nuisance in Korean law.

**Seoul-Busan Expressway Noise Case:** Courts ordered the Korea Expressway Corporation to install noise barriers and pay compensation to residents along the expressway whose homes were exposed to noise exceeding 65 dB(A) during daytime hours.

## United States

### Legal Framework

American nuisance law encompasses two distinct doctrines -- private nuisance and public nuisance -- each with different elements, standing requirements, and remedies.

### Private Nuisance

Private nuisance requires proof of substantial and unreasonable interference with the plaintiff's use and enjoyment of land. The Restatement (Second) of Torts Section 822 provides that an invasion is unreasonable if (a) the gravity of the harm outweighs the utility of the actor's conduct, or (b) the harm caused by the conduct is serious and the financial burden of compensating for the harm would not make continuation of the conduct infeasible.

**Boomer v. Atlantic Cement Co., 26 N.Y.2d 219 (1970):** The New York Court of Appeals allowed a cement factory to continue operations despite finding a nuisance, conditioning continued operation on payment of permanent damages to affected landowners. This decision introduced economic balancing into nuisance injunction analysis and became a foundational case in law-and-economics scholarship.

### Public Nuisance

Public nuisance is an unreasonable interference with a right common to the general public. Under the Restatement (Second) of Torts Section 821B, the interference must involve a significant interference with public health, safety, peace, comfort, or convenience. State attorneys general and local governments typically have standing to bring public nuisance claims; private plaintiffs must show "special injury" different in kind from that suffered by the general public.

### Novel Public Nuisance Litigation

Public nuisance theory has been deployed in several waves of mass litigation:

**Opioid Litigation:** In State of Oklahoma v. Johnson & Johnson, the Oklahoma district court initially found Johnson & Johnson liable for public nuisance, awarding $465 million for the opioid crisis. The Oklahoma Supreme Court reversed in 2021, holding that public nuisance law did not extend to the marketing and sale of lawful products. However, other jurisdictions have permitted opioid public nuisance claims, and the multidistrict litigation (MDL 2804) resulted in settlements exceeding $50 billion.

**Climate Change Litigation:** Cities including Oakland, San Francisco, and New York have filed public nuisance claims against fossil fuel companies for climate change-related harms. In City of Oakland v. BP PLC, the Ninth Circuit has addressed federal displacement and standing issues. The Supreme Court denied certiorari in several cases, leaving the question of climate public nuisance largely unresolved.

### Coming to the Nuisance

The "coming to the nuisance" defense -- arguing that the plaintiff voluntarily moved to a location near the existing nuisance source -- is recognized in some jurisdictions but is not an absolute defense. In Spur Industries, Inc. v. Del E. Webb Development Co., 108 Ariz. 178 (1972), the Arizona Supreme Court issued a novel "compensated injunction," ordering the feedlot to relocate but requiring the developer to indemnify the feedlot for reasonable relocation costs.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German nuisance law (Immissionsrecht) is codified primarily in BGB Section 906, supplemented by the comprehensive Federal Immission Control Act (BImSchG) and related technical instructions.

### BGB Section 906 -- Zuführung unwägbarer Stoffe

Section 906 establishes a graduated system of tolerance obligations for immissions (substances transmitted from neighboring land):

**Paragraph 1:** The owner of a plot of land cannot prohibit the introduction of gases, steam, smells, smoke, soot, warmth, noise, vibrations, and similar influences from another plot of land, provided they do not impair or only immaterially impair the use of the land. Impairment is generally immaterial if the limits established by statutes or regulations are not exceeded.

**Paragraph 2, Sentence 1:** The same applies to material impairments if they arise from the customary local use (ortsübliche Benutzung) of the other property and cannot be prevented by economically reasonable measures.

**Paragraph 2, Sentence 2:** If the landowner must tolerate a material impairment under this provision, they can demand appropriate compensation in money (angemessene Entschädigung in Geld) if the impairment is of such a nature that it interferes with the customary local use of their land or its revenues beyond what is to be expected.

### Technical Instructions on Noise (TA Lärm)

The TA Lärm (Technische Anleitung zum Schutz gegen Lärm), most recently updated in 2017, establishes binding noise limits for industrial, commercial, and other installations subject to the BImSchG:

| Zone Type | Daytime Limit (6:00-22:00) | Nighttime Limit (22:00-6:00) |
|-----------|---------------------------|------------------------------|
| Industrial areas | 70 dB(A) | 70 dB(A) |
| Commercial areas | 65 dB(A) | 50 dB(A) |
| Mixed-use areas | 60 dB(A) | 45 dB(A) |
| General residential areas | 55 dB(A) | 40 dB(A) |
| Pure residential areas | 50 dB(A) | 35 dB(A) |
| Health/recreation areas | 45 dB(A) | 35 dB(A) |

### Climate Nuisance: Lliuya v. RWE

In one of the most significant climate liability cases globally, Peruvian farmer Saúl Luciano Lliuya filed suit against RWE AG in the Essen Regional Court in 2015, alleging that RWE's greenhouse gas emissions contributed to glacial melt threatening his home. The Hamm Higher Regional Court ruled in November 2017 that the claim was admissible in principle, ordering an evidentiary phase on the causal connection between RWE's emissions and the glacial threat. The case remains pending and could establish a precedent for nuisance-based climate liability under BGB Section 1004 (removal and injunction claims) in conjunction with Section 906.

### Remedies

The primary remedy for nuisance under German law is the injunction claim under BGB Section 1004 (Beseitigungs- und Unterlassungsanspruch), which allows the affected landowner to demand cessation and prevention of the interference. Compensation under Section 906(2) sentence 2 is available when the immission must be tolerated because it arises from customary local use.

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR has developed groundbreaking environmental nuisance jurisprudence under Article 8 (right to respect for private and family life and home).

**López Ostra v. Spain (Application No. 16798/90, Judgment of December 9, 1994):** The Court held that severe environmental pollution may affect individuals' well-being and prevent them from enjoying their homes in such a way as to affect their private and family life adversely, constituting a violation of Article 8. Spain was found liable for failing to take adequate measures against a waste treatment plant's noxious fumes near the applicant's home.

**Fadeyeva v. Russia (Application No. 55723/00, Judgment of June 9, 2005):** The Court found Russia in violation of Article 8 for failing to resettle the applicant from the "sanitary security zone" around a steel plant in Cherepovets, despite pollution levels exceeding national health standards. The Court held that the state had a positive obligation to prevent industrial pollution from reaching levels that seriously endangered health and well-being.

**Klimaseniorinnen v. Switzerland (Application No. 53600/20, Grand Chamber Judgment of April 9, 2024):** In a landmark climate decision, the Grand Chamber found Switzerland in violation of Article 8 for failing to adopt sufficient measures to combat climate change. The Court held that Article 8 encompasses a right for individuals to effective protection by state authorities from the serious adverse effects of climate change on their lives, health, well-being, and quality of life.

### EU Environmental Noise Directive

Directive 2002/49/EC (Environmental Noise Directive) requires member states to produce strategic noise maps for major roads, railways, airports, and urban agglomerations, and to draw up action plans to manage noise issues and reduce noise pollution. The Directive does not set binding noise limits but mandates transparent noise assessment and public participation in noise action planning.

### EU Environmental Liability Directive

Directive 2004/35/CE establishes liability for environmental damage (damage to protected species and habitats, water, and land) based on the polluter-pays principle. Operators of activities listed in Annex III bear strict liability; operators of other activities bear fault-based liability. The Directive addresses environmental damage at the public/regulatory level rather than as private nuisance claims.

## Comparative Analysis

### Tolerance Thresholds

All four jurisdictions employ some form of threshold analysis to distinguish between tolerable and intolerable interference, but the thresholds differ significantly:

**South Korea:** The tolerable limit (수인한도) is a flexible multi-factor test weighing harm severity, locality character, land use priority, regulatory compliance, and public interest. Courts exercise broad discretion.

**United States:** The "unreasonable interference" standard balances the gravity of harm against the utility of the defendant's conduct, as formulated in the Restatement (Second) of Torts. Economic considerations play a prominent role.

**Germany:** BGB Section 906 creates a structured hierarchy: immaterial impairments must be tolerated; material impairments must be tolerated if they result from customary local use and cannot be prevented by economically reasonable measures. Regulatory compliance creates a rebuttable presumption of immateriality.

### Private vs. Public Enforcement

American law uniquely permits broad public nuisance claims by governmental entities and, in limited circumstances, private plaintiffs with special injury. Korean and German law focus primarily on private neighbor-to-neighbor disputes, with environmental regulation handled through administrative law. The ECHR approach addresses state failure to regulate environmental nuisance through the positive obligations framework.

### Climate Litigation

Environmental nuisance doctrine is increasingly deployed in climate change litigation across all four jurisdictions. The United States leads in volume of climate nuisance litigation, Germany hosts the potentially precedent-setting Lliuya v. RWE case, and the ECHR's Klimaseniorinnen decision establishes a human rights framework for climate protection obligations.
