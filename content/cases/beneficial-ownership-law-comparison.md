## Summary

Beneficial ownership transparency -- the legal requirement to identify and disclose the natural persons who ultimately own or control legal entities -- has emerged as a central pillar of global anti-money laundering and anti-corruption efforts, driven by high-profile scandals including the Panama Papers (2016), Paradise Papers (2017), and Pandora Papers (2021) that revealed the widespread use of opaque corporate structures to conceal illicit wealth. South Korea has progressively strengthened beneficial ownership disclosure through the Act on Reporting and Using Specified Financial Transaction Information (특정 금융거래정보의 보고 및 이용 등에 관한 법률), commonly known as the Specific Financial Information Act, and the Financial Investment Services and Capital Markets Act (자본시장과 금융투자업에 관한 법률), requiring financial institutions to identify beneficial owners with a threshold of 25 percent ownership or control, along with the recent 2022 amendments mandating enhanced due diligence for high-risk jurisdictions. The United States enacted the Corporate Transparency Act (CTA) in January 2021 as part of the National Defense Authorization Act, establishing for the first time a federal beneficial ownership reporting requirement to the Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN), though implementation has faced legal challenges including the Supreme Court's consideration of the Act's constitutionality. Germany implemented the Transparenzregister (Transparency Register) through the Geldwäschegesetz (GwG, Anti-Money Laundering Act) transposing the EU's Fourth and Fifth Anti-Money Laundering Directives, converting it from a catch-all register to a full register (Vollregister) in August 2021, requiring all legal entities to report beneficial ownership information regardless of whether the information is available from other public registers. At the European level, the EU has been the global leader in beneficial ownership transparency through the Anti-Money Laundering Directives (AMLD4 and AMLD5), which require member states to establish central beneficial ownership registers, with AMLD5 mandating public access -- though the CJEU's landmark decision in WM and Sovim SA v. Luxembourg (Joined Cases C-37/20 and C-601/20, November 22, 2022) invalidated the public access requirement as incompatible with fundamental rights under Articles 7 and 8 of the EU Charter, prompting the adoption of the comprehensive AML Package in 2024 including the Anti-Money Laundering Regulation (AMLR) and the creation of the EU Anti-Money Laundering Authority (AMLA).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's beneficial ownership transparency regime operates primarily through anti-money laundering legislation rather than a dedicated beneficial ownership act. The key statutes are:

**Specific Financial Information Act (특정 금융거래정보의 보고 및 이용 등에 관한 법률):** This Act, originally enacted in 2001 and substantially amended in 2020 and 2022, establishes the customer due diligence (CDD) framework through which financial institutions must identify beneficial owners of their customers. The Korea Financial Intelligence Unit (KoFIU, 금융정보분석원), housed within the Financial Services Commission (금융위원회), serves as South Korea's financial intelligence unit.

**Customer Due Diligence Requirements:** Under the Act, financial institutions must identify the beneficial owner of any legal person or arrangement that is a customer. A beneficial owner is defined as a natural person who directly or indirectly holds more than 25 percent of the shares or voting rights, or who otherwise exercises control over the legal entity. Where no natural person is identified through ownership, the person who exercises control through other means must be identified.

### 2020 Amendment: Virtual Asset Service Providers

The March 2020 amendment to the Specific Financial Information Act brought virtual asset service providers (VASPs, 가상자산사업자) under the AML framework, requiring them to conduct customer due diligence including beneficial ownership identification. VASPs must register with KoFIU and comply with the same CDD obligations as traditional financial institutions.

### 2022 Enhanced Due Diligence Amendment

The 2022 amendment strengthened enhanced due diligence requirements for customers from high-risk jurisdictions and for politically exposed persons (PEPs). Financial institutions must apply enhanced verification measures for beneficial ownership identification in these cases, including obtaining senior management approval and conducting enhanced ongoing monitoring.

### Enforcement

KoFIU coordinates enforcement of AML obligations including beneficial ownership requirements. Financial institutions that fail to conduct adequate customer due diligence face administrative sanctions including fines, business suspension, and license revocation. The Financial Services Commission imposed aggregate fines exceeding 30 billion KRW on financial institutions for AML compliance failures between 2020 and 2024.

### Limitations

South Korea does not maintain a centralized beneficial ownership register accessible to law enforcement or the public. Beneficial ownership information is held by individual financial institutions and is accessible to KoFIU through information requests. The Financial Action Task Force (FATF) mutual evaluation report on South Korea (2020) noted this as a significant gap and recommended the establishment of a centralized register.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States historically lacked a federal beneficial ownership disclosure requirement, with company formation handled at the state level without mandatory disclosure of natural person owners. This changed with the Corporate Transparency Act (CTA).

**Corporate Transparency Act (31 U.S.C. Sections 5336):** Enacted on January 1, 2021, as part of the National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 2021. The CTA requires "reporting companies" to report beneficial ownership information to the Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN), a bureau of the U.S. Department of the Treasury.

**Reporting Companies:** The CTA applies to corporations, LLCs, and similar entities created by filing a document with a state secretary of state or formed under the law of a foreign country and registered to do business in the United States. Twenty-three categories of entities are exempt, including publicly traded companies (already subject to SEC disclosure), banks, insurance companies, and large operating companies (more than 20 full-time employees, more than $5 million in gross receipts, and operating physical presence in the United States).

**Beneficial Owner Definition:** A natural person who directly or indirectly exercises substantial control over the entity, or who directly or indirectly owns or controls not less than 25 percent of the ownership interests of the entity.

### Implementation Challenges

The CTA's implementation has been marked by significant legal challenges:

**Texas Top Cop Shop v. Garland (2024):** A federal district court in Texas held the CTA unconstitutional, finding that Congress exceeded its enumerated powers. The government appealed, and the case proceeded to the Fifth Circuit, creating a nationwide injunction that was subsequently stayed and reinstated multiple times.

**Reporting Deadlines:** FinCEN established initial reporting deadlines: companies formed before January 1, 2024, were required to file by January 1, 2025; companies formed after January 1, 2024, had 90 days to file. These deadlines were repeatedly extended due to litigation uncertainty.

### FinCEN Database

The Beneficial Ownership Information (BOI) database maintained by FinCEN is not publicly accessible. Access is restricted to federal law enforcement agencies, state and local law enforcement with court authorization, financial institutions with customer consent, and foreign law enforcement through international agreements.

### Bank Secrecy Act Integration

The CTA supplements existing Bank Secrecy Act (BSA) requirements for financial institutions to conduct customer due diligence. FinCEN's Customer Due Diligence (CDD) Rule (2016) already required covered financial institutions to identify and verify beneficial owners of legal entity customers.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's beneficial ownership transparency framework is implemented through the Geldwäschegesetz (GwG, Anti-Money Laundering Act), which transposes EU AMLD4 and AMLD5 into German law.

**Transparenzregister (Transparency Register):** Established in 2017 under the GwG, the Transparenzregister was initially a "catch-all register" (Auffangregister) that only required reporting where beneficial ownership information was not already available from other public registers (such as the Handelsregister or Vereinsregister). The Transparenz-Finanzinformationsgesetz (TraFinG, August 2021) converted it to a full register (Vollregister), requiring all legal entities to report beneficial ownership information directly, regardless of availability in other registers.

**Beneficial Owner Definition (GwG Section 3):** A natural person who directly or indirectly holds more than 25 percent of the capital shares or voting rights of a legal entity, or who exercises control through other means. For foundations (Stiftungen) and trusts (Treuhandverhältnisse), the beneficial owners include the settlor, trustee, protector, beneficiaries, and any natural person exercising ultimate effective control.

### Reporting Obligations

| Entity Type | Reporting Requirement |
|-------------|---------------------|
| GmbH (limited liability company) | Must report beneficial owners to Transparenzregister |
| AG (stock corporation) | Must report beneficial owners |
| KG (limited partnership) | Must report beneficial owners |
| Eingetragener Verein (registered association) | Must report beneficial owners |
| Stiftung (foundation) | Must report beneficial owners including all parties |
| Foreign entities with German real estate | Must report beneficial owners |

### Access Restrictions Post-CJEU Ruling

Following the CJEU's November 2022 ruling invalidating public access to beneficial ownership registers, Germany restricted access to the Transparenzregister. Access is now available to:
- Competent authorities (law enforcement, financial intelligence unit, tax authorities)
- Obliged entities (banks, notaries, lawyers) for customer due diligence purposes
- Persons with a legitimate interest (journalists, NGOs, researchers) upon demonstrating a specific connection to anti-money laundering purposes

### Enforcement

The Bundesverwaltungsamt (Federal Office of Administration) administers the Transparenzregister and enforces reporting obligations. Penalties for non-compliance include fines up to EUR 150,000 for intentional violations and up to EUR 100,000 for negligent violations. For particularly serious cases, fines may reach EUR 1 million under the 2021 amendments.

### Immobilienregister (Real Estate Register)

In response to concerns about money laundering through German real estate, the Sanktionsdurchsetzungsgesetz II (2022) introduced additional transparency requirements for real estate transactions, requiring beneficial ownership disclosure for all property acquisitions and establishing a real estate transaction register accessible to law enforcement.

## ECHR & EU

### Anti-Money Laundering Directives

The EU has established the most comprehensive supranational framework for beneficial ownership transparency:

**Fourth Anti-Money Laundering Directive (AMLD4, Directive 2015/849):** Required member states to establish central beneficial ownership registers for corporate entities and express trusts. Access was limited to competent authorities, obliged entities, and persons with a legitimate interest.

**Fifth Anti-Money Laundering Directive (AMLD5, Directive 2018/843):** Extended public access to corporate beneficial ownership registers (Article 30(5)(c)), requiring member states to make the information accessible to any member of the general public. Also required interconnection of member state registers through the Business Registers Interconnection System (BRIS).

### CJEU Decision: WM and Sovim SA v. Luxembourg (2022)

The landmark judgment in Joined Cases C-37/20 and C-601/20 (November 22, 2022) invalidated AMLD5's public access provision. The Grand Chamber held that:

- Public access to beneficial ownership information constitutes a serious interference with the fundamental rights enshrined in Articles 7 (private life) and 8 (data protection) of the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights.
- The interference is not limited to what is strictly necessary. The provision grants access to an essentially unlimited number of persons without requiring any justification.
- The objective of preventing money laundering and terrorist financing, while legitimate, does not justify such a broad restriction of fundamental rights.
- The provision is therefore invalid.

### 2024 AML Package

In response to the CJEU ruling and broader reform needs, the EU adopted a comprehensive AML Package in 2024:

**Anti-Money Laundering Regulation (AMLR, Regulation (EU) 2024/1624):** Directly applicable regulation establishing harmonized rules for customer due diligence, beneficial ownership, and reporting obligations. Key features:
- Beneficial ownership threshold: 25% or more of ownership or control
- Nominee arrangements must be disclosed
- Multi-layered ownership structures: look-through to ultimate natural person
- Enhanced due diligence for complex structures

**Sixth Anti-Money Laundering Directive (AMLD6, Directive (EU) 2024/1640):** Establishes the framework for beneficial ownership registers with access restricted to:
- Competent authorities: full access
- Obliged entities: access for CDD purposes
- Persons with a legitimate interest: access subject to demonstration of connection to prevention of ML/TF

**Anti-Money Laundering Authority (AMLA):** Regulation (EU) 2024/1620 establishes the EU AMLA, headquartered in Frankfurt am Main, with powers to directly supervise high-risk obliged entities, coordinate national supervisory authorities, and operate the FIU.net system for information exchange between financial intelligence units.

### Council of Europe -- MONEYVAL

The Council of Europe's Committee of Experts on the Evaluation of Anti-Money Laundering Measures (MONEYVAL) conducts mutual evaluations of non-EU Council of Europe member states' AML/CFT frameworks, including beneficial ownership transparency. MONEYVAL assessments follow the FATF methodology.

## Comparative Analysis

### Centralized vs. Distributed Information

A fundamental structural distinction is whether beneficial ownership information is held in a centralized register or distributed across individual institutions. The EU (through AMLD4/5 and the new AMLR) and Germany mandate centralized registers. The United States established the FinCEN BOI database as a centralized federal registry -- a historic shift from the prior decentralized state-by-state approach. South Korea lacks a centralized register, with information held by individual financial institutions, creating potential gaps in the overall beneficial ownership picture.

### Public Access Debate

The public access question represents the most contested policy issue. AMLD5's public access provision was intended to empower journalists, civil society, and researchers to investigate corruption and money laundering. The CJEU's invalidation of this provision on fundamental rights grounds has shifted the debate toward "legitimate interest" access, which provides narrower but rights-compliant transparency. The United States has never provided public access to the FinCEN database, reflecting privacy concerns and the political sensitivity of the CTA.

### Enforcement Effectiveness

The effectiveness of beneficial ownership regimes depends critically on enforcement. The United States faces unique challenges given the scale of entity formation (approximately 5 million new entities per year) and the legal challenges to the CTA. Germany's transition to a full register has significantly improved data coverage but initial compliance rates were low. The EU's creation of AMLA represents a institutional commitment to enforcement at the supranational level.

### Scope of Covered Entities

All jurisdictions use a 25 percent ownership threshold, reflecting the FATF standard. However, the concept of "control" varies in scope and application. The CTA's "substantial control" definition is broader than some European equivalents, encompassing senior officers and persons with significant decision-making authority even without ownership stakes.
