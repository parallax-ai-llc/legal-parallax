## Summary

Workplace safety law -- the regulatory framework governing employers' obligations to protect workers from occupational hazards, injuries, and diseases -- represents a critical intersection of labor regulation, public health, and criminal law that has undergone dramatic transformation following industrial disasters in each jurisdiction. South Korea substantially strengthened its occupational safety regime through the Serious Accidents Punishment Act (중대재해 처벌 등에 관한 법률), enacted on January 26, 2021, and effective January 27, 2022, which imposes criminal liability including imprisonment of up to one year minimum on business owners and CEOs (경영책임자) whose failure to fulfill safety and health obligations results in worker death -- a direct legislative response to a series of fatal industrial accidents including the 2018 Taean thermal power plant death of Kim Yong-gyun, a 24-year-old subcontracted worker; the Act was extended to businesses with 5 or more employees on January 27, 2024. The United States established the modern workplace safety framework through the Occupational Safety and Health Act of 1970 (29 U.S.C. Section 651 et seq.), creating the Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) within the Department of Labor, which promulgates and enforces workplace safety standards, with maximum penalties for willful violations resulting in death raised to $156,259 per violation and criminal penalties of up to six months' imprisonment for first offenses. Germany regulates workplace safety through the dual system of the Arbeitsschutzgesetz (ArbSchG) of 1996, enforced by the state Gewerbeaufsichtsämter (trade supervisory authorities), and the Berufsgenossenschaften (employer liability insurance associations), which establish industry-specific safety rules, conduct inspections, and administer the workers' compensation insurance system under the Sozialgesetzbuch VII. At the European level, the EU Framework Directive 89/391/EEC on Safety and Health at Work establishes fundamental employer obligations including risk assessment, prevention, worker information and training, and consultation, while the European Agency for Safety and Health at Work (EU-OSHA) coordinates research and awareness campaigns, and the EU Strategic Framework on Health and Safety at Work 2021-2027 sets targets for reducing occupational accidents and diseases.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's workplace safety regime is governed by two primary statutes: the Occupational Safety and Health Act (산업안전보건법, OSHA Act), originally enacted in 1981 and comprehensively revised in 2019, and the Serious Accidents Punishment Act (중대재해 처벌 등에 관한 법률), enacted in 2021.

### Serious Accidents Punishment Act (중대재해처벌법)

The Serious Accidents Punishment Act represents a paradigm shift in Korean occupational safety law by imposing direct criminal liability on business owners and chief executive officers (경영책임자등) for fatal workplace accidents.

**Background:** The Act was driven by persistent industrial fatalities. South Korea's occupational fatality rate has consistently been among the highest in the OECD. Key catalyzing incidents include:

| Incident | Date | Details |
|----------|------|---------|
| Taean thermal power plant | December 2018 | Kim Yong-gyun (김용균), 24-year-old subcontracted worker, killed by coal conveyor belt |
| Pyeongtaek warehouse fire | June 2020 | 38 workers killed at Coupang logistics facility under construction |
| SPC Samlip factory | October 2022 | Worker killed in industrial mixer at bakery factory |

**Key Provisions:**

Article 4 establishes that business owners and chief executives bear a duty to establish a safety and health management system, allocate adequate personnel and budget for safety, develop and implement safety procedures, and take corrective action following accidents. Article 6 provides that where a violation of these duties results in the death of one or more workers, the responsible executive faces imprisonment for at least one year or a fine of up to 1 billion KRW. For serious injuries to workers, the penalty is imprisonment for up to 7 years or a fine of up to 100 million KRW.

**Phased Application:** The Act initially applied only to businesses with 50 or more employees (from January 27, 2022) and was extended to businesses with 5 or more employees on January 27, 2024.

### Occupational Safety and Health Act (산업안전보건법)

The revised 2019 OSHA Act (effective January 16, 2020) expanded coverage and strengthened obligations:

- **Subcontractor Protection (Art. 63-66):** The revision placed explicit safety obligations on principal contractors (원청) for subcontracted workers at their worksites, addressing the widespread practice of outsourcing dangerous work to reduce liability.
- **Risk Assessment (Art. 36):** All employers must conduct risk assessments to identify and evaluate hazards.
- **Safety and Health Committee (Art. 24):** Workplaces with 100 or more employees must establish a joint labor-management safety committee.
- **KOSHA (Art. 120-122):** The Korea Occupational Safety and Health Agency (한국산업안전보건공단) provides technical support, conducts research, and administers safety certification programs.

### Enforcement and Penalties

The Ministry of Employment and Labor conducts workplace inspections through regional labor offices. In 2023, inspectors conducted over 35,000 inspections and issued approximately 12,000 corrective orders. The first conviction under the Serious Accidents Punishment Act occurred in November 2023, when the CEO of a logistics company received a suspended prison sentence following a worker fatality.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The Occupational Safety and Health Act of 1970 (OSH Act), signed by President Richard Nixon on December 29, 1970, created the modern federal workplace safety framework. The Act established OSHA within the Department of Labor, the National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health (NIOSH) within what is now the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, and the Occupational Safety and Health Review Commission (OSHRC) as an independent adjudicatory body.

### General Duty Clause

Section 5(a)(1) of the OSH Act provides that each employer "shall furnish to each of his employees employment and a place of employment which are free from recognized hazards that are causing or are likely to cause death or serious physical harm." This General Duty Clause serves as a catch-all provision applicable even where no specific OSHA standard has been promulgated.

### Standards

OSHA promulgates detailed workplace safety standards through formal rulemaking under Section 6 of the OSH Act. Standards are organized by industry sector:

| Standard Category | Code | Key Areas |
|------------------|------|-----------|
| General Industry | 29 CFR Part 1910 | Hazard communication, electrical, PPE, machine guarding |
| Construction | 29 CFR Part 1926 | Fall protection, scaffolding, excavation, cranes |
| Maritime | 29 CFR Parts 1915-1919 | Shipyard employment, marine terminals |
| Agriculture | 29 CFR Part 1928 | Tractors, field sanitation, logging |

### Penalties

| Violation Type | Maximum Penalty (2025) |
|---------------|----------------------|
| Serious | $16,131 per violation |
| Other-than-serious | $16,131 per violation |
| Willful | $161,323 per violation |
| Repeat | $161,323 per violation |
| Failure to abate | $16,131 per day |
| Criminal (willful violation causing death) | $250,000 (individual) / $500,000 (organization); up to 6 months imprisonment |
| Criminal (repeat) | Up to 1 year imprisonment |

### State Plans

Section 18 of the OSH Act permits states to administer their own occupational safety programs provided they are "at least as effective as" the federal program. As of 2025, 22 states and territories operate OSHA-approved state plans covering private and public sector workers, while 7 additional states cover only public sector employees.

### Limitations and Criticism

OSHA has faced persistent criticism for inadequate staffing. With approximately 1,850 federal inspectors covering approximately 8 million workplaces, it has been estimated that OSHA can inspect each workplace approximately once every 165 years at current staffing levels. Criminal penalties for willful violations causing death are limited to a maximum of six months' imprisonment -- a misdemeanor -- which critics argue is insufficient deterrence. The Protecting America's Workers Act, which would have increased criminal penalties to felony level, has been repeatedly introduced but never enacted.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's workplace safety system operates through a unique dual structure combining state regulatory authority and employer self-governance through the Berufsgenossenschaften.

### Arbeitsschutzgesetz (ArbSchG)

The Arbeitsschutzgesetz (Occupational Safety Act), enacted on August 7, 1996, implements EU Framework Directive 89/391/EEC. Key provisions include:

**Risk Assessment (§ 5):** Employers must assess workplace hazards, including those arising from work organization, work equipment, working conditions, physical, chemical, and biological agents, and psychosocial factors (explicitly added by the 2013 amendment addressing mental health).

**Prevention Principles (§ 4):** Work must be organized to avoid hazards to life and health; unavoidable hazards must be minimized at the source; individual protective measures are subordinate to collective measures; and the state of the art in technology, medicine, and hygiene must be observed.

**Worker Rights (§ 9):** Workers have the right to refuse work in the event of immediate serious danger, and may not suffer adverse consequences for exercising this right.

### Berufsgenossenschaften (BG)

The Berufsgenossenschaften (employer liability insurance associations) are organized by industry sector and perform a dual function: they establish industry-specific safety rules (Unfallverhütungsvorschriften, UVV), conduct workplace inspections through their own technical inspectors (Aufsichtspersonen), and administer the statutory accident insurance system under Sozialgesetzbuch VII (SGB VII). Unlike in the U.S. and South Korea, where a government agency handles both regulation and inspection, Germany delegates substantial inspection and standard-setting authority to these quasi-public self-governing bodies.

There are currently 9 commercial BGs and additional public-sector accident insurance institutions. Key BGs include the BG Bau (construction), BG ETEM (energy, textile, electrical, media), and BGHW (trade and logistics).

### Criminal Liability

Germany addresses workplace safety violations through general criminal provisions in the StGB rather than through specific occupational safety criminal statutes:

| StGB Section | Offense | Application to Workplace Safety |
|-------------|---------|-------------------------------|
| § 222 | Negligent homicide (Fahrlässige Tötung) | Death of worker due to employer negligence |
| § 229 | Negligent bodily harm (Fahrlässige Körperverletzung) | Injury of worker due to employer negligence |
| § 319 | Building structure offenses (Baugefährdung) | Unsafe construction practices |

Organizational liability (Verbandsgeldbuße) is possible under Section 30 of the Ordnungswidrigkeitengesetz (OWiG), allowing fines of up to EUR 10 million against legal entities for regulatory violations committed by their officers.

### Safety Performance

Germany has one of the lowest occupational fatality rates among industrialized nations: 0.7 per 100,000 workers in 2022, compared to 3.4 in the United States and 1.63 in South Korea. This performance is attributed to the comprehensive dual oversight system, strong works council involvement in safety matters under Section 87(1)(7) of the Betriebsverfassungsgesetz, and the prevention-oriented approach of the Berufsgenossenschaften.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Framework Directive 89/391/EEC

Adopted on June 12, 1989, the Framework Directive on Safety and Health at Work establishes fundamental employer obligations applicable across all member states:

**General Obligations (Article 5):** The employer has a duty to ensure the safety and health of workers in every aspect related to work. This obligation is non-delegable.

**Risk Assessment (Article 6(3)):** Employers must evaluate occupational risks, integrate prevention into all activities, and adapt work to the individual.

**Prevention Hierarchy (Article 6(2)):** Measures must follow a defined hierarchy: avoiding risks, evaluating unavoidable risks, combating risks at source, adapting work to the individual, adapting to technical progress, replacing dangerous with non-dangerous, developing a coherent prevention policy, prioritizing collective over individual protection, and giving appropriate instructions.

**Worker Information and Training (Articles 10-12):** Workers and their representatives must receive adequate information about safety risks and protective measures, and sufficient training upon hiring, transfer, and introduction of new equipment.

The Framework Directive is supplemented by over 20 individual directives addressing specific risks including work equipment (Directive 2009/104/EC), personal protective equipment (Directive 89/656/EEC), carcinogens and mutagens (Directive 2004/37/EC, amended by Directive (EU) 2022/431), and asbestos (Directive 2009/148/EC).

### CJEU Jurisprudence

**Commission v. United Kingdom (Case C-127/05, Judgment of June 14, 2007):** The Court found that the UK had failed to properly transpose the Framework Directive by limiting employer obligations to what was "reasonably practicable." The CJEU held that the Directive requires an absolute obligation to ensure worker safety, subject only to force majeure.

**Stark v. Kärntner Landesregierung (Case C-350/23, Judgment of 2024):** The Court addressed the scope of the Framework Directive's application to self-employed workers, further clarifying the personal scope of EU safety obligations.

### EU Strategic Framework on Health and Safety at Work 2021-2027

Adopted on June 28, 2021, the Strategic Framework sets three key objectives: anticipating and managing change (including digital transition, AI, and climate-related risks), improving prevention of workplace accidents and diseases (targeting a "Vision Zero" approach to work-related deaths), and increasing preparedness for future health crises (lessons from COVID-19). The Framework includes a commitment to update exposure limits for lead and diisocyanates and to propose an EU-level occupational exposure limit for asbestos.

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR has addressed workplace safety primarily through Article 2 (right to life) positive obligations. In Öneryıldız v. Turkey (Application No. 48939/99, Grand Chamber Judgment of November 30, 2004), the Court held that the state's failure to enforce safety regulations at a methane gas dump that caused an explosion killing 39 residents violated Articles 2 and 13. The judgment established that states must establish an adequate legislative and administrative framework to deter threats to the right to life, including industrial hazards.

## Comparative Analysis

### Criminal Liability for Management

South Korea's Serious Accidents Punishment Act represents the most aggressive approach to corporate criminal liability for workplace fatalities, with a mandatory minimum of one year imprisonment for CEOs whose safety failures result in worker death. Germany relies on general criminal negligence provisions without specific CEO targeting. The United States imposes only misdemeanor-level criminal penalties for willful violations causing death -- widely criticized as inadequate.

### Regulatory Architecture

The three jurisdictions represent three distinct regulatory models: South Korea uses a centralized government agency (KOSHA) with ministry enforcement; the United States uses a federal agency (OSHA) with optional state delegation; Germany uses a unique dual system combining state regulatory authorities and industry-based self-governing insurance associations (Berufsgenossenschaften).

### Subcontractor Safety

South Korea's 2019 OSHA Act revision directly addresses the challenge of subcontracted workers, who face disproportionately higher fatality rates. The Kim Yong-gyun case highlighted how outsourcing dangerous work to subcontractors can create accountability gaps. Germany addresses this through the Arbeitnehmerüberlassungsgesetz and BG oversight of worksites regardless of employment relationships. The U.S. OSHA applies the "multi-employer worksite" doctrine, holding controlling and creating employers responsible for hazards affecting all workers at a site.
