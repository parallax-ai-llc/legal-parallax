## Summary

Satellite communications regulation encompasses the licensing of satellite systems, allocation and coordination of radio-frequency spectrum, management of orbital positions, regulation of mega-constellations, orbital debris mitigation, and liability for damage caused by space objects. This field operates at the intersection of national telecommunications law, international space law treaties, and the regulatory framework of the International Telecommunication Union (ITU). South Korea regulates satellite communications through its Space Development Promotion Act (우주개발 진흥법) and the Radio Waves Act (전파법), with the Korea Aerospace Administration (KASA, established 2024) serving as the primary coordinating body. The United States maintains the world's most developed satellite licensing regime through the Federal Communications Commission (spectrum licensing under 47 U.S.C. §§ 301-399), the Federal Aviation Administration (launch and reentry licensing under 51 U.S.C. §§ 50901-50923), and the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (remote sensing licensing under 51 U.S.C. § 60101ff). Germany operates within the EU framework while maintaining national satellite licensing authority under the Satellite Data Security Act (SatDSiG) and the Telecommunications Act (TKG), with the German Aerospace Center (DLR) and the Bundesnetzagentur exercising overlapping jurisdiction. The European Union coordinates satellite policy through the EU Space Programme Regulation (Regulation (EU) 2021/696), operates the Galileo navigation and Copernicus earth observation constellations, and is developing the IRIS² sovereign connectivity constellation. International governance rests on the Outer Space Treaty (1967), the Liability Convention (1972), the Registration Convention (1975), and the ITU Radio Regulations.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea has rapidly developed its satellite regulatory framework as part of its ambition to become a major space-faring nation:

- **Space Development Promotion Act (우주개발 진흥법, Act No. 7538, as amended):** The foundational legislation for South Korea's space activities. Article 8 requires government registration of all space objects launched by South Korean entities or from South Korean territory. Article 11 mandates pre-launch safety reviews. The 2024 amendments expanded the Act's scope to address commercial satellite constellations, requiring operators to submit orbital debris mitigation plans and end-of-life disposal strategies.

- **Radio Waves Act (전파법):** Governs spectrum allocation and management, including satellite frequency assignments. Article 6 establishes the Radio Spectrum Policy Committee under the Ministry of Science and ICT, which allocates satellite frequency bands consistent with ITU allocations. Article 19 requires licensing for satellite earth stations and satellite service providers. The KCC coordinates South Korea's ITU filings for orbital positions and frequency assignments.

- **Space Damage Compensation Act (우주손해배상법, enacted 2024):** Establishes the domestic liability framework for damage caused by space objects. Article 3 imposes strict liability on launch operators and satellite operators for damage caused to third parties on the ground or to aircraft in flight, implementing the Outer Space Treaty Article VII and the Liability Convention domestically. Article 7 requires mandatory insurance coverage, with minimum coverage amounts set by Presidential Decree at KRW 200 billion (approximately USD 150 million) for launch operations.

- **Korea Aerospace Administration (KASA, established May 2024):** Created as an independent government agency modeled on NASA and JAXA, KASA consolidates satellite and space regulation previously distributed across multiple ministries. KASA is responsible for satellite mission approval, orbital coordination, debris tracking, and international space cooperation.

- **Satellite Broadcasting Act (위성방송법):** Regulates direct-to-home satellite broadcasting services, requiring Korean content quotas and advertising standards compliance for satellite television operators.

### Key Principles

South Korea's satellite regulatory philosophy balances rapid space industry development with international obligations. The government's "Space Economy Roadmap 2045" (2023) targets a domestic satellite industry valued at KRW 15 trillion by 2045, encompassing communications, earth observation, and navigation augmentation satellites.

A distinctive feature is South Korea's emphasis on satellite-based internet connectivity for the Korean Peninsula's mountainous terrain, where terrestrial broadband deployment is economically challenging. The government has authorized Korea Telecom's satellite broadband subsidiary to deploy a low-earth orbit (LEO) constellation of 2,000 satellites for domestic coverage, with licensing beginning in 2025.

South Korea also faces unique geopolitical considerations in satellite regulation. Launches must avoid overflight of North Korean territory, limiting available launch azimuths from the Naro Space Center. South Korea's satellite operations coordinate closely with the US Space Surveillance Network for space situational awareness, governed by a bilateral agreement signed in 2014.

### Notable Cases

- **Naro-1 (KSLV-I) Launch Failure Investigation (2009-2010):** The failure of South Korea's first domestically assembled satellite launch vehicle triggered a comprehensive review of launch licensing procedures, resulting in amendments to the Space Development Promotion Act strengthening pre-launch safety review requirements and establishing the independent Launch Safety Review Board.

- **KCC v. Unauthorized Satellite Earth Station Operators (2022):** The KCC fined three companies a combined KRW 1.2 billion for operating satellite earth stations without valid frequency licenses under the Radio Waves Act, establishing that unauthorized reception of foreign satellite signals for commercial redistribution constitutes a violation of Article 19.

- **SpaceX Starlink Market Access Application (2024):** SpaceX's application to the KCC for satellite internet service provision in South Korea triggered a regulatory review of foreign satellite operator market access conditions, resulting in the KCC establishing a new "Foreign Satellite Service Provider" licensing category with requirements for local data storage, emergency service cooperation, and spectrum coordination with Korean satellite operators.

## United States

### Federal Framework

The United States possesses the world's most comprehensive and well-developed satellite regulatory framework, reflecting its position as the largest commercial satellite market:

- **Communications Act of 1934, as amended (47 U.S.C. §§ 301-399):** The FCC regulates all non-federal use of radio spectrum under Title III. FCC Part 25 rules (47 C.F.R. Part 25) establish the licensing framework for satellite communications, including application procedures, technical standards, orbital debris mitigation requirements, and market access for non-U.S.-licensed satellites. The FCC processes approximately 200 satellite license applications annually.

- **FCC Second Report and Order on Space Innovation (2020) and Orbital Debris Mitigation Rules (2022):** The FCC adopted the most significant update to U.S. satellite licensing rules in decades. The 2022 orbital debris rule (adopted 29 September 2022) requires all LEO satellites licensed by the FCC to deorbit within 5 years of mission completion, replacing the prior 25-year guideline. This rule applies to all satellites authorized or granted market access by the FCC, giving it extraterritorial effect for foreign operators seeking U.S. market access.

- **Commercial Space Launch Competitiveness Act (51 U.S.C. §§ 50901-50923):** The FAA's Office of Commercial Space Transportation (AST) licenses commercial launches and reentries. Launch license requirements include payload review, safety analysis, environmental review (under NEPA), and financial responsibility (third-party liability insurance) of at least $500 million per launch or the maximum probable loss, whichever is less.

- **Land Remote Sensing Policy Act (51 U.S.C. § 60101ff):** NOAA licenses private remote sensing satellite systems. The 2020 regulations (15 C.F.R. Part 960, revised) streamlined the licensing process while maintaining national security review through an interagency process.

- **Foreign Ownership Restrictions (47 U.S.C. § 310(b)):** Limits foreign ownership of FCC licensees to 20% directly and 25% indirectly (with FCC discretion to allow higher indirect ownership). The FCC's 2013 "Foreign Ownership Declaratory Ruling" established a streamlined process for reviewing foreign ownership above the 25% benchmark on a case-by-case basis.

- **ITU Coordination:** The FCC and NTIA (National Telecommunications and Information Administration) coordinate U.S. ITU satellite filings. The U.S. holds more ITU-registered orbital positions and frequency assignments than any other nation, reflecting both historical priority and ongoing commercial demand.

### State Variations

While satellite regulation is primarily federal, states play roles in:

- **Spaceport Licensing:** States including Florida (Cape Canaveral), Texas (Boca Chica), Virginia (Wallops), California (Vandenberg), Alaska (Kodiak), and New Mexico (Spaceport America) maintain state-level spaceport infrastructure and coordinate with FAA for launch site operations.

- **Space Commerce Incentives:** Florida's Space Florida (§ 331.302, Fla. Stat.) and Texas's Texas Aerospace Commission provide tax incentives, regulatory support, and infrastructure development for satellite and launch operations.

### Notable Cases

- **FCC v. Dish Network (Orbital Debris, 2023):** The FCC fined Dish Network $150,000 for failing to deorbit its EchoStar-7 satellite to the proper graveyard orbit, marking the first-ever FCC enforcement action for orbital debris rule violations. While the fine amount was modest, the precedent established the FCC's willingness to enforce debris mitigation obligations.

- **SpaceX Starlink Constellation Licensing (2018-2025):** The FCC's iterative licensing of SpaceX's Starlink constellation -- from the initial 4,425-satellite authorization (2018) through subsequent modifications totaling over 12,000 satellites and the pending Gen2 application for up to 30,000 additional satellites -- has been the most consequential satellite licensing proceeding in FCC history, raising fundamental questions about spectrum sharing, orbital congestion, and environmental review of mega-constellations.

- **Ligado Networks L-Band Controversy (2020-2024):** The FCC's approval of Ligado Networks' plan to repurpose L-Band spectrum near GPS frequencies for terrestrial mobile broadband, over the objections of the Department of Defense and GPS industry, illustrates the tension between spectrum efficiency and protection of existing satellite services. The D.C. Circuit upheld the FCC's decision in 2023.

- **Viasat v. FCC (SpaceX Starlink Environmental Review, D.C. Cir., 2023):** Viasat challenged the FCC's decision to authorize SpaceX's Starlink constellation modification without a full environmental review under NEPA, arguing that the deployment of thousands of additional satellites required environmental impact assessment for atmospheric effects and light pollution. The D.C. Circuit dismissed the petition, but the case highlighted growing concerns about cumulative environmental impacts of mega-constellations.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany regulates satellite communications through a combination of national legislation, EU framework implementation, and participation in European Space Agency (ESA) programs:

- **Satellite Data Security Act (Satellitendatensicherheitsgesetz, SatDSiG, 2007):** Germany's primary national space law regarding satellite-derived data. The Act regulates the distribution of high-resolution satellite imagery and geospatial data for national security purposes. Operators of high-resolution earth observation satellites must obtain a license from the Federal Office of Economics and Export Control (BAFA), and the distribution of satellite imagery with resolution finer than specified thresholds requires government approval (sensitivity review under § 17).

- **Telecommunications Act 2021 (TKG):** The BNetzA regulates satellite telecommunications services provided in Germany, including earth station licensing, satellite service provider authorization, and spectrum management. The TKG implements the EU Electronic Communications Code with respect to satellite services, ensuring that satellite broadband operators comply with the same consumer protection and universal service obligations as terrestrial operators.

- **Frequency Utilization Plan (Frequenznutzungsplan):** The BNetzA's comprehensive plan allocating radio spectrum for various uses, including satellite communications bands (C-band, Ku-band, Ka-band, V-band). Germany coordinates its satellite frequency assignments through CEPT (European Conference of Postal and Telecommunications Administrations) and the ITU.

- **National Space Strategy (Raumfahrtstrategie, 2023):** The Federal Government's updated space strategy prioritizes: (a) sovereign access to space (supporting Ariane 6 and future European launchers); (b) satellite-based connectivity (participation in IRIS² constellation); (c) space sustainability and debris mitigation; and (d) space-based climate monitoring. Germany is the largest national contributor to ESA (approximately EUR 4 billion annually).

- **Pending National Space Legislation (Weltraumgesetz):** Germany has been developing comprehensive national space legislation since 2020, which would consolidate satellite licensing, launch authorization, and liability provisions into a single statutory framework. As of 2025, the draft law is in interministerial consultation, with enactment expected in 2026-2027.

### Key Principles

Germany's satellite regulatory approach reflects its dual role as both a major ESA contributor and an EU member state implementing EU space policy. The German approach emphasizes:

1. **Multilateral Cooperation:** Germany channels most of its space activities through ESA and EU programs rather than pursuing fully independent satellite programs. However, the military Bundeswehr satellite communications system (SATCOMBw) represents a national sovereign capability.

2. **Data Security Focus:** The SatDSiG reflects Germany's particular sensitivity to satellite imagery security, influenced by Cold War-era reconnaissance concerns and modern national security considerations. The Act's sensitivity review mechanism (Empfindlichkeitsprüfung) for high-resolution satellite data is one of the most rigorous in the world.

3. **Orbital Debris Leadership:** Germany has been a leading advocate for international orbital debris mitigation standards through its work in the Inter-Agency Space Debris Coordination Committee (IADC) and the UN Committee on the Peaceful Uses of Outer Space (COPUOS). The DLR operates the German Space Situational Awareness Centre (GSSAC), providing debris tracking and collision avoidance services.

4. **Industrial Policy Integration:** Germany's satellite regulatory framework is closely linked to its industrial policy, supporting the European satellite manufacturing industry (Airbus Defence and Space, OHB SE) through ESA procurement and national technology development programs.

### Notable Cases

- **BNetzA Satellite Spectrum Reallocation (C-Band, 2022):** The BNetzA managed the partial reallocation of C-band spectrum (3.4-3.8 GHz) from satellite services to 5G terrestrial mobile, balancing satellite operator incumbent rights with the demand for 5G spectrum. Satellite operators received compensation for transition costs and were required to migrate to alternative frequency bands.

- **SatDSiG Enforcement against Unauthorized Satellite Data Distribution (2021):** BAFA imposed administrative fines on a German geospatial data company for distributing high-resolution satellite imagery (sub-50cm resolution) to a client in a sensitive region without obtaining the required sensitivity review approval, marking one of the rare enforcement actions under the SatDSiG.

- **DLR v. European Commission (EU Satellite Procurement, 2023):** DLR challenged the European Commission's procurement procedures for the IRIS² constellation, arguing that the selection criteria underweighted German industrial participation. The dispute was resolved through revised procurement guidelines ensuring "geographical return" principles consistent with ESA practice.

- **OLG München, Satellite Interference Liability (2024):** The Oberlandesgericht München addressed a dispute between two satellite operators over harmful interference caused by one operator's satellite drifting from its assigned orbital position, applying BGB § 823 (delictual liability) and § 906 (analogous application of neighbor law) to determine liability for satellite-to-satellite interference.

## ECHR / EU Framework

### Convention Articles

- **Article 10 -- Freedom of Expression and Information:** Satellite broadcasting is protected under Article 10 as a medium for imparting and receiving information. The ECtHR's jurisprudence on broadcasting regulation, including Groppera Radio AG v. Switzerland (1990, App. No. 10890/84), established that states may regulate the technical aspects of broadcasting (including satellite licensing and frequency allocation) provided that regulations are not used as a pretext for content-based censorship.

- **Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 -- Protection of Property:** Satellite licenses, orbital positions, and frequency assignments constitute "possessions" within the autonomous meaning of Article 1 Protocol No. 1. States' regulatory authority over spectrum allocation must respect property rights, with any deprivation requiring lawful basis, legitimate aim, and proportionality.

### EU Legislative Framework

- **EU Space Programme Regulation (Regulation (EU) 2021/696):** Establishes the legal framework for the EU's space activities, including Galileo (navigation), Copernicus (earth observation), and the forthcoming IRIS² (Infrastructure for Resilience, Interconnectivity and Security by Satellite) sovereign broadband constellation. The European Union Agency for the Space Programme (EUSPA) manages the operational exploitation of Galileo and Copernicus.

- **IRIS² Constellation (Regulation (EU) 2023/588):** Authorized the deployment of a EU sovereign satellite constellation for secure governmental communications and broadband connectivity, with an initial budget of EUR 2.4 billion for 2023-2027. The constellation aims to provide resilient connectivity for EU institutions, member states, and critical infrastructure, while also offering commercial broadband services to reduce dependence on non-EU satellite operators.

- **EU Radio Spectrum Policy Programme (Decision No. 243/2012/EU):** Establishes the policy framework for EU-level spectrum coordination, including satellite frequency bands. The programme promotes efficient spectrum use and harmonized conditions across member states for satellite services.

- **European Electronic Communications Code (Directive 2018/1972):** Articles 45-55 address spectrum management, including provisions for satellite spectrum authorization. Article 47 requires that spectrum allocation for satellite services be coordinated at EU level through the Radio Spectrum Policy Group (RSPG).

- **EU Space Traffic Management Framework (proposed, 2024):** The European Commission proposed a regulation establishing EU rules for space traffic management, including collision avoidance notification requirements, debris mitigation standards, and a space surveillance and tracking (SST) system. The proposed regulation would require all satellites operated by EU entities or serving the EU market to comply with EU debris mitigation standards.

### International Treaties

The international legal framework for satellite regulation rests on five core UN space treaties:

- **Outer Space Treaty (1967):** Article VI requires states to bear international responsibility for national space activities, including those of private entities. Article VII establishes state liability for damage caused by space objects. Article VIII provides that a state on whose registry a space object is carried retains jurisdiction and control.

- **Liability Convention (1972):** Establishes a dual liability regime: absolute liability for damage caused on the surface of the Earth or to aircraft in flight (Article II), and fault-based liability for damage caused elsewhere (Article III, i.e., satellite-to-satellite collisions in orbit). Claims are brought between states, not between private parties.

- **Registration Convention (1975):** Requires launching states to register space objects with both a national registry and the UN Secretary-General, enabling identification and attribution of space objects.

- **ITU Radio Regulations:** The ITU's Radio Regulations, revised at World Radiocommunication Conferences (WRCs), allocate radio spectrum to satellite services and establish procedures for coordinating satellite frequency assignments and orbital positions. The 2023 WRC (WRC-23) addressed spectrum allocation for non-geostationary satellite orbit (NGSO) mega-constellations, establishing new sharing and coordination frameworks for Ka-band and V-band spectrum.

### Key Case Law and Decisions

- **Cosmos 954 Incident (Canada v. USSR, 1978-1981):** The only case brought under the Liability Convention, involving the crash of a nuclear-powered Soviet satellite in northern Canada. The settlement of CAD 3 million established the practical applicability of the Convention's absolute liability regime for ground damage.

- **ITU, Spectrum Filing Disputes (Ongoing):** The ITU's Radio Regulations Board (RRB) regularly adjudicates disputes over satellite frequency coordination, including conflicts between SpaceX's Starlink filings and OneWeb's filings in Ku-band, and between Amazon's Project Kuiper filings and existing satellite operator assignments. These administrative proceedings shape the practical allocation of orbital resources.

- **ECtHR, Groppera Radio AG v. Switzerland (1990, App. No. 10890/84):** The Court upheld Switzerland's refusal to authorize retransmission of satellite broadcasts from a station operating without proper Italian authorization, finding no violation of Article 10 where the regulation pursued the legitimate aim of maintaining international telecommunications order.
