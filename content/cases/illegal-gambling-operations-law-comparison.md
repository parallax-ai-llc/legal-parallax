## Summary

Illegal gambling operations -- the organization, management, or facilitation of gambling activities outside the scope of legal authorization -- occupy a complex regulatory space where criminal law, licensing regimes, consumer protection, and increasingly digital technology intersect. South Korea maintains one of the world's most restrictive gambling regimes, with the Criminal Act (형법 제246조-제249조) criminalizing gambling and gambling operation with penalties of up to five years' imprisonment for operating a gambling establishment, supplemented by the National Gambling Control Commission Act (사행산업통합감독위원회법) and specific authorizing legislation for the handful of legal gambling venues including Kangwon Land -- the only casino where Korean nationals may gamble. The United States addresses illegal gambling through a layered federal-state framework, with the Illegal Gambling Business Act (18 U.S.C. Section 1955) targeting operations that violate state law and involve five or more persons, the Wire Act (18 U.S.C. Section 1084) addressing interstate gambling communications, and the Unlawful Internet Gambling Enforcement Act (UIGEA, 31 U.S.C. Section 5361), alongside a dramatic post-2018 expansion of legal sports betting following the Supreme Court's decision in Murphy v. NCAA, 584 U.S. 453 (2018), which struck down the federal prohibition on state-authorized sports gambling. Germany reformed its gambling framework through the Glücksspielstaatsvertrag 2021 (Interstate Treaty on Gambling), which for the first time established a federal licensing regime for online gambling, online poker, and online slot machines, replacing the previous Länder-based system; violations are prosecuted under Section 284 StGB (Unerlaubte Veranstaltung eines Glücksspiels) with penalties of up to two years' imprisonment. The EU has addressed gambling regulation primarily through the lens of internal market freedoms, with the Court of Justice establishing in Liga Portuguesa (Case C-42/07, 2009) and Placanica (Joined Cases C-338/04 et al., 2007) that member states retain broad discretion to restrict gambling services, while the European Commission withdrew its 2012 Action Plan on online gambling in 2017, effectively abandoning harmonization efforts.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea maintains one of the most restrictive gambling regimes among developed nations.

**Criminal Act Articles 246-249:**

| Offense | Provision | Penalty |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Simple gambling | Art. 246 | Fine up to 5 million KRW |
| Habitual gambling | Art. 246 | Up to 3 years or fine up to 20 million KRW |
| Operating gambling establishment | Art. 247 | Up to 5 years or fine up to 30 million KRW |
| Habitual gambling operation | Art. 247 | Up to 10 years or fine up to 50 million KRW |
| Opening lottery without authorization | Art. 248 | Up to 3 years or fine up to 20 million KRW |

### Legal Gambling Exceptions

South Korea permits gambling only through specifically authorized entities:

| Entity | Type | Korean Nationals Permitted |
|--------|------|---------------------------|
| Kangwon Land | Casino | Yes (only casino for nationals) |
| 16 foreigner-only casinos | Casino | No |
| Korea Racing Authority (한국마사회) | Horse racing | Yes |
| Sports Toto (스포츠토토) | Sports lottery | Yes |
| National Lottery (로또) | Number lottery | Yes |
| Cycle/Motorboat Racing | Pari-mutuel | Yes |

### Kangwon Land

Kangwon Land (강원랜드), opened in 2000, is the sole casino in South Korea where Korean nationals may gamble. Located in Jeongseon County, Gangwon Province, it was established under the Act on Assistance to Development of Abandoned Mine Areas (폐광지역 개발 지원에 관한 특별법) to support the economically depressed former coal mining region. The casino imposes entry restrictions including a maximum of 15 visits per month and a 180,000 KRW loss limit per visit, though enforcement has been criticized as insufficient.

### Online Gambling Enforcement

South Korean authorities have aggressively pursued online gambling operations. The Korea Communications Standards Commission (방송통신심의위원회) maintains a blacklist of gambling websites, and internet service providers are required to block access. The National Police Agency's cybercrime division has conducted numerous operations against illegal online gambling rings, with arrests exceeding 10,000 persons annually in recent years. Penalties for operating illegal online gambling sites can reach ten years' imprisonment under habitual gambling operation provisions.

### Gambling Addiction Concerns

The National Gambling Control Commission (사행산업통합감독위원회), established in 2007, oversees all legal gambling operations and funds gambling addiction treatment and prevention programs. The Commission reports that approximately 5.4 percent of adults exhibited problem gambling behavior as of 2024, significantly above the global average.

## United States

### Legal Framework

**Federal Statutes:**

| Statute | Scope | Penalty |
|---------|-------|---------|
| 18 U.S.C. § 1955 (Illegal Gambling Business Act) | Operations violating state law, 5+ persons, operating 30+ days | Up to 5 years |
| 18 U.S.C. § 1084 (Wire Act) | Interstate wire communications for gambling | Up to 2 years |
| 31 U.S.C. § 5361 (UIGEA) | Processing payments for unlawful internet gambling | Up to 5 years |
| 18 U.S.C. § 1952 (Travel Act) | Interstate travel to further gambling enterprise | Up to 5 years |
| 18 U.S.C. § 1953 | Interstate transportation of gambling devices | Up to 5 years |

### Murphy v. NCAA (2018)

The Supreme Court's decision in Murphy v. NCAA, 584 U.S. 453 (2018), struck down the Professional and Amateur Sports Protection Act (PASPA) of 1992, which had effectively prohibited states from authorizing sports betting. The Court held that PASPA violated the anti-commandeering doctrine by prohibiting states from modifying their own laws to permit sports gambling. Following the decision, more than 38 states and the District of Columbia have legalized some form of sports betting, generating billions in tax revenue.

### Tribal Gaming

The Indian Gaming Regulatory Act (IGRA) of 1988 (25 U.S.C. Section 2701) established the framework for tribal gaming operations on Native American lands. Tribal casinos, operating under compacts negotiated between tribes and state governments, have become a massive industry generating over $40 billion annually. IGRA establishes three classes of gaming, with Class III (casino-style) gaming requiring tribal-state compacts and oversight by the National Indian Gaming Commission.

### Wire Act Reinterpretation

The Department of Justice's 2011 Office of Legal Counsel opinion reinterpreted the Wire Act as applying only to sports betting, not all forms of online gambling. This reinterpretation opened the door for states to legalize online poker, casino games, and lottery sales. A subsequent 2018 OLC opinion reversed this position, but the First Circuit upheld the narrower interpretation in New Hampshire Lottery Commission v. Rosen, 986 F.3d 38 (1st Cir. 2021).

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's gambling regulation underwent a fundamental transformation with the Glücksspielstaatsvertrag 2021 (GlüStV 2021), which entered into force on July 1, 2021.

**Section 284 StGB (Unerlaubte Veranstaltung eines Glücksspiels):** "Whoever, without administrative authorization, publicly organizes or maintains a gambling operation or provides the equipment for it shall be punished with imprisonment for not more than two years or a fine."

### GlüStV 2021 Licensing Framework

| Category | License Requirements | Key Restrictions |
|----------|---------------------|-----------------|
| Online sports betting | Federal license from GGL | Monthly deposit limit of EUR 1,000 per operator |
| Online casino (slots) | Federal license from GGL | Maximum stake EUR 1 per spin; 5-second minimum spin time |
| Online poker | Federal license from GGL | Segregated player funds; responsible gambling tools |
| Land-based casinos (Spielbanken) | State license | Varies by Land |
| Slot machine arcades (Spielhallen) | Municipal license | Minimum distance between venues; reduced machine numbers |

### Gemeinsame Glücksspielbehörde der Länder (GGL)

The Joint Gambling Authority of the Länder (GGL), established under the GlüStV 2021 and based in Sachsen-Anhalt, serves as the federal licensing and enforcement body for online gambling. The GGL is authorized to issue and revoke licenses, block unlicensed gambling websites through ISP blocking orders, and coordinate enforcement actions against illegal operators.

### Constitutional Considerations

The Bundesverfassungsgericht has addressed gambling regulation in several decisions. In its 2006 sports betting decision (BVerfGE 115, 276), the court held that the state monopoly on sports betting was constitutional only insofar as it served the legitimate objective of combating gambling addiction -- not merely for fiscal purposes. This decision drove subsequent reforms requiring the state monopoly to demonstrate that it actually pursued addiction prevention rather than revenue maximization.

### Enforcement Challenges

Despite the new regulatory framework, enforcement against unlicensed online gambling operators remains challenging. Many offshore operators continue to serve the German market without licenses. The GGL has issued blocking orders against numerous domains, but operators frequently migrate to new domains.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Internal Market and Gambling

The CJEU has established that gambling services fall within the scope of the freedoms to provide services (Article 56 TFEU) and establishment (Article 49 TFEU), but that member states retain broad discretion to restrict these freedoms for reasons of public interest.

**Placanica and Others (Joined Cases C-338/04, C-359/04, C-360/04, 2007):** The Court held that while Italian gambling restrictions were justified in principle, they must be applied in a consistent and non-discriminatory manner. Criminal prosecution of individuals operating without licenses was disproportionate where the licensing system itself effectively excluded certain categories of operators.

**Liga Portuguesa (Case C-42/07, 2009):** The Court upheld Portugal's exclusive right to operate online gambling services, finding that the restriction was justified by the need to combat fraud and criminal activity in the online gambling sector.

**Dickinger and Ömer (Case C-347/09, 2011):** The Court held that member states must ensure that their gambling restrictions are genuinely aimed at limiting gambling opportunities in a consistent manner and are not merely designed to protect domestic monopoly revenues.

### No EU Harmonization

The European Commission's 2012 Action Plan "Towards a comprehensive European framework for online gambling" proposed regulatory convergence but was withdrawn in 2017 without producing binding legislation. The Commission concluded that the subsidiarity principle required leaving gambling regulation primarily to member states, though it continues to monitor compliance with internal market rules.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

The ECHR has addressed gambling primarily through Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (protection of property) in the context of license revocations and regulatory takings affecting gambling operators.

## Comparative Analysis

### Restrictiveness Spectrum

South Korea maintains the most restrictive regime, with virtually all gambling criminalized except through a handful of state-authorized monopolies. Germany has moved toward a regulated licensing model, particularly for online gambling. The United States has experienced the most dramatic liberalization, with the post-Murphy expansion of legal sports betting and online gambling.

### Online Gambling Regulation

The jurisdictions represent three distinct models for online gambling regulation. South Korea maintains blanket prohibition with aggressive enforcement. The United States permits state-by-state legalization with no federal licensing. Germany has established a federal licensing regime through the GGL.

### Revenue vs. Addiction Prevention

All jurisdictions struggle with the tension between gambling revenue and addiction prevention. South Korea's Kangwon Land generates substantial revenue for the local economy while contributing to gambling addiction. The United States' rapid expansion of sports betting has raised concerns about insufficient consumer protection. Germany's GlüStV 2021 attempts to balance market opening with strict responsible gambling requirements.
