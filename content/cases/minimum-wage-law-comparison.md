## Summary

Minimum wage legislation -- the legal establishment of a floor below which employers may not set hourly compensation -- represents one of the most consequential and politically contested areas of labor law across developed economies. South Korea's Minimum Wage Act (최저임금법), enacted in 1986 and first implemented on January 1, 1988, establishes an annually adjusted national minimum wage determined by the Minimum Wage Commission (최저임금위원회), a tripartite body comprising labor, management, and public-interest representatives; the minimum wage rose from 462.50 KRW per hour in 1988 to 10,030 KRW in 2025, with the most dramatic increases occurring during the Moon Jae-in administration (2017-2022) when the rate rose by approximately 41 percent over five years. The United States established the first federal minimum wage through the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) of 1938 (29 U.S.C. Section 201 et seq.), currently set at $7.25 per hour since July 24, 2009 -- the longest period without a federal increase in the statute's history -- while states and municipalities have enacted significantly higher minimums, with Washington State at $16.66 and several cities exceeding $18 per hour in 2025. Germany introduced its first statutory national minimum wage (Mindestlohn) on January 1, 2015, through the Mindestlohngesetz (MiLoG), ending a longstanding reliance on sectoral collective agreements; the rate was set at EUR 8.50 initially and was raised to EUR 12.00 by political decision effective October 1, 2022, with regular adjustments by the Mindestlohnkommission thereafter. At the European level, EU Directive 2022/2041 on adequate minimum wages, adopted on October 19, 2022, represents the first EU-wide framework establishing procedural requirements for minimum wage setting, mandating that member states with statutory minimum wages ensure adequacy through clear criteria including purchasing power, wage distribution, and growth rates, while respecting member states' competence to set specific rates and national traditions of collective bargaining.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's minimum wage system is governed by the Minimum Wage Act (최저임금법), Law No. 3927, enacted on December 31, 1986. The Act was initially applied only to manufacturing establishments with 10 or more employees beginning January 1, 1988, and was progressively extended to all sectors and all employers by 2000. The Act establishes a single national minimum wage applicable uniformly across all industries and regions, with no sub-minimum rates.

### Minimum Wage Commission (최저임금위원회)

The Commission, established under Article 12 of the Minimum Wage Act, is the central mechanism for determining annual minimum wage adjustments. It comprises 27 members: 9 labor representatives, 9 management representatives, and 9 public-interest members. The Ministry of Employment and Labor requests the Commission to deliberate each year, typically by March, with a determination due by June 29 for implementation the following January 1.

### Moon Jae-in Era Reforms

The most significant period of minimum wage acceleration occurred under President Moon Jae-in (2017-2022), who campaigned on raising the minimum wage to 10,000 KRW by 2020. The 16.4 percent increase in 2018 was the largest single-year increase in the system's history, followed by 10.9 percent in 2019. However, the pace of increases slowed significantly in 2020 amid COVID-19 concerns and employer resistance, and the 10,000 KRW target was not reached until 2024 under the subsequent Yoon administration.

### Scope and Coverage

Under Articles 3 and 6, the minimum wage applies to all workplaces employing one or more workers. The Act originally permitted sub-minimum rates for apprentices and workers with disabilities, but the apprentice exemption was abolished in 2012. Workers with disabilities may still receive reduced minimum wages through the disability-adjusted minimum wage system, though this provision has faced criticism from disability rights organizations.

### Enforcement and Penalties

Under Article 28, an employer who pays less than the minimum wage is subject to imprisonment for up to three years or a fine of up to 20 million KRW. The Ministry of Employment and Labor conducts regular workplace inspections. In 2023, the Ministry reported 4,127 minimum wage violations resulting in corrective orders or penalties.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) of 1938, codified at 29 U.S.C. Section 201 et seq., established the first federal minimum wage in the United States at $0.25 per hour. The Act was signed by President Franklin D. Roosevelt on June 25, 1938, and was a cornerstone of the New Deal labor reforms. The FLSA also established overtime pay requirements, recordkeeping obligations, and child labor protections.

### Federal Minimum Wage History

| Year | Rate | Legislation |
|------|------|-------------|
| 1938 | $0.25 | FLSA enacted |
| 1950 | $0.75 | 1949 Amendment |
| 1968 | $1.60 | 1966 Amendment (inflation-adjusted peak) |
| 1981 | $3.35 | 1977 Amendment |
| 1997 | $5.15 | Small Business Job Protection Act of 1996 |
| 2007 | $5.85 | Fair Minimum Wage Act of 2007 (phased increase) |
| 2008 | $6.55 | Second phase |
| 2009 | $7.25 | Final phase -- current federal rate |

The federal minimum wage has remained at $7.25 per hour since July 24, 2009, making this the longest period without an increase since the FLSA's enactment. Adjusted for inflation using the CPI, the real value of $7.25 in 2025 is approximately $5.30 in 2009 dollars, representing a significant erosion of purchasing power.

### State and Local Minimums

As of 2025, 30 states and the District of Columbia have minimum wages above the federal rate. Notable examples include:

| Jurisdiction | 2025 Rate | Indexing Mechanism |
|-------------|-----------|-------------------|
| Washington State | $16.66 | CPI-indexed annually |
| California | $16.50 | Indexed; $20.00 for fast-food workers |
| New York (NYC) | $16.50 | Administrative adjustment |
| Seattle | $20.76 | CPI-indexed |
| Denver | $18.29 | CPI-indexed |

**Tipped Employee Subminimum:** The FLSA permits a reduced minimum of $2.13 per hour for tipped employees, provided that tips bring total compensation to or above the standard minimum wage (the "tip credit" system). Eight states have eliminated the tip credit, requiring all employers to pay the full state minimum regardless of tips.

### Enforcement

The Department of Labor's Wage and Hour Division (WHD) enforces the FLSA through investigations and litigation. Remedies for minimum wage violations include back wages owed plus an equal amount in liquidated damages. Willful violations may result in criminal prosecution with penalties of up to $10,000 and six months imprisonment for repeat offenders. In fiscal year 2023, the WHD recovered over $274 million in back wages for approximately 163,000 workers across all FLSA violations.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany introduced its first statutory national minimum wage through the Gesetz zur Regelung eines allgemeinen Mindestlohns (Mindestlohngesetz -- MiLoG), enacted on August 11, 2014, with the minimum wage taking effect on January 1, 2015. Prior to this, Germany relied exclusively on sectoral collective agreements (Tarifverträge) to establish wage floors, supplemented by the Arbeitnehmer-Entsendegesetz (AEntG, Posted Workers Act) of 1996, which allowed sector-specific minimum wages to be extended to all workers in a given industry.

### Transition from Sectoral to Statutory Minimum Wage

The introduction of a statutory minimum wage was a signature policy demand of the Social Democratic Party (SPD) during the 2013 coalition negotiations with the CDU/CSU. The initial rate of EUR 8.50 per hour was chosen as a compromise. The political debate reflected Germany's traditionally strong reliance on Tarifautonomie (collective bargaining autonomy), with employer associations and some economists warning that a statutory minimum would displace bargaining and reduce employment, while labor advocates argued that declining collective bargaining coverage (from approximately 75 percent in the mid-1990s to under 50 percent by 2014) necessitated a statutory floor.

### Mindestlohnkommission

The MiLoG established a permanent bipartite Mindestlohnkommission (Minimum Wage Commission) comprising three employer representatives, three employee representatives, and a non-voting academic chair. The Commission is charged with recommending adjustments every two years based on the development of collectively agreed wages (Tarifentwicklung). The Commission's recommendation is then implemented by government regulation.

The October 2022 increase from EUR 10.45 to EUR 12.00 was notable because it was enacted by statutory override rather than through the Commission process, marking the first time the government bypassed the Mindestlohnkommission. This was implemented through the Mindestlohnerhöhungsgesetz and was a campaign promise of the SPD-led Ampelkoalition.

### Enforcement

Enforcement of the MiLoG is assigned to the Finanzkontrolle Schwarzarbeit (FKS), a division of the customs authority (Zollverwaltung), rather than to the labor inspectorate. This reflects the law's dual purpose of combating undeclared work and ensuring minimum wage compliance. Violations carry administrative fines of up to EUR 500,000. Employers must maintain records of working hours for employees earning at or near the minimum wage, and for employees in sectors prone to exploitation (construction, hospitality, logistics).

### Impact Assessment

Studies by the German Institute for Economic Research (DIW) and the Institute for Employment Research (IAB) found that the introduction of the minimum wage in 2015 raised wages for approximately 4 million workers without the significant job losses predicted by some economists. The Bundesbank estimated minimal employment effects in the range of 60,000 lost positions, primarily in marginal employment (Minijobs).

## ECHR & EU

### EU Directive 2022/2041 on Adequate Minimum Wages

Adopted on October 19, 2022, Directive (EU) 2022/2041 establishes the first EU-wide framework for minimum wage policy. The Directive does not set a European minimum wage or require member states to introduce statutory minimum wages. Instead, it establishes procedural requirements applicable to all member states.

**For Member States with Statutory Minimum Wages (21 of 27):** The Directive requires clear and stable criteria for setting and updating minimum wages, including purchasing power, the general level of wages and their distribution, the growth rate of wages, and long-term national productivity levels. It mandates regular and timely updates (at least every two years or every four years for countries using automatic indexation). Member states must establish or strengthen advisory bodies with effective participation of social partners. The Directive provides indicative reference values -- 60 percent of the gross median wage or 50 percent of the gross average wage -- that member states may use as guidance, though these are not binding thresholds.

**For All Member States:** The Directive requires action plans to promote collective bargaining coverage where coverage falls below 80 percent of the workforce. This provision was particularly aimed at member states with low bargaining coverage. The Directive also requires effective enforcement, including labor inspections, accessible complaint mechanisms, and protection against retaliation.

**Transposition Deadline:** November 15, 2024.

### European Social Charter

The European Social Charter (Revised), adopted by the Council of Europe in 1996, guarantees the right to a fair remuneration (Article 4). The European Committee of Social Rights, which monitors compliance, has interpreted Article 4(1) to require that wages are sufficient to ensure a decent standard of living. The Committee has used a benchmark of 60 percent of the net average wage, finding several member states in non-compliance.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

The European Court of Human Rights has not directly addressed minimum wage as a Convention right. However, the Court has addressed related issues under Article 4 (prohibition of forced labor and servitude), finding in Siliadin v. France (Application No. 73316/01, 2005) and Chowdury v. Greece (Application No. 21884/15, 2017) that states have positive obligations to prevent labor exploitation, including through adequate wage protections.

### ILO Framework

ILO Convention No. 131 on Minimum Wage Fixing (1970) provides the international foundation for minimum wage policy. It requires ratifying states to establish minimum wage machinery with full consultation of social partners and to consider workers' needs and economic factors. South Korea ratified Convention 131 in 2001; neither the United States nor Germany has ratified it.

## Comparative Analysis

### Setting Mechanisms

The four jurisdictions represent three distinct models for minimum wage determination. South Korea's tripartite commission model incorporates labor, management, and public interest perspectives. Germany's bipartite commission model involves only employer and employee representatives, though the October 2022 statutory override demonstrated that political intervention remains possible. The United States' legislative model requires congressional action, resulting in long periods of stagnation when political consensus cannot be reached. The EU Directive does not mandate a specific model but requires social partner involvement.

### Coverage and Exemptions

South Korea has achieved the broadest coverage, applying its minimum wage to all workers with essentially no sub-minimum rates since 2012. Germany provides limited exceptions for the long-term unemployed (first six months of new employment) and for certain internships. The United States maintains the most extensive exemption system, including the tipped employee subminimum, youth subminimum, and exemptions for certain categories of workers with disabilities.

### Adequacy

Measuring minimum wage adequacy against the EU Directive's indicative benchmarks (60 percent of median wage, 50 percent of average wage), South Korea's minimum wage is approximately 62 percent of the median wage as of 2024, Germany's is approximately 56 percent, and the U.S. federal minimum is approximately 28 percent of the median wage -- well below any international adequacy benchmark.

### Enforcement Models

The three jurisdictions use distinctly different enforcement agencies: a labor ministry (South Korea), a specialized wage enforcement division (United States), and a customs/anti-fraud authority (Germany). Germany's choice to assign enforcement to the FKS reflects the linkage between minimum wage compliance and combating undeclared work (Schwarzarbeit).
