## Summary

Corruption of minors -- the act of inducing, encouraging, or facilitating a minor's engagement in unlawful, immoral, or harmful conduct, or exposing a minor to conditions that endanger their physical, mental, or moral welfare -- encompasses a broad spectrum of offenses designed to protect children and adolescents from adult exploitation and harmful influences. South Korea addresses corruption of minors through a comprehensive statutory framework including the Juvenile Protection Act (청소년 보호법), which prohibits providing minors with alcohol, tobacco, drugs, and harmful media and imposes penalties of up to three years' imprisonment, the Child Welfare Act (아동복지법), which criminalizes emotional abuse and exploitation of children, and the Criminal Act (형법) provisions on inducing minors into prostitution (Article 242) and the Act on the Protection of Children and Youth Against Sex Offenses (아동·청소년의 성보호에 관한 법률), with South Korean law having been substantially strengthened following the 2008 "Cho Doo-soon case" (조두순 사건) involving the sexual assault of an eight-year-old girl. The United States addresses corruption of minors through diverse state statutes -- with Pennsylvania (18 Pa.C.S. Section 6301) providing a prototypical statute criminalizing conduct that tends to corrupt the morals of a minor -- alongside federal provisions including 18 U.S.C. Section 2422 (enticement of minors for sexual activity) carrying penalties of ten years to life imprisonment, and the PROTECT Act of 2003 expanding federal jurisdiction over crimes against children. Germany addresses the corruption of minors through Section 171 StGB (Verletzung der Fürsorge- oder Erziehungspflicht, violation of duty of care or upbringing) and Section 180 StGB (Förderung sexueller Handlungen Minderjähriger, facilitation of sexual acts of minors), with penalties of up to three years' imprisonment, supplemented by the Jugendschutzgesetz (Youth Protection Act) regulating minors' access to alcohol, tobacco, media, and entertainment venues. The ECHR has recognized extensive positive obligations on states to protect children from corruption and exploitation under Articles 3, 4, and 8, while the EU has adopted the Directive on combating the sexual abuse and sexual exploitation of children (2011/93/EU) and the Audiovisual Media Services Directive (2018/1808) to harmonize child protection standards across member states.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's approach to the corruption of minors reflects the country's strong emphasis on youth protection, embedded in a comprehensive statutory framework.

### Juvenile Protection Act (청소년 보호법)

| Prohibited Conduct | Penalty |
|-------------------|---------|
| Selling alcohol to minors | Up to 3 years or fine up to 30 million KRW |
| Selling tobacco to minors | Up to 3 years or fine up to 30 million KRW |
| Providing harmful drugs to minors | Up to 5 years or fine up to 50 million KRW |
| Employing minors in harmful businesses (bars, gambling establishments) | Up to 3 years or fine up to 30 million KRW |
| Distributing harmful media to minors | Up to 3 years or fine up to 30 million KRW |

### Child Welfare Act (아동복지법)

Article 17 of the Child Welfare Act enumerates prohibited acts against children, including physical abuse, emotional abuse, sexual abuse, neglect, exploitation for begging, and exposure to obscene acts. Article 71 provides penalties:

| Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|
| Sexual abuse of child | Up to 10 years or fine up to 50 million KRW |
| Physical abuse | Up to 5 years or fine up to 30 million KRW |
| Emotional abuse | Up to 5 years or fine up to 30 million KRW |
| Exploitation (labor, begging) | Up to 5 years or fine up to 30 million KRW |
| Neglect | Up to 5 years or fine up to 30 million KRW |

### Act on the Protection of Children and Youth Against Sex Offenses (아동·청소년의 성보호에 관한 법률, APCYSO)

This Act was substantially strengthened following several high-profile cases:

**Cho Doo-soon Case (2008):** The sexual assault of an eight-year-old girl by Cho Doo-soon, who received a sentence of only 12 years (reduced due to his claimed state of intoxication), provoked national outrage and led to significant legislative reforms including the introduction of electronic ankle monitoring for sex offenders, expanded sex offender registration requirements, and the elimination of the intoxication defense for sex crimes against minors.

**"Nth Room" Case (2020):** The digital sexual exploitation of minors through Telegram chat rooms by Cho Ju-bin and others led to further amendments to the APCYSO, including criminalization of possession of child sexual abuse material and enhanced penalties for digital sex crimes.

## United States

### Legal Framework

**State Corruption of Minors Statutes:**

**Pennsylvania (18 Pa.C.S. § 6301):** The prototypical corruption of minors statute provides: "Whoever, being of the age of 18 years and upwards, by any act corrupts or tends to corrupt the morals of any minor less than 18 years of age, or who aids, abets, entices or encourages any such minor in the commission of any crime... commits a misdemeanor of the first degree."

### Federal Provisions

| Statute | Conduct | Penalty |
|---------|---------|---------|
| 18 U.S.C. § 2422(a) | Enticement to travel for prostitution | Up to 20 years |
| 18 U.S.C. § 2422(b) | Online enticement of minor for sexual activity | 10 years to life |
| 18 U.S.C. § 2423 | Transportation of minor for criminal sexual activity | 10 years to life |
| 18 U.S.C. § 2251 | Sexual exploitation of children | 15-30 years (first offense) |
| 18 U.S.C. § 2252A | Child pornography | 5-20 years |

### PROTECT Act of 2003

The Prosecutorial Remedies and Other Tools to End the Exploitation of Children Today (PROTECT) Act significantly expanded federal jurisdiction over crimes against children, including:

- Eliminating the statute of limitations for child abduction and sexual abuse offenses
- Creating the AMBER Alert system at the federal level
- Increasing penalties for sex tourism involving minors
- Criminalizing computer-generated child pornography
- Establishing the "two-strikes" provision mandating life imprisonment for repeat child sex offenders

### Constitutional Vagueness Challenges

Corruption of minors statutes have faced due process challenges based on vagueness. In State v. Metzger, 211 Neb. 593 (1982), the Nebraska Supreme Court struck down a local "indecent conduct" ordinance as unconstitutionally vague. However, most state corruption of minors statutes have survived vagueness challenges when they include specific enumeration of prohibited conduct or are construed narrowly by courts.

### Mandatory Minimum Sentencing

Federal child exploitation offenses carry some of the most severe mandatory minimum sentences in the U.S. Code. Under the Adam Walsh Child Protection and Safety Act of 2006, first-time offenders convicted of sexual exploitation of a child face a mandatory minimum of 15 years, and repeat offenders face a mandatory minimum of 25 years.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany addresses corruption of minors through both the Strafgesetzbuch and the Jugendschutzgesetz.

**Section 171 StGB (Verletzung der Fürsorge- oder Erziehungspflicht):** "Whoever grossly violates their duty of care or upbringing for a person under 16 years of age and thereby exposes the person to the danger of a substantial impairment of their physical or psychological development... shall be punished with imprisonment for not more than three years or a fine."

**Section 180 StGB (Förderung sexueller Handlungen Minderjähriger):** Criminalizes the facilitation of sexual acts by persons under 16 (Section 180(1)) or by persons under 18 through exploitation of a position of dependency (Section 180(2)).

### Penalty Structure

| Offense | Provision | Penalty |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Violation of duty of care | Section 171 | Up to 3 years or fine |
| Facilitating sexual acts of persons under 16 | Section 180(1) | Up to 3 years or fine |
| Facilitating sexual acts (exploitation of dependency) | Section 180(2) | Up to 5 years or fine |
| Cybergrooming | Section 176b | 3 months to 5 years |
| Distribution of pornography to minors | Section 184(1) No. 1 | Up to 1 year or fine |

### Jugendschutzgesetz (Youth Protection Act)

The Jugendschutzgesetz regulates minors' access to alcohol, tobacco, films, video games, and entertainment venues:

| Restriction | Age Threshold |
|-------------|--------------|
| Hard liquor (spirits) | Prohibited under 18 |
| Beer, wine | Prohibited under 16 (except with parent present: 14) |
| Tobacco products | Prohibited under 18 |
| Films rated FSK 18 | Prohibited under 18 |
| Video games rated USK 18 | Prohibited under 18 |
| Nightclubs (without parent) | Prohibited under 18 |
| Gambling establishments | Prohibited under 18 |

Violations are primarily treated as Ordnungswidrigkeiten (administrative offenses) with fines rather than criminal penalties, though repeated or egregious violations may be prosecuted under Section 171 StGB.

### 2021 Reforms: Child Sexual Abuse

The Gesetz zur Bekämpfung sexualisierter Gewalt gegen Kinder (2021) reclassified child sexual abuse from a Vergehen (misdemeanor) to a Verbrechen (serious criminal offense), significantly increasing minimum penalties. Section 176 StGB now carries a minimum of one year imprisonment for sexual abuse of a child, and Section 176b (cybergrooming) was introduced with penalties of three months to five years.

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

**A v. United Kingdom (Application No. 25599/94, 1998):** The Court found a violation of Article 3 where British law permitted "reasonable chastisement" of children by parents, finding that the state had failed to provide adequate protection against treatment or punishment that crossed the Article 3 threshold.

**C.N. v. United Kingdom (Application No. 4239/08, 2012):** The Court found a violation of Article 4 (prohibition of servitude and forced labour) where British law failed to provide adequate criminal law provisions to protect against domestic servitude of a minor.

### EU Directive 2011/93/EU

The Directive on combating the sexual abuse and sexual exploitation of children establishes minimum standards across the EU:

- **Article 3:** Sexual abuse offenses with minimum maximum penalties of 5-10 years.
- **Article 4:** Sexual exploitation offenses with minimum maximum penalties of 5-10 years.
- **Article 5:** Child pornography offenses with minimum maximum penalties of 1-3 years.
- **Article 6:** Solicitation of children for sexual purposes (grooming) with minimum maximum penalty of 1 year.

### Audiovisual Media Services Directive (2018/1808)

The AVMSD requires member states to ensure that audiovisual media services providers take appropriate measures to protect minors from harmful content, including age verification systems for on-demand services and watershed restrictions for linear broadcasting.

### Lanzarote Convention (Council of Europe, 2007)

The Council of Europe Convention on the Protection of Children against Sexual Exploitation and Sexual Abuse (Lanzarote Convention) provides the most comprehensive European framework for protecting children from sexual corruption, requiring criminalization of sexual abuse, child prostitution, child pornography, solicitation (grooming), and deliberately causing a child to witness sexual activities.

## Comparative Analysis

### Scope of "Corruption"

The jurisdictions vary significantly in what constitutes "corruption" of a minor. The United States takes the broadest approach in some states, with statutes like Pennsylvania's covering any act that "tends to corrupt the morals" of a minor. Germany focuses more narrowly on violations of specific duties of care and enumerated harmful conduct. South Korea combines broad youth protection regulations with specific criminal provisions.

### Online Exploitation

All jurisdictions have significantly strengthened their response to online corruption of minors. South Korea's response to the "Nth Room" case, U.S. federal enticement statutes, Germany's Section 176b on cybergrooming, and the EU Directive 2011/93/EU all reflect the adaptation of legal frameworks to digital threats.

### Alcohol and Substance Access

All jurisdictions restrict minors' access to alcohol and tobacco, but enforcement mechanisms differ. South Korea and Germany impose direct penalties on sellers. The United States relies primarily on state licensing and enforcement against retailers. The EU has not harmonized minimum drinking ages.
