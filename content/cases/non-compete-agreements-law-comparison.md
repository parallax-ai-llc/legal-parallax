## Summary

Non-compete agreements -- contractual provisions restricting employees from working for competitors or starting competing businesses after leaving their employer -- sit at the intersection of freedom of contract, worker mobility, and employer interests in protecting trade secrets and business investments. South Korea regulates non-compete agreements (경업금지 약정) through judicial interpretation rather than specific legislation, with courts applying a multi-factor reasonableness test derived from Supreme Court precedent, assessing whether the restriction is necessary to protect legitimate employer interests, whether adequate compensation (전직금지 보상) was provided, and whether the duration, geographic scope, and activity restrictions are proportionate; Korean courts have generally limited enforceable duration to one to two years and increasingly require financial compensation during the restriction period. The United States has undergone dramatic legal upheaval regarding non-competes, with the Federal Trade Commission issuing a final rule in April 2024 banning most non-compete clauses nationwide, which was subsequently vacated by a federal district court in Ryan LLC v. FTC (N.D. Tex. 2024) and the Fifth Circuit; at the state level, California has prohibited non-competes since 1872 under Business and Professions Code Section 16600, while other states range from enforcement under reasonableness standards to near-total bans (Minnesota 2023, Colorado 2022). Germany permits post-contractual non-compete agreements (nachvertragliches Wettbewerbsverbot) under Sections 74 through 75f of the Handelsgesetzbuch (HGB), subject to strict statutory requirements including mandatory Karenzentschädigung (compensation during the restriction period) of at least 50 percent of the employee's last annual compensation, a maximum duration of two years, and writing requirements; agreements failing to meet these requirements are void and unenforceable. At the European level, there is no harmonized EU framework on non-compete agreements, though the Trade Secrets Directive 2016/943 addresses related interests, and the European Commission's 2020 study on non-competes found that approximately 18 percent of EU workers are subject to such restrictions, with significant variation across member states.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea has no specific statute governing non-compete agreements. The legality and enforceability of post-employment non-compete clauses (전직금지 약정 or 경업금지 약정) are determined entirely through judicial interpretation, primarily by the Supreme Court (대법원).

### Supreme Court Framework

The Supreme Court established the governing standard in its 2010 decision (2010다3270), holding that non-compete agreements are enforceable only when they satisfy a multi-factor reasonableness test:

| Factor | Assessment Criteria |
|--------|-------------------|
| Legitimate employer interest | Protection of trade secrets, confidential information, or customer relationships |
| Employee's position and duties | Access to sensitive information; senior vs. junior position |
| Duration | Must be reasonable; courts typically cap at 1-2 years |
| Geographic scope | Must be proportionate to employer's business area |
| Activity scope | Must be narrowly tailored to competing activities |
| Compensation | Whether adequate compensation (전직금지 보상) was provided |
| Public interest | Impact on employee's livelihood and market competition |

### Compensation Requirement

While earlier Korean case law did not consistently require compensation as a condition of enforceability, a clear judicial trend since approximately 2015 has made the presence or absence of compensation a significant factor. Courts increasingly view non-compete agreements without adequate financial consideration (전직금지 보상) as disproportionate restrictions on the employee's right to choose their occupation (직업선택의 자유), protected by Article 15 of the Constitution (헌법 제15조). Some recent lower court decisions have treated the absence of compensation as presumptive grounds for invalidation.

### Enforcement Remedies

Employers seeking to enforce non-compete agreements typically pursue two remedies: provisional injunctive relief (가처분) through the courts to prevent the employee from commencing competing employment, and damages claims for breach of the agreement. Courts have also upheld contractual penalty clauses (위약금) where the amount is proportionate.

### Technology Sector Issues

Non-compete disputes have become particularly prevalent in South Korea's semiconductor and battery technology sectors. High-profile cases involving engineers moving between Samsung and SK Hynix, and between LG Energy Solution and competitors, have tested the boundaries of non-compete enforceability and trade secret protection. The Unfair Competition Prevention and Trade Secret Protection Act (부정경쟁방지 및 영업비밀보호에 관한 법률) provides additional protections that overlap with non-compete restrictions.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States exhibits the widest variation in non-compete law of any jurisdiction, ranging from total prohibition (California) to broad enforcement (Florida).

### State Approaches

**Prohibition States:** California has banned non-compete agreements since 1872 (Bus. & Prof. Code § 16600), with limited exceptions for business sale contexts. Minnesota banned non-competes effective July 1, 2023. North Dakota, Oklahoma, and Colorado (2022, with exceptions) also substantially restrict enforcement.

**Reasonableness States (Majority):** Most states enforce non-competes that are reasonable in duration (typically 1-2 years), geographic scope, and activity scope, and protect a legitimate business interest. Common legitimate interests include trade secrets, confidential information, customer relationships, and specialized training.

**Employee Protection Reforms:** Since 2018, numerous states have enacted targeted reforms:

| State | Reform | Key Provision |
|-------|--------|--------------|
| Massachusetts (2018) | Noncompetition Agreement Act | Limits to 12 months; garden leave or mutual agreed consideration required |
| Washington (2019) | Non-compete statute | Unenforceable for employees earning under $116,593 (indexed); 18-month max |
| Illinois (2021) | Freedom to Work Act | Unenforceable for employees earning under $75,000 (increasing to $90,000 by 2037) |
| Colorado (2022) | HB 22-1317 | Banned except for highly compensated workers (earning 60% of highly compensated threshold) |
| Minnesota (2023) | SF 3035 | Complete ban on non-competes for all employees |

### FTC Final Rule (2024) -- Vacated

On April 23, 2024, the FTC issued a final rule (16 CFR Part 910) that would have banned non-compete clauses for virtually all workers nationwide, finding that non-competes constitute unfair methods of competition under Section 5 of the FTC Act. The rule would have made existing non-competes unenforceable (except for senior executives earning over $151,164 with policy-making authority) and prohibited new non-competes for all workers.

On August 20, 2024, a federal district court in Ryan LLC v. FTC (N.D. Tex.) set aside the rule nationwide, holding that the FTC lacked authority to issue substantive competition rules and that the rule was arbitrary and capricious. The Fifth Circuit upheld the vacatur in early 2025. The FTC has not indicated whether it will seek Supreme Court review.

### Blue-Pencil Doctrine

A majority of U.S. states apply the "blue-pencil" doctrine, permitting courts to modify overbroad non-competes to render them enforceable (reforming duration, scope, or geographic area). A minority of states (including Virginia and Wisconsin) follow the "red-pencil" doctrine, under which overbroad agreements are void in their entirety.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany provides the most detailed statutory framework for post-employment non-compete agreements, codified in Sections 74 through 75f of the Handelsgesetzbuch (HGB). These provisions apply to all Handlungsgehilfen (commercial employees) and have been extended by analogy to all employees by the Bundesarbeitsgericht (Federal Labor Court).

### Statutory Requirements

**Writing (§ 74(1) HGB):** The non-compete agreement must be in writing and a signed copy must be provided to the employee.

**Karenzentschädigung (§ 74(2) HGB):** The employer must pay compensation during the restriction period of at least 50 percent of the employee's last annual compensation, including all remuneration components (base salary, bonuses, benefits in kind). This mandatory compensation requirement is the most distinctive feature of the German system and has no parallel in the U.S. or Korean frameworks.

**Maximum Duration (§ 74a(1) HGB):** The restriction may not exceed two years from the date of employment termination.

**Legitimate Interest (§ 74a(1) HGB):** The restriction must serve a justified commercial interest of the employer. Restrictions that impose an unreasonable hardship on the employee's professional advancement (unbillige Erschwerung des Fortkommens) may be voided.

### Consequences of Non-Compliance

| Deficiency | Consequence |
|-----------|-------------|
| No written form | Agreement void (§ 74(1) HGB) -- employee has no obligation |
| No or insufficient Karenzentschädigung | Agreement void at employee's option; employee may choose to comply and claim the statutory minimum compensation |
| Duration exceeds 2 years | Reduced to 2 years by operation of law |
| No legitimate interest | Agreement void |

### Employer's Right to Waive (Verzichtserklärung)

Under § 75a HGB, the employer may unilaterally waive (renounce) the non-compete agreement by written declaration. After such waiver, the employee is released from the restriction, and the employer's obligation to pay Karenzentschädigung ceases one year after the waiver. This mechanism allows employers to avoid the cost of compensation for restrictions that are no longer commercially valuable.

### Deduction of Other Income

Under § 74c HGB, if the employee earns income from other employment during the restriction period, the employer may deduct such income from the Karenzentschädigung to the extent that the total (compensation plus other income) exceeds 110 percent of the employee's last annual compensation.

### Contractual Penalties

Non-compete agreements may include contractual penalty clauses (Vertragsstrafe) for breach. The BAG has upheld such clauses subject to the general proportionality review under Section 307 BGB (unconscionability control in standard contract terms). Penalties equal to several months' compensation have been upheld where proportionate.

## ECHR & EU

### No Harmonized Framework

The EU has not adopted a directive or regulation specifically addressing non-compete agreements. The European Commission's 2020 study "Non-compete clauses in labour contracts" found significant variation across member states and recommended monitoring the impact of non-competes on worker mobility and innovation.

### Trade Secrets Directive 2016/943

While not directly regulating non-competes, the EU Trade Secrets Directive (Directive (EU) 2016/943) provides a harmonized framework for trade secret protection that addresses some of the same employer interests underlying non-compete agreements. The Directive defines trade secrets, establishes remedies for misappropriation, and includes safeguards for worker mobility (Article 1(3): the Directive shall not affect the application of national rules requiring employees to act in good faith).

### Free Movement of Workers

Non-compete agreements that restrict workers from taking employment in other EU member states may conflict with Article 45 TFEU (free movement of workers). While the CJEU has not directly addressed this issue in the employment non-compete context, the general principle that restrictions on free movement must be proportionate and justified by legitimate interests would apply.

### European Commission Study (2020)

Key findings of the Commission's study included: approximately 18 percent of EU workers were subject to non-compete clauses; enforcement practices varied widely, from permissive (UK, Ireland) to strict (France, Italy require compensation); non-competes were associated with reduced worker mobility and potentially reduced wage growth; and workers with lower bargaining power were more likely to be subject to non-competes without understanding their implications.

### Council of Europe

The European Social Charter, Article 1(2), guarantees the right to earn one's living in a freely chosen occupation. The European Committee of Social Rights has not directly addressed non-compete agreements but has established that restrictions on occupational choice must be proportionate and time-limited.

## Comparative Analysis

### Compensation Requirements

Germany's mandatory 50 percent Karenzentschädigung stands alone as a statutory compensation requirement. South Korea's judicial trend toward requiring compensation is approaching but has not reached Germany's level of certainty. The United States generally does not require compensation (Massachusetts and Washington being partial exceptions), which critics argue enables employers to impose non-competes as a one-sided constraint without corresponding benefit to the employee.

### Validity Approaches

Germany uses a strict statutory checklist: any failure to meet statutory requirements voids the agreement. South Korea and most U.S. states use a multi-factor reasonableness test that grants courts more discretion but less predictability. California, Minnesota, and the (vacated) FTC rule represent the categorical prohibition approach.

### Impact on Innovation

Research by Marx, Strumsky, and Fleming (2009) and Starr, Prescott, and Bishara (2021) has demonstrated that states prohibiting non-competes (particularly California) exhibit higher rates of employee mobility, knowledge spillovers, and startup formation compared to states that enforce them. This evidence informed the FTC's rulemaking and has influenced state-level reform efforts.

### Trend Toward Restriction

Across all jurisdictions, the trend is clearly toward restricting or conditioning the enforceability of non-competes. South Korea's courts have progressively tightened requirements. U.S. states are increasingly enacting salary thresholds and duration limits. Germany's long-standing statutory framework, with its mandatory compensation requirement, is increasingly viewed as a model for other jurisdictions.
