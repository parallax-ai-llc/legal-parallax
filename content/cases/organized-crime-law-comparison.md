## Summary

Organized crime — the coordinated engagement in criminal activity by structured groups seeking power, profit, or influence — poses a fundamental challenge to the rule of law across all jurisdictions. States have developed markedly different legislative strategies to combat criminal organizations, reflecting distinct legal traditions, constitutional constraints, and historical experiences. South Korea relies on Article 114 of the Criminal Act (형법 제114조), which criminalizes the formation and membership of criminal syndicates, supplemented by the Act on the Punishment of Violence (폭력행위등처벌에관한법률) and more recent legislation addressing the migration of organized crime into cyberspace. The United States pioneered the enterprise-based prosecution model through the Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act (RICO), codified at 18 U.S.C. Sections 1961-1968, which targets the structure and economic power of criminal enterprises through both criminal penalties and civil remedies, including asset forfeiture and treble damages. Germany addresses organized crime primarily through Section 129 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), which criminalizes the formation of and participation in criminal associations, with enhanced provisions in Sections 129a and 129b for terrorist organizations. The European Union's Council Framework Decision 2008/841/JHA establishes minimum harmonization standards across member states, requiring criminalization of participation in criminal organizations, while the European Court of Human Rights (ECHR) provides an overarching human rights framework that constrains member state enforcement measures under Articles 6, 8, and 11 of the European Convention. Europol's EU Serious and Organised Crime Threat Assessment (EU-SOCTA) 2025 identified the increasing use of artificial intelligence, cyber-enabled fraud, and the exploitation of young perpetrators as defining features of the evolving organized crime landscape.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's legal framework for combating organized crime rests on several interlocking statutes: Article 114 of the Criminal Act (형법), the Act on the Punishment of Violence (폭력행위등처벌에관한법률, first enacted 1961, substantially revised 2004 and 2016), and the Act on Special Cases Concerning the Punishment of Specific Crimes (특정범죄가중처벌 등에 관한 법률). These statutes operate within the broader context of the Criminal Act of 1953 (Act No. 293), South Korea's comprehensive penal code.

### Article 114 — Organization of Criminal Groups (범죄단체조직죄)

Article 114 of the Criminal Act is the foundational organized crime provision. It provides:

- **Paragraph 1:** A person who organizes a syndicate or group with the intent to commit a crime punishable by death, or imprisonment with labor for an indefinite term, or imprisonment for at least four years, or who joins such a syndicate or group, shall be punished by the penalty prescribed for the intended crime, provided that the penalty may be mitigated.
- **Paragraph 2:** A person who organizes a group whose purpose is to reject compulsory military service or liability to pay taxes, or who joins such group, shall be punished by imprisonment or imprisonment without prison labor for not more than ten years, or by a fine not exceeding fifteen million won.

The significance of Article 114 lies in its anticipatory criminalization: mere formation of or membership in a criminal syndicate constitutes a completed offense, without requiring the commission of any substantive predicate crime. The penalty mirrors that of the intended crime, creating a powerful deterrent structure.

### Act on the Punishment of Violence (폭력행위등처벌에관한법률)

This special act, which supplements the general Criminal Act, targets collective and habitual violence with enhanced penalties:

| Offense | Provision | Penalty |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Leading a criminal group organized for violent crimes | Art. 4(1) | Death, life imprisonment, or minimum 10 years |
| Membership in a violent criminal group | Art. 4(2) | Graduated imprisonment based on role |
| Carrying deadly weapons for use in group crime | Art. 7 | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine up to 3 million won |
| Habitual commission of violent offenses | Art. 2(1) | Up to 1.5x the penalty for the underlying offense |
| Collective violence (3+ persons) | Art. 2(2) | Enhanced penalties above base offense |

Under the Violence Act, the leader of a criminal group organized with the aim of committing violent crimes is subject to the death penalty, life imprisonment, or imprisonment for at least ten years — even without any specific crimes having been committed by the leader or group members. This provision represents one of the most severe organizational liability regimes in comparative criminal law.

### Recent Developments: Digital Organized Crime

South Korea has witnessed a dramatic transformation of organized crime activity in recent years. According to figures released by the Korean National Police Agency, 2,363 organized crime members were arrested in 2024, with 56.3 percent (1,330 individuals) tied to digital crimes including voice phishing, romance scams, and illegal online gambling. In 2021, digital crimes accounted for just 5.1 percent (84 arrests) of all gang-related arrests; that figure surged to 1,330 arrests by 2024. Younger gang members in their 20s and 30s are driving this transformation, migrating to phishing and online scams as traditional income sources — extortion of local businesses and loan-sharking — become less profitable.

### Judicial Approach

South Korean courts have historically taken a strict view of organized crime offenses. The Judicial Research and Training Institute of the Supreme Court has emphasized the need for proportionate sentencing that accounts for both the organizational threat posed by criminal syndicates and the individual culpability of defendants. The Constitutional Court has upheld the enhanced penalty provisions of the Violence Act against challenges based on the principle of proportionality (비례의 원칙) and the presumption of innocence.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States possesses the most expansive and sophisticated statutory framework for prosecuting organized crime in the world, centered on the Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act (RICO), enacted as Title IX of the Organized Crime Control Act of 1970 and codified at 18 U.S.C. Sections 1961-1968. RICO was signed into law by President Richard Nixon and was originally conceived as a tool to dismantle the Italian-American Mafia, but its deliberately broad language has been applied to a vast range of criminal enterprises, from street gangs and motorcycle clubs to corporate fraud schemes and, more recently, political corruption cases.

### 18 U.S.C. Section 1961 — Definitions

RICO's definitional structure is foundational to its operation:

- **Racketeering activity:** Encompasses over 100 federal offenses and specified state felonies, including murder, kidnapping, gambling, arson, robbery, bribery, extortion, dealing in controlled substances, money laundering, obstruction of justice, witness tampering, trafficking in persons, mail fraud, wire fraud, financial institution fraud, and counterfeiting.
- **Enterprise:** Any individual, partnership, corporation, association, or other legal entity, and any union or group of individuals associated in fact although not a legal entity. This definition was critically interpreted in *United States v. Turkette*, 452 U.S. 576 (1981).
- **Pattern of racketeering activity:** Requires at least two acts of racketeering activity within ten years (excluding imprisonment), with at least one occurring after the statute's effective date.

### Criminal Penalties (18 U.S.C. Section 1963)

| Offense | Provision | Penalty |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| RICO violation (general) | § 1963(a) | Up to 20 years imprisonment per count |
| RICO violation (predicate carrying life) | § 1963(a) | Life imprisonment |
| Criminal forfeiture | § 1963(a) | Mandatory forfeiture of all RICO proceeds and interests |
| Conspiracy to violate RICO | § 1962(d) | Same penalties as substantive RICO offense |

### Civil RICO (18 U.S.C. Section 1964)

A distinctive feature of American organized crime law is the availability of civil remedies. Under Section 1964(c), any person injured in his business or property by reason of a RICO violation may recover treble damages (three times actual damages), plus costs and reasonable attorney's fees. Civil RICO does not require a prior criminal conviction and has been extensively used in commercial litigation, securities fraud cases, and insurance disputes, far beyond the statute's original organized crime focus.

### Landmark Supreme Court Cases

**United States v. Turkette, 452 U.S. 576 (1981):** The Court held that RICO applies to both legitimate and illegitimate enterprises. The indictment described the enterprise as "a group of individuals associated in fact for the purpose of illegally trafficking in narcotics and other dangerous drugs, committing arsons, utilizing the United States mails to defraud insurance companies, bribing and attempting to bribe local police officers, and corruptly influencing the outcome of state court proceedings." The Court ruled that "an enterprise includes any union or group of individuals associated in fact" and that neither the language nor structure of RICO limits its application to legitimate enterprises.

**Sedima, S.P.R.L. v. Imrex Co., 473 U.S. 479 (1985):** The Court reinforced the broad applicability of civil RICO, ruling that plaintiffs do not need to demonstrate a prior criminal RICO conviction to bring a civil RICO claim. This decision opened the floodgates for civil RICO litigation in commercial disputes.

**Salinas v. United States, 522 U.S. 52 (1997):** The Court held that a defendant need not personally commit or agree to commit two predicate acts to be convicted of RICO conspiracy under Section 1962(d). It is sufficient that the defendant agreed to the commission of two predicate acts by any co-conspirator.

**Boyle v. United States, 556 U.S. 938 (2009):** In a 7-2 decision, the Court held that any group convened to carry out a crime meets the definition of an enterprise, even if it was created solely for criminal purposes and lacks a formal hierarchical structure. The case involved Edmund Boyle, a member of the "Night Drop Crew" with alleged ties to New York's Gambino crime family.

### Continuing Criminal Enterprise (CCE) — 21 U.S.C. Section 848

Complementing RICO, the Continuing Criminal Enterprise statute targets drug trafficking organizations specifically. A CCE conviction requires proof that the defendant organized, supervised, or managed five or more persons in a continuing series of drug violations generating substantial income. Penalties range from 20 years to life imprisonment, with mandatory life imprisonment for a second CCE conviction or for CCE leaders whose enterprises involve exceptionally large quantities of drugs.

### State RICO Statutes

At least 33 states have enacted their own RICO or organized crime statutes, often modeled on the federal Act but with significant variations. Georgia's RICO statute (O.C.G.A. Section 16-14-1 et seq.) notably gained public attention in 2023 when it was used to charge a broad group of defendants in the Fulton County election interference case, demonstrating the statute's adaptability to novel forms of alleged criminal enterprise.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's approach to organized crime centers on Section 129 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), which criminalizes the formation of criminal associations (Bildung krimineller Vereinigungen). This provision has been part of the German Criminal Code since its original enactment in 1871 and has been revised multiple times to address evolving forms of organized criminality. Sections 129a (terrorist associations) and 129b (foreign criminal and terrorist associations) supplement the base provision. German law takes an association-based approach, focusing on the organizational structure rather than the enterprise model favored by U.S. law.

### Section 129 StGB — Formation of Criminal Associations

**Section 129(1):** Whosoever establishes an association or participates as a member in an association whose purpose or activity is directed toward the commission of criminal acts punishable by a maximum term of imprisonment of at least two years shall be liable to imprisonment for up to five years or a monetary penalty.

**Section 129(2) — Definition:** An association is an organized group of more than two persons in pursuit of an overriding common interest that is designed to last for a longer period of time, in which the determination of the association as a whole prevails over the will of individual members and which makes use of the mutual support of its members.

**Section 129(3) — Support and Recruitment:** Whosoever supports an association as described in subsection (1) or recruits members or supporters for it shall be liable to imprisonment for not more than three years or a monetary penalty.

| Offense | Provision | Penalty |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Forming or membership in a criminal association | § 129(1) | Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine |
| Supporting a criminal association | § 129(3) | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine |
| Ringleader or hinterman | § 129(4) | 6 months to 5 years imprisonment |
| Ringleader (serious offenses) | § 129(4) | 6 months to 10 years imprisonment |
| Forming a terrorist association | § 129a(1) | 1 to 10 years imprisonment |
| Leading a terrorist association | § 129a(4) | 3 to 15 years imprisonment |

### Political Party Exemption

Section 129(3)(2) contains a constitutionally significant exemption: the provision does not apply to political parties that the Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) has not declared unconstitutional. This carve-out reflects the privileged constitutional status of political parties under Article 21 of the Grundgesetz (Basic Law) and the principle that the Constitutional Court holds exclusive competence to determine the constitutionality of political parties (Parteienprivileg).

### Mitigation and Voluntary Disclosure

German law provides incentive-based sentencing mitigation for cooperation. Under Section 129(6), the court may mitigate the sentence or order a discharge if the offender voluntarily discloses knowledge to a government authority in time so that offenses whose planning the offender is aware of may be prevented. If the offender succeeds in preventing the continued existence of the organization, criminal liability is extinguished entirely.

### Recent BGH Jurisprudence

The Bundesgerichtshof (Federal Court of Justice) issued two significant decisions in 2023 affirming the scope of Section 129:

- **BGH, June 1, 2023 (3 StR 414/22):** The Court upheld the conviction of defendants involved in a hawala banking network that transferred assets totaling over 356 million euros from Germany to Turkey. The Court confirmed that the group constituted a criminal organization under Section 129(2), meeting the requirements of an organized group with an overriding common interest designed to last.
- **BGH, June 28, 2023 (3 StR 4003/20):** The Court held that the legal requirements of Section 129 can be met in the case of purely online communication, significantly expanding the provision's applicability to digital-age criminal networks that operate without physical meetings or traditional hierarchical structures.

### Section 129b — Foreign Criminal and Terrorist Associations

Section 129b extends criminal liability under Sections 129 and 129a to organizations based outside Germany. Prosecution under Section 129b requires authorization by the Federal Ministry of Justice, ensuring a political-legal checkpoint before extending German criminal jurisdiction to foreign entities. This provision has been used extensively in prosecutions related to foreign terrorist organizations, including cases involving the Islamic State and the PKK.

### Relationship to EU Law

Germany transposed the requirements of Council Framework Decision 2008/841/JHA through its existing Section 129 framework, which already met the minimum harmonization standards established by the Framework Decision. The 2017 amendment to Section 129 (Gesetz zur Änderung des Strafgesetzbuches) refined the definition of a criminal association in subsection (2) to align more closely with the EU definition while maintaining Germany's more detailed statutory framework.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Legislative Framework

The European Union's primary instrument for combating organized crime is Council Framework Decision 2008/841/JHA of October 24, 2008, on the fight against organised crime. This Framework Decision replaced Joint Action 98/733/JHA and established minimum standards for member state criminalization of participation in criminal organizations.

**Definition of Criminal Organisation (Article 1):** A structured association, established over a period of time, of more than two persons acting in concert in order to commit offences which are punishable by deprivation of liberty or a detention order of a maximum of at least four years or a more serious penalty, to obtain, directly or indirectly, a financial or other material benefit.

**Dual Criminalization Model (Article 2):** Member states must criminalize at least one of two forms of conduct:

1. **Participation model:** Active participation in the organisation's criminal activities, with knowledge of either the aim of the organisation or its intention to commit the offences in question.
2. **Conspiracy model:** Agreement with one or more persons to pursue an activity which, if carried out, would amount to the commission of offences referred to in Article 1, even if that person does not take part in the actual execution of the activity.

**Penalties (Article 3):**

| Conduct | Minimum Maximum Penalty |
|---------|------------------------|
| Participation in criminal activities (Art. 2(a)) | 2 to 5 years maximum imprisonment |
| Conspiracy to commit offenses (Art. 2(b)) | Same as the planned offenses, or 2 to 5 years maximum imprisonment |
| Instigation, aiding, abetting, or attempting | As determined by member state law |

Member states were required to transpose the Framework Decision by May 11, 2010.

### Directive 2024/1260 — Asset Recovery and Confiscation

Directive 2024/1260 of the European Parliament and of the Council of April 24, 2024, represents the EU's most significant recent legislative action against organized crime. The directive establishes EU-wide minimum rules on the tracing, identification, freezing, confiscation, and management of criminal property. It replaces five earlier instruments, including Council Decision 2007/845/JHA and Directive 2014/42/EU, and mandates the establishment of national Asset Recovery Offices with enhanced cross-border cooperation capabilities.

### ProtectEU Strategy (2025)

On April 1, 2025, the European Commission adopted ProtectEU: the European Internal Security Strategy, which sets out priorities to protect the EU's security from organized crime threats, both online and offline. The strategy prioritizes four pillars: tackling criminal networks by investing in crime prevention; boosting law enforcement and judicial cooperation; disrupting the organisation of criminal networks; and eliminating the profits generated by organised crime.

### EU-SOCTA 2025

Europol's EU Serious and Organised Crime Threat Assessment 2025 (EU-SOCTA), released on March 18, 2025, identified several defining characteristics of contemporary organized crime in Europe:

- Serious and organised crime is no longer just a threat to public safety but impacts the foundations of EU institutions and democratic society.
- Criminal networks are increasingly operating as proxies in the service of hybrid threat actors, blurring the line between organized crime and state-sponsored activity.
- Criminals are exploiting artificial intelligence to automate operations, avoid detection, and expand their reach.
- Online threats — including cyber-attacks, fraud schemes, and child sexual exploitation — have reached unprecedented scale.
- Physical threats persist in drug trafficking, migrant smuggling, firearms trafficking, and waste crime.

### Europol and Eurojust

The EU's operational framework for combating organized crime relies on two key agencies:

- **Europol** (European Union Agency for Law Enforcement Cooperation): Provides intelligence analysis, coordinates cross-border operations, and maintains the Europol Information System. Europol does not possess independent arrest or investigative powers but supports member state law enforcement through Joint Investigation Teams (JITs) and analytical support.
- **Eurojust** (European Union Agency for Criminal Justice Cooperation): Facilitates judicial cooperation between member states in cross-border organized crime cases, assists with mutual legal assistance requests, and coordinates parallel prosecutions.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

The European Convention on Human Rights imposes constraints on how member states may combat organized crime, balancing security imperatives against fundamental rights:

**Article 6 — Right to a Fair Trial:** The ECHR has held that organized crime prosecutions must comply with fair trial guarantees, including the right to examine witnesses, the prohibition on the use of evidence obtained by torture, and the presumption of innocence. Anonymous witness testimony, often essential in organized crime cases due to witness intimidation, must be subject to procedural safeguards that allow the defense a meaningful opportunity to challenge the evidence.

**Article 8 — Right to Respect for Private and Family Life:** Surveillance measures deployed against organized crime must be "in accordance with the law," pursue a legitimate aim, and be "necessary in a democratic society." The Court has developed detailed jurisprudence on the quality-of-law requirements for surveillance legislation, holding that domestic law must be sufficiently clear and precise, provide adequate safeguards against abuse, and establish effective oversight mechanisms.

**Article 11 — Freedom of Association:** While Article 11 protects the right to freedom of association, it permits restrictions that are "prescribed by law and necessary in a democratic society" for the prevention of disorder or crime. The Court has upheld the dissolution of associations found to constitute criminal organizations, provided that the interference is proportionate and subject to judicial review.

**Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 — Protection of Property:** Asset confiscation measures must comply with the principle of proportionality. The Court has examined whether confiscation orders in organized crime cases strike a fair balance between the public interest in combating crime and the individual's right to peaceful enjoyment of possessions.

## Comparative Analysis

### Structural Approaches

The four jurisdictions represent fundamentally different philosophical approaches to organized crime:

1. **Enterprise model (United States):** RICO focuses on the criminal enterprise as an economic entity, targeting its financial structure, leadership, and patterns of racketeering activity. The enterprise model enables prosecution of individuals whose connection to specific crimes may be attenuated but who participate in or benefit from the enterprise's pattern of criminal activity. The availability of civil remedies and mandatory forfeiture makes RICO a uniquely powerful economic weapon against organized crime.

2. **Association model (Germany):** Section 129 StGB targets the organizational form itself — the criminal association — rather than its economic output. German law requires proof of a structured group with an overriding common interest, designed to endure over time. This model emphasizes the collective danger posed by organized groups but imposes lower maximum penalties than U.S. law.

3. **Syndicate model (South Korea):** Article 114 of the Criminal Act combines elements of both approaches, criminalizing the formation of syndicates with intent to commit serious crimes while permitting penalties up to and including death. The Violence Act supplements this with enhanced penalties for collective and habitual violence, reflecting South Korea's particular historical experience with organized criminal gangs (조직폭력배).

4. **Harmonization model (EU/ECHR):** The EU framework establishes minimum standards while deferring to member state legal traditions. The dual criminalization model (participation or conspiracy) accommodates both continental and common-law approaches. The ECHR provides a human rights ceiling, ensuring that anti-organized-crime measures do not disproportionately infringe fundamental rights.

### Penalty Severity

South Korea imposes the most severe penalties, with the death penalty available for leaders of criminal organizations formed for violent purposes. The United States permits sentences up to life imprisonment where predicate offenses carry life sentences, with mandatory forfeiture adding a significant economic dimension. Germany's maximum of ten years for ringleaders in serious cases is comparatively moderate, reflecting a broader European emphasis on proportionality and rehabilitation. The EU Framework Decision sets a floor rather than a ceiling, requiring member states to provide maximum penalties of at least two to five years.

### Evidentiary and Procedural Differences

- **Burden of proof:** All jurisdictions require proof beyond a reasonable doubt in criminal proceedings, though the specific elements that must be proven differ substantially.
- **Witness protection:** The United States operates the most extensive witness protection program (WITSEC, established by the Organized Crime Control Act of 1970). Germany and South Korea have comparable programs, though on a smaller scale. The EU has recommended minimum standards for witness protection across member states.
- **Plea bargaining:** Widely used in the United States as a tool for securing cooperation from lower-ranking members against leadership. Germany introduced limited plea-bargaining provisions (Verständigung) through Section 257c of the Code of Criminal Procedure (StPO) in 2009. South Korea permits sentencing concessions for cooperation but lacks a formal plea-bargaining system. The EU Framework Decision encourages mitigating circumstances for cooperation.

### Digital Age Challenges

All four jurisdictions are grappling with the migration of organized crime into cyberspace. The BGH's 2023 ruling that Section 129 applies to purely online criminal associations; South Korea's 2024 statistics showing digital crimes surpassing traditional violent offenses among organized crime arrests; and the EU-SOCTA 2025's identification of AI-enabled crime as a defining threat all underscore the urgency of adapting legal frameworks designed for physical-world criminality to the digital environment.
