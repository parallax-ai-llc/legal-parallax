## Summary

Rights of nature -- the legal concept that ecosystems, rivers, forests, and other natural entities possess inherent rights to exist, thrive, and regenerate, independent of their utility to humans -- represents one of the most radical paradigm shifts in legal history, challenging the centuries-old anthropocentric framework that treats nature exclusively as property or a resource. South Korea has not enacted rights of nature legislation, though the Constitutional Court of Korea (헌법재판소) has recognized environmental rights under Article 35 of the Constitution (환경권), and the Framework Act on Environmental Policy (환경정책기본법) establishes the state's duty to preserve the natural environment; scholarly discussion of ecological rights (생태적 권리) has intensified following international developments. The United States has seen rights of nature emerge primarily through municipal ordinances and tribal governance, with the Community Environmental Legal Defense Fund (CELDF) assisting over 30 communities in adopting local rights of nature laws since 2006, though courts have generally struck down such ordinances as exceeding municipal authority, and the 2023 White Earth Band of Ojibwe recognition of wild rice rights (manoomin rights) represents a significant assertion of tribal sovereignty in this space. Germany does not recognize rights of nature per se, but the Grundgesetz (Basic Law) Article 20a establishes a constitutional obligation to protect natural foundations of life and animals (Schutz der natürlichen Lebensgrundlagen und der Tiere), and the BVerfG's Neubauer climate decision (2021) significantly expanded the constitutional dimension of environmental protection. The concept has seen its most dramatic legal developments outside these jurisdictions -- in Ecuador (constitutional enshrinement since 2008), New Zealand (Te Awa Tupua / Whanganui River, 2017), India (Ganges and Yamuna rivers, 2017, later stayed), Colombia (Atrato River, 2016), and Bolivia (Law of the Rights of Mother Earth, 2010) -- while the EU has engaged with the concept through the Nature Restoration Regulation (2024) and growing academic and policy discourse about ecocentric legal frameworks within the existing Treaty structure.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's Constitution (대한민국 헌법) provides the foundation for environmental protection but does not recognize rights of nature:

**Article 35(1):** "All citizens shall have the right to a healthy and pleasant environment (환경권). The State and all citizens shall endeavor to protect the environment."

**Article 35(2):** "The substance of the environmental right shall be determined by Act."

**Article 120(1):** "Permits to exploit, develop, or utilize minerals and all other important underground resources, marine resources, water power, and natural powers available for economic use may be granted for a period of time under the conditions as prescribed by Act."

### Environmental Rights Jurisprudence

The Constitutional Court (헌법재판소) has addressed environmental rights in several significant decisions:

**Constitutional Court Decision 2006Hun-Ma711 (2008):** The Court recognized that Article 35 creates both a subjective right (주관적 권리) enforceable by individuals and an objective normative obligation (객관적 규범) binding on the state. However, the Court has held that the environmental right requires legislative concretization and does not create a directly actionable right to specific environmental outcomes.

**Constitutional Court Decision 2019Hun-Ma15 (2021):** In a climate change-related petition, the Court addressed whether government climate policy was consistent with constitutional environmental obligations, though it stopped short of establishing binding emission reduction requirements.

### Academic Discourse

Korean legal scholars have increasingly engaged with rights of nature theory, particularly following developments in Ecuador and New Zealand. Professor Park Tae-hyun (박태현) at Kangwon National University and others have advocated for recognizing legal personhood of rivers and ecosystems, with the Nakdong River and Jirisan Mountain cited as potential candidates. The Korean Environmental Law Association (한국환경법학회) has hosted symposia on ecological rights (생태적 권리론).

### Practical Barriers

The practical adoption of rights of nature in South Korea faces several obstacles: the strong developmental state tradition prioritizing economic growth, the constitutional structure requiring legislative action to concretize environmental rights, the absence of indigenous legal traditions analogous to Māori or Andean concepts that have supported rights of nature elsewhere, and the dominant civil law framework treating nature as an object of legal protection rather than a subject of rights.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has no federal rights of nature legislation, and the Constitution contains no environmental right. However, rights of nature has emerged through grassroots municipal action, tribal governance, and a growing body of legal scholarship.

### Municipal Ordinances

Since 2006, over 30 U.S. communities have adopted local rights of nature ordinances, many with assistance from the Community Environmental Legal Defense Fund (CELDF):

| Jurisdiction | Year | Measure | Status |
|-------------|------|---------|--------|
| Tamaqua Borough, PA | 2006 | First rights of nature ordinance in U.S. (against sewage sludge dumping) | Adopted; not judicially tested |
| Pittsburgh, PA | 2010 | Rights of nature in anti-fracking ordinance | Adopted; superseded by state preemption |
| Santa Monica, CA | 2013 | Sustainability Rights Ordinance recognizing natural communities' right to exist | Adopted; symbolic |
| Toledo, OH | 2019 | Lake Erie Bill of Rights (voter initiative) | Struck down by federal court (Drewes Farms Partnership v. City of Toledo, 2020) |
| Orange County, FL | 2020 | Right to clean water initiative | Adopted by voters; legal challenges |

### Judicial Reception

Courts have generally been hostile to rights of nature claims:

**Drewes Farms Partnership v. City of Toledo, No. 3:19-cv-434 (N.D. Ohio 2020):** The federal court struck down the Lake Erie Bill of Rights, holding that the City of Toledo lacked authority to create legal rights for a lake that is managed by the state of Ohio and the federal government, and that the ordinance was preempted by state and federal law.

### Tribal Governance

The White Earth Band of Ojibwe in Minnesota adopted a Rights of Manoomin (wild rice) law in 2018, recognizing the inherent rights of wild rice to exist, flourish, regenerate, and evolve. In 2021, the White Earth Band filed a lawsuit in tribal court asserting the rights of manoomin against the Minnesota Department of Natural Resources' approval of a pipeline water appropriation permit. This represents a novel exercise of tribal sovereignty to advance rights of nature within indigenous governance structures.

### Academic and Policy Influence

Christopher Stone's seminal 1972 article "Should Trees Have Standing?" (published in the Southern California Law Review) provided the intellectual foundation for the movement. Justice William O. Douglas cited Stone in his famous dissent in Sierra Club v. Morton, 405 U.S. 727 (1972), arguing that "contemporary public concern for protecting nature's ecological equilibrium should lead to the conferral of standing upon environmental objects to sue for their own preservation."

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany does not recognize rights of nature, but provides robust constitutional protection for the natural environment:

**Article 20a Grundgesetz:** "Mindful also of its responsibility towards future generations, the state shall protect the natural foundations of life and animals by legislation and, in accordance with law and justice, by executive and judicial action, all within the framework of the constitutional order."

Article 20a was amended in 2002 to add explicit protection for animals (Tierschutz), making Germany the first EU member state to constitutionally protect animal welfare.

### BVerfG Neubauer Decision (2021)

The Federal Constitutional Court's climate decision of March 24, 2021 (1 BvR 2656/18) significantly expanded Article 20a's practical impact:

- Established that Article 20a creates an "intertemporal guarantee of freedom" (intertemporale Freiheitssicherung)
- Found that inadequate near-term climate action unconstitutionally restricts future generations' freedom
- Required the legislature to define a pathway for greenhouse gas reductions beyond 2030
- While not creating rights for nature itself, the decision imbues nature protection with constitutional urgency that approaches rights-like status in practical effect

### Animal Rights and Legal Personhood

Germany's constitutional protection of animals and its robust Tierschutzgesetz (Animal Protection Act) represent the closest approach to non-human rights in German law. While animals are not legal persons, Section 90a BGB (Civil Code) provides that "animals are not things" (Tiere sind keine Sachen) and that they are protected by special statutes. This represents an intermediate status between object and subject of law.

### Academic Discourse

German environmental law scholars have engaged critically with rights of nature theory. Professor Jens Kersten (Ludwig-Maximilians-Universität München) has published extensively on the legal personhood of ecosystems, arguing that German law could accommodate rights of nature through creative interpretation of existing constitutional and civil law provisions. The Sachverständigenrat für Umweltfragen (German Advisory Council on the Environment) has discussed ecological rights in its reports.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Environmental Protection

While the ECHR does not contain an explicit right to a healthy environment, the Court has developed environmental protection through Articles 2 and 8:

**KlimaSeniorinnen v. Switzerland (Grand Chamber, 2024):** The Court found that Article 8 requires states to adopt adequate measures to combat climate change, establishing positive obligations for environmental protection. While not recognizing rights of nature, the decision significantly strengthens the legal framework for nature protection.

**Duarte Agostinho and Others v. Portugal and Others (Grand Chamber, 2024):** Though declared inadmissible on procedural grounds, the case (brought by Portuguese youth against 32 European states) raised questions about intergenerational environmental justice that connect to rights of nature theory.

### EU Charter and Treaty Provisions

**Article 37 of the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights:** "A high level of environmental protection and the improvement of the quality of the environment must be integrated into the policies of the Union and ensured in accordance with the principle of sustainable development."

**Article 191 TFEU:** Establishes EU environmental policy objectives including preserving, protecting, and improving the quality of the environment, protecting human health, and the prudent utilization of natural resources.

### Nature Restoration Regulation (EU) 2024/1991

While not framed in rights-of-nature language, the Nature Restoration Regulation represents the closest EU approach to an ecocentric legal framework:
- Binding restoration targets for degraded ecosystems (at least 20% of EU land and sea areas by 2030)
- Specific targets for forest, marine, freshwater, urban, and agricultural ecosystems
- Non-deterioration obligations preventing degradation of restored ecosystems
- Implicitly treats ecosystems as having value independent of human utility

The European Economic and Social Committee adopted an opinion on "Rights of Nature" (2020) encouraging the EU to explore ecocentric legal frameworks.

## Comparative Analysis

### Constitutional Foundations

All four jurisdictions provide some constitutional basis for environmental protection, but none currently recognizes inherent rights of nature. South Korea's Article 35 creates an individual environmental right requiring legislative concretization. Germany's Article 20a creates a state obligation (Staatszielbestimmung) rather than an individual right. The EU Charter's Article 37 establishes a principle rather than a right. The ECHR's Articles 2 and 8 address environmental harm only through the lens of human rights.

### Pathways for Adoption

The most likely pathways for rights of nature adoption differ by jurisdiction. In South Korea, legislative action would be required given the constitutional structure. In the United States, tribal sovereignty offers the most promising avenue, as demonstrated by the White Earth manoomin case. In Germany, judicial interpretation of Article 20a could potentially evolve toward rights-like protections. In the EU, the Nature Restoration Regulation creates an ecocentric framework that could be deepened over time.

### Indigenous Perspectives

The rights of nature movement has been most successful where it connects to indigenous worldviews (Māori, Andean, tribal). South Korea, Germany, and the EU lack strong indigenous traditions that would support such claims. The United States' tribal sovereignty framework provides a unique bridge between indigenous ecological knowledge and formal legal recognition.

### Practical Effectiveness

The practical effectiveness of rights of nature declarations varies dramatically. Ecuador's constitutional rights have had limited enforcement impact. New Zealand's Whanganui River framework has successfully shifted governance but is embedded in a broader Treaty settlement context. Colombia's Atrato River decision has prompted government action but implementation challenges persist. The key question is whether formal recognition translates into materially better environmental outcomes.
