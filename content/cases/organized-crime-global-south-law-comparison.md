## Summary

Organized crime law across the Global South addresses fundamentally different criminal phenomena -- from Chinese triads and Xi Jinping's sweeping "Scanning Black and Eliminating Evil" campaign (扫黑除恶, sao hei chu e) to Indian organized crime syndicates operating through the Maharashtra Control of Organised Crime Act 1999 (MCOCA) and encounter killings, Mexican cartels that control significant national territory, Nigerian cyber-fraud networks and oil-theft syndicates, South African gang violence under the Prevention of Organised Crime Act 121 of 1998 (POCA), Brazilian organized criminal factions like the Primeiro Comando da Capital (PCC) and Comando Vermelho (CV), Colombian narco-paramilitary structures, and Southeast Asian organized crime encompassing Indonesian preman (criminal gangs) and Philippine syndicates. The legal responses vary from China's campaign-style enforcement mobilizing the entire party-state apparatus, to sophisticated legal frameworks modeled on the UN Convention against Transnational Organized Crime (UNTOC, Palermo Convention, 2000), to extrajudicial measures that bypass formal legal channels entirely. Common to all is the challenge of organized crime's deep penetration into state institutions, economies, and communities, making enforcement inseparable from questions of governance, corruption, and political economy.

## China

### Legal Framework

Article 294 of the Criminal Law criminalizes organizing, leading, or participating in "underworld-nature organizations" (黑社会性质组织). The SPC, SPP, Ministry of Public Security, and Ministry of Justice's 2018 Guiding Opinions defined four characteristics:

1. **Organizational structure** -- clear hierarchy with relatively stable membership
2. **Economic foundation** -- accumulating assets through criminal activity or legitimate business fronts
3. **Use of force** -- employing violence, threats, or other coercive means to commit organized crimes
4. **Territorial/industry control** -- dominating a particular geographic area or industry through illegal control, creating serious social disorder

Article 294(2) addresses organizations with "underworld-nature characteristics" that do not fully meet the definition of organized crime organizations.

The Anti-Organized Crime Law of the People's Republic of China (反有组织犯罪法), effective May 1, 2022, represents China's first comprehensive statutory framework dedicated to organized crime. Its structure includes:

- Chapter 1: General Provisions (definitions, principles)
- Chapter 2: Prevention (social governance, industry regulation)
- Chapter 3: Investigation, Prosecution, and Trial (powers, procedures, evidence)
- Chapter 4: Asset Seizure, Forfeiture, and Confiscation
- Chapter 5: International Cooperation
- Chapter 6: Legal Liability Article 22 authorizes the Public Security Bureau to freeze accounts and seize assets during investigation. Article 45 mandates that entities and individuals must not provide financial support to organized crime groups.

### Scanning Black and Eliminating Evil Campaign

The "Scanning Black and Eliminating Evil" campaign (扫黑除恶专项斗争), launched by the CPC Central Committee and State Council in January 2018, represented a three-year nationwide mobilization (extended through subsequent phases) targeting organized crime at all levels. The campaign investigated over 3,600 criminal organizations and more than 15,000 criminal gangs, with over 237,000 suspects arrested by 2020. Central Supervisory Groups (中央督导组) were dispatched to all provinces to oversee implementation and prevent local protectionism.

The campaign explicitly targeted the "protective umbrellas" (保护伞, baohu san) -- corrupt officials shielding organized crime. Over 89,000 officials were investigated for connections to organized crime during the campaign. High-profile cases included Sun Xiaoguo in Yunnan (whose prior death sentence had been commuted through corruption -- his case was retried and he was executed in 2020) and the Zhangjiajie "village tyrant" cases targeting rural clan-based criminal organizations.

Critics noted the campaign's mobilizational character -- with quotas, denunciation hotlines, and political pressure to produce results -- risked overcriminalization. The SPC issued guidance cautioning against inflating statistics by mischaracterizing ordinary crimes as organized crime. The campaign demonstrated both the CPC's capacity for mass mobilization and the risks of campaign-style justice.

### Triads and Cross-Border Crime

Traditional Chinese triads (三合会, sanhe hui) -- historically rooted in Hong Kong, Macau, and southern China -- include the Sun Yee On, 14K, and Wo Shing Wo. The Anti-Organized Crime Law Article 60 addresses cross-border organized crime cooperation. The Closer Economic Partnership Arrangement (CEPA) and Mutual Legal Assistance agreements between mainland China, Hong Kong, and Macau facilitate cross-jurisdictional enforcement. Mainland China's authorities have also targeted Chinese nationals operating transnational telecommunications fraud (电信诈骗, dianxin zhapian) networks from Southeast Asia, particularly Myanmar, Cambodia, and Laos, repatriating tens of thousands in joint operations with host-country authorities.

## India

### MCOCA and State Laws

The Maharashtra Control of Organised Crime Act 1999 (MCOCA) was India's first dedicated organized crime statute, enacted in response to the Bombay underworld (including the D-Company of Dawood Ibrahim, a designated global terrorist under UNSC Resolution 1267). MCOCA Section 2(1)(e) defines "organised crime" as "any continuing unlawful activity by an individual, singly or jointly, either as a member of an organised crime syndicate or on behalf of such syndicate, by use of violence or threat of violence or intimidation or coercion, or other unlawful means." Section 3 prescribes life imprisonment or death for organized crime resulting in death, and a minimum of five years for other organized crime. Section 18 makes intercepted communications admissible as evidence -- an exceptional provision in Indian criminal law.

Other states have enacted comparable legislation: the Karnataka Control of Organised Crime Act 2000 (KCOCA), Andhra Pradesh's state-specific provisions, and the Gujarat Control of Terrorism and Organised Crime Act 2003 (GCTOCA, modeled on the repealed POTA). Uttar Pradesh enacted the Gangsters and Anti-Social Activities (Prevention) Act 1986, amended in 2017, used extensively in Chief Minister Yogi Adityanath's "bulldozer justice" campaign targeting alleged criminals' properties.

### Encounter Killings

India's extrajudicial "encounter killing" phenomenon represents an informal parallel system of organized crime enforcement. The Mumbai Police's "encounter squad" era of the 1990s-2000s, during which officers like Daya Nayak, Pradeep Sharma, and Sachin Waze killed alleged gang members, was partly formalized through departmental commendations and media glorification. The Supreme Court's PUCL guidelines (2014) mandated independent investigation of every encounter death. Despite this, the Uttar Pradesh government under Chief Minister Adityanath reported over 180 encounter killings between 2017 and 2023, with NHRC investigations finding evidence of staged encounters.

### Sand Mafia and Resource Extraction

India's "sand mafia" -- illegal sand mining operations controlling riverbed extraction -- represents a distinctive form of organized crime intertwined with construction industry demand, political patronage, and environmental destruction. The Supreme Court in Deepak Kumar v. State of Haryana (2012) 4 SCC 629 and the National Green Tribunal have issued extensive orders regulating sand mining. Investigative journalists and government officials attempting to curb illegal mining have been murdered -- the 2017 killing of IAS officer K. Durga Prasad Rao and multiple journalist murders documented by the Committee to Protect Journalists highlight the threat. The Mines and Minerals (Development and Regulation) Act 1957, as amended in 2015, provides the statutory framework, but enforcement is hampered by corruption and the political connections of mining mafias.

## Africa

### Nigeria

Nigeria's organized crime landscape encompasses cyber-enabled fraud (known colloquially as "419" fraud after Section 419 of the Criminal Code, or "Yahoo Yahoo"), oil theft (crude oil bunkering), kidnapping for ransom, piracy in the Gulf of Guinea, and drug trafficking networks. The EFCC Act 2004 and the Money Laundering (Prohibition) Act 2011 (amended 2022) are the primary enforcement instruments. Section 419 of the Criminal Code addresses "obtaining property by false pretences" with a maximum of 7 years, while the Advance Fee Fraud and Other Fraud Related Offences Act 2006 prescribes 7-20 years for advance fee fraud.

Nigeria's Cybercrimes (Prohibition, Prevention, Etc.) Act 2015 addresses internet-facilitated organized crime, prescribing up to 7 years for identity theft (Section 22) and up to 5 years for cyberstalking (Section 24). The economic scale of cyber-fraud is substantial -- the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3) consistently identifies Nigerian nationals among the top international perpetrators of business email compromise (BEC) fraud, estimated at over $2.7 billion globally in 2022.

Oil theft (illegal crude oil bunkering) in the Niger Delta constitutes one of Nigeria's most significant organized crime challenges, with estimates of 100,000-400,000 barrels per day stolen (Stakeholder Democracy Network and NEITI reports), involving a complex network of community youth groups, local politicians, military personnel, and international criminal networks. The Petroleum Industry Act 2021 established a new regulatory framework but has not resolved enforcement challenges. The Nigerian Maritime Administration and Safety Agency (NIMASA) and Nigerian Navy conduct anti-piracy operations under the Suppression of Piracy and Other Maritime Offences Act 2019, implementing the Yaounde Code of Conduct for West and Central Africa.

### South Africa

South Africa's Prevention of Organised Crime Act 121 of 1998 (POCA) introduced a comprehensive framework modeled partly on the U.S. RICO Act. Chapter 2 criminalizes racketeering activities -- receiving property derived from a "pattern of racketeering activity" (two or more offenses within 10 years, including 35 scheduled offenses). Chapter 4 addresses criminal gang activities, defining a "criminal gang" as "any formal or informal ongoing organisation, association, or group of three or more persons, which has as one of its activities the commission of one or more criminal offences." Chapters 5 and 6 establish civil and criminal asset forfeiture respectively, with the Asset Forfeiture Unit (AFU) of the NPA conducting civil forfeiture proceedings on a balance of probabilities standard.

Cape Town's gang violence -- involving established gangs like the Hard Livings, Americans, and Fancy Boys operating primarily in the Cape Flats -- represents a persistent security crisis. The Western Cape Provincial Government declared gang violence a "provincial disaster" in 2019. The Anti-Gang Unit, established within SAPS in 2018, conducts targeted operations. The Prevention of Organised Crime Amendment Act (proposed) seeks to strengthen gang identification and prosecution provisions.

The "construction mafia" phenomenon -- organized criminal groups extorting construction projects, particularly government infrastructure contracts, through intimidation and violence in KwaZulu-Natal and Gauteng -- represents an emerging threat. The South African Forum of Civil Engineering Contractors (SAFCEC) has documented hundreds of incidents. The SIU (Special Investigating Unit) and NPA have initiated investigations, but the phenomenon reflects deeper issues of unemployment, inequality, and state weakness.

The "zama-zama" crisis -- involving illegal miners, predominantly from Zimbabwe and Mozambique, operating in abandoned and active mines, particularly on the Witwatersrand gold belt -- has produced violent turf wars, community-level conflict, and environmental contamination. The Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act 28 of 2002 and the Mine Health and Safety Act 29 of 1996 provide the statutory framework, but enforcement in abandoned mines is practically impossible.

The February 2024 Stilfontein mine siege -- in which hundreds of illegal miners were trapped underground for weeks before being forced to the surface by police operations -- exposed the scale of the zama-zama phenomenon and raised questions about the proportionality of state responses. The North West High Court issued orders regarding the treatment of the miners, while civil society organizations challenged the SAPS's approach.

The July 2021 unrest in KwaZulu-Natal and Gauteng -- triggered by the imprisonment of former President Jacob Zuma but involving organized looting of shopping centers, warehouses, and supply chains -- demonstrated the intersection of political instability and organized criminal opportunism. Over 350 people died and an estimated R50 billion in damages occurred. The South African Institute of Security Studies (ISS) and expert panel report (November 2021) found evidence of organized instigation, with criminal networks exploiting political unrest for looting operations.

## Latin America

### Brazil: PCC and Comando Vermelho

Brazil's organized crime landscape is dominated by prison-born criminal factions. The Primeiro Comando da Capital (PCC, First Capital Command), founded in the Taubate prison in 1993, operates across all 27 Brazilian states and internationally (in Paraguay, Bolivia, and Mozambique), controlling drug distribution, arms trafficking, and extortion. The PCC's 2006 attacks on Sao Paulo -- coordinated from prison via cellular phones -- killed 59 security agents and led to 505 arrests. The PCC operates through a sophisticated organizational structure with a "Party Statute" (Estatuto do Partido), internal disciplinary proceedings (debates), and a welfare system for members' families. The Comando Vermelho (CV, Red Command), founded in the 1970s in Rio de Janeiro's Ilha Grande penal colony, controls favela territories in Rio.

Law 12.850/2013 (Lei das Organizacoes Criminosas) defines a criminal organization as "the association of 4 (four) or more persons structurally ordered and characterized by the division of tasks, even if informally, with the objective of obtaining, directly or indirectly, advantage of any nature, through the practice of criminal infractions whose maximum penalties are greater than 4 (four) years, or that are transnational in character." The law authorizes colaboracao premiada (rewarded collaboration), infiltration of agents, controlled delivery, and interception of communications. The Regime Disciplinar Diferenciado (RDD, Differentiated Disciplinary Regime), established by Law 10.792/2003, permits solitary confinement of up to 360 days (extended to 2 years by the 2019 Anti-Crime Law, Law 13.964/2019) for organized crime leaders.

The milicia (militia) phenomenon in Rio de Janeiro -- armed groups composed of active and retired police officers, firefighters, and military personnel controlling territories through extortion of utility services, transportation, and construction -- has been the subject of dedicated investigation. The Parliamentary Inquiry Commission (CPI das Milicias, 2008) documented militia control of communities, and former Rio de Janeiro councilwoman Marielle Franco, an outspoken militia critic, was assassinated in March 2018, with former police officer Ronnie Lessa convicted in 2024.

### Mexico: Cartels and State Response

Mexico's organized crime crisis involves multiple drug trafficking organizations (DTOs): the Sinaloa Cartel (fractured following the 2023 arrest of Ovidio Guzman Lopez and the 2024 capture of Ismael "El Mayo" Zambada), the Cartel Jalisco Nueva Generacion (CJNG, led by Nemesio Oseguera Cervantes "El Mencho"), remnants of Los Zetas, the Gulf Cartel, and regional organizations. These groups engage in drug trafficking, extortion (derecho de piso), kidnapping, human trafficking, illegal mining, avocado industry control (in Michoacan), and oil theft (huachicol).

The Federal Law against Organized Crime (Ley Federal contra la Delincuencia Organizada, 1996) defines organized crime as the agreement of three or more persons to organize or be organized to carry out any of the crimes enumerated in Article 2 (terrorism, drug trafficking, arms trafficking, trafficking in persons, kidnapping, vehicle theft, money laundering, and others). The law authorizes special investigation techniques including undercover agents (Article 11), witness protection (Article 34), and arraigo (pre-charge detention for up to 80 days, constitutionalized in Article 16 of the Constitution by the 2008 reform, though the Inter-American Commission on Human Rights has repeatedly criticized this provision).

The autodefensa (self-defense group) phenomenon in Michoacan and Guerrero -- where communities armed themselves against cartel violence, particularly the Knights Templar (Caballeros Templarios) -- raised complex legal questions. The Federal Commissioner for the Development of Sustainable Security Zones attempted to incorporate autodefensas into a Rural Defense Corps in 2014, with mixed results. Some autodefensa leaders, including Jose Manuel Mireles, were subsequently arrested; others, like the former autodefensa leader Hipolito Mora, were killed in cartel violence.

## Southeast Asia

### Indonesia

Indonesia's organized crime landscape includes preman (from the Dutch vrijman, "free man") -- criminal gangs historically tolerated and used by political authorities. During Suharto's New Order regime (1966-1998), preman groups like Pemuda Pancasila and the Forum Komunikasi Putra-Putri Purnawirawan Indonesia maintained quasi-official roles in political intimidation and extortion. The 1983-1985 "Petrus" (Penembakan Misterius, mysterious shootings) campaign, in which security forces extrajudicially killed an estimated 5,000-10,000 suspected criminals, was later acknowledged by President Suharto in his autobiography.

Post-reformasi Indonesia has addressed organized crime through sectoral legislation rather than a consolidated framework: the Anti-Money Laundering Law (Law No. 8 of 2010), the Anti-Trafficking Law (Law No. 21 of 2007), the Narcotics Law (Law No. 35 of 2009), and the Electronic Information and Transactions Law (Law No. 11 of 2008, amended by Law No. 19 of 2016). Indonesia ratified UNTOC in 2009 through Law No. 5 of 2009. The Indonesian Financial Transaction Reports and Analysis Centre (PPATK, Pusat Pelaporan dan Analisis Transaksi Keuangan) serves as the financial intelligence unit.

Illegal mining (pertambangan tanpa izin, PETI) in Kalimantan, Sulawesi, and Bangka-Belitung involves organized networks of miners, traders, and corrupt officials. The Mineral and Coal Mining Law (Law No. 3 of 2020, amending Law No. 4 of 2009) provides the regulatory framework. Illegal, unreported, and unregulated (IUU) fishing, involving both domestic and foreign (primarily Chinese and Vietnamese) vessels, represents another significant organized crime challenge, addressed through the Ministry of Marine Affairs and Fisheries' vessel-sinking policy (initiated under Minister Susi Pudjiastuti, 2014-2019) and Law No. 45 of 2009 on Fisheries.

### Philippines

The Philippines' organized crime landscape includes drug syndicates (both domestic and Chinese-connected), kidnapping-for-ransom groups (particularly targeting ethnic Chinese-Filipino businesspersons), illegal gambling operations (including Philippine Offshore Gaming Operators, POGOs, linked to Chinese organized crime), and illegal logging and mining networks. The kidnapping epidemic in the 1990s-2000s, concentrated in Mindanao and involving the Abu Sayyaf Group, prompted the enactment of the Anti-Kidnapping and Serious Illegal Detention Act (RA 7659, amending Article 267 of the Revised Penal Code) prescribing reclusion perpetua to death.

POGO operations, regulated under the Philippine Amusement and Gaming Corporation (PAGCOR) and the Offshore Gaming Licensing regime, became a major organized crime vector. Reports of human trafficking, torture, and kidnapping at POGO compounds -- particularly in Bamban, Tarlac (linked to former mayor Alice Guo, whose citizenship was questioned) and Clark Freeport Zone -- prompted President Marcos Jr. to order a total POGO ban effective December 31, 2024 (Executive Order No. 74, 2024). The Senate Committee on Women, Children, Family Relations and Gender Equality conducted extensive hearings in 2024 exposing the POGO-organized crime nexus.

The Anti-Money Laundering Act (AMLA, RA 9160 of 2001, as amended by RA 10167, 10365, and 11521) and the Terrorism Financing Prevention and Suppression Act (RA 10168 of 2012) provide the financial crime framework. The Anti-Money Laundering Council (AMLC) investigates suspicious transactions and can petition for civil forfeiture of assets related to unlawful activities. The Philippines was removed from the FATF grey list in June 2025 after completing its action plan.

## Comparative Analysis

### Criminal Organization Definitions

Global South approaches to defining organized crime reflect different legal traditions. China's "underworld-nature organization" concept emphasizes territorial or industry domination and the nexus with corruption ("protective umbrellas"). Brazil's four-person threshold with structural ordering is relatively straightforward. Mexico's three-person threshold with enumerated offenses is narrower. South Africa's POCA adopts the "pattern of racketeering activity" model. India lacks a unified national definition, relying on state-level legislation. These definitional differences carry practical consequences for prosecution, extradition, and international cooperation.

### State-Crime Nexus

The most distinctive feature of Global South organized crime is its deep interpenetration with the state. Chinese organized crime operates through "protective umbrellas" of corrupt officials. Indian criminal organizations maintain documented ties to political parties (the "criminalization of politics" -- the Association for Democratic Reforms found that 43% of Lok Sabha members elected in 2024 face criminal charges). Nigerian oil theft involves military and political complicity at the highest levels. Brazilian milicias are composed of state security personnel. Mexican cartels have captured state and municipal governments across multiple states. This state-crime nexus means that anti-organized crime enforcement cannot be separated from anti-corruption efforts.

### Campaign Justice vs. Rule of Law

China's campaign-style approach to organized crime -- mobilizing the entire party-state apparatus through the "Scanning Black and Eliminating Evil" campaign -- contrasts with the legalistic framework approaches of Latin America and South Africa. The campaign model achieves rapid results (3,600 criminal organizations dismantled in three years) but raises concerns about due process, overcriminalization, and political manipulation. The legalistic model produces sophisticated statutory frameworks but may be ineffective against deeply entrenched criminal organizations, as Mexico's experience demonstrates.

### Extrajudicial Enforcement

Extrajudicial responses to organized crime span the Global South. Indonesia's Petrus killings, India's encounter killings, Brazil's police operations and militia activity, the Philippines' drug war, and Nigeria's military operations all represent state or quasi-state violence that operates outside formal legal channels. These extrajudicial responses typically target the most marginalized members of criminal organizations while leaving leadership and institutional structures intact.

### Cybercrime and Digital Organized Crime

The digitalization of organized crime represents an emerging challenge across all Global South jurisdictions. Nigerian "Yahoo Boys" have evolved from advance-fee fraud (419) to sophisticated business email compromise (BEC), romance scams, and cryptocurrency fraud, generating billions in illicit proceeds. Chinese telecommunications fraud networks (电信诈骗) operate from compounds in Myanmar, Cambodia, and Laos, using trafficked laborers as scam operators -- the Chinese government repatriated over 50,000 suspects from northern Myanmar alone in 2023-2024. Indian call-center scams targeting Western consumers have been documented by the CBI and international law enforcement. Brazilian cybercrime groups target banking systems through malware (Grandoreiro, Mekotio trojans). Mexican cartels use social media for recruitment and encrypted communications for coordination. Indonesian and Philippine online gambling operations serve as fronts for money laundering. The legal responses lag behind: most Global South cybercrime laws (China's Cybersecurity Law 2017, India's IT Act 2000/2008, Nigeria's Cybercrimes Act 2015, Brazil's Marco Civil da Internet 2014, Indonesia's ITE Law 2008/2016, Philippines' Cybercrime Prevention Act RA 10175/2012) were designed for an earlier technological era and struggle to address AI-enhanced fraud, cryptocurrency laundering, and cross-border digital crime networks.

### Prison-Based Organized Crime

A distinctive Global South phenomenon is the operation of organized crime from within prisons. Brazil's PCC was founded in and continues to operate from the prison system, with imprisoned leaders conducting organizational business via smuggled phones and through family and lawyer intermediaries -- the Regime Disciplinar Diferenciado was specifically designed to counter this. Mexico's cartels maintain operational control from federal prisons, as demonstrated by the 2001 escape of Joaquin "El Chapo" Guzman from Puente Grande prison (with alleged official complicity) and his second escape from Altiplano prison in 2015 via a tunnel. In the Philippines, the New Bilibid Prison drug trade -- exposed in the 2014 Senate investigation revealing drug laboratory operations within the facility under the protection of prison officials -- demonstrated the depth of organized crime's institutional penetration. Nigerian prisons serve as recruitment grounds for organized crime, with the National Bureau of Statistics reporting severe overcrowding (over 72,000 inmates in facilities designed for 50,153). South Africa's "Number" gangs (26s, 27s, 28s), originating in colonial-era prisons, maintain elaborate hierarchies and codes within the Department of Correctional Services. This prison-organized crime nexus renders mass incarceration counterproductive, as prisons function as schools and operational bases for criminal organizations rather than instruments of rehabilitation.
