## Summary

Geoengineering governance -- the legal and regulatory frameworks addressing deliberate large-scale interventions in the Earth's climate system to counteract climate change, encompassing both solar radiation management (SRM) techniques such as stratospheric aerosol injection and marine cloud brightening, and carbon dioxide removal (CDR) methods such as direct air capture, bioenergy with carbon capture and storage (BECCS), and ocean alkalinity enhancement -- represents one of the most legally underdeveloped yet rapidly evolving areas of international and domestic law, raising unprecedented questions about state sovereignty, intergenerational justice, transboundary environmental harm, and the governance of technologies with potentially planetary-scale consequences. South Korea has no specific geoengineering legislation but addresses climate intervention through the Framework Act on Carbon Neutrality and Green Growth (탄소중립·녹색성장 기본법), enacted December 2021, which establishes carbon neutrality by 2050 as a legally binding target and authorizes research into carbon capture, utilization, and storage (CCUS) technologies under Articles 36-38; the Korea Meteorological Administration (기상청) regulates weather modification under the Weather Service Act (기상법) Article 17, requiring permits for cloud seeding and other atmospheric interventions. The United States has no comprehensive geoengineering governance statute, with relevant authority scattered across the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA), the Department of Energy's Carbon Negative Shot program, and the White House Office of Science and Technology Policy, which published a five-year research plan on solar and other climate interventions in June 2023; the Weather Modification Reporting Act of 1972 (15 U.S.C. Section 330) requires reporting of weather modification activities to the Secretary of Commerce, and individual states including Tennessee have enacted outright bans on stratospheric aerosol injection. Germany addresses geoengineering primarily through the Federal Immission Control Act (Bundes-Immissionsschutzgesetz, BImSchG) and general environmental impact assessment requirements under the UVPG, with the German Bundestag's Office of Technology Assessment (TAB) publishing influential reports in 2012 and 2022 recommending precautionary governance frameworks; German research institutions lead the EU's extensive research programs under Horizon Europe. At the international and European level, the Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD) Decision X/33 (2010) established a de facto moratorium on geoengineering activities that may affect biodiversity, the London Protocol to the Convention on the Prevention of Marine Pollution by Dumping of Wastes amended Article 6bis in 2013 to regulate marine geoengineering, the UNFCCC and Paris Agreement do not explicitly address geoengineering but their emissions reduction and adaptation frameworks create the context within which geoengineering is debated, and the European Court of Human Rights' climate jurisprudence -- particularly KlimaSeniorinnen v. Switzerland (2024) -- has established that states bear positive obligations under Articles 2 and 8 ECHR to protect against climate change impacts, potentially affecting future geoengineering governance decisions.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's geoengineering governance exists within the broader framework of its ambitious climate commitments and lacks a dedicated geoengineering statute.

**Framework Act on Carbon Neutrality and Green Growth (탄소중립·녹색성장 기본법):** Enacted on September 24, 2021, and effective March 25, 2022, this landmark statute legally commits South Korea to achieving net-zero greenhouse gas emissions by 2050 and establishes interim reduction targets. Articles 36-38 specifically address carbon capture, utilization, and storage (CCUS):

- **Article 36:** The government shall establish and implement policies to promote CCUS technologies.
- **Article 37:** The Minister of Trade, Industry and Energy shall formulate a comprehensive plan for CCUS technology development and deployment every five years.
- **Article 38:** Operators of CCUS facilities must obtain permits and comply with safety and environmental standards established by presidential decree.

**Weather Service Act (기상법) Article 17:** This provision regulates weather modification activities (기상조절), defined as artificial interventions in atmospheric processes to alter weather conditions. Any person or entity intending to conduct weather modification must obtain a permit from the Korea Meteorological Administration (기상청). The KMA has conducted cloud seeding operations for precipitation enhancement and fog dispersal, particularly at Incheon International Airport.

**Environmental Impact Assessment Act (환경영향평가법):** Large-scale environmental interventions, including potential geoengineering activities, are subject to environmental impact assessment under this Act. The assessment process requires public participation and review by the Korea Environment Institute.

### Research and Policy

The Korea Institute of Ocean Science and Technology (KIOST) has conducted research on ocean-based CDR methods including ocean alkalinity enhancement and blue carbon ecosystem restoration. The National Institute of Meteorological Sciences (국립기상과학원) operates cloud seeding research programs. In 2023, the Ministry of Science and ICT announced a KRW 500 billion investment in CCUS technology development through 2030.

South Korea has been cautious about SRM research, consistent with its diplomatic emphasis on multilateral climate cooperation. The government has not taken a formal position on the CBD geoengineering moratorium but has emphasized the importance of international governance frameworks.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has no comprehensive geoengineering governance statute, and regulatory authority is fragmented across multiple agencies.

**Weather Modification Reporting Act of 1972 (15 U.S.C. Section 330):** This statute requires any person engaged in weather modification activities to submit reports to the Secretary of Commerce describing the nature, extent, and purposes of the activity. The Act does not require permits or impose substantive restrictions, functioning solely as a transparency measure. NOAA administers the reporting requirement.

**National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA, 42 U.S.C. Section 4321 et seq.):** Major federal actions significantly affecting the environment require environmental impact statements (EIS) under NEPA. Large-scale geoengineering research or deployment funded or authorized by federal agencies would trigger NEPA review.

**Clean Air Act (42 U.S.C. Section 7401 et seq.):** The EPA's authority to regulate emissions and air quality under the Clean Air Act could potentially extend to stratospheric aerosol injection, though no EPA rulemaking has addressed this application. The Supreme Court's recognition in Massachusetts v. EPA, 549 U.S. 497 (2007), that greenhouse gases are "air pollutants" under the Act may create an analogous framework for regulating intentional atmospheric interventions.

**OSTP Research Plan (2023):** In June 2023, the White House Office of Science and Technology Policy published "Congressionally Mandated Research Plan and an Initial Research Governance Framework Related to Solar Radiation Modification," responding to a directive in the Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2022. The plan recommended a five-year research program on SRM technologies, risks, and governance, while emphasizing that research should not substitute for emissions reduction.

**45Q Tax Credit (26 U.S.C. Section 45Q):** Expanded by the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022, the 45Q tax credit provides up to $180 per metric ton for direct air capture with geological storage and up to $60 per metric ton for point-source carbon capture, creating significant financial incentives for CDR deployment.

**State-Level Actions:** Several states have addressed geoengineering:

| State | Action | Year |
|-------|--------|------|
| Tennessee | SB 2691/HB 2063: bans intentional injection of chemicals into the atmosphere for weather modification or geoengineering | 2024 |
| New Hampshire | HB 1700: prohibits stratospheric aerosol injection and mandates atmospheric testing | 2024 |
| Rhode Island | H 7787: proposed ban on geoengineering activities | 2024 |

### Private Sector Activity

The absence of comprehensive federal regulation has permitted private sector geoengineering activities. Make Sunsets, a Silicon Valley startup, conducted unauthorized stratospheric aerosol injection experiments in Mexico in December 2022 using weather balloons carrying sulfur dioxide, prompting Mexico to ban solar geoengineering experiments in January 2023. The incident highlighted the governance vacuum and the potential for unilateral private action.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany addresses geoengineering through general environmental law frameworks and has been influential in shaping EU and international governance discussions.

**Bundes-Immissionsschutzgesetz (BImSchG) -- Federal Immission Control Act:** The BImSchG regulates emissions and immissions (impacts) affecting the environment, including atmospheric releases. Stratospheric aerosol injection or other atmospheric interventions would require permits under the BImSchG if they constitute installations or activities releasing substances into the atmosphere.

**Kohlendioxid-Speicherungsgesetz (KSpG) -- Carbon Dioxide Storage Act (2012):** This statute transposed the EU CCS Directive 2009/31/EC into German law, establishing a regulatory framework for carbon capture and geological storage. The Act adopted a restrictive approach, limiting CCS deployment to demonstration projects with a maximum annual storage capacity of 4 million tonnes per site and requiring federal state consent for storage sites within their territory. Several Länder effectively blocked CCS deployment through their veto power.

**Umweltverträglichkeitsprüfungsgesetz (UVPG) -- Environmental Impact Assessment Act:** Large-scale geoengineering projects would be subject to environmental impact assessment under the UVPG, which transposes EU EIA Directive 2011/92/EU.

### Bundestag Office of Technology Assessment (TAB)

The TAB has produced two influential reports on geoengineering governance:

**TAB Report No. 152 (2012) -- "Geoengineering":** This comprehensive assessment examined the technical feasibility, risks, and governance challenges of CDR and SRM technologies. The report concluded that SRM techniques raise fundamental governance challenges due to their transboundary effects and potential for unilateral deployment, and recommended international governance frameworks based on the precautionary principle.

**TAB Report (2022) -- "Climate Engineering Revisited":** Updated assessment examining the evolution of geoengineering technologies and governance since 2012, noting the increasing urgency of CDR deployment given insufficient emissions reduction progress, while reaffirming concerns about SRM governance gaps.

### Research Programs

German research institutions are leading participants in EU-funded geoengineering research. The Max Planck Institute for Meteorology in Hamburg has conducted climate modeling of SRM scenarios. The Helmholtz Association's research centers are involved in CDR research including direct air capture and enhanced weathering. The GEOMAR Helmholtz Centre for Ocean Research in Kiel leads research on ocean-based CDR.

### Political Position

Germany has advocated for precautionary governance of SRM at the international level, including at the UN Environment Assembly (UNEA). In 2023, Germany co-sponsored a Swiss-led UNEA resolution to establish an expert group on solar radiation modification, which was not adopted due to opposition from Saudi Arabia and other states.

## ECHR & EU

### Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD) Decision X/33

At the tenth Conference of the Parties (COP 10) in Nagoya, Japan, in October 2010, CBD parties adopted Decision X/33, which established a de facto moratorium on geoengineering:

Paragraph 8(w) invited parties to ensure that "no climate-related geo-engineering activities that may affect biodiversity take place, until there is an adequate scientific basis on which to justify such activities and appropriate consideration of the associated risks for the environment and biodiversity and associated social, economic and cultural impacts," with the exception of "small-scale scientific research studies that would be conducted in a controlled setting in accordance with Article 3 of the Convention."

This language creates a presumption against large-scale geoengineering deployment while permitting controlled research. The decision is non-binding but represents the most significant multilateral governance framework for geoengineering.

### London Protocol Amendment (2013)

The 2013 amendment to the London Protocol (1996 Protocol to the Convention on the Prevention of Marine Pollution by Dumping of Wastes) added Article 6bis, specifically addressing marine geoengineering. The amendment establishes a regulatory framework for marine-based climate interventions, requiring an assessment framework and permitting system. Initially focused on ocean fertilization, the framework was expanded in 2023 to cover other marine geoengineering techniques. The amendment has not yet entered into force due to insufficient ratifications.

### UNFCCC and Paris Agreement

Neither the UNFCCC (1992) nor the Paris Agreement (2015) explicitly addresses geoengineering. However, several provisions are relevant:

- **Paris Agreement Article 4:** The goal of achieving "a balance between anthropogenic emissions by sources and removals by sinks of greenhouse gases" (net-zero emissions) in the second half of the century implicitly contemplates CDR.
- **Paris Agreement Article 2:** The 1.5°C temperature goal is increasingly understood to require both aggressive emissions reduction and CDR deployment, as reflected in IPCC scenarios.
- **IPCC AR6 (2021-2022):** The Sixth Assessment Report assessed CDR as "necessary" to achieve net-zero CO2 emissions and included SRM in its assessment of response options, while noting significant governance challenges.

### EU Framework

**EU CCS Directive 2009/31/EC:** This directive establishes a legal framework for the geological storage of carbon dioxide, including site selection, monitoring, liability, and financial security requirements. The Directive requires member states to ensure that potential CO2 storage sites are characterized and assessed.

**EU Innovation Fund:** Established under the EU Emissions Trading System Directive 2003/87/EC (as amended), the Innovation Fund supports innovative low-carbon technologies including CDR. The Fund has allocated significant resources to CCUS projects across Europe.

**European Climate Law (Regulation (EU) 2021/1119):** This regulation establishes the binding target of climate neutrality by 2050 and mandates that EU-wide greenhouse gas emissions are reduced by at least 55 percent by 2030. While the regulation does not explicitly address geoengineering, its net-zero target framework creates demand for CDR technologies.

### ECHR Climate Jurisprudence

**KlimaSeniorinnen v. Switzerland (Application No. 53600/20, Grand Chamber Judgment of April 9, 2024):** The Grand Chamber found a violation of Article 8 ECHR, holding that Switzerland had failed to comply with its positive obligations by not establishing adequate quantifiable targets and timelines for greenhouse gas reduction. While not directly addressing geoengineering, this landmark ruling establishes that states bear enforceable obligations to take effective climate action, which may influence future decisions about whether states are required or permitted to pursue geoengineering as part of their climate response.

## Comparative Analysis

### Governance Gaps

All four jurisdictions exhibit significant governance gaps for geoengineering. No jurisdiction has enacted a comprehensive geoengineering statute establishing permitting, oversight, liability, and international coordination mechanisms. Existing regulatory frameworks -- environmental impact assessment, air quality regulation, weather modification reporting -- were designed for conventional activities and may be inadequate for technologies with planetary-scale effects.

### CDR vs. SRM Governance Divergence

A clear divergence is emerging between governance approaches to CDR and SRM. CDR technologies are increasingly integrated into existing regulatory frameworks: South Korea's Carbon Neutrality Act, the U.S. 45Q tax credit, and the EU CCS Directive all provide legal pathways for CDR deployment. SRM technologies remain largely ungoverned, with only the CBD moratorium and the nascent OSTP research plan providing any framework. This divergence reflects both the relative political acceptability of CDR and the fundamentally different risk profiles of the two approaches.

### Precautionary vs. Innovation Approaches

Germany and the EU framework emphasize the precautionary principle, advocating for governance frameworks before deployment. The United States emphasizes innovation and research funding while resisting comprehensive regulation. South Korea occupies a middle position, investing in CCUS research within a legally binding net-zero framework while not engaging significantly with SRM governance.

### Unilateral Action Risk

The absence of international governance creates a risk of unilateral geoengineering deployment. A single state or private actor could potentially deploy SRM technologies affecting the entire planet. The Make Sunsets incident in 2022 demonstrated that private unilateral action is already occurring. Existing international law provides limited tools to prevent such actions, as there is no treaty explicitly prohibiting SRM deployment.
