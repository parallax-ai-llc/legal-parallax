## Summary

Plastic regulation -- the legal framework governing the production, use, disposal, and pollution caused by plastic materials -- has emerged as one of the most dynamic areas of environmental law, driven by growing awareness that plastic pollution constitutes a planetary-scale crisis, with an estimated 460 million tonnes of plastics produced annually and only approximately 9 percent recycled globally. South Korea has positioned itself as a leader in plastic waste management through the Act on the Promotion of Saving and Recycling of Resources (자원의 절약과 재활용촉진에 관한 법률), the Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) system (생산자책임재활용제도) requiring manufacturers to meet recycling targets for plastic packaging, and a series of progressively stricter single-use plastic bans implemented since 2019 under the Act on Resource Circulation of Electrical and Electronic Equipment and Vehicles, with Korea targeting a 50 percent reduction in plastic waste by 2030 under the 2050 Carbon Neutrality Roadmap. The United States addresses plastic pollution primarily through the Resource Conservation and Recovery Act (RCRA), the Marine Debris Act (33 U.S.C. Sections 1951-1958), and increasingly through state-level legislation, with California, New York, and other states enacting single-use plastic bag bans and the Break Free From Plastic Pollution Act repeatedly proposed but not enacted at the federal level, while the Save Our Seas 2.0 Act (2020) and the Microbead-Free Waters Act of 2015 represent targeted federal interventions. Germany regulates plastics within the EU framework through the Verpackungsgesetz (VerpackG, Packaging Act) implementing the EU Single-Use Plastics Directive (2019/904) and the Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation (PPWR, adopted 2024), with one of the world's highest plastic recycling rates achieved through the Duales System and deposit-return systems. The EU has taken the most comprehensive approach through the SUP Directive banning specific single-use plastic items, the PPWR establishing binding recycled content targets and reuse quotas, the restriction of intentionally added microplastics under REACH Regulation, and active participation in negotiations for a legally binding Global Plastics Treaty under the UN.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's plastic regulation operates through the Act on the Promotion of Saving and Recycling of Resources (자원의 절약과 재활용촉진에 관한 법률, Act No. 19953), the Wastes Control Act (폐기물관리법), and the Act on Resource Circulation (자원순환기본법, 2018). The Ministry of Environment (환경부) coordinates policy with implementation support from the Korea Environment Corporation (한국환경공단).

### Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR)

Korea's EPR system (생산자책임재활용제도), operational since 2003, requires producers and importers of designated products and packaging to fulfill annual recycling obligations:

| Material Category | Recycling Rate Target (2024) |
|------------------|------------------------------|
| PET bottles | 80% |
| Other plastic packaging | 55-65% |
| Expanded polystyrene (EPS) | 80% |
| Plastic film packaging | 30-40% |

Producers that fail to meet obligations must pay recycling charges (재활용부과금) to the Korea Environment Corporation, which uses the funds to support recycling infrastructure.

### Single-Use Plastic Restrictions

Korea has implemented progressively stricter single-use plastic measures:

| Year | Measure |
|------|---------|
| 2019 | Ban on single-use plastic bags in large retail stores and supermarkets |
| 2020 | Mandatory separate collection of transparent PET bottles |
| 2022 | Ban on disposable cups in cafes (for dine-in); expanded bag ban |
| 2023 | Extended bans to takeout cups at chain stores above certain size |
| 2024 | Further restrictions on plastic straws and cutlery in food service |

### Transparent PET Separate Collection

Since December 2020, Korea requires separate collection of transparent PET bottles (투명 페트병 별도 분리배출) from colored or labeled PET bottles. This policy aims to improve PET recycling quality and enable bottle-to-bottle recycling, which requires high-purity transparent PET feedstock.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States lacks a comprehensive federal plastic pollution statute. Regulation is fragmented across federal environmental laws, targeted federal statutes, and a growing patchwork of state legislation.

### Federal Statutes

**Microbead-Free Waters Act of 2015 (Pub. L. 114-114):** Prohibits the manufacture and sale of rinse-off cosmetics containing intentionally added plastic microbeads. One of the few targeted federal plastic restrictions.

**Save Our Seas 2.0 Act of 2020 (Pub. L. 116-224):** Strengthened NOAA's Marine Debris Program, established a Genius Prize for innovations in reducing marine debris, and authorized post-disaster marine debris removal. Does not restrict plastic production or use.

**Marine Debris Act (33 U.S.C. 1951-1958):** Establishes the NOAA Marine Debris Program to research, prevent, and reduce marine debris including plastics.

### State-Level Action

State legislatures have been far more active than Congress:

| State | Key Measure | Year |
|-------|-------------|------|
| California | Single-use plastic bag ban (SB 270) | 2014 |
| California | SB 54: 65% plastic packaging reduction by 2032; EPR | 2022 |
| New York | Plastic bag ban | 2020 |
| New Jersey | Comprehensive ban (bags, polystyrene, straws) | 2022 |
| Maine | First state EPR for packaging law | 2021 |
| Oregon | EPR for packaging (Plastic Pollution and Recycling Modernization Act) | 2021 |

**California SB 54 (2022):** The most ambitious U.S. plastic legislation, requiring all single-use packaging to be recyclable or compostable by 2032, mandating 65 percent reduction in single-use plastic waste, requiring 25 percent recycled content in plastic packaging by 2032, and establishing a producer-funded $5 billion EPR program.

### Break Free From Plastic Pollution Act

Introduced in multiple Congressional sessions (2020, 2021, 2023), this comprehensive bill would establish a national beverage container deposit system, phase out certain single-use plastics, establish EPR for packaging, and impose a moratorium on new plastic production facilities. It has not been enacted.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany regulates plastics primarily through the Verpackungsgesetz (VerpackG, Packaging Act, 2019) and the Einwegkunststoffverbotsverordnung (EWKVerbotsV) implementing the EU Single-Use Plastics Directive.

### Duales System and VerpackG

Germany pioneered packaging EPR with the Verpackungsverordnung (1991), establishing the Duales System ("Green Dot" system). The VerpackG (2019) modernized the framework:

- Registration in the LUCID packaging register (Zentrale Stelle Verpackungsregister) is mandatory
- System participation (Systembeteiligungspflicht): all packaging entering the German market must be registered with a dual system
- Recycling quotas: 63% for plastics (increasing under PPWR)
- Financial incentives for recyclable packaging design (Ökologische Gestaltung)

### Deposit-Return System (Pfand)

Germany operates one of the most extensive deposit-return systems globally:

| Container Type | Deposit Amount |
|---------------|---------------|
| Single-use PET bottles | EUR 0.25 |
| Single-use cans | EUR 0.25 |
| Reusable glass bottles (beer, water) | EUR 0.08-0.15 |
| Reusable PET bottles | EUR 0.25 |

Since January 1, 2024, the deposit obligation was extended to milk beverage containers and other previously exempted categories.

### SUP Directive Implementation

Germany banned the following single-use plastic products from July 3, 2021:
- Cutlery, plates, straws, stirrers
- Expanded polystyrene food and drink containers
- Cotton bud sticks with plastic
- Balloon sticks
- Oxo-degradable plastics

### Lightweight Plastic Bag Ban

Germany banned lightweight plastic carrier bags (thickness 15-50 micrometers) from January 1, 2022, under the Leichte Kunststofftragetaschen Verbotsverordnung. Very lightweight bags (under 15 micrometers, used for loose food) remain permitted.

## ECHR & EU

### Single-Use Plastics Directive (2019/904)

The SUP Directive addresses the 10 single-use plastic items most commonly found on European beaches:

| Measure | Products |
|---------|----------|
| Market ban | Cutlery, plates, straws, stirrers, balloon sticks, EPS food/drink containers, oxo-degradable plastics |
| Consumption reduction | Cups, food containers |
| Product design | Caps tethered to bottles (from July 2024); 25% rPET in bottles by 2025; 30% by 2030 |
| Marking requirements | Sanitary products, wet wipes, tobacco products, cups |
| EPR | Food containers, packets/wrappers, cups, lightweight bags, wet wipes, balloons, tobacco filters |
| Collection targets | 77% of bottles by 2025; 90% by 2029 |

### Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation (PPWR)

The PPWR, adopted on November 18, 2024, replaces the Packaging and Packaging Waste Directive with a directly applicable regulation:

- **Recycled content targets:** 10% contact-sensitive plastic packaging by 2030; 50% PET bottles by 2030; 65% by 2040
- **Reuse targets:** 10% of beverages in reusable packaging by 2030; 40% by 2040
- **Deposit-return systems:** All member states must establish DRS for plastic bottles and metal cans by 2029
- **Recyclability:** All packaging must be recyclable by 2030; recyclable at scale by 2035
- **Bans:** Single-use plastic packaging for fresh fruits/vegetables, miniature hotel toiletries, single-use packaging for dine-in restaurants

### REACH Microplastics Restriction

Commission Regulation (EU) 2023/2055 restricts the placing on the market of intentionally added microplastics under REACH. The restriction covers microbeads in cosmetics, microplastics in detergents, agricultural products (fertilizer coatings), and sports surfaces (infill granules), with phased implementation from 2023 to 2035.

### Global Plastics Treaty

The UN Environment Assembly established an Intergovernmental Negotiating Committee (INC) in March 2022 to develop a legally binding instrument on plastic pollution. Negotiations continued through INC-5 (2024), with the EU advocating for binding global measures covering the full lifecycle of plastics. Key issues include production caps, problematic plastics phase-outs, and financing for developing countries.

## Comparative Analysis

### Comprehensiveness of Approach

The EU has the most comprehensive regulatory framework, addressing production (REACH microplastics), use (SUP Directive bans), packaging (PPWR), and disposal (waste directives). Germany implements the EU framework with additional national measures. South Korea has a strong EPR system and progressive single-use bans. The United States is the most fragmented, with meaningful action occurring primarily at the state level.

### EPR Maturity

Germany and South Korea have the most mature EPR systems, both operational for over two decades. The EU's PPWR will harmonize EPR across all member states. The United States is in the early stages of EPR adoption, with only a handful of states having enacted packaging EPR laws.

### Recycling Infrastructure

Germany achieves among the highest recycling rates globally through the combination of EPR funding, deposit-return systems, and separate collection infrastructure. South Korea's transparent PET separation requirement has improved recycling quality. The United States has significantly lower recycling rates (approximately 5-6 percent for plastics), hampered by fragmented collection systems and limited domestic processing capacity.

### Production-Side Regulation

None of the jurisdictions has enacted binding limits on virgin plastic production, though the Global Plastics Treaty negotiations may ultimately address this. Current regulation focuses overwhelmingly on downstream management (use, collection, recycling) rather than upstream reduction (production limits, feedstock taxes).
