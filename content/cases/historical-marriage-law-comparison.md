## Summary

Marriage -- the legally recognized union between persons establishing rights and obligations regarding property, children, inheritance, and social status -- has been constituted and regulated in fundamentally different ways across civilizations. Every major legal tradition has grappled with whether marriage is primarily a private contract between parties, a family arrangement between kinship groups, a religious sacrament or duty, or a status conferred by the state, and the answer has determined the rules for formation, dissolution, property rights, and the status of children. Ancient Near Eastern marriage, documented extensively in the Code of Hammurabi and thousands of cuneiform marriage contracts, was fundamentally a contractual arrangement between families involving payment of bride-price (terhatu) by the groom's family and provision of a dowry (sheriqtu) by the bride's, with the wife retaining property rights that were remarkable by ancient standards. Roman marriage evolved through several forms: the archaic cum manu marriage (transferring the wife into the husband's manus or legal power, through the ceremonies of confarreatio, coemptio, or usus) and the increasingly prevalent sine manu marriage (in which the wife remained under her father's patria potestas or, if sui iuris, retained full legal capacity and separate property). East Asian marriage, governed by Confucian ritual prescriptions (the "six rites" or liuli) and codified in the Tang Code, Joseon Gyeongguk Daejeon, and other codes, was conceived primarily as an arrangement between families for the continuation of the patriline, with the wife entering her husband's family and the concept of divorce heavily weighted in the husband's favor through the "seven grounds for expulsion" (qichu/chilchulgoji). Islamic marriage (nikah) is explicitly contractual in Islamic jurisprudence -- a civil contract (aqd) between the parties (or their guardians) requiring offer, acceptance, witnesses, and the payment of mahr (dower) by the husband to the wife as her exclusive property, with distinctive rules regarding polygyny (up to four wives, per Quran 4:3), divorce (including the husband's talaq and the wife's khul'), and the mandatory waiting period (idda). Medieval European marriage was transformed by the Church's assertion of exclusive jurisdiction, culminating in the doctrine (formalized at the Fourth Lateran Council of 1215 and the Council of Trent in 1563) that marriage was a sacrament created by the free consent of the parties, indissoluble except by death, and subject to elaborate impediments based on consanguinity and affinity. English common law absorbed the canonical doctrine of marriage while developing the distinctive secular consequences of coverture, under which the wife's legal personality was suspended during marriage -- she could not own property, make contracts, or sue in her own name -- creating a legal regime radically different from both Roman sine manu marriage and Islamic nikah, and not fundamentally reformed until the Married Women's Property Acts of the 19th century.

## Ancient Near East

Marriage in the Ancient Near East was a multi-stage contractual process between families, documented in thousands of cuneiform marriage contracts spanning more than two millennia.

### Mesopotamian Marriage

The Code of Hammurabi (Sections 128-184) provides the most systematic ancient code of marriage law. Section 128 establishes the foundational principle: "If a man takes a wife and does not draw up a contract for her, that woman is not a wife." Marriage was constituted by contract, not ceremony.

**Formation**: Marriage was arranged between the families, typically initiated by the groom or his father. The process involved: (1) the agreement between families; (2) payment of terhatu (bride-price) by the groom's family to the bride's father; (3) provision of sheriqtu (dowry) by the bride's family, which accompanied the bride but remained her property; and (4) execution of a written marriage contract (riksatu). The biblu (betrothal gift) from the groom to the bride sometimes preceded the formal marriage.

**Property rights**: Babylonian wives retained significant property rights. The dowry remained the wife's property and descended to her children after her death (Sections 162, 163). The husband managed it during marriage but could not alienate it. If divorced without fault, the wife took her dowry with her (Section 142). If the wife died childless, her dowry returned to her father's house (Section 163). A wife could engage in business, as attested by the naditu priestesses of Sippar who conducted extensive commercial activities documented in the Old Babylonian period archives.

**Divorce**: The husband could divorce his wife relatively freely but was required to return the dowry and pay a divorce settlement (Section 138-139). A wife could seek divorce only on specific grounds; Section 142 provides that if a wife hated her husband and declared "You shall not have me," the case was investigated by her city ward, and if she was blameless and the husband had been going out and disparaging her, she could take her dowry and return to her father's house. If she was found at fault, she was thrown into the water.

**Polygamy and concubinage**: A man could take a secondary wife (sugitu) or a slave concubine, particularly if the primary wife failed to produce children (Section 144-146). However, the secondary wife could not claim equality with the primary wife (Section 146). The naditu priestess who could not bear children might provide her husband with a slave woman as surrogate (Section 144), paralleling the biblical story of Sarah and Hagar.

Raymond Westbrook's *Old Babylonian Marriage Law* (1988) provides the authoritative treatment.

### Egyptian Marriage

Egyptian marriage was less formalized than Mesopotamian practice. No specific ceremony was legally required; marriage was established by cohabitation, though written agreements became increasingly common in the Late Period. Demotic marriage contracts from the Ptolemaic period document elaborate property arrangements, including the husband's obligation to provide maintenance and the wife's right to divorce with retention of specified property. Women in ancient Egypt enjoyed relatively strong legal capacity, including the right to own property, initiate legal proceedings, and dispose of their own assets.

## Roman Law

Roman marriage law evolved dramatically over the centuries, moving from patriarchal manus marriage to the remarkably egalitarian sine manu form.

### Forms of Marriage

**Cum manu marriage**: In the archaic period, marriage could transfer the wife into the husband's manus (legal power), effectively making her part of his family and subject to his patria potestas. Three methods created manus:

- **Confarreatio**: A solemn religious ceremony involving the offering of a spelt cake (farreum) before the Pontifex Maximus and Flamen Dialis, with ten witnesses. Reserved for patricians and required for certain priesthoods.
- **Coemptio**: A fictitious "purchase" of the wife through a ceremony involving scales and bronze, analogous to mancipatio.
- **Usus**: The wife came under manus automatically after one year of continuous cohabitation, unless she absented herself for three consecutive nights (usurpatio trinoctii, per the Twelve Tables).

Under cum manu marriage, the wife lost her connection to her natal family and became filiae loco (in the position of a daughter) in her husband's family. All her property became his.

**Sine manu marriage**: By the late Republic, free marriage without manus had become the norm. The wife retained her membership in her natal family, her legal capacity (if sui iuris), and her separate property. Marriage was constituted by consent (consensus) and marital affection (affectio maritalis) -- no ceremony, registration, or formality was legally required, though social custom involved elaborate celebrations. As the jurist Ulpian stated: "It is not cohabitation that makes a marriage, but consent" (Digest 35.1.15: nuptias non concubitus, sed consensus facit).

**Property**: Under sine manu marriage, the spouses' property remained separate. The wife's dos (dowry), provided by her family, was managed by the husband during marriage but had to be returned upon divorce (actio rei uxoriae, later reformed by Justinian into the actio ex stipulatu). The husband could make gifts to the wife donatio propter nuptias (gifts on account of marriage), paralleling the dowry.

### Divorce

Roman divorce was remarkably free. Either spouse could unilaterally dissolve the marriage by repudium (notice of divorce), without cause and without judicial proceedings. Augustus's Lex Iulia de Adulteriis (18 BCE) imposed some procedural requirements (notice before seven witnesses), and Christian emperors from Constantine onward attempted to restrict divorce, but it remained relatively accessible throughout the classical period. The contrast with medieval Christian indissolubility is stark.

### Augustus's Marriage Legislation

The Lex Iulia de Maritandis Ordinibus (18 BCE) and the Lex Papia Poppaea (9 CE) imposed penalties on the unmarried and childless (including restrictions on inheritance capacity) and rewards for married persons with children (the ius trium liberorum, granting various privileges to those with three children). These laws, motivated by Augustus's concern over declining citizen birth rates, represented an extraordinary state intervention into private marriage decisions and were much resented, as Tacitus reports (Annales 3.25).

Susan Treggiari's *Roman Marriage: Iusti Coniuges from the Time of Cicero to the Time of Ulpian* (1991) is the standard modern treatment.

## East Asian Law

East Asian marriage was governed by Confucian ritual prescriptions and codified law, conceived primarily as an arrangement between families for the continuation of the patriline.

### Chinese Marriage

**The Six Rites (Liuli, 六礼)**: The Book of Rites (Liji, 禮記) prescribed six stages for a proper marriage: nacai (proposal), wenming (exchanging names and birth dates for astrological compatibility), naji (delivery of betrothal gifts), nazheng (delivery of bride-price), qingqi (setting the wedding date), and qinying (wedding ceremony with the groom personally escorting the bride). These rites constituted the ideal, though actual practice varied by class and period.

**Tang Code regulation**: The Tang Code (Tanglü shuyi, 653 CE) codified marriage law with criminal sanctions. Article 175 prohibited marriage between persons of the same surname (tongxing bu hun, 同姓不婚), a rule rooted in exogamy principles dating to the Zhou Dynasty. Betrothal was binding once the bride-price was paid; unilateral withdrawal was criminally punishable. Article 176 voided marriages contracted under fraud regarding family status.

**Divorce -- the Seven Grounds (qichu, 七出)**: A husband could repudiate his wife for seven reasons codified in the Tang Code and transmitted from the Book of Rites: failure to serve parents-in-law, failure to bear a son, adultery, jealousy, incurable disease, excessive talking, and theft. However, the "three prohibitions" (sanbuqu, 三不去) protected the wife from repudiation if she had mourned her parents-in-law for three years, if the husband's family had become wealthy during the marriage, or if she had no natal family to return to.

**Mutual divorce (heli, 和离)**: The Tang Code (Article 189) recognized consensual divorce, evidenced by a written agreement. Dunhuang divorce documents (discovered in the Mogao Caves) from the Tang period provide remarkable evidence of mutual divorce agreements expressing wishes for each party's future happiness.

### Korean Marriage

**Goryeo Dynasty (918-1392)**: Goryeo marriage practice was relatively egalitarian, with uxorilocal marriage (the husband residing with the wife's family) common among the elite. Women retained significant property rights and social standing within marriage.

**Joseon Dynasty (1392-1897)**: The Joseon court's promotion of neo-Confucian norms gradually transformed Korean marriage. The Gyeongguk Daejeon (1485) codified marriage rules including prohibitions on same-surname, same-origin marriage (dongseong dongbon, 同姓同本), reflecting the Chinese tongxing bu hun principle adapted to Korean naming conventions. The "seven grounds for expulsion" (chilchulgoji, 七出去之) mirrored the Chinese qichu.

Crucially, early Joseon marriage remained relatively egalitarian: sons-in-law often resided with the bride's family, and women retained social standing. The transformation to strict patrilocal marriage, as documented by Martina Deuchler, occurred gradually over the 15th-17th centuries under sustained ideological pressure from neo-Confucian literati. By the 18th century, virilocal marriage (the bride moving to the husband's household) had become the norm, and women's status within marriage had correspondingly diminished.

## Islamic Law

Islamic marriage (nikah) is explicitly and unambiguously classified as a civil contract (aqd) in Islamic jurisprudence, though one with profound religious and social significance.

### Formation

**Essential elements**: A valid nikah requires: (1) offer (ijab) and acceptance (qabul) by or on behalf of the parties; (2) the consent of the bride (required in all schools, though the Hanafi school historically permitted a guardian to contract marriage for a virgin without her explicit consent -- a position reformed in modern legislation); (3) the presence of witnesses (two male witnesses in Hanafi and Hanbali law; the Maliki school accepts announcement/publicity as an alternative); (4) the specification of mahr (dower); and (5) the absence of impediments.

**Mahr (dower)**: A fundamental distinguishing feature of Islamic marriage is that the mahr is paid by the husband to the wife (not to her family), becomes her exclusive property, and is not returnable upon divorce (except in khul'). The mahr may be paid immediately (mu'ajjal) or deferred (mu'ajjal), with the deferred portion payable upon divorce or the husband's death. This institution, combined with the wife's retention of separate property throughout marriage, meant that Muslim wives had more robust financial rights than their European counterparts under coverture.

**Wali (guardian)**: The role of the bride's guardian (typically her father) in marriage formation varies among schools. The Shafi'i and Maliki schools require the wali's participation for a valid marriage, while the Hanafi school permits an adult woman to contract her own marriage.

### Polygyny

The Quran (Surah 4:3) permits a Muslim man to marry up to four wives simultaneously, subject to the condition of equal treatment: "If you fear that you cannot be equitable, then marry only one." The schools differ on the precise requirements of equal treatment, but all agree it encompasses equal provision of housing, maintenance, and time. Modern legislation in many Muslim-majority countries has restricted polygyny through judicial authorization requirements (e.g., the Tunisian Personal Status Code of 1956 effectively abolished it).

### Divorce

Islamic law recognizes multiple forms of marriage dissolution:

**Talaq (repudiation)**: The husband's unilateral right to divorce, exercised by pronouncing "I divorce you." The "triple talaq" (three pronouncements) makes the divorce irrevocable, though jurists debated whether three pronouncements uttered simultaneously constituted one revocable or three irrevocable divorces. The Quran (2:228-232) prescribes a waiting period (idda, typically three menstrual cycles) during which the husband may revoke the divorce.

**Khul'**: The wife's right to obtain divorce by returning the mahr or other compensation to the husband. Based on the hadith of Habiba bint Sahl, who sought dissolution from the Prophet by returning a garden given as mahr.

**Faskh**: Judicial dissolution, available on grounds including the husband's impotence, cruelty, absence, failure to maintain, or insanity, with variations among schools.

Judith Tucker's *Women, Family and Gender in Islamic Law* (2008) provides an accessible modern treatment.

## Medieval European Law

The medieval Church's assertion of exclusive jurisdiction over marriage created the distinctive Western institution of Christian sacramental marriage.

### The Church's Jurisdiction

From the 11th century onward, the Church successfully claimed exclusive jurisdiction over marriage formation, validity, and dissolution. Gratian's Decretum (c. 1140) compiled the patristic and canonical authorities, establishing the principle that marriage was created by the free consent of the parties, not by family arrangement, sexual consummation, or ceremony. Peter Lombard's Sentences (c. 1150) classified marriage as one of the seven sacraments.

The distinction between the "Paris school" (represented by Peter Lombard, holding that present-tense consent alone created an indissoluble marriage -- verba de praesenti) and the "Bologna school" (represented by Gratian, requiring both consent and consummation) was resolved largely in favor of the Paris school by Pope Alexander III (1159-1181), who held that verba de praesenti created an indissoluble marriage even without consummation.

### Clandestine Marriage

A persistent problem was clandestine marriage -- unions contracted by the parties' consent alone, without witnesses, ceremony, or parental knowledge. Because consent alone sufficed for validity, such marriages were valid but illicit (sanctioned by penances but not voidable). The Fourth Lateran Council (1215, Canon 51) required the publication of banns (public announcements in church on three successive Sundays) before marriage, but clandestine marriages remained valid until the Council of Trent's decree Tametsi (1563) required the presence of a parish priest and two witnesses for validity.

### Impediments and Annulment

Since sacramental marriage was indissoluble, the only escape was annulment -- a declaration that no valid marriage had ever existed, typically based on a pre-existing impediment. The elaborate system of impediments (consanguinity to the seventh degree -- later reduced to the fourth by Lateran IV -- affinity, spiritual kinship, impotence, pre-contract, force, and error) provided numerous potential grounds for annulment, which wealthy and powerful families exploited to escape undesirable unions. The annulment of Henry VIII's marriage to Catherine of Aragon, refused by Pope Clement VII, precipitated the English Reformation.

James A. Brundage's *Law, Sex, and Christian Society in Medieval Europe* (1987) is the comprehensive treatment.

## Common Law Origins

English common law adopted the canonical doctrine of marriage while imposing its own distinctive secular consequences through the doctrine of coverture.

### Coverture

William Blackstone articulated the common law doctrine in his Commentaries (1765-1769, Book 1, Chapter 15): "By marriage, the husband and wife are one person in law: that is, the very being or legal existence of the woman is suspended during the marriage, or at least is incorporated and consolidated into that of the husband." Under coverture:

- The wife could not own personal property (it vested in the husband) or manage real property (the husband had the right to its rents and profits during marriage, known as jure uxoris)
- The wife could not enter contracts or sue in her own name
- The husband was liable for the wife's pre-marital debts and torts
- The wife had no separate legal personality

Coverture was partially mitigated by equity: the Court of Chancery recognized the wife's "separate estate" (property settled to her separate use through a trust), allowing wealthy families to protect daughters' property. The common law itself provided the wife with dower (a life estate in one-third of the husband's freehold lands) and the right to maintenance.

### Lord Hardwicke's Act (1753)

The Marriage Act 1753 (26 Geo. II, c. 33), commonly known as Lord Hardwicke's Act, ended the canonical doctrine of clandestine marriage in England by requiring all marriages (except Jewish and Quaker ceremonies) to be performed by an Anglican clergyman, after banns or by license, in the parish church, and in the presence of witnesses, on pain of voidness. This was the first English statute requiring a specific ceremony for a valid marriage.

### Reform

The Married Women's Property Act 1870 and the more comprehensive Married Women's Property Act 1882 abolished coverture's property consequences, granting married women the right to own property and enter contracts in their own name. The Matrimonial Causes Act 1857 transferred jurisdiction over divorce from the ecclesiastical courts to a new civil Court for Divorce and Matrimonial Causes, establishing judicial divorce in England for the first time.

## Historical Significance and Modern Legacy

The comparative history of marriage law reveals the extraordinary diversity of legal frameworks governing the most fundamental of human institutions. The contrast between Roman sine manu marriage (with separate property and free divorce), Islamic nikah (contractual, with mahr protecting the wife's economic interests), medieval Christian marriage (sacramental and indissoluble), and English coverture (absorbing the wife's legal personality into the husband's) demonstrates that no single model of marriage is "natural" or inevitable. Modern marriage law has converged substantially toward a model of equal partnership with separate or community property, no-fault divorce, and gender-neutral rights -- synthesizing elements from multiple historical traditions while departing from each in significant ways.
