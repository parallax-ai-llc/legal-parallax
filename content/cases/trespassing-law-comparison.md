## Summary

Trespassing — the unauthorized entry onto or remaining within another person's property — sits at the intersection of criminal law, property rights, and individual liberty. Its legal treatment reveals sharp differences in how societies balance the sanctity of private space against personal freedoms and social welfare. South Korea criminalizes trespass under Articles 319-322 of the Criminal Act, imposing up to 3 years imprisonment for basic residential intrusion and up to 5 years for aggravated group trespass, with prosecution possible without a victim's complaint. The United States addresses trespass through a patchwork of state statutes and targeted federal laws such as 18 U.S.C. Section 1752 (restricted buildings) and 18 U.S.C. Section 1382 (military installations), with penalties ranging from minor misdemeanors to 10-year felonies depending on circumstances. Germany's Strafgesetzbuch Section 123 treats Hausfriedensbruch (breach of domestic peace) as a complaint-dependent offense punishable by up to 1 year imprisonment, while Section 124 elevates group trespass to up to 2 years. At the European level, no harmonized trespass statute exists; instead, the European Convention on Human Rights balances property owners' rights under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 against occupants' right to respect for their home under Article 8, producing a rich body of case law on proportionality in eviction and access disputes.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Trespass offenses in South Korea are governed by Chapter 36 of the Criminal Act (Hyeongbeop, 형법), Articles 319 through 322, under the heading "Crimes of Intrusion upon Habitation." The statute establishes a structured hierarchy:

| Article | Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Art. 319(1) | Residential trespass (주거침입) | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine up to 5 million won |
| Art. 319(2) | Refusal to leave upon request (퇴거불응) | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine up to 5 million won |
| Art. 320 | Aggravated trespass by group or with dangerous objects (특수주거침입) | Up to 5 years imprisonment |
| Art. 321 | Trespass upon premises guarded by another (주거수색) | Up to 3 years imprisonment |
| Art. 322 | Attempted trespass | Punishable |

### Key Principles

**Residential trespass (Article 319(1)):** The foundational offense criminalizes intrusion into a person's dwelling (주거), a building under management, a ship, aircraft, or an occupied room. The penalty of up to 3 years imprisonment or a fine not exceeding 5 million won reflects the seriousness Korean law attaches to the sanctity of domestic space. The Korean Constitution itself, in Article 16, guarantees the inviolability of the domicile, providing that search of a residence requires a warrant issued by a judge.

**Refusal to leave (Article 319(2)):** A person who fails to comply with a request to leave a dwelling or managed premises faces the same penalty as the basic trespass offense. This provision is significant because it criminalizes not merely unauthorized entry but also unauthorized continued presence, even if the initial entry was lawful — for example, a guest who refuses to depart when asked.

**Aggravated trespass (Article 320):** When trespass is committed by a group displaying force or by individuals carrying dangerous objects, the penalty escalates to up to 5 years imprisonment. This provision targets organized intrusions, forced entries by creditors, and intimidation-based property invasions.

**Scope of "dwelling":** Korean courts interpret "dwelling" broadly. The Supreme Court has held that common areas of apartment buildings, stairwells, and hallways fall within the protected scope when they serve the residential function of the occupants.

### Notable Cases

**Supreme Court Full Bench Decision 2021Do15507 (2022):** The Supreme Court clarified the standard for establishing trespass, ruling that the objective and external conduct of the accused at the time of entry must be evaluated to determine whether the peace of the home was actually disturbed. This shifted the analysis from a purely subjective test (whether entry was against the occupant's will) toward an objective assessment of disruption to domestic peace.

**Supreme Court Decision 2021Do3941 (2022):** The court ruled that a landlord who forces entry into a rental unit to evict a tenant — even after the lease has expired or been terminated — commits trespass under Article 319. The ruling established that landlords must pursue judicial remedies (such as a suit for delivery of possession) rather than resort to self-help evictions. Even a contractual forced-eviction clause does not authorize extrajudicial entry.

**"Partial intrusion" line of cases:** Korean courts have held that trespass does not require full-body entry. Where only part of a person's body intrudes through a window or doorway, the offense is complete if the domestic peace of the residence was disturbed.

### Civil and Administrative Dimensions

Beyond criminal liability, trespassers face civil damages claims under Article 750 of the Civil Act (tort liability for unlawful acts). Property owners may also seek injunctive relief and, in landlord-tenant disputes, the Special Act on the Protection of Housing Lease (주택임대차보호법) provides additional procedural protections for tenants against unauthorized entry by landlords.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Trespass law in the United States operates at two distinct levels: state criminal codes (which handle the vast majority of trespass prosecutions) and federal statutes (which apply to specific categories of federal property). The fundamental distinction in most states is between criminal trespass (a crime) and civil trespass (a tort).

**Federal trespass statutes:**

| Statute | Scope | Penalty |
|---------|-------|---------|
| 18 U.S.C. Section 1752 | Restricted federal buildings and grounds | Misdemeanor: up to 1 year; Felony with weapon/injury: up to 10 years |
| 18 U.S.C. Section 1382 | Military installations and property | Up to 6 months imprisonment and/or fine |
| 36 CFR Section 2.31 | National Park System trespass | Up to 6 months and/or $5,000 fine |
| 43 CFR Part 9230 | Trespass on public lands (Bureau of Land Management) | Civil penalties and removal |

**State-level penalties (selected examples):**

| State | Classification | Maximum Penalty |
|-------|---------------|-----------------|
| New York (Penal Law Section 140.05) | Criminal trespass 3rd degree (violation) | 15 days |
| New York (Penal Law Section 140.15) | Criminal trespass 2nd degree (Class A misdemeanor) | 1 year |
| New York (Penal Law Section 140.17) | Criminal trespass 1st degree (Class D felony) | Up to 7 years |
| California (Penal Code Section 602) | Misdemeanor trespass | Up to 6 months and/or $1,000 fine |
| Texas (Penal Code Section 30.05) | Class B misdemeanor to Class A misdemeanor | 180 days to 1 year |
| Arizona (ARS Section 13-1502/1503/1504) | 3rd degree to 1st degree | 30 days to 1.5 years |
| Florida (Section 810.08/810.09) | Misdemeanor to felony | 60 days to 5 years |
| Georgia (Section 16-7-21) | Misdemeanor | Up to 12 months |

### Key Principles

**Elements of criminal trespass:** Most state statutes require proof of two elements: (1) unauthorized entry onto or remaining on another's property, and (2) knowledge that the entry or continued presence is unauthorized. The knowledge requirement may be satisfied by posted signage ("No Trespassing"), verbal notice, fencing, or prior written notice from the property owner.

**Degrees of severity:** States that grade trespass into degrees typically escalate based on: the type of property (residential vs. commercial vs. open land), the presence of weapons, the time of entry (nighttime aggravation in some states), whether a protective order was violated, and whether the trespass involved a critical infrastructure facility.

**Federal restricted buildings (18 U.S.C. Section 1752):** This statute gained significant public attention following the January 6, 2021 Capitol breach, where numerous defendants were charged under its provisions. The statute criminalizes knowingly entering or remaining in any restricted building or grounds without lawful authority, engaging in disorderly or disruptive conduct, blocking ingress or egress, and committing violence on restricted grounds. Basic violations are misdemeanors (up to 1 year), but the use of a deadly weapon or causing significant bodily injury elevates the charge to a felony (up to 10 years).

**Castle Doctrine and Stand Your Ground:** Many U.S. states have adopted castle doctrine laws that authorize property owners to use force — including deadly force in some jurisdictions — against trespassers who unlawfully enter occupied dwellings. These laws effectively create a presumption that a person who unlawfully enters an occupied residence intends to commit a violent act, thereby justifying defensive force.

### Landmark Cases

**Florida v. Jardines, 569 U.S. 1 (2013):** The Supreme Court held 5-4 that police use of a trained drug-detection dog on the front porch of a private home constituted a "search" under the Fourth Amendment. Justice Scalia, writing for the majority, applied a property-based trespass test: the officers had physically intruded upon constitutionally protected property (the curtilage of the home) to gather evidence, and this physical intrusion, combined with the attempt to find incriminating evidence, constituted an unlawful search. This case reinforced that the Fourth Amendment's protections have deep roots in property law and the common law of trespass.

**Caniglia v. Strom, 593 U.S. ___ (2021):** The Court unanimously held that the "community caretaking" exception to the Fourth Amendment's warrant requirement does not extend to the home. Police officers had entered Edward Caniglia's home without a warrant during a welfare check and seized his firearms. The Court emphasized that the core of the Fourth Amendment is the right to retreat into one's home and be free from unreasonable governmental intrusion, rejecting an open-ended license for warrantless home entries under the guise of community caretaking.

**State v. Shack, 58 N.J. 297 (1971):** The New Jersey Supreme Court held that a trespass conviction could not stand against a legal aid attorney and a health services worker who entered a farmer's property to assist migrant farm workers. The court ruled that property rights are not absolute and must yield to the superior interests of providing essential services to vulnerable persons residing on the property. This case established an important limit on trespass law where it conflicts with fundamental human needs.

### Civil Trespass

In parallel with criminal liability, trespass to land is a well-established tort. Property owners may sue for compensatory damages, punitive damages in cases of willful trespass, and injunctive relief. The Restatement (Second) of Torts Sections 158-211 provides the common law framework, defining trespass as intentional entry onto land in possession of another without consent or legal privilege.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Trespass in Germany is codified as Hausfriedensbruch ("breach of domestic peace") under Sections 123 and 124 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB). The offense is situated within Chapter 7 of the StGB, titled "Offenses against Public Order," reflecting the German legal tradition of treating the sanctity of private spaces as a matter of public peace rather than purely private property.

| Section | Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Section 123(1) | Basic trespass (Hausfriedensbruch) | Up to 1 year imprisonment or fine |
| Section 124 | Aggravated group trespass (Schwerer Hausfriedensbruch) | Up to 2 years imprisonment |

### Key Principles

**Protected spaces (Section 123):** The provision protects four categories of space: (1) the dwelling (Wohnung) of another person, (2) business premises (Geschaeftsraeume), (3) enclosed property (befriedetes Besitztum — literally, "pacified possession"), and (4) spaces designated for public service or public transport. The concept of "befriedetes Besitztum" is distinctive to German law and encompasses any enclosed or fenced area that physically manifests the possessor's intent to exclude others.

**Two forms of commission:** Section 123 criminalizes two distinct acts: (a) unlawful intrusion (Eindringen) — entering against the will of the authorized person, and (b) refusal to leave (Verweilen) — remaining in a space despite being requested to leave by the authorized person. The second form mirrors the Korean provision in Article 319(2) and addresses situations where the initial entry was lawful but continued presence becomes unauthorized.

**Complaint requirement (Antragsdelikt):** A critical feature of German trespass law is that prosecution under Section 123 requires a criminal complaint (Strafantrag) from the injured party. Unlike South Korean or American law, German authorities cannot initiate prosecution ex officio for basic trespass. However, under Section 123(2), the public prosecution service may take up the case on its own initiative if it determines there is a special public interest in prosecution (besonderes oeffentliches Interesse).

**Aggravated group trespass (Section 124):** When a crowd (Menschenmenge) collectively and forcibly intrudes into a dwelling, business premises, or enclosed space, each participant faces up to 2 years imprisonment. This provision targets mob action, violent demonstrations that breach private premises, and organized forced entries. Unlike Section 123, Section 124 is prosecuted ex officio — no complaint is required.

**Constitutional dimension (Article 13 Grundgesetz):** The inviolability of the home is a fundamental right under Article 13 of the German Basic Law. Searches require a judicial warrant, and electronic surveillance of private dwellings is subject to exceptionally strict constitutional requirements. The Bundesverfassungsgericht (Federal Constitutional Court) has repeatedly upheld the centrality of Wohnungsfreiheit (freedom of the dwelling) as a core expression of human dignity and personal autonomy.

### Notable Cases

**BVerfG, 1 BvR 0820/24 (April 2024):** The Federal Constitutional Court overturned the conviction of climate activist Samuel Bosch, who had been found guilty of trespass (Section 123 StGB) and defamation after occupying a government building during a protest action. The Court held that the lower courts had failed to adequately consider the activist's freedom of expression (Article 5 GG), emphasizing that criticism of government power — even when polemical or confrontational — enjoys heightened constitutional protection. This decision clarified the boundary between trespass and constitutionally protected protest activity.

**Brokdorf Decision, BVerfGE 69, 315 (1985):** While not a trespass case in the narrow sense, this landmark decision on the right of assembly (Article 8 GG) established that demonstrations enjoy strong constitutional protection even when they involve entry onto or near contested sites. The Court held that authorities must adopt a "cooperation model" rather than reflexively applying criminal sanctions, influencing how trespass charges are evaluated in protest contexts.

**Hausrecht of commercial operators:** German courts have consistently upheld the right of commercial operators (supermarkets, shopping centers, sports stadiums) to issue Hausverbote (bans from premises). A person who enters premises after receiving a valid Hausverbot commits Hausfriedensbruch under Section 123. However, anti-discrimination law (Allgemeines Gleichbehandlungsgesetz, AGG) limits this power — a Hausverbot based on race, ethnicity, or other protected characteristics may be unlawful.

### Civil Remedies

Property owners may pursue civil remedies under the Buergerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB): Section 823(1) provides damages for violations of property rights, and Section 1004 provides a claim for removal and injunction (Beseitigungs- und Unterlassungsanspruch) against ongoing or threatened trespass. Self-help (Selbsthilfe) under Section 229 BGB is permitted in limited circumstances where official assistance cannot be obtained in time.

## ECHR & EU Framework

### Legal Framework

The European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR) does not contain a specific trespass offense. Instead, it establishes a framework of competing rights that governs how signatory states must handle trespass-related disputes. Trespass intersects with two primary Convention provisions:

| Provision | Right Protected | Relevance to Trespass |
|-----------|----------------|----------------------|
| Article 8 | Respect for private and family life, home, and correspondence | Protects occupants (including unauthorized occupants) against disproportionate eviction |
| Protocol 1, Article 1 | Peaceful enjoyment of possessions | Protects property owners' right to control access to their property |
| Article 3 | Prohibition of inhuman or degrading treatment | May be engaged if eviction of trespassers creates conditions of destitution |
| Article 14 | Prohibition of discrimination | Prohibits discriminatory enforcement of trespass laws |

### Key Principles

**Balancing property rights and home protection:** The ECHR framework requires states to balance the property owner's right to exclude others (Protocol 1, Article 1) against the occupant's right to respect for their "home" (Article 8). Critically, the ECtHR has held that a dwelling may constitute a "home" under Article 8 even if it was established unlawfully — meaning that squatters and unauthorized occupants may acquire Convention protection through long-term occupation.

**Proportionality requirement:** Any interference with the right to respect for one's home must satisfy three conditions: (1) it must be "in accordance with the law," (2) it must pursue a legitimate aim, and (3) it must be "necessary in a democratic society" — meaning proportionate to the aim pursued. This proportionality test imposes procedural obligations on states: before evicting trespassers, authorities must conduct an individualized assessment of the occupant's circumstances.

**Margin of appreciation:** The Court grants states a margin of appreciation in regulating property rights and housing, recognizing that different societies may legitimately strike different balances. However, this margin is narrower when vulnerable groups (Roma communities, persons with disabilities, families with children) are affected.

**No harmonized EU trespass law:** Trespass remains entirely within the competence of individual EU member states. The European Union has not adopted any directive or regulation harmonizing trespass or property protection law. Property law matters are generally excluded from the EU's harmonization agenda, and each member state retains full sovereignty over its criminal and civil trespass regimes. EU mechanisms such as the European Arrest Warrant (Framework Decision 2002/584/JHA) may apply when trespass-related offenses are committed across borders, but only if the underlying offense meets the dual-criminality requirement or falls within one of the 32 listed offense categories.

### Landmark Cases

**Yordanova and Others v. Bulgaria (Application No. 25446/06, 2012):** In this landmark judgment, the ECtHR unanimously ruled that Bulgaria's attempt to evict Roma families from homes unlawfully built on municipal land violated Article 8 of the Convention. Although the eviction order was lawful under domestic law and pursued legitimate aims (protecting public property and urban planning), the Court found it was not "necessary in a democratic society" because the decision-making process lacked adequate safeguards against disproportionate interference. The Court held that authorities must consider the occupants' vulnerability, the length of occupation, the availability of alternative housing, and the personal circumstances of those affected before ordering eviction. This case established that even unlawful occupants cannot be evicted without an individualized proportionality assessment.

**McCann v. United Kingdom (Application No. 19009/04, 2008):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where a local authority obtained a possession order against a tenant without any possibility of independent review of the proportionality of the eviction. The ruling established that any person at risk of losing their home must have the ability to challenge the proportionality of the measure before an independent tribunal, regardless of whether their occupation has a basis in domestic law.

**Connors v. United Kingdom (Application No. 66746/01, 2004):** The Court held that the summary eviction of a Gypsy family from a local authority caravan site violated Article 8. The absence of procedural safeguards — particularly the lack of any requirement for the local authority to justify the eviction — rendered the interference disproportionate. The decision was instrumental in prompting legislative reform in the UK regarding eviction procedures for Traveller sites.

**Winterstein and Others v. France (Application No. 27013/07, 2013):** The Court found that France violated Article 8 by ordering the eviction of Traveller families from land they had occupied for decades without adequately considering their personal circumstances, their ethnic identity as Travellers, the length of their occupation, or the availability of suitable alternative accommodation.

**Gladysheva v. Russia (Application No. 7097/10, 2011):** The Court held that evicting an individual from their home due to a defect in the chain of title caused by state fraud violated both Article 8 and Protocol 1, Article 1. The ruling affirmed that the state cannot shift the burden of its own administrative failures onto bona fide occupants.

### EU Instruments Relevant to Trespass

While no direct EU trespass law exists, several instruments indirectly affect cross-border trespass scenarios:

- **European Arrest Warrant (Framework Decision 2002/584/JHA):** Enables surrender of persons for offenses including criminal damage and participation in a criminal organization, which may overlap with aggravated trespass.
- **Directive 2012/29/EU (Victims' Rights Directive):** Establishes minimum standards for the rights of victims of crime across EU member states, potentially applicable to victims of trespass-related offenses.
- **EU Charter of Fundamental Rights, Article 17:** Guarantees the right to property, reinforcing Protocol 1, Article 1 of the ECHR within the EU legal order.
- **EU Charter, Article 7:** Mirrors ECHR Article 8 in protecting the right to respect for private and family life, home, and communications.

## Comparative Analysis

### Conceptual Foundations

The four jurisdictions reveal fundamentally different conceptual approaches to trespass:

**South Korea** treats trespass primarily as a violation of domestic peace and personal security. The Criminal Act's placement of trespass under "Crimes of Intrusion upon Habitation" emphasizes the residential dimension. Korean law is notably protective of the occupant's right to exclude, extending criminal liability even to landlords who enter their own property without following legal procedures.

**The United States** fragments trespass across multiple legal frameworks, reflecting its federalist structure. At its core, American trespass law is rooted in common-law property concepts — the right to exclude is considered a fundamental "stick in the bundle" of property rights. The Fourth Amendment adds a constitutional dimension, linking trespass to protections against unreasonable government intrusion. The castle doctrine further extends this protective logic by authorizing defensive force against trespassers.

**Germany** situates trespass within the concept of Hausfrieden (domestic peace), treating it as an offense against public order rather than purely against property. The complaint requirement reflects a view that trespass is primarily a private wrong that should be prosecuted at the victim's initiative, unless a public interest is at stake. The constitutional protection of Article 13 GG, however, elevates the home to a space of near-absolute personal sovereignty.

**The ECHR framework** uniquely approaches trespass from both sides — protecting both property owners and occupants. Its proportionality-based analysis rejects absolute rules in favor of case-by-case balancing, producing outcomes that may favor unauthorized occupants where eviction would cause disproportionate hardship.

### Penalty Severity

Comparing maximum penalties for basic trespass reveals a notable range:

1. **Germany:** Up to 1 year (least severe, complaint-dependent)
2. **United States:** Varies widely — from 15 days (New York, 3rd degree) to 7 years (New York, 1st degree felony)
3. **South Korea:** Up to 3 years imprisonment
4. **ECHR:** No direct penalty; remedial framework

For aggravated trespass, the range narrows:

1. **Germany:** Up to 2 years (Section 124, group trespass)
2. **South Korea:** Up to 5 years (Article 320, group trespass with weapons)
3. **United States:** Up to 10 years (18 U.S.C. Section 1752, restricted building with weapon)

### Prosecution Models

The jurisdictions diverge sharply on whether trespass requires a private complaint:

- **Germany:** Complaint required for basic trespass (Section 123); ex officio for aggravated (Section 124)
- **South Korea:** No complaint required; public prosecution
- **United States:** No complaint required; public prosecution (though many minor cases are practically victim-initiated)
- **ECHR:** Individual application after exhaustion of domestic remedies

### Trespass and Protest

The tension between trespass law and the right to protest is most explicitly addressed in **German** and **ECHR** jurisprudence. The BVerfG's 2024 decision overturning the climate activist's conviction and the Brokdorf precedent establish that trespass charges must be weighed against assembly and expression rights. The ECHR similarly requires proportionality analysis when trespass enforcement affects Convention rights.

In the **United States**, the First Amendment provides some protection for trespass-as-protest, particularly in publicly accessible spaces, but the Supreme Court has generally upheld trespass convictions for protesters on private property. The 2021 Capitol breach prosecutions under 18 U.S.C. Section 1752 demonstrated the serious consequences of trespass on restricted federal property.

**South Korea** offers less explicit protection for protest-related trespass, though the Constitutional Court's jurisprudence on freedom of assembly (Article 21 of the Constitution) provides a framework for challenging disproportionate trespass prosecutions in protest contexts.
