## Summary

Theft — the unlawful taking of another's property with intent to permanently deprive — is among the most ancient and universally prosecuted crimes, yet its legal treatment varies dramatically across jurisdictions. South Korea's Criminal Act organizes theft offenses into a graduated hierarchy from simple larceny (Article 329, up to 6 years) through nighttime intrusion theft and special larceny, with enhanced penalties for habitual offenders. The United States fragments theft law across 50 states with widely varying felony thresholds, distinguishing between petty theft (misdemeanor) and grand theft (felony) based on monetary value — a line that ranges from $200 to $2,500 depending on the state. Germany's Strafgesetzbuch provides a methodical three-tier structure: simple theft under Section 242 (up to 5 years), particularly serious theft under Section 243 (3 months to 10 years), and aggravated theft with weapons or gang involvement under Section 244 (6 months to 10 years). At the European level, theft remains governed by national law, but EU mechanisms such as the European Arrest Warrant and Eurojust facilitate cross-border cooperation for organized property crime.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Theft offenses in South Korea are governed by Chapter 38 of the Criminal Act (형법), Articles 329 through 346. The statute establishes a clear hierarchy of severity:

| Article | Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Art. 329 | Simple theft (절도) | Up to 6 years imprisonment or fine up to 10 million won |
| Art. 330 | Nighttime residential intrusion theft (야간주거침입절도) | Up to 10 years imprisonment |
| Art. 331 | Special theft (특수절도) | 1-10 years imprisonment |
| Art. 331-2 | Use of motor vehicles without consent (자동차등불법사용) | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine up to 5 million won |
| Art. 332 | Habitual offender enhancement (상습범) | Up to 1.5x the base penalty |

### Key Principles

**Simple theft (Article 329):** The foundational offense requires the taking of movable property belonging to another person with intent to unlawfully appropriate it. The maximum penalty of 6 years imprisonment or a fine not exceeding 10 million won applies. Courts frequently impose fines calibrated to the theft's severity for minor offenses, reserving imprisonment for repeat offenders or higher-value thefts.

**Nighttime residential intrusion (Article 330):** A person who breaks into a residence, managed building, ship, aircraft, or occupied room at night and steals property faces up to 10 years imprisonment — nearly double the basic theft penalty. The aggravation reflects the heightened danger and violation of personal security that nighttime home invasion represents.

**Special theft (Article 331):** This provision covers two scenarios warranting enhanced punishment of 1 to 10 years: (1) destroying a door, fence, or structural element at night to enter and steal, and (2) carrying a weapon or acting in concert with two or more persons. The joint-action provision is particularly significant in Korean law, as organized shoplifting rings and warehouse theft gangs trigger this elevated charge.

**Habitual offender enhancement (Article 332):** A person who habitually commits offenses under Articles 329 through 331-2 faces penalties aggravated by up to one-half of the prescribed maximum. This means a habitual simple thief faces up to 9 years imprisonment. Korean courts apply this provision aggressively, particularly for serial shoplifters and pickpockets with extensive criminal records.

**Family exemption (Article 344):** Historically, theft between close relatives was not punishable under Korean law. In 2024, the Constitutional Court ruled parts of this family theft exemption unconstitutional, finding that it failed to protect the property rights of victims of domestic theft, particularly elderly parents exploited by adult children.

### Notable Cases

**Supreme Court Decision 2017Do16249:** The court clarified the boundary between theft and fraud, holding that when a person obtains physical possession of an object through deception but the owner retains constructive possession, taking the object constitutes theft rather than fraud.

**Constitutional Court Decision 2020Hun-Ba3 (2024):** The court struck down the blanket family theft exemption in Article 344, ruling that it violated victims' right to property and equal protection by categorically barring prosecution regardless of circumstances.

## United States

### Legal Framework

U.S. theft law is primarily a matter of state jurisdiction, with each of the 50 states maintaining its own statutory framework. The fundamental distinction in most states is between petty theft (misdemeanor) and grand theft (felony), determined by the value of the stolen property.

**Felony threshold by state (selected examples):**

| State | Grand Theft Threshold | Maximum Penalty (Grand Theft) |
|-------|----------------------|------------------------------|
| California | $950 | 16 months, 2 years, or 3 years (realignment) |
| Texas | $2,500 | 2 years state jail felony |
| New York | $1,000 | Up to 4 years (grand larceny 4th degree) |
| Florida | $750 | Up to 5 years |
| Michigan | $1,000 | Up to 5 years |
| Arkansas ($1,000-$5,000) | $1,000 | Up to 6 years (Class D felony) |
| Arkansas ($5,000-$25,000) | $5,000 | 3-10 years (Class C felony) |
| Arkansas ($25,000+) | $25,000 | 5-20 years (Class B felony) |

**Petty theft** is generally classified as a misdemeanor carrying up to 6 months to 1 year in jail, depending on the state, plus fines typically ranging from $500 to $1,000.

### Federal Theft Law

Theft becomes a federal matter when it involves government property, interstate transportation of stolen goods, or theft from federally regulated institutions. Key federal statutes include:

- **18 U.S.C. Section 641:** Theft of public property — up to 10 years imprisonment if value exceeds $1,000; up to 1 year if under $1,000.
- **18 U.S.C. Section 2314 (National Stolen Property Act):** Interstate transportation of stolen property worth $5,000 or more — up to 10 years imprisonment.
- **18 U.S.C. Section 659:** Theft from interstate shipments — up to 10 years if value exceeds $1,000.

### Landmark Cases

**Lund v. Commonwealth (Virginia, 1977):** Charles Walter Lund, a graduate student at Virginia Tech, used the university's computer resources without authorization for his doctoral research. The Virginia Supreme Court reversed his larceny conviction, holding that computer time and services cannot be the subject of larceny because, under common law, labor and services are intangible and cannot be "taken and carried away." This case exposed a critical gap in traditional theft statutes and prompted numerous states to enact computer fraud and theft-of-services legislation.

**People v. Davis (California, 1998):** The defendant took a shirt from a Mervyn's department store and attempted to return it for a cash refund. The California Supreme Court held that intent to return stolen property only on condition of receiving payment constitutes intent to permanently deprive under larceny law. The court identified three scenarios where larceny exists despite no intent to keep the property: (1) intent to sell property back to its owner, (2) intent to claim a finder's reward, and (3) intent to return for a refund.

**State v. Loomis (Wisconsin, 2016):** The Wisconsin Supreme Court upheld the use of algorithmic risk-assessment tools (COMPAS) at sentencing for theft and related offenses, provided the tool is not the determinative factor. The case raised significant due process concerns about automated sentencing in property crime cases.

### Recent Trends

Several states have raised felony theft thresholds in recent years to account for inflation and reduce incarceration for low-level property crime. California's Proposition 47 (2014) raised the felony threshold from $400 to $950. Texas raised its threshold from $1,500 to $2,500 in 2015. These reforms have generated ongoing political debate about the relationship between prosecution thresholds and retail theft rates.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

The German Strafgesetzbuch (StGB) provides a highly structured, three-tier approach to theft:

### Section 242 — Simple Theft (Diebstahl)

"Whoever takes a movable item belonging to another person with the intent to unlawfully appropriate the item for themselves or a third party is punished with imprisonment of up to five years or with a fine."

The attempt is also punishable under Section 242(2). This baseline provision covers ordinary theft such as shoplifting, pickpocketing, and workplace theft. German courts may impose fines calculated in daily rates (Tagessatze) rather than imprisonment for minor offenses — for example, 30 daily rates at a rate determined by the offender's income.

### Section 243 — Particularly Serious Theft (Besonders schwerer Fall des Diebstahls)

A particularly serious case of theft carries imprisonment of 3 months to 10 years. The statute enumerates specific circumstances that typically constitute a particularly serious case:

1. Breaking into, climbing into, or entering a building, business premises, or other enclosed space using a false key or concealed tool
2. Stealing an item specially secured against removal by a locked container or other protective device
3. Stealing commercially or as a member of a group formed for the continued commission of theft
4. Stealing an item of significance for science, art, history, or technical development from a publicly accessible collection or exhibition
5. Stealing from a church or religious building an item dedicated to worship
6. Stealing an item under official seal or officially stored
7. Exploiting the helplessness of another person, an accident, or a common emergency

Notably, Section 243(2) provides an exception: the particularly serious case does not apply when the stolen item is of minor value (geringwertiger Sache).

### Section 244 — Aggravated Theft (Diebstahl mit Waffen; Bandendiebstahl; Wohnungseinbruchdiebstahl)

This provision covers three categories of aggravated theft, punishable by 6 months to 10 years imprisonment:

- **Armed theft:** Carrying a weapon or other dangerous tool during the commission of theft, even without intent to use it. Mere awareness and accessibility of the tool suffice.
- **Gang theft (Bandendiebstahl):** Committing theft as a member of a gang formed for the continued commission of theft or robbery, with at least one other gang member present.
- **Residential burglary (Wohnungseinbruchdiebstahl):** Breaking into a permanently occupied dwelling.

### Section 244a — Aggravated Gang Theft (Schwerer Bandendiebstahl)

Carries imprisonment of 1 to 10 years with no option for a fine. In less severe cases, the range is 6 months to 5 years.

### Notable Cases

**BGH 1 StR 525/18 (2019):** The Federal Court of Justice (Bundesgerichtshof) clarified that a pocket knife carried routinely and not related to the theft still qualifies the offense as armed theft under Section 244, if the perpetrator is aware of the knife and it is readily accessible. This strict interpretation has been criticized by criminal defense attorneys as disproportionate.

**BVerfG 2 BvR 1895/19 (2020):** The Federal Constitutional Court upheld the elevated minimum sentence of 1 year for residential burglary under Section 244(4), finding it proportionate given the severe psychological impact on victims of home invasion.

## European Framework

### No Harmonized Theft Law

Unlike areas such as terrorism, money laundering, and cybercrime, ordinary theft remains outside the scope of EU criminal law harmonization. Article 83(1) TFEU limits EU competence to ten enumerated "Euro crimes" with cross-border dimensions, and simple property theft is not among them. The German Federal Constitutional Court has been particularly insistent that property crime definitions must remain within national sovereignty.

### Cross-Border Cooperation Mechanisms

Despite the absence of harmonized substantive law, the EU has developed robust procedural tools for addressing cross-border theft:

**European Arrest Warrant (EAW):** Since 2004, the EAW has eliminated traditional extradition procedures among EU member states. Property offenses are included in the list of 32 categories for which dual criminality verification is waived, provided the offense carries a maximum penalty of at least 3 years in the issuing state.

**Eurojust:** Coordinates cross-border investigations involving organized property crime, including cargo theft rings, art theft networks, and transnational shoplifting operations. Eurojust facilitated coordination in major cases such as Operation Bosphorus (2019), targeting organized cargo theft across Western Europe.

**European Investigation Order (EIO):** Directive 2014/41/EU provides a standardized framework for obtaining evidence across borders in criminal cases, including property offenses.

### National Variations Across Europe

| Country | Statute | Simple Theft Maximum | Aggravated Theft Maximum |
|---------|---------|---------------------|-------------------------|
| France | Code penal Art. 311-1 | 3 years, EUR 45,000 fine | 10 years, EUR 150,000 fine (with violence) |
| United Kingdom | Theft Act 1968, s.1 | 7 years | 14 years (burglary, s.9) |
| Germany | StGB Section 242 | 5 years | 10 years (Section 244) |
| Spain | Codigo Penal Art. 234 | 6-18 months (if value > EUR 400) | 1-3 years (aggravated, Art. 235) |
| Italy | Codice Penale Art. 624 | 6 months to 3 years, EUR 154-516 fine | 1-6 years (aggravated, Art. 625) |

### Protocol 1, Article 1 — Right to Property

The European Convention on Human Rights protects property rights under Protocol 1, Article 1, obligating states to have effective legal frameworks for the protection of property. However, this does not prescribe specific criminal penalties and leaves wide discretion to member states.
