## Summary

Air quality regulation -- the legal framework governing emissions of pollutants into the atmosphere and establishing ambient air quality standards to protect human health and the environment -- has evolved from rudimentary smoke abatement statutes in the nineteenth century into one of the most technically complex and consequential areas of environmental law, directly affecting industrial operations, transportation, energy production, and public health outcomes. South Korea regulates air quality through the Clean Air Conservation Act (대기환경보전법), supplemented by the Special Act on the Improvement of Air Quality in Air Control Zones (대기관리권역의 대기환경개선에 관한 특별법) which designates air quality management zones with enhanced emission controls, and the Framework Act on Carbon Neutrality and Green Growth addressing greenhouse gases; Korea's air quality challenges include persistent fine particulate matter (PM2.5) pollution linked to both domestic emissions and transboundary transport from China, prompting the 2019 enactment of the Special Act on the Reduction and Management of Fine Dust (미세먼지 저감 및 관리에 관한 특별법) designating fine dust as a "social disaster." The United States regulates air quality through the Clean Air Act (42 U.S.C. Sections 7401-7671q), one of the most comprehensive environmental statutes globally, establishing National Ambient Air Quality Standards (NAAQS) for six criteria pollutants, New Source Performance Standards (NSPS), hazardous air pollutant (HAP) regulation under Section 112, and the Title V operating permit program. Germany implements EU air quality requirements through the Bundes-Immissionsschutzgesetz (BImSchG, Federal Immission Control Act) and the 39th BImSchV transposing the EU Ambient Air Quality Directive, with enforcement significantly influenced by the landmark Diesel Fahrverbote (driving ban) litigation. The EU Ambient Air Quality Directive (2008/50/EC), revised in 2024 as Directive (EU) 2024/2881, establishes binding limit values for key pollutants that member states must achieve, with the CJEU playing an active enforcement role through infringement proceedings against non-compliant member states.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's air quality regulatory framework is anchored by the Clean Air Conservation Act (대기환경보전법, Act No. 19953), which establishes ambient air quality standards, emission standards for stationary sources, and the emission facility permit system. The Act is supplemented by sector-specific legislation addressing fine particulate matter, vehicle emissions, and regional air quality management.

### Ambient Air Quality Standards

| Pollutant | Standard | Averaging Time |
|-----------|---------|----------------|
| PM2.5 | 15 μg/m³ | Annual mean |
| PM2.5 | 35 μg/m³ | 24-hour |
| PM10 | 50 μg/m³ | Annual mean |
| NO2 | 30 μg/m³ | Annual mean |
| SO2 | 20 μg/m³ | Annual mean |
| O3 | 100 μg/m³ | 8-hour |
| CO | 9 ppm | 8-hour |

### Fine Dust Special Measures

The Special Act on the Reduction and Management of Fine Dust (미세먼지 저감 및 관리에 관한 특별법, 2019) declared fine particulate matter a "social disaster" (사회재난), establishing:

- A National Fine Dust Information Center for real-time monitoring and public alerts
- Emergency reduction measures (비상저감조치) triggered when PM2.5 forecasts exceed 50 μg/m³, including driving restrictions for high-emission vehicles, reduced industrial operations, and construction site restrictions
- Five-year comprehensive plans for fine dust reduction

### Seasonal Management System

Korea operates a seasonal particulate matter management system (미세먼지 계절관리제) from December through March, when pollution levels are highest due to heating emissions and meteorological patterns. Measures include enhanced coal power plant output restrictions, increased industrial inspections, and expanded public transportation.

### Transboundary Pollution

Transboundary transport of pollutants from China significantly contributes to Korea's air quality challenges, particularly during winter and spring. The Korea-China Environmental Cooperation Center and joint research projects under the Trilateral Environmental Ministers Meeting (TEMM) with Japan address the issue, though quantifying the precise contribution of transboundary versus domestic sources remains contentious.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The Clean Air Act, comprehensively amended in 1970 and 1990, is one of the most successful environmental statutes in history, credited with preventing over 230,000 premature deaths annually as of EPA estimates for 2020.

### National Ambient Air Quality Standards (NAAQS)

Section 109 requires the EPA to establish primary NAAQS (protecting public health with an adequate margin of safety) and secondary NAAQS (protecting public welfare including visibility, crops, and materials) for criteria pollutants:

| Pollutant | Primary Standard | Averaging Time | Last Revised |
|-----------|-----------------|----------------|-------------|
| PM2.5 | 9 μg/m³ | Annual mean | 2024 |
| PM2.5 | 35 μg/m³ | 24-hour | 2006 |
| PM10 | 150 μg/m³ | 24-hour | 1987 |
| NO2 | 53 ppb | Annual mean | 1971 |
| SO2 | 75 ppb | 1-hour | 2010 |
| O3 | 0.070 ppm | 8-hour | 2015 |
| CO | 9 ppm | 8-hour | 1971 |
| Lead | 0.15 μg/m³ | Rolling 3-month | 2008 |

The 2024 revision of the PM2.5 annual standard from 12 μg/m³ to 9 μg/m³ represented the most significant tightening in nearly two decades, following extensive scientific review by the Clean Air Scientific Advisory Committee (CASAC).

### New Source Review and Prevention of Significant Deterioration

New and modified major stationary sources must undergo New Source Review (NSR), requiring the installation of Best Available Control Technology (BACT) in attainment areas (Prevention of Significant Deterioration program) or Lowest Achievable Emission Rate (LAER) technology in nonattainment areas.

### State Implementation Plans

States must develop State Implementation Plans (SIPs) demonstrating how they will attain and maintain NAAQS. The EPA retains authority to impose Federal Implementation Plans (FIPs) where state plans are inadequate.

### Hazardous Air Pollutants

Section 112 regulates 187 listed hazardous air pollutants (HAPs) through technology-based Maximum Achievable Control Technology (MACT) standards. The 1990 Clean Air Act Amendments replaced the prior health-based approach with technology-based standards, dramatically accelerating HAP regulation.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's air quality regulation is primarily governed by the Bundes-Immissionsschutzgesetz (BImSchG), which translates as the Federal Immission Control Act. The BImSchG implements both the EU Ambient Air Quality Directive and the Industrial Emissions Directive through a series of ordinances (Verordnungen), most importantly the 39th BImSchV (Verordnung über Luftqualitätsstandards und Emissionshöchstmengen) establishing ambient air quality standards.

### Ambient Air Quality Standards (39th BImSchV)

Current standards transpose the EU Directive 2008/50/EC:

| Pollutant | Limit Value | Averaging Time |
|-----------|------------|----------------|
| PM2.5 | 25 μg/m³ | Annual mean |
| PM10 | 40 μg/m³ | Annual mean |
| PM10 | 50 μg/m³ (35 exceedances/year) | 24-hour |
| NO2 | 40 μg/m³ | Annual mean |
| SO2 | 125 μg/m³ (3 exceedances/year) | 24-hour |

Under the revised EU Directive 2024/2881, Germany must achieve significantly tighter standards by 2030, including PM2.5 at 10 μg/m³ annual mean and NO2 at 20 μg/m³.

### Diesel Fahrverbote (Driving Bans)

Germany's most politically significant air quality enforcement involved court-ordered diesel driving bans in cities exceeding the NO2 annual limit of 40 μg/m³:

**BVerwG (Federal Administrative Court), February 27, 2018 (7 C 30.17):** The Deutsche Umwelthilfe (DUH) successfully argued that Stuttgart and Düsseldorf were required to implement diesel vehicle driving bans as part of their air quality action plans under the 39th BImSchV. The court ruled that driving bans were a permissible and potentially necessary measure to achieve compliance with EU NO2 limits. Subsequently, multiple German cities implemented either zonal or street-level diesel bans for vehicles below Euro 5/6 standards.

### BImSchG Permit System

The BImSchG requires permits (Genehmigungen) for facilities listed in the 4th BImSchV. Permits must ensure that the facility meets emission limits reflecting the best available technology (Stand der Technik), does not cause harmful environmental effects, and takes precautionary measures against harmful emissions (Vorsorgepflicht). The permit procedure includes public participation for major facilities.

## ECHR & EU

### Ambient Air Quality Directive (2008/50/EC)

The original directive established limit values for PM10, PM2.5, NO2, SO2, CO, lead, benzene, and ozone. Non-compliance with limit values has been the subject of extensive CJEU litigation:

**Commission v. Italy (C-644/18, 2020):** Italy found in breach for systematic and persistent exceedance of PM10 daily limit values.

**Commission v. Germany (C-635/18, 2021):** Germany found in breach for systematic exceedance of NO2 annual limit values in numerous air quality zones.

**ClientEarth v. Secretary of State (C-404/13, 2014):** The CJEU held that national courts must order national authorities to establish air quality plans containing adequate measures to achieve compliance in the shortest possible time, providing a legal basis for citizen enforcement.

### Revised Directive (EU) 2024/2881

Adopted on November 20, 2024, the revised directive establishes significantly tighter standards aligned more closely with WHO guidelines:

| Pollutant | Current Limit | 2030 Target |
|-----------|--------------|-------------|
| PM2.5 (annual) | 25 μg/m³ | 10 μg/m³ |
| PM10 (annual) | 40 μg/m³ | 20 μg/m³ |
| NO2 (annual) | 40 μg/m³ | 20 μg/m³ |

The revised directive also introduces a right to compensation for individuals who suffer health damage due to violations of air quality standards, enhancing private enforcement.

### National Emission Ceilings Directive (2016/2284)

The NEC Directive sets national emission reduction commitments for five pollutants (SO2, NOx, NMVOC, NH3, PM2.5) for 2020 and 2030, requiring member states to prepare National Air Pollution Control Programmes.

### Euro Emission Standards

Vehicle emission standards are set at the EU level. Regulation (EU) 2024/1257 (Euro 7) was adopted on April 12, 2024, establishing updated emission limits for cars, vans, trucks, and buses, including for the first time limits on brake and tire particle emissions and battery durability requirements for electric vehicles.

### ECHR Environmental Protection

**Fadeyeva v. Russia (Application No. 55723/00, 2005):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 due to the state's failure to resettle the applicant from an area surrounding a steel plant with severe air pollution exceeding domestic standards, establishing that industrial air pollution can constitute interference with private and family life.

**Cordella and Others v. Italy (Application No. 54414/13, 2019):** The Court found a violation of Articles 8 and 13 regarding the ILVA steelworks in Taranto, holding that Italy failed to protect residents from the health effects of toxic industrial emissions despite longstanding knowledge of the contamination.

## Comparative Analysis

### Standard Stringency

The United States has set the most stringent PM2.5 annual standard at 9 μg/m³ (2024 revision), followed by South Korea at 15 μg/m³, with the EU currently at 25 μg/m³ but targeting 10 μg/m³ by 2030 under the revised directive. These differences reflect varying balances between health protection and economic feasibility, as well as different baseline pollution levels.

### Regulatory Architecture

The U.S. Clean Air Act is uniquely comprehensive, integrating criteria pollutant standards, hazardous air pollutant regulation, mobile source standards, and acid rain provisions in a single statute. Germany and the EU distribute regulation across multiple instruments (ambient quality directive, industrial emissions directive, vehicle standards regulation). South Korea similarly uses multiple statutes but has increasingly consolidated air quality governance.

### Enforcement Innovation

The EU's citizen enforcement mechanism, established through ClientEarth and related cases, has been particularly effective in compelling government action. Germany's diesel driving bans demonstrated the real-world consequences of air quality non-compliance. The United States relies more heavily on federal enforcement through EPA and citizen suit provisions under Section 304 of the Clean Air Act.

### Transboundary Challenges

Transboundary air pollution poses unique challenges. South Korea faces significant pollution transport from China with limited legal recourse. The EU's NEC Directive addresses intra-EU transboundary pollution. The UNECE Convention on Long-Range Transboundary Air Pollution (CLRTAP) provides a framework for European and North American cooperation. The U.S. addresses cross-border pollution with Canada through the Air Quality Agreement of 1991.
