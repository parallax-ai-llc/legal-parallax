## Summary

Women's legal status across the Global South reveals a profound gap between constitutional guarantees of gender equality and the lived realities shaped by patriarchal customary law, religious personal law systems, economic dependence, and institutional failures. China's constitutional equality (Article 48 PRC Constitution) coexists with a deeply gendered labor market, the recently revised Women's Protection Law (2022), and persistent challenges including workplace discrimination, rural women's land rights, and the demographic consequences of the former one-child policy; the #MeToo movement in China, which briefly flourished in 2018, was rapidly suppressed, and the conviction of journalist Huang Xueqin in 2024 for "inciting subversion" illustrated the risks of feminist activism. India's constitutional framework (Articles 14, 15, 16, 21) and extensive protective legislation -- the Dowry Prohibition Act (1961), the Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act (2005), the Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace Act (2013), and criminal law reforms following the 2012 Delhi gang rape -- are undermined by implementation failures, low conviction rates, and the persistence of dowry-related violence (NCRB data recorded 6,753 dowry deaths in 2022). South Africa's Constitution (Section 9: equality; Section 12: freedom and security of the person) and the Domestic Violence Act (116 of 1998) provide a robust formal framework, yet the country experiences epidemic levels of gender-based violence, with a femicide rate five times the global average (WHO data). Nigeria's federal gender equality provisions (Sections 15(2), 42) are contradicted by personal law systems permitting child marriage (legal in northern states under Sharia) and the failure of the Gender and Equal Opportunities Bill (2016, rejected by the Senate). In Latin America, Brazil's Maria da Penha Law (Law No. 11,340/2006) on domestic violence and the feminicidio (femicide) provisions added to criminal codes across the region -- Brazil (2015), Mexico (2012), Colombia (2015), Argentina (2012) -- represent significant legal advances, though Latin America remains the most dangerous region for women outside conflict zones (ECLAC data). Indonesia's Marriage Law No. 1 of 1974 permits polygamy subject to judicial authorization, the minimum marriage age was raised to 19 for both sexes in 2019 (Law No. 16 of 2019), and the Sexual Violence Law (UU TPKS, Law No. 12 of 2022) marked a breakthrough after years of advocacy. The Philippines, which has no divorce law for non-Muslims, enacted the Safe Spaces Act (Republic Act No. 11313, 2019) and has been debating a comprehensive divorce bill since 2018.

## China

### Constitutional and Legislative Framework

Article 48 of the PRC Constitution provides: "Women in the People's Republic of China enjoy equal rights with men in all spheres of life, political, economic, cultural and social, and family life. The State protects the rights and interests of women, applies the principle of equal pay for equal work for men and women alike, and trains and selects cadres from among women."

The Law on the Protection of Women's Rights and Interests (funv quanyi baozhang fa), originally enacted in 1992 and comprehensively revised effective January 1, 2023, represents China's primary women's rights legislation. Key revisions include: Article 23 prohibiting sexual harassment and requiring employers to establish prevention mechanisms; Article 25 defining workplace sexual harassment to include "unwelcome sexually-related speech, text, image, physical conduct, or other conduct"; Article 40 protecting rural women's equal land contract and distribution rights; and Article 67 expanding protection orders to cover situations beyond domestic violence.

The Anti-Domestic Violence Law (fan jiating baoli fa), effective March 1, 2016, introduced personal protection orders (renshi anquan baohu ling) for the first time in Chinese law. Article 23 permits courts to issue protection orders prohibiting the respondent from committing domestic violence, harassing or contacting the applicant, or approaching the applicant's residence or workplace. According to Supreme People's Court data, courts issued approximately 15,000 protection orders between 2016 and 2023, though women's rights organizations including Equality Beijing (before its forced closure in 2021) criticized the threshold as excessively high and the enforcement mechanisms as inadequate.

### Structural Gender Inequality

Despite formal legal equality, China's gender gap manifests in several dimensions. The World Economic Forum's Global Gender Gap Report 2024 ranked China 106th of 146 countries. Women's representation in the CPC Politburo Standing Committee has been zero since 1949; the 20th Central Committee (2022) included 11 women among 205 full members (5.4%).

Rural women's land rights remain a critical issue. Although the Rural Land Contract Law (2018 revision) and the Women's Protection Law guarantee women equal land contract rights, the practice of tying land contracts to household registration (hukou) and the "marrying out" (waijia) custom mean that women who marry into other villages frequently lose access to contracted land in their natal village without gaining equivalent rights in their husband's village. The All-China Women's Federation reported that disputes over rural women's land rights constituted one of the most common categories of complaints received.

The demographic consequences of the one-child policy (1980-2015), including an estimated sex ratio at birth of 111.3 boys per 100 girls in 2020 (National Bureau of Statistics), have created a "marriage squeeze" and contribute to trafficking of women, particularly from Southeast Asia and North Korea.

### Feminist Activism and Repression

The brief flourishing of the Chinese #MeToo movement (米兔) in 2018, catalyzed by allegations against prominent academics and media figures, was curtailed through censorship and prosecution. Zhou Xiaoxuan's (Xianzi's) sexual harassment lawsuit against CCTV presenter Zhu Jun, filed in 2018 and dismissed in September 2021, became the most prominent #MeToo case. The court held that Zhou had failed to meet the burden of proof, and her appeal was rejected in 2022.

The detention and conviction of journalist Huang Xueqin, arrested in September 2021 and sentenced to five years in June 2024 for "inciting subversion of state power," was widely condemned by international human rights organizations. Huang had been a prominent advocate for sexual harassment victims and had helped publicize early #MeToo allegations. The "Feminist Five" -- five activists detained in 2015 for planning to distribute anti-sexual harassment stickers on public transportation -- illustrated the state's treatment of even moderate feminist activism as a threat to social stability.

## India

### Constitutional Framework and Key Legislation

India's constitutional equality framework is multi-layered. Article 14 guarantees equality before the law; Article 15(1) prohibits discrimination on grounds including sex; Article 15(3) permits special provisions for women and children; Article 16 guarantees equality of opportunity in public employment. Article 21 (right to life and personal liberty) has been interpreted by the Supreme Court to encompass the right to live with dignity, which includes freedom from gender-based violence.

The Dowry Prohibition Act (1961) prohibits the giving or receiving of dowry, defined as "any property or valuable security given or agreed to be given either directly or indirectly" (Section 2) in connection with marriage. Despite this prohibition, dowry-related violence remains endemic; the NCRB Crime in India 2022 report recorded 6,753 dowry deaths (under Section 304B IPC) and 12,669 cases of cruelty by husband or relatives (Section 498A IPC). Section 304B creates a presumption of dowry death where a woman's death occurs within seven years of marriage under abnormal circumstances and she was subjected to cruelty for dowry.

The Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act, 2005 (PWDVA), effective October 26, 2006, provides civil remedies including protection orders, residence orders, monetary relief, and custody orders. The Act defines domestic violence broadly to include physical, sexual, verbal, emotional, and economic abuse (Section 3). Unlike criminal prosecution under Section 498A IPC, the PWDVA provides protection to women in relationships including live-in relationships (as clarified by the Supreme Court in D. Velusamy v. D. Patchaiammal, 2010).

### Sexual Harassment: From Vishakha to the 2013 Act

The Supreme Court's landmark decision in Vishakha v. State of Rajasthan (AIR 1997 SC 3011) -- responding to the gang rape of social worker Bhanwari Devi for attempting to prevent a child marriage -- established binding guidelines on workplace sexual harassment in the absence of legislation. The Vishakha Guidelines defined sexual harassment, required employers to establish complaints committees, and imposed preventive and remedial obligations.

The Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace (Prevention, Prohibition and Redressal) Act, 2013 (PoSH Act), enacted sixteen years after Vishakha, established a statutory framework. Key provisions include: a broad definition of workplace covering both organized and unorganized sectors (Section 2(o)); requirement for Internal Complaints Committees (ICCs) in workplaces with 10 or more employees (Section 4); Local Complaints Committees for the unorganized sector and complaints against employers (Section 6); a 90-day filing deadline (Section 9); and penalties for employers who fail to constitute ICCs (Section 26).

### Criminal Law Reforms Post-2012

The December 2012 gang rape and murder of Jyoti Singh (known as "Nirbhaya") on a bus in New Delhi provoked nationwide protests and led to the Justice J.S. Verma Committee report (January 2013), which recommended comprehensive criminal law reforms. The Criminal Law (Amendment) Act, 2013, expanded the definition of rape (Section 375 IPC), introduced new offenses including voyeurism (Section 354C), stalking (Section 354D), and acid attack (Section 326A/326B), increased minimum sentences, and introduced the death penalty for repeat offenders and for rape resulting in death or permanent vegetative state.

Marital rape remains an unresolved issue. Section 375 of the IPC (now Section 63 of the BNS) contains Exception 2: "Sexual intercourse or sexual acts by a man with his own wife, the wife not being under eighteen years of age, is not rape." The Karnataka High Court in Hrishikesh Sahoo v. State of Karnataka (2022) and the Delhi High Court in RIT Foundation v. Union of India (2022) delivered split verdicts -- one judge holding the exception unconstitutional, the other upholding it -- and the matter remains before the Supreme Court.

## Africa

### South Africa: Constitutional Promise and Gender-Based Violence Crisis

South Africa's Constitution provides the strongest formal framework for women's rights in Africa. Section 9 (equality) prohibits unfair discrimination on grounds of "race, gender, sex, pregnancy, marital status, ethnic or social origin, colour, sexual orientation, age, disability, religion, conscience, belief, culture, language and birth." Section 12 guarantees the right to "freedom and security of the person," including the "right to be free from all forms of violence from either public or private sources" (Section 12(1)(c)) and the "right to bodily and psychological integrity" (Section 12(2)).

The Domestic Violence Act (116 of 1998, DVA) provides a comprehensive framework including protection orders, peace orders, and obligations on police to assist complainants. The Act covers a broad range of relationships including marriages, cohabitation, dating, and same-sex relationships (Section 1). The Criminal Law (Sexual Offences and Related Matters) Amendment Act (32 of 2007) expanded the definition of rape to be gender-neutral, criminalized marital rape explicitly, and introduced a National Register for Sex Offenders.

Despite this framework, South Africa experiences a gender-based violence crisis of staggering proportions. The South African Police Service (SAPS) recorded 42,289 sexual offenses in 2022/23, widely understood to be a fraction of actual incidents. The Medical Research Council estimated that a woman is killed every three hours, giving South Africa a femicide rate of 12.5 per 100,000 (compared to a global average of 2.3). President Ramaphosa declared GBV a "second pandemic" in 2020 and established the National Strategic Plan on Gender-Based Violence and Femicide (NSP-GBVF, 2020-2030), but implementation has been criticized for insufficient funding and coordination.

The Customary Law of Succession was reformed following the Constitutional Court's decision in Bhe v. Khayelitsha Magistrate (2005 (1) SA 580 (CC)), which struck down the principle of male primogeniture in customary law succession as unconstitutional. The Reform of Customary Law of Succession and Regulation of Related Matters Act (11 of 2009) implemented the Court's directive.

### Nigeria: Legal Pluralism and Child Marriage

Nigeria's federal structure creates a patchwork of women's rights protections. The Violence Against Persons (Prohibition) Act (VAPP, 2015) -- applicable only to the Federal Capital Territory (Abuja) -- criminalizes domestic violence, rape, female genital mutilation, emotional/verbal abuse, and abandonment. Only approximately 12 of Nigeria's 36 states had enacted equivalent state-level legislation by 2024.

Child marriage remains legal in northern Nigeria, where the Child Rights Act (2003) has not been adopted by most northern states. Section 29(4)(b) of the Nigerian Constitution defines a married woman as being "of full age," effectively permitting the marriage of girls below 18 if sanctioned by custom or religion. UNICEF estimates that 43% of Nigerian girls are married before age 18 and 16% before age 15, with rates exceeding 70% in some northwestern states. The Nigerian Senate rejected the Gender and Equal Opportunities Bill in 2016, with opponents citing religious and cultural objections.

The practice of female genital mutilation (FGM), while declining, affects approximately 20% of Nigerian women and girls (NDHS 2018 data). The VAPP Act criminalizes FGM with penalties up to 4 years imprisonment, but coverage is limited to the FCT.

### African Union and Regional Framework

The Protocol to the African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights on the Rights of Women in Africa (Maputo Protocol, 2003) is the most comprehensive regional women's rights instrument. Key provisions include: equal rights in marriage (Article 6); separation, divorce and annulment (Article 7); equal access to justice (Article 8); political participation including parity representation (Article 9); reproductive rights including access to medical abortion in cases of sexual assault, rape, incest, or risk to maternal/fetal health (Article 14(2)(c)); elimination of harmful practices including FGM (Article 5); and rights of widows (Article 20) and elderly women (Article 22).

## Latin America

### Brazil's Maria da Penha Law

Law No. 11,340/2006 (the Maria da Penha Law), named after Maria da Penha Maia Fernandes -- who was left paraplegic by her husband's assassination attempts, and whose case was decided by the Inter-American Commission on Human Rights (Report No. 54/01, Case 12.051, 2001) finding Brazil in violation of the American Convention -- is the cornerstone of Brazil's domestic violence framework.

Key provisions include: definition of domestic and family violence encompassing physical, psychological, sexual, property, and moral violence (Article 7); creation of specialized domestic violence courts (Juizados de Violencia Domestica e Familiar contra a Mulher) (Article 14); emergency protective measures (medidas protetivas de urgencia) including removal from the home, no-contact orders, and maintenance orders (Articles 22-24); prohibition of alternative sentencing measures (cestas basicas or fines) for domestic violence (Article 17); and mandatory arrest in cases of flagrant domestic violence (Article 20).

The STF in ADC 19 (2012) upheld the constitutionality of the Maria da Penha Law, rejecting arguments that it violated gender equality by providing special protections for women. In ADI 4424 (2012), the Court held that domestic violence crimes are subject to public criminal prosecution regardless of the victim's desire to proceed (acao penal publica incondicionada), removing the previous requirement that victims formally press charges.

### Feminicidio Across Latin America

The criminalization of feminicidio (femicide -- the killing of women on account of their gender) has spread rapidly across Latin America, becoming one of the region's most significant legal innovations. Mexico first incorporated feminicidio into federal criminal law in 2012 (Article 325, Federal Penal Code), defining it as the deprivation of the life of a woman when specific gender-based circumstances are present, including prior sexual violence, mutilation, prior threats or harassment, or a sentimental or intimate relationship between victim and perpetrator. Brazil added feminicidio as a qualifying circumstance for homicide (Article 121, para. 2, VI of the Penal Code) through Law No. 13,104/2015. Colombia enacted the Rosa Elvira Cely Law (Law 1761/2015), named after a woman raped, tortured, and murdered in Bogota in 2012.

The Inter-American Court of Human Rights' landmark decision in Gonzalez et al. ("Cotton Field") v. Mexico (2009) -- addressing the murder of young women in Ciudad Juarez -- established state responsibility for failure to prevent, investigate, and punish gender-based violence, creating binding obligations for OAS member states that influenced subsequent feminicidio legislation.

Despite legal advances, Latin America records the highest rates of femicide outside conflict zones. ECLAC's Gender Equality Observatory documented 4,050 femicides across 26 countries in 2022, with Brazil (1,437), Mexico (947), and Colombia (614) recording the highest absolute numbers.

## Southeast Asia

### Indonesian Women's Rights Framework

The Indonesian Constitution (Article 27(1)) provides that "all citizens shall be equal before the law and the government" without explicit gender enumeration. The Marriage Law (Law No. 1 of 1974) serves as the primary framework for marital rights, establishing marriage as "a physical as well as spiritual bond between a man and a woman" (Article 1). Article 3(2) permits courts to authorize a husband to take more than one wife, subject to conditions under Article 4 (wife's inability to fulfill duties, physical disability, or inability to bear children) and requirements under Article 5 (wife's consent, financial capacity, guarantee of equal treatment). Government Regulation No. 10/1983 imposes additional restrictions on polygamy by civil servants.

The minimum marriage age was raised from 16 (women) to 19 for both sexes by Law No. 16 of 2019, following Constitutional Court Decision No. 22/PUU-XV/2017, which held that the different minimum ages for men (19) and women (16) violated the constitutional guarantee of equality and the right of children to protection from violence and discrimination. However, Article 7(2) permits marriage below 19 with court dispensation, and data from the Religious Courts show that dispensation requests increased significantly after the reform.

The Law on the Crime of Sexual Violence (UU TPKS, Law No. 12 of 2022), enacted after a nine-year advocacy campaign led by the National Commission on Violence Against Women (Komnas Perempuan), was a landmark achievement. The law defines nine categories of sexual violence, including non-physical sexual harassment (previously uncovered), forced marriage, forced prostitution, sexual slavery, sexual exploitation, electronic-based sexual violence, sexual violence facilitated by intoxicants, and forced contraception/sterilization/abortion. It provides for victim restitution (Article 30-35), whistleblower protection, and establishes integrated service centers. Notably, the law covers sexual violence in marriage, a significant expansion from the previous domestic violence framework.

### Philippine Women's Rights

The Philippines has enacted extensive women's protection legislation. The Anti-Violence Against Women and Their Children Act (Republic Act No. 9262, 2004, Anti-VAWC Act) provides a comprehensive framework for domestic violence, including protection orders (barangay protection orders, temporary and permanent protection orders), economic abuse provisions, and mandatory filing of criminal charges. The Anti-Rape Law (Republic Act No. 8353, 1997) reclassified rape from a crime against chastity to a crime against persons, expanded the definition to include marital rape, and made it a public crime (prosecutable without a victim's complaint for certain circumstances).

The Safe Spaces Act (Republic Act No. 11313, 2019), known as the "Bawal Bastos" law, addresses gender-based sexual harassment in streets, public spaces, online, and in workplaces. It imposes escalating penalties for catcalling, wolf-whistling, unwanted sexual advances, and online sexual harassment.

The Philippines remains the only country in the world (aside from Vatican City) without a divorce law for non-Muslims. Legal separation (Family Code, Articles 55-67) does not dissolve the marriage bond. Annulment (Family Code, Articles 35-54) requires proof of specific grounds including psychological incapacity (Article 36), which the Supreme Court in Republic v. Molina (G.R. No. 108763, 1997) interpreted restrictively before relaxing the standard in Tan-Andal v. Andal (G.R. No. 196359, 2021). The House of Representatives passed House Bill No. 9349 (Absolute Divorce Bill) in 2024, but Senate action remains pending.

The Magna Carta of Women (Republic Act No. 9710, 2009) is a comprehensive women's rights statute implementing CEDAW provisions, requiring the state to take steps to realize women's rights in all spheres and mandating the establishment of gender-responsive government budgets.

## Comparative Analysis

### Constitutional Guarantees vs. Implementation

Every jurisdiction examined guarantees formal gender equality, yet outcomes diverge dramatically. China's authoritarian efficiency in some areas (education parity, labor force participation) coexists with political suppression of feminist organizing. India's extensive statutory framework is undermined by implementation failures and the persistence of personal law systems that create differential treatment by religion. South Africa's transformative constitutional framework confronts epidemic gender-based violence. The gap between law and reality is a universal feature of women's rights in the Global South, but the specific mechanisms of failure differ.

### Personal Law and Gender Inequality

The retention of religion-specific or customary personal law systems -- India's separate marriage and succession laws, Nigeria's Sharia personal law, Indonesia's religion-based marriage system, the Philippines' Muslim personal law -- creates structural gender inequality that constitutional equality provisions are unable to fully address. The tension between religious/cultural autonomy and gender equality is among the most intractable issues in Global South jurisprudence.

### Femicide as a Legal Category

Latin America's creation of feminicidio as a distinct criminal offense represents a significant contribution to global legal development. By naming gender-motivated killing as a specific crime with enhanced penalties, these provisions make visible a form of violence that generic homicide provisions obscure. The absence of equivalent provisions in Asia and Africa reflects different legal traditions rather than different realities.

### The CEDAW Factor

All jurisdictions examined have ratified CEDAW, but with significant reservations (India on Articles 5(a) and 16(1); China on Article 29). The CEDAW Committee's concluding observations provide a consistent external benchmark that has influenced domestic reform. The Committee's General Recommendation No. 35 on Gender-Based Violence Against Women (2017), updating General Recommendation No. 19 (1992), has been cited in domestic litigation across the Global South, including in Indian and South African courts.
