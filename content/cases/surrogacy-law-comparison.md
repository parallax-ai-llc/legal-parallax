## Summary

Surrogacy law -- governing agreements in which a woman carries and delivers a child for intended parents -- represents one of the most ethically and legally contentious areas of reproductive law, with jurisdictions ranging from full commercial permissibility to outright criminal prohibition. South Korea occupies a legal gray zone: no specific surrogacy statute exists, and while the practice is not explicitly prohibited, the Bioethics and Safety Act restricts related assisted reproductive technologies, and commercial surrogacy arrangements lack legal enforceability. The United States presents a patchwork of state-level approaches, with California (Family Code Sections 7960-7962) serving as the most surrogacy-friendly jurisdiction in the world, permitting compensated gestational surrogacy with pre-birth parentage orders, while states like Michigan criminalize surrogacy contracts. Germany prohibits surrogacy comprehensively through the Embryonenschutzgesetz (Embryo Protection Act, Section 1(1)(7)), making it a criminal offense for physicians to transfer an embryo to a surrogate, though German courts have increasingly grappled with the recognition of foreign surrogacy arrangements. The European Court of Human Rights, in its landmark decisions in Mennesson v. France (2014) and its Advisory Opinion P16-2018-001 (2019), has held that the right to respect for private life under Article 8 requires member states to provide a mechanism for recognizing the legal parent-child relationship between a child born through surrogacy abroad and the intended parents, at least where a genetic link exists.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea lacks specific legislation addressing surrogacy, leaving the practice in a legal vacuum. The relevant legal landscape includes:

**Bioethics and Safety Act (saengmyeong yulli mit anjeone gwanhan beomnyul):**

- **Article 23:** Prohibits the sale or purchase of human embryos, ova, and sperm for profit. While not explicitly targeting surrogacy, this provision creates legal uncertainty around commercial surrogacy compensation.
- **Article 24:** Restricts embryo research and transfer to specific medical purposes, potentially constraining the use of IVF for surrogacy purposes.
- **Article 25:** Prohibits the creation of embryos for purposes other than pregnancy, complicating the legal basis for creating embryos specifically for transfer to a surrogate.

**Civil Code (Minbeop):**

- **Article 844:** Establishes the presumption that a child born during marriage is the husband's child. Under Korean family law, the woman who gives birth is considered the legal mother (mater semper certa est principle), creating a fundamental legal conflict with gestational surrogacy where the intended mother is not the birth mother.
- **Article 855-856:** Provides for acknowledgment (inga) and legitimation of children, but does not contemplate surrogacy arrangements.

**Mother and Child Health Act:**

This act regulates assisted reproductive procedures but does not specifically address surrogacy. It establishes the framework for fertility treatments, IVF, and embryo handling without creating a permissive or prohibitive framework for surrogacy.

### Key Principles

**Mater Semper Certa Est:** Korean family law follows the principle that the birth mother is the legal mother. In surrogacy arrangements, this means the surrogate -- not the intended mother -- is recognized as the child's legal mother at birth. Intended parents must pursue adoption or other legal mechanisms to establish their parental rights, a process that is uncertain and lacks clear statutory guidance.

**Decline of Domestic Surrogacy:** While surrogacy was more common and loosely tolerated in South Korea in the 1990s and early 2000s, the practice has declined significantly due to social stigma, legal uncertainty, and the availability of cross-border surrogacy options. Korean intended parents increasingly travel to the United States (particularly California), Canada, or Southeast Asian countries for surrogacy.

**Government Study Commissions:** The Korean Ministry of Health and Welfare has convened study groups to consider surrogacy legislation on multiple occasions (2006, 2014, 2019), but no legislative proposal has advanced through the National Assembly. The deep cultural emphasis on bloodline (hyeoltong) and the complexities of the Korean family registry (hojeok, now replaced by the individual family relations registration system) have contributed to legislative inaction.

### Notable Cases

**Seoul Family Court Surrogacy Recognition Cases (2018-2020):** In several unpublished family court decisions, Seoul Family Court judges granted adoption orders to intended parents who had engaged surrogates in the United States. These decisions recognized the factual parentage of the intended genetic parents but did not establish general precedent for surrogacy arrangements.

**Korean Constitutional Court Bioethics Petitions:** Petitions challenging the Bioethics and Safety Act's restrictions as violations of reproductive autonomy have been filed but not resulted in landmark rulings specifically addressing surrogacy. The Constitutional Court has generally upheld the Act's restrictions on assisted reproduction.

## United States

### Federal Framework

The United States has no federal surrogacy legislation. Surrogacy is governed entirely by state law, resulting in a complex patchwork of approaches:

**Uniform Parentage Act (UPA):**

The Uniform Law Commission's UPA (most recently revised in 2017) includes Article 8 specifically addressing gestational surrogacy. The 2017 UPA:

- Validates gestational surrogacy agreements that comply with specified requirements
- Requires independent legal counsel for the surrogate
- Mandates a 72-hour post-birth withdrawal period for the surrogate
- Extends surrogacy provisions to both gestational and genetic (traditional) surrogacy
- Has been adopted in whole or part by approximately 15 states

**State Classification:**

| Category | States | Key Features |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| **Surrogacy-friendly (compensated permitted)** | California, Nevada, Connecticut, Maine, New Hampshire, Oregon, Rhode Island, Vermont, Washington (2019), Colorado | Pre-birth parentage orders; clear statutory frameworks; available regardless of marital status or sexual orientation |
| **Surrogacy-permitted (altruistic preferred)** | New York (2021 reform), Virginia, Utah, Illinois, Florida | Varying restrictions on compensation; some require genetic connection |
| **Surrogacy-unclear or case law only** | Massachusetts, Ohio, Pennsylvania, Georgia, Maryland | No statute; court decisions provide limited guidance |
| **Surrogacy-prohibited or void** | Michigan, Indiana (traditional only), Louisiana (commercial) | Michigan: surrogacy contracts void and unenforceable; criminal penalties for commercial surrogacy brokers (MCL 722.859) -- up to $50,000 fine and 5 years imprisonment |

### State Variations

**California (Family Code Sections 7960-7962):**

California's surrogacy framework, established by AB 1217 (2012), is the most comprehensive and permissive in the world:

- **Section 7960:** Defines "assisted reproduction agreement for gestational carriers" and establishes requirements for enforceability
- **Section 7961:** Requires both parties to have independent legal counsel; surrogate must have independent legal consultation before embryo transfer
- **Section 7962:** Pre-birth parentage orders: intended parents can obtain a court order establishing their parentage before birth, which directs the hospital to list them on the birth certificate
- Available to all intended parents regardless of marital status, sexual orientation, gender identity, or genetic relationship to the child
- No residency requirement for intended parents
- Compensated surrogacy explicitly permitted; compensation terms must be set out in the agreement

**New York (Child-Parent Security Act, 2021):**

New York legalized compensated gestational surrogacy effective February 15, 2021, after decades of prohibition under the 1992 Domestic Relations Law Section 122. The CPSA established a "Surrogates' Bill of Rights" including:

- Right to make all health and welfare decisions regarding the surrogate's body and pregnancy
- Right to comprehensive health insurance coverage throughout pregnancy and 12 months postpartum
- Right to independent legal counsel paid by the intended parents

**Baby M Case Legacy -- In re Baby M, 109 N.J. 396 (1988):**

The New Jersey Supreme Court's landmark decision invalidated a traditional surrogacy contract, holding that surrogacy agreements conflicted with public policy and statutes prohibiting the sale of children. While granting custody to the intended father, the court awarded visitation rights to the surrogate mother. This case established that surrogacy contracts cannot terminate a surrogate's parental rights absent a voluntary surrender, and profoundly shaped surrogacy legislation nationwide.

### Notable Cases

**Johnson v. Calvert, 5 Cal.4th 84 (1993):** The California Supreme Court held that in gestational surrogacy, the intended mother -- not the gestational carrier -- is the child's legal mother, reasoning that the parties who intended to bring about the birth of a child and raise the child as their own are the child's natural parents under California law. This "intent test" became a foundational principle of U.S. surrogacy law.

**In re Marriage of Buzzanca, 61 Cal.App.4th 1410 (1998):** A California appeals court held that intended parents who used both donor egg and donor sperm (with no genetic connection to the child) were the child's legal parents, extending the intent-based parentage analysis beyond genetic connection.

**Raftopol v. Ramey, 299 Conn. 681 (2011):** The Connecticut Supreme Court affirmed that same-sex intended parents could obtain pre-birth parentage orders in gestational surrogacy arrangements.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany maintains one of the most restrictive surrogacy regimes in Europe, rooted in the principle that the dignity of the birth mother and child precludes the commodification of reproductive capacity.

**Embryonenschutzgesetz (ESchG -- Embryo Protection Act, 1990):**

- **Section 1(1)(7):** Makes it a criminal offense to undertake the transfer of an embryo to a woman (the "substitute mother") who is prepared to give up the child permanently after birth. Penalty: up to three years' imprisonment or a fine.
- **Section 1(1)(2):** Prohibits artificial fertilization of an egg cell for any purpose other than bringing about a pregnancy of the woman from whom the egg cell originated (with narrow exceptions for egg donation between partners), effectively preventing the creation of embryos for gestational surrogacy.
- **Section 1(3):** Only the physician is subject to criminal penalties -- not the surrogate or the intended parents. This asymmetric liability structure was designed to avoid penalizing women who serve as surrogates.

**Adoptionsvermittlungsgesetz (AdVermiG -- Adoption Placement Act):**

- **Section 13c:** Prohibits the arrangement or brokering of surrogate motherhood. Any person who arranges a surrogacy is subject to a fine of up to 25,000 euros.
- **Section 13d:** Prohibits advertising for surrogacy services.

**Burgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB -- Civil Code):**

- **Section 1591:** "The mother of a child is the woman who gave birth to it." This provision establishes an irrebuttable presumption that the birth mother is the legal mother, directly conflicting with the premise of gestational surrogacy.

### Key Principles

**Human Dignity (Menschenwurde):** German constitutional doctrine, grounded in Article 1(1) GG ("Human dignity shall be inviolable"), underpins the prohibition on surrogacy. The prevailing legal view holds that surrogacy arrangements instrumentalize both the surrogate mother and the child, treating the child as an object of contractual exchange.

**Recognition of Foreign Surrogacy -- Evolving Jurisprudence:**

Despite the domestic prohibition, German courts have increasingly grappled with the recognition of parent-child relationships established through surrogacy abroad:

- **BGH, XII ZB 463/13 (December 10, 2014):** The Federal Court of Justice (Bundesgerichtshof) held that a Californian court order establishing the parentage of two German men (same-sex couple) as parents of a child born through surrogacy must be recognized in Germany. The court reasoned that non-recognition would violate the child's right to its legal identity and the right to respect for private and family life.
- **BGH, XII ZB 530/15 (2017):** Extended the recognition principle, holding that German authorities must recognize foreign birth certificates listing intended parents where the surrogacy was lawful in the country of birth and the intended father has a genetic link to the child.
- **BVerfG, 1 BvR 421/05 (2018):** The Federal Constitutional Court declined to rule comprehensively on surrogacy recognition but emphasized the paramountcy of the child's best interests (Kindeswohl) in all family law proceedings.

### Notable Cases

**Cologne Administrative Court Decision (2010):** A German couple who had a child through surrogacy in India sought recognition of their parentage in Germany. The Cologne court initially denied recognition, applying BGB Section 1591 strictly. The case was eventually resolved through a step-parent adoption by the intended mother, illustrating the circuitous legal path faced by intended parents.

**Hamburg Family Court -- Ukrainian Surrogacy (2019):** The Hamburg Family Court recognized the parentage of intended German parents who engaged a surrogate in Ukraine, applying the BGH's evolving recognition framework. This case highlighted the growing tension between domestic prohibition and international comity.

## ECHR / EU Framework

### Convention Articles

The European Convention on Human Rights addresses surrogacy primarily through:

- **Article 8 (Right to respect for private and family life):** The core provision invoked in surrogacy cases. The Court has held that a child's right to identity and to have their legal parent-child relationships recognized falls within the scope of "private life."
- **Article 14 (Prohibition of discrimination):** Relevant where differential treatment of children born through surrogacy raises equality concerns.
- **Article 3 of the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights:** Provides that "in the fields of medicine and biology, the following must be respected in particular: ... the prohibition on making the human body and its parts as such a source of financial gain." This has been invoked against commercial surrogacy.

### Key Case Law

**Mennesson v. France, App. No. 65192/11 (June 26, 2014):**

A French couple traveled to California, where twin girls were born through gestational surrogacy using the father's sperm and a donor egg. Upon return to France, authorities refused to transcribe the Californian birth certificates into the French civil register. The ECtHR held:

- France violated Article 8 with respect to the children's right to private life
- The Court recognized the state's wide margin of appreciation regarding surrogacy policy generally
- However, the margin narrows when the child's identity is at stake: "the effects of the non-recognition of the legal parent-child relationship under French law on the children's right to respect for their private life" could not be justified
- Where a genetic link exists between the intended parent and the child, the state must provide a mechanism for legal recognition of that relationship

**Labassee v. France, App. No. 65941/11 (2014):** Decided the same day as Mennesson, with identical reasoning -- France's refusal to recognize a parent-child relationship established through surrogacy in Minnesota violated Article 8.

**Paradiso and Campanelli v. Italy, App. No. 25358/12 (Grand Chamber, January 24, 2017):**

An Italian couple engaged a surrogate in Russia, but due to a laboratory error, the child had no genetic connection to either intended parent. Italian authorities removed the child from the couple and placed it in state care. The Grand Chamber held (11-6):

- No violation of Article 8 regarding the "family life" aspect, as the relationship was too short and legally uncertain
- The absence of a genetic link between the intended parents and the child was a relevant distinguishing factor from Mennesson
- States retain a wide margin of appreciation regarding surrogacy, particularly where no genetic link exists

**Advisory Opinion P16-2018-001 (April 10, 2019):**

Requested by the French Court of Cassation under Protocol No. 16, this advisory opinion addressed the recognition of the intended mother in gestational surrogacy. The Grand Chamber held:

- The right to respect for private life requires domestic law to provide a possibility for recognition of the legal parent-child relationship with the intended mother
- However, states are not obliged to register the details of the foreign birth certificate; adoption or other mechanisms may suffice
- The child's best interests and right to identity are paramount considerations

**D.B. and Others v. Switzerland, App. Nos. 58817/15 and 58252/15 (November 22, 2022):**

The Court found a violation of Article 8 where Swiss authorities refused to recognize the parent-child relationship between children born through surrogacy in California and their intended parents (same-sex male couple). The Court reiterated that the child's best interests require legal recognition of the parent-child relationship, regardless of the parents' sexual orientation.

### Member State Obligations

Following the ECHR case law, member states must:

1. Provide a mechanism for recognizing the legal parent-child relationship established through surrogacy abroad, at least where a genetic link exists
2. Consider the best interests of the child as a primary consideration
3. Ensure that domestic prohibitions on surrogacy do not deprive children of legal identity or parentage

Member state approaches vary significantly: the United Kingdom permits altruistic surrogacy under the Surrogacy Arrangements Act 1985 but prohibits commercial surrogacy, with parentage transferred through "parental orders" under the Human Fertilisation and Embryology Act 2008. Greece permits altruistic gestational surrogacy with court authorization. Ukraine and Russia permit commercial surrogacy and have become major surrogacy destinations for European intended parents. Portugal permits altruistic surrogacy since 2016 (Law No. 25/2016), though with significant restrictions. India, once the world's largest surrogacy market, banned commercial surrogacy for foreign nationals in 2015 and enacted the Surrogacy (Regulation) Act 2021, permitting only altruistic surrogacy for Indian married couples. Australia permits altruistic surrogacy in most states but criminalizes commercial surrogacy, with Queensland, New South Wales, and the ACT imposing penalties for overseas commercial surrogacy arrangements. Japan has no surrogacy legislation; the Japan Society of Obstetrics and Gynecology guidelines prohibit the practice, though it is not criminalized. Brazil permits altruistic surrogacy under the Federal Council of Medicine's Resolution No. 2,320/2022.

### Ethical and Policy Dimensions

The regulation of surrogacy involves competing ethical frameworks that explain the divergence across jurisdictions:

**Reproductive Autonomy vs. Commodification:** The United States (particularly California) frames surrogacy through the lens of reproductive autonomy and freedom of contract, treating surrogacy agreements as legitimate expressions of individual choice. Germany's prohibition rests on the opposing principle that surrogacy commodifies the female body and the child, violating Article 1(1) GG (human dignity). South Korea's silence reflects cultural ambivalence -- reproductive autonomy values competing with Confucian family structure norms.

**Feminist Perspectives:** Feminist scholarship is deeply divided on surrogacy. Liberal feminists argue that banning surrogacy denies women agency over their own bodies. Radical and socialist feminists contend that commercial surrogacy disproportionately exploits economically disadvantaged women, particularly in the Global South, and constitutes a form of reproductive labor extraction. The ECHR has not resolved this tension, instead deferring to national margins of appreciation.

**Cross-Border Surrogacy ("Reproductive Tourism"):** The prohibition of surrogacy in countries like Germany, France, and Italy has driven demand for cross-border surrogacy, primarily to the United States, Ukraine, Georgia, and (formerly) India and Thailand. This "reproductive tourism" creates complex legal situations where children may be stateless or lack recognized parentage in their parents' home country. The Hague Conference on Private International Law's Parentage/Surrogacy Project aims to develop an international instrument addressing cross-border parentage recognition, but negotiations remain ongoing as of 2026.

**Children's Rights Perspective:** The UN Convention on the Rights of the Child (Article 3 -- best interests of the child; Article 7 -- right to identity and nationality; Article 35 -- prevention of sale of children) provides a framework for evaluating surrogacy from the child's perspective. The Optional Protocol on the Sale of Children has been invoked by critics who argue that commercial surrogacy constitutes the "sale" of children, though this interpretation remains contested.
