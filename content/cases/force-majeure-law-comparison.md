## Summary

Force majeure -- the doctrine that excuses contractual performance when extraordinary events beyond the parties' control render performance impossible or impracticable -- stands at the intersection of contract law, risk allocation, and international commercial practice, with its scope and application varying dramatically across legal traditions. South Korea codifies force majeure principles within the Korean Civil Code (민법), particularly through Articles 390 and 537-538, which address impossibility of performance (이행불능) and the allocation of risk in bilateral contracts, with courts applying the doctrine restrictively to events that are genuinely unforeseeable and unavoidable; the COVID-19 pandemic generated significant judicial development as Korean courts evaluated whether government lockdown orders and supply chain disruptions qualified as force majeure events. The United States, lacking a unified force majeure doctrine in common law, relies primarily on contractual force majeure clauses interpreted under state law, supplemented by the related but narrower doctrines of impossibility, impracticability (codified in UCC Section 2-615 for goods contracts and Restatement (Second) of Contracts Sections 261-272), and frustration of purpose; American courts have traditionally construed force majeure clauses narrowly, requiring the specific event to be enumerated or to fall within a catch-all provision. Germany applies force majeure through the BGB's provisions on impossibility of performance (Unmöglichkeit, Sections 275-276 BGB) and the judicially developed doctrine of Störung der Geschäftsgrundlage (disturbance of the foundation of the transaction, codified in Section 313 BGB), which permits contract adaptation or termination when circumstances fundamental to the contract change so drastically that the parties would not have entered the contract or would have done so on materially different terms. At the European and international level, force majeure is addressed by the UNIDROIT Principles of International Commercial Contracts (Article 7.1.7), the United Nations Convention on Contracts for the International Sale of Goods (CISG Article 79), and the ICC Force Majeure Clause (most recently revised in 2020), which collectively establish influential model frameworks for international commercial transactions.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's approach to force majeure is rooted in the Korean Civil Code (민법), which follows the civil law tradition of codifying impossibility of performance as a ground for discharge of obligations. The Code does not use the term "force majeure" (불가항력) explicitly in its core provisions but addresses the concept through the framework of impossibility (이행불능) and fault-based liability.

**Article 390 (Liability for Non-Performance):** The obligor is liable for damages resulting from non-performance of an obligation. However, if the non-performance is not attributable to the obligor's intentional act or negligence (고의 또는 과실), the obligor is not liable. Force majeure events -- those that are unforeseeable and unavoidable even with the exercise of due care -- fall outside the scope of attributable fault.

**Articles 537-538 (Risk in Bilateral Contracts):** Article 537 addresses the situation where performance of one party's obligation becomes impossible due to a cause not attributable to either party, providing that the obligee loses the right to demand counter-performance (risk falls on the obligor). Article 538 addresses impossibility attributable to the obligee, placing the risk on the obligee.

### Judicial Interpretation

Korean courts have developed a restrictive interpretation of force majeure, requiring three elements: the event must be external to the party's sphere of control; the event must be unforeseeable at the time of contract formation; and the event must be unavoidable despite the exercise of reasonable care. The Supreme Court of Korea (대법원) has consistently held that mere economic difficulty or increased cost of performance does not constitute force majeure.

### COVID-19 Jurisprudence

The COVID-19 pandemic tested South Korea's force majeure doctrine extensively. The Seoul Central District Court and other lower courts evaluated numerous cases involving commercial lease disputes, event cancellations, and supply contract non-performance. In several significant decisions, courts held that government social distancing orders and mandatory business closure directives qualified as force majeure or analogous grounds for rent reduction under Article 628 of the Civil Code (rent reduction for partial impossibility of use). The National Assembly also enacted temporary legislation providing for rent reduction mediation and landlord-tenant dispute resolution during the pandemic period.

### Commercial Practice

In Korean commercial practice, sophisticated contracts -- particularly those involving international transactions, construction projects, and long-term supply agreements -- typically include explicit force majeure clauses (불가항력 조항) that enumerate triggering events and specify consequences. The Korean Commercial Arbitration Board (대한상사중재원, KCAB) has developed model force majeure provisions for use in domestic and international contracts administered under its rules.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States presents a unique landscape for force majeure because the doctrine has no independent existence in American common law. Instead, it operates exclusively through contractual provisions, supplemented by related common law and statutory doctrines.

**Contractual Force Majeure Clauses:** The enforceability and scope of force majeure clauses depend on the specific language of the clause as interpreted under applicable state contract law. American courts generally apply the following principles: enumerated events in the clause define its scope; catch-all language ("or any other event beyond the parties' control") is construed narrowly under the ejusdem generis canon; the event must have actually prevented, hindered, or delayed performance (not merely made it more expensive); and the affected party must have taken reasonable steps to mitigate.

**UCC Section 2-615 -- Excuse by Failure of Presupposed Conditions:** For contracts governed by the Uniform Commercial Code (sale of goods), Section 2-615 provides that performance is excused when it has been made "impracticable" by the occurrence of a contingency the non-occurrence of which was a basic assumption on which the contract was made. This standard is more lenient than strict impossibility but still requires a showing that performance is commercially impracticable rather than merely unprofitable.

### Common Law Doctrines

| Doctrine | Standard | Key Cases |
|----------|----------|-----------|
| Impossibility | Performance is objectively impossible | Taylor v. Caldwell, 122 Eng. Rep. 309 (1863) (foundational); Mineral Park Land Co. v. Howard, 172 Cal. 289 (1916) |
| Impracticability | Performance requires excessive and unreasonable expense | Restatement (Second) Section 261; Alcoa v. Essex Group, 499 F. Supp. 53 (W.D. Pa. 1980) |
| Frustration of Purpose | Principal purpose of contract substantially frustrated | Krell v. Henry [1903] 2 KB 740 (English origin); Lloyd v. Murphy, 25 Cal.2d 48 (1944) |

### COVID-19 Litigation

The COVID-19 pandemic generated an unprecedented volume of force majeure litigation in U.S. courts. Key trends included: courts required specific reference to "pandemic," "epidemic," or "government action" in force majeure clauses to excuse performance related to COVID-19 (In re Hitz Restaurant Group, 616 B.R. 374 (Bankr. N.D. Ill. 2020)); mere financial hardship caused by the pandemic was insufficient absent a clause covering economic downturns; and government closure orders were more likely to trigger force majeure relief than voluntary business decisions to close. The pandemic also prompted the American Bar Association and other organizations to issue guidance on drafting more comprehensive force majeure clauses.

### Distinction from Related Doctrines

American law sharply distinguishes between force majeure (a purely contractual concept), impossibility/impracticability (excuse doctrines that operate as implied-in-law conditions), and frustration of purpose (which addresses destruction of the value of performance rather than the ability to perform). A party whose contract lacks a force majeure clause must rely on the more demanding standards of these common law doctrines.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany provides a comprehensive statutory framework addressing force majeure through two principal mechanisms: impossibility of performance (Unmöglichkeit) under Sections 275-276 BGB and disturbance of the foundation of the transaction (Störung der Geschäftsgrundlage) under Section 313 BGB.

**Section 275 BGB -- Impossibility:** Paragraph (1) provides that a claim for performance is excluded to the extent that performance is impossible for the obligor or any other person. Paragraph (2) establishes a right to refuse performance where performance would require expenses grossly disproportionate to the interest of the obligee in performance, taking into account the content of the obligation and the requirements of good faith. Paragraph (3) permits the obligor to refuse performance where performance is to be rendered personally and cannot be reasonably expected given the impediment and the obligee's interest.

**Section 276 BGB -- Responsibility of the Obligor:** The obligor is responsible for intentional acts and negligence unless a stricter or lesser degree of responsibility is prescribed or follows from the content of the obligation. Section 276(1) sentence 1 establishes the general principle that an obligor who is not at fault for the impossibility is not liable for damages, which is the functional equivalent of force majeure discharge.

### Störung der Geschäftsgrundlage (Section 313 BGB)

Section 313 BGB, codified during the 2002 Schuldrechtsmodernisierung, represents the legislative codification of the judicially developed Wegfall der Geschäftsgrundlage doctrine. This provision addresses situations that fall short of impossibility but involve a fundamental change in circumstances:

**Section 313(1):** If circumstances which became the foundation of a contract have significantly changed after its conclusion and if the parties would not have concluded the contract or would have concluded it on different terms if they had foreseen this change, adaptation of the contract may be demanded to the extent that adherence to the unaltered contract cannot reasonably be expected of one party, taking into account all the circumstances of the particular case, in particular the contractual or statutory allocation of risk.

**Section 313(3):** If adaptation of the contract is not possible or cannot reasonably be expected of one party, the disadvantaged party may revoke the contract. In the case of continuing obligations, the right of termination replaces the right of revocation.

### COVID-19 Application

German courts applied Section 313 BGB extensively to pandemic-related disputes. The Bundesgerichtshof (Federal Court of Justice) issued a landmark decision on January 12, 2022 (XII ZR 8/21), holding that government-ordered business closures during the COVID-19 pandemic constituted a disturbance of the foundation of the transaction for commercial lease agreements, entitling tenants to claim rent adjustment under Section 313 BGB. The Court established a framework requiring case-by-case assessment of the extent of business disruption, the tenant's use of government financial assistance, and the actual revenue loss attributable to the closure order.

### Höhere Gewalt in Specific Legislation

While the BGB does not use the term "force majeure" (höhere Gewalt) in its general provisions, the concept appears in specific German legislation including Section 206 BGB (suspension of limitation periods), Section 651h BGB (traveler's right to withdraw from package travel contracts due to unavoidable and extraordinary circumstances), and various regulatory statutes governing transport, energy, and telecommunications.

## ECHR & EU

### CISG Article 79

The United Nations Convention on Contracts for the International Sale of Goods (CISG), to which 97 states are party as of 2026, contains the most widely applicable international force majeure provision.

**Article 79(1):** A party is not liable for a failure to perform any of his obligations if he proves that the failure was due to an impediment beyond his control and that he could not reasonably be expected to have taken the impediment into account at the time of the conclusion of the contract or to have avoided or overcome it or its consequences.

**Article 79(2):** Extends the exemption to failures caused by impediments affecting third parties engaged to perform all or part of the contract.

**Article 79(3):** The exemption applies for the period during which the impediment exists.

**Article 79(4):** Imposes a duty to give notice of the impediment and its effect on performance within a reasonable time; failure to give notice renders the party liable for damages resulting from non-receipt of the notice.

**Article 79(5):** Clarifies that the exemption only prevents damages claims -- it does not prevent the other party from exercising any other right under the Convention, including price reduction or termination.

### UNIDROIT Principles

The UNIDROIT Principles of International Commercial Contracts, most recently revised in 2016, address force majeure in Article 7.1.7 and hardship in Articles 6.2.1-6.2.3. The distinction between force majeure (rendering performance impossible) and hardship (rendering performance excessively onerous) is a significant conceptual contribution that has influenced domestic legal reforms worldwide.

**Article 7.1.7 (Force Majeure):** Non-performance is excused if the party proves that non-performance was due to an impediment beyond its control and that it could not reasonably be expected to have taken the impediment into account at the time of the conclusion of the contract or to have avoided or overcome it or its consequences.

### ICC Force Majeure Clause 2020

The International Chamber of Commerce published its most recent model force majeure clause in March 2020, coinciding with the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic. The 2020 clause enumerates specific force majeure events in four categories: natural disasters (plague, epidemic, natural disaster); armed conflict and political events (war, armed conflict, embargo, sanctions); governmental actions (government act, compliance with law or order); and general impediments (act of God, general labor disturbance). The clause also includes provisions on notice requirements, proof obligations, mitigation duties, and the consequences for the contract (suspension, termination, and allocation of costs).

### European Contract Law Instruments

At the EU level, force majeure is addressed in several soft-law and model instruments:

**Principles of European Contract Law (PECL):** Articles 8:101 and 8:108 address excuse for non-performance due to an impediment beyond the obligor's control.

**Draft Common Frame of Reference (DCFR):** Book III, Section 3:104 addresses excuse due to an impediment.

**EU Public Procurement Directives:** The force majeure concept appears in EU public procurement law, where Directive 2014/24/EU permits modification of contracts during their term where the circumstances triggering modification could not be foreseen by a diligent contracting authority, effectively incorporating force majeure principles into public contract management.

## Comparative Analysis

### Doctrinal Foundations

The four legal systems reveal a fundamental divide in how force majeure is conceptualized. The civil law jurisdictions (South Korea, Germany) treat impossibility and changed circumstances as statutory doctrines that operate as default rules regardless of whether the contract addresses them. The common law system (United States) treats force majeure as an exclusively contractual concept, with the related doctrines of impossibility, impracticability, and frustration operating as narrow implied-in-law exceptions. The international instruments (CISG, UNIDROIT) attempt to bridge these traditions by establishing autonomous standards that draw from both civil and common law approaches.

### Hardship versus Force Majeure

A critical distinction runs through these legal systems concerning the boundary between force majeure (which renders performance impossible) and hardship (which makes performance excessively burdensome). Germany explicitly addresses both through Section 275 BGB (impossibility) and Section 313 BGB (changed circumstances), providing a graduated response from contract adaptation to termination. The UNIDROIT Principles similarly distinguish between force majeure (Article 7.1.7) and hardship (Articles 6.2.1-6.2.3). South Korea's Civil Code addresses impossibility but lacks an explicit hardship provision, though courts have applied principles of good faith to reach similar results. U.S. law recognizes economic impracticability under UCC Section 2-615 and the Restatement but applies it very restrictively, creating a significant gap between impossibility and mere economic difficulty.

### Pandemic Stress Test

The COVID-19 pandemic served as an unprecedented stress test for force majeure doctrines worldwide. Germany's Section 313 BGB proved the most adaptable, enabling courts to order proportional rent adjustments based on actual business disruption. South Korean courts similarly found grounds for rental relief in statutory impossibility provisions. U.S. courts produced the most variable results, with outcomes hinging on the specific language of contractual force majeure clauses -- parties without explicit pandemic or government action provisions frequently failed to obtain relief. The international arbitration community reported a significant increase in force majeure claims under the CISG and ICC Rules, with tribunals generally recognizing government lockdown orders as qualifying impediments while rejecting claims based solely on financial difficulty.

### Risk Allocation

All systems agree that force majeure operates as an exception to the fundamental principle of pacta sunt servanda (contracts must be kept). However, they differ in how they allocate the risk of unforeseen events: civil law systems tend to distribute the loss between the parties through doctrines of shared risk and proportional adjustment; common law systems tend toward an all-or-nothing approach where performance is either excused or required in full; and international instruments provide flexible frameworks that prioritize contract preservation through adaptation.
